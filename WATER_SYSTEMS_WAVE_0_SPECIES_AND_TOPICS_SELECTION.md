## Detailed Topic Specifications

### Topic 1: Boiling Water for Safety

**Common name**: How to boil water safely in an emergency

**Scientific/technical name**: Thermal inactivation of waterborne pathogens

**Primary use**: Water safety—emergency purification when other methods unavailable

**NCHFP/USDA source**:
- [CDC Emergency Water Disinfection](https://www.cdc.gov/healthywater/emergency/boiling.html) — URL: https://www.cdc.gov/healthywater/emergency/boiling.html
- [EPA Emergency Disinfection](https://www.epa.gov/water-research/emergency-disinfection-drinking-water) — URL: https://www.epa.gov/water-research/emergency-disinfection-drinking-water
- [NCHFP Water Safety for Canning](https://nchfp.uga.edu/how/can_process/water.html) — URL: https://nchfp.uga.edu/how/can_process/water.html

**Public-domain status**: Yes. CDC and EPA guidance is US government work (public domain). NCHFP is USDA (public domain).

**Contributor expertise required**: Water safety, field protocols. No specialized degree needed; practical emergency preparedness experience or certified water safety instructor preferred.

**Key parameters to include**:
- Boiling time at sea level: 1 minute (CDC)
- Boiling time at 6,500+ ft elevation: 3 minutes (CDC)
- Pathogens inactivated: bacteria, viruses, protozoa (Giardia, Cryptosporidium)
- Does NOT remove chemical contaminants or suspended solids
- Required containers, cooling method, storage

**Safety warnings**:
- Boiling does not reduce turbidity or remove dissolved chemicals
- Hot water hazard—safety handling required
- For chemically contaminated water, boiling is not sufficient

**Conservation/WASH angle**: Essential WASH strategy for low-income regions with limited electricity.

### Topic 2: Simple Filtration with Sand and Gravel

**Common name**: How to make a multi-layer sand filter for water purification

**Scientific/technical name**: Multi-stage gravity filtration (coarse sand + fine gravel + charcoal)

**Primary use**: Water purification—removes turbidity and many pathogens in field contexts

**NCHFP/USDA source**:
- [WHO WASH Manual Section 4.5](https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/WHO-SDE-WSH-02.09) — URL: https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/WHO-SDE-WSH-02.09
- [Practical Action Technical Brief PA47: Water treatment](https://practicalaction.org/knowledge-centre/technical-briefs/) (search "water treatment")
- [EPA Fact Sheet: Filtration](https://www.epa.gov/water-research/rapid-sand-filtration)

**Public-domain status**: Yes. WHO publishes under CC BY 4.0. EPA is public domain. Practical Action briefs are CC BY.

**Contributor expertise required**: Filtration engineering, field construction, basic materials science. Practical experience with filter maintenance preferred.

**Key parameters to include**:
- Layer order (from top): coarse sand, fine sand, gravel, charcoal (if available)
- Recommended depths for each layer (e.g., 1 m sand, 0.5 m gravel)
- Flow rate expectations (depends on sand grain size; ~1-2 m³/day per m² common)
- Turbidity reduction: 90%+ for suspended solids
- Pathogen reduction: 90%+ for bacteria, ~60% for viruses (limitations important)
- Material sourcing (beach sand vs. pit sand; grain size verification)

**Safety warnings**:
- Does NOT guarantee pathogen removal—boiling after filtration recommended for safety-critical water
- Sand must be washed/settled before use
- Filter requires periodic cleaning/replacement
- Does not remove dissolved chemicals

**Maintenance considerations**:
- Sand replacement frequency
- Biofilm formation and management
- Backflushing technique (if applicable)

### Topic 3: Chlorine Disinfection for Household Water

**Common name**: Disinfecting water with bleach or chlorine tablets

**Scientific/technical name**: Chemical disinfection via hypochlorite (sodium/calcium hypochlorite)

**Primary use**: Water safety—bacterial and viral pathogens in household quantities

**NCHFP/USDA source**:
- [CDC Water Disinfection with Bleach](https://www.cdc.gov/healthywater/emergency/create-safe-water.html) — URL: https://www.cdc.gov/healthywater/emergency/create-safe-water.html
- [EPA Disinfectant Residuals](https://www.epa.gov/water-research/disinfection-residuals-and-disinfection-byproducts-regulations)
- [NCHFP Canning Water Requirements](https://nchfp.uga.edu/how/can_process/water.html) (mentions chlorine in context of tap water quality)

**Public-domain status**: Yes. All US government sources.

**Contributor expertise required**: Water chemistry, chemical safety, quantitative measurement. Practical experience dosing household water preferred.

**Key parameters to include**:
- Bleach concentration: 3–8% sodium hypochlorite (household bleach typically 5–6%)
- Dosing table: parts per million (ppm) based on turbidity and volume
  - Clear water: 2 drops per liter (1 ppm) or 0.5 mL per gallon
  - Turbid water: 4 drops per liter (2 ppm) or 1 mL per gallon
  - Very turbid water: Filter first, then dose
- Contact time: 30 minutes minimum for bacterial safety
- Chlorine tablets: typically 50 ppm/tablet for 1 liter (follow package instructions)
- Testing: chlorine test strips (optional but recommended)

**Safety warnings**:
- Bleach is caustic—avoid skin/eye contact; use proper ventilation
- Does NOT remove turbidity—filter first if water is cloudy
- Does NOT remove dissolved chemicals (heavy metals, pesticides)
- Water should smell slightly of chlorine when ready to drink (residual protection)
- Chlorine is toxic to fish—do not overdose
- Not for long-term storage of large quantities (breaks down over time)

**Limitations**:
- Some protozoa (Cryptosporidium) are chlorine-resistant—boiling or advanced filtration needed for full protection

### Topic 4: Ceramic Filter Pot Construction

**Common name**: Making a ceramic pot water filter (DIY bucket filter)

**Scientific/technical name**: Gravity-fed ceramic candle filtration system

**Primary use**: Water purification—accessible to households with limited resources

**NCHFP/USDA source**:
- [WHO WASH Manual — Household Water Treatment](https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/WHO-SDE-WSH-02.09)
- [Practical Action Brief PA41: Ceramic Pot Filters](https://practicalaction.org/knowledge-centre/technical-briefs/)
- [ICRC Water, Sanitation and Hygiene Promotion](https://www.icrc.org/)

**Public-domain status**: Yes. WHO CC BY 4.0, Practical Action CC BY, ICRC public use.

**Contributor expertise required**: Ceramics/clay working, filtration design, field construction. Practical filter-making experience essential.

**Key parameters to include**:
- Materials: ceramic pot (or clay pot fired in kiln), sand, gravel, charcoal, outer bucket
- Ceramic specifications: pore size ~2–20 microns (affects filtration effectiveness)
- Assembly order: outer bucket → sand layer → ceramic pot → gravel layer (water flows down through ceramic)
- Flow rate: ~1–2 liters/hour (slow but safe)
- Turbidity reduction: >99% suspended solids
- Bacterial reduction: 99%+ (viruses partially reduced; boiling recommended for viral safety)
- Maintenance: cleaning ceramic candle monthly with soft brush, replacing every 1–2 years

**Safety warnings**:
- Ceramic pots must be food-grade clay (avoid clay from contaminated sources)
- Not effective against viruses alone—combine with boiling or chlorine if needed
- Ceramic can crack if dropped or frozen
- Regular maintenance required or filter becomes ineffective

**Regional considerations**:
- Clay sourcing varies by region—procedure must account for local availability
- Kiln firing temperature affects pore size—working with local potters recommended

### Topic 5: Rainwater Collection and Storage Safety

**Common name**: Collecting and storing rainwater safely for household use

**Scientific/technical name**: Rainwater harvesting systems with microbial and chemical contamination prevention

**Primary use**: Water access—alternative supply for non-potable uses; treated for potable use

**NCHFP/USDA source**:
- [EPA Rainwater Harvesting Guidance](https://www.epa.gov/waterguide/rainwater-harvesting) — URL: https://www.epa.gov/waterguide/rainwater-harvesting
- [Texas A&M AgriLife Extension: Rainwater Harvesting](https://rainwaterharvesting.tamu.edu/) — CC BY compatible
- [CDC Rainwater Harvesting Safety](https://www.cdc.gov/healthywater/other/rainwater.html)

**Public-domain status**: Yes. EPA, CDC (public domain), Texas A&M Extension (public use).

**Contributor expertise required**: Environmental engineering, sanitation, mosquito/vector control. Practical rainwater harvesting experience preferred.

**Key parameters to include**:
- Catchment area calculation (roof area × rainfall depth = collection volume)
- First-flush diverter: removes initial dirty runoff (typically first 10–20 gallons)
- Storage tank selection: food-grade plastic, sealed (prevents mosquito breeding)
- Roof material considerations: avoid asbestos shingles, lead-based paint
- Storage volume: typically 1,000–5,000 gallons for household backup
- Sediment settling: minimum 48 hours before use or drawing from bottom
- Filtration: sand/gravel filter layer recommended before storage

**Non-potable vs. potable use**:
- Non-potable: garden irrigation, toilet flushing, cleaning (minimal treatment)
- Potable: requires complete water treatment (filtration + chlorination/boiling)

**Safety warnings**:
- Prevent mosquito breeding: all tanks must be sealed with screens
- Heavy rainfall can overwhelm tank capacity and overflow
- Gutters accumulate debris and animal droppings—gutters must be cleaned regularly
- Roof-harvested water should not be assumed safe to drink without treatment
- Check local regulations (some jurisdictions restrict rainwater collection)

**Conservation angle**: Critical for water-scarce regions and drought resilience.

### Topic 6: Water Testing Without Laboratory Equipment

**Common name**: Testing water quality using sight, smell, and simple taste methods (field assessment)

**Scientific/technical name**: Organoleptic water quality assessment + basic turbidity evaluation

**Primary use**: Water quality verification—field assessment when lab testing unavailable

**NCHFP/USDA source**:
- [WHO Field Guide to Water Quality Assessment](https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/WHO-SDE-WSH-02.09) — Section on basic testing
- [CDC Water Quality Testing](https://www.cdc.gov/healthywater/drinking/household_water/testing.html)
- [EPA Drinking Water Quality Standards](https://www.epa.gov/dwstandardsregulations)

**Public-domain status**: Yes. WHO CC BY 4.0, CDC and EPA public domain.

**Contributor expertise required**: Water microbiology, field assessment, basic chemistry. No lab equipment required; field experience essential.

**Key parameters to include**:
- Visual assessment: turbidity, color, particles (look for cloudiness)
- Odor testing: smell for hydrogen sulfide ("rotten eggs"), chlorine, musty/earthy odors
- Taste testing: tiny taste (not swallowing) for saltiness, metallic taste, bitterness
- Presence of sediment: settle water for 30 minutes, observe bottom
- What these signals mean: color indicates iron/tannins, odor suggests bacteria, taste suggests minerals/contamination
- Limitations: organoleptic testing does NOT detect many pathogens or dissolved chemicals

**Safety warnings**:
- NEVER fully taste-test unknown water—tiny tasting only
- Organoleptic testing gives directional signals, not proof of safety
- Many dangerous pathogens (E. coli, Giardia, viruses) produce no taste, odor, or visible signs
- If in doubt, boil or chemically treat water

**Field kit option**:
- Turbidity tube (DIY with clear bottle marked with depth lines)
- Chlorine test strips (if available)
- Visual color reference card

### Topic 7: Solar Still for Saltwater Desalination (Emergency)

**Common name**: Building a solar still to make freshwater from saltwater or brackish water

**Scientific/technical name**: Solar distillation via evaporation-condensation (appropriate-tech desalination)

**Primary use**: Water access (emergency)—converts saltwater/brackish to potable with no fuel input

**NCHFP/USDA source**:
- [WHO Emergency Water Supplies Manual](https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/WHO-SDE-WSH-02.09) — Section on distillation
- [Practical Action Solar Stills Brief](https://practicalaction.org/knowledge-centre/technical-briefs/)
- [US Navy Manual: Survival at Sea (public domain)](https://www.survivalmonkey.com/)

**Public-domain status**: Yes. WHO CC BY 4.0, Practical Action CC BY, US Navy public domain.

**Contributor expertise required**: Physics (evaporation/condensation), appropriate-tech engineering, survival skills. Practical solar still construction experience essential.

**Key parameters to include**:
- Materials: clear plastic sheet (6+ mil), dark ground surface, container, collection vessel
- Design: pit or basin with saltwater at bottom, plastic cover sloping toward collection point
- Depth: 2–3 feet recommended for optimal temperature differential
- Production rate: 1–2 liters per square meter of plastic per day (location/sun-dependent)
- Maintenance: refill saltwater daily, clean condensation on plastic weekly
- Output: water should be distilled (pure); post-treatment not needed for basic pathogens

**Safety warnings**:
- Solar stills produce water VERY slowly—not feasible for large-volume needs
- Requires consistent sun exposure (unreliable in cloudy/winter conditions)
- Plastic degradation over time (UV); plan for replacement every 1–2 years
- Not suitable for chemically contaminated water (volatile compounds may evaporate and recondense)

**Limitations**:
- Productivity drops in winter/high latitudes
- Sealed design can overheat and damage plastic in very hot climates
- Labor-intensive setup and monitoring

**Conservation angle**: Zero-fuel water production for island/coastal communities with limited resources.

### Topic 8: Preventing Water Contamination During Storage

**Common name**: How to store water safely to prevent microbial contamination

**Scientific/technical name**: Post-treatment water storage best practices (microbial control)

**NCHFP/USDA source**:
- [CDC Stored Water Safety](https://www.cdc.gov/healthywater/emergency/drinking-water-storage.html) — URL: https://www.cdc.gov/healthywater/emergency/drinking-water-storage.html
- [USDA Emergency Preparedness: Water Storage](https://www.usda.gov/)
- [EPA Drinking Water Storage](https://www.epa.gov/water-research)

**Public-domain status**: Yes. All US government sources.

**Contributor expertise required**: Microbiology, sanitation, water storage systems. Practical experience storing water for emergency/household use.

**Key parameters to include**:
- Container selection: food-grade plastic (HDPE) or glass (avoid metal if chlorinated)
- Container prep: wash with soap/water before first use, rinse thoroughly
- Storage location: cool, dark place (not direct sunlight) at 50–70°F if possible
- Sealing: containers must be sealed tightly (prevents mosquito breeding and dust)
- Chlorination for long-term storage: 2–8 ppm residual chlorine (use test strips to verify)
- Rotation schedule: replace storage water every 6 months (chlorinated water) or 3 months (non-chlorinated)
- Volume calculation: 1 gallon per person per day minimum

**Maintenance**:
- Check containers monthly for cracks, algae growth, contamination
- Keep inventory of stored water location and expiration dates
- Replace sediment-clouded water (indicates microbial growth or algae)

**Safety warnings**:
- Standing water breeds mosquitoes—keep containers sealed
- Do NOT store water in containers that previously held chemicals, pesticides, or gasoline
- Avoid direct sunlight (promotes algae growth)
- Chlorinated water tastes unpleasant but is safe to drink

**Shelf life**:
- Chlorinated stored water: 6–12 months
- Non-chlorinated: 1–3 months (boil before drinking)
- Bottled commercial water: 2+ years (check manufacturer)
