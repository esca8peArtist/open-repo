# Phase 5.2 Water Systems Knowledge Domains Definition

**Purpose**: Define the four primary content domains for the Water Systems pillar of open-repo's Phase 5.2 launch. Each domain includes scope definition, intended audience, learning outcomes, and rationale for inclusion.

**Prerequisite**: Audience for Water Systems content is established as global WASH practitioners, rural/off-grid designers, emergency preparedness communities, and permaculture educators. All domains prioritize practical, actionable procedures over encyclopedic knowledge.

---

## Domain 1: Water Assessment & Testing

**Scope** (85 words):
Practical methods for evaluating water quality and safety without laboratory equipment. Covers visual assessment (color, turbidity, odor), field testing kits (bacterial, chemical, pH), and parametric interpretation frameworks. Readers learn what makes water unsafe, what parameters matter most in resource-constrained contexts, how to choose between rapid test kits, and how to document and share testing data. Audience: field health workers, rural water system designers, emergency responders, and community water monitors who need defensible evidence of water quality in minutes, not weeks.

**Why this domain matters**:
- Wikipedia covers water chemistry encyclopedically; lacks field procedures for rapid assessment
- WHO WASH manuals include testing protocols but not in structured, offline, searchable form
- No existing source consolidates field testing methods + interpretation + data-sharing standards in one practical guide
- Gap: practitioners have tests but don't know when results indicate "needs treatment" vs. "safe to use"

**Audience breakdown**:
- Field health workers in under-resourced regions (priority audience)
- Water system designers assessing well/spring/surface water sources
- Emergency responders after flooding or contamination events
- Community health volunteers and school water monitors
- Off-grid homeowners managing private water systems

## Domain 2: Rainwater Harvesting & Storage

**Scope** (92 words):
Complete design, installation, and maintenance of rainwater harvesting systems from concept through long-term operation. Covers system sizing (catchment area to tank volume calculation), material selection (tank types, roofing vs. ground collection), filtration design (first-flush separators, sediment filters), water storage maintenance (algae control, contamination prevention), and legal/regulatory context by US state and international context. Readers learn whether rainwater harvesting is feasible for their site, how to size a system for their rainfall and needs, how to build basic systems from local materials, and how to maintain water quality. Audience: homeowners, farmers, permaculture designers, and community organizers in water-stressed regions.

**Why this domain matters**:
- No single source combines sizing calculations + legal frameworks + construction procedures + maintenance protocols
- Existing DIY guides are often inaccurate on sizing or ignore local regulations
- Permaculture community (strong open-repo contributor pool) treats rainwater harvesting as core design element but lacks offline reference
- Gap: practitioners start projects without understanding local legality, sizing consequences, or long-term maintenance costs

**Audience breakdown**:
- Homeowners with roof space or land seeking water independence
- Small-scale farmers and market gardeners reducing groundwater dependence
- Permaculture designers incorporating water into property layouts
- Community organizations in drought-prone regions
- Rural and off-grid residents expanding water resilience

## Domain 3: Water Treatment & Purification

**Scope** (88 words):
Methods for making unsafe or uncertain water safe for drinking and domestic use. Covers boiling (altitude-adjusted time), chemical treatment (chlorine/iodine dosing by water volume and turbidity), physical filtration (sand, ceramic, cloth filters), multi-stage treatment sequences, and when to use each method. Includes WHO and CDC sourced parameters, construction methods for low-cost filters (slow sand, biosand), troubleshooting common failures, and when treatment is insufficient (i.e., when microbes require professional laboratory water testing). Audience: emergency responders, humanitarian field staff, rural communities, and preppers seeking reliable offline reference with evidence-based dosing.

**Why this domain matters**:
- Wikipedia covers treatment methods scientifically but lacks procedural detail for field application
- WHO manuals are authoritative but not searchable or well-indexed for practitioners in a hurry
- No structured resource covers the decision tree: "I have [water type] and [equipment]—what's the minimum treatment to make it safe?"
- Gap: practitioners guess at chlorine doses, boiling times at altitude, or use outdated folk methods due to inaccessible authoritative guidance

**Audience breakdown**:
- Emergency responders and disaster relief coordinators
- MSF/humanitarian field logisticians and medics
- Rural communities with contaminated wells or surface water dependence
- Preppers and off-grid residents
- School and community health workers in LMIC settings

## Domain 4: Wastewater & Greywater Systems

**Scope** (90 words):
On-site treatment and reuse of residential and small-scale agricultural wastewater. Covers greywater classification (sources, contaminant profiles), simple treatment methods (settling tanks, sand/gravel filtration, constructed wetlands), reuse applications (landscape irrigation, toilet flushing), code compliance frameworks by US state and region, and septic system maintenance for properties with on-site treatment. Includes design principles for low-cost decentralized systems and cultural/regulatory considerations for different contexts. Audience: off-grid homeowners, rural communities, permaculture designers, and humanitarian engineers designing systems for regions with limited centralized infrastructure.

**Why this domain matters**:
- Septic system maintenance is widely understood; decentralized greywater reuse is not
- No accessible guide addresses code compliance + practical design for homeowner-scale systems
- Permaculture community strongly values water cycling; lacks offline reference for design procedures
- Gap: practitioners often overengineer or misunderstand local regulations, leading to failed projects or non-compliant installations

**Audience breakdown**:
- Off-grid and rural homeowners
- Permaculture designers incorporating water cycling into landscape design
- Humanitarian engineers in regions without centralized sewage treatment
- Residential builders and contractors in water-scarce regions
- Community organizers designing decentralized systems for rural settlements

## Cross-Domain Considerations

### Content Sourcing Priorities
All four domains prioritize sources in this order:
1. **Government/Public Health Authority** (WHO, CDC, EPA, USDA, NCHFP, MSF): Highest credibility, often already public domain
2. **Peer-Reviewed Academic** (journals, university extension publications): Verifiable, citable
3. **Practitioner Networks** (RWSN, IRC, Practical Action, community organizations): Real-world tested methods
4. **Experienced Contributors**: Domain expertise from field practitioners or system designers, vetted through review process

### Safety & Disclaimer Framework
- Water quality testing procedures: general procedures flagged for testing accuracy/false negatives
- Boiling/chlorine/filtration: sourced directly from WHO/CDC/EPA; disclaimers re: not for chemical contamination (boiling) or heavy metals (standard filtration)
- Greywater/septic: all procedures include "verify with local code before installation"; no procedures presented as universally legal
- Rainwater harvesting: procedures include state-by-state legality notes; designed to be implemented only where legal

### Offline Usability Requirements
All procedures designed for offline, no-search-engine access:
- Standalone scope (procedure works without reading cross-references)
- Plain language (explains technical terms; assumes no prior knowledge)
- Visual-friendly formatting (list-based, numbered steps, tables for parameters)
- Printable (readable on paper; good for emergency printing)
- Searchable metadata (domain, problem-type, equipment-needed tags for offline indexing)

## Measurement & Iteration

Wave 0 measurement gates at Week 6, Week 8, and Week 12 will track:
- **Domain coverage**: Which domains are producing contributor interest? (Expected: Treatment & Testing > Rainwater > Greywater)
- **Procedure quality**: Error rate in published content (target: 0 safety-critical errors; <1 minor error per 10 procedures)
- **Audience fit**: Which procedure types generate the most page views? (Expected: Treatment & Testing highest; may shift over time as search engine indexing matures)

Based on metrics, Wave 1 may expand or compress domains or add sub-domains (e.g., "Urban Water Conservation" if rooftop/apartment focus emerges as high-interest signal).
