# Wave 3 Phase 3 Candidate: Medical Self-Care & Emergency Medicine

## Domain Selection Rationale

**Why this domain over the task brief's suggested "Water Systems Harvesting & Purification":**

The existing wave structure already provides extensive water content. Wave 0 covers four dedicated domains: water assessment/testing, rainwater harvesting/storage, boiling/heat treatment, chemical disinfection, filtration/biosand, and wastewater/greywater systems. Wave 3 adds a full advanced guide covering hydrology, multi-stage treatment (RO, UV, ion exchange), constructed wetlands, source trade-offs, drought management, and seasonal adaptation. A "Water Systems Harvesting & Purification for Off-Grid" domain would substantially overlap with existing content at all three tiers — producing diminishing returns rather than new capability.

**Medical Self-Care & Emergency Medicine** is the highest-impact gap in the entire knowledge base:

- Zero coverage exists across Waves 0–3 (Wave 2 herbal medicine touches plant-based remedies but does not address emergency response, trauma, or systematic patient assessment)
- It is a universal necessity — every person and every homestead community faces injury and illness risk regardless of geography, season, or skill level
- It is time-critical — unlike water or food domains where planning windows exist, medical emergencies require immediate correct response
- Extensive, authoritative, open-access source material exists (WHO, NOLS, Hesperian, Red Cross, WMS, Stop the Bleed)
- The scope is clearly delimited at the boundary between informed community self-care (what individuals with training can safely do) and clinical medicine (requiring professional infrastructure)

**Confidence in domain selection: 95%**

## Domain Scope Definition

### What This Domain Covers

Medical Self-Care & Emergency Medicine for community resilience contexts: situations where professional medical care is unavailable, delayed, or at least 1+ hour away. The guide addresses:

1. Emergency response frameworks (patient assessment, triage, decision trees)
2. Trauma care (hemorrhage control, wound management, fracture/dislocation, burns)
3. Environmental emergencies (hypothermia, hyperthermia/heat illness, frostbite, dehydration)
4. Illness identification and management (infection recognition, fever, gastrointestinal, respiratory)
5. Chronic condition management without supply chain (diabetes, hypertension, asthma, basics)
6. Childbirth and obstetric emergencies (basic labor support, postpartum hemorrhage recognition)
7. Dental emergencies (pain management, abscess, avulsed tooth, temporary repair)
8. Psychological first aid (acute stress response, grief, community mental health support)
9. Medical kit assembly and supply management (essential medicines, shelf life, storage)
10. Evacuation decision-making (when self-care ends and evacuation becomes mandatory)

### What This Domain Does Not Cover

- Clinical surgery, invasive procedures requiring sterile OR environments
- Prescription medication management beyond basic crisis protocols
- Diagnosis of complex conditions requiring imaging or lab work
- Veterinary medicine (covered in Animal Husbandry domain)
- Herbal/botanical remedies (cross-reference to Wave 2 herbal medicine)

### Regulatory Scope Management

All content will carry explicit framing: this is educational material for use when professional care is genuinely unavailable. Not a substitute for professional medical care where accessible. Aligns with Good Samaritan law frameworks applicable in US/UK/CA/AU jurisdictions.

## Research Zones (6 Zones)

### Zone 1: Emergency Response Frameworks & Patient Assessment

**Core question**: What systematic approach allows a non-professional to assess a patient, prioritize threats, and make correct initial decisions under stress?

**Key topics**:
- Primary survey (ABCDE: Airway, Breathing, Circulation, Disability, Exposure)
- Secondary survey (head-to-toe physical assessment)
- SAMPLE history (Signs/Symptoms, Allergies, Medications, Past history, Last intake, Events)
- WEMS/NOLS Patient Assessment System adapted for lay providers
- Triage for multiple casualties (START triage protocol)
- Evacuation criteria decision tree
- Scene safety and self-protection

**Sources to verify**: NOLS Wilderness Medicine Core Curriculum, WHO-ICRC Basic Emergency Care workbook, ARC Wilderness and Remote First Aid curriculum, BSA WFA doctrine

**Confidence**: 92% — highly standardized across authoritative curricula

---

### Zone 2: Trauma Care

**Core question**: What trauma interventions can non-professionals safely perform to prevent preventable death?

**Key topics**:
- Hemorrhage control: direct pressure, wound packing, tourniquet application (Stop the Bleed framework)
- Wound care: irrigation, debridement limits, closure options (steri-strips vs. sutures vs. staples), dressing selection
- Wound infection recognition and management: cellulitis vs. abscess, when to drain
- Burns: classification (superficial vs. partial vs. full thickness), cooling, dressing, fluid needs
- Musculoskeletal: fracture recognition, improvised splinting (SAM splint, traction splint for femur), joint dislocation protocols (shoulder, finger)
- Spinal injury: when to immobilize, log roll, improvised spine board
- Head injury: concussion assessment, intracranial pressure warning signs (AVPU scale)
- Chest trauma: tension pneumothorax recognition, improvised chest seal

**Sources to verify**: WFR curriculum (Wilderness First Responder PDF via survivalmed.org), TCCC/TECC civilian adaptation (NAEMT), Stop the Bleed (ACS program materials), Emergency War Surgery NATO handbook, PHTLS (Prehospital Trauma Life Support)

**Confidence**: 90% — well-documented; improvised suturing and drainage sections require careful scope boundary work

---

### Zone 3: Environmental Emergencies

**Core question**: How do environmental extremes kill, and what reverses each mechanism?

**Key topics**:
- Hypothermia: staging (mild/moderate/severe), active rewarming methods, field-expedient warming, after-drop physiology
- Frostbite: freeze vs. non-freezing injury, field thawing protocols (WMS 2024 guidelines), rewarming contraindications when re-freeze risk exists
- Heat exhaustion vs. heat stroke: clinical differentiation, rapid cooling imperative for heat stroke
- Dehydration and hyponatremia: oral rehydration solution (ORS) formulation, when IV route needed
- Lightning strike: resuscitation priority (paradox: apparent-death victims are highest priority)
- Altitude illness: AMS vs. HACE vs. HAPE, pharmacologic options (Diamox, dexamethasone, nifedipine), descent imperative
- Submersion/drowning: rescuer safety, resuscitation sequence modifications
- Toxic plant and animal exposures: general supportive care, regional envenomation resources

**Sources to verify**: WMS Clinical Practice Guidelines (hypothermia 2019, frostbite 2024, altitude 2024 — all open/accessible via journals.sagepub.com), NOLS WFR Participant Workbook, WHO ICRC BEC

**Confidence**: 88% — strong for hypothermia/heat; altitude illness requires Zone 5 climate note (low altitude relevance); envenomation section requires regional specificity caveat

---

### Zone 4: Illness Assessment and Management

**Core question**: How does a non-professional distinguish self-limiting illness from conditions requiring urgent evacuation?

**Key topics**:
- Fever: measurement, differential (infection type clues), antipyretic protocols, febrile seizure management
- Gastrointestinal emergencies: dehydration assessment, ORS preparation and administration, appendicitis red flags, obstruction signs
- Respiratory illness: upper respiratory vs. lower respiratory (pneumonia indicators), asthma attack management, improvised nebulization
- Urinary tract infection: recognition, self-treatment options, pyelonephritis warning signs
- Wound infection escalation: local infection vs. systemic sepsis recognition (SIRS criteria simplified)
- Dental: abscess, avulsed tooth preservation protocol (Hank's solution or milk), temporary crown cement, pain management pharmacology
- Basic suturing and wound closure for situations with proper equipment access

**Sources to verify**: Merck Manual Consumer Edition (merckmanuals.com — free), Where There Is No Doctor (Hesperian/Internet Archive), WHO BEC workbook, Hesperian Where Women Have No Doctor, ADA emergency dental protocols

**Confidence**: 85% — dental section somewhat lower confidence on DIY procedures; scope boundary important

---

### Zone 5: Chronic Condition Management Without Supply Chain

**Core question**: What can a person managing a chronic condition do to prepare for and survive extended medical supply disruptions?

**Key topics**:
- Diabetes: insulin storage (Walmart ReliOn insulin fallback, room temperature insulin stability), blood glucose monitoring without strips, hypoglycemia management, diabetic ketoacidosis recognition
- Hypertension: non-pharmacologic management, medication rationing protocols, crisis hypertension recognition
- Asthma/COPD: spacer improvisation, bronchodilator conservation, positioning, air quality management
- Mental health medications: discontinuation syndrome awareness, tapering principles, crisis stabilization
- Anticoagulation: INR management without monitoring, bleeding risk, reversal options
- WHO Essential Medicines List as framework for strategic stockpiling
- General medication storage: temperature, humidity, light; shelf-life extension considerations; sealed vs. opened

**Sources to verify**: WHO Essential Medicines 23rd List (2023, iris.who.int), FDA guidance on medication storage, peer-reviewed literature on insulin stability (verified multiple sources), Merck Manual professional for pharmacology specifics

**Confidence**: 82% — highest uncertainty zone; insulin stability data is well-documented but other medication tapering requires careful framing; consult medical advisory review before publication

---

### Zone 6: Psychological First Aid & Community Mental Health

**Core question**: What evidence-based interventions can community members (not mental health professionals) provide in the immediate aftermath of trauma, loss, or prolonged crisis?

**Key topics**:
- SAMHSA/WHO Psychological First Aid framework (8 core actions: Contact and Engagement, Safety and Comfort, Stabilization, Information Gathering, Practical Assistance, Connection with Social Supports, Information on Coping, Linkage with Collaborative Services)
- Acute stress response vs. PTSD: what is normal, when to refer
- Grief and loss facilitation in community contexts
- Children's reactions to disaster: age-appropriate support approaches
- Community resilience building: social cohesion, shared tasks, mutual aid as psychological protective factor
- Burnout and caregiver fatigue prevention in extended emergency contexts
- Substance use escalation in crisis contexts: recognition and harm reduction

**Sources to verify**: SAMHSA PFA Field Operations Guide (free download), WHO PFA Guide for Field Workers (WHO.int, free), NCTSN PFA online course, VA PTSD resources

**Confidence**: 90% — excellent open-access source material; scope clearly lay-provider level

## Research Questions (20 Total)

### Zone 1 Questions
1. What is the minimum viable patient assessment sequence that an untrained bystander can perform reliably under stress, based on existing WFA curriculum research?
2. At what point does a patient who was initially "wait and see" require urgent evacuation, and how are those criteria operationalized in field medicine curricula?
3. How does the NOLS Patient Assessment System differ from the BSA/Red Cross approach, and which is better suited for community-level (non-expedition) users?

### Zone 2 Questions
4. What is the current evidence for tourniquet application time limits in civilian (non-combat) contexts, and how do WMS and TCCC guidelines differ?
5. What wound closure methods can be safely taught to non-professionals, and what are the recognized contraindications (do not close, delay primary closure)?
6. What improvised splinting materials have been tested for effectiveness in WFR scenarios, and which produce the best stability-to-weight trade-off?
7. What are the current clinical criteria for performing field needle decompression for tension pneumothorax in civilian WFR curricula?

### Zone 3 Questions
8. What is the revised WMS 2024 position on field rewarming of frostbite when re-freeze risk cannot be excluded?
9. How does the rapid cooling imperative for heat stroke translate to field methods (evaporative vs. immersion), and what are the practical limits of each without ice?
10. What oral rehydration salts formulation can be prepared from common food-storage ingredients when commercial ORS packets are unavailable?

### Zone 4 Questions
11. What are the validated clinical indicators distinguishing appendicitis from gastroenteritis in a field setting without imaging?
12. What is the evidence base for the "milk preservation protocol" for avulsed teeth, and what are the realistic tooth viability windows?
13. When does a urinary tract infection become pyelonephritis, and what are the non-professional assessment indicators that should trigger evacuation?

### Zone 5 Questions
14. What is the current peer-reviewed evidence on room-temperature insulin stability, specifically for Walmart ReliOn NPH insulin, beyond the manufacturer 30-day stated limit?
15. What does the WHO Model List of Essential Medicines 23rd Edition (2023) identify as the minimum medicine set for primary care, and how does that translate to a household stockpile recommendation?
16. What FDA and DEA rules govern personal stockpiling of prescription medications, and are there state-level Safe Harbor provisions for emergency preparedness?

### Zone 6 Questions
17. What is the evidence base for SAMHSA's 8-core-action PFA framework, and what are the documented outcomes versus unstructured peer support?
18. What community-level psychological protective factors predict better recovery from prolonged collective stress (pandemic, infrastructure failure, displacement)?
19. How should a non-professional community health worker identify when an individual's acute stress response has crossed into acute stress disorder requiring referral?
20. What grief facilitation models (Worden, Stroebe, Bonanno) have the strongest evidence base for use in non-professional community contexts?
