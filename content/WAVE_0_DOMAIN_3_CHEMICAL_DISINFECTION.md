# Chemical Disinfection: Sodium Hypochlorite, Pool Shock, and Alternatives

## Overview & Value

Chemical disinfection — primarily using chlorine compounds — is the second most universally recommended water treatment method after boiling. It is the treatment of choice when fuel for boiling is unavailable, when you need to treat larger volumes than boiling allows, or when water needs to be stored with ongoing protection against recontamination.

Chemical disinfection is how municipal water systems protect drinking water globally. It is also reliable, inexpensive, and achievable with household bleach or emergency pool chemicals available at most hardware stores.

**Who should read this**: Anyone treating water during an emergency, managing a rainwater storage system, treating a well after contamination, or preparing a household water supply for a period of municipal supply disruption. Also essential for anyone who has read the boiling article (3.1) and wants a second treatment option for situations where fire or stove access is limited.

**What you'll learn**: The exact dosing formula for sodium hypochlorite bleach at multiple concentrations, how to treat cloudy water, how to use calcium hypochlorite (pool shock) as a long-term storage option, what chemical disinfection cannot remove, and how to verify treatment was effective.

**Key decision points**:
- What concentration is your bleach? (Determines dose — see Section 2)
- Is your water cloudy? (Turbidity above ~1 NTU requires pre-filtration and doubled dose — see Section 3)
- Do you need to treat for Cryptosporidium? (Chlorine alone is insufficient — see Section 5)

## Section 1: Chemical Disinfection Basics — How It Works

Chlorine (in various chemical forms) kills waterborne pathogens by oxidizing the cell membranes of bacteria and inactivating viruses through disruption of nucleic acids. The key variables that determine effectiveness are:

1. **Chlorine concentration in the water** (measured in mg/L or ppm — parts per million)
2. **Contact time** — the time the water must sit before it is safe to drink
3. **Water turbidity** — cloudy water reduces effectiveness because particles shield pathogens
4. **Water temperature** — cold water requires longer contact time
5. **Water pH** — slightly acidic water (pH 6–8) improves disinfection effectiveness; strongly alkaline water reduces it

The standard goal is a **free residual chlorine** concentration of 0.2–0.5 mg/L after the contact period. This level kills bacteria and viruses while remaining safe to drink and leaving a faint chemical smell that indicates disinfection occurred.

**Residual protection**: Unlike boiling, chlorination leaves a residual disinfectant in the water. If stored in a covered container without contamination, chlorinated water remains protected for 24–72 hours (longer in cool conditions). This is the key advantage of chlorination over boiling for water storage.

## Section 2: Standard Dose — Sodium Hypochlorite (Liquid Bleach)

### 2.1 Identify Your Bleach Concentration

The single most important step before treating water with bleach is confirming the sodium hypochlorite concentration on the bottle label. This has changed significantly in the US market in recent years: what was universally 5–6% is now commonly sold at 8.25%.

**Use only unscented, additive-free bleach with no detergents or thickeners**. Scented bleach, "splashless" bleach, and bleach with added surfactants are not safe for water treatment. The label must list only sodium hypochlorite and water as active/inactive ingredients.

**Common bleach concentrations currently available in the US**:

| Label Claim | Sodium Hypochlorite % | Examples |
|-------------|----------------------|----------|
| "Regular" unscented | 5–6% | Older Clorox, generic store brand |
| "Concentrated" unscented | 8–8.25% | Current Clorox, most major brands 2020–present |
| "Ultra" concentrated | 10% | Some store brands; less common |
| Germicidal/hospital grade | 5.25–6% | Available at janitorial supply; lab grade |

**If you cannot read the label** (container is damaged or label is missing): Use the 8.25% dose. This is the safer direction — if the bleach is actually less concentrated, you will under-dose slightly, which may be insufficient; however, for the concentrations you will encounter in US household products, the difference between 6% and 8.25% dose is small enough that the 8.25% dose applied to 6% bleach will not reach harmful levels.

### 2.2 Standard Dose Table — Liquid Bleach

All doses are for **clear water** (turbidity <1 NTU — water you can see through clearly). See Section 3 for cloudy water.

**Contact time**: After adding bleach and mixing, wait a minimum of **30 minutes** before drinking at water temperatures above 15°C (59°F). Wait **60 minutes** if water temperature is below 15°C (cold well water, winter conditions). [Sources: 1, 4]

**Standard dose — 6% sodium hypochlorite**:

| Water Volume | Drops of Bleach | Approximate Measurement |
|-------------|-----------------|-------------------------|
| 1 liter (1 quart) | 2 drops | Less than 1/8 teaspoon |
| 4 liters (1 gallon) | 8 drops | Approximately 1/8 teaspoon |
| 20 liters (5 gallons) | 40 drops | Approximately 1/2 teaspoon |
| 100 liters (~26 gallons) | 200 drops | Approximately 2.5 teaspoons |

**Standard dose — 8.25% sodium hypochlorite (concentrated bleach)**:

| Water Volume | Drops of Bleach | Approximate Measurement |
|-------------|-----------------|-------------------------|
| 1 liter (1 quart) | 1.5 drops (round up to 2) | Less than 1/8 teaspoon |
| 4 liters (1 gallon) | 6 drops | Slightly less than 1/8 teaspoon |
| 20 liters (5 gallons) | 30 drops | Approximately 3/8 teaspoon |
| 100 liters (~26 gallons) | 150 drops | Approximately 2 teaspoons |

**Working with drops**: A standard eyedropper or a bleach bottle cap with a small hole delivers approximately 20–25 drops per milliliter. The CDC specifies 8 drops of 6% bleach per gallon of clear water as its standard recommendation. This corresponds to approximately 0.75 mL per gallon, or a 3 mg/L initial chlorine dose that produces approximately 0.5 mg/L free residual after contact time. [Source: 1]

### 2.3 Step-by-Step Procedure — Liquid Bleach

1. **Confirm bleach is appropriate**: Check label for concentration, confirm unscented with no additives, check expiration date (bleach degrades over time — see Section 2.4 on bleach shelf life).

2. **Assess water clarity**: Hold a container of water up to light. If you can see large print through it, turbidity is probably acceptable for single-dose treatment. If cloudy, see Section 3.

3. **Measure dose**: Use the table above. If you have no dropper, use a measuring spoon:
   - 1/8 teaspoon ≈ 35–40 drops (approximately the 1-gallon dose for 6% bleach, close enough)
   - 1/4 teaspoon ≈ 70–80 drops

4. **Add bleach to water**: Add the measured amount to the water container. Do not add water to bleach.

5. **Mix thoroughly**: Stir or swirl the container to distribute the bleach evenly throughout the water.

6. **Smell-check and wait**: After mixing, you should be able to detect a faint chlorine smell. If you cannot smell anything after mixing, add the same dose again and wait an additional 15 minutes. If still no chlorine smell, the water may have a very high demand (high organic matter or very high turbidity) — pre-filter and re-treat. [Source: 1]

7. **Contact time**: Leave the container undisturbed for 30 minutes (or 60 minutes if water is cold). Do not drink during this period.

8. **Taste check**: After the contact period, treated water should taste faintly of chlorine (similar to swimming pool water, but much milder — like municipal tap water). If the taste is strong and unpleasant, the dose was too high; this is uncomfortable but not dangerous at these concentrations. If there is no taste of chlorine, re-treat.

9. **Store properly**: Cover and store. Chlorinated water stays protected for 24–72 hours in a covered container at room temperature. Label with treatment date.

### 2.4 Bleach Shelf Life and Degradation

Sodium hypochlorite degrades over time. An opened bottle of 6% bleach loses approximately:
- 20% of its potency in the first 6 months
- 50% in 12 months (becoming effectively ~3%)
- Most effectiveness by 18–24 months

**Field rule**: Bleach that has been open for more than 6 months should be used at double the standard dose, or replaced. Bleach that smells very faint when the bottle is first opened may already be significantly degraded.

**Storage**: Keep bleach in a cool, dark location. Heat and UV light accelerate degradation. Do not store near ammonia products (creates toxic chloramine gas). [Source: 2]

## Section 3: Treating Cloudy Water — Turbidity Adjustment

Turbidity is the primary variable that reduces chlorination effectiveness. Suspended particles physically shield pathogens from contact with chlorine. The standard approach: pre-filter to reduce turbidity, then apply an increased dose.

### 3.1 Turbidity Decision Table

| Water Appearance | Estimated Turbidity | Pre-Filter Required? | Chlorine Dose |
|------------------|--------------------|--------------------|---------------|
| Clear — can read small print through 1-liter bottle | <1 NTU | No | Standard dose |
| Slightly hazy — can read large print but not small print | 1–4 NTU | Recommended | Standard dose |
| Visibly cloudy — cannot read print through container | 4–10 NTU | Required | Double dose; contact time 60 min |
| Muddy or brown — significant turbidity | >10 NTU | Required (multiple passes) | Chlorination alone insufficient; pre-filter until <4 NTU |

**At turbidity above 10 NTU**: Chlorination is not reliable as a sole treatment. Pre-filter through cloth multiple times, allow settling for 1–2 hours, then filter again before applying the doubled chlorine dose. Alternatively, filter and then boil instead of using chlorination — this may be more reliable for very turbid water. [Sources: 1, 4]

### 3.2 Pre-Filtration for Chlorination

For water that requires pre-filtration before chemical treatment:

**Step 1**: Filter through cloth (minimum 3 layers of cotton) to remove large particles.

**Step 2**: If water remains cloudy after cloth filtration, allow to settle in a clean container for 1–2 hours. Carefully decant the clearer top portion.

**Step 3**: If you have commercial filtration equipment (Sawyer filter, ceramic gravity filter, sand filter), pass water through it before chlorinating.

**Step 4**: Apply the appropriate doubled dose from the table above. Contact time: 60 minutes.

### 3.3 Temperature Adjustment

Cold water requires longer contact time because the rate of chemical reactions (including chlorine's kill mechanism) slows at low temperatures.

| Water Temperature | Contact Time (Standard Turbidity) | Contact Time (Cloudy Water) |
|-------------------|-----------------------------------|------------------------------|
| Above 20°C (68°F) | 30 minutes | 60 minutes |
| 15–20°C (59–68°F) | 30 minutes | 60 minutes |
| 10–15°C (50–59°F) | 60 minutes | 90 minutes |
| Below 10°C (50°F) | 60 minutes | Do not rely on chlorination alone; boil |

**Practical implication**: In winter conditions in cold climates, well water is typically 8–12°C. Water drawn from a cold well in January should receive the 60-minute contact time as standard practice. [Source: 4]

## Section 4: Calcium Hypochlorite (Pool Shock) — Long-Term Storage Option

Liquid bleach degrades within 1–2 years. Calcium hypochlorite (sold as pool shock at hardware stores and pool supply stores) stores for 5–10 years in a cool, dry location and is a far more practical long-term emergency supply.

### 4.1 What to Buy

Look for calcium hypochlorite in granular or powder form at hardware stores (Leslie's Pool, Home Depot, Lowe's) or online. It is sold as "pool shock" or "calcium hypochlorite shock treatment."

**Critical: Buy the right product**

| Product | Appropriate? | Notes |
|---------|-------------|-------|
| Calcium hypochlorite (Ca(OCl)₂), 65–78% | Yes | This is the correct product; most common pool shock |
| Sodium dichloro-s-triazinetrione ("dichlor"), 99% | No | Contains cyanuric acid; not safe for drinking water treatment |
| Trichloro-s-triazinetrione ("trichlor"), 90% | No | Contains cyanuric acid; not safe for drinking water treatment |
| "Pool shock" without specific chemical label | Verify before purchase | Read label; must be calcium hypochlorite only |

**How to identify**: Calcium hypochlorite pool shock contains only calcium hypochlorite (65–78%) and inert ingredients (often calcium carbonate). The label should not mention cyanuric acid, stabilizer, or "stabilized." Granular calcium hypochlorite is usually white or slightly off-white. [Sources: 1, 2]

### 4.2 Making a Chlorine Stock Solution from Pool Shock

Because pool shock is a concentrated powder (65–78%), it is not added directly to drinking water. Instead, make a liquid stock solution first, then use that stock solution as you would use bleach.

**Stock solution preparation** (makes approximately 1 quart of liquid chlorine equivalent to ~6% bleach):

1. Wearing gloves, measure 1 heaping teaspoon (approximately 5 grams) of calcium hypochlorite powder.
2. Add to 2 liters (2 quarts) of clean water in a labeled container. Use water you know is clean — this stock solution is not for drinking, it is a disinfectant intermediate product.
3. Stir until dissolved.
4. Label the container: "CHLORINE STOCK — NOT FOR DRINKING — [date made]"
5. Store in a cool, dark location.
6. Use within 1–2 weeks (the stock solution itself degrades like bleach; the powder in a sealed container lasts years).

### 4.3 Dose Table — Calcium Hypochlorite Stock Solution

After making stock solution per Section 4.2, use it exactly as you would 6% liquid bleach:

| Water Volume | Stock Solution Amount | Notes |
|-------------|----------------------|-------|
| 1 liter | 2 drops | Same as 6% bleach dose |
| 4 liters (1 gallon) | 8 drops | Same as 6% bleach dose |
| 20 liters (5 gallons) | ~1/2 teaspoon | Same as 6% bleach dose |

**Contact time**: Same as for liquid bleach — 30 minutes for clear water above 15°C, 60 minutes for cold or cloudy water.

### 4.4 Long-Term Storage Planning with Pool Shock

A single 1-pound container of 68% calcium hypochlorite can treat approximately 10,000 gallons of clear water. At 4 gallons per person per day for a household of four:
- Daily dose: 4 gallons × 4 people = 16 gallons/day
- Treatment capacity: 10,000 gallons ÷ 16 gallons/day = approximately 625 days (nearly 2 years)

A $5 bag of pool shock represents nearly 2 years of chemical disinfection capacity for a family of four. Stored in a sealed, labeled container in a cool, dry location away from children and flammables, it remains effective for 5–10 years. [Source: 2]

**Rotation**: Mark the purchase date on the container. Replace every 5 years or when the granules show visible moisture absorption or clumping.

## Section 5: What Chemical Disinfection Cannot Do

Like boiling, chemical disinfection is not a complete solution for all water contamination. Understanding these limits is critical.

### 5.1 Pathogens That Chlorine Cannot Reliably Kill

**Cryptosporidium parvum**: The most important chlorine-resistant waterborne pathogen in US contexts. Cryptosporidium oocysts have a robust outer wall that standard chlorine concentrations do not penetrate within practical contact times. Chlorination alone is insufficient for water suspected of Cryptosporidium contamination.

**When to suspect Cryptosporidium**:
- Source water is surface water (streams, ponds, lakes) in areas with cattle or livestock upstream
- Area has recently experienced flooding (flooding can introduce Cryptosporidium from livestock areas into surface water and sometimes groundwater)
- A local Cryptosporidium outbreak has been reported
- A boil-water advisory specifically mentions Cryptosporidium

**What to do**: If Cryptosporidium is a concern, boil water (1 minute at sea level; 3 minutes above 6,500 feet) or use a filter certified to remove Cryptosporidium (NSF/ANSI Standard 58 or NSF/ANSI Standard 53 for cyst removal). Chlorine dioxide tablets (available at outdoor supply stores) are effective against Cryptosporidium and are a useful addition to an emergency kit; standard chlorine (bleach) is not. [Sources: 1, 4]

### 5.2 Chemical Contaminants Not Removed by Chlorination

| Contaminant | Chlorination Effect | Required Treatment |
|-------------|---------------------|-------------------|
| Nitrates (agricultural runoff) | None | Distillation or reverse osmosis |
| PFAS compounds | None | Activated carbon (PFAS-rated) or RO |
| Heavy metals (lead, arsenic, cadmium) | None | Distillation or RO; source avoidance |
| Petroleum products | Partial at best (some volatilization) | Activated carbon; source avoidance |
| Pesticides (atrazine, glyphosate) | None | Activated carbon; source avoidance |
| Dissolved salts, TDS | None | Distillation or RO |

### 5.3 What Chlorination Adds (That Can Be Harmful)

**Disinfection byproducts (DBPs)**: When chlorine reacts with natural organic matter in water (leaves, soil, algae), it forms disinfection byproducts including trihalomethanes (THMs) and haloacetic acids (HAAs). At the low doses used in household water treatment, DBP formation is minimal and far outweighed by the health risk of untreated biological contamination. EPA regulates DBPs in public water systems; at the concentrations used in household emergency treatment, DBP risk is negligible. [Source: 3]

**Chlorine odor and taste**: Properly treated water has a faint chlorine smell, similar to municipal tap water. If the smell is very strong, allow treated water to stand uncovered for 5–10 minutes to let excess chlorine volatilize before drinking. At the doses in this article, the water is safe even with a strong chlorine smell — the smell is unpleasant, not dangerous.

## Section 6: Iodine and Chlorine Dioxide — Alternatives When Bleach Is Unavailable

### 6.1 Iodine Tablets (Tetraglycine Hydroperiodide)

Iodine tablets (sold as Potable Aqua, Coghlan's Iodine Tablets, or military water purification tablets) are a compact emergency backup.

**Dose**: Typically 2 tablets per liter of clear water; follow package instructions as formulations vary.

**Contact time**: 30 minutes for clear water; 60 minutes for cold or cloudy water.

**Effectiveness**:
- Kills bacteria and viruses: Yes, reliably
- Kills Giardia: Yes, with standard contact time
- Kills Cryptosporidium: No — same limitation as chlorine

**Critical contraindications — do not use iodine treatment if**:
- You are pregnant or may be pregnant (iodine can affect fetal thyroid development)
- You have thyroid disease (hyperthyroidism, hypothyroidism, or thyroid nodules)
- You have an iodine allergy (rare but severe)
- You are using iodine tablets as your only treatment for more than a few weeks continuously (chronic iodine exposure has thyroid effects)

For short-term emergency use by healthy adults, iodine tablets are safe and effective. For populations with thyroid conditions or for extended use, chlorine-based treatment is preferred. [Source: 4]

**Shelf life**: Sealed, properly stored iodine tablets are good for 4 years. Once the bottle is opened, use within 12 months. Store in a cool, dry place; do not expose to humidity.

### 6.2 Chlorine Dioxide Tablets (Aquatabs, Katadyn Micropur)

Chlorine dioxide tablets are the most effective chemical disinfection option available in tablet form. Unlike standard chlorine, chlorine dioxide is effective against Cryptosporidium given sufficient contact time.

**Dose**: Typically 1 tablet per liter; follow package instructions.

**Contact time**: 15 minutes for bacteria and viruses in clear water; **4 hours for Cryptosporidium**. This 4-hour contact time is the key practical limitation.

**Effectiveness**:
- Kills bacteria: Yes
- Kills viruses: Yes
- Kills Giardia: Yes
- Kills Cryptosporidium: Yes — with 4-hour contact time

**Practical use**: For any treatment situation where Cryptosporidium is suspected (surface water with livestock, flooding), plan ahead — add tablets and wait 4 hours before drinking. Prepare in the evening, drink in the morning.

**Shelf life**: Sealed packages good for 4 years. Opened packages, consume within 12 months.

**Cost**: More expensive than iodine or bleach — approximately $0.50–1.00 per tablet. For extended emergencies, calcium hypochlorite is far more cost-effective; chlorine dioxide tablets are best as a compact field supplement. [Sources: 1, 4]

## Section 7: Verifying Treatment — The Smell Test and Residual Chlorine

### 7.1 The Smell Test (Field Method, No Equipment)

After the required contact time, treated water should have a faint but detectable chlorine smell. This confirms some free residual chlorine remains, indicating that the initial dose was sufficient to kill pathogens and still have some chlorine left over.

**Interpretation**:
- Strong chlorine smell: Treatment successful; dose was on the high end. Acceptable and safe. Let stand 5 minutes uncovered to reduce odor if desired.
- Faint chlorine smell: Treatment successful; dose was appropriate.
- No chlorine smell after required contact time: Either the dose was insufficient, water had very high organic demand, or the bleach was too degraded to be effective. Re-treat with double dose; wait full contact time again.

### 7.2 Commercial Chlorine Test Strips (Field Equipment)

Inexpensive chlorine test strips (sold as aquarium test strips, pool test strips, or water quality test strips at hardware and pool supply stores) can confirm free residual chlorine concentration directly.

**Target reading after contact time**: 0.2–0.5 mg/L free chlorine. Readings below 0.2 mg/L indicate under-treatment. Readings above 2 mg/L indicate over-treatment (safe but taste is unpleasant; allow to stand uncovered 10 minutes). [Sources: 3, 4]

**Cost**: Approximately $10–20 for 50–100 test strips. A useful addition to any emergency kit or water storage system.

## Section 8: Worked Examples

### Worked Example 1: Treating a 55-Gallon Storage Drum with Pool Shock

**Situation**: Building a 3-month emergency water supply. A 55-gallon food-grade drum has been cleaned and filled with tap water. You want to add chlorination to extend storage life and prevent microbial growth.

**Goal**: Treat 55 gallons of clear tap water with 8.25% pool-strength bleach stock solution made from calcium hypochlorite.

**Step 1**: Make stock solution — 1 teaspoon (5g) calcium hypochlorite (68%) + 2 liters clean water.

**Step 2**: Calculate dose for 55 gallons using 6% bleach equivalency:
- 55 gallons × 8 drops per gallon = 440 drops needed
- 440 drops ÷ 25 drops per mL = approximately 17.6 mL of stock solution
- 17.6 mL ≈ approximately 3.5 teaspoons of stock solution

**Step 3**: Add 3.5 teaspoons of stock solution to the 55-gallon drum. Stir or slosh the drum (seal the lid and tip/roll briefly) to distribute.

**Step 4**: Wait 30 minutes. Open lid briefly and smell — should detect faint chlorine.

**Step 5**: Seal the drum. Label with "Treated [date]." Store in a cool, dark location.

**Maintenance**: Treated water in a sealed container remains protected for 6–12 months if stored correctly and not reopened. If you open the drum to access water, re-treat the remaining water at the same ratio after use. Rotate drum stock annually.

### Worked Example 2: Emergency Treatment of a 5-Gallon Bucket of Stream Water

**Situation**: Camp or field emergency. Source is a moving stream. Water looks slightly hazy (estimated turbidity 3–5 NTU). Standard unscented 6% bleach in kit.

**Step 1**: Pre-filter — pour water through 3 layers of bandana cloth into a clean bucket. Water is now clearer (estimated <4 NTU).

**Step 2**: Apply double dose for slightly cloudy water: 16 drops per gallon × 5 gallons = 80 drops of 6% bleach. Measure as approximately 1 teaspoon.

**Step 3**: Add 1 teaspoon of bleach to the bucket. Stir vigorously.

**Step 4**: Contact time: 60 minutes (cloudy water adjustment). Set a timer.

**Step 5**: After 60 minutes, smell — faint chlorine present. Confirm OK to drink.

**Step 6**: Decant into water bottles as needed for use. Store covered.

**Volume**: 5 gallons = approximately 19 liters = 4 person-days of minimum drinking water (1 gallon/person/day survival rate) or 1.25 person-days at comfortable use (4 gallons/person/day).

### Worked Example 3: Treating Flood-Affected Well Water

**Situation**: Private well in rural location. After major flooding, well has been potentially contaminated with surface water. Turbidity is high (muddy well water, estimated 15+ NTU). 8.25% sodium hypochlorite (concentrated bleach) is available.

**Step 1**: This level of turbidity (15+ NTU) exceeds reliable chlorination range. Proceed with pre-filtration:
- Let water settle in a 5-gallon bucket for 2 hours.
- Decant top 3 gallons carefully (leaving muddy bottom).
- Filter decanted water through cloth twice.
- Repeat settling if still visibly cloudy.

**Step 2**: After pre-filtering, water turbidity is now visually clearer but still slightly hazy (estimated 4–8 NTU). Apply double dose:
- 6 drops of 8.25% bleach per gallon × 3 gallons = 18 drops
- Contact time: 60 minutes minimum

**Step 3**: After contact time, check with chlorine test strip if available. Should read 0.2–0.5 mg/L. If reading is 0 (no residual), re-treat.

**Long-term solution**: This level of contamination means the well needs professional shock chlorination, not repeated household treatment. Contact county health department or licensed well contractor for well chlorination procedure. Household chlorination of drawn water is a short-term measure until the well is professionally treated and retested. [Source: 3]
