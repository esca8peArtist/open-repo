# Herbal Medicine and Botanical Care for Emergency Response

## Critical Safety Statement

**Read this section before using any herb described in this guide.**

This guide covers botanical interventions with the strongest evidence base for use in resource-constrained humanitarian settings — camps, disaster zones, and situations where pharmaceutical access is limited or temporarily unavailable. It is not a substitute for pharmaceutical medicine. In any situation where conventional medical care is accessible, that care takes priority.

**Three rules govern every recommendation in this document:**

1. **Evidence grades matter.** Each herb in this guide is rated by evidence quality: High (multiple rigorous randomized controlled trials or systematic reviews), Moderate (smaller RCTs, consistent observational data, or mechanistic evidence), or Preliminary (in vitro and animal studies with promising but unconfirmed human results). Use Preliminary-grade recommendations with proportionally more caution.

2. **Vulnerable populations require explicit consideration.** Pregnancy, infants and children under two, the elderly, immunocompromised individuals, and people on pharmaceutical medications require specific caution with every herb listed here. Contraindication tables appear throughout each section. When in doubt, do not administer.

3. **Escalation triggers are non-negotiable.** Certain signs always require referral to conventional medical care regardless of herbal availability. These signs appear in bold throughout this document and are consolidated in the appendix. Missing an escalation trigger can be fatal.

The WHO Global Traditional Medicine Strategy 2025-2034 explicitly supports evidence-based integration of traditional plant medicines into primary health care, particularly where pharmaceutical access is limited. This guide operationalizes that principle within strict evidence and safety boundaries.

## Section 1: Herbs with the Strongest Evidence Base

**Confidence: 87% — based on systematic reviews, meta-analyses, and WHO consensus documents**

This section covers nine herbs with the most robust evidence for humanitarian settings: garlic, ginger, honey (included here as a botanical product), turmeric, elderberry, echinacea, calendula, broadleaf plantain, and willow bark. Each entry includes mechanism of action, evidence grade, preparation methods, and safety requirements.

---

### 1.1 Garlic (Allium sativum)

**Primary uses:** Antimicrobial adjunct; cardiovascular support; intestinal parasite management

**Evidence grade:** Moderate-to-High for antimicrobial activity (extensive in vitro evidence; limited but consistent human clinical trials); Moderate for cardiovascular effects

**Mechanism of action:** When raw garlic is crushed or chopped, the enzyme alliinase converts alliin to allicin (diallylthiosulfinate), a volatile sulfur compound that inhibits bacterial, fungal, and protozoal activity. Allicin acts by binding to and inhibiting thiol-containing enzymes — including alcohol dehydrogenase and thioredoxin reductase — that are essential to microbial metabolism. This mechanism is effective against both gram-positive and gram-negative bacteria, including drug-resistant strains of *Staphylococcus aureus* and *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*.

**Critical caveat:** Allicin is volatile and unstable. Cooking destroys antimicrobial activity. Heating above 60°C inactivates alliinase and eliminates the active compound. For antimicrobial purposes, garlic must be used raw and freshly crushed.

**Preparation:**
- *Oral antimicrobial use:* 2-4 raw garlic cloves per day, crushed and rested 10 minutes before use (resting allows full allicin formation), then eaten with food to reduce gastric irritation. Do not cook.
- *Topical use:* Crush 2-3 cloves in a small amount of olive or coconut oil; apply to intact surrounding skin (not open wound tissue directly, as raw garlic causes chemical burns on open wounds); cover with clean cloth. Limit topical application to 20-30 minutes to avoid skin irritation.
- *Oxymel (honey-garlic preparation):* Pack crushed garlic cloves in raw honey (1:1 ratio by volume); allow to infuse 2-4 weeks at room temperature. Dose: 1 teaspoon up to 3 times daily. This shelf-stable preparation concentrates both garlic's antimicrobial compounds and honey's osmotic wound effects.

**Safety and contraindications:**
- Raw garlic can cause significant gastric irritation; always consume with food
- Garlic has clinically significant anticoagulant activity — **avoid in patients taking warfarin, aspirin, or other anticoagulants**; also avoid for 2 weeks before surgery
- High-dose garlic (>4 cloves/day or concentrated supplements) can potentiate the effects of antihypertensive medications
- **Pregnancy:** Culinary amounts considered safe; medicinal doses above 2 cloves/day should be avoided during third trimester due to anticoagulant activity
- **Pediatric:** Culinary amounts safe for children over 2 years; avoid medicinal doses for infants
- Topical garlic can cause chemical burns; never apply directly to open wounds or mucous membranes

**Evidence sources:** Ankri & Mirelman (1999) antimicrobial review, Ried et al. systematic review on cardiovascular effects, Diallylthiosulfinate vapor study (PMC6151386), Merck Manual dietary supplement monograph.

### 1.2 Ginger (Zingiber officinale)

**Primary uses:** Nausea and vomiting (pregnancy-related, post-procedure, motion sickness); anti-inflammatory adjunct; digestive support

**Evidence grade:** High for pregnancy-related nausea (12 systematic reviews/meta-analyses with consistent finding of modest-to-moderate benefit); Moderate for post-chemotherapy nausea and motion sickness; Preliminary for anti-inflammatory effects in arthritis

**Mechanism of action:** Gingerols and shogaols (the latter produced by drying) inhibit prostaglandin and leukotriene synthesis through 5-lipoxygenase and COX pathway inhibition. Ginger also acts on 5-HT3 serotonin receptors in the gastrointestinal tract, producing antiemetic effects. The antinausea mechanism is both peripheral (gastric motility regulation) and central (5-HT3 antagonism at the chemoreceptor trigger zone).

**Evidence summary:** A systematic review and meta-analysis (PMC3995184, 2014) of 12 trials involving 1,278 pregnant women found ginger significantly reduced nausea compared to placebo. The effect on vomiting episodes was less consistent. A broader systematic review of 11 trials involving 2,630 subjects confirmed significant improvement for both pregnancy-related nausea and motion sickness. Ginger is considered comparably effective to vitamin B6 (pyridoxine) for mild-to-moderate pregnancy nausea.

**Preparation:**
- *Nausea tea:* 1-2 grams of fresh grated ginger (approximately 1 teaspoon) or 0.5-1 gram dried ginger powder steeped in 250ml hot water for 10 minutes; strain and drink. 2-3 cups daily as needed.
- *Dried ginger capsules (if available):* 250mg capsules 4 times daily is the most consistently studied dose for pregnancy nausea.
- *Crystallized ginger:* 1-2 pieces (approx. 500mg ginger per piece) as needed for acute nausea; effective and shelf-stable.
- *Oxymel or honey preparation:* Grate fresh ginger into raw honey (1:2 ratio); dose 1 teaspoon in warm water.

**Safety and contraindications:**
- Ginger is among the safest herbs in the evidence base; mild GI effects (heartburn, belching) at high doses
- **Anticoagulant interaction:** High-dose ginger (>4g/day) has mild anticoagulant activity — use caution with warfarin users; avoid in the 2 weeks before surgery
- **Pregnancy:** Culinary and tea doses are considered safe by multiple systematic reviews; avoid concentrated supplements above 2g/day in pregnancy due to theoretical effects on fetal sex hormones and platelet aggregation; do not use in first trimester at medicinal doses without medical consultation
- **Pediatric:** Safe in culinary amounts for children over 2 years; tea (diluted) appropriate for children over 2 years; avoid in infants
- No evidence of fetal harm at standard doses in available human trials

**Evidence sources:** Thomson et al. systematic review (PMC3995184, 2014), Systematic review of dried ginger powder (ResearchGate 272407310), Lete & Allué, Journal of Natural Science Biology and Medicine 2016.

### 1.3 Honey (Apis mellifera — medical/raw grade)

**Primary uses:** Wound care and infection prevention; wound debridement; burns and abrasions; cough and sore throat

**Evidence grade:** High for wound healing in acute partial-thickness burns and acute wounds (multiple RCTs); Moderate for chronic wound management; High for cough suppression in children (Cochrane review evidence)

**Mechanism of action:** Honey produces antimicrobial effects through multiple overlapping mechanisms: (1) high osmolarity dehydrates bacteria and disrupts cell membranes; (2) low pH (typically 3.2-4.5) inhibits most bacterial growth; (3) glucose oxidase enzyme produces hydrogen peroxide at bactericidal concentrations when honey contacts wound fluid; (4) Manuka-specific methylglyoxal (MGO) provides additional non-peroxide antimicrobial activity effective against MRSA; (5) defensin-1, a bee peptide remaining in honey, adds further antimicrobial activity. Anti-inflammatory effects occur through inhibition of reactive oxygen species and modulation of cytokine release.

**Evidence summary:** A 2014 systematic review (PMC3941901) confirmed honey's efficacy for acute wound healing, pain reduction in burn patients, and decreased inflammatory response. Manuka honey has regulatory approval for clinical wound care use in Australia, New Zealand, Europe, the United States, and Canada. A 2025 porcine model study (PMC11946416) confirmed Manuka honey accelerates partial-thickness burn healing compared to standard dressings. A Cochrane review found honey-based cough preparations superior to no treatment in children under 12.

**Preparation:**
- *Wound dressing:* Apply a layer 1-2mm thick of raw unpasteurized honey (Manuka or local raw honey) directly to clean wound surface; cover with clean non-adherent dressing; change every 24-48 hours or when saturated. Do not use crystallized honey on wounds. Do not use processed supermarket honey (pasteurization reduces peroxidase activity).
- *Cough/sore throat:* 5ml (1 teaspoon) raw honey, up to 3 times daily, taken straight or dissolved in warm (not boiling) water. Lemon juice addition is complementary, not necessary.
- *Storage:* Raw honey in sealed container at room temperature; indefinite shelf life if properly sealed and free of moisture contamination.

**Safety and contraindications:**
- **Infant botulism: NEVER give honey to children under 12 months.** This is an absolute contraindication. *Clostridium botulinum* spores present in honey can germinate in the immature infant gut and cause potentially fatal infant botulism. There are no exceptions to this rule.
- Individuals with known bee/pollen allergy should use topical honey with caution (rarely, systemic reactions have been reported)
- Patients with diabetes should monitor blood glucose if consuming honey orally at medicinal doses
- Manuka honey is preferred for wound care but any unpasteurized, unfiltered raw honey retains substantial antimicrobial activity
- Do not apply honey to deep wounds with anaerobic cavities without medical consultation — anaerobic bacteria including *Clostridium* spp. may occasionally be promoted in deep hypoxic tissue

**Evidence sources:** PMC3941901 (systematic review, wound healing), PMC11946416 (Manuka, burn model 2025), PMC7693943 (MRSA systematic review), Cochrane systematic review on honey for cough in children.

### 1.4 Turmeric (Curcuma longa)

**Primary uses:** Anti-inflammatory adjunct (joint pain, wound inflammation, digestive inflammation); antioxidant support

**Evidence grade:** Moderate for reduction of inflammatory markers CRP, TNF-alpha, IL-6 (multiple systematic reviews with RCT data); Preliminary-to-Moderate for clinical pain reduction in arthritis; High for antioxidant activity

**Mechanism of action:** Curcumin, the primary polyphenolic compound in turmeric rhizome (~3-5% of dry weight), inhibits NF-kB transcription factor activation, COX-2 enzyme activity, and 5-lipoxygenase — key regulators of the inflammatory cascade. Curcumin also inhibits cytokine release (TNF-alpha, IL-1beta, IL-6) and modulates oxidative stress through Nrf2 pathway activation.

**Evidence summary:** A 2019 systematic review and meta-analysis (PubMed 31121255) of 19 RCTs found turmeric/curcumin supplementation significantly reduced CRP, TNF-alpha, and IL-6. A 2025 Frontiers in Immunology systematic review on rheumatoid arthritis found curcumin superior to placebo for joint pain and swelling reduction. A key limitation is bioavailability: standard curcumin is poorly absorbed from the gastrointestinal tract. Co-administration with piperine (black pepper extract, ~5-20mg per 500mg curcumin) increases bioavailability by up to 2,000% by inhibiting glucuronidation.

**Preparation:**
- *Anti-inflammatory paste (Golden Paste):* Mix 1/4 cup turmeric powder, 1/2 cup water, 1.5 teaspoons black pepper, and 5 tablespoons coconut or olive oil. Heat gently until paste forms; store refrigerated for 2 weeks. Dose: 1/2 to 1 teaspoon in warm milk or broth, once to twice daily. The pepper and fat in this recipe substantially improve curcumin absorption.
- *Simple tea:* 1/2 teaspoon turmeric powder + pinch black pepper + pinch ginger powder in hot water or milk. Sweeten with honey. Twice daily.
- *Topical (wound inflammation):* Make a thin paste with turmeric powder and coconut oil; apply to closed wounds, bruises, or inflamed skin. Note: turmeric stains skin and fabric permanently. Do not apply to open wounds.

**Safety and contraindications:**
- Culinary amounts are very safe; medicinal doses (500-1,000mg/day curcumin) have a good safety profile in clinical trials up to 16 weeks
- **Drug interactions:** Curcumin has anticoagulant activity — **avoid at medicinal doses with warfarin, aspirin, or NSAID therapy**; can reduce platelet aggregation; stop medicinal doses 2 weeks before surgery
- **Pregnancy:** Culinary amounts safe; avoid concentrated supplemental doses in pregnancy (uterine stimulant at high doses; insufficient human safety data for medicinal doses)
- **Gallbladder disease:** Turmeric stimulates bile release — contraindicated in patients with bile duct obstruction or active gallstones
- Iron-deficiency patients: curcumin chelates iron and can reduce iron absorption if taken with iron-rich meals; separate turmeric consumption from iron supplementation by 2 hours
- **Pediatric:** Culinary amounts safe for children over 2 years; medicinal doses not established for pediatric populations

**Evidence sources:** PubMed 31121255 (2019 meta-analysis), Frontiers in Immunology 2025 RA systematic review, Frontiers in Nutrition 2026 curcumin-piperine meta-analysis.

### 1.5 Elderberry (Sambucus nigra)

**Primary uses:** Reduction of duration and severity of upper respiratory infections (cold, influenza); immune modulation

**Evidence grade:** Moderate — multiple meta-analyses show consistent benefit for symptom duration; evidence certainty rated as low-to-moderate by Cochrane standards due to small trial sizes

**Mechanism of action:** Anthocyanins (primarily cyanidin-3-glucoside and cyanidin-3-sambubioside) inhibit viral replication by binding to and blocking hemagglutinin proteins on influenza virions, preventing cellular attachment. Elder flavonoids also stimulate cytokine production (TNF-alpha, IL-6, IL-8) by monocytes, enhancing innate immune response. At normal doses this immune stimulation is within physiological range.

**Evidence summary:** A systematic review (PMC8026097, 2021) of 7 trials found elderberry preparation significantly reduced duration of influenza symptoms; in one study upper respiratory symptoms resolved 4 days earlier than placebo. A meta-analysis found standardized elderberry reduced symptom severity by approximately 50% when taken within 48 hours of onset.

**Critical safety note:** Raw elderberries, elderberry leaves, bark, and roots contain sambunigrin, a cyanogenic glycoside that causes severe nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea. **Only properly cooked or commercially processed elderberry preparations are safe.** Raw berries must be cooked for a minimum of 15 minutes to destroy sambunigrin. Elder flowers do not contain significant cyanogenic glycosides and can be used in teas or preparations directly.

**Preparation:**
- *Elderberry syrup:* Simmer 1 cup dried elderberries (or 2 cups fresh) in 3 cups water with 1 cinnamon stick and 3-4 cloves for 45 minutes until liquid reduces by half; cool, mash and strain berries, add 1 cup raw honey when cooled to below 40°C (do not heat honey). Store refrigerated up to 3 months. Dose: 1 tablespoon (adults) or 1 teaspoon (children over 1 year) daily for prevention; every 2-3 hours at onset of symptoms.
- *Elderflower tea:* 2 teaspoons dried elder flowers steeped in 250ml hot water 10 minutes; strain; drink 2-3 cups daily. Elderflower tea is diaphoretic (promotes sweating) and may support fever management.

**Safety and contraindications:**
- **Autoimmune conditions:** Elderberry stimulates immune cytokine production — **contraindicated for patients with autoimmune disease (lupus, rheumatoid arthritis, multiple sclerosis) or immunosuppressive therapy**; immune stimulation could exacerbate disease
- Theoretical concern about cytokine storm in severe influenza (avoid high-dose elderberry in patients with severe or worsening respiratory illness)
- **Pregnancy:** Insufficient safety data; avoid during first trimester; elderflower tea is considered lower risk than berry preparations
- **Pediatric:** Safe for children over 1 year (honey restriction applies below 12 months; use elderberry preparation made without honey for infants 6-12 months; elderberry is generally avoided in infants under 6 months)
- Do not use raw or undercooked elderberries in any circumstance

**Evidence sources:** PMC8026097 (systematic review 2021), PMC3941201 (echinacea and elderberry in pregnancy review), Life Extension Magazine 2024 summary.

### 1.6 Echinacea (Echinacea purpurea, E. angustifolia)

**Primary uses:** Prevention and reduction of duration of upper respiratory infections; immune modulation

**Evidence grade:** Moderate — systematic reviews show modest, inconsistent benefit for prevention; clearer benefit for symptom duration reduction when taken at onset

**Mechanism of action:** Alkylamides, polysaccharides, and caffeic acid derivatives (echinacoside, cichoric acid) modulate innate immunity by activating macrophages and natural killer cells, increasing phagocytosis, and stimulating interferon production. Alkylamides bind cannabinoid receptors (CB2) and may also have direct anti-inflammatory effects.

**Evidence summary:** A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis (ScienceDirect, Echinacea in children) found Echinacea purpurea significantly improved upper respiratory tract infection total effective rate and reduced symptom severity compared to controls, with fewer adverse events than conventional medications. Evidence for prevention is weaker and more inconsistent.

**Preparation:**
- *Tincture:* 3-5ml of 1:5 echinacea tincture (in 50-60% alcohol) 3 times daily at the first sign of symptoms. Use for no longer than 8-10 consecutive days; then take a 1-2 week break before resuming.
- *Tea:* 1 tablespoon dried echinacea herb or 1 teaspoon dried root simmered (root requires simmering for 20 minutes; above-ground parts can be steeped) in 300ml water; up to 3 cups daily.
- *Commercial standardized preparations:* Follow manufacturer dosing; standardized products are more reliable than home preparations.

**Safety and contraindications:**
- **Autoimmune disease:** Same caution as elderberry — avoid in autoimmune conditions and with immunosuppressive medications
- **HIV/AIDS:** Some protocols caution against long-term use due to immune stimulation effects; short-term acute use at symptom onset is generally considered lower risk
- Do not use for longer than 10 consecutive days without a break; long-term continuous use may downregulate immune response
- **Pregnancy:** Insufficient RCT data; generally avoided in first trimester; a review found no increased risk of pregnancy complications with short-term use in second/third trimester, but this should not be read as safety confirmation
- **Pediatric:** Systematic review evidence supports use in children over 2 years at appropriate doses; caution in children with known atopy (allergic conditions) as echinacea is in the Asteraceae family and cross-reactions have been reported
- **Allergy alert:** Echinacea belongs to the daisy/Asteraceae family; individuals with known allergies to ragweed, chrysanthemums, marigolds, or daisies may have cross-reactive allergic responses

**Evidence sources:** PMC12847374 (PEACH systematic review 2025), PMC3941201, NCCIH echinacea fact sheet.

### 1.7 Calendula (Calendula officinalis)

**Primary uses:** Topical wound healing; skin inflammation; minor burns and abrasions; radiation dermatitis prevention

**Evidence grade:** Moderate for topical wound healing and anti-inflammatory effects (consistent in vitro and animal studies; mixed but generally positive small RCTs); Preliminary for oral anti-inflammatory use

**Mechanism of action:** Triterpenoid saponins, flavonoids (quercetin, isorhamnetin), and carotenoids in calendula flowers inhibit NF-kB activation and reduce prostaglandin release, producing anti-inflammatory effects. Polysaccharides stimulate fibroblast proliferation and collagen deposition, accelerating wound closure. Calendula extracts show direct antimicrobial activity against *Staphylococcus aureus*, *Candida albicans*, and several wound pathogens.

**Evidence summary:** A systematic review (ResearchGate 333494992) found 6 RCTs meeting criteria for topical calendula preparations; evidence was weak but generally positive for wound healing speed and infection prevention. Clinical use in oncology (preventing radiation dermatitis) is among the better-documented applications. In one RCT, calendula tincture significantly outperformed povidone-iodine for surgical wound healing scores.

**Preparation:**
- *Calendula-infused oil:* Pack fresh or dried calendula flowers in carrier oil (olive, coconut, or sunflower) in a glass jar; ratio approximately 1:3 flowers to oil by volume; seal and leave in warm location (windowsill) for 4-6 weeks, shaking daily; strain through cheesecloth. Apply topically to intact skin, minor abrasions, and around wound edges. Shelf life 6-12 months in sealed dark container.
- *Calendula salve:* Melt 1 tablespoon beeswax per 1 cup infused oil; pour into tins; allow to solidify. More portable than oil for field use.
- *Calendula tea (topical wash):* 2 tablespoons dried flowers steeped in 500ml boiled water for 20 minutes; strain; cool; use as wound wash or compress. Single-use preparation only.
- *Tincture:* 3-5ml of 1:5 calendula tincture in 40% alcohol; dilute 1:10 with clean water for wound washing.

**Safety and contraindications:**
- Calendula is among the safest topical herbs in the evidence base
- **Asteraceae family allergy:** Same cross-reactivity risk as echinacea; test on small skin area before widespread use if patient has known daisy-family allergy
- **Pregnancy:** External topical use generally considered safe; avoid oral calendula during pregnancy (uterine stimulant effects reported in animal studies)
- Avoid on deep puncture wounds or wounds with suspected foreign bodies without medical evaluation
- Diabetic wounds with poor vascular supply may not respond to calendula; one RCT showed no improvement in diabetic leg ulcers with topical calendula

**Evidence sources:** PMC12656484 (phytotherapy advances in wound healing 2025), PMC4665477 (radiotherapy skin reactions), PMC3270572 (animal model anti-inflammatory), ScienceDirect S2772417424000712.

### 1.8 Broadleaf Plantain (Plantago major)

**Primary uses:** First aid wound care; insect stings; minor burns; drawing preparations (splinters, infected material); cough demulcent

**Evidence grade:** Moderate for topical antimicrobial and wound healing (strong in vitro evidence; one case-control RCT); Preliminary for oral respiratory use

**Mechanism of action:** Plantamajoside (a caffeic acid ester) and aucubin (an iridoid glycoside) are the primary bioactive compounds. Plantamajoside inhibits inflammatory cytokines (TNF-alpha, IL-6) and reduces MMP-2/MMP-9 metalloproteinase activity that breaks down extracellular matrix in wounds. Mucilage polysaccharides coat and soothe mucous membranes. Aucubin has antimicrobial and analgesic properties in animal models.

**Critically, broadleaf plantain (Plantago major) is one of the most widely distributed plants on earth** — found in disturbed soil, footpaths, lawns, and waste ground on every inhabited continent. This makes it uniquely valuable in improvised first aid when other supplies are unavailable. It was adopted by Native Americans (who called it "white man's footprint") precisely because it followed human settlement everywhere.

**Evidence summary:** A 2022 case-control study (PMC9359829) found Plantago major extract gel applied once daily produced significantly larger wound size reduction compared to standard care controls at both one and two weeks in partial-thickness burn patients. PMC10458736 (2023) confirmed antimicrobial activity against wound pathogens including *Staphylococcus aureus*, *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*, and *Candida albicans*.

**Field preparation:**
- *Poultice (emergency field use):* Wash fresh plantain leaves. Chew or macerate thoroughly to release juices. Apply mashed leaf matter directly to insect stings, minor cuts, abrasions, or splinters. Hold in place with cloth strip. This is the historical emergency use and remains appropriate for field first aid when nothing else is available. Replace every 30-60 minutes.
- *Infused wash:* Simmer 30g fresh or 15g dried plantain leaves in 500ml water for 15 minutes; cool and strain; use to clean wounds.
- *Cough tea:* 2 teaspoons dried plantain leaves steeped in 250ml hot water for 10 minutes; add honey; drink 2-3 cups daily for cough and sore throat.

**Safety and contraindications:**
- Very safe topical herb with minimal adverse effect reports
- Ensure plant identification is correct — plantain is distinctive (oval ribbed leaves, no stem, leafy rosette from ground, seed stalk rising centrally), but inexperienced users should verify identification with images before use
- **Oral use in pregnancy:** The latex and seeds have mild laxative effect; avoid excessive oral consumption in pregnancy; topical use is considered safe
- **Psyllium sensitivity:** Plantago psyllium (psyllium husk) and Plantago major are related; individuals with psyllium allergy should be cautious with oral Plantago major use
- Wash leaves thoroughly if collected from potentially contaminated sites (roadsides, chemically treated areas)

**Evidence sources:** PMC10458736 (antimicrobial review 2023), PMC9359829 (burn wound RCT 2022), PMC7142308 (traditional uses review).

### 1.9 Willow Bark (Salix alba, S. purpurea)

**Primary uses:** Pain relief (musculoskeletal, headache, fever); anti-inflammatory

**Evidence grade:** Moderate for back pain and mild arthritis pain; mixed evidence for headache; evidence does not support equivalence with pharmaceutical NSAIDs

**Mechanism of action:** Salicin is hydrolyzed by gut flora and intestinal enzymes to salicylic acid and glucose. Salicylic acid inhibits COX-1 and COX-2 enzymes, blocking prostaglandin synthesis and producing analgesic, antipyretic, and anti-inflammatory effects — the same mechanism as aspirin. However, salicin is present in willow bark at concentrations of 1-10% by weight, yielding substantially lower salicylate exposure than standard aspirin doses. Additional non-salicylate polyphenols (flavonoids, tannins) may contribute independent anti-inflammatory effects.

**Evidence summary:** A 2023 meta-analysis (PMC10607963) of 7 RCTs found willow bark significantly reduced back pain compared to placebo, though effect sizes were smaller than diclofenac (an NSAID) in head-to-head comparison. One trial (standardized to 240mg salicin) showed 39% of patients pain-free by week four versus 6% for placebo. Evidence for headache and fever is less robust.

**Preparation:**
- *Tea/decoction:* 2-3g of dried willow bark per 250ml of water; simmer (do not steep) for 10-15 minutes; strain; drink up to 3 cups daily with food. This is the traditional preparation; commercial standardized preparations contain known salicin content.
- *Tincture:* 3-5ml of 1:5 willow bark tincture (40-60% alcohol) three times daily.
- *Standardized dose target:* 120-240mg salicin/day (equivalent to standard low-dose willow bark preparations in clinical trials).

**Safety and contraindications:**
- **Aspirin allergy:** Willow bark is an absolute contraindication in patients with aspirin or NSAID allergy — salicin converts to salicylic acid, the same pathway as aspirin
- **Warfarin and anticoagulants:** Significant interaction risk; salicylates potentiate anticoagulant effects — monitor closely or avoid
- **Reye's syndrome:** Like aspirin, willow bark salicylates may carry Reye's syndrome risk in children under 16 with viral fevers — **do not use willow bark for fever management in children and adolescents under 16 years**
- **Pregnancy:** Avoid in third trimester (salicylates can cause premature closure of ductus arteriosus and delay labor)
- **Gastrointestinal:** Can cause GI irritation; always take with food; avoid with active peptic ulcer
- **Renal and hepatic disease:** Avoid at medicinal doses
- Gout: Salicylates can reduce uric acid excretion and trigger gout attacks

**Evidence sources:** PMC10607963 (meta-analysis 2023), Versus Arthritis willow bark review, Merck Manual professional monograph, MSKCC integrative medicine herb database.

## Section 2: Infection Prevention and Wound Care

**Confidence: 83% — evidence grade varies substantially by herb and application; pharmaceutical options are preferred when available**

### 2.1 The Evidence Hierarchy for Wound Care

In resource-constrained settings, wound infection is a major source of morbidity and mortality. The evidence-based hierarchy for wound care, from strongest to weakest evidence, is:

1. **Cleaning with clean water** — the most evidence-based wound care intervention is thorough irrigation with clean water (ideally boiled and cooled, or treated per water purification protocols in this library). This single step reduces wound infection risk more than any herbal preparation.
2. **Raw honey dressings** — High evidence grade; see Section 1.3 for full protocol.
3. **Antiseptic botanical washes** — Moderate evidence; see below.
4. **Poultice preparations** — Preliminary-to-Moderate evidence for symptom relief.

**Escalation triggers — when herbal wound care is insufficient:**
- Wound with red streaking extending beyond wound margins (lymphangitis — requires systemic antibiotics)
- Fever above 38.5°C (101.3°F) with wound infection signs (systemic infection)
- Wound larger than 2cm with full-thickness tissue loss
- Animal bites (particularly dog, cat, human bites) — high infection risk requiring antibiotic prophylaxis
- Puncture wounds from rusty metal, nails, or agricultural tools — tetanus risk; clean thoroughly and refer if tetanus vaccine history is unknown
- Signs of abscess (fluctuant swelling, pus under pressure) — incision and drainage is required; this is not a herbal indication
- Wound not showing improvement within 3-4 days of herbal treatment

### 2.2 Antiseptic Herb Protocols

**Thyme (Thymus vulgaris) — Evidence grade: Moderate**

Thymol and carvacrol, the primary terpenoid phenols in thyme essential oil, disrupt bacterial cell membranes and inhibit ATP synthesis. A 2025 PMC study (PMC12391397) confirmed thyme essential oil's bactericidal activity against *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*, *Staphylococcus aureus*, and MDR clinical isolates, with significant anti-biofilm activity. Thyme is effective against wound pathogens at concentrations achievable in simple dilutions.

*Thyme wound wash:* Simmer 30g fresh thyme (or 15g dried) in 500ml water for 15 minutes with lid on to trap volatile compounds; cool; strain. Use as wound wash once daily. Alternatively, dilute pure thyme essential oil (1-2 drops per 250ml clean water) for topical wound washing only — do not ingest essential oils.

**Safety:** Thyme tea and herb preparations are safe for topical use. Thyme essential oil is highly concentrated and irritant at undiluted strength — never apply undiluted essential oil directly to skin or wounds. Avoid thyme essential oil during pregnancy. Cross-reactivity with oregano and other Lamiaceae family herbs possible.

**Oregano (Origanum vulgare) — Evidence grade: Moderate (in vitro strong; clinical wound RCTs limited)**

Carvacrol (60-80% of oregano essential oil) and thymol inhibit bacterial cell membrane integrity and respiratory chain function. Oregano essential oil shows inhibitory activity against *E. coli*, *Klebsiella*, *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*, and *MRSA* in vitro. A 2025 comparative study (MDPI Microorganisms) found oregano and thyme essential oils showed synergistic effects against *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* and *Klebsiella pneumoniae*.

*Preparation:* Same protocol as thyme above. Diluted essential oil (1-2 drops per 250ml water) for wound wash; herb decoction preferred over undiluted oil.

**Safety:** Same cautions as thyme; oregano essential oil is a strong mucous membrane irritant — never apply concentrated; avoid in pregnancy.

**Rosemary (Rosmarinus officinalis) — Evidence grade: Preliminary for wound care; Moderate for antioxidant properties**

Rosmarinic acid and carnosic acid have antimicrobial and antioxidant activity. Less evidence than thyme or oregano for wound care specifically; included here for completeness as a widely available garden herb. Tea (30g in 500ml water) can be used as a wound wash. Safe topically; avoid concentrated essential oil in pregnancy.

### 2.3 Choosing Herbal vs. Pharmaceutical Intervention

Use pharmaceutical antiseptics and antibiotics when:
- Systemic infection is present or suspected (fever, red streaking, lymphadenopathy)
- The wound involves deep tissue, tendon, bone, or joint
- The patient is immunocompromised, diabetic, or has poor circulation
- Animal bite wound (high oral flora contamination)
- Any wound that is not improving with 3-4 days of appropriate care

Use herbal wound care when:
- Wound is superficial (abrasion, minor laceration under 2cm, minor burn)
- Pharmaceutical supplies are temporarily unavailable or exhausted
- As adjunct to (not replacement for) appropriate pharmaceutical wound care

**Never use herbal preparations to treat:**
- Gas gangrene or necrotizing fasciitis (emergency surgical conditions)
- Sepsis (requires IV antibiotics and fluid resuscitation)
- Osteomyelitis (bone infection)

### 2.4 Wound Preparation and Irrigation Before Herbal Application

The single most evidence-based wound care intervention is mechanical irrigation with clean water. Before applying any herbal preparation, wound irrigation must occur. Skipping this step and applying honey or herbal preparations to a contaminated wound traps bacteria under the dressing and can accelerate abscess formation.

**Wound irrigation protocol (all wounds, all settings):**
1. Wash hands thoroughly with soap and water for at least 20 seconds before touching any wound.
2. If available, wear clean gloves. If not available, ensure hands are as clean as possible.
3. Irrigate the wound with the cleanest available water — ideally boiled and cooled to body temperature. Water treated by chemical disinfection (chlorine, iodine) is appropriate if boiled water is not available; allow the full disinfection contact time before use.
4. Use a syringe (10-20ml), a clean plastic bag with a small hole punctured, or a clean cup to direct pressurized water through the wound. Gravity-poured water does not adequately clean wound margins and depths.
5. Irrigate with a minimum of 250ml of water for a 2cm wound; larger wounds need proportionally more.
6. Do not use hydrogen peroxide, full-strength iodine, or full-strength alcohol directly in wound beds — these destroy fibroblasts and delay healing. Dilute betadine (1-5% povidone-iodine solution) is acceptable for contaminated wounds if available.
7. Remove visible foreign material with clean tweezers if accessible without deep probing.
8. Only after thorough irrigation: apply herbal preparation (honey dressing, calendula wash, thyme decoction) as described above.

**Wound documentation in humanitarian settings:**
In settings where multiple practitioners may see the same patient, document: date of injury, mechanism (sharp object, blunt trauma, bite, burn, puncture), wound dimensions, signs of infection at presentation, treatment applied, and date of next review. This creates accountability and enables escalation decisions to be based on objective trajectory rather than subjective impression.

## Section 3: Gastrointestinal and Digestive Support

**Confidence: 85% — ORS evidence is high-grade; herbal adjuncts are Moderate; clear escalation triggers defined**

### 3.1 Diarrhea in Humanitarian Settings: The Mortality Context

Diarrheal disease is the second leading cause of death in children under five globally and accounts for a substantial proportion of mortality in displacement camps. In the 1971 Bangladesh refugee crisis, oral rehydration therapy (ORT) developed on-site prevented an estimated hundreds of thousands of deaths from cholera. A 2024 systematic review found ORS use reduces diarrheal mortality by 69%.

**The most important medical intervention for diarrheal illness is oral rehydration — not herbal medicine.** No herbal preparation replaces ORS.

### 3.2 WHO Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS)

**WHO/UNICEF reduced-osmolarity ORS formula:**
- Sodium chloride (table salt): 2.6g
- Trisodium citrate (or sodium bicarbonate/baking soda): 2.9g (citrate) or 2.5g (bicarbonate)
- Potassium chloride: 1.5g
- Glucose (or glucose-containing sugar): 13.5g (or substitute 20-25g sucrose/table sugar)
- Clean water to 1 litre

**If commercial ORS packets are unavailable:**
- Emergency improvised ORS: 6 level teaspoons of sugar + 0.5 level teaspoon of salt dissolved in 1 litre of clean water. This is a safe approximation for the glucose and sodium components; potassium is absent but acceptable for short-term use (24-48 hours).
- Rice water ORS: Cook plain white rice in 3 times the usual water volume; strain the starchy water; add 0.5 teaspoon salt per litre; use as ORS. Rice-based ORS reduces stool output and duration of diarrhea more effectively than glucose-based ORS in some cholera studies.

**Dosing:** Adults and children: 200-400ml ORS after each loose stool. For infants: 50-100ml after each stool. Continue until diarrhea resolves.

### 3.3 Herbal Adjuncts for Gastrointestinal Conditions

**Ginger — Evidence grade: High for nausea; Moderate for GI cramping**

See Section 1.2 for full protocol. For acute gastroenteritis with nausea: 1-2 cups ginger tea between ORS doses. Do not replace ORS with ginger tea.

**Peppermint (Mentha piperita) — Evidence grade: High for IBS-type cramping; Moderate for acute gastroenteritis cramping**

L-menthol, the primary volatile compound in peppermint oil, blocks calcium channels in smooth muscle cells of the GI tract, producing direct antispasmodic effects. A meta-analysis (PMC6337770) found peppermint oil superior to placebo for IBS symptom reduction, with a number needed to treat of 2.5. For acute gastroenteritis cramping and nausea, the antispasmodic effect is clinically relevant.

*Preparation:* 2 teaspoons dried peppermint leaves steeped in 250ml hot water for 10 minutes; strain; drink up to 3-4 cups daily. Do not use peppermint oil capsules for acute gastroenteritis — enteric-coated forms are designed for lower GI delivery and are a separate clinical product.

*Safety:* Peppermint tea is safe for adults and children over 5 years. **Do not use peppermint (menthol) near the face, nose, or chest of infants under 2 years** — menthol can cause reflex laryngospasm and respiratory arrest in very young children. Avoid peppermint with GERD (acid reflux) as it relaxes the lower esophageal sphincter; avoid high-dose peppermint with gallstones.

**Chamomile (Matricaria chamomilla) — Evidence grade: Moderate for GI antispasmodic; Preliminary for diarrhea**

Bisabolol and apigenin in chamomile flowers produce antispasmodic effects via smooth muscle relaxation and mild sedative-analgesic properties via GABA receptor modulation. Chamomile also has anti-inflammatory effects on the GI mucosa. Traditional use for colic, gastroenteritis, and functional GI cramping across hundreds of years in dozens of cultures represents substantial indirect evidence.

*Preparation:* 2 tablespoons dried chamomile flowers steeped in 250ml hot water for 10-15 minutes; strain. Up to 3-4 cups daily. Safe combined with ginger or peppermint.

*Safety:* **Asteraceae allergy:** Chamomile belongs to the daisy family; individuals with ragweed, chrysanthemum, or daisy allergies may have anaphylactic reactions to chamomile — test with small amount first. **Pregnancy:** Generally considered safe at tea doses; avoid concentrated chamomile extracts in pregnancy (emmenagogue effects at high doses). Safe for children over 2 years in tea form.

**Berberine/Goldenseal (Hydrastis canadensis) — Evidence grade: Moderate for acute infectious diarrhea (cholera, E. coli); important limitations**

Berberine, the primary alkaloid in goldenseal (and also found in Oregon grape and barberry), reduces intestinal secretion and has direct antimicrobial activity. Two small RCTs (1987 and earlier) found berberine reduced diarrhea volume in cholera patients and showed activity against *Vibrio cholerae* and *E. coli* ETEC. However, in one trial berberine-treated cholera patients had more severe symptoms than tetracycline-treated patients. Berberine reduced stool volume but did not replace ORS or match antibiotic effectiveness.

**Critical context:** Goldenseal should not be used as a primary antibiotic for infectious diarrhea when pharmaceutical antibiotics are available. Its role is as an adjunct in settings where antibiotics are unavailable, specifically for non-bloody, non-fever watery diarrhea (which may include traveler's diarrhea or cholera).

*Preparation:* Goldenseal root tincture (1:5 in 60% alcohol): 3-5ml three times daily for no more than 7-10 days. Goldenseal is overharvested in the wild; prefer organically cultivated sources. Alternatively, Oregon grape (Mahonia aquifolium) root or barberry (Berberis vulgaris) root contain equivalent berberine concentrations.

*Safety:* **Pregnancy: Berberine is absolutely contraindicated in pregnancy** — it stimulates uterine contractions and can cross the placenta; berberine has caused neonatal jaundice in newborns whose mothers used it near term. **Do not use goldenseal in pregnancy at any dose.** Avoid in breastfeeding. Not recommended for children without professional guidance. Drug interaction: berberine inhibits CYP3A4 and may increase levels of drugs metabolized by this pathway.

### 3.4 Escalation Triggers for GI Illness

**Refer immediately to conventional medical care if:**
- Blood in stool (dysentery pattern — requires antibiotic evaluation and possible IV fluids)
- Diarrhea in children with signs of dehydration: dry mouth, no tears, no urine in 6+ hours, lethargy, sunken eyes
- High fever (above 39°C / 102.2°F) with diarrhea
- Vomiting that prevents ORS administration (IV rehydration required)
- Diarrhea lasting more than 5 days in adults or 3 days in children without improvement

## Section 4: Respiratory and Immune Support

**Confidence: 80% — respiratory evidence base is moderately strong for symptom management; prevention data is weaker**

### 4.1 Upper Respiratory Infections in Crowded Settings

Displacement camps and crowded shelters create high-risk environments for respiratory infection transmission: close quarters, poor ventilation, nutritional stress, and inadequate sanitation all elevate transmission rates. Upper respiratory infections (URIs) are the most common presentation in MSF camp clinical data, accounting for approximately 42% of primary care consultations.

Herbal respiratory support falls into four functional categories:
1. **Antiviral/immune support** — elderberry, echinacea (see Section 1)
2. **Demulcent/soothing** — honey, plantain, licorice root
3. **Expectorant** — thyme, ginger, licorice
4. **Antipyretic/analgesic** — willow bark, elderflower

### 4.2 Fever Management

Normal body temperature variation: 36.1-37.2°C (97-99°F). Low-grade fever: 37.3-38°C. Moderate fever: 38-39°C. High fever: above 39°C.

**Fever is a physiological immune response, not inherently dangerous.** Suppressing low-to-moderate fever may prolong illness. The goal in resource-constrained settings is comfort care and monitoring, not aggressive fever suppression.

**Herbal fever management (for moderate fever in adults with no contraindications):**
- Elderflower tea (diaphoretic): 2 teaspoons dried elderflowers in 250ml hot water; drink warm; promotes diaphoresis (sweating); avoid in patients who are already significantly dehydrated
- Peppermint or spearmint tea (cooling, mild antipyretic): 2 cups daily; do not apply peppermint preparations to infants' skin or near airways
- Willow bark tea (salicylate-based antipyretic): see Section 1.9; **do not use in children under 16 with viral fever**

**Escalation triggers for fever:**
- Fever above 40°C (104°F) in adults or above 39°C (102.2°F) in children — requires pharmaceutical antipyretics and evaluation
- Febrile seizure — requires emergency intervention
- Fever plus stiff neck and/or light sensitivity — meningitis until proven otherwise; immediate referral
- Fever in infants under 3 months — always refer; infants have immature thermoregulation and fever in this age group requires evaluation regardless of cause
- Fever lasting more than 5 days without identifiable cause

### 4.3 Cough and Sore Throat

**Honey (for cough in children over 12 months and adults):**
A Cochrane-reviewed RCT found honey superior to placebo and equivalent to dextromethorphan for suppressing nocturnal cough in children. Dose: 5ml honey at bedtime. **Absolute contraindication under 12 months (infant botulism risk).**

**Thyme and honey syrup (expectorant + demulcent):**
Simmer 30g fresh thyme in 200ml water for 15 minutes; cool; strain; add equal volume of raw honey when cooled below 40°C. Dose: 1 tablespoon up to 4 times daily for adults; 1 teaspoon up to 3 times daily for children 2-12 years. Evidence base for this specific preparation draws on both the RCT evidence for honey and the antimicrobial/expectorant properties of thyme.

**Licorice root (Glycyrrhiza glabra) — expectorant, demulcent — Moderate evidence:**
Glycyrrhizin is a potent anti-inflammatory and has direct antiviral activity against several respiratory viruses in vitro. Licorice root tea produces mucoprotective effects on the respiratory mucosa and has expectorant properties. Simmer 1 teaspoon dried licorice root in 250ml water for 10 minutes; drink 2 cups daily.

**Safety — licorice root critical warning:** Glycyrrhizin inhibits the enzyme 11-beta-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase, causing cortisol-like effects including sodium retention and potassium loss. At doses above 100mg glycyrrhizin/day (approximately 3-4g licorice root), this causes hypertension, hypokalemia, and water retention. **Avoid licorice root in patients with hypertension, heart failure, kidney disease, or hypokalemia.** Do not use for longer than 4-6 weeks continuously. **Contraindicated in pregnancy** (increases risk of premature birth). Deglycyrrhizinated licorice (DGL) products do not carry these risks but have reduced respiratory efficacy.

### 4.4 High-Risk Populations for Respiratory Illness

**Infants and young children:** Higher susceptibility to respiratory infection; signs of respiratory distress (respiratory rate above 50 breaths/min in infants, above 40 in toddlers, nasal flaring, accessory muscle use, cyanosis) require immediate referral. Use only honey (over 12 months), peppermint-free preparations, and chamomile for children under 2.

**Elderly:** Impaired cough reflex and immune response; aspiration pneumonia risk is elevated; monitor for rapid deterioration. Willow bark dose should be reduced 50% in elderly patients.

**Immunocompromised:** Avoid echinacea and elderberry (immune stimulants). Focus on supportive care (hydration, fever management, nutritional support) and prioritize referral.

**Pregnant:** Avoid willow bark (third trimester especially), licorice root, elderberry at high doses. Honey (after first trimester) and ginger tea are safe options.

## Section 5: Pain, Inflammation, and Chronic Condition Management

**Confidence: 78% — herbal analgesics have real but modest effects; managing expectations is critical for practitioner effectiveness**

### 5.1 The NSAIDs Gap in Resource-Constrained Settings

Ibuprofen, naproxen, aspirin, and acetaminophen/paracetamol are the workhorses of pain and fever management in primary care. When these are unavailable, herbal options can fill a partial gap — managing mild-to-moderate pain and inflammation, though with meaningfully weaker analgesic effect than standard pharmaceutical doses. This is not false modesty: a practitioner who oversells herbal analgesics will lose credibility and harm the people they serve.

The realistic expectation is: herbal analgesics (willow bark, turmeric, ginger combined) may reduce pain by 20-40% — comparable to the lower end of NSAID effects, and sufficient for many musculoskeletal, arthritis, and headache presentations. They are not adequate for moderate-to-severe acute pain (fractures, post-surgical pain, severe dysmenorrhea) and should not be used as such.

### 5.2 Pain Management Protocols

**Musculoskeletal pain (back pain, joint pain, sprains):**
- Willow bark tea/tincture (240mg salicin/day equivalent): primary analgesic herb; see Section 1.9
- Turmeric Golden Paste (500-1,000mg curcumin equivalent twice daily with piperine and fat): anti-inflammatory adjunct; see Section 1.4
- Topical arnica (Arnica montana) — if available: Moderate evidence for blunt trauma and muscle soreness; apply arnica gel or infused oil to intact skin around (not on) bruised area 3-4 times daily; **do not apply to broken skin; do not ingest arnica at any dose (toxic)**.
- Ginger compress: Grate 3-4 tablespoons fresh ginger into muslin cloth; immerse in hot water; apply as warm compress to painful joint for 15-20 minutes. Counter-irritant and mild analgesic effect.

**Headache:**
- Willow bark tea: 2 cups, sipped slowly over 30 minutes at headache onset
- Peppermint essential oil (topical): 2 drops diluted in 1 teaspoon carrier oil; apply to temples and forehead (avoid eyes); a 2016 RCT found topical peppermint oil equivalent to acetaminophen 1000mg for tension headache at 60 minutes. Evidence grade: Moderate for tension headache specifically.
- Ginger tea: Moderate evidence for migraine prophylaxis and acute migraine when taken at onset

**Menstrual pain (dysmenorrhea):**
- Ginger: A 2014 RCT found 250mg ginger powder four times daily equivalent to 250mg mefenamic acid and 400mg ibuprofen for dysmenorrhea pain. Evidence grade: Moderate.
- Raspberry leaf (Rubus idaeus): Traditional use for uterine cramping; preliminary evidence; considered safe in second and third trimester (avoid first trimester due to uterine stimulant effect).

**Topical pain preparations:**
- Cayenne pepper (capsaicin) cream: Capsaicin depletes substance P from peripheral pain nerve endings. Apply homemade cream (1/4 teaspoon cayenne powder mixed into 1/4 cup coconut oil) to painful area 3-4 times daily. Initial burning sensation is expected. Wash hands thoroughly after use; avoid contact with eyes and mucous membranes. Avoid on broken skin. Takes 1-2 weeks of consistent use to show full analgesic benefit (nerve desensitization). Evidence grade: Moderate for osteoarthritis and neuropathic pain.

**Note on St. John's Wort:** Despite popular use for depression, St. John's Wort is one of the most pharmacokinetically active herbs known — it is a potent inducer of liver CYP3A4 and P-glycoprotein, enzymes responsible for metabolizing a wide range of drugs. It is not covered elsewhere in this guide because safer alternatives exist for its primary indication and because its interaction profile makes it inappropriate for general use in humanitarian settings where pharmaceutical medication history may be incomplete.

### 5.4 Chronic Condition Support in Displacement

Individuals with chronic conditions (hypertension, diabetes, epilepsy, mental health conditions) who are displaced frequently experience medication disruption. Herbal medicine has a limited role here — it does not replace insulin, antiepileptics, or psychiatric medications. The priorities are:

1. Reconnect patients with their pharmaceutical medications through humanitarian supply chains (WHO Essential Medicines List access)
2. Identify any herbal remedies the patient is already using that may interact with resumed medications
3. Provide supportive care (nutrition, stress reduction, appropriate physical activity within limitations)

**Hibiscus (Hibiscus sabdariffa) for hypertension — Evidence grade: Moderate**
Multiple small RCTs find hibiscus calyx tea (1-2 teaspoons dried in 250ml water, twice daily) produces modest antihypertensive effects (average systolic reduction 7-8 mmHg) compared to placebo. This is not equivalent to pharmaceutical antihypertensive therapy but may reduce peak blood pressure burden during pharmaceutical supply interruptions. Safe in adults without pharmaceutical antihypertensive combinations (additive hypotensive effect can cause dizziness). **Pregnancy:** Hibiscus has uterotonic and abortifacient effects in animal studies; avoid in pregnancy.

**Valerian (Valeriana officinalis) for anxiety and sleep — Evidence grade: Moderate**
Displacement and emergency settings produce significant psychological stress, insomnia, and anxiety, all of which amplify physical health vulnerability. Valerian root contains isovaleric acid and valepotriates that potentiate GABA activity, producing mild sedative and anxiolytic effects. A systematic review of 16 RCTs found inconsistent but generally positive evidence for sleep improvement. Dose: 300-600mg dried root extract 30-60 minutes before sleep, or 5-10ml of 1:5 tincture in water. Effects require 2-4 weeks of consistent use for maximum benefit.

*Safety:* Valerian is considered safe for short-to-medium-term use (up to 28 days in most reviewed trials). Avoid with benzodiazepines, barbiturates, or other CNS depressants (additive sedation). Avoid in pregnancy and breastfeeding (insufficient safety data). May cause morning grogginess at higher doses. **Paradoxical stimulant effect** occurs in some individuals — if this occurs, discontinue.

**Lemon balm (Melissa officinalis) for mild anxiety — Evidence grade: Preliminary-to-Moderate**
Rosmarinic acid in lemon balm inhibits GABA transaminase (prolonging GABA activity) and has mild anxiolytic effects at doses of 300-600mg dried herb equivalent per day. Lemon balm tea (2 tablespoons fresh or 1 tablespoon dried in 250ml water, 10-minute steep) is safe, pleasant-tasting, and available from container gardens. Combines well with chamomile for combined GI and anxiety support. Safe in pregnancy at tea doses; safe for children. No significant drug interactions at tea doses.

### 5.5 Malnutrition and Herbal Bitters for Digestive Restoration

Severely malnourished individuals in humanitarian settings often have impaired digestive function, reduced gastric acid, and poor nutrient absorption even when food becomes available. Herbal bitters — preparations of bitter-tasting herbs — stimulate gastric acid and bile production via the bitter taste receptors (TAS2Rs) on the tongue and GI mucosa. This can support the transition from starvation to adequate feeding.

**Gentian (Gentiana lutea) and dandelion root (Taraxacum officinale) are the most accessible bitters:**
- *Dandelion root tea:* 1 teaspoon dried and roasted dandelion root simmered in 250ml water for 10 minutes; drink 20 minutes before meals. Dandelion root is a mild liver and bile stimulant. The entire dandelion plant (leaves, roots, flowers) is nutritionally significant — young leaves are high in vitamins A and C, calcium, and iron, making dandelion both a bitter herb and an accessible nutritional food.
- *Gentian tincture:* 1-2ml of 1:5 tincture in water, 20 minutes before meals. Do not use with active peptic ulcer (increases acid production; contraindicated). Gentian is safe during short-term use in adults; avoid in pregnancy.

These preparations work over days to weeks; they are not acute interventions. Their primary value is in the recovery phase after acute illness or extended nutritional deprivation.

## Section 6: Cultivation, Preservation, and Cultivation Planning

**Confidence: 88% — horticulture and preservation practices are well-documented; seed viability data sourced from USDA and university extension systems**

### 6.1 Priority Herbs for Cultivation in Limited Space

These ten herbs provide the highest therapeutic value relative to growing space requirements. All can be grown in containers (15-30cm minimum depth for root herbs; 10-15cm for leafy herbs), raised beds, or border plantings.

| Herb | Growing requirements | Harvest parts | Seed or root start | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Calendula | Full sun; poor to average soil; cool-season annual | Flowers | Seed direct-sow | Self-seeding; harvest flowers every 2-3 days to extend bloom; extremely easy to grow |
| Chamomile (German) | Full sun; tolerates poor soil; annual | Flowers | Seed surface-sow | Self-seeding prolifically; harvest flowers when petals reflex back; grows in gravel paths |
| Peppermint | Part shade tolerant; moist soil; spreading perennial | Leaves | Division or root cutting (does not breed true from seed) | Must be contained in pot or bed border — spreads aggressively by runners; high oil content in leaves from early morning harvest |
| Thyme | Full sun; well-drained, dry soil; perennial | Leaves, stems | Seed or cuttings | Drought-tolerant; container-friendly; harvest before flowering for maximum volatile oil |
| Garlic | Full sun; rich well-drained soil; annual | Bulbs | Cloves (planted individually) | Plant largest cloves in autumn for summer harvest; saves easily from year to year |
| Echinacea | Full sun; average to poor soil; perennial | Roots (3-year-old plants), flowers, leaves | Seed (slow) or root division | 3-5 year commitment before root harvest; flowers and leaves usable from year 1 |
| Plantain (broadleaf) | Any soil; full sun to shade; perennial | Leaves, seeds | Volunteer weed in most temperate areas — locate, do not eradicate | Identify and preserve existing plants rather than transplanting |
| Ginger | Part shade; rich moist soil; tropical annual (or container in temperate zones) | Rhizomes | Rhizome sections | Grow in pots that can be moved indoors in cold climates; harvest in autumn when leaves die back |
| Elderberry | Full to part sun; moist rich soil; shrub/small tree | Berries, flowers | Hardwood cuttings or transplant | 2-3 years to significant fruit production; grows as hedge; highly productive once established |
| Lemon balm | Part shade; average soil; perennial | Leaves | Seed or division | Quick growing; spreads but less aggressively than mint; calming and carminative; lemony flavor |

### 6.2 Drying and Storage Protocols

Proper drying is the single most critical step in herbal medicine preservation. Improperly dried herbs develop mold that can produce mycotoxins and reduce or eliminate medicinal activity. Volatile aromatic herbs (thyme, peppermint, chamomile) lose potency rapidly if dried at excessive temperatures.

**Air-drying (low-tech, all contexts):**
- Harvest in the morning after dew dries and before afternoon heat diminishes volatile oils
- Bundle 5-8 stems loosely with twine (over-bundling prevents airflow and causes mold)
- Hang upside-down in warm, well-ventilated, dark location — darkness prevents photo-oxidation of volatile compounds; temperature 25-35°C is ideal; above 40°C degrades volatile oils
- Drying time: leafy herbs 7-14 days; roots 3-6 weeks; flowers 3-10 days
- Test for dryness: leaves and flowers should crumble between fingers; stems should snap cleanly; roots should be hard throughout with no flexible core
- In humid climates (above 70% relative humidity), air-drying is unreliable and encourages mold — use dehydrator or oven at lowest setting (55°C / 130°F maximum) instead

**Dehydrator drying:**
- Maximum temperature 40°C (104°F) for leaves and flowers — higher temperatures drive off volatile oils
- Roots: 55-60°C is acceptable (roots are non-volatile)
- Single layer on trays; rotate trays every 2 hours

**Oven drying (emergency backup):**
- Use lowest possible setting; prop door slightly open; maximum 60°C for non-volatile material
- Check every 30 minutes; risk of over-drying and burning is high

**Storage:**
- Whole dried herbs retain potency longer than powdered; grind only what you need
- Store in airtight containers (glass jars with tight-fitting lids preferred; clean paper bags inside sealed tins acceptable)
- Label all containers with: herb name, plant part, harvest date, location
- Store in dark, cool, dry location — away from kitchen heat and humidity
- Expected potency shelf life: dried leaves and flowers 12-18 months at good storage conditions; dried roots 2-3 years; seeds 3-5 years
- Discard material that smells musty, shows any visible mold, or has lost color significantly

### 6.3 Seed Saving for Medicinal Herbs

Seed saving ensures a continuously self-renewing supply of medicinal herbs. See also the Wave 1 seed preservation domain for detailed protocols applicable to all annual and biennial crops.

**Annual herbs (calendula, chamomile, basil, cilantro/coriander):**
- Allow a subset of plants (at least 15-20% of the planting) to flower and set seed fully without harvesting flowers
- Harvest seed heads when they turn tan or brown and begin to dry on the plant — before they shatter and self-sow beyond collection
- Place harvested heads in paper bags; shake to release seeds; winnow by gently blowing across seeds spread on clean cloth (chaff is lighter and blows away)
- Store in labeled paper envelopes inside airtight jars with a silica gel desiccant packet

**Biennial herbs (caraway, fennel):** Flower and set seed in their second year; allow one-third of plants to complete their cycle each year to maintain continuous seed supply.

**Perennial herbs (thyme, peppermint, echinacea, elderberry):**
- Propagate vegetatively when possible — cuttings, root divisions, and suckers maintain exact genetic characteristics and begin producing immediately
- Peppermint and most mints are sterile hybrids that do not breed true from seed; propagate only by cuttings or root division
- Echinacea does produce viable seed; harvest seed heads in autumn when dark and dry; cold-stratify seeds for 30-60 days in refrigerator before spring planting

**Cross-pollination considerations:**
- Chamomile, calendula, and echinacea are open-pollinated by insects; maintain 30-100 meter isolation from other varieties of the same species if maintaining specific cultivars for known medicinal profile
- Most common medicinal herbs are not hybridized and breed true within a species

### 6.4 Seasonal Availability Planning

Medicinal herb availability follows predictable seasonal patterns that must inform planning for humanitarian or emergency contexts. No fresh herb is available year-round in temperate climates, and dried herb supplies must bridge seasonal gaps.

**Spring harvest priority:** Roots that were stored through winter reach their lowest starch/highest alkaloid concentration in spring; echinacea root and willow bark are best harvested in spring (willow bark from 2-3 year old branches in early spring before leaf burst, when salicin concentration is highest). Plantain leaves emerge early and are highly potent in spring.

**Summer harvest priority:** Flowers (calendula, chamomile, elderflower) must be harvested at peak bloom — calendula every 2-3 days; chamomile when petals reflex back; elderflower when flowers are just opening (before pollen falls). Leafy herbs (thyme, peppermint, lemon balm) reach peak volatile oil content just before flowering. Harvest leaves in morning.

**Autumn harvest priority:** Elderberries; roots of perennial herbs (echinacea, valerian, licorice) — root harvest in autumn when above-ground portions die back concentrates active compounds in root. Garlic harvest in late summer/early autumn.

**Year-round availability:** Dried stocks prepared during appropriate seasons; garlic in storage; ginger rhizomes (can overwinter in containers brought indoors); honey (indefinite shelf life).

**Minimum dry stock target for a 10-person community (3-month supply):**
- Calendula flowers (dried): 200g
- Chamomile flowers (dried): 150g
- Peppermint leaf (dried): 100g
- Thyme leaf (dried): 150g
- Elderberry (dried): 300g
- Ginger (dried powder or rhizomes): 200g
- Garlic (fresh stored): 1kg
- Raw honey: 2kg minimum
- Willow bark (dried): 200g
- Echinacea root or herb (dried): 150g

### 6.5 Integration with Other Domains

**Water systems (cross-link: WAVE_0_DOMAIN_3_CHEMICAL_DISINFECTION.md):**
All herbal preparations require clean water. Boiling water for herb decoctions provides dual benefit — simultaneous water sterilization and herb preparation. Where chemical disinfection has been used, allow disinfectant to fully work (20-30 minutes for chlorine) before using water for herbal preparation, as residual chlorine reacts with and degrades some polyphenolic compounds.

**Seed preservation (cross-link: Wave 1 seed saving protocols):**
Medicinal herb seed saving follows identical protocols to vegetable seeds — temperature, moisture, darkness, and airtight storage are the critical variables. Medicinal herb seeds should be stored separately from food crops in clearly labeled containers.

**Sanitation (cross-link: WAVE_0_DOMAIN_4_WASTEWATER_AND_GREYWATER_SYSTEMS.md):**
Herb garden greywater (water used for rinsing herbs or cleaning equipment) is safe for irrigation return. Herb bed location should observe the same setback from latrines and wastewater systems as food gardens: minimum 30 meters from pit latrines, 15 meters from greywater disposal sites.

**Composting (cross-link: Wave 1 composting guide):**
Spent herb material after infusion, drying, and processing is excellent compost material. Avoid composting material from plants treated with antimicrobial compounds (concentrated essential oils) as these can inhibit compost microbial activity. Garlic skins and herb stems compost well.

**Small-scale animal care (cross-link: Wave 2 Domain 2 — small-scale livestock):**
Several herbs in this guide are used in traditional veterinary practice: calendula (wound care), chamomile (digestive), thyme (respiratory). Dosing for animals differs significantly from human dosing — consult species-specific veterinary references before applying these protocols to livestock.

## Appendix A: Escalation Triggers — Master Reference

**Always refer to conventional medical care regardless of herbal availability when any of these signs are present:**

**General:**
- High fever (above 40°C / 104°F in adults; above 39°C / 102.2°F in children)
- Fever in infants under 3 months (any fever)
- Signs of severe dehydration: extreme thirst, no urine in 8 hours, very dry mouth, confusion
- Confusion, altered consciousness, or inability to wake person normally
- Any sign that is worsening rapidly despite treatment

**Wounds:**
- Red streaking extending from wound margin
- Fever accompanying wound infection
- Wound larger than 2cm with full-thickness tissue loss
- Animal bites
- Wounds on hands, feet, or face with deep tissue involvement
- No improvement after 3-4 days of appropriate wound care

**Gastrointestinal:**
- Blood in stool
- Severe vomiting preventing fluid intake
- Signs of dehydration in children
- Fever above 39°C with diarrhea
- Diarrhea lasting over 5 days (adults) or 3 days (children)

**Respiratory:**
- Rapid breathing: >60 breaths/min in infants, >50 in toddlers, >30 in adults at rest
- Nasal flaring, chest wall retractions, cyanosis (blue lips or fingertips)
- Inability to speak or feed due to breathlessness
- Stridor (high-pitched sound on inhale)
- Suspected bacterial pneumonia (high fever, productive cough, rapid deterioration)

**Pain:**
- Severe pain not controlled by herbal analgesics
- Pain after trauma with suspected fracture, dislocation, or internal injury
- Headache described as "worst headache of my life" — thunderclap headache (possible subarachnoid hemorrhage)
- Chest pain or left arm pain
- Abdominal pain with rigid abdomen
