# Herbal Medicine and Medicinal Plants: Preparation, Profiles, and Cultivation

---

## Safety Notice

**This guide is educational material only. It does not constitute medical advice and must not be used as a substitute for consultation with a qualified healthcare provider.**

Before using any herbal preparation:

- Consult a licensed healthcare provider if you are pregnant, nursing, or planning pregnancy. Many medicinal plants are contraindicated during pregnancy, including but not limited to: St. John's Wort, blue cohosh, pennyroyal, tansy, and high-dose ginger.
- Consult a healthcare provider if you are taking prescription or over-the-counter medications. Herb-drug interactions are real and clinically significant. St. John's Wort, for example, accelerates the metabolism of dozens of medications including oral contraceptives, antiretrovirals, cyclosporine, warfarin, and certain antidepressants.
- Consult a healthcare provider if you have a chronic medical condition (diabetes, cardiovascular disease, liver or kidney disease, autoimmune conditions, epilepsy).
- **Defer to a licensed practitioner for any serious, worsening, or undiagnosed symptoms** — particularly chest pain, difficulty breathing, high fever, severe pain, neurological symptoms, or signs of systemic infection.
- Do not use herbal preparations as the sole treatment for conditions requiring medical management.
- This guide describes preparation techniques for common, generally recognized safe herbs in normal culinary and low-dose medicinal ranges. It does not cover toxic plants, high-potency alkaloid-containing species, or prescription botanicals.

The preparation methods and dosing ranges described here represent general traditional and evidence-informed guidelines. Individual response to botanicals varies. Start with the lowest effective dose, observe your response, and discontinue if adverse effects occur.

## Introduction

Medicinal plants represent the oldest documented form of human therapeutics. Archaeological evidence places plant-based medicine in human communities at least 60,000 years ago, and every major traditional medicine system — Ayurveda, Traditional Chinese Medicine, Indigenous North American medicine, Western European herbalism, Unani-Tibb — developed extensive botanical pharmacopoeias over millennia of empirical observation.

In low-resource and off-grid contexts, medicinal herbs serve several practical functions that pharmaceuticals cannot easily replicate: they grow where people live, they can be produced and processed without industrial infrastructure, they address the most common health challenges (respiratory infections, digestive complaints, wound care, sleep disturbance, mild pain) effectively and safely when used correctly, and they represent a layer of health resilience that remains available when supply chains break down.

This guide does not advocate replacing modern medicine with herbalism. Rather, it treats medicinal plant knowledge as a foundational skill for resilient communities — alongside food preservation, water treatment, and sanitation — that complements, rather than competes with, professional healthcare. A family that knows how to prepare a proper elderberry syrup before cold season, dress a minor wound with calendula salve, or manage a sleepless night with lemon balm tea has meaningful health tools that require no prescription, no pharmacy, and no electricity.

The 14 plant profiles in this guide were selected on three criteria: global cultivability (most grow in USDA Zones 4–9 and have analogs in tropical and subtropical climates), evidence quality (traditional use supported by phytochemical research and clinical data), and preparation accessibility (methods requiring no specialized equipment beyond basic kitchen tools). Growing notes are integrated because the most reliable, affordable herbal medicine is the kind you grow yourself.

## Section 1: Preparation Methods

Herbal preparations extract and concentrate bioactive compounds from plant material using water, alcohol, oil, or other solvents. The preparation method determines which constituents are captured, how stable the preparation is, and what form is most therapeutic for a given application. Understanding the underlying principles of each method allows you to improvise safely and troubleshoot problems in the field.

### 1.1 Infusions (Herbal Tea)

An infusion is the simplest herbal preparation: hot water extraction of plant material, typically flowers, leaves, and soft stems. Water extracts water-soluble compounds: polyphenols, flavonoids, volatile oils (to varying degrees), mucilages, and many glycosides. Alkaloids and resins extract poorly into water; those compounds require alcohol.

**Water temperature matters.** Different compounds extract at different temperatures, and excessive heat destroys some volatile constituents.

| Plant Part | Optimal Temperature | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Delicate flowers, aromatic leaves | 80–90°C (175–195°F) | Below boiling to preserve volatile oils |
| Standard leaves, flowering tops | 90–100°C (195–212°F) | Near-boiling is appropriate |
| Roots, bark, seeds, hard material | Full boil — see decoction | These require decoction, not infusion |

**Standard infusion protocol:**
1. Bring water to appropriate temperature (not boiling for aromatic herbs)
2. Measure **1–2 teaspoons dried herb** (or **2–3 teaspoons fresh herb**) per 8 oz (240 mL) cup
3. Pour hot water over herb in a covered vessel — covering is essential to retain volatile oils
4. Steep **5–15 minutes** depending on desired strength and plant
5. Strain and drink while warm, or cool for refrigerated storage

**Medicinal-strength infusion (nourishing infusion):** For mineral-rich herbs like nettle, use 1 oz (28g) dried herb per quart (950 mL) water, steep covered for 4–8 hours or overnight. This cold-overnight method (see Section 1.6) extracts substantially more minerals and mucilaginous compounds than a standard steep.

**Storage and shelf life:**
- Freshly brewed infusion: drink immediately or refrigerate
- Refrigerated infusion: consume within **24–48 hours** — aqueous preparations are microbiologically vulnerable
- Do not store hot infusions in sealed thermoses for extended periods; this promotes bacterial growth

**Dosing:** Standard therapeutic range is **1–3 cups per day**, typically taken between meals for digestive herbs, or 30–60 minutes before bed for calming herbs. Acute symptom relief (sore throat, mild nausea) may use more frequent dosing — up to every 2–3 hours.

### 1.2 Decoctions (Simmered Roots and Bark)

A decoction applies sustained heat to extract constituents from dense plant material — roots, rhizomes, bark, seeds, woody stems, and dried berries — that do not yield their compounds to simple steeping. The heat breaks down cell walls and dissolves compounds that are not water-soluble at lower temperatures.

**Standard decoction protocol:**
1. Measure **1 tablespoon (5–10g) dried root or bark** per 8 oz (240 mL) water, or **2 tablespoons fresh material**
2. Place material in cold water (starting in cold water improves extraction compared to adding to hot water)
3. Bring to a low boil, then **reduce to a simmer**
4. Simmer **20–45 minutes** depending on material density:
   - Thin roots (ginger, licorice): 20 minutes
   - Dense roots (valerian, ashwagandha): 30–40 minutes
   - Hard bark (slippery elm, oak): 40–45 minutes
5. Strain while hot through fine mesh or cloth
6. For a more concentrated preparation (2:1 or 4:1), continue simmering uncovered until volume reduces by half or three-quarters

**Concentration and dosing:**
Standard decoction: 1 cup water → 1 cup medicine (1:1)
Concentrated decoction: 2 cups water → 1 cup medicine after reduction (2:1) — half the standard dose, twice the potency

**Preservation:** Decoctions are aqueous and must be refrigerated. They keep **3–5 days** refrigerated. For longer storage, add **10–20% grain alcohol by volume** (roughly 2–3 tablespoons of 80-proof vodka per cup of decoction) to extend shelf life to 2–4 weeks. Alternatively, freeze in ice cube trays for single-dose portions; frozen decoctions keep **6 months**.

**Elderberry syrup** (described in detail in Plant Profiles) is a decoction that is concentrated and preserved with honey — shelf life reaches 2–3 months refrigerated.

### 1.3 Tinctures (Alcohol Extraction)

A tincture is an alcohol extraction of plant material that captures both water-soluble and fat-soluble compounds — including alkaloids, resins, and volatile oils that are poorly extracted by water. Tinctures are the most potent, most stable, and most shelf-stable form of liquid herbal medicine.

**Alcohol requirements:**

**Minimum: 40% ABV (80-proof) for preservation and extraction.** At this concentration, alcohol inhibits microbial growth and preserves the tincture for 5+ years. Below 40% ABV, the preparation is not adequately preserved and will ferment or harbor pathogens. Standard vodka (80-proof) is the most accessible 40% ABV menstruum.

- **Leafy herbs and fresh plant material**: 40–50% ABV (80–100 proof vodka) — works well for most common herbs
- **Resins, alkaloids, dry woody material**: 60–70% ABV — requires high-proof spirits (Everclear diluted, or grain alcohol + water to target percentage)
- **Mucilaginous herbs (marshmallow root, slippery elm)**: 25–30% ABV + glycerin — high alcohol disrupts mucilage extraction; these are better prepared as glycerites or cold infusions

**Folk method (weight/volume approximation):**
1. Fill a clean glass jar with dried plant material (loosely, not compacted): roughly **1 part herb by weight** to **5 parts menstruum by volume** (1:5 ratio for dried herbs)
2. For fresh plant material: roughly **1:2** ratio by weight
3. Pour alcohol over herb until fully submerged and covered by at least 1 inch (2.5 cm)
4. Cap tightly, label with date and contents
5. Store in a cool, dark location; **shake or turn daily** for the first week, then weekly
6. Macerate for **4–6 weeks minimum** (6 weeks is standard; 8 weeks for dense roots)
7. Strain through cheesecloth, pressing out all liquid; discard marc (spent plant material)
8. Bottle in dark glass; label with herb, menstruum, date, and batch

**Standard dosage:** Tinctures are potent. Typical adult dosing is **1–4 mL (20–80 drops)** taken in a small amount of water, **2–3 times daily**. Start at the low end. Children's dosing follows Clark's Rule: child's weight in pounds ÷ 150 × adult dose = child's dose. Always consult a practitioner before giving tinctures to children under 12.

**Shelf life: 5–10 years** properly stored in dark glass, cool temperature, sealed. This is the primary advantage of tinctures in off-grid contexts — a well-made tincture provides medicine-cabinet stability for a decade.

**Glycerites (alcohol-free alternative):** Substitute food-grade vegetable glycerin (diluted to 60% glycerin / 40% water) for menstruum. Extract time doubles to 8–10 weeks. Shelf life: 1–2 years. Glycerites are appropriate for those who avoid alcohol, for children, and for some mucilaginous herbs. Potency is lower than alcohol tinctures for many compounds.

### 1.4 Salves and Poultices

Salves and poultices deliver medicinal compounds topically — directly to skin tissue, wounds, muscle, and joints. They are particularly valuable in wound care, skin conditions, and musculoskeletal complaints.

**Infused oil (the base of all salves):**

Salves begin with a **medicated oil** — plant material extracted into a fixed carrier oil (olive oil, coconut oil, almond oil). Two methods:

*Cold infusion method (highest quality):*
1. Fill a clean glass jar with dried herb (moisture in fresh herbs can cause rancidity — always dry thoroughly first)
2. Cover completely with carrier oil, leaving no air space
3. Cap tightly; place in a sunny window or warm spot (25–30°C)
4. Infuse **4–6 weeks**, shaking every few days
5. Strain through fine cloth; press out all oil from herb
6. Store in dark glass; label with herb, oil, and date
7. Shelf life of infused oil: **1–2 years** (carrier oil dependent — olive oil lasts longer than nut oils)

*Warm infusion method (faster):*
1. Combine dried herb and oil in a double boiler or slow cooker
2. Maintain temperature at **40–60°C (104–140°F)** — never above 70°C, which destroys compounds
3. Infuse for **24–72 hours** with gentle heat
4. Strain and cool before bottling

**Beeswax binding for salve:**
1. Gently warm infused oil in a double boiler
2. Add **1 oz (28g) beeswax per cup (240 mL) of infused oil** for a standard salve consistency
3. Test consistency: place a drop on a cold plate — if too hard, add more oil; if too soft, add more beeswax
4. Pour immediately into clean tins or glass jars before beeswax solidifies
5. Do not disturb until fully cooled and set
6. Label with contents, date, and intended use

**Shelf life of finished salve: 1–2 years** stored in a cool, dark location. Heat and light accelerate rancidity.

**Poultice (fresh application):**
A poultice applies fresh or slightly processed plant material directly to skin. It is the most basic of all herbal preparations and often the most immediately effective for wounds, stings, and skin reactions.

*Fresh leaf poultice (plantain, comfrey):*
1. Select fresh leaves; wash thoroughly
2. Chew briefly, or bruise by rolling between palms, or crush with a mortar — mechanical damage releases cellular fluids and active compounds
3. Apply directly to affected area
4. Cover loosely with a clean cloth; secure if needed
5. Change every 1–4 hours; clean wound between applications

*Dried herb poultice:*
1. Moisten powdered herb with enough water to form a paste
2. Apply paste to affected area; cover with cloth
3. Leave 30–60 minutes; clean area after removal

**Safety:** Never apply poultices to deep puncture wounds, infected wounds with systemic signs (fever, spreading redness), or wounds requiring suturing. Poultices are appropriate for minor cuts, abrasions, insect stings, superficial burns, and skin irritation.

### 1.5 Dried Preparations and Powders

Drying plant material concentrates compounds by removing water weight, extends shelf life significantly compared to fresh preparations, and enables powdering for encapsulation, cooking, or direct consumption.

**Drying methods ranked by quality:**

1. **Air drying (best for aromatic herbs):** Tie small bundles (not tightly compacted) and hang upside down in a warm, dry, well-ventilated space with **no direct sunlight**. Ideal conditions: 25–35°C, below 50% relative humidity, good air circulation. Complete drying takes **7–21 days** depending on material density and ambient humidity. Herbs are fully dried when leaves crumble rather than bend, and stems snap rather than flex.

2. **Dehydrator drying (most controllable):** Set temperature at **35–45°C (95–113°F)** for aromatic herbs (higher temperatures drive off volatile oils). Roots and bark can tolerate **50–60°C**. Drying time: **4–12 hours** depending on material. Dehydrators are worth the investment in humid climates where air drying is unreliable.

3. **Oven drying (acceptable for roots and non-aromatic material):** Use the lowest oven setting, prop door slightly open for moisture escape. Aromatic herbs lose significant volatile oil content in the oven — reserve this method for roots and bark that tolerate heat.

4. **Solar drying:** Effective in dry, warm climates. Screen material in thin layers, cover with lightweight mesh to keep debris off. Rotate material 1–2 times daily. Risk: UV light degrades some photosensitive compounds. Reserve for roots and less photosensitive material; dry aromatic herbs in shade.

**Storage:** Dried herbs must be stored properly to maintain medicinal quality.
- **Container:** Airtight glass jars, preferably amber or kept in a dark location. Moisture is the primary enemy.
- **Humidity:** Below 60% relative humidity. Silica gel desiccant packets help in humid climates.
- **Temperature:** Cool, consistent temperature. Avoid locations that experience wide temperature swings.
- **Light:** Dark storage (cabinet, closet) is preferable; UV degrades many active compounds.
- **Label every container:** Include herb name, part used, date harvested/purchased, and source.

**Shelf life of dried herbs (properly stored):**
- Leaves and flowers: **1–2 years** maintaining medicinal strength; acceptable up to 3 years
- Roots and bark: **2–4 years**
- Seeds and berries: **2–3 years**
- Powdered herbs: **6–12 months** (powdering dramatically increases surface area and accelerates oxidation)

**Capsule filling:** Powdered herbs can be packed into size 00 gelatin or vegetarian capsules (approximately 500–700 mg per capsule). A simple capsule filling machine ($15–30) speeds the process significantly. Standard dosing is **2 capsules 2–3 times daily**, though this varies substantially by herb and compound concentration.

### 1.6 Cold Infusions (Overnight Steep)

A cold infusion extracts water-soluble compounds at room temperature or refrigerator temperature over an extended period — typically 4–12 hours or overnight. This method is particularly suited to:

- **Mucilaginous herbs** (marshmallow root, slippery elm, comfrey leaf) — heat can destroy or alter the mucilaginous polysaccharides that are therapeutically important; cold extraction preserves them intact
- **Delicate aromatic herbs** where heat accelerates volatile oil evaporation
- **Nourishing herbal infusions** (nettle, oatstraw, red clover) where extended extraction time dramatically increases mineral content
- **Summertime preparation** — no heating required; can be left overnight in the refrigerator

**Protocol:**
1. Place **1 oz (28g) dried herb** (or 2 oz fresh) per quart (950 mL) water — this is a concentrated medicinal preparation, not a light tea
2. Use room temperature or cool filtered water
3. Cover vessel; steep **4–8 hours** at room temperature, or **8–12 hours** refrigerated
4. Strain and drink throughout the day
5. **Consume within 24 hours** — cold infusions are particularly vulnerable to microbial growth because no pasteurization step has occurred; refrigerate and discard any remainder after one day

**Water quality:** Cold preparations do not benefit from the microbe-killing effect of boiling. Use filtered, spring, or otherwise clean water. In off-grid contexts, always treat water before using for cold infusions if microbiological quality is uncertain.

**Advantage over hot infusion:** Studies on marshmallow root (Althaea officinalis) have demonstrated 40–50% higher mucilage yield from cold extraction compared to hot extraction at equivalent steep times. Similar advantages apply to other mucilaginous herbs.

### Profile 1: Echinacea (*Echinacea purpurea*, *E. angustifolia*, *E. pallida*)

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Family | Asteraceae |
| Parts used | Aerial parts in flower (*E. purpurea*); root (*E. angustifolia*, *E. pallida*) |
| Primary uses | Immune support; upper respiratory tract infections; wound care |
| Evidence level | Moderate clinical evidence for reducing cold duration and severity |
| Safety profile | Generally safe; avoid in progressive systemic diseases, autoimmune conditions |

**Traditional use:** Native to central North America, echinacea has been used medicinally by Plains Indigenous nations — including the Comanche, Kiowa, and Oglala Lakota — for centuries, primarily for wound care, snake bite, and infections. Introduced to European herbalism in the 1800s, it became one of the best-selling herbal supplements globally by the late 20th century.

**Active compounds:** Alkylamides (particularly in *E. purpurea* and *E. angustifolia*) appear to be primary immunomodulating constituents; caffeic acid derivatives (echinacoside, cichoric acid) contribute anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects; polysaccharides (arabinogalactans) enhance innate immune response. The relative importance of each constituent class remains debated in the literature.

**Evidence:** A 2015 Cochrane review of 24 randomized controlled trials found evidence that some echinacea preparations reduce the incidence of the common cold and shorten its duration, though results varied substantially by preparation and species. The European Medicines Agency approved *E. purpurea* aerial parts for symptomatic treatment of common cold based on established traditional use combined with clinical data.

**Preparation and dosing:**

*Tincture (most clinically studied form):*
- Fresh aerial parts of *E. purpurea* in 40–50% ABV: 1:2 ratio (1 part fresh herb by weight, 2 parts menstruum by volume)
- Dried root of *E. angustifolia*: 1:5 ratio in 60% ABV
- Dose: **2–4 mL tincture, 3–4 times daily** at onset of cold symptoms; continue for **5–7 days**
- Not recommended for continuous use exceeding 8–10 weeks without a 2-week break

*Infusion (aerial parts):*
- 1–2 teaspoons dried flowering tops per cup, steep 10 minutes covered
- Drink 3 cups daily during acute illness

**Contraindications:** Avoid in autoimmune diseases (multiple sclerosis, lupus, rheumatoid arthritis) — immune stimulation may worsen autoimmune processes. Avoid in progressive systemic diseases (tuberculosis, HIV). Asteraceae allergy is a contraindication (though rare cross-reactivity). EMA advises against use during pregnancy; evidence insufficient for safety in nursing mothers.

**Growing notes:** *E. purpurea* is the most cultivable species — perennial in Zones 3–9, tolerates moderate drought, grows in average garden soil. Direct seed in fall (stratification by winter cold improves germination) or start indoors 8–10 weeks before last frost. Space plants 18–24 inches apart. Harvest aerial parts when the plant is in early full flower (typically July–August). First-year plants should not be harvested heavily — allow establishment. *E. angustifolia* root can be harvested in fall of the third year. See Section 3 for drying protocols.

### Profile 2: Ginger (*Zingiber officinale*)

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Family | Zingiberaceae |
| Parts used | Rhizome (fresh and dried) |
| Primary uses | Nausea and vomiting; digestive complaints; anti-inflammatory; circulation |
| Evidence level | Strong evidence for nausea and vomiting; moderate for anti-inflammatory effects |
| Safety profile | Very safe at culinary doses; high doses may interact with blood thinners |

**Traditional use:** Ginger has been used medicinally in Ayurvedic and Traditional Chinese Medicine for over 3,000 years — referenced in the Charaka Samhita (approximately 300 BCE) as a digestive aid, and in the Shennong Bencao Jing (206 BCE–220 CE) for warming the middle burner and expelling cold. It remains one of the most globally consumed medicinal plants and spices.

**Active compounds:** Gingerols (primary constituents in fresh ginger) and their dehydration products shogaols (formed during drying and heating) inhibit prostaglandin and leukotriene biosynthesis. Zingerone contributes antinausea properties. Paradols, sesquiterpenes, and various volatile oils contribute to the circulatory and carminative effects.

**Evidence:** Cochrane review (2014) of 12 trials found ginger superior to placebo for nausea and vomiting in pregnancy at doses of 1–1.5g dried ginger per day. Multiple studies confirm efficacy for postoperative nausea and chemotherapy-induced nausea. Anti-inflammatory effects supported in osteoarthritis trials at 255 mg standardized extract twice daily.

**Preparation and dosing:**

*Fresh ginger decoction:*
- **1 tablespoon (approximately 10g) freshly grated or sliced ginger per cup of water**
- Simmer covered for **10–15 minutes**
- Strain; add honey and lemon as desired
- Drink **2–3 cups daily** for digestive complaints or nausea; drink hot for warming circulatory effect
- For nausea of pregnancy: sip slowly, begin with smaller amounts

*Dried ginger infusion/decoction:*
- 1 teaspoon powdered dried ginger per cup; pour boiling water over, steep 10 minutes
- Lower potency than fresh root preparation; suitable for maintenance use

*Tincture:*
- Fresh ginger: 1:2 ratio in 40% ABV
- Dried ginger: 1:5 ratio in 50% ABV
- Dose: **1–2 mL, 3 times daily** for digestive support

*Fresh dosing notes:* Fresh ginger (higher gingerol content) is superior for acute nausea. Dried ginger (higher shogaol content) is more effective for warming circulation and chronic inflammatory conditions. These are different medicines from the same plant.

**Contraindications:** Generally very safe. High doses (**above 4g dry equivalent daily**) may increase bleeding risk — **avoid combining with warfarin, aspirin, or antiplatelet medications** without provider supervision. Consult provider before using therapeutically during pregnancy, though culinary amounts are universally considered safe. May lower blood glucose — monitor if diabetic and on medications.

**Growing notes:** Tropical/subtropical perennial (Zones 9–12 outdoors; annual in Zones 6–8 with lifting). Plant rhizome sections ("seed ginger") in spring after last frost in moist, rich, part-shade soil. Water regularly; does not tolerate drought or waterlogging. Harvest in fall when leaves yellow. Tropical gardeners can divide and replant indefinitely. In cool climates, bring rhizomes indoors for winter storage (55–60°F/13–16°C, not refrigerator).

### Profile 3: Turmeric (*Curcuma longa*)

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Family | Zingiberaceae |
| Parts used | Rhizome (fresh and dried/powdered) |
| Primary uses | Anti-inflammatory; antioxidant; liver support; joint pain |
| Evidence level | Moderate; bioavailability is a significant limitation |
| Safety profile | Safe at culinary doses; high isolated curcumin supplements warrant caution |

**Traditional use:** Central to Ayurvedic medicine as *haridra* — listed in the Sushruta Samhita as a wound healer, anti-inflammatory, and liver tonic. Used in Traditional Chinese Medicine (*jianghuang*) for moving stagnant blood and qi. Consumed daily in South Asian cuisines for 4,000+ years, providing both medicinal and nutritional benefits.

**Active compounds:** Curcuminoids — curcumin (70–80%), demethoxycurcumin, and bisdemethoxycurcumin — inhibit NF-κB (a master regulator of inflammation), COX-2, and 5-LOX enzymes. Volatile oils (ar-turmerone, α-turmerone) contribute to bioavailability and have independent activity. **Critical limitation:** Curcumin has poor oral bioavailability when consumed alone; black pepper's piperine (BioPerine) increases curcumin absorption by approximately 2,000% by inhibiting glucuronidation. Always combine turmeric with black pepper for medicinal use.

**Preparation and dosing:**

*Golden milk (traditional daily preparation):*
- 1 teaspoon dried turmeric powder + **1/8 teaspoon fresh ground black pepper** + 1/4 teaspoon ground ginger per cup of warm milk or plant milk
- Add honey to taste
- Drink 1–2 cups daily for anti-inflammatory support
- This preparation has been consumed as *haldi doodh* in Indian households for centuries

*Fresh root preparation:*
- Grate 1–2 inches fresh turmeric root, steep or decoct 10–15 minutes
- Always add a pinch of black pepper

*Tincture:*
- Fresh turmeric root: 1:2 ratio in 40–50% ABV
- Dried powder: 1:5 in 50% ABV
- Dose: **2–4 mL 2–3 times daily**

**Contraindications:** Avoid in gallstones or bile duct obstruction (curcumin stimulates bile production). High-dose isolated curcumin supplements (1g+ daily) may increase bleeding risk — avoid combining with anticoagulants. **Recent liver toxicity reports (2022–2023, FDA and case reports) are associated with high-dose curcumin supplements, not culinary turmeric.** Culinary amounts remain safe for general population. Consult provider for therapeutic supplementation. May increase menstrual flow in high doses.

**Growing notes:** Tropical perennial (outdoor Zones 8–12; container growing in cooler climates). Identical growing requirements to ginger: moist, rich soil, partial shade, warm temperatures. Rhizomes are slower to establish than ginger — plant 3–5 inches deep. Harvest 8–10 months after planting when leaves yellow. Dry and powder rhizomes using a food dehydrator and high-speed blender or grain mill.

### Profile 4: Lemon Balm (*Melissa officinalis*)

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Family | Lamiaceae |
| Parts used | Leaves and flowering tops |
| Primary uses | Digestive calming; anxiety; sleep; cold sores (topical) |
| Evidence level | Moderate for anxiety/sleep; clinical evidence for topical cold sore application |
| Safety profile | Excellent safety profile; one of the safest medicinal herbs for all ages |

**Traditional use:** Lemon balm has been grown in Mediterranean gardens since at least 300 BCE. Documented in ancient Greek and Roman medicine as a calming, digestive, and heart-toning herb. The 16th-century herbalist John Gerard called it "renewing youth." Carmelite Water, a 14th-century monastic preparation of lemon balm with angelica and other herbs, was sold across Europe as a calming tonic for centuries.

**Active compounds:** Rosmarinic acid (also found in rosemary, sage) inhibits MAO-A and GABA transaminase, supporting its anxiolytic and calming effects. Flavonoids (luteolin, apigenin) contribute sedative properties. Citral, geranial, and neral volatile oils provide the characteristic lemon scent and are antiviral (particularly active against herpes simplex virus in vitro and in clinical trials).

**Preparation and dosing:**

*Infusion (standard):*
- **2–3 teaspoons fresh leaves** or **1–2 teaspoons dried** per cup of water at 85°C (just below boiling)
- Steep **10–15 minutes** covered; the covering is essential — volatile oils evaporate readily
- For sleep: drink 1 cup 30–60 minutes before bed
- For digestive calming: drink after meals, up to 3 cups daily
- For children (6+): 1 teaspoon dried herb per cup, 1 cup as needed; consult provider for children under 6

*Tincture:*
- 1:5 dried leaf in 40% ABV
- Dose: **2–4 mL, 2–3 times daily** for anxiety; **4–6 mL** single dose for acute sleep support

*Topical application for cold sores:*
- Strong infusion (2 tablespoons dried herb per cup, steep 15 minutes) applied with clean cotton to lesion at onset and every 2–4 hours during active outbreak
- Cream containing 1% dried leaf extract (70:1 standardized preparation) demonstrated significant reduction in cold sore healing time in clinical trials

**Contraindications:** Essentially none at normal doses — one of the safest herbs in the Western pharmacopoeia. Theoretical concern about thyroid-suppressing effects at very high doses (rosmarinic acid may inhibit TSH binding); avoid high-dose supplementation with diagnosed thyroid disease. May enhance sedative effects of pharmaceutical sedatives — use caution with benzodiazepines or other sleep medications.

**Growing notes:** Incredibly easy to grow — perennial in Zones 4–9, spreads readily (plant in contained area or be prepared to manage spread). Thrives in average soil, tolerates part shade, reasonably drought-tolerant once established. Direct seed or transplant in spring; harvest freely once established (cutting stimulates bushy growth). Harvest before flowering for highest rosmarinic acid content. Air dry quickly — leaves brown if dried too slowly in humid conditions.

### Profile 5: Plantain (*Plantago major*, *P. lanceolata*)

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Family | Plantaginaceae |
| Parts used | Leaves (primary); seeds (*P. ovata* — psyllium) |
| Primary uses | Wound care; skin inflammation; respiratory (expectorant); minor burns |
| Evidence level | Traditional use well-documented; clinical evidence emerging for wound healing |
| Safety profile | Excellent; common weed with no significant toxicity at normal uses |

**Traditional use:** Plantain (*Plantago major*) is one of the most geographically widespread medicinal plants on earth — a European native now naturalized on every inhabited continent, growing in disturbed soil, roadsides, lawns, and garden paths. Called "white man's foot" by eastern North American Indigenous peoples because it appeared wherever European settlers walked. This ubiquity makes it the most immediately accessible herbal first-aid plant for most of the world's population.

**Active compounds:** Aucubin (iridoid glycoside) has demonstrated anti-inflammatory activity. Allantoin (also found in comfrey) promotes cell proliferation and wound healing. Mucilaginous polysaccharides soothe irritated mucous membranes (respiratory and digestive). Tannins contribute astringent and antimicrobial properties. Zinc and other minerals are present in nutritional amounts.

**Preparation and dosing:**

*Fresh leaf poultice (first-aid use):*
- Wash fresh leaf; chew briefly or crush between fingers to release fluids
- Apply directly to insect stings, minor cuts, scrapes, splinters, or superficial burns
- Hold in place with cloth; replace every 1–2 hours
- This is one of the most well-known and widely verified "field expedient" herbal preparations

*Infusion (respiratory/digestive):*
- 1–2 teaspoons dried leaf per cup, steep 10 minutes covered
- Drink 3 cups daily for dry, irritated cough; for digestive irritation
- The mucilaginous quality is more pronounced in a cold overnight infusion (see Section 1.6)

*Infused oil and salve:*
- Infuse dried leaf in olive oil (4–6 weeks cold method)
- Combine with beeswax for a wound-healing salve appropriate for cuts, cracked skin, and eczema
- See Section 1.4 for full salve preparation protocol

**Contraindications:** None significant. *P. ovata* (psyllium) seeds can cause bowel obstruction if taken without adequate water — always take psyllium seed preparations with at least 8 oz (240 mL) water per dose.

**Growing notes:** *Plantago major* almost certainly grows within walking distance of most readers — it colonizes compacted soil in lawns, roadsides, and disturbed areas. No cultivation needed for most; harvest from unsprayed areas well away from roads and agricultural chemical application zones. For cultivating for harvest: plant seeds in average garden soil, tolerates shade, almost impossible to kill. Harvest leaves anytime during growing season; best before flowering for highest allantoin content.

### Profile 6: Calendula (*Calendula officinalis*)

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Family | Asteraceae |
| Parts used | Flower heads (fully open) |
| Primary uses | Wound healing; skin inflammation; fungal infections; burns; eczema |
| Evidence level | Strong traditional use; clinical evidence for wound healing and anti-inflammatory effects |
| Safety profile | Excellent; rare allergy risk in Asteraceae-sensitive individuals |

**Traditional use:** Calendula's bright orange and yellow flowers have been used in European medicine since at least the 12th century — documented in Hildegard von Bingen's *Causae et Curae*. Also significant in Ayurvedic (as *zergul*) and Unani-Tibb traditions. Its name derives from "kalendae" (Roman calendar month beginnings) because it flowers continuously through the growing season in temperate climates.

**Active compounds:** Triterpenoid saponins (oleanolic acid glycosides) drive anti-inflammatory effects. Flavonoids (isorhamnetin, narcissin, quercetin) contribute antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activity. Carotenoids (primarily lutein and zeaxanthin) give flowers their color and contribute wound-healing properties. Polysaccharides stimulate immune activity at wound sites. Essential oils contribute antimicrobial and antifungal properties.

**Evidence:** A 2009 study in *Phytotherapy Research* demonstrated that calendula extract significantly accelerated wound closure and increased tissue tensile strength compared to controls. Clinical trials have shown efficacy for radiation dermatitis (superior to trolamine in one RCT), episiotomy wound healing, and diaper rash. The Commission E (Germany's official phytomedicine authority) approves calendula for topical treatment of wound healing and inflammatory conditions.

**Preparation and dosing:**

*Infused oil (for salve):*
- Dry flower heads thoroughly — residual moisture is the primary cause of oil rancidity in calendula preparations
- Fill jar with dried flowers; cover with olive oil
- Cold infuse 4–6 weeks; warm infuse 48–72 hours at 40–50°C
- Strain; the oil will be golden-orange in color
- Proceed to salve preparation (Section 1.4)

*Infusion (for washes, compresses):*
- 2 tablespoons dried flowers per cup of boiling water; steep 15 minutes
- Use as a wound wash, eye wash (well-strained, cooled), skin compress, or foot soak
- Drink 1–2 cups daily as an internal anti-inflammatory (traditional use for gastric inflammation)

*Salve formulation for wound care:*
- 1 cup calendula-infused oil + 1 oz beeswax + optionally 5–10 drops lavender essential oil
- Apply to minor wounds, burns, eczema, and cracked skin 2–4 times daily

**Contraindications:** Asteraceae allergy (ragweed, chrysanthemum, daisy) is a contraindication. Avoid during pregnancy (traditional use includes uterotonic applications; insufficient safety data). Otherwise an exceptionally safe topical herb.

**Growing notes:** Annual (or short-lived perennial in frost-free areas); one of the easiest and most rewarding herbs to grow from seed. Direct sow 2–3 weeks before last frost (tolerates light frost). Full sun, average soil, good drainage. Plants bloom prolifically from spring until frost — harvest flower heads when fully open every 2–3 days to maintain continuous blooming. The more you harvest, the more the plant produces. Dry harvested flowers in a single layer on screens immediately — fresh flowers with a high water content will mold if piled.

### Profile 7: Thyme (*Thymus vulgaris*)

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Family | Lamiaceae |
| Parts used | Aerial parts (leaves, stems, flowers) |
| Primary uses | Respiratory (cough, bronchitis); antimicrobial; digestive |
| Evidence level | Strong — Commission E approved; clinical trials for bronchitis |
| Safety profile | Excellent at culinary and medicinal doses; essential oil requires caution (see below) |

**Traditional use:** Thyme appears in ancient Egyptian embalming recipes, in classical Greek medicine (Hippocrates used it for pulmonary complaints), and throughout medieval European monastic herbalism. Its antimicrobial properties were partially understood long before germ theory — thyme bundles were used to prevent the spread of plague.

**Active compounds:** Thymol and carvacrol (phenolic volatile oils) are potent antimicrobials that disrupt bacterial cell membranes, demonstrated active against respiratory pathogens including *Streptococcus pneumoniae*, *Haemophilus influenzae*, and *Staphylococcus aureus* in laboratory studies. Rosmarinic acid provides anti-inflammatory activity. Flavonoids (luteolin, apigenin) contribute antispasmodic properties that explain thyme's antitussive (cough-suppressing) efficacy.

**Evidence:** The German Commission E approves dried thyme herb for treatment of symptoms of bronchitis and upper respiratory catarrh. Clinical trials of a standardized thyme-ivy syrup (Bronchipret) demonstrated equivalence to standard expectorant medications for acute bronchitis in multiple trials.

**Preparation and dosing:**

*Infusion (respiratory use):*
- **2 teaspoons fresh thyme** or **1 teaspoon dried** per cup of boiling water
- Steep **10–15 minutes** covered
- Add honey for enhanced antimicrobial effect and palatability
- Drink **3–4 cups daily** during acute respiratory illness
- Gargle with cooled infusion for sore throat and oral infections

*Thyme and honey syrup (preservation of respiratory preparation):*
1. Prepare strong infusion: 2 oz (56g) dried thyme per quart water; steep 30 minutes covered; strain
2. Return liquid to pot; simmer uncovered until volume reduces by half
3. Remove from heat; when cooled to below 40°C, stir in **equal volume raw honey** by weight
4. Bottle in clean glass jars; refrigerate
5. **Shelf life: 2–3 months refrigerated**
6. Dose: 1 tablespoon every 3–4 hours for cough; 1 teaspoon 3 times daily for preventive use

*Tincture:*
- 1:5 dried herb in 40% ABV
- Dose: **2–4 mL, 3–4 times daily** for respiratory complaints

**Important — essential oil vs. herb:** Thyme essential oil is highly concentrated and **must not be taken internally** at standard essential oil dilutions — it is hepatotoxic and mucosal-irritating at concentrations above trace. The herb and the essential oil are different preparations. Herb-based preparations (infusion, tincture, syrup) are safe at described doses.

**Contraindications:** None significant for herb preparations at therapeutic doses. Avoid essential oil internally. Avoid during pregnancy in amounts exceeding culinary use (thymol may stimulate uterine contractions at high doses). May interact with thyroid medications.

**Growing notes:** Perennial in Zones 5–9; marginally hardy in Zone 4 with mulching. Extremely drought-tolerant once established. Poor-to-average soil (overly fertile soil produces lush growth but lower essential oil concentration — nutrient stress concentrates volatile oils). Full sun is essential. Propagate by seed (slow), cuttings (fast), or division. Harvest stems before and during flowering for highest thymol content. Air dry or dehydrate at 35–40°C.

### Profile 8: Chamomile (*Matricaria chamomilla* / German Chamomile)

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Family | Asteraceae |
| Parts used | Flower heads |
| Primary uses | Digestive antispasmodic; sleep; anxiety; anti-inflammatory (topical) |
| Evidence level | Moderate clinical evidence; one of the most studied medicinal herbs globally |
| Safety profile | Excellent; very safe for all ages including children with appropriate dosing |

**Traditional use:** German chamomile has been used medicinally for at least 3,000 years — documented in ancient Egyptian, Greek, and Roman medical texts. In Germany, chamomile is sometimes called *alles zutraut* ("capable of anything") reflecting its broad traditional applications. Among the top five best-selling herbal supplements globally.

**Active compounds:** Apigenin (flavonoid) binds to benzodiazepine receptors in the CNS, providing mild anxiolytic and sedative effects without the side effects or dependence risk of pharmaceutical benzodiazepines. Alpha-bisabolol and chamazulene (formed from matricine during steam distillation) are potent anti-inflammatory compounds. Spiroether is antispasmodic. The striking blue color of distilled chamomile essential oil comes from chamazulene.

**Evidence:** A systematic review in *Journal of Clinical Pharmacy and Therapeutics* (2010) found significant evidence for chamomile's efficacy in generalized anxiety disorder, insomnia, GI spasm, and wound healing. Clinical trials demonstrated superior outcomes versus placebo for generalized anxiety disorder at 220–1100 mg standardized extract over 8 weeks.

**Preparation and dosing:**

*Infusion (standard):*
- **1–2 tablespoons dried flower heads** per cup of water at **90°C** (just below boiling)
- Steep **5–10 minutes** covered (longer steeping produces a more bitter preparation)
- Drink **1 cup 30 minutes before bed** for sleep support
- For digestive spasm or gas: drink after meals and before bed, up to **3 cups daily**
- For children (2–12): 1 teaspoon dried flowers per cup, steeped 5 minutes; consult provider for dose

*Topical:*
- Strong infusion (3 tablespoons per cup) applied as compress to eczema, minor wounds, eye inflammation (well-strained, cooled, sterile application only)
- Combine infused oil with calendula oil for wound/skin salves

*Tincture:*
- 1:5 in 40% ABV; dose **2–4 mL, 2–3 times daily**

**Contraindications:** Asteraceae allergy — anaphylaxis risk (rare but documented). Do not use chamomile tea eye washes if pollen allergy is present. Otherwise no significant contraindications. May mildly potentiate anticoagulants (apigenin inhibits platelet aggregation in vitro at high doses) — use normally at tea doses; monitor at tincture doses if on anticoagulant therapy.

**Growing notes:** Annual; self-seeds prolifically once established. Direct sow in fall or early spring on soil surface (needs light to germinate — do not cover seeds). Tolerates poor soil, moderate drought. In good conditions, can become weedy. Harvest flower heads when petals begin to reflex downward (just past peak bloom) — this is when volatile oil and flavonoid content is highest. Spread on screens and air dry immediately.

### Profile 9: Sage (*Salvia officinalis*)

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Family | Lamiaceae |
| Parts used | Leaves (fresh and dried) |
| Primary uses | Throat and oral infections; antiseptic gargle; menopausal hot flashes; cognitive support |
| Evidence level | Moderate; Commission E approved for throat; clinical evidence for menopausal symptoms |
| Safety profile | Safe at culinary and medicinal infusion doses; avoid high-dose isolated thujone (essential oil, extended high-dose tincture) |

**Traditional use:** Salvia from the Latin *salvare* ("to save" or "to heal") reflects the herb's central importance in Western botanical medicine. The Salerno medical school's 12th-century saying: *"Why should a man die in whose garden sage grows?"* Sage was considered a panacea in medieval European medicine. Used across cultures for throat and mouth infections, digestive problems, and women's health.

**Active compounds:** Thujone (a terpene ketone) and camphor are the primary volatile oil constituents with antimicrobial properties. Rosmarinic acid and carnosic acid are potent antioxidants and anti-inflammatory agents. Salvianolic acids contribute to the cognitive effects demonstrated in clinical studies (inhibit acetylcholinesterase, similar mechanism to Alzheimer's medications). The antimicrobial activity against *Streptococcus* species is particularly relevant for throat infections.

**Preparation and dosing:**

*Gargle for sore throat (primary medicinal use):*
- 2 tablespoons dried or fresh leaves per cup of boiling water
- Steep **15 minutes** covered; cool to comfortable temperature
- Gargle for **30 seconds** and spit (do not swallow large amounts)
- **Repeat every 2–3 hours** during active throat infection
- May add 1/4 teaspoon salt and 1/8 teaspoon apple cider vinegar for enhanced antimicrobial effect

*Infusion (internal use):*
- 1–2 teaspoons dried leaf per cup; steep 10 minutes
- Drink 2–3 cups daily for digestive support, mild menopausal symptom management
- Limit to **6 cups daily maximum** — excessive amounts of thujone accumulate

*Tincture:*
- 1:5 dried leaf in 50% ABV
- Dose: **2–3 mL, 2–3 times daily**; do not exceed this dose long-term due to thujone content

**Contraindications:** **Avoid during pregnancy** — thujone is potentially uterotonic in high doses (culinary use during cooking is universally considered safe; therapeutic preparations are not). Avoid in seizure disorders — thujone is a GABA antagonist and can lower seizure threshold at high doses. Safe for menopausal symptom management at standard infusion doses. Spanish sage (*Salvia lavandulifolia*) contains less thujone than common sage and may be preferable for extended therapeutic use.

**Growing notes:** Perennial in Zones 5–8; short-lived perennial, often replaced every 3–4 years as plants become woody and less productive. Full sun, well-drained soil (crown rot in wet conditions), drought-tolerant when established. Propagate by seed, cuttings, or layering. Harvest leaves freely from established plants throughout the growing season. Leaves can be harvested year-round in mild climates.

### Profile 10: Peppermint (*Mentha × piperita*)

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Family | Lamiaceae |
| Parts used | Leaves and aerial parts |
| Primary uses | Digestive; IBS (enteric-coated oil); headache (topical); respiratory decongestant |
| Evidence level | Strong — multiple systematic reviews; Commission E approved |
| Safety profile | Safe as infusion; enteric-coated capsules appropriate for IBS; essential oil caution |

**Traditional use:** Peppermint is a natural hybrid of watermint and spearmint, first cultivated in England around 1750. It rapidly became one of the most widely used medicinal and culinary herbs globally. Virtually all traditional medicine systems have incorporated some mint species for digestive complaints.

**Active compounds:** Menthol (the dominant volatile oil constituent at 35–55%) activates TRPM8 cold-sensing receptors, providing the characteristic cooling sensation and topical analgesic effect. Menthone and menthyl acetate are secondary volatile oils. Rosmarinic acid and flavonoids contribute anti-inflammatory properties. Enteric-coated peppermint oil relaxes smooth muscle of the gastrointestinal tract through calcium channel antagonism.

**Evidence:** Multiple meta-analyses demonstrate enteric-coated peppermint oil capsules are superior to placebo for irritable bowel syndrome (IBS), reducing abdominal pain and total symptom scores. Topical menthol solution (10% in ethanol) is equivalent to 1,000 mg acetaminophen for tension headache in one blinded RCT. Commission E approves peppermint leaf for GI spasm and flatulence.

**Preparation and dosing:**

*Infusion (digestive, calming):*
- **1 tablespoon fresh leaves** or **1–2 teaspoons dried** per cup at **85°C** (not boiling — protects volatile oils)
- Steep **5–10 minutes** covered
- Drink **1 cup after each meal** for digestive complaints; do not exceed 4 cups daily
- Do not give to infants — menthol can cause respiratory distress in very young children

*Topical headache application:*
- Strong infusion or dilute peppermint essential oil (1–2% in carrier oil — **2 drops per teaspoon of olive or coconut oil**)
- Apply to temples and forehead; avoid eye area
- The cooling sensation provides rapid headache relief in many individuals

*Decongestant steam:*
- 2 tablespoons dried leaves or 3–5 drops essential oil in a bowl of hot water
- Cover head with towel; inhale steam for 5–10 minutes
- Effective for nasal congestion, sinus pressure, and respiratory infections

*IBS use:* Enteric-coated peppermint oil capsules (187–225 mg) are the clinically studied form for IBS — these specifically release in the small intestine rather than the stomach. Standard infusion does not replicate this effect because menthol released in the stomach can cause heartburn.

**Contraindications:** Do not apply menthol (peppermint essential oil preparations) to the face or chest of infants — respiratory suppression risk. Avoid strong menthol preparations in individuals with G6PD deficiency. The tea is safe for children over 2; essential oil is not recommended under 6. Heartburn and GERD may be worsened by peppermint tea (relaxation of lower esophageal sphincter).

**Growing notes:** Extremely vigorous grower — invasive in garden beds if not contained. Plant in containers, or install a root barrier (12 inches deep plastic edging) before planting in-ground. Perennial in Zones 3–8. Spreads by underground rhizome. Tolerates partial shade (one of the few medicinal herbs that does). Harvest before flowering for highest menthol content. Air dry quickly to preserve volatile oils.

### Profile 11: St. John's Wort (*Hypericum perforatum*)

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Family | Hypericaceae |
| Parts used | Aerial parts in flower (buds, open flowers, and small leaves) |
| Primary uses | Mild-to-moderate depression; anxiety; nerve pain (topical); wound healing |
| Evidence level | Strong for mild-to-moderate depression; multiple meta-analyses |
| Safety profile | **Significant drug interactions** — see contraindications carefully |

**Traditional use:** Hypericum has been used in Western herbal medicine for at least 2,000 years — Dioscorides described it as a wound herb and treatment for "anxiety." The name derives from a tradition of harvesting on St. John's Day (June 24) when the plant is in peak flower. Traditional use for "melancholy" (depression) dates to medieval European medicine.

**Active compounds:** Hypericin and pseudohypericin (naphthodianthrone pigments, red in oil — the source of the red color of St. John's Wort oil) were originally thought to be the primary antidepressant compounds, but current research focuses more on hyperforin (acylphloroglucinol) as the primary active constituent — it inhibits the reuptake of serotonin, dopamine, norepinephrine, and GABA. Flavonoids (including amentoflavone, quercetin) contribute to overall activity.

**Evidence:** A 2008 Cochrane review of 29 trials (5,489 participants) found St. John's Wort extracts significantly superior to placebo for mild-to-moderate depression, with similar efficacy to standard antidepressants and fewer side effects. The EMA maintains an approved monograph for standardized extract preparations.

**Preparation and dosing:**

*Tincture (primary therapeutic preparation):*
- Fresh plant in flower, including buds — do not use dried plant for tincture, as hypericin and hyperforin degrade significantly during drying
- **1:2 ratio, fresh plant weight to 60% ABV menstruum**
- The extraction should turn a beautiful wine-red to ruby color from hypericin — this color indicates proper extraction
- Macerate 4–6 weeks; strain
- Dose: **2–4 mL, 3 times daily** — clinical trials typically use 300 mg standardized extract (0.3% hypericin) three times daily; most properly prepared tinctures approximate this
- **Therapeutic effect for depression takes 4–6 weeks** to develop — this is not a medication with immediate mood effects

*Infused oil (topical use):*
- Fresh flowering tops in olive oil using warm infusion method (40–50°C, 24–48 hours)
- Oil turns a deep red-wine color from hypericin when properly prepared
- Apply to nerve pain, bruises, muscle soreness, and minor wounds
- Traditional use for sciatica, post-herpetic neuralgia, and burns

*Infusion (mild traditional use):*
- 1–2 teaspoons dried herb per cup; steep 10 minutes
- Less potent than tincture; appropriate for mild anxiety or as a tonic

**Contraindications:**

**CRITICAL — Drug Interactions:** St. John's Wort is a potent inducer of CYP3A4 enzymes and P-glycoprotein, dramatically increasing the metabolism (and reducing the blood levels) of a wide range of medications. **Do not combine with:**
- Antiretrovirals (HIV medications) — can cause treatment failure
- Cyclosporine (organ transplant rejection) — documented organ rejection cases
- Oral contraceptives — documented contraceptive failures
- Warfarin (anticoagulant) — reduced anticoagulant effect
- Digoxin (cardiac medication)
- Certain chemotherapy agents
- Tricyclic antidepressants or SSRIs — serotonin syndrome risk
- Triptans (migraine medications)

**This is not an exhaustive list** — inform all providers if using St. John's Wort, and check drug interactions before combining with any prescription medication.

**Photosensitivity:** Hypericin is a photosensitizer. High doses can cause skin sensitivity to UV light, particularly in fair-skinned individuals. Limit sun exposure during therapeutic use.

**Growing notes:** Perennial in Zones 3–8; thrives in poor, dry, well-drained soil in full sun. Spreads aggressively and is considered an invasive weed in some western US states — check regulations before planting. Identify carefully — yellow 5-petal flowers with prominent stamens, leaves with translucent dots (oil glands) visible when held to light. Harvest in early to peak flowering (typically June–July); use fresh for tinctures and oil infusions.

### Profile 12: Elderberry (*Sambucus nigra*)

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Family | Adoxaceae |
| Parts used | Ripe berries (cooked); flowers |
| Primary uses | Immune support; upper respiratory infections; influenza |
| Evidence level | Moderate clinical evidence; systematic review data available |
| Safety profile | **Ripe berries must be cooked** — raw berries and unripe berries cause nausea/vomiting |

**Traditional use:** Elder has been called the "medicine chest of country folk" in European herbalism. Virtually every part of the plant — flowers, berries, inner bark, leaves — has been used medicinally across European and North American traditions. Elder flowers for fever reduction; berries for immune support and respiratory infections; bark for diuresis.

**Active compounds:** Anthocyanins (primarily cyanidin-3-glucoside and cyanidin-3-sambubioside) are the primary bioactive constituents of the berries, providing antioxidant and antiviral activity. These compounds interfere with viral hemagglutinin (the viral binding protein), inhibiting viral attachment to host cells. Flavonoids (quercetin, kaempferol) contribute to immune-modulating activity. Polysaccharides stimulate cytokine production.

**Evidence:** A 2016 randomized crossover study in *Nutrients* found elderberry extract reduced duration and severity of colds in air travelers. A 2019 meta-analysis found elderberry significantly reduced upper respiratory symptoms compared to placebo. NCCIH considers evidence "promising but not definitive" for influenza.

**Preparation and dosing:**

*Elderberry syrup (standard preparation):*
1. Combine **1 cup (100g) dried elderberries** (or 2 cups fresh ripe berries) with **3 cups water** in a saucepan
2. Add optional spices: 1 cinnamon stick, 3–4 whole cloves, 1 tablespoon fresh grated ginger
3. Bring to boil, reduce to simmer; cook **40–45 minutes** until liquid reduces by approximately half
4. Remove from heat; cool slightly; strain and press berries to extract all liquid
5. Allow to cool to below 40°C (critical — heat destroys honey's antimicrobial enzymes)
6. Stir in **1 cup raw honey** by volume (approximately 340g / 12 oz)
7. Bottle in clean glass jars; refrigerate
8. **Shelf life: 2–3 months refrigerated**

*Dosing:*
- Preventive: **1 tablespoon (15 mL) daily** for adults; **1 teaspoon daily** for children
- Acute illness: **1 tablespoon every 3–4 hours** for adults; **1 teaspoon 3–4 times daily** for children; continue for up to 5 days during illness

*Elder flower infusion (fever, early cold):*
- 2 teaspoons dried flowers per cup; steep 10 minutes
- Traditionally used as a diaphoretic (fever-promoting) tea to help break fevers
- Drink hot; encourages perspiration

**Safety — CRITICAL:** 
- **Raw elderberries and elderberry juice are mildly toxic** — they contain cyanogenic glycosides (sambunigrin) that cause nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea. Cooking destroys these compounds. **Always cook elderberries** before consumption in any preparation.
- Elder leaves, bark, and unripe berries are more toxic and should not be used in food/medicine preparations.
- Wild harvesting requires confident identification — many look-alike species have black or blue-black berries. *S. nigra* flowers are white, flat-topped (compound umbel), with a distinctive light floral scent; berries hang in clusters.

**Growing notes:** Large deciduous shrub or small tree (Zones 4–9); grows 8–12 feet tall and wide. Adaptable to most soil conditions; prefers moist, fertile soil. Plant at least two different varieties for cross-pollination and fruit set. Berries ripen in late summer–early fall; harvest when berries are fully dark purple-black and berries hang heavily in clusters. Remove berries from stems using a fork before cooking (stems are mildly toxic).

### Profile 13: Nettle (*Urtica dioica*)

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Family | Urticaceae |
| Parts used | Young leaves (spring harvest); root (autumn harvest for BPH use) |
| Primary uses | Nutritive tonic; iron-deficiency support; allergic rhinitis; joint support (leaf); benign prostatic hyperplasia (root) |
| Evidence level | Moderate for clinical applications; strong nutritional evidence |
| Safety profile | Excellent when properly prepared; fresh plant causes contact urticaria (wear gloves) |

**Traditional use:** Nettle is one of the most medicinally significant plants in Northern European and Indigenous North American herbalism. Its sting — the formic acid-loaded hollow trichomes on fresh leaves — was actually used therapeutically in a practice called urtication, deliberately stinging arthritic joints to reduce inflammation. More commonly, nettle leaves are dried or cooked (both methods neutralize the sting) for food and medicine.

**Active compounds:** Leaf: iron (3.8 mg/100g dry weight), calcium, magnesium, potassium, and vitamins A, C, K in unusually high concentrations for a green vegetable. Flavonoids (kaempferol, quercetin) contribute to antihistamine effects demonstrated in clinical studies for allergic rhinitis. Lectins (agglutinins) and polysaccharides have immune-modulating activity. Root: sterols and lectins interfere with sex hormone binding globulin, relevant to BPH therapy.

**Evidence:** A double-blind pilot study found freeze-dried nettle leaf moderately effective for allergic rhinitis. The American Botanical Council (HerbClip, 2018) notes substantial evidence for nettle leaf's nutritional value, particularly iron content in highly bioavailable form. German Commission E approves nettle root for BPH.

**Preparation and dosing:**

*Nourishing herbal infusion (primary nutritional use):*
- This is the preferred preparation for nettle's mineral and nutritive compounds
- **1 oz (28g) dried nettle leaf** per quart (950 mL) water
- Cold infuse 4–8 hours, or hot steep 4+ hours covered
- The extended steep time extracts substantially more minerals than a standard 10-minute tea
- Drink **1–4 cups daily** as a nutritional tonic
- Color: deep forest green; taste: grassy, rich — many describe it as pleasant with honey

*Standard infusion (quicker preparation):*
- 2 tablespoons dried leaf per cup; steep 15 minutes
- Drink 3 cups daily for allergic rhinitis support (begin 4–6 weeks before allergy season)

*Food preparation (spring greens):*
- Young spring nettles (before flowering) are among the most nutritious wild greens available — comparable in iron and vitamin content to spinach
- Blanch in boiling water 1–2 minutes; this neutralizes the sting completely
- Use as a cooked green, in soups, pesto, or grain dishes

**Harvesting safety:** Always wear gloves when handling fresh nettles. Grab stem firmly and harvest with downward strokes — the hollow trichomes on the leaf surface inject formic acid on skin contact. Once dried or cooked, nettles lose all stinging ability. For sustainable wild harvesting, see Section 3.

**Contraindications:** May interact with blood pressure medications (diuretic effect at high doses). Avoid root preparations during pregnancy; leaf preparations are generally considered safe in normal dietary amounts during pregnancy. Monitor blood glucose if diabetic and on medications.

**Growing notes:** Perennial in Zones 3–10; spreads aggressively by rhizome. Best planted where spread is acceptable (ditch edges, woodland margins, designated patch with barriers). Prefers moist, nitrogen-rich soil — it naturally colonizes areas with high organic matter. No cultivation usually needed — wild stands are abundant in most temperate regions. Harvest spring growth before flowering (highest nutritional and medicinal content); use gloves throughout.

### Profile 14: Garlic (*Allium sativum*)

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Family | Amaryllidaceae |
| Parts used | Bulb (cloves), fresh and fermented |
| Primary uses | Immune support; antimicrobial; cardiovascular (cholesterol, blood pressure); respiratory infections |
| Evidence level | Strong for cardiovascular effects; moderate for antimicrobial; clinical data varies by preparation |
| Safety profile | Excellent at culinary doses; higher medicinal doses may affect bleeding and medication metabolism |

**Traditional use:** Garlic appears in virtually every major world food and medicine tradition — Egyptian papyri (Ebers Papyrus, 1500 BCE) list 22 garlic preparations for heart problems, headaches, parasites, and tumors; Hippocrates prescribed it for pulmonary complaints; it is a cornerstone of Ayurvedic, TCM, and folk medicine across Asia, Europe, the Americas, and Africa.

**Active compounds:** **Allicin** (allyl 2-propenethiosulfinate) is the primary bioactive compound, formed enzymatically from alliin when garlic tissue is crushed or chopped — the enzyme alliinase (stored separately from alliin in intact cells) catalyzes the conversion. **This is why crushing or chopping garlic and allowing it to rest 10 minutes before cooking or consuming dramatically increases allicin content** — immediate heat inactivates alliinase before conversion is complete. Allicin is highly volatile and short-lived; downstream products including ajoene and diallyl sulfide are more stable and contribute to cardiovascular and immune effects. S-allylcysteine (SAC) is the primary stable compound in aged garlic extract preparations.

**Evidence:** A 2016 Cochrane review found garlic may reduce total cholesterol and LDL-cholesterol at consistent therapeutic doses. Multiple studies document reduction in frequency and severity of common colds with regular garlic consumption. NIH Office of Dietary Supplements (2023) notes evidence for blood pressure-lowering effects is promising but mixed.

**Preparation and dosing:**

*Standard therapeutic use (raw garlic):*
- **2–4 cloves raw garlic daily** — crush or mince, allow 10 minutes for allicin formation, then consume
- May be mixed into food after the 10-minute rest period; some allicin activity is preserved even after brief cooking exposure following the resting period
- Effective for immune support, respiratory infections, and cardiovascular maintenance

*Garlic honey (antimicrobial preservation):*
1. Peel and slightly crush garlic cloves (to initiate allicin formation); allow 10 minutes
2. Place cloves in a clean glass jar; cover completely with raw honey
3. Allow to ferment at room temperature 1–4 weeks — natural wild yeasts in honey will ferment the garlic
4. The fermented product develops a mellow, complex flavor and is less pungent than raw garlic
5. **Shelf life: 1 year or more** at room temperature; the honey acts as preservative
6. Dose: 1–3 cloves daily

*Oxymel (garlic in vinegar + honey):*
- An ancient preservation and preparation method: fill jar with crushed garlic; cover with **equal parts apple cider vinegar and honey**
- Macerate 2–4 weeks; strain
- Dose: 1 tablespoon (15 mL) 1–3 times daily
- Shelf life: **6–12 months**

*Fire cider (traditional herbal oxymel):*
Traditional combination of garlic, ginger, horseradish, onion, hot peppers, and herbs in apple cider vinegar + honey — a warming, circulatory, and immune-supporting preparation with documented antimicrobial activity from multiple constituents.

**Contraindications:** **At high therapeutic doses, garlic has antiplatelet and mild anticoagulant effects — avoid combining with warfarin or antiplatelet medications without provider supervision.** May interact with saquinavir (HIV medication — clinical reports of reduced drug levels). May enhance hypoglycemic effects in diabetics on medications. Allergy is possible (rare, typically manifests as contact dermatitis). Avoid concentrated garlic supplements before surgery (stop 2 weeks prior; inform surgeon).

**Growing notes:** Annual (hardneck varieties) or biennial; plant cloves in fall (2–4 weeks before first hard frost) for late-spring/early-summer harvest. Good drainage is essential — garlic does not tolerate wet roots. Plant cloves pointed end up, 2–3 inches deep, 6 inches apart, in rows 12 inches apart. Harvest when lower 3–4 leaves have browned (typically late June–July depending on climate). Cure in a warm, dry, well-ventilated area for 3–4 weeks before storage. Properly cured garlic stores **6–12 months** at room temperature.

## Section 3: Growing and Harvesting Medicinal Plants

### 3.1 Propagation Methods

Medicinal herbs can be established from seed, division, cuttings, or layering depending on species. Understanding the appropriate propagation method for each herb minimizes establishment time and cost.

**From seed:** Most annual herbs (chamomile, calendula, cilantro) and some perennials (echinacea, valerian, motherwort) grow readily from seed. Key principles: (1) Annual herbs can be direct-seeded; perennials often benefit from indoor starting; (2) Some seeds require **cold stratification** — a period of moist cold to break dormancy (echinacea, elderberry, St. John's Wort all benefit from this; store seeds in moist paper towel in refrigerator for 4–6 weeks before spring planting). (3) Follow light requirements — some seeds require light to germinate (chamomile — scatter on soil surface); others require darkness (calendula — cover seeds). Johnny's Selected Seeds and Strictly Medicinal Seeds provide germination requirements for each species.

**Division:** Clump-forming perennials (lemon balm, echinacea, comfrey, valerian) can be divided in early spring or fall. Dig the entire plant, separate root mass into sections with a spade or knife, and replant divisions. This is the fastest and most reliable way to establish new plants of perennial herbs and does not require seed purchasing.

**Root and stem cuttings:** Many Lamiaceae (mint, thyme, sage, rosemary) root readily from stem cuttings taken in late spring or early summer. Take 4–6 inch cuttings from non-flowering stems; remove lower leaves; dip cut end in rooting hormone (or honey); insert in moist, well-drained rooting medium (perlite/vermiculite mix). Keep humid and in indirect light; roots form in 2–6 weeks. This is particularly valuable for mints, which cross-pollinate readily and are best maintained as vegetative clones.

**Layering:** Perennial shrubs with long branches (rosemary, sage, elder) can be propagated by layering — bending a branch to the ground, covering a section with soil while the tip remains free, and waiting for roots to form at the buried section. Sever from parent plant once rooted. No specialized equipment required.

### 3.2 Seasonal Harvest Timing

Medicinal compound concentrations in plant tissue are not constant — they peak at specific stages of growth, and harvesting at the right time is the difference between medicine and inert plant material.

| Plant Part | Peak Harvest Time | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Leaves (aromatic) | Just before flowering | Volatile oil concentration peaks as flowering energy is redirected |
| Flowers | At first open bloom / early flowering | Potency drops as flowers age and seed development begins |
| Roots | Fall (after aerial die-back) or early spring | Starches converted to active compounds in fall; spring roots lose stored energy to new growth |
| Bark | Late spring (sap flowing) | Easier to peel; compounds concentrated |
| Seeds/berries | When fully ripe | Unripe seeds may contain antinutrients; alkaloids may be higher in unripe state |
| Spring greens (nettle) | Before flowering | Young leaves have highest mineral content; older leaves develop cystoliths (calcium carbonate crystals) that can irritate kidneys |

**Time of day:** Harvest aromatic herbs in the **mid-morning after dew has dried but before peak afternoon heat** — this window captures highest volatile oil content. Roots can be harvested at any time of day.

**Harvest frequency:** For productive perennials like lemon balm, mint, and thyme, cutting back to 50% of growth every 3–4 weeks encourages bushy regrowth and provides multiple harvests per season. Never harvest more than 1/3 of a perennial plant in a single harvest during the first year of establishment.

### 3.3 Drying and Processing Workflow

The goal of drying is to remove water content quickly enough to prevent mold and microbial growth, while preserving volatile oils and other active compounds. **Speed and air circulation matter more than temperature.**

1. **Harvest at the right time** — damp or wet material must be surface-dried before bundling; never bundle wet herbs
2. **Process immediately** — begin drying within hours of harvest
3. **Remove damaged, yellowed, or insect-damaged material** before drying
4. **Avoid thick bundles** — air cannot circulate through tightly packed bundles; use small, loose bundles or single-layer screens
5. **Label immediately** — at minimum, herb name, date, and source location
6. **Test for dryness** — leaves should crumble between fingers; stems should snap; roots should be hard throughout (not leathery in the center)
7. **Store in airtight glass jars in a cool, dark location** — jar the herbs promptly after drying; humidity reabsorption begins immediately in moist climates

See Section 1.5 for complete drying method comparison (air drying, dehydrator, oven, solar).

### 3.4 Storage Conditions

Medicinal herb quality degrades through four primary mechanisms: oxidation, UV light exposure, moisture reabsorption, and heat. Proper storage addresses all four.

- **Airtight containers:** Glass Mason jars with sealed lids are ideal. Avoid plastic bags (permit moisture exchange) and paper bags (both moisture and light penetration) for long-term storage.
- **Dark location:** UV light degrades flavonoids, volatile oils, and many other active compounds. A closed cabinet, pantry, or drawer is sufficient.
- **Cool temperature:** Consistent temperature below 21°C (70°F) is ideal. Avoid above refrigerators, above stoves, or near windows.
- **Humidity control:** Below 60% relative humidity. In humid climates (Southeast US, tropical areas), include food-grade silica gel desiccant packets in storage containers and replace when saturated.
- **Labeling:** Include herb name, plant part, date dried, source, and preparation notes. Memory is less reliable than labels.

### 3.5 Sustainable Wild Harvesting Protocols

Wild harvesting medicinal plants — also called wildcrafting — is an ancient practice that connects people to local plant communities and provides access to species not easily cultivated. It requires knowledge and restraint to practice without depleting wild populations.

**Core principles:**

1. **Identification before harvest.** Never harvest a plant you have not positively identified. Use multiple field guides cross-referenced with regional botanical resources. When uncertain, do not harvest. The United Plant Savers (UPS) At-Risk Species list (2022) documents medicinal plants under collection pressure — check this list before harvesting any plant.

2. **The one-third rule.** Never harvest more than 1/3 of any individual plant, and no more than 1/3 of plants in a given stand. This leaves sufficient plant material for self-reproduction and for wildlife that depends on the plant.

3. **Avoid harvesting from depleted populations.** If a stand is sparse, small, or shows signs of previous over-harvesting, leave it entirely and find another stand.

4. **At-risk species.** The following commonly sought medicinal plants are on the United Plant Savers At-Risk list and should be cultivated rather than wildcrafted: American ginseng (*Panax quinquefolius*), bloodroot (*Sanguinaria canadensis*), goldenseal (*Hydrastis canadensis*), black cohosh (*Actaea racemosa*), slippery elm (*Ulmus rubra*), echinacea (*E. angustifolia* wild populations). If you use these herbs, purchase from cultivated sources or grow your own.

5. **Avoid contaminated areas.** Do not harvest within 100 feet of roads (vehicle exhaust and road salt contamination), within 500 feet of agricultural fields using pesticides or synthetic fertilizers, in areas with known heavy metal contamination (former industrial sites, downstream from mining operations), or in public parks where herbicides may be used.

6. **Leave no trace.** Do not trample surrounding vegetation, compact soil, or disturb habitat more than necessary for harvest.

7. **Cross-links:** See the WAVE_1_COMPOSTING guide for practices to build soil quality that supports medicinal garden establishment and reduces reliance on wild harvesting. See WAVE_2_DOMAIN_3 (sanitation) for wound care protocols that integrate herbal preparations with hygiene practices.
