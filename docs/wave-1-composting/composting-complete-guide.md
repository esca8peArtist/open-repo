# Composting & Soil Amendment: Complete Practical Guide

## Overview & Purpose

Composting converts organic waste — kitchen scraps, yard trimmings, cardboard — into a soil amendment that improves plant growth, water retention, and soil structure. It is one of the highest-leverage things a household or small farm can do: it eliminates a disposal problem and produces a resource worth $20–80 per cubic yard if purchased.

**What this guide covers**:
1. The biology of composting and why it matters for method selection
2. Hot composting — the fastest method; produces finished compost in 4–8 weeks
3. Cold composting — the easiest method; requires almost no effort; takes 6–18 months
4. Vermicomposting — indoor-capable; uses worms; produces the highest-quality amendment
5. Using finished compost as a soil amendment and mulch
6. Troubleshooting the 12 most common problems

**Who should read this**: Gardeners, homesteaders, small farmers, and urban households looking to reduce waste and improve soil fertility. No prior composting experience required.

**What you'll need**: At minimum, a pile of organic material and some outdoor space. Most methods require no purchased equipment. Vermicomposting requires a container and worms.

## Section 1: The Biology of Composting — What Is Actually Happening

Understanding the biology of decomposition is not academic — it directly determines which method to choose and why problems occur.

### 1.1 Who Does the Work

Composting is performed by microorganisms: primarily bacteria and fungi. These organisms consume organic carbon and nitrogen in the pile, producing heat, carbon dioxide, and water as byproducts. What is left after their work is finished is stable humus — dark, crumbly organic matter that does not break down further under normal soil conditions.

**Bacteria** are the primary decomposers. They are present on all organic matter and require no introduction. There are three temperature-based groups:

- **Psychrophilic bacteria** (cold-tolerant): Active at 0–15°C (32–60°F). These start the process even in winter, but work slowly.
- **Mesophilic bacteria** (moderate temperature): Active at 15–40°C (60–104°F). These are the primary workforce in cold composting. Fastest at 35–40°C.
- **Thermophilic bacteria** (heat-loving): Active at 40–70°C (104–158°F). These produce the high temperatures of hot composting and are responsible for killing pathogens and weed seeds.

**Fungi** are secondary decomposers. They break down woody, lignin-rich materials (wood chips, straw, cardboard) that bacteria cannot easily access. A white or grayish-white mat in a compost pile is fungal mycelium — this is healthy and indicates active decomposition.

**Larger organisms** (earthworms, pill bugs, millipedes, beetles) appear in cold or mature piles and further fragment material. They are beneficial but not required; they will arrive naturally if conditions are right.

### 1.2 What Microorganisms Need

Microorganisms need four things. The method you choose determines how much you manage each:

1. **Carbon** — from "brown" materials: dried leaves, straw, wood chips, cardboard, paper. Carbon provides energy.
2. **Nitrogen** — from "green" materials: fresh grass clippings, food scraps, coffee grounds, fresh plant trimmings, animal manures. Nitrogen supports growth and reproduction.
3. **Water** — the pile should be as moist as a wrung-out sponge. Too dry: decomposition stops. Too wet: anaerobic conditions develop, producing foul odors.
4. **Oxygen** — for aerobic (non-smelly) decomposition. Turning the pile or building it with bulky materials provides oxygen.

### 1.3 The Carbon-to-Nitrogen Ratio (C:N Ratio)

The C:N ratio is the single most important variable in composting. The ideal range for fast, hot composting is **25:1 to 30:1** — 25 to 30 parts carbon for every 1 part nitrogen.

**Why this ratio matters**:
- Below 20:1 (too much nitrogen): Excess nitrogen escapes as ammonia gas — the pile smells like urine or fertilizer; nitrogen is wasted.
- Above 40:1 (too much carbon): Microorganisms run out of nitrogen; decomposition slows dramatically; pile may not heat up at all.
- 25–30:1: Maximum microbial activity; pile heats rapidly; decomposition is fast.

**C:N ratios of common materials** (approximate values; ratios vary with material age and condition):

| Material | C:N Ratio | Category |
|----------|-----------|----------|
| Autumn leaves (dried) | 40–80:1 | Brown/Carbon |
| Straw (wheat, oat, rice) | 40–100:1 | Brown/Carbon |
| Wood chips (fresh) | 100–500:1 | Brown/Carbon |
| Sawdust | 200–500:1 | Brown/Carbon |
| Cardboard (corrugated, uncoated) | 350–560:1 | Brown/Carbon |
| Newspaper | 560:1 | Brown/Carbon |
| Corn stalks | 60–75:1 | Brown/Carbon |
| Fresh grass clippings | 15–25:1 | Green/Nitrogen |
| Vegetable food scraps | 12–20:1 | Green/Nitrogen |
| Coffee grounds | 20:1 | Green/Nitrogen |
| Cow manure (aged) | 20:1 | Green/Nitrogen |
| Horse manure | 25–30:1 | Brown-Green |
| Chicken manure | 5–10:1 | Green/Nitrogen |
| Fresh clover or legume greens | 12–16:1 | Green/Nitrogen |
| Tea leaves (used) | 4:1 | Green/Nitrogen |
| Fruit scraps (citrus, peels) | 35:1 | Neutral |

**Practical application — the 2:1 rule**: For most household composting, mixing browns and greens at a 2:1 ratio by volume (not weight) produces a pile in the 25–35:1 range. This is close enough. You do not need to calculate exact ratios; use this rule and adjust based on whether the pile heats up (good) or smells like ammonia (add more brown) or won't heat (add more green).

### 1.4 What Composting Does NOT Do (Realistic Expectations)

- Composting does **not** eliminate all pathogens. Cold composting rarely reaches pathogen-kill temperatures. Hot composting does — if managed correctly. See Section 2.4.
- Composting does **not** make weed seeds inactive. Cold composting almost never kills weed seeds. Hot composting does — if the temperature is maintained. See Section 2.4.
- Composting does **not** eliminate heavy metals or PFAS chemical contamination. These pass through into finished compost unchanged. Do not compost materials contaminated with heavy metals (certain industrial wastes, treated railroad ties, arsenate-treated wood) or PFAS-containing materials (some sewage sludge biosolids from municipal sources near industrial sites). This is rare for household composting but worth knowing.
- Finished compost is **not a complete fertilizer**. It improves soil structure and provides moderate nutrients, but heavy-yielding crops (corn, tomatoes) may need additional nitrogen fertilizer even with regular compost applications. See Section 5.

## Section 2: Hot Composting — Fast, Active, High-Quality

Hot composting is the method of choice when you want finished compost in 4–8 weeks, need to kill weed seeds and pathogens, and are willing to invest some labor.

### 2.1 What Hot Composting Requires

For a pile to reach thermophilic temperatures (55–70°C / 131–158°F):

1. **Minimum pile volume**: 1 cubic yard (3 feet × 3 feet × 3 feet). Smaller piles do not retain enough heat to thermophile. Larger piles (up to 5 feet in each dimension) work well; above 5 feet, the center may become anaerobic.
2. **Correct C:N ratio**: 25–30:1. Use the 2:1 browns-to-greens rule.
3. **Correct moisture**: 40–60% — wet as a wrung-out sponge. Dry to the touch means it needs water. Water dripping when squeezed hard means too wet.
4. **Adequate oxygen**: The pile should be turned every 3–7 days for maximum speed; at minimum once per week.

**Tools needed**:
- Pitchfork or compost fork (most important tool)
- Compost thermometer ($20–40) — optional but strongly recommended for pathogen kill verification
- Bins or open pile space (detailed below)

### 2.2 Building the Hot Compost Pile — Step by Step

**Step 1: Gather and prepare materials**

Collect roughly equal volumes of dry brown materials and half as much fresh green material. Chop or shred everything as finely as practical — smaller pieces decompose faster because they expose more surface area to microorganisms. A piece of cardboard 6 inches square takes months; shredded, it takes weeks.

**Ideal starting materials for a backyard pile**:
- Autumn leaves (brown) — 2–4 garbage bags
- Fresh grass clippings or kitchen vegetable scraps (green) — 1–2 garbage bags
- Any woody prunings, chopped to 2–4 inch pieces (brown)
- Coffee grounds (green), mixed throughout

**Optional accelerators**: Adding a shovelful of finished compost or garden soil introduces beneficial microbes. This is helpful but not required — organic matter already carries the organisms needed.

**Step 2: Prepare your bin location**

Hot composting works in open piles, wooden bins, wire mesh bins, or commercial plastic bins. The minimum is a 1 cubic yard pile on bare soil or a concrete pad. Contact with bare soil (not pavement) allows earthworms and other soil organisms to migrate in as the pile matures.

**Bin types and trade-offs**:

| Bin Type | Cost | Pros | Cons |
|----------|------|------|------|
| Open pile | $0 | No cost; easy access for turning | Less attractive; may spread in wind or rain |
| Wire mesh cylinder (hardware cloth) | $20–40 | Easy to build; good aeration | Needs stakes; not contained |
| 3-bin wooden system (pallets) | $0–$50 | Best for continuous production; efficient turning | More space; labor to build |
| Plastic tumbler | $80–$200 | Easy turning; contained; pest-resistant | Small volume; less aeration; struggles in cold |
| Commercial black bin (single unit) | $30–$80 | Contained; municipal-program subsidized in many areas | Too small for hot composting; better suited to cold method |

**Step 3: Build the pile in layers (or mix everything at once)**

Two approaches — both work:

**Layered approach** (better for organizing mixed materials):
- Layer 4–6 inches of brown material (leaves, straw, wood chips)
- Layer 2–3 inches of green material (scraps, clippings)
- Repeat until pile is 3 feet tall
- Moisten each layer if the brown material is dry
- Top with a thin layer of soil or finished compost (optional; speeds startup)

**Mixed approach** (faster setup, common for practitioners):
- Combine all materials in a wheelbarrow or on the ground, mix roughly, pile together. Works just as well once turning begins.

**Step 4: Monitor and turn**

Check the pile temperature in the center 3 days after building. A compost thermometer inserted 12 inches into the center should show 55–65°C (131–149°F) within 2–4 days if materials and moisture are correct.

**Turning schedule for fastest compost (4–6 weeks)**:
- Turn every 3–4 days (every time temperature drops from 60°C peak toward 45°C)
- Each turn moves the outer cooler material into the center
- Pile should reheat to 55–65°C within 1–2 days of turning

**Turning schedule for moderate speed (6–12 weeks)**:
- Turn once per week
- Less labor; acceptable for most situations

**What to check when turning**:
- Moisture: If dry (no steam or clumping), add water while turning. Pour 1–3 gallons and remix.
- Structure: If soggy and dense (smells like rotten eggs), add dry brown material and fluff.
- Color: Darkening throughout is good. Sections still showing original material need more turning.

### 2.3 How to Know When Hot Composting Is Active

**Signs of active hot composting**:
- Center temperature 55–65°C (131–149°F): measured by thermometer
- Steam rising from pile when turned: visible thermophilic activity
- Volume decreasing: pile shrinks by 50–70% as decomposition proceeds
- Material darkening and losing recognizable form
- Earthy, mushroom-like smell (not rotten egg, not ammonia)

**Signs something is wrong**:
- No heating after 5 days: pile is too dry, too small, or C:N ratio is off. Add water, nitrogen, or more volume.
- Ammonia smell: too much nitrogen. Add 2–3 times as much brown material; mix thoroughly.
- Rotten egg or sulfur smell: too wet and anaerobic. Turn pile; add dry brown material.
- Temperature over 70°C (158°F): too hot; some microorganisms die; slows process. Turn to cool and aerate.

### 2.4 Pathogen and Weed Seed Destruction

Hot composting reliably kills pathogens and weed seeds — but only if these temperature and time requirements are met:

**Required conditions** (based on US Composting Council and EPA standards):
- Temperature: 55°C (131°F) or higher
- Duration: Maintained for at least **3 consecutive days** for in-vessel composting; **5 consecutive days** for turned windrow composting at household scale, the pile must reach and hold 55°C+ in all parts of the pile (not just the center) to kill organisms throughout

**How to ensure all material is treated**:
1. Turn the pile so that outer material rotates through the hot center at least 3 times.
2. Use a thermometer to verify that the outer portions reach temperature, not just the center.
3. After 3 full turn cycles with documented temperature, the material should be pathogen-safe.

**What this kills**: E. coli, Salmonella, most common plant diseases, most weed seeds (those with hard seed coats — morning glory, bindweed — may survive even at 55°C+ in some cases).

**What this does NOT kill**: Some heat-resistant fungal spores may survive. Do not compost diseased club root (Plasmodiophora brassicae) or late blight (Phytophthora infestans) infected material unless your pile consistently reaches 65°C+ for multiple days. When in doubt, exclude diseased plants from the compost.

**Animal manures**: Composting at 55°C+ for the required duration makes manure-derived compost safe for vegetable garden application. Raw (unComposted) manure from chickens, pigs, and cattle should not be applied to vegetables within 90 days of harvest for food-safety reasons (FDA Food Safety Modernization Act guidelines). Thermophilic composting eliminates this waiting period.

### 2.5 What Hot Composting Produces

Finished hot compost characteristics:
- Dark brown to black, crumbly texture
- Earthy smell (like forest floor)
- Original materials are no longer recognizable
- Temperature within 10°F of ambient (pile is no longer heating)
- Passes the "radish germination test": if radish seeds germinate and grow normally in the finished compost, it is mature; if seedlings die, the compost needs more curing

**Curing**: After active heating is complete, leave the pile undisturbed for 2–4 weeks. This curing phase allows remaining compounds that can be phytotoxic (plant-inhibiting) to stabilize. Compost applied before curing is complete can temporarily suppress plant growth.

**Approximate timeline**:
- Active turning (every 3–4 days): 4–6 weeks
- Active turning (every 7 days): 6–10 weeks
- Curing after active phase: 2–4 weeks
- **Total minimum to usable product**: 6–14 weeks

## Section 3: Cold Composting — Low Labor, Long Timeline

Cold composting (also called passive composting) requires almost no active management. The pile is built and left. Decomposition proceeds slowly, driven by mesophilic bacteria and fungi, typically taking 6–18 months.

### 3.1 When Cold Composting is the Right Choice

Choose cold composting when:
- You have time and no urgency for finished compost
- You prefer minimal labor
- You are composting only yard waste (leaves, grass, prunings) without food scraps
- You have limited pile-building capacity (adding material gradually over months)

**Limitations of cold composting**:
- Does NOT reliably kill weed seeds or pathogens
- Takes much longer (6–18 months vs. 4–12 weeks)
- Produces compost of slightly lower quality (less processed, more variable)
- Food scraps, if added to open cold piles, can attract rodents and flies (mitigated by burial, covered bin, or exclusion)

### 3.2 Cold Composting Setup

**Simple pile method**: Add materials to a pile as they become available. No special structure needed. Over 6–12 months, the bottom of the pile decomposes while you continue adding to the top. Harvest finished compost from the bottom.

**Enclosed bin method**: A single plastic compost bin (the black "Darth Vader helmet" style available from most municipalities, often subsidized) is the standard cold composting setup. Add kitchen scraps and yard waste, close the lid, wait. Turn occasionally if desired to speed decomposition; ignore if not. Compost accumulates at the bottom and can be scooped out with a hand trowel.

**Cold composting with a bin — step by step**:

1. Site the bin in a convenient, level spot (near the kitchen door or garden)
2. Add materials as they become available — no layering required, but mixing carbon and nitrogen materials helps
3. Bury food scraps in the center if using an open-bottom bin to deter rodents
4. Check moisture every 2–4 weeks (especially in summer); add water if dry
5. Turn the pile every 2–4 weeks if you want faster results; ignore if you prefer minimum labor
6. After 6–12 months, check the bottom of the bin for finished dark material; scoop out as needed

### 3.3 What to Compost — and What to Exclude

**Safe to cold compost**:
- Fruit and vegetable scraps (cooked or raw)
- Coffee grounds and paper filters
- Tea bags (check for plastic mesh; if present, remove paper from mesh)
- Eggshells (add calcium; slow to decompose)
- Yard trimmings: leaves, grass clippings, spent garden plants
- Paper (uncoated): newspaper, paper towel rolls, egg cartons, unbleached cardboard
- Wood chips, bark, sawdust (add to slow cold pile in moderation)
- Hair (human or animal), cotton rope, natural fibers

**Exclude from cold composting** (all of these can be hot-composted if pile management is rigorous):
- Meat, fish, or poultry (raw or cooked) — attracts rodents and flies
- Dairy products (milk, cheese, butter) — same issue
- Fats, oils, grease — repel water; create hydrophobic zones
- Dog and cat feces — may contain Toxoplasma and other pathogens not killed at cold-pile temperatures
- Diseased plants (club root, late blight, crown gall) — pathogens survive and reinfect garden
- Weeds with seed heads (e.g., dandelion in seed, annual grasses in seed) — unless you hot compost
- Invasive plants that can propagate from cuttings (e.g., bishop's weed, Japanese knotweed) — even fragments may survive and root in garden
- Treated or painted wood — may contain chromated copper arsenate (CCA) or other preservatives
- Glossy or coated paper (food packaging, magazines with clay coating) — decomposes poorly; not a safety issue but adds no value

**A note on citrus peels**: Citrus peels are safe to compost. The claim that citrus kills beneficial organisms or repels worms is not well-supported. In large quantities, the acidity may slightly slow decomposition at the decomposition site, but this is minor and irrelevant in any reasonably sized pile.

### 3.4 Improving Cold Compost Results

Simple practices that speed cold composting without converting to full hot composting:

1. **Chop materials**: Cut kitchen scraps into 1–2 inch pieces. Tear up cardboard. Shred leaves with a lawn mower. Smaller particle size is the single highest-leverage improvement.
2. **Turn once a month**: Even one turn per month substantially improves decomposition speed.
3. **Balance C:N**: Even in cold composting, avoid piling all grass clippings (too wet, anaerobic) or all leaves (too dry, slow). Mix as you add.
4. **Maintain moisture**: A pile that dries out in summer stops decomposing. Check occasionally and add water if the material is dry to the touch in the center.
5. **Add activators**: A shovelful of finished compost, garden soil, or aged manure adds microbial diversity and speeds startup.

## Section 4: Vermicomposting — Worm Bins Indoors or Out

Vermicomposting uses composting worms — not garden earthworms — to process organic matter into worm castings, one of the most nutrient-dense soil amendments available. A well-run worm bin can be kept indoors, processes kitchen scraps year-round, and produces finished castings in 1–4 months.

### 4.1 The Right Worm: Red Wigglers vs. Earthworms

**Red wigglers** (*Eisenia fetida*, also called redworms or manure worms) are the correct species for worm bins. They are NOT the large pinkish-gray earthworms found in garden soil.

Key characteristics:
- Live and feed at the surface layer (top 6–8 inches), not burrowing deep
- Thrive in dense populations in bins
- Process organic matter 2–3× faster than soil earthworms per unit weight
- Tolerate a wide range of organic materials

**Garden earthworms** (*Lumbricus terrestris* and related species) do NOT work in bins. They require deep soil, cannot tolerate the dense conditions of a bin, and will die. Do not substitute earthworms for red wigglers.

**Where to obtain red wigglers**: Online suppliers (search "red wigglers composting worms"), local garden centers, community composting exchanges. One pound of red wigglers (approximately 1,000 worms) is the standard starting quantity for a household bin.

### 4.2 Bin Sizing

**Key rule**: Worms need 1 square foot of surface area per pound of food scraps processed per week.

**Household sizing calculation**:
- Average household of 4 produces approximately 1 lb of compostable kitchen scraps per day = ~5–7 lbs per week
- At 1 sq ft per lb/week, you need 5–7 sq ft of bin surface area
- A 2 lb worm population can process approximately 2–4 lbs of scraps per week under normal conditions
- **Standard starter recommendation**: 2 lbs of worms in a bin with at least 4 sq ft surface area (approximately 24 inches × 24 inches, or a standard 18-gallon storage tote)

**Bin depth**: 8–12 inches is optimal. Too shallow dries out. Too deep creates anaerobic zones at the bottom.

**Volume**: A typical household worm bin holds 10–20 gallons. Commercial flow-through systems (stacked trays) hold more and allow easier harvesting.

**Common DIY bins**:
- 14–18 gallon plastic storage tote: Drill 1/8-inch holes on sides (every 2 inches) and bottom (for drainage). Add a second tote underneath to catch leachate (worm tea). Cost: $5–15.
- Wooden bin (1-inch cedar or untreated wood, 24" × 16" × 10"): Lasts longer; better insulation; no drainage concerns (absorbent). Cost: $20–40 materials.

### 4.3 Bedding Preparation

Worms live in bedding, not in food scraps. Bedding provides moisture, aeration, and carbon. The food scraps are buried in the bedding.

**Best bedding materials**:
- Shredded newspaper (black ink only, not colored glossy inserts): Tear in strips 1–2 inches wide.
- Shredded corrugated cardboard (remove any tape or staples)
- Coconut coir (cocofiber): Sold in compressed blocks ($10–15); expands with water; excellent moisture retention
- Aged leaves (moist, not dry)
- A small amount of finished compost or garden soil mixed in (adds microorganisms)

**Do NOT use**: Plastic, chemically treated paper, colored glossy paper, fresh sawdust (often too acidic), peat moss (too acidic, pH-lowering).

**Preparing the bedding**:
1. Shred or tear bedding materials into 1–2 inch pieces
2. Soak in water until thoroughly damp — squeeze test: a handful should release 1–2 drops when squeezed firmly. If water streams out, it is too wet.
3. Fill the bin to 6–8 inches depth with prepared bedding
4. Add a handful of grit (coarse sand, crushed eggshells) — worms have no teeth and use grit for grinding food
5. Add worms and let them acclimate for 24 hours before first feeding

### 4.4 Feeding the Worm Bin

**Feeding rate**: Red wigglers eat approximately half their body weight per day under optimal conditions. A 1 lb population of worms can process approximately 0.5 lbs of food per day. At suboptimal conditions (lower temperature, adjustment period), feed less.

**Practical feeding schedule**: Feed every 2–4 days. Each feeding should add enough food that the worms consume it within 3–4 days. If food scraps are still visible and unprocessed from a previous feeding, wait. Overfeeding causes odor problems and fruit fly infestations.

**How to feed**: Bury food scraps in the bedding rather than leaving them on top. This:
- Reduces fruit fly attraction
- Maintains moisture balance
- Ensures contact between food and worms (which live in bedding)
- Reduces odor

Rotate burial locations around the bin (divide into quadrants; bury each feeding in the next quadrant in sequence). This creates an efficient "migration harvest" later.

**Good foods for worm bins**:
- Fruit and vegetable scraps (including citrus — in moderation)
- Coffee grounds (worms love them; do not exceed 25% of the volume fed)
- Tea bags (paper ones; cut open if plastic mesh)
- Eggshells (crushed fine — adds calcium and grit)
- Cooked grains (pasta, rice, bread — in small amounts; can ferment if overfed)

**Avoid or use with caution**:
- Onions, garlic, leeks — high acidity; can create pH problems in large quantities; small amounts are fine
- Meat, dairy, fish — odors attract pests; not needed; worms don't require animal protein
- Salty foods or large amounts of vinegar — salt draws moisture from worms through osmosis
- Hot peppers in large quantities — capsaicin irritates worms

### 4.5 Environmental Requirements

**Temperature**: 59–77°F (15–25°C) is optimal. Worms slow feeding below 50°F (10°C); below 40°F (4°C) they become dormant. Above 95°F (35°C) they die. Key implications:
- Outdoor worm bins are not suitable for winter in zones 5 and colder without insulation or relocation indoors
- Worm bins work well indoors year-round in most homes (most homes are held at 65–72°F)
- In hot climates, keep bins out of direct sun; high summer temperatures can kill worms even in shaded outdoor locations if ambient temperatures exceed 90°F

**Moisture**: The bedding should always feel like a wrung-out sponge — moist, not dripping. Check moisture every 1–2 weeks. If the bin surface is dry, mist with water. If water is pooling in the bottom tray, add dry bedding material.

**Acidity**: Worms prefer near-neutral pH (6.0–7.0). Signs of acid problems: worms clustering at the top or sides of the bin (they are trying to escape the acid zone). Solution: add crushed eggshells or a small amount of agricultural lime (calcium carbonate). Do not add garden lime (calcium hydroxide) — it is too alkaline.

**Light**: Worms avoid light. Keep the bin covered. A cover also reduces moisture evaporation and deters fruit flies.

### 4.6 Harvesting Worm Castings

Worm castings (also called vermicompost) are ready when the bedding has been transformed into dark, crumbly, coffee-ground-textured material and the original food scraps and bedding are no longer recognizable.

**Harvest timing**: First harvest 2–4 months after starting the bin for a well-fed system at optimal temperature.

**Harvesting methods**:

**Method A — Migration Harvest (recommended for continuous bins)**:
1. Push all finished material to one side of the bin
2. Add fresh, damp bedding and new food scraps to the empty side
3. Stop feeding the finished side
4. Within 2–4 weeks, worms migrate to the fresh food side
5. Scoop finished castings from the empty side; most worms will have left

**Method B — Light Separation (works for smaller bins)**:
1. Dump all bin contents onto a bright surface (outside in sunlight, or under a work lamp)
2. Form the material into several cone-shaped mounds
3. Worms burrow away from light and congregate at the center of each cone
4. Remove the outer layers of castings from each cone
5. Continue for 15–20 minutes; worms cluster smaller; recover remaining worms for the bin

**Method C — Screen Separation**:
- Pass material through 1/4-inch hardware cloth
- Worms and large scraps remain on top; finished castings fall through
- Most efficient but requires hardware cloth screen

**After harvesting**: Restart the bin with fresh bedding and the recovered worms. No need to purchase new worms; the population self-regulates.

**Worm tea (leachate)**: Liquid that drains from the bin is not technically "worm tea" (which is aerated tea brewed from finished castings) but is useful as a diluted liquid fertilizer. Dilute 10:1 with water before applying to plants. Do not apply undiluted — the high concentration of nitrogen can burn plants.

### 4.7 Troubleshooting Worm Bins

| Problem | Likely Cause | Solution |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| Bad odor (rotten smell) | Overfeeding; too wet; anaerobic zones | Stop feeding for 1 week; add dry bedding; turn gently |
| Fruit flies | Food left on surface; too acidic | Bury all food; add a layer of dry bedding on top; fruit fly trap (apple cider vinegar + dish soap) |
| Worms escaping bin | Too wet; too acidic; too warm; lights | Check and adjust moisture; add eggshells; move to cooler location |
| Bin not processing (food sitting) | Too cold; too few worms; wrong foods | Check temperature; reduce feeding; ensure correct worm species |
| Worms dying (flattened, string-like) | Protein poisoning (too much nitrogen), overheating | Remove any meat/dairy; reduce feeding; check temperature |
| White mites appearing | Overfeeding; too moist | Reduce feeding; allow bin to dry slightly; remove excess food |

## Section 5: Using Finished Compost — Application Rates and Methods

Knowing how to apply compost correctly determines whether it improves soil or wastes effort.

### 5.1 What Finished Compost Provides

Nutrient content of typical finished compost (plant-based, which is most common from household composting):

| Nutrient | Typical Range | Notes |
|----------|--------------|-------|
| Total Nitrogen (N) | 1.0–3.5% | 10–25% plant-available first year |
| Total Phosphorus (P) | 0.2–1.5% | 40–60% plant-available first year |
| Total Potassium (K) | 0.4–1.4% | Most is immediately plant-available |
| Organic matter | 35–65% | The primary long-term soil benefit |

**Important note on nitrogen**: Only 10–25% of total compost nitrogen is available to plants in the first year. The rest releases slowly over 2–5 years as microorganisms continue breaking down organic matter. This is why compost is not a replacement for fertilizer in high-demand crops; it is a soil conditioner that also provides nutrients gradually.

Compost also provides:
- Micronutrients (iron, manganese, zinc, copper, boron) in trace amounts
- Billions of beneficial microorganisms per teaspoon
- Humic substances that improve nutrient availability and water retention

### 5.2 Application Rates by Use

**New garden beds or raised beds (amendment before planting)**:
- Apply 2–4 inches of compost over the entire bed
- Till or fork in to the top 6–8 inches of soil
- This is the highest-impact single application you can make to new beds

**Established vegetable gardens (annual maintenance)**:
- Apply 1 inch of compost over the bed each season (before spring planting or in fall)
- Lightly incorporate into the top 2–3 inches, or leave as a mulch-amendment combination
- 1 inch depth over 100 sq ft = 8.33 cubic feet = approximately 0.31 cubic yards

**Lawn renovation or overseeding**:
- Apply 1/4 to 1/2 inch of compost over the lawn surface
- Do not use finished hot compost for established lawns (nitrogen is too concentrated for slow release onto turf)
- Rake lightly to contact soil; seed into the compost layer

**Container potting mix**:
- Use compost at 20–30% by volume mixed with potting medium, sand, and/or perlite
- Do not use 100% compost in containers — even good compost can become compacted and waterlogged
- Example recipe: 30% compost + 40% peat or coir + 30% perlite = excellent general potting mix

**Tree and shrub planting**:
- Mix compost with native soil at 20–25% by volume in the planting hole
- Do not fill the planting hole with 100% compost — roots need to eventually grow into native soil
- After planting, apply a 1-inch ring of compost as a surface mulch 6–18 inches from the trunk (do not pile against trunk)

**Surface mulching**:
- Apply 1–2 inches of compost around established plants as a mulch
- Keep compost 2–3 inches away from plant stems and tree trunks
- Benefits: suppresses weeds, retains moisture, gradually feeds plants as it breaks down

### 5.3 Soil Types and Application Adjustments

**Clay soils** (heavy, sticky when wet, prone to compaction):
- Compost improves clay soil more than any other amendment
- Use higher initial application rates (3–4 inches incorporated) for new clay beds
- Annual applications of 1 inch dramatically improve drainage and structure over 3–5 years

**Sandy soils** (fast-draining, dry out quickly, poor nutrient retention):
- Compost improves sandy soils by adding organic matter that holds water and nutrients
- Use higher initial rates (3–4 inches) to compensate for rapid breakdown in hot, well-drained conditions
- In very sandy soils, may need to reapply twice per year initially

**Loam (balanced soils)**:
- Standard 1–2 inch annual maintenance applications are adequate
- Compost maintains rather than corrects; best use is maintaining the biology already present

### 5.4 What Compost Does Not Replace

Compost is a soil conditioner and slow-release nutrient source. It does NOT replace:

**Nitrogen fertilizer for high-demand crops**: Sweet corn, brassicas, and leafy vegetables often need more available nitrogen than compost alone provides. Even with annual compost application, a nitrogen-heavy crop like corn may show yellowing and yield loss without supplemental fertilizer. A soil test (see below) identifies this before planting.

**Lime for acid soil correction**: Compost has near-neutral pH (6.5–7.5) and mildly raises soil pH, but cannot correct significantly acidic soil (below 5.5). For blueberries, potatoes, or acid-demanding plants, compost alone is not a pH management tool.

**Soil testing**: The best guide to what your soil needs is a standard soil test from your state Extension service or a private lab. A basic test (pH, N, P, K) costs $10–30 and tells you exactly what your soil needs. Compost applications benefit all soil types, but the additional interventions (lime, fertilizer) depend on test results. Contact your county Extension office to obtain soil test kits and instructions specific to your state.

## Section 6: Troubleshooting Reference — 12 Common Problems

| Problem | Symptom | Cause | Solution |
|---------|---------|-------|---------|
| Pile not heating | No temperature rise after 4 days | Too small, too dry, or insufficient nitrogen | Add more nitrogen (fresh greens, manure); add water; ensure minimum 1 cubic yard volume |
| Ammonia smell | Pungent urine-like odor | Too much nitrogen (low C:N) | Add carbon: dry leaves, cardboard, straw. Target 2:1 carbon-to-nitrogen by volume |
| Rotten egg / sulfur smell | Anaerobic conditions | Too wet or compacted; oxygen depleted | Turn pile; add dry bulky material (wood chips, straw) to improve structure |
| Pile too wet | Slimy, compacted, not heating | Excess moisture; possibly too much food scraps | Add dry brown material; turn; cover pile in sustained rain |
| Pile too dry | Dry and light; not composting | Insufficient moisture | Add water gradually while turning; cover to retain moisture |
| Attracts rodents | Digging evidence; animal holes | Exposed meat, dairy, or food scraps | Exclude animal products; bury all food scraps in center; use enclosed bin |
| Attracts flies | Fly clouds when bin disturbed | Exposed food scraps; too much fruit | Bury all food under bedding; maintain carbon layer on top |
| Weed seeds surviving | Weeds germinate where compost applied | Pile did not reach kill temperature | If weeds are a problem, switch to managed hot composting with temperature verification |
| Slow decomposition (cold pile) | Pile unchanged after 3 months | Expected in cold composting | Normal in cold process; speed up with turning, moisture, nitrogen additions |
| Greasy or waxy patches | Matted white or yellow material | High fat content (avocado, nut butters, cooking oil) | Remove fat-laden materials; these compost poorly; add more browns and mix |
| Bin attracting ants | Ant colony visible | Pile too dry | Add water; ants prefer dry conditions |
| Finished compost burns plants | Seedlings die; yellowing after application | Immature compost (not fully cured) | Allow more curing time; test with radish germination; apply only mature, stable compost |

## Section 7: Compost Quality Testing

Before applying compost to a food garden, especially commercial or shared compost, a simple quality check verifies maturity and absence of contaminants.

### 7.1 The Radish Germination Test

**Materials**: A small amount of test compost, 10 radish seeds, a container, water.

**Procedure**:
1. Fill a small pot with test compost
2. Plant 10 radish seeds at 1/4-inch depth
3. Water and place in warm location (65–75°F)
4. Observe for 5–7 days

**Results**:
- 7–10 seeds germinate and grow normally: compost is mature and safe to use
- 4–6 seeds germinate, some die or grow poorly: compost is borderline; cure for 2–4 more weeks and retest
- Fewer than 4 seeds germinate or all fail: compost is immature and contains phytotoxic compounds; do not use; cure for 4–8 more weeks

### 7.2 Signs of Contamination to Avoid

**Biosolids-based compost**: Municipal sewage sludge biosolids compost may contain PFAS (per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances) and heavy metals. If purchasing bulk compost, ask the supplier whether it is biosolids-based. For food gardens, prefer plant- or manure-based compost.

**Commercial bulk compost quality indicators**:
- US Composting Council STA (Seal of Testing Assurance) certification: means the product has been tested per TMECC protocols; look for STA label
- Request a Certificate of Analysis (COA) from bulk suppliers; reputable suppliers provide this on request
- Avoid compost that smells of petroleum, sewage, or chemicals

## Section 8: Seasonal Composting Guide

### Spring (March–May, Zones 4–7)

- Turn overwintered cold pile to reactivate; add nitrogen if pile is all browns
- Hot compost setup is ideal: pile has time to mature by fall planting
- Apply finished compost to garden beds before planting
- Start or restart vermicomposting indoors; move worm bins outside once nighttime temperatures are consistently above 50°F

### Summer (June–August)

- Hot composting is easiest: high ambient temperatures accelerate thermophilic activity
- Monitor moisture closely; piles dry out quickly in summer heat
- Harvest worm castings; worm populations are at peak activity
- Apply compost as mulch around established plants to reduce water needs

### Fall (September–November)

- The best time to build a hot pile: ample leaves (carbon) + end-of-season garden material (nitrogen)
- Shred leaves with a lawn mower before adding; unshredded leaves form impermeable mats
- Apply fall compost application to garden beds after harvest; organisms have all winter to break down compost into soil organic matter
- Move worm bins indoors before first hard frost in zones 5 and colder

### Winter (December–February, Zones 4–7)

- Hot composting: possible if pile is large and insulated; challenging below 20°F
- Cold composting: pile dormant but resumes immediately in spring; do not disturb; continue adding materials
- Vermicomposting: maintain indoors; reduce feeding slightly as worm metabolism slows in cool homes
