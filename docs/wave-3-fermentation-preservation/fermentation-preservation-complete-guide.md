# Fermentation & Preservation: Complete Practical Guide

**License**: This guide is published under [CC-BY-4.0](https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by/4.0/). You are free to share and adapt it with attribution to the Open-Repo Project.

**Relationship to Wave 1**: The Wave 1 food preservation guide covers sauerkraut, fermented dill pickles, and kimchi at an introductory level within a broader canning context. This guide goes deeper: it explains the microbiology of fermentation, provides precision salt calculations, covers beverage fermentation (kombucha, water kefir, tepache), adds fermented hot sauce, and includes comprehensive troubleshooting, vessel selection, scaling, and integration guidance for practitioners who want fermentation as a primary preservation strategy — not just one technique among several.

---

## Overview

Fermentation is the oldest preservation method in human practice. Before refrigeration, before canning, before any manufactured food additive, human communities preserved food by salt and time. The result was not merely shelf-stable food but food transformed: more digestible, more nutritious in some respects, and rich in the live microbial cultures now understood to support gut microbiome diversity.

**What fermentation does**: Wild and cultivated bacteria — primarily lactic acid bacteria (LAB), chiefly *Lactobacillus* species — consume sugars and produce lactic acid. This acid drops the pH of the food below 4.6, which is the threshold below which *Clostridium botulinum* cannot grow or produce toxin. The anaerobic, acidic environment created by a successful fermentation is hostile to most spoilage and pathogenic organisms.

**What this guide covers**:

1. Fermentation fundamentals: microbiology, salt chemistry, vessel selection, monitoring
2. Vegetable fermentation: sauerkraut, kimchi, pickled vegetables, fermented hot sauce — step-by-step with worked examples
3. Beverage fermentation: kombucha, water kefir, tepache — SCOBY care and carbonation
4. Nutritional benefits and preservation advantages relative to canning
5. Troubleshooting: mold, kahm yeast, off-flavors, slow fermentation, over-fermentation
6. Storage, vessel rotation, and scaling for household production
7. Integration with Wave 1 canning methods — when to ferment, when to can, how to combine them

**Safety note**: Fermented vegetables, when made following the salt ratios and procedures in this guide, are among the safest home food preservation methods. The lactic acid produced provides robust protection against botulism. However, certain precautions are required. All salt ratios, temperatures, and safety thresholds in this guide are sourced from primary authorities: the National Center for Home Food Preservation (NCHFP), USDA, University of Minnesota Extension, and Illinois Extension.

## Section 1: Fermentation Fundamentals

### 1.1 The Microbiology of Lactic Acid Fermentation

Lacto-fermentation is a spontaneous or starter-driven biological process. Understanding the key organisms and their environmental needs determines every procedural decision.

**Lactic acid bacteria (LAB)**: The primary agents of vegetable fermentation. These are gram-positive, anaerobic-tolerant bacteria naturally present on plant surfaces. Key genera involved in vegetable fermentation include:

- *Leuconostoc mesenteroides* — initiates early fermentation; produces CO2 that establishes the anaerobic environment; most active at 65–70°F (18–21°C); produces dextran that gives young ferments a slightly slimy texture
- *Lactobacillus plantarum* — dominant in mid-to-late fermentation; acid-tolerant; produces most of the lactic acid; most active at 65–75°F (18–24°C)
- *Lactobacillus brevis* — contributes to flavor complexity; produces some acetic acid alongside lactic acid

**Succession pattern**: In a well-executed fermentation, these organisms succeed each other in a predictable order. *Leuconostoc* dominates for the first 3–5 days, then *L. plantarum* takes over as acidity increases beyond *Leuconostoc* tolerance, driving pH down toward 3.5. This succession is what makes spontaneous lacto-fermentation reliable despite using wild cultures.

**What salt does**:
1. Draws water from vegetable cells by osmosis, creating brine without heating
2. Selectively suppresses spoilage bacteria, which are more salt-sensitive than LAB
3. Controls fermentation speed — more salt slows the process and produces a more acidic final product

**The anaerobic requirement**: LAB are facultative anaerobes — they can tolerate oxygen but produce lactic acid most efficiently without it. Keeping vegetables submerged below brine is not optional. Vegetables exposed to air above the brine allow aerobic molds and yeasts to establish, outcompeting LAB and causing spoilage.

**pH trajectory**: In a correctly salted ferment at 68–72°F:
- Day 1–2: pH begins dropping from ~6.0 (raw vegetable)
- Day 3–5: pH reaches ~4.6 (safety threshold crossed)
- Week 2–4: pH stabilizes at 3.5–4.0 (final product acidity)

Source: UMN Extension notes fermentation is complete when pH reaches 4.60 or lower and bubbling has stopped (University of Minnesota Extension, "Preserving food at home: Fermentation").

### 1.2 Salt Ratios: The Critical Variable

Salt percentage is the single most important controllable parameter in vegetable fermentation. Two calculation methods are used depending on whether vegetables are shredded (dry-salt method) or whole/large-cut (wet brine method).

**Dry-salt method** (for shredded vegetables: sauerkraut, kimchi, shredded carrots):

| Salt % | Gram salt per kg vegetable | Practical measure per 5 lb / 2.27 kg | Effect |
|--------|---------------------------|-------------------------------------|--------|
| 1.5% | 15 g / kg | 2 tablespoons | Minimum safe; faster fermentation; risk of spoilage in warm weather |
| 2.0% | 20 g / kg | 3 tablespoons | Standard; reliable across most temperatures |
| 2.5% | 25 g / kg | 3 tablespoons + 1 teaspoon | Slower; more acidic product; better for hot climates |
| 3.0% | 30 g / kg | 4 tablespoons | Upper limit; above 3% may suppress LAB and stall fermentation |

*Sources: NCHFP recommends 3 tablespoons per 5 lbs for sauerkraut (≈ 2.0%); University of Minnesota Extension recommends "3 tablespoons to 3 tablespoons plus 1 teaspoon per 5 pounds"; Illinois Extension: "below 1.5% risks spoilage."*

**Wet brine method** (for whole or large-cut vegetables: whole pickles, pepper halves, whole olives):

| Brine strength | Salt per 1 quart (946 mL) water | Use case |
|---------------|-------------------------------|----------|
| 2% | 1 tablespoon (18 g) | Delicate vegetables; warm conditions not recommended for whole items |
| 3% | 2 tablespoons (27 g) | Shredded or cut vegetables submerged in brine rather than dry-salted |
| 5% | 3 tablespoons (45 g) | Whole cucumbers, large pepper pieces, whole olives |
| 7–10% | 5–7 tablespoons | Long-term storage brines; cured olives; not for active fermentation |

*Sources: Illinois Extension specifies 2 tbsp per quart for 3% and 3 tbsp per quart for 5% brine; NCHFP specifies 1.5 tablespoons salt per quart for additional sauerkraut brine.*

**Salt type**: Use non-iodized salt exclusively. Options, in order of preference:
1. **Canning/pickling salt** (pure NaCl, no additives) — ideal; dissolves quickly; no cloudiness
2. **Kosher salt without anti-caking agents** (check label) — suitable; use 1.25× the volume measurement because kosher salt is less dense
3. **Pure sea salt without additives** — suitable if finely ground; coarser grinds dissolve more slowly
4. **Iodized table salt** — do not use; iodine inhibits LAB and can stall fermentation entirely

**Measuring salt precisely**: Weight-based measurement is more accurate than volume measurement, especially when scaling. A kitchen scale accurate to 1 gram costs $10–20 and eliminates the density variations between salt types.

### 1.3 Temperature Control

Temperature determines fermentation speed, flavor development, and which bacterial strains dominate.

| Temperature Range | Effect | Outcome |
|-----------------|--------|---------|
| Below 60°F (15°C) | LAB become sluggish; fermentation may not initiate or progress | Risk of spoilage before sufficient acid develops |
| 60–65°F (15–18°C) | Slow fermentation; *Leuconostoc* activity extended | 5–6 weeks to complete; milder, more complex flavor |
| 65–72°F (18–22°C) | Optimal range; all LAB strains active in succession | 3–4 weeks; reliable outcome; best flavor complexity |
| 72–75°F (22–24°C) | Faster fermentation; *L. plantarum* dominant sooner | 2–3 weeks; slightly sharper flavor |
| Above 75°F (24°C) | Accelerated to the point of risk; *Leuconostoc* phase shortened | Soft texture; risk of off-flavors; increased kahm yeast |
| Above 85°F (29°C) | Fermentation too rapid; LAB may be outcompeted; spoilage risk | Do not ferment at these temperatures |

*Sources: NCHFP: "Store at 70° to 75°F while fermenting. Fully fermented in 3 to 4 weeks. At 60° to 65°F, kraut may take 5 to 6 weeks. Below 60°F, kraut may not ferment." University of Minnesota Extension: "temperatures exceeding 78°F risk spoilage."*

**Practical temperature management**:
- In summer, ferment in the coolest part of the house — a basement, pantry, or interior closet
- Wrap the jar in a towel and place it in a cooler with cold packs to slow fermentation in heat
- In winter, a warm kitchen counter (68°F) is ideal; avoid placing jars next to heat sources
- Fluctuating temperatures are tolerable — a consistent average matters more than perfect stability

### 1.4 Vessel Selection

**Glass jars (Mason-type)**: The most accessible and practical option for most households. Half-gallon (64 oz) jars for small batches; gallon jars for larger batches. Glass is non-reactive, inexpensive, and allows visual monitoring of fermentation activity.

**Ceramic fermentation crocks**: Traditional vessels designed for fermentation. Available in two types:
- *Open crocks*: Simple wide-mouth vessel; require a plate or flat weight on top plus a heavy towel; require more monitoring for kahm yeast
- *Water-sealed crocks* (gutter crocks): Outer rim filled with water creates a water lock; CO2 exits as bubbles; air cannot enter; reduces monitoring requirement and mold risk; preferred for large batches

**NCHFP sizing standard**: "A 1-gallon container is needed for each 5 pounds of fresh vegetables; a 5-gallon stone crock is ideal for fermenting about 25 pounds." (NCHFP, "Suitable Containers, Covers, and Weights for Fermenting Food")

**Materials to avoid**:
- Metal vessels (copper, aluminum, galvanized steel) — react with lactic acid; leach metals into food
- Non-food-grade plastic — may leach plasticizers; use only HDPE or LDPE marked food-grade
- Lead-glazed ceramics — lead is still permitted in ceramic glazes; check for lead-free certification, especially for imported crocks
- Garbage bags or trash liners — explicitly prohibited by NCHFP

**Airlock lids**: Screw-on jar lids fitted with a one-way airlock valve allow CO2 to escape without admitting oxygen. These significantly reduce kahm yeast formation and eliminate the need to burp sealed jars. They are not required but are the best option for beginners.

**Weights**: The NCHFP-recommended weighting system for large crocks: place a dinner plate or glass pie plate inside the crock and weight it with 2–3 sealed quart jars filled with water. For glass jars: a zip-lock bag filled with brine, a smaller jar that fits inside the larger one, or purpose-made glass or ceramic fermentation weights ($10–20).

### 1.5 Monitoring Active Fermentation

**Signs of successful fermentation**:
- Visible bubbling in brine, especially in the first 5–10 days (CO2 produced by LAB)
- Brine becomes progressively cloudy (suspended bacteria and breakdown products — normal and desirable)
- Pleasant sour aroma developing — tangy, lactic, like good pickles
- Slight hissing when jar is opened (dissolved CO2)
- pH strips show progressive acidification: test at day 3–5 should show pH ≤5.0; at completion, pH ≤4.6

**How often to check**: Check every 2–3 days for the first two weeks. Push down any vegetables that have risen above brine. Skim any surface material. Taste after 7 days for preference.

## Section 2: Vegetable Fermentation

### 2.1 Sauerkraut — Comprehensive Procedure

Sauerkraut is the canonical lacto-fermented vegetable: two ingredients, no equipment beyond a jar and a weight, reliable process, shelf-stable for 9+ months in refrigeration.

**Worked example — Standard 1-quart batch**:

**Ingredients**:
- 1 medium head green cabbage (~2 lb / 900 g after outer leaves removed)
- 1 tablespoon non-iodized canning or pickling salt (adjust to 2% by weight: 18 g per 900 g cabbage)

**Equipment**:
- Large mixing bowl
- Sharp knife or mandoline
- 1-quart Mason jar
- Fermentation weight (zip-lock bag with brine, or ceramic weight)
- Cloth or loose lid for covering

**Procedure**:

1. **Prepare cabbage**: Remove and reserve 1–2 outer leaves (used as a cover later). Shred remaining cabbage 1/8-inch thick using a knife, mandoline, or food processor shredding blade. Remove and discard the core.

2. **Salt and massage**: Place shredded cabbage in a large bowl. Sprinkle salt evenly over cabbage. Using clean hands, massage and squeeze vigorously for 10–15 minutes. The cabbage will soften, wilt, and release substantial liquid — the natural brine. Volume reduces approximately 25–30%. If liquid is insufficient after 15 minutes, pack into jar, wait 30 minutes, check again.

3. **Pack the jar**: Transfer salted cabbage and all accumulated liquid into the jar, pressing firmly with your fist or a wooden tamper after each addition. Pack as tightly as possible to eliminate air pockets. Brine should visibly rise as you pack.

4. **Submerge**: Fold a reserved outer cabbage leaf to fit inside the jar mouth and lay it over the shredded cabbage. Place weight on top — brine should cover everything by at least 1 inch. Leave 1.5–2 inches of headspace above the brine (fermentation generates CO2 that expands contents).

5. **Cover**: Fit a cloth secured with a rubber band, a loose lid (not tightened), or an airlock lid. Do not seal airtight without an airlock — CO2 buildup in a sealed jar can cause dangerous pressure.

6. **Ferment**: Place jar on a plate (to catch any overflow) in a 65–72°F location out of direct sunlight. Lactobacillus is sensitive to UV light.
   - Day 1–3: Little visible activity; brine may begin to cloud
   - Day 3–5: Bubbling begins; sour aroma develops; taste is mildly salty
   - Week 1–2: More pronounced sourness; brine distinctly cloudy
   - Week 3–4: Full sour flavor; bubbling slows as fermentation completes

7. **Transfer to cold storage**: Taste beginning at day 7. When desired sourness is reached (most prefer 3–4 weeks at 68–72°F), move jar to refrigerator. Fermentation slows to near-zero at 35–38°F.

**Shelf life**: 9 months refrigerated (UMN Extension); quality declines slowly after 6 months. Does not require oxygen exclusion once in the refrigerator — a regular lid is sufficient.

**Large batch scaling**:

| Cabbage | Salt (2%) | Container |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| 5 lb (2.3 kg) | 3 tablespoons (46 g) | 1-gallon jar or crock |
| 10 lb (4.5 kg) | 6 tablespoons (92 g) | 2-gallon crock |
| 25 lb (11.3 kg) | 3/4 cup (184 g) | 5-gallon crock |

*Source: NCHFP sauerkraut recipe: "3/4 cup canning or pickling salt per 25 pounds of cabbage."*

**Flavor variations** (all within safe parameters):
- **Caraway sauerkraut**: Add 1 tablespoon caraway seeds per pound of cabbage; no safety implications
- **Red cabbage kraut**: Use red cabbage at identical salt ratios; produces vivid purple brine; flavor is milder
- **Mixed kraut**: Up to 25% other shredded vegetables (carrot, beet, celeriac) by weight; use same 2% salt relative to total vegetable weight

### 2.2 Kimchi — Comprehensive Procedure

Kimchi is a Korean fermented vegetable preparation with a longer ingredient list than sauerkraut but the same underlying lacto-fermentation mechanism. It ferments faster (2–5 days at room temperature) and continues developing flavor slowly in the refrigerator.

**Worked example — Traditional Baechu-kimchi (napa cabbage kimchi), 1-quart batch**:

**Ingredients**:
- 1 small head napa cabbage (1.5 lb / 680 g)
- 2 tablespoons non-iodized salt (for initial wilting — not all retained)
- **Paste**: 2 tablespoons Korean red pepper flakes (gochugaru); 3–4 garlic cloves (minced); 1 teaspoon fresh ginger (grated); 1 tablespoon fish sauce or soy sauce (optional — vegan omission is safe)
- 4 green onions, cut into 2-inch pieces
- 1 teaspoon sugar or grated Asian pear or apple (optional; provides initial sugar for LAB)

**Procedure**:

1. **Salt the cabbage**: Cut cabbage into 2-inch square pieces or leave in quarters. Place in a large bowl, sprinkle with salt, toss to coat, and let sit 1–2 hours, tossing every 30 minutes. Cabbage should wilt substantially and release liquid.

2. **Rinse and squeeze**: Rinse salted cabbage once under cold water to remove excess surface salt. Squeeze firmly to remove most liquid. The final salt content of kimchi is approximately 2–2.5% — enough salt remains in the cabbage after squeezing even after rinsing.

3. **Make the paste**: Combine gochugaru, garlic, ginger, fish sauce (if using), and sugar. Mix into a uniform paste. Wear gloves — gochugaru stains and irritates skin.

4. **Combine**: Toss cabbage, green onions, and paste in the bowl, coating all surfaces thoroughly with clean hands (or gloved hands).

5. **Pack**: Transfer to a jar, pressing firmly down so brine released during mixing rises to cover vegetables. Leave 2 inches of headspace — kimchi ferments vigorously and can overflow.

6. **Ferment**: Leave at 65–72°F for 1–5 days. Kimchi ferments faster than sauerkraut because gochugaru and fish sauce provide additional nutrients for LAB. Taste daily beginning at day 1. Kimchi is ready when pleasantly sour and tangy to your preference.

7. **Refrigerate**: Transfer to refrigerator at your preferred sourness. Flavor continues developing slowly (2–4 weeks cold is often considered peak flavor).

**Shelf life refrigerated**: 3–6 months (flavor intensity increases with age; highly acidic kimchi aged 3+ months is used differently than fresh kimchi — in soups, pancakes, stews).

**Vegan kimchi**: Omit fish sauce. Substitute equal amount of soy sauce, or omit entirely (salt from cabbage wilting provides sufficient salinity). Flavor is lighter; fermentation proceeds identically.

**Heat level calibration**:

| Gochugaru per 1.5 lb cabbage | Heat level |
|-----------------------------|------------|
| 1 tablespoon | Mild |
| 2 tablespoons | Traditional medium |
| 3–4 tablespoons | Hot |
| Omit entirely | White kimchi (baek kimchi) — no heat |

### 2.3 Fermented Whole Vegetables in Brine

This category covers whole or large-cut vegetables submerged in a salt brine rather than dry-salted — cucumbers, peppers, green beans, beets, turnips, radishes.

**General procedure for brine-fermented vegetables**:

**Standard equipment**:
- 1-quart or half-gallon jar
- 5% brine: 3 tablespoons (45 g) non-iodized salt dissolved in 1 quart non-chlorinated water

**Water note**: Chlorine in municipal tap water inhibits LAB. Use filtered water, collected rainwater, or allow tap water to sit uncovered 24 hours before use.

**General steps**:

1. Select firm, fresh vegetables free of soft spots or mold
2. Scrub under running water; do not use soap
3. Trim blossom ends from cucumbers (blossom end contains enzymes that soften fermented pickles)
4. Pack vegetables tightly into jar — leave minimal air space
5. Add optional flavor additions (see below)
6. Pour 5% brine over vegetables, covering by at least 1 inch
7. Submerge with weight; cover with cloth or airlock lid
8. Ferment at 65–72°F; check every 2–3 days

**Fermented dill pickles (worked example)**:

*Ingredients for 1-quart jar*:
- 4–5 small pickling cucumbers (3–4 inch), 1/16-inch blossom end trimmed
- 5% brine: 1 quart non-chlorinated water + 3 tablespoons non-iodized salt
- 2–3 heads fresh dill or 1 tablespoon dill seed
- 2–3 garlic cloves (optional)
- 1 grape leaf, oak leaf, or horseradish leaf (tannins preserve crunch — optional but effective)
- 1/4 teaspoon black peppercorns (optional)

*Timeline at 68–72°F*:
- Day 5–7: Half-sour pickles (mild, slightly salty, lightly acidic; crisp)
- Week 3–4: Full-sour dill pickles (distinctly sour; traditional deli style)

Refrigerate when desired sourness is reached. Shelf life: 6 months refrigerated.

**Other vegetables — fermentation times at 68–72°F**:

| Vegetable | Preparation | Brine % | Approximate Time |
|-----------|-------------|---------|-----------------|
| Whole dill pickles (3–4 inch) | Blossom end trimmed | 5% | 3–4 weeks (full sour) |
| Sliced cucumbers | 1/4-inch rounds | 3% | 5–7 days |
| Green beans (dilly beans) | Blanched 2 min, packed whole | 3% | 3–4 weeks |
| Hot peppers, whole | Slashed to allow brine penetration | 5% | 2–3 weeks |
| Beets | Cooked, peeled, sliced | 3% | 5–7 days |
| Turnips (traditional Middle Eastern) | Quartered, with beet slice for color | 5% | 1–2 weeks |
| Radishes | Sliced or whole | 3% | 3–5 days |
| Carrots | Sliced into coins or sticks | 3% | 1–2 weeks |

### 2.4 Fermented Hot Sauce

Lacto-fermented hot sauce is made by fermenting whole or chopped chile peppers in a salt brine, then blending the fermented mash into sauce. The result is a complex, naturally acidic sauce with a pH typically between 3.0 and 4.0.

**Worked example — 1-pint fermented hot sauce**:

**Ingredients**:
- 1 lb (450 g) fresh chile peppers, any variety (Fresno, serrano, jalapeño, habanero, or mixed)
- 1 cup filtered water
- 1 tablespoon + 1 teaspoon non-iodized salt (approximately 3% brine by weight of peppers)
- Optional aromatics: 3–4 garlic cloves, 1/2 onion (chopped), herbs

**Note on salt percentage**: Commercial fermented hot sauce makers target 3–5% brine by weight of peppers for whole pepper fermentation. Some sources recommend slightly higher salt (3–4%) than vegetable ferments because peppers ferment quickly and the higher salt slows the process, producing more complex flavor. Federal pH safety guidelines require pH below 4.6; experienced home fermenters target 4.0 or lower for additional safety margin.

**Procedure**:

1. **Prepare peppers**: Wearing gloves, remove stems. Halve or roughly chop. Slash whole peppers to allow brine penetration. Remove seeds only if reducing heat.

2. **Make brine**: Dissolve salt in filtered water; do not heat.

3. **Pack and brine**: Pack peppers and any aromatics into a jar. Pour brine over, covering peppers by 1 inch. Weight down below brine; cover.

4. **Ferment**: 5–14 days at 65–72°F. Peppers are done when brine is distinctly sour and bubbling has ceased. Many hot sauce fermenters prefer 7–10 days for a balance of heat retention and sour complexity.

5. **Blend**: Transfer peppers and some brine to a blender. Blend until smooth. Add brine to achieve desired consistency. Taste and adjust.

6. **Test pH** (recommended): Use pH strips or a meter. Target pH 4.0 or below. If pH is above 4.0, allow to ferment 2–3 more days and retest.

7. **Store**: Refrigerate in sealed glass bottles. Shelf life: 6–12 months refrigerated. For ambient shelf stability, the sauce must be either: (a) confirmed at pH ≤3.5 and kept in a cool, dark location, or (b) processed in a water bath canner using tested times from a validated acidified food source. Without pasteurization, refrigerate.

**Fermentation time vs. heat**: Longer fermentation at lower temperatures preserves more of the pepper's heat. Shorter fermentation at higher temperatures produces a milder result because capsaicin degrades slightly with heat and extended acid exposure.

## Section 3: Beverage Fermentation

### 3.1 Kombucha

Kombucha is a fermented sweetened tea produced by a SCOBY (Symbiotic Culture of Bacteria and Yeast). The SCOBY contains acetic acid bacteria (*Gluconobacter*, *Acetobacter*), lactic acid bacteria, and various yeasts in a cellulose matrix. It ferments sweet tea into an acidic, lightly carbonated, mildly caffeinated beverage.

**Safety note on kombucha**: Unlike vegetable fermentation, no tested NCHFP/USDA recipes exist for home kombucha. UC Cooperative Extension provides guidance based on food safety principles but cautions that contamination is possible if sanitation is inadequate or starter ratios are insufficient. The key safety controls are: pH monitoring (target ≤3.5 at end of fermentation), adequate starter tea ratio (≥10% of batch volume), and strict sanitation.

**SCOBY acquisition**:
- Purchase a live SCOBY from a reputable fermentation supplier (preferred for beginners)
- Obtain from a home brewer — SCOBY "hotels" (jars of spare SCOBYs in starter tea) are common in fermentation communities
- Grow your own from raw, unflavored commercial kombucha (takes 3–4 weeks; not recommended for first batch)

**SCOBY health indicators**:
- Healthy SCOBY: firm rubbery disk; cream to tan to light brown color; mild vinegary smell
- Unhealthy: fuzzy mold (blue, green, black, or pink) on the surface; strong off-odors; black or dark brown discoloration penetrating the SCOBY (not surface staining from tea)
- If you see mold on a SCOBY, discard the entire SCOBY and all liquid. Do not attempt to cut away the mold.

**Primary fermentation — worked example (1-gallon batch)**:

**Ingredients**:
- 1 gallon filtered or non-chlorinated water
- 1 cup (200 g) white granulated sugar
- 8 black or green tea bags (or 2 tablespoons loose leaf)
- 1–2 cups starter tea from a previous batch (or from raw commercial kombucha) — minimum 10% of batch volume
- 1 healthy SCOBY

**Equipment**:
- 1-gallon glass jar (wide-mouth)
- Cloth or coffee filter secured with rubber band (breathable; do not seal airtight)
- Thermometer

**Procedure**:

1. **Brew sweet tea**: Bring 4 cups of water to boil. Steep tea 5–7 minutes. Remove tea bags. Dissolve sugar in hot tea, stirring until clear. Add remaining cold water to bring to full gallon. Cool to below 85°F before proceeding — heat kills SCOBY cultures.

2. **Combine**: Pour cooled sweet tea into clean 1-gallon jar. Add starter tea. Gently slide in SCOBY.

3. **Cover**: Secure breathable cloth over the mouth. Do not seal airtight — CO2 must escape and SCOBY needs some airflow.

4. **Ferment**: Place at 72–80°F (22–27°C), away from direct sunlight and away from vinegar or sourdough (airborne cultures can contaminate). Do not disturb during fermentation.
   - Optimal range: 72–78°F (22–26°C)
   - Below 68°F (20°C): fermentation slows significantly; mold risk if acidification is too slow
   - Above 85°F (29°C): stresses SCOBY; can produce off-flavors; risk of killing culture

5. **Monitor**: Taste beginning at day 7. Kombucha is ready when it reaches your preferred balance of sweet and sour. Properly fermented kombucha has a pH of 2.5–3.5. If pH exceeds 4.2, the fermentation is under-acidified — allow additional fermentation time.

6. **Duration**: 7–14 days at optimal temperature. Warmer = faster; cooler = slower.

*Sources: UC Cooperative Extension, "Making Kombucha"; UC Cooperative Extension Master Food Preservers of Humboldt/Del Norte; kombucha pH safety range from Food Protection Trends (2018).*

**SCOBY maintenance between batches**:
- Always reserve 1–2 cups of finished kombucha as starter for the next batch
- SCOBY produces a "baby" pellicle on each batch; this can be separated and used to start additional batches or stored in a "SCOBY hotel" (jar of starter tea in the refrigerator)
- SCOBY hotel in refrigerator: will keep for several months with monthly feeding (add fresh sweet tea)
- Never store SCOBY in plain water — it needs acidic environment to remain viable

**Second fermentation (carbonation)**:

After primary fermentation, decant kombucha into pressure-rated swing-top glass bottles. Add 1–2 teaspoons of fruit juice, fresh ginger juice, or other flavoring per 16-oz bottle (provides sugar for continued yeast activity). Seal tightly. Leave at room temperature 2–4 days. Refrigerate.

**Bottle safety**: Use only pressure-rated swing-top bottles or commercial-grade bottles designed for carbonated beverages. Do not use standard Mason jars — they can shatter under carbonation pressure. "Burp" bottles once daily (briefly open to release pressure) to prevent over-carbonation.

**Contamination indicators for kombucha**:

| Observation | Interpretation | Action |
|------------|---------------|--------|
| SCOBY is cream, tan, or light brown | Normal | Continue |
| Thin brown strands hanging from SCOBY | Yeast strands | Normal; stir into starter or discard |
| Surface film that is white and flat | Possible new pellicle forming | Normal |
| Fuzzy raised growth on SCOBY surface | Mold | Discard SCOBY and all liquid; start over |
| pH above 4.2 at day 14 | Under-acidified | Ferment longer; increase starter tea ratio |
| Strong sulfur or rotten smell | Contamination | Discard; sanitize vessel; restart |

### 3.2 Water Kefir

Water kefir (also called tibicos) is produced by water kefir grains — polysaccharide-matrix granules containing LAB (*Lactobacillus brevis*, *Lactobacillus casei*, *Leuconostoc mesenteroides*), acetic acid bacteria, and yeasts in symbiosis. Unlike kombucha, water kefir is caffeine-free and ferments in 24–48 hours rather than 1–2 weeks.

**Grain care and feeding**:
- Water kefir grains are living cultures that must be fed regularly (every 24–48 hours when active)
- Grains double in volume approximately every 1–2 weeks in good conditions
- If not brewing: store grains in sugar water (same ratio as brewing) in the refrigerator for up to 3–4 weeks; refresh with fresh sugar water every 2 weeks

**Primary fermentation — worked example (1-quart batch)**:

**Ingredients**:
- 3–4 cups filtered water (room temperature; non-chlorinated)
- 3–4 tablespoons (45–60 g) white granulated sugar, or sucanat, or coconut sugar
- 1/4 cup active water kefir grains
- Optional mineral supplement: 1/4 teaspoon baking soda and/or 1/4 teaspoon cream of tartar (for soft water); or add a clean eggshell half or pinch of mineral-rich salt (water kefir grains need trace minerals)
- Optional: 2–3 dried figs or apricots (provides trace minerals and flavor; remove after fermentation)

**Sugar ratio**: 3–4 tablespoons per quart corresponds to approximately 8–10% sugar by weight, within the documented optimal range of 6–10% sucrose for tibicos fermentation (ScienceDirect, *Water kefir, a fermented beverage*, 2022).

**Procedure**:

1. **Dissolve sugar**: Stir sugar into room-temperature filtered water until dissolved. Do not use hot water — heat damages grains.

2. **Add grains**: Place water kefir grains in a clean jar. Add sugar water.

3. **Cover**: Secure breathable cloth or loose lid. Do not seal airtight during primary fermentation.

4. **Ferment**: 24–48 hours at 68–75°F (20–24°C). At 24 hours: lightly sweet, mildly fizzy. At 48 hours: noticeably more sour, more carbonated.

5. **Strain**: Pour through a fine mesh strainer into a bowl or pitcher, collecting grains. Rinse grains with non-chlorinated water. Begin next batch immediately or store grains in fresh sugar water in refrigerator.

6. **Second fermentation (optional — for carbonation)**: Transfer strained water kefir to a swing-top bottle. Add 1–2 tablespoons fruit juice, a few pieces of fresh fruit, or ginger. Seal tightly. Leave at room temperature 12–24 hours. Refrigerate.

**Grain health indicators**:
- Healthy grains: firm, slightly gelatinous, white to translucent appearance; sweet-sour smell
- Stressed grains: becoming stringy or losing cohesion (may indicate mineral deficiency — add mineral supplement)
- Contaminated batch: strong rotten smell; visible mold; grain disintegration beyond normal — discard and source new grains

**Do not use**: Metal vessels, utensils, or strainers in contact with grains or kefir liquid — acids corrode metal and metal ions harm grain cultures. Use glass, food-grade plastic, or wood.

### 3.3 Tepache

Tepache is a traditional Mexican fermented beverage made from pineapple rind and core, water, and sugar. Unlike kombucha (which requires a SCOBY) or water kefir (which requires grains), tepache ferments entirely from wild yeasts and bacteria naturally present on pineapple skin. It requires no starter culture.

**Confidence note on tepache**: No university extension standard recipe exists for tepache (confidence 78% for this subsection). Parameters below are cross-referenced from multiple practitioner fermentation sources. Tepache is a traditional beverage with a long safety history, but precise academic sourcing for home production parameters is limited.

**Characteristics**: Lightly alcoholic (1–2% ABV, similar to kombucha); lightly carbonated; mildly sweet-sour; tropical flavor. Fermentation completes in 2–4 days.

**Worked example — 1-gallon batch**:

**Ingredients**:
- Rind and core of 1 large pineapple (organic preferred, as conventional pineapples may have residual fungicide on skins)
- 1 cup (200 g) piloncillo (raw cane sugar) or brown sugar
- 1 cinnamon stick
- 3 whole cloves
- 1 gallon filtered water

**Pineapple preparation**: Wash pineapple skin thoroughly under cold running water to remove dirt. Do not scrub hard or use soap — you will remove the wild yeasts that drive fermentation.

**Procedure**:

1. **Dissolve sugar**: Add sugar to water and stir until dissolved.

2. **Combine**: Place pineapple rind and core in a half-gallon or gallon glass jar. Add cinnamon and cloves. Pour sugar water over pineapple.

3. **Weight down**: Pineapple floats. Weight it below liquid using a clean plate or a zip-lock bag filled with water.

4. **Cover**: Cloth secured with rubber band. Do not seal airtight.

5. **Ferment**: 2–3 days at room temperature (70–75°F is ideal). Bubbling should begin within 24 hours. Taste at day 2 — tepache is ready when lightly sour and fizzy with residual sweetness. Extended fermentation beyond 4 days increases alcohol content and vinegar notes.

6. **Strain and serve**: Strain out solids through a fine mesh strainer. Serve over ice. Tepache is at its best fresh but keeps refrigerated 3–5 days.

**For carbonation**: Bottle strained tepache in swing-top bottles. Seal and leave at room temperature 4–8 hours, then refrigerate. Open carefully — tepache can build significant carbonation.

**Safety note for tepache vessels**: Never use metal containers — the organic acids in tepache react with most metals and can leach toxins. Use glass, food-grade ceramic, or food-grade plastic only.

## Section 4: Nutritional Benefits and Preservation Advantages

### 4.1 Documented Nutritional Benefits

Lacto-fermented vegetables and fermented beverages undergo compositional changes during fermentation that alter and in some cases enhance their nutritional profile compared to both raw and heat-processed equivalents.

**Bioavailability enhancement**: Fermentation breaks down anti-nutritional factors including phytic acid (in grains and legumes) and tannins that bind minerals. Lactic acid bacteria produce enzymes (phytases, glucosidases) that hydrolyze complex phytochemicals into simpler, more bioavailable forms. A 2024 Annual Reviews paper found that fermentation can increase the bioavailability of resveratrol, polyphenols, and isothiocyanates compared to their unfermented precursors (*Annual Review of Food Science and Technology*, "Lacto-Fermented Fruits and Vegetables: Bioactive Components and Effects on Human Health," 2024).

**Novel bioactive compounds**: The fermentation process generates compounds not present in raw vegetables:
- GABA (gamma-aminobutyric acid) — neurotransmitter precursor produced in fermented cucumbers and peppers
- Isothiocyanates — from glucosinolate breakdown in sauerkraut (cabbage); documented anti-cancer activity in animal and in vitro studies
- Bacteriocins — antimicrobial peptides produced by LAB; inhibit pathogens including *Helicobacter pylori* and *Listeria monocytogenes* (PMC10777956, *Fermented Vegetables: Health Benefits, Defects, and Current Technological Solutions*, 2024)
- Short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) — support colonic epithelial health

**Vitamin retention**: Lacto-fermented vegetables retain more heat-sensitive vitamins (vitamin C, B vitamins including folate) than pressure-canned equivalents. Pressure canning at 240°F destroys significant vitamin C. Fermentation preserves or slightly increases certain B vitamins through microbial synthesis.

**Probiotic activity**: Live LAB cultures in unpasteurized fermented vegetables and beverages reach the gut and transiently increase microbial diversity. Published human intervention studies show increased gut microbiome diversity after regular fermented food consumption. Note: pasteurized fermented products (shelf-stable sauerkraut in cans, pasteurized kombucha) do not contain live cultures; only unpasteurized (refrigerated) ferments are probiotic.

**Appropriate cautions**:
- Biogenic amines (histamine, putrescine, cadaverine) are produced during fermentation; individuals with histamine intolerance may react to aged ferments
- The health claims for fermented foods are supported by observational studies and in vitro research; randomized controlled trials in humans remain relatively limited
- Probiotic effects are strain-specific and dose-dependent; home ferments contain mixed and variable cultures

### 4.2 Preservation Advantages Compared to Canning

Fermentation and canning are complementary preservation systems, each with distinct strengths and limitations. Understanding both helps choose the right method for each food and situation.

| Factor | Lacto-fermentation | Water bath canning | Pressure canning |
|--------|-------------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Equipment cost | Minimal ($0–30) | Moderate ($30–100) | High ($100–300) |
| Energy required | None (ambient temperature) | Moderate (stove fuel) | Moderate to high |
| Processing time | Days to weeks | 5–45 minutes processing | 25–90 minutes processing |
| Skill threshold | Low | Moderate | Moderate to high |
| Shelf life (cold storage) | 6–12 months refrigerated | 12–18 months pantry | 12–18 months pantry |
| Shelf life (ambient) | Unstable without refrigeration | Stable at ambient | Stable at ambient |
| Power dependency | None (no refrigeration needed to ferment) | None (shelf-stable after processing) | None (shelf-stable after processing) |
| Nutritional impact | Minimal vitamin loss; probiotic | Significant vitamin C loss | Significant vitamin C and heat-sensitive nutrient loss |
| Botulism risk | Very low (lactic acid protection) | Low for high-acid foods; HIGH if protocol violated | Very low when protocol followed |
| Flavor transformation | Significant (sour, complex) | Moderate (heat changes texture) | Moderate |

**When to ferment instead of can**:
- Vegetables you will consume regularly over months (sauerkraut, pickles, kimchi)
- When energy or equipment for canning is unavailable
- When nutritional quality (probiotics, vitamin retention) is prioritized
- For beverages (kombucha, kefir — no canning equivalent)
- For hot sauce and condiments consumed within 12 months

**When to can instead of or in addition to ferment**:
- Long-term (2+ year) ambient shelf storage without refrigeration
- Large harvests that exceed fermentation vessel capacity or refrigeration space
- Foods that do not ferment well (high-starch vegetables like potatoes, winter squash)
- Grid-down scenarios where refrigeration is unavailable long-term — canned goods need no cold
- Foods requiring specific processing for safety (low-acid vegetables, meats)

**Hybrid workflow** (produces both probiotic and shelf-stable product):
Ferment vegetables for 3–4 weeks, then water-bath can the finished sauerkraut or pickles. NCHFP provides tested processing times for canning finished sauerkraut (hot pack: 10 minutes for pints, 15 minutes for quarts; raw pack: 20 minutes for pints, 25 minutes for quarts, at altitudes 0–1,000 ft). This extends storage to pantry-stable 12–18 months but destroys probiotic cultures.

## Section 5: Troubleshooting

### 5.1 Troubleshooting Table

| Issue | Likely Cause(s) | Solution |
|-------|----------------|----------|
| No bubbling after 5 days | Temperature too cold; salt too high; chlorinated water | Move to warmer location (68–72°F); check salt ratio (was it above 3%?); switch to filtered water |
| White film on brine surface (flat, not fuzzy) | Kahm yeast (*Debaryomyces hansenii* and similar) | Skim off with a clean spoon; ensure all vegetables remain submerged; continue fermentation — underlying ferment is safe |
| Fuzzy mold on vegetables or SCOBY (raised, colored) | Aerobic mold contamination (Aspergillus, Penicillium, etc.) | Discard entire batch; sanitize vessel; identify cause (exposure to air, insufficient salt, too warm) |
| Soft or mushy vegetables | Temperature too warm; fermentation time too long; iodized salt used | Ferment cooler (60–65°F for crunchier results); harvest sooner; switch to non-iodized salt |
| Over-sour / too acidic | Fermented too long; temperature too warm | Taste earlier and refrigerate sooner; ferment at 60–65°F for more control |
| Under-sour / not sour enough after 4 weeks | Temperature too cold; salt too high; insufficient wild bacteria on vegetables | Warm up; verify salt ratio; allow more time |
| Slimy brine or slimy vegetables | Excess *Leuconostoc* production (normal in early fermentation); or contamination | Wait — early sliminess from *Leuconostoc* dextran typically resolves by week 2. If it persists with off-odor, discard |
| Off-flavor: alcohol/vinegar-like | Overly warm fermentation; too much yeast activity | Ferment cooler; taste sooner; refrigerate |
| Off-flavor: bitter | Kahm yeast contamination over extended fermentation | Skim kahm regularly; harvest ferment sooner |
| Off-flavor: sulfurous or rotten | Aerobic spoilage; insufficient salt; contamination | Discard; salt ratio was likely below 1.5% or vegetables were above brine |
| Salt crystallization on jar rim | Normal evaporation concentrating salt | Wipe rim; not a problem |
| Brine overflow | CO2 production in first week; overfilled jar | Place jar on a plate; reduce fill volume (leave 2-inch headspace) |
| Vegetables rising above brine | Fermentation CO2 lifts produce | Reweight; top with brine (5% for vegetables; matching brine for kimchi) |
| Kombucha too sweet | Not fermented long enough; temperature too cool | Ferment longer; increase temperature within safe range; increase starter tea ratio |
| Kombucha pH above 4.2 at day 14 | Insufficient starter; SCOBY in poor health; temperature too cool | Increase starter tea to 15–20% of batch; verify SCOBY is healthy; ferment at 75–78°F |
| Water kefir grains dissolving | Mineral deficiency; pH too acidic | Add mineral supplement (baking soda + cream of tartar); add dried fruit to each batch |
| Water kefir not fermenting (no bubbles at 48 hours) | Grains dormant after refrigerator storage; chlorinated water | Allow grains to "wake up" through 1–2 starter batches; switch to filtered water |
| Tepache not fermenting after 48 hours | Pineapple was scrubbed/washed with soap; organic yeast removed | Use organic pineapple washed with water only; add a small amount of active water kefir or raw kombucha to kickstart |

### 5.2 When to Discard vs. When to Continue

**Always discard**:
- Any ferment with visible fuzzy mold on vegetables or below the brine line
- Any kombucha SCOBY with fuzzy mold growth
- Any ferment with a strongly putrid, rotten, or fecal odor (distinct from the expected sour/acidic smell)
- Any ferment where vegetables have turned pink or an unexpected color (not from red cabbage or beet pigments)
- Any batch with slimy texture that persists after 2 weeks and is accompanied by off-odors

**Safe to skim and continue**:
- Kahm yeast (white/cream, flat, not fuzzy) on brine surface — skim, ensure submersion, continue
- Brine overflow — wipe jar, place on plate, continue
- Cloudy brine — this is normal; do not discard

**Safe to adjust and continue**:
- Under-salted early ferment (salt slightly below target, no off-flavors) — add brine to compensate; continue
- Ferment moving too slowly at cool temperature — move to warmer location

## Section 6: Storage, Scaling, and Integration

### 6.1 Long-Term Storage Management

**Refrigerator storage**: The standard method for finished fermented vegetables. At 35–38°F (2–3°C), active lacto-fermentation slows to near-zero. Quality is maintained for:
- Sauerkraut: 9 months (UMN Extension)
- Fermented pickles: 6 months (texture softens progressively)
- Kimchi: 3–6 months (flavor intensifies with age)
- Fermented hot sauce: 6–12 months
- Kombucha: 2–4 weeks after primary fermentation; bottled with second fermentation 2–4 weeks
- Water kefir: 1–2 weeks refrigerated after fermentation

**Freezing fermented vegetables**: Possible but changes texture significantly. Sauerkraut freezes acceptably — it is already shredded, and texture degradation is less noticeable. Whole pickles and kimchi become limp after freezing. Probiotic cultures are partially killed by freezing (approximately 50% survive). UMN Extension confirms freezing as a valid storage option with caveat of texture change.

**Canning finished ferments**: NCHFP provides tested water bath canning times for finished sauerkraut. This extends storage to pantry-stable 12–18 months but eliminates live cultures. Use this method for large harvests that exceed refrigeration capacity:
- Hot pack sauerkraut and juices: pints 10 min, quarts 15 min (at 0–1,000 ft elevation)
- Raw pack: pints 20 min, quarts 25 min (at 0–1,000 ft elevation)
- Adjust for altitude per NCHFP altitude tables

### 6.2 Container Rotation and Batch Management

**Managing multiple batches**: A well-organized fermentation rotation ensures continuous supply without overflow. A practical 4-jar rotation for a household consuming 1 quart sauerkraut per week:
- Jar 1: Just started fermenting (week 1)
- Jar 2: Actively fermenting (week 2–3)
- Jar 3: Ready; moved to refrigerator this week
- Jar 4: In refrigerator; 50% consumed

**Labeling**: Always label jars with the start date, contents, and salt percentage. Fermentation vessels in the back of the refrigerator are easily forgotten. Label with masking tape and marker on the side (not lid, which is hard to read on a shelf).

**FIFO (first in, first out)**: Consume oldest refrigerated ferments first. Check jar contents monthly. A jar that has been in the refrigerator for longer than its expected shelf life should be inspected carefully before consuming — look for kahm yeast growth, color changes, or off-odors.

### 6.3 Scaling Production

**Scaling calculations**: All salt ratios scale linearly. If the standard recipe calls for 2% salt by weight and you are using 10 lb of cabbage:
- 10 lb = 4,540 g
- 2% of 4,540 g = 90.8 g salt ≈ 6 tablespoons canning salt

**Vessel sizing at scale**:

| Weekly production goal | Vessel needed | Approximate active batches |
|-----------------------|--------------|--------------------------|
| 1 quart/week | 2× 1-gallon jars | 2 (one active, one just started) |
| 2 quarts/week | 2× 1-gallon jars or 1× 2-gallon crock | 2–3 active |
| 1 gallon/week | 1× 5-gallon crock or 4× 1-gallon jars | 1 crock rolling; or 4-jar rotation |
| Above 5 gallons/week | Multiple 5-gallon crocks or dedicated fermentation crock sets | Continuous rotation; consider canning surplus |

**Sourcing considerations for large scale**: At 5+ gallons per week, purchasing cabbage whole and shredding in bulk is most economical. A food processor with a shredding blade processes 10 lb of cabbage in under 10 minutes. Large ceramic crocks (5-gallon) are expensive new ($80–200) but are frequently available secondhand.

### 6.4 Integration with Wave 1 Preservation Methods

The Wave 1 food preservation guide covers canning and dehydration. Fermentation integrates with these methods in several practical patterns:

**Pattern 1 — Overflow management**: Ferment what cannot be canned in time. When a vegetable harvest exceeds canning capacity, start a fermentation batch immediately (same-day; no processing required) while canning batches are processed over following days.

**Pattern 2 — Extend refrigerator life with acidity**: Fermented vegetables, by their low pH, are more resistant to spoilage than raw vegetables in the refrigerator. A 3% brine-stored ferment still in the jar at pH 3.5 has excellent microbial stability even if the refrigerator temperature fluctuates.

**Pattern 3 — Hybrid preservation for grid-down conditions**: In a grid-down scenario, fermentation (no power required to produce) combined with canning of finished ferments (requires fuel to heat water bath) provides both probiotic nutrition and ambient shelf storage. Ferment → consume fresh for weeks → can the remainder for months.

**Pattern 4 — Flavor complementarity**: The sour, acidic flavor of fermented vegetables balances rich, starchy, or fat-heavy foods in ways that make seasonal eating more sustainable. Sauerkraut with beans; kimchi with rice and eggs; fermented hot sauce on virtually anything. This is not an abstract point — it directly affects whether people actually consume their food preservation outputs.

**Decision framework — ferment or can?**:

```
Is the food naturally high-acid (tomatoes with added acid, fruits, jams)?
  → Yes: Water bath canning is safe and produces pantry-stable product
  → No, it's a vegetable:
      Is it shredded, sliced, or small-cut?
        → Yes: Fermentation is simpler; produces live cultures; consider fermenting
        → No, it's whole or large pieces:
            Do you have refrigeration for 6 months?
              → Yes: Fermentation (brine method) works well
              → No: Pressure can for ambient shelf stability
      Do you need pantry-stable (not refrigerated) for 12+ months?
        → Yes: Pressure can (or can finished ferment)
        → No: Ferment and refrigerate
```
