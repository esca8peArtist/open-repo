# Sanitation and Hygiene Systems for Off-Grid and Low-Resource Settings

## Overview and Purpose

Sanitation — the safe management of human excreta, solid waste, and hygiene water — is the single largest environmental health determinant in low-resource and off-grid settings. Globally, 3.5 billion people lack safely managed sanitation (WHO/UNICEF JMP 2022). Unsafe excreta disposal drives diarrhoeal disease, cholera, typhoid, hepatitis A, and soil-transmitted helminths. The WHO 2018 Sanitation and Health Guidelines estimate that improving basic sanitation reduces diarrhoeal disease incidence by 36–48% and that handwashing with soap alone reduces diarrhoea by 23–40%.

This guide covers the five most critical sanitation domains for off-grid and low-resource settings: pit latrines, composting toilets, handwashing facilities, solid waste management, and emergency hygiene response. Each section is structured around practical implementation — siting, construction, operation, and failure-mode troubleshooting — drawing on WHO, UNHCR, Practical Action, Sphere, and peer-reviewed WASH engineering literature.

**What this guide covers:**
1. Pit latrines: design types, siting rules, construction steps, maintenance, and failure modes
2. Composting toilets: aerobic and anaerobic systems, carbon management, pathogen inactivation
3. Handwashing facilities: low-water designs, soap sourcing, integration with sanitation infrastructure
4. Solid waste management: sorting, organic composting, safe incineration, hazardous waste, vermin control
5. Emergency hygiene: cholera and flood-response protocols, water treatment, vector control, community procedures

## Section 1: Pit Latrines

Pit latrines are the most widely deployed off-grid sanitation technology globally, used by approximately 1.8 billion people (Sphere 2018). They are low-cost, require no water connection, and — when correctly sited and constructed — represent a safe and durable solution for rural and peri-urban settings. The critical variables are siting relative to groundwater, structural integrity of the superstructure and slab, and a maintenance discipline that prevents premature filling and vector breeding.

### 1.1 Design Options

**Simple Pit Latrine (SPL)**

The baseline design: a covered pit, a slab with a squatting hole, and a superstructure for privacy. Cost at 2026 prices: USD 30–120 depending on materials and labor. Lifespan when correctly dimensioned: 5–15 years.

- Pit dimensions: **1.0–1.5 m diameter** or **1.0 m × 1.0 m** square; **2.0–3.0 m depth** (never deeper than 0.5 m above the maximum seasonal water table)
- Effective pit volume: calculated at 0.04–0.06 m³ per user per year; a family of 6 needs approximately 0.3 m³/year
- Slab: minimum 75 mm reinforced concrete, or pre-cast ferrocement; squat-hole cover required to block flies
- Superstructure: local materials (timber, brick, corrugated iron) sufficient; key requirement is a door that closes and privacy for women and girls

**Ventilated Improved Pit (VIP) Latrine**

The VIP latrine, developed by Peter Morgan and colleagues in Zimbabwe in the 1970s (Morgan 2004), addresses the two principal user complaints of simple pit latrines: odor and flies. The design exploits a vertical vent pipe to create airflow that draws odors out of the pit rather than through the squat hole.

Key VIP design parameters:
- Vent pipe: **minimum 150 mm diameter** (PVC or galvanized steel); **height 500 mm above superstructure roofline** to create thermal draft
- Fly screen: **fiberglass mesh at top of vent pipe** (1.5 mm aperture); flies breed in the dark pit, fly toward light, exit the vent pipe, and are trapped by the screen
- Superstructure interior: **dark** — the darkness directs flies toward the lit vent pipe rather than the squat hole
- Squat hole: covered with a lid when not in use
- Two-pit VIP variant: uses alternating pits, allowing one to be sealed and composted while the other is active

Practical Action (2015) cites VIP latrines as reducing fly nuisance by 80–90% versus simple pit latrines and achieving 95%+ user satisfaction in Zimbabwe field trials.

**Double-Pit (Alternating) Latrine**

The double-pit design — recommended by Sphere (2018) and UN-Habitat (2008) for permanent settlement use — alternates between two pits, each sized for 2–3 years of use. When the active pit is nearly full, the inlet is switched to the second pit. The full pit is sealed and undergoes 1–2 years of anaerobic stabilization before the contents can be safely removed.

Configuration:
- Two pits of identical dimensions, positioned 2.0–3.0 m apart
- Shared superstructure with a divider wall or separate offset structures
- Switchable inlet slab or dividing flap
- Sealed full pit: no oxygen entry, anaerobic pathogen die-off over **12–24 months at 25°C** (Niwagaba et al. 2009)

The double-pit design dramatically extends the service life of the structure and produces a sanitized, soil-like material that can be used as soil conditioner (not direct food contact). See Section 2.4 for pathogen inactivation timelines.

### 1.2 Siting Rules

Incorrect siting is the primary cause of groundwater contamination from pit latrines. The contamination pathway is direct: pathogen-laden leachate migrates through soil to a water source. The following rules are grounded in the WHO (2018) guidance and the Cairncross and Feachem (1993) engineering standard.

**Minimum horizontal setback distances:**

| Water source type | Minimum setback |
|---|---|
| Unlined hand-dug well | **30 m** (WHO minimum); **50 m** recommended |
| Lined/cased borehole | **50 m** minimum |
| Spring (unprotected) | **50 m** minimum |
| River or stream | **30 m** minimum |
| Rainwater tank (surface) | **10 m** minimum |
| Household garden (root vegetables) | **5 m** minimum |

**The WHO recommends a default of 50 m from any groundwater source** where soil type and water table are not precisely known. This is the value to use in the field absent a formal hydrogeological assessment.

**Slope and drainage rules:**
- Pits must be positioned **downslope** from water sources whenever possible
- On sloped terrain, a pit positioned upslope from a well can contaminate it even at distances exceeding 50 m if the soil is highly permeable (sandy or gravelly)
- Never site a pit in a seasonal flood zone or drainage depression — flooding re-suspends pathogens and can contaminate wells over a wide area

**Soil permeability test (simple field method):**
1. Dig a test hole 300 mm × 300 mm to the intended pit base depth
2. Fill with water and allow to drain (pre-soak, 24 hours)
3. Refill and time how long 300 mm of water takes to drain
4. Percolation rate (mm/hour):
   - < 15 mm/hour: low permeability (clay), seepage minimal, setback 30 m may suffice
   - 15–50 mm/hour: medium (loam), standard 50 m setback applies
   - > 50 mm/hour: high permeability (sandy/gravel), **increase setback to 100 m** or consider a sealed vault toilet
5. If bedrock is within 1.0 m of surface, pit latrine is not appropriate — use a raised sealed vault system

**Worked example: siting on a sloped rural plot**

Scenario: A household with a hand-dug well (uncased) at elevation 105 m, main dwelling at 104 m, and a slope running north at 2% grade. Available land extends to 30 m south (downslope, elevation 103 m) and 15 m east (crossslope).

Analysis:
- South position (30 m downslope): adequate distance but 30 m barely meets WHO minimum for uncased well; soil test needed; if percolation > 15 mm/hour, increase to 50 m
- East position (15 m crossslope): insufficient — on moderate permeability soil, lateral transport over 15 m is feasible; rejected
- Verdict: site at 50 m south if plot allows; if plot constraint limits to 30 m, line the pit bottom with 150 mm of clay or use a closed-bottom vault

### 1.3 Construction Steps

The following procedure is adapted from Practical Action (2012) and is appropriate for a VIP or double-pit latrine.

**Step 1: Mark and excavate**
- Mark pit footprint with pegs and string
- Excavate pit by hand (preferred) or machine to target depth
- Shore loose walls with timber or stone during excavation to prevent collapse
- Keep excavated spoil a minimum of 500 mm from pit edge

**Step 2: Pit lining**
- Line the bottom 0.5–1.0 m of pit walls with mortared stone or brick to prevent collapse
- Leave the lower 1.5 m unlined to allow leachate to infiltrate (unless sealed vault required)
- If high-permeability soil: line full depth with mortared brick but leave 150 mm weep-holes at base

**Step 3: Slab casting**
- Form slab on flat ground using smooth formwork
- Reinforce with 8 mm steel bar at 200 mm spacing in both directions
- Concrete mix: 1 part cement : 2 parts sand : 3 parts aggregate, water-cement ratio 0.45
- Squat hole: 200 mm × 280 mm oval; smooth edges to prevent fouling; position over pit, not at slab center
- Cure for minimum 7 days before installation; 28 days for full strength
- Handle holes: cast two 40 mm handle holes to allow removal for emptying

**Step 4: Vent pipe installation (VIP)**
- Install vent pipe vertically adjacent to pit, connecting to pit interior through wall aperture at slab level
- Seal junction with cement mortar
- Cap with fly screen; check screen is intact before use and annually thereafter

**Step 5: Superstructure**
- Foundation: 200 mm strip footing, mortared stone or concrete
- Walls: locally available materials; minimum height 2.0 m for dignity; roof overhang to shed rain from slab
- Door: hung to close automatically (spring hinge or weighted rope) to exclude flies
- Ensure interior is dark (no windows) for VIP design; light-colored interior increases fly nuisance in VIPs

**Step 6: Covering the squat hole**
- Provide a snug-fitting lid (wood, concrete, or plastic) for the squat hole
- Lid must be replaced after each use — this is the single most important behavioral requirement

### 1.4 Maintenance and Emptying

**Routine maintenance (weekly/monthly):**
- Sprinkle one cup of dry ash, lime, or dry soil into the pit after each use to reduce odors and slow fly breeding
- Inspect slab for cracks annually; repair promptly
- Inspect vent pipe screen; replace if torn
- Keep superstructure door functional

**Monitoring pit fill level:**
- Check pit fill level every 6 months using a marked stick
- Begin planning alternatives or emptying when pit is **2/3 full**
- A pit that reaches 90% full before action is taken risks structural failure and overflow

**Pit emptying methods:**

| Method | Equipment | Appropriate when |
|---|---|---|
| Manual emptying (gulper pump) | Gulper hand pump, 200 L drum | Pits accessible, no heavy equipment |
| Motorized vacuum tanker | Vacuum tanker truck | Urban/peri-urban, accessible road |
| Dig-and-cover (pit full, abandon) | Excavator or hand | Single-pit, end of service life |
| Double-pit rotation | None (passive) | Double-pit design, planned rotation |

The ICRC (2010) manual and WaterAid (2021) guidance both recommend the **gulper pump** as the best low-cost, low-infrastructure option for rural pit emptying. A gulper pump costs approximately USD 25–50 to fabricate locally and can empty a 2 m³ pit in 4–8 hours with two operators.

**Post-emptying pathogen safety**: Fresh pit contents (< 12 months in double-pit) contain viable pathogens. Treat all fresh sludge as hazardous material. Bury in a dedicated trench at 0.5 m depth, **20 m from any water source**. Double-pit contents aged > 12 months at > 20°C are substantially safer but should still be buried, not applied directly to food crops.

### 1.5 Failure Modes and Troubleshooting

| Problem | Likely cause | Solution |
|---|---|---|
| Strong odor from squat hole | Lid not replaced; no vent pipe | Enforce lid use; install/repair vent pipe |
| Flies emerging from squat hole | Lid not replaced; vent screen torn | Enforce lid use; replace fly screen |
| Pit filling faster than expected | More users than designed; water entry | Reduce users; check for roof runoff entering pit; inspect slab seals |
| Slab cracking or subsidence | Poor concrete mix; inadequate reinforcement; soil collapse | Evacuate immediately; re-excavate and recast with correct mix |
| Water in pit (flooded) | High water table; seasonal flooding | Abandon pit; elevate new pit on raised platform or use sealed vault |
| Pit contents overflowing | No maintenance monitoring | Emergency: dig soakaway trench 10 m away; long-term: design double-pit |
| Collapse of pit walls | Unlined pit in sandy soil | Shore with timber immediately; reline with brick |

## Section 2: Composting Toilets

Composting toilets process human excreta through aerobic or anaerobic biological activity, producing a stable, humus-like end product that can be safely returned to soil — closing the nutrient loop instead of disposing of it as waste. The EcoSan (Ecological Sanitation) framework, developed by the GTZ EcoSan Programme and documented extensively by the Eawag/SANDEC Compendium (Tilley et al. 2014), treats human waste as a resource: a source of nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, and organic matter.

### 2.1 Aerobic versus Anaerobic Systems

**Aerobic composting toilets** maintain oxygen availability in the composting chamber through aeration — either forced (electric fan) or passive (stack-effect vent). Aerobic conditions support faster decomposition, higher temperatures (**50–70°C** in active pile zones), and more complete pathogen inactivation. They require active carbon management and regular inspection.

**Anaerobic (sealed vault) systems** process waste in the absence of oxygen. Decomposition is slower, temperatures remain ambient, and the primary pathogen inactivation mechanism is time — pathogen die-off over 6–24 months. Anaerobic systems are simpler to construct, require less management attention, but need longer treatment times for pathogen safety.

**Selection criteria:**

| Factor | Aerobic preferred | Anaerobic preferred |
|---|---|---|
| Manager skill level | Higher skill available | Minimal management capacity |
| Carbon material availability | Sawdust/leaves plentiful | Carbon materials scarce |
| Time to safe product | Need product in < 6 months | 12+ months acceptable |
| Climate | Warm, dry | Cold or wet climates |
| Scale | Single household | Multiple households |

### 2.2 Active versus Passive Systems

**Active systems** use mechanical components — fans, heaters, rotating drums — to accelerate decomposition. NSF/ANSI 41 certified commercial units (e.g., Clivus Multrum, Sun-Mar) are active systems with electric heating elements that maintain 50–60°C, achieving pasteurization in days rather than months. Cost: USD 1,500–5,000+ for certified residential units.

**Passive systems** rely only on natural airflow and microbial activity. The double-vault design from EcoSan is the most field-proven passive approach: two chambers alternate, with one chamber composting while the other accepts fresh material. Passive systems cost USD 50–500 to construct with local materials and are appropriate for low-resource settings.

### 2.3 Urine Diversion (UDD Toilets)

Urine-diverting dry toilets (UDD or UDDT) separate urine from feces at the point of collection. The rationale: urine contains **90% of the nitrogen and 50–80% of the phosphorus** excreted by humans (EcoSan Club 2003), is essentially sterile in healthy individuals, and can be safely stored and applied as fertilizer after 1 month (WHO guidelines). Feces, which carry the pathogenic load, are diverted to a composting chamber as a dry, low-volume material.

**UDD toilet design:**
- Pedestal with a urine collection pan (front) and a feces receiving chamber (rear)
- Urine drain to a sealed storage tank (20 L/person/month); apply to soil at 1:8 dilution with water
- Feces chamber: after each deposit, cover with one scoop of **dry carbon material** (sawdust, dry soil, ash, rice husks) to absorb moisture, reduce odor, and maintain the C:N ratio
- Two chambers alternated as with double-pit design

UDD toilets reduce the volume of material entering the composting chamber by 80–90%, dramatically shortening treatment times and reducing management burden.

### 2.4 Carbon Management

Carbon management is the single most critical operational factor for composting toilet performance. The target carbon-to-nitrogen ratio for effective aerobic composting is **20:1 to 30:1** (Jenkins 2019; Tilley et al. 2014). Human feces have a C:N ratio of approximately 6:1 — highly nitrogen-rich. Carbon additions shift the ratio upward.

**Carbon material comparison:**

| Material | C:N ratio | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Sawdust (fresh) | 400:1 | Excellent; add in small quantities (one handful per deposit) |
| Dry straw | 80:1 | Good; needs to be chopped small |
| Dry leaves | 60:1 | Good; widely available |
| Rice husks | 120:1 | Good; slow to decompose, improves aeration |
| Wood ash | 25:1 | Moderate; also raises pH, which inhibits pathogens |
| Dry soil | ~12:1 | Low but still useful; reduces odor |
| Fresh grass clippings | 15:1 | Avoid — too nitrogen-rich, promotes anaerobic conditions |

**Worked example: dosing calculation**

A household of 4 adults deposits approximately 150 g of feces per person per day (total 600 g/day). Feces C:N ≈ 6:1.

Target C:N = 25:1.

Carbon needed = (25 - 6) / 6 × 600 g/day × (carbon fraction of sawdust) ≈ 1,900 g raw sawdust per day (using dry sawdust, 80% organic matter, 400:1 C:N). In practice, this works out to approximately one large coffee mug (200 mL) of dry sawdust per deposit. This matches the field recommendation in Jenkins (2019) and GTZ EcoSan (2006).

**If carbon material is scarce:** wood ash is the fallback. Apply one tablespoon of ash per deposit. Ash does not provide much carbon but raises pH above 9.0, which suppresses pathogens and reduces odor while reducing nitrogen losses.

### 2.5 Pathogen Inactivation Timelines

Pathogen inactivation is the safety-critical requirement. The following timelines are drawn from Niwagaba et al. (2009), Cairncross and Feachem (1993), and the WHO 2018 Sanitation and Health guidelines:

| Pathogen class | Inactivation conditions | Time to safe levels |
|---|---|---|
| Ascaris eggs (most resistant) | 50°C aerobic | **7 days** at sustained 50°C |
| Ascaris eggs | Ambient (25°C) anaerobic | **6 months minimum** |
| Ascaris eggs | Ambient (15°C) anaerobic | **12 months minimum** |
| E. coli / fecal coliforms | 50°C aerobic | **< 1 hour** |
| E. coli / fecal coliforms | Ambient (25°C) anaerobic | **3–6 months** |
| Salmonella spp. | 55°C aerobic | **< 30 minutes** |
| Vibrio cholerae | 50°C aerobic | **< 30 minutes** |
| Hepatitis A virus | 70°C aerobic | **< 1 minute** |
| Hepatitis A virus | Ambient (25°C) anaerobic | **3–6 months** |
| Helminth ova (general) | 55°C aerobic | **1–7 days** |

**Key design rule**: For passive composting toilets in warm climates (**> 20°C average**), a minimum **6-month retention time** in a sealed chamber provides substantial pathogen reduction. For cold climates (< 15°C), extend to **12 months or longer**. The double-vault design achieves this automatically when chambers are rotated annually.

**The 25°C/6-month threshold** (bolded because it is the standard field benchmark from WHO 2018 and Sphere 2018) is sufficient for helminths, coliforms, and most enteric viruses but does not guarantee Ascaris egg inactivation at ambient temperatures. If Ascaris infection is prevalent locally, either extend to **12 months** or achieve **50°C** through hot composting of the finished chamber contents (see Wave 1 Composting guide for hot compost procedure).

### 2.6 Troubleshooting

| Problem | Likely cause | Solution |
|---|---|---|
| Strong ammonia odor | Too little carbon; too much urine mixing | Increase carbon addition; improve urine diversion |
| Flies in chamber | Lid not closed; carbon layer not applied | Apply carbon after every deposit; seal lid |
| Wet or slimy contents | Excess moisture; insufficient carbon | Add extra sawdust or ash; check for water ingress |
| No decomposition (contents staying wet) | Anaerobic conditions; too moist | Reduce moisture; add high-C material; aerate manually with a stick |
| Vault filling too fast | Insufficient carbon absorption; too many users | Audit carbon-to-deposit ratio; reduce users per vault |
| Finished compost has strong odor | Insufficient treatment time | Return to vault for additional 3 months before use |

## Section 3: Handwashing Facilities

Handwashing with soap is one of the most cost-effective public health interventions available. The Fewtrell and Colford (2004) World Bank meta-analysis found handwashing interventions reduce diarrhoeal disease incidence by **23–40%**. WHO (2019) notes that handwashing before food preparation and after latrine use interrupts the F-route transmission pathways (Fingers, Fluids, Food, Fields, Flies) that account for the majority of fecal-oral disease transmission.

The practical challenge in off-grid settings is providing a handwashing station that is (a) physically located where it will be used, (b) water-conserving, (c) equipped with soap or soap substitute at all times, and (d) maintained by the household without external supply chains.

### 3.1 Location Rules

**The handwashing station must be within 5 m of the latrine.** This is the WEDC (2012) and Sphere (2018) standard. Stations more than 10 m away are rarely used consistently. The ideal position is immediately outside the latrine entrance, so the behavioral sequence — exit latrine, wash hands — requires no detour.

Secondary handwashing stations should be positioned at:
- The kitchen entry or food preparation area
- The water collection point (if separate from the dwelling)
- Near infant care areas

### 3.2 Low-Water Station Designs

Standard tapwater handwashing uses 1–2 liters per wash. In water-scarce settings, purpose-designed low-flow stations can achieve adequate hand hygiene with **0.5–1.0 liters per wash** (Howard and Bartram 2003).

**Tippy Tap**

The tippy tap is a foot-operated, gravity-fed handwashing station requiring no plumbing. It is the most widely deployed low-cost design in Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia.

Construction materials:
- Two forked sticks, 1.0 m tall, driven into the ground 0.5 m apart
- One horizontal crossbar
- A 1–1.5 L plastic bottle with a small hole (2–3 mm) in the cap — this is the water reservoir
- The bottle is suspended from the crossbar by a rope, offset at 30° from vertical
- A foot pedal (stick) connects via rope to the bottle: pressing the pedal tips the bottle forward, producing a narrow stream

Operation: User presses foot pedal, water streams from bottle onto hands. Release pedal, water stops. Total water use per wash: **0.2–0.4 liters**. Soap or ash is hung adjacent on a small platform or tied rope.

Cost: near-zero (reused materials). Effective lifespan: 6–12 months for a PET bottle before UV degradation; sticks are replaceable.

WEDC (2012) trials found tippy tap use rates of 72–85% when installed adjacent to latrines, versus 30–45% for bucket-and-cup stations at the same locations.

**Foot-Pump Station**

A more robust option for institutional settings (schools, health posts): a foot-actuated pump delivers water from a sealed 20–50 L container through a nozzle. No hands touch the water source during washing. Construction cost: USD 15–40 using PVC pipe and a rubber foot bellows. Instructions available in the WaterAid (2021) technical brief.

**Leaky Tin / Gravity Drip**

A 5–20 L container (tin or jerry can) with a small hole in the base, hung from a nail or post, produces a continuous slow drip. Less water-efficient than a tippy tap (wastes water between washes) but requires no foot coordination — useful for young children and the elderly. Drip rate should be **30–50 mL/minute** (test by timing fill of a 100 mL cup). Larger holes waste water.

### 3.3 Soap Sourcing and Alternatives

**Commercial bar soap** is the WHO-recommended handwashing agent and is effective against the bacteria and viruses responsible for fecal-oral disease transmission (E. coli, cholera, rotavirus, hepatitis A). Any soap — regardless of antibacterial claims — is effective because the mechanical action of lathering disrupts pathogen cell envelopes.

**Cost**: Bar soap in low-resource settings costs approximately USD 0.20–0.50 per 100 g bar; a family of 6 using soap twice daily (post-latrine and pre-food) consumes approximately 200 g/month.

**Soap alternatives when commercial soap is unavailable:**

| Alternative | Effectiveness | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Ash (wood or charcoal) | Moderate | Raises pH, removes some pathogens; mechanically abrasive; widely available |
| Sand (fine, clean) | Low-moderate | Mechanical only; no chemical action |
| Plant ash lye | High | Wood ash + water produces a potassium hydroxide solution (pH 10–12); effective; caustic if concentrated |
| Homemade soap (saponification) | High (if correctly made) | Requires animal fat/plant oil + lye; see Wave 2 Domain 4 for plant-based soap-making guidance |

Ash is the most practical backup when soap is unavailable. The WEDC recommendation (2012) is that ash be offered as the handwashing agent in preference to no agent, particularly for critical hand-hygiene moments (post-defecation, pre-food preparation).

**Critical hand-hygiene moments** (WHO 2019, Sphere 2018):
1. After using the latrine (post-defecation)
2. Before preparing food
3. Before eating
4. After handling infants or young children's feces (nappy/diaper change)
5. After contact with soil or animal feces

### 3.4 Integration with Latrine Infrastructure

The handwashing station should share infrastructure with the latrine where possible:
- Shared superstructure: a handwashing alcove attached to the latrine exterior wall reduces construction cost
- Shared drainage: wastewater from handwashing (greywater) must not pool near the latrine — route to a soakpit **at least 3 m** from the pit latrine to avoid creating a separate contamination source
- Shared water supply: if a gravity tank is used for the latrine cistern (in pour-flush variants), a branch line to the handwashing station reduces daily water-carrying burden

**Note on wastewater from handwashing**: Latrine wash water is low-risk greywater (see Wave 0 Domain 3: Greywater Management). A simple soakpit (0.5 m × 0.5 m × 0.5 m gravel-filled pit) is sufficient for disposal in most soil types.

## Section 4: Solid Waste Management

Solid waste in off-grid and low-resource settings is often managed through open dumping, which creates significant public health hazards: vector breeding (rodents, flies, mosquitoes), surface water contamination through leachate, and respiratory hazards from open burning. The WHO 2021 Compendium and UNEP 2012 small-scale incineration guidelines provide a risk-based framework: the goal is not perfection but a systematic reduction in the highest-risk waste streams.

### 4.1 Waste Characterization and Sorting

Effective waste management begins with sorting at the household level. In most rural low-resource settings, the waste stream is approximately:
- 60–70% organic matter (food scraps, agricultural residues)
- 10–20% paper and cardboard
- 5–10% plastic
- 2–5% glass and metal
- 1–3% hazardous (batteries, medical sharps, chemicals)

**Sort categories for household management:**

| Category | Disposition |
|---|---|
| Organic (food, plant matter) | Compost or animal feed |
| Paper/cardboard | Compost (as brown material), fuel, or recycle |
| Clean plastic | Reuse, recycle collection (where available) |
| Metal | Reuse, sell to scrap collector |
| Glass | Reuse, bury in dedicated pit |
| Hazardous | Sealed container pending proper disposal |
| Medical/sharps | Sharps container; see Section 4.4 |

A dedicated sorting area near the kitchen — even just labelled bins or baskets — increases sorting rates significantly. Murray et al. (2011) found that households with designated containers for organic versus non-organic waste composted 40% more material than households with instructions but no containers.

### 4.2 Organic Waste Composting

Organic solid waste from the kitchen and garden should be composted rather than buried or burned. This is covered in detail in the Wave 1 Composting guide; the key integration points for solid waste management are:

- **All kitchen scraps** (vegetable peelings, coffee grounds, eggshells, fruit waste) go directly to the compost pile — target **C:N of 20:1 to 30:1** (see Wave 1 Composting for detailed ratios)
- **Meat, fish, and dairy** must be excluded from open compost piles (vermin attraction, pathogen risk); bury these at 300 mm depth, or compost in a sealed bokashi system (anaerobic fermentation with inoculant)
- **Livestock manure** (from goats, chickens, rabbits — see Wave 2 Domain 2: Small-Scale Livestock) is a high-value compost additive; add at no more than 30% by volume to avoid anaerobic conditions and odor

**Bokashi fermentation** for meat/dairy scraps:
1. Layer food scraps in a sealed, airtight bucket
2. Sprinkle commercial bokashi inoculant (dried wheat bran inoculated with Lactobacillus, yeast, and photosynthetic bacteria) between layers
3. Press down to exclude air; seal lid; drain liquid every 2–3 days (dilute 1:100 as fertilizer)
4. After 2 weeks sealed fermentation, bury the pre-digested material 200 mm deep in garden soil; decomposes fully within 2 weeks in soil

### 4.3 Incineration (Controlled Burn)

Open burning of mixed solid waste is a significant health hazard. The UNEP 2012 small-scale incineration guidelines identify dioxins and furans — released when chlorinated plastics (PVC) burn at low temperatures — as the primary concern, along with particulate matter and heavy metals from batteries and electronics.

**Never burn**: PVC plastic (grey pipes, vinyl, packaging labeled "3" or "V"), electronics, batteries, aerosol cans, painted wood, medical waste (biohazard)

**Safe to burn (dry conditions, high temperature)**: untreated wood, paper, cardboard, dry plant matter

**Simple pit incinerator construction** (for non-plastic solid waste only):
1. Excavate a pit 0.6 m diameter × 0.6 m depth
2. Line base and walls with a single course of brick (improves draft)
3. Install a steel mesh grate at 150 mm above pit base — this elevates fuel, improves airflow, and sustains higher temperatures
4. Burn only dry material in small batches; fire should be bright and hot (> 850°C at flame base) — black smoke indicates incomplete combustion and higher dioxin formation
5. Allow ash to cool completely before removing; ash can be used in the latrine (odor control) or as soil pH amendment
6. Inspect pit walls for cracks monthly; decommission when 2/3 full of ash; excavate new pit

**Burn barrel alternative**: A 200 L steel drum with 50 mm holes drilled at 100 mm intervals on the lower half provides good combustion for paper and cardboard. Place on three bricks for underdraft. Keep a bucket of water adjacent.

**UNEP (2012) key guidance**: Any incineration facility should be located **downwind** of dwellings (**50 m minimum** separation); never burn during wind conditions that carry smoke toward inhabited areas or water sources.

### 4.4 Hazardous Waste Handling

Hazardous household waste in low-resource settings typically includes:
- **Batteries** (dry cell, lead-acid): contain mercury, lead, cadmium — leach into groundwater when buried
- **Pesticide containers and residues**: highly toxic; persistent in soil
- **Medical sharps** (needles, lancets): puncture-wound injury and bloodborne pathogen transmission risk
- **Pharmaceuticals** (expired medicines)
- **Fluorescent lamps**: contain mercury (UNEP/WHO 2015 mercury release guidance)

**Management rules:**
- **Never bury or burn** batteries, fluorescent lamps, or pesticide containers
- Collect hazardous waste in labeled, sealed containers (old paint tins with lids, HDPE bottles with screw caps)
- Store in a dry location away from children, animals, and water sources
- Dispose of through the nearest municipal hazardous waste collection point; where none exists, bury in a **dedicated sealed pit** (lined with 150 mm of clay and capped with concrete) — this is a last resort, not a preferred option

**Medical sharps procedure:**
1. Use a puncture-resistant container (commercial sharps box or thick-walled plastic bottle with screw cap)
2. Do not recap needles; drop directly into container after use
3. When 3/4 full, seal permanently and deposit at the nearest health facility for medical waste disposal
4. Where no health facility disposal exists: incinerate in a small pit at > 800°C (flames, not smoldering); ashes may be buried; do not attempt to recycle or disassemble

**Pesticide container decontamination** before disposal (FAO triple-rinse protocol):
1. Empty container completely
2. Add 20% of container volume of clean water; shake for 30 seconds; pour into spray tank
3. Repeat twice (triple rinse)
4. Puncture container to prevent reuse
5. Bury in a clay-lined pit at 500 mm depth, away from water sources

### 4.5 Vermin Control

Vermin (rats, mice, flies, cockroaches) are vectors for disease and indicators of waste management failure. Control targets the three requirements of vermin: food, water, and shelter.

**Rodent control:**
- Store all food in hard-sided sealed containers (tins or HDPE buckets); never in woven sacks
- Clear organic waste within 24 hours — never leave food scraps exposed overnight
- Maintain a **3 m clearance zone** around the base of all structures (remove debris, vegetation, and stacked materials where rodents shelter)
- Compost piles: use a rodent-proof bin (hardware cloth at 6 mm mesh, bent 300 mm outward at base to prevent burrowing) if rodent pressure is high
- Traps (spring traps or live traps): position along wall runways where droppings are present; check daily; dispose of carcasses by burial at 300 mm depth

**Fly control:**
- Primary measure: latrine management (Section 1.5) — covered slab, VIP vent screen, no fecal matter exposed
- Organic waste: cover all exposed compost and kitchen waste; compost bins should have lids
- Burn ash or dry lime can be scattered over waste areas to reduce odor and fly attraction

**Cockroach control:**
- Seal cracks and gaps in kitchen walls and floors — cockroaches require a harborage gap of only 1.5 mm
- Food-grade diatomaceous earth scattered along wall/floor junctions is non-toxic and effective
- Remove cardboard and paper clutter — these are primary cockroach harborage materials

## Section 5: Emergency Hygiene — Cholera and Flood Response

Emergency conditions — epidemic disease, flooding, displacement — create sanitation crises by disrupting normal infrastructure, concentrating populations, and contaminating water sources simultaneously. The CDC (2023) cholera clinical guidelines and the Sphere 2018 WASH standards define the minimum response; the UNHCR Emergency Handbook (2022) and WaterAid (2021) emergency guide provide implementation protocols.

This section covers: water treatment, vector control, waste containment, and community protocols for the two most common WASH emergency scenarios: cholera outbreak and flood response.

### 5.1 Cholera Response

Cholera (Vibrio cholerae O1 and O139) spreads via the fecal-oral route through contaminated water and food. It can kill a healthy adult within hours through dehydration; case fatality rates in untreated outbreaks exceed 25–50%. With early rehydration, case fatality falls below 1% (CDC 2023; Luna 2021).

**Immediate response priorities (first 24 hours):**

1. **Confirm water source contamination** — assume all surface water and uncovered well water in the affected area is contaminated; switch to treated water immediately
2. **Establish oral rehydration** — begin ORS (oral rehydration solution) production and distribution; WHO formula: 1 liter clean water + 6 level teaspoons sugar + 0.5 teaspoon salt
3. **Isolate suspected cases** — separate latrines, bedding, and water vessels; all vomitus and diarrhea are highly infectious
4. **Chlorinate water supply** — target **0.5 mg/L free residual chlorine** at point of use (CDC recommendation); test with colorimetric chlorine test strip
5. **Establish handwashing stations** — at minimum one station per latrine used by symptomatic individuals; soap must be present

**Water treatment during cholera outbreak:**

| Method | Dose | Contact time | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Chlorine (sodium hypochlorite, 5%) | 2 drops per liter | 30 minutes | Standard; effective against V. cholerae |
| Chlorine (sodium hypochlorite, 5%) | 4 drops per liter | 30 minutes | Use if water is turbid or source unknown |
| Boiling | Full rolling boil | 1 minute at < 2,000 m elevation | Most reliable; fuel-intensive |
| Solar disinfection (SODIS) | Fill clear PET bottle | 6 hours (full sun) or 2 days (cloudy) | Low-cost; slow; cannot treat turbid water |
| Household water filter (ceramic/biosand) | Per manufacturer instructions | Depends on flow rate | Effective but slow; maintain wet to prevent bacterial regrowth |

Turbid water must be settled or filtered before chlorination — turbidity neutralizes chlorine. A simple sedimentation method: allow water to stand 30 minutes, then draw from the upper 2/3 of the container.

**Fecal waste containment during cholera outbreak:**

- **All fecal waste from symptomatic patients is extremely high-risk** — V. cholerae can survive 2–8 days in the environment
- If latrines are unavailable: provide plastic-lined collection containers (defecation buckets with lids); add chlorine solution (10% sodium hypochlorite diluted to 2%) to each container before use and after each deposit
- Waste must be buried immediately at **0.5 m depth** at a designated site **20 m from water sources**
- Soiled clothing and bedding: soak in 0.05% chlorine solution (dilute household bleach 1:100) for 30 minutes before washing
- Corpse management: all cholera fatalities must be buried (not cremated) or handled with full barrier precautions (gloves, apron, face shield); do not wash the body in shared water sources

**Community protocol — 5 essentials (adapted from Sphere 2018 and CDC 2023):**
1. Drink only treated water; do not drink from rivers, ponds, or unprotected wells
2. Wash hands with soap (or ash) after toilet use and before eating
3. Cook food fully; do not eat raw vegetables or fruit that cannot be peeled
4. Notify health workers immediately when watery diarrhea begins — do not delay seeking ORS
5. Do not share utensils, towels, or cups with symptomatic individuals

### 5.2 Flood Response

Flooding creates WASH emergencies through three simultaneous mechanisms: contamination of water sources (pit latrines overflow, wells are inundated), physical destruction of sanitation infrastructure, and displacement of populations into areas with inadequate facilities.

**Immediate water safety assessment (first 48 hours):**

- Inspect all wells for inundation; a flooded well must be assumed contaminated with fecal matter, agricultural chemicals, and debris
- **Well disinfection procedure** (CDC 2022):
  1. Remove all debris from well
  2. Allow well to equilibrate to normal water level (may take 24–48 hours after flood recedes)
  3. Pour chlorine solution into well to achieve **50 mg/L** in the well water (for a 1 m diameter well at 3 m depth, this is approximately 1 liter of 5% sodium hypochlorite)
  4. Draw water several times to distribute chlorine; leave for **24 hours** (well out of use during this period)
  5. Pump or draw water until chlorine odor dissipates (free residual < 5 mg/L); water is then safe with continued monitoring at **0.5 mg/L free residual**

- Rainwater tanks: inspect for overflow or backflow contamination; disinfect with chlorine as above at lower dose (10 mg/L)

**Latrine assessment after flooding:**

| Condition | Action |
|---|---|
| Pit latrine inundated but intact | Abandon; site new emergency latrine 20 m uphill; mark flooded pit with warning sign |
| Pit latrine slab cracked or collapsed | Barricade immediately; cover pit with soil |
| Double-pit latrine (one pit flooded) | Switch to second pit if not inundated; treat flooded pit as contaminated zone |
| VIP latrine structure intact, pit flooded | Close latrine; relocate; pump out pit after flood recedes if feasible |

**Emergency trench latrine (rapid deployment):**

When existing latrines are destroyed or inaccessible, a trench latrine can be dug by a community group in 2–4 hours:
- Dimensions: **1.5 m deep × 0.3 m wide × 1 m long per 20 users per day** (Sphere 2018 minimum standard)
- Cover: fresh-dug soil is sprinkled over deposits after each use
- Privacy: a tarpaulin or woven screen on three sides; segregate male/female sections
- Abandon when 2/3 full (approximately 300–400 mm from surface); cover with 150 mm of soil, mark with a stick, dig new section
- Minimum distance from water sources: **30 m**; position downslope

**Vector control in flood conditions:**

Flooding creates standing water that is prime mosquito breeding habitat. Aedes and Anopheles mosquito populations can surge within 3–7 days of flooding, elevating dengue and malaria transmission risk.

- **Eliminate standing water** within 100 m of dwellings within 72 hours of flood receding: overturn containers, drain ditches, fill low-lying areas with soil
- **Untippable containers** (water storage barrels, latrines): cover with tight-fitting lids or fine mesh (< 1.5 mm aperture)
- **Larval control** (where elimination not possible): apply Bacillus thuringiensis israelensis (Bti) granules to standing water — 5 g/m² surface area; non-toxic to humans, fish, and most non-target insects; effective within 24 hours
- **Sleeping protection**: hang insecticide-treated nets (ITNs) over sleeping areas; repair any tears
- **Household spraying**: pyrethroid-based insecticide applied to interior walls and ceilings provides 6–12 weeks of residual protection against indoor mosquitoes; apply per manufacturer label

**Solid waste in flood conditions:**

- Flood debris (mud, plant matter, household waste) is mixed with latrine content and animal waste — treat all flood debris as potentially contaminated
- Use gloves and boots when handling flood debris; wash hands immediately after
- Clear debris to a designated waste collection point; do not burn wet debris (high smoke, low temperature, dioxin risk)
- Medical waste, batteries, and chemicals that have been displaced by flood must be identified, collected, and isolated — these items may be found scattered in flood debris and represent a disproportionate hazard

### 5.3 Community Protocols and Hygiene Promotion

Technical infrastructure is necessary but not sufficient. Murray et al. (2011) and Jordaan et al. (2020) both find that WASH infrastructure without concurrent hygiene promotion achieves 30–50% of the health benefit of combined infrastructure-plus-behavior programs.

**Community-Led Total Sanitation (CLTS)** is a behavior change methodology developed and systematically reviewed by Jordaan et al. (2020) that triggers collective community action through facilitated sanitation mapping and "triggering" exercises. CLTS has achieved open-defecation-free (ODF) status in communities across Sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and Southeast Asia without subsidizing hardware.

**Key message framework for community hygiene promotion** (adapted from WHO WASH 2019 and UNICEF WASH Cluster 2020):

| Moment | Key message | Supporting behavior |
|---|---|---|
| Post-defecation | Wash hands with soap or ash — feces is the source | Station within 5 m of latrine |
| Pre-food preparation | Wash hands; wash raw vegetables with treated water | Station in kitchen area |
| Child feces management | Child feces are as dangerous as adult feces | Designated child toilet or potty; flush into latrine |
| Water storage | Collect treated water in a covered container; do not dip hands into it | Narrow-neck container; pour spout |
| Flood / emergency | Treat all water; increase handwashing frequency | Extra soap or ash distributed |

**Communication channels**: In oral-culture communities, radio, community meetings, and demonstration ("show, don't tell") outreach are more effective than written materials. WEDC (2012) and WaterAid (2021) both emphasize that the most effective hygiene promoters are community members who already practice good hygiene, not external health workers.

## Conclusion

Sanitation infrastructure in off-grid and low-resource settings does not require advanced technology — it requires correct application of well-tested engineering principles and consistent behavioral practices. The five systems covered in this guide address the full waste management pathway:

- **Pit latrines** contain and isolate fecal waste safely for 5–15 years when correctly sited (**50 m from groundwater sources**), constructed, and maintained
- **Composting toilets** close the nutrient loop by converting waste into a soil resource, requiring **6 months minimum at 25°C** or **12 months in cold climates** for pathogen safety
- **Handwashing stations** — even a tippy tap costing nothing to build — reduce diarrhoeal disease by 23–40% when positioned within 5 m of the latrine and stocked with soap
- **Solid waste management** through sorting, composting organics, and controlled burn of combustibles reduces vermin pressure and environmental contamination; **hazardous waste must never be burned or buried without isolation**
- **Emergency protocols** for cholera and flooding can prevent epidemic escalation: the key variables are immediate water chlorination (**0.5 mg/L free residual**), rapid deployment of containment latrines, and community messaging that drives behavior change in hours, not days

These systems are most effective as an integrated set. A latrine without a handwashing station fails to break the fecal-oral transmission chain at the hand-contact step. A handwashing station without a latrine eliminates one transmission route while leaving the primary source open. A composting toilet without correct carbon management produces a product that is unsafe to apply to soil. The integration of all five systems — aligned with the cross-domain guidance on water supply (Wave 0 Domain 2), greywater management (Wave 0 Domain 3), and composting principles (Wave 1 Composting) — produces a closed-loop sanitation system that substantially eliminates environmental pathogen transmission under normal conditions and maintains resilience under emergency conditions.
