# Home Food Preservation: Complete Practical Guide

## Critical Safety Statement

**Read this before beginning any canning project.**

Incorrect home canning can cause botulism, a life-threatening illness. *Clostridium botulinum* produces a toxin that is odorless and invisible — you cannot tell by looking at or smelling a jar whether it is contaminated. Even a small taste of food containing botulinum toxin can be fatal.

**The rules that prevent botulism**:
1. All processing times in this guide are taken verbatim from the USDA Complete Guide to Home Canning (2015 revision) as maintained by the National Center for Home Food Preservation (NCHFP). Do not reduce processing times, do not substitute recipes from untested sources, and do not add ingredients that lower acidity in water-bath recipes.
2. Low-acid foods (vegetables, beans, meat, poultry) must be pressure canned. Water bath canning cannot achieve the temperatures required to destroy *C. botulinum* spores in low-acid food. There is no safe substitute.
3. When in doubt, throw it out. If a jar shows any of these signs, discard without tasting: spurting liquid when opened, off or unusual odor, mold, unsealed lid, or any appearance of spoilage.

**Source of authority**: All processing times in Section 2 (water bath canning) and Section 3 (pressure canning) are sourced from the USDA Complete Guide to Home Canning, Agriculture Information Bulletin No. 539, Revised 2015, as maintained at nchfp.uga.edu. This is a public domain US government document.

## Overview

This guide covers four home food preservation methods:

1. **Water bath canning** — for high-acid foods: fruits, tomatoes (with added acid), jams, jellies, pickles
2. **Pressure canning** — for low-acid foods: vegetables, beans
3. **Lacto-fermentation** — for vegetables: sauerkraut, pickles, kimchi; no equipment required; no heating required
4. **Food dehydration** — for fruits, vegetables, herbs, jerky; requires a dehydrator or oven

Each method preserves food through a different mechanism. Understanding the mechanism determines which foods each method is safe for.

| Method | Preservation Mechanism | Best For | Not Safe For |
|--------|----------------------|----------|-------------|
| Water bath canning | Heat + acidity (pH < 4.6) kills mold, yeast, enzymes | High-acid foods: fruits, tomatoes+acid, jams, pickles | Low-acid vegetables, meat, poultry, fish |
| Pressure canning | High heat (240°F/116°C at 10 PSI) kills all pathogens | Any food; required for low-acid foods | Nothing excludes — but overkill for high-acid foods |
| Lacto-fermentation | Lactic acid lowers pH below 4.6; anaerobic environment | Most vegetables; high-salt tolerance foods | Foods with very low sugar content that can't sustain fermentation |
| Dehydration | Reduces water activity below 0.85; inhibits microbial growth | Fruits, vegetables, herbs, jerky | Foods requiring pH or heat for botulism safety (always combine with acid/heat for meat) |

## Section 1: Essential Equipment and Preparation

### 1.1 Canning Jars and Lids

**Use only Mason-type canning jars** (Ball, Kerr, or equivalent). These are specifically designed to withstand repeated thermal cycling. Do not reuse commercial food jars (pickle jars, mayonnaise jars) — they are not tempered for home canning and can crack or fail to seal under processing conditions.

**Jar sizes for common uses**:
- Half-pint (8 oz / 240 mL): jams, jellies, preserves
- Pint (16 oz / 475 mL): pickles, tomatoes, green beans, fruits
- Quart (32 oz / 950 mL): tomatoes, peaches, green beans, soups

**Lids**: Use two-piece metal canning lids (flat lid + screw band). The flat lid is single-use — do not reuse flat lids from previous canning batches. Screw bands are reusable indefinitely if not rusty or deformed.

**Before each use**:
1. Inspect jars for nicks, cracks, or chips on the rim — any defect prevents sealing; discard damaged jars
2. Wash jars in hot soapy water or dishwasher; rinse well
3. Keep jars hot until filling (in simmering water or a warm oven at 200°F); prevents thermal shock when hot food is added
4. Wash lids and bands in hot soapy water; do not boil lids (modern lids do not need pre-softening)

**Jar sterility for jams and jellies**: For jams and jellies processed for fewer than 10 minutes (standard 5-minute jams), jars must be sterile before filling. To sterilize: submerge empty jars in boiling water for 10 minutes before filling. If processing time is 10 minutes or more, pre-sterilization is not required (the processing itself sterilizes).

### 1.2 Water Bath Canner

A water bath canner is a large pot (typically 21–33 quart capacity) with a fitted rack and lid. The rack holds jars off the bottom so water circulates underneath.

**DIY water bath canner**: Any large stockpot can work if it is deep enough for jars to be covered by 1–2 inches of water with room for vigorous boiling. A small round cake rack or folded kitchen towel in the bottom substitutes for the canning rack.

**Do not use** aluminum water bath canners if your water is very soft (below 50 ppm hardness) — soft water reacts with aluminum. For most households, this is not an issue.

### 1.3 Pressure Canner

A pressure canner is not the same as a pressure cooker. Pressure canners are larger (16 quart minimum capacity per USDA) and have:
- A locking lid with a gasket
- A pressure gauge (dial-type or weighted-type)
- A vent pipe / petcock for releasing steam
- A safety overpressure plug

**Two types of pressure gauges**:
- **Dial-gauge canners**: Show exact PSI reading. Must be tested for accuracy annually at your local Extension office or with a certified tester. An inaccurate dial gauge is the leading cause of botulism risk in pressure canning — if the gauge reads 10 PSI but the actual pressure is 8 PSI, the food is under-processed.
- **Weighted-gauge canners**: Use weighted jiggling mechanisms at 5, 10, or 15 PSI. Inherently accurate — no calibration needed. Pressure is regulated by when the weight jiggles. Preferred for accuracy; only available in 5-PSI increments.

**Annual dial gauge testing**: Contact your county Extension office (search "[your county] Extension canning") for free or low-cost gauge testing. NCHFP recommends annual testing.

### 1.4 Testing for Seals After Canning

Within 24 hours after processing:
1. **Press the center of each lid**: If it flexes down and up with a clicking sound, the jar did not seal. Refrigerate and use within 2 weeks.
2. **Tap the lid**: A sealed jar produces a high-pitched ringing sound. An unsealed jar produces a dull thud.
3. **Look and feel**: Sealed lids are concave (dome curves inward) when cool. Unsealed lids may bulge or feel soft.

**If a jar did not seal**: Either reprocess within 24 hours (with a new lid; reprocessing degrades quality), freeze the product, or refrigerate and use promptly.

## Section 2: Water Bath Canning

Water bath canning is safe only for high-acid foods — those with pH at or below 4.6. The heat of boiling water (212°F/100°C at sea level) is insufficient to destroy *C. botulinum* spores, but at pH 4.6 or below, spores cannot germinate and produce toxin. The combination of heat and acidity destroys pathogens and inactivates enzymes that cause spoilage.

### 2.1 High-Acid Foods Safe for Water Bath Canning

**Naturally high-acid** (pH < 4.6 without addition):
- Most fruits (apples, peaches, pears, plums, apricots, cherries, grapes, berries)
- Most pickles (properly acidified with 5% vinegar)
- Jams, jellies, and fruit preserves

**Require acidification before water bath canning**:
- Tomatoes — modern tomato varieties vary in acidity; USDA requires added acid
- Figs — naturally low-acid; must have citric acid or lemon juice added

**NOT safe for water bath canning** (regardless of any recipe or claimed technique):
- Any vegetable without pickle-level acidification (green beans, beets without vinegar pickle, carrots, squash, potatoes, corn)
- Meat, poultry, seafood
- Dairy products
- Mixed products with low-acid components (stews, soups with vegetables)
- "Butter" canned in water bath — this is a common but unsafe practice

### 2.2 Water Bath Canning Procedure

**Overview**: Fill sterilized jars with prepared hot food, wipe jar rims, apply lids, process in boiling water for the required time (varies by food and jar size), cool, and verify seals.

**Equipment**: Water bath canner with rack, large pot for heating food, ladle, jar lifter, lid lifter, wide-mouth funnel, bubble remover (or plastic knife), clean damp cloth, timer.

**Step 1: Prepare the canner**

Fill the canner halfway with water. Begin heating. The water should be simmering (but not boiling) when filled jars are loaded. For a hot-pack product (food heated before packing), use warm water; for raw-pack (uncooked food packed into jars), use warm water.

**Step 2: Prepare jars**

Fill a second pot with water and keep jars submerged and hot. Keep lids and bands in hot (not boiling) water.

**Step 3: Prepare food per specific recipe**

See Sections 2.3, 2.4, 2.5, and 2.6 for specific food instructions. General principles:
- Use ripe, firm produce free from bruising or mold
- Prepare according to tested recipe only — do not substitute ingredients
- Pack food hot into hot jars (hot pack) or raw into hot jars (raw pack) depending on recipe

**Step 4: Fill jars**

Remove a hot jar from the water. Using a funnel, fill to the headspace specified in the recipe. Headspace is the empty space between the food and the top of the jar:
- Jams, jellies, fruit: 1/4 inch
- Fruits, tomatoes: 1/2 inch
- Pickles: 1/2 inch

**Step 5: Remove air bubbles**

Run a bubble remover, plastic knife, or thin spatula around the inside of the jar between food and glass. This removes trapped air pockets that can cause false seals or underprocessing.

**Step 6: Wipe jar rims**

Using a clean, damp paper towel, wipe the rim of each jar. Any food residue on the rim prevents sealing.

**Step 7: Apply lids**

Apply lid (flat disk) and tighten screw band to "fingertip tight" — tight enough to resist opening, but not forcing. Over-tightening can prevent air from escaping during processing, causing lids to buckle.

**Step 8: Load canner and process**

Lower filled jars onto the canner rack using a jar lifter. Add hot water if needed to cover jars by 1–2 inches. Bring to a full rolling boil. Once water reaches a full rolling boil, start timing. Maintain a steady boil throughout the process. If boiling stops, bring back to a full boil and restart timing from zero.

**Step 9: Remove jars**

When processing time is complete, turn off heat, remove lid. Allow jars to rest in water 5 minutes. Lift jars straight up (do not tilt) using jar lifter. Place on a clean towel or cooling rack, spacing jars 1 inch apart for air circulation. Do not re-tighten bands.

**Step 10: Cool and check seals**

Allow jars to cool undisturbed for 12–24 hours. Do not press, tip, or jostle jars during cooling — this can interfere with seal formation. After 12–24 hours, check seals (see Section 1.4).

### 2.3 Tomatoes — Crushed, Whole, and Diced

**Critical safety note**: All tomato products processed in a water bath canner must have acid added to ensure pH < 4.6. This requirement was updated in the early 1990s when research confirmed that current tomato varieties are lower in acid than older varieties. Do not skip the acidification step.

**Acidification (required for all tomato water bath canning)**:
- Per pint jar: 1 tablespoon bottled lemon juice OR 1/4 teaspoon citric acid
- Per quart jar: 2 tablespoons bottled lemon juice OR 1/2 teaspoon citric acid

Use bottled lemon juice (not fresh) — bottled lemon juice has a standardized and consistent acid level. Fresh lemon juice varies in acidity.

**Crushed tomatoes (hot pack; no added liquid)**:

*Preparation*: Wash tomatoes. Dip in boiling water 30–60 seconds until skins crack. Dip in cold water; slip off skins. Core; remove blemishes. Quarter. Heat 1/6 of the tomatoes in a large pot, crushing and stirring to release juice. Add remaining tomatoes and bring to a boil. Add acid to each jar before filling.

*Headspace*: 1/2 inch.

*Processing times (water bath canner)*:

| Jar Size | 0–1,000 ft | 1,001–3,000 ft | 3,001–6,000 ft | Above 6,000 ft |
|----------|-----------|---------------|---------------|---------------|
| Pints | 35 min | 40 min | 45 min | 50 min |
| Quarts | 45 min | 50 min | 55 min | 60 min |

*Processing times (pressure canner, dial-gauge at specified PSI)*:

| Jar Size | Time | 0–2,000 ft | 2,001–4,000 ft | 4,001–6,000 ft | 6,001–8,000 ft |
|----------|------|-----------|---------------|---------------|---------------|
| Pints & Quarts | 20 min | 6 PSI | 7 PSI | 8 PSI | 9 PSI |

*Source: USDA Complete Guide to Home Canning, 2015 revision, Guide 3 (Tomatoes), Table 1 and Table 3.*

**Whole or halved tomatoes (packed in water or tomato juice)**:
- Same acidification requirements as crushed tomatoes
- Hot pack or raw pack both acceptable
- For raw pack whole tomatoes (quart jars in water bath): 85 minutes at 0–1,000 ft; 90 min at 1,001–3,000 ft; 95 min at 3,001–6,000 ft; 100 min above 6,000 ft
- *Source: USDA Complete Guide to Home Canning, 2015 revision, Guide 3 (Tomatoes), Table 2.*

### 2.4 Jams and Jellies

Jams and jellies are among the easiest water bath canning projects and produce products stable for 1–2 years at room temperature.

**Jam vs. jelly**:
- Jam: Made from crushed or chopped fruit; contains fruit pieces; may or may not be clear
- Jelly: Made from fruit juice only; clear; firm gel
- Preserves: Jam with whole or large pieces of fruit
- Conserves: Jam with mixed fruits; often with nuts and raisins
- Marmalade: Jam made from citrus with suspended peel

**How pectin works**: Pectin is a naturally occurring polysaccharide in fruit cell walls. In the presence of sugar and acid at sufficient concentration, heated pectin forms a gel when cooled. Low-pectin fruits (strawberries, peaches, cherries, grapes) require added commercial pectin. High-pectin fruits (apples, currants, plums, crabapples, quince) may not require added pectin.

**Types of commercial pectin**:
- Powdered pectin: Added to cold fruit juice before boiling; brings fruit juice to a boil, then add all sugar at once; boil hard 1 minute
- Liquid pectin: Added to hot fruit juice after all sugar is dissolved and mixture reaches boiling; boil hard 1 minute
- Low-sugar/no-sugar pectin: Designed for reduced-sugar recipes; follow manufacturer instructions exactly — these use different gelling mechanisms and are NOT interchangeable with regular pectin recipes

**Do not reduce sugar in standard pectin recipes**: Sugar is not only for sweetness; it is a critical part of the gel chemistry. Reducing sugar in a recipe that calls for added pectin will produce runny jam that does not set. Low-sugar recipes require low-sugar pectin.

**Basic jam procedure with powdered pectin**:

1. Crush or chop fruit; measure exactly (do not adjust quantities)
2. Combine fruit and pectin in a large heavy-bottomed saucepan; stir to mix
3. Bring to a full rolling boil that cannot be stirred down
4. Add all measured sugar at once; stir to dissolve
5. Return to a full rolling boil; boil hard exactly 1 minute, stirring constantly
6. Remove from heat; skim foam if desired (foam is not harmful, just aesthetic)
7. Ladle into hot sterilized jars leaving 1/4-inch headspace
8. Wipe rims; apply lids and bands fingertip tight
9. Process in water bath canner

*Processing times for jams and jellies (water bath canner)*:

| Altitude | Processing Time |
|----------|----------------|
| 0–1,000 ft | 5 minutes (sterile jars) |
| 1,001–6,000 ft | 10 minutes |
| Above 6,000 ft | 15 minutes |

If jars are not sterile (not pre-boiled for 10 minutes), increase processing time by 5 minutes for all altitudes.

*Source: USDA Complete Guide to Home Canning, 2015 revision, Guide 7 (Jams and Jellies); NCHFP General Information on Jams and Jellies.*

**Testing for gel without commercial pectin (for high-pectin fruits)**:
- Plate test: Place a small spoonful of hot jam on a chilled plate; push finger through it after 30 seconds. If the jam wrinkles and holds its shape, it is ready. If it runs, continue boiling.
- Spoon test: Dip a cool metal spoon into the jam; lift and tilt. Ready jam sheets off the spoon in a single sheet rather than dripping in individual drops.
- Temperature test: Jelly made without added pectin is done when it reaches 220°F (104°C) at sea level (boiling point + 8°F). At higher altitudes, target boiling point + 8°F for your elevation.

**Strawberry jam with pectin — example quantities**:
- 2 cups crushed strawberries (approximately 1 quart whole strawberries)
- 1 package powdered pectin (1.75 oz / 49g)
- 4 cups sugar
- Makes approximately 5 to 6 half-pint jars

*Source: NCHFP Strawberry Jam with Pectin recipe; nchfp.uga.edu/how/make-jam-jelly/jams/strawberry-jam-pectin/*

### 2.5 Pickles — Vinegar-Pickled Cucumbers and Vegetables

Vinegar pickling preserves vegetables by lowering pH to 4.6 or below. The critical safety parameter is vinegar acidity: only vinegar with a labeled acidity of **5% (50 grains) or higher** is safe for canning pickles. Do not use homemade vinegar (acidity is unknown and variable) or reduced-acidity vinegars.

**Types of pickles**:
- **Fresh-pack (quick) pickles**: Raw cucumbers (or other vegetables) packed in vinegar brine; processed in water bath. Crisp texture; ready in 2–4 weeks.
- **Fermented pickles (brine pickles)**: Cucumbers fermented in salt brine for 3–4 weeks before canning. Traditional kosher dill flavor; more complex than fresh-pack.
- **Refrigerator pickles**: Not canned; stored in refrigerator; shelf life 1–3 months. Not covered in this section.

**General pickle safety rules**:
- Use standard canning salt (pickling salt) or kosher salt without anti-caking additives. Regular table salt with iodine or anti-caking agents can darken pickles and affect brine clarity.
- Do not reduce vinegar quantities below tested recipe proportions.
- Maintain correct cucumber-to-brine ratio (do not increase the quantity of cucumbers without increasing brine proportionally).
- Use pickling cucumbers, not slicing cucumbers — slicing cucumbers have seeds and softer flesh; they make mushy pickles.

**Quick dill pickle procedure**:

*Ingredients per 7 quart jars*:
- 17–18 lbs 3–5 inch pickling cucumbers
- 1 1/2 cups canning salt
- 1 1/4 cups white vinegar (5% acidity)
- 6 cups water
- Garlic cloves, dill heads (optional)

*Preparation*: Scrub cucumbers; remove 1/16-inch blossom end (enzyme here causes soft pickles). Refrigerate in ice water 2 hours to firm.

*Brine*: Combine vinegar, water, salt; bring to boil.

*Packing*: Place dill (2 heads or 1 tsp dill seed) and garlic (2 cloves) in each jar. Pack cucumbers tightly. Pour hot brine leaving 1/2-inch headspace.

*Processing times for dill pickles (water bath canner)*:

| Jar Size | 0–1,000 ft | 1,001–6,000 ft | Above 6,000 ft |
|----------|-----------|---------------|---------------|
| Pints | 10 min | 15 min | 20 min |
| Quarts | 15 min | 20 min | 25 min |

*Source: USDA Complete Guide to Home Canning, 2015 revision, Guide 6 (Pickles).*

**Beet pickles (vinegar)**: Beets are a low-acid vegetable that becomes safe for water bath canning when pickled with sufficient vinegar. The vinegar-to-water ratio in tested beet pickle recipes is required for safety. Do not use less vinegar. Processing: pints 30 minutes at 0–1,000 ft (add 5 minutes per altitude tier per NCHFP table). *Source: USDA Guide 6.*

## Section 3: Pressure Canning

Pressure canning is the only safe method for low-acid foods. It achieves temperatures of 240–250°F (116–121°C) at 10–15 PSI — temperatures that destroy *Clostridium botulinum* spores that would survive water bath canning temperatures.

**Who needs to pressure can**: Anyone preserving vegetables (green beans, corn, carrots, potatoes, squash), legumes (dried beans, lentils), soups, or stews for room-temperature shelf storage.

### 3.1 How a Pressure Canner Works

Water boils at 212°F (100°C) at sea level under normal atmospheric pressure. In a sealed pressure canner, steam builds up and cannot escape, raising the internal pressure. At 10 PSI above atmospheric pressure, water boils at 240°F (116°C). This temperature, maintained for the required time, destroys *C. botulinum* spores throughout the food.

The "PSI" in processing times refers to pounds per square inch above atmospheric pressure (gauge pressure, not absolute pressure). At sea level, atmospheric pressure is 14.7 PSI absolute; 10 PSI gauge = 24.7 PSI absolute.

**Altitude adjustment for pressure canning**: At higher altitudes, atmospheric pressure is lower, so the canner must maintain higher gauge pressure to achieve the same processing temperature. This is why altitude adjustments for pressure canning increase required PSI (not time, unlike water bath adjustments where time increases).

### 3.2 Pressure Canning Procedure

**Step 1: Fill canner and prepare jars**

Add 2–3 inches of hot (not boiling) water to the pressure canner. Keep jars hot. Prepare food per specific recipe.

**Step 2: Fill jars**

Pack food (hot pack or raw pack per recipe) into hot jars. Wipe rims, apply lids fingertip tight.

**Step 3: Load canner**

Lower filled jars onto the canner rack. Lock the canner lid. Leave vent pipe/petcock open.

**Step 4: Exhaust steam (venting)**

Heat canner on high. Allow steam to flow freely from the vent for **10 full minutes** before closing the petcock or placing the weight. This venting step removes air from the canner — air pockets prevent the canner from reaching correct pressure and temperature. Do not skip or shorten this step.

**Step 5: Pressurize the canner**

After 10 minutes of venting, close the petcock or place the weighted gauge.
- **Dial-gauge canner**: Watch the gauge; start timing when it reaches the required PSI for your altitude (see tables below).
- **Weighted-gauge canner**: When the weight jiggles steadily (about 3–4 times per minute), processing has begun.

**Step 6: Maintain pressure**

Adjust heat to maintain steady pressure. Dial-gauge: keep needle within 1 PSI of target. If pressure drops below required PSI, bring it back up and restart timing from zero.

**Step 7: Depressurize**

When processing time is complete, turn off heat. Allow canner to cool undisturbed at room temperature until pressure returns to zero (typically 30–45 minutes for a full canner). Do not rush cool-down with water or by opening vents — rapid depressurization can cause jar breakage or siphoning (food being pulled out of jars).

**Step 8: Remove jars**

When pressure is at zero and the canner has cooled, wait 10 more minutes. Open vent or carefully remove weight. Remove canner lid (tilt away from you; steam may remain). Using a jar lifter, remove jars and cool on a towel or rack 12–24 hours. Check seals.

### 3.3 Green Beans (Snap Beans) — Processing Times

**There is no safe option for processing green beans in a water bath canner.** This is one of the most common dangerous mistakes in home canning. Green beans are a low-acid food and must be pressure canned.

*Preparation*: Wash beans; snap ends; break into 2-inch pieces. Hot pack: boil 5 minutes; fill jars loosely; add 1 tsp canning salt per quart if desired; cover with hot cooking liquid. Raw pack: fill jars tightly with raw beans; add salt if desired; fill with boiling water.

*Headspace*: 1 inch.

*Processing times — dial-gauge pressure canner*:

| Jar Size | Time | 0–2,000 ft | 2,001–4,000 ft | 4,001–6,000 ft | 6,001–8,000 ft |
|----------|------|-----------|---------------|---------------|---------------|
| Pints | 20 min | 11 PSI | 12 PSI | 13 PSI | 14 PSI |
| Quarts | 25 min | 11 PSI | 12 PSI | 13 PSI | 14 PSI |

*Processing times — weighted-gauge pressure canner*:

| Jar Size | Time | 0–1,000 ft | Above 1,000 ft |
|----------|------|-----------|---------------|
| Pints | 20 min | 10 PSI | 15 PSI |
| Quarts | 25 min | 10 PSI | 15 PSI |

*Source: NCHFP "Beans, Snap and Italian — Pieces, Green and Wax"; nchfp.uga.edu/how/can/canning-vegetables-and-vegetable-products/beans-snap-and-italian-pieces-green-and-wax/*

### 3.4 Carrots — Processing Times

*Preparation*: Wash and peel carrots; slice in 1/4-inch rounds or leave small carrots whole. Hot pack: cover with boiling water, boil 5 minutes, pack into jars with cooking liquid. Raw pack: tightly pack raw carrot rounds, add boiling water.

*Headspace*: 1 inch. Add 1 tsp canning salt per quart if desired.

*Processing times — dial-gauge pressure canner*:

|----------|------|-----------|---------------|
| Pints | 25 min | 10 PSI | 15 PSI |

*Source: USDA Complete Guide to Home Canning, 2015, Guide 4 (Vegetables).*

## Section 4: Lacto-Fermentation

Lacto-fermentation is the simplest preservation method in this guide. It requires no canning equipment, no heating, and no electricity. Wild Lactobacillus bacteria already present on vegetables convert sugars into lactic acid, which drops the pH to 3.5–4.0 and creates conditions hostile to pathogens.

**Why lacto-fermented food is safe from botulism**: *Clostridium botulinum* cannot grow or produce toxin below pH 4.6. Lacto-fermentation drives pH below this threshold within 48–72 hours for most vegetables. Fermented vegetables do not require pressure canning. However, fermented foods are not shelf-stable at room temperature indefinitely — they require refrigeration for long-term storage to slow continued fermentation.

**Nutritional benefit**: Lacto-fermented vegetables retain more vitamins than canned equivalents (heat destroys some vitamins). They contain live Lactobacillus cultures (probiotics) with documented gut health associations when consumed fresh and unheated.

### 4.1 Equipment

No special equipment is required for basic lacto-fermentation. You need:
- A clean glass jar (1-quart Mason jar works well)
- Something to keep vegetables submerged below brine (a small zip-lock bag filled with brine, a weight, a smaller jar that fits inside the larger jar)
- A lid or cloth to cover (loose — do not seal airtight during active fermentation, as CO2 must escape)

**Optional equipment**: Fermentation crock (ceramic; traditional; designed for this purpose), airlock lids (allow CO2 to escape without letting oxygen in), digital pH meter or strips ($10–30; useful for beginners confirming fermentation is proceeding).

### 4.2 Salt: The Critical Variable

Salt performs three functions in lacto-fermentation:
1. Draws water from vegetable cells by osmosis, creating the brine
2. Suppresses spoilage bacteria (which are more salt-sensitive than Lactobacillus)
3. Controls fermentation speed (more salt = slower fermentation)

**Salt type**: Use canning/pickling salt, kosher salt (without anti-caking additives), or pure sea salt. Do not use iodized table salt — iodine inhibits bacterial growth and can stall fermentation.

**Salt ratio for dry-salt method** (for shredded vegetables: sauerkraut, kimchi):
- **Standard**: 2% salt by weight of vegetables
- In practical terms: approximately 1 tablespoon non-iodized salt per 1–1.5 lbs shredded vegetables
- USDA/NCHFP-recommended range: 1.5–2.5% salt; below 1.5% risks spoilage; above 3% may suppress Lactobacillus and stall fermentation

**Salt ratio for brine method** (for whole or large-cut vegetables: whole pickles, pepper halves):
- **3% brine** (for cut vegetables): 1 quart water + 2 tablespoons (27g) non-iodized salt
- **5% brine** (for whole or large pieces): 1 quart water + 3 tablespoons (45g) non-iodized salt

*Sources: UMN Extension "Preserving food at home: Fermentation"; Illinois Extension "Fermenting" factsheet; NCHFP fermentation guidance.*

### 4.3 Sauerkraut — Step-by-Step Procedure

Sauerkraut is the simplest lacto-fermented food. It requires only cabbage and salt.

**Ingredients**:
- 1 medium head cabbage (approximately 2 lbs / 900g) — green or red; Savoy works but produces softer kraut
- 1 tablespoon non-iodized pickling or kosher salt (adjust to 2% by weight of shredded cabbage if precise)

**Equipment**: Large mixing bowl, knife or mandoline or food processor, 1-quart Mason jar or fermentation crock, something to weight down the cabbage (clean rock, jar of water, purpose-made ceramic weight).

**Step 1: Shred the cabbage**

Remove outer leaves; set aside. Shred cabbage thinly — 1/8-inch thick shreds. Remove and discard the core.

**Step 2: Salt and massage**

Place shredded cabbage in a large bowl. Sprinkle with salt. Massage and squeeze vigorously for 10–15 minutes until the cabbage releases substantial liquid (brine). The cabbage volume reduces by about 25–30% during this step. The released liquid is the brine that will cover the cabbage during fermentation.

If insufficient liquid has released after 15 minutes: pack tightly into the jar and wait 30 more minutes; the salt will continue drawing liquid. If still insufficient: add a small amount of brine (1 cup water + 1/2 teaspoon salt) to bring liquid level just above cabbage.

**Step 3: Pack tightly into jar**

Pack cabbage into a clean 1-quart jar as tightly as possible, pressing down with your fist or a wooden tamper. The goal is to eliminate air pockets. Brine should rise to cover the cabbage. Leave 1–2 inches of headspace (cabbage expands during fermentation and CO2 gas produces upward pressure).

**Step 4: Weigh down the cabbage**

Lay a reserved outer cabbage leaf over the top of the shredded cabbage, folding it to fit inside the jar. Place a weight on top: a small zip-lock bag filled with brine, a smaller jar filled with water, or a purpose-made fermentation weight. The goal is to keep all cabbage below the brine surface.

**Step 5: Cover**

Cover the jar with a cloth secured with a rubber band, a loose lid (not tightened), or an airlock lid. The covering should keep out insects and dust while allowing CO2 gas produced during fermentation to escape.

**Step 6: Ferment at room temperature**

Place at room temperature (68–72°F / 20–22°C is ideal). Cooler temperatures produce a more complex flavor over more weeks; warmer temperatures ferment faster but produce a less complex flavor and a greater risk of off-flavors if above 75°F.

**Day 1–3**: Small bubbles should appear by day 2–3, indicating active fermentation. The brine may cloud slightly — this is normal.

**Week 1**: Taste occasionally. Young sauerkraut has a mild, slightly salty flavor with a hint of sour.

**Week 2–4**: More pronounced sourness develops. Most people prefer sauerkraut fermented 3–4 weeks at 68–72°F.

**Step 7: Taste and transfer to refrigerator**

When the sauerkraut reaches your preferred sourness (taste every 3–4 days after the first week), transfer to the refrigerator. Cold temperatures stop or dramatically slow fermentation. The sauerkraut is now shelf-stable in the refrigerator for up to 9 months.

**Signs of good fermentation**:
- Bubbles visible in brine (especially in first 1–2 weeks)
- Pleasantly sour smell (like sour pickles, tangy vinegar)
- Cloudy brine (normal; indicates active bacterial culture)
- pH 3.5–4.5 if testing with strips or meter

**Signs of a problem — discard if present**:
- Pink or red brine (not from red cabbage — unexpected color): discard
- Strong unpleasant smell (putrid, rotten, slimy — different from sour): discard
- Slimy texture on vegetables: discard
- Fuzzy mold on vegetables beneath brine: discard the batch. White or gray surface film (kahm yeast) on the brine surface is not mold, is common, and is harmless — skim it off and continue fermentation.

### 4.4 Fermented Dill Pickles (Brine Pickles) — Procedure

Fermented pickles (kosher dill style) are made by submerging whole cucumbers in a salt brine, without vinegar. Fermentation develops the flavor over 3–4 weeks. The result is distinctly different from fresh-pack vinegar pickles — more complex, crunchier, and probiotic.

**Ingredients for 1-quart jar**:
- 4–5 small pickling cucumbers (3–4 inch), scrubbed, 1/16-inch blossom end removed
- 5% brine: 1 quart cold non-chlorinated water + 3 tablespoons (45g) non-iodized canning salt
- 2–3 heads fresh dill or 1 tablespoon dill seed
- 2–3 garlic cloves (optional)
- Grape leaf, oak leaf, or horseradish leaf (1, to add tannins for crunchiness — optional but helpful)

**Water note**: Chlorine in municipal tap water can inhibit fermentation. Use filtered water, well water, or allow tap water to sit uncovered for 24 hours for chlorine to dissipate.

**Procedure**:
1. Make brine: dissolve salt in water; do not heat
2. Place dill and garlic in the bottom of a clean jar
3. Pack cucumbers vertically as tightly as possible
4. Pour brine over cucumbers, covering by at least 1 inch
5. Add tannin leaf if using; weight cucumbers below brine
6. Cover with cloth or loose lid
7. Ferment at 68–72°F for 3–4 weeks, checking weekly
8. Taste; transfer to refrigerator when desired sourness is reached

**Timeline**: At 68–72°F, half-sour pickles (mild, slightly salty, lightly sour) are ready in 5–7 days. Full-sour traditional dill pickles are ready in 3–4 weeks.

### 4.5 Kimchi — Simplified Procedure

Kimchi is a Korean fermented vegetable, traditionally made with napa cabbage, hot pepper flakes, garlic, and ginger. It ferments quickly (2–7 days for a ready-to-eat version) and keeps refrigerated for months.

**Simplified ingredients for 1 quart**:
- 1 small head napa cabbage (approximately 1.5 lbs / 680g), cut into 2-inch squares
- 2 tablespoons non-iodized salt (for initial salting)
- 2 tablespoons Korean red pepper flakes (gochugaru) — more or less to taste
- 3–4 garlic cloves, minced
- 1-inch piece fresh ginger, grated
- 1 tablespoon fish sauce (optional; use salt or soy sauce for vegan version)
- 4 green onions, cut into 2-inch pieces

**Procedure**:
1. Toss cabbage with salt; let sit 1–2 hours, tossing occasionally, until cabbage wilts and releases liquid. Rinse once; squeeze out excess water.
2. Mix pepper flakes, garlic, ginger, and fish sauce into a paste.
3. Combine cabbage, green onions, and paste; mix thoroughly with clean hands (wear gloves if sensitive to pepper).
4. Pack tightly into a jar; press down so brine rises. Cover with outer cabbage leaves; weight down below brine.
5. Leave at room temperature (65–72°F) for 1–5 days. Taste daily. Kimchi is ready when pleasantly sour and tangy.
6. Transfer to refrigerator. Active flavor development continues slowly in refrigerator for several weeks; flavor peaks at 2–3 weeks cold.

*Source: UMN Extension fermentation guidance; Illinois Extension lacto-fermentation factsheet.*

## Section 5: Food Dehydration

Dehydration removes the water that microorganisms require for growth, extending shelf life without heat-sealing (no canning equipment needed). Dehydrated food is shelf-stable, lightweight, and space-efficient.

**Preservation mechanism**: Most bacteria require water activity (Aw) above 0.91; most molds require Aw above 0.80. Dehydration reduces Aw to 0.60–0.75 for most products, inhibiting growth. Dehydration does NOT eliminate all organisms — it suppresses them. If rehydrated and left at room temperature, dried food can spoil.

**What dehydration does NOT do**: Dehydration alone does not make low-acid vegetables safe from botulism for room-temperature storage of moist products. Dried vegetables at very low moisture (brittle, snap-dry stage) are shelf-safe because Aw is too low for botulism growth. However, insufficiently dried vegetables that are stored airtight at room temperature can still support botulism. Always dry to the correct final moisture stage.

### 5.1 Equipment

**Dehydrator (preferred)**: An electric food dehydrator with a thermostat provides the most consistent and efficient drying. Look for models with:
- Adjustable temperature 95–165°F
- Horizontal airflow (avoids flavor cross-contamination between trays)
- Capacity for your typical batch size

Cost: $40–$200 for household units. A good entry-level dehydrator ($50–80) is sufficient for regular household use.

**Oven drying**: A standard oven can dehydrate food, but has disadvantages:
- Minimum oven temperature of many ovens is 170°F — higher than ideal for herbs and some fruits; works adequately for vegetables at 140°F if oven will go that low
- Energy-inefficient compared to a dehydrator (oven heats the full oven; dehydrator only heats drying chamber)
- Requires propping door slightly open to allow moisture to escape (prevents condensation)

**Sun drying**: Only practical in arid climates with low humidity, temperatures above 86°F (30°C), and good air circulation. Not recommended where humidity is high (risk of mold before drying is complete) or where insect contamination is likely.

### 5.2 Dehydration Temperatures and Times by Food Type

| Food Category | Temperature | Approximate Time | Doneness Test |
|--------------|-------------|-----------------|---------------|
| Herbs and leafy greens | 95–115°F (35–46°C) | 1–4 hours | Crumble between fingers; no moisture |
| Fruit (sliced, 1/4 inch) | 135°F (57°C) | 6–16 hours | Pliable; leathery; no moisture pockets |
| Fruit leather (pureed) | 135°F (57°C) | 6–8 hours | Peels cleanly from tray; not sticky |
| Vegetables (sliced, 1/4 inch) | 125–135°F (52–57°C) | 6–14 hours | Crisp; brittle; snap when bent |
| Jerky (beef, marinated, 1/4 inch) | 145–165°F (63–74°C) | 4–8 hours | Bends without breaking; no moist center |

*Times are approximate; actual time depends on food moisture content, slice thickness, airflow, and humidity.*

**Jerky safety note**: The USDA Meat and Poultry Hotline issued guidance in 2001 confirming that typical dehydrator temperatures (130–150°F) may not raise meat internal temperature to the required 160°F (beef) or 165°F (poultry) before surface drying occurs. **Two safe approaches**:
1. Pre-cook meat to 160°F in a 325°F oven before dehydrating (produces jerky with slightly firmer texture)
2. After dehydrating, heat finished jerky in a 275°F oven for 10 minutes to ensure pathogen kill

*Source: USDA Meat and Poultry Hotline jerky guidance; Salt & Prepper dehydration temperature and time guide.*

### 5.3 Pre-Treatment for Fruits and Vegetables

**Blanching vegetables** (recommended for most vegetables): Briefly boiling or steaming vegetables before dehydrating stops enzyme activity that causes browning and flavor loss during storage. Blanch 2–5 minutes (depending on vegetable), cool quickly in ice water, pat dry, then dehydrate.

Vegetables that benefit from blanching: green beans, corn, carrots, broccoli, cauliflower, potatoes, peas.

Vegetables that do not need blanching: onions, garlic, mushrooms, tomatoes, peppers.

**Ascorbic acid treatment for fruits** (prevents browning): Dip sliced fruits (apples, pears, peaches, bananas) in a solution of 1/2 teaspoon ascorbic acid powder (Vitamin C) per quart of water for 2 minutes before dehydrating. This prevents oxidative browning and preserves color. Commercial anti-browning solutions (Fruit-Fresh) contain ascorbic acid.

**Sulfite dipping**: Commercial sulfite dipping dramatically extends shelf life and preserves color but is not recommended for home use due to reactions in sulfite-sensitive individuals (approximately 1% of population; higher among asthmatics). Ascorbic acid is the safe equivalent for home dehydrators.

### 5.4 Testing for Correct Dryness

**Fruits**: Should be pliable and leathery with no moisture when squeezed. No wet spots visible when a piece is cut in half. Not brittle (brittle fruits were overdried and lose quality, but are safe).

**Vegetables**: Should snap cleanly. Brittle, not pliable. Squeeze tightly — no moisture visible; no sticking.

**"Conditioning" dried food**: After dehydrating, allow dried food to sit in an unsealed container at room temperature for 7–10 days before packaging for long-term storage. Stir or shake daily. This "conditioning" step equalizes moisture within the batch — some pieces may be drier than others; conditioning prevents condensation in sealed storage containers from the wetter pieces.

**Test for adequate dryness before long-term storage**: Pack into a sealed jar. Leave in a sunny window for 1 hour. If moisture condenses on inside of jar, food is not sufficiently dry — return to dehydrator for more time.

### 5.5 Storage for Dehydrated Foods

| Food | Storage Life (room temp, airtight, cool, dark) | Storage Life (freezer) |
|------|-----------------------------------------------|----------------------|
| Vegetables | 1–2 years | 5+ years |
| Fruits | 6–12 months | 3–5 years |
| Herbs | 1–3 years | — |
| Jerky | 1–2 months | 6 months |
| Fruit leather | 1 month (room temp); 3–4 months (refrigerator) | 12 months |

**Storage containers**: Glass jars with tight lids, vacuum-sealed bags, or Mylar bags with oxygen absorbers. Avoid plastic bags without vacuum sealing (oxygen causes oxidation; moisture can penetrate).

**Oxygen absorbers**: Iron-based oxygen absorbers in sealed containers reduce oxygen from 21% to below 0.1%. This dramatically extends shelf life and prevents insect infestation. 100cc oxygen absorbers are appropriate for 1-quart jars; 300cc for 1-gallon containers.

## Section 6: Troubleshooting Canning Problems

### 6.1 Common Water Bath Canning Problems

| Problem | Symptom | Cause | Solution |
|---------|---------|-------|---------|
| Jar didn't seal | Lid flexes up and down | Food on rim; overfilled; lid defect; insufficient processing | Refrigerate and use within 2 weeks, OR reprocess within 24 hours with new lid |
| Siphoning (food pulled out of jar) | Liquid level lower than when processed; food visible above liquid | Rapid pressure change during cooling; overfilling; under-filled brine | Process per instructions; do not adjust jars during cooling; fill to correct headspace |
| Soft or mushy texture | Vegetables or fruits are mushy | Overprocessed; overripe produce; incorrect headspace | Use firmer produce; follow processing times exactly |
| Floating fruit | Fruit rises above liquid | Raw pack; under-ripe or over-ripe fruit; heavy syrup | Use hot pack; use uniform ripeness fruit; use lighter syrup |
| Jam didn't gel | Runny or watery consistency | Undercooked; pectin failure; too much/too little sugar; humidity | Test for set properly during cooking; use fresh pectin; measure exactly |
| Darkened food at top of jar | Oxidation (not safety issue) | Insufficient brine/syrup to cover food; headspace too large | Pack food more tightly; cover fully with liquid |

### 6.2 Common Pressure Canning Problems

| Problem | Symptom | Cause | Solution |
|---------|---------|-------|---------|
| Pressure won't rise | Gauge stuck; no pressure buildup | Vent not closed properly; gasket leak; petcock clogged | Check petcock; inspect gasket; replace if damaged |
| Pressure drops mid-process | Gauge drops during processing | Heat fluctuation; draft | Restart timing from zero when pressure drops below required PSI |
| Siphoning | Liquid pulled from jars | Pressure dropped suddenly; canner cooled too fast | Allow canner to depressurize naturally; do not rush |
| Gasket issues | Steam escaping around lid | Worn or dirty gasket | Clean gasket; replace annually; never force lid |
| Under-pressurized processing | Gauge reading incorrect | Dial gauge inaccurate | Test gauge annually; use weighted gauge for accuracy |

### 6.3 Signs a Canned Product is Unsafe — Discard Without Tasting

Discard any home-canned product that shows:
- Spurting liquid when opened
- Cloudy (not naturally cloudy) or foamy liquid in a non-fermented product
- Off or unusual odor (beyond normal product smell)
- Mold visible in product (not just surface mold on stored fermented products)
- Lid that is unsealed, bulging, or loose
- Food that is slimy, discolored, or has unusual texture

**Disposal of suspect canned food**: Do not taste; do not compost; do not put down sink drain. Seal in heavy-duty plastic bags and discard in household waste. Wash hands and all surfaces that may have contacted the food with hot soapy water.
