# Wildcraft Safety and Preservation: Verifying, Processing, and Preserving Wild-Harvested Plants and Fungi

**License**: This guide is published under [CC-BY-4.0](https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by/4.0/). You are free to share and adapt it with attribution to the Open-Repo Project.

**What this guide is NOT**: This is not a plant identification guide, and it does not describe how to distinguish species in the field. Field identification requires hands-on training, multiple physical field guides specific to your region, and — ideally — time spent with an experienced local forager, herbalist, or mycologist before you ever harvest anything for consumption. Misidentification is the single largest cause of serious and fatal wild-food poisoning, and no written guide, this one included, can replace that training. If you have not yet learned to identify a plant or fungus with full confidence, do not use this guide as a basis for eating it.

**What this guide covers instead**: The steps that come *after* tentative identification — verifying you actually have what you think you have, assessing how serious a mistake would be, and then processing and preserving the harvest safely once you have confirmed it. These are the steps most existing foraging content skips, and they are where a large share of both safety failures and wasted harvest actually occur.

**Relationship to other Waves**: This guide is the wild-harvest-specific complement to Wave 3's [Fermentation & Preservation guide](../wave-3-fermentation-preservation/fermentation-preservation-complete-guide.md) and Wave 4's [Food Storage and Preservation Beyond Canning guide](../wave-4-food-storage-preservation-advanced/food-storage-preservation-advanced-complete-guide.md), which cover the underlying food-science principles (water activity, pH, curing, smoking, rendering) in more depth for garden and farm-scale harvests. It also complements the [Herbal Medicine guide](../wave-2-herbal-medicine/herbal-medicine-and-medicinal-plants.md), which covers preparation methods and plant profiles for cultivated medicinal herbs. This guide focuses on what changes when the source material is wild: variable moisture and contamination, ambiguous identification, legal harvesting limits, and processing techniques (cordage, dyeing, wild fat rendering) that are specific to wildcrafted material.

## Overview

Wild-harvested plants, fungi, and animal products can meaningfully extend a household's food and medicine supply — but two things make wild harvest different from garden or store-bought material: **you cannot always be certain what you have**, and **once you are certain, wild material often arrives in inconvenient forms and quantities** (a single morning's harvest of mixed greens, three pounds of chanterelles, a bark strip, a fistful of roots) that need immediate processing decisions.

This guide is organized around that two-part problem:

1. **Part 1 — Safety Verification**: What to do once you've tentatively identified a plant or fungus and need to decide whether it is actually safe to harvest and use, before you invest any preservation effort in it.
2. **Part 2 — Preservation**: How to dry, ferment, smoke, infuse, store, can, or freeze wild-harvested material so it doesn't spoil or go to waste.
3. **Part 3 — Processing for Use**: How to turn preserved wild material into usable food, medicine, dye, or cordage.
4. **Part 4 — Legal and Ethical Boundaries**: What you are and are not allowed to harvest, where, and how much.
5. **Part 5 — Integration**: How wild harvest fits into a broader resilience food and medicine system as a supplement, not a sole source.

## Part 1: Safety Verification for Wild-Harvested Plants

### 1.1 Verification Is a Different Task Than Identification

Field guides and foraging classes teach *identification* — how to recognize a species. This section addresses the separate, narrower task of *verification*: given a plant or fungus you have tentatively identified, how do you confirm that identification before you commit to eating or extensively processing it?

The distinction matters because verification failures, not identification failures in principle, are what actually poison people. Experienced foragers get poisoned too — usually not because they don't know how to identify plants in general, but because they skip verification on a specific specimen: harvesting in poor light, harvesting a look-alike growing intermixed with the target species, or trusting a single diagnostic feature that turned out to be shared by a toxic relative. University extension guidance is consistent on this point: before eating any wild plant, use at least three independent resources to confirm identification, and cross-reference at least two reliable sources (a field guide plus an extension website plus, ideally, an experienced local forager) before sampling anything [Source: Mississippi State University Extension, *Forgotten Foods: Introduction to Wild Edible Plants*].

### 1.2 Multi-Characteristic Confirmation

No single feature — color, general shape, or habitat alone — is a reliable safety signal. Color in particular is explicitly called out by extension sources as *not* a safety cue; toxic and edible plants of the same family frequently share coloration, and the diagnostic features that actually separate them are usually a *combination* of leaf arrangement, stem cross-section, flower/seed head structure, smell, and habitat [Source: RV Life, *Wild Plants With Toxic Look-Alikes*; MasterClass, *Universal Edibility Test*].

A defensible verification protocol checks multiple, independent characteristics and requires all of them to agree before you proceed:

1. **Leaf arrangement and shape** — opposite vs. alternate vs. whorled leaves, compound vs. simple leaves, leaf margin (toothed, smooth, lobed). This is usually the most reliable single feature but must be checked against the specific target species, not the general plant family.
2. **Stem characteristics** — solid vs. hollow, ridged vs. smooth, presence of hairs, color and any spotting (purple stem-spotting is a classic, though not universal, warning sign in the carrot family — see 1.5 below).
3. **Smell** — many safe plants in the carrot/parsley family (wild carrot, wild parsnip in its edible stages) and the allium family (wild onion, ramps) have a distinctive, unmistakable smell when leaves or bulbs are crushed. The *absence* of an expected smell is a red flag; a foul, medicinal, or "off" smell should stop harvest entirely.
4. **Habitat and companion species** — where the plant is growing relative to known toxic look-alikes. Extension sources note that the most dangerous lookalike pairs frequently grow in the *same* hedgerows, wet meadows, and ditches as their safe counterparts (for example, pokeberry, baneberry, and deadly nightshade grow alongside blackberry, raspberry, and elderberry) [Source: Farmers' Almanac, *How to Identify Common Wild Berries*]. Shared habitat is not disqualifying, but it means habitat alone confirms nothing and increases the importance of the other checks.
5. **Growth stage** — many species are only reliably distinguishable from toxic relatives at a specific growth stage (flowering vs. pre-flowering, root morphology at maturity). If your only reliable diagnostic feature is not currently present (e.g., no flowers yet), treat the identification as unconfirmed.

**Rule**: if any one of these checks contradicts the others, do not harvest. Agreement across three or more independent characteristics is the minimum bar recommended by extension literature before treating an identification as confirmed [Source: Battlbox, *How to Identify Edible Plants: Survival Foraging Guide*, citing standard multi-source cross-referencing practice].

### 1.3 The Taste-Spit Test, Smell Test, and Bruise Test — What They Can and Cannot Tell You

These field tests are widely taught as part of "universal edibility" protocols, but they have real limits and should never be used as the sole basis for eating an *unidentified* plant, fungus, or mushroom.

- **Smell test**: A foul, bitter-almond, "mousy," or otherwise off-putting smell is a legitimate reason to discard a specimen even if other features look correct. But a "normal" or absent smell does not confirm safety — some of the deadliest plants and fungi (poison hemlock in its early rosette stage, *Amanita phalloides*) have mild or unremarkable smells.
- **Touch/skin test**: Touching a small amount of plant material to the inside of the wrist and waiting 15 minutes for a reaction (burning, itching, redness) can catch contact-irritant species (e.g., some *Euphorbia* species, poison ivy relatives) before ingestion is even considered [Source: RV Life, *Wild Plants With Toxic Look-Alikes*]. It does **not** screen for internal toxins that cause no skin reaction (most fatal plant and mushroom poisons, including amatoxins and coniine, produce no skin signal at all).
- **Taste-spit test**: Placing a tiny piece on the tongue, holding it for a few seconds without swallowing, and spitting it out, watching for burning, numbing, or acrid taste. This test has real value for a narrow category of *mildly* irritating plants (identifying an unpalatably bitter or acrid specimen) but is explicitly **not safe** for anything suspected to be in the amatoxin-mushroom, hemlock, nightshade, or water hemlock category, because the toxic dose in these species can be small enough that even brief tongue contact carries real risk, and because taste is simply not a reliable indicator for these toxins.
- **Bruise/cut test (mushroom-specific)**: Some mushroom groups show diagnostic color changes when cut or bruised (bluing in some *Boletus*, color change in *Amanita* species). This is a genuinely useful confirmatory characteristic **for foragers who already know the specific diagnostic reaction for their target species** — it is not a general-purpose safety test, and relying on "if it doesn't bruise blue it's fine" logic without species-specific knowledge is not a valid protocol.

**Bottom line from public health sources**: none of these field tests substitute for identification confidence. The CDC's own outbreak reporting on *Amanita phalloides* poisonings emphasizes that even careful foragers using traditional "folk" identification heuristics have died or required liver transplants because the death cap resembles both edible *Amanita* species used in some culinary traditions and, in its young "button" stage, common cultivated mushrooms [Source: CDC MMWR, *Amanita phalloides Mushroom Poisonings — Northern California, December 2016*].

### 1.4 Poisoning Risk Assessment: Which Mistakes Are Dangerous vs. Merely Unpleasant

Not all misidentification carries the same stakes, and understanding the risk tier of what you're handling should change how cautious you are.

**Tier 1 — Low risk, self-limiting (GI upset only)**: A number of wild plants cause mild, self-resolving digestive upset if eaten raw, unripe, or in excess (e.g., unripe pokeweed shoots improperly prepared, some raw brassica-family greens in large quantity). Unpleasant but not medically dangerous for a healthy adult in modest quantities.

**Tier 2 — Moderate risk, treatable, dose-dependent**: Plants causing more significant but generally treatable reactions — vomiting, diarrhea, temporary cardiac or neurological symptoms — where prompt medical attention resolves the issue and permanent harm is unlikely if treated (e.g., moderate ingestion of some *Amanita* species causing GI-predominant syndromes, some green potato/nightshade-family alkaloid exposure).

**Tier 3 — Severe risk, organ damage, delayed onset**: This is the highest-stakes category and the one that kills. Amatoxin-containing mushrooms (*Amanita phalloides* — the death cap — and several related species) cause a deceptively delayed-onset syndrome: no symptoms for 6–24 hours, then severe GI distress that appears to resolve, followed by silent liver failure over the following days. Death cap mushrooms cause roughly 90% of mushroom-poisoning fatalities worldwide, with a mortality rate of 10–20% among poisoned patients even with treatment, and as little as half a mushroom cap can contain a fatal dose for an adult [Source: NRFMP, *Death Cap Mushroom Poisoning*; UC Davis Health, *What you need to know about wild mushroom poisoning* (2025)]. A November 2025 cluster of at least 23 poisoning cases (including one death) in the San Francisco Bay Area, and a 2016 Northern California cluster investigated by the CDC, both trace back to death caps being mistaken for edible species familiar to foragers from other regions or cuisines [Source: NBC News, *Poisonings from 'death cap' mushrooms in California prompt warning against foraging* (2026); CDC MMWR (2016); CDC MMWR, *Amanita Species Mushroom Poisonings* (2026 update)].

Poison hemlock (*Conium maculatum*) sits in the same severe-risk tier for plants: it is routinely mistaken for wild carrot, wild parsnip, or wild parsley (all in the same family), grows in the same disturbed ground and roadside habitat as those edible look-alikes, and severe neurological symptoms (progressive paralysis, respiratory failure) can begin within 15 minutes of ingestion. Extension sources are explicit that most human hemlock poisonings result directly from this misidentification pattern [Source: Iowa State University Extension, *Weed watch: Wild parsnip and poison hemlock*; Cleveland Clinic, *Hemlock Poisoning: Symptoms, Treatment & Prevention*].

**A related but distinct hazard — contact toxicity**: Wild parsnip (itself edible when correctly identified and prepared) contains psoralen compounds in its sap that cause phytophotodermatitis — severe burn-like blistering — when sap contacts skin and the skin is then exposed to sunlight. This is a contact/handling hazard, not an ingestion hazard, and it means that safe *harvesting technique* (gloves, covered forearms, avoiding sap contact during cutting) matters even for a plant you have correctly and safely identified as edible [Source: Ohio State University BYGL, *Poison Hemlock and Wild Parsnip: Control Them Now!*].

**Practical implication**: your verification rigor should scale with the risk tier of the plant/fungus family you're working in. For low-risk greens with no dangerous look-alikes in your region, three-characteristic confirmation by an experienced forager is reasonable. For anything in the amatoxin-mushroom family, the carrot/parsley family, or any Tier 3 category, the standard should be **positive identification by an experienced, ideally credentialed, local expert** (a mycological society member, a state-certified wild ginseng dealer, an extension-trained forager) — not self-taught field-guide matching alone.

### 1.5 High-Risk Lookalike Pairs Worth Naming Explicitly

While this guide does not teach identification, naming the highest-consequence lookalike relationships is a safety-relevant fact, not an identification tutorial:

- **Death cap / button mushrooms and some edible Amanita species**: young death caps can resemble immature edible *Agaricus* (button) mushrooms and, in some regional culinary traditions, edible *Amanita* species eaten elsewhere in the world; misapplied cross-cultural identification experience is a documented cause of poisoning clusters in North America [Source: CDC MMWR (2016, 2026); KQED, *After Mushroom Poisonings, California Says 'Don't Forage'* (2026)].
- **Poison hemlock / wild carrot, wild parsnip, and wild parsley (Apiaceae family)**: all share the family's characteristic umbrella-shaped flower clusters; hemlock is distinguished by purple-blotched stems (not universally present or obvious), a foul smell when crushed, and hairless stems, but extension sources caution these features require training to apply reliably, and hemlock and wild parsnip frequently grow intermixed, which can further confuse both identification and symptom attribution if exposure occurs [Source: Iowa State University Extension; Ohio State BYGL].
- **Pokeberry, baneberry, and deadly nightshade / blackberry, raspberry, elderberry**: extension and consumer-safety sources note these dangerous and safe pairs commonly co-occur in the same hedgerow and meadow habitat, meaning proximity to a known-safe patch is not itself a safety signal [Source: Farmers' Almanac].
- **Purslane / prostrate spurge**: an edible, nutritious low-growing succulent (purslane) frequently grows in the same disturbed soil as its toxic look-alike (spurge), distinguished by leaf shape, stem sap (spurge exudes a milky latex sap when broken; purslane does not), and growth habit [Source: Mississippi State University Extension].

### 1.6 When Identification Is Ambiguous: Harvest or Skip?

The extension-literature consensus is unambiguous: **when in doubt, leave it out**. This is not overcaution — given that the worst-case outcome in the Tier 3 category is death or liver transplant, and the benefit of any single wild-harvested specimen is small, the expected-value calculation overwhelmingly favors discarding ambiguous specimens rather than risking consumption [Source: Mississippi State University Extension; RV Life].

A practical decision framework:

1. **If any single diagnostic characteristic is missing, absent, or ambiguous** (no flowers present at a growth stage where flowers are the key diagnostic feature; a smell that's "close but not quite right"), treat identification as unconfirmed and do not harvest for consumption. You may still photograph and note the location for a future visit when the missing feature becomes available.
2. **If the specimen is in a family with severe-risk look-alikes** (Apiaceae/carrot family, *Amanita* mushrooms, nightshades), require positive confirmation from an experienced second party before use, regardless of your own confidence level.
3. **If you are harvesting for a vulnerable household member** (children, pregnant or nursing individuals, elderly, immunocompromised), apply a stricter standard than you would for yourself — err toward skipping ambiguous specimens entirely.
4. **Environmental contamination is a separate ambiguity category**: even a correctly identified, unambiguously safe plant can be unsafe to harvest from contaminated ground. Extension guidance recommends avoiding roadsides, utility rights-of-way, industrial buffer zones, and agricultural field margins subject to spray drift, since plants readily take up herbicides, heavy metals, and other contaminants from soil, water, and air [Source: Mississippi State University Extension].

### 1.7 Documentation for Post-Harvest Verification

Because verification is fallible and consequences can be delayed (as with amatoxin poisoning), documenting each harvest creates a resource for retrospective diagnosis if symptoms appear:

- **Photograph before harvesting**: whole plant/fungus in habitat, close-up of the diagnostic features you used (leaf underside, stem base, gill structure and attachment for mushrooms, spore print if taken), and a wide shot showing surrounding vegetation and habitat.
- **Log harvest location and date**: GPS coordinates or a written location description, plus date and any co-located species observed. This matters because delayed-onset poisoning symptoms (amatoxin mushrooms in particular) can appear a day or more after consumption, by which point memory of exactly what was eaten is unreliable — but photographs and a harvest log are not.
- **Keep a small reference specimen when preserving mushrooms or unusual plants for later medicinal use**: a spore print (mushroom cap placed gill-down on paper overnight) or a pressed leaf/flower sample retained separately from the consumed portion gives poison control or a mycologist something physical to examine if a reaction occurs. This is standard advice from mycological societies and is one of the few genuinely low-cost, high-value safety practices available to home foragers.
- **If any symptoms develop after consuming wild-harvested food**, contact Poison Control (in the US: 1-800-222-1222) immediately and bring the photographs, spore print, or retained specimen with you if you seek medical care — amatoxin poisoning in particular is treatable if caught early, but the delayed-onset pattern means "I feel fine so far" is not a reliable signal in the first 24 hours [Source: CDC MMWR outbreak reports consistently note that delayed care-seeking, driven by the symptom-free interval, worsens outcomes].

## Part 2: Preservation of Wild Harvest

### 2.0 Science Foundation Recap

The two preservation guides referenced above ([Wave 3 Fermentation](../wave-3-fermentation-preservation/fermentation-preservation-complete-guide.md) and [Wave 4 Food Storage Beyond Canning](../wave-4-food-storage-preservation-advanced/food-storage-preservation-advanced-complete-guide.md)) cover water activity (aw) and pH as the two master variables controlling microbial safety in depth; this section gives only the summary needed for wild-harvest-specific decisions.

**Water activity (aw)**: the amount of "free" water available for microbial growth, distinct from total moisture content. FDA guidance treats aw 0.85 as the threshold below which most pathogenic bacteria (including *Staphylococcus aureus* and, critically, *Clostridium botulinum*) cannot grow or produce toxin, making a food shelf-stable without refrigeration from a pathogen standpoint [Source: FDA, *Water Activity (aw) in Foods*]. Mold, however, can grow at lower aw than bacteria — most molds are inhibited only below about aw 0.70–0.75, and essentially all microbial activity stops below aw 0.60 [Source: AQUALAB, *Water Activity 101*]. This matters directly for wild material: **dried herbs and mushrooms need to reach a lower moisture target than "dry to the touch" to be truly mold-safe**, not just bacteria-safe.

**pH**: the acidity threshold of 4.6 is the dividing line the FDA and USDA NCHFP use between high-acid foods (safely water-bath canned) and low-acid foods (which require pressure canning to reach the 240–250°F needed to destroy *C. botulinum* spores) [Source: NCHFP, *Ensuring Safe Canned Foods*; Clemson HGIC, *Canning Foods—the pH Factor*]. Almost all wild greens, roots, and mushrooms are low-acid foods and therefore require pressure canning, not water-bath canning, if canned at all (see 2.8).

### 2.1 Drying Leaves, Roots, Bark, and Berries

**General target**: dried plant material should reach roughly 10% residual moisture, at which point leaves and stems crumble and break rather than bend — this is the standard readiness test used by extension drying guidance [Source: Michigan State University Extension, *Dehydrating Produce Basics*]. At this moisture level, aw typically falls well below the 0.60–0.70 range needed to halt both bacterial and mold growth, provided drying was even and material is stored properly afterward (see below).

**Leaves and aerial parts (most wild greens, many medicinal herbs)**:
- Dehydrator or oven set to 95–115°F (up to 125°F in high-humidity climates), with drying times of roughly 1–4 hours for thin leafy material depending on thickness and humidity [Source: Oregon State University Extension, *Drying herbs*; National Center for Home Food Preservation, *Herbs*].
- Air-drying (hang-drying in small bundles in a warm, dark, well-ventilated space) works well for lower-moisture herbs and is the traditional method for bulk medicinal herb processing, but takes days rather than hours and carries higher mold risk in humid climates — it is not recommended for high-moisture wild greens (nettle, dandelion, wild lettuce) without supplemental airflow (fan) or a very dry climate.
- Darkness during drying matters for medicinal potency (light degrades many volatile and photosensitive compounds) even where it doesn't change food safety.

**Roots and rhizomes** (burdock, dandelion, chicory, wild ginger, echinacea): higher moisture content and denser tissue than leaves means roots need to be sliced thin (1/4 inch or less) or shredded before drying to dry through in a reasonable time and avoid case-hardening (a dry exterior sealing in a still-moist, mold-prone interior). Root slices generally take longer than leaves at the same dehydrator temperature (95–125°F) and should be tested for brittleness at the thickest point of the largest piece, not the thinnest.

**Bark** (willow, cherry, slippery elm, cottonwood): bark is typically stripped in narrow strips or small chips, which dry readily at room temperature or low dehydrator heat given bark's naturally low moisture content relative to leaf or root tissue; the main practical concern is complete drying through the full thickness of thicker bark pieces, tested by snapping — a properly dried piece snaps cleanly rather than bending.

**Berries**: whole small berries (elderberries, wild blueberries, chokecherries) dry slowly because their skin is relatively impermeable; cutting larger berries in half or lightly crushing smaller ones before drying substantially speeds drying and reduces mold risk from trapped interior moisture. Elderberries in particular should always be fully cooked or thoroughly dried before consumption — raw elderberries and unripe/uncooked elderberry parts (stems, leaves, unripe berries) contain cyanogenic glycosides that cause GI upset; this is a Tier 1–2 risk (unpleasant, not typically life-threatening in a healthy adult, but avoidable) addressed by standard preparation, not avoidance of the plant altogether.

**Wild mushrooms — the sharpest safety caveat in this section**: drying does **not** neutralize mushroom toxins. Amatoxins, muscarine, and other mushroom poisons are heat- and dehydration-stable, meaning a misidentified toxic mushroom is exactly as dangerous dried as fresh [Source: Oregon State University Extension, *Preserving mushrooms*]. Only dry mushrooms you have already fully verified as safe under the Part 1 protocol above — drying is never a remediation step for identification uncertainty. Beyond toxin identity, dried mushrooms carry a documented food-safety concern around pathogen survival if drying doesn't reduce aw below 0.85 quickly enough, and around bioaccumulation of environmental toxic elements (heavy metals) in wild-grown specimens, which is a separate concern from acute mushroom-species toxicity and is best managed by avoiding harvest from contaminated sites (industrial areas, roadsides, old orchards with historical pesticide use) [Source: peer-reviewed food-safety literature via PMC, *Health risk assessment of exposure to toxic elements resulting from consumption of dried wild-grown mushrooms*]. Good species for drying (chanterelles, shiitake, oyster, lobster mushroom, morel) should be thinly sliced and evenly spaced on drying trays [Source: Missouri Extension, *Preserve It Fresh, Preserve It Safe*].

**Storage after drying**: airtight, moisture- and vapor-resistant containers (glass jars, food-grade plastic, metal tins with gasket seals), stored in a cool, dark location. Light and heat degrade both flavor/potency and shelf life even when moisture is well controlled [Source: Michigan State University Extension].

### 2.2 Fermentation of Wild Greens

Lacto-fermentation of wild greens uses the same microbiology as fermenting cultivated cabbage or cucumbers (naturally occurring lactic acid bacteria outcompeting spoilage organisms in a salt-controlled, low-oxygen environment) — see the [Wave 3 Fermentation guide](../wave-3-fermentation-preservation/fermentation-preservation-complete-guide.md) for the full microbiology and troubleshooting reference. What's specific to wild greens:

**Salt ratio**: 2% salt by weight of fresh plant material is generally adequate for wild greens (dandelion, nettle, wild garlic/ramps, chickweed, lamb's quarters, sea beet/wild spinach relatives), with up to 3% acceptable depending on ambient temperature and desired firmness; this sits within the broader 1.5–5% tolerance range documented for lactic acid bacteria in vegetable fermentation generally, with 2–3% considered the flavor and safety sweet spot for leafy ferments specifically [Source: Cultured Guru, *The Perfect Lacto Fermentation Salt Ratio*; Galloway Wild Foods, *How to ferment wild greens*].

**Method**: the salt draws liquid out of the leaves via osmosis rather than acting as a standalone preservative; leaves are massaged with salt (similar to sauerkraut technique) until they release enough liquid to submerge themselves, then packed into a fermentation vessel under that liquid, weighted to keep material submerged and away from oxygen, and left at ambient temperature. Wild green ferments typically show the characteristic "fizzy," sour, lactic-tang mouthfeel within about a week at room temperature, though this varies with ambient temperature (faster in warm conditions, slower and often better-textured when fermented cooler) [Source: Totally Wild UK, *Lacto Fermenting Wild Ingredients*; Galloway Wild Foods].

**Nettle-specific note**: stinging nettle's formic-acid-based sting is fully neutralized by both the fermentation process and simple blanching/cooking, making nettle one of the most reliably safe and nutrient-dense wild greens for fermentation once past the sting-handling stage (gloves recommended during harvest and initial handling only).

**What fermentation does NOT solve**: fermentation reduces pH and creates an inhospitable environment for most spoilage and pathogenic bacteria, but it does not neutralize the mushroom or plant toxins discussed in Part 1. As with drying, fermentation is a preservation step for material already verified safe, never a remediation step for identification uncertainty.

### 2.3 Smoking Wild Mushrooms, Fish, and Game

Smoking is both a flavor process and — depending on temperature — a genuine preservation method, and the distinction matters for safety.

**Hot smoking** (used for most home fish and game preservation): cooks the food while smoking it, and USDA/CFIA guidance sets the safety target at reaching an internal temperature of at least 145°F, with many home-preservation sources recommending holding fish at 160°F internal for at least 30 minutes at some point in the smoking cycle (typically toward the end) to ensure a full pathogen kill [Source: Smoking Fish 101, *Fish Smoking Temperature Chart & Guide*; Food Safety Magazine, *Understanding Hot and Cold Smoked Fish Processing and Safety*]. Hot smoking temperatures are high enough to kill essentially all vegetative bacterial pathogens present.

**Cold smoking** (traditional for some fish and cured meats, typically below 90°F): does **not** cook the product and does not reach pathogen-killing temperatures on its own — cold smoking relies entirely on a preceding cure (salt/nitrite), controlled drying, strict sanitation, refrigeration, and process control to manage microbial risk, and cold smoke temperatures can actually *promote* certain bacterial growth if the preceding cure step is skipped or inadequate [Source: Food Safety Magazine; Oregon State University Seafood and Fisheries Extension, *Smoked Fish and Fishery Products Potential Food Safety Hazards*]. Cold smoking is meaningfully higher-risk than hot smoking for a home producer and should not be attempted without a properly executed brine/dry cure step first.

**Curing before smoking**: game and fish strips are typically cured in a brine solution for roughly 24 hours held at or below 40°F, then "cleared" — soaked in cold (<40°F) water for 30 minutes to 2 hours to draw excess surface salt back out and equalize salt distribution through the product — before smoking begins [Source: Penn State Extension, *Proper Processing of Wild Game and Fish*; UAF Cooperative Extension Service, *Smoking Fish at Home*].

**Parasite control**: fish that will be eaten without further cooking after cold smoking must be frozen first (standard commercial parasite-kill freezing protocols, generally -4°F for at least 7 days or -31°F for shorter periods per FDA fish and fishery products guidance) to destroy parasites that smoking temperatures alone will not reliably kill [Source: FDA, *Fish and Fishery Products Hazards and Controls Guidance*, Chapter 14; UAF Cooperative Extension Service].

**Smoking wild mushrooms**: mushrooms are smoked primarily for flavor (they don't carry the same bacterial-pathogen profile as raw meat/fish) but the same identification-verification rule from Part 1 applies without exception — smoking does not neutralize mushroom toxins any more than drying does.

**Wood/smoke source**: hardwoods (hickory, oak, apple, cherry, maple) are standard; softwood/conifer smoke (pine, fir, cedar) imparts bitter, resinous compounds and is generally avoided for food smoking, a longstanding practical rule reflected across extension smoking guidance rather than a formal safety prohibition.

### 2.4 Oil and Vinegar Infusions — The Botulism Risk

This is one of the highest-consequence, most commonly mishandled preservation methods for wild-harvested herbs, roots, and mushrooms, and deserves explicit treatment.

**The mechanism**: fresh plant material (garlic, wild herbs, roots, mushrooms) carries *Clostridium botulinum* spores from soil as a normal, unavoidable background presence. Submerging fresh, low-acid plant material (pH above 4.6) in oil creates exactly the anaerobic (oxygen-free), low-acid, room-temperature environment in which those spores can germinate and produce botulinum toxin — one of the most potent toxins known, undetectable by taste, smell, or appearance in contaminated oil [Source: Big Horn Olive Oil, *Botulism Risks in Infused Oils*; Foodie, *The Safety Risk To Know Before Making Garlic-Infused Olive Oil*]. The FDA has required commercial garlic-in-oil products to be acidified since 1991 specifically because of this risk, and the same physical chemistry applies identically to a home-infused wild-herb oil [Source: FDA guidance cited via Big Horn Olive Oil and Foodie reporting].

**Safe practices for home infused oils**:
1. **Use fully dried plant material, not fresh.** Properly dried herbs, roots, or garlic (see 2.1 above — moisture reduced to the point of brittleness) contain essentially no free water, and *C. botulinum* cannot germinate or produce toxin in the absence of water, regardless of pH or oxygen exposure. This is the single most reliable home-safe method for infusing oil with wild herbs [Source: Big Horn Olive Oil].
2. **Acidify fresh material before infusing**, if fresh flavor/potency is specifically needed — a documented, published acidification procedure (not ad hoc vinegar addition) is required to reliably lower pH to a botulism-safe level; consumer acidification protocols for garlic and common culinary herbs exist in the food-safety literature but require accurate measurement, not estimation [Source: *Acidification of Garlic and Herbs for Consumer Preparation*, Food Protection Trends, July/August 2014].
3. **If using fresh material without acidification** (not recommended for shelf-stable storage), refrigerate immediately at all times and use within 2–4 days — refrigeration slows but does not eliminate the *C. botulinum* risk (the organism can still grow, very slowly, at refrigerator temperatures in some circumstances), so this is a short-term-use compromise, not a shelf-stable preservation method [Source: Big Horn Olive Oil, *Guide to Safe Storage and Labeling of Infused Oils*].
4. **Never store fresh-material infused oil at room temperature "to develop flavor."** This is the single most common home-kitchen practice responsible for actual botulism poisoning cases and directly contradicts food-safety guidance.

**Vinegar infusions are a different, much safer category**: vinegar's low pH (typically 2.4–3.4, well below the 4.6 botulism threshold) makes vinegar-based herbal infusions (herbal vinegars, fire cider-style preparations) inherently much lower risk for botulism than oil infusions, because the anaerobic-but-neutral-pH environment that enables *C. botulinum* growth in oil simply doesn't exist in an acidic vinegar base. The main safety consideration for vinegar infusions is mold growth if plant material is not kept fully submerged, which is a quality issue rather than an acute toxin risk.

### 2.5 Root Storage

Wild roots (burdock, chicory, wild carrot family roots once positively identified as safe, Jerusalem artichoke/sunchoke, cattail rhizome, wild onion/ramp bulbs) follow the same cold-storage physics as cultivated root vegetables, covered in depth in the [Wave 4 Food Storage guide's root cellaring section](../wave-4-food-storage-preservation-advanced/food-storage-preservation-advanced-complete-guide.md). The wild-harvest-specific considerations:

- **Curing/cleaning before storage**: wild roots typically carry more adhering soil and require more thorough (but non-damaging) cleaning than farmed roots grown in cultivated, rock-free beds; avoid washing roots intended for extended storage — brush off loose soil and store unwashed, washing only just before use, since added surface moisture from washing accelerates rot in storage.
- **Damage sensitivity**: wild-dug roots are more prone to digging damage (nicks, breaks) than carefully cultivated roots, and damaged roots should be used first or processed immediately (dried, fermented) rather than placed into extended cold storage, since damaged tissue is a primary entry point for rot organisms.
- **Target storage conditions**: cool (32–40°F) and humid (90–95% relative humidity) for most root vegetables, achieved via a root cellar, a buried clamp/pit, or a refrigerator crisper drawer at household scale — full construction and management detail is in the Wave 4 guide's root cellaring section rather than repeated here.
- **Species-specific note — cattail rhizome**: cattail rhizomes store poorly fresh due to high starch and moisture content that promotes rapid rot; drying and grinding into flour is generally a more reliable long-term storage strategy than attempting fresh cold storage.

### 2.6 Seed Storage and Viability Testing

Wild-collected seed (for replanting wild-foraged food or medicinal plants, or for eating as a food source such as acorns, hickory nuts, or wild grass seed) needs both proper storage conditions and periodic viability verification if intended for propagation rather than immediate consumption.

**Storage conditions**: the two controlling variables are seed moisture content and storage temperature. General guidance is to store seed cool, dry, and dark — ideally around 40°F, in an airtight container if refrigerated, to exclude ambient humidity [Source: Dane County (WI) Extension, *Testing Seed Viability*]. Cold storage measurably preserves seed vigor and germination rate compared to room-temperature storage in published trials [Source: PMC, *Assessment of Seed Viability Before and After Storage in Forage Pea*].

**Viability testing methods**:
- **Germination test (most reliable for small seed lots)**: place a sample of 10 seeds on a damp paper towel, roll or fold it, seal in a plastic bag to retain moisture, and hold in a warm location (above 70°F) for several days to a week, then count the germination rate [Source: Illinois Extension, *Test Seed Viability*].
- **Water float test (quick screen for larger seeds)**: submerge seed in water; seeds that sink are generally more likely to be viable (fuller, denser), while floaters are more likely non-viable or insect-damaged — this is a fast triage test, not a definitive viability measurement, and should be confirmed with a germination test for seed you're depending on.
- **Tetrazolium staining (higher-precision, requires reagent)**: a chemical test where living tissue stains red/pink and dead tissue does not; more precise than a float test but requires access to tetrazolium chloride solution, making it less practical for casual home use than the paper-towel germination test [Source: PMC, *Seed viability testing for research and conservation of epiphytic and terrestrial orchids*, describing the broader methodology].

**Viability duration varies widely by species** — some seed (many alliums, parsley-family seed) remains viable only a year or two even under good storage, while other seed (many brassicas, some tree nuts if handled correctly) remains viable for four or more years [Source: Dane County Extension]. Because published viability-duration data is sparse for many wild species specifically, periodic re-testing (annually, before a planned planting) of any wild-collected seed you're relying on is more reliable than assuming duration figures from cultivated relatives transfer directly.

### 2.7 Canning Wild-Harvested Foods

Canning wild-harvested greens, roots, mushrooms, or meats/fish follows exactly the same pH-driven safety rules as canning cultivated equivalents — there is no wild-specific canning method, and the danger of treating wild food as a special case is exactly backwards: **almost all wild-harvested plant and animal food is low-acid** (pH well above 4.6) and therefore requires pressure canning, not water-bath canning, to reach the 240–250°F needed to destroy *C. botulinum* spores [Source: NCHFP, *Ensuring Safe Canned Foods*; Clemson HGIC, *Canning Foods—the pH Factor*].

Key points specific to wild material:
- **Never water-bath can wild greens, roots, mushrooms, or wild meat/fish** — these are uniformly low-acid foods regardless of any tartness or sourness in raw taste; taste is not a reliable pH indicator.
- **Only wild fruit (berries) with confirmed high acidity** may be appropriate for water-bath canning as jam, jelly, or fruit preserves, and even then, only using a tested recipe from NCHFP or an equivalent research-based source, not an improvised ratio.
- **Use only tested, published recipes** for processing time and pressure — because wild ingredients vary more in density, moisture, and piece size than uniform cultivated produce, deviations from a tested recipe's cut size or jar-fill method can compromise heat penetration in ways that are hard to predict without lab testing, which is exactly the process NCHFP recipes have already been through [Source: NCHFP general canning guidance].
- **No home method reliably measures whether a batch reached a safe internal temperature/time combination inside a pressure canner** other than using a calibrated pressure canner correctly per its manual and a validated recipe — pH test strips can confirm a food's acidity category but cannot substitute for correct processing time and pressure once that category is established [Source: Oklahoma State University Extension, *pH and Home Canning*].

Given the added handling and quantity variability of wild harvest, freezing (2.8) or drying (2.1) are frequently more forgiving and lower-risk preservation choices for a home producer than pressure canning, particularly for anyone without an already-calibrated pressure canner and established recipe library.

### 2.8 Freezing vs. Fresh Storage Trade-offs

Freezing is, in food-safety terms, the most forgiving preservation method available for wild harvest — freezing does not destroy *C. botulinum* spores or most other pathogens, but it also does not create the anaerobic-plus-low-acid-plus-room-temperature conditions those organisms need to grow and produce toxin, so freezing carries essentially none of the acute botulism risk profile that oil infusion or improper canning does.

**Trade-offs specific to wild material**:
- **Texture loss**: high-water-content wild greens and some mushrooms lose significant texture on freeze-thaw (ice crystal cell damage), more so than firmer cultivated vegetables — blanching before freezing (a brief boil or steam, then ice-water shock) reduces enzymatic degradation during frozen storage and is standard practice for wild greens intended for more than a few weeks of freezer storage.
- **Mushroom-specific**: most wild mushrooms benefit from being cooked (sautéed) before freezing rather than frozen raw, both for texture and because this partially reduces the water content that otherwise causes freezer-burn-prone, soggy results on thawing.
- **Freezer dependency risk**: freezing trades away shelf-stability for dependence on continuous power — in a resilience-planning context, an all-freezer strategy for a wild harvest is a single point of failure during extended power loss, which is the core argument (developed further in Part 5) for diversifying wild-harvest preservation across drying, fermenting, and freezing rather than relying on any single method.
- **Fresh/short-term storage**: for the shortest-term case (a few days), most wild greens and mushrooms store better refrigerated loosely wrapped in a breathable material (paper, not sealed plastic, which traps moisture and accelerates rot/sliming) than frozen immediately, preserving flavor and texture for near-term use while reserving freezing capacity for the portion of harvest that won't be used within a few days.

## Part 3: Processing for Use

### 3.1 Decoctions vs. Infusions for Medicinal Wild Plants

These are the two basic hot-water extraction methods for medicinal plant material, and the choice between them is driven by which part of the plant you're using, not personal preference.

**Infusions** (like tea): used for delicate plant parts — leaves, flowers, and soft aerial parts — where prolonged boiling would destroy the desired volatile oils or heat-sensitive compounds. Standard method: pour water just off the boil (not actively boiling) over 1–2 teaspoons of dried herb (or roughly double for fresh) per cup, cover to retain volatile compounds, and steep 10–20 minutes depending on the plant and desired strength [Source: general herbalist preparation-method literature, consistent across herbal medicine references].

**Decoctions**: used for dense, structural plant parts — roots, bark, seeds, and hard berries — where the desired compounds are bound more tightly in cell structure and require sustained heat to extract fully. Standard method: place plant material in cold water, bring to a simmer (not a hard boil), and simmer covered for 20–45 minutes depending on material density, then strain. Roots and bark generally need the longer end of that range; seeds and softer bark the shorter end.

**Cold infusions**: for compounds that are actually degraded by heat (some mucilaginous plants, certain delicate volatile-oil-bearing herbs), a cold or room-temperature overnight steep extracts effectively without heat degradation, at the cost of a much longer preparation time (typically 6–12 hours).

**Water vs. alcohol as solvent**: water-based preparations (infusion, decoction) extract water-soluble compounds well (many minerals, mucilage, some alkaloids, tannins) but do not effectively extract resins, many essential oils, or fat-soluble constituents — for those, alcohol extraction (tincturing, 3.2) is the more effective method. Herbalist practice commonly recommends alcohol extraction specifically for plants where the therapeutically active compounds are known to be poorly water-soluble.

### 3.2 Tincture Extraction

A tincture is an alcohol (or alcohol/water/glycerin) extraction of plant material, valued for concentration, extraction of both water- and alcohol-soluble compounds, and long shelf life compared to water-based preparations.

**The folk method** (volume-based, no scale required): fill a clean jar loosely with plant material, cover completely with alcohol, cap, and steep — this is the most widely used home approach and produces a working, if imprecisely standardized, extract [Source: Southern Exposure Seed Exchange, *Herbal Tinctures: The Folk Method*].

**Standard ratios** (weight-to-volume method, more reproducible): fresh herb is typically tinctured at roughly 1:2 (herb weight to alcohol volume), while dried herb is tinctured at roughly 1:5, reflecting dried material's concentrated, lower-moisture state; the folk method's loose-fill approach naturally lands in a comparable 1:4 to 1:6 range for dried herb, which is why it works reasonably well as an approximation of formal clinical-herbalism ratios without requiring a scale [Source: Herbal Academy, *Demystifying Weight-to-Volume Tinctures*; Remedys Nutrition, *Folk Method for Tinctures*].

**Alcohol proof**: dried-herb tinctures are generally made with 80–100 proof (40–50% ABV) alcohol — dried material's low residual moisture (roughly 5–10%) means it doesn't dilute the alcohol significantly, keeping the final ABV stable enough to inhibit microbial growth and mold. Fresh-herb tinctures need higher-proof alcohol (100 proof/50% ABV rather than 80 proof) because the water content of fresh plant material dilutes the alcohol as it macerates, and the jar is typically filled only half with fresh plant material (rather than loosely packed as with dried) to control this dilution effect [Source: Remedys Nutrition; Herbal Academy].

**Maceration time**: standard practice is 4–6 weeks, with daily agitation (shaking the jar) recommended especially during the first week to keep plant material from settling and to promote even extraction, followed by straining through cheesecloth or a fine mesh and pressing plant material to recover retained liquid.

**Shelf life**: alcohol-based tinctures at proper proof are self-preserving and commonly cited as retaining potency for several years when stored in a cool, dark location in a sealed, ideally dark-glass, container — this significantly exceeds the shelf life of dried herb alone (see 3.3) or water-based preparations, which is the core practical advantage of tincturing for a resilience medicine cabinet.

### 3.3 Powdering and Capsule Filling from Dried Wild Plants

Powdering fully dried herb (using a dedicated herb grinder, coffee grinder reserved for herbs, or mortar and pestle) increases surface area and makes material usable in capsules, as a wound-dusting powder, or as a more easily dosed food/drink additive — but this comes at a real shelf-life cost.

**Shelf-life trade-off**: powdering an herb roughly halves its remaining shelf life compared to leaving it in whole dried form, because the increased surface area accelerates oxidative and volatile-compound loss; practical guidance is to use powdered herb within 6–12 months of grinding, and to grind material close to time of use rather than powdering a large batch far in advance [Source: The Herbal Academy, *What Every Herbalist Should Know About Herbal Preparation Shelf Life*; Chestnut Herbs, *Storing Dried Herbs and Herbal Preparations*].

**Capsule filling**: using empty gelatin or vegetarian (HPMC) capsules, a digital kitchen scale, and a bowl for hand-filling, calculate the total powder needed by multiplying the target daily dose by the number of days' supply desired, then divide by capsule size to determine capsule count — most concentrated dried-herb powder dosing in herbalist practice tops out around 9 grams/day, though this varies substantially by specific herb and intended use [Source: Herbal Academy, *Herbal Capsules 101*].

**Labeling and storage**: every batch of filled capsules should be labeled with the herb(s) used, fill date, dosage per capsule, and an estimated expiration date, then stored in a cool, dark, dry container — properly stored capsules are generally usable for up to about a year, consistent with the general powdered-herb shelf-life guidance above [Source: Herbal Academy, *Herbal Capsules 101*].

### 3.4 Rendering Wild Fats

Rendering — melting raw animal fat to separate pure fat from connective tissue, blood, and membrane — turns wild game fat (deer/venison tallow, wild boar lard, bear fat) into a stable, long-shelf-life cooking fat and preservation medium (see also Section 2.4's confit/oil-packing cross-reference in the Wave 4 guide).

**Wet rendering**: chop fat into small pieces, place in a pot with a small amount of added water (which prevents scorching during the initial melt and helps separate impurities), and heat slowly on low — slow, gentle heat produces a cleaner, better-tasting result than rushing the process with high heat [Source: MeatEater Wild Foods, *How to Render Deer Fat into Tallow*; Montana Homesteader, *How to render fat into tallow and lard*].

**Dry rendering**: the same slow-melt process without added water, requiring closer heat monitoring to prevent scorching since there's no water buffer, but producing fat with no residual moisture to remove afterward — a practical advantage for fat intended for room-temperature (not refrigerated) storage.

**Clarification**: straining rendered fat through cheesecloth removes solid particulate, and a secondary wash step — remelting the strained fat with a small amount of water, letting it cool so blood and impurities settle to the water layer at the bottom while clean fat solidifies on top, then scraping off the fat cake and discarding the water/impurity layer — produces a cleaner, longer-storing final product [Source: Wild Reid Ranch, *How to Wet Render Tallow*].

**Contamination and rancidity control**: the dominant practical failure mode is not microbial spoilage but oxidative rancidity, driven primarily by (a) contamination with hair, blood, or gut contents during initial fat collection and (b) delayed processing after warm-temperature exposure — wild game fat, being relatively high in polyunsaturated fat compared to some farmed animals, is more rancidity-prone if the carcass sits in above-freezing conditions before fat is collected and rendered [Source: The Rustic Elk, *Venison Fat & Rendering Deer Tallow*; American Hunter/NRA, *How to Render and Use Wild Game Fat*].

**Storage and spoilage signal**: properly rendered and clarified tallow/lard, being highly saturated, is shelf-stable at room temperature for roughly a year or longer in a sealed container in a cool, dark location; rancidity is reliably detectable by smell (a rotten, "off" odor distinct from the fat's normal mild smell) and any rancid-smelling fat should be discarded rather than used [Source: Montana Homesteader; The Rustic Elk].

### 3.5 Dyeing with Wild Plants

Natural dyeing extracts pigment from wild plant material (bark, roots, berries, nuts, flowers, lichens) to color natural fiber, and — while primarily a craft rather than a food-safety topic — carries its own safety and technique considerations relevant to a resilience skillset.

**Mordants**: a mordant is a chemical bridge that binds plant pigment to fiber, dramatically improving colorfastness (resistance to fading/washing out) compared to unmordanted dyeing [Source: My Golden Thimble, *Using Natural Mordants for Dyeing*]. Alum (potassium aluminum sulfate) is the most widely used and is considered the safest common mordant for home use — it produces bright, clear colors and is generally regarded as safe to handle with basic precautions (gloves, avoiding ingestion, adequate ventilation) [Source: Casa Caribe, *The Art of Fixing Colors: A Guide to Mordants*; Little Yellow Wheelbarrow, *Mordants for Natural Dyes*]. Iron (ferrous sulfate) is the other commonly recommended mordant for home use, producing darker, often grey-toned shifts in color, and is likewise considered comparatively safe when handled with basic precautions.

**Mordants to avoid**: historically used heavy-metal mordants — tin, chrome/dichromate, and lead — carry meaningfully greater toxicity and environmental-disposal concerns and have been largely abandoned in contemporary natural-dyeing practice specifically because of their health impact; this guide does not recommend their use in a home/household setting [Source: ResearchGate, *Natural dye mordants and their health hazards*].

**Mordant-free wild dyeing**: some wild and semi-wild plant dyes bond reasonably well to fiber without a separate mordant step, including black walnut hull (deep brown, notably colorfast without mordant due to walnut's naturally tannin-rich, substantive dye chemistry), pokeberry (though notably less colorfast), and various barks and lichens rich in natural tannins — tannin-rich plant material generally performs best without added mordant because tannins themselves act as a natural fixative.

**Skin safety**: most plant-based dyes and the alum/iron mordant process are low-risk for skin contact with basic precautions (gloves during the mordanting and dye-bath stages, since prolonged contact can dry or mildly irritate skin, particularly with alum in concentrated form) — the greater safety concern in natural dyeing is inhalation exposure during the mordant-preparation and simmering stage (adequate ventilation) rather than skin absorption.

### 3.6 Making Cordage from Wild Fibers

Cordage — twisted plant fiber rope or string — is a core wildcraft skill with direct resilience value (repair, construction, snares, tool-hafting) and draws on a distinct set of wild plant species valued specifically for long, strong bast (inner bark) fiber.

**Key fiber sources**: stinging nettle, dogbane (*Apocynum*), and milkweed all contain long, tough bast fibers located between the outer bark/skin and the woody stem core, and are among the most commonly used cordage plants in North American wildcraft tradition [Source: Better Bushcraft, *How to Craft Cordage from Natural Fibers*]. Western red cedar and eastern arborvitae inner bark produces cordage that remains notably strong and flexible even when wet, historically valued for cordage used in wet environments; basswood (linden) inner bark is prized for producing long-lasting, easily processed cordage fiber [Source: WildCraft Survival, *Cordage from Natural Fibers*].

**Bark stripping timing**: bark is generally easiest to strip, and yields the best fiber, during the growing season (spring through summer) when sap is actively flowing and the cambium layer is moist — this is when bark separates most cleanly from the wood beneath it. Cedar bark cordage material is typically harvested by making long, parallel longitudinal cuts and peeling extended strips, which can be used directly as coarse cordage or split further into finer fiber bundles for refined work [Source: WildCraft Survival]. Willow bark can often be split and used as cordage fiber directly, without a retting step, distinguishing it from bast-fiber plants that require retting to separate fiber from surrounding plant tissue.

**Retting (soaking) process**: for bast-fiber plants (nettle, dogbane, milkweed) where usable fiber is bound within the stem rather than easily peeled as with bark, stems are submerged in water for roughly one to two weeks, with periodic checking, until the outer stem tissue softens and sloughs away, leaving the inner fiber strands intact and separable by hand without breaking [Source: Trail Trek Tribe, *Making Cordage from Plant Fibers*; Better Bushcraft]. Over-retting (too many days) weakens and degrades the fiber itself, so periodic checking rather than a fixed timeline is the correct method.

**Drying**: after retting or bark stripping, fiber is typically dried before final twisting into cordage, since dry fiber twists more predictably and produces tighter, more durable finished cordage than fiber twisted while still wet.

**Twisting technique — the reverse wrap**: the standard hand-twisting method divides a fiber bundle into two roughly equal strands, twists each strand individually in one direction (commonly clockwise) to build torque, then wraps the two twisted strands around each other in the *opposite* direction, which locks the twist in place and produces a stable, balanced two-ply cord that won't spontaneously untwist under tension — this reverse-wrap method is the standard technique taught across bushcraft and primitive-skills sources for producing durable natural cordage [Source: Wilderness College, *Cordage: Making Natural Rope*; Better Bushcraft].

## Part 4: Legal and Ethical Boundaries

### 4.1 State and Federal Regulations Vary Significantly by Jurisdiction and Land Type

There is no single "wild harvesting law" in the United States — rules vary by land ownership (federal, state, county, private), specific agency (National Forest vs. National Park vs. state park vs. state forest), and species, and they change over time. The following representative examples illustrate the *range* of regulatory approaches; they are not a substitute for checking current rules for your specific location before harvesting.

**California**: harvesting wild plants in California State Parks is illegal in nearly all units — the state park regulation prohibits picking, digging, or cutting any tree, plant, flower, foliage, berry, fruit, grass, shrub, cone, or dead wood except where specifically authorized and posted by park management, with penalties up to six months in jail or a $1,000 fine for unauthorized harvesting on state lands [Source: California Department of Fish and Wildlife, *Permits for State-listed Plant Species*; SOMA (Sonoma County Mycological Association), *Mushroom Picking Rules and Regulations*]. A narrow exception exists at specific California state forest units (e.g., Jackson Demonstration State Forest) where mushroom foraging is allowed under a paid permit system, distinct from state park rules [Source: SOMA].

**Oregon**: considerably more permissive than California for personal-use foraging. Oregon state law allows gathering berries, fruits, mushrooms, and similar edibles for personal consumption up to one gallon per person per day in state parks and recreation areas without a permit; National Forest rules in Oregon vary by forest but commonly allow one to two gallons of mushrooms per person per day either with no permit or with a free-use permit, and truffles are regulated separately under a dedicated Oregon permit system (House Bill 2615) applying to both private and state forestland [Source: Oregon Discovery, *Oregon Mushroom Picking Guide*; Siuslaw National Forest, *Mushroom Permits*, USDA Forest Service; Cascade Mycological Society, *Mushroom Picking Permits*].

**Appalachian region (wild American ginseng)**: ginseng is the most heavily regulated wild plant in the eastern US due to international trade demand and documented overharvest pressure. Nineteen states allow wild ginseng collection during a restricted fall harvest season; federal law requires that exported roots be legally dug in season from plants at least five years old (identifiable by having at least three prongs/leaf clusters — one- and two-prong plants are too young and diggers are directed not to harvest them) [Source: US Fish & Wildlife Service, *Ginseng*]. Because American ginseng is listed under CITES Appendix II due to international overharvest concerns, wild and "wild-simulated" (forest-grown but unmanaged) ginseng roots must be certified by the state or tribal authority where harvested before any interstate or international transport, and export shipments are inspected by USDA-APHIS, which verifies CITES permits and examines root stem-scar counts to confirm harvest-age compliance [Source: USFWS, *American Ginseng Export Program (CITES)*; USFWS, *Operation Root Cause*]. The bulk of legal wild ginseng exports historically originate from southern Appalachian states — Kentucky, West Virginia, and Tennessee prominent among them [Source: USFWS].

**Midwest / National Forest special forest products (general pattern)**: on most National Forest land, small personal-use quantities of non-timber forest products (mushrooms, berries, boughs, cones) are treated as "incidental use" and don't require a permit, while any quantity intended for sale is classified as commercial harvest and requires a separate paid permit; state forest and state DNR lands (e.g., Minnesota DNR) generally follow a similar personal-use/commercial distinction, with specific harvest permits required for larger-scale or decorative-product harvesting [Source: USDA Forest Service, *Forest Product Permits*, BLM; Minnesota DNR, *Harvesting decorative forest products*; Alaska Division of Mining, Land, and Water, *Non-Timber Forest Product Commercial Harvest Permit*, illustrating the personal-use/commercial permit structure used across multiple state systems].

### 4.2 Private Land vs. Public Land Harvesting Rights

Private land harvesting requires explicit landowner permission in virtually every US jurisdiction — there is no general public right to forage on private land absent specific landowner authorization, and this applies even to land that appears unused or unposted [Source: consistent across Forest Service, state, and foraging-advocacy sources reviewed]. Public land rules are land-type-specific as detailed in 4.1: National Forests generally permit some personal-use harvest without a permit, National Parks generally prohibit foraging entirely (with very narrow historical/tribal-treaty exceptions), and state park/state forest rules vary widely by state as illustrated by the California/Oregon contrast above [Source: Lewis & Clark National Park Association, *Foraging in National Parks*].

### 4.3 Sustainable Harvesting Practices

**The 10% rule and related harvest-limiting practices**: ethical wildcrafting guidance from conservation-oriented herbalist organizations recommends taking no more than roughly 10% of any given wild stand in a season, leaving the oldest and largest individual plants in place specifically because their genetics have proven most successful/resilient in that site and their continued presence supports stand recovery and reproduction [Source: United Plant Savers guidance as summarized in *A Beginner's 5-Step Guide to Wildcrafting*, Ann Marie Gianni]. Preferentially harvesting younger plants, or a scattered subset across a wide area rather than concentrating harvest in one patch, further reduces pressure on any single population.

**United Plant Savers At-Risk List**: United Plant Savers (UpS), formed in response to documented decline in wild medicinal plant populations, maintains a formal "At-Risk" list of 40+ medicinal plant species under significant wildcrafting and habitat-loss pressure — American ginseng and goldenseal are flagship examples — along with a stricter "Critical" sub-category (currently including species such as trillium, peyote, and Venus flytrap) reserved for species that are federally or state protected and/or at very high extinction risk [Source: United Plant Savers, *Species At-Risk List*]. UpS guidance is direct: for at-risk-listed species, do not wildcraft at all — seek cultivated sources instead — and where wild harvesting of a non-listed species is undertaken, work within all applicable federal, state, and local regulations and harvest limits [Source: United Plant Savers; Mountain Rose Herbs blog, *United Plant Savers: Protecting At-Risk Native Medicinal Plants*].

**Regeneration-aware harvest technique**: for root harvest specifically, taking only a portion of a rhizome or root clump and leaving the remainder in place with growing points intact allows many perennial plants to regenerate rather than being killed outright by harvest — a widely cited but species-specific technique that requires knowing which species tolerate partial-root harvest and which do not.

### 4.4 Invasive vs. Native Species — Which to Harvest, Which to Discourage

Not all wildcrafting decisions carry the same conservation stakes — harvesting an invasive, non-native species that is actively displacing native ecosystems is functionally the opposite of harvesting a rare native species, and treating them identically under a "minimize impact" ethic misses this distinction.

**Garlic mustard** (*Alliaria petiolata*) is a widely cited example: a European biennial invasive that displaces native forest-floor plant communities across much of the eastern and midwestern US, garlic mustard is also fully edible — young leaves are usable fresh in salads, pesto, and cooked dishes, and the plant is nutritionally notable for vitamin C, zinc, and vitamin E content [Source: Michigan United Conservation Clubs, *Garlic Mustard: The Edible Invasive*; National Geographic, *Invasive garlic mustard hurts native species*]. It does contain cyanogenic compounds, but at levels lower than many common staple foods (cassava, lima beans, sorghum), and the cyanide risk is straightforwardly managed by standard preparation — chopping (which releases most of the volatile cyanide compound within minutes) or cooking/soaking, both of which are standard culinary practice for the plant regardless of the cyanide question [Source: National Geographic]. Extension services in affected states (Illinois, Minnesota) explicitly promote foraging as one practical control strategy alongside mechanical removal, framing harvest as ecologically beneficial rather than merely neutral [Source: University of Illinois Extension, *Invasive Garlic Mustard*; University of Minnesota Extension, *Garlic mustard*].

**General principle**: for confirmed invasive species with no lookalike-safety concerns and no conservation downside, unrestricted harvest is both legal in most jurisdictions (invasive species are frequently exempt from the harvest-limit and permit structures that apply to native species) and ecologically constructive. For native species — especially UpS At-Risk-listed or CITES-regulated species like ginseng — the sustainable-harvest practices in 4.3 apply in full. The practical takeaway for a resilience wildcrafter: learn to identify a handful of common, confidently-distinguishable invasive food/medicine species in your region (garlic mustard, Japanese knotweed, and multiflora rose hips are common examples across much of the eastern US) as a lower-stakes, higher-volume harvest category to prioritize over native at-risk species.

## Part 5: Integration with Food and Medicine Systems

### 5.1 Reliability Assessment and Seasonal Calendar

Wild harvest is inherently less predictable than cultivated or stored food — yield varies year to year with weather, and availability is sharply seasonal, concentrated into short windows for many high-value wild foods (morels for perhaps 3–6 weeks in spring in a given region; ramps for a similarly narrow spring window; most fruiting mushroom flushes triggered by specific rain-and-temperature conditions rather than a fixed calendar date). A resilience food plan that treats wild harvest as a *reliable, plannable* caloric or nutritional source is building on a weak foundation; a plan that treats it as a *valuable but variable supplement*, with preservation capacity built to bank the good years and good weeks, is far more robust.

**Practical seasonal calendar logic** (temperate North America, adjust to local climate):
- **Early spring**: young greens (nettle, dandelion, chickweed, wild garlic/ramps), tree sap (maple, birch) if applicable to region.
- **Mid-late spring**: morel mushrooms (short window), more greens, early berries in southern/warm regions.
- **Summer**: berries (many species), summer greens, early nuts in some regions, many culinary/medicinal flowering herbs at peak potency.
- **Late summer–fall**: tree nuts (acorns, hickory, walnut), fall mushroom flush (chanterelles, boletes, oysters depending on region and rainfall), roots at peak size and storage-readiness, ginseng (in-season states only, per 4.1).
- **Winter**: bark harvest (some species, dormant-season sap chemistry), very limited fresh options in most temperate climates — this is the season where preserved wild harvest (Part 2) is consumed rather than gathered.

### 5.2 Nutritional and Caloric Contribution — Realistic Expectations

Most wild greens and many wild mushrooms are nutritionally valuable (vitamins, minerals, some unique phytochemicals) but caloric-density-poor relative to cultivated staple crops or stored grain/legume calories — a pound of foraged greens contributes meaningfully to micronutrient intake but trivially to total caloric need compared to a pound of stored rice, beans, or potatoes. The wild-harvested foods that *do* contribute meaningfully to caloric planning are concentrated in a small number of categories: tree nuts (acorns after leaching, hickory, black walnut — genuinely calorie-dense and storable), wild game and fish (protein- and fat-dense, addressed in the Wave 4 guide's curing/smoking/rendering sections and this guide's Section 3.4), and to a lesser extent starchy wild roots (cattail rhizome flour, some tuber species) processed into flour or meal. A realistic resilience plan treats wild greens, berries, and most mushrooms primarily as nutritional and dietary-variety supplements, and treats nuts, wild game/fish, and starchy roots as the categories with genuine caloric-planning relevance.

### 5.3 Backup Strategies When Wild Sources Are Unreliable

Because wild yield varies year to year and is subject to factors outside household control (drought suppressing fruiting, an early frost cutting short a harvest window, land-access changes), a resilient system builds in explicit backups rather than assuming a given wild resource will be available on a given schedule:

1. **Diversify preservation methods across a single harvest** rather than committing an entire good-year harvest to one method (freezer-dependent, in particular, is a single point of failure) — split a large harvest across drying, fermenting, and freezing so a freezer failure or a mold problem in one batch doesn't eliminate the entire year's preserved supply.
2. **Maintain cultivated backup sources for anything load-bearing** — if a specific wild medicinal herb or food plant is central to household planning, growing a cultivated stand (garden bed, forest-garden planting per the [Wave 4 Permaculture and Agroforestry guide](../wave-4-permaculture-agroforestry/permaculture-agroforestry-complete-guide.md)) removes dependence on a single wild population's year-to-year variability and on continued land access.
3. **Track multi-year yield patterns** where possible (a simple harvest log noting date, location, and approximate quantity year over year) to build a realistic, household-specific sense of reliability rather than assuming any single good year represents a dependable baseline.
4. **Build slack into any plan that assumes wild-harvested calories or medicine as anything more than a supplement** — treat wild harvest as upside (extending and diversifying a food/medicine system built on stored and cultivated staples) rather than as core infrastructure the plan depends on.

### 5.4 Comparison to Cultivated Alternatives

**Wild mushrooms vs. cultivated mushrooms**: cultivated mushrooms (oyster, shiitake, button) offer a predictable, year-round, zero-misidentification-risk alternative to wild foraging, at the cost of requiring dedicated cultivation infrastructure (logs, substrate, controlled humidity) and generally offering less species diversity than what's available wild in a good season. For a household prioritizing food-safety certainty over variety, cultivated mushroom production is the lower-risk choice; for a household with strong identification skills and access to good wild mushroom habitat, wild foraging offers meaningfully greater diversity and zero cultivation infrastructure cost.

**Foraged greens vs. garden greens**: garden-grown greens offer predictable yield, known growing-condition history (no contamination-site uncertainty), and no identification risk, but require garden space, season-appropriate planning, and ongoing cultivation labor; wild greens (nettle, dandelion, lamb's quarters, chickweed) often appear as "free" volunteer nutrition with minimal effort but carry the identification-verification burden covered in Part 1 and the contamination-site caution from Section 1.6. Many of the most valuable wild greens (nettle, lamb's quarters, dandelion) are also easily semi-domesticated — encouraged or transplanted into a managed garden bed — which captures much of the low-effort benefit of wild greens while reducing both identification uncertainty (you know what you planted) and contamination risk (you control the growing site), representing a practical middle path between fully wild and fully cultivated food sourcing.

## Conclusion

Wild-harvested plants, fungi, and other wild food and medicine sources can meaningfully diversify and extend a household's resilience food and medicine system — but the two steps that actually determine whether that harvest is safe and useful are not identification (which is a separate, hands-on skill this guide deliberately does not teach) but **verification** and **preservation**. Verification means applying rigorous, multi-characteristic confirmation before committing any effort to a specimen, understanding which mistakes are genuinely dangerous versus merely unpleasant, and defaulting to discarding ambiguous material rather than risking a Tier 3 poisoning outcome. Preservation means applying the same water-activity, pH, and temperature science that governs all food safety — not inventing new rules for wild material — while being alert to the specific ways wild-harvested material differs in practice from cultivated equivalents: greater moisture variability, higher contamination-site risk, more irregular harvest timing and volume, and processing categories (cordage, natural dyeing, wild fat rendering) that rarely apply to garden or store-bought food.

Handled with this discipline, wild harvest is a genuine asset to a resilience system: a source of nutritional diversity, medicinal plant material at a fraction of purchased cost, and skills (cordage, dyeing, fat rendering) with real utility beyond food and medicine alone. Handled without it — treating field-guide matching as sufficient verification, treating wild material as exempt from standard food-safety chemistry, or treating a good wild-harvest year as a dependable planning baseline — the same practices carry real risk of poisoning, wasted effort, or a false sense of food security. The gap between those two outcomes is exactly the verification and preservation discipline this guide has laid out.
