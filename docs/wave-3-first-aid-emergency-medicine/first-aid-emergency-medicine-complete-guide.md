## 1. Patient Assessment System

The patient assessment system (PAS) is a structured method for evaluating any injured or ill person. Using it consistently prevents you from missing life-threatening problems because a dramatic but non-fatal injury captured your attention. NOLS Wilderness Medicine teaches ABCDE as the backbone of this system.

### 1.1 Scene Safety

Before touching a patient, pause for three to five seconds and ask: Is it safe to approach? Hazards include traffic, unstable terrain, downed power lines, fire, toxic fumes, an agitated bystander, or an active shooter. A rescuer who becomes a casualty helps no one. If the scene is not safe, do not enter until it is controlled.

Once safe, put on gloves if available. Any bodily fluid is a potential pathogen source. Nitrile gloves cost pennies and belong in every first aid kit.

### 1.2 Initial Assessment — ABCDE

Work through these in order. Address each life threat before moving to the next letter.

**A — Airway**

Is the airway open? In an unconscious patient, the tongue commonly falls back and blocks the throat. Use the head-tilt chin-lift maneuver: place one hand on the forehead, two fingers under the chin, tilt the head back and lift the chin forward. For suspected spinal injury, use the jaw-thrust maneuver instead: position yourself behind the patient's head, place hands on either side of the jaw, and thrust the jaw forward without moving the neck.

Look inside the mouth. Clear visible debris with a finger sweep. Do not perform blind finger sweeps on children — you may push an obstruction deeper.

**B — Breathing**

With the airway open, look, listen, and feel for breathing for no more than ten seconds. Is the chest rising? Can you hear breath sounds? Can you feel exhaled air on your cheek? If the patient is not breathing and has no pulse, begin CPR immediately (see Section 3.2).

Note the rate (normal adult: 12–20 breaths per minute) and quality. Rapid shallow breathing suggests shock, pain, or respiratory distress. Slow breathing below 8 per minute may indicate opioid overdose or severe head injury.

**C — Circulation**

Check for major bleeding. Run your hands over the patient's entire body — scalp, neck, back, groin, axilla — looking for blood. Any life-threatening external hemorrhage must be controlled immediately (see Section 2). Check pulse: carotid in the neck (most accessible in adults) or radial at the wrist. A rapid weak pulse, or one you cannot find, suggests shock.

**D — Disability (Neurological Status)**

Use the AVPU scale:
- **A** — Alert and oriented (knows who they are, where they are, what day it is)
- **V** — Responsive to Voice but not fully oriented
- **P** — Responsive to Pain only (sternal rub, pinch)
- **U** — Unresponsive

Any level below A warrants urgent evacuation.

Check pupils: both should be equal and react to light by constricting. Unequal pupils, or pupils that do not react, suggest serious brain injury.

**E — Expose**

Cut away or remove clothing over suspected injuries. You cannot assess what you cannot see. In cold environments, expose briefly, assess, then re-cover.

### 1.3 Secondary Survey

Once life threats are controlled, conduct a head-to-toe physical examination. Run your hands firmly over every body part, noting pain, deformity, swelling, or instability. Include:

- Head and scalp: lacerations, step-offs, raccoon eyes (bruising around both orbits suggests skull base fracture)
- Neck: midline tenderness, tracheal deviation
- Chest: instability, paradoxical movement (one section moving opposite to rest of chest = flail chest), breath sounds equal on both sides
- Abdomen: rigidity, guarding, distension
- Pelvis: compress gently inward and downward — pain or instability means possible pelvic fracture (do this test once only)
- Extremities: sensation, motor function, circulation (pulses, capillary refill below 2 seconds, skin temperature)
- Spine: palpate each vertebra for midline tenderness

### 1.4 SAMPLE History

Gather this information from the patient or bystanders:

- **S** — Signs and symptoms: What is the patient experiencing?
- **A** — Allergies: Medications, foods, environmental
- **M** — Medications: Current prescriptions, OTC drugs, supplements
- **P** — Pertinent past medical history: Diabetes, heart disease, seizure disorder
- **L** — Last oral intake: When did they last eat or drink?
- **E** — Events leading up to the emergency: What happened?

### 1.5 Vital Signs

Record and track these at regular intervals (every 15 minutes for unstable patients, every 30 minutes for stable):

| Vital Sign | Normal Adult Range | Concern |
|---|---|---|
| Pulse | 60–100 bpm | >120 or <50 suggest emergency |
| Respiratory rate | 12–20 per minute | <10 or >30 is critical |
| Skin color/temp | Pink, warm, dry | Pale, cool, clammy = shock |
| Level of consciousness | Alert and oriented × 4 | Any drop is significant |
| Capillary refill | <2 seconds | >2 seconds suggests poor perfusion |

**Worked example**: A 45-year-old farmer was kicked by a horse. Scene safe (horse secured). ABCDE: airway open, breathing 24 per minute and shallow, skin pale and clammy, patient answers questions but confused (A→V on AVPU). You suspect internal bleeding causing shock. Control visible scalp laceration with direct pressure, lay patient flat with legs elevated 6–12 inches if no spinal concern, keep warm, and activate EMS immediately.

## 2. Hemorrhage Control

Uncontrolled external bleeding is one of the leading causes of preventable death in trauma. The Hartford Consensus and Stop the Bleed campaign (American College of Surgeons) have established that bystanders with minimal training can stop life-threatening bleeding.

### 2.1 Direct Pressure

The first and most effective intervention for most bleeding wounds.

**Technique**:
1. Put on gloves if available.
2. Place a clean cloth, gauze pad, or clothing directly over the wound.
3. Apply firm, uninterrupted pressure using the heel of your hand.
4. Do not lift the dressing to check — if blood soaks through, add more material on top and continue pressing.
5. Maintain pressure for a minimum of 10 minutes without interruption. Most bleeding stops within this window.

**Common mistake**: Releasing pressure too early to check. Each lift restarts the clotting process.

### 2.2 Wound Packing

For wounds with a cavity — gunshot wounds, deep punctures, axillary or groin wounds where a tourniquet cannot be placed — direct surface pressure is ineffective. Pack the wound directly.

**Technique**:
1. Use gauze, preferably hemostatic gauze (QuikClot, Celox, or HemCon) if available. Regular gauze works.
2. Use a finger to press gauze into the deepest part of the wound.
3. Continue packing gauze on top, filling the cavity completely.
4. Apply firm, sustained direct pressure over the packed wound for 3 full minutes.
5. Hold in place with a pressure bandage.

**Hemostatic gauze** contains kaolin (QuikClot) or chitosan (HemCon) that accelerates clotting. A 2025 systematic review (PMC11766969) confirmed tourniquet and hemostatic wound packing superiority over simple pressure dressings for major limb and junctional hemorrhage.

### 2.3 Tourniquet Application

Use a tourniquet for bleeding from a limb that cannot be controlled by direct pressure, or when direct pressure is not feasible (mass casualty, single rescuer managing multiple patients). TCCC 2024 guidelines recommend applying a tourniquet high and tight on any life-threatening limb bleed — do not attempt direct pressure first in arterial bleeding.

**Commercial tourniquet (CAT, SOFTT-W) technique**:
1. Apply 2–3 inches above the wound, or as high on the limb as possible ("high and tight") to avoid the need for replacement.
2. Route the strap through the buckle and pull firmly.
3. Twist the windlass rod until bleeding stops completely.
4. Lock the windlass in the clip.
5. Mark the time of application in permanent marker on the tourniquet or the patient's forehead.

**Improvised tourniquet**: A belt, torn clothing strip, or paracord at minimum 1.5 inches wide, folded for additional width, applied 2–3 inches above the wound, tied with an overhand knot, a stick placed under the knot, twisted until bleeding stops, stick secured with additional tie. This works but is less reliable than commercial devices — use a commercial tourniquet whenever possible.

**Tourniquet time**: Previously taught as a 2-hour absolute limit. Current TCCC 2026 guidance notes tourniquet conversion (replacement with hemostatic pressure dressing) should be attempted after the first 2 hours if the casualty is stable, bleeding is controlled, and the provider is trained in conversion technique. Tourniquets have been left in place up to 6 hours with limb survival when professionally managed. Do not remove or loosen a tourniquet without training.

**Contraindications**: Do not apply a tourniquet to the neck or torso.

### 2.4 Pressure Bandage

Once bleeding is controlled with direct pressure or wound packing, secure the dressing with a pressure bandage:
- Israeli bandage: commercial pressure bandage with wrap and pressure bar
- Improvised: wrap gauze around the limb while maintaining pressure, secure tightly with tape or a cloth knot

## 3. Airway and Breathing Emergencies

### 3.1 Choking

**Adult and Child (over 1 year)**

Mild choking (can cough, speak, or cry): Encourage forceful coughing. Do not intervene — coughing is the most effective clearance mechanism.

Severe choking (cannot cough effectively, cannot speak, blue lips, silent):
1. Tell the person you are going to help them.
2. Stand behind them, place one foot between their feet for stability.
3. Give 5 firm back blows between the shoulder blades with the heel of your hand.
4. Follow with 5 abdominal thrusts (Heimlich): hands just above the navel, locked fist, sharp upward thrusts.
5. Alternate 5 back blows + 5 abdominal thrusts until the object is expelled or the person becomes unconscious.

If unconscious: Lower to floor, call 911, begin CPR. Each time you open the airway to give breaths, look in the mouth — remove any visible obstruction before giving breaths.

**Infant (under 1 year)**

The AHA/Red Cross 2024 guidelines eliminated abdominal thrusts for infants due to internal organ injury risk.
1. Hold infant face-down along your forearm, head lower than chest.
2. Deliver 5 firm back blows between the shoulder blades.
3. Flip infant face-up, support head, deliver 5 chest thrusts using two fingers on the lower half of the breastbone.
4. Alternate until object is expelled or infant becomes unresponsive.

### 3.2 Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation (CPR) — 2024 AHA/Red Cross Guidelines

Begin CPR if the victim is unresponsive AND not breathing normally (no breathing, or only gasping).

**Adult CPR — Compression-Only (untrained or single rescuer)**
1. Call 911 or direct someone else to call while you begin.
2. Place the heel of one hand on the center of the chest (lower half of breastbone). Place the other hand on top, interlock fingers.
3. Arms straight, compress at least 2 inches (5 cm) deep.
4. Rate: 100–120 compressions per minute (to the beat of "Stayin' Alive" by the Bee Gees).
5. Allow full chest recoil between compressions — do not lean on the chest.
6. Continue until AED arrives, EMS takes over, or you cannot continue.

**Adult CPR — With Rescue Breaths (trained rescuer)**
- 30 compressions followed by 2 rescue breaths.
- Head-tilt chin-lift to open airway, pinch nose, create complete seal, give breath over 1 second. Watch for chest rise.
- If first breath does not go in, reposition head and try again. Then return to compressions.
- With an AED: apply as soon as available, follow AED prompts, minimize interruptions to compressions.

**Child CPR (1 year to puberty)**
- Same rate (100–120/min), same depth guideline (at least 2 inches), same 30:2 ratio.
- One hand may be sufficient for small children. Technique varies with child size.

**Infant CPR (under 1 year)**
- Two-thumb encircling hands technique: wrap both hands around the infant's chest, thumbs side by side on the lower third of the breastbone, fingers supporting the back.
- Single rescuer: two-finger technique (index and middle fingers on lower half of breastbone).
- Depth: ~1.5 inches (4 cm), or one-third of the chest diameter.
- Rate: 100–120 per minute.
- 30:2 ratio for single rescuer; 15:2 for two trained rescuers.

**AED Use**
Automated external defibrillators are safe and effective when used by untrained bystanders. Turn on, attach pads as diagrammed, allow the AED to analyze, stand clear and deliver shock if advised. Resume CPR immediately after shock delivery; do not wait for the AED's next analysis prompt.

**When to stop CPR**
Continue until: the victim shows obvious signs of life, a trained rescuer takes over, you are physically unable to continue, or a physician pronounces death. In extended remote scenarios without definitive care, CPR longer than 30 minutes for cardiac arrest without a shockable rhythm is rarely successful.

### 3.3 Opioid Overdose

The 2024 AHA/Red Cross guidelines added opioid overdose response as a core first aid competency.

Recognition: Unresponsive, very slow or stopped breathing (fewer than 8 breaths per minute), pinpoint pupils, blue or gray lips.

**Response sequence**:
1. Call 911.
2. If available, administer naloxone (Narcan): nasal spray — insert tip into one nostril, press plunger firmly. Repeat in opposite nostril with second dose after 2 minutes if no response. Naloxone reverses opioid overdose within 2–5 minutes but wears off in 30–90 minutes — the patient may re-overdose when it wears off.
3. If not breathing: provide rescue breaths (1 breath every 5 seconds).
4. If no pulse: begin CPR.
5. Place in recovery position once breathing resumes.

Naloxone is available over the counter at most pharmacies in the United States without a prescription. It belongs in every community first aid kit.

## 4. Wound Care and Closure

### 4.1 Wound Assessment

Assess every wound for:
- **Bleeding**: controlled or ongoing?
- **Depth**: superficial (skin only) vs. deep (entering fat, muscle, tendon, joint, or bone)
- **Contamination**: clean vs. dirty (soil, feces, organic matter, animal bites)
- **Location**: hands and face need careful closure (cosmetic and functional importance); joints must not be closed until tendon injury is excluded
- **Time since injury**: primary closure is appropriate within 6–8 hours for most body sites, 12 hours on the face; older wounds should be left open or closed loosely

**Wounds that must be seen by a physician** (evacuate the patient):
- Wounds entering joints
- Animal or human bites (high infection risk)
- Wounds with exposed tendon, bone, or nerve
- Wounds with retained foreign bodies you cannot remove
- Large wounds (>3 cm) requiring sutures for closure
- Signs of infection (discussed below)
- Any wound in an immunocompromised patient

### 4.2 Wound Irrigation

Irrigation is the single most effective intervention for reducing wound infection. The goal is to mechanically flush bacteria, debris, and organic material out of the wound.

**Technique**:
1. Fill a 20–60 mL syringe with clean water. Potable water is adequate; sterile saline is ideal but not essential.
2. Attach an 18-gauge catheter, needle tip, or improvised nozzle (nail through a plastic bag cap).
3. Hold the tip 1–2 cm from the wound.
4. Flush with force (at least 8 psi pressure) — you should see the stream distorting wound tissue.
5. Volume: 200–1,000 mL minimum. When in doubt, irrigate more.
6. Tap water has been shown equivalent to sterile saline for wound irrigation in multiple randomized controlled trials (Wound Repair and Regeneration, 2013).

**Do not**: close a wound you have not irrigated. Closure locks contamination inside and dramatically increases infection risk.

Antiseptic solutions (hydrogen peroxide, povidone-iodine full-strength, Dakin's solution) damage healthy tissue and impair healing. If used for decontamination, dilute povidone-iodine to 0.5–1% (1 mL in 100 mL water) and flush briefly; do not leave in the wound.

### 4.3 Wound Closure Techniques

**Steri-Strips and butterfly closures**: Appropriate for clean, low-tension wounds under 2.5 cm (1 inch), in areas not subject to significant movement.
1. Dry the wound edges with gauze.
2. Apply skin closure strips perpendicular to the wound, starting at the center.
3. Pull edges together gently before pressing each strip.
4. Do not apply strips too tightly — moderate swelling will occur.
5. Leave in place 5–10 days, trimming loose ends if they begin to peel.

**Improvised butterfly closure**: Cut a strip of medical tape into a bow-tie shape (narrow at center, wider at the ends). Apply the same way as Steri-Strips.

**Tissue adhesive (Super Glue/surgical glue)**: Acceptable for small scalp lacerations and clean, tension-free wounds. Apply to wound edges held together, not inside the wound. Do not use in infected wounds, on mucous membranes, or under constant motion.

**Sutures**: Beyond scope for most lay responders. If available and the provider is trained: simple interrupted sutures in the skin, 3-0 or 4-0 non-absorbable monofilament (nylon or polypropylene). Remove facial sutures at 5 days, extremity sutures at 10–14 days.

### 4.4 Wound Dressing

After closure or for wounds left open:
- Apply a non-adherent gauze pad (Telfa, petrolatum gauze) directly over the wound.
- Cover with dry gauze and secure with tape or bandage wrap.
- Change dressing daily or when soiled/saturated.
- Keep the wound moist but not wet — dry wounds heal more slowly.

### 4.5 Infection Recognition and Treatment

Wound infection typically appears 24–72 hours after injury. Signs:
- Increasing rather than decreasing pain after the first 24 hours
- Redness spreading from the wound edges
- Warmth
- Swelling
- Purulent discharge (cloudy or yellow/green fluid)
- Red streaks tracking from the wound toward the body (lymphangitis — serious, requires immediate evacuation)
- Fever above 38°C (100.4°F)
- Swollen lymph nodes near the wound

**Field management**:
- Open the wound if it was closed — do not leave pus trapped inside.
- Irrigate aggressively.
- Keep moist with clean dressings.
- Administer oral antibiotics if available and the patient has no contraindications (amoxicillin-clavulanate, doxycycline, or cephalexin are commonly carried).

**Evacuation immediately if**: red streaks, fever with systemic illness, or no improvement after 24 hours of treatment.

## 5. Fractures, Dislocations, and Sprains

### 5.1 Assessment

Assess suspected fractures for:
- **Pain** at the injury site, especially point tenderness directly over bone
- **Deformity**: abnormal angulation, shortening, or rotation
- **Swelling** and ecchymosis (bruising)
- **Loss of function**: inability to bear weight or use the injured part
- **Neurovascular status**: pulse, sensation, and movement below the injury

The presence of circulation, sensation, and motor function (CSM) below a fracture determines urgency of reduction (realignment) or evacuation. Check CSM before and after any splinting or reduction.

**Open (compound) fracture**: bone is protruding through the skin, or the wound is clearly in communication with the fracture. Treat as a wound emergency:
1. Control bleeding with gentle direct pressure around (not on) the bone.
2. Do not push bone back in.
3. Cover with a moist, clean dressing.
4. Splint in position found.
5. Evacuate urgently — open fractures require surgical debridement within hours to prevent serious bone infection (osteomyelitis).

### 5.2 Principles of Splinting

The goal of splinting is to immobilize the injury and prevent further soft tissue damage from bone movement. A well-applied splint also reduces pain dramatically.

**Rules**:
1. Immobilize the joint above and below the fracture.
2. Pad bony prominences to prevent pressure sores.
3. Splint in the position of comfort, or the position found, unless circulation is compromised.
4. Check CSM before and after splinting.

**Improvised splinting materials**:
- Hiking poles (excellent for leg and forearm splints)
- Straight sticks or branches
- Rolled sleeping pad
- Rolled magazine or newspaper
- Tent poles
- Cardboard

**Simple arm splint technique**:
1. Pad any hard material with extra clothing.
2. Place the padding/splint along the injured limb, extending from above the elbow to below the wrist for a forearm fracture.
3. Secure with strips of cloth, bandana, or bandage wrap — three ties minimum (above injury, at injury, below injury).
4. Apply a sling to support the arm close to the body.
5. Check CSM after every adjustment.

**Simple leg splint technique**:
1. Pad a hiking pole or two sticks heavily.
2. Place one on each side of the injured leg, extending past the ankle.
3. Tie at multiple points above, at, and below the fracture.
4. Do not bear weight on a splinted fracture.

### 5.3 Specific Fractures

**Clavicle (collarbone)**: Most common shoulder-girdle fracture. Treat with a sling and swathe (wrap the upper arm to the chest to prevent arm movement). Patient can often walk out with this.

**Rib fractures**: Suspected by pain that worsens with deep breath, cough, or palpation of the rib. Apply a broad wrap (not tight — breathing must be maintained). Watch for increasing respiratory distress, which may indicate pneumothorax (collapsed lung). Signs of tension pneumothorax (tracheal deviation, absent breath sounds one side, shock) require emergency needle decompression if trained.

**Ankle fractures**: Apply a SAM splint or improvised rigid splint in a "stirrup" configuration (U-shaped around the heel, stabilizing both sides). Keep non-weight-bearing.

**Suspected femur (thigh) fracture**: A high-energy injury with significant blood loss potential (1–2 liters into the thigh). Apply a traction splint if available and trained. If not, apply best improvised long-leg splint and evacuate with urgency.

### 5.4 Spinal Assessment

The Wilderness Medical Society updated spinal cord protection guidelines in 2024. Key principles:

**When to suspect spinal injury**: Significant mechanism (fall from height >3×patient height, high-speed MVA, axial load, direct blow to spine), spine pain, neurological symptoms (weakness, numbness, tingling in extremities).

**Focused Spinal Assessment**: The patient can be cleared for walking (not requiring spinal motion restriction) if ALL of the following are true:
- Awake and alert, not intoxicated, no distracting painful injury
- No tenderness to firm palpation along the entire spine
- No pain, numbness, tingling, or weakness in extremities
- No incontinence

If any criterion fails, restrict spinal motion: minimize movement, manually stabilize the head and neck in a neutral position, and plan the evacuation accordingly. Modern guidelines no longer recommend rigid backboards and hard cervical collars for wilderness settings — they cause pain and impair breathing without improving outcomes. Manual stabilization and careful movement is preferred.

**Neurological deficit present**: If the patient has weakness, numbness, or loss of bladder/bowel control, this is a spinal cord injury emergency requiring the most careful possible evacuation and immediate definitive care.

### 5.5 Sprains

Sprains are ligament injuries. Severity:
- Grade 1: Mild stretch, minimal swelling, able to bear weight
- Grade 2: Partial tear, significant swelling and bruising, weight-bearing painful
- Grade 3: Complete ligament rupture, instability

**RICE protocol**:
- **R** — Rest: Avoid activities that cause pain
- **I** — Ice: 20 minutes every 2–3 hours for first 48 hours. Never apply ice directly to skin — wrap in cloth.
- **C** — Compression: Elastic bandage from toes to above the ankle. Not so tight it impairs circulation.
- **E** — Elevation: Keep the injured limb above heart level when possible.

A Grade 2 or 3 ankle sprain may be more comfortable with a figure-8 taping. For wilderness travel, a pair of trekking poles and taping can enable careful walking out.

## 6. Burns

### 6.1 Classification

| Degree | Depth | Appearance | Pain | Prognosis |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Superficial (1st) | Epidermis only | Red, dry, no blisters | Painful | Heals in 3–5 days |
| Superficial partial-thickness (2nd) | Epidermis + superficial dermis | Red, moist, blistered | Very painful | Heals in 14–21 days |
| Deep partial-thickness (2nd deep) | Epidermis + deep dermis | Pale/red mottled, blistered | Less painful (nerve damage) | Requires >21 days, may need grafting |
| Full-thickness (3rd) | All skin layers | White/brown/black, leathery, dry | Painless (nerves destroyed) | Does not heal without grafting |
| Fourth degree | Down to bone/tendon | Charred | Painless | Amputations common |

### 6.2 Severity Assessment

**Rule of Nines** estimates total body surface area (TBSA) affected:
- Head: 9%
- Each arm: 9%
- Chest: 9%
- Abdomen: 9%
- Upper back: 9%
- Lower back: 9%
- Each thigh: 9%
- Each lower leg: 9%
- Genitalia: 1%

Children have proportionally larger heads (18%) and smaller legs; use the Lund-Browder chart for pediatric burns.

**Palm method** for irregular burns: the patient's palm (fingers together) represents approximately 1% TBSA. Useful for burns under 15%.

**Evacuation criteria** (American Burn Association referral guidelines):
- Any full-thickness burn
- Partial-thickness burn >10% TBSA in any age group
- Burns involving face, hands, feet, genitalia, major joints, or circumferential (wrapping around) burns of any area
- Chemical or electrical burns
- Burns with inhalation injury (hoarse voice, singed nasal hair, carbonaceous sputum)
- Burns in patients with significant comorbidities

### 6.3 Field Treatment

**Immediately**:
1. Stop the burning: remove from heat source, extinguish flame by rolling the patient (stop, drop, roll), remove burning clothing unless fused to skin, remove jewelry.
2. Cool the burn: flush with cool (not ice-cold) running water for 20 minutes. Cooling is effective up to 3 hours after the injury. Do not use ice, butter, toothpaste, or any grease-based product — these worsen outcomes.
3. Assess airway: singed eyebrows, nasal hair, or hoarse voice suggest inhalation injury — evacuate immediately; airway swelling can close the airway within hours.

**After cooling**:
- Do not pop blisters (intact blisters protect the wound from infection).
- Apply a clean, non-adherent dressing. Cling film (plastic wrap) is ideal: essentially sterile from the roll, non-adherent, transparent. Avoid anything that will stick to the wound.
- Give pain relief: ibuprofen or acetaminophen if available.

**Chemical burns**:
- Brush off dry chemical before adding water.
- Flush with large amounts of water for at minimum 30 minutes.
- Do not attempt to neutralize the chemical — the neutralization reaction generates heat.
- Remove contaminated clothing and jewelry.

**Electrical burns**:
- Do not touch the patient while in contact with an electrical source. Shut off power first.
- Electrical burns have a small entry and exit wound but extensive internal damage along the current path.
- Cardiac dysrhythmia can occur — monitor for abnormal pulse.
- All electrical burns require evacuation and cardiac monitoring.

### 6.4 Burn Fluid Replacement

Burns >15% TBSA cause significant fluid loss (plasma leaks from damaged vessels). In the field without IV access:
- Push oral fluids aggressively: 1.5 liters per hour is the target for the first hour.
- Oral rehydration salts (ORS) are preferable to plain water.
- Watch for signs of shock (pallor, rapid weak pulse, confusion) indicating inadequate fluid replacement.

## 7. Environmental Emergencies

### 7.1 Hypothermia

Core temperature drops when the body cannot maintain heat production or loses heat faster than it generates it. Wet clothing, wind, and physical exhaustion accelerate cooling dramatically.

**Stages**:

| Stage | Core Temp | Symptoms |
|---|---|---|
| Mild | 32–35°C (90–95°F) | Shivering, clumsiness, pale skin, slurred speech |
| Moderate | 28–32°C (82–90°F) | Shivering may stop, confusion, muscle stiffness |
| Severe | <28°C (<82°F) | Unconscious, no shivering, rigid muscles, pulse may be absent |

**Treatment**:
1. Prevent further heat loss: remove wet clothing, get out of wind and weather.
2. Insulate: sleeping bag, emergency blanket (space blanket), extra clothing.
3. Insulate from ground: foam sleeping pad, backpacks, even leaves — cold ground draws heat out faster than cold air.
4. Add heat: body-to-body contact in a sleeping bag, hot water bottles in armpits and groin (not directly on skin).
5. Provide warm (not hot) sweetened drinks to conscious patients.
6. Handle gently: rough movement of a severely hypothermic patient can trigger ventricular fibrillation. Do not allow the patient to walk or exert themselves.

**Critical**: A severely hypothermic patient may appear dead. Check for pulse for a full minute before concluding absence. "Not dead until warm and dead." Hospital rewarming with IV fluids and active core warming is the definitive treatment; maintain CPR if no pulse and evacuation is possible.

### 7.2 Frostbite

Freezing of tissue. Affects extremities, ears, nose.

**Frostnip** (not true frostbite): Skin appears white or red, numb, but soft when pressed. Rewarm by placing against warm skin (axilla, groin). No tissue loss.

**Superficial frostbite**: Skin is white/yellow, firm to touch but soft underneath. Blisters may form after rewarming.

**Deep frostbite**: Skin is white/yellow-gray, hard throughout, wooden feel. Can extend into muscle and bone.

**Field rewarming**:
- Rewarm in the field only if: refreezing cannot occur AND the patient can be evacuated without walking on the frostbitten foot.
- Warm water immersion: 37–39°C (98–102°F) — hot enough to feel warm, not hot enough to burn. No hotter. Immerse for 20–30 minutes until the tissue becomes red and pliable.
- After rewarming: do not rub, drain clear blisters, leave blood-filled blisters intact, wrap loosely in dry gauze, keep elevated.
- Walking on frozen feet before rewarming is less damaging than walking on thawed feet.

### 7.3 Heat Illness

**Heat cramps**: Painful muscle cramps in heavily used muscles (legs, abdomen). Treatment: rest, oral fluids with electrolytes, gentle stretching. Not dangerous but a warning sign.

**Heat exhaustion**: The body is struggling to maintain core temperature. Symptoms: heavy sweating, weakness, cool pale clammy skin, fast weak pulse, nausea, possible fainting. Core temperature normal or mildly elevated (<40°C/104°F), normal or near-normal mental status.

Treatment:
1. Move to a cool environment immediately.
2. Remove excess clothing.
3. Apply wet cloths and fan the patient.
4. Oral rehydration: water and electrolytes.
5. Lay patient on back, elevate feet slightly if no nausea.
6. Most patients recover in 30–60 minutes.

**Heat stroke**: A life-threatening emergency. Core temperature >40°C (104°F) AND altered mental status (confusion, agitation, seizure, unconsciousness). Two types:
- Classic heat stroke: in elderly, medically compromised patients; skin may be hot and DRY
- Exertional heat stroke: in active individuals; skin often still wet from sweating

**Treatment — aggressive cooling is the priority**:
1. Call 911.
2. Remove clothing.
3. Immerse in the coldest water available — ice bath if possible. Evidence from the Wilderness Medical Society 2024 guidelines confirms ice-water immersion is the most effective field treatment. Pour ice over the patient, fan with wet cloth.
4. Target cooling to 38.3–38.8°C (101–102°F) — then stop active cooling to avoid overshoot to hypothermia.
5. Do NOT give antipyretics (ibuprofen, acetaminophen) — these do not work for heat stroke as the thermoregulatory setpoint is not elevated.
6. Do NOT give oral fluids to an altered patient.
7. Continue cooling during evacuation.

### 7.4 Drowning

A drowning person often appears surprisingly calm — they may be vertical in the water, unable to call out (the instinctive drowning response eliminates voluntary arm movement and the ability to call for help). Rescue must prioritize rescuer safety: throw a rope or flotation device, extend an object, then go.

**After rescue**:
1. Begin CPR immediately if not breathing and no pulse. Drowning is primarily a breathing emergency — CPR is effective and should not be withheld on the assumption that saltwater or freshwater aspiration makes it futile.
2. Do not perform Heimlich or abdominal thrusts to expel water — this is not effective and delays CPR.
3. Keep the patient warm — even in summer, cold water causes hypothermia.
4. All drowning survivors require medical evaluation even if they appear recovered — secondary drowning (pulmonary edema) can occur up to 24 hours later.

## 8. Medical Emergencies

### 8.1 Shock

Shock is inadequate tissue perfusion — cells are not receiving sufficient oxygen and nutrients. It is a pathway to organ failure and death if untreated.

**Types**:
- **Hypovolemic**: most common in trauma — blood or fluid loss. Treatment: stop bleeding, oral/IV fluids, keep warm, elevate legs.
- **Distributive/Septic**: widespread infection or severe allergic reaction causes blood vessels to dilate. Warm shock (early): flushed skin, bounding pulse. Cold shock (late): pale, clammy, rapid weak pulse.
- **Obstructive**: physical obstruction of blood flow (tension pneumothorax, cardiac tamponade). Requires specific interventions beyond lay scope.
- **Cardiogenic**: heart failure. Cannot pump effectively. Keep patient seated, not flat.

**Signs of shock** (compensated):
- Rapid heart rate (>100 bpm)
- Pale, cool, clammy skin
- Rapid respiratory rate
- Delayed capillary refill (>2 seconds)
- Restlessness or anxiety

**Late shock signs** (decompensated — imminent death):
- Mental status decline
- Blood pressure dropping
- Weak or absent pulse
- Cyanosis (blue lips, fingertips)

**General shock treatment**:
1. Address the cause (hemorrhage control, anaphylaxis treatment).
2. Lay patient flat, elevate legs 6–12 inches (not with abdominal or chest trauma, head injury, or difficulty breathing).
3. Keep warm — hypothermia worsens coagulopathy.
4. Oral fluids if conscious and no abdominal injury: water, electrolyte solution.
5. Rapid evacuation — field treatment buys time; definitive care requires IV access and often surgery.

### 8.2 Anaphylaxis

A life-threatening systemic allergic reaction. Common triggers: foods (nuts, shellfish), medications (penicillin, NSAIDs), bee/wasp stings, latex.

**Recognition**: The diagnosis is clinical — no test is needed or possible in the field. Anaphylaxis is likely when, within minutes to hours of a trigger, there is skin or mucosal involvement (hives, flushing, swollen lips/tongue) PLUS at least one of:
- Respiratory involvement (wheezing, shortness of breath, stridor, hoarse voice)
- Cardiovascular involvement (low blood pressure, rapid pulse, fainting)
- Gastrointestinal involvement (nausea, vomiting, cramping)

**Critical**: 10–20% of fatal anaphylaxis reactions have no skin findings. If vital signs are deteriorating after a clear allergen exposure, treat for anaphylaxis.

**Treatment**:
1. **Epinephrine first** — do not delay for antihistamines. EpiPen (0.3 mg, adults), EpiPen Jr (0.15 mg, children <25 kg): remove blue safety cap, press orange tip firmly against outer thigh (through clothing if needed), hold 10 seconds, remove. Second dose in 5–15 minutes if no improvement. Epinephrine degraded by heat and light; store appropriately.
2. Call 911. Epinephrine wears off in 30–90 minutes; 20% of reactions are biphasic (return after apparent resolution). Hospital observation is mandatory.
3. Position: sitting upright if breathing difficulty, lying flat with legs raised if blood pressure is low. Do not allow the patient to stand suddenly — this can cause cardiac arrest.
4. Antihistamines (diphenhydramine): useful for itch, hives, but do not treat the systemic reaction. Never substitute for epinephrine.
5. If epinephrine is unavailable, position for comfort, call 911, and prepare to perform CPR.

### 8.3 Stroke — FAST Assessment

A stroke occurs when blood supply to part of the brain is interrupted (ischemic) or a blood vessel ruptures (hemorrhagic). Time is brain — minutes matter.

**FAST** (American Stroke Association):
- **F** — Face drooping: Ask the person to smile. Is one side drooping?
- **A** — Arm weakness: Ask them to raise both arms. Does one drift downward?
- **S** — Speech difficulty: Ask them to repeat "The sky is blue." Is speech slurred or strange?
- **T** — Time to call 911 immediately if ANY of these are present. Note the exact time symptoms started — this determines treatment eligibility.

Additional signs: sudden severe headache ("thunderclap"), sudden vision loss in one eye, sudden dizziness or loss of balance.

Do not give anything by mouth. Keep the patient calm and still. Do not give aspirin unless specifically trained and directed — it can worsen a hemorrhagic stroke.

### 8.4 Chest Pain and Heart Attack

**Recognition**: Classic symptoms — chest pressure, tightness, squeezing (often described as an elephant on the chest), pain radiating to left arm, jaw, or back. Nausea, sweating, shortness of breath. Women and diabetics more often present with atypical symptoms: fatigue, jaw pain, nausea, shortness of breath without chest pain.

**Treatment**:
1. Call 911.
2. Help the patient into a comfortable position (usually seated, slightly reclined).
3. Aspirin: if not allergic, have the patient chew (not swallow whole) 325 mg regular or 4 × 81 mg low-dose aspirin. Aspirin's anti-platelet effect can limit clot extension. Do not give if patient is already on blood thinners, has active bleeding, or is allergic.
4. Loosen constrictive clothing.
5. If the patient becomes unresponsive and stops breathing: begin CPR and apply AED.

### 8.5 Seizures

**Active seizure**:
1. Protect from injury: clear hard/sharp objects, cushion the head with clothing.
2. Do NOT restrain limb movements — this can cause fractures.
3. Do NOT put anything in the mouth — this is not possible and causes injury.
4. Time the seizure.
5. After the seizure, place the patient in the recovery position (on their side, top leg bent to prevent rolling). They will be confused and exhausted.

**Call 911 if**:
- Seizure lasts more than 5 minutes (status epilepticus — a medical emergency)
- A second seizure follows without recovery
- The patient is injured, pregnant, or diabetic
- The patient does not regain consciousness within 10 minutes
- This is the patient's first known seizure

### 8.6 Diabetic Emergencies

**Hypoglycemia** (low blood sugar, <70 mg/dL or by symptoms):
- Onset: fast (minutes to hours)
- Symptoms: shakiness, sweating, pale skin, rapid heartbeat, anxiety, hunger, confusion, headache
- Conscious patient: 15–20 grams of fast-acting carbohydrate — 4 glucose tablets, 4 oz fruit juice, 3–4 teaspoons sugar, or a tablespoon of honey. Recheck symptoms in 15 minutes; repeat if not improving.
- Unconscious patient: Do not give anything by mouth. Rub glucose gel or honey on the inside of the cheek (absorbs through mucous membranes). Intranasal glucagon (Baqsimi) if available (no injection required). Call 911.

**Hyperglycemia** (high blood sugar): Develops slowly over hours to days.
- Symptoms: excessive thirst, frequent urination, nausea, abdominal pain, fruity breath (ketones), confusion.
- In the field: oral fluids, and evacuate — hyperglycemia requires insulin, which is not a field intervention.
- If unconscious: assume hypoglycemia (treat) until proven otherwise — glucose is safe even in high blood sugar; insulin given unnecessarily is dangerous.

### 8.7 Psychological First Aid

Extended emergencies cause acute stress reactions in up to 90% of survivors. Reactions include: shock and disbelief, fear, anxiety, grief, anger, guilt, physical symptoms (nausea, headache, fatigue, insomnia), intrusive thoughts, withdrawal.

**SAMHSA Psychological First Aid principles**:
1. **Safety**: Ensure physical and psychological safety. Reduce immediate threats.
2. **Calming**: Use a calm tone and steady presence. Slow, deliberate speech reduces panic.
3. **Efficacy**: Help people regain a sense of control. Give them tasks appropriate to their capacity.
4. **Connectedness**: Connect people with social support. Isolation worsens outcomes.
5. **Hope**: Acknowledge the difficulty while emphasizing realistic positive outcomes.

Active listening, normalizing reactions ("What you are feeling is a normal response to an abnormal situation"), practical problem-solving, and facilitating family reunification are the core activities.

**Panic attacks**: Rapid breathing, heart pounding, sweating, tunnel vision — terrifying but not dangerous. Breathing coaching (breathe in for 4 counts, hold for 4, out for 4) helps break the hyperventilation cycle. Grounding techniques (name 5 things you can see, 4 you can hear, 3 you can touch) interrupt dissociation.

Do not diagnose or attempt to treat PTSD in the field. Refer all individuals showing signs of ongoing impairment (unable to perform basic tasks, continued high distress after days) to mental health professionals as soon as accessible.

## 9. First Aid Kit Assembly

### 9.1 Essential Supplies

This list covers a family of 4 for a 72-hour emergency. Scale quantities as needed.

**Bleeding control**:
- 2 × commercial tourniquet (CAT or SOFTT-W)
- 4 × QuikClot or Celox hemostatic gauze (4-inch rolls)
- 10 × 4×4-inch sterile gauze pads
- 2 × conforming gauze rolls (Kerlix or equivalent)
- 2 × Israeli pressure bandages (6-inch)
- 1 roll medical tape (2-inch)
- 2 pairs nitrile gloves (multiple sizes)
- 1 × trauma shears

**Wound care**:
- 1 × 30 mL irrigation syringe with 18-gauge catheter
- Assorted adhesive bandages (5–10 each: small, medium, large, knuckle)
- 1 package Steri-Strips or wound closure strips
- 1 tube antibiotic ointment (bacitracin or triple antibiotic)
- 1 roll non-adherent dressing (Telfa)
- 1 roll cling film (plastic wrap — burns and wound cover)

**Splinting and support**:
- 2 × SAM splints (36-inch and 18-inch)
- 2 × elastic bandages (3-inch and 4-inch)
- 1 × triangular bandage (sling, tie)

**Airway and breathing**:
- 1 × pocket mask or face shield (CPR barrier device)
- 1 × nasopharyngeal airway (NPA) if trained in use
- 2 × naloxone nasal spray (Narcan 4 mg) — available OTC in US

**Environmental emergencies**:
- 2 × emergency (space) blankets
- 1 × digital thermometer (with rectal tip for hypothermia assessment)
- 1 chemical cold pack (instant, for strains/stings)

**Medications (discuss with your physician)**:
- Epinephrine auto-injector (EpiPen) if any family member has anaphylaxis history — prescription required
- Diphenhydramine (Benadryl) 25 mg tablets — allergic reactions, mild
- Ibuprofen 200 mg tablets
- Acetaminophen 500 mg tablets
- Aspirin 81 mg tablets (cardiac events, not for pain in children)
- Antidiarrheal (loperamide)
- Antacid (calcium carbonate)
- Oral rehydration salts (Liquid IV, Nuun tablets, or homemade formula: 6 level teaspoons sugar + 0.5 level teaspoon salt per 1 liter clean water)

**Tools and reference**:
- This guide and a laminated quick-reference card
- Permanent marker (tourniquet time documentation)
- Small flashlight or headlamp
- Safety pins

### 9.2 Storage and Maintenance

- Store in a waterproof container or bag.
- Keep in a consistent, accessible location — every family member should know where it is.
- Inspect every 6 months: check expiration dates, replace used items, assess for moisture damage.
- Store medications away from extreme heat and direct sunlight — a car glove compartment gets too hot in summer.
- Label the kit with its contents and last inspection date.

### 9.3 Improvised Supplies

When commercial supplies are unavailable:

| Commercial Item | Improvised Alternative |
|---|---|
| Tourniquet | Belt or clothing strip ≥ 1.5 inches wide + stick |
| Pressure dressing | Cloth pad + wrap and tie |
| SAM splint | Hiking poles, straight sticks, rolled sleeping pad, magazine |
| Steri-Strips | Medical tape cut to shape |
| Nitrile gloves | Plastic bags over hands |
| Wound irrigation syringe | Water bottle with small hole punched in cap |
| Antiseptic | Isopropyl alcohol ≥70% (dilute), povidone-iodine (dilute) |
| Thermometer | None suitable — assess by clinical signs |
| Emergency blanket | Wool blanket, sleeping bag, plastic sheeting |

## 10. Special Situations

### 10.1 Emergency Childbirth

Normal delivery in a non-hospital setting:
1. Support the mother's preferred position (semi-reclined, hands and knees, or squat).
2. Wash hands thoroughly. Use gloves.
3. Do not pull on the baby — support the head as it emerges, guiding gently.
4. Check for umbilical cord around the neck: if loose, slip it over the head. If tight, do not pull — carefully attempt to ease delivery while keeping cord slack.
5. After the head delivers, the body typically follows with the next contraction.
6. Dry the baby immediately and vigorously — this stimulation prompts breathing.
7. Keep baby skin-to-skin with the mother (warm and bonded).
8. The umbilical cord can be left uncut for 1–3 minutes (delayed cord clamping improves outcomes). Cut only when it stops pulsing, using sterile scissors or clean knife, tied in two places at least 6–8 cm apart.
9. The placenta delivers within 30 minutes. Do not pull on the cord. Gentle maternal pushing is sufficient.

**Call EMS**: Any delivery should be attended by medical personnel if possible. Complications requiring immediate evacuation: baby not breathing after stimulation (begin infant CPR), heavy maternal bleeding after delivery, umbilical cord prolapsing before the baby (cord comes first — immediate emergency).

### 10.2 Pediatric Differences

Children are not small adults. Key differences:

- **Airway**: proportionally larger and more compliant. Hyperextension closes the pediatric airway — use slight tilt only.
- **Breathing rate**: higher than adults (infants: 30–60; toddlers: 24–40; school-age: 18–30).
- **Heart rate**: higher than adults (infants: 100–160; toddlers: 90–150; school-age: 70–120).
- **Temperature regulation**: less efficient. Children become hypothermic and hyperthermic more quickly.
- **Medication dosing**: always weight-based. Know your child's weight in kilograms.
- **Head injury**: children are prone to occult (hidden) brain injuries after seemingly minor mechanisms. Any confusion, vomiting, or loss of consciousness after a head injury requires evaluation.

### 10.3 Building a Patient Evacuation Plan

Before an emergency, know:
- Your nearest emergency room and trauma center
- Distance and drive time from likely locations (home, work, common recreation areas)
- Helicopter landing zone nearest to backcountry locations
- How to contact search and rescue in your region (most US counties have a sheriff's office SAR team)
- Emergency communications where cell service is unavailable: satellite communicator (Garmin inReach, SPOT), PLB (Personal Locator Beacon, registered with NOAA)

**PLB registration**: Free at beaconregistration.noaa.gov. PLBs transmit on the international distress frequency (406 MHz) and are detectable by Cospas-Sarsat satellites globally. Activation initiates a rescue response within ~90 minutes anywhere in the world.
