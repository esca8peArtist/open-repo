# Natural Building Techniques: Cob, Earthbag, Strawbale, and Compressed Earth Block

## Introduction

Natural building encompasses construction methods that use locally available, minimally processed materials — earth, straw, and stone — to create structures with low embodied energy, low cost, and deep historical precedent across every inhabited continent. This domain covers four techniques well-documented by open-source practitioners and research institutions: cob, earthbag, strawbale, and compressed earth block (CEB). A fifth section covers repair and maintenance applicable across all earthen structures.

These techniques are not alternatives to professional construction for complex habitable buildings. They are appropriate technology for a specific category of structures: storage buildings, workshops, root cellars, animal shelters, garden structures, walls, outdoor kitchens, and other non-habitable accessory uses. In most US rural jurisdictions, structures under 200 square feet classified as non-habitable accessory structures are exempt from structural building permits, though work must still be constructed to code standards and trade permits (electrical, plumbing) still apply where those systems are present. International contexts vary. Always check with your local building department before beginning any structure you intend to use long-term.

Within that scope, the four techniques covered here offer compelling properties: cob walls achieve compressive strengths of 60–231 psi and function as thermal mass in climates with large day-night temperature swings; earthbag structures tested by the United Nations Development Programme and UNHCR have housed refugees in seismic zones; strawbale walls deliver R-33 per 23-inch wall, outperforming most conventional insulation assemblies; and CEB blocks pressed at 5–10% Portland cement stabilization exceed 1,200 psi compressive strength, comparable to low-grade concrete block.

Each sub-topic in this guide follows the same structure: materials selection and sourcing, step-by-step procedure, structural performance parameters, tool list, and a troubleshooting table. Cross-links to the Wave 0 water systems domains and Wave 1 composting content appear throughout, because earthen structures do not stand alone: they integrate with rainwater catchment, greywater management, and soil management systems documented elsewhere in this library.

**A note on reader assumptions.** This guide assumes no professional construction background. It does assume you are capable of learning careful, hands-on work, reading a level, mixing materials by volume, and following sequential procedures with attention to safety margins. Natural building is forgiving of imperfection in ways that steel-frame or concrete construction is not — but that forgiveness is not unlimited. The tolerances and tests in each section are not suggestions; they are the practical minimum for structures that perform safely over years and decades.

## Section 1: Cob Construction

### 1.1 Overview and Where It Fits

Cob is a hand-formed earthen building material made from a mixture of clay subsoil, coarse sand, and straw fiber. Walls are built up from the foundation in successive "lifts" (courses) of freshly mixed material, each course bonding chemically and mechanically to the last as the whole assembly dries. The result is a monolithic, sculpted wall with no joints and no formwork.

Cob is among the oldest building techniques in continuous use — the Devon region of England contains inhabited cob farmhouses over 500 years old. Per the Cob Research Institute (CRI), which has conducted peer-reviewed structural testing at the University of San Francisco in collaboration with Oregon State University, cob walls with well-designed mixes meet minimum compressive strength requirements of 60 psi for standard walls and 85 psi for braced wall panels. Individual mix tests have recorded strengths from 41 psi (long-straw mixes) to over 230 psi depending on clay content, sand gradation, and water content at time of pressing.

Cob is best suited to:
- Climates with hot, dry summers and cold, dry winters (thermal mass effect is most valuable with large temperature swings)
- Sites with accessible clay subsoil that reduces sourcing costs
- Structures requiring irregular shapes, arches, curved walls, or sculptural elements
- Builders who prefer hand-work and can invest extended build time

Cob is poorly suited to:
- Persistently wet climates without exceptional overhangs and base protection (rising damp destroys cob)
- Time-constrained projects (full wall drying takes 6–9 months for thick walls)
- Very cold climates that require high insulation R-values (cob's R-value is approximately 0.22–0.60 per inch, making a 12-inch wall R-2.6 to R-7.2 — below most modern energy codes)

### 1.2 Materials Selection and Sourcing

**Clay subsoil**

The target clay content in your raw soil is 15–25% by weight. Clay is not topsoil — it is the subsoil, typically found 6–18 inches below the surface layer. Topsoil contains organic matter that will decompose inside a wall and cause structural failure; it must never be used.

Field test for clay content before mixing: take a small sample and roll it between your palms into a thin snake. Clay-rich soil forms snakes without cracking; sandy soil crumbles. A more precise test: fill a quart jar one-third full of soil, add water to the top, cap it, shake vigorously, and let it settle overnight. Sand settles first (bottom layer), silt settles above it, and clay settles last in the top layer. The clay layer should be 15–25% of the total soil depth in the jar.

**Sourcing**: On most rural sites, clay subsoil is available from foundation excavation, pond digging, or road cuts. If importing, masonry supply yards sell dry clay powder (bentonite or kaolinite). Avoid expansive clays (smectite/montmorillonite) — they swell dramatically when wet and crack severely on drying.

**Coarse sand**

The target is sharp, angular sand with variable particle sizes ranging from fine through 1/4-inch gravel. Concrete sand (sold at building supply yards) is an excellent choice. Beach sand and river sand with rounded particles lack the interlocking surface texture needed for structural bond and should not be used.

A basic ratio: 2 parts coarse sand to 1 part clay by volume. This is a starting point, not a fixed formula — every clay source is different. You must run test batches and evaluate results (see Section 1.4) before mixing at scale.

**Straw**

Wheat, oat, and rye straw are all suitable. The straw provides tensile fiber — it holds the wall together across cracks that develop during shrinkage drying. Target strand length: 6–12 inches. Shorter strands provide less reinforcement; longer strands become tangled and difficult to mix evenly.

Critical: use straw, not hay. Hay contains seed heads and green plant material that decompose rapidly inside the wall, creating voids and organic acids. Check by smell (straw smells faintly of grain; hay smells grassy and may smell of fermentation) and look for seed absence. Straw must be dry and free of mold before use.

**Volume rule of thumb**: approximately 1 compressed five-gallon bucket of straw per 5 buckets of dry sand-clay mix.

**Water**

Mixing water should be clean and low in salts. In most rural settings, well water or rainwater is appropriate. Avoid water from salt-contaminated sources or streams with high dissolved mineral loads.

### 1.4 Step-by-Step: Mixing Cob — The Tarp Method

The tarp method requires no machinery and produces correctly mixed cob with 2–3 people working together. Per documented practitioner procedure at The Year of Mud and This Cob House (both CC-licensed resources):

**Step 1: Stage materials.**
Position your tarp on flat ground. Place sand and clay buckets within easy reach. Fluff straw with a pitchfork and have it accessible but not yet on the tarp.

**Step 2: Combine dry ingredients.**
Dump your sand onto the center of the tarp. Add clay on top. Starting ratio: 2 parts sand : 1 part clay by volume (e.g., 4 buckets sand + 2 buckets clay). Do not add water yet.

**Step 3: Dry-mix.**
Two people each take two corners of the tarp and walk toward each other, folding the pile in half. Walk it back out, folding the other direction. Repeat 8–10 times until sand and clay are evenly blended. You can see the blend is uniform when there are no distinct clay clumps.

**Step 4: Create a crater and add water.**
Form a depression in the center of the mix. Add water slowly — start with approximately 2–3 gallons per 6-bucket dry mix. Too much water at once creates an unworkable slurry; add conservatively.

**Step 5: Stomp the mix.**
Step into the mix and stomp with your body weight. Push the outer edges of the dry material into the wet center. Stomp until the mix holds together without crumbling and no longer has dry pockets. This takes 5–10 minutes.

**Step 6: Roll into a "burrito."**
Use the tarp to roll the wet mix into a log shape by walking toward the center. Unfold, stomp flat again. Repeat 3–4 times.

**Step 7: Add straw.**
Sprinkle straw evenly over the flattened mix. Stomp it in. Roll the burrito again. Unroll, add more straw, stomp again. Repeat until straw is distributed throughout and each handful of cob pulled apart shows fibers running through it.

**Step 8: Test the batch (see 1.5 below).**

**Step 9: Apply to the wall.**
Pick up cob in loaves (roughly 2-foot-long logs) and slap them against the wall with moderate force. This compresses and bonds the fresh cob to the course below. Work the loaves together by pressing with fingers and palms, eliminating any air pockets. Build in lifts of 8–10 inches maximum per course.

### 1.5 Testing Cob Mixes

Run all four tests on every new soil source and whenever you change your sand or clay proportions. Make a test sample the size of a grapefruit and a test tile (3 inches x 6 inches x 1 inch flat slab). Allow both to dry fully in the shade (not oven-dried) for 48–72 hours before evaluating.

**Test 1: The Shake Test (dry strength)**
Hold the dried grapefruit-sized sample in one hand and shake it vigorously while trying to break it with the other. A good mix holds together firmly. If it crumbles or breaks easily, the mix is too sandy — increase clay proportion by 10% and re-test.

**Test 2: The Squeeze Test (wet workability)**
Fresh cob should hold a sharp thumb impression without crumbling and without the thumbprint filling back in with slumping material. If it crumbles under thumb pressure, the mix is too dry or too sandy. If it slumps and fills in, the mix is too wet or too clay-rich — add sand.

**Test 3: The Drop Test (cohesion)**
Roll fresh cob into a ball approximately 2 inches in diameter. Drop it from shoulder height onto firm ground. A good mix flattens but stays together. A too-sandy mix shatters. A too-wet mix pancakes and spreads.

**Test 4: The Tile Shrinkage Test (clay calibration)**
Press a tile flat (3 inches x 6 inches x 1 inch) on a flat board. Mark the length with a pencil. Allow to dry 48 hours. Measure again. Acceptable shrinkage: 2–4% per dimension. If shrinkage exceeds 4%, the mix is too clay-rich — add sand. If shrinkage is under 1%, add clay.

**Interpreting results**: It is common to need 3–5 test batches before finding the right ratio for a new soil source. Keep notes on every batch and adjust one variable at a time.

### 1.6 Wall Construction: Coursing and Drying Schedule

**Foundation requirements**

Cob walls require a foundation that keeps them above grade moisture. Minimum options:
- Rubble trench: 18-inch-deep trench filled with compacted gravel, finished with a stem wall of stone, brick, or concrete raising the cob base 6–12 inches above finished grade
- Concrete footing and stem wall: Standard 16-inch-wide footing, stem wall 8–12 inches above grade

The stem wall must be waterproofed or constructed of a material that does not wick moisture. A course of gravel-filled polypropylene bags (from earthbag technique, see Section 2) makes an excellent moisture break at the base of a cob wall and is a hybrid approach used by practitioners in wet climates.

**Wall thickness**

Standard thickness for single-story non-habitable structures: 12–16 inches. For load-bearing walls supporting a roof: minimum 12 inches for single-story. The Cob Research Institute's code-approved specifications confirm minimum 12-inch thickness for load-bearing applications.

**Coursing schedule**

Build up lifts of 8–10 inches per session. This is the practical maximum before the fresh cob begins to slump under its own weight. Do not try to rush by building more height per session.

Between courses: roughen the top surface of the previous lift with your fingers or a stick before the next application. This "tooth" improves mechanical bond. Lightly mist the roughened surface with water if it has dried fully, to re-wet the contact zone. Bond is strongest when the previous course is firm but not fully cured.

**Drying times between lifts**: In hot, dry summer conditions, a 10-inch lift of cob may be firm enough to support the next course within 3–7 days. In cool, humid conditions, allow 2–4 weeks. The practical test: press your thumb firmly against the top of the previous lift. If your thumb leaves an impression deeper than 1/4 inch, the wall is not ready for the next course. If the surface feels hard and dry under your thumb and you can walk on top of the wall without it compressing, proceed.

**Full wall cure**: A completed cob wall typically requires 6–9 months to dry fully from the interior out. Do not plaster until the wall has fully dried. Plastering a wet cob wall traps moisture inside and causes long-term deterioration.

**Even building across perimeter**: Never build one section of wall much higher than adjacent sections. Differential drying across uneven heights causes differential shrinkage and large cracks. Keep the perimeter within 2 courses (18–20 inches) of evenness.

**Expected shrinkage**: Cob shrinks 2–4% per dimension as it dries. A 12-inch wall may end up 11–11.5 inches wide. Account for this in final dimension planning.

Cob functions as thermal mass rather than insulation. Its value is in moderating temperature swings, not in blocking heat transfer the way insulation does. In climates where daytime temperatures are 20°F or more above nighttime temperatures, a thick cob wall absorbs heat during the day and releases it at night, reducing heating and cooling demand. In consistently cold or consistently hot climates with small diurnal swings, the thermal mass advantage diminishes.

### 1.8 Cob Troubleshooting

| Problem | Likely Cause | Solution |
|---|---|---|
| Mix crumbles when dry | Too little clay, too much sand | Increase clay by 10%; re-test |
| Mix slumps on wall during application | Too wet or too clay-rich | Allow to dry before application; add sand to next batch |
| Wide cracks (>1/4 in) in drying wall | Drying too fast in sun/wind | Shade and mist wall during cure; slow drying prevents large cracks |
| Cracks between courses | Insufficient tooth/bond at course interface | Roughen surface more aggressively; mist before each new course |
| Straw clumping instead of distributing | Straw too long or mixed too little | Chop straw shorter; increase mixing cycles |
| Wall section slumping despite dry previous course | Building too high per session | Limit lifts to 8–10 inches; wait for full firmness |
| Crumbling at wall base | Rising damp from foundation | Check foundation drainage; install moisture break if absent |
| Surface erosion from rain splash | No base protection | Install gravel skirt 12–18 inches from base; check roof overhang |

## Section 2: Earthbag Construction

### 2.1 Overview and Where It Fits

Earthbag construction uses polypropylene woven bags filled with compacted soil or gravel, stacked in courses like giant bricks with strands of barbed wire between each course providing tensile reinforcement. The technique was developed and standardized by Iranian architect Nadir Khalili, who built 15 refugee shelters in Iran in collaboration with UNDP and UNHCR in response to refugee displacement from the Persian Gulf crisis. Earthbag structures have since been used by UNHCR and other humanitarian organizations in seismic zones in Iran, Iraq, and other countries.

The structural logic of earthbag is derived from the same principle as sandbag flood control: compressed granular fill in flexible containment is extremely strong under compression and the barbed wire between courses interlocks the bags against outward sliding. Per Owen Geiger and Kelly Hart at EarthbagBuilding.com (a primary open-source practitioner documentation hub), earthbag domes with proper barbed wire and a bond beam perform well in earthquake tests when constructed symmetrically.

Earthbag is best suited to:
- Hot, dry climates (thermal mass walls absorb heat by day, release by night; R-value is modest at R-1 to R-3 for 18-inch walls)
- Round or dome structures (the strongest earthbag geometry; straight walls need additional buttressing)
- Sites with locally available fill soil that reduces cost
- Emergency or rapid construction: a small dome can be erected with an organized crew in a few days of work

Earthbag is poorly suited to:
- Cold climates (poor insulation value, high thermal mass means walls absorb interior heat and release it overnight)
- Long-term structures in persistently wet climates without excellent plaster maintenance
- Structures over 20 feet interior diameter without structural engineering

### 2.2 Materials Selection and Sourcing

**Bags**

Standard earthbag bags are woven polypropylene, 18 inches x 30 inches when laid flat. After filling and tamping, they measure approximately 5 inches x 20 inches in cross-section. Budget approximately 143 bags per 100 square feet of wall face area (one bag covers roughly 0.7 sq ft of wall face per the Natural Building Blog materials calculator).

Polypropylene degrades rapidly under UV exposure — typical degradation begins within weeks of direct sun exposure. UV-treated bags extend this to several years, but all earthbag walls must be plastered within 4–6 weeks of completing the bag work. Never leave bags exposed indefinitely.

For foundation courses: use double-walled bags (one bag inside another) filled with gravel, not soil. The gravel foundation course provides drainage and prevents moisture from wicking up into the earthen fill courses above.

**Fill soil**

The ideal earthbag fill is 30% clay and 70% sand/gravel by volume. This mixture compacts firmly under tamping and gains additional cohesion as clay particles bond under pressure. Test soil in a jar (see cob section) — the clay layer should represent 25–35% of settled material depth.

If local soil is too sandy: add dry clay. If too clay-rich: add coarse sand or decomposed granite (sold at landscape supply yards as "DG"). Avoid expansive clays.

Foundation courses and any courses below grade or near grade: fill with gravel only (3/4-inch crushed gravel or road base) to ensure drainage.

**Barbed wire**

Use 4-point, 12.5-gauge barbed wire. The heavier gauge is significantly stronger than lighter 15.5-gauge wire. Two strands run along the entire perimeter of each course. The barbed points interlock mechanically with the bag fabric of the course above and below, resisting outward sliding and providing the tension component of the compression-tension structural system.

Budget approximately 200 linear feet of barbed wire per 50 bags of wall height, accounting for the perimeter circumference of your structure times two strands.

### 2.4 Step-by-Step: Foundation and Wall Construction

**Step 1: Site preparation and layout**

Excavate to stable subsoil (remove all topsoil and organic material). Dig a trench 12 inches deep and 24 inches wide around the perimeter of your structure. Fill the trench with compacted gravel to 2 inches below grade. This rubble trench foundation provides drainage below and around the base of your wall.

For a round structure: establish the center point with a stake. Attach a string equal to the interior radius. Walk the string to mark the circle. Dig your perimeter trench on that line.

**Step 2: First course (gravel drainage course)**

Fill double-walled bags with 3/4-inch gravel. Fold the open end under and tamp firmly. The first course must sit on your compacted gravel trench fill, at or slightly above grade. The top of the first course should be 2–4 inches above finished grade minimum — this keeps soil fill courses away from ground moisture.

**Step 3: Barbed wire between every course**

After each completed course, lay two strands of barbed wire flat on top of the course, running parallel to the wall direction. Overlap wire ends by 12 inches where they meet. For round walls, wire curves naturally. For rectangular walls, use staples or bent wire to hold wire flat at corners.

**Step 4: Soil fill courses**

Fill bags to approximately 75–80% capacity — not full. Overfilled bags won't lay flat and create uneven courses. Fill with your prepared soil mix, fold the open end under, and place the bag against the previous course with the fold facing the direction you are building (fold side in the direction of travel, not facing outward).

Tamp firmly with the cast iron tamper. Each bag must be tamped to a uniform height across the course. Target approximately 5 inches of finished height per course.

Stagger the joints between courses like brickwork (running bond): center each bag over the joint of the two bags below it. This distributes loads and improves structural integrity.

**Step 5: Continuous checks**

Check level across every course. Use your string line from the center pole to verify the perimeter of round walls. Earthbag domes are extremely sensitive to asymmetry — a wall that is not circular or symmetrical loses its structural advantage.

**Step 6: Bond beam**

At wall top (before beginning dome construction or before attaching roof), install a continuous reinforced bond beam: either a poured concrete ring beam or a built-up timber plate (doubled 2x6 or 2x8 lumber, bolted together and secured to the wall with anchor bolts set into a final poured concrete course). The bond beam ties the structure together and provides a secure attachment point for roof framing.

### 2.5 Dome Geometry: Calculating Course Height for a 14-Foot Interior Diameter Dome

A stable earthbag dome follows the profile of a catenary curve (the curve formed by a hanging chain). The practical rule documented by EarthbagBuilding.com practitioners: **the dome height should equal approximately the interior diameter** for a stable corbelled earthbag dome. A 14-foot interior diameter dome should therefore rise to approximately 14 feet at the apex.

**The center pole method** (primary geometry guide for earthbag domes):

Set a vertical pole at the exact center of your dome, length equal to the interior radius (7 feet for a 14-foot diameter). Attach a string from the top of this pole to the outer edge of each course as you lay it. The string, kept taut, ensures each course stays on the correct hemispherical radius. As you build higher, the string guides each successive course to step inward by the correct amount.

**Corbelling increments**: Each course steps inward by 2–3 inches per course in the lower dome section (below loft height, approximately 8 feet). Above that, larger inward increments are possible as the geometry changes. Do not exceed a 30-degree overhang angle (measured from vertical) on any single course — this is the corbelling limit for unsupported earthbag.

**Bag count estimate for a 14-foot dome**:

Using the formula: total bags = hemispherical dome surface area ÷ area per bag.

Hemispherical surface area = 3.14 × radius² × 2 = 3.14 × 7² × 2 ≈ 308 square feet (for a full hemisphere)

At 0.7 sq ft per bag: 308 ÷ 0.7 ≈ 440 bags for the dome shell, not including the foundation courses or the vertical wall section below dome height.

For a 14-foot dome with 4 feet of vertical wall below the dome curve: add the cylindrical wall area (3.14 × 14 ft diameter × 4 ft height = 176 sq ft ÷ 0.7 sq ft per bag ≈ 250 bags).

**Total estimate: 690–750 bags** for a complete 14-foot dome with 4-foot vertical walls, plus 10–15% contingency for rejects and adjustments: **800–870 bags total**.

Run your specific numbers through the Natural Building Blog's earthbag dome materials calculator (naturalbuildingblog.com) for a site-specific estimate once you know your bag dimensions after a test tamp.

## Section 3: Strawbale Construction

### 3.1 Overview and Where It Fits

Strawbale construction uses compressed rectangular bales of grain straw as wall material. Bales are either load-bearing (the "Nebraska-style" or load-bearing method, where bales carry the roof load directly) or infill (a structural timber or steel frame carries loads; bales fill cavities as insulation). Both configurations are plastered on both interior and exterior surfaces with earthen or lime plaster, which protects the bales from moisture and provides structural contribution in the load-bearing system.

A 23-inch-thick strawbale wall achieves R-33 per the Green Building Advisor and ACEEE research, making it one of the highest-performing natural insulation assemblies available. By comparison, a standard 2x6 stud wall with fiberglass insulation achieves approximately R-19. The R-33 performance makes strawbale well-suited to cold climates where other natural building techniques underperform.

Strawbale is currently listed in the International Residential Code as Appendix S (Strawbale Construction), and multiple states including California, Arizona, Oregon, Colorado, Nevada, Texas, and New Mexico have adopted local strawbale building codes. For non-habitable accessory structures under the permit-exempt threshold, formal code compliance is less critical, but the code specifications document best practices worth following regardless.

Strawbale is best suited to:
- Cold climates requiring high-R insulation assemblies
- Sites near grain farming operations with easy bale access
- Load-bearing single-story structures (Nebraska style) or post-and-beam infill
- Builders comfortable with plastering

Strawbale is poorly suited to:
- Wet climates without significant roof overhangs and careful moisture management
- Two-story load-bearing structures (most codes limit Nebraska-style to single story)
- Situations where bales cannot be sourced and kept dry until installation

### 3.2 Materials Selection: Bale Specifications

**Bale type**: Two-string bales and three-string bales are both used. Three-string bales (typically 14–16 inches wide, 23–24 inches tall, 36–48 inches long) are preferred for load-bearing construction because of their higher density and greater cross-section. Two-string bales (14 inches wide, 18 inches tall, 36 inches long) are used in infill construction where the structural frame carries loads.

**Bale density**: Minimum 6.5–7.25 pounds per cubic foot (pcf) dry density per most strawbale building codes. The 2024 IRC Appendix BJ specifies minimum 7.25 pcf. Higher density is better — bales up to 12 pcf are available from commercial operations and provide superior performance. A typical 3-string bale at acceptable density weighs 75–80 pounds.

How to check bale density in the field: measure bale dimensions (L × W × H in feet = cubic feet), then weigh the bale on a scale. Divide weight by cubic feet. Example: 14" x 23" x 48" bale = (1.17 ft × 1.92 ft × 4.0 ft) = 8.98 cubic feet. If it weighs 75 lbs: 75 ÷ 8.98 = 8.35 pcf. Acceptable.

**Straw species**: Wheat straw is the most common and well-documented. Rice and oat straw are also acceptable. The key is that bales must be straw (post-harvest, dried grain stems) not hay. Hay contains higher protein and moisture content, decomposes faster, and supports mold growth.

**Visual inspection**: Each bale should be uniformly colored (golden tan), firm to the touch throughout, and smell faintly of grain. Reject bales that are: (a) discolored (gray or black indicates mold), (b) soft and spongy in any section, (c) smell musty or fermented, or (d) have visibly degraded straw fibers.

### 3.3 Moisture Testing: Four Field Tests and Pass/Fail Thresholds

Moisture is the primary failure mode for strawbale construction. Mold growth begins at approximately 20% moisture content by weight. All bales must be below 20% moisture at time of installation; below 15% is preferred; below 10% is ideal.

**Test 1: Hay probe moisture meter** (most reliable, required for final acceptance)

A hay probe moisture meter has two long steel probes, 8–12 inches, that insert into the bale center where moisture accumulates and is hardest to detect externally. Standard two-pin surface moisture meters designed for wood do not penetrate bale centers and are not adequate for bale testing.

Procedure: Insert the probes fully into the bale center. Take readings at multiple depths and locations. Test 10–15 bales randomly from every delivery. Any reading above 20% = reject the bale. Any reading above 15% = flag for monitoring or dry further before installation.

**Pass/fail thresholds**:
- Below 15%: Acceptable for installation
- 15–20%: Proceed with caution; check again in 30 days post-installation through electrical box access
- Above 20%: Do not install; dry under cover in ventilated stack, re-test before use

**Test 2: Visual center cut** (fast field screen, not a replacement for meter testing)

Cut a bale in half with a chainsaw or long handsaw. Examine the cut face. Acceptable: uniform golden or tan color throughout. Reject if: any gray, black, or green discoloration; any visible mold; damp or clammy feel at the center.

**Test 3: Sniff test** (screening only)

Push your nose into the bale. Good bales smell faintly of dried grain. Moldy bales smell musty, fermented, or of mildew. Fail any bale that fails the sniff test and confirm with meter.

**Test 4: Post-installation monitoring at electrical boxes**

During construction and for 12 months post-plastering: insert a hay probe moisture meter through the back of electrical outlet boxes (positioned against the bales) to monitor bale moisture without damaging plaster. Any reading trending above 15% warrants investigation of the exterior plaster for cracks or gaps. This test is documented by Strawbale.com as the standard ongoing monitoring procedure.

### 3.4 Load-Bearing vs. Infill Configurations

**Load-bearing (Nebraska style)**

Bales are stacked like masonry, with the wide face down (on-flat orientation). The bale-and-plaster composite carries roof loads. Maximum single-story height. The composite wall system (bales + plaster skins) achieves structural load-carrying capacity.

Key requirement per the Green Building Advisor: load-bearing strawbale walls must be pre-compressed before plastering. Without pre-compression, the wall settles under roof weight after plastering, pulling the plaster surface down and causing cracks. Pre-compression is achieved by running threaded rod or tension straps from the foundation plate up through the bale stack to the roof plate, and tightening them to compress the stack before attaching plaster.

**Infill (post-and-beam)**

A structural timber or steel frame is erected first and carries all building loads. Bales fill in between frame members as insulation and plaster substrate. Pre-compression is not required because the frame carries loads independently of the bales.

Most building codes accept infill strawbale construction more readily than load-bearing, per the Green Building Advisor. For permit-exempt structures, Nebraska style is more buildable without engineering input because it requires no separate structural frame.

**For the scope of this guide** (non-habitable structures under 200 sq ft): load-bearing Nebraska style is simpler to build and appropriate for single-story walls up to 8–9 feet in height.

### 3.5 Pinning

Per IRC Appendix S specifications and Coconino County's supplemental strawbale guidelines (a publicly available reference standard):

- **Bottom course**: each bale in the bottom course must be pinned to the foundation plate or sill using vertical rebar pins, 1/2-inch diameter, driven through the bale and anchored into the foundation. Minimum 2 pins per bale.
- **Between courses**: bales in upper courses are pinned to the course below with wooden stakes (2-inch x 2-inch wood, sharpened to a point) or 1/2-inch rebar, driven vertically through at least 2 courses.
- **Infill walls**: bales must be pinned to the structural frame at minimum every 4 feet horizontally and every 2 courses vertically.
- **Top course**: must be connected to the roof plate with rebar pins and tied with baling wire or tension straps.

Pinning stabilizes the wall during construction and prevents differential movement that would crack plaster. It does not significantly affect the final structural performance of a fully plastered wall.

### 3.6 Step-by-Step: Wall Construction

**Step 1: Foundation and moisture protection**

Strawbale walls require the same "good hat and good boots" principle as cob: excellent roof overhang (minimum 18 inches from wall face) and a raised foundation platform that keeps bales off grade. Minimum: bales must be at least 12–18 inches above finished grade. Set a treated lumber or concrete sill plate on your foundation with a capillary break (sheet metal flashing or bitumen strip) between foundation and sill.

**Step 2: Lay first course**

Lay first bales flat on the sill plate, butt end to butt end. Insert foundation pins (rebar through bale center, driven into foundation). Check level and alignment. Bales should have consistent joint staggering — butt-to-butt of same course, with running bond to the course above.

**Step 3: Build up courses**

Alternate bale orientation at corners: one course has bales running perpendicular to the previous at each corner, providing mechanical interlock. Insert inter-course pins after every second course.

**Step 4: Window and door openings**

Build wooden "bucks" (frames of rough lumber) that will hold the full thickness of the bale wall. Typical 3-string bale wall is 23–24 inches thick — your buck must span that depth. Anchor bucks to foundation or frame before stacking bales around them. Sills must slope outward with a drip groove at the underside to prevent water from running back to the wall face.

**Step 5: Top plate and pre-compression (load-bearing only)**

Install a built-up double top plate (doubled 2x6) that bridges all bales and distributes roof loads evenly. Run threaded rods or tensioning straps from foundation plate up through or alongside the wall to the top plate. Tighten progressively — a little on each side, working around the perimeter — until you see 1–2 inches of compression in the full wall height. Allow the wall to settle for 24–48 hours, then tighten again before applying plaster.

**Step 6: Plastering**

Plaster material choice depends on climate:
- **Earthen plaster**: clay-based plaster applied in 3 coats (scratch coat, brown coat, finish coat). Vapor-open (allows moisture to escape from wall). Appropriate in dry climates. Not water-resistant — requires lime finish coat or annual maintenance in wet climates.
- **Lime plaster**: hydraulic lime or natural hydraulic lime (NHL). More durable and water-resistant than earthen plaster while still vapor-open. Appropriate for most climates. Cement stucco must not be used on strawbale walls in wet climates — it traps moisture inside the wall and causes decay.

Plaster application schedule (three-coat system):
1. Scratch coat: 3/8–1/2 inch thick, applied to both faces of wall. Wet the bale surface first. While wet, scratch horizontal grooves with a notched tool to provide mechanical key for the next coat. Cure 7–14 days (keep damp, not wet, in hot weather).
2. Brown coat: 1/2–5/8 inch thick over scratch coat. Brings wall to near-final plane. Cure 7–14 days.
3. Finish coat: 1/4–3/8 inch, final texture coat. Lime finish coats may require carbonation curing for several weeks.

**Do not plaster over wet bales.** Check moisture at multiple bale depths with your hay probe before applying any plaster. Sealing moisture inside traps it where it cannot escape.

## Section 4: Compressed Earth Block (CEB)

### 4.1 Overview and Where It Fits

Compressed Earth Block (CEB) construction uses a mechanical press to compress moistened soil into uniform rectangular blocks, which are then stacked and mortared like conventional masonry. The technique has been documented extensively by Open Source Ecology (OSE) as part of the Global Village Construction Set (GVCS), which includes open-source plans for a hydraulic CEB press (the "Liberator") capable of producing 6 blocks per minute with 12.5 gallons per minute hydraulic flow.

CEB differs from the other techniques in this section in that it separates the manufacturing phase from the construction phase: blocks are pressed, cured for 28 days, then used like bricks. This allows quality-control on each block before wall assembly begins. Stabilized CEB blocks (with 5–10% Portland cement) have achieved compressive strengths of 1,200–2,000+ psi, comparable to low-grade conventional brick.

CEB is best suited to:
- Sites with suitable soil (15–40% clay, balance sand and silt)
- Projects requiring high compressive strength in a natural material
- Organized group builds where a production team can press and cure blocks over several weeks before construction begins
- Structures requiring precision — CEB walls lay as accurately as conventional masonry
- Hot, dry climates or climates where wall stabilization controls moisture performance

CEB is not suited to:
- Sites without a press (hand ramming produces lower density blocks; the CINVA Ram manual press is the minimum)
- Sites with very high clay content soils (above 40% clay) that cause excessive shrinkage
- Very wet climates without stabilization — unstabilized CEB blocks dissolve in sustained rain

### 4.2 Soil Selection and Testing

Suitable CEB soil composition (per Open Source Ecology GVCS documentation and research literature):
- Clay: 15–40%
- Silt: 25–40%
- Sand to small gravel: 40–70%
- Plasticity Index (PI): not to exceed 12–15 for the pressed mix (PI is the difference between the Liquid Limit and Plastic Limit, as determined by Atterberg limits testing; field approximation tests follow below)
- Organic matter: none (use subsoil only)

**Field Test 1: The Ribbon Test** (clay content screening)

Moisten a soil sample to a stiff plastic consistency (like firm modeling clay). Roll it into a ball, then push it between your thumb and forefinger to extrude a ribbon. Measure how long the ribbon extends before it breaks under its own weight.

- Ribbon breaks under 1 inch: soil is too sandy; may need clay amendment
- Ribbon 1–2 inches: low-clay soil; may be acceptable with testing
- Ribbon 2–3.5 inches: moderate clay; likely suitable for CEB
- Ribbon over 3.5 inches without breaking: high clay; likely too plastic; may need sand amendment

**Field Test 2: The Ball Test** (plasticity screening)

Form a 1-inch diameter ball of moistened soil. Drop it from waist height onto hard ground.
- Shatters: too dry or too sandy
- Holds shape perfectly: correct workability
- Flattens and spreads significantly: too wet or too clay-rich

**Field Test 3: The Jar Sedimentation Test**

As described in the cob section: fill a quart jar one-third with soil, add water, shake, let settle overnight. Measure layer proportions. Target: clay layer (top) = 15–40% of total settled depth; sand/gravel (bottom) = 40–70%.

**Field Test 4: The Dry Strength Test**

Allow a small sample of moistened soil to dry fully. Try to break it between your fingers. Very hard to break (requires significant force): good clay content. Breaks easily: too sandy. Crumbles as powder: too silty.

**Soil amendments**:
- Too clay-rich: add coarse sand until tests pass
- Too sandy: add dry clay (bentonite or kaolin) or source a more clay-rich subsoil
- Expansive clay suspected: use only with Portland cement stabilization; expansive clays alone are not suitable for CEB

### 4.3 Stabilization Options

Unstabilized CEB blocks are suitable for interior walls in protected structures but are not durable in rain exposure. For exterior walls and weather-exposed applications:

**Portland cement stabilization**: 5–8% Portland cement by dry soil weight is the most common approach. Produces blocks with compressive strengths of 500–2,000 psi at 28 days. Open Source Ecology recommends testing: press blocks at 5%, 7.5%, and 10% cement; cure 7 days; drop each in a bucket of water overnight; select the lowest percentage that survives without significant disintegration.

**Lime stabilization**: 6–10% hydrated lime by dry soil weight. Lime reacts with clay through pozzolanic and cation exchange reactions, increasing strength and reducing plasticity. Produces blocks with 28-day strengths of 300–800 psi depending on clay mineralogy. Lime-stabilized blocks continue to gain strength over months and years. Appropriate for clay-rich soils.

**Combined lime + cement**: Small percentages of both (e.g., 4% lime + 4% cement) often outperform either alone in clay-rich soils, per Auroville Earth Institute research (the primary global CEB research institution).

**Unstabilized**: Acceptable for interior walls and protected applications in dry climates. Compressive strength 150–400 psi when well-pressed with suitable soil. Monitor for moisture exposure.

### 4.4 Step-by-Step: Pressing and Curing CEB Blocks

**Step 1: Soil preparation**

Screen soil through a 1/2-inch mesh to remove rocks and debris. Break up clay clumps. Mix amendments (sand, lime, cement) dry before adding water.

If using cement stabilization: mix the dry ingredients (soil + cement) thoroughly before adding water. Once water is added to cement, the working window is approximately 1–2 hours before cement begins to set. Press all cement-stabilized blocks within this window.

**Step 2: Moisture content calibration**

Target moisture content: 10–12% by weight. Field test: grab a handful of mix and squeeze hard. When you open your hand, the soil should hold its shape (not crumble) but not exude water or leave wet marks on your palm. If it crumbles: too dry, add water. If water squeezes out: too wet, spread mix to dry.

**Step 3: Pressing**

Load the press mold with a measured quantity of prepared mix. Different presses specify different fill heights — follow your specific press manual. Press to full mechanical advantage (the lever or hydraulic cylinder should reach its travel limit, indicating full compression).

Release the block. Inspect immediately: it should hold its shape without crumbling, have uniform geometry, and have no visible cracks from excessive clay content.

Standard block dimensions from the OSE Liberator press: approximately 14 inches x 7 inches x 4 inches (354 x 178 x 100 mm). Actual dimensions vary by soil composition.

**Step 4: Handling fresh blocks**

Fresh blocks are fragile. Handle them minimally — one person lifts, places onto a flat curing surface. Do not drop or stack multiple blocks immediately.

**Step 5: Curing**

Lay blocks on a flat, level surface in shade (not direct sun, which causes rapid surface drying and cracking). Do not stack during first 24 hours.

For cement-stabilized blocks: moist-cure for 7–14 days. Cover with burlap or plastic sheet and keep damp. This replicated standard concrete curing protocol — the cement requires moisture to hydrate and develop strength.

For lime-stabilized blocks: keep moist for 28 days; lime carbonation curing is slower than cement hydration.

Minimum cure time before construction: 28 days for all stabilized blocks.

**Step 6: Quality acceptance test**

After 28 days of curing, test a sample of 5–10% of your blocks:
- Drop test: drop from waist height on hard ground — should not break or chip significantly
- Water immersion (stabilized blocks for exterior use): place block in a bucket of water for 24 hours. Remove and allow to dry. Acceptable: some surface softening but block retains shape and structural integrity. Fail: block significantly swells, cracks, or begins to dissolve.

Reject blocks that fail the water immersion test for exterior use; they can be used in interior applications.

### 4.5 Wall Construction with CEB

CEB walls are laid identically to conventional masonry:

**Mortar options**:
- Soil mortar: same soil mix as blocks, without pressing. Used for interior or protected applications. No cement required.
- Lime mortar: hydraulic lime + sand (1:3 ratio). Standard for exterior CEB walls. Allows vapor transmission.
- Cement mortar: cement + lime + sand (1:1:6 ratio). High durability; less vapor-open. Appropriate for very exposed conditions.

**Joint thickness**: 1/2–3/4 inch. Thicker joints indicate inconsistent block dimensions; adjust pressing consistency.

**Running bond**: stagger vertical joints identically to conventional masonry. Lap each block over the joint of the two blocks below it by at least half the block length.

**Corners**: alternate which block runs perpendicular at corners (same as conventional masonry corner-lapping).

**Openings**: Standard masonry lintels above window and door openings. Reinforced concrete lintels are most common; timber lintels work for narrow openings under modest loads.

**Reinforcement**: For seismic zones and load-bearing applications, vertical rebar through hollow cores (where the press produces hollow blocks) or alongside wall faces, grouted with cement-soil mix, provides lateral reinforcement. Consult OSE GVCS technical documentation for specific reinforcement schedules.

## Section 5: Repair and Maintenance of Earthen Structures

### 5.1 Overview

All earthen structures — cob, earthbag, CEB, adobe, rammed earth — share the same vulnerabilities: water infiltration, freeze-thaw damage, surface erosion, and cracking. This section covers crack assessment and triage, repointing, weatherproofing, emergency tarping, and water damage remediation. Strawbale structures share some of these maintenance needs (plaster maintenance, moisture monitoring) but have the additional critical concern of bale moisture monitoring, which is covered in Section 3.

The governing principle for earthen structure maintenance is the same as for traditional lime-mortar masonry: keep the "hat and boots" in good condition. The hat is the roof — wide overhangs, intact roofing material, functioning gutters where applicable. The boots are the base — the raised foundation, drainage, and splash protection that keep ground moisture and rain splash away from the lower wall. Neglect either, and eventual deterioration is certain.

Annual inspection is the minimum maintenance schedule. Post-major-storm inspections are recommended in climates with significant rainfall, freeze-thaw cycles, or wind-driven rain.

### 5.2 Crack Assessment: Distinguishing Structural from Shrinkage Cracks

Cracks in earthen walls fall into two categories with very different implications:

**Category 1: Shrinkage cracks (cosmetic)**

Shrinkage cracks are caused by the drying and curing of the earthen material after construction. They are normal and expected in every earthen structure. Characteristics:
- Appear within the first 6–18 months of construction, particularly after the first dry season
- Typically hairline to 1/16 inch (1.5 mm) wide
- May form a network of fine lines across wall surfaces ("crazing")
- Even distribution across a wall face
- Do not affect structural performance
- Can be filled with earthen plaster or left for the final plaster coat to address

**Category 2: Structural cracks (require investigation)**

Structural cracks indicate movement, differential settling, or load concentration that must be addressed:
- Wider than 1/4 inch (6 mm)
- Extending through the full wall thickness (visible on both faces)
- Stepped cracks following masonry joints (in CEB walls)
- Cracks radiating from window or door corners
- Cracks that are actively growing — mark both ends with pencil and date; recheck in 30 days; a crack that has grown is actively moving and requires engineering assessment

**Field triage protocol for cracks**:

1. Measure width: hairline to 1/16 inch = cosmetic; 1/16 to 1/4 inch = monitor; over 1/4 inch = investigate root cause before repairing
2. Check depth: insert a feeler gauge or thin knife. Shallow surface cracks in plaster only = cosmetic repair. Full-depth cracks in the wall substrate = investigate
3. Check pattern: random fine network = normal shrinkage; large diagonal cracks from corners of openings = concentrated stress; horizontal cracks at one elevation = lateral loading concern (retained earth, snow load)
4. Mark and monitor: any crack wider than 1/8 inch, mark and re-inspect in 30 and 90 days before deciding whether to repair or investigate structurally

### 5.3 Repair Procedures

**Cosmetic crack repair (hairline to 1/16 inch)**

Materials: earthen plaster patch mix (same clay-sand ratio as original plaster, without straw); or pre-mixed lime putty plaster for lime-plastered surfaces.

Procedure:
1. Moisten the crack with a spray bottle — this prevents the patch from drying too fast and pulling away at the edges
2. Force the patch mix into the crack with a flexible putty knife or your finger, working from the bottom of the crack upward
3. Feather the edges to blend with the surrounding surface
4. Allow to dry (shade and mist for 3–5 days in hot weather)
5. Sand lightly if needed for final texture match

**Medium crack repair (1/16 to 1/4 inch): repointing**

Procedure per Building Conservation (buildingconservation.com) guidelines for earthen and lime masonry:
1. Rake out loose material from the crack to a depth of at least 3/4 inch (rake out more in width than depth, to key the new mortar in)
2. Remove all loose, powdery, or crumbled material. Use a stiff brush and vacuum if possible
3. Wet the crack thoroughly with water. Allow to absorb; re-wet. Working into dry substrate causes the repair to pull away
4. Pack the crack with fresh material (earthen mortar for earthen walls; lime mortar for lime-plastered walls). Work in layers no more than 3/8 inch thick, allowing each layer to firm up before adding the next
5. Scratch the surface of each layer before it fully sets to key the next layer
6. Finish flush with the surrounding surface or with a slight recess for a final finish layer

**Large crack investigation and repair (over 1/4 inch)**

Do not repair large structural cracks until the root cause is identified and addressed. Filling a crack caused by active foundation movement will cause the same crack to re-open within one season.

Root cause investigation:
- Check foundation drainage: saturated soil under or alongside the foundation causes settling; dig alongside the wall and inspect moisture conditions
- Check for erosion at wall base: splash erosion removes wall material at base, undercuts the wall
- Check load distribution: uneven roof loads, improperly supported beams, or blocked downspouts concentrating roof runoff alongside the wall are common structural crack causes

Once root cause is addressed, repair follows the same procedure as medium cracks but may require backing material for very large gaps: stuff clean straw, fiber, or metal lath into wide cracks before pointing, to prevent the mortar from falling through.

### 5.4 Weatherproofing

**Lime wash and lime paint**

Lime wash is a diluted lime putty slurry (approximately 1 part lime putty to 5–10 parts water) brushed onto exterior earthen or lime plaster surfaces. It provides moderate water repellency, seals hairline cracks, and adds a protective sacrificial layer that can be re-applied annually. Lime wash is vapor-open — it does not trap moisture in the wall. Annual lime wash application is the simplest and most effective exterior maintenance protocol for cob, earthbag, and CEB structures.

Traditional recipe: 1 part mature lime putty + 5–8 parts water + optional pigment (ochre, lampblack, or natural mineral pigments for color). Brush onto dampened surface in multiple thin coats.

**Earth renders (recoating)**

Earthen plaster exteriors erode over time from rain splash and wind. In wet climates, plan to re-apply a finish coat of earthen plaster every 3–5 years. In dry climates, every 5–10 years. Signs that recoating is needed: surface texture loss, pinhole erosion, pitting, areas where aggregate is visibly exposed from binder erosion.

**Splash protection**

The base of every earthen wall requires physical protection from rain splash, which erodes wall material regardless of plaster quality. Options:
- Gravel skirt: 12–18 inch wide band of 3/4-inch gravel laid flat around the wall perimeter, graded slightly away from the wall. Eliminates splash contact.
- Stone plinth: low stone or masonry course at the base of the wall (2–4 courses, up to 18 inches) provides a hard surface that deflects rain splash and resists rising damp. Best long-term solution.

**Roof maintenance**

Because the "hat" is so critical to earthen structure durability, annual roof inspection is mandatory. Check: roofing material for gaps or failures; overhangs intact; gutters (if present) functional and not allowing overflow down wall faces; ridge caps and flashing intact.

### 5.5 Emergency Tarping for Damaged or Incomplete Walls

An earthen wall that loses its roof covering — from storm damage, fire, or mid-construction pause — must be protected within 24–48 hours if significant rain is possible. Saturated cob, earthbag, or CEB walls that dry out once are still structurally sound, but repeated wet-dry cycles cause permanent erosion and loss of strength.

**Emergency tarping procedure**:

1. Use a tarp 10–12 feet wider than the wall (to cover the top and overhang at least 3–4 feet down each face)
2. Lay the tarp flat over the wall top, centered
3. Weight the tarp top with sandbags, bricks, or stones placed every 4 feet — do not place weights at the tarp edges (wind will use the unsecured sides as a lever)
4. Tie the tarp sides down at the base, anchored to stakes, bricks, or the foundation
5. For temporary open structures (mid-construction), tarp over each day's work before leaving — morning dew and overnight humidity can exceed a drying-day's moisture removal

**Restarting after rain exposure**:
- If the wall top was exposed to rain, probe the wall to 12 inches depth at several points before proceeding
- Surface softening is cosmetic; allow to dry to firm and proceed
- If the wall is saturated through (probe comes out with wet soil at depth), allow to dry in the shade with good airflow for 1–2 weeks before adding new work on top

### 5.6 Water Damage Remediation

Water damage in earthen structures ranges from surface erosion (simple recoating) to structural saturation (serious). Assessment:

**Stage 1 — Surface erosion only** (most common)

Signs: pitting, rilling, aggregate exposure, loss of surface detail. Wall still firm to a deep probe.

Repair: Clean loose surface material with a stiff brush. Wet the surface. Apply fresh plaster coat (earthen or lime) in the procedures described in 5.3.

**Stage 2 — Plaster delamination**

Signs: hollow-sounding when tapped; plaster lifting away from substrate; visible gap between plaster and wall behind it.

Repair: Remove the delaminated plaster section entirely — do not try to bond it back over the gap. Investigate why the plaster failed: usually either applied over a wet wall, or moisture has entered through a crack above the delaminated area and undermined the bond. Address the moisture entry point. Allow the substrate to dry fully (test with moisture meter if available). Apply new plaster in full three-coat sequence.

**Stage 3 — Structural wall saturation**

Signs: wall is soft to probing at depth; wall is visibly rounding or melting at corners or base; wall is swelling (CEB with expansive clay).

This is a serious condition. Structural saturation is rare in structures with adequate hat and boots, but can occur from: roof failure, blocked foundation drainage causing flooding around base, repeated freeze-thaw of saturated wall material.

Immediate action:
1. Install emergency tarping (Section 5.5)
2. Ensure drainage — water must not be standing against the wall
3. Allow to dry completely before any repair — this may require 4–6 weeks of dry weather
4. After drying, probe and assess whether structural material remains viable or has lost cohesion
5. If wall core has lost cohesion and crumbles when dry: affected sections may need to be rebuilt
6. Do not rebuild over a wet core — the moisture will migrate into the new material and cause the same failure

### 5.7 Cross-Domain Integration: Earthen Structures and Water Management

Earthen structures do not stand alone in a homestead or community setting. They integrate directly with the water systems covered in Wave 0 of this library.

**Foundation drainage and rainwater harvesting (cross-link: WAVE_0_DOMAIN_2)**

The same rubble trench foundation used under cob and earthbag walls can be extended to direct foundation drainage water toward a downslope infiltration basin or rainwater holding tank. If you are designing a root cellar, workshop, or storage building with an earthen wall, coordinate the foundation drainage layout with any rainwater catchment system. A drainage perimeter that evacuates water away from the building and toward a cistern or swale converts a structural necessity (keep moisture off the wall) into a resource.

**Cistern integration with earthbag**

Earthbag is commonly used for the walls of below-grade or semi-below-grade cisterns — the same gravel-fill foundation courses that protect aboveground earthbag walls are equally applicable to water storage containment walls. An earthbag cistern requires a liner (EPDM or reinforced polyethylene) inside the earthbag wall structure. See WAVE_0_DOMAIN_2 for cistern sizing calculations and liner specifications.

**Greywater and composting toilets (cross-link: WAVE_0_DOMAIN_4)**

If you are building a composting toilet building or a greywater processing building (earthen structures are ideal for these non-habitable applications), the 200-sq-ft permit exemption scope in this domain covers both. Locate greywater treatment and composting toilet structures downslope from the main residence, with drainage directed away from both the earthen building foundations and any rainwater catchment areas, per the contamination setback protocols in WAVE_0_DOMAIN_4.

**Seed storage conditions (cross-link: WAVE_1_SEED_PRESERVATION)**

Earthen walls (cob, CEB, adobe) naturally moderate humidity because clay is hygroscopic — it absorbs moisture when relative humidity is high and releases it when low. A well-plastered cob storage building maintains a more stable internal relative humidity than a wood-frame building, which makes it a favorable environment for seed storage. Target RH for seed storage: below 50% (see WAVE_1_SEED_PRESERVATION for seed moisture content targets). In dry climates, a cob seed storage structure may require no additional humidity control. In humid climates, active desiccant management (silica gel or rice-husk ash) remains necessary even in earthen structures.

**Composting integration (cross-link: WAVE_1_COMPOSTING)**

Building site assessment for cob and CEB involves digging into subsoil — the same excavation that reveals clay content also produces topsoil that must be disposed of. The topsoil stripped from a building site is high-quality organic material suitable for composting or direct use in garden beds. Coordinate the site clearing phase with the composting infrastructure described in WAVE_1_COMPOSTING: stockpile topsoil in the composting area rather than hauling it off-site.

## Conclusion

The four natural building techniques in this domain — cob, earthbag, strawbale, and compressed earth block — together cover the full range of performance requirements for small-scale resilient construction: thermal mass, high insulation, high compressive strength, and rapid emergency deployment. None requires a licensed contractor for the non-habitable accessory structures they are most suited to. All require careful attention to materials selection, moisture management, and foundational drainage.

The common thread across all sections is the relationship between earthen structures and water. Every failure mode in natural building traces back to unmanaged water: too much moisture in the mix, too little roof overhang, inadequate foundation drainage, moisture trapped inside plaster, or standing water against a wall base. Getting the "hat and boots" right is not an afterthought — it is the primary engineering decision in any earthen structure, made before the first bucket of clay is mixed.

The cross-domain connections in this guide are not decorative: they reflect real system integration that practitioners building self-sufficient homesteads and communities encounter. A root cellar built from earthbags shares its foundation drainage with the rainwater harvesting system. A seed storage building built from cob requires the same humidity management principles as the sealed containers described in the seed preservation domain. A composting toilet structure built from CEB blocks stands 20 feet from the water source setback distance described in the water assessment domain. These systems are designed together or they work poorly in isolation.

Use this guide as a starting point. Document your soil tests, your mix ratios, your drying times, and your test results. Every site is different, and the practitioner knowledge built on your specific soil, your specific climate, and your specific structure will be more valuable than any general guide. Share what you learn.
