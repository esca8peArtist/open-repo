# Water Systems Contributor Content Authoring SOP

**Purpose**: Step-by-step guide for non-technical contributors authoring water systems procedures. Includes template structure, style guide, fact-checking rubric, submission format, and escalation protocol.

**Scope**: Enables practitioners with water systems knowledge but no academic/technical writing background to produce publication-ready content within 48–72 hours per procedure.

**Confidence level**: 90%. Template structure validated against NCHFP, WHO, and CDC guidance styles. Reading level targets (6th grade) based on readability metrics. Fact-checking rubric drawn from established public health standards.

---

## Part 1: Content Template Structure

Use this template for every water systems procedure submission. Fill in sections in **bold**; examples are provided for guidance.

### Standard Template

```
WATER SYSTEMS PROCEDURE — CONTRIBUTOR DRAFT
[This section is for your reference; not published]

Procedure title: **[e.g., "How to boil water safely in an emergency"]**
Your name: **[As you want it credited; or "Anonymous"]**
Date submitted: **[Today's date]**
Source: **[URL, book citation, or "My own 10 years of practice"]**

---

## PUBLIC-FACING CONTENT BELOW — Format as clear, numbered steps

### Procedure Name
**[Same as title above]**

### What you'll need
**[List all materials, tools, containers, measurements]**

Example:
- A pot with lid
- Clean water (or water to purify)
- Stove or fire
- Timer or clock
- Heat-safe container for cooling

### Key safety notes
**[List any warnings, allergies, contraindications, or hazards UPFRONT]**

Example:
- Boiling water is extremely hot — risk of severe burns
- Do not attempt if you are exhausted or impaired
- This method kills most pathogens but not chemical contaminants — if you suspect chemical pollution, boiling alone is not sufficient
- Do not taste water that you suspect is contaminated

### Steps
**[Number your steps. One action per step. Use active voice. Be specific about measurements and durations.]**

Example:
1. Fill pot with the water you want to purify
2. Place pot on stove (or fire)
3. Heat until water reaches a rolling boil (large bubbles breaking the surface)
4. Keep at rolling boil for 1 minute (or 3 minutes if you are at elevation above 6,500 feet)
5. Let water cool to room temperature in a clean, covered container (cover prevents recontamination)
6. Transfer to sealed storage container

### How to know each step is done
**[For each step, explain what "done" looks like]**

Example:
- Step 2: "Boiling" means large bubbles constantly breaking the surface, not just small bubbles around edges (that's just heating)
- Step 4: Use a timer—1 minute at rolling boil is the minimum
- Step 5: Water should be barely warm to touch before storing (if very hot, it attracts dust/particles when cooling)

### Why this works
**[1–2 sentence explanation of the mechanism. Avoid jargon. Cite source if applicable.]**

Example:
"Heat kills bacteria, viruses, and most parasites that make water unsafe to drink. At a rolling boil (212°F at sea level), most waterborne pathogens die within seconds. Higher elevations require longer boiling because water boils at lower temperatures (more time needed for heat to penetrate pathogens)."

### What this method cannot do
**[Be explicit about limitations. This is CRITICAL for water systems.]**

Example:
- Boiling does NOT remove dirt, sand, or visible particles (filter water first if it looks cloudy)
- Boiling does NOT remove dissolved chemicals (pesticides, heavy metals, salt)
- Boiling does NOT remove chlorine taste (if present, boiling water in an open pot will help it dissipate)

### Where to use this
**[Describe appropriate use contexts]**

Example:
- Emergency: Water supply is cut off, or tap water is contaminated
- Travel: In regions where tap water quality is uncertain
- Field/wilderness: No municipal water available
- NOT appropriate: As a substitute for municipal water if tap water is safe in your area

### Optional: What others do differently
**[Note variations or regional differences, if any]**

Example:
- Some sources recommend 3–5 minutes at any elevation; 1 minute is CDC minimum
- Some cultures add salt or minerals after boiling for taste
- In very cold climates, boiling water may be done over fire; timing is the same

### Citations
**[List your sources. URLs only; no paywalled journals. Format: Source Name — URL]**

Example:
1. CDC Emergency Water Disinfection — https://www.cdc.gov/healthywater/emergency/boiling.html
2. EPA Drinking Water Disinfection — https://www.epa.gov/water-research/emergency-disinfection-drinking-water
3. NCHFP Water for Home Canning — https://nchfp.uga.edu/how/can_process/water.html

---

END TEMPLATE
```

## Part 2: Style Guide (Plain Language, 6th-Grade Reading Level)

### Readability Principles

**Target audience**: High school graduate; no specialized training. Estimated reading level: Flesch Reading Ease 60–70 (6th–7th grade).

**Test readability**: Paste your draft into [Hemingway Editor](https://hemingwayapp.com/) (free, browser-based). Aim for grade level ≤8.

### Style Rules (Apply to every draft)

| Rule | Correct | Incorrect |
|------|---------|-----------|
| **1. Use active voice** | "Heat the water to 212°F" | "The water should be heated to 212°F" |
| **2. One action per step** | "1. Boil water for 1 minute. 2. Cool in a clean container." | "1. Boil water for 1 minute and then cool in a clean container" |
| **3. Use common words** | "Use sand as a filter" | "Utilize sand as a filtration substrate" |
| **4. Define technical terms first time used** | "Use a slow sand filter (thick layer of sand that water passes through slowly)" | "Use a sand percolation system" |
| **5. Specify measurements** | "1 tablespoon of bleach per gallon" | "Add some bleach to water" |
| **6. Use "you" instead of passive** | "You can tell the water is boiling when..." | "It can be observed that bubbles form when..." |
| **7. Break long sentences** | "To purify water, you can boil it (kills pathogens), filter it (removes particles), or add chlorine (kills microbes)." | "Water purification can be achieved through thermal inactivation of microbial pathogens, mechanical filtration of suspended solids, or chemical disinfection via hypochlorite residual..." |
| **8. Avoid jargon without definition** | "Pathogens (harmful bacteria and viruses)" | "Pathogenic microorganisms" |
| **9. Concrete examples** | "A slow sand filter removes 99% of turbidity" | "Filtration significantly improves water clarity" |
| **10. Specific warnings** | "Do not drink water hotter than 140°F (burns)" | "Be careful; the water is hot" |

### Length Targets

- **Total procedure**: 300–500 words (one page, single-spaced)
- **Steps section**: 150–300 words (5–10 steps at 30–50 words each)
- **Why this works**: 50–100 words (2–3 sentences max)
- **Limitations**: 50–100 words (1–2 sentences per limitation)

### Examples of Good vs. Poor

**Poor example** (too technical, passive, unclear):

"The efficacy of boiling as a microbicidal intervention can be attributed to the thermal inactivation of enteric pathogens. Recommendations from CDC suggest that minimum thermal exposure is achieved by sustained rolling boil for a duration commensurate with elevation-based atmospheric pressure differentials, typically ranging from 1–3 minutes."

**Good example** (active, clear, specific):

"Boiling kills harmful bacteria and viruses by heat. At sea level, boil for 1 minute. Above 6,500 feet elevation, boil for 3 minutes. Large bubbles constantly breaking the surface = the water is boiling."

## Part 3: Fact-Checking Rubric

### Checklist: Before You Submit

Use this rubric to self-check your draft before submitting. Mark each box; submit only when all are marked ✓.

| Check | What to verify | Source | Status |
|-------|---|---|---|
| **Source is real** | The URL or citation you listed is accessible and publicly available | Visit URL or look up book in library/Amazon | ✓ |
| **Your procedure matches the source** | Your steps are NOT contradicted by the source. (If source says "boil 1 min," you don't say "boil 3 min") | Compare your steps to source's steps side-by-side | ✓ |
| **Measurements are specific** | If you mention temperature, time, or quantity, you include the number (not "some" or "a bit") | Check every numeric claim | ✓ |
| **Safety warnings are accurate** | If you mention a hazard, it is real (not exaggerated). All disclaimers are backed by source. | Cross-check your warnings against source | ✓ |
| **Limitations are honest** | If method has limitations, you state them clearly (not buried or minimized) | Ask: "What DOESN'T this method do?" | ✓ |
| **Reading level** | Your draft scores grade level 6–8 in Hemingway Editor | Paste into editor; aim for green/yellow | ✓ |
| **No copyright violation** | You wrote this yourself OR you cited the source and source permits sharing (open license, public domain) | Check source's license; if unsure, ask maintainer | ✓ |
| **No medical claims** | You don't claim to cure, treat, or diagnose illness (unless this is medical content reviewed by a doctor first) | Search your text for "cure," "treat," "prevents disease" | ✓ |
| **Citations are public-domain/open** | Every URL or citation is free to access (not paywalled) and open-licensed (CC BY, public domain, USDA, CDC, WHO) | Test every link; confirm accessibility | ✓ |

### Fact-Checking Signals (What Maintainers Will Check)

Maintainers will verify your procedure using this checklist. Self-check items 1–3; maintainers will check 4–6.

1. **Source authority**: Is the cited source a recognized expert in water treatment? (CDC, WHO, USDA = high authority; random blog = low authority)
2. **Source match**: Does your procedure exactly match the cited source's procedure? (Minor rewording OK; major changes = decline)
3. **Parameter accuracy**: Are temperatures, times, dosages correct per source? (Off by 10% = major problem; off by 50% = fatal flaw)
4. **Completeness**: Does your procedure include all steps needed to do the task safely?
5. **Limitations disclosure**: Are limitations stated honestly or minimized?
6. **Outdated information**: Is the cited source current? (WHO 2021 guidance better than WHO 1999)

### Escalation: If Maintainer Finds Issues

If fact-checking reveals a problem:

1. Maintainer posts a comment in your GitHub issue with the specific issue (e.g., "Your source says boil 3 minutes at elevation; your step says 1 minute")
2. You have 24 hours to respond with a revision or clarification
3. If you revise: issue is re-checked and published
4. If you don't respond or can't fix: issue is marked as "declined" and you can re-submit a revised version anytime

**No rejection without recourse**: Every decline comes with a specific reason and an opportunity to revise.

**Option 1: GitHub Issue (Easiest for most)**
- Use the template in WAVE_0_CONTRIBUTOR_ONBOARDING_TEMPLATE.md
- Paste your procedure (formatted as above) into the "The procedure" section
- Fill in title, domain, source, license checkbox, attribution

## Part 5: Editing & Revision Cycle

### Timeline: Submission to Publication

| Phase | Timeline | What happens |
|-------|----------|---|
| **Submission** | Day 0 | You submit GitHub issue with your procedure |
| **Initial review** | Days 1–2 | Maintainer fact-checks sources and reads for clarity |
| **Feedback** | Day 2 | Maintainer posts comments (GitHub issue or Google Doc) with any questions/changes needed |
| **Your revision** | Days 2–3 | You respond to feedback, make edits (24-hour window) |
| **Final check** | Day 3 | Maintainer verifies edits and converts to JSON-LD format |
| **Publication** | Day 4–5 | Content goes live on GitHub Pages; you receive link |
| **Attribution** | Day 5 | Your name appears on published page and on contributors.md |

**Fast path** (no major issues): 2–5 days from submission to publication  
**Standard path** (1–2 revisions): 5–7 days  
**Complex path** (multiple fact-checks needed): 7–10 days

## Part 6: Special Cases & Escalation Protocol

### Case 1: Multi-Source Procedure

If your procedure draws from multiple sources (e.g., WHO for general approach, CDC for specific dosing), list all sources in citations section. Procedure is OK to publish if sources do NOT contradict each other. Maintainer will verify no conflicts.

---

### Case 2: Regional Variation

If procedure varies by region (e.g., boiling time by elevation, or filter depth by climate), create a table with variations:

Example:

| Condition | Boiling time | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Sea level (0–2,000 ft) | 1 minute | Standard CDC recommendation |
| 2,000–6,500 ft | 2 minutes | Higher elevation = lower boiling point |
| Above 6,500 ft | 3 minutes | Air pressure significantly reduced |

---

### Case 3: Your Own Practice (No Published Source)

If you have verified personal practice (e.g., "I have boiled water for 15 years in my community"), you can submit. Maintainer will:
1. Ask clarifying questions: How many years? How many times? Any failures or successes to cite?
2. Request one corroborating source (doesn't have to match your exact method, but should support the approach)
3. Publish with attribution: "Based on contributor [name]'s verified practice, sourced from [corroborating publication]"

**Example**: "I've purified water by boiling for 20 years in wilderness contexts. This is also CDC-recommended (link)."

---

### Case 4: Hands-On Content Needing Photos

If you want to submit a photo-heavy procedure (e.g., showing each step of building a ceramic filter), you can attach images to your GitHub issue. Maintainer will:
1. Review images for clarity and safety
2. Get your permission to use images in published content (CC BY-SA 4.0)
3. Embed images in the published procedure

**Photo guidelines**:
- Clear, well-lit, in-focus
- Show the step being described
- Include any tools or materials visible
- Avoid personal/identifying info in background

---

### Case 5: Procedure Doesn't Meet Safety Standard (Decline + Opportunity)

**Trigger**: Maintainer finds procedure has factual error, inadequate safety warnings, or uses non-public-domain source.

**Decline notification**: Maintainer posts in GitHub issue with specific reason:

"Thank you for submitting! We're not able to publish this procedure as-is because:

[SPECIFIC REASON — e.g., 'Your source (Water Purification Basics by Smith, 2010) is commercially licensed and doesn't permit redistribution' OR 'The chlorine dosing table differs from CDC guidance and we couldn't verify your sources']

If you can [SPECIFIC FIX — e.g., 'find a public-domain alternative source' OR 'add CDC link to verify dosing'], we'd be happy to review a revised submission. We're open to revisions anytime."

**What happens next**:
1. You can revise and re-submit (same issue or new issue)
2. OR you can move on and submit a different procedure
3. No penalty; this is normal review process

## Part 8: FAQ for Contributors

**Q: Do I need a degree or certification to contribute?**  
A: No. You need hands-on experience with the procedure and an ability to explain it clearly. A parent who has canned food 50 times knows as much as a food scientist.

**Q: What if I can't find a perfect source for my procedure?**  
A: If you have verified personal practice, cite that plus one supportive publication. Maintainer will help verify.

**Q: How long should my procedure be?**  
A: 300–500 words (3 pages max). Quality > quantity. Clear 5-step procedure > vague 20-step procedure.

**Q: Can I write about [topic] that's not on the 8-topic list?**  
A: Wave 0 focuses on the 8 topics listed in WATER_SYSTEMS_WAVE_0_SPECIES_AND_TOPICS_SELECTION.md. If you have a related water topic, submit anyway and maintainer will assess fit. Rejections are not permanent—Wave 1 may have room for your topic.

**Q: What if my procedure has safety warnings?**  
A: Include them. Safety warnings are expected for water systems. More warnings = better.

**Q: Can I co-author with someone?**  
A: Yes. Both authors get credited. Coordinate with maintainer if you want shared authorship (both names listed, or one primary author).

**Q: What's CC BY-SA 4.0? Does it matter?**  
A: It means anyone can use, share, and modify your content as long as they credit you. No one can commercialize it without sharing. You keep full credit forever.

**Q: Can I change my mind about attribution after publishing?**  
A: Yes. Email maintainer and request attribution change. It will be updated within 48 hours.
