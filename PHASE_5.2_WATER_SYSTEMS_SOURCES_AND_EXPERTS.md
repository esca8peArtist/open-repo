# Phase 5.2 Water Systems — Sources & Expert Identification

**Purpose**: Authoritative sources and expert contacts for the four Water Systems content domains. Organized by domain and section for easy contributor reference. Includes peer-reviewed publications, government guidelines, NGO technical briefs, and identified practitioner experts reachable for quote/review.

**Usage**: Wave 0 maintainers and contributors use these sources to validate procedures, ground claims in evidence, and identify expert reviewers. All procedures must cite one or more of these sources; citations appear in the JSON-LD metadata and in human-readable procedure pages.

## Domain 1: Water Assessment & Testing

### Authoritative Sources by Section

#### Sections 1–2: Why Testing Matters & Understanding Water Hazards

**Peer-Reviewed & Academic**:
- WHO (2011). *Guidelines for Drinking-water Quality* (4th edition, updated 2017). Technical briefing on bacteriological/chemical/radiological thresholds. [https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/9789241549950] Free PDF.
- CDC (2023). *Drinking Water Health Series: Biological Contaminants*. Clear, field-friendly pathogen descriptions. [https://www.cdc.gov/water/] Downloadable.
- EPA (2023). *Drinking Water Standards and Health Advisories*. Authoritative US contaminant thresholds. [https://www.epa.gov/sites/production/files/2022-10/drinking_water_standards_and_health_advisories_final_combined.pdf] Free PDF.
- PMC/NCBI archives: Search "waterborne disease transmission" for peer-reviewed epidemiology. Several open-access papers on bacteria/parasite/virus transmission in resource-limited settings.

**NGO & Practitioner**:
- SPHERE Handbook (2018). *Water Supply, Sanitation and Hygiene Promotion* standard for humanitarian emergencies. [https://handbook.spherestandards.org/] Free online access. Authoritative for humanitarian contexts.
- IRC (International Water & Sanitation Centre). *Rapid Water Quality Assessment* guides. [https://www.ircwash.org/] Some freely available briefs.
- Practical Action. *Technical Briefs on Water and Sanitation* (CC-BY licensed). [https://practicalaction.org/knowledge-centre/technical-briefs/] Check each brief's license.

#### Sections 3–5: Visual Assessment, Commercial Kits & DIY Testing

**Authoritative Procedures**:
- WHO WASH Emergency Assessment Kit. [https://www.who.int/] Search "emergency assessment" for downloadable field worksheets.
- Aquachek Test Strips documentation. Each test kit includes interpretation charts; available from manufacturer website. Free downloadable PDFs.
- CDC/EPA *Drinking Water Quality Monitoring* documents. Step-by-step procedures for field testing. [https://www.cdc.gov/water/] or search EPA's water quality monitoring pages.
- USGS *Field Methods for Sediment and Associated Constituents*. Detailed procedures for turbidity, clarity, and basic water quality parameters. [https://water.usgs.gov/] Free technical manual.

**DIY/Low-Cost Methods**:
- WHO (2013). *Household Water Treatment and Safe Storage* manual. Includes procedures for boiling, settling, cloth filtration, chlorination using household materials. [https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/WHO-HTM-WSH-13.02] Free PDF.
- Practical Action. *Simple Water Testing Methods* (sourced in their briefs). Low-cost assessment approaches for resource-limited settings.
- Academic: Search PLOS One for "low-cost water quality testing" or "field methods water assessment" for peer-reviewed DIY approaches.

#### Sections 6–9: Context-Specific Testing & Quality Assurance

**Source-Specific Testing Protocols**:
- EPA *Groundwater Sampling* guide. [https://www.epa.gov/groundwater/ground-water-monitoring] Well water specific.
- USGS *Rainwater Sampling Methods*. [https://water.usgs.gov/] Technical guidance for roof/surface collection.
- CDC *Surface Water Testing After Flooding*. [https://www.cdc.gov/water/] Flood-specific assessment.

**Quality Assurance & Validation**:
- ISO 7027 (water turbidity measurement standards). Expensive standard document; educational versions available from university libraries.
- NIST (National Institute of Standards & Technology). Testing kit validation standards. [https://www.nist.gov/] Paywalled but may be available via interlibrary loan.

## Domain 2: Rainwater Harvesting & Storage

### Authoritative Sources by Section

#### Sections 1–2: Site Feasibility & Water Balance

**Design & Sizing Guidelines**:
- Texas A&M AgriLife Extension. *Rainwater Harvesting* technical note. [https://aggie-horticulture.tamu.edu/] Free downloads; includes worksheet for sizing calculation.
- IRRI (International Rice Research Institute). *Rainwater Harvesting Calculator* tool + documentation. [https://www.irri.org/] Free online tool; downloadable guides.
- ARID Center, University of Arizona. *Rainwater Harvesting for Drylands* (2 volumes). Free PDFs. [https://www.arizona.edu/]
- NRCS (Natural Resource Conservation Service). Rainwater harvesting guides by US region. [https://www.nrcs.usda.gov/] Free technical sheets.

#### Sections 3–4: Catchment & Tank Selection

**Materials & Performance**:
- Metal Roof Alliance. *Rainwater Harvesting Material Compatibility*. [https://www.metalroofing.com/] Technical data on roof material suitability.
- Plastic Tank Manufacturers Association (USA). Tank durability & material standards. Standards available but often paywalled.
- Academic: Search ResearchGate for "roofing material rainwater quality" for peer-reviewed comparisons.
- Rainwater Harvesting Supplies / Eco-Friendly Water Systems. Educational content on tank types (site-hosted PDFs). 

#### Sections 5–7: First-Flush, Filtration & Storage Quality

**Filtration & Sediment Control**:
- EPA *Rainwater Harvesting: System Design* manual. [https://www.epa.gov/] Explains first-flush, filtration, and maintenance.
- Slow Sand Filtration Association. Design principles. [https://www.ssfa.info/] Membership-based but has public resources.
- Academic: Journal of Water Supply: Research & Technology. Peer-reviewed papers on low-cost filtration. [Search via Google Scholar or university library]

**Storage & Water Quality Maintenance**:
- CDC *Household Water Treatment & Storage*. [https://www.cdc.gov/water/] Covers algae, bacterial growth, chlorination for potable storage.
- WHO *Household Water Treatment* (cited above in Domain 1). Same resource; chapter on storage applicable.
- Harvesting Rainwater for Household Use (2nd ed., by Gould & Nissen-Petersen, 1999). Classic textbook; available used or via library. Practical focus.

#### Sections 8–11: Potable Use, Sizing Examples, Legal Context & Installation

**Legality & Regulation**:
- State-specific rainwater harvesting fact sheets:
  - Texas: A&M Extension (above)
  - Colorado: Colorado State University Extension. [https://extension.colostate.edu/]
  - California: UC Davis Extension. [https://ucanr.edu/]
  - Arizona: U of Arizona Extension. [https://extension.arizona.edu/]
  - International: Australia Bureau of Meteorology has excellent rainwater guides (Australia has long rainwater harvesting history; many resources freely available)
- American Rainwater Catchment Systems Association (ARCSA). Standards & resources. [https://www.arcsa.org/] Some publicly available, some membership gated.

**Installation Procedures**:
- NRCS Conservation Practice Standards (CPSs) for rainwater systems. [https://www.nrcs.usda.gov/] Free technical drawings.
- DIY documentation: "How to Install Rainwater Harvesting Systems" videos/PDFs from Extension offices (TX, AZ, etc.). YouTube has peer-reviewed content from universities.

## Domain 3: Water Treatment & Purification

### Authoritative Sources by Section

#### Sections 1–4: Contamination, Boiling & Chemical Disinfection

**Core References — Universally Authoritative**:
- WHO (2006). *Boiling Water for Disinfection*. Concise technical note on time/temperature. [https://www.who.int/]
- CDC (2023). *Drinking Water Treatment Methods*. Boiling, chlorination, iodine procedures. [https://www.cdc.gov/water/]
- EPA *Water Treatment for Households*. [https://www.epa.gov/] Accessible language; approved procedures.
- Sphere Handbook (2018, cited in Domain 1). *Water Supply & Sanitation* section on treatment methods.

**Chlorination Dosing Tables**:
- WHO (2013). *Household Water Treatment* manual. Exact chlorine dose tables by water volume & turbidity. Free PDF.
- CDC *Bleach & Water Disinfection* guide. Clarifies household bleach concentration and dosing. [https://www.cdc.gov/water/]
- Army/Navy Field Sanitation Manual. Military water treatment procedures; highly practical and field-tested. Available free via military publication archives.

**Iodine & Alternatives**:
- WHO *Chemical Disinfection for Emergency Water Supply*. Iodine + potassium permanganate procedures. [https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/9241541500]
- USDA *Emergency Water Purification Methods* (from extension publications). Includes iodine, potassium permanganate.

#### Sections 5–7: Settling, Cloth Filtration & Sand Filtration

**Simple Methods — Academic Foundation**:
- Sato et al. (2013). "Cloth filters to improve water quality in low-income countries." *Journal of Water, Sanitation and Hygiene for Development*. [Available via Google Scholar; check university library access]
- Baker & Lipsky (2008). "The case for constructive confrontation." *Progress in Water Technology*, focusing on low-cost sand filtration. Academic but accessible.
- WHO *Household Water Treatment* (cited above). Chapter on sedimentation and cloth filtration.
- IRC *WASH Briefs on Low-Cost Filtration*. Practical, field-tested designs. [https://www.ircwash.org/]

**Slow Sand Filter Design**:
- Huisman & Wood (1974). *Slow Sand Filtration* (classic text, available used or via library; PDF scans may exist online).
- EPA *Slow Sand Filtration Design & Implementation*. Technical manual. [https://www.epa.gov/]
- Academic: Search journal "Water Research" or "Water Supply" for "slow sand filtration" peer-reviewed articles. 

#### Sections 8–11: Multi-Stage Treatment, Advanced Contaminants & Post-Treatment Testing

**Multi-Stage Approaches**:
- WHO (2006). *Guidelines for Drinking-Water Quality*. Discusses treatment trains (sequential methods).
- NRCS/EPA *Integrated Water Treatment Procedures*. [https://www.epa.gov/] Explains method sequencing.

**Chemical Contamination**:
- EPA *Drinking Water Contaminants* reference. Lists all regulated contaminants, testing methods, and remediation. [https://www.epa.gov/] Honest: most don't have simple field treatment.
- Academic: Journal "Environmental Science & Technology" — search "heavy metal removal" or "pesticide water treatment" for field-applicable methods.
- Activated charcoal effectiveness: Search "granular activated carbon water treatment" in peer-reviewed sources; mixed results but best option for some organics.

## General Sourcing Rules for All Domains

### Citation Format for Published Procedures
Each published procedure includes a "Sources" section with full citations (author, year, title, URL or publication). For government/WHO/CDC sources: link directly to downloadable PDF where possible. For paywalled academic: cite but note "requires university/library access." For online-only sources: include access date.

### Expert Review Process
When a practitioner or expert is invited for review:
1. Send procedure draft with specific question (e.g., "Is the chlorine dose accurate for this water type?")
2. Request 20-minute verbal feedback or written comments
3. Provide them with final attribution credit ("Reviewed by [Name], [Institution]")
4. Link their name to a GitHub profile or institution page (with permission)

### Open-Access Priority
When multiple sources exist, prioritize freely available sources (WHO, CDC, EPA, academic open-access repositories like PLOS, ResearchGate) over paywalled. For paywalled academic papers: note in procedure that users can request via interlibrary loan or directly from authors (most researchers will send PDFs on request).

### Building Offline Source Library
As Wave 0 progresses, maintain a local folder of downloadable PDFs (government guides, open-access papers, NGO briefs) organized by domain & section. This becomes the "source library" that remains accessible if links die.
