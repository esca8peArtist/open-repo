# Domain 10: Tools & Fabrication

> Tools are a force multiplier for every other domain. Without the right tools and the skills to use
> them, every problem costs more labor, more money, or remains unsolved. With them, you build your
> own water systems, repair your own equipment, fabricate the brackets and fittings that would
> otherwise require a store run, and keep every other system functional. The homesteader who cannot
> weld, sharpen a blade, or mill a board from a log is dependent on supply chains in ways that
> undermine the entire project of self-sufficiency.
>
> This chapter covers the complete toolset for off-grid self-reliance: hand tools, power tools,
> metalworking, woodworking, improvised fabrication, and field repair. It is opinionated. Quality
> matters — a cheap chisel that loses its edge in one use costs more than a good one. But "quality"
> does not always mean "expensive." Many of the best hand tools come from estate sales and farm
> auctions. The goal is a tool collection that will outlast you and can be passed down.
>
> **Philosophy**: Skill first, tools second. A skilled carpenter with mediocre tools outperforms a
> beginner with a $10,000 shop. Build competence in parallel with building your collection.

## Quick-Reference: Tool Acquisition by Phase

| Phase | Budget | Priority tools | Where to buy |
|---|---|---|---|
| Day 1 — land clearing, first structures | $500–$1,000 | Chainsaw, splitting maul, hand tools, shovel, wheelbarrow | New for chainsaw; estate sale for hand tools |
| Month 1–3 — building infrastructure | +$1,000–$2,000 | Cordless drill/driver, circular saw, angle grinder, come-along | New power tools; mid-grade brands |
| Month 3–12 — fabrication capable | +$2,000–$4,000 | Welder (MIG/stick), table saw or miter saw, sharpening system | New or used |
| Year 2+ — advanced | +$3,000–$8,000 | Forge, lathe, bandsaw mill, pneumatic tools | Auction, used market, or new |

The first $500 spent on high-quality hand tools and a reliable chainsaw will accomplish more than $5,000 in specialized equipment you don't yet know how to use.

## 10.1 Why Tools Matter: The Force Multiplier

### 10.1.1 Tool Infrastructure as Capability

Every domain in this guide depends on tools. Water systems require pipe cutting, threading, and repair. Energy systems need wire crimping, conduit work, and battery terminal maintenance. Food production needs sharpened blades, tillage tools, and irrigation infrastructure. Shelter requires framing, cutting, fastening. The absence of a single critical tool at the wrong moment can stop all progress.

**Force multiplier math:**
- A dull hoe requires 3× the effort of a sharp one. Sharpening takes 5 minutes.
- A hand drill (brace and bit) makes holes in wood. A cordless drill makes 50 holes in the same time.
- A come-along can move 3 tons of material that would otherwise require a crew.
- A MIG welder repairs a broken trailer hitch in 20 minutes vs. buying a replacement or waiting for a welder to arrive.

The key insight: tools compress labor and extend capability. Off-grid life is labor-intensive by nature. Tools that multiply your effective labor hours are the best investment you can make.

### 10.1.2 Capability Loss Scenarios

**Chainsaw failure** (most common single-tool failure on a homestead):
- Consequence: No firewood, no land clearing, no lumber milling
- Recovery without backup: hand bucksaw for small material; very slow
- Prevention: spare chain, spark plug, air filter, fuel line, carburetor rebuild kit; know how to sharpen and tension a chain

**Drill/driver battery failure**:
- Consequence: All fastening reverts to manual screwdrivers or hand-driven nails
- Recovery: brace and bit for holes; hammers and nails for fastening
- Prevention: keep 2+ batteries per platform; maintain manual equivalents

**Welder failure**:
- Consequence: Cannot repair broken metal equipment; cannot fabricate
- Recovery: cold connections (bolting, riveting, drilling); far more time-intensive
- Prevention: stock welding consumables; have a backup stick welder alongside MIG (stick welders are simpler and more field-repairable)

**No sharpening system**:
- Consequence: All cutting tools — axes, chisels, hoes, saws — become progressively less effective and more dangerous
- Recovery: none until replacement stones are sourced
- Prevention: a complete sharpening kit is $50–$150 and lasts decades; this is a non-negotiable day-one purchase

### 10.1.3 Tool vs. Skill Balance

There is a temptation to buy capability before building it. The result is a shop full of underused tools and a person who cannot perform basic repairs. The correct sequence:

1. Acquire minimum necessary tools for the immediate phase
2. Use them until you hit their limits
3. Identify the skill or tool gap that is the binding constraint
4. Fill that gap — skill first if possible, tool second if necessary

A person who has spent 200 hours with hand tools has body knowledge that makes power tool use safer and more effective. Do not skip the hand tool phase.

## 10.2 Hand Tools

Hand tools are the backbone of off-grid tooling. They require no power, can be repaired or improvised, and the best vintage examples are often superior to new production. A complete hand tool kit can be assembled for $300–$600 through estate sales and farm auctions at a quality level that would cost $1,500–$3,000 new.

### 10.2.2 Cutting Tools

#### Handsaws

Modern handsaws are either "hardpoint" (inexpensive, not sharpenable, throw away when dull) or traditional (filed teeth, sharpenable, last indefinitely). For off-grid use, traditional saws are preferable long-term but require learning to sharpen. Hardpoint saws (Bahco, Irwin, Stanley) are acceptable workhorses that can be replaced cheaply.

**Handsaw types:**

| Type | TPI | Application | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Rip saw | 4–7 TPI | Cutting with the grain (making boards from planks) | Vintage Disston or Atkins 7 TPI |
| Crosscut saw | 8–12 TPI | Cutting across the grain (dimensional cuts) | Bahco 244 (hardpoint), or vintage Disston |
| Panel saw | 10–12 TPI | General carpentry, sheet goods | Stanley FatMax or Irwin 15-inch |
| Japanese pull saw | 14–22 TPI | Fine work, tight cuts; cuts on pull stroke | Suizan or Gyokucho; $25–$60 |
| Two-man crosscut saw | 4–5 TPI | Felling and bucking large timber without a chainsaw | 5–7 foot vintage; $50–$200 at auction |
| Coping saw | 15–17 TPI | Curves, cutting out shapes | Bahco 301 ($15–$25) |
| Hacksaw | 18–32 TPI | Cutting metal | Stanley or Lenox; 24 TPI for most metal work |
| Keyhole/jab saw | 6–8 TPI | Starting cuts in the middle of a panel | Any; $10–$20 |

**Saw sharpening** (traditional saws only):
Requires: triangular taper file (size matched to TPI), saw vise or improvised clamp, optional saw set tool.
Process: Joint the teeth (flat file across tops to make all equal height) → Set the teeth (bend alternating teeth for kerf clearance) → File each tooth to a sharp point at the correct angle. Crosscut: 60°–65° rake, 12°–15° fleam. Rip: 0° rake, 0° fleam (chisel-like).
Learning time: 2–4 hours for basic competence; 20 hours for consistent results.

#### Chisels

Chisels are among the most important hand tools and among the most abused. A dull chisel is dangerous — it requires excessive force and wanders unpredictably.

**Chisel sets:**

| Tier | Brand | Price | Quality |
|---|---|---|---|
| Budget/acceptable | Narex (Czech) | $50–$80/set of 4 | Excellent value; good steel, holds edge well |
| Mid-range | Two Cherries (Germany) or Stanley 750 | $80–$150/set | Reliable workhorses |
| Lifetime quality | Lie-Nielsen, Veritas, or vintage Stanley | $150–$400/set | Heirlooms; buy vintage Stanley on eBay for $30–$80/set |

**Set composition**: 1/4", 1/2", 3/4", 1" covers 90% of joinery. Add 1/8" and 1-1/4" for a full set.

**Chisel types:**
- Bench chisel: Standard work; beveled back for paring
- Mortise chisel: Thick, stiff; driving into wood to create mortises
- Paring chisel: Long, thin; refining cuts without mallet
- Corner chisel: L-shaped; squaring corners on router cuts

#### Drawknives and Spokeshaves

**Drawknife**: Two-handled blade drawn toward the user. For debarking logs, shaping handles, rough shaping of curved work. Essential for anyone working green wood or milling their own lumber. Vintage drawknives from flea markets ($15–$40) are usually excellent quality. Modern options: Flexcut ($60–$100), Mora ($50–$80).

**Spokeshave**: Small plane with handles on both sides; for shaping curved surfaces. Flat-sole for convex curves; curved-sole for concave. Stanley 151 (flat) and 151R (round): $30–$50 each, widely available vintage.

#### Axes

No homestead tool receives more abuse or is more critical than the axe. A quality axe, properly maintained, lasts generations.

| Tool | Head weight | Use | Recommendation | Price |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Felling axe | 3–4 lb | Felling trees, limbing | Gransfors Bruks Forest Axe or Council Tool | $120–$180 / $60–$90 |
| Splitting maul | 6–8 lb | Splitting firewood | Fiskars X27 or Council Tool 8 lb | $50–$80 / $40–$60 |
| Hudson Bay axe | 2–2.5 lb | Camping, trail work, small limbing | Gransfors Bruks Small Forest or Husqvarna | $140–$160 / $40–$60 |
| Hatchet | 1–1.5 lb | Camp kitchen, kindling, close work | Estwing E24A or Gransfors Bruks | $40–$60 / $130–$150 |
| Adze | 3–5 lb | Hewing flat surfaces on logs, timber framing | Vintage carpenter's adze; new Ochsenkopf | $40–$80 vintage / $150–$250 new |
| Broad axe | 5–8 lb | Hewing round logs to flat timber | Vintage; nearly impossible to buy new | $50–$200 at auction |

**Axe maintenance:**
- *Sharpening*: Bastard file (10-inch mill file) to restore bevel; follow with diamond stone (200-grit → 400-grit); strop on leather to polish. Bevel angle: 20°–25° for splitting (more obtuse is fine), 15°–20° for felling. Never use a bench grinder — overheats and destroys the temper.
- *Handle replacement*: Hickory handles are standard and correct. Fiberglass is more crack-resistant but transmits vibration (fatiguing). Hang the head with a wooden wedge driven perpendicular to the grain direction, followed by an expansion wedge at 90°. A properly hung head is not removable without effort.
- *Storage*: Hang or store with blade covered. Oil metal with a thin coat of linseed oil. Do not leave head embedded in a log — moisture swells the wood and loosens the hang.

**Scythe:** The most underrated farm tool for large-scale production. An experienced scythe user can mow 1/4–1/2 acre per hour. This is not nostalgia — in many contexts (wet conditions, rough terrain, around obstacles) a scythe outperforms a string trimmer and requires no fuel. **Broadfork:** A two-handled fork with 8–12-inch tines, used to aerate and loosen soil without inverting it. Essential for no-till gardening. Replaces mechanical tillage for bed prep. Meadow Creature is the best domestic brand.

**Sharpening angles by tool type:**

| Tool | Bevel angle | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Bench chisel (hardwood work) | 25° bevel, 30° micro-bevel | Micro-bevel optional but extends time between full sharpenings |
| Bench chisel (softwood) | 20–25° bevel | Lower angle cuts cleaner in pine |
| Plane blade | 25° primary, 30° micro-bevel | Same logic as chisel |
| Drawknife | 20–25° | Single-bevel; sharpen the bevel side only |
| Carving knives | 15–20° per side | Lower angle than kitchen knives |
| Kitchen knives | 15–20° per side | Use diamond plates + strop |
| Axes (felling) | 20–25° per side | More acute = sharper but fragile; use for clean wood |
| Axes (splitting) | 30–35° | Wedge geometry; doesn't need to be sharp, just smooth |
| Hoes and cultivators | 45°–60° | Approximate; use file and mill file |
| Scythe | 5–10° per side (very acute) | Thin edge is essential; peen + whetstone system |
| Drawknife | 20–25° | Bevel side only |

**Progression for chisels and plane blades:**
1. If edge is chipped or significantly worn: DMT coarse diamond until flat
2. DMT medium diamond to refine
3. Arkansas hard stone until edge is refined
4. Arkansas translucent or waterstrone 6000 until scratch pattern is fine
5. Strop with chromium oxide compound 20–30 passes per side
6. Test on end grain pine — clean shavings with zero tearing = sharp

Total sharpening time per chisel from scratch: 10–20 minutes. Touch-up (steps 4–6 only) after each use session: 2–3 minutes. This is not a time burden — it is the cost of owning cutting tools.

## 10.3 Power Tools

### 10.3.1 Off-Grid Power Tool Strategy

Power tools on an off-grid homestead require a different calculus than in a grid-connected shop:

- **Cordless tools** are the primary platform: no cord management, work anywhere, charge from solar system
- **Corded tools** are still valuable for stationary applications (table saw, planer) when a generator is available or when inverter capacity allows
- **Pneumatic tools** are excellent for high-volume fastening (framing, roofing) if you already have a compressor

The single most important decision: **pick one battery platform and stick to it.** Cross-platform compatibility is limited. Mixing Milwaukee and DeWalt means you need separate chargers and cannot share batteries in the field.

**Battery platform comparison (18V/20V class):**

| Brand | Platform | Interoperability | Battery capacity | Reputation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Milwaukee M18 | M18 (18V) | Large ecosystem; FUEL line is brushless (preferred) | 2.0 Ah–12.0 Ah | Best power tools; most complete ecosystem; US-based engineering |
| DeWalt 20V MAX | 20V MAX (nominal, 18V actual) | FlexVolt bridges 20V and 60V tools | 1.5 Ah–9.0 Ah | Strong ecosystem; widespread availability; OEM cross-compatibility with some tools |
| Makita 18V LXT | 18V LXT | Largest 18V ecosystem by SKU count; also 40V XGT | 1.5 Ah–6.0 Ah | Best battery technology; lower power output than Milwaukee FUEL |
| Ryobi ONE+ | 18V ONE+ | Widest variety including outdoor power | 1.3 Ah–6.0 Ah | Budget tier; acceptable for light use; not for daily heavy use |

**Recommendation for off-grid**: Milwaukee M18 FUEL for primary; supplement with Milwaukee M12 for compact tools. The FUEL (brushless) line delivers longer runtime, higher torque, and better durability in demanding conditions. If budget is tight, DeWalt 20V MAX is a close second with excellent parts availability.

**Battery purchasing strategy:**
- Do not buy kits with batteries you don't need — buy bare tools and add batteries strategically
- Purchase 5.0 Ah or 8.0 Ah batteries for high-drain tools (grinder, circular saw)
- Purchase 2.0 Ah batteries for light tools (jigsaw, drill)
- A starter kit: 2× 5.0 Ah batteries + dual charger is the most efficient first battery purchase ($150–$200)

**Runtime estimates (Milwaukee M18 FUEL, 5.0 Ah battery):**
- Circular saw: 80–120 cuts in 2×4 lumber per charge
- Impact driver: 200–300 screws (2" deck screw) per charge
- Angle grinder: 15–25 minutes continuous grinding per charge
- Reciprocating saw: 30–60 minutes light cutting per charge

**Solar charging of cordless batteries:**
Standard M18 and DeWalt 20V chargers require 120V AC. Charging options from a solar/battery system:
- Through an inverter (most common): 500W+ inverter sufficient; charger draws 150–200W
- DC-DC charger adapters exist for Milwaukee M18 (third-party; search "M18 12V charger") — charge from 12V battery bank directly, bypassing inverter losses (~5–8% efficiency gain)
- Charge during peak solar hours to avoid pulling from battery bank at night

**Table saw recommendation for off-grid:**

The rolling stand is essential for solo use. Limitation: less power than contractor or cabinet saw — do not push it through wet or thick hardwood.

**Generator requirements:**

## 10.4 Metalworking

Metalworking capability is what separates a homestead that limps along patching problems from one that is genuinely self-sufficient. Broken trailer hitch, cracked plow frame, failed bracket on the solar mount — all are solved in minutes by someone who welds vs. weeks of waiting for a replacement or fabricator.

### 10.4.1 Welding Processes Overview

| Process | Skill level | Portability | Power requirements | Applications |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| MIG (GMAW) | Beginner-friendly | Moderate; needs shielding gas | 120V or 240V | General fabrication, repair, sheet metal |
| Stick (SMAW) | Moderate | High; DC inverter runs from generator | 120V or 240V | Structural, field repair, dirty/rusty metal |
| TIG (GTAW) | Advanced | Moderate | 240V typically | Precision, thin material, stainless, aluminum |
| Oxy-acetylene | Moderate | High (tank-based) | None (bottled gas) | Brazing, cutting, heating, antique-style fabrication |
| Flux-core (FCAW) | Beginner-friendly | High; no shielding gas needed | 120V or 240V | Outdoor use, dirty/painted metal |

**For off-grid use, priority order:**
1. **Stick welding** — most resilient; DC inverter welders run from 120V (small units) or 240V; no shielding gas to run out of; handles dirty and rusty metal; most field-repairable; excellent for structural work
2. **MIG welding** — fastest, cleanest welds; ideal for fabrication; requires CO2/Argon shielding gas (regulator + cylinder); less forgiving on dirty metal
3. **Oxy-acetylene** — unique capabilities (brazing, cutting, heating metal for bending); completely independent of electricity; tanks are a long-term consumable

Having both a stick and MIG welder covers all practical needs.

### 10.4.3 Welding Safety

- **UV radiation**: Welding arc produces UV light that causes arc eye (photokeratitis) — extremely painful, like a sunburn on the corneas. Never look at the arc without a proper welding helmet. Auto-darkening helmets (Lincoln Viking 3350 or Miller Digital Elite: $200–$350) are the recommended standard.
- **Welding fumes**: Metal fumes cause serious respiratory harm. Galvanized metal releases zinc fumes (toxic), painted metal releases various toxins. Weld outdoors or with mechanical ventilation. Wear P100 or organic vapor respirator for galvanized/coated metals.
- **Fire**: Spatter travels 20–30 feet. Clear combustible materials from the area. Keep a fire extinguisher within reach. Never weld on or near fuel tanks, even "empty" ones — vapor remains.
- **Electrical safety**: Do not weld in rain or standing water. Keep cables in good condition. The work clamp must be near the weld, not 20 feet away on the opposite end of a vehicle.
- **PPE required**: Auto-darkening helmet, leather gloves, leather jacket or sleeves, leather boots. No synthetic clothing — nylon and polyester melt and adhere to skin.

### 10.4.4 Angle Grinder Work

The angle grinder is the most versatile metalworking tool short of a welder. With different discs, it cuts, grinds, finishes, removes rust, and sharpens.

**Disc types:**

| Disc type | Application | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Cutting disc (Type 1, 1/16") | Cutting rebar, pipe, bolt heads | Thin = fast cuts; never use for grinding |
| Grinding disc (Type 27, 1/4") | Removing weld spatter, shaping, beveling | Thick = pressure work; not for cutting |
| Flap disc (40–120 grit) | Surface preparation, weld finishing | Aggressive removal to fine finish; more forgiving than grinding disc |
| Wire wheel (knotted) | Rust and paint removal | Very aggressive; for steel only |
| Wire cup (knotted) | Rust removal in bowls, pipes | Same; for tight spots |
| Strip disc (Scotch-Brite type) | Surface prep without metal removal | For final cleanup before painting |
| Carving disc | Wood carving and shaping | Carbide or chain; creates concave profiles |

**Grinder selection**: 4-1/2" (Milwaukee 2780-20 cordless or Dewalt DWE402 corded) covers 90% of tasks. 5" and 7" grinders are for heavier work — structural steel, large welds. Corded grinders have no runtime limit; use cordless for field work.

**Safety**: Angle grinders are responsible for more serious shop injuries than any other tool. Never remove the guard. Always use the side handle. Keep the wheel perpendicular to the work when cutting — lateral force causes wheel shattering. Wear a face shield over safety glasses (not just glasses — grinding discs fail catastrophically). Wear leather gloves, long sleeves.

### 10.4.5 Cold Connections

When welding is not available or appropriate, mechanical fastening can achieve structural results:

**Bolted connections:**
- Grade 2 bolts: General use, non-structural (galvanized hardware store bolts)
- Grade 5 bolts: Structural connections; common in automotive and machinery repair
- Grade 8 bolts: High-strength; critical structural applications (yellow chromate finish, typically)
- Metric equivalent: Class 4.6 (Grade 2), 8.8 (Grade 5), 10.9 (Grade 8)
- Use proper torque specs; dry threads vs. lubricated have different torque requirements
- Lock washers or Loctite (blue = removable, red = permanent) prevent loosening from vibration

**Drilling and tapping:**
- Tap drill sizes: 75% thread engagement is standard; charts widely available
- Common taps: 1/4"-20, 5/16"-18, 3/8"-16 (SAE); M6, M8, M10 (metric)
- Cutting fluid is essential for tapping steel — use Tap Magic, WD-40, or dark thread cutting oil
- Broken taps are difficult to remove; do not force

**Riveting:**
- Pop rivets (blind rivets): Easy, one-side access, adequate for non-structural sheet metal
- Solid rivets: Stronger; require bucking bar; used in aircraft and historic structural work
- Monel rivets: For stainless and marine applications

## 10.5 Woodworking

### 10.5.1 Milling Your Own Lumber

A standing dead tree on your property is worth $200–$1,000 in lumber if you can mill it. A mature walnut or white oak log is worth far more. Milling capability converts a resource (trees) into a material (lumber) and eliminates one of the highest homestead material costs.

**Chainsaw mills:**

| Type | Manufacturer | Setup | Width (max) | Price | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Granberg Alaskan Mark IV | Granberg | Bar-mounted jig | Up to 36" wide | $220–$280 | Most popular chainsaw mill; mounts to chainsaw bar |
| Haddon Lumbermaker | Haddon | Similar to Granberg | Up to 24" wide | $100–$150 | Simpler; slightly less accurate |
| Peterson Swingmill (chainsaw version) | Peterson | More complex setup | Up to 36" | $400–$600 | More accurate; wider material possible |

**Chainsaw mill operation:**
1. Fell and delimb the log; cut to desired bolt length (8, 10, 12, or 16 feet)
2. Establish a flat reference surface: screw a straight 2×4 or aluminum ladder section to the top of the log as a guide rail for the first slab cut
3. Mount chainsaw mill to bar; set slab thickness
4. Cut the first "cant" (flattened log)
5. Flip and use the flat side as reference for all subsequent cuts
6. Board-foot output per hour: 20–50 BF/hour depending on species, log diameter, and operator experience
7. Chainsaw requirement: minimum 50cc, ideally 60–90cc for logs over 16" diameter; use ripping chain (lower-angle teeth: 5°–10° vs. standard 30°–35°)

**Chainsaw ripping chain:** Granberg G729 or Oregon square-ground chain. Cuts parallel to grain much more efficiently than standard chain. Required for acceptable mill performance.

**Portable band sawmill:**

| Brand | Model | Max log width | Power | Price |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Wood-Mizer | LT15 (manual) | 28" | No engine (manual) | $3,000–$4,500 |
| Wood-Mizer | LT35 (gas engine) | 30" | 23 HP gas | $8,000–$12,000 |
| TimberKing | 1400 | 24" | 15 HP | $5,000–$7,000 |
| Norwood | LumberMate LM29 | 29" | Electric or gas | $3,000–$6,000 |
| Hudson | Oscar 328 | 32" | Electric or gas | $2,500–$5,000 |

**Band sawmill advantages over chainsaw mill**: Much less kerf loss (1/16" vs. 1/4"–3/8"), smoother surface, faster cutting rate, less operator fatigue. Disadvantages: higher initial cost, cannot be brought to the log (log must come to the mill). For a serious homestead producing significant lumber, a portable band sawmill pays for itself in 2–4 years of production.

**Lumber drying:**

| Method | Time | Requirements | Quality | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Air drying (stickered) | 1 year per inch of thickness | Stickers (1" × 1" × board width), shade, airflow | Adequate; slight warp risk | Stack boards with 1" stickers between each layer; weight the top |
| Solar kiln (DIY) | 4–8 weeks per inch | Greenhouse material, fan, thermometer | Good | Plans from Virginia Tech Extension (free online) |
| Dehumidifier kiln | 2–4 weeks per inch | Insulated room, dehumidifier, fan | Excellent | Best for small batches; achieves 6–8% MC |
| Commercial kiln | 1–2 weeks per inch | Hired out | Excellent | $0.10–$0.25 per BF; worthwhile for valuable species |

**Target moisture content (MC) by application:**
- Exterior furniture or decking: 12–15% MC
- Interior furniture: 6–8% MC
- Framing lumber: 19% MC maximum (building code); 15% preferred
- Flooring: 6–9% MC

**Moisture meter**: Essential tool; $25–$150 (Calculated Industries or General Tools). Pin-type is most accurate for lumber.

**Board-foot calculation:**
Board-feet (BF) = (Thickness in inches × Width in inches × Length in feet) ÷ 12

Example: 8/4 (2") × 8" × 10' = (2 × 8 × 10) ÷ 12 = 13.33 BF

At $3–$5 per BF for rough hardwood at a sawmill, that 10-foot log section is worth $40–$66 in material.

### 10.5.2 Hand Tool Joinery

The ability to cut joints by hand eliminates dependence on expensive machinery and produces furniture and structures that can be repaired anywhere.

**Essential joints:**

| Joint | Strength | Applications | Difficulty |

## 10.6 Improvised Fabrication

### 10.6.1 Aluminum Casting (Lost-Foam)

Lost-foam casting is the most accessible metal casting method for a homesteader. You carve a pattern from foam (EPS — expanded polystyrene), bury it in dry compacted sand, pour molten aluminum over it; the foam vaporizes instantly and is replaced by the casting.

**Equipment:**
- Furnace: Propane foundry furnace (Lindberg or homemade from steel pail + ceramic fiber blanket + propane burner); must reach 1,400°F (760°C) for aluminum
- DIY furnace cost: $80–$150 in materials (steel pail + Kaowool + Reil burner)
- Crucible: #6 silicon carbide/graphite crucible ($25–$50); or a cut-down stainless steel pipe section (temporary)
- Sand: Dry fine silica sand (play sand works); do not use wet sand — steam explosion risk
- Foam: EPS foam (Styrofoam) packing material or foam board

**Process:**
1. Carve or hot-wire cut the foam pattern to desired shape; attach sprue (foam rod for pour path) and risers (for gas escape)
2. Place foam in wooden flask or open-top steel box; pack dry sand around it firmly
3. Melt aluminum in crucible; common stock: aluminum cans, automotive pistons, wheels (clean, no oil or paint)
4. Skim dross from surface with steel rod; pour steadily into sprue
5. Wait 10–15 minutes; unbury casting; remove sprue; clean up with file and grinder
6. Aluminum melting point: 1,220°F (660°C); keep furnace above 1,350°F for good pour temp

**Safety**: Molten aluminum reacts violently with water and moisture. Never pour wet sand; never pour near water; dry all tools before contact with melt. Wear full face shield, leather gloves, leather apron, leather boots. A single drop of water in a crucible of molten aluminum can cause an explosion.

**Useful castings**: Replacement pulley hubs, custom brackets, hinges, bushing blanks, weights, fishing jigs, custom handles, tool parts.

### 10.6.2 Concrete Casting

Concrete is the most useful casting material for a homestead: steps, drainage blocks, fence post bases, water tanks, washboards, countertops, tool holders.

**Mix ratios:**

| Application | Cement:Sand:Aggregate ratio | Water:Cement | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Structural concrete | 1:2:3 | 0.45–0.50 | Standard; keep water:cement ratio low for strength |
| High-strength (footings, posts) | 1:1.5:2.5 | 0.40–0.45 | Less water = stronger but harder to work |
| Mortar (no aggregate) | 1:3:0 | 0.50–0.60 | For brickwork, stucco, patching |
| Slump/poured (flowable) | 1:2:3 + superplasticizer | 0.40 | Use plasticizer to improve workability without adding water |
| Fiber-reinforced | 1:2:3 + 1 lb polypropylene fiber per yard | Standard | Reduces cracking; not a substitute for rebar |

**28-day compressive strength by w/c ratio:**
- w/c 0.40: ~5,500 PSI
- w/c 0.50: ~4,000 PSI
- w/c 0.60: ~3,000 PSI
- w/c 0.70: ~2,000 PSI

**Rebar sizing:**
- #3 rebar (3/8"): Light slabs, thin walls
- #4 rebar (1/2"): Standard slabs, walls, footings
- #5 rebar (5/8"): Heavy structural; retaining walls
- Rule of thumb: rebar covers the bottom 1/3 of a slab, centered side-to-side; minimum 1.5" concrete cover on all sides

**Curing:** Concrete gains strength by hydration, not drying. Keep wet and covered for 7–28 days. Covering with plastic and wet burlap is standard. In cold weather (below 40°F): use insulated blankets; heat if possible; don't allow freezing for first 24 hours.

### 10.6.3 Plastic Welding and Forming

Plastic welding repairs HDPE tanks, AV body panels, tool housings, irrigation fittings, and vehicle bumpers.

**Plastic identification:**

| Plastic | Symbol | Temperature | Behavior | Common applications |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| HDPE | #2 | ~275°F melt | Waxy; good chemical resistance; weldable | Water tanks, pipe, cutting boards |
| LDPE | #4 | ~220°F melt | Flexible; weldable | Squeeze bottles, tarps, bags |
| PP (polypropylene) | #5 | ~320°F melt | Harder; weldable; automotive trim | Containers, car bumpers, battery cases |
| ABS | (none on most) | ~400°F melt | Rigid; weldable; bonds to itself with acetone | Electronics housings, automotive |
| PVC | #3 | ~340°F soften | Weldable; releases HCl fumes when burned | Pipe, sheet, profiles |
| Polycarbonate | #7 | ~300°F soften | Very strong; scratch prone | Lexan/safety glazing |

**Plastic welding with heat gun:**
1. Identify plastic type (check recycling symbol or burn test — HDPE/PP produce white smoke and drip; ABS produces black smoke and chars; PVC releases acrid chlorine smell — avoid burning PVC)
2. Obtain welding rod of matching plastic (HDPE rod from supplier; or strip from same material source)
3. V-groove the crack or joint with a utility knife or rotary tool
4. Heat gun to appropriate temperature for the plastic; preheat joint area
5. Feed rod while heating joint simultaneously; rod should flow into groove without bubbling
6. Allow to cool without moving; then file or sand flush

**Heat gun**: Milwaukee 2688-20 cordless ($80–$120 bare) or DeWalt D26950 corded ($60–$80). Variable temperature is important for plastic work.

### 10.6.4 Rope and Cordage

Understanding rope is essential for rigging, lifting, tying loads, and making field repairs.

**Rope types:**

| Material | Strength | Stretch | UV resistance | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Manila (natural fiber) | Low | Low | Poor | Cheap; biodegradable; gets weaker when wet |
| Sisal | Very low | Very low | Poor | Interior use only |
| Nylon | High | High (25–30%) | Good | Best for shock absorption (anchor lines, tie-downs) |
| Polyester (Dacron) | High | Low | Very good | Best general use rope; minimal stretch |
| Polypropylene | Moderate | Moderate | Poor | Floats; cheap; degrades in sunlight |
| Dyneema/Spectra | Very high | Near zero | Good | Extremely strong; used in rigging; expensive |
| Paracord (550 cord) | 550 lb | Moderate | Moderate | Inner strands useful; outer sheath for lashing |

**Load ratings**: Always derate rope by 5:1 from published breaking strength for working load. A rope rated at 5,000 lb breaking strength = 1,000 lb safe working load.

**Essential splices:**
- *Eye splice* (braided or three-strand): Creates a permanent loop at the end of a rope. For shackles, hooks, cleats. Stronger than a knot in the same rope.
- *Back splice* (three-strand): Permanent end finish; prevents unraveling; slightly thicker than the rope diameter.
- *Short splice*: Joining two ropes end-to-end; maintains most of the rope's strength.

**Essential knots for a homestead:**
- Bowline: Non-slip loop; the most important knot; holds under load, releases easily
- Clove hitch: Temporary attachment to posts and trees; adjustable
- Round turn and two half hitches: Secure attachment with load
- Truckers hitch: Mechanical advantage for tensioning; essential for securing loads
- Sheet bend: Joining two ropes of different diameter
- Square lashing: Joining two poles at right angles (scaffolding, structures)
- Diagonal lashing: Preventing movement in opposing directions

### 10.6.5 Leather Work

Leather is one of the most durable and repair-friendly materials available. Good leather harness, belting, and boot leather lasts decades with proper care and can be repaired repeatedly.

**Tools:**

| Tool | Use | Price |
|---|---|---|
| Stitching awl | Punching holes and sewing heavy leather | $10–$20 |
| Saddle stitching needles (harness needles) | Two-needle hand sewing | $5–$15 |
| Linen thread (waxed) | Hand stitching; far more durable than polyester in leather | $8–$20 |
| Leather hole punch (rotary) | Punching clean holes for buckles and rivets | $15–$30 |
| Wing dividers / stitching groover | Marking stitch lines | $10–$30 |
| Skiver | Thinning edges for folds | $10–$25 |
| Edge beveler | Beveling cut edges to prevent fraying | $10–$20 |
| Swivel knife | Carving decorative lines | $20–$50 |
| Leather mallet | Driving tools without marking leather | $15–$30 |

**Saddle stitching technique:**
Two needles, one thread. Thread goes through each hole twice from opposite sides, forming a lock stitch that will not unravel if individual stitches break. Far superior to machine sewing (single-needle chain stitch) for durability. Use 1 thread length = 4× the stitch length to cut.

**Boot resoling:** Remove old sole (heat with heat gun to soften adhesive), roughen both surfaces with sandpaper, apply contact cement (Barge Cement: $15/quart), let tack, press firmly. For heavy-use boots, saddle-stitch through the welt after gluing. Leather soles: order from Springfield Leather ($20–$40/pair). Vibram rubber outsoles from Vibram directly or cobbler suppliers.

**Leather conditioning:**
- Neatsfoot oil: Best for harness and outdoor leather; penetrates and restores suppleness
- Beeswax (pure): Water resistance; for boots and exterior use
- Lanolin: Excellent; from wool; penetrating; food-safe
- Saddle soap: Cleaning only; follow with conditioner
- Avoid petroleum products (mineral oil, Vaseline) on leather — degrades fiber over time

### 10.6.6 Clay and Ceramics

Functional pottery is achievable without a potter's wheel or kiln. Pit-fired coil-built pottery has been used for 10,000 years to make water vessels, cooking pots, crocks, and storage containers.

**Hand-building techniques:**
- *Coil building*: Roll clay into ropes, stack in circles, smooth with fingers or wooden tool. Achieves any shape; thin walls possible. Best technique for off-grid
- *Slab building*: Roll clay flat, cut shapes, join with slip (watery clay). Good for angular forms
- *Pinch pot*: Push thumb into ball, pinch walls thin. Excellent for small bowls

**Clay sourcing:**
- Wild clay: Found in stream cuts, road cuts, construction sites. Test: roll ball, drop from 4 feet — shatters = too lean (add clay); deforms without cracking = too fat (add sand)
- Shrinkage test: Roll a 10-cm slab, allow to dry; shrinkage should be 5–15%
- Commercial clay: Cone 06 earthenware for pit firing; cone 10 for stoneware (high-fire kiln)

**Pit firing:**
1. Build and dry pots thoroughly (2+ weeks); any moisture in the clay = explosive steam failure in firing
2. Dig pit 2 feet deep × 2 feet wide, or use a steel barrel with holes
3. Layer: wood, pots, wood, pots, wood; cover
4. Fire slowly; reach 1,000–1,200°F over 2–3 hours
5. Allow to cool slowly (12+ hours); unburried pots are fragile — handle gently until fully cool
6. Pit-fired pottery is earthenware (porous); not suitable for water storage without a sealer (beeswax seals water vessels adequately)

**Slip glazing for water resistance:** Apply slip (watery clay) to surface before firing; produces a partial seal. Terra sigillata (polished slip) with burnishing creates a smoother, less porous surface for earthenware.

## 10.7 Improvised Repairs

### 10.7.1 Small Engine Maintenance

Small engines (generators, chainsaws, tillers, pumps) are among the most commonly failed equipment on a homestead and among the most straightforward to repair.

**Standard maintenance schedule:**

| Component | Interval | Service |
|---|---|---|
| Air filter (paper/foam) | 25–50 hours or annually | Clean or replace |
| Spark plug | 100 hours or annually | Inspect gap, clean, replace if worn |
| Oil (4-stroke) | 25–50 hours | Change; use SAE 30 for warm weather, 5W-30 for cold |
| Fuel filter | Annually or as needed | Replace; cheap insurance |
| Carburetor cleaning | When engine runs rough, surges, or won't idle | See below |
| Valve adjustment (4-stroke) | Every 100–200 hours | Requires feeler gauge; often skipped until engine performance drops |
| Belts | Annually (inspect), replace when cracked or glazed | Keep spares for all belts |

**Carburetor cleaning (most common small engine repair):**
Most fuel-related running problems trace to a clogged carburetor. Ethanol fuel absorbs water and leaves a varnish residue.

1. Remove air filter; remove carburetor (typically 2 bolts and fuel line)
2. Disassemble: remove float bowl (bolt on bottom), float, needle, main jet, pilot jet
3. Spray all passages with carburetor cleaner; hold jets up to light — should see through the passages
4. Clean stubborn deposits with a strand of copper wire or ultrasonic cleaner
5. Replace O-rings and gaskets from carburetor rebuild kit (specific to your model; $5–$15 on eBay)
6. Reassemble; start on full choke, then open choke as engine warms

**Spare parts to keep on hand:**
- Spark plugs (2 per engine type on your property)
- Air filters (2 per engine)
- Fuel filters (2 per engine)
- Carburetor rebuild kits (1 per engine; include gaskets, O-rings, needle)
- Drive belts (1 per machine that uses belts)
- Pull start cord (1 spool; learn to replace recoil starter)
- Chainsaw: 2 spare chains, 1 bar, chain file set, bar oil, proper gas/oil mix ratio card

**Ignition troubleshooting:**
- No spark: test with inline spark tester; if no spark, check for: broken kill wire (ground short), fouled plug, bad ignition module (coil)
- Weak spark: plug gap wrong (typically 0.025"–0.030" for small engines); replace plug
- Timing: rarely adjustable on small engines; if flywheel key is sheared (from hard stop), engine will run retarded — replace flywheel key ($2–$5 part)

### 10.7.2 Pump Repair

**Hand pump rebuild (Bison/Simple Pump style):**
1. Common failure: worn cylinder leather (piston cup) or cracked cylinder body
2. Lower cylinder to surface (pull pipe sections)
3. Replace leather cup or full cylinder assembly; spare cylinder is $30–$60
4. Lubricate with food-grade petroleum jelly; reassemble

**Centrifugal pump (submersible or surface):**
- Impeller wear: reduced flow, noise; replace impeller (~$20–$60 for small pump)
- Mechanical seal failure: water leaking from shaft; seal kits $15–$40
- Motor failure: check capacitor first (common failure mode; $5–$15 part), then windings
- Priming failure (surface pump): check foot valve and suction line for air leaks; foot valve screen may be clogged

### 10.7.3 Tire Repair

**Plug and patch kit (tubeless tires):**
1. Locate puncture (submerse in water or spray soapy water)
2. Ream hole with included T-handle tool
3. Thread rubber plug strip through needle tool; push through hole; pull needle out, leaving plug
4. Trim flush; reinflate
5. Permanent fix: patch from inside after dismounting tire; vulcanizing patch is more reliable than plug for long-term

**Bead sealing (tire won't hold air at bead):**
- Spray starter fluid around bead and ignite (controlled burn seats bead) — effective but dangerous without practice; safer: use a ratchet strap cinched around tread to compress and seat bead with compressor
- Slime tire sealant (OEM method): inject through valve stem; coats interior

**Sidewall damage assessment:**
- Cords visible or exposed: tire is unsafe; replace
- Small cut not through cords: inspect regularly; may be managed temporarily
- Sidewall is never safely patchable; this is a safety-critical assessment

**Tube repair:**
1. Remove wheel, dismount tire with tire irons
2. Find puncture (inflate tube off rim, submerge in water)
3. Scuff area with sandpaper, apply vulcanizing cement, let tack 5 minutes, press patch
4. Remount with care not to pinch tube between tire bead and rim

### 10.7.4 Electrical Repair

**Wire splicing (field-grade):**
1. Strip 1" from each wire end
2. Twist conductors together (Western Union splice for inline; pigtail for taps)
3. Solder: flux first, apply heat to wire (not to solder), let solder flow into joint
4. Wrap with self-amalgamating silicone tape (Rescue Tape or 3M 70: $5–$10/roll) — this is superior to PVC electrical tape for field use; forms a waterproof bond without adhesive

**Crimp terminal selection:**
- Uninsulated: Use heat-shrink tubing over crimp; best for high-quality connections
- Insulated (standard): Acceptable; color code: red = 22–18 AWG, blue = 16–14 AWG, yellow = 12–10 AWG
- Marine-grade (tinned copper): Required for wet environments; Ancor or Blue Sea Systems
- Tool: Ratcheting crimper (Molex or Klein) for consistent crimp — never use diagonal pliers

**Fuse replacement:**
- Always replace with the same amperage rating; never "upsize" a fuse to stop blowing
- A fuse blows because something is drawing more current than the circuit was designed for — find and fix the cause before replacing the fuse
- Fuse types: ATC blade (automotive), AGC glass tube (older equipment), MIDI/ANL (main battery fuses), circuit breaker (resettable alternative)
- Stock: assortment of ATC fuses 5A–30A; ANL fuses for main battery connections

### 10.7.5 Structural Repair

**Sistering joists (for a rotted or broken floor joist):**
1. Expose the joist (remove subfloor if needed)
2. Cut a new joist to full span length from same species and size lumber
3. Apply construction adhesive (Liquid Nails Subfloor or equivalent) to full length
4. Clamp or screw sister joist to damaged joist along its full length (16" OC with structural screws or 3" nails at 16" OC)
5. Sister should bear on the same plates/beams as the original; if original has significant sag, jack up carefully before sistering

**Post rot repair:**
1. Assess: if less than 25% of the cross-section is compromised, consolidant + epoxy filler is viable
2. Consolidant (LiquidWood by Abatron: $30–$50/quart): penetrates soft rotted wood, hardens fiber
3. Epoxy filler (WoodEpox by Abatron: $30–$50/quart): fills voids, shapeble when mixed, paints and stains
4. For structural posts: jack temporarily, sister with new timber alongside, bolt together; do not trust epoxy alone for structural loads

**Roof leak temporary repair:**
- Roof cement (Henry 208: $15–$20/quart): embed into wet surface; seals around flashings and nail heads
- Self-adhering bitumen tape (Henry FlexFlash or similar: $20–$30/roll): excellent temporary repair for shingle tears and flashing failures
- Tarp (UV-resistant, 5-mil polyethylene): with battens screwed over the tarp at edges; can protect for 1–2 seasons

**Spare structural parts to stock:**
- Joist hanger hardware (Simpson LUS26, LU24 — common sizes)
- Post cap hardware (Simpson ABA44, ABU44)
- Structural screws (2-1/2" and 3" GRK or Spax structural screws, 1 lb each)
- Hurricane ties (Simpson H2.5A)
- Lag screws (1/2" × 3" and 1/2" × 5", 25 each)
- Carriage bolts (3/8" × 3.5" and 1/2" × 5", 25 each)

## 10.8 Tool Maintenance and Storage

### 10.8.1 Rust Prevention

Rust is the primary enemy of metal tools. In humid climates, unprotected bare metal rusts overnight. A rust prevention system is essential.

**Rust prevention products:**

| Product | Type | Application | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Boeshield T-9 | Wax-based aerosol | Spray on tools; wipe | Excellent for hand tools, saw blades, chisels |
| Fluid Film | Lanolin-based | Spray or wipe | Long-lasting; penetrates rust; excellent for outdoor equipment |
| 3-in-1 Oil | Light mineral oil | Wipe on | Basic protection; use for general tool oiling |
| Renaissance Wax | Microcrystalline wax | Buff onto blades | Museum-grade; excellent on high-value items |
| Camellia oil | Natural | Wipe on | Traditional Japanese plane/chisel preservation |
| WD-40 | Light petroleum | Spray on temporarily | Water displacement only; not a long-term protectant — evaporates and leaves surface dry |
| Rust-Oleum cold galvanizing compound | Zinc-based paint | Brush/spray | For structural metal; equipment with known rust risk |

**Storage environment:**
- Humidity above 60% promotes rust. Target: 40–50% RH in tool storage area
- Silica gel packets (rechargeable) in enclosed metal storage boxes
- Eva-Dry renewable dehumidifier ($20–$30): excellent for medium-sized enclosed spaces
- Tool box liners: anti-corrosion foam liner (Kaison or Panasad: $15–$30) that emit VCI (volatile corrosion inhibitor)
- Hanging: hand tools hung vertically or in shadow boards stay drier than piled in drawers

**VCI (Volatile Corrosion Inhibitor) products:**
Emit a vapor that coats metal surfaces and inhibits rust. VCI bags, papers, and foam liners are excellent for storage of tools that won't be used for months. Armor Wrap and BullFrog VCI are reliable brands ($15–$30 for a bag of 50 VCI foam strips).

### 10.8.2 Shadow Boards and Organization

A shadow board is a board (plywood, Peg-Board, or sheet metal) with the outline of each tool painted or marked. Immediately shows missing tools; prevents "where is the 3/4" wrench" problem that wastes hours per week.

**Construction:**
1. Arrange all tools on a 4×8 plywood sheet; trace outlines with marker
2. Install pegboard hooks, hangers, and holders for each tool
3. Paint silhouette of each tool behind its holder position
4. Label with name and location code if in a multi-area shop
5. Hang on wall or mount on stand

**Tool chest vs. shadow board:**
- Tool chest: better for mobile/mobile use; protection during transport; condensation risk in humid climates
- Shadow board: better for stationary shop; instant visual inventory; never rummage through piles

**Shadow board materials by environment:**
- Dry interior shop: 3/4" birch plywood with pegboard overlay
- Humid shop or outdoor covered workspace: 3/4" PVC sheet or marine plywood (no OSB — swells)
- Mobile: steel pegboard panels (Global Industrial or Uline)

### 10.8.3 Power Tool Maintenance

**Circular saw:**
- Replace blade when it burns wood rather than cutting; teeth are dull
- Clean blade with oven cleaner or CMT blade cleaner; removes pitch and sap
- Check baseplate flatness annually (drop blade against edge of a framing square)
- Retract and oil blade guard pivot; carbide blades don't need sharpening — replace them

**Cordless battery care:**
- Storage charge: 40–60% state of charge for long-term storage; not 100%, not 0%
- Temperature: store between 50–75°F; cold reduces capacity temporarily; heat causes permanent capacity loss
- Cycle count: modern lithium cells last 400–800 full charge cycles; partial cycles are better
- Do not leave on charger indefinitely (some chargers have a "maintenance" mode that is safe; others trickle charge, which overcharges)
- If a battery won't accept a charge: check for pack voltage with a multimeter (should be within 1–2V of nominal); individual dead cells are the common failure — not easily repaired by homesteaders

**Carbon brush replacement (brushed motors):**
Many older power tools use brushed motors. Brushes are graphite blocks that contact the commutator and wear over time. Signs: sparking from vents, reduced power, intermittent operation.
- Remove the two brush caps (usually on opposite sides of the motor body with a coin slot)
- Pull old brushes; match dimensions; install new brushes with spring correctly oriented
- Cost: $5–$20 per set; available from tool-specific suppliers or eBay

**Saw blade storage:**
Never stack saw blades. They scratch and dull each other. Hang individually on a pegboard hook, or store in the original packaging or in a blade case. Keep blades coated with Boeshield T-9 for storage.

### 10.8.4 Handle Replacement

Replacing a broken handle is faster and cheaper than buying a new tool, and the quality of a hickory replacement handle often exceeds the original.

**Handle materials:**
- Hickory: Standard; best combination of shock absorption, strength, and availability
- Ash: Similar to hickory; slightly lighter; traditional English choice
- Fiberglass: More durable against mechanical abuse and moisture; transfers more vibration to hands; accept or wrap with tape
- Steel (tubular): For sledgehammers and picks in construction; very durable

**Handle installation (for struck tools — hammers, axes, hatchets):**
1. Remove old handle: drill out or burn out the handle from the eye; file eye clean
2. Fit new handle: the wood should fit snugly into the eye; whittle or plane if needed; the grain should run perpendicular to the striking face
3. Drive the head onto the handle with a mallet (striking the bottom of the handle on a hard surface, using momentum); the head should seat 1/2"–3/4" below the top of the handle
4. Cut excess handle flush with the eye
5. Kerf the exposed end (hacksaw, two cuts perpendicular and crossing)
6. Drive a wooden wedge into each kerf; hammer until tight
7. Drive a metal expansion wedge at 90° to the wooden wedge
8. Trim flush; sand; apply boiled linseed oil to the handle

## 10.9 CBRN Considerations

### 10.9.1 Tool Use in Contaminated Environments

**Radiological (fallout) scenarios:**

Hand tools are the primary tool class for post-fallout environments. No electronics, no batteries, no circuit boards to malfunction from radiation or EMP.

Electronic measuring tools (digital calipers, multimeters with digital displays, laser levels) use CMOS integrated circuits sensitive to radiation. In areas with significant radiation exposure:
- Expect digital tools to malfunction or provide erroneous readings after cumulative radiation exposure above 1 Gy (100 rad)
- Analog equivalents: analog multimeters (Simpson 260 or Triplett 630), mechanical beam torque wrenches, analog dial calipers and indicators, mechanical pressure gauges
- Mechanical tools are inherently radiation-hardened

**Contaminated tool decontamination:**
- Beta/gamma surface contamination (fallout particles): Wash with water and detergent; most external contamination is mechanically removable
- Check with Geiger counter after washing; repeat if elevated readings persist
- Metal tools: can be decontaminated more effectively than porous materials (rubber grips, wood handles)
- Wood handles that are contaminated: remove and replace; wood absorbs and retains radionuclides

**Biological contamination:**
- Suspected anthrax/biological agent: tools exposed to an unknown biological agent should be treated as contaminated until confirmed otherwise
- Decontamination: 10% bleach solution (1:10 bleach:water) is effective against most biological agents; 30-minute contact time; rinse
- Do not use contaminated tools on food production areas; designate separate tools for contaminated-area work

### 10.9.2 Emergency Fabrication Under Duress

When normal tool access is compromised (displacement, bug-out, grid-down extended scenario):

**Improvised cutting tools:**
- Broken glass: edges are sharp and can process food, cut cordage, scrape hides
- Flint/chert knapping: making a sharp stone edge is a skill that takes days to learn; worth knowing conceptually
- Saw from hacksaw blade: wrap one end in paracord for a grip; fits in a pocket; cuts metal and wood

**Improvised fastening:**
- Wooden pegs (treenails): Oak or locust pegs driven into pre-bored holes; traditional shipbuilding technique; no metal required
- Lashing with natural cordage: dogbane, stinging nettle, inner bark (basswood, elm, cedar) can all be processed into cordage
- Wedge and notch joinery: no fasteners; wood pieces lock together by geometry

**Fire-hardened wood tools:**
Harden wooden points and shovels by charring the working end in fire, then scraping charred material with a stone; repeat 3–4 times. The carbonized surface is significantly harder and more moisture-resistant than raw wood.

## 10.10 Sourcing and Acquisition

### 10.10.1 Where to Get Tools Without Amazon

**Estate sales and farm auctions:**
The best source for vintage hand tools. Quality mid-century American tools (Stanley, Disston, Nicholson, Plumb, True Temper, Diamond Tool) are typically superior to new import equivalents and available at a fraction of new tool prices.

- Estate sales: Check estatesales.net; show up early (first hour); hand tools often $1–$20 each
- Farm auctions: Auctioneers in agricultural regions run farm dispersal auctions; entire tool collections sell cheap when family liquidates a farm
- Flea markets: Quality is unpredictable; worth attending regularly in agricultural areas
- Craigslist "free" and "tool" sections: Regular checking; often excellent finds
- Facebook Marketplace: Good for power tools (chainsaws, welders, drills) in local area
- Habitat for Humanity ReStore: Power tools and hardware at 30–70% discount

**What to look for at auctions:**
- Wooden planes (Stanley #4, #5, #7, #80 cabinet scraper): $5–$40 each
- Brace and bits: $5–$20 for a set
- Handsaws (Disston or Atkins): $5–$30; check that handle is intact and plate is straight
- Chisels (Stanley 750, Firmer chisels): $3–$15 each; handle damage is fine, edge condition is less important (you'll sharpen it anyway)
- Marking gauges, squares, levels: $2–$15 each
- Post vises (leg vises): $50–$200 depending on jaw width; inspect jaw for cracks
- Anvils: $2–$5/lb for quality vintage; $1/lb for cast iron

**What to avoid at auction:**
- Anything with significant rust that's not surface rust (pitting indicates structural compromise)
- Antique/collector's items (priced as collectibles, not tools)
- Electrical equipment without testing (buy a cheap outlet tester, test all power tools before bidding)

### 10.10.4 Community Tool Sharing

Not every tool needs to be owned by every household. Community tool libraries and sharing arrangements reduce individual cost and space requirements while building mutual interdependence (which is good).

**Tools worth owning individually:**
- Daily-use tools (your personal set of hand tools, your cordless drill)
- Safety equipment (your own helmet, gloves, PPE)
- Chainsaw (too critical and too frequently needed to borrow)

**Tools worth sharing in a community:**
- Large stationary tools: jointer, planer, bandsaw mill
- Specialized equipment: concrete mixer, post-hole auger, trencher
- High-cost/occasional use: welder, plasma cutter, sandblaster
- Pneumatic systems: compressor + nail gun sets

**Sharing logistics:**
- Record checkout (who has it, since when)
- Establish maintenance responsibilities (returner is responsible for returning in working order)
- Agreement on wear consumables (who replaces saw blades, welding wire, compressor oil)
- Liability: tool sharing is informal in most community arrangements; document agreements for expensive items

### 10.11.2 Six-Month Skill Development Plan

**Month 1: Hand tools and sharpening**
- Week 1: Set up sharpening station; sharpen all chisels and plane blades to working condition
- Week 2: Practice with saws (crosscut technique, keeping a line); cut and fit basic joints (lap joint)
- Week 3: Marking and layout; practice squaring up lumber; build a simple box
- Week 4: Work with axes; practice splitting wood; hang a handle if needed

**Month 2: Basic construction skills**
- Week 5–6: Frame a small structure (shed, outhouse, or chicken coop)
- Week 7: Pour and finish a simple concrete pad or footing
- Week 8: Basic plumbing (cut and join PEX; install a hand pump or pressure tank)

**Month 3: Power tools and metalworking basics**
- Week 9: Practice with circular saw, drill/driver; build something that requires accuracy
- Week 10: Start welding; MIG on scrap steel; learn to run a bead and join flat plate
- Week 11: Welding: tee joints, lap joints, corner joints; practice on projects
- Week 12: Small engine maintenance; change plugs, clean carb, adjust valves on one engine

**Month 4: Woodworking deepening**
- Week 13: Build a proper workbench if not already done
- Week 14: Chainsaw milling practice: fell a small tree, set up Alaskan mill, cut first slabs
- Week 15: Hand joinery: cut a mortise and tenon joint from scratch; test fit
- Week 16: Begin a real furniture project (stool, bench, or storage box)

**Month 5: Metalworking and repair**
- Week 17: Set up forge (propane); practice drawing out, bending; make hooks and S-hooks
- Week 18: Continue blacksmithing: make a leaf (decorative practice), then a functional gate latch
- Week 19: Pump repair practice: disassemble and reassemble a small pump; replace seals
- Week 20: Tire repair: practice plugging a tube, patching a tube; learn bead seating

**Month 6: Advanced and integration**
- Week 21: Aluminum casting: build or use a small furnace; cast a simple bracket or weight
- Week 22: Leather work: stitch a belt or small pouch from start to finish
- Week 23: Complete a multi-material project that uses welding + woodworking + concrete
- Week 24: Document your toolkit gaps; make a prioritized acquisition list for year 2

### 10.11.3 Learning Resources

**Books:**
- *The Complete Modern Blacksmith* — Alexander Weygers: Still the best technical introduction to blacksmithing, casting, and metalwork. Written by a working craftsman; practical above theory.
- *The Audel Millwrights and Mechanics Guide* — Latest edition: Industrial reference; machinery installation, repair, power transmission, alignment. Heavy but comprehensive.
- *Hand Tool Essentials* — Popular Woodworking: Good survey of hand tool use; beginner-friendly
- *Tage Frid Teaches Woodworking* (3 volumes): The most thorough hand and power tool woodworking text; comprehensive joinery coverage
- *The Complete Walker's Guide to Small Engine Repair* — Chilton: Covers all small engine types; carburetors, ignition, timing
- *The Art of Blacksmithing* — Alex Bealer: History and technique; good companion to Weygers
- *The Backyard Foundry* — B. Terry Aspin: Metal casting for the home craftsman
- *Leatherwork* — Al Stohlman (3-volume set): Definitive saddle stitching and leather craft reference
- *Welder's Handbook* — Richard Finch: MIG, TIG, and stick welding for the non-specialist

**YouTube channels:**
- Paul Sellers: Hand tool woodworking; possibly the best woodworking teacher on YouTube; free
- Rex Krueger: Hand tools with a practical and philosophical bent; beginner-friendly
- MyFordBoy: Aluminum casting, small foundry, metalworking; highly accessible
- Blondihacks: Machining and metal work for beginners; clear and methodical
- This Old Tony: Machining; very technical but entertaining
- Fireball Tool: MIG welding and fabrication; professional quality instruction
- Miller Welding: Official channel; technique videos for all welding processes
- AvE: Teardown, repair, engineering humor; advanced; learn what's inside everything
- Project Farm: Comparative testing of tool brands; genuinely useful buying guidance
- Alaskan Mill Forum (YouTube search): Various channels documenting chainsaw milling

**Community resources:**
- ABANA (Artist-Blacksmith's Association of North America): abana.org; affiliate guilds in every region; beginner workshops
- Community colleges: Almost every community college has welding, machining, and woodworking programs; semester courses ($200–$800) with proper equipment and instruction
- County extension services: Agricultural skills (fencing, equipment repair); often free workshops
- Maker spaces: Urban areas; full shop access for $50–$150/month; excellent for learning without buying all equipment
