# Domain 13: Community Organization, Governance, and Mutual Aid

> The homesteader who goes it alone is not self-sufficient. They are fragile.
> A single serious injury, a failed crop, a medical emergency — any of these
> can end the project. The homesteader embedded in a functional community
> is genuinely resilient: ten pairs of hands where there was one, ten skill
> sets where there was one, ten eyes watching where there was one.
>
> This chapter is not about utopian community ideals. It is about the practical
> architecture of human cooperation under pressure: how to build governance
> structures that hold when people disagree, mutual aid networks that function
> without money, conflict resolution that doesn't destroy relationships, and
> emergency decision-making that works when there is no time for meetings.
>
> Every intentional community that has failed — and most of them do fail —
> failed because of human organization problems, not technical ones. They had
> enough food, enough water, enough solar panels. What they did not have were
> functioning conflict resolution mechanisms, clear decision-making authority,
> and agreed-upon rules for when those rules could be changed.
>
> **The central principle**: Human systems require as much design and
> maintenance as physical ones. Plan them. Document them. Test them.
> Expect them to break and have a repair procedure ready.

## Quick-Reference Scale Guide

| Scale | Population | Governance model | Mutual aid model | Best analogy |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Household cluster | 2–5 adults | Informal consensus, explicit agreements | Direct reciprocity, shared tools | Extended family |
| Small community | 5–15 adults | Consensus or council of 3 | Skill exchange network, work parties | Amish work frolic |
| Medium community | 15–50 adults | Representative council, committees | Formal skill inventory, ledger barter | Small cooperative |
| Large community | 50–200 adults | Representative council + officers, bylaws | Time bank, local currency, formal roles | Small town, kibbutz |

## 13.1 Why Community Is Not Optional

### 13.1.1 The Math of Solo Homesteading

A single adult needs roughly 2,000 hours of labor per year just to maintain
the core systems of an off-grid homestead at a basic level of comfort:

```
Task                              Hours/year (solo)
--------------------------------  -----------------
Food production (garden)              400
Food preservation / processing        150
Firewood (harvest, split, stack)      200
Water system maintenance               50
Animal care (minimum, chickens)       150
Shelter maintenance                   100
Cooking from scratch                  300
Equipment repair                      100
Health and hygiene                     50
--------------------------------  -----------------
TOTAL                               1,500–2,000 hrs
```

A 40-hour work week is 2,080 hours per year. Doing everything above leaves
essentially zero time for emergency response, learning new skills, growing
food surplus, or any form of economic activity. A single illness or injury
that puts you down for two weeks can cascade into a failed food preservation
season, a waterlogged shelter, or animals dying.

Two adults cut per-person burden by roughly 35% (shared infrastructure,
complementary tasks). Four adults sharing systems cut it by 55%. Ten adults
sharing an off-grid community are working at 30% of the solo burden —
enough to have margins, do things well, and absorb disruption.

**Community is not a lifestyle choice. It is a systems redundancy decision.**

### 13.1.2 Documented Failures Without Community

The research literature on intentional communities — settlements deliberately
organized around self-sufficiency — is extensive and sobering.

**The Twin Oaks / Acorn model** (Virginia): Founded 1967, still operating as
of 2025 with ~100 members. Key features: explicit labor credit system (each
member contributes ~42 hours/week of community work), defined governance,
formal conflict resolution processes. These systems were not established
initially — they evolved after early crises nearly destroyed the community.

**The Farm** (Tennessee): Founded 1971. At its peak, ~1,400 people. Near-
collapse in 1983 from economic mismanagement, unclear roles, and governance
failures. Survived by restructuring into individual homesteads with a
cooperative overlay. Key lesson: scale increases governance requirements
non-linearly.

**Rajneeshpuram** (Oregon, 1981–1985): 2,000+ people. Catastrophic failure
from concentrated authority and no accountability mechanisms. Illustrates
that governance without checks produces predictable outcomes.

**Common failure patterns:**
1. No agreed-upon decision-making process → disputes escalate to personal conflicts
2. No formal conflict resolution → people leave or factions form
3. Informal labor agreements → resentment when contribution is unequal
4. No financial transparency → suspicion and accusations of theft
5. No exit procedures → trapped members become hostile members
6. Charismatic founders with no succession plan → community dies with founder

### 13.1.3 Successful Models Worth Studying

**Mondragon Cooperative Corporation** (Spain, 1956–present): 80,000+ workers,
100+ cooperatives. Democratic governance with worker ownership. Key principle:
wage ratio between highest and lowest paid capped at 6:1 (historically; now
~9:1 for some companies). Demonstrates that democratic governance works at
large scale when the economic stakes align with governance structure.

**Amish Communities**: 350,000+ members in North America. Successfully
maintained off-grid-adjacent self-sufficiency for 300+ years. Key features:
Gmay (district council) governance, Ordnung (community rules), formal
mutual aid (barn raisings, healthcare sharing), defined membership criteria,
and a clear exit path (Rumspringa / leaving). Not a utopia — but a system
that has survived across centuries and continents.

**Kibbutz movement** (Israel, 1910–present): 270+ kibbutzim, 170,000 members.
Evolved from full communalism to mixed models. Key lesson: full commons
creates free-rider problems; mixed systems (individual income plus community
services) have proven more durable.

**Findhorn Foundation** (Scotland, 1962–present): 400+ residents. Decision
making by "attunement" (spiritual consensus). Works partly because membership
self-selects for patience, but also because they developed formal governance
structures after early failures.

**What the successful models share:**
- Explicit, written agreements on decision-making
- Formal labor contribution expectations
- Conflict resolution process before conflicts arise
- Financial transparency
- Defined membership criteria and exit process
- Regular community ritual (shared meals, celebrations, work parties)

## 13.2 Governance Models

### 13.2.1 Choosing the Right Model for Your Scale

No single governance model is correct for all situations. The right model
depends on size, values alignment, and the nature of shared resources.

| Model | Best scale | Decision speed | Conflict risk | Documentation burden | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Consensus | 2–12 people | Slow | Low if it works | Low | Breaks down with bad actors |
| Modified consensus | 8–25 people | Medium | Medium | Medium | Need clear fallback rules |
| Majority vote | Any | Fast | High (winners/losers) | Low | Poor for long-term planning |
| Council (representative) | 15–200+ | Medium | Medium | Medium | Scales well |
| Sociocracy / holacracy | 10–100+ | Medium | Low | High | Requires training |
| Benevolent dictator | Any | Fast | Very high | Low | Fails at succession |

**For most off-grid communities of 5–50 people, modified consensus with a
documented fallback is the correct starting model.** You want the benefits
of consensus (buy-in, relationship preservation) without its failure mode
(single bad actor veto, decision paralysis).

### 13.2.2 Consensus Decision-Making

Consensus does not mean unanimous enthusiasm. It means that every member
can say: "I can live with this decision and will not actively work against it."

**The three positions in consensus:**
1. **Agreement**: "I support this."
2. **Stand aside**: "I have reservations, but I will not block it. Record my concerns."
3. **Block**: "This violates a core principle I cannot compromise. I cannot live with this."

A block should be rare and used only for fundamental ethical objections.
Overuse of the block destroys consensus processes. Communities should have
explicit guidelines about what constitutes a valid block.

**The consensus process:**
1. Proposal is presented with written description, rationale, and financial impact
2. Clarifying questions only — no debate in this round
3. Discussion round — concerns, amendments proposed
4. Revised proposal presented
5. Temperature check: Facilitator reads the room — thumbs up/sideways/down
6. If any thumbs down: those concerns are heard, proposal is modified or tabled
7. Final decision call: any blocks? Stand-asides noted in minutes

**When consensus fails:**
- Set a time limit: "If we cannot reach consensus in 30 minutes, we table
  for one week and bring back a revised proposal."
- Define a fallback: "After two failed consensus attempts, we vote by
  supermajority (75%)."
- Escalation path: "Deadlocked issues go to the council for binding decision."

**Facilitator role:** Rotating facilitation prevents power accumulation.
Train everyone in facilitation basics. Monthly rotation works for small
groups; quarterly for larger ones.

### 13.2.3 Council Governance (Medium to Large Communities)

At 15+ people, direct consensus becomes unwieldy. A council model — where
a small group of representatives makes most operational decisions, with the
full community retained for major decisions — is more practical.

**Council structure for 15–50 person community:**

```
FULL COMMUNITY ASSEMBLY
├── Meets: Monthly, or for major decisions (land, finance, membership)
├── Quorum: 75% of adult members
├── Supermajority (66%) required for: amending bylaws, large purchases,
│   new members, property changes
└── Simple majority for: operational decisions, committee appointments

    COMMUNITY COUNCIL (5–7 members)
    ├── Elected: Annual elections, staggered terms
    ├── Meets: Weekly
    ├── Authority: Operational decisions under $500 (or defined threshold)
    └── Reports to full assembly monthly

        COMMITTEES (ad hoc or standing)
        ├── Food & Agriculture
        ├── Infrastructure & Maintenance
        ├── Health & Medical
        ├── Finance
        ├── Conflict Resolution / Mediation
        └── Emergency / Security
```

**Council member terms:**
- 1-year terms with maximum two consecutive terms prevents entrenchment
- Stagger terms so 2–3 council members rotate out each year — institutional
  memory is preserved while fresh perspectives come in
- Recall mechanism: 60% community vote can remove a council member

**Decision tiers (example thresholds for a 20-person community):**

| Decision type | Authority | Quorum | Vote requirement |
|---|---|---|---|
| Daily operations | Individual or committee lead | N/A | N/A |
| Expenditures < $200 | Committee lead | N/A | N/A |
| Expenditures $200–$1,000 | Council | Majority of council | Simple majority |
| Expenditures > $1,000 | Full assembly | 66% of adults | Simple majority |
| New member admission | Full assembly | 75% of adults | 75% supermajority |
| Bylaw changes | Full assembly | 80% of adults | 75% supermajority |
| Emergency declaration | Council Chair + 2 council members | N/A | N/A |
| Exit / dissolution | Full assembly | 90% of adults | 80% supermajority |

### 13.2.4 Rotating Roles and Shared Responsibility

Fixed roles create bottlenecks and resentment. Rotating roles build
community-wide competence and prevent the "that's not my job" failure mode.

**Roles to rotate (with training period):**
- **Facilitator** (monthly rotation): Runs community meetings, enforces
  process, calls for votes
- **Record Keeper / Secretary** (quarterly rotation): Minutes, decision log,
  maintains community records
- **Treasurer** (annual, with 2-month overlap): Tracks income, expenses,
  produces monthly reports
- **Kitchen week** (weekly, paired): Responsible for community meals that week
- **Safety officer** (monthly): Walks the perimeter, checks fire extinguishers,
  ensures tools are stored safely
- **Communications coordinator** (monthly): Monitors radio nets, coordinates
  information with outside communities, manages message board

**The overlap period is critical:** No role should transfer without a
minimum two-week period where outgoing and incoming role holders work
together. Institutional knowledge lives in people; transfer it explicitly.

**Role documentation**: Each role should have a one-page written description
of tasks, schedule, resources, and who to contact for questions. This is
the "role manual." Whoever holds the role updates the manual when they leave.

### 13.2.5 Bylaws: What Must Be in Writing

Bylaws are not bureaucracy. They are the community's operating system — the
agreed-upon rules that everyone follows even when they disagree with a
specific decision. Without them, every dispute requires relitigating the
entire community's values from scratch.

**Bylaws skeleton for an intentional community:**

---

**COMMUNITY BYLAWS SKELETON**

*[Community Name] — Charter and Operating Agreement*

**Article I: Name, Purpose, and Values**
- Community name and legal structure (LLC, 501c3, trust, etc.)
- Statement of purpose (2–3 sentences)
- Core values (5–7 principles that will be referenced in disputes)

**Article II: Membership**
- Eligibility criteria (age, values alignment, etc.)
- Application process (trial period, full membership criteria)
- Membership categories (founding, full, associate, guest)
- Rights and responsibilities of each category
- Membership fees and labor contribution requirements
- Suspension and removal procedures (grounds, process, appeals)
- Voluntary exit procedures (notice period, departure support)

**Article III: Governance Structure**
- Full community assembly: meeting frequency, quorum, voting rules
- Council: size, election process, terms, authorities, recall
- Committees: standing committees, formation process, authorities
- Decision tiers: what requires what level of approval
- Consensus vs. vote fallback rules

**Article IV: Financial Management**
- Community accounts and who can access them
- Spending authorization levels
- Budget process (annual, quarterly review)
- Financial reporting requirements (monthly to all members)
- Audit process (annual, or triggered by member request)
- Individual vs. community financial obligations
- How shared assets are valued and tracked

**Article V: Land and Property**
- Legal ownership structure
- Individual private space definitions (if any)
- Common area rules
- Asset acquisition and disposal process
- What happens to assets if community dissolves

**Article VI: Conflict Resolution**
- Informal resolution first (describe process)
- Mediation process (who, how, what outcomes are possible)
- Formal arbitration for unresolved conflicts
- Consequences for policy violations (range, escalation)

**Article VII: Amendments**
- How bylaws can be changed (supermajority required)
- Proposal process for bylaw changes
- Emergency temporary modifications (allowed with automatic sunset)

**Article VIII: Dissolution**
- Trigger conditions for dissolution vote
- Vote required
- Asset distribution process
- Member support obligations during dissolution

---

*The bylaw skeleton should be 5–15 pages for a small community. A 50-page
document that no one has read is worse than a 5-page document everyone knows.*

### 13.2.6 Sociocracy / Holacracy for Off-Grid Communities

For communities with a strong learning culture, sociocracy offers a more
sophisticated governance structure that distributes authority more fully.

**Core sociocracy concepts:**
- **Circles**: Semi-autonomous units with specific domains (food circle,
  infrastructure circle, governance circle)
- **Double-linking**: Each circle sends a representative up to the wider
  circle and receives a representative down — information flows both ways
- **Consent not consensus**: A proposal passes if no one has a "paramount
  objection" (different from the block in consensus — it must be a genuine
  harm, not just a preference)
- **Role elections**: Roles are filled by sociocratic elections with
  discussion, not just voting

**When sociocracy is worth the overhead:**
- Community has 20+ adults
- Members are motivated to learn the method
- There is a facilitator trained in sociocracy

**Resources:**
- *Many Voices One Song* by Ted J. Rau and Jerry Koch-Gonzalez (free PDF online)
- Sociocracy For All (sociocracyforall.org) — training, templates

## 13.3 Mutual Aid Networks

### 13.3.1 What Mutual Aid Actually Is

Mutual aid is not charity and not market exchange. It is generalized
reciprocity — I help you when you need it, you help me when I need it,
without an expectation of immediate quid pro quo. The debt is to the
network, not to any individual.

This is how human communities survived before markets and governments.
The Amish barn raising is the archetype: the community builds your barn
in a day; you will attend future barn raisings and bring food and labor.
No one is keeping a ledger. But everyone understands the obligation.

Mutual aid requires:
1. Trust — people must believe others will reciprocate
2. Shared norms — what kinds of help are expected, what are optional
3. Network visibility — you have to know who needs what and who can provide it
4. Reciprocity tracking — informal or formal, there must be some accounting
   or free-riders will degrade the network

### 13.3.2 Setting Up a Mutual Aid Network: Step-by-Step

**Phase 1: Build the network (months 1–3)**

Step 1: Identify your geographic network. Draw a radius (5–10 miles typical
for rural) and identify every household you might want in the network. This
does not have to be everyone — start with people you already have some
relationship with.

Step 2: Host a first gathering — a shared meal, a work party, something
social that is not explicitly about "organizing a mutual aid network." The
relationship precedes the network. At the end, mention that you are thinking
about more formal mutual support and ask if others are interested.

Step 3: Second meeting, explicit purpose. Walk through the questions:
- What kinds of help are you willing to offer? (Labor, tools, skills, food,
  childcare, medical, emotional support)
- What are the circumstances under which you would request help?
  (Emergency only? Or ongoing support for common projects?)
- What are your limits? (Can help with physical labor but cannot provide
  cash loans)
- What are your communication methods? (Radio, phone, in-person, note drop)

Step 4: Document a simple network charter. One page. Who is in, what we
offer each other, how to make a request, and how to decline gracefully.
No request is refused without offering an alternative or a reason.

**Phase 2: Build the infrastructure (months 3–6)**

Step 5: Complete a skill and resource inventory (see 13.4 below). Every
household completes the inventory form. This is the network's map of
its own capabilities.

Step 6: Establish communication channels:
- Primary: GMRS radio net (daily check-in at a fixed time — 7 AM works
  for most) or established phone/radio schedule
- Secondary: Physical message board at a central location, checked daily
- Emergency: Pre-agreed signal (three radio transmissions at the hour,
  or a flag system visible from the road)

Step 7: Run a first work party. Something concrete — clearing brush on
someone's land, putting up a greenhouse, a canning day. This builds the
relationship and tests the network's labor capacity.

**Phase 3: Deepen and sustain (ongoing)**

Step 8: Establish regular gathering points — monthly shared meals, quarterly
work parties, seasonal celebrations. These are not optional overhead; they
are the network's maintenance schedule.

Step 9: Create a rotating hospitality obligation. Each household hosts one
gathering per year. This distributes the burden and ensures everyone's
space is known to the network.

Step 10: Annual network review. What went well? What requests couldn't be
met? What skills does the network lack? Who has left, who has joined?

### 13.3.3 Reciprocity Systems: From Informal to Formal

**Informal reciprocity (2–8 households)**

Best for small, tight-knit groups with strong pre-existing relationships.
No formal tracking. Operates on social norms — everyone knows roughly
who contributes and who doesn't. Works until:
- One person consistently receives without contributing
- A dispute over an unmet obligation has no mechanism for resolution
- The network grows beyond the point where everyone knows everyone

Failure mode: "free rider" gradually degrades the network's willingness
to give. Over months, people start saying no more often.

**Informal work ledger (5–15 households)**

A simple shared notebook or spreadsheet. Each work party entry notes:
who came, how many hours, what was done. No formal accounting — just
visibility. When someone has not contributed in three months, others
can gently ask. Provides data for the annual network review.

**Time bank (15–100 households)**

A time bank formalizes mutual aid reciprocity. One hour of service equals
one time credit, regardless of the service type. An hour of childcare is
worth the same as an hour of medical consultation. This explicitly rejects
market valuation.

Software options:
- hOurworld (hourworld.org) — free, widely used
- TimeBanks USA software — available to member time banks
- Timeoverchaos — open source, can run locally

Time banks work best when there is a coordinator who actively matches
requests to offers and manages the ledger. This is a real labor commitment
(5–10 hours/week for a 50-household bank).

**Local currency (50–200 households)**

When a community is large enough and wants to include local businesses,
local currency (a LETS — Local Exchange Trading System) goes beyond
pure time banking to include goods, not just services.

Examples:
- BerkShares (Berkshire region, MA) — printed scrip exchangeable with
  local businesses at par with USD
- Ithaca HOURS (NY, 1991) — early model, still operating in modified form
- Bristol Pound (UK) — digital and physical currency

For a small rural community, a simple paper scrip system works:
- Denominations: 1/4 hour, 1/2 hour, 1 hour
- Issued by the community treasury for community labor
- Accepted by any community member for goods and services
- Periodic "clearing" events where large balances are discussed

### 13.3.4 Resource Sharing and Tool Libraries

A tool library is one of the highest-return mutual aid structures.
A community that shares a $3,000 tractor, a $1,500 log splitter, and
a $800 pressure canner allows each household to access $5,300 in equipment
for a fraction of the ownership cost.

**Tool library setup:**

1. **Inventory**: Each household contributes what they are willing to share
   to the common pool. Tools are tagged with owner name and condition.

2. **Check-out system**: Simple notebook — name, tool, date out, date back.
   Maximum loan period: 1 week standard, 2 weeks with permission.

3. **Maintenance fund**: Each use of a shared tool contributes a small amount
   to a maintenance fund. A table saw use = $0.50. A tractor day = $5.00.
   Fund covers sharpening, oil, repair.

4. **Damage policy**: Normal wear is community responsibility. Negligent
   damage is borrower responsibility. Define "negligent" in writing
   (borrowed tool left in rain, chainsaw used without bar oil, etc.)

5. **Priority system**: Owners of contributed tools get priority scheduling.
   Non-owners may borrow but yield to the contributing household.

**High-priority tools for community sharing:**
- Tractor / tiller
- Log splitter (hydraulic)
- Pressure canner (23-quart All American)
- Freeze dryer (if community can afford one, ~$3,000)
- Cement mixer
- Chainsaw (shared as backup, with clear maintenance responsibility)
- Post hole digger (powered auger)
- Grain mill
- Trailer for hauling

## 13.4 Skill Inventories and Knowledge Mapping

### 13.4.1 Why a Formal Skill Inventory Matters

In an emergency, "I think someone knows how to do X" is much less useful
than "Sarah has a Wilderness First Responder certification and a complete
trauma kit, and she is in the north cabin." The skill inventory is the
community's human capital balance sheet.

A skill inventory also reveals dangerous gaps: if only one person knows
how to maintain the water filtration system, you have a single point of
failure. That knowledge needs to be shared.

### 13.4.3 Knowledge Gap Analysis

Once inventory forms are collected, map the gaps:

**Critical single points of failure** (only one person holds this knowledge):
- If one person is the only one who can do X, and they leave or become
  incapacitated, the community cannot do X.
- Identify these during the annual review.
- For each critical gap: assign a backup learner within 6 months.

**Critical absences** (no one in the network holds this knowledge):
- Priority order: medical, water, food preservation, construction
- Recruit specifically for gaps: "We need someone with medical background"
- Create a bridge training plan while recruiting

**Knowledge transfer schedule:**
Create a simple teaching calendar. Each quarter, two or three people with
critical knowledge run a hands-on workshop:
- Q1: Water filtration system maintenance (taught by whoever installed it)
- Q2: Pressure canning (taught by most experienced canner)
- Q3: Radio operations (taught by the licensed ham)
- Q4: First aid refresher (taught by WFR-certified member)

This should be in the calendar, not ad hoc. Attendance is a community
obligation, not optional.

## 13.5 Conflict Resolution

### 13.5.1 Why Conflict Is Inevitable and Plannable

Every community of adults with real stakes will have serious conflicts.
The question is not whether conflict will happen but what happens next.

Communities without conflict resolution mechanisms have two options when
serious conflict arises: the conflict is suppressed (it goes underground,
festers, and resurfaces worse later) or the conflict explodes (people leave,
relationships are destroyed, community splits). Neither is acceptable.

Communities with conflict resolution mechanisms have a third option:
structured, fair processing that may not make everyone happy but does
not destroy the community.

**The most common conflict triggers in off-grid communities:**
1. Unequal labor contribution ("free riders")
2. Resource allocation disputes (who gets the good farmland, who uses the tractor)
3. Parenting differences when children share space
4. Financial disputes (money owed, unequal contribution to shared costs)
5. Noise, lifestyle differences (sleep schedules, music, children)
6. Political and values disagreements that become personal
7. Romantic relationships within the community that end badly
8. New member who doesn't fit as expected
9. Leadership disputes and power struggles
10. External threats that the community disagrees about how to handle

### 13.5.2 The Resolution Ladder

Handle conflicts at the lowest level possible. Escalation should be a
last resort, not a first response.

**Level 1: Direct conversation (within 48 hours of issue arising)**
- The two parties talk privately. No witnesses, no audience.
- Focus: "When X happened, I felt Y. I need Z."
- Goal: mutual understanding and an agreed resolution
- This resolves 60–70% of conflicts if done promptly

**Level 2: Facilitated conversation (within 2 weeks if Level 1 fails)**
- A mutually agreed third party facilitates (not the council — a peer)
- The facilitator's role: ensure both parties are heard, identify shared
  interests, help design a solution
- Not binding — the parties still decide the resolution
- If no resolution: document the issue and move to Level 3

**Level 3: Community mediation panel (within 1 month)**
- Three-person panel: one chosen by each party, one neutral party
  (could be from outside the community)
- Panel reviews the situation, hears both parties separately and together
- Panel proposes a resolution; parties may accept, reject, or modify
- If modified resolution is rejected by either party: Level 4

**Level 4: Binding arbitration (within 6 weeks)**
- The council (or a designated arbitration committee) hears the case
- Both parties present; witnesses may speak
- The council issues a binding decision
- Both parties must agree in advance to honor the decision or face
  the membership consequences defined in bylaws

**Level 5: Membership review**
- For serious violations of community norms or failure to honor a Level 4
  decision
- Council convenes a formal review
- Outcomes: written warning, temporary suspension of community rights,
  required community service, probationary status, or removal

**The most important principle: every person must know this ladder exists
before any conflict arises.** It is introduced to all new members and
reviewed annually. The expectation is: when conflict arises, use the ladder.

### 13.5.3 Mediation Skills

The community should have at least two trained mediators. This is not a
professional credential — a 2-day training in basic mediation is sufficient
for most community disputes.

**Core mediation skills:**
- Active listening (reflect back what you hear before adding your own input)
- Distinguishing positions from interests ("I want the tractor on Tuesday"
  is a position; "I need to finish plowing before the rain comes" is an
  interest; there may be many ways to meet the interest)
- Normalization ("It's understandable to feel that way given what happened")
- Reframing (turning attacks into concerns: "You never contribute" → "You
  want to know that the labor is balanced")
- Reality-testing proposed solutions ("What would make this hard to follow
  through on? What would help you succeed?")
- Agreement writing: any resolution should be documented and signed

**Mediation resources:**
- *Getting to Yes* by Fisher, Ury, and Patton — foundational theory
- *The Mediator's Handbook* by Beer and Stief — practical manual
- Community Boards (SF) — free conflict resolution training resources online

### 13.5.4 Community Norms and the Behavior Code

Community norms are the written list of expected behaviors that every
member agrees to on joining. They are not aspirational statements; they
are behavioral commitments with consequences.

**Sample community behavior code:**

*We commit to:*
1. Address conflicts directly with the other person before involving others
2. Speak for ourselves, not for others ("I feel X" not "everyone thinks X")
3. Attend community meetings and community labor with the frequency agreed
   in our membership agreement
4. Keep community information confidential when asked — including financial
   details, health information, and internal conflicts
5. Maintain our personal space and shared spaces to agreed standards
6. Honor financial commitments to the community within the agreed timeframe
7. Participate in conflict resolution processes when asked
8. Respect the privacy and space of other households
9. Support new members during their integration period
10. Complete role responsibilities when we accept them, or give adequate
    notice when we cannot

*We will not:*
1. Use alcohol or recreational substances in shared spaces without advance
   community agreement
2. Bring unauthorized guests for extended stays (more than 3 days) without
   community discussion
3. Make financial commitments on behalf of the community without authorization
4. Share confidential community information with outsiders without permission
5. Retaliate against someone who raised a conflict through proper channels
6. Allow children to be unsupervised in hazardous areas (list specific areas)

## 13.6 Trade, Barter, and Economic Coordination

### 13.6.1 The Internal Economy

Every community has an internal economy even if it is not recognized as one.
Someone grows more food than they eat; someone has mechanical skills; someone
has more time than goods. Making this visible and facilitating exchange
is one of the community council's core functions.

**The spectrum of internal economic arrangements:**

**Full communalism**: All resources are pooled and distributed by community
decision. High cohesion required; extreme vulnerability to disagreement over
distribution. Works in religious communities with strong shared values
and acceptance of authority (Hutterites). Very difficult to sustain secularly.

**Income sharing**: All earned income goes to the community pool; community
distributes living expenses equally. Twin Oaks and Acorn operate this way.
Requires very high trust and very clear governance.

**Mixed commons**: Each household maintains private finances and private
production, but contributes labor to community projects and shares certain
resources (land, heavy equipment, knowledge). Most common model for secular
intentional communities. Recommended starting point.

**Neighbor network**: Households are independent but maintain formalized
mutual aid and trade relationships. Lowest governance burden; most scalable.

### 13.6.2 Setting Up a Trade Ledger

For a mixed commons or neighbor network model, a trade ledger makes
exchanges visible and prevents disputes about what was agreed.

**Simple paper trade ledger format:**

```
DATE       FROM         TO           ITEM/SERVICE           VALUE   BALANCED
--------   ----------   ----------   --------------------   ------  --------
Apr 3      Johnson      Rivera       8 hrs fence work       8 hrs   Rivera owes
Apr 5      Rivera       Johnson      20 lbs potatoes        2 hrs   6 hrs Rivera owes
Apr 10     Johnson      Rivera       6 hrs tractor use      6 hrs   Balanced
```

Keep the ledger in a common location (community hall, community radio station).
Anyone can see any entry. Monthly review: any balance over 20 hours in either
direction triggers a conversation — not automatically a problem, but visibility
is accountability.

**Digital ledger option:**
A shared spreadsheet (Google Sheets or Nextcloud if you want local hosting)
with one row per transaction. Simple formulas calculate running balances.
Print monthly for offline reference.

### 13.6.3 Fair Exchange Principles

Barter without a framework for valuing goods and services generates disputes.
Establish these principles before trades, not after.

**Labor valuation:**
- Community work (benefits everyone) is its own category — not a credit
  unless the community explicitly creates labor credits for communal projects
- Skilled vs. unskilled labor: communities must decide whether to equalize
  (time bank model — one hour = one hour) or differentiate (skilled labor
  worth more). Both work; what matters is clarity
- Recommendation for small communities: equalize labor time. The hierarchy
  of skill valuation imports market logic and can create resentment.

**Goods valuation:**
- Reference local market prices as a starting point, then adjust for
  community production costs
- Handmade goods are valued at a premium over store-bought equivalents
  (labor is embedded in them)
- Perishable surplus (food about to go bad) is traded at steep discount
  rather than wasted — better to get something than nothing

**Services valuation:**
- Medical consultations: tricky. Do not commodify emergency care —
  that is always free within the community. Non-emergency advice or
  routine care can be included in the time bank at 1:1 ratio.
- Teaching: 1:1 time ratio
- Care work (childcare, eldercare): 1:1 time ratio

**Anti-hoarding norm:**
Communities should have an explicit norm: members with large surpluses of
food or critical supplies offer them to the community at fair exchange
before selling outside. This is not confiscation — it is right of first
refusal. Widely accepted in intentional community ethics.

### 13.6.4 External Trade: When and How

Community members will trade with the outside world. The community's role
is to facilitate and occasionally coordinate, not control.

**Community bulk purchasing:**
For items used by multiple households, cooperative purchasing reduces cost:
- Propane: order together, split one delivery cost
- Bulk seed: order a seed catalog together, divide
- Soil amendments and compost materials
- Hardware and building materials (common lumber sizes, plumbing fittings)

Designate one person per category to coordinate orders. Rotate annually.

**Farmers market coordination:**
If multiple households are selling at the same market, coordinate:
- Avoid undercutting each other
- Designate one booth for community branding if desired
- Coordinate which products each household brings to avoid redundancy

**Cash emergency protocol:**
Define what happens if a community member has a genuine cash emergency
(medical bill, tax, vehicle repair) and has community trade credits but
not cash. Options:
- Community loan fund (if the community has one)
- Community buys trade credits at a discount for cash
- Emergency member contribution request (voluntary, community-wide)

## 13.7 Community Meetings and Records

### 13.7.1 Meeting Structure That Actually Works

Bad meetings destroy communities slowly. People begin dreading meetings,
attendance drops, decisions are made without representation, resentment builds.

**The four kinds of community meetings:**

**1. Check-in (daily or weekly, 10–15 minutes)**
- Stand-up format — no chairs, everyone stands
- Each person: one sentence on their status, one need, one offer
- Purpose: daily operational coordination, early detection of problems
- No decisions made; issues raised get scheduled for the next working meeting

**2. Working meeting (weekly or biweekly, 60–90 minutes)**
- Agenda published 48 hours in advance
- Consent agenda: items with no objection are approved en bloc (saves time)
- Discussion items: one at a time, time-boxed (15 minutes per item default)
- Action items are assigned with person, task, and deadline
- Minutes distributed within 24 hours

**3. Community assembly (monthly, 2–3 hours)**
- Full participation required (or send alternate)
- Financial report, infrastructure update, project reports
- Major decisions requiring full community input
- Celebration of accomplishments — explicitly built into agenda
- Open forum: any member can raise any issue

**4. Emergency meeting (as needed, duration varies)**
- Called by council chair or any two council members
- Purpose: respond to a crisis requiring immediate community decision
- See 13.9 for emergency protocols

**Agenda template for working meeting:**

```
AGENDA — [Community Name] Working Meeting
Date: ___________    Facilitator: ___________    Time-keeper: ___________
Record Keeper: ___________

0:00 — Check-in (1 minute each person)
0:10 — Consent agenda (items approved without discussion unless objection)
        [ ] Minutes from previous meeting
        [ ] Routine expenditures under $50
        [ ] [Other routine items]
0:15 — Item 1: [Title] — [Owner] — [15 min]
0:30 — Item 2: [Title] — [Owner] — [20 min]
0:50 — Item 3: [Title] — [Owner] — [15 min]
1:05 — Action item review (who, what, by when)
1:10 — Check-out (1 sentence each)
1:15 — End
```

**Meeting facilitation rules:**
- Facilitator stays neutral — if they have a strong opinion on an agenda item,
  they pass facilitation for that item
- Time-keeper signals when 2 minutes remain on a topic; facilitator calls time
- "Stack" system for speaker order: facilitator keeps a list of people who
  want to speak; no interrupting
- "Parking lot" for off-topic but important items: write them down, schedule
  for next meeting

### 13.7.2 Records and Documentation

**The community records system** is the institutional memory. Without it,
decisions made years ago are disputed; resources are forgotten; who owes
what to whom becomes contested.

**What must be recorded:**
- Every meeting's minutes (decisions and action items at minimum)
- All financial transactions (income, expenses, contributions, trade ledger)
- Membership records (joining date, membership agreement signed, role history)
- Skills inventory (updated annually)
- Asset inventory (what the community owns, value, location)
- Land and property documents (deeds, surveys, easements)
- Emergency contacts for every household
- Medical information that members consent to share (blood type, allergies,
  chronic conditions relevant to emergency care)

**The records format:**
- Physical binder for critical documents (deeds, bylaws, membership agreements)
  in a waterproof container in a known location
- Digital backup on a locally hosted server (Nextcloud on a Raspberry Pi
  works well — no cloud dependency) with encrypted backup to a second physical
  location
- Annual print of key records (financial summary, membership list, skill
  inventory) in the physical binder

**Records retention:**
- Financial records: 7 years
- Membership agreements: indefinitely
- Meeting minutes: indefinitely
- Asset inventory: current version + last 3 years
- Correspondence: 3 years minimum

## 13.8 Community Mental Health and Social Cohesion

### 13.8.1 The Underrated Risk

Surveys of failed intentional communities consistently show that mental
health and social cohesion failures precede and cause physical system
failures — not the reverse. People stop maintaining the water system because
they are depressed or in conflict, not the other way around.

The specific stressors of off-grid life compound standard mental health risks:
- **Isolation**: Limited access to people outside the community; few escape
  routes when relationships in the community are difficult
- **Physical exhaustion**: Year-round physical labor with few days off;
  recovery deficit accumulates
- **High stakes**: Every failure has real consequences (no food, no heat);
  this creates chronic low-level anxiety
- **Economic precarity**: Limited access to cash; skill deficits expose
  vulnerability; comparison with grid-connected life can feel like failure
- **Seasonal depression**: Especially in northern climates with short winter
  days; off-grid settings often reduce access to light, warmth, social activity
- **Interpersonal density**: Living in close proximity with a small group of
  people means there is no escape from difficult relationships

### 13.8.2 Prevention: Building Resilient Social Fabric

**Scheduled solitude and privacy:**
Every community member needs guaranteed private time and private space.
Define this in the community norms: "Closed door means no interruption
except emergency." Schedule community-free days — one per week where
no communal obligations are required.

**Regular celebration:**
Build celebrations into the calendar before the year begins:
- Seasonal solstice / equinox gatherings
- Harvest celebration
- Individual household birthdays and anniversaries acknowledged
- Community anniversary
- First snow / first frost / first flower — mark the seasons together

The quality of a community's celebrations predicts its longevity better
than the quality of its infrastructure.

**Physical exercise and movement:**
Structure community work to include physical variety. A person who only
does sedentary intellectual work and a person who only does heavy physical
labor are both at risk. Pair them on tasks. Create opportunities for
physical play: community swimming, hiking, sports.

**Cultural and intellectual life:**
Reading circles, film nights (with solar power for projection), music
evenings, storytelling, game nights. These seem like luxuries. They are
not. They are what makes community life worth living and what keeps
people there through the difficult seasons.

**Mentorship and apprenticeship:**
Pair experienced members with newer members for skill transfer — but also
for relationship. Having someone who looks to you for guidance, and someone
you can turn to for guidance, is deeply anchoring.

### 13.8.3 Crisis Mental Health Response

**Signs that a community member is in distress:**
- Withdrawal from community activity (missing meals, meetings, work parties)
- Change in sleep patterns (observed in shared space)
- Verbal statements of hopelessness or overwhelm
- Increased irritability, conflict frequency
- Neglect of personal hygiene or responsibilities
- Substance use increase

**Community response protocol:**
1. Check in directly and privately: "I've noticed you seem to be having a
   hard time. I wanted to check in."
2. Listen without fixing: "I hear that. That sounds really hard."
3. Offer practical help: "Is there something I can take off your plate
   for this week?"
4. Connect to community support: "Would it help to talk to [name] — they've
   been through something similar."
5. Evaluate safety: If someone is expressing suicidal ideation, this is
   a medical emergency. Remote crisis lines (988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline),
   satellite internet for telehealth, or evacuation to care if needed.

**Designated mental health contacts:**
The skill inventory should identify members with mental health training.
The community should also maintain contact information for:
- Telehealth mental health services (available via Starlink internet)
- 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline
- Local mental health crisis team (know their response time — may be hours
  in rural areas)

**Grief protocols:**
Deaths, miscarriages, serious illness — these happen in off-grid communities
and often with less access to outside support. Have a community grief protocol:
- Communal acknowledgment (gathering, shared meal, ritual)
- Work relief: bereaved household's labor obligations suspended for 2 weeks
- Check-in structure: someone checks in daily for 2 weeks, weekly for 2 months

## 13.9 Security and Defense Within Community Governance

*Note: This section covers the organizational and governance dimension of
security. Physical security systems and hardware are covered in the dedicated
Security & Defense chapter (12-security-defense.md).*

### 13.9.1 The Security Council Role

Security decisions — who watches, when, what to do when a threat is detected —
must be pre-decided, not invented in the moment. The time to decide how to
respond to an approaching vehicle at 2 AM is not at 2 AM.

**Standing security committee:**
- 3–5 members, including the community's most experienced person in
  security or military background
- Authority: make immediate tactical decisions during active threats
- Reports to council: post-incident review within 48 hours
- Prepares: threat assessment, watch schedule, incident response protocols

**Threat assessment process (annual):**
The security committee reviews the community's threat environment annually:
- What are the realistic threat categories? (Animal, human, weather, CBRN)
- What is the probability of each, given current conditions?
- What is the community's current capability to respond to each?
- What are the gaps?
- What is the budget for addressing gaps?

### 13.9.2 Watch Rotations and Perimeter Security

For communities with active security concerns, a watch rotation distributes
the burden and ensures coverage without burning out individuals.

**Watch rotation framework (medium-threat scenario):**

- Overnight watch: midnight to dawn, 2-person teams, 4-hour shifts
- For a 20-adult community: each person stands watch ~3 nights per month
- Watch pairs should include one experienced and one less experienced person
- Watch logbook: every observation recorded, no matter how minor (vehicle
  on road at 0300, dog barking direction, lights seen)

**Watch protocols:**
- Check-in required by radio every 30 minutes (GMRS or ham)
- If check-in is missed by 15 minutes: second person goes to check
- Immediate alarm signal (agreed in advance): 3 short radio bursts, or
  physical signal if radio silence is required
- Response to alarm: defined roles (who responds, who shelters in place,
  who accounts for children and vulnerable people)

**Threat response levels:**

| Level | Trigger | Response |
|---|---|---|
| Green — Normal | Routine operations | Standard watch, normal activity |
| Yellow — Elevated | Unconfirmed threat, unusual activity | Increased watch, account for all community members, review emergency supplies |
| Orange — Serious | Confirmed threat approaching or regional emergency declared | All members notified, defensive positions established, evacuation option reviewed |
| Red — Immediate | Active threat on property | Security committee takes command, shelter-in-place or evacuation, radio emergency net activated |

### 13.9.3 De-escalation as Primary Protocol

**The most important security principle for community governance:**
The decision to use defensive force must require the agreement of the
security committee (at least 2 members), not any individual member acting alone.

Unilateral armed response by a single community member is the fastest
way to create legal liability, community trauma, and regional hostility.

**De-escalation first:**
Most human threats — even aggressive ones — can be addressed by:
1. Presence and communication (do not hide; make clear the property is
   occupied and the occupants are watching)
2. Clear, calm communication: "This is private property. Are you lost?
   Can I help you with something?"
3. Documentation: photograph vehicles, note physical descriptions
4. Separation: "I need you to leave now. I am calling the sheriff."
5. Alert: notify neighbors and regional network

**When force is authorized by community protocol:**
- Immediate threat to life or serious bodily harm with no retreat option
- Specific criteria should be in writing and reviewed by a lawyer
- Self-defense law varies by state — know your jurisdiction

**Post-incident protocol:**
Any security incident — even one resolved without force — receives a
full review within 48 hours:
- What happened, start to finish?
- What did we do well?
- What would we do differently?
- Do we need to change protocols?
- Does anyone need emotional support from the incident?
- Do we need to notify outside parties (sheriff, community network)?

## 13.10 Emergency Governance: Decision-Making Under Crisis

### 13.10.1 Why Normal Governance Fails in Emergencies

Consensus processes and full community assemblies require time. Emergencies
do not provide time. A tornado warning gives you 10 minutes. A wildfire
jump gives you less. Nuclear fallout requires shelter-within-15-minutes
decisions.

Normal governance is suspended during genuine emergencies — but suspended
does not mean abandoned. It means that a smaller, pre-authorized group
makes decisions that the full community reviews afterwards.

**The emergency governance rule:**
"In a declared emergency, the Emergency Committee has full decision-making
authority for actions necessary to protect life and critical infrastructure.
All emergency decisions are reported to the full community within 48 hours
of the emergency's end."

### 13.10.2 The Emergency Committee and Chain of Authority

**Emergency Committee composition:**
- 3 people: Community Council Chair + Security Committee Lead + Medical Lead
- If any is unavailable: pre-designated alternates (every role has an alternate)
- Command authority: Emergency Committee Chair (Council Chair, or alternate)

**Chain of command (written, posted in common area):**
1. Council Chair
2. Council Vice-Chair
3. Security Committee Lead
4. Medical Lead
5. Most senior council member by length of service

**What the Emergency Committee can authorize:**
- Immediate expenditure of up to 20% of community cash reserves
- Evacuation of all or part of the community
- Shelter-in-place order
- Restriction of entry / exit
- Commandeering community resources for emergency use
- Establishing communication silence (radio blackout)
- Seeking outside help (emergency services, regional mutual aid network)

**What requires full community vote even in emergency:**
- Armed defense (unless immediate and unavoidable)
- Expulsion of a member
- Transfer or encumbrance of land

### 13.10.3 Emergency Protocols by Scenario

**Severe weather (tornado, hurricane, flash flood):**
1. NOAA weather radio trigger or radio net warning received
2. Emergency Committee convenes (can be done by radio)
3. Decision: shelter-in-place or evacuate?
   - Criteria: type and severity of weather, community location, road conditions
4. If shelter: all members to designated shelter, accountability check by radio
5. If evacuate: pre-planned routes, pre-designated rally point (two options —
   primary and alternate in different directions)
6. Accountability check: each household confirms all members present
7. Emergency supplies pre-staged in shelter location

**Wildfire:**
1. Immediate trigger: visible smoke within 5 miles or red flag warning + wind
2. Emergency Committee: stay or go decision must be made early
   - Defensible space: can we safely defend? (see 12-security-defense.md)
   - Wind: if fire has wind behind it, evacuation is almost always correct
3. Go-bags must be packed and at the door within 30 minutes of decision
4. Community vehicles loaded: priority order pre-established (children, elderly,
   medical supplies, food, seeds, documents, tools)
5. Animal protocols: pre-decided — which animals evacuate, which are turned loose
6. Communication: radio net activated, regional mutual aid network notified
7. Rally point: two options in different directions from the property

**Nuclear/radiological event:**
1. Trigger: NOAA EAS alert, ham radio emergency net, or visible bright flash
   (in absence of any warning, the flash itself is the trigger)
2. Immediate action (first 15 minutes): shelter in the most hardened structure
   available — do not wait for Emergency Committee decision
3. If warning received: Emergency Committee convenes, radiation monitoring begins
4. Shelter-in-place order: all members in fallout shelter, accounting complete
5. Communication: battery-powered radio for EAS updates; limit transmissions
6. Supplies: 14-day water and food supply must be pre-staged in shelter
7. Radiation monitoring: Geiger counter readings logged every 4 hours
8. Exit decision: Emergency Committee, based on radiation levels (target: < 2 mR/hr
   ambient before extended outdoor time)
9. Post-fallout: decontamination protocols before re-entry of exposed members

**Pandemic / biohazard:**
1. Trigger: community member confirmed case of serious communicable disease,
   or regional health emergency declared
2. Immediate decision: quarantine individual or shelter entire community?
3. If community shelter: restrict all non-essential entry/exit; begin 14-day
   quarantine period for suspected exposures
4. Medical lead takes authority on health protocols: PPE requirements, isolation
   procedures, symptom monitoring schedule
5. Supply assessment: do we have adequate food, water, medicine for 30 days?
   If not, designated person makes resupply run with full PPE
6. Communication: maintain contact with regional network by radio; report
   community status daily
7. Mental health: structured daily schedule essential during extended lockdown

**Grid-down / extended power outage:**
1. First 24 hours: no emergency governance required — community systems are
   designed for grid independence
2. 72 hours: Emergency Committee assesses duration; is this local or regional?
   (Ham radio monitoring)
3. 1 week: inventory community resources formally; share findings with regional
   mutual aid network
4. 2 weeks: establish regular schedule with regional mutual aid network;
   coordinate food and fuel sharing; consider taking in isolated elderly neighbors

**Intruder / security threat:**
See 13.9.3. Security Committee takes command, de-escalation first, force
only when no alternative exists and life is at immediate risk.

### 13.10.4 CBRN Shelter-in-Place Authority

For CBRN events specifically (chemical, biological, radiological, nuclear),
one person must have clear authority to order immediate shelter-in-place
without convening anyone. The time cost of consultation in these scenarios
is measured in casualties.

**CBRN immediate authority protocol:**
- Any one of: Emergency Committee Chair, Medical Lead, Security Lead can
  order immediate shelter-in-place for CBRN
- The order is communicated by: 3 rapid radio transmissions on community
  GMRS channel, then verbal if close enough
- Response required: within 5 minutes, all members respond by radio with
  their location
- No one challenges the order until the immediate threat is past
- Post-incident review within 48 hours to evaluate whether the order was
  correct and what to do differently

## 13.11 Integration With Outside Communities

### 13.11.1 The Isolation Trap

The impulse toward isolation is understandable — distance from the grid
reduces dependency. But complete isolation is fragile in different ways
than dependency. A community with no outside relationships has:
- No intelligence about regional threats
- No ability to source supplies when internal ones are exhausted
- No labor reserve for large projects
- No goodwill when disputes with neighbors arise
- No political representation when government actors come to the property

Every successful long-term off-grid community maintains a calibrated
level of outside connection. The Amish maintain extensive trade relationships
and political engagement while preserving cultural separation. Israeli
kibbutzim sell produce internationally while maintaining internal governance.
The Zapatista communities in Chiapas maintain political alliances while
managing internal autonomy.

**The principle**: Autonomy in governance and daily life is compatible with
strategic engagement with outside communities and institutions.

### 13.11.2 Relationship Tiers with Outside Communities

**Tier 1: Immediate neighbors (within 5 miles)**
- These relationships are the most important. Regardless of your community's
  political views or lifestyle choices, you are neighbors. Fence disputes,
  water rights, road maintenance, emergency response — these require
  functional neighbor relationships.
- Minimum maintenance: know their names, wave, occasionally help when you
  see they need it, accept help when they offer
- Do not create enemies at Tier 1. It is not worth it.

**Tier 2: Regional mutual aid network (5–30 miles)**
- Other homesteaders, farmers, rural households with shared values
- Maintained through: regional events, radio networks, cooperative purchasing
- These are your emergency reserve: if your community is in crisis, these
  are the people you call
- Reciprocal: you commit to responding to their emergencies too

**Tier 3: Nearest town (commerce, medical, institutional)**
- Where you buy what you cannot produce
- Where the hospital and sheriff and county clerk are
- Maintain functional relationships with local businesses (they know you,
  you know them — preferential treatment in emergencies)
- Political participation: vote, attend planning meetings, know your
  county commissioner and sheriff by name

**Tier 4: Broader region and national networks**
- Organizations: National Young Farmers Coalition, Practical Farmers of Iowa,
  ATTRA (Appropriate Technology Transfer for Rural Areas), NOFA (Northeast
  Organic Farming Association) and regional equivalents
- These provide: educational resources, political advocacy, peer networks
- Worth membership dues; worth sending one community member to annual events

### 13.11.3 Deciding When to Stay Autonomous vs. Cooperate

**Framework for cooperation decisions:**

Ask three questions:
1. What does the community gain from cooperating? (Resources, security, access)
2. What does the community risk from cooperating? (Dependency, surveillance,
   loss of autonomy, values compromise)
3. Is the cooperation reversible? (Can we withdraw if it stops working?)

**Low-risk, high-gain cooperation:**
- Cooperative purchasing (propane, seeds, supplies)
- Emergency mutual aid agreements
- Skill and knowledge sharing
- Political advocacy on shared issues (property rights, water rights, zoning)

**Medium-risk cooperation (require careful terms):**
- Formal agreements with government agencies (agricultural assistance,
  emergency management registration)
- Participation in regional food systems (farmers markets, co-ops)
- Media coverage or public profile (increases visibility, which has
  security implications)

**High-risk cooperation (evaluate carefully):**
- Any agreement that gives outside parties authority over land or resources
- Financial relationships that create dependency (loans, subsidies with strings)
- Agreements that compromise the community's ability to make autonomous decisions

**The autonomy test:**
"If we enter into this cooperation and then decide we want to leave it,
what is the cost and process of exit?" If exit is very difficult or very
costly, be cautious entering.

### 13.11.4 Opsec and Privacy in Community Operations

What outside parties know about your community affects your safety and autonomy.

**Principles:**
- The community's existence and general purpose are not secret — being
  known as "a self-sufficient community" is fine and reduces the "what
  are they hiding" speculation from neighbors
- Internal operations — food stores, security arrangements, financial
  holdings, member personal information — are community business
- Decide together what goes on social media; one member's Instagram is
  the community's public profile whether intended or not
- Visitors to the property see what you decide to show them; think about
  what is visible to anyone who drives past

**The stranger protocol:**
Every community should have an explicit protocol for visitors:
- Who can invite visitors?
- What notification is required? (24 hours' notice to council chair)
- What orientation do visitors receive? (Where to go, what not to touch,
  who to ask for help)
- Where can visitors go unaccompanied? (Common areas only is a reasonable default)
- What is the maximum stay without community approval? (3 days is common)
- How are visitors vetted for extended stays or membership consideration?

## 13.12 Membership: Entry, Integration, and Exit

### 13.12.1 New Member Process

The single most impactful governance decision you make is who becomes a member.
A community that accepts members poorly will spend enormous energy on conflict
and departure. Taking the time to vet membership carefully pays for itself
many times over.

**The three-stage membership process:**

**Stage 1: Guest visit (1–7 days)**
- Prospective member visits, participates in community life
- No commitment from either side
- Community designates a host for the visit
- End of visit: brief check-in from host to council (any concerns?)
- If positive: invite to extended trial

**Stage 2: Trial membership (30–90 days)**
- Prospective member participates in community work
- Pays community fees at trial rate (typically reduced)
- Attends meetings but does not vote
- Has designated mentor who checks in weekly
- At end of trial: prospective member and council both assess fit
- Both parties must agree to continue; either can end the trial

**Stage 3: Full membership decision**
- Council presents prospective member's background, trial period assessment
- Prospective member attends and speaks to their interest and intentions
- Community votes (75% supermajority required for admission)
- If admitted: membership agreement signed, orientation to all community
  systems completed, mentor assigned for 6-month integration period

**What to look for in prospective members:**
- Skills the community needs (check against skill inventory gaps)
- Financial contribution capacity (not elitist filtering — realistic about
  whether someone can meet community financial obligations)
- Values alignment on key community norms (de-escalation, conflict process,
  labor contribution)
- Relationship style: can they raise conflict directly? Can they take feedback?
- Prior community experience: what did they learn? Why did they leave?
- Physical capacity relative to expectations (be explicit and honest)

**Red flags:**
- History of leaving multiple communities under difficult circumstances
  without self-reflection
- Expecting the community to solve personal problems (financial, relational)
  without contributing
- Unwillingness to use conflict resolution processes
- Strong charisma that has historically led people to defer to them
  (concentrates power in problematic ways)

### 13.12.2 Member Exit: Graceful Departures

People leave. Life circumstances change, relationships end, the community
does not turn out to be what someone expected. How exits are handled
determines whether a departure damages the community or merely saddens it.

**The exit process:**
1. Member notifies council chair of intent to leave (written, 60-day notice
   recommended; negotiable in genuine emergencies)
2. Exit interview with council chair (optional but encouraged): what worked,
   what didn't, what could the community do better
3. Financial settlement: any credits or debts in the trade ledger resolved;
   membership fees prorated; shared asset contributions addressed per bylaws
4. Skills transfer: the departing member documents any critical knowledge
   they hold that is not otherwise documented; participates in one knowledge
   transfer workshop before leaving
5. Celebration: the community holds a send-off gathering. This is not optional.
   Even difficult departures deserve acknowledgment.

**Asset and financial exit:**
- If the departing member contributed to land purchase or major infrastructure,
  the bylaws must address the repayment or redemption process in advance
- This must be pre-agreed, in writing, and not negotiated at the moment of departure
  when emotions are high and money is contested

**Relationship continuity:**
Former members are often valuable external allies. They know your systems,
they know your people, they may have skills and resources you will need.
Do not burn bridges unless there is a genuine safety reason to do so.

## 13.13 Scale-Specific Guidance

### 13.13.1 Small Group: 5–15 Adults

At this scale, governance is mostly interpersonal. Formal structures are
lighter but must still be explicit.

**What you need:**
- A written one-page agreement on: decision-making, labor expectations,
  resource sharing, and exit process
- Weekly check-in (can be informal — a shared meal works)
- Simple trade ledger
- One person designated as "first among equals" — not a dictator, but
  someone who calls the meeting and ensures things get done
- A basic skill inventory so everyone knows what everyone can do
- A defined conflict resolution process (even if it's just "we talk to
  each other directly first, then ask [trusted third party] to help")

**What you don't need yet:**
- Elected council (everyone is the council)
- Formal bylaws (an operating agreement is sufficient)
- Time bank software (a notebook is fine)
- Standing committees (ad hoc works at this scale)

**Key risk at this scale:**
Informality. Everything is agreed informally, lives in people's heads,
and breaks down when one person leaves or when a serious conflict arises.
Push to write things down even when it feels unnecessary. It is an act
of care for the future community.

### 13.13.2 Medium Community: 15–50 Adults

This is the most common scale for intentional communities and the most
challenging to govern. Too large for full informality; too small to justify
heavy professional governance infrastructure.

**What you need:**
- Written bylaws (full, not a skeleton)
- Elected council (5–7 members)
- Standing committees for: finance, mediation, food/agriculture, infrastructure
- Full skill inventory with annual update
- Formal trade ledger or time bank
- Monthly community assembly
- Emergency committee with written protocols
- Trained mediators (at least 2)
- Regular celebrations and social programming

**Key risks at this scale:**
- Faction formation: 15–50 is large enough for factions but small enough
  that factions can destroy the community
- Governance fatigue: the meeting load can become crushing; delegate to
  committees aggressively; keep assemblies focused
- Old-guard / new-member dynamic: founding members accrue informal power;
  must actively distribute it through governance structures

### 13.13.3 Larger Community: 50–200 Adults

At this scale you have a small town and should govern like one, with appropriate
accommodations for your community's values.

**What you need:**
- Professional-quality bylaws drafted or reviewed by an attorney
- Representative council with distinct elected positions (president, treasurer,
  secretary) plus at-large members
- Standing committees with real autonomy and budget authority
- Formal financial management: bookkeeper (paid or very committed volunteer),
  annual audit or review by outside accountant
- Formal time bank or local currency with a coordinator (paid role)
- Conflict resolution panel with trained community members plus outside
  mediators available for serious disputes
- Clear succession planning for all key roles
- Regular all-hands assembly (monthly or bimonthly)
- Emergency management plan that meets or exceeds FEMA ICS standards
  (Incident Command System — free training at fema.gov/ics)

**What changes at this scale:**
- Anonymity increases — not everyone knows everyone
- Free-rider problem becomes real and requires structural response
- Decision-making speed slows; find ways to accelerate without sacrificing
  legitimacy (strong committee autonomy within defined scopes)
- External visibility increases; need a deliberate media and external
  relations strategy

### 13.14.1 New Member Orientation Checklist

```
NEW MEMBER ORIENTATION — [Community Name]
Assigned Mentor: _______________  Start Date: _______________

SYSTEMS ORIENTATION (complete in first 2 weeks)
[ ] Tour of all community land and facilities
[ ] Water system: source, filtration, distribution, maintenance

[ ] Security: threat levels, watch schedule if active, entry/exit protocols

GOVERNANCE ORIENTATION (complete in first week)
[ ] Read community bylaws — sign acknowledgment
[ ] Read community behavior code — sign acknowledgment
[ ] Read trade ledger system — understand how to log transactions
[ ] Attend one community meeting as observer before first full meeting
[ ] Meet all council members and committee leads

EMERGENCY ORIENTATION (complete in first week)
[ ] Emergency shelter location and supplies
[ ] Emergency communication protocol (radio frequency, channel, check-in time)
[ ] Bug-out route (primary and alternate) and rally point
[ ] Medical information shared with medical lead (optional but recommended)
[ ] Know who has medical training in the community

SKILL AND RESOURCE INVENTORY (complete in first month)
[ ] Submit completed skill inventory form
[ ] Tour of personal tools and equipment you are sharing
[ ] First community work party attendance

SOCIAL INTEGRATION (first 90 days)
[ ] Attend community meal / social gathering
[ ] Participate in at least one community celebration
[ ] Meet all adult community members one-on-one or in small groups
[ ] Complete 30-day check-in with mentor
[ ] Complete 90-day check-in with council chair
```

### 13.14.2 Annual Community Review Agenda

A full-day annual review keeps governance functional and prevents drift.

```
ANNUAL COMMUNITY REVIEW AGENDA — [Community Name]

Morning (4 hours):
  - Year in review: what did we accomplish? (20 min)
  - Financial review: budget vs. actual, trends (30 min)
  - Infrastructure assessment: what needs attention in the next year? (30 min)
  - Skill inventory review: gaps, transfer needs (20 min)
  - Membership review: who joined, who left, who is in trial? (15 min)
  - Security and emergency preparedness: what changed? (20 min)
  - Conflict review (anonymized): what conflicts arose? Were they resolved?
    What process worked? What didn't? (30 min)
  - Celebration: what went well? (15 min)

Break + shared lunch

Afternoon (3 hours):
  - Goal-setting for next year: what are the three community priorities? (45 min)
  - Budget process: proposed budget for next year, community input (60 min)
  - Governance review: any bylaws changes needed? (30 min)
  - Council elections or confirmations (30 min)
  - Role assignments for the year: who takes which rotating roles (15 min)
  - Closing ritual: whatever the community finds meaningful

Record keeper produces: written summary with decisions and assignments,
distributed within one week.
```

## Summary: What to Build and When

| Phase | Timing | Governance | Mutual Aid | Records |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pre-community | Before any shared decisions | 1-page operating agreement | Handshake agreements + notebook | Nothing formal yet |
| Formation | First 3 months | Weekly check-ins, written norms | First work party, skill inventory started | Meeting minutes, trade ledger |
| Establishment | Months 3–12 | Bylaws drafted, council formed | Full skill inventory, GMRS net active | Financial records, membership agreements |
| Operating | Year 1+ | Annual review, rotating roles | Time bank or ledger, regular work parties | Full records system, annual print |
| Mature | Year 3+ | Council elections, committee autonomy | Regional mutual aid network, local currency | Professional financial review |

The temptation is to build all of this before the community exists. Do not.
Build the minimum structure needed for the current scale, then add structure
when the absence of it causes a real problem. Over-governing an early community
is as dangerous as under-governing a mature one — it signals distrust and
drives people away.

**The irreducible minimum** before any community begins: a written, signed
agreement on decision-making authority, labor expectations, financial obligations,
and the exit process. Everything else can be built as needed. Those four
things must exist from day one.
