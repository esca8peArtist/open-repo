# Domain 16: Skills & Knowledge

> Every system documented in this guide is only as reliable as the person
> operating it. A solar array that nobody can troubleshoot is hardware waiting
> to fail. A pressure canner that someone used once, three years ago, is a
> liability. A chainsaw in the hands of someone who never learned falling
> technique is a death waiting to happen.
>
> Skills are not the same as knowledge. You can read every chapter in this
> guide and still be dangerously incompetent when the situation requires
> action. Knowledge is necessary but insufficient. Competence requires
> practice — hands-on, repetitive, feedback-rich practice — until the
> procedure lives in your muscle memory rather than on a shelf.
>
> This is the capstone domain. It synthesizes every skill required across
> the preceding fifteen domains, structures them into an honest priority
> order, provides specific learning pathways with real resources and real
> costs, and gives you the tools to assess your current position and plan
> the road to genuine self-sufficiency.
>
> **The central principle**: No one person needs to be expert at everything.
> But every critical skill must be held at proficiency by at least two people
> in your household or community. A single point of failure in any Tier 1
> skill is an existential risk to the system.

## 16.1 Skill Assessment Framework

### 16.1.1 Proficiency Levels

Every skill in this guide should be rated honestly against four levels.
Self-delusion at this stage costs you later.

| Level | Definition | Operational meaning |
|-------|------------|---------------------|
| **Novice** | You understand the concept; you have done it once or a few times with guidance | Cannot be relied upon under pressure; needs supervision |
| **Competent** | You can perform the task correctly and consistently without guidance, in normal conditions | Can handle standard situations; may struggle with complications |
| **Proficient** | You can perform the task under adverse conditions, trouble-shoot failures, and teach others | Reliable under pressure; can identify and fix problems |
| **Expert** | Deep theoretical and practical mastery; can improvise and innovate; recognized by peers | Can train others to proficiency; handles novel edge cases |

**The minimum standard for your household**: Every Tier 1 skill must be
held at **Competent** or above by at least two people before you move
off-grid. Every Tier 2 skill must be at Competent by at least one person
within the first six months. Aiming for Proficient in Tier 1 is the goal
by end of year one.

### 16.1.3 Household Skill Gap Analysis

After completing the inventory, identify your gaps using this process:

**Step 1 — Mark all blanks in Tier 1 as RED.** These are your immediate priorities
regardless of any other consideration.

**Step 2 — Mark all Tier 1 cells rated Novice as ORANGE.** Novice is insufficient
for any Tier 1 skill before going off-grid.

**Step 3 — Count single-person coverage.** Any skill where only one person is at
Competent or above is a fragile single point of failure. Mark these YELLOW.

**Step 4 — Total your gaps:**
- RED gaps: Cannot move off-grid until resolved
- ORANGE gaps: Must be closed within 60 days of going off-grid
- YELLOW gaps: Cross-train a second person within 6 months

**Step 5 — Create a 90-day learning sprint** for your RED gaps, a 6-month plan
for ORANGE, and a 12-month plan for YELLOW. The sections that follow give
you the specific learning pathways for each skill.

## 16.2 Tier 1: Critical Survival Skills

### 16.2.1 Water: Sourcing and Purification

**Why it is Tier 1**: Death from dehydration occurs in 3–5 days. Death from
waterborne pathogens (cholera, typhoid, giardia) can occur in 1–2 weeks from a
bad source.

**Sourcing competencies required:**
- Locating and evaluating water sources (visual assessment, upstream contamination awareness)
- Constructing a basic rain catchment system (roof, downspout, first-flush diverter, cistern)
- Developing a spring: excavating, installing tile or perforated pipe, capping
- Hand-digging a shallow well: casing, sealing, hand pump installation
- Risk assessment: what is upstream, what is nearby (agriculture, industrial, septic)

**Purification — four methods, all Competent before go-live:**

| Method | Use case | How to perform | Equipment needed | Failure modes |
|--------|----------|---------------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Boiling** | No equipment available; known biological contamination | Rolling boil 1 minute (3 minutes above 6,500 ft elevation) | Pot, heat source | Does not remove chemicals or heavy metals |
| **Filtration** | Everyday use with sediment + biological risk | Pump, gravity, or squeeze filter; replace cartridge per manufacturer schedule | Sawyer Squeeze, Berkey, ceramic pot filter | Cracked ceramic; expired filter; viral pathogens (not all filters remove viruses) |
| **Chemical** | Backup when filter unavailable; viral risk | Unscented liquid bleach: 8 drops/gallon clear water, 16 drops/gallon cloudy; 30-minute wait. OR iodine tablets per package. | Bleach + dropper, or iodine tablets | Ineffective against Cryptosporidium without 4-hour wait; taste |
| **UV** | Best for viruses; clear water only | SteriPen: stir in water per instructions (60–90 seconds for 1 liter) | SteriPen Adventurer; spare batteries | Does not work in turbid water; battery failure |

**Water testing — minimum competencies:**
- Collect a proper sample (sterile container, mid-stream or post-pump)
- Interpret a basic test kit (coliform, nitrates, pH, hardness)
- Know when to send to a lab vs. when field test is sufficient
- Interpret a lab report: which contaminants at which levels are dangerous

**Practice drill**: Shut off your main water supply for 24 hours. Source, filter,
purify, and test water from a backup source. Document results. Repeat quarterly.

### 16.2.2 Fire: Starting by Multiple Methods

**Why it is Tier 1**: Hypothermia kills faster than almost anything else.
Fire is also cooking, water purification, food smoking, and light.

**Required competencies — four methods minimum:**

**Method 1 — Lighter or matches** (Novice level): Everyone knows this, but do you
have multiple lighters stored? Waterproof matches? A lighter in every go-bag?
Practice: Start a fire in wet conditions with a lighter. This is harder than it sounds.

**Method 2 — Ferro rod (fire steel)**
- Strike at 90-degree angle to rod; scrape firmly away from body
- Have proper tinder prepared before striking: char cloth, birch bark, fatwood shavings, cattail fluff
- Practice: 20 successful ignitions in a row before calling this Competent
- Cost: $10–$30 for a quality rod (Light My Fire Scout is reliable)

**Method 3 — Bow drill**
- This takes serious practice: spindle, hearth board, handhold, bow, and tinder bundle
- Wood pairing matters: dry willow, cedar, cottonwood, basswood are good combinations
- Expect 50+ failed attempts before your first coal. This is normal.
- Practice time to Competent: 4–8 hours of practice across multiple sessions
- Why you need it: zero consumables; can be made entirely from materials in most forests

**Method 4 — Flint and steel (or lens fire)**
- Lens (magnifying glass, fresnel lens) requires direct sun: limited but zero consumables
- Flint and steel: charred material (char cloth or amadou fungus) catches spark from struck high-carbon steel

**Fire management and safety:**
- Fire triangle awareness: fuel, heat, oxygen — how to starve any leg
- Charcoal CO risk: never burn inside any enclosed structure without ventilation
- Wildfire risk assessment: when NOT to have a fire (wind, drought conditions, dry season)
- Fireplace/wood stove operation: damper position, draft management, creosote prevention
- Putting fires out completely: stir and soak, not just douse; test with bare hand near coals

**Competence check**: Can you start a fire from scratch in 5 minutes or less in
good conditions? In 15 minutes or less in challenging conditions (light rain, no
dry tinder visible)? If not, practice more.

### 16.2.3 Food: Preservation and Basic Foraging

**Why it is Tier 1**: Without food preservation skills, a productive summer
harvest rots while February goes hungry. One bad batch of improperly canned
food can kill you from botulism.

**Pressure canning — the single most important food skill after growing it:**
- Understand botulism: anaerobic, heat-resistant, produced in low-acid foods; no taste or smell
- Altitude adjustment: every 2,000 feet above sea level requires pressure increase
- USDA-tested recipes only for pressure canning: do not improvise processing times
- Competence requires: 20+ successful batches across multiple food types
- Practice: Can meat, vegetables, and beans before you depend on the skill
- Equipment required: 16–23 qt pressure canner ($80–$200); dial or weighted gauge

**Water bath canning:**
- High-acid only (pH below 4.6): tomatoes + added acid, fruits, jams, jellies, pickles
- Simpler than pressure canning; wider margin for error
- Common mistakes: using jars not designed for canning; skipping headspace requirements
- Practice: 10+ batches, confirmed seals on all jars

**Dehydrating:**
- Solar dehydrator (DIY: $30–$80 materials) or electric ($50–$400)
- Target moisture: below 10% for most produce, below 5% for jerky
- Temperature by food type: herbs 95–115°F; fruits/vegetables 125–135°F; meat 160°F
- Storage: vacuum-sealed mason jars or Mylar bags with oxygen absorbers; 2–5 year shelf life

**Fermentation basics:**
- Lacto-fermentation requires salt + time (no heat, no special equipment)
- Sauerkraut: 1.5–2% salt by weight of cabbage; pack tight; weight to keep submerged
- Recognizing successful ferment vs. spoilage: CO2 bubbling = active; pink/black mold = discard
- Reference: "The Art of Fermentation" by Sandor Katz (Chelsea Green, 2012)

**Foraging — 10 most common edible plants by region:**

*Eastern North America (top 10 by caloric/nutritional value and abundance):*
1. **Cattail** (Typha spp.) — pollen, green heads, rhizome starch; abundant near water
2. **Dandelion** (Taraxacum officinale) — leaves, flowers, roots; year-round
3. **Garlic mustard** (Alliaria petiolata) — leaves and seeds; invasive, no harvest limits
4. **Lamb's quarters** (Chenopodium album) — nutritionally superior to spinach; everywhere
5. **Purslane** (Portulaca oleracea) — high omega-3; grows in disturbed soil
6. **Wood sorrel** (Oxalis spp.) — lemony leaves; entire plant edible
7. **Elderberry** (Sambucus nigra) — flowers and cooked berries; raw berries cause nausea
8. **Pawpaw** (Asimina triloba) — highest calorie fruit in eastern US; late summer
9. **Hickory nuts** (Carya spp.) — high fat and calories; fall harvest
10. **Acorns** (Quercus spp.) — require leaching tannins in cold running water; flour and meal

*Key rule*: Never eat anything you have not positively identified with a physical
field guide in hand. Two field guides minimum. Find it in the guide before tasting it.

**Animal processing — poultry:**
- Kill method: cervical dislocation (by hand for experienced handlers) or cone + blade
- Scalding: 145–150°F water for 45–60 seconds; agitate to loosen feathers
- Plucking: pull in direction of feather growth; defeathering machine optional ($200–$600)
- Evisceration: remove organs without rupturing gut (bile and intestine contaminate meat)
- Chill immediately to below 40°F; butcher or freeze within 24 hours
- Practice: process minimum 10 birds before relying on the skill in production

### 16.2.4 First Aid: Life-Threatening Emergencies

**Why it is Tier 1**: Emergency services response in rural areas averages 18–30
minutes; in true grid-down scenarios, it may be hours or never. The person who
can act in the first 3 minutes prevents the person from dying before help arrives —
or replaces help entirely.

**CPR and AED:**
- American Red Cross or American Heart Association certification: 6–8 hour class, $50–$80
- Recertify every 2 years; practice on a mannequin at least annually between certifications
- Hands-only CPR: 100–120 compressions per minute, 2-inch depth for adults
- AED: automated; follow voice prompts exactly; do not delay compressions to find AED
- Cost: AED unit for home — $1,200–$1,600 (Philips HeartStart or Zoll AED Plus); worth it

**Hemorrhage control:**
- MARCH protocol: Massive hemorrhage → Airway → Respiration → Circulation → Hypothermia
- Tourniquet application: 2–3 inches above wound, tighten until bleeding stops, note time
- Wound packing: QuikClot or Combat Gauze, pack firmly into wound cavity, apply pressure 3+ minutes
- Junctional hemorrhage (groin, axilla, neck): tourniquet cannot work; wound packing only
- Equipment: CAT tourniquet ($30 each; have 4+), hemostatic gauze ($15–$25 per pack)
- Training: Stop the Bleed course (free, 2 hours; stopthedbleed.org)

**Wound care:**
- Irrigation: the most important step; 60 mL syringe, clean water, irrigate at pressure
- Closure: sterile strips, staples, or sutures after irrigation; do not close infected wounds
- Signs of infection: increasing redness, warmth, swelling, streaking, pus, fever
- Infected wound management: open it, irrigate, pack loosely with iodoform gauze, antibiotics

**Fracture splinting:**
- Principle: immobilize the joint above and below the fracture
- SAM splints: moldable aluminum foam splint ($8–$12 each); keep 6 on hand
- Check circulation, sensation, and movement (CSM) before and after splinting
- Traction splint for femur fractures: prevents internal bleeding (femur fractures can be fatal)
- When to evacuate vs. manage in place: open fracture, vascular compromise, femur fracture = evacuate

**Anaphylaxis:**
- Recognize: hives + airway compromise + hypotension = anaphylaxis (not just "allergic reaction")
- Treatment: epinephrine (EpiPen) IM injection into outer thigh; 0.3 mg adult dose
- Second dose 5–15 minutes if no improvement
- Follow with diphenhydramine (Benadryl) 50 mg oral or IM
- EpiPen: $600–$800 for name brand; epinephrine auto-injector generics $100–$200; check expiration annually

**Childbirth basics:**
- Normal delivery: support head, guide delivery, do not pull
- Cord care: wait for cord to stop pulsating; clamp and cut if necessary
- Postpartum hemorrhage: most common complication; uterine massage, controlled cord traction
- Neonatal resuscitation basics: dry and stimulate; if no cry in 30 seconds, begin assisted breathing
- Reference: "Emergency Childbirth" by Dr. Gregory White (free download); "Where There Is No Doctor" Chapter 19

### 16.2.5 Shelter: Emergency Construction and Principles

**Emergency shelter construction — priority order:**
1. **Debris hut**: leaf litter over stick frame; insulation R-value of dead leaves is
   surprisingly good; can maintain survivable temperature in near-freezing conditions
2. **Tarp shelter**: A-frame or lean-to with 8×10 tarp and paracord; 10 minutes to build
3. **Snow shelter (quinzhee)**: pile of snow, let sinter 2 hours, hollow out; maintains 30°F inside
   regardless of outside temperature
4. **Vehicle**: if available, car is a good emergency shelter; beware of CO from running engine

**Insulation principles:**
- Dead air space is insulation: fur, feathers, down, fiberglass, straw all work by trapping air
- The ground is your biggest heat drain: always insulate from ground before insulating from air
- R-value targets by climate zone: Zone 4 (mid-Atlantic/Midwest): R-40 ceiling, R-20 walls;
  Zone 6 (northern/mountain): R-60 ceiling, R-30 walls; Zone 2 (deep south): R-30 ceiling, R-13 walls

**Vapor barrier:**
- Warm moist air condenses when it hits the cold surface of your wall
- Vapor barrier placement: on the warm side of the insulation (inside face in cold climates)
- Getting this wrong = wet insulation = mold and zero R-value

### 16.2.6 Navigation

**Map reading and compass:**
- Declination adjustment: magnetic north vs. true north varies by up to 20 degrees depending on location;
  look up your local declination (ngdc.noaa.gov) and adjust compass accordingly
- Triangulation: take two bearings to known landmarks; your position is the intersection
- Topographic reading: contour lines, saddles, ridges, valleys; 10 most common topo features
- Practice: navigate 2 miles cross-country with map and compass only (no phone); do this 3 times

**GPS:**
- Download offline maps before entering remote areas (Gaia GPS, OnX Hunt, Avenza)
- Know how to enter coordinates manually when screen is broken
- Battery management: cold kills batteries; keep warm body-side in winter
- Datum awareness: NAD83 vs. WGS84 difference is small but matters for precision navigation

**GPS-free fallback:**
- Pace count calibration: count your paces per 100 meters, use to estimate distance
- Deliberate offset: aim intentionally to one side of target to know which way to turn at road
- Dead reckoning: track bearing and distance traveled; update position estimate every 30 minutes

**Celestial navigation basics:**
- North Star (Polaris): find Big Dipper's outer edge stars, extend 5× to Polaris; due north
- Southern Cross (if south of 25° N): the long axis of the cross points to south celestial pole
- Sun: rises roughly east, sets roughly west; true noon is when it is highest in sky
- Shadow stick: place stick vertically; mark tip of shadow; wait 15 minutes; mark again;
  line between marks is an east-west line; first mark is west

## 16.3 Tier 2: Infrastructure Skills

### 16.3.1 Carpentry and Woodworking

**Why it is Tier 2**: You will need to build structures, repair structures, make
furniture, and improvise solutions continuously. Every off-grid system has wooden
components that require maintenance.

**Tool list for structural carpentry:**

| Tool | Purpose | Cost | Notes |
|------|---------|------|-------|
| Framing hammer (28 oz) | Framing | $30–$60 | Estwing or equivalent |
| Circular saw | Dimensional lumber cuts | $80–$200 | Cordless or corded |
| Speed square | Marking angles | $10–$20 | Essential |
| Level (4-foot) | Plumb and level | $30–$60 | Stabila preferred |
| Tape measure (25 ft) | Measurement | $15–$30 | Fat Max or similar |
| Chisels (1/4", 1/2", 3/4", 1") | Joinery | $40–$80 (set) | Keep sharp |
| Hand saw | Detail cuts, no power | $25–$50 | Crosscut 8 TPI |
| Block plane | Fitting and finishing | $30–$60 | Bench-top tune-up |
| Drill / driver (cordless) | Fastening | $80–$200 | 18V; 2 batteries |
| Miter saw | Accurate cross-cuts | $150–$350 | Optional for basic work |

**Practice project sequence — building competency progressively:**

1. **Project 1 (Weekend 1)**: Build a raised garden bed, 4×8 feet, from 2×8 lumber.
   Skills practiced: measurement, square cuts, fastening, basic joinery.

2. **Project 2 (Weekend 2–3)**: Build a chicken coop for 4–6 birds.
   Skills practiced: framing, roofing (simple lean-to), door hanging, hardware cloth installation.

3. **Project 3 (Month 2–3)**: Build an 8×12 foot storage shed with loft.
   Skills practiced: foundation layout, wall framing, roof framing, roofing, siding, windows.

4. **Project 4 (Month 4–6)**: Build a 200-square-foot addition or outbuilding.
   Skills practiced: all of the above plus load-bearing assessment, beam sizing, permit navigation.

**Joinery reference:**
- **Butt joint**: simplest; weakest; use with screws or nails only
- **Lap joint**: halved surfaces glued and fastened; much stronger
- **Mortise and tenon**: the foundation of timber framing; strongest without hardware
- **Pocket screw joinery**: Kreg jig; fast, strong, good for cabinetry and furniture
- **Dovetail**: extremely strong in tension; furniture drawers and box corners

**Roofing:**
- Metal roofing (standing seam or corrugated): best long-term choice for off-grid; 50-year lifespan;
  supports rainwater collection; requires metal shears or tin snips for cutting
- Asphalt shingles: easier to install; 20-25 year lifespan; adequate
- Minimum roof pitch for asphalt: 4:12 (4" rise per 12" run); for metal: 2:12
- Ice dam prevention in cold climates: air sealing + adequate insulation; not just ice shield membrane

**Books:**
- "Carpentry and Building Construction" by Feirer and Hutchings — the definitive textbook
- "Housebuilding: A Do-It-Yourself Guide" by R.J. DeCristoforo
- "Timber Framing for the Rest of Us" by Rob Roy (New Society Publishers)

### 16.3.2 Plumbing Basics

**Materials overview:**

| Material | Use | Advantages | Disadvantages | Skill barrier |
|---------|-----|------------|---------------|---------------|
| **PEX** | Interior supply lines | Flexible, freeze-tolerant, no soldering | Requires crimp or clamp tools | Low |
| **Copper** | Interior supply, water heater connections | Long-lasting, reliable | Requires soldering; expensive | Medium |
| **CPVC** | Interior supply (hot/cold) | No soldering; easy to cut | Brittle in cold; not freeze-tolerant | Low |
| **Schedule 40 PVC** | Drain/waste/vent, cold supply (non-potable) | Very cheap; easy to work | Not for hot water; not freeze-tolerant | Low |
| **HDPE** | Underground water lines | Extremely durable; UV-resistant | Requires heat fusion or compression fittings | Medium |
| **Galvanized** | Old structures; not recommended for new | Pre-existing systems only | Corrodes; restricts flow | Low (removal) |

**PEX crimping — the most important off-grid plumbing skill:**
1. Cut PEX square with tubing cutter ($15–$25)
2. Slide crimp ring over tube
3. Insert brass fitting fully into PEX
4. Position crimp ring 1/8" from end
5. Crimp at 90 degrees with PEX crimp tool ($50–$150 for manual; $200+ for ratcheting)
6. Check with go/no-go gauge ($10)
7. Test under pressure before insulating or burying

**Copper soldering:**
1. Cut cleanly; remove burr with reamer
2. Clean with emery cloth until shiny
3. Apply lead-free flux to both surfaces
4. Heat fitting (not pipe) with propane torch until flux sizzles
5. Touch solder to joint — solder is drawn in by capillary action, not pushed
6. Wipe with damp cloth immediately
7. Let cool 5 minutes before pressure testing

**Gravity system design:**
- 1 PSI per 2.31 feet of elevation head
- Minimum flow pressure for household: 20 PSI (46 feet of head)
- Comfortable pressure: 40–60 PSI (92–138 feet of head)
- Pipe sizing: friction loss tables determine minimum pipe diameter for flow rate at distance
- Rule of thumb: for gravity-fed homestead at 40-foot head and 50-foot pipe run, 3/4" is minimum

**Pressure testing:**
- Cap all outlets; pressurize to 60–80 PSI with pump
- Wait 30 minutes; any pressure drop indicates a leak
- For PEX, inspect all connections visually while under pressure; look for weeping

**Winterization:**
- All exterior supply lines must be blown out or drained by first freeze
- Use compressed air ($30 blow-out kit) or gravity drain valves at low points
- In-floor radiant: use non-toxic propylene glycol antifreeze in closed loop
- Insulate pipes in unconditioned spaces: foam pipe insulation ($0.30–$0.60 per foot)

**Books:**
- "Plumbing, Heating, and Air Conditioning" by Burkybile and Peel (Craftsman Book Co.)
- "Code Check Plumbing and Mechanical" — quick reference for code compliance

### 16.3.3 Electrical Basics

**DC circuit fundamentals:**
- Ohm's law: Voltage (V) = Current (A) × Resistance (Ω); memorize this
- Power: Watts = Volts × Amps; Amp-hours = Capacity
- Wire sizing: higher current = larger wire required; use wire sizing charts (AWG vs. amps)
- Voltage drop: long wire runs at low voltage (12V) suffer significant loss; use 24V or 48V systems
  for runs longer than 30 feet; or oversize wire

**Battery bank wiring:**
- Series wiring: increases voltage, capacity unchanged (two 12V batteries = 24V)
- Parallel wiring: increases capacity, voltage unchanged (two 12V 100Ah = 12V 200Ah)
- Series-parallel: combine for both; always use identical batteries from same batch
- Balancing: in a series bank, individual cells must be balanced; use a balancer or BMS
- BMS (Battery Management System): required for lithium; protects against overcharge, over-discharge, thermal runaway
- LiFePO4 (lithium iron phosphate): preferred chemistry for off-grid; $200–$400 per 100Ah;
  4,000–6,000 cycle life vs. 300–500 for AGM

**Solar panel installation:**
- Tilt angle: set to latitude for year-round optimization; latitude + 15° for winter bias
- Azimuth: true south in northern hemisphere (not magnetic south; apply declination)
- Shading: even 10% shade on one panel can reduce entire string output by 50% with mismatched MPPT
- MPPT charge controller: must match panel Voc (open circuit voltage) × 1.25 for safety margin
- Wiring: use UV-rated MC4 connectors; torque to specification; weatherproof all junction boxes

**Safety — non-negotiable requirements:**
- **Arc fault protection**: AFCI breakers on any circuit with lithium batteries; arc-fault can start fires
- **GFCI protection**: all circuits in wet locations (kitchen, bath, outdoor, within 6 feet of water)
- **Grounding**: all metal enclosures, panels, battery boxes bonded to earth ground
- **Overcurrent protection**: fuse or breaker within 18 inches of battery positive terminal
- **Disconnect**: lockable main disconnect accessible without tools; required for safe maintenance
- **Labeling**: every circuit labeled; every wire tagged at both ends

**Books:**
- "Photovoltaics: Design and Installation Manual" by Solar Energy International (SEI)
- "12 Volt Bible for Boats" by Miner Brotherton — excellent DC fundamentals despite title
- "Wiring a House" by Rex Cauldwell (Taunton Press) — comprehensive reference

### 16.3.4 Small Engine Maintenance and Repair

**Why it is Tier 2**: Your generator, chainsaw, tractor, and water pump will all
fail eventually. The ability to diagnose and fix common failures extends their
life indefinitely and eliminates the dependence on a mechanic.

**Generator maintenance schedule:**

| Interval | Task |
|---------|------|
| Every 8 hours (or each use) | Check oil level; check air filter; check fuel system for leaks |
| 25 hours | Change oil (break-in period); clean air filter |
| 50 hours | Change oil (ongoing); replace air filter; check spark plug |
| 100 hours | Replace spark plug; replace fuel filter; clean carburetor if hard starting |
| 200 hours | Check valve clearance; inspect carburetor; replace fuel lines if cracked |
| Annually (regardless of hours) | Drain fuel or add stabilizer; fog cylinders if storing; test battery |

**Chainsaw maintenance:**
- Chain sharpening: file at correct angle (30 degrees for most chains; 10 degrees down angle);
  sharpen every 2–3 tanks of fuel or when cutting produces dust instead of chips
- Bar maintenance: rotate bar every 5–10 sharpenings; clean bar groove with tool or screwdriver
- Spark plug: inspect every 10 hours; replace annually; gap to 0.020"
- Air filter: clean after every tank in dusty conditions
- Carburetor adjustment: H (high), L (low), T (idle) screws; adjust starting from factory settings
- Chain brake: test before every use; grasp saw and push wrists forward; brake should engage

**Troubleshooting flowchart:**
```
Engine won't start
├── Has fuel? → No: Add fuel
├── Spark? → No spark: Replace plug; check kill switch wire
├── Compression? → Low: Check valve, head gasket, piston rings
└── Fuel reaching carb? → No: Check fuel valve, filter, primer bulb
    └── Carb blocked: Clean jets; rebuild or replace carb

Engine starts but won't run at load
├── Air-fuel mixture: Check/adjust carb H needle
├── Air filter blocked: Clean or replace
└── Fuel delivery: Check fuel cap vent; check filter
```

**Oil changes — all engines:**
- Use viscosity correct for ambient temperature (SAE 30 for warm; 10W-30 for cold)
- Change while warm (oil flows better; suspends contaminants)
- Always replace drain plug before adding oil (obvious but costly mistake)
- Record oil change in maintenance log with date and hours

**Spare parts inventory (critical to stock):**
- For each gasoline engine: 2 spark plugs, 1 air filter, 1 fuel filter, 1 carb rebuild kit,
  fuel lines, primer bulb, pull cord, starter spring
- For chainsaw specifically: 2 chains per bar, 1 spare bar, guide bar sprocket
- Universal: assorted metric and SAE bolts/nuts, JB Weld, thread-locking compound

**Books:**
- "Small Engines and Outdoor Power Equipment" by Peter Hunn (Cool Springs Press)
- Specific service manual for each engine you own (Stihl, Husqvarna, Honda service manuals
  available as PDFs or from dealers)

### 16.3.5 Welding — MIG Basics

**When you need welding:**
- Cracked equipment frames (trailer, tractor, ATV)
- Fabricating brackets, mounts, tool hangers
- Repairing broken gates, implements, water tank supports
- Making custom tools when nothing commercially available fits

**Safety — non-negotiable:**
- Auto-darkening welding helmet ($40–$200; Lincoln Electric Viking 3350 is excellent at $350)
- Leather gloves; leather jacket or leather sleeves; long pants, no synthetic fabrics
- Fume extraction: weld outdoors or with fan pulling fumes away from face; never weld galvanized
  (zinc fume fever); never weld coated, painted, or plated metal without grinding clean first
- Fire watch: sparks travel 30+ feet; clear combustibles; water or sand within reach

**Equipment:**
- MIG welder: Lincoln Electric 140C or Miller Millermatic 141 ($500–$700) — good entry machines
  for up to 3/16" steel; 120V so runs on standard outlet
- For 1/4"+ steel: need 220V machine ($700–$1,500) or stick welder (simpler, more versatile)
- Wire: ER70S-6 0.030" wire for most mild steel work
- Gas: 75% Argon / 25% CO2 (C25) for clean welds on mild steel; $30–$60 to fill a cylinder

**Basic bead practice:**
1. Set machine to manufacturer's recommended settings for material thickness
2. Drag or push? Drag (backhand) is more common; gives better penetration and visibility
3. Maintain 3/8" arc length (wire-to-work distance)
4. Travel at consistent speed; observe puddle, not the arc
5. Practice on scrap until you can lay a flat, consistent bead with no spatter
6. Test your welds: bend test; grind and inspect penetration

**Structural vs. cosmetic welds:**
- Cosmetic: holds light loads; any visible spatter is acceptable; thin material
- Structural: must have full penetration; multi-pass on thick material; grind and test
- If a structural weld fails and causes injury or death, it is your responsibility
- For structural work on critical loads: weld multiple passes; test by overloading before service

**Books:**
- "Welding Essentials: Questions and Answers" by Galvery and Marlow (Industrial Press)
- Lincoln Electric's free online welding guide (lincolnelectric.com/education)

## 16.4 Tier 3: Food Production Skills

### 16.4.1 Market Garden Planning

**Crop rotation (4-bed system, minimum):**

```
Year 1: Bed A=Nightshade(tomato/pepper), Bed B=Brassica(cabbage/broccoli), 
        Bed C=Root(carrot/beet), Bed D=Legume(bean/pea)+cover crop
Year 2: Rotate 1 position clockwise. Never nightshade after nightshade.
```

**Rationale**: Nightshades are heavy feeders and accumulate soil pathogens (early blight,
late blight, fusarium); brassicas attract different pests and fix no nitrogen; legumes
fix nitrogen; roots improve soil tilth. Four-year rotation breaks pest and disease cycles.

**Companion planting reference:**

| Combination | Benefit | Mechanism |
|------------|---------|-----------|
| Corn + beans + squash (Three Sisters) | Mutual support and yield | Corn trellises beans; beans fix nitrogen; squash shades weeds |
| Tomato + basil | Pest reduction | Basil oils repel thrips and aphids |
| Carrot + onion | Reduced carrot fly | Onion scent masks carrot scent |
| Brassica + dill | Aphid control | Dill attracts beneficial wasps |
| Cucumber + nasturtium | Aphid trap crop | Aphids prefer nasturtium; easy to remove |

**Soil building schedule (annual cycle):**
- **Fall**: Apply 2–4" compost after harvest; plant winter cover crop (winter rye, hairy vetch)
- **Late winter**: Knock down cover crop before it sets seed; leave as mulch
- **Spring**: Broadfork to break compaction without disturbing soil layers; do not till
- **Spring/Summer**: Side-dress heavy feeders (corn, tomatoes, squash) with compost at planting and again 4 weeks later
- **Ongoing**: Compost all kitchen scraps, garden waste; aim for 1 cubic yard of finished compost per 100 sq ft per year

**Spacing and yield table (per 100 sq ft):**

| Crop | Plants per 100 sq ft | Days to harvest | Yield |
|------|---------------------|----------------|-------|
| Tomato (determinate) | 9 | 70–80 | 50–80 lb |
| Potato | 33 (12" apart) | 70–100 | 60–80 lb |
| Dry beans | 100 (every 4") | 90–100 | 10–15 lb |
| Sweet corn | 25 | 70–90 | 25–30 ears |
| Winter squash | 4 (hills) | 80–100 | 60–100 lb |
| Carrots | 400 (every 2") | 60–80 | 25–35 lb |
| Kale | 25 | 50–60 | 40–60 lb |

**Books:**
- "Eliot Coleman's Four-Season Harvest" (Chelsea Green) — Northeast climate focus; excellent rotation system
- "The Market Gardener" by Jean-Martin Fortier (New Society Publishers) — intensive production at scale
- "Building Soils for Better Crops" by Fred Magdoff (SARE) — free download at sare.org

### 16.4.2 Seed Saving

**Open-pollinated vs. hybrid:**
- OP varieties breed true: seeds from an OP tomato will produce the same plant
- F1 hybrids: two distinct parent lines crossed; seeds are genetically unreliable
- Heirloom: OP variety with documented history; not a legal designation
- **Bottom line**: save seeds only from OP/heirloom varieties

**Isolation distances (minimum for home garden):**

| Crop | Pollination | Min. Isolation | Notes |
|------|-------------|----------------|-------|
| Tomato | Self | None needed | Save from interior flowers; little cross-pollination |
| Pepper | Self/insect | 300–500 ft | Hot and sweet should not cross |
| Bean/pea | Self | None needed | Almost no crossing |
| Corn | Wind | 1,000 ft minimum | Cross-pollinates aggressively; must isolate by distance or time |
| Squash | Insect | 1/4 mile to 1 mile | Same species will cross (C. pepo, C. maxima, etc.) |
| Carrot | Insect | 1/4 mile | Will cross with Queen Anne's lace |
| Brassica | Insect | 1/4 to 1 mile | Same species cross easily |
| Lettuce | Self | Minimal | Save from bolt-resistant plants |

**Seed processing and storage:**
- **Wet process** (tomato, cucumber, squash): ferment 2–3 days to remove germination-inhibiting gel;
  rinse; float test (discard floaters); dry on screen, not paper towels
- **Dry process** (beans, corn, peppers): allow to fully dry on plant; thresh; winnow
- Moisture content target: below 8% for storage; seal in glass jar with silica desiccant packet
- Storage: cool, dark, dry; every 10°F temperature decrease doubles seed life
- Longevity: beans and peas 3–5 years; tomatoes 4–6 years; corn 2–3 years; onion/carrot 1–2 years

**Essential resource**: Seed Savers Exchange (seedsavers.org) — catalog, community, and knowledge base

### 16.4.3 Animal Husbandry

**Chickens — the minimum livestock:**

*Feeding:*
- 1/4 lb (4 oz) of layer feed per hen per day; supplement with kitchen scraps and forage
- Calcium supplementation: crushed oyster shell free-choice; essential for strong egg shells
- Grit: if not on pasture, provide insoluble grit (granite chips) for digestion
- Water: 1/2 pint per hen per day minimum; check twice daily in summer; prevent freezing in winter

*Health:*
- Respiratory disease: rattling, discharge, facial swelling → Infectious Bronchitis or Mycoplasma;
  isolate; consult vet or culling if contagious
- External parasites: dust baths prevent mites; check under wings and around vent monthly
- Marek's disease: vaccinate all chicks at hatch; fatal neurological disease, very common
- Coccidiosis: bloody droppings in young birds; treat with Corid (amprolium) in water
- Egg eating: remove eggs 3× per day; use roll-away nest boxes; darken nest boxes

*Molt management:*
- Natural molt in autumn: hens stop laying for 6–12 weeks; protein increase (18–20%) speeds recovery
- Force molt (not recommended): rapid protein reduction and restriction; stressful and controversial
- Lighting to extend season: 14–16 hours light per day suppresses molt; red spectrum bulb in coop

*Egg collection and storage:*
- Collect twice daily; unwashed eggs room temperature up to 3 weeks; refrigerated 3 months
- Wash only just before use; washing removes bloom (cuticle) that protects against bacteria
- Float test for age: fresh sinks flat; old stands up; floaters are bad

**Goats — high-value protein and milk:**
- Nigerian Dwarf or Pygmy: manageable size; good for small operations
- Dairy breeds: Nubian, Alpine, LaMancha; 1–3 gallons/day at peak lactation
- Feeding: quality hay (timothy, orchard grass) free choice; grain only for lactating does
- Hoof trimming: every 6–8 weeks; neglected hooves cause lameness and foot rot
- Breeding: November–January cycle; 150-day gestation; wean kids at 8–12 weeks
- CAE (Caprine Arthritis Encephalitis): test annually; buy CAE-negative stock

**Beekeeping basics:**
- Minimum equipment: two 10-frame deep brood boxes, one medium super, frames, foundation,
  smoker, hive tool, veil ($250–$400 starter kit)
- Package bees: ~$175–$225 for 3-lb package with mated queen; install in April
- Varroa mite management: the single biggest threat to modern beekeeping; test monthly
  with alcohol wash or sticky board; treat with oxalic acid vapor or ApiLife Var when count
  exceeds 2 mites per 100 bees
- Honey harvest: extract when cells are 80%+ capped; 40–80 lb per hive in a good year
- Winter prep: minimum 60–80 lb honey stores in hive; reduce entrance; add insulation to top

### 16.4.4 Hunting and Fishing

**Hunting — legal framework:**
- State hunting license: $15–$50 for residents; required for all hunting
- Hunter Safety Course: required for first-time hunters in all 50 states; 8–16 hours; free or
  low-cost ($10–$20)
- Tag/permit system: some species (deer, elk, moose) require additional tags drawn by lottery;
  apply annually, know your state's deadlines and quotas

**Firearms safety — fundamental rules:**
1. Treat every firearm as if it is loaded
2. Never point at anything you do not intend to shoot
3. Keep finger off trigger until sights are on target and you are ready to fire
4. Know your target and what is beyond it
- Safe storage when not in use: biometric or combination quick-access safe; 100% non-negotiable
  if children are present

**Field dressing (deer):**
1. Bleed animal if not already done (cut throat immediately after harvest)
2. Position on back; make incision from sternum to pelvis without puncturing gut
3. Remove entrails into gut pile; leave offal for wildlife unless keeping heart and liver
4. Remove windpipe and esophagus from forward cavity
5. Prop carcass open for airflow; cool immediately (below 40°F within 2 hours)
6. Age if possible: 1–7 days at 34–38°F dramatically improves meat quality
7. Butcher: debone or keep cuts by muscle group; vacuum seal; freeze

**Catch and preserve (fish):**
- Clean fish within 2 hours of catch; gut and gills out immediately in warm weather
- Smoking: brine (1 cup salt per gallon water) for 2–4 hours; cold smoke 80–90°F for 8–12 hours
  OR hot smoke 145°F+ for food safety; smoked fish stores 1–2 weeks refrigerated, 6 months frozen
- Canning fish: pressure canning only (not water bath); USDA tested recipes; 100 minutes at 10 PSI
- Drying: salt-dried fish (bacalao method) stores 6+ months without refrigeration;
  1 part salt to 3 parts fish by weight; 24-hour brine; dry in sun or dehydrator

### 16.4.5 Wild Food Identification — 30-Plant Standard

**The 30-plant goal**: Know 20 edible plants and 10 medicinal plants well enough
to identify them confidently in the field in multiple seasons. Know their dangerous
lookalikes better than you know the plants themselves.

**High-calorie edibles (beyond the top 10 in §16.2.3):**
11. **Jerusalem artichoke** (Helianthus tuberosus) — starchy tubers; prolific producer
12. **Wild onion/garlic** (Allium spp.) — entire plant edible; smell confirms ID
13. **Black walnut** (Juglans nigra) — high fat, high protein; husks stain skin for weeks
14. **Stinging nettle** (Urtica dioica) — cooking deactivates sting; nutritionally excellent
15. **Hawthorn** (Crataegus spp.) — berries high in pectin; cooked into jelly; heart tonic
16. **Wild grape** (Vitis spp.) — fruit, young leaves; leaves edible raw or cooked
17. **Sumac** (Rhus glabra/copallinum) — tart berries for lemonade-like drink; NOT poison sumac
18. **Wild plum** (Prunus americana) — small, tart; abundant in hedgerows
19. **Ground nut** (Apios americana) — protein-rich tubers; Native American staple
20. **Redbud** (Cercis canadensis) — flowers edible raw; early spring; sweet and nutritious

**Medicinal plants (10 most useful):**
1. **Yarrow** (Achillea millefolium) — wound staunching; antiseptic; fever management
2. **Plantain** (Plantago major) — drawing poultice for splinters, stings, bites
3. **Elderberry** (Sambucus nigra) — antiviral; immune support; flowers are antiseptic
4. **St. John's Wort** (Hypericum perforatum) — nerve pain, mild depression; oil extract
5. **Mullein** (Verbascum thapsus) — respiratory support; leaf tea for cough, bronchitis
6. **Echinacea** (Echinacea purpurea/angustifolia) — immune stimulant; taken at onset of illness
7. **Goldenrod** (Solidago spp.) — anti-inflammatory; urinary tract support; often misidentified as ragweed
8. **Wild ginger** (Asarum canadense) — digestive; warming; NOT same as commercial ginger
9. **Black cohosh** (Actaea racemosa) — menstrual and menopausal support; anti-spasmodic
10. **Lemon balm** (Melissa officinalis/naturalized) — calming; antiviral; digestive

**CRITICAL dangerous lookalikes:**

| Edible | Lookalike | How to distinguish |
|--------|-----------|-------------------|
| Wild carrot (Queen Anne's lace) | Poison hemlock | Hemlock has purple-spotted hollow stem; carrot has hairy stem with purple flower in center of cluster |
| Wild garlic/onion | Death camas | Garlic smells like garlic; death camas does NOT; smell before eating any Allium |
| Elderberry | Pokeweed (young shoots) | Elderberry has compound leaves; pokeweed has simple alternate leaves; check whole plant |
| Chanterelle mushroom | Jack-o'-lantern (toxic) | Chanterelle has forked false gills (ridges not true gills); jack-o'-lantern has true gills; grows in clusters |
| Morel mushroom | False morel | True morel is completely hollow when cut lengthwise; false morel has cottony interior |

**Rule**: Mushrooms require their own course of study. Do not forage mushrooms
from a book alone. Take a guided foray first. The stakes for misidentification are lethal.

**Books:**
- "A Field Guide to Edible Wild Plants" by Lee Allen Peterson (Peterson Field Guides) — eastern US
- "Edible Wild Plants" by Thomas Elias and Peter Dykeman — photo guide, excellent for beginners
- "The Forager's Harvest" by Samuel Thayer — two volumes; the most thorough practical guide available
- "Botany in a Day" by Thomas Elpel — pattern-based plant identification

## 16.5 Tier 4: Advanced and Specialized Skills

### 16.5.1 Medical Skills Beyond First Aid

**Scope caveat**: The following skills are intended for genuine grid-down or
truly remote scenarios where professional medical care is unavailable. In any
situation where professional medical care IS accessible, use it.

**Suturing:**
- Irrigation first: always. A badly sutured contaminated wound is worse than an open clean wound.
- Simple interrupted suture: most versatile; each stitch independent (failure of one doesn't
  open entire wound)
- Stapling: faster for scalp wounds; good for linear lacerations under low tension
- Steri-strips/wound closure strips: adequate for many wounds; preferred for face and areas of low tension
- Learning: "Suturing for Beginners" by Latarsha Haynes MD (Amazon); suture practice kit ($20–$40 on Amazon)
- Never suture: puncture wounds; animal bites (except face); wounds over 12 hours old with signs of infection

**Herbal medicine preparation:**
- **Tinctures**: plant material (fresh or dried) in 80-proof alcohol or higher; 4–6 week extraction;
  ratio 1:5 (1 oz herb per 5 oz alcohol) for dried; 1:2 for fresh
- **Decoctions**: hard materials (roots, bark, seeds); simmer 20–40 minutes in water;
  use 1 tablespoon herb per cup water
- **Infusions (tea)**: soft parts (leaves, flowers); steep 5–15 minutes in just-off-boiling water;
  do not boil — destroys volatile compounds
- **Poultices**: mashed fresh plant applied directly to wound; moistened dried herb works too
- **Salves**: infused oil (herb + carrier oil in jar in warm water bath 4–8 hours) + beeswax ($0.25 per oz)
  to thicken; pour into tins; allow to solidify

**Dental emergency care:**
- Tooth abscess: the most dangerous dental emergency in a grid-down scenario; can become septic
  - Clean: rinse with warm salt water (1 tsp per 8 oz) every 2 hours
  - Antibiotics: amoxicillin 500 mg 3× daily for 7 days; or metronidazole if allergic
  - Drainage: if abscess is pointing (visible pus collection on gum), puncture with sterile lance and evacuate
- Temporary filling: zinc oxide eugenol (Cavit or similar, $8); dry tooth, pack cavity
- Extraction: dental extraction kit ($40–$80); local anesthetic is difficult without prescription;
  topical benzocaine 20% provides some relief; clove oil (eugenol) is a traditional topical analgesic
- Reference: "Where There Is No Dentist" by Murray Dickson (Hesperian Foundation) — free PDF

**References for advanced medical:**
- "Where There Is No Doctor" by David Werner et al. (Hesperian Foundation) — the gold standard; free PDF
- "The Survival Medicine Handbook" by Joseph Alton MD (DOOM and Bloom Press)
- "Wilderness Medicine" by Paul Auerbach MD (Elsevier) — the clinical reference

### 16.5.2 Ham Radio Operation

**License pathway:**
- **Technician**: entry level; covers VHF/UHF (local repeaters, APRS, satellite, line-of-sight);
  35-question multiple choice exam; study 2–4 weeks; $15 exam fee
- **General**: adds HF privileges (shortwave; worldwide communication); 35 additional questions
- **Extra**: full privileges; advanced exam

**Study resources:**
- HamStudy.org — free; adaptive flash cards; excellent pass rate
- ARRL Ham Radio License Manual ($30) — comprehensive with practice exams
- Gordon West's license manuals — organized by topic, easy for beginners
- Local radio clubs typically offer exam sessions monthly; find via ARRL.org club search

**Emergency net protocols:**
- RACES (Radio Amateur Civil Emergency Service): official government-activated net
- ARES (Amateur Radio Emergency Service): volunteer coordinated nets
- Check in to local nets regularly so your call sign is known before an emergency
- Phonetic alphabet: Alpha, Bravo, Charlie, Delta, Echo, Foxtrot, Golf, Hotel, India, Juliet,
  Kilo, Lima, Mike, November, Oscar, Papa, Quebec, Romeo, Sierra, Tango, Uniform, Victor,
  Whiskey, X-ray, Yankee, Zulu — must be memorized

**Operating procedures:**
- Always listen before transmitting
- State your call sign at beginning and end of each transmission (legal requirement)
- Emergency net priority: emergency > priority > welfare > routine
- Tactical traffic: "NET CONTROL, THIS IS [CALL], I HAVE EMERGENCY TRAFFIC"
- Digital modes (WINLINK): email via radio when internet is down; requires basic software setup

**Cross-reference**: Domain 12 for full communications system setup, EMP hardening,
and satellite alternatives.

### 16.5.3 Blacksmithing Basics

**When blacksmithing justifies the time investment:**
- You have abundant wood fuel and no metal supplier nearby
- You need to fabricate custom tools not commercially available
- You want an indefinitely sustainable metalworking capability

**Forge setup:**
- Brake drum forge (DIY): brake drum + pipe + hair dryer or hand blower; $50–$100 in parts
- Commercial propane forge: easier temperature control; $300–$600; uses propane
- Coal forge with firepot: most traditional; requires metallurgical coal (not charcoal)
- Anvil: 100–300 lb cast steel anvil; new $500–$1,500; used anvils at farm auctions $1–$3/lb

**Heat treating basics:**
- Red heat: ~1,400°F — can be bent and forged; too cool for most forming
- Orange heat: ~1,800°F — optimal for most forging
- Yellow-white heat: ~2,200°F — maximum; steel oxidizes rapidly; work fast
- Quenching: plunge rapidly into water or oil to harden; creates brittle martensite
- Tempering: re-heat to 400–500°F (blue-purple color in bright light) to relieve brittleness;
  watch color progression up the steel from the quench point

**First projects (skill sequence):**
1. Make a set of tongs from square stock (practice basic drawing and bending)
2. Forge a punch or drift from hex bar stock (practice working to a point)
3. Make a simple hook from square rod (curves, pickling, surface treatment)
4. Forge a knife blade from an old file (shaping, heat treating, finishing)

**Safety:**
- Scale (iron oxide chips) flies at 1,800°F; always wear safety glasses (not regular glasses)
- Water on scale = steam explosion; keep work area and floor dry
- Slag and clinker in coal forge: clean pot hourly; clinker restricts airflow

### 16.5.4 Soap and Candle Making

**Soap — lye safety:**
- Lye (sodium hydroxide for bar soap; potassium hydroxide for soft/liquid soap) is caustic
- Always add lye to water, never water to lye (exothermic reaction; never in aluminum)
- PPE required: safety glasses, nitrile gloves, long sleeves; keep vinegar nearby to neutralize
- Saponification: lye + fat → soap + glycerin; use a soap calculator to get exact ratios
  (soapcalc.net; brambleberry.com calculator)
- Cold process timeline: mix at 100°F; trace (emulsification); pour into mold; insulate;
  remove after 24–48 hours; cure 4–6 weeks (lye finishes reacting)

**Fat selection:**
- **Tallow** (rendered beef fat): hard bar; long shelf life; close to skin's natural oils; free if
  you butcher cattle
- **Lard** (rendered pork fat): slightly softer; good lather; traditional
- **Coconut oil**: adds lather; creates hard bar; cannot use alone (too drying)
- **Olive oil** (castile): mild, conditioning; slow trace; soft bar; excellent for sensitive skin
- **Combination**: 40% tallow, 30% coconut, 30% olive = balanced, durable bar

**Candle making:**
- Beeswax: highest quality; burns clean; natural honey scent; expensive ($4–$8/lb); melts at 144°F
- Paraffin: cheap ($1–$2/lb); requires petroleum supply chain; adequate
- Rendered tallow candles: dipped or molded; traditional; free if you butcher; smoky if wick is wrong
- Wick sizing: too thin = tunneling; too thick = sooting; test in small batches before large production
- Molds: mason jars, tin cans, silicone; beeswax requires hotter pour temperature than paraffin

### 16.5.5 Leatherworking

**Tanning basics:**
- **Brain tanning** (traditional): every animal has enough brain to tan its own hide;
  work brain paste into flesh side of hide; repeat 3–5 times; smoke to waterproof
- **Bark tanning** (vegetable tanning): long process (weeks to months); uses oak or hemlock bark
  tea; produces the firmest leather for soles and straps
- **Commercial chrome-tanned leather**: available pre-tanned; skip the tanning process and learn
  working/stitching/finishing instead — practical for most homesteaders

**Basic repairs:**
- Saddle stitch (hand stitching): stronger than machine stitch; one stitch cannot run
- Hole punch: 1/8" rotary punch $15; creates clean holes for rivets and stitching
- Edge beveling: prevents edge cracking and delamination
- Riveting: Chicago screws or copper rivets for high-stress points (harness, straps)
- Conditioning: neatsfoot oil or beeswax to prevent drying and cracking; apply 2× per year

### 16.5.6 Natural Building Techniques

**When natural building is appropriate:**
- You have abundant local soil, clay, straw, or stone and limited cash
- You are building in a permitting-exempt jurisdiction (often outbuildings under 200 sq ft)
- You want extreme thermal mass for passive heating/cooling
- You have abundant hand labor and limited mechanical equipment

**Cob:**
- Mixture: clay-heavy subsoil + sand + straw + water; optimal ratio requires testing (jar test)
- Test: mix soil sample in jar of water, shake, let settle; clay settles last; need 15–30% clay
- Application: build in lifts of 6–12"; let cure between lifts to prevent slumping
- Strength: compression-strong; weak in tension; add straw fibers for tensile strength
- Not appropriate for: regions with sustained freeze-thaw cycles; areas with high seismic risk

**Straw bale:**
- Walls are bales of straw (not hay; hay rots; straw is the stalks after grain harvest)
- Two systems: load-bearing (bales carry roof weight; simpler) and infill (timber frame carries load)
- R-value: approximately R-35–50 depending on orientation and plaster thickness
- Moisture is the enemy: keep foundation dry; eave overhang minimum 24"; stucco both sides immediately
- Best in: dry climates with good eave design; problematic in humid climates without excellent design

**Earthbag:**
- Polypropylene bags or tubes filled with subsoil; stacked like brickwork with barbed wire between rows
- Strongest of the earth building methods in compression; can dome and arch
- Excellent for: root cellars, storm shelters, earthen retaining walls
- Book: "Earthbag Building" by Kaki Hunter and Donald Kiffmeyer (New Society Publishers)

### 16.6.5 Practice Schedules

**"Skills degrade without use" is not a metaphor — it is neuroscience.** Motor skills stored

### 16.7.2 The Specialist vs. Generalist Balance

**The failure mode of full specialization**: A community where Alice does all the
medical care and Bob does all the electrical work has created single points of
failure for both. When Alice breaks her leg, the community has no medical response.
When Bob leaves, the solar system cannot be maintained.

**The failure mode of pure generalism**: A community where everyone knows everything
at a low level has no depth. When the complex situation arises — the difficult birth,
the structural weld that must hold, the septic failure during flood conditions — nobody
has the judgment that comes from 500 hours of hands-on experience.

**The balanced model**: Each person develops 2–3 deep skills (Proficient to Expert) and
10–15 competent skills across all critical domains. The deep skills become community
assets; the competent skills provide redundancy.

**Minimum depth requirements by household size:**

| Household size | Minimum deep skills per person | Required coverage |
|---------------|-------------------------------|-------------------|
| 2 adults | 4–5 | All Tier 1 must be at Competent by both |
| 4 adults | 3–4 | All Tier 1 at Competent; Tier 2 split |
| 8+ adults | 2–3 deep; 10+ competent | Full matrix coverage possible |

### 16.7.3 Skill Exchange Agreements

**Formalizing skill barter prevents resentment and ensures delivery.**

A skill exchange agreement includes:
1. What skill/service is being provided
2. Estimated hours or deliverable (not just "I'll help you some day")
3. What is given in return (labor hours, product, another skill)
4. Timeline commitment
5. Renegotiation clause if scope changes

**Example exchange agreements:**
- "I will help you install your solar array (4 days of electrical labor) in exchange for
  you training me to Competent in chainsaw maintenance (3 practice sessions this fall)"
- "I will teach your family pressure canning (one full weekend workshop) in exchange for
  1/4 share of the harvest from the section of market garden I'm helping you build"
- "I will provide first aid at your community events (annual commitment) in exchange for
  access to your smithy and mentorship in blacksmithing (10 sessions)"

**Cross-reference**: Domain 13 §13.5 for formal barter ledger systems and time bank protocols.

### 16.7.4 Teaching and Knowledge Transfer

**Why structured knowledge transfer matters**: Informal transfer is lossy. The details
that seem obvious to the expert are the details the novice needs most — they are exactly
the things the expert stopped noticing years ago.

**Structured transfer protocol for any critical skill:**
1. **Demonstrate**: expert performs complete task while apprentice observes and takes notes
2. **Narrate**: expert repeats while explaining each step verbally, including "why" not just "what"
3. **Guided practice**: apprentice performs with expert watching; expert corrects in real time
4. **Solo practice with review**: apprentice performs independently; expert reviews result
5. **Test**: apprentice teaches back to expert; expert verifies understanding
6. **Document**: apprentice writes the procedure from memory; expert reviews and corrects

**What to document in written knowledge transfer (for any critical system):**
- Who installed or built it and when
- Model numbers, serial numbers, key specifications
- Normal operating parameters (what does "normal" look like?)
- Maintenance schedule and procedure
- Most common failures and their symptoms
- Emergency shutdown procedure
- Contact and sourcing information (where to get parts)

### 16.7.5 Succession Planning for Critical Skills

**The 10-year question**: If the most skilled person in each critical domain left tomorrow,
what would break? That is your single-point-of-failure inventory. Succession planning closes
those gaps before the departure happens.

**Succession planning steps:**
1. Identify the person currently holding each critical skill at highest proficiency
2. Confirm there is a trained backup at Competent or above
3. If not: immediately begin cross-training a backup
4. Document the skill in enough detail that a Competent person can maintain (not rebuild) the system
5. Annual review: skills rotate, people leave, circumstances change

**The "what if" drill** (run annually in any community of 4+ people):
- Draw two names randomly from a hat
- Ask: "If these two people left tomorrow, what systems would be compromised?"
- Any compromised Tier 1 or Tier 2 system is a cross-training priority for the next 6 months

## 16.8 Skill Degradation and Maintenance

### 16.8.1 Why Skills Degrade

**The dentist-drill analogy**: If you had dental surgery once, five years ago, you cannot
perform it today without extensive retraining. Skills stored in procedural memory are maintained
by use; dormant skills fade. This is not a character flaw — it is how human neuroscience works.

**Degradation rates vary by skill type:**

| Skill type | Degradation rate without practice | Notes |
|-----------|----------------------------------|-------|
| Declarative knowledge (facts, procedures) | Moderate; refreshable by reading | Refreshable but not executable |
| Complex motor skills (bow drill, welding bead) | Fast — significant loss in 3–6 months | Full degradation in 1–2 years without practice |
| High-stress procedural skills (CPR, tourniquet) | Fast under stress; techniques drift | Annual certification essential |
| Cognitive-procedural skills (navigation, electrical) | Moderate; slows with complexity | Practice quarterly |
| Physical conditioning | Fast — measurable loss in 2–4 weeks without training | Requires weekly maintenance |

## 16.9 Children and Education

### 16.9.1 Age-Appropriate Skill Development

Off-grid living provides an unmatched environment for practical education.
The work is real, the feedback is immediate, and the skills are applicable
for life. The challenge is structuring development age-appropriately so
children are genuinely capable without being pressured beyond their
developmental readiness.

**Ages 5–7 — Foundation skills (supervised; learning by participation):**
- Garden: identify 5–10 common vegetables; plant seeds; pull weeds; harvest
- Animals: collect eggs; fill waterers; observe animal care with adult
- Cooking: measure and mix; simple prep tasks; understand where food comes from
- Safety: know basic fire stop-drop-roll; understand why water from the creek is not safe to drink;
  know where the first aid kit is; learn home phone number and address
- Nature: identify 10 common local plants (including 3–5 edibles); name 10 local birds or animals
- Tools: proper grip on hand tools; hammering nails in scrap wood; nothing with engine or blade

**Ages 8–11 — Building competency (supervised but capable):**
- Garden: plan a small bed; apply basic companion planting; save seeds from 2–3 crops
- Animals: primary care of chickens (feed, water, collect eggs) with adult oversight
- Cooking: complete simple meals from scratch; understand food preservation basics;
  participate in canning with adult
- Safety: CPR basics (not certified; awareness); recognize and respond to minor injuries; call for help
- Tools: hand saw, hammer, basic carpentry; fire starting (matches and lighter); learn knife safety
- Navigation: read a basic map; use a compass; know cardinal directions
- Nature: identify 15 edible plants; 10 common trees; read basic weather signs
- Household systems: know how to check generator oil; know how to shut off the main water valve

**Ages 12–15 — Genuine capability (guided independence):**
- Garden: manage a significant plot independently; plan full crop rotation; full seed saving
- Animals: independent management of poultry; assist with larger animals
- Cooking: full meal planning and preparation; water bath canning independently; assist with pressure canning
- First aid: complete Stop the Bleed training; basic first aid certification
- Tools: use circular saw and drill under supervision; sharpening; basic welding safety awareness
- Navigation: navigate cross-country with map and compass; use GPS with offline maps
- Communications: study for Technician ham radio license; get licensed
- Electrical: understand DC circuits; assist with solar system maintenance
- Nature: 25 edible plants; 10 medicinal plants; dangerous lookalikes mastered

**Ages 16+ — Near-adult capability (increasing independence):**
- Full Tier 2 skill acquisition: can perform carpentry, plumbing, basic electrical
- First aid: Wilderness First Aid certification; Stop the Bleed trained
- Ham radio: Technician licensed; working toward General
- Garden: fully independent management of major food production
- Animals: full care including health management, breeding decisions, processing
- Food preservation: fully independent across all methods
- Vehicle and equipment: basic maintenance and troubleshooting with guidance
- Teaching: begin teaching younger children skills they have mastered

### 16.9.2 Homeschooling Resources

**Legal landscape**: Home education is legal in all 50 US states, though requirements
vary from "no notification required" to "structured curriculum and annual assessment."
Know your state's requirements before beginning.

**Resources by subject:**

| Science | Noeo Science curriculum | $50–$150/year | Literature-based; no textbooks |

**Integration principle**: On a functioning off-grid homestead, practical skills ARE

and soap making is chemistry. The biology of animal husbandry is biology. The history
of traditional crafts is history. Formal academic subjects should reinforce and be
illustrated by the practical work happening around the child every day.

### 16.9.3 Making Learning Practical and Engaging

**The problem with purely book-based education on a homestead**: Children on a
homestead see real work happening constantly. Abstract academic exercises feel
meaningless by comparison. The solution is not to eliminate academics — it is to
anchor academics in the real work.

**Practical integration examples:**
- Calculate how much feed the chickens will need for the next 30 days before placing an order
- Write a narrative description of how to process a chicken — covers writing, biology, and skill documentation
- Map the garden and calculate square footage; plan next year's planting on graph paper
- Track the weather for one month; graph temperature and precipitation
- Write a persuasive essay arguing for or against a new garden technique
- Learn fractions and percentages by scaling a canning recipe up or down
- Study electricity by wiring a simple 12V circuit with LED, switch, and battery

**The apprenticeship principle for teenagers**: By 14–16, a motivated teenager
can apprentice in real work with a mentor in the community. The goal is not to
deprive them of a childhood — it is to recognize that meaningful, respected work
IS an enriching childhood. Adolescents need to matter; off-grid living gives them
real ways to matter.

## 16.10 Physical Fitness and Mental Resilience

### 16.10.1 Baseline Fitness Requirements

**What off-grid life actually demands (honest assessment):**

| Task | Physical demand | Fitness requirement |
|------|----------------|---------------------|
| Firewood splitting (2 hours) | Upper body strength, cardiovascular | Moderate |
| Hand-digging (post holes, trenching) | Full body strength, endurance | Moderate–High |
| Roof work | Balance, upper body, cardiovascular | Moderate |
| Carrying water (5-gallon buckets) | Grip strength, carrying endurance | Moderate |
| Scything or hand-harvesting | Endurance, rhythm | Moderate |
| Livestock handling | Variable; reactive strength for large animals | Moderate |
| Emergency response (carrying injured person) | High; 100–200 lb carry for 50+ yards | High |
| Extended outdoor work in heat | Heat tolerance, cardiovascular | Moderate–High |

**Minimum baseline (what you need to function, not excel):**
- Cardiovascular: Walk 3 miles with a 20-lb pack without stopping or difficulty
- Strength: Carry 50 lb awkward load (like a bag of feed) 100 yards
- Endurance: Work physically for 4 hours with a 30-minute break
- Flexibility: Bend, squat, kneel, and rise without significant difficulty

**Honest note**: You do not need to be an athlete. Most off-grid work is moderate,
sustained effort — not high intensity. Chronic conditions are more limiting than
simple fitness. If you have chronic pain, cardiovascular disease, or orthopedic
issues, design your homestead around your limitations rather than hoping fitness
will compensate.

### 16.10.2 Functional Fitness Training

**Aligned with off-grid demands — no gym required:**

**Strength:**
- Deadlift or heavy carry: stacking wood, moving equipment, carrying animals — practice with real loads
- Overhead pressing: moving materials to roof, placing items on high shelves
- Grip work: axe swinging, hand tool use, bucket carrying — develops naturally with work

**Cardiovascular:**
- Walk or hike with a pack: most functional cardio for homesteaders
- Rucking: walking with 20–45 lb pack; used by military for sustained endurance
- Cycling or rowing if joint limitations prevent walking

**Mobility:**
- Hip flexor and piriformis stretching: prevents lower back injury from repetitive bending
- Rotator cuff strengthening: prevents shoulder injury from axe work and overhead lifting
- Daily: 10 minutes of movement; squat and hip hinge patterns

**Work capacity:**
- The best training for homestead work is homestead work. Build up gradually.
- Week 1: 2 hours of physical work per day; Week 4: 4 hours; Week 12: 6–8 hours
- Do not go from sedentary to full homestead work in one season — injury is guaranteed

### 16.10.3 Mental Health in Isolation

**The most underrated risk in off-grid planning.** More intentional communities fail
from interpersonal and psychological collapse than from any technical failure. More
solo homesteaders give up from loneliness, depression, or burnout than from any
practical obstacle.

**Loneliness:**
- Rural isolation significantly increases depression risk; research shows physical health effects
  comparable to smoking 15 cigarettes per day
- Mitigation: scheduled regular contact with people outside the homestead; community meals and events;
  phone/radio connections; in-person town visits as a deliberate practice, not an afterthought
- Build community before you need it. The time to form relationships is not during a crisis.

**Seasonal depression (SAD):**
- Affects 5% of Americans; subclinical seasonal mood changes affect far more
- Risk factors in off-grid context: reduced light in winter (especially if cloudy region);
  reduced social contact; reduced activity options
- Mitigation: light therapy lamp ($30–$80; use 30 minutes in morning); maintain exercise through winter;
  vitamin D supplementation (2,000–4,000 IU/day in winter, especially at high latitudes);
  plan winter social events proactively

**Burnout:**
- The off-grid motivation curve: initial excitement → increasing competence → grinding plateau → crisis
- The plateau phase is when most people quit or conflicts peak
- Prevention: build rest into the rhythm from the beginning; take one full day off per week
  (this is not a luxury; it is maintenance); celebrate milestones; document progress to see how far you've come

**Conflict escalation:**
- Isolation amplifies conflict; minor irritations become major ones with no relief
- Prevention: clear governance structure (see domain 13); agreed conflict resolution protocol before
  conflict arises; individual autonomy and private space are non-negotiable
- The two most common sources of conflict in intentional communities: labor imbalance and money
- Structure both explicitly; do not rely on goodwill to cover structural problems

### 16.10.4 Stress Management and Recovery

**What high-stress events do to cognition**: Under acute stress, the prefrontal cortex
(executive function, judgment) is partially offline. You will make worse decisions than
you think you are making. Protocols and checklists exist precisely for this reason —
they replace compromised real-time judgment with pre-committed good judgment.

**Practical stress management:**
- Recognize stress physiological signals: elevated heart rate, tunnel vision, time distortion, shaking
- Breathing technique: 4-7-8 breath (inhale 4 counts, hold 7, exhale 8); activates parasympathetic
- Physical activity is the most evidence-based stress reduction intervention available
- Sleep: chronic sleep deprivation compounds stress; enforce sleep as a system requirement
- Debrief after events: what happened, what worked, what to do differently — without blame

### 16.10.5 Dealing with Failure

**Crops fail.** Systems break. Animals die. People get hurt. People leave. Plans that
took years to build collapse in an afternoon. This is not pessimism — it is the nature
of the endeavor. The question is not whether failure will occur but how you respond.

**The resilient mindset:**
- Failure is data. What does it tell you? What will you do differently?
- Emotional processing and practical problem-solving are not the same phase. Do them separately.
- Document failures with the same rigor as successes. The failure log is often more valuable.
- Distributed systems fail smaller: one crop failure in a diversified garden is manageable;
  one crop failure in a monoculture is catastrophic

**The recovery protocol after any significant failure:**
1. Safety first: is anyone hurt? Any ongoing physical hazard?
2. Stop the bleeding: what needs to be done immediately to prevent further loss?
3. Assess: what was lost? What remains? What is the actual impact on the homestead's function?
4. Plan: what is the path forward? (Not "fix what broke" — what does success look like now?)
5. Debrief: what caused this? Was it foreseeable? What would have prevented it?
6. Document: write it down; it will prevent the same failure next time

## 16.11 Documentation and Knowledge Management

### 16.11.1 What to Document

**Underdocumented systems create emergencies when the person who built them is absent.**
Every system that took skill to build requires documentation that allows a competent
person to maintain it without the builder present.

**Priority documentation list:**

| Document | Contents | Format | Update frequency |
|---------|----------|--------|-----------------|
| Water system map | Source, pipe routes, valve locations, filter schedules | Paper + digital | When system changes |
| Electrical schematic | Panel layout, circuit breaker map, battery bank wiring | Paper + laminated | When system changes |
| Maintenance log | All maintenance performed, by system, with date and hours | Physical binder | After every task |
| Crop records | What was planted where, yield, pest/disease notes | Physical journal | Each season |
| Animal health records | Vaccinations, illnesses, treatments, breeding dates | Physical binder per animal type | Monthly |
| Medical records | Vaccinations, medications, known allergies, chronic conditions | Waterproof folder per person | Annually and after events |
| Seed inventory | What seeds on hand, year packaged, viability test results | Spreadsheet + paper backup | Each fall/winter |
| Food storage inventory | What is stored, quantity, expiration, location | Updated spreadsheet | Monthly |
| Emergency contacts | Community members, nearest hospital, veterinarian | Laminated card, posted | Annually |
| Skill matrix | Community skill inventory (see §16.7.1) | Binder | Annually |

### 16.11.2 Paper vs. Digital vs. Both

**The off-grid answer is: both, with paper as primary.**

A solar system can fail. A hard drive can crash. A tablet can break. But a three-ring
binder in a waterproof sleeve, stored in a cool dry location, will function after an
EMP, after a flood (if sealed), after a power outage, and after a computer failure.

**Digital advantages:**
- Searchable
- Easy to update
- Can be backed up to multiple locations
- Better for complex spreadsheets (inventory, calculations)

**Paper advantages:**
- Functions without power
- Functions without software
- Harder to accidentally delete
- Readable without screen
- Survives EMP if stored (not in a powered device)

**Recommended system:**
- Primary documentation: physical binders; organized by domain; waterproof sleeves for critical pages
- Digital backup: encrypted flash drives in Faraday bag; offline laptop with full documentation
- Cloud backup: encrypted; useful only while internet and power exist; not primary
- Update policy: update digital first; print and replace physical pages quarterly

### 16.11.4 Digital Archiving for Offline Use

**Kiwix** (kiwix.org): download entire Wikipedia (90 GB full; 40 GB English) to local hard drive;
works completely offline; free; runs on Windows, Mac, Linux, and Android.

procedures; more clinically detailed than the main Wikipedia.

Historical farming and craft books no longer in copyright are often excellent.

**Setup:**
- Dedicated offline laptop or Raspberry Pi 4 ($50–$80) with 256 GB+ storage
- Kiwix desktop installed; key archives downloaded
- Store in EMP-protected case (antistatic bag + metal enclosure)
- Connect to local homestead network (no internet required after setup)
- Solar-charged battery for power independence

## Summary: The Five Principles

**1. Skills are the system that makes all other systems work.**
Every piece of equipment in this guide is only as reliable as the person operating it.
The solar array, the well, the root cellar — all of them depend on humans who know
what they are doing. Investing in skills is not supplementary to building physical
infrastructure. It is the same thing.

**2. Depth in Tier 1; breadth across all tiers.**
The design goal is two people at Proficient in every Tier 1 skill, and at least one
person at Competent in every Tier 2–4 skill. Not everyone needs to be an expert at
everything. Everyone needs to be capable at the things that prevent death.

**3. Practice is not optional — it is how skills exist.**
Knowing the procedure is not the same as holding the skill. The difference is
practice — hands-on, repetitive, feedback-rich practice conducted before you need
the skill, not during the emergency that requires it. Build your drill schedule
and defend it the same way you defend your food and water systems.

**4. Knowledge must survive the person who holds it.**
Documentation, cross-training, and succession planning are not bureaucratic overhead.
They are the mechanism by which the community's knowledge outlasts any individual.
A skill held by one person in a community is a single point of failure. A skill
documented, cross-trained, and drilled is a community asset.

**5. The human system is the hardest system to build and the most important.**
Technical problems have technical solutions. A failed crop can be replanted.
A broken pump can be fixed. But a community torn apart by conflict, a household
paralyzed by depression and burnout, a family that gave up because the accumulated
weight of the work outpaced the accumulated satisfaction — these are not solved
by better tools. They are prevented by the human infrastructure built in domains
13, and by the physical resilience and mental health practices in this chapter.
Build the human systems with the same deliberateness you bring to the technical ones.
