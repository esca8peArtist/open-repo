# Domain 3: Water Systems

> Water is the first system to get right and the most likely to kill you if
> you get it wrong. Every other domain in this project depends on reliable,
> clean water. Design this system with redundancy, not optimism.

---

## Quick-Reference Decision Framework

Before reading further, locate yourself in one of these starting positions:

| Starting condition | Primary source | Backup source | Treatment priority |
|---|---|---|---|
| Drilled well, tested clean | Well pump | Hand pump on same well | Annual bacteria/nitrate test; UV if surface contamination risk |
| Drilled well, untested or failed | Well pump | Rainwater or surface | Full treatment chain required |
| Spring on property | Spring box + gravity | Rainwater cistern | Sediment + UV minimum |
| High rainfall, no well | Rainwater primary | Stored reserve | First-flush + sediment + UV |
| Arid region, no reliable rain | Drilled well only option | Deep tank reserve | Per well test results |
| Surface water (stream/pond) | Intake + pump | Rainwater | Full treatment chain, mandatory |

Work through this document section by section in the order your situation demands. A well owner can skip to Section 2 pumping immediately; a rainwater-primary system builder needs Section 1 math before buying anything else.

## 1. Source Selection and Assessment

### 1.1 Drilled Wells

A drilled well is the most reliable off-grid water source in most of the US. The water is pre-filtered by soil and rock, chemically consistent, and not subject to drought the way surface sources are. The main variables are depth, yield, and local geology.

**Depth by region (typical ranges):**

| Region | Typical depth | Geology notes |
|---|---|---|
| Southeast coastal plain (GA, FL, SC) | 50–200 ft | Sand and limestone; easy drilling |
| Midwest (IA, IL, IN, OH) | 80–300 ft | Glacial till over bedrock; consistent |
| Appalachian (WV, PA, eastern KY) | 100–400 ft | Fractured rock; yield can be low |
| Great Plains (KS, NE, SD) | 100–500 ft | Ogallala aquifer; declining water table |
| Southwest (AZ, NM, NV) | 200–1,000 ft | Deep aquifers; expensive to reach |
| Pacific Northwest (WA, OR west of Cascades) | 50–200 ft | Abundant but often shallow and contaminated |
| Rocky Mountain states | 100–600 ft | Highly variable; hard rock common |
| Texas Hill Country | 150–500 ft | Edwards and Trinity aquifers; harder rock |

These are ranges, not guarantees. Buy the hydrogeological survey for your county from USGS before drilling — it costs nothing and can save you from drilling in the wrong spot.

**Drilling costs (2026):**

Drilling costs vary by geology more than by region. Soft formations (sand, unconsolidated sediment) run $25–40/ft. Sedimentary rock runs $35–55/ft. Hard crystalline rock (granite, quartzite) runs $55–85/ft. These are drill-only costs.

Total well system costs including casing, well development, pump, pressure tank, and electrical hookup:

| Component | Cost range |
|---|---|
| Drilling and casing (per foot, all-in) | $25–$65/ft |
| Pump installation (submersible electric) | $800–$2,500 |
| Pressure tank (20–44 gal) | $200–$600 |
| Electrical wiring to well head | $500–$2,000 |
| Well cap, sanitary seal, pitless adapter | $200–$500 |
| Permit and inspection | $200–$1,500 |
| **Complete system, 200 ft well, typical** | **$8,000–$18,000** |
| **Complete system, 400 ft well, hard rock** | **$18,000–$35,000** |

These numbers assume a hired driller. There is no DIY option for drilled wells above about 50 feet — the drilling rig alone costs $50,000+.

**Well yield testing:**

Never accept a driller's word on yield. Demand a pump test. A standard pump test runs the well for 4–8 hours at a known GPM and measures the drawdown (how far the water level drops). Key outputs:

- **Static water level**: depth to water at rest
- **Pumping water level**: depth to water during pumping
- **Sustainable yield (GPM)**: how much can be drawn without running dry
- **Recovery rate**: how fast the level returns after pumping stops

Minimum acceptable yield for a homestead with livestock and irrigation: 3–5 GPM sustained. For drinking and household use only: 1 GPM is workable if you have adequate storage. At less than 0.5 GPM, you need a storage-and-draw system rather than direct pumping.

**Reading the driller's log:**

The driller's log (required by most states) records the formation encountered at each depth. Look for:
- Layers marked "sand and gravel" or "fractured rock" — these are your water-bearing zones
- "Tight" or "dense" rock between water zones — these are low-yield sections
- Depth at which water was first encountered — the "first water strike"
- Final casing depth vs. open (screened) section at bottom

If the log shows water first hit at 60 ft but the driller went to 300 ft before stopping, ask why. Sometimes that means the shallow water was contaminated or insufficient. Get the explanation in writing.

**Well casing and sanitary seals:**

Minimum 6-inch diameter steel or Schedule 40 PVC casing is required in most states. The top must be grouted 20–30 ft below surface to prevent surface water infiltration (the leading cause of coliform contamination in wells). A pitless adapter allows the water line to exit below the frost line without breaching the casing. If your well lacks a pitless adapter, this is a contamination risk that must be fixed.

### 1.2 Spring Development

A spring is groundwater emerging naturally at the surface. A well-developed spring can provide gravity-fed water with no pump and no power — the most resilient possible system.

**Assessment first:**

Before developing, confirm:
1. The spring flows year-round. Visit in late summer (driest period) and early spring (high flow) to measure both extremes.
2. The water doesn't smell of sulfur (H2S), doesn't have visible iron staining (red/orange), and doesn't have a sheen.
3. The source is upgradient from any septic systems, livestock areas, or outhouses.

Minimum acceptable flow for primary water source: 0.5 GPM continuously (720 gallons per day). To measure flow, time how long it takes to fill a known volume at the seep point.

**Spring box construction:**

A spring box collects and protects the water at the source. Basic design for a seep spring:

1. Excavate 2–3 feet into the hillside upslope of where water is emerging. You are looking for saturated gravel or fractured rock.
2. Place a perforated collection pipe (4-inch PVC) in a gravel bed (washed 3/4-inch river gravel) running horizontal across the seep zone. Wrap in geotextile fabric to prevent fine sediment infiltration.
3. Route the collection pipe to a concrete or HDPE spring box (minimum 50-gallon capacity). The box provides a settling chamber.
4. Install three pipe penetrations in the box:
   - **Outlet pipe** at the bottom (or low on the side) — goes to your storage or distribution system
   - **Overflow pipe** 6 inches from top — diverts excess during high flow; route to daylight away from the box
   - **Cleanout/drain** at the very bottom with a valve
5. Install a tight-fitting, locking lid. Cover the entire excavation with compacted clay or bentonite to prevent surface water from entering. Mound soil so water drains away from the spring box.
6. Install a fence 100 feet around the spring box to exclude livestock.

**Gravity distribution from a spring:**

Gravity-fed means no pump, no electricity, but requires the source to be above the use point. The math:

- Every 2.31 feet of elevation head = 1 PSI of pressure
- Typical household minimum working pressure: 20 PSI (requires 46 ft of elevation difference)
- Comfortable working pressure: 40–60 PSI (requires 92–138 ft)
- If your spring is only 10 feet above your house, you have 4.3 PSI — adequate for gravity-fed gravity-discharge fixtures (livestock tanks, garden irrigation) but not adequate for a household shower without a pump boost

If elevation is insufficient for household pressure, store in a cistern at the spring and add a small 12V pump to boost to the house. Still far more reliable than a single-pump system because the cistern provides buffer.

**Yield math for a spring-primary system:**

Your daily demand divided by your spring's minimum daily flow gives you the buffer you need in storage.

Example: 4 people at 25 gallons/day each = 100 gallons/day demand. Spring minimum yield in late summer = 0.5 GPM = 720 gallons/day. You have 7x more supply than demand at minimum. Storage of 500 gallons provides 5 days buffer against the spring temporarily running dry.

### 1.3 Surface Water (Streams and Ponds)

Surface water is available on many rural properties but requires the most aggressive treatment of any source. Never use surface water as a primary source without a full treatment chain.

**Legal access:**

Before drawing from any surface water, confirm your water rights (see Section 1.5). In most western states, riparian presence does not give you the right to divert surface water. In eastern riparian-doctrine states, you generally can use adjacent water but not impair other users downstream.

**Intake design:**

1. Site the intake away from livestock crossings, septic field discharge zones, and stagnant areas.
2. Use a screened intake (1/16-inch stainless mesh minimum) to exclude debris, fish, and aquatic vegetation.
3. Place the intake at mid-depth in a stream — not at the very bottom (sediment) or very top (floating debris, temperature swings).
4. Install a foot valve (check valve) at the intake to maintain prime in the suction line.
5. Bury the supply line below frost depth.

**Seasonal variability:**

In most of the US, streams run highest in spring (snowmelt or spring rains) and lowest in late summer. Size your storage for the low-flow period. A stream that runs 50 GPM in April may be a trickle or dry in August.

**Treatment requirements for surface water:** See Section 4, Treatment Chain. Surface water requires: pre-sediment screen + 5-micron filter + 1-micron filter + UV sterilization + regular testing. For drinking water from ponds or slow-moving water with risk of Giardia or Cryptosporidium, add a 0.5-micron absolute-rated hollow-fiber membrane filter (Sawyer or equivalent). UV alone does not reliably kill Cryptosporidium at standard doses.

**Ponds:**

A constructed or natural pond provides large-volume storage with gravity feed potential. Minimum viable drinking-water pond:
- Volume: 100,000+ gallons to provide adequate dilution and treatment buffer
- Inlet water quality: no upstream agricultural runoff (pesticides, nitrates), no feedlot drainage
- Setback from septic: 300 feet minimum
- Surface water is subject to algae blooms (cyanobacteria can produce hepatotoxins), bird feces, runoff, and temperature variation. Treat accordingly.

Ponds are much better suited to irrigation and livestock water than drinking water. Do not make a small backyard pond your household water primary.

### 1.4 Rainwater Harvesting

In high-rainfall areas, rainwater harvesting can supply all household needs. In arid areas, it is a useful supplement or emergency reserve. The math is straightforward.

**Collection formula:**

```
Gallons collected = Catchment area (sq ft) × Rainfall (inches) × 0.623 × Efficiency factor
```

The 0.623 constant converts inch-feet to gallons. Efficiency factor accounts for losses from evaporation, first-flush diversion, and splash: use 0.85 for metal roofs, 0.75 for asphalt shingle, 0.70 for wood shake. Avoid roofs with lead flashing, asbestos, or treated wood for drinking water catchment.

**Example calculations:**

| Scenario | Catchment | Annual rainfall | Collection formula | Annual yield |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Small cabin, Pacific NW | 800 sq ft | 50 in/yr | 800 × 50 × 0.623 × 0.85 | 21,182 gal/yr |
| Large home, Southeast | 2,000 sq ft | 55 in/yr | 2,000 × 55 × 0.623 × 0.85 | 58,200 gal/yr |
| Desert Southwest, AZ | 1,500 sq ft | 12 in/yr | 1,500 × 12 × 0.623 × 0.75 | 8,411 gal/yr |
| Texas Hill Country | 1,500 sq ft | 32 in/yr | 1,500 × 32 × 0.623 × 0.85 | 25,423 gal/yr |

Typical household water demand (4 people, indoor use only, no irrigation): 50–80 gallons/day = 18,250–29,200 gallons/year. Compare your calculated yield to your expected demand. The Pacific NW cabin and Southeast home examples above are self-sufficient on rainwater alone. The desert Southwest example is not — at 8,411 gallons/year vs. 18,250 minimum demand, you have less than half what you need.

**First-flush diverters:**

The first water off a roof after dry weather is the dirtiest — bird feces, dust, leaf debris, and particulates concentrate on roof surfaces between rain events. A first-flush diverter automatically discards this water before routing cleaner water to storage.

Sizing rule: discard 1 gallon per 100 square feet of catchment area per flush event.

For a 1,500 sq ft roof: discard first 15 gallons. A 4-inch standpipe approximately 8 feet tall holds about 6 gallons — use two in parallel, or a larger diameter pipe. Commercial first-flush diverters ($40–$120 each) are available; DIY versions from PVC are straightforward and cost $15–$30.

**Tank sizing for seasonal systems:**

In areas with distinct wet and dry seasons, you must store enough water during the wet season to carry through the dry. The sizing calculation:

1. Determine your driest consecutive period (months with insufficient rainfall to meet demand).
2. Calculate total demand for that period.
3. Size your tank(s) to that demand plus 20% safety margin.

Example (Central Texas): Dry season May–August (4 months). Demand 60 gallons/day × 120 days = 7,200 gallons minimum storage. With 20% buffer: 8,640 gallons. Round up to two 5,000-gallon tanks.

Metal roofs (steel, aluminum, copper) are the best catchment surface for drinking water. Avoid: pressure-treated wood, roofs with lead-based paint, roofs near heavy vehicle exhaust. Galvanized metal roofs are acceptable but not ideal — zinc leaches slightly in acidic rainfall.

### 1.5 Water Rights Law

**Prior appropriation states (primarily western):**

The prior appropriation doctrine governs most western states: Arizona, Colorado, Idaho, Montana, Nevada, New Mexico, Utah, Wyoming use it exclusively. California, Oklahoma, Oregon, Texas, Washington use hybrid systems.

The core rule: "First in time, first in right." Water rights are attached to a specific use, at a specific location, at a specific volume, established by seniority. You do not automatically have the right to use water on your land just because a stream runs through it or a spring emerges on it. You must hold a water right.

What this means practically:
- Diverting surface water without a water right is illegal and enforceable, even on your own land
- In severe drought years, senior rights holders can legally shut off junior holders entirely
- Buying land in a prior appropriation state: check the deed and state water rights database for what water rights transfer with the property
- Groundwater rights in western states vary — some states (Colorado, Arizona) also regulate groundwater; others less so

**Riparian states (primarily eastern):**

Eastern states from Minnesota east to the Atlantic use riparian doctrine. Landowners with property adjacent to a watercourse generally have the right to make "reasonable use" of that water, provided they do not unreasonably interfere with other riparian owners.

This is more permissive for most off-grid purposes: if you own land bordering a stream, you can divert water for domestic use. But "reasonable use" has limits — large-scale diversion for irrigation can trigger legal challenges from downstream users.

**Rainwater collection legality by state (2026):**

Collecting rainwater is legal in all 50 states, but restrictions vary significantly:

| Category | States | Restrictions |
|---|---|---|
| Effectively unrestricted | Most eastern states, AL, AK, FL, GA, HI, IL, LA, ME, MI, MS, MN, MO, NC, ND, NH, NJ, NY, PA, SC, SD, TN, VT, WI, WY | No permit or size limit for residential use |
| Size limited but permitted | CO, KS, NE, NV, OH, OK, VA | CO: 110 gal max (2 barrels); others vary |
| Registration required above threshold | UT | Must register system over a certain size |
| Permit and/or code compliance required | AR, CA, GA, ID, NM, OR, WA | State plumbing code compliance; permit for larger systems |
| Incentivized (tax credits) | RI, TX, VA | Equipment tax credits available |

Colorado's 110-gallon limit is the most severe restriction in the US — effectively limiting collection to two rain barrels. This reflects the prior appropriation doctrine taken to its logical extreme: even rain falling on your roof is considered part of the stream system and owned by prior appropriators.

**Bottom line on water rights:**

Before designing your water system, determine:
1. What doctrine governs your state for surface water
2. Whether any water rights transfer with the land you're buying
3. Whether your intended rainwater collection volume is permitted under state law
4. Whether your state regulates groundwater separately from surface water

This due diligence can save you from building an illegal system and should happen before land purchase.

## 2. Pumping Systems

### 2.1 Hand Pumps

A hand pump on a drilled well is the single most important resilience addition you can make to an off-grid water system. When the power is out, the solar charge controller fails, or the submersible pump breaks, a hand pump keeps water flowing.

**Simple Pump:**
- Maximum depth: 325 ft static water level
- Output: approximately 1 GPM at average pumping effort (varies with depth)
- Material: stainless steel cylinder, fiberglass-reinforced drop pipe
- Can be installed alongside existing submersible pump without pulling the submersible
- Cost: $1,500–$3,000 complete installed kit depending on depth; deeper installations require more drop pipe and higher cost. A 200-ft installation typically runs $2,200–$2,800.
- Can be motorized for electric backup operation
- Made in USA, excellent customer support and replacement parts

**Bison Pump:**
- Maximum depth: 250 ft static water level
- Output: approximately 20 oz per stroke at a 3-inch cylinder; roughly 0.8–1.2 GPM with normal effort
- Side-by-side installation with submersible — does not require removing existing pump
- Freeze proof design
- Cost: approximately $1,800 for standard system; deeper installations cost more
- Also made in USA

**Comparison:**

| Feature | Simple Pump | Bison Pump |
|---|---|---|
| Max depth | 325 ft | 250 ft |
| Max depth for motorized option | Yes | No (hand only) |
| Installation with existing pump | Yes | Yes |
| Freeze protection | Yes | Yes |
| Approximate cost (100 ft well) | $1,600 | $1,500 |
| Approximate cost (200 ft well) | $2,200 | $1,900 |
| Made in USA | Yes | Yes |

If your static water level is under 250 ft, either is an excellent choice. If it's between 250 and 325 ft, Simple Pump is the only hand pump option. Both require basic mechanical aptitude to install; two people, one day.

**Do not skip a hand pump.** It is the lowest-cost, most-reliable backup system available. The difference between a homestead that has water during a grid-down event and one that doesn't is often just the $1,500–$2,000 spent on a hand pump.

### 2.2 Solar Submersible Pumps

For primary pumping duty, a solar-powered submersible is the standard off-grid solution.

**Grundfos SQFlex:**
The SQFlex is the benchmark solar submersible pump. It accepts variable DC input (30–300V DC) and variable AC input (90–240V AC), meaning it can be driven directly by solar panels (varying voltage), a battery bank, a generator, or the grid. This flexibility is its primary advantage: it runs at partial power on cloudy days rather than stopping entirely.

Available models:
- 3-inch diameter (fits 4-inch or larger well casing)
- 4-inch diameter (fits 5-inch or larger well casing)
- Flow range: 4.5 GPM at 820 ft head to 65 GPM at 20 ft head (model dependent)
- Maximum head: approximately 820 ft

Typical homestead sizing: SQF 2.5-2 (2.5 GPM at 200 ft head) or SQF 5A-6 (5 GPM at 300 ft head) covers most well depths up to 400 ft. Consult Grundfos WinCAPS or an authorized dealer for precise model selection — input the static water level, pumping rate required, and number of solar panels.

Solar panel sizing for SQFlex:
- Rule of thumb: 100W of solar per 100 ft of total dynamic head per GPM needed
- For a 200 ft well drawing 2 GPM: ~400W minimum solar, 600W recommended for adequate pumping hours
- For a 400 ft well drawing 3 GPM: ~1,200W minimum

A float switch in your storage tank automatically shuts the pump off when the tank is full, protecting both the pump and the well from over-pumping.

Cost: SQFlex pump units run $800–$2,500 depending on model. Add wiring, conduit, float switch, pressure switch, and installation labor: $2,000–$4,500 total for a complete solar pump system excluding the solar panels.

**Alternative solar submersibles:**
- Shurflo 9300 (3/4 HP, 12/24V DC, up to 230 ft, 4.5 GPM): $400–$600; simpler but limited depth
- Lorentz PS series: direct competitor to Grundfos, similar variable-voltage design, $900–$2,500

**Wiring considerations:**

Solar-direct pumping (panels wired directly to pump without a battery) is the simplest configuration but means no pumping after dark or on heavily overcast days. Battery-backed pumping allows 24-hour pumping capability at the cost of battery bank complexity.

For most homesteads with adequate storage (1,000+ gallons), solar-direct pumping is fine: pump during daylight hours into storage, use from storage at any hour. This is the simplest, lowest-cost, most maintainable configuration.

Wire gauge: use the pump manufacturer's wire sizing chart. Undersized wire causes voltage drop, reducing pump performance and generating heat. As a general rule, size wire for less than 3% voltage drop at maximum pump current. For a 24V pump at 300 ft submergence, use 10 AWG minimum for runs under 200 ft.

### 2.3 12V DC Pump Systems for Low-Power Setups

For shallow wells (under 25 ft static level), springs, or cistern transfers, a 12V DC diaphragm or centrifugal pump is a low-cost option.

- Flojet, Shurflo, and SHURflo 12V diaphragm pumps: $60–$200, self-priming, 1–3 GPM, run on any 12V source
- Suitable for: transferring from spring box to cistern, boosting gravity-fed systems, RV/cabin with shallow source
- Not suitable for: deep wells, high-volume use, sustained whole-house duty cycle

A 12V diaphragm pump drawing 5A at 12V uses 60W. A 100Ah battery bank can run it for approximately 20 hours at that draw before requiring recharge. On a small solar system, this is manageable.

**Pressure switch integration:**

Wire the pump through a pressure switch (set to cut in at 20 PSI, cut out at 40 PSI) and a small pressure tank (2–5 gallons). The pump cycles on and off to maintain pressure rather than running continuously. This extends pump life, reduces power consumption, and provides brief flow surge capacity.

### 2.4 Gravity-Fed Systems

The elevation head formula:

```
PSI = Elevation difference (feet) × 0.433
```

Or inverted:

```
Feet of elevation needed = Target PSI ÷ 0.433
```

| Target PSI | Elevation required |
|---|---|
| 10 PSI (gravity livestock tank, garden drip) | 23 ft |
| 20 PSI (minimum for fixtures, some showers) | 46 ft |
| 30 PSI (adequate household pressure) | 69 ft |
| 40 PSI (comfortable household pressure) | 92 ft |
| 60 PSI (high pressure, typical utility) | 138 ft |

Gravity-fed systems require no electricity, have no pump to fail, and provide uninterrupted flow as long as the elevated tank has water. They are silent and have essentially zero operating cost.

Practical requirements for gravity-fed household pressure:
- The storage tank must be physically located 70–100 ft in elevation above the house
- In flat terrain this requires a tower — either a dedicated water tower (expensive: $5,000–$20,000 installed) or an elevated platform (DIY with timber or steel, $1,000–$5,000)
- In hilly terrain, position the cistern on a hillside above the house — natural terrain provides the elevation for free

The most common off-grid gravity setup: spring or rainwater fills a hilltop cistern by natural flow or a small pump; cistern elevation provides 30–50 PSI to the house via gravity with no ongoing energy input.

**Ram pumps:**

A hydraulic ram pump uses the momentum of flowing water (with no electricity) to pump a small fraction of that flow to a higher elevation. They require:
- A flowing stream with at least 3–10 ft of elevation drop over a 10–30 ft run
- Continuous source flow of 1.5–5 GPM minimum

Output: roughly 1/7 to 1/10 of input flow, raised to an elevation 5–10x the drive head. A ram pump with 10 ft of drive head can pump water to 50–100 ft elevation at 0.1–0.5 GPM continuously. This is low output but requires zero energy and runs 24/7. Over a week, 0.2 GPM = 2,016 gallons.

Ram pumps cost $200–$600 for commercial units (Rife, Green and Carter brands) or can be built from PVC fittings for $30–$50. They require periodic valve maintenance (check valve wears) but no external energy input.

### 2.5 Pump Sizing Worksheet

Use this to determine your minimum pump capacity:

```
Step 1: Calculate daily demand (gallons/day)
  - People: __ × 25 gal/day (basic indoor use) = __
  - Livestock: consult demand table below
  - Garden irrigation (drip): __ sq ft × 0.03 gal/sq ft/day = __
  - Total daily demand: __ gal/day

Step 2: Set pumping window
  - Solar: realistic pumping hours per day = 4–6 hrs (sunny climate), 3–4 hrs (cloudy)
  - Battery: 24-hr available, but battery bank limits total energy
  - Choose pumping hours: __ hrs/day

Step 3: Calculate required pump GPM
  - Required GPM = Daily demand ÷ (Pumping hours × 60 min)
  - Example: 200 gal/day ÷ (5 hrs × 60 min) = 0.67 GPM minimum
  - Add 20% safety margin: 0.67 × 1.2 = 0.80 GPM — select next pump size up

Step 4: Confirm pump can lift from your static water level
  - Total dynamic head = Static water level + pumping drawdown + pipe friction + surface elevation
  - Pipe friction: roughly 1–2 ft per 100 ft of pipe for typical flow rates
  - Look up pump curve to confirm selected pump delivers required GPM at that head
```

**Livestock water demand:**

| Animal | Gallons/day |
|---|---|
| Dairy cow | 30–50 |
| Beef cow | 15–25 |
| Horse | 10–15 |
| Pig | 3–5 |
| Goat / sheep | 1–3 |
| Chickens (per 10 birds) | 1 |
| Ducks (per 10 birds) | 2–3 |

A homestead with 2 dairy cows, 4 goats, 20 chickens, and 4 people needs: 80 + 8 + 2 + 100 = 190 gallons/day minimum, not counting irrigation.

## 3. Storage

### 3.1 Sizing the Storage Volume

**Minimum buffers:**

- Drinking and household use: 1,000 gallons per person as a 10-day buffer minimum
- Full homestead (including livestock): size to 2–4 weeks of total demand
- Rainwater-primary system: size to carry through the longest dry period (see Section 1.4)

The "1 gallon per person per day" figure from FEMA and the CDC is a survival-only emergency minimum. For actual off-grid living — cooking, hygiene, toilet flushing — plan on 20–30 gallons per person per day for basic comfort, 40–60 for comfort with laundry and garden.

**30-day reserve calculation:**

4 people at 30 gal/day = 120 gal/day × 30 days = 3,600 gallons. This is your minimum cistern size for a family of four if you want one month of true water independence. For a rainwater-primary or spring-fed system, size storage larger — 5,000–10,000 gallons is not excessive.

### 3.2 Cistern Materials Comparison

| Material | Size range | Cost (installed) | Lifespan | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Polyethylene above-ground | 500–10,000 gal | $600–$4,000 | 20–30 yr | Cheap, easy, food-grade, portable | UV degradation if exposed, must be covered or painted, above-ground freezes |
| Polyethylene underground | 500–20,000 gal | $2,500–$15,000+ | 25–40 yr | Freeze protection, out of way | Excavation cost, harder to inspect, must be traffic-rated if under driveway |
| IBC totes (275 or 330 gal) | 275–330 gal each | $100–$300 used, $400–$600 new | 10–20 yr | Very cheap, stackable, portable | Small volume per unit, must inspect/clean if food-grade history unknown, UV vulnerable |
| Ferro-cement (DIY) | 500–50,000 gal | $0.10–$0.30/gal materials | 50+ yr | Extremely durable, any shape, large capacity possible, DIY-able | Skilled labor required, cures 28 days, cracks if poorly built, hard to relocate |
| Poured concrete (buried) | 1,000–100,000 gal | $1.00–$2.50/gal installed | 50–100 yr | Massive capacity, excellent freeze protection, strongest structure | Most expensive, requires contractor in most cases, can leach lime (lower pH slightly) |
| Fiberglass (buried) | 500–10,000 gal | $1.50–$4.00/gal | 30–50 yr | Corrosion proof, smooth interior | Expensive, UV sensitive if above ground, specialized installation |

**Practical recommendation:**

For most homesteads, 2–3 linked poly tanks totaling 2,500–5,000 gallons covers all needs at reasonable cost ($1,500–$3,500 in materials). Bury or shade them to prevent UV degradation and algae growth. Link them with a manifold pipe so they fill and draw as a single unit.

For very large storage (10,000+ gallons), poured concrete or ferro-cement is more cost-effective per gallon than buying multiple large poly tanks.

**IBC totes note:**

Used IBC totes sold on Craigslist and farm supply stores are commonly $50–$150. Before using for drinking water, verify the previous contents. Food-grade totes that held food-safe liquids (juice concentrate, vegetable oil, soft drink syrup) can be cleaned and used. Totes that held industrial chemicals — even if labeled as rinsed — should not be used for drinking water. The plastics can absorb certain chemicals that don't fully rinse out.

### 3.3 Elevated Tank Gravity Pressure

For an elevated tank system, you need to know what pressure you'll get at each fixture. Use the formula:

```
PSI at fixture = (Tank elevation above fixture, in feet) × 0.433 - friction losses
```

Friction losses in typical 3/4-inch PEX: approximately 1–3 PSI per 100 linear feet at 2 GPM flow.

**Design target:** Position the bottom of the elevated tank at least 70 feet above the highest fixture in the house for a 30 PSI working pressure at that fixture.

For smaller elevation differences (20–40 ft), gravity systems work well for:
- Toilet flushing (requires only 8–10 PSI)
- Outdoor irrigation (10–15 PSI acceptable for drip emitters)
- Low-flow sinks and outdoor wash stations

They do not work well at low head for: showers requiring strong spray, washing machines, dishwashers, or any fixture expecting 40+ PSI.

---

### 3.4 Seasonal Storage for Rainwater-Primary Systems

In seasonal rainfall climates, your tank array must hold enough water from wet season to dry season. This changes the sizing calculus entirely from a simple buffer calculation.

Example: Oregon coast homestead, 4 people, indoor use only (no irrigation from rainwater):

- Wet season (Oct–May): 50+ inches rain, 2,000 sq ft roof, plentiful collection
- Dry season (Jun–Sep): 4 months, 60 gal/day demand = 7,200 gallons needed
- Design approach: fill tanks to 10,000 gallons by end of wet season; draw down through summer

Tank array: Three 3,000-gallon poly tanks = 9,000 gallons, plus the collection system continues to add water during any summer rain events. Size to 12,000 gallons for 20% safety margin.

## 4. Treatment Chain

### 4.1 The Principle of Sequential Barriers

Never rely on a single treatment step. Each step in the treatment chain has a failure mode; sequential barriers mean that when one step fails (and it will), the others maintain safety.

**Treatment path by source type (decision tree):**

```
SOURCE TYPE?
├── Drilled well, tested clean, no surface contamination risk
│   └── Sediment filter (5-micron) → Annual bacteria test → Use
│       Optional: UV sterilizer for extra margin
├── Drilled well, not tested or near surface contamination
│   └── Sediment (5-micron) → Sediment (1-micron) → UV sterilizer → Use
│       Add RO if arsenic, nitrates, or heavy metals detected in test
├── Spring, developed and protected
│   └── Sediment (5-micron) → Sediment (1-micron) → UV sterilizer → Use
├── Rainwater from metal roof
│   └── First-flush diverter → Sediment (5-micron) → Carbon block → UV sterilizer → Use
├── Surface water (stream, pond) — FULL CHAIN REQUIRED
│   └── Coarse screen → Sediment (50-micron pre-filter) → Sediment (5-micron)
│       → Sediment (1-micron absolute or 0.5-micron hollow fiber for Crypto)
│       → UV sterilizer → Carbon block (final taste/odor) → Test quarterly
└── Unknown or suspect source
    └── Treat as surface water above; add RO if any chemical contamination concern
```

---

### 4.2 Sediment Filtration

Sediment filters are rated by micron size — the size of particle they capture. Smaller number = finer filtration = higher flow restriction.

| Filter size | What it catches | Position in chain | Change frequency |
|---|---|---|---|
| 50–100 micron (screen or spin-down) | Sand, gravel, large debris | First, before everything | Clean monthly; replace screen annually |
| 5 micron (spun polypropylene) | Fine sediment, rust, silt | Second | Every 3–6 months depending on turbidity |
| 1 micron (spun or melt-blown) | Very fine particles, some cysts | Third (before UV/carbon) | Every 1–3 months |
| 0.5 micron absolute (hollow fiber membrane) | Bacteria, Cryptosporidium, Giardia | Final barrier if no UV | Per manufacturer; typically 6 months–1 year |

Sequential sizing matters. Running a 1-micron filter without a 5-micron pre-filter will blind the 1-micron filter quickly, requiring much more frequent changes and increasing cost.

Filter housings run $15–$60 for standard 10-inch housings (the most common residential size). Stock replacement cartridges: a 6-month supply minimum.

---

### 4.3 Activated Carbon Filtration

Activated carbon removes:
- Chlorine and chloramines (relevant only if you're on treated municipal water, but sometimes used in rainwater systems)
- Volatile organic compounds (pesticide residues, solvents, petroleum products)
- Taste and odor compounds
- Some heavy metals (lead, mercury) when carbon block type is used

Carbon does **not** remove: bacteria, viruses, nitrates, fluoride, arsenic, or dissolved minerals.

Carbon filters come as:
- **GAC (granular activated carbon)**: loose granules in a housing; good flow rate, less contact time
- **Carbon block**: densely compressed carbon; better contaminant removal, higher flow restriction
- **Catalytic carbon**: specialized for chloramines and hydrogen sulfide removal

For off-grid use, place the carbon block filter after UV sterilization (position: UV → carbon). This removes any taste or odor from the UV treatment and provides final polishing.

Carbon block replacement: every 6–12 months for most off-grid use levels. Spent carbon does not warn you when it's exhausted — replace on schedule, not by taste.

---

### 4.4 UV Sterilization

UV kills or inactivates: bacteria (E. coli, coliform, Salmonella, Legionella), viruses (hepatitis A, norovirus, rotavirus), and protozoa (Giardia) at standard dose. It does not remove Cryptosporidium reliably at standard dose (requires 40 mJ/cm² minimum; many residential units deliver 30 mJ/cm²).

**Sizing by flow rate:**

| Household size | GPM demand | UV unit size | Common units |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1–2 people, 1 bath | 4–6 GPM | 6 GPM | Viqua VH200, Trojan UVMax B4 |
| 3–4 people, 2 bath | 7–9 GPM | 9–12 GPM | Viqua VP600, Trojan UVMax C4 |
| 5–6 people, 3 bath | 10–13 GPM | 12–15 GPM | Viqua VH410 |
| Large homestead | 15+ GPM | 15–20 GPM | Viqua VP950 |

UV unit cost: $150–$600 for residential units. Professional-grade units: $600–$2,000.

**Critical prerequisites:**

UV is rendered ineffective by turbidity. Water entering the UV chamber must be clear. This means the sediment filtration chain (Section 4.2) must come before UV. The UV lamp irradiates the water passing through a quartz sleeve in a stainless steel chamber; if the water is cloudy, the UV is absorbed by particles before it reaches pathogens.

Iron above 0.3 mg/L, manganese above 0.05 mg/L, or hardness above 7 gpg can all reduce UV transmittance. Test your water and address these with pre-treatment (iron filter or water softener) before relying on UV.

**Maintenance schedule:**

- Lamp replacement: annually (even if lamp is still lit — UV output degrades before visible light output fails)
- Quartz sleeve cleaning: every 6 months (iron, hardness deposits reduce UV transmission)
- Quartz sleeve replacement: every 2–3 years or when clouded
- Annual lamp cost: $50–$120 for residential units

Stock spare lamps. A burned-out UV lamp with no spare means you're on untreated water until the new lamp arrives.

---

### 4.5 Reverse Osmosis

RO forces water through a semi-permeable membrane under pressure, rejecting 90–99% of dissolved solids. Use cases for off-grid:

**When RO is necessary:**
- Arsenic above EPA limit of 10 ppb (common in Southwest, Pacific NW, parts of New England)
- Nitrates above 10 mg/L (common near agriculture — animal waste, fertilizer)
- High TDS (total dissolved solids) above 500 ppm — water tastes bad, scale buildup
- Fluoride concerns (activated alumina or RO required)
- PFAS contamination (RO removes 94–99%)

**When RO is overkill:**
- Well water that tests clean for chemicals — UV plus sediment is adequate
- Source water under 200 ppm TDS
- Low-flow systems where waste water is a problem (RO wastes 25–60% of input)

**RO specifications:**
- Typical residential recovery ratio: 40–60% (for every 1 gallon produced, 0.7–1.5 gallons goes to drain as concentrate)
- High-efficiency units: 70–75% recovery
- Output TDS: typically 10–50 ppm from input water of 100–500 ppm
- Flow rate: residential units produce 50–150 gallons per day (not gallons per minute — RO is slow)
- Tank storage is built into residential RO systems (2–5 gallon holding tank)

**For off-grid use:** RO works on 40–80 PSI input pressure. A well pump with pressure tank handles this. Gravity-only systems typically don't provide adequate pressure for RO without a booster pump.

Cost: under-sink RO systems $150–$500. Whole-house RO systems: $1,500–$4,000+.

The waste stream (concentrate) in an off-grid system goes to... where? In a connected house, it goes to the drain. Off-grid, it goes to your septic or greywater system, which must be sized to handle it. At 50% recovery, running 150 gallons/day through RO means 150 gallons/day of concentrate waste going to your septic.

---

### 4.6 Distillation

Water distillation boils water and condenses the steam, leaving behind virtually all contaminants (minerals, heavy metals, pathogens, most VOCs). It is the most thorough purification method available.

Off-grid distillation options:
- **Solar still**: 0.1–1 gallon per day per square foot of still surface; very low output; appropriate for emergency
- **Electric countertop distiller** (Megahome, Pure Water brand): 1 gallon per 5.5 hours; 580W; adequate for drinking water only; $150–$300
- **Propane or wood-fired still**: distillation at any fuel source; can produce 5–20 gallons/day; commercial units $500–$2,000

Distillation removes almost everything, including beneficial minerals. Long-term consumption of distilled water may require mineral supplementation (trace minerals or remineralization filters). The practical role of distillation in an off-grid system is as a backup or finishing step for worst-case water quality, not as a primary treatment method for large volumes.

---

### 4.7 Simple Decision Matrix

| Water quality problem | Treatment solution |
|---|---|
| Bacteria/viruses/Giardia | UV sterilizer (confirm dose 40 mJ/cm²) |
| Cryptosporidium | 0.5-micron absolute hollow-fiber membrane |
| Turbidity (cloudy) | Sediment filtration chain, settling tank |
| Iron/manganese staining | Oxidizing filter (Birm, Greensand) or air injection + sediment |
| Hydrogen sulfide (rotten egg smell) | Aeration + carbon or catalytic carbon filter |
| High TDS / hard water | RO or water softener (softener replaces Ca/Mg with Na) |
| Arsenic | RO point-of-use (for drinking and cooking only is cost-effective) |
| Nitrates | RO (carbon and UV do not remove nitrates) |
| Pesticide/herbicide residues | Carbon block filter; RO for higher concentrations |
| Lead | Carbon block (NSF 53 rated) or RO |
| pH too low (acidic, corrosive) | Calcite neutralizer filter; raises pH and adds calcium |

## 5. Distribution

### 5.1 Pressure Tanks and Pump Cycles

A pressure tank stores a small volume of pressurized water and is the interface between the pump (which runs intermittently) and your household fixtures (which demand water in small amounts continuously).

**How it works:** The tank contains an air bladder pre-charged to a pressure slightly below the pump cut-in pressure (typically 28 PSI for a 30/50 pump switch). When water pressure in the system drops to 30 PSI, the pump kicks on and refills the tank. The pump shuts off at 50 PSI. Between 50 PSI and 30 PSI, the bladder pushes water out to the fixtures. A properly sized tank means the pump cycles no more than 1–2 times per minute maximum.

**Sizing:**

Too small a pressure tank causes the pump to short-cycle (turns on and off rapidly), wearing out the motor. Rule of thumb: minimum 1 gallon of drawdown capacity per GPM of pump flow rate. Most residential submersible pumps produce 5–15 GPM, so minimum pressure tank drawdown capacity should be 5–15 gallons.

Actual tank sizes (total, not drawdown): 20-gallon tanks offer about 5–6 gallons of drawdown. 44-gallon tanks offer about 14 gallons. For most off-grid homesteads, a 44-gallon tank is appropriate. Larger (80–120 gallon) tanks reduce cycling further and provide more buffer.

Pressure tank cost: $100–$350 for 20–44 gallon tanks. Larger tanks: $300–$600.

**Pre-charge pressure check:**

Check the tank's air pre-charge pressure annually (or whenever pump short-cycles). Use a standard tire gauge at the Schrader valve on top of the tank with system pressure bled down. Pre-charge should be 2 PSI below the pump cut-in pressure.

### 5.2 Pipe Materials

| Material | Cost (1/2-in) | Cost (3/4-in) | Freeze resistance | Max temp | Ease of install | Lifespan |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| PEX-A (cross-linked polyethylene) | $0.50–$0.80/ft | $0.70–$1.10/ft | Excellent — expands without bursting | 200°F | High — flexible, expansion fittings | 40–50 yr |
| PEX-B | $0.40–$0.65/ft | $0.55–$0.90/ft | Good — somewhat less flexible than A | 200°F | High | 40–50 yr |
| CPVC | $0.40–$0.70/ft | $0.55–$0.90/ft | Poor — cracks in freeze-thaw cycles | 200°F | Medium — solvent cement | 40–50 yr |
| Copper (Type L) | $2.50–$4.00/ft | $3.50–$5.50/ft | Moderate — will burst if frozen solid | 400°F | Medium — solder or press | 50–70 yr |
| Galvanized steel | $1.50–$2.50/ft | $2.00–$3.50/ft | Moderate | 300°F | Low — threading required | 40–50 yr (corrosion risk) |
| HDPE (buried main line) | $0.60–$1.20/ft | $0.80–$1.50/ft | Excellent | 140°F | Medium — heat fusion or barb fittings | 50–100 yr |

**For off-grid use, PEX-A is the clear winner for most applications:**
- It is the most freeze-tolerant of all common pipe materials
- It is flexible enough to snake through walls with minimal fittings
- Uponor Wirsbo expansion fittings (for PEX-A) are extremely reliable — no leaks
- Cost is low and it is available at every hardware chain

Use copper only where aesthetics or very high temperatures require it (boiler connections, certain solar thermal fittings).

**For buried main lines from well to house or spring to cistern:** HDPE is the best choice. It handles freeze-thaw well when buried below frost depth, is inert to soil chemicals, and lasts 50–100 years. Fuse with a heat-fusion tool or use insert fittings with stainless clamps.

### 5.3 Freeze Protection

In climates with freezing temperatures, any water line or tank exposed to freezing air will eventually freeze and burst. This is the most common and most preventable catastrophic failure in off-grid water systems.

**Frost depth by region:**

| Region | Frost depth |
|---|---|
| Deep South (FL, GA coast, LA, TX Gulf) | 0–6 inches |
| Southeast interior (NC, VA, TN, AR) | 8–18 inches |
| Midwest (OH, IN, IL, IA, MO) | 24–42 inches |
| Northern Midwest (MN, WI, ND, SD) | 42–60+ inches |
| Pacific Northwest coast | 12–24 inches |
| Mountain West (CO, ID, MT, WY) | 36–60 inches |
| New England | 36–60 inches |

All buried pipes must go below local frost depth. Check your county's frost depth map (NOAA maintains this data).

**Above-ground pipe freeze protection options:**

1. **Heat tape (pipe heating cable):** Self-regulating heat tape ($1–$3/ft installed) attached to exposed pipes and wrapped in foam insulation. Requires power. Use self-regulating (not constant-wattage) to prevent overheating.
2. **Buried cistern:** Burying a poly or concrete cistern below frost depth prevents freezing without any energy input.
3. **Insulated pump house:** A small insulated structure over the wellhead and pressure tank. A single 60W light bulb can maintain above-freezing temperatures in a well-insulated pump house down to -20°F.
4. **Continuous slow drip:** Keeping a slow drip flowing through the system prevents freezing in borderline temperatures. Wastes water; not appropriate as primary strategy.
5. **Drain-back system:** Install drain valves and a sloped supply line so the exposed above-ground section can be drained completely when not in use (cabins, seasonal use only).

**Winterizing a pressure tank:** If the pump house or basement is unheated, insulate the pressure tank and install a low-wattage tank heater. A frozen pressure tank splits at the welds; this is a $200–$500 replacement.

---

### 5.4 Gravity Distribution Design

For a header tank + distribution system without a pump:

- **Header tank** (elevated cistern, 200–500 gallons) feeds the distribution system continuously
- Distribution pipe from header tank runs downhill at minimum 1/8-inch drop per foot to prevent air locks
- Branch lines to each fixture; install shutoff valves at each branch junction
- All branches slope slightly downward from the main to prevent trapping air
- Install a ball valve and hose bib at the lowest point of each line run for draining

With gravity systems, pressure at any fixture is determined by the elevation difference, not by pump settings. There is no pressure tank needed. However, flow rate may be lower than a pumped system — acceptable for most off-grid use if fixtures are chosen appropriately.

Low-flow faucets and showerheads designed for 0.5–1.5 GPM work well with gravity-fed pressure in the 15–25 PSI range. Standard high-flow fixtures may feel weak.

## 6. Water Quality Testing

### 6.1 What to Test, How Often

**Baseline testing (new well or new source):**

|---|---|---|---|
| Coliform bacteria and E. coli | Sewage/animal waste contamination | State-certified lab | $20–$50 |
| Nitrates (NO3) | Agricultural runoff, septic influence | State-certified lab | $10–$25 |
| pH | Corrosiveness, scale potential | Lab or DIY meter | $5–$15 |
| Total hardness | Scale buildup, water softener need | Lab or DIY strips | $5–$10 |
| Iron and manganese | Staining, filter clogging, taste | Lab | $15–$30 |
| Arsenic | Natural geology (SW, NE, NW risk areas) | State-certified lab | $20–$40 |
| Lead | Old plumbing, not usually a source issue | Lab | $20–$40 |
| TDS (total dissolved solids) | General mineral load | DIY TDS meter ($10–$20) | $10–$20 |
| **Basic residential panel (all above)** | | | **$150–$300** |

After establishing baseline, test minimally:
- Coliform bacteria: every year, or after any work on the well, flooding near the well, or sewage spill upgradient
- Nitrates: every year if you have agriculture nearby; every 2 years otherwise
- Full panel re-test: every 5 years, or if water changes appearance, taste, or smell

**For rainwater systems:** Test annually for coliform bacteria, pH, and turbidity. Add heavy metals panel every 5 years or if you change roofing materials.

**For surface water:** Test quarterly for coliform, turbidity, and pH. Test annually for pesticides/herbicides if within agricultural watershed. Do not reduce testing frequency.

State health departments often have free or subsidized well-water testing programs for primary residences. Check before paying private lab rates.

## 7. Greywater and Wastewater

### 7.1 Greywater Defined

Greywater is all household wastewater except toilet waste: kitchen sink, bathroom sink, shower, bathtub, and laundry. It contains soap, skin cells, hair, food particles, and trace fecal contamination (from handwashing). It is not potable but is significantly less hazardous than blackwater.

In an off-grid context, reusing greywater reduces the burden on your septic system and provides irrigation water during dry periods. A family of four produces approximately 50–80 gallons of greywater per day.

---

### 7.2 Greywater Systems

**Laundry-to-landscape (L2L):**

The simplest system. Greywater from the washing machine is diverted directly to mulched basins around trees and shrubs. No permit required in Arizona and many other states. Design rules:
- Minimum 1 cubic yard of mulch per discharge point (prevents pooling)
- Minimum 2 inches of mulch cover over distribution point (prevents surface contact)
- 100-foot setback from wells
- Do not use on edible root crops or vegetables where leaves/fruit contact soil
- Use greywater-compatible laundry detergent (low sodium, no boron, biodegradable)
- Alternate discharge points to prevent saturation

Materials cost: $50–$200 for pipe, fittings, and mulch.

**Branched drain system:**

A gravity-fed network distributes bathroom sink, shower, and laundry greywater to multiple landscape zones through sequential splitters (branched distribution fittings). Each branch terminates in a mulched basin. Requires continuous 2% slope from source to each discharge point.

Materials cost: $100–$400.

**Surge tank with pump:**

When gravity isn't available or distribution distances are long, install a 50–100 gallon surge tank (HDPE, sealed) that collects greywater and a small pump discharges it to distribution areas on demand. The tank prevents the pump from running during low-flow periods.

**Constructed wetland (reed bed):**

A gravel-filled, plant-root-zone bed receives greywater and treats it through biological and filtration processes. Can handle 50–150 gallons/day per 10 sq ft of bed area. Extremely effective, no moving parts, but requires 2–4 months for biological establishment.

Plants used: cattail, bulrush, reed, iris — all root-zone water purifiers. The treated effluent can be used for sub-surface irrigation of non-edible plantings.

Materials cost: $200–$800 for a household-scale system.

---

### 7.3 Legal Status by State

Greywater law is more complex than rainwater law. Most states still operate under plumbing codes that treat all wastewater as requiring full septic treatment.

| Legal status | States | Practical situation |
|---|---|---|
| Most permissive — simple permit or no permit for basic systems | AZ, CA (post 2025 SB 966), MT, NM, TX, UT | L2L and branched drain explicitly allowed; permits vary by county |
| Moderate — permit required, must meet code | CO, ID, OR, WA | Permitted systems allowed but need inspection |
| Complicated — technically requires septic for all greywater, but enforcement weak in rural areas | Most remaining states | Assess local enforcement reality; composting toilet + greywater diversion often tolerated in rural settings |
| Strictest — any diversion from sewer technically illegal | GA, AR, and some others | Rural enforcement rare; risk assessment is individual decision |

Arizona is the most greywater-friendly state: residential systems under 400 gallons/day require no permit and no inspection if they meet basic setback and mulch requirements. Arizona also offers utility rebates up to $3,000 for qualifying systems.

Montana requires a permit for greywater systems under state Rule 17.36.319 but the permitting process is defined and workable.

**The enforcement reality in rural areas:**

Most rural counties in most states do not proactively inspect for unpermitted greywater systems. The primary risk is during property sale (a buyer's inspector may flag it) or if you have a dispute with a neighbor that leads to a complaint. This is not a legal recommendation — it is a realistic description of enforcement patterns in low-density rural areas.

---

### 7.4 Blackwater Elimination with Composting Toilets

A composting toilet converts toilet waste into a dry, pathogen-reduced compost over 6–12 months. Eliminating blackwater removes:
- The need for a septic system (saving $5,000–$20,000)
- All toilet flushing water consumption (typically 20–30 gallons/person/day with standard toilets; 1 gallon/person/day with composting toilets)
- The largest source of pathogens in household waste

Composting toilets are legal for primary residences in most states, but permit requirements vary. States that have explicitly legalized composting toilets for primary residences include all but a handful; the most restrictive states require an approved septic system regardless.

**Commercial composting toilets:**
- Nature's Head: $1,000–$1,100; 1–2 person capacity continuous, or short-term cabin use for 6–8; requires frequent emptying for large families
- Sun-Mar Excel: $1,600–$1,800; better capacity for full-time 2–4 person use; uses small heating element (6W) to accelerate composting
- Separett: $1,400–$1,600; separating toilet (urine diverted separately); minimal odor; popular in Scandinavia and off-grid US

Carbon material (sawdust, peat, coconut coir) must be added with each use: approximately 1 cup per use. This is the consumable cost of a composting toilet: roughly $30–$60/year in materials for a family.

**DIY composting toilets:**
- Twin-chamber design: one chamber in use, one composting. Chambers alternate every 12–18 months.
- Construction cost: $200–$500 in materials (concrete block or lumber + HDPE liner)
- Well-documented plans available from Humanure Handbook (J. Jenkins; free PDF)

The finished compost (after minimum 1 year) is pathogen-reduced and usable on fruit trees and non-edible plantings. Do not use fresh humanure on edible crops.

---

### 7.5 Septic Systems Where Greywater Reuse Is Not Legal or Practical

A conventional septic system consists of a septic tank (1,000–1,500 gallon, where solids settle and anaerobic digestion begins) and a leach field (subsurface perforated pipes distribute effluent for soil absorption).

Requirements:
- Minimum lot size and setback from wells, property lines, watercourses (varies by state: typically 50–100 ft from well, 25 ft from property line)
- Soil percolation test required before permit: a "perc test" measures how fast water absorbs into the soil; very slow-perc soils require engineered alternatives
- Permit and inspection required in virtually all jurisdictions
- Professional installation typically required for permit compliance

Cost: $5,000–$20,000 depending on soil conditions, system size, and local labor. Mound systems (required on slow-perc soils) cost $15,000–$30,000+.

Greywater diversion reduces the septic system's load significantly — typically by 50–65%. If you divert laundry, showers, and sinks to a greywater system, your septic handles only toilet waste (blackwater), requiring a smaller, simpler, cheaper system or extending the life of the existing system dramatically.

## 8. Emergency and Resilience

### 8.1 The 1 Gallon/Day Myth

FEMA and the CDC recommend 1 gallon per person per day for emergency water storage. This figure is for survival-only conditions in a short-duration emergency. It covers drinking and minimal hygiene and assumes no cooking, no dishwashing, no sanitation beyond bare minimum.

Actual daily consumption for real off-grid living:
- Drinking: 0.5 gallons
- Cooking: 0.5 gallons
- Handwashing: 0.5 gallons
- Dishwashing: 2–3 gallons
- Toilet flushing (composting toilet): 0.1 gallons
- Shower/bath: 5–10 gallons (even a military 2-minute shower uses 4 gallons)
- Laundry: 3–5 gallons per load per person per week
- **Realistic minimum for functional off-grid living: 15–25 gallons/person/day**

The 1 gallon figure is an urban grid-failure short-term planning number. For permanent off-grid living, it is a number you never want to be near.

Livestock and garden water requirements add substantially to this (see Section 2.5 sizing worksheet).

---

### 8.2 Backup Water Sources and Cache

A resilient off-grid water system has three functional sources:

**Tier 1: Primary system** (well pump, spring system, or rainwater primary) — handles all daily needs under normal conditions

**Tier 2: Backup pump** (hand pump on the same well, or gravity-fed backup source) — provides water when the primary pump fails; no power required

**Tier 3: Emergency storage cache** — a dedicated reserve separate from the main cistern; sealed, not drawn from in normal operation; 30-day supply for household (drinking and cooking only; not irrigation or livestock)

For Tier 3, minimum sizing: 1 gallon/person/day × 30 days (survival only, not comfort) = 30 gallons per person. For a family of 4: 120 gallons minimum, 250 gallons recommended. Use dedicated food-grade poly containers (Saratoga Springs 5-gallon jugs, 55-gallon food-grade drums). Rotate annually.

---

### 8.3 Emergency Purification Without Power

**Boiling:**
Boiling water kills all pathogens. Bring to a rolling boil for 1 minute (3 minutes above 6,500 ft elevation). This is the gold standard for emergency purification of turbid or unknown water — filtration first (cloth, coffee filter), then boil.

**Chemical disinfection (bleach):**

Use unscented household bleach (5.25–8.25% sodium hypochlorite, no added surfactants or perfumes). Post-2010 bleach in the US is typically 8.25% — use the lower dose figures for 8.25%:

| Water condition | Bleach 5.25% | Bleach 8.25% |
|---|---|---|
| Clear water, 1 gallon | 8 drops (1/8 tsp) | 6 drops |
| Cloudy water, 1 gallon | 16 drops (1/4 tsp) | 12 drops |
| Clear water, 5 gallons | 40 drops | 30 drops |

Wait 30 minutes after adding bleach before drinking. Water should smell faintly of chlorine; if it doesn't, repeat the dose and wait 15 minutes. Bleach loses effectiveness with age — shelf life is about 1 year at room temperature. Stock fresh bleach and rotate it.

Bleach does not effectively kill Cryptosporidium. Boiling is required.

**Portable filters:**

- **Sawyer Squeeze / Sawyer Mini (0.1-micron hollow fiber):** Removes bacteria and protozoa including Giardia and Cryptosporidium; does not remove viruses. $25–$45. Rated for 100,000 gallons. The most cost-effective portable filter available.
- **LifeStraw Personal:** Similar to Sawyer Mini; 0.2-micron; $15–$20. Adequate for bacteria and protozoa.
- **Katadyn BeFree / Hiker Pro:** Mid-range; adds carbon for taste; $50–$80.
- **MSR Guardian:** Military-spec; removes viruses as well as bacteria and protozoa; $350. For use in areas with known viral contamination risk.

For most off-grid US scenarios (well, spring, or surface water without significant viral contamination risk from human sewage), a Sawyer Squeeze plus boiling for suspected viral contamination covers all bases.

**SODIS (Solar Disinfection):**

SODIS is WHO-recognized for emergency use. Fill clear PET plastic bottles (Nalgene, standard water bottles) with relatively clear water (turbidity under 30 NTU — if you can read text through it, it's clear enough), and leave in direct sunlight for 6 hours (full sun) or 48 hours (cloudy). Above 35°C water temperature, required exposure time is halved.

SODIS limitations:
- Does not work in opaque or heavily turbid water — pre-filter if necessary
- Very slow: 1-2 liter batches per bottle
- Not reliable against chemical contamination
- Requires sunny weather

SODIS is appropriate as a last-resort backup or for generating small volumes of drinking water in remote conditions. It is not a substitute for a proper treatment chain.

### 8.4 Nuclear Fallout: Water Considerations

Following a nuclear detonation, water system concerns are: particulate contamination of surface water and open cisterns, and the iodine-131 contamination of rainfall for the first 1–2 weeks.

**Pre-event:** Pre-fill all closed storage (cisterns, indoor tanks) before any anticipated nuclear event if warning time permits. Water in sealed underground or indoor tanks is not subject to fallout contamination.

**Post-event, first two weeks:**
- Do not collect rainwater for drinking during the first 14 days after fallout deposition — rain will wash fallout isotopes (including I-131) from the atmosphere.
- Do not draw from open ponds, streams, or surface water in the first 2 weeks if the watershed is in the fallout plume.
- Water from sealed, pre-filled underground cisterns is safe if the cistern was sealed before fallout arrived.
- Well water (from deep, properly cased wells) is generally safe — fallout does not penetrate the water table immediately. However, surface runoff near the wellhead should be evaluated; ensure the sanitary seal is intact.
- Shallower wells (under 50 ft) and those with inadequate grouting are higher risk from surface contamination.

**Filtration approach for unknown fallout water:**

1. Pre-filter through multiple layers of fabric to remove particulates (fallout particles are physically large — most are visible dust)
2. Sediment filter chain (5-micron then 1-micron)
3. The above removes most radioactive particulates (which are bound to dust and soil particles)
4. Dissolved radioactive isotopes (Cs-137, Sr-90, I-131) are not removed by particle filtration
5. For dissolved isotope removal: RO removes approximately 90–95% of most dissolved radionuclides including Cs-137 and Sr-90
6. Distillation removes essentially all dissolved radionuclides
7. I-131 has an 8-day half-life — by day 56 (7 half-lives), it is at 0.78% of initial concentration and effectively gone. Avoiding rainwater collection for the first 2–4 weeks addresses most of the I-131 risk.

**Potassium iodide (KI):**
KI blocks thyroid uptake of radioactive iodine-131. It does not protect against other radionuclides and does not decontaminate water. Adult dose: 130 mg (one standard tablet). Do not take unless a credible authority confirms radioiodine exposure risk — KI is not a general radiation countermeasure. Stock it as part of your nuclear preparedness kit, but do not rely on it as a substitute for shielding and source control.

Cross-reference: `15-disaster-scenarios.md` for full nuclear scenario planning including shelter-in-place design and decontamination.

### 9.3 DIY vs. Contractor Tradeoffs

**Do yourself:**
- Pressure tank installation and replacement
- Sediment/carbon/UV filter installation
- RO system installation
- PEX interior plumbing
- Greywater system construction
- Spring box construction (with appropriate guidance)
- Cistern positioning and plumbing connections
- Hand pump installation (both Simple Pump and Bison provide detailed installation guides)

**Hire a professional for:**
- Well drilling (no DIY option for depths over 50 ft)
- Well pump replacement in a deep well (requires pipe-pulling equipment or special tools for smaller pumps)
- Any work requiring state-licensed well contractor sign-off (most states require this for new well permits)
- Septic system design and installation (percolation test and design engineering typically required for permit)

**The critical point on well pump replacement:**

A submersible pump in a 300-ft well is 300 ft of drop pipe + pump + wire, suspended from the wellhead. Pulling and re-setting this requires a well-pulling tool set ($400–$800) and two strong people, or a contractor with a pump truck. Deep well pump failures are not a self-fix job during a crisis — have the right tools on hand and know the procedure before you need it at 2 am in January.

## 10. Annual Maintenance Schedule

**Monthly:**
- Check pressure tank pre-charge pressure (if pump short-cycles)
- Visually inspect sediment filter cartridges; change if heavily discolored or flow drops
- Check all accessible pipe connections for drips
- Inspect spring box lid and screen (if applicable)

**Every 3–6 months:**
- Replace 5-micron and 1-micron sediment filters (or per manufacturer schedule)
- Replace carbon block filter
- Clean UV quartz sleeve
- Check greywater mulch basins: add mulch if decomposed, ensure no surface pooling

**Annually:**
- Replace UV lamp (even if still lit)
- Water quality test: coliform bacteria, nitrates, pH minimum; full panel every 5 years
- Inspect wellhead: check grout seal, cap condition, vent screen (should be screened to exclude insects)
- Test pressure tank pre-charge; replace bladder or tank if logged
- Lubricate hand pump cylinder (per manufacturer instructions; some are sealed for life)
- Drain and inspect cistern interior if accessible; remove any sediment accumulation
- Verify emergency water cache is properly sealed and schedule rotation if over 1 year old
- Check all shutoff valves: exercise them (open and close fully) to prevent seizure

**Every 5 years:**
- Full water quality panel (all parameters)
- Inspect buried pipe runs at any accessible joints or cleanouts
- Well video inspection (camera into well): check casing integrity, screen condition — $300–$600 from a well service company; worth doing once per decade
