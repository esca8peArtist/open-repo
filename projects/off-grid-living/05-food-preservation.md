# Domain 5: Food Preservation & Storage

> You can grow enough food in four months to eat for twelve — but only if you
> can preserve it. Preservation is not optional and not secondary to production.
> It is the discipline that converts seasonal abundance into year-round survival.
> This document covers every major preservation method in operational depth:
> temperatures, PSI ratings, chemical processes, equipment costs, failure modes,
> and the specific numbers you need to make real decisions. Where section 7 of
> Domain 4 gave a 9-row comparison table, this document gives you the full process.

## Section 1: The Preservation Imperative

### 1.1 The Spoilage Problem

In the developing world, post-harvest losses average **40–60%** of food produced.
Even in developed-world home gardens without preservation, losses commonly reach
**30–50%** — the July zucchini glut, the September tomato avalanche, the October
apple dump. None of these translate to February nutrition without deliberate intervention.

The math is stark: a family of four producing 95% of its own food but losing 40%
to spoilage is effectively operating a food system sized for 60% self-sufficiency.
Preservation is not an enhancement to food production — it is a load-bearing
component of the same system.

**Why winter is the test**: In Zone 5, the frost-free growing window is roughly
May 15 – October 1: 138 days. The preservation gap — the period when nothing
productive comes from the garden — runs approximately **210 days**, November
through April. Every calorie consumed during that window was either preserved
from the prior growing season, stored as dry goods, or purchased.

The goal of a mature homestead preservation system is to transform every
surplus from every harvest peak into stable, long-lasting food stores that
cover the full 210-day gap with margin.

### 1.2 Food Loss by Mechanism

| Loss mechanism | Typical timeline | Prevention method |
|---|---|---|
| Microbial (bacteria, mold, yeast) | Hours–days | Canning, fermentation, drying, salting |
| Enzymatic browning/softening | Days–weeks | Blanching, acidification, drying |
| Oxidation (rancidity, color loss) | Weeks–months | Vacuum sealing, oxygen absorbers, cool storage |
| Physical damage and bruising | Immediate → days | Careful harvest, proper packing |
| Pest damage (rodents, insects) | Days–weeks | Sealed containers, cold, diatomaceous earth |
| Moisture loss (wilting) | Hours–days | High-humidity storage, proper wrapping |
| Moisture gain (mold from condensation) | Days | Proper ventilation, temperature-stable storage |

### 1.3 The Nine Preservation Categories

This document covers nine methods in sequence from lowest to highest energy input:

1. Root cellaring and cold storage
2. Canning (water bath and pressure)
3. Lacto-fermentation
4. Dehydration (sun, solar, electric)
5. Freezing and freeze-drying
6. Smoking and curing
7. Vinegar pickling
8. Oil preservation and confit
9. Grain, legume, and seed dry storage

## Section 2: Root Cellaring & Cold Storage

### 2.1 The Physics of Cold Storage

Root cellaring exploits a fundamental fact of soil thermodynamics: at depths
below **4 feet**, ground temperature in most of the continental US stabilizes
between **45–55°F** year-round, independent of surface weather. This is the
earth's free refrigerator. Getting into that temperature range — and controlling
humidity — is the entire task.

Different crops require different temperature/humidity combinations. Getting
these wrong causes more loss than any other storage error.

**Temperature and humidity targets by crop category:**

| Crop category | Temp (°F) | Relative humidity | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Potatoes | 38–40 | 90–95% | Warmer = sprouting; below 36°F = starch converts to sugar (sweet, won't store well) |
| Carrots, beets, parsnips, turnips | 32–40 | 90–95% | Pack in damp sand or sawdust |
| Cabbage, kohlrabi | 32–40 | 90–95% | Store away from apples (ethylene) |
| Apples | 30–32 | 90–95% | Keep away from everything else; ethylene emitters |
| Pears | 29–31 | 90–95% | Harvest before fully ripe; ripen at room temp when needed |
| Winter squash (Hubbard, Butternut) | 50–55 | 60–70% | Never store near freezing; skin damage causes early rot |
| Sweet potatoes | 55–60 | 85–90% | Cured 10 days at 85°F/90% RH before storage |
| Onions and garlic | 32–40 | 65–70% | Low humidity critical — high humidity causes rot; cure completely |
| Dried beans/corn | 32–50 | Below 50% | Moisture is the enemy; not for high-humidity cellars |

**Companion conflicts — ethylene management**: Apples, pears, and ripe tomatoes
emit ethylene gas, which accelerates ripening and spoilage of neighboring
produce. **Never store apples with potatoes, carrots, or cabbage.** Apples cause
potatoes to sprout prematurely and cabbages to develop off-flavors. Keep
ethylene emitters in a separate section or use individual boxes.

### 2.2 Construction: Four Approaches

**Option A: Underground cellar (most effective, most expensive)**

Excavate to minimum **4 feet below grade** — 6–8 feet preferred in Zone 4–5.
Standard design: 8×10 ft footprint, concrete block or poured concrete walls,
earthen or concrete floor (not waterproofed — moisture should be able to wick up),
2-layer insulated door (two doors with air gap), ventilation pipe.

- Ventilation: two 4-inch PVC pipes — one low intake, one high exhaust, positioned
  on opposite walls. Controls temperature by mixing outside air in transition seasons.
  Install sliding dampers to close during deep winter.
- Drainage: slope floor to 4-inch French drain at low corner; cellar must not flood.
- Vapor barrier: on the outside of walls only — prevents water intrusion while
  allowing interior humidity from earthen floor.
- Thermometer/hygrometer placement: mount at mid-height away from doors and vents;
  two units at different locations for monitoring accuracy.
- **Cost**: $800–$2,000 for DIY block construction; $3,000–$8,000 contractor-built.

**Option B: Hillside bank cellar (most affordable)**

Build into an existing hillside: excavate into slope, construct front wall only
(the other three sides are earth), add insulated door. Requires good hillside
drainage to prevent water infiltration. Inherits earth temperature naturally.
- **Cost**: $200–$800 in materials; significant excavation labor.

**Option C: Insulated garage or basement corner**

In climates that do not drop below 20°F consistently, an insulated corner of
an unheated garage can be converted. Build two insulated stud walls (R-19 minimum),
insulated ceiling, vented to outside. Works if outside winter temps stay 20–40°F
— natural cold provides the refrigeration.
- **Cost**: $200–$600 in materials.

**Option D: Basement pit or buried bin**

Bury a large plastic barrel or galvanized trash can 4+ feet in the ground with
an insulated lid. Access hatch at ground level. Good for 50–100 lb of root
vegetables in a small-scale system. Humidity controlled by packing medium.
- **Cost**: $50–$150.

### 2.3 Storage Containers and Packing

- **Wooden crates with slatted sides**: allow airflow; stack with gaps between layers.
- **Damp sand packing**: layer carrots, beets, turnips, and parsnips in boxes of
  slightly damp (not wet) sand or fine sawdust. Maintains humidity at root surface,
  prevents shriveling. Sand should clump slightly when squeezed but not drip.
- **Newspaper wrapping for apples and pears**: wrap individually to prevent one
  rotten fruit from contacting neighbors. Check weekly.
- **Mesh bags**: good for onions and garlic where airflow is critical.
- **Never use plastic bags without holes**: traps ethylene and moisture, causes rot.

**Rotation rule**: First in, first out. Label all storage containers with harvest
date and variety. Consume oldest stock first. Inspect regularly — one rotten
item contaminates neighbors. When in doubt, compost it.

## Section 3: Canning

### 3.1 The Core Science — Botulism and pH

**The single most important fact in home canning**: *Clostridium botulinum*
spores are ubiquitous in soil and on raw produce. At temperatures below **240°F**,
those spores survive cooking and can germinate in sealed, anaerobic jars to
produce botulinum toxin — one of the most lethal substances known. A fraction
of a microgram is a fatal dose.

The two-variable defense is pH and temperature:

- **pH below 4.6**: Acid prevents *C. botulinum* spore germination. Water bath
  canning (maximum 212°F at sea level) is safe **only** for foods at or below pH 4.6.
- **240°F for 3+ minutes**: Kills spores regardless of pH. Only achievable under
  pressure (10 PSI adds roughly 28°F above the 212°F boiling point).

**High-acid foods (water bath safe)**: fruits, fruit juices, most jams and jellies,
tomatoes (with added acid), pickles (vinegar-brined), fermented products.

**Low-acid foods (pressure canning required)**: all vegetables (except acidified
tomatoes), meats, poultry, fish, shellfish, beans, soups, mixed dishes.

**Tomatoes are borderline** (pH 4.0–4.6, sometimes higher in low-acid varieties).
USDA mandates adding **1 tablespoon lemon juice** or **1/4 teaspoon citric acid**
per pint, or **2 tablespoons lemon juice** per quart, to ensure safety regardless
of tomato variety.

### 3.2 Equipment

**Water bath canning:**
- Large enamel or stainless pot, minimum 21-quart, with lid and rack — $30–$60
- Jar lifter — $8–$12
- Wide-mouth funnel — $5–$8
- Lid wand (magnetic) or tongs — $5
- Bubble remover / headspace tool — $5
- Total startup: $50–$85

**Pressure canning:**
- **All American 21.5 qt pressure canner** (~$300–$380): no gasket; metal-to-metal
  seal; most reliable; lasts decades; preferred for serious homestead use.
- **Presto 23 qt pressure canner** (~$90–$110): gasket-sealed; works well;
  replace gasket every 2–3 years (~$8); good value entry point.
- **Do not use electric pressure cookers (Instant Pot) for canning**: heat
  distribution and pressure regulation are not validated for canning safety.
- Add jar lifter, funnel, headspace tool, and rack — same as WB above.
- Total startup (Presto): $115–$140; (All American): $325–$410.

**Jars**: Ball or Kerr wide-mouth mason jars. Half-pint ($10–$12/dozen), pint
($12–$15/dozen), quart ($14–$18/dozen). Jars are reusable indefinitely if
unchipped. **Lids are single-use only** — the sealing compound degrades after
one use. Bands are reusable. Budget $30–$60/year for lid replacement.

### 3.3 Water Bath Process

1. Inspect jars: no chips, cracks, or nicks on the rim sealing surface.
2. Wash jars in hot soapy water. Sterilize only if processing time is under 10 minutes
   (most recipes do not require separate sterilization).
3. Keep jars hot (in simmering water or oven at 200°F) until filled — prevents thermal shock.
4. Prepare lids: simmer in hot water 5 minutes to soften sealing compound. Do not boil.
5. Fill jar, leaving correct headspace. Remove air bubbles with bubble remover tool.
6. Wipe rim with damp cloth — any food residue prevents sealing.
7. Apply lid and band fingertip-tight (not overtightened — air must escape during processing).
8. Lower jars into boiling water with lifter; water must cover lids by **1–2 inches**.
9. Start timing **after** full boil resumes.
10. Remove; do not tilt; do not touch lids for 12–24 hours.
11. Check seals: center of lid should be concave and not flex when pressed. Unsealed
    jars go into refrigerator and are consumed within 2 weeks.

**Headspace by food type:**
| Food type | Headspace |
|---|---|
| Jams, jellies, preserves | 1/4 inch |
| Fruits, tomatoes, pickles | 1/2 inch |
| Juices | 1/4 inch |

### 3.4 Pressure Canning Process Specifications

After filling jars and seating lids (same prep as above), place in pressure canner
on rack with 2–3 inches of hot water in the canner. Lock lid. Heat on high. Vent
steam for **10 minutes** before closing petcock or weighted gauge — this purges
air from the canner (air pockets lower effective temperature at equivalent pressure).
Then bring to target pressure and begin timing.

|---|---|---|---|---|
| Green beans | Pint | 11 | 10 | 20 |
| Green beans | Quart | 11 | 10 | 25 |
| Potatoes (cubed) | Pint | 11 | 10 | 35 |
| Potatoes (cubed) | Quart | 11 | 10 | 40 |
| Corn (whole kernel) | Pint | 11 | 10 | 55 |
| Corn (whole kernel) | Quart | 11 | 10 | 85 |
| Carrots | Pint | 11 | 10 | 25 |
| Carrots | Quart | 11 | 10 | 30 |
| Beets | Pint | 11 | 10 | 30 |
| Beets | Quart | 11 | 10 | 35 |
| Beans (dried, presoaked) | Pint | 11 | 10 | 75 |
| Beans (dried, presoaked) | Quart | 11 | 10 | 90 |
| Tomatoes (crushed, with acid) | Quart | 11 | 10 | 15 |
| Chicken (raw pack, bone-in) | Quart | 11 | 10 | 75 |
| Ground meat | Pint | 11 | 10 | 75 |

*All times assume 0–1,000 ft elevation. See altitude adjustments below.*

**Altitude adjustment (dial gauge):**
| Elevation | Dial gauge PSI |
|---|---|
| 0–2,000 ft | 11 PSI |
| 2,001–4,000 ft | 12 PSI |
| 4,001–6,000 ft | 13 PSI |
| 6,001–8,000 ft | 14 PSI |

**Altitude adjustment (weighted gauge)**: Switch from 10-lb to 15-lb weight above 1,000 ft.

**Depressurization**: Do not rush. After processing, turn off heat. Allow canner
to depressurize naturally — **do not remove the weight or open the petcock to
speed this process.** Rapid depressurization causes liquid loss (siphoning) and
can break jar seals. Wait until pressure reads zero, then wait an additional
10 minutes before opening.

### 3.5 Common Failures and Causes

| Problem | Likely cause | Prevention |
|---|---|---|
| Lid did not seal | Food on rim; overtightened band; lid re-used | Wipe rim; fingertip-tight bands; new lids |
| Siphoning (liquid loss) | Pressure dropped too fast; overfilled jar | Natural depressurize; correct headspace |
| Jar cracked | Thermal shock; chipped rim; overfilled | Keep jars hot before filling; inspect jars |
| Floating fruit | Not hot-packed; too much syrup | Hot pack preferred for fruits |
| Discolored food | Air in jar; under-processing | Remove bubbles; follow tested time exactly |
| Seal failed in storage | Poor initial seal; band too tight | Check all seals before shelving |

**Any jar with a broken seal, spurting liquid, off-odor, or mold: discard without
tasting. Do not smell closely — aerosolized botulinum toxin can cause illness.**

### 3.6 Economics of Home Canning

Home-canned tomatoes: $0.30–$0.60/quart in labor and supplies (home-grown tomatoes,
commercial lids, propane). Store-bought equivalent: $2.50–$4.00/quart. At 100
quarts per season, savings of $200–$350/year from tomatoes alone.

Home-canned chicken broth: $0.40–$0.80/quart. Store: $3.00–$5.00/quart.

**Storage duration:**
- High-acid WB-canned (fruits, pickles): 12–18 months best quality; safe 2+ years if sealed.
- Pressure-canned vegetables: 2–3 years best quality; safe 5+ years if stored cool and dark.
- Pressure-canned meats: 3–5 years best quality.

## Section 4: Lacto-Fermentation

### 4.1 The Science

Lactobacillus bacteria are naturally present on the surface of all raw vegetables.
In the right conditions — salt brine, anaerobic environment, moderate temperature —
they out-compete pathogenic bacteria by consuming sugars and producing **lactic acid**.
The lactic acid drops the pH of the environment below 4.6, making it hostile to
*Listeria*, *Salmonella*, *E. coli*, and *C. botulinum*.

This is a self-preserving system: the process that creates the product also creates
the preservation conditions. No heat, no equipment beyond a jar. Fermentation is
arguably the most resilient preservation method for off-grid conditions.

**Salt concentration**: 2% by weight is the standard working brine. Below 1.5%,
the wrong bacteria may dominate. Above 3%, fermentation slows dramatically.

- 2% brine by weight: 20 grams salt per 1 kilogram of water = **approximately
  1 tablespoon non-iodized salt per 2 cups water** (close enough for practical use).
- **Never use iodized table salt**: iodine is bactericidal and kills Lactobacillus.
  Use kosher salt, sea salt, or pickling salt.

### 4.2 Equipment

- Wide-mouth mason jars (quart or half-gallon) — already on hand for canning
- Fermentation weights (glass, ceramic, or zip-lock bag filled with brine): keeps
  produce submerged below brine surface. Any exposure to air risks kahm yeast or mold.
- Airlock lids (optional): allow CO2 to escape without letting air in; helpful but
  not required. A regular lid loosened slightly works; open daily to release pressure.
- Ceramic crocks (1–5 gallon): traditional vessel; ideal for large batches; check
  for food-safe glazing (no lead); $40–$200 depending on size.

### 4.3 Sauerkraut — Full Process

Sauerkraut requires no brine — the cabbage provides its own water when salted.

1. Shred 1 medium cabbage (about 2 lb) thinly (1/8-inch). Discard outer leaves (keep
   2 clean outer leaves for later use as surface cover).
2. Weigh the shredded cabbage. Add **2% of that weight in non-iodized salt**.
   For 2 lb (907 g) of cabbage: add 18 g salt (approximately 1 tablespoon).
3. Massage and squeeze firmly for 5–10 minutes until cabbage releases significant
   liquid — enough to submerge when packed.
4. Pack tightly into a quart jar, pressing after each handful. Brine should rise
   above cabbage surface. Place reserved cabbage leaf as a gasket over the top to
   help keep shreds submerged.
5. Place weight on top. Brine must completely cover solids.
6. Cover loosely. Ferment at **65–75°F** for 1–4 weeks. Taste after 7 days.
   Shorter ferment = milder, crunchier; longer = more sour, softer.
7. When desired flavor is reached, seal and refrigerate or move to cold cellar (38–40°F).
   Cold dramatically slows further fermentation.

**Fermentation temperature guide:**
| Temperature | Result | Timeline |
|---|---|---|
| 60–65°F | Slow, complex flavor, good crunch | 4–6 weeks |
| 65–75°F | Ideal balance of speed and quality | 2–4 weeks |
| 75–85°F | Fast but mushy; overpowering acid | 1–2 weeks |
| Above 85°F | Risk of wrong bacteria; avoid | Not recommended |

### 4.4 Kimchi

Traditional kimchi uses Korean red pepper flakes (gochugaru), fish sauce, garlic,
ginger, and scallions. Accessible off-grid adaptation if gochugaru is unavailable:
substitute any dried red chile flakes (adjust to heat preference).

Basic napa cabbage kimchi process:
1. Quarter napa cabbage; salt with **1/4 cup coarse salt per 1 large cabbage** (2–3 lb).
   Rub salt between layers. Let sit 1–2 hours until wilted, turning periodically.
2. Rinse thoroughly (3× cold water), squeeze out excess liquid.
3. Mix paste: 3–4 tbsp gochugaru (or substitute), 1 tbsp fish sauce (or soy sauce
   for vegan), 1 tsp sugar, 3–4 garlic cloves (minced), 1-inch ginger (grated).
4. Add sliced scallions and daikon (if available). Coat everything with paste.
5. Pack into jar, press firmly, seal loosely.
6. Ferment 1–5 days at room temperature depending on desired sourness.
7. Refrigerate or cold cellar when at preferred flavor.

### 4.5 Fermented Vegetables Reference

| Vegetable | Brine % | Ferment time | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Carrots (sticks) | 2% | 5–10 days | Add garlic and dill; crunchy |
| Beets (sliced) | 2% | 5–7 days | Deep earthy-sour; add orange peel |
| Green beans (dilly) | 2% | 7–10 days | Pack with dill, garlic, hot pepper |
| Garlic cloves | 2% | 2–4 weeks | Turns teal-blue briefly (safe); complex flavor |
| Peppers (sliced or whole) | 2% | 5–14 days | Hot fermented peppers → hot sauce base |
| Cucumbers (real pickles) | 2–3% | 3–7 days | Must use **pickling cucumbers**, not slicers |
| Cauliflower florets | 2% | 5–10 days | Add turmeric for color; spicy blend works well |
| Mixed vegetables (curtido) | 2% | 2–3 days | Cabbage + carrots + oregano; Salvadoran style |

**Note on cucumbers**: Genuine lacto-fermented pickles use **only salt and water**
(no vinegar). Vinegar pickles are a different product. Real fermented pickles are
alive, probiotic, and must be kept cold once opened (or they continue fermenting).
Use pickling varieties (Kirby, National, Calypso) — slicing cucumbers go mushy.

### 4.6 Kvass and Beet Kvass

**Beet kvass**: Gut-healing, nutrient-dense fermented beverage. Cube 2–3 medium
raw beets (do not grate or juice — too much sugar overwhelms Lactobacillus). Place
in a quart jar, add **1 tsp salt** and filtered water to fill. Ferment 2–3 days
at room temperature. Drink 4 oz daily. Strain and refrigerate when pleasantly sour.
Beets can be refilled once with fresh water and salt for a second batch.

### 4.7 Troubleshooting

| Problem | Diagnosis | Action |
|---|---|---|
| White film on surface | Kahm yeast (wild, harmless) | Skim off; keep produce submerged; not dangerous |
| Pink, black, or green mold on surface | True mold | If colored mold: discard entire jar |
| Soft, mushy vegetables | Too warm; too long; too little salt | Eat quickly; adjust next batch |
| No bubbles after 3 days | Too cold; not enough bacteria | Move to warmer spot; be patient |
| Very salty taste | Too much salt | Use less in next batch; dilute won't help |
| CO2 overflow | Active fermentation in sealed jar | Loosen lid daily; use airlock lid |

### 4.8 Nutritional Benefits

Lacto-fermentation increases **vitamin C** content in vegetables (by 40–100% in
some studies), increases **B vitamins** (particularly B12 from some organisms),
and improves bioavailability of minerals by reducing anti-nutrients (phytic acid,
oxalates). The live Lactobacillus cultures function as probiotics — important for
gut health in populations eating primarily preserved and dried foods during winter.

**Storage**: Finished ferments kept at 38–42°F (cold cellar or refrigerator) will
continue fermenting very slowly and remain palatable for **6–18 months**. At room
temperature, continued fermentation eventually makes products too sour to eat.

## Section 5: Dehydration

### 5.1 Sun Drying

The oldest preservation method. Requirements for successful sun drying:
- Air temperature above **90°F** consistently
- Relative humidity below **60%**
- At least **3–5 full sun days** to complete most foods

These conditions limit sun drying to arid and semi-arid climates (Southwest,
portions of Great Plains) during summer. Humid climates (Southeast, Pacific NW,
Great Lakes) cannot reliably sun-dry food — partial drying in humidity breeds mold.

**Frame construction**: Build simple screens from 1×2 lumber stapled with
food-grade fiberglass window screen (not galvanized metal screen — zinc contamination).
Cover food frames with cheesecloth or a second screen frame to exclude insects.
Elevate on sawhorses for airflow. Bring inside before evening dew falls.

**Doneness test**: Food should be pliable (fruit) or brittle (vegetables and herbs)
with no surface moisture. Tear a piece — no visible moisture should appear in the interior.
Condition finished product: loosely fill jars 2/3 full, seal, shake daily for 7–10 days.
Moisture redistributes; if condensation appears, dry further.

### 5.2 Solar Dehydrator

A solar dehydrator extends sun-drying capability to temperatures of **100–140°F**,
shortens drying time to **1–3 days**, and works in moderately humid climates by
moving large volumes of heated air across the food.

**Basic passive solar box design (4-tray unit):**
- Box: 24"×36"×12" plywood box, painted matte black inside and out
- Glazing: single layer of clear corrugated polycarbonate or old storm window
  angled at local latitude angle toward south
- Reflective back panel: aluminized mylar or aluminum foil glued to back wall
  of collector directs additional radiation onto trays
- Trays: fiberglass window screen on 1×2 frames; sized to slide into box
- Ventilation: 2-inch inlet holes at bottom of box, 3-inch exhaust holes at top;
  screen-covered to exclude insects; creates natural convection airflow
- Internal temp: typically **110–140°F** on a clear day at 70°F ambient — adequate
  for all vegetables and most fruits

**Materials cost**: $40–$80. Plans: NCAT (National Center for Appropriate Technology)
publishes a free solar food dryer design (attra.ncat.org).

### 5.3 Electric Dehydrator

**Excalibur 9-tray** (~$270–$320): horizontal airflow; consistent temperature across
all trays; 15 sq ft drying space; timer; preferred for serious homestead use.

**Round/stack-style dehydrators** ($30–$80): avoid for anything except occasional
use — airflow is radial from center post; outer edges and center tray dry at
different rates; must rotate trays constantly; frustrating in practice.

**Temperature guide by food category:**
| Food type | Temperature (°F) | Approximate time |
|---|---|---|
| Herbs (delicate: basil, parsley) | 95–105 | 1–4 hours |
| Herbs (woody: rosemary, thyme) | 105–115 | 2–4 hours |
| Vegetables (most) | 125–135 | 4–12 hours |
| Fruits | 130–140 | 6–18 hours |
| Mushrooms | 125 | 4–8 hours |
| Meat / jerky (USDA method) | 155–165 | 4–10 hours |
| Fruit leather | 135 | 6–8 hours |

### 5.4 Pre-Treatment Methods

- **Blanching** (vegetables): Boil for 1–4 minutes (by crop; see table), immediately
  plunge into ice water bath. Stops enzymatic activity that causes off-flavor and color
  loss during drying. Required for most vegetables, optional for most fruits.
- **Ascorbic acid dip** (fruits): Dissolve 1 teaspoon vitamin C powder or 1 tablespoon
  lemon juice in 1 cup water; soak cut fruit for 5 minutes. Prevents enzymatic
  browning in apples, pears, peaches.
- **Sulfiting**: Sodium bisulfite or potassium metabisulfite dips prevent browning and
  extend shelf life significantly; used commercially. Not necessary for home use if
  oxygen absorbers are used in storage. Some people are sulfite-sensitive.
- **Salt brine for meat** (pre-jerky): 1/4 cup non-iodized salt + 1/4 cup soy sauce
  + seasonings per 1 lb meat; marinate 6–24 hours refrigerated before dehydrating.

### 5.6 Jerky — USDA-Safe Method

**The problem with traditional jerky**: most traditional methods dry meat at
temperatures where the meat never reaches an internal temperature of 160°F,
leaving pathogens (particularly *E. coli* O157:H7 and *Salmonella*) viable during
the initial drying phase.

**Two safe USDA methods:**

**Method A (pre-heat)**: Marinate meat; place in 275°F oven for 10 minutes
before dehydrating. Meat reaches 160°F internal quickly at oven temperature.
Transfer to dehydrator at 155°F until dried to desired texture. This partially
cooks before drying — texture is slightly different but safe.

**Method B (post-heat)**: Dehydrate fully at 155–165°F (most dehydrators; verify
with instant-read thermometer on actual food surface). When finished, place finished
jerky on baking sheet in 275°F oven for 10 minutes. Validates safety with heat
spike at end.

**Venison jerky**: Game meat has higher *E. coli* and *Salmonella* risk. Use
Method A (pre-heat). Slice across the grain (tender) or with grain (chewy) — both
are traditional. Against-grain slices dry faster. Freeze venison for 30 days at
0°F before processing to kill *Trichinella* if present.

### 5.7 Fruit Leather

Puree ripe or overripe fruit (berries, peaches, applesauce). Sweeten to taste
with honey or sugar (optional). Pour 1/4-inch thick onto non-stick dehydrator
sheets or parchment paper on baking sheet. Dehydrate at 135°F for 6–8 hours.
Finished leather peels cleanly, is pliable and not sticky. Roll in parchment paper,
store in sealed jar. **Shelf life: 1 month at room temperature, 1 year refrigerated.**

**Common problem**: leather stays sticky in center but cracks at edges = uneven
thickness. Use a silicone spatula to spread to consistent depth; edges tend to run
thin.

### 5.8 Storage of Dehydrated Foods

Moisture and oxygen are the enemies of dried food shelf life.

| Storage method | Shelf life | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Open jar, pantry | 2–4 months | Adequate for frequent-use herbs and spices |
| Sealed glass jar, cool/dark | 6–12 months | Good baseline for most foods |
| Glass jar + oxygen absorber | 1–2 years | Use 100cc absorber per quart jar |
| Mylar bag + oxygen absorber | 2–5 years | Best long-term method |
| Mylar bag + O2 absorber + 5-gal bucket | 5–10 years | Bulk storage; pest-proof |

**Oxygen absorber sizing**: 100cc per quart jar; 300cc per gallon container; 2000cc
per 5-gallon bucket. Use immediately after opening absorber package — they begin
working on contact with air and have a short working window (~15 minutes once open).

## Section 6: Freezing

### 6.1 Off-Grid Relevance

Freezing is not energy-free, which makes it the most grid-dependent preservation
method. On a hybrid system (solar + generator), a well-sized chest freezer is
practical and expands preservation options considerably. On a pure off-grid solar
system, a small chest freezer is achievable but requires careful energy accounting.

Freezing is best used for: **meats, fish, partially processed produce, dairy, and
foods that lose quality when canned or dried** (blanched corn, berries, broccoli).

### 6.2 Blanching Before Freezing

Without blanching, enzymes in vegetables continue operating at freezer temperatures,
causing flavor degradation, color changes, and texture breakdown over months.
Blanching deactivates enzymes. It is **required for most vegetables** before freezing.

**Blanching times (boiling water, then ice bath):**
| Vegetable | Blanch time (min) |
|---|---|
| Green beans | 3 |
| Broccoli florets | 3 |
| Corn (cut) | 4 |
| Corn (on cob) | 7–9 (by diameter) |
| Carrots (sliced) | 2 |
| Peas | 1.5–2 |
| Zucchini (sliced) | 3 |
| Kale/spinach | 2 |
| Beets (whole, then slice after cooling) | 25–30 |
| Brussels sprouts | 3–5 |
| Cauliflower florets | 3 |
| Asparagus | 2–4 (by thickness) |

Fruits do not require blanching. Freeze on sheet pans first (flash freeze) to
prevent clumping, then transfer to bags.

### 6.3 Packaging

- **Vacuum sealer**: Foodsaver-style ($80–$120) or commercial Weston ($150–$200).
  Vacuum packaging extends freezer shelf life **2–3× compared to zip bags** by
  eliminating oxygen that causes freezer burn. Best for meats.
- **Freezer zip bags**: squeeze out air manually; adequate for 3–6 month storage.
  Avoid regular storage bags — thinner plastic allows ice crystal migration.
- **Freezer paper**: butcher wrap; good for odd-shaped cuts; write species, cut,
  and date with permanent marker.
- **Glass jars in freezer**: use wide-mouth jars only; leave 1-inch headspace for
  liquids (expansion); never freeze jar more than 4/5 full.

### 6.4 Storage Duration by Food Type

| Food | Optimal freezer temp | Duration (vacuum sealed) | Duration (zip bag) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ground meat (beef/pork) | 0°F | 9–12 months | 3–4 months |
| Steaks, chops, roasts | 0°F | 12–18 months | 4–6 months |
| Whole poultry | 0°F | 12 months | 9 months |
| Fish (fatty: salmon, trout) | 0°F | 6–9 months | 2–3 months |
| Fish (lean: bass, perch) | 0°F | 12 months | 6 months |
| Blanched vegetables | 0°F | 18–24 months | 8–12 months |
| Fruit (unsweetened) | 0°F | 12–18 months | 8–12 months |
| Cooked casseroles | 0°F | 6 months | 3 months |
| Bread and baked goods | 0°F | 6 months | 3 months |
| Butter | 0°F | 12 months | 9 months |
| Cheese (hard) | 0°F | 6 months (texture changes) | 3–4 months |

### 6.5 Chest Freezer Solar Sizing

A **5 cu ft chest freezer** (smallest practical homestead unit) uses approximately
**100–130 kWh/year** — about **0.28–0.36 kWh/day** average.

Solar math for chest freezer support:
- Daily load: 0.35 kWh
- 200W solar panel produces ~0.8–1.0 kWh/day in moderate climates (4–5 peak sun hours)
- 200Ah 12V battery = 2.4 kWh capacity (usable: 1.2 kWh at 50% DOD with AGM,
  1.9 kWh at 80% DOD with LiFePO4)
- **Minimum workable system**: 200W panel + 200Ah LiFePO4 battery dedicated to freezer;
  add 100W/100Ah for each additional cubic foot of freezer space.

**Chest vs. upright**: A 5 cu ft chest freezer uses ~100 kWh/year; a 5 cu ft upright
uses ~180–250 kWh/year. Chest freezers are dramatically more efficient because cold
air is denser than warm air and falls to the bottom when the lid opens — versus an
upright where cold air spills out the front every time it opens.

### 6.6 Freeze-Drying

**Harvest Right home freeze-dryer** ($2,800–$5,000 depending on size). Freeze-drying
removes moisture by sublimation (ice converts directly to vapor under vacuum without
passing through liquid phase). Result: **95%+ moisture removal**, compared to 70–80%
for conventional dehydration.

- Power draw: **1,100W average**, running **30–40 hours per batch** = roughly
  **35–50 kWh per batch**. Significant energy demand for off-grid systems.
- Shelf life: **25 years** in sealed mylar with oxygen absorbers. No other method
  achieves this without commercial-scale equipment.
- Pre-treatment: not required. No blanching, no pre-drying, no acid dips needed.
- Texture: rehydrates nearly identically to fresh, unlike conventional dehydration.
- Cost per pound: freeze-dried food costs $0.50–$2.00/lb to produce at home
  (factoring equipment amortization and energy); commercially freeze-dried retails
  at $5–$25/lb. ROI is strong for a household that uses it consistently.

**Practical limitation**: the 35–50 kWh/batch energy requirement means freeze-drying
is most practical with generator power or a large solar system with surplus capacity
in summer. Budget it as a generator task.

### 6.7 Power Failure Protocol

**Refrigerator**: food is safe for **4 hours** with door kept closed.
**Full chest freezer**: food is safe for **48 hours** if kept closed.
**Half-full chest freezer**: food is safe for **24 hours**.

Actions when power failure is confirmed as extended:
1. Do not open freezer; insulate externally with blankets if no dry ice available.
2. Obtain dry ice: 25 lb dry ice maintains a 5 cu ft freezer for 2–3 days.
3. If no dry ice: prioritize meats for preservation — can raw meat in pressure canner
   or cook and consume immediately. Vegetables and fruit for jam or dehydrate.
4. Once 50% thawed, do not refreeze. Cook immediately.

## Section 7: Smoking & Curing

### 7.1 Cold Smoke vs. Hot Smoke

**Cold smoking** (below 90°F, often 70–80°F): Provides smoke flavor and surface
antimicrobial effect but **does not cook or fully preserve food**. Cold-smoked
salmon is still raw. Cold smoking is a flavor step, not a complete preservation
method — it must be combined with curing (salt, nitrates) to be safe for extended
storage at room temperature.

**Hot smoking** (165–250°F, maintaining internal food temp of 145–165°F+):
Cooks the food fully and provides some drying. Hot-smoked products are shelf-stable
for short periods without refrigeration. Hot-smoked fish to 145°F internal is safe;
hot-smoked whole poultry to 165°F internal is fully cooked.

### 7.2 Curing Chemistry

**The botulism problem with cured meats**: Smoking alone, without curing salt,
does not guarantee safety against *C. botulinum* in the anaerobic interior of
thick cuts. The smoke penetrates surface; the core of a 4-inch-thick ham may
never reach antimicrobial temperatures or have antimicrobial salt concentration.

**Nitrites inhibit *C. botulinum* germination** in cured meat by reacting with
the myoglobin in muscle tissue, turning it pink (the characteristic color of cured
meat) and chemically blocking the toxin production pathway.

- **Prague Powder #1 (Pink Curing Salt #1)**: Contains 6.25% sodium nitrite (NaNO2)
  and 93.75% salt. Used for **short-cure products**: bacon (7–14 day cure), ham
  (7–21 days), smoked sausage, hot dogs. Nitrite breaks down during cooking.
- **Prague Powder #2 (Pink Curing Salt #2)**: Contains 6.25% sodium nitrite,
  4% sodium nitrate (NaNO3), and salt. Used for **long-cure dry-aged products**:
  prosciutto, dry-cured salamis (curing weeks to months). The nitrate acts as
  a slow-release reservoir, converting to nitrite over the extended cure period.
- **Do not confuse pink curing salt with pink Himalayan salt**: they look similar
  but are chemically unrelated. Pink Himalayan salt has no curing function.

### 7.3 Equilibrium Curing Method

Equilibrium curing (EQ cure) is the most reliable and forgiving method for home
use. It applies a precise percentage of curing ingredients relative to meat weight,
eliminating the risk of under-curing or over-curing.

**Standard EQ cure ratios (per kg of meat):**
| Ingredient | Amount per kg meat |
|---|---|
| Non-iodized salt | 17.5 g (1.75%) |
| Prague Powder #1 | 2.5 g (0.25%) |
| Sugar (optional, flavor) | 5–10 g |
| Black pepper, garlic, herbs | To taste |

Apply cure to all surfaces, vacuum seal or wrap tightly in plastic, refrigerate.
Allow **1 day per 0.5 inch of thickness**, minimum 7 days for any cut. Maximum
14 days in refrigerator; or flip daily if not vacuum sealed to redistribute cure.

### 7.4 Smoker Construction Options

**Barrel smoker (intermediate)**: 55-gallon food-grade steel drum (never used for
chemicals; check origin). Cut circular lid at top for chimney; install coal grate
8 inches from bottom; install food grates at 18 inches and 30 inches; add door at
bottom for fuel access; install thermometer at grate level. Materials: $50–$120.

**UDS (Ugly Drum Smoker)**: Same basic construction as above but designed around
charcoal basket at bottom and air intake valves for precise temperature control.
Highly efficient; holds 250°F for 10+ hours on single charcoal load. Standard
homestead hot smoker. Total cost with lid and grates: $80–$150.

**Brick smokehouse (permanent)**: 8×8 ft brick or block structure; separate firebox
connected by underground flue to smoking chamber (for cold smoking); chimney at
top with damper; hanging rods inside for whole hams, fish, and sausages. Capacity
for 200+ lb of product per batch. Materials: $400–$1,200; significant labor.

### 7.5 Wood Species Guide

| Wood type | Flavor profile | Best for | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Apple | Mild, sweet, fruity | Poultry, pork, fish | Available wherever apple orchards exist |
| Cherry | Mild, slightly sweet, mahogany color | Pork, poultry, beef | Dark color on surface |
| Hickory | Strong, classic BBQ smoke | Pork ribs, bacon, beef | Use sparingly; overpowering in quantity |
| Oak | Medium, slightly earthy | All meats, long smokes | Most versatile; widely available |
| Alder | Light, slightly sweet | Fish (traditional for salmon) | Traditional Pacific NW salmon wood |
| Maple | Mild, slightly sweet | Poultry, pork, vegetables | Good general-purpose |
| Mesquite | Very strong, earthy, harsh | Beef short smokes only | Burns very hot; bitter if over-smoked |
| Pecan | Medium, nutty, rich | Pork, poultry, beef | Similar to hickory, milder |
| **Softwoods (pine, spruce, cedar, fir)** | **Acrid, resinous** | **Never use** | **Resins produce PAHs and off-flavor; toxic** |
| **Treated lumber or plywood** | **Chemical** | **Never use** | **Arsenic, formaldehyde; dangerous** |

### 7.6 Fish Smoking Process

**Brine**: Dissolve 1 cup non-iodized salt + 1/2 cup brown sugar per gallon of
cold water. Submerge fish fillets (skin on; 1/2–3/4 inch thick). Brine 8–24 hours
refrigerated (longer = saltier/firmer; shorter = milder).

**Pellicle formation**: Remove from brine; rinse briefly; pat dry; place on racks
in cool, breezy location **1–3 hours** until surface feels tacky. This protein
film (pellicle) is essential — it accepts smoke adhesion and forms a barrier.
Skipping pellicle formation produces pale, patchy smoke uptake.

**Cold smoke** (flavor only; not shelf-stable without refrigeration): 70–85°F,
4–12 hours. Product is still raw. Refrigerate; consume within 5 days or freeze.

**Hot smoke** (cooked, short shelf life): Bring smoker to 225°F; smoke to internal
fish temp of **145°F** (typically 2–4 hours for 1/2-inch fillets). Shelf stable
at room temperature 3–5 days; refrigerated 1–2 weeks.

### 7.7 Approximate Shelf Life of Smoked and Cured Products

| Product | Method | Unrefrigerated shelf life | Refrigerated |
|---|---|---|---|
| Properly cured/smoked bacon (EQ cure, hot smoke) | Dry cure + hot smoke | 3–4 weeks if fully cooked | 3 months |
| Whole cured ham (dry cure) | EQ cure only, unsmoked | 3–6 months (country ham style) | 9–12 months |
| Cold-smoked salmon | Cold smoke only (no full cure) | 3–5 days | 2–3 weeks |
| Hot-smoked fish (to 145°F) | Hot smoke | 3–5 days | 2 weeks |
| Smoked beef jerky (fully dried) | Dry cure + hot dehydrate | 1–2 months | 6 months |
| Smoked sausage (fully cooked) | Cure + hot smoke to 165°F | 1–2 weeks | 6 weeks |

## Section 8: Vinegar Pickling

### 8.1 The Chemistry

Pickling in vinegar preserves food through **acidification**: acetic acid in
vinegar drops the pH below 4.6, inhibiting *C. botulinum* and most other pathogens.
**This only works if the vinegar maintains at least 5% acidity throughout the product.**

Standard commercial white vinegar and apple cider vinegar are **5% acidity** —
the minimum safe concentration. Do not use homemade vinegar of unknown acidity
for shelf-stable canning. **Never dilute vinegar below 5%** in a canning recipe —
if a recipe specifies 1:1 water:vinegar, that ratio is already calculated into
the pH safety test.

**Quick pickles (refrigerator pickles)**: Use any vinegar concentration; not
shelf-stable; consume within 3 months refrigerated. Safe because they stay cold.

**Shelf-stable pickles**: Require water bath canning after filling; must use
tested recipes with validated brine ratios.

### 8.2 Standard Brine Ratios

| Brine type | Ratio | Use case | Flavor profile |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sharp pickling | Full-strength vinegar (no water) | Peppers, pickled eggs | Very sharp/sour |
| Standard pickling | 1 cup vinegar : 1 cup water | Cucumbers, green beans, beets | Balanced |
| Mild/bread & butter | 2 cups vinegar : 1 cup water + sugar | Sweet pickles | Sweet-sour |

**Salt role**: Draws water out of vegetables (osmotic effect), seasons the brine,
contributes to texture. Use **pickling salt** (pure sodium chloride, no additives)
or kosher salt. Table salt with anti-caking agents causes cloudy brine — not unsafe
but unattractive.

**Sugar role**: Flavor balance; slightly softens acidity. Not required for safety.

### 8.3 Essential Pickling Spices

- Dill (fresh head or seeds): cucumber pickles, green beans
- Mustard seed (yellow or brown): all pickles; adds complexity
- Black peppercorns: heat and depth
- Bay leaf: subtle herbal background
- Garlic cloves: flavor; use 1–2 per pint
- Red pepper flakes: heat; adjust to preference
- Celery seed: brine-forward flavor
- Coriander seed: aromatic; bread-and-butter style
- **Pickle Crisp (Ball brand, calcium chloride)**: add 1/8 tsp per pint to maintain
  crunchiness. Replaces the old alum (aluminum sulfate) used in historical recipes.
  **Avoid alum** — large amounts are toxic; calcium chloride is the safe modern alternative.

### 8.4 Crop-Specific Pickling Guide

| Crop | Variety/prep | Brine | Processing time (WB) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cucumbers (dill spears) | Pickling variety; 1/4" spears | 1:1 VW + dill, garlic | 15 min/pint, 20 min/quart | Keep cold before pickling; soft = overripe |
| Cucumbers (bread & butter) | Thin slices; 1-hr salt soak, rinse | Vinegar + sugar + turmeric | 10 min/pint | Classic sweet pickle |
| Green beans (dilly) | Whole, trimmed to jar height | 1:1 VW + dill, garlic, pepper | 10 min/pint | Add red pepper for heat |
| Beets (whole, small) | Cook until barely tender; peel | Straight vinegar + sugar, cloves | 30 min/pint, 35 min/quart | Slippery but delicious; add orange peel |
| Peppers (banana, Anaheim) | 1/4" rings | Straight vinegar or 3:1 V:W | 10 min/half-pint | Roast first for depth |
| Hot peppers | Whole or sliced | Straight vinegar | 10 min/half-pint | Wear gloves |
| Cauliflower florets | Cut 1.5" florets | 1:1 VW + turmeric, mustard seed | 15 min/pint | Add red pepper |
| Carrots (sticks) | 1/4" sticks, jar height | 1:1 VW + dill, garlic | 10 min/pint | Good quick-pickle option |
| Onions (pearl or rings) | Peel; rings 1/4" | 1:1 VW + sugar, peppercorns | 10 min/pint | Quick-pickle holds up well |
| Pickled garlic cloves | Peeled whole cloves | Straight vinegar + herbs | 10 min/half-pint | Mellow in flavor over months |
| Watermelon rind | Peel green; 1" cubes | 1:1 VW + sugar, cloves, cinnamon | 10 min/pint | Classic Southern preservation |

### 8.5 Shelf Life

- Sealed, WB-processed pickles: **12–18 months** best quality; safe 2 years if sealed.
- After opening: refrigerate; consume within **3 months**.
- Quick pickles (never processed): refrigerate immediately; 1–3 months.

## Section 9: Oil Preservation

### 9.1 Safety Warning — Garlic-in-Oil

**Garlic-in-oil at room temperature is a documented botulism risk and should not
be stored this way.** Garlic is a low-acid, low-moisture vegetable. Submerged in
oil, it is in an anaerobic environment. Any water content on the garlic provides
the moisture needed for *C. botulinum* spore germination. The oil provides the
neutral pH and anaerobic environment. Room temperature provides the warmth.
All three conditions for toxin production are present simultaneously.

USDA position: acidify garlic (in water/acid solution) before adding to oil, OR
refrigerate and consume within **1 week**, OR freeze.

**Safe applications for oil preservation:**
- **Dry herb-infused oils**: dried (not fresh) herbs have no water content that
  supports pathogen growth. Dry rosemary, thyme, bay leaf, dried chiles in oil
  are safe at room temperature; use within 1 month for best flavor.
- **Dried chile oils**: dry chile flakes or whole dried chiles submerged in oil
  — safe; use within 1 month.
- **Sun-dried tomatoes in oil**: commercially produced sun-dried tomatoes in oil
  are acidified or have low enough water activity. Home sun-dried tomatoes must
  be dehydrated until brittle and bone-dry before packing in oil; refrigerate and
  use within 2 weeks.

### 9.2 Traditional Confit

Confit is the technique of cooking low-and-slow in fat, then preserving submerged
in that fat. The fat excludes air (anaerobic), and the thorough cooking eliminates
active bacteria. This is not indefinite shelf-stable preservation — it is
refrigerator-extended storage.

**Duck leg confit** (classic): Season legs with salt, pepper, thyme, garlic; cure
24 hours refrigerated. Submerge in rendered duck fat or lard in a Dutch oven.
Cook at **250°F for 2.5–3 hours** until meat is very tender and pulls easily from
bone. Cool, then pack legs into jars fully covered by fat. Refrigerate.

- Shelf life in refrigerator: **3–4 weeks** (fat must completely cover meat at all times)
- Shelf life canned in fat (water bath 30 min/pint): **12+ months** — this applies
  the same water bath acid argument; confit at neutral pH should technically be
  pressure canned. This is an area where traditional practice and USDA guidance
  diverge. For maximum safety: pressure can confit at 11 PSI for 75 minutes/quart.

**Garlic confit (safe method)**: Peeled garlic cloves simmered in olive oil at
250°F for 45 minutes until soft and golden. Garlic is fully cooked; internal
temperature well above 212°F through extended cooking. Refrigerate immediately;
consume within 1 week. Do not store at room temperature.

### 9.3 Lard Rendering

Lard is the traditional preservation fat of temperate-climate homesteads. When
properly rendered and sealed, it keeps well without refrigeration.

**Sources:**
- **Leaf lard** (from kidney fat deposits): highest quality; nearly flavorless;
  ideal for pastry and baking.
- **Back fat**: good quality; mild flavor; most abundant from a whole hog.
- **Fatback and caul fat**: slightly stronger flavor; good for cooking lard.

**Dry rendering** (preferred): Cut fat into small cubes (1/2 inch); place in
heavy pot over very low heat. Stir frequently. Fat liquifies slowly over 2–4 hours.
Cracklings (protein residue) will brown and sink. Strain through fine mesh or
cheesecloth into sanitized glass jars while hot. Seal immediately.

**Wet rendering**: Add small amount of water to fat initially; prevents scorching
but produces a slightly lower shelf-life product (residual water speeds rancidity).

**Shelf life of properly rendered, sealed lard:**
- Room temperature (cool pantry, below 65°F): **6 months**
- Refrigerator: **12 months**
- Freezer: **24 months**

**Rancidity check**: fresh lard is white/cream with mild or neutral odor. Rancid
lard smells strongly of "old fat" or paint. Do not consume rancid lard; it contains
oxidation byproducts that cause inflammation and off-flavors.

## Section 10: Grain, Legume & Seed Storage

### 10.1 The Dry Storage Triad

Long-term grain storage operates on three variables, all of which must be
controlled simultaneously:

1. **Moisture below 10%** (ideally below 8%): Above 10%, mold begins; above 14%,
   grain generates heat through microbial activity and can spontaneously combust
   in large quantities. **This is the most critical variable.**
2. **Temperature below 70°F**: Lower is better. At 40°F, even slightly moist grain
   stores well. At 80°F, properly dried grain still degrades faster.
3. **Oxygen exclusion**: Oxygen supports insect metabolism, fat oxidation in germ,
   and aerobic mold. Eliminate with oxygen absorbers or CO2 purge.

**Grain moisture testing**:
- Fingernail test: press thumbnail into grain kernel. Proper dryness = kernel is hard,
  thumbnail does not penetrate. Soft or doughy = too moist.
- Moisture meter (Grain Pro or equivalent: $40–$80): accurate to ±0.5%; worth
  owning if storing significant quantities.
- The jar test: fill jar with grain, seal overnight. If condensation appears inside
  jar in the morning, grain is too moist. Continue drying.

### 10.2 Mylar Bags and Oxygen Absorbers

**Mylar bags** (4-7 mil thickness) provide excellent oxygen and moisture barrier
properties far superior to polyethylene zip bags or food-grade buckets alone.
The industry standard for 20+ year grain storage combines:

1. 5-gallon HDPE food-grade bucket (structural; pest barrier; handles)
2. 5-gallon mylar bag liner (oxygen/moisture barrier)
3. 2000cc oxygen absorbers placed on top of grain after filling
4. Heat-seal mylar bag (clothes iron on highest cotton setting; seal across top in
   a single pass; or impulse sealer for faster, more consistent results)
5. Snap gamma-seal lid on bucket (allows regular access without destroying the bucket
   seal system)

**Oxygen absorber sizing:**
| Container volume | O2 absorber size |
|---|---|
| 1-quart jar | 100cc |
| 1-gallon container | 300cc |
| 5-gallon bucket | 2000cc (or 4×500cc) |

**Sourcing buckets**: Restaurant supply stores and bakeries discard food-grade 5-gallon
buckets in volume. Food-grade buckets have a recycling symbol with a "2" (HDPE) and
often "HDPE" stamped on the bottom. Avoid non-food-grade buckets — chemical residue
cannot be fully cleaned. Inspect for cracks and odors before use.

### 10.3 Storage Duration by Commodity

| Commodity | Moisture threshold | Storage duration (optimal conditions) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| White rice (long-grain) | Below 8% | **25–30 years** | Brown rice stores only 6–12 months (oils in bran go rancid) |
| Hard red/white wheat berries | Below 10% | **25–30 years** | Grind as needed; stone mill preferred |
| Soft white wheat | Below 10% | 25 years | Better for pastry/cakes |
| Dry beans (pinto, black, kidney) | Below 10% | **8–10 years** (germination); indefinite (food) | After 10 years, cooking time increases significantly; still nutritious |
| Lentils | Below 10% | 8–10 years | Cook faster than beans even after extended storage |
| Rolled oats | Below 8% | **20–30 years** (mylar/O2) | Plain oats; not instant (added ingredients shorten life) |
| Cornmeal (degerminated) | Below 10% | **5–10 years** | Degerminated lasts longer than whole-grain; loses some nutrition |
| Whole corn (field/dent) | Below 13% | 10–15 years | Grind to cornmeal as needed |
| All-purpose flour (white) | Below 10% | **5 years** (mylar/O2) | Oils in bran removed; more stable than whole wheat flour |

### 10.4 Pest Management

**Grain weevils** (*Sitophilus granarius*) and **Indian meal moths** (*Plodia
interpunctella*) are the primary grain pests. Both are introduced either via
contaminated grain or as eggs/larvae before storage. Prevention is the only
effective strategy — elimination after infestation is extremely difficult.

**Prevention methods:**
- **Freeze before storing**: place grain in freezer at 0°F for **1 week** before
  sealing. Kills eggs, larvae, and adults of all common grain pests. Most effective
  and reliable prevention.
- **Oxygen absorbers**: eliminating oxygen kills aerobic insects within days to weeks.
  Does not kill eggs as quickly as freezing, but prevents population growth.
- **Diatomaceous earth (food-grade, not pool-grade)**: Add **1 cup per 40 lbs of grain**.
  Food-grade DE is fossilized diatoms; microscopic sharp edges damage insect exoskeletons,
  causing death by desiccation. Does not affect nutritional value if used at correct
  rates. **Do not inhale** — fine silica particles cause respiratory irritation.
- **Temperature control**: below 40°F kills adults; below 0°F kills all life stages.
  Above 95°F also kills insects but damages grain quality.
- **Bay leaves**: placed around storage area (not in grain); anecdotal evidence of
  deterrent effect. Not reliable as a primary method.

### 10.5 First-In, First-Out Rotation

**Shelving strategy**: Store newest stock at the back; consume oldest stock from
the front. Label every container with: **commodity name, variety, harvest/package
date, and quantity**. Use permanent marker directly on mylar or on label adhered
with tape.

**Annual inventory**: Every fall, before new harvest is stored, take full inventory
of existing stores. Calculate consumption rate (how much was consumed in the past
year) and compare to remaining stock. This determines whether this year's harvest
is surplus (add to stores), adequate (replace consumed), or deficit (ration or
increase next year's production).

**Consumption planning guide (per person per year):**
| Commodity | Estimated annual consumption | Storage space (5-gal buckets) |
|---|---|---|
| Wheat berries (for bread + grain meals) | 200–300 lb | 5–7 buckets |
| White rice | 100–150 lb | 2–3 buckets |
| Dry beans (all varieties) | 75–100 lb | 2 buckets |
| Oats | 50–75 lb | 1–2 buckets |
| Cornmeal | 25–50 lb | 1 bucket |
| Sugar | 40–60 lb | 1 bucket |
| Salt | 8–12 lb | 0.25 bucket |

### 10.6 The Whole-Grain Advantage

**Whole wheat berries store 15–20× longer than whole wheat flour**. The reason:
the oils concentrated in the wheat germ oxidize rapidly once the bran layer is
broken. Flour has enormous surface area; oxidation is rapid. Whole berries have
intact bran protecting the germ.

**Hand grain mills** for on-demand grinding:
- **Country Living Grain Mill** (~$600–$680): cast iron; American-made; grinds
  corn, wheat, rice, and coffee; highest throughput of manual mills (~1 lb/minute
  for coarse flour by a strong adult). The homestead standard.
- **Wonder Junior Deluxe** (~$280–$320): lighter; grinds wheat, oats, some wet
  grains. Good for smaller households. Slower throughput.
- **Diamant grain mill** (~$800+): Danish-made; cast iron; extremely durable;
  highest quality but heaviest and most expensive.
- **Grinding estimate**: producing 1 cup of flour takes approximately 5–8 minutes
  of moderate effort on a hand mill. For regular bread baking, expect 15–20 minutes
  of grinding per loaf. Budget this time into the off-grid daily routine.

## Section 11: Integrated Preservation Calendar

*Calibrated for Zone 5 (Chicago/Denver/Pittsburgh latitude). Adjust ±4 weeks per
zone: Zone 4 = start 4 weeks later; Zone 6 = start 4 weeks earlier.*

### January
- Cellar inspection: check all stored roots; remove anything soft, sprouting, or
  molded. Rotate stock (consume oldest first).
- Fermentation projects: winter is ideal for long, slow sauerkraut and kimchi ferments.
- Grain inventory: count all sealed buckets; calculate remaining months of supply.
- Review seed stock: inventory and plan seed orders for spring.
- Dehydrate any cellar items showing signs of aging before they are lost.

### February
- Continue cellar rotation; apple storage often peaks in deterioration now.
- Begin fermenting winter squash surplus (cut, roast, puree, freeze if needed).
- Clean and inspect canning equipment: check jar rims, replace lids, test canner
  pressure gauge (dial gauge models require annual calibration by extension office).
- Order canning lids, pickling salt, citric acid in advance of spring season.

### March
- Assess cellar: most storage crops are finishing. Consume quickly or preserve
  what remains (can beets and carrots that are softening; ferment cabbage scraps).
- Start lacto-fermented beverages (kvass, water kefir) for spring.
- Last chance to process any frozen meat that has been in freezer since fall.

### April
- Root cellar empties. Clean, air out, inspect for mold, and repair as needed.
- First greens from garden (cold frames, tunnels) — too fresh to preserve; eat now.
- Asparagus begins in mid-April to early May (Zone 5): too few for canning;
  pickle small quantities in pints if surplus arises.

### May
- **Rhubarb**: high acid; ideal for water bath canning as sauce, jam, or compote.
  Rhubarb-strawberry jam if freezer strawberries remain.
- Asparagus: lacto-ferment dilly asparagus; can pickled asparagus.
- Early greens: dehydrate nettle, dandelion greens, and other spring wild edibles
  for tea and cooking herbs.
- Set up solar dehydrator for the season.

### June
- **Strawberries** (peak Zone 5: mid-June): make jam (high-sugar preserves shelf-stable
  12+ months), dehydrate as fruit leather, freeze surplus. High-pectin varieties
  jam well without added pectin; low-pectin varieties need Sure-Jell or Pomona pectin.
- **Peas**: blanch and freeze immediately after harvest — peas lose sugar to starch
  conversion within hours of picking; freeze is better than canning for quality.
- Herb harvest: basil, dill (before flowering), parsley — dehydrate at low temp;
  freeze basil in oil in ice cube trays.
- Begin cucumber pickles as cucumbers start producing (late June Zone 5).

### July
- **Blueberries**: freeze (easiest), dehydrate, or make jam. Flash freeze on sheet
  pans before bagging.
- **Green beans** begin: lacto-ferment dilly beans; pressure can green beans;
  dehydrate. High-production weeks — plan to process every 2–3 days.
- **Summer squash** (zucchini): dehydrate excess; shred and freeze for winter
  baking; zucchini relish (water bath).
- **Cucumbers** peak: primary pickling window. Process every 3–5 days at peak.
  Cucumbers deteriorate fast — pickle within 24 hours of harvest for best crunch.
- **Garlic** harvest and cure: hang in dry, airy space 3–4 weeks before storage.

### August
- **Tomato preservation peak**: the most intensive preservation month on most homesteads.
  Prioritize: crushed tomatoes (canned), salsa (water bath), tomato paste (long-cook
  reduction then can), sun-dried tomatoes, tomato sauce (pressure can or freeze).
  Budget 6–8 hours per bushel (53 lb) for processing.
- **Corn**: blanch and freeze; pressure can cream-style corn; dehydrate off-cob.
- **Peppers** (bell and hot): roast and freeze bells; ferment hot peppers into hot sauce
  or dry and dehydrate whole or as flakes.
- **Onions**: harvest when tops fall over; cure 2–3 weeks in field or barn; then
  store in cellar or braid.
- **Peaches/nectarines**: water bath can in light syrup or juice; dehydrate; freeze.
- **Apples** (early varieties): applesauce (water bath); dehydrated rings; freeze
  for pie filling.

### September
- **Apple harvest** (main crop): root cellar storage (wrapped), applesauce, cider
  (fermented or fresh-frozen), apple butter (WB), dehydrated.
- **Winter squash**: cure 10–14 days at 80–85°F before cellar storage. Insulated
  garage or warm room; one spot of curing failure spoils the entire squash.
- **Root vegetable harvest**: carrots, beets, parsnips, turnips — harvest before
  hard freeze; clean, pack in damp sand in root cellar.
- **Potatoes**: harvest when tops die back; cure 2 weeks at 60–65°F, 85% humidity
  before moving to cold cellar.
- **Dry beans**: allow to dry on vine; harvest before frost on bean pods; shell and
  store.
- **Tomatoes (green)**: green tomato pickle or relish; green salsa; chutney.

### October
- **Late apple varieties**: store and process. Make vinegar from apple scraps and
  juice (6–8 week fermentation to cider, then 4–8 more weeks to vinegar with
  mother). Natural 5% acidity; test before using for shelf-stable canning.
- **Meat preservation season begins**: hunting season opens; process venison and
  other wild game — can, freeze, dry, cure.
- **Cellar is full**: conduct full inventory before winter; label everything.
- Inspect root cellar insulation and door seals before temperatures drop.

### November
- **Pig processing** (if running seasonal pigs): slaughter after first hard frost
  (flies gone; natural cold assists chilling); process within 48 hours of slaughter.
  Render lard, cure bacon and hams, grind sausage, can headcheese and broth.
- **Deer processing**: butcher; freeze steaks/roasts; can burger and stew meat;
  make jerky from lean cuts; render tallow.
- Final fermentation projects: sauerkraut batch to carry through winter.
- Assess grain stores: are buckets properly sealed? Any signs of pest activity?

### December
- **Cellar maintenance only**: check monthly; remove problems immediately.
- **Planning season**: review what worked, what failed, what ran out too early.
  Adjust next year's preservation targets.
- Open stored ferments; begin consuming oldest stock.
- Assess freezer inventory; plan protein consumption through February.

## Section 12: Disaster Scenarios

### 12.1 Power Grid Failure

**Priority actions (first 4 hours)**:
1. Do not open freezer or refrigerator.
2. Assess likely duration (generator fuel, battery reserves, weather).
3. If loss is likely > 24 hours, begin triage: what must be consumed or preserved?

**Triage order** (process in this sequence):
1. Raw fresh meats and fish — preserve immediately (can, smoke, or cook and consume)
2. Dairy — consume fresh milk within 4 hours; make cheese or butter
3. Opened canned goods — refrigerate in cold water; consume within 8 hours
4. Frozen vegetables — blanch and can, or dehydrate on propane camp stove
5. Frozen fruits — jam or dehydrate

**Emergency canning on propane camp stove or open fire**: Yes, pressure canning
can be performed on a propane burner outdoors. Requirements:
- Burner must maintain consistent high heat to hold pressure without fluctuation.
- **Do not use open campfire** for pressure canning — the BTU output is inconsistent
  and impossible to regulate; proper pressure cannot be maintained reliably.
- A single-burner 15,000–30,000 BTU propane camp stove works adequately.
- Processing times are the same as indoor stove; verify pressure gauge continually.

### 12.2 Extended Winter / Crop Failure

**Minimum caloric storage math for total crop failure — family of 4 for 12 months:**

| Category | Quantity needed | Weight | Buckets (5-gal) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Wheat berries | 900 lb | 900 lb | 20 | Primary carbohydrate + protein |
| White rice | 400 lb | 400 lb | 8 | Secondary carb; fast cooking |
| Dry beans | 300 lb | 300 lb | 6 | Protein + fiber |
| Oats | 200 lb | 200 lb | 4 | Breakfast; thickening |
| Sugar | 150 lb | 150 lb | 3 | Calories + preserving |
| Salt | 40 lb | 40 lb | 1 | Essential; no substitute |
| Oil/fat | 80 lb | 80 lb | — | Canned lard or sealed oil |
| **Total** | **2,070 lb** | | **~42 buckets** | Plus canned goods, dehydrated food |

At 2,500 calories/person/day, this represents approximately **2,000 calories/person/day
for 12 months** — slightly low; supplement with canned goods, dehydrated food, and
anything foraged or produced from animals. A hunting season produces 80–150 lb of
venison (processed weight) which adds significant protein margin.

**Caloric density of the above mix**: approximately 1,400–1,600 cal/lb average.
Total: 2,070 lb × 1,500 cal/lb = **3,105,000 calories** = approximately
**2,120 cal/person/day** for a family of 4 for 365 days.

### 12.3 Water Shortage During Preservation

**Canning** requires significant water: typically 3–4 gallons per pressure canning
batch, 4–6 gallons per water bath batch. During water shortage:

- **Water bath canning water is reusable** for multiple consecutive batches. Let it
  cool, re-examine for debris, reheat and reuse. Replace after 3–4 batches or if
  visibly cloudy.
- **Pressure canning condensate**: collect steam that condenses on lid during venting.
  It is essentially distilled water; use it for the next batch's initial water.
- **Dehydration requires no water**: during water shortage, pivot preservation strategy
  toward dehydration. Electric or solar dehydrator operates without water input.
- **Fermentation requires minimal water**: a 2% brine uses only enough water to
  submerge produce — typically 1–2 quarts per gallon jar.
- **Root cellaring and dry storage require no water**: continue without modification.

### 12.4 Nuclear Contamination

**Does cooking or canning remove radiation from food?**

No. **Cooking and canning do not remove radioactive isotopes** from contaminated
food. Cesium-137 (Cs-137, 30-year half-life) and Strontium-90 (Sr-90, 29-year
half-life) are systemic isotopes — they are absorbed into the plant's cellular
structure from contaminated soil or water. Heat processing has no effect on
radioactive atoms.

**What actually works:**
- **Source control**: avoid contaminated soil and water entirely. This is the only
  effective strategy. Pre-event stored food (sealed grains, canned goods, sealed
  water cisterns) is uncontaminated and safe.
- **Peeling**: removes a portion of surface-deposited fallout particles but does
  not remove systemic isotopes.
- **Washing**: reduces surface contamination; does not eliminate systemic uptake.

**Food safety by source post-event:**
| Food source | Safety status | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-stored sealed grains and canned goods | Safe | No contamination if stored before event |
| Pre-filled covered water cisterns | Safe | Covered before fallout; surface deposition minimal |
| Root vegetables from contaminated soil | Moderate risk | Peel before consuming; Cs-137 uptake depends on soil K+ levels |
| Leafy greens from open garden | High risk | Direct deposition; large surface area; avoid |
| Milk from grazing animals | High risk | Cs-137 concentrates in milk; I-131 risk (short-term) |
| Wild mushrooms | Very high risk | Cs-137 hyperaccumulators; avoid for 1–3 years post-event |
| Fish from open water | Moderate risk | Bioaccumulation; avoid fish from local ponds |
| Grain harvested after event (from contaminated soil) | High risk | Avoid if soil is contaminated |

**Preservation implications**: preserve all pre-event food stocks as long-term stores
before ground-level contamination becomes an issue. Sealed canning, vacuum-sealed
grains, and frozen meats from pre-event are safe and should be treated as the
primary food supply during the 2-week shelter period and for months following.

### 12.5 Equipment Failure Mid-Process

| Equipment failure | Situation | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Pressure canner seal fails mid-process | Discovered before batch completion | Repair seal (check gasket, lid alignment); re-bring to pressure; restart full timing |
| Jar did not seal (discovered same day) | Lid flexes; no seal | Refrigerate and consume within 1 week OR reprocess with new lid within 24 hours (reprocessing degrades quality) |
| Dehydrator dies mid-batch | Food partially dried | Finish in oven at lowest setting (150–170°F) with oven door propped 1 inch for airflow |
| Power outage during smoking (hot smoker) | Smoker temperature dropping | Transfer to oven; finish to 165°F internal (poultry) or 145°F (fish, pork) using instant-read thermometer |
| Power outage during freeze-drying | Mid-cycle | If machine is cold and food is frozen, seal and hold; restart when power restored; food will still be processable |
| Canning pot boils dry | During water bath processing | Carefully add boiling water (not cold — thermal shock can break jars); add down the side, not directly on jars; resume timing from where it stopped if jar tops are still submerged |
