# Domain 12: Security & Defense

> Security is not a product you buy — it is a layered system of habits, relationships,
> physical hardening, and contingency plans. The goal is not to turn your homestead
> into a fortress but to make it unrewarding to attack and resilient enough to absorb
> the most likely threats. Prevention beats response every time.

---

## Quick-Reference Decision Framework

Locate your threat profile, then jump to the relevant section.

| Scenario | Primary concern | First action | Key section |
|---|---|---|---|
| New rural property, starting out | Establish baseline awareness | Dog + lights + cameras | Sections 2, 3 |

Read the full document once to understand how the layers interlock. Security measures that ignore context — buying a gun before installing a door deadbolt, for example — are poor investments.

## 1. Threat Assessment Framework

### 1.1 Threat Matrix

Effective security planning requires calibrating response to actual risk. The following matrix scores threats by probability (how often it happens to rural properties) and consequence (severity of harm if it occurs). High-probability, high-consequence threats get resources first.

| Threat | Probability | Consequence | Priority | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Livestock predation (coyotes, foxes) | Very high | Low–medium | **High** | Universal in rural US; consistent attrition if unaddressed |
| Aerial predators (hawks, owls) | High | Low–medium | **High** | Especially devastating to free-range poultry |
| Opportunistic theft (tools, fuel, equipment) | Medium–high | Medium | **High** | Rural properties are soft targets; outbuildings often unlocked |
| Trespassing / scouting | Medium | Low–medium | Medium | Often precedes theft; note and address |
| Vehicle/equipment theft | Medium | High | **High** | ATVs, trailers, chainsaws; rarely recovered |
| Break-in while unoccupied | Medium | High | **High** | Most rural burglaries occur when residents are away |
| Break-in while occupied | Low–medium | Very high | **High** | Less common but higher stakes |
| Organized criminal activity | Low | Very high | Medium | Regional variance; increases with economic stress |
| Domestic/personal dispute spillover | Low–medium | Variable | Medium | More common than strangers; de-escalation key |
| Bear depredation | Low–high (regional) | Medium–high | Medium | Essential in Appalachians, Rockies, Pacific NW |
| Mountain lion / wolf attack | Very low | Very high | Low (general); medium (affected regions) | Know your region |
| Civil unrest spillover from urban areas | Low | High | Medium | Depends heavily on proximity to cities |
| Organized group / raider scenario | Very low | Catastrophic | Low | Plan for it but don't organize your entire life around it |

**Using this matrix:** For any investment over $200, ask which row it addresses and whether that row warrants the spend. Installing a $3,000 safe to protect against organized raiders when you have no deadbolt on your front door is the wrong order.

### 1.2 Property Characteristics and Risk Level

Your property's physical characteristics directly affect which threats you face.

**Visibility from road:**
- High visibility (structures within 100–200 ft of road) = more opportunistic risk; people see what you have
- Low visibility (structures 500+ ft from road, screened by trees) = less opportunistic risk but longer response time from passersby in an emergency

**Remoteness:**
- Nearest neighbor within 1 mile: moderate response time; community aware of your activity patterns
- Nearest neighbor 3–10 miles: long response time; self-reliance is default, not exception
- Law enforcement response time (rural): typically 20–60 minutes; in frontier areas 1–4 hours

**Visible signs of prosperity:**
- New trucks, visible solar arrays, large fuel tanks, well-kept fencing: signal resources worth taking
- This is not an argument against having nice things — it is an argument for discretion about where they are visible

**Building layout:**
- Single access road with natural choke point: easy to monitor, harder to approach undetected
- Multiple approach vectors across flat terrain: requires more coverage

### 1.3 OPSEC Basics

Operational security — not broadcasting your vulnerabilities or your assets — is the single cheapest security measure and one of the most effective.

**Do not post on social media:**
- Photos that reveal your food storage quantity ("finally got the pantry full for winter!")
- Generator, fuel tanks, or fuel quantity
- Firearms, ammunition quantities, or safe location
- Extended absence from property ("heading to town for a week")
- Security gaps ("our fence is still down on the east side")
- Exact property location combined with infrastructure details

**Do not volunteer information to strangers:**
- Do not tell delivery drivers, casual visitors, or new acquaintances about your generator setup, water system, food stores, or security measures
- "We're pretty prepared" is fine. "We have 400 gallons of diesel and a year of food" is not.

**Casual visitors and unknown contractors:**
- Escort people to where they need to be; do not give free-roaming access
- Unlock only what is needed; re-lock after
- Trust is built over time; new neighbors who seem friendly are still unknown

**Operational patterns:**
- Vary your schedule and routines when security conditions warrant it
- Know who monitors your property's patterns (neighbors, regular delivery drivers, anyone who might notice you are gone)

### 1.4 Security vs. Paranoia

Security planning can spiral into pathological levels of concern that damage mental health, community relationships, and quality of life — without making you meaningfully safer. A calibrated approach:

**Productive security mindset:**
- Identify the two or three most probable threats and address them first
- Implement deterrence measures that are largely invisible day-to-day (good locks, lights, a dog)
- Build community relationships — this is the most powerful long-term security asset
- Have contingency plans but do not rehearse them obsessively
- Keep security measures proportionate to the actual threat environment you live in, not to worst-case scenarios

**Warning signs of disproportionate response:**
- Significant debt to pay for tactical gear and weapons while basic perimeter security is absent
- Treating neighbors and visitors as suspects by default
- Security planning consuming time that should go to food production, health, community
- Children growing up with excessive fear

The baseline question: "Is this investment reducing a real, probable threat to my household, or is it addressing a fantasy scenario?" Be honest with the answer.

## 2. Perimeter and Site Security

### 2.1 Defensible Site Layout

The best security decisions happen before you build. Site selection and initial layout create permanent security characteristics.

**Key principles:**
- Position primary structures with sight lines toward the driveway and access road
- Locate the main dwelling so occupants can see vehicles approaching from inside the house
- Keep work areas (workshop, barn, garden) visible from the house where possible
- Avoid placing large screening vegetation between the house and the access road
- If possible, position the house so the front faces the road and the rear faces a hillside or tree line (limits approach vectors)

**Choke point design:**
- A single driveway that passes within 30 feet of the house before reaching other structures forces any vehicle to pass the house
- A gate at the road — even a simple farm gate — creates a pause point and a psychological barrier
- A cattle guard or speed bump before the house forces slowing; useful for cameras

**Sight lines and dead ground:**
- Walk your property at night and identify where someone could approach without being seen from the house
- Fill dead ground with motion-activated lighting or cameras, not necessarily fencing
- Thick hedgerows and outbuildings close to the house create concealment — a two-edged sword

---

### 2.2 Natural Barriers

Topography and vegetation provide passive, no-maintenance security.

| Natural feature | Security value | Practical notes |
|---|---|---|
| Stream or ditch on property boundary | Channelizes foot traffic to crossing points | Good for monitoring; easy to bridge |
| Dense thorny hedgerow (hawthorn, osage orange, multiflora rose) | Effective barrier; 5–10 years to establish | Plant on perimeter; doubles as wildlife habitat |
| Steep slope on one or more sides | Eliminates vehicle approach from that direction | Very valuable; limits your camera/light coverage need |
| Stand of dense trees | Screens property from road; reduces visibility | Also screens approaching threats; tradeoff |
| Rock walls (existing stone fences) | Excellent barrier and sightline obstruction | Supplement with lighting if on approach side |

**Thorny hedgerow species by region:**
- Midwest/East: Osage orange (*Maclura pomifera*), multiflora rose (invasive but effective), hawthorn
- Southeast: Cherokee rose, American holly, hawthorn
- West: Barberry, native rose species, pyracantha
- Everywhere: Russian olive (invasive; check local regulations)

A well-established osage orange hedge is impenetrable to vehicles and most livestock, requires no maintenance after establishment, and is free from seed if you have access to a mother tree.

### 2.3 Fencing Costs and Options

Fencing serves double duty: livestock management and perimeter deterrence. The right fence depends on what you are containing or deterring.

**Field fence / woven wire (livestock primary, light human deterrence):**

| Type | Wire gauge | Post spacing | Cost installed | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 4-ft field fence (47-in) | 12.5 ga | 8–10 ft | $4–$7/linear ft | General livestock, basic perimeter |
| 5-ft field fence (55-in) | 12.5 ga | 8 ft | $5–$8/linear ft | Deer exclusion, taller livestock |
| 6-ft woven wire | 11 ga | 8 ft | $7–$12/linear ft | Serious perimeter; hard to climb |
| 2"×4" welded wire | 14 ga | 6–8 ft | $6–$10/linear ft | Small livestock, poultry perimeter |
| No-climb horse fence | 12.5 ga | 8 ft | $5–$9/linear ft | Good general purpose; stiff; hard to cut through |

Materials cost (DIY install, typical): 4-ft field fence is $0.80–$1.20/ft for wire; T-posts are $3–$5 each. A 10-acre perimeter (~4,200 linear ft) costs $3,000–$5,000 in materials plus labor.

**Chain-link:**
- 6-ft chain-link with top rail: $18–$30/linear ft installed around a small yard/compound
- Effective deterrent but expensive; use for high-value enclosures (generator enclosure, tool storage compound) rather than whole-property perimeter

**Electric fence (deterrence):**
- Single-strand perimeter (pulsed): ~$0.50/ft wire; energizer $100–$400 (solar or AC)
- 5-strand electric with insulators: ~$1–$2/ft materials
- Effective deterrent for animals and a significant inconvenience for humans
- Limitations: not a barrier to determined entry; must be maintained (vegetation contact kills the charge)
- Solar energizer (Parmak, Zareba, Premier 1): $150–$350 for up to 30 miles of fence; adequate for homestead perimeter

**A practical note on perimeter fencing:** A full security perimeter fence around a large rural property is expensive and rarely the best use of security dollars. The more valuable approach is fencing the immediate compound (house, workshop, animal area) and using a combination of cameras, lights, and natural features for the broader property boundary.

### 2.4 Exterior Lighting

Motion-activated exterior lighting is one of the highest-ROI security measures available. It deters casual opportunists and wakes you up at night when triggered.

**Coverage priorities:**
1. Driveway entrance
2. Vehicle parking area
3. Front and rear doors
4. Workshop/outbuilding entries
5. Any approach path not visible from the house

**Solar vs. wired:**

| Type | Pros | Cons | Cost range |
|---|---|---|---|
| Solar integrated (all-in-one) | No wiring; easy install; goes anywhere | Weaker output; battery degrades in 3–5 years | $20–$80/unit |
| Solar separate panel (high-power) | Bright (2,000–5,000 lm); good battery life | More complex install | $60–$150/unit |
| Wired 120V (with conduit) | Reliable; bright; no battery concern | Requires grid or long wire run from inverter | $30–$100/fixture + wiring |
| 12V DC (direct from battery bank) | Low power draw; no inverter needed | Fewer products; DIY wiring | $20–$60/fixture |

**Recommended products (2026):**
- Leonlite 2-head solar floodlight (2,400 lm, adjustable panel): $45–$65/unit; good general use
- LITOM 120 LED solar motion sensor: $25–$40; adequate for walkways and secondary entries
- Ameritop 2,500 lm solar spotlight: $35–$55; strong beam for driveways
- For wired DC application: any 12V 20W LED floodlight with motion sensor module ($15–$30 parts); DIY straightforward

**Placement tips:**
- Mount lights high enough (10–14 ft) to prevent easy removal
- Angle so the light illuminates the subject, not your windows
- Test the detection zone before finalizing; most PIR sensors have 90–120° detection arc at 20–30 ft range
- Keep lens clear of spider webs and dirt; check quarterly

### 2.5 Camera Systems

A camera system does three things: deters casual crime (if cameras are visible), provides documentary evidence, and can notify you of activity while away.

**For off-grid use, key requirements:**
- Solar power (or low-power wired DC)
- Local recording to NVR or SD card — no cloud dependency, no subscription
- Night vision (IR or color night vision)
- Weather rating IP66 or better

**Recommended systems:**

| System | Type | Recording | Cost (4-camera kit) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Reolink RLK8-800B4 | IP PoE | Local NVR + HDD | $300–$400 | Best overall value; 4K; no subscription; PoE over single cable |
| Annke C800 | IP PoE | Local NVR | $280–$380 | Good 4K; local only; works with Annke NVR or Blue Iris |
| Reolink Argus 3 Pro (solar) | WiFi + solar | SD card local | $90–$130/camera | Battery-solar; no wired run needed; ideal for outbuildings |
| Amcrest 4K IP67 | IP PoE | Synology NAS or NVR | $80–$120/camera | Good quality; ONVIF compatible; any NVR |
| Hikvision DS-2CD2147G2 | IP PoE | Local NVR | $100–$150/camera | Professional grade; AcuSense reduces false alerts |

**NVR and storage:**
- A 4–8 channel NVR with 2TB HDD stores 30+ days of motion-triggered footage at 4K
- Blue Iris software (Windows PC): $70 one-time license; runs on any PC; highly configurable; better than most standalone NVRs
- Synology NAS with Surveillance Station: $400–$600 for NAS + HDD; supports 2 cameras free, then per-camera license

**Power for off-grid cameras:**
- PoE cameras draw 5–13W each; a 4-camera system plus NVR draws 35–60W continuous
- A small dedicated solar panel (100W) + 50Ah LiFePO4 battery provides 12–16 hours of autonomous operation through overnight periods
- Alternatively, run cameras on the main homestead battery bank — 60W constant draw is 1.44 kWh/day, manageable

**Camera placement:**
- Entry points first: driveway camera covering license plates and faces at road entrance
- Each exterior door
- Wide-angle on vehicle and equipment parking
- Barn and outbuilding entries if valuable contents
- Avoid aiming at neighbor's property

### 2.6 Dogs for Security

A dog is one of the most effective and cost-efficient security tools for a rural homestead. Dogs provide detection (hearing and smell far exceed humans), deterrence (most criminals prefer unguarded properties), and alarm (barking wakes you at night).

**Roles:**
- **Alert dog:** small to medium breed that barks at anything unusual; does not need to be intimidating; does need to distinguish normal from abnormal
- **Deterrent dog:** large breed with imposing presence; bark alone deters most casual threats
- **LGD (Livestock Guardian Dog):** lives with the herd/flock, bonds to them, protects against animal and human threats; separate from house dogs

**Breed selection by role:**

| Role | Breed options | Temperament notes | Cost (puppy) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Alert (house) | Australian Cattle Dog, Jack Russell, German Shepherd | High energy; needs work and training | $400–$1,500 |
| Alert (house) | Mixed breed rescue | Highly variable but often excellent; low cost | $50–$300 |
| Deterrent / protection | German Shepherd, Belgian Malinois, Rottweiler | Needs significant training and socialization | $800–$3,000+ |
| Deterrent / family | Doberman, Giant Schnauzer | Loyal; needs socialization; good alarm | $1,000–$3,000 |
| LGD (livestock) | Great Pyrenees | Independent; bonds to livestock; barks at night | $400–$1,000 |
| LGD (livestock) | Anatolian Shepherd / Kangal | More active and aggressive; excellent guardian | $600–$1,500 |
| LGD (livestock) | Akbash | Calmer than Kangal; good for sheep and goats | $400–$1,000 |
| All-purpose rural | Mixed LGD cross | Often excellent balance of bonding and guarding | $100–$400 |

**Training basics:**
- Any dog must know "sit," "stay," "come," "quiet" — basic obedience is security infrastructure
- A dog that can't be called off a threat is a liability; train a reliable recall
- LGDs need to be introduced to livestock as puppies (8–16 weeks) — bonding occurs during a critical window
- Bark-and-alert dogs should not be punished for barking at strangers — that is their job; train "quiet" on command, not "never bark"

**Food and veterinary costs:**
- Large breed (80–100 lb): $60–$100/month in quality kibble; $200–$400/year in routine vet care
- Annual vaccines (DHPP + rabies): $80–$150
- Heartworm prevention: $6–$15/month
- Total annual cost for one large dog: $1,000–$1,800

## 3. Livestock Protection

### 3.1 Predator Identification by Region

Knowing which predators are present determines your fencing and management strategy. Underspend on protection against predators that are not in your area; do not underspend on the ones that are.

**Predator distribution and primary livestock targets:**

| Predator | Regions | Primary targets | Activity | Threat level |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Coyote (*Canis latrans*) | All 48 contiguous states | Chickens, rabbits, ducks, lambs, cats | Mostly dawn/dusk; sometimes daytime | Very high; most common livestock killer |
| Red fox (*Vulpes vulpes*) | Nationwide | Chickens, ducks, rabbits | Night, dawn, dusk | High for poultry; dig under enclosures |
| Gray fox | East and Southwest | Similar to red fox | Night | Medium |
| Raccoon (*Procyon lotor*) | Nationwide | Poultry (especially at night), eggs, corn | Night | Very high; extremely clever with latches and wire |
| Opossum | East, Midwest, South | Eggs, chicks, occasionally adults | Night | Medium |
| Weasel / Mink / Stoat | Nationwide | Poultry; will kill entire flock, eat very little | Night | High; can enter any gap >1 inch |
| Bobcat | Nationwide except Great Plains | Lambs, kid goats, poultry, rabbits | Night, dawn/dusk | Medium–high |
| Mountain lion / Cougar | West, Texas Hill Country, Florida | Sheep, goats, calves, pigs, dogs | Night | High where present |
| Black bear | Appalachians, Southeast, Rockies, Pacific NW | Beehives, pigs, chickens, grain stores | Dawn/dusk, night | High; determined, strong |
| Gray wolf | Northern Rockies (ID, MT, WY), Great Lakes (MN, WI, MI), reestablishing in WA, OR | Cattle, sheep, goats | Mostly night | High where present; pack behavior |
| Great horned owl | Nationwide | Chickens, rabbits, ducks | Night | High; silent; very difficult to stop without overhead cover |
| Red-tailed hawk | Nationwide | Chickens, young rabbits, ducklings | Daytime | Medium; requires overhead cover |
| Cooper's hawk | Nationwide | Chickens, small poultry | Daytime | Medium |
| Bald eagle | Nationwide (increasing) | Ducks, young lambs, chickens | Daytime | Low–medium |

### 3.2 Fencing Specifications by Predator

No single fence stops every predator. Use this table to design based on your actual threats.

| Target predator | Minimum fence spec | Key vulnerability | Additional measure |
|---|---|---|---|
| Coyote | 5-ft field fence, no-climb | Climbing, digging | Add electric wire 8" out, 8" up from base |
| Fox | 4-ft field fence with buried apron | Digging | 12" galvanized hardware cloth apron, buried or L-shaped on surface |
| Raccoon | 4-ft welded wire, must be fully enclosed top | Climbing, manipulating latches | Add electric wire at top; use spring-loaded carabiners on latches |
| Weasel / mink | ½" hardware cloth; no gap >1 inch | Tiny gaps anywhere | Full enclosure; no gaps at corners, vents, or doors |
| Bear | 5-strand electric fence (hi-tensile) | Pure determination | 5 strands at 10, 20, 30, 40, 50 inches; 5,000+ volt energizer |
| Mountain lion | 6-ft woven wire with coyote roller or inward lean | Climbing over | Inward-angled top; LGD essential companion measure |
| Hawk / owl | Covered run with bird netting or hardware cloth | N/A (aerial) | Cover top of all poultry runs; let birds in at dusk |
| Wolf | Hi-tensile electric, 5–7 strands, properly maintained | Determined pack | LGD essential; 5,000+ volt energizer; inspect regularly |

**Coyote roller:** A rolling pipe attached to the top of a fence prevents coyotes from getting a grip to pull themselves over. PVC pipe ($0.50/ft) or commercial rollers ($2–$4/ft) on top of an existing fence is cheaper than replacing with taller fence.

### 3.3 Poultry Coop and Hutch Security

Poultry represents the most vulnerable livestock category. A single determined raccoon or weasel can eliminate an entire flock in one night.

**Hardware cloth vs. chicken wire:**

This distinction matters. Chicken wire (hexagonal twist wire) has two critical weaknesses: it can be torn by a determined fox or dog, and the standard 1-inch holes pass a weasel's head — and where a weasel's head goes, the body follows. Hardware cloth (welded ½-inch square mesh) is structurally rigid and blocks all common predators.

| Material | Gauge | Hole size | Cost/roll | Blocks |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Chicken wire | 20–22 ga | 1" hex | $25–$45 per 50 ft (4 ft wide) | Hawks (overhead only), very light duty |
| Hardware cloth | 19 ga | ½" | $60–$100 per 25 ft (4 ft wide) | All predators including weasels |
| Hardware cloth | 16 ga | ½" | $90–$140 per 25 ft (4 ft wide) | All predators; won't be bitten or torn through |
| Welded wire | 14 ga | 2"×4" | $50–$80 per 50 ft (4 ft wide) | Dogs, coyotes; NOT weasels or mink |

**Minimum coop security standard:**
- ½-inch 16-gauge hardware cloth on all sides and top of run
- Buried apron: hardware cloth bent 90° at ground level and extended 12" horizontally outward underground (or lay flat on surface and cover with soil/gravel); blocks diggers
- Door latches: spring-loaded carabiner or two-step latch (raccoons routinely open simple hook latches)
- All vents covered with ½-inch hardware cloth
- No gaps at door frames, corner joints, or where different materials meet

**Night lockup:**
The single most effective anti-predator measure for poultry is locking them in a secure coop every night. Raccoons and foxes are almost entirely nocturnal. A solid lockup routine eliminates 80%+ of poultry predator attacks. Automatic pop-hole doors (Omlet, Ador) with light sensors or timers cost $150–$300 and remove human error from the equation.

### 3.4 Livestock Guardian Dogs

LGDs are the best long-term investment in livestock protection on a diversified homestead. One or two well-trained LGDs can protect flocks and herds across multiple acres around the clock.

**How LGDs work:**
- Bond to livestock during critical imprinting period (8–16 weeks of age)
- Live with the animals 24/7; do not leave them unattended indoors
- Guard by presence, patrol, scent-marking, and confrontation — they do not need to kill predators, only to drive them off
- Work best in pairs; two dogs protect larger areas and support each other against large predators

**Breed comparison:**

| Breed | Size | Temperament | Special traits | Best with | Annual food cost |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Great Pyrenees | 85–115 lb | Independent, gentle, calm | Heavy coat for cold; barks extensively | Sheep, goats, chickens | $720–$960 |
| Anatolian Shepherd | 90–150 lb | Alert, territorial, less biddable | Exceptional instinct; heat tolerant | All livestock | $840–$1,200 |
| Kangal | 100–145 lb | Confident, courageous, reserved with strangers | Fastest LGD; effective against wolves and bears | All livestock; wolf country | $840–$1,200 |
| Akbash | 80–120 lb | Calm, less aggressive than Kangal | Good for small operations; easier to handle | Sheep, goats | $720–$960 |
| Maremma Sheepdog | 66–100 lb | Loyal, energetic, bonds tightly | Good with poultry; Italian origin; excellent where smaller animals need protection | Poultry, sheep | $600–$840 |
| Polish Tatra / Slovensky Cuvac | 80–120 lb | Calm, trainable, friendly with family | Easier for new LGD owners | Sheep, goats | $720–$960 |

**Training and management:**
- Do not treat LGDs like house dogs — they are working animals with different behavioral needs
- They will roam if not fenced in with livestock; perimeter fencing must be adequate
- Supplement instinct with basic obedience training; a dog you cannot call off is a hazard
- Socialization with humans during puppyhood prevents aggression problems; LGDs should be comfortable with family and known visitors
- Spay/neuter to prevent reproductive distraction from guarding duties
- Veterinary costs similar to other large breeds ($800–$1,500/year per dog)

**Night protocols:**
- Ensure LGDs are inside the livestock perimeter at dusk
- Do not pen livestock in areas where the LGD cannot access them at night
- A well-bonded LGD will alert and investigate any unusual sound; a barking LGD at 2am is information — investigate

## 4. Physical Security for Structures

### 4.1 Door and Frame Hardening

Most residential door breaches happen through the door frame, not the door itself. A standard hollow core door in a pine frame with a basic single-cylinder deadbolt takes one kick. The good news: hardening is cheap.

**Priority upgrades in order of cost-effectiveness:**

| Upgrade | Cost (DIY) | What it stops |
|---|---|---|
| Replace hollow-core exterior door with solid wood or steel | $150–$400 | Kick-throughs, physical beating |
| Door jamb reinforcement kit (StrikeMaster II, Door Armor) | $60–$90 | Kick-in attacks; upgrades the weakest point |
| 3-inch screws in all hinge hardware and strike plates | $5–$10 | Frame splitting on kick |
| High-security deadbolt (Schlage B60N, Medeco) | $60–$200 | Lock picking, bump keys |
| Door barricade bar (Master Lock 265DCCSEN, OnGARD bar) | $35–$55 | Battering when home; secondary backup |
| Outswing door or security hinges with set screws | $50–$150 | Hinge attack on outswing doors |
| Double cylinder deadbolt (keyed both sides) | $60–$130 | Entry via window near door (fire egress concern) |

**The frame is the weak point.** Most deadbolts are rated for the bolt itself; the 2-inch screws in the strike plate's 1/4-inch plate going into soft pine is what fails. A door armor kit or replacing strike plate screws with 3-inch screws into the structural stud adds $5–$80 and multiplies resistance by 3–5x.

**Sliding doors:**
- Charlie bar (secondary rod in track): $15–$25
- Through-bolt lock with double keying if desired
- Pin or bolt through door frames to prevent lifting off track
- Security film on glass (see 4.2)

---

### 4.2 Window Security

Windows are not primary breach points in most rural scenarios (too noisy, conspicuous), but they are a vulnerability worth addressing at low cost.

**Security film:**
- 3M Safety Series or BDF window film: $30–$80 per 4'×8' roll; one roll covers 4–6 standard windows
- Purpose: holds glass shards together on impact; requires 3–6 additional blows to clear vs. one; significant deterrent
- Does not prevent entry indefinitely but adds time and noise
- Easy DIY install with a squeegee and soapy water

**Window locks:**
- Pin locks (key-operated or keyed alike): $8–$15/window; prevents opening even if latches are bypassed
- Sash locks with screws (upgrade from standard flip latches): $5–$10/window

**Bars:**
- Consider only on basement or ground-floor windows in very high-risk scenarios
- Fire egress must be maintained: any room used for sleeping needs at least one egress window, bars must have interior quick-release mechanisms
- Not generally recommended for rural homesteads; cost, fire hazard, and aesthetic cost outweigh benefit in most cases

### 4.3 Storage Security

Rural theft is disproportionately targeted at outbuildings, not dwellings. A barn full of fuel, a workshop full of tools, or a pantry in a detached building are high-value targets.

**Tiered storage security:**

| Value | Storage approach | Cost |
|---|---|---|
| Low (garden tools, general equipment) | Locked outbuilding door (padlock + hasp) | $20–$50 |
| Medium (chainsaws, power tools, small engines) | Locked metal cabinet inside locked building | $150–$400 |
| High (fuel, generators, critical spare parts) | Locked metal building or dedicated locked cage | $500–$2,000 |
| Very high (firearms, cash, critical documents) | Gun safe, bolted to floor or wall | $400–$2,500 |
| Critical (food stores in extended-scenario) | Locked root cellar or dedicated room with hasp | $50–$200 |

**Padlock selection:**
- Hardware store padlocks (Master Lock No. 3) are cut in 3 seconds with bolt cutters
- Hardened steel shackle padlocks (ABUS 55/40, Master Lock 6271, Abloy Protec2): $30–$100; substantially more resistant
- Closed shackle design (shackle barely extends beyond body) prevents tool access
- Security hasps with hidden screws prevent removing hasp screws as an alternative to cutting

**Fuel storage:**
- Diesel and propane tanks are both a fire risk and a theft target
- Chain or cable-lock propane tank to a fixed anchor (rebar driven into concrete, structural post)
- Diesel IBC totes: chain lid handles and lock with hardened padlock; record tank volume periodically
- Cage enclosure for propane tanks: 1/2-inch rebar welded cage (can be DIY fabricated) + padlock

Equipment theft in rural areas is a major problem. ATVs, trailers, riding mowers, and chainsaws are high-loss items that are rarely recovered.

| Trailer | Hitch coupling theft | Hitch lock + coupler lock | $30–$70 |

- Anchor rings: 1/2-inch eye bolt epoxied or anchor-bolted into concrete; $5–$15/ring

### 4.5 Safe Room and Shelter-in-Place

A safe room provides a hardened retreat inside the structure for a short-duration threat (break-in while occupied). It also doubles as a tornado shelter in tornado-prone regions.

**Minimum viable safe room:**
- Interior room with no exterior windows
- Solid-core door with reinforced frame and deadbolt
- Charged cell phone or radio inside
- Clear communication plan (who to call and in what order)
- Basic supplies: water, flashlight, first aid kit

**Enhanced safe room:**
- Poured concrete walls or reinforced masonry
- Steel door (commercial hollow metal) with security hinges and dual deadbolts
- NOAA weather radio
- 72-hour supplies cache

A root cellar, already recommended in the food storage and nuclear scenarios, also serves naturally as a safe room. Adding a steel door and communication equipment requires modest additional investment.

## 5. Firearms

### 5.1 Philosophy and Context

Firearms are one layer of a comprehensive security plan. They are the last resort tool in a defense scenario and the primary tool for pest control and hunting. Both of these roles are legitimate and important on a homestead.

The priorities are:
1. Deterrence and prevention — most threats never reach the point of needing a firearm
2. De-escalation — the ability to talk someone down is more valuable than shooting ability in most actual encounters
3. Proficiency and safety — an untrained person with a firearm is more likely to have an accident than to successfully use it in defense
4. Legal compliance — every firearm decision must be made within the law of your jurisdiction

Do not acquire firearms as a substitute for the foundational security layers in Sections 2, 3, and 4. A locked door and a dog prevent the situation that requires a firearm in the first place.

---

### 5.2 Legal Considerations

**Federal law (ATF, United States):**
- Background check (NICS) required for all FFL dealer purchases; no exceptions for "assault weapons" vs. other firearms at federal level
- Machine guns (full-auto) manufactured after 1986 are prohibited for civilian ownership
- NFA items (suppressors, SBRs under 16" barrel, SBSs) require Form 4 tax stamp ($200), ATF approval (6–18 month wait), and registration; legal in most states
- Certain accessories (bump stocks as of 2024 ruling) may have changed status; verify current law

**Safe storage laws:**
- 11 states (including CA, CO, CT, MA, NY, WA) require safe storage of firearms at home; violations are misdemeanor or felony depending on state
- Even where not legally required, a gun safe is standard best practice with children in the home
- Quick-access vaults allow defense-oriented access while satisfying storage requirements

**State-specific considerations:**
- Suppressor ownership: currently legal in 42 states with NFA compliance; illegal in CA, DE, HI, IL, MA, MN, NJ, NY, RI
- SBR (short-barreled rifle): similar restrictions; 42-state legal with NFA
- Magazine capacity restrictions: CA (10), CO (15), CT (10), HI (10), MA (10), MD (10), NJ (10), NY (10), WA (10)
- Constitutional carry (no permit required for concealed carry): 29+ states as of 2026
- Reciprocity for concealed carry: varies; check state-by-state before traveling with a firearm

**Document everything:**
- Keep copies of all purchase receipts, Form 4 approvals, and serial number records off-site
- Serial number etching or photo records help with recovery if stolen

### 5.6 Training

Owning a firearm without training is like owning a surgical kit without medical training. The firearm itself provides no protection; the person's skill and decision-making do.

**Minimum training path:**
1. **Firearms safety course** (NRA Basic Pistol or Basic Rifle): foundational safe handling, storage, legal considerations; $50–$100; typically one day
2. **Handling and proficiency**: ability to load, unload, clear malfunctions, and fire accurately; 500+ rounds of deliberate practice
3. **Scenario-based training** (USCCA Fundamentals, Massad Ayoob course, or local defensive shooting class): judgment and decision-making in threat scenarios; $150–$400

**Practical minimums before relying on a firearm for defense:**
- Can safely clear a malfunction without looking at hands
- Can accurately hit a 12-inch target at 25 yards (rifle/shotgun) or 10 yards (handgun) under mild time pressure
- Understands your jurisdiction's use-of-force law (Castle Doctrine vs. Stand Your Ground vs. duty to retreat)
- Has discussed and agreed on household protocols with all adults in the home

**Annual maintenance:**
- Minimum 100 rounds per firearm per year to maintain proficiency
- Annual inspection: function check, clean, oil, inspect for wear or corrosion

## 6. Information and Communications Security

### 6.1 OPSEC in Practice

OPSEC (Operational Security) is the discipline of not broadcasting information that could be used against you. For a homestead, the primary concern is not sophisticated adversaries — it is casual opportunism enabled by publicly available information.

**Social media review:**
- Audit your profiles: what is public vs. private? What has been posted in the last year?
- Photos contain metadata (EXIF) including GPS coordinates when taken with a phone; disable geotagging on photos before posting
- Location services: disable location access for social media apps
- "Check-in" features: never use these for your home location
- Neighborhood apps (Nextdoor): useful for community watch but also reveals your routines to people you don't know

**What to share carefully:**
- Specific infrastructure (exact location of your generator, well, fuel storage)
- Departure dates and return dates for travel
- Photographs of your interior storage areas
- Specific quantities of food or supplies
- Any identified security gaps ("I still haven't gotten around to fixing that fence section")

**Online footprint:**
- Whitepages, Spokeo, BeenVerified, and similar data brokers aggregate your address, phone, and relatives from public records
- Annual "data broker opt-out" process removes most: send opt-out requests to major aggregators (Spokeo, Whitepages, Intelius, BeenVerified, PeopleFinder); takes 2–4 hours initially; repeat annually
- Do not use your home address as the registered address for online activities if a P.O. box is available

---

### 6.2 Digital Security Basics

**Device and network security:**
- Change default passwords on all routers, cameras, and network devices; default credentials are publicly listed by manufacturer
- IP cameras and NVRs: segment them on a separate VLAN if possible; never expose camera admin interfaces to the internet
- Router: enable WPA3 if supported; disable remote management; keep firmware updated
- Offline backups: critical documents (land deed, water rights, birth certificates, insurance, medication records) on encrypted USB or printed copies in a fireproof location

**Communications while grid is down:**
- Assume all digital communications are potentially monitored or interceptable
- Ham radio traffic is inherently public — never transmit critical information (food stores, security status, cache locations) on repeater nets or open frequencies
- GMRS and FRS are easily monitored with a $30 scanner
- For sensitive local communication: face-to-face, or digital modes with encryption on ham radio (JS8Call, Winlink on HF)
- See 11-communications.md for full radio protocol setup

---

### 6.3 Guest and Visitor Protocols

**Vetting:**
- New neighbors and visitors are unknown quantities regardless of how friendly they seem initially
- Trust is built through consistent behavior over time, shared work, and reciprocal contribution
- It is not paranoid to limit access to sensitive areas; it is standard good sense

**Practical protocols:**
- Meet visitors at the road or at the front of the property rather than inside the compound
- Escort contractors to their work area; do not give roaming access
- Avoid discussing quantities, locations, or capabilities with people who have not earned trust
- After any contractor or service visit, check the areas they accessed

**Children and information:**
- Children often share what they know; limit what they know about security specifics
- Age-appropriate security awareness (where to go, who to call, what to do) is valuable; detailed security knowledge is not necessary

## 7. Community Defense

### 7.1 Why Individual Security Fails in Extended Scenarios

Individual security is adequate for the vast majority of rural threat scenarios: opportunistic theft, animal predation, an isolated home invasion. However, individual security has a fundamental structural limitation — the need to sleep.

In an extended scenario where external threats are elevated and law enforcement cannot respond (multi-week grid-down, regional civil unrest), a single household or family cannot maintain 24-hour vigilance indefinitely. Fatigue accumulates within 72 hours. Decision-making degrades. Mistakes happen.

Community security distributes the watch burden across multiple households, allows rotation and rest, provides backup for medical emergencies during incidents, and creates a social deterrent far more powerful than any individual security measure. A community where everyone knows each other, watches for each other, and can respond collectively is exponentially harder to victimize than isolated individual households.

This is not an argument against individual preparedness — it is an argument that individual preparedness is a foundation, not the whole structure.

See 13-community-organization.md for community formation, governance, and mutual aid frameworks.

---

### 7.2 Mutual Aid Networks for Security

**Neighborhood watch (informal):**
- Exchange contact information (phone, radio channel) with every neighbor within 2–3 miles
- Establish a shared communication habit: text thread, GMRS channel, or weekly radio check-in
- Agree on what to report: unfamiliar vehicles parked on the road, signs of break-in at absent neighbor's property, unusual nighttime activity
- No confrontation expectation — observe and report; police and owner notification is the action

**Coordinated patrols (extended scenarios only):**
- Only worth implementing when threat level is elevated above baseline
- Two-person minimum for any patrol; never patrol alone
- Fixed schedule with predictable structure allows rest; use a watch bill
- Radio check-in requirement: each patrol checks in at defined intervals; missed check-in triggers response
- Patrol routes: know the ground; practice in daylight before doing it at night

**Resource pooling:**
- Shared watch tower or elevated observation point (if terrain warrants)
- Communal alerting system: radio on a shared channel as a minimum; physical alarm (signal bell, horn) as backup
- Shared NVR footage access for community members with cameras

---

### 7.3 Communication Protocols for Threats

**Radio infrastructure:**
- Designate a shared GMRS channel for community security communication (GMRS channels 15–22 are less congested; channel 20 is a common off-grid community default)
- Establish a callsign convention for your community (e.g., homestead name + number: "Ridgeline-1, Ridgeline-2")
- Schedule morning and evening radio check-ins to confirm all households are OK
- See 11-communications.md for full GMRS setup, repeater options, and grid-down protocols

**Code words:**
- Simple code words for threat levels allow communication on monitored channels without revealing details to potential adversaries
- Example structure: "I'm hearing coyotes tonight" = elevated awareness, possible surveillance; "The deer are in the garden" = confirmed security incident; "Going fishing" = all clear
- Do not use complex codes that will be forgotten under stress; keep it to 4–6 phrases

**Contact tree:**
- Primary: direct radio/phone contact
- Secondary: through a relay neighbor if direct contact fails
- Tertiary: physical check (go to the property and look)
- Document the tree on paper at each household; do not rely on digital records only

---

### 7.4 External Relationships

**Local sheriff:**
- Introduce yourself to the local sheriff or county sheriff's office before you need them
- Know the direct number for the county dispatch (not just 911)
- Report unusual activity consistently — this establishes a pattern that helps deputies allocate attention
- Be a good-faith contributor to public safety: report incidents accurately, cooperate with investigations
- Know the response time for your address: ask the dispatch center directly; they usually know

**Neighboring communities:**
- Identify other homestead groups, farms, and intentional communities within 20 miles
- Make contact at local farmers markets, extension service events, or directly
- Information sharing (scanner traffic, notable incidents, traveling strangers) is valuable; build those relationships before you need them

**Local emergency management:**
- Most counties have an Emergency Management Director; introduce yourself
- CERT (Community Emergency Response Team) training is free and provides relationship with local emergency infrastructure
- Ham radio operators who are RACES/ARES affiliated have formal connections to emergency management

---

### 7.5 Rules of Engagement and Decision-Making

In a community security context, pre-established rules of engagement prevent dangerous ad-hoc decisions under stress.

**Establish before it is needed:**
1. What triggers a community alert vs. individual response?
2. Who has authority to call a coordinated response?
3. What is the escalation ladder? (Observe → Report → Alert neighbors → Coordinated response → Physical intervention)
4. Under what circumstances do community members physically intervene vs. call for outside help?
5. Who is the communications coordinator during an incident?

**Default principles:**
- Observation and documentation (video, notes) before any other action
- De-escalation is always the first attempt where safe to do so
- No physical intervention without at least two community members present and a clear, unambiguous threat
- All use of force decisions are individual, lawful, and documented
- Post-incident debrief within 24 hours; update protocols based on what was learned

## 8. Threat-Specific Response Protocols

Quick reference for the most common scenarios. Print and post in your home and share with community members.

### 8.1 Response Table

| Scenario | Immediate action | Secondary action | Avoid |
|---|---|---|---|
| Coyote pack targeting livestock | Move animals to secure pen; LGD alert; loud deterrent (horn, shout) | Assess fence perimeter at daylight; repair gaps | Night pursuit into tree line; leave livestock unpenned at dusk |
| Hawk / aerial predator | Lock poultry in run; add temporary overhead netting | Permanent overhead cover for main run | Leaving free-range without supervision during daytime until resolved |
| Raccoon in coop | Remove raccoon (trap or eliminate); inspect all latch and mesh points | Add electric wire at top of run; add carabiner latches | Assuming it will not return; it will |
| Bear in orchard / livestock area | Do not approach; move indoors; let bear leave naturally | Set up electric fence around affected area; ensure no attractants (food waste, birdfeeders) | Cornering the bear; approaching a bear with cubs |
| Unfamiliar vehicle parked on road | Note time, description, license plate; radio community alert | Watch property from inside; alert neighbor | Confronting occupants alone; assuming innocence without investigation |
| Signs of trespassing / scouting | Document evidence (footprints, moved items); camera review; notify neighbors | Assess and repair any perimeter gaps; increase light coverage | Publicizing the incident on social media; confronting unknown individuals alone |
| Break-in while away | Do not enter property alone; call sheriff; notify neighbors | Photograph damage before touching anything; document losses for insurance | Touching entry point or disturbing evidence before police arrive |
| Break-in while occupied | Move to safe room; call 911 if available; radio community alert; stay away from doors | Wait for confirmation of departure before exiting safe room | Pursuing the intruder outside your home |
| Armed confrontation at home | De-escalate first; make clear you are armed; give opportunity to leave | If retreat is cut off and threat is imminent, act decisively | Chasing a retreating threat outside your home; warning shots (negligent discharge liability) |
| Organized group threat | Do not engage alone; shelter in place; radio community immediately; conserve resources | Community response within pre-agreed protocols | Broadcasting your exact location and defenses on open radio channels |
| Civil unrest in nearby town | Monitor scanner and ham radio; increase overnight awareness; brief all household members | Check community network status; confirm watch schedule; no unnecessary travel | Driving toward the unrest; broadcasting your response posture |

### 8.2 Government Enforcement and Legal Considerations

Legal scenarios (code enforcement, warrant service, regulatory inspection) are handled differently than criminal threats. The approach is compliance with rights assertion, not resistance.

**Key rights in all enforcement scenarios:**
- Fourth Amendment: law enforcement generally requires a warrant to enter your home or search your property; exceptions apply for exigent circumstances
- Fifth Amendment: you are not required to answer questions or provide information that may incriminate you; "I don't consent to searches and I'd like to speak with an attorney" is always appropriate
- This is not a guide to resisting lawful authority; it is a guide to knowing your rights

**Practical approach:**
- Know your local ordinances: zoning, permitted agricultural uses, well and septic permits, building permits for structures
- Keep all permits, water rights documentation, livestock registration, and firearm records organized and accessible
- If inspectors or law enforcement arrive, remain calm, be polite, ask to see their identification and warrant if applicable, then cooperate appropriately
- Have a local attorney contact who knows land use law; a $150 consultation before you build is worth far more than $15,000 in code enforcement battles after

**Confiscation scenarios:**
- Federal and state authorities do occasionally seize property under civil forfeiture or emergency powers
- Document all assets (photos, serial numbers, inventory) and store documentation off-site or digitally
- Firearms: maintain records of legal purchase for all NFA items; never discuss illegal modifications; federal firearms law enforcement is serious

## 9. Maintenance and Drills

### 9.1 Security Audit Checklist (Quarterly)

Run through this list every three months — ideally at the same time as other quarterly maintenance (filter changes, battery checks).

**Perimeter:**
- [ ] Walk the full perimeter fence line; repair any damage, vegetation overgrowth on electric fence, broken posts
- [ ] Test all electric fence sections with a fence tester
- [ ] Inspect all gate latches and padlocks; replace any showing rust or wear
- [ ] Check tree fall risk onto fence; clear encroaching branches

**Lighting:**
- [ ] Test all motion-activated lights during low light; replace any dead bulbs or failed units
- [ ] Clean all solar panels on solar lights; wipe lens of all fixtures
- [ ] Verify detection zones still cover intended areas; adjust PIR angle if needed
- [ ] Check battery condition on all battery-backed lights

**Cameras:**
- [ ] Review one week of stored footage for any gaps or failures
- [ ] Clean camera lenses
- [ ] Check NVR or SD card storage availability; archive and clear if near capacity
- [ ] Verify camera night vision is functioning; test at night
- [ ] Check camera mount hardware for vibration loosening

**Entry points:**
- [ ] Test all deadbolts; lubricate with graphite if sticking
- [ ] Check door frame condition around strike plates; any splitting or movement?
- [ ] Test all window locks; replace cracked or failed hardware
- [ ] Check security film condition on windows; re-apply if peeling

**Livestock:**
- [ ] Inspect coop and hutch hardware cloth for chewing, tears, or pulled sections at ground level
- [ ] Test all automatic coop door mechanisms
- [ ] Check LGD condition, vaccination status, and fence-line for any LGD escape points
- [ ] Review predator sign around perimeter (tracks, scat, feathers)

**Firearms:**
- [ ] Function check all firearms; clean and oil as needed
- [ ] Inspect ammunition storage for moisture or damage
- [ ] Check safe battery level (for electronic locks); replace annually
- [ ] Verify all household members know safe combination or access method

**Community:**
- [ ] Confirm all neighbor contact information is current
- [ ] Verify radio communication with community network (test call)
- [ ] Review any security incidents since last audit; update protocols if needed

### 9.3 Tabletop Exercise: Two-Scenario Drill

Run these exercises with all household members and relevant community participants once per year. No equipment needed — sit at a table and talk through each scenario.

**Scenario A: Break-in While Away (1 hour)**

Setup: "You are at the farmers market, 45 minutes from home. Your camera system sends a motion alert from the workshop. The next alert shows the workshop door open."

Questions to work through:
1. Who do you call first? What do you say?
2. What do they do when they arrive? (Do not enter alone — who do they call?)
3. What do you do before returning? (Call sheriff — what information do you give?)
4. What do you do when you arrive home?
5. What do you document and in what order?
6. How do you secure the breach point temporarily?
7. What did this incident reveal about your security gaps? (Discuss 3 specific changes)

**Scenario B: Threat Escalation (1 hour)**

Setup: "It's 11pm. Your dog is barking aggressively at the east side of the property — not its normal alarm bark, but sustained and intense. You look out a window and see a flashlight moving near the tool shed."

Questions to work through:
1. What do you do in the first 60 seconds?
2. Who do you wake up and what do you tell them?
3. Do you go outside? Under what conditions? With who?
4. Do you call 911? What do you say? When is 911 not available?
5. Do you call community members? Which ones? On what channel?
6. The person at the shed sees your light and flees across the north fence line. What now?
7. How do you secure the household for the rest of the night?
8. What do you document and share with neighbors the next morning?

**Debrief after each scenario:**
- What felt unclear? What needs a written protocol?
- Who didn't know their role? Address with individual discussion
- What physical or equipment gaps were revealed?
- Update written protocols within 48 hours while the discussion is fresh

---

### 9.4 Annual Security Plan Review

Security threats, personal circumstances, and community relationships change. Review the full plan annually:

1. **Threat reassessment**: Has the threat environment changed? New neighbors? Crime uptick reported by sheriff? Regional economic stress?
2. **Infrastructure changes**: New buildings, fencing, animals, or people added to the property since last review?
3. **Equipment updates**: Has any security equipment failed, degraded, or been superseded by better options?
4. **Community status**: Are all community relationships current? Have any households moved, changed posture, or changed capability?
5. **Legal changes**: Any new state or federal laws affecting firearms, storage, or use of force in your jurisdiction?
6. **Training currency**: When did each adult last fire their firearm? Take a refresher course? Practice a security drill?

Document the review date and major changes. Keep the current plan posted in a consistent location (inside a cabinet door, in a binder on the shelf) and ensure all household adults know where it is.

## 10. Regional Threat Profiles

Security challenges vary significantly by region. This section maps the dominant concerns by geography so you can weight your investment accordingly.

### 10.1 Southeast (AL, AR, FL, GA, LA, MS, NC, SC, TN, VA)

**Animal threats:** Coyotes universal; black bears common (especially Appalachians and Ozarks); feral hog damage to fencing and gardens is a serious problem unique to this region; raccoons and opossums prolific; fire ants can injure livestock

**Human threats:** Rural theft is a persistent concern across the region; meth-related crime in Appalachian communities; equipment theft from farms and construction sites; hurricane and tornado corridors affect threat posture (evacuation creates opportunistic theft windows)

**Key adaptations:**
- Feral hog fencing: standard field fence does not stop hogs; 4-ft high-tensile 12-gauge no-climb with T-posts at 6-ft spacing; electric wire at pig-nose height (6–8 inches off ground) is essential
- Hurricane shutters or impact windows for coastal areas serve double duty as security hardening
- Heat affects outdoor security lighting battery life; choose high-capacity solar units

**Dominant security investment:** Electric fencing (hogs + coyotes), LGD, camera system covering vehicle areas

---

### 10.2 Midwest (IA, IL, IN, KS, MI, MN, MO, NE, ND, OH, SD, WI)

**Animal threats:** Coyotes dominant; wolves in northern Minnesota and Wisconsin; foxes common; raccoons and skunks universal; mink and weasels are significant poultry threat near waterways

**Human threats:** Rural areas generally lower crime than national average; grain and farm equipment theft is specific regional problem ($100,000+ combines targeted); meth-related property crime in economically distressed counties; winter creates specific vulnerabilities (frozen locks, condensation on cameras)

**Key adaptations:**
- Equipment theft: combines, tractors, and grain augers — hidden kill switches and GPS trackers (Optimus 2.0, $30 device + $15/month) are worth it for high-value equipment
- Winter camera maintenance: lithium batteries in cold-climate solar cameras; wired power preferred below 0°F
- Tornado shelter doubles as safe room and fallout shelter; underground construction investment serves triple purpose

**Dominant security investment:** Equipment security (GPS + kill switch), electric fencing, heavy-duty padlocks on grain bins

---

### 10.3 Great Plains and Southwest (AZ, CO, KS, NM, NV, OK, TX, UT, WY)

**Animal threats:** Coyotes dominant; mountain lions throughout; javelinas (peccaries) in AZ/NM/TX — will kill small dogs; rattlesnakes are a livestock and personal threat; in wolf recovery zones (NM, CO), emerging wolf presence; golden eagles are a greater threat to lambs and kid goats than in eastern US

**Human threats:** Long distances between residences and law enforcement response times often 30–90 minutes; cattle theft (rustling) is still a real crime in this region; border areas of southern AZ/NM/TX have specific concerns around cartel smuggling corridors; remote property vulnerability during hunting season

**Key adaptations:**
- Night vision capability is worth more in the Southwest than most regions; large dark skies and determined threats
- Water source security: in arid regions, stock water tanks and wells are extremely high-value targets; lock well housings and consider water tank alarm (float-switch alert)
- Livestock trailers: GPS trackers essential; theft market exists; lock trailer hitch and wheel when parked
- Mountain lion: LGDs are less effective against mountain lions than against coyotes; motion-activated lights and covered livestock areas are primary countermeasures; Kangal or Anatolian Shepherd preferred over Great Pyrenees for lion country

**Dominant security investment:** LGD (Kangal/Anatolian), well and water source protection, livestock trailer GPS

---

### 10.4 Pacific Northwest (ID, MT, OR, WA) and Northern Rockies

**Animal threats:** Bears (black and grizzly in MT/ID); wolves (established and expanding); mountain lions; coyotes and foxes universal; ravens and eagles target young livestock

**Human threats:** Generally lower property crime; grow-op related crime in forested areas; cannabis legalization has reduced but not eliminated illicit grow operations and associated theft; remote areas attract squatters in vacant cabins

**Key adaptations:**
- Bear-aware food and compost storage is not optional in this region; bear boxes or bear-rated canisters for any stored food in outbuildings; electric fence essential around chicken coops and pig areas
- Grizzly bear: higher voltage electric fence (5,000–7,000 V), proper grounding; LGDs are useful but should not be relied on alone for grizzly encounters
- Wolf country: five-strand electric perimeter or heavy woven wire; LGD pairs are significantly more effective than singles against pack behavior; coordinate with state wildlife agency for depredation compensation programs (exist in ID, MT, WA, OR)
- Cabin security for seasonal/remote cabins: remote cameras with satellite notification (Reolink Go, 4G/satellite); deadbolt plus hasp; remove obvious valuables when leaving for extended periods

**Dominant security investment:** Bear-electric fence systems, LGD pairs, remote camera with satellite notification

---

### 10.5 Appalachian Region (KY, TN, WV, western VA, western NC)

**Animal threats:** Black bears are common and food-motivated; coyotes; foxes; raccoons prolific; Eastern wild turkey sometimes destroys gardens

**Human threats:** This region has higher-than-average rural crime rates correlating with economic distress; meth-related property crime; prescription drug theft (medicine cabinets); copper theft from outbuildings and HVAC equipment; historically tight-knit communities where outsiders are noticed

**Key adaptations:**
- Copper theft: copper wire in workshops, HVAC units, and outbuildings is a target; use aluminum wiring in outbuildings or cage copper runs; remove obvious HVAC copper when leaving for extended periods
- Medicine security: all medications including OTC meds in a locked cabinet; prescription medications in the gun safe
- Community density: this region has the cultural infrastructure for effective mutual aid networks — invest in relationships early; outsiders who become part of the community are well-protected; outsiders who remain isolated are more vulnerable

**Dominant security investment:** Community integration, bear fencing, locked medicine storage, copper protection
