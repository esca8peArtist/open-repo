# Regional Implementation Guide: South Atlantic

## Climate Overview

The South Atlantic region — Georgia, South Carolina, and adjacent areas of North Carolina and northern Florida — spans two sharply different climate bands: the hot-humid coastal plain and the temperate Appalachian foothills and mountains. The coastal plain features long, brutally hot and humid summers (daily highs of 90–98°F with 80–95% relative humidity June through September), mild winters with occasional hard freezes, and a four-season structure compressed into a narrow temperature band. The Piedmont and Blue Ridge areas offer more temperate conditions, four genuine seasons, and substantially reduced hurricane exposure. The region's defining agricultural advantage is a long growing season — 200–260 days at the coast, 180–210 days in the Piedmont — offset by the defining challenge of intense humidity that promotes fungal disease, insect pressure, and food storage difficulties. Hurricane risk is real and underestimated inland: Hurricane Helene (2024) caused catastrophic damage in the western Carolinas 400 miles from the coast, demonstrating that mountain geography provides limited protection from inland flooding.

USDA Hardiness Zones: Georgia spans zones 6b (north Georgia mountains) to 9a (Savannah/Brunswick coast); South Carolina spans 7b (upstate) to 9a (Sea Islands). The upstate Piedmont and mountain areas (zones 6b–7b) are the most practical for year-round off-grid food production.

## Food Production

### Growing Season and Frost Dates

| Subregion | Last Spring Frost | First Fall Frost | Season Length |
|-----------|------------------|------------------|---------------|
| Coast / Low Country (Zones 8b–9a) | Feb 15–Mar 1 | Dec 1–15 | 270–300 days |
| Midlands / Piedmont (Zones 7b–8a) | Mar 15–Apr 1 | Nov 1–15 | 210–235 days |
| Upper Piedmont (Zone 7a–7b) | Apr 1–Apr 15 | Oct 15–Nov 1 | 185–210 days |
| Mountains / N. GA foothills (Zones 6b–7a) | Apr 15–May 1 | Oct 1–Oct 15 | 160–185 days |

The critical planning insight for this region: summer heat and humidity are more limiting than frost. Tomatoes stop setting fruit above 90°F; most cool-season crops bolt in May. The productive calendar is not simply "first to last frost" — it is two distinct cool-season windows bracketing a difficult summer.

### Recommended Crops and Varieties

Variety selection for heat and humidity tolerance is as important as timing. Georgia and South Carolina extension services have decades of trial data:

**Spring/Fall cool-season crops (the bread and butter):**
- Greens: 'Southern Giant' mustard, 'Tendergreen' mustard, 'Georgia' collards — all bred for southeastern conditions; 'Red Sails' lettuce is more bolt-resistant than heading types
- Brassicas: collards (year-round in mild winters at coast), 'Packman' broccoli (fall crop, plant August), 'Early Jersey Wakefield' cabbage
- Root crops: 'Danvers 126' carrot (more heat-tolerant than northern varieties), 'White Half-Long' daikon (fall crop; also a soil-breaking cover crop)
- Legumes: crowder peas, field peas, black-eyed peas — these are the quintessential summer crop for the region; heat-loving, nitrogen-fixing, and highly productive
- Sweet potatoes: North Carolina and Georgia are major sweet potato states; plant slips after frost, harvest before first frost; 'Beauregard' and 'Covington' are standard; 1 lb yield per sq ft is realistic in good soil

**Summer heat crops (June–September):**
- Okra: the most heat-adapted vegetable in the American repertoire; 'Clemson Spineless' is standard; also productive as emergency calories
- Yard-long beans (asparagus beans): thrive in summer heat when regular beans fail
- Southern peas (cowpeas): the southern staple; harvest dried for protein storage or fresh shelled; plant May–July
- Peppers: thrives; include both hot and sweet; Padron peppers, Carolina Reaper for value-added trade
- Eggplant: often outproduces tomatoes in summer heat

**Perennials for the humid south:**
- Figs: 'Brown Turkey' and 'Celeste' are standard Georgia varieties; high yield, minimal care, partial drought-tolerance
- Pecans: native to the region; 50-year investment but enormous caloric yield; Georgia leads the nation in pecan production
- Muscadine grapes: native to the Southeast; disease and humidity-resistant unlike European vinifera; 'Carlos' and 'Magnolia' are proven varieties
- Rabbiteye blueberries: native to the coastal plain; require little supplemental water once established; 'Tifblue' and 'Climax' are Georgia standards

### Seasonal Calendar

| Month | Coastal Plain | Piedmont/Mountains |
|-------|--------------|-------------------|

| Sep | Cool season restart; plant greens, brassicas | Greens and root crops; fall tomatoes possible |
| Oct–Nov | Peak fall production; harvest sweet potatoes | Harvest sweet potatoes; cold-hardy greens |
| Dec | Frost-protected greens still producing at coast | Cover crops; root cellar management |

### Humidity Management in the Garden

Fungal disease is the primary crop loss mechanism in this climate:
- Grow varieties with documented disease resistance (check NCSU or UGA extension trial data)
- Never water from above; drip irrigation or soaker hoses only
- Stake tomatoes and other vining crops to maximize airflow; don't let foliage touch soil
- Rotate all nightshades, cucurbits, and brassicas on a 3-year minimum cycle
- Mulch at 3–4 inches to prevent soil splash (a primary disease vector)
- Copper fungicide (organic) is a routine preventive tool for tomatoes and brassicas; apply before disease is visible

## Shelter and Insulation

### Building Materials

The South Atlantic climate demands a different priority stack than northern climates: moisture management, pest resistance, and cooling efficiency outrank raw insulation value.

- **Structural insulated panels (SIPs) or ICF (insulated concrete forms)**: excellent performance in hot-humid climate; ICF provides thermal mass that moderates interior temperature swings and provides hurricane-resistant structure
- **Framing**: advanced framing techniques (2x6, 24 OC) reduce thermal bridging; pressure-treated lumber required for all ground contact in this termite-active region
- **Pest management**: subterranean termites are present throughout Georgia and South Carolina; concrete slab foundations with physical termite barriers (stainless steel mesh, Termimesh) are more reliable long-term than chemical treatment for off-grid structures
- **Siding**: fiber cement (HardiePlank) is the dominant regional choice; resists rot, termites, and impact; holds paint better than wood in high humidity
- **Roofing**: metal roofing is the off-grid standard for this region — reflects radiant heat (critical in summer), handles hurricane-speed wind better than asphalt shingles, and is compatible with rainwater harvesting

### Insulation Requirements

Georgia, SC, and NC are primarily in IECC Climate Zones 2A–4A (mixed-humid). Building code requirements:

| Assembly | Zone 2A (coastal GA/SC) | Zone 3A (Piedmont) | Zone 4A (mountains) |
|----------|------------------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Attic | R-38 | R-49 | R-49 |
| Wall | R-13 cavity | R-20 or R-13+5ci | R-20 or R-13+5ci |
| Floor | R-13 | R-19 | R-30 |

Off-grid recommendation: in the hot-humid climate, invest heavily in air sealing (a continuous air barrier) and radiant barrier in the attic before adding insulation — radiant heat gain through the roof accounts for 30–40% of summer cooling load and adding R-49 over a leaky air barrier is far less effective than a tight R-38 assembly.

### Roof and Foundation

- **Wind uplift**: South Carolina coastal zones require 140–160 mph design wind speed (ASCE 7 based); all roof connections must be hurricane-rated with H2.5 or H10 hurricane ties; this is code in coastal counties but strongly recommended inland
- **Foundation**: in coastal and low-lying areas, FEMA flood maps determine whether structures must be elevated on piers or pilings. V-zones (coastal wave action) require open foundation on piers with breakaway walls; A-zones require elevation to Base Flood Elevation plus freeboard. Inland elevated crawlspace on concrete block is standard
- **Roof pitch and gutters**: 6:12 minimum pitch recommended; steep pitch handles heavy rainfall and reduces hurricane uplift surface
- **Green roofs**: impractical in hurricane zone without substantial engineered anchoring

### Ventilation and Moisture Management

This is the most technically demanding aspect of building in the hot-humid south. The moisture drives inward in summer (hot humid outside, air-conditioned inside) — opposite of northern climates.
- **No vapor barrier on the interior wall face** in hot-humid climates — vapor barriers trap moisture in the wall assembly; use vapor-permeable materials throughout
- **Exterior continuous insulation** (foam board or mineral wool) acts as the thermal break AND the vapor control layer at the right dew point position in the assembly
- **Mechanical ventilation**: in tight construction, an ERV (energy recovery ventilator) is strongly preferred over HRV; the ERV's desiccant wheel removes moisture from incoming humid outdoor air, which is the primary summer load
- **Attic ventilation**: either sealed/conditioned attic (spray foam underside of roof deck) or ventilated attic with ridge and soffit vents — no in-between; a partially ventilated attic accumulates moisture
- **Crawlspace**: sealed and conditioned crawlspace with vapor barrier on ground and dehumidifier is the current best practice; vented crawlspaces in hot-humid climates accumulate moisture and promote rot and mold

## Water Systems

### Water Source Options

The South Atlantic receives 45–65 inches of annual precipitation, relatively well-distributed, making water quantity rarely the primary constraint. Water quality and contamination are the primary issues.

**Rainwater harvesting:**
- Georgia: legal for non-potable use; potable use requires permitted treatment system; no state-level permit for collection; county health departments govern indoor use
- South Carolina: legal; some municipalities have incentive programs
- Yield: 50 inches of rain on a 2,000 sq ft roof = approximately 60,000 gallons per year — easily sufficient for domestic use; size cisterns for 30-day drought events

**Wells:**
- Shallow wells (20–60 ft) tap the surficial aquifer; adequate for domestic use but vulnerable to contamination from agricultural runoff and septic fields
- Deeper artesian wells (100–300 ft) tap confined limestone aquifers in the Coastal Plain; higher quality but higher drilling cost
- Coastal wells are vulnerable to saltwater intrusion as sea levels rise; well yields and quality in low-lying coastal counties are increasingly unreliable; prioritize inland sites or elevated coastal sites for long-term well viability

**Surface water:**
- Abundant; rivers and streams throughout the region; must be filtered and treated (Giardia and agricultural chemical contamination common)
- Georgia and SC require a surface water withdrawal permit for anything beyond very small quantities

### Freezing and Thawing

Coastal plain: hard freezes (below 25°F) occur 2–10 nights per year; pipes in unheated spaces need insulation or heat tape, but elaborate freeze protection is unnecessary. Piedmont: freezes more common; budget 2–4 nights below 20°F per winter. Mountains: standard freeze protection for all outdoor water systems; bury lines 18 inches.

### Seasonal Availability

Year-round in most locations; summer thunderstorm season (June–September) supplements storage. Extended droughts (3–6 weeks without rain) occur most commonly in fall. Size cisterns for 6-week bridging.

## Energy Generation

### Solar Potential

The South Atlantic is a strong solar region, often underestimated due to its cloud cover reputation:

| Location | Avg peak sun hours/day | Notes |
|----------|----------------------|-------|
| Savannah, GA | 5.2–5.5 | Excellent coastal resource |
| Atlanta, GA | 4.8–5.2 | Good year-round with seasonal variation |
| Columbia, SC | 5.0–5.3 | Strong; above national average |
| Asheville, NC | 4.5–5.0 | Mountain shading reduces potential |
| Northern FL border | 5.5–6.0 | Best solar in the region |

Seasonal variation is moderate — winter is shorter and milder than northern states. Summer output is affected by heat (panels lose 15–20% in peak summer heat) and cloud cover from afternoon thunderstorms.

### Wind Potential

Onshore wind potential in the region is moderate and highly site-dependent. Coastal sites and ridge-top Piedmont sites may see 10–13 mph average winds. Valley and inland forest sites are poor wind sites. The South Atlantic coast has significant offshore wind potential but this is utility-scale. For most off-grid homesteads inland, solar PV is the primary and most reliable source.

### Hydroelectric Viability

The Piedmont and mountain regions have excellent micro-hydro potential. The fall line (where Piedmont plateau meets coastal plain) creates natural drop zones; numerous streams in the Appalachian foothills have sufficient head and flow year-round. A 500W–2kW micro-hydro system on a perennial stream provides a hurricane-resilient generation source that complements solar perfectly during cloudy storm periods.

### Backup Energy Options

- **Propane generator**: standard backup; required for the post-hurricane period when solar array may be damaged or occluded; 500-gallon minimum tank; propane stores indefinitely
- **Manual/non-electric backup**: wood cookstove provides cooking and supplemental heat independence from the primary system
- **Battery sizing**: 3-day autonomy is sufficient for this solar zone; LiFePO4 preferred; keep batteries out of unconditioned spaces that exceed 85°F in summer (heat degrades battery life)

## Seasonal Maintenance and Protocols

**Spring (March–May):**
- Deep garden bed preparation before summer heat and humidity arrive
- Chimney cleaning after heating season; inspect for hurricane-season readiness
- Inspect and test generator; verify hurricane preparedness kit is complete (see hazards section)
- Apply pre-emergent weed control in paths and open areas before summer weed explosion
- Mosquito management: eliminate standing water; stock Bti (mosquito dunks) for unavoidable water features; budget more time for this than most guides suggest — mosquito pressure affects outdoor work hours significantly

**Summer (June–August):**
- Harvest-intensive period; canning and dehydrating season
- Monitor for pest and disease pressure daily; early intervention prevents total crop loss
- Check well yield and water quality during dry spells
- Hurricane preparedness condition: by June 1, all preparations should be complete (see hazards section below)
- Battery bank cooling: if battery enclosure exceeds 85°F, add ventilation or relocate; heat kills LiFePO4 over time as surely as cold kills lead-acid

**Fall (September–November):**
- Hurricane season peak (August–October); maintain alert posture through October
- Fall planting is the premier growing season; plant brassicas and greens September 1–15 for best production
- Preserve final summer harvest (tomatoes, peppers, dried beans); complete canning before cold arrives
- Inspect roof before winter; replace any hurricane-loosened shingles or metal panels
- Sweet potato harvest before first frost

**Winter (December–February):**
- Low-input management: cool-season crops producing with minimal work
- Orchard and perennial management: pruning, mulching, dormant spray
- Deep maintenance on tools and equipment
- Plan and order seeds for spring; review previous season's notes

## Regional Hazards and Preparedness

### Hurricanes (Primary Risk)

Hurricane risk extends far inland in Georgia and South Carolina. Hurricane Matthew (2016) caused catastrophic flooding in eastern SC hundreds of miles from landfall. Hurricane Helene (2024) destroyed communities in western NC and northeast GA 300–400 miles from the coast via record rainfall and river flooding.

Wind zones for SC: Wind Zone 1 (130–140 mph) inland, Wind Zone 2 (140–150 mph) near coast, Wind Zone 3 (150–160 mph) within 1 mile of coastline — South Carolina Building Code windload.solutions/south-carolina-wind-load-requirements. Georgia coastal counties use similar ASCE 7 wind maps.

**Pre-hurricane season checklist (complete by June 1):**
- [ ] 7-day minimum food and water supply independent of any grid or delivery
- [ ] Generator fueled, tested, and ready (propane: full tank; gas: stabilized fuel)
- [ ] All critical documents in waterproof container (land deed, insurance, medical records, IDs)
- [ ] Chain saw fueled, sharpened, and readily accessible for post-storm road clearing
- [ ] Tarps, rope, and roofing nails for emergency roof repair
- [ ] 30-gallon or larger water storage filled before storm arrival
- [ ] Medications: 30-day supply
- [ ] Cash: $500+ in small bills (ATMs and card systems down for 1–2 weeks post-storm)
- [ ] Know your evacuation zone and route (SC: scemd.org/prepare/know-your-zone)
- [ ] All loose materials (tools, pots, chairs) secured or stored before storm arrival

**Structure hardening:**
- Hurricane straps (H2.5 or H10 minimum) on all rafter-to-wall connections
- Garage doors are the primary structural failure point; reinforce with horizontal bracing
- Install impact windows or have plywood cut to size for all windows; store plywood on-site
- Metal roofing with concealed fasteners performs significantly better than exposed-fastener standing seam in hurricane winds

### Flooding

Flash flooding and river flooding are increasing risks across the entire region. FEMA FIRM maps are the baseline but are frequently outdated; consult local land records for historical flood accounts. Develop a site-specific flood plan: know your elevation above the nearest stream's 100-year flood level; do not put critical systems (cisterns, battery bank, generator, food storage) below that elevation.

### Wildfire

Wildfire risk in the South Atlantic is moderate but real, particularly in the Piedmont pine-dominated landscapes (longleaf and loblolly pine are highly fire-adapted but also highly flammable) and in drought years. Spring (February–April) is the peak fire season, not summer. Maintain 30-ft defensible space; class A roofing; no fuel accumulation against the structure.

### Heat Events

Inland heat waves in summer (5+ consecutive days above 98°F with 80%+ humidity) can be fatal without cooling. The off-grid heat protocol: a designated cool room with the most insulation and least solar gain; a backup cooling source; neighbor check-in agreement during heat events. Never rely on a single cooling system without a backup plan.

## Regional Resources and Community

### Agricultural Extension Offices
- **University of Georgia Cooperative Extension**: extension.uga.edu — the primary resource for Georgia homesteaders; county offices in every county; outstanding vegetable gardening calendar and publications (fieldreport.caes.uga.edu)
- **Clemson Cooperative Extension** (SC): hgic.clemson.edu — Home and Garden Information Center; excellent pest ID and management resources
- **NC State Extension**: extension.ncsu.edu — strong programs in Appalachian foothills food systems and mountain farming

### Seed Companies with Regional Varieties
- **Southern Exposure Seed Exchange** (Mineral, VA): the premier seed company for the South Atlantic; decades of trialing in southeast conditions; exceptional heat-tolerant and traditional southern varieties — southernexposure.com
- **Sow True Seed** (Asheville, NC): Appalachian and mountain-adapted varieties; open-pollinated focus — sowtrueseed.com
- **Johnny's Selected Seeds**: ships nationally; good frost-date tools; strong disease-resistant tomato variety selection
- **Clemson Vegetable Breeding Program**: trial data on commercially released varieties is publicly available and directly applicable to SC/GA conditions

### Building and Technical Resources
- Georgia State Fire Marshal / Building Codes: dca.ga.gov
- South Carolina Building Codes Council: llr.sc.gov/bcc
- FEMA Flood Map Service Center: msc.fema.gov — look up your specific parcel's flood zone before purchasing
- SC DHEC: scdhec.gov — water well permitting and water quality testing
- NOAA hurricane storm surge viewer: coast.noaa.gov/hurricanestormsurge/atlas.html — visualize inundation for specific storm categories at your site

### Community Networks
- **Sustainable Agriculture Research and Education (SARE) Southeast**: sare.org — grants and technical support for sustainable farms in the region
- **Georgia Organics**: georgiaorganics.org — network of organic and sustainable farmers; conference and mentorship programs
- **Carolina Farm Stewardship Association**: carolinafarmstewards.org — SC and NC sustainable agriculture network
- Local grange halls and county fair agricultural societies remain active in rural SC and GA; valuable for peer knowledge, seed swapping, and local market connections
