# Domain 7: Heating & Cooling

> Thermal comfort determines survivability in extreme climates. An off-grid home
> that cannot be reliably heated in a northern winter or adequately cooled in a
> southern summer is not truly livable. Get this system right before adding
> anything else. Heating is higher stakes — hypothermia kills faster than
> discomfort. Fuel security (can you keep it running for 6 weeks in a storm?)
> is the design constraint that drives every decision here.

---

## Quick-Reference Decision Framework

| Climate and situation | Primary heating | Primary cooling | First priority |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cold climate (Zone 5–7), forested land | Wood stove or rocket mass heater | Cross-ventilation + ceiling fans | Fuel supply: secure 3–5 cords before first winter |
| Cold climate, limited timber access | Propane + passive solar design | Same | 500-gal LP tank + passive solar redesign |
| Mild climate (Zone 3–4 shoulder) | Masonry heater or mini-wood stove | Evaporative cooler (dry) or ventilation (humid) | Insulation quality — reduces both loads |
| Hot humid climate (Zone 1–2) | Minimal — propane as backup | Earth sheltering + ceiling fans + mini-split | Cooling dominates — passive design is the solution |
| Hot dry climate (Southwest) | Mini wood stove or propane | Evaporative cooler (swamp cooler) | Passive solar + thermal mass + swamp cooler |
| Disaster / grid-down scenario | Wood (stored) or propane (stored) | Manual — shade, ventilation, root cellar retreat | Fuel inventory: know exactly what you have |

## Section 1: Building Envelope First

**The most cost-effective heating and cooling investment is insulation — not equipment.**

No heating or cooling system compensates for a leaky, poorly insulated building.
Every dollar spent on the envelope saves more over the building's lifetime than
the same dollar spent on a better furnace. Design in this order:

1. **Air sealing** — the single highest-impact step. Blower door test reveals leaks.
   Foam, caulk, and weatherstrip before adding insulation.
2. **Insulation** — R-value targets by climate zone (see table below).
3. **Windows** — thermal weak points. Triple-pane in cold climates; low-e coating
   reduces solar gain in hot climates.
4. **Thermal mass** — concrete, stone, and adobe absorb heat during the day and
   release it at night. Dramatically moderates swings.
5. **Ventilation** — heat recovery ventilators (HRV) in tight cold-climate homes
   maintain air quality without bleeding heat.

### R-Value Targets by Climate Zone

| Zone | Description | Wall R-value | Ceiling R-value | Floor R-value |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1–2 | Hot/humid (FL, HI, Gulf Coast) | R-13 to R-20 | R-30 to R-38 | R-13 |
| 3 | Mixed/warm (Southeast, Southwest) | R-20 to R-25 | R-38 | R-19 |
| 4 | Mixed (Mid-Atlantic, PNW) | R-25 to R-30 | R-49 | R-25 |
| 5–6 | Cold (Midwest, Upper Plains, Mountains) | R-30 to R-38 | R-60 | R-30 |
| 7 | Very cold (Northern MN, Alaska borderline) | R-38+ | R-60+ | R-38+ |

*Zone 5 is the most common serious off-grid zone. R-60 ceiling, R-30 walls,
and airtight construction reduces a 2,000 sq ft home's heating load to ~40,000 BTU/hr
on a -10°F design day — manageable with a mid-sized wood stove alone.*

### Insulation Materials Comparison

| Material | R-value per inch | Pros | Cons | Cost (installed) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Fiberglass batts | R-3.2–3.8 | Cheap, DIY | Loses R-value if wet, air gaps | $0.50–$1.00/sq ft |
| Mineral wool (Rockwool) | R-3.7–4.2 | Fire-resistant, water-resistant, soundproof | More expensive than fiberglass | $1.00–$1.50/sq ft |
| Cellulose (blown-in) | R-3.5–3.8 | Recycled, fills gaps well, seals air | Settles over time, needs vapor management | $0.80–$1.20/sq ft (wall) |
| Spray foam (open cell) | R-3.5 | Air seals and insulates simultaneously | Professional install required, expensive | $1.50–$3.00/sq ft |
| Spray foam (closed cell) | R-6.0–7.0 | Highest R/inch, vapor barrier, rigid | Expensive, HFO blowing agents (GHG concern) | $3.00–$6.00/sq ft |
| Rigid foam (EPS/XPS) | R-3.8–5.0 | Waterproof, used under slab and on exterior | Flammable, requires fire-rated covering | $0.60–$1.50/sq ft |
| Straw bale | R-30–35 (18" bale) | Nearly free if local, very high R | Requires moisture management, permits | ~$1.00–$3.00/sq ft owner-built |
| Natural wool batts | R-3.5 | Renewable, vapor-buffering, non-toxic | Expensive, limited availability | $2.00–$4.00/sq ft |

## Section 2: Passive Solar Design — Free Heat (and Cooling)

Passive solar is not a supplemental strategy — it's a core design decision.
A well-designed passive solar home in Climate Zone 5 reduces heating load by
30–70%. This is achieved at build time through orientation and window placement,
costs nothing extra in energy, and lasts the building's lifetime.

### The Four Passive Solar Principles

**1. Orientation**: Place the primary glazing (windows) on the south-facing wall
(north-facing in southern hemisphere). South receives 3–5x more winter sun than
east or west. Within 15° of true south is acceptable; beyond 30° off south, gains
decline sharply.

**2. Glazing ratio**: South glass = 7–12% of conditioned floor area for direct-gain
systems. More glass → more gain → more risk of overheating without adequate mass.

**3. Thermal mass**: 3–6 inches of concrete, tile, brick, or stone on floors and
walls that receive direct south sunlight. Mass stores heat and releases it slowly,
preventing temperature spikes and moderating overnight loss. Ratio: 5–6 sq ft of
4-inch thick mass per sq ft of south glass.

**4. Overhangs**: A properly sized south-facing overhang shades south glass in
summer (when sun is high) while admitting winter sun (when sun is low). The
overhang projection formula:

```
Overhang depth = Window height × tan(90° - summer solstice altitude angle)

For 40° latitude: summer solstice altitude = 73°
Overhang = Window height × tan(90° - 73°) = Window height × 0.325

Example: 4-ft window → 1.3-ft overhang provides ~100% summer shading
```

### Passive Solar Heating Estimate

A rough rule of thumb for direct-gain passive solar in Zone 5 (40° latitude):

- Every sq ft of south glass gains ~0.6 BTU/hr on a clear winter day at 35°F
- 200 sq ft of south glass × 0.6 = ~120 BTU/hr average gain
- A well-insulated 1,500 sq ft home has a peak heat loss of ~30,000–40,000 BTU/hr at 0°F
- South glass covers roughly 10–20% of heating load on sunny winter days
- Combined with thermal mass, it can cover 30–50% of a heating season

**Passive solar in practice**: Design for it, don't retrofit it. A stick-frame home
built facing the wrong direction cannot be fixed cheaply. Passive solar orientation
costs nothing extra if you plan it when you break ground.

### Passive Cooling Principles

Passive cooling works through five mechanisms:

1. **Shade**: Trees on south and west sides reduce cooling load significantly.
   A deciduous tree shades summer sun while letting in winter light after leaf drop.
   Roof overhangs, awnings, and exterior shutters reduce solar gain more effectively
   than interior blinds (heat is already inside with interior blinds).

2. **Cross-ventilation**: Openings on opposite walls allow wind-driven air movement.
   Hot air rises — high openings on the leeward side exhaust heat. Stack effect:
   warm air exits through high openings, drawing cooler air in through low openings.
   Requires no electricity.

3. **Night flushing**: In dry climates, nights are cool even when days are hot.
   Open windows at night to cool thermal mass; close during the day. Mass absorbs
   heat load during the day without the interior heating up significantly.

4. **Earth sheltering**: Ground temperature at 8–12 ft depth is 50–55°F year-round
   across most of the continental U.S. An earth-sheltered home with 3+ ft of soil
   on the roof and bermed north/east/west walls uses ground temperature to moderate
   interior temps dramatically — effectively free cooling.

5. **Earth tubes (cool tubes)**: Air drawn through buried 6–8" diameter PVC pipes
   at 6–8 ft depth is pre-cooled before entering the home. Effective in dry climates.
   Simple construction: 50–100 ft of buried pipe with intake protected from insects/rain.

## Section 3: Wood and Biomass Heating

Wood is the most resilient heating fuel: it grows back, requires no supply chain,
and can be processed with hand tools. In most rural off-grid contexts, wood is
the primary or sole heating fuel. The design question is not whether to use wood
but which wood-burning system to use.

### 3.1 Wood Stove

The standard off-grid heating appliance. A cast-iron or steel firebox burns wood
and radiates heat into the room. Simple, reliable, has been used for 200 years.

**How it works**: Combustion air enters below the firebox, wood burns, flue gases
exit through a chimney. Secondary combustion in clean-burn stoves burns smoke
gases for additional heat and less creosote.

**Selecting a wood stove**:

| Factor | Guidance |
|---|---|
| Heating area | Match BTU output to room volume. Undersized = never warm; oversized = open windows in January. |
| BTU output | 1,000–1,200 BTU/hr per 100 sq ft in moderate insulation; less with tight envelope |
| Firebox volume | Larger firebox = longer burn time between reloads. Look for 3+ cu ft for overnight burns |
| Material | Cast iron holds heat longer; plate steel heats faster. Cast iron preferred for primary heat source. |
| EPA certification | 2020+ EPA Phase 2 certified stoves burn significantly cleaner — less creosote, more heat/cord |
| Catalytic vs. non-catalytic | Catalytic: more efficient at part load but combustor wears out ($100–$200 to replace). Non-cat: simpler, less maintenance |

**Sizing chart**:

| Home size (sq ft) | Insulation level | Recommended BTU/hr output | Approximate stove size |
|---|---|---|---|
| 500–800 | Good (Zone 5) | 25,000–35,000 | Small (Vermont Castings Aspen) |
| 800–1,500 | Good (Zone 5) | 35,000–50,000 | Medium (Jøtul F 500, Hearthstone) |
| 1,500–2,500 | Good (Zone 5) | 50,000–70,000 | Large (Pacific Energy Super LE) |
| 2,000+ | Moderate (Zone 5) | 70,000–90,000 | Extra-large or multiple stoves |
| Any size | Tight passive solar | -30 to -50% from above | Step down one size |

**Cost (2025 USD)**:
- Small quality stove (EPA certified): $800–$1,500
- Medium quality stove: $1,500–$2,500
- Large/premium stove: $2,500–$4,000
- Chimney (8" insulated double-wall, through roof): $1,500–$3,000 installed
- Hearth pad and clearances: $200–$600 DIY
- Total installed, medium stove: $3,500–$6,000

**Maintenance**:
- Creosote inspection: before every heating season; clean if >1/8" accumulation
- Annual chimney sweep: $150–$250 hired; DIY with round wire brush
- Door gasket replacement: every 3–5 years; $20–$50 in rope gasket
- Firebrick replacement: when cracked or crumbling; $5–$15/brick

### 3.2 Rocket Mass Heater

The most thermally efficient wood-burning option. Burns 3–6x less wood than a
conventional stove for the same heat output. Excess heat is stored in a large
thermal mass (adobe, cob, or brick bench) that radiates slowly for 12–24 hours
after the fire goes out.

**How it works**: A J-tube combustion chamber creates a powerful draft through
geometry alone. Air enters at the wood feed, fuel burns horizontally in the
combustion tunnel, then flue gases make a sharp turn upward through a highly
insulated heat riser (inner barrel). The rapid temperature change drives strong
convection without a tall chimney. Gases exit through the mass bench at near
room temperature — most heat is captured before it leaves the building.

**Key characteristics**:
- Combustion temperature: 1,200–1,800°F in heat riser (hot enough to burn all gases)
- Flue exit temperature: often below 120°F (hard to touch the exhaust pipe)
- Wood consumption: 1–2 cords/season for a well-insulated 1,500 sq ft home in Zone 5
- Burn window: 1–3 hour intense fire per day heats the mass; mass radiates for 12–24 hours
- Emissions: extremely low when operated correctly; creosote minimal

**Tradeoffs vs. wood stove**:

| Factor | Wood stove | Rocket mass heater |
|---|---|---|
| Efficiency | 70–80% (EPA certified) | 85–95% |
| Wood consumption | 3–5 cords/year | 1–2 cords/year |
| Heat response | Fast (30–60 min to warm room) | Slow (mass heats over 2–3 hours, releases 12–24 hr) |
| Installation | Professional or experienced DIY | DIY (cob/brick build) but complex |
| Permits | Standard chimney permits | May face permit resistance in some counties |
| Maintenance | Annual sweep | Annual check; mass rarely needs repair |
| Aesthetics | Industrial (nice stoves can be beautiful) | Sculptural — integrated into floor/furniture |
| Relocation | Portable | Permanent; built into structure |

**DIY build cost**: $200–$800 materials (firebrick, refractory cement, cob, barrel)
**Contractor-built**: $2,000–$6,000

**Learning curve**: Build from a proven design (Ianto Evans / Ernie Wisner plans).
Improvising the heat riser dimensions leads to smoke problems. The geometry is
non-obvious and the J-tube proportions are critical.

### 3.3 Masonry Heater

A European tradition (kachelofen, contra-flow heater, Finnish contraflow). A large
firebox of firebrick surrounded by hundreds to thousands of pounds of soapstone,
tile, or brick. Burns a single hot fire (1–4 hours) once or twice a day; mass
stores 50,000–100,000+ BTU and radiates slowly at a comfortable surface temperature.

**Key advantages**:
- Burns very hot and very clean: high temperature burns all gases, minimal creosote
- Radiates even heat all day from a single fire
- Surface temperature of 150–180°F (comfortable to touch; not a burn hazard like a stove)
- Can be integrated with a baking oven (traditional design)
- Lasts generations — some European masonry heaters are 100+ years old

**Disadvantages**:
- Extremely heavy (2,000–8,000 lbs) — requires engineered foundation
- Expensive ($5,000–$25,000 installed by a certified mason)
- Long lag time: fire lit, mass heats over 2–4 hours, radiates 12–24 hours
- Not fast-response: if you neglected it on a cold day, the room is cold now

**Best application**: Primary heat source in a tight, well-insulated home where
you maintain a consistent firing schedule. Not suitable as a "sometimes heat"
backup.

### 3.4 Wood Furnace with Ductwork

An outdoor wood furnace (OWF) or indoor wood gasification boiler that heats
water or air distributed through the home via ductwork or hydronic radiant.

**Outdoor wood furnace (OWF)**:
- Firebox surrounded by a water jacket; hot water piped underground to home
- Can heat home, domestic hot water, and outbuildings from a single fire
- Large fireboxes accept 3-4 ft logs, reducing splitting labor
- Burns less cleanly than EPA-certified insert stoves; some municipalities regulate them
- Cost: $5,000–$15,000 installed

**Wood gasification boiler (indoor)**:
- More efficient than OWF; upper chamber gasifies wood, lower chamber burns gases
- Very clean combustion (EPA certified); 85–92% efficiency
- Integrated with radiant floor or baseboard hydronic system
- Requires buffer tank (200–500 gallon) for heat storage
- Cost: $5,000–$12,000 for boiler + tank; hydronic system adds $3,000–$10,000+

**Best application**: Larger homesteads heating multiple structures; those who prefer
not to tend a fire inside the living space; radiant floor hydronic systems.

### 3.5 Wood Supply Planning

**The fundamental question**: Can you cut, split, stack, and dry enough wood every
year to sustain the heating system indefinitely without exhausting your timber resource?

**Cord volume**:
- 1 cord = 128 cubic feet stacked (4 ft × 4 ft × 8 ft)
- In reality, a cord of stacked wood is 80–90 cubic feet of actual wood (rest is air gaps)
- 1 cord hardwood ≈ 15–25 million BTU usable heat (varies significantly by species)

**Species heat value comparison**:

| Species | BTU/cord (million) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Osage orange (hedge apple) | 32.9 | Extremely dense; difficult to split; excellent |
| Black locust | 27.9 | Fast-growing; excellent BTU; splits well when green |
| Shagbark hickory | 27.7 | Top-tier; excellent for all-day burn |
| White oak | 26.4 | Dense, slow-burning; excellent coal bed |
| Red oak | 24.6 | Common, good BTU, splits well |
| Hard maple | 24.0 | Excellent firewood, splits cleanly when dry |
| Yellow birch | 23.6 | Good; bark is a natural firestarter |
| Ash | 23.6 | Premium firewood; splits easily; can burn slightly green |
| Cherry | 20.4 | Medium BTU; low smoke; pleasant smell |
| Elm | 20.0 | High BTU but difficult to split (interlocked grain) |
| Soft maple | 19.0 | Burns faster than hard maple; decent BTU |
| Cottonwood / poplar | 15.8–17.0 | Low BTU; burns fast; starter wood only |
| Pine (softwood) | 15.9–17.1 | Faster to season; higher creosote risk; acceptable for shoulder seasons |

**Seasoning requirements**:
- Green wood (fresh cut): 40–60% moisture content — produces steam, low heat, heavy creosote
- Properly seasoned: <20% moisture content — burns hot and clean
- Time to season:
  - Split hardwood stacked off ground with airflow: 6–12 months minimum; 12–24 months is better
  - Softwood: 4–6 months
  - Dense hardwood (oak, hickory): 18–24 months for best performance
- Moisture meter ($15–$40) is worth having — test the split face, not the bark

**Wood consumption estimates by system and climate**:

| System | Insulation | Climate | Cords/year (1,500 sq ft home) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Wood stove (standard) | Moderate | Zone 5 | 4–6 |
| Wood stove (EPA certified) | Good | Zone 5 | 3–4 |
| Wood stove | Tight passive solar | Zone 5 | 1.5–3 |
| Rocket mass heater | Good | Zone 5 | 1–2 |
| Masonry heater | Good | Zone 5 | 1.5–3 |
| Wood stove | Good | Zone 6–7 | 5–8 |
| Wood stove | Good | Zone 3–4 | 1.5–3 |

**Forest yield for sustainable harvest**:
- A well-managed woodland on good productive land: 1/2 to 1 cord/acre/year sustainable
- Targeting 4 cords/year: need 4–8 acres of productive woodland in perpetual rotation
- Coppicing (cutting to stump, allowing regrowth): accelerates yield; black locust and willow regrow vigorously
- Agroforestry guild planting (black locust + apple + elderberry rows): combines fuel + food

**Processing equipment**:

| Task | Manual tool | Power tool | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Felling | Crosscut saw | Chainsaw (16–18" bar) | Crosscut works; chainsaw is 10× faster |
| Bucking to length | Crosscut, bow saw | Chainsaw | 16–18" lengths for most stoves |
| Splitting | Maul (8 lb), wedges | Hydraulic log splitter | Maul sufficient for < 3 cords; splitter saves hours above that |
| Stacking | Manual | N/A | Elevated off ground; air gap on sides; covered top |

**Chainsaw maintenance** is non-negotiable: keep chain sharp, bar oiled, air filter clean.
A dull chain is the primary cause of saw injuries and doubles cutting time.

**Annual fuel audit** (do this each spring):
1. Count remaining cords from last season
2. Calculate cords consumed (= starting cords − remaining cords)
3. Order or cut replacement wood immediately after heating season
4. Stack and begin seasoning; this year's cut is next year's fuel

## Section 4: Propane / LP Gas

Propane is the standard backup or supplemental heating fuel. It requires no
labor to produce, stores indefinitely, and burns cleanly in modern appliances.
The tradeoff: it must be purchased and delivered — a supply-chain dependency
that disappears in a true grid-down scenario.

### 4.1 System Overview

**Components**:
1. **Tank** — buried or above-ground; 250–1,000 gallon residential
2. **Regulator** — steps pressure from tank (~100 PSI) down to appliance pressure (~0.5 PSI)
3. **Appliances** — furnace, wall heater, range/cooktop, water heater, generator
4. **Distribution** — black iron pipe (required for buried) or CSST flexible tubing

**Tank sizing guidance**:

| Tank size | Common use | Fill frequency | Typical cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| 120-gal above ground | Space heater or range backup | 2–4 fills/year | $400–$600 tank (owned) |
| 250-gal above ground | Small home supplemental | 1–2 fills/year | $600–$900 (owned) |
| 500-gal above ground | Primary heat for small home | 1 fill/year | $900–$1,400 (owned) |
| 1,000-gal above ground | Primary heat + cooking + generator | 1 fill/year or less | $1,500–$2,500 (owned) |
| 500-gal buried | Aesthetic, freeze protection | 1 fill/year | $1,800–$3,500 installed |

*Note: Propane suppliers often offer "free" tank rental with a purchasing contract.
Owning the tank allows price shopping. Rental locks you to one supplier.*

**Current propane prices (2025)**: $1.80–$3.50/gallon depending on region and season
(lower in warmer months; spike in cold snaps). Budget $2.50–$3.00/gallon average.

**Propane energy content**: ~91,500 BTU/gallon. A 90% efficient furnace delivers
~82,000 BTU of usable heat per gallon.

**Heating cost estimate**:
- 1,500 sq ft Zone 5 home, moderate insulation: ~100 million BTU/heating season
- At 80,000 BTU/gallon (furnace efficiency) = ~1,250 gallons/season
- At $2.50/gallon = **~$3,125/season** for propane primary heat
- Compare wood at 4 cords × $250/cord = $1,000/season (purchased), or near-free if self-cut

### 4.2 Propane Appliances for Off-Grid Use

**Wall heaters (direct-vent or ventless)**:
- No electricity required (thermocouple ignition, natural convection)
- Rinnai, Empire, Mr. Heater Big Buddy (ventless up to 18,000 BTU)
- Ventless heaters produce CO and moisture — use with ventilation; not for bedrooms
- Direct-vent (pipe through wall): safer; sealed combustion draws outside air
- Cost: $200–$800 for unit; $200–$500 installation (direct-vent)

**Propane furnace**:
- Forced-air distribution through ductwork
- 90%+ efficiency units available
- Requires electricity for blower and ignition — add a 12V inverter or battery backup
  for grid-down operation
- Cost: $1,500–$4,000 for unit + installation

**Radiant floor boiler (propane)**:
- Highest comfort heating method — warms surfaces, not air
- Boiler heats water through tubing embedded in slab or under flooring
- Very efficient at low temperatures (80–120°F water)
- Requires no ductwork — ideal for open floor plans
- Combines well with wood gasification boiler (propane backup)
- Cost: $5,000–$15,000+ (boiler + tubing + manifolds + installation)

### 4.3 Propane Safety and Storage

- Propane is heavier than air — pools in low spots; basements and crawlspaces are hazard zones
- Required: CO + propane detector at floor level near appliances
- Tank placement: 10 ft minimum from structure (above-ground residential)
- Never bury a tank not rated for underground installation (different steel treatment)
- Keep fill valve, regulator, and relief valve accessible and not buried in snow
- Extended storage: propane does not degrade — a full 500-gal tank is good indefinitely

## Section 5: Electric and Heat Pump Options

For off-grid electric heating to be viable, the energy system (Domain 6) must
generate abundant surplus. In most climates, electric resistance heating (1 BTU
per watt) is prohibitively expensive on solar. **Heat pumps** change the math.

### 5.1 Mini-Split Heat Pump

A mini-split (ductless heat pump) is the most efficient electric heating and
cooling system available. It moves heat rather than generating it — delivering
2–4 BTU of heat for every watt consumed (COP 2.0–4.0).

**How it works**: The outdoor unit extracts heat from outside air (even at -13°F
with modern cold-climate models) and transfers it inside via refrigerant cycle.
Reversed in summer: extracts heat from inside and rejects it outside.

**Cold-climate performance**:

| Outdoor temp | COP (older unit) | COP (cold-climate, e.g., Mitsubishi Hyper-Heat) |
|---|---|---|
| 47°F | 3.5–4.0 | 3.5–4.5 |
| 17°F | 1.5–2.0 | 2.5–3.0 |
| 0°F | ~1.0 (resistance backup) | 1.5–2.0 |
| -13°F | Does not function | 1.0–1.5 |

**Power requirements**: A 12,000 BTU (1-ton) mini-split draws ~1,200–1,500W.
On solar with battery backup, this is a significant load — plan for it in Domain 6.

**Best use case**: Primary cooling system; supplemental heat in mild climates (Zone 3–4);
backup heat source in Zone 5+ when the wood stove is not running (shoulder seasons).
As a primary heat source in Zone 6–7, the solar bank required is substantial.

**Cost**:
- 9,000 BTU (750 sq ft): $800–$1,200 unit + $500–$1,000 installation
- 12,000 BTU (1,000 sq ft): $1,000–$1,500 unit + $500–$1,000 installation
- 18,000 BTU (1,500 sq ft): $1,200–$2,000 unit + $800–$1,500 installation

**DIY installation**: "DIY mini-split" pre-charged line sets (MRCOOL DIY, Mr. Cool)
eliminate the need for an HVAC technician for refrigerant line connection.
Electrical connection still requires a permit and may require an electrician.

### 5.2 Electric Resistance Heaters (Last Resort)

Baseboard heaters, space heaters, and radiant ceiling panels are 100% efficient
(1 watt = 3.41 BTU) but expensive to run on a solar bank.

**When acceptable**:
- Small, tight rooms where spot heating for a few hours replaces heating the whole home
- Bathroom warming (20–30 minutes/day = manageable load)
- Emergency backup when primary system fails

**Never acceptable**: Whole-home electric resistance heating on an off-grid system
unless you have massive solar + battery or micro-hydro surplus.

## Section 6: Cooling Systems

In most of the continental U.S., a well-designed passive home with proper
orientation, shading, and night ventilation is sufficient for Zone 4 and below.
Active cooling becomes necessary in Zone 1–3 (hot/humid climate) or in a tight,
poorly oriented home in Zone 4.

### 6.1 Evaporative Cooler (Swamp Cooler)

**Works in**: Hot, dry climates (Southwest, Mountain West, parts of Great Plains)
**Does not work in**: High-humidity climates (Gulf Coast, Southeast, Midwest in summer)

**How it works**: A pump wets pads; a fan draws dry outside air through the pads.
Water evaporates, dropping air temperature by 15–40°F. The cooled, now-humid
air is blown into the home. Works best when outdoor humidity is below 40%; above
60%, cooling effect is minimal.

**Power requirements**: 200–500W (fan and pump) — very solar-compatible.

**Two-stage evaporative cooling**: A 2-stage cooler pre-cools air indirectly
(without adding humidity first), then uses a second evaporative stage. Extends
usable range to 50% outdoor humidity.

**Sizing**: 2 CFM per square foot of cooled area is standard.
- 1,500 sq ft home → 3,000 CFM unit

**Cost**:
- Portable window unit (1,500–3,000 CFM): $200–$600
- Whole-house rooftop unit (4,000–6,000 CFM): $1,000–$2,500 + installation
- DIY swamp cooler from IBC tote + fan + pump: $200–$400 materials

**Maintenance**: Clean pads every 1–2 weeks during use season; replace pads
annually ($20–$60); clean reservoir; check pump motor.

### 6.2 Ceiling Fans

Often overlooked, but ceiling fans are the most cost-effective cooling tool:
25–75W creates a wind-chill effect that allows thermostat setpoint to rise 4°F
without perceived discomfort, reducing AC load significantly.

- Run fans counterclockwise in summer (downdraft creates wind chill)
- Run fans clockwise in winter on low speed (pulls warm air down from ceiling)
- Off-grid compatible: 12V DC ceiling fans available; or standard 120V on an inverter

### 6.3 Ground-Coupled Cooling (Earth Tubes)

Air pulled through buried pipes is pre-cooled by ground temperature (50–55°F year-round).

**Design**:
- 6–8" diameter smooth HDPE or PVC pipe
- Buried at 6–8 ft depth (below frost line)
- Length: 50–100 ft minimum; 100–200 ft for best effect
- Inlet protected with insect screen, raised 18" off ground
- Slope slightly toward exhaust for condensate drainage
- Condensate is significant — must drain away from structure

**Performance**: Reduces inlet air temperature to within 10–15°F of ground temperature.
In 95°F weather, incoming air is pre-cooled to ~65–70°F before entering the home.

**Cost**: $400–$1,500 DIY (pipe + excavation is the major cost if you lack equipment)

**Maintenance**: Inspect for moisture/mold accumulation annually; occasional UV-C
treatment of the pipe interior (low-cost UV light wand works).

### 6.4 Whole-House Fan

A whole-house fan in the ceiling pulls air through open windows and exhausts into
the attic, then out through attic vents. Used during cooler morning/evening hours
to rapidly cool thermal mass.

- 2,000–4,000 CFM replaces home air in 3–5 minutes
- Very effective at night flushing in Zone 3–5 climates
- Power: 400–1,000W; use for 1–3 hours at night = 1–3 kWh/day
- Cost: $300–$800 unit + $200–$600 installation
- Not suitable for hot/humid climates (pulls humid air in)

### 6.5 Earth Sheltering and Root Cellar as Thermal Refuge

In extreme heat events (115°F+), underground spaces remain at ground temperature.
A root cellar (Section 8 of the master outline) at 8+ ft depth maintains 45–55°F
year-round — a literal refuge during dangerous heat events when cooling systems fail.

This is not hypothetical planning: heat domes in the Pacific Northwest (2021: 116°F
in Portland) and ongoing Southwest heat events have made heat lethality a serious
concern even in historically temperate regions. Know where your thermal refuge is.

## Section 7: Heating Integration System Design

For most cold-climate off-grid homes, the optimal system is a primary + backup
hierarchy with clearly defined roles:

### Recommended System Hierarchy (Zone 5, Forested Land)

| Tier | System | Role | Failure response |
|---|---|---|---|
| Primary | Wood stove or rocket mass heater | 80–95% of annual heating load | Next tier |
| Backup | Propane direct-vent wall heater | Coverage when away or fuel exhausted | Next tier |
| Emergency | Insulated interior sleeping space + sleeping bags | Bare survival (0°F+ indoor) | Retreat to neighbors |

**Design the primary system to handle any realistic scenario on its own.**
The backup is for convenience and short absences, not for continuous parallel operation.

### Integrated Domestic Hot Water (DHW) from Wood

A wood stove with a built-in water coil (or a separate tankless water back, aka
thermosiphon loop) provides domestic hot water nearly free during the heating season.

**Options**:
1. **Coil inside firebox**: Water coil welded inside firebox; circulates via thermosiphon
   to a 40–80 gallon storage tank. Passive, no pump required.
   - Risk: If fire dies without water flow, coil can overheat and create steam (pressure hazard)
   - Requires pressure-relief valve and expansion tank
2. **Wet back (back boiler)**: Water jacket on back or sides of firebox. Common in
   European wood stoves. Engineered from factory.
3. **Solar DHW + wood backup**: During heating season, wood heats water;
   during non-heating season, solar thermal panels carry the load. No propane needed.

### Heating Season Preparation Checklist

**July/August** (before heating season):
- [ ] Wood inventory: confirm 3+ cords of seasoned wood on hand; cut and stack next year's supply
- [ ] Chimney sweep and inspection; replace damaged liner sections
- [ ] Test wood stove door gasket seal (paper test: paper should resist being pulled out when door closed)
- [ ] Inspect and test propane backup; check tank level; schedule fill if < 30%
- [ ] Test all smoke and CO detectors; replace batteries
- [ ] Clear 3 ft radius around stove of combustibles
- [ ] Inspect all heat-related pipes and connections (water heating loop if applicable)

**November** (when heating season begins):
- [ ] First real fire — watch for smoke, smell for creosote, confirm damper operation
- [ ] Test propane backup ignition
- [ ] Confirm wood supply is accessible and protected from rain/snow
- [ ] Set up firewood staging area indoors (3–5 day supply in covered porch or mudroom)

## Section 8: Cooling Season Preparation and Heat Adaptation

### Passive Cooling Preparation Checklist

**March/April** (before cooling season):
- [ ] Clean and test ceiling fans; reverse direction
- [ ] Install window shading on south and west exposures (awnings, exterior roll shades, deciduous trees check)
- [ ] Test whole-house fan if equipped; clean grille and verify airflow
- [ ] Inspect earth tube condensate drain; run UV-C wand through pipe if equipped
- [ ] Test mini-split / swamp cooler; clean filters
- [ ] Check swamp cooler pads; replace if worn

### Heat Dome Protocol (Extreme Heat Event)

For temperatures above 105°F sustained:
1. Seal home during the day: close all windows, exterior shades down, minimize door openings
2. Ground floor / basement is coolest — sleep and retreat downstairs
3. Night flushing: open windows after 10 PM when outdoor temp drops below indoor temp
4. Hydration protocol: 1 cup water every 20–30 minutes regardless of thirst sensation
5. Root cellar / underground space: temperature refuge for animals and vulnerable people
6. Minimize cooking inside (generates significant heat); use outdoor rocket stove or solar cooker
7. Wet towel / evaporative vest cooling for persons, especially elderly or children

### Cold Snap Protocol (Extreme Cold Event)

For temperatures below -20°F sustained for 3+ days:
1. Reduce heated space to 1–2 rooms: close off unused rooms, hang insulating curtains at doorways
2. Concentrate thermal mass and people in one room — body heat is significant (250 BTU/hr/person)
3. Wood consumption will spike: -20°F needs 2–3× more heat than 20°F
4. Water pipe freeze prevention: allow faucets to drip; wrap exposed pipes; keep under-sink cabinets open
5. Generator / battery check: cold reduces battery capacity (LiFePO4: -10% at 14°F; lead-acid: -30%)
6. Keep propane backup tank warm: bury line below frost depth; never let tank fall below 20% in winter
7. Stock emergency sleeping bags rated to -20°F or lower — know exactly where they are

## Section 10: Disaster and Grid-Down Scenarios

### Extended Power Outage (1–4 weeks)

**Heating**: Wood stove operates without electricity (convective, no blower needed).
Propane direct-vent wall heater operates without electricity (thermocouple ignition).
Propane furnace with electric blower: add a 12V battery + 300W inverter to run
the blower (draws ~200–300W). A 100Ah 12V battery runs the blower for 6–12 hours.

**Cooling**: Evaporative cooler (small pump + fan, 300W) — run from a car battery or
solar panel. Whole-house fan (400–1,000W) — requires inverter + battery.
Passive cooling (shade, night ventilation, thermal mass) requires no electricity.

**Critical inventory list** (check annually, maintain always):

| Item | Minimum | Ideal |
|---|---|---|
| Seasoned firewood | 1 cord | 3+ cords |
| Propane | 100 gallons | 250+ gallons |
| Sleeping bags (rated -20°F) | 1 per person | 2 per person |
| Wool blankets | 2 | 4+ |
| Emergency candles or oil lamps | 12 candles | 24+ / 1 gal lamp oil |
| Backup lighter / matches | 2 lighters | Lighters + 200 matches |

### Extended Winter Emergency (6+ weeks off-grid, no resupply)

The "heating season survival" scenario: grid down, roads impassable, no propane
delivery. This is the binding constraint that wood heating was designed for.

**Fuel math for 6-week survival**:
- 6-week demand at 1 cord/month = 1.5 cords minimum
- In a cold snap (-10°F to -20°F), demand doubles: 3 cords for 6 weeks
- **Recommendation**: Always maintain a 3-cord minimum seasoned inventory.
  Never burn below 1-cord reserve without cutting replacement immediately.

**Reducing heating demand in extremes**:
- Seal unused rooms (drop heating zone to 500 sq ft = 50–60% fuel reduction)
- Insulating window inserts: cut rigid foam to window size; R-10+ at night, store during day
- Thermal curtains: double-cell cellular shades + blackout — adds R-2 to R-4 per window
- Draft dodgers (door bottoms): 20–30% of heat loss is air infiltration under doors

### Nuclear Fallout Shelter Thermal Management

An earth-sheltered fallout shelter has natural thermal stability (50–55°F ambient)
but requires active heating if occupants will be inside for 2+ weeks in winter.

**Fuel for shelter use**:
- Wood burning inside a sealed shelter is impossible (CO + O2 depletion)
- Propane with adequate ventilation: possible but risky — CO detector mandatory
- Preferred: heat the shelter before sealing (thermal mass stores heat for 48–72 hours)
- Electric resistance heater from battery bank: safest for sealed space;
  a 1,500W space heater on a 200Ah 48V LiFePO4 bank = ~6 hours of heat;
  size bank accordingly
- Insulate the shelter entrance (double-door airlock with insulation) to reduce heat loss
- Body heat of multiple occupants in an insulated space is significant: 4 people = ~1,000 BTU/hr

**Ventilation in sealed space**: The NCRP shelter ventilation guidance allows
filtered air intake (HEPA + activated carbon for fallout particles) after 48–72 hours
(when immediate fallout decay has reduced radiation by ~90%). At that point,
fresh air ventilation resumes and CO/propane risk drops dramatically.

*See Domain 15 (Disaster Scenarios) for full nuclear event protocol.*
