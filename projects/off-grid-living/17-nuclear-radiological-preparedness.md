# Domain 17: Nuclear & Radiological Preparedness

> Most people treat nuclear preparedness as either impossible or irrelevant. Both
> positions are wrong. The threat is real, probabilistically low but non-zero, and
> the measures that meaningfully reduce mortality are neither exotic nor expensive.
> Staying inside a concrete basement for 24 hours reduces exposure by a factor of
> 200. Removing outer clothing eliminates 90% of surface contamination. A $30 KI
> tablet supply protects thyroid tissue completely. This domain translates the
> technical guidance of FEMA, CDC, IAEA, and NCRP into practical decisions for
> off-grid households and communities.

---

## 17.1 Threat Overview

### 17.1.1 Types of Nuclear and Radiological Incidents

Four distinct event types require different responses. Confusing them is the first
planning error.

**Improvised Nuclear Device (IND)**
A ground-burst or low-altitude nuclear detonation by a non-state actor. Most credible
threat involves a weapon in the 1–10 kiloton range. Produces three immediate hazards:
blast overpressure (lethal within ~1 km of a 10 kT detonation), thermal pulse (fire
and flash burns within ~2 km), and prompt radiation (lethal without shielding within
~0.8 km). The delayed threat — fallout — is survivable with preparation and extends
danger into the 10–20 mile downwind corridor.

**Nuclear Power Plant Accident**
Chernobyl and Fukushima scale events. No blast or prompt radiation hazard for the
general public. Primary threat is airborne release of radioactive iodine (I-131),
cesium (Cs-137), and strontium (Sr-90). Protective actions are shelter-in-place,
potassium iodide, and controlled evacuation within the emergency planning zone (EPZ,
typically 10–50 miles). Timeline for protective decisions is hours, not minutes.

**Radiological Dispersal Device (RDD / "Dirty Bomb")**
Conventional explosive wrapped with radioactive material. Primarily a psychological
and economic weapon. Actual radiological health risk to the general public is low —
the explosion itself causes more casualties than the radiation. Primary concern is
contamination of property and psychological disruption. Response is decontamination
and containment, not mass evacuation.

**Nuclear Weapon State Attack**
Strategic or tactical nuclear strike. Involves weapons in the 100 kT to multi-megaton
range. Immediate effects dominate within 5–15+ miles. Surviving populations at greater
distances face the same fallout hazard as an IND scenario, scaled up. Planning
approach is the same; only the scale changes.

### 17.1.2 Fallout: What It Is and How It Behaves

Fallout is radioactive debris created when a nuclear fireball vaporizes ground material
and fuses it with fission products. The particles rise into the mushroom cloud, then
descend downwind over hours. Key characteristics that govern your response:

**Particle size**: Fallout particles are visible to the naked eye — the size of fine
sand or table salt. They are not easily inhaled into the lungs, which is relevant
because the primary hazard is external gamma irradiation from deposited particles, not
internal inhalation. This is actionable: brushing particles off skin and removing
clothing is highly effective.

**Arrival time**: Fallout can begin arriving as early as 15 minutes downwind in heavy
wind conditions. In most scenarios, you have 10–15 minutes after a visible detonation
to reach shelter before significant fallout arrives.

**Geographic extent**: A 10 kT detonation produces dangerous fallout concentrations
within roughly 10–20 miles downwind. Wind speed and direction determine the fallout
plume shape. The pattern is not a uniform circle — it is a narrow elongated ellipse
stretching downwind.

**Decay rate — the 7:10 Rule**: Fallout radiation decays rapidly and predictably.
For every seven-fold increase in time after detonation, radiation dose rate decreases
by a factor of ten. In practical terms:

| Time After Detonation | Relative Dose Rate |
|----------------------|-------------------|
| 1 hour               | Reference (1,000 R/hr at ground zero) |
| 7 hours              | ~100 R/hr (1/10) |
| 2 days (49 hrs)      | ~10 R/hr (1/100) |
| 2 weeks              | ~1 R/hr (1/1,000) |

The 7:10 rule is accurate to within ±25% through the first two weeks and approximately
±factor of 2 through six months. It means that staying sheltered for the critical
first 24–72 hours provides enormous risk reduction even in moderately contaminated
areas.

**Long-lived isotopes**: After the first few weeks, short-lived fission products are
gone. Cs-137 (half-life ~30 years) and Sr-90 (half-life ~29 years) dominate long-term
contamination. These drive the years-to-decades timeline for rehabitation of heavily
contaminated areas, but are generally relevant only in the immediate vicinity of a
detonation or in Chernobyl-scale power plant releases.

## 17.2 Shelter Design and Construction

The single most important survival action after a nuclear event is getting inside a
substantial structure and staying there. The measure used to quantify shelter
effectiveness is the **Protection Factor (PF)**: a PF of 40 means occupants receive
1/40th (2.5%) of the outdoor dose. FEMA's minimum standard for public fallout shelters
is PF 40 (FEMA TR-87). Most households can significantly exceed this with modest
preparation.

### 17.2.1 Protection Factors by Building Type and Location

| Shelter Type | Approximate PF | Occupant Receives |
|---|---|---|
| Open field, no cover | 1 | 100% outdoor dose |
| Wood-frame house, ground floor | 2–5 | 20–50% |
| Brick/masonry house, ground floor | 10–15 | 7–10% |
| Center of large office building | 20–40 | 2.5–5% |
| Wood-frame house, basement | 10–20 | 5–10% |
| Concrete/brick house, basement | 200+ | <0.5% |
| Underground parking structure | 200+ | <0.5% |
| Subway system | 1,000+ | <0.1% |

The key physical principles:

- **Mass stops gamma radiation**: Density and thickness of material between you and
  fallout particles is what matters. Halving thickness (HVT) — the thickness needed
  to halve gamma dose — is approximately 5 cm of concrete, 5 cm of brick, 8.4 cm of
  packed earth, or 22.4 cm of wood.
- **Distance from contaminated surfaces**: Move away from exterior walls, roof, and
  ground level. Middle floors of large buildings balance distance from roof (fallout
  deposit) and ground (fallout deposit). Basement + interior location is optimal.
- **Seal the building**: Shut all windows, doors, fireplace dampers, and ventilation
  systems that draw outside air.

### 17.2.2 Basement Hardening (Most Practical Option)

For most off-grid households, the basement is the shelter. Measured enhancements,
in order of cost-effectiveness:

**Tier 1: Free / Zero cost**
- Identify the interior corner farthest from exterior walls and below grade.
- Pre-position supplies (see Section 17.6) in that location.
- Know in advance that you will shut all ventilation, close all windows and dampers.

**Tier 2: $100–$500**
- Stack sandbags (or 5-gallon buckets of water, or concrete blocks) along interior
  faces of above-grade basement walls to add shielding mass. Water provides ~5.6 cm
  halving thickness per 20 cm depth — comparable to concrete.
- Fill any wall penetrations (pipes, electrical conduit) with caulk or foam to reduce
  particle infiltration.
- Heavy bookshelves, full water storage containers, or dense appliances stacked against
  walls raise the effective PF significantly.
- Pre-cut plastic sheeting and duct tape for sealing HVAC vents.

**Tier 3: $500–$3,000**
- Add a continuous concrete or CMU inner wall segment (4–8 inch block) along the
  above-grade portion of basement walls. An 8-inch CMU adds approximately 1.5 halving
  thicknesses per layer.
- Install a positive-pressure air filtration system using HEPA filters to prevent
  particle infiltration while maintaining breathable air. This is more relevant for
  multi-week stays.
- Consider a dedicated food/water cache in a purpose-built interior closet.

**Tier 4: $10,000–$50,000+ (purpose-built shelter)**
Commercial or custom-built underground bunkers achieve PF 1,000+. For most
households this is economically unjustifiable given the probability of use, but it is
appropriate for high-risk geographic areas (within 20 miles of military installations,
strategic population centers) or community-scale planning.

### 17.2.3 No-Basement Improvised Shelter

Where no basement exists:

- **Interior closet, center of building**: Stack heaviest available materials (water
  containers, books, concrete blocks, soil-filled bags) around and above the closet.
  Aim for at least 4 halving thicknesses in each direction (approx. 32 cm of packed
  earth or 20 cm of concrete on all sides).
- **Under staircase**: Brick, concrete blocks, or sandbags on stair treads above and
  along exposed sides. Cover with plywood to contain material.
- **Pole-covered trench**: A hole in the ground covered with poles and 60+ cm of
  packed earth can achieve PF 100+. Digging depth and earth thickness are the
  dominant variables. This is a legitimate option if you have hours of warning and
  hand tools.

### 17.2.4 Shelter Duration Guidance

The 7:10 rule drives the shelter timeline decision:

- **First 24 hours**: Highest risk period. Stay sheltered regardless of dose rate
  outside unless an immediate life-threatening hazard (fire, gas leak, building
  collapse) requires exit.
- **24–72 hours**: Dose rate is declining rapidly. If you have a detector and
  outdoor readings are below 2 mR/hr (0.02 mSv/hr), brief exits for critical needs
  are defensible. Keep excursions under 30 minutes.
- **72 hours – 2 weeks**: Continue sheltering. Dose rates should be approaching
  background in most contaminated-but-survivable areas. Official guidance is to
  await emergency management authorization before full exit.
- **2 weeks and beyond**: In most fallout-affected areas (but not immediate
  detonation zones), dose rates have declined sufficiently for limited outdoor
  activity. Monitor with a dosimeter; follow reentry guidance from Section 17.8.

## 17.3 Potassium Iodide and Medical Countermeasures

### 17.3.1 What KI Does and Does Not Do

Potassium iodide (KI) is a **thyroid-only** protective agent. It works by saturating
the thyroid gland with stable (non-radioactive) iodine, blocking uptake of radioactive
iodine-131 (I-131). It has no effect on any other radioactive isotope or any other
organ. It does not protect against whole-body radiation exposure, ARS, or any radiation
effect other than thyroid cancer from I-131. This distinction matters because KI is
sometimes misunderstood as a general radiation antidote — it is not.

KI is most relevant in two scenarios:
1. Nuclear power plant accidents (large I-131 releases over hours to days)
2. Nuclear detonations in the first 24–72 hours when I-131 is dominant

After ~3 months post-event, I-131 (half-life 8 days) is negligible and KI has no
further protective value.

### 17.3.2 KI Dosing Protocol

The FDA-approved dosing protocol (FDA Guidance, December 2001) is based on age and
body weight. A single dose provides approximately 24 hours of thyroid protection.

| Age Group | KI Dose | 130 mg tablet | 65 mg tablet | Oral solution (65 mg/mL) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Adults >40 years* | 130 mg | 1 tablet | 2 tablets | 2 mL |
| Adults 18–40 years | 130 mg | 1 tablet | 2 tablets | 2 mL |
| Pregnant/lactating women | 130 mg | 1 tablet | 2 tablets | 2 mL |
| Adolescents 12–18 years** | 65 mg | 1/2 tablet | 1 tablet | 1 mL |
| Children 3–12 years | 65 mg | 1/2 tablet | 1 tablet | 1 mL |
| Children 1 month–3 years | 32 mg | Use solution | 1/2 tablet | 0.5 mL |
| Infants birth–1 month | 16 mg | Use solution | Use solution | 0.25 mL |

*Adults over 40 should take KI only when predicted exposure is ≥500 cGy (very high
levels) due to increased risk of thyroid disease from KI itself at this age.
**Adolescents weighing ≥150 lbs receive the adult dose (130 mg).

**Timing is critical**: KI is most effective when taken 1–2 hours before or shortly
after exposure. Effectiveness drops sharply after 4 hours and is minimal at 12+ hours.
When official guidance says "take KI now," act immediately.

**Contraindications**: Persons with known iodine hypersensitivity, dermatitis
herpetiformis, or hypocomplementemic vasculitis should avoid KI. Those with nodular
thyroid disease, Graves' disease, or autoimmune thyroiditis require medical
consultation. Seafood allergy alone does not indicate iodine hypersensitivity.

**Making liquid from tablets for infants**: Crush one 65 mg tablet, dissolve in 4 oz
(120 mL) of water to create a solution of approximately 0.5 mg/mL. Draw the required
volume using a dropper. May be mixed with juice or milk to improve palatability.

**Shelf life**: Commercial KI tablets are stable indefinitely if stored cool, dry, and
in sealed original packaging. The FDA has confirmed no expiration date concern for
properly stored KI. Stock a minimum 14-day supply (14 doses per person).

**Sourcing**: IOSAT (65 mg) and ThyroSafe (65 mg) are FDA-approved brands available
without prescription at pharmacies and online for approximately $8–$12 per 14-tablet
blister pack. Some states distribute KI free to residents within 10 miles of nuclear
power plants — check your state radiation control program.

### 17.3.3 Other Medical Countermeasures

KI is the only FDA-approved over-the-counter radiological countermeasure. Additional
items to stock:

- **DTPA (diethylenetriamine pentaacetate)**: FDA-approved for internal contamination
  with plutonium, americium, or curium. Prescription only; relevant primarily for
  nuclear worker accidents, not general public planning.
- **Prussian blue (Radiogardase)**: FDA-approved for internal Cs-137 or thallium
  contamination. Prescription only; reduces biological half-life of Cs-137 by ~69%.
- **Growth factors (G-CSF/Neupogen)**: Used in hospitals to stimulate bone marrow
  recovery after high-dose radiation exposure (ARS). Prescription, requires cold chain
  storage. Community stockpiling is not realistic.
- **Standard medical supplies with elevated value post-event**: antibiotics (infection
  is the primary killer in hematopoietic ARS), anti-nausea medications (ondansetron
  or promethazine), IV fluids and electrolyte replenishment, wound care supplies.

See Domain 08 (Medical & Health) for the full medical supply inventory.

## 17.4 Detection and Monitoring

### 17.4.1 Why You Need a Detector

Without measurement, you cannot make rational decisions about shelter duration, exit
timing, food safety, or casualty triage. Estimated or guessed dose rates lead to
either unnecessary prolonged sheltering (lost productivity, psychological cost) or
premature exit (unnecessary exposure). A radiation detector is not optional for serious
nuclear preparedness.

### 17.4.2 Detector Types and Recommendations

**Geiger-Mueller (GM) Counter with Pancake Probe**
The standard civilian radiation detector. Detects beta and gamma radiation, with some
alpha capability via thin-window pancake probes. Reads in CPM (counts per minute) or
converted mR/hr / mSv/hr. Best for: environmental survey, contamination screening,
determining when it is safe to exit shelter.

Recommended models with realistic cost:
- Radex RD1503+ (~$80): Simple, rugged, accurate, widely available. Good for basic
  monitoring.
- GQ GMC-500+ (~$150): More sensitive, data logging, USB output.
- Mazur PRM-9000 (~$350): Professional-grade sensitivity, appropriate for community
  use or first-responder level.

**Personal Dosimeter (Electronic)**
Measures accumulated dose, not rate. Tells you how much radiation you have received
total. Essential for anyone making excursions out of shelter or performing community
response tasks. Electronic dosimeters (vs. film badges) provide real-time readings.

- Soeks Quantum (~$100): Compact, LED display, reasonable accuracy.
- RADOS RAD-60 (~$300): Professional-grade, used by nuclear workers.

**Direct-Reading Dosimeter (DRD)**
Pen-style ionization chambers reading in mR or R. Simple, reliable, no batteries
required. Often available through civil defense surplus. Used in conjunction with a
charger. A set of 4–6 DRDs for a household costs $40–$80 from surplus.

**The Kearny Fallout Meter (KFM) — Improvised Option**
Developed by Cresson Kearny at Oak Ridge National Laboratory (1978), the KFM is a
functional electroscope-based radiation meter buildable from household materials:
a soup can, aluminum foil, thread, plastic, wire, drywall scraps, and tape. No
batteries required — charged by rubbing a hard plastic object on dry paper.

Operating range: 30 mR/hr to 43 R/hr. Accuracy: ±25%. This matches the range
needed for fallout monitoring. Plans are freely available from the Oak Ridge archives
and OISM (oism.org/nwss). Construction takes 2–4 hours with the manual. Every
preparedness household should have either a commercial detector or the materials and
skills to build a KFM.

### 17.4.3 Operational Zones by Dose Rate

| Dose Rate (outdoor) | Situation | Action |
|---|---|---|
| <2 mR/hr (<0.02 mSv/hr) | Near background levels | Brief excursions acceptable |
| 2–10 mR/hr | Low contamination | Limit outdoor time; decontaminate on return |
| 10–100 mR/hr | Moderate contamination | Shelter in place; essential exits only with PPE |
| 100–1,000 mR/hr | High contamination | No exit; full shelter protocol |
| >1,000 mR/hr (1 R/hr) | Extreme contamination | Immediate life threat; total shelter only |

**Dangerous radiation zone** (operational definition for first responders): >10 R/hr
(>0.1 Sv/hr). No entry without full dosimetric monitoring and time limits.

### 17.4.4 Measurement Protocol

1. Take and log outdoor readings at fixed intervals (every 4 hours for the first 48
   hours; every 12 hours thereafter). Note time, location, and reading in a written
   log.
2. Apply the 7:10 rule to project future dose rates and estimate cumulative exposure
   if you must exit.
3. For contamination screening of returning individuals: sweep the GM probe slowly
   (1–2 cm from skin/clothing) head-to-toe. Any reading >3x background warrants
   decontamination before entry.
4. Check food and water containers with probe contact before consuming.

## 17.5 Decontamination

### 17.5.1 Core Principle

Early gross decontamination is far more effective than delayed thorough decontamination.
Getting 90% of contamination removed immediately is better than 100% removed an hour
later. Do not wait for formal decontamination teams if you can self-decontaminate.

Urgent medical treatment for life-threatening injuries always takes priority over
decontamination. Do not withhold care or shelter to an injured person due to
contamination concerns.

### 17.5.2 Personal Decontamination

**Step 1: Remove outer clothing (highest priority)**
Removing and bagging outer clothing eliminates approximately 90% of surface
contamination. Do this outside the shelter or in a vestibule. Place clothing in a
heavy-duty trash bag, seal it, move it away from occupants.

**Step 2: Gross particulate removal**
Brush or shake off visible particles from hair and skin. Do this before water washing
to avoid spreading particles into the water stream over the face.

**Step 3: Wash with soap and water**
Shower thoroughly if water is available. Wash hair with shampoo but do not scrub the
scalp aggressively — vigorous scrubbing can create skin abrasions and drive particles
into pores. Use a gentle lathering motion. If a shower is unavailable, wipe exposed
skin systematically with damp cloths, moving from face downward, using each cloth
section once.

**Step 4: Clean eyes, nose, ears**
Flush eyes with clean water if you believe particles may have contacted them. Blow
nose gently. Wipe ear canals with a damp cloth.

**Step 5: Change to clean clothing** stored inside the shelter.

**Step 6: Verify with detector** before re-entering the clean shelter zone.

### 17.5.3 Shelter Decontamination

- Do not allow outside shoes inside the shelter zone. Establish a shoe-off transition
  area at the shelter entrance.
- Wipe all surfaces that may have been contacted by potentially contaminated persons
  or items with damp cloths. Dispose of cloths in sealed bags.
- If particles have entered the shelter, vacuum (if power is available) rather than
  sweeping, which re-suspends particles in the air. Bag and seal vacuum contents.
- For external shelter surfaces: spray water from a hose (do not use a misting
  nozzle that atomizes water into potentially contaminated spray) to wash particles
  off the exterior and downslope away from shelter entry points.

### 17.5.4 Food and Water Decontamination

**Safe without action**:
- All food stored in sealed containers inside the shelter prior to the event
- Canned goods with intact seals (wipe exterior with damp cloth before opening)
- Factory-sealed bottled water
- Water from a sealed, underground cistern or deep well with a sealed casing

**Requires testing or discard**:
- Garden produce exposed to fallout: wash thoroughly; outer leaves of leafy
  vegetables should be discarded; root vegetables with intact skin may be safe after
  washing.
- Water from surface sources (ponds, streams, open collection tanks): treat as
  contaminated until tested with a detector or until authorities confirm safety.
  Standard water filtration does NOT remove radioactive contamination. Distillation
  removes most (but not all) radionuclides. Ion exchange resins can remove specific
  isotopes (Cs-137, Sr-90) but require proper setup.
- Milk from animals that grazed outdoors in the fallout period: discard until tested.
  I-131 concentrates in milk. If you must consume it, KI provides thyroid protection.

**IAEA/Codex Alimentarius reference levels** (for long-term planning, not acute phase):
The WHO/IAEA reference level for drinking water is 0.1 mSv/year from all radioactive
sources. Codex Alimentarius guideline levels for imported food following a nuclear
emergency are based on 1 mSv/year reference, measured in Bq/kg (becquerels per
kilogram). In the acute phase, follow FEMA/CDC guidance rather than trying to
measure Bq/kg without laboratory equipment.

**Pets and livestock**: Confine animals indoors during fallout period. Brush coats
before allowing re-entry. Do not consume milk or eggs from animals exposed to fallout
until cleared.

## 17.6 Long-Term Storage Under Fallout Conditions

Nuclear preparedness adds specific requirements on top of standard off-grid food and
water storage (see Domain 05).

### 17.6.1 Storage Prioritization

**Unaffected by fallout (no special precautions)**:
- Commercially sealed canned goods, mylar-pouch dry goods, vacuum-sealed containers
- Deep-well or sealed-cistern water with intact casing
- Food stored below grade before the event

**At elevated risk (requires planning)**:
- Open-container water storage: switch to sealed containers before an event if
  warning time exists; otherwise treat per 17.5.4.
- Freeze-dried and dehydrated food in sealed buckets: safe if sealed before fallout.
  Do not open outside the shelter during the acute phase.

### 17.6.2 Minimum Shelter Supply Inventory

FEMA guidance recommends a minimum 14-day self-sufficiency supply. For nuclear
events, 21–30 days is preferable given uncertainty about reentry timelines.

| Category | Quantity per Person | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Water | 1 gallon/day minimum | 2 gallons/day for comfort; heat and medical needs |
| Calories | 2,000 kcal/day | Pre-positioned sealed storage |
| KI tablets | 14+ doses | Per age-appropriate dose table |
| N95/P100 respirators | 4–6 per person | For excursions; does not protect vs. gamma |
| Nitrile gloves | 2 boxes | Contamination handling |
| Tyvek coveralls | 4–6 per person | For decontamination excursions |
| Heavy trash bags (6 mil) | 50 bags | Contaminated clothing, waste |
| Duct tape + plastic sheeting | 4 rolls tape; 20' x 25' plastic | Window/vent sealing |
| Battery-powered NOAA radio | 1 per household | Emergency broadcasts |
| Handheld radiation detector | 1 per household minimum | See Section 17.4 |
| Personal dosimeters | 1 per adult | Track cumulative dose |
| First aid kit (extended) | 1 | Emphasize anti-nausea, antibiotics (RX) |
| Medications (30-day supply) | Per household needs | Critical for ARS supportive care |
| Sanitation supplies | Per Domain 09 | Bucket toilet, lime or kitty litter |
| Lighting (battery/solar) | Multiple sources | Power may be out |

### 17.6.3 Agricultural Recovery Considerations

After the acute fallout phase, off-grid food production requires careful management.

- **Cs-137 in soil**: Binds tightly to clay particles; does not wash out easily.
  High-potassium fertilization (wood ash, potassium sulfate) reduces plant uptake
  by competitive ion exchange. This is the primary mitigation for contaminated
  growing areas.
- **Sr-90 in soil**: More mobile than Cs-137; taken up through plant roots. Liming
  the soil reduces uptake by adjusting pH and competing with calcium channels.
  Strontium mimics calcium in biological systems.
- **Root vegetables**: In moderately contaminated soil, root vegetables accumulate
  less Cs-137 than leafy greens. Washing and peeling removes surface contamination.
- **Greenhouse growing**: Starting seedlings indoors and growing in containers with
  pre-event stored soil eliminates soil contamination concern entirely.
- **Animal products**: Livestock that graze on contaminated pasture will accumulate
  Cs-137 and Sr-90. Feeding stored grain during the acute phase and monitoring
  thereafter is the practical mitigation.

## 17.7 Community Planning and Triage

### 17.7.1 Pre-Event Community Planning

Nuclear preparedness is one area where community coordination provides step-change
value. Individual households cannot survey for fallout, conduct mass decontamination,
or triage casualties alone.

**Assign roles in advance**:
- Radiation Safety Officer (RSO): Has or will obtain a calibrated radiation detector;
  maintains the measurement log; makes shelter entry/exit recommendations.
- Medical lead: Trained in ARS recognition and supportive care protocols (see REMM
  guidelines); maintains medical supply cache.
- Decontamination lead: Manages decontamination station outside shelter entry,
  trains community members on self-decontamination protocol.
- Communications lead: Monitors NOAA radio, coordinates with external emergency
  management per Domain 12.

**Pre-position community shelter supplies**: Centralized shelter with enhanced
basement is more effective than dispersed individual household shelters. Consider
community-scale supply pre-positioning (see cost table in Domain 14).

### 17.7.2 Acute Radiation Syndrome — Recognition and Triage

ARS occurs when a person receives whole-body radiation exposure above approximately
1 Gy (100 rad). In a nuclear detonation, survivors close to the detonation are at risk.
In power plant accidents, ARS risk is effectively zero for the general public.

**ARS dose thresholds and syndromes**:

| Dose | Syndrome | Expected Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| <0.5 Gy | Subclinical | No acute symptoms; possible long-term cancer risk |
| 0.5–2 Gy | Mild hematopoietic | Nausea in many; generally survivable without treatment |
| 2–6 Gy | Moderate to severe hematopoietic | Significant bone marrow suppression; survivable with hospital care; >50% survive without care |
| 6–10 Gy | Severe hematopoietic + GI onset | High mortality; hospital care required for any survival |
| 10–30 Gy | Gastrointestinal syndrome | >95% fatal even with intensive care |
| >30 Gy | Neurovascular syndrome | Nearly universally fatal within days |

**ARS clinical phases** (for community medical lead):
1. **Prodromal (0–48 hours)**: Nausea, vomiting, headache, diarrhea, fatigue. Onset
   timing of vomiting is a dose indicator: vomiting within 30 minutes of exposure
   suggests ≥6 Gy; within 1–2 hours suggests 2–6 Gy; delayed or absent suggests
   <2 Gy.
2. **Latent (2–20 days)**: Apparent improvement. Patient may feel well. Do not
   discharge from medical observation during this phase.
3. **Manifest illness (3 weeks – 3 months)**: Bone marrow failure, infection,
   hemorrhage. This is the killing phase for hematopoietic ARS. Absolute lymphocyte
   count (ALC) at 48 hours predicts severity: ALC >1.5 × 10⁹/L = mild; <0.5 × 10⁹/L
   = severe.
4. **Recovery or death**: Survivors with <6 Gy exposure generally recover in 1–3
   months with adequate supportive care.

**Community-level triage using SALT framework** (adapted from REMM):
- **IMMEDIATE (Red)**: ARS patients with vomiting onset <2 hours post-exposure, still
  ambulatory, no trauma. Aggressive supportive care, antibiotics, IV fluids if
  available.
- **DELAYED (Yellow)**: ARS patients with vomiting onset >2 hours, no significant
  trauma. Monitor in latent phase; prepare for manifest illness.
- **MINIMAL (Green)**: Worried well, no symptoms, low-dose exposure. Reassure, monitor.
- **EXPECTANT (Black)**: Severe trauma combined with high-dose radiation exposure;
  or vomiting within 30 minutes with severe burns/blast injuries. Comfort care only.

**Without hospital resources, focus supportive care on**:
- Preventing and treating infection (highest mortality cause in ARS): strict hygiene,
  any available antibiotics (broad-spectrum), wound care.
- Maintaining hydration: oral rehydration salts; IV if available.
- Anti-nausea medications (ondansetron, promethazine) to allow oral hydration.
- Protecting from secondary trauma and stress: rest, shelter, thermal comfort.

### 17.7.3 Community Decontamination Station

Set up outside the main shelter at a downwind, downslope location:

1. **Undress area**: Screens or partitions; heavy trash bags for contaminated clothing.
2. **Brush/shake station**: Remove gross particulate before water.
3. **Wash station**: Garden sprayers with soap water, or a shower setup. Rinse water
   should drain away from the shelter perimeter.
4. **Dry and redress station**: Pre-positioned clean clothing sets.
5. **Survey station**: GM counter sweep before clearing individuals for shelter entry.

Workers at the decontamination station wear N95/P100 respirators, nitrile gloves,
and Tyvek coveralls. Rotate workers every 30 minutes during active decontamination
operations to limit individual dose.

## 17.8 Recovery Timelines and Rehabitation

### 17.8.1 Short-Term (Days to Weeks)

**48 hours**: Major reduction in dose rate. Dose rate is 1/100th of 1-hour value.
Most contaminated areas outside the immediate detonation zone are approaching
10 mR/hr or below.

**1 week**: Dose rate is 1/1,000th of 1-hour value. Brief outdoor excursions for
water retrieval, assessment, and essential tasks are generally feasible in all but
the most heavily contaminated zones.

**2 weeks**: In areas beyond 10–20 miles downwind from a moderate IND, outdoor
dose rates are often at or near background. Partial rehabitation begins. Continue
monitoring. Do not consume garden produce or surface water without testing.

**Decision trigger for partial rehabitation**: Outdoor dose rate <2 mR/hr sustained
for 24 hours, with no indication of continuing release. Record all dose accumulation
and compare against the EPA Protective Action Guides (PAGs):
- General public early-phase reference level: 10–50 mSv (1–5 rem) projected dose
  over the protective action period.
- Emergency worker reference level: 500 mSv (50 rem) for life-saving actions.

### 17.8.2 Medium-Term (Months to Years)

- **I-131** (half-life 8 days): Essentially gone after 3 months. Thyroid risk from
  this isotope ends. KI is no longer needed.
- **Cs-137** (half-life 30 years): Persists in soil. Monitoring of food grown in
  contaminated areas required for years. Potassium fertilization mitigates plant
  uptake. Soil testing with gamma spectrometer (laboratory or community cooperative)
  provides Bq/kg values for comparison against Codex Alimentarius reference levels.
- **Sr-90** (half-life 29 years): Persists; taken up through plant roots. Liming
  and monitoring required. More mobile in groundwater than Cs-137.

**Rehabitation decision**: In areas with Cs-137 deposition below ~40 kBq/m² (the
IAEA threshold distinguishing restricted from contaminated zones in post-Chernobyl
frameworks), normal agricultural and residential use has been permitted with
monitoring. Areas above 1,480 kBq/m² (the Chernobyl exclusion zone threshold) require
permanent or near-permanent exclusion.

### 17.8.3 Long-Term Community Agricultural Protocol

1. **Year 0–2**: Eat pre-event stored food only. Test all soil and water before
   consuming anything from the land.
2. **Year 2–5**: Greenhouse and container gardening in uncontaminated substrate.
   Gradually expand outdoor growing as soil testing confirms acceptable levels.
3. **Year 5+**: Full monitoring-based agricultural program. Annual soil testing.
   Potassium amendment for Cs-137 mitigation. Liming for Sr-90 mitigation.
   Prioritize growing crops with lower uptake factors (grains over leafy greens).
4. **Continuous**: Maintain relationship with a laboratory for periodic food and
   water testing. Document all results. Share data with neighboring communities.

## 17.9 Quick Reference Card (Print and Laminate)

```
NUCLEAR EVENT — IMMEDIATE ACTIONS

1. GET INSIDE. Go to basement or center of building.
   Best: concrete/brick basement. Better: any basement.
   Worse: ground floor. Worst: outside.

2. SEAL THE BUILDING.
   Close all windows, doors, dampers. Turn off HVAC.
   Use plastic sheeting and duct tape on vents.

3. TAKE KI IF DIRECTED BY OFFICIALS (or if nuclear plant
   accident suspected). See dosing table (Section 17.3.2).

4. REMOVE OUTER CLOTHING if you were outside.
   Bag it. This removes ~90% of contamination.
   Wash with soap and water. Change clothes.

5. STAY INSIDE for at least 24 hours. 
   Longer = better. Two weeks = ideal minimum.

6. MONITOR with detector. Log dose rates every 4 hours.
   Apply 7:10 rule to project fallout decay.

7. DO NOT go outside to rescue others until outdoor dose 
   rate is below 2 mR/hr OR unless life-safety emergency.

8. EAT AND DRINK only pre-sealed indoor supplies.
   Do not consume outdoor water, garden produce, or
   fresh milk until authorized or tested.

9. MONITOR NOAA radio for official guidance.

10. TREAT injured first. Decontaminate second.
    Deny shelter to no one due to contamination.
```
