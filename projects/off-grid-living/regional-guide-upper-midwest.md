# Regional Implementation Guide: Upper Midwest

This guide adapts the comprehensive off-grid-living guide to the Upper Midwest climate and geography — specifically Minnesota, Wisconsin, Upper Michigan, and the northern tier of Iowa. This is the most thermally demanding region for off-grid living in the contiguous United States. Winter lows of -30°F to -40°F in northern Minnesota are routine, frost depths reach 60 inches, and the growing season in the northern tier compresses to 90–110 days. These are real constraints, not discouraging ones: the region also has some of the deepest and most reliable aquifers in the country, abundant timber for heating, productive prairie soils in the south, and wind resources that rival the Great Plains. The challenge is engineering a system that survives winter without grid power; thousands of homesteads already do it successfully.

## Food Production

### Growing Season and Frost Dates

The Upper Midwest is defined by late springs, abbreviated summers, and early falls. Cold air pooling and lake-effect micro-variations are significant — a site on the south-facing slope of a drumlin may have 2–3 more frost-free weeks than a valley bottom a quarter mile away.

| Subregion | Last Spring Frost | First Fall Frost | Frost-Free Days | USDA Zone |
|-----------|-------------------|------------------|-----------------|-----------|
| Southern MN / Southern WI (Madison, Rochester) | Apr 20–May 5 | Oct 1–Oct 15 | 148–175 | 4b–5b |
| Twin Cities / Central WI (Green Bay) | May 1–May 15 | Sep 25–Oct 5 | 135–155 | 4a–4b |
| Northern WI / Iron Range MN | May 15–June 1 | Sep 10–Sep 25 | 105–130 | 3b–4a |
| Boundary Waters / Northern MN | June 1–June 15 | Sep 1–Sep 15 | 85–105 | 3a–3b |

Minneapolis–St. Paul averages 158 frost-free days (last frost May 2, first frost October 7). Duluth averages approximately 120 frost-free days. These figures are starting points; microtopography, proximity to Lake Superior or other large water bodies, and urban heat effects all modulate actual conditions by 1–3 weeks.

### Recommended Crops and Varieties

Short-season variety selection is not optional — it determines success or failure. A variety rated 75 days to maturity cannot produce a ripe crop when first frost arrives at 105 days in a cool year.

**Core annual crops for the northern tier:**
- Tomatoes: 'Sub-Arctic Plenty' (45 days), 'Glacier' (55 days), 'Siletz' (52 days) — all set fruit in cool temperatures as low as 55°F; avoid indeterminate heirlooms in short-season sites
- Corn: 'Seneca Horizon' (65 days), 'Kandy Korn' (89 days, southern zone only), or grow dried 'Bloody Butcher' as a storage crop (requires 90 days)
- Winter squash: 'Bush Delicata' (80 days), 'Sunshine Kabocha' (95 days, southern zone); 'Table Ace' acorn (80 days) is the most reliably productive
- Potatoes: the premier Upper Midwest storage crop; 'Kennebec', 'Yukon Gold' (70–80 days); even in short seasons, yield 1–1.5 lbs per sq ft; store for 6–8 months in a root cellar at 38–40°F
- Dry beans: 'Jacob's Cattle', 'Vermont Cranberry', 'Black Coco' — all 70–85 day dry beans; plant immediately after last frost for a just-in-time harvest
- Brassicas: the most reliable cool-season crops; 'Deadon' cabbage, 'Jade Cross' Brussels sprouts, 'Waltham 29' broccoli — all take light frost and can be left in the field through October

**Hardy perennials with multi-decade returns:**
- Hazelnuts (Corylus americana): native to the region; produces in Zone 3; highly caloric nut crop; 'Weschcke' and 'Lakeland' varieties from the University of Minnesota
- Hardy apple varieties: 'Honeycrisp' (developed by UMN, Zone 4), 'Haralson' (Zone 3), 'Zestar!' (Zone 4); select varieties with demonstrated performance north of the Twin Cities
- Asparagus: 20-year bed; plant once, harvest forever; 'Jersey Knight' is the standard for the region
- Aronia (chokeberry): native; extremely cold-hardy; high-antioxidant berry with long-term caloric and medicinal value
- Strawberries: 'Sparkle' and 'Cavendish' (June-bearing); day-neutral 'Seascape' extends season

**Season extension is not optional at northern sites — it IS the growing system.** Cold frames, low tunnels, and a single unheated hoop house add 4–6 weeks at each end of the season and transform an 105-day site into an effective 130+ day growing environment. University of Minnesota Extension has detailed guidance on extending the growing season that should be considered foundational reading for any Upper Midwest homesteader.

### Seasonal Calendar

| Month | Southern MN/WI | Northern MN/WI |
|-------|---------------|----------------|
| Jan–Feb | Seed catalogs; start onions/leeks indoors (Feb 1) | Seed planning; deep winter; root cellar inventory |
| Mar | Start brassicas, celery indoors; check seed viability | Start onions, brassicas indoors; last month of deep freeze |
| Apr | Harden off brassicas; direct sow peas, spinach outdoors; prep beds | Continue indoor starts; soil still frozen until late April |
| May | Transplant after last frost (May 1–15); plant potatoes, beans | Last frost May 15–June 1; hardened transplants go out late May |
| Jun | Full-speed planting; succession sow greens and beans | All direct sowing; season is short, plant densely |
| Jul | Irrigation management; thin root crops; early potatoes | Peak season; weed pressure intense; squash vines expanding |
| Aug | Harvest tomatoes, beans; begin fall brassica succession | Harvest early crops; first fall frost possible Sept 1 at elevation |
| Sep | Main harvest; dig potatoes; squash; canning/dehydrating peak | Harvest all warm-season crops by Sept 15; rapid succession |
| Oct | Root cellar loading; cover crops; frost-hardy greens still out | First hard freeze; finish root cellar; cover crops seeded by Sept 20 |
| Nov–Dec | Root cellar management; firewood audit; indoor projects | Full winter; check cellar temps; monitor firewood consumption |

### Soil and Drainage Notes

Much of the Upper Midwest was glaciated; soils are often heavy clay-loam with drainage problems in low areas. Raised beds (8–12 inches) dramatically improve drainage and warm the soil faster in spring — the two most common limiting factors for early planting. Southern Wisconsin's driftless area has exceptional soil on gentle limestone terrain; central Minnesota's prairie soils are some of the most fertile in the world (historically). The Iron Range of northern Minnesota has thin, acidic soils over glacial till; lime annually and build topsoil with heavy compost inputs.

## Shelter and Insulation

### Building Materials

The extreme cold of the Upper Midwest demands thermal performance at a different scale than any other region in this guide. The governing objective is minimizing heat loss over a 5–6 month heating season with lows that regularly reach -20°F to -30°F and design temperatures for mechanical systems at -25°F to -35°F.

**Structural approaches:**
- **Double-stud wall construction**: two parallel 2x4 stud walls with a 3.5–5.5 inch gap filled entirely with cellulose or mineral wool; total wall depth 10–12 inches; achieves R-40 to R-50 without thermal bridging; this is the current best practice for cold climate construction in the region
- **SIP (Structural Insulated Panel)**: factory-built panels with EPS foam core; R-27 for 6.5-inch panel, R-39 for 10-inch; fast construction with minimal thermal bridging; panels must be taped and sealed at joints to maintain air barrier continuity
- **Standard 2x6 framing**: code minimum at R-20 cavity plus R-5 continuous exterior insulation; below the performance level recommended for off-grid use (heating costs are the primary recurring expense)
- **Local timber frame with dense-pack insulation**: traditional approach using locally milled lumber; timber frame creates the structure, insulation is applied separately; beautiful and functional if properly detailed

**Material selection:**
- Wood: abundant locally; birch, aspen, oak all viable framing species from local mills; treated lumber for all sill plates and ground contact; cedar for exterior trim
- Concrete and masonry: frost depth of 42–60 inches means foundations require substantial excavation; full basement below frost line is the standard and provides valuable year-round cold storage space at near-constant 40°F

### Insulation Requirements

The Upper Midwest spans IECC Climate Zones 6A and 7. Code minimums and off-grid recommended values:

| Assembly | Zone 6A (southern MN/WI) | Zone 7 (northern MN) | Off-Grid Recommended |
|----------|--------------------------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Attic | R-49 | R-49–R-60 | R-60 to R-80 |
| Wall (framed) | R-20 cavity + R-5 ci | R-21 + R-5 ci | R-40 (double stud) |
| Floor over basement | R-30 | R-30–R-38 | R-38–R-50 |
| Basement walls | R-15 | R-15 | R-20 with continuous ci |
| Slab | R-10 perimeter | R-10–R-15 | R-15–R-20 |

For an off-grid homestead where heating fuel cost equals hours of wood cutting, chainsaw maintenance, and fire-tending time, the economic case for exceeding code insulation is overwhelming. A tight, well-insulated structure with R-60 walls and R-80 attic in Zone 7 may require 2 cords of firewood per winter for a small house; a code-minimum structure in the same location may need 6–8 cords.

### Air Sealing and Ventilation

Air leakage at these temperatures causes immediate moisture problems: cold outside air infiltrating through an unsealed assembly picks up moisture as it warms and deposits it on cold surfaces, leading to mold and rot. A continuous air barrier (Intello Plus or equivalent variable-permeance membrane on interior face; house wrap on exterior) is not a luxury but a system-critical component.

Mechanical ventilation: HRV (heat recovery ventilator) is the correct technology for this climate. At -20°F, the HRV's 80%+ heat recovery efficiency means the cost of mechanical ventilation is minimal. Size at 0.35 ACH per Minnesota code. Operating a tight house without mechanical ventilation in winter results in dangerous CO buildup from wood combustion appliances and moisture accumulation.

### Roof and Foundation

- Snow load design: 40–60 psf ground snow load for most of southern Minnesota and Wisconsin; 70–80 psf for northern Minnesota and Upper Michigan; specify roof framing per local AHJ
- Roof pitch: steep (8:12 minimum) sheds snow load and prevents ice dam formation at eaves; metal roofing eliminates ice dam risk entirely (ice dams cause major water infiltration damage in conventional shingle roofs)
- Foundation: full basement below the 42–60 inch frost depth is standard; a frost-protected shallow foundation (FPSF) uses horizontal insulation to keep the ground from freezing under a shallow footing — the NAHB Research Center has engineering guidance; viable for outbuildings but less common for primary residences in Zone 7
- Crawlspace: do not build a vented crawlspace in Zone 6 or 7; the cold air infiltration and moisture accumulation are catastrophic; use conditioned or semi-conditioned crawlspace with insulated walls

## Water Systems

### Water Source Options

The Upper Midwest sits atop excellent groundwater resources. The Jordan, Prairie du Chien, and other deep sandstone aquifers underlie much of Minnesota and Wisconsin with artesian or near-artesian pressure in many areas. This is one of the best regions in the country for reliable, high-quality well water.

**Drilled wells:**
- Depth: 50–300 ft for most southern and central Minnesota/Wisconsin locations; deeper (200–600 ft) in some northern Minnesota granite areas
- Yield: typically 5–15 gpm for domestic wells; more than adequate for household and garden use
- Quality: generally excellent; iron, manganese, and hardness are the common treatment needs; agricultural areas may have nitrate contamination in shallow (<50 ft) wells
- Cost: $15–35 per foot drilling plus casing and pump; budget $6,000–15,000 for a complete well installation in most Upper Midwest locations
- Well permit: required in both Minnesota (Minnesota Department of Health Well Permit) and Wisconsin (DNR Well Code)

**Rainwater harvesting:**
- Annual precipitation: 26–36 inches across most of the region; adequate for supplemental supply but insufficient as a sole source
- Rainwater harvesting is legal in both Minnesota and Wisconsin; Minnesota has no state-level permit requirement for cisterns; Wisconsin requires compliance with state plumbing code for any indoor use
- Practical limitation: the rain season is June–September; any winter collection infrastructure must be winterized by October 1 and is not available for 6 months; rain harvesting is most practical as a summer irrigation supplement, not a primary domestic source
- Spring snowmelt represents a large pulse of water; collecting via swales and infiltration improves groundwater recharge and reduces spring flooding on the property

**Frost depth and water line design:**
Minnesota frost depth ranges from 42 inches (southern MN/southern WI) to 60 inches (northern MN). All buried water lines must be below frost depth. In Zone 7, this means 5-foot minimum burial for supply lines — a significant excavation cost. Heat tape on any above-grade section is essential. Well casings should be insulated with foam pipe insulation from the pitless adapter to at least 12 inches below grade. Above-grade cisterns must be drained before November 1 and cannot be used through winter without being housed in a heated space.

### Seasonal Protocols

- Spring: commission water system after last freeze; flush lines after winter dormancy; test well water quality annually (bacteria, nitrates, iron)
- Summer: irrigation demand peaks July–August during corn pollination and drought periods; monitor well static level if irrigation is heavy
- Fall: winterize all outdoor water fixtures, disconnect garden hoses, drain outdoor lines by October 1; service pump and pressure tank
- Winter: check pump house temperature monthly; maintain above 40°F in any unheated structure housing water equipment; keep 30-gallon emergency supply inside the heated structure

## Energy Generation

### Solar Potential

The Upper Midwest is a viable but demanding solar zone. The critical insight: winter solar is drastically reduced by the low sun angle (sun elevation at noon in Minneapolis in December is only 22°), while summer solar is excellent (sun elevation 69° at noon in June with up to 16 hours of daylight). This seasonal asymmetry drives system design.

| Location | Avg peak sun hours/day (annual) | Summer (Jun–Aug) | Winter (Dec–Feb) |
|----------|---------------------------------|------------------|-----------------|
| Minneapolis, MN | 4.0–4.5 | 5.5–6.5 | 2.0–2.5 |
| Madison, WI | 4.2–4.8 | 5.5–6.5 | 2.2–2.8 |
| Duluth, MN | 3.8–4.3 | 5.2–6.0 | 2.0–2.5 |
| Green Bay, WI | 4.0–4.5 | 5.5–6.0 | 2.2–2.8 |

The winter solar deficit means that a system sized for summer energy independence will produce 40–50% of its rated output December through February. Design for winter minimum, not summer average. Two practical approaches:

1. **Oversize the array aggressively for the winter load**: a 10 kW array in Minneapolis produces adequate winter power for a modest household; this is expensive but reliable
2. **Hybrid solar/wood**: size solar for spring-through-fall needs (lights, communication, water pump, refrigeration); use a wood gasifier or wood-fired thermoelectric generator to supplement December–February heating-season power

Battery sizing must account for the 2–4 day overcast events that occur frequently in winter. Minimum 5 days autonomy for an Upper Midwest off-grid system. LiFePO4 battery banks should be kept above 32°F (0°C) at all times — cold significantly reduces both capacity and cycle life; house the battery bank in the heated basement or an insulated, internally heated battery enclosure.

Snow: panels pitched at 35–45° typically self-clear; lower-pitched arrays accumulate snow and lose production. Use a panel tilt adjustable mount to steepen angle in winter.

### Wind Potential

The Upper Midwest, particularly southern Minnesota and western Wisconsin, sits on the western edge of the Great Plains wind corridor. Annual average wind speeds at 80m hub height range from 7.0–8.5 m/s (16–19 mph) in southwestern Minnesota to 5.5–7.0 m/s in central Wisconsin — among the best small-wind resources in the eastern US. Wind is the natural complement to solar in this region: winter months when solar is weakest are exactly when cold fronts drive the strongest winds. A combined solar+wind system dramatically reduces battery bank requirements compared to either alone.

Small wind turbines (1–10 kW) are practical at ridge-top and open-field sites in the region. Turbines require approximately 1 acre of clearance from obstructions and a site free from tall trees within 300 feet. Tower height 60–100 ft is standard for residential turbines. In heavily forested northern Minnesota, wind resources at turbine height are poor; solar is the dominant option.

### Backup and Heating Energy

Wood is the quintessential Upper Midwest backup fuel. The region has enormous timber resources — a managed 40-acre woodlot in Wisconsin can produce 40+ cords per year sustainably. Wood heat is the correct primary heating technology for most off-grid Upper Midwest sites; it is also the most economically rational given local availability. Critical specifications:

- **Wood stove**: EPA-certified, catalytic or secondary-combustion; 2021 EPA standards apply; size for the room you want to heat, not the whole house (zone heating is more efficient)
- **Outdoor wood boiler (OWB)**: hydronic heat circulated through the house; allows a larger fire less frequently; reduces creosote vs. small hot fires; EPA Phase 2 certified models are mandatory in many Minnesota counties
- **Propane backup**: 500-gallon minimum; LP freezes below -44°F (rarely reached but note that propane delivery trucks cannot access remote roads after heavy snow); maintain 25% minimum tank level from November through March
- **Firewood quantification**: a small (800 sq ft), well-insulated Zone 6 home burns 2–3 cords per winter; a poorly insulated structure the same size can burn 8–10 cords; insulation investment pays in reduced wood-cutting labor

## Seasonal Maintenance Protocols

**Spring (March–May):**
- Commission water system as frost leaves the ground (typically April 15 – May 15 depending on location)
- Chimney inspection and cleaning after heating season; creosote accumulation from wet or improperly dried wood is the primary chimney fire risk
- Roof inspection after snow load season; check for ice dam damage at eaves and around skylights
- Agricultural equipment maintenance: rototillers, mowers, chainsaws — sharpen, service, and test before planting season
- Mud season access management: rural roads are extremely soft March–April; heavy vehicle access can destroy a driveway permanently; keep a plan for delivery timing and vehicle weight limits
- Plant cold-hardy crops as soon as soil temperature reaches 40°F; peas, spinach, and onions tolerate light snow

**Summer (June–August):**
- Full garden management; irrigation during July–August dry spells
- Firewood cutting and splitting: the correct time is June–August so wood dries for 12–18 months before burning; fresh-cut wood burns at 30–40% of dry wood efficiency and produces 5x the creosote
- Monitor for tornado season (peak June–July in MN/WI); maintain a basement or interior shelter room; NOAA Weather Radio is essential
- Well pump maintenance: check pressure tank pre-charge, inspect foot valve and check valve annually
- Inspect and clean solar panels; trim vegetation growing into wind turbine clearance zone

**Fall (September–November):**
- Harvest all warm-season crops before the first fall frost (often before October 1 in northern locations)
- Root cellar stocking: potatoes, carrots, beets, winter squash, cabbage; target cellar temperature 32–40°F, 90–95% humidity for root crops
- Winterize irrigation and outdoor water lines by October 1 (northern MN) to October 20 (southern WI)
- Stack and inventory firewood; minimum target: 6 cords for Zone 6 well-insulated home, 4 cords for Zone 5; have all firewood stacked and covered before November 1
- Generator test-run; oil change; verify fuel supply; test battery bank charge controller settings for winter charge parameters

**Winter (December–February):**
- Snow removal from solar panels after each accumulation event (a 6-inch snowfall eliminates production entirely)
- Structural snow load monitoring: 40–60 psf design loads; an 8-inch wet snow event can deposit 30–40 psf; inspect ridge beam and main structural members after heavy events
- Keep a clear path to the generator, propane tank shutoff, and battery bank
- 30-day emergency food supply completely independent of road access; blizzard conditions can close rural roads for 3–5 days
- Monitor battery bank state of charge during extended overcast periods; run backup generator before batteries reach 20% SOC (below this, LiFePO4 performance degrades rapidly in cold)
- Pipe heat tape inspection monthly; a failed heat tape on the main supply line is an emergency

## Regional Hazards and Preparedness

### Extreme Cold and Blizzards (Primary Winter Risk)

The Upper Midwest does not have a mild seasonal risk profile — it has a sustained, months-long thermal challenge. The primary off-grid cold risk is not a single catastrophic event but a cumulative failure of heating systems during an extended cold snap. Temperatures of -30°F to -40°F in northern Minnesota with a wind chill of -60°F are not unusual. In these conditions, an unheated structure reaches outdoor ambient temperature in 4–6 hours if heating fails.

**Minimum winter resilience protocol:**
- Primary heat source (wood stove, OWB) with 48-hour fuel supply accessible without going outside
- Backup heat source: catalytic propane heater or secondary wood stove capable of maintaining one room above 50°F
- All critical water supply lines heat-taped with monitored heat trace (not standard heat tape)
- Emergency 30-gallon water supply inside the heated living space
- 30-day food supply at all times November through March
- Communication: satellite communicator (Garmin inReach or equivalent) for rural locations where cellular coverage fails in bad weather

**Blizzard preparedness:**
- Rural driveways in heavy snow areas should have a tractor or skid-steer capable of moving 12–18 inch snowfalls; relying on a pickup truck with a blade is unreliable in sustained blizzard conditions
- Stock 3 days of generator fuel inside the heated space at all times
- Know where your neighbor is; rural winter mutual aid is the most reliable emergency response system in the Upper Midwest

### Tornadoes (Primary Spring/Summer Risk)

Minnesota averages 46 tornadoes per year; Wisconsin averages 23. The primary tornado season is June–July, with a secondary peak in late May. Unlike the classic Tornado Alley profile, Upper Midwest tornadoes frequently occur in forested and hilly terrain where visibility is reduced and warning time is shortened.

**Tornado shelter:**
- The basement is the correct tornado shelter in Upper Midwest frame construction — most homes have them due to the frost depth requirement
- Interior basement room (away from windows and below grade) provides the best protection
- Storm shelter room: FEMA criteria or ICC 500 standard for above-grade safe rooms are applicable if no basement exists
- NOAA Weather Radio with county-specific S.A.M.E. alert is the reliable warning system for rural sites without cell coverage
- The standard warning time for most Minnesota tornadoes is 13 minutes; develop a habit of shelter decision before the storm is visible

### Ice Storms and Power Line Damage

The January thaw followed by re-freeze creates dangerous ice accumulation in the transition zone (southern MN/WI). Ice-loaded trees and branches fall on structures and access roads. Trees within falling distance of the roof or access road are a routine winter hazard; maintain clearance for the most vulnerable trees. Keep a chainsaw accessible (not buried in a shed) for emergency road clearing.

### Flood Risks

Spring snowmelt flooding is common in river valleys throughout the region. The 2019 spring flooding in the Midwest was a multi-billion-dollar event from combined snowmelt and rain-on-saturated-soil conditions. Site structures above the 100-year floodplain; basements in flood-prone areas are particularly vulnerable and can fill rapidly in snowmelt events. FEMA FIRM maps are the baseline; supplement with local knowledge about historical flood extents.

## Regional Resources and Community

### Agricultural Extension Offices
- **University of Minnesota Extension**: extension.umn.edu — the essential resource for Upper Midwest off-grid growers; outstanding publications on season extension, cold-hardy varieties, and cover crops; county offices across the state
- **University of Wisconsin-Madison Extension**: horticulture.wisc.edu — strong programs in organic vegetable production and small-farm economics; county-level contacts for soil testing and variety recommendations
- **Michigan State University Extension** (Upper Michigan): canr.msu.edu/uploads/resources — Upper Michigan-specific publications on short-season farming and northern berry crops

### Regional Seed Companies
- **Fedco Seeds** (Clinton, ME, ships nationally): the premier cold-climate seed company for the Northeast and Upper Midwest; short-season varieties specifically tested for northern growing conditions; cooperative structure; excellent catalog — fedcoseeds.com
- **Prairie Moon Nursery** (Winona, MN): native prairie plants, food-forest species, and native fruit; established for Upper Midwest conditions — prairiemoon.com
- **Seed Savers Exchange** (Decorah, IA): national nonprofit; strong cold-climate variety preservation — seedsavers.org
- **Albert Lea Seed** (Albert Lea, MN): regional cover crop and field crop seed; excellent guidance on cover crop selection for cold climates — alseed.com
- **Shakopee Mdewakanton Sioux Community Seeds**: heritage Native American corn, squash, and bean varieties adapted to upper midwestern conditions

### Building and Technical Resources
- Minnesota State Building Code: dli.mn.gov/business/codes-and-inspections/building-codes — frost depth map and energy code guidance
- Wisconsin Uniform Dwelling Code: legis.wisconsin.gov/code/admin_code/sps — Chapter SPS 322 covers insulation and energy requirements
- Minnesota Department of Health Well Management Program: health.state.mn.us/communities/environment/water/wells — well permits, drilling contractor licensing
- Wisconsin DNR Well Code: dnr.wisconsin.gov/topic/Wells — groundwater quality data and well permit system

### Community Networks
- **Minnesota Farmers Union**: mfu.org — rural political advocacy and community mutual aid networks
- **Land Stewardship Project** (MN): landstewardshipproject.org — family farm support and sustainable agriculture education
- **MOSES Organic Farming Conference** (La Crosse, WI): mosesorganic.org — the largest Midwest organic farming conference; held February annually; excellent networking
- **Upper Midwest Permaculture**: active communities in the Twin Cities and Madison; permies.com forum has active threads on northern permaculture design
- Local co-ops (Seward Co-op in Minneapolis, Willy Street Co-op in Madison) maintain networks connecting off-grid producers to buyers
