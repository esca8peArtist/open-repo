# Domain 11: Shelter & Construction

> Shelter is the second-most urgent human need after water. An inadequate
> shelter — too cold, too wet, structurally unsound, or poorly sited — will
> compromise every other system. Get this right once and it lasts generations.
> Get it wrong and you spend decades compensating for the mistake.
>
> This chapter is organized around decisions you must make in sequence: where
> to build, what to build on, what to build with, and how to make it perform.
> Every section leads with the practical decision, not the theory. Costs are
> 2025–2026 materials pricing for owner-builders unless noted otherwise.
>
> **The golden rule**: A modest, well-insulated, well-sited, well-drained
> structure outperforms a grand building with poor fundamentals every time.
> Drainage first. Air sealing second. Insulation third. Everything else follows.

## Quick-Reference Decision Framework

| Scenario | Recommended system | Foundation | Roofing | Insulation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cold climate (Zone 5–7), $40K–$80K, owner-builder | Platform frame 2×6 | Monolithic slab with foam, or frost-protected shallow | Standing seam metal | Blown cellulose + mineral wool |
| Cold climate, $15K–$35K, experienced builder | Timber frame + straw bale infill | Rubble trench + earthbag stem wall | Metal roofing | Straw bale (R-30+) |
| Mild climate (Zone 3–4), $20K–$50K | Timber frame + SIPs | Pier and beam or stem wall | Metal | SIPs panels |
| Hot humid climate (Zone 1–2), any budget | Elevated platform frame | Pier and beam (ventilation critical) | Metal with vented ridge | Open-cell spray foam air seal + batts |
| Hot dry climate (Southwest), $10K–$30K | Adobe or rammed earth | Rubble trench or earthbag | Flat parapet or shed | Thermal mass replaces insulation |
| CBRN concern, any climate | ICF or poured concrete | Full basement, poured concrete | Metal | Closed-cell spray foam |
| Minimal budget ($5K–$15K), any climate | Earthbag or cob | Earthbag stem wall | Metal shed roof | Earthen walls (thermal mass) |

## 11.1 Site Selection

Site selection is the single decision with the longest tail. A bad site cannot
be fixed after construction begins. Walk the land in every season if possible —
or at minimum in wet season and in winter.

### 11.1.1 Topography and Drainage

**Ideal slope**: 2–5% grade sloping away from the building on all sides.
- Less than 1%: water pools at foundation. Risk of subslab moisture, frost heave, mold.
- Greater than 10%: excavation costs rise sharply. Retaining walls add $5,000–$40,000.
- Flat land in wet climates: requires cut-and-fill, French drains, or raised building pad.

**Drainage assessment checklist:**
1. Walk the site during or immediately after heavy rain. Where does water move?
2. Identify the natural drainage path — do not build across it.
3. Look for hydric soils: gray, blue-gray, or mottled colors indicate seasonal saturation.
4. Check USDA Web Soil Survey (websoilsurvey.sc.egov.usda.gov) — free soil maps to parcel level.
5. Note any seasonal wet areas, springs, or seeps within 200 feet.

**French drain installation**: perforated 4" drain tile, bedded in 3/4" crushed stone,
wrapped in filter fabric, daylighted downslope. Cost: $8–$25/linear foot owner-installed.

**Cut-and-fill grading**: for sloped sites, cut uphill and fill downhill to create a
level pad. Fill must be compacted in 6" lifts. Never build on uncompacted fill — 
settlement will crack foundations and floors.

### 11.1.2 Solar Orientation

For passive solar design and maximum winter sun capture:
- **Primary axis**: building's long dimension runs east-west.
- **Main glazing**: faces within 15° of true south (not magnetic south).
- **Sun angle by latitude** (winter solstice noon angle above horizon):
  - 30°N (Atlanta, Texas): 36.5°
  - 35°N (Tennessee, NC): 31.5°
  - 40°N (Colorado, Pennsylvania): 26.5°
  - 45°N (Oregon, Michigan): 21.5°
  - 50°N (northern border states): 16.5°

**Implication for overhangs**: at 40°N latitude, a south-facing window needs an overhang
depth equal to 40% of the window height to block summer sun while admitting winter sun
(see Section 11.10 for full calculation).

**Shade assessment**: use a solar pathfinder tool or SunEye app. Deciduous trees on the
south are neutral or positive (shade in summer, sun in winter). Evergreens on the south
are always negative. Neighboring buildings or hills that cast winter shadows on the south
wall are disqualifying or severely degrading.

### 11.1.3 Wind Exposure and Windbreaks

**Prevailing wind direction** by region:
- Most of the US: winds predominantly from the west or southwest
- Great Plains: strong southerly component; also north wind in winter
- Pacific Coast: onshore west winds; Chinook/Föhn events notable
- Gulf Coast: persistent southerly flow, tropical systems from SE

**Wind effects on heating load**: a house exposed to 20 mph winds loses heat
2–3× faster than the same house in calm conditions. Every mph of average wind
speed increases heating load by approximately 1–2%.

**Windbreak design**:
- Locate windbreak 2–5× building height upwind (200–500 feet from building)
- Evergreen trees (Norway spruce, white pine, eastern red cedar) in multiple staggered rows
- Effective windbreak reduces wind speed by 75–80% in the protected zone
- 50+ foot tall mature windbreak provides protection over 500–1,000 feet downwind
- Time to effective protection: 7–15 years; plant early

**Building form**: L-shaped or U-shaped buildings around a protected courtyard
dramatically reduce wind exposure. Compact square footprint has less surface area
per volume than long rectangles.

### 11.1.4 Soil Bearing Capacity and Testing

**Why it matters**: a 1,500 sq ft home weighs 100,000–200,000 lbs loaded. Soft soils
will allow uneven settlement, cracking foundations, and stuck doors and windows.

**Soil classification** (bearing capacity):
| Soil type | Bearing capacity | Foundation implication |
|---|---|---|
| Bedrock | 12,000+ psf | Any foundation; minimal footing depth needed |
| Dense sand/gravel | 3,000–4,000 psf | Standard footings adequate |
| Compact sandy loam | 1,500–2,000 psf | Standard footings; test recommended |
| Loose sand | 500–1,000 psf | Spread footings; possible pier alternative |
| Soft clay | 250–500 psf | Engineering required; helical piers often best |
| Peat / fill | <250 psf | Avoid slab/conventional; piers only |

**Simple field tests**:
- **Rod penetration test**: drive a 1/2" steel rod into soil with a 10 lb hammer. If it penetrates
  more than 6" per blow at 3 feet depth, soil is soft and requires engineering attention.
- **Jar test for clay content**: fill a jar with soil + water, shake, let settle 24 hours.
  Sand settles in 2 min, silt in 2 hours, clay remains suspended. More than 30% clay = expansive clay risk.
- **Percolation test** (required for septic, relevant for drainage): dig 12" hole, fill with water.
  Acceptable drainage: less than 1 inch per hour remaining after 24 hours indicates poor drainage site.

**Expansive clay** (common in Texas, Colorado, California valleys): swells 30%+ when wet,
shrinks when dry. Causes dramatic foundation movement. Solutions:
- Pier foundations bearing below the active zone (typically 4–8 feet deep)
- Pre-wetting and compacting the subgrade before slab pour
- Structural slab with grade beams (post-tension engineering required, $15–$25K premium)
- Moisture barrier apron extending 4 feet from foundation on all sides

**Frost depth**: footings must extend below frost depth or frost heave will lift foundations.
| Region | Frost depth |
|---|---|
| Zone 1–2 (FL, Gulf Coast, HI) | 0–6" |
| Zone 3 (Deep South, AZ, NM) | 6–18" |
| Zone 4 (Mid-Atlantic, Pacific NW) | 18–30" |
| Zone 5 (Midwest, Colorado) | 30–48" |
| Zone 6–7 (Upper Midwest, New England, Mountain) | 48–72" |
| Zone 8 (Interior Alaska) | 72–120" |

**Frost-Protected Shallow Foundation (FPSF)**: an ASCE 32 technique using rigid foam
insulation to prevent frost penetration without deep footings. Works well for heated
structures in Zone 5–6 with proper design. Reduces footing depth to 12–16" even in
Zone 5. Material cost premium: $1,500–$3,000 in foam.

### 11.1.5 Access and Site Infrastructure

**Road and driveway**:
- Minimum driveway width for emergency vehicles: 12 feet traveled surface, 14 feet preferred.
- Maximum grade for reliable vehicle access: 10–12%; 8% preferred in ice/snow climates.
- Turning radius for emergency vehicles: 20 feet minimum; fire apparatus needs 40 feet.
- Gravel base: 4–6" crushed stone minimum. Clay base requires geotextile fabric beneath
  gravel to prevent migration. Cost: $3–$8/linear foot for basic gravel driveway.
- Culverts at drainage crossings: 18" diameter minimum, corrugated metal or HDPE.

**Setbacks from road**: typically 25–50 feet from road centerline (varies by jurisdiction).
Verify before siting the building — violations require relocation or variance.

**Emergency access**: most fire codes require a building within 150 feet of a road or fire
apparatus access lane. Rural codes vary widely — check local jurisdiction.

### 11.1.6 Water Table and Basement Feasibility

**High water table** (within 6 feet of surface) makes basements problematic:
- Hydrostatic pressure causes wall and floor cracking and water infiltration
- Requires waterproofing systems ($5,000–$20,000 additional cost)
- Sump pump dependency creates grid-power vulnerability
- In humid climates: subslab drainage + sump + interior drain tile is minimum

**Basement feasibility rule of thumb**:
- Water table seasonally above 4 feet: avoid basement; use slab or pier foundation
- Water table consistently below 8 feet: basement is low-risk with standard waterproofing
- Between 4–8 feet: evaluate drainage carefully; interior drain tile system required

**Identify water table depth**:
- USGS Water Resources site (waterdata.usgs.gov) has local well data
- Dig or auger a test hole 6–8 feet deep in late spring (highest water table)
- Observe neighboring well depths (shallower wells = higher table)

### 11.1.7 Legal, Zoning, and Owner-Builder Status

**Setback requirements** (typical):
- From front property line: 25–50 feet
- From side property lines: 5–15 feet
- From rear property line: 15–25 feet
- From streams/wetlands: 50–300 feet (state-specific riparian buffers)
- From septic system: 10 feet (house) to 100 feet (well)

**Owner-builder exemptions** by state:
Many states allow property owners to build their own primary residence without
a licensed contractor. Key states and rules:

| State | Owner-builder exemption | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Texas | Yes, broadly permissive | Many rural counties have no building codes at all |
| Arizona | Yes | Owner-builder permit available; must not sell within 2 years |
| Tennessee | Yes, with permit | Many rural counties are code-optional |
| Missouri | Yes, rural counties | Many counties have no zoning or building codes |
| West Virginia | Yes | Minimal rural building code enforcement |
| Florida | Yes | Must personally occupy; cannot use for sale within 1 year |
| California | Yes, with permit | Full code compliance required; no shortcuts on inspections |
| New York | Limited | Local jurisdiction controls; NYC and suburbs very restrictive |
| Oregon | Yes, rural | Outside urban growth boundaries, permissive |
| Washington | Yes, rural | Eastern WA counties are very permissive |

**Code-free / permit-free counties**: exist primarily in rural Texas, rural Tennessee,
rural Missouri, rural Kansas, parts of Colorado, and parts of Montana. Verify with
county assessor and building department. Absence of code does not mean absence of
safety requirements — structural failure remains your liability.

**> WARNING**: Building without required permits creates title problems. Banks will not
lend on unpermitted structures. Insurance may be denied. Future buyers may demand
retroactive permits or price reductions. If you ever plan to sell, permit the work.

### 11.1.8 Red Flags: Hazardous Site Conditions

**Wildfire risk** (WUI — Wildland-Urban Interface):
- USFS Fire Hazard Severity Zone maps (calfire.ca.gov for CA; county GIS for others)
- High or Very High zone: metal roof mandatory, 100-foot defensible space, ember-resistant vents
- Insurance costs in WUI zones: $3,000–$8,000/year vs. $800–$1,500 in low-risk areas

**Floodplains**:
- FEMA Flood Map Service Center (msc.fema.gov): free, parcel-level lookup
- Zone AE (100-year floodplain): flood insurance required for mortgaged property; $800–$4,000/yr
- Zone X500 (500-year): low risk, but still worth documenting seasonal high water
- **Never build the primary structure in Zone AE** — even with flood insurance, remediation
  after major floods costs $50,000–$200,000 and may be structurally non-recoverable

**Seismic hazards**:
- USGS Seismic Hazard Maps (earthquake.usgs.gov) — free lookup
- Zone 3+ (Pacific Coast, Intermountain West): seismic bracing, hold-down anchors, and
  shear wall design required; adds $3,000–$15,000 to a typical residence
- Fault line proximity: California's Alquist-Priolo Act prohibits habitable structures
  within 50 feet of active faults; other states have no equivalent law but same physics apply

**Radon**:
- EPA Zone 1 areas (IA, MN, OH, PA, WY, CO, MT, ND, SD, MN, WI): test before building
- Passive sub-slab depressurization during construction costs ~$500 and prevents the
  need for active systems ($800–$2,500) later
- Test: EPA-approved short-term kit (~$15) placed in basement/lowest level for 48–96 hours

## 11.2 Foundation Types

### 11.2.1 Concrete Slab — Monolithic and Stem Wall

**Monolithic slab**: poured in a single continuous pour. Thickened edges
(12–18" deep × 12–18" wide) replace footings. Suitable for warm climates with
stable soils and minimal frost risk.

**Construction sequence**:
1. Excavate and grade 4–6" below finish grade
2. Compact subbase; install 4" gravel bed
3. Place rigid foam insulation (2" min, 4" preferred at slab edges — FPSF)
4. Install 6-mil poly vapor barrier
5. Place rebar grid: #4 rebar at 18" OC each direction, or WWF 6×6-W1.4
6. Place radiant heat tubing if desired (PEX, 9–12" centers)
7. Pour 4" slab (5" under load-bearing walls, 6" in garage/workshop)
8. Finish: broom, trowel, or stamp

**Cost**: $4–$8/sq ft owner-managed, $8–$15/sq ft contractor-poured (materials only
for a basic owner-managed pour: ~$3–$5/sq ft). 1,200 sq ft slab: approximately
$5,000–$10,000 materials.

**Stem wall slab**: continuous footing below frost depth supports a stem wall (8–12"
block or poured concrete) up to grade, with slab poured inside. Required in frost zones.
More expensive than monolithic but provides solid frost protection.

**Radiant heat integration**: PEX tubing embedded in slab connected to a hydronic
system (propane, wood boiler, or solar thermal) is the most comfortable and efficient
heating option for slabs. Adds $2,000–$5,000 to slab cost for tubing and manifold.
Pairs ideally with thermal mass floor exposed to passive solar gain.

**Frost heave risk**: monolithic slab without foam skirt will heave in Zone 5+. FPSF
design (2–4" vertical foam at edge + 2–4" horizontal foam skirt extending 2–4 feet
horizontally) allows shallow footings even in Zone 6.

### 11.2.2 Pier and Beam

Posts (wood, steel, or concrete) bear on individual footings or pads. Floor
framing is elevated above grade.

**Advantages**:
- Allows access below floor for plumbing, wiring, and inspection
- Adapts to sloped sites without major excavation ($5,000–$20,000 savings vs. slab on slope)
- Adjustable: sagging floors can often be shimmed and corrected without major work
- Better for high water table situations

**Disadvantages**:
- Cold floors if not insulated properly (R-25+ under floor in Zone 5)
- Moisture management critical: vapor barrier on ground (6-mil poly), good ventilation,
  or encapsulated crawlspace required
- Susceptible to termites in southeastern US (treat posts, use pressure-treated lumber,
  maintain clearance from wood to soil: 18" minimum)

**Footing sizing** (rule of thumb):
- 8" diameter concrete footing supports 8,000 lbs (adequate for single-story, 8-foot tributary)
- 12" diameter: 18,000 lbs (two-story, heavy roof loads)
- Depth: 6" below frost depth minimum, or bearing on firm soil

**Wood post sizing**:
- 6×6 post at 8-foot height: handles 10,000+ lbs
- 4×4 post: 4,000 lbs max; marginal for most applications; use 6×6

**Adjustable steel posts** (Lally columns): telescoping steel post with screw
adjustment. Excellent for retrofitting sagging floors. $30–$60 each.

**Cost**: $3–$6/sq ft for basic pier and beam; $5–$10/sq ft with encapsulated
crawlspace and proper insulation.

### 11.2.3 Rubble Trench / Gravel Foundation

Attributed to Frank Lloyd Wright's Usonian houses and Rodale Press publications.
A perforated drain tile set in a trench of compacted rubble (crushed stone or
mixed rubble) provides drainage and frost protection without deep concrete footings.

**How it works**: water that would otherwise freeze and heave is continuously
drained away before it can freeze. Works only where drainage outlet is available.

**Construction**:
1. Trench: 24–36" deep, 18–24" wide, sloped to outlet
2. 4" perforated drain tile at bottom, sloped minimum 1/8"/foot to daylight
3. Fill trench with clean 3/4" crushed stone, compacted in layers
4. Grade beam (poured concrete or concrete blocks) on top of rubble, bearing above grade
5. Frost must not penetrate below rubble depth — works in Zones 3–5 with good drainage;
   marginal in Zone 6+ unless trench is very deep

**Climate constraints**:
- Works reliably: Zones 3–5 with good soil drainage
- Marginal: Zone 6 (use 36" depth minimum)
- Not recommended: Zone 7+ or sites with poor drainage (clay soils)

**Cost savings**: eliminates need for formed and poured footings. Saves
$1,500–$5,000 vs. conventional footing and foundation wall on a typical home.

**> WARNING**: Rubble trench must drain freely or it becomes a subsurface
water retention system — the opposite of its intent. Outlet must daylight
to lower grade; never discharge into a dry well or septic field.

### 11.2.4 Earthbag Foundation

Polypropylene bags filled with moist subsoil, compacted in courses with barbed wire
between courses for tensile connection. Commonly used as the stem wall and lower
courses of earthen buildings where concrete is expensive.

**Construction sequence**:
1. Trench to undisturbed soil (below frost depth in cold climates)
2. Gravel fill for drainage (same as rubble trench)
3. First course: bags filled with gravel or stabilized soil, barbed wire on top
4. Subsequent courses: fill with moist inorganic subsoil (remove organic topsoil)
5. Tamp each course with a manual tamper after placing
6. Barbed wire (4-point, 17 gauge) between every course for shear connection
7. Plaster exterior when complete for moisture protection

**Material cost**: 50-lb polypropylene bags: $0.30–$0.60 each. A 4-foot stem wall
around a 1,000 sq ft building requires approximately 500–800 bags. Total material
cost for foundation stem wall: $200–$800.

**> WARNING**: Earthbag foundations require drainage as critically as any earthen system.
They wick moisture rapidly without protection. Exterior plaster and roof overhang are
non-negotiable.

### 11.2.5 Helical Pier / Screw Pile

Steel pipe piers with helical plates screwed into the ground with hydraulic
torque equipment. Can be installed in difficult conditions inaccessible to
conventional equipment.

**Best applications**:
- Unstable soils (soft clay, loose fill, expansive soils)
- Steep sites where excavation is impractical
- High water table
- Retrofit foundations under existing structures
- Fast installation: a typical residential installation takes 1–2 days

**Limitations**:
- Requires professional installation with specialized equipment
- Cannot be DIY-installed
- Cost: $200–$600 per pier installed; a typical home requires 12–30 piers
- Total installed cost: $5,000–$15,000 for typical residential application

### 11.2.6 Foundation Decision Matrix

| Climate zone | Soil type | Lot | Budget | Recommended foundation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Zone 1–3 | Sand/gravel | Flat | Low | Monolithic slab |
| Zone 1–3 | Clay | Flat | Medium | Stem wall slab with moisture control |
| Zone 4–5 | Any | Flat | Low | FPSF monolithic slab with foam skirt |
| Zone 4–5 | Good drainage | Sloped | Low–medium | Rubble trench + grade beam |
| Zone 5–7 | Any | Flat | Medium | Stem wall slab or full basement |
| Zone 5–7 | Any | Sloped | Medium | Pier and beam |
| Any | Soft/unstable | Any | Medium–high | Helical piers |
| Earthen building | Any | Any | Very low | Earthbag stem wall + gravel trench |
| CBRN concern | Any | Flat | High | Full poured concrete basement |

## 11.3 Conventional Framing (Stick Frame / Platform Frame)

Platform framing is the most permit-compatible, most widely understood, and
easiest to self-build method in North America. Materials are available everywhere.
Skills are teachable in weeks.

### 11.3.1 Framing Sequence

1. **Sill plate**: 2×6 or 2×8 pressure-treated lumber, anchored to foundation with
   J-bolts or post-installed anchors at 48" maximum spacing. Seal with sill gasket
   (foam tape or closed-cell sill sealer) to eliminate air infiltration at foundation.

2. **Floor framing** (for pier/beam or basement):
   - Rim joist (band joist): 2× stock same depth as floor joists, on all sides
   - Floor joists: typically 2×10 at 16" OC for 12-foot spans; 2×12 for 14–16-foot spans
   - Blocking at midspan over 8 feet for torsional stability
   - Subfloor: 3/4" tongue-and-groove OSB or plywood, glued and nailed

3. **Wall framing**:
   - Bottom plate: 2× lumber (pressure-treated if near concrete)
   - Top plate: double 2× (single plate possible with advanced framing)
   - Studs: 2×6 at 16" OC (standard); 2×6 at 24" OC (advanced framing, better R-value)
   - King studs and jack studs at all openings
   - Headers over all openings (see header table below)
   - Corner assemblies: California corner or advanced framing corner (less wood, better insulation)

4. **Second floor / ceiling**: same as floor framing; lay out for ceiling joists or
   rafter plates before sheathing.

5. **Roof framing**: see Section 11.3.3

6. **Sheathing**:
   - Walls: 7/16" OSB or 1/2" plywood on exterior, nailed per code schedule (typically 6" edge/12" field)
   - Roof: 7/16" OSB or 1/2" plywood; 5/8" for 24" rafter spacing with heavy snow loads

### 11.3.2 Wall Assembly: 2×6 Advanced Framing

Standard 2×4 construction provides only R-13 cavity (fiberglass). 2×6 walls at 24" OC
(advanced framing / optimum value engineering) provide:
- R-21 cavity depth
- 33% fewer studs = less thermal bridging
- 24" OC layout aligns with panel products for waste reduction
- Structural performance equal to 2×4 at 16" OC for single-story and most two-story applications

**Thermal bridging**: wood studs conduct heat 5× better than insulation. In a standard
2×4 wall, studs occupy 20% of wall area and reduce effective R-value by 20–30%. Exterior
continuous rigid foam (1.5" polyiso = R-10) eliminates most thermal bridging and adds
dramatically to effective wall performance.

**Example high-performance wall assembly** (cold climate):
- Interior: drywall or plaster
- 6-mil poly vapor barrier (cold climates only; omit in mixed/humid climates)
- 2×6 advanced frame at 24" OC
- Mineral wool batts (Rockwool Comfortbatt): R-23
- 1.5" polyiso rigid foam exterior: R-10
- Housewrap (Tyvek or Zip System sheathing)
- 1×4 rain screen strapping
- Fiber cement or wood cladding
- **Total effective R-value: approximately R-28–R-32**

### 11.3.3 Header Sizing

Every window and door opening requires a header to carry the load above.
Undersized headers will sag, causing binding doors and windows.

| Opening width | Single story | Two story |
|---|---|---|
| Up to 3'-6" | 2× 2×6 | 2× 2×8 |
| 3'-6" to 5'-0" | 2× 2×8 | 2× 2×10 |
| 5'-0" to 7'-0" | 2× 2×10 | 2× 2×12 |
| 7'-0" to 9'-0" | 2× 2×12 | Engineered LVL required |
| 9'-0" to 12'-0" | Engineered LVL 3.5×11.25 | Engineered beam + post |

Use LVL (laminated veneer lumber) or PSL (parallel strand lumber) for spans exceeding 8 feet.
LVL: approximately $3–$6/linear foot for 3.5×11.25"; PSL: slightly stronger and more expensive.

### 11.3.4 Roof Framing: Rafters vs. Trusses

**Stick-framed rafters**:
- Cut and installed one at a time on-site
- Require a ridge board (non-structural, rafter-supported) or ridge beam (structural LVL/steel)
- Creates usable attic space
- More labor-intensive: 2–3× the cutting and nailing time of trusses
- Cost advantage: lumber is cheap; labor is the cost
- Span limits: 2×10 at 16" OC spans to approximately 15 feet; 2×12 to 19 feet

**Ridge beam vs. ridge board**:
- Ridge board: rafters on both sides push against each other; no structural role; requires
  ceiling joists or rafter ties to resist outward thrust.
- Ridge beam: carries vertical load; requires posts or walls at each end; eliminates
  outward thrust; allows cathedral ceilings. Must be sized as a beam — typically
  LVL 3.5×11.25" to 5.5×16" depending on span and snow load.

**Engineered trusses**:
- Fabricated off-site; delivered and set by crane or by hand for smaller sizes
- No engineered ridge required; each truss is self-spanning
- Cannot modify after installation without re-engineering
- Typical cost: $5–$15 per linear foot of roof (shop-fabricated)
- Faster installation: a crew can set an entire roof in 1–2 days
- No usable attic space (webbing fills the space)
- Best for simple rectangular buildings in code-compliant construction

**Snow load design**: every roof structure must be designed for local ground snow load.
- ASCE 7 ground snow load maps are the standard reference
- Ground snow load × 0.7 = typical roof design snow load (sloped roof conversion factor)
- Example: Denver CO ground snow load = 30 psf; roof design = 21 psf + 10 psf dead load
- Metal roofs with 4:12+ slope shed snow naturally; low-pitch roofs accumulate

**Roof pitch recommendations**:
- Snow country (Zone 5+): minimum 6:12 pitch; 8:12 or greater preferred for natural shedding
- Rain-heavy climates: 4:12 minimum to drain quickly
- Hot dry climates: 2:12 or even flat roofs are traditional (parapet + scupper drainage)
- Living roofs: 1:12 to 3:12 (flat to low slope)

### 11.3.5 OSB vs. Plywood

| Property | OSB | Plywood |
|---|---|---|
| Cost | $25–$35 per 4×8 sheet (7/16") | $40–$60 per 4×8 sheet (1/2") |
| Strength | Slightly less racking resistance | Slightly higher racking resistance |
| Moisture response | Swells at edges when wet; slow to dry | Delaminates with repeated wetting/drying |
| Recovery | Swollen edges may telegraph through cladding | Delaminated panels must be replaced |
| Nail holding | Slightly less in wet conditions | Slightly better |
| Best use | Protected from weather promptly | High-moisture or coastal applications |

**Practical recommendation**: OSB is adequate for most applications if protected from
extended rain exposure during construction. In coastal or very wet climates, plywood
or Zip System sheathing (OSB with integral WRB) is worth the premium.

**Zip System sheathing**: OSB panels with factory-applied weather-resistant barrier on face.
Tape seams with Zip tape = structural sheathing + air barrier + WRB in one step.
Premium: $45–$65 per sheet. Eliminates housewrap labor and improves air sealing significantly.

## 11.4 Timber Frame and Post-and-Beam

Timber frame construction uses large-section lumber (typically 6×6 minimum,
often 8×8 to 12×12) connected with traditional joinery. It is the most durable
and aesthetically timeless building method for owner-builders with patience.

### 11.4.1 Traditional Joinery

**Mortise and tenon**: the workhorse connection. A rectangular tenon is cut on
the end of one timber; a matching mortise is cut into the receiving timber.
Secured with hardwood pegs (treenails/trunnels) driven through the joint.

**Key measurements**:
- Tenon width: 1/3 of timber width
- Tenon depth: 1.5–2" for 6×6; 2.5–3" for 8×8
- Mortise depth: 1" deeper than tenon length
- Peg diameter: 1" for 6×6 timbers; 1.25–1.5" for 8×8+
- Peg species: black locust, osage orange, or white oak (must be harder and drier than the timber)

**Scarf joint**: for splicing timbers end-to-end. Bridle scarf or bladed scarf
joints are strongest. Used where single timber lengths are inadequate.

**Knee brace**: diagonal brace from post to beam at 45°, preventing racking.
Typically 4×4 or 6×6, minimum 24" cut length on each end. Critical in timber frames
that do not have infill sheathing for lateral bracing.

**Timber frame tools**:
- 1.5–2" timber frame chisel (slick): $80–$200
- Timber framing mallet: $30–$80
- Chain mortiser or dedicated mortising machine (rent or buy used): $200–$500/day
- Circular saw or timber framing saw (wide-cut, e.g., Alaskan saw): $400–$1,500
- Drawknife for green timber work: $40–$120
- Timber layout tools: framing square, marking gauge, layout pencils

### 11.4.2 Bent Assembly and Raising

**Bent**: a structural frame consisting of two vertical posts and a horizontal
tie beam, often with rafters and knee braces included. Multiple bents connected
by horizontal purlins and plates form the complete frame.

**Assembly sequence**:
1. Cut all timbers; test-fit joints on the ground (dry assembly)
2. Assemble each bent flat on the ground or on a low platform
3. Raise bents one by one using gin poles, come-alongs, or rental crane
4. Plumb, brace temporarily with diagonal 2× lumber
5. Install connecting girts, plates, and purlins
6. Drive all pegs

**Raising crew**: a typical 1,000 sq ft timber frame requires 4–8 people for
the raising day. The actual raise typically takes 1–3 days if joints are well-cut.

**DIY feasibility**: high, with adequate preparation. Tedd Benson's books are
the standard reference. The critical path is accurate joinery — practice on
small outbuildings before the main frame.

### 11.4.3 Timber Sizing Rules

**Span tables**: AFPA National Design Specification (NDS) or TimberTools software.
General rules of thumb:

| Application | Minimum timber size |
|---|---|
| Post, 8–10 foot height, single story | 6×6 |
| Post, 10–14 foot height, or two story | 8×8 |
| Floor beam, 12-foot span | 6×10 or 8×10 |
| Floor beam, 16-foot span | 6×12 or 8×12 |
| Roof rafter/purlin, 12-foot span | 4×8 or 6×8 |
| Roof ridge beam, 20-foot clear span | 8×12 or 10×12 |

**L/360 deflection rule**: the allowable deflection of a beam under design load
is span length divided by 360. For a 16-foot span: max deflection = 192/360 = 0.53".
More permissive for roofs (L/240); stricter for tile floors (L/480).

**Safety factor in traditional timber frames**: traditional frames were typically
designed to 4:1 safety factor (4× ultimate load before failure). Modern engineering
uses 2:1–3:1. Over-sizing is the traditional approach; do not reduce unless you
have an engineer stamp the design.

### 11.4.4 Timber Species Comparison

| Species | Bending strength (Fb) | Workability | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Douglas fir | 1,500–2,100 psi | Good | Most available; standard for structural applications in West |
| White oak | 1,250–1,800 psi | Moderate (hard) | Very durable; traditional for New England frames; hard to work green |
| Eastern white pine | 900–1,200 psi | Excellent | Soft, easy to work; traditional in New England; less strong |
| Hemlock (eastern) | 1,000–1,400 psi | Good | Common in Northeast; resists checking less well than others |
| Southern yellow pine | 1,500–2,200 psi | Moderate | Very strong; heavy; durable; widely available in South |
| Black locust | 2,000+ psi | Difficult | Extremely rot-resistant; excellent for sills and posts in ground contact |

**Green vs. seasoned**:
- Green timber: freshly cut, high moisture content (>19%). Easier to work (cut and shape);
  strong; will check (crack along grain) as it dries — cosmetic, not structural.
- Seasoned timber: air-dried 1+ year; stable dimensionally; harder to work; joints
  tighter long-term. Air drying rate: approximately 1" of thickness per year.
- Recommendation for DIY: use green Douglas fir or SYP for the frame; joints cut slightly
  oversized to allow for movement; peg after a season of assembly.

## 11.5 Earthen Building

Earthen buildings are among the oldest on earth. They are cheap in materials,
labor-intensive to build, excellent thermal mass performers in semi-arid climates,
and fragile in wet or freeze-thaw climates without careful design.

**The boots and a hat principle**: every earthen building needs a high foundation
(boots — keeps the wall above splash zone and ground moisture) and a wide roof
overhang (hat — sheds rain away from the wall face). Without both, earthen walls
erode within a decade in rainy climates.

### 11.5.1 Cob Construction

**What is cob**: a hand-formed mixture of subsoil, sand, and straw worked into
a monolithic structural wall. No forms, no blocks. Walls are sculpted in place.

**Mix ratios** (adjust based on local soil):
- Subsoil with 20–30% clay content: 1 part subsoil
- Sharp sand (washed, not beach sand): 1.5–2 parts sand
- Straw (long-fiber, undamaged): 1–2 handfuls per gallon of mix
- Water: minimum to achieve plastic consistency (rolls into ball without cracking)

**Simple soil test (thumb test)**: take a handful of moist subsoil. Form a cylinder
2" in diameter and 2" long. Let it air dry on a flat surface for 2 days. Press your thumb
firmly into the dried cylinder. If it does not compress: good clay content for cob.
If it crumbles easily: too sandy, needs more clay. If it cracks deeply: too much clay, add sand.

**Construction sequence**:
1. Foundation stem wall (concrete, stone, or earthbag) at least 12–18" above grade
2. Mix cob by foot on a tarp or in a pit; let rest (slake) for 1 hour if using dry clay
3. Form coblets (fist-sized balls), place on wall, compress and blend into course
4. Build in 12–18" lifts; allow each lift to firm up 24–48 hours before adding more
5. Keep wall plumb with a 4-foot level; cob has a natural tendency to lean outward
6. Set window and door bucks (wood frames) in place as you build
7. Plaster exterior with clay-lime or lime plaster after building has cured (minimum 3–6 months)

**Wall width rules**: minimum 12" for single-story, 16–18" typical, 24" for taller buildings.
Wider walls = more thermal mass and greater structural depth.

**Plaster systems for cob**:
- Clay slip + clay plaster (earthen): breathable, easy to repair, must be maintained every 5–10 years
- Clay-lime plaster: more durable, still breathable, better moisture resistance
- Lime plaster (NHL or Type S lime): most durable, water-resistant, allows minor vapor movement
- Never use Portland cement on cob: prevents vapor movement, causes delamination and wall damage

**Moisture management**:
- Cob must never stay wet. The interior of a wet cob wall loses 40–60% of its compressive strength.
- Test moisture before plastering: insert a 16d nail at 45°; if you can pull it back out easily, wait.
- Roof overhangs: minimum 24" on all sides; 36" in rainy climates
- Foundation: minimum 18" above finished grade

**Climate limitations**:
- Excellent: semi-arid (Southwest US, Mediterranean, parts of Pacific NW in rain shadow)
- Marginal: humid climates with proper roof and maintenance regimen
- Not recommended: freeze-thaw climates without exterior lime render (wet cob + freeze = spalling)

### 11.5.2 Adobe Block Construction

**Adobe blocks** are molded and air-dried rather than hand-formed. They are
stackable, dimensionally consistent, and easier to build plumb walls with than cob.

**Block production**:
- Forms: typically 10×14×4" or 10×14×4" (varies by region; historic New Mexico adobe
  was often larger at 10×14×4")
- Fill mix: clay soil + fine sand + straw (same ratios as cob, slightly wetter)
- Dry time: 2–3 days per face, then flip; 2–4 weeks before use in wall
- Straw content: 2 lbs per cubic foot of mix (bundles of straw torn to 4–6" length)
- Production rate: 100–150 blocks/day by an experienced team of 2–3

**Mortar**: same mix as blocks (clay-sand, no straw), wet consistency.
Bed joints: 1/2–3/4" thick. Full coverage of block face required.

**Seismic concerns**: adobe is historically vulnerable to seismic damage.
Standard improvements:
- Bond beam at top of wall: concrete or wooden bond beam, full wall width, reinforced
- Vertical rebar at corners and openings: 1/2" rebar in grouted cores
- Tie system: plywood bond beam or continuous timber plate
- In high seismic zones (California, New Mexico), full engineered seismic retrofit
  is required to meet modern codes; adds $5,000–$15,000 to typical home

### 11.5.3 Rammed Earth

Dense, mechanically compacted earth (stabilized or unstabilized) in formwork.
Produces walls of exceptional density and compressive strength — 300–1,200 psi
depending on soil and stabilizer.

**PISE (Pneumatically Impacted Stabilized Earth)**: a variant where a slurry of
soil and Portland cement stabilizer is pneumatically sprayed into formwork rather
than manually tamped. Faster; requires professional equipment.

**Formwork**: typically plywood or steel form panels, similar to concrete formwork,
but taller and less precise. Reusable forms are essential for cost efficiency.

**Stabilized vs. unstabilized**:
- Unstabilized: purely compacted earth. Lower strength (400–600 psi). Requires
  excellent roof protection. Less moisture-resistant.
- Stabilized (3–8% Portland cement): higher strength (800–1,200 psi); more moisture-
  resistant; easier permitting; more energy-intensive to produce.

**Frost limits**: rammed earth in freeze-thaw climates requires exterior insulation
(typically EPS rigid foam) applied during formwork. Uninsulated rammed earth in Zone 5+
will spall and erode at the surface over time.

**Compressive strength of typical rammed earth**: 300–1,200 psi (vs. concrete at 3,000–5,000 psi).
Adequate for single-story to moderate two-story with proper design.

### 11.5.4 Earthbag Construction (Full Wall System)

Used as both foundation (Section 11.2.4) and complete wall system.

**Tube vs. individual bags**:
- Polypropylene tubes (100-foot rolls): faster; fewer seams; more continuous wall.
  Cut to length as you go. $30–$60 per 100-foot roll.
- Individual 50-lb bags: easier to fill and move; more seams. $0.30–$0.60 each.
- For walls: tubes are generally preferred for continuous coursing.

**Fill materials**:
- Inorganic subsoil (clay-sand mix): standard; available on-site
- Stabilized soil (2–5% Portland): better moisture resistance for lower courses
- Gravel: for foundation courses and drainage-critical locations
- Never: organic soil, topsoil, or soil with more than 30% organic matter

**Barbed wire**: 4-point, 17-gauge galvanized. 2 strands per course. Creates shear
connection between courses. Non-negotiable structural element — omitting it produces
an unstable stack, not a wall. Cost: $0.05–$0.08 per linear foot of wall perimeter.

**Wall stability**: Earthbag walls are inherently stable (mass structure) and resist
compression well. Lateral forces (seismic, wind) are a concern. Buttresses, corners,
and rounded or domed plans are more stable than long flat runs.

**Plaster**: required on all earthbag walls. Exterior: 2-coat lime stucco or
earthen plaster with lime finish coat. Interior: clay plaster. Minimum 3/4" total thickness.

### 11.5.5 Earthen Building Cost Comparison

| System | Materials cost/sq ft | Skill requirement | Climate suitability |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cob | $10–$25 | Moderate (teachable) | Semi-arid to mild |
| Adobe | $12–$30 | Moderate (block production) | Dry to semi-arid |
| Rammed earth (unstabilized) | $15–$35 | High (formwork) | Dry to semi-arid |
| Rammed earth (stabilized) | $25–$50 | High | Wide (with EPS exterior) |
| Earthbag | $15–$35 | Low–moderate | Wide (with proper drainage) |
| Stick frame (for comparison) | $80–$130 | Moderate | Wide |

**All earthen systems**: add $10–$25/sq ft for plaster systems and $5–$10/sq ft
for roof and foundation upgrade relative to stick frame.

## 11.6 Straw Bale Construction

Straw bale construction produces among the highest insulation values of any
wall system, with good fire resistance when plastered, and low material cost
where straw is available. It is an excellent choice for cold, semi-arid, and
temperate climates.

### 11.6.1 Load-Bearing vs. Post-and-Beam Infill

**Load-bearing (Nebraska style)**:
- The bale wall itself carries roof loads
- Bales stacked running bond (like bricks), with rebar pinning through courses
- Top plate (double 2× frame) distributes roof load across wall
- Simpler construction; no separate frame needed
- Limited to single-story, low-pitch roofs
- No openings wider than 6 feet without special header design

**Post-and-beam infill (hybrid)**:
- Timber, steel, or engineered post-and-beam carries all loads
- Bales fill between structural posts; bales carry no structural load
- Allows taller walls, larger openings, two-story design
- More expensive (requires structural frame)
- More flexible architecturally
- Easier permitting in many jurisdictions

**Recommendation**: post-and-beam infill for most applications, especially two-story
and large opening scenarios. Load-bearing for small single-story structures in relaxed
permit environments.

### 11.6.2 Bale Standards

**2-string bales**: smaller cross-section (14"×18" typical). Lower R-value (~R-26 on flat).
Easier to handle; one person can lift and place. More commonly available.

**3-string bales**: larger cross-section (16"×23" typical). Higher R-value (~R-30 on flat).
Heavier (80–100 lbs). More structural depth. Required in load-bearing applications.

**Density requirements**: minimum 7 lbs/cubic foot for load-bearing applications.
Test: drop bale from waist height — it should bounce, not splat. You should not be
able to pull straw out with your fingers easily.

**Moisture content**: maximum 20% moisture content at installation. Test with a
moisture meter (pin type, $15–$50). Installing wet bales is the most common cause of
straw bale wall failure.

**Bale cost**: $3–$8 per bale depending on region and season. A 1,000 sq ft home
with 9-foot walls on 3 sides requires approximately 200–300 bales.

### 11.6.3 Raising Sequence

1. Frame openings with rough bucks (pressure-treated 2×6 or 2×8 frames) before baling
2. Lay bales on foundation stem wall; check level and plumb
3. Running bond: stagger bale joints by half a bale length
4. Drive 3/8" or 1/2" rebar pins vertically through courses at 24–36" spacing
5. Notch bales around electrical boxes and conduit (sawzall or chainsaw for trimming)
6. Install all-thread rods from top plate through all courses to foundation at corners
   and at maximum 48" intervals; tighten for compression
7. Install window and door frames as you reach each opening
8. Apply clay slip coat (thin clay-water slurry) to all bale faces before plastering
9. Apply first plaster coat within 30 days of raising; do not leave bales exposed

### 11.6.4 Plaster Systems

| Plaster type | Pros | Cons | Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| Clay plaster | Breathable, cheap, DIY-friendly, repairable | Soft, must be sheltered, limited moisture resistance | $0.50–$1.50/sq ft materials |
| Lime plaster (Type S or NHL) | Durable, vapor-open, traditional, beautiful | Slower work (cure between coats), alkaline (wear gloves) | $1.50–$3.00/sq ft materials |
| Lime-cement (stucco) | Tough, fast, widely available | Less breathable than pure lime; can trap moisture | $1.00–$2.00/sq ft materials |
| Earthen-lime combination | Best of both: vapor-open + durable | Requires skill to balance mix | $1.00–$2.50/sq ft materials |

**Never use** Portland-dominated (90%+ cement) stucco on straw bale. It prevents
moisture vapor movement, trapping moisture in the wall and rotting bales.

**Minimum coat schedule**:
- Scratch coat (keyed into bale): 3/4"–1"
- Brown coat (smooth, float finish): 1/2"–3/4"
- Finish coat: 1/8"–1/4"

### 11.6.5 Moisture Management — The Critical Failure Mode

Moisture is the only meaningful threat to a well-built straw bale wall. Straw
at moisture content below 20% will not rot. Above 20%, mold and rot begin within 60 days.

**Sources of moisture intrusion**:
1. Roof leaks — most common; inspect soffits and flashing annually
2. Foundation splash — stem wall must be 12"+ above grade
3. Window and door flashing failures — use flexible membrane flashing, tape seams
4. Condensation from interior moisture — vapor management on cold-climate walls
5. Installation of wet bales — check moisture content before installation

**Monitoring**: install wooden dowel "moisture pins" in the base of walls at
installation. Check with a moisture meter annually. Any reading above 20% requires
investigation and remediation.

**Remediation**: cut out plaster in affected area; allow to dry with heat gun or
fans; re-plaster when below 15% moisture. In severe cases, bales must be replaced.

### 11.6.6 Performance Data

- **R-value**: approximately R-26 (2-string, 14" thick) to R-35 (3-string, 23" thick)
- **Fire resistance**: plastered straw bale wall achieves 2-hour fire resistance rating
  in ASTM E119 testing (better than standard stick frame at 1-hour or less)
- **Compressive strength**: load-bearing bale walls sustain 350–500 psf when compressed
  with all-thread rods — more than sufficient for single-story

| State | Status |
|---|---|
| California | Allowed under state code (SB 1766, now incorporated into CBC Appendix S) |
| New Mexico | Allowed under state code; long tradition |
| Texas | No statewide prohibition; local jurisdiction applies |
| Arizona | Allowed; AZ has had straw bale code since 1996 |

## 11.7 Roofing Systems

The roof is the first line of defense for every building component below it.
A roof failure allows water damage to insulation, structure, walls, and contents.
Get the roof on quickly and get it right.

### 11.7.1 Roof Forms and Performance

| Roof type | Complexity | Snow performance | Wind resistance | Water shedding |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Shed (single slope) | Simplest | Good with high-pitch orientation | Moderate | Excellent |
| Gable | Simple | Excellent (symmetric shedding) | Good | Excellent |
| Hip | Moderate | Excellent | Best (aerodynamic) | Excellent |
| Gambrel | Complex | Fair (low section traps snow) | Moderate | Good |
| Flat/parapet | Simple | Poor (snow accumulates) | Good | Requires membrane, scuppers |

**Recommendation**: gable roof is the default for most owner-builders. Maximum versatility,
easiest framing, good in all climates. Hip roofs are worth the added complexity in
high-wind zones (hurricanes, tornadoes).

### 11.7.2 Metal Roofing

**Best single roofing material for off-grid use.** Lifespan: 40–70 years with minimal
maintenance. Compatible with rainwater collection. High snow-shedding capability.
Resists wildfire embers (Class A fire rating).

**Standing seam**:
- Panels interlock at raised seams; no exposed fasteners
- Lifespan: 50–70+ years
- Gauge: 24–26 gauge for residential (24 is heavier/better)
- Material: Galvalume (55% aluminum/43.5% zinc/1.5% silicon coated steel)
  or Kynar-coated steel (Kynar 500 PVDF coating = 40-year color warranty)
- DIY difficulty: moderate — requires metal brake or factory-formed panels, special clips
- Cost: $4.50–$10.00/sq ft installed; $2.50–$5.00/sq ft materials only

**Corrugated / ribbed panels (exposed fastener)**:
- Simpler installation; fasteners exposed at each rib
- Lifespan: 25–40 years (fastener rubber washers degrade first; re-fastening extends life)
- Gauge: 26–29 gauge (use 26 gauge minimum)
- Material: G-90 galvanized (0.90 oz/sq ft zinc per ASTM A653) or Galvalume
- DIY difficulty: low — standard screws, no special clips
- Cost: $1.50–$3.50/sq ft materials; $2.50–$5.00/sq ft installed

**G-90 galvanized vs. Galvalume**:
- G-90 galvanized: better in coastal salt-air environments; more sacrificially protective
- Galvalume: better corrosion resistance in most inland environments; longer life in standard conditions
- Painted Galvalume (Kynar/SMP coating): best all-around

**Rainwater collection**: metal roofs are the cleanest surface for potable water
collection. Avoid lead-painted roofs; avoid galvanized (zinc leaching) for potable
collection. Galvalume or painted steel is acceptable; first-flush diverters required.

### 11.7.3 Asphalt Shingles

Still widely used; still practical when permit compliance or resale value matters.
Not ideal for long-term off-grid use but acceptable.

- Lifespan: 15–25 years (3-tab); 25–50 years (architectural/dimensional)
- Cost: $1.20–$2.50/sq ft materials; $3–$6/sq ft installed
- Fire rating: Class A (fiberglass-based)
- Weight: 235–360 lbs per square (100 sq ft)
- Poor match for steep or ice-dam-prone climates without ice/water shield
- Not compatible with rainwater harvesting (mineral oils, biocides leach off)

### 11.7.4 Living / Sod Roofs

A vegetated roof provides excellent insulation, stormwater management, and longevity
for the membrane beneath. Used extensively on turf houses in Scandinavia and Iceland.

**Assembly (bottom up)**:
1. Structural deck: must support 80–150 lbs/sq ft when saturated
2. Root barrier: 60-mil HDPE or copper foil (prevents root penetration)
3. Drainage layer: 1–2" gravel or dimple mat
4. Filter fabric (non-woven geotextile): separates growing media from drainage
5. Growing media: 4–6" lightweight engineered growing mix (not garden soil — too heavy)
6. Plants: sedums (Sedum acre, S. spurium) are the standard; drought-tolerant, winter-hardy

**Load requirements**:
- Dry: 40–80 lbs/sq ft depending on media depth
- Saturated: 80–150 lbs/sq ft
- **Structural implications**: roof framing must be designed for this; consult engineer
  for spans over 10 feet. Minimum 2×10 rafters at 12" OC for 12-foot spans with living roof.

**Membrane**: EPDM rubber roofing (60-mil) is the standard for living roofs.
Lifespan: 50+ years under grow media. Cost: $1.50–$3.00/sq ft materials.

**Slope**: ideal 1:12 to 3:12. Above 3:12 requires anti-slip layer in growing media.
Below 1:12 requires positive drainage design (drains at perimeter).

### 11.7.5 Snow Load Design

**Ground snow load maps**: ASCE 7 Figure 7.2-1 (US) provides ground snow loads by location.
Available free via SEAOC or any structural engineering software.

| Region | Ground snow load (psf) |
|---|---|
| Gulf Coast, Southern California | 0–5 |
| Pacific NW coastal, Mid-Atlantic | 5–20 |
| New England, Great Lakes | 20–50 |
| Mountain states (CO, WY, MT, ID) | 40–150+ at elevation |
| Coastal Alaska | 40–100 |
| Interior Alaska | 25–60 (varies dramatically) |

**Roof snow load calculation**:
Roof design snow load = Ground snow load × Cs × Ct × Ce × I
- Cs = slope factor (1.0 for flat; 0 for 70°+ pitch)
- Ct = thermal factor (1.0 heated; 1.3 unheated — attic)
- Ce = exposure factor (0.9 open terrain; 1.2 sheltered)
- I = importance factor (1.0 standard residential)

**Ice dam formation** (Zones 5–7): when roof sheathing is above 32°F (heat loss through
ceiling melts snow) but eaves are below 32°F (ice forms at cold overhang). Ice dams force
water under shingles and into structure.
- Prevention: continuous R-50+ ceiling insulation; vented cold-roof assembly
- Ice and water shield: peel-and-stick membrane under shingles at eaves; minimum 24" from
  interior face of exterior wall (typically 36–48" from eave edge)

## 11.8 Insulation Systems

Insulation is the most leverage per dollar in building performance. Every R-value
point added to the ceiling of a cold-climate home has 3–5× more impact than the
same R-value in walls (heat rises; ceiling is where most heat escapes).

### 11.8.1 Batt Insulation

**Fiberglass batts (standard)**:
- R-value: R-3.1–3.8 per inch (R-11 for 2×4, R-19 for 2×6, R-30 for 2×10)
- Cost: $0.40–$0.80/sq ft installed (DIY-friendly)
- Fire performance: melts; does not inhibit fire spread; rated 25/50 flame/smoke index
- Moisture: loses R-value when wet; glass fibers not damaged by moisture but
  will harbor mold if paper facing is wet
- Air sealing: does NOT air-seal; gaps around batts are thermal bypasses; requires
  separate air barrier strategy

**Mineral wool batts (Rockwool Comfortbatt, Knauf EcoBatt)**:
- R-value: R-3.7–4.2 per inch; Comfortbatt 3.5" = R-15; 5.5" = R-23
- Cost: $1.00–$1.60/sq ft installed
- Fire performance: rated Class A; does not burn; rated fire-stopping in cavity
- Moisture: hydrophobic; water beads off; resists mold; can get wet and dry without R-value loss
- Sound attenuation: significantly better than fiberglass (STC 45–52 with gypsum)
- Recommendation: mineral wool is worth the 2× price premium for fire resistance,
  moisture performance, and soundproofing in any permanent structure.

### 11.8.2 Rigid Foam

**EPS (Expanded Polystyrene — "beadboard")**:
- R-value: R-3.6–4.0 per inch (increases slightly in cold)
- Vapor permeance: 2–5 perms (vapor-open); breathes; good for climate zones
  where moisture management requires drying to exterior
- Cost: $0.35–$0.65/sq ft per inch of thickness
- Applications: under slab, exterior wall continuous layer, roofing

**XPS (Extruded Polystyrene — "pink/blue board")**:
- R-value: R-5.0 per inch (decreases over 75 years to ~R-4.5 as blowing agents diffuse)
- Vapor permeance: <1 perm (2" and above); acts as vapor retarder
- Cost: $0.60–$0.90/sq ft per inch
- Applications: below grade (superior moisture resistance); under slab; exterior wall

**Polyisocyanurate (polyiso)**:
- R-value: R-6.0–7.0 per inch (decreases in cold — R-5.6 at 0°F; use full R at
  climate temperatures of 40°F+; derate 20% for cold climate use)
- Vapor permeance: 0.5–3 perms depending on facing
- Cost: $0.70–$1.20/sq ft per inch
- Applications: roofing (best R/inch above deck); exterior wall; does not perform
  below grade or below freezing as well as XPS

**Thermal bridging with rigid foam**: continuous exterior rigid foam is the only
reliable way to eliminate thermal bridging through studs. 1.5" polyiso on 2×6 wall:
effective wall assembly R-value increases from ~R-20 to ~R-30 (40% improvement).

### 11.8.3 Spray Foam

**Open-cell spray foam (SPF-OC)**:
- R-value: R-3.5–3.8 per inch
- Air barrier: yes — effective at 3.5" thickness
- Vapor barrier: no — high permeance; dries readily
- Applications: cathedral ceilings, rim joists, wall cavities in hot-humid climates
- Off-gassing: amine and isocyanate compounds off-gas during application (24–72 hours);
  occupants must vacate; applicator requires full PPE (supplied air)
- Cost: $1.50–$3.00/sq ft installed per inch

**Closed-cell spray foam (SPF-CC)**:
- R-value: R-6.0–7.0 per inch
- Air barrier: yes at 2" thickness
- Vapor retarder: yes (Class II at 2"; Class I at 3"+) — important in cold climates
- Structural benefit: adds approximately 300% racking strength to sheathed walls
- Applications: exterior walls in very cold climates, below-grade, marine environments
- Cost: $3.00–$7.00/sq ft installed per inch (expensive)
- Off-gassing: more serious than OC; high isocyanate content; must cure 24 hours minimum

**> WARNING**: Improperly mixed spray foam (wrong ratio) remains soft and reactive
permanently. Always verify installer qualifications and request yield reports. DIY
two-component foam kits (Froth Pak) work for small areas; not appropriate for whole-wall application.

### 11.8.4 Blown-In Insulation

**Cellulose (blown-in)**:
- R-value: R-3.5–3.8 per inch
- Content: 80%+ recycled newsprint; treated with borate for fire and pest resistance
- Excellent air infiltration reduction when dense-packed into wall cavities
- Cost: $0.60–$1.00/sq ft per inch; rental blower with bag purchase from big-box stores
  makes DIY ceiling installation practical
- Settling: attic cellulose settles 15–20% over time; over-install to compensate
- Moisture: absorbs moisture (wicks); can dry readily; slight R-value reduction when wet

**Fiberglass blown-in**: similar performance to cellulose; more settled; slightly
less air sealing; slightly less fire resistant.

**Dense-pack installation**: dense-pack cellulose (3.5–4.0 lbs/cubic foot) in wall
cavities is superior to batts: no gaps, no compression, and good air sealing.
Requires a dense-pack machine and fill tubes. Rental or contractor.

### 11.8.5 Natural Insulation Options

| Material | R-value/inch | Cost | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sheep's wool batts | R-3.5–3.8 | $2.50–$5.00/sq ft | Vapor buffering, natural, non-toxic; sourced from domestic wool |
| Hemp batts | R-3.5–3.7 | $1.50–$3.00/sq ft | Rapidly renewable; lower embodied carbon than fiberglass |
| Wood fiber board (Gutex, Steico) | R-3.6–4.0 | $1.50–$3.00/sq ft | Exterior continuous insulation; vapor-open; impact-resistant |
| Cork board | R-3.6–4.0 | $2.00–$5.00/sq ft | Natural, renewable, termite-resistant, vapor-open |
| Loose straw (wall cavity) | R-1.5–2.0 | Very low | Fire risk without treatment; not recommended for insulation |

**Vapor buffering**: sheep's wool and hemp naturally absorb and release moisture vapor
without losing R-value significantly. This "smart" behavior reduces condensation risk
in wall assemblies where vapor barriers are omitted (as in European passive house practice).

### 11.8.6 Vapor Barriers and Building Science

**Moisture moves two ways in buildings**:
1. **Bulk water**: leaks, flooding, splash — always prevent; mechanical barrier required
2. **Vapor diffusion**: moisture molecules moving from high to low concentration through materials

**Hot-humid climate (IECC Zones 1–3)**: vapor drive is from outside in (hot humid air
wants to enter cool interior). Vapor barrier goes on EXTERIOR of insulation.
Interior poly vapor barriers in hot-humid climates trap moisture inside walls. Wrong.

**Cold-dry climate (IECC Zones 5–8)**: vapor drive is from inside out (warm humid
interior air wants to escape). Vapor retarder (not necessarily full barrier) goes
on WARM SIDE (interior) of insulation.

**Mixed climate (IECC Zone 4)**: vapor direction changes seasonally. Smart vapor
retarders (Intello Plus, MemBrain) that change permeance with humidity are the
correct solution. Cost: $0.40–$0.80/sq ft.

**> RULE**: vapor barriers trap moisture on one side. If they are on the wrong side
relative to the climate, they guarantee condensation within the wall. Get building
science right before specifying vapor control.

### 11.8.7 R-Value Targets by Climate Zone (IECC 2021)

| Zone | Walls | Ceiling | Floor/Slab | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1–2 (Hot-humid) | R-13 | R-30 | R-13 | Minimize; cooling dominates |
| 3 (Warm) | R-20 | R-38 | R-19 | Good middle ground |
| 4 (Mixed) | R-20+R-5 ci | R-49 | R-19 | Continuous insulation starts to matter |
| 5–6 (Cold) | R-20+R-10 ci | R-60 | R-30 | ci = continuous exterior insulation |
| 7 (Very cold) | R-20+R-15 ci | R-60 | R-38 | Every BTU saved is hard-won |
| 8 (Subarctic) | R-20+R-20 ci | R-60+ | R-38+ | Engineering required |

Note: these are minimum IECC code requirements. Off-grid buildings should exceed
code by 25–50% to reduce heating load and improve resilience.

## 11.9 Windows and Doors

### 11.9.1 Performance Metrics

**U-factor**: rate of heat loss through the window assembly (lower = better).
Standard double-pane: U-0.30–0.35. Triple-pane: U-0.15–0.22. Passive house standard: U-0.15.

**SHGC (Solar Heat Gain Coefficient)**: fraction of solar energy transmitted through glass.
- High SHGC (0.40–0.65): good for passive solar south-facing windows in cold climates
  (you want free solar heat)
- Low SHGC (0.20–0.35): good for east/west/north windows and hot climates (you want
  to exclude solar heat gain)

**VT (Visible Transmittance)**: how much visible light passes through. Typically 0.30–0.70.
Higher numbers = brighter interior.

### 11.9.2 Window Types

| Type | Operability | Air sealing | Best application |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fixed/picture | None | Best (no moving parts) | Passive solar south glazing |
| Casement (crank open) | Excellent | Excellent (compression seal when closed) | Ventilation windows, side openings |
| Awning (hinge at top) | Good | Good | Below operating windows; stays open in rain |
| Double-hung (two sashes) | Both sashes | Moderate (weatherstrip degrades) | Conventional; familiar; lower air sealing than casement |
| Slider | One side | Moderate | Wide openings; limited air sealing |

**Recommendation**: casement or awning windows for maximum air sealing. Fixed windows
(no moving parts) for passive solar glazing that is never opened.

### 11.9.3 Double vs. Triple Pane

| Feature | Double pane | Triple pane |
|---|---|---|
| U-factor (typical) | 0.28–0.35 | 0.15–0.22 |
| SHGC (typical) | 0.25–0.40 | 0.20–0.35 |
| Cost premium | Baseline | 30–60% more than double |
| Weight | Moderate | Heavy (installation team required for large units) |
| Payback | N/A | 8–15 years in Zone 5–6 typical |

**When triple-pane is clearly worth it**: Zone 6–8 climates; passive house design;
large north-facing windows; any window adjacent to a sleeping area in a cold climate.

**When double-pane is adequate**: Zone 1–4; passive solar south glazing where high
SHGC is the priority (triple-pane reduces SHGC by 10–20%); budget-constrained builds.

### 11.9.4 Low-E Coatings

**Low-e (low emissivity)** coating: microscopically thin metallic coating on glass surface
that reflects infrared heat while transmitting visible light.

**Hard coat (pyrolytic)**: applied during float glass production; embedded in glass.
More durable; slightly lower performance. Can be used as exterior surface (single-pane
applications).

**Soft coat (magnetron sputtered)**: applied after production; superior performance
(lower U-factor, better SHGC control); must be in sealed air space (position 2 or 3
of a double-pane unit) — exposed to air it degrades rapidly.

**Gas fill**:
- Air fill: baseline; U-factor ~0.35 for standard double-pane
- Argon fill: 34% more insulating than air; most common upgrade; adds $5–$20/window
- Krypton fill: 50% more insulating than argon; very expensive; reserved for triple-pane

### 11.9.5 Passive Solar Window Sizing

**Rule of thumb**: south-facing glass area = 7–12% of conditioned floor area.
- 7%: thermally conservative; comfortable but modest solar gain
- 12%: aggressive solar heating; requires thermal mass to prevent overheating
- Above 15%: overheating risk in spring/fall without careful shading; requires engineering

**Example**: 1,200 sq ft home; 8% passive solar target: 96 sq ft south glazing.
With typical 3×5 windows: 96 / 15 = 6–7 windows on south face.

### 11.9.6 Doors

**Solid core wood**: best aesthetics; moderate R-value (~R-2 to R-3); heavy; requires
maintenance (paint or oil); can swell/stick in humidity. Best for interior doors.

**Steel insulated**: highest security; R-5 to R-8 with foam core; most energy efficient.
Prone to denting; can rust at edges if not painted. Best for exterior entry.

**Fiberglass (Therma-Tru, Plastpro)**: R-5 to R-7 foam core; will not rot, warp, or rust;
can be wood-grain embossed; excellent for exterior in harsh climates. Most expensive.

**Weatherstripping**: replace annually in cold climates. Magnetic weatherstrip (like
refrigerator door) provides superior seal vs. foam compression type.

**Rough opening**: standard door: 2'-8" to 3'-0" × 6'-8"; RO is nominal width + 2",
nominal height + 2.5" for shimming. Verify header size before framing (see Section 11.3.3).

## 11.10 Passive Solar Design

Passive solar design captures, stores, and distributes solar energy using building
geometry, materials, and orientation — no mechanical systems required.

### 11.10.1 South Glazing and Overhang Calculation

**Overhang sizing formula**:
Overhang depth = Window height × tan(90° - latitude - 23.5°) ÷ tan(latitude - 23.5°)

**Simplified rule** (good for most applications):
At 40°N latitude: overhang depth = window height × 0.55
At 45°N latitude: overhang depth = window height × 0.45
At 35°N latitude: overhang depth = window height × 0.65

**Example**: 40°N, 4-foot tall window: overhang = 4 × 0.55 = 2.2 feet.
This overhang shades the full window on June 21 (highest sun angle) and
admits full sun on December 21 (lowest sun angle).

**Fixed overhang limitations**: a fixed overhang optimized for summer is
partially wrong for spring and fall. Removable lattice, exterior roller shades,
or deciduous vine trellises extend the seasonal control.

### 11.10.2 Thermal Mass Sizing

**Thermal mass** (concrete, stone, brick, adobe, water) stores heat absorbed
during the day and releases it at night, moderating indoor temperature swings.

**Sizing rules**:
- Minimum: 3 sq ft of 4" thick mass per sq ft of south glazing
- Optimal: 5–9 sq ft of 4" thick mass per sq ft of south glazing
- Maximum useful: beyond 9:1 ratio, mass cannot fully charge in available sunlight

**Materials** (heat capacity per square foot per inch of thickness):
| Material | Heat capacity (BTU/°F/sq ft/inch) |
|---|---|
| Concrete (density ~145 lb/cf) | 12.0 |
| Brick | 10.0 |
| Adobe | 7.5 |
| Water | 62.4 per 12" (most effective per volume) |
| Stone (varies) | 8–14 |

**Rule of thumb**: a well-designed passive solar home (8% south glazing, proper thermal
mass, good insulation) maintains comfortable temperatures in Zone 5 with 30–50% of its
heating load provided by passive solar alone.

### 11.10.3 Natural Ventilation

**Cross ventilation**: openings on windward and leeward walls; air moves through
at 60–80% of outdoor wind velocity when inlet equals outlet area.
- Minimum: inlets facing prevailing summer winds; outlets on opposite face
- Optimal: inlets low (3–4 feet), outlets high (ceiling level)

**Stack effect**: warm air rises and exits through high openings; cooler air
drawn in at low openings. Driving force increases with building height and
temperature differential.
- A 20-foot tall space with 2% of floor area as high openings and 2% as low openings
  will move air at 1–3 mph in still conditions — adequate for comfort to 95°F with low humidity

**Venturi ridge vent**: a raised ridge cap creates negative pressure at roof peak,
drawing air from soffit vents through insulated attic or living space.
Effective only with continuous soffit and ridge venting (no blockages).

### 11.10.4 Trombe Wall

An uninsulated glazed south wall with a dense thermal mass wall immediately
behind the glass (4–8" of concrete or masonry). Air is heated in the gap
between glass and mass; vents at top and bottom allow thermosiphon circulation.

**Construction**:
- Glazing: single or double glass, 2–4" gap from mass wall
- Mass wall: 8–18" concrete, brick, or stone; dark (absorptive) exterior surface
- Vents: 4–6" diameter, spaced 24–36" apart at top and bottom of wall
- Optional: reflective night insulation (shutters or blinds) reduces overnight heat loss

**Performance data**: Trombe walls provide 0.1–0.5 BTU/hr/sq ft of wall area on
average winter day (varies widely by climate and design).

**Limitations**: they are thermally massive and slow to respond; they work best
as a supplement to other heating, not a primary system. They create a visual
barrier on the south facade.

### 11.10.5 Earth Sheltering

Bermed or fully underground structures take advantage of the earth's stable
temperature (50–55°F year-round at 6+ feet depth) to reduce both heating and cooling loads.

**Berming (partial earth shelter)**: earth pushed against one or more walls, typically
the north and east/west walls of a hillside site. Reduces wind exposure and thermal loss
through bermed walls.

**Fully underground**: requires substantial waterproofing engineering. Waterproofing
layers: EPDM or HDPE sheet membrane over rigid foam, drainage mat, and filter fabric.
Total cost of waterproofing system: $15–$30/sq ft of underground wall area.

**Frost and waterproofing challenges**:
- Frost depth: below frost depth (Zone 5: 4 feet), the soil is a stable insulator, not a freezer
- Above frost depth: external face of underground wall must be insulated to prevent
  frost from penetrating to structure
- Drainage: critical — must have positive drainage around and away from all underground walls
- Typical waterproofing system: crystalline additive in concrete + bituminous membrane +
  dimple mat drainage + drainage tile at footing

**Sunspace/greenhouse attachment**: a glazed sunspace on the south side pre-heats
air before it enters the main structure through vents or sliding doors. Thermal gain:
30–50% heating load reduction in optimized designs. Also provides year-round food production.

## 11.11 CBRN Hardening

Chemical, Biological, Radiological, and Nuclear threats require specific structural
and filtration responses. This section covers practical measures available to
owner-builders — not full military hardening, but meaningful improvement in survivability.

### 11.11.1 Structural Hardening: Blast Overpressure

**Conventional stick frame** provides essentially zero blast protection. A 1 psi
blast wave (typical at 1+ mile from a 1-kiloton surface detonation) will fail
light wood frame construction.

**ICF (Insulated Concrete Forms)**: polystyrene forms remain in place as insulation
while serving as concrete forming system. Structural concrete cores are 4–8" thick.
- Typical residential ICF: 4" concrete core; provides 2–4 psi overpressure resistance
- Enhanced ICF: 6–8" core with 1/2" rebar each way: 4–8 psi resistance
- Cost premium vs. stick frame: $25–$50/sq ft
- Additional benefit: R-22+ (foam on both sides); excellent fire resistance;
  tornado-resistant; reduced sound transmission

**Reinforced poured concrete**: mass and thickness are the primary variables.
- 6" reinforced slab wall (horizontal or vertical): 5–8 psi
- 8" reinforced slab wall: 8–15 psi
- 12" reinforced slab wall: 20+ psi
- Poured concrete construction cost: $100–$200/sq ft (full structure)

**Earthen berming**: earth is an excellent blast absorber. 3 feet of compacted
soil over a reinforced concrete roof reduces blast transmission by 80–90%.

**Double-wythe brick**: 9" total brick wall with collar joint; provides 2–4 psi
resistance; better than wood but far below concrete.

**Earthbag ring**: surrounding a below-grade shelter with earthbag walls provides
additional blast absorption at very low cost.

### 11.11.2 Below-Grade Safe Room

A room designed to protect occupants from blast, fallout, and forced entry.

**Construction specifications**:
- Walls: minimum 8" poured reinforced concrete, 1/2" rebar at 12" OC both ways
- Roof/ceiling: minimum 8" reinforced concrete slab (same as walls) with 12–24"
  of earth cover above for fallout/blast supplementation
- Floor: 4" reinforced concrete slab
- Minimum interior dimensions: 8 ft × 10 ft per 4 occupants; 10 ft × 12 ft for 6

**Blast door specifications** (for meaningful protection):
- Steel plate: minimum 1/4" (3/16" is marginal); 3/8" or 1/2" for better protection
- Frame: welded steel channel, 3/8" plate, anchored into concrete with 1/2" anchor bolts
  at 6" spacing on all sides
- Hinges: minimum 4 heavy-duty weld-on hinges, rated 500+ lbs each
- Latch: military-style 3-point locking bar mechanism
- Gasket: automotive-grade door gasket for NBC seal when compressed
- Typical fabrication cost (DIY): $500–$1,500 for materials; $2,000–$5,000 from specialty vendor

**> WARNING**: the blast door must be inswing (opens inward). An outswing door can be
blocked by debris from a blast event, trapping occupants.

### 11.11.3 NBC Filtration

**NBC (Nuclear/Biological/Chemical) filtration** system maintains positive pressure
inside the shelter using filtered outside air.

**System components**:
1. **Hand-operated air pump / blower**: powers the system with or without electricity.
   HEPA filter + activated carbon canister in series. Swiss SARATOGA overpressure systems
   are the reference standard. Cost: $800–$2,500 for a complete unit.
2. **HEPA filter**: removes particles > 0.3 microns (99.97%+ efficiency). Removes
   fallout particles, biological agents, and most chemical agents bound to particles.
3. **Activated carbon filter**: adsorbs chemical warfare agents, industrial chemicals,
   and radiological iodine vapors. Replace every 3–5 years or as contaminated.
4. **Pre-filter**: protects HEPA from large particles; extends HEPA life.

**Air-lock entry**: a double-door vestibule where occupants decontaminate before
entering the main shelter. Minimizes contamination ingress. Minimum size: 3 ft × 6 ft.

**Sealing protocol**:
- All penetrations (electrical, plumbing, communications): sealed with foam or mechanical
  packing glands before use in NBC scenario
- Ventilation system: positive pressure mode only (outward leakage prevents inward infiltration)
- Door gaskets: test annually with a lit incense stick at the gasket perimeter; no smoke ingress

**Pressurization rate**: minimum 1 CFM positive pressure per occupant for NBC protection.

### 11.11.4 Fallout Shelter Design

**The FEMA Rule of 7**: radiation intensity drops by a factor of 10 for each 7-fold
increase in time after detonation.
- 7 hours: 1/10th of initial intensity
- 49 hours: 1/100th
- 2 weeks: 1/1,000th

**Implication**: the first 24–48 hours are the most lethal. A shelter that provides
protection through 72 hours provides the majority of the benefit.

**Protection Factor (PF)**: how much radiation the shelter attenuates.
- PF-10: reduces dose by 90%. Minimum useful.
- PF-40: reduces dose by 97.5%. Practical target for homestead shelter.
- PF-100: reduces dose by 99%. Underground room with 3' earth cover over reinforced roof.
- PF-1,000+: deep basement or fully engineered shelter.

**Mass required for meaningful protection**:
| Material | Thickness for PF-40 | Thickness for PF-100 |
|---|---|---|
| Earth/soil | 24" | 36" |
| Concrete | 12" | 18" |
| Brick | 18" | 24" |
| Water (density ~1.0) | 24" | 36" |
| Lead | 1.5" | 2.5" |

**Minimum fallout shelter for homestead**:
- Below grade (basement, root cellar, purpose-built room)
- Walls and ceiling: 8" reinforced concrete minimum; 12" preferred
- Earth berm to ceiling: 24"+ wherever possible
- Interior dimensions: 8 × 10 × 7' minimum for 4 occupants
- Supplies pre-positioned: 2-week supply of food and water
- Radiation monitoring: Geiger counter + dosimeter badges
- Communications: battery/crank radio for emergency broadcasts
- Sanitation: chemical toilet bucket
- Ventilation: NBC filtration system or sealed room with limited ventilation and CO2 monitoring

**Potassium iodide (KI tablets)**:
- Protects thyroid from I-131 ONLY — not general radiation protection
- Dosing: adults (18–40): 130 mg; adults 40+: 65 mg; children 3–12 years: 65 mg;
  infants: 16 mg; pregnant/lactating: 130 mg
- Take immediately before or within 4 hours of exposure
- Source: IOSAT, ThyroSafe — available OTC; $10–$15 for 14 tablets
- Stock minimum 2 courses per person

### 11.11.5 EMP Hardening

**EMP (Electromagnetic Pulse)** from a high-altitude nuclear detonation (HEMP) can
disable unshielded electronics at continental scale. Solar CME (Carrington event)
produces similar effects.

**What is vulnerable**:
- Sensitive microelectronics: all modern computers, phones, radio circuits, vehicle ECUs
- Long conductors (antenna effect): power lines, phone lines, long cable runs amplify EMP
- Equipment with switched-mode power supplies: nearly all modern electronics

**What is NOT vulnerable** (or much less vulnerable):
- Vacuum tubes (older radios and amplifiers)
- Simple electromechanical equipment (relay-based, non-microchip)
- Disconnected equipment in a properly designed Faraday cage

**Faraday cage design for protection**:
- Metal container (no gaps) completely surrounding the device
- Not electrically grounded (grounding can introduce more current)
- Any cable penetrating the cage must use surge suppressor or EMI filter
- Effective options: metal garbage cans with tight-fitting lids, ammo cans, mylar-lined
  wooden boxes lined with aluminum foil (multiple layers for better attenuation)
- Conductive gap sealing: aluminum tape over any seam or lid joint

**What to protect**:
- Backup communications radio (handheld HF/VHF ham radio)
- Solar charge controller and inverter (or entire solar electronics package)
- Backup Geiger counter
- Vehicle ignition components for older pre-1980 vehicles (no ECU)
- Laptop with essential documentation offline
- Spare critical circuit boards for water pump, HVAC controllers

**Attenuation of typical Faraday cages**:
- Ammo can (sealed): 30–50 dB attenuation (factor of 30–300)
- Metal garbage can (lid taped): 20–40 dB
- Nested cans (double layer): 50–80 dB (much more effective)

### 11.11.6 Detection Equipment

**Geiger counter / radiation detector**:
| Model | Type | Range | Cost | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Inspector Alert (SE International) | GM tube | 0.001–125 mR/hr | $400–$500 | Industry standard; audio + digital display; data logging option |
| RadAlert Ranger (SE International) | GM + alpha window | 0.001–100 mR/hr | $600–$700 | Alpha/beta/gamma capable |
| GQ GMC-500+ | GM tube | 0–999 uSv/hr | $80–$120 | Budget option; USB logging; adequate for most applications |
| RADTriage Personal Dosimeter | Film badge | Cumulative dose only | $25–$35 for 3 | No live readout; wear and read after event; good supplemental |

**Threshold action levels**:
- 0.01 mR/hr (baseline): background, no action
- 0.1 mR/hr: elevated; stay inside; monitor
- 1 mR/hr: significant; shelter in place required
- 10 mR/hr: significant fallout present; full shelter required; limit exposure
- 100 mR/hr: extreme; 1 hour of exposure = 1/10th of emergency dose limit

**Procurement**: Inspector Alert and RadAlert are the recommended units for
reliability and longevity. Buy from SE International directly or authorized dealers.
Avoid untested units from unknown Chinese suppliers for life-safety applications.

## 11.14 Skills, Timeline, and Learning Resources

### 11.14.1 Skill Feasibility Table

| System | Novice owner-builder feasibility | What a novice can do | What requires professional |
|---|---|---|---|
| Platform frame (stick frame) | High | All framing, sheathing, roofing | Foundation forming, concrete pour (can learn), electrical rough-in (can DIY in many states) |
| Timber frame | Moderate | Joinery with practice, bent assembly, raising | Initial layout/engineering review |
| Straw bale infill | High | All baling, all plastering | Foundation and roof structure |
| Cob | High | All of it after practice | Nothing, if site conditions are favorable |
| Adobe | High | Block production, laying, plastering | Seismic engineering in high-zone areas |
| Rammed earth | Moderate | Earth filling, tamping | Formwork design, engineering in stabilized mix |
| Earthbag | High | All of it | Plaster finish (learnable) |
| ICF | Moderate | Form stacking, bracing, concrete placement | Engineering design, poured concrete (often own-poured with pump truck) |
| Poured concrete basement | Low–moderate | Forming (learnable), backfill | Concrete pumping, waterproofing inspection |
| Metal roofing (corrugated) | High | All installation | Nothing |
| Metal roofing (standing seam) | Moderate | Seaming with proper tool | Custom flashings (learnable with practice) |
| Spray foam (closed-cell) | Low | Small kit applications | Whole-wall requires professional equipment |
| Passive solar design | High (with calculation) | Orientation, overhangs, mass | Energy modeling for complex designs |

### 11.14.2 Learning Sequence for New Owner-Builders

Build in this order. Each project develops skills needed for the next:

1. **Chicken coop** (Month 1–2): framing square, level, plumb, basic cuts, roofing, doors.
   Cost: $200–$800 materials. Skills: layout, framing basics, simple roofing.

2. **Outbuilding / tool shed** (Month 3–6): full foundation (post footings), wall framing,
   roof framing, sheathing, door installation. Cost: $800–$3,000 materials.
   Skills: foundation layout, compound cuts, window installation.

3. **Root cellar** (Month 6–12): excavation, drainage planning, concrete work, waterproofing,
   venting. Cost: $1,500–$5,000. Skills: concrete, drainage, working underground.

4. **Small guest cabin or studio** (Year 1–2): all systems at smaller scale with real
   habitation demands. Plumbing rough-in, insulation, electrical rough-in. 200–400 sq ft.
   Cost: $8,000–$25,000 materials. Skills: integrated systems, code requirements (if any).

5. **Main house** (Year 2–4): full-scale construction with proven skills.

**Timeline for owner-built main house** (1,000 sq ft, owner-builder with 20–30 hours/week):
| System | Platform frame | Timber frame + bale | Earthbag/cob |
|---|---|---|---|
| Foundation | 2–4 weeks | 2–4 weeks | 3–6 weeks |
| Structure (walls + roof) | 6–12 weeks | 12–20 weeks | 16–40 weeks |
| Roofing | 2–3 weeks | 2–3 weeks | 1–2 weeks |
| Windows/doors | 1–2 weeks | 1–2 weeks | 2–4 weeks |
| Insulation | 1–2 weeks | integrated | integrated |
| Plaster/siding | 1–2 weeks | 6–10 weeks | 6–12 weeks |
| Rough electrical/plumbing | 3–5 weeks | 3–5 weeks | 3–5 weeks |
| Interior finish | 4–8 weeks | 4–8 weeks | 4–8 weeks |
| **Total** | **20–38 weeks** | **30–52 weeks** | **35–77 weeks** |

Note: "total calendar time" is typically 1.5–3× elapsed weeks due to cure times,
weather delays, material delivery, and part-time schedules.

### 11.14.4 Cost of Learning vs. Hiring

**The numbers**: owner-builder savings on a 1,200 sq ft home in the US average
$60,000–$120,000 relative to full contractor construction. Learning costs:
- Books and manuals: $200–$500
- Weekend workshops (1–2 per system): $500–$2,000
- Small project materials (learning builds): $2,000–$8,000
- Tools (see Domain 10): $2,000–$8,000 initial investment

**ROI on learning**: the first house is the tuition. Skills compound — every
subsequent structure is faster, cheaper, and better.

**Where to hire vs. DIY**: Hiring is worthwhile for:
- Excavation and grading (equipment-dependent)
- Concrete pumping (time-sensitive, equipment-dependent)
- Professional electrical (code compliance in most jurisdictions)
- Engineered systems review (stamps on drawings)
- Anything requiring licensed trade work for insurance or resale

**Where DIY is clearly optimal**: framing, roofing, insulation, plaster, earthen
construction, door and window installation, interior finish, drainage and grading.

## Cross-Domain Notes

- **Domain 3 (Water)**: foundation drainage ties directly into well placement and setbacks.
  Roof design (overhang, metal surface) is the primary variable in rainwater collection.
- **Domain 6 (Energy)**: south roof orientation for passive solar also maximizes solar PV
  production. ICF and insulated buildings require less heating energy.
- **Domain 7 (Heating/Cooling)**: building envelope — this domain — is the primary lever
  for reducing heating and cooling loads. Every R-value decision here reduces fuel consumption.
- **Domain 9 (Waste/Sanitation)**: septic system must be sited relative to building; foundation
  drainage cannot intersect with drainfield.
- **Domain 10 (Tools)**: framing tools, concrete tools, and earthen construction tools are
  covered there; this domain focuses on design and materials decisions.
- **Domain 15 (Disaster)**: the fallout shelter and NBC systems here expand on the nuclear
  scenario in Domain 15. CBRN hardening section is this guide's most detailed treatment.
