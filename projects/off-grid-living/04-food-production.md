# Domain 4: Food Production

> Food self-sufficiency is the most complex off-grid domain and the one that
> most homesteaders underestimate. A solar array can be sized in an afternoon;
> a productive food system takes 3–5 years of iteration to mature. The core
> discipline is caloric math — knowing exactly how many pounds of food your land
> must produce per year, and then building layered systems (annual vegetables,
> perennials, livestock, soil biology) that reliably hit that number under adverse
> conditions. No vague aspirations here: this document gives specific numbers for
> every crop, animal, and system so you can plan with precision.

---

## Quick-Reference Decision Framework

| Household size | Minimum productive land | Priority crops | First livestock |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 adult | 0.25 acre garden + 0.1 acre grain | Potatoes, beans, greens, squash | 4 laying hens |
| 2 adults | 0.5 acre garden + 0.25 acre grain | Above + corn, sunflowers | 8 hens + 2 rabbits |
| 2 adults + 2 children | 0.75 acre garden + 0.5 acre grain | Above + full root crops | 12 hens + 4 rabbits + 1 goat |
| 4 adults | 1 acre garden + 0.5 acre grain | Full diversity, surpluses for storage | Above + pigs (seasonal) |

*These figures assume intensive growing, succession planting, and good soil.
Raw land with no fertility history needs 1.5–2x these areas while soil is building.*

## Section 1: Caloric Math

**The foundation of any serious food plan is a precise caloric target.** Every
other decision — how many beds, how many animals, how much storage — flows from
knowing how many calories your household needs to produce annually.

### 1.1 Caloric Needs by Activity Level

| Person type | Daily calories | Annual calories |
|---|---|---|
| Sedentary adult (desk work, minimal physical activity) | 1,800–2,000 | 657,000–730,000 |
| Moderately active adult (light homestead work) | 2,200–2,600 | 803,000–949,000 |
| Highly active adult (heavy manual labor: logging, construction, farming) | 3,000–4,000 | 1,095,000–1,460,000 |
| Child age 5–12 | 1,400–2,000 | 511,000–730,000 |
| Child age 13–18 | 2,000–2,800 | 730,000–1,022,000 |
| Elderly adult (65+) | 1,600–1,800 | 584,000–657,000 |

**Working rule**: Budget 2,500 cal/day per active homesteading adult. This accounts
for the physical nature of off-grid life and provides a safety margin. For a
two-adult homestead: **5,000 cal/day, or 1,825,000 cal/year**.

*The caloric figure alone is insufficient — nutrient density matters. A potato-only
diet meets caloric needs but causes deficiencies. Plan for proteins (beans, meat,
eggs, dairy), fats (lard, oils, nuts), and vitamins (greens, fermented foods).
The systems below achieve this when combined.*

**Key insight**: Sweet potatoes and potatoes produce 4–5 million calories per acre
under intensive cultivation. A single acre of potatoes can feed 2–3 adults all
their caloric needs for a year. This is why root crops anchor every serious
self-sufficient food system.

## Section 2: Annual Vegetables

### 2.1 Planting Calendar by US Climate Zone

**USDA Hardiness Zones** define minimum winter temperatures and correlate to
growing season length. Note: hardiness zones describe winter cold, not summer
heat. Use frost dates (available from the USDA's plant hardiness map) as the
practical planning tool.

| Zone | Last spring frost | First fall frost | Growing season | Strategy |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 3 (ND, MN, northern MT) | June 1–15 | Aug 15–Sept 1 | 60–90 days | Short-season varieties; high tunnels critical |
| 4 (WI, upper MI, MT highlands) | May 15–June 1 | Sept 15–Oct 1 | 90–120 days | Succession plant aggressively; cold frames extend |
| 5 (IA, OH, CO, PA, NY) | May 1–15 | Oct 1–15 | 150–180 days | Full range of crops; standard US benchmark |
| 6 (VA, KS, NM mountains, OR coast) | Apr 15–May 1 | Oct 15–Nov 1 | 180–210 days | Spring and fall are long; direct-sow more |
| 7 (TN, NC, OK, TX panhandle, OR valley) | Mar 15–Apr 15 | Nov 1–15 | 200–240 days | Two distinct growing seasons; summer heat breaks |
| 8 (GA, AL, central TX, Pacific coast) | Feb 15–Mar 15 | Nov 15–Dec 15 | 250–300 days | Winter growing for roots; summer heat limits |
| 9–10 (FL, S. TX, S. CA) | Jan 1–Feb 1 or none | Dec 15–Jan 1 or none | Year-round | Reverse calendar: grow Oct–May; rest June–Sept |

**General 4-season planting calendar (Zone 5 as baseline)**:

```
EARLY SPRING (4–6 weeks before last frost, soil workable):
  Start indoors: tomatoes, peppers, eggplant, celery, leeks, onions
  Direct sow outdoors: peas, spinach, kale, chard, lettuce, carrots, beets, radishes
  Transplant: onion starts, leek starts

LATE SPRING (last frost date):
  Transplant outdoors: tomatoes, peppers, eggplant, basil, cucumbers (starts)
  Direct sow: beans, squash, corn, cucumbers, sunflowers, sweet potatoes

SUMMER (June–August):
  Direct sow succession: beans every 3 weeks, beets, lettuce (heat-tolerant)
  Succession transplants: fall broccoli, cauliflower, kale (start July 1)
  Maintain: irrigation, pest monitoring, trellising

EARLY FALL (8–10 weeks before first frost):
  Direct sow: spinach, arugula, kale, carrots (for root cellaring), turnips, beets
  Transplant: fall broccoli, cabbage, kohlrabi
  Harvest and storage: winter squash before first frost, potatoes after tops die back

LATE FALL / WINTER:
  Cold frame / hoop house: kale, mache, spinach survive hard frosts under cover
  Root cellar: carrots, beets, turnips, parsnips in damp sand
  Season: garlic planting (October)
```

*Adjust by 2–3 weeks earlier for each zone above 5, 2–3 weeks later for each zone below 5.*

### 2.3 Succession Planting Strategy

**Succession planting** prevents feast-or-famine cycles. Instead of planting
all your lettuce at once, plant a small block every 2–3 weeks for continuous harvest.

**Crops that benefit most from succession planting**:
- Lettuce / salad mix — sow every 2 weeks; 50 sq ft per planting
- Bush beans — sow every 3 weeks from last frost to 60 days before first frost
- Beets — sow every 3 weeks spring through midsummer
- Radishes — sow every 2 weeks; 30-day turnaround
- Spinach — spring and fall only (bolts in heat); sow every 2 weeks in those windows
- Cilantro — bolts fast; sow every 3 weeks

**Space planning for continuous supply (2 adults)**:

```
Lettuce: 3 beds × 4×4 ft = 48 sq ft total, rotated every 2 weeks
Bush beans: 4 beds × 4×8 ft = 128 sq ft total, staggered 3 weeks apart
Radishes: 2 beds × 2×4 ft = 16 sq ft, rotated every 30 days
Beets: 3 beds × 4×4 ft = 48 sq ft, rotated every 3 weeks
Result: Fresh salad greens, beans, and roots every week from May to October
```

## Section 3: Perennial Food Systems

**Perennials are the most time-leveraged investment in a food system.** Plant
once, harvest for decades. The tradeoff is time: most fruit trees take 3–8 years
to reach productive yield. Plant perennials in year one, even before the annual
garden is fully established. By year five, the perennial system contributes
meaningful food with near-zero annual labor.

### 3.1 Fruit Trees: Selection by Climate Zone

| Fruit tree | Hardy zones | Years to first fruit | Mature yield/tree | Self-fertile? | Storage |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Apple | 3–9 | 3–5 years | 200–400 lb | Usually no (plant 2 varieties) | 2–6 months (root cellar) |
| Pear | 4–9 | 4–6 years | 100–250 lb | Usually no (plant 2) | 2–4 months |
| Asian pear | 5–9 | 3–4 years | 100–200 lb | Partially | 2–4 months |
| Plum | 4–9 | 3–5 years | 50–150 lb | Some varieties yes | 2–4 weeks fresh; can/dry |
| Cherry (sweet) | 5–9 | 4–7 years | 50–100 lb | Mostly no | 1–2 weeks fresh; can/dry |
| Cherry (tart/sour) | 4–9 | 3–5 years | 50–150 lb | Yes | Same; easier to can |
| Persimmon (American) | 4–9 | 4–6 years | 75–150 lb | Yes | 2–3 months dried |
| Persimmon (Asian) | 6–10 | 3–5 years | 75–200 lb | Mostly yes | 1 month fresh; dry |
| Pawpaw | 5–9 | 4–7 years | 25–75 lb | No (plant 2 genetically distinct) | 1 week fresh; freeze pulp |
| Mulberry | 5–10 | 2–4 years | 50–200 lb | Yes | 1–2 days fresh; dehydrate |
| Fig | 7–11 (or container) | 2–3 years | 50–100 lb | Yes | 1 week fresh; dry or can |
| Quince | 5–9 | 4–6 years | 50–150 lb | Yes | 2–3 months (cool, dry) |

**Climate zone guidance**:
- Zone 3–4: Focus on apple, tart cherry, hardy pear, pawpaw, mulberry.
  Use Honeycrisp, Zestar (apple), Carmine Jewel (cherry), Luscious (pear).
- Zone 5–6: Full range above. Add Asian pear, sweet cherry, persimmon (American).
- Zone 7–8: Add fig, Asian persimmon. Southern varieties of apple require low-chill.
- Zone 9–10: Citrus, avocado, fig, low-chill apple and pear (Anna, Dorsett).

**Orchard spacing**: Standard rootstock 20–25 ft apart. Semi-dwarf 12–15 ft.
Dwarf 6–10 ft (requires staking, lower yield per tree but fits small spaces).
**A 10-tree orchard on semi-dwarf rootstock, 15-ft spacing = roughly 2,250 sq ft
(0.05 acre). Ten trees at mature production: 1,000–2,000 lb fruit/year.**

**Cost**: Bare-root trees at purchase: $15–$40 each. Potted or larger specimens:
$40–$80. Ten-tree orchard: $150–$400 bare-root planted yourself.

### 3.2 Nut Trees: Caloric Powerhouses

Nut trees are the highest-calorie perennial food system. Mature trees require no
annual labor beyond harvest.

| Nut tree | Hardy zones | Years to produce | Mature yield | Calories/lb | Calories/tree at maturity |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Black walnut | 4–9 | 8–10 years | 50–150 lb (nutmeat) | 3,000 | 150,000–450,000 |
| Hazelnut / filbert | 4–9 | 3–5 years | 10–25 lb (nutmeat) | 2,800 | 28,000–70,000 |
| American chestnut (hybrid) | 4–8 | 5–8 years | 25–75 lb | 800 | 20,000–60,000 |
| Chinese chestnut | 4–9 | 4–7 years | 30–100 lb | 800 | 24,000–80,000 |
| Almond | 7–9 | 3–5 years | 25–50 lb (nutmeat) | 2,600 | 65,000–130,000 |
| Pecan | 6–9 | 7–10 years | 50–150 lb (nutmeat) | 3,100 | 155,000–465,000 |
| Shagbark hickory | 4–9 | 8–15 years | 10–40 lb | 3,000 | 30,000–120,000 |

**Hazelnut** is the most practical starter nut: produces in 3–5 years (vs.
10+ for walnut/pecan), grows as a shrub so fits smaller spaces, and yields
calorie-dense nuts with minimal processing. Plant a row of hazelnuts in year
one and begin harvesting by year 4.

**Chestnut** is the only nut with roughly the same caloric profile as a grain
(carbohydrate-forward, lower fat). Historically called "the grain that grows on
trees." Well-suited to steep or rocky land not suitable for garden beds.

**Note on black walnut**: Produces **juglone**, a chemical that inhibits growth
of many plants within 50–80 ft. Valuable timber tree but site carefully — do not
plant near garden, apple trees, or vegetable beds.

Caloric density: ~390 cal/lb.

### 3.5 Food Forest Principles

A **food forest** is a designed perennial system mimicking the structure of a
natural forest with edible species at every layer. Once established (5–10 years),
it requires minimal labor and is resilient to drought and pest pressure.

**Seven layers**:

```
1. CANOPY (30–80 ft): Large fruit trees, nut trees, mulberry
   Spacing: 25–40 ft; plant 1–2 per 1,000 sq ft

2. UNDERSTORY (10–30 ft): Semi-dwarf apple, pear, plum, persimmon
   Spacing: 12–20 ft; fills gaps in canopy

3. SHRUB (3–10 ft): Elderberry, gooseberry, currant, hazelnut, blueberry
   Spacing: 4–8 ft; multiple per canopy gap

4. HERBACEOUS (1–4 ft): Comfrey, yarrow, herbs, rhubarb, Jerusalem artichoke
   Interplanted; fills understory floor

5. GROUND COVER (0–1 ft): Strawberry, thyme, clover, mint
   Covers soil; suppresses weeds; nitrogen fixation (clover)

6. VINE (climbing): Grapes, hardy kiwi, hops, raspberries on edges
   Climbs canopy and shrub layer; no horizontal space used

7. ROOT: Horseradish, skirret, burdock, perennial leeks
   Underground layer; often overlooked
```

**Establishment cost**: 1/4 acre food forest with 3–4 canopy trees, 8–10 understory,
15–20 shrubs, perennial herb/ground cover: **$500–$2,000 planted yourself** over 2–3 years.
Paid landscaping installation: $5,000–$20,000+.

**Comfrey (Symphytum officinale)** is a keystone plant in a food forest: deep taproot
(6–10 ft) mines subsoil minerals; leaves are 25% protein dry weight; chop and drop
as mulch; self-regenerates. Plant 1 comfrey per fruit tree. Do not plant **Bocking 14**
(sterile) if you want it to spread; **common comfrey** spreads by root fragments.

## Section 4: Livestock

Livestock transform feed (grass, kitchen scraps, grain byproducts) into high-value
protein and fat that are difficult to obtain in sufficient quantity from plants alone.
A complete food system combines annual vegetables, perennials, and animals.

### 4.1 Chickens: Egg Production

**Laying hen math**:

- A quality laying hen produces **240–300 eggs/year** in her first two years.
- Production declines to 150–200 eggs/year in year 3, then drops further.
- Most homesteaders cull at year 2–3 for meat; replace with pullets.
- **To produce 1 dozen eggs/week (52 dozen/year = 624 eggs)**: 2–3 hens.
- **To produce 2 dozen eggs/week (1,248 eggs)**: 5–6 hens plus buffer for molting.
- **Practical flock size for 2 adults**: 8–10 hens = surplus eggs for preservation,
  feeding back, or trade.

**Feed and cost**:
- Layer feed consumption: ~0.25 lb/hen/day (4 oz).
- 8 hens × 0.25 lb × 365 days = **730 lb feed/year**.
- Layer feed cost: $0.30–$0.50/lb → **$220–$365/year in purchased feed**.
- On pasture with good forage: reduce purchased feed 30–50%.
- Supplement with kitchen scraps, garden waste, insects — reduces costs further.

**Housing requirements**:
- **4 sq ft/bird inside coop** (minimum); 10 sq ft/bird preferred.
- **10 sq ft/bird outdoor run** (minimum); 40+ sq ft on pasture.
- 8 hens: coop 6×6 ft (36 sq ft); run 10×30 ft (300 sq ft).
- Nesting boxes: 1 box per 3–4 hens; 12×12 in box minimum.
- Roosts: 8–10 inches of perch per bird, elevated 18–36 in above floor.

**Winter production**: Hens naturally slow or stop laying when days fall below
14 hours of light. Options:
- Add artificial light (6W LED, 14-hour timer) to extend production — hens don't
  need warm light, just light hours. Cost: minimal on solar.
- Accept winter slowdown and rely on preserved eggs (water-glassing in lime water
  preserves eggs 8–12 months at room temperature).
- Cold-hardy breeds (Rhode Island Red, Barred Plymouth Rock, Black Australorp)
  handle Zone 4–5 winters better than Mediterranean breeds (Leghorn).

**Startup cost**: 6 pullets ($4–$8 each) + basic coop build ($200–$600 DIY,
$600–$1,500 purchased) = **$225–$650 total startup**. Egg production offsets
cost in 6–18 months depending on local egg prices.

### 4.2 Rabbits: Fast Protein

**Rabbits are the most efficient small livestock for meat production** in a small
space. A trio (1 buck, 2 does) produces more meat per square foot of space than
any other species.

**Reproduction and meat cycle**:
- Does breed at 5–6 months of age.
- Gestation: **31 days**.
- Average litter: **7–10 kits** (healthy doe in good condition).
- Kits reach market weight (4–5 lb live, 2–2.5 lb dressed) in **8–10 weeks**.
- Doe can produce **4–6 litters/year** (allow 4–6 weeks recovery between).
- 2 does × 5 litters × 8 kits × 2 lb dressed = **160 lb dressed meat/year**.
- At current grocery prices ($6–$10/lb for rabbit), this represents $960–$1,600 in food value.

**Feed conversion ratio**: Rabbits convert feed to meat at approximately **3:1 to 4:1**
(lb feed per lb weight gain). Chickens are 2:1; pigs are 3.5:1; cattle are 7:1.
Rabbits are highly efficient.

**Housing**:
- Hutch per rabbit: **2 sq ft minimum**; 4 sq ft preferred.
- Doe with litter: 6 sq ft minimum.
- Wire-bottom hutches keep them clean; solid resting board required.
- A basic 3-rabbit operation: hutch 3×6 ft (18 sq ft), elevated. **Cost: $100–$300 DIY**.
- Rabbits are heat-sensitive above 85°F — shade and ventilation critical.

**Feed**: Pellets (~$0.60–$0.80/lb); 3 oz/day/adult rabbit. Supplement with hay
(free if you cut it), garden trimmings, grass. A 3-rabbit operation consumes
~$150–$250 in pellets/year.

**Manure**: Rabbit droppings are **cold manure** — apply directly to garden without
composting (unlike chicken manure, which burns if raw). High nitrogen, phosphorus.
3 rabbits produce ~100 lb manure/year.

### 4.3 Goats: Milk and Browse

**Two primary dairy breeds for homestead use**:

| Characteristic | Nigerian Dwarf | Nubian |
|---|---|---|
| Size | 50–75 lb | 135–180 lb |
| Daily milk (peak) | 1–2 quarts | 3–5 quarts |
| Butterfat content | 6–10% | 4–5% |
| Cheese yield (per gallon milk) | High (rich fat) | Moderate |
| Feed requirement/day | 0.5–1 lb grain + browse | 2–3 lb grain + browse |
| Space needed | 200 sq ft/goat | 400–600 sq ft/goat |
| Tether-friendly | Yes | Difficult |
| Kid size | Small (less meat) | Good meat kids |

**Milk production math (Nubian doe)**:
- Peak production: 3–4 quarts/day, months 2–4 of lactation.
- Typical lactation: 10 months (breed annually; dry 2 months before kidding).
- Annual milk: **600–900 gallons** from a high-production doe; more typically **400–600 gallons**.
- From 1 gallon milk: ~1 lb hard cheese, or 1.5 lb soft cheese (chèvre), or 3.5 lb ricotta.
- A single Nubian doe provides full dairy needs for 1–2 adults plus excess for cheese.

**Goats must not be kept alone** — they are highly social and deteriorate alone.
Minimum: 2 animals. Second doe, a wether (castrated male), or companion species.

**Browse vs. hay**:
- Goats are browsers (prefer shrubs, weeds, vines) not grazers.
- 1 goat requires **0.5–1 acre of browse** or supplemental hay year-round.
- Hay requirement (winter or supplemental): ~3–4 lb/day for a 150-lb doe.
- Annual hay: **500–700 lb/year per goat** if pasture is limited. At $8–$15/bale
  (50 lb): **$80–$210/year**.

**Fencing for goats**: This is the primary challenge. Goats test every fence.
Minimum: 4-ft woven wire (2×4 in openings) with one electric offset wire at
nose height. High-tensile electric (4–5 strands) also works. **Budget
$1.50–$3.00/linear foot for perimeter fencing**.

**Startup cost**: 2 Nigerian Dwarf does (bred): $150–$400 each. Shelter: $200–$600.
Fence: $300–$800 for 100-foot perimeter. **Total: $800–$2,200**.

### 4.4 Pigs: Efficient Bulk Protein and Fat

Pigs are a seasonal livestock system — buy spring feeders, raise through summer
and fall, slaughter before winter. No year-round commitment required.

**Feed conversion and timeline**:
- Piglet (feeder pig at 8 weeks): 30–50 lb live weight.
- Market weight: 250 lb live (180–190 lb hanging weight; ~130 lb retail cuts).
- Time from feeder pig to market weight: **4–6 months** on good feed.
- Feed conversion ratio: **3.5:1 to 4:1** (lb feed per lb gain).
- To produce 250 lb live pig: ~750–1,000 lb of feed.
- Commercial feed: $0.30–$0.50/lb → **$225–$500 in feed costs/pig**.
- On pasture + garden surpluses + whey (from goat cheese): significantly lower.

**Breeds for homestead**: Berkshire, Tamworth, Duroc for good meat quality and
hardiness. Commercial breeds (Yorkshire, Landrace) grow faster but less suited
to outdoor conditions.

**Pasture rotation**: 2 pigs per 1/4 acre; rotate every 4–6 weeks to prevent
overrooting. Pigs are excellent soil cultivators — use them to break new ground
before garden establishment.

**Startup cost per pig**: Feeder pig: $75–$200. Processing (if hired): $200–$400.
Total per pig: $400–$1,000, producing 130 lb retail-equivalent meat.
Self-processing eliminates that cost; skills required.

### 4.6 Small-Scale Aquaculture

**IBC tote aquaponics** (beginner-level):
- 275-gallon IBC tote = basic fish tank + media bed on top for vegetables.
- Fish: **tilapia** (warm water, 65°F minimum; ideal 75–85°F) or **bluegill/catfish**

- Stocking density: 0.5–1 lb live fish per gallon → 100–150 lb fish in 275-gal tote.
- Fingerlings to harvest weight: **6–8 months** for tilapia (1 lb); 10–14 months for catfish.
- Feed conversion: tilapia at **1.5:1 to 1.7:1** (most efficient of all species).
- Annual harvest from single IBC system: **50–100 lb fish** + vegetables from media bed.

## Section 5: Seed Saving

Seed saving closes the loop on food production: a homesteader who saves seeds is
genuinely independent of the commercial supply chain. **Open-pollinated (OP) and
heirloom varieties** are the foundation; hybrid (F1) varieties cannot be reliably
saved.

### 5.1 Open-Pollinated vs. Hybrid

**Open-pollinated (OP)**: Pollinated by wind, insects, or self. Seed saved from
OP plants grows true-to-type (with correct isolation). All heirlooms are OP.

**Hybrid (F1)**: Deliberately crossed between two parent lines to express specific
traits (uniformity, disease resistance, yield). Seed from F1 plants is unpredictable —
may revert to either parent or produce weak plants. **Never save F1 seed**.

**Identifying hybrid seeds on a packet**: Look for "F1" on the label. If absent,
it's likely OP. "Heirloom" always means OP.

**Why it matters**: If your seed supply is cut off (supply chain failure, financial
collapse, or seed company discontinues a variety), OP seed-savers can replant
indefinitely. F1 growers must repurchase annually.

### 5.2 Isolation Distances by Crop Family

Cross-pollination between varieties of the same species produces off-type seeds.
Maintain these minimum separation distances between varieties you intend to save
seed from (or grow only one variety of each cross-pollinating species per season):

| Crop family | Examples | Pollination method | Minimum isolation (home scale) | Strict isolation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Brassicaceae | Cabbage, broccoli, kale, collards, kohlrabi, turnips | Insect | 300 ft | 1,000 ft |
| Cucurbitaceae | Cucumbers, squash, melons, pumpkins | Insect | 500 ft | 1,500 ft |
| Solanaceae | Tomatoes, peppers, eggplant | Self-pollinating | 10–35 ft | 150 ft |
| Alliaceae (Amaryllidaceae) | Onions, leeks, shallots | Insect | 500 ft | 1,000 ft |
| Fabaceae | Beans, peas | Self-pollinating | 10–25 ft | 150 ft |
| Asteraceae | Lettuce, chicory | Self-pollinating mostly | 25 ft | 200 ft |
| Chenopodiaceae | Beets, chard, spinach | Wind | 1,000 ft | 5 miles |
| Poaceae | Corn | Wind | 1,000 ft | 2 miles (for strict purity) |

**Important note on Cucurbita species**: Different Cucurbita **species** do not
cross. A Cucurbita pepo (acorn squash, pumpkin) will cross with another C. pepo
(zucchini) but NOT with C. maxima (hubbard, buttercup) or C. moschata (butternut).
Plan which species you grow to avoid within-species crosses.

**Practical approach**: Grow only one variety per species per season for any crop
you intend to save seed from. Stagger planting to offset flowering times if space
is limited.

### 5.3 Seed Storage: Viability Table

**Ideal seed storage conditions**: 32–50°F, below 50% relative humidity, dark.
The sum of temperature (°F) + relative humidity (%) should stay below 100 for
maximum longevity. A sealed glass jar with a silica gel packet in a cool, dark
location achieves this in most climates.

| Crop | Typical viability (years) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Onion / leek | 1–2 years | Shortest-lived; save fresh annually |
| Parsnip | 1–2 years | Replace every year |
| Corn | 2–3 years | Test germination before planting |
| Spinach | 3–4 years | |
| Carrot | 3–4 years | |
| Lettuce | 3–5 years | |
| Pea | 3–5 years | |
| Bean | 3–5 years | |
| Tomato | 4–6 years | Very reliable with good storage |
| Pepper | 4–5 years | |
| Cucumber | 5–7 years | |

### 5.4 Wet vs. Dry Processing

**Dry processing** (beans, peas, corn, brassicas, lettuce, onion):
- Leave seed on plant until fully dry and mature.
- Harvest stalks/pods; thresh by beating against a bucket or rubbing between hands.
- Winnow (pour between two buckets in a breeze) to separate seed from chaff.
- Dry additional 1–2 weeks on paper in a warm room before sealing.

**Wet processing** (tomatoes, cucumbers, squash):
- Tomatoes: squeeze seeds + gel into a jar of water; let ferment 2–3 days at room
  temperature. The gel coat (germination inhibitor) breaks down. Rinse; spread on
  paper to dry. Seeds are viable when they snap, not bend.
- Cucumbers, squash: leave fruit until overripe (seeds mature fully). Scoop seeds;
  rinse; dry on a screen 1–2 weeks.

### 5.5 Building a Seed Bank

**Minimum viable seed bank for a 2-adult homestead**:
- One season's worth of each annual crop you grow.
- Minimum packet sizes: 25 seeds for large-seeded crops (squash, beans);
  200–500 seeds for small-seeded crops (tomato, pepper, carrot).
- Label every envelope: crop, variety, harvest year.

**Selection criteria when saving seed**:
1. Choose the best-performing plants, not average ones.
2. Select for traits important to you: disease resistance, taste, storage life, productivity.
3. Let multiple plants set seed (not just one) to maintain genetic diversity.
4. For tomatoes: choose from fully ripe fruit of plants that ripened earliest, had
   highest yield, or showed disease resistance.

**Quantity to save per crop**:

| Crop | Minimum seeds to save | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Tomato | 50–100 | 3–4 fruits gives ample seed |
| Pepper | 50–100 | 2–3 fully ripe fruits |
| Bean | 100–200 seeds (~0.5 lb) | Multiple plants; variety maintenance |
| Corn | 200–400 seeds | Minimum 100 plants for pollination; save from center of block |
| Squash | 50–100 | 1–2 fruits; clean thoroughly |
| Brassicas | 200–500 | Let 3–5 plants bolt; extensive seed |
| Carrot | 500–1,000 | Small seeds; save generously |

## Section 6: Soil Systems

**Soil is not dirt — it is a living ecosystem.** A productive garden depends on
billions of microorganisms, fungi, earthworms, and arthropods that convert organic
matter into plant-available nutrients. Every soil decision should support this
biology, not destroy it.

### 6.1 Starting Soil Assessment

Before amending anything, assess what you have. A basic soil test ($20–$50 at
most extension services, or $30–$100 from a private lab) reveals:

- **pH**: Most vegetables prefer 6.0–7.0. Blueberries: 4.5–5.5. Potatoes:
  5.0–6.0 (helps suppress scab).
- **Macro-nutrients**: Nitrogen (N), Phosphorus (P), Potassium (K).
- **Organic matter %**: Typical agricultural soil: 1–3%. Target: 5%+.
- **Cation exchange capacity (CEC)**: Measures soil's ability to hold nutrients.
  Sandy soils: low CEC; clay soils: high CEC.
- **Texture (feel test)**: Roll a moist handful. Forms ribbon? Clay-heavy. Falls apart?
  Sandy. Stays together but doesn't ribbon? Loam.

**pH amendment guide**:
- Too acidic (below 6.0): Add agricultural limestone (calcitic or dolomitic).
  50–100 lb/1,000 sq ft raises pH ~0.5 points. Effects take 3–6 months.
- Too alkaline (above 7.5): Add elemental sulfur. 10 lb/1,000 sq ft lowers pH ~0.5
  points; take 3–6 months. Also add organic matter (acidifies over time).

### 6.2 Building Fertility

**Compost is the foundation of soil fertility in a closed system.**

#### Compost System Design

**Hot pile method** (fastest, kills weed seeds and pathogens):
- **Minimum size**: 3 ft × 3 ft × 3 ft (27 cubic feet). Smaller piles cannot
  sustain thermophilic temperatures.
- **Optimal size**: 4 ft × 4 ft × 4 ft to 5 ft × 5 ft × 5 ft.
- **Target internal temperature**: 131–160°F for 3 consecutive days (kills weed
  seeds and most pathogens). Probe with a long-stem thermometer.
- **C:N ratio**: Target 25:1 to 30:1.

**C:N ratios of common materials**:

| Material | C:N ratio | Category |
|---|---|---|
| Fresh grass clippings | 15–20:1 | Green (N-rich) |
| Kitchen vegetable scraps | 15–20:1 | Green |
| Chicken manure (fresh) | 7–10:1 | Green |
| Coffee grounds | 20:1 | Green |
| Garden trimmings (fresh) | 25–30:1 | Borderline |
| Straw (wheat, oat) | 75–150:1 | Brown (C-rich) |
| Dry corn stalks | 60–100:1 | Brown |
| Cardboard (plain) | 350–500:1 | Brown |
| Sawdust | 250–500:1 | Brown |
| Dry leaves | 40–80:1 | Brown |
| Wood chips | 100–500:1 | Brown |

**Building a hot pile**: Alternate 2–3" layers of greens (kitchen scraps, fresh
manure, grass) with 4–6" layers of browns (straw, leaves, cardboard). Maintain
moisture of a "wrung-out sponge." Turn every 3–7 days for fastest decomposition.

**Timeline**:
- Active hot composting with turning: **4–8 weeks** to finished compost.
- Passive cool composting (no turning): **6–18 months**.
- Finished compost: dark, crumbly, smells like forest floor, no identifiable
  original materials.

**Application rate**: 2–4 inches of finished compost worked into beds annually.
1,000 sq ft garden needs 170–340 cubic feet of compost = **6.3–12.6 cubic yards/year**.
Plan multiple piles in rotation to meet this demand.

#### Vermicomposting (Worm Castings)

**Red wiggler worms** (Eisenia fetida) convert kitchen scraps into
**worm castings** — the highest-quality soil amendment available. Castings contain
plant-available nutrients, beneficial microorganisms, and humic acids.

- Bin size for 2-adult household kitchen waste: 2×2×1 ft (minimum).
- Worm stocking: 1 lb worms per sq ft of surface area.
- Feed: kitchen scraps (no meat, dairy, citrus in excess). Cover with damp newspaper.
- Temperature range: 55–80°F. Keep indoors in winter.
- Time to finished castings: **2–3 months** from feeding.
- **Castings application rate**: 10–20% by volume in potting mixes; top-dress at
  1/4 inch per 100 sq ft around plants.

#### Cover Crops

**Cover crops** suppress weeds, prevent erosion, add organic matter, and (with
legumes) fix atmospheric nitrogen.

| Season | Cover crop | Function | Days before termination | N-fixation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Fall (Zone 5) | Winter rye | Biomass; erosion control | Till/cut at anthesis (spring) | None |
| Fall | Hairy vetch | N-fixation; winter-kill or till | Till before flowering | 80–150 lb N/acre |
| Fall | Winter wheat | Biomass; grain option | Harvest or till spring | None |
| Spring | Field peas | N-fixation; quick growth | Terminate at 50% flowering | 60–140 lb N/acre |
| Spring | Crimson clover | N-fixation; pollinator | Terminate at 50% flowering | 70–130 lb N/acre |
| Summer | Buckwheat | Smothers weeds; attracts beneficials; kills at frost | Till before seed set | None |
| Summer | Sorghum-sudangrass | Biomass; heat-tolerant | Terminate before maturity | None |
| Summer | Cowpeas | N-fixation; heat-tolerant | Terminate before maturity | 100–180 lb N/acre |

**Practical schedule for a Zone 5 homestead**:
```
Early spring (March–April): Sow field peas or crimson clover in empty beds
May: Terminate cover crops; plant main crops after 2-week waiting period
Late summer (August): After early crops harvested, sow buckwheat
September: Terminate buckwheat; sow hairy vetch + winter rye mix
Following spring: Terminate vetch/rye mix; plant season's crops
```

### 6.3 No-Till vs. Tilling Tradeoffs

| Factor | No-till / broadfork | Mechanical rototilling |
|---|---|---|
| Soil fungal network | Preserved | Disrupted (takes 1–2 years to rebuild) |
| Earthworm populations | Maintained | Reduced temporarily |
| Weed seeds | Fewer brought to surface | Buried seeds germinate — weed flush |
| Compaction | Reduced over time | Can create plow pan at tilling depth |
| Organic matter | Stays in layers | Incorporated; faster short-term decomposition |
| Time/effort | More up-front bed building; less ongoing | Faster initial bed prep; ongoing tillage required |
| Equipment cost | Broadfork $80–$200 | Rototiller $300–$2,000 (or hire $100–$200/pass) |

**No-till method**: Lay cardboard (suppresses weeds); cover with 6–8 inches of
compost or wood chips. Plant directly. Over time, cardboard breaks down and
earthworms integrate layers.

**Broadfork**: A two-handled fork with 12–14" tines. Pushed into soil, rocked
back — aerates and loosens without inverting. The workhorse of no-till intensive
systems. **Cost: $80–$200**.

### 6.4 Raised Beds vs. In-Ground vs. Hugelkultur

| System | Best for | Advantages | Disadvantages | Cost |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Raised beds (framing) | Small intensive gardens; poor native soil | Drainage control; frost extension; ergonomics; fast establishment | Material cost (lumber); limited scale; can dry out fast | $1–$5/linear ft lumber |
| In-ground rows | Large-scale; grain crops; potatoes | No material cost; large scale; easier mechanization | Poor drainage on clay; weeds; slower establishment | Minimal |
| Hugelkultur | Hilly or wet terrain; debris use | Moisture retention; fertility from decomposing wood; no inputs | 2–3 year wait for wood to begin decomposing; initial labor | Free (uses on-site debris) |

**Raised bed sizing**: 4-ft width is the ergonomic maximum (allows reaching the
center from either side). Length is flexible; 8–12 ft is common. Height: 6–12
inches for most crops; 18–24 inches for deep roots (carrots, parsnips) or very
poor subsoil.

**Hugelkultur mound construction**:
1. Dig trench 12–18 inches deep.
2. Fill with logs, branches, woody debris (any species; avoid black walnut and
   black locust which decompose very slowly).
3. Cover with upside-down sod, compost, and soil.
4. Mound height: 3–6 feet at construction; settles to 18–36 inches as wood decomposes.
5. Plant into sides and top. Crops in years 1–2: moisture-hungry crops that benefit
   from fungal root zone (squash, beans). By year 3–5: very fertile, moisture-retentive.

### 6.5 Biochar

**Biochar** is charcoal produced from organic materials under low-oxygen conditions
(**pyrolysis**). Unlike charcoal burned for fuel, biochar is incorporated into soil
where it persists for **hundreds to thousands of years**, increasing CEC,
water-holding capacity, and microbial habitat.

**Making biochar (retort method)**:
- Requires: a sealed metal container (steel drum or pipe) inside a fire.
- Fill inner container with dry woody material (chips, sticks, cobs).
- Build fire around outer container. Heat drives off volatile gases through a
  small vent; if vent emissions are routed back into the fire, these gases combust
  (improving efficiency and reducing smoke).
- Process time: 2–4 hours at 500–900°F.
- Result: **10–25% of starting wood weight** as biochar (the rest off-gasses).
- A 55-gallon drum fire: produces 5–15 lb biochar per burn.

**Inoculating biochar before use** (critical step):
- Raw biochar is sterile and will initially absorb soil nutrients.
- Mix biochar with compost tea, liquid fertilizer, or compost at 1:1 by volume.
- Let sit 2–4 weeks before incorporating into soil.
- Alternatively: add to compost pile during hot phase; worms will inoculate it.

**Application rate**: 2–5% by soil volume (1–2 inches per 12-inch depth) is typical.
For a garden bed 4×8 ft × 8 inches deep: 170 lb soil volume; add 3–8 lb biochar.
**Over-application (more than 10% by volume) can reduce plant growth** by binding
nutrients excessively in un-inoculated form.

## Section 8: Water for Food Production

### 8.1 Garden Water Requirements

A garden requires **1–2 inches of water per week** during peak growing season
(equivalent to 0.6–1.2 gallons per sq ft per week). This comes from rainfall plus
irrigation deficit.

**Water need calculation**:

```
Garden area:          1,000 sq ft
Target: 1.5 in/week  = 1.5 in / 12 in × 1,000 sq ft × 7.48 gal/cu ft
                      = 1.5 × 1,000 × 7.48 / 12
                      = 935 gallons/week at peak

Seasonal irrigation window: 16 weeks (typical Zone 5)
If average rainfall covers 50% of need:
  Irrigation need: 935 × 0.5 × 16 = 7,480 gallons/season per 1,000 sq ft

For a 1/2-acre garden (21,780 sq ft):
  Irrigation need: 7,480 × 21.78 = ~163,000 gallons/season
  (reduced to ~80,000 with mulch + drip; see below)
```

### 8.2 Rainwater Harvesting for Irrigation

**Collection math**:
```
Roof collection: 1 inch of rain on 1,000 sq ft = 623 gallons
(accounting for 5% loss: 0.95 × 1,000 × 0.623 gal/sq ft/inch)

To supply 1/4-acre garden (10,890 sq ft) irrigation:
  Seasonal need (with mulch): ~30,000 gallons
  Annual rainfall (Zone 5, 35 in/year): 35 × 623 = 21,805 gal/1,000 sq ft roof

  Required roof area: 30,000 / 21.8 = ~1,375 sq ft collection area
  A 40×35 ft barn roof = 1,400 sq ft ✓

For storage tank sizing: store 2× the longest expected dry period.
  Typical Zone 5 dry period: 3–4 weeks
  Weekly garden need: ~1,900 gal (10,890 sq ft × 1.5 in / 12 × 7.48 × 50% rain dependency)
  Storage: 2 × 4 weeks × 1,900 = ~15,200 gallons
  Use: 3× 5,000-gallon cisterns or 1 large ferro-cement tank
```

### 8.3 Drip Irrigation vs. Overhead

| Method | Water efficiency | Cost | Best for | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Drip tape (in-row) | 85–95% | $0.10–$0.30/ft | Row crops; beds | Clogs with sediment; filter required |
| Soaker hose | 80–90% | $0.30–$0.60/ft | Beds; shrubs | Higher flow than drip tape |
| Overhead sprinkler | 60–75% | $0.50–$2.00/ft | Lawns; broadcast | Water on leaves; disease risk in humid climates |
| Flood / furrow | 40–60% | Minimal | Field crops; orchards | Massive water use; simple infrastructure |
| Hand watering (hose) | 70–80% | Minimal | Small areas | Time-intensive; inconsistent |

**Drip recommendation**: A 1,000 sq ft garden with drip tape saves **200–400 gallons
per week** vs. overhead. Over a 16-week season: 3,200–6,400 gallons saved. At $0.01/gallon
collection cost, the drip system pays for itself in 1–2 seasons.

### 8.4 Mulching for Water Retention

**3–4 inches of organic mulch** (wood chips, straw, leaves) reduces soil moisture
evaporation by **50–70%** and suppresses weeds simultaneously.

| Mulch material | Depth | Water retention | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Straw | 4 in | 50–60% reduction | Cheap; sourced from farms; may contain seeds |
| Wood chips (arborist) | 4 in | 60–70% reduction | Often free; best for perennials and paths |
| Leaves (shredded) | 3 in | 40–55% reduction | Free; mow to shred; mat if thick |
| Grass clippings | 2 in | 40–50% reduction | Mat and anaerobe if too thick; layer thin |
| Black plastic | Full cover | 80–90% reduction | Kills weeds; warms soil; not organic; disposal issue |

**Practical guideline**: A mulched garden bed needs watering **every 7–10 days**
vs. every 2–4 days for bare soil in summer. In Zone 5 with 35 in/year rainfall
and good mulching, irrigation may be minimal in most years.

## Section 10: Disaster Scenarios

### 10.1 Crop Failure: Diversification and Emergency Storage

**Cause**: A single-system garden that fails (late blight destroys all tomatoes;
late frost kills all beans; hail destroys all above-ground crops) can eliminate a
season's production.

**Prevention through diversification**:
- Never rely on a single crop for more than 30% of caloric production.
- Grow at least 8–10 different crops each year.
- Include underground storage crops (potatoes, sweet potatoes, beets) that
  are protected from hail and many above-ground diseases.
- Stagger plantings so all of one crop is not at the same vulnerable stage.

**Emergency food storage minimum** (3-month supply, 2 adults):

```
Target: 90 days × 5,000 cal/day = 450,000 calories

Approximate store:
  Dry beans: 100 lb × 1,500 cal/lb = 150,000 cal
  White rice: 75 lb × 1,600 cal/lb = 120,000 cal
  Oats: 50 lb × 1,600 cal/lb = 80,000 cal
  Wheat or cornmeal: 50 lb × 1,500 cal/lb = 75,000 cal
  Cooking oil: 5 gallons × ~30,000 cal/gal = 150,000 cal
  Honey/sugar: 20 lb × 1,500 cal/lb = 30,000 cal
  Total: ~605,000 cal (covers 90 days + buffer)

Storage: 5-gallon food-grade buckets with oxygen absorbers
Cost: ~$300–$600 for this quantity at current prices
Shelf life: 10–25 years with O2 absorbers
```

### 10.2 Extended Drought: Water Conservation

**Signs you are in drought stress**:
- Soil dries 1 inch deep within 2 days of watering.
- Plants wilt before midday and do not fully recover overnight.
- Soil temperature above 90°F at 2-inch depth.

**Response protocol**:
1. Install all available drip irrigation immediately. Eliminate overhead watering.
2. Apply 4-inch mulch layer to every planted bed.
3. Shade cloth (30–50% shade cloth) reduces transpiration demand by 30–40%.
4. Prioritize watering: calorie crops first (potatoes, beans), then storage crops,
   then greens.
5. Let some greens bolt and save seed rather than watering through failure.
6. **Drought-tolerant varieties**: Cowpeas, amaranth, sorghum, and tepary beans
   tolerate 30–40% less water than standard varieties. Switch annual seed purchases
   toward these.

**Shade cloth sizing**: A 50% shade cloth on a 100 sq ft bed reduces transpiration
by ~25–30% and reduces soil temperature by 10–15°F. Cost: $30–$80 for 100 sq ft.

### 10.3 Pest and Disease Outbreak

**Integrated Pest Management (IPM) hierarchy**:
1. **Prevention** (primary): Healthy soil → healthy plants → natural pest resistance.
   A plant growing in fertile, well-drained soil with correct spacing resists pests
   better than a stressed plant.
2. **Physical barriers**: Row cover (Reemay), copper tape for slugs, sticky traps,
   hand-picking large pests (hornworms, beetles).
3. **Biological controls**: Encourage beneficial insects (plant dill, fennel, yarrow,
   alyssum). Introduce lacewings, parasitic wasps (available by mail).
4. **Organic sprays**: Neem oil (broad-spectrum; disrupt insect hormones), insecticidal
   soap (soft-bodied insects), diatomaceous earth (mechanical kill of crawling insects),
   Bt (Bacillus thuringiensis) for caterpillar control.
5. **Chemical controls**: Last resort; avoid in soil you're building.

**Crop rotation for disease prevention**:
- **Rule**: Never plant the same crop family in the same bed more than once every
  3–4 years.
- **Brassicas** (cabbage, broccoli, kale): clubroot and black rot persist in soil
  4+ years. Strict 4-year rotation.
- **Solanaceae** (tomato, potato, pepper): late blight and early blight spores
  overwinter. 3-year minimum rotation.
- **Cucurbits** (squash, cucumber): powdery mildew spores persist; rotate annually.

**Basic 4-bed rotation plan**:
```
Bed 1: Brassicas this year → Legumes (year 2) → Roots (year 3) → Solanaceae (year 4)
Bed 2: Legumes this year → Roots (year 2) → Solanaceae (year 3) → Brassicas (year 4)
Bed 3: Roots this year → Solanaceae (year 2) → Brassicas (year 3) → Legumes (year 4)
Bed 4: Solanaceae this year → Brassicas (year 2) → Legumes (year 3) → Roots (year 4)
```

### 10.4 Post-Nuclear Contamination

*For full nuclear event protocols, see 15-disaster-scenarios.md.*
This section covers food production-specific considerations.

**Contamination timeline and food safety**:

| Time after detonation | Radiation level | Food production status |
|---|---|---|
| 0–48 hours | Extremely high | Shelter in place; do not enter garden |
| 48 hours–2 weeks | Declining rapidly (7-10 rule) | Remain sheltered; do not harvest |
| 2–4 weeks | Survivable with precautions | Outdoor activity possible; avoid food from open area |
| 1–3 months | Substantially reduced | Begin assessment; see guidance below |
| 6–12 months | Long-lived isotopes dominate | Cs-137 (30-yr half-life) and Sr-90 (29-yr half-life) require soil testing |

**Which crops are safest post-contamination**:

| Crop type | Relative contamination risk | Reasoning |
|---|---|---|
| Root vegetables (potatoes, carrots, beets) | Lower | Soil protects; peel before eating |
| Grain (corn, wheat, dry beans) | Moderate | Harvest after 3–6 months; shell and clean |
| Tree fruit | Moderate | Wash thoroughly; peel; risk from air deposition |
| Leafy greens (kale, lettuce, spinach) | Highest | Large surface area; direct deposition |
| Milk (from grazing animals) | High | Grazing animals concentrate I-131 (shorter term) and Cs-137 |
| Mushrooms | Very high | Hyperaccumulators of Cs-137; avoid |

**Decontamination protocols for garden beds**:
1. Remove the top 1–2 inches of soil (majority of fallout particles).
2. Deep till (turn to 12 inches): dilutes surface contamination by 10–20x.
3. Apply high potassium fertilizer: potassium competes with Cs-137 uptake in plants.
4. Zeolite (clinoptilolite) application: binds Cs-137 and Sr-90 in soil.
   Application rate: 50 lb/100 sq ft.
5. Raised beds with imported clean soil are safest for short-term production
   (build 12-inch raised beds above contaminated ground).

**Pre-positioned supplies for contamination scenario**:
- Emergency food store (see Section 10.1) covers 90+ days while fallout decays.
- Pre-filled water cisterns (covered before event) are safe; open rain collection
  is contaminated for 30–60 days post-event.
- Seed bank (sealed jars, stored indoors or underground): uncontaminated seeds
  for replanting post-crisis.
- **Potassium iodide (KI)** protects thyroid from I-131 only; does not protect
  from other isotopes. Single-dose adults: 130 mg. Take within hours of exposure;
  not after exposure has occurred.

## Section 11: Synthesis and Year-by-Year Plan

### Year 1 Priorities

1. Soil assessment: test pH and basic nutrients before planting anything.
2. Establish 1/4-acre annual garden: focus on potatoes, beans, squash, tomatoes, greens.
3. Build compost system: minimum two hot piles rotating.
4. Get 6–8 laying hens established.
5. Plant perennials: 4–6 fruit trees, 10–20 berry bushes, hazelnuts.
6. Build 3-month emergency food store (dry goods, sealed buckets).

### Year 2 Priorities

1. Expand annual garden based on year 1 data — increase what worked, reduce what failed.
2. Add rabbits (breeding trio) for protein.
3. Begin seed saving on 3–5 crops.
4. Establish hugelkultur bed(s) using on-site debris.
5. Plant 10–20 additional perennials; install food forest framework.
6. Assess irrigation needs; install drip tape if deficit watering is common.

### Year 3 Priorities

1. Add goat(s) if dairy is needed.
2. Begin seasonal pig operation.
3. Expand seed bank: saving from 10+ crops.
4. First significant fruit tree harvest (mulberry, plum, some apple varieties).
5. Assess soil organic matter: it should be measurably improving.
6. Fine-tune rotation and succession planting based on two full seasons of data.

### Years 5–10

- Food forest canopy closes: significant passive food production.
- Nut trees begin producing (hazelnut by year 4–5, chestnut by 6–8).
- Soil health at target: 5%+ organic matter, earthworm populations visible.
- Seed saving self-sufficient: no commercial seed purchases necessary.
- Annual vegetable production approaching 80%+ of household caloric needs.
