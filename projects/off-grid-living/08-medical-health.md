# Domain 8: Medical & Health

> **Scope**: Comprehensive off-grid medicine from wound care and dental emergencies to childbirth, CBRN scenarios, and chronic disease management. Written for a prepared homesteader with no background in medicine — not a replacement for training, but a knowledge foundation that makes training more effective and fills gaps when professionals are unavailable.
>
> **Central premise**: The goal is not to replace physicians. The goal is to prevent the 80% of emergencies that become fatal only because of delayed care, and to buy time for evacuation in the 20% that genuinely require a hospital.

---

## 8.1 Philosophy and Approach

### The Scope of Off-Grid Medicine

Off-grid medicine sits between wilderness medicine and austere-environment medicine. Wilderness medicine assumes you will reach a hospital within 24–72 hours; austere medicine assumes you may not reach one at all. The realistic homestead scenario is somewhere between: you probably can reach a hospital within 1–4 hours, but roads can be impassable, fuel can be unavailable, and situations do not always announce themselves at convenient times.

**What is realistic to handle without a hospital:**
- Most wounds: lacerations, abrasions, punctures, burns up to 2nd degree covering less than 10% TBSA
- Fractures of limb bones (not spine, pelvis, or femur shaft) with proper splinting
- Most infections: skin, respiratory, urinary tract, dental — with appropriate antibiotics
- Dehydration and most GI illness
- Dental emergencies: pain management, temporary fillings, abscess drainage
- Normal uncomplicated childbirth
- Mental health crises (stabilization, not definitive treatment)
- Chronic disease management for stable conditions

**What requires evacuation — always:**
- Chest pain with exertion, jaw pain, or sweating (possible MI) — minutes matter
- Stroke signs (FAST: Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech, Time to call) — 4.5-hour thrombolytic window
- Spinal injury with neurological signs (weakness, numbness, bowel/bladder changes)
- Open fractures (bone through skin) — infection risk is extreme
- Burns >15% TBSA, full thickness, or involving airway/face/hands/genitalia
- Peritonitis signs (rigid abdomen, rebound tenderness, fever) without capacity for surgical intervention
- Obstetric complications: preeclampsia, shoulder dystocia that does not resolve, placenta previa, prolapsed cord
- Psychiatric emergency: active suicidality with plan and means
- Any condition deteriorating despite treatment after 24–48 hours

### Decision Framework: Treat vs. Evacuate

Apply this in sequence:

1. **Is this immediately life-threatening?** Airway compromised, uncontrolled hemorrhage, tension pneumothorax, anaphylaxis → stabilize simultaneously with initiating evacuation.
2. **Is this time-critical?** Stroke, MI, spinal cord compression, ectopic pregnancy rupture → evacuate without delay even if stable.
3. **Can I manage this long-term without definitive care?** Stable fracture, moderate infection, dental pain → treat and monitor; evacuate if no improvement in 48–72 hours.
4. **What is the cost and risk of evacuation itself?** In winter conditions, evacuating may be more dangerous than sheltering and treating. Account for this.

### Terminology: Three Tiers

- **Wilderness First Aid (WFA)**: 16–20 hour course. Focuses on assessment and stabilization while awaiting evacuation. Does not include advanced skills.
- **Wilderness First Responder (WFR)**: 70–80 hour course. Covers prolonged field care, medication administration, advanced assessment. The practical standard for remote homesteads.
- **Austere medicine**: The additional body of knowledge for scenarios where evacuation is not possible within days to weeks. Includes improvised interventions, advanced wound management, field surgery basics.

## 8.2 First Aid Essentials

### Wound Care

**Irrigation is the most important intervention.** Infection prevention depends more on thorough irrigation than on antibiotic coverage. The mechanism: pressure dislodges bacteria and debris; antiseptic alone cannot penetrate established contamination.

**Optimal irrigation:** 1 liter of normal saline (or clean water) through an 18-gauge needle attached to a 35–60 mL syringe. This produces approximately 8 PSI of irrigation pressure — enough to clear bacteria, not so much as to damage tissue. Higher is not better; pressure greater than 15 PSI drives bacteria deeper.

**Improvised irrigation:** If you lack supplies, a plastic bag with a pinhole or a 1-liter water bottle with a pinhole cap approximates adequate pressure.

**Wound closure hierarchy** (from least to most invasive):
1. **Steri-strips / wound closure strips**: For small superficial lacerations, clean wounds, minimal tension. Dry skin thoroughly first. Apply perpendicular to wound, overlapping edges.
2. **Staples**: Faster than sutures, adequate for scalp and trunk wounds where cosmesis is secondary. Apply with surgical stapler; 5mm staples standard.
3. **Sutures**: For wounds under tension, gaping wounds, or areas requiring precise closure. Deep absorbable sutures (Vicryl 3-0) close dead space; surface non-absorbable (nylon 4-0 or prolene) close skin.

**Infection watch protocol:** Monitor all wounds for:
- Erythema (redness) expanding beyond wound margins — mark with pen and date to track
- Warmth and induration (hardening)
- Purulent discharge (pus)
- Red streaks radiating from wound = lymphangitis = systemic spread → antibiotics immediately
- Fever >38°C = systemic infection → broadened antibiotic coverage, consider evacuation

**Tetanus risk:** Any wound contaminated with soil, animal feces, rust, or debris has tetanus risk. If vaccination not current (within 5 years for high-risk wounds, 10 years for clean wounds), tetanus toxoid booster needed. Tetanus immune globulin (TIG) for unvaccinated patients with high-risk wounds. Stockpile Td or Tdap vaccine if long-term off-grid.

**Wounds NOT to close primarily (leave open):**
- Animal or human bite wounds
- Wounds older than 6–8 hours
- Highly contaminated wounds
- Puncture wounds (counter-intuitive but sealing anaerobic bacteria in is worse)
Close these wounds by secondary intention (pack loosely with moist gauze, allow to granulate from inside out).

### Burns

**Identify degree:**
- **First degree**: Red, painful, no blisters. Sunburn is the classic example. Epidermis only.
- **Second degree (partial thickness)**: Blisters, wet/weeping appearance, extremely painful. Epidermis + dermis involved.
- **Third degree (full thickness)**: White, brown, or black; leathery; painless (nerves destroyed). Requires skin grafting.

**Rule of Nines (adult):** Each region equals approximately:
- Head and neck: 9%
- Each arm: 9%
- Chest (anterior): 9%
- Abdomen (anterior): 9%
- Upper back: 9%
- Lower back: 9%
- Each thigh: 9%
- Each lower leg: 9%
- Genitalia: 1%

Evacuate if: >10% TBSA 2nd degree, any 3rd degree, burns to face/hands/feet/genitalia/airway.

**Treatment:**
1. Cool water for 20 minutes (not ice — causes vasoconstriction and worsens depth)
2. Do NOT apply butter, toothpaste, oil, or any food product — these trap heat and introduce bacteria
3. Silvadene (silver sulfadiazine 1%) cream if available — apply daily after cleaning, excellent antimicrobial
4. For 2nd degree: wrap loosely with non-adherent dressing (Telfa or petrolatum gauze), change every 1–2 days
5. Burns are painful — ibuprofen + acetaminophen alternating every 3 hours
6. Keep burn wound moist (dries slowly, re-epithelializes faster under moist conditions)

### Fractures and Sprains

**Ottawa Ankle Rules** (if ALL criteria met, X-ray not required — bone fracture unlikely):
- Can bear weight for 4 steps
- No point tenderness at posterior edge or tip of either malleolus
- No point tenderness at base of 5th metatarsal or navicular

**SAM splint application:**
1. Measure splint against uninjured limb
2. Pad bony prominences (malleoli, olecranon, radial head)
3. Mold splint to anatomical position (ankle at 90°, knee slightly flexed, wrist slight extension)
4. Apply with elastic bandage starting distal, ending proximal
5. Check CSM (Circulation, Sensation, Motion) before and immediately after — pulse distal to splint, sensation (sharp vs. dull in distal dermatome), motion of distal digits

**Traction splints for femur fractures:** Femur fractures can cause 1–2 liters of blood loss into the thigh. Traction reduces pain, reduces blood loss, and prevents fat embolism. Commercial: Sager or Kendrick traction device ($150–300). Improvised: rigid pole with traction applied via ankle hitch. Apply enough traction to restore limb length (typically 10–15 lbs).

**RICE protocol for sprains:** Rest, Ice (20 min on/20 min off, never direct contact), Compression (elastic bandage), Elevation above heart level. NSAIDs (ibuprofen 400–600 mg every 8 hours with food) reduce inflammation.

### Shock

**Recognition:**
- Altered mentation (confusion, agitation, then unconsciousness)
- Systolic BP <90 mmHg (radial pulse absent = roughly SBP <80)
- Heart rate >100 bpm
- Skin: pale, cool, clammy

**Approximate blood loss by class:**
- Class I (<15% = ~750 mL): minimal symptoms, normal BP
- Class II (15–30% = 750–1,500 mL): tachycardia, anxiety, delayed capillary refill
- Class III (30–40% = 1,500–2,000 mL): tachycardia, hypotension, altered mentation
- Class IV (>40% = >2,000 mL): life-threatening, immediate surgery required

**Treatment:**
- Control hemorrhage first (direct pressure, tourniquet)
- Lay patient flat, elevate legs 6–12 inches if no spinal injury suspected
- Maintain warmth (hypothermia worsens coagulopathy — the lethal triad)
- IV fluid resuscitation if available: 1–2 L NS or LR to restore BP, then slow to KVO rate
- Avoid aggressive fluid resuscitation in penetrating trauma (permissive hypotension: SBP ~80–90 is adequate)

### Airway Management

**Basic maneuvers (always try first):**
- **Head-tilt chin-lift**: For unconscious patient without spinal injury. Tilt head back, lift chin.
- **Jaw thrust**: For possible spinal injury. Place fingers behind angle of jaw, thrust forward.
- **Recovery position**: Unconscious but breathing, no spinal injury — lateral position to prevent aspiration.

**Obstruction:**
- Conscious adult with airway obstruction: abdominal thrusts (Heimlich maneuver)
- Infant: 5 back blows + 5 chest thrusts (no abdominal thrusts)
- Unconscious: sweep mouth for visible foreign body, attempt rescue breaths, if unsuccessful — blind finger sweep risks pushing object deeper; proceed to CPR

**Improvised airway adjuncts:**
- **Nasopharyngeal airway (NPA)**: Soft rubber tube inserted through nostril; measured from nostril to earlobe; lubricate with water-based lubricant. Contraindicated in facial fractures or coagulopathy.
- **Oropharyngeal airway (OPA)**: Hard plastic; only in deeply unconscious patients (gag reflex absent). Size: corner of mouth to earlobe.
- **Cricothyrotomy** (last resort, advanced skill): Horizontal incision through cricothyroid membrane (between thyroid and cricoid cartilage in midline), insert improvised tube (pen casing, IV tubing). Used when intubation is impossible and patient will die without airway.

## 8.4 Herbal Medicine

**Evidence levels used below:**
- **Strong**: Multiple RCTs with consistent results
- **Moderate**: Some clinical trials with mixed or limited results
- **Traditional/Low**: Historical use, animal studies, minimal human clinical data

### 1. Yarrow — *Achillea millefolium*

**Treats:** Wound bleeding (topical), fever (diaphoretic), menstrual irregularity
**Evidence:** Moderate (topical hemostasis), Traditional (systemic)
**Growing:** Full sun, well-drained soil; extremely vigorous; direct sow or transplant. Zones 3–9.
**Preparation:**
- Fresh leaf poultice: crush leaf, apply directly to bleeding wound; the traditional field bandage
- Tea (fever): 1 Tbsp dried aerial parts, steep 10–15 min covered, drink 3×/day
- Tincture: 1:5 ratio in 40% alcohol (vodka works); 2–4 mL 3×/day
**Contraindications:** Avoid in pregnancy (uterine stimulant). Ragweed allergy cross-reaction possible.

### 2. Plantain — *Plantago major* (broadleaf) / *P. lanceolata* (narrowleaf)

**Treats:** Insect stings, minor wounds, splinters, coughs, UTI (mild, preventive)
**Evidence:** Moderate (wound healing), Traditional (cough)
**Growing:** Weedy; probably already in your lawn. Disturbed soil, partial shade.
**Preparation:**
- Chew and apply poultice: fastest field application for sting or bite
- Salve: infuse in oil (olive oil, 4 weeks cold infusion or 2 hr warm), strain, add beeswax (1 oz per cup oil)
- Tea for cough: 1–2 Tbsp fresh or dried leaf, steep 15 min
**Contraindications:** None significant. Possible blood thinner interaction in very large doses.

### 3. Comfrey — *Symphytum officinale*

**Treats:** Bone healing (fractures, sprains), wound healing, bruises — EXTERNAL USE ONLY
**Evidence:** Moderate (topical; clinical trials show accelerated healing)
**Critical distinction:** Comfrey root contains higher pyrrolizidine alkaloids (PA) than leaf. Both are for EXTERNAL use only — internal use causes hepatotoxicity. Do not apply to open wounds (traps infection by healing surface too fast).
**Growing:** Zones 3–9, deep tap root, extremely vigorous, partial shade tolerant. Use Russian comfrey (*S. × uplandicum*) — lower PA content.
**Preparation:**
- Root poultice: grate fresh root, apply over intact skin on fracture or sprain, wrap; change daily
- Infused oil and salve: dried leaf in olive oil, infuse 4 weeks, strain, add beeswax
- Do NOT make internal tinctures

### 4. Elderberry — *Sambucus nigra*

**Treats:** Upper respiratory illness (shortened duration), fever, influenza
**Evidence:** Moderate-Strong (several RCTs show 2–4 day reduction in flu duration)
**Note:** All parts of the plant except ripe berries and flowers are toxic. Cook berries before consumption (destroys sambunigrin).
**Growing:** Zones 3–9, moist soil, full to partial sun; fast-growing shrub; harvest second year. Plant 2 for cross-pollination.
**Preparation:**
- Elderberry syrup: 1 cup dried berries (or 2 cups fresh) + 3 cups water + 1 cup honey, simmer 45 min, mash, strain, store refrigerated 6 weeks or process in water bath canner
- Therapeutic dose: 1 Tbsp syrup 4×/day at illness onset; 1 tsp/day preventive during outbreak
- Tincture: 1:5 in 50% alcohol
**Contraindications:** Autoimmune conditions (immune stimulant effect). Do not eat raw unripe berries.

### 5. Echinacea — *Echinacea purpurea* / *E. angustifolia*

**Treats:** Upper respiratory immune support, wound healing (topical)
**Evidence:** Moderate (reduces duration/severity of colds, not prevention)
**Growing:** Zones 3–9, full sun, well-drained. *E. purpurea* easiest to grow; use both root and aerial parts. Harvest root in fall of third year.
**Preparation:**
- Tincture root: 1:5 in 60% alcohol; 2–4 mL 3–5×/day at illness onset; use for max 10 days, then rest 3 days
- Tea: 1 tsp dried root per cup, simmer 20 min; or 1 Tbsp aerial parts, steep 15 min
**Contraindications:** Autoimmune disease, immunosuppressant drugs. Allergy to daisy family (Asteraceae).

### 6. St. John's Wort — *Hypericum perforatum*

**Treats:** Mild-to-moderate depression (clinically), nerve pain, topical wound healing
**Evidence:** Strong (depression — multiple meta-analyses show equivalence to SSRIs for mild/moderate); Moderate (nerve pain)
**Growing:** Full sun, well-drained, Zones 5–9. Weedy in many areas. Harvest flowers when buds are just opening (red oil comes from flower buds, not leaves).
**Preparation:**
- Oil infusion: pack fresh flower buds in olive oil, leave in sun 4–6 weeks (turns deep red); use topically for nerve pain, bruises
- Tincture: 1:5 in 60% alcohol; standard dose 2–4 mL 3×/day; takes 4–6 weeks for antidepressant effect
**Contraindications:** CRITICAL drug interaction — major CYP3A4 inducer. Reduces plasma levels of: HIV antiretrovirals, cyclosporine, oral contraceptives, warfarin, digoxin, many chemotherapy agents. Do not use with serotonergic medications (serotonin syndrome risk). Photosensitization (light skin + sun exposure may cause burns).

### 7. Valerian — *Valeriana officinalis*

**Treats:** Insomnia, anxiety, muscle spasm
**Evidence:** Moderate (sleep latency reduction), Traditional (anxiety)
**Growing:** Zones 4–9, moist soil, partial shade; tall (4–5 ft), harvest root in fall of second year.
**Preparation:**
- Tincture root: 1:5 in 40% alcohol; 3–5 mL, 30–45 min before bed
- Tea: 1 tsp dried root, steep 15 min (smells terrible — tincture preferred)
- Dry capsule: 400–600 mg standardized extract
**Contraindications:** Avoid with sedative medications, alcohol. May cause paradoxical stimulation in some individuals. Not for long-term use without breaks.

### 8. Calendula — *Calendula officinalis*

**Treats:** Wound healing, burns, rashes, fungal infections, hemorrhoids (topical)
**Evidence:** Moderate (wound healing and anti-inflammatory); some RCTs for radiation dermatitis
**Growing:** Annual, Zones 2–11, full sun, cool season preferred. Dead-head continuously for extended bloom. Harvest fully open orange/yellow flowers.
**Preparation:**
- Infused oil: 1:5 dried flowers in olive oil, 4–6 weeks cold infusion; base for all topical preparations
- Salve: infused oil + 1 oz beeswax per cup oil, melt together, pour into tins
- Tea: 1–2 Tbsp fresh or dried flowers, steep 10–15 min; also as wound wash
**Contraindications:** Ragweed allergy cross-reaction. Avoid internal use in pregnancy.

### 9. Lavender — *Lavandula angustifolia*

**Treats:** Anxiety, sleep (aromatherapy), minor burns, antiseptic (topical), headache
**Evidence:** Moderate (anxiety — oral lavender oil, Silexan, several RCTs); Traditional (topical)
**Growing:** Zones 5–9, full sun, excellent drainage; drought tolerant. Harvest just before full bloom.
**Preparation:**
- Essential oil: steam distillation; or purchase (Tisserand, $10–15/10mL)
- Direct application (one of few EOs safe to apply neat to small areas): small burns, insect bites
- Aromatherapy: diffuse or apply diluted (3–5% in carrier oil) for anxiety and sleep
**Contraindications:** Internal essential oil use requires medical supervision. Avoid undiluted on sensitive skin.

### 10. Ginger — *Zingiber officinale*

**Treats:** Nausea (strong evidence), motion sickness, GI motility, anti-inflammatory, morning sickness
**Evidence:** Strong (nausea/vomiting across multiple RCTs including pregnancy and chemotherapy)
**Growing:** Zones 8–12 outdoors; container grown indoors in cold climates. Harvest rhizomes after 8–10 months.
**Preparation:**
- Fresh tea: 1–2 inch piece fresh root, sliced thin, steep 15–20 min in near-boiling water
- Tincture: 1:5 in 60% alcohol; 2–3 mL as needed
- Candied ginger: boil sliced root in equal parts water and sugar until translucent; roll in sugar
- Therapeutic dose for nausea: 250 mg dried ginger (1/4 tsp powder) every 4 hours; or 1–1.5g/day
**Contraindications:** High doses may potentiate anticoagulants. Mild interactions only.

### 11. Garlic — *Allium sativum*

**Treats:** Cardiovascular (modest BP and cholesterol reduction), antimicrobial (topical), respiratory infections, immune support
**Evidence:** Moderate (cardiovascular); Traditional (antimicrobial — allicin is a real broad-spectrum antimicrobial, but poorly absorbed systemically)
**Growing:** Annual; plant individual cloves in fall, harvest following summer when lower leaves brown. Hardneck varieties best for cold climates.
**Preparation:**
- Raw: 2–4 cloves/day, crushed and let rest 10 min before eating (allicin formation)
- Oil-infused (topical only — ear infections, skin antiseptic): fresh garlic in olive oil; DO NOT infuse at room temp for internal use (botulism risk); use immediately
- Tincture: 1:5 in 60% alcohol
**Contraindications:** Potentiates anticoagulants (warfarin, aspirin). Hypoglycemia risk with diabetes medications. Large amounts may cause GI upset.

### 12. Willow Bark — *Salix alba* / *S. nigra*

**Treats:** Pain, fever, inflammation — the original aspirin
**Evidence:** Moderate (studies show pain reduction comparable to low-dose aspirin; salicin converts to salicylate in body)
**Growing:** Native along waterways; Zones 4–9; coppice as shrub for continuous bark harvest. Harvest young branch bark in spring.
**Preparation:**
- Decoction: 1–2 tsp dried bark per cup water, simmer 20 min; drink 2–3 cups/day
- Tincture: 1:5 in 40% alcohol; 5–8 mL up to 4×/day
- Equivalent dose: 240 mg salicin (standardized extract) ≈ 81 mg aspirin effect
**Contraindications:** Same as aspirin — avoid in children under 16 (Reye's syndrome risk), avoid with aspirin sensitivity, peptic ulcers, anticoagulants. Do not use for fever in children.

### 13. Mullein — *Verbascum thapsus*

**Treats:** Respiratory — cough, bronchitis, mild asthma; ear pain (ear drop oil)
**Evidence:** Traditional/Low; some antimicrobial properties in vitro
**Growing:** Biennial; grow large rosette first year, flower spike second year. Full sun, dry poor soil. Common roadside plant.
**Preparation:**
- Leaf tea (respiratory): 2 Tbsp dried leaves, steep 15 min THROUGH FINE CLOTH (leaf hairs irritate throat); honey to taste
- Flower oil (ear drops): flowers infused in olive oil 4–6 weeks; 2–3 drops warmed oil in ear for otitis media pain
**Contraindications:** Always strain leaf tea through fine cloth. Contraindicated in eardrum perforation (oil drops).

### 14. Goldenseal — *Hydrastis canadensis*

**Treats:** Mucous membrane infections (topical), eye wash (conjunctivitis), wound wash; berberine is the active compound
**Evidence:** Moderate (berberine); Traditional (goldenseal specifically)
**Note:** Wild goldenseal is at-risk; grow your own. Slow to establish (3–5 years to harvestable root).
**Growing:** Zones 3–8; shaded moist woodland conditions. Plant rhizome divisions. Slow grower.
**Preparation:**
- Root tincture: 1:10 in 60% alcohol (potent; low dose); 1–2 mL 3×/day, short course only
- Eye wash: 1 tsp tincture in 1 cup cooled boiled water; use within 24 hrs
- Topical powder: dried root powder on infected wound or as mouthwash
**Contraindications:** AVOID in pregnancy (uterine stimulant). Hepatotoxic in large doses. Not for long-term use. May raise blood pressure.

### 15. Oregon Grape — *Mahonia aquifolium*

**Treats:** Skin conditions (psoriasis, eczema), GI infections, liver support; berberine source (easier to grow than goldenseal)
**Evidence:** Moderate (topical psoriasis — several RCTs)
**Growing:** Zones 5–9, shade tolerant, evergreen shrub. Excellent substitute for goldenseal as berberine source.
**Preparation:**
- Root tincture: 1:5 in 60% alcohol; 2–4 mL 3×/day
- Topical cream: standardized berberine cream 10% for psoriasis
**Contraindications:** Similar to goldenseal. Avoid in pregnancy.

### 16. Usnea — *Usnea* spp. (Old Man's Beard, beard lichen)

**Treats:** Respiratory infections, urinary tract infections, skin infections — usnic acid has genuine antimicrobial properties
**Evidence:** Moderate (antimicrobial in vitro and limited clinical); used historically as field antibiotic
**Harvest:** Found on tree branches in clean-air forests; long stringy gray-green growth; pull apart and look for white inner cord (distinguishes from Alectoria which lacks white cord). Harvest from living trees.
**Preparation:**
- Tincture: 1:5 in 60% alcohol (alcohol extracts usnic acid better than water); 2–4 mL 4×/day
- Tea: simmer in water 45+ min; less effective extract
**Contraindications:** Hepatotoxic in very high doses (usnic acid); do not exceed recommended doses. Avoid internal use in liver disease.

### 17. Turmeric — *Curcuma longa*

**Treats:** Inflammation (systemic and topical), arthritis pain, GI inflammation
**Evidence:** Moderate (curcumin studies mixed; bioavailability is the main problem; piperine — black pepper — increases absorption 2000%)
**Growing:** Zones 8–10 outdoors; container grown in colder zones. Harvest rhizomes after 8 months.
**Preparation:**
- Golden milk: 1 tsp powder + pinch black pepper in warm milk; daily anti-inflammatory
- Capsule: 500–1,500 mg curcumin with piperine (bioperine), 3×/day
- Topical paste: mix with honey and coconut oil for wound/burn application
**Contraindications:** May thin blood at high doses; caution with anticoagulants. GI upset at high doses. Avoid high doses in gallstones (bile stimulant).

### 18. Chamomile — *Matricaria chamomilla* (German) / *Chamaemelum nobile* (Roman)

**Treats:** Anxiety, GI spasm (carminative), sleep, topical inflammation, infantile colic
**Evidence:** Moderate (GI and anxiety); Strong (topical anti-inflammatory)
**Growing:** Annual (German) or perennial (Roman); full sun; sow from seed in cool weather. Harvest fully open flowers.
**Preparation:**
- Tea: 2–3 Tbsp fresh or 1–2 tsp dried flowers; steep covered 10–15 min (cover to preserve volatile oils)
- Tincture: 1:5 in 40% alcohol; 2–4 mL 3×/day
- Topical steam: face over bowl of hot chamomile tea for congestion
**Contraindications:** Ragweed allergy cross-reaction (Asteraceae family). Possible interaction with warfarin at high doses.

## 8.5 Common Conditions

### Infection and Antibiotics

**Empiric antibiotic selection guide:**

| Condition | First Choice | Allergy Alternative | Duration | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|---------------------|----------|-------|
| Skin/soft tissue (cellulitis) | Cephalexin 500 mg QID | Clindamycin 300 mg TID | 5–7 days | For MRSA risk: TMP-SMX + cephalexin |
| Wound infection | Amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg BID | Clindamycin 300–450 mg QID | 5–7 days | |
| Respiratory (bacterial signs) | Amoxicillin 1g TID or Azithromycin 500 mg day 1 then 250 mg | Doxycycline 100 mg BID | 5–7 days | |

| Tick-borne (Lyme, Rocky Mtn) | Doxycycline 100 mg BID | Amoxicillin 500 mg TID | 14–21 days | Start empirically in endemic area |
| Dental abscess | Amoxicillin 500 mg TID | Clindamycin 300 mg TID | 5–7 days | |

**Fish antibiotics:** Fish amoxicillin, fish azithromycin, fish doxycycline, and fish cephalexin sold for aquarium use are manufactured by pharmaceutical-grade facilities and have been tested to contain the same API (active pharmaceutical ingredient) as human-labeled equivalents. A 2018 study in *Clinical Infectious Diseases* confirmed this. They are legal to purchase without prescription; their use for human treatment is in a legal gray zone. In a genuine austere scenario where no alternative exists, they are a reasonable option. Formulations that say "for ornamental fish only" are the same chemistry. Avoid: fish metronidazole for anything without clear indication (mutagenic in high cumulative doses, Candida overgrowth); avoid combination products with unclear dosing.

**Systemic infection (sepsis) recognition:**
- SIRS criteria (2 or more): Temp >38°C or <36°C; HR >90; RR >20; WBC >12,000 or <4,000
- Sepsis = SIRS + suspected infection source → IV antibiotics, aggressive fluid resuscitation, evacuate
- Septic shock = sepsis + hypotension despite fluids → mortality high without intensive care

### Fever

**Fever is protective** — do not reflexively treat every fever. Elevation to 38–39°C inhibits bacterial and viral replication, activates immune cells, and reduces pathogen load. Evidence does not support routine fever reduction for improving outcomes.

**Treat fever when:**
- >39.5°C in adults (risk of febrile seizure, comfort)
- >38.5°C in children <3 months (serious bacterial infection must be excluded)
- Causing significant distress, preventing adequate fluid intake
- Underlying cardiac or seizure condition

**Treatment protocol:**
- Ibuprofen 400 mg + acetaminophen 500 mg simultaneously every 6 hours (different mechanisms, safe to combine)
- Alternating: acetaminophen every 4 hours, ibuprofen every 6 hours, staggered at 2-3 hour intervals
- Tepid sponge bath if >40°C and medications not reducing within 30–45 minutes
- Children: ibuprofen 10 mg/kg every 6–8 hours; acetaminophen 15 mg/kg every 4–6 hours

### Dehydration

**ORS (Oral Rehydration Solution) recipe:** 1 liter water + 6 level teaspoons sugar (36g) + 0.5 level teaspoon salt (3g). Optional: squeeze of citrus for potassium. WHO-formulation equivalent. Rice water or dilute juice acceptable if not available.

**Severity assessment:**
- Mild: thirst, slightly decreased urine output, normal mentation
- Moderate: dry mouth, sunken eyes, decreased skin turgor (skin tent test — pinch abdomen, slow return = >5% dehydration), dark urine, irritability
- Severe: altered mentation, unable to drink, no urine output, sunken fontanelle (infants), mottled skin → IV access if available; 500 mL NS rapid infusion, reassess

**Oral rehydration volume guide:** 50 mL/kg over 4 hours for moderate dehydration, then maintenance. Children: 5–10 mL every 1–2 minutes initially if vomiting.

### UTI

**Diagnosis:** Urine dipstick — positive leukocyte esterase + nitrites is >85% sensitive for UTI. Symptoms (dysuria, frequency, urgency, suprapubic pain) guide decision if dipstick unavailable.

**Treatment:**
- TMP-SMX 160/800 mg BID × 3 days (first choice where resistance <20% local prevalence)
- Nitrofurantoin 100 mg ER BID × 5 days
- Fosfomycin 3g single dose (if available) — excellent for uncomplicated UTI

**Cranberry:** Evidence supports prevention (reduces adhesion of bacteria to uroepithelium), not treatment of active UTI. Cranberry extract capsules (36 mg PAC, proanthocyanidins) more effective than juice.

**Upper UTI (pyelonephritis) signs:** Fever >38.5°C + flank pain + CVA tenderness (costovertebral angle, fist palpation over kidney area) = kidney infection. Requires 10–14 days antibiotics; fluoroquinolone (ciprofloxacin 500 mg BID) or TMP-SMX preferred for 14 days. Evacuate if vomiting prevents oral medication or no improvement in 48 hours.

### GI Illness

**Diarrhea:**
- Non-infectious (traveler's diarrhea, dietary): loperamide 4 mg initially, then 2 mg after each loose stool (max 16 mg/day)
- DO NOT use loperamide with bloody diarrhea (may signal invasive bacteria — Shiga toxin-producing E. coli, Salmonella, Campylobacter — loperamide can worsen)
- Rehydration is priority at all times
- Giardia: foul-smelling, greasy, floating stools, no blood, incubation 1–3 weeks → Metronidazole 500 mg TID × 5 days or tinidazole 2g once

**Vomiting:**
- Ondansetron (Zofran) 4–8 mg is the gold standard antiemetic; try to maintain some stockpile
- Promethazine 25 mg or prochlorperazine 10 mg if available
- Ginger tea (see herbal section) with real evidence for nausea
- If unable to keep fluids down >24 hours → IV access for antiemetic and fluids

### Skin Conditions

- **Tinea (ringworm, athlete's foot, jock itch):** Clotrimazole or terbinafine cream BID; continue 1 week beyond clearing (4–6 weeks total for tinea pedis). Oral fluconazole 150 mg weekly × 4 weeks for resistant or extensive cases.
- **Impetigo (superficial bacterial, honey-crusted lesions):** Mupirocin 2% ointment (Bactroban) TID × 5–10 days topically. For extensive: dicloxacillin 250 mg QID or cephalexin 500 mg QID × 7 days.
- **Eczema/atopic dermatitis:** Hydrocortisone 1% BID (mild) or betamethasone 0.05% BID (moderate; Rx or veterinary source) during flares; fragrance-free moisturizer (petroleum jelly is excellent) applied immediately after bathing.
- **Scabies:** Permethrin 5% cream applied to entire body below chin, leave 8–10 hours, wash off; repeat in 1 week. All linens washed hot. Ivermectin 200 mcg/kg oral single dose is alternative.
- **Botfly (myiasis):** Cover the breathing hole with petroleum jelly, bacon, or nail polish to suffocate the larva (it must breathe); after 12–24 hours, larva backs out partially; remove by steady traction. Do not rupture the larva in situ (anaphylaxis risk from contents).

### Respiratory Illness

**Viral vs. Bacterial distinction — antibiotics are appropriate when ≥3 of:**
- Fever >38.5°C persisting >5 days
- Purulent (yellow/green) productive cough
- Unilateral chest pain
- Percussion dullness or focal crackles on exam

**Honey for cough:** Genuinely evidence-based — 1–2 tsp honey at bedtime superior to dextromethorphan and placebo for nocturnal cough in children (Cochrane review). Adults: same effect. Use local raw honey.

**Steam:** Humidified air (not necessarily steam) loosens mucus and relieves irritated mucous membranes. Add eucalyptus essential oil (contains 1,8-cineole, a mucokinetic agent).

**Pneumonia signs:** Productive cough + fever + tachypnea (>20/min) + focal chest exam findings → community-acquired pneumonia: azithromycin 500 mg day 1 then 250 mg days 2–5, or amoxicillin 1g TID × 5 days. If no improvement in 48 hours or hypoxia (pulse ox <93%) → evacuate.

## 8.6 Off-Grid Dental Care

Dental emergencies are among the most common and most overlooked aspects of off-grid medical planning. Toothache can be incapacitating; dental abscess can be lethal if it spreads to the mediastinum.

### Pain Management

1. **Clove oil (eugenol):** Soak a small cotton pellet in clove essential oil; use tweezers to pack into the cavity; extremely effective local anesthetic. Over-the-counter eugenol-based preparations: Red Cross Toothache Medicine ($8–12). Avoid contact with gingival tissue (caustic).
2. **Ibuprofen 600 mg + acetaminophen 500 mg** every 6 hours simultaneously — better analgesia than either alone for dental pain.
3. **Oral benzocaine 20% gel** (Orajel Maximum Strength): apply to tissue; provides 15–30 minutes of surface anesthesia; useful for temporary access.
4. **Clove oil + zinc oxide (ZOE paste):** Mix eugenol drops with zinc oxide powder until reaches putty consistency; temporary sedative filling material.

### Temporary Fillings

- **Cavit** (3M): The clinical standard for temporary filling. Zinc oxide + calcium sulfate + polyvinyl acetate; moisture-sets in the mouth; lasts days to weeks; available OTC on Amazon ($8–12). Dry the cavity thoroughly with gauze or air before placing.
- **Den-Temp, Dentemp OS:** Similar OTC products; mix powder and liquid, pack into cavity.
- **Procedure:** Remove loose decay/debris with explorer or toothpick; dry; pack material; patient bites gently to shape; avoid chewing on that side.

### Abscess Management

**Recognition:** Localized swelling adjacent to a tooth, pain, possible fluctuance (fluid-filled pocket = time for I&D); possible systemic fever and lymphadenopathy.

**Danger signs requiring immediate evacuation:**
- Swelling spreading below the jaw line (submandibular)
- Difficulty swallowing or opening mouth (trismus) >2 finger widths
- Stridor or drooling (airway compromise = Ludwig's angina — fatal without surgical airway)
- High fever + chills + sick appearance

**Incision and drainage (I&D):** If fluctuant (fluid-filled, soft center):
1. Topical benzocaine on mucosa
2. Infiltrate with 1.8 mL lidocaine 2% with epinephrine using dental syringe
3. Stab incision with #11 blade at most dependent, fluctuant point
4. Insert hemostat or gloved finger, gentle spreading to break loculations
5. Irrigate with saline; leave drain (small piece of gauze wick) for 24–48 hours
6. Antibiotics: amoxicillin 500 mg TID or clindamycin 300 mg QID × 5–7 days

### Dental Extraction

**Indications:** Irreparably fractured tooth, non-restorable decay, abscess with no I&D option, pericoronitis (wisdom tooth), patient preference in resource-limited setting.

**Required equipment:**
- Aspirating dental syringe + 27-gauge needle
- Dental carpules: lidocaine 2% with epinephrine 1:100,000 (Septocaine available from some veterinary suppliers)
- Periosteal elevator / dental elevator (straight #34)
- Extraction forceps: #150 (upper universals), #151 (lower universals), #16 (upper molars), #17 (lower molars)
- Bite block, gauze

**Procedure overview (upper single-rooted tooth):**
1. Infiltration anesthetic: buccal and palatal infiltration totaling 1.8–3.6 mL; wait 5 minutes
2. Sever gingival attachment with elevator, circumferentially around tooth neck
3. Wedge elevator between tooth and bone on mesial surface, rotate to loosen
4. Apply forceps as apically as possible; beak tips grip tooth root not crown
5. Gentle buccal-lingual rocking motion to expand socket (bone is somewhat elastic); molar: figure-eight rotation
6. Delivery: controlled, steady force in direction of least resistance
7. Pack socket with gauze; bite pressure for 30–45 minutes
8. Antibiotics only if infected; ibuprofen for pain

**Socket packing:** If bleeding continues or dry socket develops (3–5 days post-extraction, severe throbbing pain): pack socket with eugenol-soaked gauze (change every 2 days) or commercial dental dressing (Alvogyl).

### Prevention

- Fluoride toothpaste: the single most evidence-based preventive measure; stockpile substantial quantities
- Xylitol (gum or mints, 6–10g/day in divided doses): reduces Streptococcus mutans colonization; strong evidence
- Diet: minimize refined sugars and acid exposure; dried fruits are particularly cariogenic (acidic + sticky)
- Oil pulling with coconut or sesame oil (20 min daily): marginal evidence, but low risk; reduces some plaque bacteria

## 8.7 Prescription Medication Management

### Building a Stockpile

**90-day supply as baseline.** Request 90-day mail order prescriptions from insurance; most plans allow this and reduce co-pay. During international travel, request vacation override from your pharmacy (typically allows early refill 30 days before depletion).

**Rotate stock:** Label each prescription container with expiration date; use oldest first. Store in cool, dry, dark location (not the bathroom — humidity degrades medications faster than almost anything else).

**Temperature-sensitive medications:** Insulin, some vaccines, epinephrine autoinjectors, and reconstituted liquid antibiotics require refrigeration. A 12V propane-electric refrigerator (Norcold, Dometic) or buried cache at consistent 55°F ground temperature extends useful life significantly.

### Extended Shelf Life Reality

The SLEP (Shelf Life Extension Program) conducted by the US military tested 100+ drugs stored properly in original sealed containers. **88–96% retained ≥90% potency at an average of 5.5 years beyond labeled expiration.** Many were effective at 10–15 years.

**Exceptions (real degradation products):**
- **Tetracyclines**: Older formulations (not doxycycline) degraded to toxic anhydro-tetracycline causing Fanconi syndrome (renal tubular acidosis). Modern doxycycline has not demonstrated this problem, but conservatism is warranted — discard expired tetracyclines if any alternative exists.
- **Liquid formulations:** Reconstituted liquid antibiotics (amoxicillin suspension), insulin, and liquid epinephrine degrade meaningfully; use within labeled open periods.
- **Nitroglycerin:** Volatile; loses potency rapidly outside original packaging.

**Most medications — ibuprofen, acetaminophen, antibiotics (solid oral forms), antihistamines, antihypertensives, metformin, levothyroxine, antidepressants — are safe and effective years beyond expiration when stored properly.**

### Essential Chronic Medications

| Medication | Use | Storage | Notes |
|-----------|-----|---------|-------|
| Metformin 500/1000 mg | Type 2 diabetes | Room temp | Remarkably stable; also extends life (TOR pathway) |
| Levothyroxine 50–200 mcg | Hypothyroidism | Room temp, dark | Refrigerate if possible; degrades slowly with heat |
| Lisinopril 5–40 mg | Hypertension, heart failure | Room temp | ACE inhibitor; stable |
| Metoprolol succinate 25–200 mg | HTN, heart failure, arrhythmia | Room temp | Beta blocker; do not stop suddenly |
| Amlodipine 5–10 mg | Hypertension | Room temp | Calcium channel blocker; stable |
| Albuterol inhaler | Asthma, COPD | Room temp | Propellant-dependent; check canister; 2-yr shelf life |
| Prednisone 20 mg | Inflammation, anaphylaxis, asthma | Room temp | Taper required; stockpile substantial quantity |
| Warfarin 5 mg | Atrial fibrillation, DVT | Room temp | Requires INR monitoring — near impossible off-grid |
| Sertraline 50–100 mg | Depression, anxiety | Room temp | SSRI; takes 4–6 weeks for full effect; stable |

### Insulin Management

**Without refrigeration:** Unopened insulin vials are stable at room temperature (<86°F/30°C) for 28 days (regular human insulin, NPH) to 28–56 days (modern analogs per manufacturer). Once opened, most analogs are stable 28 days at room temp.

**Walmart ReliOn insulin:** Human regular insulin (Novolin R) and NPH (Novolin N) sold over the counter in most US states without prescription for ~$25/vial. This is actual pharmaceutical insulin, not veterinary. Regular (R) is short-acting (peak 2–4 hr); NPH is intermediate (peak 4–10 hr). These were the standard before analog insulins; many diabetics do well on them with adjustment to diet and timing.

**Signs of insulin degradation:** Regular insulin should be clear and colorless — discard if cloudy, particulate, or any color. NPH is uniformly cloudy when gently rolled (never shaken) — discard if frosted or clumped.

**Emergency insulin rationing:** In true shortage, reduce carbohydrate intake dramatically; a very low carbohydrate diet may allow Type 2 diabetics to discontinue insulin temporarily and significantly reduces Type 1 insulin requirements (but Type 1 must never be without basal insulin).

### Veterinary and Alternative Sources

**Ivermectin horse paste (1.87%):** Each notch on the plunger delivers 10 mg ivermectin. Human dose for parasites: 200 mcg/kg. For 80 kg adult: 16 mg = 1.6 notches. This is the same pharmaceutical compound as human Stromectol. Legal to purchase OTC at farm stores (Tractor Supply). Use ONLY for confirmed or suspected parasitic infection (strongyloides, pinworm, scabies, lice); not for undifferentiated illness.

**Note on antibiotic resistance:** Using antibiotics without indication drives resistance. The fish antibiotic option should be reserved for genuine need, not prophylaxis or uncertain illness.

## 8.8 Childbirth and Reproductive Health

### Prenatal Care

**Essential nutrients:**
- **Folic acid:** 400–800 mcg from 3 months before conception through first trimester; prevents neural tube defects (strong evidence)
- **Iron:** 27 mg/day during pregnancy; deficiency causes severe anemia in mother and fetal growth restriction; red meat, legumes, dark leafy greens + vitamin C (enhances absorption)
- **Calcium:** 1,000 mg/day; fetus will strip from maternal bone if insufficient
- **Iodine:** 220 mcg/day; critical for fetal thyroid development; iodized salt or supplement
- **Vitamin D:** 1,500–2,000 IU/day; most people are deficient; critical for bone development

**Monitoring tools:**
- **Fetoscope ($15–25, Amazon):** Wooden or Pinard horn; fetal heart tones audible after 18–20 weeks. Normal FHT: 120–160 bpm. DeLee-Hillis fetoscope places it against your forehead, amplifying via bone conduction.
- **Fundal height:** Measure from pubic symphysis to top of uterus in centimeters. After 20 weeks, centimeters roughly equals weeks gestation (±2 cm is normal). Lagging growth = possible IUGR; assess and consider evacuation for obstetric care.
- **Fetal kick counts:** After 28 weeks, fetus should move ≥10 times in 2 hours. Fewer than 10 = assess further; consider evacuation.
- **Blood pressure:** HTN in pregnancy = >140/90. Preeclampsia: HTN + protein in urine (dipstick 2+ or more) + headache/visual changes → EVACUATE. Preeclampsia can progress to eclampsia (seizures) within hours.

**Warning signs requiring evacuation:**
- Severe headache + visual disturbances + elevated BP = preeclampsia
- Vaginal bleeding at any time (especially 3rd trimester)
- Severe abdominal pain (abruption, ectopic, other)
- Preterm labor <37 weeks
- Fluid leaking before labor starts (PROM) if >37 weeks: labor will likely follow within 24 hours; if no labor in 24 hours → antibiotics and careful monitoring or evacuation

### Normal Delivery — The Five P's

1. **Passenger** (baby): presentation (head/vertex vs. breech), position, size
2. **Pelvis** (mother): adequate shape and dimensions for delivery
3. **Powers:** uterine contractions + maternal pushing
4. **Position:** maternal position — upright, side-lying, or hands-and-knees often more effective than supine
5. **Psychology:** maternal confidence, support, informed consent for everything

**Labor stages:**
- **Stage 1:** Cervical dilation 0→10 cm. Early labor (0–6 cm): slow; active labor (6–10 cm): faster. Transition (8–10 cm) is most intense.
- **Stage 2:** Pushing + delivery of baby. Normal duration: 1–3 hours primipara, 30 min–1 hr multipara.
- **Stage 3:** Placenta delivery. Within 30 minutes; if not → manual removal risk (hemorrhage).

**Crowning management:**
- Support perineum with warm compress and gentle counterpressure to prevent tearing
- Guide head out with gentle pressure between contractions; do NOT pull
- Check for nuchal cord (cord around neck) — if loose, slip over head; if tight, somersault maneuver (deliver body under cord) or clamp-cut-deliver
- Suction mouth then nose only if meconium present (clear fluid requires no routine suctioning)

**Shoulder dystocia (emergency):**
- Turtle sign: head delivers then retracts against perineum
- McRoberts maneuver: flex thighs hyperflexed against abdomen (flattens lumbar lordosis, widens pelvic outlet); usually resolves this
- Suprapubic pressure (push down and lateral on the baby's anterior shoulder)
- Gaskin maneuver: mother to hands-and-knees position
- Do NOT pull on the head — brachial plexus injury or spinal damage

**Postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) — >500 mL blood loss after delivery:**
- Uterine atony (most common cause): bimanual uterine compression/massage; fundal massage
- Oxytocin 10 IU IM (if available) is first-line; misoprostol 600–800 mcg sublingual or rectal is OTC in some countries, excellent alternative
- Retained placenta: manual removal (requires sterile technique, preparation, and some skill)
- Improvised Bakri balloon: 30Fr Foley catheter advanced into uterine cavity, inflate balloon with 100–500 mL saline → tamponade hemorrhage; traction on catheter while packing vagina with gauze

### Contraception

- **Copper IUD:** Most reliable non-hormonal method; 10-year effectiveness; can be inserted by trained provider; highly effective as emergency contraception (insert within 5 days); does not degrade or expire while in place
- **Hormonal IUD (Mirena):** 5–8 years; reduces periods significantly; requires provider insertion
- **Depo-Provera:** 3-month injectable; can be self-administered IM; stockpile requires cold chain; effective immediately after injection
- **Barrier methods:** Male and female condoms, diaphragm; reliable only with consistent correct use; stockpile large quantities
- **Fertility awareness method (FAM):** Effective (98%+) only with rigorous tracking of temperature (basal body temperature charting), cervical mucus, and cycle length using an app or paper chart; fails with illness, irregular sleep, or poor tracking

### Menstrual Care

- **Menstrual cup (silicone):** Diva Cup, Lunette, or generic (~$25–40); 10+ year lifespan; rinse with water between uses, boil monthly; dramatically reduces long-term resource needs
- **Reusable cloth pads:** Flannel or organic cotton; wash with soap and water; sun dry; 5+ years per pad; make from flannel fabric (pattern: $0, material: $20 for 10 pads)
- **Menstrual disc:** Single or reusable; worn differently than cup; good option for IUD users who find cups uncomfortable

## 8.9 Mental Health in Isolation

### The Adjustment Arc

Research on isolated populations (Antarctic winterovers, submarine crews, remote work camps) consistently shows a characteristic adjustment arc: an initial honeymoon period (weeks 1–8), followed by a difficult adjustment phase (months 2–6) characterized by irritability, sleep disruption, interpersonal tension, and nostalgia. Most people adjust by month 6–12. Failure to adjust by 12 months — persistent depression, social withdrawal, or behavioral changes — is clinically significant.

**The adjustment phase is not pathology.** It is a normal response to a profoundly abnormal environment. Communities that normalize this experience, create structured support, and avoid pathologizing normal adjustment have significantly better long-term outcomes.

### Non-Pharmacological Depression and Anxiety Treatment

These are not consolation prizes — they are evidence-based first-line treatments:

- **Exercise (30–45 min/day moderate intensity):** Multiple meta-analyses show equivalence to SSRIs for mild-to-moderate depression. The mechanism involves BDNF upregulation, HPA axis regulation, and endorphin release. Physical labor on a homestead often provides this automatically — make it intentional.
- **Sleep (7–9 hours):** Sleep deprivation is mechanistically causal in depression, anxiety, and cognitive impairment. Sleep hygiene: consistent schedule, dark room, cool temperature, no alcohol within 3 hours of sleep.
- **Light exposure:** Seasonal affective disorder (SAD) is extremely common in northern latitudes. Light therapy (10,000 lux, 30 min within 1 hour of waking) is FDA-approved first-line for SAD and effective for non-seasonal depression. Cost: $30–80 for a quality SAD lamp (Verilux HappyLight, Carex). Natural sunlight (>10,000 lux at midday) is free and superior.
- **Social connection:** Isolation without intentional social structure degrades mental health faster than any other variable. Even digital connection (HF radio, internet via Starlink) matters. Physical co-presence in a weekly shared meal or work party is qualitatively different from async communication.
- **Meaningful work and accomplishment:** Depression correlates with loss of agency. The homestead, done well, provides continuous concrete feedback (food grown, animals healthy, repairs completed). Frame daily tasks as accomplishments, not chores.

### Community Mental Health Structures

- **Weekly group activity:** Shared meal, work party, skill share, or celebration. Non-negotiable.
- **Rotating roles:** Prevents resentment from perceived unfairness; ensures everyone contributes visibly.
- **Conflict resolution protocol:** Establish before conflict occurs. Suggested framework: direct conversation first, then mediation with designated third party, then community vote for disputes affecting shared resources. Written down and agreed upon before move-in.
- **Regular check-ins:** Brief individual check-in with community health-designated person weekly. Not therapy — a structured conversation. "How are you sleeping? Eating? What's been hard this week?"

### PTSD in the Field

Off-grid communities may experience traumatic events: injury, death, severe weather, crime. PTSD responses are normal and expected after genuine trauma.

**Grounding technique (5-4-3-2-1):** Name 5 things you can see, 4 you can touch, 3 you can hear, 2 you can smell, 1 you can taste. Engages sensory cortex and interrupts amygdala-driven hyperarousal.

**Avoid numbing:** Alcohol reduces acute anxiety but dramatically worsens PTSD trajectory and increases chronicity. Limit alcohol use in aftermath of traumatic events.

**Normalize:** Most people experiencing significant traumatic stress are NOT developing clinical PTSD. Intrusive thoughts, sleep disruption, and hypervigilance in the 30 days following trauma are acute stress responses and usually self-resolve.

**When to evacuate for psychiatric emergency:**
- Active suicidal ideation with plan and access to means
- Psychosis (break from reality, hallucinations, command voices) — medical emergency
- Homicidal ideation with specific plan
- Severe self-neglect (refusing food/water, inability to care for self) >72 hours

## 8.10 Trauma and Surgical Emergencies

### Hemorrhage Control

**Priority sequence (MARCH protocol):**
1. **Massive hemorrhage control** → direct pressure, wound packing, tourniquet
2. **Airway** → head-tilt/jaw thrust, adjuncts, position
3. **Respiration** → assess breathing, seal chest wounds, decompress tension PTX
4. **Circulation** → fluid resuscitation, monitor
5. **Hypothermia/Hypoxia** → warm patient, supplemental oxygen if available

**Tourniquet application:**
- Apply 2–3 inches (5–7 cm) proximal (above) the wound
- Tighten until bleeding stops and distal pulse is absent
- Write TIME of application on the tourniquet or patient's forehead
- Maximum continuous application: 2 hours; if must leave on longer, notify receiving care — muscle death begins at 2 hours, reperfusion injury is significant at 6 hours
- Do NOT loosen to "let the limb breathe" — this is wrong and dangerous

**Wound packing (for junctional wounds where tourniquet won't work — groin, axilla, neck):**
1. Identify the bleeding point within the wound
2. Pack hemostatic gauze (QuikClot, Celox) directly onto the bleeding point, not just into the cavity
3. Pack tightly — this requires deliberate firm pressure
4. Maintain direct pressure for 3–5 minutes minimum (5 for hemostatic gauze)
5. Do not remove packing until definitive hemostasis achieved

### Wound Closure — Detailed

**Primary closure (close now):** Clean wound, <6 hours old, minimal contamination. Irrigation → debridement → layered closure.

**Delayed primary closure (close in 4–5 days):** Contaminated wound, bite wound, wound >6–8 hours old, uncertain status. Pack open, check for infection daily, close when clean.

**Secondary intention (let heal open):** Deep puncture wounds, heavily contaminated wounds. Change moist packing daily; granulation tissue fills from base upward.

**Suturing technique — simple interrupted:**
1. Load needle on holder at 90° (needle bites tissue most cleanly perpendicular)
2. Enter skin 3–5 mm from wound edge; needle curves through dermis; exit opposite side equidistant
3. Tie instrument knot: 2 clockwise loops, pull; 1 counter-clockwise, pull; repeat
4. Leave 5–7 mm tails
5. Spacing: 4–5 mm between sutures; remove in 5–7 days (face), 10–14 days (extremities/trunk)

### Appendicitis in the Field

**Classic presentation:** Periumbilical pain migrating to right lower quadrant (McBurney's point, 1/3 of the way from ASIS to umbilicus) over 12–24 hours; nausea and vomiting; fever >38°C; loss of appetite.

**Physical exam signs:**
- Rebound tenderness: pain worse on sudden release of deep palpation at RLQ
- Rovsing's sign: pressure at LLQ causes pain at RLQ
- Psoas sign: pain with right hip extension (inflamed appendix on psoas muscle)

**Conservative management (Appendix antibiotics trial):** Multiple RCTs now support non-surgical management of uncomplicated appendicitis with antibiotics. Protocol: amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg BID × 10 days, or cefdinir 300 mg BID + metronidazole 500 mg TID × 10 days. Success rate ~70% at 1 year; ~30% require eventual surgery. Appropriate only if patient has no peritoneal signs (rigid abdomen, rebound) and is not deteriorating.

**Evacuate if:** Signs of peritonitis (rigid abdomen, generalized rebound), fever >39.5°C, no improvement in 24–48 hours on antibiotics, any sign of perforation.

### Tension Pneumothorax

**Recognition:** Sudden respiratory distress; absent or decreased breath sounds one side; tracheal deviation (away from tension) — this is a LATE sign; distended neck veins; hypotension. Usually follows penetrating chest trauma or rib fracture.

**Needle decompression:**
- Site: 2nd intercostal space, midclavicular line (or 4th–5th ICS, anterior axillary line — less chance of hitting internal mammary artery)
- 14-gauge needle-over-catheter (angiocath); insert perpendicular to chest wall, over top of lower rib to avoid neurovascular bundle
- Successful decompression: rush of air, immediate clinical improvement
- Convert to chest tube or improvised chest drain if available and trained

### Improvised Chest Tube

For massive hemothorax or non-resolving pneumothorax in extended field care:
1. Lateral decubitus position (affected side up); mark site: 4th–5th ICS, anterior-mid axillary line
2. Lidocaine infiltration through all layers including pleura
3. 2 cm skin incision with scalpel
4. Blunt dissection with hemostat over the rib to avoid neurovascular bundle
5. Controlled puncture of pleura with hemostat (finger-guided)
6. Insert tube (improvised: IV tubing, urinary catheter for small pneumothorax)
7. Underwater seal: other end in water container 20 cm below chest
8. Secure tube; occlusive dressing

## 8.11 CBRN and Nuclear Scenarios

### Radiation Physics

- **Alpha particles:** Stopped by paper, skin, clothing. Dangerous only if inhaled or ingested. Radon is alpha-emitting.
- **Beta particles:** Stopped by a few mm of plastic, glass, or several meters of air. Can cause skin burns and eye damage with direct contact. Radioactive iodine (beta + gamma) concentrates in thyroid.
- **Gamma rays/X-rays:** Penetrating electromagnetic radiation; requires significant mass to attenuate. 1 inch lead, 6 inches of concrete, or 36 inches of packed earth reduces intensity by ~50% (half-value layer). Multiple HVLs needed for substantial protection.
- **Neutrons:** Produced only near active fission reactions; most concerning within blast radius; not relevant to fallout scenarios.

### Acute Radiation Syndrome (ARS)

| Dose (Gy) | Classification | Symptoms | Prognosis (no treatment) |
|-----------|---------------|----------|--------------------------|
| <0.5 Gy | Subclinical | None apparent | No significant effect |
| 0.5–1 Gy | Mild ARS | Possible transient nausea | 100% survival |
| 1–2 Gy | Mild hematopoietic | N/V within hours; latent period weeks; some WBC depression | >95% survival |
| 2–4.5 Gy | Moderate hematopoietic | N/V/D; latent 2–3 weeks; significant WBC/platelet depression | ~50% survival without treatment (LD50 ~4.5 Gy) |
| 4.5–6 Gy | Severe hematopoietic | Rapid-onset N/V; bone marrow failure | 50–70% mortality without treatment |
| 6–10 Gy | GI syndrome | Persistent GI symptoms; mucosal sloughing; sepsis | >90% mortality |
| >10 Gy | Neurovascular | Incapacitation hours; ataxia, shock | Nearly 100% mortality |

**Field management of ARS:**
- Supportive care is primary: antiemetics, fluid/electrolyte replacement, infection prevention
- Filgrastim (G-CSF) stimulates white blood cell production; medical stockpile if possible
- Amoxicillin prophylaxis reduces infectious mortality in bone marrow suppression
- Transfusion requires matched blood — not feasible in austere settings

### Potassium Iodide (KI)

**Purpose:** Saturates the thyroid with stable iodine, preventing uptake of radioactive iodine-131 (I-131) released in nuclear detonation or reactor accident.

**Protects only the thyroid** — does not protect against whole-body gamma radiation or other isotopes.

**Dosing:**
- Adults 18–40: 130 mg (most critical group; highest thyroid cancer risk from I-131)
- Adults 40+: 130 mg only if expecting very high thyroid dose; lower priority
- Children 3–18 years: 65 mg
- Children 1 month–3 years: 32 mg
- Infants <1 month: 16 mg

**Timing:** Most effective if taken within 1–4 hours BEFORE exposure. Up to 6–8 hours post-exposure is still beneficial. After 24 hours, benefit is minimal.

**Source:** Available OTC as iosat ($10–15 for 14 tablets), ThyroSafe. Stockpile 14 days' supply per person.

**Do not substitute:** Topical iodine, Betadine, or iodized salt are NOT substitutes — amounts required would be toxic.

### Decontamination

**Field decontamination (immediately after fallout exposure):**
1. Remove outer clothing (removes ~70–80% of contamination) — leave outside; do not shake
2. Soap and water shower (do not scrub vigorously — opens skin pores)
3. Blow nose repeatedly; wipe around eyes and ears with damp cloth
4. Do not induce vomiting if radioactive material ingested — more harm than good
5. Change into clean clothing from sealed bag or indoor storage

### Food and Water Safety Post-Fallout

**Water:** Pre-positioned water in sealed containers before the event is safe. Open surface water (streams, ponds, rain) may be contaminated with radioactive particulates. Standard water filters and chemical treatment (bleach, iodine) do NOT remove radioactive isotopes dissolved in water. Distillation reduces contamination significantly but not completely. Reverse osmosis removes some particulates.

**Cesium-137 (Cs-137):** Long half-life (30 years), distributes like potassium in food chains. Cannot be removed by cooking. Concentrated in meat and dairy. After a major event, avoid milk from grazing animals and meat for weeks to months (check monitoring data).

**Iodine-131 (I-131):** 8-day half-life; most dangerous in first weeks. Concentrates in leafy vegetables, grass, milk. After 3 months, I-131 is essentially gone (10 half-lives = 99.9% reduction).

**Strontium-90 (Sr-90):** 29-year half-life; concentrates in bone (acts like calcium). Bone-in meat is more concerning than muscle meat. Found in grains in contaminated areas.

**Garden food safety:** Root vegetables (potatoes, carrots, beets) are significantly less contaminated than leafy greens — contamination is primarily surface deposition on leaves. Peel root vegetables. Avoid leafy greens for 30+ days after event. Wash all produce thoroughly.

### Shelter Factors

**7/10 Rule:** Every 7-fold increase in time after detonation yields a 10-fold reduction in dose rate. This means:
- 7 hours: 1/10th initial dose rate
- 49 hours (2 days): 1/100th
- 2 weeks: 1/1,000th
- 3 months: 1/1,000,000th

**Implication:** The first 24–48 hours are critical; thereafter risk drops dramatically.

**Improvised shelter factor:** Center of a multi-story concrete building (midfloor) provides protection factor of ~100. A basement provides PF 20–200 depending on construction. An earthen root cellar with 18+ inches of soil overhead provides PF 200+.

**Geiger counter:** A radiation detector is essential. The RADEX RD1503+ (~$70) is reliable and affordable. Dosimeter cards (RADTriage, $25 for 5-pack) provide cumulative dose tracking. Know your local background rate (0.05–0.3 μSv/hr is normal).

### Chemical Agents

**Nerve agents (sarin, VX, novichok):**
- Mechanism: organophosphate; inhibits acetylcholinesterase; cholinergic crisis
- SLUDGE signs: Salivation, Lacrimation, Urination, Defecation, GI distress, Emesis; + miosis (pinpoint pupils), bronchospasm, seizures
- Antidote: Atropine 2–4 mg IV/IM every 5–10 minutes until secretions dry; Pralidoxime (2-PAM) 600 mg IM within first 12 hours to reactivate cholinesterase before "aging" occurs
- Military autoinjectors (ATNAA — atropine + pralidoxime combined): unavailable civilian; closest civilian approach is 0.4 mg atropine vials

**Blister agents (mustard, lewisite):**
- Mechanism: alkylating agent; DNA crosslinking; delayed blistering (hours to days)
- Treatment: copious decontamination with water immediately (within minutes has best outcome); symptomatic care; dimercaprol (BAL) for lewisite specifically
- No definitive antidote for mustard

**Biological agents:**
- PPE: N95 minimum for any biological aerosol; P100 (HEPA face piece) preferred
- Isolation protocol: full PPE during contact; droplet precautions (surgical mask) within 3 feet
- Anthrax: ciprofloxacin 500 mg BID or doxycycline 100 mg BID × 60 days post-exposure
- Plague: doxycycline 100 mg BID or ciprofloxacin 500 mg BID × 7 days
- Botulinum toxin: no field antidote; supportive care; antitoxin only via government stockpile

## 8.12 Skills and Training

### Recommended Training Stack for Homesteaders

**Tier 1 — Do these first (total cost: ~$200–300):**

1. **CPR/AED certification:** $50–100 per person; 2-year renewal. BLS (Basic Life Support) for Healthcare Providers if possible — includes bag-mask ventilation. Hands-only CPR is the minimum baseline for everyone in the community.

2. **Stop the Bleed course:** Free 2-hour course (stopthebleed.org); trains tourniquet and wound packing. Run this with your entire community at once.

**Tier 2 — The homestead minimum (~$700–1,100):**

3. **Wilderness First Aid (WFA):** 16–20 hours, $200–350. NOLS, SOLO, Wilderness Medical Associates, Wilderness Medical Institute all offer this. Focused on assessment and evacuation decisions. Good starting point; not sufficient alone for deep off-grid.

4. **Dental First Aid:** Community college dental assistant courses ($300–500) cover radiograph interpretation, temporary restoration, assisting with procedures. Online continuing education from dental schools (many are free). Practice suturing on foam pads; practice injection on oranges.

**Tier 3 — The gold standard (~$1,500–2,000 cumulative):**

5. **Wilderness First Responder (WFR):** 70–80 hours over 8–10 days. $700–900 (NOLS, SOLO, Wilderness Medical Associates). Covers: patient assessment system, wilderness trauma care, medical emergencies, prolonged patient care, medication administration, improvised rescue. Valid 3 years; must recertify. This is the correct training level for any serious remote homestead.

**Optional advanced:**

6. **EMT-Basic:** 120–150 hours, $1,000–1,500. Includes hospital and EMS rotations. Most permits IV medication administration and advanced assessment. Some states have rural-scope expansions. Renewable every 2 years (24 CE credits).

7. **Suture practice:** Foam pads ($15 on Amazon); pig feet from grocery store or butcher shop (similar tissue layers to human skin); practice simple interrupted, mattress, and running locked sutures before you need them.

8. **Herbal medicine apprenticeship:** 6–12 months with an experienced practitioner outweighs any book. Local herb walks, ethnobotany extension programs, herbalism schools (Chestnut School of Herbal Medicine has rigorous online curriculum, ~$400–800).

9. **Obstetrics:** "Skills and Drills" obstetric emergency simulations. Midwifery apprenticeship (Midwives Alliance of North America, MANA). Direct Entry Midwife (DEM) certification is an option for community members who want to serve this role formally.

### Skill Maintenance

Skills degrade without practice. Schedule:
- CPR recertification every 2 years (required)
- WFR recertification every 3 years (required)
- Suture practice: 2× per year on foam or tissue
- Dental drill (mock extractions, filling placement): annually
- Mass casualty drill with community: annually — table-top or live exercise

## Summary: Priority Sequence

For a homestead just getting started, prioritize in this order:

1. **Training first**: CPR, Stop the Bleed, then WFR — skills outlast any kit
2. **Tier 1 supplies**: Every household needs the basics; build to Tier 2 as budget allows
3. **Prescription stockpile**: 90-day supply of all chronic medications; fish antibiotics for the medicine cabinet
4. **Herbal medicine garden**: Low cost, high long-term value; establish first-season
5. **Reference library**: Print copies of the core books; download digital offline references
6. **Dental kit**: Dental emergencies are common and undertreated in off-grid planning
7. **Nuclear preparedness**: KI tablets, Geiger counter, shelter plan — low cost, high consequence prevention

> **Final principle**: The most powerful medical intervention available off-grid is sanitation — clean water, proper waste disposal, and food safety. An ounce of prevention is not a cliche; it is the accurate ratio at which infectious disease operates in resource-limited environments. Build the water system first. The medical system saves the lives that prevention misses.
