### 1. Site Selection & Land

**Purpose**: Choosing and acquiring land is the foundational decision. Every
other domain is constrained by it. A good site makes everything else easier;
a bad site fights you at every turn.

**Key questions:**
- Rainfall, watershed, groundwater — is water abundant or scarce?
- Climate zone and growing season — what can be grown and for how long?
- Soil quality — can it be amended? What's the baseline?
- Access — roads, power grid proximity (for initial build), distance to town
- Legal — zoning, deed restrictions, HOA, building permits, off-grid regulations by state/county
- Natural hazard exposure — flood plain, wildfire interface, earthquake, tornado corridor
- Cost per acre by region — rural Midwest vs. Southeast vs. Pacific Northwest vs. Southwest

**Sections to cover:**
1. How to evaluate a parcel (checklist + scoring system)
2. Water rights and wells — legal frameworks, well drilling costs, yield testing
3. Soil testing — lab tests, what to look for, amendment strategies
4. Zoning and permits — which states are off-grid friendly vs. restrictive
5. Regional comparison — cost/acre, growing season, rainfall, climate risk by US region
6. Due diligence before purchase — title, liens, easements, mineral rights
7. Transition planning — how long to keep a day job / external income while building

**Cost estimates:**
- Rural land (Midwest/Southeast): $1,000–$5,000/acre
- Rural land (Pacific Northwest): $5,000–$20,000/acre
- Rural land (Southwest arid): $500–$3,000/acre
- Well drilling: $5,000–$30,000 depending on depth and geology
- Soil testing: $30–$150 per test
- Permit fees: $500–$10,000+ depending on jurisdiction

### 2. Shelter & Construction

**Purpose**: A durable, weathertight, comfortable, energy-efficient dwelling
built with minimal skilled labor. Primary construction, outbuildings, and
ongoing maintenance.

**Structure types to cover:**
- Stick-frame (conventional, most permit-compatible)
- Timber frame (longevity, can be owner-built with training)
- Straw bale (excellent insulation, low material cost where straw is available)
- Cob / earthen construction (free materials, highly thermal, no permits in some areas)
- Shipping container (cheap steel shell, significant insulation challenges)
- Yurt (low cost, fast deployment, good temporary solution)
- Underground / earth-sheltered (extreme thermal stability, storm and nuclear shelter value)

**Key subsections:**
1. Foundation types — slab, pier, crawlspace, basement; when each is appropriate
2. Framing and structure — load calculations, span tables, code requirements
3. Insulation — R-value targets by climate, materials (fiberglass, mineral wool, spray foam, natural)
4. Windows and doors — passive solar positioning, thermal performance, cost
5. Roofing — metal (longevity, rainwater collection), asphalt, living roof
6. Rainwater catchment integration into roof design
7. Passive solar design — south-facing windows, thermal mass, overhang calculations
8. Outbuildings — root cellar, workshop, animal shelter, greenhouse
9. Maintenance schedule — what to inspect annually, 5-year, 10-year
10. Owner-builder legality by state

**Cost estimates (rough, owner-built):**
- Straw bale home (1,000 sq ft): $30,000–$80,000
- Timber frame shell (1,000 sq ft): $40,000–$100,000
- Cob (1,000 sq ft): $10,000–$40,000 (very labor-intensive)
- Stick-frame (1,000 sq ft, owner-built): $60,000–$120,000
- Root cellar (earthen): $1,000–$5,000 materials

### 3. Water Systems

**Purpose**: Reliable, clean water for drinking, cooking, sanitation, irrigation,
and livestock. Water security is non-negotiable — it's the first system to get
right and the one most likely to fail catastrophically if neglected.

**Sources:**
- Well (drilled or hand-dug)
- Rainwater harvesting — rooftop collection, cisterns
- Spring development — capping, piping, gravity-fed distribution
- Stream/river — legal access, filtration requirements
- Pond / surface water — treatment requirements, giardia/crypto risk

**Treatment and purification:**
1. Sediment filtration — first pass, removes particulates
2. Carbon filtration — removes chlorine, VOCs, taste/odor
3. Ceramic / hollow-fiber membrane — removes bacteria and protozoa
4. UV sterilization — viruses and pathogens, no chemicals needed
5. Reverse osmosis — heavy metals, nitrates, arsenic (point-of-use)
6. Slow sand filter (biosand) — passive, long-term, DIY-viable
7. Distillation — off-grid distiller for absolute purification
8. Testing — what to test for (nitrates, coliform, hardness, pH, arsenic), frequency

**Distribution systems:**
1. Gravity-fed from elevated cistern — low-tech, no pump needed
2. 12V DC pump with pressure tank — low power, solar-compatible
3. Ram pump — no electricity needed where stream has elevation change
4. Hand pump backup (Bison, Simple Pump) — manual fallback

**Storage:**
- Poly cisterns (500–2,500 gal) — UV-resistant, food-grade
- Ferro-cement tanks — DIY, very durable, high capacity
- Buried concrete — freeze protection, large capacity
- IBC totes — cheap, food-grade secondary storage

**Cost estimates:**
- 2,500-gallon poly cistern: $800–$1,500
- Hand pump (Bison/Simple Pump): $1,500–$3,000 installed
- Biosand filter (DIY): $50–$200
- UV sterilizer (point-of-use): $100–$400
- Well pump (solar-powered): $1,500–$4,000

**Daily needs**: 1 gallon/person/day minimum (survival); 2–5 for comfort;
15–50 for garden irrigation per 100 sq ft per week in hot climates.

### 4. Food Production

**Purpose**: Growing, raising, and harvesting the majority of caloric and
nutritional needs on-site. Target: 80%+ food self-sufficiency.

**Subsystems:**
1. **Annual vegetables** — high-yield calorie crops (potatoes, squash, corn, beans),
   greens (kale, chard, lettuce), roots (carrots, beets, parsnips)
2. **Perennial systems** — fruit trees, berry bushes, asparagus, rhubarb, nut trees;
   10–30 year investment with minimal ongoing labor
3. **Small livestock** — chickens (eggs + meat), rabbits (meat), ducks, goats (milk/meat)
4. **Large livestock** — pigs (lard, protein), cattle (draft, dairy, meat), sheep (wool, meat)
   *Note: large livestock require significant land (1+ acre per cow)*
5. **Aquaculture** — tilapia, catfish, or perch in pond or tank system (IBC tote aquaponics)
6. **Foraging** — wild edibles as supplement (see Seedwarden native plants guide)
7. **Mushroom cultivation** — log inoculation (shiitake, oyster, lion's mane), indoor growing
8. **Seed saving** — open-pollinated varieties, isolation distances, storage methods

**Caloric math:**
- 2,000 calories/day × 365 days = 730,000 calories/year per adult
- Potatoes: ~350 cal/lb, yield 10,000–15,000 lb/acre → ~4,000,000 cal/acre
- Corn (grain): ~1,600 cal/lb, yield 2,000–4,000 lb/acre → ~5,000,000 cal/acre
- Beans: ~1,500 cal/lb, yield 500–1,500 lb/acre → ~1,500,000 cal/acre
- **Minimum land for 2 adults**: 0.5–1 acre intensive garden + grain crops

**Soil systems:**
1. No-till / lasagna gardening — builds soil, low labor after establishment
2. Raised beds — frost extension, drainage control, less bending
3. Hugel beds (Hugelkultur) — buried wood, moisture retention, long fertility
4. Composting — hot compost, vermicomposting, compost tea
5. Green manures / cover crops — nitrogen fixation, weed suppression
6. Biochar — carbon sequestration, soil amendment

**Tools:**
- Broadfork, hoe, wheel hoe, seeder — manual cultivation
- Scythe — grain and hay harvesting (1 acre/day by skilled practitioner)
- Hand thresher / flail — grain processing
- Pressure canner, root cellar, dehydrator — storage pipeline

**Cost estimates:**
- 1/4-acre intensive garden setup: $500–$2,000 (beds, soil amendments, tools)
- 4 laying hens + coop: $300–$800
- 2 meat rabbits + hutch: $100–$300
- Fruit tree orchard (10 trees): $300–$800 planted
- Manual grain seeder (push type): $200–$600

### 5. Food Preservation & Storage

**Purpose**: Bridging seasonal abundance to year-round supply. Without
preservation, a productive summer doesn't prevent a hungry February.

**Methods:**
1. **Root cellar storage** — potatoes, carrots, apples, cabbage, squash (no energy required)
2. **Canning** — water bath (high-acid: tomatoes, fruit, pickles) and pressure canning
   (low-acid: meat, beans, vegetables, soups)
3. **Dehydration** — solar dehydrator (free energy) or electric dehydrator
4. **Fermentation** — lacto-fermented vegetables (sauerkraut, kimchi, kvass), vinegar,
   kombucha; extends shelf life, improves nutrition
5. **Smoking** — cold smoke (curing) and hot smoke (cooking + preservation)
6. **Salting and curing** — salt pork, bacon, salt-packed fish
7. **Freezing** — requires power; propane or solar chest freezer
8. **Fat / lard packing** — confit-style storage, potted meat
9. **Ash storage** — eggs in wood ash for months

**Storage requirements per person per year (rough guide):**
- Grains/flour: 300 lb
- Beans/legumes: 100 lb
- Canned/preserved vegetables: 200 lb
- Fats (oil/lard): 20 lb
- Sugar/honey: 60 lb
- Salt: 10 lb (preservation + cooking)
- Dried fruit: 50 lb
- Canned meat/fish: 75 lb

**Root cellar specs:**
- Optimal temperature: 32–40°F
- Optimal humidity: 85–95% for root vegetables
- Needs venting to prevent CO2 buildup
- Construction: underground (6+ feet) for natural temperature stability

### 6. Energy & Power

**Purpose**: Electrical power for lighting, refrigeration, communications,
tools, water pumping, and medical equipment. Target: reliable 24/7 power
for essentials with sun/wind backup.

**Primary systems:**
1. **Solar PV + battery bank** — most practical for most climates
   - Sizing: watts of panels, amp-hours of storage, inverter capacity
   - Battery chemistry: lithium (LiFePO4 recommended), AGM, flooded lead-acid
   - Charge controllers: MPPT vs. PWM
2. **Micro-hydro** — highest energy density if you have a year-round stream
   with head (elevation drop); 1 kW micro-hydro > 5 kW solar in continuous output
3. **Wind turbine** — good complement to solar in cloudy/windy areas; high maintenance
4. **Generator backup** — propane or gasoline; essential during extended cloud periods
5. **Wood gasifier** — converts wood to combustible gas; can run a generator;
   viable long-term as a backup if wood is abundant

**Battery bank sizing for basic homestead:**
- Essentials only (lights, water pump, freezer, comms): 10–20 kWh storage
- Comfortable living (above + washing machine, tools): 20–40 kWh
- Solar panel array: 1.5–3× daily consumption in kWh = kW of panels needed

**Cost estimates:**
- 5 kW solar + 20 kWh LiFePO4 + MPPT + inverter (DIY): $8,000–$15,000
- 5 kW solar + 20 kWh LiFePO4 (installed): $20,000–$35,000
- 1 kW micro-hydro (installed): $8,000–$25,000
- 2 kW wind turbine + tower: $5,000–$15,000
- Propane generator (7 kW): $1,500–$3,000 + propane cost

**Energy conservation strategies:**
- LED lighting throughout
- DC-native appliances (avoids inverter losses)
- Propane cooking and refrigeration (removes largest electrical loads)
- Passive solar design reduces heating energy by 30–70%
- Solar water heating reduces water heating energy by 50–80%

### 7. Heating & Cooling

**Purpose**: Maintaining livable temperatures year-round without grid energy.
Heating is typically higher stakes (hypothermia vs. discomfort).

**Heating options:**
1. **Wood / biomass** — most resilient; requires 3–6 cords/year for cold climate;
   chainsaw, splitting maul, storage shed, seasoning (1–2 years)
   - Masonry heater / rocket mass heater: burns very hot and clean, stores heat in mass
   - Wood stove: faster response, less efficient, standard in most homesteads
   - Wood furnace with ductwork: whole-home from single fire
2. **Propane / LP gas** — reliable backup; 500-gal tank, fill annually
3. **Passive solar** — proper design reduces heating load by 30–70%
4. **Geothermal / earth sheltering** — ground temperature is 50–55°F year-round;
   earth-sheltered structures need almost no heating in mild climates

**Cooling options:**
1. **Passive cooling** — shade, cross-ventilation, thermal mass, earth tubes
2. **Evaporative cooler (swamp cooler)** — effective in dry climates; uses very little power
3. **Mini-split AC** — most efficient AC option; 12V-compatible models exist for solar
4. **Underground/earth-sheltered rooms** — passive 55°F ambient

**Wood supply math:**
- 1 cord = 128 cubic feet of stacked wood ≈ 20 million BTU
- A well-insulated home in cold climate: 3–4 cords/year
- A tight passive solar home: 1–2 cords/year
- Harvest timing: cut in late winter/spring, split and stack, dry through summer

### 8. Waste & Sanitation

**Purpose**: Human waste, greywater, solid waste, and compost management
that protects health and soil.

**Human waste:**
1. **Composting toilet** — legal in most states off-grid; turns waste into compost
   in 6–12 months; requires carbon material (sawdust/wood chips)
   - Options: commercial (Sun-Mar, Nature's Head: $1,000–$2,500) or DIY ($200–$500)
2. **Outhouse** — simple, durable; setback rules (100+ feet from water sources);
   pit lined to prevent groundwater contamination
3. **Septic system** — required by code in many areas; expensive ($5,000–$20,000);
   greywater diversion reduces volume significantly

**Greywater (sinks, shower, laundry):**
1. **Mulch basin system** — greywater from laundry directly to mulched tree basins
2. **Branched drain** — gravity-fed distribution to multiple landscape areas
3. **Constructed wetland / reed bed** — passive biological treatment
4. Legal status: varies by state (California, Arizona are the most permissive)

**Solid waste:**
1. Compost all organic kitchen and garden waste
2. Metal recycling — store and haul to recycling center
3. Reduce/reuse — buy less, repair more; minimal single-use plastics
4. Glass reuse (canning jars)
5. Burn pit / biochar production for non-recyclables

**Cost estimates:**
- Composting toilet (commercial): $1,000–$2,500
- DIY composting toilet: $200–$500
- Outhouse construction (DIY): $300–$800
- Septic system: $5,000–$20,000

### 9. Medicine & Health

**Purpose**: Maintaining health without regular access to hospitals or pharmacies.
Primary care, dental, trauma, childbirth, chronic disease management.

**Essential knowledge and skills:**
1. **First aid and trauma** — hemorrhage control (tourniquets, wound packing),
   fracture splinting, CPR/AED, burn care, shock treatment
2. **Wilderness/remote medicine** — evacuation decisions, improvised care
3. **Herbal medicine** — common medicinal plants, preparation (tinctures, teas, poultices)
4. **Dental basics** — tooth extraction, temporary fillings, abscess treatment
5. **Obstetrics basics** — prenatal nutrition, normal delivery, complications to recognize

**Pharmaceutical stockpile (consult physician):**
- Broad-spectrum antibiotics (amoxicillin, doxycycline, metronidazole, azithromycin)
- Antiparasitic (ivermectin, albendazole)
- Antifungal (fluconazole)
- Pain and fever (ibuprofen, acetaminophen, aspirin)
- Antihistamine (diphenhydramine, cetirizine)
- Epinephrine auto-injector (if any anaphylaxis risk)
- Oral rehydration salts
- Wound care (sutures, staples, steri-strips, iodine, hydrogen peroxide)
- Chronic medications for known conditions (3–6 month supply minimum)

**Herbal medicine garden:**
- Elderberry — antiviral, immune support
- Echinacea — immune stimulant
- Valerian — sleep, anxiety
- Calendula — wound healing, anti-inflammatory
- Yarrow — wound staunching, fever
- Comfrey — bone and tissue healing (external use)
- St. John's Wort — mild depression, nerve pain
- Peppermint — digestive, headache
- Lavender — antiseptic, calming
- Garlic — antibiotic properties, cardiovascular

**Equipment:**
- Blood pressure cuff, stethoscope
- Glucose meter
- Thermometer (oral and rectal)
- Pulse oximeter
- Suture kit
- Dental extraction kit
- Splints and SAM splints
- Otoscope/ophthalmoscope

**Prevention is primary:**
- Clean water is the single biggest health intervention
- Good nutrition prevents most chronic disease
- Physical fitness reduces injury and recovery time
- Mental health — community, purpose, connection; isolation is a major risk

### 11. Communications

**Purpose**: Staying informed, coordinating with community, and maintaining
contact with the outside world — including during grid-down scenarios.

**Tiers:**
1. **Local (site-to-site)** — walkie-talkies / GMRS radios (FCC license required, $35)
2. **Regional** — ham radio (Technician license is entry level, free to study)
   - HF (shortwave): worldwide communication without internet
   - VHF/UHF (2m/70cm): local repeater networks, 50–100+ mile range
3. **Satellite** — Starlink for internet (works off-grid with solar); Iridium or Globalstar
   for satellite phone/messaging when Starlink fails
4. **Shortwave radio receiver** — listen to WWVB time signals, VOA, BBC, news
5. **Emergency band scanner** — monitor local emergency services

**Analog fallback:**
- AM/FM/NOAA weather radio (battery + solar charged)
- Pre-arranged meeting schedules and message drops with community
- Physical runners for emergencies
- Signal mirror, flares, colored smoke for emergency signaling

**Key licenses:**
- GMRS: $35 / 10-year family license, no test
- Technician ham: free test prep, ~$15 exam fee, unlocks VHF/UHF
- General ham: next tier, unlocks HF bands

### 12. Security & Defense

**Purpose**: Protecting people, food, animals, and infrastructure from
human and animal threats. Personal safety without reliance on law enforcement
response times that may be hours in rural areas.

**Approach philosophy:**
- Prevention and deterrence first — most threats never materialize if deterrents are visible
- Community cohesion is the best long-term security
- Avoid armed conflict whenever possible; it creates cascading problems

**Animal threats:**
- Livestock protection: livestock guardian dogs (LGD) for predators (coyotes, foxes, hawks)
- Fencing: 6-strand electric fence deters most predators; hardware cloth for poultry
- Secure coops and hutches: wire buried 12" underground prevents digging predators

**Human threats:**
- Perimeter awareness: dogs, motion sensor lights, camera systems (can be solar-powered)
- Secure storage for fuel, food, and critical equipment
- Community watch — neighbors who watch for each other
- Physical security: solid doors, good locks, window security film

**Firearms (where legal and desired):**
- General purpose rifle: .22 LR (pest control, small game, low cost to stock)
- Defensive shotgun: 12-gauge (versatile, widely available ammunition)
- General hunting rifle: .30-06 or .308 (large game, deer)
- Handgun: personal preference; common calibers for parts availability
- Training is non-negotiable: safe handling, storage (locked safe), proficiency

**Legal considerations:**
- All firearms must comply with state and federal law
- Safe storage required if children are present
- Know your jurisdiction's self-defense laws

### 13. Community & Governance

**Purpose**: Long-term off-grid living is not sustainable as a solo or nuclear
family operation. Community multiplies capabilities, distributes labor, provides
resilience, and is essential for mental health.

**Community structures:**
1. **Informal neighbor network** — most common; mutual aid, tool sharing, skill exchange
2. **Intentional community** — shared land or adjacent parcels; formal agreements
3. **Cooperative / LLC** — shared ownership structure with formal governance
4. **Religious or affinity communities** — cohesion around shared values

**Labor division and specialization:**
- Medical — someone with nursing or EMT training
- Mechanical — diesel, small engine, welding
- Animal husbandry — livestock management
- Food production — growing expertise
- Construction and maintenance
- Communications / radio
- Education / childcare
- Food preservation / cooking

**Governance basics:**
- Decision-making process (consensus vs. majority vote)
- Conflict resolution mechanism
- Resource sharing agreements (what's communal, what's private)
- New member vetting process
- Exit and dissolution procedures

**Psychological considerations:**
- Isolation is the most underrated risk in off-grid planning
- Regular community gathering events (meals, work parties, celebrations)
- Clear roles and meaningful contribution prevents resentment
- Plan for disagreement — it will happen

### 14. Finances & Trade

**Purpose**: Transitioning to partial self-sufficiency without going broke.
Managing the economic bridge period and building local trade networks.

**Financial transition:**
- Do not quit income until systems are proven and debt is low
- Phase 1 (1–2 years): build while employed; land acquisition, major systems
- Phase 2 (2–5 years): income from homestead supplementing job; transition
- Phase 3 (5+ years): homestead income primary or sufficient

**Revenue streams from homestead:**
- Specialty produce (farmers market, CSA, restaurants)
- Eggs and pastured poultry
- Raw milk / artisan cheese (state regulations vary widely)
- Medicinal herbs and dried products
- Instruction / apprenticeships
- Digital products (see Seedwarden model)
- Skilled services (welding, construction, food preservation consulting)

**Barter and local trade:**
- Surplus food for services
- Skills exchange (dental work for labor, medical knowledge for food)
- Tool libraries and equipment sharing with neighbors
- Community currency (LETS systems, time banks)

**Property taxes and carrying costs:**
- Property taxes on rural land: $500–$5,000/year depending on state and acreage
- Homestead exemptions — most states have property tax breaks for primary residence
- Agricultural use designation — can significantly reduce taxes
- LLC / trust structures for asset protection

### 15. Disaster Scenarios

**Purpose**: Planning for high-impact disruptions — from common (extended power
outage, severe storm) to extreme (pandemic, regional grid failure, nuclear event).

#### 15a. Extended Power Outage (1 week – 6 months)
- Off-grid systems provide continuity automatically
- Key risks: supply chain disruption, fuel shortages, communication blackout
- Preparation: well-stocked pantry, full propane tank, generator fuel

#### 15b. Severe Storm / Natural Disaster
- Tornado: underground shelter or interior room, early warning radio
- Flood: site selection (not in flood plain), elevated storage, evacuation plan
- Hurricane (coastal): category 3+ evacuation vs. shelter, storm shutters, water pre-storage
- Wildfire: defensible space (30–100 foot clearance), metal roof, fire-resistant plants, go-bag
- Ice storm: wood heat backup, freeze protection for pipes and water systems

#### 15c. Pandemic / Biological Emergency
- Self-sufficient food and water removes grocery store dependency
- Medical stockpile (see Section 9) covers most acute needs
- Isolation is the core response — off-grid living provides natural social distancing
- PPE stockpile: N95 masks, gloves, eye protection

#### 15d. Economic Collapse / Supply Chain Failure
- Self-sufficiency in food, water, and energy provides insulation
- Barter economy accelerates — community relationships are the asset
- Cash and hard goods (ammunition, fuel, tools, alcohol, salt) hold value
- Debt is dangerous — minimize before collapse scenario

#### 15e. Civil Unrest / Societal Breakdown
- Remote rural location provides natural deterrence and response time
- Community security protocols (see Section 12)
- Information gathering: ham radio, scanner, shortwave
- Low profile — avoid broadcasting wealth or food stores
- Pre-established mutual aid networks with trusted neighbors

#### 15f. Nuclear Event

**Types of nuclear events:**
1. **Dirty bomb** — conventional explosive dispersing radioactive material; limited fallout
   radius; decontamination and evacuation key
2. **Nuclear reactor accident (Chernobyl/Fukushima model)** — downwind contamination,
   iodine-131 thyroid risk, evacuation if within 50-mile plume
3. **Nuclear detonation (strategic weapon)** — the scenario requiring the most planning

**Nuclear detonation: survival considerations**

*Blast zone survival* (within 10 miles of ground zero):
- Virtually impossible without deep hardened shelter
- Best action: evacuation before any warning if in high-target-value area

*Fallout survival* (10–300+ mile radius):
- Fallout is most lethal in first 24–48 hours
- 7-10 rule: radiation intensity drops 90% with every 7× time increase
  (7 hours → 10× less; 49 hours → 100× less; 2 weeks → 1,000× less)
- **Goal**: maximize shielding during first 2 weeks, especially first 24 hours

**Radiation shielding:**
- Best: underground (1–3 feet of earth reduces exposure by 99%)
- Good: basement (reduce exposure 90%+)
- Moderate: center of large concrete building, upper floors (above fallout)
- Poor: wood-frame houses, mobile homes, outdoors

**Fallout shelter requirements:**
- Below-grade construction (basement, root cellar, underground room)
- 2+ feet of earth or 1+ foot of concrete provides good shielding
- Sealed entry to limit particle ingestion
- 2-week food and water supply (see pre-positioned storage plan)
- Radiation dosimeter (Geiger counter, RADTriage cards)
- Potassium iodide (KI) tablets — protect thyroid from I-131 ONLY;
  follow dosing protocol; adults 130 mg; children weight-adjusted
- Air filtration: HEPA filter on any ventilation; positive pressure preferred

**Fallout shelter design for a homestead:**
- Root cellar doubles as fallout shelter with minor modifications
- Add: sealed door with gasket, HEPA ventilation, 14-day supplies
- Radiation monitoring gear: Geiger counter ($100–$500), dosimeter badges
- Communications: battery/hand-crank radio for emergency broadcasts

**Post-fallout:**
- After 2 weeks outdoors is usually survivable with precautions
- Wash exposed produce; avoid leafy greens, milk from grazing animals for 30 days
- Long-lived isotopes (Cs-137, Sr-90) require soil testing before farming resumes
- Deep-tilled garden beds can dilute surface contamination
- Root vegetables less contaminated than leafy greens (soil vs. air deposition)
- Rainwater cisterns (pre-filled before event) safer than open-surface water

**Realistic targets in the US:**
- Strategic military assets, major ports, major cities
- Rural, non-strategic locations are not primary targets
- FEMA's NUREG/CR-7002 maps show fallout plumes by weapon yield and wind patterns
