# Phase 6 Preliminary Source Index
## Domains B–F: 275+ Sources

> **Source landscape overview**: Domain B (Economic Foundations) has the strongest academic-to-practitioner pipeline — complementary currency, cooperative economics, and commons governance are all well-theorized and well-practiced. Domain C (Political Governance) has deep historical and case study foundations (Ostrom, Mondragon, Rojava) and growing contemporary deliberative democracy literature. Domain D (Judicial Independence) has strong restorative justice literature but thin transformative justice documentation (practitioner knowledge outpaces publication). Domain E (Multilateral Coordination) has robust federation theory but thin practitioner documentation at community scale. Domain F (Implementation Feasibility) has the most fragmented literature — institutional research on implementation gaps exists but community-scale practical documentation requires synthesis from adjacent fields.

> **Phase 3 coverage gap statement**: Phase 3 covered governance theory (Ostrom), community coordination models (Zapatista, Rojava, Mondragon, mutual aid networks), information infrastructure (AREDN, GMRS, Kiwix), security, and scaling thresholds (Dunbar's number). Phase 6 B–F fills the gaps Phase 3 explicitly identified: (1) full economic architecture beyond labor coordination; (2) detailed governance models for communities above 150 persons; (3) dispute resolution and rule of law without state; (4) inter-community federation design; (5) practical implementation phasing and bottleneck analysis with cost estimates.

## Domain B: Economic Foundations

**Source landscape**: Strongest domain for academic-practitioner alignment. Complementary currency has 30+ years of rigorous scholarship (Lietaer, Kennedy, Douthwaite); cooperative economics has the Mondragon literature; commons governance has Ostrom and a rich 2024–2025 application literature. The weakest area is crisis economics and scarcity allocation at community scale — the best sources are disaster sociology and historical rationing studies.

**Phase 3 coverage gap**: Phase 3 covered supply chain coordination and mutual aid networks at a logistics level. Phase 6 Domain B fills the gap in full economic architecture: currency design, cooperative economic models, commons governance, and the economic theory of community food sovereignty.

## Domain C: Political Governance

**Source landscape**: Strongest academic literature of all five domains (political science, deliberative democracy, cooperative governance). The practitioner layer is solid for participatory democracy tools (Sociocracy For All, Participatory Budgeting Project, Transition Towns). The gap is in long-duration empirical data on accountability mechanisms in horizontal organizations — most studies are short-term.

**Phase 3 coverage gap**: Phase 3 covered governance basics (Ostrom, Zapatista, Mondragon, Rojava, sortition) at a survey level. Phase 6 Domain C goes deeper into participatory democracy technology, accountability design, emergency authority, and governance failure taxonomy.

## Domain D: Judicial Independence

**Source landscape**: Restorative justice has a robust and growing academic literature (10–25% recidivism reduction documented). Indigenous peacemaking has strong practitioner documentation but underrepresentation in peer-reviewed literature. Transformative justice for serious harms has the weakest published literature relative to practitioner knowledge — this is the domain's primary gap. Community mediation has excellent practitioner documentation through NAFCM and ADR networks.

**Phase 3 coverage gap**: Phase 3 covered internal conflict as a security issue but did not address structured conflict resolution, judicial independence, or enforcement mechanisms. Domain D fills this gap entirely.

## Domain E: Multilateral Coordination

**Source landscape**: Strong theoretical foundation (federation theory, polycentric governance, democratic confederalism). Solid practitioner documentation for Rojava and Zapatista inter-community coordination. The weakest area is formal case studies of federation-scale resource sharing and economic coordination at community level — the closest analogues are rural cooperative federations (NRECA, rural water associations) and the Mondragon cooperative congress.

**Phase 3 coverage gap**: Phase 3 covered single-community coordination (mutual aid networks, supply chains) and noted federated sub-units as the 500–2,000 person solution. Phase 6 Domain E goes deep on the actual design of those federations: constitutional structure, resource sharing protocols, inter-community conflict resolution, and communication infrastructure at regional scale.

## Domain F: Implementation Feasibility

**Source landscape**: The most fragmented domain. Institutional capacity building research is sophisticated but community-scale practical synthesis is sparse. NIST's Community Resilience Planning Guide is the best single practitioner resource. The Zurich Flood Resilience Alliance's 19-country study (2024–2025) is the best recent empirical source. Implementation failure literature is rich in corporate/government contexts but thin for community-scale horizontal organizations.

**Phase 3 coverage gap**: Phase 3 identified confidence gaps explicitly across all domains (cost estimates for communication infrastructure, security in extended disruptions, scaling extrapolations) but did not have a systematic implementation feasibility analysis. Domain F fills this gap with a cost, human capital, sequencing, and risk framework.
