## Why These Modules Now

Phase 5 research (66,442 words) answers how to organize and operate resilient communities. The moment communities receive this content, they will ask three follow-up questions that the Phase 5 corpus does not fully answer:

1. **"How do we actually govern ourselves at scale?"** — The Conflict Resolution Framework and Implementation Playbook give tools, but not a complete governance architecture for communities that are growing or federating.

2. **"How does my household apply this before we have a full community?"** — Phase 5 is community-scale (15-100 people). Most readers are still at household or small-group scale.

3. **"How does this work in our specific region?"** — Zone 5 is named throughout Phase 5, but the Midwest-specific adaptations are unevenly covered across the 12 documents.

These are not gaps in Phase 5 — they are natural extensions that belong to Phase 4 (individual-household-community synthesis and implementation architecture). They can launch June 1 without any Phase 5 publication dependency, because the research base already exists.

All three modules can run in parallel if coordination capacity is available. If capacity is constrained, prioritize in the order listed: Module 1 (governance) has the broadest leverage across the most audience types.

## Module 1: Governance Structures for Community-Scale Organizations

### What

A comprehensive governance framework for 15-100 person communities — extending Phase 5's Conflict Resolution Framework and Community Implementation Playbook into a complete institutional design document.

Phase 5 covers conflict resolution protocols and implementation structure. Module 1 fills the layer above: the permanent governance architecture that communities need once they are past the initial organization phase and scaling toward more complex decisions (resource allocation, land use, membership, long-term planning).

### Scope

Six major sections (6,000-7,000 words, 30-40 citations):

1. **Decision-making architecture** — consensus vs. consent vs. voting; sociocracy, holacracy, and commons governance models; choosing the right model for community size and culture
2. **Leadership structures** — rotating facilitation, elected councils, functional role circles; preventing leadership capture; accountability mechanisms
3. **Scaling governance** — what changes at 15, 30, 60, and 100+ people; governance structures that grow with membership
4. **Representation and inclusion** — how to ensure all voices are heard as the community grows; working groups, committees, councils; decision rights by domain
5. **Conflict within governance** — when the governance process itself generates conflict; meta-governance (governing the governance system)
6. **Implementation roadmap** — 4-phase governance launch sequence for new communities adopting a formal structure for the first time

### Research Foundation

Available without new research sprints:
- Ostrom's commons governance principles (well-documented, high-quality sources available)
- Intentional community governance case studies: Dancing Rabbit (Missouri, Zone 5), Twin Oaks, Earthaven
- Sociocracy and consent-based governance literature (practical implementation resources)
- Worker cooperative governance (extensive NCBA CLUSA, US Federation of Worker Cooperatives documentation)
- Indigenous governance structures (Long House, tribal council models — relevant to relationship-based governance in non-hierarchical settings)

### Distribution Targets

- Foundation for Intentional Community (FIC) — direct relevance
- US Federation of Worker Cooperatives (governance architecture for cooperative enterprises)
- Cooperative Development Foundation
- NCBA CLUSA (cooperative network)
- Permaculture organizations (governance for permaculture design courses and land cooperatives)

### Timeline and Resources

- Duration: 4-6 weeks (June 1 – July 5-15)
- Estimated hours: 30-40 (research + drafting + revision)
- Prerequisites: None — Phase 5 provides the reference layer, not a dependency
- Output: 6,000-7,000 words, 30-40 citations, production-ready document

### How It Bridges Phase 5

Communities reading the Phase 5 Community Implementation Playbook will arrive at the governance sections and want more depth. Module 1 is the explicit next step: "You've organized your community; here's how to govern it over time." Every Phase 5 distribution channel is a potential Module 1 distribution channel.

## Module 2: Household and Individual Scale Adaptation

### What

A household-scale adaptation of the Phase 5 Wave 1+2 core frameworks, covering 1-15 person groups (families, intentional households, multi-household networks that are not yet at community scale).

Phase 5 is explicitly designed for 15-100 person communities. Most readers are still at household scale — a family, a small group of friends, a neighborhood cluster that hasn't formalized yet. Module 2 translates Phase 5's community-scale tools into household-applicable versions.

### Scope

Five documents (5,000-6,000 words combined, 25-35 citations):

1. **Household-scale microgrids** (1,000-1,500 words) — Solar sizing for a 1-2 family residence or 3-4 household cluster; cost analysis for Zone 5; battery options for single-household vs. multi-household; integration path toward community microgrid when the group grows
2. **Household psychological support** (1,000-1,200 words) — Applying Phase 5 trauma and cohesion frameworks to family units; conflict in smaller groups (different dynamics from community-scale); children and adolescent-specific considerations
3. **Household conflict resolution** (800-1,000 words) — Consent-based decision-making in 2-8 person groups; the difference between household and community conflict (intimacy, power dynamics, exit costs); practical exercises adapted from Phase 5
4. **Household implementation playbook** (1,200-1,500 words) — A 4-phase household resilience ramp (individual → household coordination → multi-household network → community integration); decision points for expanding the group
5. **Multi-household to community transition** (800-1,000 words) — When does a household network become a community? How to transition governance, resources, and relationships when crossing the threshold; Phase 5 documents to read at each stage

### Research Foundation

Available without new research sprints:
- Family systems theory (Bowen, structural family therapy models — applicable to resilience contexts)
- Cohousing literature (robust body on small-group coordination and household-scale governance)
- Solar sizing guides for residential installations (widely available from NREL and state extension programs)
- Zone 5 residential energy data (Illinois, Michigan, Iowa, Indiana, Wisconsin — state-level data available)

### Distribution Targets

- Cohousing Association of the United States
- Intentional communities forums (household-scale readers)
- Permaculture networks (individual and household practitioners are primary audiences)
- Homesteading communities (r/homesteading, Homesteaders of America)
- Extended family networks and multigenerational households

### Timeline and Resources

- Duration: 3-4 weeks (June 1 – June 25-30)
- Estimated hours: 20-30 (shorter documents, lighter research)
- Prerequisites: Phase 5 Wave 1+2 should be published (or at minimum available for reference) — Module 2 makes explicit "see Phase 5, Section X for community-scale version" cross-references throughout
- Output: 5 documents, 5,000-6,000 words combined, 25-35 citations

### How It Bridges Phase 5

Most people who receive Phase 5 are not yet organizing a 15-person community. They are a household trying to get more resilient. Module 2 is their entry point — it explicitly references Phase 5 at every scale threshold, creating a natural reading path: household guide → Phase 5 community guide as the household grows.

## Module 3: Zone 5 Regional Deep-Dives

### What

A Midwest-specific supplement showing how climate, soil, infrastructure, and cultural context shape resilience strategies across Illinois, Michigan, Iowa, Indiana, and Wisconsin.

Phase 5 names Zone 5 throughout but covers it unevenly. Microgrids has strong Zone 5 content (winter solar analysis, frost-depth trenching). Food Preservation has moderate Zone 5 content (seasonal calendars). Healthcare Offline and Educational Governance have minimal Zone 5 adaptation. Module 3 produces a single dedicated supplement that practitioners can read alongside Phase 5 to understand how their specific regional context modifies the Phase 5 recommendations.

### Scope

Two documents (4,000-5,000 words combined, 35-45 citations):

**Document 1: Infrastructure and Climate Adaptations in Zone 5** (2,500-3,000 words)

- Winter solar output by state (IL/MI/IA/IN/WI actual insolation data; how to size battery storage for 120+ hour overcast periods)
- Frost dates, growing season length, and implications for food security planning
- Water system freeze/thaw cycles (pipe burial depth, cistern winterization, spring development in frozen ground)
- Zone 5 crop calendars for heirloom varieties (specific cultivars that perform well in each state)
- Aquifer depth and contamination profiles by region (agricultural runoff in Iowa; industrial contamination in Michigan; Lake Michigan basin)

**Document 2: Social and Institutional Context in the Midwest** (1,500-2,000 words)

- Agricultural economy and farmer networks (Practical Farmers of Iowa, Land Stewardship Project, MOSES)
- Rural geography and distance constraints (service radius, mutual aid coordination across sparse populations)
- County extension infrastructure (land-grant universities as local knowledge repositories — University of Minnesota, Michigan State, Purdue, Iowa State, University of Illinois)
- Regional disaster patterns (tornado frequency zones, flood plains, ice storms, derecho corridors)
- Cultural context (agrarian vs. suburban vs. small-city community types across Zone 5 states; different governance norms)

### Research Foundation

High-quality public sources available without new research sprints:
- NOAA climate data by county for Zone 5 states (frost dates, precipitation, solar insolation)
- USDA NRCS soil surveys (aquifer data, contamination maps)
- NREL solar resource maps (seasonal output data by state)
- State cooperative extension publications (all 5 states have robust online libraries)
- Practical Farmers of Iowa, Land Stewardship Project (practitioner-level regional knowledge)

### Distribution Targets

- Land-grant universities: University of Illinois, Michigan State, Purdue, Iowa State, University of Minnesota
- State cooperative extension networks (each of the 5 Zone 5 states)
- NRCS state offices
- Practical Farmers of Iowa, Land Stewardship Project (regional farmer networks)
- State resilience and emergency management councils

### Timeline and Resources

- Duration: 4-6 weeks (June 1 – July 5-15)
- Estimated hours: 25-35 (research-heavy; Zone 5 data requires state-by-state verification)
- Prerequisites: Phase 5 full corpus should be available (Wave 1+2 minimum) to ensure accurate cross-references
- Output: 2 documents, 4,000-5,000 words combined, 35-45 citations

### How It Bridges Phase 5

Every Phase 5 document is more useful to a Zone 5 practitioner when read alongside the regional supplement. Module 3 is the explicit "how this applies to your region" companion. It also opens distribution channels that Phase 5 cannot access directly: land-grant universities and county extension offices are institutional audiences that respond better to regionally specific content than to general community resilience frameworks.

## Execution Options

### Option 1: Full Parallel Launch (4-6 Weeks Total)

All three modules launch June 1. All three are complete by July 5-15.

**Requirements**: 40-45 hours/week (or 3 concurrent researchers, ~15 hours/week each)

**Benefit**: Fastest time to complete supplement layer; all three available for Phase 5 distribution simultaneously

**Risk**: High coordination overhead; if one module falls behind, it may pull focus from Phase 5 publication execution (which should be the higher priority through June 30)

**Recommended only if**: Phase 5 publication is running smoothly on its selected option and additional coordination capacity is available

### Option 2: Sequential (16 Weeks Total)

Module 1 (June 1-July 5), Module 2 (July 5-Aug 1), Module 3 (Aug 1-Sep 1)

**Requirements**: 10-15 hours/week (single researcher)

**Benefit**: Sustainable pace; each module gets full attention; no coordination overhead

**Risk**: Module 3 (Zone 5 deep-dives) won't be available until September — after the primary June Phase 5 distribution window

**Recommended for**: single-person or low-capacity teams

### Option 3: Priority-First Sequential (10 Weeks Total)

Module 1 (June 1-July 5), then Module 2 and Module 3 in parallel (July 5-Aug 1)

**Requirements**: 10-15 hours/week for Module 1; 20-25 hours/week for Modules 2+3 parallel

**Benefit**: Module 1 (highest leverage) launches first; Modules 2+3 follow quickly

**Recommended for**: teams that can expand capacity for a 4-week intensive after Module 1 is complete
