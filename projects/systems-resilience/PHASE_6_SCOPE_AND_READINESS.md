# Phase 6 Scope and Readiness Assessment

> **Purpose**: Define the most valuable Phase 6 domains given what Phase 1-5 has produced. Identify the 2-3 domains that should launch Week 1. Give the user a decision tool to select domain priorities.
> **Research depth**: Lightweight assessment (gaps and feasibility, not full production).
> **Basis**: Phase 1-5 content gap analysis + existing Phase 6 pre-research already committed.

---

## What Phase 1-5 Produced — and What It Left Open

Phase 5 covers 66,442 words across 12 domains. The combined corpus addresses:

- **Community energy infrastructure**: Microgrid architecture, LiFePO4 storage, solar + generator hybrid, interconnection (Wave 1)
- **Animal and ecological systems**: Veterinary care in crisis contexts, livestock care, seed saving (Waves 2+3)
- **Human systems**: Psychological support, conflict resolution, community governance (Wave 2)
- **Household operations**: Food preservation, water purification, healthcare offline, fuel production, educational governance (Wave 3)
- **Communication infrastructure**: Meshtastic mesh networking, amateur radio fallback, FCC Part 15 (Phase 6 pre-research, committed May 26)
- **Equipment repair**: Farm equipment (hand tools, small engine, hydraulics), right-to-repair legal landscape (Phase 6 pre-research, committed May 26)

**What the corpus does NOT cover yet:**

The Phase 5 documents tell communities what to do in each domain in isolation. What they do not provide is:

1. How to **coordinate economic activity** when cash, credit, and supply chains are disrupted
2. How to **rebuild or adapt physical structures** with limited material access and labor (not emergency shelter — permanent adaptation)
3. How to **develop and transmit skills** systematically within a community across generations
4. How **governance structures evolve** as a community scales from 20 to 200 people and faces increasingly complex decisions
5. How communities **interface with or replace** failed institutional systems (courts, schools, clinics, banks)
6. What **psychological recovery** looks like at a 12-month to 3-year timescale (Phase 5 covers acute crisis; the long tail is undocumented)

These are the load-bearing gaps Phase 6 should address.

## Phase 6 Candidate Domains

Ten candidate domains are assessed below. Each entry covers: what problem it solves, how it extends Phase 5, research depth needed, citability, and timeline estimate.

---

### Domain A: Community Economic Resilience

**Problem it solves**: When commercial supply chains, banking, and cash fail, communities need a functioning internal economy. Without this, labor doesn't flow to where it's needed, surpluses rot while shortages deepen, and community cohesion fractures over perceived unfairness. Phase 5 documents produce food, water, and energy — but don't address how communities coordinate exchange.

**How it extends Phase 5**: The community playbook (Phase 5 Wave 2) describes organizational structure. Economic resilience is the economic layer that sits on top — how does the community decide who gets what, how are surpluses managed, how are skills compensated, what replaces money at short to medium term?

**Research depth needed**: Medium. The academic literature on community exchange systems (mutual credit, labor hours, gift economies, cooperative structures) is well-developed. The applied literature (Transition Towns, Mondragon cooperative, LETS systems, community currencies) provides operational precedent. The Zone 5 rural context narrows this to specific, applicable models.

**Citability**: High. Academic (Elinor Ostrom's commons governance work, E.F. Schumacher Institute, New Economics Foundation), practice-based (Transition Towns UK network, Vermont community currency documentation, Berkshire region Time Banks), case studies (Totnes Pound, Salt Spring Island Dollars, Detroit SOUP).

**Timeline estimate**: 20 hours. Well-documented territory; the synthesis work is identifying which models translate to Zone 5 rural/suburban contexts at 20-200 person scale.

---

### Domain B: Household Infrastructure Adaptation

**Problem it solves**: Phase 5 Wave 3 covers operational domains (food, water, fuel) as practices. What it does not cover is the physical infrastructure of the household itself — how to adapt an existing suburban house for wood heat, greywater reuse, expanded food production, or communal-use conversion. This is the bridge between the aspirational (off-grid operations) and the structural (what do you actually modify in your house?).

**How it extends Phase 5**: Complements Phase 5 Wave 3 (food preservation, water systems, fuel production) with the built-environment layer. A household running the water purification protocols from Phase 5 needs specific physical modifications to do so efficiently. This domain provides the construction guidance.

**Research depth needed**: Medium-to-deep. FEMA P-320 (safe rooms), passive solar design for Zone 5, envelope improvements (air sealing, insulation to code and beyond), greywater system codes (Illinois, Iowa, Wisconsin — significant state variation), basement food storage modifications, rainwater harvesting legality. This domain requires more technical precision than most Phase 5 content.

**Citability**: High. FEMA P-320, Building Science Corporation (Joe Lstiburek's cold climate work), Zone 5 passive solar tables (Sustainable Sources, NREL), university extension publications (University of Wisconsin, Iowa State cooperative extension), ISC Storm Safe Room specifications, EPA WaterSense program.

**Timeline estimate**: 30-40 hours. Higher technical precision required; Zone 5-specific building codes add complexity. This is the deepest domain in the candidate list.

### Domain C: Skills Development and Knowledge Transmission

**Problem it solves**: Phase 5 produces knowledge documents. But knowing that you need to rotate grazing paddocks is different from having the skill to do it, the knowledge of how to teach it, and a community system for ensuring the skill doesn't die with one person. This domain covers how communities build, verify, and transmit practical competency — not as an educational philosophy exercise, but as a survival-critical infrastructure layer.

**How it extends Phase 5**: Phase 5's educational governance document (Wave 3) covers schooling structures. This is different — it covers adult skill development, apprenticeship models, skill inventory systems, and knowledge preservation across death, injury, or departure of key community members.

**Research depth needed**: Medium. The literature on adult learning, apprenticeship, and knowledge preservation is large. The Zone 5 resilience context narrows it: which skills are most critical (from Phase 5 domains), how are they taught without formal institutions, how does a community audit its skill gaps and address them?

**Citability**: Medium-high. Academic (situated learning theory, legitimate peripheral participation), practice-based (guild apprenticeship models, Amish knowledge transmission, permaculture design courses as case studies), applied (FEMA Community Emergency Response Team training architecture, Red Cross disaster preparedness training sequences), some peer-reviewed education research.

**Timeline estimate**: 20 hours. The conceptual framework is well-developed; the application to resilience communities requires focused synthesis but not deep new research.

---

### Domain D: Governance Evolution and Scaling

**Problem it solves**: Phase 5 Wave 2 covers conflict resolution and community governance under stress. That document assumes a roughly stable community size and crisis context. This domain addresses what happens when a community of 20 people grows to 50, then 100, then 200 — and the governance structures that worked at small scale fail at large scale, generating factionalism, authority contests, and decision paralysis.

**How it extends Phase 5**: The conflict resolution framework (Phase 5 Wave 2) provides tools for managing acute conflict. This domain provides the constitutional and structural layer — how do you design governance that scales, how do you amend it as conditions change, how do you manage succession when founding members leave or die?

**Research depth needed**: Medium. The academic literature on sociocracy, consensus governance, and deliberative democracy is directly applicable. Case studies (Mondragon cooperative evolution, Zapatista community governance, Israeli kibbutz governance failure and reform) provide real-world scaling experience. The Phase 5 conflict resolution document can be extended rather than replicated.

**Citability**: High. Sociocracy literature (Polis Institute, Sociocracy for All), deliberative democracy theory (Habermas, Rawls), case studies (Mondragon via Whyte & Whyte "Making Mondragon"), commons governance (Ostrom "Governing the Commons"), community governance failure analysis (kibbutz movement studies).

**Timeline estimate**: 20 hours. Strong academic and practice foundation; synthesis and Zone 5 application is the primary work.

---

### Domain E: Mutual Aid Network Architecture

**Problem it solves**: Individual communities cannot be fully self-sufficient in all domains. A community with good food production but weak healthcare needs connection to a community with medical capacity, and vice versa. This domain covers how inter-community mutual aid networks are architected, formalized, maintained, and activated — including trust-building before crisis, resource coordination during crisis, and obligations when one community fails.

**How it extends Phase 5**: The Meshtastic communications pre-research (Phase 6, committed May 26) provides the communication infrastructure for inter-community coordination. This domain provides the social and governance architecture that sits on top of it — what are the agreements, what are the obligations, how does the network handle defection?

**Research depth needed**: Medium. Hurricane Helene mutual aid networks (2024), COVID-19 mutual aid explosion (2020-2021), and the academic literature on disaster mutual aid all provide documented precedent. The challenge is translating from informal emergency mutual aid (which is well-documented) to durable formalized networks (which is less documented).

**Citability**: Medium. Academic (disaster sociology literature, Solnit "A Paradise Built in Hell"), practice-based (Hurricane Helene NC Mesh and mutual aid documentation, COVID mutual aid coordinators' retrospectives, Mutual Aid Hub documentation), some peer-reviewed emergency management research. The Zone 5 specific data is sparse.

**Timeline estimate**: 20 hours. Good general literature; Zone 5 rural inter-community dynamics require some extrapolation from documented cases.

---

### Domain F: Psychological Recovery — Long Arc (Months 6-36)

**Problem it solves**: Phase 5 Wave 2's psychological support guide covers acute crisis (weeks to months). The 6-36 month recovery window is structurally different: grief is no longer acute but becomes chronic, community conflict rooted in sustained scarcity becomes entrenched, burnout among community leaders peaks, and post-traumatic growth (or post-traumatic stress disorder) is determined. This domain covers what communities need psychologically beyond the acute phase.

**How it extends Phase 5**: Directly extends the psychological support guide (Phase 5 Wave 2), adding the longitudinal dimension that the Phase 5 document explicitly deferred.

**Research depth needed**: Medium-deep. Post-disaster psychology literature at the 6-36 month scale is well-developed (post-Katrina, post-9/11, post-COVID burnout studies). The application to intentional resilience communities (as opposed to disaster-struck populations) requires more synthesis work.

**Citability**: High. Peer-reviewed trauma psychology literature (Herman "Trauma and Recovery"), post-disaster longitudinal studies (Katrina 3-year follow-up, Hurricane Maria 2-year study), burnout research (Maslach burnout inventory validated literature), community resilience frameworks (Norris et al. "Community Resilience as a Metaphor").

**Timeline estimate**: 25 hours. Dense peer-reviewed literature requires more time to synthesize than practice-based domains; longitudinal studies span multiple years of data.

---

### Domain G: Legal Continuity and Community Institutions

**Problem it solves**: When courts, law enforcement, and government administrative systems are disrupted or delegitimized, communities need to manage property rights, dispute adjudication, contractual obligations, and the basic rule-of-law functions that make sustained cooperation possible. This is not the same as conflict resolution (Phase 5 Wave 2) — it is the question of legitimate authority and formal record-keeping under conditions where the state is absent or hostile.

**How it extends Phase 5**: The scaling pathways document (Phase 3) identifies legal structure as a load-bearing institutional bridge. This domain builds the actual content of that bridge — what legal continuity looks like, what communities can formalize without state recognition, and where state law still provides a framework even under crisis conditions.

**Research depth needed**: Medium-deep. Legal resilience is underresearched in the academic literature compared to physical preparedness domains. Closest analogs: legal aid clinics in conflict zones, Indigenous communities operating outside state law, disaster law (FEMA authorities, state emergency powers), community land trusts and their governance. This requires more original synthesis.

**Citability**: Medium. Legal scholarship (disaster law journals, Brennan Center rule-of-law research), practice-based (community land trust governance documents, Transition Town legal frameworks, Zapatista autonomous legal structures as documented by Chiapas scholars), UN rule-of-law and community governance documentation.

**Timeline estimate**: 30 hours. Less accessible literature; more original synthesis work required; Zone 5-specific legal context (Illinois, Iowa, Wisconsin state law) requires state-level research.

---

### Domain H: Supply Chain Alternatives and Local Production Networks

**Problem it solves**: Phase 5 documents assume the community can source what it needs to run the documented systems — fuel for chainsaws, medications, fertilizer, hardware. This domain addresses what happens when external supply chains are persistently disrupted: which inputs can be produced locally, which can be substituted, which dependencies are unavoidable, and how do communities build local production capacity before crisis?

**How it extends Phase 5**: The fuel production document (Phase 5 Wave 3) covers one supply chain alternative. This domain systematizes the analysis across all Phase 5 domains — identifying the critical external dependencies in each and mapping local production alternatives.

**Research depth needed**: Medium. NRCS, ATTRA, and university extension all have documentation on local input production. The synthesis work is mapping dependencies across the Phase 5 corpus and identifying which gaps are addressable locally.

**Citability**: High. NRCS documentation, ATTRA, Iowa State and University of Wisconsin cooperative extension, community-scale manufacturing literature (appropriate technology movement), small-scale chemical production (soap, lye, basic pharmaceuticals) from period literature and current practice.

**Timeline estimate**: 20 hours. The individual pieces are well-documented; the synthesis across all Phase 5 domains is the primary work.

---

### Domain I: Organizational Redundancy and Succession Planning

**Problem it solves**: Communities fail not because their plans are bad but because key people leave, burn out, or die — taking institutional knowledge with them. This domain covers how communities build redundancy into their organizational structure: role duplication, knowledge documentation, succession protocols, and the social architecture that distributes authority rather than concentrating it.

**How it extends Phase 5**: Directly extends the community implementation playbook (Phase 5 Wave 2) and the governance documents (Phase 3 + Phase 5 Wave 2) with the operational resilience layer — how do you keep the community functioning when founding members are gone?

**Research depth needed**: Light-to-medium. Organizational continuity literature (business continuity planning, nonprofit succession), knowledge management (documented expertise capture), family business succession research (closest analog to small community succession). Well-documented in organizational studies.

**Citability**: High. Business continuity standard (ISO 22301), knowledge management literature (Davenport & Prusak "Working Knowledge"), nonprofit succession planning guides (BoardSource), family business research.

**Timeline estimate**: 15 hours. Well-documented in organizational literature; translation to community resilience context is the primary synthesis work.

---

### Domain J: Children, Youth, and Intergenerational Capacity

**Problem it solves**: Children and youth are the long-term insurance policy for community resilience. A community that fails to engage and develop its youth loses institutional knowledge within one generation. This domain covers age-appropriate skill development, youth governance participation, the psychological needs of children in prolonged crisis, and the social structures that transmit community values and practical competency across generations.

**How it extends Phase 5**: The educational governance document (Phase 5 Wave 3) covers schooling structures. This domain goes deeper into the developmental and sociological dimensions — how children learn in crisis contexts, what age-appropriate participation looks like, how intergenerational relationships build community cohesion.

**Research depth needed**: Medium. Child development research (stress and learning), applied models (Amish youth work integration, kibbutz youth governance, Indigenous coming-of-age education), crisis education literature (UNICEF education in emergencies), Zone 5 homeschool networks.

**Citability**: Medium-high. Developmental psychology literature, UNICEF education in emergencies documentation, homeschool cooperative literature, applied case studies.

**Timeline estimate**: 20 hours. Solid academic foundation; the resilience community application requires focused synthesis.

## Critical Path: Which Domains Launch Phase 6 Week 1

### Recommended Week 1 Launches (3 domains, June 1-7)

**Domain A (Community Economic Resilience)** — launches Week 1

This is the highest-value domain not covered in Phase 1-5. Every other domain in Phase 5 produces something (food, water, power, healthcare) but says nothing about how the community coordinates exchange of those goods and services. Without an economic layer, even a well-organized community produces resource distribution failures. Starting here fills the most glaring gap in the current corpus.

**Domain C (Skills Development and Knowledge Transmission)** — launches Week 1

Phase 5 assumes communities have the skills to execute the documented procedures. That assumption is false for most communities. The skills development domain is the enabler layer — it makes Phase 5 actually usable. It is also highly feasible: 20 hours, well-cited literature, and directly extends the educational governance document already produced.

**Domain D (Governance Evolution and Scaling)** — launches Week 1

The conflict resolution and community playbook documents (Phase 5 Wave 2) already exist. Domain D extends them without replicating them. The scaling question — what breaks at 50, 100, 200 people — is the next unanswered question for any community that successfully implements Phase 5. Starting this in Week 1 ensures the governance thread continues from Phase 5 without a gap.

### Week 2-3 Domains

**Domain E (Mutual Aid Network Architecture)** — Week 2

Depends on the Meshtastic communications pre-research (already complete). Week 1 is needed to confirm how the governance and economic domains frame inter-community relationships before building the network architecture on top.

**Domain I (Organizational Redundancy)** — Week 2

Short timeline (15 hours), accessible literature, directly extends the community playbook. Can be executed in parallel with other Week 2 work without significant research overlap.

**Domain F (Psychological Recovery — Long Arc)** — Week 2

Extends Phase 5 Wave 2's psychological support guide. Can begin once Domain D (governance scaling) is underway, since community governance and psychological health at the 6-36 month mark are interdependent.

**Domain H (Supply Chain Alternatives)** — Week 2

Requires Phase 5 corpus review to map dependencies. Cannot be fully scoped until the researcher has identified which inputs each Phase 5 domain requires. Week 2 start allows Week 1 to establish the dependency framework.

### Week 3+ Domains

**Domain B (Household Infrastructure Adaptation)** — Week 3

Deep domain (35 hours, technical precision, state-specific building codes). Should not be a Week 1 launch — it needs the community-scale domains (A, D) to establish context before drilling into household physical adaptation.

**Domain G (Legal Continuity)** — Week 3

Most complex domain (30 hours, sparse literature, requires state-level legal research). Week 3 allows the governance scaling work (Domain D) to inform the legal continuity framing — the two domains are interdependent.

**Domain J (Children, Youth, Intergenerational)** — Week 3

Extends educational governance (Phase 5 Wave 3). Lower urgency than governance, economics, and mutual aid. Important for long-term community health but not load-bearing for community formation and initial stabilization.

## Recommended Top-3 Phase 6 Starting Domains

Based on gap analysis, feasibility, and what the Phase 1-5 corpus most conspicuously lacks:

1. **Domain A — Community Economic Resilience** (20 hours, high value, no prerequisites): The single largest gap. Every Phase 5 domain produces goods or services but none addresses exchange coordination.

2. **Domain C — Skills Development and Knowledge Transmission** (20 hours, high value, no prerequisites): The enabler layer that makes Phase 5 usable. Phase 5 documents procedures; this domain builds the human capacity to execute them.

3. **Domain D — Governance Evolution and Scaling** (20 hours, high value, no prerequisites): Extends the Phase 5 governance documents with the constitutional and scaling dimension that the current corpus defers.

These three domains can be researched in parallel during Phase 6 Week 1. They are independent of each other, they are independent of Phase 6 pre-research (Farm Equipment, Meshtastic), and they together fill the most critical structural gap in the current corpus: the community is organized and operational (Phase 5) — now it needs to sustain, scale, and coordinate economically.
