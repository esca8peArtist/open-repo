# Phase 5 Research Briefing — All Three Paths

> **Purpose**: Extend Phase 4b options analysis with new research on hidden constraints, refined cost/timeline estimates, and specific resources for each path. This is preliminary depth — not execution-level — to inform the June 1 decision.
>
> **What is new here**: The Phase 4b options comparison established the case for each path. This document goes deeper on the concrete implementation questions that comparison document left open: actual per-acre costs with 2024–2025 data, hidden constraints that alter sequencing, government funding angles, and practical staging recommendations.

---

## Most Important New Finding

**Agricultural Intensification has a hidden capital constraint the Phase 4b analysis understated.** Establishing even 1 acre of perennial food forest at minimal viable density (hazelnut, elderberry, serviceberry, chestnuts) costs $2,800–$4,500 in Year 1 plant stock and site preparation alone — before labor. At community scale (3–5 acres), capital requirements reach $8,500–$18,000 before any trees reach production. This is not a reason to abandon the path. It is a reason to pursue NRCS EQIP cost-share aggressively before planting: up to 75% of establishment costs are reimbursable under Practice Standard 381 (Silvopasture) and AF1016 (Agroforestry/Woody Crop Establishment). An application submitted in spring 2026 positions for FY2026 payments.

**Governance Scaling's FEMA BRIC angle is weaker than the Phase 4b analysis suggested.** BRIC 2026 prioritizes infrastructure projects with 30–100% design completion and quantified benefit-cost ratios. Planning documents and inter-municipal agreements alone are not eligible for project funding — BRIC no longer funds standalone planning. The path is still valuable for reasons documented in Phase 4b, but the FEMA funding argument requires a shovel-ready infrastructure project underneath the governance framework, not just the framework itself.

**Knowledge Preservation has a new urgency signal from 2025.** A November 2025 Cloudflare outage took down 20% of the global web — including AI services, major platforms, and cloud-hosted tools — for a multi-hour period. This prompted a measurable surge in Kiwix deployments and offline knowledge infrastructure in late 2025. The practical implication: the Kiwix deployment guide component of this path is directly applicable to disruption scenarios that are closer to present conditions than the "Year 2+" framing in Phase 4b suggested.

## Path 1: Agricultural Intensification — Updated Analysis

### Revised Capital Cost Estimates

The Phase 4b analysis cited $2,000–$5,000 as the capital range for initial planting. More granular 2024–2025 data from nursery pricing and USDA cost-of-production research sharpens this considerably.

**Per-plant costs (Zone 5, bare-root or container stock, 2024–2025 prices):**
- Hybrid hazelnut seedlings (1st year): $5 per plant; 2nd year seedlings $10 per plant
- Elderberry rooted cuttings: $3–$6 per plant from regional propagators
- Serviceberry bare-root: $8–$15 per plant
- Hybrid chestnut (grafted): $18–$35 per plant (grafted varieties for blight resistance)
- Hardy kiwi ('Issai' self-fertile): $12–$20 per plant

**1/4 acre demonstration planting (20 hazelnuts, 15 elderberries, 10 serviceberries, 5 chestnuts):**
- Plant stock: $350–$700
- Tree tubes (50 x $5): $250
- Soil test: $25
- Compost amendment (10 cubic yards at $30/yd): $300
- Wood chips for sheet mulch: $0–$150 (often free from tree services)
- Total: $925–$1,375 in Year 1

**1-acre food forest establishment (community scale, Year 1):**
- 30 hazelnut, 20 elderberry, 15 serviceberry, 10 chestnut, 6 hardy kiwi, 4 pawpaw
- Plant stock: $900–$1,800
- Tree tubes (80 x $5): $400
- Biochar amendment (3 tons at $350/ton, one-time): $1,050
- Compost (20 cubic yards at $30/yd): $600
- Soil testing and site preparation: $500
- Labor for planting day (community: volunteer, 20 people × 1 day): $0 if mutual aid
- **Total Year 1 capital: $3,450–$4,350**

**3-acre community system (adds Kernza field + annual garden site):**
- Additional plant stock for acres 2–3: $2,500–$4,000
- Kernza seed (3 acres × $40/acre): $120
- Irrigation infrastructure for establishment years: $500–$1,500
- **Total Year 1 capital: $6,570–$9,970**

**NRCS cost-share opportunity (critical, not in Phase 4b analysis):**
EQIP Practice Standard 381 (Silvopasture) and AF1016 (Agroforestry Woody Crop Establishment via MU Extension) cover up to 75% of establishment costs for qualifying perennial systems. The Savanna Institute explicitly documents how to find this assistance and partners with NRCS Wisconsin and Minnesota on demonstration sites. A FY2026 EQIP application submitted through the local NRCS field office by late spring 2026 could recover $2,500–$7,500 on a community-scale planting — substantially reducing net capital. This is the single most important financial angle in the Agricultural Intensification path and should be pursued in parallel with planting, not after.

### Yield Estimates — What's Confirmed vs. Projected

The Phase 4b analysis cited Savanna Institute and Red Fern Farm yield data. This briefing distinguishes confirmed from projected:

**Confirmed by multi-year Midwest field trials:**
- Elderberry: 7,000–12,000 lbs/acre at full production (Year 3–5); first production Year 1–2. Most reliable early-return species.
- Serviceberry: 8–10 lbs/plant at Year 5–8; early June harvest window is confirmed as Zone 5's first tree fruit.
- Kernza: 400–600 lbs/acre in Year 2 from fall seeding confirmed by Land Institute and University of Minnesota Extension 2023 report. Year 1 yields are negligible.
- Hardy kiwi 'Issai': 40–70 lbs/vine at full production confirmed (Penn State Extension); Year 1–2 first small yields confirmed.

**Projected from limited trial data (higher uncertainty):**
- Hybrid hazelnut: 1,500–2,500 lbs/acre at full production. Actual Midwest trial yields vary widely by cultivar, site, and blight pressure. Commercial hazelnut production does not yet exist in the upper Midwest at scale; the Midwest Hazelnut Development Initiative's own economic data is drawn from production trials rather than commercial operations.
- Hybrid chestnut: Red Fern Farm (Wapello, Iowa, Zone 5b) is the most credible single-site data source. The 10-acre figure of 35,000–40,000 lbs at maturity reflects one exceptional operator; community-scale projections should use 60–70% of this for planning.
- Pawpaw understory yields: The 50–75% of full-sun yield figure from Red Fern Farm is based on one site integration with chestnuts. More conservative planning should assume 40–60%.

**Practical implication**: The berry and vine layer (elderberry, serviceberry, hardy kiwi) produces reliably by Year 2–5 with lower uncertainty. The nut layer (hazelnut, chestnut) is the backbone of caloric contribution but carries the highest execution uncertainty. Plan food security around the reliable early layer; treat nuts as a bonus that compounds over time.

### Hidden Constraints and Blockers

**Nursery stock availability**: Bare-root perennial stock in Zone 5 sells out early. Hazelnuts from Midwest Hazelnut and Savanna Institute partner nurseries frequently sell out by early April. An order placed in late May is likely too late for bare-root stock; container-grown plants are the fallback but cost 2–3x more and have lower establishment rates in summer heat. **Action: Order by mid-April at the absolute latest. Ideally by March.**

**Deer pressure**: Identified in the Phase 4b pre-research document but worth underscoring as a hard cost. In rural Zone 5, deer pressure without exclusion fencing or tree tubes is essentially certain to kill all young plantings within 1–3 seasons. Tree tubes are non-negotiable and must be budgeted at $3–8 per tree for the entire planting, not just a subset.

**Eastern Filbert Blight (EFB) for hazelnuts**: The upper Midwest (Minnesota, Wisconsin) has lower EFB pressure than the Eastern US, but it is present. Only Badgersett Research Farm (Canton, MN) and Savanna Institute partner nurseries are reliably producing EFB-resistant hybrid selections for upper Midwest conditions. Do not order European hazelnut (Corylus avellana) varieties — they are highly susceptible. Specifically request American × European hybrid material bred in the upper Midwest.

**Zone 5a vs 5b distinction**: Minnesota and northern Wisconsin are Zone 5a (-20°F minimum); Illinois and southern Iowa are Zone 5b (-15°F). This distinction matters for chestnuts: most Chinese-American hybrids rated Zone 5 are calibrated for Zone 5b. In Zone 5a, confirm cultivar cold-hardiness specifically, particularly for grafted material.

## Path 2: Knowledge Preservation — Updated Analysis

### Revised Timeline and Capital Estimates

The Phase 4b analysis estimated 13–19 hours of research/writing effort and minimal capital ($50–$200). This remains accurate. The new finding is that the capital cost breakdown is more granular and lower than the range suggests for a minimum viable deployment:

**Kiwix server minimum viable build (2025 pricing):**
- Raspberry Pi 4 (4GB RAM): $55
- 128GB high-endurance microSD card (for smaller ZIM files): $20
- 2TB USB SSD drive (for full Wikipedia + Khan Academy + iFixit): $60–$80
- Case and power supply: $15
- Total hardware: $150–$170

**Key ZIM file sizes (2025 data):**
- English Wikipedia with images: ~109 GB (requires SSD, not SD card)
- English Wikipedia without images: ~50 GB
- Khan Academy (all subjects): ~8 GB
- iFixit repair manuals: ~3 GB
- Wiktionary: ~6 GB
- Medical resources (WikiMed): ~3 GB
- Offline package for Zone 5 resilience (Wikipedia + iFixit + WikiMed + Khan Academy): ~124 GB total — fits on a 2TB SSD with substantial room for additional resources

**Power draw**: Raspberry Pi 4 draws approximately 3.4–7.6W under load; a solar panel and small battery bank can sustain it indefinitely off-grid. This is the correct spec for an off-grid knowledge server.

**Archival print materials (analog backup):**
- Acid-free archival paper (500 sheets): $15–$25
- Pigment-based ink cartridge set (for longevity): $30–$60
- Acid-free archival boxes (3): $25–$45
- Total for print archival kit: $70–$130

**Total capital for minimum viable knowledge preservation system: $220–$300**

### What the 2025 Cloudflare Outages Reveal

Two major Cloudflare outages in late 2025 (November 18 and December 5) each took down approximately 20% of the global web for multi-hour periods. Services affected included AI assistants, social media platforms, SaaS tools, and content delivery networks. The disruptions were temporary, but they demonstrated a structural dependency: most communities have zero offline information infrastructure.

The practical implication for this path: Kiwix is not only a Year 2+ disruption tool. It is immediately relevant for any event that degrades internet connectivity — whether a major outage, a regional emergency, a prolonged ice storm, or a deliberate infrastructure attack. The framing in Phase 4b as a "Year 2+ failure mode" should be updated. Kiwix deployment is a Day 30 action (after a community has basic water, food, and shelter stable), not a Year 2 action.

This also shifts the priority weighting within the path: the Kiwix deployment guide is the most urgently applicable output, followed by the Community Knowledge Audit worksheet, followed by the oral transmission and archival infrastructure sections.

### Hidden Constraints and Blockers

**Kiwix SD card reliability**: The standard Raspberry Pi SD card is not designed for continuous read-write operations. Standard SD cards fail within 6–18 months of continuous server use. **Required mitigation**: specify high-endurance SD cards (designed for dashcam use) or SSD storage. Samsung Pro Endurance or SanDisk MAX Endurance cards are appropriate. An SSD via USB is more reliable for Wikipedia-scale deployments and should be the default specification in the document.

**ZIM file update cycle**: ZIM files for Wikipedia and other sources are updated periodically (Wikipedia: approximately annually). A knowledge server that is not periodically updated becomes stale. The document must specify: (1) annual ZIM file refresh during internet availability, (2) the specific URL for the Kiwix ZIM file library (library.kiwix.org), and (3) a procedure for downloading updated files on a laptop and transferring via USB to the Pi without requiring internet on the Pi itself.

**Community Knowledge Audit — adoption risk**: The biggest risk in Knowledge Preservation is not the technology — it is that the Community Knowledge Audit worksheet is completed but the identified gaps are never acted upon. The Phase 4b description says the audit "can be conducted in an evening," which is accurate, but it does not address what happens next. The document needs to include a follow-up protocol: what to do when you discover that only one person knows how to operate the community's water treatment system, and that person is 74 years old. The Foxfire apprenticeship model provides this: structured intergenerational pairing, defined competency milestones, and documentation of the transfer. This section needs to be the heart of the document, not a footnote.

**NEH grant cycle timing**: The Phase 4b analysis noted NEH grant cycles typically close in January. A community that completes its Knowledge Audit and oral history documentation in summer/fall 2026 would be well-positioned to apply in January 2027. This is a realistic funding pathway that should be explicitly described in the document with the NEH Cultural and Community Resilience Program URL and eligibility criteria.

## Path 3: Governance Scaling — Updated Analysis

### FEMA BRIC Angle — Correction to Phase 4b Analysis

The Phase 4b options comparison stated that a written governance framework and inter-municipal agreements position a community to apply for FEMA BRIC funding. This is partially correct but requires important qualification based on the 2026 NOFO.

**What BRIC 2026 actually funds**: Infrastructure projects with 30–100% design completion and quantified benefit-cost ratios. Transportation, utilities, water systems, public buildings — tangible infrastructure with measurable risk reduction. Standalone planning documents are explicitly ineligible.

**What BRIC does not fund**: Planning activities, governance frameworks, or inter-municipal agreements by themselves.

**Where governance documents help with BRIC**: A community that has a signed inter-municipal agreement for shared water infrastructure, plus 30%+ design for a water system hardening project, is substantially more competitive than one without the agreement. The governance document enables the fundable project — it is a prerequisite layer, not the application itself.

**Practical implication**: The FEMA BRIC argument should be reframed. Governance Scaling's value is not "write a document and get FEMA money." It is: "establish the governance agreements that enable infrastructure projects, which then become fundable." This is still valuable and strategically correct, but communities should not expect BRIC funding for governance work alone. The July 23 deadline is relevant only if there is an underlying infrastructure project to fund.

**Alternative funding for governance work**: The FEMA Hazard Mitigation Grant Program (HMGP) and Building Codes Plus-Up do fund planning activities — but typically only after a declared disaster. Pre-disaster governance planning is more appropriately funded through state-level programs, USDA Rural Development Community Facilities grants, or the Pew Charitable Trust's pre-disaster mitigation advocacy funding.

### Revised Governance Timeline — What Research Shows

The Phase 4b analysis described Governance Scaling as the fastest to complete (12–17 research/writing hours). This is correct for document production. But the more important question for the June 1 decision is: how long does it take to implement functional governance for a small community, and what are the failure modes?

**From intentional community research:**

Sociocracy for All documents that the upper effective limit for a single-circle decision process is approximately 40 people — groups larger than this need to subdivide into linked circles. For a community of 10–50 people (the core Phase 5 target), a single-circle sociocratic model works well. The consent decision-making process (acceptance when there are "no reasoned objections") is faster than consensus and better-suited to emergency governance contexts than voting.

**Timeline to functional governance (from co-housing and intentional community field experience):**
- Draft governing agreements: 2–4 weeks (document production)
- First consensus on roles, responsibilities, and decision scope: 3–6 weeks of meetings
- First real contested decision under the new governance model: 2–6 months after formation
- Stable, reliable governance with conflict resolution track record: 12–24 months

**This is the key hidden constraint**: governance documents written in 2026 will not produce stable, tested governance systems until 2027–2028 at earliest. This is not an argument against doing the work — it is an argument for starting earlier rather than later. Communities that have governance agreements in writing and have held even a few meetings under them are far better positioned when a crisis forces the first contested resource allocation decision.

**Most common failure modes (co-housing and ecovillage research):**
1. Governance structure exists on paper but is abandoned when the first major conflict arises and the group defaults to informal power dynamics
2. Louder or longer-tenured members dominate decisions despite nominal consensus processes
3. Volunteer governance roles burn out because responsibilities are not clearly bounded and distributed
4. No conflict resolution escalation path — interpersonal conflicts escalate to community crises

**Mitigations documented in the literature:**
- Clear, written role descriptions with defined scope and rotation schedule
- Separate channels for operational decisions (one person with authority) and policy decisions (full group consent)
- Pre-agreed conflict resolution escalation: self-resolution → peer mediation → external mediator → binding arbitration
- Decision log accessible to all members (written record prevents "we never decided that" disputes)

### Iowa, Minnesota, and Wisconsin Township Law — What's Findable

The Phase 4b analysis promised township authority documentation for all three states but did not provide it. This briefing fills the gap with publicly available legal sources:

**Iowa**: Township trustees have authority under Iowa Code §359.17 to conduct meetings and manage township functions. Iowa townships may contract for fire and emergency services under Section 28E.32 — written contracts required, filed with Secretary of State. The Taylor County Iowa Township Trustee-Clerk Reference Manual provides a model fire/emergency services contract (Appendix C) that can be adapted for inter-municipal coordination.

**Wisconsin**: Wisconsin Statute §66.0301(7) authorizes municipalities to enter intergovernmental cooperation contracts to "jointly carry out municipal responsibilities." This is the enabling statute for any inter-municipal agreement in Wisconsin — the IMA template in the Governance Scaling document should cite this statute.

**Minnesota**: Minnesota has both township and municipal government structures. Township boards (elected three-person boards) have authority over township roads, fire districts, and emergency services under Minnesota Statutes Chapter 365. The Association of Minnesota Counties and the Minnesota Association of Townships both publish template agreements for joint services.

The Phase 4b document stated this material "adds 2–3 hours but is findable in public documents." This is accurate. The IMA template in the Governance Scaling document should be structured with fill-in-blank state-specific authority citations.

### Sociocracy as the Recommended Decision Framework

The Phase 4b analysis referenced Ostrom's 8 Design Principles as the theoretical backbone. This remains the right foundation for commons resource management. But for the practical decision-making process in a 10–50 person community, sociocracy (specifically, Sociocracy 3.0 / S3) is the most field-tested framework with the strongest implementation support:

- **Consent-based** (not consensus — no "everyone must agree," only "no reasoned objections")
- **Circle structure** scales naturally from 10 to 500+ people through linked circles
- **Role-based operations** with accountable individuals (not committees with diffuse responsibility)
- **Upper practical limit**: 40 people in a single circle with full discussion rounds
- **Foundation for Intentional Community** provides workshops and resources for implementing sociocracy

The Governance Scaling document should specify sociocracy as the recommended decision-making model for communities of 10–50 people, with Ostrom's Design Principles providing the normative framework for resource allocation and conflict resolution.

## Recommended Staging Order and Hybrid Approach

### The Case for All Three in Sequence

Based on this research, the strongest recommendation is to execute all three paths in the following sequence, rather than treating them as mutually exclusive:

**Phase 5A (May–June 2026): Agricultural Intensification**
The biological deadline is irreversible. Order plants immediately if not already done. Pursue EQIP application in parallel with planting — contact the local NRCS field office this week to start the paperwork. The EQIP process takes 4–8 weeks; submitting now means payment could arrive before or during planting. Research and document production (15–22 hours) runs in parallel with planting execution.

**Phase 5B (June–July 2026): Governance Scaling**
Start the governance document after Agricultural Intensification is underway. The timing is right: the federal devolution of emergency management is accelerating, the FEMA BRIC window (July 23) creates a near-term catalyst even if BRIC itself only funds infrastructure (it motivates town council conversations about resilience planning), and the Governance Scaling content builds the inter-community coordination layer that Agricultural Intensification will eventually require (shared labor, shared equipment, shared seed stock across communities). If BRIC is a meaningful target, community contact with the State Hazard Mitigation Office should begin no later than June 15 — states establish earlier internal deadlines than the federal July 23 date.

**Phase 5C (August–September 2026): Knowledge Preservation**
Most deferrable of the three. No seasonal deadline. Execute after the other two paths are complete and underway. The Kiwix deployment can be assembled in a weekend; the Community Knowledge Audit can be conducted in an evening once the worksheet is written. The document production (13–19 hours) is the fastest of the three.

**Combined effort: 40–58 hours, May–September 2026.**

This is the complete Phase 5 scope if all three are pursued sequentially. The phasing avoids document production conflicts (each path has a natural start date staggered by the previous path's completion), creates a logical layering (agricultural intensification establishes what to govern; governance establishes how communities coordinate; knowledge preservation documents what communities know), and captures all three June–July funding/timing windows.

### Hybrid Approaches for Constrained Resources

**No land access (suburban or renting):**
Skip or miniaturize Agricultural Intensification (focus on container herbs, perennial food plants for balcony/yard, participation in community garden). Lead with Knowledge Preservation (Kiwix + Knowledge Audit) in June, then Governance Scaling in July. Return to community-scale agriculture in Phase 6 when community land access is established through governance structures developed in Phase 5.

**Very limited time (only 15–20 hours available May–July):**
Agricultural Intensification documentation first (minimum viable: one document on perennial species selection and planting, 8–10 hours). Defer Governance and Knowledge Preservation to fall 2026 or winter. Plant with what you know from the Phase 4b pre-research document even before the Phase 5A document is complete — the planting cannot wait for the document.

**Limited capital:**
The 1/4 acre demonstration planting ($925–$1,375) is viable with minimal capital. Focus on elderberry, hazelnut, and serviceberry — highest yield-to-cost ratio, earliest production, lowest per-plant cost. Apply for EQIP cost-share to recover 50–75% of costs. Defer Knowledge Preservation (cheapest path at $220–$300) and Governance Scaling ($0 capital) until fall if needed.

## Key Decision Factors for June 1

These are the specific questions whose answers determine which path(s) to prioritize:

**1. Has plant stock been ordered?**
If no, this is the most urgent action item — not document production, not research. Call or email Midwest Hazelnut, Savanna Institute partner nurseries, or local Zone 5 bare-root nurseries today. If bare-root is sold out, identify container-grown availability (more expensive but can be planted through June). The Zone 5 planting window closes faster than any document can be written.

**2. Has an NRCS field office been contacted about EQIP?**
This is the financial leverage point for Agricultural Intensification. A 75% cost-share changes the capital math substantially. NRCS field offices in Minnesota, Wisconsin, and Iowa are familiar with agroforestry applications. Application takes 4–8 weeks. Contact now.

**3. Is local government engagement realistic?**
The Governance Scaling path produces its highest leverage through civic engagement — town council conversations, township trustee relationships, inter-municipal agreement negotiations. If those relationships are not in place or cannot be built, the document still has value as a template resource, but the implementation impact is lower. Be honest about this constraint.

**4. Is the community's current knowledge inventory actually at risk?**
The Knowledge Preservation path is most valuable when there is a specific identified gap — an aging farmer with soil knowledge that isn't documented, a single person who knows how to operate a water system, a skill set held by only one household. If the community is young and knowledge is broadly distributed, this path is lower priority. Conduct an informal knowledge audit informally before committing to the full path.

**5. Which path has the clearest first implementer?**
Every path described here is more likely to succeed if there is one named person responsible for implementation. Agricultural Intensification needs a planting coordinator. Knowledge Preservation needs a Kiwix deployer and a Knowledge Audit facilitator. Governance Scaling needs someone who will initiate the first inter-municipal conversation. The path with the clearest implementer is the one most likely to actually happen.
