# Phase 5 Path Options Framework

> **Purpose**: Translate research findings (Phases 1–4) into a decision framework for Phase 5 execution.  
> **Timeline**: Decision required by June 1, 2026. Execution: June 2 — September 30, 2026.  
> **Audience**: Project lead evaluating which strategic path(s) unlock the most value for personal constraints, timeline, skills, and risk tolerance.

---

## Executive Summary

Phase 3 completed five community-scale research domains (governance, food systems, information infrastructure, security, scaling pathways) with 28,700+ words and 170+ citations. Phase 4 identified gaps and synthesis points. Phase 5 is **execution selection**: which strategic path best addresses the highest-impact resilience gaps given your constraints?

**This is a decision framework, not a recommendation.** The matrix below helps you evaluate four distinct Phase 5 paths against your personal resources. Choose the path (or combination) that maximizes resilience value within your real constraints.

## Four Phase 5 Path Options

### Option A: Community-Scale Implementation Planning (15–50 Person Groups)

**Focus**: Build a functional community-scale resilience plan (governance, food systems, information infrastructure, security) for a 15–50 person intentional community, neighborhood cluster, or extended-family network.

**Scope of Work**:
- Community charter and governance structure (based on Phase 3 delegate council model)
- Resource inventory system and communication infrastructure (GMRS radio setup)
- Food systems design (aggregation, storage, distribution, production planning)
- Conflict resolution and security framework
- 6-month implementation roadmap with monthly milestones

**Time Requirement**: 60–90 hours total (June–September)
- Week 1–2: Stakeholder meetings, charter drafting, governance structure decisions (15 hours)
- Week 3–4: Information inventory and GMRS system setup (20 hours)
- Week 5–8: Food systems design and security framework (25 hours)
- Week 9–12: Implementation roadmap, testing protocols, documentation (20 hours)

**Capital Investment**: Low to moderate ($2,000–$5,000)
- GMRS handhelds and base station: $800–$1,500
- Documentation and printing: $300–$500
- Testing/simulation exercises: $500–$1,500
- Optional: Professional facilitation for governance meetings: $1,000–$3,000

**Reversibility**: High—agreements can be revised or dissolved; no long-term commitments beyond planning

**Key Assumption**: You have a defined group (neighborhood, family, intentional community) ready to engage in planning

**Strongest for**:
- Groups of 15–50 people who want to formalize informal networks into resilience infrastructure
- Neighborhoods where residents already know each other
- Extended family networks planning coordinated resilience (multiple households)
- Intentional communities formalizing governance before growth

**Integration with existing projects**:
- Direct: off-grid-living community organization work applies; resistance-research governance frameworks parallel Phase 3 structures
- Moderate: seedwarden applies if group is focused on food production; cybersecurity-hardening applies if documentation is digital

### Option B: Individual & Household Hardening Guides (1–25 Person Personal Resilience)

**Focus**: Produce implementation guides for individual-scale and household-scale (8–25 person) resilience that people can execute independently without community coordination required.

**Scope of Work**:
- Individual water/food/energy/shelter systems (detailed build guides for each)
- Household coordination templates (charters, meeting agendas, role descriptions for small groups)
- Skill-building guides (education, veterinary care, conflict resolution, psychological support)
- Personal medical readiness (family health records, first-aid training, psychological first aid)
- Knowledge preservation for personal library (Kiwix offline server setup, printed reference guides)

**Time Requirement**: 50–80 hours total (June–September)
- Week 1–3: Document individual systems (water, shelter, energy, food); validate with existing docs (20 hours)
- Week 4–6: Household coordination templates; adapt Phase 3 governance for 8–25 person scale (18 hours)
- Week 7–9: Skill-building guides and education framework (20 hours)
- Week 10–12: Knowledge preservation setup and personal testing protocols (12 hours)

**Capital Investment**: Very low ($200–$1,000)
- Kiwix server setup: $100–$300 (basic Raspberry Pi)
- Printing (reference guides, templates): $200–$500
- Testing supplies (water filters, food storage): included in personal resilience budget
- No community meetings or GMRS required

**Reversibility**: High—modular guides can be adopted or abandoned independently

**Key Assumption**: You have individuals or small groups (not formal communities) interested in personal resilience

**Strongest for**:
- Solo practitioners or households of 3–6 wanting structured implementation guides
- Dispersed groups (family spread across multiple locations, not co-located)
- Knowledge preservation as primary goal (documenting skills and offline access)
- Households wanting to harden independently before joining larger groups

**Integration with existing projects**:
- Direct: builds on Phase 1 individual documents; extends with education, veterinary, psychological support
- Moderate: seedwarden seed-saving guides integrate; off-grid-living tools and skills frameworks apply
- Indirect: cybersecurity-hardening applies to Kiwix setup and document encryption

### Option C: Integrated Governance + Infrastructure Templates (150+ Person Federation)

**Focus**: Develop templates and playbooks for multi-community federation governance (delegate councils, inter-community coordination, institutional interfaces) and linked infrastructure (grain aggregation, shared cold storage, coordinated water systems).

**Scope of Work**:
- Federated governance templates (150-person threshold transition, delegate council procedures, conflict resolution between communities)
- Inter-community coordination infrastructure (agreements, communication channels, resource-sharing protocols)
- Food systems at federation scale (aggregation across multiple communities, storage allocation, distribution equity)
- Institutional interface templates (legal structure, county government relationships, FEMA coordination)
- NRCS cost-share and FEMA BRIC funding applications (if applicable to infrastructure projects)
- Implementation roadmap for federation building (6–18 month timeline)

**Time Requirement**: 80–120 hours total (June–September, with ongoing strategic guidance)
- Week 1–3: Research and adapt governance models; existing federations (Mondragon, Zapatista); US precedent (1940s rationing boards) (20 hours)
- Week 4–6: Design inter-community coordination infrastructure (agreements, communication, resource protocols) (22 hours)
- Week 7–9: Food systems and institutional interface templates (20 hours)
- Week 10–14: Legal structure research and funding strategy (NRCS EQIP, FEMA BRIC) (20 hours)
- Week 15+: Implementation roadmap and federation launch support (18 hours, may extend beyond September)

**Capital Investment**: Minimal to moderate ($1,000–$3,000)
- Legal documentation and contracts: $800–$1,500
- NRCS/FEMA application preparation: $500–$1,000
- Shared infrastructure (initial fund for inter-community projects): $0–$5,000 (outside Phase 5 budget)

**Reversibility**: Moderate—federation agreements can be dissolved, but require legal process

**Key Assumption**: Multiple established communities (or potential communities) are ready to coordinate at scale

**Strongest for**:
- Leaders or coordinators of 2–5 established communities wanting to formalize coordination
- Regions with NRCS/FEMA funding opportunities for infrastructure projects
- Areas where food aggregation or shared infrastructure is economically valuable
- Long-term vision (18+ month horizon) for multi-community resilience network

**Integration with existing projects**:
- Direct: builds on Phase 3 governance and scaling documents; resistance-research institutional resilience applies
- Moderate: seedwarden regional network aspects; off-grid-living community coordination
- Indirect: cybersecurity-hardening applies to shared digital infrastructure

### Option D: Institutional Bridge Framework (Clinic + Legal + School Relationships)

**Focus**: Develop relationship templates and coordination frameworks between household/community resilience networks and surviving institutions (rural clinics, schools, county government, legal services).

**Scope of Work**:
- School-as-resilience-hub templates (building access agreements, educational continuity protocols, mutual support plans)
- Clinic partnership guides (pre-crisis skill-sharing agreements, pharmaceutical access planning, medical staff support relationships)
- Legal structure guide (lightest-weight legal vehicles: cooperatives, nonprofit 501(c)(3)s, LLC, township commissions for Midwest context)
- County emergency management interface (understanding county emergency plan, becoming ARES/CERT affiliate, liability and authorization)
- Communication protocols between informal networks and formal institutions
- Case studies and implementation roadmap (6–12 month timeline for relationship building)

**Time Requirement**: 40–70 hours total (June–September)
- Week 1–3: Institutional research (FQHCs, CAHs, school emergency procedures, county plans) (18 hours)
- Week 4–6: Legal structure research (state-specific cooperatives, nonprofit formation, federal vs. state regulations) (16 hours)
- Week 7–9: Relationship template development and case studies (16 hours)
- Week 10–12: Implementation roadmap and sample agreements (10 hours)

**Capital Investment**: Very low ($300–$1,000)
- Legal consultation (1–2 hours): $200–$400
- Document preparation and printing: $100–$300
- No capital required for relationships themselves

**Reversibility**: High—relationship agreements are typically informal and non-binding

**Key Assumption**: You have access to institutional partners willing to engage (school superintendent, clinic director, county emergency manager)

**Strongest for**:
- Leaders with existing relationships to institutional stakeholders
- Areas where schools or clinics have adequate resources and leadership interest
- Contexts where legal structure enables funding access (grant writing, cost-share programs)
- Long-term focus (institutional relationships build over 12–24 months)

**Integration with existing projects**:
- Moderate: Phase 3 governance applies to community-institution coordination; resistance-research institutional resilience frameworks parallel this work
- Indirect: seedwarden regional network aspects; NRCS partnerships

## Decision Matrix: Path Selection by Constraint

| **Constraint** | **Best Path(s)** | **Why** | **Fallback** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Limited time (10–20 hours available)** | Option B (household guides) | Can be completed in 50–80 hours with flexible pacing | Option D (25–35 hours minimum, low capital) |
| **No defined community/group** | Option B (individual guides) | Don't require coordination | Option D (relationships can start anywhere) |
| **Have 15–50 person group ready** | Option A (community-scale) | Exactly matches scope and group size | Option C (if group is part of larger federation opportunity) |
| **Have 2–5 established communities** | Option C (federation) | Multi-community coordination is core need | Option A + Option D (build community plans, then federate) |
| **Institutional access/relationships** | Option D (bridges) | Can't do without access; if access exists, use it | Option A or B (proceed without institution interface) |
| **Focused on food production/sovereignty** | Option A + Option C | Community + federation scale unlock food system aggregation | Option B (household food security only) |
| **Focused on knowledge preservation** | Option B (guides + Kiwix) | Offline infrastructure is personal-scale focused | Option A (add knowledge preservation component to community planning) |
| **Long-term vision (18+ months)** | Option C or Option D | Require sustained relationship/institution building | Option A (6-month horizon) |
| **Risk-averse (want to test before scaling)** | Option B → Option A | Start with personal resilience; move to community only after testing | Option A (built-in 6-month testing) |
| **Risk-tolerant (want to move fast to community scale)** | Option A or Option C | Can launch with parallel work streams | Option B (skip to community planning directly) |

## Hybrid Paths: Combining Options

The framework above treats paths as distinct, but they are combinable:

**Hybrid A+B**: Community planning (Option A) + household guides (Option B)
- *Why*: Guides document individual/household systems; community plan coordinates them
- *Time*: 110–170 hours (sum of both)
- *Best for*: Communities that want both formal coordination and household independence

**Hybrid A+D**: Community planning (Option A) + institutional bridges (Option D)
- *Why*: Community governance + formal relationships to schools/clinics unlock more resources
- *Time*: 100–160 hours
- *Best for*: Communities where institutional access exists; want both independence and collaboration

**Hybrid B+D**: Household guides (Option B) + institutional bridges (Option D)
- *Why*: Personal guides + relationship to institutions as backup/amplification
- *Time*: 90–150 hours
- *Best for*: Individuals/households wanting community backup without formal community planning

**Hybrid A+B+C**: Sequential work (A first, B second, C ongoing)
- *Why*: Build community first (A), document as guides for replication (B), federate with other communities (C)
- *Time*: 190–290 hours (spread over 6–12 months)
- *Best for*: Long-term vision with phased execution; want to test and refine before replication

## Success Criteria: How to Know Your Path Is Working

### Option A (Community-Scale) at Month 6
- [ ] Charter ratified by community assembly
- [ ] Monthly governance meetings held; decisions documented
- [ ] Resource inventory maintained and updated (monthly minimum)
- [ ] GMRS communication tested; all coordinators equipped
- [ ] Food distribution protocols established; no rationing conflicts
- [ ] At least 2 people trained for every critical role
- [ ] Attendance at governance meetings: 60%+ of members, 80%+ of specialists

### Option B (Household Guides) at Month 6
- [ ] Individual guides piloted by at least 3 test readers; feedback integrated
- [ ] Household coordinate templates used by at least 2 test groups
- [ ] Personal water, food, shelter systems documented and tested
- [ ] Family health records completed for all participants
- [ ] Kiwix server (if applicable) set up and populated with reference materials
- [ ] Guides available for distribution (printed or digital)

### Option C (Federation) at Month 12
- [ ] Federation governance agreement drafted; communities engaged in review
- [ ] Inter-community coordination infrastructure (communication, resource protocols) documented
- [ ] Food aggregation plan developed; at least one pilot project (shared grain storage, coordinated planting) initiated
- [ ] NRCS or FEMA applications submitted (if applicable)
- [ ] Federation sustainability plan documented (funding, governance, decision-making)

### Option D (Institutional Bridges) at Month 12
- [ ] School building access agreement drafted (or formal MOU in negotiation)
- [ ] Clinic partnership agreement established (skill-sharing, pharmaceutical access, mutual support)
- [ ] Legal structure decided and formation process initiated (if applicable)
- [ ] County emergency management relationship established; community registered for ARES/CERT coordination
- [ ] Implementation roadmap documented; relationship-building milestones set for Year 2

## Final Decision Process

**Step 1: Constraint Inventory (by June 1)**
- How many hours can you dedicate (June–September)? 40 hours? 100+ hours?
- Do you have a defined group or are you working individually?
- Do you have institutional access?
- What is your time horizon (6 months? 18 months? 2+ years)?

**Step 2: Path Ranking (by June 1)**
Using the decision matrix above, rank the four options by fit to your constraints. What is your top choice? Your second choice?

**Step 3: Hybrid Decision (by June 1)**
Should you combine paths? Option A+D is common (community + institutional). Option B+D is common (personal resilience + backup institutional relationships). Option A+B is less common but valid if you want to document and replicate your plan.

**Step 4: Launch (June 2)**
Start with your top-ranked path. Use quick-start modules from Phase 4 to begin execution immediately without additional research.

## Conclusion: From Decision Framework to Execution

Phase 4 built implementation architecture. This framework translates that architecture into a decision structure matched to your real constraints. You do not need to choose one path exclusively; you can sequence them (start with B, move to A) or combine them (A+D). The key is **selecting your path by June 1, 2026** so execution can begin without decision paralysis.

Every path delivers value. Option A scales fastest. Option B is fastest to complete. Option C has the longest reach. Option D unlocks institutional resources. **Which path maximizes value for your situation?**
