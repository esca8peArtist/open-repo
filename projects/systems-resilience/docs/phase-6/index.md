# Phase 6 — Community Economic Resilience

> How the farmer compensates the electrician. How the medic who spends all day on patient care gets food. How a community with specialized labor prevents that specialization from collapsing into interpersonal debt over time.

Phase 6 addresses the **exchange gap**: Phase 5 documented how communities produce food, water, and power. Phase 6 answers what happens next — how that production gets distributed equitably when conventional markets and currencies fail.

---

## Domain A: Community Economic Resilience (published)

**[Community Economic Resilience: Exchange, Capital, and Production Coordination When Conventional Money and Supply Chains Fail](community-economic-resilience.md)**

- **Length**: 6,800 words
- **Citations**: 38 primary sources
- **Zone**: Midwest Zone 5
- **Scale**: Individual → Household → Community

**Covers**:
1. How economic failure propagates and distributes its impacts (currency collapse, supply chain disruption, capital exclusion)
2. Community-scale economic institutions: cooperatives, credit unions, mutual aid networks, local and complementary currencies
3. Policy barriers and reform pathways (current federal and Zone 5 state landscape, 2025-2026)
4. International models with Zone 5 transferability: Mondragon Corporation, Emilia-Romagna cooperative economy, Argentina's 2001 barter networks, Japan's Fureai Kippu
5. Implementation roadmap: individual (Year 1), household (Years 1-3), community (Years 3-10)
6. Failure mode prevention: cooperative degeneration, mutual aid burnout, currency debasement, credit union governance capture

**Key empirical findings**:
- Argentina 2001 crisis produced barter clubs serving 3 million people within 18 months
- Switzerland's WIR mutual credit system has operated for 90 years as a countercyclical liquidity mechanism
- Emilia-Romagna cooperatives account for 30% of regional GDP; the region went from Italy's poorest post-WWII to one of Europe's wealthiest
- Mondragon Corporation: €11.2 billion annual sales, 70,085 worker-owners, employment stability through 2008 recession
- Food co-ops generate $1.604 in local economic activity per $1 spent vs $1.365 for conventional grocers
