# Midwest Resilience Guide

**Practical, actionable guidance for surviving and stabilizing when critical infrastructure fails.**

Region: Midwest US (Zone 5) | Tracks: Rural + Suburban | Scale: Individual → Household → Community

---

## What This Is

This guide assumes that modern infrastructure — grid power, water utilities, supply chains, medical systems, communication networks — will experience extended failures. It provides step-by-step guidance for three scales of response:

1. **Individual** — one person survives and stabilizes alone
2. **Household** — 2-6 people sustain together
3. **Community** — 25-150 people coordinate during cascading failures

Every document follows four standards:

- **Specific, not conceptual** — exact quantities, measurements, and calculations
- **Actionable without infrastructure** — works when stores, internet, and professionals are unavailable
- **Progressive disclosure** — Day 1-3 (survive), Week 1-4 (stabilize), Month 1-6 (build), Year 1+ (sustain)
- **Primary sources cited** — real schematics, plans, books, and field guides

## The Rule of 3s

This prioritization shapes every document:

| Timeline | Need | Priority |
|---|---|---|
| 3 minutes | Air | Not covered here |
| 3 hours | Shelter (Midwest winter) | Critical |
| 3 days | Water | Highest priority |
| 3 weeks | Food | High priority |

Water comes first. Then shelter and heat. Then food and energy. Healthcare and knowledge follow.

## Midwest Zone 5 Context

Zone 5 (annual minimum -20°F to -10°F, 5,000-6,000 growing degree days) covers:

- Illinois, Wisconsin, Iowa, Minnesota, Michigan, Indiana
- Tornado and derecho risk (April-October)
- Ice storm and polar vortex risk (November-March)
- 90+ day frost-free season, hot humid summers, cold winters
- Strong agricultural cooperative infrastructure (Midwest farming tradition)

Every document includes Midwest-specific notes, Zone 5 growing calendars where relevant, and extreme weather protocols.
