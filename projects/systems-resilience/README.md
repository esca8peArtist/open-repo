# Systems Resilience — Midwest Zone 5 Practical Guide

> **A knowledge base for when current societal systems fail**  
> Practical, actionable guidance for Individual → Household → Community resilience  
> Midwest US (Zone 5), Rural + Suburban tracks, 12–36 month implementation

---

## Overview

This project provides a comprehensive, step-by-step resilience framework for surviving and stabilizing when critical infrastructure fails. It assumes modern infrastructure (grid power, water utilities, supply chains, medical systems, communication networks) will eventually experience extended failures — whether from natural disasters (ice storms, derechos, tornadoes, floods), climate cascades, or systemic collapse.

The framework addresses three scales:

1. **Individual-scale (Phase 1)**: One person survives and stabilizes alone
2. **Household-scale (Phase 2)**: 2–6 people sustain together
3. **Community-scale (Phase 3)**: 25–150 people coordinate response during cascading failures

The content is:
- **Specific, not conceptual** — exact quantities, measurements, procedures
- **Actionable without professionals** — works when experts, stores, and internet are unavailable
- **Grounded in primary sources** — schematics, plans, books, research papers
- **Progressive disclosure** — Day 1–3 survival through Year 1+ self-sufficiency

**Region**: Midwest US, Zone 5 (5,000–6,000 growing degree days, cold winters, hot summers, variable precipitation, tornado/derecho/ice storm risk)  
**Target audience**: Midwest residents (rural landowners, suburban homeowners, town planners)  
**Implementation time**: 12–36 months (parallel)

### What You Need to Know First

All documents use the **Rule of 3s**:
- **3 minutes** without air (not covered here)
- **3 hours** without shelter (Midwest winter: life-threatening)
- **3 days** without water (most critical)
- **3 weeks** without food (manageable with preparation)

This prioritization shapes every document. Water comes first. Then shelter and security. Then food and energy. Healthcare and knowledge preservation follow.

## The Three Phases

### Phase 1: Individual Scale

**Goal**: One person can filter water, grow food, shelter from weather, generate power, respond to health emergencies, and self-teach without external support.

**Audience**: Anyone, especially those building foundational resilience skills.

**Core documents**:
- Water: surface sources → filtration → storage → drilling wells → manual pumping
- Food: 1-person 1-year garden (400 sq ft, ~60 lbs food/month), hunting, foraging
- Shelter: immediate (weatherproof), hardened (fire-resistant), tornado-safe
- Energy: minimal solo power needs (solar basics, generators, batteries)
- Healthcare: medical response without professional support
- Plus: calendar (timing for all tasks), extreme weather protocols, Midwest foraging species

**Implementation**: 3–6 months (learning curve is steep; expect failures and iteration)

**Success criteria**:
- [ ] Can filter and store water for 30 days with on-site sources
- [ ] Can grow 60 lbs food/month in a 400 sq ft garden
- [ ] Can maintain body temperature during Midwest winter (sub-zero temps)
- [ ] Can start a fire and cook food without grid power
- [ ] Can respond to basic medical emergencies (wounds, dehydration, hypothermia)

---

### Phase 2: Household Scale

**Goal**: 2–6 people sustain together through coordinated systems — shared water, food production, energy, healthcare, and governance.

**Audience**: Families, small groups, household stewards.

**Core documents**:
- Household coordination overview: introduces scaling from individual to 2–6 person groups
- Five domain coordinations: water (cistern, well), food (shared preservation), energy (load-shedding), healthcare (medical supply hub), communication
- Community bridge: introduces next scale (how households network into villages)

**Implementation**: 6–12 months (builds on Phase 1 competencies)

**Success criteria**:
- [ ] Household water system (well + backup pump + gravity distribution) supplies 20+ gal/person/day
- [ ] Household food system (shared garden + preservation hub) supplies 30+ days storage
- [ ] Household energy plan (solar + generator + storage) handles critical loads during grid outage
- [ ] Household medical supply hub (medications, bandages, equipment) inventoried and accessible
- [ ] Household governance charter (1 page) documenting decision-making and conflict resolution

---

### Phase 3: Community Scale

**Goal**: 25–150 people (town or village) activate coordinated response during cascading failures — grid outage + supply disruption + medical stress occurring simultaneously.

**Audience**: Town leadership, emergency managers, planners, domain stewards, cluster stewards.

**Core documents**:
- Phase 3 overview: integrates the three Phase 3 deep dives
- Emergency response infrastructure: command structure (ICS), communication networks, medical surge, shelter coordination
- Infrastructure interdependency: maps cascade pathways and identifies high-leverage interventions
- Mutual aid networks: pre-crisis identification of skills, assets, and exchange mechanisms

**Implementation**: 12–24 months (parallel governance, infrastructure, and social coordination)

**Success criteria**:
- [ ] Written governance charter (selectboard authority, emergency council scope, decision process)
- [ ] Incident Commander designated + succession plan documented
- [ ] Communication cascade tested (phone tree / radio net practiced)
- [ ] Infrastructure dependencies mapped (cascades identified, redundancy designs created)
- [ ] Mutual aid network documented (skills census, exchange mechanism)
- [ ] At least one tabletop exercise conducted
- [ ] Cluster stewards trained and networked

## Core Principles

All documents in this project follow four standards:

### 1. Specific, Not Conceptual
- NOT: "Plant root vegetables to cover part of your diet"
- BUT: "Plant 400 sq ft of potatoes (yield ~400 lbs, covering ~20% of 2,000-calorie/day diet for one adult for one year)"

### 2. Actionable Without Infrastructure
Instructions work when:
- Electricity is unavailable (solar/manual power, no grid)
- Internet is down (books, not websites)
- Professionals are unavailable (step-by-step procedures, not "hire a well contractor")
- Stores are closed (DIY sourcing, salvage, growing)

### 3. Progressive Disclosure
Each document follows the **Rule of 3s**:
- **Day 1–3**: Immediate survival (water, shelter, security)
- **Week 1–4**: Stabilization (food storage, medical response, communication)
- **Month 1–6**: System building (gardens, wells, power systems, food preservation)
- **Year 1+**: Self-sufficiency (scaling, redundancy, knowledge preservation)

### 4. Primary Sources
Every claim is sourced to:
- Peer-reviewed research (APSR, Nature, Science, etc.)
- Government standards (USDA, EPA, NFPA, FEMA)
- Field guides and practical manuals (Foxfire Series, Peterson Field Guides, etc.)
- Direct data (Sunrise/sunset times, frost dates, growing degree days)
- Case studies (Blue Lake Rancheria microgrid, Hurricane Maria mutual aid, etc.)

## Implementation Support

### Do It Alone
Use the documents as self-study guides. Each includes checklists, worksheets, and scenario exercises. Expect 3–6 months per phase for individual work, 6–12 months for household coordination, 12–24 months for community systems.

### Community Workshop Series
Organize 6–8 community workshops covering one Phase 2 domain per session (water, food, energy, healthcare, governance). Partner with local extension office, library, or community center.

### Municipal Planning
If you're a town planner: use Phase 3 documents as a foundation for emergency management and infrastructure resilience planning. Budget 12–18 months for full implementation.

### Curriculum Integration
Use the documents in high school or college courses on:
- Emergency management
- Sustainable systems
- Infrastructure engineering
- Public health
