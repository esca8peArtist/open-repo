## Attribute Analysis

### 1. Audience Impact Speed
**A (4)**: Community practitioners get 43K words of high-leverage content on June 1 — immediately usable. Wave 1+2 covers the domains most immediately actionable for organized groups (energy planning, animal care, human support systems, governance under stress).

**B (2)**: No content reaches any reader until June 15. The 14-day wait benefits no one and costs early impact.

**C (3)**: First content arrives May 30, but it is only 2 documents (~15K words). Each subsequent week adds more, but the full corpus is not available until early-to-mid July.

---

### 2. Community Readiness Fit
**A (4)**: Wave 1+2 is the right content for organized groups preparing to act. Microgrid planning, veterinary protocols, conflict resolution, and the community playbook are the documents that governance leaders, facilitators, and coordinators will use first. Wave 3 (food preservation, fuel, education) is the practical operational layer that households need once the community is organized.

**B (3)**: All content arrives simultaneously. A community reading the full corpus faces a 66K-word document and must self-organize which to prioritize. No natural entry point is signaled.

**C (5)**: Weekly themes can be matched precisely to where different audiences are in their readiness journey. A community just getting organized reads the Week 1 "foundations" pair first; an established community jumps directly to Week 3 veterinary content. The highest score here reflects the best audience routing — but this precision comes at high coordination cost.

---

### 3. Feedback Velocity
**A (4)**: June 1 readers engage with Wave 1+2 content immediately. Two to four weeks of feedback from actual practitioners can be collected before Wave 3 is finalized, enabling light revision. This is the most realistic scenario for incorporating real-world signal into the final corpus.

**B (2)**: All feedback arrives post-June 15, after all documents are finalized and published. No revision window exists between production and distribution. If Wave 3 documents have any structural gaps (they were written faster, in a single sprint), those gaps are locked in.

**C (5)**: Each weekly release generates focused feedback on that week's content. Six weeks of iterative feedback is theoretically possible. However, this requires active monitoring and willingness to revise documents while others are still being published — creating execution complexity that may not be worth the marginal quality gain.

---

### 4. Discoverability and Coherence
**A (3)**: Two-volume structure creates modest discoverability friction. A reader who finds Wave 1+2 must then find Wave 3. If the two releases use the same URL or repository, cross-reference works. If they are in separate distributions, some readers will miss Wave 3.

**B (5)**: Single release, single URL, single announcement. The definitive reference position is strongest here. "The 66,000-word systems resilience guide" is a more compelling artifact than "the 43,000-word guide with a 23,000-word supplement."

**C (2)**: Six separate releases with no obvious master index (until all are complete) creates significant discoverability problems. A reader who finds Week 3 must actively seek Weeks 1-2 and later weeks. The fragmented structure weakens the authority position of the corpus.

---

### 5. Orchestration Resource Cost
**A (4)**: Two editorial passes instead of one. The June 1 pass is light (Wave 1+2 are already polished from multiple revision cycles). The Wave 3 pass is the standard single review. Coordination overhead is low — two publication events with a simple "Volume 2 coming June 30" note in the Wave 1+2 release.

**B (3)**: One editorial pass, but it covers all 12 documents simultaneously. Cross-referencing Wave 3 documents back to Wave 1+2 (they were written independently) requires a non-trivial integration effort. Master table of contents requires careful organization across 66K words. Estimated 10-15 hours of editorial work.

**C (2)**: Six editorial/announcement cycles across 6 weeks. Each requires: preparing the release, drafting the announcement, monitoring feedback, deciding whether to revise before the next cycle. The cumulative coordination cost is the highest of the three options.

---

### 6. Risk Profile
**A (4)**: Primary risk is Wave 3 feeling like an afterthought if it arrives more than 45 days after Wave 1+2. Mitigation: set and communicate the June 30 date at the June 1 release. Wave 1+2 is already fully polished; it carries no quality risk.

**B (3)**: Primary risk is that Wave 3 documents, written quickly in a final sprint, have structural inconsistencies that are not caught before the June 15 release. A 14-day editorial window is tight for 12 documents. Also: the June 15 date has a harder dependency — any delay pushes into July.

**C (2)**: Multiple risk vectors: if one weekly release generates significant criticism, do you revise it before publishing subsequent documents? If the schedule slips one week, does the whole tail slide? The rolling format creates ongoing execution pressure for 6-8 weeks.

---

### 7. Digestibility for Readers
**A (4)**: 43K words is large but manageable for a motivated practitioner over 2-3 reading sessions. Wave 1+2 has a coherent internal arc (infrastructure → human systems → implementation). Wave 3 arrives separately and is organized around practical domains.

**B (2)**: 66K words in a single release is challenging to digest. Without a strong navigational structure (master TOC, clear domain groupings, cross-references), readers will struggle to find the content relevant to their current situation.

**C (5)**: Each weekly pair (15-20K words) is the most digestible unit. Readers can fully absorb and discuss a week's content before the next pair arrives. Best for audience engagement and understanding.

---

### 8. Distribution Leverage
**A (3)**: Two distribution events. The June 1 event is the primary one; Wave 3 supplements it. Unless the Wave 3 release is actively promoted (second announcement, explicit "Volume 2 is here" messaging), it may not reach readers who already engaged with Wave 1+2.

**B (4)**: One high-density distribution event. A 66K-word comprehensive guide is a stronger artifact for outreach to networks, organizations, and communities who want a single authoritative reference. "Download the complete guide" is a simpler ask than "here's Volume 1, Volume 2 coming soon."

**C (3)**: Six distribution events — but each is smaller and requires sustained promotional energy. The ongoing schedule creates continued presence in community channels but also risks attention fatigue.

---

## Trade-off Summary

**The core tension is between impact speed and corpus coherence.**

Option A wins on impact speed, feedback integration, and execution sustainability. The Wave 1+2 corpus is independently coherent and production-ready — it covers community organization, energy infrastructure, human psychology, and governance. Publishing it on June 1 means real communities can begin using it while Wave 3 is still being finalized.

Option B wins on discoverability and authority positioning. A single 66K-word release is a stronger artifact than a staged release, and it reduces the risk that Wave 3 readers miss the Wave 1+2 foundation. However, Option B sacrifices two weeks of impact and creates a significant editorial burden in the June 1-14 window.

Option C wins on reader digestibility and feedback velocity, but at the highest coordination cost. The weekly schedule requires sustained promotion and editorial attention for 6-8 weeks — a meaningful commitment for marginal quality gains over Option A.

**The hidden asymmetry**: Wave 1+2 content (community infrastructure, human systems, governance) is the content that organized community groups and facilitators will use first. Wave 3 content (food preservation, water, fuel, education) is the content that household-scale practitioners need once a community is functional. The natural reading sequence matches Option A's publication sequence.

## Recommendation: Option A

Publish Wave 1+2 on June 1-5. Announce Wave 3 ("Volume 2: Practical Operations") for June 30.

**Rationale:**

1. **Wave 1+2 is ready.** Multiple revision passes, polished writing, consistent cross-references. No additional editorial work is required before June 1.

2. **The Wave 1+2 arc is independently complete.** A reader who receives 43K words covering microgrids, veterinary care, psychological support, conflict resolution, and a community playbook has everything they need to begin organizing. Wave 3 is the operational supplement for communities already in motion.

3. **Feedback before finalization.** Publishing Wave 1+2 on June 1 creates a 3-4 week feedback window before Wave 3 is finalized. If practitioners identify gaps (missing Zone 5 specifics, unclear procedures, absent equipment details), those gaps can be addressed in Wave 3 before it publishes.

4. **Lower execution risk.** Two clean publication events with a 25-30 day gap is operationally simpler than a 14-day integrated editorial push (Option B) or a 6-week rolling schedule (Option C).

5. **The June 30 date is realistic.** Wave 3 is written. The June 30 publication requires only: a light editorial pass for cross-references to Wave 1+2, frontmatter standardization, and a master Wave 3 table of contents. This is 8-12 hours of work, schedulable during the June 1-30 window without urgency pressure.
