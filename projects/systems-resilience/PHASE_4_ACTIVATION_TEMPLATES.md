### 1A. Governance Synthesis Workshop Outline

**3-Day Facilitation Plan for 15–30 Person Groups**

**Purpose**: Walk a group of 15–30 people through the transition from reading Phase 5 governance frameworks to drafting their own community governance architecture. This is not a training; it is a decision-making process. By Day 3, the group must have made real governance decisions, not just discussed models.

**Participant prerequisites**: Read Phase 5 Community Implementation Playbook (8,619 words) before Day 1. No other preparation required.

**Facilitator prerequisites**: Read Phase 5 Conflict Resolution Framework (8,596 words) + Phase 4 Framework Section 3 (community-scale synthesis order, pages 10–14 of PHASE_4_FRAMEWORK.md). Facilitator does not need domain expertise — the role is process clarity, not governance content expertise.

**Materials**: Large sheets of paper or whiteboard space for each breakout group; markers in four colors; printed governance charter template (one per participant); printed household coordinator role cards (one set per household sub-group); timer; sticky notes.

---

#### Day 1: Situation Mapping and Governance Principles (6 hours)

**Goal for the day**: By end of Day 1, every participant knows the difference between implicit norms (what you do now) and explicit governance (what you are committing to), and understands the three governance architectures that communities this size realistically use.

**Morning Block (3 hours)**

*Hour 1: Current State Mapping (Plenary)*

The opening question is not "what governance do you want?" It is "how do decisions get made right now?" This distinction matters. People arrive with aspirations; the workshop needs to start with reality.

Facilitator instruction: Post a blank sheet labeled "How We Actually Decide Right Now." Ask three seed questions in sequence, spending 10 minutes each:

- "Think of the last significant decision your group made together — who actually made it?" (Not who was supposed to; who did.)
- "Was there anyone who disagreed with that decision? What happened to their objection?"
- "If a new resource arrived tomorrow — say, a generator or a large food donation — who would decide how it is distributed?"

Record every answer without editorial comment. The goal is to make the implicit visible. Expected patterns: one or two people dominate, decisions happen in side conversations outside meetings, urgent decisions bypass process entirely, resource allocation produces the most disagreement.

*Hour 2: Pain Points and Governance Failures (Plenary)*

Transition: "Now that we can see how decisions are actually made, let's identify where that breaks down."

Ask: "What decision in the last year left people feeling like their voice didn't count?" Give 5 minutes for written individual responses before group sharing — this prevents the loudest voices from shaping the answer.

Gather responses into three categories on a second sheet: (1) information failures ("we didn't know what we were deciding about"), (2) authority failures ("the wrong person made the decision or no one had authority"), (3) fairness failures ("the process was right but the outcome felt unjust"). Label these explicitly. These three categories will become the three targets of the governance structure you design.

*Hour 3: Governance Architecture Options (Plenary + Breakout Prep)*

Introduce the three architectures used by communities of 15–30 people. Keep each description to 8 minutes.

**Architecture 1: Direct Assembly**. Every member votes on every significant decision. No delegation. Assembly meets regularly (weekly or bi-weekly). Decisions require quorum and majority or supermajority. Works well when: community is small enough for full attendance (15–25 people max), decisions are not time-sensitive, and trust levels are high. Breaks when: decisions need to be made faster than assembly can convene, attendance is variable, or a subset of members becomes de facto leaders regardless of the formal structure.

**Architecture 2: Delegate Council**. Members elect or rotate delegates who meet more frequently to handle routine and time-sensitive decisions. Full assembly meets monthly for major decisions and policy. Domain coordinators (food, water, energy, health) have authority over daily decisions in their domain. Escalation is explicit: daily decisions stay with coordinator, resource commitments above a set threshold go to delegates, constitutional questions (new members, major conflict, policy changes) go to full assembly. Works at 15–30 people and scales to 150+. Most intentional communities at this size operate some variant of this model.

**Architecture 3: Nested Working Groups**. Small working groups (5–8 people) govern each domain autonomously. Each group has a liaison who participates in a cross-group coordination circle. The coordination circle has no direct authority over working groups — it identifies conflicts between groups and escalates to full assembly when needed. Works well when: expertise is highly domain-specific, autonomy within domains is valued over unified decision-making, and the group is willing to tolerate some inconsistency across domains. Breaks when: cross-domain decisions require coordination the circle cannot provide, or working groups develop conflicting policies that erode community coherence.

Announce the afternoon breakout assignment: "Each of three groups will work with one of these architectures for the afternoon. Your task is not to defend it — it is to understand its failure modes."

**Afternoon Block (3 hours)**

*Hour 4: Architecture Failure Mode Analysis (3 Breakout Groups)*

Assign groups (5–10 people each). Give each group one architecture and three scenarios. The task for each group: "Work through each scenario using your assigned architecture. Where does it break? What would you add to prevent it?"

Scenarios:
- Scenario A (Food shortage): "Your food coordinator projects a 25% shortfall in stored food for the winter. She wants to activate rationing at 1,800 calories per person per day. Who decides? How fast can the decision be made? What happens if a third of the community disagrees?"
- Scenario B (Leadership incapacity): "Your primary facilitator (or lead delegate) is in a mental health crisis and cannot function for 3 weeks. Who runs the next meeting? Who breaks tie votes? Does governance pause?"
- Scenario C (New members): "Three neighboring families ask to join the community. They have skills you need (a doctor and two farmers) but adding them would push population from 22 to 30 — and two current members feel threatened. Who decides whether they join? By what process?"

Give each group 45 minutes for scenario work. The output is NOT "our architecture handles all scenarios fine." It is a list of specific failure modes and the amendments that would prevent them.

*Hour 5: Group Reporting and Cross-Architecture Synthesis (Plenary)*

Each group reports (10 minutes each). Facilitator records failure modes on a shared sheet.

After all three groups report, identify the two or three failure modes that appeared across all architectures. These are structural problems, not architecture-specific problems. Common cross-architecture findings:
- Information is the limiting factor in all scenarios (governance cannot make good decisions without accurate data)
- Single points of failure appear in all three models (one person doing a critical role without backup)
- Escalation speed is never fast enough for emergencies in assembly-only models

*Hour 6: Architecture Selection (Plenary)*

Ask the group to vote — not to commit, but to identify which architecture they want to develop further on Days 2 and 3.

Simple show of hands: "Who wants to work with Architecture 1?" "Architecture 2?" "Architecture 3?"

If the vote is split (common), name it directly: "There is genuine disagreement here, and that is governance data. It tells us that we have some people who value autonomy more than consistency, and some who value unified decision-making more than speed. We are going to spend Day 2 designing a hybrid that addresses both concerns." A hybrid of Architecture 2 (delegates for routine decisions) and Architecture 3 (working group autonomy within domains) is the most common outcome for groups in the 15–30 person range.

Day 1 closing commitment: Each participant writes one sentence answering: "The single most important thing governance needs to get right for me to trust it." Collect these. The facilitator reads five of them aloud. These statements become the evaluation criteria for the charter on Day 3.

**Day 1 Deliverable**: Whiteboard output showing current decision reality, three failure modes identified, architecture preference direction established.

---

#### Day 2: Charter Design and Domain Authority Mapping (6 hours)

**Goal for the day**: Produce a working draft charter. By end of Day 2, the group has explicit text — not aspirational goals — covering decision method, authority distribution, quorum, escalation, and information infrastructure requirements.

**Morning Block (3 hours)**

*Hour 1: Charter Skeleton (Plenary)*

The facilitator presents a blank charter skeleton. The group will fill it in over the next three hours. The skeleton has six sections:

1. Decision-Making Method (which architecture; what hybrids)
2. Authority Distribution (who decides what; at what threshold does a decision escalate)
3. Quorum and Voting Rules (what makes a decision valid)
4. Escalation Protocol (what happens when the process breaks down)
5. Information Infrastructure (what data does governance need, and who provides it)
6. Scaling Thresholds (at what community size does governance restructure)

Begin with the easiest section: Quorum and Voting Rules. Ask: "If 10 of 22 people attend a meeting, is that decision legitimate?" The group usually converges quickly on a number (often 51–60% of adults). Record it. Add: "What percentage of votes is needed to adopt a policy?" Record. Add: "Are there any decisions that require a higher threshold — say, accepting new members or changing the charter itself?" Record. This warm-up builds momentum because the group makes real decisions quickly.

*Hour 2: Authority Distribution (Domain Coordinators + Assembly Lines)*

Create a two-column table for each domain: "Coordinator Authority" (what the coordinator decides daily without escalating) and "Assembly Authority" (what requires group vote). Walk through each domain in order:

Food: Coordinator decides daily distribution, rationing up to 10% reduction, emergency procurement below a set dollar threshold. Assembly decides rationing below 1,800 cal/day, adding or removing people from food system, allocation disputes unresolved in 48 hours.

Water: Coordinator decides daily system checks, filter replacement, minor repairs. Assembly decides water rationing, emergency backup activation lasting more than 3 days, any capital investment above threshold.

Energy: Coordinator decides daily load shedding, fuel monitoring, routine maintenance. Assembly decides any backup fuel purchase above threshold, system upgrade decisions, load priority changes.

Health: Coordinator decides first-aid protocols, routine medical supply ordering. Assembly decides external medical consultation requiring community resources, crisis intervention involving privacy override, long-term care commitments.

Governance and Conflict: Facilitator manages meetings, runs mediation for disputes where both parties consent. Assembly decides if mediation fails, if a member's standing is questioned, if constitutional changes are proposed.

Write out text for each cell. Not bullet points — actual policy sentences. "The food coordinator may reduce individual daily rations by up to 10% without assembly approval, provided she notifies the delegate council at the next meeting." Text that is ambiguous when written will be ambiguous in a crisis.

*Hour 3: Escalation and Information Infrastructure*

Escalation protocol: "When the process breaks down, what happens?" The group needs two things here: a named person who has authority to make emergency decisions, and a time limit on how long that authority lasts before escalating to assembly. Example text: "In an emergency requiring immediate decision, the delegate council chair may make a binding decision without assembly vote. That decision must be reported to all community members within 24 hours and ratified or overturned by the assembly at its next meeting."

Information infrastructure: This section is the one most often neglected and most often the cause of governance failure. Ask the group: "Before governance can make a good decision, what information does it need?" Expected answers: food inventory, fuel levels, health status of community members, population headcount, external threat assessment. Assign a named role (information coordinator) responsible for maintaining this data. Agree on update frequency (weekly minimum for inventory; daily during crisis).

**Afternoon Block (3 hours)**

*Hours 4–5: Breakout Charter Drafting (3 Breakout Groups)*

Split the group into three breakout groups. Each group takes two of the six charter sections and drafts actual text — not notes, not bullet points, but policy text in complete sentences. Give 90 minutes.

Regroup. Each breakout group reads their two sections aloud. The full group amends in real time (facilitator is strict: amendments must be proposed as specific text changes, not general concerns). By end of hour 5, the six sections of the charter are drafted.

*Hour 6: First Full Read and Gap Identification*

Facilitator reads the full charter draft aloud at normal speed. This takes about 15 minutes. The group's task is to listen for: gaps (things not covered that they know matter), contradictions (two sections that conflict), and ambiguities (phrases that could be interpreted two different ways in a crisis).

Record all gaps, contradictions, and ambiguities on a sheet labeled "Day 3 Work." Close with a count: this is the incomplete list the group will resolve on Day 3.

**Day 2 Deliverable**: Full charter draft (may have gaps). Day 3 Work list. Each participant has a printed copy.

---

#### Day 3: Charter Finalization and Household Integration (5 hours)

**Goal for the day**: Resolve all Day 3 Work gaps and ambiguities. Run the household coordinator integration. Reach a formal adoption vote. The day ends with a signed charter and named coordinators.

*Hour 1: Gap Resolution (Plenary)*

Work through the Day 3 Work list systematically. Each gap gets a maximum of 10 minutes. If the group cannot resolve a gap in 10 minutes, the facilitator calls a procedural vote: "We are going to adopt the following default text for now and flag it for a 30-day review. Who supports this default?" This prevents gaps from stalling the process. Most groups can resolve 6–10 gaps in one hour.

*Hour 2: Household Coordinator Integration (Breakout by Household)*

If the 15–30 person group is divided into household units (families, living groups, neighborhood clusters), break into those units now. Each unit (5–10 people) has one hour to:

1. Name coordinators for each domain (the people who will fill the coordinator roles in the charter)
2. Agree on the household's internal decision process (how does the household decide when it disagrees with a community-level decision)
3. Designate their governance liaison (the person who attends community assemblies and votes on behalf of the household)

The output is a one-page household summary naming all coordinators and the liaison. This is not the full household charter template from PHASE_4_ACTIVATION_OPTION_A.md — it is the interface document between household and community governance.

*Hour 3: Integration Verification (Plenary)*

Each household presents their coordinator names and liaison. The facilitator maps them against the charter — "You said food coordinator is Jane. In the charter, the food coordinator has authority to reduce rations by 10%. Jane, do you understand and accept that authority?" This step prevents the charter from naming an authority that the named person does not actually accept.

Check for gaps in coordinator coverage: any domain with no named coordinator must be addressed before adoption.

*Hour 4: Formal Adoption Vote (Plenary)*

The adoption vote is a structured process, not an informal show of hands.

Step 1: Final amendment window (15 minutes). Any participant may propose a final amendment. Amendments must be submitted as specific text. The group votes on each amendment separately (simple majority).

Step 2: Read the final charter aloud (15 minutes). No discussion during reading.

Step 3: Adoption vote. "All in favor of adopting this charter as our community governance starting [date]?" Supermajority required (recommend 75%). If supermajority is not reached, facilitator identifies the specific objections and calls a final negotiation (30 minutes maximum) before a second vote. If second vote fails, governance launch is postponed — note that this outcome is governance data, not failure.

Step 4: Record vote. Note the exact count. Record names of abstentions or objections.

*Hour 5: Implementation Launch and First Assembly Scheduling*

Once charter is adopted:

1. First governance assembly scheduled (within 7 days of Day 3)
2. Information infrastructure startup assigned: information coordinator has 72 hours to post the first community inventory (food, water, fuel, medical supply levels)
3. Household charters completed by Day 3 + 3 days
4. First delegate meeting (if delegate model) scheduled within 14 days

Closing: Facilitator reads back the five "what governance needs to get right" statements collected at the end of Day 1. Ask: "Does our charter address these?" If a statement is not addressed, add it to the 30-day review agenda.

**Day 3 Deliverable**: Signed charter with all coordinator names. First assembly date confirmed. Household summaries submitted to information coordinator.

### 1B. Household Scaling 1-Pager

**How Individual Households Implement Phase 5 Principles — First 48 Hours, First Week, First Month**

This guide is for a single household of 5–15 people reading Phase 5 for the first time. It does not require the 3-day workshop. It is designed to be executed independently, then integrated into community governance once community governance is established.

**What Phase 5 asks of households**: Phase 5 (Waves 1 and 2) was written for community-scale groups of 50–200 people. The principles apply at household scale, but the mechanisms are different. A household does not hold assemblies; it holds household meetings. A household does not elect delegates; it designates coordinators. A household does not need an information infrastructure system; it needs a shared log. This guide translates Phase 5 community-scale mechanisms to household-executable equivalents.

---

**First 48 Hours: Orientation and Role Assignment**

The first 48 hours are about making accountability visible, not about making decisions. Do not try to implement anything yet — establish who is responsible for what.

Read the Phase 5 Community Implementation Playbook together as a household (the introduction and "how to use this" sections — approximately 45 minutes). The reading is not optional; it establishes a shared vocabulary for the conversations that follow.

Hold a household meeting (60–90 minutes maximum). Agenda:

Item 1 (20 minutes): "What is our current situation?" Go around the room and have each adult answer: "What is the thing I am most worried about for our household's resilience in the next 6 months?" Write every answer without evaluation. These answers are your household risk assessment.

Item 2 (30 minutes): Domain coordinator assignment. For each of the five primary domains, name one person who will take primary responsibility. This is not a permanent appointment — it is a starting designation. Rotate if needed.

- Water coordinator: Tracks current storage, filter status, and backup water source availability
- Food coordinator: Tracks current inventory in days of supply, manages preservation calendar, oversees growing plan
- Energy coordinator: Tracks fuel levels, generator/battery status, load priorities in a shortfall
- Health coordinator: Maintains one-page health records for each household member (medications, allergies, conditions); tracks medical supply inventory
- Governance coordinator (if connected to a larger community): Attends community meetings, reports back, represents household position on shared decisions

Item 3 (10 minutes): First household meeting scheduled. The second meeting should be within 10–14 days.

Immediate post-meeting action (same day): Each coordinator takes a 30-minute inventory of their domain. Not a deep audit — a quick baseline. How many days of food? Is the water filter clean? What is the fuel level? Record these numbers in a shared household log (one notebook, one shared document, one physical location everyone knows).

---

**First Week: Audit and Gap Identification**

The first week's work is a structured audit of where the household actually is, relative to the Phase 5 operational standards. This audit produces a gap list that becomes the household's next 3-month work plan.

Each coordinator conducts their domain audit in the first 5 days. The audit format is the same for all domains:

**Current State**: What is the actual situation right now? (Quantities, conditions, capabilities)

**Phase 5 Target**: What does Phase 5 say a household in Zone 5 should have for resilience? (Reference the relevant Phase 5 document for your domain)

**Gap**: What is the difference between current state and target?

**Closing Timeline**: What is the minimum effort and timeline to close the gap?

Water coordinator audit (Day 1–2): Determine water source, storage capacity, filtration method, and backup. Phase 5 target: 20 liters per person per day from on-site sources, independent of municipal supply, for at least 7 days. Calculate your gap in stored gallons. If you are on a municipal supply with no backup, the gap is significant — identify the single highest-leverage action (a 55-gallon drum with a filter is a realistic start).

Food coordinator audit (Day 2–3): Inventory all stored food and calculate days of supply at current household consumption rate. Phase 5 target: 90 days stored supply per person. Most households are at 7–21 days. Calculate the gap in dollar cost and storage space. Identify preservation capacity (canning equipment, freezer space, dehydrator access) — this matters more than raw purchasing power because preservation capacity determines what you can do with a harvest.

Energy coordinator audit (Day 3–4): Identify all power-dependent critical loads (medical equipment, refrigeration, water pump, communication). Phase 5 target: 72-hour backup for critical loads, with a 14-day backup for heating in Zone 5 winter. Assess what backup capacity exists (generator, battery bank, propane, wood). In Zone 5 specifically: if heating backup is not confirmed by October 1, it is a life-safety gap.

Health coordinator audit (Day 4–5): Complete a one-page health record for every household member (name, date of birth, medications with dosage, known allergies, significant conditions, blood type if known). Inventory first aid supplies against a target kit list (Phase 5 healthcare offline document, Wave 3). Confirm at least one household member has documented first aid training (TCCC, WFR, or CPR/first aid certification minimum).

End of week: Household meeting 2 (60 minutes). Each coordinator presents their gap in plain language: "We have X days of food; we need 90. The gap is Y. We can close half of it in 30 days by doing Z." This meeting is about prioritization — the household has limited resources, and the coordinators' gap lists will add up to more than the household can address at once. The household decides the order.

---

**First Month: Three Priority Actions**

The first month is not about closing all gaps — it is about closing the three most critical gaps and establishing the household operational rhythm that will persist.

**Priority 1: Food inventory to 30 days**. Go from wherever you are to 30 days stored supply. This does not require a large purchase — it requires deliberate grocery shopping for an extra week's worth of shelf-stable food every week for 3–4 weeks, combined with a rotation system that prevents waste. In Zone 5, the first-month food priority is also to confirm that preservation equipment exists and is tested before August. If a canning pressure canner is not in the household inventory, acquire one before July 15.

**Priority 2: Water backup confirmed**. By the end of month one, the household should have a documented backup water plan that does not depend on municipal supply. Minimum viable: 55 gallons of stored water plus a gravity filter (e.g., Berkey or equivalent). Better: a hand pump on an existing well, or rainwater capture with filtration. The key metric is not the equipment — it is the number of days of supply available from on-site sources without power.

**Priority 3: Health records complete**. Every household member has a one-page health summary. This sounds administrative, but it has a specific operational function: in an emergency, if the person who knows a household member's medical history is unavailable (incapacitated, absent), the health coordinator can still respond appropriately. This is the most underdone preparedness action in most households and the one with the highest payoff in a real crisis.

**Establishing operational rhythm**: By the end of the first month, the household meeting cadence should be set. Recommend: one coordination meeting per month (30–45 minutes), with a structured inventory check at each meeting. The meeting is not for processing information — coordinators do that independently. The meeting is for making decisions that cross domain boundaries (do we spend $300 on a generator or $300 on food storage?) and for ensuring no coordinator is isolated with a problem they cannot solve alone.

**Month-one integration with community governance**: If the household is part of a community running the 3-day governance workshop, the governance coordinator attends the Day 1 community session during the first month and brings back: the governance architecture the community is exploring, the domain coordinator structure, and the escalation thresholds. The household does not need to wait for community governance to be established before beginning its own internal coordination — household resilience proceeds on its own timeline and integrates with community governance when community governance is ready.

### 1C. Zone 5 Adaptation Guide

**Regional Specifics for Midwest Implementation — Season-Driven Constraints, Crop and Livestock Context, Community Network Mapping**

**Why Zone 5 is different**: Generic resilience frameworks (including Phase 5's community-scale documents) are written for temperate climates with extended growing seasons and moderate winter temperatures. Zone 5 (Illinois, Indiana, Michigan, Iowa, Wisconsin, Minnesota, Missouri) violates three assumptions: growing season is compressed to 4 months, winter temperatures regularly reach -10°F to -20°F, and agricultural infrastructure (grain storage, livestock housing, irrigation) is built for commodity production rather than subsistence resilience.

This guide does not replace Phase 5. It identifies the five places where Zone 5 conditions require Phase 5 frameworks to be applied differently, and specifies what "differently" means in each case.

---

**Zone 5 Adaptation 1: Implementation Sequencing (Governance Before October)**

Phase 5 Community Implementation Playbook gives a 12-week baseline implementation sequence. In Zone 5, this sequence must compress and reorder.

The Zone 5 hard constraint: governance must be operational by October 1. This is not a goal — it is a survival threshold. Three reasons:

First, heating fuel allocation is the highest-conflict governance decision communities in Zone 5 will face. If governance is not operational when heating season begins (typically late October in Zone 5), that decision will be made by whoever controls the fuel supply — not by a legitimate community process. Communities that improvise fuel rationing mid-winter fracture.

Second, food preservation peaks August through October. Communities without a functioning coordination structure during peak preservation lose the harvest window. Preservation decisions (who has canning equipment, how is root cellar space allocated, who coordinates the harvest work parties) require governance authority.

Third, Zone 5 winter reduces coordination capacity. People do not attend outdoor meetings at 10°F. Assembly frequency and informal coordination both drop in December through February. If governance is not built and ratified before November, the earliest realistic opportunity to rebuild it is April.

**Implication for Option A timeline**: The June 1–30 governance activation window is not generous — it is the minimum viable window for Zone 5. Communities that take 4–6 weeks to get through the 3-day workshop (scheduling delays, attendance issues) must still be fully operational before October 1. That leaves less than 3 months from June 30 to October 1. Communities in Zone 5 should treat the governance workshop as a June 1–15 priority, not a June activity that can slip to July.

---

**Zone 5 Adaptation 2: Agricultural Calendar Integration**

Phase 5 Wave 3 (food preservation, seed saving) assumes communities can plan preservation concurrently with other governance activities. In Zone 5, the agricultural calendar creates fixed deadlines that governance must accommodate.

Zone 5 frost dates (approximate, by state):

| State | Last Spring Frost | First Fall Frost | Growing Season |
|-------|------------------|-----------------|----------------|
| Illinois (central) | May 1–15 | Oct 1–15 | 140–165 days |
| Indiana (central) | May 1–10 | Oct 10–20 | 155–165 days |
| Iowa | May 1–15 | Oct 1–10 | 140–155 days |
| Michigan (southern) | May 10–25 | Oct 1–15 | 130–150 days |
| Wisconsin (southern) | May 10–20 | Oct 1–10 | 130–145 days |
| Minnesota (southern) | May 10–25 | Sept 20–Oct 10 | 120–140 days |

**Fixed agricultural deadlines that governance must plan around**:

May 15 (last spring frost across most of Zone 5): Cool-season crops (lettuce, spinach, broccoli, peas) should already be in the ground. Governance cannot hold a 3-day workshop that conflicts with spring planting. Schedule the workshop June 1–3, after spring planting is complete.

June 1–15: Warm-season crops (tomatoes, squash, beans, corn) transplanted. Food coordinator must have the planting plan confirmed and assigned. This is a governance-dependent decision (who plants what on shared land) that cannot slip.

July–August: Peak water demand for crops. Energy and water coordinators must have irrigation backup plans in place before July 1, because irrigation failures during this window produce the largest yield losses.

August 1 – October 1: Preservation window. All canning, drying, and root cellar loading happens in these 9 weeks. If the food coordinator has not confirmed preservation equipment capacity by July 15, there is no time to acquire equipment before the window opens.

October 1: Winterization deadline for water infrastructure. Outdoor pipes, hand pumps, and surface water systems must be protected from freezing before this date.

**Governance calendar for Zone 5 Option A communities**:

| Date | Governance Action | Why This Date |
|------|------------------|---------------|
| June 1–3 | 3-day governance workshop | Before summer crop peak; after spring planting |
| June 5 | Household charters completed | Coordinators named before agricultural season peaks |
| June 10 | Planting allocation decision by assembly | All households need to know what they're responsible for growing |
| June 15 | Preservation equipment audit due | 6 weeks lead time to acquire equipment before August harvest |
| July 1 | Water/irrigation backup plans confirmed | Before peak demand |
| July 15 | Winter fuel procurement authorized by assembly | Households need 6–8 weeks to arrange fuel delivery |
| August 15 | Preservation work parties scheduled | Coordination before peak harvest |
| September 1 | Heating system test | 30-day buffer before consistent heating demand |
| September 15 | Winterization complete (water, pipes, backup systems) | Before October freeze risk |
| October 1 | Winter governance mode active | Quarterly review only; no new major decisions until April |

**Zone 5 Adaptation 3: Livestock and Crop Specifics**

Phase 5 veterinary care and livestock documents were written for general temperate climates. Zone 5 has three livestock management constraints that differ from warmer-region guidance.

**Winter housing**: All livestock require heated or well-insulated winter housing in Zone 5. Chickens can tolerate temperatures down to about 0°F if the coop is draft-free and has 3–4 sq ft per bird. Goats and pigs require more active heating below 20°F. Cattle are more cold-tolerant but need windbreak protection and dry bedding. Water systems for livestock freeze at the same temperatures as household systems — heated water buckets or insulated water lines are not optional in Zone 5 winter.

**Zone 5 crop varieties**: Seed saving decisions (Phase 5 Wave 3) should prioritize cold-tolerant varieties selected for Zone 5 conditions.

Best-performing open-pollinated varieties for Zone 5 community production, by crop type:

- Tomatoes: Stupice (Czech heirloom, early maturing, performs well in short seasons), Legend (OSU release, blight-resistant, good for wet years)
- Squash: Butternut (long storage, 110 days — tight but achievable in southern Zone 5), Delicata (100 days, excellent keeper)
- Beans: Provider bush bean (50 days, high yield, good for successive planting), Dragon Tongue wax bean (57 days, dual-purpose fresh/dried)
- Corn: Painted Mountain flour corn (85 days, selected for cold tolerance, Zone 4–5 proven), Golden Bantam sweet corn (78 days, heirloom)
- Greens: Bloomsdale Spinach (45 days, cold-tolerant to 20°F), Red Russian Kale (overwinters in Zone 5 with protection)
- Root vegetables: Chantenay carrots (70 days, good for heavy Midwestern soils), Detroit Dark Red beets (60 days, reliable storage)

**Network resources specific to Zone 5**:

**The Rolling Week Structure (6 weeks, June 1 – July 12)**

**Tuesday (5 days before release)**: Phase 4 preview preparation

*6:00 PM UTC*: Phase 5 cluster publishes. Each cluster is a themed pair of documents (see 12-week schedule in Section 3C).

*6:00–7:00 PM*: Reading window. Community members read the new content. Not the full documents — focus on: (1) the first section of each document (orientation), (2) any section explicitly labeled "Governance" or "Decision Points," (3) the closing section (what to do next). This is 45–60 minutes of focused reading.

*7:00–9:00 PM*: Governance assembly (Thursday evening, same time each week, non-negotiable).

Assembly agenda (fixed structure, 120 minutes):

- **7:00–7:20** (20 min): Facilitator summary. The facilitator (who read content Tuesday) summarizes the new Phase 5 content in 15 minutes. No questions yet — this is orientation. Questions open at 7:20.
- **7:20–7:30** (10 min): Questions on content. Clarifying questions only. Not "what do we think about this?" — that's for the debate segment. Only "what does this document mean?"
- **7:30–8:10** (40 min): Governance decision debate. Work through the pre-distributed governance questions. Each question gets a maximum of 8 minutes. Facilitator calls for a vote at the 8-minute mark regardless of whether discussion is finished. Unresolved questions go to the following Tuesday's preparation cycle.
- **8:10–8:30** (20 min): Authority matrix update. Fill in the new domain rows (coordinator name, daily authority, escalation threshold). This is administrative, not deliberative — confirm names and fill in the matrix.
- **8:30–8:45** (15 min): Progress dashboard update. Review the prior week's implementation dashboard (from 3B below). What got done? What slipped? Who needs help?
- **8:45–9:00** (15 min): Next week preview. Facilitator previews the following Thursday's content. Community nominates coordinator candidates for the next domain.

*Friday–Sunday*: Implementation and feedback

Friday: Newly named coordinators receive their domain responsibilities and authority. Governance coordinator posts the updated authority matrix (physically and/or digitally).

Saturday–Sunday: Community members who had feedback on the new content submit it to the Phase 4 coordination lead (written note, brief conversation, message — any format). This is not a formal review process — it is informal signal collection.

**Monday (3 days after release)**: Feedback synthesis

Phase 4 coordination lead synthesizes Friday–Sunday feedback into one page: "What worked, what didn't, what we need to revisit." This page goes to the Tuesday preparation cycle.

**Tuesday**: Cycle repeats for the following week.

**Managing Governance Stability in a Rolling Model**

The primary risk in Option C is governance whiplash — so many changes week to week that community members stop trusting the governance structure because it keeps changing. Four rules prevent this:

Rule 1: Maximum 2 amendments per week to existing charter sections. New domain additions do not count as amendments — adding a row to the authority matrix is not changing governance, it is extending it. Amendments to existing text (quorum, escalation thresholds, authority boundaries) are limited to 2 per week.

Rule 2: No retroactive authority changes. If Week 2 assigns a food coordinator authority to reduce rations by 10%, Week 4 cannot reduce that authority to 5% without the food coordinator's explicit agreement and a community assembly vote with 5-day notice.

Rule 3: The facilitator role does not change during the 6-week cycle. Even if community members feel the facilitator is not performing well, the facilitator serves until the July 5 reflection meeting. Changing the facilitator mid-cycle creates instability. Address performance concerns at the July 5 reflection.

Rule 4: Voted decisions are final until the July 5 reflection meeting. This prevents re-litigation of prior decisions when new content arrives. If Week 2's psychological support decision seems wrong after reading Week 4's food preservation content, note the conflict but do not reopen the Week 2 decision. Bring it to July 5.

### 3B. Governance Module Precedence

**Why the Governance Module Should Come First — and Why It Can Start in Parallel with Phase 5 Publication**

The question "which Phase 4 module do we start with?" has a definitive answer in the Zone 5 context, and it is worth explaining the reasoning explicitly so Phase 4 teams do not default to the easier modules (household scaling, Zone 5 adaptation) when the harder one (governance) has the highest stakes.

**The governance module is load-bearing for all other modules**

Every other Phase 4 module (household scaling, Zone 5 adaptation, agricultural intensification, technology repair) requires governance to be functional to execute at community scale. The household scaling module tells households how to coordinate internally — but households connect to community governance for resource allocation, conflict resolution, and shared infrastructure decisions. The Zone 5 adaptation module specifies winter governance decisions (heating rationing, fuel procurement) — but those decisions require a governance structure to authorize them. Agricultural intensification requires governance to coordinate shared land use, labor allocation, and surplus distribution.

If governance is not functional, the other Phase 4 modules produce excellent individual household practice with no community integration. They still have value — but they have significantly less value than when governance is operational.

**The governance module is the one that cannot be improvised under pressure**

Phase 5 research (Phase 4 Framework, Section 4) established that governance cannot be improvised under crisis pressure. All other domains — food storage, water systems, energy backup — can be improvised to some degree. Governance cannot. A community that improvises governance when a crisis arrives will get either informal strongman authority (whoever speaks most confidently) or committee paralysis (no one has authority, nothing gets decided). Neither is functional. The governance structure must exist before the crisis it will be called to manage.

This means the governance module has higher activation priority than any other module, and it has higher time sensitivity than any other module. A household can stockpile food in July and still be adequately prepared. A community cannot ratify governance in October (heating crisis month in Zone 5) and be adequately prepared.

**Why governance can start in parallel with Phase 5 publication (not after)**

The governance module does not require Phase 5 content to be published before it starts. The Phase 3 governance document (covering community-scale governance for 100+ person communities) and the Phase 4 Framework (covering implementation sequencing) both exist and are production-ready. A Phase 4 governance workshop can run June 1, before the Option C Week 1 release (Community Implementation Playbook + Microgrids), because the workshop draws on Phase 3 and Phase 4 documents, not on Phase 5 content.

In Option C's rolling model, this creates an important sequencing advantage: governance is operational before the first Phase 5 cluster publishes. When the Week 1 cluster (Playbook + Microgrids) reaches community members, there is already a functioning governance structure to make the decisions the Playbook identifies. Communities that wait for Phase 5 content before starting governance are missing this window.

**Recommended Phase 4 activation sequence under Option C**:

May 29 (pre-Week-1): Governance skeleton created and minimal assembly held. Facilitator and emergency coordinator elected.

June 1–3: Governance synthesis workshop (3 days). Charter drafted.

June 5 (Week 1 Thursday assembly): Charter draft reviewed against the Playbook and Microgrids content. Amendments as needed. Charter not yet formally adopted — one more week of stress-testing before adoption vote.

June 12 (Week 2 Thursday assembly): Charter formally adopted by community assembly, concurrent with the psychological support and conflict resolution governance decisions. This is the right moment for formal adoption because the community now knows how governance handles both practical decisions (microgrids, energy) and emotionally charged decisions (mental health escalation, conflict).

June 13 onward: Governance is operational. All subsequent Phase 4 modules (household scaling, Zone 5 adaptation, agricultural intensification) execute within the governance framework established June 1–12.

**Parallel execution is not coordination overhead — it is risk reduction**

Running the governance module in parallel with Phase 5 publication (rather than after) does not create extra coordination burden. It eliminates one coordination problem: what to do when Phase 5 publication raises a governance question the community is not organized enough to answer. Under parallel execution, governance is already functional when Phase 5 raises the question. Under sequential execution (governance after Phase 5), the community must interrupt Phase 5 implementation to build governance before they can use it — which delays both.
