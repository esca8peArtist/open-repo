# Phase 6 Author-Domain Mapping Rubric
## Expertise Requirements, Capability Gap Profiles, Matching Algorithm, Tier Scoring

> **How to use this document**: On June 14, run each confirmed author through the Tier Scoring section (Section 2) using their completed AUTHOR_READINESS_INTAKE_FORM.md. Then run the Domain-to-Author Matching Algorithm (Section 3) to generate an optimal assignment. Use the Gap Profiles (Section 4) to set onboarding depth. Use Section 5 to decide solo vs. split-domain assignment.

## Section 1: Expertise Requirements Per Domain

Each domain requires a combination of research background and lived or practitioner experience. Both dimensions matter: a domain expert who cannot write practitioner-accessible long-form content will produce a document that does not serve Zone 5 communities. A strong writer without domain grounding will produce accurate-sounding but shallow research.

### Domain 60 — International Coordination

**Research background required**: International development studies, political science with international focus, NGO management or humanitarian coordination, or community organizing with cross-border experience. Political science with purely domestic focus is insufficient.

**Lived or practitioner experience required**: Direct work with international networks, diaspora organizations, transnational advocacy networks, or international NGOs — at community or sub-national scale. National government foreign policy experience does not qualify unless the author has specifically worked on community-to-community international connections.

**Minimum competency threshold**: Author must be able to identify how a Zone 5 community would access international seed libraries, mutual aid networks, or governance frameworks independently of the US federal government. If the author cannot sketch this pathway in a brief intake conversation, they lack the necessary grounding.

**Writing challenge specific to this domain**: The tendency to write at the level of nation-states and intergovernmental organizations rather than communities. The scope must remain resolutely at community and sub-national scale. Authors with primarily academic international relations backgrounds need explicit scope guidance.

---

### Domain 61 — Intergenerational Knowledge Transmission

**Research background required**: Adult education, non-formal education, knowledge management, cultural anthropology with education focus, or agricultural extension education. Formal K-12 or higher education backgrounds require supplementary grounding in non-formal and community-based learning.

**Lived or practitioner experience required**: Direct experience designing or delivering non-formal skill transmission (mentorship programs, cooperative learning, apprenticeship coordination, oral history documentation, or community training programs). Teaching in formal educational institutions without community-facing component is insufficient.

**Minimum competency threshold**: Author must understand the distinction between tacit and explicit knowledge (Polanyi) and its practical implications for community skill transmission — specifically, why documentation alone does not transmit skilled practice. If the author frames all knowledge transmission as documentation, they lack the core conceptual grounding.

**Writing challenge specific to this domain**: Scope creep into K-12 educational reform or formal curriculum design. The hard scope boundary is: transmission without institutional infrastructure. Authors from formal education backgrounds need weekly scope audits.

---

### Domain 62 — Infrastructure Interdependencies

**Research background required**: Engineering (civil, systems, mechanical, or electrical), urban planning with infrastructure focus, systems science, or emergency management with infrastructure analysis experience. Systems ecology and complex adaptive systems backgrounds are also valid.

**Lived or practitioner experience required**: Direct experience with infrastructure failure, repair, or resilience design at community or regional scale — OR practitioner experience in permaculture design, off-grid systems design, or community resilience planning that has engaged with interdependency questions. Purely theoretical engineering backgrounds need supplementation.

**Minimum competency threshold**: Author must be able to describe a failure cascade from a specific starting point (e.g., grid failure during Zone 5 January) through at least three dependent systems, and identify where community action can interrupt the cascade. If the author can only describe failure at the system level but not the community-intervention level, they lack practitioner grounding.

**Writing challenge specific to this domain**: Academic infrastructure research operates at municipal and national scale; community-scale cascade analysis requires translation and extrapolation. Authors must be willing to synthesize from adjacent literature where community-scale data is sparse.

---

### Domain 63 — Ecosystem Restoration

**Research background required**: Soil ecology, agroecology, permaculture design, restoration ecology, or regenerative agriculture with research competency. Academic ecology without practitioner grounding is insufficient; practitioner grounding without research synthesis ability is also insufficient.

**Lived or practitioner experience required**: Hands-on field experience implementing soil restoration, native plant establishment, food forest design, or agroforestry in a temperate climate — NOT only reading about it. The tacit knowledge required to write accurately about soil biology recovery, earthworks design, and native plant establishment requires field experience.

**Minimum competency threshold**: Author must be able to describe a Zone 5-specific soil restoration protocol from existing field or organic matter percentage to target, including the succession of interventions (cover cropping, no-till, compost application) and realistic timeline for measurable improvement. Authors who can only describe these interventions in abstract terms lack the grounding.

**Writing challenge specific to this domain**: The temptation to write at the ecological theory level rather than the practitioner intervention level. Every section must answer: "What does a community member or farmer actually do?" Theory is background, not content.

---

### Domain 64 — Community Economic Resilience

**Research background required**: Economics (heterodox, cooperative, or ecological preferred — neoclassical economics without exposure to alternative economic frameworks is insufficient), community development, cooperative development, or social entrepreneurship with economic analysis competency.

**Lived or practitioner experience required**: Direct participation in or analysis of cooperative enterprises, local currency systems, mutual aid networks, community land trusts, or timebanking — at community scale. Banking or finance industry experience without cooperative or community economic grounding does not qualify.

**Minimum competency threshold**: Author must be able to identify at least three distinct types of complementary currency systems (e.g., time banks, mutual credit, local commodity currency) and their different design implications for a Zone 5 agricultural community. Authors who cannot make this distinction will not produce a document with adequate differentiation.

**Writing challenge specific to this domain**: The gap between elegant economic theory (Ostrom, Graeber) and the practical messiness of community implementation. The document must bridge this gap with real implementation case studies and Zone 5-specific adaptation. Theory-first authors need explicit instruction to ground every section in implementation reality.

---

### Domain 65 — Institutional Learning and Governance Scaling

**Research background required**: Political science or public administration with community governance focus, organizational development or sociology with a focus on institutional design, community organizing with governance infrastructure experience, or cooperative governance experience.

**Lived or practitioner experience required**: Direct experience with governance structures in growing or stressed communities — cooperative board service, intentional community governance, watershed council coordination, rural electric cooperative governance, or post-disaster community governance. National or state government experience does not substitute.

**Minimum competency threshold**: Author must be able to articulate what fails in community governance when a group grows from 25 to 100 people, and identify at least one structural intervention that addresses this. Authors who cannot identify specific governance failure modes (founder concentration, legitimacy erosion, emergency power creep) lack practitioner grounding.

**Writing challenge specific to this domain**: Academic governance literature documents successful models; failure cases are underrepresented. This domain requires active synthesis from failure cases — which demands an author who is willing to document and analyze things going wrong, not only things going right.

## Section 2: Tier A/B/C Scoring Adapted for Phase 6

The Phase 6 Tier system adapts the 16-point rubric from the Phase 5 Wave 2 AUTHOR_READINESS_INTAKE_FORM.md. Phase 6 adds a fifth dimension (Domain Grounding) to the four original dimensions, raising the maximum score to 25.

### Five-Dimension Scoring Matrix

**Dimension 1 — Domain Knowledge** (same as Phase 5, 1–5 scale)

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Academic familiarity only; limited practical experience |
| 2 | Some hands-on experience; comfortable with basic concepts |
| 3 | Direct working experience; can solve real domain problems |
| 4 | Multiple years applied practice; has taught others in this domain |
| 5 | Recognized expertise; written or trained in this domain |

**Dimension 2 — Long-Form Practitioner Writing** (same as Phase 5, 1–5 scale)

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Little to no long-form writing; primarily short-form or academic |
| 2 | Has written 4,000+ words but for academic or internal audiences |
| 3 | Has written practitioner guides, training materials, or detailed blog posts for non-specialist audiences |
| 4 | Regular practitioner writing; multiple guides for non-specialist audiences; comfortable with structural editing |
| 5 | Extensive practitioner writing history; published in extension, mutual aid, or practitioner publications; used as training material |

**Dimension 3 — Markdown and Digital Collaboration** (same as Phase 5, 1–5 scale)

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | No Markdown experience |
| 2 | Some exposure; can handle basic formatting with reference |
| 3 | Comfortable with headings, lists, links, tables; semi-regular use |
| 4 | Proficient; writes Markdown without reference; comfortable with YAML |
| 5 | Expert; daily Markdown use; familiar with Obsidian or similar |

**Dimension 4 — Research and Citation** (same as Phase 5, 1–5 scale)

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Limited synthesis experience; citation checking unfamiliar |
| 2 | Can synthesize sources with time; citation verification is new |
| 3 | Comfortable with synthesis; can write cited section with 10–15 sources |
| 4 | Strong synthesis; comfortable with contested evidence; source quality prioritization |
| 5 | Expert; has produced annotated bibliographies, literature reviews, or evidence hierarchies |

**Dimension 5 — Domain-Specific Practitioner Grounding** (NEW for Phase 6, 1–5 scale)

This dimension captures the lived or field experience that distinguishes an author who can write convincingly about a domain from one who has only read about it.

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | No practitioner experience in this domain; purely reading/academic |
| 2 | Adjacent practitioner experience (a related domain with transferable knowledge) |
| 3 | Direct practitioner experience in this domain; has implemented or managed systems in this area |
| 4 | Substantial practitioner experience; has solved domain-specific problems in community or field settings; has mentored others |
| 5 | Deep practitioner expertise; is a recognized practitioner in this domain; can assess edge cases from experience |

### Tier Assignment

Sum the five dimensions (maximum 25):

| Score | Tier | Description | Phase 6 Implications |
|-------|------|-------------|----------------------|
| 20–25 | **Tier A** | Experienced practitioner-researcher | Minimal scaffolding; can self-manage research; eligible for split-domain assignment |
| 14–19 | **Tier B** | Domain expert, new to this project | Standard onboarding; T+3 check-in added; solo domain assignment recommended |
| 6–13 | **Tier C** | Emerging contributor | Extended onboarding; reduced scope (2,500–3,500 words); weekly check-ins; solo domain only |
| Below 6 | **Hold** | Insufficient grounding | Do not assign until Dimension 1 or 5 score improves to 3+ |

### Modified Tier Assignment Rules

These rules override the matrix score in specific circumstances:

- **Dimension 1 (Domain Knowledge) = 1**: Do not assign regardless of total score. A writer with no domain knowledge cannot produce accurate content.
- **Dimension 5 (Domain-Specific Practitioner Grounding) = 1 and Dimension 1 = 2**: Assign Tier C maximum with explicit scope reduction and project lead source sprint support.
- **Dimension 2 (Long-Form Writing) = 1**: Do not assign regardless of other scores. Writing production is the core deliverable.
- **Dimension 4 (Research and Citation) below 3**: Tier B maximum; project lead performs citation audit at T+7.

## Section 3: Domain-to-Author Matching Algorithm

Run this algorithm for each confirmed author on June 14. The algorithm produces a ranked list of domain assignments.

### Step 1 — Dimension 1 + Dimension 5 Domain Screen

For each of the six domains (60–65), compare the author's Dimension 1 score in that domain and their Dimension 5 practitioner grounding score. Both must be 3 or higher for an author to be eligible for Tier A or Tier B assignment in that domain.

If only one is 3+: eligible for Tier C assignment in that domain (with scope reduction and project lead support).

If both are below 3: the author is ineligible for that domain. Do not assign.

### Step 2 — Score Eligible Domains

For each domain where the author is eligible, calculate the assignment score:

```
Assignment Score = (D1 × 2) + (D5 × 2) + D2 + D4
Maximum: 24
```

This formula double-weights Domain Knowledge (D1) and Practitioner Grounding (D5) because these are the hardest to supplement at runtime. Writing (D2) and Research (D4) can be improved with project lead support; domain grounding cannot.

### Step 3 — Apply Domain Difficulty Modifiers

Domains 60 and 62 have higher source readiness gaps (55–72% vs. 72–82% for Domains 63 and 64). Apply a modifier:

| Domain | Difficulty Modifier | Rationale |
|--------|---------------------|-----------|
| 60 — International Coordination | -2 | Lowest source readiness; sub-national community-scale literature is sparse |
| 61 — Intergenerational Knowledge | -1 | Crisis-context literature gap requires synthesis from adjacent fields |
| 62 — Infrastructure Interdependencies | -2 | Community-scale literature gap; requires extrapolation from larger-scale research |
| 63 — Ecosystem Restoration | 0 | Richest source base; most publication-ready |
| 64 — Economic Resilience | 0 | Strong source base; Zone 5 rural community application is the primary gap |
| 65 — Institutional Learning | -1 | Governance failure documentation requires active synthesis from difficult sources |

Apply the modifier: `Adjusted Assignment Score = Assignment Score + Difficulty Modifier`

### Step 4 — Rank and Assign

Rank domains by Adjusted Assignment Score (highest = best fit). Primary assignment goes to highest-ranked eligible domain. If multiple authors are eligible for the same domain, the higher Tier author takes priority.

### Step 5 — Conflict Resolution

If two Tier A authors are eligible for the same domain:
- Assign the author with higher D5 (practitioner grounding) to that domain
- Assign the other author to their second-ranked eligible domain

If no Tier A or Tier B author is eligible for a domain:
- Assign the highest-scoring eligible Tier C author with project lead co-research support
- Flag domain for additional source sprint before author begins production

## Section 4: Capability Gap Profiles

These profiles describe what an author with expertise in one set of domains lacks when assigned to adjacent domains. Use these to design targeted onboarding supplements.

### If author has Domain 63 (Ecosystem Restoration) expertise, assigning to Domain 60 (International Coordination):

**What they have**: Systems thinking, ecological network analysis, long-term horizon research, comfort with complexity and uncertainty.

**What they lack**: International governance literature, cross-border legal frameworks, diaspora and NGO network knowledge. The translation from ecological systems to social-political systems is non-trivial.

**Onboarding supplement**: Provide the expert contact starter list from Domain 60 (Section 1 of PHASE_6_DOMAIN_60_65_RESEARCH_LANDSCAPE.md) plus a 90-minute orientation to the international coordination landscape before Day 1 of research. Scope must restrict to community-scale international networks only.

### If author has Domain 64 (Economic Resilience) expertise, assigning to Domain 65 (Institutional Learning):

**What they have**: Ostrom commons governance framework (directly applicable), cooperative enterprise governance knowledge, resource allocation analysis.

**What they lack**: Governance failure analysis methodology, post-disaster governance literature, federation design beyond cooperative models, Dunbar scale threshold analysis.

**Onboarding supplement**: Provide the failure analysis reading list from PHASE_6_DOMAINS_B_E_F_RESEARCH_OUTLINES.md Domain B (Solnit, Aldrich, Tierney) plus a scope note emphasizing that governance failure documentation is the differentiating content of this domain.

### If author has Domain 61 (Intergenerational Knowledge) expertise, assigning to Domain 62 (Infrastructure Interdependencies):

**What they have**: Systems thinking about knowledge transmission, tacit/explicit knowledge distinction, community learning dynamics.

**What they lack**: Engineering infrastructure models, cascade failure analysis, redundancy design principles. The technical infrastructure domain requires engineering literacy.

**Onboarding supplement**: Provide the Post Carbon Institute "Community Resilience Reader" as a practitioner-accessible infrastructure interdependency primer. Pair with an engineering-background peer reviewer. Scope the author's contribution toward community decision-making and prioritization rather than technical infrastructure design.

### If author has Domain 65 (Institutional Learning) expertise, assigning to Domain 61 (Intergenerational Knowledge):

**What they have**: Institutional memory design, documentation standards, governance transition management, collective decision-making about knowledge.

**What they lack**: Situated learning theory, tacit knowledge transmission methodology, apprenticeship design, non-formal education models.

**Onboarding supplement**: Assign Lave and Wenger "Situated Learning" (1991) and Polanyi "The Tacit Dimension" (1966) as pre-production reading. Scope the author's contribution toward institutional transmission infrastructure and documentation standards, with explicit boundary at individual skill acquisition theory (which the author lacks grounding in).

## Section 5: Solo vs. Split-Domain Decision Logic

The default assignment for Phase 6 Wave 1 is one author per domain. A split-domain assignment (one author researches two domains simultaneously) is appropriate only under specific conditions.

### When Split-Domain is Appropriate

Apply all three conditions:

1. **Tier A only**: The author must score 20+ out of 25 on the five-dimension rubric for BOTH domains being considered. Split-domain assignment for Tier B or C authors is not appropriate — it stretches authors who are already operating near capacity.

2. **Domain adjacency**: The two domains must share significant literature overlap. Approved adjacent pairings for Phase 6:
   - Domain 63 (Ecosystem Restoration) + Domain 64 (Economic Resilience): Ostrom commons framework, regenerative agriculture economics
   - Domain 61 (Intergenerational Knowledge) + Domain 62 (Infrastructure Interdependencies): systems complexity, tacit knowledge in technical systems
   - Domain 64 (Economic Resilience) + Domain 65 (Institutional Learning): Ostrom governance framework, cooperative enterprise design
   - Non-adjacent pairings (e.g., Domain 60 + Domain 63): require project lead approval and explicit scope reduction plan

3. **Bandwidth confirmation**: The author must confirm they can commit 8–10 hours per week across both domains within the Phase 6 12-week window. Standard commitment is 4–6 hours per week for one domain. Split-domain doubles this.

### When to Default to Solo

Default to solo assignment when:
- Author is Tier B (score 14–19)
- Author is Tier A but the two candidate domains are not in the approved adjacent pairings
- Author's time confirmation is below 8 hours per week
- Either domain has a source readiness below 65% (Domains 60 and 62 in current assessment) — sparse sources require more research time per output word

### Word Count Implications

| Assignment type | Target word count per author | Research hours estimate |
|-----------------|------------------------------|------------------------|
| Solo (one domain) | 8,000–10,000 words | 40–55 hours over 12 weeks |
| Split-domain (two adjacent domains, Tier A) | 14,000–18,000 words total | 70–90 hours over 12 weeks |

Do not set a higher per-domain target for split-domain authors. The quality floor for each domain remains the same; the author simply covers more ground in the same time frame due to source reuse and conceptual overlap.

### Split-Domain Red Flags

Flag for project lead review if:
- Author proposes split-domain assignment during intake (rather than project lead initiating it)
- Author's confirmed availability is below 6 hours per week for either domain
- The two proposed domains have zero literature overlap
- Either domain has a source readiness modifier of -2 (Domains 60 or 62)
