# Phase 6 Domain 60–65 Research Landscape
## Literature Map for Author-Domain Matching — June 14, 2026

> **Lead finding**: The six Phase 6 domains have sharply different publication landscapes. Domains 63 (Ecosystem Restoration) and 64 (Economic Resilience) have the richest practitioner-accessible literature with 70–80% source readiness. Domain 60 (International Coordination) and Domain 62 (Infrastructure Interdependencies) have deep academic literature but a thin practitioner-community layer — the most significant authorship challenge. Domain 61 (Intergenerational Knowledge) and Domain 65 (Institutional Learning) sit in the middle: solid theoretical foundations with documented gaps in crisis-context application. No domain has a closed research frontier; all six have identified active debates and knowledge gaps suitable for original synthesis.

---

## Domain 60: International Coordination Frameworks

### Definition for Phase 6 Scope

How communities and sub-national coalitions access, adapt, and integrate international knowledge, mutual aid networks, and governance frameworks when national-level coordination fails or is adversarial. This includes: treaty-based resource-sharing precedents, international NGO coordination models, diaspora mutual aid networks, and the legal frameworks governing cross-border community cooperation.

### Publication Landscape

**Leading academic journals**: Global Governance (Lynne Rienner); International Organization (Cambridge); Third World Quarterly; Journal of International Organizations Studies; Global Policy (Wiley).

**University centers**: Balsillie School of International Affairs (Waterloo, Ontario) — international governance under fragmented multilateralism; Center for Global Development (Washington, DC) — cross-national resilience and aid coordination; Notre Dame's Kroc Institute for International Peace Studies — sub-national peace and coordination research; UN University World Institute for Development Economics Research (UNU-WIDER) — community-level international integration.

**Think tanks and NGOs**: Chatham House (UK) — international governance failure analysis; International Crisis Group — sub-national coordination in fragile states; Overseas Development Institute (ODI, UK) — international aid coordination models at community level; CARE International — practical community coordination across borders.

**Key foundational works**: Elinor Ostrom's polycentric governance research applies here at transboundary scale; David Held's "Democracy and the Global Order" (1995) documents cosmopolitan governance theory; Anne-Marie Slaughter's "A New World Order" (2004) — transnational governmental networks; James Scott's "Seeing Like a State" (1998) is the counterweight (when state-centric coordination fails communities).

**2025–2026 publication activity**: The post-COVID governance literature on international coordination failure (WHO coordination during COVID) has produced a surge of publications. The IMF and World Bank have both published 2025 analyses on community-level access to international resources outside national government channels.

### Knowledge Gaps

- Sub-national community access to international resources without national government intermediation — the legal and practical pathways are not consolidated in practitioner-accessible literature
- Diaspora-to-community resource flows: documented in development economics but not synthesized for resilience practitioner audiences
- Mutual aid precedents across national borders in non-disaster contexts: LETS systems that operate cross-nationally, seed banks with international reach (Navdanya, Seed Savers International) — scattered case studies, no synthesis
- International food sovereignty networks (La Via Campesina, GRAIN) and their practical community-level frameworks — rich primary sources, under-synthesized

### Expert Contact Starter List (3–5 Organizations)

1. **La Via Campesina** — https://viacampesina.org — international peasant and smallholder network; community food sovereignty frameworks with cross-border reach; publishes practitioner policy documents
2. **GRAIN** — https://grain.org — agricultural biodiversity and food sovereignty research; practitioner-accessible reports; international seed and land sovereignty focus
3. **Transnational Institute** — https://www.tni.org — community and social movement coordination across borders; water and land commons governance at international scale
4. **Overseas Development Institute (ODI)** — https://odi.org — community-level international development coordination; practical guidance on accessing international resources outside state channels
5. **International Association for Community Development (IACD)** — https://www.iacdglobal.org — international community development network; practitioner membership and case study library

## Domain 61: Intergenerational Knowledge Transmission

### Definition for Phase 6 Scope

Systems for preserving and transmitting critical practical skills, community history, and adaptive knowledge across generations without formal educational infrastructure. Includes: apprenticeship architecture, oral history and documentation methods, elder knowledge capture, and educational continuity under disruption. Distinct from Domain 65 (which focuses on institutional learning) — Domain 61 focuses on the transmission channel itself, not governance of the channel.

### Publication Landscape

**Leading academic journals**: Adult Education Quarterly; Journal of Extension (land-grant university extension network); Learning, Culture and Social Interaction; International Journal of Lifelong Learning.

**University centers**: Cooperative Extension System (all land-grant universities — Iowa State, University of Illinois, Purdue, UW-Madison) — the most direct institutional research base for non-formal adult skill transmission in Zone 5; Cornell University's Polson Institute for Global Development — rural youth development; University of Wisconsin Center for Cooperative Studies — cooperative education and skill transmission.

**Think tanks and NGOs**: JFF (Jobs for the Future) — non-traditional pathways for skill acquisition; National 4-H Council — largest non-formal youth skill education network in the US; Cultural Survival — indigenous knowledge transmission and documentation.

**Key foundational works**: Lave and Wenger, "Situated Learning" (1991) — communities of practice; Kolb, "Experiential Learning" (1984) — four-stage learning cycle; Polanyi, "The Tacit Dimension" (1966) — tacit vs. explicit knowledge distinction (critical for understanding what manuals cannot transmit); UNESCO's convention on intangible cultural heritage — international documentation standards for knowledge transmission.

**2024–2025 publication activity**: UNESCO published a 2025 synthesis on climate impacts on indigenous knowledge systems; ERIC has a growing literature on non-formal and community-based learning post-COVID; arXiv published a 2025 paper on AI-assisted knowledge transmission across generations (relevant to digital archiving subsection).

### Knowledge Gaps

- Crisis-context education: what happens when institutional infrastructure collapses — Cuba post-Soviet, Puerto Rico post-Maria are the best-documented analogs but remain undertheorized
- Learning curve data for specific physical skills in low-resource settings — FEMA CERT and Red Cross curricula provide proxies; peer-reviewed data sparse
- Community-scale tacit knowledge documentation protocols: video as transmission medium is underresearched relative to written documentation
- Digital archiving standards for 20–50 year preservation horizon in communities without institutional maintenance infrastructure

### Expert Contact Starter List

1. **National 4-H Council** — https://4-h.org — largest rural skills education network in US; practitioner network includes thousands of mentorship coordinators; Zone 5 chapters active
2. **Cooperative Extension System** — Iowa State (https://extension.iastate.edu), UW-Madison (https://extension.wisc.edu), Purdue (https://extension.purdue.edu) — primary practitioner-to-farmer knowledge transmission infrastructure in Zone 5; peer reviewer and expert contact source
3. **Cultural Survival** — https://www.culturalsurvival.org — indigenous knowledge transmission and documentation specialists; cross-cultural transmission models applicable to community contexts
4. **Oral History Association** — https://www.oralhistory.org — practitioner standards for elder knowledge documentation; methodology resources for community oral history programs
5. **iFixit** — https://ifixit.com — open-source repair documentation model; community-built knowledge archive methodology that has produced 100,000+ practical repair guides; relevant to tacit knowledge capture for mechanical skills

## Domain 62: Infrastructure Interdependencies

### Definition for Phase 6 Scope

How critical infrastructure systems (energy, water, food, communications, transportation) interact, and how failures cascade across those interdependencies at community and regional scale. Includes: failure cascade modeling, redundancy design principles, community-scale resilience auditing, and the decision logic for prioritizing infrastructure investment under resource constraints. Cross-domain: draws on Phase 5 microgrid work and Track B Meshtastic communication infrastructure.

### Publication Landscape

**Leading academic journals**: International Journal of Critical Infrastructure Protection (Elsevier); Natural Hazards and Earth System Sciences (EGU); Reliability Engineering & System Safety; Journal of Infrastructure Systems (ASCE).

**University centers**: MIT Lincoln Laboratory — infrastructure interdependency modeling; University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign (UIUC) National Center for Supercomputing Applications — regional infrastructure modeling; ETH Zurich's Future Resilient Systems program — community infrastructure interdependency; Carnegie Mellon Engineering and Public Policy — infrastructure risk and resilience.

**Think tanks and NGOs**: RAND Corporation — infrastructure resilience analysis; Pacific Northwest National Laboratory — energy-water-food nexus research; Rocky Mountain Institute — energy system resilience and interdependency; Post Carbon Institute — community infrastructure analysis for resilience practitioners.

**Key foundational works**: Stephen Flynn, "America the Vulnerable" (2004) — systemic infrastructure fragility; Perrow, "Normal Accidents" (1984) — tightly coupled systems and cascade failure; Taleb, "Antifragile" (2012) — design for resilience; Tierney and Bruneau's "Conceptualizing and Measuring Resilience: A Key to Disaster Loss Reduction" (2007) — practitioner-accessible resilience framework.

**2025–2026 publication activity**: The energy-water-food nexus literature has grown substantially since 2023. DHS CISA released updated critical infrastructure protection guidance in 2025. FEMA's 2026 Community Resilience Planning Guide integrates infrastructure interdependency analysis for the first time.

### Knowledge Gaps

- Community-scale (20–200 person) infrastructure interdependency modeling: most research addresses municipal or national scale; community-scale cascade failure maps are rare
- Zone 5-specific failure cascade data: agricultural equipment failure + grid failure during planting window interactions are documented in practitioner experience but not in the academic literature
- Partial failure modes: most infrastructure research models complete failure; partial degradation scenarios (reduced grid, intermittent internet, limited medical supply) are underrepresented
- Redundancy design for community-scale energy-water-food systems: engineering standards exist for industrial scale; community-scale redundancy design is fragmented across permaculture, preparedness, and engineering literature

### Expert Contact Starter List

1. **Rocky Mountain Institute** — https://rmi.org — energy resilience and distributed energy systems; community-scale energy-water interdependency research; practitioner-accessible publications
2. **Post Carbon Institute** — https://www.postcarbon.org — community infrastructure resilience for practitioners; publishes practical community resilience guides including "Community Resilience Reader"
3. **FEMA Community Resilience** — https://www.fema.gov/community-resilience — updated 2026 Community Resilience Planning Guide includes infrastructure interdependency analysis; federal contact for methodology and data
4. **American Lifelines Alliance** — https://www.americanlifelinesalliance.com — infrastructure interdependency technical guidance; peer reviewer network for infrastructure resilience research
5. **Practical Action** — https://practicalaction.org — community-scale infrastructure resilience in resource-constrained contexts; practitioner methodology with international case study base applicable to Zone 5

## Domain 63: Ecosystem Restoration

### Definition for Phase 6 Scope

Interventions for reversing land degradation and restoring ecosystem function at household and community scale (0.25 acres to 200 acres), producing measurable results within 3–7 years. Includes: soil biology restoration, water infiltration engineering, native habitat establishment, and integration with food production systems. Zone 5 Midwest focus: Mollisol soil restoration, tallgrass prairie reestablishment, agroforestry on agricultural land.

### Publication Landscape

**Leading academic journals**: Restoration Ecology (Wiley/Society for Ecological Restoration); Ecological Restoration (University of Wisconsin Press); Journal of Applied Ecology; Soil & Tillage Research; Agroforestry Systems.

**University centers**: University of Wisconsin-Madison Department of Agronomy — Midwest agricultural soil restoration research; Iowa State University Department of Ecology, Evolution, and Organismal Biology — tallgrass prairie restoration; University of Illinois Prairie Research Institute — Illinois-specific restoration ecology; Tallgrass Prairie Center (University of Northern Iowa) — practitioner-accessible prairie restoration resources.

**Think tanks and NGOs**: Rodale Institute — regenerative agriculture research with 40+ year farming systems trial; Xerces Society for Invertebrate Conservation — pollinator habitat restoration; Practical Farmers of Iowa — farmer-led restoration research with Zone 5 field data; Chicago Botanic Garden's Midwest Native Seed Network — launched 2024, 300+ institutions.

**Key foundational works**: Gabe Brown, "Dirt to Soil" (2018) — regenerative soil building; Mark Shepard, "Restoration Agriculture" (2013) — Zone 5 agroforestry and silvo-pasture; Bill Mollison, "Permaculture: A Designers' Manual" (1988) — water earthworks and ecosystem design; Daryl Smith et al., "Tallgrass Prairie Center Guide to Prairie Restoration in the Upper Midwest" (University of Iowa Press, 2010).

**2025–2026 publication activity**: Exceptionally active. ScienceDirect 2025 published on mycorrhizal community restoration methodology; Wiley 2025 on mycorrhizal colonization in post-agricultural soils; biorXiv 2025 on soil carbon gains from regenerative practices in US Midwest specifically; Science Societies August 2025 on regenerative agriculture and food systems. This domain has the most current publication momentum of the six.

### Knowledge Gaps

- Zone 5-specific mycorrhizal network recovery timelines: general soil biology recovery research exists; Zone 5 climate-specific data is fragmented across extension publications
- Community-scale water earthworks (swales, constructed wetlands) implementation without heavy equipment: household-scale earthworks protocols are underrepresented
- Climate zone adaptation for seed selection: Zone 5 is shifting toward Zone 6a in many areas; seed sourcing research is catching up but lags behind climate change
- Multi-landowner restoration coordination: watershed-scale restoration requires governance across property boundaries — the governance-ecology interface is underresearched

### Expert Contact Starter List

1. **Rodale Institute** — https://rodaleinstitute.org — 40+ year farming systems trial data; regenerative agriculture practitioner network; peer reviewer source for soil science content
2. **Tallgrass Prairie Center (University of Northern Iowa)** — https://tallgrassprairie.uni.edu — primary US institution for Midwest prairie restoration; publishes practitioner guides; seed mix recommendations
3. **Xerces Society for Invertebrate Conservation** — https://xerces.org — pollinator corridor and native habitat restoration; practitioner methodology and Zone 5-specific guidance
4. **Practical Farmers of Iowa** — https://practicalfarmers.org — farmer-led research on regenerative agriculture; Zone 5 field data; farmer practitioner network for peer review
5. **NRCS State Offices (IL, IA, WI)** — National Resources Conservation Service state offices manage EQIP and CRP programs; provide technical guidance and cost-share for community-scale restoration; peer reviewer source with regulatory knowledge

## Domain 64: Community Economic Resilience

### Definition for Phase 6 Scope

How communities trade, store value, and coordinate economic activity when cash economies, banking infrastructure, or supply chains are disrupted or deliberately reduced. Includes: complementary currencies, timebanking systems, barter network design, cooperative enterprise governance, commons resource management, and inter-community trade federation models. Zone 5 focus: agricultural cooperative tradition, credit union networks, Midwest rural economy precedents.

### Publication Landscape

**Leading academic journals**: Journal of Economic Behavior and Organization; Cambridge Journal of Economics; Ecological Economics; Review of Social Economy (cooperative and alternative economics); Journal of Rural Studies.

**University centers**: Schumacher Center for a New Economics (Great Barrington, MA) — local currency systems research; E.F. Schumacher Society — practical economics of small communities; University of Wisconsin Center for Cooperatives — cooperative enterprise governance research; St. Mary's University Centre for the Study of Co-operatives (Saskatchewan) — largest co-op research center in North America.

**Think tanks and NGOs**: New Economics Foundation (UK) — community currency and wellbeing economics research; Resilience.org — community economic resilience practitioner publications; Cooperative Development Foundation — Midwest cooperative performance data; Transition Network — community economic initiatives documentation.

**Key foundational works**: Elinor Ostrom, "Governing the Commons" (1990) — commons resource governance framework; David Graeber, "Debt: The First 5,000 Years" (2011) — anthropological foundation for non-monetary exchange; E.F. Schumacher, "Small is Beautiful" (1973) — small-scale economic design; Michael Shuman, "The Local Economy Solution" (2015) — supply chain localization frameworks.

**2025–2026 publication activity**: Journal of Economic Behavior and Organization published a 2025 study on mutual credit resilience. Resilience.org published a March 2025 analysis on community currencies. Rapid Transition Alliance has ongoing case study publications. The cooperative movement literature is active around ESG integration and cooperative governance under economic stress.

### Knowledge Gaps

- US rural community currency implementation: most documented LETS and local currency systems are urban or semi-urban; Zone 5 Midwest agricultural context requires adaptation
- Timebanking quantitative effectiveness: qualitative case studies are extensive; quantitative outcome data (economic value exchanged, community cohesion impact) is thin
- Regional economic simulation models for 200–2,000 person communities: scattered in grey literature; not synthesized for practitioner use
- Agricultural barter and surplus exchange protocols at community scale: anecdotal documentation is extensive; formal protocol research is sparse

### Expert Contact Starter List

1. **Schumacher Center for a New Economics** — https://centerforneweconomics.org — primary US institution for local currency research; BerkShares and Ithaca Hours documentation; practitioner network for peer review
2. **US Federation of Worker Cooperatives (USFWC)** — https://www.usfwc.org — worker cooperative governance and economics; practitioner network for cooperative enterprise content review
3. **Cooperative Development Foundation** — https://cdf.coop — Midwest cooperative development support; agricultural cooperative performance data; practitioner connections
4. **New Economics Foundation** — https://neweconomics.org — community currency and complementary economics research; international peer review network
5. **Transition Network** — https://transitionnetwork.org — community economic transition practitioner network; 992 registered groups with documented community economy initiatives; peer review source

## Domain 65: Institutional Learning and Governance Scaling

### Definition for Phase 6 Scope

How governance structures that function at 20–50 person scale adapt, formalize, and federate as communities grow to 150–500 people; and how institutions develop systematic learning from governance failures. Includes: scale threshold analysis, federation design, deliberative democracy tools, post-disaster governance adaptation, and institutional memory systems. Distinct from Domain 61 (individual skill transmission) — Domain 65 focuses on institutional adaptation and organizational learning.

### Publication Landscape

**Leading academic journals**: Policy Sciences; Governance: An International Journal of Policy, Administration, and Institutions; Public Administration Review; Journal of Public Administration Research and Theory; Ecology and Society (open-access; most directly relevant to community resilience governance).

**University centers**: Arizona State University Center for Behavior, Institutions and the Environment (Vincent Ostrom's legacy institution) — polycentric governance research; Indiana University's Ostrom Workshop in Political Theory and Policy Analysis — commons governance and institutional analysis; Harvard Kennedy School's Center for Public Leadership — leadership transition in institutions; Cornell Law School's Digital Commons Library.

**Think tanks and NGOs**: Foundation for Intentional Community (FIC) — practitioner documentation of community governance success and failure; Sociocracy For All — consent-based governance practitioners; P2P Foundation — commons governance and peer production; IASC (International Association for the Study of the Commons) — commons governance academic network.

**Key foundational works**: Elinor Ostrom, "Governing the Commons" (1990) — eight design principles for sustainable governance; Jane Mansbridge, "Beyond Adversary Democracy" (1980) — unitary vs. adversarial democracy in small groups; Robin Dunbar, "Neocortex Size as a Constraint on Group Size in Primates" (1992) — empirical basis for scale threshold analysis; Rebecca Solnit, "A Paradise Built in Hell" (2009) — post-disaster governance emergence.

**2025–2026 publication activity**: Ecology and Society published on state-reinforced self-governance frameworks (2025). IASC's Elinor Ostrom Award proceedings document current commons governance research frontiers. DeGruyter published on Mondragon cooperative governance under economic stress (2025). Ecology and Society has an active special issue on adaptive governance of social-ecological systems.

### Knowledge Gaps

- Governance failure case studies in resource-constrained settings: systematically underreported in academic literature because failures receive less funding and interest than successes; FIC practitioner network holds unpublished failure documentation
- Optimal federation size empirically: the 150-person Dunbar threshold is well-documented; what happens between 150 and 500 persons in voluntary communities is underresearched
- Emergency authority sunset mechanisms: how communities prevent temporary crisis governance from becoming permanent is addressed theoretically but rarely documented with field data
- Institutional learning systems at community scale: how communities create feedback loops from governance failures is a genuine research frontier — mostly practitioner blogs and organizational development literature, not peer-reviewed research

### Expert Contact Starter List

1. **Foundation for Intentional Community (FIC)** — https://www.ic.org — practitioner documentation network for community governance; annual conference and practitioner directory; governance failure case studies (unpublished but accessible to researchers)
2. **Sociocracy For All** — https://www.sociocracyforall.org — consent-based governance practitioners; Zone 5 rural community applications documented; peer reviewer source for governance structure content
3. **Ostrom Workshop (Indiana University)** — https://ostromworkshop.indiana.edu — premier academic center for commons governance research; peer review source for institutional analysis; working paper archive
4. **P2P Foundation** — https://p2pfoundation.net — commons governance and peer production practitioner network; extensive case study library; international peer reviewer contacts
5. **Transition Network** — https://transitionnetwork.org — community governance case studies across 992 registered groups including governance successes and failures; practitioner network
