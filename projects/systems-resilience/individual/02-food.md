## Day 1–3: Immediate Survival

In a sudden failure scenario, you are eating what you have. The gap between today and your first harvest is 2–8 months (depending on when failure occurs).

**Inventory your pantry now:**
- Dried beans: 1 lb = 1,600 cal — how many lbs do you have?
- Rice/pasta: 1 lb rice = 1,650 cal; 1 lb pasta = 1,700 cal
- Canned goods: most vegetables 50–100 cal/can; beans 350 cal; meat 200–400 cal
- Cooking oil: 1 cup = ~1,900 cal (highest caloric density of any common food)
- Flour: 1 lb = 1,600 cal

**Minimum emergency food storage (one person, 30 days):**
- 15 lbs dried beans: $15
- 15 lbs rice or dried corn: $12
- 5 lbs cooking oil: $8
- Salt (5 lbs): $3
- Total: ~$38 — buys 30+ days of survival calories

**Bridge strategy**: Immediately after a failure scenario, focus on what can be foraged, fished, or hunted NOW while garden is being established. Spring (April-May) and fall (August-October) are the richest foraging seasons in Midwest.

## Week 1–4: Stabilization

### Garden Planning (If No Garden Exists)

If it's spring when failure occurs, get seeds in the ground immediately. Even a poorly executed garden beats no garden. Plant in this priority order for fastest return:

**Fast-return crops (30–60 days to first harvest):**
| Crop | Days to Harvest | Plant | Cal/sq ft |
|---|---|---|---|
| Radishes | 25–30 | As soon as soil works | Negligible |
| Lettuce/greens | 30–45 | April | 5 |
| Spinach | 40–50 | April | 8 |
| Bush beans (fresh) | 50–60 | May | 20 |
| Summer squash/zucchini | 50–60 | After frost | 15 |
| Beets (greens edible early) | 55–70 | April | 20 |

**Medium-return crops (60–90 days):**
Cucumbers, Swiss chard, kale, turnips, potatoes (first small ones)

**Long-term caloric crops (90–150 days — plant these FIRST even though you can't eat them yet):**
Dried beans, dent corn, winter squash, potatoes (main crop), sweet potatoes

**Rule**: Plant your caloric crops first (beans, corn, squash, potatoes), then fill in with salad crops. The temptation is to plant what you know — tomatoes and zucchini — but these are low-calorie crops.

### Emergency Protein Sources Requiring No Garden

**Fishing**: One of the fastest returns on effort in Midwest.
- Equipment needed: $20 Walmart rod-and-reel + hooks + line; or improvised from any stiff branch + monofilament
- Catfish: productive at night from any Midwest river or lake; no license required in most states in genuine survival situations
- Bluegill: easiest fish to catch; abundant in ponds; a child can catch them with a worm on a hook
- 100 bluegill (each ~3 oz cleaned) = ~4 lbs protein = ~2,400 calories

**Rabbit/squirrel trapping**: Legal in most Midwest states year-round for survival; requires no firearms
- Box trap (Havahart style): $25–40; can be improvised from wire
- One rabbit/day = 700–900 cal; sustainable small game population in typical Midwest countryside

### Seed Saving — The Key to Multi-Year Food Security

Without commercial seed supply, your garden depends on saved seeds. Start saving from Day 1.

**Easiest crops to save (open-pollinated varieties required — NO F1 hybrids):**

| Crop | How to Save | Isolation Needed | Viability |
|---|---|---|---|
| Beans (dried) | Let pods fully dry on vine; thresh; dry further 2 weeks | 25 ft (self-pollinating) | 3–5 years |
| Corn (dent) | Leave on stalk until fully dry/brown; shell; dry; store | 1,000 ft+ (wind-pollinated) | 2–3 years |
| Tomatoes | Ferment seeds 3 days in water; rinse; dry | 25–50 ft (usually self-pollinates) | 3–5 years |
| Squash/pumpkin | Scoop from ripe squash; rinse; dry 3 weeks | 1/4 mile (cross-pollinates by type) | 3–5 years |
| Peppers | Dry seeds from fully ripe peppers | 300 ft | 2–4 years |
| Lettuce | Let bolt; heads form; collect brown fluff/seeds | 25 ft | 3–5 years |

**Seed storage**: Cool (35–40°F), dry (<8% humidity), dark. Mason jars with silica gel packets in root cellar or chest freezer. Label variety, year, and source.

**Recommended open-pollinated varieties for Zone 5:**
- Potatoes: Kennebec, Yukon Gold, Red Norland (all OP)
- Corn: Bloody Butcher (flour/dent), Hickory King (hominy), Reid's Yellow Dent
- Beans: Jacob's Cattle, Calypso, Mortgage Lifter, Vermont Cranberry
- Winter squash: Hubbard Blue, Butternut (OP), Long Island Cheese Pumpkin
- Tomatoes: Brandywine, Cherokee Purple, Mortgage Lifter, San Marzano
- Source: Seed Savers Exchange (seedsavers.org) — Iowa-based, Zone 5 specialist

## Hunting — Midwest Basics for Beginners

### Whitetail Deer

One deer provides a significant caloric supplement. A field-dressed doe: 50–70 lbs venison (edible). At 800 cal/lb: 40,000–56,000 cal — roughly 55–77 days of meat calories (at 500–700 cal from meat/day combined with vegetables).

**Deer biology essentials:**
- Whitetail are crepuscular: most active at dawn and dusk
- Rut (breeding): October–November (peak activity, deer less cautious)
- Feeding: agricultural fields, acorn mast areas, forest edges
- Water: deer need water daily; find trails near ponds/streams
- Bed during midday in dense cover

**Legal hunting season (varies by state — verify current regulations):**
- Iowa, Illinois, Indiana, Ohio, Wisconsin, Minnesota, Missouri all have fall archery (Sept–Jan) and firearm (Nov–Dec) seasons
- Many states allow year-round doe harvest with appropriate permits
- Hunting licenses: $25–35 resident; required unless in genuine survival scenario

**Basic equipment for deer:**
| Option | Cost | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Rifle (.308, 30-06, .243) | $400–900 | Most versatile; effective 200+ yards |
| Shotgun (12 gauge, slugs) | $250–500 | Required in many Midwest shotgun-only zones; effective to 100 yards |
| Compound bow | $300–600 | Silent; longer season; steep learning curve |
| Crossbow | $300–700 | Easier to learn than vertical bow; longer season in many states |

**Where to hunt:**
- Public land: Illinois' state forests, Iowa's wildlife management areas, Indiana DNR public lands — all free with license
- Permission hunting: knock on farmers' doors; many will grant access in exchange for meat share or help with crop damage
- Your own property: no trespass issues

**Field dressing basics** (do immediately after harvest — meat spoils fast):
1. Lay deer on back
2. Cut from sternum to pelvis, avoiding puncturing stomach/intestines
3. Remove all internal organs (keep heart and liver — edible immediately)
4. Prop chest cavity open to cool
5. Hang in cool location (under 40°F) 1–7 days to age meat
6. Butcher into roasts, steaks, grind scraps into burger

**Processing without electricity:**
- Salt curing: 1 lb salt per 5 lbs meat; coat evenly; hang in cool location 3–6 weeks
- Jerky: thin strips, salt + smoke, 8–12 hours in smoker or 4–6 hours in 165°F oven
- Pemmican (long-term storage): rendered fat + dried powdered meat = 3,500 cal/lb; stores 1–5 years

### Small Game

**Rabbit (cottontail)**: Most productive small game in Midwest. One rabbit = 700–900 calories.
- Best season: November–February (population high; brush cover down for visibility)
- Method 1: Shotgun with #6 shot in brushy areas; or .22 rifle at sitting rabbit
- Method 2: Box trap (Havahart #1.5) baited with apple slice or cabbage
- Method 3: Snare (wire loop on game trail — check daily; may be illegal in your state)
- Field dress: same as deer but simpler; skin by making cuts at ankles, pulling peel over body

**Squirrel**: Gray and fox squirrel; excellent eating.
- Season: August–October when hickory/walnut/acorn mast falls
- Equipment: .22 LR; look for cuttings and tracks under mast trees at dawn
- Yield: 6–8 oz meat per squirrel; ~200–250 cal

**Wild turkey**: One of the highest-yield wild game animals in Midwest.
- Season: Spring (April–May) and Fall (October–November) in most Midwest states
- Equipment: 12-gauge shotgun with turkey choke and #4–6 shot; decoy helpful
- Yield: 15–25 lbs live weight; 8–12 lbs dressed; ~5,000–7,000 cal

**Waterfowl**: Major seasonal protein source during migration.
- Season: September–January depending on species and state
- Equipment: Shotgun (12 or 20 gauge, non-toxic shot required); decoys; blind
- Best areas: Mississippi River flyway, Midwest lakes and ponds during migration

### Fishing — Midwest Species Guide

**Catfish** (channel and flathead): Best survival fish in Midwest.
- Season: Year-round; best May–September; night fishing most productive
- Habitat: Any Midwest river, large creek, or lake
- Method: Bottom rig with chicken liver, worms, or cut bait; heavy hook (2/0–5/0); bottom weight
- Equipment: Heavy rod (10–12 lb test line minimum); no license needed for trotlines in many states
- Yield: Channel cat 1–5 lbs typical; flathead 5–20+ lbs; ~400–500 cal/lb cleaned
- Preservation: Excellent smoked or salted; fry in lard/oil for calorie-dense meal

**Bluegill (bream)**: Easiest fish to catch; excellent food.
- Season: Year-round; best May–August during spawn (very aggressive)
- Habitat: Any pond, lake, or slow river edge
- Method: Small hook (#6–10), red worm or cricket, float 2 ft deep near structure
- Equipment: Lightest setup possible; even a cane pole works
- Yield: 3–5 oz cleaned per fish; need 10–15 per meal; prolific — can catch limits quickly
- Note: Pound-for-pound, bluegill may be the most efficient caloric return per hour of effort

**Walleye**: Premium eating; significant protein source.
- Season: Spring (April–May after ice-out) and fall (September–October)
- Habitat: Natural Midwest lakes (Minnesota, Wisconsin especially); rivers
- Method: Jig or spinner at dawn/dusk near points and transitions

**Carp**: Overlooked but extremely catchable; very caloric.
- Season: Year-round; most active May–June when spawning in shallows
- Method: Dough ball (flour + water + cornmeal), heavy hook near river backwater areas
- Yield: 3–10 lbs typical; 800+ cal/lb (fatty fish)
- Preparation: Strong flavor; best smoked heavily or made into fish cakes with spices

**Trotline (catfish)**: A passive fishing method that works overnight without attendance.
- 50 ft main line tied between stakes on opposite bank
- Drop lines every 2 feet with baited hooks
- Check every 12–24 hours; re-bait as needed
- Can catch 10–20 fish overnight passively
- Regulations vary by state: legal in Illinois and Iowa for survival use; check regulations

### Trapping

**Wire box traps**: Most practical for beginners; no kill-before-you're-ready issue.
- Havahart #1.5 ($30): rabbits, squirrels, mink, small predators
- Havahart #3 ($50): raccoon, opossum, skunk, medium game
- Bait: peanut butter, apples, corn, fish remains

**Foothold traps**: More effective; longer learning curve; legally regulated.
- Duke #1 or Victor Coilspring #1: rabbits, squirrels, mink
- Must check every 24 hours (state law in most Midwest states)

**Snares**: Wire loop on game trail; passive and cheap.
- 24-gauge wire; loop should be 4–5" diameter for rabbits; 8–10" for fox
- Legal for survival use in most Midwest states; check regulations before collapse

## Food Preservation Without Modern Infrastructure

### Root Cellar (Best long-term option)

**What it does**: Maintains 32–40°F at 85–95% humidity — perfect for potatoes, carrots, beets, winter squash, onions, garlic, cabbage, apples.

**DIY root cellar options** (in priority order for Midwest):

**Option A: Use existing basement corner** (cheapest; works for most suburban/rural homes)
- Uninsulated concrete basement corner (northeast corner = coolest)
- Line with shelves; add door to separate from heated house
- Vent: one intake pipe near floor (outside cold air in), one exhaust near ceiling
- Cost: $50–200 in lumber and pipe

**Option B: Buried trash can cellar** (works for apartments and suburban yards)
- 55-gallon galvanized trash can, buried vertically with 12" above grade
- Insulate lid with straw bale
- Holds 40–50 lbs of root vegetables
- Cost: $30–50

**Option C: Full basement root cellar addition**
- Section off a 6×8 ft corner with insulated walls and ceiling
- Door with threshold to separate from rest of basement
- Dual vents for temperature regulation
- Cost: $300–800 materials; weekend project

**Option D: Separate buried structure (rural)**
- 4×8 ft concrete block structure, 4 ft underground
- Roof: wooden frame with dirt cover for insulation
- Access hatch on top
- Cost: $400–1,000 materials

**Storage durations in root cellar:**

| Produce | Temp (°F) | Humidity | Duration |
|---|---|---|---|
| Apples | 32–40 | 90–95% | 2–5 months |
| Beets | 32–40 | 90–95% | 2–5 months |
| Carrots | 32–40 | 90–95% | 4–8 months (can stay in ground) |
| Garlic | 32–40 | 60–70% | 5–8 months |
| Onions | 32–40 | 65–70% | 4–8 months |
| Potatoes | 38–40 | 90–95% | 4–6 months |
| Sweet potatoes | 55–60 | 85–90% | 4–6 months (warmer than regular cellar) |
| Winter squash | 50–55 | 60–70% | 3–6 months |
| Turnips/parsnips | 32–40 | 90–95% | 2–5 months |
| Dried corn | Any cool/dry | <15% humidity | Years |
| Dried beans | Any cool/dry | <15% humidity | Years |

### Pressure Canning

**The only safe method for low-acid foods** (meats, beans, corn, potatoes, all vegetables except tomatoes).

**Equipment:** All American 921 or 930 pressure canner ($300–350 — worth the investment; mechanical seal, no rubber gasket to replace). Presto ($80) acceptable; has rubber gasket.

**Critical**: Never use boiling water bath for non-acidic foods — botulism risk. Only USDA-tested recipes for times and pressures.

**Processing times (weighted gauge, Midwest altitudes <1,000 ft):**
| Food | Pressure | Jar Size | Time |
|---|---|---|---|
| Green/snap beans | 10 PSI | Pint / Quart | 20 / 25 min |
| Corn (whole kernel) | 10 PSI | Pint / Quart | 55 / 85 min |
| Tomatoes + liquid | 10 PSI | Pint / Quart | 20 / 25 min |
| Potatoes (cubed) | 10 PSI | Pint / Quart | 35 / 40 min |
| Venison/game meat | 10 PSI | Pint / Quart | 75 / 90 min |
| Chicken | 10 PSI | Pint / Quart | 75 / 90 min |
| Fish | 10 PSI | Pint | 100 min |
| Dried beans (cooked) | 10 PSI | Pint / Quart | 75 / 90 min |

**Source**: USDA Complete Guide to Home Canning — free at nchfp.uga.edu

### Fermentation (No Equipment Needed)

Fermentation is the oldest food preservation method and requires only salt, a container, and time.

**Lacto-fermented vegetables** (safe, simple, highly nutritious):
- **Sauerkraut**: 1 head cabbage, 1 tsp salt per pound of cabbage. Shred, massage with salt until liquid releases. Pack tightly in jar, submerge under brine, cover loosely. Room temperature 3–4 weeks. Finished sauerkraut keeps 1+ years refrigerated or in cool root cellar.
- **Kimchi-style**: Cabbage + carrots + garlic + ginger + salt + any hot pepper. Same process.
- **Cucumber pickles**: Pack cucumbers with dill and garlic in 2% salt brine (1 tbsp salt per quart). 3–7 days at room temperature.
- **Fermented beans**: Reduce dried bean cooking time; extend preservation 6–12 months
- **Miso/tempeh**: Longer process; requires specific mold cultures but can be self-propagated

**Sourdough**: Wild yeast culture from flour + water. Maintained indefinitely. Replaces commercial yeast for all bread-baking. Start: mix 1:1 flour:water, leave at room temperature, feed daily with equal flour:water. Active in 5–10 days.

### Dehydration

**Sun drying** (works in Midwest July–August, low humidity days):
- Slice thin (1/4 inch), spread on screens or dark-colored metal
- 90°F+ day, direct sun, 2–3 days until completely dry (no moisture when squeezed)
- Bring in at sunset; cover if humidity rises
- Best for: tomatoes, peppers, herbs, mushrooms, apple slices

**Wood/fire drying**: Same principle but more controlled
- String mushrooms, apple rings, herb bundles — hang near (not over) wood stove
- 4–6 hours minimum; until completely dry

**Jerky without electricity**:
- Slice meat 1/4" thick against grain
- Brine: 1/4 cup salt + 2 tbsp sugar per quart water; marinate 8–24 hours
- Hang over string near wood heat; 8–12 hours or until fully dry and leathery
- Keeps 1–2 months at room temperature; years in cool/dry storage

### Smoking

Both preserves and adds flavor. Requires only a smoker (improvised from any metal container) and wood.

**Improvised smoker**: 55-gallon steel drum with offset firebox (smaller drum or metal box attached at side); vent at top; racks inside. Total build: $50–100 + whatever metal is available.

**Cold smoke** (under 90°F): Preserves; does not fully cook. Best for fish, bacon, sausage before cooking.

**Hot smoke** (190–250°F): Cooks and preserves. For poultry, venison, fish.

**Midwest smoking woods**: Hickory (strong, for red meat), apple (mild, for poultry/fish), cherry (medium, for everything), oak (medium-strong), sassafras (traditional, sweet).

## Rural Track Variations

### What Rural Changes
- **Scale**: A full 1-acre garden is feasible (vs. 0.25 acre for suburban); proportionally higher yields
- **Livestock**: Possible with any meaningful acreage:
  - 2 goats: 1–2 quarts milk/day; requires 1/4 acre pasture rotation
  - 6 chickens: 4–5 eggs/day; negligible space; process 2–3/year for meat
  - 2 pigs: 200 lbs pork/pig in 6 months; requires only small pen; feed scraps + extra garden production
- **Hunting**: Direct property access; can hunt without arranging permission
- **Woodlot**: Firewood from your property; maple syrup if sugar maples exist; nuts from established trees
- **Pond**: Fish pond (catfish/bluegill) on property eliminates need to find fishing locations

### Rural Priority Additions

**Asparagus**: Plant once, harvest for 20+ years. 100 sq ft = 30–50 lbs/year after Year 3. Plant in permanent bed, never till.

**Fruit trees**: Apple, pear, plum, pawpaw, mulberry. 5+ years to production but incredibly productive once established. Pawpaw requires no spray, pest-resistant, Zone 5 native.

**Beekeeping**: 2 hives provide 50–100 lbs honey/year (sweetener + medicine), plus pollination boost to all garden crops. Initial cost: $500; ongoing minimal.

**Maple syrup**: If sugar maples exist on property: 40 gallons sap → 1 gallon syrup; one mature tree yields 10–15 gallons sap/season. Non-electric tap and bucket system: $100–200.

## Suburban Track Variations

### Constraints
- Typical suburban lot: 0.1–0.3 acres total; garden space often 1,000–3,000 sq ft after house/garage/lawn
- HOA restrictions on height, appearance of structures (may prohibit trellises, certain structures)
- No hunting from property; must travel to public land or negotiate access
- Limited root cellar options (use basement corner strategy above)

### Maximizing Suburban Food Production

**Every square foot counts**:
- Convert front lawn to food garden (increasingly accepted; "food forest" landscaping)
- Vertical growing: cucumbers, beans, tomatoes up trellises on fence lines
- Container growing: Sweet potatoes, tomatoes, herbs in 5-gallon buckets on patio/deck
- South-facing wall: plant espaliered fruit trees against house wall (Zone 5 heat sink extends season)

**High-calorie options in small space**:
- 100 sq ft of dried beans: 60 lbs dried = 90,000 calories — largest caloric return per square foot
- 10-gallon potato grow bags: 10 bags × 10 lbs = 100 lbs; stacked on patio
- Dwarf apple trees: semi-dwarf on M-7 rootstock; 4–6 ft diameter; 40–80 lbs/year in 3 years

**Community lot sharing**: Suburban blocks often have underused lawns. Trading garden use for food share is a straightforward arrangement. One willing neighbor's 2,000 sq ft lawn = significant production boost.

**Suburban hunting**: 
- Most Midwest states allow hunting within town limits with landowner permission and appropriate regulations
- Deer in suburban/exurban edges are often abundant and accessible (Illinois, Indiana, Ohio specifically)
- Fishing: most suburbs have retention ponds (stocked by HOA or naturally), city parks with fishing access, rivers within driving distance

## Midwest-Specific Notes

### Soil Reality
Midwest soils range from excellent (deep topsoil over glacial till in Iowa/Illinois) to challenging (clay-heavy with poor drainage in many areas). Before relying on a garden:

- **Soil test**: University of Illinois Extension soil tests ($20); tells you pH, macronutrients, OM%
- **Target**: pH 6.0–7.0 for most vegetables; potatoes prefer 5.5–6.5
- **Common Midwest problems**: High clay (add compost + sand), low organic matter (add compost), occasional high pH from limestone subsoil (add sulfur, use acidic mulches)
- **Amendment**: 4 inches of compost tilled in → dramatically improves any Midwest soil in Year 1

### Pest Reality
Midwest pest pressure can devastate gardens. Key pests and organic solutions:

| Pest | Targets | Control |
|---|---|---|
| Colorado potato beetle | Potatoes | Hand pick; neem oil; row cover early season |
| Squash vine borer | Squash, zucchini | Row cover until flower; bury vines to root; Butternut somewhat resistant |
| Aphids | Everything | Strong water spray; introduce ladybugs; insecticidal soap |
| Japanese beetle | Beans, roses, corn tassels | Hand pick into soapy water; kaolin clay |
| Deer | Everything | 8-ft fence is only reliable solution |
| Raccoon | Corn | Electric fence; most important for corn |
| Cutworms | Transplants | Cardboard collar around stem base |

### Extended Growing Season Tools

**Frost protection** extends both ends of the season (adding 4–8 weeks):
- Row cover fabric (Agribon 19 or similar): $20–30 for 25 ft; good to 28°F
- Cold frames: single glazed frame on south-facing slope; grow greens October–April
- Low tunnels: hoops + plastic sheeting over beds; adds 4–6°F; grow March–May and September–November
- High tunnel (hoop house): 12×24 ft structure; unheated but extends to year-round production in Zone 5; cost $500–1,500 materials
