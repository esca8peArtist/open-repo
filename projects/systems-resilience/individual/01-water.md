## Day 1–3: Immediate Survival

### How Much Water You Need
- **Bare minimum (survival only)**: 1 gallon/person/day (drinking + minimal cooking)
- **Basic sanitation**: 2–3 gallons/person/day
- **Comfortable with hygiene**: 5–7 gallons/person/day
- **Garden irrigation**: Additional 0.5–1 gallon/sq ft/week during growing season

### What You Already Have
Before any emergency water sourcing, inventory what's available right now:

| Source | Approximate Volume | Treatment Needed |
|---|---|---|
| Water heater tank | 30–80 gallons | None if undisturbed |
| Toilet tank (not bowl) | 1–3 gallons each | None |
| Ice in freezer | Variable | Melt, no treatment |
| Canned goods liquid | Variable | None |
| Water pipes (drain to lowest faucet after shutoff) | 5–15 gallons | None |

**Action**: Before city water shuts off, fill every container you have — bathtubs, pots, buckets, trash cans lined with trash bags.

**WaterBOB**: A bathtub bladder that holds 100 gallons cleanly (~$30, store in emergency kit). Fill as soon as warning of disruption.

### Finding Surface Water in the Midwest
In a failure scenario, Midwest water sources in order of quality:

| Source | Quality | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Groundwater spring | Excellent | Rare in flat Midwest; find by watching where green stays green in drought |
| Deep well (pre-existing) | Excellent | Only if you have a hand pump (electric pump won't work without power) |
| Moving stream | Good | Better upstream of agriculture; still requires treatment |
| Pond/lake | Fair | Higher biological and chemical load; treat aggressively |
| Roadside ditch | Poor | High ag chemical and nitrate contamination — last resort |
| Flood water | Very poor | Sewage + chemical contamination — treat aggressively |

**Critical Midwest warning**: Agricultural runoff carries nitrates (from fertilizer) and pesticides. Filtering and boiling does NOT remove nitrates. A stream running through cropland is a chemical risk, not just biological. Use groundwater or treat nitrate contamination with distillation or reverse osmosis if RO unit is available.

### Emergency Treatment: The Three-Step Method
For any uncertain surface water:

**Step 1 — Pre-filter (removes sediment, extends filter life)**
- Cloth (cotton t-shirt, bandana) — removes large particles
- Improvised sand filter (gravel → sand → charcoal in a container) — removes finer particles
- Commercial: Sawyer Squeeze, LifeStraw ($20–40) — removes bacteria/protozoa but NOT viruses or chemicals

**Step 2 — Disinfect (kills biological threats)**
- Boiling: 1 minute rolling boil at Midwest elevation (no altitude adjustment needed)
- Chemical: 8 drops of 6% sodium hypochlorite bleach per gallon; let stand 30 min (60 min if cold or cloudy)
- Chemical: 2 chlorine dioxide tablets per liter — most effective, kills Cryptosporidium that iodine misses; wait 4 hours
- UV: SteriPen UV wand ($50–80) — fast and effective, requires batteries

**Step 3 — Store properly**
- Food-grade containers only (HDPE #2 marked with recycling symbol)
- Dark location (UV degrades stored water and kills any residual disinfectant)
- Rotate treated water every 6 months
- Never store in milk jugs (protein residue grows bacteria)

## Week 1–4: Stabilization

### Rainwater Collection

**The math (Midwest Zone 5):**
- Annual average rainfall: 35–42 inches (varies: southern Indiana gets more; western Minnesota less)
- Seasonal distribution: ~50% falls May–September; very dry December–February
- Catchment formula: `Gallons = Roof_sq_ft × Rainfall_inches × 0.623 × 0.85`
  - 0.623 converts inches to gallons per sq ft
  - 0.85 accounts for ~15% loss to evaporation and roof texture

**Example**: 1,000 sq ft of roof, 3-inch May rain event:
`1,000 × 3 × 0.623 × 0.85 = 1,589 gallons from one rain event`

**Basic suburban setup:**
```
Gutter → Diverter → Downspout → First-flush diverter → Storage tank
                                        ↓
                               First ~10 gallons flush to ground
                               (removes bird droppings, debris)
```

**Minimum collection system:**
- Two 55-gallon food-grade plastic drums: ~$30–50 each (often free from food/drink industry)
- Connector hose between drums: $5
- First-flush diverter: $15 DIY or $30 commercial
- Screen over inlet (prevents mosquito breeding): critical
- Total cost: ~$80–150

**Suburban legality note**: Most Midwest states have no rainwater collection restrictions. Illinois, Indiana, Iowa, Ohio, Wisconsin — all legal. Check local ordinances for volume limits (rare but possible under HOA rules).

**Winter problem**: Midwest winters freeze outdoor tanks. Move collection system indoors (basement) or drain and cover October–April. Supplement with snowmelt (pack snow in buckets, let melt at room temperature — treat before drinking).

### Interim Filtration Setup
While building long-term infrastructure, a gravity-fed ceramic filter handles 1–5 gallons/hour:

**Berkey-style gravity filter (DIY):**
- Two food-grade 5-gallon buckets ($5 each)
- Two ceramic or carbon filter elements ($30–60 for pair)
- Drill: 3/4" hole in bottom of upper bucket, spigot hole in lower bucket
- Assembly: Stack buckets; water pours through ceramic from top to bottom
- Flow rate: ~1–3 gallons/hour depending on filter element

Commercial options: Big Berkey ($250), Alexapure ($200) — worth it if available.

**Slow sand filter (fully DIY, long-term):**
A biological filter that removes 95-99% of pathogens through a microbial "schmutzedecke" layer that forms on top.
- Container: 55-gallon drum or concrete box
- Layers (bottom to top): gravel (4"), coarse sand (2"), fine sand (18"), biological layer (develops over 2–4 weeks)
- Flow rate: 0.04–0.12 gallons/sq ft/hour (slower = more effective)
- Startup: 2–4 weeks to develop biological layer; test water quality before drinking
- Source: WHO Slow Sand Filtration guide (see Sources section)

## Month 1–6: Building Systems

### If You Have an Existing Well: Adding a Hand Pump

Most Midwest residential wells are drilled wells, 4" or 6" diameter casing, 80–300 ft deep. An electric submersible pump sits at the bottom and fails without grid power.

**The solution**: A hand pump that fits inside the same well casing alongside the submersible pump.

**Two reliable options for Midwest well depths:**

| Pump | Max Depth | Cost | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Simple Pump** | 325 ft | $1,500–2,200 installed | Most reliable for deep Midwest wells; retrofit fits 4" and 6" casing |
| **Bison Pump** | 250 ft | $1,200–1,800 | Similar quality; compatible with submersible pump in same casing |
| **Baker Monitor** | 200 ft | $800–1,200 | Older design, still made; reliable cast iron construction |
| **PVC pitcher pump** | 25 ft (suction limit) | $30–80 DIY | Shallow wells only; loses prime frequently; backup option |

**Hand pump installation basics** (Simple Pump / Bison):
1. Measure well depth and static water level (string + weight, or well log from drilling company)
2. Pump cylinder must be below static water level
3. Drop pipe (1" galvanized or stainless) connects surface handle mechanism to subsurface cylinder
4. Pump head replaces or sits adjacent to existing pressure tank
5. Installation: 2–4 hours with basic tools; one person can do it with manual

**Critical**: Get your well log before purchasing. The well log shows depth, casing diameter, static water level, and yield (GPM). If you don't have it, contact your county health department — Midwest states maintain well records.

**Output rate**: Simple Pump at 200 ft depth = ~2 gallons/minute with steady pumping. For one person needing 3 gallons/day, that's 90 seconds of pumping.

### If You Do NOT Have a Well: Drilling Options

**Option A: Hire a driller** (recommended for unknown geology or depth >60 ft)
- Cost: $15–30/linear foot + casing ($8–15/ft) + development + pump
- Midwest average well: 100–200 ft; total cost $5,000–15,000
- Timeline: Schedule 2–6 weeks out in busy season
- Verify: Licensed driller, pull permit, get well log on completion
- Find drillers: National Ground Water Association (ngwa.org) member search

**Option B: Driven point well** (sandy/gravel soils only, max 25–30 ft depth)
A steel pointed screen screwed onto steel pipe sections, driven into the ground.

Materials:
- Drive point (1¼" or 2") with screen: $30–50
- 5-ft galvanized steel pipe sections (1¼" or 2"): $15–20 each
- Pipe couplings: $3–5 each
- Drive cap to protect threads: $10
- Pitcher pump or pitcher pump + 30-ft drop line: $40–80
- Total for 20-ft shallow well: ~$200–300

Process:
1. Identify location with sandy/gravelly soil (glacial outwash areas in upper Midwest: central/northern Illinois, Indiana, Wisconsin, Minnesota common)
2. Screw drive point onto first pipe section
3. Drive with fence post driver, sledgehammer, or tripod-and-rope setup
4. Add pipe sections as you go (use pipe thread sealant at each joint)
5. Test: pour water in top, listen for it to drain into aquifer (not drain back fast = stuck in clay)
6. Once at water-bearing layer: water rises and stays in pipe
7. Attach pitcher pump to top

**Works best in**: Northern Indiana, Illinois Mississippi River corridor, Wisconsin river bottoms, Minnesota glacial outwash plains.
**Does NOT work in**: Clay-heavy soils (most of Iowa's glacial till), solid rock (rare in Midwest flatlands).

**Option C: Jetted well** (soft soil, up to 60–100 ft)
Uses water pressure to wash a hole into the ground while inserting casing.

Requires: Water pump (min 4 GPM @ 40 PSI), water source, 1¼" well point, sections of pipe
More complex than driven point but reaches deeper. Covered in detail in: Howard Penrose's "Water Well Handbook" (see Sources).

**Option D: Hand-dug well** (last resort, water table <20 ft)
Labor intensive (days to weeks), requires shoring, unsafe without proper technique.
Best resource: "How to Make a Cement Water Filter or Well" by VITA (Volunteers in Technical Assistance) — public domain.

### Water Storage

**Target**: 2-week minimum supply (42 gallons for one person at 3 gal/day).

| Container | Volume | Cost | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 7-gallon Aquatainer | 7 gal | $20 | Stackable, portable, food-grade |
| 55-gallon drum | 55 gal | $30–50 | Best value; heavy when full (460 lbs) |
| 275-gallon IBC tote | 275 gal | $100–200 used | Excellent for long-term; stackable |
| 500-gallon poly tank | 500 gal | $400–600 | Requires cistern setup or basement |

## Year 1+: Self-Sufficiency

### Complete Individual Water System

At full operation, one person's water system:

```
[Rainfall]
    ↓
[Roof collection: 1,000 sq ft]
    ↓
[First-flush diverter]
    ↓
[2,000-gallon underground cistern]

    ↓
[Hand pump (Simple Pump)]
    ↓
[50-gallon pressure tank]
    ↓
[Garden irrigation + sanitation use: ~2 gal/day]
```

Total daily pumping effort for one person: ~5 minutes.

### Annual Maintenance Schedule

| Month | Task |
|---|---|
| March | Test stored water; begin rainwater collection restart |
| April | Test well water (bacteria + nitrates) — spring runoff risk |
| June | Clean first-flush diverter; inspect gutters |
| September | Last major canning-water pressure use before harvest |
| October | Drain outdoor collection system before freeze |
| December | Test stored water quality; refill any depleted tanks |

### Water Quality Testing

**Annual tests for Midwest well water:**

| Test | Why | Lab Cost | DIY Test |
|---|---|---|---|
| Total coliform + E. coli | Bacterial contamination from surface water | $25–40 | Yes (home kits $10–15) |
| Nitrates | Ag runoff (risk in corn/soy belt) | $20–30 | Yes ($10 strips, less accurate) |
| pH | Affects plumbing and taste | $10 | Yes (pool test strips) |
| Hardness | Scaling in pipes/filters | $10 | Yes (strips) |
| Iron | Common in Midwest glacial aquifers | $20 | Smell/color tell-tale |
| Arsenic | Natural in some Midwest geology | $30–50 | Lab only |

**Submit samples to**: State-certified lab (find via state health department). In Illinois: Illinois Environmental Laboratory Certification Program. In Iowa: State Hygienic Laboratory at University of Iowa.

**Nitrate action levels**: >10 mg/L is EPA limit; dangerous for infants and pregnant women. In Midwest agricultural areas, spring readings often exceed this. Fix: reverse osmosis unit, distillation, or haul water from unaffected source.

## Rural Track Variations

**Advantages:**
- Can drill a proper well (no HOA, adequate lot size, septic setback rules easier to manage)
- Can build underground cistern and large above-ground storage
- Surface water often accessible (ponds, streams, intermittent creeks)
- Legal rainwater collection at any scale

**Additional rural water options:**
- **Farm pond**: If you have or can dig a 1/4-acre pond, it holds ~1–2 million gallons. Treat before drinking; excellent for irrigation.
- **Artesian well**: Some Midwest areas have artesian pressure (water rises on its own). Check state geological survey maps.
- **Spring development**: If a spring exists on property, develop it: concrete spring box, overflow pipe, gravity-feed line to house. Zero pumping energy.

**Well setback rules (Midwest typical)**:
- 50 ft from septic tank; 100 ft from drain field
- 100 ft from any chemical storage
- Check county/state well code before siting

## Suburban Track Variations

**Constraints:**
- No well drilling possible on typical suburban lot (setback from septic impossible; most suburbs on municipal sewer but well still can't be within 50 ft of property line in many codes)
- Limited roof surface area (typical suburban: 1,200–2,000 sq ft)
- HOA may restrict visible storage tanks
- Water heater + pipes as primary emergency reserve

**Suburban-specific strategy:**

**Tier 1 (Always ready, no infrastructure):**
- WaterBOB in primary bathroom ($30) — fill during warning, 100 gallons
- 12 cases of bottled water rotated every 6 months (~$50/year)
- LifeStraw or Sawyer Squeeze for any outdoor water use

**Tier 2 (Some setup, ~$200–400):**
- Rainwater collection from 2 downspouts: 2 × 55-gallon drums = 110 gallons
- Discreet location: behind garage, screened by landscaping for HOA compliance
- Berkey gravity filter for treatment (~$200–250)
- Total: ~400 gallons on-hand at all times + collection capacity

**Tier 3 (Significant infrastructure, ~$500–1,500):**
- 275-gallon IBC tote in garage or basement (~$150 used) fed from downspout
- Basement slow-sand filter setup
- 5-stage reverse osmosis unit ($150–300) for chemical/nitrate removal
- Total: 300–500 gallons stored + ongoing collection

**Emergency surface water in suburban Midwest:**
- Most Midwest suburbs are within 2 miles of a creek, river, or retention pond
- Retention ponds: poor water quality (lawn chemicals, geese), treat aggressively
- Rivers/creeks: better quality upstream; still treat — city pipes can break contaminating storm drains
- Rain: collect directly from gutters but divert first 10–15 minutes (roof debris)

**Know your nearest water sources** before you need them. Map them now:
- Google Maps "lakes near [your address]"
- USGS National Map (apps.nationalmap.gov) shows streams and water bodies

## Midwest-Specific Notes

### Seasonal Challenges

| Season | Challenge | Solution |
|---|---|---|
| Winter (Dec–Feb) | Ground frozen, pipes freeze, rainwater collection impossible | Pre-store October; insulate outdoor pipes; use basement cistern |
| Spring (Mar–May) | High runoff = contaminated surface water; well risk from flooding | Test well water April; use stored reserves during flooding |
| Summer (Jun–Aug) | Drought periods; evaporation; irrigation demand peaks | Maximize storage before summer; mulch garden to reduce water need |
| Fall (Sep–Nov) | Best collection season; prep for winter | Fill all storage before freeze; drain outdoor collection |

### Aquifer Geography

**Best groundwater areas of Midwest:**
- Northern Illinois / Indiana: Cambrian-Ordovician aquifer system, 200–400 ft, excellent quality
- Wisconsin: glacial aquifers near river systems, 80–200 ft
- Minnesota: Prairie du Chien and Jordan aquifers in southeast, 200–500 ft
- Iowa: Des Moines Lobe glacial drift, shallow but often high nitrates; go deeper for better quality
- Ohio: Buried valley aquifer system along major river corridors

**Nitrate hotspots**: Central Iowa, northern Illinois, and Indiana corn belt — agricultural tile drainage sends nitrates directly to streams and shallow groundwater. Always test.

### Emergency Water in Extreme Events

**Tornado aftermath**: Lines may rupture; boil order typically follows. Have 72-hour supply sealed before storm season. Keep 5-gallon sealed container in interior room/basement.

**Ice storm / polar vortex**: Pipes freeze and burst. Shut off main at meter if leaving for extended period. Thaw pipes with hair dryer (never open flame). Know location of main shutoff.

**Flood**: Stop using municipal water until boil order lifted — broken mains can allow sewage intrusion. Switch to stored reserves immediately.

## Step-by-Step Procedures

### Procedure 1: Treating Contaminated Water (30 minutes)

1. Fill container with source water
2. Pre-filter: pour through folded cotton cloth to remove sediment
3. If still cloudy: improvised filter (gravel → sand → cloth layers in bucket with hole)
4. Treat with bleach: add 8 drops of 6% bleach per gallon using eyedropper; stir
5. Wait 30 minutes (60 minutes if water is cold or still slightly cloudy)
6. Smell: should have slight chlorine smell — if not, repeat bleach step and wait 15 more minutes
7. Pour between two containers to aerate and improve taste
8. Store in sealed container, label with date

### Procedure 2: Building a Slow Sand Filter (2–4 hours + 4 weeks curing)

Materials: 55-gallon drum or large trash can, gravel (1/2–1"), coarse sand, fine play sand, spigot kit

1. Drill 3/4" hole at bottom of drum, install spigot
2. Add 4" of gravel at bottom (washed)
3. Add 2" of coarse sand
4. Add 18" of fine sand (washed — rinse until runoff is clear)
5. Add 1" of very fine sand or crushed charcoal at top (optional — improves initial quality)
6. Fill with source water to 4" above sand — let drain slowly (1–2 gal/hour)
7. Do NOT rush first 4 weeks — biological layer (schmutzedecke) must develop
8. After 4 weeks: water quality improves dramatically — should pass bacteria test
9. Maintain by never letting top layer dry out; add water from top gently to avoid disturbing surface

### Procedure 3: Installing a Driven Point Well (2–4 hours if soil cooperates)

Materials: Drive point (2"), 5-ft pipe sections (2"), drive cap, pipe thread sealant, pitcher pump

1. Identify site: 50+ ft from any septic, 100 ft from any chemical storage
2. Test soil: dig 2-ft test hole — if clay-only, driven point will not work here
3. Thread first pipe section onto drive point; apply pipe thread sealant
4. Stand pipe upright; begin driving with fence post driver or 10-lb sledgehammer on drive cap
5. Every 12–18 inches of depth: check alignment with level
6. Every 5 ft: add new pipe section; apply sealant at joint, thread coupling tight with pipe wrench
7. Indicator of water-bearing layer: resistance suddenly changes; pour water in top — if it drains slowly and rises back up, you've hit saturated zone
8. Drive 2–3 ft into water-bearing layer
9. Attach pitcher pump to top
10. Prime pump: pour water into top of pump body; begin pumping — may take 5–10 minutes of continuous pumping to establish suction
11. Pump until water runs clear before using
12. TEST BEFORE DRINKING: submit water sample to lab
