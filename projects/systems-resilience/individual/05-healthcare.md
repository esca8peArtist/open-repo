## Quick Reference Card

**Midwest health threat hierarchy — ranked by lethality without professional care:**

| Threat | Kills in | Warning signs | First action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Uncontrolled hemorrhage | Minutes | Blood pooling, pale/cold/confused | Tourniquet or direct pressure immediately |
| Airway obstruction | 3–5 minutes | Cannot speak, cyanosis, clutching throat | Heimlich / head-tilt chin-lift |
| Anaphylaxis | Minutes–1 hour | Hives + throat tightening + falling BP | Epinephrine autoinjector + lay flat |
| Septic shock | Hours–days | High fever + confusion + low BP | IV fluid + broad-spectrum antibiotics + evacuate |
| Heat stroke (June–Aug Midwest) | Hours | Confusion + hot dry skin + no sweating | Cold water immersion — core temp to 38.9°C |
| Severe hypothermia (Nov–Mar) | Hours | Shivering stops + confusion + weak pulse | Insulate + active rewarming — no rough movement |
| Cardiac event (MI/stroke) | Minutes–hours | Chest pain/jaw/arm + sweating; or FAST signs | Aspirin 324 mg for MI; evacuate both immediately |
| Tick-borne illness (Lyme, RMSF) | Days–weeks if untreated | Bull's-eye rash (Lyme) or spotted fever | Doxycycline 100 mg BID immediately — do not wait for lab |
| Dental abscess spreading | Days | Jaw swelling, trismus, fever | Amoxicillin + I&D if fluctuant; evacuate if neck swelling |
| Appendicitis | Days | RLQ pain migrating from navel + fever | Antibiotics trial; evacuate if rigid abdomen |

**Primary principle**: The goal is never to replace a physician. The goal is to prevent the 80% of emergencies that become fatal only because of delayed care and to buy time for evacuation in the 20% that genuinely require a hospital.

**The most powerful medical intervention available**: Clean water and sanitation. An ounce of prevention is the accurate ratio at which infectious disease operates in resource-limited environments.

## Day 1–3: Immediate Survival

### The MARCH Protocol — Your Decision Framework

When someone is injured or suddenly ill, work through this sequence every time:

1. **Massive hemorrhage** — Is there uncontrolled bleeding? Address first. Everything else waits.
2. **Airway** — Is the airway open? If unconscious and not breathing normally, open the airway.
3. **Respiration** — Are they breathing? Seal any open chest wounds. Assess effort.
4. **Circulation** — Signs of shock? Lay flat, elevate legs, conserve warmth, start fluids if available.
5. **Hypothermia/Hypoxia** — Protect from cold. Oxygen if available.

### Hemorrhage Control — The 5-Minute Response

**Tourniquet (extremity wounds with arterial bleeding):**
1. Apply 2–3 inches (5–7 cm) above the wound — not on the joint
2. Tighten the windlass until bleeding stops AND distal pulse is absent
3. Write the time of application on the tourniquet or on the skin with marker
4. Do NOT loosen — ever — once applied for arterial bleeding
5. Maximum 2 hours before muscle damage begins; note time and communicate it to any receiving care
6. CAT tourniquet (Combat Application Tourniquet) Gen 7: $30–35 at North American Rescue. Buy genuine; counterfeits fail under pressure.

**Direct pressure (wounds where tourniquet won't fit — neck, groin, axilla):**
1. Pack hemostatic gauze (QuikClot, Celox) directly onto the bleeding point — not just into the cavity
2. Apply firm, deliberate pressure for 3–5 minutes minimum; set a timer; do not lift to check
3. Maintain pressure — add more gauze on top if soaking through; do not remove the first layer

**Wound irrigation (after bleeding is controlled):**
- This is the most important infection-prevention step: 1 liter of clean water through an 18-gauge needle on a 35–60 mL syringe, producing 8 PSI pressure
- Improvised: pinhole in a 1-liter water bottle produces adequate pressure
- Irrigate before any closure attempt

### Airway Emergencies

**Unconscious adult (not breathing normally):**
1. Head-tilt chin-lift (if no spinal injury suspected): tilt head back, lift chin
2. Jaw thrust (if spinal injury possible): fingers behind angle of jaw, thrust forward without moving neck
3. Look, listen, feel for breath — 10 seconds
4. No breathing: begin CPR — 30 chest compressions to 2 rescue breaths; compress 2–2.5 inches deep; 100–120/min rate
5. Continue until breathing resumes, AED arrives, or you cannot continue

**Choking — conscious adult:**
- Stand behind, lean them forward, give 5 back blows with heel of hand between shoulder blades
- If not cleared: 5 abdominal thrusts (Heimlich) — make fist below sternum, sharp inward-upward thrust
- Alternate back blows and abdominal thrusts until cleared or unconscious
- Unconscious: lower to floor; attempt 30 compressions (may dislodge object); check mouth; attempt breath

### Anaphylaxis — Minutes to Act

**Recognition:** Hives or skin flushing + ANY of: throat tightening, difficulty breathing, falling blood pressure, vomiting after bee sting or food exposure.

**Treatment sequence:**
1. Epinephrine autoinjector (EpiPen or generic): outer mid-thigh, outer clothing is fine, hold 10 seconds; repeat in 5–10 minutes if no improvement
2. Lay patient flat — sitting upright drops blood pressure further; if airway compromised, slight elevation is acceptable
3. Diphenhydramine (Benadryl) 50 mg orally — does NOT replace epinephrine; addresses histamine component
4. If breathing, keep flat 4–6 hours; biphasic reactions (second wave) occur in up to 20% of cases
5. Evacuate regardless of apparent recovery — biphasic risk and potential rebound

**Epinephrine storage:** Autoinjectors expire; check annually. GoodRx and Walmart generics: $35–100/2-pack with prescription. Store at room temperature, away from light. If you have a known allergy, carry two doses minimum.

### Shock Recognition and Response

**Signs of compensated shock (body still maintaining BP):**
- Anxiety, restlessness, or altered mentation — this is the earliest sign
- Heart rate >100 bpm
- Pale, cool, clammy skin
- Capillary refill >2 seconds (press fingernail, release, count to color return)

**Radial pulse rule:** If you can feel the radial pulse at the wrist, systolic BP is roughly >80 mmHg. No radial pulse = systolic likely <80 = decompensating shock.

**Response:**
1. Control the source (hemorrhage, anaphylaxis, sepsis)
2. Lay flat; elevate legs 6–12 inches if no spinal injury suspected
3. Insulate from cold — hypothermia worsens coagulopathy (the "lethal triad" of cold + acidosis + coagulopathy)
4. IV fluids if available: 500 mL–1 L NS or LR, then reassess; avoid aggressive over-resuscitation
5. Oral fluids ONLY if patient is conscious, not nauseated, and not potentially requiring surgery

### Heat Emergencies — Midwest Summer Priority

Zone 5 Midwest heat and humidity create life-threatening conditions in July and August. Heat stroke kills by causing coagulopathy, liver failure, and brain injury when core temperature exceeds 40°C (104°F).

**Heat exhaustion (core temp <40°C, mental status normal):**
- Move to shade or cool interior immediately
- Remove outer clothing
- Cool with whatever is available: ice packs to neck, armpits, groin; wet towels with fanning
- Oral rehydration: ORS or water with salt (0.5 tsp salt per liter) — NOT plain water alone in quantity
- Rest 24–48 hours in cool environment

**Heat stroke (core temp >40°C OR altered mental status regardless of temperature):**
- Medical emergency: mortality rises steeply with time above 40°C
- Definitive treatment: cold water immersion — fill bathtub, cattle trough, or any large container with cold/ice water; immerse torso and limbs to neck
- Target rectal temperature 38.9°C (102°F) before removing from water — do not overshoot hypothermia
- If immersion impossible: cold wet towels to neck/armpits/groin + aggressive fanning + ice packs
- DO NOT give aspirin or acetaminophen — ineffective for heat stroke and adds hepatic stress
- Oral fluids only if fully conscious; IV access preferred
- Evacuate after stabilization: organ damage monitoring requires hospital care [^1]

### Hypothermia — Midwest Winter Priority

Hypothermia staging by shivering:

| Stage | Core temp | Signs | Treatment |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mild | 32–35°C (90–95°F) | Shivering, clumsiness, confusion | Insulate, remove wet clothing, warm environment |
| Moderate | 28–32°C (82–90°F) | Shivering stops, increasing confusion | Insulate + active warming; no rough movement |
| Severe | <28°C (<82°F) | Unconscious, no shivering, appears dead | Active rewarming; "not dead until warm and dead" — begin CPR if pulseless |

**The critical rule for moderate/severe hypothermia:** Handle extremely gently. The heart is irritable and prone to ventricular fibrillation from movement, pressure, or rough handling. Do not jostle; do not make them stand; do not rub extremities vigorously.

**Field rewarming:**
- Warm the core, not the periphery — applying heat to cold limbs sends cold blood to the heart
- Core warming: warm packs to neck, armpits, and groin only; warm sweet fluids if fully conscious
- Insulate entire body including head (40% of heat loss is from head) with sleeping bags, blankets, vapor barrier
- Remove wet clothing carefully while replacing immediately with dry insulation
- If severely hypothermic and pulseless: begin CPR and transport — field rewarming of severe hypothermia requires hospital capability [^2]

## Week 1–4: Stabilization

### Infection Management — The Antibiotic Framework

In the first weeks without professional support, bacterial infection is your primary adversary. Viral illness resolves without antibiotics; bacterial infection can kill.

**Diagnosing bacterial vs. viral (clinical distinction without labs):**

| Feature | Suggests Bacterial | Suggests Viral |
|---|---|---|
| Fever duration | >5 days | <5 days, self-resolving |
| Fever height | >39°C | Typically 37.5–39°C |
| Sputum or discharge | Yellow/green/purulent | Clear, watery, or white |
| Severity progression | Worsening after day 3–5 | Improving after day 3–5 |
| Focal symptoms | Focal (one area) | Diffuse |
| WBC if measurable | Elevated (>12,000) | Normal or slightly elevated |

**Empiric antibiotic selection — what to use when:**

| Condition | First choice | Alternative | Duration | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Skin/cellulitis | Cephalexin 500 mg QID | Clindamycin 300 mg TID | 5–7 days | For MRSA risk: add TMP-SMX |
| Wound infection | Amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 BID | Clindamycin 300 mg QID | 5–7 days | |
| Pneumonia (bacterial) | Amoxicillin 1g TID | Azithromycin 500 mg day 1 then 250 mg | 5 days | Evacuate if pulse ox <93% |
| UTI (uncomplicated) | TMP-SMX 160/800 mg BID | Nitrofurantoin 100 mg BID | 3–5 days | |
| Pyelonephritis | Ciprofloxacin 500 mg BID | TMP-SMX 160/800 mg BID | 14 days | Evacuate if vomiting |
| Tick-borne (Lyme/RMSF) | Doxycycline 100 mg BID | Amoxicillin 500 mg TID (Lyme only) | 14–21 days | Start empirically; do not wait for lab |
| Dental abscess | Amoxicillin 500 mg TID | Clindamycin 300 mg TID | 5–7 days | Plus I&D if fluctuant |
| Appendicitis (trial) | Amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 BID | Cefdinir 300 mg BID + metronidazole 500 mg TID | 10 days | Only if no peritoneal signs |
| Giardia | Metronidazole 500 mg TID | Tinidazole 2g single dose | 5 days (metro) | From contaminated water |
| Scabies | Permethrin 5% cream (apply, 8hr, wash) | Ivermectin 200 mcg/kg oral | 2 applications 1 week apart | All linens hot wash |

**Fish antibiotics — practical reality:** Amoxicillin, doxycycline, cephalexin, and azithromycin sold for aquarium use are manufactured in pharmaceutical-grade facilities. A 2018 study in *Clinical Infectious Diseases* confirmed they contain the same active pharmaceutical ingredients as human-labeled equivalents. They are legal to purchase without prescription and reasonable for use in genuine austere scenarios where no alternative exists. Source: Tractor Supply, Amazon. Store cool and dry; 3+ year shelf life in original containers.

**What NOT to use antibiotics for:** Viral respiratory illness, most sore throats, most ear infections in adults, the common cold. Antibiotic resistance is caused by misuse; conserve your supply for genuine bacterial infections.

### Fever Management

Fever is a feature, not a disease. Do not reflexively suppress every fever — temperatures 38–39°C inhibit bacterial and viral replication and activate immune responses.

**Treat fever when:**
- >39.5°C in adults (comfort and risk of febrile seizure at extremes)
- Causing inability to drink fluids (dehydration risk)
- Underlying cardiac or seizure history
- Persistent without improvement at day 5

**Treatment protocol:**
- Ibuprofen 400–600 mg + acetaminophen 500 mg simultaneously every 6 hours — different mechanisms, safe to combine, better total effect than either alone
- Alternating: acetaminophen every 4 hours, ibuprofen every 6 hours, staggered at 2–3 hour intervals
- Tepid (not cold) sponge bath if >40°C and medications not reducing fever within 45 minutes

**When fever signals evacuation:** Fever + stiff neck + photophobia = possible meningitis — evacuate immediately. Fever + confusion + no sweating in heat = heat stroke. Fever + right lower quadrant tenderness = appendicitis until proven otherwise.

### Dehydration and Oral Rehydration

Diarrheal illness is the most common cause of life-threatening dehydration when water sanitation is compromised.

**WHO Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS) — make at home:**
- 1 liter clean water
- 6 level teaspoons sugar (36g)
- 0.5 level teaspoon salt (3g)
- Optional: squeeze of citrus for potassium
This is the WHO standard formulation. [^3]

**Severity guide:**
- Mild: thirst, slightly decreased urine, normal alertness → drink ORS 50 mL/kg over 4 hours
- Moderate: dry mouth, sunken eyes, slow skin return when pinched, dark urine → same volume ORS; if vomiting, give 5–10 mL every 2 minutes
- Severe: confusion, no urine output, unable to drink → IV access if available; 500 mL NS rapid; this is an evacuation case

**Diarrhea specific:** Loperamide (Imodium) 4 mg initially then 2 mg per loose stool (max 16 mg/day). Critical: DO NOT use loperamide if bloody diarrhea — signals invasive bacteria where slowing gut motility makes the infection worse.

### Common Wounds — The Closure Decision

The decision tree for wound management:

**Close primarily now (sutures/staples/strips):** Clean wound, <6 hours old, minimal contamination, adequate blood supply
**Delayed primary closure (close in 4–5 days):** Contaminated wound, bite wound, wound >6–8 hours old, uncertain infection status — pack open, monitor daily, close when clean
**Heal by secondary intention (do not close):** Deep puncture wounds, heavily infected wounds, most animal bites — pack loosely with moist gauze; granulation fills from inside out

**Wound closure techniques:**
1. **Steri-strips**: Small, clean, low-tension wounds. Dry skin first. Apply perpendicular to wound, not over wound. Leave in place until they fall off naturally.
2. **Skin stapler**: Scalp and trunk wounds. Faster than sutures. 30-count disposable staplers: $15–25 on Amazon.
3. **Simple interrupted sutures**: Gaping wounds, wounds under tension. 3-0 Vicryl for deep layer; 4-0 nylon for skin. Enter 3–5 mm from edge; exit equidistant opposite. Instrument tie.

**Infection watch — mark with pen:** Circle the redness margin and date it. Expanding redness over 24 hours = cellulitis spreading → start antibiotics. Red streaks radiating from wound = lymphangitis = systemic spread → antibiotics immediately + evacuation if streaks reach a lymph node region.

**Tetanus:** Any wound contaminated with soil, animal waste, rust, or debris has tetanus risk. If not vaccinated within 5 years (10 years for clean minor wounds), tetanus toxoid booster is needed. Stockpile Td/Tdap vaccines. No practical field treatment for tetanus once established.

### Burns

**Identification:**
- First degree: Red, painful, no blisters — epidermis only
- Second degree (partial thickness): Blisters, wet, extremely painful — epidermis and dermis
- Third degree (full thickness): White/brown/black, leathery, painless — nerves destroyed; requires hospital

**Rule of Nines:** Head 9%, each arm 9%, chest anterior 9%, abdomen anterior 9%, upper back 9%, lower back 9%, each thigh 9%, each lower leg 9%, genitalia 1%.

**Evacuate immediately:** >10% TBSA second degree; any third degree; burns to face, hands, feet, genitalia, or airway.

**Treatment (second degree, field setting):**
1. Cool with water for 20 minutes — never ice; ice causes vasoconstriction and worsens depth
2. Do NOT apply butter, toothpaste, oil, or any food product — traps heat, introduces bacteria
3. Silvadene cream (silver sulfadiazine 1%): apply daily after gentle cleaning — best available field antimicrobial for burns; available OTC on Amazon
4. Non-adherent dressing (Telfa or petrolatum gauze); change every 1–2 days
5. Ibuprofen + acetaminophen alternating every 3 hours — burns are intensely painful
6. Monitor for infection: increasing pain at day 3–5 (burns typically improve), purulent discharge, fever

### Fractures and Sprains

**Ottawa Ankle Rules** (if ALL are met, fracture is unlikely and X-ray is not required):
- Can bear weight for 4 steps immediately and in the office/field
- No point tenderness at posterior edge or tip of either malleolus
- No point tenderness at base of 5th metatarsal or navicular

**SAM splint application:**
1. Measure against uninjured limb; bend to anatomical position (ankle at 90°, wrist neutral to 20° extension)
2. Pad bony prominences: malleoli, olecranon, radial styloid
3. Apply with elastic bandage starting at farthest point, wrapping toward body
4. Check CSM (Circulation + Sensation + Motion) at distal digits before and immediately after — if circulation impaired, loosen wrap

**Femur fracture — high-risk situation:** Femur fractures cause 1–2 liters of blood loss into the thigh. This is life-threatening from hemorrhage alone. Apply traction to restore limb length (10–15 lbs); improvise with rigid pole and ankle hitch if no commercial device available. Evacuate as priority.

**RICE for sprains:** Rest, Ice (20 minutes on/20 off, never direct contact), Compression (elastic bandage), Elevation above heart. Ibuprofen 400–600 mg every 8 hours with food.

### Prescription Medication Stockpile

**Build a 90-day supply as the minimum baseline.** Request 90-day mail-order prescriptions; most insurance plans allow this with reduced co-pay. Ask for a "vacation override" early refill when traveling.

**Chronic medications most important to stockpile:**

| Medication | Condition | Storage | Shelf life notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Albuterol inhaler × 6 | Asthma/COPD | Room temp | Check propellant level; 2-year labeled shelf life |
| Levothyroxine | Hypothyroidism | Cool, dark | Slowly degrades with heat; refrigerate if possible |
| Metformin | Type 2 diabetes | Room temp | Highly stable; well beyond labeled expiration |
| Lisinopril / amlodipine | Hypertension | Room temp | Stable |
| Sertraline / escitalopram | Depression/anxiety | Room temp | SSRI; full effect takes 4–6 weeks; stable |
| Prednisone 20 mg | Inflammation, asthma, anaphylaxis | Room temp | Taper required on any course >5 days |
| Epipen × 2 | Anaphylaxis | Room temp, away from light | Check annually; do not discard until new supply obtained |

**The SLEP reality:** The US military's Shelf Life Extension Program tested 100+ medications in original sealed containers. 88–96% retained ≥90% potency at an average of 5.5 years past labeled expiration. Many were effective at 10–15 years. Exception: liquid formulations, reconstituted suspensions, and older (pre-2000) tetracyclines degrade meaningfully. Modern doxycycline has not shown this problem. Most solid oral medications are safe and effective years beyond their stamped expiration when stored cool, dry, and dark.

**Storage rule:** Never store medications in the bathroom. Humidity accelerates degradation faster than almost anything else. Use a dedicated shelf in a cool, dark interior closet.

**Insulin without refrigeration:** Unopened insulin vials are stable at room temperature (<86°F) for 28–56 days depending on formulation. Walmart ReliOn human insulin (Novolin R and N) is sold OTC in most US states for ~$25/vial — actual pharmaceutical-grade insulin, no prescription required in most states.

### Herbal Medicine — Midwest Garden Pharmacy

A medicinal herb garden is the lowest-cost, highest-long-term-value medical investment available. These plants grow in Zone 5, provide real clinical benefit, and are self-renewing.

**The 10 most valuable medicinal plants for Zone 5 Midwest:**

**1. Yarrow (Achillea millefolium)** — Native to Midwest; grows anywhere
- *Use*: Wound bleeding (topical hemostasis), fever (diaphoretic), menstrual irregularity
- *Evidence*: Moderate (topical hemostasis); Traditional (systemic)
- *Preparation*: Fresh leaf crushed and applied directly to bleeding wound — the classic field dressing. Tea for fever: 1 Tbsp dried aerial parts steeped 10 min, 3x/day.
- *Grow*: Full sun, well-drained soil; vigorous spreader; Zone 3–9; direct sow or transplant
- *Caution*: Avoid in pregnancy (uterine stimulant). Ragweed allergy possible cross-reaction.

**2. Elderberry (Sambucus nigra / S. canadensis)**
- *Use*: Upper respiratory illness (shortened duration and severity), influenza
- *Evidence*: Moderate-Strong — multiple RCTs show 2–4 day reduction in flu duration
- *Preparation*: Syrup: 1 cup dried berries + 3 cups water + 1 cup honey, simmer 45 min, strain, refrigerate. Dose: 1 Tbsp 4x/day at illness onset. CRITICAL: cook all berries before use — raw berries cause vomiting from sambunigrin.
- *Grow*: Zone 3–9; moist soil; fast-growing shrub; plant 2 for cross-pollination; harvest second year
- *Caution*: Autoimmune conditions (immune stimulant effect).

**3. Echinacea (Echinacea purpurea)**
- *Use*: Upper respiratory immune support; reduces cold duration and severity
- *Evidence*: Moderate — reduces duration/severity of upper respiratory infections, not prevention
- *Preparation*: Tincture root 1:5 in 60% alcohol; 2–4 mL 3–5x/day at illness onset for max 10 days then rest. Or tea: 1 tsp dried root per cup, simmer 20 min.
- *Grow*: Zone 3–9; full sun; well-drained; harvest root in fall of third year
- *Caution*: Autoimmune disease; daisy family allergy.

**4. Calendula (Calendula officinalis)**
- *Use*: Wound healing, burns, rashes, fungal skin infections
- *Evidence*: Moderate (wound healing, anti-inflammatory); RCTs for radiation dermatitis
- *Preparation*: Infused oil: 1:5 dried flowers in olive oil, cold infuse 4–6 weeks. Salve: infused oil + 1 oz beeswax per cup oil, melt, pour. Apply to wound margins, burns, rashes.
- *Grow*: Annual; Zone 2–11; cool season; dead-head continuously for extended bloom
- *Caution*: Ragweed allergy cross-reaction.

**5. St. John's Wort (Hypericum perforatum)**
- *Use*: Mild-to-moderate depression, nerve pain, topical wound healing
- *Evidence*: Strong (depression — multiple meta-analyses show equivalence to SSRIs for mild/moderate)
- *Preparation*: Tincture 1:5 in 60% alcohol; 2–4 mL 3x/day; takes 4–6 weeks for antidepressant effect. Infused oil from fresh flower buds for nerve pain.
- *Grow*: Zone 5–9; full sun; well-drained; weedy in many Midwest areas; harvest flowers when buds just opening
- *CRITICAL drug interactions*: Major CYP3A4 inducer — dramatically reduces plasma levels of oral contraceptives, antiretrovirals, warfarin, digoxin, cyclosporine, many chemotherapy agents. Do NOT combine with any serotonergic medication (serotonin syndrome). Do not use if on any of these medications.

**6. Plantain (Plantago major / P. lanceolata)**
- *Use*: Insect stings, minor wounds, splinters, mild cough, UTI prevention
- *Evidence*: Moderate (wound healing); Traditional (cough and UTI)
- *Preparation*: Chew fresh leaf and apply directly to sting or bite — fastest field application. Salve from infused oil for chronic use.
- *Grow*: Already in your lawn. Weed — no cultivation required.

**7. Comfrey (Symphytum officinale / S. × uplandicum)**
- *Use*: Fractures, sprains, bruises, wound healing — EXTERNAL USE ONLY
- *Evidence*: Moderate (topical — clinical trials show accelerated healing of fractures and sprains)
- *Preparation*: Root poultice: grate fresh root, apply over intact skin on fracture/sprain, wrap; change daily. Do NOT apply to open wounds — heals surface too fast and traps infection.
- *Grow*: Zone 3–9; deep taproot; vigorous; partial shade tolerant. Use Russian comfrey (lower alkaloid content).
- *CRITICAL*: Internal use causes liver damage (pyrrolizidine alkaloids). External use only, and only on intact skin.

**8. Mullein (Verbascum thapsus)**
- *Use*: Respiratory — cough, bronchitis; ear pain (flower oil drops)
- *Evidence*: Traditional/Low; in vitro antimicrobial properties
- *Preparation*: Leaf tea: 2 Tbsp dried leaves steeped 15 min THROUGH FINE CLOTH (leaf hairs irritate throat). Flower oil: flowers in olive oil 4–6 weeks; 2–3 drops warmed oil in ear for otitis media pain.
- *Grow*: Biennial; full sun, dry poor soil; common roadside plant in Midwest; easiest to harvest wild
- *Caution*: Always strain leaf tea through fine cloth. Do not use ear drops if eardrum perforated.

**9. Valerian (Valeriana officinalis)**
- *Use*: Insomnia, anxiety, muscle spasm
- *Evidence*: Moderate (reduces sleep latency); Traditional (anxiety)
- *Preparation*: Tincture root 1:5 in 40% alcohol; 3–5 mL 30–45 min before sleep. Capsule: 400–600 mg standardized extract.
- *Grow*: Zone 4–9; moist soil; partial shade; harvest root in fall of second year
- *Caution*: Do not combine with sedative medications or alcohol.

**10. Willow bark (Salix alba / S. nigra)**
- *Use*: Pain, fever, inflammation — the original aspirin (salicin converts to salicylate)
- *Evidence*: Moderate — pain reduction comparable to low-dose aspirin
- *Preparation*: Decoction: 1–2 tsp dried bark per cup, simmer 20 min; drink 2–3 cups/day. Native black willow grows along every Midwest waterway.
- *Caution*: Same contraindications as aspirin — avoid in children under 16 (Reye's syndrome), peptic ulcers, anticoagulant therapy.

**Tincture making — standard method for any herb:**
- Ratio: 1 part dried herb to 5 parts menstruum (liquid) by weight
- Menstruum: vodka (40% ethanol) for most herbs; higher proof (60%) for roots and resins
- Fill glass jar with herb; pour menstruum to cover; seal; shake daily; steep 4–6 weeks; strain through cheesecloth; press marc (spent herb); bottle in dark glass
- Label: herb name, plant part, alcohol percentage, date
- Shelf life: 3–5 years if stored cool and dark

### Off-Grid Dental Care

Dental emergencies are among the most common and most overlooked aspects of medical preparedness. A dental abscess can spread to the mediastinum and kill within days. Toothache can be incapacitating for weeks.

**Immediate pain relief — hierarchy:**
1. **Clove oil (eugenol):** Soak a small cotton pellet; pack directly into the cavity using tweezers. This is extremely effective local anesthesia. Red Cross Toothache Medicine ($8–12) is the commercial preparation.
2. **Ibuprofen 600 mg + acetaminophen 500 mg** simultaneously every 6 hours — better than either alone for dental pain
3. **Oral benzocaine 20% gel** (Orajel Maximum Strength): apply to tissue; 15–30 minutes of surface anesthesia

**Temporary filling — Cavit method:**
1. Remove loose decay or debris with a toothpick or explorer
2. Dry cavity thoroughly with gauze or by blowing air
3. Pack Cavit (3M, $8–12 on Amazon) into the cavity
4. Patient bites gently to shape
5. Avoid chewing on that side
6. Lasts days to several weeks; replace as needed

**Dental abscess recognition and response:**
- Signs: swelling adjacent to tooth, localized pain, possible soft fluctuant center, fever, lymph node tenderness
- **Evacuate immediately if:** Swelling below the jawline; difficulty swallowing or opening mouth <2 finger widths; any stridor or drooling (Ludwig's angina = airway emergency); high fever + sick appearance
- Field treatment of fluctuant abscess (fluid-filled, soft center): topical benzocaine → infiltrate lidocaine 2% with epinephrine → stab incision at most dependent point with #11 blade → gentle spreading with hemostat → irrigate with saline → place gauze wick 24–48 hours → amoxicillin 500 mg TID or clindamycin 300 mg TID × 7 days

**Dental extraction** (tooth irreparably damaged, non-restorable, patient preference in resource-limited setting):

Required tools: Aspirating dental syringe + 27g needle + lidocaine 2% carpules, straight elevator (#34), extraction forceps #150 (upper) and #151 (lower).

Procedure for upper single-rooted tooth:
1. Infiltrate buccal and palatal mucosa with 1.8–3.6 mL lidocaine; wait 5 minutes
2. Sever gingival attachment circumferentially with elevator
3. Wedge elevator mesially; rotate to loosen
4. Apply forceps as far apically as possible; grip root, not crown
5. Gentle buccal-lingual rocking motion; molar: figure-eight rotation
6. Controlled delivery in direction of least resistance
7. Pack socket with gauze; bite pressure 30–45 minutes

**Prevention — the highest ROI in dental care:**
- Fluoride toothpaste: the single most evidence-based preventive measure; stockpile 2+ years' supply
- Xylitol (gum or mints, 6–10g/day): reduces Streptococcus mutans colonization; genuinely evidence-based
- Minimize refined sugars and dried fruit (particularly cariogenic — acidic + sticky)

## Year 1+: Self-Sufficiency

### Skills Stack — Training Over Equipment

A trained person with minimal equipment outperforms an untrained person with a fully stocked kit. Build skills before you need them.

**Tier 1 — Do these immediately (total cost: ~$200–350):**

1. **Stop the Bleed course** (free, 2 hours): American College of Surgeons program teaching tourniquet application, wound packing, and direct pressure. Find a course at [stopthebleed.org](https://www.stopthebleed.org). Train every person in your household.

2. **CPR/AED certification** ($50–100/person; 2-year renewal): Basic Life Support (BLS) for Healthcare Providers is ideal — includes bag-mask ventilation. Required baseline for every household member.

**Tier 2 — The homestead minimum (total cost: ~$700–1,100):**

3. **Wilderness First Aid (WFA)** (16–20 hours; $200–350): NOLS, SOLO, Wilderness Medical Associates all offer this. Covers assessment, stabilization, and evacuation decisions. Good starting point; not sufficient alone for extended grid-down.

4. **Dental First Aid basics** ($0–300): Community college dental assistant courses or free dental school continuing education cover temporary restoration, recognition of dental emergencies, injection technique practice. Practice on foam training pads and pig feet from a butcher shop.

**Tier 3 — The gold standard (~$850–950 additional):**

5. **Wilderness First Responder (WFR)** (70–80 hours; $825–895 through Midwest Outdoor Skills Training / NOLS in Chicago, IL): [midwestoutdoorskillstraining.com](https://www.midwestoutdoorskillstraining.com). Covers: patient assessment, wilderness trauma, medical emergencies, prolonged patient care, medication administration, improvised rescue. Valid 3 years; recertification every 3 years (~$425 for hybrid recert). This is the correct training level for any serious off-grid scenario.

**Skill maintenance schedule:**
- CPR recertification: every 2 years (required)
- WFR recertification: every 3 years (required)
- Suture practice: 2× per year on foam training pads ($15 on Amazon) or pig feet
- Dental drill: mock temporary fillings and incision practice annually
- Mass casualty tabletop drill: annually

### Mental Health — The Underestimated Priority

Mental health degrades faster than physical health in isolated, high-stress, or grid-down conditions. The Midwest's seasonal extremes (short winter daylight at latitude 41–47°N) compound this.

**Seasonal affective disorder (SAD) — Midwest-specific:**

Zone 5 Midwest at 41–47°N latitude loses significant daylight hours between November and February. SAD affects an estimated 6% of the US population (winter-pattern) with another 14% experiencing subsyndromal SAD ("winter blues").

Treatment:
- **Light therapy (10,000 lux SAD lamp):** 30 minutes within 1 hour of waking; sit 20–24 inches from lamp; do not stare directly. This is FDA-accepted first-line treatment for SAD. Multiple systematic reviews confirm efficacy. Start in early October; continue through March. Verilux HappyLight or Carex Day-Light Classic: $30–80. [^4]
- **Outdoor light exposure:** Get outside within 1 hour of dawn regardless of cloud cover — ambient outdoor daylight (10,000+ lux on clear days; 1,000 lux overcast) exceeds indoor lighting by a factor of 10–100.
- **Vitamin D**: Most northern-latitude adults are deficient by February. Supplement 2,000–4,000 IU/day October through April. Evidence for mood benefit is moderate; the deficiency is real regardless.

**Exercise as antidepressant:** Multiple meta-analyses confirm 30–45 minutes of moderate-intensity exercise has antidepressant efficacy equivalent to SSRIs for mild-to-moderate depression. Physical labor on a homestead or farm provides this automatically — make it intentional, not incidental.

**Sleep discipline:** Consistent wake time is the single most important sleep hygiene variable. Sleep deprivation is mechanistically causal in depression and anxiety. Prioritize sleep schedule consistency above all other variables.

**The isolation arc:** Research on isolated populations (Antarctic winterovers, submarine crews) shows a characteristic arc: initial motivation → difficult adjustment at 2–6 months (irritability, sleep disruption, nostalgia) → adaptation by 6–12 months in most people. The adjustment phase is not pathology; communities that normalize it and provide structured support have significantly better outcomes.

**PTSD field response:** After any traumatic event (injury, death, severe weather, assault):
- 5-4-3-2-1 grounding technique: name 5 things you see, 4 you can touch, 3 you hear, 2 you smell, 1 you taste — interrupts amygdala-driven hyperarousal and restores sensory cortex engagement
- Limit alcohol — alcohol reduces acute anxiety but dramatically worsens PTSD trajectory and increases chronicity
- Intrusive thoughts, sleep disruption, and hypervigilance for 30 days following trauma are acute stress responses, not clinical PTSD; most self-resolve
- Evacuate for psychiatric care if: active suicidal ideation with plan and means; psychosis (hallucinations, command voices); inability to care for self >72 hours

**Chronic disease without specialists:**
- Hypertension: salt restriction (<2g/day sodium), daily aerobic exercise, and weight loss are each evidence-based lifestyle interventions that can reduce systolic BP 5–20 mmHg. Combined, they frequently reduce or eliminate medication need for mild hypertension. Stockpile lisinopril/amlodipine.
- Type 2 diabetes: a very-low-carbohydrate diet frequently eliminates the need for diabetes medications in Type 2 (not Type 1 — never stop basal insulin without medical guidance). The research on low-carb for glycemic control is robust. Monitor with ReliOn glucose meter.
- Hypothyroidism: levothyroxine is stable and inexpensive; build 90-day supply. Desiccated thyroid (Armour Thyroid) as an alternative if levothyroxine unavailable — contains both T3 and T4.
- Asthma: stockpile albuterol rescue inhalers and budesonide/formoterol controller inhaler; learn proper inhaler technique (spacer device significantly improves pulmonary deposition).

### Vision Care Without Optometrist

**Maintaining existing prescriptions:**
- Zenni Optical, Coastal, Warby Parker all provide glasses from a saved prescription with no examination required. Order backup pairs before a crisis.
- Hard contact lenses (rigid gas permeable) have much longer useful life than soft lenses; discuss with optometrist.
- Pinhole glasses: a pinhole mask creates improvised depth of field correction for reading; not a substitute for prescription lenses but allows reading when glasses are lost/broken.

**Eye emergencies in the field:**
- Chemical splash (acid, alkali, plant sap): copious irrigation with clean water for 20+ minutes, immediately. Alkali burns (lye, ammonia, lime) penetrate deeper than acid and require longer irrigation — 30+ minutes. Do not neutralize — just irrigate.
- Foreign body: irrigate first; if embedded: do not attempt removal — cover eye with rigid shield (foam cup), patch the unaffected eye to prevent sympathetic movement, evacuate.
- Traumatic eye injury: cover, do NOT apply pressure, evacuate.
- Corneal abrasion (scratch): intensely painful but usually heals without treatment; antibiotic eye drops (polymyxin B sulfate/trimethoprim, or ciprofloxacin 0.3%) reduce infection risk; most heal within 48–72 hours.

## Rural Track Variations

### Expanded Opportunities

**Medicinal plant cultivation at scale:**
Rural land allows dedicated medicinal herb garden of 500–2,000 sq ft — large enough to produce meaningful quantities for medicine-making. Priority crops for Zone 5 Midwest: elderberry (shrub; 4–6 plants = 20+ lbs berries/year), echinacea (perennial; harvest root at year 3), comfrey (highly productive; harvest leaf continuously), calendula (annual; plant 50+ for substantial oil production).

**Wildcrafting on rural land:**
- Black willow (*Salix nigra*): native along every Midwest creek and river; harvest young bark in spring for willow bark tea
- Elderberry (*Sambucus canadensis*): native edge species in many Midwest counties
- Mullein: roadside biennial; legal to harvest from your own land or public rights-of-way
- Usnea lichen ("old man's beard"): on older tree bark in undisturbed woodland; genuine antimicrobial properties; harvest from already-fallen branches
- Goldenrod (*Solidago* spp.): anti-inflammatory, kidney support; abundant Midwest native

**Animal-based medicine:**
- Honey (raw, local): genuine evidence-based wound treatment — topical honey reduces infection and accelerates healing (osmotic and hydrogen peroxide antimicrobial mechanisms). Cochrane review confirms superiority over conventional dressings for some wound types. Manuka is highest evidence but any raw honey works.
- Lard/tallow: the historical wound dressing and skin protectant; fat-based salve base; highly stable without refrigeration

**Extended isolation preparation:**
Rural areas may have days-long medical inaccessibility after severe weather (road flooding, ice storms, blizzards). Stock for 2 weeks of medical self-sufficiency, not 72 hours:
- Antibiotics covering all major infection categories (at least 2 courses each)
- 30-day supply of all chronic medications beyond normal 90-day stockpile
- Blood pressure cuff, glucose meter — vital sign monitoring for weeks, not hours

### Rural Tick Risk is Higher

Rural Midwest has significantly higher tick exposure than suburban settings. The black-legged tick (*Ixodes scapularis*) carrying Lyme disease is expanding its range into Illinois, Iowa, Indiana, Wisconsin, and Ohio. The lone star tick (*Amblyomma americanum*) causes ehrlichiosis and STARI throughout the lower Midwest.

**Post-outdoor tick protocol (non-negotiable):**
1. Shower within 2 hours of coming indoors — reduces risk of tick attachment
2. Tumble dry clothes on high heat for 10 minutes — kills ticks that survive washing
3. Full-body tick check with mirror: pay particular attention to scalp, behind ears, underarms, behind knees, groin
4. Attached tick: use fine-tipped tweezers or tick key; grasp as close to skin as possible; pull upward with steady, even pressure; do not twist or crush the body
5. Clean bite area with alcohol wipe

**Prophylactic doxycycline for tick bites (evidence-based):** A single dose of doxycycline 200 mg taken within 72 hours of a deer tick bite that was attached ≥36 hours reduces Lyme disease risk by 87%. This is the CDC-recommended protocol for high-risk bites. [^5]

## Midwest-Specific Notes

### Tick-Borne Disease — The Expanding Midwest Threat

The Midwest's tick-borne illness landscape is changing rapidly. Species and diseases by risk level:

**Black-legged tick (Ixodes scapularis) — Highest risk in northern Midwest:**
- Lyme disease (*Borrelia burgdorferi*): bull's-eye rash (erythema migrans) appears in 70–80% of cases; begins as a small red spot expanding outward with partial central clearing; NOT always a perfect ring. Other signs: fatigue, fever, joint pain. Treatment: doxycycline 100 mg BID × 14–21 days; amoxicillin 500 mg TID as alternative.
- Anaplasmosis (*Anaplasma phagocytophilum*): fever, headache, muscle aches, low white blood cell count; no rash. Treatment: doxycycline 100 mg BID × 10 days.
- Babesiosis (*Babesia microti*): malaria-like illness; hemolytic anemia; fever, chills, fatigue. Treatment: atovaquone + azithromycin × 7–10 days; or clindamycin + quinine.

**Lone star tick (Amblyomma americanum) — Lower Midwest (southern Illinois, Indiana):**
- Ehrlichiosis: fever, headache, muscle aches — no rash; can be severe. Treatment: doxycycline 100 mg BID × 5–10 days.
- STARI (Southern Tick-Associated Rash Illness): bull's-eye-like rash; not Lyme but clinically similar presentation. Treatment same as Lyme (doxycycline).

**American dog tick (Dermacentor variabilis) — Statewide:**
- Rocky Mountain spotted fever (RMSF): fever + headache + classic spotted rash (appears after 2–4 days, palms and soles); can kill in 5 days without treatment. Treatment: doxycycline 100 mg BID × 7 days minimum — start immediately on clinical suspicion; do NOT wait for lab confirmation. [^6]

**The practical rule for Midwest tick-borne disease:** Doxycycline treats all three major tick-borne disease categories in the Midwest (Lyme, Anaplasmosis, RMSF). If you find an attached tick with >36 hours estimated attachment AND develop fever/rash/flu-like symptoms within 30 days, start doxycycline empirically. The cost of missed RMSF (potentially fatal within days) vastly exceeds the cost of a brief unnecessary antibiotic course.

### Midwinter Mental Health

Zone 5 Midwest has among the most adverse conditions for mental health of any region in the continental United States:
- Shortest daylight of year (Chicago: 9 hours, 7 min on Dec 21)
- Extended overcast periods — Lake Michigan's lake-effect cloud cover persists for weeks
- Social isolation worsened by road conditions
- Physical activity restricted by cold and ice

Prevention protocol for Midwest winter:
1. SAD lamp: start October 1; 30 minutes within 1 hour of waking; every morning through March
2. Vitamin D 2,000–4,000 IU/day from October through April
3. Outdoor light exposure daily even on overcast days — 1,000 lux overcast outdoor vs. 200 lux indoor
4. Weekly social contact commitment — this is structural, not aspirational; put it in the calendar
5. Physical activity: cold outdoor work, indoor exercise; the exercise antidepressant effect requires actual intensity and consistency

### Poisonous Plant Dermatitis — Midwest Specifics

**Poison ivy (Toxicodendron radicans):** The primary contact hazard across all Midwest states. Grows as a vine (often climbing tree trunks with "hairy" appearance) and ground cover. Classic "leaves of three, let it be." All parts of the plant, all year (including bare vines in winter), carry urushiol oil.

**Treatment of urushiol exposure:**
1. Within 5–10 minutes: wash exposed skin with soap and cold water; cold water closes pores; do not scrub
2. Wash all clothing immediately; urushiol stays active on fabric for months
3. Developing rash (1–3 days after contact): the rash does NOT spread; it appears in sequence based on how much urushiol touched different areas
4. Mild rash: hydrocortisone 1% cream BID; calamine lotion; oatmeal baths; oral diphenhydramine for itch
5. Moderate to severe (weeping, widespread, involving face or genitalia): oral prednisone taper (40 mg day 1, reduce by 5 mg/day over 8 days = 8-day taper); without this, severe cases last 3+ weeks
6. DO NOT use topical diphenhydramine (Benadryl cream) on large areas — absorption through inflamed skin can cause toxicity

**Poison sumac** (Midwest swamp areas): More potent than poison ivy; grows in wet areas; distinctive 7–13 leaflets in pairs on red rachis; pale green/cream berries. Same treatment protocol.

### Heat and Humidity — Midwest Summer Pattern

Zone 5 Midwest (Chicago, Indianapolis, Des Moines, Milwaukee) regularly experiences heat index values of 100–115°F during July heat waves. Humidity is the danger amplifier — it impairs sweat evaporation, which is the primary cooling mechanism.

**Heat illness prevention in Midwest summer work:**
- Adjust outdoor heavy work to before 10am and after 5pm during heat waves
- Acclimatization: it takes 7–14 days of heat exposure for the body to adapt (increased plasma volume, earlier sweating); don't attempt heavy outdoor labor during the first heat wave of the season
- Drink before thirsty: 500 mL water 2 hours before outdoor work; 250 mL every 20 min during; electrolytes (sodium + potassium) in any session >1 hour
- Urine color check: pale yellow = adequate hydration; dark yellow = drink more; no urine = emergency

## Step-by-Step Procedures

### Procedure 1: Tick Removal and Post-Bite Protocol (10 minutes)

**Materials:** Fine-tipped tweezers or tick removal tool, alcohol wipe, marker, notepad.

1. Use fine-tipped tweezers; grasp tick as close to skin surface as possible
2. Pull upward with steady, even pressure — do not twist or jerk; twisting can break the mouthparts off
3. If mouthparts break off: leave them and let skin heal; don't dig
4. Clean bite area with alcohol; wash hands
5. Mark on calendar: date, estimated attachment time (was it there at last check?), tick species if identifiable
6. Monitor the site and your health for 30 days
7. If attached >36 hours: consider doxycycline 200 mg single dose prophylaxis within 72 hours (CDC protocol)
8. If any flu-like symptoms develop within 30 days (fever, fatigue, headache, joint pain): start doxycycline 100 mg BID immediately; do not wait for a rash

### Procedure 2: Wound Irrigation and Closure (30–60 minutes)

**Materials:** 60 mL syringe, 18g needle, 1 L clean water or saline, sterile gauze, closure material, nitrile gloves.

1. Stop bleeding first (direct pressure, tourniquet if needed)
2. Put on gloves
3. Assemble syringe with 18g needle; fill with clean water or saline
4. Irrigate: 8 PSI pressure; start at wound edge, work inward; use full 1 liter for any significant wound
5. Remove any visible debris with tweezers during irrigation; do not probe deeply
6. Assess wound: size, depth, contamination, tissue viability, structures visible
7. Decide on closure method: Steri-strips for shallow/small, sutures for gaping/deep, staples for scalp/trunk
8. For sutures: simple interrupted technique; enter 3–5 mm from edge; tie instrument knot; space sutures 4–5 mm apart
9. Apply non-adherent dressing; secure with medical tape
10. Mark redness margin with pen and date for infection monitoring
11. Check daily: increasing redness = antibiotic. Purulent discharge = open wound, irrigate, antibiotics.

### Procedure 3: Making an Herbal Tincture (5 minutes active + 4–6 weeks steeping)

**Materials:** Glass jar with tight lid, dried herb, 80-proof vodka (or higher proof for roots/resins), kitchen scale, dark glass dropper bottles.

1. Weigh dried herb; weigh out 5× that weight in vodka (1:5 ratio by weight)
2. Pack herb into clean glass jar
3. Pour vodka to cover completely; there should be no air pockets in the herb mass
4. Seal jar; label with herb name, plant part, alcohol percentage, date
5. Shake daily; store at room temperature away from direct light
6. After 4–6 weeks: pour through cheesecloth into a bowl; squeeze and press the spent herb thoroughly (this is the "marc")
7. Pour strained liquid into dark glass dropper bottles; label; store cool and dark
8. Shelf life: 3–5 years

### Procedure 4: Temporary Dental Filling with Cavit (15 minutes)

**Materials:** Cavit (3M), dental explorer or toothpick, gauze, small mirror.

1. Rinse mouth with warm water to remove loose debris
2. Dry the cavity with a rolled piece of gauze held against it for 30 seconds
3. Take a small amount of Cavit on the end of a toothpick or dental probe
4. Pack firmly into the cavity; slightly overfill
5. Have patient bite down gently on wet gauze to shape the material to their bite
6. Check with tongue: filling should not feel raised above surrounding tooth surface
7. Avoid chewing on that side for 30 minutes while material sets
8. Instruct: soft foods only; avoid hard or sticky foods on that side
9. Replace as needed if filling falls out; lasts typically 1–4 weeks
