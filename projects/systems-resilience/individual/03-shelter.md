## Quick Reference Card

**Midwest shelter threat hierarchy:**

| Threat | Kills in | Warning time | Protection needed |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tornado (EF3+) | Seconds | 0–20 minutes | FEMA-rated safe room or reinforced basement |
| Polar vortex (heat failure) | Hours | Days | Backup heat source; insulation; one-room strategy |
| Derecho | Seconds | 5–30 minutes | Interior room; same as tornado |
| Ice storm (roof/tree collapse) | Minutes–hours | 12–48 hours | Tree mitigation; roof load awareness |
| House fire (wood stove / candle) | Minutes | Seconds | Smoke detector; extinguisher; escape plan |

**Immediate tornado location in any Midwest home:**
- **Best**: Below-grade basement, away from windows, under stairs or heavy table
- **Acceptable**: Interior bathroom or closet, lowest floor, no windows
- **Never**: Mobile home (leave and go to solid building), vehicle, room with large windows

**Emergency heat priority if heat fails:**
1. Close off all but one room; seal doors with towels/blankets
2. Propane heater (Mr. Heater Big Buddy or similar — 18,000 BTU/hr) with ventilation
3. Candles in small enclosed space (minimal heat but measurable)
4. Emergency sleeping bag (0°F rated) + vapor barrier layer + wool socks + hat

## Day 1–3: Immediate Survival

### Tornado — What To Do RIGHT NOW

**Before any tornado:**
- Identify your shelter location in your home today (below grade = safest)
- Put a bike helmet, shoes, and flashlight at that location
- Know the difference between Watch (conditions favorable) and Warning (tornado confirmed)
- Get NOAA weather radio or phone alert — be asleep when tornado hits without warning

**When warning sounds:**
1. Go immediately to pre-identified shelter location — do not wait
2. If in basement: get under stairs, under a workbench, or under a mattress — away from windows
3. If no basement: interior bathroom or hallway, lowest floor, cover head and neck with hands
4. If in car: do NOT outrun tornado. If tornado is distant, drive perpendicular to its path. If close, abandon car and lie in lowest ditch available, hands over head.
5. Stay in shelter until warning is canceled or at least 10–15 minutes after storm passes

**Injuries in aftermath:**
- Most tornado deaths from structural collapse and flying debris — prioritize head/neck protection
- Watch for gas leaks (smell): do not use electrical switches; ventilate and leave
- Watch for downed power lines — treat all lines as live
- Structural assessment before re-entering: look for foundation cracks, wall separation, sagging roof

### Heat Failure — Immediate Protocol

If heating system fails in Midwest winter (furnace out, power out, pipe burst):

**First 2 hours:**
1. Close interior doors to all rooms except one — choose smallest livable room with south-facing windows
2. Seal door gaps with rolled towels or tape + blankets
3. Identify heat sources: propane camp heater, kerosene heater, electric space heater (if generator available), fireplace/wood stove if present
4. Layer clothing immediately: wool base layer, mid layer, outer layer, wool hat — do not wait until cold
5. Check on vulnerable neighbors immediately if safe to do so

**Critical temperature thresholds:**
- Pipes freeze at 20°F in uninsulated spaces (exterior walls, crawlspaces, garages)
- Hypothermia risk for adults: extended exposure below 40°F internal temperature of space
- Frostbite: fingers/toes/ears/nose at sub-freezing temperatures within 30 minutes in wind

**Pipe freeze immediate action:**
- If heat is out: open cabinet doors under all sinks on exterior walls
- Drip faucets (a thin stream) on both hot and cold — moving water resists freezing
- Wrap vulnerable pipes: foam pipe insulation ($0.50/ft) or towels temporarily
- Shut off main water supply if leaving home for extended period in extreme cold

## Week 1–4: Stabilization

### Tornado Shelter Assessment

For your existing home, assess your best available shelter:

**Option A: Basement (existing)** — Best available without construction
- Identify safest quadrant: away from windows, ideally under stairs or interior wall
- Reinforce with shelter kit: mattress/sleeping bag, bike helmets, flashlight, water (1 gallon), first aid kit
- If basement floods seasonally: identify alternative or address drainage issue before relying on it

**Option B: Interior bathroom/closet (no basement)**
- Choose interior room with no exterior walls or windows
- Small bathrooms have plumbing stack walls (heavy) — slightly better structural protection
- Critical limitation: provides minimal protection above EF2 level; this is a last resort, not a safe room

**Option C: Neighbor/community shelter** (suburban)
- Know your nearest FEMA-rated community tornado shelter (school, library, city hall — check local emergency management website)
- Know how long it takes to get there and whether you can make it in 10 minutes of warning

**What does NOT work:**
- Closets with exterior walls (one exterior wall is better than windows, but debris penetrates)
- Highway overpasses (actually more dangerous — wind accelerates through gap)
- Mobile homes regardless of tie-downs (leave and go to solid building)

### Emergency Heat Infrastructure

**Propane heater setup** (suburban or rural — most practical short-term solution):
- Mr. Heater Big Buddy: 18,000 BTU/hr, runs 3–12 hours per 1-lb cylinder; $100
- Connect to 20-lb tank with adapter hose ($30): 50–120 hours of heat
- **Critical safety**: Only run in ventilated space; CO buildup is the main risk
  - Open a window 1/4 inch in the room being heated
  - Install CO detector before first use
  - Turn off when sleeping; use alternative (sleeping bag) at night
- Rural alternative: whole-house propane backup — tank-top monitor and appropriate plumbing

**Wood stove as permanent backup heat:**

A wood stove is the single most resilient backup heat source because it works with no electricity, no fuel delivery infrastructure, and local fuel that can be self-harvested.

Key specs for Zone 5 Midwest:
- Home 1,200 sq ft: stove rated 20,000–40,000 BTU/hr
- Home 2,000 sq ft: stove rated 40,000–60,000 BTU/hr
- Wood fuel need: 2–4 cords per winter (depends on insulation quality; well-insulated home uses 2 cords)
- 1 cord = 128 cubic feet stacked = approximately 2 full-size pickup truck loads

Installation requirements:
- EPA Phase 2 certification required (efficiency + emissions standard)
- Clearances to combustibles: typically 12–16" on sides, 18–36" on front (check specific stove specs)
- Chimney: must extend 3 ft above roof penetration, 2 ft above any structure within 10 ft
- Permit: required in most jurisdictions; suburban HOA may restrict (see suburban track)
- Cost: $1,500–3,500 for stove + pipe + installation

**Kerosene heater** (no-installation alternative):
- Suitable for Zone 5 as primary emergency heat in power outage scenarios
- Produces CO — must ventilate (crack window)
- 1–1.5 gallons/24 hours depending on BTU setting
- Store 10–20 gallons of fresh kerosene (rotate annually) = 7–14 days of emergency heat
- Cost: $100–200 heater + storage tank

### Home Winterization (Highest ROI Work)

Air sealing before insulation — the most cost-effective improvement by far (30–40% of heat loss is air infiltration):

**Attic air sealing** (do before adding attic insulation):
- Seal around all ceiling penetrations: light fixtures, fans, plumbing/electrical chases
- Use fire-rated caulk around light fixtures; expanding foam (spray) around wiring penetrations
- Seal attic hatch with weatherstripping and rigid foam board on hatch
- Cost: $50–150 in materials; 4–8 hours DIY

**Basement/foundation air sealing:**
- Foam sill plate area (where house frame meets foundation): expanding foam, sill gasket, or backer rod + caulk
- Seal rim joist: 2" rigid foam board cut to fit + caulk at edges; or spray foam
- Cost: $100–200 materials; 4–8 hours

**Door and window sealing:**
- Weatherstrip all exterior doors; threshold seal at bottom
- Caulk exterior window perimeters where frame meets siding
- Interior: plastic window film kits for drafty old windows ($30–50 for whole house)
- Replace broken window seals (foggy double-pane glass)

**Attic insulation** (after air sealing):
- Zone 5 target: R-49 to R-60 in attic
- Existing attic: add blown cellulose or fiberglass over existing insulation
- 10" of blown cellulose = approximately R-35
- Adding 6" more = brings to R-49
- DIY option: rent blowing machine from box store (free with purchase of batts); ~$500–800 materials for average home

## Month 1–6: Building Systems

### Tornado Safe Room — Selection and Planning

**The fundamental decision**: in-ground vs. above-ground safe room

| Type | Protection Level | Cost Range | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| In-ground (garage floor) | Highest — near absolute | $4,500–9,000 installed | Rural + suburban with attached garage |
| Above-ground concrete block | Very high — FEMA rated | $2,500–6,000 installed; $1,500–3,000 DIY | Any home with slab foundation |
| Above-ground steel unit | Very high — FEMA rated | $3,500–8,000 installed | Homes where concrete construction isn't feasible |
| Reinforced basement room | High — significant improvement | $1,000–3,000 materials | Homes with existing basement |
| Saferoom retrofitted closet | Moderate | $2,000–5,000 | Suburban; no basement, no garage slab |

**FEMA-rated means**: designed and tested to resist 250 mph wind and flying debris per FEMA P-320 criteria.

**FEMA grant programs**: The BRIC (Building Resilient Infrastructure and Communities) and HMGP (Hazard Mitigation Grant Program) can fund up to 75% of safe room cost through your local emergency management agency. Contact your county emergency management office — this can make a $6,000 safe room cost you $1,500 out of pocket.

### Above-Ground Concrete Block Safe Room — DIY Construction Plan

This is the most practical DIY safe room for most Midwest homes. Built in a corner of a garage or interior room.

**Specifications (FEMA P-320 compliant for reference; verify with local building department):**
- Walls: 8" CMU (concrete masonry unit) filled solid with concrete + #4 rebar every 24" vertically
- Horizontal reinforcement: #4 rebar in bond beam every 4 courses (every 32")
- Foundation: anchor into existing 4" concrete slab with 5/8" anchor bolts, 6" embedment, 12" spacing
- Roof: 3–4" reinforced concrete slab (built in place) OR 3/8" steel plate on structural steel frame
- Door: FEMA-listed steel safe room door, inward-opening, with deadbolt
- Minimum interior size: 4×6 ft per person (8×8 ft is more practical for entry and equipment)
- Ventilation: 4" PVC vent pipe with 90° bend facing away from prevailing storm direction

**Materials list for 8×8 ft above-ground safe room:**
| Item | Quantity | Unit Cost | Total |
|---|---|---|---|
| 8" CMU blocks | 200 | $2.50 | $500 |
| Ready-mix concrete (to fill cells + bond beams) | 1.5 yards | $175/yard | $263 |
| #4 rebar (20 ft lengths) | 30 | $8 | $240 |
| Rebar ties + wire | 1 box | $15 | $15 |
| 5/8" anchor bolts (12 per wall = 48) | 1 box | $40 | $40 |
| Mortar (80 lb bags) | 20 | $8 | $160 |
| FEMA-listed safe room door (steel) | 1 | $800–1,500 | $1,200 |
| Steel lintel over door | 1 | $50 | $50 |
| Roof: structural steel angle + 3/8" steel plate | Per design | $400–800 | $600 |
| 4" PVC vent + cap | 1 | $30 | $30 |
| **Total materials** | | | **$3,098** |

Labor: If self-building (masonry experience needed or hired mason for walls): $0–1,200
Total DIY with some hired help: $3,500–4,500

**Step-by-step construction sequence:**
1. Choose location: interior corner of garage or interior room on concrete slab
2. Mark layout; clean slab thoroughly
3. Set anchor bolts into slab with epoxy anchors at marked locations
4. Lay first course of CMU on mortar bed; check plumb and level
5. Insert vertical rebar into cells; tie to anchor bolts
6. Fill cells with concrete every 3–4 courses
7. Install horizontal rebar (bond beams) every 4 courses
8. Continue up to door height; install FEMA door frame (steel, embedded in masonry)
9. Continue walls to full height (typically 8 ft)
10. Build top bond beam; install roof structure
11. Pour or install roof
12. Install vent pipe

**Building permit**: Required. Submit to your local building department; bring FEMA P-320 as reference for compliance. Most inspectors are familiar with safe room construction.

### In-Ground Safe Room (Garage Floor)

Pre-cast concrete units are the most common in-ground option.

**Process:**
1. Core drill or saw-cut garage floor to template dimensions (typically 4×8 ft or 5×8 ft)
2. Excavate below (typically 6–7 ft below floor level)
3. Set pre-cast unit into excavation with crane or skid loader
4. Backfill sides; concrete lid flush with garage floor
5. Install FEMA-rated door (horizontal, in lid; or vertical, through wall — depends on unit)
6. Seal with waterproof membrane

**Key issues in Midwest:**
- Water table depth is critical — if water table is within 6 ft of surface (common in low-lying Midwest areas), in-ground safe rooms flood. Install sump pump or choose above-ground option.
- Check USGS groundwater maps for your area before committing

### Backup Heat — Wood Stove Installation

**Choosing a stove:**
| Stove Type | Best For | Price Range | Key Consideration |
|---|---|---|---|
| Freestanding cast iron | Rural home, large space | $800–2,500 | Best heat output; needs floor protection |
| Insert (into masonry fireplace) | Existing fireplace homes | $1,200–3,000 + liner | Most efficient use of existing chimney |
| Catalytic (high efficiency) | Any setting | $1,500–3,500 | 72-80% efficiency; needs annual catalyst replacement |
| Non-catalytic (simpler) | Rural/suburban | $800–2,000 | 65-75% efficiency; easier maintenance |

**Chimney installation (most critical and expensive component):**
- All-fuel Class A chimney pipe for any wood-burning appliance
- Double-wall insulated pipe (required for all exposed vertical runs)
- Clearance: 2" from combustibles for insulated pipe
- Cap with rain cap and spark arrestor: required in fire-risk areas
- Cost: $500–1,500 for materials; $300–500 to hire installer for roof penetration + cap

**Firewood math:**
- 1 cord = 128 cubic feet stacked (4×4×8 ft standard cord)
- Hardwood BTU content: hickory = 28M BTU/cord; oak = 24M; ash = 23M; maple = 22M
- Zone 5 Midwest average winter: 2–4 cords depending on home size and insulation
- Softwoods (pine, poplar): 15-17M BTU/cord — lower quality but acceptable; faster to dry
- Drying time: freshly cut wood is 50% moisture; must dry to 20% or less — 6–18 months seasoning
- **Rule**: split and stack in spring to use next winter

**Midwest firewood sourcing:**
- Free firewood: city tree removal programs, Facebook Marketplace, Craigslist "free" section (ask any tree service)
- State forests: many Midwest states allow dead wood removal permits for personal use
- Purchase: $150–350/cord delivered; $80–150/cord you-haul (pick up at farm or lot)

## Year 1+: Self-Sufficiency

### Full Resilient Shelter System

A complete shelter system for long-term grid-down living in Zone 5:

```
HEAT (no grid required)
├── Wood stove (primary) ← firewood from your property or local sources
│   ├── 2–4 cords stacked and seasoned by October each year
│   └── Backup propane (100-lb tank) for extreme cold snap wood conservation
├── Passive solar design (reduces heat need 20-40%)
│   ├── South-facing windows (low-e glass or added window area)
│   ├── Thermal mass (concrete, brick, or tile floor in solar zone)
│   └── Properly sized overhang (blocks summer sun, allows winter sun)
└── Superinsulation (reduces heat loss)
    ├── Attic: R-60 target
    ├── Walls: R-21 minimum (R-30+ if new construction)
    └── Slab/basement: R-10 perimeter

TORNADO PROTECTION
├── FEMA-rated safe room (in-garage or interior concrete)
├── Emergency supplies at safe room location
│   ├── 72-hour water supply (3 gallons)
│   ├── First aid kit and medications
│   ├── Flashlights + batteries + phone charger
│   ├── Blankets and change of clothes
│   └── Bike helmets (head protection)
└── NOAA weather radio (battery backup)

STRUCTURAL HARDENING
├── Trees: annual assessment; dead limbs over house removed
├── Roof: metal roofing (most resilient to ice and wind vs. shingles)
├── Windows: double or triple pane; storm shutters optional
└── Gutters: cleaned before winter; ice dam prevention (attic ventilation)
```

### Passive Solar for Zone 5 — Adding to Existing Home

Passive solar works best as new construction but can be partially retrofitted:

**Zone 5 solar window sizing:**
- Target: south-facing glass = 7–12% of floor area
- For 1,500 sq ft home: 105–180 sq ft of south-facing glass
- Too little: minimal benefit. Too much: overheating in summer and shoulder seasons

**Overhang sizing (Zone 5 latitude ~41°N):**
- Summer sun angle at noon: 41 + 23.5 = 64.5° above horizon — overhang must block this
- Winter sun angle at noon: 41 - 23.5 = 17.5° — overhang must allow this
- Overhang projection = window height × 0.32 (for 41°N)
- Example: 4-ft tall south window → needs 1.3 ft of overhang to block summer sun

**Thermal mass:**
- Material: concrete slab, brick, tile, or water containers (water has highest heat capacity)
- Must be in direct sun during the day to be effective
- Rule of thumb: 3–4 sq ft of 4" concrete per sq ft of south glass

**Practical retrofit steps (in order of ROI):**
1. South-facing trees: remove any deciduous trees blocking winter sun (plant on north side instead)
2. Replace south windows with larger units (low-e glass, argon-filled)
3. Add dark tile over existing floor in sun-facing rooms (improves heat absorption)
4. Install thermal curtains to all windows for night insulation

### Metal Roofing — Long-Term Resilience Upgrade

Standard asphalt shingles: 15–25 year lifespan; fails under ice load; doesn't shed snow well.
Metal roofing (standing seam): 40–70 year lifespan; sheds snow; handles ice; most resilient to wind.

- 26-gauge steel standing seam: most common residential metal; $5–12/sq ft installed
- 24-gauge (thicker): better durability; $8–15/sq ft installed
- Benefits for Midwest: sheds ice dams; rated to 120 mph winds; handles hail better than shingles
- Cost premium over shingles: 2–3× upfront; pays back in reduced replacement and maintenance

## Rural Track Variations

### Advantages
- **Building without permits**: many rural Midwest counties have minimal building code enforcement outside of incorporated areas; always check your county, but outbuilding and accessory structure construction is often permit-free
- **Earthen or alternative construction**: compressed earth block, straw bale, or rammed earth are feasible on rural land; not feasible in suburban context; highest thermal mass of any construction type
- **Underground/earth-sheltered homes**: excellent thermal performance; basement tornado protection built in; common in Midwest prairie tradition
- **Woodlot access**: on-property firewood sourcing; free if you do the labor

### Earth-Sheltered or Underground Construction

The Midwest prairie tradition of building into hillsides or underground offers exceptional performance:
- Constant underground temperature at 6+ ft depth: 50–55°F in Midwest (Zone 5)
- This means a well-designed underground structure needs minimal heat in winter and no cooling in summer
- Requires site with drainage slope and proper waterproofing (critical — water intrusion kills underground structures)
- Cost: comparable to conventional construction if done well; can be cheaper with earthbags or rammed earth
- Tornado protection: built in — underground is the ideal safe room

**Berm home** (partially underground):
- Built into hillside on south-facing slope; earth covers north, east, west sides
- All windows face south
- Most practical version of earth-sheltered construction for Midwest
- Natural tornado protection without separate safe room
- Frost-free on buried walls — heating costs dramatically reduced

### Cordwood Construction (Rural)

Logs set perpendicular to wall face, mortared with cob or lime mortar:
- Excellent insulation (R-1 to R-1.5 per inch of log thickness — 24" wall = R-24 to R-36)
- High thermal mass
- Fire-resistant (logs don't ignite readily in wall configuration)
- Labor-intensive but low material cost if you have a woodlot
- Best combined with earthen floor and metal roof
- Resources: Cordwood Construction Association (free guides at cordwoodconstruction.org)

## Suburban Track Variations

### Working Within Constraints

**What you can almost certainly do:**
- FEMA safe room in garage (most HOAs have no restriction — it's in the garage)
- Weatherstrip and insulate (invisible from outside)
- Install propane heater as backup (tank in garage)
- Replace windows with higher-efficiency units
- Add wood stove IF local codes allow (Zone 5 Midwest suburban — approximately 50/50 HOA restrictions; check before purchase)

**What requires investigation first:**
- Wood stove: check city code + HOA before purchase; some suburban areas have air quality restrictions; EPA Phase 2 certification typically required
- Visible wood pile: HOA may require screening
- Metal roofing: most HOAs allow any roofing material that meets ICC standards
- Rainwater barrels: address before adding solar or collection systems

**What typically isn't possible in suburb:**
- Earth-sheltered construction on existing property
- Berm home on flat suburban lot
- Significant structural changes to attached units (condo/townhouse)

### Suburban Safe Room Priority

If you have an attached garage with a concrete slab: **above-ground concrete block safe room in garage corner** is the ideal suburban solution. It's hidden from HOA, doesn't require permit drama (confirm with your building department), and provides FEMA-rated protection.

If you have no garage: **reinforced interior room** is the best available option short of paying $4,000–6,000 for a steel safe room installation in an interior closet.

**Reinforced interior room without full safe room construction:**
- Steel door (replace hollow-core with solid steel door): $200–400
- Add steel plate (1/4") behind drywall on interior wall faces (visible walls only): $500–1,000 materials
- Add steel bolt brackets from door frame to wall studs: $100
- This is NOT FEMA-rated but provides substantially more protection than standard interior room

### Suburban Generator + Transfer Switch

When grid power fails during ice storm or polar vortex:

**Portable generator + manual transfer switch** (most practical suburban option):
- 7,500-watt portable generator: $600–1,200 (enough for furnace fan, refrigerator, lights, device charging)
- Manual transfer switch (6–10 circuits): $150–300 + electrician install ($200–400)
- Extension cord approach (no transfer switch): generator in garage, heavy-gauge extension cord to essential appliances — legal but less convenient
- Key: generator must run in garage with door open or outside — CO kills in 10 minutes in enclosed space

**Standby generator** (automatic, natural gas or propane):
- 11–14 kW: powers whole average home; starts automatically within 30 seconds of outage
- Cost: $3,500–6,000 + installation
- Requires natural gas line or propane tank

**Power priority in outage (manual load shedding):**
1. Furnace fan (essential — makes heat source work): 300–600 watts
2. Refrigerator: 100–400 watts
3. Chest freezer (if full of preserved food): 100–200 watts
4. Device charging: 50–200 watts
5. Lights (LED): 10–40 watts per bulb

## Midwest-Specific Notes

### Tornado Reality

Zone 5 Midwest sits at the northern edge of tornado activity. While Tornado Alley's most intense activity is in Kansas/Oklahoma/Texas, the Midwest sees significant tornado activity:

- Illinois: averages 54 tornadoes/year; nearly all counties have history
- Iowa: averages 47/year
- Indiana: averages 22/year
- Wisconsin: averages 23/year
- Ohio: averages 19/year

Peak season: April 15 – June 15, secondary peak August–September
Peak time of day: 3pm–9pm (but can occur anytime)

**Midwest-specific tornado patterns:**
- Bow echoes and supercells most common severe storm type
- Lake Michigan creates unique tornado patterns in northeast Illinois and northwest Indiana — any direction is possible
- Flat terrain means tornadoes travel longer distances and affect larger areas than in hilly regions
- Average warning time: 13 minutes nationally; in Midwest flat terrain, radar detection can extend this to 20–30 minutes

### Polar Vortex / Extreme Cold Protocol

Zone 5 Midwest experiences polar vortex events approximately every 2–4 years. Chicago hit -27°F in 2019 with -52°F wind chills.

**Pipe protection by location:**

| Location | Risk | Prevention |
|---|---|---|
| Exterior wall cavity (supply lines) | High | Keep cabinet doors open; drip hot and cold |
| Garage (unheated) | Very high | Drip any fixtures connected; wrap with heat tape |
| Crawlspace (common in midwest older homes) | High | Insulate crawlspace and/or heat it; seal vents in extreme cold |
| Attic supply lines (rare but exists) | Extreme | Insulate; emergency: portable heater near attic hatch |
| Basement (conditioned) | Low | Standard precautions |

**If pipes freeze:**
- Shut off main water supply immediately
- Open all faucets (this releases pressure and allows drainage as ice thaws)
- Apply heat: hair dryer working from faucet toward frozen area; never open flame; never electrical heat in wet area
- If pipe has burst (water released when thawed): replace before turning on main

**Extended power outage in polar vortex (72+ hours at -20°F):**

A well-insulated home drops approximately 1–2°F per hour without heating. A poorly insulated home drops 3–5°F/hour.

At -20°F outdoor temperature, interior will reach 40°F (dangerous range) in:
- Well-insulated home: 12–20 hours
- Average home: 6–12 hours
- Poor insulation: 3–6 hours

**One-room survival strategy:**
1. Select smallest interior room (bathroom or closet works; bedroom is too large)
2. Seal all gaps (towels under door, tape around frame)
3. Propane heater at minimal setting + cracked window for CO venting
4. 4 adults in one small room = meaningful body heat contribution
5. Target: keep room above 50°F; pipes won't freeze above 55°F indoors

### Derecho — The Underappreciated Midwest Threat

Derechos are organized wind-damage events associated with fast-moving bands of severe thunderstorms. Unlike tornadoes, they:
- Cover huge areas (hundreds of miles long)
- Produce sustained winds 58–80 mph, gusts to 100+ mph
- Often happen at night with less warning than tornadoes
- May produce embedded tornadoes within the bow echo

**Midwest derecho history:**
- June 29, 2012: major Midwest derecho killed 29, $2.9B damage
- August 10, 2020: Iowa derecho caused $7.5B in agricultural damage — largest crop loss event in Iowa history; 5.8 million acres damaged; 770,000 lost power

**Preparation:**
- Same protocol as tornado — get to interior room when bow echo is approaching
- Outdoor furniture, propane tanks, anything not anchored must be secured before storm season
- Trees: most derecho damage is falling trees — annual hazard tree assessment is the single most effective preparation

### Ice Storm — Tree and Roof Priority

Ice storms are the most destructive non-tornado weather in Zone 5:
- Freezing rain accumulates: 1/2" ice = hazardous; 1" = major damage
- 1" of ice on tree limbs weighs 500 lbs per 100 sq ft of canopy area
- Power lines: 1/2" ice adds 500 lbs per 100 ft of line

**What to do before ice storm season (September – October each year):**
1. Hire arborist for hazard tree assessment: $150–400; look for dead limbs, co-dominant stems, trunk cracks, root plate heaving
2. Remove dead limbs over house, garage, power line, driveway
3. Large trees with trunk cracks or dead tops near structures: consult certified arborist about removal vs. cabling
4. Roof: ensure attic is properly ventilated to prevent ice dams (insulation + venting = even roof temperature)

**Ice dam prevention:**
- Ice dams form when warm attic melts roof snow; meltwater refreezes at cold eave overhang
- Prevention: proper attic insulation (R-49+) + continuous soffit-to-ridge ventilation
- Existing ice dam: use calcium chloride (not rock salt — damages shingles) in a sock laid across dam
- Roof rake: pull snow off lower 3 ft of roof before ice forms

## Calculations & Sizing Reference

### Heat Loss Math for Zone 5 Home

**Heating degree days (HDD) for Zone 5 Midwest:**
- Chicago: 6,493 HDD/year (base 65°F)
- Indianapolis: 5,614 HDD/year
- Des Moines: 6,588 HDD/year
- Milwaukee: 7,635 HDD/year

**Annual firewood need calculation:**
```
Annual heat need (BTU) = HDD × 24 × House_sq_ft / R-value_of_envelope
Stove efficiency: 70% for non-catalytic; 80% for catalytic
Cords needed = Annual heat need / (Cord BTU × efficiency)
```

Example: 1,500 sq ft home, R-25 envelope, Chicago (6,493 HDD):
- Heat need: 6,493 × 24 × 1,500 / 25 = 9,351,000 BTU/year
- Hickory at 70% efficiency: 9,351,000 / (28,000,000 × 0.70) = 0.48 cords
- Oak at 70%: 9,351,000 / (24,000,000 × 0.70) = 0.56 cords

Note: This is theoretical. Real-world factors (infiltration, occupant habits, humidity) typically mean 1.5–3× more wood is needed. Plan for 2–4 cords in Chicago-area Zone 5.

### Safe Room Sizing

| Users | Minimum sq ft | Recommended sq ft | Notes |

## Step-by-Step Procedures

### Procedure 1: Winterize Your Home in One Weekend (8–12 hours)

**Materials**: Expanding foam, latex caulk, weatherstripping, attic insulation, rigid foam board for basement rim joists

Day 1 — Air Sealing:
1. Attic: locate all penetrations (light fixtures, fan boxes, plumbing stacks, electrical chases); seal each with foam or fire-rated caulk
2. Basement rim joist: cut rigid foam to fit each joist bay; caulk edges with spray foam
3. All exterior doors: apply new weatherstripping; replace door threshold seal if worn
4. Windows: caulk exterior perimeter; apply interior plastic film to single-pane windows
5. Outlets/switches on exterior walls: install insulating gaskets behind cover plates ($0.25/each)

Day 2 — Insulation Check:
1. Attic: measure existing insulation depth; note R-value (blown fiberglass = R-2.5/inch; blown cellulose = R-3.5/inch)
2. If below R-38: plan to add blown-in insulation
3. Basement walls: check for existing insulation; add 2" rigid foam if none
4. Pipes: wrap any visible supply pipes in unconditioned spaces with foam pipe insulation

### Procedure 2: Emergency Heat Failure Protocol (30 minutes)

1. **Assess**: How cold is it outside? How long until repair? Is there a propane/kerosene option?
2. **Contain**: Close all interior doors; choose one room to maintain (smallest interior room)
3. **Layer**: Put on all layers immediately (wool base, fleece mid, down outer, hat, wool socks)
4. **Heat**: Set up propane heater at lowest setting; crack window 1/4" for CO ventilation; install CO detector
5. **Pipes**: Open all cabinet doors under sinks; drip all faucets (hot and cold); turn off irrigation/exterior lines
6. **Food**: Move refrigerated/frozen food to coldest part of garage if power out; outdoor temps can serve as natural refrigeration at 32–40°F
7. **Communicate**: If extended outage, let one person outside your household know your status daily

### Procedure 3: Pre-Storm Tornado Checklist (15 minutes when Watch is issued)

1. Charge all phones and battery packs to 100%
2. Fill bathtubs and all containers with water (water disruption often follows tornado)
3. Move to first floor; bring pets in
4. Locate flashlights, first aid kit, shoes, medications — place at shelter location
5. Move car out of garage IF you have an in-ground safe room in garage (must be able to open clear)
6. Park cars away from trees and under solid roof if possible
7. Bring in all outdoor furniture, propane tanks, light items that can become projectiles
8. When Warning issued or rotation visible: GO TO SHELTER IMMEDIATELY — do not wait
