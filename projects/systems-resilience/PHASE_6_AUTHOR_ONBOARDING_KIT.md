## Part 3: Outline Review SLA

*Orchestrator-facing. This section defines the timeline and criteria for the T+3 outline gate.*

### Outline Submission Format

The author submits their outline by T+3 17:00 UTC (June 4). The outline must contain:

1. **Document title** (working title is fine)
2. **Section list** (8–12 sections)
3. **Per-section fields**:
   - Section title
   - Scope description (2–3 sentences: what this section covers and why it matters)
   - Primary sources assigned to this section (minimum 3; list by title or URL)
   - Estimated word count (4,000–6,000 words is typical; some sections may be shorter or longer with justification)
   - Zone 5 application note (1–2 sentences: how does this section apply to Midwest communities?)
   - Out-of-scope note (what would naturally expand this section but is explicitly excluded?)
4. **Total word count estimate**
5. **Integration notes** (which sections reference other Phase 6 domains; which sections build directly on specific Phase 5 documents)
6. **Known gaps** (any research questions the author cannot yet answer from pre-staged sources — flag now, not at first draft)

An outline that meets this format takes 4–6 hours to produce. If an author delivers less than this on time, accept it and provide specific feedback on what is missing — do not reject a brief outline without feedback, because the outline review is a collaborative gate, not a gatekeeping one.

### Orchestrator Review Criteria

Review each section of the outline against these criteria:

**Criterion 1 — Core question coverage**: Collectively, do the sections answer all three core research questions from the domain candidate file? Map each question to one or more sections. If a core question is not addressed by any section, this is a mandatory revision.

**Criterion 2 — Zone 5 grounding**: Does every section have a Zone 5 application note that is specific (names a geography, institution, crop, climate condition, or Midwest community context) rather than generic ("communities should consider...")? Generic zone notes are advisory feedback; absent zone notes are mandatory revision for any section over 3,000 words.

**Criterion 3 — Scope boundary**: Does the outline stay within the confirmed out-of-scope boundaries? Check specifically: does any section replicate Phase 5 content? Does any section enter another Phase 6 domain's territory? Cross-references are fine; coverage overlap is not.

**Criterion 4 — Source adequacy**: Are the pre-staged sources adequate for the section's stated scope, or has the author identified new sources not in the pre-staged library? New sources are a positive signal (the author is researching actively) as long as they are accessible. If the author has listed sources they cannot access, flag them as source access problems (CT-2 in the execution checklist).

**Criterion 5 — Integration coherence**: Do the cross-domain integration notes make sense? Ensure the author is referencing existing Phase 5 documents (not rewriting them) and realistic Phase 6 outputs (not requiring documents that do not yet exist).

### Feedback Tiering

Classify each piece of outline feedback:

- **Mandatory revision (R)**: Section cannot proceed to writing without addressing this. Outline must be resubmitted.
- **Write-in note (W)**: Author should incorporate this note as they write but outline is approved to proceed.
- **Advisory (A)**: Author may take or leave; will not affect publication readiness assessment.

If an outline receives more than three (R) items, it requires resubmission by T+5 EOD. One or two (R) items can be resolved by write-in commitment from the author (written reply by T+4 17:00 UTC stating how each (R) item will be addressed) — in that case, writing can begin without waiting for a revised outline document.

## Part 4: First-Draft Checkpoint Protocol

*Orchestrator-facing. This section defines the T+7 checkpoint structure and evaluation criteria.*

### Author T+7 Deliverable Package

Due: T+7 12:00 UTC (June 8)

The author submits four items:

1. **Draft sections** (minimum 2 complete sections; target 8,000–15,000 words total)
   - Clearly label sections as COMPLETE or IN PROGRESS
   - Do not submit raw notes or bullet points as draft sections — drafts should be prose, even if rough
   - [CITATION NEEDED] markers are acceptable placeholders for sources the author is still tracking down

2. **Running bibliography** (informal format acceptable)
   - Title + URL for every source cited in the draft
   - Note any sources cited but not yet accessible (flag for orchestrator source research)

3. **Status note** (3–5 sentences)
   - What sections are complete, in progress, and not yet started
   - Any research gaps discovered since the outline was approved
   - Any scope questions requiring orchestrator input
   - Honest assessment: is the author on track for T+14 publication readiness?

4. **Word count projection**
   - Current word count
   - Projected word count at T+14
   - Confidence level: HIGH (projections based on current pace) / MEDIUM (pace may change) / LOW (significant uncertainty)

### Orchestrator Evaluation Criteria

The T+7 evaluation answers one question: is this domain on track to reach publication readiness by T+14?

**Quality threshold — all required for Green status:**

| Criterion | Minimum standard | How to evaluate |
|-----------|-----------------|----------------|
| Word count trajectory | ≥50% of section targets met, or credible projection to T+14 completion | Count words in draft; multiply by expected sections ratio; extrapolate |
| Citation depth | ≥15 distinct source citations in submitted sections | Count unique sources in bibliography for submitted sections only |
| Zone 5 presence | ≥2 sections contain specific community application | Check each section for Zone 5 place names, institutions, or operational specifics |
| Practitioner voice | Reads as practitioner documentation, not academic essay | Compare 500-word excerpt against Phase 5 model document excerpt |
| Scope integrity | No Phase 5 replication; no other-domain overlap | Skim for section headings or paragraph topics that duplicate Phase 5 content |

**Feedback structure for authors:**

Feedback at T+7 is organized in four categories (communicate to the author in this order — clear what's working before what needs work):

1. **What is working well** (2–4 specific sentences): name the sections, the framing choices, or the citation patterns that are exactly right. This is not courtesy — it calibrates the author's understanding of what "good" looks like for the rest of the document.

2. **Mandatory adjustments** (cite specific sections): factual errors, missing citations in sections that state specific claims without sourcing, scope violations, Zone 5 specificity absent from required sections. Author must address these before T+10.

3. **Advisory notes** (cite specific sections): voice suggestions, framing alternatives, additional sources worth considering, structural observations. Author takes or leaves these at their discretion.

4. **Go/no-go for next milestone**: explicitly state whether the author is Green (proceed at pace), Amber (specific adjustments needed; follow-up at T+8), or Red (escalation conversation today).

**Response timeline**: Orchestrator feedback reaches the author by T+8 09:00 UTC (21-hour turnaround from T+7 12:00 UTC submission).

## Part 5: Publication Template

*Orchestrator-facing. This section defines the contact stratification, distribution timeline, and follow-up sequences to apply once a domain document is at publication readiness.*

### Tier 1/2/3 Contact Stratification

Contacts for Phase 6 domain distribution are organized in three tiers by engagement priority:

**Tier 1 — Direct endorsement and amplification partners** (5–15 contacts per domain)

These are organizations and individuals who: (a) have an existing relationship with the project or can be addressed as peers, (b) have direct operational experience in the domain, and (c) have platforms or networks that reach the primary reader audience (Zone 5 community organizers, rural resilience practitioners, cooperative networks).

Characteristics:
- They will read the document, not just receive it
- They may co-author a response, write a foreword, or endorse publicly
- They are worth a customized, personal outreach email (not a template blast)

Domain-specific Tier 1 examples:
- Economic resilience: NCBA CLUSA cooperative development staff, Schumacher Center for a New Economics, P2P Foundation network leads, Iowa State University cooperative extension researchers with cooperative economics publications
- Infrastructure interdependencies: FEMA Regional offices (Zone 5 states), CISA critical infrastructure coordinators, land-grant university extension engineers, National Rural Electric Cooperative Association regional staff
- International coordination: UNDRR (UN Office for Disaster Risk Reduction) regional office contacts, ICRC (International Committee of the Red Cross) community resilience program staff, relevant academic centers (Hazard Reduction Innovation Center, Disaster Research Center)
- Intergenerational transmission: 4-H national network leadership, Cooperative Extension rural youth coordinators, Highlander Research and Education Center staff, Indigenous knowledge documentation organizations
- Ecosystem restoration: NRCS state conservationists for Zone 5 states, The Land Institute (Salina, Kansas), Rodale Institute Midwest contacts, academic agroecology centers
- Institutional learning: Harvard Kennedy School executive education governance programs, Ash Center for Democratic Governance and Innovation, Zone 5 regional planning councils

**Tier 2 — Network amplifiers** (20–50 contacts per domain)

These organizations share the document with their own networks but are unlikely to engage directly with the research team. Outreach is templated but personalized by organization type.

Characteristics:
- Email list, newsletter, or social media following that reaches the target reader
- Will share if the document is clearly relevant to their audience and professionally packaged
- Outreach should state: what the document is, why their audience cares, how to share it

Domain-specific Tier 2 examples:
- Economic resilience: Mutual aid network coordinators, food cooperative associations, Transition Town chapters in Zone 5
- Infrastructure interdependencies: Rural emergency management associations, county emergency management offices in Zone 5 states, rural electric cooperative communications staff
- International coordination: NGO resilience program staff, university global development and disaster response programs, international practitioner listservs
- Intergenerational transmission: Homeschool cooperative networks, craft and trade guild associations, permaculture design course networks
- Ecosystem restoration: ATTRA/NCAT state representatives, sustainable agriculture working groups, land trust organizations in Zone 5 states
- Institutional learning: State association of counties, rural governance networks, community foundation staff focused on rural capacity

**Tier 3 — Broad audience channels** (50–200 contacts per domain)

These are newsletter lists, Reddit communities, Substack networks, or other mass-reach channels where the document can be shared without individual personalization.

Characteristics:
- Low effort per contact; high volume
- May produce unexpected high-value connections (a practitioner who finds the document through a newsletter and becomes a Tier 1 partner retroactively)
- Outreach is copy-paste from the publication announcement template

Examples (cross-domain): preparedness subreddits, rural resilience Facebook groups, permaculture and homesteading newsletter lists, cooperative economics email lists, local food system practitioners' listservs

**Tier 1 follow-up sequence:**

I'd welcome your reaction, and if you're inclined to share it with your network, here's a short description for your audience: [2-sentence abstract]."

## Appendix C: Domain Scope Summary Cards

*One-paragraph summaries for each domain. Use in author outreach, briefings, and peer reviewer introductions.*

**Economic Resilience**: Addresses how communities coordinate economic activity — exchange, labor compensation, resource allocation, surplus management — when cash, credit, and supply chains are disrupted. Covers mutual credit systems, labor-hour accounting, gift economies, cooperative structures, and community currencies. Extends Phase 5's community implementation playbook (which covers governance and social organization) with the economic layer. Primary case studies: Transition Town complementary currencies (Totnes, Bristol), Vermont time-banking networks, WIR franc (Switzerland), Mondragon cooperative economic architecture, Detroit SOUP micro-grant model.

**Infrastructure Interdependencies**: Maps how Zone 5 community infrastructure systems — energy, water, food production, communication, transportation, medical supply — depend on each other, how failures cascade, and how communities design for graceful degradation rather than cascading collapse. Extends Phase 5's microgrid architecture and communication infrastructure pre-research by adding the system-of-systems perspective: what breaks first, what does it take down with it, and where should resilience investment be concentrated?

**International Coordination**: Examines how communities and regional networks coordinate across jurisdictional boundaries — sharing resources, managing refugee and population flows, maintaining trade relationships, participating in mutual aid networks that cross state and national lines. Uses documented case studies from UNDRR frameworks, post-disaster regional coordination (Katrina, Helene), and functioning cross-border community resilience networks. Produces an actionable framework for Zone 5 communities that may need to coordinate with communities in neighboring states.

**Intergenerational Knowledge Transmission**: Addresses how communities build, audit, and transmit practical competency across generations — ensuring that critical skills (food production, equipment repair, medical care, governance) do not die with the generation that holds them. Covers adult apprenticeship models, skill inventory systems, knowledge documentation practices, and the social structures that make knowledge transmission reliable rather than accidental. Extends Phase 5 Wave 3's educational governance document with the operational competency layer.

**Ecosystem Restoration**: Covers how communities restore degraded agricultural and ecological systems as a resilience asset — improving soil fertility, reestablishing native plant communities, managing water in the landscape, and sequestering carbon while increasing food production capacity. Focuses on Zone 5 specific practices: prairie restoration in agricultural margins, no-till and cover crop integration, silvopasture in the upper Midwest, and the agroecological transition from conventional to resilient food production systems.

**Institutional Learning**: Examines how communities adapt their governance, decision-making, and operational structures over time — incorporating lessons from crises, integrating new members without losing institutional memory, and evolving rules and norms as conditions change. Extends Phase 5's conflict resolution and governance documents with the longitudinal dimension: not how you govern in crisis, but how you learn from crisis and build adaptive capacity into the institutional structure itself.
