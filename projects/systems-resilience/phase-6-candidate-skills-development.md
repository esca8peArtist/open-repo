# Phase 6 Candidate: Intergenerational Skills Development

## What This Is

**Intergenerational Skills Development** focuses on how communities preserve, transmit, and scale critical knowledge and practical skills across generations when formal educational institutions, apprenticeship systems, and professional certification no longer function. This domain asks: how does a community that's been through a multi-year collapse actually teach the next generation the skills they need to sustain themselves?

This is the **knowledge operating system** of resilience — the answer to "how does hard-won technical competence not evaporate in one generation?"

---

## Research Scope

This domain would research and answer three core questions:

1. **What knowledge and skills are actually critical for community resilience, and what are the learning time requirements?**
   - Priority skill mapping: medical response, water system maintenance, food preservation, energy system operation, structural repair, materials science basics
   - Learning time curves: how long does it take an adult to learn well-drilling? To manage a solar microgrid? To perform emergency surgery without formal training?
   - Knowledge prerequisites: what foundational knowledge must come first? (e.g., basic chemistry before water treatment)
   - Case studies: Puerto Rico hurricane recovery, Cuba post-Soviet skill transmission, Christchurch earthquake community rebuild

2. **How do communities transmit complex technical skills without schools or credentialing institutions?**
   - Apprenticeship models: one-on-one, small group, peer teaching, learning by doing
   - Documentation strategies: how to write procedures, create diagrams, preserve knowledge for offline use
   - Testing and validation: how to certify competence without professional boards or licensing?
   - Cultural transmission: embedding skills in narratives, rituals, and social structures (why some knowledge "sticks")
   - Cross-generational teaching: how do 60-year-olds teach teenagers? What fails? What works?

3. **What institutions and governance structures support skill transmission at community scale?**
   - Learning cooperatives and skill-sharing networks (how do communities organize who teaches what?)
   - Knowledge management: libraries, oral history, apprenticeship registries
   - Resource provision: tools, materials, and time allocation for skill building
   - Quality assurance: how communities maintain standards when external certification is unavailable
   - Midwest case studies: 4-H clubs, extension services, apprenticeship networks

## Why This One

### Unique Leverage

**Knowledge Preservation Crisis**: Phase 1–5 documents HOW to do things. This domain documents HOW TO TEACH IT and how to ensure that knowledge doesn't evaporate when the expert dies. It's the bridge between documentation and implementation that most resilience work misses.

**Highest Opportunity Cost**: If a community loses its water system operator, its solar technician, or its medical responder to age or illness, and no one was trained to replace them, the entire system fails. This is the failure mode that kills communities post-collapse.

**Multi-Generational Framing**: This domain explicitly addresses 10–20 year timescales (how do you keep teaching teenagers complex skills for a decade?) rather than just immediate crisis response.

### Complementary to Existing Work

- Phase 1–5 are all "action" oriented: filter water, grow food, organize communities. This domain is "teaching" oriented: how does that knowledge transfer?
- Phase 3 covered governance. This domain extends it to *educational governance* (how communities decide what to teach and who gets to teach it).
- Phase 5 psychological support covered trauma and group cohesion. This domain extends it to the role of shared learning in building collective identity.

### Market Relevance

Midwest has strong extension service tradition and 4-H infrastructure. Homeschooling and experiential education interest is high. There's clear demand for "how to build educational systems when schools fail."
