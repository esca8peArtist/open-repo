### 1A. Community Economic Resilience

**What gap this fills**

Phase 1-5 documents how communities produce food, water, power, and healthcare. None of it addresses the coordination layer above production: how surpluses are managed, how skilled labor is compensated when there is no cash, what replaces market price signals when a doctor or mechanic needs to be paid. Without an economic operating layer, communities with food and water still fracture over perceived unfairness in exchange. This is the gap that has ended real historical communities after the acute crisis phase — not resource shortages, but breakdown in exchange coordination.

**Research scope — four core questions**

1. What economic architectures sustain when the dollar fails? (Local currencies, mutual credit systems, barter protocols, gift economies; design constraints at 20-200 person scale)
2. How do communities allocate capital and coordinate production without market signals? (Ostrom's commons governance principles, cooperative enterprise models, land trusts, participatory budgeting, surplus-management protocols)
3. What economic bridges prevent community isolation? (Inter-community trade federation models, comparative advantage without global supply chains, barter protocols for non-monetary exchange between communities)
4. What policy and institutional barriers currently obstruct community economic self-organization, and how are communities navigating them? (USDA cooperative development programs, regulatory landscape for local currencies)

**Expected word count**: 45,000-55,000 words (10-12 sections), including Zone 5 Midwest implications and minimum two implementation templates (local currency design guide; commons management charter template)

**Source library readiness**: 75-80%

All foundational sources confirmed: Elinor Ostrom's *Governing the Commons* (eight design principles directly applicable), David Graeber's *Debt: The First 5,000 Years* (anthropological framing on money origins), WIR mutual credit system documentation (Switzerland, 70+ years operational, $3B+ annual transaction volume), Bristol Pounds evaluation reports, ICA cooperative movement standards, UN Habitat local economic development reports, Cooperative Development Foundation Midwest data, Argentina 2001-2002 barter network case studies. A 2025 peer-reviewed study in the *Journal of Economic Behavior and Organization* provides new empirical evidence on mutual credit system resilience under stress. Gaps: timebanking quantitative effectiveness data is thin (qualitative case studies available); regional economic simulation models for communities under 2,000 persons are sparse.

**Estimated production hours**: 20 hours for source library finalization (June 1-7) plus approximately 200 hours of production (11 weeks at 20-22 hours/week). Fastest ramp of the three candidates. First-draft research outline deliverable by June 15.

**Cross-domain bridges to Phases 1-5**

- Phase 3 governance structures provide the decision-making framework that economic systems operate within. Economic resilience is the resource allocation layer sitting on top of governance.
- Phase 5 Wave 2 community implementation playbook describes organizational structure; this domain adds the economic layer — who gets what, how skilled labor is compensated, what replaces money.
- Phase 4 power mapping extends naturally into economic power analysis: how do capital accumulation and resource control patterns emerge in small communities over time?
- Phase 6 Governance Scaling (Domain D) is the structural prerequisite; economic systems cannot function without legitimate allocation authority. These two domains should be integrated in final synthesis.

**Unique contribution**: The resilience literature has extensive coverage of food, water, and energy systems. Almost no rigorous published work addresses the economic operating systems of small communities at 20-500 person scale. This domain fills that gap and produces the most immediately actionable implementation templates of the three candidates — a community can take the local currency design guide or the commons charter template and begin implementation without further research.

**June 1 kickoff readiness**: 85% — source library finalized June 3-5; production begins June 8.

### 1B. Intergenerational Skills Development

**What gap this fills**

Phase 1-5 documents how to do things: filter water, grow food, organize communities, manage conflict. It does not document how to teach those things, or how to ensure that knowledge does not evaporate when the expert ages out. If a community's water system operator, solar technician, or medical responder dies or becomes incapacitated and no one was trained to replace them, the entire system fails. That is the failure mode that kills communities on a 5-20 year timescale — not the initial crisis, but knowledge loss in the second generation.

**Research scope — three core questions**

1. What knowledge and skills are actually critical for community resilience, and what are the realistic learning time requirements? (Priority skill mapping across 12-15 domains; learning time curves for adult learners; knowledge prerequisites and sequencing)
2. How do communities transmit complex technical skills without schools or credentialing institutions? (Apprenticeship models, documentation strategies for offline use, competence validation without licensing boards, intergenerational teaching dynamics)
3. What institutions and governance structures support skill transmission at community scale? (Learning cooperatives, skill-sharing networks, knowledge management for offline libraries, quality assurance without external certification, Midwest case studies via 4-H and cooperative extension)

**Expected word count**: 48,000-58,000 words (10-12 sections), including Zone 5 seasonal learning calendar and implementation templates for community apprenticeship program design

**Source library readiness**: 70-75%

Confirmed sources: Lave and Wenger's situated learning theory (foundational on apprenticeship), David Sobel's place-based education work, Kolb's experiential learning cycle, andragogy literature (adult learning theory), Cuba's documented adaptation of education post-Soviet collapse, Puerto Rico hurricane recovery education rebuilding reports, UNESCO non-formal education in low-resource contexts, USDA/land-grant university 4-H documentation, Extension Service apprenticeship records, peer-reviewed literature on apprenticeship learning outcomes. Gaps: learning curve data for specific trades (well-drilling, solar installation, emergency medical response) in low-resource settings exists primarily as practitioner reports rather than rigorous academic studies; intergenerational teaching dynamics for North American contexts is underresearched; knowledge transmission effectiveness metrics are contested.

**Estimated production hours**: 15 hours for source library finalization (June 1-8) plus approximately 220 hours of production (12 weeks). Recommend pairing with an author who has education or adult learning background — this reduces research uncertainty by approximately one week.

**Cross-domain bridges to Phases 1-5**

- Every Phase 1-5 "how to do X" document generates an implicit "how to teach X" question. This domain provides the general pedagogical framework that makes all Phase 1-5 content teachable across generations.
- Phase 3 governance covers community decision-making; this domain extends it to educational governance — how communities decide what to teach, who teaches, and how competence is validated.
- Phase 5 psychological support covered trauma and group cohesion; shared learning is one of the most reliable mechanisms for rebuilding group identity and collective purpose after disruption.
- Phase 6 Economic Resilience has a direct bridge: what skills are economically viable to teach depends on what the community's economic system can sustain; skill transmission and economic structure co-evolve.

**Unique contribution**: Most resilience work focuses on Days 1-365 of disruption. This domain explicitly addresses Years 5-20: how does community knowledge survive into the second and third generation? It is the only one of the three Phase 6 candidates that operates on a genuinely multi-generational time horizon.

**June 1 kickoff readiness**: 80% — source library finalized June 5-8; production can begin June 10 if paired with a lower-dependency domain in Week 1.

### 1C. Governance Scaling + Institutional Adaptation

**What gap this fills**

Phase 3 documented community governance at 25-150 person scale. This domain extends governance to 150-500+ person federations — the scale at which communities that survive the acute crisis phase will actually be operating. More critically, it addresses the failure modes of scaled governance: what breaks when 3-5 communities try to coordinate without a central authority, and how do governance institutions learn from those failures to avoid repeating them.

**Research scope — three core questions**

1. What governance structures allow multiple communities to coordinate without a central authority? (Federation models including Swiss cantons, Iroquois Confederacy, agricultural cooperative federations; consensus and deliberation protocols that function at 300-500 person scale; inter-community conflict resolution; information flow and communication cascades across federations)
2. How do institutions learn from failures and adapt governance to prevent repeated failure modes? (Post-crisis review cycles, adaptive management protocols, institutional memory and documentation, feedback loops and early warning indicators for governance degradation)
3. What incentive structures and accountability mechanisms prevent governance corruption at scaled levels? (Checks and balances across federation layers, transparency and information access in distributed governance, resource allocation mechanisms that prevent predation, term limits and rotation protocols, Zone 5 case studies)

**Expected word count**: 50,000-60,000 words (11-13 sections) — largest of the three candidates due to scope complexity. Includes Zone 5 implications and governance charter template for multi-community federation.

**Source library readiness**: 65-70%

Confirmed sources: Elinor Ostrom's commons governance principles and Vincent Ostrom's polycentric governance work (foundational), Indigenous governance systems literature (nation-to-nation governance, treaty frameworks), Swiss cantonal system analysis, post-disaster governance case studies (Christchurch, Haiti, Puerto Rico), UNDRR disaster governance frameworks, adaptive management literature, institutional change theory (North, Williamson), Midwest regional governance documentation (county systems, soil-water conservation district cooperatives, agricultural federation structures). Significant gaps: detailed case studies of failed multi-community governance are underreported in academic literature (success stories dominate); consensus decision-making at 300+ person scale has limited empirical documentation; institutional learning mechanisms in resource-constrained settings are frontier research with no single authoritative source.

**Estimated production hours**: 25 hours for source library finalization (June 1-10, with paywall access challenges) plus approximately 240 hours of production (13 weeks). This is the highest-effort domain. Author with political science or public administration background strongly recommended — without it, add 2-3 weeks.

**Cross-domain bridges to Phases 1-5**

- Phase 3 covered governance structure at single-community scale. This domain extends it to failure modes at scale and provides the adaptive mechanisms Phase 3 explicitly did not address.
- Phase 5 conflict resolution covered mediation protocols; this domain extends to institutional learning from conflicts — what governance changes as a result of the conflict-resolution process.
- Phase 4 power mapping extends into this domain: economic power accumulation over time is a governance accountability problem at federation scale.
- Phase 6 Economic Resilience has critical integration: economic systems cannot function without legitimate allocation authority, and governance structures shape which economic models are viable.

**Unique contribution**: Extensive literature exists on national and international governance. Very little rigorous research addresses how 3-5 federated communities in practice make decisions together — the gap between Ostrom's theoretical design principles and the actual mechanics of a 300-person federation deciding to share water infrastructure. This domain addresses the practical frontier.

**June 1 kickoff readiness**: 75% — source library finalized June 8-10; strong integration needs with institutional learning literature suggest production should begin no earlier than June 10 to avoid scope drift.

## Part 2: Decision Matrix

Five criteria, rated 1-5 (5 = strongest). Rationale from full Phase 6 screening research.

| Criterion | Economic Resilience | Skills Development | Governance Scaling |
|---|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| **Urgency** (how time-critical is June start?) | 3 | 2 | 4 |
| **Source Availability** (how ready to produce now?) | 4 | 3 | 2 |

| **Unweighted Total** | **19** | **17** | **18** |

**Urgency**: Governance Scaling scores highest (4) because the 2025-2026 post-COVID governance failure examination window is currently open; research produced in 2026 speaks to live institutional questions. Economic Resilience scores 3 because the USDA Rural Cooperative Development Grant program (H.R. 1951) opens for applications in fall 2026 — communities with this framework by August 30 can apply. Skills Development scores 2 because knowledge transmission is evergreen; September start has no meaningful strategic cost.

**Source Availability**: Economic Resilience is 75-80% source-ready and is the clear leader. Skills Development at 70-75% has a manageable gap (learning curve data for specific trades). Governance Scaling at 65-70% has the most significant gap — governance failure case studies are underreported and paywall access to political science literature is a real bottleneck.

**Leverage**: Skills Development and Governance Scaling both score 5. Skills Development operates on a 5-20 year time horizon and is the foundation of long-term community viability — if knowledge evaporates, Phase 1-5 investment is worthless in the second generation. Governance Scaling is foundational to all other Phase 6 work and to Phase 7 operationalization. Economic Resilience scores 4 — it is the immediate exchange infrastructure layer and enables specialized labor sustainability, but it depends on governance to function.

**Scope Fit**: Economic Resilience has the tightest scope (three bounded research questions, clear citation target, low expansion risk). Skills Development and Governance Scaling both score 3 — educational research and political science literature are among the most expansive bodies of literature; both require explicit scope ceilings enforced weekly.

**User Interest Signals**: All three score 4. The Midwest cooperative tradition is deep and documented for Economic Resilience. The 4-H and cooperative extension infrastructure provides strong primary evidence for Skills Development. The post-COVID cooperative governance discussions and rural water authority coordination provide clear demand signals for Governance Scaling.
