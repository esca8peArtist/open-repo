# Livestock Care: Zone 5 Husbandry, Feed Systems, and Emergency Response

## Overview

Livestock integration is one of the highest-leverage investments a resilient household or community can make. A small mixed herd — cattle, goats, chickens, and rabbits — provides protein, dairy, eggs, traction, fiber, fertility, and pest control simultaneously. In Zone 5 (Illinois, Michigan, Iowa, Indiana, Wisconsin, with average annual minimum temperatures of -20°F to -10°F), livestock selection and management differs substantially from milder climates. This guide covers breed selection, feed production and storage, emergency veterinary response, predator protection, and waste management for Zone 5 conditions.

The veterinary guide produced in Phase 5 Wave 2 (`phase-5-wave-2-veterinary-care-guide.md`) covers clinical protocols and medication stockpiling in detail. This document focuses on husbandry systems — the infrastructure and daily management practices that prevent the emergencies that guide addresses.

## 1. Zone 5 Breed Selection

### Cattle

Zone 5 winters demand breeds with dense winter coats, compact body conformation to reduce heat loss, and robust immune systems adapted to cold-wet conditions. The following breeds are recommended:

**Scottish Highland** — The gold standard for cold-climate small-scale beef production. Double-layered coat (oily outer hair over downy inner coat) provides insulation to -40°F without supplemental heat. Low maintenance, forage-efficient, thrive on grass alone through most of the year. Adults reach 800-1,200 lbs (smaller than commercial breeds), well-suited to small-acreage operations. [1]

**Dexter** — A dual-purpose (beef and dairy) Irish breed standing only 36-44 inches tall. Exceptionally efficient converters of forage, produce 1.5-3 gallons of milk per day with 4% butterfat. Thrive on rough pasture that would not sustain larger breeds. Ideal for 2-5 acre homesteads. [2]

**Devon** — One of the oldest British breeds, historically called "Red Rubies." Proven cold-weather genetics, excellent grazers, calm temperament. Produce moderate milk and quality beef. Suited to Zone 5 without additional shelter requirements beyond a three-sided windbreak.

**Icelandic** — Descended from 9th-century Norwegian stock, adapted to extreme cold and rough forage. Can graze through light snow cover. Require supplemental hay only in deep winter. [3]

### Goats

Goats present a paradox in Zone 5: they are metabolically efficient and disease-resistant, but their minimal subcutaneous fat makes them susceptible to hypothermia when wet. The key is dry shelter, not necessarily heated shelter.

**Kiko** — Developed in New Zealand for low-input resilience. Superior parasite resistance compared to dairy breeds — critical in the humid Midwest where internal parasites are the leading cause of small ruminant mortality. Excellent mothers, early maturity, efficient foragers. [4]

**Boer x Kiko Crosses** — The hybrid vigor of Boer muscle mass combined with Kiko hardiness produces animals that outperform both parent breeds on pasture. Many Zone 5 small farms run this cross for meat production.

**Nigerian Dwarf** — For dairy production on limited acreage. Produce 1-2 quarts of milk per day with 6-10% butterfat (highest of any dairy breed), year-round breeding cycle, minimal feed requirements. Their small size (17-21 inches at shoulder) makes them manageable for children and older adults.

**Note on dairy goats**: Nubian, LaMancha, and Alpine goats can succeed in Zone 5 with proper housing. They require a draft-free, dry shelter with bedding. A "goat coat" (neoprene or wool vest) is advisable for thin-bodied does in temperatures below 15°F. [5]

### Chickens

Cold-hardy chicken breeds share two characteristics: small or rose combs (which resist frostbite on large single combs) and dense plumage. The Livestock Conservancy and commercial hatcheries both maintain good breed records. [6]

**Chantecler** — Bred in Quebec specifically for northern winters. Cushion comb nearly flush with the skull, virtually eliminating frostbite risk. Dual-purpose (meat and eggs). Calm temperament.

**Wyandotte** — Rose comb, thick plumage, excellent winter egg production (200+ eggs/year). Hens continue laying through short winter days better than most breeds. Available in multiple color varieties.

**Dominique** — America's oldest recognized breed, rose comb, heavy down layer, proven Zone 5 hardiness. Hens are good foragers and reasonable layers (180 eggs/year).

**Rhode Island Red** — Not the most cold-resistant comb conformation, but exceptional layer (250-300 eggs/year), cold-adaptable with minor comb protection (petroleum jelly application in extreme cold). Widely available, disease-resistant. [7]

**Practical note**: Frostbite on wattles and combs occurs when birds roost in drafty conditions, not simply cold conditions. A dry, draft-free coop at 20-25°F will not produce frostbite; a drafty coop at 35°F may.

### Rabbits

Rabbits are the most space-efficient protein source available to small homesteads. A breeding trio (one buck, two does) can produce 150-200 lbs of dressed meat per year from a 12-square-foot hutch using grain and forage.

**New Zealand White / Californian** — Standard commercial breeds, 9-11 lbs adult weight, 8-12 week market weight, excellent feed conversion (3.5:1 feed-to-meat ratio). Cold-tolerant to -20°F with basic shelter.

**American Chinchilla** — Heritage breed, good fur and meat production, 9-12 lbs, calm disposition. Listed as "Threatened" by the Livestock Conservancy — maintaining breeding stock has conservation value. [8]

Cold management: Rabbits carry subcutaneous fat and thermoregulate effectively. They should not be heated artificially (temperature swings are more dangerous than sustained cold). Insulate hutches with straw bales on three sides, provide extra bedding, and ensure water does not freeze (ceramic crocks retain heat longer than plastic).

## 2. Feed Production and Storage

### Pasture Management

Zone 5 growing season runs approximately late April through October — roughly 150-180 days of productive grazing. The remaining 150-180 days require stored feed. Pasture management determines how much hay you must purchase or produce.

**Rotational grazing** divides pasture into paddocks (typically 6-12 sections) and moves livestock every 3-7 days, allowing 30-60 days of rest and regrowth before return. This practice:
- Increases forage yield by 25-40% compared to continuous grazing [9]
- Reduces internal parasite reinfestation (larvae concentrate in the bottom 2-3 inches of grass; rotating before livestock eat below 3-4 inches breaks the cycle)
- Maintains root depth and soil organic matter

For a 2-acre pasture supporting 2 goats and 20 chickens, divide into six paddocks of approximately 1,500 square feet each. Move animals when forage height drops to 3-4 inches; allow return when regrowth reaches 8-10 inches.

**Perennial forage establishment** in Zone 5 should prioritize:
- **Orchardgrass** — tolerates cold, shade, and wet soils; regrows quickly; good palatability
- **Tall fescue (endophyte-free varieties)** — persistent, drought-tolerant once established
- **Red clover** — fixes nitrogen, high protein content (18-22% crude protein), improves soil fertility
- **Chicory** — deep tap root, tolerates drought, natural anthelmintic (deworm) properties for small ruminants [10]

**Winter feeding zones**: Designate sacrifice areas for late fall, winter, and early spring feeding. These are areas where soil compaction and pasture damage are acceptable — typically the lowest-value land near water or shade. Feeding on good pasture during wet or frozen conditions destroys root systems and triggers erosion. Penn State Extension recommends using permanent feeding pads of crushed gravel or concrete in sacrifice areas. [11]

### Hay Production and Storage

For small operations (under 10 acres), purchasing hay is often more economical than producing it. However, hay supply chains are vulnerable to drought, fuel costs, and regional price spikes. Producing or stockpiling 6-12 months of hay inventory is a core resilience strategy.

**Hay quality targets** for Zone 5 livestock:
- Grass hay: 10-12% crude protein, 55-60% TDN (total digestible nutrients)
- Legume hay (alfalfa, clover): 16-20% crude protein, 60-65% TDN
- Mixed hay: intermediate values, often most practical

**Storage methods and dry matter loss**:
- Stored in barn on pallets: 5-8% dry matter loss
- Round bales stored outside uncovered: 20-40% loss
- Round bales stored on gravel with tarps: 10-15% loss [12]

Target hay moisture content below 18% at baling to prevent mold and spontaneous combustion. Use a hay moisture probe if custom-baling. For small quantities, small square bales (40-60 lbs) are more manageable and stack efficiently in standard storage spaces.

**Feed calculation**: A 1,000-lb beef cow requires approximately 26-30 lbs of hay per day (2.5-3% of body weight). A 150-lb goat requires 3-5 lbs/day. A laying hen requires approximately 0.25 lbs of grain/day plus forage access. Calculate days of winter (typically 150-160 for Zone 5), multiply by daily consumption, add 20% buffer.

### Grain Preservation

Whole grains store substantially longer than ground or cracked grain. Under dry (below 14% moisture), cool conditions:
- Whole corn: 2-3 years
- Whole oats: 2-3 years
- Whole barley: 3-5 years
- Whole wheat (for supplementation): 5+ years

Store in metal or food-grade plastic containers to exclude rodents and moisture. Adding food-grade diatomaceous earth (1 cup per 50 lbs of grain) controls stored grain insects without chemical residues. [13]

## 3. Emergency Veterinary Response

The Phase 5 Wave 2 veterinary guide provides detailed clinical protocols. This section summarizes the triage framework for common emergencies.

### Priority One: Life-Threatening Emergencies

**Bloat (Ruminants)** — Distension of left flank in goats/cattle, animal distressed, unable to belch. Act within 2 hours. Walk animal, massage rumen, administer 200-400ml simethicone (Gas-X equivalent) orally. In severe cases, pass stomach tube. Frothy bloat requires additional oil (mineral or vegetable oil) to break foam. Without intervention, animals die from diaphragm compression within 4-6 hours. [14]

**Dystocia (Difficult Birth)** — If no progress 30 minutes after water sac ruptures, intervention is required. Clean hands, lubricate, check presentation. Normal presentation: both front hooves forward, head resting on hooves. Malpresentations require repositioning inside birth canal before traction. Call veterinarian if repositioning is not possible within 30-45 minutes.

**Hypothermia in Newborns** — Core temperature below 94°F (normal 101-104°F). Warm slowly using warm-water bath (100-102°F) or hair dryer on low heat. Rub vigorously. Once alert, tube-feed colostrum (10% of body weight in first 2 hours — critical for immune transfer). Do not let newborns chill after birth in Zone 5 winters.

**Urinary Calculi (Wethers and Rams)** — Male small ruminants fed high-grain diets frequently develop urinary stones. Signs: straining to urinate, hunching, penile extension. Emergency: ammonium chloride (1 tsp/day) as preventive; in acute cases, veterinary catheterization is required. Prevention is critical — maintain calcium:phosphorus ratio 2:1 in all rations. [15]

### Priority Two: Significant but Non-Immediate

**Foot rot / Hoof problems** — Trim hooves every 6-8 weeks. Foot rot (foul smell, interdigital tissue decay) requires zinc sulfate foot bath (10% solution) and in severe cases, penicillin injection.

**Internal Parasites (Barber Pole Worm, *Haemonchus contortus*)** — The FAMACHA scoring system assesses anemia by checking eyelid color (pale = anemic = parasite burden). Only treat animals scoring 3-5 on FAMACHA scale to delay resistance development. Fenbendazole (SafeGuard) and ivermectin are front-line dewormers; rotate classes to slow resistance.

**Pinkeye (Infectious Keratoconjunctivitis)** — Contagious, spread by flies. Isolate affected animals. Treat with oxytetracycline eye ointment or LA-200 injection (20mg/lb).

### Stockpile Recommendations

Coordinated with Phase 5 Wave 2 veterinary guide, maintain:
- Ivermectin (injectable and pour-on)
- Fenbendazole (SafeGuard)
- Penicillin G (injectable)
- Oxytetracycline (LA-200)
- Simethicone
- Electrolyte powder
- Sterile tubing for nasogastric tube feeding
- Digital thermometer
- Disposable OB sleeves and lubricant
- Ammonium chloride

## 4. Predator Protection and Fencing

Zone 5 predator pressure includes coyotes, foxes, raccoons, opossums, weasels, mink, hawks, owls, and occasionally domestic dogs. Multi-layer protection is required.

### Livestock Guardian Dogs (LGDs)

Great Pyrenees, Anatolian Shepherd, and Kangal breeds have been selectively bred for millennia to bond with livestock and deter predators through presence rather than confrontation. An LGD patrols the perimeter nightly, barks to deter coyotes (which prefer to avoid confrontation), and physically intervenes if necessary. [16]

Critical management points:
- Introduce LGD puppies to target livestock (goats, chickens) before 8 weeks of age for proper imprinting
- LGDs are not reliable with poultry unsupervised until 18-24 months old
- Two LGDs provide better coverage than one (they patrol in alternating shifts)
- Do not treat LGDs as pets — excessive affection with owners reduces working drive

### Electric Fencing

High-tensile electric fencing (12.5 gauge, 5,000-7,000 volt charger) provides the most cost-effective perimeter protection per linear foot.

**Goat/sheep perimeter**: 5-7 strands at 6, 12, 18, 24, 32, 40, and 48 inches, alternating hot-and-ground or all-hot with ground stakes. Goats respect well-charged electric wire once trained. [17]

**Poultry**: Electronet (Premier1 PoultryNet or equivalent) — 42-48 inch tall electrified netting, moveable, 164-foot rolls. Provides exclusion from all ground predators when properly tensioned and charged. First strand at 4-6 inches prevents digging under. Ground contact anywhere shorts the fence — clear grass weekly. [18]

**Coop security**: Hardware cloth (19-gauge welded wire, 1/2-inch openings) is required, not chicken wire. Chicken wire excludes chickens; it does not exclude weasels, mink, or determined raccoons. Bury hardware cloth 12 inches underground in an L-shape outward from the coop base to prevent digging.

**Aerial predator protection**: Hawks and owls are significant threats in open pasture. Provide covered runs or runs with overhead netting. Guinea fowl (noisy, excellent alarm birds) can be kept with chickens as a predator alert system.

## 5. Off-Grid Waste Management

### Manure Composting

Livestock manure is a primary soil fertility input. Properly composted, it eliminates pathogens and weed seeds while creating a stable amendment for garden beds and food forest.

**Hot composting protocol** (kills parasites and pathogens):
1. Build pile minimum 3x3x3 feet (27 cubic feet)
2. Layer 2 parts brown (straw, wood chips, dry leaves) to 1 part green (fresh manure)
3. Maintain moisture at 50-60% (squeeze test: feels like a wrung-out sponge, no drip)
4. Core temperature should reach 130-160°F within 3-7 days; maintain for 15-21 days
5. Turn pile every 3-5 days to introduce oxygen and ensure even heating
6. Finished compost is dark, crumbly, earthy-smelling, with no recognizable material [19]

**Manure nutrient values per ton (fresh)**:
- Beef manure: 11 lbs N, 7 lbs P, 10 lbs K
- Goat/sheep manure: 21 lbs N, 5 lbs P, 16 lbs K (drier, higher nutrient density)
- Chicken manure: 25 lbs N, 18 lbs P, 8 lbs K (hottest — requires thorough composting before garden use)
- Rabbit manure: "cold" fertilizer — can be applied directly to garden beds without composting [20]

Iowa State University Extension (ISU Small Farm Sustainability) recommends calculating manure accumulation early: a 1,000 lb cow produces approximately 65 lbs of manure per day. Over a 150-day winter with 5 animals of mixed species, a small farm generates several tons of manure requiring management. Plan storage areas — concrete pads or clay-lined berms — before bringing livestock home. [21]

### Parasite Management

Internal parasites are cyclical and managed through environmental disruption:

**Rotational grazing** — As described in Section 2, prevents larvae reinfestation. Most parasite larvae die within 60 days without a host in hot-dry or frozen conditions.

**Pasture resting** — In Zone 5, winter kills most surface parasite larvae. Spring grazing on pastures rested since fall carries lower parasite risk.

**Species mixing** — Sheep/goat parasites do not infect cattle and vice versa. Running cattle through a paddock after small ruminants "vacuums" the pasture of ovine/caprine parasite larvae. [22]

**Targeted selective treatment (TST)** — Treating only animals showing clinical signs (FAMACHA score 3-5, bottle jaw, poor body condition) rather than whole-herd deworms slows the development of anthelmintic resistance — now the primary challenge in small ruminant parasite management. [23]

**Composting** — Hot composting kills parasite eggs and larvae. Spreading uncomposted manure on pasture dramatically increases parasite burden.

## 6. Integration with Other Systems

Livestock integrate tightly with other resilience systems:

**Water**: Cattle require 30-50 gallons/day, goats 1-2 gallons/day, chickens 0.5 pints/day per bird. Water systems must account for livestock demand. Stock tanks require frost-free hydrants or tank heaters in Zone 5 winters. Reference `phase-5-wave-3-water-systems-and-purification.md` for gravity-fed and off-grid watering solutions.

**Food Preservation**: Dairy production (goat milk, butter, cheese) and meat processing (home butchering, smoking, curing) connect directly to preservation systems. Reference `phase-5-wave-3-food-preservation-and-storage.md`.

**Seed Saving**: Chickens serve as biological pest control in food gardens (tractor systems). Rabbit manure is the highest-quality direct-application fertilizer for garden beds. Goats clear brush and weed trees that would otherwise encroach on food production areas.
