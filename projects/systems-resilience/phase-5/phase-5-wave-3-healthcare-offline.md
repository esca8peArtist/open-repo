# Healthcare Offline: Trauma Response, Chronic Disease Management, and Herbal Protocols

## Overview

Extended infrastructure disruption — whether from natural disaster, economic collapse, or deliberate grid interruption — exposes the medical fragility of communities that have offloaded healthcare entirely to institutions. The United States healthcare system assumes continuous pharmaceutical supply chains, powered hospitals, functioning communication networks, and 24/7 emergency services. When these fail, even partially, communities with no medical self-sufficiency face preventable deaths from conditions that were entirely manageable.

This guide is not a replacement for professional medical training. It is a map of the competency gaps that must be filled — through training, supplies, and community organization — before crisis conditions arrive. It covers: trauma response and wound management, chronic disease protocols under supply-chain disruption, evidence-supported herbal medicine, pediatric care, and psychological first aid.

The psychological support dimension is covered in depth in `phase-5-wave-2-psychological-support-guide.md`. The veterinary guide (`phase-5-wave-2-veterinary-care-guide.md`) includes overlapping pharmacology for situations where veterinary-supply-chain medications must substitute for unavailable human equivalents — a common contingency planning consideration.

## 1. Trauma Response: Stop the Killing, Buy the Time

The framework used by military combat medicine (TCCC — Tactical Combat Casualty Care) and adapted for civilian wilderness and disaster medicine is "Stop the Bleeding, Get the Airway, Get Them Warm and Moving." In scenarios without hospital access within hours, the goal shifts from stabilize-and-transport to stabilize-and-manage.

### Hemorrhage Control

Uncontrolled bleeding is the leading cause of preventable death in trauma. The principles, in order of application:

**Direct pressure**: Apply firm, continuous pressure with the cleanest material available. For wounds that are not on an extremity (chest, abdomen, neck, groin), direct pressure is the primary and often only option.

**Tourniquets**: Required when direct pressure fails on an extremity, when the wound is too large to close with pressure, or when you cannot maintain pressure because of patient transport or multi-casualty management.

Application protocol:
- Apply 2-3 inches above the wound (not over a joint)
- Tighten until bleeding stops completely — partial occlusion makes bleeding worse
- Note time of application on the tourniquet or patient's forehead
- Do not remove in the field — reassess only in definitive care setting
- Commercial tourniquets (CAT — Combat Application Tourniquet, or SOFTT-W) are more reliable than improvised tourniquets; both should be available in a community medical kit [1]

**Wound packing**: For groin, axilla (armpit), or neck wounds where tourniquets cannot be applied: pack the wound firmly with gauze (preferably hemostatic gauze such as QuikClot or Celox), applying 3-5 minutes of direct pressure over packed material. Do not remove packed gauze in the field. [2]

**Pressure bandages**: Israeli bandage (Emergency Bandage) with integrated pressure bar allows single-handed application and can maintain pressure during patient transport.

### Wound Care and Infection Prevention

In scenarios without hospital access, wound infection (particularly with Staphylococcus aureus and, in contaminated wounds, Clostridium tetani) becomes the primary secondary threat. Wilderness medicine protocols prioritize irrigation over antiseptics:

**Irrigation**: Irrigate wounds with the largest volume of the cleanest water available at the highest practical pressure. A 20-60ml syringe with an 18-gauge needle tip, or a sport water bottle with a pinhole cap, delivers effective irrigation pressure. Volume matters more than sterility — contaminated water injected under pressure produces better outcomes than dry wound care. [3]

**Antiseptic application**: After irrigation, dilute betadine (povidone-iodine diluted 10:1 with water to create a pale yellow solution) applied topically is broadly antimicrobial without the tissue toxicity of full-strength betadine. Hydrogen peroxide and alcohol damage healthy tissue and delay healing — avoid on open wounds.

**Wound closure**: Close wounds under 6 hours old if clean, well-irrigated, and without signs of infection. Wound closure options in order of accessibility:
1. Steri-strips or butterfly closures (for linear cuts)
2. Sutures (with training) — interrupted sutures with 3-0 or 4-0 nylon, every 0.5-1 cm, without tension
3. Staples (requires staple remover)
4. Secondary intention (leave open, pack with damp gauze, change daily) for contaminated or bite wounds

Wounds showing signs of infection (increasing redness, warmth, swelling, pus, red streaking = lymphangitis) are medical emergencies. Oral antibiotics: amoxicillin-clavulanate (Augmentin) is first-line for infected lacerations; ciprofloxacin for wounds with Gram-negative contamination risk (fecal contact, soil).

**Tetanus risk**: Any puncture wound, burn, crush injury, or wound contaminated with soil or animal material requires tetanus prophylaxis assessment. Stock and maintain tetanus toxoid booster immunization in community medical supplies. [4]

### Fractures and Sprains

**Splinting**: Immobilize fractures in the position found (do not reduce in the field unless circulation is absent distal to the fracture). Traction splints for femur fractures reduce pain and blood loss. SAM splints, cardboard, sticks and clothing, or moldable thermoplastic splinting materials all work.

**Open fractures**: Cover exposed bone with damp, clean dressing. Begin antibiotics (cephalexin or amoxicillin). These are limb-threatening emergencies — infection rate without antibiotics approaches 50% within 48 hours.

## 2. Chronic Disease Management Without Pharmaceutical Supply Chains

Approximately 60% of American adults have at least one chronic condition requiring ongoing medication. [5] In extended supply disruption, the leading causes of preventable mortality shift from acute trauma to: diabetic ketoacidosis, hypertensive stroke, and asthma status asthmaticus.

### Diabetes

**Type 1 diabetes** is the most immediately life-threatening. Without insulin, a Type 1 diabetic enters diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) within 24-72 hours of insulin deprivation. Stockpiling insulin (requires refrigeration at 36-46°F; stable at room temperature up to 28-30 days for common formulations) is an individual medical preparedness imperative.

**Practical considerations**:
- OTC insulin (Walmart ReliOn NPH and Regular at $25/vial) is available without prescription in most states — an emergency supply option, though not equivalent to modern analogs
- Insulin dilution (1:10 U-100 to U-10) allows precise dosing with limited supply
- Glucose monitoring strips, lancets, and glucometer batteries must be stocked alongside insulin
- Sick-day management: during illness, insulin requirements increase by 20-50%; monitor glucose every 2-4 hours [6]

**Type 2 diabetes management** is more forgiving. Dietary intervention can substantially reduce or eliminate medication requirements:
- Low-carbohydrate diet (under 50g/day) reduces blood glucose effectively without medication
- Intermittent fasting protocols reduce insulin resistance
- Walking 30-45 minutes after meals blunts postprandial glucose spikes
- Cinnamon (Ceylon, not cassia): evidence suggests modest glucose-lowering effect (0.5-1% reduction in HbA1c); used as adjunct, not replacement [7]

### Hypertension

Blood pressure management without pharmaceutical supply chains relies on the same lifestyle interventions known to reduce BP: dietary sodium restriction, the DASH dietary pattern, physical activity, and stress reduction.

**Community-scale sodium reduction** is achievable through food preservation choices: reducing canned food reliance, avoiding high-sodium preserved meats, using herbs and acids (vinegar, citrus) instead of salt for preservation flavor.

**Exercise**: 30-45 minutes of moderate activity (brisk walking, cycling) 5 days/week reduces systolic BP by 5-8 mmHg on average — equivalent to low-dose medication.

**Garlic**: Meta-analyses confirm a 4-7 mmHg systolic reduction with aged garlic extract or fresh garlic (4-10 cloves/day). Not a replacement for severe hypertension (>160 systolic) but meaningful adjunct. [8]

**Hawthorn (*Crataegus monogyna*)**: Systematic review (2008, *Cochrane Database*) found hawthorn extract modestly reduces both systolic and diastolic BP; acts through vasodilation. Safe for long-term use. Hawthorn is a Zone 5-native shrub easily grown on homestead edges.

**Emergency management**: For hypertensive crisis (severe headache, visual changes, BP >180/120), cool environment, supine position, minimize stimulation. Without medications, outcomes depend on severity. These patients should be prioritized for evacuation to medical care.

### Asthma

**Trigger management**: The first-line offline intervention is rigorous trigger identification and elimination:
- Dust mites: encase mattresses and pillows, wash bedding at 130°F weekly
- Mold: maintain indoor relative humidity below 50%; address moisture sources
- Wood smoke: inefficient combustion from wood stoves is a major asthma trigger; clean-burning stoves and dry, well-seasoned wood reduce particulates
- Animal dander: structural separation of sleeping areas from livestock areas

**Bronchodilator stockpiling**: Albuterol inhalers expire but retain significant potency for 2-3 years post-expiry. Epinephrine auto-injectors (EpiPen) expire but retain potency. Both should be stocked.

**Caffeine**: A moderate bronchodilator (equivalent to approximately 1/3 the effect of theophylline). Strong coffee (400mg caffeine) can provide temporary relief during an asthma episode when bronchodilators are unavailable. [9]

**Mullein (*Verbascum thapsus*)**: Traditional use as a lung herb. Mullein leaf tea has a long history as a respiratory herb; current evidence is limited but it may provide mild bronchial soothing. It is not a rescue treatment but may help in mild, non-emergency respiratory distress. Mullein is common in disturbed soils throughout Zone 5.

### Other Conditions

**Infections**: Antibiotics are the most critical pharmaceutical to stockpile. Fish antibiotics (identical compounds, FDA-regulated in 2023 to require prescriptions in most states — check current status) have been used as contingency supplies. Antibiotic courses include: amoxicillin, amoxicillin-clavulanate, cephalexin, azithromycin, doxycycline, ciprofloxacin, metronidazole. Store in sealed containers in a cool, dark location; many retain potency 2-5 years past labeled expiry. [10]

**Pain management**: Ibuprofen and aspirin have 3-5 year stability. Willow bark (*Salix spp.*) contains salicin (metabolized to salicylic acid); tea or tincture provides moderate analgesia. Dose: 1-2g dried bark, 3x daily.

## 3. Herbal Protocols

Evidence quality for herbal medicine ranges from robust meta-analyses to traditional use with no formal study. The following represents the best-evidenced subset with practical applicability in Zone 5. The SeedWarden Phase 3 documentation covers cultivation protocols for many of these species.

### Wound and Skin

**Yarrow (*Achillea millefolium*)**: A Zone 5 native perennial. Fresh leaves applied directly to cuts and abrasions stop bleeding (hemostatic alkaloids), reduce infection (antimicrobial volatile oils), and reduce inflammation. Also effective as a tea for fevers: acts as a diaphoretic (promotes sweating), particularly in combination with elderflower and peppermint. [11]

**Plantain (*Plantago major* and *lanceolata*)**: The quintessential first aid herb. Apply bruised fresh leaves to insect stings, minor cuts, splinters, and inflamed skin. Anti-inflammatory (aucubin glycoside), mild antimicrobial. Ubiquitous in lawns throughout Zone 5. [12]

**Calendula (*Calendula officinalis*)**: Dried flowers infused in oil produce a salve widely used for wound healing, skin inflammation, and minor burns. Strong evidence for anti-inflammatory action; reduces wound healing time in clinical studies. Easy annual in Zone 5. [13]

**Honey**: Raw, local honey has clinically demonstrated wound-healing properties. Used topically on burns, infected wounds, and pressure ulcers. Manuka honey (medical-grade) shows the strongest evidence; local raw honey is a practical substitute. Do not give to infants under 1 year (botulism risk).

### Immune and Respiratory

**Elderberry (*Sambucus nigra*)**: 2018 meta-analysis found substantial reduction in upper respiratory symptom duration and severity. Syrup preparation: simmer berries 20 minutes, strain, add honey at 1:1 ratio. Dose: 1 tablespoon every 3-4 hours for active illness; 1 tablespoon daily for prevention. Elder grows in Zone 5 hedgerows. Do not consume raw elderberries — they contain sambunigrin (cyanogenic glycoside) that causes nausea; cooking or fermentation removes this. [14]

**Echinacea (*Echinacea purpurea*, *angustifolia*)**: Meta-analyses (Cochrane 2015 update) show modest reduction in cold incidence and duration. Effective as short-course treatment at illness onset; not for continuous daily use. Zone 5 native prairie plant, easily cultivated. Tincture (1:5, 60% alcohol): 3-5ml 3x daily for 7-10 days maximum course. [15]

**Garlic (*Allium sativum*)**: Allicin (active compound released on crushing) is broadly antimicrobial. Clinical evidence for reduction in cold incidence and severity. Dose for acute infection: 2-4 fresh cloves (crushed and allowed to rest 10 minutes before consumption) 2x daily. [16]

### Nervous System and Pain

**Valerian (*Valeriana officinalis*)**: Sedative and anxiolytic herb. Tincture or tea preparation for insomnia and anxiety. Effect is mild-to-moderate; most useful for sleep maintenance. Does not cause next-day grogginess at appropriate doses.

**St. John's Wort (*Hypericum perforatum*)**: Multiple meta-analyses support efficacy for mild-to-moderate depression equivalent to low-dose SSRIs. Not effective for severe depression. Caution: significant drug interactions (reduces effectiveness of many medications via CYP450 induction — not relevant in supply-chain-disrupted context where drug interactions are moot, but note for current-medication users). [17]

**Willow Bark (*Salix spp.*)**: As above. Contains salicin; analgesic and anti-inflammatory. Tea preparation: 1-2 tsp dried bark, steep 20 minutes. Multiple cups per day for ongoing pain management.

## 4. Pediatric Care

Children's physiology differs substantially from adults in ways that affect emergency response:

### Fever Management

**Fever is a symptom, not a disease.** Fever up to 104°F is rarely dangerous in children over 3 months with other normal signs. Fevers serve immune function — routine suppression of low-grade fevers (under 102°F) is not universally recommended by current pediatric guidelines.

**Concerning fever signs** (requiring urgent intervention even without medical access):
- Under 3 months: any fever (rectal temp above 100.4°F) — immature immune systems, risk of serious bacterial infection
- Seizure (febrile convulsion) — mostly benign but requires cooling
- Stiff neck with fever — meningitis until proven otherwise
- Petechial rash (small, non-blanching purple dots) — meningococcal septicemia, life-threatening

**Cooling interventions**: Remove excessive clothing and blankets, cool (not cold) sponge bath, ensure hydration. Aspirin is contraindicated in children under 16 (Reye's syndrome risk). Ibuprofen (approved for children over 6 months) and acetaminophen are the standard.

**Yarrow and elderflower tea** (tepid, diluted) is a traditional pediatric fever herb. Diaphoretic; mild, safe for children over 1 year. Not a replacement for fever management in serious illness. [18]

### Diarrhea and Dehydration

Dehydration from diarrheal illness kills more children globally than any other single cause. The mechanism of death is volume depletion, not the underlying infection.

**Oral rehydration therapy (ORT)**: The WHO ORS formula can be prepared with household ingredients:
- 1 liter of clean water
- 6 teaspoons sugar (glucose preferred, but table sugar works)
- 0.5 teaspoon salt

Give small sips continuously. In severe dehydration (sunken fontanelle, no tears, no urine in 8+ hours), the rate should be 100ml/kg over 3-4 hours. [19]

**Signs requiring medical escalation**: Blood in stool, high fever with diarrhea, child cannot keep any fluids down, signs of severe dehydration unresponsive to ORT.

### Respiratory Illness

**Croup**: A common pediatric viral illness producing the characteristic barking cough and stridor (harsh inhaled breath sound). Management: cool mist humidifier (or steam from hot shower), calm the child (crying worsens airway obstruction), night air (traditional remedy — cold night air reduces subglottic edema). Severe stridor at rest or cyanosis requires emergency intervention.

**Bronchiolitis**: RSV and other viruses, primarily in infants. Supportive care: suctioning nasal secretions (bulb syringe), upright positioning, hydration. Antibiotics are not effective.

## 5. Community Health Organization

Individual training and supplies are insufficient without community coordination:

**Skills matrix**: Map community members' medical training (certified first responders, EMTs, RNs, physicians, CNMs, paramedics, herbalists, veterinarians with overlapping pharmacology knowledge). Build a rotation of available responders.

**Trauma training**: Community members should complete at minimum: Stop the Bleed (FEMA/ACS), Psychological First Aid (Red Cross), and ideally Wilderness First Aid (WFA, 2-day course) or Wilderness First Responder (WFR, 8-day course). These courses are available through NOLS, SOLO, and Wilderness Medical Institutes. [20]

**Medical supply tiers**:
- Tier 1 (every household): CAT tourniquet, Israeli bandage, SAM splint, wound irrigation syringe, sterile gauze, hemostatic gauze, digital thermometer, oral rehydration salts, basic OTC medications (ibuprofen, acetaminophen, antihistamine, antidiarrheal)
- Tier 2 (community cache): Advanced airway kit (BVM, NPA/OPA), IV fluid and lines (with training), suture kit, dental kit, ophthalmology kit, blood pressure cuff, pulse oximeter, stethoscope
- Tier 3 (medical officer): Prescription antibiotics cache, controlled pain medications, insulin, advanced diagnostics [21]

**Herbal pharmacy maintenance**: Reference `phase-3/seedwarden` for cultivation protocols for the medicinal plants listed above. Maintain dried herb stocks with date labels; most dried herbs retain meaningful potency for 1-3 years.

**Documentation**: Maintain individual medical records for each community member, including: known medications, allergies, vaccination history, chronic conditions, blood type. Store copies off-site.
