# Fuel Production & Storage: Firewood, Propane, Generators, and Alternative Fuels in Zone 5

## Overview

Energy resilience is the force multiplier that determines whether all other resilience systems remain operational during grid failure. Zone 5 winters are non-negotiable: without reliable heat, a household can face hypothermia risk within 24-48 hours at peak cold (-20°F to 0°F). Without stored fuel for cooking, food preservation systems that require processing (canning, fermenting, dehydrating) stall. Without generator capacity, communication, refrigeration, and medical equipment fail.

This guide covers the fuel inputs most accessible to Zone 5 households: firewood (primary and most resilient), propane (bridging fuel for transition periods and high-output applications), liquid fuel and generator systems, and emerging alternative fuels (biodiesel, biogas) for communities operating at larger scale.

The microgrid research from Phase 5 Wave 2 (`phase-5-wave-2-microgrids-research.md`) covers the electrical generation and distribution layer. This guide focuses on thermal and combustion fuel systems.

## 1. Firewood: The Primary Zone 5 Thermal Fuel

Wood heat is the most genuinely local, renewable, and supply-chain-independent thermal fuel available to Zone 5 households with forest access. A single mature white oak provides approximately 20-25 cords of wood over its lifetime — enough to heat a well-insulated 1,500 sq ft home for 20-25 winters.

### Species Selection and BTU Values

Hardwood density determines heat output. Dense hardwoods produce more heat per cord, burn longer, and produce better coals for overnight fire maintenance. The following BTU values are per cord (128 cubic feet, fully dried):

| Species | Million BTU/Cord | Notes |
|---------|-----------------|-------|
| Black Locust | 26.8 | Highest heat output; excellent coals; invasive in some Midwest areas but legal to harvest |
| Shagbark Hickory | 24.6-25.5 | Premium heat; long-lasting coals; outstanding wood stove fuel |
| White Oak | 24.0 | Classic all-purpose hardwood; widely available in Midwest |
| Sugar Maple | 24.0 | Excellent coals; widely available in Michigan, Wisconsin, Illinois |
| Red Oak | 21.6 | Common, widely available; burns slightly faster than white oak |
| White Ash | 20.0 | Fast-drying; 6-month seasoning often sufficient; good heat output |
| Beech | 21.8 | Dense, splits with difficulty; excellent heat |
| Black Walnut | 20.0 | Available in Midwest; moderate heat; wood has value, so may be better sold |
| Elm | 19.5 | Difficult to split; interlocked grain; best processed green with hydraulic splitter |
| Cottonwood / Soft Maple | 13-16 | Lower heat; useful as kindling or supplemental wood; poor choice for primary heating |
| Basswood / Poplar | 10-14 | Very low heat output; avoid for primary heating |

Sources: Wisconsin Wood Furnace, Midwest Hearth, Outdoor Boiler [1][2][3]

**Practical rule**: Prioritize white and red oak, hickory, sugar maple, and ash for primary heating wood in Zone 5. These species are native and abundant throughout Illinois, Michigan, Iowa, Indiana, and Wisconsin.

### Seasoning: Time, Method, and Targets

Green wood typically contains 30-60% moisture by weight. Burning green wood wastes 20-40% of the available energy evaporating water rather than producing usable heat, produces excessive creosote (chimney fire risk), and outputs more particulate emissions. The target moisture content for optimal combustion is 15-20%. [4]

**Seasoning timelines**:
- Oak, hickory, maple: 12-18 months minimum (thick bark slows drying)
- Ash, locust: 6-12 months (ash is the fastest-drying hardwood)
- Softwoods (pine, spruce, fir — supplemental use): 6-12 months

The classic principle: cut, split, and stack wood one year before you intend to burn it. In Zone 5, this means harvesting and processing April-June for use the following heating season (November-March).

**Stacking protocol**:
- Stack in single rows (not crisscrossed piles) for maximum airflow
- Elevate from ground on pallets, logs, or concrete blocks to prevent moisture absorption from below
- Cover top only (not sides) with metal roofing, tarps, or a simple lean-to shed
- South or west-facing exposure, not shaded — direct sun dramatically accelerates drying
- Split wood dries 2-3x faster than rounds — split everything to maximum 6-inch diameter [5]

**Moisture measurement**: A moisture meter ($15-30, available at hardware stores) measures moisture content in 10 seconds. Wood below 20% is ready to burn; below 15% is ideal. This is a practical investment that prevents wasted energy and creosote problems.

### Storage Volume Calculations

Zone 5 heating season is approximately 5-6 months (November through March/April). A well-insulated 1,500 sq ft home typically consumes 3-5 cords of hardwood per heating season. An older, poorly insulated structure may consume 8-10 cords. A cord is 4x4x8 feet (128 cubic feet stacked); a "face cord" is 4x8 feet stacked to the length of the specific pieces cut.

**Recommendation**: Maintain a minimum 2-season supply (6-10 cords depending on home size and insulation level). This buffer absorbs poor harvest years, extended cold snaps, and provides the time required for proper seasoning.

### Chimney and Stove Maintenance

Creosote accumulation in chimney flues is the primary fire hazard of wood heating. A chimney sweep (annual, prior to heating season) is not optional — it is maintenance that prevents house fires. If professional service is unavailable:

1. Use a chimney brush of appropriate diameter with extension rods
2. Seal the fireplace opening to capture falling debris
3. Scrub from the top down; clean from below at the cleanout
4. Inspect flue liner for cracks (flashlight and mirror)
5. Burn at high temperatures regularly — low-and-slow smoldering fires produce the most creosote [6]

## 2. Propane: Bridging Fuel and High-Output Applications

Propane (LPG — liquefied petroleum gas) is the most practical liquid fuel for Zone 5 households not on natural gas service. It stores indefinitely (unlike gasoline), is available in tank sizes from 1 lb (camping) to 1,000+ gallon (buried residential tanks), and provides reliable high-output heat for cooking, water heating, and generator backup.

### Tank Sizing and Storage

**500-gallon above-ground or buried tank**: Standard residential size for a whole-home system. At 60 BTU/sq ft Zone 5 heating load, a 2,000 sq ft home may consume 100-200 gallons/month in peak winter. A 500-gallon tank provides 2-5 months of supply. [7]

**100-lb cylinders**: Portable, do not require permanent installation, can be stored in multiples. Each 100-lb cylinder contains approximately 23.6 gallons of propane, filled to 80% capacity for safety (about 18.9 gallons usable). At 30,000 BTU/hr output (typical propane heater), a 100-lb cylinder provides approximately 6-8 hours of continuous operation. [8]

**Storage safety**:
- Store outdoors in well-ventilated areas — never inside buildings, basements, or enclosed garages
- Keep cylinders upright at all times (liquid propane must not enter regulator)
- Maintain clearance: 10 feet from any ignition source, 3 feet from building openings
- Propane is heavier than air and sinks — ensure any storage area cannot accumulate vapors near ground level
- External temperatures between -40°F and 120°F are within operational range; no special winter protection needed beyond keeping valve caps on and preventing physical damage [9]

**Midwest weather consideration**: In tornado and hail regions (all of Zone 5), chain or stake above-ground tanks to prevent displacement. A tank rolling into an ignition source or valve being sheared in high winds creates an immediate emergency.

### Propane Applications Prioritized by Resilience Value

1. **Cooking** (highest priority): An outdoor propane burner or camp stove extends the ability to cook, process, and preserve food during power outages. A single 1-lb cylinder lasts 1-2 hours of cooking; a 20-lb cylinder (barbecue size) lasts 25-30 hours of cooking time.

2. **Water heating**: Tankless propane water heaters can operate without electricity with appropriate ignition systems (battery ignition, not grid-required electronic control).

3. **Heating supplement**: Propane forced-air heaters (Mr. Heater Big Buddy and similar) rated for indoor use provide emergency space heating. Ventilation is required; CO detectors are mandatory.

4. **Generator fuel**: Dual-fuel generators (propane + gasoline) provide fuel flexibility and the indefinite storability advantage of propane over gasoline.

## 3. Generator Systems and Liquid Fuel Management

Generators bridge the gap between grid failure and energy resilience systems (solar, wind, biogas). They are not a long-term solution but an essential short-to-medium-term capability.

### Fuel Stabilization for Gasoline

Gasoline has a shelf life of 3-6 months without treatment. Ethanol-blended gasoline (E10, the current US standard) absorbs moisture, phases out (water and ethanol separate from gasoline), and degrades faster — as little as 30 days in humid conditions. [10]

**Fuel stabilization protocol**:
- Use fuel stabilizer (STA-BIL or PRI-G) at manufacturer's recommended rate; add to fresh fuel and run engine for 5-10 minutes to circulate throughout system
- Store in sealed, approved containers away from heat sources
- Use ethanol-free premium gasoline when available (marinas, aviation suppliers) — significantly longer shelf life
- Rotate stock every 6-12 months: use oldest fuel in equipment, replace with fresh
- For stored generators not in use: run monthly for 15-20 minutes under load to prevent carburetor varnishing; drain fuel entirely for storage over 30-60 days

**Treated gasoline**: STA-BIL rated for 24 months; PRI-G rated for 2+ years with annual re-treatment. These are the longest realistic gasoline storage windows in Zone 5 humidity conditions. [11]

### Generator Maintenance Schedule

Deferred maintenance is the leading cause of generator failure at the moment of need. Maintenance schedule for a 5,000-7,000 watt generator:

- **Every 50 hours or annually**: Change engine oil (SAE 30 or 10W-30 per manufacturer spec for temperature range), inspect and clean or replace air filter, inspect spark plug (replace if electrode worn or deposits present)
- **Every 100 hours or every 2 years**: Replace spark plugs, replace fuel filter, clean carburetor (if ethanol fuel used)
- **Annual**: Inspect and clean battery terminals (electric start models), test under load, check all electrical connections, test automatic transfer switch if present [12]

**Oil for Zone 5 cold starts**: SAE 30 oil does not flow adequately in sub-40°F conditions. Use 5W-30 or 10W-30 multi-grade oil for generators stored outdoors or in unheated spaces. Attempting to cold-start with SAE 30 at 20°F can damage the engine.

### Load Management and Fuel Efficiency

A generator running at 25-50% load consumes nearly as much fuel as at 75-90% load, with far less useful output per gallon. Concentrate generator use into "power windows" where high-draw appliances (well pump, refrigerator, chest freezer) run simultaneously rather than cycling the generator continuously at low load. A chest freezer (run 2-3 hours) will maintain temperature for 8-12 hours with the lid closed; batch refrigerator access similarly.

## 4. Alternative Fuels: Community-Scale Options

### Biogas

Small-scale biogas production converts livestock manure and food waste into methane-rich gas suitable for cooking, lighting (in gas lamps), and potentially running modified generators.

A single dairy cow produces approximately 85 cubic feet of biogas per day from daily manure — roughly 3 hours of cooking fuel. For a community with 5-10 cattle, a properly sized anaerobic digester provides meaningful cooking gas. [13]

**Construction**: A simple plug-flow digester consists of:
- Inlet tank (mixing waste with water at 1:1 ratio)
- Sealed digestion chamber (polyethylene tank, concrete vault, or HDPE pipe)
- Gas collection (bladder or water-seal system)
- Outlet tank (effluent — high-quality liquid fertilizer)

Digestion requires temperatures above 59°F for mesophilic bacteria; Zone 5 winters make year-round biogas production challenging without insulation or supplemental heating. Covered digesters in greenhouses or buried below frost line (approximately 4 feet in Zone 5) can operate year-round. [14]

**Safety**: Biogas is approximately 60% methane — explosive. Install gas leak detectors near any indoor gas connections. Do not store compressed biogas without professional engineering guidance.

### Biodiesel

Biodiesel can be produced from waste vegetable oil collected from restaurants, lard, tallow, or seed oil crops (sunflower, canola) grown on-site. At community scale, a simple transesterification process (combining oil with methanol and lye catalyst) produces biodiesel suitable for unmodified diesel engines (B20 blends) or modified engines running B100.

**Minimum equipment**: Stainless steel reaction tank, wash station, storage containers. Safety requirements: methanol is toxic and flammable; sodium hydroxide (lye) is caustic; methoxide (the catalyst solution) is extremely dangerous. Personal protective equipment (face shield, chemical-resistant gloves, apron) is mandatory. [15]

**Yield**: 1 gallon of waste vegetable oil produces approximately 0.9 gallons of biodiesel. At 200 gallons/year input (from a small restaurant or community food processing), approximately 180 gallons of biodiesel is achievable — significant fuel for tractor, generator, or vehicle use.

**Zone 5 cold weather note**: Biodiesel gels at higher temperatures than petroleum diesel. B20 blends gel around 0°F to -10°F; B100 gels around 32°F. Cold-weather additives or winter blending with petroleum diesel is required for Zone 5 operation below freezing. [16]

### Wood Gasification

Wood gasifiers convert dry biomass into producer gas (syngas — CO and H2 mixture) that can run modified gasoline engines. A 10-15 kW wood gasifier system can run a generator producing meaningful electrical output from locally-grown biomass.

Technology is proven (FEMA published wood gasifier plans in 1989) but requires significant engineering and operational knowledge. More appropriate for community-scale implementation than individual households. [17]

## 5. Zone 5 Heating Load and Fuel Planning

**Heating degree days (HDD)**: Zone 5 Midwest locations typically accumulate 5,000-7,000 HDD annually (base 65°F). Chicago averages 6,155 HDD; Des Moines 6,588 HDD; Grand Rapids, MI 6,894 HDD.

**Estimating annual fuel consumption**: A home requiring 60,000 BTU/hour at design temperature (-10°F to -20°F for Zone 5), with 70% heating system efficiency, needs approximately 200-300 MMBtu of heat energy annually. This translates to:
- Firewood: 10-15 cords of green hardwood (7-10 cords seasoned hardwood at 20 MMBtu/cord)
- Propane: 2,000-3,000 gallons (91,500 BTU/gallon at ~70% furnace efficiency)
- Natural gas: 200-300 MCF (thousand cubic feet)

**Resilience planning recommendation**:
1. Primary heat: Wood (renewable, local, indefinitely available with forest access)
2. Backup heat: Propane (6-12 month supply, 250-500 gallon tank minimum)
3. Cooking backup: Propane camp stoves + wood cookstove or rocket stove
4. Electrical bridging: Dual-fuel generator with 50-gallon gasoline + propane option [18]
