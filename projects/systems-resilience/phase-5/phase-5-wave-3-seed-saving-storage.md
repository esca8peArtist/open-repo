# Seed Saving & Storage: Open-Pollinated Varieties, Protocols, and Zone 5 Timing

## Overview

Seed saving is the foundation of food sovereignty. A household or community that controls its own seed supply is insulated from commercial seed market disruptions, supply chain failures, or corporate variety discontinuation. In Zone 5 (USDA hardiness zones 5a-5b, covering most of Illinois, Michigan, Iowa, Indiana, and Wisconsin), the growing season runs approximately 150-180 frost-free days, with last spring frost dates from April 15 to May 10 and first fall frost from October 1 to October 15 depending on microclimate.

This guide covers the principles for selecting open-pollinated varieties suited to Zone 5, managing cross-pollination, applying fermentation and dry-processing protocols, testing viability, and maintaining storage conditions that extend seed longevity across decades.

---

## 1. Why Open-Pollinated Varieties

**Open-pollinated (OP) varieties** reproduce true-to-type when pollinated by the same variety. Their offspring carry the same genetic characteristics as the parent plant. This is the biological foundation of seed saving.

**Hybrid (F1) seeds** are produced by crossing two distinct parent lines. The first generation (F1) expresses hybrid vigor but produces offspring (F2) that segregate unpredictably — you may get plants that resemble either parent or neither. Saving seeds from F1 hybrids does not reliably reproduce the desired plant. [1]

**Heirloom varieties** are open-pollinated varieties with documented histories, often more than 50 years of continuous selection. Many carry disease resistances, flavor profiles, or productivity characteristics lost in commercial breeding programs focused on shelf life and mechanical harvest. The Seed Savers Exchange (Decorah, Iowa — Zone 5) maintains one of the world's largest heirloom seed libraries and is a primary resource for Zone 5 growers. [2]

**What to prioritize** for a Zone 5 resilience seed library:
- Short-season varieties (days-to-maturity that fit within frost dates with margin)
- Demonstrated local adaptability over multiple seasons
- Varieties selected for open-pollination and vigor over many generations
- Disease resistance relevant to Midwest conditions (bacterial wilt, early blight, powdery mildew)

## 2. Cross-Pollination Prevention

Not all vegetables require the same isolation strategy. Understanding pollination biology determines how much effort is required to maintain seed purity.

### Self-Pollinating Crops (Easiest to Save)

These crops pollinate themselves before flowers fully open, minimizing risk of cross-pollination. They can be grown in proximity to other varieties with minimal precaution:

- Tomatoes: 10-25 feet between varieties for practical purity (some bee-assisted crossing occurs)
- Beans (dry and snap): 10-25 feet for home-scale purity
- Peas: 10-25 feet
- Lettuce: 12-25 feet
- Peppers: technically self-pollinating but insects will cross them; bag flowers or isolate by 150-300 feet for pure seed [3]

### Insect-Pollinated Crops (Require Distance or Caging)

These crops require insects to transfer pollen and will readily cross between varieties:

- **Squash (Cucurbita pepo, maxima, moschata)**: Varieties within the same species cross freely. C. pepo includes zucchini, acorn, delicata, and most pumpkins; saving seed from one C. pepo type in a field of another will produce crosses. Isolation distance: 1/4 to 1/2 mile for reliable purity; alternatively, hand-pollinate and bag flowers before insects visit. [4]
- **Cucumbers**: 1/4 mile isolation or hand-pollination
- **Melons**: 1/4 mile (cantaloupes and other muskmelons cross; watermelons are a different genus and do not cross with muskmelons)
- **Brassicas (broccoli, cabbage, kale, kohlrabi, Brussels sprouts)**: All Brassica oleracea species cross freely. Biennial (require two years to set seed in Zone 5 — must overwinter as roots or be protected). Isolation 1,000 feet or more between varieties.

### Wind-Pollinated Crops (Require Significant Distance)

- **Corn**: Pollen travels 1/4 mile or more on wind. Isolation distance: 1/4 to 2 miles for pure seed, or temporal isolation (stagger planting by 3+ weeks so silk emergence does not overlap with pollen shed from another variety). Minimum population of 200-400 plants per variety to maintain genetic diversity and prevent inbreeding depression. [5]
- **Spinach**: 1/2 mile isolation; biennial
- **Beets/chard**: 1 mile isolation; cross with each other

### Practical Strategies for Small Gardens

When isolation distances are not achievable:
1. **Cage isolation**: Cover individual plants or groups with floating row cover (Agribon-19 or equivalent) from bud formation through flower close. Remove to allow pollination by hand, then re-cover. Works for cucumbers, squash, peppers, brassicas.
2. **Blossom bagging**: Place small fabric or paper bags over individual flowers before opening. Hand-pollinate, then re-bag. Practical for tomatoes, peppers, eggplant.
3. **Population selection**: For wind-pollinated crops like corn, maintain as large a population as practical and accept minor crossing — then select intensively for desired traits each generation.
4. **Single-variety rule**: Grow only one variety of each cross-susceptible species per season for seed saving. Grow other varieties in alternate years. [6]

## 3. Seed Harvest and Processing Protocols

### Dry-Processed Seeds

Most vegetables produce dry seeds that can be harvested and processed without fermentation:

**Harvest timing**: Allow seeds to mature fully on the plant — past eating stage. Most dry seeds are ready when pods or seed heads are brown and papery. If frost threatens before maturity, pull entire plants and hang upside down indoors for 2-4 weeks to allow seeds to finish maturing using plant reserves.

**Threshing and cleaning**:
1. Spread seed heads or pods on a tarp or old sheet
2. Flail with a stick or walk on material to break pods
3. Winnow: pour material in front of a fan or gentle breeze; seeds (heavier) fall to ground while chaff blows away
4. Screen through mesh of appropriate size to separate debris
5. Spread cleaned seeds on screens or paper towels; dry at room temperature (65-80°F) for 2-4 weeks until seeds snap rather than bend when folded [7]

**Crops using dry processing**: Beans, peas, corn, onion, leek, carrot, parsnip, beets, chard, brassicas, flowers

### Fermentation Protocol (Wet Seeds)

Fermentation is required for tomatoes and recommended for cucumbers. The process removes the gel coat surrounding seeds (which contains germination inhibitors and harbors some seed-borne pathogens) and mimics natural decomposition in the fruit.

**Tomato seed fermentation**:
1. Squeeze seeds and gel into a glass jar or container
2. Add equal volume of water (or leave as-is if pulp is abundant)
3. Stir 2-3 times daily; leave at 65-75°F (room temperature)
4. Within 48-72 hours, a layer of white or gray mold develops on the surface and dead/hollow seeds float
5. Living, viable seeds sink to the bottom
6. At 72 hours maximum, pour off floating material plus mold layer
7. Rinse viable seeds 3-4 times with clean water in a fine mesh strainer
8. Spread on a glass plate, ceramic tile, or wax paper to dry (do not use paper towels — seeds stick)
9. Dry 2-3 weeks in a single layer; stir daily for first week [8]

Do not ferment longer than 72-96 hours — germination rates drop sharply as fermentation continues. Temperature matters: at 85°F, 48 hours is sufficient; at 65°F, 72 hours may be needed.

**Cucumber seed processing**: Ferment 1-2 days only (cucumber seeds are more vulnerable to over-fermentation). Alternatively, wash seeds directly in a strainer under running water, rubbing away gel, and dry immediately.

## 4. Viability Testing

Before relying on stored seeds for a critical planting season, test germination rates.

**Standard paper towel germination test**:
1. Count out 10 or 25 seeds from the lot to be tested
2. Dampen a paper towel (not soaking wet — mold will develop)
3. Lay seeds in a row, fold towel over, place in a zip-lock bag with 2-3 pinholes
4. Keep at consistent warm temperature (65-75°F for most crops; 75-85°F for warm-season crops)
5. Check daily from minimum germination time:
   - Beans, corn, squash: check from day 5
   - Tomatoes, peppers: check from day 7
   - Parsley, carrots: check from day 14
6. Count germinated seeds at appropriate days (see crop-specific tables) [9]

**Interpretation**:
- 80-100%: excellent vigor; plant at standard rate
- 50-79%: acceptable; increase seeding rate by 25-50%
- Below 50%: marginal; plant densely and thin, or obtain fresh seed
- Below 20%: discard and replace [10]

**Iowa State University Extension** recommends testing seeds before each planting season, particularly for onions, parsnips, and parsley — species with naturally short seed longevity. [11]

## 5. Storage Conditions and Longevity

Seed longevity follows two core principles documented in USDA seed science research:

**Rule of thumb**: The sum of temperature (°F) plus relative humidity (%) should be less than 100 for safe long-term storage. A room at 60°F with 35% relative humidity (sum = 95) provides good preservation. A room at 75°F with 60% relative humidity (sum = 135) destroys seeds quickly. [12]

**Doubling rule**: For each 10°F increase in temperature, seed longevity is approximately halved. For each 1% increase in seed moisture content, longevity is halved. This means proper drying before storage is as important as temperature control.

### Target Conditions by Storage Tier

**Tier 1 — Short-term (1-3 years)**: Cool, dark, dry room. Sealed glass jars with silica gel desiccant packets. Temperatures of 50-65°F. Suitable for: active use seeds, seeds rotated annually.

**Tier 2 — Medium-term (3-7 years)**: Refrigerator at 35-40°F. Seeds must be completely dry before refrigerator storage (refrigerators are humid — unsealed containers will absorb moisture). Use vacuum-sealed mason jars or Mylar bags with desiccant. Place jars in secondary plastic bag before refrigeration to prevent condensation when removed. [13]

**Tier 3 — Long-term (10-50+ years)**: Freezer at 0°F or below. Seeds must be dried to 8% moisture content or below (achieved by conditioning with desiccant for 1-2 weeks before freezing). Professional seed banks use -18°C (-0.4°F) with 15% relative humidity. Home freezers at 0°F achieve meaningful decades-long storage for most crops when seeds are properly dried.

### Physical Storage Formats

**Sealed glass mason jars**: Best for medium-long storage. Airtight when properly sealed with new lids. Add a 10g silica gel packet per quart jar; replace desiccants when indicator shows saturation (typically color change from orange/blue to white).

**Mylar bags with oxygen absorbers**: For long-term cache storage. Seal with heat impulse sealer or household iron. Label permanently (Sharpie on exterior; pencil on paper insert inside bag — ink fades inside Mylar).

**Paper envelopes in sealed outer container**: Appropriate only for short-term (1-2 year) storage in consistently cool-dry conditions. Allows seeds to breathe but provides no moisture protection.

**Avoid**: Plastic bags (not moisture-proof long-term), humid basements, attics (extreme temperature swings), any area with temperature exceeding 75°F regularly.

## 6. Zone 5 Timing and Inheritance Planning

### Zone 5 Seed Production Calendar

Successful seed saving requires understanding that seed maturity requires additional time beyond eating maturity:

| Crop | Eating Maturity | Seed Maturity (after eating) | Zone 5 Direct Sow |
|------|----------------|------------------------------|-------------------|
| Tomato | Mid-July | August-September | Transplant May 15 |
| Pepper | August | September-October | Transplant May 20 |
| Corn | Late July | Late September | May 1-15 |
| Winter Squash | September | Full season; harvest Oct | May 15 |
| Bean (dry) | August | September-October | May 15 |
| Cucumber | July | August | May 20 |

For biennials (crops that produce seed in their second year): carrots, beets, parsley, leeks, and all brassicas must survive a Zone 5 winter. Strategies:
- Dig roots in fall, store in cool root cellar over winter, replant in spring
- Mulch heavily in place (works for kale, parsley, and some carrots in Zone 5b)
- Construct cold frames or low tunnels to extend survival [17]

### Inheritance and Community Seed Planning

A resilience seed library is a community asset requiring governance:

**Minimum viable collection** (for a household food garden):
- 10-15 core crops with 2-3 varieties each
- 100-300 seeds per variety (enough for a season's planting plus saving)
- Backup copies in separate physical locations

**Community seed library model** (for a village or intentional community):
- Distribute varieties across multiple seed keepers
- Annual seed swap events to refresh library, share varieties, and build collective knowledge
- Document growing notes, harvest dates, selection criteria, and any observed disease resistance per variety
- Designate a seed librarian responsible for tracking inventory and viability dates [18]

**Documentation**: For each variety maintained, record:
- Variety name and source
- Year seeds were harvested
- Germination rate at last test
- Growing observations (disease resistance, exceptional yield, flavor)
- Cross-pollination precautions needed
- Storage location and format

This documentation system is the inheritance mechanism — the knowledge that makes seeds viable across generations is inseparable from the seeds themselves.
