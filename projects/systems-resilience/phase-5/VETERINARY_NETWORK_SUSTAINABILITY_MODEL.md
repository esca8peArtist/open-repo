# Veterinary Network Sustainability Model
## Revenue, Grants, Financing, Staffing, and Long-Term Viability

> **Purpose**: Define how a community veterinary network funds itself at launch and sustains operations beyond grant dependency. Covers revenue stream design, grant funding inventory, equipment financing, staffing schedules, and the indicators that distinguish a viable network from an unsustainable one.

---

## Lead Finding

Community veterinary networks die in one of three ways: the hub DVM leaves and no succession plan exists; grant funding ends and no revenue base has been built; or equipment degrades because no one owns maintenance. Every sustainability design choice should be evaluated against these three failure modes. A network with a modest but diversified revenue base (membership fees + equipment rental + training fees), a DVM succession plan, and a funded Equipment Steward role is more sustainable than a better-funded network without these structural elements.

The most important insight from the East Africa CAHW literature (30+ years operational): fee-for-service models survive; grant-dependent volunteer models do not. The U.S. rural health cooperative precedent confirms this: agricultural cooperatives have sustained member-owned service infrastructure for over a century precisely because member ownership aligns incentives. The veterinary network's sustainability model is ultimately a cooperative economics problem, not a grant-writing problem.

## Part 1: Revenue Streams

### Revenue Stream 1: Membership Fees

The network's base revenue. Every livestock-keeping household in the network pays an annual membership fee that funds the overhead structure (Equipment Steward, insurance, maintenance, platform upkeep) and contributes to the DVM retainer or network salary.

**Fee design principles**:
- Fees must be affordable for the actual income profile of rural households — median household income in rural Zone 5 counties is $45,000–$65,000
- Tiers based on species count and complexity, not household income, make the fee structure transparent and non-stigmatizing
- Member households receive: VCPR establishment, telehealth access, discounted equipment access, CAHW first-contact service, network platform access

**Suggested fee tiers**:

| Tier | Profile | Annual Fee | Monthly Equivalent |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tier 1: Companion animals only (dogs/cats) | Households with no livestock | $150–$250 | $12.50–$20.83 |
| Tier 2: Small flock/herd (<20 animals) | Backyard chickens, goats, rabbits | $350–$500 | $29–$42 |
| Tier 3: Medium flock/herd (20–100 animals) | Active homestead | $600–$900 | $50–$75 |
| Tier 4: Large herd (100+ animals) | Commercial small farm | $1,200–$2,000 | $100–$167 |

**Revenue projection by network size**:
- 20-member micro-rural network (mix): $8,000–$12,000/year
- 60-member small rural network (mix): $25,000–$40,000/year
- 200-member large rural network (mix): $90,000–$140,000/year

### Revenue Stream 2: Equipment Rental to Non-Members

Network equipment (ultrasound, portable scale, diagnostic tools) can be rented to non-member livestock keepers in the region at market rates. The network subsidizes member access; non-members pay full rate.

**Rental rate examples**:
- Handheld ultrasound: $75–$150/day to non-members ($25–$40 to members)
- Portable livestock scale: $40–$80/day to non-members
- Obstetric kit loan: $30–$60/day to non-members (deposit required)
- Mobile clinic visit (non-member): market rate for mobile vet services in region ($75–$200/visit depending on services)

**Projected equipment rental revenue**: Modest in small networks ($3,000–$8,000/year); more significant in large networks with high-value equipment ($15,000–$40,000/year).

### Revenue Stream 3: Training and Education Fees

The network's CAHW training program is a service that has value beyond the network itself. Adjacent livestock-keeping communities, county extension programs, 4-H programs, and agricultural high schools may pay for CAHW training access.

**Fee structure**:
- CAHW basic course (6 weeks): $300–$500/participant (covering instructor time and materials)
- FAMACHA workshop (1 day): $75–$100/participant (ACSRPC standard rates)
- Species-specific workshops (e.g., backyard poultry health day): $25–$50/participant
- CAHW annual refresher day: free to network members; $50/day to non-members

**Projected training revenue**: $5,000–$20,000/year depending on training demand in region.

### Revenue Stream 4: Knowledge Platform Licensing

Once the network's knowledge platform is developed and refined with real cases, it has value to other networks. Licensing the platform content (decision trees, protocols, species guides) to other community networks or agricultural extension programs generates revenue without adding operational overhead.

**Fee structure**: $500–$2,000/year for platform content license to another community network; $5,000–$15,000 for extension program licensing. This revenue stream is a Year 3+ opportunity — the platform needs 12–24 months of real-world refinement before it is licensable.

### Revenue Stream 5: Telehealth/Consultation Fees

The hub DVM's telehealth consultations for VCPR-established member households can be offered at below-market rates to members (driving membership value) and market rates to non-members. With 8 states now having explicit telehealth law, and the landscape evolving quarterly:

- Member telehealth consult: $25–$40 per consultation (vs. $50+ market rate for platforms like VetTriage)
- Non-member tele-advice: refer to platforms like VetTriage ($50/call) and collect a referral acknowledgment fee

### Revenue Stream 6: Grant and Charitable Funding

Grant funding should be treated as startup capital and capacity-building fuel, not operating revenue. A network that requires ongoing grant renewal to meet payroll is structurally fragile. Design the revenue model to be grant-independent by Year 3.

**Appropriate uses of grant funding**:
- Initial equipment purchase (capital investment, not recurring)
- Initial CAHW training program development
- First-year salary support during ramp-up (before membership base is established)
- Technology platform development

## Part 2: Grant Funding Inventory

### Tier 1: Federal Agricultural Grants (Highest Priority)

**USDA NIFA Veterinary Services Grant Program (VSGP)**
- Two types: Rural Practice Enhancement (RPE) and Education, Extension, and Training (EET)
- RPE award range: $125,000–$200,000
- EET award range: $250,000–$300,000
- FY2026 total appropriation: $3,809,708 (up from prior years due to Rural Veterinary Action Plan)
- FY2026 applications due: April 16, 2026 (annual deadline; check NIFA for future cycles)
- Eligible uses (RPE): equipment, overhead expenses, mobile veterinary facilities, telehealth infrastructure
- Eligible uses (EET): training programs, recruitment, placement, curriculum development
- Key requirement: must be in or serving a USDA-designated shortage area (245 areas across 47 states as of 2026)
- Source: https://www.nifa.usda.gov/grants/programs/veterinary-services-grant-program

**USDA NIFA Veterinary Medicine Loan Repayment Program (VMLRP)**
- For individual DVMs, not networks — but the network's hub DVM is the beneficiary, reducing the salary premium the network must offer
- Up to $25,000/year for 3 years ($75,000 total)
- Rural Veterinary Workforce Act (H.R. 2398/S. 1163) would make awards tax-exempt, increasing effective value by ~22%
- Applications accepted annually; check NIFA calendar
- Source: https://www.nifa.usda.gov/grants/programs/veterinary-medicine-loan-repayment-program

**USDA Rural Development Programs**
- Rural Business Development Grants: for development of small rural businesses; cooperatives eligible
- Rural Cooperative Development Grants: specifically supports rural cooperative development
- Community Facilities Grants and Loans: for essential community services including health care; veterinary cooperatives may qualify in shortage areas
- Source: https://www.rd.usda.gov/programs-services/all-programs

**USDA NIFA Rural Health and Safety Education Program**
- Supports education programs for rural populations on human and animal health issues
- Community veterinary education programs (CAHW training, biosecurity education) are eligible
- Award range: up to $100,000/year
- Source: https://www.nifa.usda.gov/grants/programs/rural-health-safety-education

### Tier 2: State Agricultural and Rural Development Grants

**State Department of Agriculture programs**
- Nebraska $150,000 rural veterinary grant (April 2025): the highest state-level incentive currently documented
- Iowa Farm Bureau loan repayment program: specific to Iowa; targets new graduates
- Wisconsin loan repayment bill (in development)
- Kansas: KDA assessment report (July 2025) suggests state-level action forthcoming
- Oklahoma: collaborative approach framework in development (May 2025)
- Check state DOA for current programs; landscape is shifting rapidly in response to the shortage crisis

**State Rural Development Authorities**
- Most states have a rural economic development authority or equivalent; cooperative formation and essential services are typically eligible
- Research state-specific programs through USDA Rural Development state offices: https://www.rd.usda.gov/newsroom/federal-funding-opportunities

### Tier 3: Private Foundation and Industry Grants

**Zoetis Foundation**
- Primary funder backing Farm Journal Foundation's State Readiness Program
- Focuses on rural veterinary workforce and access; community programs are well-aligned
- No open grant program identified in research; approach through Farm Journal Foundation partnership or direct inquiry
- Contact: https://www.farmjournalfoundation.org/veterinary-workforce-program

**AVMA Foundation**
- Supports veterinary education, rural access, and profession sustainability
- Community training programs and rural access projects are eligible
- Source: https://www.avmf.org/

**Cargill Foundation, Land O'Lakes Foundation, and ag sector foundations**
- Agricultural sector foundations from major agribusiness companies regularly fund rural community programs
- Veterinary access programs that support agricultural community resilience are well-aligned with their missions
- These are relationship-based funding channels; approach through Farm Bureau and state DOA connections

**Local and regional community foundations**
- Many rural counties have community foundations with open grant programs
- Awards are smaller ($5,000–$25,000) but accessible and relationship-driven
- A network's initial CAHW training program is a compelling proposal for a community foundation

## Part 3: Equipment Financing

### Buy vs. Lease Decision Summary (Detailed Analysis in Equipment Model)

**For initial network launch**: Maximize grant funding for capital equipment purchase; buying new has better long-term unit economics than leasing if the equipment will be used for 7+ years and maintained properly.

**For networks with capital constraints**: Lease with option to buy for the first 2–3 years. Monthly lease cost ($2,000–$3,500 for a mobile clinical unit) is budget-predictable and avoids the large capital outlay that requires grant timing alignment.

**For specific high-value equipment**: Leasing through manufacturer programs (Antech Diagnostics offers ultrasound equipment programs; GE Healthcare offers veterinary imaging leases) sometimes includes maintenance agreements — converting unpredictable maintenance costs into predictable monthly expenses.

### Equipment Financing Timetable by Network Size

**Micro-rural network (5K population)**
- Year 1: VSGP RPE grant ($125,000–$200,000) funds initial equipment purchase + mobile clinic conversion; operating cash from membership fees
- Year 2: Equipment fully owned; operating on membership revenue + equipment rental
- Year 3: Evaluate technology upgrade or expansion based on case volume and revenue

**Small rural network (15K population)**
- Year 1: VSGP RPE + EET grants (up to $500,000 combined); initial equipment purchase + mobile unit + CAHW training program
- Year 2: Operating on membership + rental + training fees; target financial self-sufficiency
- Year 3: If not financially self-sufficient, evaluate fee structure, not grant renewal

**Large rural network (50K+ population)**
- Year 1–2: Multi-source capital raise (grants + member capitalization + cooperative loans)
- Year 3: Operating revenue should cover 80%+ of annual operating costs
- Year 5: Full financial self-sufficiency target

### Member Capitalization

In a cooperative structure, members can be asked for a buy-in contribution at founding — $500–$2,000 per member household. For a 100-member cooperative this generates $50,000–$200,000 in initial capital without grants. This is the traditional agricultural cooperative formation model (grain elevators, credit unions, Farm Credit) that has sustained rural cooperative infrastructure for over a century. The capital buy-in also creates genuine member ownership incentive — members who have invested money take the cooperative's sustainability more seriously.

## Part 4: Staff Scheduling and Capacity Planning

### DVM Scheduling Model

A hub DVM's time is the network's most constrained resource. Scheduling must protect the DVM's capacity for high-value functions (complex case handling, telehealth supervision, VCPR establishment) while delegating everything else.

**Weekly time allocation model (40-hour work week)**:

| Function | Hours/Week | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Direct patient care (farm visits, clinic) | 20–25 | Core revenue-generating; can be partly delegated to CVTs |
| Telehealth consultations (member/satellite) | 4–6 | Scalable; can batch schedule |
| Case record review (CAHW documentation) | 3–5 | Non-negotiable; this is the feedback loop |
| VCPR establishment visits (new members) | 2–4 | Front-loaded in network launch; decreasing over time |
| Protocol review and platform updates | 1–2 | Quarterly intensive vs. weekly light |
| Training and education (CAHW, CVT) | 2–3 | Front-loaded in training periods |
| Administration | 1–3 | Keep minimal; this is not the DVM's function |

**On-call coverage**: The hub DVM cannot be on-call 24/7/365 alone — this is the primary burnout driver in solo rural practice. Network design must address on-call sharing:
- Reciprocal on-call agreement with adjacent veterinary practices
- After-hours telehealth routing to VetTriage ($50/call for members who need immediate triage outside DVM hours) as a first line
- CAHW network as emergency triage layer — well-trained CAHWs prevent many 2 a.m. calls by accurately triaging "this can wait until morning"

**DVM succession planning**: The network's biggest existential risk is the hub DVM leaving without a replacement. Succession planning must begin at Year 1:
- Document the DVM's clinical protocols, client relationships, and institutional knowledge
- Identify a DVM candidate in Year 2 for shadowing/mentorship
- Build a succession timeline into the network's governance documents
- Consider a two-DVM hub structure once revenue supports it — shared call schedule and built-in redundancy

### CVT Scheduling

Satellite CVTs operate on a defined schedule that matches the service demand of their satellite area. Key scheduling principles:

- **Predictable scheduled clinic days** at the satellite location (e.g., Tuesday and Thursday, 9 a.m.–4 p.m.) enable households to plan around veterinary access rather than experience it as unpredictable
- **Mobile farm visits** on alternate days for households that cannot transport animals
- **Emergency callback** protocol: satellite CVT is available by phone during business hours; after-hours non-emergencies go to next-day appointment; after-hours emergencies route to hub DVM on-call

**CVT capacity planning**:
- A full-time satellite CVT can reasonably serve 25–40 active member households (livestock-keeping) with bi-weekly contact
- Companion animal-only members require less frequent contact; a CVT can handle 50–75 companion animal members alongside 25–40 livestock members

### CAHW Capacity Planning

Each CAHW serves a defined geographic cluster — "their households" — with the expectation of:
- Monthly farm visit to each assigned household (preventive observation, documentation)
- Response within 4 hours to any emergency contact from their assigned households
- Participation in quarterly network training days

**Capacity per CAHW**: 8–15 actively livestock-keeping households within practical travel distance (typically 15–20 mile radius in rural Zone 5). A CAHW serving 12 households at monthly visit = 12 visits/month + emergency response calls.

**Total network coverage**: A network with 15 CAHWs each covering 10 households reaches 150 livestock-keeping households — the Zone 5 equivalent of approximately 750–1,500 animals (depending on species mix and herd size).

## Part 5: Financial Sustainability Indicators

### The Minimum Viable Revenue Test

A community veterinary network is financially sustainable when:

1. **Membership revenue covers at least 60% of annual operating costs** — reduces grant dependency to capacity-building supplement rather than operating necessity
2. **Equipment maintenance fund** is separately capitalized at 10–15% of equipment value per year — avoids deferred maintenance failures
3. **DVM succession reserve** exists — at minimum, 3 months of DVM salary in reserve to cover transition when the hub DVM leaves
4. **No single revenue source exceeds 40% of total revenue** — diversification as a resilience principle

### Year 1–3 Financial Trajectory

**Year 1 targets** (grant-heavy, revenue-building):
- Grant funding: 60–70% of operating budget
- Membership revenue: 20–30% of operating budget
- Other earned revenue: 5–10%
- Goal: Establish VCPR with all member households; full CAHW cohort trained; equipment deployed

**Year 2 targets** (transition phase):
- Grant funding: 40–50% of operating budget
- Membership revenue: 35–45%
- Other earned revenue: 10–15%
- Goal: Equipment rental and training revenue established; second CAHW training cohort complete

**Year 3 targets** (financial self-sufficiency goal):
- Grant funding: <25% of operating budget (equipment upgrades only, not operations)
- Membership revenue: 50–60%
- Other earned revenue: 20–30%
- Goal: Annual operating costs covered by earned revenue without grant dependency

### Signs a Network Is Heading Toward Failure

The following indicators should trigger a governance review before crisis:

- DVM turnover without succession plan activated
- Equipment maintenance deferred more than one cycle
- Membership attrition above 15%/year without replacement
- CAHW case documentation rate below 70% (indicates disengagement)
- Grant funding covering more than 50% of operating budget in Year 3+
- Hub DVM on-call more than 5 nights/week (burnout precursor)
- Any case of unlicensed practice allegation (legal risk that requires immediate response)

## Part 6: Failure Mode Analysis and Mitigation

### Failure Mode 1: Hub DVM Leaves

*Probability*: High if succession planning is not built in from Year 1. Rural veterinary turnover is well-documented.

*Impact*: Network loses clinical authority, VCPR establishment, prescription access, and telehealth supervision simultaneously. The network cannot legally operate most clinical functions without a DVM anchor.

*Mitigation*:
- Document all protocols, client relationships, and VCPR records in network files (not DVM's personal records)
- Succession candidate identified and in relationship with network by Year 2
- Interim cover: veterinary school clinical faculty, extension veterinarians, or adjacent practice can provide hub function for 30–90 day bridge
- Contract structure: DVM employment agreement includes 90-day notice period and succession cooperation clause

### Failure Mode 2: Grant Funding Ends Without Revenue Base

*Probability*: High if membership fee structure is under-designed or not enforced.

*Impact*: Network cannot fund Equipment Steward, maintenance, insurance, or DVM salary supplement.

*Mitigation*:
- Membership fees are non-negotiable from Day 1 — do not offer free membership to build enrollment; this establishes a precedent of non-payment
- Build earned revenue (rental, training) before grants end
- Maintain a 6-month operating reserve from the first VSGP grant proceeds

### Failure Mode 3: Equipment Degradation

*Probability*: High if Equipment Steward role is unfilled or unfunded.

*Impact*: Network equipment becomes unreliable; members lose trust; clinical capability degrades before anyone notices.

*Mitigation*:
- Equipment Steward role is filled before network launches, even if volunteer
- Maintenance fund is separately capitalized from the first grant
- Annual equipment audit with documented results; replace or repair immediately

### Failure Mode 4: CAHW Attrition

*Probability*: Moderate if CAHWs are unpaid volunteers with no career development path.

*Impact*: First-contact layer disappears; hub DVM and CVTs absorb the load; hub burns out faster.

*Mitigation*:
- Fee-for-service or stipend structure from Day 1; do not rely on purely volunteer CAHWs
- Annual refresher training as a community event (not just a training obligation)
- Clear career development path: CAHW → advanced CAHW → CVT bridge program for motivated individuals
- CAHW advisory seat on network governance board — ownership culture reduces attrition
