### 1A. Community Economic Resilience

**The problem this domain solves**

Phase 5 documents how communities produce food, water, power, and healthcare. None of it addresses the layer above production: how does a community coordinate who gets what, how are surpluses managed, how is skilled labor (medic, mechanic, teacher) compensated when there is no cash, and what replaces market price signals? Without this economic operating layer, surpluses rot, critical labor stops, and communities fracture over perceived unfairness. Historical record is consistent: communities that survive the acute crisis phase frequently fail in months 6-24 over resource distribution breakdown, not food shortages.

**Research scope definition**

Three tightly bounded questions:

1. What economic architectures sustain when the dollar fails? Covers local currencies, mutual credit systems, barter protocols, and gift economies at 20-200 person scale. Case studies: WIR mutual credit (Switzerland, 70+ years, $3B+ annual volume), Bristol Pounds impact evaluation, Vermont Time Banks, Totnes Pound.

2. How do communities allocate capital and coordinate production without market signals? Covers Elinor Ostrom's eight commons governance design principles, cooperative enterprise models, land trusts, participatory budgeting, and surplus-management protocols. Midwest agricultural cooperative network provides direct regional case material.

3. What economic bridges prevent community isolation? Covers inter-community trade federation models, comparative advantage without global supply chains, and barter protocols for non-monetary exchange between communities. Directly relevant to the Midwest's existing cooperative federation tradition.

**Expected word count and citation depth**

- Word count: 45,000-55,000 words across 10-12 sections
- Citations: 120-150 (academic: 60-70%; case studies: 20-30%; primary documents: 10-15%)
- Mandatory elements: Zone 5 Implications section (2,000-2,500 words); minimum 2 implementation templates (local currency design template; commons management charter); minimum 4 case studies (200-800 words each)

**Source library readiness: 75-80%**

Confirmed accessible: Ostrom's *Governing the Commons* (foundational), Graeber's *Debt: The First 5,000 Years* (anthropological framing), WIR system documentation, Bristol Pounds evaluation reports, cooperative movement literature (ICA standards), UN Habitat local economic development reports, Midwest cooperative data via Cooperative Development Foundation, 2025 peer-reviewed study in *Journal of Economic Behavior and Organization* on mutual credit resilience.

Known gap: timebanking quantitative effectiveness data is thin (qualitative case studies available; not a blocker). Regional economic simulation models for 200-2,000 person communities are sparse (manageable; document gap explicitly and use best-available evidence).

**Estimated production hours**

- Source library finalization: 20 hours (June 1-7)
- Full production: approximately 200 hours (10-11 weeks at 20-22 hours/week)
- First-draft outline deliverable: June 15
- Final delivery confidence: 95% by August 9

**Cross-domain bridges to Phase 1-5**

- Phase 3 governance structures provide the decision-making framework within which economic systems operate; this domain adds the resource allocation layer on top
- Phase 5 Wave 2 community playbook describes organizational structure; this domain adds who gets what, what replaces money, how skilled labor is compensated
- Phase 4 power mapping extends naturally into economic power analysis (how capital accumulation and resource control patterns emerge in small communities)
- Phase 6 Track B Meshtastic pre-research provides the communication backbone that inter-community trade coordination messages run over; this domain specifies what those messages coordinate

**Unique contribution beyond Phase 5**

The resilience literature has extensive coverage of food, water, and energy systems. It has almost no rigorous research on the economic operating systems of small communities in crisis. This domain fills a documented gap in the existing literature. It also produces the most immediately actionable templates of the three candidates: a community can take a local currency design template or a commons charter and begin implementation directly, without further research or expert guidance.

### 1B. Intergenerational Skills Development

**The problem this domain solves**

Phase 5 assumes communities have the practical skills to execute its documented procedures. That assumption is false for most communities at formation. The gap between "knowing that you need to rotate grazing paddocks" and "having the skill to do it, teaching it, and ensuring it survives when the expert dies or leaves" is the failure mode that kills communities over the medium term — not acute crisis failure, but gradual degradation of technical competence across the 5-15 year horizon. Phase 5 documents what to do. This domain documents how communities ensure they can keep doing it.

**Research scope definition**

Three tightly bounded questions:

1. What knowledge and skills are actually critical for community resilience, what are the learning time requirements, and what are the skill prerequisites? Practical learning curve analysis for the 12-15 skills Phase 5 depends on. Covers medical response, water system maintenance, food preservation, energy system operation, structural repair.

2. How do communities transmit complex technical skills without schools or professional credentialing? Covers apprenticeship models (one-on-one, small group, peer teaching), documentation strategies, competency verification within the community, and cross-generational teaching dynamics. Lave and Wenger's situated learning framework provides the theoretical foundation.

3. What institutional structures support skill transmission at community scale? Covers learning cooperatives, skill-sharing networks, knowledge management systems, oral history and narrative-based transmission, and quality assurance without external certification. Midwest 4-H and Extension Service infrastructure provides direct regional case material.

**Expected word count and citation depth**

- Word count: 48,000-58,000 words across 10-12 sections
- Citations: 130-160 (adult learning theory, apprenticeship research, disaster recovery cases, anthropological literature on knowledge transmission, Midwest extension data)
- Mandatory elements: Zone 5 seasonal learning calendar (when outdoor skills can be taught, agricultural timing, indoor winter skill-building — a unique structural element); minimum 2 implementation templates (community apprenticeship design; skill-sharing network charter); minimum 4 case studies

**Source library readiness: 70-75%**

Confirmed accessible: Lave and Wenger's situated learning framework (most-cited educational literature on practical knowledge transmission), adult learning theory (Kolb's experiential learning cycle, andragogy), historical guild and apprenticeship records, 4-H documentation from USDA land-grant university records and Iowa State Extension, Cuba's post-Soviet collapse education adaptation (documented in peer-reviewed journals), Puerto Rico hurricane recovery education rebuilds, UNESCO non-formal education reports.

Known gap: learning curve data for specific trades (well-drilling, solar installation, medical response) in low-resource settings is limited. Practitioner reports exist but rigorous academic data is sparse. This is the primary source bottleneck — requires a dedicated research sprint June 8-12. Manageable; document gap explicitly and use best-available practitioner evidence.

**Estimated production hours**

- Source library and learning curve research: 20 hours (June 1-7, with additional sprint June 8-12)
- Full production: approximately 220 hours (12 weeks at 18-20 hours/week)
- First-draft outline deliverable: June 15 (one week later than Economic if paired together)
- Final delivery confidence: 88% by August 23

**Cross-domain bridges to Phase 1-5**

- Phase 6 Track A Farm Equipment Repair: the farm equipment repair guide documents what to do; this domain provides the apprenticeship architecture for how to teach it — they are companion documents
- Phase 5 Wave 3 educational governance covers schools and formal education structures; this domain covers adult skill development — the execution layer that makes Phase 5 procedures usable for communities lacking formal training
- Phase 5 Wave 2 psychological support identified skill concentration in 1-2 people as a community vulnerability; this domain is the systematic institutional response to that vulnerability
- Phase 6 Governance Scaling (if concurrent): educational governance and institutional learning are tightly coupled — who decides what skills to teach is a governance question

**Unique contribution beyond Phase 5**

This is the only Phase 6 candidate that explicitly addresses the 10-20 year sustainability horizon. Phase 5 covers years 0-3. This domain covers years 3-20. A community using Phase 5 water purification procedures in 2026 must be able to teach those procedures to someone 15 years younger by 2030. Without an intentional transmission architecture, that knowledge dies with the founding generation. This domain documents how. No equivalent rigorous synthesis exists in the resilience literature.

### 1C. Governance Scaling

**The problem this domain solves**

Phase 3 and Phase 5 Wave 2 document governance structures for communities of roughly 20-50 people: councils, decision processes, roles, conflict resolution. These structures work at 20 people. They fail predictably at 100. Consensus decisions that functioned with 20 people create deadlock at 100. Unwritten norms break down. Authority contests emerge. Founding-member departure leaves vacuums. Factionalism deepens. This domain documents what breaks at each scale threshold, what structural interventions prevent the breaks, and — the harder problem — how institutions actually learn from governance failures and adapt their structures to prevent recurrence.

**Research scope definition**

Three tightly bounded questions:

1. What governance structures allow multiple communities (150-500 people across 3-5 villages) to coordinate decisions without a central authority? Covers federation models, consensus and deliberation protocols that scale, inter-community conflict resolution, and information flow architecture. Case studies: Swiss cantonal system, Indigenous governance networks (Iroquois Confederacy), post-disaster governance in Christchurch and Puerto Rico.

2. How do institutions learn from failures and adapt governance to prevent recurrence? Covers post-crisis review cycles, adaptive management, institutional memory and knowledge transfer to new leaders, and feedback loop design. This is the domain's most academically novel contribution — it is frontier research, not synthesized literature.

3. What incentive structures and accountability mechanisms keep scaled governance honest? Covers checks and balances across federation layers, transparency and information access, resource allocation mechanisms that prevent predation, term limits, and succession design. Midwest case studies: Wisconsin soil-water conservation district cooperatives, Minnesota county systems, Iowa agricultural cooperative federations.

**Expected word count and citation depth**

- Word count: 50,000-60,000 words across 11-13 sections (largest of the three due to governance failure mode analysis complexity)
- Citations: 140-180 (political science, commons governance theory, Indigenous governance studies, post-conflict governance, federation structures)
- Mandatory elements: Zone 5 implications section; governance charter template; minimum 4 case studies; institutional learning cycle diagram

**Source library readiness: 65-70%**

Confirmed accessible: Ostrom and Vincent Ostrom on polycentric governance (directly applicable to federated community structures), Indigenous governance systems literature (extensive academic work on nation-to-nation governance without central authority), Swiss cantonal system documentation, post-disaster governance case studies (Christchurch, Puerto Rico, Haiti), UNDRR international disaster governance frameworks, adaptive management literature (Holling, Folke), institutional change theory (North, Williamson).

Known gaps: detailed case studies of governance failure in resource-constrained settings are systematically underreported (successes are better documented than failures — a known limitation of governance literature that must be flagged); consensus decision-making at 300+ person scale has very limited documented case studies; institutional learning mechanisms in small communities are at the research frontier with no single authoritative source. Approximately 30% of desired sources are behind academic paywalls — real but manageable bottleneck.

**Estimated production hours**

- Source library and governance failure case study research: 20-25 hours (June 1-10, extended window)
- Full production: approximately 250-280 hours (13 weeks at 20-22 hours/week)
- Final delivery confidence: 78% by August 30 (lower due to scope risk and source bottleneck; 85% if paired with Economic Resilience which provides framing context that constrains governance scope expansion)

**Cross-domain bridges to Phase 1-5**

- Phase 3 community governance (the direct ancestor document) — this domain extends Phase 3 to multi-community federation scale and adds the institutional learning layer
- Phase 5 Wave 2 conflict resolution is the micro-level intervention; governance scaling is the structural architecture that reduces how often conflict needs resolution
- Phase 5 Wave 2 community implementation playbook assumes stable, small-community governance; this domain provides the constitutional architecture for what happens when size increases or founding conditions change
- Phase 6 Economic Resilience: economic structures shape what governance models are feasible; governance structures shape what economic models work — these two domains are the most tightly coupled of the three candidates

**Unique contribution beyond Phase 5**

There is extensive political science literature on national and international governance. There is almost no rigorous synthesis of how 3-5 federated communities (150-500 people) actually make decisions together in practice. The Ostrom polycentric governance framework provides the theoretical foundation but has not been applied at this specific scale and context. The institutional learning question — how do communities change their governance when it fails — is frontier research. This domain would be the most academically novel contribution of the three candidates, with the highest risk and longest timeline.

### Criterion Commentary

**Urgency**

- Economic (3): Moderate. Alternative economics literature is stable — no 2026-specific policy window that closes on deferral. However, post-pandemic interest in complementary currencies and cooperatives is at a measurable peak in 2026; research done now captures current practitioner momentum better than 2027. No hard deadline.
- Skills (2): Low time-sensitivity. Knowledge transmission is evergreen. Can be deferred 3-6 months without meaningful strategic cost. Interest in homeschooling and experiential education is growing, but there is no closing window.
- Governance (4): High. Institutional governance reform is gaining traction in disaster management and climate adaptation communities in 2025-2026. Regional governance initiatives — multi-county coordination for water systems, agricultural cooperative federation restructuring — are active in 2026. A governance research contribution in mid-2026 lands in an open policy conversation. Deferring to 2027 reduces relevance.

**Availability (Source Readiness)**

- Economic (4): 75-80% ready. All foundational sources confirmed accessible. Minimal June source bottleneck. Production begins June 8.
- Skills (3): 70-75% ready. Core theory accessible. Learning curve data for specific trades in low-resource settings requires a dedicated June 8-12 research sprint before full production. Manageable bottleneck.
- Governance (2): 65-70% ready. Foundational sources solid but case studies of governance failure are systematically underreported. Consensus decision-making at 300+ person scale has limited documented cases. Governance literature has ~30% paywall exposure. Source completion takes 8-10 days. This is the real schedule risk.

**Leverage (Movement/Policy Impact)**

- Economic (4): High. Downstream of all Phase 1-5 work. Directly enables Phase 7 operationalization (implement a local currency, set up a cooperative). Templates are immediately reusable by any community regardless of Phase 1-5 context. Good cross-project multiplier.
- Skills (5): Highest of the three by the 10-20 year horizon metric. Without systematic skill transmission, all Phase 1-5 systems degrade when the founding generation ages out. Phase 6 Farm Equipment Repair (Track A) is a direct companion — repair documentation without transmission architecture is incomplete. Unlocks Phase 7 education system design.
- Governance (5): Governance architecture is foundational to all other Phase 6 domains. Economic systems, skill transmission networks, and infrastructure coordination all depend on functioning governance. Institutional learning is the enabling mechanism for avoiding repeated failures. Highest multiplier — but requires the most careful execution.

**Scope Fit (Alignment with Phase 1-5)**

- Economic (4): Three core questions are tightly bounded. Research universe is "economics of small communities" — not "comprehensive economics." Zone 5 + 20-200 person + crisis-context filters narrow the literature to a manageable set. Low expansion risk.
- Skills (3): Three questions are well-defined, but educational research is deep. Risk of expanding into comprehensive education theory is real. Requires active boundary-setting at kickoff: 12-15 critical skills for resilience only, not full educational system design.
- Governance (3): Three questions are clear, but governance and institutional change literature is vast. High risk of scope creep into national governance theory, comparative political systems, or institutional economics. Requires active scope management with hard limit at 150-500 person federation scale.

**User Interest Signals**

- Economic (Yes): Phase 5 Wave 2 community playbook explicitly identifies resource distribution fairness as a key unresolved question. The mutual aid framing in Phase 3 and cooperative context in Phase 4 power mapping both point toward economic coordination as a community priority. Midwest agricultural cooperative tradition is a clear regional affinity.
- Skills (Yes): Phase 5 Wave 2 psychological support document identified skill concentration in 1-2 people as a community vulnerability. Phase 6 Track A Farm Equipment Repair is explicitly about skill-based practice. The 4-H and extension service tradition emphasized throughout Zone 5 research signals strong interest in systematic skill development.
- Governance (Partial): Phase 3 and Phase 5 Wave 2 both engaged deeply with governance at the 20-50 person scale. Phase 6 pre-research focused more on economic and skills themes. Federation-scale governance is a logical extension but has fewer direct user signals than the other two candidates.

## Section 3: Sequencing Recommendation

### If You Select One Domain

**Select Community Economic Resilience.** Highest source readiness (75-80%), lowest scope expansion risk, fastest production ramp. Completes by August 9 with 95% confidence. Produces the most immediately actionable outputs (local currency template, commons charter). Unlocks Phase 7 operationalization directly.

### If You Select Two Domains

**Recommended pairing: Economic Resilience + Governance Scaling.**

These two domains are the most tightly coupled. Economic structures shape what governance models are feasible; governance structures shape what economic models work. Researching them in the same wave ensures conceptual alignment.

Production order and rationale:
- June 1-7: Source library work for both domains in parallel. Economic author onboards June 1-5 (fast ramp, 75% source-ready). Governance author begins extended source research June 1-10 (65% ready; needs more time).
- June 8: Economic Resilience enters full production. Governance Scaling continues source work.
- June 15: Governance Scaling enters production. The delayed start is offset by having Economic domain framing available to constrain governance scope expansion.
- July 13: Economic Resilience first draft complete (milestone). Share with Governance author. This framing reduces the risk that Governance drifts into macro-governance theory.
- July 13-August 9: Governance Scaling full production with Economic domain framing available.
- August 9: Economic Resilience final delivery.
- August 9-23: Governance Scaling finalization and cross-review.
- August 23: Both domains complete.

Time-bound dependency: Governance author must receive Economic Resilience draft by July 13 to benefit from the framing. If Economic Resilience is delayed beyond July 20, Governance scope management must fall back on active editorial review alone.

**Alternative pairing: Economic Resilience + Skills Development** (if Governance Scaling is deferred to September)

- Lowest coordination overhead of any two-domain pairing — the domains are largely independent
- Both complete by August 23 with 90% confidence
- No required cross-domain syncs until integration check at August 10
- Governance Scaling moves to Phase 6b, September 1-October 15

### If You Select Three Domains

Do not attempt all three in a single 12-week window. Three simultaneous research tracks with the Governance Scaling scope management risk produces 65% on-time confidence. That is not acceptable.

**Recommended three-domain approach:**

- Phase 6a (June 1-August 23): Economic Resilience + Skills Development in parallel (90% confidence both complete)
- Phase 6b (September 1-October 15): Governance Scaling standalone (78-85% confidence with active scope management)

This produces all three domains by mid-October at high quality rather than all three at reduced quality by August 30. Phase 6b launches immediately after Phase 6a completes — no gap.
