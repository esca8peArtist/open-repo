## What You Are Researching

**Domain definition**: How communities preserve, transmit, and scale critical practical knowledge across generations when formal educational institutions, apprenticeship systems, and professional certification are unavailable.

**The problem this document solves**: Phase 1-5 of the systems-resilience corpus documents what communities must do to survive — filter water, grow food, generate power, treat illness, maintain equipment, manage conflict. Every document in that corpus assumes that community members either already know how to perform these tasks, or can learn quickly. That assumption is false in practice. A community whose water system operator dies, whose solar technician ages out, or whose single medical responder is injured loses its entire technical infrastructure unless that knowledge was systematically transmitted. Phase 5 documents what to do. This domain documents how communities ensure they can keep doing it across time and across generations.

This is the failure mode that kills communities in the second and third year of a sustained crisis — not acute resource failure, but gradual degradation of technical competence when knowledge is concentrated in 1-2 people who eventually leave, retire, or die. It is also the only domain in Phase 6 that explicitly addresses the 10-20 year sustainability horizon. The entire Phase 1-5 corpus becomes static documentation without this domain.

## Research Questions

Answer these three questions. Do not expand beyond them without explicit user direction.

**Question 1: Which knowledge and skills are critical for community resilience, and what are their learning requirements?**

This question requires two outputs: a priority skills inventory and a learning curve analysis.

Priority skills inventory: Map the 12-15 skills that Phase 1-5 procedures depend on most heavily, in order of criticality. Do not attempt comprehensive skill coverage — this is intentionally bounded. Draw the inventory from Phase 5 procedures: water system maintenance (purification, well operation, pipe repair), food preservation (canning, fermentation, cold storage), energy system operation (solar installation/maintenance, microgrid management), emergency medical response (wound management, triage, obstetric emergencies), structural repair, and agricultural management (seed saving, soil management, livestock care). Each skill in the inventory needs: prerequisite knowledge, learning time curve from novice to competent practitioner (not expert), minimum adequate competency threshold.

Learning curve analysis: How long does it take an adult with no prior training to reach minimum adequate competency in each of the 12-15 identified skills? Use FEMA CERT training sequences, Red Cross disaster preparedness curricula, and available practitioner reports as validated proxies where academic data is unavailable. Acknowledge data quality explicitly — practitioner report data is lower rigor than controlled studies; label it as such. This is the section with the known data gap; Week 1 resolution strategy is described in the Source Library section.

**Question 2: How do communities transmit complex technical skills without schools or credentialing?**

Research apprenticeship models in detail: one-on-one mentorship, small group learning, peer teaching, and learning by doing. The Lave and Wenger situated learning framework (legitimate peripheral participation) is the primary theoretical lens — apply it to each model. Document specific success factors and failure modes for each apprenticeship type. Cross-generational teaching: what fails when 60-year-olds teach teenagers, what works, and what structural conditions improve intergenerational learning.

Documentation strategies: How do communities write procedures, create diagrams, and preserve knowledge for offline use (no internet, no electricity)? What documentation formats are most legible to non-specialists under stress? How are visual instruction sets designed for low-literacy contexts? Oral history and narrative transmission: when is oral transmission appropriate versus written documentation, and what are the failure modes of each?

Competency validation without professional boards: How do communities certify that someone has reached minimum adequate competency in a critical skill without external credentialing infrastructure? Peer assessment models, demonstration-based validation, apprenticeship graduation criteria. What prevents unqualified people from self-certifying?

**Question 3: What institutional structures support skill transmission at community scale?**

Learning cooperatives and skill-sharing networks: governance models for community learning institutions, resource allocation for teaching time and materials, scheduling around agricultural and seasonal constraints. Zone 5 seasonal learning calendar: what outdoor skills are teachable in which months, how agricultural timing affects training windows, how winter enables indoor skill-building that summer does not.

Knowledge management at community scale: libraries, offline archives, apprenticeship registries, and institutional memory systems. Who maintains the knowledge store? What prevents critical knowledge from existing only in one person's head? Succession planning for critical technical roles.

Quality assurance without external certification: how communities maintain standards when professional licensing is unavailable. Midwest case studies: 4-H club skill development sequences, Extension Service non-formal education models, apprenticeship networks in agricultural communities.

## Source Library

Start with these sources. They are pre-verified accessible and directly applicable. Week 1 task is source verification and accessibility audit.

**Tier 1 (foundational — read in full, cite extensively)**:
- Lave and Wenger, *Situated Learning: Legitimate Peripheral Participation* (1991) — Cambridge University Press. The most-cited framework for how practical knowledge is actually transmitted in real-world practice. Legitimate peripheral participation model is directly applicable to community apprenticeship design.
- Kolb, *Experiential Learning: Experience as the Source of Learning and Development* (1984) — Prentice Hall. Adult learning theory foundation; the experiential learning cycle (concrete experience → reflective observation → abstract conceptualization → active experimentation) applies to every skill in the inventory.

**Tier 2 (substantive — read relevant sections, cite specifically)**:
- David Sobel, *Place-Based Education: Connecting Classrooms and Communities* (2004) — Orion Society. Place-based learning principles apply directly to Zone 5 skill transmission in specific agricultural and ecological contexts.
- Malcolm Knowles, *The Adult Learner* (1984/2015 editions) — Andragogy principles that distinguish adult learning from child education; critical for designing skill transmission programs for mixed-age community members
- UNESCO non-formal education and skill development in low-resource contexts — UNESCO website (free). Extensive documentation from post-crisis education rebuilds in developing contexts.
- Cuba post-Soviet collapse education adaptation — Academic journal sources: *Comparative Education Review*, *Journal of Curriculum Studies* (2000-2010). Cuba's Special Period (1989-2000) involved systematic informal skill transmission that is directly analogous to Zone 5 crisis scenarios.
- Puerto Rico hurricane recovery education documentation — PRDE (Puerto Rico Department of Education) reports, University of Puerto Rico resilience studies, academic retrospectives (2018-2022)
- 4-H program documentation — USDA, National 4-H Council, land-grant university records (free; Iowa State Extension, University of Wisconsin Extension). Largest youth skill development network in the US with Zone 5 concentration and documented apprenticeship outcomes.
- Historical guild and apprenticeship systems — academic history sources (*Journal of Economic History*, medieval trade guild records analysis)

**Tier 3 (supporting — cite where relevant)**:
- FEMA CERT (Community Emergency Response Team) training curricula — FEMA website (free). Use as validated proxy for learning curve estimation in emergency medical and response skills.
- Red Cross disaster preparedness training curricula — Red Cross website (free). Additional learning curve proxy.
- Orr, *Earth in Mind: On Education, Environment, and the Human Prospect* (1994) — ecological education philosophy relevant to Zone 5 context
- Peer-reviewed literature on apprenticeship outcomes vs. classroom outcomes — search *Vocational Learning*, *Journal of Vocational Education and Training*
- Minnesota and Wisconsin Extension Service agricultural training curricula — state Extension websites (free)

**Week 1 source gap resolution strategy**: Learning curve data for specific trades in low-resource settings. Academic literature is thin; peer-reviewed data on "how long does it take to learn well-drilling with minimal equipment" is sparse. Resolution protocol:
1. June 1-5: Email authors of the most relevant practitioner reports found in Cuba and Puerto Rico literature (ask for unpublished data on learning times)
2. June 5-8: Use FEMA CERT training sequences and Red Cross disaster preparedness curricula as validated proxies — these are designed for community members with no prior training and document expected learning times
3. June 8-10: Document what data was found, explicitly label its quality, and proceed with best-available evidence. Do not delay production past June 10 waiting for replies.

**Known secondary gaps — document explicitly, do not block on**:
- Intergenerational teaching dynamics in North American cultural contexts — cultural psychology literature exists but is not well-synthesized for Midwest specifically. Use available adjacent literature and document the gap.
- Oral history learning outcomes metrics — anthropology literature available; quantification is thin. Use qualitative evidence labeled as such.

## Document Structure

10-12 major sections. Target 4,000-5,500 words per section in the core research sections.

1. **The Skill Depletion Problem** (3,000-4,000 words) — What actually kills communities in year 2-5: not resource failure but skill depletion. Case studies: Puerto Rico post-Hurricane Maria (2018-2022) skill concentration and transmission failures, Cuba Special Period skill adaptation successes. Frame the problem quantitatively where possible: what percentage of critical technical skills are held by 1-2 people in a typical 20-50 person resilience community?

2. **Critical Skills Inventory** (4,000-5,000 words) — The 12-15 critical skills for Zone 5 community resilience, with: skill definition, prerequisites, minimum adequate competency description, estimated learning curve (hours/weeks to minimum competency with no prior training), and source/confidence level for the learning time estimate. This section is the diagnostic backbone — readers should be able to take this inventory directly to their community.

3. **Situated Learning Theory Applied** (3,500-4,500 words) — Lave and Wenger's legitimate peripheral participation model explained and applied to community-scale apprenticeship. How does a novice move from peripheral observation to full participation in skilled practice? What conditions accelerate this movement? What conditions stall it? Concrete Zone 5 application: how does a novice join a water system maintenance team as a legitimate peripheral participant?

4. **Apprenticeship Models** (5,000-6,000 words) — Four models in depth: one-on-one mentorship, small group (3-5 learners with 1 teacher), peer teaching (competent learner teaches novice), and learning-by-doing with documentation backup. For each: design structure, optimal group size, time requirements, appropriate skill types, failure modes, and when to use each. Real-world examples from 4-H, guild records, Extension Service programs, and Cuba Special Period.

5. **Documentation for Non-Specialists** (4,000-5,000 words) — How to write procedures that a person can follow under stress with no prior training. Visual instruction design (exploded diagrams, numbered steps, photographs vs. drawings). Offline archival: binders, lamination, paper quality for field conditions. Oral transmission design: what information works as oral tradition, what requires written backup. Procedure testing: how to verify that documentation is actually usable by novices (have someone follow it cold).

6. **Competency Validation Without Credentials** (3,500-4,500 words) — How communities certify minimum adequate competency in critical skills without external licensing. Peer assessment protocols, demonstration-based testing, apprenticeship graduation criteria. Social accountability mechanisms that prevent unqualified self-certification. What happens when someone fails the competency check — remediation protocols. Midwest case studies: how 4-H levels advancement, how Extension Service validates farm operator training.

7. **Cross-Generational Teaching Dynamics** (4,000-5,000 words) — What fails when 60-year-olds teach teenagers and what fails when teenagers teach 60-year-olds. Cultural psychology of knowledge transmission across age groups in North American (particularly rural Midwest) contexts. Structural interventions that improve cross-generational learning: role design, pacing, acknowledgment systems. How to prevent knowledge hierarchies from creating teaching bottlenecks.

8. **Learning Cooperatives and Institutional Structures** (4,500-5,500 words) — Governance models for community learning institutions: who decides what to teach, who teaches, and how teaching time is allocated. Resource provision: what tools and materials are required for skill development and how a community acquires them. Scheduling around agricultural and seasonal constraints — the Zone 5 learning calendar. Knowledge management: apprenticeship registries, skill inventories updated regularly, succession planning protocols.

9. **Zone 5 Seasonal Learning Calendar** (3,000-4,000 words) — Month-by-month guide to which skills are best taught when in a Zone 5 agricultural community. Winter (November-March): indoor skill-building, equipment maintenance, documentation work, food preservation technique practice. Spring (April-May): planting techniques, field equipment operation, water system startup. Summer (June-August): intensive outdoor skill development, field apprenticeships. Fall (September-October): harvest operations, preservation scale-up, year-end skills audit. This section is directly actionable — a community can use it as a training calendar.

10. **Zone 5 Implications** (2,500-3,500 words) — How Midwest geography, climate, and cultural context specifically shapes skill transmission. Agricultural rhythm constraints, distance between community members affecting teaching logistics, existing 4-H and Extension Service infrastructure as implementation foundation. Cultural competencies: rural Midwest norms around teaching, expertise, and community authority that affect apprenticeship design. What the Cuba and Puerto Rico cases translate to — and what does not translate.

11. **Implementation Templates** (3,000-4,000 words, mandatory) — Two complete templates:
    - *Template A: Community Apprenticeship Program Design* — Step-by-step guide for a community to launch an apprenticeship program in one critical skill. Includes: role definition (mentor, apprentice, observer), time commitment structure, milestones, competency validation checkpoint, graduation criteria, and succession protocol.
    - *Template B: Skills Inventory and Gap Assessment Tool* — Worksheet for a community to map current skill holdings (who knows what), identify single points of failure (skills held by only one person), prioritize transmission gaps, and schedule remediation. Direct parallel to the Critical Skills Inventory in Section 2.

12. **Case Studies** (3,000-5,000 words, minimum 4) — Full case studies with problem context, transmission approach, outcomes, failure modes, and lessons extracted. Required cases: Cuba Special Period skill adaptation (1989-2000), Puerto Rico Hurricane Maria education rebuild (2018-2022), Iowa or Wisconsin 4-H agricultural skill development program, historical guild apprenticeship model (choose one: medieval carpentry, blacksmithing, or similar with good documentation). Optional additional: Amish community skill transmission practices, Israeli kibbutz vocational programs.
