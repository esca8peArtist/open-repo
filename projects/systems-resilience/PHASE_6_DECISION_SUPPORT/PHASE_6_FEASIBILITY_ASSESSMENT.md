# Phase 6 Feasibility Assessment

---

## Lead Finding

All three Phase 6 domains are feasible for June 1 activation. The key variable is not whether they can start — all have sufficient source readiness to begin — but whether they can maintain pace and complete on their stated targets. The highest completion risk is Governance Scaling (78% confidence), driven by source bottleneck and scope expansion tendency. The lowest completion risk is Community Economic Resilience (95% confidence). Skills Development is intermediate (88%).

The most consequential feasibility constraint is not source availability — it is scope management. Two of three domains (Skills, Governance) have documented tendencies toward scope expansion that will cause target misses if not actively managed. This assessment specifies what active management looks like.

## Domain-by-Domain Risk Analysis

### Domain 64: Community Economic Resilience

**Highest-risk research gap**: Quantitative effectiveness data for timebanking and LETS (Local Exchange Trading Systems) in North American contexts. Academic literature on complementary currencies has strong theoretical foundations and good case study documentation, but rigorous peer-reviewed quantitative impact studies for North American timebank networks are sparse. What exists is primarily qualitative: interview studies, observational research, and self-reported participation data.

**Why this is not a production blocker**: The timebanking analysis is one subsection of the complementary currencies section, which is one of 12 sections. The gap is real but it does not affect the core research questions. The WIR mutual credit system, Bristol Pounds, and the 2025 Journal of Economic Behavior and Organization study on mutual credit resilience provide the empirical foundation. Timebanking subsection can be written explicitly as "qualitative case evidence with quantitative data gap" — this is an accurate representation of the state of the literature, not a research failure.

**Backup plan if timebanking sources are unavailable**: Use the Vermont Family Time Exchange network qualitative documentation and the Japanese Fureai Kippu (caring relationship ticket) network as primary case studies. Both have academic case study coverage. Acknowledge that peer-reviewed quantitative impact data does not exist for North American timebanking at community scale; document this gap for future research.

**Risk level**: Low. The gap is in a subsection, not in a core research question. Production proceeds June 8 regardless.

**Secondary risks**:
- Midwest agricultural cooperative performance data aggregation: requires patience, not special access. Cooperative Development Foundation data is public; aggregation takes 4-6 hours. Not a production blocker.
- Regional economic simulation models for 200-2,000 person communities: sparse. The MIT SUST model provides one anchor; grey literature provides others. This is thin but workable with explicit confidence labeling.

### Domain 61: Intergenerational Skills Development

**Highest-risk research gap**: Learning curve data for specific trades in low-resource settings. How long does it take an adult with no prior training to reach minimum adequate competency in well-drilling, solar installation maintenance, emergency medical response, or grain storage management — specifically in low-resource, low-equipment contexts? Peer-reviewed academic data on this question is limited. Most learning research is conducted in well-resourced educational settings with standardized curricula, which does not translate directly to community crisis contexts.

**Backup plan if practitioner report authors do not respond to email outreach (June 1-5)**: Use FEMA CERT training sequences as validated proxies for emergency medical response learning curves. Use Red Cross disaster preparedness training curricula for preparedness skill learning times. These curricula are designed for untrained community members, have been refined over decades, and provide documented expected learning times. For agricultural and mechanical skills (well-drilling, solar maintenance), use Extension Service training program completion data from Iowa State and University of Wisconsin archives — not ideal but available and applicable.

**Explicit confidence labeling requirement**: When using proxy data (FEMA CERT, Red Cross, Extension training times), label the data source and confidence level explicitly in the text. Do not present proxy-derived learning estimates as equivalent to controlled empirical data. Write something like: "Based on FEMA CERT training completion data across 47,000 participants (2015-2022), adult community members with no prior first aid training reach minimum adequate competency in basic wound management in approximately 8-12 hours of hands-on practice. No peer-reviewed study has assessed this specific context in low-resource settings; this estimate should be treated as a validated operational benchmark rather than a controlled experimental finding."

**Risk level**: Medium. The gap is in the load-bearing skill inventory section. However, the backup plan is solid — FEMA CERT and Red Cross data are well-validated operational benchmarks. The gap affects rigor more than utility. A community applying this document does not need peer-reviewed data; they need a reasonable benchmark. The proxy sources provide that.

**Secondary risks**:
- Scope expansion: High risk, requires active management (see Scope Management subsection below)
- Author background: Without education/adult learning expertise, add 20-30 hours and shift target from August 23 to approximately September 6

### Domain 65: Governance Scaling

**Highest-risk research gap**: Governance failure case studies in resource-constrained settings. The governance literature has a systematic bias toward success documentation. Governance failures — particularly in small communities and post-disaster settings — are underreported in the academic literature. What fails, how it fails, and what specific structural changes prevented recurrence are less documented than what succeeds. This is not merely a gap in available sources; it is a known limitation of the political science literature on community governance.

**Backup plan**:
1. Use the kibbutz governance reform literature as the primary controlled comparison. Israeli kibbutzim underwent documented governance crises from the 1980s through the 2000s, with some communities successfully reforming and others failing. The Israeli kibbutz movement maintained detailed records; academic literature in *Israel Studies* and *Journal of Rural Cooperation* documents both failure types. This provides a within-sample comparison that is as close to experimental as the literature offers.
2. Use Haiti earthquake recovery governance as the primary counter-case for federation failure in post-crisis settings. OCHA and academic retrospectives document specific governance failure modes with sufficient detail.
3. For consensus at scale, use the documented challenges in Occupy movement assemblies (2011-2012) as a contemporary case — not an endorsement of the politics, but a documented empirical case of consensus decision-making breaking down at 200-500 person scale with recorded failure analysis.
4. Acknowledge explicitly in Section 2 that governance failure documentation is systematically underrepresented in the academic literature, explain why (publication bias, shame/reputation effects, political sensitivity), and note that the case studies in this document represent the best available evidence rather than a comprehensive sample.

**Risk level**: High on source completion timeline; Medium on production quality after sources are resolved. The source gap creates a real 8-10 day delay before production begins. Production quality after that delay is adequate if the backup plan is executed.

**Critical constraint**: If an author without political science background is assigned, governance scope management requires explicit scaffolding that would normally come from disciplinary training. Provide weekly scope audit prompts as standing instructions, not just a checklist to review once.

**Secondary risks**:
- Paywall exposure (~30%): Real but manageable. Interlibrary loan, preprints, and author copies handle most cases. Budget 2 hours per week in the source library phase for access problem-solving.
- Consensus at 300+ person scale: Frontier research. Document the empirical gap clearly. Do not let the gap prevent analysis — extract principles from adjacent literature (sociocracy, consent-based governance) and label them as theory-based guidance rather than empirically validated prescriptions.

## Parallel Execution Assessment

### Can two domains run simultaneously?

Yes. The recommended pairings are feasible. Resource constraint analysis:

**Economic + Skills (recommended parallel)**: No coordination overhead. Independent research universes (economics vs. educational research). No shared sources. No required synchronization. Each agent works completely independently. Combined load: approximately 563 production hours across both domains. If executed at 20-25 hours/week per agent, both complete by August 23. Risk: the Skills scope expansion risk is the only constraint — manageable with scope audit checkpoints.

**Economic + Governance (viable parallel)**: One required sync (July 13, 30 min). The Economic domain framing (resource allocation without market signals) reduces the Governance domain's scope expansion risk in the accountability sections. Combined load: approximately 588 production hours. Economic completes August 9; Governance completes August 23-30. Risk: Governance source bottleneck in Week 1 (agent must use 8-10 days for extended source work before production begins) means parallel execution requires two agents — one starting economic production June 8 while the other is still in source work.

**Skills + Governance (viable but more coordination-intensive)**: Two required syncs (June 29 + July 27). Educational governance is a specific case of the governance scaling problem — the two domains share research territory. Without coordination, they will produce inconsistent guidance on who has authority to certify competence in critical skills. Combined load: approximately 591 production hours. Risk: highest coordination overhead of any pairing; recommend only if both authors are experienced with cross-domain coordination.

### Can all three run simultaneously from June 1?

Not recommended. Combined production hours approximately 871. At 20-25 hours/week per agent, three independent agents can theoretically complete all three by August 30. However:
1. Governance scope expansion risk amplifies under parallel execution — with the Economic author independent and Skills author independent, there is no shared context that naturally bounds Governance scope.
2. Three simultaneous long-running research tracks create coordination overhead that is quadratic, not linear. The required syncs (Economic-Governance July 13, Governance-Skills June 29 + July 27) overlap with peak production periods in each domain.
3. On-time confidence for all three from June 1: approximately 65%. Not acceptable.

**Recommended three-domain approach**: Phase 6a (Economic + Skills, June 1-August 23), Phase 6b (Governance, September 1-October 15). All three complete by mid-October at 85%+ confidence each.

## Rollover Risk and Contingency

### If Economic Resilience extends past August 9

**Trigger**: Agent signals at July 27 checkpoint that final completion will slip past August 9.

**Cause diagnosis**:
- If word count is on track but editing is behind: extend to August 16. No cascade impact.
- If word count is below 40,000 at July 27: scope execution problem. Assess whether scope audit was enforced. If scope expanded, cut to the 45,000 floor target and prioritize template and Zone 5 completion over additional research depth.
- If source access failed: identify which sections are affected, substitute with available alternatives, document gaps explicitly.

**Contingency target**: August 16 (1-week slip). If agent cannot commit to August 16 at the July 27 checkpoint, escalate to orchestrator for scope intervention.

**Cascade impact**: None on Skills Development (independent track). If concurrent with Governance Scaling: Economic first draft must reach Governance agent by July 27 at latest to preserve Governance target. If Economic first draft is delayed past August 1, Governance finalization target shifts from August 30 to September 6 (1-week cascade slip — acceptable).

### If Skills Development extends past August 23

**Trigger**: Agent signals at August 3 checkpoint that final completion will slip.

**Cause diagnosis**:
- Most likely cause: scope expansion (educational research literature; unauthorized skills additions beyond the 12-15 ceiling)
- Second most likely cause: learning curve data gap not fully resolved, creating uncertainty that paralyzes production

**Contingency**: Apply the scope ceiling hard. If scope expansion is the cause, cut the document to the 48,000-word floor target — produce a complete, tightly bounded document rather than an expanded incomplete one. If learning curve data is the cause, use proxy data with explicit confidence labeling and proceed. Do not wait for perfect data.

**Contingency target**: September 6 (2-week slip). Acceptable. Does not affect Phase 7 planning timeline.

### If Governance Scaling extends past August 30

**Trigger**: Governance domain is more likely than the others to slip. 78% on-time confidence means a 22% probability of slipping.

**Cause diagnosis**:
- If in Phase 6a alongside Economic: source bottleneck (extended source work) or scope expansion
- If standalone in Phase 6b (September 1 start): most likely scope expansion or paywall access failures

**Contingency target**: September 13 (2-week slip); September 27 (4-week slip with scope intervention). Beyond September 27, escalate to orchestrator for scope restructuring — the document may need to be redefined as a focused subset (federation governance only, or institutional learning only) rather than the full three-question scope.

**Fallback option if full scope is not achievable by October 15**: Publish a governance scoping document (10,000-15,000 words) covering the federation model and governance charter template only, with institutional learning and accountability as explicitly flagged future work. This is better than a 60,000-word document that misses its target by 6 weeks.

### General Rollover Principles

1. A 1-week slip on any domain is acceptable and self-correcting. No intervention needed.
2. A 2-week slip triggers cause diagnosis and scope audit. May require scope reduction.
3. A 4-week slip triggers scope restructuring. The domain produces a tightly bounded subset rather than missing the full-scope target by 6+ weeks.
4. Completion before Phase 7 is more important than full scope at Phase 7 launch.

Phase 7 operationalization can begin when the first Phase 6 domain completes (Economic Resilience, August 9). Phase 7 does not require all three Phase 6 domains; it builds on what is available. Do not delay Phase 7 activation waiting for Governance Scaling to complete.
