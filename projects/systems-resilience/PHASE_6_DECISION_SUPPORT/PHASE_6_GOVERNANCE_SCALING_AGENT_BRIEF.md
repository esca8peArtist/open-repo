## What You Are Researching

**Domain definition**: How governance structures that function at community scale (25-50 people) are adapted and extended to coordinate multiple communities (150-500 people across 3-5 villages), and how those governance institutions learn from failures to prevent the same failures from recurring.

**The problem this document solves**: Phase 3 of the systems-resilience corpus documents governance decision-making structures for a single community at approximately 20-50 person scale. Phase 5 Wave 2 documents conflict resolution at the same scale. Neither document addresses what happens when a governance structure designed for 25 people encounters 150 people. Consensus-based decisions that functioned with 20 people create deadlock at 100. Unwritten norms break down. Authority contests emerge when there are too many members to maintain reputational accountability. Founding-member departure leaves governance vacuums. Factionalism deepens along value lines that were previously suppressed.

This domain documents two things. First, the scaling problem: what breaks at each population threshold, and what structural interventions prevent or repair those breaks. Second, the harder problem: how institutions actually learn from governance failures and update their structures. Communities that only survive their first crisis still fail when they repeat avoidable mistakes. Institutional learning — the community equivalent of the SuperClaude ReflexionPattern — is what distinguishes communities that improve from communities that collapse in the second crisis they encounter.

## Research Questions

Answer these three questions. Do not expand beyond them without explicit user direction.

**Question 1: What governance structures allow multiple communities (150-500 people) to coordinate decisions without a central authority?**

Research federation models: how communities maintain autonomy while coordinating on shared challenges (energy infrastructure, water systems, inter-community trade, security). Document consensus and deliberation protocols that work at 500+ person scale — with specific attention to what works at 50, 100, 200, and 500 persons and what breaks at each threshold. Inter-community conflict resolution mechanisms: how disputes between communities (not within a community) are adjudicated. Information flow and communication cascades across federation levels: how decisions made at the village level reach the federation, and how federation decisions reach individuals.

Case studies for this question: Swiss cantonal system (Landsgemeinde direct democracy at canton scale); Iroquois Confederacy governance (multi-community confederation without central state); UNDRR regional coordination structures in post-disaster governance; Christchurch earthquake recovery governance (community-level decision-making federated into citywide coordination); Haiti earthquake governance failures (as a counter-case documenting what breaks when federation fails). Midwest regional governance: Wisconsin soil-water conservation district federations, Iowa county extension governance structures.

**Question 2: How do institutions learn from failures and adapt governance to prevent recurrence?**

This is the institutional learning question. Research post-crisis review cycles: how a community formally documents what went wrong in a governance failure, extracts lessons, changes procedures, and verifies the change worked. Adaptive management literature (C.S. Holling, Carl Folke): how to change governance procedures when external conditions change (drought, population decline, technology changes, new leadership). Institutional memory: how communities document governance lessons and transfer them to new leaders who were not present for the original failure. Feedback loop design: what early warning signals indicate that a governance structure is approaching a failure threshold?

Failure mode analysis for governance: what governance structures fail under what conditions? Research this specifically in resource-constrained settings — where the literature is thinner and you must build from adjacent cases. Sources: kibbutz governance reform studies (Israeli kibbutzim attempted multiple governance reforms from 1980s-2000s, with documented successes and failures), Venezuelan parroquia governance breakdowns (community council reforms 2006-2012, documented in Latin American politics literature), post-disaster management retrospectives that explicitly analyze governance failures (not just physical infrastructure failures).

**Question 3: What incentive structures and accountability mechanisms keep scaled governance functional over time?**

Research checks and balances across federation layers: how do communities prevent power concentration when there is no state to enforce accountability? Transparency mechanisms in distributed governance: what information flows are required for members to hold leaders accountable? Resource allocation mechanisms that prevent predation: how communities ensure that access to shared resources does not become concentrated in a small governing group over time. Term limits, rotation systems, and leadership succession: structural mechanisms that prevent entrenchment.

What are the incentive conditions under which community leaders act in the collective interest rather than their personal interest? Research this through agency theory applied to commons governance, not through national-level political science. Midwest cases: how agricultural cooperative boards have handled governance accountability over multi-decade histories; how rural water authority boards have managed accountability; how soil-water conservation district governance has adapted.

**Tier 1 (foundational — read in full, cite extensively)**:
- Elinor Ostrom, *Governing the Commons* (1990) — Cambridge University Press. The eight design principles apply to governance as directly as to economic management. This is the primary theoretical framework.
- Vincent Ostrom, *The Meaning of American Federalism: Constituting a Self-Governing Society* (1991) — Institute for Contemporary Studies. Polycentric governance theory: how governance functions without a single central authority. Directly applicable to multi-community federation design.
- Charles Manski, works on social interactions and information in governance decision-making — for information cascade design in Question 1

**Tier 2 (substantive — read relevant sections, cite specifically)**:
- C.S. Holling and Lance Gunderson (eds.), *Panarchy: Understanding Transformations in Human and Natural Systems* (2002) — Island Press. Adaptive management theory; institutional resilience and adaptation under stress. Primary source for Question 2.
- Douglass North, *Institutions, Institutional Change, and Economic Performance* (1990) — Cambridge University Press. Institutional change theory: how institutions evolve, what prevents them from adapting, and what enables rapid institutional change. Critical for Question 2.
- Vine Deloria Jr. and Clifford Lytle, *American Indians, American Justice* (1983) and related Indigenous governance literature — academic sources on nation-to-nation governance without central authority. Directly applicable to inter-community coordination in Question 1.
- Swiss cantonal system documentation — Swiss Federal Chancellery publications (free), and academic analyses in *Publius: The Journal of Federalism*
- Christchurch earthquake recovery governance documentation — Canterbury Earthquake Recovery Authority (CERA) reports, academic retrospectives (NZ Journal of Public Administration, 2013-2018)
- Haiti earthquake governance failure documentation — UN OCHA reports, academic retrospectives in *Disasters* journal and *Journal of International Development*
- UNDRR Sendai Framework implementation governance reports — UNDRR website (free)
- Wisconsin and Iowa soil-water conservation district governance documents — state government websites (free)

**Tier 3 (supporting — cite where relevant)**:
- Robert Putnam, *Making Democracy Work: Civic Traditions in Modern Italy* (1993) — social capital and governance performance across regions; applicable to community-level governance
- Elinor Ostrom et al., *Rules, Games, and Common-Pool Resources* (1994) — detailed empirical governance analysis
- Rebecca Solnit, *A Paradise Built in Hell* (2009) — community governance emergence in disaster contexts (New Orleans, 9/11, Blitz); useful case material for Section 1

## Document Structure

11-13 major sections. Target 4,000-5,500 words per section in the core research sections.

1. **The Governance Scaling Problem** (3,000-4,000 words) — What breaks and when. Document the failure modes empirically: consensus deadlock at 100+ people, authority contests when reputational accountability disappears, informal norm breakdown, factional emergence at population thresholds. Frame with concrete community scenarios from the Phase 1-5 corpus: a 25-person community doubles to 50 people after a regional crisis — what governance structures just failed?

2. **Governance Failure Mode Catalog** (4,000-5,000 words) — Systematic documentation of governance failure modes in resource-constrained settings. This section is the diagnostic backbone of the domain. Organize by failure type, not by case. Failure types: consensus failure (deadlock, exclusion), authority vacuum (leadership departure), institutional memory loss (founding member departure), predation (resource capture by governing minority), factionalism (value-line splits), information failure (decisions made without adequate community input). For each failure type: conditions that trigger it, early warning signals, documented cases, and structural interventions that prevent it.

3. **Federation Models** (5,000-6,000 words) — How communities coordinate decisions across 3-5 villages without central authority. The Iroquois Great Law of Peace as a historical architecture for multi-community confederation (specifically the decision-making protocols, not the full cultural context). Swiss cantonal direct democracy: what makes Landsgemeinde scale to canton size, and what conditions enable it. Polycentric governance (Vincent Ostrom): how authority is distributed across multiple centers without a single apex. Design parameters for Zone 5 inter-community federation: meeting cadence, delegation protocols, veto rights, and exit mechanisms.

4. **Consensus at Scale** (4,500-5,500 words) — Deliberation protocols that work at 100, 200, and 500+ person scale. Sociocracy and sociocratic circles: how decision-making authority is distributed into nested circles to prevent full-community paralysis. Consent-based decision-making (vs. consensus): distinction, when each is appropriate, and how to structure meetings for each. Threshold-based decision rules: what decisions require unanimous consent, what require supermajority, what require simple majority, and who decides which category a decision falls into. Information management: how to summarize information for 200 people when full deliberation is impossible.

5. **Inter-Community Conflict Resolution** (4,000-5,000 words) — Dispute mechanisms for conflicts between communities (not within communities — Phase 5 Wave 2 conflict resolution framework covers intra-community conflict). Federation-level adjudication: who resolves a dispute over water rights between two federated communities? Mediation at federation scale: neutral third parties, process design, and legitimacy. Trade dispute resolution: when inter-community exchange breaks down, what is the repair process? Historical cases: Iroquois Confederacy dispute resolution mechanisms, Swiss cantonal arbitration, academic literature on inter-organization conflict in rural cooperative networks.

6. **Information Architecture for Federated Governance** (3,500-4,500 words) — How decisions, meeting minutes, grievances, and resource status flow through a federation without electronic infrastructure. Cascading communication protocols: village meeting → delegate report → federation meeting → delegate return report. Information summarization: how to compress complex deliberation outcomes into forms that can be communicated by a single delegate. Meshtastic mesh network integration: what governance communications are appropriate for LoRa mesh networks (decision outcomes, resource status alerts) versus what requires in-person meeting (major decisions, conflict resolution).

7. **Post-Crisis Review and Institutional Learning** (5,000-6,000 words) — How communities formally learn from governance failures. The after-action review protocol adapted for community governance (not military AAR — extract principles and redesign for community contexts). Documentation of governance failures: what gets recorded, in what format, and who maintains it. Lesson extraction: how communities move from "what went wrong" to "what rule we change." Implementation verification: how do communities confirm the new rule actually prevents recurrence? Time horizons: immediate post-event review (1-2 weeks), medium-term structural review (3-6 months), and long-term governance evolution review (annually).

8. **Adaptive Management: Changing Governance When Conditions Change** (4,000-5,000 words) — Holling's adaptive management cycle applied to community governance. How to change governance procedures when the conditions that shaped them no longer apply (population change, new technology, environmental shift, leadership transition). Constitutional amendment protocols at community scale: how do the rules about changing rules work? Governance evolution without crisis: what enables communities to update governance proactively rather than only in response to failure?

9. **Institutional Memory** (4,000-5,000 words) — How communities document governance lessons and transfer them to leaders who were not present for the original decisions. Succession protocols: what must be transferred when a founding member departs, and how? Governance archives: what documents must be maintained, in what format, and with what redundancy? Oral governance tradition: when is narrative-based transmission appropriate for governance knowledge and when does it fail? The minimum institutional memory required for a community to survive complete founding-member turnover.

10. **Accountability and Anti-Predation Mechanisms** (4,500-5,500 words) — Structural mechanisms that prevent power concentration over time. Term limits and rotation: design principles, costs and benefits, when rotation creates more instability than it prevents. Transparency mechanisms: what information must be public, what formats work at community scale, and how to prevent information asymmetry between leaders and members. Resource allocation oversight: how communities verify that resource allocation decisions (who gets what from community stores) are not being captured by a governing minority. Midwest agricultural cooperative governance as a long-running empirical case: how 100-year-old cooperatives have handled accountability and what structural mechanisms are most durable.

11. **Zone 5 Implications** (2,500-3,500 words) — How Midwest geography, climate, and institutional tradition shapes governance implementation at federation scale. Spatial dispersion: communities separated by 10-30 miles in winter conditions — what meeting cadences are feasible? Existing regional governance infrastructure (county governance structures, soil-water conservation districts, Extension Service district organizations) as implementation anchors. Cultural norms: rural Midwest norms around authority, consensus, and collective decision-making that enable or constrain federation design. Seasonal governance: which governance activities are appropriately timed to agricultural cycles (annual charter reviews after harvest, not during planting).

12. **Implementation Templates** (3,000-4,000 words, mandatory) — Two complete templates:
    - *Template A: Federated Community Governance Charter* — A governance document template for a federation of 3-5 communities (150-500 people total). Includes: member community roles, federation council structure, decision thresholds (unanimous/supermajority/majority by category), meeting schedule, resource allocation protocols, conflict resolution procedure, amendment procedure, and exit mechanism for member communities.
    - *Template B: Post-Crisis Governance Review Protocol* — Step-by-step process for conducting a governance failure review after a crisis or conflict. Includes: timing, participants, documentation format, lesson extraction template, rule change proposal format, implementation tracking, and verification checkpoint.

13. **Case Studies** (4,000-6,000 words, minimum 4) — Full case studies with problem context, governance approach, outcomes, failure modes, and lessons extracted. Required cases: Swiss cantonal system (direct democracy at scale), Iroquois Confederacy governance (multi-community confederation without state), Christchurch earthquake recovery governance (contemporary community-level coordination), Iowa or Wisconsin agricultural cooperative federation (Midwest-specific governance over decades). Optional additional: kibbutz governance reform (success and failure within a controlled comparative context), Haiti earthquake governance failures (counter-case).
