## What You Are Researching

**Domain definition**: How communities trade goods, store value, allocate capital, and coordinate economic activity when conventional money, banking infrastructure, and global supply chains fail.

**The problem this document solves**: Phase 5 of the systems-resilience corpus documents how communities produce food, generate power, maintain water systems, treat illness, and manage conflict. None of it addresses the layer above production: how community members exchange what they produce, how skilled labor is compensated when there is no cash, and how communities prevent surplus from rotting and scarcity from breeding resentment. Without an economic coordination layer, communities that survive the acute crisis phase fracture in the medium term — not from resource failure, but from breakdown in fair exchange.

This domain fills that gap. It is the economic operating system that makes specialized labor sustainable. A community can grow food, operate a microgrid, and provide emergency healthcare — but the farmer, the electrician, and the medic all need a way to compensate each other without functioning currency. This document provides that system.

## Research Questions

Answer these three questions. Do not expand beyond them without explicit user direction.

**Question 1: What economic architectures function when the dollar fails?**

Research local currencies, mutual credit systems, barter protocols, gift economies, and hybrid models that operate without central banking infrastructure. The WIR mutual credit system (Switzerland, 70+ years of operation, approximately $3 billion in annual transaction volume) is the primary case study. Secondary cases: Bristol Pounds (UK, 2009-2022 — an intentional closure providing controlled end-of-life data), timebanking networks in Japan and Vermont. The research question is not "what existed historically" but "what design principles produce durable economic alternatives at 20-200 person scale?" Extract and document those design principles.

**Question 2: How do communities allocate capital and coordinate production without market price signals?**

Research commons-based resource management using Elinor Ostrom's eight design principles for common-pool resource governance as the analytical framework. Document cooperative enterprise models (worker cooperatives, land trusts, mutual aid networks) with particular attention to how they handle resource allocation decisions that markets handle via price signals. Research participatory budgeting implementations at community scale. Document gift economy mechanics and their failure modes. Midwest case studies: Iowa and Wisconsin agricultural cooperatives, Minnesota tool-share networks, local food enterprise models in Zone 5 communities.

**Question 3: What economic bridges prevent community isolation?**

Research inter-community trade network models and federation structures. Document how communities with different surpluses coordinate exchange — the agricultural community with grain surplus and the urban community with manufacturing capacity. Investigate comparative advantage without global supply chains: how do small communities specialize and exchange at 5-10 community federation scale? Research barter protocols specifically for non-monetary inter-community trade. Midwest specificity: grain-livestock-labor trade networks, rural-urban economic bridging.

## Document Structure

10-12 major sections. Target 4,000-5,500 words per section in the core research sections.

1. **The Exchange Problem** (3,000-4,000 words) — What breaks when conventional currency fails? Historical case studies of currency failure (Weimar hyperinflation, Argentine 2001 peso crisis, Zimbabwe dollarization) with focus on what community-scale economic alternatives emerged and how well they functioned. Frame the problem at Zone 5 scale.

2. **Economic Failure Modes** (3,500-4,500 words) — Catalog the specific failure modes that end communities economically: barter inefficiency at scale, interpersonal debt networks that degrade cohesion, surplus rot from lack of storage coordination, skill concentration in unpaid critical workers. This is the diagnostic section; the rest of the document is prescription.

3. **Complementary Currencies: Design Principles** (5,000-6,000 words) — Mutual credit systems (WIR primary), local currencies (Bristol Pounds), LETS systems, timebanking. Design parameters for each: optimal scale, governance structure, demurrage mechanics, fraud prevention, failure modes. Extract actionable design principles for a community of 20-2,000 people.

4. **Commons Management: The Ostrom Framework** (5,000-6,000 words) — Apply Ostrom's eight design principles to Zone 5 community resource management. Concrete case studies for each principle. What resources are managed as commons (water, grazing, tools, seeds, skills) versus allocated by other mechanisms? What conditions cause commons governance to fail? Tragedy of the commons — what actually causes it and how the design principles prevent it.

5. **Cooperative Enterprise Models** (5,000-6,000 words) — Worker cooperatives, consumer cooperatives, agricultural cooperatives, land trusts. How each model handles surplus distribution, capital formation, and leadership accountability. Historical performance of cooperatives in economic stress periods. Midwest case studies: Iowa Farm Bureau cooperative network, Wisconsin dairy cooperatives, Minnesota food co-ops.

6. **Capital Formation Without Banks** (4,000-5,000 words) — How communities accumulate and allocate investment capital without bank lending: mutual aid funds, rotating savings and credit associations (ROSCAs), community investment clubs, land trust capital accumulation. Governance of community capital: how decisions are made about what gets funded.

7. **Replacing Market Price Signals** (4,000-5,000 words) — What replaces prices when markets don't function? Participatory budgeting (how it works, scale limits, what it allocates well and poorly), consensus resource allocation, labor hour accounting, needs-based distribution. Case studies: participatory budgeting in Porto Alegre (scale reference), local implementation guides. What coordination failures emerge without price signals and how alternative mechanisms address them.

8. **Gift Economies and Reciprocity Networks** (3,500-4,500 words) — Gift economy mechanics: how they function, what prevents free-rider problems, how reciprocity norms are established and enforced without formal contracts. Real-world gift economy implementations in agricultural communities. How gift economy layers integrate with mutual credit and barter systems. When gift economy logic is appropriate versus when more formal exchange structures are needed.

9. **Inter-Community Trade and Federation Economics** (5,000-6,000 words) — How 5-10 communities coordinate surplus exchange without global supply chains. Comparative advantage at small community scale: which specializations are viable (grain, livestock, manufacturing, medical skills). Trade protocols: how communities negotiate exchange rates when there is no common currency. Information systems for inter-community economic coordination (what Meshtastic mesh networks carry). Midwest regional economic integration case studies.

10. **Zone 5 Implications** (2,500-3,500 words) — How does Midwest geography, climate, and economic tradition shape implementation? Seasonal economic rhythms (harvest surplus, winter scarcity), temperature effects on storage and exchange, distance effects on inter-community trade feasibility. Existing Midwest cooperative infrastructure as implementation foundation. USDA extension office resources actively available. Zone 5 timeline for implementing each economic architecture.

11. **Implementation Templates** (3,000-4,000 words, mandatory) — Two complete templates:
    - *Template A: Local Currency Design Checklist* — step-by-step process for a community to design, launch, and govern a mutual credit or scrip currency system. Includes governance structure, initial credit allocation, anti-hoarding mechanisms, and exit procedures.
    - *Template B: Community Commons Charter* — governance document template for establishing commons management of a specific resource. Includes boundary definition, membership rules, monitoring system, graduated sanctions, and dispute resolution. Directly usable by a community of 25-200 people.

12. **Case Studies** (3,000-5,000 words, minimum 4) — Full case studies with: problem context, solution implemented, governance structure, economic outcomes, failure modes encountered, lessons extracted. Required cases: WIR Bank, Bristol Pounds, Vermont timebank network, Midwest agricultural cooperative (user's choice of Iowa or Wisconsin case). Optional additional: Argentina 2001 community currencies (Redes de Trueque), Japanese community currency networks.
