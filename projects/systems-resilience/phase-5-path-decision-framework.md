# Phase 5 Path Decision Framework

> **Purpose**: Translate Phase 4b options into a personalized decision framework. Help you evaluate which of three paths (Agricultural Intensification, Education/Knowledge Preservation, Governance Scaling) aligns with *your* resource constraints, timeline, skills, and risk tolerance.
>
> **Context**: Phase 4a (Technology Repair) is complete. Phase 4b decision is due by June 1, 2026, due to the Zone 5 agricultural planting window (hard deadline: June 15 for perennials).
>
> **Your decision**: Which path(s) unlock the most value given YOUR specific situation? This document helps you answer that.

---

## Executive Summary

**The core tension**: Agricultural Intensification has an irreversible biological deadline (perennial planting May–June 2026), but it requires sustained time investment over 15–22 hours in the next three weeks. Education/Knowledge Preservation is fastest to complete (13–19 hours, no seasonal deadline) but addresses Year 2+ failure modes. Governance Scaling fills a critical gap in Phase 3 (inter-community coordination) and unlocks FEMA BRIC funding (July 23 deadline) but requires institutional relationships.

**Most likely recommendation**: Start with Agricultural Intensification NOW (May 18–29) if you have at least 15 hours available in the next two weeks. This is the only path with an irreversible annual deadline. If Agricultural Intensification is already underway or deferred, Governance Scaling + Knowledge Preservation is the optimal second pairing.

**Decision checklist at end of this document will clarify your resource constraints and determine the exact path for you.**

## Path A: Agricultural Intensification Analysis

### When to Choose This Path

**Choose this path if:**
- You have land (1+ acres) OR access to community land
- You can commit 5–7 hours/week for the next 3 weeks (May 18–June 8)
- You want to establish food sovereignty that doesn't depend on external inputs
- You have gardening or farming background (or can hire/partner for the heavy labor)
- You care about supplying 10–30% of a community's calories within 5–7 years

### Time Requirements

**Document A (Perennial Systems & Food Forest Design)**: 8–12 hours
- Species selection for Zone 5 (1–2 hours)
- Food forest layering and spatial design (2–3 hours)
- Savanna Institute yield calculations and Kernza integration (2–3 hours)
- Intercropping intensification and Three Sisters at field scale (1–2 hours)

**Document B (Community Seed Saving & Variety Selection)**: 7–10 hours
- Open-pollinated vs. hybrid fundamentals and isolation distances (1–2 hours)
- Heirloom variety selection for Zone 5 (2–3 hours)
- Seed library infrastructure and governance models (2–3 hours)
- Case studies and implementation planning (1–2 hours)

**Hard deadline**: June 15, 2026 (end of Zone 5 perennial planting window). Perennials planted after June 15 face transplant stress, reduced survival, and shift all yield dates by a full year.

### Skill Gaps & Learning Curve

| **Skill** | **Current level assumed** | **Gap** | **Mitigation** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Agroforestry design** | Basic gardening experience | Moderate (understanding layering, spacing, light competition) | Savanna Institute case studies + UMN Extension field guides provide templates |
| **Species selection** | Know what grows in Zone 5 | Moderate (which varieties for resilience, disease resistance, multi-use) | Pre-research document (04-phase-4b-agricultural-intensification.md) contains full species tables |
| **Seed saving** | Never done | High (isolation distances, pollination knowledge, storage protocols) | Seed Savers Exchange network (Decorah, Iowa, 13,000+ members) available for mentoring |
| **Soil building** | Basic | Low-Moderate (organic matter sequestration, cover crop sequencing) | Practical Farmers of Iowa + MOSES provide guides |

**Learning curve**: Moderate if you have gardening background; steeper if starting from scratch (would recommend partnering with experienced farmers or MOSES workshop attendance).

### Integration with Existing Projects

- **seedwarden**: Seed library governance is *directly* aligned — seedwarden project is building the physical vault; Agricultural Intensification provides the governance, variety selection, and community coordination layer
- **mfg-farm**: Extends mfg-farm food production from household/small-community scale to community-scale perennials
- **off-grid-living**: Food forest is the primary Year 5+ food production method for off-grid communities
- **cybersecurity-hardening**: Minimal direct integration (food forests are low-tech)
- **resistance-research**: Minimal direct integration (agricultural production doesn't address political resilience)

### Risk Profile

**Best case**: Perennials planted May 2026, first supplemental yields 2028–2029 (asparagus, berries), meaningful community-scale production 2031–2032 (tree fruits, nuts). Community has documented seed varieties + year-round food security.

**Worst case**: Perennials fail due to poor site selection, wrong species for microclimate, or insufficient labor during establishment. Recovery: replant next spring (1-year delay). Seed library is maintained but provides only supplemental production (not community-scale).

**Most likely scenario**: Partial success — perennials establish on 50–70% of intended land, yield reaches 15–20% of community calories by Year 5 (not the 25–30% target). Seed library becomes operational for heirloom varieties. Community gains genuine food independence but not at projected scale.

**Reversibility**: Low. Perennial commitment spans 5+ years. Shifting to different food production system is disruptive (loses years of establishment).

## Path B: Education & Knowledge Preservation Analysis

### When to Choose This Path

**Choose this path if:**
- You have 3–5 hours/week available for 4 weeks (no hard deadline)
- You want to protect against Year 2+ knowledge loss (aging farmers, skill transmission gap)
- You're focused on building community learning infrastructure (schools, apprenticeships)
- You can defer to summer if Agricultural Intensification runs late

### Time Requirements

**Single Document (Knowledge Preservation & Community Learning)**: 13–19 hours total
- Community Knowledge Audit worksheet design (2–3 hours)
- Oral history methodology and Foxfire model (3–4 hours)
- Kiwix deployment guide and ZIM file curation (3–4 hours)
- Analog archival infrastructure and physical documentation (2–3 hours)
- Learning pod design and curriculum sequencing (2–3 hours)

**Calendar deadline**: None. This work can be built in Month 1–6 of a disruption. Soft deadline: January 2027 (NEH grant cycle for funding formal oral history projects).

### Skill Gaps & Learning Curve

| **Skill** | **Current level assumed** | **Gap** | **Mitigation** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Oral history methodology** | None required | Low (NEH methodology is documented; Foxfire model is published) | Alliance for California Traditional Arts model available (replicated nationally) |
| **Kiwix server setup** | Basic tech competency | Low (hardware: $50 Raspberry Pi; software: straightforward SD card flashing) | Full deployment guide available; 2025 field data shows 5W power draw, <$150 total |
| **Knowledge audit design** | None | Low (worksheet is a checklist; training can be conducted in an evening) | Community Boards model provides template |
| **Archival practices** | None | Low-Moderate (acid-free paper, pigment ink, storage protocols) | UNESCO Disaster Risk Management guide is authoritative; easily summarizable |

**Learning curve**: Low. This is primarily curation and documentation, not novel methodology. The research overhead is lowest of the three options.

### Integration with Existing Projects

- **resistance-research**: Direct parallel — resistance-research addresses institutional memory; Knowledge Preservation addresses community practitioner memory. Frameworks are compatible and cross-referenceable.
- **mfg-farm**: Indirect — Kiwix provides offline agricultural knowledge library (preserves mfg-farm documentation against digital infrastructure failure)
- **seedwarden**: Indirect — Kiwix hosts heirloom variety guides; Knowledge Audit identifies elder farmers at risk of knowledge loss
- **off-grid-living**: Direct — Kiwix serves offline technical manuals (iFixit for equipment repair, Khan Academy for troubleshooting)
- **cybersecurity-hardening**: Indirect — Kiwix is a decentralized alternative to cloud infrastructure

### Risk Profile

**Best case**: Community conducts Knowledge Audit, identifies 3–5 critical knowledge gaps (soil science, heritage variety cultivation, equipment maintenance). Kiwix deployed on Raspberry Pi in community center. Apprenticeship pairings begin. Formal NEH grant applied for 2027. Knowledge transmission becomes systematic rather than accidental.

**Worst case**: Community Knowledge Audit is conducted but apprenticeship pairings don't materialize due to scheduling. Kiwix server sits unused. Knowledge preservation infrastructure exists but is underutilized.

**Most likely scenario**: Knowledge Audit is completed and useful (identifies actual bottlenecks). Kiwix is deployed and accessed by 5–15 people regularly. One or two apprenticeship pairings begin. Oral history documentation starts informally.

**Reversibility**: Very high. Kiwix can be deployed or removed in a weekend. Community audit is non-binding. Apprenticeships can be paused without penalty.

## Path C: Governance Scaling Analysis

### When to Choose This Path

**Choose this path if:**
- You're engaged with local government (town council, township trustees) or civic organizations
- You have 2–4 hours/week available for 4–5 weeks
- You want to position your community for FEMA BRIC funding (July 23, 2026 deadline)
- You're thinking about inter-community coordination (shared water, roads, medical facilities)

### Time Requirements

**Single Document (Governance Scaling for Midwest Communities)**: 12–17 hours total
- Governance gap analysis and Ostrom framework (2–3 hours)
- Formal governance structures (Midwest township law, IMA templates) (3–4 hours)
- Resource allocation and rationing protocol design (2–3 hours)
- Conflict resolution and restorative justice models (2–3 hours)
- Regional coordination for 500–2,000 people (Transition Towns case studies) (2–3 hours)

**Calendar deadline**: Soft but concrete. FEMA BRIC application deadline is July 23, 2026. Communities with written governance frameworks, IMAs, and continuity-of-government plans are substantially more competitive for $1 billion in federal resilience funding.

### Skill Gaps & Learning Curve

| **Skill** | **Current level assumed** | **Gap** | **Mitigation** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Inter-municipal law (Iowa/Illinois/Minnesota)** | None required | Moderate (township authority, selectboard emergency powers are distinct and not well-documented) | FEMA Local Mitigation Planning Policy Guide (April 2025) is authoritative; state league of municipalities has templates |
| **Elinor Ostrom's governance framework** | None | Moderate (Nobel lecture is dense but is the canonical reference) | Simplified application framework provided in Phase 4b options analysis |
| **FEMA compliance** | None | Low-Moderate (BRIC application process is documented; compliance checklist is available) | FEMA Local Mitigation Planning Policy Guide is complete; Pew Charitable Trust has simplified guides |
| **Conflict resolution training** | None | Low (Community Boards model provides 3-day curriculum outline) | Alliance for Community Boards provides trainer network |

**Learning curve**: Moderate. This requires policy/legal reading (heavier than Knowledge Preservation) but less technical depth than Agricultural Intensification.

### Integration with Existing Projects

- **resistance-research**: Direct complement — resistance-research addresses democratic institutions and institutional resilience; Governance Scaling addresses practical inter-municipal coordination. Frameworks are synergistic.
- **cybersecurity-hardening**: Indirect — governance documents themselves need security (signed, stored redundantly, encrypted against tampering or loss)
- **mfg-farm**: Indirect — food production infrastructure (tool libraries, shared equipment) operates under governance rules you establish
- **seedwarden**: Indirect — seed library governance is enabled by inter-community coordination (sharing varieties across communities)
- **off-grid-living**: Indirect — off-grid systems operate under zoning rules and inter-community resource-sharing agreements you establish

### Risk Profile

**Best case**: Two or more neighboring communities develop signed Inter-Municipal Agreements (water sharing, road maintenance, medical facility access). Community council is established with rotating representation. Rationing protocol is drafted and tested in tabletop exercise. FEMA BRIC application is submitted with governance documentation; $5–$15 million in funding is awarded. Phase 5 regional federation has a solid institutional foundation.

**Worst case**: IMAs are drafted but are not politically viable (town councils hesitate to sign). Governance framework exists as a theoretical document but is not operationalized. No FEMA funding is pursued or awarded.

**Most likely scenario**: One solid inter-municipal agreement is signed (water sharing or medical facility access, most likely). Community governance council meets 3–4 times. Rationing protocol exists but hasn't been tested under real scarcity. FEMA BRIC application is submitted but faces competition.

**Reversibility**: High. Governance agreements can be renegotiated or dissolved. Unlike perennial planting (multi-year commitment), governance is iterative and adjustable.

## Recommendation & Next Steps

**Primary recommendation: Agricultural Intensification NOW (start May 18), then one secondary path June–July.**

**Rationale**:
1. **Irreversible time pressure**: The June 15 perennial planting window closes this year. Delaying by one year delays all yields by one year (2029 → 2030, 2032 → 2033). Every other path can be done anytime.
2. **Phase 5 dependency**: Agricultural Intensification is the explicit prerequisite for Phase 5 food system expansion (livestock, large-scale grain). Without it, Phase 5 is incomplete.
3. **Cross-project synergy**: Agricultural Intensification directly feeds seedwarden (variety selection) and off-grid-living (food production methods).

**Secondary path decision**:
- If you're engaged with local government: **Governance Scaling** (June 1–20) to position for July 23 FEMA BRIC deadline
- If governance is not a personal priority: **Knowledge Preservation** (July 1–14) for broadest community benefit and lowest implementation friction

**What changes the recommendation**:
- **If you don't have land access**: Skip Agricultural Intensification in Phase 4b; focus on Governance Scaling and Knowledge Preservation. Return to Agriculture in Phase 5 when community-scale land coordination is established.
- **If you must defer Agricultural Intensification**: Governance Scaling becomes primary (June–July for FEMA positioning). Knowledge Preservation can follow in August.
- **If July 23 FEMA deadline is not relevant to you**: Knowledge Preservation is the fastest secondary path (13–19 hours, no deadline pressure).
