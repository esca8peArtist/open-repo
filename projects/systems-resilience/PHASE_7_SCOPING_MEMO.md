# Phase 7 Research Scoping Memo
## Systems Resilience — What Comes After Phase 6

**Prepared**: June 3, 2026
**Decision context**: Phase 6 research (six domains: International Coordination, Intergenerational Knowledge Transmission, Infrastructure Interdependencies, Ecosystem Restoration, Economic Resilience, Institutional Learning) is in production June–August 2026. This memo scopes the research content question for Phase 7, distinct from the distribution and operationalization work described in `PHASE_7_ROADMAP.md`.

---

## What Phase 6 Left Undone

Before identifying Phase 7 options, it is necessary to state clearly what Phase 6 does and does not resolve.

Phases 1–3 built the operational foundation: individual, household, and community resilience at 1–150 person scale. Phase 4 added integration analysis (cross-domain cascade mapping) and practical repair and technology maintenance. Phase 5 added depth in five hard domains: water systems, food preservation, psychological support, conflict resolution, and community-scale microgrids. Phase 6 adds the enabling-environment layer: the six macro conditions under which local resilience either works or doesn't (international coordination, intergenerational knowledge, infrastructure interdependencies, ecosystem restoration, economic architecture, and institutional learning).

After Phase 6, the knowledge corpus answers:

- What to do, in most operational domains, at most scales
- Why systems fail in predictable, cascade-driven ways
- What macro conditions (governance, economics, ecology, knowledge transmission) determine whether operational resilience holds over time

What the corpus does not yet answer — the genuine gaps left after Phase 6 — are three things. First, it has no worked examples: real communities applying the framework, with documented results. Second, it is not yet calibrated to how a real community would apply the framework under pressure: what breaks first, what gets skipped, how do practitioners adapt when conditions don't match theory. Third, it addresses each domain separately, but a practitioner trying to launch resilience infrastructure doesn't encounter domains sequentially — they encounter the whole system at once, and the framework doesn't yet show how the parts connect in time under real operational conditions.

These three gaps map directly to three candidate Phase 7 paths.

## Candidate 1: Implementation Case Studies

### What It Is

A research phase that documents 4–6 real communities applying elements of the systems-resilience framework — or analogous frameworks — and produces case study documents covering what they did, what succeeded, what failed, and what general lessons transfer. This is applied validation: does the framework hold up when it meets reality?

Communities are selected across a range of contexts: a rural Midwest agricultural community, an urban mutual aid network, a post-disaster recovery context (e.g., Puerto Rico, Appalachia), and at least one international case (e.g., Cuba's community food security adaptation, or a Pacific Island community operating under climate stress). Case studies are cross-referenced explicitly against Phase 1–6 framework recommendations to identify where the framework is validated, where it is incomplete, and where it is actively wrong.

### Scope and Hours

- Research production: 4–6 case studies at 8,000–12,000 words each = 40,000–72,000 words total
- Source work: each case study draws primarily from primary-source material (journalistic coverage, academic case studies, direct reports, NGO documentation) plus secondary synthesis
- Estimated hours: 160–200 hours across 10–12 weeks (roughly parallel to Phase 5 or 6 scale)
- Author profile: social science researcher or journalist with community resilience field experience preferred

### Key Research Questions

1. What aspects of resilience frameworks do communities actually implement, and in what order? (Revealed priorities vs. stated frameworks)
2. What are the primary failure modes in real implementation — governance breakdown, resource shortfall, social fragmentation, technical failure?
3. Where does Zone 5 specificity in the existing corpus transfer to other contexts, and where is it a limitation for broader audiences?
4. What does the gap between documented best practice and actual community behavior tell us about what the framework is missing?

### Expected Deliverables

- 4–6 standalone case study documents (one per community)
- A cross-case synthesis document (15,000–20,000 words) drawing the framework implications from the cases
- A framework revision appendix identifying corrections, additions, and confidence adjustments to Phase 1–6 claims based on case study evidence
- A "common failure modes" reference document for Phase 1–3 practitioners

### Timeline Fit

Earliest start: August 2026, once Phase 6 research is complete enough to frame case study cross-references.
Realistic production window: September–November 2026 (10–12 weeks).
No blocking dependencies. Phase 6 content informs but does not gate case study research.

### Confidence Assessment

Source availability: Medium-high. Real community documentation exists (Puerto Rico, Cuban food security, Appalachian community resilience networks, Transition Town documented cases, post-Katrina Gulf Coast mutual aid) but is scattered and requires significant synthesis effort. No single repository covers the full case library needed.

Framework validation risk: This is the only Phase 7 candidate that could produce findings that require revising Phase 1–6 content. That is also its highest value — the framework has never been externally stress-tested. If a case study shows that the governance structure recommended in Phase 3 fails at 80-person scale under food scarcity conditions, that is a critical correction that changes the advice given to every practitioner who reads the corpus.

## Candidate 2: Cross-Scale Integration Guide

### What It Is

A research phase that produces a single integration document showing how the individual, household, and community scales of the framework connect — and specifically, how a practitioner or community moves between scales under real operational conditions. The current corpus treats each scale separately. Phases 1, 2, and 3 have their own documents. Phase 6's six domains are each standalone. But no document in the corpus answers the question a practitioner actually faces: "I am a household of four in rural Zone 5, we have completed Phase 2, and we want to help our 30-household village start Phase 3 — where do we start and how do the systems we've already built connect to what the community needs?"

This is a synthesis and integration research phase, not new primary research. It draws on the existing 100,000+ word corpus and the Phase 6 production documents and produces the missing connective tissue.

### Scope and Hours

- Primary deliverable: one 35,000–50,000 word integration guide
- Supplementary: 3–5 bridge documents (each covering one transition: individual to household, household to community, community to federation scale)
- Estimated hours: 100–140 hours across 8–10 weeks (lower than Candidates 1 and 3 because this is synthesis, not primary research)
- Author profile: generalist with strong synthesis skills and familiarity with the full Phase 1–6 corpus; does not require domain expertise

### Key Research Questions

1. What are the actual transition thresholds — the moments where household resilience infrastructure either integrates with community infrastructure or becomes a liability (incompatible systems, asymmetric resource control)?
2. What existing research on collective action, common-pool resource management, and social movement scaling describes how the individual-to-community transition works in practice?
3. What are the critical sequencing questions — which Phase 1 capabilities are prerequisites for Phase 3 participation, and which Phase 3 systems require Phase 2 completion first?
4. How do the Phase 6 enabling-environment domains (particularly economic resilience and institutional learning) interact with Phase 1–3 operational decisions at the household level?

### Expected Deliverables

- One cross-scale integration guide (the primary deliverable): organized as a decision framework for practitioners at each scale transition point
- Scale transition diagrams: visual maps showing system connections across individual, household, and community layers
- Three bridge protocol documents: one per scale transition (1→2, 2→3, 3→federation)
- A revised quick-start guide for the README (replacing the current three-option guide with a cross-scale-aware entry point)

### Timeline Fit

Earliest start: July 2026 — can begin as soon as Phase 6 domain outlines are complete (research outlines, not final documents, provide sufficient source material).
Realistic production window: July–September 2026.
This is the only Phase 7 candidate that can begin before Phase 6 is complete. Phase 6 final documents improve the output but are not required to start.
Blocking dependencies: None. This is entirely internal to the existing corpus plus standard academic literature on collective action and social scaling.

### Confidence Assessment

Source availability: High. The academic literature on collective action (Ostrom, Putnam, Hirschman), social movement scaling (McAdam, Tarrow), and transition management (Rotmans, Geels) is mature and well-documented. The primary research challenge is synthesis, not discovery.

Scope risk: Lower than both other candidates. The integration guide is bounded by the existing corpus. It does not generate new knowledge claims; it organizes existing ones. This makes it easier to scope, easier to complete on time, and less likely to require revision of other documents. The tradeoff is that it does not extend the framework's coverage — it makes the existing coverage more usable.

Unique risk: If Phase 6 produces findings that substantially revise the Phase 1–5 framework assumptions, the integration guide written before Phase 6 is final will need updating. This argues for sequencing it after at least Phase 6 Domains 62 (infrastructure interdependencies) and 65 (institutional learning) are complete, since these are the domains most likely to affect Phase 1–3 operational recommendations.

## Candidate 3: Emergency Scenario Response Playbooks

### What It Is

A research phase that produces 3–5 fully developed emergency scenario response playbooks — specific, step-by-step guides for how a community equipped with the systems-resilience framework would respond to distinct, high-consequence crisis types. Each playbook is built around a single scenario: a prolonged grid outage (5+ days), a food supply collapse (harvest failure or supply chain rupture), a pandemic or regional disease outbreak, a displacement event (flood or wildfire forcing evacuation and return), or a political disruption (governance breakdown, civil unrest, or infrastructure sabotage).

This is not a general "emergency preparedness" guide. Each playbook assumes the community has implemented Phase 1–3 plus relevant Phase 5/6 systems, and documents how those specific systems would be activated in sequence, what decision points arise, who has authority, what can go wrong, and what the recovery arc looks like. It is the framework in motion — the difference between having a fire extinguisher and knowing how to use it in an actual fire.

### Scope and Hours

- 3–5 scenario playbooks at 10,000–15,000 words each = 40,000–75,000 words
- Each playbook includes: scenario parameters, Day 1–3 immediate response protocol, Week 1–4 stabilization sequence, Month 1–6 recovery arc, decision authority map, failure mode analysis, cross-domain cascade diagram specific to the scenario
- Estimated hours: 180–240 hours across 12–14 weeks
- Author profile: emergency management practitioner or someone with scenario planning and risk analysis experience; academic background in disaster studies is a significant advantage

### Key Research Questions

1. What do empirically documented community responses to the five scenario types reveal about what framework elements are load-bearing vs. nice-to-have under pressure?
2. What decision points in real disasters most commonly exceed community capacity — and how does the framework need to be more specific to actually help at those points?
3. How does the cascade structure in each scenario interact with Phase 6's infrastructure interdependencies and governance domains — and does Phase 6's analysis predict the actual sequence correctly?
4. What does the existing FEMA/ICS emergency management literature not cover that community-scale resilience frameworks specifically address?

### Expected Deliverables

- 3–5 scenario playbooks (one per crisis type, standalone documents)
- A scenario comparison matrix: which framework systems are activated in which scenarios, revealing which investments have the highest cross-scenario leverage
- A practitioner decision support guide: the minimal viable subset of Phase 1–6 systems that covers 80% of the scenarios
- A tabletop exercise guide for communities to practice each scenario

### Timeline Fit

Earliest start: September 2026 — requires Phase 6 Domains 62 (infrastructure interdependencies) and 65 (institutional learning) to be substantially complete, since these domains directly inform the cascade and governance response portions of each scenario.
Realistic production window: October–December 2026.
Hard dependency: Phase 6 Domains 62 and 65 must be in late draft before scenario playbook production begins. Starting without them produces playbooks that will need significant revision.

### Confidence Assessment

Source availability: Medium. Primary sources exist for each scenario type (FEMA after-action reports, RAND disaster response studies, UNDRR case documentation, academic emergency management literature, community disaster recovery memoirs and oral histories). The challenge is that high-quality primary source material is scenario-specific and requires significant scoping work per playbook. Playbooks also require synthesis across operational, governance, and social domains simultaneously — higher integration complexity than Candidates 1 or 2.

Leverage: Highest of the three candidates for practitioner utility. A practitioner who has read Phase 1–6 in its entirety may still feel uncertain about what to do on Day 1 of a real event. Playbooks resolve that uncertainty directly. They are also the most distributable artifact — a community emergency manager can use a single well-written scenario playbook without needing the full corpus.

Risk: This is the most demanding candidate to execute well. A poorly researched scenario playbook is worse than no playbook — it creates false confidence. The source work and scenario parameter calibration require domain expertise in emergency management, not just resilience theory.

## Recommendation: Cross-Scale Integration First, Then Case Studies

### Why Cross-Scale Integration (Candidate 2) is the Highest-Impact First Move

Three factors make it the strongest starting point.

**It resolves the most immediate practitioner gap.** The corpus currently has roughly 100,000 words in isolated domain documents and phase-specific guides. There is no document that tells a household-scale practitioner how to connect with the community-scale work, or that tells a community organizer which household-scale prerequisites they need their participants to have completed. This is the gap most likely to produce practitioner abandonment — not content gaps, but navigational failure. Cross-scale integration directly addresses it.

**It can start now.** Unlike Candidates 1 and 3, the integration guide does not require Phase 6 to be complete. It can begin in July 2026 while Phase 6 is in production, using Phase 6 outlines as source material. This means Phase 7 research progress does not have to wait until September. The integration guide can be substantively complete by September and ready for publication before Phase 6 final documents are done.

**It makes everything else more useful.** The integration guide is the navigational layer for the full corpus. Once it exists, the Phase 6 distribution and operationalization work (described in the existing `PHASE_7_ROADMAP.md`) becomes substantially more effective because practitioners have a clear path through the material. Case studies (Candidate 1) become more useful because they can be cross-referenced against the integration guide. Scenario playbooks (Candidate 3) become more targeted because they can assume readers have used the integration guide.

### Why Case Studies (Candidate 1) is the Best Phase 7b Follow-On

Once the integration guide is complete (roughly September 2026), the case study phase should begin as Phase 7b. The case studies do two things the other candidates cannot: they validate the framework against reality, and they produce the story-form content that is most effective for the distribution work. Practitioners are persuaded by examples, not frameworks. The case study documents are the artifacts most likely to be cited, shared, and acted on.

The framework revision appendix produced by the case study phase (identifying where Phase 1–6 recommendations need correction based on real-world evidence) is also the highest-integrity contribution the project can make to the resilience literature — most resilience documentation has not been tested against real community outcomes.

### Sequencing Summary

| Phase | Content | Start | End | Dependencies |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Phase 7a | Cross-Scale Integration Guide | July 2026 | September 2026 | Phase 6 domain outlines complete |
| Phase 7b | Implementation Case Studies | October 2026 | December 2026 | Phase 7a complete, Phase 6 final docs near-complete |
| Phase 7c (optional) | Emergency Scenario Playbooks | January 2027 | March 2027 | Phase 6 Domains 62 + 65 finalized, Phase 7b synthesis complete |

Candidate 3 (scenario playbooks) is kept as Phase 7c rather than Phase 7b because it requires Phase 6 Domain 62 and 65 to be final-draft quality, and it requires the case study synthesis to inform its failure mode analysis. Sequencing it before case studies would produce playbooks that haven't been calibrated against how communities actually behave under pressure.

## What Not to Do

Two options from the initial list are not recommended as Phase 7 priorities.

**Regional deep-dives** are a scope extension (more of the same type of content, different geographies) rather than a framework advance. The Zone 5 specificity of the corpus is an asset in the target market. Diluting it with additional regional versions before the Zone 5 corpus is validated against real community outcomes would spread effort without deepening the framework.

**Monitoring and adaptation frameworks** are legitimate as a Phase 8 or publication companion, but are premature as Phase 7 content. A monitoring framework requires first knowing what the framework actually recommends in practice (integration guide) and whether those recommendations hold (case studies). Writing a monitoring protocol for a framework that hasn't been tested against real communities is writing specifications for an unvalidated system.

---

**Confidence level for this recommendation**: High on the diagnosis of gaps (cross-scale integration and case study validation are both clearly absent from the current corpus and clearly needed). Medium on timeline estimates (these reflect Phase 5 and 6 production rates; Phase 7 complexity is comparable but the synthesis work in Candidate 2 is faster than primary research). Medium on case study source availability (the community documentation needed exists but is distributed and requires scoping effort per case; two to three of the five candidate cases have strong source libraries, two have thin practitioner documentation).

**Primary uncertainty**: Whether Phase 6 Domains 62 (infrastructure interdependencies) and 65 (institutional learning) produce significant revisions to Phase 1–3 framework recommendations. If they do, the cross-scale integration guide will need a revision pass after Phase 6 is complete. This is manageable (targeted revision of integration points, not a full rewrite) but should be planned for.
