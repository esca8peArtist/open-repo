# Conflict Resolution and Governance Framework
## A Tier 2 Facilitation Skills Deep Dive

> **Region**: Midwest US (Zone 5) | **Scale**: Household (8–25 people); extends to inter-household
> **Phase**: 5 — Gap Fill | **Tier**: 2 — Household Operational Domain
> **Prerequisite reading**: phase-5/tier-2-household-coordination-infrastructure-guide.md (establishes four-step conflict protocol this document extends)
> **Read alongside**: phase-5/tier-2-psychological-support-guide.md (de-escalation overlap)

## The Most Important Finding

The Phase 3 security document establishes that internal conflict — not external threat — is the dominant security burden in any sustained disruption beyond two weeks. The Household Coordination guide makes the same finding at household scale. This document exists because a governance structure that says "step 4 is assembly review" is not the same as having a facilitator who knows what to do in the room. The gap between documented process and actual practice is where household cohesion fails.

The most important insight from the conflict resolution literature for household-scale application: **the type of conflict determines the correct process**. Applying relationship mediation to a resource allocation dispute wastes time and generates false intimacy that breaks down at the next allocation dispute. Applying policy review to a relationship conflict suppresses it rather than resolving it. Values conflicts require structured dialogue, not mediation. Structural conflicts require governance change, not facilitation. Before any intervention begins, the facilitator's first task is to correctly identify what kind of conflict they are dealing with.

The second finding: a household with a trained lay mediator is a more secure household than one with better locks and more weapons. Conflict resolution capacity is security infrastructure.

Evidence note: NVC's 2024 PMC scoping review (PMC 10916228) confirms effectiveness in interpersonal conflict reduction, primarily in healthcare settings. [1] Restorative justice has a 2025 meta-analysis (Sage, Fulham et al.) confirming moderate effect on harm reduction in community settings. [2] The intersection of NVC + restorative circles in homestead community contexts remains under-studied; this document's claims are practitioner consensus with the strongest available evidence anchors stated accurately.

## Section 1: Conflict Typology — Not All Conflicts Are the Same

### The Classification Framework

The most common facilitation error is applying the wrong process to the wrong type of conflict. This section establishes a four-category working typology for household-scale conflicts. Before any intervention, the facilitator asks: "What kind of conflict is this?" The correct identification determines whether the situation requires policy application, emotional facilitation, values dialogue, or structural governance change.

**Type 1 — Resource Conflicts**

Disputes about allocation of scarce resources: food ration quantities, labor assignment, physical space, equipment use time, access to shared equipment or vehicles. These are the most common conflict type in early disruption, when scarcity first becomes palpable and the household's allocation rules are untested.

*Distinguishing feature*: The core dispute is about fairness of allocation or clarity of rules, not about the relationship between the parties. Both parties often accept that the resource is genuinely limited; the disagreement is about the fairness of the distribution method.

*Practical examples*: 
- "I'm doing the water hauling four times a week and others are doing it once. That's not fair." (Labor fairness)
- "The food rationing isn't clear — some people are getting larger portions." (Allocation clarity)
- "I can't work on my projects because the shared workshop is booked constantly; I need dedicated time." (Access fairness)

*Correct process*: Domain coordinator review with clear documented criteria. If the household has a written allocation protocol (and it should — the Household Coordination guide establishes this), the coordinator applies it to resolve the dispute. The decision is made on the basis of stated criteria, not on sympathy for one party's argument. If no protocol exists: this conflict reveals a governance gap that must be closed before the conflict can be resolved. The appropriate response is: "We don't have a clear rule about this. Let's develop one together" — not mediation, but protocol development.

*Common error*: Sending a resource dispute to mediation. This produces an outcome that satisfies neither party (the mediator creates a compromise rather than applying a standard) and signals to the household that rules are negotiable under pressure. Over time, this destroys confidence in household governance and generates more conflicts as people learn that they can negotiate rules through dispute.

**Type 2 — Relationship Conflicts**

Disputes arising from interpersonal history, communication patterns, unmet relational needs, or perceived disrespect. The stated cause is often a specific incident, but the emotional charge indicates a deeper pattern.

*Distinguishing feature*: The emotional charge is disproportionate to the stated cause. The dispute recurs in similar form. Both parties have strong feelings about what the other person *meant*, not just what they did. Both parties may describe the other as intentionally disrespecting them.

*Practical examples*:
- "In the household meeting, you always interrupt me. You don't respect my opinion." (Perceived disrespect)
- "You treat me like a subordinate instead of an equal. I'm tired of it." (Power dynamics perceived as unequal)
- "He never remembers what I say. He pretends to listen but he doesn't. I feel invisible." (Attention and valuing)
- "Every time I volunteer for something, she assumes I can't do it and takes it over. It's insulting." (Perceived underestimation)

*Correct process*: Facilitated conversation with emotional space. NVC framework (Section 2). The goal is not agreement on who was right but mutual understanding of what happened, what it meant to each person, and a specific behavioral agreement going forward. The facilitator creates a structured container in which both parties can speak and be heard without interruption.

*Common error*: Applying policy review to a relationship conflict ("the rule says we listen respectfully in meetings, so you need to follow the rule"). This suppresses the emotional content and creates a compliance response rather than a relational repair. The person whose disrespect has been named follows the rule externally while feeling unheard and unseen. The conflict resurfaces within weeks, often with added bitterness.

**Type 3 — Values Conflicts**

Disputes about what the household should prioritize when fundamental values are in tension: security versus openness to new members; conservation versus physical comfort; individual autonomy versus collective decision-making; risk tolerance; religious or spiritual practice versus secular operation.

*Distinguishing feature*: Neither party is objectively wrong within their own framework. The dispute has a philosophical dimension that cannot be resolved through factual evidence — it reflects different priorities about what matters most. The conflict recurs regardless of policy adjustments because the underlying value difference remains unaddressed.

*Practical examples*:
- Person A: "We need strict vetting of new members for security. This household must be isolated and protected." Person B: "We should be open to refugees and vulnerable people. Exclusion violates our values." (Security vs. compassion)
- Person A: "We need to conserve resources for the long haul. No luxuries." Person B: "People need some comfort and joy to survive psychologically. Quality of life matters as much as quantity." (Conservation vs. quality of life)
- Person A: "Decisions should be made by people with expertise in that domain." Person B: "Every household member should have equal say in all decisions." (Efficiency vs. equality)

*Correct process*: Structured dialogue to identify both the values being prioritized and the underlying concerns. This is not mediation, which implies finding a middle point between two positions. Not policy review, which can only apply values that already exist in written rules. The facilitator's role is to map the value systems onto concrete situations and find decisions that satisfy the most important concerns on both sides. The question is not "Who is right?" but "How do we honor both of these values?" Sometimes the answer is creative policy that addresses both concerns (e.g., a balance between security and openness — clear vetting process that allows bringing people in under defined conditions). Sometimes the answer is that the household must make an explicit values choice and accept that one value takes priority in this decision.

*Common error*: Attempting to resolve a values conflict by declaring one value superior to the other or by suppressing the minority value. This is governance, not facilitation. If the household assembly has not made a values-level decision, a mediator cannot make it for them. The correct outcome may be: "We need to convene the full household assembly to make a values decision. That is outside the scope of what I can facilitate."

**Type 4 — Structural Conflicts**

Disputes that keep recurring because the household's governance structure creates them: a role assignment that systematically disadvantages one person, a decision process that consistently silences certain demographic groups, a resource allocation rule with predictably inequitable outcomes, a communication pattern that prevents certain people from being heard.

*Distinguishing feature*: Multiple parties affected, not just two. The pattern predates the current dispute — it may have emerged with the first person in that role, and it persists with each new person. Changing the relationship between the current disputants does not change the outcome for the next persons in the same structural position.

*Practical examples*:
- The person coordinating childcare is always called into other work and childcare gets deprioritized, leaving children unsupervised. The third person to hold the role complains about being unable to do the job. (Structural deprioritization)
- Certain demographic groups (e.g., women, younger members, people from a particular socioeconomic background) consistently lose in household votes. The problem is not any single decision but a decision-making pattern. (Structural marginalization)
- The household's main coordinator makes all decisions; when conflicts emerge, they always decide in favor of the parties they have the best personal relationship with. (Structural personalism instead of principle)

*Correct process*: Structural change, not facilitation. The facilitator's job in a structural conflict is to name it accurately — "This conflict keeps happening because the structure creates it. Mediation won't fix a structure problem" — and refer it to the household assembly as a governance matter. The assembly may decide to change the role, change the decision process, change the allocation rule, or implement other structural changes.

*Common error*: Repeatedly mediating the same conflict between different people because no one names the structural cause. The conflict resolves temporarily with each mediation, then reemerges when the next person enters the structural position. This creates the false impression that the current disputants are unusually difficult, when in fact the structure is the problem.

**Source anchors**: Fisher and Ury, "Getting to Yes" [3]; Racial Equity Tools Conflict Transformation [4]; Habitatista communal living conflict resolution [5]; Community Finders decision-making frameworks [6]; Ostrom design principles [21].

## Section 2: Nonviolent Communication as the Household Facilitation Language

### Why NVC as the Foundation

NVC is not the only conflict resolution framework available, and its limitations must be acknowledged upfront: the evidence base is strongest in healthcare settings; it assumes cultural comfort with direct emotional expression (which varies by family background and culture); and it requires sufficient cognitive capacity to identify feelings and needs (which may not be available during acute crisis). However, NVC remains the most widely practiced, most accessible to lay practitioners, and most teachable in the time constraints of a household training context. Its four components are learnable in a weekend and applicable without credentials or specialized training. More importantly, its structure — observation before judgment, separation of feeling from thought, naming of underlying needs — addresses the most common household facilitation failure mode: the escalation that happens when two people interpret each other's actions as intentional disrespect rather than understanding the need that motivated the action.

Marshall Rosenberg's four NVC components are the foundation:

1. **Observation**: What happened, without evaluation or interpretation. "You were 20 minutes late to the water rotation this morning" is an observation. "You always blow off your responsibilities" is an evaluation. The observation is specific, time-limited, and factual. It answers: What did I see or hear?

2. **Feeling**: How the situation lands emotionally. "I felt anxious" or "I felt frustrated" or "I felt scared" — a single emotion word. "I felt like you don't care" is not a feeling; it's a thought (an interpretation of the other person's motivation). "I felt unheard" is a thought. "I felt sad" is a feeling. The distinction matters because feelings are universal and relatable, while thoughts can be debated. It answers: What emotion arose in me?

3. **Need**: The underlying need behind the feeling. "I need to be able to count on the water rotation being covered so I can plan my morning." "I need to know that my work matters and is recognized." "I need to feel secure that household resources are being managed fairly." The need is the legitimate concern beneath the frustration — it's universal and shared, even when the two people disagreed about how to meet it. It answers: What matters to me fundamentally?

4. **Request**: A specific, actionable, time-bounded ask. Not a demand; a request that the other person can say yes or no to. "Would you be willing to let me know by 7 AM if you can't make your shift so I can find coverage?" "Would you be willing to check in with me after the next work rotation to tell me how you thought it went?" The request addresses the need, is specific enough to be actionable, and is modest enough to be realistic. It answers: What do I need from you specifically?

### Translating Common Household Complaints into NVC

This is the practical work that makes NVC usable in household contexts. The power of translation is that it reframes a complaint that sounds like attack ("You always take more than your share") into a request that is specific and actionable ("Would you be willing to help me verify the food portions are equal by documenting them?"). This reframing doesn't diminish the concern — it clarifies it and makes resolution possible.

### Empathic Listening vs. Agreement — The Mediator's Core Skill

The distinction that breaks down most often under stress: hearing someone's concern fully does not require agreeing with them, believing they are right, or accepting their interpretation of what happened. The mediator who paraphrases a grievance accurately — "So what I'm hearing is that you feel the allocation was unfair and you need to know the criteria were applied consistently — is that right?" — is not endorsing the grievance. They are confirming understanding. This is the most underused de-escalation move in lay facilitation.

The psychological mechanism: When a person feels truly heard — when someone has accurately captured what they said and how they feel about it — the amygdala's threat response (fight/flight/freeze) begins to deactivate. The person can think more clearly. They are less likely to escalate. The conversation becomes possible. If the mediator appears to be disagreeing, defending the other person, or dismissing the concern, the threat response intensifies.

Empathic listening technique:
- Mirror the observation back: "You said that [specific incident] happened. Is that what happened?"
- Mirror the feeling: "And you felt [emotion] about it. Is that right?"
- Confirm the need: "You need [stated need]. Have I got that right?"
- Do not add your own interpretation or judgment. Do not say "I'm sure they didn't mean it that way" (which dismisses the concern). Do not say "You're overreacting" (which minimizes it). Mirror the temperature: if someone is angry, acknowledge "You sound really angry about this" — do not try to calm them down by soothing language.

The mediator can then turn to the other person: "What did you hear them say? Did we get it right?" and give the other person a chance to respond to what was actually said, not to what they assumed was meant.

## Section 3: Facilitation Skills — The Mediator's Toolkit

### The Pre-Meeting — The Foundation of Effective Mediation

The single most important facilitation tool is the conversation that happens before the facilitated conversation. Running separate pre-meetings with each party before bringing them together serves multiple critical functions:

1. **Safety assessment**: The mediator learns about any safety concerns (physical threat, intimidation history, fear) before creating a joint space. If safety concerns exist, mediation may not be appropriate (see Section 5). The pre-meeting is where this becomes visible.

2. **Emotional preparation**: Each party gets to express their full story without interruption; they arrive at the joint meeting having already been heard at least once. This reduces the urgency to interrupt or defend themselves in the joint session, because they have already had a chance to express themselves fully.

3. **Goal clarification**: The mediator learns what outcome would constitute resolution for each party. This is critical: the person who says "I just want them to understand they hurt me" has a different need than the person who says "I need a clear process so this doesn't happen again." The mediator can identify in advance where those goals are compatible and where they directly conflict.

4. **Reality-testing**: The mediator learns what each person's actual account of the facts is. Often, the two accounts of what happened differ significantly. Understanding both versions before the joint session allows the mediator to frame the joint conversation around the discrepancy ("You experienced this event differently; let's understand both sides") rather than around accusations ("You're lying").

**The pre-meeting structure** (20–30 minutes per person):
- *Opening*: "I'm meeting with [other person] separately too. Everything you tell me is confidential from them, except for safety concerns. My job is to understand your perspective fully before we meet together."
- *Story gathering*: "Tell me what happened from your perspective. Take your time; I want to understand."
- *Feeling and need*: "How did this affect you? What was most difficult about it?"
- *Goal clarification*: "What would need to happen for you to feel this is resolved? What outcome would work for you?"
- *Willingness to change*: "What, if anything, are you willing to do differently going forward?"
- *Closing*: "I'll meet with [other person] now. Then I'll invite both of you to talk together about this. If that doesn't seem like it will work, I'll let you know."

The three essential questions to answer in pre-meetings:
- **"What outcome would feel like resolution to you?"** — This may be very different from what the other person thinks. Person A might want an apology and recognition of harm. Person B might want to establish a clear rule so this doesn't happen again. Both are legitimate needs; the mediator's job is to see if both can be partially met.
- **"What do you need the other person to understand that you don't think they currently do?"** — This surfaces the core grievance beneath the surface issue. Often it's not the incident itself but what the person believes the incident means about how they are viewed ("You think I'm incompetent" or "You don't respect me" or "You care more about rules than about people").
- **"What are you willing to offer or change in this situation?"** — The person who enters the mediation saying "I won't change anything, they're the problem" is unlikely to reach resolution. The mediator needs to know this in advance. A person might be willing to change communication patterns ("I'll tell you if I can't make my shift instead of just not showing up") but not willing to change the underlying resource allocation.

### Active Listening Under Duress

The mediator's primary tool during the joint session. Under stress, both parties are operating with reduced cognitive capacity — the prefrontal cortex (deliberate reasoning) is partially offline, the amygdala is activated. This is not a moral failure; it's neurobiological. The body's threat response redirects blood flow and mental resources toward fight/flight/freeze, away from calm reasoning.

Reflective listening — accurately paraphrasing what was said with correct emotional temperature — serves multiple functions:
- De-escalation (signals: you are being heard, understood, taken seriously)
- Comprehension check (allows the speaker to correct misunderstandings before they compound)
- Model for the other person (demonstrates how to listen without immediately defending)

**Critical distinctions in reflective listening**:
- Reflect the emotional content accurately. Do not over-soothe ("I'm sure they didn't mean it that way," which suggests the person's feelings are wrong). Do not minimize ("that doesn't sound so bad," which suggests the concern is trivial). Mirror the temperature accurately: "You sound really angry about this, and you're also scared about what it means for the household — is that right?" This confirms you have understood both the intensity and the specific quality of the emotion.
- Do not insert your own interpretation or judgment. "So you felt hurt when they said that" is reflective listening. "So you felt hurt, but I think they were trying to be helpful" is judgment.
- Use the person's own language when possible. If they said "I felt disrespected," use "disrespected," not "upset" or "hurt." The specific word choice shows you have listened precisely.

**The reflection format**: "When [observation], you felt [feeling] because [need]. Have I got that right?" This invites confirmation and correction.

### Impasse Tools — When the Conversation Stalls

When conversation reaches an impasse (both parties are repeating the same points, neither is hearing the other, or the conversation is becoming heated), the facilitator has a structured toolkit to move past the stall:

**The hypothetical** ("underlying interest exploration"):
"If you could change one thing about this situation that would make it workable for you, what would it be?" 
This technique bypasses stated positions (what people say they want) and moves toward underlying interests (what they actually care about). Often, two people are stuck in competing positions when their underlying interests could be met together. Example: Person A's position is "No new members." Person B's position is "We should welcome refugees." The underlying interests might be: Person A needs to know the household can survive the winter with current resources (security interest). Person B needs to know the household is living according to its values (values interest). A path forward emerges: "What if we screened new members carefully and brought someone in only if we could confirm food security for that person?" This meets both interests.

**The cooling pause** ("physiological reset"):
"We're reaching a point where we might start saying things we don't mean. Let's take a 20-minute break and come back to this."
Not a multi-day recess (which allows factions to form, grievances to harden, and stories to grow worse), but a short physiological reset. Specify the return: "We'll resume in 20 minutes at this table with coffee." The specificity prevents the break from feeling like abandonment. Twenty minutes allows the amygdala to calm down and the prefrontal cortex to come back online. Use the break to check in with each party separately ("How are you doing? Do you want to continue?") and to assess whether the conversation is productive or whether mediation may not be the right process.

**The reframe** ("shared goal identification"):
"Both of you are saying you want the household to function well and people to feel valued. Let's talk about what 'functioning well' and 'feeling valued' look like for each of you."
This is not false agreement or therapeutic smoothing. It is an accurate observation that competing parties often share terminal goals (what they ultimately care about) while disagreeing about instrumental approaches (how to get there). By naming the shared goal, the mediator creates psychological space for problem-solving: "We agree on what matters. We disagree on how to get there. Let's figure that out together."

**The interests clarification** ("separating interests from positions"):
When two people are locked in a position debate ("You say X is the answer, I say Y is the answer"), the mediator asks each person separately: "What are you actually trying to protect or achieve here? What matters most to you about this?" Often the answer is not the stated position. Person A says the position is "We need a strict work schedule," but the interest is "I need to feel like everyone is contributing fairly." Person B says the position is "We should work flexibly based on what needs to be done," but the interest is "I need to feel trusted, not controlled." Once the interests are clear, solutions emerge that neither position alone would have generated.

**The values inventory** ("values clarification in values conflicts"):
In a values conflict that is masquerading as a resource or relationship conflict, a structured values comparison can surface shared values that neither party realized they held. The structured process: Each party independently lists their top-five values from a standardized values list (or generates their own; Schwartz's basic human values framework is comprehensive and neutrally framed). The two lists are then compared. Often, there is more overlap than expected, with disagreement not about what matters but about priority or how to express the value. This can shift the conversation from "You don't care about what I care about" to "We care about some of the same things, but we're prioritizing them differently."

Example values list (derived from Schwartz's framework and adapted for household contexts):
- Security/Safety
- Community/Connection
- Fairness/Equity
- Freedom/Autonomy
- Growth/Learning
- Creativity/Self-expression
- Health/Wellbeing
- Stability/Tradition
- Contribution/Service
- Honesty/Integrity

When both parties identify "Community/Connection" and "Fairness" as top values, the conversation can shift: "You both care about community and fairness. How do we make decisions that honor both of those?"

## Section 4: Restorative Circles — After a Harm Event

### The Distinction Between Conflict and Harm

A conflict is a dispute between parties with competing interests or perspectives. A harm event is a situation where one party's action has damaged another person, their wellbeing, or the household community. These require different processes.

The restorative circle — rooted in Anishinaabek and other indigenous circle traditions that are culturally proximate to the Zone 5 Midwest — is the appropriate process after a harm event. Its goal is not to determine who was "right" but to:
- Acknowledge what happened
- Name the impact on those harmed
- Create accountability for specific repair
- Restore the household's functioning relationships

Reed College's restorative harm process and the Center for Justice Innovation's RJ circle framework are the primary structural models. [12][13]

### Step-by-Step Facilitation Guide

**Preparation phase (before the circle)**:
- Separate meetings with the person who caused harm: do they acknowledge it? Are they willing to hear the impact? What repair are they able to offer?
- Separate meetings with those harmed: what do they need to be heard? What would repair look like? Are they ready to be in the same space?
- Do not convene the circle if either party is not ready. Premature circles can deepen harm.

**Opening the circle**:
- Talking piece (any object the facilitator designates): only the holder speaks
- Introductions: each person states their name and their relationship to the situation
- Intention-setting: the facilitator names the purpose — "We are here to hear what happened, understand its impact, and find a path to repair — not to assign blame or punishment"

**The story**:
- The person who caused harm speaks first: what happened from their perspective (in NVC terms: observation, not judgment)
- Those harmed speak: what happened from their perspective
- The facilitator reflects and checks: "Is there anything important that hasn't been said yet?"

**The impact**:
- Each person present (including household members not directly involved who were affected) has the opportunity to speak to how the event affected them
- The facilitator holds space for emotional expression without allowing it to become attack
- The person who caused harm listens; the talking piece ensures they do not interrupt

**The accountability and repair plan**:
- The facilitator invites the person who caused harm to acknowledge what they understand about the impact
- The facilitator invites a proposal for repair: "What would you be willing to do to address the harm?"
- The circle negotiates the repair plan: specific, time-bounded, observable actions
- Both the harmed party and the person causing harm must find the plan acceptable

**The closing**:
- Brief affirmation from each person: one word or one sentence about what they are taking away
- The talking piece completes its circuit
- The facilitator documents the repair plan in writing and gives copies to all parties

### When Restorative Circles Fail

Restorative circles are not appropriate in every situation:
- When the harm-causer does not acknowledge the harm and has no apparent capacity or willingness to do so
- When the harm pattern is ongoing (the circle cannot address a pattern in progress — safety must be established first)
- When the harm rises to the level of abuse (see Section 5 — escalation thresholds)

**Source anchors**: Reed College Harm Process [12]; Center for Justice Innovation RJ Circles [13]; PMC 8751228 [14]; Sage Fulham et al. Meta-Analysis 2025 [2]; Wiley RJ Programs 2024 [15]; MOST Policy Initiative [16].

## Section 5: Escalation Thresholds — When Facilitation Is Not the Right Tool

### The Mediator's Exit Decision

The most important skill in conflict facilitation is knowing when to stop. Applying facilitation where it is not appropriate creates genuine harm: it normalizes dangerous patterns, creates false equivalence between harm-causers and those harmed, delays the safety interventions that are actually needed, and can increase risk for the person being harmed. The facilitator must be trained to recognize when mediation stops being helpful and starts being harmful, and to exit cleanly and completely.

**Stop facilitation immediately when**:

1. **Physical safety threat (imminent danger)**: A household member poses an immediate or credible threat of physical harm — explicit threats ("I'll hurt you"), weapon possession in the context of conflict, recent physical violence, or escalating physical contact. This is a security matter, not a facilitation matter. The Security Coordinator (as established in the Household Coordination guide) manages this. The facilitator's role is to step out of the mediation frame entirely and name: "This is no longer a mediation situation. I'm stopping this process. We need to activate our household safety protocol." The facilitator does not mediate safety concerns.

2. **Coercive control patterns (abuse, not conflict)**: A pattern of behavior (not a single incident) that includes:
   - Intimidation (making the person afraid through threats, aggressive displays, weapons, abuse of animals/property)
   - Isolation (preventing contact with family, friends, or support; controlling communication)
   - Economic control (controlling money, preventing work, requiring permission for purchases)
   - Control of movement or space (tracking location, preventing leaving the house, restricting physical space)
   - Threats as leverage in disputes (threatening to leave, take children, report to authorities, harm self if demands aren't met)
   - Decision dominance (the controlling person makes all major decisions; the other person's opinions are ignored or overridden)

Mediation of coercive control patterns is contraindicated — it creates a false equivalence between the person controlling and the person being controlled, which can increase harm. The research on intimate partner violence and mediation is clear: the presence of coercive control predicts post-separation escalation of violence and fear far better than the presence of physical violence alone. [24] Mediation in the presence of coercive control has been shown to increase harm risk. [25] The correct process is household assembly convened on a membership and safety question, with the person being harmed having safety and support in place before any assembly process begins.

3. **The irresolvable values conflict**: When the values difference between household members is fundamental and appears truly irresolvable through dialogue. The honest statement from the facilitator: "I've tried to help us find common ground here, and I'm noticing that your values on this issue seem fundamentally different and we're not finding a resolution path. I don't think I can make this workable through a facilitated conversation. We may need to make a structural decision about household composition or a values-level decision from the full household assembly rather than trying to mediate this particular dispute. Let me talk with the household coordinator about whether we need to convene the full group."

4. **Membership threshold reached**: When behavior has reached the threshold for membership termination review (documented behavioral cause, evidence pattern, escalation), facilitation ends and governance begins. The facilitator does not mediate membership decisions — they may facilitate the assembly conversation about membership if requested, but the decision to proceed with membership review is not a mediation matter.

5. **Repeated pattern violation after mediation**: When the same conflict has been mediated, an agreement was reached, and the same behavior recurs in the same form. This indicates either that the agreement was not genuine, or that a structural issue (not a relationship issue) is creating the conflict. Either way, mediation is not the appropriate tool. The facilitator's response: "We've been here before. Mediation isn't solving this because the problem is deeper than we can address through facilitation."

### Distinguishing Conflict from Abuse — Critical Recognition Framework

The most difficult judgment call in household facilitation. The key distinguishing factors provide a framework for recognition:

| Characteristic | Conflict (mediate) | Coercive Control / Abuse (escalate to security/assembly) |
|---|---|---|
| **Pattern** | Episodic, usually context-triggered (conflicts about specific events, resources, decisions) | Ongoing, pervasive, not situation-specific (the controlling behavior is the baseline, not a response to specific events) |
| **Power dynamics** | Both parties have leverage; both can walk away; negotiation is possible | Systematic power asymmetry; one person controls key resources (money, transportation, communication) or the other person's access to them; the controlled person cannot walk away without severe consequence |
| **Response to limits** | Both parties may push back on boundaries, but when a boundary is set, they eventually accept it (though they may not like it) | The controlling party rejects any limits as attack; their response to being told "no" escalates the control (more surveillance, more isolation, more threats) |
| **Harm direction** | Mutual frustration; both parties are frustrated and both feel their concerns are not being heard | Primarily in one direction; one person is afraid, isolated, or controlled; the other person is not experiencing those effects |
| **Fear response** | Both parties may feel frustrated, but neither is afraid of the other or afraid to speak up | One party shows signs of fear: hesitates before speaking, looks to the other person for permission, is overly apologetic, minimizes their own needs, or becomes very quiet when the other person enters the room |
| **Accountability pattern** | When harm happens, both parties can eventually acknowledge their impact and work on change; mistakes are addressed through discussion | When harm happens, the controlling party denies it happened, blames the other person ("you made me do this"), or justifies it ("you needed to be taught a lesson"); there is no genuine acknowledgment or willingness to change |

**Critical warning signs specific to household settings**:
- The person expresses that they are afraid to disagree with the other person or to say no to requests
- The person's access to resources, communication, or people is restricted ("I can't leave the property without permission," "I have to ask before spending money," "I'm not allowed to call my family")
- The person's opinions are consistently overridden or mocked in public settings (meetings, group work) and they appear to have learned not to voice them
- The person's responsibilities are used as leverage ("If you don't do this my way, I'll tell the household you can't handle your job")
- The other person tracks the person's location, time, or activities beyond what is necessary for household coordination
- The other person uses threats about the person's safety, children, animals, or status in the household as a way to control behavior

**When in doubt about whether this is conflict or abuse, default to safety.** The cost of misidentifying abuse as conflict and attempting to mediate it is much higher than the cost of declining to mediate a situation that might have been resolvable. The person being harmed is safer if the facilitator errs on the side of caution.

## Section 6: Building the Household's Mediation Capacity

### Who Needs What Training

**Minimum viable mediation capacity**: Two household members with basic training in conflict recognition, NVC, and the household's four-step conflict protocol. This is achievable with 8–16 hours of training time spread over a few months (CNVC online workshops, most free or under $100). These two serve as a backup pair if one is unavailable or is personally involved in a conflict.

**Recommended mediation capacity**: One designated Conflict Resolution Facilitator (CRF) with comprehensive training in:
- NVC fundamentals (CNVC workshop or equivalent; 8–16 hours)
- Restorative circle facilitation (RSI training, local community mediation center, or online training; 20–40 hours)
- The four-category conflict typology from Section 1 (can be taught by the household once one person has learned it)
- The escalation thresholds from Section 5 — specifically, how to recognize coercive control and when to stop mediating (this is the single most important training priority; a household with a trained CRF but inadequate training on abuse recognition is at risk)
- Active listening and de-escalation techniques
- The household's specific governance structure and conflict protocol

**Note on the CRF role**: The Conflict Resolution Facilitator should not be a primary governance authority figure (the household's primary coordinator, decision-maker, or resource allocator, for example). Mediation requires trust and perceived neutrality that is compromised if the mediator is also the person making binding decisions. The person in this role must be respected and trusted by the household but should not be the primary power center. Ideally, the CRF is someone whose personal strength is listening and relationship-building rather than task management or authority.

**Training timeline**: A household can have a minimally trained CRF in place within 2–3 months (self-study plus a weekend workshop). Full training (40–60 hours) takes 3–6 months with consistent engagement. The investment pays for itself immediately if it prevents even one conflict from escalating to membership termination or household dissolution.

### Training Pathways and Resource Quality

**Tier 1 — Free or very low-cost self-study**:
- CNVC website (cnvc.org): Free introduction to NVC, videos, and some exercises. Sufficient for basic understanding; not a substitute for facilitated training but a good starting point.
- Foundation for Intentional Community (ic.org): Free resources on conflict resolution, decision-making, and governance. Specifically written for household and community contexts.
- Restorative Justice 101 (restorativejustice101.com): Free online training modules on circle practices and restorative approaches.
- YouTube channels: CNVC maintains a channel with introductory material; search "restorative justice circles facilitation" for recorded training.

**Tier 2 — Structured online workshops (low cost, 8–16 hours)**:
- CNVC online workshops: Typically $50–200 for 2–4 hour introductions; occasionally offers sliding-scale or free sessions. Quality is high; instructors are certified NVC trainers.
- National Association for Community Mediation (nafcm.org): Maintains a directory of local mediation centers (search by state/county). Most offer 20–40 hour volunteer mediator training free or at minimal cost ($25–100). These are structured, vetted, and taught by trained mediators. This is the single best resource for community mediation training at scale.
- Restorative Justice Training Institute (RJTI): Offers online courses and certification. Less expensive than in-person; can be accessed anywhere.

**Tier 3 — Intensive in-person training (higher cost, higher depth, 40–80 hours)**:
- Local community mediation centers: Many offer advanced mediator training or specialist certification. Quality is very high; training is by working mediators with real case experience.
- CNVC certified trainers: In-person intensive workshops, typically 3–5 days, $300–800 depending on location and sliding scale. Highest depth of NVC training.
- Restorative Justice certification programs: Some local organizations offer 4–8 week programs leading to certification. Cost varies; often $500–2000.

### Practice Before Crisis — The Simulation Approach

The conflict resolution literature consistently identifies a critical failure mode: households that try to learn their conflict protocols during an actual conflict, when stress is high and cognitive capacity is reduced. The solution is structured practice on low-stakes situations, treating them as learning experiences:

**Monthly conflict resolution practice drills**:
- **Week 1**: Run a brief NVC exercise on a minor household preference (work schedule preference, space organization, meal timing) to keep people familiar with the language. 15 minutes, no stakes.
- **Week 2**: Practice the pre-meeting format with a volunteer pair who are not in actual conflict. One person simulates being upset about something minor; the mediator practices the pre-meeting questions. 30 minutes total, learning-focused.
- **Week 3**: Practice a restorative circle for a very low-stakes situation (a minor borrowed-and-not-returned item, a tool left outside that got wet, a small breach of a household agreement). 45 minutes, full circle format. The goal is learning the format before it is needed for a high-stakes situation.
- **Week 4**: Debrief: the CRF reviews what worked and what was difficult. Other household members reflect on what they learned.

This monthly practice keeps the skills fresh, makes people familiar with the process, and creates a culture in which conflict resolution practice is normal. When an actual conflict emerges, people already know what to expect and the process is less threatening.

### The Conflict Resolution Facilitator's Standing Protocol

What the CRF does proactively, not just reactively when conflicts emerge:

**Monthly** (1–2 hours):
- Brief individual check-ins with household domain coordinators (Medical Coordinator, Resource Coordinator, Security Coordinator, Work Coordinator): "How are things going in your domain? Any interpersonal tensions building? Any resource disputes or concerns I should know about?" The goal is early warning — to catch tensions before they escalate to formal conflict.
- These check-ins are confidential but not secret; the CRF may mention patterns ("I'm hearing from a couple of people that there's tension about work allocation") without naming sources.

**Quarterly** (2–3 hours):
- Review of the household conflict log (maintained in the household information binder, digital or paper): documented conflicts, how they were resolved, time taken, satisfaction level from both parties.
- Pattern analysis: Are the same people involved in recurring conflicts? Is the same resource type (food, space, labor) generating disputes repeatedly? Is there a pattern of escalation (conflicts starting small and growing)? Is mediation solving the conflicts or just postponing them?
- If patterns are found, discuss with the household coordinator whether structural changes are needed (protocol revision, role change, governance change).

**Bi-annually** (1 hour):
- Review with the household coordinator: How is the conflict resolution system working? Are there situations where mediation is being used inappropriately? Are there conflicts that need to escalate to the household assembly for governance decision rather than mediation?

**Annually** (2 hours):
- Full household meeting: Review the four-step conflict protocol with the entire household to ensure everyone knows the steps and the CRF's role. Discuss what people see working and what feels difficult. Assess whether new people (who have joined since the last review) need to be trained on the process. Celebrate resolution successes.

**Standing documentation**:
- The household maintains a simple conflict log (date, parties, type of conflict, how resolved, time taken, outcome satisfaction). This serves dual purposes: it provides the pattern data for quarterly review, and it documents that the household has a working conflict resolution system (which is important for household cohesion and also for any legal situation that might emerge).
