## A Tier 2 Facilitation Skills Deep Dive

## The Most Important Finding

The Phase 3 security document establishes that internal conflict — not external threat — is the dominant security burden in any sustained disruption beyond two weeks. The Household Coordination guide makes the same finding at household scale. This document exists because a governance structure that says "step 4 is assembly review" is not the same as having a facilitator who knows what to do in the room. The gap between documented process and actual practice is where household cohesion fails.

The most important insight from the conflict resolution literature for household-scale application: **the type of conflict determines the correct process**. Applying relationship mediation to a resource allocation dispute wastes time and generates false intimacy that breaks down at the next allocation dispute. Applying policy review to a relationship conflict suppresses it rather than resolving it. Values conflicts require structured dialogue, not mediation. Structural conflicts require governance change, not facilitation. Before any intervention begins, the facilitator's first task is to correctly identify what kind of conflict they are dealing with.

The second finding: a household with a trained lay mediator is a more secure household than one with better locks and more weapons. Conflict resolution capacity is security infrastructure.

Evidence note: NVC's 2024 PMC scoping review (PMC 10916228) confirms effectiveness in interpersonal conflict reduction, primarily in healthcare settings. [1] Restorative justice has a 2025 meta-analysis (Sage, Fulham et al.) confirming moderate effect on harm reduction in community settings. [2] The intersection of NVC + restorative circles in homestead community contexts remains under-studied; this document's claims are practitioner consensus with the strongest available evidence anchors stated accurately.

## Section 1: Conflict Typology — Not All Conflicts Are the Same

### The Classification Framework

The most common facilitation error is applying the wrong process to the wrong type of conflict. This section establishes a four-category working typology for household-scale conflicts. Before any intervention, the facilitator asks: "What kind of conflict is this?"

**Type 1 — Resource Conflicts**

Disputes about allocation of scarce resources: food ration quantities, labor assignment, physical space, equipment use time. These are the most common conflict type in early disruption, when scarcity first becomes palpable.

*Distinguishing feature*: The core dispute is about fairness of allocation or clarity of rules, not about the relationship between the parties.

*Correct process*: Domain coordinator review with clear documented criteria. If the household has a written allocation protocol (and it should — see Household Coordination guide, Section 3), the coordinator applies it. If no protocol exists: this conflict reveals a governance gap that must be closed, not a relationship that must be healed.

*Common error*: Sending a resource dispute to mediation. This produces an outcome that satisfies neither party (the mediator creates a compromise rather than applying a standard) and signals to the household that rules are negotiable under pressure.

**Type 2 — Relationship Conflicts**

Disputes arising from interpersonal history, communication patterns, unmet relational needs, or perceived disrespect. "You never listen to me in meetings." "She treats me like a subordinate." "He always takes credit for work I did."

*Distinguishing feature*: The emotional charge is disproportionate to the stated cause. The dispute recurs. Both parties have strong feelings about what the other person *meant*, not just what they did.

*Correct process*: Facilitated conversation with emotional space. NVC framework (Section 2). The goal is not agreement on who was right but mutual understanding and a specific behavioral agreement going forward.

*Common error*: Applying policy review to a relationship conflict ("the rule says X, so you have to do X"). This suppresses the emotional content and creates a compliance response rather than a relational repair. The conflict resurfaces within weeks.

**Type 3 — Values Conflicts**

Disputes about what the household should prioritize when fundamental values are in tension: security versus openness to new members; conservation versus physical comfort; individual autonomy versus collective decision-making; risk tolerance.

*Distinguishing feature*: Neither party is objectively wrong within their own framework. The dispute has a philosophical dimension. It recurs regardless of policy adjustments.

*Correct process*: Structured dialogue to identify shared values and to find policy that expresses both frameworks. Not mediation, which implies a middle point. Not policy review, which can only apply values that already exist in written rules. The facilitator's role is to map the value systems onto concrete situations and find decisions that satisfy the most important concerns on both sides.

*Common error*: Attempting to resolve a values conflict by declaring one value superior to the other. This is governance, not facilitation. If the household assembly has not made a values-level decision, a mediator cannot make it for them. The correct outcome may be: "We need to convene the full household assembly to make a values decision. That is outside the scope of what I can facilitate."

**Type 4 — Structural Conflicts**

Disputes that keep recurring because the household's governance structure creates them: a role assignment that systematically disadvantages one person, a decision process that consistently silences certain demographic groups, a resource allocation rule with predictably inequitable outcomes.

*Distinguishing feature*: Multiple parties affected, not just two. The pattern predates the current dispute. Changing the relationship between the current disputants does not change the outcome for the next persons in the same structural position.

*Correct process*: Structural change, not facilitation. The facilitator's job in a structural conflict is to name it accurately — "this is a structural issue that will keep producing the same conflict until the structure changes" — and refer it to the household assembly as a governance matter.

*Common error*: Repeatedly mediating the same conflict between different people because no one names the structural cause.

**Source anchors**: Fisher and Ury, "Getting to Yes" [3]; Racial Equity Tools Conflict Transformation [4]; Habitatista communal living conflict resolution [5]; Community Finders decision-making frameworks [6].

## Section 2: Nonviolent Communication as the Household Facilitation Language

### Why NVC as the Foundation

NVC is not the only conflict resolution framework available, and the full draft should acknowledge its limitations (healthcare-setting evidence base, cultural assumptions about emotional expression). But it is the most widely practiced, most accessible to lay practitioners, and most teachable in the time constraints of a household training context. Its four components are learnable in a weekend and applicable without credentials.

Marshall Rosenberg's four NVC components:
1. **Observation**: What happened, without evaluation or interpretation. "You were 20 minutes late to the water rotation this morning" is an observation. "You always blow off your responsibilities" is an evaluation.
2. **Feeling**: How the situation lands emotionally. "I felt anxious" not "I felt like you don't care" (which is a thought, not a feeling).
3. **Need**: The underlying need behind the feeling. "I need to be able to count on the water rotation being covered so I can plan my morning."
4. **Request**: A specific, actionable, time-bounded ask. "Would you be willing to let me know by 7 AM if you can't make your shift so I can find coverage?"

### Translating Common Household Complaints into NVC

This is the practical work that makes NVC usable. The full draft will provide a table of common household complaints translated into NVC requests:

| Common Complaint | NVC Translation |
|---|---|
| "You always take more than your share" | "When food isn't documented (observation), I feel anxious about adequacy (feeling). I need to be able to trust the allocation system (need). Would you be willing to record each allocation in the logbook? (request)" |
| "Nobody respects my decisions as Medical Coordinator" | "When my protocols aren't followed without consultation (observation), I feel undermined (feeling). I need the authority boundaries of my role to be clear (need). Can we discuss and document what decisions I can make independently? (request)" |
| "The work is not distributed fairly" | "When I track work hours and see consistent differences between households (observation), I feel resentment building (feeling). I need to believe the system is equitable (need). Would the group be willing to review the work assignment data together? (request)" |

### Empathic Listening vs. Agreement

The distinction that breaks down most often under stress: hearing someone's concern fully does not require agreeing with it. The mediator who paraphrases a grievance accurately — "So what I'm hearing is that you feel the allocation was unfair and you need to know the criteria were applied consistently — is that right?" — is not endorsing the grievance. This is the most underused de-escalation move in lay facilitation.

**Source anchors**: CNVC, "Nonviolent Communication: A Language of Life" [7]; PMC 10916228 [1]; Multiphysics Journal NVC 2024 [8]; PubMed 29056619 [9].

## Section 3: Facilitation Skills — The Mediator's Toolkit

### The Pre-Meeting

The single most important facilitation tool is the conversation that happens before the facilitated conversation. Running separate meetings with each party before bringing them together serves three functions:

1. **Safety**: The mediator learns about any safety concerns before creating a joint space
2. **Preparation**: Each party gets to express their full story without interruption; they arrive at the joint meeting having already been heard at least once
3. **Goal clarification**: The mediator learns what outcome would constitute resolution for each party — and identifies in advance where those goals are compatible and where they are not

The three questions to ask in the pre-meeting:
- "What outcome would feel like resolution to you?"
- "What do you need the other person to understand that you don't think they currently do?"
- "What are you willing to offer or change in this situation?"

### Active Listening Under Duress

The mediator's primary tool. Under stress, both parties are operating with reduced cognitive capacity — the prefrontal cortex (deliberate reasoning) is partially offline, the amygdala is activated. Reflective listening — accurately paraphrasing what was said with correct emotional temperature — is both a de-escalation technique (it signals: you are being heard) and a comprehension check (it allows the speaker to correct misunderstandings before they compound).

Critical distinction: reflect the emotional content accurately. Do not over-soothe ("I'm sure they didn't mean it that way") and do not minimize ("that doesn't sound so bad"). Mirror the temperature: "You sound really angry about this, and you're also scared about what it means for the household — is that right?"

### Impasse Tools

When conversation stalls, the facilitator has a toolkit:

**The hypothetical**: "If you could change one thing about this situation that would make it workable for you, what would it be?" This bypasses the stated positions and moves toward underlying interests.

**The cooling pause**: A 20-minute break. Not a multi-day recess (which allows factions to form and grievances to harden) but a short physiological reset. Specify: "We'll resume in 20 minutes at this table." The specificity prevents the break from becoming an abandonment.

**The reframe**: Identify the shared goal that both parties are arguing toward from different positions. "Both of you are saying you want the household to function well. Let's talk about what 'functioning well' looks like for each of you." This is not false agreement — it is an accurate observation that competing parties often share terminal goals while disagreeing about instrumental approaches.

**The values inventory**: In a values conflict that is masquerading as a resource or relationship conflict, a structured values inventory (each party lists their top-five values from a standardized list) and a comparison of the two lists often surfaces shared values that neither party realized they held. ## Section 4: Restorative Circles — After a Harm Event

### The Distinction Between Conflict and Harm

A conflict is a dispute between parties with competing interests or perspectives. A harm event is a situation where one party's action has damaged another person, their wellbeing, or the household community. These require different processes.

The restorative circle — rooted in Anishinaabek and other indigenous circle traditions that are culturally proximate to the Zone 5 Midwest — is the appropriate process after a harm event. Its goal is not to determine who was "right" but to:
- Acknowledge what happened
- Name the impact on those harmed
- Create accountability for specific repair
- Restore the household's functioning relationships

Reed College's restorative harm process and the Center for Justice Innovation's RJ circle framework are the primary structural models. [12][13]

### Step-by-Step Facilitation Guide

**Preparation phase (before the circle)**:
- Separate meetings with the person who caused harm: do they acknowledge it? Are they willing to hear the impact? What repair are they able to offer?
- Separate meetings with those harmed: what do they need to be heard? What would repair look like? Are they ready to be in the same space?
- Do not convene the circle if either party is not ready. Premature circles can deepen harm.

**Opening the circle**:
- Talking piece (any object the facilitator designates): only the holder speaks
- Introductions: each person states their name and their relationship to the situation
- Intention-setting: the facilitator names the purpose — "We are here to hear what happened, understand its impact, and find a path to repair — not to assign blame or punishment"

**The story**:
- The person who caused harm speaks first: what happened from their perspective (in NVC terms: observation, not judgment)
- Those harmed speak: what happened from their perspective
- The facilitator reflects and checks: "Is there anything important that hasn't been said yet?"

**The impact**:
- Each person present (including household members not directly involved who were affected) has the opportunity to speak to how the event affected them
- The facilitator holds space for emotional expression without allowing it to become attack
- The person who caused harm listens; the talking piece ensures they do not interrupt

**The accountability and repair plan**:
- The facilitator invites the person who caused harm to acknowledge what they understand about the impact
- The facilitator invites a proposal for repair: "What would you be willing to do to address the harm?"
- The circle negotiates the repair plan: specific, time-bounded, observable actions
- Both the harmed party and the person causing harm must find the plan acceptable

**The closing**:
- Brief affirmation from each person: one word or one sentence about what they are taking away
- The talking piece completes its circuit
- The facilitator documents the repair plan in writing and gives copies to all parties

### When Restorative Circles Fail

Restorative circles are not appropriate in every situation:
- When the harm-causer does not acknowledge the harm and has no apparent capacity or willingness to do so
- When the harm pattern is ongoing (the circle cannot address a pattern in progress — safety must be established first)
- When the harm rises to the level of abuse (see Section 5 — escalation thresholds)

**Source anchors**: Reed College Harm Process [12]; Center for Justice Innovation RJ Circles [13]; PMC 8751228 [14]; Sage Fulham et al. Meta-Analysis 2025 [2]; Wiley RJ Programs 2024 [15]; MOST Policy Initiative [16].

## Section 5: Escalation Thresholds — When Facilitation Is Not the Right Tool

### The Mediator's Exit Decision

The most important skill in conflict facilitation is knowing when to stop. Applying facilitation where it is not appropriate creates harm: it normalizes dangerous patterns, creates false equivalence between harm-causers and those harmed, and delays the structural response that is actually needed.

**Stop facilitation immediately when**:

1. **Physical safety threat**: A household member poses or poses a credible threat of physical harm. This is a security matter. The Security Coordinator (as established in the Household Coordination guide) manages this — the facilitator does not. The facilitator's role is to remove the facilitation frame and name: "This is no longer a mediation situation. I'm suspending this process."

2. **Coercive control patterns**: A pattern of behavior (not a single incident) that includes intimidation, isolation of another person, control of their resources or movements, or threats as leverage in disputes. Mediation of coercive control patterns is not appropriate — it creates a false equivalence between the person controlling and the person being controlled. Research on domestic violence confirms that mediation with an active abuser increases harm risk. [Note for full draft: source needed from domestic violence prevention literature on mediation and abuse.] The correct process is household assembly convened on a membership and safety question.

3. **The irresolvable values conflict**: When the values difference between household members is fundamental and irresolvable through dialogue. The honest statement from the facilitator: "I don't think I can make this workable through a facilitated conversation. We may need to make a structural decision about household composition rather than trying to mediate this particular dispute."

4. **Membership threshold**: When behavior has reached the threshold for membership termination review (as established in the Household Coordination guide: documented behavioral cause, two-thirds majority assembly decision), facilitation ends and governance begins. The facilitator does not mediate membership decisions — they facilitate governance conversations about membership if the assembly requests it.

### Distinguishing Conflict from Abuse

The most difficult judgment call in household facilitation. The key distinguishing factors:

| Characteristic | Conflict | Coercive Control / Abuse |
|---|---|---|
| Pattern | Episodic, usually context-triggered | Ongoing, pervasive, not situation-specific |
| Power | Both parties have some leverage | Systematic power asymmetry; one person controls resources/safety of other |
| Response | Parties seek resolution | Controlling party resists accountability; defines any limits as attack |
| Harm direction | Mutual frustration | Primarily in one direction |
| Fear | Both parties may feel frustrated | One party shows fear of the other |

When in doubt, default to safety. The cost of misidentifying abuse as conflict and mediating it is much higher than the cost of declining to mediate a conflict.

## Section 6: Building the Household's Mediation Capacity

### Who Needs What Training

**Minimum viable**: Two household members with NVC training and familiarity with the four-step conflict protocol from the Household Coordination guide. This is achievable with 8–16 hours of training time (CNVC online workshops, free or low-cost).

**Recommended**: One designated Conflict Resolution Facilitator with training in:
- NVC (CNVC online or in-person workshop)
- Restorative circle facilitation (RSI training, local community mediation center, or online from the National Association for Community Mediation)
- The four-category typology from Section 1
- The escalation thresholds from Section 5 (the most important single training priority)

**Note on the CRF role**: The Conflict Resolution Facilitator should not be a primary governance authority figure (the household's primary coordinator, for example). Mediation requires trust and perceived neutrality that is harder to maintain if the mediator is also the decision-maker. Where possible, this role should be held by someone who is respected and trusted but not in the primary authority position.

### Practice Before Crisis

The conflict resolution literature consistently identifies a critical failure mode: households that learn their conflict protocols during an actual conflict. The solution is structured practice:

- Run the four-step protocol on minor disagreements (work schedule preferences, space organization) before any significant dispute
- Practice the pre-meeting format with a volunteer "disputant"
- Practice a brief restorative circle for a low-stakes situation (a minor borrowed-and-not-returned item, a small breach of household agreement) to learn the format before it is needed for a high-stakes situation

### Training Resources

**NVC training**: CNVC.org offers workshops ranging from free introductory webinars to multi-day residential intensives. The free materials on the CNVC website provide sufficient grounding for household application.

**Community mediation training**: The National Association for Community Mediation (NAFCM) maintains a directory of local community mediation centers, most of which offer volunteer mediator training (20–40 hours) either free or low-cost.

**Restorative justice facilitation**: RSI (Resolution Services Institute) offers online and in-person training. The Center for Justice Innovation (innovatingjustice.org) provides free implementation guides.

**Sociocracy for governance conflicts**: Sociocracy For All (sociocracyforall.org) offers free learning materials for consent-based decision-making, which addresses the governance dimension of structural conflicts.

### The Conflict Resolution Facilitator's Standing Protocol

What the CRF does proactively (not reactively):

- Monthly: brief check-in with all household domain coordinators — any interpersonal tensions building? Any resource allocation disputes not yet escalated?
- Quarterly: review household conflict log (maintained in the household information binder) for patterns — are the same people involved in recurring conflicts? Is the same resource type generating disputes repeatedly?
- Annually: review the four-step conflict protocol with the full household to ensure everyone knows the steps and the CRF's role

**Source anchors**: CNVC [7]; RSI Community Mediation Basics [10]; Sociocracy For All [17]; Foundation for Intentional Community sociocracy [18].
