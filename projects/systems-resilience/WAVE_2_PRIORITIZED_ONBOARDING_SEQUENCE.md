# Wave 2 Prioritized Onboarding Sequence
## Tier-Based Launch Order and Contingency Reassignment Paths

---

## Purpose and Use

This document describes the onboarding launch sequence that the June 14-15 author matching session produces. The sequence is not a flat list — it has a deliberate ordering logic that ensures the highest-risk situations are addressed first, the highest-value authors are onboarded without unnecessary delay, and no domain falls through the gap if an author withdraws.

The actual sequence for the real Wave 2 cohort will be generated by `AUTHOR_MATCHING_AUTOMATION_SCRIPT.py` once intake responses arrive (June 8-12). This document explains the ordering logic, the contingency paths per domain, and how to use the output at the matching session.

---

## Ordering Logic

The onboarding sequence is not simply sorted by tier. Four factors combine to determine who gets addressed first at the June 14 matching session:

### Factor 1: Blocker resolution (highest priority)

Any author with a HOLD tier or an unresolved blocker flag is handled at the very start of the session. These authors cannot be onboarded until their blockers are cleared — but clearing the blockers may take days, so the earlier the project lead acts on them, the less they affect the sprint timeline.

Typical blocker situations and what must happen:
- D2 = 1 (no long-form writing): Author is placed on hold. Project lead contacts them immediately to determine if they can demonstrate writing competency by June 12. If not, the slot is released to the secondary candidate list.
- Hours below minimum: Direct conversation to confirm availability, or release the slot.
- D1 = 1 for their primary domain: Reassign to a different domain if they have a higher D1 elsewhere, or release the slot.

### Factor 2: Domain criticality (Domains 60 and 62 get earlier attention)

Domains 60 (International Coordination) and 62 (Infrastructure Interdependencies) have the lowest source readiness (55% and 60% respectively) and the strongest difficulty modifiers. Authors assigned to these domains need more lead time for source-finding and scope consultation — regardless of their tier. Within the same tier, authors assigned to Domains 60 or 62 are sequenced before authors assigned to Domains 63 or 64.

### Factor 3: Tier (Tier A before Tier B before Tier C)

Tier A authors can receive their full onboarding kit immediately — scope document plus annotated bibliography, no extra guidance needed. Getting these out quickly frees the project lead's attention for the more intensive Tier B and C work. Tier B authors need an introductory scope constraint note drafted specifically for them. Tier C authors need a 15-minute scope consultation before they receive any materials.

### Factor 4: Warning flags (address before sending kit)

Authors with warning flags (peer review window conflict, batch communication style, limited Zone 5 familiarity) need the project lead to address those explicitly in the onboarding communication. They are sequenced to give the project lead time to draft a tailored communication.

## Standard Onboarding Sequence Structure

The sequence produced by the script follows this structure:

```
TIER: HOLD — Blocker resolution
  [Author name] | Domain [X] | [Blocker description] | Action: contact by June 12

TIER: A — Immediate onboarding, critical domains first
  [Author name] | Domain [60 or 62] | Score [X]/25 | Action: send kit immediately
  [Author name] | Domain [63, 64, 65] | Score [X]/25 | Action: send kit immediately
  [Author name] | Split-domain [X + Y] | Score [X]/25 | Action: send dual-domain kit

TIER: B — Standard onboarding with scope notes
  [Author name] | Domain [X] | Warnings: [list] | Action: draft scope note, send, schedule T+3
  ...

TIER: C — Scope consultation first
  [Author name] | Domain [X] | Action: schedule June 14 scope consultation
  ...
```

## Domain-Level Contingency Reassignment Paths

For each of the six Phase 6 domains, the matching session must identify a primary author, a secondary candidate, and a fallback path. The script's domain assignment plan section provides the primary and secondary. The fallback paths below are standard for all domains.

### Domain 60 — International Coordination (Contingency Priority: High)

**Why high priority**: Lowest source readiness (55%). The most likely domain to require project lead source sprint support regardless of author tier.

**Primary author contingency**: If the primary author withdraws before June 20:
1. Check the ranked-eligible author list from the script output
2. If a Tier B author is eligible: assign with enhanced source sprint (project lead provides 20-25 vetted sources before author begins)
3. If only a Tier C author is eligible: project lead co-researches with author; scope reduced to 2,500 words; two project lead check-ins per week
4. If no eligible author in pool: self-execute fallback activated (project lead researchers Domain 60 directly in the production window)

**Timeline impact of vacancy**: Domain 60 has the longest expected research time per output word. A vacancy discovered after June 20 pushes the Domain 60 first draft from June 27 to July 10 minimum. Flag immediately if the primary author becomes unavailable.

### Domain 61 — Intergenerational Knowledge Transmission (Contingency Priority: Medium)

**Why medium priority**: Crisis-context literature is sparse but the general adult education literature is rich. A Tier B or C author with strong D2 (writing) can still produce a credible document with project lead source support.

**Primary author contingency**:
1. If vacancy before June 20: reassign from ranked-eligible list; add source sprint support
2. If vacancy after June 20: project lead evaluates whether a condensed scope (5,000 words, three core sections) is feasible with the remaining author time
3. If no eligible author: self-execute fallback; non-formal education and knowledge transmission literature is well-developed enough for project lead authorship

**Adjacent domain note**: Domain 61 and Domain 62 share literature on tacit knowledge in technical systems (Polanyi, communities of practice). If both domains have authors, consider a peer review exchange at T+7 — Domain 61's author reviewing Domain 62's outline for community-learning framing, and vice versa.

### Domain 62 — Infrastructure Interdependencies (Contingency Priority: High)

**Why high priority**: Requires engineering literacy that is the hardest to improvise. Community-scale cascade failure analysis is not well-documented; the author must synthesize from adjacent large-scale research. A Tier C author in Domain 62 is a significant risk to document quality.

**Primary author contingency**:
1. If vacancy before June 20: immediately scan for engineering or permaculture design background authors in the secondary candidate list
2. If no engineering-background author available: restructure Domain 62 scope toward community decision-making and prioritization (the sections requiring less technical infrastructure knowledge), with explicit scope restriction noted in the document header
3. If no eligible author at all: self-execute fallback with explicit engineering-review peer at publication stage

**Split-domain restriction**: Domain 62 has a -2 difficulty modifier. Do not assign any author to a split-domain pairing that includes Domain 62, regardless of Tier A status. Sparse sources plus technical complexity exceeds what any author can manage at split-domain commitment levels.

### Domain 63 — Ecosystem Restoration (Contingency Priority: Low)

**Why low priority**: Richest source base of the six domains (82% source readiness). The practitioner literature in soil ecology, agroecology, and permaculture is deep. Even a Tier B author with strong D5 (field experience) can produce a high-quality document.

**Primary author contingency**:
1. If vacancy before June 20: highest-confidence of all six domains to fill from secondary candidates; the practitioner community (permaculture designers, ecological restoration practitioners, agroecology researchers) has broad eligible author overlap
2. If vacancy after June 20: project lead can provide a source-rich brief that a Tier B author can execute in 25 days
3. If split-domain author withdraws: split the two domains (63 and 64) and assign separately to two available authors; both have strong source bases and can be executed independently

### Domain 64 — Community Economic Resilience (Contingency Priority: Low)

**Why low priority**: Strong source base (78% readiness). Cooperative economics, Ostrom commons theory, and mutual aid literature are well-documented. Zone 5 rural application is the primary gap, but this is addressable with a context briefing rather than deep source-finding.

**Primary author contingency**:
1. If vacancy before June 20: secondary candidates from cooperative movement and economics backgrounds are readily available
2. If vacancy after June 20: 20-day condensed scope (6,000 words, six core sections: local currency, mutual credit, cooperatives, commons governance, inter-community trade, crisis activation) is feasible for a Tier A or strong Tier B author

### Domain 65 — Institutional Learning and Governance Scaling (Contingency Priority: Medium)

**Why medium priority**: The governance failure documentation requirement is unusual. Most governance literature documents successes; this domain explicitly needs failure case analysis. Only an author with direct governance experience (not just academic knowledge) can write this credibly.

**Primary author contingency**:
1. If vacancy before June 20: look for cooperative board members, intentional community governance practitioners, watershed council coordinators in the secondary candidate list — not governance academics
2. If vacancy after June 20: reduce scope to governance scaling only (the institutional learning section is the hardest to write without failure documentation experience); 5,000-word target
3. If no eligible author: this domain can be deferred to Wave 3 if Wave 2 has no eligible author — governance scaling is a Phase 7 precondition but not required for publication of the other five domains

## Reassignment Decision Protocol at the Matching Session

The June 14-15 matching session should address reassignments in this order:

1. Review script output — accept or override primary recommendations
2. Resolve all HOLD-tier authors (15 minutes maximum per author; if unresolvable, release slot)
3. Confirm split-domain assignments — project lead verifies bandwidth directly with author before finalizing
4. Address all warning flags — draft tailored communication for each warning author
5. Confirm contingency authors for each domain — the session ends only when every domain has a named primary and a named secondary or a documented fallback path

If the session cannot produce a secondary author for a domain, document "No secondary available — self-execute fallback active for this domain" and ensure the project lead knows they hold the fallback responsibility.
