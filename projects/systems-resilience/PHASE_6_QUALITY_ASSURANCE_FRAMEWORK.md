# Phase 6 Democracy Tools — Quality Assurance Framework

**Lead finding**: Quality assurance for Domain G operates at four levels: zone gate criteria (minimum thresholds to close each research zone), weekly check-in structure (if two-person team), escalation procedures with pre-built decision trees (if a zone falls behind), and a final synthesis checklist (December 12–20) for cross-zone coherence and distribution readiness. Every gate is stated as a binary pass/fail against objective criteria — not a judgment call.

---

## Part 1: Zone Gate Criteria

A research zone is closed and ready for integration when it meets all criteria in its row. The researcher submits the zone draft and self-rates against these criteria. The coordinator spot-checks the three starred items.

### Zone Gate Scorecard

| Criterion | Zone 1 | Zone 2 | Zone 3 | Zone 4 |
|-----------|--------|--------|--------|--------|
| Minimum verified source count | 15 ★ | 10 ★ | 12 ★ | 10 ★ |
| Minimum citation count in zone draft | 20 | 15 | 18 | 15 |
| Primary sources included (legislative docs, court decisions, government reports) | 3+ | 2+ | 3+ (include 1 official government source per country) | 2+ |
| All research questions answered | 5 of 5 (Q1–Q5) | 3 of 3 (Q4–Q6) | 3 of 3 (Q7–Q9) | 3 of 3 (Q10–Q12) |
| Zone 5 subsection present | Yes (state VRA status) | Yes (Zone 5 platform access) | Yes (per model: transferability paragraph) | Yes (per-state ballot thresholds) |
| No open placeholder sections | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Minimum word count | 8,000w | 7,000w | 8,000w | 8,000w |

★ Coordinator spot-check items: pull 3 sources from the zone draft and verify the description in the draft matches the primary source content.

**To close a zone**: All 7 criteria must be met. If any criterion is not met, the zone is not closed — the researcher addresses the gap before Synthesis A begins. A zone that is 95% complete on content but missing the Zone 5 subsection is not closed.

### Full-Document Gate Criteria (Pre-Synthesis B Checkpoint, December 8)

Before Synthesis B begins (Week 6, December 9), the full document in rough integrated form must meet:

- Total word count: 30,000+ words across all four zones in integrated draft form
- Citation count: 45+ citations total (rough, will be audited to 60+ in synthesis)
- All four zone gates closed
- Synthesis A outline complete: 12 sections mapped, zone outputs placed, Zone 5 subsections in at least 8 of 12 sections

### Final Document Gate Criteria (December 20)

The document is distribution-ready when all criteria in the final gate are met:

**Source and citation criteria**:
- 40+ sources across all four zones (verified, not counted-from-references)
- 60+ citations total in the document body (not just references list — citations doing argumentative work)
- 3+ primary sources per zone: legislative documents, court decisions, or official government publications
- Citation density: no major section (2,000+ words) with fewer than 8 citations

**Evidence synthesis criteria**:
- Every statistical claim cited from the originating organization's primary publication
- Every claim that a Zone 5 state does or does not have a specific law (AVR, SDR, state VRA) verified against a primary state source (Secretary of State website, Ballotpedia with date-of-access note, state legislature database)
- Counterargument addressed in Zones 2 and 3: the TurboVote attribution question (Zone 2) and the Estonia security critique (Zone 3) must be addressed in the text, not avoided
- Confidence levels stated explicitly where the evidence base is genuinely uncertain or contested

**Structural criteria**:
- All 12 sections present (no placeholder sections)
- Zone 5 subsections in every major section (Sections 2–11)
- Implementation templates (Section 12) complete: both voter registration drive template and ballot guidance session template
- No broken URLs (all links verified within 5 days of December 20 submission)

## Part 2: Weekly Check-In Structure (Two-Person Team)

If the two-person configuration is activated (per PHASE_3_RESEARCHER_PROFILE_AND_RECRUITMENT_PLAN.md Section 2, Option B criteria — activated if no solo researcher with firm commitment by October 1), the following weekly coordination structure applies.

### Monday Standup (Week Plan)

Every Monday, both researchers post a written week plan to the shared project folder by 10:00 AM Eastern. Format:

```
Week [N] plan — [Researcher Name] — [Date]

Zone / Section: [current work]
Goal by Friday: [specific output, word count]
Sources being used this week: [3–5 sources, to enable deconfliction]
Anything needed from Researcher B: [specific question or coordination need]
Coordinator question (if any): [one question — not for general discussion]
```

The coordinator reviews both week plans by noon Monday and responds to any coordination conflicts or coordinator questions by 3:00 PM Monday. No call required; this is an async written exchange.

**Why Monday matters**: The Zone 2 and Zone 3 research windows overlap in Week 3 (Researcher A completing Zone 2; Researcher B beginning Zone 3). The Monday plan in Week 3 is the primary deconfliction checkpoint — both researchers name the specific sources they are using that week. If both researchers are planning to use the same Brennan Center analysis or the same vTaiwan Participedia entry, they coordinate the citation approach before writing to avoid inconsistent characterizations of the same source.

### Wednesday Delta Check (Obstacles)

Every Wednesday, the researcher who is further behind their week-plan target sends a brief written status note: what is blocked, what is slower than expected, what decision is needed. This is not a report — it is a flag. Format:

```
Wednesday delta — [Researcher Name] — [Date]

Slower than planned: [one sentence]
Obstacle: [one sentence — what specifically is the block]
Request: [one of: "I'll handle it", "Coordinator input needed", "Researcher B coordination needed"]
```

If no obstacles: no message needed. Silence = on track.

The coordinator responds to delta notes within 4 hours on Wednesdays. If the response requires a call, the call happens same-day or Thursday — not Friday, because Friday is the review day.

### Friday Review (Sources Added, Zones on Track)

Every Friday, both researchers post a written Friday review by 3:00 PM Eastern. Format:

```
Friday review — [Researcher Name] — [Date]

Output this week: [word count produced, zone/section]
Sources added this week: [count and types — e.g., "4 sources: 2 legal, 1 academic, 1 organization report"]
Citations in zone draft (running total): [count]
Zone gate status: [On track / Needs attention — be specific]
Next week's critical path item: [one sentence]
```

The coordinator reviews both Friday reviews by end of day Friday. Any zone that is "Needs attention" on Friday triggers a coordinator response with a specific plan by Saturday morning — not a general check-in, a specific plan with word count targets or scope adjustment for the following week.

**Two-person Synthesis A session (December 6–8)**: In the Synthesis A phase, both researchers conduct one joint session (2–3 hours, call or shared document synchronous editing) to review each other's zone outputs and flag: (a) terminology inconsistencies (same concept named differently in Zone 1 vs. Zone 4); (b) contradictions (Zone 1 says X about SAVE Act; Zone 4 names the same organizations but in different order with different capacity descriptions); (c) cross-zone connection points (where Zone 2 technology tools directly address Zone 1 legal barriers — these must be made explicit in the synthesis). The output of this joint session is the Synthesis A coherence memo. The coordinator integrates both researchers' zone outputs into the final document structure using the coherence memo as a guide.

## Part 3: Escalation Procedures

### If a Zone Falls 1 Week Behind

**Definition**: A zone is 1 week behind if its gate deadline passes without all criteria met. Example: Zone 1 gate is November 10. If Zone 1 criteria are not met by November 10, the zone is 1 week behind.

**Immediate response** (same day the gate is missed, not end of week):

1. Coordinator and researcher identify which specific criteria are unmet. Run through the Zone Gate Scorecard: is it source count? Word count? Missing Zone 5 subsection? The response depends on which criterion is failing.
2. If the gap is word count only (research is complete but prose is not written): researcher commits to a specific catch-up schedule — 2 extra hours per day for 3 days. No scope change needed.
3. If the gap is source count or research question coverage: coordinator provides 5 targeted source suggestions for the specific unmet criterion. Researcher incorporates within 48 hours.
4. **Compress next zone's opening**: the standard overlap (Zone 2 opens on Day 10, overlapping with Zone 1 completion) is delayed by 4–5 days. Zone 2 does not open until Zone 1 is closed.
5. No scope is compressed yet at the 1-week-behind trigger. Scope compression is the 2-week-behind response.

**2-person team specific**: If Researcher A is 1 week behind on Zone 2 and Researcher B is on schedule in Zone 3, Researcher B does not wait. Researcher B continues Zone 3. The coordinator tracks whether the Zone 2 delay will create a synthesis integration problem — if Zone 2 closes more than 5 days after Zone 3 opens, the Synthesis A joint session schedule adjusts to accommodate.

### If a Zone Falls 2+ Weeks Behind

**Definition**: A zone is 2+ weeks behind if it is not closed within 1 week of its gate deadline and the specific gap (source count, word count, research coverage) has not been resolved.

**Activate 2-person sprint** (if solo researcher):

If the solo researcher is 2+ weeks behind on any zone, contact the coordinator immediately. The coordinator activates the 2-person fallback configuration for the remaining zones. Specifically: the project recruits a second researcher for the zones not yet started. For example: if a solo researcher closes Zone 1 on schedule but falls 2+ weeks behind on Zone 2, a second researcher is brought in to run Zone 3 in parallel while the primary researcher completes Zone 2.

This is not a failure scenario — it is a pre-planned contingency. The Phase 3 Researcher Profile and Recruitment Plan maintains a list of second-researcher candidates from the original recruitment pool. The second researcher can be onboarded within 5–7 days with this onboarding kit.

**Scope compression option** (if 2-person sprint is not viable or if timeline is too close to December 20):

Apply the scope compression options from PHASE_6_DEMOCRACY_TOOLS_RESEARCH_SCHEDULE.md in order:
- Option C-1: Reduce Section 9 (Canada) to 1,000 words; reduce Section 12 to one template. Saves approximately 8–10 hours.
- Option C-2: Option C-1 plus convert Section 11 Zone 5 toolkit to table format. Saves additional 6–8 hours.
- Option C-3 (last resort): Deliver Zones 1–3 complete by December 20; deliver Zone 4 by January 10.

## Part 4: Final Synthesis Checklist (December 12–20)

The final synthesis checklist runs in two passes: a cross-zone coherence review (December 12–15) and a distribution readiness review (December 16–20).

### Cross-Zone Coherence Review (December 12–15)

**Item 1 — Terminology consistency**: Do the four zones use the same terminology for the same concepts? Specifically: "same-day voter registration" versus "election day registration" (both terms are used in the literature; the document should pick one and use it consistently); "documentary proof of citizenship" versus "SAVE Act-style requirement" (use "documentary proof-of-citizenship requirement" as the primary term, with SAVE Act as the policy shorthand); "Zone 5" (always defined on first use in each section as "Iowa, Minnesota, Wisconsin, Illinois, and Missouri").

Check by searching the full document for the five most-used technical terms and confirming consistent usage throughout.

**Item 2 — Statistical consistency**: Are the core statistics cited consistently across sections? Specifically: the 19.1 million purge figure (EAC 2024 EAVS); the 21 million eligible voters lacking ready proof-of-citizenship documents; TurboVote's 79% vs. 64% turnout premium; the 26 states with SDR; the 24 states with AVR. Each of these statistics appears in multiple sections. All instances must cite the same source and use the same figure.

Check by searching for each key statistic and confirming all instances match.

**Item 3 — Do Zones 1–4 integrate?**: The document's core argument is that voter-centric tools (Zones 2, 3, 4) respond to specific legal barriers (Zone 1). This integration must be explicit — not implied, not assumed. Section 5 (Civic Technology) should reference at least two specific Zone 1 barriers that TurboVote or ballot guidance technology addresses. Section 7 (Estonia) should reference at least one Zone 1 constraint that makes i-Voting's specific requirements relevant or inapplicable to U.S. contexts. Section 11 (Zone 5 Civic Action Toolkit) should synthesize Zone 1 state law data, Zone 2 technology access, and Zone 4 organizational contacts for each Zone 5 state.

Check by reading the transition paragraph between Zone 1 sections and Zone 2 sections. If the transition does not make the legal-to-solution connection explicit, add it.

**Item 4 — Counterarguments addressed**: Three counterarguments must appear in the document and receive substantive responses, not dismissal:

(1) The TurboVote selection effects question (Zone 2, Q4): civic technology may be reaching already-engaged voters rather than expanding participation. The document must engage this — the response is that even if selection effects explain part of the premium, first-time voter data (86% vs. 57% nationally) is harder to explain by selection alone.

(2) The Estonia security critique (Zone 3, Q7): the FSFE critique and cybersecurity committee concerns are documented and must be represented accurately before the transferability assessment. The response is not "Estonia is secure"; it is "here are the specific conditions under which the system has operated for 20 years, and here is the honest assessment of which of those conditions are or are not present in U.S. contexts."

(3) The ballot initiative vulnerability argument (Zone 4, Q11): the BISC has documented 150+ anti-ballot-measure bills in 2025 — this means the Zone 4 pathway of ballot initiative infrastructure is itself under active attack. The document must acknowledge this tension: recommending ballot initiative use while documenting that the pathway is being systematically restricted.

**Item 5 — Narrative flow**: Read the full document's section leads in sequence (just the first paragraph of each section, not the full sections). Does the document move from crisis (Sections 1–4) to tools (Sections 5–6) to models (Sections 7–9) to action (Sections 10–12) in a way that would make sense to a reader who only has 30 minutes? The section leads must carry the argument even if the reader skips the full section text.

### Distribution Readiness Review (December 16–20)

**Landing page summary (500 words)**: Write after the coherence review is complete. The landing page summary appears at the top of the distribution email and on the publication platform. It must state: (1) what the legal environment is (2026 crisis snapshot in 3 sentences); (2) what the document covers (4 zones, 1 sentence each); (3) what a reader can do with the document (practitioner implication, 2 sentences); (4) who the document is for (civic organizations, community groups, individuals — not attorneys, not election administrators). Word limit: 500 words. No more.

**Email distribution template (one page)**: A complete email for distribution to civic organization contacts. Includes: subject line, 3-sentence document description, link to full document, 3-sentence Zone 5-specific note (why this matters for Iowa/Minnesota/Wisconsin/Illinois/Missouri specifically), call to action. Format: copy-paste ready; coordinator fills in the distribution link before sending.

**Social media posts (3 versions)**: One for each of three platforms — written assuming platform-standard character limits and norms. Each post must stand alone (a reader who only sees the post and clicks the link should understand what they are clicking on). Do not use the same text for all three; platform norms differ.

**Pitch document (1 page, PDF-ready)**: For Tier 1 distribution to Brennan Center, LWV, ACLU, NAACP LDF, Democracy Works — organizations that receive hundreds of research documents and make rapid decisions about whether to share internally. The pitch document is a 1-page framing: the specific finding from each zone that is most relevant to their organizational mandate; the Zone 5 angle; the January 3 Congress-seating context. One version for advocacy organizations (Brennan Center, ACLU, NAACP LDF); one version for civic technology organizations (Democracy Works); one version for civic engagement organizations (LWV).

## Part 5: Researcher Unavailability Protocol

If the primary researcher becomes unavailable (medical leave, family emergency, loss of project access, or other cause reducing availability to under 10 hours/week for 2+ consecutive weeks), the following sequence activates within 24 hours of confirmed unavailability.

### Immediate Response (Day 0)

1. Coordinator assesses the current zone completion status against the gate calendar. Answer three questions: (a) Which zone gate is next and when? (b) What percentage of that zone's content is complete? (c) What is the earliest a replacement researcher could be onboarded?

2. If the unavailable researcher can produce a zone handoff document (written summary of research progress, open questions, sources used, and sources planned), request it within 48 hours. This document becomes the backup researcher's briefing.

3. If a handoff document is not possible, the coordinator reconstructs zone status from: the shared source log (what sources were accessed), the Slack standup archive (what was reported done), and any draft files in the project folder.

### Backup Researcher Activation

Three backup researchers per zone are identified in advance from the original Phase 3 recruitment pool. Activation is in ranked order; contact each within 24 hours of confirmed unavailability. Target: backup researcher confirmed within 5 business days.

**Zone 1 backup researchers** (Constitutional Vulnerabilities, voting rights law):
- Backup Z1-A: Researcher with Brennan Center or voting rights litigation background from original recruitment pool; proficiency in Voting Rights Act case law and SAVE Act state legislation tracking. Activation lead time: 5–7 days.
- Backup Z1-B: Academic researcher with election law focus (law school or political science); sufficient from published bibliography to replicate Zone 1 source library. Activation lead time: 7–10 days.
- Backup Z1-C: If Zone 1 is less than 40% complete at unavailability: coordinator completes Zone 1 research using the DEMOCRACY_TOOLS_RESEARCH_OUTLINE.md and PHASE_3_DEMOCRACY_TOOLS_RESEARCH_READINESS_CHECKLIST.md as sole guides. This is the contingency of last resort and should not be assumed achievable under time pressure.

**Zone 2 backup researchers** (Technology Solutions, civic tech):
- Backup Z2-A: Researcher with Democracy Works / civic technology background from original recruitment pool. Onboarding: PHASE_6_RESEARCHER_ONBOARDING_KIT.md Section 2 Zone 2 questions (Q4–Q6) plus prior TurboVote and ElectionGuard source library. Activation lead time: 5–7 days.
- Backup Z2-B: Researcher with academic civic technology background. Activation lead time: 7–10 days.
- Backup Z2-C: Coordinator completes Zone 2 using published Democracy Works reports and existing Zone 2 source library (all sources identified in onboarding kit). Time estimate: 25–30 hours.

**Zone 3 backup researchers** (International Models, comparative democracy):
- Backup Z3-A: Researcher with comparative government or international election systems background. Zone 3 relies heavily on English-language published case studies (Participedia, Elections Canada, e-Estonia). No language specialization required beyond English. Activation lead time: 5–7 days.
- Backup Z3-B: Researcher with digital democracy or civic technology background who can assess the Estonian and Taiwanese models without comparative government specialization. Activation lead time: 7–10 days.
- Backup Z3-C: Scope reduction to two international models (Estonia + Taiwan; drop Canada to 1,000-word summary). This reduces Zone 3 research hours from 45h to 32h, enabling the coordinator to complete Zone 3 in a stretch scenario.

**Zone 4 backup researchers** (Movement Infrastructure, U.S. democracy organizing):
- Backup Z4-A: Researcher with democracy movement organizing background (Brennan Center, LWV, NAACP LDF, or state-level equivalent); Zone 4 source library is composed of U.S. organizational reports rather than academic literature. Activation lead time: 3–5 days (lighter onboarding requirement due to organizational familiarity).
- Backup Z4-B: Researcher with ballot initiative expertise (BISC contact, state legislature policy analyst, or advocacy organization program director with direct knowledge of Zone 5 ballot initiative thresholds). Activation lead time: 5–7 days.
- Backup Z4-C: Zone 4 scope reduction — drop Section 11 Zone 5 state-by-state toolkit to table format (saves 8h). Coordinator can complete Zone 4's ballot initiative research using state legislative databases and Ballotpedia in a stretch scenario.

### Backup Researcher Onboarding Timeline

A backup researcher starting cold on this project requires:
- Day 1: Read PHASE_6_RESEARCHER_ONBOARDING_KIT.md (2 hours). Review zone handoff document from previous researcher (1–2 hours).
- Day 2: Source library review — pull and skim the primary sources for their assigned zone (3–4 hours).
- Day 3: First research session with coordinator check-in at end of day (4–6 hours research + 30-min coordinator call).
- Day 4 onward: Normal research pace (5–6 hours/day) with daily Slack standup.

Full replacement researcher at normal productivity within 5–7 days of start date.

## Part 6: Source Access Blockage Protocol

If 3 or more paywalled or otherwise inaccessible sources are required and blocked simultaneously, the following pre-identified open-access alternatives activate. This protocol is triggered when the source access log shows 3+ sources marked "blocked, no alternative identified."

### Blocked Source Categories and Open-Access Fallbacks

**Academic journal articles (JSTOR, Wiley, Sage, Oxford Academic)**:
- Primary fallback: SSRN preprint server (ssrn.com) — most voting rights and election law academic papers have SSRN preprint versions.
- Secondary fallback: Author direct email (60–70% success rate, 3–5 day response). Template in onboarding kit Section 3.
- Tertiary fallback: Google Scholar "all versions" link often surfaces an institutional repository or author website PDF. Check before paying.
- JSTOR JPASS: $199/year annual plan, 24-hour activation. Purchase as project expense if 3+ JSTOR sources blocked simultaneously and SSRN/author email does not resolve within 48 hours.

**Government documents (state legislative databases, Secretary of State websites)**:
- Primary fallback: Ballotpedia — covers state voting laws, bill tracking, and ballot initiative qualification requirements for all Zone 5 states.
- Secondary fallback: National Conference of State Legislatures (NCSL) — legislative tracking and state voting law summaries (ncsl.org).
- Tertiary fallback: Brennan Center State Voting Laws Roundup — synthesizes state-by-state legal changes monthly.

**Court documents (federal district court, circuit court)**:
- Primary fallback: CourtListener (courtlistener.com) — free access to federal court opinions, filings, and dockets.
- Secondary fallback: Brennan Center litigation tracker, Campaign Legal Center, Democracy Docket — all publish primary documents for cases they are involved in.
- Tertiary fallback: PACER ($0.10/page, waived under $30/quarter for low-use accounts). Create account at pacer.uscourts.gov before November 4.

**International sources (Elections Canada, e-Estonia, Taiwan government documents)**:
- Primary fallback: Participedia.net — peer-reviewed case study database covering vTaiwan, Estonia i-Voting, Canada Inspire Democracy; continuously updated; no access barriers.
- Secondary fallback: e-estonia.com (no paywall, direct government source); Elections Canada elections.ca reports (free); g0v community documentation (GitHub, free).
- If a specific Elections Canada report is unavailable: email ElectionsCan-ElectionsCanada@elections.ca referencing the specific report title and requesting a PDF.

### Activation Threshold and Coordinator Notification

If 3+ sources in any single zone are simultaneously blocked and the fallback alternatives above have been checked and do not resolve the access issue within 48 hours: notify coordinator immediately (not at end of week). Coordinator response within 4 hours on weekdays.

Coordinator options: (a) provide institutional access credentials for the specific database; (b) confirm the scope reduction option (drop the affected sources and document the limitation explicitly in the research); (c) authorize the JSTOR JPASS purchase; (d) confirm a scope reduction that removes the research requirement for the blocked sources.

Do not delay zone research by more than 48 hours waiting for access resolution. If 48 hours pass without resolution, document the blockage and continue with available sources. Note the limitation in the zone draft explicitly: "The following sources were inaccessible during the research window; the analysis proceeds from available primary sources and notes where unpublished data would strengthen the claim."
