# Veterinary Care in Crisis Contexts
## A Tier 2 Household Guide for Zone 5 Midwest Homesteads

> **Region**: Midwest US (Zone 5) | **Scale**: Household (5–25 people) with livestock
> **Phase**: 5 — Gap Fill | **Tier**: 2 — Household Operational Domain
> **Prerequisite reading**: individual/06-agriculture.md (Zone 5 livestock basics), individual/05-healthcare.md (zoonotic response)

---

## The Most Important Finding

The rural veterinary shortage that motivates this document is not a future disruption scenario — it is the current baseline. As of 2026, the USDA has designated 245 veterinary shortage areas across 47 states, the highest ever recorded. [1] Food animal practice — the specialty relevant to livestock-keeping homesteads — represents only 3.4% of the U.S. veterinary workforce (3,424 of approximately 130,415 licensed veterinarians). [2] In Zone 5 Midwest rural counties, the distance to a large-animal veterinarian capable of emergency farm calls already exceeds 45–90 minutes for many homesteaders under normal conditions. Under extended infrastructure disruption, that gap becomes structurally uncrossable.

This document does not train the reader to be a veterinarian. It trains a Zone 5 household to: (a) prevent the most common livestock health crises through evidence-based biosecurity and vaccination protocols; (b) triage accurately when crisis occurs — distinguishing manageable-at-home emergencies from ones requiring immediate outside intervention; (c) perform specific first-response procedures that are learnable by lay practitioners and documented in publicly available extension resources; and (d) build a supply cache and knowledge system that extends the household's veterinary self-sufficiency window from hours to weeks.

**What this document does not cover**: basic husbandry (feeding, shelter, routine care) — see Seedwarden field manual. Companion animal care specific to dogs and cats — see appendix note. Herd-scale commercial operations — this document is scoped to homestead-scale (1–30 head, multiple species).

## Section 1: Zone 5 Livestock Profile and Disease Calendar

### Why Zone 5 Creates a Distinctive Disease Environment

Zone 5 Midwest homesteads face a disease environment shaped by three intersecting factors: extreme seasonal temperature variation (−20°F to 100°F across the year), high wildlife interface pressure (white-tailed deer, raccoon, waterfowl, wild turkey sharing territory with domestic livestock), and the density increases in winter housing that concentrate pathogens at exactly the moment when immune stress is highest.

The species profile typical of a Zone 5 homestead resilience operation:

**Laying and meat chickens**: Most common entry point. 6–50 birds, mixed breeds, managed for egg production and meat supplementation. Primary disease risks: respiratory illness (Mycoplasma gallisepticum, infectious bronchitis, Newcastle disease), Marek's disease in unvaccinated flocks, coccidiosis in chicks, Avian Influenza biosecurity threat during spring and fall migration windows (Zone 5 sits on the Mississippi Flyway, the highest-risk AI corridor in North America). [3]

**Meat and dairy goats**: High labor-to-yield ratio justifies keeping at homestead scale. 4–20 head. Primary risks: enterotoxemia (Clostridium perfringens — the leading cause of sudden death in goats), urinary calculi in wethers, respiratory illness in winter, Caseous Lymphadenitis (CL), Caprine Arthritis Encephalitis (CAE), and dystocia during kidding season (February–April in Zone 5). [4]

**Meat rabbits**: Lower infrastructure requirement; highest feed conversion. Primary risks: GI stasis, respiratory infections (Pasteurellosis), fly strike (cuterebrasis) in summer, and heat stress above 85°F — a Zone 5 summer management challenge. [5]

**Pigs**: Highest caloric output per acre but greatest biosecurity risk. H1N1-variant swine influenza circulates in Zone 5 feral pig populations; wild boar/feral pig contact through fence lines is a documented transmission route. Erysipelas (diamond skin disease) is endemic in Midwest soils. Pigs require the most rigorous biosecurity of any common homestead species. [6]

### Zone 5 Disease Calendar

The following summarizes peak disease risk periods for Zone 5 homestead species. Full species-specific calendars are available from CFSPH Iowa State.

## Section 2: Preventive Protocols — Vaccination and Biosecurity

### What Level of Vaccination Protection Is Achievable Without a Veterinarian?

Significant protection is achievable. The foundational truth: most catastrophic livestock disease events on Zone 5 homesteads are preventable through vaccination and biosecurity protocols that do not require a veterinarian to administer, only to design once (ideally in a pre-crisis veterinary relationship). The following schedules reflect USDA APHIS and CFSPH recommendations for small-scale producers. Vaccines represent the most cost-effective intervention available to homestead producers: a single CD&T dose for a goat costs approximately $3 and prevents enterotoxemia, which kills animals within hours and is universally fatal once clinical signs appear. No other intervention — no treatment regimen, no management change — can match this ratio of cost to prevented loss.

### Species-Specific Vaccination Schedules

**Goats (all types)**

*CD&T (Clostridium perfringens types C&D + Tetanus)* is the single most critical goat vaccine and the foundation of preventive practice. Enterotoxemia (caused by Clostridium perfringens types C and D) kills otherwise healthy animals within hours of clinical signs appearing. A goat found dead in the morning with no preceding illness is the classic presentation. CD&T is universally available OTC at farm supply stores (Tractor Supply, Rural King, agricultural co-ops). The protocol is straightforward: adult animals receive an annual vaccination; does receive a booster 4–6 weeks before kidding season to provide maternal antibody protection for newborn kids. Kids are vaccinated at 6–8 weeks of age, with a second dose 4 weeks later to ensure robust immunity before weaning (typically weeks 8–12). Annual revaccination for adults maintains immunity. The cost ($2.50–4.00 per dose) and ease of administration (subcutaneous injection) make this entirely within household capacity. [9]

*Caseous Lymphadenitis (CL) Vaccine (CASE-BAC)* is available OTC but its use depends on herd exposure risk. CL is a chronic bacterial infection (caused by *Corynebacterium pseudotuberculosis*) that causes abscessed lymph nodes, particularly in the head and neck region. Infected animals shed the bacteria and serve as vectors. The vaccine is warranted for homesteads that purchase animals from auctions or from herds with unknown disease status. For closed herds that do not purchase animals, or that purchase from verified CL-negative sources, vaccination may be unnecessary. The trade-off: vaccination occasionally causes localized swelling at the injection site in some animals; the protective benefit usually outweighs this minor risk for farms with exposure potential. [4]

*Caprine Arthritis Encephalitis (CAE)* has no vaccine available. Management is entirely preventive through testing and management: test adult animals annually; separate positive animals from negative ones; never feed colostrum from positive dams to kids (this is the primary transmission route). CAE is endemic in many commercial goat herds and creates chronic joint disease and encephalitis (neurological disease) in infected animals. Prevention through separation is more practical than attempting to eradicate it from an infected herd. [4]

*Note on the Veterinary Feed Directive (VFD, revised 2023)*: The VFD affects antibiotic use in feed and water but does not restrict vaccine access. All vaccines listed in this section remain OTC as of 2026 and are not affected by FDA Guidance for Industry No. 263 (the June 2023 guidance that moved 91 livestock antibiotics to prescription-only status). [10]

**Poultry (chickens)**

*Marek's Disease* is the only widely available poultry vaccine for backyard and small-flock producers, but its timing is critical: vaccination must occur within 24 hours of hatch. This means vaccination must happen at the hatchery before chicks are shipped. When purchasing chicks, verification of Marek's vaccination is essential — document the vaccination date and hatchery name in the household veterinary notebook. Marek's is a herpesvirus that causes paralysis, blindness, and tumors, and is endemic in most poultry environments. Vaccinated flocks experience dramatically lower disease incidence. Without vaccination, Marek's typically appears when chicks are 6–8 weeks old, producing rapidly progressive lameness and death in 50–80% of affected flocks. [11]

*Newcastle Disease and Infectious Bronchitis* vaccines are combination live vaccines available OTC, typically administered in drinking water or as eye drops. These are recommended for flocks of 25+ birds, or for any flock in the Mississippi Flyway (which includes all of Zone 5) during the peak avian influenza transmission windows (March–May and September–November). Zone 5 homesteads sit directly on the Mississippi Flyway, the highest-risk corridor for avian influenza transmission in North America. Newcastle and infectious bronchitis vaccination provides a baseline of respiratory protection. Administration is simple enough for household administration but requires attention to water quality (chlorinated water inactivates live vaccines, so vaccination protocols specify fresh water only during vaccination period) and timing (vaccines work best in unvaccinated or recently vaccinated flocks; effectiveness is lower in birds with pre-existing immunity). [3]

*Avian Influenza* has no commercially available vaccine for backyard flocks in the United States as of 2026. This is a regulatory constraint, not a technical one — vaccines exist but are not licensed for backyard use. Biosecurity is therefore the only tool available to homestead producers for avian influenza prevention. (See Zone 5-Specific Biosecurity Failure Modes below.) The threat level is real and has increased: highly pathogenic avian influenza (HPAI) is now considered endemic in wild bird populations across North America, with periodic spillover into domestic poultry. [3]

**Rabbits**

No USDA-approved commercial vaccines for rabbits are available as of 2026 in the United States. Rabbit Hemorrhagic Disease Virus Type 2 (RHDV2) has entered the U.S. and is established in multiple Midwest states as of 2025. This virus is universally fatal in rabbits (nearly 100% mortality), causes death within 1–3 days of clinical signs, and is transmitted through feces, urine, and saliva. Rabbits can shed virus without clinical signs (carrier animals). The absence of a vaccine makes biosecurity the sole preventive tool: quarantine of all new animals, protection from wild rabbits and rodents, and housing management that prevents fecal contamination of feed and water. Some private farms are investigating experimental vaccines, but nothing is available for household use. [5]

**Pigs**

*Erysipelas vaccine* is available OTC at farm supply stores and should be administered annually to all animals on homestead operations. Erysipelas (*Erysipelothrix rhusiopathiae*) is endemic in Zone 5 soils and causes acute septicemia with high mortality. The organism gains entry through skin abrasions, often from fighting or pen damage. Vaccination is the only practical prevention for outdoor-housed pigs. [6]

*Swine influenza (H3N2 and H1N1 variants)* vaccines exist but require a veterinary prescription for administration — they are not available OTC. The reason: swine influenza vaccines are considered medically important and fall under VFD restrictions. Establishing a veterinary relationship before any disruption allows the household to obtain this vaccine with a standing prescription. The alternative prevention is biosecurity: feral pig contact prevention through secure fencing, exclusion of wildlife from feed areas, and prevention of any contact between the homestead's pigs and feral populations. Zone 5 has documented feral pig populations in multiple states; the contact risk is real. [6]

### Quarantine Protocols

Any animal entering the homestead herd from outside — whether purchased, borrowed, returning from a fair or exhibition, or gifted — must be quarantined before integration into the existing herd. This is the single most important infection prevention practice. Quarantine failure is the leading cause of disease introduction in small farm settings, documented in both extension research and herd health studies across species. [7]

The minimum quarantine protocol:

- **Duration**: 30 days for small ruminants, poultry, and rabbits; 60 days for pigs (longer duration accounts for pigs' longer incubation periods for swine influenza and other respiratory diseases)
- **Housing**: Completely separate from the existing herd, with no possibility of direct contact. Ideally at least 50 feet from other animals; minimum physical separation is a separate shed or area where no shared air space exists
- **Resources**: Dedicated feeders, waterers, and tools that do not move between quarantine area and existing herd areas. If tools must be shared, they must be thoroughly disinfected (10% bleach solution is effective) between areas
- **Personnel**: Dedicated clothing and footwear for quarantine area work; change clothing before entering other animal areas. This prevents mechanical transmission of pathogens on clothing and hands
- **Observation and documentation**: Daily observation for the full 30–60 day period; document any signs of illness (lethargy, nasal discharge, coughing, diarrhea, loss of appetite, fever), appetite changes, or behavioral abnormalities. Photographs of any health concerns are valuable if veterinary consultation is later needed
- **Vaccination update**: Full vaccination update before integration into the main herd (CD&T for goats, any recommended vaccines for the species)
- **Testing** (when feasible): For goats, a test for CAE before integration is ideal if the animal came from an unknown source; testing costs approximately $8–15 per animal through a veterinary clinic. This is a worthwhile investment if the goat is expected to be a long-term herd member

Integration occurs only after the full quarantine period has elapsed with no health issues observed. If any illness develops during quarantine, the isolation period resets: the 30 or 60 days begins again from the end of the illness episode.

### Zone 5-Specific Biosecurity Failure Modes

**Wildlife interface**: Zone 5 rural homesteads are embedded in a landscape with high-density white-tailed deer, raccoon, waterfowl (ducks, geese, wild swans), wild turkeys, and foxes. This wildlife proximity creates specific pathogen transmission risks that require active mitigation.

Raccoons and waterfowl are the primary avian influenza vectors. Both species shed high concentrations of avian influenza virus in their feces. Raccoons can access poultry housing through small gaps; waterfowl droppings contaminate water sources and pastures where poultry forage. Mitigations: hardware cloth (¼-inch maximum gap) over all poultry housing vents, not standard chicken wire (which raccoons can tear); netting over outdoor poultry runs to exclude waterfowl; dedicated waterer systems for poultry that prevent waterfowl access (elevated troughs or closed systems, not open ponds).

White-tailed deer are the primary reservoir for Chronic Wasting Disease (CWD) and for tick-borne Lyme disease and other pathogens. For goat and sheep operations, the concern is indirect: tick exposure occurs from the same environment deer occupy. Fencing to exclude deer is expensive and challenging but is the most effective mitigation. Eight-foot fencing is standard; alternatives include electric fencing at 6,000+ volts, angled outrigger fencing, or combinations of height and angle.

Feral pig intrusion is a documented transmission route for swine influenza and other swine diseases. In Zone 5 states with established feral pig populations, secure perimeter fencing and regular fence-line inspection are essential for any swine operation. Feral pigs can weigh 200–400 pounds and will breach inadequate fencing.

**Winter housing density**: Zone 5 November–March housing creates the conditions for respiratory disease outbreaks. Temperature, humidity, ventilation, and stocking density interact multiplicatively: poor ventilation + high humidity + high stocking density + freezing temperatures = respiratory disease risk. The equation is density × humidity × respiratory pathogen load = outbreak risk.

Mitigation begins with ventilation without drafts. This is the hard engineering problem in Zone 5 winter housing. Natural ventilation (windows, vents) that provides air movement without creating cold drafts requires careful design. Ridge vents combined with soffit or low-side vents create upward air flow that removes moist air from animal breathing spaces without creating direct cold drafts on animals. Mechanical ventilation (fans) is effective if power is available. Without power, the standard practice is moveable houses that allow frequent relocation to clean ground, combined with deep bedding changes (removing wet bedding, adding fresh dry bedding 2–3 times weekly).

Stocking density directly affects disease risk. For chickens in winter housing, 2 square feet per bird is dangerously dense and creates both behavioral (pecking, feather loss) and health (respiratory disease, coccidiosis) problems. Four to five square feet per bird is the minimum safe density; six is comfortable. For goats, approximately 20–25 square feet per animal is standard; for pigs, 6–8 square feet per finishing pig. Winter housing that forces animals into tighter quarters than these minimums should be expanded or destocked.

Daily observation frequency should double in winter: animals should be observed at least twice daily (morning and evening) for signs of respiratory illness (nasal discharge, coughing, labored breathing), lethargy, or feed refusal. Early detection of illness allows early isolation and treatment, preventing spread to other animals.

**Pasture parasite management**: Barber Pole Worm (*Haemonchus contortus*) is the dominant internal parasite threat for goats and sheep in Zone 5. The adult worm lives in the abomasum (the true stomach), where it feeds on blood, causing severe anemia. Heavy infections can cause death within weeks. The worm produces eggs that are shed in feces, contaminating pastures. Larvae develop in pasture vegetation (particularly in moist areas) and are ingested by grazing animals, perpetuating the cycle.

Dewormer resistance is now widespread across Zone 5. Random deworming schedules — treating all animals on a fixed schedule (e.g., every 6 weeks) — are counterproductive because they create selective pressure for resistant worms. The evidence-based approach is FAMACHA scoring: evaluate the color of each animal's eyelid mucosa (conjunctiva) monthly; deworm only animals scoring 3–5 on the FAMACHA scale (increasingly pale to white, indicating anemia). Animals scoring 1–2 (normal color) are managed solely with pasture rotation. This approach reduces anthelmintic use by 60–75% while maintaining control. [12]

Pasture rotation is essential. Barber Pole Worm larvae die if pasture is not grazed for at least 6 weeks (longer in cool seasons). Never graze pasture below 4 inches; set a threshold at which animals are rotated to new pasture (6–8 inches is standard for goats). Short grass means closer contact with soil and larval contamination. Maintain a rest-grazing rotation: if you have three pastures, animals graze one for 2–3 weeks, move to the next, and do not return to the first for a minimum of 6 weeks.

FAMACHA training is available through extension (approximately 2–3 hours of training) and is valuable for any goat or sheep keeper. The scoring scale is learnable and reliable; with practice, assessment takes 30–60 seconds per animal.

## Section 3: First Response — Injury, Illness, and Obstetric Emergency

### The Triage Decision Framework

The single most important skill in livestock first response is the triage decision: is this condition manageable at home with household resources, or does it require outside veterinary assistance? This question must be answered quickly and accurately under stress, often with incomplete information. Delay in calling when outside help is needed costs lives. Premature panic and unnecessary evacuation for a manageable situation wastes scarce transportation resources and separates an animal from the home environment where it recovers best. The triage skill is learnable and improves with practice.

**Manage at home when**:
- The animal is physiologically stable: standing or lying calmly, alert, breathing without distress, body temperature normal or mildly elevated (up to 103°F in small ruminants)
- The condition is clearly identified and matches a documented, home-treatable scenario (bloat, minor wound, mild diarrhea)
- The household has the necessary supplies and someone who has trained on the specific protocol
- Treatment can begin within 1–4 hours and recovery is likely within 24–72 hours

**Call immediately or plan immediate transport when**:
- The animal is down and cannot rise, or rises with severe difficulty and immediately lies back down — indicates systemic illness, advanced metabolic derangement, or severe compromise
- Respiratory distress is severe: open-mouth breathing in a goat or pig, neck extension ("stargazing") in a chicken, or audible wheezing/stridor in any species
- Fever >104°F in small ruminants, or fever that does not respond to initial care measures (cold water, shade, fluids) — fever of this magnitude suggests systemic infection
- Obstetric emergency with active straining >30 minutes showing no progress toward delivery
- Suspected acute toxin exposure (sudden behavioral changes, seizures, uncoordinated gait)
- Obvious catastrophic injury (severe laceration with heavy bleeding, bone protrusion, evisceration)
- Signs of toxemia: sudden death in companion animals (indicating toxin or acute sepsis), or multiple animals affected simultaneously within hours
- Uterine prolapse (tissue protruding from the vulva after birth) — this is a true emergency with high mortality if not treated within hours

**Document and monitor closely (check at minimum every 6–8 hours) when**:
- Single animal, mild symptom, unclear diagnosis but no emergency warning signs
- Animal continues eating and drinking (a strongly positive prognostic sign)
- Condition is improving with initial response (more alert, better appetite, reduced fever)
- Mild diarrhea or nasal discharge without systemic illness signs
- Minor wound or lameness that is not progressive

The key distinction: if the animal is eating, alert, and not running fever, the condition is usually not immediately life-threatening and can be managed at home with observation. If the animal has stopped eating, is depressed, or has high fever, the risk of rapid deterioration is high and veterinary help should be obtained.

### The Three Most Acute Crises Per Species

**Goats: Bloat (Ruminal Tympany)**

*Recognition and types*: Bloat presents as either frothy bloat or free gas bloat, with very different management implications. The distended left flank (sometimes with clear outline of the rumen on the left side) is the visual sign. The animal stops eating, shows obvious abdominal discomfort (tail flicking, getting up and down repeatedly, grinding teeth), and may have labored breathing from the distended rumen pressing on the diaphragm.

Frothy bloat results from lush pasture (spring or fall fresh flush), legume-heavy grazing, or grain overload. Bubbles of gas are trapped in the rumen fluid and cannot be released by normal eructation. Free gas bloat results from slower causes (obstruction, inactivity) and gas accumulates but remains free rather than trapped in foam.

*Triage decision*: Frothy bloat is an emergency requiring intervention within 30–60 minutes. Animals with frothy bloat can die from respiratory failure if the distended rumen compresses the diaphragm completely. Free gas bloat, while uncomfortable, is often manageable with repositioning and is less immediately dangerous, though it can progress to frothy bloat if the cause is not addressed.

*First response*:
1. **Remove from pasture and reposition**: Move the animal to a quiet area. Walk the animal slowly; movement and position changes aid gas release. Some practitioners advocate tipping the animal's front end uphill (using a ramp or slope) to facilitate rumen contraction and gas release.

2. **Stomach tube assessment**: Insert a French nasogastric tube (14–16 Fr gauge, available OTC at farm supply stores for approximately $5–8) through the nostril, down the esophagus, and into the rumen. The tube should travel approximately 24–32 inches in an adult goat (measure on the animal beforehand and mark the tube with tape for reference). If gas releases immediately with a distinctive sound, this is free gas bloat — management is repositioning and time. If gas does not release despite tube placement and gentle pressure, this is frothy bloat requiring active treatment.

3. **Frothy bloat treatment**: Administer 60–90 ml of vegetable oil (mineral oil, olive oil, or commercial anti-bloat agents like GasX for livestock) through the tube to break down foam and allow gas to be eructated. After administering, leave the tube in place for 5–10 minutes to allow gas to release. Some oil aspiration is a risk if the tube is not placed correctly; if oil enters the trachea it causes aspiration pneumonia, which is fatal. Use deliberate technique and be certain the tube is in the rumen, not the trachea (you should be able to feel the tube pass into the esophagus and hear/feel the difference in resistance).

4. **No improvement in 15–20 minutes**: If gas does not improve after oil treatment and the animal is in severe distress (extremely labored breathing, panic, collapse risk), trocar insertion becomes a life-saving option. A trocar is a large-bore needle (typically 12–14 gauge) or specialized trocar device that is inserted through the left flank into the rumen to release gas. This is traditionally considered a veterinary procedure, but it is learnable by trained household members as a last-resort, life-saving intervention. The Pet Emergency Academy Livestock First Aid course includes trocar placement training. The risk of trocar insertion (peritonitis, rumen leakage) is lower than the risk of death from asphyxia if it is not done. If trocar insertion is performed, veterinary care is still necessary afterward.

5. **Post-bloat management**: Call a veterinarian regardless of outcome if possible. Even if the bloat is relieved, the underlying cause (overfeeding grain, access to lush legume pasture) must be identified and addressed to prevent recurrence. Secondary complications (rumen ulceration, peritonitis from trocar placement) may develop over 24–72 hours.

**Prevention**: Restrict access to fresh, lush pasture (particularly legumes like clover) during rapid growth periods. Introduce grazing on lush pasture gradually. Do not allow hungry animals to gorge on grain — spread feeding throughout the day or use a slow feeder. Provide long-stem hay before grazing or feeding grain.

**Goats: Dystocia (Difficult Birth)**

*Recognition*: Active labor without progress is the key sign. A doe in early labor may strain intermittently and get up and down. Active labor is continuous or near-continuous straining. Normal presentation: the doe will show continuous straining for 5–15 minutes and then deliver one kid; she may have 15–30 minute breaks between kids. Active straining for >30 minutes with no appearance of kid nose/feet indicates a problem.

Normal birth presentation: both front feet and the head visible first, with the nose resting on or between the front hooves (like a diving position). Any deviation is abnormal: breech (rear legs first or coming backward), head-back (only front legs visible, head bent backward), leg-back (one or both front legs folded back), or head-coming-wrong (head coming first without front legs).

*Safe intervention window*: 20–30 minutes of active straining without progress. After this point, the doe is fatiguing and the kid's exposure time (amniotic fluid reserves, placental oxygen exchange) is declining. Continued straining without progress accelerates both doe exhaustion and kid compromise. This is a finite window; decisions about intervention must happen during this period.

*First response*:
1. **Setup and preparation**: Wash hands and arms thoroughly to the elbow with soap and hot water. Trim fingernails short. Put on obstetric gloves (shoulder-length is standard, available OTC). Lubricate hands and forearms with obstetric lubricant (OB lube, available OTC; Lubri-Lube is a common brand). Have additional lubrication available; reapply frequently as the lubricant dries out during manipulation.

2. **Initial assessment**: Insert a lubricated hand into the vagina and assess what you feel. A normal presentation has two front hooves pointing downward and the hard forehead of the kid between them. Document what you feel: which parts are present, orientation, and whether the doe's cervix is fully dilated (it should be fully open and not restrictive to your hand).

3. **Correctable presentations**: The household can learn to correct some abnormal presentations:
   - **Head-back**: The kid's head is flexed backward instead of resting on the front legs. Gently push the kid's body forward while trying to extend the head forward and downward. This requires space in the uterus and is not always possible.
   - **Leg-back** (one or both front legs folded backward): Cupping your hand around the hoof, gently flex the leg to fold it under the body, then extend it forward into position. Move slowly; tearing the uterine wall is the primary complication of excessive force. If you cannot extend a leg after 2–3 gentle attempts, stop.
   - **Slight malposition**: If the kid is slightly anterior (coming forward) but not quite right, sometimes patient waiting and gentle traction during contractions allow the doe's own efforts to complete the correction.

4. **When to stop**: If you cannot correct the presentation in two gentle attempts (taking a total of perhaps 10–15 minutes of work), stop. Further manipulation is likely making the situation worse. Call for veterinary assistance. Transport is better than continued failed intervention — the doe still has better odds of a live kid and survival with veterinary help than with continued household manipulation.

5. **Gentle traction**: If presentation is correct, gentle downward and backward traction during a contraction (as the doe strains) can assist delivery. Traction should be slow and steady, not jerking. The kid's shoulders are the widest point and require the most space. Once the kid's thorax is visible, most kids deliver with the doe's own efforts.

6. **Dystocia complications**: Be aware of potential serious complications: uterine torsion (the uterus rotates on its ligaments, making impossible), uterine tear (any manipulation carries this risk), and ringwomb (the cervix fails to dilate fully, preventing delivery). These require veterinary intervention. If the doe seems to have a tightening resistance to your manipulation, or if you feel heat and fluid indicating possible rupture, stop immediately and seek help.

*Post-dystocia*: Monitor the doe for 24–72 hours for fever, foul-smelling discharge, lethargy, or poor appetite — signs of metritis (uterine infection). Retained fetal membranes (placentas not expelled within 2–4 hours after kidding) require veterinary attention; they lead to infection. If complications develop, veterinary care is necessary.

**Prevention**: Overfeeding grain in late pregnancy increases kid size. Limit grain to what is necessary for body condition. Genetics matter: breeds or lines with large-boned kids have higher dystocia rates. Feed good quality hay (not poor-quality hay alone) to maintain body condition.

**Chickens: Egg Binding (Dystocia)**

*Recognition*: A hen in the late stages of egg binding will show obvious distress. She strains repeatedly, tail pumping (the abdomen and tail pump with effort), stands hunched, and may be unable to stand normally. If you palpate the lower abdomen gently, you often feel a hard, oval object (the egg) in the peritoneal cavity.

*First response*: Egg binding is typically a problem in young pullets (first-time layers) or in animals raised in cool temperatures without supplemental light (which causes reduced shell quality and retention). Warm water immersion is the primary treatment: place the hen in warm (but not hot) water (approximately 100–102°F) for 30 minutes. The warmth relaxes the muscular contractions of the oviduct and the water soaking relieves pain and stress.

After the soak, dry the hen gently and place her in a warm, quiet hospital pen (a small box with soft bedding, in a warm location) with food and water. Lubricate the vent with a small amount of vegetable oil. Many hens will lay the egg within the next 12–24 hours once relaxed and without environmental stress.

If no egg is laid within 24–48 hours, the prognosis becomes poor. Egg binding that does not resolve leads to uterine prolapse (the reproductive tract prolapses through the vent), which is rapidly fatal without intervention and difficult to treat even with veterinary care. At 48 hours without egg laying, euthanasia is typically more humane than prolonged suffering.

Prevention: Keep pullets and hens at consistent, comfortable temperatures, provide supplemental light in winter to maintain 14+ hours of daylight, and ensure adequate calcium (layer feed contains adequate calcium; ensure it is the primary diet, not a supplement to scratch grains).

**Goats/Pigs: Emergency Slaughter Decision**

When an animal is suffering from an untreatable condition and cannot recover, euthanasia or emergency slaughter is the responsible decision. This decision is emotionally difficult but ethically required. The AVMA Guidelines for the Euthanasia of Animals (2020 edition) is the reference document and is available free online. [18]

*Acceptable methods for lay practitioners*:

- **Penetrating captive bolt**: A specialized tool (available online and at farm supply stores for $50–300) delivers a bolt that penetrates the skull and destroys the brain. Used correctly, it is rapid and causes immediate unconsciousness. Requires proper training on placement. Appropriate for goats, pigs, cattle. Not suitable for small animals like chickens.

- **Gunshot**: AVMA-acceptable for livestock when other methods are unavailable. A .22 rifle with proper placement destroys the brain and is rapid. Requires familiarity with firearms and proper safety protocols. UC Davis provides position diagrams showing the correct placement for different species.

- **Cervical dislocation**: Appropriate for poultry (chickens, turkeys, waterfowl) under 3 kg (approximately 6–7 lbs). Requires proper technique. The method involves holding the bird's body securely, grasping the head, and applying firm, rapid flexion of the neck to separate the cervical vertebrae and sever the spinal cord. This is rapid and causes immediate unconsciousness but requires practice or training before first use. Merck Veterinary Manual and the AVMA Guidelines describe correct technique.

*Meat safety after emergency slaughter*: The USDA FSIS "Home Slaughter and Processing of Livestock" guidance applies. General rule: meat from an animal with known systemic bacterial infection (septicemia, peritonitis from rupture) or unknown toxin exposure is not safe to consume. Meat from an animal with acute trauma (broken leg, wound), reproductive complication (dystocia, uterine rupture), or localized injury may be safe depending on the severity of the condition and the care with which the carcass is processed (removing contaminated areas). When in doubt, do not consume; compost, bury, or render the carcass. For a homestead in extended disruption, the loss of a carcass is less dangerous than foodborne illness affecting multiple household members.

## Section 4: Zoonotic Disease Recognition and Management

### The Household Overlap Between Animal and Human Health

The Zone 5 homestead household manages the same physical space as its livestock. Animals live in barns and shelters adjacent to or within sight of the living area. In extended disruption, this spatial overlap intensifies: reduced distance to water sources, increased observation frequency, and closer handling of animals during health crises. Zoonotic diseases — those transmissible from animals to humans — are a constant background risk under normal conditions and a significant health burden under disruption when hygiene infrastructure is reduced and sick animal contact increases.

This section covers the Zone 5-relevant zoonoses by species. For each: transmission route specific to homestead farming practice, early human symptom recognition, initial response, and the threshold for escalation to medical attention. All recommendations should be read alongside individual/05-healthcare.md, which provides the parallel guidance on human infection response and management within a household context.

### Species-Specific Zoonoses

**Chickens: Salmonella (Salmonellosis)**

*Transmission and risk factors*: Salmonella is shed in the feces of infected chickens — but critically, Salmonella is also shed by asymptomatic carrier birds. A chicken that appears perfectly healthy can be shedding Salmonella. Transmission occurs through direct contact with infected birds, contact with their droppings, or contact with contaminated surfaces (feeders, waterers, nesting boxes, soil in chicken areas). The organism survives in soil and on surfaces for weeks. In Zone 5 summer heat (July–September, temperatures regularly exceeding 85°F), bacterial loading increases significantly — warmer temperatures accelerate bacterial multiplication in droppings and environmental contamination. Infections are most common in late spring and summer.

*Human symptom timeline and clinical course*: Incubation period is 12–72 hours after exposure (typically 24–48 hours). Symptoms include diarrhea (often loose or watery, sometimes with blood or mucus), fever (101–103°F, usually moderate), abdominal cramps and discomfort, nausea, and sometimes vomiting. The illness is usually self-limiting in 4–7 days in healthy adults. However, in infants, elderly individuals, and immunocompromised people, the course can be much longer and more severe.

*Household management*: The primary concern during extended disruption is dehydration. Salmonellosis causes fluid loss through diarrhea and fever. Manage with oral rehydration solution (electrolyte drink — homemade version: 6 teaspoons sugar + ½ teaspoon salt + 1 liter water; or commercial Pedialyte-equivalent). Keep the ill person eating bland foods once nausea passes. Monitor for signs of dehydration (dry mouth, dark urine, dizziness when standing). Isolation of the ill person from food preparation is critical because Salmonella can be shed for weeks after acute symptoms resolve.

*Medical evacuation threshold*: Escalate to medical care immediately if: any infant under 12 months develops symptoms (Salmonella is particularly dangerous in infants), any immunocompromised household member develops symptoms, symptoms persist beyond 7 days, signs of severe dehydration develop (confusion, inability to stand, severe dizziness), or blood in stool becomes heavy and persistent. [23]

**Goats and Cattle: Q Fever (Coxiella burnetii)**

*Why this is Zone 5-critical*: Q fever is one of the most underappreciated zoonotic risks for homestead operations, particularly those with goats. It is endemic in cattle and sheep populations and particularly prevalent in goats. The disease often goes undiagnosed in animals (infected animals may show no clinical signs) while humans shedding exposure can become quite ill. The transmission route — airborne inhalation — is particularly concerning for homestead operations where humans and animals share enclosed spaces.

*Transmission route*: Q fever is shed in highest concentrations in birth fluids, placental tissue, amniotic fluid, and (in infected animals) milk. The organism is extremely hardy — it survives drying, freezing, and desiccation for long periods, and it remains infectious in dried secretions for months or years. Airborne transmission is the primary human route: a person standing in a barn during a goat birth, or shortly after, with an infected animal can inhale aerosolized particles from birth fluids and become infected. Even brief exposure — standing in a barn for 30 minutes during or immediately after a birth — is sufficient for infection if the animal is a shedder. This is why Q fever is particularly risky for anyone attending livestock births in early spring (February–April in Zone 5, peak kidding season).

*Human symptom timeline and clinical course*: Incubation is 2–3 weeks (sometimes up to 3 weeks). Acute Q fever presents with high fever (104°F+, sometimes up to 105°F), severe frontal headache (often the most prominent symptom), severe muscle and joint aches (myalgia and arthralgia), and profound fatigue and malaise. Patients often describe it as feeling like severe influenza. Respiratory symptoms (cough, chest pain) can occur but are not always present. The acute illness typically lasts 1–2 weeks, though fatigue can persist for weeks afterward.

*Chronic Q fever*: A small percentage of infected individuals (estimated 5–10%, higher in those with pre-existing heart disease or immune compromise) develop chronic Q fever weeks to months after acute infection. Chronic Q fever is primarily endocarditis (heart valve infection) and can be fatal if untreated. Any person with a history of Q fever exposure who develops cardiac symptoms months later (shortness of breath, palpitations, peripheral edema) requires urgent medical evaluation.

*Zone 5 risk seasonality*: Goat kidding season (February–April) is the peak risk period. Cattle and sheep also shed during birthing, but goat dairy and meat operations are more common on small homesteads in Zone 5. Pregnant women should absolutely not attend livestock births if there is any possibility of Q fever exposure — Q fever in pregnancy is associated with spontaneous abortion, stillbirth, and placental complications with high frequency. If a pregnant household member lives with goats, she should avoid the birthing area entirely during kidding season unless the goat is confirmed Q fever antibody-negative (testing is available through veterinary clinics).

*Household management*: Acute Q fever responds to doxycycline (an antibiotic) if available. The treatment course is 2–3 weeks. If doxycycline is unavailable, supportive care (fluids, rest, fever management) is the only option, and recovery usually occurs over 2–4 weeks. The risk is chronic Q fever developing if untreated — but acute infection without treatment is self-limiting in most cases.

*Medical evacuation threshold*: Any person with fever, headache, and myalgia in late February through April following possible birth exposure should be considered a potential Q fever case. Seek medical evaluation if possible. Any person with cardiac symptoms (shortness of breath, palpitations, peripheral swelling) months after a possible Q fever exposure requires urgent medical evaluation for endocarditis. [24]

**All Livestock: Ringworm (Dermatophytosis)**

*Transmission and characteristics*: Ringworm is a fungal infection (not a worm, despite the name) caused by dermatophyte fungi. It is transmitted through direct contact with infected animals or through contaminated surfaces, equipment, and bedding. Ringworm is highly contagious and spreads rapidly in close-contact settings. It is particularly common in young animals: calves, kids, and piglets. Infected animals develop circular, hairless lesions with raised, scaly borders — typically appearing on the face, neck, and sides.

*Human symptoms and timeline*: Incubation is typically 1–2 weeks after exposure. Infected humans develop circular, scaly, itchy lesions that are often red or inflamed. The lesions grow slowly if untreated. The primary discomfort is itching; secondary bacterial infection from scratching is the main complication.

*Household management*: Topical antifungal treatment is effective. Over-the-counter topical antifungals (clotrimazole cream, miconazole, tolnaftate — all available at drugstores) applied directly to the lesion 2–3 times daily typically clear infection within 2–4 weeks. Simultaneously, the source animal should be treated with topical antifungal (livestock-appropriate shampoo or wash, available through farm supply stores). The infection is transmissible from animal to human and back to animal; treating only the person while the infected animal is in close contact risks reinfection. Decontaminate equipment that contacted the infected animal (brushes, halters, etc.) with 10% bleach solution. [25]

*Practical note*: Ringworm in young animals is self-limiting in many cases (will clear on its own over weeks to months), but it is highly transmissible during this period. Treatment reduces transmission and shortens the duration of contagion.

**Goats and Cattle: Cryptosporidiosis**

*Transmission route*: Cryptosporidium is a protozoan parasite shed in feces, particularly in diarrheal feces. Young animals (calves and goat kids under 4 weeks of age) shed the highest concentrations. Transmission is fecal-oral: a person handling young animals with diarrhea and then touching their face, eating, or drinking without handwashing can ingest oocysts (the infectious form of the parasite). The oocysts are extremely resistant to environmental stress and can survive in soil and water for months.

*Human symptom timeline and clinical course*: Incubation is 2–10 days. Symptoms include profuse, watery diarrhea (sometimes explosive), abdominal cramps and pain, nausea, and low-grade fever (usually 100–102°F). The diarrhea is often the most prominent symptom and can be voluminous, causing significant fluid loss. Duration in healthy adults is typically 1–2 weeks, though some individuals have longer courses.

*Zone 5 risk seasonality*: Spring (March–May) is the peak risk period for cryptosporidiosis, coinciding with goat and cattle kidding/calving season. Young animals with neonatal scours (diarrhea) shed the highest levels. Additionally, spring mud and outdoor conditions increase fecal contamination of hands, clothing, and equipment.

*Household management*: There is no specific treatment for cryptosporidiosis in immunocompetent individuals — management is purely supportive: oral rehydration (critical, as fluid loss can be significant), rest, and monitoring. Most immunocompetent people recover on their own. Antimotility agents (like loperamide/Imodium) should be avoided because they slow fecal transit and can prolong the infection.

*Medical evacuation threshold*: Immunocompromised household members require immediate medical attention if exposed or symptomatic. Cryptosporidium is opportunistic in immunocompromised individuals and can cause severe, prolonged, and life-threatening illness. HIV/AIDS patients with low CD4 counts, patients on immunosuppressive therapy (including some cancer treatments), and patients with inherited immunodeficiency can have severe, prolonged infections. [26]

**Wildlife Interface: Lyme Disease (Borreliosis)**

*Transmission route*: Lyme disease is transmitted by black-legged ticks (Ixodes scapularis), which are infected with the spirochete bacterium *Borrelia burgdorferi*. The tick is acquired while managing outdoor animal areas, mowing, moving through brush, or in any outdoor activity in Zone 5 during tick season (April–October, with peak in May–July). Zone 5 is a high-prevalence area for Lyme disease; the black-legged tick is established in all Zone 5 states.

*Human symptoms and timeline*: The classic early symptom is erythema migrans (EM), the "bullseye" rash — a circular, expanding red rash with possible central clearing. However, only 70–80% of people infected develop a rash, so absence of a rash does not mean absence of infection. Symptoms appear 3–30 days after tick bite. Accompanying symptoms include fever, fatigue, joint pain, and headache. If untreated, later manifestations can include arthritis (particularly in the knees), neurological symptoms (facial palsy/Bell's palsy, meningitis symptoms), and cardiac symptoms (myocarditis, conduction abnormalities).

*Household management*: Early treatment with antibiotics (amoxicillin or doxycycline) for 2–4 weeks is highly effective when initiated early (within the first 2–4 weeks of symptom onset). After this window, treatment is still possible but involves longer duration and sometimes different antibiotics. Prevention is the best strategy: daily tick checks after outdoor work (check entire body, removing any ticks found), appropriate clothing (long sleeves, pants tucked into socks in high-risk areas), and using permethrin-treated clothing when working in high-risk areas.

*Medical evacuation threshold*: Any person with late-stage Lyme complications requires medical evaluation: neurological symptoms (facial palsy, meningitis symptoms, peripheral neuropathy), severe joint swelling and pain (Lyme arthritis), or cardiac symptoms (arrhythmia, myocarditis). Early-stage disease with the rash or acute symptoms can often be managed at home with available antibiotics. [25]

### Protective Practices — Prevention Infrastructure

Prevention reduces both animal and human health burden and is far easier than managing illness during disruption:

- **Gloves** during birth attendance, wound care, and manure management. This is the most important single practice for preventing Q fever exposure during births. Gloves prevent aerosol exposure to birth fluids and prevent mucous membrane contact.

- **Handwashing** before eating, drinking, and face-touching. This is standard practice but is critical in a farm context where hands contact animal feces and environmental contamination. Wash with soap and warm water, at least 30 seconds. Hand sanitizer alone is not sufficient for many zoonotic pathogens (it does not work well on Cryptosporidium oocysts, for example).

- **Dedicated footwear** for livestock areas. Do not wear barn shoes or boots into household living spaces, kitchen, or sleeping areas. This prevents tracking environmental contamination into the home.

- **No eating or drinking in animal housing areas**. This prevents accidental ingestion of contaminated material.

- **Dedicated clothing** for livestock work. Clothes that contact animal environments should be laundered separately with hot water immediately after use. This prevents contamination of household clothing and prevents dried infectious material from being aerosolized in the home.

- **Tick checking** after outdoor work during tick season. Check entire body, remove ticks found, and consider keeping ticks in a sealed container if testing is possible in case Lyme disease develops (testing the actual tick can be informative).

- **Shower or bathe after outdoor work**, particularly during spring (Q fever risk) and tick season (Lyme risk). This removes any potential tick exposure and removes environmental contamination.

## Section 5: Household Veterinary Supply Cache and Knowledge System

### Critical Update: FDA Guidance for Industry No. 263 (June 2023)

A critical medication access change occurred on June 11, 2023, that substantially affects household veterinary supply planning. FDA Guidance for Industry No. 263 (GFI 263) transitioned 91 livestock antibiotics from over-the-counter status to prescription-only status. Several medications listed as "OTC" in older veterinary guides are now prescription-only, and this is a permanent regulatory change, not a temporary restriction. The most significant for homestead operations:

- **Injectable Penicillin G Procaine** (historically the most accessible broad-spectrum antibiotic at farm supply stores) is now prescription-only
- **LA-200 (oxytetracycline) injectable** (historically widely available for respiratory infections) is now prescription-only
- **Injectable tylosin** (respiratory disease) is now prescription-only
- **Oral penicillin** (for small animals) is now prescription-only
- **Injectable and oral sulfamethazine and other sulfonamides** are now prescription-only

**Banamine (flunixin meglumine)** has been prescription-only for years and never had genuine OTC availability — it remains prescription-only and has never been available without a veterinary prescription.

The practical implication: establishing a veterinary relationship *before* any disruption is critical because it is now the gateway to most injectable antibiotics. A standing prescription obtained from a veterinarian during normal times allows a household to obtain these antibiotics from farm supply retailers using the prescription, even during disruption. Without a pre-crisis relationship, these medications become unavailable.

### What Remains OTC After GFI 263

The following categories remain over-the-counter and are not affected by the 2023 guidance:

**Vaccines** (all species, all types, all formulations):
- All vaccines listed in Section 2 (CD&T, Marek's, Newcastle, Infectious Bronchitis, Erysipelas, etc.) remain OTC
- Vaccines do not fall under "medically important antibiotics" and are therefore unaffected by VFD and GFI 263 restrictions

**Dewormers/Anthelmintics** (oral and injectable):
- Ivermectin (Ivomec, pour-on and injectable): OTC, continues to be widely available
- Fenbendazole (Panacur): OTC, oral formulation widely available
- Moxidectin (Quest): OTC for equines and cattle
- These are not classified as medically important antibiotics and remain OTC

**Ionophores** (coccidiostats for prevention):
- Monensin, lasalocid, and other ionophore-based coccidiostats remain OTC
- These are used in poultry and ruminant feed and water for coccidiosis prevention

**Probiotics**:
- Probios, Lactobacillus-based products, and yeast-based probiotics remain OTC
- Restore gut flora after antibiotic treatment or during diarrheal illness
- Safe to administer freely with no prescription

**Electrolytes**:
- Bounce Back, Calf-Lyte, and other oral electrolyte solutions remain OTC
- Potentially life-saving for dehydration from diarrhea or heat stress in kids, piglets, and young poultry
- Essential supply cache item

**Epinephrine (1:1000)**:
- For anaphylaxis following vaccination
- Should be on hand whenever administering vaccines, particularly to animals with unknown prior exposure (when rare allergic reactions occur)

**Wound care supplies** (all OTC):
- Betadine (povidone-iodine 10% solution): wound wash and antiseptic
- Hydrogen peroxide: wound cleaning
- Veterinary wound powder (Blu-Kote, scarlet oil): antimicrobial wound dressing and fly prevention
- Sterile gauze pads and veterinary bandaging wrap (Vetrap)
- Surgical staples and staple gun: for large laceration closure in livestock
- Suture material and needles (if the household member has training in suturing)
- 18-gauge and 20-gauge needles, 6ml and 12ml syringes (for vaccine administration and injectable drug delivery)

**Obstetric supplies** (all OTC):
- French nasogastric tube (14–16 Fr) for goat bloat treatment: approximately $5–8 per tube
- OB lubricant (Lubri-Lube or similar water-based lubricant)
- OB gloves (shoulder-length, for dystocia intervention): approximately $1–2 per pair
- Chain snare or calf puller for cattle (if cattle are present): approximately $30–60

### The Veterinary Relationship: Gateway to Prescription Medications

The most important step a Zone 5 livestock household can take before any disruption is establishing a working relationship with the nearest large-animal veterinarian. This relationship is now the essential prerequisite for accessing the medications that have moved to prescription-only status. With a veterinary relationship, a household can:

1. **Obtain standing prescriptions** for essential medications (penicillin, tetracycline, pain medication, reproductive hormones) that can be filled at farm supply retailers, allowing the household to stockpile a reasonable supply during normal times
2. **Receive design guidance** for vaccination and biosecurity protocols tailored to the household's specific species mix and exposure risks
3. **Establish phone consultation protocols** for emergency situations during disruption when on-farm visits are impossible
4. **Arrange documentation** (written protocols, medication records, baseline animal health records) that increases the household's independent diagnostic capacity

**How to establish the relationship**:

1. **Find the nearest large-animal veterinarian** using the USDA NIFA Shortage Area Map (https://www.nifa.usda.gov/vmlrp-map), which includes veterinary clinic locations. In shortage areas, this may require contacting the nearest veterinary school (Purdue College of Veterinary Medicine, University of Illinois College of Veterinary Medicine, Michigan State University College of Veterinary Medicine, or Iowa State University College of Veterinary Medicine).

2. **Schedule a farm visit** before any health crisis occurs. The veterinarian comes to your property, sees the animal housing, assesses the species mix, and understands your operational baseline. This establishes the relationship under non-emergency conditions.

3. **Request a written protocol** at the initial visit: which vaccines are appropriate for your species mix? What is the timing for each? Which medications does the veterinarian recommend for on-farm use? What is the phone consultation policy if you have questions during off-hours? Document this in the household veterinary notebook.

4. **Establish prescription access**: Ask which farm supply stores can fill prescriptions from the veterinarian, and ask about standing prescriptions for essential items (penicillin, pain medication, anti-inflammatories). Understand any limitations: some veterinarians use prescriptions sparingly; others are comfortable with standing prescriptions for established clients.

5. **Maintain records** of all animal vaccinations, health treatments, and any illnesses. These records increase the credibility of any phone consultation and allow the veterinarian to provide better guidance.

This relationship is worth more than any supply cache because it ensures access to prescription medications even during disruption.

### The Household Veterinary Notebook

Every livestock-keeping household should maintain a physical (paper) veterinary notebook. Digital-only records are useful but are inaccessible if communication infrastructure fails. The notebook should be stored in a waterproof container and kept current.

**Contents**:

**Individual animal records**: For each animal, maintain: individual identifier (name or ID number), birth date, breed, source (where purchased or born), current weight/body condition score (updated monthly during grazing season), vaccination history (vaccine name, date, batch number, veterinarian or administrator), illness history (date, symptoms, treatment, outcome), breeding records (if applicable), and offspring.

**Emergency contact card**: Nearest large-animal veterinarian with name, phone number, after-hours emergency number if available. Nearest veterinary school emergency clinic with location and phone number. Nearest extension agent contact.

**Species-specific quick-reference cards** (laminated, water-resistant):
- Normal vital signs (temperature, heart rate, respiratory rate) for each species kept
- The triage decision framework from Section 3 (when to call for help vs. manage at home)
- Vaccination schedule for each species (from Section 2)
- Age-specific milestones (weaning, reproductive readiness, production expectations)

**Zone 5 disease calendar**: The month-by-month risk table from Section 1

**OTC supply inventory**: List of items in the supply cache with expiration dates (audit quarterly); specific quantities on hand and reorder thresholds

**Medication records**: Log of any prescribed medications obtained (date, medication, quantity, cost, source, veterinary prescription number if applicable)

**Post-visit documentation**: Notes from any veterinary visits, including assessment of animals, recommendations, and treatment plans

### Recommended Physical Reference Texts

Given that internet and digital resources may be unavailable during disruption, physical reference books should be acquired and kept accessible:

1. **Merck Veterinary Manual** (current edition): The most comprehensive offline reference for animal health. Available new ($80–120); used copies may be found from veterinary schools or farmers at livestock auctions. Covers all common livestock species and provides diagnostic and treatment information.

2. **Gail Damerow, "Storey's Guide to Raising Chickens"** (4th edition, 2017): The definitive backyard and small-flock guide for poultry health and management. Zone 5-applicable and very practical.

3. **Cheryl K. Smith, "Storey's Guide to Raising Meat Goats"** (2009): Focused guide for goat management and health. Oriented toward meat production but includes dairy-relevant health information.

4. **Ann Larkin Hansen, "The Backyard Goat"** (2010): Accessible first reference for households new to goat keeping. Less comprehensive than the Smith guide but easier to navigate.

5. **Pet Emergency Academy course materials** (if enrolled): The Livestock Emergency First Aid certification course provides written materials on assessment, triage, and first-response procedures.

### Training Recommendation

The Pet Emergency Academy Livestock Emergency First Aid and Wellness Care Certification Course (approximately 8–12 hours of online coursework) is the recommended training pathway for the household member designated as the veterinary coordinator. The course covers:
- Vital sign assessment and interpretation
- Triage decision-making
- Wound care and wound closure techniques
- Bloat response and management
- Birthing assistance and dystocia intervention
- Basic medication administration (injection, oral, topical)
- First aid protocols for common emergencies

Available through petemergencyacademy.com; cost approximately $150–300 depending on certification level. Valuable for any serious livestock keeper.
