### Week 3: June 15–21 — Learning Theory Foundations

**Primary task**: Write Section 3, Learning Theory Foundations. This section must translate theory into practitioner tools — not a literature review but a framework practitioners can use.

**Writing target**: 4,000–6,000 words cumulative.

Key frameworks to cover:
- Lave and Wenger situated learning: the original mode of human learning is apprenticeship in a community of practice. What this means for community skill transmission design.
- Kolb's experiential learning cycle: experience → reflection → conceptualization → experimentation. How to design community learning experiences that complete the cycle rather than stopping at the experience step.
- Cognitive apprenticeship (Collins, Brown, Newman): modeling, coaching, scaffolding, and fading. The four-stage process for expert-to-novice transfer.
- Andragogy (Knowles): adults learn differently from children. Adults need relevance (why does this matter now?), experience integration (connect to what they already know), self-direction (control over their own learning pace), and immediate applicability (they want to use it now, not in future courses).
- Zone of Proximal Development (Vygotsky): the gap between what a learner can do alone and what they can do with guidance. Community apprenticeship is designed to operate in this zone.

**Section must not become a literature review.** Each theoretical framework should occupy no more than 400–600 words and conclude with a specific, actionable community implication.

**Cumulative word count target at end of Week 3**: 6,000–8,000 words.

### Week 4: June 22–28 — Apprenticeship Models

**Primary task**: Write Section 4, Apprenticeship Models. This section documents operational structure, not philosophy.

**Writing target**: 8,000–11,000 words cumulative.

Required content:
- Three structural models documented with mechanics: (a) One-on-one master-apprentice (how teaching sessions are structured, what ratio of demonstration to practice, how progress is assessed); (b) Small group learning cooperative (how 4–8 learners share instruction time, how peer teaching is organized, how the instructor monitors multiple learners); (c) Rotating expert model (multiple experts each teach a portion of the skill set; used in communities where no single person holds the full skill).
- Failure modes of each model and documented mitigations.
- Community apprenticeship contract template — a fillable document defining: the skill being taught, the teacher and learner, the estimated timeline, the competence milestones and how they will be assessed, compensation or reciprocal obligations, and what happens if the apprenticeship breaks down.
- Time allocation structures: how does a community with full work demands find time for teaching? Historical models (guild apprentices were 50% labor, 50% learning; agricultural extension "field days" scheduled around growing season); Zone 5 implementation (winter months, pre-planting weeks, and evenings during non-harvest periods).

**Cumulative word count target at end of Week 4**: 9,500–12,500 words.

### Week 5: June 29–July 5 — Documentation and Competence Validation

**Primary task**: Write Sections 5 and 6 concurrently. Documentation and validation are operationally linked: good documentation enables validation.

**Writing target**: 13,000–17,000 words cumulative.

Documentation section content:
- Procedure documentation format standards for community use (step-by-step, troubleshooting-first, decision-tree formats — when to use each)
- Visual documentation for mixed-literacy audiences (line drawings, annotated diagrams, photo sequences)
- Offline knowledge management system design (physical library organization, USB archive structure, printed procedure binder hierarchy, maintenance schedule)
- Knowledge maintenance practices (who reviews procedures, how often, what triggers an update)

Competence validation section content:
- Observable performance criteria framework (what does "knows how to maintain the water system" look like in behavioral, observable terms?)
- Three-instance rule (competence is demonstrated by performing the task correctly, independently, three consecutive times under field conditions)
- Peer validation structure (who is qualified to validate? How do you validate the validator?)
- Community skills registry design template (who knows what, at what level, who is currently being trained)
- Quality assurance without external certification: drawing on CBME literature and aviation checklist practices

**Cumulative word count target at end of Week 5**: 13,000–17,500 words.

### Week 6: July 6–12 — Intergenerational Teaching Dynamics

**Primary task**: Write Section 7, Intergenerational Teaching Dynamics. This section is the most sociologically complex and requires the most synthesis from disparate literature.

**Writing target**: 17,000–22,000 words cumulative.

Content:
- Cross-generational failure modes with documented mitigations (expert blind spot, authority gradient, contextual disconnection — see DOMAIN_C_SCOPE_AND_READINESS.md Section 2, Question 2e for details)
- Cultural transmission mechanisms with historical case studies: Amish embedded knowledge, Pacific navigation oral tradition, guild ritual and initiation, agricultural storytelling traditions
- Peer teaching design: when same-age learners teach each other under instructor oversight, learning outcomes improve for both teacher and learner (the protégé effect — teaching others increases retention by 50–90% vs. passive learning alone). How to structure peer teaching in a community apprenticeship program.
- Role of Zone 5 seasonal rhythms in cross-generational transmission: agricultural knowledge is most effectively transmitted during the season it is used (planting instruction during planting, harvest instruction during harvest). The domain must produce a Zone 5 seasonal learning calendar.

**Cumulative word count target at end of Week 6**: 17,000–22,000 words.

### Weeks 7–8: July 13–26 — Learning Cooperatives, Knowledge Management, Zone 5 Integration

## 4. Potential Bottlenecks and Mitigation

**Bottleneck 1: Learning curve data gap (primary risk)**
- Risk level: Medium
- Consequence: Section 2 (Critical Skills Inventory) incomplete or under-sourced
- Mitigation: Use FEMA CERT training sequences as the primary benchmark (these are the most rigorously validated available data for emergency skills). Use Extension Service adult education hour estimates for agricultural skills. Use NABCEP professional training hours as an upper bound for solar skills. Document all estimates as practitioner evidence vs. peer-reviewed data. The difference between a rigorous and a weak Critical Skills Inventory is not whether the data is peer-reviewed but whether the uncertainty is acknowledged clearly.
- If unresolved by June 10: Escalate to user with a specific question about whether to proceed with practitioner evidence explicitly labeled, or whether to wait and narrow the scope of the learning curve data to skills where better evidence exists.

**Bottleneck 2: Intergenerational teaching dynamics section synthesis (moderate risk)**
- Risk level: Medium
- Consequence: Section 7 becomes surface-level or theoretical rather than practitioner-useful
- Mitigation: Anchor the section in three concrete case studies rather than theoretical frameworks. The Amish knowledge transmission literature is the richest available source for North American community contexts. Use it extensively. Supplement with Extension Service peer teaching evaluation literature.
- Interview alternative: If the agent identifies a Zone 5 4-H county director willing to respond to a structured written question (not a real-time interview), an emailed 5-question survey can provide primary practitioner evidence on intergenerational teaching dynamics without requiring synchronous access.

**Bottleneck 3: Scope expansion into K-12 or educational technology (moderate risk)**
- Risk level: Medium-High (educational research is among the most expansive bodies of literature)
- Consequence: Document exceeds word count, blurs its practitioner purpose, loses Zone 5 focus
- Mitigation: Weekly scope audit at Weeks 3, 6, and 9 (the three highest-expansion-risk writing weeks). The audit question is simple: does any paragraph in this week's production address K-12 curriculum design, formal education policy, or online learning platforms as a primary subject? If yes, remove or reframe.
- Hard boundary enforcement: The 12–15 critical skills ceiling is the primary scope control mechanism. If the skills list stays bounded, the document stays bounded.

**Bottleneck 4: Practitioner interviews (if needed)**
- Risk level: Low (the document does not require practitioner interviews to complete — practitioner literature is sufficient)
- Mitigation: All practitioner evidence can be gathered from published practitioner reports, FEMA and Red Cross training documentation, Extension Service publications, and 4-H program records. Real-time interviews are not required. If a practitioner contact opportunity arises organically, it is a quality bonus, not a critical path item.
- Async email alternative: If the agent identifies a specific knowledge gap that a practitioner expert could resolve, structured written questions sent by email are the correct protocol — not real-time calls, which introduce scheduling dependencies. Expect 5–10 business days for responses; build into Week 6–7 buffer if used.
