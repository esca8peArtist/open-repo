# Candidate D: Multi-Community Governance Scaling and Decision-Making

---

## Research Scope Summary

Multi-Community Governance documents how decision-making structures that work at village scale (20–50 people) can coordinate across multiple communities (150–500+ people), and how governance institutions actually learn from failures to prevent the same failures from recurring. It answers: how do 3–5 federated communities make decisions together without a central authority, and how do they fix their governance when it breaks?

This is not a repetition of Phase 5 Wave 2's conflict resolution and community playbook — those documents cover governance within a single community of roughly stable size. This domain covers the constitutional and scaling dimension: what breaks when a community doubles or triples in size, how to design governance structures that remain legitimate across that growth, and what institutional mechanisms prevent the same disputes from recurring.

The failure mode this domain addresses is well-documented: successful small communities frequently collapse or fragment when they reach 50–150 people, not because of resource scarcity but because governance structures designed for 20 people cannot scale to 150 without modification. Mondragon, the Israeli kibbutz movement, and the Zapatista autonomous communities all confronted this problem explicitly. The solutions they developed — federation, rotating leadership, sociocratic governance, consensus protocols — are the core subject matter.

The domain answers three bounded questions:

1. What governance structures allow multiple communities to coordinate decisions without central authority? (federation models, consensus protocols at 500+ person scale, inter-community conflict resolution, information flow in federations)
2. How do institutions learn from failures and adapt governance to prevent repeated failure modes? (crisis learning cycles, adaptive management, institutional memory, feedback loops, early warning systems)
3. What incentive structures and accountability mechanisms keep scaled governance honest? (checks and balances in federations, transparency, resource allocation without predation, term limits, rotation)

Out of scope: national and international governance theory, comparative constitutional law, and macro political science not applicable to 150–500 person federations.

**Known gaps (real, not refinement-level)**:

- Detailed case studies of governance failure in resource-constrained settings are underreported in academic literature. Success stories get published; failures are suppressed. Sourcing governance failure documentation requires active search beyond the standard academic literature.
- Consensus decision-making at 300+ person scale has limited documented examples — most case studies are below 200 people. The research may require synthesizing multiple smaller cases.
- Institutional learning mechanisms in small community settings are frontier research without a single authoritative synthesis; the agent will need to build this from adjacent literature.

## Unique Contribution to the Resilience Framework

Phase 5 documents what a resilient community does. This domain documents how a resilient community governing body learns from what it does wrong. Without institutional learning mechanisms, communities repeat governance failures. With them, each governance failure becomes a documented lesson that reduces the probability of recurrence.

The domain also addresses the most common failure mode of successful intentional communities: governance structures designed for 20 people that become illegitimate or dysfunctional when the community reaches 100. The Ostrom framework, sociocracy, and federation models collectively address this failure mode with documented design principles. This domain synthesizes those principles and translates them to Zone 5 community federation contexts.

**The Midwest governance context is rich with primary sources**: agricultural cooperative federations (Land O'Lakes, Organic Valley, Cenex), rural water authorities, county-level soil and water conservation districts, and township governance all operate as multi-stakeholder federations in Zone 5. They are living case studies, not theoretical constructs. The Swiss cantonal system's 700-year operating history provides the longest-running empirical validation of polycentric governance — multiple autonomous units coordinating on shared challenges without central authority.

**The primary documented failure mode** in community governance scaling is "founder syndrome": governance authority that concentrates in founding members who resist structural adaptation as the community grows. Term limits, role rotation, and explicit succession protocols are the three most evidence-supported preventive mechanisms — all translatable to Zone 5 contexts.

## Strategic Timing Rationale

Domain D's case for a June 1 start rests on leverage and an active governance window:

- **Post-COVID governance failure window**: 2020–2025 generated extensive documentation of governance collapse in community organizations under sustained stress. This research is being published and synthesized in 2025–2026. A domain produced now can integrate this contemporaneous evidence; waiting until 2027 means this window closes as the literature shifts to post-acute analysis.
- **Highest-leverage candidate for Phase 6 as a whole**: economic systems, skill transmission, and mutual aid network architecture all depend on functioning governance at scale. A community that cannot make decisions at scale cannot implement any of the other Phase 6 domains effectively. Governance Scaling is the enabler layer for everything else in Phase 6.
- **Federation demand in Zone 5**: growing interest in multi-entity resilience coordination at the county level has been documented in agricultural cooperative networks and rural planning organizations. Zone 5 communities are actively grappling with coordination at scales beyond what their existing governance structures were designed for.
- **Scheduling reality**: Domain D has the lowest production confidence (78%) and the longest timeline (13 weeks). If this domain is desired for Phase 6, it should start June 1 — not September — because a September start produces an uncertain December finish and a tight distribution window. A June 1 start with August 30 delivery maintains the overall Phase 6 schedule.
- **Co-authoring benefit**: this domain has the highest benefit from co-authoring of the three candidates. A June 1 start allows time to identify and onboard a co-author (political science or community organizing background) before Week 2 production begins. A later start compresses this.
