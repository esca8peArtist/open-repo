# Phase 6 Domain G — Zone 1 & 2: Voter Registration Barriers and Technology Solutions

**Research date**: July 4, 2026. This document represents the Week 1-2 early-start deliverable for the Phase 6 Democracy Tools domain, executed four months before the scheduled November 4 launch. All statistical claims are sourced and dated. The legal and legislative landscape is moving rapidly; readers should verify current litigation status against Brennan Center and Democracy Docket trackers.

## Executive Summary

The voter participation infrastructure of the United States is experiencing a convergence of structural shocks without precedent in the post-civil-rights era. Three overlapping crises define the environment as of July 2026:

The Supreme Court's April 29, 2026 decision in *Louisiana v. Callais* rewrote the forty-year enforcement framework of Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act in three ways that collectively make minority vote dilution claims nearly impossible to win. Within weeks, Tennessee eliminated its only majority-minority congressional district, Florida passed a new map the day the ruling was handed down, and Democracy Docket documented that at least 28 active pro-voting lawsuits were derailed. The first application of the new Callais standard to local government redistricting — a June 24, 2026 ruling in DeSoto County, Mississippi — demonstrated that the decision's damage cascades from congressional maps to school boards, county supervisors, and justice courts.

The citizenship documentation barrier is proliferating at the state level even after the federal SAVE America Act failed in the Senate. As of July 2026, seven states have enacted proof-of-citizenship requirements for voter registration; five will enforce them for the November 2026 midterms. The Kansas precedent — where a similar law blocked 31,000 eligible citizens (12 percent of all applicants) — is the primary empirical predictor of their effect. The Brennan Center documents that more than 21 million American citizens lack ready access to qualifying documentation.

Voter roll purges have reached a new institutional dimension: the Trump Department of Justice demanded confidential voter data from all 50 states and Washington, D.C. to build a national purge database. Eleven federal courts dismissed the DOJ's lawsuits as constitutional overreach. Meanwhile, at least 9 states enacted 12 new restrictive voting laws in the first four months of 2026, pushing the 2025-2026 legislative cycle to 44 restrictive laws — surpassing the previous high of 43 enacted in the entire 2021-2022 cycle.

Against this backdrop, the technology solutions ecosystem offers concrete and documented alternatives. TurboVote achieved 79 percent turnout among registered users in the 2024 general election — 15 percentage points above the 64 percent national registered voter rate — including 89 percent turnout among infrequent voters. Same-day registration in 26 states and automatic voter registration in 24 states have produced measurable equity gains where implemented. The ACLU deployed its largest-ever midterm election safeguarding effort ($24.5 million) for 2026. The ERIC consortium, which cross-matches voter roll data across member states, saw two major states rejoin in late 2025 and early 2026 after partisan departures.

The core finding for civic organizers and communities: federal legislative solutions are blocked; federal enforcement is actively hostile. The actionable terrain is (1) state-level same-day and automatic voter registration where available, (2) technology platforms that raise registered-user turnout above baseline, (3) state voting rights acts in ten states providing alternative litigation pathways, and (4) civic organization coalitions deploying election protection infrastructure at scale.

## Zone 1: Voter Registration Barriers Post-Callais

### 1.1 Louisiana v. Callais: What the Decision Actually Changed

On April 29, 2026, the Supreme Court issued a 6-3 ruling in *Louisiana v. Callais* (consolidated with *Robinson v. Callais*), with Justice Alito writing for the majority. The decision's significance lies not in any single holding but in three interlocking changes to the Thornburg v. Gingles framework that, taken together, have dismantled Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act as an enforcement mechanism for vote dilution claims.

**Change 1: From a results standard to an intent floor.** The Gingles framework, established in 1986, allowed plaintiffs to prevail on Section 2 claims by showing that a voting practice resulted in denial or abridgement of minority voting rights — a results standard that did not require proving discriminatory intent. The Callais majority reversed this. Justice Alito's opinion returned Section 2 "to a mere restatement of the constitutional prohibition on purposeful vote dilution," as the Harvard Election Law Clinic characterized it. Because mapmakers routinely justify minority-diluting maps by citing partisan rather than racial motivations, the intent requirement sets an evidentiary bar that plaintiffs rarely can clear. The Campaign Legal Center assessed the ruling as having "eviscerated the Voting Rights Act" and "opened the door for states to enact discriminatory voting maps and laws."

**Change 2: Heightened burden at all three Gingles preconditions.** The original Gingles three-part test required plaintiffs to demonstrate: (1) the minority group is sufficiently large and geographically compact to constitute a majority in a single-member district; (2) the group is politically cohesive; and (3) the majority votes as a bloc to defeat minority-preferred candidates. Callais heightened the evidentiary showing required at each step, including requiring plaintiffs' illustrative maps to "meet all the State's legitimate districting objectives, including traditional districting criteria and the State's specified political goals." This allows states to insist plaintiffs demonstrate compliance with the state's own partisan preferences — a virtually impossible circularity.

**Change 3: Section 2 compliance recharacterized as unconstitutional gerrymander.** In what Professor Pamela Karlan of Stanford Law characterized as Alito "killing the Voting Rights Act," the Court held that Louisiana's creation of a second majority-Black district — created specifically to comply with Section 2 after years of litigation — was itself an unconstitutional racial gerrymander. The logical result is that states that respond to Section 2 litigation by creating majority-minority districts face constitutional challenge, while states that ignore Section 2 now face near-impossible plaintiff burdens. There is no compliant path available under the current framework.

The SCOTUSblog analysis published in June 2026 described Callais as having a "blast radius" extending beyond redistricting into all forms of voting rights litigation, including voter roll purge challenges and at-large voting system challenges. Democracy Docket's research found that at least 28 active pro-voting lawsuits were derailed by the ruling, with many cases paused as courts waited for the Supreme Court to clarify the new standard. The first application of Callais to local government redistricting — a federal judge's June 24, 2026 ruling in DeSoto County, Mississippi — found that plaintiffs "failed all three preconditions of the Gingles test" under the new standard, despite facts that would have supported a Section 2 claim under the pre-Callais framework.

The redistricting cascade in the weeks after the decision was immediate and severe. Tennessee Republicans drew and passed a new congressional map eliminating the state's only majority-minority House district. Florida legislators passed a redistricting bill the same day Callais was decided. Georgia opened a special redistricting session. Mississippi's governor called a legislative special session for judicial redistricting. Alabama was permitted to implement a map that a lower court had found to reflect intentional discrimination. The Supreme Court also directly granted an emergency stay allowing Alabama to use a discriminatory map for 2026 elections, notwithstanding the lower court finding.

**State Voting Rights Acts as the post-Callais litigation landscape.** The NAACP LDF documents ten states that have enacted state-level voting rights acts: California (2002), Washington (2018), Oregon (2019), Virginia (2021), New York (2022), Connecticut (2023), Minnesota (2024), Colorado (2025), Illinois, and Maryland (2026 — enacted one day before the Callais decision). Stateline's June 10, 2026 analysis "States Step Into Voting Rights Void Left by Federal Rulings" documented that while Callais directly targeted federal Section 2 protections, it did not directly address state VRAs — meaning state-level vote dilution protections, where enacted, remain available. Maryland's law protects the voting rights of voters of color in elections for municipal and county governments. Virginia's law prohibits drawing legislative districts that minimize voting power based on race and requires voting materials in additional languages. Washington enacted two laws that expand opportunities to challenge practices that violate voting rights laws.

However, whether state VRAs can survive a state-level application of Callais's reasoning remains legally uncertain. The Stateline analysis noted that "some lawmakers are concerned that the Callais ruling could weaken their state laws," and a legal challenge to a state VRA applying Callais's racial gerrymander logic is foreseeable.

### 1.2 Voter Roll Purges: The Statistical Baseline and the New Federal Threat

**The documented baseline.** The U.S. Election Assistance Commission's 2024 Election Administration and Voting Survey (EAVS), released June 30, 2025, documented 19.1 million voter registrations purged between 2020 and 2022 — a 21 percent increase from the 2014-2016 cycle, which itself represented a 33 percent increase from the 2006-2008 baseline. The Brennan Center's analysis of 6,600 jurisdictions found the median purge rate was 40 percent higher in counties previously covered by the Voting Rights Act's preclearance requirement (before Shelby County v. Holder, 2013) than in other jurisdictions. Eleven states purged over one million voter records in 2022; the Brennan Center estimates approximately 20 percent of purged voters remained eligible.

The next comprehensive purge dataset — the 2026 EAVS — is not expected until June 2027. The precise post-Callais purge rate impact is therefore not yet captured in published federal data. However, four documented 2026 developments indicate the 40 percent formerly-covered-jurisdiction premium is widening rather than stabilizing.

**Ohio SB 293 (December 2025, effective 2026).** This law directs the Ohio Secretary of State to conduct systematic voter roll comparisons at least once monthly, including during the 90 days before a federal election. This directly violates the National Voter Registration Act's 90-day quiet period prohibition on systematic removal of voters from rolls. The Campaign Legal Center, ACLU Voting Rights Project, and ACLU of Ohio filed suit on February 13, 2026, on behalf of CAIR-Northern Ohio and the League of Women Voters of Ohio, arguing that SB 293 violates both the NVRA's quiet-period requirement and the due process clause of the Fourteenth Amendment (because voters receive insufficient notice to challenge removal before their registration is cancelled). As of July 2026, the case is in federal district court.

**Texas SAVE system purges (ongoing).** Texas uses the federal SAVE (Systematic Alien Verification for Entitlements) database — designed for immigration status verification, not voting — for voter roll maintenance. The SAVE system's citizenship data may be years old and cannot reflect naturalizations since database entry, producing false positives that disproportionately affect naturalized citizens. The Campaign Legal Center filed suit in March 2026 challenging this practice.

**The DOJ national voter database initiative (April-June 2026).** In a historically unprecedented action, the Trump Department of Justice demanded confidential voter files from all 50 states and Washington, D.C. — including names, addresses, birthdates, driver's license numbers, and partial Social Security numbers — with the stated intent of building a centralized national database and then instructing states to remove voters from their rolls within 45 days of receiving DOJ-generated lists. As of late June 2026, twelve states voluntarily complied. Voting rights groups including the ACLU, Protect Democracy, and the Brennan Center filed suit to block the database; the Brennan Center separately sued seeking records on whether the DOJ had already generated voter removal lists. Eleven federal courts dismissed the DOJ's suits to compel handover, ruling that the requests trampled states' constitutional role as primary election administrators. The Sixth Circuit became the first appellate court to affirm dismissal in the Michigan case on June 24, 2026. The ACLU press release describes the initiative as creating a "national voter surveil-and-purge database."

**State SAVE Act equivalents accelerating.** The Brennan Center's May 2026 State Voting Laws Roundup documents that between January 1 and May 1, 2026, at least 9 states enacted 12 new restrictive voting laws, with 9 slated to be in effect for the November 2026 midterms. Across the 2025-2026 cycle, at least 19 states have enacted 44 restrictive laws — surpassing the previous high of 43 enacted in the entire 2021-2022 cycle. Lawmakers in at least 41 states considered 302 restrictive voting bills in 2026 alone.

### 1.3 The SAVE America Act and Its State-Level Proliferation

**What the law requires.** The Safeguard American Voter Eligibility (SAVE) Act, also designated the SAVE America Act (H.R. 22, 119th Congress), would amend the National Voter Registration Act to require all persons registering to vote in federal elections to present documentary proof of citizenship in person before completing registration. Qualifying documents include a U.S. passport, military ID, enhanced REAL ID, or a government photo ID combined with a birth certificate or naturalization certificate. The practical effect, documented by the Center for American Progress, would be to eliminate online voter registration (used by 42 states), end voter registration by mail, prohibit voter registration drives (since applicants could not submit required documentation by mail or digitally), and overwhelm election offices with in-person processing demand.

**Federal legislative trajectory.** The bill passed the House 218-213 on February 11, 2026, with one Democratic vote (Representative Henry Cuellar). The Senate voted in March 2026; the bill needed 60 votes to overcome a filibuster. Republicans held 53 seats and needed seven Democratic votes that did not materialize. Four Republican Senators — Lisa Murkowski, Mitch McConnell, Susan Collins, and Thom Tillis — joined Democrats in a 48-50 vote rejecting the amendment. The bill is currently dead as federal legislation.

**State-level proliferation.** The federal failure has not stopped the law's effects from appearing at the state level. As of July 2026, seven states have enacted proof-of-citizenship requirements for voter registration. Five of those laws will be enforced for the November 2026 general election: New Hampshire, Wyoming, South Dakota (signed March 26, 2026), Ohio, and Utah. Florida and Louisiana enacted laws that are not yet implemented for 2026. Kentucky, Kansas, Mississippi, Indiana, and Tennessee require some voters whose eligibility has been challenged through citizenship verification to provide proof of citizenship to prevent their registration from being cancelled. The Center for American Progress tracks this state-level proliferation under the "SAVE Act: Explained" series.

**The Kansas precedent.** The most comprehensive empirical data on the effects of documentary proof-of-citizenship requirements comes from Kansas, which implemented the Kansas Secure and Fair Elections Act in 2013. The 10th Circuit Court of Appeals found, in upholding the injunction against the law, that the requirement "unconstitutionally burdens the right to vote" and that it prevented 31,000 eligible Kansas citizens — approximately 12 percent of all applicants — from registering. The law tied citizenship verification to the driver's license database; the connection produced cascading errors. Federal judges found it violated the NVRA and disproportionately disenfranchised qualified voters. The Supreme Court declined to hear the case, closing the book on Kansas's law. The Kansas experience is the primary empirical predictor for what newly enacted state equivalents will produce.

**The 21 million eligible citizens without documentation.** The Brennan Center for Justice found that more than 21 million American citizens lack ready access to documentary proof of citizenship — that is, they do not have a U.S. passport, birth certificate, or naturalization certificate readily available. People of color are disproportionately represented in this group. Applying the Kansas 12 percent blockage rate across the national population of eligible voters would translate to blocking millions of eligible registrants per election cycle. The Brennan Center additionally documented that the documentation barrier disproportionately affects naturalized citizens, rural residents, low-income voters, and elderly voters.

**Confusion with the SAVE verification system.** A persistent source of public and media confusion: the "SAVE Act" (voting legislation) is distinct from the "SAVE system" (Systematic Alien Verification for Entitlements, an immigration status database managed by USCIS). The SAVE Act for voting is a pure election law measure amending the NVRA; it has no immigration enforcement provisions. The SAVE immigration system is the database that Ohio and Texas are using for voter roll purges — a different and separately litigated problem. Research and civic education materials should explicitly distinguish the two.

### 1.4 Same-Day Registration: Current Landscape and Documented Impact

As of March 2026, 26 states and the District of Columbia have implemented some form of same-day voter registration (SDR). The structure varies: 16 states allow SDR during both the early voting period and Election Day; 4 states (Maine, Vermont, Wisconsin, and Wyoming) allow only Election Day registration; 3 states (Massachusetts, New York, and North Carolina) permit SDR only during the early voting period; and 2 states (Alaska and Rhode Island) allow SDR only for presidential and vice-presidential votes.

**Turnout impact.** Research published in the Journal of Politics found that same-day registration disproportionately increases turnout among individuals aged 18-24, with an effect between 3.1 and 7.3 percentage points for that cohort. The CIRCLE research center at Tufts found that youth turnout averaged 49 percent in states with the easiest participation policies (including SDR), compared to 44 percent in states with the most restrictive policies — a 5-point gap that compounds across election cycles. Minnesota's registration rate of 83.6 percent — the highest in the nation — is directly attributable to its combination of automatic voter registration, online registration, same-day registration at polling places, and pre-registration for 16-year-olds.

**Adoption since 2018.** Delaware, Minnesota, and Pennsylvania all passed AVR legislation in 2023, with implementation phased over the following legislative sessions. The MIT Election Data and Science Lab tracks SDR adoption and maintains a bibliography of empirical research on SDR outcomes. The Ballotpedia SDR tracker is continuously updated and should be verified at the November 2026 research launch for any new state adoptions or modifications enacted in 2026 legislative sessions.

**Remaining barriers.** Twenty-four states do not have any form of SDR. The documented barriers to SDR adoption in non-SDR states cluster around: (1) election administrator concerns about processing capacity on Election Day, (2) voter roll integrity arguments (that SDR allows insufficient time to verify eligibility), and (3) partisan political opposition in Republican-controlled legislatures. The evidence does not support election administrator concerns at the level of documented electoral fraud; SDR states have not experienced significant fraud problems.

**Sources**: [Ballotpedia, same-day voter registration](https://ballotpedia.org/Same-day_voter_registration); [NCSL, same-day voter registration](https://www.ncsl.org/elections-and-campaigns/same-day-voter-registration); [MIT Election Lab, same-day registration research](https://electionlab.mit.edu/research/same-day-registration); [CIRCLE, youth voter turnout 2026](https://www.thecivicscenter.org/blog/youth-voter-turnout-2026); [Journal of Politics, "Rock the Registration: Same Day Registration Increases Turnout of Young Voters"](https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/abs/10.1086/714776); [MIT Election Lab voter registration research](https://electionlab.mit.edu/research/voter-registration).

### 1.5 Automatic Voter Registration: Evidence and Equity Outcomes

**Current adoption.** As of November 2025, 24 states and the District of Columbia have enacted automatic voter registration (AVR). Under AVR, eligible citizens who interact with designated government agencies (principally motor vehicle offices, but also health agencies, social services agencies, and others depending on the state) are automatically registered to vote unless they opt out. The Center for American Progress projects that universal AVR adoption would register more than 22 million new voters in the first year of implementation.

**Documented impact.** Academic research distinguishes between back-end AVR (where the agency submits registration data to election officials, and the voter can opt out) and front-end AVR (where the voter must affirmatively opt in at the point of transaction). Back-end AVR produces an 8.1 percent increase in overall registration rates and a 3.3 percent increase in voter turnout among eligible voters — significantly larger than front-end AVR's 2.9 percent registration gain and 1.1 percent turnout gain. The equity argument for back-end AVR rests on this differential: lower-income and less civically engaged citizens are disproportionately more likely to benefit from automatic registration because the affirmative opt-in requirement (front-end) still requires a deliberate civic decision that non-habitual voters may not make.

State implementation records confirm the research findings. Oregon, the first state to implement AVR in 2016, saw registration rates quadruple at DMV offices in the first year. Vermont saw a 62 percent increase in registration in the first six months after AVR implementation (January-June 2017). Minnesota, which combines AVR with SDR and online registration, achieved an 83.6 percent registration rate — the highest in the nation. The Brennan Center documented that Georgia's active registration rate increased from 78 percent to 98 percent of eligible citizens in the first four years of AVR implementation.

**The Movement Advancement Project (MAP) tracker** monitors AVR policy adoption on an ongoing basis. The MAP research program on automatic voter registration also tracks best practices across states. At the November 2026 research launch, the MAP tracker should be verified for any new state AVR adoptions during 2026 legislative sessions — Virginia's passage of six expansive voting laws through May 2026 may include AVR expansions.

**NVRA Motor Voter and Section 7 compliance.** The National Voter Registration Act's Section 7 requires all state agencies that provide public assistance (including Medicaid, SNAP, and WIC offices, as well as disability agencies) to offer voter registration services. Compliance gaps in Section 7 represent a major lost registration opportunity: millions of Americans who interact with public assistance agencies are not being offered registration as the law requires. The Department of Justice under previous administrations brought Section 7 compliance suits; as of 2026, the Trump DOJ's enforcement priorities have shifted away from Section 7 and toward voter file data demands. The Campaign Legal Center and ACLU continue to bring private Section 7 compliance suits in individual states.

**Sources**: [Brennan Center, Automatic Voter Registration summary](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/automatic-voter-registration-summary); [Brennan Center, AVR issues page](https://www.brennancenter.org/issues/ensure-every-american-can-vote/voting-reform/automatic-voter-registration); [NCSL, automatic voter registration](https://www.ncsl.org/elections-and-campaigns/automatic-voter-registration); [Movement Advancement Project, AVR tracker](https://www.lgbtmap.org/democracy-maps/automatic_voter_registration); [MAP research, AVR best practices](https://www.mapresearch.org/policy-and-issue-analysis/automatic-voter-registration-best-practices); [Ballotpedia, automatic voter registration](https://ballotpedia.org/Automatic_voter_registration); [Voting Rights Lab, AVR tracker](https://tracker.votingrightslab.org/issues/automatic-voter-registration); [DOJ Civil Rights Division, NVRA overview](https://www.justice.gov/crt/national-voter-registration-act-1993-nvra).

### 1.6 The Legislative Environment: 2025-2026 Restriction Acceleration

The Brennan Center's comprehensive tracking of state voting legislation documents a historically severe pattern of restriction in the 2025-2026 cycle. Between January 1 and May 1, 2026 alone, at least nine states enacted twelve new restrictive voting laws. Nine of those laws will be in effect when voters go to the polls in November 2026. The cumulative total for the two-year 2025-2026 legislative cycle reached 44 restrictive laws — surpassing the previous record of 43 restrictive laws enacted during the entire 2021-2022 cycle. Lawmakers in at least 41 states have considered 302 restrictive voting bills in 2026.

The laws restrict access in three primary categories: (1) citizenship documentation requirements (South Dakota, Utah, and partially Florida, Kentucky, and Mississippi, as detailed in section 1.3 above); (2) strict voter ID provisions beyond existing requirements; and (3) expanded eligibility challenge mechanisms that allow third-party challenges to individual voter registration. The third category deserves specific attention: expanded challenge mechanisms create a pathway for organized groups to mass-challenge the eligibility of targeted voter populations, potentially overwhelming election office capacity to adjudicate individual challenges before the election.

The ACLU's analysis of the constitutional landscape documents an executive order in 2026 directing interference with voter rolls and mail-in ballots that a federal court ruled unconstitutional and unlawful — a ruling that voting rights groups applauded as a significant check on executive overreach in election administration.

The Brennan Center's monthly State Voting Laws Roundup series is the definitive real-time tracker of restrictive legislation. The October 2026 and November 2026 roundups (expected at brennancenter.org) will reflect the complete 2026 legislative cycle and should be the primary source at the November 4 research launch.

### 1.7 Zone 1 Summary: The Legal Landscape for Civic Organizations

Four structural facts define the Zone 1 landscape for civic organizations and communities navigating voter registration in 2026:

First, federal litigation under Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act is effectively closed as a tool for addressing new vote dilution schemes. The Callais decision's three-part transformation of the Gingles framework has made it nearly impossible to prevail on minority vote dilution claims. Organizers should treat VRA Section 2 litigation as unavailable except in the ten states with state voting rights acts, where state-level protections survive under state constitutional provisions.

Second, the citizenship documentation threat is real and proliferating. Seven states have enacted proof-of-citizenship requirements; five will enforce them in November 2026. In states without such requirements, registration by mail, online, and at voter registration drives remains legal and accessible — and civic organizations should prioritize maximizing registration through these channels before additional states adopt documentation requirements.

Third, voter roll purges are at a historically high rate and have a new federal-level mechanism (the DOJ national database) that eleven federal courts have rejected but that could re-emerge in modified form. Voters in purge-risk states (formerly-VRA jurisdictions with 40 percent higher purge rates) should be advised to check their registration status regularly and understand the process for challenging erroneous removal. The Campaign Legal Center and ACLU's voting rights trackers are the primary resources for identifying which purge challenges are active and what legal protections exist in specific states.

Fourth, same-day registration and automatic voter registration — where enacted — are documented registration equity tools. Civic organizations in the 26 SDR states and 24 AVR states should incorporate these mechanisms into registration drive design; same-day registration eliminates registration deadline risk entirely. In the 24 non-SDR, non-AVR states, registration deadlines range from 7 to 30 days before the election, and deadline awareness is a critical civic education component for the 2026 midterm cycle.

## Zone 2: Technology Solutions Ecosystem

### 2.1 TurboVote and Democracy Works: Documented Turnout Premium

Democracy Works, the nonprofit civic technology organization that operates TurboVote, published its 2024 impact report documenting the platform's performance in the 2024 general election. The core finding: 79 percent of TurboVote registered users voted in the general election, compared to an estimated 64 percent national turnout rate of registered voters — a 15 percentage point outperformance.

**Disaggregated impact.** The turnout premium appears across demographic groups. Among TurboVote users under 25, the platform achieved 72 percent turnout versus 56 percent nationally for that age group — a 16-point premium. Among infrequent voters (voters who have participated in fewer than half of elections for which they were eligible), TurboVote users turned out at 89 percent, compared to 65 percent nationally for that category — a 24-point gap. This is the most significant finding: 55 percent of TurboVote's registered user base consisted of infrequent voters, compared to only 32 percent of the overall electorate. TurboVote is not only reaching habitual voters; it is disproportionately reaching and activating low-frequency participants.

**Methodology note.** Democracy Works measures registration and turnout by matching user data to official state voter files through a process developed internally and using voter file data from TargetSmart, a political data firm. Among all TurboVote subscribers active through the 2024 election cycle, 76 percent were successfully matched to state voter files as registered voters. The turnout comparison uses matched users as the denominator, not all subscribers, which means the 79 percent figure reflects users successfully registered, not all who attempted to use the platform. This is the appropriate denominator for measuring the platform's activation effectiveness among registered users.

**Attribution question.** The central methodological question is whether TurboVote's turnout premium reflects platform effectiveness or selection effects — that is, whether users who seek out TurboVote are already more civically motivated than the general electorate, making the comparison to national turnout unfair. Democracy Works addresses this through the infrequent voter data: infrequent voters are, by definition, not habitual participants, and TurboVote achieves 89 percent turnout among this group compared to 65 percent nationally. The infrequent-voter finding provides the strongest evidence against pure selection effect, because a pure selection effect would not explain why the platform outperforms on precisely the group that prior to registration was least likely to vote.

**Scale and institutional reach.** Approximately 200 partner organizations worked with TurboVote in 2024 to encourage registration and voting. Democracy Works co-founded the Civic Alliance, a nonpartisan coalition that as of 2026 includes more than 1,300 member businesses providing civic engagement resources to their workforces. For the 2026 midterms, Democracy Works entered a partnership with OpenAI to provide accurate, reliable U.S. election information through OpenAI's products; separately, Anthropic's Claude directs users to TurboVote for reliable, real-time information about voter registration, polling locations, election dates, and ballot information.

**Sources**: [Democracy Works, TurboVote 2024 turnout data](https://www.democracy.works/news/turbovote-user-turnout-surged-past-national-average-in-2024); [Democracy Works about page](https://www.democracy.works/about); [TurboVote features](https://www.democracy.works/features); [Democracy Works / Twilio case study](https://customers.twilio.com/en-us/democracy-works); [TurboVote Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TurboVote); [Harvard Alumni, "Turbocharging Democracy"](https://alumni.harvard.edu/community/stories/turbocharging-democracy).

### 2.2 Federal Registration Infrastructure: Vote.gov and the EAC

**Vote.gov.** The official federal voter registration information portal was established in 2016 and expanded following President Biden's 2021 Executive Order on Promoting Access to Voting, which tasked the General Services Administration with modernizing the site. Vote.gov provides voter registration information in 12 languages, with content based on user research and plans for further language expansion. The site provides a central navigation hub directing users to their state's registration system, and offers the National Mail Voter Registration Form for download in states that accept it. Importantly, Vote.gov does not process registrations directly for most users — it routes to state systems — making it a directory and education tool rather than a registration platform.

**A significant threat to federal registration infrastructure.** On July 16, 2025, the Election Assistance Commission received a petition for rulemaking asking to amend the federal voter registration form to require documentary proof of citizenship. The EAC is seeking public comments on the petition. If the EAC were to amend the federal form to add a citizenship documentation requirement, it would effectively nationalize the SAVE Act's documentation barrier even without legislation — an administrative pathway that the Campaign Legal Center and Brennan Center have identified as a major threat to registration access. Civic organizations should monitor EAC rulemaking proceedings closely.

**Election Assistance Commission resources.** The EAC produces the biennial Election Administration and Voting Survey (EAVS) — the most comprehensive public dataset on election administration in the United States — and maintains a clearing house of election technology resources. The EAC also commissioned the Rutgers Program for Disability Research (Lisa Schur and Douglas Kruse) to produce research on voting experiences for people with disabilities; the April 2024 report found that 40.2 million eligible voters with disabilities (a 5.1 percent increase since 2020) face distinct accessibility barriers, including polling place inaccessibility and digital divide gaps.

**Sources**: [Vote.gov register](https://vote.gov/register); [USAGov, vote.gov explainer](https://www.usa.gov/blog/2023/05/get-to-know-vote.gov-helping-more-people-register-to-vote); [EAC studies and reports](https://www.eac.gov/research-and-data/studies-and-reports); [EAC/Rutgers disability voting report](https://www.eac.gov/sites/default/files/2024-04/EAC_2024_Rutgers_Report_PDF.pdf); [Brennan Center, federal agency voting access](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/how-federal-agencies-are-increasing-access-voting); [Center for Election Innovation & Research, expansion of voter registration methods](https://electioninnovation.org/research/expansion-voter-registration-methods/).

### 2.3 Registration Technology Platforms: Rock the Vote and Vote.org

**Rock the Vote.** Rock the Vote's Online Voter Registration platform has processed more than 14 million voter registration applications. The platform is mobile-friendly, available in 13 languages, and supports pre-registration for 16- and 17-year-olds in states that allow it. The technology suite includes Online Voter Registration, Voter Registration Status Lookup, Absentee Ballot Request, and Pledge to Vote tools. Partner organizations receive embed codes for all tools, allowing integration into any organizational website. The "Election Reminders Program" provides timely, localized election information via email or SMS — step-by-step guidance covering relevant dates and deadlines for federal, state, and major local elections. Basic access to Rock the Vote's tools is free for organizations; premium tools with customization and data analytics are available for large-scale programs.

**Vote.org.** Vote.org describes itself as the largest 501(c)(3) nonprofit nonpartisan voting registration and GOTV technology platform in the United States. The Vote.org platform offers a 50-state voter registration status check (under 30 seconds per check), voter registration, absentee ballot request, ballot information access, ballot tracking, and polling place finder. Vote.org+ extends these tools to hundreds of partner organizations, public figures, and businesses. The tool suite is designed for embedding in partner websites and communications.

**U.S. Vote Foundation Civic Data Products.** The U.S. Vote Foundation operates a civic data licensing program providing APIs and data products for voter registration, absentee ballot requests, election dates, and polling place lookup. These products are used by third-party ballot guidance developers and civic technology organizations, providing a data infrastructure layer beneath the consumer-facing tools operated by Democracy Works, Rock the Vote, and Vote.org.

### 2.4 State Online Registration Models: California and Colorado

**California: VoteCal and the integration model.** VoteCal is California's centralized voter registration database, implemented in 2016. It connects to all 58 county election official offices and multiple state agencies, providing near-real-time data integration across the state's decentralized election administration structure. Public voter portal functions through VoteCal include: voter registration and registration update, polling place lookup, and ballot tracking for vote-by-mail ballots. California also allows pre-registration at age 16, with automatic registration activation at 18, and implemented automatic voter registration through the DMV. The integration of multiple registration pathways (online, DMV, agency, mail) into a single centralized database provides a model for data accuracy that other states can benchmark against.

**Colorado: GoVoteColorado.gov and the accessibility model.** Colorado's voter registration system is accessible at GoVoteColorado.gov for all voters with a valid Colorado driver's license, state ID, or the last four digits of a Social Security number. Colorado voters can register online, through the mail, at a voter registration agency, or at a driver's license examination facility. Same-day registration is permitted through Election Day. All active registered voters automatically receive a mail ballot in Colorado, eliminating the absentee ballot request step. The Colorado model is notable for removing every optional step that requires a deliberate voter action: registration (automatic at DMV), ballot receipt (universal mail ballot), and Election Day access (same-day registration as backstop). The combination has produced consistently high registration and turnout rates.

**Gap states.** States without online registration, same-day registration, or automatic voter registration face the widest participation gaps. These states concentrate in the South and are disproportionately former VRA Section 5 preclearance jurisdictions. The SAVE Act state-equivalent laws are also concentrated in this geographic pattern, creating a compounding barrier: states with the weakest registration infrastructure are also now layering citizenship documentation requirements.

**Sources**: [VoteCal, California Secretary of State](https://www.sos.ca.gov/elections/voter-registration/votecal-project); [Colorado voter registration FAQs](https://www.sos.state.co.us/pubs/elections/FAQs/VoterRegistrationFAQ.html); [Colorado DMV voter registration](https://dmv.colorado.gov/voter-registration-information); [Democracy Works, Colorado registration](https://voting.democracy.works/en/register/co.html); [Fair Elections Center, Colorado](https://fairelectionscenter.org/map/colorado/).

### 2.5 ERIC: Cross-State Voter Roll Accuracy Infrastructure

The Electronic Registration Information Center (ERIC) is a nonprofit organization that helps member states maintain accurate voter rolls by cross-matching data across state lines. At least every 60 days, each member state submits its voter registration data and motor vehicle licensing data to ERIC. ERIC's technical staff matches this against data from all other member states and Social Security death records, identifying voters who have moved, voters who have died, and duplicate registrations. ERIC reports results back to member states, which use the data for registration updates and lawful removal of ineligible registrants.

ERIC's approach addresses the core tension in voter roll maintenance: how to remove ineligible registrants (moved, deceased, or duplicate) without erroneously removing eligible ones. The Brennan Center and Campaign Legal Center have documented that mass purge operations using non-ERIC data sources produce disproportionately high false-positive rates — erroneously removing eligible voters — particularly in states using mismatched databases like the SAVE immigration system.

**Current membership and political controversy.** As of April 13, 2026, ERIC's membership includes 27 states and DC, covering approximately 41 percent of the U.S. population. Member states include Alaska, Arizona, Colorado, Connecticut, Delaware, Georgia, Hawaii, Illinois, Kentucky, Maine, Maryland, Massachusetts, Michigan, Minnesota, Nevada, New Jersey, New Mexico, New York, Oregon, Pennsylvania, Rhode Island, South Carolina, Utah, Vermont, Virginia, Washington, and Wisconsin. New York Governor Kathy Hochul signed legislation requiring the state to join ERIC in December 2025. Virginia Governor Abigail Spanberger signed an executive order directing the Commissioner of Elections to rejoin ERIC in March 2026 — Virginia had left in 2023 during a period when Republican-led states departed citing concerns about ERIC's nonpartisan governance (concerns subsequently investigated and found to lack substance).

Multiple Republican-governed states left ERIC between 2022 and 2024, reducing its membership and geographic coverage. NPR documented in October 2023 that GOP-led states were searching for replacement systems after leaving ERIC; as of mid-2026, no functionally equivalent replacement system has been deployed in departing states, leaving those states with less effective voter roll maintenance infrastructure than ERIC provided.

**Sources**: [Electronic Registration Information Center, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_Registration_Information_Center); [ERICSTATES.org](https://ericstates.org/); [MAP, ERIC membership tracker](https://www.mapresearch.org/democracy-maps/membership_in_electronic_registration_information_center_eric); [Ballotpedia, ERIC](https://ballotpedia.org/Electronic_Registration_Information_Center_(ERIC)); [NPR, GOP states leaving ERIC](https://www.npr.org/2023/10/20/1207142433/eric-investigation-follow-up-voter-data-election-integrity); [League of Women Voters, ERIC explainer](https://www.lwv.org/blog/what-electronic-registration-information-center-eric).

### 2.6 Ballot Tracking, ElectionGuard, and Verification Technology

**BallotTrax.** BallotTrax is the primary commercial ballot tracking platform used by state election offices for mail ballot status notification. California, Colorado, and Georgia contracted with BallotTrax in 2020. A USC study of voters in these three states in the 2022 midterms found that voters who signed up for and used ballot tracking reported higher confidence in the electoral process than non-users, and had higher turnout rates than non-users — a finding that held across all racial, ethnic, and age groups. BallotTrax uses patented technology to notify voters via text, email, or voice of their ballot's status at each step of the mail voting process. Approximately 15 states have counties using at least one of three major ballot tracking systems (including BallotTrax). The MIT Election Lab maintains data on ballot tracking adoption by state.

**ElectionGuard.** ElectionGuard is an open-source software development kit (SDK) developed by Microsoft's Defending Democracy Program. Its core innovation is cryptographic end-to-end verifiability: when used in an election, voters receive a tracker that allows them to confirm their ballot was counted without revealing how they voted. ElectionGuard has been used in actual public U.S. elections in Wisconsin, California, Idaho, Utah, and Maryland. Microsoft piloted ElectionGuard in Fulton, Wisconsin in February 2020 and partnered with Hart InterCivic for a November 2022 General Election deployment in Franklin County, Idaho. In 2023, Microsoft transitioned ElectionGuard's ongoing development to the Election Technology Initiative (ETI), a partnership between the Council of State Governments and The Turnout, while maintaining financial and technical contributions from Microsoft Research. The ETI continues active development with the goal of scaling ElectionGuard deployment across more jurisdictions.

**Accessibility technology.** The Help America Vote Act (HAVA) requires that all voters with disabilities have the right to mark, cast, and verify their ballots privately and independently. The EAC and Rutgers Program for Disability Research (Lisa Schur and Douglas Kruse) produced a 2024 study ("Disability, the Voting Process, and the Digital Divide") documenting the accessibility gap: 40.2 million eligible voters with disabilities face distinct barriers including polling place inaccessibility and the digital divide. The EAC's 2022 study documented progress under HAVA but found persistent gaps in preparation and programming at the local election office level. Remote voting options and electronic ballot delivery have potential to increase disability accessibility, but implementation is uneven.

**Sources**: [BallotTrax](https://ballottrax.com/); [USC study, ballot tracking voter confidence](https://priceschool.usc.edu/news/vote-by-mail-ballot-tracking-tool-boosts-voter-confidence-in-electoral-process-says-new-usc-study-of-voters-in-three-states/); [MIT Election Lab, ballot tracking](https://electionlab.mit.edu/articles/ballot-tracking-use-united-states); [MAP, ballot tracking systems](https://www.lgbtmap.org/democracy-maps/ballot_tracking_systems); [ElectionGuard](https://electionguard.vote/); [Microsoft, ElectionGuard blog](https://blogs.microsoft.com/on-the-issues/2019/05/06/protecting-democratic-elections-through-secure-verifiable-voting/); [CSG Election Technology Initiative](https://www.csg.org/2023/07/26/csg-and-the-turnout-launch-the-election-technology-initiative-with-support-from-microsoft-working-to-increase-voter-confidence-in-elections/); [EAC/Rutgers disability voting 2024](https://www.eac.gov/news/2024/04/18/us-election-assistance-commission-releases-voting-experiences-hava-perspectives).

### 2.7 AI-Assisted Civic Engagement: Evidence and Risks

**The AI and civic engagement landscape for 2026.** The 2026 midterms represent the first federal election cycle in which AI-generated content is a widespread and significant factor in the information environment. AI-generated deepfakes — fabricated audio and video of candidates — have emerged as a documented tactic in 2026 campaigns. The Center for Democracy and Technology has published analysis of social media platform policies and the AI threat landscape ahead of the midterms. The Carnegie Endowment for International Peace published "AI and Democracy: Mapping the Intersections" in January 2026 as the most comprehensive current assessment of the intersection.

Research on U.S. voters' ability to distinguish AI-generated from authentic content is concerning: only 8 percent of California voters surveyed in summer 2025 reported being "very confident" in their ability to tell the difference between real and fake content online. The Federal Election Commission remains divided along partisan lines and has not established clear guidelines governing AI use in campaign advertising. The AI Elections Accord from 2024 — a voluntary agreement among AI developers on election safeguards — was not extended after the 2024 cycle.

**Positive AI applications.** Despite the risks, there are documented positive applications of AI tools in civic engagement. Democracy Works entered a partnership with OpenAI for the 2026 midterms to provide accurate, reliable U.S. election information through AI products. Anthropic's Claude has an election banner directing users to TurboVote for voting information. The potential for AI to help voters navigate registration bureaucracy, understand ballot measures, and find polling place information is real — and distinct from the disinformation risk. The vTaiwan platform (addressed in Zone 3) provides an international model of AI-assisted civic deliberation using Pol.is and subsequently AI-powered transcript and opinion cluster analysis.

The Civic Alliance — co-founded by Democracy Works and the CAA Foundation — provides 1,300+ member businesses with civic engagement resources and guidance. For the 2026 midterm cycle, Civic Alliance members are providing employees with accurate election information, including TurboVote access.

**Sources**: [CDT, social media platform policies and midterms](https://cdt.org/insights/countdown-to-the-midterms-social-media-platform-policies-and-the-information-environment/); [CDT, changing AI threat landscape](https://cdt.org/insights/countdown-to-the-midterms-the-changing-ai-threat-landscape-for-elections/); [Carnegie Endowment, AI and Democracy 2026](https://carnegieendowment.org/research/2026/01/ai-and-democracy-mapping-the-intersections); [CampaignNow, deepfakes 2026 midterms](https://www.campaignnow.com/blog/regulators-scramble-as-ai-deepfakes-flood-the-2026-midterms); [Anthropic election safeguards](https://www.anthropic.com/news/election-safeguards-update); [Democracy Works homepage](https://www.democracy.works/).

### 2.8 Election Protection Infrastructure for 2026

The organizational infrastructure protecting voter access in the 2026 midterm cycle is operating at historically large scale, reflecting the severity of the threat environment.

**ACLU Defending Our Vote.** The ACLU committed $24.5 million to election safeguarding for the 2026 midterms — described as the largest investment in election safeguarding in a midterm cycle in the organization's history. The campaign deploys more than 100 paid staff members and more than 3,000 volunteer leaders across targeted states, with thousands of additional volunteers coordinated by those leaders. The ACLU has already trained 5,000 people on election work and plans to train 5,000 more before November. The organization identified 25 specific threat scenarios to voting access, election administration, and certification, and maintains rapid response legal capacity. The campaign spans 25 swing states and includes Know Your Rights trainings, get-out-the-vote activations, mass mobilization, rapid response coordination, and direct litigation.

**Democracy Docket.** Marc Elias' Democracy Docket maintains the most comprehensive public tracker of voting rights litigation, including the full post-Callais redistricting challenge database. The organization is actively tracking redistricting challenges in Tennessee, Florida, Georgia, and Mississippi following the Callais cascade, as well as the DOJ national voter database cases, the Ohio SB 293 challenge, and the Texas SAVE system litigation. Democracy Docket serves as both a litigation organization and a public information resource for organizers monitoring the legal landscape.

**NAACP Legal Defense Fund — Voting Rights 2026.** The NAACP LDF maintains a dedicated Voting Rights 2026 campaign (voting.naacpldf.org) covering VRA litigation, redistricting challenges, and voter protection resources. LDF's state VRA tracker is the definitive resource for monitoring state-level voting rights act status and litigation outcomes in the ten-state network.

**League of Women Voters — Unite and Rise 8.5.** The League of Women Voters launched the Unite and Rise 8.5 initiative in May 2025, targeting mobilization of 8.5 million individuals — representing 3.5 percent of the U.S. population, a threshold that research from the Carr-Ryan Center for Human Rights associates with successful nonviolent movements — to engage in civic action before the 2026 midterms. The initiative encompasses advocacy, mobilization, civic education, and engagement across the LWV's national network.

## Cross-Zone Integration Points

The research across Zones 1 and 2 reveals four structural intersections where technology solutions directly address the Zone 1 registration barriers:

**1. Same-day registration as technology backstop.** In the 26 SDR states, the registration deadline problem in Zone 1 is structurally solved for Election Day voters. The technology need in SDR states shifts from pre-election registration drives (still valuable for mail ballot participation) to same-day polling place navigation and line-protection. TurboVote and Vote.org's polling place finder tools are the highest-priority Zone 2 tools in SDR states.

**2. ERIC and the purge protection infrastructure.** The 27 ERIC member states have significantly better voter roll accuracy than non-ERIC states. For voters in ERIC member states, the purge risk from bad-data cross-match operations (the Texas SAVE system model) is substantially reduced. The technology integration point: civic organizations in ERIC states can reassure voters that roll accuracy is higher, while organizations in non-ERIC states (including former members that left) need to emphasize the importance of registration status verification (Vote.org's 50-state status check tool, Rock the Vote's status lookup).

**3. Citizenship documentation barrier met by pre-registration verification.** In states with SAVE Act-equivalent laws (South Dakota, Utah, New Hampshire, Wyoming, Ohio, and partially Florida, Kentucky, and Mississippi), voters who lack qualifying documentation face registration barriers that no civic technology can overcome — the barrier is structural, not informational. The technology contribution in these states is (a) early verification: helping voters check well before election day whether their existing ID or DMV record satisfies the state's proof-of-citizenship requirement, and (b) navigation assistance: directing voters to the specific documentation remedy (e.g., whether a birth certificate combined with a state ID meets the requirement in that specific state).

**4. Election protection technology as voter confidence infrastructure.** BallotTrax ballot tracking in the 15+ states using major tracking systems provides documented confidence benefits (USC study: higher confidence and higher turnout among users in California, Colorado, and Georgia). In the 2026 threat environment — where the DOJ voter database initiative, aggressive purge operations, and the Callais redistricting cascade are occurring simultaneously — voter confidence in whether their ballot was received and counted is a material participation factor. Expanding ballot tracking access and awareness is a Zone 2 investment that directly addresses a Zone 1-driven confidence gap.
