## Part 4: Writing Standards

### Tone and Voice

This document is for community organizers, planners, and practitioners — not academics. Write for a reader who wants to do something with this information.

**Specific, not conceptual:**
- Wrong: "Communities should consider resilient economic models."
- Right: "A 100-household community in Zone 5 can establish a functional food cooperative in 6–12 months for $3,000–$8,000 in founding capital, using the USDA Rural Cooperative Development Grant for technical assistance."

**Evidence-based:**
- Support every factual claim with at least one citation
- When evidence is limited, say so directly ("practitioner evidence suggests..." or "no peer-reviewed data exists; the following case studies indicate...")
- Never speculate without labeling it as extrapolation

**Practical:**
- Every section should answer "what should a community actually do with this?"
- Every major concept should connect to a Zone 5 example
- Every section should end with a concrete next step or implementation consideration

### Section Writing Structure

Each section follows this pattern:

1. **Open with a concrete problem** (1–2 paragraphs)
   - State specifically what fails when this domain is not addressed
   - Reference a real example or case study

2. **Present research findings** (bulk of section, 1,500–3,500 words)
   - What does the academic and practice literature show?
   - What do case studies demonstrate about what works and what fails?
   - What are the key design principles or mechanisms?

3. **Translate to Zone 5** (300–500 words)
   - How do these findings apply in Midwest rural and suburban contexts?
   - What changes when you move from documented cases to Zone 5 conditions?
   - What Zone 5 examples already demonstrate this?

4. **Provide actionable guidance** (300–500 words)
   - What should a community do first?
   - What resources are available?
   - What are the common failure modes and how are they prevented?

### Citation Format

Inline citations use numbered format: [1], [2], [3]. Full bibliography at end of document.

Aim for:
- 60–70% academic sources (peer-reviewed journals, university press books, government research)
- 20–30% case studies and reports (NGO evaluations, disaster recovery reports, implementation case studies)
- 10–15% primary documents (legislation, government program descriptions, official standards)

## Part 8: Getting Unstuck

**Scenario: Source not found for a key claim**
Resolution: Search Google Scholar with related terms; check whether the claim is supported by the general literature even if not by a specific targeted source; document what you found and flag the gap. Do not leave a blank or speculative sentence.

**Scenario: Section is not cohering**
Resolution: Return to the three core research questions. Write one sentence answering: "This section addresses Core Question [1/2/3] by showing [specific claim]." If you cannot write that sentence, the section may not belong in this document.

**Scenario: Word count is far ahead of or behind schedule**
Resolution: Flag at the nearest checkpoint. Sections can be expanded or contracted; the overall structure can shift. Early warning at checkpoints is always better than a surprise at final delivery.

**Scenario: Research reveals the domain is larger or different than the outline predicted**
Resolution: Do not expand unilaterally. Document the finding and flag it to the orchestrator with a proposed scope adjustment. The three core questions are the scope anchor — changes to them require orchestrator approval.

**Scenario: Concurrent domain author's framing conflicts with yours**
Resolution: Flag to orchestrator. Do not unilaterally change your framing to match the other domain. The integration deliverable will resolve any conflicts after both drafts are complete.
