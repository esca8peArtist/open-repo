- Confidence the gap can be resolved by June 12: High (source confirmed accessible)

**Gap 1.2: WIR countercyclicality evidence specificity**
- What's needed: Specific transaction volume data correlated with Swiss GDP contraction years; the quantitative evidence that WIR is countercyclical
- Current evidence: Stodder's analysis confirmed (base.socioeco.org); WIR annual reports exist
- Resolution path: Download and read Stodder's WIR and Swiss National Economy analysis in Week 1; extract GDP correlation data
- Confidence: High (source confirmed accessible)

**Gap 1.3: Vermont timebanking quantitative outcomes**
- What's needed: Measurable social or economic outcomes from Vermont timebanking beyond qualitative case studies
- Current evidence: Seyfang (2004) provides UK data; U.S. data is thinner
- Resolution path: Search TimeBanks USA program evaluations; check RWJF (Robert Wood Johnson Foundation) timebanking health outcomes research
- Confidence if not resolved by June 12: Moderate — proceed with Seyfang UK data as primary evidence; note explicitly that U.S. quantitative data is thin; use medrxiv 2025 scoping review as the current evidence source

### Priority 2 — Important Gaps (Resolve in Weeks 1–2)

**Gap 2.1: Zone 5 credit union performance data during downturns**
- What's needed: Comparative performance (loan loss rates, member retention, branch closures) of Zone 5 credit unions vs. commercial banks during 2008 and 2020

**Gap 2.2: Commons failure case studies**
- What's needed: Documented cases where commons governance failed (complement to Ostrom's success cases)
- Current evidence: Ostrom's design principles implicitly document failure modes by inversion; trueque governance failure covers currency commons; specific food commons failure cases not yet identified
- Resolution path: Foundation for Intentional Community (FIC) documentation of food commons failures in intentional communities; search JSTOR for "commons failure" case studies
- Alternative evidence: Use the trueque case for commons governance failure (the trueque nodos were commons-governed; their failure modes are the same as commons failure modes generally)

**Gap 2.3: Local economic multiplier data**
- What's needed: Specific economic multiplier estimates for cooperative vs. conventional enterprise in rural Midwest communities
- Current evidence: General evidence that local businesses have higher multipliers than chains (ILSR data); cooperative-specific data not yet compiled
- Resolution path: Institute for Local Self-Reliance (ILSR) economic impact studies; Cooperative Development Foundation Midwest data; Rutgers University cooperative economic impact research
- Priority: Useful for making the economic efficiency argument in Section 4; not blocking

## Part 3: Optional Deep-Dive Topics

The following topics are not required for the core document but would substantially strengthen specific sections if research time permits:

### Deep-Dive A: COVID Supply Chain Response Case Study
**Priority**: High — COVID is the most recent and most documented supply chain resilience test; Zone 5 food system responses are documented

**Scope**: What did Zone 5 communities do when national supply chains failed in 2020? Which Zone 5 cooperative and local food systems maintained supply? Which failed?

**Key sources to pursue**:
- Leopold Center for Sustainable Agriculture (Iowa State) COVID-era food system research
- Chicago Council on Global Affairs food system resilience report (2020–2021)
- USDA Economic Research Service COVID food supply chain research

**Output if pursued**: A 400–600 word case study box in Section 6 (Supply Chain Resilience) using the standardized format: context → mechanism → outcomes → failure modes → transferable design principles

### Deep-Dive B: Mondragon Cooperative Federation Model (Full Analysis)
**Priority**: Medium — Mondragon is the world's most documented cooperative federation; Zone 5 communities scaling cooperative enterprise need this model

**Scope**: Full governance analysis of Mondragon's three-body structure (General Assembly, Governing Council, Social Council); how labor allocation decisions are made across cooperative network during downturns; worker ownership and wealth-building mechanisms

**Key sources to pursue**:
- Mondragon Corporation annual reports (https://www.mondragon-corporation.com)
- Cheney (1999) and Whyte & Whyte (1991) — confirmed accessible; read full texts rather than summaries
- 2025 DeGruyter paper on Mondragon and utopian legacy (https://www.degruyterbrill.com/document/doi/10.1515/auk-2025-2001/html)

**Output if pursued**: Extended Mondragon analysis in Section 4 (Cooperative Enterprise) — upgrade from 400-word summary to 700-word detailed governance analysis

### Deep-Dive C: Japanese JA Agricultural Cooperative System
**Priority**: Low (Zone 5 specificity is weak, but instructive for scale)

**Scope**: JA (Japan Agricultural Cooperatives) serves 90%+ of Japanese farmers; multi-function cooperative (agricultural, insurance, banking); documented resilience during 2011 Tōhoku earthquake. Most instructive comparison is scale vs. Zone 5 agricultural cooperatives — JA's financial services function (cooperative banking integrated with agricultural cooperative) is a model Zone 5 communities do not currently have.

**Key sources to pursue**:
- Godo, Yoshihisa. "JA-Group: Japan's Cooperative System." In *Cooperatives in a Global Economy*. Edward Elgar, 2003.
- Japan National Federation of Agricultural Cooperative Associations annual data

**Output if pursued**: One-page comparative analysis in Section 4 sidebar: JA vs. Zone 5 agricultural cooperative structure comparison
