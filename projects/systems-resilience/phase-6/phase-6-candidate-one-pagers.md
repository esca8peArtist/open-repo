## Candidate 1: Community Economic Resilience

### What This Is

Community Economic Resilience documents the economic operating system of a resilient community — how people trade goods and services, store value, and coordinate specialized labor when national supply chains, banking, and cash stop functioning reliably. It is the answer to the question Phase 1 through 5 never asks: *how does the farmer compensate the electrician?* Every other phase produces something (food, water, power, governance). This domain explains how a community distributes what it produces without relying on dollar-denominated markets.

The empirical foundation is solid. Argentina's 2001 crisis generated barter clubs serving three million people within eighteen months. Switzerland's WIR mutual credit system has operated continuously since 1934, surviving the Great Depression, World War II, and three recessions. Emilia-Romagna, Italy, where cooperatives produce roughly 30 percent of regional GDP, went from one of Italy's poorest regions after World War II to one of Europe's wealthiest. These are not theories — they are operational systems with documented outcomes.

### Research Scope

The domain answers three bounded questions:

1. What economic architectures sustain when the dollar fails? (local currencies, mutual credit, barter protocols with documented designs)
2. How do communities allocate capital and coordinate production without market price signals? (cooperatives, land trusts, commons management, Elinor Ostrom's design principles)
3. What economic bridges prevent community isolation? (inter-community trade, barter protocols, comparative advantage without global supply chains, regional economic federation)

Out of scope: national macroeconomic policy, Federal Reserve dynamics, corporate finance, and monetary history before the 20th century.

### Expected Word Count

45,000–55,000 words across 10–12 sections. This is consistent with Phase 5 domain size. The pre-research document (`phase-6/01-community-economic-resilience.md`, ~6,800 words, 38 citations) provides a complete production-ready opening chapter that covers economic failure propagation, cooperative models, credit unions, mutual aid, complementary currencies, international case studies, and implementation templates. The remaining 9–11 sections (roughly 38,000–48,000 words) need full research.

### Cross-Domain Bridges

**To Phase 5:**
- Phase 5 Wave 2 Community Implementation Playbook: that document covers governance structures for community decision-making. This domain adds the economic governance layer — how communities allocate resources through those structures.
- Phase 5 Wave 2 Conflict Resolution Framework: economic disputes (resource allocation, debt, unequal exchange) are among the most common community conflict drivers. Clear economic institutions reduce conflict frequency.
- Phase 5 Wave 3 Food Preservation and Storage: Phase 5 shows how to produce and preserve food. This domain answers how to exchange it equitably — the food cooperative and LETS models are the institutional bridge between production and distribution.
- Phase 5 Wave 3 Water Systems: water infrastructure is capital-intensive. Cooperative ownership structures are the legal architecture for collective infrastructure that Phase 5 documents operationally.

**To other Phase 6 candidates:**
- Governance Scaling: economic structures shape what governance models are feasible (economically fragile systems need different governance than stable ones), and vice versa. The two domains are tightly coupled; they are strongest when produced together.
- Skills Development: the economic system determines which skills are economically viable to teach. No point training well-drillers without wells; no point running a cooperative without members skilled in democratic decision-making.
- Phase 6 pre-research (Farm Equipment + Meshtastic): a community Meshtastic network can carry seed inventory broadcasts, tool availability requests, and bulk-order coordination — all economic coordination functions documented in this domain.

### Unique Contribution to the Resilience Framework

Phase 1 through 5 creates an implicit assumption: production automatically leads to equitable distribution. It does not. A community that has learned to grow food, purify water, and generate power still faces the question of who gets what, how specialized labor is compensated, and how to prevent economic resentment from fracturing the group.

This domain closes the gap between production capability and economic sustainability. Without it, the resilience framework is operationally complete but socially fragile. Communities with clear economic institutions and exchange infrastructure are measurably more stable in crisis contexts than those relying on informal reciprocity alone. This is the load-bearing economic layer under everything else in the corpus.

### Key Findings from Pre-Research

The `phase-6/01-community-economic-resilience.md` document (6,800 words, 38 citations, verified as production-ready) establishes several findings that will anchor the full domain:

- Argentina's 2001 barter networks demonstrated that informal mutual aid networks with pre-existing social trust form effective exchange systems faster than formally designed currency systems. Trust infrastructure is built before crisis, not during it.
- WIR's 90-year operating history shows mutual credit is viable at scale and provides countercyclical liquidity — credit expands when conventional markets contract, which is exactly when communities need it most.
- USDA's $24.3 million annual Rural Cooperative Development Grant program (reauthorized through FY2029 by H.R. 1951) is currently available to Zone 5 communities for cooperative formation technical assistance. This is a live resource, not a historical one.
- Complementary currency design failures (Argentina's créditos, Bristol Pound) cluster around two causes: insufficient anti-hoarding mechanism and insufficient anchor transactions. Both are solvable with known design principles.
- The implementation pathway from zero infrastructure to functioning cooperative ecosystem runs through four phases spanning 1–10 years; Phase A (anchor institution, typically a food cooperative) can begin with 20 committed households and $2,000–$10,000 in founding capital.

## Candidate 2: Skills Development and Knowledge Transfer

### What This Is

Skills Development and Knowledge Transfer documents how communities preserve and transmit practical competency across time — when formal educational institutions, apprenticeship systems, and professional certification no longer function. It answers: how does hard-won technical knowledge not evaporate in one generation?

This is distinct from Phase 5's educational governance document (which covers schooling structures). This domain covers adult skill development, the mechanics of apprenticeship, knowledge preservation when experts age or leave, and the community systems that ensure critical skills are redundantly held. It treats skill transmission as survival-critical infrastructure, not as educational philosophy.

The failure mode that motivates this domain is concrete: a community loses its water system operator to illness, its solar technician to relocation, or its experienced medic to age. If no one was trained to replace them, the entire system they maintained fails. The question is not whether this happens — it does, in every multi-generational organization — but whether the community has the knowledge infrastructure to survive it.

### Research Scope

The domain answers three bounded questions:

1. What skills are critical for community resilience and what are their actual learning time requirements? (priority skill mapping from Phase 1–5 domains, learning curves for adult learners in low-resource contexts)
2. How do communities transmit complex technical skills without schools or credentialing institutions? (apprenticeship models, documentation strategies, competence validation, cultural transmission)
3. What institutions and governance structures support skill transmission at community scale? (learning cooperatives, skill-sharing networks, knowledge management, oral history, quality assurance)

Out of scope: comprehensive K-12 curriculum design, educational technology for children, national education policy, and neuroscience of learning at the laboratory level.

### Expected Word Count

48,000–58,000 words across 10–12 sections. The domain is somewhat larger than the economic domain because the skill inventory section (12–15 critical skills with documented learning curves and prerequisites) is inherently extensive. No pre-research document exists in the phase-6/ directory for this candidate — the candidate outline (`phase-6-candidate-skills-development.md`) is the starting point.

### Source Library Readiness: 70–75%

Core sources are accessible: Lave and Wenger's situated learning theory (peer-reviewed, widely available), Kolb's experiential learning model (foundational adult education literature), UNESCO reports on non-formal education in low-resource contexts (free UN documents), USDA 4-H and Extension Service documentation (land-grant university archives), Cuba post-Soviet education adaptation case studies (academic journals, documented), Puerto Rico hurricane recovery reports (FEMA and academic sources). These cover apprenticeship models, adult learning theory, and the institutional layer.

Known gaps: quantitative learning curve data for specific trades (well-drilling, solar installation, medical response) in low-resource settings is mostly practitioner reports rather than peer-reviewed studies. Intergenerational teaching dynamics in North American contexts are underresearched compared to European apprenticeship systems. These are refinement gaps; the domain can proceed with practitioner evidence documented as such.

Estimated additional sources needed: 55–85 (from current ~45 core sources to the 130–160 citation target). Research overhead is moderate; citation chasing from the core apprenticeship literature will yield most of the needed sources.

### Estimated Production Hours

**22–27 hours** for full production (source library at 70–75% readiness, no pre-research chapter).

The absence of a pre-research chapter (unlike the economic domain) adds approximately 4–6 hours compared to Domain A. The skill inventory section is the most research-intensive component: identifying 12–15 critical skills and finding defensible learning curve estimates for each requires systematic source review.

Timeline: June 1 decision → June 5–10 source library finalized → June 10–12 production begins → July 20 draft complete → August 23 final. Confidence: 88% on schedule, higher with an author who has adult education experience.

### Cross-Domain Bridges

**To Phase 5:**
- Phase 5 Wave 3 Educational Governance: that document covers schooling structures (who teaches children what). This domain covers adult skill transmission — a different question with different mechanics. The two documents are complementary rather than overlapping.
- Phase 5 Wave 2 Community Implementation Playbook: skill distribution across a community determines which roles are redundantly covered. The playbook documents role structures; this domain ensures the skills behind those roles are not single-person dependencies.
- Phase 5 Wave 2 Psychological Support Guide: shared learning builds collective identity and psychological cohesion. This domain's learning cooperative model is also a social bonding structure.
- All Phase 5 Wave 3 operational domains (food preservation, water systems, healthcare offline, fuel production): each domain contains skills that must be actively transmitted. This domain provides the transmission architecture for those skills.

**To other Phase 6 candidates:**
- Community Economic Resilience: economic systems determine which skills are economically viable to teach and compensate. Teaching skills that have exchange value is more sustainable than teaching skills that don't.
- Governance Scaling: decisions about what to teach, who teaches, and how to validate competence are governance questions. Educational governance is a subset of community governance design.
- Phase 6 pre-research (Farm Equipment): Track A of the Phase 6 pre-research documents farm equipment repair procedures. The Skills Development domain provides the pedagogical architecture for how communities actually teach those repair skills — the "how to teach" layer above the "how to do" layer.

### Unique Contribution to the Resilience Framework

Phase 1 through 5 documents procedures: how to filter water, grow food, organize communities, and resolve conflicts. It assumes the reader — or someone in their community — already has or can acquire the relevant skills. That assumption becomes increasingly fragile over time. Skills concentrated in one or two people evaporate when those people leave, age, or die.

This domain is the bridge between documentation and sustained capability. It answers: how does a community ensure that 10 years from now, the person who knows how to maintain the microgrid has trained two successors? How does it ensure that skill isn't locked in someone's head but embedded in the community's social structure? The resilience framework is operationally complete; this domain makes it temporally durable.

The Midwest has existing infrastructure to build on: 4-H has operated for 120 years as a practical skill-development system for youth in agricultural communities. Extension services have taught adult farmers for a century using demonstrated, field-tested pedagogical methods. This domain synthesizes what works from that tradition and translates it to the resilience context.

### Key Findings from Pre-Research

No pre-research chapter exists; findings are derived from the candidate outline and the Phase 5 scope document:

- The highest-leverage insight from the PHASE_6_SCOPE_AND_READINESS.md assessment: Phase 5 assumes communities have the skills to execute documented procedures. That assumption is false for most communities entering crisis without preparation. Skills Development is the enabler layer — it makes Phase 5 actually usable.
- Cuba's post-Soviet adaptation (1990s "Special Period") provides the closest documented case of systematic adult skill transmission under crisis conditions: the government rapidly reoriented education toward practical agricultural and technical skills when oil-dependent industrial agriculture collapsed. The outcomes are documented in academic literature.
- The Amish knowledge transmission model — embedding skill practice in daily work rhythms and seasonal events rather than formal instruction — is a functioning North American example of intergenerational technical transmission without formal credentialing. It is directly applicable to Zone 5 rural communities.
- Learning curves for specific trades vary enormously: basic food preservation requires 4–8 hours of supervised practice; small engine repair to basic competence requires 40–80 hours; solar microgrid commissioning requires 60–120 hours. These estimates shape which skills communities can realistically develop before crisis and which require multi-year apprenticeship investment.

## Candidate 3: Governance Scaling and Decision-Making

### What This Is

Governance Scaling and Decision-Making documents how decision-making structures that work at village scale (20–50 people) can coordinate across multiple communities (150–500+ people), and how governance institutions actually learn from failures to prevent the same failures from recurring. It answers: how do 3–5 federated communities make decisions together without a central authority, and how do they fix their governance when it breaks?

This is not a repetition of Phase 5 Wave 2's conflict resolution and community playbook — those documents cover governance within a single community of roughly stable size. This domain covers the constitutional and scaling dimension: what breaks when a community doubles or triples in size, how do you design governance structures that remain legitimate across that growth, and what institutional mechanisms prevent the same disputes from recurring.

The failure mode this domain addresses is well-documented: successful small communities frequently collapse or fragment when they reach 50–150 people, not because of resource scarcity but because governance structures designed for 20 people cannot scale to 150 without modification. Mondragon, the Israeli kibbutz movement, and the Zapatista autonomous communities all confronted this problem explicitly. The solutions they developed — federation, rotating leadership, sociocratic governance, consensus protocols — are the core subject matter.

### Research Scope

The domain answers three bounded questions:

1. What governance structures allow multiple communities to coordinate decisions without central authority? (federation models, consensus protocols at 500+ person scale, inter-community conflict resolution, information flow in federations)
2. How do institutions learn from failures and adapt governance to prevent repeated failure modes? (crisis learning cycles, adaptive management, institutional memory, feedback loops, early warning systems)
3. What incentive structures and accountability mechanisms keep scaled governance honest? (checks and balances in federations, transparency, resource allocation without predation, term limits, rotation)

Out of scope: national and international governance theory, comparative constitutional law, and macro political science not applicable to 150–500 person federations.

### Expected Word Count

50,000–60,000 words across 11–13 sections. This is the largest candidate — governance literature is extensive and federation-scale case studies require detailed treatment. No pre-research chapter exists in the phase-6/ directory.

### Source Library Readiness: 65–70%

This is the lowest source readiness of the three candidates, and that fact matters for production planning. Core sources are solid: Elinor Ostrom's *Governing the Commons* (foundational, freely available), Vincent Ostrom's polycentric governance work, Swiss cantonal system documentation, post-disaster governance case studies from Christchurch and Puerto Rico, UNDRR disaster governance frameworks, Midwest regional governance examples (soil-water conservation districts, agricultural cooperative federations, county governance). These cover approximately 40–50% of what the full document needs.

The gaps are real: detailed case studies of governance failure in resource-constrained settings are underreported in academic literature (success stories get published; failures are suppressed). Consensus decision-making at 300+ person scale has limited documented examples — most are below 200 people. Institutional learning mechanisms in small community settings are frontier research without a single authoritative synthesis.

Estimated additional sources needed: 90–130 (to reach the 140–180 citation target). Research overhead is higher than the other two candidates; a meaningful portion of Week 1 must be dedicated to source discovery.

### Estimated Production Hours

**28–35 hours** for full production (lowest source readiness at 65–70%, no pre-research chapter, largest expected word count, highest scope management overhead).

This is the most demanding candidate. Scope management is active, not passive — the governance and institutional change literature is vast and can pull research in directions that don't serve the 150–500 person federation focus. An author with political science or public administration background cuts approximately 5–8 hours off this estimate by arriving with source familiarity.

Timeline: June 1 decision → June 5–15 extended source library compilation → June 15–22 production begins (later start than other candidates) → August 9 draft complete → August 30 final. Confidence: 78% on schedule solo; higher with tight scope management and/or co-authoring.

### Cross-Domain Bridges

**To Phase 5:**
- Phase 5 Wave 2 Community Implementation Playbook: that document covers governance structures for roughly stable community sizes. This domain extends it to the scaling and constitutional questions — what breaks at 50, 100, 200 people.
- Phase 5 Wave 2 Conflict Resolution Framework: conflict resolution produces outcomes; institutional learning from those outcomes is what this domain covers. The two documents form a cycle: conflicts reveal governance gaps; governance adaptation prevents recurrence.
- Phase 5 Phase 3 (Community Infrastructure): Phase 3 identified legal structure and formal governance as a load-bearing bridge. This domain builds the actual content of that bridge.
- Phase 4 Power Mapping: that document identifies where power concentrates. This domain provides the accountability mechanisms that prevent unhealthy concentration — the structural complement to the diagnostic.

**To other Phase 6 candidates:**
- Community Economic Resilience: governance structures shape what economic models are feasible, and economic structures shape governance design. The two are tightly coupled and strongest when produced together. Specifically: how a federation allocates economic surplus determines whether member communities remain willing participants.
- Skills Development: decisions about what to teach and who gets to teach are governance questions. Educational governance is a subset of community governance design; the two domains should share conceptual framing on institutional decision-making.
- Phase 6 pre-research (Meshtastic): the communication infrastructure (Track B) enables governance coordination across geographic distances. The governance domain determines what that communication infrastructure is *used for* — what decisions require coalition-wide input, what information flows up versus down the federation hierarchy.

### Unique Contribution to the Resilience Framework

Phase 5 documents what a resilient community does. This domain documents how a resilient community governing body learns from what it does wrong. Without institutional learning mechanisms, communities repeat governance failures. With them, each governance failure becomes a documented lesson that reduces the probability of recurrence.

The domain also addresses the most common failure mode of successful intentional communities: governance structures designed for 20 people that become illegitimate or dysfunctional when the community reaches 100. The Ostrom framework, sociocracy, and federation models collectively address this failure mode with documented design principles. This domain synthesizes those principles and translates them to Zone 5 community federation contexts.

The Midwest governance context is particularly rich: agricultural cooperative federations (Land O'Lakes, Organic Valley, Cenex), rural water authorities, county-level soil and water conservation districts, and township governance all operate as multi-stakeholder federations in Zone 5. They are living case studies, not theoretical constructs.

### Key Findings from Pre-Research

No pre-research chapter exists; findings are derived from the candidate outline and Phase 6 scope assessment:

- The PHASE_6_SCOPE_AND_READINESS.md analysis rates Governance Scaling as the **highest-urgency** candidate (score 4/5 vs. 3/5 for Economic and 2/5 for Skills) based on the observation that the 2025–2026 institutional governance window is open. Post-COVID governance failures in community organizations created documented demand for better scaling frameworks.
- Governance scaling is also the **highest-leverage** candidate for Phase 6 as a whole: economic systems, skill transmission, and mutual aid network architecture all depend on functioning governance. A community that cannot make decisions at scale cannot implement any of the other Phase 6 domains effectively.
- The Swiss cantonal system's 700-year operating history provides the longest-running empirical validation of polycentric governance — multiple autonomous units coordinating on shared challenges without a central authority. The design principles are documented and transferable.
- The primary documented failure mode in community governance scaling is "founder syndrome": governance authority that concentrates in founding members who resist structural adaptation as the community grows. Term limits, role rotation, and explicit succession protocols are the three most evidence-supported preventive mechanisms.
- Governance Scaling has the highest co-authoring benefit of the three candidates: the domain is large enough (50,000–60,000 words) and the scope risk is high enough that two authors with complementary backgrounds (one political science, one community organizing) produce better outcomes than one author alone.
