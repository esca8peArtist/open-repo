## Part 1: The Five Evaluation Criteria

These five criteria are designed to surface the trade-offs between candidates rather than produce a mechanical answer. Score each criterion 1–5 using the definitions below.

---

### Criterion 1: Urgency

*How time-sensitive is this domain? Does starting in June 2026 versus deferring to September 2026 meaningfully change the value delivered?*

| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 1 | Evergreen — can defer 6+ months without significant cost; no time-limited windows |
| 2 | Low urgency — deferral costs something but not much; interest may cool slightly |
| 3 | Moderate urgency — June start is better than September; some momentum or window at stake |
| 4 | High urgency — meaningful window or opportunity cost from deferral; institutional or policy timing matters |
| 5 | Must-start-June — deferral closes a specific opportunity or leaves a critical gap in a time-sensitive community need |

**What creates urgency in Phase 6:**
- The USDA Rural Cooperative Development Grant program (H.R. 1951) is reauthorized through FY2029 with current application cycles opening in fall 2026. Communities that have the economic resilience framework in hand by August 30 can apply in the fall 2026 cycle.
- The 2025–2026 post-COVID governance failure examination window is active in the cooperative and resilience community network; research in 2026 speaks to documented current events.
- Skills Development is the most evergreen candidate — the literature is stable and non-time-sensitive.

### Criterion 3: Leverage and Cross-Domain Multiplier

*How much does this domain amplify the value of Phase 1–5 and unlock Phase 7+ work? Does it enable other Phase 6 domains?*

| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 1 | Standalone value — adds independently useful content but does not meaningfully enable other domains |
| 2 | Moderate multiplier — some connection to adjacent domains but not foundational |
| 3 | Good multiplier — clearly enables or deepens at least 2–3 other domains |
| 4 | High multiplier — foundational for Phase 7 operationalization and bridges multiple Phase 5 domains |
| 5 | Maximum multiplier — all other Phase 6 domains build on or connect to this; without it, other domains are weaker |

**Leverage analysis:**
- Community Economic Resilience: directly extends all Phase 5 domains (every production domain needs exchange infrastructure) and feeds directly into Phase 7 operationalization templates. The pre-research chapter already integrates with Phase 5 Wave 2 and Wave 3.
- Skills Development: highest long-term leverage — without skill transmission, all Phase 5 knowledge evaporates in one generation. The multiplier operates on a 5–20 year timescale rather than immediately.
- Governance Scaling: foundational for other Phase 6 candidates — economic and skills systems both depend on functioning governance at scale. The highest urgency score in PHASE_6_SCOPE_AND_READINESS.md assessment reflects this.

---

### Criterion 4: Scope Fit and Clarity

*How tightly bounded is the research scope? What is the risk of expansion, mission creep, or boundary ambiguity?*

| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 1 | Very broad — scope ambiguity is high; many directions the research could go; requires constant boundary management |
| 2 | Moderately broad — meaningful expansion risk; scope needs active management |
| 3 | Acceptable — some expansion risk but manageable; three core questions are defined |
| 4 | Tight — research questions are clear; scope expansion would be visible and easy to catch |
| 5 | Very tight — scope is unambiguous; hard to accidentally expand; the three questions are self-limiting |

**Scope analysis:**
- Community Economic Resilience: three questions are tightly bounded. The out-of-scope list (national monetary policy, corporate finance, ancient history) is easy to apply. The main expansion risk is including too many international case studies; a soft ceiling of 4–6 cases contains this.
- Skills Development: moderate scope risk. Educational research is a vast literature; the domain can easily expand into K-12 curriculum design, educational neuroscience, or technology-mediated learning — none of which serve the core question. Requires an explicit ceiling of 12–15 critical skills.
- Governance Scaling: highest scope risk. Political science and institutional change theory are among the most expansive bodies of academic literature. The "150–500 person federation" boundary is clear in principle but requires active enforcement; macro-governance theory can creep in through case studies that are too large-scale.

### Criterion 5: User Interest Signals and Market Demand

*How strong is the evidence that communities in Zone 5 actually need this guidance? How applicable is it to the Midwest audience?*

| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 1 | Niche interest — demand is real but limited; Midwest applicability requires significant extrapolation |
| 2 | Moderate demand — some clear signals but not broadly distributed; most relevant to specific community types |
| 3 | Good demand — clear audience; Midwest context is addressable with Zone 5-specific translation |
| 4 | High demand — multiple clear signals; Zone 5 applicability is direct and strong |
| 5 | Very high demand — community organizations are actively requesting this; Midwest examples are primary, not supplementary |

**Demand analysis:**
- Community Economic Resilience: Midwest cooperative tradition is deep and active (agricultural cooperatives, food cooperatives, credit unions). USDA extension offices are actively distributing local food system economics guides. Post-pandemic interest in timebanking and alternative currencies is documented. Zone 5 examples are primary sources, not illustrations.
- Skills Development: 4-H operates 6.8 million youth participants in Zone 5 states; cooperative extension serves adult farmers through a tested pedagogical tradition. COVID-19 education disruptions created visible demand for alternative skill-development models. Midwest examples (4-H, extension, agricultural apprenticeship) are primary, not illustrative.
- Governance Scaling: Midwest has extensive cooperative governance experience (Land O'Lakes, Organic Valley, rural water authorities, soil conservation districts). Growing interest in multi-entity resilience coordination at county level. Zone 5 governance examples are primary sources.
