## 1. The Callais Decision: What It Actually Changed

Louisiana v. Callais, 24-109, was decided 6-3 on April 29, 2026, with Justice Alito writing for the majority. The decision's significance lies not in a single holding but in three changes to the forty-year Thornburg v. Gingles framework that collectively dismantled the enforcement architecture of Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act.

**Change 1: Intentional discrimination required as floor, not ceiling.** Gingles (1986) established that Section 2 prohibits voting practices that result in denial or abridgement of the right to vote on account of race — a results standard that allowed plaintiffs to prevail without proving discriminatory intent. Callais rewrote this. Justice Alito's majority opinion returned Section 2 "to a mere restatement of the constitutional prohibition on purposeful vote dilution" (Harvard Kennedy School analysis, May 2026). Proving intent in redistricting — where mapmakers routinely claim partisan rather than racial motivation — sets a near-impossible evidentiary bar.

**Change 2: Heightened plaintiff burden at all three Gingles preconditions.** The Gingles three-part test required plaintiffs to show: (1) the minority group is sufficiently large and geographically compact to form a majority in a single-member district; (2) the minority group is politically cohesive; (3) the majority votes as a bloc to defeat minority-preferred candidates. Callais heightened the evidentiary showing required at each precondition. The DeSoto County federal ruling of June 24 — applied to local redistricting for the first time — shows how the heightened standard operates in practice: the court found "plaintiffs failed all three preconditions of the Gingles test" under the new Callais standard, despite facts that would have supported a Section 2 claim under the pre-Callais framework (NAACP LDF press release, June 2026; redistrictingonline.org, June 25, 2026).

**Change 3: Compliance with Section 2 recharacterized as unconstitutional racial gerrymander.** In a move Professor Pamela Karlan (Stanford Law) characterizes as Alito "killing the Voting Rights Act" (Stanford Law blog, June 11, 2026), the Court held that Louisiana's creation of a second majority-Black district — done specifically to comply with Section 2 after years of litigation — was itself an unconstitutional racial gerrymander. The logical result: states that respond to Section 2 litigation by creating majority-minority districts face constitutional challenge, while states that ignore Section 2 now face near-impossible plaintiff burdens. There is no compliant path.

The Harvard Election Law Clinic's analysis (May 2026) goes further: Callais effectively overruled the Supreme Court's 2023 Allen v. Milligan holding despite Justice Alito's explicit claim otherwise — a point with implications for any remaining litigation under the pre-Callais framework.

**State-level redistricting cascade (April-June 2026)**: In the weeks after Callais, Tennessee Republicans drew and passed a new congressional map eliminating the sole majority-minority House district. Georgia opened a special redistricting session. Mississippi's governor called a legislative special session for judicial redistricting. Alabama was permitted to implement a discriminatory 2026 election map, notwithstanding a lower court finding of intentional discrimination. SCOTUSblog's June 2026 constitutional analysis characterizes Callais as having a "blast radius" extending beyond redistricting into all forms of voting rights litigation.

**Practical implication for Phase 3 research**: The research outline's Zone 1, Question 5 (Callais practical effect on voting rights litigation) has moved from a question requiring research to one requiring updated documentation of a cascade already in motion as of June 2026. The research window opens five months after the decision; by November 4, an additional cycle of post-Callais redistricting and litigation will have occurred.

## 2. Voter Purge Rate: Statistical Baseline and Post-April 2026 Trend

**The 19 million baseline**: The U.S. Election Assistance Commission documented 19.1 million voter registrations purged between 2020 and 2022 — a 21 percent increase from the 2014-2016 cycle. The Brennan Center's analysis of 6,600 jurisdictions found the median purge rate was 40 percent higher in counties formerly covered by the Voting Rights Act's preclearance requirement (before Shelby County v. Holder, 2013) than in other jurisdictions.

**Post-Callais purge trend (April-June 2026)**: The EAC's next comprehensive purge dataset (for the 2024-2026 cycle) will not be released until approximately June 2027, so precise post-Callais purge rate data does not yet exist in published form. However, four documented developments signal that the 40 percent formerly-covered-jurisdiction premium is likely widening rather than stabilizing:

1. **Ohio SB 293** (effective 2026): Directs systematic voter purges on a monthly schedule, including in the 90-day window before federal elections — a direct violation of the National Voter Registration Act's 90-day quiet period. The Campaign Legal Center and ACLU filed suit in February 2026. As of June 2026, the case is in federal district court (CLC, February 2026).

2. **Texas SAVE system purges** (ongoing): Texas is using the federal SAVE (Systematic Alien Verification of Entitlements) system for voter roll purges, but the SAVE system uses citizenship data that may be years old and cannot reflect naturalizations since database entry. CLC filed suit in March 2026 (CLC, March 2026).

3. **DOJ national voter database initiative** (April-June 2026): The Trump Department of Justice demanded unredacted voter files from all 50 states and D.C. — including names, addresses, birthdates, driver's license numbers, and partial Social Security numbers — with the stated intent of building a centralized database and then instructing states to remove voters from the rolls within 45 days of receiving DOJ lists (Brennan Center, June 2026). As of late June 2026, 12 states voluntarily complied. Six federal courts dismissed the DOJ's suits to compel handover. On June 24, the Sixth Circuit became the first appellate court to affirm dismissal (Michigan case). The initiative represents a qualitatively new purge mechanism: federal-level rather than state-level, using data quality methods the Brennan Center has documented as producing false positives disproportionately affecting naturalized citizens (Brennan Center, "Watch Out for False Voter Fraud Claims Fueled by SAVE Program," 2026).

4. **State SAVE Act equivalents accelerating** (2026): As of May 2026, South Dakota, Utah, Kentucky, Mississippi, and Florida enacted citizenship documentation requirements for voter registration or roll maintenance. Five states now require documents (passport, birth certificate) that 21 million eligible citizens lack ready access to — the same vulnerability the federal SAVE Act would have created had it passed the Senate (Brennan Center May 2026 Roundup).

**Assessment of >40% purge increase in formerly-VRA jurisdictions**: No published peer-reviewed data confirms a specific post-Callais purge rate increase in formerly covered jurisdictions as of June 2026. The existing Brennan Center data establishes the 40 percent premium as a pre-Callais baseline (measured from 2013-2018 data). The structural conditions for acceleration — loss of Section 2 litigation threat, DOJ database initiative targeting primarily Southern states, state SAVE equivalents concentrated in formerly covered jurisdictions — are present. The EAC's 2026 EAVS report (expected June 2027) will provide the first hard post-Callais purge rate data. Phase 3 researchers should treat the 40 percent formerly-covered premium as a floor, not ceiling, and monitor Brennan Center and Campaign Legal Center litigation trackers for case-level evidence of accelerating purges before that data is published.

## 3. SAVE Act Federal Status as of June 29, 2026

The SAVE Act (H.R. 22, 119th Congress) passed the House 218-213 on February 11, 2026, with one Democratic vote (Rep. Henry Cuellar). It required documentary proof of citizenship — passport, military ID, enhanced REAL ID, or a government photo ID plus a birth certificate or naturalization certificate — to register to vote in federal elections.

The Senate voted on the SAVE Act in March 2026. The bill required 60 votes to overcome a filibuster. Republicans held 53 seats; seven Democratic votes did not materialize. Senator Lisa Murkowski (R-AK) voted against proceeding. The Senate failed to advance the bill (Ballotpedia News, March 25, 2026).

**Current federal status**: SAVE Act failed in Senate. It is not law. No vote is currently scheduled.

**Practical impact of federal failure**: The Senate defeat did not prevent the law's effects from appearing at the state level. As of June 2026, five states have enacted SAVE Act-equivalent policies independently. The Brennan Center ("States Already Enacting Harmful SAVE Act Policies," 2026) tracks this state-level proliferation. Kansas's prior implementation of documentary proof-of-citizenship prevented 31,000 eligible citizens — 12 percent of all applicants — from registering. That Kansas precedent is the primary empirical predictor for the impact of the five newly enacted state equivalents.

**Voting-related provisions distinct from immigration context**: The SAVE Act is purely a voting rights measure — it amends the National Voter Registration Act of 1993 and pertains exclusively to voter registration for federal elections. It has no immigration enforcement provisions. The confusion between "SAVE Act" (voting) and "SAVE system" (Systematic Alien Verification of Entitlements, an immigration status database) is a source of public and media confusion that Phase 3 research should explicitly address.

## 6. Decision Framework: Phase 3 Research Priority

**The three options**: (a) Federal solutions — voter registration infrastructure, legislative restoration; (b) State-level litigation defense — challenging new purge mechanisms; (c) Voter-centric tools — validation guides, rapid-response networks.

**Assessment against current conditions**:

Option (a) — Federal solutions — is the correct long-term goal but the wrong near-term priority for Phase 3. The SAVE Act failed in the Senate, which demonstrates that federal legislative solutions require vote margins that do not currently exist. The Freedom to Vote Act and John Lewis Voting Rights Advancement Act have failed multiple Senate votes since 2021. Phase 3 research that focuses on federal legislative solutions will primarily document advocacy targets rather than actionable tools for the November 2026 window.

Option (b) — State-level litigation defense — is the field where the most active work is happening (ACLU, Campaign Legal Center, NAACP LDF, Democracy Docket). But litigation is a professional advocacy domain; Phase 3's target audience is civic organizations, communities, and individuals, not attorneys. Litigation defense is important context for understanding what legal mechanisms exist, but it should not be the primary frame.

Option (c) — Voter-centric tools — is the correct primary orientation for Phase 3, with state-level litigation defense as essential supporting context and federal restoration as long-term aspiration framing. The reason: the 2026-2028 window is defined by registration barriers, purge threats, and documentation requirements that confront individual voters and civic organizers right now. TurboVote's 79 percent registered-user turnout versus 64 percent nationally, the Canada Inspire Democracy network expansion from 293 to 900 organizations, same-day registration in 26 states — these are immediately actionable by civic organizations and communities without waiting for federal legislation or litigation victories. The most durable contribution Phase 3 can make is a practitioner guide that helps individuals and organizations navigate the current legal environment, know their rights under state VRAs (where applicable), use validated civic technology, and understand which state-level pathways remain open.

**Recommended framing for Phase 3**: Lead with voter-centric tools and state-level options. Embed litigation defense as context within each section — "what the law now prohibits" and "what is being challenged" — rather than as a separate zone. Frame federal restoration (the John Lewis Act, state VRA expansion) as the long-term policy direction that civic organizing in 2026-2028 is building toward, not as the immediate action available to readers.
