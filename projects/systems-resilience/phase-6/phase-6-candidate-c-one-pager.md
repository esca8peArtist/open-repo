# Candidate C: Intergenerational Skills Development and Knowledge Transfer

---

## Research Scope Summary

Skills Development and Knowledge Transfer documents how communities preserve and transmit practical competency across time — when formal educational institutions, apprenticeship systems, and professional certification no longer function. It answers: how does hard-won technical knowledge not evaporate in one generation?

This domain is distinct from Phase 5's educational governance document (which covers schooling structures). This domain covers adult skill development, the mechanics of apprenticeship, knowledge preservation when experts age or leave, and the community systems that ensure critical skills are redundantly held. It treats skill transmission as survival-critical infrastructure, not educational philosophy.

The failure mode that motivates this domain is concrete: a community loses its water system operator to illness, its solar technician to relocation, or its experienced medic to age. If no one was trained to replace them, the entire system they maintained fails. The question is not whether this happens — it does, in every multi-generational organization — but whether the community has the knowledge infrastructure to survive it.

The domain answers three bounded questions:

1. What skills are critical for community resilience and what are their actual learning time requirements? (priority skill mapping from Phase 1–5 domains, learning curves for adult learners in low-resource contexts)
2. How do communities transmit complex technical skills without schools or credentialing institutions? (apprenticeship models, documentation strategies, competence validation, cultural transmission)
3. What institutions and governance structures support skill transmission at community scale? (learning cooperatives, skill-sharing networks, knowledge management, oral history, quality assurance)

Out of scope: comprehensive K-12 curriculum design, educational technology for children, national education policy, and neuroscience of learning at the laboratory level.

## Source Library Readiness: 70–75%

Core sources are accessible:

- Lave and Wenger, *Situated Learning: Legitimate Peripheral Participation* (foundational, peer-reviewed, widely available)
- Kolb, *Experiential Learning* (foundational adult education literature, library access)
- UNESCO reports on non-formal education in low-resource contexts (un.org, free)
- USDA 4-H and Cooperative Extension Service documentation (land-grant university archives, free)
- Cuba post-Soviet "Special Period" education adaptation case studies (academic journals, documented)
- Puerto Rico hurricane recovery learning reports (FEMA + academic sources, available)
- Amish knowledge transmission research (ethnographic literature, university library access)

These sources cover apprenticeship models, adult learning theory, and the institutional layer. They represent roughly 70–75% of what the full document needs.

**Known gaps**: Quantitative learning curve data for specific trades (well-drilling, solar installation, medical response) in low-resource settings is mostly practitioner reports rather than peer-reviewed studies. Intergenerational teaching dynamics in North American contexts are underresearched compared to European apprenticeship systems. These are refinement gaps — the domain can proceed with practitioner evidence documented explicitly as such.

## Unique Contribution to the Resilience Framework

Phase 1 through 5 documents procedures: how to filter water, grow food, organize communities, and resolve conflicts. It assumes the reader — or someone in their community — already has or can acquire the relevant skills. That assumption becomes increasingly fragile over time. Skills concentrated in one or two people evaporate when those people leave, age, or die.

This domain is the bridge between documentation and sustained capability. It answers: how does a community ensure that 10 years from now, the person who knows how to maintain the microgrid has trained two successors? How does it ensure that skill is embedded in the community's social structure rather than locked in someone's head? The resilience framework is operationally complete; this domain makes it temporally durable.

The Midwest has existing infrastructure to build on. 4-H has operated for 120 years as a practical skill-development system for youth in agricultural communities. Extension services have taught adult farmers for a century using demonstrated, field-tested pedagogical methods. This domain synthesizes what works from that tradition and translates it to the resilience context — leveraging infrastructure that already exists rather than inventing a new system from scratch.

**Learning curve estimates** (from candidate outline assessment): basic food preservation requires 4–8 hours of supervised practice to reach competence; small engine repair requires 40–80 hours; solar microgrid commissioning requires 60–120 hours. These estimates define which skills communities can realistically develop before crisis and which require multi-year apprenticeship investment — shaping the implementation planning that Phase 5 assumes but does not provide.

## Strategic Timing Rationale

Domain C's case for a June 1 start rests on long-horizon leverage rather than immediate policy windows:

- **Highest long-term leverage in the Phase 6 set**: without skill transmission infrastructure, all Phase 5 knowledge evaporates in one generation. A community can have every Phase 5 document but lose its water operator in year seven. This domain is the durability layer for the entire corpus.
- **4-H and Extension seasonal alignment**: Zone 5 extension programming follows an agricultural calendar. Research and implementation planning done June–August maps to fall programming cycles, when adult learners are most available for structured skill-building after the main growing season.
- **Cuba and Puerto Rico case study contemporaneity**: the COVID-19 pandemic (2020–2023) generated a new wave of documentation on rapid adult skill adaptation under crisis conditions, much of it peer-reviewed by 2025–2026. The research library is richer now than it was three years ago.
- **Evergreen but not urgency-free**: Domain C is the most temporally stable of the three candidates — no specific policy window closes if it is deferred to September. However, its leverage multiplier on Phase 5 is highest, and communities that begin implementing skills development programs in fall 2026 will see measurable returns by 2027–2028. Earlier start creates earlier compounding.

If sequencing two domains, Domain C pairs cleanly with either Domain A (economic systems define which skills have exchange value) or Domain D (governance structures determine how skill transmission is organized). The interaction overhead with either pairing is low.
