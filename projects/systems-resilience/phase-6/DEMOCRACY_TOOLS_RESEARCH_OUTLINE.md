# Democracy Tools — Phase 6 Domain G Research Outline

**Lead finding**: The voter participation infrastructure in the United States has entered a period of acute structural stress. The Supreme Court's April 29, 2026 decision in Louisiana v. Callais (6-3) weakened Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act to the point of functional inoperability for vote dilution claims. The SAVE America Act, requiring documentary proof of citizenship from an estimated 21 million eligible voters who lack ready access to it, passed the House in February 2026. Between 2020 and 2022, more than 19 million voter registrations were purged — a 21 percent increase from the 2014-16 cycle. Against this backdrop, civic technology tools (TurboVote reached 79% turnout among registered users in 2024 vs. 64% nationally), international models (Estonia's i-Voting system, Taiwan's vTaiwan platform, Canada's Inspire Democracy network expansion from 293 to 900 organizations for the April 2025 election), and grassroots ballot initiative infrastructure (Ballot Initiative Strategy Center tracked 150+ anti-ballot-measure bills in 2025 alone) offer concrete, documented alternatives. This research outline maps the four zones that together constitute the Democracy Tools domain.

---

## Domain Scope

**One-sentence scope**: How do individuals, communities, and civic organizations navigate voter registration barriers, exercise ballot guidance rights, and deploy civic action infrastructure in the current federal and state legal environment — and what international models demonstrate what is achievable?

**What this domain adds to Phase 3-5**: Phase 5 documents community resilience in food, water, energy, governance, and psychological support. It does not address the political infrastructure through which communities influence the laws and policies that govern those systems. Democracy Tools is the Phase 6 domain that addresses civic participation as infrastructure — the mechanisms through which communities exercise collective power within and alongside state political systems.

**What is out of scope**: national electoral reform (structural reform to the Electoral College, campaign finance systems, or congressional term limits) is not covered. Federal legislative history prior to 2010 is background only. Electoral fraud prevention and election administration from the administrator's perspective are out of scope — this domain is written for participants (voters, organizers, civic technologists), not administrators.

## Research Zone 1: Current State of Voter Registration Barriers

**Zone definition**: The structural, legal, and administrative barriers preventing eligible citizens from registering to vote or having their registration maintained — with special attention to developments since 2020 and the post-Callais environment.

**Research questions for Zone 1:**

1. What is the empirical record of same-day voter registration (SDR) adoption and impact? As of March 2026, 26 states and D.C. have some form of SDR. What do registration rate improvements and turnout impacts look like across the states that adopted SDR after 2018 (Delaware, Minnesota, Pennsylvania in 2023)? What are the documented barriers to SDR adoption in the remaining 24 states?

2. What is the current state of automatic voter registration (AVR)? As of November 2025, 24 states and D.C. have enacted AVR. The Center for American Progress projects that universal AVR adoption would register more than 22 million new voters in the first year. What does the state-by-state evidence show about AVR's impact on registration rates, with particular attention to Georgia (active registration increased from 78% to 98% of eligible citizens in the first four years of AVR)?

3. What is the documented impact of the SAVE America Act's proof-of-citizenship requirement? The Act passed the House in February 2026 and failed in the Senate as of June 2026, but at least one state has enacted equivalent policies (Brennan Center, "States Already Enacting Harmful SAVE Act Policies," 2026). Kansas's implementation of a documentary proof-of-citizenship requirement prevented 31,000 eligible citizens (12% of all applicants) from registering. What does the Kansas experience predict for states enacting SAVE Act equivalents?

4. What is the current scope and rate of voter roll purges? The U.S. Election Assistance Commission documented over 19 million voter registrations purged between 2020-2022 — a 21% increase from the 2014-16 cycle. The Brennan Center's analysis of 6,600 jurisdictions found the median purge rate rose from 6.2% to 7.8% since 2008. In counties previously covered by the Voting Rights Act's preclearance requirement (before Shelby County v. Holder in 2013), the median purge rate is 40% higher than in other jurisdictions. What are the legal mechanisms for challenging erroneous purges, and what is their documented success rate?

5. What is the Callais decision's practical effect on voting rights litigation? Louisiana v. Callais (April 29, 2026) reworked the 40-year-old Thornburg v. Gingles framework in three ways that collectively make Section 2 vote dilution claims significantly harder to win. State VRAs have been enacted in California (2002), Washington (2018), Oregon (2019), Virginia (2021), New York (2022), Connecticut (2023), Minnesota (2024), Colorado (2025), and Maryland (2026). What protection do state VRAs provide in the post-Callais landscape, and what is the Zone 5 state-by-state status?

## Research Zone 2: Technology Solutions

**Zone definition**: Civic technology tools for voter registration, ballot guidance, voter protection, and civic action — emphasizing tools with documented impact data rather than platforms in early development.

**Research questions for Zone 2:**

6. What is the documented impact of TurboVote and comparable voter registration technology platforms? Democracy Works' TurboVote achieved 79% turnout among registered users in 2024, outperforming the national registered voter turnout rate of 64% by 15 percentage points. Among users under 25, TurboVote achieved 72% turnout versus 56% nationally. First-time TurboVote voters turned out at 86% versus 57% nationally. What accounts for the turnout premium, and is it attributable to the technology or selection effects?

7. What ballot guidance technology tools exist and what are their documented effectiveness rates? The U.S. Vote Foundation provides civic data products and APIs used by third-party ballot guidance developers. Rock the Vote offers Online Voter Registration, Voter Registration Status Lookup, and Absentee Ballot Request tools to partner organizations. What is the evidence on whether ballot guidance technology increases informed participation versus serving only already-engaged voters?

8. What is the status and security architecture of open-source election technology? VoteSecure (Bradley Tusk Foundation) and ElectionGuard (Microsoft, open-source) represent two different approaches to election security technology. ElectionGuard creates an encrypted record allowing voters to confirm their ballot was counted. What are the adoption barriers, security review processes, and deployment prerequisites for community-facing election security tools?

9. What is the evidence on AI-assisted civic engagement? Democracy Works is examining how misinformation and AI-generated content are shaping the election information environment ahead of the 2026 midterms. vTaiwan has deployed AI for transcript recording, opinion clustering, and analysis during deliberation processes. What is the documented evidence on AI tools that support (rather than undermine) informed civic participation?

10. What voter protection technology infrastructure exists for the 2026 midterms? Protect The Vote 2026 (No Small Act) is building a 500,000-volunteer election protection program across swing states. The ACLU committed $24.5 million to voter protection for 2026. What technology platforms support volunteer coordination, voter incident reporting, and real-time election protection?

## Research Zone 3: International Models

**Zone definition**: Documented international examples of voter registration reform, digital civic participation, and civic action infrastructure — with specific attention to transferability and what Zone 5 communities can adapt.

**Research questions for Zone 3:**

11. What does Estonia's i-Voting system demonstrate about the conditions necessary for secure digital participation? Estonia has offered nationwide internet voting since 2005. After twenty years of practice, the system emphasizes that "digitalisation of democracy is not only a matter of technology or access, but of values, continuity, and the kind of future we are willing to design collectively." The Standing Committee on Cybersecurity identified risk scenarios in October 2024 including auditability and misinformation concerns. The Free Software Foundation Europe has criticized partial source code publication. What is the honest assessment of what Estonia's model proves — and what it does not prove — for U.S. civic contexts?

12. What has Taiwan's vTaiwan platform demonstrated about AI-assisted digital deliberation? vTaiwan emerged after the Sunflower Movement in 2014 as a decentralized consultation process. It has facilitated over 20 legislative reviews. In 2019, vTaiwan hosted online deliberation leading to regulatory adjustments for Uber in Taiwan, gathering input from over 4,000 participants and visualizing consensus across stakeholder groups. The platform deployed Pol.is (opinion mapping) followed by AI-assisted deliberation. Since 2024, vTaiwan operates without direct government support as a volunteer-driven civic laboratory. What do its successes and limitations teach about digitally-mediated civic participation?

13. What does Canada's Inspire Democracy program demonstrate about civic engagement infrastructure at scale? For Canada's 45th general election (April 28, 2025), the Inspire Democracy program expanded its contracted organization network from 27 to 40 organizations (48% increase), while the overall network of contracted and non-contracted stakeholders grew from 293 to approximately 900 organizations. What organizational models, outreach methodologies, and measurement frameworks does the Inspire Democracy system use — and which elements are adaptable to U.S. contexts?

14. What do comparative international voter registration systems reveal about best practices? Countries with automatic enrollment (most European democracies, Canada, Australia) consistently achieve registration rates above 90%. The U.S. active registration system produces registration rates of approximately 87% of citizen voting-age population (2024 EAVS data), but this masks significant disparities by age, income, and race. What specific design choices in international registration systems produce superior equity outcomes, and which require national legislation versus administrative action versus civic technology?

## Research Zone 4: Movement Infrastructure

**Zone definition**: The organizations, coalitions, toolkits, and activation sequences that constitute the U.S. democracy movement — with specific attention to Zone 5 (Iowa, Minnesota, Wisconsin, Illinois, Missouri) infrastructure and November 2026-November 2028 activation opportunities.

**Research questions for Zone 4:**

15. What is the current state of the voter registration movement's organizational infrastructure? Key organizations include: Brennan Center for Justice (constitutional lawyer-advocates; monthly voting law roundups), League of Women Voters ("Unite & Rise 8.5" initiative targeting 8.5 million individuals), ACLU ($24.5 million 2026 voter protection commitment), NAACP Legal Defense Fund (Voting Rights 2026 campaign: voting.naacpldf.org), Movement Voter Project (tech platform access for voter registration and voter contact), Democracy Works (TurboVote; Elections API reaching millions of voters), Ballot Initiative Strategy Center (tracking 150+ anti-ballot-measure bills in 2025), and Protect The Vote 2026 (500,000-volunteer election protection). How do these organizations coordinate, and what is the current organizational capacity gap?

16. What ballot initiative and direct democracy infrastructure exists at the state level in Zone 5? Ballot initiative signature thresholds for 2026 state-wide measures range from 15,582 (North Dakota proposed statute) to 880,062 (Florida constitutional amendment). What are the specific thresholds, deadlines, and qualification processes in Iowa, Minnesota, Wisconsin, Illinois, and Missouri — and which Zone 5 states have the most accessible direct democracy pathways for civic action?

17. What are the activation triggers for 2027-2028 civic action organizing? The 2026 midterm results (November 3, 2026) will reshape state legislative maps, congressional districts, and governor's mansions that control 2028 presidential election administration. The post-Callais redistricting process will be largely complete by spring 2027. What are the critical decision points at which civic organizing can most effectively intervene in the 2026-2028 cycle?

18. What does the civic action toolkit literature show about organizational best practices? Beyond the Ballot's 50x2026 campaign aims to elevate civics education requirements in all 50 states. The Ballot Initiative Strategy Center's Defend Direct Democracy campaign responds to 150+ anti-ballot-measure bills. Muster's "Grassroots Advocacy 101: A Practical Guide for 2026" offers a practitioner framework. What does the evidence show about which civic action approaches have demonstrated measurable policy impact versus which primarily serve organizational development?
