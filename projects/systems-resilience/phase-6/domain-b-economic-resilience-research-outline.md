# Phase 6 Domain B: Community Economic Resilience
## Research Outline — Production-Ready for June 5 Activation

**Lead finding**: Phases 1–5 of the systems-resilience corpus documents how communities produce food, purify water, generate power, deliver healthcare, and organize governance. None of it answers the question that emerges the moment specialized production begins: how does the farmer compensate the electrician? How does the medic get food if she spends all day on patient care? Community Economic Resilience is the coordination layer between production and distribution — the economic operating system that makes specialized labor sustainable and prevents the breakdown of exchange that has historically ended otherwise-viable communities more reliably than resource shortages. This domain fills that gap.

**Scope in one sentence**: How communities trade goods, store value, allocate capital, and coordinate economic activity when conventional money, banks, and supply chains fail — with specific implementation guidance for Zone 5 Midwest communities at 20–500 person scale.

## Section 1: The Exchange Problem — Why Production Without Distribution Fails

**Target: 600–800 words**

### Core Question
What is the failure mechanism that breaks communities which have solved their production problems but not their exchange problems?

### Causal Pathway
Phase 1–5 communities solve the production question (food, water, power, healthcare). The exchange question emerges within the first season: how does surplus food reach the person who produced surplus energy? How does the medic receive food without spending hours growing it? Without a formal exchange mechanism, communities default to one of three informal arrangements — interpersonal debt, barter at transaction cost, or informal redistribution governed by perceived fairness. All three degrade social cohesion over time under resource scarcity, as perceived inequities accumulate without a legitimate adjudication mechanism.

Historical evidence is unambiguous: documented communities that survived resource crises collapsed on political and economic grounds (Argentina 2001, Soviet bloc rural communities 1991–1993, post-Maria Puerto Rico 2017–2018). The triggering event in most documented cases is not resource exhaustion but breakdown in the exchange system that allowed surplus to reach deficit.

### Key Questions
- What are the documented failure modes of informal barter in communities with specialized labor?
- At what community size does informal redistribution become unworkable, and what evidence supports that threshold?
- What does the anthropological record show about the historical function of money as a coordination mechanism (Graeber's debt-precedes-money thesis)?

### Evidence Checkpoints
- David Graeber, *Debt: The First 5,000 Years* (2011): Graeber's anthropological evidence that debt, not barter, is the historically prior form of exchange — with implications for how communities design exchange systems
- Argentina 2001 trueque network documentation: 2.5–7 million participants at peak; documenting both the coordination success and the governance failure modes that eventually collapsed the network
- Puerto Rico post-Maria informal exchange documentation: Centro for Puerto Rican Studies (CUNY) case studies

### Scope Boundaries
- In scope: exchange failure as a community resilience problem; historical and anthropological evidence on exchange system design
- Out of scope: macroeconomic theory; Federal Reserve policy; inflation mechanics at national scale

### Cross-Domain Bridges
- Phase 3 governance: the governance structures in Phase 3 provide the legitimacy layer that makes economic allocation decisions binding. Economic systems without governance authority fail because no one can enforce exchange rules.
- Phase 5 Wave 2 community implementation playbook: the playbook documents community role structures; this section adds the economic coordination layer that compensates those roles.

## Section 2: What Money Actually Does — Design Principles for Community Currency

**Target: 700–900 words**

### Core Question
What functions does a currency perform, and which of those functions can a community currency realistically replicate at 20–500 person scale?

### Causal Pathway
Classical economics defines money by three functions: medium of exchange, unit of account, and store of value. Community currencies typically succeed at the first (medium of exchange) and the second (unit of account), but fail at the third (store of value) because they lack the institutional backing and scale required to make the community currency worth holding rather than spending. Understanding this design constraint prevents communities from promising features their currency cannot deliver.

The WIR franc (Switzerland) has operated since 1934 as a mutual credit system for small businesses — 50,000+ SME participants, CHF 1.43B+ annual transaction volume. WIR is countercyclical: transaction volume increases during Swiss economic downturns, functioning as an automatic stabilizer. It succeeds because it targets the medium-of-exchange function only, does not compete with the Swiss franc as a store of value, and is designed for B2B (business-to-business) exchange rather than retail consumer exchange.

Community design choices:
1. **Backed vs. mutual credit**: Is the currency backed by a reserve (food, hours, gold) or issued as mutual credit (each member can issue credit to another)?
2. **Demurrage**: Does holding the currency cost something over time? Demurrage currencies (like WIR) circulate faster and are less likely to be hoarded.
3. **Convertibility**: Can the community currency be exchanged for national currency? Convertibility increases trust but creates drainage risk (currency exits the community).

### Key Questions
- What is the minimum community size at which a local currency provides efficiency gains over barter?
- What design features differentiate WIR (70+ years operational) from the Bristol Pound (2012–2021, discontinued)?
- How do mutual credit systems handle default — when a member issues credit and then cannot provide the corresponding goods or services?

### Evidence Checkpoints
- WIR Bank annual reports and academic analyses: Stodder, James. "Reciprocal Exchange Networks." (multiple publications documenting WIR countercyclicality)
- Bristol Pound evaluation report (2020): government-commissioned evaluation of what worked and what failed; freely available
- Lietaer, Bernard. *The Future of Money* (2001): design taxonomy for complementary currencies; foundational framework
- Seyfang, Gill. "Sustainable Consumption, the New Economics and Community Currencies." *Regional Studies* 40(7): 781–791, 2006.
- 2025 research: "For better or for worse: How Mutual Credit Systems bolster resilience of small firms." *Journal of Economic Behavior and Organization* 230 (2025) — newest peer-reviewed evidence on mutual credit resilience

### Scope Boundaries
- In scope: currency design principles; WIR, Bristol Pound, timebanking case studies; design choices and tradeoffs
- Out of scope: cryptocurrency and blockchain-based systems (relevant only as brief technical footnote); national monetary policy

## Section 3: Commons Management — Allocating Shared Resources Without Price Signals

**Target: 700–900 words**

### Core Question
When communities share resources (food stores, tools, land, labor) without market price signals, what allocation systems prevent depletion and maintain fairness legitimacy?

### Causal Pathway
The "tragedy of the commons" (Hardin 1968) predicted that shared resources would be inevitably depleted by self-interested individual actors. Elinor Ostrom's empirical research demolished this prediction: she documented hundreds of commons that self-governed successfully for centuries, deriving eight design principles that reliably distinguish successful commons from failed ones. Those principles are directly applicable to Zone 5 community resource allocation.

The eight Ostrom design principles:
1. Clearly defined boundaries
2. Rules congruent with local conditions
3. Collective-choice arrangements (those affected participate in rule-making)
4. Monitoring
5. Graduated sanctions
6. Conflict resolution mechanisms
7. Recognized rights to organize
8. Nested enterprises (for larger systems)

Community food stores, tool libraries, seed libraries, and shared manufacturing equipment all function as commons. Each requires explicit governance design using these principles — or they will fail.

### Key Questions
- Which of Ostrom's eight design principles are most commonly violated in documented commons failures?
- How do communities set allocation priorities when the commons is stressed (scarcity protocols)?
- What is the evidence on the role of monitoring (Principle 4) in preventing commons depletion? What monitoring mechanisms work at community scale without professional staff?

### Evidence Checkpoints
- Ostrom, Elinor. *Governing the Commons*. Cambridge University Press, 1990. (foundational — 8 design principles, 100+ case studies)
- Ostrom, Elinor. "Design Principles in Long-Enduring Irrigation Institutions." *American Political Science Review* 87(3): 677–678, 1993.
- Bollier, David. *Think Like a Commoner*. New Society Publishers, 2014. (accessible practitioner introduction)
- The Farmers Land Trust (Zone 5): "How the Farmland Commons integrates all Eight of Elinor Ostrom's Principles" — documented Zone 5 implementation

### Scope Boundaries
- In scope: commons design principles; food stores, tool libraries, land trusts as community commons; allocation protocols
- Out of scope: government-managed public goods; national fisheries management

### Cross-Domain Bridges
- Phase 6 Domain A (Community Platform Architecture): community platforms provide the coordination infrastructure for commons management — member databases, resource tracking, contribution records.

## Section 4: Cooperative Enterprise Models — Production at Community Scale

**Target: 700–900 words**

### Core Question
What enterprise structures allow communities to organize specialized production, distribute labor obligations, and share the proceeds of collective effort?

### Causal Pathway
Individual households producing for individual consumption is inefficient and does not build community economic interdependence. Cooperative enterprise — shared ownership and governance of productive resources and their outputs — is the mechanism by which community economic systems develop comparative advantage, specialize labor, and distribute the gains from specialization.

Three cooperative models are directly applicable to Zone 5 community scale:

**Worker cooperatives**: Worker-members own and govern the enterprise; labor is compensated by share of net surplus. The Mondragon federation (Basque Country, 70,000+ worker-owners, €11B+ annual revenue, founded 1956) is the most documented large-scale worker cooperative. In 2008 financial crisis, Mondragon transferred ~2,000 workers between cooperatives to avoid layoffs — governance and economic structure enabled crisis solidarity that market firms cannot provide.

**Agricultural cooperatives**: Producer cooperatives aggregate production and purchase inputs collectively. Zone 5 has a strong cooperative tradition: Land O'Lakes (dairy, founded 1921, $14B+ annual revenue), Organic Valley (organic dairy, founded 1988, 1,800+ farmer-members), Growmark (agronomy, founded 1927). These are not crisis institutions but demonstrate that cooperative enterprise is productive at large scale in Zone 5.

**Mutual aid organizations**: Non-market reciprocal exchange systems. COVID-19 (2020–2022) produced a documented surge in mutual aid networks in Zone 5 cities and rural areas — food distribution, skills exchange, childcare, transportation. These are the proto-economic structure from which more formal cooperative enterprises emerge.

### Key Questions
- What governance structures distinguish Mondragon's federational success from cooperative failures?
- At what scale does a community agricultural cooperative provide production efficiency gains over household production?
- How do Zone 5 agricultural cooperatives handle the conflict between large-member interests and small-member interests — what governance mechanisms prevent large-member capture?
- What is the documented track record of food cooperatives in economic downturns?

### Evidence Checkpoints
- Cheney, George. *Values at Work: Employee Participation Meets Market Pressure at Mondragon*. ILR Press, 1999.
- Whyte, William F. and Kathleen K. Whyte. *Making Mondragon*. ILR Press, 1991.
- ICA (International Cooperative Alliance) statistical report: global cooperative data including North American cooperative sector
- USDA National Agricultural Statistics Service: Midwest agricultural cooperative production and membership data
- NCBA CLUSA (National Cooperative Business Association): U.S. cooperative sector research and case studies

### Scope Boundaries
- In scope: worker cooperatives, producer cooperatives, food cooperatives, mutual aid organizations at community and regional scale; Zone 5 cooperative tradition
- Out of scope: investor-owned enterprises with cooperative features; producer cooperative commodity marketing at national scale

## Section 5: Capital Formation Without Banks — Cooperative Finance and Community Investment

**Target: 600–800 words**

### Core Question
How do communities raise and allocate capital for productive investment without access to conventional banking and credit systems?

### Causal Pathway
Conventional capital formation requires banks (lending), investors (equity), or governments (grants). In post-crisis contexts where banking systems are disrupted, communities need alternative capital formation mechanisms. Three documented mechanisms exist:

**Rotating savings and credit associations (ROSCAs)**: A group of members contributes a fixed sum each period; one member receives the full pool each period. No interest; no institution required. ROSCAs operate in virtually every culture globally and have operated for centuries without banking infrastructure. In Kenya, ROSCAs (chamas) manage an estimated $4B+ annually.

**Community Development Financial Institutions (CDFIs)**: Certified by U.S. Treasury; operate as mission-driven lenders in underserved communities. 78% of rural CDFIs reported increased demand in 2024. In Zone 5, credit unions (member-owned cooperative financial institutions) are the most accessible CDFI equivalent — Illinois has 300+ state-chartered credit unions; Iowa has 90+ credit unions.

**Community investment pools**: Communities pool capital to invest in productive infrastructure (processing equipment, cold storage, tool libraries) and recover investment through shared use fees. The tool library model (Chicago's Chicago Tool Library, Indianapolis Tool Library) demonstrates this principle at community scale.

### Key Questions
- What is the maximum ROSCA size at which informal trust mechanisms remain sufficient (before formal governance is required)?
- How do Zone 5 credit unions differ from conventional banks in resilience during economic downturns?
- What capital investments have the highest return for Zone 5 community resilience communities (processing infrastructure vs. transportation vs. energy vs. tools)?

### Evidence Checkpoints
- Bouman, F.J.A. "Rotating and Accumulating Savings and Credit Associations." *World Development* 23(3): 371–384, 1995.
- CDFI Coalition: "CDFIs: Driving Rural Development" — 2024 data on CDFI activity in rural Zone 5 states
- USDA Rural Development: Rural Cooperative Development Grant program — 2024 awardees documentation (NCBA CLUSA)
- Richmond Fed: "2025 CDFI Survey: Perspectives From Small-Town Mission-Driven Lenders" (2025)

### Scope Boundaries
- In scope: ROSCAs, credit unions, community investment pools, CDFIs in Zone 5 context
- Out of scope: securities markets, private equity, national-scale cooperative finance

## Section 6: Supply Chain Resilience — Regional Production and Trade Networks

**Target: 700–900 words**

### Core Question
What supply chain structures allow Zone 5 communities to meet their needs from regional production when national supply chains are disrupted?

### Causal Pathway
COVID-19 supply chain disruptions (2020–2022) revealed the fragility of just-in-time global supply chains: toilet paper shortages, PPE scarcity, and baby formula crises all stemmed from the same structural problem — production concentrated in distant locations with no regional backup. Zone 5 has the agricultural and manufacturing base to supply its own basic needs but lacks the regional distribution infrastructure to do so efficiently.

Three supply chain resilience mechanisms are documented:

**Regional food hubs**: Aggregation, distribution, and marketing organizations that connect local producers to regional buyers. USDA Regional Food System Partnerships awarded $4P Foods $1.6M in 2024 to build regional transportation infrastructure. Zone 5-specific: Illinois Farm Direct program; Indiana Farm Bureau regional market development.

**Nested supply chains**: Multi-tier supply chains where local production serves local markets first, regional markets second, national markets third. Communities with nested supply chains (local → regional → national rather than national → regional → local) maintained supply during COVID-19 disruptions better than communities dependent on national distribution.

**Strategic reserves**: Community-managed food and materials stockpiles. COVID-19 demonstrated that household stockpiling is inferior to community-organized strategic reserves — community reserves can be rationed; household stockpiles cannot. Six-month food reserve at community scale (200 households) requires approximately 450,000 lbs of dry goods and 300,000 lbs of preserved food — achievable with community cold storage and dry storage infrastructure.

### Key Questions
- What Zone 5 regional food hubs currently exist, and what is their capacity relative to community-scale demand?
- What is the evidence on the production efficiency of nested supply chains versus just-in-time national supply chains under stress conditions?
- What strategic reserve design principles prevent reserve depletion and hoarding?

### Evidence Checkpoints
- Nature Cities: "Infrastructure for regional food systems to feed cities at a time of deglobalization" (2026) — strongest recent peer-reviewed evidence on regional food system capacity
- Tandfonline: "Investigating capabilities of Food Hubs in short food supply chains" (2025)
- Resilience.org: "Beyond Community Currencies: Strengthening Your Local Economy" (2025)
- USDA Agricultural Marketing Service: Community Supported Agriculture Directory — Zone 5 CSA data
- FairShare CSA Coalition (Wisconsin): regional food system structure and producer network documentation

### Scope Boundaries
- In scope: regional food hubs, nested supply chains, strategic reserves, CSA networks in Zone 5
- Out of scope: national supply chain policy; import/export economics; logistics optimization for corporate supply chains

### Cross-Domain Bridges
- Phase 5 Wave 3 Food Preservation and Storage: the food preservation techniques in Phase 5 are the household layer; this section addresses the community and regional layer above it.
- Phase 6 Domain A (Community Platform Architecture): digital coordination platforms for regional food system coordination — matching producers to buyers, managing strategic reserves.

## Section 7: Alternative Financial Institutions — Timebanking, Labor Exchange, and Non-Monetary Compensation

**Target: 600–750 words**

### Core Question
How do communities compensate specialized labor (healthcare, teaching, governance, infrastructure maintenance) when monetary systems are unavailable or inadequate?

### Causal Pathway
Some community labor cannot be compensated by barter because the services are not fungible with goods. A medic who works 40 hours a week on patient care needs food, shelter, and clothing — but cannot barter medical services directly for those things in a simple transaction. Timebanking — a system where one hour of any member's time earns one hour of any other member's time — provides the accounting layer that makes non-fungible service exchange possible.

Edgar Cahn (founder of TimeBanks USA) documented timebanking's effectiveness at rebuilding social capital in distressed communities. The co-production principle (those who receive services also provide services) changes the economic relationship from charity to reciprocal exchange — with documented effects on community cohesion.

Madison Mutual Aid Network (MAN) Cooperative (Madison, Wisconsin) is a Zone 5 case: nested cooperative structures pooling labor, skills, and resources through timebanking and business-to-business mutual credit. Active as of 2024.

### Key Questions
- What is the documented effectiveness of timebanking for compensating non-fungible skilled labor (healthcare, education, governance)?
- What is the 2025 research evidence on timebank participation barriers? (The medrxiv scoping review "Willingness, Motivators and Barriers to Time Bank Participation" provides current evidence.)
- How do communities prevent timebank inflation (members issuing more time credits than they can redeem)?

### Evidence Checkpoints
- Cahn, Edgar. *No More Throw-Away People*. Essential Books, 2004. (TimeBanks USA founder; foundational timebanking design)
- Seyfang, Gill. "Time Banks: Rewarding Community Self-Help in the Inner City?" *Community Development Journal* 39(1): 62–71, 2004.
- medrxiv: "Willingness, Motivators and Barriers to Time Bank Participation: A Scoping Review" (2025) — most current peer-reviewed evidence
- Madison MAN Cooperative: https://madisonman.coop — Zone 5 mutual aid network case study (active)
- TimeBanks USA: https://timebanks.org — implementation resources

### Scope Boundaries
- In scope: timebanking, labor exchange, non-monetary compensation systems; Zone 5 mutual aid networks
- Out of scope: gig economy labor platforms; non-monetary compensation in corporate contexts

## Section 8: Governing the Community Economy — Decision-Making for Economic Systems

**Target: 600–750 words**

### Core Question
What governance structures allow communities to make legitimate, enforceable economic decisions (pricing, rationing, investment, distribution) without market signals?

### Causal Pathway
Economic systems require governance — someone must decide what the exchange rate is, who adjudicates disputes, what happens when a member defaults. Communities that build economic systems without explicit governance structures find that economic authority defaults to the person with the most social power or the most resources — which produces exactly the concentration of economic power that community economic systems are designed to prevent.

Three governance mechanisms for community economies are documented:

**Participatory budgeting**: Annual community process for allocating shared economic resources by democratic vote. As of 2024, 64+ U.S. cities/counties use participatory budgeting for public funds; the mechanism is directly transferable to community economic systems. The Participatory Budgeting Project's 2024 highlights document include Medellín, Colombia's 30-year track record — the strongest case study for transformative economic governance.

**Cooperative governance structures**: General Assembly (all member-owners) + Governing Council (elected, strategic authority) + Social Council (worker welfare). This three-body structure (derived from Mondragon) separates strategic authority from operational authority and worker welfare from management authority — preventing any single body from capturing all economic power.

**Economic arbitration**: Communities need an explicit mechanism for resolving economic disputes (defaults, pricing disagreements, allocation conflicts). Ostrom's design principle 6 (accessible conflict resolution) applies directly: the mechanism must be accessible, low-cost, and legitimate.

### Key Questions
- What governance models from cooperative enterprise are directly applicable to community-scale economic governance?
- How does participatory budgeting handle non-monetary resource allocation (labor, food, tools)?
- What is the evidence on the governance failure modes most common in community economic systems (the Argentina trueque collapse case is the clearest documented failure)?

### Evidence Checkpoints
- Participatory Budgeting Project: "PBP 2024 Highlights" — current state of practice
- Cabannes, Yves. "Participatory Budgeting: A Significant Contribution to Participatory Democracy." *Environment and Urbanization* 16(1), 2004.
- Wiley: "Participatory Budgeting for Social Equity: A Comparative Analysis" (2025) — newest peer-reviewed evidence on PB outcomes
- Academia.edu: "Mastering the crisis? Sustaining governance in the Argentine Redes de Trueque" — the trueque failure analysis

### Scope Boundaries
- In scope: participatory budgeting adapted for community economic governance; cooperative governance structures; economic dispute resolution
- Out of scope: municipal government finance; national fiscal policy

### Cross-Domain Bridges
- Phase 3 community governance: this section is the economic layer of Phase 3 governance — the same decision structures applied to economic rather than political questions.

## Section 9: Zone 5 Midwest Implementation — Cooperative Tradition and Regional Context

**Target: 700–900 words**

### Core Question
What does the Zone 5 Midwest's cooperative tradition and regional economic structure mean for community economic resilience implementation?

### Causal Pathway
Zone 5 (Illinois, Michigan, Iowa, Indiana, Wisconsin) has the deepest cooperative tradition in the United States. Agricultural cooperatives, rural electric cooperatives, and credit unions are not hypothetical models for Zone 5 — they are living infrastructure that Zone 5 communities have used for generations. The cooperative tradition provides:

1. Institutional knowledge (how cooperatives work, how to govern them, how to finance them)
2. Legal infrastructure (cooperative law is well-developed in all five Zone 5 states)
3. Active networks (USDA Rural Cooperative Development Grant recipients in Zone 5 include organizations in Illinois, Iowa, Indiana, Wisconsin)
4. Cultural legitimacy (cooperatives are familiar, not exotic)

Zone 5-specific economic resilience assets:
- Iowa: 90+ credit unions; Land O'Lakes, Growmark, and Organic Valley headquartered or major-presence in Iowa; Iowa Rural Water Association (multi-community cooperative water infrastructure)
- Illinois: 300+ state-chartered credit unions; Illinois Farm Direct; Prairie Crossing Farm (Grayslake — CSA model); Chicago Tool Library (community tool commons)
- Wisconsin: Organic Valley headquarters (La Farge); FairShare CSA Coalition; Madison MAN Cooperative (mutual aid); 1,000+ cooperatives across all sectors
- Indiana: Purdue Extension agricultural cooperative development; Indiana Cooperative Development Center; Bloomington Community Farmers Market (one of largest in Midwest)
- Michigan: Michigan State University Extension cooperative development; Michigan Food Hub Network; Lake Odessa (producer cooperative)

Zone 5 implementation constraints:
- Seasonal agricultural economy: 90% of Zone 5 communities have agricultural rhythms that shape economic activity (income concentrated in fall; input expenditure concentrated in spring)
- Rural-urban divide: rural communities and small cities have different economic structures; community economic systems must bridge them
- Climate risk: Zone 5 agricultural income is exposed to drought (2012 drought reduced Iowa corn yield 50%), flooding, and early/late frost; community economic resilience must account for seasonal volatility

### Key Questions

- How do Zone 5 rural electric cooperatives (member-owned, grid-independent infrastructure) provide a governance model for other community economic systems?
- What is the documented performance of Zone 5 credit unions compared to commercial banks during the 2008 financial crisis and COVID-19?

## Section 10: Implementation Templates and Case Studies

**Target: 800–1,000 words**

### Core Question
What are the minimum viable designs for two Zone 5 community economic systems that a community could implement without further research?

### Template 1: Community Local Currency Design Guide
A step-by-step implementation guide covering:
- Determining the appropriate currency type (mutual credit vs. backed vs. time-based) for community size and economic structure
- Governing the currency: issuance authority, conversion limits, default resolution
- Launch mechanics: seeding the currency, on-boarding members, setting exchange rates for goods and services
- Monitoring and adjustment: how to detect and correct hoarding, inflation, and default
- Zone 5 seasonal considerations: agricultural communities need currencies that handle seasonal income concentration

### Template 2: Commons Management Charter
A fillable governance document for community shared resources (food stores, tool libraries, seed libraries, shared processing equipment):
- Defining the commons: what is included, what is excluded
- Membership and access rules: who can use the commons, at what cost, under what conditions
- Contribution requirements: what members must provide to maintain access
- Monitoring and enforcement: how the commons is monitored, what happens when rules are violated
- Graduated sanctions: from warning to temporary exclusion to permanent exclusion
- Conflict resolution: who adjudicates disputes, what the process is

### Required Case Studies (standardized format for each: context → economic mechanism → governance structure → outcomes → failure modes → Zone 5 transferable design principles)

3. **Vermont Timebanking Network**: US-based timebanking with documented social cohesion and community resilience outcomes; community health improvement data
4. **Zone 5 Agricultural Cooperative Case** (Land O'Lakes or Organic Valley): documenting governance structure, member benefit, and crisis resilience (2008 financial crisis, 2020 supply chain disruption)

## Analytical Framework: How to Synthesize Sources

**Do not organize the document by economic school of thought** (Keynesian, Austrian, heterodox). Organize by implementation level and mechanism.

**The three-layer framework**:
- Layer 1: Exchange mechanisms (currencies, barter protocols, timebanking) — the transaction layer
- Layer 2: Resource governance (commons, cooperatives, capital formation) — the allocation layer
- Layer 3: Economic institutions (cooperative enterprise, CDFIs, participatory budgeting) — the governance layer

Every section operates within one layer. When a section spans layers (currency governance spans Layer 1 and Layer 3), explicitly call out the layer shift.

**The practitioner lens**: At every point, ask: could a Zone 5 community organizer with no economics background read this section and know what to do? If the answer is no, the section is too theoretical. Rewrite with a specific Zone 5 implementation scenario as the organizing example.

**Uncertainty documentation**: When case study evidence is from non-peer-reviewed sources (practitioner reports, NGO evaluations, government documents), document this explicitly. A sentence like "The Argentina trueque network peak participation estimates range from 2.5–7 million depending on source methodology (non-peer-reviewed estimate; academic analysis confirms 2.5M as conservative floor)" is stronger than false precision.
