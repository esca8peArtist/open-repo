# Phase 6 Domain C: Intergenerational Skills Development and Knowledge Transfer
## Scope Definition and Source Readiness Assessment

**Lead finding**: Domain C fills the temporal durability gap that Phases 1–5 leave open. Every other domain in the systems-resilience corpus documents procedures — how to filter water, grow food, generate power, organize communities. Domain C documents how communities ensure those procedures survive the death, departure, or incapacitation of the people who hold them. It is the only Phase 6 candidate operating explicitly on a 10–20 year time horizon, and the only one whose failure mode is silent: knowledge does not disappear in a crisis, it disappears quietly in year seven when the person who knew is gone.

---

## 1. Domain Definition and Scope Boundaries

### What Domain C Is

Intergenerational Skills Development and Knowledge Transfer is the research domain that documents how resilient communities:

1. Identify which practical skills are survival-critical versus merely useful
2. Transmit complex technical skills without relying on formal schools, professional credentialing boards, or institutional certification
3. Build the community structures — learning cooperatives, apprenticeship registries, skill-sharing networks — that make skill transmission systematic rather than accidental
4. Ensure critical knowledge is redundantly held across multiple community members, so no single individual's loss collapses the system they maintained

The domain treats skill transmission as infrastructure, not education policy. A community whose single water system operator dies in year three of a grid-down scenario is not facing an educational problem; it is facing an infrastructure failure. Domain C provides the design principles and implementation templates for building resilient knowledge infrastructure before that failure mode occurs.

### What Domain C Is Not

The following are explicitly out of scope and must be enforced as hard boundaries throughout production:

- K-12 curriculum design for children (Phase 5 Wave 3 educational governance covers schooling structures; Domain C covers adult skill development and cross-generational transmission)
- National or state-level education policy
- Online or technology-mediated learning as primary delivery mechanisms (relevant only as backup documentation strategy in crisis contexts, not as a subject of extended analysis)
- Neuroscience of learning at the laboratory level (not applicable to community implementation)
- Corporate training, professional development, or HR-adjacent learning design
- Comprehensive general literacy and numeracy curricula (addressed briefly under "foundational skills" but not as a primary section)

**Scope enforcement mechanism**: The agent must maintain a hard ceiling of 12–15 critical skills under investigation. Educational research literature expands readily; the ceiling is the primary scope control. Any skill proposed for inclusion beyond the 15 threshold requires explicit justification against the survival-criticality criterion (does loss of this skill threaten the community's ability to sustain its core systems?).

## 2. Research Questions — The Three Core Questions Plus Sub-Questions

### Question 1: Which skills are actually critical for community resilience, and what are their realistic learning time requirements?

This question has three sub-components that must each produce concrete, usable outputs:

**1a. Critical skill identification**: The domain must produce a priority skill map across 12–15 domains derived from the Phase 1–5 corpus. The skill inventory must name each skill specifically (not "water systems" but "maintaining a gravity-fed slow sand filtration system," not "energy" but "commissioning and diagnosing a household-scale 12V lithium system"). The inventory must distinguish between skills the community absolutely cannot function without (water system operation, food preservation, basic medical response) and skills that are high-value but recoverable from external knowledge (e.g., large-scale grain threshing).

**1b. Learning time curves**: For each critical skill, the domain must provide a learning time estimate with the following structure: (a) the minimum hours of supervised practice to reach functional competence for an adult learner without prior background; (b) the hours to reach independent reliability; (c) the prerequisite knowledge that must precede the skill. These estimates will draw from FEMA CERT training sequences, Red Cross disaster preparedness curricula, Extension Service adult education records, and practitioner evidence where peer-reviewed data is unavailable. Uncertainty must be documented explicitly.

**1c. Prerequisite sequencing**: Some skills require foundational knowledge. Solar microgrid commissioning requires basic DC circuit knowledge. Emergency wound closure requires basic anatomy and sterile field technique. The domain must produce a prerequisite map that allows communities to sequence skill development rationally rather than attempting advanced skills before foundations are in place.

**Case studies required**: Cuba's post-Soviet Special Period (1991–2000), when Cuba lost 85% of its import capacity and adapted its food, medicine, and education systems within 36 months. Puerto Rico's post-Hurricane Maria (2017) community education and skill rebuilding. Christchurch, New Zealand post-earthquake community self-organization. Historical guild apprenticeship structures (European craft guilds, 13th–18th century). These case studies must answer the same question with specificity: which skills were learned, how were they transmitted, over what timeline, with what failure modes.

### Question 2: How do communities transmit complex technical skills without schools or credentialing institutions?

**2a. Apprenticeship models**: The domain must document the structural mechanics of apprenticeship — not the philosophy, but the operational design. What is the ratio of demonstration to supervised practice to independent practice in effective apprenticeship for technical skills? What does a community apprenticeship contract look like (who teaches what, for how long, what the competence threshold is)? What are the documented failure modes of apprenticeship (novice sees expert doing but doesn't practice; expert assumes competence is higher than it is; single expert becomes bottleneck)?

**2b. Documentation strategies**: How does a community create instructional documentation that works without power, internet, or professional technical writers? The domain must cover: procedure documentation formats (step-by-step vs. decision-tree vs. troubleshooting-first), diagrams and visual documentation for non-literate or semi-literate learners, offline knowledge management (physical library design, USB archive organization, printed procedure binders), and maintenance of documentation accuracy over time.

**2c. Competence validation**: How does a community certify that someone knows how to maintain the water system without a licensing board? The domain must develop a validation framework: observable performance criteria (the person can perform the task to specification without prompting, three times consecutively, under field conditions), peer validation by an established community expert, and documentation of validated competencies in a community skills registry.

**2d. Cultural transmission mechanisms**: The domain must address how communities embed technical knowledge in social structures that survive institutional failure. Historical examples: the Amish practice of embedding agricultural and construction knowledge in barn raisings and mutual aid work; Pacific island navigation knowledge encoded in oral tradition and song; medieval craft guild knowledge embedded in apprenticeship initiation and guild ritual. These mechanisms increase knowledge durability by making the act of transmission a community event rather than a private transaction.

**2e. Cross-generational teaching dynamics**: The domain must document specifically what fails when experienced adults teach teenagers technical skills, and what methods reduce those failure rates. Documented failure modes: expert blind spots (expert cannot explain the foundational assumptions they have internalized); authority gradients (teenager does not challenge instructor even when instruction is unclear); contextual disconnection (teenager cannot see why the skill matters). Documented mitigations: peer teaching (same-age learners teach each other with instructor oversight), learning-by-doing before explanation (experience before theory), and competence portfolios that give learners visible evidence of progress.

### Question 3: What institutions and governance structures support skill transmission at community scale?

**3a. Learning cooperatives**: The domain must document the operational design of a community learning cooperative — not just the concept but the mechanics. How is teaching time allocated among community members? How are teaching obligations recorded and compensated (in a non-monetary economy, what does a teacher receive for teaching)? How are teaching quality and student progress monitored without external oversight?

**3b. Apprenticeship registries**: Communities need a visible, maintained record of who knows what, who is currently being trained, and where gaps exist. The domain must produce a design template for a community skills registry that includes: skill categories, current holders with competence level, current apprentices with progress status, and identified gaps (critical skills held by only one person or no one).

**3c. Knowledge management infrastructure**: Communities in crisis contexts often lose knowledge not because no one was trained but because documentation was inaccessible when needed. The domain must address: library design for offline resilience communities (printed reference library with redundant copies, USB archive hierarchy, maintenance schedule), oral history programs that embed procedural knowledge in narrative (used by the Amish and in many indigenous communities), and documentation maintenance practices (who is responsible for keeping procedures current, how often are they reviewed, what triggers an update).

**3d. Quality assurance without external certification**: The domain must produce a framework for community-internal quality assurance — how communities ensure that validated competencies are actually at the level required, rather than validated by an expert who is too close to the learner or too pressed for time. This section draws on the medical literature on competence-based medical education and the aviation literature on crew resource management and checklist validation.

## 3. Expected Output Structure — Section Outline

The document target is 48,000–58,000 words across 10–12 major sections, with two required implementation templates and minimum four case studies. Suggested section architecture:

1. **Introduction: The Second-Year Failure Mode** (~2,500 words) — The knowledge loss failure mode that kills communities after the acute crisis. Why documentation alone is insufficient. The resilience corpus's temporal dependency on Domain C.

2. **Critical Skills Inventory** (~8,000–10,000 words) — 12–15 skills with learning time estimates, prerequisite maps, and competence thresholds. This is the highest-value section for practitioners and must be produced with rigor.

3. **Learning Theory Foundations for Practitioners** (~4,000–5,000 words) — Lave and Wenger situated learning, Kolb experiential learning cycle, andragogy (adult learning theory), problem-based learning. Presented as practitioner tools, not academic theory.

4. **Apprenticeship Models: Design and Implementation** (~5,000–6,000 words) — Structural mechanics of apprenticeship, failure modes, community apprenticeship contract template, competence milestones.

5. **Documentation Strategies: Building Knowledge That Survives** (~4,000–5,000 words) — Procedure documentation formats, offline knowledge management, visual documentation, documentation maintenance.

6. **Competence Validation: Certifying Without Boards** (~3,000–4,000 words) — Observable performance criteria, peer validation framework, community skills registry design.

7. **Intergenerational Teaching Dynamics** (~4,000–5,000 words) — Cross-generational failure modes, cultural transmission mechanisms, Amish knowledge system, peer teaching design.

8. **Learning Cooperatives and Skill-Sharing Networks** (~4,000–5,000 words) — Community learning cooperative operating model, teaching time allocation, governance of teaching obligations.

9. **Knowledge Management Infrastructure** (~3,000–4,000 words) — Library design, oral history programs, documentation maintenance practices.

10. **Zone 5 Implications and Seasonal Learning Calendar** (~3,000–4,000 words) — 4-H and Extension Service infrastructure, agricultural calendar constraints on learning schedules, Zone 5 skill mix priorities.

11. **Case Studies** (~4,000–5,000 words) — Cuba Special Period, Puerto Rico post-Maria, Christchurch, historical guild apprenticeship.

12. **Implementation Templates** (~3,000–4,000 words) — Community apprenticeship program design guide; skills inventory and gap assessment tool.

## 4. Source Library Readiness Assessment

### 4a. Confirmed Sources — Available Now (70–75% of Production Need)

**Foundational learning theory (all accessible, peer-reviewed):**
- Lave, Jean and Etienne Wenger. *Situated Learning: Legitimate Peripheral Participation*. Cambridge University Press, 1991. (Foundational on apprenticeship as the original mode of learning. Directly applicable.)
- Kolb, David A. *Experiential Learning: Experience as the Source of Learning and Development*. Prentice Hall, 1984. (The learn-by-doing cycle. Core reference for documentation of practical skill acquisition.)
- Knowles, Malcolm. *The Adult Learner: A Neglected Species*. Gulf Publishing, 1973. (Andragogy: what differentiates adult from child learning. Foundational for community skill-building design.)
- Schon, Donald. *The Reflective Practitioner*. Basic Books, 1983. (How practitioners develop expertise; directly applicable to community apprenticeship design.)
- Sobel, David. *Place-Based Education*. Orion Society, 2004. (Learning embedded in local context. Applicable to Zone 5 agricultural skill transmission.)
- Collins, Allan, John Seely Brown, and Susan Newman. "Cognitive Apprenticeship." *Knowing, Learning, and Instruction*. 1989. (The cognitive apprenticeship framework — the most operationally useful theoretical tool in the source library.)

**Crisis-context case study literature (accessible, some paywalled):**
- Cuba Special Period education adaptation: Multiple peer-reviewed sources in *Latin American Perspectives*, *NACLA Report on the Americas*, and Cuban Studies journals. Garrard-Burnett's work on the religious community response provides the skills transmission angle. Available via JSTOR and university library access.
- Puerto Rico post-Maria education reports: FEMA Individual Assistance reports (free, fema.gov), Center for Puerto Rican Studies (CUNY, free), Harvard Kennedy School case studies (some open access).
- UNESCO. *Non-Formal Education in Low-Resource Contexts*. Multiple editions available at en.unesco.org (free).
- FEMA. *Community Emergency Response Team Training Materials*. Freely available at training.fema.gov. These provide the best available standardized learning curve benchmarks for emergency skills.

**Midwest/Zone 5 primary sources (all free):**
- 4-H National Headquarters data and programming guides (4-h.org, free). 6.8 million youth participants in Zone 5 states provides primary evidence of practical skill transmission infrastructure already operational.
- USDA Cooperative Extension Service adult education publications (land-grant university extension portals — Iowa State, University of Illinois, Purdue, University of Wisconsin. All free.)
- State 4-H archives at Iowa State University Extension (extension.iastate.edu): agricultural apprenticeship records, Extension master gardener program documentation.

**Apprenticeship and guild historical literature (mixed access):**
- Historical guild apprenticeship records: Epstein, S.R. "Craft Guilds, Apprenticeship, and Technological Change in Preindustrial Europe." *Journal of Economic History*, 1998. (Accessible via JSTOR.)
- Stahl, Genevieve. "Medieval Guild Apprenticeship and Knowledge Transmission." Available via academic library access.
- General English apprenticeship history: Dunlop, O.J. *English Apprenticeship and Child Labour*. Public domain, Archive.org.

**Amish knowledge transmission (accessible ethnographic literature):**
- Kraybill, Donald. *The Riddle of Amish Culture*. Johns Hopkins University Press. (Library access, widely held.)
- Kraybill, Donald. *Amish Society*. Multiple editions. Covers the Amish system of embedded knowledge transmission through work and community practice.

**Competence validation and quality assurance literature:**
- Frank, Jason R. et al. "Competency-Based Medical Education: Theory to Practice." *Medical Teacher*, 2010. (The CBME framework. Directly translatable to community competence validation.)
- McGaghie, William C. et al. "A Critical Review of Simulation-Based Mastery Learning." *Academic Medicine*, 2011. (Mastery learning thresholds applicable to skill certification design.)

**Learning cooperative models:**
- Schumacher, E.F. *Small Is Beautiful*. 1973. (Community-scale organization principles relevant to learning cooperative design.)
- Mondragon cooperative education documentation: Mondragon University annual reports (freely available, mondragon.edu).

### 4b. Sources Requiring Week 1 Research (the 25–30% Gap)

**Trade-specific learning curve data in low-resource settings** — This is the primary gap. Peer-reviewed data on learning times for specific resilience-critical trades (well-drilling, solar installation, emergency medical response, water system maintenance) in low-resource, non-institutional settings is sparse. Practitioner reports exist and are the primary resolution path.

Resolution strategy (to be executed June 1–8):
- FEMA CERT training sequences provide standardized time estimates for emergency skills (40-hour basic training for CPR/first aid/search and rescue). These are the most rigorous available benchmarks.
- Red Cross Disaster Preparedness curricula provide additional validated benchmarks.
- Extension Service and 4-H program guides document time estimates for agricultural skill acquisition.
- Homesteading practitioner community (Mother Earth News archive, homesteadingtoday.com forum archives) provides practitioner estimates for non-emergency trades. These should be documented as practitioner evidence with explicit uncertainty notation.
- Solar microgrid installation training: NABCEP (North American Board of Certified Energy Practitioners) training hour requirements provide a professional baseline; the domain uses this as an upper bound and estimates community learning curve from practitioner reports.

**Intergenerational teaching dynamics in North American contexts** — Cultural psychology literature exists (Vygotsky's zone of proximal development, more recent research on expert-novice knowledge transfer) but is not well-synthesized for Midwest rural contexts specifically. Resolution: search for rural community health worker training literature and agricultural Extension peer-teaching evaluations, which provide the closest available documented analog.

**Knowledge transmission effectiveness metrics** — How do communities measure whether learning actually occurred and was retained? Academic literature on formative assessment and competence-based education provides the theoretical tools; the gap is applying these tools to non-institutional community contexts. Resolution: interview-free — available from CBME literature (Frank et al.), aviation checklist literature (Gawande, *The Checklist Manifesto*), and open-source quality management frameworks.

**Comparative case studies beyond Cuba and Puerto Rico** — The source library is strong on Cuba and Puerto Rico. Three additional case studies would strengthen the domain: (1) community rebuild in Christchurch after 2011 earthquake (New Zealand academic literature, accessible); (2) Zapatista autonomous communities' education system (Chiapas, Mexico — documented in *Education, Globalization and Social Change*, Bonal and Rassell); (3) North Dakota oil-boom community skill adaptation (practitioner documentation, NDSU Extension records).

## 5. Success Criteria

Research is complete when the document meets all of the following:

**Content completeness:**
- Critical skills inventory with 12–15 skills, each with learning time estimates, prerequisites, and competence threshold
- Apprenticeship models section documents at least three distinct structural models with operational mechanics
- Implementation templates: community apprenticeship program design guide and skills inventory/gap assessment tool — both usable by a Zone 5 community without further research
- Minimum four case studies answering the transmission question with specificity (not just describing the case but extracting transferable design principles)

**Source requirements:**
- 130–160 citations total
- At least 45 peer-reviewed academic sources
- At least 25 practitioner/primary sources (Extension publications, FEMA materials, 4-H documentation)
- At least 4 international comparative case studies
- All cited sources accessible (URL or library access noted; no ghost citations)

**Zone 5 integration:**
- Zone 5 seasonal learning calendar (which skills can be taught outdoors in which months, indoor skills for winter, harvest-season scheduling constraints)
- 4-H and Extension Service data integrated throughout, not just in a single section
- Minimum two Zone 5-specific implementation recommendations per major section

**Implementation usability:**
- A Zone 5 community organizer with no prior educational background could read the implementation templates and begin building a community apprenticeship program
- Skills inventory tool allows community to conduct a self-assessment of current capabilities and gaps in one working session
- Community apprenticeship contract template is complete and fillable
