## The Domain in Depth: What You Are Researching

### The Problem This Domain Solves

Communities that survive an acute crisis frequently fail on a 5–10 year timescale because of knowledge loss, not resource loss. The water system operator dies. The solar technician relocates. The single medical responder is injured. If no one was trained to replace them, the systems they maintained fail. Phase 1–5 documents procedures. Domain C documents how communities build knowledge infrastructure so those procedures survive one generational turnover.

The failure mode is silent and slow. It does not appear in the first year. It appears in year seven, when the community discovers that the person who knew how to maintain the water filtration system is gone, and no one else does. This domain is the durability layer for the entire Phase 1–5 corpus.

### Scope Boundaries

**In scope:**
- Adult skill development and transmission in community resilience contexts
- Apprenticeship models, peer teaching, and learning-by-doing methodologies
- Community governance structures for knowledge transmission (learning cooperatives, skill-sharing networks)
- Knowledge preservation and documentation strategies for offline, low-power contexts
- Competence validation without external certification
- Intergenerational teaching dynamics — specifically what fails when experienced adults teach teenagers technical skills, and what works
- Zone 5 Midwest implementation: 4-H, Extension Service, agricultural apprenticeship traditions

**Hard out-of-scope (enforce actively throughout production):**
- K-12 curriculum design for children (Phase 5 already covers schooling structures)
- National or state education policy
- Online or technology-mediated learning as a primary subject (briefly relevant as offline backup documentation method only)
- Neuroscience of learning at the laboratory level
- Corporate training and HR-adjacent learning design

**The critical scope ceiling**: You are researching **12–15 specific critical skills**, not a comprehensive education system. Every proposed addition to the skills list above 15 requires explicit justification against this criterion: does loss of this skill threaten the community's ability to sustain its core systems? If you cannot answer yes clearly, the skill does not belong in the inventory.

## 12–15 Research Questions the Document Must Answer

These are your research questions. The document is complete when all 15 are answered with rigor and specificity.

**Skills Inventory:**
1. What are the 12–15 skills most critical for Zone 5 community resilience, and what are their realistic learning times for an adult learner without prior background? (Specify minimum hours to functional competence and hours to independent reliability for each skill.)
2. What prerequisite knowledge must precede each critical skill, and in what sequence should communities develop them to minimize dead-end learning? (E.g., DC circuit basics before solar microgrid commissioning; sterile field technique before wound care.)
3. What is the minimum safe redundancy level for each critical skill — how many community holders does "safe" require, and what evidence from crisis-context cases supports that threshold?

**Transmission Mechanisms:**
4. What are the operational mechanics of effective community apprenticeship — specifically the ratio of demonstration to supervised practice to independent practice for technical skills, and what does the research on cognitive apprenticeship (Collins, Brown, Newman 1989) show about the four stages of modeling, coaching, scaffolding, and fading?
5. What are the documented failure modes of apprenticeship in non-institutional settings, and what specific structural changes reduce each failure mode? (Expert blind spot, authority gradient, contextual disconnection — with documented mitigations, not just identification.)
6. How do communities create instructional documentation that functions without power, internet, or professional technical writers? What formats (step-by-step, decision-tree, troubleshooting-first) are best suited to which skill types, and what visual documentation strategies work for mixed-literacy audiences?
7. How did Cuba's post-Soviet Special Period (1991–2000) adapt skill transmission when 85% of imports disappeared within 18 months? Which specific mechanisms worked (community polytechnic system, enterprise-level training, informal brigades) and what failure modes occurred?
8. How did Puerto Rico's community organizations rebuild practical skill transmission within 12 months after Hurricane Maria (2017), without functioning institutional infrastructure? What was the role of Puerto Rican community health workers (promotoras) and agricultural cooperatives in this process?

**Governance and Institutions:**
9. What is the operational design of a community learning cooperative — specifically how are teaching obligations allocated, recorded, and compensated in a non-monetary economy? What are the governance failure modes (teacher overload, learner dropout, quality drift) and their documented mitigations?
10. How does a community skills registry function — what information does it contain, who maintains it, how often is it updated, and what format allows it to be maintained without digital infrastructure?
11. What quality assurance mechanisms allow communities to certify that a validated competency is actually at the required level, without an external licensing board? (Draw on CBME — competency-based medical education — literature and aviation crew resource management literature for the validation framework.)
12. What is the evidence on the effectiveness of the "protégé effect" — do same-age peer learners who teach each other under instructor oversight achieve better retention than passively receiving instruction? How should Zone 5 communities structure peer teaching to maximize this effect?

**Zone 5 and Implementation:**
13. What does the Zone 5 seasonal learning calendar look like — which skills require outdoor conditions (and thus specific seasonal windows), which are best taught in winter months, and how do the agricultural calendar constraints (planting, harvest) shape the teaching schedule for a Zone 5 community?
14. What does the 4-H cooperative extension infrastructure in Zone 5 already provide — specifically what programming, documentation, and trained personnel exist that a resilience community can directly leverage without building from scratch?
15. What are the two non-negotiable components of a community apprenticeship program that a Zone 5 community organizer with no prior educational background can implement in the first 60 days — and what can be deferred without consequence?

## Source Categories and Priority Reading Order

### Priority 1 — Read in Week 1 (Foundational, Freely Available)

- **Lave and Wenger, *Situated Learning: Legitimate Peripheral Participation*** (1991) — The single most important theoretical source. Apprenticeship is the original human learning mode. Every community skill transmission design decision should be filtered through this framework.
- **FEMA CERT Training Materials** (training.fema.gov, free) — The most rigorously validated learning time estimates available for emergency skills. These are your primary benchmark for learning curve data.
- **4-H programming guides and outcome documentation** (4-h.org) — Primary source evidence for Zone 5 practical skill transmission. Do not treat 4-H as merely illustrative; treat it as primary evidence.
- **USDA Cooperative Extension adult education publications** (extension.iastate.edu, extension.illinois.edu, extension.purdue.edu) — Contain adult learning time estimates for agricultural skills that are directly relevant to your Critical Skills Inventory.
- **Kolb, *Experiential Learning*** (1984) — The experiential learning cycle. Read Chapter 2 and Chapter 5; the rest is less directly applicable.
- **Collins, Brown, and Newman, "Cognitive Apprenticeship"** (1989) — The four-stage framework for expert-to-novice technical skill transfer. This is more directly applicable to your assignment than Lave and Wenger for the practical section.

### Priority 2 — Read in Weeks 1–2 (Case Studies, Accessible)

- **Cuba Special Period education literature** — Search JSTOR for "Cuba Special Period education" and "Cuba Special Period agriculture." The academic literature is in *Latin American Perspectives* and *Cuban Studies*. The specific angle you need is: what skills were taught through what mechanisms, over what timeline.
- **Puerto Rico post-Maria community recovery** — Start with Center for Puerto Rican Studies (Hunter College CUNY) reports, freely available at centropr.hunter.cuny.edu. The Harvard Kennedy School case studies provide additional depth.
- **UNESCO Non-Formal Education Reports** — en.unesco.org. Particularly relevant: reports on non-formal education in low-resource contexts, apprenticeship outcomes in developing country settings.
- **Kraybill, Donald. *The Riddle of Amish Culture*** — Available in most university libraries. The Amish knowledge transmission system (embedded in work, community events, and apprenticeship) is the most detailed documented case study of informal skill transmission in a North American rural community.

### Priority 3 — Read in Weeks 3–5 (Theory and Validation, Some Paywalled)

- **Knowles, Malcolm. *The Adult Learner*** (1973) — Andragogy: adult learning theory. Available in most libraries.
- **Frank, Jason R. et al., "Competency-Based Medical Education: Theory to Practice"** (*Medical Teacher*, 2010) — The CBME framework for competence validation. Directly applicable to community certification design. Available via PubMed (some open access versions available).
- **Schon, Donald. *The Reflective Practitioner*** (1983) — How practitioners develop expertise through reflection. Useful for the intergenerational teaching dynamics section.
- **Gawande, Atul. *The Checklist Manifesto*** (2009) — Aviation and medical checklist practices for quality assurance. A practitioner-usable framework for community validation protocols.

### Priority 4 — Search and Compile in Weeks 2–3 (Practitioner Evidence)

- **NABCEP solar installation training documentation** — Search nabcep.org for training hour requirements. This is your professional baseline for solar skill learning time estimates.
- **Red Cross disaster preparedness training curricula** — redcross.org. Skill-specific time estimates for first aid, CPR, and emergency response.
- **Extension Service master gardener program** — Documents community volunteers who are trained to teach gardening skills. The training model is directly applicable; the subject matter overlaps with your food production skill category.
- **Homesteading practitioner literature** — *Mother Earth News* archive (archive.org), homesteadingtoday.com forum archives. These contain practitioner learning time estimates for off-grid skills not covered in academic literature. Document these explicitly as practitioner estimates, not peer-reviewed data.

### Priority 5 — Use as Needed (Historical and Comparative)

- **Guild apprenticeship historical literature** — Epstein, S.R., "Craft Guilds, Apprenticeship, and Technological Change in Preindustrial Europe," *Journal of Economic History* (1998), accessible via JSTOR. Dunlop, O.J., *English Apprenticeship and Child Labour* (public domain, Archive.org).
- **Christchurch post-earthquake community organization** — New Zealand Ministry of Civil Defence recovery reports (civildefence.govt.nz, free). Academic literature in *Disasters* journal and *Natural Hazards*.
- **Zapatista autonomous community education** — Bonal and Rambla, *Education and Globalization* (access via library). Less central than Cuba/Puerto Rico but useful for governance of educational autonomy question.

## Analytical Framework: How to Synthesize Sources

Do not organize the document chronologically or by geographic region. Organize by mechanism and implementation level.

**The three-layer framework**:
- Layer 1: What skills (the knowledge layer) — what must be transmitted
- Layer 2: How to transmit (the pedagogy layer) — the mechanisms of transfer
- Layer 3: What structures support transmission (the institutional layer) — how communities organize and sustain the process

Every section should operate within one of these three layers and be clearly labeled by which layer it addresses. When a section spans layers (e.g., apprenticeship touches both Layer 2 and Layer 3), explicitly call out the layer shift.

**The practitioner lens**: At every point, ask: could a Zone 5 community organizer with no educational background read this and know what to do? If the answer is no, the section is too theoretical. Rewrite it with a specific community implementation scenario as the organizing example.

**Case study integration**: Do not write a separate case studies section and leave theory in other sections. Integrate case study evidence into the section where it is most relevant. The case study section at the end should provide structured summaries of each case — Cuba, Puerto Rico, guild apprenticeship, Zone 5 4-H — using a standardized format: context, skills transmitted, mechanisms used, timeline, failure modes, transferable design principles.

**Uncertainty documentation**: When you use practitioner evidence rather than peer-reviewed data for learning time estimates, document this explicitly in the text. Do not obscure the difference. A sentence like "Extension Service master gardener training data suggests 40–60 hours to competence in cold-weather food preservation techniques (practitioner estimate; no peer-reviewed equivalent available for low-resource contexts)" is stronger than citing no source at all. Honest uncertainty builds more reader trust than false precision.
