## The Domain in Depth: What You Are Researching

### The Problem This Domain Solves

Every community resilience system breaks at the same point: the moment the community needs to compensate specialized labor without market infrastructure. A medic who provides care 40 hours a week needs food. A solar technician who maintains the community microgrid needs shelter. A teacher who transmits critical skills needs clothing and tools. In the early crisis period, informal reciprocity covers this. Over months and years, informal reciprocity degrades because it cannot handle the calculation problem: how much is 40 hours of medical care worth in firewood equivalents? How do you account for the fact that three community members received care but only one provided it this month?

The communities that fail on a 1–3 year timescale after surviving the acute crisis almost always fail at this exchange coordination problem — not at the resource production problem. Phase 1–5 solves the production problem. Domain B solves the exchange coordination problem.

The research must answer three bounded questions:

**Question 1**: What economic architectures function when the dollar fails? (Local currencies, mutual credit, barter protocols, gift economies — design principles at 20–500 person scale)

**Question 2**: How do communities allocate capital and coordinate production without market signals? (Ostrom's commons governance, cooperative enterprise models, capital formation without banks, participatory budgeting, surplus management)

**Question 3**: What economic bridges prevent community isolation? (Inter-community trade networks, regional food hubs, comparative advantage without global supply chains, nested supply chains)

### Scope Boundaries

**In scope:**
- Currency and exchange mechanism design at community scale (20–500 people)
- Commons management and resource allocation without market prices
- Cooperative enterprise models (worker cooperatives, agricultural cooperatives, mutual aid)
- Capital formation without conventional banking (ROSCAs, credit unions, CDFIs, community investment)
- Supply chain resilience and regional food/goods networks
- Alternative labor compensation (timebanking, labor exchange)
- Economic governance (participatory budgeting, cooperative governance structures)
- Zone 5 Midwest implementation: cooperative tradition, credit unions, CSA networks, regional food hubs
- Case studies: WIR (Switzerland), Argentina trueque, Vermont timebanking, Zone 5 agricultural cooperative

**Hard out-of-scope (enforce actively throughout production):**
- National macroeconomic policy (Federal Reserve, inflation, national GDP)
- Cryptocurrency and blockchain as primary subjects (brief technical mention only in a currency design footnote)
- Corporate finance and investor-owned enterprise
- Ancient historical barter without modern community application
- Food production techniques (Phase 5 territory — reference it, do not re-document it)
- Governance structures for non-economic decisions (Phase 3 and Phase 6 Domain B institutional governance cover this)

**The critical scope ceiling**: You are researching communities at **20–500 person scale**. Every analysis, every design principle, every implementation recommendation must be calibrated to this scale. Mondragon (70,000 workers) and Land O'Lakes ($14B revenue) are instructive case studies, but every lesson extracted must be translated into what it means for a 200-person Zone 5 community before the section is complete.

## 15 Research Questions the Document Must Answer

These are the core research questions. The document is complete when all 15 are answered with rigor and specificity appropriate for a Zone 5 practitioner audience.

**Exchange Mechanisms:**
1. What is the documented evidence that informal barter degrades social cohesion over time in communities with specialized labor? What is the timeline (months, years) and what are the documented warning signs before breakdown?
2. What are the design criteria that differentiated the WIR franc's 90-year operational success from the Bristol Pound's 9-year lifespan? (Answer must be specific: what governance feature, what design choice, what economic mechanism?)
3. What is the minimum community size at which a local currency provides coordination efficiency over bilateral barter, and what is the academic evidence for that threshold?
4. How does mutual credit issuance work at community scale — specifically, who issues credit, how is the credit backed, what happens when a member issues credit they cannot redeem, and who adjudicates default?

**Commons and Cooperative Enterprise:**
5. Which of Ostrom's eight design principles are most commonly violated in documented commons failures, and what is the best-documented Zone 5 example of each?
6. How does a worker cooperative's general assembly make binding economic decisions — specifically, what is the governance process for decisions about investment, labor allocation during downturns, and member exclusion?
7. What Zone 5 agricultural cooperative institutions provide a governance model for community-scale economic organization, and what is the documented performance of those institutions during the 2008 financial crisis and 2020 supply chain disruption?

**Capital Formation:**
8. What is the maximum size at which an informal ROSCA (rotating savings and credit association) operates without requiring formal governance structures, and what evidence supports that threshold?
9. What USDA cooperative development programs are currently active in Zone 5, and what are the minimum requirements for a Zone 5 community to qualify for a Rural Cooperative Development Grant?

**Supply Chain and Regional Trade:**
10. What percentage of Zone 5 community caloric needs could theoretically be met by regional food production (within 200 miles), and what is the distribution infrastructure gap between current capacity and full regional self-sufficiency?
11. What is the design of a community strategic food reserve — specifically, how much storage, what food types, what rotation schedule, and what governance mechanism prevents hoarding during a drawdown event?

**Alternative Labor Compensation:**
12. What is the documented evidence on timebanking's effectiveness at compensating non-fungible skilled labor (healthcare, teaching, governance) in communities where monetary compensation is unavailable? What are the documented failure modes?
13. What specific participation barriers prevent timebanking adoption in rural communities, and what design modifications have been shown to reduce those barriers? (Use medrxiv 2025 scoping review as the primary source.)

**Zone 5 Implementation:**
14. What is the Zone 5 seasonal agricultural economy's effect on community economic system design — specifically, how do cooperative and community economic systems handle the income-concentration problem (most income arrives in fall, most input expenditure in spring)?
15. What are the two most immediately implementable community economic tools that a Zone 5 community with no prior cooperative infrastructure can begin in the first 90 days, and what is the evidence base for that recommendation?

## Analytical Framework: How to Synthesize Sources

Do not organize the document by economic school of thought. Organize by implementation level and mechanism.

**The three-layer framework:**
- Layer 1: Exchange mechanisms (currencies, barter protocols, timebanking) — the transaction layer
- Layer 2: Resource governance (commons, cooperatives, capital formation) — the allocation layer
- Layer 3: Economic institutions (cooperative enterprise, CDFIs, participatory budgeting) — the governance layer

Every section operates within one layer. When a section spans layers (currency governance spans Layer 1 and Layer 3), explicitly call out the layer shift.

**The practitioner lens**: At every point, ask: could a Zone 5 community organizer with no economics background read this section and know what to do? If the answer is no, the section is too theoretical. Rewrite with a specific Zone 5 implementation scenario as the organizing example. The community in your examples should be 200 people, Zone 5 climate, mixed rural-suburban, with a functioning agricultural cooperative and no conventional banking access.

**Scale calibration**: Every principle must be calibrated to 20–500 person scale before the section is complete. Mondragon's governance structure is instructive; translate it: "A 200-person Zone 5 community using the Mondragon three-body model would have a General Assembly of all 200 adult members meeting twice yearly, a Governing Council of 7 elected members meeting monthly, and a Social Council of 5 worker representatives meeting monthly to monitor labor conditions." That specificity is what makes the document usable.

**Uncertainty documentation**: When you use practitioner evidence rather than peer-reviewed data, document this explicitly. A sentence like "The Argentina trueque network peak participation ranges from 2.5–7 million depending on source methodology; the conservative peer-reviewed floor is 2.5M (ResearchGate academic paper, 2011)" is stronger than citing no source. Honest uncertainty builds more practitioner trust than false precision.

**Case study integration**: Do not write a separate case studies section at the end and leave theory in earlier sections. Integrate case study evidence into the sections where it is most relevant. The structured case study summaries in Section 10 should be brief (300–400 words each) summaries using the standardized format: context → economic mechanism → governance structure → outcomes → failure modes → transferable design principles.
