## Executive Summary

Three domains compete for Phase 6 production: Community Economic Resilience (A), Intergenerational Skills Development (C), and Multi-Community Governance Scaling (D). Each closes a real gap in the existing corpus. The question is not which one matters — all three do — but which produces the best return on a 13-week production window given available source material and schedule risk.

**The pre-scoring positions Domain A as the lowest-risk, highest-confidence single-domain selection.** It has a 6,800-word pre-research chapter already written, the highest source readiness (75–80%), the tightest scope boundaries, and a live policy window: the USDA Rural Cooperative Development Grant cycle opens fall 2026, and communities that have the Domain A framework in hand by August 30 can apply. A solo Domain A run carries 95% on-schedule confidence.

**Domain D carries the strongest urgency argument** — the post-COVID governance failure documentation window is active now and closes by 2027–2028 — but its 78% on-schedule confidence and lowest source readiness (65–70%) make it the riskiest single-domain option. The case for Domain D is compelling but contingent on production capacity and, ideally, a co-author with political science or community organizing background.

**Domain C has the highest long-term leverage** (knowledge transmission affects every Phase 5 document on a generational timescale) but also the weakest urgency argument. No specific policy window closes if it starts in September. It is the strongest candidate for a Phase 6b start.

**If selecting two domains**, the A+D pairing captures the maximum strategic value and both live policy windows, at 85% confidence for both completing by August 23. The A+C pairing is the safest two-domain option at 90% confidence with low coordination overhead. The C+D pairing is high-leverage but the highest-risk two-domain combination.

One candidate has sufficiently superior fit on schedule confidence and source readiness to merit a clear recommendation: **if production capacity is limited to one domain, Domain A is the recommended selection.** If two domains are feasible, **A+D** is the recommended pairing. The reasoning below supports your own scoring and weighting if you disagree with either recommendation.

## Risk Assessment Per Candidate

### Domain A — Community Economic Resilience

**Schedule risk: LOW**

- Completion confidence: **95%** by August 9, 2026
- Production hours: 20–24
- Pre-research chapter (6,800 words, 38 verified citations) eliminates Week 1 cold-start risk
- Source library 75–80% confirmed; verification, not discovery, is the primary Week 1 task
- Final delivery August 9 leaves three weeks before August 30 if anything slips

**Source gaps**: Quantitative impact data for timebanking is thin — qualitative case studies are available but academic rigor is limited. This is a refinement gap, explicitly documentable with honest-uncertainty language. It does not block production.

**Scope-creep likelihood: LOW**. The out-of-scope list (no national monetary policy, no monetary history before the 20th century, no corporate finance) is simple to apply. The main expansion risk is too many international case studies; a soft ceiling of 4–6 cases handles it. The three core research questions are self-limiting.

**Blocking dependency**: none. No co-authoring required. No paywalled source critical to production. Full production can begin June 8 after a one-week source library confirmation.

### Domain C — Intergenerational Skills Development

**Schedule risk: MODERATE**

- Completion confidence: **88%** by August 23, 2026 (92% with adult education background author)
- Production hours: 22–27
- No pre-research chapter — adds approximately 4–6 hours and shifts production start to June 10–12
- The skill inventory section (12–15 critical skills with documented learning curves and prerequisites) is the highest-risk component: trade learning curve data for low-resource settings exists primarily as practitioner reports, not peer-reviewed studies

**Source gaps**: Quantitative learning curve data for specific trades (well-drilling, solar installation, emergency medical response) in low-resource settings is the main gap. Intergenerational teaching dynamics in North American contexts are underresearched compared to European apprenticeship literature. Both gaps are documentable with explicit uncertainty framing; neither blocks production.

**Scope-creep likelihood: MODERATE**. Educational research is expansive. The domain can drift into K-12 curriculum design, neuroscience of learning, or technology-mediated learning — none of which serve the core questions. The mitigation is a hard ceiling of 12–15 critical skills and explicit out-of-scope language. Requires active monitoring; not passive.

**Blocking dependency**: none. No co-authoring required. No specific external deadline. The 88% confidence figure assumes a research agent without specialized adult education background; 92% with one.

### Domain D — Multi-Community Governance Scaling

**Schedule risk: HIGH**

- Completion confidence: **78%** solo; **82%** with political science or community organizing background co-author
- Production hours: 28–35 (the widest range of the three candidates — a 7-hour variance reflects genuine uncertainty)
- No pre-research chapter; extended source library compilation required through June 15 (versus June 7–8 for Domain A)
- Production does not begin until June 15–22, leaving only 9–10 productive writing weeks before the August 30 deadline

**Source gaps (real, not refinement-level)**:
1. Governance failure case studies in resource-constrained settings are underreported in academic literature. Success stories are published; failure documentation is suppressed. Active search beyond standard academic databases is required.
2. Consensus decision-making at 300+ person scale has limited documented examples — most case studies are under 200 people. Synthesis from multiple smaller cases may be the only available path.
3. Institutional learning mechanisms in small community settings are frontier research without a single authoritative synthesis. The author must build this from adjacent literature.

These are not refinement gaps. They are real source discovery challenges that require non-trivial research time before writing begins.

**Scope-creep likelihood: MODERATE-HIGH**. Political science and institutional change theory are among the most expansive bodies of academic literature. The "150–500 person federation" boundary is clear in principle but requires active enforcement. Weekly scope audits at Weeks 3, 6, and 9 are specified in the production timeline — not as a best practice but as a requirement.

**Co-authoring dependency**: Domain D has the highest benefit from co-authoring of the three candidates. Without a co-author, the 78% confidence figure applies. A political science or community organizing background author cuts 5–8 hours off the estimate and materially reduces scope-creep risk.

**If selecting Domain D**: starting June 1 is essential. A September start produces an uncertain December finish and a tight or missed distribution window.

## Two-Domain Pairing Analysis

### Pairing A + D: Economic Resilience + Governance Scaling

**Feasibility: HIGH**
**Production confidence: 85% both complete by August 23, 2026**
**Strategic value: HIGHEST of any two-domain option**

Economic and governance structures are tightly coupled — economic models constrain what governance structures are feasible, and governance structures determine what economic models are sustainable. Producing both together allows a 3,000–5,000 word co-authored integration section documenting how they co-evolve. This integration section would be the most novel analytical contribution of Phase 6.

**Coordination structure:**
- Domain A begins full production June 8–10; Domain D completes extended source library work through June 15–22
- Domain A's early completion (draft by July 13) provides context to Domain D for the federation section
- Two orchestrator syncs required: June 22 (economic-governance interface framing) and July 5 (inter-community trade to federation section)
- Integration section co-authored August 10–15 after both primary documents are draft-complete

**Interaction overhead: MODERATE**. Two syncs plus co-authored integration section. Neither agent is blocked by the other. If Domain D slips, Domain A still completes on schedule.

**Resource requirement**: two simultaneous research agents. The Domain D agent ideally has political science or community organizing background.

**Key risk**: Domain D's 78% solo confidence means a 22% chance of missing August 30. Domain A's completion is not at risk in this scenario — only the pairing's integrated delivery date slips.

### Pairing A + C: Economic Resilience + Skills Development

**Feasibility: VERY HIGH**
**Production confidence: 90% both complete by August 23, 2026**
**Interaction overhead: LOWEST of any two-domain option**

The two domains are complementary but not structurally dependent. Economic systems define which skills have exchange value; skill transmission depends on economic compensation. The connection is real but loose — each document is coherent without the other.

**Coordination structure:**
- Both source libraries compiled in parallel June 1–7
- Both agents work nearly independently June 8 through August
- One orchestrator sync June 21 (15 minutes; confirm no scope overlap)
- Light cross-review August 10–13 (4 hours each agent)
- Integration deliverable: 1,500–2,000 word note on the economic-skills interface

**Interaction overhead: LOW**. One sync, one integration note, no blocking dependencies.

**Key risk**: Domain C's skill inventory section (trade learning curve data) is the main uncertainty. If practitioner evidence is thinner than expected, the section requires explicit uncertainty framing — additional writing time, not a blocking gap. Domain A is unaffected.

**What this pairing does not do**: it does not capture Domain D's governance window. If the post-COVID governance failure documentation is a priority for Phase 6, this pairing defers it.

### Pairing C + D: Skills Development + Governance Scaling

**Feasibility: MODERATE**
**Production confidence: 82% both complete by August 30, 2026**
**Interaction overhead: MODERATE**

The conceptual coupling is genuine — decisions about what to teach, who teaches, and how to validate competence are governance questions. Educational governance is a direct subset of community governance design.

**The compounding risk** is the problem. Both domains have moderate-to-high scope expansion risk. Domain D has the lowest source readiness of any candidate. Running both without Domain A means losing the pre-research chapter's production advantage and the USDA grant window. This pairing makes sense only if Domain A is explicitly deferred to Phase 6b — a choice with real costs (the fall 2026 cooperative development grant cycle is missed).

**Coordination structure**: similar to A+D but with higher scope-management overhead. Weekly scope audits are required for both agents, not just Domain D. Both agents benefit from relevant domain expertise.

**Recommended only if**: both agents have political science and adult education backgrounds, and the orchestrator can provide active weekly scope oversight. Not recommended for a solo agent running both concurrently.

## Zone 5 Applicability Assessment

All three candidates have genuine, direct Zone 5 applicability — this is not a differentiating factor. The differences are in how central Midwest examples are to the core argument:

### Domain A — Zone 5 applicability: PRIMARY SOURCES

The Midwest cooperative tradition is the strongest regional foundation of any candidate. Agricultural cooperatives (Land O'Lakes, Cenex, Organic Valley), food cooperatives, credit unions, and USDA cooperative development infrastructure are not illustrative examples — they are the primary case study material. Zone 5 communities are not being asked to adapt international models; they are being asked to recognize and build on institutions they already have. The USDA Rural Cooperative Development Grant is specifically accessible to Zone 5 communities, and application support from extension offices is already available.

**Midwest application readiness**: very high. The institutions described in Domain A already exist in Zone 5. The domain's function is to help communities understand, use, and extend them — not to transplant foreign models.

### Domain C — Zone 5 applicability: STRONG EXISTING INFRASTRUCTURE

4-H operates in Zone 5 with 6.8 million youth participants. Cooperative Extension has served Zone 5 adult farmers for a century using pedagogical methods that are field-tested in Midwest agricultural conditions. The Amish knowledge transmission literature (one of Domain C's core sources) is directly applicable to Zone 5 rural contexts. COVID-19 disruptions to formal education created visible demand for alternative skill-development models in Zone 5 communities specifically.

**Midwest application readiness**: high. 4-H and Extension provide the implementation infrastructure — Domain C provides the theoretical synthesis and adaptation framework for resilience-specific skill transmission. Zone 5 communities do not start from zero.

### Domain D — Zone 5 applicability: ACTIVE LIVING LABORATORIES

Agricultural cooperative federations (Land O'Lakes, Organic Valley), rural water authorities, county-level soil and water conservation districts, and township governance all operate as multi-stakeholder federations in Zone 5. They are living implementations of the exact governance problems Domain D addresses. The Swiss cantonal system is the long-running theoretical validation; Midwest agricultural cooperatives are the locally familiar analogues.

**Midwest application readiness**: high but more abstract than Domain A. The governance structures Domain D proposes require communities to be actively grappling with scaling problems — which Zone 5 cooperative federations are, but which individual small communities may not yet be. Domain D's Zone 5 value increases as communities reach the 50–150 person threshold where governance structures designed for 20 people begin to fail.

**Zone 5 verdict**: For communities at early organization stages, Domain A has the most immediate practical value. For communities at scaling inflection points (50–150+ people, federating with neighbors), Domain D is directly relevant. Domain C applies continuously across all community sizes and time horizons. If your primary Zone 5 audience is early-stage organizers, A is the highest-value selection. If it includes communities already federating at county scale, D becomes more valuable.

## Appendix: Scoring Criterion Definitions

For reference when completing Part 3 of the selection framework with your own weights.

**Urgency (1–5)**: Does starting June 2026 versus September meaningfully change value delivered? 5 = must start June or a specific opportunity closes. 1 = evergreen, defer 6+ months with minimal cost.

**Source Readiness (1–5)**: How quickly can full production begin? 5 = 85%+ sources confirmed, production starts June 5–7. 1 = under 50% ready, substantial discovery before writing.

**Leverage (1–5)**: How much does this domain amplify Phase 1–5 and enable Phase 7+ work? 5 = all other Phase 6 domains connect to this. 1 = standalone value only.

**Scope Fit (1–5)**: How tightly bounded is the research scope? 5 = scope is unambiguous and self-limiting. 1 = many possible directions, constant boundary management required.

**Demand / Zone 5 Applicability (1–5)**: How strong is evidence that Zone 5 communities need this? 5 = community organizations actively requesting it; Midwest examples are primary sources. 1 = demand real but niche.
