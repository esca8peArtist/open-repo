## Domain Scoring

Each domain scored 1-5 on five criteria (definitions below). Scores use the same framework as the Phase 6 Domain Selection Framework (May 2026).

**Scoring criteria:**

- **Urgency**: Does a November 4 start versus deferral to 2027 meaningfully change value delivered? 5 = specific opportunity closes; 1 = evergreen.
- **Source Readiness**: How quickly can full production begin given existing pre-research? 5 = 85%+ sources identified; 1 = under 50% ready.
- **Leverage**: How much does this domain amplify Phase 1-5 work and enable downstream content? 5 = foundational for other domains; 1 = standalone.
- **Scope Fit**: How tightly bounded is the research scope? 5 = three questions, unambiguous boundaries; 1 = requires constant boundary management.
- **Audience Demand**: How strong is evidence that the target audience needs this? 5 = organizations actively requesting it; 1 = niche interest.

## Domain Profiles

### Domain A — Community Economic Resilience (Published)

**Status**: Published. 6,800 words. Not a candidate for the November production window — included for reference as the anchor document.

**Role in Phase 6**: Establishes the economic exchange layer that all other domains require. The November domains should cross-reference Domain A's cooperative, mutual credit, and complementary currency frameworks.

---

### Domain B — Fiscal Architecture and Community Finance

**Research complexity**: 16-20 hours of focused research-writing. Five research zones mapped: revenue architecture, capital formation and asset protection, participatory fiscal governance, inter-community financial flows, and fiscal resilience under shock.

**Community interest signals**: Moderate-to-high. USDA cooperative development programs are actively subscribed. Community Development Financial Institutions (CDFIs) serve over 100 million Americans. However, the specific fiscal architecture questions (participatory budgeting at sub-municipal scale, inter-community lending governance) have thinner community demand signals than Domain G.

**Prerequisite knowledge**: Domain A provides the economic operating system; Domain B adds the financial architecture layer. Ideally starts after Domain A is complete (it is). Can proceed independently for the November window.

**Publication pathway**: Policy makers, local government administrators, cooperative development practitioners, community land trust managers. Narrow but high-impact audience.

**November feasibility**: Feasible in 6 weeks if scope is held to three or four of the five research zones. Full five-zone treatment requires 8-10 weeks.

---

### Domain C — Political Governance (Formal Structure and Legitimate Authority)

**Research complexity**: 18-22 hours. Five research zones: foundations of legitimate authority, constitutional design, electoral and representative mechanisms, rights without state enforcement, external political relations.

**Community interest signals**: Strong. The 2025-2026 political environment has produced visible demand for community-level governance frameworks that do not depend on federal or state institutions. Transition Town networks (992 groups globally as of 2024) and intentional community governance literature indicate active practitioner interest.

**Prerequisite knowledge**: Conceptually prior to Domain E (multilateral coordination) and Domain D (dispute resolution). Can proceed independently of other domains.

**Publication pathway**: Activist and organizer networks, intentional community practitioners, local government reformers, academic political science.

**November feasibility**: Tight but possible in 6 weeks if restricted to three of the five zones. Full treatment requires 10-12 weeks.

---

### Domain D — Dispute Resolution and Community Justice

**Research complexity**: 14-18 hours. Five research zones: dispute typology, traditional and indigenous models, restorative justice design, arbiter selection, enforcement without coercive power.

**Community interest signals**: Moderate. Conflict resolution demand is evergreen but dispute resolution specifically (as opposed to broader community cohesion) has a narrower direct audience. Best suited as a companion to Domain C rather than a standalone domain.

**Prerequisite knowledge**: Ideally follows Domain C (Political Governance), as community justice depends on established governance structure. Not blocking — can proceed independently.

**Publication pathway**: Mediators, community organizers, restorative justice practitioners, legal aid organizations.

**November feasibility**: Achievable in 6 weeks. The narrower scope (compared to C or G) makes this the most producible standalone domain in the window.

---

### Domain E — Multilateral Coordination (Inter-Community Alliance)

**Research complexity**: 15-19 hours. Five research zones: coordination architecture, shared infrastructure governance, mutual aid pact design, conflict between allied communities, external institutional relations.

**Community interest signals**: Lower than other domains. The audience (communities actively federating with neighbors) is a subset of Phase 5's already-engaged readership. A community must reach organizational maturity before multilateral coordination becomes a live question.

**Prerequisite knowledge**: Strongest sequential dependency of any domain — ideally follows Domains B and C. Producing Domain E without B and C requires significant standalone framing.

**Publication pathway**: Regional resilience networks, emergency management practitioners, inter-municipal cooperation specialists.

**November feasibility**: Possible in 6 weeks at reduced scope (3 zones), but the domain's value increases substantially if Domains B and C have preceded it.

---

### Domain F — Implementation Feasibility (Transition Pathways)

**Research complexity**: 12-16 hours. Five research zones: transition pathway typology, pre-implementation assessment, pilot design and scale-up, failure mode analysis, Zone 5 implementation context.

**Community interest signals**: High. Implementation feasibility is the most universal need across Phase 5's readership — any community that has engaged with Phase 3-5 material and wants to act needs this. "How do we actually start?" is the most frequently asked question in resilience organizing contexts.

**Prerequisite knowledge**: Designed to be produced last, as a synthesis of all other Phase 6 domains. However, Zone F1-F3 (transition typology, assessment, pilot design) can be produced independently and are not blocked by other domains. Zone F5 (Zone 5 context) is directly applicable to Phase 5's target audience.

**Publication pathway**: The broadest publication pathway in Phase 6. Applicable to all Phase 3-5 audiences: community organizers, household practitioners, policy makers, extension service networks.

**November feasibility**: 6 weeks is sufficient for a partial treatment (3-4 zones). Full treatment (all 5 zones, synthesizing all prior Phase 6 domains) requires 10+ weeks and benefits from Domains A-E being complete.

---

### Domain G — Democracy Tools (NEW)

**Research complexity**: 12-15 hours for a tightly scoped treatment (4 research zones; see detailed outline in `DEMOCRACY_TOOLS_RESEARCH_OUTLINE.md`). The research literature on voter registration barriers, civic technology, and international models is well-documented and highly accessible. Key databases (Brennan Center, MIT Election Lab, National Conference of State Legislatures) are open access.

**Community interest signals**: Highest of any Phase 6 domain. The 2026 midterm election cycle and the post-Callais v. Louisiana environment (April 29, 2026: Supreme Court eviscerated Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act in a 6-3 ruling) have produced acute, urgent demand. Organizations including the ACLU ($24.5 million voter protection commitment for 2026), Protect The Vote 2026 (targeting 500,000 volunteers), and the Brennan Center (monthly state voting law roundups through 2026) indicate the field is in active mobilization. The SAVE America Act's House passage in February 2026 — which would require proof of citizenship documentation affecting an estimated 21 million eligible voters — increases the urgency of voter registration guidance.

**Prerequisite knowledge**: Standalone domain. Does not require completion of any prior Phase 6 domain. Phase 5's community resilience framework provides thematic context (civic participation as resilience infrastructure) but is not technically prerequisite.

**Publication pathway**: Broadest audience of any Phase 6 domain. Primary: voting rights organizations, civic action networks, community organizers, libraries and civic education programs. Secondary: academic political science, policy makers, election administrators. International models section is relevant to democracy researchers globally.

**November feasibility**: High confidence for a 6-week window. Source readiness is approximately 65-70% (strong foundation, needs supplementation on technology solutions and international models). The scoped research outline (4 zones, 15 research questions) is completable in 6 weeks by a solo researcher. See detailed timeline in `PHASE_6_RESEARCH_TIMELINE_AND_CAPACITY.md`.

**Urgency score 5 rationale**: The 2026 midterm election is November 3, 2026 — one day before the proposed research launch. Distribution of research findings December 12-20 positions the content for January-February 2027 organizing cycles, which are the primary planning windows for 2028 presidential election voter registration campaigns. The post-Callais redistricting chaos (multiple states initiating redistricting in summer-fall 2026) and the SAVE Act's ongoing Senate maneuvering create a documentation and guidance window that narrows rapidly as the 2026 post-election landscape crystallizes.

## Recommendation: 2-3 Primary Phase 6 Domains

**Primary recommendation: Domain G (Democracy Tools) + Domain F (Implementation Feasibility)**

**Rationale:**

Domain G is the highest-urgency, highest-demand domain in the Phase 6 candidate pool. Its November 4 research launch aligns precisely with the post-midterm organizing window. Its audience is the broadest available, and its source readiness is sufficient for 6-week production.

Domain F is the highest-leverage completion domain. It synthesizes Phase 3-6 into actionable transition guidance — the "how to start" document that Phase 5's readership most frequently requests. Its partial production (Zones F1-F3 and F5) is achievable in 6 weeks without waiting for all prior Phase 6 domains. If Domains G and F launch together, the combined publication package addresses both the political moment (G) and the practical "what now" question (F).

**Secondary recommendation: Domain C (Political Governance)** if two-domain concurrent production proves feasible or if a second researcher is available. Domain C provides the governance architecture that makes Democracy Tools effective at community scale — the two domains are complementary. A community that understands both voter participation barriers (G) and community governance design (C) is better positioned for 2028 mobilization than one that has only either.

**Domains to defer to Phase 6b (2027 launch):** Domains B, D, and E. Domain B (Fiscal Architecture) is evergreen. Domain D (Dispute Resolution) is best produced after Domain C. Domain E (Multilateral Coordination) benefits from all prior domains being complete and has the lowest standalone demand signal.

## Phase 6 Architecture Consistency

The November 4 launch maintains Phase 3-5 architectural consistency:
- Research/analysis document model (not local tool deployment — consistent with user rejection of local Pi server)
- GitHub Pages publication (consistent with existing platform)
- Zone 5 (upper Midwest) primary audience with broader applicability framing
- 45,000-55,000 word target per domain (consistent with Phase 6 Domain A and Phase 5 documents)
- 120-150 citation target (consistent with existing Phase 6 standards)

Democracy Tools (Domain G) departs slightly from Phase 5's community resilience framing in one respect: voter registration and ballot guidance have a national and state-law dimension that food preservation and water systems do not. The Zone 5 section will need to address specific state laws in Iowa, Minnesota, Wisconsin, Illinois, and Missouri — a more legally specific adaptation than prior domains. This is a feature, not a complication; Zone 5 readers need state-specific guidance, and the research will provide it.
