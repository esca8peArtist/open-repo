# Water Systems Resilience — Comprehensive Research Outline

## Executive Summary

The most important finding from this research: **the existing Phase 5 Wave 3 water document is technically sound for individual and household treatment/storage but has three critical gaps that could undermine community-scale implementation**. First, it does not address greywater and blackwater system design — communities that manage water in without managing wastewater out will contaminate their own sources within months. Second, it does not address water governance frameworks for when water becomes scarce — the social infrastructure is as load-bearing as the physical infrastructure. Third, it does not integrate the specific climate trajectory for Zone 5, which shows a counterintuitive pattern: increasing annual precipitation combined with intensifying summer drought, meaning communities must plan simultaneously for surplus capture and dry-season storage at a scale the existing document does not specify.

The Kansas LEMA model — where community-designed water use reduction of 25-31% stabilized a declining aquifer without reducing agricultural yields — is the single most transferable governance precedent for Zone 5 communities establishing shared water systems. Combined with Ostrom's eight design principles (applied to groundwater in Southern California's Raymond Basin, now formalized in California's SGMA), this gives communities a field-tested governance framework before their first water conflict, not after.

## Section 1: Zone 5 Baseline — Groundwater, Precipitation, and Climate Trajectory

### 1.1 Precipitation Baseline and Seasonal Distribution

Zone 5 Midwest annual precipitation ranges from 27 inches (Minnesota) to 42 inches (Indiana), with Illinois averaging 36 inches, Iowa 34 inches, and Wisconsin 32 inches [NCA5 Chapter 24]. This is adequate for household rainwater self-sufficiency on most properties — a 1,500 sq ft roof in Illinois collects approximately 26,000 gallons per year at average rainfall, substantially exceeding annual drinking water needs for a family of four at 2 gallons per person per day (2,920 gallons) while being insufficient for combined drinking, sanitation, and irrigation at full household function.

**The critical Zone 5 seasonal distribution problem**: Annual precipitation totals are deceptive. Zone 5 receives approximately:
- 35-40% of annual precipitation as winter precipitation (November-March), mostly snow
- 30-35% in spring (April-June), with the highest intensity events
- 20-25% in summer (July-September), often as intense convective storms
- 10-15% in fall (October-November)

The summer months — when water demand for irrigation, livestock, and human comfort is highest — are the driest months. In an average Zone 5 year, July and August combined deliver approximately 5-7 inches of rain, far below the 10-15 gallons/person/day (with irrigation) that a productive homestead requires. Storage systems must be sized to bridge the June 15-September 15 deficit using water collected during spring and stored.

**Snowmelt as a primary collection opportunity**: Zone 5 receives 20-50 inches of annual snowfall (Wisconsin north: 50+ inches; central Illinois: 20-25 inches). Snowmelt in March-April is the highest-volume single collection event of the year. A cistern system designed to capture snowmelt via roof runoff and directed surface flow in March-April can fill a 2,000-gallon system in 2-3 snowmelt events. Snowmelt water quality is generally excellent — low in dissolved minerals, low in biological contamination unless it has contacted animal or vehicle surfaces — and requires only sediment filtration and basic disinfection before storage.

**Extreme precipitation events**: Zone 5 receives the majority of its most intense rainfall in convective summer storms (3-5 inches in 24 hours is a 2-5 year event in most Zone 5 counties). These events can fill large cisterns rapidly but also drive turbid, contaminated runoff. First-flush diverter capacity should be sized for these extreme events, not average events — specify diverters that can handle at least 2 gallons per 100 sq ft of roof area, not the standard 1 gallon per 100 sq ft.

### 1.2 Climate Trajectory: The Wet-Dry Paradox

The Fifth National Climate Assessment (NCA5), Chapter 24 documents a Zone 5 paradox that fundamentally shapes water resilience planning [NCA5 2023]:

- Annual precipitation has **increased 5-15%** across the Midwest 1992-2021 relative to the 1901-1960 baseline
- Projections show annual precipitation increasing **8-20% by midcentury** under intermediate to high emissions scenarios
- **However**: summer precipitation is projected to *decrease* and become more variable
- Rapid transitions between precipitation extremes (from flood conditions to drought within a single season) are expected to increase across all of Zone 5 by late century
- Flash droughts — rapid-onset droughts that develop within 2-5 weeks — are increasing in frequency

**Practical implication for water planning**: Communities should plan for wetter winters, springs, and falls combined with drier, more variable summers. This argues for maximum-capacity spring capture systems (to fill large storage), not just average-year sizing. The planning scenario is not "average year minus 20%" — it is "excellent spring followed by 12-week summer drought followed by October flooding."

### 1.3 Groundwater Availability in Zone 5

Zone 5 sits above several significant groundwater systems, with highly variable accessibility by subregion:

**Illinois**: Multiple distinct aquifer systems. The Cambrian-Ordovician aquifer system underlies most of the state at 200-800+ feet depth — a reliable confined aquifer with artesian pressure in many areas. Shallow glacial drift aquifers (30-150 feet) provide water to most existing domestic wells but are more vulnerable to agricultural contamination. Northern Illinois (DuPage, Kane, McHenry counties) faces genuine supply stress: population growth has drawn down the sand-and-gravel aquifers significantly since the 1950s, with some municipalities having switched entirely to Lake Michigan water [ISGS Geological Records].

**Indiana**: Glacial drift aquifers dominate. The north-central Indiana region shows notable depletion under irrigated agriculture pressure [Drought.gov Midwest Update March 2026]. Most domestic wells in Indiana draw from 50-200 foot glacial sand and gravel deposits. Bedrock aquifers (Silurian dolomite, Devonian limestone) are accessible at 200-400 feet in central Indiana.

**Iowa**: Iowa sits above the highly productive Dakota Aquifer (confined sandstone aquifer at 200-1,000 feet depth in western Iowa) and extensive glacial drift aquifers. The primary rural domestic well source is glacial drift at 50-200 feet. Iowa's deeper bedrock aquifers (Devonian, Silurian) are mineralized and often require treatment.

**Wisconsin**: The Eastern Dolomite aquifer underlies most of eastern Wisconsin at 50-300 feet and provides high-quality water to most rural domestic wells. The Central Sands region (Adams, Portage, Waushara counties) faces significant irrigation-induced groundwater depletion — a documented case of community-scale over-extraction that has reduced stream baseflows and lake levels. This is Wisconsin's most active groundwater governance conflict and provides a direct regional precedent [Wisconsin DNR Groundwater Data].

**Michigan**: Michigan sits above the Michigan Basin bedrock aquifer system and extensive glaciofluvial deposits. Most rural wells draw from 60-200 foot drift aquifers. The state has experienced limited groundwater depletion due to lower irrigation pressure but is actively monitoring potential impacts from large-scale water transfers and industrial withdrawal.

**Well drilling costs (2025-2026 data)**: Zone 5 well drilling averages $28-55 per linear foot for standard residential wells in glacial terrain. A 100-foot well costs approximately $5,000-$8,500 total installed (drilling plus casing, pump, pressure tank, electrical connection). A 200-foot well runs $8,000-$15,000. Wisconsin wells average $7,350 for wells averaging 145 feet deep. Illinois residential wells range from $10,000-$30,000 depending on depth and geology [WellDrillingCosts.com 2026; SCWellService 2026].

### 1.4 Historical Drought and Flood Case Studies

**The 2012 Drought — Zone 5's Reference Catastrophe**

The 2012 drought was the most severe Midwest drought since the 1988 drought and affected the full Zone 5 region. Peak conditions in August 2012: 35% of the Midwest in extreme drought, 7% in exceptional drought (D4, the highest category). Missouri, Illinois, Indiana, and Kentucky experienced the most severe impacts.

Specific documented impacts [Illinois DNR 2012 Drought Report; Illinois State Water Survey]:
- At peak in August 2012, most shallow groundwater wells in Illinois showed steep declines beginning mid-winter to late spring
- Six Illinois streamflow monitoring stations set new record lows; 17 tied record lows; the Sangamon River at Monticello set its lowest 7-day flow in 105 years of record
- Municipal water supply shortages were threatened in dozens of small Illinois municipalities dependent on surface water or shallow wells
- The crisis was partially relieved by remnants of Hurricane Isaac producing abundant rainfall in late August 2012 — a fortuitous intervention that would not recur in a true prolonged drought scenario

**What worked in 2012**: Communities with diversified water sources (well plus surface water plus municipal connection) maintained supply. Communities with only one source — particularly those dependent on surface reservoirs that dropped to critically low levels — faced rationing or emergency water hauling.

**What failed in 2012**: Single-source municipal systems dependent on shallow reservoirs. Small towns on the upper Sangamon River watershed in Illinois faced the most acute pressure. The 2012 event prompted NIDIS to establish the Midwest Drought Early Warning System (Midwest DEWS) specifically because the region lacked adequate monitoring and response infrastructure.

**The Kansas LEMA Response (Applicable Governance Model)**

In response to mounting High Plains Aquifer depletion (a slower-moving but structurally analogous crisis to what Zone 5 shallow aquifers face under intensified irrigation), Kansas enacted legislation in 2012 establishing Local Enhanced Management Areas. The Sheridan County #6 LEMA, implemented 2013-2017, achieved [KGS Kansas Geological Survey; Stanford Water in the West]:
- 31.2% reduction in water use on irrigated corn
- Water-level decline rates reduced by approximately 65%
- No notable reduction in crop production
- Achieved through community-designed allocation system with enforceable annual pumping limits

The Wichita County LEMA (active 2021) targeted 25% reduction and achieved 40% reduction. These results demonstrate that community-managed water allocation — when designed by the users, not imposed from outside — consistently exceeds reduction targets while maintaining productive use.

**The 2019 Midwest Flooding — Flood Zone Water Quality**

March 2019 precipitation and snowmelt produced catastrophic flooding across western Iowa, Nebraska, and Missouri, with the Missouri River flooding reaching levels not seen since 1952 [Wikipedia 2019 Midwest Floods; American Rivers 2019]. Documented Zone 5 impacts:
- 56,000 acres submerged in Atchison County, Missouri, some remaining underwater through December 2019
- 166 homes flooded in Atchison County, 278 residents evacuated
- Municipal water quality compromised in multiple river communities as floodwaters overwhelmed treatment systems
- Agricultural drainage networks (tile drains) — which cover approximately 35% of Zone 5 cropland — accelerated runoff and increased flood peaks

**Flood-zone water management lessons**:
- Surface water and shallow wells near flooded areas must be considered compromised until tested post-flood
- The Nature Conservancy's levee setback projects in Missouri (farmers advocated for setbacks at November 2019 meetings) demonstrated that working-with-the-river approaches reduced flood damage in subsequent events versus fight-the-river levee strategies
- Floodplain buyout programs (FEMA HMGP) enabled strategic property acquisitions that converted repetitively flooded land to natural floodplain — the most durable long-term flood management approach, though requiring pre-disaster planning and multi-year implementation [FEMA HMGP Case Studies; Headwaters Economics 2019]

## Section 2: Water Sourcing Strategies

### 2.1 Well Drilling — The Gold Standard for Zone 5

A properly constructed deep well accessing a confined aquifer is the most reliable off-grid water source in Zone 5. Confined aquifers maintain year-round temperature stability, natural pressure, and physical isolation from surface contamination. A well that accesses the Cambrian-Ordovician system in central Illinois or the Eastern Dolomite in Wisconsin provides water that is inherently protected from agricultural runoff, drought-induced surface contamination, and most emergency contamination scenarios.

**Rural landowner decision framework**:
- Properties with existing wells: assess depth, aquifer accessed, yield rate, and current pump type. If yield is 2+ gpm and depth is 100+ feet in a confined aquifer, the primary investment is a hand pump backup ($300-900 installed alongside the electric submersible, brands: Bison, Simple Pump, Flojak) and annual water quality testing
- Properties without wells: obtain well records from neighboring properties via USGS National Water Information System and state geological surveys before drilling. Well records predict likely depth, aquifer, and yield with reasonable accuracy at minimal cost before committing to $8,000-30,000 drilling investment
- High nitrate risk areas: shallow wells (under 100 feet) in corn/soybean intensive landscapes in central Illinois, Indiana, and Iowa are high risk for nitrate contamination above MCL (10 mg/L). Test before investing; if already >8 mg/L, plan for either deeper well or rainwater as primary drinking source

**Suburban homeowner decision framework**:
- Urban and suburban areas are typically not permitted to drill new residential wells where municipal water is available (varies by municipality; check local health department before planning)
- Existing suburban wells: many suburban properties in the Zone 5 collar counties (counties adjacent to Chicago, Indianapolis, Detroit, Milwaukee) have older shallow wells from pre-municipal-water era — many capped but structurally intact. Assess feasibility of bringing back into service with hand pump
- Alternative: focus on rainwater cistern as backup and emergency source, with municipal water as primary

### 2.2 Spring Development

Natural springs in Zone 5 represent artesian groundwater under pressure reaching the surface. Where they exist on or near property, springs offer gravity-fed water delivery without pumping costs or electricity dependency.

**Spring identification**: Springs typically emerge at geological contact zones — where permeable rock or gravel overlies impermeable clay or bedrock, forcing groundwater to the surface. In Zone 5, springs are most common in areas with thin till over fractured bedrock (parts of southwestern Wisconsin, southeastern Minnesota, southern Indiana limestone karst region, and the hilly terrain of western Illinois).

**Spring box construction standards** [Penn State Extension; NC State Extension; SSCEH Spring Development Guidelines]:
- Concrete box: 4x4 ft footprint minimum, 4-6 inch poured concrete walls reinforced with 3/8-inch rebar, waterproof to prevent surface water infiltration
- Interior gravel filter: 1-2 ft layer of washed gravel surrounds spring emergence point, preventing sediment from entering collection pool
- Outlet pipe at 5-10 cm below overflow level; cleanout drain at bottom
- U-shaped surface diversion ditch at minimum 50 feet uphill to redirect surface runoff away from spring
- Tight-fitting insulated lid — critical for Zone 5 winter; exposed spring boxes can freeze if not insulated or buried at or below frost line
- Sanitary seal: grout the top 2 feet of spring box walls with bentonite clay to prevent surface water infiltration

**Minimum viable yield**: Springs intended as primary water supply should yield minimum 2 gallons per minute (2,880 gallons/day) year-round. Many Zone 5 springs have variable yield, with highest flow in spring (snowmelt recharge) and lowest in late summer drought. Test flow rate in August, not March, to assess worst-case yield [Minnesota Dept. of Health Springs FAQ].

**Spring protection zone**: Protect the recharge area — the higher ground that feeds the spring — from contamination sources. A 100-foot no-contamination radius around the spring box is standard; the recharge area itself may extend hundreds of yards uphill on the same property or onto neighboring land. In community contexts, spring recharge protection is a governance issue requiring inter-parcel agreements.

### 2.3 Rainwater Catchment — Scaling for Zone 5 Drought Buffers

The existing Wave 3 document covers rainwater collection mechanics well. This section addresses the drought-buffer sizing question not covered there.

**Cistern sizing for 90-day summer drought (rural household, Zone 5)**:

Using the formula: Daily demand × Drought days = Required storage

For a rural household at 5 gallons/person/day (drinking, cooking, basic sanitation) × 4 people = 20 gallons/day × 90 days = 1,800 gallons **drinking-and-cooking minimum** drought storage.

For combined drinking plus livestock plus garden irrigation at a functional homestead level (30-50 gallons/day total) × 90 days = 2,700-4,500 gallons.

For garden-intensive operation (drip irrigation for 1/4 acre food production in addition to household): Add 500-1,000 gallons/week during growing season. A 90-day summer drought requires 7,000-12,000 gallons if irrigation is included.

**Practical recommendation**: A 2,500-gallon below-ground polyethylene cistern ($800-1,500 installed) covers household drinking and cooking for 90 days at 4 people. A 5,000-gallon cistern ($1,500-2,500 installed) adds livestock and basic garden irrigation. For serious food production integration, two 2,500-gallon cisterns (sequenced collection) plus swales for passive groundwater recharge provides the most resilient architecture.

**The "one-third rule" for sizing**: A widely cited guideline from cistern design literature recommends sizing your cistern at approximately one-third of annual collectible rainfall. For a 1,500 sq ft Zone 5 roof collecting 26,000 gallons annually: one-third = approximately 8,700 gallons. This is a maximum useful size; many communities find 2,500-5,000 gallons is the practical sweet spot balancing cost, space, and realistic demand.

**Suburban constraints**: Suburban properties typically have smaller roof areas (800-1,200 sq ft usable), more restricted yard space for cistern installation, and less water demand (no irrigation-intensive food production, no livestock). A 500-1,500 gallon cistern provides 25-75 days of drinking water at 1 gallon/person/day for 4 people — adequate for emergency buffer but not for prolonged self-sufficiency.

### 2.4 Surface Water — Use, Risk, and Agricultural Contamination Context

Surface water in the Zone 5 agricultural landscape is among the most contaminated in the United States. The Mississippi River watershed, which drains most of Zone 5, delivers approximately 1.7 million metric tons of nitrogen to the Gulf of Mexico annually — a quantity that reflects the intensity of fertilizer application across the region. Local tributaries, farm ponds, and drainage ditches in the tile-drained corn and soybean belt (approximately 35 million acres in the region) carry:

- Nitrates: typically 10-25 mg/L in intensively farmed watersheds in spring; EPA MCL is 10 mg/L
- Total coliform bacteria: often 10,000-100,000 CFU/100mL after rain events from livestock operation runoff
- Herbicides (atrazine, acetochlor): present in virtually all agricultural surface water in Zone 5 at measurable concentrations; atrazine has an EPA MCL of 3 ppb; rural stream atrazine peaks of 20-50 ppb are documented in Illinois during spring application season
- Pesticide loading: most intensive during May-June application season and first major rain after application

**Practical guidance**: In a crisis scenario, surface water from agricultural watersheds in Zone 5 should be considered a last resort and treated with the full 5-stage sequence (settling, coarse filter, ceramic filter, chemical disinfection, boiling). Even after full treatment, dissolved chemical contamination (nitrates, herbicides) cannot be reliably removed without reverse osmosis or ion exchange equipment that most households will not have. Position surface water as "we have completely exhausted all other options" — not as a planned backup source.

**Safer surface water sources in Zone 5**: Small headwater streams (first-order streams) above any significant agricultural land, particularly in the Driftless Area of southwestern Wisconsin and southeastern Minnesota, carry substantially lower contamination loads. Springs discharging into streams above agricultural influence are also dramatically cleaner. Know your local watershed — which streams drain mostly forested or pasture land versus row-crop agriculture — before any crisis.

## Section 3: Water Treatment — Technology Comparison for Crisis Context

### 3.1 Treatment Method Decision Framework

The critical distinction for crisis contexts: **power dependency**. Municipal power may be unavailable for extended periods. Any treatment method requiring grid electricity must have a documented backup.

**Tier 1 — Zero electricity required (always available)**:
- Boiling: 100% effective vs. bacteria, viruses, protozoa; fails for dissolved chemicals; requires fuel (plan 0.5 kWh equivalent per gallon)
- Calcium hypochlorite (pool shock): kills bacteria and most viruses; 3-5 year shelf life; $0.02/gallon treated; fails for Cryptosporidium and dissolved chemicals
- Ceramic gravity filter (0.5 micron): removes bacteria, protozoa, turbidity; does not remove viruses or dissolved chemicals; no power needed; 2,000-6,000 gallon element life; $25-60/element
- Biosand filter (mature): 90-99% bacteria removal, 70-99% virus removal after 2-3 week maturation; fails in Zone 5 winter without heated indoor installation; zero consumables once built
- SODIS (solar): >99.9% bacteria kill in clear PET bottles after 6 hours sun; fails in Zone 5 winter (insufficient UV); fails in turbid water; zero consumables

**Tier 2 — Low-power options (battery, solar, small generator)**:
- UV purification lamp: 40-watt power draw (equivalent); kills all pathogens including viruses and Cryptosporidium; does not remove chemicals or turbidity; requires annual lamp replacement ($30-80); requires pre-filtered clear water; ideal with solar panel + small battery
- Hollow fiber ultrafiltration (UF): physically removes bacteria and protozoa down to 0.02 microns; removes Cryptosporidium better than ceramic; no power needed for gravity-fed versions; cannot remove viruses without additional disinfection step

**Tier 3 — Infrastructure-dependent (avoid as primary plan)**:
- Reverse osmosis: removes nitrates, dissolved chemicals, most contaminants; requires pressure (30-60 psi) — either pump power or elevated storage; membrane has 2-5 year life; produces 3-4 gallons reject water per gallon purified (significant water waste in drought); not suitable for primary crisis purification
- Distillation: removes everything; extremely fuel-intensive (approximately 1.2 kWh per gallon); impractical at household scale for primary water supply

**Recommended treatment stack for Zone 5 crisis context** (rural groundwater source):
- Primary: well water + hand pump — no treatment required beyond annual testing if confined aquifer
- Backup collection (rainwater): ceramic gravity filter + calcium hypochlorite stock solution (belt and suspenders)
- Emergency surface water: full 5-stage sequence (settle → coarse filter → ceramic filter → hypochlorite → boil)

**Recommended treatment stack for suburban municipal backup**:
- Primary stored tap water (chlorinated, rotate every 6-12 months)
- Rainwater cistern → ceramic gravity filter → hypochlorite maintenance dose
- UV system (solar-powered) for any high-volume purification when surface water must be used

### 3.2 UV Systems for Off-Grid Use

A UV purification system drawing 40 watts can be powered by a single 100-watt solar panel and a 20 amp-hour battery bank — providing around-the-clock UV disinfection capacity on a $200-350 total hardware budget (panel + battery + UV unit + basic mounting). This is the single highest-leverage technology investment for communities that must rely on surface water or rainwater for extended periods, as UV kills viruses (which ceramic filters miss) without requiring chemical consumables.

**Critical UV limitation**: UV does not work in turbid water (above approximately 10 NTU). Pre-filtration with a ceramic or hollow fiber filter is mandatory before UV treatment of any collected or surface water. For Zone 5, this means ceramic filter → UV is the minimum viable virus-safe treatment stack for non-well sources.

**Maintenance in crisis**: UV lamp replacement (annual) requires the replacement lamp to be stocked in advance. Recommend a 2-year supply (2 replacement lamps) as minimum pre-crisis inventory. Lamps typically cost $30-80 each.

## Section 4: Water Storage — Cisterns, Materials, and Freeze Management

### 4.1 Tank Material Comparison for Zone 5

| Material | Longevity | Cost (1,000 gal) | Freeze-thaw resistance | Contamination risk | Maintenance |
|----------|-----------|------------------|----------------------|-------------------|-------------|
| HDPE polyethylene (below ground) | 30-50 years | $300-600 | Excellent if below frost line; frost-proof material | Low (food-grade liner) | Minimal; inspect inlet/outlet annually |
| HDPE polyethylene (above ground) | 20-35 years | $150-350 | Poor without insulation; must drain or insulate | Low | Winterize annually |
| Fiberglass (below ground) | 30-50 years | $500-1,200 | Good below frost line | Low | Inspect for cracking every 5 years |
| Concrete (below ground) | 50-100+ years | $800-2,500 | Excellent if below frost line | Moderate (pH alters taste; leach risk in acidic water) | HDPE liner recommended; inspect every 10 years |
| Galvanized steel | 15-25 years | $200-500 | Poor (corrosion accelerated by freeze-thaw) | High (rust, zinc leaching) | Not recommended for long-term water storage |
| IBC tote (HDPE, above ground) | 5-15 years (UV degradation) | $100-200 (used) | Poor — will freeze solid in Zone 5 winter | Moderate (verify food-grade origin) | Must insulate or drain seasonally |

**Recommendation by scenario**:
- Rural landowner, primary: 2,500-5,000 gallon below-ground HDPE cistern or concrete with HDPE liner, installed at minimum 48 inches (Illinois, Indiana) to 54-60 inches (Minnesota, Wisconsin) below grade
- Suburban homeowner: 500-1,500 gallon below-ground HDPE at minimum 48 inches; or indoor basement tank (300-500 gallons) if outdoor installation is impractical
- Community shared cistern: 5,000-10,000 gallon below-ground concrete with HDPE liner or steel-reinforced polyethylene (SRPE), professionally installed

### 4.2 Freeze Prevention and Winterization Protocol

Zone 5 frost depth ranges from 42 inches (southern Illinois) to 60 inches (northern Wisconsin/Minnesota). Any tank with water at or above this depth will freeze in a Zone 5 winter without additional protection. Frozen tanks do not burst unless the water has no expansion room (full, sealed tank) but become unavailable for the duration of freezing, which can extend from December through March in Zone 5a.

**Below-grade installation (best practice)**:
- Dig to depth of 48-60 inches minimum for tank base
- Backfill with sand or crushed stone (not soil — soil subsidence creates uneven pressure on tank)
- Inlet and outlet pipes must also be buried below frost line or insulated with closed-cell foam (minimum R-10) where they transition from buried to above-grade
- Access hatch must be insulated; specify a 3-inch foam insulation insert for the lid

**Above-grade winterization (seasonal use)**:
- Drain completely before first hard freeze (October 15-November 1 in Zone 5, 5a to 5b range)
- Leave drain valve open with visible indicator so spring refill is deliberate, not accidental
- Insulate tank with R-20 closed-cell foam panels and weatherproof wrap if using tank heater through winter (requires power — not infrastructure-independent)

**Tank heater option (with power)**:
- Stock tank heaters or pond heaters rated 150-300 watts maintain above-freeze temperatures in a 275-gallon IBC tote through typical Zone 5 nights
- For below-ground concrete cisterns, the earth itself provides thermal mass; rarely need heating if properly buried
- For above-ground tanks in an insulated outbuilding: a 60-watt aquarium heater is often sufficient in a 100-gallon tank inside a garage, reducing energy demand

## Section 5: Greywater and Blackwater Systems

### 5.1 Greywater Sources and Volume

Greywater comprises approximately 50-75% of household wastewater volume: shower, sink, and laundry effluent [Greywater Action]. Blackwater (toilet waste) comprises the remaining 25-50%. In Zone 5 water resilience planning, separating these streams enables:
- Treating greywater for irrigation reuse (reducing freshwater demand by 30-40%)
- Maintaining sanitation when municipal wastewater systems are compromised
- Extending the capacity of conventional septic systems during population influx

**Average greywater volumes** at typical household function:
- Shower: 15-25 gallons/person/shower
- Laundry (top-loader): 40-55 gallons/load; (front-loader, HE): 15-25 gallons/load
- Bathroom sink: 1-5 gallons/day/person
- Kitchen sink and dishwasher: should be treated as blackwater (food particles, fats, pathogens) and directed to septic

A household of 4 at 2 showers/person/day generates 120-200 gallons/day of shower greywater alone — water that in a crisis context could irrigate a substantial garden if treated and reused.

### 5.2 Greywater Treatment Systems — Cold Climate Design

The Zone 5 challenge: standard constructed wetland greywater systems freeze in winter, losing treatment capacity exactly when indoor water use is highest. Research identifies three approaches [Springer Nature 2024 constructed wetland review; Greywater Action cold climate systems]:

**Approach 1 — Subsurface horizontal flow constructed wetland with insulating mulch layer**

This is the most researched cold-climate greywater system. Design specifications:
- Subsurface flow (not surface flow) prevents ice formation on the water surface
- 12-18 inch mulch cover (wood chips, straw) provides insulation; demonstrated to prevent freezing of treatment zone in temperatures down to -20°C in research trials
- Planted with cold-tolerant species: cattail (Typha latifolia), common reed (Phragmites australis), bulrush (Scirpus spp.) — all Zone 5 native or naturalized
- Minimum cell dimensions for household greywater (4-person household, ~80 gallons/day): 6 ft wide × 12 ft long × 2 ft deep media bed
- Performance at 0-5°C: BOD removal 60-75% (reduced from summer 80-90%); coliform removal 90-95%
- Limitation: mulch layer reduces oxygen transfer, reducing performance of aerobic microorganisms
- **Zone 5 verdict**: Functional year-round with mulch insulation and subsurface flow design, but expect reduced performance in winter; supplement with constructed wetland effluent going to subsurface drip irrigation (not surface application) in winter months

**Approach 2 — Laundry-to-Landscape (L2L) simple greywater reuse**

The simplest greywater system: redirect washing machine drain hose to outdoor landscape through 3-point distribution emitters buried under mulch. Zero treatment, minimal infrastructure.
- Zero electricity required
- Cost: $20-50 (hosing, emitters, mulch)
- Legal in many Zone 5 states for landscape use of laundry greywater; check local health codes
- Winter use: system drains by gravity when not in use; no freeze concern
- Limitation: provides landscape irrigation only, not potable water; distributes surfactants and detergents to soil (use plant-safe detergents)
- **Integration with food systems**: use mulched tree basins around fruit trees; avoid use near root vegetables or leafy greens (pathogen contact risk)

**Approach 3 — Constructed wetland + storage tank sequence (summer surplus for winter use)**

In Zone 5 summer, a properly designed greywater wetland system produces more treated effluent than immediate irrigation demand. This surplus can be stored in a sealed tank for winter subsurface irrigation of greenhouse or cold frame operations. A 500-gallon tank of summer-treated greywater, applied via drip irrigation to cold frame growing beds through winter, substantially extends food production while managing greywater volume.

### 5.3 Blackwater — Septic Systems in Crisis Context

Conventional gravity-fed septic systems (concrete or polyethylene tank + leach field) operate entirely without electricity and are the most resilient blackwater management system for Zone 5 rural properties.

**Aerobic treatment units (ATUs)**: Many suburban Zone 5 properties built since 1990 use ATUs (aerobic bacteria-assisted systems) that require electrical aeration pumps. **ATUs fail during power outages** — the aerator stops, anaerobic conditions develop within 24-48 hours, and treatment quality drops sharply. In a prolonged power outage, an ATU becomes a conventional septic tank without proper leach field design for that loading. Communities relying on ATUs should:
1. Know their system type before a crisis
2. Maintain a generator connection for the aerator pump (low power draw, typically 100-500 watts)
3. Consider transitioning to gravity-fed conventional septic if property permits

**Composting toilets**: A composting toilet eliminates blackwater entirely, converting toilet waste to aerobically composted solids over 2-6 months. Modern systems (Envirolet, Sun-Mar, Separett) handle Zone 5 cold winters if installed indoors or in heated outbuildings. No water required. No electricity required for passive models. Mature compost output can be applied to non-food landscape (not food gardens — human pathogen risk requires at least 12 months composting before horticultural use). Cost: $1,200-3,000 for household-scale unit.

**Crisis blackwater overflow**: In worst-case scenarios (flooding, system failure), temporary pit latrines remain the lowest-tech adequate solution for communities without functioning septic. WHO emergency sanitation standards: 1 latrine per 20 people minimum, located downhill and downwind from water sources, minimum 100-foot buffer from wells or water sources [WHO Emergency WASH standards]. Zone 5 winter challenge: latrines must be warmed to prevent freezing (a temporary enclosed structure with a small heat source).

### 5.4 Integration with Food Systems

The greywater-to-food-system integration opportunity is significant: a 4-person household generating 80-120 gallons per day of laundry and shower greywater can, after simple treatment, supply 30-50% of the irrigation demand for a 1/4-acre intensive vegetable garden during summer months — significantly reducing demand on freshwater storage.

**Safe integration rules**:
- Treated greywater (wetland effluent meeting Class C standards): suitable for drip irrigation of above-ground crops (tomatoes, cucumbers, squash) where effluent does not contact edible portion
- Not suitable for: direct spray on leafy greens, root vegetables, strawberries
- Subsurface drip irrigation preferred: delivers water to root zone without surface contamination risk
- After any flooding event: flush and test system before resuming food production irrigation

## Section 6: Water Governance in Communities

### 6.1 The Commons Problem Applied to Shared Water Sources

Water is the archetypal common-pool resource: rival (one person's use reduces availability for others) and difficult to exclude (physical exclusion from a spring or well is hard without fencing and monitoring). Elinor Ostrom's Nobel Prize-winning research identified eight design principles for successfully managed commons, based on cases including irrigation communities in Spain, Philippines, Japan, and Switzerland — and critically, Southern California groundwater basins [Ostrom 1990; Cambridge Transnational Environmental Law 2019].

Ostrom's eight principles, applied to Zone 5 community water:

1. **Clearly defined boundaries**: Who has rights to this well/spring/cistern? All 50-person community, or only the 10 founding households? Boundaries must be explicit and agreed before scarcity occurs.

2. **Rules fitted to local conditions**: A water allocation scheme that works in drought (strict rationing) must be different from the scheme in normal years. Rules must specify triggers for each regime.

3. **Collective choice arrangements**: People who will be affected by allocation rules must have a meaningful role in creating those rules. Top-down imposed rules fail in most documented commons cases.

4. **Monitoring**: Someone accountable to the community must monitor both the resource state (cistern level, well yield, flow rate) and users' compliance. This is where Meshtastic sensor telemetry becomes directly relevant (see Section 6.3).

5. **Graduated sanctions**: First-time overuse gets a warning; repeated overuse gets a temporary reduction in allocation; persistent violation gets exclusion. The sanction must be proportionate and pre-agreed.

6. **Conflict resolution mechanisms**: Fast, cheap, local resolution. A water dispute that takes 6 months to resolve through courts will have destroyed the community before resolution. Pre-agree: self-resolution → peer mediation → named external mediator → binding community vote.

7. **Minimal recognition of rights to organize**: External authorities (county health department, DNR) must not undermine the community governance structure. In Zone 5 states, community water associations can be legally structured as private water companies, nonprofit associations, or municipal water districts — the legal form matters for regulatory recognition.

8. **Nested enterprises**: For resources spanning multiple communities or properties, link local governance to regional structures. A community water association that manages a shared well should have formal linkage to the county water district or state groundwater management framework.

### 6.2 Allocation Protocols Under Scarcity

**The three-tier Australian priority framework** (applied from Murray-Darling Basin Plan [Water Partnership Australia 2024]):

When water supply drops below normal availability, the Basin Plan framework prioritizes in strict order:
1. **Essential human needs** (drinking, cooking, basic sanitation — minimum 5 liters/person/day): non-negotiable, cut last
2. **Water delivery system maintenance** (water needed to keep infrastructure operational): second priority
3. **Agricultural and other productive use** (irrigation, livestock beyond survival minimum): cut first in extremis

This framework translates directly to Zone 5 community water governance: establish the three tiers in writing before any scarcity event, with specific quantities that trigger each tier's restrictions.

**Zone 5 community allocation tiers (illustrative)**:

| Tier | Trigger | Restriction | Specifics |
|------|---------|-------------|-----------|
| Normal operations | Cistern >70% capacity; well yield normal | No restrictions | All household and agricultural use as normal |
| Conservation (Tier 2) | Cistern 40-70%; drought monitor D1 or D2 | Agricultural use restricted | Irrigation reduced to critical crops only; no landscape watering; household use unrestricted |
| Rationing (Tier 3) | Cistern 20-40%; drought monitor D3 | All non-essential use suspended | 5 gallons/person/day for human consumption + livestock survival water only; all irrigation suspended |
| Emergency (Tier 4) | Cistern <20%; well yield below 1 gpm | Emergency distribution | Central distribution point; daily allocation per household; livestock triage decisions |

**Agricultural vs. domestic priority**: This is the most contentious governance question in Zone 5 communities where productive farming depends on irrigation. The general principle from legal and governance research: **domestic water use takes priority over agricultural use** in crisis — human survival over crop survival. This must be stated explicitly in community water agreements, not assumed, because the farmer who has relied on a shared well for 20 years of irrigation may not accept restriction without a pre-agreed governance structure that makes the priority clear.

**Conflict resolution precedent — the Israeli model and its limits**: Israeli water governance allocates water administratively, without market mechanisms, under a framework that treats agricultural allocations as entitlements. This led to a politically difficult 1986 drought response where agricultural allocations were cut 15% and farmers received "compensation" for unused allocations — a costly and legally complex outcome [ResearchGate Israeli Water Policy]. The lesson: communities that define agricultural water rights as *contingent* (dependent on available supply above human needs threshold) rather than *vested entitlements* avoid this legal complexity. Define agricultural use as a licensed privilege, not a property right, in community water agreements.

### 6.3 Meshtastic Integration for Water System Telemetry

A Zone 5 community managing a shared well, cistern, or spring box system gains significant governance capacity by deploying continuous water level monitoring with automated mesh network reporting.

**Technical architecture**:
- Sensor node: Heltec LoRa 32 V3 or RAK WisBlock module with ultrasonic distance sensor (HC-SR04 or JSN-SR04T waterproof variant) mounted above cistern water surface; measures distance from sensor to water surface, calculates fill level
- Secondary MCU (Arduino or ESP32) processes sensor data and converts to Meshtastic telemetry format, sent via Meshtastic device over LoRa mesh [Meshtastic Telemetry Module Documentation; CreatLabz Heltec Tutorial]
- Power: 6-watt solar panel + 10,000 mAh LiPo battery provides months of unattended operation
- Cost per sensor node: $40-80 (Heltec module + sensor + battery + solar)
- Range: LoRa reaches 1-5 km in typical Zone 5 terrain; mesh relay nodes extend range to 10+ km
- Data: cistern level percentage, daily consumption rate, alert when below 40% and 20% thresholds
- Community access: any Meshtastic-connected device in range receives level readings; no internet required

**Governance integration**: Cistern level readings that automatically trigger Tier 2 and Tier 3 allocation restrictions (per Section 6.2) remove ambiguity from governance decisions. When a sensor shows cistern at 38% and declining at 0.5% per day, triggering a pre-agreed Tier 2 restriction is a mechanical outcome of the governance protocol, not a contested judgment call.

**IoT research context**: A 2024 academic study demonstrated IoT cistern monitoring with four sensors (cistern level, tank level, flow rate, water quality) providing real-time monitoring at infrastructure costs below $100 per deployment [MDPI Sensors 2024; Preprints.org 2024]. The Meshtastic protocol's mesh architecture eliminates the cellular or WiFi dependency that makes conventional IoT systems fail during infrastructure disruption.

### 6.4 Legal Frameworks for Community Water Associations

Zone 5 states have varying legal frameworks for community-managed water systems:

**Illinois**: Communities of fewer than 25 people using a shared private well are exempt from state public water supply regulations under IEPA rules. For communities of 25 or more, a community water supply (CWS) must comply with Safe Drinking Water Act requirements. A community of 10-15 households using a shared well can legally operate as a private association without IEPA oversight — though this also means no state testing requirement, making self-governance critical.

**Wisconsin**: The Water Council (Wisconsin's interagency water policy body) coordinates state groundwater governance. Community water associations can be structured as private water utilities under PSC (Public Service Commission) jurisdiction or as member-owned nonprofit cooperatives. The nonprofit cooperative structure provides member governance authority while maintaining legal recognition.

**Indiana**: Indiana requires a water service authority for new water systems serving more than one household, created under IC 13-18-15. The authority must file a water system plan and meet DEQ drinking water standards. For truly private (shared well between 2-5 households), no state authority is required but written agreements between users are strongly recommended.

**Iowa**: Iowa Code §455B authorizes the DNR to regulate public water supplies (25+ people or 15+ service connections). Below these thresholds, shared well agreements are private matters. Iowa townships have authority under Iowa Code §359.17 to address public water supply issues within their territory — a potential vehicle for township-level water governance [Iowa Legislature Township Governance publication].

## Section 7: Climate Adaptation Case Studies

### 7.1 The Kansas LEMA Model — Scalable Groundwater Governance

The Sheridan County #6 LEMA (2013-2017) and the subsequent expansion of the LEMA program across Kansas represent the most rigorously documented community-managed groundwater conservation success in the US [Kansas Geological Survey; Stanford Water in the West].

**Mechanism**: Irrigators within a designated area voluntarily (initially; enforced once adopted) accept annual pumping allocations that total less than historical use. The groundwater management district develops the allocation plan; irrigators vote on adoption; once adopted, allocations become legally enforceable permits.

**Results (SD-6 LEMA)**:
- 31.2% reduction in pumping on irrigated corn
- Water-level decline rates reduced by 65%
- No measurable reduction in crop yields (achieved through improved irrigation efficiency)
- Irrigators reported improved relationships with neighbors and reduced conflict over shared resource

**Why it works** (applying Ostrom's framework): The LEMA design embodies all eight principles — user-defined boundaries, locally calibrated rules, collective choice (irrigators vote), state-sponsored monitoring with user accountability, graduated sanctions via permit enforcement, efficient dispute resolution through the GMD structure, legal recognition from state statute, and nested linkage to the larger state water rights framework.

**Zone 5 application**: A Zone 5 community of 20-50 households sharing a well or aquifer could apply this model at micro-scale: a community-designed annual extraction budget, individual household allocations tracked via meter or sensor telemetry, and a pre-agreed adjustment mechanism tied to aquifer level monitoring.

### 7.2 California's SGMA — Large-Scale Commons Governance

California's Sustainable Groundwater Management Act (2014) represents a state-mandated application of Ostrom-style governance at basin scale [Tandfonline SGMA Review 2020; USGS SGMA]. Groundwater Sustainability Agencies (GSAs) must develop and implement Groundwater Sustainability Plans (GSPs) bringing critically overdrafted basins into sustainability within 20 years.

**Critical finding for Zone 5 planning**: A 2023 study published in PNAS found that despite SGMA's intent, **thousands of domestic and public supply wells in California's Central Valley face failure even under sustainability reform** because large agricultural users' rights are prioritized over small domestic wells [PMC NCBI 2023]. The lesson: governing for "basin sustainability" and governing for "domestic water security" are not the same thing, and community-scale governance must explicitly specify domestic water priority or larger users will capture governance outcomes.

### 7.3 Keyline Design and Landscape-Scale Water Retention

Keyline design, developed by P.A. Yeomans in Australia in the 1950s for arid-zone agriculture, addresses watershed-scale water retention through earthworks that slow runoff and direct water from valleys (where it concentrates) to ridges (where it's needed) [US Fish & Wildlife Keyline Project; permaguide.com; Midwest Permaculture].

**Zone 5 application and limitations**: The keyline system was designed for Australian geology, where aquifers are typically simple single-level systems. US aquifers are more complex — typically 5+ layers — meaning the precise keyline topography may not align with subsurface water movement in Zone 5. However, the core principle — using landscape earthworks to increase infiltration time and reduce runoff velocity — is directly applicable.

**Practical Zone 5 earthworks**:

*Swales*: Horizontal trenches along contour lines with downhill berm. Slow and spread runoff, dramatically increasing infiltration. In Zone 5 clay soils, a swale system on a 1/4 acre sloped lot can increase effective precipitation capture by 25-40% by reducing surface runoff. Construction: hand-dig or small excavator; depth 12-18 inches; width 24-36 inches; berm from excavated soil on downhill side.

*Rain gardens*: Bowl-shaped depressions planted with deep-rooted native plants (prairie species: switchgrass, cup plant, prairie dropseed) that receive diverted rooftop runoff. A 100 sq ft rain garden can manage runoff from 500-1,000 sq ft of impervious surface. Both a water retention strategy and a groundwater recharge contribution.

*Pond as community water reserve*: A 1/2-acre farm pond in Zone 5 with average precipitation can accumulate 500,000-1,000,000 gallons of surface runoff annually. After settling and treatment, this provides community-scale emergency water supply. Pond water in agricultural Zone 5 requires full treatment before drinking but can supply livestock, irrigation, and fire suppression without treatment.

**Aquifer recharge argument for keyline**: By slowing runoff and increasing infiltration across a watershed, keyline-scale earthworks can contribute meaningfully to local groundwater recharge — particularly recharging the shallow drift aquifers that most Zone 5 domestic wells access. This is the Zone 5 adaptation of keyline thinking most supported by hydrology: not as a precise irrigation engineering system, but as a landscape-scale infiltration enhancement.

### 7.4 Flood-Zone Water Management — 2019 Midwest Lessons

The 2019 Midwest flooding demonstrated three flood management approaches with different outcomes [American Rivers 2019; Headwaters Economics 2019; FEMA HMGP Case Studies]:

**Approach 1 — Levee defense (most common in 2019)**: Standard agricultural levee systems along the Missouri River were overwhelmed in March 2019 by flows that exceeded design capacity. Many levees were breached or overtopped; communities behind them faced sudden inundation. Lesson: levees protect until they fail, then they catastrophically fail.

**Approach 2 — Levee setback with floodplain restoration (most effective long-term)**: In Atchison County, MO, a levee setback project implemented in partnership with The Nature Conservancy provided additional floodplain area that absorbed flow, reducing downstream flood peaks. Farmers who initially opposed setbacks became advocates after the 2019 event demonstrated that the setback area absorbed what would otherwise have been catastrophic flooding on their remaining land.

**Approach 3 — Voluntary property buyouts (most decisive long-term)**: FEMA HMGP buyout programs in flood-prone communities converted repetitively flooded properties to open space, permanently removing the recurring exposure. The limitation: buyout programs are slow (2-5 years from disaster to completion), require federal funding appropriation, and depend on willing sellers.

**Water quality in flood aftermath**: Communities in or adjacent to flood zones face specific post-flood water risks:
- Private wells: assume contaminated after inundation; mandatory testing before use; shock chlorination if coliform present
- Spring boxes: likely contaminated by surface water intrusion through overflow; test and shock chlorinate
- Above-ground cisterns: if inundated, drain, clean with 1:10 bleach solution, refill and test
- Municipal systems: verify with local utility before drinking; boil order protocols in effect in most flooded municipalities

## Section 8: Integration Bridges to Food Systems and Communication

### 8.1 Water-Food Systems Integration

Water and food production are the two most tightly coupled resilience domains. The connections:

**Irrigation demand**: A 1/4-acre intensive vegetable garden requires 500-800 gallons per week during peak summer production. At Zone 5's typical July-August rainfall (5-7 inches total), the remaining demand (500-1,500 gallons/week deficit) must come from stored cistern water or well. Size water storage to include garden irrigation in the calculation, not just household drinking water.

**Livestock water**: Goats (minimum 0.5 gallons/day), chickens (0.5 pints/bird/day in summer heat), pigs (4-6 gallons/day) all require clean water supply. A small livestock operation (4 goats, 20 chickens, 2 pigs) adds 8-12 gallons/day to water demand — manageable from a well, but significant from a cistern in drought.

**Greywater as irrigation**: As noted in Section 5, treated greywater can supply 30-50% of irrigation demand during summer, significantly reducing freshwater drawdown. The food system integration requires matching greywater treatment system output timing with peak irrigation demand.

**Canning and food preservation**: Canning requires approximately 10-15 gallons per canning session (sterilization, processing water, steam). For a household canning 100+ pounds of produce (a reasonable annual food preservation goal), this adds 50-100 gallons of water demand during the August-September peak season.

### 8.2 Communication Infrastructure for Water Monitoring

The Meshtastic sensor telemetry system described in Section 6.3 integrates with the broader Phase 5 Meshtastic communication network documented elsewhere in the Phase 5 series. A water monitoring node is functionally identical to any other Meshtastic sensor node — it uses the same hardware platform, same LoRa frequencies (915 MHz in the US), and same mesh architecture.

**Community-scale monitoring network**: A community of 20-50 households, each with a Meshtastic device (for communication), can designate 2-3 as sensor nodes with added ultrasonic water level sensors. No additional infrastructure is required. Community members with Meshtastic apps receive water level broadcasts during their daily check-ins. An alert function can be configured to send a high-priority message when any monitored cistern drops below 40% or 20% threshold.

**Power independence**: Solar-powered Meshtastic nodes run indefinitely off-grid. A water monitoring node in a Zone 5 rural setting, powered by a 10-watt solar panel and 10,000 mAh battery, maintains continuous operation through Zone 5 winters given adequate panel exposure — though winter solar availability in Zone 5 (4.5 peak sun hours average in December, versus 6+ in summer) may require slightly larger battery reserve.
