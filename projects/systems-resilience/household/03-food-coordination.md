# Household Food Coordination — 3-Household Scale
> **Region**: Midwest US (Zone 5) | **Tracks**: Rural + Suburban
> **Scale**: 3 households, 10–14 people, shared or adjacent parcels
> **Cross-references**: [individual/02-food.md](../individual/02-food.md) · [01-household-coordination-overview.md](01-household-coordination-overview.md) · [02-water-coordination.md](02-water-coordination.md) · [individual/04-energy.md](../individual/04-energy.md) · [midwest/calendar.md](../midwest/calendar.md)

## The Most Important Finding

A single household trying to produce, store, and preserve a full year of food for 4 people from 1 acre operates at the edge of physical capacity even in a good year — and a bad year becomes a crisis. A 3-household cluster producing from 3 coordinated acres with shared storage infrastructure, differentiated labor roles, and a written distribution protocol can sustain the same 12 people through two simultaneous partial crop failures and still enter winter with 90 days of food supply in the shared root cellar. The math is not complicated: distributed planting means different crop exposures to drought, flood, pest, and freeze; shared storage means one household's good tomato harvest covers another household's loss; written governance means that when squash comes in faster than anyone expected, everyone knows who processes it and who gets first access.

This document assumes each household in the cluster has already built or is building the individual-scale food competencies described in `individual/02-food.md` — seed saving, preservation basics, caloric planning, emergency storage. This document begins where that one ends: at the boundary between one household's garden and the next.

## Section 1: Production Coordination — Land Allocation and Labor Division

### Why Coordination Changes the Math

`individual/02-food.md` covers the 1-acre intensive garden: a single person managing all crops, all tasks, all preservation from a single plot. At cluster scale, three things change: (1) land can be allocated by specialization rather than duplicated three times, (2) labor can be staged so that not every household is trying to harvest and can simultaneously, and (3) risk can be distributed so that one household's bad tomato year is absorbed by another's good one. The 3-household cluster does not simply scale individual capacity by three — it qualitatively changes what is possible.

### Land Allocation: 3 Acres Across 3 Households

The reference scenario's 3 productive acres are organized into differentiated production zones rather than three identical quarter-acre plots:

**Zone A — Caloric staples (1.0 acre, managed primarily by Household A)**
Primary crops: dent corn, dried beans, winter squash (Three Sisters polyculture)
- 0.5 acre Three Sisters: corn as trellis, beans as nitrogen fixers, squash as ground cover — reduces weeding labor by 40–60% compared to monoculture rows, provides complementary nutrition (caloric grain + protein + vitamins), and achieves Land Equivalency Ratios (LER) of 1.2–1.4 vs. monoculture across documented research trials [4]
- 0.5 acre overflow: rotating between cowpeas, sunflowers, and amaranth based on what stored well the previous year
- Annual caloric output estimate: 600–900 lbs of dried corn + 400–600 lbs of dried beans + 500–800 lbs of winter squash = 1,500–2,300 lbs of high-density food

**Zone B — Root crops, potatoes, and garlic (0.75 acre, managed primarily by Household B)**
Primary crops: potatoes, sweet potatoes, carrots, beets, turnips, garlic, onions
- 0.25 acre potatoes: 2,500–3,750 lbs at Zone 5 yields — the cluster's single highest-calorie-per-land-area investment after dried corn
- 0.15 acre sweet potatoes: 1,200–1,800 lbs; heat-loving crop planted in Zone B's warmest microsite
- 0.15 acre root vegetables (succession-planted): carrots, beets, turnips, parsnips — excellent root cellar storage, 3–6 month viability
- 0.2 acre garlic and onion: planted in October, harvested July — largest food investment for time spent; garlic stores 6–10 months without refrigeration and commands high culinary value per pound
- Annual output estimate: 5,000–7,000 lbs across all root crops and alliums

**Zone C — Market garden, perennials, and animal integration (1.25 acres, managed primarily by Household C)**
Primary crops: tomatoes, peppers, cucumbers, herbs, asparagus, soft fruit, fruit trees, animal forage
- 0.4 acre intensive market garden: high-value, high-labor crops providing vitamins, fresh eating, and canning feedstock (tomatoes especially)
- 0.35 acre perennial integration: asparagus bed, berry bushes (currants, gooseberries, elderberries — all excellent Zone 5 performers [5]), rhubarb
- 0.3 acre fruit trees: 4–6 dwarf apples + 2 pears + 2 plums; begin bearing year 3–5; negligible maintenance after establishment
- 0.2 acre animal forage / paddock integration: grazing rotation for any livestock the labor household maintains (see Section 3)

**Why differentiation, not triplication**: If all three households each managed their own 1-acre "complete garden," they would each attempt everything, labor would be redundant on low-efficiency tasks, and the single point of failure risk is not distributed — one bad soil microsite means one household loses everything simultaneously. Differentiated zones mean Household B's excellent potato ground is not replicated on Household A's sandier soil; instead, Household A's better-drained land goes to corn.

### Crop Sequencing — Planting Calendar

Zone 5 has a 160–175 day frost-free window (last frost April 10–25, first frost October 10–20). The cluster's planting calendar staggers sowings to distribute labor and maximize the harvest window:

**Spring (April–May):**
- April 1–15: Cold-hardy crops in Zone B — garlic established (from fall planting), onion sets, peas, spinach, kale, turnips. These are Household B's first labor surge.
- April 15–30: Zone A starts Three Sisters bed prep. No planting yet — soil still cold for corn.
- May 1–15: Tomato, pepper, squash transplants started indoors (if not already); potato planting begins in Zone B
- May 15–30: Corn + beans planted in Zone A after last frost risk passes. Sweet potato slips planted in Zone B warm microsite. Zone C market garden transplants go in.

**Summer (June–August):**
- June: All three zones require weeding — highest overall labor demand. Succession-plant Zone B carrots and beets for fall harvest. Zone C first tomatoes ripen for immediate fresh eating.
- July: Garlic harvest (Zone B) — major single-week event requiring all household labor available. Begin curing for storage.
- August: Earliest corn and bean dry-down; first winter squash harvest; tomato canning season begins (Zone C primary, shared canning kitchen). This is the cluster's most labor-intensive month.

**Fall (September–October):**
- September: Major harvest across all zones. Potatoes come up (Zone B). Winter squash cured and stored. Second tomato canning surge.
- October 1–15: Final harvest before first frost. Everything must be in the root cellar, canned, or covered by October 15. Late-season greens (kale, spinach) tolerate light frost.
- October 15–31: Garlic planting for next year. Perennial beds mulched. Root cellar fully stocked — this is the cluster's single most important deadline.

**Winter dormancy (November–March):**
Zero fresh production from Zone 5 soil December–February. The cluster eats from what was stored. Planning, equipment maintenance, seed inventory, and seed catalog orders happen in this window. This is not a design failure — it is the constraint around which all production planning is organized.

### Labor Division and Scheduling

The household type framework from `01-household-coordination-overview.md` maps directly to food production labor:

| Task | Primary Responsible | Support | Timing | Hours |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Spring soil prep (Zone A) | Household A | All households | April | 15–25 hrs total |
| Potato planting (Zone B) | Household B | Household C | Early May | 8–12 hrs |
| Corn + bean planting (Zone A) | Household A | Rotating | Late May | 10–15 hrs |
| Market garden transplanting (Zone C) | Household C | All households | May–June | 12–20 hrs |
| Weekly weeding rotation | Rotating by week | — | June–Aug | 4–6 hrs/week |
| Garlic harvest (Zone B) | Household B | All households | July 1–15 | 20–30 hrs total |
| Tomato processing + canning | Household C primary | Shared canning kitchen | Aug–Sep | 40–60 hrs/household |
| Main harvest coordination | All households | — | Sep–Oct | 40–80 hrs/household |
| Root cellar stocking | Household B (storage role) | All households | Oct | 15–20 hrs total |
| Seed saving | Rotating by crop zone | Seed vault: Household B | Aug–Oct | 8–12 hrs total |
| Winter planning + seed ordering | All households | — | Jan–Feb | 4–6 hrs total |

**Weeding rotation protocol**: During June–August, each household is assigned one day per week as their "weeding day" across all three zones, rotating so that each zone is weeded at least every 4–5 days. This is more efficient than each household weeding only their own zone (reduces task-switching overhead) and distributes labor more evenly. The rotation is simple: Household A weeds Zone B, Household B weeds Zone C, Household C weeds Zone A — then rotate the following week.

## Section 2: Livestock Integration

### Small Animal Production at Cluster Scale

The `01-household-coordination-overview.md` overview describes livestock as the most governance-intensive element of cluster coordination. That is true — but for a 3-household cluster that wants animal protein without depending entirely on hunting, small animals are the most space-efficient and labor-divisible option.

**Option A: Laying hen flock (Household C, labor household)**
- 18–24 hens (Household C manages; other households contribute feed costs)
- Expected production: 200–280 eggs/hen/year for productive breeds (Rhode Island Red, Plymouth Rock, Sussex) [6]
- 18 hens × 220 average = 3,960 eggs/year ÷ 12 people = 330 eggs/person/year ≈ 6 eggs/person/week
- Space: 4 sq ft/hen inside coop + 10 sq ft/hen outside run = 72 sq ft coop + 180 sq ft run for 18 hens [7]
- Feed: 0.25 lbs/hen/day = 4.5 lbs/day = 1,642 lbs/year; supplement with garden scraps from all three households (reduces feed cost 20–30%)
- Labor: 15–30 minutes daily (water, feed, egg collection); deep litter method reduces cleaning frequency to seasonal
- Manure value: Chicken manure at 15–18 lbs per bird per year [8] = 300 lbs/year for 18 hens; composted, this covers 10–15% of Zone A's nitrogen needs, reducing fertilizer cost

**Option B: Meat rabbit operation (Household C or rotating)**
- 3 breeding does + 1 buck = 75–150 kits/year
- Dressed meat yield: 125–250 lbs/year from 3 does at typical production rates [9]
- 250 lbs/12 people = ~21 lbs/person/year — meaningful protein supplement
- Space: 4 cages at 36" × 30" × 18" each; fits in 50 sq ft of covered space
- Feed: Pellets + hay + garden scraps (alfalfa from Zone B perimeter plantings reduces feed cost)
- Labor: 30–45 minutes/day for feeding, water, cage maintenance
- Manure: Cold droppings — do not need composting; can be applied directly under fruit trees in Zone C

**Option C: Duck flock (5–8 ducks, integrated with Zone C pond or water source)**
- Khaki Campbells lay 250–340 eggs/year, nearly matching chickens but more cold-hardy and better foragers
- Ducks require more water than chickens; integrate with any Zone C greywater or pond feature
- Slug and pest control in the market garden: Ducks actively forage slugs, reducing pesticide need in Zone C's high-value beds
- Space: 5 sq ft/duck inside + 15 sq ft outside (larger than chicken requirement)
- Recommended only if Zone C has a dedicated water feature; otherwise chickens are lower-maintenance

**Which option to choose**: For a first-year cluster, start with laying hens only. Meat rabbits are lower-visibility to neighbors and require less neighbor-facing governance, but they add a butchering step that requires dedicated equipment and at least one person with animal processing skill. Add rabbits in year 2 if the chicken system is well-established. Ducks in year 3 if Zone C water infrastructure supports it.

**Livestock governance essentials** (written before acquiring any animals):
1. Designated primary caretaker (Household C or rotating by month)
2. Feed cost allocation: equal thirds, paid monthly into a shared livestock fund
3. Egg/meat distribution formula: see Section 5 (Distribution and Governance)
4. Vacation coverage protocol: who covers daily care when the primary caretaker is away (minimum 48-hour advance notice; neighbor households take turns)
5. Sickness/injury protocol: cluster veterinary fund ($100/year per household) covers routine care; emergency vet spending over $150 requires joint decision within 24 hours
6. Exit clause: if any household wants to exit livestock coordination, 90-day transition period; remaining households absorb or rehome animals before any formal separation

## Section 3: Shared Storage Infrastructure

### Why Shared Storage Is the Cluster's Highest-Leverage Investment

Individual food production is improved by coordination; individual food storage is transformed by it. Three households each building their own root cellar to handle their own harvest spend $3,000–9,000 each ($9,000–27,000 total) and end up with three small, underused spaces that compete for the same cold-storage temperature and humidity requirements. One shared root cellar sized for the full cluster output costs $4,000–8,000 total and operates more efficiently at scale because thermal mass scales better than storage volume — a large underground space stays cold more consistently than a small one.

### Shared Root Cellar Design and Specifications

**Target specifications for a 3-household, 12-person cluster:**
- Volume: 400–600 sq ft usable floor area (a 20' × 25' or similar layout)
- Temperature: 32–40°F (0–4°C) for root vegetables and most produce; achievable without mechanical refrigeration in Zone 5 underground installation [10]
- Humidity: 85–95% for root crops; 60–70% for cured onions/garlic; managed via controlled ventilation and a separate dry storage bay
- Depth: 6–8 feet below grade (Zone 5 frost line is 42–48 inches; 6–8 feet ensures temperature stability year-round)

**Storage sections within one shared cellar:**

| Section | Crops | Temperature | Humidity | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Root zone (main area) | Potatoes, carrots, beets, turnips, parsnips | 32–38°F | 90–95% | Highest humidity; wooden bins or crates, off-floor |
| Curing bay | Onions, garlic, winter squash | 45–55°F | 60–70% | Separate from root zone; ventilated; slatted shelves |
| Dry storage | Dried corn, beans, grains, seeds | 40–55°F | Below 60% | Cool, dry; sealed containers (5-gallon buckets with gamma lids) |
| Canning shelf area | Jars of canned produce | 40–55°F | Below 70% | Away from freezing zone; organized by household and type |
| Seed vault corner | Heirloom seeds, seed envelopes | 35–45°F | Below 50% | Sealed metal containers; desiccant packets inside |

**Construction cost at cluster scale** [10, 11]:
- Poured concrete or CMU block walls on poured footing + concrete floor: $4,000–7,000 materials + $1,500–3,000 contractor labor for excavation and form work
- Alternative: 8-foot diameter concrete culvert pipe (20 feet long) buried horizontally at 7 feet depth; pre-built end caps; $2,500–4,500 installed — the "Poor Man's Root Cellar" documented extensively in small-farm literature
- Per household, sharing construction cost three ways: $1,333–3,333/household — dramatically less than the $3,000–9,000 each household would spend on an individual cellar

**Siting the shared root cellar**: Place it on whichever household's property has the most favorable conditions: north-facing hillside if any slope exists (stays cooler naturally), high enough to avoid flood zone, accessible to all three households by foot path within 200 yards. The owning household grants a recorded access easement (permanent access right) to the other two households — this is documented in the cluster's MOU (see `01-household-coordination-overview.md`).

### Shared Canning Kitchen

A dedicated canning kitchen — separate from any household's cooking kitchen — eliminates the single greatest bottleneck in cluster food preservation: competition for stove time and counter space during August–October peak season.

**Minimum canning kitchen specifications:**
- Location: At or adjacent to the shared root cellar, or in a dedicated outbuilding (shed, barn annex)
- Power: Propane burners preferred (2–3 burner propane range, $300–600) to avoid overloading any single household's electrical panel during simultaneous processing sessions [12]
- Water: A dedicated feed from the cluster's water system (see `02-water-coordination.md` — note the large water demand during canning season: 50+ gallons per multi-hour session)
- Equipment (shared, stored in canning kitchen):
  - Two 23-quart All American pressure canners ($350–450 each): one for each concurrent session; cluster processes 200–400 quarts per harvest season
  - One 7-quart water bath canner for high-acid fruits and tomatoes ($50–80)
  - Jar lifter, lid wand, funnel, headspace tool: 2–3 sets
  - Scale (for recipe accuracy): one commercial-grade unit ($80–150)
  - Jar storage: open shelving rated for 50 lbs/shelf, labeled by household and date

**Shared canning kitchen scheduling** (posted in August, before harvest peak):
Each household books time blocks in advance (posted on a physical calendar at the canning kitchen door). Blocks are 4-hour minimum to allow a full canning session without rushing. The kitchen is available daily 7 AM–9 PM during harvest season; overnight sessions require advance notice and a working smoke alarm.

### Shared Freezer and Electrical Storage

In addition to the root cellar, one shared chest freezer (20–22 cu ft, $400–600) in the canning kitchen/storage building handles overflow for crops that do not preserve well by other means: meat, excess garden produce before it can be processed, blanched greens.

- The shared freezer supplements — it does not replace — each household's individual freezer
- Draw priority: meats (highest; freezer failure is immediate loss), then excess produce scheduled for later processing
- Energy integration: The shared freezer runs from the cluster's shared solar + battery system (see `01-household-coordination-overview.md` Section 5). Its energy load is approximately 1,000–1,500 Wh/day for a 20 cu ft unit [13]; this is budgeted into the cluster's daily energy plan

## Section 4: Preservation at Cluster Scale

### The Four Methods and When to Use Each

At individual scale, a household typically masters one or two preservation methods. At cluster scale, all four major methods can be deployed in parallel because labor is distributed — one household runs the pressure canner while another is fermenting, and a third is dehydrating herbs.

**Method 1: Pressure canning (Household B primary operator; shared equipment)**
- Best for: low-acid vegetables (beans, corn, potatoes), meats, soups, stews — anything that requires 240°F for botulism safety [14]
- Cluster scale: 200–400 quart jars per season; 40–60 canning sessions at 7 quarts/session using 23-qt canner
- Energy: Each canning session on a propane burner uses approximately 1.5–2 hours of high heat; plan 3–4 hours total per session including prep and cooldown. Propane burner fuel cost per session: $0.30–0.60 at $2.50/gallon bulk propane (see `01-household-coordination-overview.md` Section 5 on shared propane)
- Skill requirement: At least 2 people per household trained on USDA-approved canning procedures [14]; process failures cause spoilage or, rarely, botulism — no improvisation on tested recipes
- National Center for Home Food Preservation (NCHFP) [14] is the authoritative source; all cluster canning follows NCHFP-tested process times without deviation

**Method 2: Fermentation (all households independently, no shared equipment required)**
- Best for: vegetables (sauerkraut, kimchi-style, pickles), dairy (if any goats or cow are added), grains (sourdough)
- At cluster scale: 20–40 gallons of fermented vegetables per season (2–3 gallons/household per vegetable type); stored in ceramic crocks or half-gallon mason jars
- Energy: Zero. Fermentation is the lowest-barrier, no-energy preservation method [15]. Salt + vegetable + time.
- Nutritional value: Lacto-fermented vegetables retain and enhance vitamins (especially B and C vitamins) compared to canning; fermented foods provide probiotic cultures with documented health benefits [16]
- Zone 5 winter integration: Fermented crocks stored in the root cellar's 40–50°F range continue slow fermentation (flavor development) without spoiling — sauerkraut, pickled beets, and fermented carrots in the root cellar in November are still excellent in April

**Method 3: Dehydration (shared Excalibur 9-tray dehydrator; additional solar-passive option)**
- Best for: tomatoes (sun-dried), herbs, mushrooms, apples, beans, corn (for future grinding), jerky
- Cluster scale: 30–50 dehydrating sessions per season; shared dehydrator operates on a schedule (post in canning kitchen)
- Electric dehydrator energy: 600W × 12 hours/session = 7.2 kWh/session [17]; schedule during peak solar hours (10 AM–4 PM) to draw from solar generation rather than battery
- Solar passive alternative: Build a simple tray dehydrator from a screened wooden frame and dark backing board; zero energy cost; Zone 5 works June–August when 3+ consecutive sunny days are available. This handles herbs, light tomato halving, and flowers without competing for the electric unit
- Volume efficiency: Tomatoes lose 90%+ of water weight when dried; 20 lbs of fresh tomatoes = 2 lbs dried; plan storage accordingly (dried tomatoes are stored in the dry bay of the root cellar in sealed glass jars)

**Method 4: Cool/root cellar storage (no energy once properly built)**
- Best for: potatoes, carrots, beets, turnips, parsnips, winter squash, apples, pears, onions, garlic
- The cluster's most important preservation infrastructure: a properly built root cellar at 34–38°F with good humidity keeps potatoes through April, carrots through March, garlic through June, and winter squash through February [10]
- Critical timing: Everything going into the root cellar must be in before October 15 in Zone 5. After the first hard freeze, some root crops (carrots, parsnips) can be mulched and left in the ground for later harvest — but this is a short-term strategy, not a substitute for cellar stocking

### Preservation Volume Targets — Annual

For a 12-person cluster aiming for 150 days of food storage (November through late March):

| Category | Target quantity | Primary method | Storage location |
|---|---|---|---|
| Potatoes | 800–1,200 lbs | Root cellar | Root zone (32–38°F) |
| Winter squash | 400–600 lbs | Cool storage | Curing bay (50–55°F) |
| Dried corn (grain) | 150–200 lbs | Dry storage | Sealed 5-gal buckets |
| Dried beans | 150–200 lbs | Dry storage | Sealed 5-gal buckets |
| Canned tomatoes (whole/sauce) | 200–300 quart jars | Pressure/water bath canning | Canning shelf |
| Canned beans/corn (ready-to-eat) | 100–150 quart jars | Pressure canning | Canning shelf |
| Fermented vegetables | 20–30 gallons | Lacto-fermentation | Root cellar (40–50°F) |
| Dehydrated vegetables and herbs | 30–50 lbs | Dehydration | Dry bay (sealed glass) |
| Onions/garlic | 200–400 lbs | Curing + cool storage | Curing bay (55–65°F) |
| Fruit (canned or dried) | 80–120 quart jars or 20 lbs dried | Canning + dehydration | Canning shelf + dry bay |
| Frozen meat/fish | 200–300 lbs | Freezing | Shared + individual freezers |

These quantities assume each of the 12 people consumes approximately 2,000 calories per day from stored food during the dormant window, supplemented by hunting/fishing for fresh protein. If the cluster is also managing laying hens, eggs provide 6+ eggs/person/week year-round (hens continue laying in winter with 14–16 hours of light, maintained via a simple timer-controlled coop light [6]).

## Section 5: Distribution and Governance

### The Core Problem: Who Gets What

Food governance is the domain where cluster relationships most often break down. A clear protocol written before any food is produced eliminates ambiguity about who has access to what, who made the decision, and what happens when supply is short or excess. There are three defensible distribution formulas for a 3-household cluster; the cluster must choose one and write it down before the first harvest.

**Formula A: Equal per-capita distribution**
All food produced on shared land goes into shared storage; distribution is by person count (Household A: 4 people = 4/12 = 33%; Household B: 4 people = 33%; Household C: 4 people = 33%).

*Best for*: Clusters where all households contribute roughly equal labor, or where tracking labor precisely is not practical.
*Downside*: Does not account for differences in household need (a household with a nursing mother or elderly person has different nutritional needs than the average). Does not reward extra labor.

**Formula B: Labor-weighted distribution**
Each household tracks hours contributed to shared food production; distribution at harvest is proportional to hours worked. Example: if Household A worked 120 hours, B worked 100 hours, C worked 130 hours (total 350 hours), then A gets 34%, B gets 29%, C gets 37%.

*Best for*: Clusters where labor contribution is highly variable between households and fairness requires accountability.
*Downside*: Requires a reliable log (see record-keeping below); disputes arise over what counts as "cluster labor" vs. household labor; high administrative overhead. Not recommended for a first-year cluster.

**Formula C: Baseline equal + needs-based supplement**
All households receive the same base per-capita share. Any surplus beyond the 150-day storage target is distributed by need — households with children under 12, elderly members over 70, or documented medical nutritional needs receive proportionally higher shares of fresh produce and high-nutrient items (eggs, meat, fermented foods).

*Best for*: Clusters with diverse household compositions. Mirrors the logic of documented community food allocation frameworks — what Elinor Ostrom called "proportionality" in commons governance, where rules governing distribution of benefits are related to local conditions and contributions [18].
*Recommendation*: Formula C for most clusters. Equal baseline maintains cohesion; needs-based supplement handles real-world variation without resentment.

### Spoilage Protocol — Excess and Priority

**Fresh produce spoilage prevention:**
- Any fresh vegetable or fruit approaching spoilage (soft spots, mold onset) is processed immediately — that day's canning or fermentation session takes priority over planned tasks
- Cluster WhatsApp group or radio channel alert: "Zone C: 15 lbs of tomatoes going soft — canning kitchen open tonight if anyone wants to process." Other households can participate or decline; the producing household processes whatever remains
- Compost protocol: Any food beyond recovery goes to the compost system, which feeds Zone A and B beds the following spring. Nothing goes to municipal waste during growing season

**Fresh produce priority rotation (first-access rights):**
- The household that grew/harvested a fresh item has first access to it (within 24 hours)
- After 24 hours, any cluster household may draw from the shared fresh supply
- This applies to all Zone production, not just the designated primary household for that zone — if Household C grows tomatoes in Zone C, they have first pick; unclaimed tomatoes are shared among all households after one day

**Shortfall protocol:**
When shared storage falls below 30 days of supply (approximately 2,400 lbs of mixed stored food for a 12-person cluster), the cluster activates shortfall procedures:
1. All households inventory individual emergency stores (see `individual/02-food.md` — each household maintains 30-day independent supply)
2. Cluster meeting within 48 hours to assess total available food supply
3. Consumption shift: increase hunting and fishing effort; reduce processed-food consumption in favor of calorie-dense stored staples (dried beans, corn, potatoes)
4. Supplemental sourcing: bulk purchase from regional food cooperative, CSA, or farm wholesale if grid economy is functional
5. If supply falls below 15 days for any household: cluster consolidates cooking into one household to reduce waste and improve efficiency (see `01-household-coordination-overview.md` Section 10, Scenario A parallel)

### Record-Keeping

A shared food log — maintained by the household with storage responsibility — tracks production, inventory, and consumption. Minimum contents:

**Production log** (recorded at harvest time):
- Date, crop, weight or volume, producing zone, processing method applied
- Example: "September 14 — Zone B: 380 lbs potatoes harvested, split to root cellar. Condition: excellent except 12 lbs culled (soft spots). Net cellar: 368 lbs."

**Storage inventory** (updated monthly, October through April):
- Itemized list: potato sacks (count + estimated lbs), squash count + variety, canned jar count by type, fermented gallon count, dry goods in sealed buckets (weight)
- Monthly reading establishes the drawdown rate — if October 1 inventory was 8,000 lbs and November 1 inventory is 6,500 lbs, the cluster is consuming 1,500 lbs/month, which against a 12-person cluster implies 5.4 months of supply remaining

**Consumption tracking** (quarterly, not monthly):
- Quarterly inventory reconciles actual consumption against projected rates
- If consumption exceeds projection (e.g., one household is drawing heavily on the shared supply), a cluster conversation happens before the discrepancy becomes a resentment

**The shared food log is a social document, not just an accounting spreadsheet.** Clusters that track food together build shared ownership of the system's health. When everyone knows what's in the cellar, everyone has a stake in conserving it.

## Section 6: Integration with Water, Energy, and Healthcare

### Water Integration

Food production and water coordination interact in four specific ways (see `02-water-coordination.md` for full water system detail):

**Irrigation scheduling**: The cluster's shared cistern drought protocol (Stage 1 and Stage 2) must be integrated with the food production schedule. The most water-intensive food production period (July–August) coincides with the Midwest's driest months. The food coordinator and water steward should share data: if the cistern is declining in August during a drought, the food coordinator needs to know which beds to sacrifice first (Zone C market garden herbs are more expendable than Zone B potatoes).

**Canning water demand**: A full pressure canning session uses 5–10 gallons of water in the canner + additional for jar washing and prep. Alert the water steward before any session using more than 20 gallons. During Stage 2 drought protocol, canning sessions are restricted to water that can be recycled: the canner water (clean after processing) can be reused as garden water for non-edible plants after cooling.

**Rainwater for irrigation priority**: Zone C market garden beds and Zone B root crops benefit from drip irrigation from individual household cisterns rather than the shared well water. Prioritizing rainwater capture for food irrigation reduces cistern drawdown during growing season — this is the behavioral complement to the cistern design choices described in `02-water-coordination.md`.

**Food-water quality note**: Canning water should be tested for nitrates before use during spring (April–May) thaw season (see `02-water-coordination.md` Section 4 on spring contamination risk). Do not use well water exceeding 5 mg/L nitrates for infant food preparation even if below the 10 mg/L EPA maximum.

### Energy Integration

Food processing is the single highest intermittent electrical load in the cluster's energy system (see `01-household-coordination-overview.md` Section 9 on seasonal load planning):

**Scheduling food processing for peak solar**: The pressure canner and electric dehydrator together draw 2,500–3,500 watts during operation. Schedule processing sessions between 10 AM and 4 PM during August–September when Zone 5 solar generation is near maximum (5.5–6.5 peak hours/day). Running the canner at 8 PM draws from battery reserves needed overnight.

**Propane-first canning strategy**: The shared propane-fueled canning kitchen burners (see Section 3) eliminate the electrical load concern entirely for pressure canning. Propane is the preferred energy source for canning — it is not weather-dependent, provides more controllable heat than electric for dial-type pressure canners, and does not compete with the solar-battery system's other loads.

**Freezer priority**: During any solar shortage period (see `01-household-coordination-overview.md` Section 9, load management triggers), the shared freezer and individual household freezers are the highest-priority non-medical electrical loads. A freezer failure in October (before the root cellar is fully loaded for winter) can mean 200+ lbs of food loss. If battery reserves drop below 40% for more than 2 consecutive days during harvest season, run the generator for 4 hours to maintain freezer temperature.

**Energy-free food strategies as backup**: If energy systems are severely constrained for an extended period, the cluster should shift toward energy-free preservation: lacto-fermentation, root cellar storage, and dried goods. A cluster that has dehydrated sufficient dried corn and beans requires almost no energy to access its food supply all winter.

### Healthcare Integration

The nutrition quality of the cluster's food supply directly affects healthcare outcomes (see `individual/05-healthcare.md` for health baseline):

**Nutrient-dense crop prioritization**: A cluster that grows primarily caloric staples (corn, potatoes, beans) will be calorically adequate but may be deficient in vitamins C, A, D, and B12 during the winter dormant window. The market garden in Zone C specifically exists to provide diversity: leafy greens (vitamin K, folate, calcium), carrots (beta-carotene, vitamin A precursor), tomatoes (vitamin C, lycopene). Fermented vegetables provide B vitamins and probiotic cultures that support immune function [16].

**Caloric contingency for medical events**: The shortfall protocol (Section 5) notes that one household's medical crisis eliminates that household's food production labor. The cluster's food governance agreement should specify that during a medical crisis, other households cover the affected household's food production responsibilities for up to 4 weeks (mirroring the crisis support protocol in `01-household-coordination-overview.md` Section 6). Food distribution to the affected household continues at full per-capita share regardless of reduced labor contribution.

**Kitchen hygiene during food preservation**: Botulism risk is real and severe in home pressure canning. The shared canning kitchen must maintain basic sanitation: jar lids must be new for each canning session, processing times must follow USDA-tested recipes exactly [14], and any jar that does not seal during processing must be refrigerated and consumed within a week (not re-canned, not stored unsealed). At least one cluster member should complete the National Center for Home Food Preservation online canning certification course (free at nchfp.uga.edu).

## Section 7: Midwest-Specific Challenges and Adaptations

### Spring Thaw Flooding (March–April)

Low-lying garden areas in the Midwest can receive surface flooding during spring thaw — snowmelt + spring rain can saturate the soil profile and deposit surface runoff contamination in garden beds within 48 hours. Zones in the 100-year floodplain should not be used for root crops intended for fresh eating without testing for bacterial contamination post-flood.

**Mitigation**: Site root crop production (Zone B) at least 12 inches above the FEMA 100-year floodplain elevation. Install raised beds for Zone C market garden to ensure drainage during wet springs. Any bed flooded by surface water should be rested for the current season and planted with nitrogen-fixing cover crops instead of vegetables. Soil test for E. coli if flooding occurred in a zone scheduled for harvest.

### May Tornado Season and Labor Disruption

Zone 5 Midwest sees peak tornado activity May through June — exactly when corn, beans, and sweet potato planting is underway [19]. A tornado event affecting one household during the critical planting window can delay corn planting by 1–2 weeks, which in Zone 5 reduces yield by 3–5% per week of delayed planting.

**Contingency protocol:**
- If any household experiences a property-disrupting event during May 1–31, other households immediately take over that household's planting responsibilities in Zone A or Zone B — no negotiation required, this is written into the labor sharing agreement
- "Planting party" protocol: If Household A cannot plant Zone A during the May 15–25 corn window, all available labor from B and C reports to Zone A on the next suitable day. Missing the window by 2 weeks is acceptable; missing it by 4+ weeks means lower caloric yield for the season and triggers a fall storage adjustment
- Tornado damage to the shared root cellar or canning kitchen: immediate cluster assessment within 24 hours; use individual household storage as bridge; repair before October 15 is the hard deadline

### October Freeze-Thaw Cycles and Perennial Management

October in Zone 5 typically sees multiple freeze-thaw cycles before the first sustained hard freeze (below 28°F). These cycles are particularly hard on perennials that have not properly hardened off.

**Perennial protection protocol for Zone C:**
- October 1–15: Apply 4–6 inch mulch layer (straw or wood chips) over all perennial root zones — asparagus crowns, strawberry beds, berry bushes, young fruit tree root flares
- October 15: Hard deadline for all harvest from perennial fruit trees; remaining fruit left on trees after this date is likely to be damaged by freeze-thaw and lost
- Garlic planting (Zone B): October 15–31, after first frost but before the ground freezes solid. Garlic planted in this window overwinters successfully and emerges in March for the cluster's earliest spring harvest. This is one of the cluster's most valuable scheduling tasks — 20 minutes of planting in October returns 6–10× yield per square foot in July [3]
- Young fruit trees: Water deeply before ground freezes; this is critical for first- and second-year trees, which can be killed by winter desiccation more readily than by cold

### Winter Storage Gap (November–March) — The Hard Constraint

Zone 5 produces zero fresh vegetables from the ground December through February. The first 6 months of the cluster's operation must be oriented toward solving this problem. The root cellar, canning system, and preservation protocols described in Sections 3–4 are not optional features — they are the architecture of winter food security.

**Practical implications:**
- All production planning starts from October 15 working backward: "What do we need in the root cellar by October 15, and how much land and labor does that require?"
- The cluster needs approximately 10,000–12,000 lbs of stored food to feed 12 people from November through late March at 2,000 cal/day/person — accounting for the mix of calorie-dense staples and high-water-content canned goods
- Indoor growing as supplement: A single household with a south-facing window bank can grow microgreens and sprouts (30–50 lbs/year; negligible caloric contribution but significant vitamin value) throughout winter. One grow light shelf ($100–150) in Zone B's storage household adds fresh vitamin C through February

### Tick and Pest Seasonal Patterns (May–October)

Lyme disease and Rocky Mountain Spotted Fever risk peaks May–October (see `midwest/calendar.md`). Field work during this period — weeding, harvesting, animal care — requires standard tick precautions: permethrin-treated clothing for outdoor work, full-body tick checks after field time, prompt removal within 24 hours.

**Pest management at crop level:**
- Squash vine borer (Zone A, July–August): Lays eggs at base of squash stems; larvae kill plants from inside. Row cover during the egg-laying period (June 15–July 15), then removed for pollination. An alternative is direct-seeding a second succession planting of butternut squash in July — these miss the first borer generation and often produce through October
- Colorado potato beetle (Zone B, May–August): Hand-pick adults and egg masses; introduce parasitic wasps if available; consider Bt-based spray (approved organic, low toxicity). This is the single most significant pest threat to Zone B's caloric staples
- Aphid pressure (Zone C, June–September): Integrated pest management: encourage ladybugs and lacewings; use water blast; introduce ducks if duck flock is operational (ducks eat aphids). Neem oil spray as last resort before any synthetic chemical

**Root cellar pest management:**
- Mouse exclusion is critical: all root cellar entry points must be sealed with hardware cloth (1/4 inch mesh); a single mouse family in a root cellar can destroy 50+ lbs of stored produce. Inspect and re-seal every October before stocking
- Apple and pear storage: Wrap individually in newspaper; separate from root vegetables (ripening ethylene from apples accelerates potato sprouting); store in slatted wooden crates in the curing bay

## Section 8: Implementation Timeline

### Phase 1: Foundation (Months 1–6, Pre-Season)

**Months 1–2: Planning, agreement, site assessment**
- Draft and sign the cluster food coordination addendum to the main MOU (`01-household-coordination-overview.md` Worksheet templates): zone assignments, labor rotation schedule, distribution formula, storage governance, livestock governance if applicable
- Complete a soil test on all three production zones ($15–25/zone through county extension) — this is the most valuable $45–75 the cluster will spend before planting; it determines what amendments are needed and where each crop zone should be located
- Identify the shared root cellar site; obtain any needed permits (most rural counties require a building permit for any structure over 100 sq ft, but buried root cellars are often permitted-exempt; check local code)

**Months 3–4: Individual household food hardening**
Before any shared infrastructure is built, each household must have:
- Minimum 30-day independent food supply (per `individual/02-food.md`)
- Functional individual food preservation equipment (at least one canning pot and 24 quart jars)
- Basic seed starting setup for spring transplants

**Months 5–6: Shared equipment acquisition**
- Purchase and store shared processing equipment: pressure canner(s), grain mill, food dehydrator
- Begin root cellar construction if timeline allows (April–May is ideal for excavation in Zone 5 when ground is workable but not waterlogged)
- Draft the shared food log template; designate which household maintains it

**Phase 1 capital cost per household**: $800–2,500 depending on root cellar construction method and what equipment each household already owns

### Phase 2: Pilot Season (Year 1 Growing Season)

**April–October: First full growing season under cluster coordination**
- Execute the zone assignments and labor rotation calendar as designed — do not improvise mid-season
- Accept that the first season will reveal problems the planning did not anticipate: planting timing conflicts, labor imbalances, crop failures in one zone that the others were not prepared to absorb
- Document every deviation from the plan in the shared food log, not to assign blame but to improve the plan for Year 2
- Aim for 60–75% of the preservation volume targets in Section 4 — a first-season cluster achieving this benchmark is on track

**October: Year 1 harvest review (full cluster meeting)**
- Compare actual production against plan by zone and crop
- Review labor log: which household overcontributed? Which under-contributed? Adjust Year 2 zone assignments if needed
- Assess root cellar stocking: is the cluster entering winter with 90+ days of supply for all 12 people?
- Identify which governance provisions worked and which need revision

### Phase 3: Scale and Optimize (Year 2+)

**Year 2 improvements based on pilot season data:**
- Adjust crop allocations based on actual yield data (if Zone B's sweet potato bed underperformed, reduce its area and add to Zone A's bean acreage)
- Add a second livestock type if Year 1 chicken system is operational and relationships are strong
- Complete any root cellar construction that was deferred from Phase 1
- Add garlic as a primary crop in Zone B (first fall planting in October of Year 1, harvest July of Year 2)
- Begin perennial establishment in Zone C: plant fruit trees and berry bushes in April, knowing they will not produce for 2–3 years — this is the most important Year 2 investment for long-term cluster food security

**Year 3+: Perennial system begins producing**
- Apple and pear trees first harvest (modest, 10–30 lbs/tree)
- Asparagus bed established and producing (planted Year 1 or 2; takes 2 years to first harvest)
- Hazelnut bushes reaching productive size (first meaningful harvest Year 4–5) [20]
- Berry bushes (currants, gooseberries) at peak production
- The perennial system, once established, provides roughly 500–800 lbs of fruit and nuts per year with 80% reduction in labor vs. annual crops. This is the cluster's most significant long-term resilience investment.

## Section 9: International Precedent

### Cuba — Special Period Household Food Clusters (1990–1995)

When the Soviet Union collapsed in 1989–1991, Cuba lost approximately 85% of its import capacity and food production dropped 50% between 1989 and 1994. Average Cuban caloric intake fell by 35–40%. The government's response was decentralized: urban households were given access to land and an agrarian reform dismantled state farms in favor of small cooperatives [21, 22]. By 2002, Cuba had met the goal of providing every settlement of 15+ houses with its own food production capacity through organopónicos (urban raised-bed gardens), community gardens, or individual plots. Havana alone now has 26,000 gardens covering 2,439 hectares producing 25,000 tons of food annually, with 40% of all households involved in urban agriculture [22].

The transferable lesson for the Midwest 3-household cluster: household-scale food production was not a marginal improvement during the Cuban crisis — it was the primary food security response for an entire nation. Cuba's organopónico model documented that small-group coordination (neighbors sharing land, tools, and knowledge) was more productive per unit area than either isolated household plots or state-organized agriculture. The cluster food governance structure described in this document is in direct lineage with what Cuba implemented under extreme constraint.

### Andean Traditional Polyculture — Elevation-Tier Risk Distribution

Andean agricultural systems are among the longest-running documented polyculture models on Earth, operating at altitude gradients from 7,000 to 14,000 feet across the Peruvian, Bolivian, and Ecuadorean highlands [23]. The core innovation is vertical diversification: different crops at different elevations create a portfolio of production zones with different microclimates, different frost timing, and different rainfall patterns. A crop failure at one elevation is rarely replicated at all elevations simultaneously — precisely the same logic that justifies distributing the Midwest cluster's production across three different household sites with different soil profiles, sun exposures, and drainage characteristics.

The FAO has recognized Andean agricultural systems as Globally Important Agricultural Heritage Systems (GIAHS) specifically because of their demonstrated long-term food security performance through polyculture and risk distribution [24]. The Midwest cluster does not need to operate at Andean complexity to benefit from the core principle: never concentrate production in one place, one crop, or one set of conditions.

### West African Family Compound Farming — Perennial Integration

Traditional West African compound farming systems provide the most direct model for the cluster's Zone C perennial integration strategy. The compound homestead places intensive production at the center (the Zone C equivalent) surrounded by more extensive field crops (Zone A and B equivalents), with perennials integrated throughout at multiple canopy heights [25]. Fertility maintenance occurs through integration of household refuse, crop residues, and animal manure — precisely the chicken manure → compost → Zone A soil amendment loop described in Section 2.

FAO documentation of West African compound farming notes: "The compound farm or home garden, together with animals, forms one of the most sustainable traditional farming systems; it maximizes biological production, protects the soil against erosion and can provide a varied and nutritious diet on a continuous basis, thus ensuring food security at the household level." [25] This is also a description of what the Zone C cluster household is designed to achieve.

The particular relevance for Zone 5: the compound farming model demonstrates that perennial integration at the household scale is sustainable over multi-generational timescales, not merely over one or two production seasons. The fruit trees and hazelnut bushes the cluster plants in Year 1–2 are not short-term investments — they are the foundation of the cluster's food system 20 years from now.
