# Household Healthcare Coordination — 3-Household Scale
> **Region**: Midwest US (Zone 5) | **Tracks**: Rural + Suburban
> **Scale**: 3 households, 10–14 people, mixed ages including children and elderly
> **Cross-references**: [individual/05-healthcare.md](../individual/05-healthcare.md) · [01-household-coordination-overview.md](01-household-coordination-overview.md) · [02-water-coordination.md](02-water-coordination.md) · [03-food-coordination.md](03-food-coordination.md) · [04-energy-coordination.md](04-energy-coordination.md) · [midwest/calendar.md](../midwest/calendar.md)

## The Most Important Finding

A single adult managing healthcare alone — the `individual/05-healthcare.md` model — is constrained in two ways that no amount of skill or supplies can overcome: they cannot monitor themselves during unconsciousness, and they cannot sustain 24-hour care for a seriously ill person without collapsing from exhaustion within 48 hours. A 3-household cluster eliminates both constraints. Three households, each with a trained first responder, a documented supply reserve, and a written care rotation protocol, can monitor a critically injured person around the clock, manage a multi-person gastrointestinal outbreak while maintaining supply operations, and sustain care for a chronic-condition decompensation through a multi-day outage — none of which is achievable alone.

The cluster healthcare model rests on four structural advantages over the individual model: shared expertise distributed across three trained responders rather than concentrated in one; supply redundancy at 12-month depth rather than 30-day depth; rotation protocols that prevent caregiver burnout during prolonged illness; and multi-household accessibility that prevents isolation from compounding a medical crisis into a mental health crisis. These advantages do not emerge automatically from proximity — they require a skills audit, a governance agreement, a documented chronic-disease registry, and a coordinated supply plan built before any emergency occurs.

Cuba's consultorios system, which assigned one trained community health worker pair per 50–150 households as neighborhood-level primary care managers, demonstrated that prevention-oriented community-based primary care dramatically outperforms individual or hospital-only models in resource-constrained settings — infant mortality fell from 46 per 1,000 live births in 1960 to 4.3 per 1,000 by 2016, including through the supply-constrained Special Period [1]. Rwanda's post-genocide community health worker program, built when the health system had essentially no trained professionals, achieved a 70% reduction in under-five mortality between 2000 and 2017 using three-person village health worker teams — an architecture very close to the 3-household cluster [2]. The lesson from both: coordination and training are more effective than any single piece of equipment.

This document covers cluster-level healthcare coordination exclusively. It assumes each household in the cluster has already built or is building the individual-scale medical capabilities described in `individual/05-healthcare.md` — the MARCH protocol, hemorrhage control, wound management, shock recognition, anaphylaxis response, antibiotic selection, oral rehydration, fracture management, and Midwest-specific health threats. This document begins where that one ends: at the boundary between one person's first-aid kit and the cluster's shared medical infrastructure.

## Section 1: Cluster Medical Readiness Audit

### Skills Inventory — Who Knows What

The starting point for every cluster healthcare plan is an honest inventory of existing skills. Most clusters will have more competence than they expect — former military, nurses who left practice, EMT recertifications, veterinary experience, midwifery training. The audit surfaces that knowledge and identifies training gaps before a gap matters.

**Skill categories to document for each adult cluster member:**

| Skill Level | Description | Source | Refresher Needed |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Basic CPR/AED** | Chest compressions, rescue breathing, AED operation | AHA/Red Cross certification | Every 2 years [3] |
| **First Aid** | Wound care, bleeding control, burn management, fractures, splinting | Red Cross First Aid course | Every 2–3 years |
| **Advanced bleeding control** | Tourniquet, wound packing, chest seals, hemostatic gauze | STOP THE BLEED or TECC course | Every 2 years |
| **Chronic disease management** | Glucose monitoring, insulin administration, blood pressure interpretation | Prior caregiver experience, diabetes education | Annual review |
| **Medication administration** | Oral, IM, or IV medications; reading drug labels; pediatric dosing | Nursing, pharmacy, caregiver experience | Annual review |
| **Dental emergency** | Temporary filling, abscess recognition, dental extraction (advanced) | Prior training | Annual review |
| **Mental health first aid** | Crisis de-escalation, suicidal ideation response, panic management | MHFA certification (8 hours) | Every 3 years |
| **Obstetric/neonatal** | Childbirth assistance, neonatal assessment, postpartum hemorrhage recognition | Prior training or midwife course | Continuous |

### Training Rotation Design

The cluster's training architecture should distribute competence so that no single household is the only source of critical skill. The target is: every household has at least one member certified in Basic CPR/AED and First Aid, and at least two of three households have a member with advanced bleeding control training.

**Recommended sequence for a cluster starting from scratch:**

- **Month 1**: All adult cluster members complete AHA Heartsaver CPR/AED course (4 hours, ~$60/person). Certifications valid 2 years [3].
- **Month 2**: One member per household completes Red Cross First Aid certification. At least one completes STOP THE BLEED instructor training so they can teach others in the cluster.
- **Month 3**: Cluster runs two tabletop scenarios: (1) severe laceration with arterial component, (2) unconscious adult found unresponsive. Debrief using MARCH protocol.
- **Months 4–6**: Any members with prior nursing, EMT, or clinical experience teach skill sessions informally — medication administration, wound irrigation, fracture splinting.
- **Annually**: CPR recertification for all; scenario drill simulating the most likely cluster emergency (tornado injury, multi-person GI outbreak).

The American Red Cross documents that CPR skill retention declines within a few months of initial training and less than half of participants can pass a skills test one year after training [3]. Monthly scenario practice — even 20-minute tabletop discussions — substantially compensates for this decay.

### Communication and Accessibility Protocols

Healthcare communication within the cluster must accommodate every member, not just English-speaking able-bodied adults. Document in advance:

- **Non-English speakers**: Maintain a laminated symptom chart in each household member's primary language. Key phrases: "I have pain here," "I cannot breathe," "I am very dizzy," "I cannot see clearly," rated by severity.
- **Young children**: Children under 6 reliably cannot localize pain or describe symptoms. Train cluster adults to use the FLACC scale (Face, Legs, Activity, Cry, Consolability — 0–2 points each, total 0–10) for non-verbal pain assessment [4].
- **Deaf or hard-of-hearing**: Keep a whiteboard and markers accessible in the shared medical supply hub. Ensure at least one responder knows basic medical ASL signs.
- **Cognitive impairment (dementia)**: Document each affected person's baseline behaviors, pain expressions, and communication patterns. Deviation from baseline is a better illness indicator than verbal complaint.

## Section 2: Shared Medical Supply Infrastructure

### Architecture: Hub Plus Per-Household Reserve

The cluster supply model mirrors the water and energy coordination architecture: each household maintains a 30-day reserve of critical supplies (the individual layer), and a centralized hub — located in the largest, most accessible household — maintains 12-month depth on shared supplies.

**Hub location criteria**: Easily accessible to all three households (not behind locked gates, not upstairs if elderly present), climate-controlled (medications degrade above 77°F and in high humidity), secure from children, and with a printed inventory on the door visible without opening cabinets.

## Section 3: Chronic Disease Management Coordination

### Confidential Registry — Know Before the Emergency

The cluster's chronic disease registry is a single document, kept in the shared medical hub and in each household's copy. It covers every cluster member's relevant conditions, current medications, medication schedules, and upcoming refill dates. The registry is not shared outside the cluster. Access is by any cluster adult in an emergency. Format:

```
Name: [First name or code]
Conditions: [Hypertension, asthma, Type 2 diabetes, psychiatric, autoimmune, other]
Medications: [Name, dose, frequency, prescribing physician]
Next refill due: [Date]
Current supply on hand: [Days]
Equipment needed: [Glucometer, inhaler, BP cuff, CPAP, refrigeration]
Emergency contacts: [Physician name and phone]
Allergies: [Drug, environmental, food — severity]
Advance directive location: [Where document is stored]
```

### Insulin and Cold-Chain Management

If any cluster member uses insulin, refrigeration is a medical necessity — not a convenience. Insulin should be stored at 36–46°F (2–8°C) until opened; most formulations remain effective for 28 days at room temperature not exceeding 86°F (30°C) [8]. This creates two distinct risk windows in Zone 5:

**Summer power outage (June–August)**: Ambient temperatures routinely exceed 86°F. A closed refrigerator maintains safe temperature for approximately 2–4 hours [9]. For outages beyond 4 hours:
- Step 1: Move insulin to an insulated cooler with ice packs; do NOT allow insulin to freeze.
- Step 2: If the cluster's shared LiFePO4 battery bank (per `household/04-energy-coordination.md`) is above 40% SOC, maintain the refrigerator as a Tier 1 load.
- Step 3: If grid and battery both fail: a solar-powered cooler drawing 40–60W maintains safe temperature indefinitely. Dedicate this load to Tier 1 status in the energy shedding protocol.
- Step 4: If all power lost: a vacuum-insulated flask with ice water can maintain 40°F for 12–24 hours. Ice production from a hand-cranked system or shared chest freezer during preserved power is the supply input.

**Winter (November–March)**: Outdoor temperature is cold enough to use as a natural refrigerator, but freezing destroys insulin [10]. Keep insulin in a chest or bag insulated enough to stay above 32°F even overnight. Frozen insulin loses structural integrity and becomes ineffective.

### Medication Coordination and Bulk Supply Planning

The primary supply chain risk for chronic medications is not emergency unavailability — it is simply that most people maintain 30-day supplies. A 5-day power outage with closed roads can exhaust that buffer.

**Target**: Each cluster member taking a chronic daily medication maintains a 90-day personal supply at all times. Cluster goal is that no more than one member's supply falls below 30 days simultaneously.

**Coordination mechanism**: Quarterly review at cluster meeting. Each member reports days of supply on hand. Any member under 45 days triggers a discussion: can they request a 90-day fill? Can another cluster member with the same medication supplement short-term? Is there a prescriber-approved therapeutic alternative that can be stored in the shared hub?

**Particularly time-sensitive medication classes** (withdrawal or decompensation risk within days):
- Insulin (diabetes): decompensation within 24–72 hours in Type 1; days to weeks in Type 2
- Antihypertensives: acute hypertensive crisis in some formulations with abrupt discontinuation
- Anticonvulsants (seizure medications): seizure risk within hours to days of missed doses
- Psychiatric medications (SSRIs, SNRIs, antipsychotics): discontinuation syndrome affects 15–50% of patients and can begin within 1–4 days depending on half-life [11]
- Asthma controller medications (inhaled corticosteroids): deterioration over days; acute bronchospasm within minutes without rescue inhaler

**Psychiatric medication protocol**: SSRIs and SNRIs with short half-lives (paroxetine, venlafaxine) carry the highest discontinuation syndrome risk — symptoms including dizziness, electric-shock sensations, severe anxiety, and insomnia can begin within 24 hours [11]. Pre-plan with each affected member's prescriber: (1) which medications can be safely missed for 1–3 days, (2) which require a tapering protocol if supply is lost, and (3) whether there is a longer-half-life alternative (fluoxetine) that can be substituted in emergencies. Document this plan in the chronic disease registry.

### CPAP and Electrically-Dependent Medical Equipment

Sleep apnea therapy via CPAP is the most common electrically-dependent non-medication medical need in this demographic. A single night without CPAP therapy causes measurable drops in blood pressure regulation and cognitive performance [12]. For the cluster energy plan (per `household/04-energy-coordination.md`), CPAP is a Tier 1 medical load.

**CPAP backup architecture:**
- CPAP with humidifier: 60–150W; without humidifier: 30–60W
- A 300 Wh dedicated battery (EcoFlow RIVER 2, ~$250) runs a humidifier-off CPAP for one full night
- The cluster's shared battery bank handles CPAP as a baseline Tier 1 load if above 20% SOC
- Efficiency tip: disable heated humidifier during outages; a DC power cord (available for most CPAP models) reduces consumption by 20–30% vs. AC adapter conversion

Document every cluster member's wattage in the energy coordination load catalog. An unaccounted 60W medical load running 8 hours/night adds 480 Wh/day to the cluster energy budget — significant in a winter deficit scenario.

## Section 4: Pediatric Care Specialization

### Why Children Require a Separate Protocol

Children under age 12 differ from adults in ways that matter acutely in a resource-limited medical environment: they cannot reliably self-report symptoms, medications are dosed by weight not age, dehydration progresses faster (higher surface-area-to-volume ratio), febrile seizures occur exclusively in this age group, and developmental regression under prolonged isolation can cause lasting harm if not recognized.

### Pediatric Weight-Based Dosing Quick Reference

The following doses apply to children 6 months and older. For children under 6 months, consult a physician for all medications. Weight should be used over age whenever possible [5].

| Medication | Dose | Max Single Dose | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Acetaminophen | 15 mg/kg every 4–6 hours | 1,000 mg | Not to exceed 5 doses in 24 hours; do not combine with other acetaminophen products |
| Ibuprofen | 10 mg/kg every 6–8 hours | 400 mg | Over 6 months only; with food; avoid if dehydrated (renal effect) |
| Diphenhydramine | 1.25 mg/kg every 4–6 hours | 50 mg | Over 2 years only; causes sedation; not for infants |
| Oral rehydration solution | 5 mL/kg per hour for mild dehydration | Unrestricted | WHO formula preferred; see Section 7 |

**Reference weights by age for estimation when scale is unavailable** (Broselow tape preferred):
- 1 year: ~10 kg | 2 years: ~12 kg | 4 years: ~16 kg | 6 years: ~21 kg | 8 years: ~26 kg | 10 years: ~32 kg

### Common Pediatric Crises — Cluster Management

**Febrile seizures (6 months–5 years)**:
Febrile seizures occur in 2–5% of children and are typically benign — they do not cause brain damage, affect intelligence, or predict epilepsy [13]. Management during the event: place child on the floor on their side (recovery position), clear surrounding hazards, do not restrain, never place anything in the mouth. Seizures typically resolve within 1–3 minutes. Post-seizure: assess airway and consciousness, give age-appropriate acetaminophen for fever comfort. A child who returns to their normal self within 30–60 minutes after a brief seizure (<15 minutes, single event) requires monitoring but not emergency evacuation. Prolonged seizure (>15 minutes), multiple seizures in 24 hours, or failure to return to baseline within 1 hour: these are evacuation criteria [13].

**Croup (6 months–3 years, fall/winter)**:
Barking cough, stridor, worse at night. Management: take child outside into cool night air for 15–20 minutes (cold air reduces airway swelling), or run a hot shower and sit with child in steam. Humidified air helps. If stridor at rest (not just when crying or coughing): emergency — compromised airway.

**Dehydration from rotavirus or norovirus**:
Children dehydrate faster than adults. Assess: sunken eyes, dry mucous membranes, decreased urination (no wet diaper in 8 hours), skin tenting, altered mentation. Mild dehydration: ORS at 5 mL/kg/hour for 3–4 hours, then resume age-appropriate diet. Moderate dehydration: ORS aggressively, 10 mL/kg/hour; alert all cluster healthcare responders. Severe dehydration with altered mentation: evacuation priority [14].

**Otitis media (children under 3)**:
Ear pain, often with fever, irritability, pulling at ears. High-pitched screaming in non-verbal children is a common presentation. Management: age-appropriate ibuprofen or acetaminophen for pain and fever. Many cases resolve without antibiotics in 2–3 days. Amoxicillin (40–45 mg/kg/day divided BID for 10 days) is first-line if fever persists beyond 48 hours, symptoms worsen, or child is under 2 years.

### Developmental and Social Continuity During Extended Isolation

Children under age 5 are in critical windows for language, social-emotional, and cognitive development. Prolonged social isolation causes measurable developmental regression: reduced vocabulary growth, increased behavioral difficulties, and impaired development of peer interaction skills [15]. For a cluster planning for multi-week or multi-month isolation scenarios:

- **Structured daily schedule**: Children thrive on routine. Maintain consistent wake times, mealtimes, activity windows, and bedtimes even when external schedules collapse. Post the schedule on the wall.
- **Cross-household socialization**: Children in different households should interact daily — the cluster structure enables this in ways unavailable to isolated single households.
- **Curriculum continuity**: Khan Academy works offline if downloaded; physical math workbooks and phonics readers are grid-independent. Designate an "education hour" in the schedule.
- **Physical activity**: Children need 60+ minutes of vigorous physical activity daily [16]. This is achievable outdoors regardless of outage status.
- **Symptom watchlist for developmental regression**: Language regression (previously verbal child stops talking), unusual clinginess beyond first 2 weeks, enuresis (bedwetting relapse) in a toilet-trained child, significant sleep disruption — all warrant focused adult attention and adjustment of the social schedule.

## Section 5: Geriatric Care Coordination

### Functional Mapping Before Any Emergency

For every cluster member over age 65, complete a functional map during the preparation phase:

- **Mobility**: Can they navigate their home independently? Can they use stairs? Which stairways? Can they reach the bathroom from their bedroom at night without assistance?
- **Upper extremity reach**: Can they access kitchen cabinets, overhead shelves? Where are medications — accessible without step stool?
- **Outdoor access**: Can they exit the home unassisted via at least two routes? Is there a ramp or step-free entry for emergencies?
- **Sensory**: Hearing aids required? Glasses required for safe movement?
- **Cognitive**: Any documented or suspected cognitive impairment? Who should accompany them in disorienting conditions?

This mapping drives the home modification list in Section 11 (implementation).

### Falls Prevention — The Single Highest-Probability Harm

One in four Americans age 65 and older falls each year [17]. Falls in this age group cause 38,000 deaths annually [17]. In the context of a multi-day outage with reduced lighting, disrupted routines, and physical fatigue, fall risk is substantially elevated. The interventions with the strongest evidence:

- Grab bars in bathroom (at toilet and shower): among the most cost-effective single interventions; bathroom modifications reduce fall incidence most strongly across systematic reviews [17]. Installation cost averages $125–$1,300 depending on wall type [17].
- Remove tripping hazards: loose rugs, electrical cords crossing walkways, clutter on floors. This is a pre-outage task.
- Lighting: LED battery lanterns placed in key locations (bathroom, bedroom-to-bathroom path, stairways) before any outage begins. Pre-position as part of the energy coordination protocol.
- Appropriate footwear: Socks without grip are a fall risk. Stock non-slip socks in the medical supply hub.
- Medication review: Some antihypertensives, sleep aids, and antidepressants increase fall risk. Review any elder's medication list for balance-affecting drugs during the preparation phase.

### Polypharmacy and Simplified Outage Regimens

Elderly cluster members taking 5 or more daily medications face a complexity problem during multi-day outages: cognitive load of managing multiple schedules increases error risk, some medications require refrigeration, and some combinations require monitoring (e.g., warfarin with dietary changes possible during a long outage). Pre-plan with each elder's primary prescriber before any emergency:

- Which medications are most critical (anticoagulants, cardiac medications, diuretics)?
- Which can be safely paused for 3–5 days without significant risk if supply is unavailable?
- Is there a simplified "emergency regimen" — the 3–4 most critical medications — that the prescriber documents in writing?

Document this in the chronic disease registry. Keep it updated at each refill cycle.

### Dementia and Cognitive Impairment Protocols

People with dementia require maintained routine as a primary management strategy. Deviations from familiar environments and schedules cause acute confusion, behavioral agitation, and increased wandering risk. For cluster members with cognitive impairment:

- Document their daily routine in writing: wake time, morning sequence, mealtimes, nap time, evening activities, bedtime.
- Document their safe navigation area: which rooms they know, which they get confused in.
- Designate a primary caregiver within the cluster with secondary backup. If primary is ill, secondary takes over using the written routine.
- Use physical orientation cues: labeled room signs, consistent placement of personal items, familiar music as ambient cue.
- Wandering prevention: Door alarms (battery-operated) on exterior exits. Bell on the front door handle (grid-independent).

## Section 6: Outbreak and Communicable Disease Response

### Tick-Borne Illness Surveillance — Coordinated Cluster Protocol

The Upper Midwest — Illinois, Wisconsin, Indiana, Minnesota — carries the nation's highest burden of Lyme disease and anaplasmosis, transmitted by *Ixodes scapularis* (black-legged tick, also called deer tick) [18]. Ehrlichiosis is present in southern Illinois. Rocky Mountain spotted fever (RMSF), despite the name, causes scattered cases throughout the Midwest, transmitted by the American dog tick [19]. Risk season: April through October, peaking May–July when nymphal ticks are active.

**Cluster tick surveillance protocol:**
- Every cluster member performs a full-body tick check within 2 hours of returning from outdoor work, especially after working in brush, tall grass, or wooded edges.
- If a tick is found: remove with fine-tipped tweezers (grasp close to skin surface, pull upward steadily — no twisting, no petroleum jelly, no heat). Record: date of discovery, estimated attachment duration (was it engorged?), body location, and tick species if identified.
- Log every tick discovery in the cluster health log. If multiple members find ticks in the same work area within a week, adjust work protocols (long sleeves, permethrin-treated clothing, more frequent checks).
- **Post-exposure prophylaxis**: Doxycycline 200 mg single oral dose within 72 hours of a confirmed *Ixodes* tick attached for 36+ hours prevents Lyme disease with 87% efficacy in the landmark Nadelman et al. (2001) trial [6]. This is an IDSA-recommended intervention and the single best return on the antibiotics in the shared supply hub. Keep doxycycline accessible.
- **Symptom monitoring** (next 30 days): Erythema migrans (bull's-eye rash) at bite site, fever, fatigue, muscle aches, headache. Any of these within 30 days of tick exposure: begin empirical doxycycline 100 mg BID for 10–14 days and consider evacuation for confirmation.
- RMSF recognition: fever + headache + rash beginning on wrists and ankles spreading centrally within 3 days of a tick bite. High mortality without immediate treatment. Doxycycline 100 mg BID empirically — do not wait for rash to fully develop.

### Seasonal Viral Outbreak Management

**Influenza (October–March, Zone 5)**: Pre-position antivirals if accessible via prescription (oseltamivir / Tamiflu), which reduces influenza duration by 1–2 days and hospitalization risk by up to 63% when taken within 48 hours of symptom onset [20]. More practically: rest, hydration, acetaminophen for fever and myalgia. Isolation of symptomatic household within the cluster structure (remain in own household, reduce inter-household contact for 5–7 days).

**Norovirus** (year-round, peak Nov–Apr): The most likely cause of a multi-person cluster GI outbreak. Incubation 12–48 hours; symptoms 1–3 days; contagious from onset through 72 hours after symptom resolution [21]. Transmission: fecal-oral (unwashed hands, contaminated surfaces, contaminated water). Cluster management protocol:
1. Identify index case and probable exposure event.
2. All symptomatic persons remain in their own household; minimize inter-household contact for the duration.
3. Aggressive handwashing (soap and water — alcohol sanitizers have limited efficacy against norovirus [21]).
4. Disinfect high-touch surfaces in the affected household three times daily with bleach solution (1,000–5,000 ppm; 5–25 tablespoons of 6% bleach per gallon of water).
5. ORS for every symptomatic person. Monitor children and elderly for dehydration progression (see pediatric and geriatric sections above).
6. Food preparation for affected household should be done by a non-symptomatic person from another household if possible, using separate serving utensils.
7. Duration of cluster containment: release isolation 72 hours after the last symptomatic person's symptoms resolve [21].

**RSV (October–March, children)**: For children under 2, RSV is the leading cause of hospitalization. No specific antiviral. Management: suctioning nasal passages (bulb syringe), hydration, positioning upright, fever management. Severe distress, fast breathing, or oxygen saturation below 90% on pulse oximeter: evacuation priority.

### Gastrointestinal Illness from Water or Food

The cluster's water testing protocol (`household/02-water-coordination.md`) includes regular testing for coliform bacteria and nitrates. Any positive coliform test is a medical alert: switch immediately to treated water for all consumption. Chlorination backup: 8 drops of 6% household bleach per gallon, stir and wait 30 minutes before use [22].

Cluster-wide GI illness arising within 48 hours of a shared meal is a foodborne illness investigation. Document: which foods were shared, preparation method, who is affected and who is not. Listeria, Salmonella, E. coli O157:H7, and Campylobacter are the most likely agents in a rural Midwest homestead setting involving home-produced eggs, raw milk, or preserved foods. Bloody diarrhea changes the picture: avoid antimotility agents (loperamide) and consider antibiotic treatment for Salmonella with high fever, or consider evacuation for bloody diarrhea with fever + significant dehydration.

### Fungal Infections Under Humid Conditions

Greywater reuse systems, root cellars, humid basements, and composting areas create conditions favorable for superficial fungal infections. Athlete's foot (tinea pedis) and candidal intertrigo are the most common. Stock topical antifungals: clotrimazole 1% cream (OTC). Oral fluconazole 150 mg single dose is first-line for vaginal candidiasis. Prevention: adequate ventilation in humid working areas, foot hygiene and dry socks, inspection of skin folds in hot weather.

## Section 7: Mental Health and Psychosocial Resilience

### The Invisible Infrastructure

Mental health is the one healthcare domain that a 3-household cluster can provide structurally that an individual cannot: social presence, role assignment, conflict resolution, and mutual accountability. WHO data shows that nearly all people affected by disasters experience significant psychological distress [23]; the cluster's protective factor is that two-thirds of directly exposed disaster survivors do not develop psychopathology when social support is sustained [23].

The cluster's mental health architecture has five layers:

**1. Medication continuity** (highest priority): Psychiatric medications are healthcare infrastructure. Any cluster member on antidepressants, antipsychotics, mood stabilizers, anxiolytics, or ADHD medications should be treated with the same supply-chain rigor as insulin. 90-day supply minimum, documented in the chronic disease registry, tapering plan pre-agreed with prescriber, and emergency contact for prescriber documented.

**2. Structured social schedule**: Unstructured time in high-stress isolation produces rumination, conflict, and mental health deterioration. The cluster should maintain a weekly structure: regular mealtimes as social events, designated work tasks, shared recreation time, and a weekly cluster meeting to discuss logistics, grievances, and plans.

**3. Role assignment with rotation**: Being assigned a meaningful, visible role — water steward, food steward, energy steward, healthcare steward — preserves purpose and agency during extended disruption. Roles should rotate quarterly to prevent burnout and ensure cross-training. Roles assigned to specific adults should include elderly members in capacity-appropriate forms (health log keeper, seed inventory manager, children's reading hour facilitator).

**4. Peer support and conflict resolution**: Twelve people in close proximity under stress will generate interpersonal conflict. Pre-agree on a conflict escalation protocol: (1) direct conversation between parties; (2) mediation by a third cluster member agreed to by both parties; (3) temporary role reassignment to reduce contact if needed; (4) physical space separation (the three-household structure provides this option, unlike a single household). Do not defer conflict resolution to "when things settle down" — unresolved conflict is a medical risk.

**5. Crisis response** (suicidal ideation, panic disorder, acute psychosis): At least one cluster member should complete Mental Health First Aid certification (8-hour course, annually renewable, ~$50). MHFA trains lay people in the ALGEE protocol: Assess for risk, Listen non-judgmentally, Give reassurance and information, Encourage appropriate professional help, Encourage self-help and other support strategies [24]. In a communications-intact scenario, a crisis can be escalated to 988 (Suicide and Crisis Lifeline) by phone or text. In a communications-down scenario: ensure the person is not alone, remove access to lethal means if imminent risk, and involve all cluster adults in observation rotation.

**Creative outlets and normalcy maintenance**: Stock art supplies, musical instruments, physical books, board games, and journaling materials in the supply inventory. These are not luxuries — they are evidence-based mental health interventions. The creative resiliency of isolated communities (accounts from Antarctic expedition teams, submarine crews, COVID-era lockdowns) consistently identifies craft, music, and narrative (storytelling, journaling) as primary coping mechanisms [23].

## Section 8: Maternal and Reproductive Health

### Pregnancy Planning in the Cluster

Any cluster member who may become pregnant during an extended self-sufficiency period should discuss with their OB or midwife what prenatal care elements are feasible at home. The cluster cannot replace prenatal care, but can support it:

- **Prenatal vitamin supply**: Iron, folate, vitamin D — include in the 12-month supply plan.
- **Blood pressure monitoring**: Gestational hypertension and pre-eclampsia are pregnancy emergencies detectable with a blood pressure cuff. Record BP weekly from week 20 onwards. Signs requiring immediate evacuation: BP above 160/110 on two readings 4 hours apart, or BP elevation with severe headache, vision changes, or right upper quadrant pain.
- **Labor preparedness**: If transport to hospital is genuinely at risk during the third trimester (winter outage, flooding), the cluster should discuss with a midwife what supplies and skills would be needed for an assisted home delivery. Minimum kit: sterile gloves, cord clamp or clean ties, sterile scissors, bulb syringe for infant airway, clean towels and receiving blanket, pitocin or misoprostol for postpartum hemorrhage if prescribable, and a trained birth attendant.

### Postpartum and Newborn Support

Postpartum hemorrhage (blood loss >500 mL after vaginal delivery) is the leading cause of preventable maternal death globally [25]. Recognition: soaking more than one pad per hour in the first 24 hours. Response: fundal massage (firm circular massage above the pubic bone to help the uterus contract), oxytocin if available, IV fluids, evacuation.

Postpartum depression affects 10–15% of mothers and can begin anytime in the first year [26]. The cluster structure is a protective factor — the social presence and shared caregiving that characterizes multi-household coordination directly counteracts the isolation that exacerbates PPD. Designate a postpartum support coordinator (ideally a cluster member with prior obstetric or nursing experience) during any cluster member's pregnancy.

### Contraception and Family Planning

Supply-chain disruption affects contraception access. Cluster members should align their family planning intentions with their supply plan:

- Hormonal methods (pill, patch, ring): Maintain 6-month supply. The cluster healthcare planning process is an appropriate time to discuss supply security with individual members.
- IUD and implant: Grid-independent; no supply issue.
- Barrier methods (condoms): Maintain a documented supply in the hub.
- Emergency contraception (levonorgestrel): Maintain 2 units in the hub supply; effective up to 72 hours after unprotected sex, diminishing efficacy to 120 hours.
- Natural family planning / fertility awareness: As a backup if other methods unavailable; efficacy with typical use 76–88% [27]. Training and written cycle-tracking materials should be available.

## Section 9: Integration with Other Phase 2 Systems

### Water (household/02-water-coordination.md)

Water contamination is the single most medically consequential supply chain failure in the cluster. The water coordination document specifies regular coliform and nitrate testing as the cluster's primary disease-prevention intervention. From the healthcare perspective:
- Any cluster member with compromised immunity (chemotherapy, autoimmune disease, transplant) should use boiled or UV-treated water even when coliform tests are negative.
- Oral rehydration solution prepared with contaminated water is contraindicated — maintain bottled or tested water specifically for ORS preparation.
- Post-flood: chlorinate all wells before use, regardless of visual clarity. Floodwater contaminates well heads.

### Food (household/03-food-coordination.md)

Nutrition is medical management for three chronic conditions common in the cluster demographic:

- **Hypertension**: Low-sodium diet. Coordinate with the food preservation section: canning and fermentation often involve salt. Designate low-sodium preserved batches for the hypertensive cluster member. Fresh vegetables from Zone C are the primary low-sodium food supply.
- **Type 2 diabetes**: Carbohydrate management requires access to higher-protein, lower-glycemic foods. The cluster food plan should include adequate legumes, eggs, and greens. The Three Sisters polyculture (Zone A in `household/03-food-coordination.md`) provides beans as a low-glycemic protein source.
- **Wound healing**: Protein requirements increase 50–100% in active wound healing [28]. If a cluster member is recovering from a significant injury, coordinate with the food steward to ensure adequate protein intake from eggs, legumes, and preserved meat in the short-term food plan.

### Energy (household/04-energy-coordination.md)

Medical devices are Tier 1 loads — never shed. These include:
- Insulin refrigeration: 100–150W continuous (refrigerator share)
- CPAP: 30–60W for 8 hours/night (240–480 Wh/night)
- Nebulizer (asthma): 60–80W for 15 minutes/treatment (occasional, but non-deferrable)
- Any ventilator or home oxygen concentrator (rare, but if present: 150–500W continuous — must be sized before any cluster energy planning)

The energy coordination plan should explicitly list every medical load by wattage and hours-per-day in the load catalog. If these loads are not in the catalog, they will be the first things accidentally shed when the battery SOC drops. They must never be shed.

## Section 10: Disaster Scenarios and Contingency Planning

### Scenario 1: Spring Tornado — Traumatic Injury, Single Member

**Event**: Household A member is struck by debris during a spring tornado — suspected fracture (deformity of forearm), scalp laceration, and possible concussion (witnessed head impact, brief loss of consciousness).

**Immediate response (first 30 minutes)**:
1. Activate MARCH protocol from `individual/05-healthcare.md`: control scalp bleeding with direct pressure (scalp is highly vascular; a minor laceration can look dramatic). Assess airway and breathing.
2. Glasgow Coma Scale assessment: eye opening, verbal response, motor response. Score below 14 (any impairment) = evacuation priority.
3. Immobilize suspected fracture: padded splint in position of function (forearm in neutral rotation, slight dorsiflexion of wrist), not over-tight.
4. Wound irrigation and closure: scalp lacerations close well with skin stapler after hemostasis.
5. Assign concussion monitoring role: every 2 hours for first 24 hours — wake the person if sleeping to check orientation and pupil symmetry.
6. Decide transport: if roads are passable, transport to ED for imaging (CT head if any LOC). If not: continue monitoring, maintain 24-hour observation rotation using all three households.

**Care rotation if transport unavailable**: Three households, two-adult alert capacity per household, allows continuous 4-hour monitoring shifts with 8-hour rest periods between shifts.

### Scenario 2: GI Outbreak — Norovirus, 6 of 12 People Affected

**Event**: Six cluster members from two households develop simultaneous nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea within 24 hours of a shared meal. Suspected norovirus based on rapid onset and multi-person involvement.

**Response**:
1. Identify index household(s): affected members confine to their own household. Unaffected household (C) provides food preparation and supply delivery to affected households.
2. ORS for all symptomatic members; fluids every 30 minutes. Children and elderly monitored for dehydration signs hourly.
3. Household C takes over laundry (non-contact dropoff/pickup), cooking, and medication dispensing.
4. Bleach surface disinfection in affected households three times daily; food contact surfaces and bathroom fixtures priority.
5. Alcohol-based sanitizers are insufficient for norovirus — soap and water handwashing required at every contact [21].
6. Isolation lift: 72 hours after last person's symptoms resolve [21].
7. Review food preparation and water source for outbreak origin — if foodborne, identify and discard implicated items; if water-related, emergency chlorination and testing.

### Scenario 3: Diabetic Decompensation — 5-Day Winter Outage

**Event**: Cluster member with Type 2 diabetes, previously well-controlled, develops symptoms consistent with hyperglycemia (excessive thirst, frequent urination, fatigue, blood glucose 350+ mg/dL on cluster glucometer) during day 3 of a 5-day outage with closed roads.

**Response**:
1. Assess: is this DKA or hyperosmolar state? DKA symptoms: fruity breath, nausea, abdominal pain, rapid breathing. If DKA suspected with Type 1 diabetes: evacuation is the goal; this is not manageable in cluster.
2. For Type 2 with hyperglycemia (no DKA): review recent medications (were insulin or oral medications missed?), review food intake (were high-carbohydrate foods consumed in excess during stress?).
3. Re-establish medication schedule. If insulin is available but not being used because patient was "managing with diet" — consider initiating under guidance of any cluster member with clinical familiarity.
4. Increase hydration; monitor glucose every 2–4 hours.
5. If glucose fails to respond to medications within 12 hours, or patient develops any altered mentation: evacuation priority even through difficult conditions.

### Scenario 4: Mental Health Crisis — Prolonged Isolation, Week 3

**Event**: A cluster adult begins expressing hopelessness, withdraws from cluster activities, makes statements about "being a burden," and stops eating reliably. Another cluster member privately expresses concern about possible suicidal ideation.

**Response**:
1. Activate MHFA-trained cluster member for private, non-judgmental assessment. Ask directly about suicidal ideation — asking does not increase risk, and non-asking fails to identify the need [24].
2. If active suicidal ideation with plan: do not leave person alone; involve all cluster adults; remove access to means (medications, firearms, sharp implements) where possible without confrontation; contact 988 by any available communication.
3. If passive ideation (hopelessness, worthlessness, no specific plan): increase contact frequency, assign a specific daily "check-in" partner from a different household, involve person in high-purpose visible roles.
4. Access psychiatric medications if applicable and not already taking them.
5. Structural response: convene a full cluster meeting to collectively acknowledge the difficulty of the situation. Normalizing distress reduces shame. Assign every adult a visible role for the next 7 days. Increase recreational/creative activity time.

## Section 11: Cluster Governance and Medical Ethics

### Triage in Multi-Casualty Cluster Events

If a tornado or other mass-casualty event injures multiple cluster members simultaneously, one trained responder must perform triage before treating anyone. The cluster pre-agrees on START triage principles:
- **Red (Immediate)**: Uncontrolled bleeding, obstructed airway, severe respiratory distress, no consciousness — treat first
- **Yellow (Delayed)**: Fractures, closed wounds, significant but not immediately life-threatening injuries — treat after red
- **Green (Minor)**: Walking wounded, minor lacerations, sprains — can self-manage or assist others
- **Black (Deceased/Expectant)**: No breathing after airway opening, massive unsurvivable trauma — do not divert resources

This protocol overrides personal relationships. Pre-agree that the trained first responder makes triage calls with full cluster authority during a mass-casualty event. Debrief afterward.

### Advance Directives — Cluster Knowledge

Every cluster adult should complete, or have in accessible storage, their advance directive: living will plus designated healthcare proxy. The cluster administrator should know where these documents are — not necessarily read them, but know their location and the healthcare proxy's identity.

Key cluster agreements to document in writing and have all adults acknowledge:
- In an emergency where a person is unconscious with a known allergy (e.g., bee venom), the cluster first responder has pre-agreed authority to administer an epinephrine autoinjector without explicit consent.
- If a person has a documented DNR, all cluster members know and will not initiate CPR in an end-of-life scenario (even if that feels counterintuitive under stress).
- The designated healthcare proxy for each member is documented — this is the person who speaks for them if they cannot speak for themselves.

### Medication Sharing Ethics and Authority

Pre-agree: the healthcare steward (rotated monthly) has authority to access the shared hub and dispense shared OTC medications. Prescription medications for one person's chronic condition are that person's property — they are never redistributed without explicit consent, except in a life-threatening emergency involving that person directly.

### End-of-Life Care in the Cluster Setting

Comfort care at end of life — for an elderly member, a terminally ill person, or after a catastrophic injury — requires cluster preparation. Discuss in advance, not during crisis:
- Who will be present?
- What pain management is available? (Acetaminophen, ibuprofen, and topical lidocaine for procedural pain are accessible; no opioids unless prescribed)
- What spiritual or cultural rituals matter to this person?
- Who has authority to pronounce or record time of death?
- What are the local protocols for death outside a medical facility?

## Section 13: International Precedent

### Cuba's Consultorio System — Proof of Concept for Cluster-Scale Health Coordination

Cuba's primary care model, built around the *consultorio* (neighborhood clinic with one physician-nurse pair serving 120–150 families), is the only modern example of sustained population-level healthcare delivery organized explicitly at neighborhood rather than facility scale. During the Special Period (1990–2000), when the collapse of Soviet subsidies produced severe shortages of medical supplies, pharmaceuticals, and fuel, the system maintained coverage because knowledge and relationships were distributed at community level rather than concentrated in hospitals [1]. Infant mortality fell from 11.1 to 7.2 per 1,000 live births during this period despite collapsing pharmaceutical supply chains [1] — a result directly attributable to prevention-oriented community health work and early identification of illness before it became severe.

The architectural parallel to a 3-household cluster: trained workers embedded in the community, not summoned from outside; preventive surveillance (vaccination, health monitoring) as primary activity; and a documented registry of community members' health conditions maintained at the neighborhood level.

### Rwanda's Binôme CHW Model — Knowledge Distribution Under Extreme Constraint

Rwanda reconstructed its healthcare system from near-zero professional capacity after the 1994 genocide, deliberately choosing a community health worker architecture rather than waiting for hospital and physician infrastructure to be rebuilt. The Binôme model assigns two CHWs per village (a male-female pair plus a maternal specialist), responsible for health screening, treatment of common childhood illnesses, malaria management, maternal monitoring, and linkage to higher-level care when needed [2].

Under-five mortality fell 70% (from 158 to 47 per 1,000 live births) between 2000 and 2017, achieving Millennium Development Goals for maternal and child health three years early — an outcome that Partners in Health researchers explicitly attribute to the CHW infrastructure rather than facility improvements [2]. The relevant structural lesson: distributed knowledge in trained lay health workers operating at the smallest social unit (village, cluster) dramatically outperforms reliance on facility-based care for all but the most complex conditions.
