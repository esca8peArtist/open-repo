## Week 1 (June 1–7): Individual-Scale Deepening

### Purpose

Option B's extended timeline creates space that Options A and C do not have: a week to expand Phase 3 individual-scale frameworks with two or three new methodologies before folding them into the household scaling synthesis in Week 2. This deepening is what justifies the 14-day ramp — the unified June 15 corpus is more comprehensive precisely because Option B did the work Options A and C skip.

### Methodology Expansion 1: Adaptive Capacity Mapping (2 days, June 1–2)

Phase 3 individual-scale documents establish a baseline of preparedness skills and resources. Adaptive capacity mapping goes one level deeper: it identifies not just what a household has, but how well the household can adapt when conditions change faster than plans allow.

The two dimensions of adaptive capacity that Phase 3 does not cover in depth:

**Skill flexibility**: The ability to apply a skill developed in one context to a different context under pressure. A household with one skilled canner has lower adaptive capacity than a household where two people can can. Map this for the five core domains (food, water, energy, health, governance) at the household level.

**Decision speed**: How fast can the household make a non-routine decision? A household that requires full consensus for any change has lower adaptive capacity than one with named domain leads who can act immediately within their authority scope. Map this alongside the coordinator assignment process.

Deliverable by June 2: one-page adaptive capacity worksheet that any household can complete in 30 minutes, producing a self-assessed score on skill flexibility and decision speed for each domain.

### Methodology Expansion 2: Resource Dependency Chain Analysis (2 days, June 3–4)

Phase 3 treats household resources (food stores, water backup, energy backup) as discrete categories. Resource dependency chain analysis reveals the upstream dependencies that determine whether those resources can actually be used.

Example: A household has a hand pump as water backup. But the hand pump requires a specific seal kit for maintenance. The seal kit is sourced from one supplier in the region. If that supplier closes, the backup is non-functional within 18 months. The resource dependency chain is: water backup → hand pump → seal kit → single-supplier availability. A household that has mapped this chain knows to stockpile 3 spare seal kits.

For Week 1, produce a template that guides a household through mapping dependency chains for the three highest-stakes resources: primary water source, primary heating source, primary food production or storage system. The template asks three questions per resource: What inputs does this resource require to function? Where do those inputs come from? What is the backup if the primary input source fails?

Deliverable by June 4: dependency chain analysis template, 2 pages, with one completed example per resource type.

### Methodology Expansion 3: Stress-Test Scenarios for Individual Scale (1 day, June 5)

Phase 5 stress-testing (in PHASE_4_ACTIVATION_OPTION_B.md, Integration Task 3) runs at community scale. Phase 3 individual-scale documents do not include stress-test scenarios. Option B's individual-scale deepening is the right moment to add them.

Three scenarios for household-scale stress testing:

**Scenario 1 (Primary income loss)**: The household's primary earner cannot work for 4 weeks. What financial resources are available? What is the cash runway at current expenses? What household expenses are deferrable? What is the governance impact (does the household coordinator structure still function if one person is incapacitated)?

**Scenario 2 (Infrastructure failure)**: Municipal water service is interrupted for 14 days. Work through the household's backup plan: How many gallons of stored water? How long does that last at current consumption? What is the backup source (hand pump, rain capture, neighbor's well)? How many days before the household is relying on external emergency supply?

**Scenario 3 (Governance incapacity)**: The household's primary decision-maker (most experienced, most respected person) is unavailable for 6 weeks (hospitalization, travel, crisis). Does the household governance structure function without them? Who runs household meetings? Who makes emergency decisions?

Deliverable by June 5: three stress-test scenario templates with guided prompts, one completed example each.

**Week 1 checkpoint** (June 7 evening): Review the three methodology expansions. Confirm each is production-ready: complete, tested on at least one household example, formatted consistently with Phase 3 documents.

## Week 2 (June 8–14): Household Scaling Synthesis and Integration

### Purpose

Week 2 takes the three methodology expansions from Week 1 and integrates them with the household scaling framework (how individual-scale Phase 5 documents adapt to multi-household arrangements) and the Zone 5 adaptation guide. Week 2 also runs the governance charter adoption and Phase 5 integration checkpoints that gate the June 15 publication.

### June 8–10: Household Scaling Synthesis

The household scaling synthesis is the core Week 2 deliverable. It answers the question: for a multi-household arrangement of 2–5 households (8–25 people total), how do the individual-scale methodology expansions from Week 1 change?

For **adaptive capacity mapping**: in a multi-household arrangement, adaptive capacity is pooled across households. A skill that only one household has is still a community asset. The synthesis adds one column to the adaptive capacity worksheet: which households in the arrangement have this skill? This pooling often reveals that multi-household groups have significantly higher adaptive capacity than they perceive, because they are assessing each household independently.

For **resource dependency chain analysis**: multi-household groups can share supply chain risk. If two households both depend on the same single supplier for a critical input, that is a community-level risk. If different households have different suppliers for the same input, that is community-level resilience. The synthesis adds a step: compare dependency chains across households to identify shared vulnerabilities and potential redundancy.

For **stress-test scenarios**: run each scenario at the multi-household level, not just the individual household level. Scenario 1 (income loss): if one household loses primary income, can the other households temporarily absorb some of that household's costs? What is the informal mutual aid structure? Does it need to be formalized?

Deliverable by June 10: household scaling synthesis document (3–4 pages), integrating adaptive capacity mapping, dependency chain analysis, and stress-test scenarios at the 2–5 household scale.

### June 11–12: Governance Charter Adoption

Governance synthesis workshop runs June 1–3 (same 3-hour agenda as Option A Deliverable 1). Between June 3 and June 11, the charter draft circulates for household review.

June 11 evening: community assembly meets for formal charter adoption. Agenda:

- Charter read aloud (15 minutes) — no discussion during reading
- Amendment window (15 minutes) — specific text changes only, not general concerns
- Adoption vote — supermajority required (75%)
- Coordinator names confirmed for all five domains

June 12: amended charter signed by all household governance liaisons. Amendment log completed. Governance is operational.

**Integration checkpoint**: Does the governance charter account for all authority gaps identified during the individual-scale deepening in Week 1? Specifically: does the charter specify what happens when a household's primary decision-maker is incapacitated (Stress-Test Scenario 3)? If not, add one sentence before adoption.

### June 13–14: Phase 5 Integration and Release Readiness

**June 13**: Integration verification. For each of the 12 Phase 5 documents (Waves 1–3), confirm that every decision point named in the document has a corresponding named authority in the Phase 4 governance charter. Check using the authority matrix from PHASE_4_ACTIVATION_OPTION_B.md Integration Task 1.

Expected count of ungoverned decision points: 0–2. If 3 or more ungoverned decision points exist, the Phase 5 editorial track must either add a cross-reference note ("see your governance charter, authority matrix Section [X]") or the Phase 4 charter must add one sentence per ungoverned point before publication.

**June 14 afternoon**: Go/no-go decision.

Go criteria (all four must be met):
1. All 12 Phase 5 documents are in final version — no quality flags, no unfilled placeholders, frontmatter standardized
2. Master table of contents covers all 12 documents with clear wave labels and reading order
3. Phase 4 governance charter is adopted (June 12 vote confirmed) and signed
4. Authority matrix has 0–2 ungoverned decision points remaining

If all four criteria are met: publish June 15 6 PM UTC.

If 1–2 criteria are not met: identify the specific gap, assign a completion owner and a deadline of June 14 11 PM UTC. Proceed to June 15 publication if the gap is closed.

If 3 or more criteria are not met: delay publication to June 17. The unified release advantage is negated if the corpus is not actually unified. Two days of additional integration work is better than a publication with known coherence gaps.

## Integration Checkpoints (3 mandatory syncs)

Three touch-points where Phase 4 and Phase 5 must sync to prevent conflicts. Missing any of these checkpoints creates integration debt that shows up on June 14 as unresolvable last-minute conflicts.

### Checkpoint 1: June 3 Evening — Charter Draft vs. Phase 5 Document List

After governance workshop Day 3, the Phase 4 lead reviews the charter draft against the full list of Phase 5 decision points. "Does any Phase 5 document name an authority that is not in the charter?" Compile a gap list. This list is the Phase 4 work queue for June 4–11.

What makes this checkpoint clear: the gap list exists, has a named owner for each item, and has a resolution target date (no later than June 11).

What to do if the gap list is long (more than 10 items): the Phase 4 methodology deepening in Week 1 is deprioritized in favor of closing the charter gaps. The charter integration is a harder dependency for June 15 publication than the individual-scale methodology expansions.

### Checkpoint 2: June 10 Evening — Cross-References vs. Authority Matrix

After Phase 5 cross-references are drafted (editorial track, June 8–10) and household scaling synthesis is complete (Phase 4 track, June 10), cross-check: do the Phase 5 cross-references point to governance mechanisms that exist in the Phase 4 charter? Example: if Phase 5 Psychological Support says "escalate to community health coordinator," does the Phase 4 charter name a health coordinator with escalation authority?

Map mismatches go on the June 11–12 amendment list. No more than 5 mismatches expected at this checkpoint — if there are more, the governance workshop did not cover all the relevant domains and needs an emergency 2-hour catch-up session before charter adoption.

What makes this checkpoint clear: the cross-reference map has been checked against the authority matrix, all mismatches are identified and assigned, and the amendment list is ready for the June 11 adoption assembly.

### Checkpoint 3: June 14 Morning — Go/No-Go

See Go/No-Go criteria in Week 2 section above. This checkpoint is a formal decision point, not a review. The user makes the call: publish June 15 or delay.

The person who makes the go/no-go decision must have seen: (1) the final version of at least 3 Phase 5 documents (spot-check), (2) the governance charter with amendment log, (3) the authority matrix with ungoverned decision point count.
