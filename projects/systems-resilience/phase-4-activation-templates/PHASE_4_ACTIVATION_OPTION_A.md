## Deliverable 1: Governance Synthesis Workshop Outline (500 words)

### Purpose

Walk 15–30 community members through a 3-hour structured process that converts the governance theory in Phase 3 and the community implementation framework in Phase 5 into an actual, adopted decision-making procedure. This is not a training session. By the end of the 3 hours, the group must have voted on a governance architecture, named coordinators for primary domains, and set a first assembly date.

### Pre-Workshop (30 minutes, facilitator only)

Read: Phase 5 Community Implementation Playbook introduction (45 minutes estimated, read in advance). Print or share access to the household coordinator role cards (from Phase 4 Household Scaling One-Pager below). Prepare one large sheet per segment topic: Current Decisions, Architecture Options, Authority Draft, Household Roles.

### Hour 1: Reality Check (60 minutes)

Open with a concrete, recent decision: "Think of a real decision this group made in the last month — who actually made it?" (Not who was supposed to make it. Who did.) Write every answer on the Current Decisions sheet. This makes implicit governance visible.

Spend 20 minutes on failure mapping: "What decisions have made people angry or feel unheard?" Group answers into three failure types: information failure (we didn't know what we were deciding about), authority failure (the wrong person decided or no one had authority), fairness failure (the process was right but the outcome felt unjust). These three categories are the design targets for the next 2 hours.

Present the three governance architectures that function at 15–30 people. Limit to 5 minutes each:

**Direct Assembly**: Everyone votes on every significant decision. Works at 15–25 people. Breaks when decisions need to be faster than assembly can convene.

**Delegate Council**: Elected or rotating delegates handle routine decisions. Full assembly meets monthly. Domain coordinators (food, water, energy, health) hold daily authority in their domain. Scales to 150+. Most common outcome for groups this size.

**Nested Working Groups**: Small groups govern each domain autonomously; liaisons coordinate across groups. Best when domain expertise is high and autonomy within domains is valued. Breaks when cross-domain decisions require coordination the liaison circle cannot provide.

Close hour 1 with a show of hands on architecture preference. If genuinely split, name it: "We have people who value speed and people who value autonomy — we are designing a hybrid." The most common hybrid: Delegate Council structure with Working Group autonomy within each domain.

### Hour 2: Authority Distribution (60 minutes)

Create a two-column table for each domain: Coordinator Authority (decides daily without escalating) and Assembly Authority (requires group vote). Fill in the five core domains in order: food, water, energy, health, governance. Write actual policy sentences, not bullet points. "The food coordinator may reduce individual rations by up to 10% without assembly approval, provided she notifies the delegate council at the next meeting" is a policy. "Food coordinator manages food" is not.

Assign the escalation protocol: who makes emergency decisions when assembly cannot convene quickly enough? Name one person. Set a time limit on emergency authority (recommend: 48 hours before mandatory assembly notification).

Establish information infrastructure: "Before governance can make a good decision, what does it need to know?" Assign one information coordinator responsible for weekly inventory updates (food, water, fuel, health status). Governance without data is improvisation.

### Hour 3: Adoption and Household Integration (60 minutes)

30-minute breakout by household unit (families or living clusters of 5–10 people). Each unit names coordinators for each domain and designates a governance liaison who will attend community assemblies.

Regroup. Each household reports coordinator names aloud. Facilitator maps them to the policy table created in Hour 2: "You said food coordinator is Jane. In the policy we wrote, Jane has authority to reduce rations by 10%. Jane, do you understand and accept that?" This step prevents the charter from naming an authority that the named person does not actually hold.

Adoption vote: supermajority required (recommend 75%). If supermajority is not reached, the facilitator isolates the one or two contested clauses, adopts the rest, and flags the contested clauses for a 30-day review. Do not delay adoption for a single contested sentence.

Close: state the first assembly date and time aloud. Write it on the wall. Community governance is now operational.

## Deliverable 2: Household Scaling One-Pager (400 words)

### Purpose

Phase 5 Wave 1+2 documents were written for organized communities of 50–200 people. This one-pager translates Phase 5 community-scale mechanisms into household-executable equivalents for multi-household living arrangements (8–25 people across 2–5 physically adjacent households).

### The Translation

| Phase 5 community mechanism | Household equivalent |
|-----------------------------|----------------------|
| Community assembly | Household meeting (all adults) |
| Delegate council | Two-household coordination call (15 min, weekly) |
| Domain coordinator with community authority | Domain lead with household authority |
| Information infrastructure | Shared log (one notebook or shared doc) |
| Governance charter | One-page household agreement |
| Escalation to assembly | Call a household meeting within 24 hours |

### First 48 Hours

Read the Phase 5 Community Implementation Playbook introduction together as a household (allow 45 minutes). Then hold a 60-minute household meeting with three agenda items:

**Item 1** (20 minutes): Current situation assessment. Each adult answers: "What is the thing I am most worried about for our household's resilience in the next 6 months?" Write every answer without evaluation. This is the household risk register.

**Item 2** (30 minutes): Domain lead assignment. For each domain, name one person as lead. This is a starting designation, not a permanent appointment.

- Water lead: tracks storage, filter status, backup source
- Food lead: tracks inventory in days, manages preservation calendar, oversees growing
- Energy lead: tracks fuel, generator/battery status, load priorities
- Health lead: maintains one-page health records per household member, tracks medical supplies
- Governance liaison (if part of a larger community): attends community meetings, reports back

**Item 3** (10 minutes): Schedule the second household meeting (within 10–14 days).

### First Week

Each lead conducts a 30-minute domain audit: Current State (actual situation), Phase 5 Target (what Phase 5 says the household should have), Gap (difference), and Closing Timeline (minimum effort and timeline to close the gap). The second household meeting reviews all five audit results and sets the household's three priority actions for the next 30 days.

### Multi-Household Coordination

When 2–5 households are closely associated (physically adjacent or intentionally cooperating), add one weekly 15-minute coordination call between the governance liaisons of each household. Agenda: what did each household accomplish this week, what is each household struggling with, are there shared decisions that need a joint meeting? This coordination call prevents households from discovering in a crisis that their preparations are incompatible.

## Deliverable 3: Zone 5 Adaptation Guide (400 words)

### Why Zone 5 Differs

Phase 5 documents were written at community-generic level. Zone 5 (Illinois, Indiana, Iowa, Michigan, Wisconsin, Minnesota) has three constraints that change implementation sequence and timing:

1. Winter harshness (November–March): heating is life-or-death. Communities without confirmed backup heating before October 1 are at survival risk.
2. Agricultural compression (May–September): 4-month growing window means every planting deadline is hard. One week late on warm-season crop transplanting (June 10) produces 10–15% yield loss.
3. Weather volatility: summer storms, spring flooding, road closures in winter. Logistics planning is not optional.

### Sequencing Change

Phase 5 Community Implementation Playbook recommends: Information infrastructure → Governance → Food → Security.

For Zone 5, the order is: Governance (governance must be operational before heating season or it will fracture during heating season) → Information infrastructure (in parallel) → Food + Winter preparation (parallel, August is the production deadline) → Security.

The reason for the change: in Zone 5, winter heating allocation is the highest-conflict governance decision a community will face. If governance is not operational before October, that decision will be made by whoever controls the fuel supply — not by a legitimate process. Communities that improvise fuel rationing in January fracture. The decision must be made in June, under no pressure, with full community buy-in.

### Zone 5 Hard Deadlines (June–October)

| Date | Required action | Consequence of missing |
|------|----------------|------------------------|
| June 10 | Warm-season crops (tomatoes, squash, beans) transplanted; planting allocation decided by governance | 10–15% yield loss per week of delay |
| June 15 | Preservation equipment audit complete | No time to acquire equipment before August harvest window |
| July 15 | Winter fuel procurement authorized by governance assembly | Bulk fuel delivery in September (peak demand, higher prices); stockpiling window closes |
| August 1 | Preservation work begins | Peak harvest August–September; preservation window is 9 weeks, not flexible |
| September 1 | Heating system tested at operational temperature | 30-day buffer before consistent heating demand; failure discovered in testing, not in January |
| September 15 | All water infrastructure winterized | October freezes are variable; pipes not winterized by September 15 risk freeze damage |
| October 1 | Governance in winter mode (quarterly review only) | New governance decisions under winter stress conditions; governance fracture risk high |

The governance workshop (Deliverable 1) must include a June assembly agenda item for Zone 5 communities: "If heating fuel reaches 30 days of supply, activate winter ration protocol — [specify temperature target, space consolidation plan, rationing procedure]." This decision must be voted on and recorded before October, not improvised in January.

### Zone 5 Resource Network

Four existing institutions provide Zone 5-specific support that Phase 5 does not cover:

**NRCS county offices**: Free soil health and crop planning consultations. Every county in Zone 5 has one. The single most underused resource for Zone 5 community farming.

**Land-grant extension services**: University of Illinois Extension, Iowa State Extension, Michigan State Extension, Purdue Extension, and UW-Extension all publish Zone 5-specific crop trial data, pest management schedules, and livestock care calendars. Free, annually updated.

**Practical Farmers of Iowa**: farmer-to-farmer research network with Zone 5 field trial data across crop varieties and production methods. Most relevant for communities in Iowa and the western half of Illinois.

**Rural electric cooperatives**: Zone 5 rural areas are predominantly served by RECs with member governance (one member, one vote). Many are exploring community solar and grid-edge storage. A community with a REC relationship has a partner for microgrid development that investor-owned utilities do not provide.
