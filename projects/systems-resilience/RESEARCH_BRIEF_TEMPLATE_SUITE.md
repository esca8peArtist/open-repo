**Author**: [AUTHOR_NAME]

**Brief Version**: 1.0 (pre-author)
**Brief Date**: June 2026

#### Scope Definition

**In scope:**
- Sub-national and community-level access to international networks, seed banks, mutual aid systems, and governance frameworks — specifically how a Zone 5 community accesses these WITHOUT national government intermediation
- International precedents for community-to-community coordination across national borders (food sovereignty networks, diaspora mutual aid, transnational cooperative federations)
- Legal and practical pathways for communities to participate in international knowledge and resource exchange under current US law
- Case studies of communities that have successfully integrated international frameworks at the local level (La Via Campesina local chapters, Transition Towns international network, international seed library affiliates)

**Out of scope:**
- National foreign policy, international trade agreements, or diplomatic relations
- International governance at the state or federal level (WHO, WTO, UN) except where those frameworks have direct community-accessible tools
- Comparative political systems or international law as academic disciplines
- Scenarios requiring communities to act outside US law

**Zone 5 anchor**: Northern Illinois, Iowa, and southern Wisconsin communities. International coordination is most immediately relevant through: agricultural diaspora networks (immigrant farming communities in Zone 5), international seed sovereignty networks with US affiliates, and transnational cooperative federations with Midwest chapters.

---

#### Research Questions (Primary — address all six)

1. What international networks or frameworks can a Zone 5 rural community join or access today, without federal government involvement, and what does membership or participation actually provide?
2. How have communities in comparable democratic countries (Canada, Germany, Netherlands, Australia) accessed international resources and frameworks during domestic governance failure or disruption?
3. What legal constraints on community-level international coordination exist under current US law, and how have communities navigated them?
4. What international seed sovereignty, food sovereignty, or knowledge commons networks have active US affiliates that Zone 5 communities can connect with, and what is the practical onboarding process?
5. What diaspora mutual aid networks in Zone 5 states have demonstrated international resource flows at community scale, and what governance structures make them functional?
6. What international cooperative federation models are applicable at the scale of a Zone 5 community (20–200 people) — specifically, what does a Zone 5 agricultural cooperative gain from international federation membership?

- Crisis-context education: what communities have done when formal schools were unavailable (Cuba post-Soviet, Puerto Rico post-Maria, Midwest cooperative homeschool networks)

#### Scope Definition

**In scope:**
- How energy, water, food, communications, and transportation systems interact at community scale (20–200 people) and how failures cascade across these interdependencies
- Community-scale redundancy design: what backup capacity, what alternatives, and what decision protocols prevent cascade failure from propagating across all systems
- Zone 5-specific failure cascades: agricultural equipment failure during planting window, grid failure in January, internet disruption during harvest coordination
- Partial failure scenarios: reduced grid, intermittent internet, limited medical supply — the scenarios communities are most likely to face
- Resilience audit methodology: how a community assesses its own interdependency vulnerabilities

**Out of scope:**
- Municipal or national infrastructure policy
- Engineering design at industrial or national grid scale
- Financial analysis of infrastructure investment (except community-scale cost comparisons)

**Zone 5 anchor**: The specific interdependency profile of northern Illinois, Iowa, and southern Wisconsin agricultural communities — where grid failure cascades to water pump failure cascades to livestock loss; where equipment failure during planting cascades to food supply reduction; where internet failure cascades to coordination failure.

#### Scope Definition

**In scope:**
- Soil biology restoration for Zone 5 Mollisol soils degraded by industrial agriculture: organic matter rebuilding, mycorrhizal network restoration, microbial community recovery
- Water system restoration at household and community scale: keyline design, swales, constructed wetlands, pond design for drought resilience
- Native habitat restoration: tallgrass prairie reestablishment, pollinator corridor design, food forest integration with native species
- Climate-adapted seed sourcing: Zone 5 to Zone 6a climate shift and its implications for perennial and annual plant selection
- Integration of restoration interventions with food production systems

**Out of scope:**
- Landscape-scale conservation biology at watershed or regional scale (except as the context within which community actions take place)
- Wildlife management or hunting and fishing regulations
- Commercial agricultural production (agribusiness scale)
- Ornamental horticulture without food or ecological function

### Institutional Learning and Governance Scaling

**Author**: [AUTHOR_NAME]
**Tier Assignment**: [TIER — A / B / C]
**Domain**: 65 — Institutional Learning and Governance Scaling
**Brief Version**: 1.0 (pre-author)
**Send Date**: [SEND_DATE — target June 18]

---

#### Scope Definition

**In scope:**
- Governance structures that scale from 20–50 people to 150–500 people without collapse; what structural interventions are required at each scale threshold
- Governance failure modes: founder concentration, legitimacy erosion, emergency power creep, resource control capture — and the structural designs that prevent each
- Federation models for inter-community coordination: how multiple communities coordinate without one dominating the others
