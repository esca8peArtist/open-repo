# Phase 6 Execution Readiness Checklist — Domains B-F

**What this document answers**: Is Phase 6 Domains B-F research executable, and if so, in what order, with what resources, and with what realistic confidence?

**Short answer**: Yes, domains B-F are executable. Recommended sequence is C-alt → B → D-alt → E → F, with B and D-alt running simultaneously where researcher capacity allows. Total estimated hours across all five domains: 77–105 hours. Confidence that all five domains can be completed to Phase 5 quality standards by December 2026: 82%.

### Domain C-alt: Political Governance

**Source readiness**: 65–70% — 34 sources identified; polycentric governance literature is the strongest cluster. Key gaps: constitutional design evidence for non-indigenous intentional communities (the ecovillage literature covers this but is younger and less rigorous than the indigenous constitutional literature), and leadership legitimacy collapse case studies with quantitative evidence.

**Research complexity**: High. Political governance is among the most expansive bodies of academic literature. Scope discipline is the primary production risk — the domain can expand into comparative politics, constitutional law, democratic theory, or political philosophy, none of which serve the core practitioner question. Active scope enforcement required throughout production.

**Production estimate**:

| Phase | Activity | Hours |
|-------|----------|-------|
| Source library audit + scope enforcement session | Verify sources; write explicit out-of-scope list; set ceiling on political theory citations | 6–8 |
| Research writing — Zones C1+C2 (legitimacy foundations + constitutional design) | 14,000–18,000 words | 12–14 |
| Research writing — Zones C3+C4 (electoral mechanisms + rights protection) | 10,000–14,000 words | 9–11 |
| Research writing — Zone C5 (external political relations) | 8,000–10,000 words | 7–8 |
| Citation integration, Zone 5 subsections, implementation templates | Final pass | 4–6 |
| **Total** | | **38–47 hours** |

**Word count target**: 45,000–55,000 words
**Citation target**: 120–150
**Confidence (on-time, on-quality)**: 72% — scope creep is the highest individual-domain risk in the entire Phase 6 B-F set. A researcher with political science background will need explicit weekly scope audits. An economist or community development researcher may actually be better suited here precisely because they are less likely to chase political theory tangents.

**Recommended scope ceiling rules for Domain C-alt**:
- No more than 6,000 words total on political theory background (Locke, Rousseau, Rawls, etc.) — cite the concepts but do not explain them in depth
- No more than 4 international governance case studies (Swiss cantons, Iroquois, one ecovillage network, one Zone 5 cooperative federation)
- Every section must be answerable with "what does this mean for a community of 150–500 people in Zone 5?" — if not, the section is out of scope

### Domain D-alt: Dispute Resolution and Community Justice

**Source readiness**: 72–78% — 30 sources identified; restorative justice and indigenous justice literature is strong and well-organized. Key gaps: enforcement mechanisms without coercive state power (thin academic literature; Braithwaite's reintegrative shaming is the strongest framework but was published in 1989 and 2002), and Zone 5 community-specific dispute resolution case studies (likely requires practitioner interviews with rural mediation centers).

**Research complexity**: Medium. The domain is more bounded than C-alt because dispute resolution has clearer success metrics (did the dispute resolve without escalation to external authority?) and a more established practitioner literature. The main complexity is translating professional mediator frameworks into community self-implementation guides.

### Domain E: Multilateral Coordination

**Source readiness**: 68–73% — 32 sources identified; UNDRR/Sendai/MCR2030 documents are strong foundational anchors. Key gaps: empirical evidence on small-community inter-community coordination (most literature is on city-level or government-level coordination); historical case studies (Iroquois, Hanseatic League) require additional source discovery.

**Research complexity**: Medium-high. The domain requires synthesis across disaster risk management, international coordination theory, and historical precedent — three fields that use different methodologies and terminologies. The practitioner audience is unusual for this topic: most inter-community coordination literature is written for government officials, not community organizers. Practitioner translation is a significant authoring challenge.

## Section 3: Resource Requirements

### Researcher Profile Requirements

**For Domain B (Economic Foundations)**: Researcher with background in public finance, community economics, or cooperative development. Should be comfortable with quantitative fiscal data and cooperative incorporation law. Zone 5 familiarity is an asset (agricultural cooperative tradition is a primary source base). Research background in USDA programs is a plus.

**For Domain C-alt (Political Governance)**: Researcher with political science, legal, or community organizing background. Must have demonstrated ability to bound scope in political theory — this domain collapses under a researcher who follows theoretical threads. Practitioner orientation is more important than academic depth here. Community organizing or intentional community experience is an asset.

**For Domain D-alt (Dispute Resolution)**: Researcher with mediation, restorative justice, legal, or social work background. NACRJ membership or equivalent practitioner network access would accelerate Zone 5 practitioner interview sourcing. Academic-practitioner hybrids are ideal.

**For Domain E (Multilateral Coordination)**: Researcher with international relations, emergency management, or NGO coordination background. Comfort with translating governmental-scale coordination frameworks to community scale is the critical skill. No Zone 5 specificity required in background — Zone 5 applicability can be developed during production.

**For Domain F (Implementation Feasibility)**: Generalist researcher who has read all other Phase 6 domains. This is the integration domain — the researcher must be able to synthesize across B-E findings. A researcher who produced one of the B-E domains and is then assigned F is ideal.

### Tooling Requirements

All domains use the same tooling as Phase 5 and existing Phase 6 production:
- Markdown with YAML frontmatter (same format as all prior production)
- Citation format: numbered inline ([1], [2], [3]) with bibliography at end
- Word count verification: `wc -w` before delivery
- Source verification: URL access confirmed before citing
- Zone 5 subsection per major section (300–500 words minimum each)

No new tooling required. Phase 6 B-F uses identical standards to Phase 6 Domain A.

### Source Access Requirements

**Open access**: approximately 60% of identified sources are fully open access (NIST, FEMA, UNDRR, arxiv, PMC, ResearchGate, organization websites).

**Institutional library access required**: approximately 20% of sources (Tandfonline, Wiley, ScienceDirect, Oxford Academic, Cambridge). Most of these have abstract-level access sufficient for citation; full text access is needed for approximately 8–10 sources total across all five domains.

**Library acquisition required**: approximately 15% of sources are books (Ostrom *Governing the Commons*; Braithwaite *Crime, Shame and Reintegration* and *Restorative Justice and Responsive Regulation*; Tilly *Coercion, Capital, and European States*; Rogers *Diffusion of Innovations*). These are all widely held in US academic libraries; WorldCat access or Inter-Library Loan will suffice for most.

**Practitioner interviews** (recommended, not required): Zone D-alt benefit significantly from interviews with Zone 5 community mediation practitioners. Zone F benefits from interviews with Zone 5 transition community practitioners (Land Stewardship Project, Practical Farmers of Iowa). These are not required for production but increase Zone 5 specificity from generic to primary-source-grounded.

### Known Risks

**Risk 1: Scope creep in Domain C-alt (Political Governance) — HIGH severity, MEDIUM probability**

Political governance literature is among the most expansive in social science. A researcher following citation trails in constitutional design or democratic theory can spend 30+ hours on theoretical background that serves no Zone 5 practitioner. Mitigation: explicit scope ceiling rules documented in the research brief (above, Section 1); weekly scope audit required. If scope discipline fails in Week 3, production should pause for scope reset before continuing.

**Risk 2: Thin empirical evidence on community-scale inter-community coordination (Domain E) — MEDIUM severity, HIGH probability**

The academic literature on inter-community coordination is primarily at city-government scale or larger. Documented cases of voluntary coordination between small autonomous communities (50–500 people) with primary-source data on what worked are rare. The UNDRR/Sendai framework provides policy structure but not empirical community case studies. Mitigation: (a) explicit honest-uncertainty language in Zone E1 and E3 where evidence is thin; (b) use historical cases (Iroquois, Hanseatic) as the primary evidence base with appropriate temporal caveats; (c) practitioner interviews with Zone 5 mutual aid networks as supplementary source.

**Risk 3: Book access for enforcement/implementation theory (Domains D-alt, F) — LOW severity, MEDIUM probability**

Braithwaite, Tilly, and Rogers are all foundational sources that improve quality substantially but are not individually irreplaceable. If library access is unavailable, each has a partial substitute: Braithwaite → NACRJ practitioner frameworks; Tilly → Iroquois case study literature; Rogers → CFIR/RE-AIM implementation science papers (already identified in source index). Mitigation: identify library access path within first week of each domain's production; if unavailable, document the gap in the source log and use identified substitutes.

**Risk 4: Zone 5 primary source gaps in fiscal and political governance (Domains B, C-alt) — MEDIUM severity, MEDIUM probability**

Zone 5-specific fiscal architecture data (how cooperative revenue systems actually perform in Iowa, Minnesota, Wisconsin) and Zone 5 political governance cases are sparsely documented in peer-reviewed literature. USDA Extension publications and cooperative annual reports are the primary source bases, but these are gray literature. Mitigation: document all gray literature sources with access dates and explicit gray-literature labeling; where peer-reviewed sources are unavailable for Zone 5-specific claims, use peer-reviewed national evidence and explicitly translate to Zone 5 context with labeling.

**Risk 5: Parallelism coordination overhead (Option 2 only) — MEDIUM severity, LOW probability**

Running B + D-alt simultaneously adds integration overhead. If the same researcher produces both domains (unlikely given scope sizes), the overlap risk is minimal. If two researchers are involved, the week-13 integration sync is critical — without it, Zone B3 (participatory fiscal governance) and Zone D3 (restorative justice design) can drift into each other's scope. Mitigation: use integration sync agenda from Phase 6 Domain A template; 30-minute sync with documented scope boundary confirmation.

## Section 6: Sequencing Decision Guide

**If you can start one domain now**: Start Domain C-alt (Political Governance). It is the conceptual foundation for all other domains, has the highest scope risk (which means deferral increases the risk that it is never executed well), and has no production dependencies on other Phase 6 B-F domains.

**If you can start two domains simultaneously**: Add Domain D-alt (Dispute Resolution) alongside C-alt. D-alt has the highest production confidence (84%), lowest scope risk, and is independent of C-alt's political governance framing — community justice is self-standing enough to be researched in parallel.

**If you need to defer to Phase 6b**: The domain with the lowest deferral cost is Domain E (Multilateral Coordination). Inter-community coordination is important but Phase 6 Domain D (Governance Scaling, already in production) covers the coordination-between-communities question at a high level. Domain E deepens that coverage but is not the bottleneck for Phase 6 utility. Domain F (Implementation Feasibility) has the second-lowest deferral cost if and only if the other domains are not yet complete — F's value is maximized as a synthesis after B-E, so deferring F until B-E are done is actually the correct sequencing rather than a compromise.

**If Phase 6 must publish as a single integrated volume**: Target completion of all five B-F domains before October 1, 2026 for a November 2026 integration and December 2026 publication. This requires 20+ hours/week production capacity starting immediately (June 2026). At 20 hours/week, parallel B+D-alt production, all five domains complete in approximately 22 weeks, landing in mid-November 2026. Tight but feasible.

**If Phase 6 can publish as a series**: Recommended: C-alt + D-alt in Phase 6a (September-October 2026, two months), then B + E in Phase 6b (October-December 2026), then F as the capstone in Phase 6c (January 2027, 4–5 weeks). This approach produces three publication events and maintains quality at each release without rushing.
