# Phase 6 Research Zones Mapping — Domains B-F

**What this document is**: A pre-production research zone map for five Phase 6 domain candidates. Each domain has 4-5 research zones with 3-4 specific research questions per zone. This document enables source identification and author briefing without waiting for full production to begin.

**Relationship to existing Phase 6 work**: Domains A (Community Economic Resilience), C (Skills Development), and D (Governance Scaling) have active production roadmaps. This document maps five additional domains — labeled B, C-alt/E, D-alt/F for sequencing flexibility — that extend Phase 6's scope into fiscal architecture, formal governance structure, dispute resolution, inter-community coordination, and transition feasibility.

**Zone structure follows Phase 3 candidate model**: each zone is bounded enough for a single researcher to work through in 3-5 days; cross-zone integration is explicit rather than assumed.

---

## Domain B: Economic Foundations — Fiscal Architecture and Community Finance

**One-sentence scope**: How do communities establish, manage, and sustain the fiscal infrastructure — revenue systems, capital formation, asset protection, and inter-community financial flows — that makes all other resilience work financially viable?

**Relationship to existing Phase 6 domains**: Domain A (Community Economic Resilience) covers exchange mechanisms (local currencies, cooperatives, mutual credit). Domain B goes deeper on the structural fiscal layer: how communities generate revenue, protect collective assets, allocate capital across long-term investment horizons, and build financial relationships with neighboring communities. Domain A is the economic operating system; Domain B is the financial architecture that funds and protects it.

**Word count target**: 42,000–50,000 words
**Citation target**: 110–140
**Production window estimate**: 16–20 hours of focused research-writing

---

### Zone B-1: Revenue Architecture Without Central Government

**Zone definition**: How communities generate revenue from their own resources when federal, state, and county transfers are unavailable, unreliable, or extractive.

**Research questions**:

1. What revenue mechanisms have small communities (50–2,000 people) successfully implemented without relying on transfer payments? Which mechanisms generate stable recurring revenue versus one-time capital? What is the minimum viable revenue base for a community of 150 households to fund shared infrastructure maintenance?

2. How do property-based revenue systems function in communities with mixed tenure (owned, rented, CLT, cooperative)? What design choices determine whether property-based revenue is stable, equitable, and collectible without enforcement mechanisms that require state backing?

3. What is the historical and contemporary evidence on local income-based revenue (labor tithes, production levies, service fees) as alternatives to property taxation? Which have succeeded and which have collapsed, and what determined the difference?

4. How have communities navigated the transition from federal-transfer-dependent budgets to self-generated revenue — specifically the 2-5 year gap when transfers decline before local revenue systems are fully operational?

**Key sources to verify**: Lincoln Institute of Land Policy (property tax autonomy research); ITEP local income tax analysis (https://itep.org/the-mostly-untapped-power-of-local-income-taxes/); ScienceDirect fiscal autonomy empirical test (2024); International Center for Public Policy Working Paper 24-10 (December 2024, GSU).

**Zone B-1 scope boundary**: Out of scope — national tax policy, federal revenue sharing design, corporate taxation. In scope — what individual communities can implement without legislative change at the state or federal level.

---

### Zone B-2: Capital Formation and Asset Protection

**Zone definition**: How communities build, protect, and transmit collective capital assets across generations without access to conventional banking or investment markets.

**Research questions**:

1. What institutional structures (community land trusts, limited-equity cooperatives, commons trusts, cooperative corporations) most effectively protect collective assets from dispossession, foreclosure, or individual extraction? What are the formation costs, legal requirements, and maintenance overhead for each in Zone 5 states?

2. How do communities finance large capital investments (water infrastructure, energy systems, shared equipment) without access to municipal bond markets or commercial credit? What is the evidence on revolving loan funds, community development financial institutions (CDFIs), and internal capital pools as alternatives?

3. What mechanisms protect accumulated community wealth during political transitions, bankruptcy proceedings, or hostile takeovers by external actors? Which asset structures have survived state receivership, hostile municipal annexation, or hostile corporate acquisition?

4. How does intergenerational asset transmission work in non-market community settings — specifically, how do communities ensure that assets built by one generation remain available to the next without inheritance-law complications?

**Key sources to verify**: Grounded Solutions Network CLT database (https://groundedsolutions.org/); Shelterforce 2024 CLT + limited-equity cooperative analysis; Brookings CLT structural applications analysis; Tandfonline 2024 CLT unit count study; community-wealth.org Ehlenz report on CLT-cooperative combinations; New Economy Project NYC commercial CLT acquisition (June 2026).

**Zone B-2 scope boundary**: Out of scope — individual household wealth-building, stock market investment. In scope — collective and institutional capital structures available to communities of 50–2,000 people.

---

### Zone B-3: Participatory Fiscal Governance

**Zone definition**: How communities make collective decisions about resource allocation, budget priorities, and spending tradeoffs without delegating those decisions entirely to elected representatives.

**Research questions**:

1. What does the evidence show about participatory budgeting's effectiveness at the sub-municipal scale (neighborhood and village levels)? What participation rates, equity outcomes, and satisfaction metrics distinguish successful from failed implementations?

2. How do communities design budget deliberation processes that remain functional when 30–50% of members lack formal financial literacy? What pedagogical and process design choices enable genuine participation from economically marginalized community members?

3. What mechanisms prevent budget capture by high-information minorities in participatory systems — specifically, how do communities prevent technically sophisticated members from systematically dominating resource allocation decisions?

4. How do communities integrate short-term crisis response spending with long-term investment priorities within a participatory budgeting framework? What tension-management mechanisms have worked in documented cases?

**Key sources to verify**: People Powered 2025 participatory budgeting research compilation (https://www.peoplepowered.org/news-content/the-latest-research-on-the-impacts-of-participatory-budgeting-2025); Tandfonline 2024 PB + local development study; National Civic League PB + citizens' assemblies integration; Cambridge Handbook of Community Empowerment (PB chapter); Democracy Collaborative participatory budgeting resources.

**Zone B-3 scope boundary**: Out of scope — municipal-scale PB (NYC, Porto Alegre city-level). In scope — village, neighborhood, and small-community scale, typically 50–1,000 participants.

---

### Zone B-4: Inter-Community Financial Flows

**Zone definition**: How communities build financial relationships with each other — lending, pooling risk, coordinating investment, and managing disputes over shared financial assets.

**Research questions**:

1. What governance structures have successfully managed shared financial pools across multiple autonomous communities? What makes inter-community lending relationships stable versus prone to default and conflict?

2. How do communities establish and enforce financial agreements without access to courts capable of enforcing contracts — specifically, what non-court mechanisms (reputation, social collateral, inter-community sanctions) have served contract-enforcement functions?

3. What is the evidence on regional mutual insurance pools as alternatives to commercial insurance? Under what conditions have community-managed risk-sharing arrangements proven actuarially sound, and where have they failed?

4. How do communities handle asymmetric financial relationships — specifically when one community is consistently wealthier, better-capitalized, or more financially sophisticated than its regional partners?

**Key sources to verify**: NIST Community Resilience Economic Decision Guide (https://nvlpubs.nist.gov/nistpubs/specialpublications/nist.sp.1197.pdf); NAsCo intergovernmental roles in disaster resilience; UNDRR 2025 Global Status of National DRR Strategies; Schumacher Center for New Economics polycentricity and commons.

---

### Zone B-5: Fiscal Resilience Under Shock

**Zone definition**: How community fiscal systems behave during and after acute shocks — what collapses, what survives, and what design choices predict fiscal survival.

**Research questions**:

1. What is the empirical record of community fiscal systems during extended supply-chain disruptions, currency crises, or hyperinflation? Which community fiscal architectures proved most shock-resistant and why?

2. How do communities rebuild fiscal capacity after a shock that destroys the tax base — for example, a major natural disaster that eliminates most taxable property or a population exodus that reduces the revenue-generating workforce by 40–60%?

3. What fiscal reserves (liquidity buffers, stored commodities, credit lines) are necessary and sufficient to maintain community operations during a 6-month shock? What is the evidence on reserve adequacy requirements from post-disaster reconstruction literature?

4. How do communities triage essential versus deferrable fiscal commitments during a fiscal crisis — specifically, what decision protocols determine which services get cut first and which are protected regardless of fiscal pressure?

**Key sources to verify**: Candid community foundations resilience blog; FEMA Building Resilient Infrastructure and Communities grant framework; Resilient Virginia Six Foundations; arxiv community resilience quantitative modeling (2202.09567); Tandfonline place-based funding lessons from England's levelling up policy (2025).

## Domain C-alt / Domain E: Political Governance — Formal Structure and Legitimate Authority

**Note on labeling**: This domain covers formal political governance structure for resilient communities. It is distinct from Phase 6 Domain D (Governance Scaling + Institutional Adaptation), which focuses on operational multi-community coordination. This domain asks the prior question: what legitimate political authority structure does a resilient community adopt, and how does it maintain legitimacy over time?

**One-sentence scope**: How do communities establish, formalize, and maintain legitimate political authority structures that remain functional across leadership transitions, internal conflict, and external pressure?

**Word count target**: 45,000–55,000 words
**Citation target**: 120–150
**Production window estimate**: 18–22 hours

---

### Zone C1: Foundations of Legitimate Authority at Community Scale

**Zone definition**: The theoretical and empirical basis for political authority in small communities — what makes governance legitimate, how legitimacy is established, and how it is lost.

**Research questions**:

1. What sources of political legitimacy operate in small (50–500 person) communities that function independently of state-backed law? How do traditional authority, procedural legitimacy, charismatic leadership, and performative legitimacy interact, and which is most durable under stress?

2. How does political legitimacy differ in communities formed deliberately (intentional communities, resilience networks, cooperative settlements) versus communities formed organically (existing rural towns, urban neighborhoods)? What design choices affect whether deliberately-formed communities can generate genuine legitimacy rather than procedural compliance?

3. What triggers legitimacy collapse in small autonomous communities — what behavioral signals indicate a community is approaching a governance crisis before formal breakdown occurs?

4. How do communities re-establish legitimacy after a governance failure — what reconciliation, restructuring, or reform processes have successfully restored functional authority without external enforcement?

**Key sources to verify**: Polycentric governance academic literature — Policy Studies Journal 2024 Baldwin empirical research piece; Oxford Academic polycentric governing chapter; MIT Press Global Environmental Politics polycentric climate governance 2024; PA TIMES Online polycentric governance public administration analysis.

---

### Zone C2: Constitutional Design for Small Communities

**Zone definition**: How communities design, adopt, and amend foundational governance documents that define the scope of collective authority and individual rights.

**Research questions**:

1. What constitutional design choices most strongly predict governance durability in small communities? Specifically: written versus unwritten constitutions; amendment procedures (supermajority versus consensus); enumerated powers versus general authority grants; individual rights provisions versus collective governance principles?

2. How have deliberately designed community constitutions (intentional communities, indigenous nation constitutions, cooperative governance charters) performed against their stated governance goals over 5-, 10-, and 20-year timescales? What design elements appear in surviving documents but not in failed ones?

3. What constitutional provisions most effectively protect minority community members — economically marginalized, culturally distinct, or recently arrived — from majoritarian exclusion in small communities where formal legal recourse is unavailable?

4. How do communities adapt constitutional frameworks when conditions change substantially — population doubling, resource scarcity, external threat, or internal ideological shift? What amendment and adaptation mechanisms have maintained constitutional continuity while allowing necessary change?

**Key sources to verify**: K'ómoks First Nation constitution (2025 adoption); Haida Nation Recognition Act (2024 Royal Assent); IDEA International on indigenous self-determination (https://www.idea.int/news/self-determination-indigenous-peoples); Springer Nature chapter on indigenous representation in constitutional conventions; Cultural Survival 2024-2025 indigenous wins documentation.

---

### Zone C3: Electoral and Representative Mechanisms

**Zone definition**: How communities choose their leaders, allocate representation, and manage transitions of authority without access to state-backed electoral infrastructure.

**Research questions**:

1. What voting and selection mechanisms have small communities successfully used to choose leaders while maintaining broad participation and perceived fairness? How do sortition (lottery selection), ranked-choice voting, consensus selection, and rotating leadership compare on durability, equity, and administrative complexity?

2. How do communities design representative structures that avoid founder-dominance — the documented tendency for charismatic founders to entrench control over ostensibly democratic community institutions?

3. What recall and removal mechanisms are effective for community-scale governance, where formal impeachment processes backed by state courts are unavailable? What non-court removal mechanisms have successfully removed leaders without destroying community cohesion?

4. How do communities maintain effective governance during leadership transitions — specifically the 2-4 week gap between when a leader's legitimacy collapses and when a successor is established?

**Key sources to verify**: arxiv patterns in founder-leadership to community governance transitions (2509.16295); Ostrom Workshop Indiana University polycentric governance key examples; Post-growth meets polycentric governance ScienceDirect (2025); Baldwin 2024 polycentric governance empirical research (Policy Studies Journal).

---

### Zone C4: Rights and Freedoms Without State Enforcement

**Zone definition**: How communities protect individual rights and freedoms when state-backed enforcement is unavailable or hostile.

**Research questions**:

1. What is the empirical record of rights protection in small autonomous communities? Where have rights protections proven robust and where have they collapsed into majoritarian abuse — what design choices differentiated the outcomes?

2. How do communities protect the rights of members who challenge community consensus or leadership? What mechanisms have successfully protected dissidents, critics, and reformers from social sanction or expulsion without equivalent mechanisms to formal legal protection?

3. What rights are most frequently violated in small autonomous community governance — and what structural features of small-community governance make these violations predictable rather than exceptional?

4. How do communities establish rights frameworks for non-members who interact with the community — traders, visitors, refugees, or workers from outside?

**Key sources to verify**: Transition town ecovillage governance literature (MDPI Lands 2025 Austrian case study); Transition Town Wikipedia (992 global groups as of May 2024); ScienceDirect C40/doughnut cities/Transition Towns urban degrowth analysis.

---

### Zone C5: External Political Relations and Sovereignty

**Zone definition**: How communities manage political relationships with state and federal authorities, neighboring jurisdictions, and external actors who do not recognize community authority.

**Research questions**:

1. What is the documented range of state and federal government responses to autonomous or semi-autonomous small communities — from tolerance to active suppression? What factors appear to predict which response a community will encounter?

2. How do communities negotiate recognition and accommodation from state authorities without surrendering the autonomy that makes their governance model viable?

3. What strategies have successfully protected community political autonomy during hostile state or federal administrations — legal incorporation structures, geographic remoteness, political alliance-building, international attention?

4. How do communities maintain internal political cohesion when under sustained external political pressure — specifically, how do they prevent external hostility from triggering internal factional conflict?

**Key sources to verify**: UNDRR Making Cities Resilient 2030 (1,800+ local governments participating); Sendai Framework Priority 2 (governance); preventionweb.net Sendai implementation documentation; FEMA intergovernmental disaster resilience frameworks.

## Domain D-alt / Domain F: Judicial Independence — Dispute Resolution and Community Justice

**Note on labeling**: This domain covers internal community dispute resolution and justice functions — distinct from the resistance-research project's Judicial Independence domain (which covers federal court reform). In the systems-resilience context, "judicial independence" means: how communities create legitimate, impartial dispute resolution without access to state courts.

**One-sentence scope**: How do communities establish, sustain, and protect internal dispute resolution mechanisms that are perceived as legitimate, impartial, and effective across the range of disputes that arise in small autonomous communities?

**Word count target**: 38,000–45,000 words
**Citation target**: 100–130
**Production window estimate**: 14–18 hours

---

### Zone D1: Dispute Typology and Frequency in Small Communities

**Zone definition**: What disputes actually arise in small autonomous communities, how frequently, and what makes them resolvable or intractable.

**Research questions**:

1. What is the empirical distribution of dispute types in small autonomous communities — resource disputes, relational conflicts, governance disagreements, boundary conflicts, and rights violations? What proportion require formal resolution versus informal mediation?

2. What dispute characteristics predict escalation from interpersonal conflict to community-level crisis? What early intervention mechanisms have successfully prevented escalation?

3. How does dispute frequency change as communities grow from 50 to 500 members — are some dispute types scale-dependent, and how should dispute resolution infrastructure anticipate growth?

4. What disputes are categorically unresolvable through internal community mechanisms, requiring either external referral or community separation — and what are the triggers for each?

**Key sources to verify**: Researchgate Indonesia local wisdom ADR (2025); disputes centre indigenous conflict resolution; WJARR 2024 restorative justice bridging gap study; AALS reflections on ADR and restorative justice; Preprints.org 2025 restorative justice indigenous courts.

---

### Zone D2: Traditional and Indigenous Dispute Resolution Models

**Zone definition**: The documented evidence on non-state dispute resolution systems — indigenous courts, village justice, elder mediation, and traditional arbitration — as models for contemporary community implementation.

**Research questions**:

1. What structural features distinguish dispute resolution systems that have maintained legitimacy across generations from those that collapsed or were captured by power? What makes indigenous mediation systems durable where comparable formal systems have failed?

2. Which indigenous and traditional dispute resolution mechanisms are most directly adaptable to non-indigenous contemporary communities — and what design principles translate across cultural context versus which are culturally specific and non-transferable?

3. How do traditional dispute resolution systems handle cases where the disputing parties have significantly unequal power — and what mechanisms prevent stronger parties from capturing the process?

4. What is the evidence on Gacaca (Rwanda), Sulha (Middle East), Ho'oponopono (Hawaii), Panchayat (South Asia), and Mato Oput (Uganda) as case studies in community justice at scale?

**Key sources to verify**: Tandfonline restorative justice + indigenous justice system (2012, foundational); IJRPR comparative analysis indigenous and community justice (Vol 6 Issue 10); ResearchGate restorative justice indigenous justice system PDF; UAB Institute for Human Rights indigenous justice history blog (2023).

---

### Zone D3: Restorative Justice Design and Implementation

**Zone definition**: How communities design and operate restorative justice systems — focusing on repair rather than punishment — adapted from documented models.

**Research questions**:

1. What restorative justice design elements are most critical for small-community legitimacy — specifically, what process features must be present for participants to accept outcomes they personally dislike?

2. How do communities handle serious harms (violence, sexual assault, theft at significant scale) within restorative frameworks when the affected parties want punitive responses? What documented cases show successful navigation of this tension?

3. What restorative justice process designs have proven effective in contexts where practitioners have no formal training — specifically, can structured facilitation guides enable lay mediators to run legitimate processes without professional credentials?

4. How do restorative processes handle repeat offenders and community members who participate in mediation processes without genuine accountability — what escalation pathways exist beyond restorative frameworks?

**Key sources to verify**: WJARR 2024 bridging offenders and victims study; AALS reflections on ADR and restorative justice (US and international); Preprints.org 2025 restorative justice indigenous courts Canada.

---

### Zone D4: Arbiter Selection and Institutional Impartiality

**Zone definition**: How communities select, train, and hold accountable dispute resolution practitioners — and how they protect the institutional independence necessary for perceived impartiality.

**Research questions**:

1. What selection mechanisms most reliably identify community members suited to dispute resolution roles — what competencies predict effectiveness, and are they trainable or selection-dependent?

2. How do communities protect arbiter impartiality in small communities where all members know each other personally? What structural features — disclosure, recusal, panel selection — reduce bias without creating a system too cumbersome for small-community administration?

3. What accountability mechanisms exist for arbiters who perform badly — who rules on arbiters, and how do communities remove ineffective dispute resolution practitioners without destroying confidence in the institution?

4. How do communities handle cases where a dispute involves the arbiters themselves, or where arbiter social relationships make true impartiality structurally impossible?

**Key sources to verify**: Arkansas AALS ADR reflections on comparative systems; Ostrom's eight design principles as applied to governance institutions (adaptable to dispute resolution); polycentric governance framework literature.

---

### Zone D5: Enforcement Without Coercive State Power

**Zone definition**: How communities enforce dispute resolution outcomes when they lack coercive enforcement capacity (police, courts, incarceration).

**Research questions**:

1. What non-coercive enforcement mechanisms — social sanction, reputational consequences, graduated exclusion, community service requirements — have proven effective in small community settings? What is the enforcement rate and compliance rate for each?

2. How do communities handle members who refuse to comply with dispute resolution outcomes? What graduated response sequences have maintained community cohesion while addressing non-compliance?

3. What is the evidence on voluntary compliance rates with restorative justice outcomes in documented case studies — and what factors (process legitimacy, relationship quality, community embeddedness) predict voluntary compliance?

4. When does community justice require external enforcement referral — specifically, what offenses or behaviors exceed community enforcement capacity and require engagement with state systems even when those systems are hostile?

**Key sources to verify**: PMC 2025 inter-organizational partnership in disasters framework; NAsCo intergovernmental disaster roles; ResearchGate Indonesia ADR local wisdom study.

## Domain E: Multilateral Coordination — Inter-Community Alliance and Shared Governance

**One-sentence scope**: How clusters of autonomous communities build, sustain, and govern shared infrastructure, mutual aid agreements, and coordinated response frameworks that require genuine multi-community commitment without a superior authority capable of enforcing compliance.

**Relationship to Phase 6 Domain D (Governance Scaling)**: Domain D addresses how governance structures within a single community adapt as that community scales. Domain E addresses relationships between separate autonomous communities — specifically, how they coordinate without surrendering autonomy and without the external enforcement mechanisms that typically backstop inter-governmental agreements.

**Word count target**: 40,000–48,000 words
**Citation target**: 105–135
**Production window estimate**: 15–19 hours

---

### Zone E1: Coordination Architecture Between Autonomous Communities

**Zone definition**: The structural design of multi-community coordination arrangements — treaty frameworks, alliance structures, and shared governance bodies.

**Research questions**:

1. What formal and informal coordination structures have proven most durable between small autonomous communities? What distinguishes treaty-based coordination from looser alliance structures — and what conditions favor each?

2. How do communities design coordination agreements that survive leadership transitions in member communities — specifically, how do they ensure that agreements bind communities rather than just the individuals who negotiated them?

3. What scope limitations in coordination agreements have proven critical — which decisions should be reserved for individual communities, and which can be delegated to multi-community bodies without triggering defection?

4. What is the evidence from the Iroquois Confederacy, Swiss cantonal system, Hanseatic League, and other documented multi-community coordination frameworks on what design choices predicted longevity?

**Key sources to verify**: PMC developing conceptual framework inter-organizational partnership disasters (2025); NAsCo intergovernmental roles disaster resilience; UNDRR 2025 Global Status report; Philippines ERIC interoperability model (ReliefWeb).

---

### Zone E2: Shared Infrastructure Governance

**Zone definition**: How communities co-own, co-manage, and co-finance shared physical infrastructure — water systems, roads, communication networks, energy grids — that crosses community boundaries.

**Research questions**:

1. What governance structures have successfully managed shared physical infrastructure across multiple autonomous communities? What cost-allocation formulas, maintenance-responsibility frameworks, and upgrade-decision mechanisms have proven stable?

2. How do communities manage disagreements about shared infrastructure priorities — specifically, when one community wants to invest in upgrades the others cannot afford, or when maintenance responsibilities are disputed?

3. What is the evidence on inter-municipal cooperation and shared service agreements as models for community-level infrastructure coordination? What makes shared service agreements stable versus prone to defection?

4. How do communities plan for infrastructure failure affecting multiple communities simultaneously — what multi-community response coordination mechanisms have worked under real stress conditions?

**Key sources to verify**: FEMA Building Resilient Infrastructure and Communities; NIST Community Resilience Economic Decision Guide; Resilient Virginia Six Foundations framework; arxiv community resilience critical structures (2202.09567).

---

### Zone E3: Mutual Aid Pact Design and Stress-Testing

**Zone definition**: How communities design mutual aid agreements that actually deliver assistance during crises rather than collapsing precisely when needed.

**Research questions**:

1. What design features distinguish mutual aid agreements that delivered promised support during crises from those that collapsed under crisis pressure? What evidence exists on mutual aid pact failure modes — and are failures predictable from pact design alone?

2. How do communities calibrate mutual aid commitments — what is the maximum fraction of a community's own capacity it can commit to external aid without endangering its own resilience?

3. What triggers and response protocols make mutual aid pacts actionable under time pressure — specifically, how do communities make rapid aid-deployment decisions when normal deliberative processes are too slow?

4. How have communities managed situations where mutual aid partners could not deliver promised support — what contingency frameworks have prevented single-partner dependency from becoming a catastrophic failure point?

**Key sources to verify**: PMC multisectoral partnerships Houston underserved communities (2025); PMC inter-organizational partnership disasters conceptual framework (2025); PMC NGO coordination natural hazards systematic review; UNDRR Making Cities Resilient 2030 initiative.

---

### Zone E4: Conflict and Defection Between Allied Communities

**Zone definition**: How multi-community alliances manage conflict between member communities, handle defection from shared commitments, and repair relationships after breach.

**Research questions**:

1. What is the documented evidence on defection rates from inter-community mutual aid and coordination agreements — and what triggers defection most reliably? Is defection primarily driven by resource scarcity, leadership change, internal political pressure, or external incentive?

2. What graduated-response frameworks have successfully addressed partial defection without triggering full alliance breakdown? At what point does breach of commitment become unrecoverable?

3. How do alliances manage disputes between member communities — specifically, when two alliance members have a bilateral conflict, how does the wider alliance adjudicate without taking sides in ways that destroy broader cohesion?

4. What is the evidence on re-integration of defecting communities — under what conditions can communities that withdrew from alliances or breached commitments be successfully re-integrated?

**Key sources to verify**: Polycentric governance failure modes literature; Ostrom design principles applied to inter-community coordination; Baldwin 2024 Policy Studies Journal on polycentric governance outcomes.

---

### Zone E5: Multilateral Coordination with External Institutions

**Zone definition**: How community alliances manage relationships with government agencies, NGOs, international organizations, and commercial entities — extracting support without surrendering governance autonomy.

**Research questions**:

1. What strategies have community alliances used to engage effectively with disaster-response agencies (FEMA, state emergency management) while maintaining governance autonomy — specifically avoiding the dependency traps that have historically made communities vulnerable to post-crisis consolidation?

2. How do community alliances interact with international frameworks (Sendai, MCR2030, UNDRR) to access resources and recognition without being coopted into frameworks that do not serve their actual needs?

3. What coordination structures between NGOs and community alliances have proven most effective — and what NGO behaviors most reliably destroy community autonomy in the name of support?

4. How do community alliances maintain coordination effectiveness when one or more member communities are receiving significant external support (grants, technical assistance, emergency aid) that others are not?

**Key sources to verify**: UNDRR Sendai Framework implementation (https://www.undrr.org/implementing-sendai-framework/what-sendai-framework); ADB Disaster Risk Management Action Plan 2024-2030; UNDRR Pact for the Future disaster risk reduction; Second World Summit on Social Development 2025 Sendai acceleration analysis.

## Domain F: Implementation Feasibility — Transition Pathways and Execution Risk

**One-sentence scope**: How do communities actually transition from conventional dependency-based systems to resilient autonomous systems — what sequencing works, what assessment frameworks are useful, what implementation risks are highest, and how do transitions fail?

**Relationship to other Phase 6 domains**: All other Phase 6 domains document what resilient systems look like. Domain F documents how to get there — the transition pathway, sequencing logic, feasibility assessment tools, and failure mode analysis that communities need before beginning implementation.

**Word count target**: 35,000–42,000 words
**Citation target**: 90–115
**Production window estimate**: 12–16 hours

---

### Zone F1: Transition Pathway Typology

**Zone definition**: The documented range of transition approaches communities have used — rapid versus gradual, top-down versus bottom-up, planned versus emergent — and their comparative outcomes.

**Research questions**:

1. What is the empirical record of planned versus emergent community resilience transitions? Do communities that systematically plan transitions achieve better outcomes than those that respond to crisis, or does planning overhead reduce adaptive capacity?

2. What transition phases appear consistently across successful community resilience implementations — and what phase-transition failures are most common and most consequential?

3. What is the optimal sequencing of resilience investments — which systems must be in place before others can function, and what are the dependency chains that determine build sequence?

4. How long do documented community resilience transitions actually take — from initial organizing to functional autonomy — and what factors most strongly predict faster versus slower transitions?

**Key sources to verify**: Transition Town Totnes case study (Plymouth thesis); Transition town Wikipedia movement data; ScienceDirect C40/degrowth/Transition Towns analysis; MDPI Lands Austrian ecovillage case study (2025); ResearchGate Totnes localisation and resilience.

---

### Zone F2: Pre-Implementation Assessment and Readiness

**Zone definition**: How communities assess their own readiness to begin resilience transitions — what they need to know about themselves before committing to implementation.

**Research questions**:

1. What pre-implementation assessment frameworks most accurately predict community implementation success? Which factors — social capital, existing organizational capacity, resource base, leadership quality — are most predictive?

2. What is the minimum viable organizational capacity for beginning a resilience transition — and how do communities lacking that capacity build it before the transition begins?

3. How do communities assess their specific vulnerability profile — which failure modes are most likely given their geography, economy, social structure, and external relationships — and how does this assessment shape implementation sequencing?

4. What readiness assessment failures have led to premature launches that damaged communities' long-term resilience capacity — and what assessment elements would have predicted the failures?

**Key sources to verify**: NIST Review of Community Resilience Frameworks and Assessment Tools (annotated bibliography, https://www.nist.gov/publications/review-community-resilience-frameworks-and-assessment-tools-annotated-bibliography); PMC analysis inventory community resilience frameworks (2022); PMC community health system resilience scoping review 2025; Tandfonline strengthening community resilience through participation (2025).

---

### Zone F3: Pilot Design and Scale-Up Sequencing

**Zone definition**: How communities design and execute small-scale pilots before committing to full-scale resilience investments, and how successful pilots translate into scaled implementation.

**Research questions**:

1. What pilot design choices most reliably produce evidence that generalizes to full-scale implementation — and what pilot designs produce false positives that lead to failed scale-ups?

2. How large does a pilot need to be to generate statistically reliable implementation evidence — and what is the minimum viable pilot for each major resilience domain (energy, water, food, governance)?

3. What is the documented evidence on implementation velocity after successful pilots — how quickly do communities move from proven pilot to scaled system, and what determines the pace?

4. What pilot failure modes should communities anticipate — specifically, how do pilot successes that depend on exceptional participants, external resources, or unusual conditions mislead communities about full-scale implementation feasibility?

**Key sources to verify**: NIST PRIME CyberGIS platform resilience inference (arxiv 2404.09463); PMC 5-year CAPABLE implementation RE-AIM CFIR frameworks (2025); PMC community readiness assessment pilot implementation; Tandfonline place-based funding England levelling up lessons (2025).

---

### Zone F4: Failure Mode Analysis and Risk Management

**Zone definition**: How communities identify, prioritize, and mitigate the implementation risks most likely to derail resilience transitions before they produce durable outcomes.

**Research questions**:

1. What are the most common implementation failure modes in documented community resilience transitions — and at what phase of implementation do they typically occur?

2. How do communities distinguish between temporary setbacks (expected during all transitions) and structural failures (indicating the transition design is fundamentally flawed)? What decision criteria should trigger implementation review versus continuation through difficulty?

3. What resource requirements are most frequently underestimated in resilience transition planning — and how do communities calibrate resource estimates before committing to implementation?

4. What external shocks most commonly disrupt in-progress resilience transitions, and what transition designs are most robust to mid-transition disruption?

**Key sources to verify**: PRIME resilience inference measurement framework; PMC 2025 inter-organizational partnership disaster framework; UNDRR 2025 global status inadequate funding analysis; community health system scoping review (PMC 2025).

### Zone F5: Zone 5 Implementation Context

**Zone definition**: The specific implementation constraints, opportunities, and precedents relevant to Zone 5 (upper Midwest: Iowa, Minnesota, Wisconsin, Illinois, Missouri) community resilience transitions.

**Research questions**:

1. What Zone 5-specific factors — agricultural calendar, winter climate, rural density patterns, cooperative tradition, existing mutual aid networks — most significantly shape resilience implementation sequencing and feasibility?

2. What existing Zone 5 institutions (agricultural cooperatives, soil conservation districts, rural water authorities, county emergency management, land-grant extension systems) provide the most useful organizational foundations for resilience transitions?

3. What Zone 5 communities have attempted significant autonomy-building or resilience transitions in the last 20 years — what can be learned from their documented experience?

4. What are the state-level regulatory constraints in Iowa, Minnesota, Wisconsin, Illinois, and Missouri that most directly limit community resilience implementation — and what strategies have successfully navigated those constraints?

**Key sources to verify**: Iowa State University Extension cooperative economics reports; University of Wisconsin-Madison Extension community development resources; Purdue Extension (Indiana adjacent) resilience frameworks; USDA Rural Development Zone 5 state programs; Land O'Lakes and Organic Valley governance histories as cooperative models.
