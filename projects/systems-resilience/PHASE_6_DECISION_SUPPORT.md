### 1A. Community Economic Resilience

**The problem it solves**

Phase 5 documents how a community produces food, water, power, and healthcare. None of it addresses the layer above production: how a community coordinates who gets what, how surpluses are managed, how skilled labor (the medic, the mechanic, the teacher) is compensated when there is no cash, and what replaces market price signals. Without this economic operating layer, surpluses rot, critical labor stops being done, and communities fracture over perceived unfairness. This is the gap that has ended real historical communities after the acute crisis phase — not food shortages, but breakdown in exchange coordination.

**Research scope**

Three core questions: (1) What economic architectures sustain when the dollar fails — local currencies, mutual credit, barter protocols, gift economies, and their design constraints at 20-200 person scale? (2) How do communities allocate capital and coordinate production without market signals — Ostrom's commons governance design principles, cooperative enterprise models, land trusts, participatory budgeting, and surplus-management protocols? (3) What economic bridges prevent community isolation — inter-community trade federation models, comparative advantage without global supply chains, and barter protocols for non-monetary exchange between communities?

**Expected word count**: 45,000-55,000 words across 10-12 sections, including Zone 5 implications and minimum two actionable templates (how to design a local currency; how to set up a commons management charter).

**Source library readiness**: 75-80%. All foundational sources confirmed: Elinor Ostrom's *Governing the Commons* (foundational; eight design principles directly applicable), David Graeber's *Debt: The First 5,000 Years* (anthropological framing), WIR mutual credit system documentation (70+ years operational in Switzerland; $3B+ annual transaction volume), Bristol Pounds impact evaluation, cooperative movement literature (ICA standards), UN Habitat local economic development reports, and Midwest agricultural cooperative data through the Cooperative Development Foundation. A 2025 peer-reviewed study in *Journal of Economic Behavior and Organization* provides new firm-level evidence on mutual credit system resilience. Gaps: timebanking quantitative effectiveness data is thin (qualitative case studies available); regional economic simulation models for 200-2,000 person communities are sparse.

**Estimated production hours**: 20 hours for source library finalization (June 1-7) plus approximately 200 hours of production (10-11 weeks at 20-22 hours/week). Fastest ramp of the three candidates due to source readiness. First-draft outline deliverable by June 15.

**Cross-domain bridges to Phases 1-5**:
- Phase 3 governance structures provide the decision-making framework that economic systems operate within; economic resilience is the resource allocation layer that sits on top
- Phase 5 Wave 2 community playbook describes organizational structure; this domain adds the economic layer — who gets what, what replaces money, how is skilled labor compensated
- Phase 4 power mapping extends naturally into economic power analysis — how do capital accumulation and resource control patterns emerge in small communities?
- Meshtastic mesh networking (Phase 6 Track B pre-research) can carry inter-community trade coordination messages; the economic domain specifies what those messages coordinate

**Unique contribution**: The resilience literature has extensive coverage of food, water, and energy systems. It has almost no rigorous research on the economic operating systems of small communities. This domain fills a specific gap in the available literature and in the Phase 1-5 corpus. It also produces the most immediately actionable templates of the three candidates — a community can take a local currency design template or a commons charter and begin implementation without further research.

**June 1 kickoff readiness**: 85%. Source library finalization takes 3-5 days; production begins June 8. No cross-domain dependencies that would delay a solo launch.

**Confidence in on-time delivery**: 95% complete by August 9 (single-domain selection); 90% complete by August 23 if paired with Governance Scaling.

### 1B. Skills Development and Knowledge Transmission

**The problem it solves**

Phase 5 assumes the community has the practical skills to execute its documented procedures. That assumption is false for most communities at formation. The gap between "knowing that you need to rotate grazing paddocks" and "knowing how to do it, teaching it, and ensuring the skill survives when the expert dies or leaves" is the failure mode that kills communities over the medium term — not acute crisis failure, but gradual degradation of technical competence across the 5-15 year horizon. Phase 5 documents what to do. This domain documents how to ensure communities can keep doing it.

**Research scope**

Three core questions: (1) What knowledge and skills are actually critical for community resilience, what are the learning time requirements, and what are the skill prerequisites — a practical learning curve analysis for the 12-15 skills Phase 5 depends on? (2) How do communities transmit complex technical skills without schools or professional credentialing — apprenticeship models, documentation strategies, knowledge preservation, competency verification within the community, and cross-generational teaching dynamics? (3) What institutional structures support skill transmission at community scale — learning cooperatives, skill-sharing networks, knowledge management systems, oral history and narrative-based transmission, and quality assurance without external certification?

**Expected word count**: 48,000-58,000 words across 10-12 sections. The Zone 5 seasonal learning calendar (when outdoor skills can be taught, agricultural timing, indoor winter skill-building) is a key structural element of this domain.

**Source library readiness**: 70-75%. Strong on apprenticeship theory: Lave and Wenger's situated learning framework (the most-cited work on how knowledge is actually transmitted in practice), adult learning theory (Kolb's experiential learning cycle, andragogy), historical guild and apprenticeship records. Good Midwest data: 4-H documentation from USDA land-grant university records and Iowa State extension, university extension curricula on practical agricultural skills. Solid disaster recovery cases: Cuba's post-Soviet collapse education adaptation (well-documented in academic journals), Puerto Rico hurricane recovery education rebuilds, UNESCO non-formal education reports. Key gap: learning curve data for specific trades (well-drilling, solar installation, medical response, hydraulics repair) in low-resource settings is limited — practitioner reports exist but rigorous academic data is sparse. This gap is manageable; document it explicitly and use best-available practitioner evidence.

**Estimated production hours**: 20 hours for source library and learning curve data research (June 1-7, with additional research sprint June 8-12 for learning curve data specifically) plus approximately 220 hours of production (12 weeks at 18-20 hours/week). One week longer than Economic Resilience due to learning curve data gap and scope management requirements.

**Cross-domain bridges to Phases 1-5**:
- Phase 6 Track A (Farm Equipment Repair): The farm equipment repair guide documents *what to do*; this domain provides the apprenticeship architecture for *how to teach it* — they are companion documents
- Phase 5 Wave 3 educational governance covers schools and formal education; this domain covers adult skill development — the execution layer that makes Phase 5 procedures usable for communities that lack formal training
- Phase 5 psychological support identified skill concentration in 1-2 people as a vulnerability; this domain is the systematic response to that vulnerability
- Phase 6 Governance Scaling (if concurrent): educational governance and institutional learning are tightly coupled — who decides what skills to teach is a governance question

**Unique contribution**: The highest leverage domain of the three by one specific metric: without intergenerational skill transmission, all Phase 1-5 systems degrade when the founding generation ages out. This is the only domain that explicitly addresses the 10-20 year sustainability horizon. A community using Phase 5 water purification procedures in 2026 must be able to teach those procedures to someone 15 years younger in 2030. This domain documents how.

**June 1 kickoff readiness**: 80%. Source library finalized June 5-8. Recommend pairing with an author who has education or adult learning background; without this background, add 1-2 weeks to timeline. Production begins June 10.

**Confidence in on-time delivery**: 88% complete by August 23 (single-domain selection); 90% complete by August 23 if paired with Economic Resilience (minimal coordination overhead).

### 1C. Governance Scaling

**The problem it solves**

Phase 3 and Phase 5 Wave 2 document governance structures for communities of roughly 20-50 people: councils, decision processes, roles, conflict resolution. These work at 20 people. They fail at 100. Consensus-based decisions that functioned with 20 people create deadlock at 100. Unwritten norms break down. Authority contests emerge. Founding-member departure leaves governance vacuums. Factionalism deepens along value lines that were previously suppressed. This domain documents what breaks at each scale threshold and what structural interventions prevent or repair the breaks. It also addresses the harder problem: how do institutions actually learn from governance failures and adapt their structures?

**Research scope**

Three core questions: (1) What governance structures allow multiple communities (150-500 people across 3-5 villages) to coordinate decisions without a central authority — federation models, consensus and deliberation protocols that scale, inter-community conflict resolution, and information flow architecture? (2) How do institutions learn from failures and adapt governance to prevent recurrence — post-crisis review cycles, adaptive management, institutional memory and knowledge transfer to new leaders, and feedback loop design? (3) What incentive structures and accountability mechanisms keep scaled governance honest — checks and balances across federation layers, transparency and information access, resource allocation mechanisms that prevent predation, and term limits and succession design?

**Expected word count**: 50,000-60,000 words across 11-13 sections. Larger than the other two candidates due to the complexity of governance failure mode analysis.

**Source library readiness**: 65-70%. Foundational sources are solid: Elinor Ostrom and Vincent Ostrom on polycentric governance (directly applicable to federated community structures), Indigenous governance systems literature (extensive academic work on nation-to-nation governance without central authority), Swiss cantonal system documentation, post-disaster governance case studies (Christchurch earthquake recovery, Puerto Rico hurricane governance). UNDRR international disaster governance frameworks, adaptive management literature (Holling, Folke), institutional change theory (North, Williamson). Zone 5 Midwest sources: Wisconsin soil-water conservation district cooperatives, Minnesota county governance structures, Iowa agricultural cooperative federations. Key gaps: detailed case studies of governance failure in resource-constrained settings are underreported in the literature (failures are less documented than successes); consensus decision-making at 300+ person scale has limited case studies; institutional learning mechanisms in small communities are at the frontier of research. These gaps are real — they are not dealbreakers but they require the author to acknowledge evidence limits explicitly and build from adjacent literature.

**Estimated production hours**: 20-25 hours for source library and governance failure case study research (June 1-10, extended because of the source gap) plus approximately 250-280 hours of production (13 weeks at 20-22 hours/week). Longest timeline of the three. Source bottleneck is the key schedule risk.

**Cross-domain bridges to Phases 1-5**:
- Phase 3 community governance structures (the primary ancestor document) — this domain extends Phase 3 to multi-community federation scale and adds the institutional learning layer
- Phase 5 Wave 2 conflict resolution directly underlies this domain — conflict resolution is the micro-level intervention; governance scaling is the structural architecture that reduces how often conflict occurs
- Phase 5 Wave 2 community implementation playbook assumes stable, small-community governance; this domain provides the constitutional architecture for what happens when size increases or founding conditions change
- Phase 6 Economic Resilience: economic structures shape what governance models are feasible; governance structures shape what economic models work — these two domains are the most tightly coupled of the three candidates

**Unique contribution**: There is extensive political science literature on national and international governance. There is almost no rigorous synthesis of how 3-5 federated communities (150-500 people) actually make decisions together in practice. The Ostrom polycentric governance framework provides the theoretical foundation but has not been applied at this specific scale and context. The institutional learning question — how do communities change their governance when it fails — is frontier research. This domain would be the most academically novel contribution of the three candidates.

**June 1 kickoff readiness**: 75%. Source library requires 8-10 days to complete (longer than the other two). Strongly recommended: pair with an author who has political science or public administration background; scope management is active, not passive (governance literature is vast and will expand without active boundary enforcement). Recommend weekly scope audits at weeks 3, 6, and 9.

**Confidence in on-time delivery**: 78% complete by August 30 (single-domain selection, aggressive scope management); 85% complete by August 23 if paired with Economic Resilience (Economic domain framing reduces Governance scope expansion risk).

### Criterion Rationale

**Urgency**
- Economic: Yellow. Complementary currency and cooperative literature is stable — no 2026-specific policy window that closes if research is deferred. However, post-pandemic interest in alternative economics is at a 2026 peak; research done now captures current practitioner momentum better than deferral to 2027. No hard deadline.
- Skills: Yellow. Knowledge transmission is evergreen. Homeschooling and experiential education interest is growing in the Midwest (COVID disruptions created visible demand), but there is no closing window. Can be deferred 3-6 months without significant strategic cost.
- Governance: Green. Institutional governance reform is gaining attention in disaster management and climate adaptation communities in 2025-2026. Regional governance initiatives (multi-county coordination for water systems, agricultural cooperative federation restructuring) are active in 2026. A governance research contribution in mid-2026 lands in an active policy conversation. Deferring to 2027 reduces relevance.

**Availability**
- Economic: Green. 75-80% source library readiness. Core foundational sources (Ostrom, Graeber, WIR, cooperative movement literature) are all freely accessible. Minimal source bottleneck June 1-7. Production begins June 8.
- Skills: Yellow. 70-75% source library readiness. Core theory (Lave/Wenger, Kolb, 4-H extension data) is accessible. Learning curve data for specific trades in low-resource settings requires a dedicated research sprint June 8-12 before full production begins. Manageable bottleneck.
- Governance: Red. 65-70% source library readiness. Foundational sources are solid, but case studies of governance failure in resource-constrained settings are systematically underreported. Consensus decision-making at 300+ person scale has limited case studies. Source completion takes 8-10 days (June 1-10). Governance literature has high paywall exposure (~30% of desired sources). This is not a dealbreaker but it is a real production risk.

**Leverage**
- Economic: Yellow. Downstream of all Phase 1-5 work (a community with food and water still needs an economic coordination layer). Directly enables Phase 7 operationalization work (implement a local currency, set up a cooperative). Good cross-project reuse — the templates are usable by any community regardless of Phase 1-5 context.
- Skills: Green. Highest leverage of the three by the 10-20 year horizon metric. Without systematic skill transmission, all Phase 1-5 systems degrade when the founding generation ages out. Unlocks Phase 7 education system design. Critical for community sustainability well beyond the acute crisis window. Phase 6 Farm Equipment Repair (Track A) is a direct companion — repair documentation without transmission architecture is incomplete.
- Governance: Green. Governance architecture is foundational to all other Phase 6 domains. Economic systems, skill transmission networks, and infrastructure coordination all depend on functioning governance. Institutional learning is the enabling mechanism for avoiding repeated failures. If governance breaks at scale, all other work fails. The highest multiplier — but requires the most careful execution.

**Scope fit**
- Economic: Green. Three core questions are tightly bounded. Research universe is "economics of small communities" — not "comprehensive economics." Expansion risk is low because the Zone 5 + 20-200 person + crisis-context filters narrow the literature to a manageable set. Clear success criteria.
- Skills: Yellow. Three core questions are well-defined. But educational research is deep; the risk of expanding into comprehensive education theory is real. Requires active boundary-setting at kickoff: scope is limited to 12-15 critical skills for resilience, not full educational system design.
- Governance: Yellow. Three questions are clear, but governance and institutional change literature is vast. High risk of scope creep into national governance theory, comparative political systems, or institutional economics. Requires active scope management (hard limit: 150-500 person federation scale only).

**User interest signals**
- Economic: Green. Phase 5 Wave 2 community playbook explicitly identifies resource distribution fairness as a key unresolved question. The mutual aid framing in Phase 3 and the cooperative context in Phase 4 power mapping both point toward economic coordination as a community priority. Midwest agricultural cooperative tradition is a clear regional affinity.
- Skills: Green. Phase 5 Wave 2 psychological support document identified skill concentration in 1-2 people as a community vulnerability. Phase 6 Track A (Farm Equipment Repair) is explicitly about skill-based practice. The 4-H and extension service tradition emphasized throughout Zone 5 research signals strong interest in systematic skill development.
- Governance: Yellow. Phase 3 and Phase 5 Wave 2 both engaged deeply with governance — but at the 20-50 person scale. Phase 6 pre-research focused more on economic and skills themes. Governance at federation scale is a logical extension but has fewer direct user signals than the other two.

## Confidence Levels and Evidence Notes

**Community Economic Resilience**: High confidence on source availability (Ostrom, Graeber, WIR, cooperative movement literature are all stable and accessible). High confidence on Midwest applicability (cooperative tradition, extension service data, agricultural cooperative network). Medium confidence on timebanking quantitative effectiveness (qualitative case studies available; quantitative impact data limited — document gap explicitly). A 2025 peer-reviewed study in *Journal of Economic Behavior and Organization* provides the most recent firm-level evidence on mutual credit resilience.

**Skills Development**: High confidence on apprenticeship theory and situated learning (Lave/Wenger is one of the most-cited educational frameworks; solid empirical foundation). High confidence on Midwest applicability (4-H and extension service data from land-grant universities is extensive). Medium confidence on learning curve data for specific trades in low-resource settings (exists as practitioner reports; not as rigorous academic data — document gap, use best-available evidence). Medium confidence on intergenerational teaching dynamics (cultural psychology literature exists; not well-synthesized for North American contexts specifically).

**Governance Scaling**: High confidence on foundational sources (Ostrom polycentric governance, Indigenous governance literature, Swiss cantonal system). High confidence on Midwest applicability (soil-water conservation districts, county governance, agricultural cooperative federations all provide regional case material). Medium confidence on governance failure case studies in resource-constrained settings (failures are systematically underreported relative to successes — this is a known limitation of the governance literature). Low confidence on "optimal federation size" research (frontier question; limited rigorous empirical guidance — this is the research frontier, not a gap that will close before publication).
