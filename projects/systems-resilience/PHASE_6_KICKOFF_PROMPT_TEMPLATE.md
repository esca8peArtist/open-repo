## SECTION A: Community Economic Resilience Kickoff Prompt

Copy this entire block and send to the assigned research agent on June 1:

---

You are beginning Phase 6 research on Community Economic Resilience for the systems-resilience project. This is a standalone research assignment producing a 45,000-55,000 word research document. You are working within an existing corpus (Phases 1-5, approximately 110,000 words) that covers individual, household, and community-scale resilience across food, water, energy, healthcare, governance, and psychological support. Your domain fills the gap Phase 1-5 left open: how communities coordinate economic exchange when formal money and supply chains fail.

**Your domain in one sentence**: Document the economic operating systems that allow small communities (20-500 persons) to coordinate exchange, allocate resources, compensate specialized labor, and trade with neighboring communities when the dollar and formal market infrastructure are unavailable.

**Three core research questions** (scope boundaries — do not expand beyond these):

1. What economic architectures sustain when the dollar fails? Cover: local currencies and mutual credit systems (design, governance, and failure modes at 20-500 person scale); barter protocols and valuation frameworks; gift economies and reciprocity systems; historical and contemporary case studies (WIR Switzerland, Bristol Pounds UK, Argentina 2001-2002 barter network, Mondragon cooperative system, Emilia-Romagna cooperative economy). Do not cover national monetary policy, cryptocurrency, or financial markets.

2. How do communities allocate capital and coordinate production without market signals? Cover: Elinor Ostrom's eight design principles for commons governance (apply directly to resource allocation); cooperative enterprise models (agricultural cooperatives, tool libraries, land trusts, mutual aid enterprises); participatory budgeting and consensus allocation processes; how communities manage surpluses, deficits, and redistribution without price signals; how skilled labor (medic, mechanic, teacher, electrician) is compensated in non-monetary systems. Do not cover macroeconomic theory or aggregate demand analysis.

3. What economic bridges prevent community isolation? Cover: inter-community trade network design; comparative advantage without global supply chains (what does one community produce that others need?); federation models for inter-community exchange coordination; barter protocols and exchange value metrics for non-monetary trade; regional economic integration (5-10 community clusters). Do not cover international trade theory or global supply chain optimization.

**Scope ceiling** (enforce these boundaries throughout):
- Community scale: 20-500 persons. Not individual scale. Not national scale.
- Time horizon: 1-20 years post-disruption. Not short-term crisis response (covered in Phase 5).
- Geography: Midwest US Zone 5 implications must be present in every section, but do not limit sources to Midwest — draw on international case studies and apply Midwest lens.
- Word count: 45,000-55,000 words. Do not compress below 40,000 or expand above 60,000.
- Citations: 120-150. Academic sources, case study documentation, and practitioner reports all count.

**Source library — confirmed high-priority sources** (verify access June 1-5):
- Elinor Ostrom, *Governing the Commons* (1990) — all eight design principles apply directly
- David Graeber, *Debt: The First 5,000 Years* (2011) — anthropological framing
- WIR system: Stodder, J. (2009), "Complementary Credit Networks and Macroeconomic Stability: Switzerland's Wirtschaftsring" — peer-reviewed case study
- Bristol Pounds impact evaluation (2015, New Economics Foundation)
- Cooperative Development Foundation Midwest agricultural cooperative data (publicly available)
- UN Habitat: *Resilient Cities — Local Economic Development in Post-Disaster Contexts* (2013)
- International Cooperative Alliance standards and performance literature
- Community land trust case studies (Burlington Community Land Trust, Champlain Housing Trust)
- 2025 *Journal of Economic Behavior and Organization* study on mutual credit system resilience (search for 2025 publication on complementary currencies)
- USDA Rural Cooperative Development Grant program documentation (H.R. 1951)

**Delivery schedule**:

## SECTION B: Intergenerational Skills Development Kickoff Prompt

Copy this entire block and send to the assigned research agent on June 1:

---

You are beginning Phase 6 research on Intergenerational Skills Development for the systems-resilience project. This is a standalone research assignment producing a 48,000-58,000 word research document. You are working within an existing corpus (Phases 1-5, approximately 110,000 words) that documents how communities do specific things: filter water, grow food, manage conflict, organize governance. Your domain fills the gap all of Phase 1-5 leaves open: how communities teach those things across generations, and how they prevent critical knowledge from evaporating when the expert ages out or dies.

**Your domain in one sentence**: Document the knowledge transmission systems, apprenticeship models, and institutional structures that allow communities to preserve, teach, and scale critical practical skills when formal schools, professional certifications, and credentialing institutions are unavailable.

**Three core research questions** (scope boundaries — do not expand beyond these):

1. What knowledge and skills are actually critical for community resilience, and what are the realistic learning time requirements? Produce a critical skills inventory of 12-15 skills (drawn directly from Phase 1-5 document domains: water system operation, food preservation, energy system maintenance, structural repair, medical response, agricultural management, governance facilitation). For each skill: describe the learning time curve for an adult learner with no prior background, identify prerequisite knowledge, note the failure mode if the skill is lost from the community. Do not cover comprehensive education curriculum design or K-12 schooling.

2. How do communities transmit complex technical skills without schools or credentialing institutions? Cover: formal and informal apprenticeship models (one-on-one, small group, cohort-based, peer teaching); documentation strategies for offline knowledge preservation (how to write procedures, create diagrams, format for non-digital distribution); competence validation without licensing boards (how communities certify that someone can actually maintain the water system); cultural transmission mechanisms that make knowledge "stick" across generations; failure modes of knowledge transmission (what causes knowledge to evaporate even when effort is made). Do not cover online learning, formal educational technology, or professional certification reform.

3. What institutions and governance structures support skill transmission at community scale? Cover: learning cooperative models (how communities organize who teaches what, how teachers are compensated, how curricula are decided); skill-sharing networks (registry design, matching teachers to learners, scheduling); knowledge libraries for offline access; quality assurance without external standards bodies; Zone 5 Midwest examples (4-H, cooperative extension, agricultural apprenticeship traditions). Do not cover university or community college systems.

**Scope ceiling** (enforce these boundaries throughout):
- Focus on 12-15 critical skills for community resilience. Not comprehensive education.
- Adult learners primarily (age 16+). Intergenerational means adults teaching youth, not K-12 curriculum design.
- Community scale: 20-500 persons. Not individual tutoring. Not school systems.
- Midwest Zone 5 implications in every section (seasonal learning calendars, agricultural skill timing).
- Word count: 48,000-58,000 words. Citations: 130-160.

**Source library — confirmed high-priority sources** (verify access June 1-8):
- Lave, J. and Wenger, E., *Situated Learning: Legitimate Peripheral Participation* (1991) — foundational apprenticeship theory
- Kolb, D.A., *Experiential Learning* (1984) — experiential learning cycle
- Knowles, M., *The Adult Learner* (any edition) — andragogy / adult learning theory
- Sobel, D., *Place-Based Education: Connecting Classrooms and Communities* — place-based learning
- UNESCO, *Non-Formal Education in Post-Crisis Contexts* (2013) — non-institutional skill development
- USDA 4-H program documentation and outcomes research (publicly available at 4-h.org)
- Cooperative Extension Service outcomes research (land-grant university archives)
- Cuba post-Soviet education adaptation: Mesa-Lago, C. and Pérez-López, J. on Cuba's Special Period education response
- Puerto Rico post-Hurricane Maria education rebuilding reports (University of Puerto Rico archives, 2018-2020)
- Academic literature on apprenticeship outcomes: *Journal of Vocational Education and Training*, *Human Resource Development Quarterly*
- Historical guild and apprenticeship records (European guild system, US colonial apprenticeship records)

**Delivery schedule**:
- June 8: Source library verified; escalate if learning curve data for specific trades is unavailable (this is the most likely gap)
- June 15: Critical skills inventory complete (12-15 skills, learning curves, prerequisites, failure modes)
- June 15: Research outline complete (all 10-12 section headings with summaries)
- July 5: Sections 1-5 first draft (knowledge failure modes, critical skills inventory, apprenticeship models, documentation, peer teaching)
- August 2: Sections 6-10 first draft (intergenerational dynamics, learning cooperatives, Zone 5 calendar, implementation templates)
- August 23: Full draft complete (48,000-58,000 words, all citations)
- August 28: Final edited draft

**Output format**: Same as Economic Resilience — Obsidian Markdown, YAML frontmatter, `/projects/systems-resilience/phase-6/` directory, file naming `phase-6-skills-development-[section-name].md`.

**Cross-domain integration**:
- Every major Phase 1-5 document generates a "how to teach this" application for the critical skills inventory. Draw from Phase 1-5 directly.
- Phase 3 governance document informs educational governance (who decides curriculum, who certifies competence).
- Phase 5 psychological support covers trauma and group cohesion; shared learning is a community cohesion mechanism — note the bridge.

Scope escalation rule: if you find yourself writing about K-12 curriculum design, online learning platforms, or university extension reform, stop and log it in `scope-notes.md`. The scope is non-formal skill transmission in small communities, not education policy reform.

## SECTION C: Governance Scaling + Institutional Adaptation Kickoff Prompt

Copy this entire block and send to the assigned research agent on June 1:

---

You are beginning Phase 6 research on Governance Scaling and Institutional Adaptation for the systems-resilience project. This is a standalone research assignment producing a 50,000-60,000 word research document. You are working within an existing corpus (Phases 1-5) that documents community governance at 25-150 person scale. Your domain extends governance to 150-500+ person federations and adds the failure mode analysis that Phase 3 explicitly did not address: what breaks when you scale governance, and how do institutions learn from those failures?

**Your domain in one sentence**: Document how multiple communities (150-500 persons across 3-5 villages or neighborhoods) make decisions together without a central authority, and how those governance institutions learn from failures to prevent repeated breakdown.

**Three core research questions** (scope boundaries — do not expand beyond these):

1. What governance structures allow multiple communities to coordinate without a central authority? Cover: federation models from real case studies (Swiss cantons, Iroquois Confederacy, agricultural cooperative federations, Venezuelan parroquias, Zapatista autonomous municipalities — apply Midwest lens to each); consensus and deliberation protocols that function at 300-500 person scale (most documented examples are <200 — this is a research frontier; cover what exists and note the gap); inter-community conflict resolution and dispute mechanisms; information flow and communication cascades across federations without telecommunications infrastructure. Do not cover national or international governance, UN governance systems, or democratic theory at state/federal level.

2. How do institutions learn from failures and adapt governance to prevent repeated failure modes? Cover: post-crisis review cycles (how communities conduct after-action reviews and translate them into governance changes); adaptive management protocols for governance (how to change procedures when conditions change); institutional memory — how communities document lessons and transfer them to new leaders; feedback loops and early warning indicators that a governance failure is imminent; failure mode analysis (what governance structures fail under what specific conditions — centralization, information gaps, legitimacy collapse, etc.). Do not cover organizational learning theory at corporate or national scale.

3. What incentive structures and accountability mechanisms keep scaled governance honest? Cover: checks and balances across federation layers; transparency and information access in distributed governance (how do community members know what decisions are being made?); resource allocation mechanisms that prevent predation and rent-seeking; term limits, rotation, and leadership accountability without formal recall mechanisms; Zone 5 Midwest case studies (county governance, rural water authorities, soil conservation districts, agricultural cooperative federations — Land O'Lakes, Organic Valley). Do not cover electoral systems, political party theory, or constitutional design.

**Scope ceiling** (enforce weekly — this domain has the highest expansion risk):
- Scale: 150-500 persons across 3-5 federated communities. Not townships. Not counties. Not municipalities.
- Federation size: never model structures requiring more than 500 persons total. If a case study involves larger governance, apply it only at the federation layer and extract the applicable principles.
- Literature scope: commons governance and community-scale federalism only. Political science theory at state/national scale is background context, not primary content.
- Word count: 50,000-60,000 words. Citations: 140-180.

**CRITICAL SCOPE MANAGEMENT PROTOCOL**: At Week 3 (June 21), Week 6 (July 12), and Week 9 (August 2), conduct a scope audit: review everything written and confirm it addresses 150-500 person federation governance. Flag any sections that have drifted into macro-governance theory. Log drift in `scope-notes.md` and trim before next section begins.

**Source library — confirmed high-priority sources** (verify access June 1-10):
- Ostrom, E., *Governing the Commons* (1990) — eight design principles apply to federation governance
- Ostrom, V., *The Meaning of American Federalism* (1991) — polycentric governance theory
- Bookchin, M. and Bookchin, D., works on libertarian municipalism and democratic confederalism
- Zapatista governance research: Harvey, N., *The Chiapas Rebellion* — community-scale autonomous governance
- Indigenous governance: Alfred, T., *Wasáse: Indigenous Pathways of Action and Freedom* — nation-to-nation governance frameworks
- Swiss cantonal system: Feld, L. and Kirchgässner, G., "The Role of Direct Democracy in Swiss Economic Policy" (peer-reviewed)
- Christchurch earthquake governance: Canterbury Earthquake Recovery Authority reports (publicly available, New Zealand government)
- Haiti earthquake governance: Schuller, M., works on NGO governance and community coordination failures
- UNDRR: *Sendai Framework for Disaster Risk Reduction 2015-2030* — regional coordination structures
- North, D., *Institutions, Institutional Change and Economic Performance* — institutional adaptation theory
- Midwest cooperative governance: National Council of Farmer Cooperatives documentation; USDA RBS cooperative governance research

**Delivery schedule**:
- June 10: Source library verified; escalate if governance failure case studies are unavailable (most likely gap — failures are underreported)
- June 10: Scope boundary document complete (1 page defining hard edges of 150-500 person federation focus)
- June 21: Sections 1-3 research outline complete (governance failure modes, federation models, consensus protocols)
