### Option B: Unified — Full 66.4K Release June 15

**Publish June 15:**
- All 12 documents in a single comprehensive release
- Master table of contents integrating all 5 waves
- Unified cross-reference structure

**Pros:**
- Single coherent artifact: "The 66,000-word systems resilience guide" is a stronger positioning than "Volume 1 + Volume 2"

**Timeline**:

- June 1–14: Editorial integration (tight window)

  - Day 11–14: Final proofs, any last-minute corrections
- June 15: Publish unified corpus

**Success metrics:**
- June 15 publication on schedule
- Zero quality issues in Wave 3 documents that would have been caught with more review time
- Master TOC is clear and navigable

### Option C: Rolling — 6 Weekly Thematic Releases May 30–July 4

**Publish sequence:**
- **Week 1 (May 30)**: Community Implementation Playbook + Microgrids (~15K words)
- **Week 2 (June 6)**: Psychological Support + Conflict Resolution (~18K words)
- **Week 3 (June 13)**: Veterinary Care + Livestock Care (~13.6K words)
- **Week 4 (June 20)**: Food Preservation + Seed Saving (~7.3K words)
- **Week 5 (June 27)**: Water Systems + Healthcare Offline (~7.9K words)
- **Week 6 (July 4)**: Fuel Production + Educational Governance (~4.7K words)

**Pros:**
- Best audience digestibility: 15–20K words per week vs. 43K+ in one release
- Highest feedback velocity: each release generates focused feedback on that week's content
- Best matching to audience readiness: Week 1 for organizing communities; Week 3 for established groups
- Sustained visibility: 6 distribution moments vs. 1–2

**Cons:**
- Requires sustained promotional effort for 6 consecutive weeks
- High coordination complexity: if one release gets critical feedback, do you revise before subsequent releases publish?
- Fragmented discoverability until all 6 are published
- Risk of audience fatigue or declining engagement after Week 2

**Timeline**: 6-week rolling execution; high coordination overhead throughout

**Success metrics:**
- All 6 releases on schedule (no slippage)
- Engagement (shares, questions, adoption reports) remains consistent through Week 6
- Per-release feedback is actionable and addressed in feedback notes

---

### Recommendation: **Option A (Staged — Waves 1+2 June 1 + Wave 3 June 30)**

**Why Option A:**

1. **Wave 1+2 is independently complete.** It covers community infrastructure → human systems → execution playbook. Practitioners get everything they need to begin organizing without waiting for operational household content.

2. **Feedback before finalization.** The 4-week gap (June 1–30) is a real advantage. If practitioners identify gaps (missing Zone 5 specifics, unclear procedures, absent equipment details), those gaps can be addressed in Wave 3 before it publishes — a feature Option B doesn't have.

3. **Lower execution risk.** Two clean publication events is operationally simpler than either a 14-day editorial marathon (Option B) or a 6-week rolling campaign (Option C).

4. **Natural reading sequence.** Communities need to organize first (Wave 1+2) before they can implement household-level operations (Wave 3). Publishing in that sequence matches how audiences will use the material.

5. **June 30 is realistic.** Wave 3 requires ~8–12 hours of editorial work (cross-reference updates, frontmatter standardization, master TOC). This is easily accomplished in the June 1–30 window without urgency pressure.

**If you select Option A**: Does Wave 3 need any content additions before June 30, or is the current corpus sufficient?

## Decision 2: Phase 6 Domain Selection + Sequencing

**Current status**: Phase 6 groundwork is complete (May 27). Three candidate domains are production-ready.

### Phase 6 At a Glance

Phase 6 researches how communities scale from the Phase 1–5 foundations into regional, multi-decade resilience systems. Three domains are recommended:

| Domain | Primary Question | Word Count | Source Readiness | Production Time | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **A: Community Economic Resilience** | How do communities exchange goods and services when formal money fails? | 45–55K | 75–80% ready | 11 weeks | Lowest risk, highest confidence |
| **C: Intergenerational Skills Development** | How do communities preserve and transmit critical knowledge across generations? | 48–58K | 70–75% ready | 12 weeks | Highest long-term leverage |
| **D: Governance Scaling** | How do multi-community federations make decisions and learn from failures? | 50–60K | 65–70% ready | 13 weeks | Foundational for Phase 7 operationalization |

**Current status of each domain:**

#### Domain A: Community Economic Resilience

**What's ready**: Pre-research chapter (6,800 words, 38 citations) — production-ready and committed.

**Research scope**: 
1. What economic structures enable exchange when formal money fails? (cooperatives, credit unions, timebanking, local currencies, barter networks)
2. How do communities establish trade relationships with neighboring communities? (supply chain coordination, negotiation, dispute resolution)
3. What policy and institutional barriers prevent community economic self-organization? (current federal landscape, USDA funding, regulatory obstacles)

**Expected output**: 45,000–55,000 words covering economic failure scenarios, cooperative models, international case studies (Mondragon, Emilia-Romagna, WIR, Argentina barter network), Midwest examples (agricultural cooperatives, credit unions, food hubs), policy landscape, and templates for minimum viable exchange networks.

**June 1 readiness**: 95% confidence completion by August 9.

**Why select this domain**:
- **Fastest ramp**: 75–80% source-ready means production begins June 8–10 with minimal pre-research.
- **Extends Phase 5 directly**: Every Phase 5 domain (food, energy, healthcare, education) requires exchange infrastructure. This domain provides the framework all other Phase 6 work depends on.
- **USDA timing urgency**: Rural Cooperative Development Grant program (H.R. 1951) opens fall 2026 for applications. Communities with this framework in hand by August 30 can apply in fall cycle.
- **Highest delivery confidence**: Lowest source gaps, clearest research questions, tightest scope.

---

#### Domain C: Intergenerational Skills Development

**What's ready**: Full candidate outline and readiness assessment (production-ready).

**Research scope**:
1. What are the critical skills for community resilience, and how long does it take to learn them? (medical response, water system maintenance, food preservation, energy operation, repair, materials science)
2. How do communities transmit complex technical skills without schools or credentialing institutions? (apprenticeship models, documentation, peer teaching, learning-by-doing)
3. What governance structures support skill transmission at community scale? (learning cooperatives, skill-sharing networks, quality assurance without licensing)

**Expected output**: 48,000–58,000 words covering knowledge transmission failure modes, critical skills inventory with learning curves, apprenticeship models, documentation and knowledge preservation strategies, intergenerational teaching dynamics, learning cooperatives, 4-H and extension case studies, and implementation templates for community apprenticeship programs.

**June 1 readiness**: 88% confidence completion by August 23.

**Why select this domain**:
- **Highest long-term leverage**: Phase 1–5 documents HOW to do things. This domain documents HOW TO TEACH IT — the bridge that prevents knowledge evaporation when the expert dies.
- **Existential failure mode**: If a community loses its solar technician or medical responder and no one was trained to replace them, the entire system fails. This is what kills communities post-collapse.
- **Midwest strength**: 4-H (6.8M participants in Zone 5), extension services, agricultural apprenticeship tradition provide rich primary sources.
- **5–20 year timescale**: Most resilience work is Days 1–365. This domain addresses Years 5–20 — critical for communities that survive the initial crisis.

---

#### Domain D: Governance Scaling

**What's ready**: Full candidate outline, selection matrix, comprehensive sequencing recommendation.

**Research scope**:
1. What governance structures allow multiple communities to coordinate without a central authority? (federation models, consensus protocols, inter-community conflict resolution)
2. How do institutions learn from failures and adapt to prevent repeated failure modes? (post-crisis review cycles, adaptive management, institutional memory)
3. What incentive structures and accountability mechanisms prevent governance corruption at scaled levels? (checks and balances, transparency, term limits, resource allocation fairness)

**Expected output**: 50,000–60,000 words covering governance failure modes at scale, federation models (Swiss cantons, Iroquois Confederacy, agricultural cooperatives, water authorities), consensus decision-making at 150–500 person scale, inter-community conflict resolution, institutional learning mechanisms, information management in distributed systems, Zone 5 case studies, and governance charter templates.

**June 1 readiness**: 78% confidence completion by August 30 (82% with political science background author).

**Why select this domain**:
- **Foundational for all other Phase 6 work**: Economic systems, skill transmission systems, and every other community structure depend on functioning governance. This domain is the backbone.
- **Extends Phase 3 directly**: Phase 3 documented governance at 25–150 person scale. This extends it to 150–500 person federations and adds failure mode analysis — what governance structures break at scale?
- **Current relevance**: 2025–2026 post-COVID governance failure examination is active in cooperative and resilience networks. Research in 2026 speaks to current events.
- **Midwest applicability**: Extensive cooperative governance experience (Land O'Lakes, Organic Valley, rural water authorities, soil conservation districts), county governance, multi-entity resilience coordination at state level.
