## The Problem Each Domain Solves

### Domain A: Community Economic Resilience (20 hours)

**The gap**: Phase 5 documents how to produce food, water, power, and healthcare. None of it addresses how a community coordinates who gets what when there's no money, no supply chains, and no state. Without an economic layer, surpluses rot, critical labor doesn't get done, and communities fracture over perceived unfairness.

**What it covers**:
- Systems for exchange when cash fails (mutual credit, labor-hour systems, gift economies)
- Resource allocation models (need-based, skill-based, labor-contributed)
- Surplus management and storage coordination
- Compensation mechanisms for essential but undesirable work
- Commons governance and fair resource distribution
- Transition models from market to alternative economics

**Extension of Phase 5**: Sits on top of the community playbook (Phase 5 Wave 2) — governance structure + economic layer = complete decision framework.

**Zone 5 applicability**: Rural/suburban communities (20-200 people), Midwest context. Case studies: Transition Towns, LETS systems, cooperative networks in region.

**Precedent**: Highly documented. Elinor Ostrom's commons work, Transition Towns (UK), Time Banks, community currencies (Totnes Pound, Salt Spring Dollars).

**Why start with this**: If your community has made it through the acute crisis phase and is asking "how do we ensure fairness in resource distribution," this is foundational.

---

### Domain C: Skills Development & Knowledge Transmission (20 hours)

**The gap**: Phase 5 assumes communities have the skills to execute the documented procedures. That assumption is false for most communities. "Knowing that you need to rotate grazing paddocks" is different from "knowing how to do it," "being able to teach it," and "having a system to ensure the skill doesn't die when the expert leaves."

**What it covers**:
- Adult skill development (apprenticeship models, situated learning)
- Skill inventory systems (what does our community know, what do we need to learn?)
- Knowledge preservation (documentation, redundancy, cross-training)
- Teaching structures (mentorship, peer learning, formal apprenticeship)
- Skill verification and certification within the community
- Intergenerational knowledge transmission
- Response to skill loss (death, injury, departure)

**Extension of Phase 5**: The educational governance document (Phase 5 Wave 3) covers schools. This covers adult skill development — the practical execution layer.

**Zone 5 applicability**: Midwest context. Case studies: Amish knowledge transmission, permaculture design courses, FEMA CERT training architecture.

**Precedent**: Well-documented in adult learning theory, apprenticeship literature, and crisis response training (Red Cross, FEMA).

**Why start with this**: If your community has good organization but realizes "we're concentrated expertise in 2-3 people" or "nobody under 30 knows how to fix a chainsaw," this is the enabler layer that makes Phase 5 usable.

---

### Domain D: Governance Evolution & Scaling (20 hours)

**The gap**: Phase 5 Wave 2's conflict resolution and community playbook documents assume a roughly stable, small community under acute stress. This domain addresses what happens when a community of 20 successfully scales to 50, then 100, then 200. Consensus-based decisions that worked at 20 people create deadlock at 100. Unwritten norms break down. Authority contests emerge. Factionalism deepens.

**What it covers**:
- Constitutional governance (how to design structures that scale)
- Consensus mechanisms at different scales (consent-based, sociocracy, deliberative)
- Decision-making structures for 20 vs. 50 vs. 200 people
- Amendment processes and governance evolution
- Succession planning and founding-member departure
- Faction management and deep disagreement
- Nested governance (individual-household-neighborhood-community)

**Extension of Phase 5**: Directly extends the conflict resolution and community playbook documents — governance tools + constitutional layer = scalable structure.

**Zone 5 applicability**: Midwest context. Case studies: Mondragon cooperative (scaling from small to 80,000+ employees), Zapatista governance, kibbutz governance failure and reform.

**Precedent**: Strong academic literature (sociocracy, deliberative democracy). Applied precedent in cooperative movements.

**Why start with this**: If your Phase 5 question is "our governance works now at 20 people—what happens when we're 100?" or "we need founding documents, not just emergency procedures," this is foundational.
