## 2. Research Gaps — What Needs Depth

**Gap 1: Quantitative impact data for timebanking (refinement gap, not blocking)**

The existing chapter documents timebanking qualitatively. Academic literature on timebank effectiveness is primarily qualitative case studies. Two supplementary searches are needed: (a) Timebanks USA national data on member exchange volumes and retention rates; (b) UK Time Banking evaluation literature (Nesta Foundation, 2011–2019 systematic reviews). This gap will be documented explicitly with honest uncertainty language rather than obscured. Estimated additional research: 3 to 4 hours.

**Gap 2: Midwest-specific cooperative formation barriers (high priority)**

The chapter covers cooperative models comprehensively but needs depth on the specific barriers Zone 5 rural communities face: state-level cooperative incorporation law variation, USDA Rural Development office processing timelines for cooperative technical assistance grants, credit union chartering in rural counties with populations under 5,000. Sources: USDA RD state-by-state cooperative formation guides, National Federation of Community Development Credit Unions, state cooperative extension service reports. Estimated research: 4 to 5 hours.

**Gap 3: Inter-community trade protocols (highest priority — not covered in pre-research)**

The pre-research chapter covers internal community exchange. The domain's second research question — how communities establish trade relationships with neighboring communities — requires original research on: comparative advantage without global supply chains, Zone 5 regional food hub models (Good Food Network, Regional Food Hub Convergence data), historical barter network coordination across communities (Argentina's Red de Trueque regional nodes), and the governance structure of inter-community agreements. This is the largest gap. Estimated research: 6 to 8 hours.

**Gap 4: USDA grant application mechanics (moderate priority)**

The chapter cites H.R. 1951 and the Rural Cooperative Development Grant program. Full coverage requires: current USDA RBCS (Rural Business-Cooperative Service) application timeline, eligibility requirements, example successful grantees in Zone 5 states (IA, MN, WI, IL, MO), and what a community needs to prepare before applying. This is the most directly actionable section for 2026 practitioners. Sources: usda.gov/rurdev, NSAC (National Sustainable Agriculture Coalition) cooperative program guides. Estimated research: 3 to 4 hours.

**Gap 5: Legal and institutional framework for community land trusts (low priority)**

The chapter mentions community land trusts as an asset-locking mechanism. A full section needs: CLT formation requirements by state (some states have enabling legislation, others do not), Champlain Housing Trust and Homestead CLT as case studies, and Zone 5 CLT inventory (currently 42 CLTs in the five-state region per Grounded Solutions Network data). Sources: Grounded Solutions Network, Lincoln Institute of Land Policy CLT database. Estimated research: 3 hours.

## 3. USDA Grant Angle and Timing Urgency

**The window**: The USDA Rural Cooperative Development Grant (RCDG) program, reauthorized by H.R. 1951 (119th Congress, Strengthening Rural Cooperatives and Communities Act) at $24.3M annually through FY2029, runs annual application cycles. Based on prior cycles, NOFA (Notice of Funding Availability) is typically published August to October for the following fiscal year's funding. For FY2027 funding, communities that complete cooperative formation technical assistance in summer 2026 are positioned to apply.

**Why the August 9 deadline is a real constraint, not an arbitrary one**: A community that completes Domain A research and begins cooperative formation in August 2026 has time to: identify the appropriate cooperative development center (USDA funds 27 across the country), apply for technical assistance, receive feasibility study support, and have a complete application ready for a fall 2026 or spring 2027 NOFA. A community that receives this information in October 2026 misses the feasibility study window and applies cold, with lower success probability.

**How this shapes the research**: The policy and program landscape section (Section 3 in the planned outline) should be written as a practitioner action guide, not a background section. Readers should be able to identify their closest cooperative development center, understand eligibility requirements, and know what documentation to begin assembling, from the Domain A document itself.

## 4. Citation Strategy

**Tier 1 — Canonical sources (cite directly, treat as primary)**

- Elinor Ostrom, *Governing the Commons* (Cambridge University Press, 1990; multiple editions) — the foundational governance framework for commons management
- WIR Bank annual reports (1934 to present) — 90 years of operational data on mutual credit at community scale
- Mondragon Corporation annual reports 2020 to 2024 — current data on worker cooperative performance through crisis
- USDA Rural Business-Cooperative Service (RBCS) program documentation — authoritative on federal programs
- Argentina barter club documentation (Red de Trueque, 2001 to 2004) — documented exchange volume data (three million participants, 400 nodes at peak)
- Emilia-Romagna cooperative economy data (Legacoop regional reports, EU regional statistics) — 30 percent regional GDP from cooperatives

**Tier 2 — Supporting empirical sources (cite for specific claims)**

- Bernard Lietaer and Jacqui Dunne, *Rethinking Money* (Berrett-Koehler, 2013) — complementary currency design principles
- E.F. Schumacher, *Small Is Beautiful* — intermediate technology economics framework
- NCBA CLUSA cooperative development reports (annual) — US cooperative formation data
- Food Co-op Impact Report 2024 — $1.604 local multiplier data
- National Center for Employee Ownership (NCEO) meta-analysis on employee ownership performance
- Bristol Pound evaluation (University of Bristol, 2015) — local currency circulation and economic retention data

- Illinois Institute for Rural Affairs cooperative formation guides
- Land O'Lakes cooperative governance history
- Organic Valley annual reports (worker and agricultural cooperative hybrid model)
- Great Plains Food Bank regional food hub data
- Iowa State University Extension cooperative economics reports

From each Tier 2 source, verify the specific claim being cited has a primary source (do not cite secondary summaries of primary data).
