# Wave 2 Publication-Ready Criteria
## Numeric Thresholds, Structure Requirements, and Publication Checklist — Phase 6 Domains 60–65

> **Lead finding**: Phase 6 Wave 2 quality standards are consistent with Phase 5 baselines (8,000–10,000 words, 93+ citations, zero placeholder markers) but adapted for six domains with sharply different source readiness levels. Domain 60 (International Coordination) and Domain 62 (Infrastructure Interdependencies) carry higher word targets and citation floors to compensate for sparse practitioner-accessible literature. Tier C authors operate under a graduated standard (4,500–5,500 words, ≥35 citations) with the same actionability and structure requirements — the bar is the same, the scope is narrower.

---

## Part 1: Global Standards (Apply to All Six Domains)

These thresholds apply regardless of domain, author tier, or source readiness. They derive from Phase 5 Wave 1+2 production standards and are not negotiable downward for any domain.

### 1.1 Core Word Count Floor and Ceiling

**Tier A and Tier B authors:**

| Target | Minimum | Maximum |
|--------|---------|---------|
| 8,000 words (preferred) | 6,500 words | 10,000 words |

The 6,500-word floor is absolute: a document below 6,500 words has not covered its domain to publication depth. The 10,000-word ceiling is a scope control measure — a document above 10,000 words is almost certainly out of scope, and the author should split or cut, not pad. Phase 5 Wave 2 documents ranged from 8,304 to 10,698 words; this range is the empirically validated production target.

**Tier C authors** (score 6–13 on five-dimension rubric):

| Target | Minimum | Maximum |
|--------|---------|---------|
| 5,000 words (preferred) | 4,500 words | 6,500 words |

Tier C authors produce a narrower document — one to two sections rather than the full six or seven — at full depth. A Tier C 5,000-word focused section is publication-ready. A Tier C 8,000-word document that is shallow throughout is not. Depth over breadth for Tier C.

### 1.2 Citation Minimum

**Tier A and Tier B: ≥40 citations per domain document.**

The Phase 5 integrated corpus averaged 18.6 citations per document (93 citations across 5 documents). Wave 2 domains require a higher floor because they enter more contested, sparse, and multi-disciplinary territory than Phase 5 did. Forty citations is achievable for all six domains based on the source readiness assessments in PHASE_6_DOMAIN_60_65_RESEARCH_LANDSCAPE.md.

**Tier C: ≥25 citations.** The narrower scope justifies a lower count, but citations must still satisfy the quality requirements below.

**Citation quality requirements (apply to all tiers):**

- At least 50% of citations must be accessible without institutional login (open-access, direct URL, or freely available grey literature).
- At least 30% must be peer-reviewed or formally published (academic journals, government reports, UN/NGO primary publications). Grey literature, practitioner blogs, and media do not count toward this 30%.
- No more than 20% may come from a single source type (e.g., no more than 8 of 40 citations from a single organization's publications — this prevents the document from being a single-institution literature review).
- Every citation must have a working URL or a clear bibliographic reference that an independent reader could verify. URLs are strongly preferred.

**Citation count cap**: A document with more than 100 citations has likely lost focus. Above 100 citations is a scope creep red flag (see Part 5, Scope Creep Detection).

### 1.3 Structure Requirements

Every Wave 2 document must follow a six-to-seven section structure. The exact section titles are domain-specific (see Part 2), but the function of each section is fixed:

| Position | Function | Required? |
|----------|----------|-----------|
| Section 1 — Foundation | What is the problem, why does it matter at community scale, what Phase 5 work does it build on | Yes — all tiers |
| Section 2 — Mechanisms | How the core systems work (economic, ecological, social, technical — domain-dependent) | Yes — all tiers |
| Section 3 — Landscape | Who is doing this, what have they learned, what precedents exist | Yes — Tier A/B; Tier C: abbreviated |
| Section 4 — Pathways | How a Zone 5 community actually begins: first steps, sequence, decision logic | Yes — all tiers |
| Section 5 — Implementation | Detailed protocols, timelines, resource requirements, failure modes | Yes — Tier A/B; Tier C: one subsection |
| Section 6 — International Precedent | What has worked elsewhere that applies here (with Zone 5 translation) | Yes — Tier A/B; Tier C: integrated into Section 3 |
| Section 7 — Summary | Key findings, practitioner decision matrix, cross-domain connections | Yes — all tiers |

**Section minimum word counts (Tier A/B):**

| Section | Minimum Words | Notes |
|---------|--------------|-------|
| Foundation | 700 | Sets the problem frame; must cite Phase 5 work |
| Mechanisms | 1,200 | Domain-specific depth; most citations concentrate here |
| Landscape | 900 | Practitioner and academic precedents; no more than 40% academic |
| Pathways | 1,100 | Must include decision logic, not just description |
| Implementation | 1,800 | Longest section; protocols must be specific and reproducible |
| International Precedent | 700 | At least two non-US examples |
| Summary | 600 | Must include practitioner decision matrix (as table) and cross-domain connections |
| **Total minimum** | **7,000** | Satisfies the 6,500-word floor with margin |

**Tier C section requirements**: Foundation, Mechanisms, Pathways (abbreviated), Summary. Minimum 4,500 words across four sections. Sections 3, 5, and 6 may be integrated into adjacent sections rather than standing alone.

### 1.4 Actionability Filter

Every section in every Wave 2 document must pass this three-part actionability filter before publication. This filter catches the "reads like a research outline" failure mode.

**Filter Item A — Claim/Source Binding**: Every factual claim — every statement of what works, what fails, what a community needs, what a process requires — must have at least one cited source. Unsourced assertions are not permitted in publication-ready Wave 2 documents, regardless of how obvious the claim appears to be. Test: run a scan for paragraphs with more than 4 sentences and no citation. If found, each paragraph needs source verification before publication.

**Filter Item B — Implementation Pathway**: Every domain document must identify at least one specific, reproducible implementation pathway for a Zone 5 community with no prior infrastructure in this domain. "Reproducible" means: a community coordinator could follow the pathway from the document alone, without additional expert consultation for the initial steps. The pathway must include: starting condition, sequence of steps, resource requirements at each step, and success indicators. Theory sections and landscape sections are exempt, but each must point toward sections that satisfy this requirement.

**Filter Item C — Success Metrics**: Every implementation pathway must be accompanied by at least three measurable success metrics. Metrics must be observable at community scale (not requiring academic measurement infrastructure). Examples of acceptable metrics: "soil organic matter percentage at 12-month test" (observable, community-testable), "number of households participating in timebank after 6 months" (observable, no infrastructure required). Examples of unacceptable metrics: "improved community resilience" (not measurable), "greater awareness of international coordination options" (not measurable).

### 1.5 Author Confidence Threshold

Authors self-rate their confidence in the publication-ready state of their document on a 0–100% scale at two points: submission for peer review, and resubmission after peer review revisions. The threshold and its consequences:

| Self-Rated Confidence | Consequence |
|----------------------|-------------|
| ≥85% | Standard publication path; proceed |
| 70–84% | Author must identify and document the specific sources of uncertainty before submission; peer reviewer gets a copy of the uncertainty log; one additional peer review cycle may be warranted |
| <70% | Escalate to project lead before peer review begins; author and project lead do a 30-minute scope alignment call; may trigger scope reduction or additional research support |

**For Tier C authors specifically**: If a Tier C author submits with self-rated confidence <70%, this triggers additional peer review rounds automatically (two reviewers instead of one). The lower experience baseline means a confidence gap is more likely to reflect genuine content gaps rather than author perfectionism.

The self-confidence rating is not a gatekeeping tool — it is a calibration signal that routes authors to the right level of support before the peer review round, saving turnaround time.

### 1.6 Cross-Domain Bridge Requirements

Every Wave 2 document must document at least three explicit connections to other Phase 6 or Phase 7 domains. A connection is "explicit" when: (a) it is named and described in text (not just implied), (b) it references the specific domain by name, and (c) it explains the directionality (does this domain draw from another, contribute to another, or create a feedback loop?).

Cross-domain bridges may appear anywhere in the document but must be consolidated in the Summary section's practitioner decision matrix. The minimum three bridges may include:

- Phase 5 domains (governance, food, information, security, scaling) — at least one bridge to Phase 5 is required, since Wave 2 builds on Wave 1's community foundation
- Other Phase 6 domains (60–65) — the primary cross-domain connections
- Phase 7 domains (if known at time of writing — these connections may be provisional)

**Example of an acceptable cross-domain bridge** (from a Domain 64 document): "The complementary currency design in Section 4 depends on the governance legitimacy framework developed in Domain 65 — specifically, the consent-based decision-making protocols described there provide the authorization mechanism for a local currency board. Communities should not implement a local currency without first having a governance structure that has passed the 50-person transition threshold analyzed in Domain 65."

**Example of an insufficient bridge**: "This domain connects to governance issues addressed elsewhere in the Phase 6 corpus." (Too vague — does not name the domain, does not describe the connection, does not explain directionality.)

## Part 2: Per-Domain Word Count Targets and Structure Notes

The global standards in Part 1 apply universally. These per-domain parameters adjust for source readiness differences and domain-specific structural needs.

### Domain 60 — International Coordination Frameworks

**Source readiness**: 55–65% (lowest of six domains — sparse practitioner-accessible literature)

**Word target (Tier A/B)**: 7,000–9,000 words (lower ceiling than other domains; scope must remain community-scale; sprawl into intergovernmental literature is the primary failure mode)

**Word target (Tier C)**: 4,500–5,500 words

**Citation minimum (Tier A/B)**: ≥45 citations. The higher floor compensates for source sparseness — the author must work harder to document what exists at community scale and must draw from adjacent literatures (food sovereignty networks, diaspora economics, international NGO coordination manuals). Forty-five citations is achievable given the expert contact starter list (La Via Campesina, GRAIN, ODI, IACD, Transnational Institute).

**Citation minimum (Tier C)**: ≥28 citations

**Section emphasis**: Section 3 (Landscape) and Section 6 (International Precedent) are the core of this domain — they are what distinguishes it from domestic community organizing literature. Both sections must be fully developed by Tier A/B authors; Tier C may integrate them but must include substantial international precedent content regardless.

**Scope hard boundary**: Community and sub-national scale only. Nation-state foreign policy, intergovernmental treaty analysis, and multilateral institution design are out of scope. Every paragraph must answer: "What does a Zone 5 community coordinator actually do with this information?"

**Domain-specific structure note**: Section 6 (International Precedent) should lead with at least two non-English-language precedents in translation — Andean ayllus, Zapatista caracoles coordination, European food sovereignty networks. The practitioner audience is North American but the precedent base is intentionally international.

---

### Domain 61 — Intergenerational Knowledge Transmission

**Source readiness**: 68–72%

**Word target (Tier A/B)**: 7,500–9,500 words

**Word target (Tier C)**: 4,500–5,500 words

**Citation minimum (Tier A/B)**: ≥40 citations

**Citation minimum (Tier C)**: ≥25 citations

**Section emphasis**: Section 2 (Mechanisms) is the domain's distinctive contribution — the tacit/explicit knowledge distinction (Polanyi), situated learning theory (Lave and Wenger), and their Zone 5 agricultural applications. This section must go beyond theory to describe actual transmission mechanisms: apprenticeship structures, oral history recording protocols, skill demonstration documentation. Section 5 (Implementation) must include specific apprenticeship program designs, not general apprenticeship principles.

**Scope hard boundary**: Formal K-12 or higher educational reform is out of scope. The domain is non-formal skill and knowledge transmission without institutional infrastructure. Every section must be answerable without a licensed educational institution.

**Domain-specific structure note**: Section 4 (Pathways) for this domain should include a specific skill inventory methodology — a process by which a community identifies which skills are at risk of being lost in the next generation. This is the entry point for communities; without it, implementation cannot begin.

---

### Domain 62 — Infrastructure Interdependencies

**Source readiness**: 65–72% (second-lowest, but engineering literature is dense and requires heavy community-scale translation)

**Word target (Tier A/B)**: 7,500–9,500 words

**Word target (Tier C)**: 4,500–5,500 words

**Citation minimum (Tier A/B)**: ≥45 citations. Higher floor for the same reason as Domain 60 — community-scale cascade analysis requires synthesis from large-scale infrastructure literature plus permaculture, preparedness, and engineering literature. The author must bridge multiple source types.

**Citation minimum (Tier C)**: ≥28 citations

**Section emphasis**: Section 2 (Mechanisms) must include at least one worked cascade failure scenario — a specific starting condition (e.g., grid failure during Zone 5 January cold snap) traced through at least three dependent system failures, with specific community intervention points identified at each cascade step. This is the document's core practical value; a Mechanisms section without a worked example is insufficient.

**Scope hard boundary**: National or municipal infrastructure planning is background material only. Every intervention pathway must be achievable by a community of 20–500 people with access to Zone 5 materials and no special regulatory approval.

**Domain-specific structure note**: This domain has the highest cross-domain bridge requirement in practice (not in minimum count, which is three for all domains) because infrastructure interdependencies explicitly touch energy (Phase 5 microgrids), water, food, communications (Phase 5 information infrastructure), and governance. The Summary section should include a visual or tabular dependency map showing which Phase 5 and Phase 6 domains are implicated in each infrastructure failure cascade.

---

### Domain 63 — Ecosystem Restoration

**Source readiness**: 72–80% (richest source base; most publication-ready)

**Word target (Tier A/B)**: 8,000–10,000 words (full range; domain has sufficient literature)

**Word target (Tier C)**: 5,000–6,500 words (upper Tier C range; stronger source base supports fuller treatment)

**Citation minimum (Tier A/B)**: ≥40 citations

**Citation minimum (Tier C)**: ≥25 citations

**Section emphasis**: Section 5 (Implementation) is this domain's core deliverable — specific zone-appropriate protocols for soil restoration, water management, and native plant establishment. "Specific" means: named cover crop species, seed mix formulations, application rates, timeline benchmarks, equipment requirements for hand-tool scale operations. General descriptions of regenerative agriculture principles belong in Sections 1 and 2; Section 5 must deliver actionable protocols.

**Scope hard boundary**: Academic ecological theory belongs in background citations only. Climate change policy and advocacy are out of scope. Every intervention must be implementable on a specific piece of land in Zones 4b–6a within the next growing season.

**Domain-specific structure note**: This domain's success metrics are unusually strong — soil organic matter percentage, infiltration rate (inches/hour), earthworm count per square foot, native plant species count per acre. Section 7 (Summary) should include a specific monitoring protocol with measurement methods and target timelines (12 months, 3 years, 7 years) so communities can assess progress without professional soil scientists.

---

### Domain 64 — Community Economic Resilience

**Source readiness**: 75–82% (second-richest; strong theoretical foundation, weaker rural US implementation base)

**Word target (Tier A/B)**: 8,000–10,000 words

**Word target (Tier C)**: 4,500–6,000 words

**Citation minimum (Tier A/B)**: ≥40 citations

**Citation minimum (Tier C)**: ≥25 citations

**Section emphasis**: Section 4 (Pathways) must distinguish between at least three complementary economic mechanisms (timebanking, mutual credit, local commodity currency, barter network) and provide decision logic for which mechanism fits which community context. A document that treats "alternative economics" as a single approach has not differentiated adequately. Each mechanism must have its own Zone 5 implementation pathway with specific startup steps.

**Scope hard boundary**: National macroeconomic policy, cryptocurrency/blockchain advocacy, and Silicon Valley fintech applications are out of scope. Every mechanism must be operable without internet access and without specialized technical knowledge.

**Domain-specific structure note**: Section 6 (International Precedent) must include at least one agricultural community precedent (not only urban LETS or city-based local currencies). The Zone 5 audience is predominantly rural and agricultural; an international precedent section that only documents urban currency systems will not transfer. Palekastro community currencies, Andean potato barter networks, and Irish Land League rent strikes are examples of agricultural-context precedents.

---

### Domain 65 — Institutional Learning and Governance Scaling

**Source readiness**: 65–73%

**Word target (Tier A/B)**: 7,500–9,500 words

**Word target (Tier C)**: 4,500–5,500 words

**Citation minimum (Tier A/B)**: ≥40 citations

**Citation minimum (Tier C)**: ≥25 citations

**Section emphasis**: Section 5 (Implementation) must include at least one worked governance failure case study — a specific community or cooperative where governance structures failed as the organization grew, with diagnosis of the failure mechanism and identified structural intervention. The academic governance literature documents successful models; this document's distinctive contribution is failure documentation and the design principles that emerge from it.

**Scope hard boundary**: National or state government institutional reform is out of scope. The entire document must remain within the scale range of 20–500 person voluntary communities, cooperatives, and watershed councils.

**Domain-specific structure note**: This domain's Section 4 (Pathways) should include a scale threshold table — specific governance adaptations that communities should make at the 25-person, 50-person, 150-person (Dunbar threshold), and 500-person marks. These thresholds are supported by empirical governance research (Dunbar, Ostrom, Mansbridge) and give practitioners a clear sequence. Without this table, the pathways section is too abstract to be actionable.

## Part 3: Publication-Ready Checklist (8 Objective Go/No-Go Items)

This checklist is the final gate before a document enters peer review (initial submission) and again before publication (final approval). All 8 items must be YES for publication-ready status. Any NO is a hold — not a no-go on the document, but a hold until that item is resolved.

**How to use this checklist**: The project lead runs this checklist on the submitted draft before assigning it to peer review. Authors run it as a self-check before submission. After peer review revisions, the checklist runs again. Publication does not proceed until all 8 items are YES.

---

**Item 1 — Word Count Within Range**

YES: Document word count falls within the per-domain range for the author's tier (see Part 2). Measure with `wc -w [filename]` — within 2% of target range is acceptable (word count tools differ slightly from manual counts).

NO: Document is more than 2% below the minimum floor OR more than 25% above the maximum ceiling. Below floor: insufficient depth. Above ceiling: scope creep — must cut before peer review.

---

**Item 2 — Citation Count and Quality**

YES: Document has ≥40 citations (Tier A/B) or ≥25 citations (Tier C). At least 30% are peer-reviewed or formally published. At least 50% have accessible URLs. No more than 20% from any single source organization. All citations have verifiable references.

NO: Any of the citation quality conditions above are not met. Count using a grep on citation formatting OR manual count of footnotes/inline references. Check 5 random citations for URL accessibility.

---

**Item 3 — Zero Placeholder Markers**

YES: A full-document scan finds zero instances of `[TODO]`, `[TBD]`, `[PLACEHOLDER]`, `[FILL]`, `[NEEDS]`, `[IN PROGRESS]`, `[CITATION NEEDED]`, or `FILL_IN`. Run: `grep -in "TODO\|TBD\|PLACEHOLDER\|FILL\|NEEDS.*citation\|IN PROGRESS" [filename]`. Expected output: no results.

NO: Any placeholder marker found. The document is a draft, not a publication candidate. Return to author.

---

**Item 4 — Section Structure Complete**

YES: Document contains all required sections for the author's tier (see Part 1, Section 1.3). Each required section has a distinct H2 heading, is identifiable as that section by its function, and meets the minimum word count for that section.

NO: Any required section is absent, has fewer than 60% of its minimum word count, or is present as a heading with no substantive content beneath it.

---

**Item 5 — Every Claim Has at Least One Citation**

YES: Spot-check three consecutive paragraphs from each of three different sections. In each paragraph examined, every factual claim has an associated citation. Citation may be inline `[Author, Year]` or footnote or URL. "Every claim" means: if a paragraph says "timebanks work better in communities with existing social trust," that statement requires a citation.

NO: Two or more uncited factual claims found in the spot-check. Return to author with the specific paragraphs flagged.

This item is always a spot-check (not a full document scan) at submission — a full citation audit happens during peer review (see WAVE_2_PEER_REVIEW_PAIRING_PROTOCOL.md, Item 6 on the peer review checklist).

---

**Item 6 — At Least One Implementation Pathway Is Specific and Reproducible**

YES: The document contains at least one implementation pathway (anywhere in the text) that includes: (a) named starting condition, (b) numbered or sequenced steps, (c) resource requirements at each step (labor hours, materials, tools, or cost estimate), (d) at least three measurable success metrics.

NO: All implementation content is descriptive ("communities can...") without specific steps, resources, or metrics. No pathway can be followed by a community coordinator reading the document alone.

---

**Item 7 — At Least Three Cross-Domain Bridges Documented**

YES: The document names at least three other Phase 5 or Phase 6 domains by name and explains the connection with sufficient specificity that a coordinator could act on it. At least one bridge points to a Phase 5 domain. All three bridges appear in or are consolidated in the Summary section.

NO: Fewer than three bridges found, OR bridges are present but too vague ("connects to governance issues"). OR no Phase 5 bridge exists.

---

**Item 8 — Internal Cross-Links Verified and No Broken References**

YES: Every internal reference ("see Section 4") resolves to a section that exists. Every external URL cited in the text returns HTTP 200 or valid redirect when tested. A 20% sample of URLs must be manually tested (same standard as Phase 5, per PHASE_5_PUBLICATION_READINESS_CHECKLIST.md Section 4.2). Overall working rate must be ≥87% (Phase 5 baseline was 90%; Wave 2 allows a 3-point tolerance for newer sources in less-indexed domains).

NO: Any internal cross-reference that cannot be resolved. OR URL working rate below 87% on the 20% sample. Broken URLs must be replaced or cited bibliographically before publication.

---

**Publication-Ready Status: All 8 Items YES**

| Item | Check | Result |
|------|-------|--------|
| 1 — Word count within range | `wc -w` | YES / NO |
| 2 — Citation count and quality | Manual count + URL spot-check | YES / NO |
| 3 — Zero placeholder markers | `grep -in "TODO\|TBD..."` | YES / NO |
| 4 — Section structure complete | Manual review | YES / NO |
| 5 — Claims cited (spot-check) | Manual spot-check | YES / NO |
| 6 — Implementation pathway specific | Manual review | YES / NO |
| 7 — Three cross-domain bridges | Manual review | YES / NO |
| 8 — Internal links and URLs verified | Sample URL test | YES / NO |
| **Overall** | All 8 YES = GO | GO / HOLD |

## Part 4: Worked Examples — Domain 60 Progression

These three examples illustrate the progression from weak draft to publication-ready for Domain 60 (International Coordination Frameworks). Domain 60 is the most challenging domain for scope and source work, which makes it the most useful calibration example.

### Example A: Outline Stage (Not Yet a Draft — Do Not Submit to Peer Review)

The following is a representative outline that a newly onboarded Tier B author might produce by T+3 (June 23, three days post-kickoff):

```
# International Coordination Frameworks for Zone 5 Communities

## 1. Introduction
- Why international coordination matters for US communities
- History of international cooperation

## 2. International Organizations
- United Nations structure
- World Trade Organization
- International Monetary Fund

## 3. NGO Coordination
- How NGOs work internationally
- Examples

## 4. Case Studies
- Europe
- Latin America
- Africa

## 5. Implementation
- Steps communities can take

## 6. Conclusion
```

**What is wrong with this outline (and the course correction to give the author by June 23):**

- Sections 2 and 3 operate at nation-state and intergovernmental scale — entirely out of scope. The UN, WTO, and IMF are not usable by Zone 5 communities directly.
- No Mechanisms section that explains how community-to-international connection actually works (the legal and practical pathways).
- "Case Studies" with continent-level tags has no Zone 5 lens applied — Europe is not a case study.
- "Steps communities can take" in Section 5 is a placeholder title, not a section plan.
- No cross-domain bridges present.

**Course correction message for author**: "Your outline has a scope problem — Sections 2 and 3 describe international institutions that Zone 5 communities cannot access directly. Please revise so that the entire document focuses on pathways that a community coordinator can use without going through the US federal government. Sections to add: (a) legal frameworks for community-to-community international connection (not intergovernmental), (b) networks that are specifically designed for sub-national community participation (La Via Campesina, GRAIN, food sovereignty networks), (c) diaspora mutual aid as an already-functioning international coordination mechanism. The UN and WTO belong in one sentence of background, not two sections."

## Part 5: Scope Creep Detection

These red flags are observable at the checklist stage. If any red flag is present, do not proceed to peer review — resolve the scope issue first.

**Red Flag 1 — Word Count 25%+ Over Target Ceiling**

If a document is more than 25% over the per-domain ceiling (e.g., Domain 60 ceiling is 9,000 words; red flag triggers at 11,250+ words), the author has scope-creeped. At this word count, the document is almost certainly covering material that belongs in a different domain or in a separately commissioned appendix. Resolution: author and project lead scope review call; identify which sections are out-of-scope; cut or defer.

**Red Flag 2 — Citation Count Over 100**

A document with more than 100 citations is a literature review, not a practitioner guide. The depth of coverage required to cite 100+ sources cannot be synthesized into a 6,000–10,000 word document — something has gone wrong with the author's understanding of the synthesis task. Resolution: citation audit — identify which citations are doing real argumentative work and which are decorative; cut citations that are not directly supporting a claim in the text.

**Red Flag 3 — Eight or More Major Sections**

The standard structure has six to seven sections. Eight or more major sections (H2 headings) indicates the author is trying to cover too many topics at depth. This is a domain split signal — the content may be sufficient for two documents, not one. Resolution: project lead reviews section list; identifies which two or three sections constitute a coherent standalone document; the remaining sections become a second document or are deferred.

**Red Flag 4 — Author Self-Rate Below 70%**

Described in Part 1, Section 1.5. Below 70% at any point signals foundational uncertainty — the author is not confident the content is correct. This is different from perfectionism (70–84%): below 70% means the author has identified specific areas where they are uncertain whether their claims are accurate. Resolution: project lead scope call; may involve providing additional source support, narrowing scope to what the author is confident about, or bringing in a domain expert as second author.
