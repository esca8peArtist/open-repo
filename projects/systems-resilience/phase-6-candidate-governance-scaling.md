# Phase 6 Candidate: Governance Scaling + Institutional Adaptation

## What This Is

**Governance Scaling + Institutional Adaptation** focuses on how governance structures that work at one scale (household, village, region) can scale to coordinate multiple communities, and how institutions actually learn from crises to prevent repeated failure modes. This domain asks: how does a community cluster (150–500 people across 3–5 villages) make decisions together, and how do those decision-making institutions adapt when they fail?

This is the **decision-making operating system** of resilience — the answer to "how do multiple communities governed themselves jointly, and how do they fix their own governance when it breaks?"

---

## Research Scope

This domain would research and answer three core questions:

1. **What governance structures allow multiple communities to coordinate decisions without a central authority?**
   - Federation models: how communities maintain autonomy while coordinating on shared challenges (energy, water, supply)
   - Consensus and deliberation protocols that work at 500+ person scale
   - Inter-community conflict resolution and dispute mechanisms
   - Information flow and communication cascades across federations
   - Case studies: Swiss cantons, Indigenous governance networks, UNDRR regional coordination, disaster recovery governance in Haiti and Christchurch

2. **How do institutions learn from failures and adapt governance to prevent the same failures from recurring?**
   - Crisis learning cycles: post-crisis review, institutional change, implementation, re-testing
   - Adaptive management: how to change procedures when conditions change (drought, new technology, new population)
   - Institutional memory: how communities document lessons and transfer them to new leaders
   - Feedback loops and early warning systems: how do communities know when they're about to repeat a failure?
   - Failure mode analysis for governance: what governance structures fail under what conditions?

3. **What incentive structures and accountability mechanisms keep scaled governance honest?**
   - Checks and balances across federation layers
   - Transparency and information access in distributed governance
   - Resource allocation mechanisms that prevent predation
   - Term limits, rotation, and leadership accountability
   - Midwest case studies: county governance, agricultural cooperative federations, regional water authorities

## Why This One

### Unique Leverage

**Governance Failure Prevention**: Phase 3 addressed community governance at 25–150 person scale. This domain extends it to 150–500+ person clusters and explicitly addresses institutional failure modes. It answers "what goes wrong when you scale governance, and how do you fix it?"

**Learning Loop Closure**: Most resilience work documents *what to do*. This domain documents *how to change what you do when it doesn't work*. It's the institutional equivalent of reflexion and self-correction.

**Micro-Governance Frontier**: There's extensive literature on national and international governance. Very little rigorous research on how 3–5 federated communities actually make decisions together in practice (as opposed to theory).

### Complementary to Existing Work

- Phase 3 documented governance *structure* (councils, roles, decision processes). This domain documents *failure modes* of those structures and *adaptation mechanisms* when they break.
- Phase 5 covered conflict resolution and psychological support. This domain extends it to *institutional learning* from conflicts (what changes as a result of the conflict-resolution process?).
- Phase 4 covered power mapping. This domain extends it to *power accountability* (how does governance prevent power concentration over time?).

### Market Relevance

The Midwest has extensive experience with cooperative governance (agricultural cooperatives, rural water authorities, soil conservation districts). There's also growing interest in resilience at the county/multi-town level. This domain has clear audience and practical applicability.
