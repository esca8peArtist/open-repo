## Sample Domain 1: Technology Repair (15–21 hours research)

### Topic Scope
**The Problem**: Phase 1-3 documents assume equipment works (solar panels, hand pumps, well systems, water treatment, radios, generators). In a 2+ year disruption, equipment fails. Modern supply chains cannot replace parts. Communities need repair skills, documentation, and spare parts strategies.

**The Solution**: This document teaches equipment diagnosis, common failure modes, repair procedures (with step-by-step photos/diagrams), and preventative maintenance schedules for critical Phase 1-3 equipment.

**Zone 5 Midwest Context**: Winter freeze damage (well pipes, solar thermal), lightning strikes, icing of surface water intake systems; seasonal maintenance windows (spring post-thaw, fall pre-freeze).

---

### Research Outline Skeleton

#### **Section 1: Equipment Inventory & Failure Modes** (1.5 hours research)
*What to research*:
- Phase 1-3 equipment list (from all prior documents)
- Failure mode for each: solar panels (microcracks, junction box failure, inverter degradation), hand pumps (leather seal failure, corrosion, frozen pipes), well systems (pump failure, submersible motor burnout), water treatment (filter saturation, UV bulb failure), radios (dead batteries, antenna corrosion)
- Typical failure rate/timeline for Midwest climate

*Sources to prioritize*:
- iFixit repair manuals (publicly available online)
- Manufacturer repair guides (often available in PDF on manufacturer websites)
- NREL solar panel failure analysis (public research)
- USGS groundwater well standard (public, Zone 5 specific)
- Homesteading/permaculture forums (real-world experience data: r/homesteading, homesteading blogs)

*Deliverable*: Table: Equipment type → Primary failure modes → Timeline to failure → Midwest climate factors

#### **Section 2: Preventative Maintenance Schedule** (1 hour research)
*What to research*:
- Manufacturer recommended maintenance (solar panels, pumps, etc.)
- Midwest-specific maintenance windows (freeze precaution Sept–Oct, post-thaw April–May)
- No-replacement maintenance (seals, lubrication, cleaning) vs replacement parts (fuses, filters, batteries)

*Sources to prioritize*:
- Manufacturer maintenance manuals
- Homesteading year-round maintenance calendars (e.g., backwoods home magazine archives)
- Extension service recommendations (Iowa State University Extension & Outreach for climate-specific guidance)

*Deliverable*: Month-by-month checklist: what to inspect, clean, lubricate, test, replace

#### **Section 3: Repair Skills & Training** (2 hours research)
*What to research*:
- Electrical safety (which repairs are safe for non-electrician? Where to draw the line?)
- Mechanical repair (hand pump disassembly, bearing replacement, seal inspection)
- Plumbing repair (PVC fitting replacement, isolation valve maintenance, freeze-protection procedures)
- Diagnosis procedures (how to test if a solar inverter is actually dead, or just needs a reset)
- Tools required per repair type (proprietary vs standard tools)

*Sources to prioritize*:
- Professional repair manuals (often free in PDF: Shurflo pump manuals, Grundfos pump manuals, SMA inverter manuals)
- YouTube channels specializing in equipment repair (channels for off-grid living, homesteading, solar)
- Community college extension programs (community solar workshops, pump repair classes)
- NFPA electrical safety standards (safe vs unsafe repair attempts)

*Deliverable*: Repair procedures for 8–10 highest-impact equipment; includes: required tools, safety precautions, step-by-step with photos/diagrams, common errors, when to give up and replace

#### **Section 4: Spare Parts Strategy** (1.5 hours research)
*What to research*:
- Critical vs replaceable parts (e.g., solar panel replacement very hard; inverter capacitor replacement moderate; well pump seal replacement easy)
- Minimum spare parts list (what ONE person can stockpile to keep essential systems running 2+ years)
- Stockpiling location (climate-controlled, protected from freeze/corrosion)
- Shelf life of spare parts (capacitors degrade; rubber seals harden in cold; electronics can be stored decades)
- Cost-benefit of spare parts vs repair-skills-only strategy

*Sources to prioritize*:
- Off-grid living cost-of-ownership studies
- Homesteading supply reviews (which spare parts actually break first in practice)
- Manufacturer recommendations for spare parts kits (if they offer them)
- Electrical/mechanical supply catalogs (identify which parts are commodity vs single-source)

*Deliverable*: Spare parts inventory: equipment type → critical parts → quantity to stockpile → cost per unit → shelf life notes

#### **Section 5: Zone 5 Midwest Specific Repair Scenarios** (1.5 hours research)
*What to research*:
- Freeze damage repair (burst pipes, ice-clogged intake, solar thermal system drainage)
- Lightning strike aftermath (surge damage to electronics, well pump failure, radio damage)
- Seasonal transition failures (summer dust clogging solar panels, spring melt flowing into intake, fall freeze-up procedures)
- Real case studies from Midwest off-grid or homesteading communities

*Sources to prioritize*:
- Homesteading blogs from Iowa/Illinois/Wisconsin (real experience data)
- Extension service weather/climate guides
- Renewable energy systems installed in Midwest (user reviews, failure reports)
- Community off-grid networks (Facebook groups, local meetups)

*Deliverable*: 3–4 detailed case studies: what went wrong, how to diagnose, repair procedures, prevention for next year

#### **Section 6: Knowledge Preservation** (1 hour research)
*What to research*:
- How to print repair manuals (file formats, ink durability, binding methods)
- Offline storage of troubleshooting guides (laminated checklists, waterproof formats)
- Training documentation for the person who learns repair skills to teach others
- Video/photo capture for documenting procedures (what format survives 20 years?)

*Sources to prioritize*:
- Archival printing standards (acid-free paper, pigment ink, binding durability)
- Kiwix/Wikipedia offline archives (iFixit often in offline archives)
- Homesteading knowledge preservation projects
- DIY video documentation best practices

*Deliverable*: Knowledge preservation plan: which manuals to print, how to store, how to teach others

  - Document 1: Maintenance schedules + equipment inventory + diagnosis procedures (8,000 words)

## Sample Domain 2: Agricultural Intensification (15–22 hours research)

### Topic Scope
**The Problem**: Phase 1 agriculture covers a 400 sq ft garden (individual). Phase 2 covers household coordination of production. Phase 3 addresses community food security strategies. Phase 4 addresses long-term food production without external inputs: seed saving, perennial food systems, community-scale production (50–150 people), soil regeneration over decades.

**The Solution**: This document teaches community seed-saving networks, perennial polyculture design, soil carbon building, and 5–10 year food system management.

**Zone 5 Midwest Context**: Frost dates (May 15 last frost, Sept 15 first frost = 120 day season); major crops (corn heritage varieties, soybeans, beans, squash, potatoes); perennials (apple/pear/plum, chestnut, elderberry, asparagus); seed saving (drying/humidity conditions specific to Midwest).

---

### Research Outline Skeleton

#### **Section 1: Community Seed-Saving Networks** (2 hours research)
*What to research*:
- Heritage crop varieties suited to Zone 5 Midwest (open-pollinated corn, dry beans, winter squash, potatoes)
- Isolation distances for seed purity (corn requires 600 ft separation, beans 20 ft, squash 1000 ft for different varieties)
- Seed storage conditions (humidity, temperature, pest protection, lifespan by crop)
- Seed-saving documentation protocols (record-keeping for F1/F2/variety stability)
- Existing seed-saving networks in Midwest (Seed Savers Exchange, Native Seeds/SEARCH, local groups)

*Sources to prioritize*:
- Seed Savers Exchange Heritage Farm documentation (Decorah, Iowa — Zone 5)
- USDA heritage variety catalogs (publicly available)
- Fedco Seeds, Johnny's Seeds, Baker Creek Heritage Seeds (supplier notes on zone adaptation)
- Academic literature: corn breeding for short seasons, bean variety performance by zone
- Regional heritage crop data (University of Minnesota seed studies, University of Wisconsin studies)

*Deliverable*: Seed-saving handbook: 20 varieties selected for Zone 5, isolation requirements, storage protocols, documentation templates

#### **Section 2: Perennial Polyculture Design** (2.5 hours research)
*What to research*:
- Perennial food-bearing plants hardy to Zone 5 (apple, pear, plum, cherry, chestnut, elderberry, mulberry, asparagus, rhubarb, berry bushes)
- Polyculture design principles (spacing, soil requirements, nutrient cycling, guild relationships)
- Productivity per plant/per acre (yield data for food security planning: how many apple trees to feed 50 people?)
- Establishment timeline (5–10 years to full productivity for trees; 3 years for berry systems)
- Maintenance without external inputs (pruning, pest management, disease prevention using integrated pest management)

*Sources to prioritize*:
- Academic polyculture studies (Mollison permaculture, Toby Hemenway, Mark Shepard "New Forests" research)
- Fruit tree productivity data (USDA/Extension service publications by crop)
- Zone 5 perennial crop trials (U of M Agricultural Extension, U of WI)
- Chestnut breeding for Midwest (American Chestnut Foundation research; blight-resistant varieties)
- Permaculture design case studies (temperate climate, production-focused, not ornamental)

*Deliverable*: Polyculture garden design template: 1 acre layout for 50–100 people, 30-year food system, specific varieties, spacing, maintenance schedule

#### **Section 3: Soil Regeneration & Carbon Building** (2 hours research)
*What to research*:
- Soil organic matter building in Midwest soils (pre-contact prairie was 15–18% organic matter; agricultural land now 2–4%; target rebuild = 5–8%)
- Cover cropping systems for food production land (hairy vetch, rye, clover rotation compatible with food crops)
- Compost/manure management at scale (community composting for 50 people; feedstock available from households + gardens)
- Mycorrhizal relationships (soil biology, fungal networks, how to foster rather than destroy with tillage)
- Legume/nitrogen-fixing integration (alley cropping, clovers in food systems)

*Sources to prioritize*:
- Soil science literature (Kansas State soil health studies, Rodale Institute organic transition research)
- SARE (Sustainable Agriculture Research and Education) program case studies
- Indigenous Midwest agriculture (three sisters polyculture, prairie management by fire, pre-contact soil data)
- Compost science (C:N ratios, decomposition rates, temperature management)
- Academic soil biology (mycorrhizal networks, biochar, long-term organic matter trends)

*Deliverable*: Soil management plan: 10-year transition from depleted to regenerated soil, cover crop rotations, community composting system, monitoring protocol

#### **Section 4: Community-Scale Production Planning** (2 hours research)
*What to research*:
- Crop yield data for food security (calories/pound by crop; protein requirements per person; storage/preservation needs)
- Land requirements (how many acres to feed 50 people? 100? With what crop mix?)
- Seasonal labor requirements (planting, tending, harvest, preservation timeline)
- Crop diversity for nutritional balance (carbs, protein, fats, micronutrients)
- Integration with Phase 3 community infrastructure (distribution hubs, food preservation centers, labor coordination)

*Sources to prioritize*:
- Food security research (FAO nutrition data, caloric requirements by age/activity)
- Farming productivity data by region (USDA NASS data for Midwest)
- Organic vegetable yield studies (USDA, Extension service)
- Historical documentation (pre-industrial food production per capita, food system anthropology)
- Community-supported agriculture (CSA) research (who farms how much land for how many people?)

*Deliverable*: Production-planning toolkit: crop selection for 50-person community, land requirements, seasonal calendar, caloric adequacy analysis, preservation needs

#### **Section 5: Pest & Disease Management Without External Inputs** (1.5 hours research)
*What to research*:
- Integrated pest management (IPM) for common Midwest crops (apple maggot, Japanese beetle, squash vine borer, bean beetle, corn earworm)
- Non-chemical control methods (row covers, traps, habitat manipulation, trap crops, beneficial insects)
- Crop-specific disease prevention (apple scab, bean rust, powdery mildew, potato blight)
- Resistant varieties (disease-resistant apples, powdery-mildew-resistant melons, bean varieties with resistance)
- Biological controls available without modern input chains (parasitic wasps, ladybugs, Bt, neem oil from plants grown locally)

*Sources to prioritize*:
- Rodale Institute organic farming handbook
- University extension IPM guides (University of Minnesota, University of Illinois)
- Organic farming associations (MOSES, Practical Farmers)
- Academic entomology for organic systems
- Indigenous pest management practices (traditional knowledge)

*Deliverable*: Pest management manual: 10 most problematic crops × common pests/diseases → prevention + cultural control + acceptable intervention (with safety notes)

#### **Section 6: Zone 5 Specific Food System Stories** (1 hour research)
*What to research*:
- Case studies from Midwest: who is doing perennial food systems? What works, what fails?
- Historical food systems in Zone 5 (what did pre-industrial Midwest families grow/eat? Preservation methods?)
- Current Midwest food security projects (community gardens, food forests, land trusts experimenting with resilience)
- Seasonal calendar specifics (when does frost come? When is last harvest window? When to plant for storage?)

*Sources to prioritize*:
- Homesteading blogs from Midwest (real experience)
- Seed Savers Exchange (based in Decorah, Iowa; has real data on what grows)
- Extension service historical documentation
- Midwest farming community groups (Facebook, forums, real people doing this)
- Historical cookbooks + food preservation manuals from Midwest (what did people actually preserve?)

*Deliverable*: 3–4 case studies: "A 50-person food system in Iowa," "10-acre perennial polyculture in Illinois," etc.

  - Document 1: Seed-saving networks + polyculture design + soil regeneration (9,000 words)

## Sample Domain 3: Governance Scaling (12–17 hours research)

### Topic Scope
**The Problem**: Phase 3 documents govern 150-person community (Dunbar's number). Phase 4 governance addresses scaling to 500+ people without re-centralizing authority or losing responsiveness. Methods: delegate councils, federalism with subsidiarity, inter-municipal coordination.

**The Solution**: This document provides governance templates (charters, council structures, decision procedures, conflict resolution) based on international precedent (Zapatista committees, Rojava federalism, Mondragon cooperatives, Swiss cantons) adapted for Midwest rural context.

**Zone 5 Midwest Context**: Existing township/county governance structures; land grant institutions (universities with agricultural extension); pre-existing cooperatives (dairy co-ops, grain co-ops); farmland tenure patterns (individual ownership, leases, trusts).

---

### Research Outline Skeleton

#### **Section 1: Delegate Council Models** (2 hours research)
*What to research*:
- Dunbar's number (150 person threshold) and governance scaling research
- Delegate councils: elected representatives from subgroups, rotating roles, consensus/supermajority decision procedures
- Precedent: Zapatista autonomous municipalities (rotational leadership, mandatory recall, community assembly), Rojava cantons (women's councils, minority councils, consensus), Mondragon cooperatives (general assembly, elected council, recall procedures)
- Midwest applicability: existing county structures, township meetings, cooperative governance models
- Committee structure for 500+ people (food, security, water, energy, health, education subcommittees, each with 9–15 people)

*Sources to prioritize*:
- Debbie Bookchin / Rojava federalism literature
- Zapatista governance research (academic study of Chiapas autonomous communities)
- Mondragon cooperative governance manuals (publicly available)
- Swiss canton governance (academic comparison of federalism)
- American participatory democracy literature (deliberative democracy, community governance experiments)

*Deliverable*: Governance charter template: 500-person community, delegate council structure, assembly procedures, role descriptions, conflict resolution mechanism

#### **Section 2: Inter-Municipal Federation** (2.5 hours research)
*What to research*:
- Federation with subsidiarity (decisions made at lowest competent level; escalate only when necessary)
- Pre-existing Midwest governance: township meetings, county government, inter-municipal agreements (IMAs)
- Regional council structure (how do 3-5 neighboring communities coordinate without centralizing?)
- Decision authority at each level (what's decided by community? what by region? what by federation?)
- Communication infrastructure (how do councils communicate without modern telecommunications?)

*Sources to prioritize*:
- Inter-municipal agreement case studies (communities in Wisconsin, Minnesota, Illinois doing this)
- Federalism political theory (subsidiarity principle, federal structures)
- Historical confederations (New England colonial confederations, Iroquois Confederacy, Swiss cantons)
- Rural governance structures (Texas county commissioners, Minnesota township government models)
- Consensus and deliberative decision-making procedures (Quaker meetings, consensus-based decision-making)

*Deliverable*: Federation template: 3–5 communities (each 150 person scale) + regional council, decision authority matrix, communication protocol, escalation procedures

#### **Section 3: Conflict Resolution & Justice Systems** (2 hours research)
*What to research*:
- Restorative justice (moving beyond punishment toward accountability + repair)
- Community accountability for serious crimes (how do you handle a murderer in a 500-person community that can't incarcerate?)
- Mediation and conflict resolution procedures
- Property dispute resolution (land, resources, water rights)
- Precedent: Indigenous dispute resolution, Rwandan gacaca courts, Restorative Justice Innovation programs

*Sources to prioritize*:
- Restorative justice literature (Howard Zehr, TJ Thorne, academic studies)
- Indigenous justice systems (Navajo restorative justice, Australian Aboriginal law, tribal sovereignty)
- Conflict resolution training materials (mediation procedures, communication protocols)
- Historical frontier justice (how did pre-modern communities handle serious crime?)
- Intentional communities + co-housing conflict resolution case studies

*Deliverable*: Conflict resolution manual: procedures for mediation, community accountability, serious crime protocols, justice principles adapted to decentralized context

#### **Section 4: Rationing & Resource Allocation** (2 hours research)
*What to research*:
- Fair allocation mechanisms (consensus, lottery, contribution-based shares, need-based distribution)
- Precedent: WWII rationing systems, kibbutz resource allocation, commons management
- Food rationing in scarcity (how to distribute limited harvest fairly?)
- Labor equity (if work is mandatory, how to ensure distribution is fair? How to handle those who can't work?)
- Incentives and disincentives (how to motivate contribution without markets or compulsion?)

*Sources to prioritize*:
- Commons management theory (Elinor Ostrom, tragedy of the commons solutions)
- WWII rationing research (how rationing actually worked, how to administer it)
- Kibbutz resource allocation studies
- Anthropology of gift economies and distribution systems
- Intentional community resource allocation case studies

*Deliverable*: Rationing protocols: fair allocation procedures, rationing documentation, labor equity standards, precedent-based guidelines

#### **Section 5: Governance Meeting Procedures** (1.5 hours research)
*What to research*:
- Deliberative procedures (agenda setting, discussion protocols, decision rules)
- Consensus vs supermajority decision-making (when to use which? How to achieve both legitimacy and efficiency?)
- Meeting facilitation without professional facilitators (training + rotating facilitation)
- Documentation (how to record decisions, maintain records, share with larger community)
- Meeting frequency and duration (how often to gather? How long should meetings take?)

*Sources to prioritize*:
- Quaker meeting procedures (consensus-based decision-making methodology)
- Sociocracy (consent-based governance, rounds decision procedure)
- Robert's Rules of Order (traditional parliamentary procedure; often too formal but useful reference)
- Co-housing meeting research (how do small intentional communities actually run meetings?)
- Participatory budgeting procedures (practical method for community decision-making)

*Deliverable*: Meeting facilitation handbook: procedures for different meeting types (assembly, council, working group), facilitation training outline, documentation templates

#### **Section 6: Zone 5 Midwest Case Studies** (1.5 hours research)
*What to research*:
- Existing Midwest governance that could scale to crisis: township meetings, rural cooperatives, tribal government (where applicable)
- Historical Midwest governance: pre-contact Native governance, early settlement governance, county formation
- Current experiments: intentional communities in Midwest, cooperative experiments, local governance reforms
- Real stories: how did Midwest communities actually make decisions pre-modern? What worked?

*Sources to prioritize*:
- County history societies (township meeting records, governance documentation)
- Tribal government structures (if relevant to specific Zone 5 location)
- Cooperative governance cases (dairy co-ops, grain marketing associations, credit unions)
- Midwest community archaeology/history
- Oral history from long-term residents

*Deliverable*: Case studies: "Wisconsin township governance," "Minnesota Mondragon-style cooperative," "Tribal sovereignty models applicable to Midwest"
