#### What This Document Adds

The second document picks up where it leaves off: annual seed-saving governance for the community, production planning to feed 50–150 people, pest management without external inputs, and Zone 5 case studies grounding the abstract in real Midwest farms.

#### Section-by-Section Research Outline

**Section 1: Community Seed-Saving Networks** (3 hours research)

*Problem being addressed*: A community that cannot save its own seed is one supplier away from agricultural collapse. Industrial seed supply chains (Monsanto/Bayer, Pioneer, Syngenta) are not resilient. Open-pollinated and heirloom varieties saved within the community eliminate that dependency.

*What to research*:
- Isolation distances by crop type (corn: 600–1,200 ft between varieties; dry beans: 20–50 ft; squash: 1,000 ft; tomatoes: 25 ft self-pollinators)
- Seed viability storage lifespans by crop under Zone 5 conditions (cool, dry — beans 4–6 years; corn 1–3 years; squash 4–6 years; tomato 4–6 years; onion 1–2 years)
- Seed cleaning and processing without electricity: threshing, winnowing, fermentation (tomato), drying racks and timelines
- Heritage variety selection specifically adapted to Zone 5 Midwest: open-pollinated corn (Bloody Butcher, Hopi Blue, Reid's Yellow Dent), dry beans (Hidatsa Red, Jacob's Cattle, Good Mother Stallard), winter squash (Hubbard, Butternut, Delicata), potatoes (Kennebec, Butte, Yukon Gold)
- Community seed library governance: intake criteria (must be open-pollinated, must include growing notes), distribution protocols (borrow-and-return vs. gift-and-replace), record-keeping for variety lineage

*Sources to prioritize*:
- Seed Savers Exchange Heritage Farm (Decorah, Iowa — Zone 5, 25,000+ varieties in living collection): `seedsavers.org`
- Suzanne Ashworth, *Seed to Seed: Seed Saving and Growing Techniques for Vegetable Gardeners* (2nd ed., 2002) — the reference standard for isolation distances and processing
- Jere Gettle / Baker Creek Heirloom Seeds, regional variety data: `rareseeds.com`
- USDA GRIN (Germplasm Resources Information Network): grin.ars.usda.gov — publicly searchable heritage variety performance data by zone
- University of Minnesota Extension Zone 5 seed-saving guides: `extension.umn.edu`
- USDA Agricultural Research Service heritage variety trials for Upper Midwest
- Gary Paul Nabhan, *Enduring Seeds* — indigenous seed-saving governance models
- Native Seeds/SEARCH drought-resilient variety database (cross-applicable for short-season/stress conditions)

*Deliverable*: Seed-saving handbook section: 20 priority varieties for Zone 5 with isolation requirements, storage conditions, processing procedures, viability timelines, and governance charter template for a community seed library

**Section 2: Community-Scale Production Planning for 50–150 People** (3 hours research)

*Problem being addressed*: Phase 1 covers one person and 400 sq ft. Phase 2 covers household coordination. Phase 3 describes community food distribution. None of them bridge to a specific production plan: how many acres does a community of 75 people need, growing which crops, in what sequence, to reach caloric self-sufficiency?

*What to research*:
- Caloric requirements by demographic mix (2,000–2,500 kcal/day adults, 1,600–2,000 kcal/day children and elderly)
- Caloric density per acre by crop (potatoes: 4–7 million kcal/acre; dry beans: 1.4–1.8 million kcal/acre; winter squash: 800K–1.2M kcal/acre; corn: 3–5 million kcal/acre depending on yield)
- Land requirements for 75-person community at caloric sufficiency (working estimate: 5–8 acres of intensive mixed vegetables and grain for primary calories; 2–3 acres orchard and perennial for micronutrients and caloric supplement)
- Seasonal labor requirements: spring planting (high intensity, 2–3 weeks), summer maintenance (low intensity), fall harvest (high intensity, 4–6 weeks), winter processing (moderate)
- Crop rotation for Zone 5: 4-year rotation model (corn → beans → squash → cover crop) to maintain soil fertility without synthetic inputs
- Integration with Phase 4b perennial food forest: perennials provide caloric supplement and biodiversity stability while annuals carry the caloric baseline

*Sources to prioritize*:
- John Jeavons, *How to Grow More Vegetables* (10th ed., 2017) — the foundational biointensive production data source; includes caloric yield per bed-foot by crop
- Eliot Coleman, *Four-Season Harvest* and *The New Organic Grower* — Northeast/Zone 5 applicable production data
- USDA NASS Midwest crop yield data (official statistics, publicly available at nass.usda.gov)
- FAO Food Energy methods paper — nutritional requirements methodology
- CSA sizing research: how many acres do working CSAs farm per member? (Range: 0.05–0.10 acres/share in intensive production systems; implies 4–8 acres for 75 people)
- SARE (Sustainable Agriculture Research & Education) community food production case studies
- Practical Farmers of Iowa production data for Zone 5 crops

*Deliverable*: Production planning toolkit — crop selection table (15 priority crops), caloric yield per acre, land requirement calculation for 50/75/100/150 person communities, seasonal labor calendar, 4-year crop rotation schedule

**Section 3: Pest and Disease Management Without External Inputs** (2 hours research)

*What to research*:

- Biological controls: what can a community maintain without supply chain access? (Beneficial insect habitat, natural predator populations, Bt — Bacillus thuringiensis — which can be cultured or purchased in bulk and stored)
- Resistant varieties as primary defense: disease-resistant apple varieties (Liberty, Enterprise, Goldrush), blight-resistant potato varieties (Elba, Rosa), powdery mildew-resistant squash
- Fermented plant preparations (nettle tea, compost tea, horsetail silica spray) — Biodynamic and OMRI-listed inputs that can be produced on-site

*Sources to prioritize*:
- Rodale Institute *Organic Farming Guide* and IPM resources
- University of Minnesota Extension IPM publications (free online, Zone 5 specific): `extension.umn.edu/pest-management`
- ATTRA National Sustainable Agriculture Information Service: `attra.ncat.org` — free practical guides for organic IPM
- MOSES (Midwest Organic and Sustainable Education Service) IPM materials
- Cornell Cooperative Extension's organic apple production IPM guide (directly applicable to Zone 5 orchard management)
- Eliot Coleman's *Four-Season Harvest* section on pest-resistant variety selection

*Deliverable*: Pest management reference table — 10 crops × common pests/diseases → prevention measures → cultural control → biological control → last-resort intervention; variety selection guidance for disease resistance

#### Section-by-Section Research Outline

**Section 1: Delegate Council Models and Charter Templates** (3 hours research)

The core structural gap in Phase 3: Phase 3 governance documents cover the 150-person direct assembly well. What is missing is the structural shift when a community grows to 250, 500, or 1,000 people — when everyone cannot be in the same room, and when informal consensus fails because the group is too large for everyone to know everyone.

*What to research*:
- Dunbar's cognitive thresholds and their governance implications: 150-person direct democracy ceiling, 500-person working-group limit, 2,000-person representative-government threshold
- Delegate council structure: how are delegates selected? Rotation vs. election? Recall mechanisms? Term limits? Quorum requirements?
- Case studies: Zapatista Caracales (autonomous municipalities with rotating juntas de buen gobierno, mandatory recall, gender parity); Rojava TEV-DEM structure (commune councils, district councils, cantonal assembly, with women's councils at each level); Swiss Landsgemeinde (direct-democracy assembly for cantons up to ~40,000)
- Practical charter template elements: delegate selection, term length, decision authority (what does the council decide vs. the assembly?), escalation procedures (when does council refer back to full assembly?), accountability mechanisms (how is a delegate recalled?)
- Midwest applicability: township meeting structure in Minnesota, Wisconsin, Illinois as the legal analog; cooperative governance structures at dairy co-ops and grain marketing cooperatives as the institutional precedent

*Sources to prioritize*:
- Debbie Bookchin, "The Future We Deserve" and interviews on Rojava's federated governance structure
- Raul Zibechi, *Territories in Resistance* — Zapatista governance analysis
- Mondragon Cooperative Corporation bylaws (publicly available) — governance architecture for 80,000-member cooperative federation
- Michael Hardt and Antonio Negri, *Assembly* — theoretical framework for constituent governance
- Minnesota Township Officers Association governance guides (real Midwest legal structure)
- National Center for Employee Ownership (NCEO) cooperative governance database: `nceo.org`

*Deliverable*: Delegate council charter template (2–3 pages, fillable) covering: delegate selection procedure, term length and recall mechanism, meeting cadence, decision authority matrix, escalation procedures, accountability mechanisms

**Section 2: Inter-Municipal Federation with Subsidiarity** (3 hours research)

*What to research*:
- Subsidiarity principle: decisions are made at the lowest competent level; only escalate when the decision affects multiple communities or requires coordination across boundaries
- Inter-Municipal Agreements (IMAs): the existing Midwest legal mechanism for inter-community coordination (road maintenance, utility sharing, emergency response coordination)
- Federation design for 3–5 communities: what is decided locally vs. regionally? What triggers escalation? How are inter-community disputes resolved?
- Resource sharing protocols: how does food, labor, and equipment flow between communities during crisis? What precedents exist?
- Communication infrastructure for federation: how do councils communicate without modern telecommunications? (Documented precedents from pre-radio rural governance, and from existing GMRS/AREDN mesh extensions)

*Sources to prioritize*:
- Elinor Ostrom, *Governing the Commons* — polycentric governance principles; Ostrom's Design Principles for stable common-pool resource governance directly applicable to inter-community federation
- Wisconsin Municipal Attorneys Association IMA templates: `wmaa.com`
- Minnesota Association of Townships governance resources: `mntownships.org`
- National League of Cities inter-municipal cooperation resources
- Iroquois Confederacy / Haudenosaunee governance model — historical precedent for federation of sovereign communities; applicable to Midwest Native Nations context
- Swiss Federal Council decision-authority matrix (subsidiarity in practice at national scale, scalable downward)

*Deliverable*: Federation template — decision authority matrix (3 tiers: community, regional council, federation), inter-community communication protocol, resource sharing agreement framework, dispute resolution escalation ladder

**Section 3: Conflict Resolution and Restorative Justice** (2 hours research)

*What to research*:
- Interest-based negotiation (Fisher and Ury, *Getting to Yes*) — the foundational framework for principled negotiation without positional bargaining
- Restorative circles (Dominic Barter's model) — structured dialogue process for repairing harm; used in school systems, community organizations, and explicitly in indigenous communities
- Community accountability processes for serious harm — how do communities that cannot incarcerate handle violence or chronic antisocial behavior? (Documented precedents: INCITE! community accountability model, Quaker clearness committees, indigenous talking circles)
- Mediation training for non-professionals — what is the minimum training a community mediator needs? (Community Mediation Centers typically offer 40-hour basic mediation training; condensed 8-hour essential skills version for community contexts)
- Midwest relevance: existing community dispute resolution programs in rural Midwest (extension service mediation programs; Iowa Peace Institute; Wisconsin Mediation Center)

*Sources to prioritize*:
- Roger Fisher and William Ury, *Getting to Yes* (3rd ed., 2011) — interest-based negotiation methodology
- Dominic Barter, Restorative Circles facilitation guides: `restorativecircles.org`
- Howard Zehr, *The Little Book of Restorative Justice* — accessible primer
- INCITE! Women of Color Against Violence, *The Revolution Will Not Be Funded* — community accountability models

*Deliverable*: Conflict resolution manual (community mediator reference): three-stage mediation process, restorative circle facilitation script, escalation criteria (when does mediation escalate to community assembly?), serious harm protocols

**Section 4: Rationing and Resource Allocation Under Scarcity** (2 hours research)

*What to research*:
- Documented rationing systems: WWII US rationing (OPA administration, ration books, exempt categories, black market suppression); Cuban Special Period rationing (libreta system, which broke down and what replaced it); Kibbutz resource allocation (contribution-based shares, need-based supplements, democratic override mechanisms)
- Allocation criteria — what makes a rationing system accepted as legitimate? (Equal shares vs. need-based vs. contribution-based; the empirical literature is clear that perceived process fairness predicts acceptance even when outcome is unfavorable)
- Labor equity: if community labor is required contribution, how are non-laborers (elderly, disabled, children, caregiving parents) treated? What has worked?
- Elinor Ostrom's design principles applied to food/fuel rationing: clear group boundaries, proportional cost-sharing, collective choice in rule-making, monitoring, graduated sanctions

*Sources to prioritize*:
- Elinor Ostrom, *Governing the Commons* — design principles for commons management (Nobel laureate; foundational for legitimate rationing systems)
- Amy Cohen, "Rationing and the Law" in *Yale Law Journal* — legal and normative analysis
- Wartime Rationing records (US National Archives, OPA administrative documentation)
- Orlando Sentinel investigation on Cuban Special Period food rationing: analysis of what failed and why
- Richard Wilkinson and Kate Pickett, *The Spirit Level* — equality of resource distribution and community cohesion outcomes

*Deliverable*: Rationing protocol manual — three-tier allocation model (survival baseline, need supplement, contribution reward), administrative procedure, monitoring and audit protocol, rule revision mechanism
