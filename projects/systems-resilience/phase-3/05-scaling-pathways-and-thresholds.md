# Scaling Pathways & Governance Transitions
> **Region**: Midwest US (Zone 5) | **Scale**: 100–10,000 people

## The Most Important Finding

Communities grow or consolidate in crisis. A neighborhood of 100 people absorbs refugees. Villages merge for safety or resource sharing. What worked as governance for 100 people fails catastrophically at 300 without deliberate redesign.

The transition points where governance structures must change are not arbitrary. They are determined by cognitive and social limits: the Dunbar threshold (~150 people, the cognitive limit for stable personal relationships), the working-group coordination threshold (~500 people, the limit for a single assembly meeting), and the federation scaling threshold (~2,000 people, where representative governance becomes necessary).

Communities that anticipate these transitions and plan structural changes before they occur maintain stability through growth. Communities that improvise governance during crisis at exactly the moment when social trust is most fragile experience governance failure, internal conflict, and often dissolution. This domain covers what deliberate transition management looks like at each scale threshold.

## The 100-Person Community: Direct Participation

At 100 people, direct participation is possible and optimal. Everyone knows everyone (or close to it). Social reputation is the primary accountability mechanism. Governance structures should be minimal and focused on coordinating specialists (water manager, medical lead, security coordinator) while keeping decision-making authority at the community level.

### Governance Structure

**Assembly-based decision-making:** Community assembly meets at regular intervals (weekly during crisis, monthly during stability). All community members are invited; attendance is voluntary but socially expected for major decisions. Simple majority vote decides most questions; 75% majority required for decisions affecting core resource allocations or constitutional changes.

**Domain specialists:** Identified experts have operational authority within their domain (water/sanitation, medical/health, food/supplies, security, communication, coordination). Specialists make day-to-day decisions within their domain; major decisions requiring community resources require assembly approval.

**Authority accountability:** Specialists are selected by the assembly and can be recalled by the assembly if they fail to fulfill their role. Selection is ideally based on competence and existing community relationships, not elected position (election introduces political competition; communities of 100 are too small to sustain healthy internal political competition).

**Responsibility coordination:** One community member serves as overall coordinator (some traditions call this "facilitation," "stewardship," or "elder"). This person's role is coordination only: calling meetings, tracking decisions, ensuring follow-through. They have no special authority on substantive questions and can be recalled like any other specialist.

### Infrastructure Requirements

1. **Regular communication:** Weekly assembly meetings with documented agendas and decisions (written notes, regardless of literacy level — notes can be oral and documented by designation of a scribe)
2. **Shared inventory:** Written or oral ledger of all major resources, updated daily or weekly
3. **Skill inventory:** Roster of who has what skills and experience (medical, technical, agricultural), so the community knows what capabilities it has
4. **Emergency protocols:** Written or memorized response procedures for fires, medical emergencies, security alerts, water system failures

### Critical Failures at 100 People

**Failure 1: Specialist accumulation of power.** One or more specialists begins making decisions without consulting assembly. Prevention: explicit assembly oversight, regular recall elections even if the person is performing well (prevents entrenchment), and transparent decision-making process.

**Failure 2: Suppression of conflict.** Community suppresses internal disagreements to maintain harmony, which causes resentment and underground factional tension that explodes during crisis. Prevention: establish explicit conflict resolution mechanisms (see Domain 1: Governance), designate neutral mediators, create space for disagreement without shaming.

**Failure 3: Loss of institutional memory.** The coordinator or key specialist leaves (death, injury, relocation), taking their knowledge with them. Prevention: all major decisions are documented, apprenticeships are established for key roles, and knowledge is explicitly transferred before the person leaves.

## The 500–1,000 Person Community: Federated Sub-Units

The 150-person threshold (Dunbar's number) arrives somewhere in the 300–500 range as communities grow. At this point, a single assembly becomes unmanageable, personal relationships cannot span the whole group, and informal trust-based accountability breaks down.

The transition is dangerous because it arrives under pressure: communities growing from refugee absorption or merger are under maximum stress at exactly the moment when governance systems must change. Communities that improvise through this transition intact are exceptions. The norm is governance crisis — conflicts over authority, resource hoarding by sub-groups, internal violence.

### The Federated Model

Rather than a single assembly, maintain sub-units of 100–150 people each, each with their own local governance and resource management. Coordination across sub-units happens through delegate councils — representatives from each sub-unit meet to make decisions affecting the entire community, then return to their sub-units to implement decisions and report back.

**Structure:**
- **Sub-unit assemblies:** Each 100–150 person cluster has local assembly meetings, local specialists, local decision-making authority
- **Delegate councils:** One or two representatives from each sub-unit meet (weekly during crisis, monthly during stability) to coordinate: external communication, mutual defense, inter-unit resource allocation, conflict resolution between units
- **Domain working groups:** Specialists from each sub-unit (water managers, medical coordinators, security leads) meet separately to coordinate their domain across all sub-units

**Key principle:** Sub-units retain authority over sub-unit-level decisions (daily life, local resource management, local conflict resolution). Domain-level and inter-unit decisions go to the delegate council.

### Documentation and Institutional Memory

At 500+ people, informal knowledge transfer is insufficient. Institutional memory must be explicit and documented:

1. **Meeting records:** All delegate council meetings are formally documented with attendees, agenda items, decisions made, implementation assignments, and timelines for follow-up
2. **Decision registry:** Searchable record of major decisions and the reasoning behind them (so future councils don't re-litigate settled questions)
3. **Role descriptions:** Explicit documentation of what each domain specialist role entails, competency requirements, and accountability mechanisms
4. **Process documentation:** Written procedures for emergency response, resource allocation, inter-unit conflicts, decision-making quorum requirements

The 2025 LA fires documentation showed that communities with explicit decision logs and role descriptions recovered faster and experienced fewer governance conflicts than communities relying on shared memory. [1]

### Common Failures at 500+ People

**Failure 1: Delegate capture.** Delegates begin making decisions independent of their sub-units' input, or use their position to exercise authority beyond their mandate. Prevention: short terms (3–6 months maximum), mandatory reporting back to sub-units after each council meeting, explicit recall mechanisms, rotation of delegates to prevent permanent emergence of a "leadership class."

**Failure 2: Inter-unit conflict escalation.** Disputes between sub-units are not resolved at the delegate level, leading to factional conflict at community level. Prevention: explicit conflict resolution mechanisms, neutral mediators selected jointly by both units, escalation protocols (try mediation → try elder intervention → try community-wide assembly if necessary).

**Failure 3: Unequal resource distribution.** Some sub-units monopolize resources or control distribution to other units. Prevention: clear allocation formulas (population-based, need-based, or contribution-based but explicitly decided in advance), regular audits of resource distribution, explicit escalation if allocation rules are violated.

**Failure 4: Information asymmetry.** Delegates have more information than other community members, creating power imbalance. Prevention: explicit requirement to share all relevant information, no confidential meetings (all delegate council meetings are open to observation), regular public information updates on major issues.

## The 1,000–2,000 Person Community: Regional Coordination

At 1,000+ people, the federated sub-unit model continues but requires additional coordination layers:

1. **Zone councils:** Multiple federated clusters (each with 500–1,000 people and their own delegate councils) each send representatives to a zone council that coordinates between clusters
2. **Information cascade:** Information flows bottom-up (sub-unit → delegate council → zone council) and top-down (zone council → delegate councils → sub-units)
3. **Infrastructure specialization:** At this scale, infrastructure becomes specialized (a single water treatment facility serves multiple sub-units, a single emergency medical center serves multiple clusters). Specialization creates dependencies; dependencies require explicit coordination agreements.

### Mondragon Model Application

The Mondragon cooperative network provides the most extensively documented model at this scale, operating continuously for 65+ years. Each individual cooperative (100–500 worker-members) retains full autonomy. The Cooperative Congress (assembly of representatives from all cooperatives) meets annually to set network-wide policy. Domain-specific councils (finance, education, labor) coordinate across cooperatives. [2]

The key success factor: **subsidiarity principle** — decisions are made at the lowest level capable of making them. A single cooperative decides internal questions independently. Only decisions affecting multiple cooperatives go to the Cooperative Congress.

### Critical Transitions at 1,000+ People

**Cultural coherence becomes a governance challenge.** At 1,000+ people, communities likely include multiple ethnic groups, religious traditions, and cultural norms around authority, gender roles, and conflict resolution. Governance structures designed for cultural homogeneity exclude meaningful minorities and create internal security threats.

**Solution:** Explicitly inclusive governance. Minority groups have guaranteed representation on delegate councils (not token representation; minimum proportional representation). Cultural differences in conflict resolution are acknowledged and accommodated where possible (some traditions use mediation; some use elder judgment; some use consensus-building). The governance system itself is periodically reviewed for potential exclusion of minority norms. [3]

### Resource Infrastructure Specialization

At 1,000+ people, centralized infrastructure (water treatment, grain milling, electrical generation) becomes more efficient than distributed infrastructure. But centralized infrastructure creates fragility: failure of the central system is a community-wide crisis.

**Resilience strategy:** Maintain redundancy at the infrastructure level. Two water treatment systems instead of one. Multiple grain mills. Backup electrical generation. The redundancy is inefficient in stable conditions but becomes the difference between community survival and community collapse if the primary system fails.

## The 10,000+ Person Community: Regional Scale

At 10,000+ people, direct democracy is impossible. Representative government becomes necessary. The Midwest does not have extensive case studies of 10,000-person autonomous communities under extended disruption, but the scale is achievable through federation of smaller units:

5 zones of 2,000 people each = 10,000 people
Each zone = 10 federated clusters of 200 people
Governance: cluster assemblies → cluster councils → zone councils → regional parliament

This model theoretically scales to arbitrary size through federation. The Zapatista structure scaled to approximately 300,000 people across 55 municipalities through exactly this nested federation approach. [4]

### Infrastructure at Regional Scale

Infrastructure dependencies become the primary governance issue:

1. **Regional transportation:** Roads connecting zones; vehicles and fuel for inter-zone movement
2. **Communications:** Radio networks (AREDN or HF) connecting all zones for coordination
3. **Food distribution:** Regional coordination of production and distribution; inter-zone trade or gifting
4. **Medical specialization:** Trauma centers, surgical capacity, pharmaceutical production at regional scale; distribution to zones
5. **Defense coordination:** Mutual defense agreements between zones; unified threat assessment and response

Each of these requires coordination mechanisms (committees, councils, working groups) and explicit decision-making procedures. The governance complexity is substantial. Communities attempting to govern at 10,000+ person scale should study existing models in detail before implementing.

## Transition Management: Planning Structural Changes in Advance

The most dangerous governance moment is the transition between scales. A community growing from 100 to 200 people or merging two 300-person communities needs a deliberate transition plan.

### Pre-Transition Planning (Before You Grow Past the Threshold)

1. **Anticipate the transition.** When you reach 120 people and still functioning, start planning for 200. When you reach 400 people, start planning for federation.

2. **Study models.** Read documented case studies of communities that have successfully transitioned. Identify which model fits your cultural context and constraints.

3. **Design structures explicitly.** Before crisis forces growth, design what the new governance will look like: How many sub-units? Who will be delegates? What decisions stay local vs. go to the council? What happens to the current community assembly — does it become a zone assembly or a neighborhood sub-unit assembly?

4. **Practice the new structure.** With the current (100-person) community, hold experimental delegate councils even though you don't need them yet. Simulate decisions. Identify friction points in the new structure and adjust before you depend on it.

### During Transition (Absorbing Growth)

1. **Explicit transition announcement.** The community leadership explicitly states: "We've reached 180 people. Our current assembly model will become insufficient at 250. We're transitioning to sub-units + delegate council structure starting [date]."

2. **Volunteer roles.** Asking for sub-unit coordinators and delegates is a call for volunteers, not a political election. People self-select into role; community assembly ratifies the selection.

3. **Explicit role descriptions.** The new roles (delegate, sub-unit coordinator, delegate council facilitator) have written descriptions of what they entail, what authority they hold, what accountability mechanisms constrain them.

4. **Transition period redundancy.** For 2–4 weeks during transition, both the old structure (full community assembly) and new structure (sub-unit + delegate councils) operate simultaneously. Old decisions are ratified by both bodies. This prevents discontinuity if someone forgets to do something in the new system.

5. **Explicit sunset of old structures.** When the transition period ends, the old governance model is formally sunset and will not operate anymore.

## Reference Table: Scaling Thresholds and Structural Requirements

| Population | Threshold | Governance Model | Critical Infrastructure | Most Common Failure Mode |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 50–100 | Small community | Direct assembly, minimal formal structure | Regular meetings, shared inventory | Loss of institutional memory |
| 100–150 | Dunbar's number | Assembly + domain specialists | Documentation, decision-making protocols | Specialist power accumulation |
| 150–300 | Transition zone | Either: (1) careful assembly management, or (2) early federation | Explicit facilitation, sub-group coordination | Governance confusion during transition |
| 300–500 | Federation trigger | Federated sub-units + delegate council | Delegate rotation, documented decisions, inter-unit conflict resolution | Delegate capture and factional conflict |
| 500–1,000 | Complex federation | Multiple 100–150 person clusters, domain working groups | Decision registry, institutional memory systems, information cascade | Information asymmetry and elite capture |

| 2,000–10,000 | Regional scale | Nested federation (theoretical, less tested) | Multiple decision-making levels, representative layers | Governance complexity and slow decision-making |

## The Cross-Domain Integration Point

Governance scaling directly impacts every other domain:

- **Food systems:** At 100 people, centralized storage works. At 500 people, distributed sub-unit storage with inter-unit coordination becomes necessary. At 1,000 people, regional food production and distribution requires explicit zone-level coordination.

- **Information infrastructure:** Communication systems scale with governance. GMRS works for 100 people. AREDN becomes necessary for 500+. HF becomes necessary for regional coordination at 1,000+.

- **Security:** Security coordination scales with governance. A single security coordinator works for 100 people. Sub-unit security volunteers with central coordination work for 500 people. Zone-level security planning becomes necessary above 1,000 people.

- **Healthcare:** Medical expertise is distributed at 100 people (everyone has basic first aid; one person is the trauma lead). At 500 people, medical working groups coordinate across sub-units. At 1,000+ people, regional medical specialization becomes possible and necessary.

Treating these domains as independent during community growth leads to governance failures when scaling decisions in one domain conflict with infrastructure built for a different scale.
