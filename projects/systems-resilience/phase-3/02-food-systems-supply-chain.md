# Community Food Systems & Supply Chain Resilience
> **Region**: Midwest US (Zone 5) | **Scale**: 100–1,000 people
> **Phase**: 3 — Community | **Domain**: 1 — Food Systems
> **Cross-references**: [individual/02-food.md](../individual/02-food.md) · [household/03-food-coordination.md](../household/03-food-coordination.md) · [phase-3/01-governance-decision-making.md](./01-governance-decision-making.md)

## The Most Important Finding

The global food supply chain is structurally fragile at the points that communities cannot control — and structurally robust at the points that communities can build redundancy into. A community that understands this distinction can become significantly more food-secure without attempting the impossible task of becoming completely self-sufficient.

The practical finding: 85% of US beef processing is controlled by four companies (JBS, Tyson, Cargill, and National Beef), and just 12 federally inspected plants produce nearly half the country's beef supply. [1] A single plant fire in 2019 caused a 27% drop in cattle trading in the following week. [2] These are the fragile nodes. A single rural Midwest community can do nothing about those. But the same community can establish direct relationships with 5–10 local farms covering 60–70% of its vegetable and grain needs; build 6-month dry goods storage against supply chain disruption; develop food production capacity on available land to cover caloric baselines in an extended emergency; and create distribution protocols that ensure food reaches vulnerable members first.

Food resilience at community scale is not food independence. It is building enough redundancy at the local level that when the global system is disrupted — as it demonstrably is with regularity — the community can sustain itself through the disruption and not experience the worst outcomes.

## Why Community-Scale Food Systems Are Different

### From Household to Community: What Changes

The [household/03-food-coordination.md](../household/03-food-coordination.md) document covers cooperative food production at 3–5 household scale. Community-scale (100–1,000 people) introduces qualitatively different challenges:

**Scale changes the aggregation problem.** A 5-household cluster can coordinate informally about who grew what and who needs what. A 500-person community cannot. At community scale, food supply requires deliberate logistics — aggregation, storage, distribution, and accounting — that does not occur without designated roles and infrastructure.

**Scale changes the diversity requirement.** At household scale, diversifying crops means 15–20 varieties. At community scale, dietary diversity across 100–1,000 people with different medical needs, allergies, cultural preferences, and labor capacities means 40–80 distinct food production streams and a supply chain architecture that serves all of them.

**Scale creates the possibility of specialization.** A 5-household cluster cannot support a full-time food coordinator. A 500-person community can. Specialization increases efficiency and expertise but introduces dependencies — if the community's only grain grower leaves, the community loses grain production capacity. Managing these dependencies is a governance and agricultural planning function.

**Scale enables aggregation advantages that individual households cannot access.** Community-scale bulk purchasing, collective cold storage, shared processing equipment, and pooled food reserves are all more cost-effective and resilient per person than equivalent individual systems. These advantages are only realized if the logistics infrastructure is built to use them.

## Structural Vulnerabilities in the Current Food Supply Chain

### The Concentration Problem

The COVID-19 pandemic provided the clearest recent demonstration of food supply chain structural vulnerability. In 2020, school closures and restaurant shutdowns removed key markets for dairy farmers, forcing them to dump up to 3.7 million gallons of milk per day at the peak of the disruption. [3] At the same time, meatpacking plant closures due to COVID-19 outbreaks among workers created bottlenecks that cascaded through the entire supply chain from ranchers to grocery store shelves. The plants could not be substituted because of the extreme concentration of processing capacity in a small number of large facilities.

By 2024, 16 countries had implemented 22 food export bans in response to supply disruptions. [4] The cascading nature of these disruptions — triggered by natural gas price spikes that increased fertilizer costs, which reduced crop yields, which triggered export restrictions — demonstrates that the supply chain vulnerabilities are not isolated but systemically connected.

**The specific concentration points most relevant to a Midwest rural community:**

- **Grain processing**: Regional grain elevators, flour mills, and feed processors have consolidated dramatically since 1970. Many rural areas that once had multiple competing processors now have one, meaning a single business failure or disruption removes the processing capacity for the entire region.
- **Protein processing**: The Big Four meatpackers process 85% of US beef. [5] Outside the industrial system, a Midwest community has limited access to USDA-inspected slaughter capacity. Small-scale custom processors have declined significantly and typically have long wait lists even under normal conditions.
- **Cold storage**: Centralized refrigerated distribution warehouses serve large geographic areas. A disruption affecting a regional distribution center removes access to refrigerated products for multiple counties simultaneously.
- **Retail distribution**: The majority of rural Midwest grocery supply flows through a small number of regional distribution chains. Communities with a single grocery option are one supply disruption away from empty shelves.

### The "Missing Middle" Problem in Local Food Systems

Between the household garden and the large commodity farm lies what food system researchers call the "missing middle" — the local food infrastructure (food hubs, regional processors, direct-market channels, local aggregators) that would allow community-scale food systems to function. This infrastructure was largely dismantled between 1950 and 2000 as consolidation economics favored large-scale centralized processing. Rebuilding it is the central challenge of community food system resilience at the 100–1,000 person scale.

A 2025 National Food Hub Survey found that food hubs — "mission-driven businesses or organizations that manage aggregation, distribution, and marketing of source-identified food products" — represent a rebuilding of this missing middle. [6] Of 100 surveyed hubs across 27 states: 81% include community members in decisions; 55% reinvest profits in the surrounding community; 51% recruit community residents as employees. By December 2024, local food hubs and direct procurement programs had generated $747 million in economic activity on $400 million in direct food purchases. [7]

The food hub model is not a charitable exercise — it is the infrastructure solution to the missing middle problem. A Midwest community planning food system resilience should either connect to an existing regional food hub or develop the capacity to function as one.

## Community Food System Architecture

### The Four Layers

A resilient community food system at the 100–1,000 person scale requires four distinct, interdependent layers:

**Layer 1: Emergency reserve (minimum viability baseline)**
Pre-positioned stored food sufficient to sustain the community's minimum caloric needs for 6 months without any external supply. This is the floor — if everything else fails, this is what prevents starvation. It is not a substitute for the other three layers; it is the last line of defense.

**Layer 2: Local production capacity (primary supply)**
Agricultural land and production capacity sufficient to produce the majority of the community's caloric needs in a sustained emergency. This requires direct community relationships with farmers, agricultural land access, and production planning.

**Layer 3: Regional supply networks (normal operation and resilience redundancy)**
Established relationships with multiple regional farmers, food hubs, processors, and cooperatives providing food diversity, production redundancy, and economic efficiency during normal operation and early-phase disruptions.

**Layer 4: Distribution and allocation infrastructure (logistics)**
Physical and organizational infrastructure for receiving, storing, processing, and distributing food to all community members, with specific provisions for vulnerable members who cannot easily access distribution points.

These layers are not sequential build-outs — they should be developed in parallel, with each layer improving the community's position against different disruption scenarios.

### Caloric Planning: How Much Does a Community Need?

The planning calculation for community food sufficiency starts with caloric needs and works backward to land and production requirements.

**Baseline caloric needs (per day, Zone 5 Midwest community):**
- Adults (18–64): 2,200 cal/day
- Elderly (65+): 1,800 cal/day
- Children (under 12): 1,500 cal/day
- Heavy labor in crisis conditions: add 300–600 cal/day to adults

**Example: 300-person Midwest community**
Assume age distribution: 60% adults (180), 15% elderly (45), 25% children (75).
Daily caloric need: (180 × 2,200) + (45 × 1,800) + (75 × 1,500) = 396,000 + 81,000 + 112,500 = **589,500 cal/day**.
Annual caloric need: 589,500 × 365 = **215 million calories/year**.

**Production capacity required:**
At intensive Zone 5 production of 6–10 million cal/acre/year (potatoes, winter squash, dry beans, corn, and supplementary vegetables), a 300-person community at 80% local food production would require 17–29 cultivated acres under intensive production. [8] This is feasible for a rural community with access to land — roughly 1 acre per 10–17 people. The challenge is not land — most Midwest rural communities have access to far more agricultural land than this — but the production knowledge, labor, and distribution infrastructure to convert land access into reliable food supply.

**Emergency reserve calculation:**
6-month reserve at minimum caloric need (1,800 cal/person/day for all 300 people):
1,800 × 300 × 180 days = 97.2 million calories.
In storable form: approximately 60,000 lbs of dry goods at average 1,600 cal/lb (grains, legumes, oil). This requires approximately 1,200 cubic feet of climate-controlled dry storage space, or roughly the equivalent of a 20×30 foot room at standard ceiling height with efficient shelving.

## Agricultural Production Models for Community Scale

### Community Supported Agriculture (CSA) and Direct Farm Relationships

The Community Supported Agriculture model — where households purchase shares in a farm's production before the growing season — is the most documented mechanism for connecting communities to local food production. As of 2020, 7,244 US farms sold products through CSA arrangements. [9]

During the COVID-19 pandemic, CSA demonstrated precisely the resilience characteristic it is designed for: when restaurant and institutional markets collapsed, farms with direct consumer relationships through CSA were more resilient and better positioned to deal with the pandemic's impacts than farms dependent on wholesale channels. [10] CSA enrollment surged dramatically in spring 2020, peaked in August 2020, and then declined through 2022 as restaurants and institutions reopened — demonstrating that the CSA model serves as a resilience mechanism that communities can activate during disruption.

**For a 300-person community**, establishing CSA relationships with 3–5 local farms producing complementary crops — one specializing in vegetables, one in dry staples (corn, beans, squash), one in proteins (poultry or pork), one in orchard/perennials — provides supply diversity at a manageable coordination scale. The key design requirement: each farm in the network should independently cover at least 20% of the community's needs in their specialty crop category, so the loss of any one farm does not create a critical shortage.

**Direct farm relationship structuring:**
- Multi-year contracts (2–3 year terms) provide farm planning security and community supply predictability
- Pre-season advance payment (20–30% of seasonal cost) provides farmer operating capital and community investment in production outcomes
- Production diversity requirements in contracts prevent over-reliance on single crops from individual farms
- Emergency protocol clauses specify what happens if a farm fails mid-season (e.g., partial refund plus mutual effort to source from alternatives)

### Food Hub Integration: The Aggregation Solution

A community food hub — even at minimal operational scale — solves the aggregation problem that prevents direct farm-to-household distribution from working at scale. Food hubs "actively manage the aggregation, distribution, and marketing of source-identified food products primarily from local and regional producers." [11]

At community scale, a minimal food hub function requires:
- **Physical space**: A receiving, sorting, and distribution location with loading access. Ideally climate-controlled; at minimum, weather-protected. An existing community building, barn, or cooperative facility can serve this function if adapted.
- **Cold storage**: 200–400 square feet of walk-in refrigeration ($8,000–$25,000 to build; $3,000–$8,000 to rent annually) serving the whole community. Individual household refrigerators are inadequate for managing fresh produce at community scale.
- **Dry storage**: As described above, the 6-month emergency reserve requires approximately 1,200 cubic feet of climate-controlled dry storage.
- **Coordination role**: One part-time community food coordinator (10–15 hours/week, more during harvest seasons) to manage farm relationships, coordinate distribution logistics, maintain inventory, and manage allocation decisions.
- **Distribution schedule**: Regular pickup or delivery schedule (weekly is the functional minimum) with specific provisions for community members who cannot travel to pickup locations.

Indiana's Resilient Food Systems Infrastructure grant program (RFSI) provides a federal model for this approach, awarding $6.7 million in 2025 for 17 food hub and local food infrastructure projects across the state. [12] The Beneker Family Farms food hub and the Sprout Growers and Makers Marketplace in Minnesota both demonstrate community-scale implementation of this model.

### Emergency Food Production: Rapid Expansion Capacity

During a severe multi-month supply disruption, a community's food system will be stressed beyond normal operating conditions. The agricultural system must be able to expand production rapidly. Planning for this expansion in advance — identifying available land, establishing access agreements, and maintaining seed reserves — means the community can respond in weeks rather than the months it would take to negotiate access and acquire seeds during a crisis.

**Rapid expansion protocol for Zone 5 Midwest:**

*Month 1 of crisis*: Activate existing CSA farm relationships; inventory community food storage; implement allocation governance (see [01-governance-decision-making.md](./01-governance-decision-making.md)).

*Month 2–3*: Expand existing farm production onto pre-identified unused or underutilized agricultural land. Emergency planting focuses on highest-calorie-per-acre crops: potatoes (8,000–10,000 lbs/acre at Zone 5 yields), dry beans (1,500–2,000 lbs/acre), winter squash (8,000–12,000 lbs/acre), corn (for grinding: 100–150 bu/acre). [13]

*Month 4+*: Transition from crisis response to sustained community production. Establish crop rotation to maintain soil health for multi-year extended disruption. Build or expand root cellar and dry storage to accommodate expanded harvest.

**Pre-crisis preparation requirements:**
- Land access agreements with landowners of currently unused agricultural land within 5-mile radius (verbal or written agreement committing to crisis-access permission)
- Heirloom seed reserve adequate for expanded production (minimum: 50–100 lbs dry beans, 100 lbs dry corn, 50 lbs squash, 25 lbs potato seed stock, supplementary vegetable seeds — approximately $400–$800 from regional seed sources)
- Agricultural equipment access (tractor with tillage implements for minimum 30 acres, owned collectively or on lease/loan agreement with farmer partner)
- Community labor pool assessment: how many community members have agricultural experience? What is the maximum labor hours per day available for food production?

## Distribution: Getting Food from Production to Households

### The Last-Mile Problem at Community Scale

Food distribution fails most predictably not at the production end but at the last mile — getting food from aggregation point to households, particularly households whose members are elderly, disabled, very young, or otherwise unable to travel to a distribution point. This failure mode is documented across disaster response contexts. [14]

After Hurricane Ida, emergency food distribution in New Orleans reached many low-income communities but identified operational failures in real-time information about available resources and nutritional adequacy tracking. [15] The communities best served had pre-established distribution networks with specific provisions for non-mobile members.

**Community food distribution infrastructure:**

*Pickup distribution (default)*: Weekly community food pickup at a central location, organized by household ID to reduce congestion. Clear schedule published to all households. 2-hour pickup window minimum.

*Delivery routes for non-mobile households*: Pre-mapped routes covering households with elderly members, disabled members, young children, or medical conditions preventing travel. Volunteer rotation (2-hour monthly commitment per household) covers delivery. Non-mobile households identified during community inventory before crisis, not discovered during it.

*Emergency distribution protocol*: When supply is significantly below normal (below 70% of expected distribution), a tiered distribution system activates, prioritizing: (1) medical need households; (2) households with children under 12; (3) elderly households; (4) all other households. This protocol must be established by community assembly before any shortage occurs — not negotiated under the pressure of actual scarcity.

### Distribution Record-Keeping

Distribution at community scale requires written records to be legitimate. A household that received no allocation last week because records weren't kept has no recourse. A community where allocations cannot be verified is vulnerable to accusations of favoritism that can fracture governance.

Minimum distribution record requirements:
- Household ID, composition (number adults/children/elderly), and any special dietary or medical needs
- Weekly allocation received by household (quantity and type)
- Running total allocated per household (for equity review)
- Any allocation adjustments with documented reasons and who authorized them

Paper records are the baseline — they function without power. Digital records (offline database on a Raspberry Pi or similar low-power device) are a supplement, not a replacement.

## Emergency Food Storage: Building the Reserve

### What to Store and Why

Community-scale emergency food storage is distinct from individual household storage in three ways: (1) it is held communally, requiring governance of access and allocation; (2) it is stored in larger quantities requiring commercial-scale storage infrastructure; and (3) it must account for dietary diversity across all community members, including those with medical dietary requirements.

**Priority storage categories for Zone 5 Midwest communities:**

| Category | Storage target (per person/6 months) | Storage form | Shelf life |
|---|---|---|---|
| Grains (wheat, corn, oats, rice) | 150 lbs | Whole grain in sealed containers with oxygen absorbers | 10–25 years |
| Legumes (dry beans, lentils, chickpeas) | 60 lbs | Whole dry in sealed containers | 8–10 years |
| Oil and fats | 3 gallons | Commercial-grade sealed containers; refrigeration extends life | 1–4 years (rotate annually) |
| Salt | 5 lbs | Sealed containers; indefinite shelf life | Indefinite |
| Sugar/honey | 15 lbs | Sealed containers | 2 years (sugar); indefinite (honey) |
| Powdered milk or protein equivalent | 20 lbs | Sealed nitrogen-flushed containers | 2–10 years |
| Medical dietary supplements | Variable | Per community health assessment | Per product |

At these quantities for a 300-person community: approximately 45,000 lbs grain, 18,000 lbs legumes, 900 gallons oil, 1,500 lbs salt, 4,500 lbs sugar/honey, 6,000 lbs powdered milk. This represents roughly $50,000–$80,000 in current (2026) commodity costs for a 6-month reserve — a one-time community capital investment equivalent to the cost of approximately 2–3 months of community-level food spending.

**Storage infrastructure requirements:**
- Cool, dry, dark conditions (below 70°F, below 60% relative humidity) — most critical for grain longevity
- Pest exclusion: sealed concrete or metal-lined room; regular monitoring and treatment
- Fire protection: storage building should be separated from primary community structures
- Access control: physically secured; allocation documented; keys/access held by domain lead and one backup
- Rotation schedule: FIFO (first in, first out) with clear date labeling; annual partial rotation to maintain freshness

**Cost reduction strategies:**
- Bulk purchasing through a regional buying cooperative or food hub reduces per-pound cost by 20–40% compared to retail prices
- Harvesting and drying community-produced grains and legumes directly into storage cuts costs substantially but requires processing equipment (grain cleaner, dryer, sealed bin capacity)
- Cooperative pooling of existing household storage — incentivizing households to bring reserves toward the community storage target — reduces new purchases required

### The Rotation Challenge

Emergency storage that is not rotated becomes waste. Grains stored improperly or for too long develop mold, off-flavors, or pest infestations that make them unusable. Oil becomes rancid. The storage system must include a rotation protocol that is actually implemented.

**Practical rotation protocol for community storage:**
- Annual rotation event (early June, after winter meal-planning season, before harvest): community food preparation event uses 15–20% of stored grain and legume reserves to produce preserved foods (dried meals, grain mixes, preserved vegetables), which are distributed to households. New bulk purchases replace the consumed stock.
- Oil: entire oil reserve replaced annually (or biannually for more stable saturated fats like coconut oil). Old oil redirected to community food production or soap making rather than discarded.
- Monthly visual inspection by food storage lead: check seals, look for moisture, pest signs, or damaged containers. Any compromised containers quarantined immediately.

## Supply Chain Resilience: Building Redundancy

### The Three-Source Rule for Critical Foods

For each food category that the community cannot produce locally (spices, salt, certain oils, coffee, tropical fruits), the supply chain should include at minimum three distinct sources with different geographic origins and distribution channels. The three-source rule prevents a single sourcing disruption from eliminating an entire food category.

This does not require three full-volume suppliers — it requires three viable suppliers, any one of whom could supply a meaningful portion of need in an emergency. Maintaining active relationships with all three — even if only one is the primary day-to-day supplier — is the key. A supplier that has never received a community order is not actually a resilient backup option.

**For Zone 5 Midwest communities, three-source priority list:**

*Salt*: Regional grocery cooperative + local grain elevator (often resells agricultural salt) + direct purchase from a salt distributor at or near wholesale. Salt has indefinite shelf life at stored quantities; the community's annual purchase requirement is modest.

*Cooking oils*: Local soybean crush facility (Midwest has substantial capacity) + regional grocery cooperative + a seed stock and processing capability (sunflower or flax can be pressed locally with appropriate equipment).

*Proteins beyond local production*: Regional CSA farm for poultry and eggs + community hunting and fishing program (formal or informal, with documented harvest areas) + legume protein from community storage.

*Preserved foods and variety*: Community canning and fermentation program + relationships with specialty producers (orchards, bee keepers, mushroom cultivators) + seasonal market purchases at peak supply and price.

### Managing the Risk of Single-Farm Dependency

A community whose primary vegetable supply comes from a single farm is one crop failure, disease event, or landowner decision away from losing most of its vegetable supply. Distributing risk across multiple farms is more complex to coordinate but substantially more resilient.

**The failure modes to protect against:**
- Crop failure from weather (hail, drought, late frost in Zone 5): most severe when community is dependent on a single farm or a single crop
- Disease and pest events: crop diseases spread within a growing region; farms specializing in different crop families face different disease pressure
- Farm infrastructure failure (well, irrigation, storage)
- Farmer incapacity (injury, illness, departure) — particularly acute for small farms where one person holds most of the production knowledge
- Pricing disagreement mid-season

**Protection mechanisms:**
- No single farm should provide more than 40% of any single crop category's supply
- Relationships with 5–10 farms of varying sizes, distances, and crop specializations
- Community members with agricultural knowledge and access to at least some land should maintain production capacity independent of farm partnerships — even at modest scale, 10–20 community members growing substantial gardens provides resilience against farm-level failures
- Seed and production capability transfers: if a partner farm fails, who can expand production on short notice? This is a governance and labor coordination question that should be answered before it is needed.

## Local Food in Disaster Response: Case Studies

### COVID-19: The Stress Test of Local Food Networks

The 2020–2022 pandemic stress-tested local food systems in ways that revealed both their resilience and their limitations. The documented findings:

**Local food networks outperformed industrial supply chains** in the first 72 hours of any given disruption. CSA farms, farmers markets, and community buying clubs could redirect product and adjust distribution faster than large distributors because their supply chains were shorter and their relationships more direct. [16]

**Equity was the central challenge.** Community food networks that had been built primarily by and for middle-income households with time, transportation, and social capital to navigate non-standard food channels did not automatically serve low-income community members. Communities that built equity into their network design — sliding-scale CSA pricing, accessible pickup locations, home delivery for non-mobile members — fared better on this dimension. [17]

**Digital communication infrastructure determined distribution effectiveness.** Communities with existing communication channels (email lists, neighborhood apps, radio networks) distributed information about available food resources faster and more accurately than those relying on formal institutional communication. The informal network was the faster one.

**Farm financial viability is a prerequisite for community food resilience.** Farms with CSA relationships were more resilient, but many still experienced decreased profitability during the pandemic because adaptation costs — changing pickup logistics, purchasing contactless payment systems, adding home delivery — exceeded the revenue gains from increased enrollment. [18] Communities that want reliable farm partners must consider farm financial sustainability as a community resilience question, not just a private business matter.

### Hurricane Ida: Distribution Failures and Lessons

Emergency food distribution after Hurricane Ida in New Orleans (2021) provides documented evidence of the failure modes in emergency food systems. Research published in *Frontiers in Public Health* found that while food reached many communities, operational failures significantly reduced effectiveness:

- **No real-time information** on types and quantities of meals available at distribution points, preventing residents from making informed decisions about whether to travel to a distribution site
- **Nutritional inadequacy** not tracked or reported, making it impossible to assess whether emergency food was meeting actual nutritional needs
- **Access barriers** for non-mobile residents not systematically addressed in distribution planning [15]

These failures are not unique to Ida recovery — they are the predictable outcomes of distribution systems designed without advance planning for the specific challenges of emergency food access. The correction for each is straightforward: real-time information requires a communication protocol; nutritional adequacy requires a minimum dietary standard definition; access for non-mobile residents requires a delivery protocol. None of these can be improvised during a crisis.

### France's Territorial Food Projects: Pre-Built Network Resilience

Since 2014, France's *Projets Alimentaires Territoriaux* (Territorial Food Projects or PAT) have brought together local government, farmers, distributors, and community organizations around shared regional food system goals. [19] During the COVID-19 crisis, communities with active PAT networks demonstrated significantly greater adaptability: established relationships and communication channels enabled rapid organization of "solidarity baskets" of locally produced food for vulnerable community members within days of lockdown, using distribution networks that already existed.

The lesson: the relationship infrastructure that enables resilient emergency response cannot be built during the emergency. It must exist before.

## Implementation: Building Community Food Resilience

### Phase 1: Inventory and Relationship (Months 1–6)

**Community food inventory:**
- Map all significant food production in the community and immediate region: household gardens, small farms, orchards, food forests, hunting and fishing access areas
- Assess current food storage by household: what does the community actually have? What is the collective months-of-supply baseline?
- Identify community members with agricultural, food preservation, and food distribution experience

**Farm relationship initiation:**
- Identify 3–5 farms within reasonable distance (under 30 miles) producing crops that complement community production
- Initiate direct relationship conversations: not transactional purchasing discussions, but community food security conversations that include the farmer's perspective on what they need to be reliable long-term partners
- Determine which farms are open to multi-year agreements and advance purchase arrangements

**Food system governance:**
- Designate a food systems domain lead and backup (see [01-governance-decision-making.md](./01-governance-decision-making.md))
- Establish food system committee with community member representation across geographic sub-areas, income levels, and dietary needs
- Draft provisional allocation protocol for review by community assembly

### Phase 2: Infrastructure Investment (Months 6–18)

**Storage buildout:**
- Identify or develop community storage location: secured, climate-controlled, accessible, fire-separated from primary structures
- Begin bulk purchasing toward 3-month storage reserve target (50% of 6-month target); reach 6-month target within 18 months
- Implement rotation protocol and monthly inspection cadence

**Distribution infrastructure:**
- Establish community food pickup location and weekly schedule
- Map non-mobile households; establish volunteer delivery rotation
- Build community food distribution record system (physical ledger primary; digital backup)

**Network expansion:**
- Formalize farm relationships through written agreements covering supply quantities, payment terms, emergency protocols, and multi-year commitments
- Connect to or develop food hub function for aggregation and cold storage
- Identify 2–3 backup farm relationships that can expand production on short notice

### Phase 3: Production Capacity (Year 2+)

**Agricultural land access:**
- Secure written access agreements for minimum 1 acre per 10 community members of productive agricultural land
- Develop community seed library: 40–60 heirloom varieties covering all major food categories
- Train minimum 5–10% of community members in intensive food production techniques

**Processing capacity:**
- Acquire or develop access to community grain mill, pressure canner fleet, dehydrators, and fermentation facilities
- Establish preservation calendar aligned with Zone 5 harvest seasons
- Develop community nutrition planning to address dietary diversity needs within emergency food allocation

## Decision Trees for Food System Choices

### Decision Tree A: Assessing Community Food Resilience Status

```
Does your community have 6-month food storage for all members?
  NO → Months of storage currently: ___. Priority: expand storage.
  YES → Continue.

Does your community have established relationships with at least 3 farms
producing distinct crop categories?
  NO → Priority: initiate farm relationships before other investments.
  YES → Continue.

Does your community have a food distribution protocol that reaches
non-mobile members?
  NO → Priority: map non-mobile households; establish delivery rotation.
  YES → Continue.

Does your community have a food allocation governance structure for
shortage conditions?
  NO → Priority: adopt allocation protocol by community assembly before
       shortage occurs (see 01-governance-decision-making.md).
  YES → Continue.

Does your community have agricultural production capacity to cover
at least 30% of caloric needs in an extended emergency?
  NO → Priority: land access agreements, seed reserves, equipment access.
  YES → Your community food system has reached baseline resilience.
        Focus on deepening and redundancy in all layers.
```

### Decision Tree B: Responding to a Food Supply Disruption

```
How severe is the disruption?

MILD (supply reduced <20%, expected recovery <4 weeks):
  → Activate community food storage buffer for affected categories
  → Communicate through community information channels
  → No allocation changes required; continue monitoring

MODERATE (supply reduced 20–50%, recovery uncertain):
  → Activate all farm relationships; request maximum supply
  → Implement distribution record-keeping if not already active
  → Inventory current storage and calculate months-of-supply
  → Notify community through all channels
  → Community assembly review within 72 hours

SEVERE (supply reduced >50%, or extended disruption >2 months expected):
  → Activate emergency allocation protocol (tiered priority distribution)
  → Begin rapid agricultural expansion (identify land, mobilize labor)
  → Implement full distribution record-keeping and weekly allocation
  → Establish communication with adjacent communities for mutual aid
  → Community assembly weekly or more frequently as needed
```

## Failure Mode Catalog

### Failure Mode 1: Single-Farm Dependency Collapse

**Pattern**: Community establishes a strong relationship with one large farm that provides 60–70% of fresh produce. Farm experiences a crop failure, the farmer sells the land, or a land dispute develops. Community loses the majority of its fresh food supply mid-season.

**Prevention**: 40% maximum from any single source (any category); written agreements with 90-day notice requirements; 2–3 backup farm relationships with regular (if small) orders to maintain active status; community production capacity of at least 20% of vegetable needs independently.

**Recovery**: Activate backup farm relationships; implement emergency triage on fresh produce allocation (prioritize households with children and medical needs); accelerate community garden production; purchase emergency stock at regional farmers markets and food hubs.

### Failure Mode 2: Storage Spoilage from Neglect

**Pattern**: Community invests in bulk grain and legume storage but does not implement a rotation and inspection protocol. After 18–24 months, stored goods are significantly degraded by pest infestation, moisture, or oxidation. The emergency reserve that the community believed it held is largely unusable.

**Prevention**: Monthly visual inspection assigned to food storage domain lead with written log. Annual rotation event (June, after winter meal planning) uses 15–20% of storage and replaces it. Temperature and humidity monitoring in storage facility.

**Recovery**: Assess extent of spoilage; salvage what is viable; treat facility for pests before restocking; review and strengthen storage conditions before new purchases. Community assembly debrief to determine root cause and prevent recurrence.

### Failure Mode 3: Distribution Inequity Under Scarcity

**Pattern**: When supply is reduced, distribution defaults to whoever can access the pickup location — typically households with vehicles, time flexibility, and social connections to the distribution coordinators. Non-mobile elderly members, households with young children and no transportation, and lower-income households with less flexible schedules receive inadequate allocation.

**Prevention**: Non-mobile household map completed before any shortage; delivery rotation assigned and trained before crisis; allocation record-keeping active before shortage makes it contested; tiered priority protocol adopted by community assembly before any scarcity occurs.

**Recovery**: Audit recent distribution records for equity; immediate delivery program for under-served households; governance review of allocation protocol; expand delivery volunteer pool.

### Failure Mode 4: Governance Capture of Food Allocation

**Pattern**: The food distribution function concentrates authority in 2–3 people who develop informal power over allocations. Over time, allocations favor households they prefer, creating real or perceived inequity. Community fractures over food allocation, which is the fastest path to governance collapse.

**Prevention**: Distribution requires two-person verification of all allocation decisions. Record-keeping accessible to all community members (public log). Monthly community assembly review of distribution data. Rotation of distribution coordinator role on 3-month cycle.

**Recovery**: Governance intervention (see [01-governance-decision-making.md](./01-governance-decision-making.md)); full audit of previous allocations; allocation protocol revision by community assembly; temporary oversight by community-elected review panel.

## Regional Specifics: Zone 5 Midwest

**Climate constraints**: Zone 5 means last frost approximately April 15–May 15, first fall frost September 15–October 15. Growing season approximately 120–150 days. Heat-loving crops (tomatoes, peppers, corn) have a narrow production window. Cold-tolerant crops (brassicas, root vegetables, beans, squash) are the backbone of the food production system. Season extension through cold frames and high tunnels can add 4–8 weeks on both ends.

**Soil advantages**: Most Zone 5 Midwest communities sit on deep, high-organic-content soils built over millennia of prairie ecosystem. Productive yields per acre are among the highest in the world for grain crops, and intensive vegetable production on well-managed soil is highly productive. The challenge is usually knowledge and labor, not soil capability.

**Crop priority list for Zone 5 community food production**:
1. Winter squash (highest calories per acre for vegetable crops; stores 3–6 months without processing)
2. Dry beans (protein; stores 8+ years; nitrogen-fixing; multiple Zone 5 varieties)
3. Corn (calories; grinding capability required; stores 2+ years dried)
4. Potatoes (highest calories per acre of any common crop; stores 6+ months with root cellar)
5. Brassicas (nutrition; fall-harvest; cold-tolerant)
6. Alliums (preservation; anti-microbial; nutrition)
7. Sunflowers (oil; seed protein; drought-tolerant; grows on marginal land)

**Water considerations**: Zone 5 Midwest averages 35–45 inches of precipitation annually, generally adequate for most crops without irrigation. Extended drought years (occurring with increasing frequency) may require irrigation capacity for at least the critical calorie crops. Any community food production plan should identify water sources and irrigation capacity before assuming rain-fed sufficiency.
