# Community-Scale Governance & Decision-Making Structures
> **Region**: Midwest US (Zone 5) | **Scale**: 100–1,000 people
> **Phase**: 3 — Community | **Domain**: 2 — Governance
> **Cross-references**: [household/01-household-coordination-overview.md](../household/01-household-coordination-overview.md) · [phase-3/02-food-systems-supply-chain.md](./02-food-systems-supply-chain.md)

---

## The Most Important Finding

Governance is the load-bearing structure beneath every other community resilience domain. Communications systems can be improvised under pressure. Food distribution can be reorganized mid-crisis. Security postures can be adjusted as threats evolve. Governance cannot be improvised under contested authority — and once social trust fractures over a governance failure, retrofitting it is orders of magnitude harder than building it before the crisis.

The empirical literature on this is unambiguous: communities that cross the 150-person threshold without consciously designing governance structures will have structures imposed on them — by whoever is most organized, most aggressive, or most willing to use force at the moment of crisis. The transition from informal trust-based coordination to formal governance is not optional above roughly 150 people. It is only a question of whether that transition is designed deliberately or by default.

This document covers what that deliberate design looks like at the 100–1,000 person scale, with specific implementation guidance, case study analysis, and failure mode documentation drawn from the best-documented long-running examples: Zapatista autonomous communities (30+ years), Rojava communes (12+ years under active conflict), Mondragon cooperatives (65+ years), and the WWII-era US rationing boards (3+ years, 100,000+ volunteers).

## Why Governance at This Scale Is Different

### The Dunbar Transition

British anthropologist Robin Dunbar's research on social group sizes identifies roughly 150 people as the cognitive limit for stable trust-based relationships. [1] Below this threshold, everyone knows everyone, reputation is the primary accountability mechanism, informal norms govern behavior, and coordination happens through direct communication without formal structures.

Above 150 people, these conditions break down systematically. Not because people become untrustworthy, but because the social web becomes too large to maintain through personal relationships alone. An unknown person in a 50-person group is anomalous and immediately visible. An unknown person in a 500-person group is simply someone you haven't met yet. The difference in accountability is profound.

Communities that grow from 80 to 300 people — through organic growth, planned expansion, or refugee absorption during a crisis — cross this threshold without necessarily recognizing it. Their governance systems were designed for informal trust. They now need something different. Designing that transition before it is needed is the central task of Phase 3 governance planning.

### What Governance Actually Needs to Do

A governance system for a 100–1,000 person community under extended disruption conditions must accomplish six functions simultaneously:

1. **Allocate scarce resources** — food, water, energy, labor, medical supplies — in ways the community accepts as legitimate
2. **Make decisions under time pressure** without requiring universal agreement
3. **Maintain accountability** — those who hold authority can lose it for cause, through a process that does not require violence or departure
4. **Preserve institutional memory** across personnel changes, so that knowledge does not depart when key individuals do
5. **Manage internal conflict** through mechanisms that de-escalate before confrontation becomes destabilizing
6. **Coordinate with external entities** — adjacent communities, regional governance, emergency services — without surrendering internal decision-making authority

No informal system reliably accomplishes all six of these above 150 people under crisis conditions. The question is which formal systems accomplish them best at the specific scales in question.

## Structural Models: What the Evidence Shows

### The Delegate Council Model (Best Evidence for 150–500 People)

The delegate council model — where an assembly makes decisions and delegates implement them — is the best-documented governance structure at the 150–500 person scale. It appears in three independent long-running case studies with significantly different cultural contexts, which increases confidence that the structural logic is sound rather than culturally contingent.

**Zapatista mandar obedeciendo** ("governing by obeying") is the most directly documented version. [2] Political leaders in Zapatista autonomous communities (governed roughly 300,000 people across 55 municipalities at peak operation) do not make decisions on behalf of their communities — they act as delegates implementing decisions made in local assemblies. Leaders are chosen through the indigenous *cargo* tradition — obligation to serve one's community without remuneration. Communities retain the explicit right to revoke the mandate of any official who fails to fulfill their duty through the same assembly that granted it. [3]

The governance unit is the village-level assembly, covering 100–400 households. This is the decision-making body. Delegates carry decisions to higher-level coordination councils, report back, and return with those councils' responses. Delegates are specialists in nothing — this is deliberately maintained. The non-specialization of political authority is a structural feature, not a bug. It prevents the accumulation of decision-making power in the hands of those who hold formal roles.

**Rojava commune structure** provides a second independent data point, operating under conditions considerably more extreme than anything a Midwest community is likely to face. [4] As of 2025, Rojava's system includes 3,950 communes organized as the basic unit of governance, each consisting of 30–400 households. Each commune has six permanent committees — women, youth, social, economic, peace, and self-defense — providing structural diversity of voice within the governance unit. Delegates from communes send representatives to town councils; town councils send representatives to regional councils.

The Rojava evidence is mixed in instructive ways. Communes took strongest hold in Kurdish communities with existing shared cultural frameworks. In Arab-majority regions, many communes struggled to maintain participation against the pressures of ongoing conflict, religious and tribal structures, and economic embargo. [5] This is not evidence against the model — it is evidence that commune governance requires investment in relationship and trust-building before crisis, not during it.

**Mondragon cooperative governance** provides a 65-year longitudinal data set in a non-crisis context that demonstrates the structural logic at commercial scale. [6] Each individual Mondragon cooperative retains legal and functional autonomy. Worker-members vote one-person, one-vote. Elected governing councils review cooperative actions. A General Assembly of 650 worker-members from across cooperatives meets annually to set policy. When facing economic recession, cooperatives responded by developing community-wide governance structures for the first time — demonstrating that the federated model creates mechanisms for collective response to shared threats. Workers in cooperatives that faced closure were relocated to other cooperatives rather than laid off, at rates significantly better than comparable private-sector firms.

**Structural requirements common to all three models:**
- Decisions are categorized by scope before any meeting begins (household-affecting, community-affecting, inter-community-affecting)
- Delegates implement assembly decisions rather than making independent decisions
- Accountability operates through the same body that granted authority
- Rotation limits prevent permanent accumulation of authority in any role
- Records of decisions, resources, and reasoning are maintained in writing

### Federated Sub-Units (Best Evidence for 500–1,000 People)

At 500–1,000 people, the single-assembly model breaks down regardless of facilitation quality. Meetings become too large for everyone to speak; information distribution across the community becomes bottlenecked; specialized knowledge concentrates in working groups that develop informal authority not accountable to the larger community.

The solution documented across case studies is federation: sub-units of 100–150 people each maintain their own governance and resources, coordinating across sub-units through delegate councils. This preserves the functional governance unit at a manageable size while creating inter-unit coordination infrastructure.

**Mondragon's federal structure** is the best-documented long-running example. Each cooperative — functionally analogous to a 100–500 person sub-community — retains its own General Assembly, Governing Council, and management structures. Cross-cooperative decisions go to the Cooperative Congress. The power of each cooperative resides in its social base, preventing the federation from overriding the sub-unit on decisions within the sub-unit's domain.

**Zapatista scaling** from village-level assemblies through municipal councils to the Regional Autonomous Government and Caracoles (regional coordination centers) provides a rural community model that more closely maps the Midwest context. [7] The 2023 reorganization into "Local Autonomous Governments" (GAL), area-wide "Zapatista Autonomous Government Collectives" (CGAZ), and zone-wide assemblies represents a deliberate redesign to improve the scaling of governance as communities have grown. This is valuable evidence that even long-running models require structural redesign at transition thresholds.

**The WWII rationing board network** provides a US-specific case study that is under-cited in community resilience literature. By the end of World War II, over 100,000 citizen volunteers administered rationing through approximately 5,600 local boards — "committees of neighbors" that allocated sugar, coffee, meat, gasoline, fuel oil, tires, and shoes. [8] These boards operated at the county and sub-county level, making daily allocation decisions that directly affected household material conditions. They worked because they combined local knowledge (neighbors knew whose needs were genuine and who was hoarding) with federally-set rules (allocation formulas that reduced the need for local judgment on contested questions) and an accountability structure (boards could be reported to federal OPA monitors for malfeasance).

The rationing board model is directly relevant to Midwest community governance because: it was tested in the US rural and small-town context; it operated at the scale of 100–500 households per board; and it worked through an extended disruption (3+ years) without widespread breakdown. Its failure modes are also documented: boards were sometimes captured by local elites who used their position to favor associates; enforcement of violations was inconsistent; and geographic variation in strictness created resentment at community boundaries.

### The Ostrom Framework: Cross-Cutting Governance Principles

Elinor Ostrom's 8 Design Principles for commons governance, derived from field research across more than 800 communities managing shared resources across four continents, provide the most empirically grounded framework for community resource governance at any scale from 50 to 5,000 people. [9] A 2021 review of 91 studies evaluating these principles found strong empirical support across contexts. [10] The principles are:

1. **Clearly defined boundaries** — who is a member and what resources are governed must be unambiguous
2. **Rules match local conditions** — no universal algorithm replaces local knowledge
3. **Collective choice arrangements** — those affected by rules participate in modifying them
4. **Monitoring** — community members or accountable external parties verify rule compliance
5. **Graduated sanctions** — violations meet proportional responses; immediate expulsion creates martyrs
6. **Conflict resolution mechanisms** — low-cost, rapid dispute resolution is available
7. **Minimal recognition of rights** — external authorities recognize the community's right to organize
8. **Nested governance** — for larger systems, governance is organized in nested layers

Principles 2, 5, and 6 are the most consistently violated in informal community governance and the most important to design explicitly. Rules imported from other contexts without customization fail when they conflict with local conditions. Expulsion as the primary sanction polarizes communities rather than correcting behavior. Disputes without formal resolution mechanisms escalate to confrontations that governance systems cannot survive.

The Ostrom framework's design was validated in fisheries, irrigation systems, and forest commons in Switzerland, Japan, Spain, and the Philippines. Subsequent research has extended it to water management, community forests, grazing land, and digital commons. [11] It has been adapted by the Wilson group into behavioral principles applicable to human groups of all kinds, strengthening the theoretical basis for its application to community resilience governance. [12]

## Resource Allocation Under Scarcity

### Need-Based vs. Contribution-Based: The Evidence

The question of how to allocate scarce resources is the most contentious governance problem in extended disruptions. The empirical literature on this is surprisingly clear.

**Need-based allocation is more stable over time.** Contribution-based allocation — each household receives in proportion to what it contributes — is more motivating in early phases and during periods when most community members can contribute. But disruptions produce injured people, sick people, pregnant women, elderly people, and children who cannot contribute at the same rate as healthy working adults. A contribution-based system that begins excluding these groups faces a moral crisis that, in every documented case, is resolved either by incorporating exceptions (which effectively converts the system to need-based with bureaucratic overhead) or by community fracture.

**The transition point is the dangerous moment.** Communities that start contribution-based and then need to shift to need-based as some members become unable to contribute experience significant conflict. Members who have been contributing disproportionately under the contribution model resist paying into a system that now benefits those who have not contributed. The disaster sociology literature is consistent on this: **design the allocation system need-based from the start, with contribution recognition mechanisms (social credit, public acknowledgment, status signals) that do not affect material distribution.** [13]

The WWII rationing system embedded this principle in its point-value system — everyone received the same number of rationing points regardless of income, occupation, or contribution to the war effort, except for specific occupational needs documented to rationing boards. The social status of contribution was maintained through victory gardens, war bond purchases, and public recognition — none of which affected the material allocation.

### Ostrom Principle 2 in Practice: Rule Customization

No community begins a disruption with perfect knowledge of what allocation rules will work for its specific composition. A community of 200 people where 30% are elderly requires different allocation ratios than one with a young average age. A community where 40% of food production comes from three large farms requires different production allocation rules than one with distributed small-plot production.

The practical protocol derived from the Ostrom framework for community food allocation:

1. **Month 1**: Operating rules adopted provisionally by community assembly; designated as trial rules subject to revision after 30 days of operational experience
2. **Month 1–2**: Designated monitors track actual distribution and complaints; document friction points
3. **Month 2**: Community assembly reviews operational data and revises rules based on experience
4. **Month 3+**: Ongoing quarterly review with explicit revision mechanism

The key structural requirement: rules have explicit revision procedures built in from the start. Communities that adopt rules as permanent policies experience more conflict over rules than communities that adopt them as provisional and revisable.

## Accountability: The Hardest Problem

### Why Accountability Fails in Practice

The most consistently documented failure mode in horizontal governance organizations is what community governance practitioners call "accountability theater" — mechanisms that exist on paper but are never used because using them requires confronting trusted community members. This failure is not unique to any ideological orientation; it appears in progressive intentional communities, conservative religious communities, and traditional mutual aid societies.

The structural cause is clear: accountability mechanisms that rely on social confrontation will be avoided in high-trust communities precisely because members value the trust relationships and do not want to damage them through confrontation. The result is that violations that should trigger minor graduated responses instead accumulate until they reach a severity that forces confrontation — at which point the response is rarely graduated. [14]

**Solutions from the evidence base:**

**Regular structured review replaces reactive accountability.** Rather than waiting for violations to trigger accountability responses, regular structured reviews (monthly or quarterly) normalize the examination of decisions and behavior as a routine governance function. The Mondragon Social Council — a worker-elected body that monitors working conditions and management decisions — meets regularly regardless of whether violations have occurred. Its existence normalizes scrutiny without requiring accusations. [15]

**Documentation serves as the first line of accountability.** Communities that maintain records of decisions, reasoning, and outcomes create an accountability structure that operates before human confrontation is required. A documented decision that produced a bad outcome can be evaluated against documented reasoning without requiring that someone be accused of bad faith. Communities without documentation must rely on memory, which is unreliable and contested, forcing conflicts over facts before anyone can evaluate decisions.

**Rotation limits reduce the social cost of accountability.** The Zapatista three-year maximum term for most governance roles reduces the social investment any single person has in maintaining a permanent position. When no one expects to hold a role permanently, the social cost of asking someone to step down is lower and the motivation to protect a position through social pressure is reduced. [16]

**Graduated sanctions must be specifically defined in advance.** Ostrom Principle 5 — graduated sanctions — works only if the gradations are explicitly defined before violations occur. A community that agrees in principle that "violations should be met with proportional responses" but has not defined what proportional means for specific violations will default to either no response (accountability theater) or maximum response (expulsion) when violations occur. The governance agreement must specify: warning for first infraction; formal community review for second; temporary loss of allocation priority or governance role for third; potential exclusion for persistent or severe violations.

## Decision-Making Protocols Under Time Pressure

### The Consensus Problem at Scale

Consensus decision-making — where decisions move forward only when all participants agree — works well at small scales with shared values. It fails above approximately 50 people without structural scaffolding, and it fails entirely in crisis conditions where time pressure, partial information, and high stakes combine. [17]

The failure mode is well-documented: large consensus processes become dominated by the most persistent voices, not the most informed ones. People with strong objections can block decisions indefinitely. Under scarcity pressure, this produces either paralysis (no decision) or illegitimate override (the most forceful person makes the call anyway, undermining the consensus model).

**Structural scaffolding that allows consensus to function at larger scales:**

**Consent (not consensus).** Replacing "does everyone agree?" with "does anyone have a reasoned objection?" changes the decision threshold. Proposals move forward if no one objects with a specific, reasoned concern. This is the sociocratic consent model, documented extensively in intentional communities of 20–200 people. [18] It is more efficient than consensus while maintaining more genuine participation than majority vote.

**Time limits on discussion.** Facilitators with explicit authority to enforce discussion time limits prevent the most persistent participants from dominating. The Rojava commune model separates facilitation from deliberation — the person managing process is not simultaneously advocating a position.

**Decision categorization.** Before any community meeting begins, decisions on the agenda are categorized as Type 1 (household-affecting: informs relevant households, no community decision required), Type 2 (community-affecting: requires community assembly approval), or Type 3 (inter-community-affecting: requires delegate coordination before community assembly vote). This prevents single-household concerns from occupying community assembly time and prevents community-wide decisions from being made by the subset of people who showed up to a given meeting.

**Standing delegation for operational decisions.** Not every operational decision requires assembly review. Designated domain leads — water manager, food coordinator, medical lead, security coordinator, information coordinator — have explicit operational authority within their domain for decisions that do not cross domain lines or exceed defined resource thresholds. Decisions that cross domains or exceed thresholds return to the assembly. This mirrors the Zapatista cargo system and the Mondragon working group model.

### Failure Mode: Governance Under Active Conflict

The most challenging governance scenario is not scarcity but internal conflict between community factions. Scarce resources create stress but generally unite communities against a shared external problem. Internal conflict divides communities against each other.

The Barcelona en Comú experiment (2015–2023) provides instructive evidence from a large-scale participatory democracy. Over eight years, the platform distributed approximately €30 million through participatory budgeting, ran hundreds of participatory processes, and engaged over 100,000 registered users on the Decidim platform. [19] The most significant challenges were not resource allocation but legitimacy maintenance — as the platform turned from activist organization to governing body, recruitment of activists slowed, and processes designed for horizontal input were increasingly captured by organized interest groups capable of mobilizing participation more efficiently than unorganized citizens.

The community-scale lesson: participatory processes require active maintenance investment, particularly in ensuring that participation is not captured by organized minorities. A 100-person community with five people who attend every meeting and 95 who attend occasionally will be governed by the five in practice even if the formal structure is fully democratic. Governance design must include mechanisms for reaching the non-attending majority: written updates, rotating participation requirements, direct household consultation on major decisions.

## Conflict Resolution: Before Governance Breaks

### Conflict as a System Input, Not a System Failure

Every extended community faces internal conflict. Treating conflict as a failure of community cohesion rather than a normal input into community governance produces two bad outcomes: first, conflict is suppressed rather than processed, building pressure toward eventual explosion; second, the governance system is designed without conflict resolution mechanisms, so when conflict occurs the community has no established process for handling it.

The literature on community conflict resolution is consistent on several structural points:

**Conflict resolution must be faster and cheaper than exit.** If the easiest way to resolve a conflict is for one party to leave the community, conflict resolution will produce a constant attrition of community members during disputes. The process for resolving disputes must be accessible (no lengthy procedure required to initiate), fast (resolution within days or weeks, not months), and low-cost in social capital (parties are not permanently marked as having been in a dispute).

**Peer mediators outperform formal arbitrators in small communities.** External arbitrators — including formal legal processes — are not accessible during infrastructure disruptions and produce outcomes that neither party helped design, reducing compliance. Trained peer mediators — community members with basic mediation training and community-conferred authority to facilitate resolution processes — produce agreements that parties have shaped and therefore support. [20]

**The process matters as much as the outcome.** Communities where dispute resolution is perceived as fair (all parties were heard, the process followed established rules, the outcome was explained) accept outcomes they disagree with at higher rates than communities where the process is perceived as arbitrary. This finding from legal psychology and restorative justice research is directly applicable to community governance: invest in process design, not just outcome formulas. [21]

**Pre-crisis relationship building is conflict prevention.** Communities with higher social capital — measured by trust, shared history, cooperative experience — experience lower rates of conflict escalation during disruptions. [22] The most valuable conflict prevention investment happens before any conflict: joint projects, shared celebration, cross-household relationship building, regular gatherings that are not governance-focused. This is not soft: it is the infrastructure on which conflict resolution depends when it is needed.

## Implementation: Building Governance Before It Is Needed

### Phase 1: Foundation (Months 1–6, Before Any Crisis)

**Governance begins with relationships, not documents.** The most common error in community governance planning is jumping to formal structures before relationships and shared understanding exist. A governance document written by three people and presented to the community is not community governance; it is a governance proposal from three people.

**Month 1–2:**
- Convene community governance conversations (not decision-making meetings) in settings that allow genuine dialogue: dinner gatherings, work projects, informal discussions
- Identify natural leaders, domain experts, and respected mediators through observation rather than election — who do people turn to for advice in different domains?
- Inventory existing governance experience: which community members have cooperative governance, labor union, agricultural cooperative, or formal organizational experience?

**Month 3–4:**
- Draft provisional governance agreement collaboratively — all households contribute to at least one section
- Adopt decision categorization system (Type 1, 2, 3 as described above)
- Identify domain leads through community nomination process; confirm for 6-month trial periods
- Establish regular meeting cadence before any crisis pressure exists

**Month 5–6:**
- Complete first full resource inventory (food, water, energy, medical, skills — worksheets in this document)
- Document conflict resolution process; identify and provisionally designate peer mediators
- Test governance systems through low-stakes decisions; identify friction points before they are crisis-critical

### Phase 2: Formalization (Months 6–18)

- Adopt formal governance agreement by community assembly; review and revise annually
- Establish community records system (physical and backed-up digital)
- Train domain leads and peer mediators formally; cross-train backups
- Develop emergency decision-making protocol for situations where the full assembly cannot be convened

### Phase 3: Integration (Year 2+)

- Conduct first full governance review including accountability assessment
- Evaluate whether community size has crossed any transition thresholds and adapt structure accordingly
- Establish inter-community coordination channels with adjacent communities

## Decision Trees for Community Context

### Which Governance Model for Your Community?

**Decision Tree A: Selecting a governance structure**

```
Is your community currently fewer than 50 people?
  YES → Informal consensus with one-page written agreement on three
        most contested domains (resource allocation, emergency authority,
        exit terms). Schedule formal governance adoption at 100 people.
  NO → Continue below.

Is your community 50–150 people?
  YES → Domain-specialist authority with community assembly ratification
        for major decisions. Consent-based (not consensus) decision-
        making with defined time limits. Rotation limits for domain leads.
  NO → Continue below.

Is your community 150–500 people?
  YES → Delegate council model. Community divides into sub-units of
        80–150 for direct assembly. Delegates carry sub-unit decisions
        to coordination council. Formal written records required.
  NO → Community is 500–1,000 people.
        → Federated sub-units (see above). Each sub-unit has full
          governance autonomy within its domain; cross-unit coordination
          through delegate council with explicit scope limits.
```

**Decision Tree B: Handling a contested resource allocation**

```
Is the resource individually consumed (food, medicine, personal fuel)?
  YES → Apply need-based formula with provisionally adopted rules.
        No means-testing required for basic allocations. Flag edge cases
        for monthly review.

Is the resource shared infrastructure (water system, generator, tools)?
  YES → Domain lead has operational authority within defined parameters.
        Major maintenance decisions (>$500 equivalent) require assembly
        notification if not approval. Emergency decisions by domain lead
        documented and reviewed within 72 hours.

Is the resource a one-time windfall (donated supplies, traded goods)?
  YES → Community assembly decides allocation by consent process.
        Default: need-based with labor contribution recognition noted.

Is the allocation being contested by a specific household?
  YES → Initiate conflict resolution process. Peer mediator convenes
        within 24 hours. Resolution within 7 days before next allocation
        cycle. Assembly review only if mediation fails.
```

## Failure Mode Catalog

### Failure Mode 1: The Single-Point Leader

**Pattern**: One person becomes the operational authority for multiple domains, either through competence (others defer to their expertise) or through ambition (they insert themselves into domains beyond their mandate). After 12–18 months, the community's operational knowledge is concentrated in one person, and that person's incapacity, departure, or corruption becomes a community-level crisis.

**Prevention**: Strict domain separation with documented cross-training. Backup designees for every critical role. Regular rotation with overlap period for knowledge transfer.

**Recovery (if already established)**: Structured transition — identify backup designees, document current state of all systems, establish 90-day knowledge transfer period before leadership change. Do not attempt sudden removal of a single-point leader during a crisis.

### Failure Mode 2: Governance Capture by an Organized Minority

**Pattern**: A small group of 5–15 people attends every meeting, proposes most decisions, and gradually controls community governance even though the formal structure remains "democratic." The majority of community members experience governance as happening without their meaningful participation.

**Prevention**: Rotating facilitation (no person facilitates more than 3 consecutive meetings). Decisions documented and distributed to all households, not just those who attended. Quorum requirements for major decisions. Direct household consultation for significant resource allocation changes.

**Recovery**: Mandate rotation of roles; introduce direct-consultation mechanisms for specific decision types; investigate whether attendance barriers exist (meeting time, location, language, disability) and remove them.

### Failure Mode 3: Accountability Freeze

**Pattern**: A governance role-holder makes poor decisions, misallocates resources, or violates community norms. The community knows this but takes no action because the person is liked, because confrontation would cause social pain, or because the accountability mechanism is unclear. The frozen accountability accumulates into an explosive crisis.

**Prevention**: Regular structured reviews as a routine governance function (not triggered by violations). Documented graduated sanctions defined before any violation occurs. Destigmatized reporting mechanism that does not require direct accusation.

**Recovery**: Convene a structured accountability review — not a confrontation, but a process-governed examination of specific documented decisions. Focus on decisions and outcomes rather than character or intent. The goal is correction and restoration of community trust, not punishment.

### Failure Mode 4: The 150-Person Transition Cliff

**Pattern**: A community grows from 80 to 250 people (through expansion, merger, or refugee absorption during crisis) and its governance systems, designed for informal trust, break simultaneously. No one recognizes the structural cause; the community attributes the governance failures to personal conflicts, bad leadership, or external pressure.

**Prevention**: Anticipate and plan the 150-person transition before it occurs. Know what governance structures will replace current informal systems when the threshold is crossed.

**Recovery**: Pause community expansion temporarily if possible. Convene a governance design process with the explicit goal of designing structures appropriate to the new scale. Be explicit with the community that the old governance worked at the old scale and structural change is not a failure.

## Regional Context: Midwest Rural Specifics

Zone 5 Midwest communities attempting to implement these governance structures face specific contextual factors that modify standard frameworks:

**Existing governance infrastructure**: Most Midwest rural communities have existing quasi-governance structures — township boards, county extension offices, rural fire departments, church councils, farm bureau chapters — that provide both models and potential adversaries for new community governance. Designing in relationship to existing structures (rather than treating them as irrelevant) is more likely to succeed.

**Distance and weather barriers**: Community assemblies that require physical attendance in a Midwest winter have lower participation than those with remote participation options. Governance design must account for periods of 2–8 weeks when roads are impassable or conditions make travel dangerous. Pre-distributed written decision packets, radio-based assembly participation protocols, and documented proxy authority are not optional redundancies but core governance infrastructure.

**Agricultural labor rhythms**: Governance meeting cadence that ignores planting and harvest seasons will fail. April through June and August through October are periods of maximum labor intensity and minimum governance capacity. Quarterly reviews that fall in October are competing with harvest; move them to December and March.

**Social homogeneity and heterogeneity**: Many Midwest rural communities are demographically similar in ways that reduce some governance challenges (shared cultural frameworks, shared language) while creating others (less experience with formal governance processes, less practice with explicit conflict resolution, stronger norms against public disagreement). Governance designs that rely on explicit verbal objection as the accountability mechanism will be less effective in communities with strong norms against public dissent; written proposal-and-response mechanisms provide accountability without requiring public confrontation.

## Cross-Domain Dependencies

**Governance and Food Systems (Domain 1 / Phase 3 Document 02)**: The food allocation system is the most likely trigger for governance stress during extended disruptions. Governance design must specify food allocation authority explicitly — who decides, by what process, with what accountability mechanisms — before food scarcity exists.

**Governance and Information Infrastructure (Domain 3)**: Governance decisions that cannot be communicated to all community members are not actually community decisions. Information infrastructure is the substrate on which governance operates. Every governance meeting cadence and decision distribution protocol must have a corresponding communication method that reaches all households.

**Governance and Healthcare (Domain 4)**: Medical triage and priority during scarce resource conditions requires governance-level authority. Who decides how medical resources are allocated when they are insufficient? This decision cannot be delegated to the medical domain lead alone — it requires community-level input and legitimacy. Build the decision process before it is needed.
