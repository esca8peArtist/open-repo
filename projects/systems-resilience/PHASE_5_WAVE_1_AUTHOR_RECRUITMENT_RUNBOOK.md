## Section 1: Executive Summary

### Purpose

Phase 5 Waves 1+2 (45,380 words, 5 domains, publication-ready June 5, 2026) establishes the technical and governance foundation for community resilience at the 50–1,000 person scale. Wave 1 author recruitment expands this foundation by inviting 18 practitioners and researchers to contribute articles across the five Phase 3 community-scale domains:

1. Governance and decision-making
2. Food systems and supply chain
3. Information infrastructure
4. Security and defense
5. Scaling pathways and thresholds

These contributors are not paid writers. They are domain experts — researchers, practitioners, cooperative developers, emergency managers — who are invited to contribute an article (3,000–6,000 words) to the published corpus under a CC BY-SA open license. The exchange value: they gain attribution in a 45,000-word publicly-released knowledge base, exposure to a practitioner-facing audience, and the opportunity to shape how their domain expertise reaches communities actively building resilience.

## Section 4: Verification Procedure (5 Minutes Per Contact)

Before sending on June 5, run each contact through this 5-minute check. Log results in the tracking spreadsheet (Section 8 format).

### Verification Steps

**Step 1: Email address format check (30 seconds)**
- Confirm the email address matches a verifiable institutional or public-facing domain
- Cross-reference against the author's current faculty page, LinkedIn, or organization website
- Flag any addresses that are >12 months old (faculty move institutions; check for redirect messages)
- Mark: VERIFIED / UNVERIFIED / UPDATE NEEDED

**Step 2: Current affiliation check (90 seconds)**
- Google "[Author Name] [Current Institution]" to confirm they are still at the listed affiliation
- Check for recent publications, news mentions, or conference presentations (2024–2026) to confirm active status
- Mark: ACTIVE / POSSIBLY MOVED / EMERITUS/RETIRED

**Step 3: Deliverability spot-check (60 seconds)**
- For institutional addresses (@university.edu): these are almost always deliverable; no additional check needed
- For personal domains or organization emails (@org.net): check that the domain resolves (browser visit to the domain)
- For contact form submissions (La Via Campesina, Max Rameau): note "contact form only" in tracking log

**Step 4: Response deadline alignment (30 seconds)**
- Note in the tracking log: response deadline is June 15 09:00 UTC
- If author is in a non-US time zone, convert: June 15 09:00 UTC = June 15 05:00 EDT / June 15 11:00 CEST / June 15 19:00 JST
- Note time zone in log

**Step 5: Personalization check (90 seconds)**
- Confirm the specific personalization element in your email is accurate (e.g., the paper you reference is actually their paper, not a colleague's)
- Confirm you're using the correct template for their audience type (V1/V2/V3)
- Mark: READY TO SEND / PERSONALIZATION NEEDS UPDATE

## Section 6: Domain-Author Pairing Rationale

### Domain 1 — Governance: Why These Authors

**Michael McGinnis** is the operational choice: he spent 30+ years as an Ostrom Workshop researcher and has published extensively on how polycentric governance institutions function at community scale. Phase 3 Domain 1 is explicitly built on Ostrom's design principles; McGinnis can add the critical next layer — what happens when communities attempt to implement those principles and where they fail. His institutional email is publicly listed and he is actively publishing as of 2024.

**Ted Scholte / Sociocracy For All** provides what McGinnis cannot: practitioner implementation experience. Sociocracy is a working implementation of the delegate council model at the center of Phase 3's governance framework. Scholte's contribution would be the "how it actually works on the ground" that McGinnis's scholarship describes from the outside.

**Aaron Vansintjan** provides a different angle: how commons governance transitions play out in post-growth and community economics contexts — the political-economic backdrop against which Phase 3 governance is being deployed.

### Domain 2 — Food Systems: Why These Authors

**Saria Lofton** brings the mutual aid lens to food systems — how communities coordinate food access when market systems fail. Her published research (PMC8458839 on mutual aid organizations and food insecurity in Chicago) is directly applicable to Phase 3 Domain 2's supply chain coordination questions.

**Lauren Gwin** contributes food systems resilience research framing from a university extension context — she understands the practitioner-facing synthesis challenge and has done it successfully in peer-reviewed publication.

**Jack Kloppenburg** adds the seed sovereignty and food commons dimension that Phase 3 Domain 2 gestures at but does not develop: who controls seed stock is a governance question as much as a technical one. Kloppenburg has been the foundational voice on this for 30 years.

### Domain 3 — Information Infrastructure: Why These Authors

**Greta Byrum** is the leading practitioner on community mesh networks in the US context. Her New York-based work is the most documented example of a community network built and governed by non-technical community members — the exact use case Phase 3 Domain 3 addresses.

**Chris Puttick / Rhizomatica** adds the rural and off-grid dimension. Rhizomatica's community mobile networks in areas without commercial coverage have more in common with Zone 5 rural connectivity challenges than any urban mesh project.

**Primavera De Filippi** provides the theoretical infrastructure for understanding why community information infrastructure must be governed as a commons — her work on digital commons and governance failures of centralized platforms is the legitimizing framework.

### Domain 4 — Security and Defense: Why These Authors

**Daniel Aldrich** reframes the security question in the most useful way possible: his empirical research across dozens of disasters shows that communities with stronger social networks recover faster and lose fewer members than communities with more physical security hardware. This is both empirically grounded and directly actionable — building social infrastructure is within community reach; militarizing is not.

**Rachel MacNair** provides the conflict resolution and non-violent community safety literature that makes Phase 3 Domain 4 credible to a wide audience, including communities who would not engage with militia-adjacent framing.

**Max Rameau / Pan-African Community Action** contributes the community-of-color, mutual aid-centric framing for community safety that is essential for the corpus to reach beyond its most likely initial audience (rural white homesteaders).

### Domain 5 — Scaling Pathways: Why These Authors

**Tom Henfrey** is the direct match. His co-edited volume "Resilience, Community Action and Societal Transformation" covers exactly what Phase 3 Domain 5 addresses — how Transition and permaculture communities move from single-group action to regional coordination. He is both a published academic and an active practitioner.

**Ethan Miller** provides the solidarity economics framing for scaling that the Phase 3 document alludes to (Mondragon, cooperative models) but does not develop: how communities build cooperative economic infrastructure that scales without concentrating power.

**Shareable / Neal Gorenflo** contributes documented case studies of communities at scale — Shareable's archive is one of the few repositories of verified, narrative-form accounts of communities that successfully navigated governance transitions.

### Platform Agnosticism Confirmed

**The platform choice (Nextcloud+Matrix vs. Discourse) has zero effect on this recruitment process.** Outreach is conducted by email. Author onboarding uses email attachments (domain brief, source library PDF or Markdown files). Collaboration tooling is not relevant until after the go/no-go decision (June 15) and the author onboarding completion (post-June 20).

- How the final articles are published and cross-referenced
- Community discussion infrastructure once the corpus is live

The platform choice does NOT affect:
- Who is recruited
- What email templates are used
- The June 5 send date
- The June 15 go/no-go decision
- Author compensation or attribution model

**If platform decision is delayed past June 3 23:59 UTC**: Proceed with outreach on June 5 regardless. The onboarding kit references "collaboration platform (to be confirmed)" until the platform decision is locked.

### Attribution Model

All contributors receive:
- Named authorship in the corpus ("Contribution by [Name], [Title], [Affiliation]")
- CC BY-SA 4.0 license on their contribution
- Option to include a 50-word bio and link to their primary work
- Listed in the Phase 5 Wave 1 contributor acknowledgments

No cash compensation is offered in the initial outreach. If an academic or organization requests payment, the response is: "We're a volunteer-maintained open-knowledge project. We can't offer cash compensation at this time, but your contribution would be CC BY-SA licensed and fully attributed — the same license as the rest of the corpus."

### Tone Guidance

The corpus is grounded, practical, and non-ideological in framing — it serves a politically diverse audience (rural conservatives, urban progressives, religious communities, secular cooperatives). Recruitment emails should reflect this:
- Do not frame the project as anti-capitalist, even when recruiting from explicitly anti-capitalist networks
- Do not frame it as preparedness-for-collapse — the framing is "extended disruption of critical infrastructure," which is politically neutral and accurate
- Domain 4 (Security/Defense) is the most sensitive: avoid militia-adjacent language; use "community safety" and "social capital" framing

The specific email addresses are plausible institutional formats but **must be verified against live faculty/staff directories before sending**.
