# Phase 6 Candidate: Community Economic Resilience

## What This Is

**Community Economic Resilience** focuses on the economic structures and local financial systems that allow communities to sustain themselves when national supply chains, currency systems, and central banking infrastructure fail. This domain asks: how do small communities trade goods and services, store value, allocate capital, and coordinate economic activity when traditional money no longer works?

This is the **economic operating system** of resilience — the answer to "how does a community that's fed, watered, and sheltered actually organize work and exchange?"

---

## Research Scope

This domain would research and answer three core questions:

1. **What economic architectures sustain when the dollar fails?**
   - Local currencies, mutual credit systems, and barter protocols that function without central banking
   - Case studies: Complementary currency adoption (Bristol Pounds, WIR system in Switzerland, timebanks in Japan)
   - Design principles for currency systems in small communities (200–10,000 people)

2. **How do communities allocate capital and coordinate production without market signals?**
   - Gift economies, commons-based resource allocation, Elinor Ostrom's design principles for common-pool resources
   - Enterprise models: cooperatives, land trusts, mutual aid networks
   - Decision-making structures that replace price signals (consensus, participatory budgeting, ranked preference voting)
   - Midwest case studies: agricultural cooperatives, tool-share networks, local food enterprises

3. **What economic bridges prevent community isolation?**
   - Inter-community trade networks and federation models
   - Specialized production (what does one community make that others need?) and comparative advantage without global supply chains
   - Barter protocols and exchange value metrics for non-monetary trade
   - Regional economic integration: how 5–10 communities coordinate supply (surplus grain from rural area feeds urban community; urban community provides manufactured goods back)

## Why This One

### Unique Leverage

**Market Gap**: The resilience literature has extensive coverage of food production, water systems, and energy infrastructure. But very little rigorous research on the economic operating systems of communities. This domain fills a specific gap.

**Cross-Domain Bridge**: Economic resilience is downstream of ALL other Phase 1–5 domains. A household with food and water still needs to ask: "How do we exchange what we produce for what we can't?" Economic architecture determines whether specialized labor (medics, teachers, metalworkers) is sustainable.

**Operationalization Ready**: This domain produces immediately actionable templates (how to design a local currency, how to set up a cooperative, how to create a commons management charter). Phase 7 operationalization work can use these directly.

### Complementary to Existing Work

- Phase 3 covered *governance* (decision-making structures). This domain covers *economic governance* (how decisions allocate resources and determine exchange value).
- Phase 5 Wave 2 covered *psychological support and conflict resolution*. Economic systems prevent conflict (clear allocation rules) and enable psychological resilience (everyone has valued work).
- Phase 4 covered *power mapping*. This domain extends it to economic power and capital accumulation in small communities.

### Market Relevance

The Midwest has a strong cooperative tradition (agricultural, manufacturing, credit unions). Communities are actively exploring timebanking, tool libraries, and local currencies (especially post-pandemic). Demand signal is clear.
