## 1. Domain A Scope

### What This Document Is

Domain A is the economic operating system for Phase 6. Phase 5 documents what communities produce — food, water, energy, governance, psychological support, veterinary care. Phase 5 does not answer the harder question that follows production: who gets what, who decides, and how surplus is managed when the dollar fails.

Domain A answers that question across four scales:

- **Household to neighborhood**: how individuals and families exchange goods and services without cash (barter networks, timebanks, mutual credit ledgers, gift economies with reciprocity norms)
- **Neighborhood to community**: how a community of 25–500 people organizes economic institutions — worker cooperatives, food cooperatives, credit unions, community land trusts, commons governance structures
- **Community to region**: how neighboring communities establish trade relationships, comparative advantage frameworks, and regional exchange architectures when global supply chains are unavailable
- **Community to policy**: how communities access federal and state programs (USDA Rural Cooperative Development Grant, IRS 501(c)(3)/cooperative tax status, state enabling legislation) to formalize and protect economic institutions

### The Three Core Research Questions

Every section of Domain A must, in some measure, contribute an answer to one of three questions:

1. What economic architectures allow communities to coordinate production and consumption without functional currency or financial markets?
2. How do communities allocate scarce goods and skilled labor equitably when market price signals are absent or distorted?
3. How do neighboring communities establish durable trade relationships without global supply chains — and what governance structures make those relationships stable?

### Zone 5 Midwest Context

Domain A is not a general treatise on alternative economics. It is grounded in Zone 5 (USDA Hardiness Zone 5 — Iowa, Illinois, Minnesota, Wisconsin, Missouri, and southern Michigan). This context shapes:

- **Agricultural cooperative emphasis**: Zone 5 has the densest agricultural cooperative infrastructure in the United States (Land O'Lakes, Organic Valley, GROWMARK, Dairy Farmers of America). The cooperative tradition is culturally legible to rural Zone 5 readers in a way it is not to urban coastal readers.
- **Seasonal economic rhythms**: Zone 5 communities face a distinctive seasonal pattern — spring capital needs (seed, fuel, equipment repair), summer surplus (produce, dairy), fall harvest and preservation demands, winter scarcity and mutual aid pressure. Economic institutions must be designed for these rhythms.
- **Rural county infrastructure gaps**: Many Zone 5 rural counties have populations under 5,000. Credit union chartering, cooperative incorporation, and USDA Rural Development grant access all have different feasibility profiles at this scale than in urban contexts. The document must address the small-population case directly.
- **Existing cooperative extension infrastructure**: Iowa State, University of Wisconsin-Madison, University of Minnesota, and University of Illinois all have cooperative extension services with Zone 5 practitioner resources. These are Tier 3 sources and should be cited for local specificity.

### Pre-Research Chapter (Already Complete)

The pre-research chapter is at `phase-6/01-community-economic-resilience.md` — 6,800 words, 38 verified citations, committed to master. It covers: economic failure propagation mechanics, cooperative models (worker cooperatives, food cooperatives), credit unions, mutual aid networks, complementary currencies (WIR, LETS, timebanking, local currencies), international case studies (Argentina 2001, Mondragon, Emilia-Romagna), USDA policy landscape, and a minimum viable implementation template.

### Relationship to Domains B, E, and F

- **Domain B (Institutional Governance)** builds the decision-making architecture that operates on top of Domain A's economic institutions. Cooperative governance and commons governance are the overlap zone. Domain A should note, without duplicating, that Section 3 of Domain B (governance failure modes) documents what happens when Domain A economic institutions lose legitimacy.

### Workstream 1: Economic Failure Mechanics and Distribution of Impacts

**Final document sections**: Section 1 (Economic Failure Scenarios) — approximately 8,000–10,000 words

**Research questions this workstream must answer**:
- What mechanisms cause economic failure to propagate from financial markets to household food security in Zone 5 rural communities? (Not the same as urban communities — rural Zone 5 communities have less cash dependence but more fuel and equipment dependency.)
- How do supply chain disruptions affect rural communities differently from urban communities, and what economic institutions offer the most resilience at each shock type?
- What is the documented timeline of community economic response after crisis — when do formal institutions fail, when do informal institutions emerge, and what determines whether informal institutions stabilize or collapse?

**Source targets** (in priority order):

1. Elinor Ostrom, *Governing the Commons* (Cambridge University Press, 1990) — foundational for understanding how communities manage shared resources without markets; the eight design principles are the structural backbone of Sections 5 and 6 but the failure analysis in Chapter 2 is directly relevant to Section 1
2. USDA Economic Research Service (ERS) rural household income and expenditure data — authoritative on rural Zone 5 economic baseline; ERS Rural America at a Glance (annual) provides county-level data on cash dependency, agricultural income share, and off-farm income dependence; accessible at ers.usda.gov
3. Charles Perrow, *Normal Accidents* (Princeton University Press, 1984; updated 1999) — supply chain failure cascade mechanics; tightly coupled vs. loosely coupled systems analysis maps directly to the economic infrastructure failure scenarios in Section 1
4. Adam Tooze, *Crashed* (Viking, 2018) — 2008 financial crisis propagation mechanics with rural economy impact data; more recent than earlier crisis literature and more empirically grounded for Zone 5 readers than theoretical models
5. Federal Reserve Bank of Kansas City, *The Rural Mainstreet Index* (quarterly, 2015–2026) — primary data on rural Midwest economic conditions; free, well-maintained, directly Zone 5 applicable; quarterly releases through Q1 2026 should be available
6. Argentina barter club documentation (Red de Trueque, 2001–2004): Collom, Ed. "Community Currency in the United States: The Social Environments in which it Emerges and Survives." *Environment and Planning A* 37(9): 1565–1587, 2005 — documents how informal exchange emerged as formal economy collapsed; the 3 million participants / 400-node scale is the largest documented case of community currency replacing formal markets in a modern economy
7. David Graeber, *Debt: The First 5,000 Years* (Melville House, 2011) — historical analysis of what economic coordination looks like without functional money; Part IV (the crisis years, 1450–1971) is directly applicable to Section 1's framing of dollar failure scenarios

**Expected word count from this workstream**: 8,000–10,000 words
**Estimated research hours**: 6–8 hours
**Key gap to document explicitly**: Zone 5-specific rural supply chain data is available in ERS publications and Federal Reserve Kansas City reports but is not synthesized with the crisis literature. The synthesis is the value-add — do not assume the reader can make the connection independently.

### Workstream 2: Community Economic Institutions — Cooperatives, Credit Unions, and Mutual Aid

**Final document sections**: Sections 2 and 3 (Community Economic Institutions; Policy and Reform Landscape) — approximately 12,000–15,000 words

**Research questions this workstream must answer**:
- What are the specific formation requirements, governance structures, and operational mechanics of the four primary community economic institutions (worker cooperatives, food cooperatives, credit unions, mutual aid networks)?

- What are the specific USDA Rural Cooperative Development Grant application requirements, eligibility criteria, and Zone 5 precedents? What documentation must a community prepare before approaching a cooperative development center?
- What state-level barriers (incorporation law variation, credit union chartering, land trust enabling legislation) affect Zone 5 communities specifically?

**Source targets** (in priority order):

1. National Center for Employee Ownership (NCEO), *Employee Ownership and Economic Security* (2020 meta-analysis) — synthesizes 200+ studies on cooperative vs. investor-owned firm performance; specific data on job retention during recessions, wage levels, and benefit provision; accessible at nceo.org
2. USDA Rural Business-Cooperative Service (RBCS), *Rural Cooperative Development Grant Program* documentation — authoritative on federal programs; current NOFA, eligibility requirements, and grantee list (searchable by state); accessible at rd.usda.gov/programs-services/business-programs/rural-cooperative-development-grant-program
3. NCBA CLUSA (National Cooperative Business Association), *Cooperative Economy 2024* — annual data on US cooperative formation rates, sector breakdown, and asset size; the most current aggregate US cooperative data available
4. National Federation of Community Development Credit Unions (Inclusiv), *CDCU Data and Statistics* (2024 annual report) — specific data on credit union formation in low-income and rural communities; includes Zone 5 state-level data on rural credit union chartering
5. Food Co-op Initiative, *Food Co-op Impact Report 2024* — primary data on the $1.604 local economic multiplier (vs. $1.00 for conventional grocery); includes Zone 5 food cooperative case studies
6. Grounded Solutions Network, *Community Land Trust Database* — searchable database of 42+ Zone 5 CLTs; provides Zone 5 inventory data and formation precedents; accessible at groundedsolutions.org/tools-resources/clt-database
7. Illinois Institute for Rural Affairs (Western Illinois University), *Cooperative Development Resources* — Zone 5-specific cooperative formation guides, including state cooperative incorporation law comparison for IL, IA, MN, WI, MO; accessible at iira.org
8. Ostrom, Elinor, and Charlotte Hess, eds. *Understanding Knowledge as a Commons* (MIT Press, 2007) — extends Ostrom's commons governance principles to knowledge and information; directly applicable to the commons governance section
9. Lincoln Institute of Land Policy, *Community Land Trusts: Understanding Who They Serve* (2020) — Champlain Housing Trust and Homestead CLT case study data; CLT formation requirements by state

**Expected word count from this workstream**: 12,000–15,000 words
**Estimated research hours**: 7–9 hours

### Workstream 3: Complementary Currency Systems and Mutual Credit

**Final document sections**: Section 4 (Complementary Currencies and Mutual Credit) — approximately 7,000–9,000 words

**Research questions this workstream must answer**:
- What are the four primary complementary currency models (WIR mutual credit, LETS local exchange trading systems, timebanking, local fiat currencies), and what are the specific design decisions that determine whether each model succeeds or fails?
- What is the quantitative evidence on complementary currency effectiveness? (This is a literature with honest uncertainty — document the methodological limits of the evaluation literature, not just the positive findings.)
- How does a community design a complementary currency for Zone 5 conditions — specifically addressing seasonal economic rhythms, small population size (under 5,000), and integration with agricultural cooperative structures?
- What governance structures are required for a complementary currency to maintain legitimacy over time? (Refer to Domain B's governance failure analysis for the political economy of currency legitimacy failures.)

**Source targets** (in priority order):

1. WIR Bank annual reports (WIR Genossenschaftsbank, Basel, 1934–2024) — 90 years of primary operational data on mutual credit at community scale; WIR's counter-cyclical behavior (usage expands in recessions, contracts in booms) is the most important empirical finding about complementary currency mechanics; annual reports available at wir.ch
2. Bernard Lietaer and Jacqui Dunne, *Rethinking Money* (Berrett-Koehler, 2013) — the most comprehensive practitioner-accessible treatment of complementary currency design principles; Chapters 6–8 directly applicable to Zone 5 design questions
3. Collom, Ed. "Community Currency in the United States: The Social Environments in which it Emerges and Survives." *Environment and Planning A* 37(9): 1565–1587, 2005 — primary empirical study on US LETS and timebank survival rates; documents which social conditions predict success vs. failure
4. Timebanks USA, *National Data Report* (annual) — primary data on US timebank scale, member demographics, exchange volumes, and retention rates; Timebanks USA methodology note is important for honest uncertainty documentation
5. New Economics Foundation (NEF) and Nesta Foundation, *Timebanking: An Exchange with a Difference* (2008) + *Timebanking: A Practical Guide* (2010) — most comprehensive UK evaluation literature on timebanking; the UK has higher density of well-documented timebanks than the US; some findings transfer to Zone 5 context, some do not (note differences explicitly)
6. University of Bristol, *Bristol Pound Evaluation: Summary Report* (2015) — independent evaluation of the Bristol Pound local currency; provides the most rigorous methodology of any local currency impact evaluation; the $1.15 local circulation multiplier is the primary quantitative finding
7. Schumacher, E.F. *Small Is Beautiful: Economics as if People Mattered* (Harper Perennial, 1973/1989) — intermediate technology economics framework; Chapter 4 (Buddhist economics) and Chapter 5 (a question of size) provide the theoretical scaffolding for the scale-appropriate institution design the domain advocates

**Expected word count from this workstream**: 7,000–9,000 words
**Estimated research hours**: 5–6 hours
**Key gap to document explicitly**: The timebanking effectiveness literature has honest methodological problems — most evaluations lack control groups, rely on member self-report, and conflate economic exchange value with social cohesion benefits. The domain should document this uncertainty explicitly rather than overclaiming. The WIR Bank data is the most rigorous because it has 90 years of operational ledger data; the timebanking data is the least rigorous. State this distinction clearly.

### Workstream 4: International Case Studies — Argentina, Mondragon, Emilia-Romagna

**Final document sections**: Section 5 (International Case Studies) — approximately 8,000–10,000 words

**Research questions this workstream must answer**:
- What can the Argentina 2001 barter club network (Red de Trueque) teach Zone 5 communities about rapid informal exchange network formation? What caused the network's peak-to-collapse trajectory, and what would have extended its useful life?
- What governance and ownership structures explain Mondragon Cooperative Corporation's survival through the 2008 financial crisis (which was devastating to the Spanish Basque economy) when investor-owned competitors failed?
- What specific policies did Emilia-Romagna (Italy) implement to build the cooperative economy that now represents 30% of regional GDP? Can Zone 5 communities replicate any of these at smaller scale without regional government support?
- What is the range of documented outcomes from community economic institution building — including failures — so Zone 5 practitioners have a realistic picture of the risk-reward profile?

**Source targets** (in priority order):

1. Dinerstein, Ana Cecilia. *The Politics of Autonomy in Latin America: The Art of Organising Hope* (Palgrave Macmillan, 2014) — documents Argentina 2001 economic crisis political economy and the emergence of piqueteros and barter clubs as autonomous economic forms; Chapter 5 directly covers the Red de Trueque at its peak
2. Mondragon Corporation, *Annual Report 2023* — primary data on worker cooperative performance through post-pandemic period; current employment (80,000+), revenue, sectoral breakdown, and governance structure; accessible at mondragon.com
3. Whyte, William Foote, and Kathleen King Whyte. *Making Mondragon: The Growth and Dynamics of the Worker Cooperative Complex* (ILR Press, 1991; revised 2008) — the authoritative English-language account of Mondragon's founding and governance structure; still the most detailed treatment of how the Mondragon federation governance model developed
4. Legacoop (Lega Nazionale delle Cooperative e Mutue), *Emilia-Romagna Regional Cooperative Report* (2022) — primary data on the Emilia-Romagna cooperative economy; 30% regional GDP figure; sectoral breakdown of cooperative activity; accessible at legacoop.it
5. Putnam, Robert D., Robert Leonardi, and Raffaella Nanetti. *Making Democracy Work: Civic Traditions in Modern Italy* (Princeton University Press, 1993) — the foundational study on why Emilia-Romagna's cooperative institutions succeeded while southern Italy's failed; the social capital argument is directly applicable to Zone 5 community economic institution building
6. Schneiberg, Marc. "What's on the Path? Path Dependence, Organizational Diversity and the Problem of Institutional Change in the US Economy, 1900–1950." *Socio-Economic Review* 5(1): 47–80, 2007 — documents how US cooperative and mutual aid institutions developed in the early twentieth century; directly applicable to the Zone 5 historical cooperative tradition (agricultural cooperatives in Iowa, dairy cooperatives in Wisconsin)

**Expected word count from this workstream**: 8,000–10,000 words
**Estimated research hours**: 6–8 hours
**Key gap to document explicitly**: The Argentina barter club collapse (2002–2004) is as important as the peak (2001–2002). Primary documentation on the collapse is in Spanish and not well-synthesized in English-language literature. Dinerstein covers it politically; Collom covers the US parallels. Explicitly note what is and is not documented in accessible English-language sources on the collapse dynamics.

### Workstream 5: Inter-Community Trade Protocols and Regional Exchange Architecture

**Final document sections**: Sections 6 and 7 (Inter-Community Trade Architecture; Regional Food and Resource Hubs) — approximately 10,000–12,000 words

**Research questions this workstream must answer**:
- How do communities establish stable trade relationships with neighboring communities when they lack a shared currency, common legal jurisdiction, or central coordination authority?
- What is the documented operational structure of regional food hub networks, and how do they coordinate supply-demand matching across multiple communities without centralized price signals?
- What governance structures make inter-community trade agreements durable? (This is where the Ostrom design principles intersect with trade architecture — applying polycentric governance theory to multi-community exchange.)
- What is the role of comparative advantage in Zone 5 inter-community trade when transportation fuel is scarce? (Classical comparative advantage assumes low transport costs — this assumption fails in crisis conditions.)

**Source targets** (in priority order):

1. Ostrom, Elinor. *Governing the Commons* (Cambridge University Press, 1990) — Chapters 4 and 5 on nested governance and polycentric systems are the theoretical foundation for inter-community exchange governance; the case studies in Chapter 4 (Spanish Huertas, Philippine Zanjeras) document how communities at different scales coordinate resource management without central authority
2. Wallace Center at Winrock International, *Regional Food Hub Resource Guide* (USDA, 2012) — primary documentation on regional food hub operational models; while 2012, this is still the most comprehensive treatment of food hub governance structures; USDA AMS has more recent data available at ams.usda.gov/local-food-programs
3. Fischer, Michael, and Tom Kelly. *The Farm to Institution New England (FINE) Case Study* (2019) — documents inter-institutional food procurement coordination across multiple communities and institutions; applies directly to Zone 5 regional food hub governance design
4. Red de Trueque documentation (Argentina 2001–2004) — the network's node governance structure (how 400 nodes coordinated without central authority) is the largest documented inter-community exchange network in modern history; Collom (2005) and Dinerstein (2014) both contain relevant sections; primary Spanish-language documentation should be noted as the more authoritative source
5. Good Food Network (GFN), *Food Hub Benchmarking Study* (annual, 2012–2024) — primary data on food hub viability, volume, and governance structure; current data available at ngfn.org; includes Zone 5 food hub cases
6. Dawson, John. "Localization and the Retail Industry." *The Localization Reader: Adapting to the Coming Downshift*, ed. Frank Thayer and Gary Gardner (MIT Press, 2012) — documents how local exchange networks operate at the regional scale; the comparative advantage without transport infrastructure chapter is directly applicable
7. USDA Agricultural Marketing Service (AMS), *Local Food Directories: Food Hub Directory* — searchable database of active food hubs by state; Zone 5 inventory; accessible at ams.usda.gov/local-food-directories/foodhubs

**Expected word count from this workstream**: 10,000–12,000 words
**Estimated research hours**: 7–9 hours (this is the highest-priority gap; no pre-research chapter coverage exists)
**Key gap to document explicitly**: Classical economic comparative advantage theory assumes elastic transport capacity. In crisis conditions with fuel scarcity, the effective radius of inter-community trade contracts sharply. The research must document what Zone 5 communities can feasibly trade within a 10-mile, 25-mile, and 50-mile radius under different fuel availability scenarios. This is original synthesis — it is not pre-documented anywhere in the source library and will require applying existing data to a new framing.

## 3. Source Strategy

### Primary vs. Secondary Source Balance

The target is 120–150 total citations in the final deliverable, of which 38 are already verified in the pre-research chapter. The remaining 82–112 citations should follow this distribution:

| Tier | Description | Target Count | Examples |
|------|-------------|-------------|---------|
| Tier 1 — Canonical | Foundational works cited as primary authority; treated as definitional | 15–20 | Ostrom *Governing the Commons*; WIR Bank reports; Mondragon annual reports; Graeber *Debt* |
| Tier 2 — Empirical | Secondary sources providing specific quantitative claims or case study data | 40–55 | NCEO meta-analysis; Bristol Pound evaluation; Food Co-op Impact Report; Federal Reserve Kansas City Rural Mainstreet Index |
| Tier 3 — Zone 5 Primary | State extension publications, cooperative association data, USDA Zone 5 program documentation | 20–30 | ISU Extension; UW-Madison cooperative resources; USDA RBCS program documentation; Grounded Solutions CLT database |
| Tier 4 — Practitioner | Operational guides, how-to documents, template libraries | 10–15 | Timebanks USA launch guide; USDA cooperative formation guides; NCBA CLUSA formation templates |

### Official vs. Practitioner Source Calibration

The domain must balance two reader profiles: (a) community practitioners who need actionable guidance and will distrust purely academic framing; and (b) USDA grant reviewers and cooperative development center staff who expect rigorous sourcing. The solution is to cite official sources for program mechanics (USDA, NCUA, state statutes) and to cite practitioner sources for operational implementation, explicitly labeling the distinction.

Do not cite secondary summaries of primary data when the primary data is accessible. If the claim is "WIR Bank participation is counter-cyclical," cite the WIR annual reports, not a secondary summary of them. If the claim is "food cooperatives generate a $1.604 local economic multiplier," cite the Food Co-op Impact Report 2024, not a general cooperative advocacy document.

### Citation Chasing Protocol

From each Tier 1 canonical source, identify its three most-cited secondary sources and evaluate whether they should be added to the bibliography. From Ostrom alone, this yields Hardin's "Tragedy of the Commons" (1968), McKean's community commons studies (1992), and Schlager and Ostrom's property rights analysis (1992) — all directly applicable. Do not add a source to the bibliography without reading at least the abstract and conclusion to confirm the specific claim being attributed to it.

### Handling Gaps and Honest Uncertainty

Three categories of gap require explicit documentation rather than evasion:

1. **Data gaps** (timebanking effectiveness literature): note the methodological limits of the evaluation literature, cite the best available evidence with appropriate confidence language, and direct readers to Timebanks USA for current operational data
2. **Language barriers** (Argentina Red de Trueque primary Spanish documentation): note that primary Spanish-language documentation exists and is more authoritative than English-language secondary sources; cite the accessible English-language sources and flag the limitation
3. **Zone 5 specificity gaps** (quantitative data on rural Zone 5 inter-community trade): explicitly note when general cooperative or commons literature is being applied to Zone 5 conditions by inference rather than direct documentation; do not present inferred Zone 5 applicability as documented Zone 5 data

They are not activated for author recruitment until after the June 3 Domain A decision (unless user directs otherwise). In the PATH C autonomous execution scenario, Domains B/E/F remain on standby until September 1 (Phase 6b activation).

Domain A must lay the following specific groundwork for each secondary domain:

**For Domain B (Institutional Governance)**:
- Document how cooperative governance (one member, one vote; democratic control; transparent financials) is distinct from commons governance (Ostrom's eight design principles for shared resource management) and where they overlap
- Establish that economic conflict is the most common trigger for governance breakdown in small communities, so economic institutions that reduce distributional conflict are prerequisite to governance stability
- Cross-reference explicitly at the cooperative governance section: "for the full governance architecture — meeting procedures, decision authority, enforcement mechanisms, and failure modes — see Domain B §3–5"

**For Domain E (Ecosystem Restoration)**:
- Document community land trust and commons governance as the institutional mechanism for coordinating land stewardship across multiple households
- Establish that ecosystem restoration at the landscape scale requires governance of land use across multiple parcels — a commons governance problem, not a single-household decision
- Cross-reference at the commons governance section: "for the specific restoration practices — soil amendment, mycorrhizal network recovery, polyculture design — that these governance structures should incentivize, see Domain E §4–6"

**For Domain F (Intergenerational Knowledge Transmission)**:
- Document apprenticeship and timebanking as the economic compensation mechanism for skilled knowledge transfer
- Establish that Zone 5 communities face a specific risk: skilled practitioners in their 60s and 70s who hold irreplaceable knowledge (seed saving, equipment repair, traditional food preservation) are not economically compensated for teaching, so knowledge transfer does not happen at sufficient scale before practitioners die or become unable to teach
- Cross-reference at the timebanking section: "for the educational architecture — structured apprenticeship programs, community knowledge inventory, oral history documentation — see Domain F §3–5"

**Avoiding Duplication**:
- Domain A covers economic institutions. Domain B covers governance institutions. The overlap zone is cooperative and commons governance. Domain A should document the economic design of these institutions (who is a member, how surpluses are distributed, what the economic incentive structure is). Domain B should document the decision-making architecture (how votes are counted, how disputes are resolved, how authority is delegated). Write Domain A to leave Domain B's governance territory clearly unoccupied.
- Domain A covers labor compensation (timebanking, apprenticeship wages, mutual credit for skilled services). Domain F covers knowledge transmission pedagogy (how learning happens, how knowledge is documented). Domain A should not document curriculum design or learning methods. Domain F should not document economic compensation structures.

## 6. Expected Deliverable

### Format and Standards

The final deliverable must be consistent with Phase 3 and Phase 5 standards. The Phase 5 integrated corpus (`PHASE_5_WAVE_1_2_INTEGRATED_CORPUS.md`) is the reference document for formatting, citation style, and tone. Specific standards:

- **Headers**: H2 for major sections, H3 for subsections, H4 for sub-subsections — no deeper
- **Citations**: inline parenthetical author-date (Ostrom 1990, p. 91) for direct quotes; author-date without page for general claims (Ostrom 1990); full citation in numbered bibliography at document end
- **Tone**: practitioner-accessible, but not condescending. Assume readers can understand a cost-benefit analysis; do not assume they know what "polycentric governance" means without explanation
- **Zone 5 specificity**: every section must contain at least one Zone 5-specific example, program, or data point; Zone 5 specificity is not optional decoration
- **Actionability**: every section of 2,000+ words must contain at least one specific action a community can take, with enough specificity to begin planning without external expertise

### Quantitative Targets

| Metric | Target |
|--------|--------|
| Total word count | 45,000–55,000 (including pre-research chapter) |
| Citations | 120–150 total |
| Zone 5 examples | Minimum 1 per section (10–12 sections = 10–12 minimum) |
| Templates | 2 required (Local Currency Design Worksheet; Commons Charter); 1 preferred (Cooperative Formation Checklist) |
| T+14 peer review | Practitioner reviewer feedback integrated |
| Placeholders | Zero in final deliverable |
| On-time confidence | 85% (PATH C autonomous); 95% (PATH A author-led) |

### Confidence Assessment

The 85% on-time confidence for PATH C autonomous execution reflects three risks:

1. **Practitioner voice risk** (primary): The domain's practitioner audience requires writing that reads like it was produced by someone who has run a timebank or helped incorporate a worker cooperative — not like academic synthesis. Autonomous research-and-writing can produce accurate content but risks academic register. Mitigation: the T+14 peer reviewer is the primary check on this; if the peer reviewer flags academic register in Sections 1–4, course-correct in Sections 5–12 before they are written.

2. **Inter-community trade section risk** (secondary): Workstream 5 is the largest research gap and requires original synthesis (comparative advantage under fuel scarcity is not pre-documented). If the synthesis produces findings that do not meet the actionability standard, the section will require additional research time. Mitigation: five-day schedule buffer between August 15 final draft and August 30 publication.

3. **Peer reviewer availability risk** (tertiary): If no practitioner peer reviewer is available by July 9, the T+14 checkpoint is skipped and the self-review at August 10 carries full quality assurance responsibility. The 85% confidence figure already accounts for this risk — a scenario with no peer review drops confidence to approximately 78%, still within acceptable range given the existing source library quality.
