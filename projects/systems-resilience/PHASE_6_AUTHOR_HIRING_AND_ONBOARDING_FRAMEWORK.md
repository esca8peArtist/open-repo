# Phase 6 Author Hiring and Onboarding Framework

**Target**: Confirmed author ready for June 10 production start  
**Onboarding window**: June 1–9 (9 days)  
**Production window**: June 10–August 9 (Economic Resilience, 60 days)  
**This framework**: Works regardless of which domain the user selects. Domain-specific details are flagged at each step.

---

## 1. Author Profile Definition

Phase 6 domain documents are 45,000–60,000 words of practitioner-facing research. The author must be able to produce at scale, work from a staged source library, and communicate with the Zone 5 Midwest community organizer as their primary reader — not an academic or policy professional.

### Universal Requirements (All Domains)

**Writing capacity**: Has produced a single long-form document of 5,000+ words grounded in research for a non-academic audience. Academic papers do not count as a proxy — the practitioner voice is different. Preferred: published reporting, grant-funded research reports, organizational white papers, or in-depth online publishing.

**Source discipline**: Can work from a pre-staged bibliography with annotation. Does not require self-directed literature review from scratch — the Phase 6 pre-research provides a 30–40 source foundation; the author supplements and verifies.

**Availability window**: June 1–August 9. This means the author must be genuinely available for the full 60-day window, not nominally available while managing other full-time commitments. Estimate: 20–25 hours/week of writing and research time. Less than 15 hours/week and the timeline slips.

**Comfort with Midwest context**: Does not need to be a Zone 5 resident. Does need to be able to engage with Iowa agricultural cooperative records, Minnesota county governance structures, and regional food hub data without treating them as foreign material.

**Collaboration style**: Comfortable with weekly checkpoint emails (not calls), open to editorial feedback in the second draft cycle, and capable of distinguishing between "you are wrong" and "this needs a citation" — two different types of feedback that require different responses.

### Domain-Specific Profile Requirements

**Domain A: Community Economic Resilience**

Credential floor: Direct experience with cooperative economics, commons governance, complementary currency systems, or mutual aid networks. Academic background in economics, sociology, or organizational studies is a plus but not required — practitioner knowledge of how real cooperatives operate is more valuable than theoretical economics credentials.

Preferred profile indicators: Has facilitated a cooperative startup process, worked with a USDA Rural Development cooperative grant program, published analysis of WIR-style systems or timebanking networks, or holds an ICA (International Cooperative Alliance) affiliated role.

Red flags: Background purely in macroeconomics or corporate finance. Tendency to frame "alternative economics" as utopian or as a hobby rather than a documented operational history.

**Domain C: Skills Development and Knowledge Transmission**

Credential floor: Adult learning practice, apprenticeship facilitation, or curriculum development for non-formal education contexts. Familiarity with situated learning theory (Lave/Wenger) is a meaningful signal. 4-H, Cooperative Extension, or similar land-grant university education network experience is highly relevant.

Preferred profile indicators: Has designed and delivered a skills-transfer curriculum for a community context; has experience with trades education or vocational training; has published on informal learning systems or traditional knowledge preservation.

Red flags: Background purely in formal academic pedagogy. Framing all education questions through credentialing and institutional schooling lenses.

**Domain D: Governance Scaling**

Credential floor: Political science, public administration, community organizing, or organizational design background. Experience with federated governance structures (watershed councils, cooperative federation boards, multi-community organizations) is the most direct qualification.

Preferred profile indicators: Has analyzed or facilitated governance transitions as communities grew; published work on polycentric governance, sociocracy, or consensus decision-making at scale; direct experience with post-disaster community governance reconstruction.

Red flags: Background in corporate governance without community governance grounding. Strong opinion that democratic governance inherently "doesn't scale" — this framing conflicts with the research position that good design mitigates scale failure.

## 2. Hiring Flowchart

The hiring process has four stages: source channels, initial contact, qualification screen, and confirmation.

### Stage 1: Source Channels by Domain

**All domains — primary channels**:

*Academic and research networks*:
- Direct outreach to departments: Economics/Sociology (Domain A), Education/Adult Learning (Domain C), Political Science/Public Administration (Domain D) at land-grant universities in Zone 5 (Iowa State, University of Minnesota, University of Wisconsin-Madison, University of Illinois). Request referrals to researchers with practitioner communication experience.
- ResearchGate and Academia.edu searches for relevant publication keywords by domain. Identify authors whose papers show practitioner orientation in their writing style.

*Policy and practitioner organizations*:
- Domain A: National Center for Employee Ownership (NCEO), NCBA CLUSA (cooperative development), USDA Rural Development cooperative program staff lists, P2P Foundation network, Schumacher Center for a New Economics
- Domain C: USDA Cooperative Extension regional directors, 4-H national network, National Skills Coalition, Highlander Research and Education Center, International Society for Self-Directed Learning
- Domain D: National Association of Counties (rural county governance staff), Foundation for Intentional Community (FIC) governance network, International Association for Public Participation (IAP2), Sociocracy For All facilitator directory

*Freelance platforms (vetted carefully)*:
- Reedsy: Filter for "research" + "long-form non-fiction"; review sample work before outreach
- Upwork: Filter by "policy research" or "white paper" category; request 2,000+ word sample in the domain
- LinkedIn: Search by keyword combination relevant to domain + "freelance writer" or "consultant"
- Local and regional: Zone 5 cooperative networks, Midwest food hub networks, community land trust practitioner lists

**Shortlist target**: 3–5 candidates per domain. Do not proceed with a single candidate who has not been screened — build redundancy into the pipeline.

### Stage 2: Initial Contact (3-5 business days before needed confirmation)

For Option A timeline (author confirming by May 30, onboarding June 1), outreach should go out no later than May 27. For the contingency path (author confirming by June 3–5), outreach should go out by May 31.

See `AUTHOR_ONBOARDING_TEMPLATE.md` for the full email text. The outreach email should:
- State the project scope in two sentences (systems resilience research, Zone 5 Midwest, practitioner audience)
- Specify the commitment (20–25 hours/week, June 1–August 9)
- Describe the compensation range (see rate ranges below)
- Request a brief (500-word) writing sample from a document they have produced
- Set a response deadline (3–5 business days)

**Rate ranges** (per domain, total project fee):
- Domain A (Community Economic Resilience): $3,000–$5,000 total for final production document (45K–55K words)
- Domain C (Skills Development): $3,500–$5,500 total (48K–58K words)
- Domain D (Governance Scaling): $4,000–$6,000 total (50K–60K words; higher rate reflects scope complexity and governance expertise premium)

Milestone payment structure (recommended): 20% on signed agreement, 30% on June 29 draft milestone, 30% on July 27 draft milestone, 20% on final acceptance.

### Stage 3: Qualification Screen (1–2 business days)

If a candidate responds and submits a writing sample, screen against three gates:

**Gate 1 — Writing voice**: Does the sample read like practitioner documentation or academic writing? The Phase 5 model document is `phase-5-wave-2-community-implementation-playbook.md`. If the candidate's sample is closer to academic than practitioner, ask for a different sample that shows non-academic voice. If no such sample exists, move to the next candidate.

**Gate 2 — Domain knowledge signal**: Does the sample demonstrate engagement with the relevant literature (cooperative systems, adult learning, governance theory)? Not deep mastery — but signal that the candidate knows the difference between Ostrom's commons governance and standard corporate governance, for example. A 15-minute phone or video call can substitute for written evidence of domain knowledge.

**Gate 3 — Timeline confirmation**: Explicitly confirm the June 1–August 9 availability. Ask: "What other commitments are you managing in this window?" A candidate with a summer teaching position, conference presentations, or another major project in July is a timeline risk regardless of credentials.

### Stage 4: Confirmation

Once a candidate passes all three gates, issue:
1. Project brief (from `AUTHOR_ONBOARDING_TEMPLATE.md`)
2. Agreement letter or contract (short-form, 1–2 pages: scope, timeline, payment milestones, intellectual property assignment to project, confidentiality)
3. Source library folder (pre-staged 30–40 sources with annotation)
4. Access to project files (Phase 5 documents for context, Phase 6 pre-research for Domain A/C/D baseline)

Confirmation deadline: May 30 16:00 UTC (Option A timeline) or June 3 08:00 UTC (contingency timeline).

## 3. Onboarding Checklist

The onboarding window is June 1–9. By June 10, the author begins production writing. Everything in this checklist must be complete before June 10.

### Administrative (June 1–2)

- [ ] Agreement signed and countersigned; copy sent to author
- [ ] First milestone payment (20%) processed or confirmed pending processing
- [ ] Project Slack channel (or email thread) created with the author; introduce yourself
- [ ] Confirm author's preferred working hours, time zone, and communication preference (async vs. scheduled check-ins)
- [ ] Share project file access (Google Drive folder or equivalent): Phase 5 documents, Phase 6 pre-research, onboarding kit, source library

### Orientation Reading (June 1–4)

The author must read four documents before writing begins. Track confirmation in the Slack channel or by email.

- [ ] **Model document**: `phase-5-wave-2-community-implementation-playbook.md` — this sets the tone, section structure, citation density, and Zone 5 specificity the author must match
- [ ] **Domain candidate file** (relevant to assigned domain): `phase-6-candidate-community-economic-resilience.md`, `phase-6-candidate-skills-development.md`, or `phase-6-candidate-governance-scaling.md` — scope definition, research questions, hard out-of-scope boundaries
- [ ] **Phase 6 Author Onboarding Kit**: `phase-6-author-onboarding-kit.md` — project history summary, domain scope, format and style guide, success criteria
- [ ] **Pre-research chapter** (Domain A only): `phase-6/01-community-economic-resilience.md` — 6,800 words of pre-existing research the author is building on, not replacing

### Source Library Verification (June 3–7)

The author independently verifies accessibility and relevance of the 30–40 pre-staged sources.

- [ ] Open each source URL; confirm accessible (open access or institutional access)
- [ ] Flag any sources behind hard paywalls without available abstracts
- [ ] For each flagged source, propose 1–2 alternatives within 48 hours
- [ ] Submit verified source list as a Google Sheet or markdown table: Source | URL | Accessible (Y/N) | Primary section it supports | Any annotation

Orchestrator review of source list: 2 hours on June 8 or 9. Flag any sourcing concerns before production begins.

### Research Outline (June 5–9)

The author delivers a section-by-section research outline by June 9.

- [ ] 10–12 sections identified, each with a 2–3 sentence scope description
- [ ] Primary sources assigned to each section (minimum 5 per section)
- [ ] Word count estimate per section (target: 4,000–6,000 words/section)
- [ ] Zone 5 implication note for each section (1–2 sentences: how does this section apply specifically to Midwest geography, climate, and community context?)
- [ ] Out-of-scope note for each section (what literature or topics would naturally expand this section but are explicitly excluded?)

Orchestrator review of outline: 1–2 hours on June 9. This is the first editorial gate. If the outline shows scope drift, correct it before production begins.

### Day 1 Orientation Call or Kickoff Email (June 1 or 2)

If the author prefers a synchronous kickoff, schedule a 30-minute video call June 1 or 2. If asynchronous is preferred, send a structured kickoff email with the same content. The kickoff covers:

1. Project history (5 minutes or 3 paragraphs): Phases 1–5 completed, what the corpus has already established, where Domain A/C/D fits
2. The three research questions the domain must answer (verbatim from the domain candidate file) — confirm the author can restate them in their own words
3. The out-of-scope boundaries — confirm the author understands what the document is NOT
4. Checkpoint schedule: brief status email every Friday by 17:00 in the author's time zone; 30-minute progress calls at June 29, July 13, July 27 (non-negotiable)
5. Editorial feedback process: Orchestrator provides feedback within 48 hours of any draft submission; feedback is structured as (a) factual corrections, (b) citation needs, (c) zone specificity notes, (d) scope observations

## 4. Quality Gates

Four checkpoints govern production from June 10 to August 9. Each has a specific pass/fail criterion. Missing a checkpoint triggers the escalation protocol, not automatic concern — one checkpoint slip is manageable; two consecutive slips require a timeline review.

### Checkpoint 1: June 29 — Sections 1–4 Drafted

**Deliverable**: 10,000–15,000 words submitted for review. Sections 1–4 complete; at minimum 60 citations integrated; Zone 5 implications present in at least two sections.

**Orchestrator review**: 3–4 hours. Check for scope drift, citation quality (are sources being used or just listed?), Zone 5 specificity, and practitioner voice consistency.

**Pass criteria**: Sections are drafted at publishable quality, not polished; direction is correct; no major scope violations detected.

**Fail criteria**: Sections are significantly under 10,000 words without explanation, citations are sparse (fewer than 40), or voice has drifted academic. Escalation: 30-minute call within 48 hours to diagnose root cause and agree on correction.

### Checkpoint 2: July 13 — Sections 1–6 Drafted (Approximately 50% Complete)

**Deliverable**: 25,000–30,000 words submitted. Sections 1–6 complete, all case studies underway (not complete — in progress is acceptable). 80+ citations integrated.

**Orchestrator review**: 4–5 hours. This is the major mid-point review. Check cross-domain consistency (do the framing and terminology match Phase 5 documents?), evidence quality in case study sections, and template development progress (the document needs minimum 2 implementation templates — are they in the outline?).

**Pass criteria**: Direction confirmed; author is tracking to word count; no irrecoverable scope violations.

**Fail criteria**: Document is under 20,000 words, case studies have not been started, or templates are absent from the outline. Escalation: full timeline review within 48 hours; consider whether delivery date adjustment is needed.

**Domain A only — cross-domain sync**: If Domain D (Governance Scaling) is being produced in parallel, July 13 is the synchronization point. The Economic Resilience first draft is shared with the Governance author for cross-domain framing alignment. 30-minute synchronization call scheduled at this checkpoint if both authors are active.

### Checkpoint 3: July 27 — Full Draft Complete (All Sections)

**Deliverable**: 40,000–45,000 words submitted. All sections drafted; Zone 5 implications integrated in every section; minimum 2 implementation templates present; all case studies complete.

**Orchestrator review**: 5–6 hours. Full document review: completeness, citation density (minimum 100 citations by this checkpoint), Zone 5 specificity in every section, template quality (are they actionable? Can a community organizer use them directly?), and cross-references to Phase 1–5 documents verified.

**Pass criteria**: Document is structurally complete. Sections requiring expansion or additional citation are identified but the architecture is sound.

**Fail criteria**: Total word count under 38,000; templates are absent or non-actionable; Zone 5 implications are thin in more than 3 sections. Escalation: if timeline can accommodate, grant a 1-week extension (to August 3) for completion of weak sections. If timeline cannot accommodate, engage Orchestrator self-execute for specific gaps.

### Checkpoint 4: August 9 — Final Document Delivered

**Deliverable**: Production-ready document committed to master branch. 45,000–55,000 words; 120–150 citations verified and formatted; all cross-references checked; YAML frontmatter complete; zero placeholders.

**Orchestrator final review**: 6–8 hours. This is the publication gate. Check citation formatting (every citation verified accessible), Zone 5 specificity audit (random sample of 5 sections — does each one mention a specific Midwest institution, geography, or practice?), template completeness (both templates present and formatted for direct use), and placeholder scan (zero [fill] or [TBD] markers).

**Pass criteria**: Document passes publication gate. Committed to master. Author receives final payment (20% holdback).

**Fail criteria**: Document has more than 5 unresolved citations, contains placeholders, or critical sections are under 3,000 words without explanation. Escalation: engage Orchestrator self-execute for specific outstanding items; author receives partial final payment pending resolution.

## 5. Contingency: Author Unavailability and Slippage

### If Author Is Unavailable by May 30 (Pre-Confirmation Contingency)

If the primary author does not confirm by May 30 16:00 UTC, the contingency sequence from `author-outreach-tracking.md` activates:

1. Contact Fallback Author 1 simultaneously by email and phone
2. If Fallback 1 does not respond within 4 hours, contact Fallback 2 in parallel
3. If neither fallback is available by May 31 08:00 UTC, activate self-execute path with peer review

**Self-execute path**: Orchestrator produces the domain document using the Phase 6 pre-research as a foundation, with peer review from a domain-adjacent reviewer (cooperative extension network contact for Domain A, education researcher for Domain C, governance practitioner for Domain D). Production timeline extends by 2–3 weeks (delivery August 23 rather than August 9).

### If Author Slips by 1 Week

One-week slippage at Checkpoint 1 or 2 is recoverable. Activate 1-week extension at the relevant checkpoint. Final delivery slips from August 9 to August 16. Still above the August 30 distribution preparation deadline.

### If Author Slips by 2 Weeks

Two-week slippage indicates a systemic production problem. At the July 13 checkpoint: if word count is under 20,000, initiate a timeline review call within 48 hours. Options:
- Author accelerates production (increase hours for 2 weeks); Orchestrator provides daily micro-feedback to reduce turnaround time
- Orchestrator self-executes specific gaps (sections with clear research in the pre-research chapter)
- Delivery date adjusted to August 30 (still above the September distribution preparation window)

### 1-Week Slippage Buffer

The Option A timeline (June 10 production start, August 9 delivery) has a 21-day buffer before the August 30 distribution preparation deadline. This means a final delivery of August 9 + 1 week = August 16 still provides 14 days for distribution preparation. The buffer absorbs one week of slippage without any cascade.

If Phase 6a delivery is August 16 and Phase 6b (Governance Scaling) launches September 1, the 2-week gap provides enough time for Phase 6b author recruitment (if needed) and onboarding preparation.
