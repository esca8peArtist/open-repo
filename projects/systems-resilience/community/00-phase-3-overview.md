# Phase 3 — Community-Scale Resilience Overview

> **Scale**: 25–150 people, 10–50 households, town or village governance  
> **Region**: Midwest US (Zone 5)  
> **Tracks**: Rural + Small-Town  
> **Build-out time**: 12–24 months (parallel implementation)

---

## What Phase 3 Accomplishes

If Phase 1 establishes individual competency (one person can survive alone) and Phase 2 establishes household systems (a small group can sustain itself), **Phase 3 establishes community resilience** — the structural, governance, and operational systems that allow 25–150 people to survive and stabilize together when external systems fail.

Three critical findings distinguish Phase 3 from earlier phases:

### Finding 1: Cascading Failures Require Coordinated Response

Individual and household failures are survivable via redundancy and preparation. Community-scale cascades are not. When a 48+ hour grid outage coincides with fuel supply disruption and medical system stress (common during ice storms, derechos, and major hurricanes), survival depends on:

- **Pre-negotiated command structure** that prevents decision paralysis (who decides when rationing begins?)
- **Distributed resource transparency** (who knows where fuel, food, and medical supplies are stored and how much is available?)
- **Cross-domain coordination protocols** (food hub, water system, medical clinic, and communication network don't make decisions independently)

The Blue Lake Rancheria case study illustrates this perfectly. Their microgrid islanded automatically within seconds during PG&E's October 2019 power shutoff—but only because the system was designed for that exact scenario and had been tested repeatedly. The response structure was not invented on Day 1 of the crisis; it was already built and practiced [1].

### Finding 2: Infrastructure Interdependency is a Cascade Risk

Water pumping requires electricity. Electricity generation requires fuel. Fuel distribution requires roads. Food refrigeration requires electricity. Medical equipment requires power. Communication requires power and/or fuel for generators.

A naive assumption is that each system operates independently—shut down the grid, keep the generator running. Reality is far more complex. When grid power fails, backup generators activate. When generators run out of fuel, water treatment stops. When water treatment stops, medical surge capacity collapses. When medical capacity collapses, injury severity becomes life-threatening [2].

Phase 3 maps the full cascade and identifies intervention points where small investments in distributed, redundant infrastructure prevent the entire system from failing. The infrastructure interdependency document in this section identifies these leverage points and provides the specific design choices to implement them.

### Finding 3: Mutual Aid Networks Pre-Exist Crises—They Cannot Be Invented During One

Communities that survived Hurricane Maria, the 2019 California power shutoffs, and the 2011 Christchurch earthquake did not organize mutual aid networks when the crisis arrived. They activated networks that existed before [3]. Centros de Apoyo Mutuo in Puerto Rico, the Blue Lake Rancheria tribal knowledge networks, Christchurch's time banking system—all had governance structures, trusted leadership, and practiced coordination before the crisis began.

A community without pre-existing mutual aid structures cannot build them at crisis time. Decision-making becomes fragmented, resource allocation becomes contested, and coordination fails exactly when it's most critical.

This does not require massive upfront investment. It requires identifying skilled community members, documenting their capabilities, establishing how they coordinate, and practicing coordination on low-stakes scenarios (simulations, exercises) before a real crisis. The mutual aid networks document in this section provides the specific steps.

## The Three Phase 3 Deep Dives

This phase consists of three integrated documents, each addressing a critical dimension of community-scale resilience:

### 1. Emergency Response Infrastructure (01-emergency-response-infrastructure.md)

**Scope**: Command structure, communication networks, medical surge protocols, and shelter coordination during cascading failures (grid outage + supply disruption + medical stress).

**Audience**: Emergency council members, incident commanders, clinic directors, shelter coordinators, communications officers.

**Key topics**:
- Adapted Incident Command System (ICS) for village scale
- Communication cascades and radio nets
- Resource allocation protocols under scarcity
- Medical triage and surge capacity
- Shelter and mass care procedures
- Three realistic Midwest scenario plans (ice storm, derecho, grid failure)

**Build-out**: Designate roles, document radio frequencies, create laminated command cards, run annual tabletop exercises.

**Outcome**: When a crisis declares, the community can activate coordinated response within 30 minutes instead of spending Day 1-2 figuring out who decides what.

---

### 2. Infrastructure Interdependency Mapping (02-infrastructure-interdependency.md)

**Scope**: The cascade pathways when water, power, food, healthcare, and communication systems fail simultaneously. Identifies high-leverage intervention points.

**Audience**: Selectboard members, domain stewards (water, energy, food, health), infrastructure engineers, planners.

**Key topics**:
- 6×6 dependency matrix (what fails if each infrastructure fails?)
- Cascade timeline (how fast does failure propagate?)
- Five high-leverage distributed infrastructure interventions
- Backup generator sizing, fuel storage, and load-shedding procedures
- Microgrid feasibility and islanding thresholds
- Water system redundancy (gravity distribution, manual pumping, treatment backup)
- Food hub distribution under grid failure

**Build-out**: 3–6 months of infrastructure audit + design, 12–18 months of implementation.

**Outcome**: Instead of hoping each system survives independently, the community makes intentional infrastructure choices that make failure unlikely (distributed, redundant systems) and managed (load-shedding protocols, tiered service).

---

### 3. Mutual Aid Networks (03-mutual-aid-networks.md)

**Scope**: Pre-crisis identification and coordination of community skills, assets, and exchange mechanisms that enable sustained cooperation during long failures.

**Audience**: Community organizers, selectboard members, domain stewards, cluster stewards, any resident interested in community coordination.

**Key topics**:
- Skills census and asset mapping (who can do what? what tools/equipment exist?)
- Time banking and exchange frameworks
- Barn-raising and work-sharing coordination
- Tool libraries and equipment sharing
- Governance for mutual aid (what happens if someone free-rides?)
- Three operational models (time banking, direct barter, labor shares)
- Building trust and practicing before crisis

**Build-out**: 2–4 months (low infrastructure cost, primarily social coordination).

**Outcome**: A community with pre-existing, practiced mechanisms for sharing labor and skills can sustain higher output and faster recovery than a community discovering these mechanisms in real time during crisis.

## How the Three Documents Fit Together

These three documents are not alternatives. They are complementary dimensions of the same system:

- **Emergency Response Infrastructure** answers: *Who decides what during a crisis?* (governance/command)
- **Infrastructure Interdependency** answers: *What physical systems support survival?* (technical/engineering)
- **Mutual Aid Networks** answers: *How does the community sustain itself?* (social/coordination)

All three must be built in parallel. A well-designed command structure with no backup power fails. A community with distributed microgrid infrastructure but no coordination protocol wastes resources. A community with excellent mutual aid culture but no access to emergency supplies and no coordinated medical response cannot handle surge.

## Relationship to Phase 1 and Phase 2

**Phase 1 (Individual Scale)** established what one person can do alone:
- Filter and store water
- Grow food for one person
- Shelter from weather
- Generate minimal power
- Respond to personal health emergencies
- Self-teach critical knowledge

**Phase 2 (Household Scale)** scaled these competencies to 2–6 person clusters:
- Shared water systems (cistern, well, gravity distribution)
- Household-scale food production and preservation
- Shared shelter infrastructure (backup heating, safe room)
- Household energy coordination and load-shedding
- Coordinated household medical response
- Household governance and conflict resolution

**Phase 3 (Community Scale)** scales further to 25–150 people:
- Coordinated response during cascading infrastructure failures
- Distributed, redundant physical infrastructure (multiple water tanks, decentralized energy, multiple food hubs)
- Practiced command structure and communication networks
- Pre-existing mutual aid networks for sustained recovery

Each phase builds on the previous. A Phase 3 community is not 50 individuals practicing Phase 1 competencies in isolation. It is 50 individuals whose households (Phase 2) are connected into coordinated community systems (Phase 3) that tolerate infrastructure cascades.

## Implementation Roadmap: 12–24 Month Timeline

Phase 3 can be built in parallel across three tracks:

### Track 1: Governance & Command (Months 1–3)
- Establish selectboard/council if not already present
- Create emergency council charter and authority limits
- Designate Incident Commander and succession plan
- Establish monthly community meeting schedule
- Document governance framework (3–5 page town charter)
- **Lead**: Selectboard / existing municipal government
- **Effort**: 20–30 hours total

### Track 2: Infrastructure Audit & Design (Months 1–6)
- Conduct asset inventory (water sources, fuel, food storage, power, medical equipment)
- Map dependencies and cascade points
- Design distributed infrastructure improvements (well backup, solar/generator, food hub redundancy)
- Engineer load-shedding protocols
- Plan microgrid feasibility (if applicable)
- **Lead**: Domain stewards + volunteer engineers
- **Effort**: 40–80 hours assessment; 100–200 hours design

### Track 3: Social Coordination (Months 1–4)
- Conduct skills census and asset mapping
- Establish mutual aid governance (time bank rules, work-share protocols)
- Launch monthly skills-sharing meetings
- Create tool library and equipment sharing system
- Document exchange mechanisms
- **Lead**: Community organizers / cluster stewards
- **Effort**: 20–40 hours

### Track 4: Implementation & Practice (Months 6–24)
- Execute infrastructure builds (well backup, generator installation, food hub setup, solar/microgrid)
- Conduct tabletop exercises (quarterly)
- Practice communication cascades (annual)
- Pilot mutual aid exchange (quarterly)
- Document lessons learned
- **Effort**: Varies by infrastructure scope (200–500+ hours for major installations)

## How to Use This Section

### For Town Leadership (Selectboard, Incident Commander)
1. Start with `01-emergency-response-infrastructure.md` to understand governance and command structure
2. Use the ICS templates and role cards to designate who does what
3. Run a tabletop exercise (Section 7 of 01-* provides a full scenario)
4. Read `02-infrastructure-interdependency.md` to understand what infrastructure gaps exist
5. Commission an infrastructure audit (work with domain stewards)

### For Infrastructure/Engineering People
1. Start with `02-infrastructure-interdependency.md`
2. Conduct the dependency mapping exercise for your town (Section 2)
3. Identify your community's critical failure points
4. Design specific infrastructure improvements (Sections 3–5 provide design guidance)
5. Integrate with selectboard on timeline and resource allocation

### For Community Organizers
1. Start with `03-mutual-aid-networks.md`
2. Conduct the skills census and asset mapping (Section 2)
3. Choose an exchange model (time banking vs. direct barter vs. labor shares)
4. Launch a pilot mutual aid coordination meeting
5. Integrate outcomes with infrastructure team (so mutual aid can redistribute resources during cascade)

### For Cluster Stewards
1. Read all three documents (2–3 hours total)
2. Identify which sections apply to your cluster
3. Designate a cluster coordinator for emergency response (becomes part of cluster chain)
4. Ensure your cluster can feed itself for 30 days on stored + produced food (Phase 2)
5. Participate in community mutual aid network

## Success Criteria: Phase 3 Complete

Phase 3 is complete when:

- ✅ Written governance charter (selectboard authority, emergency council scope, decision-making process)
- ✅ Designated incident commander + succession plan + ICS roles documented
- ✅ Communication cascade tested (phone tree / radio net / backup procedures practiced)
- ✅ Infrastructure dependencies mapped (asset inventory + cascade analysis complete)
- ✅ Design specifications for at least 2 critical infrastructure redundancies (well backup, generator backup, food hub redundancy, etc.)
- ✅ Mutual aid network documented (skills census, exchange mechanism, governance)
- ✅ At least one tabletop exercise conducted with full participation
- ✅ Cluster stewards trained and networked
- ✅ All three Phase 3 documents reviewed and understood by at least 5 community leaders

When these criteria are met, the community is positioned to activate coordinated response during actual crises rather than discovering its governance failures in real time.
