# Mutual Aid Networks and Reciprocal Economy — Community Scale
> **Region**: Midwest US (Zone 5) | **Tracks**: Rural + Small-Town
> **Scale**: 25–150 people, extended to neighboring communities
> **Cross-references**: [household/01-household-coordination-overview.md](../household/01-household-coordination-overview.md) · [household/03-food-coordination.md](../household/03-food-coordination.md) · [household/06-community-scale-overview.md](../household/06-community-scale-overview.md) · [community/01-emergency-response-infrastructure.md](01-emergency-response-infrastructure.md) · [midwest/calendar.md](../midwest/calendar.md)

## The Most Important Finding

Mutual aid networks are not crisis-response mechanisms — they are pre-existing social infrastructure that determines how well communities respond to crises when they arrive. The communities that survived Puerto Rico's 11-month grid outage after Hurricane Maria with the least harm were not those with the most resources but those with the most pre-existing social trust and reciprocal exchange networks. The Centros de Apoyo Mutuo in Caguas and Barrio Mariana were not invented in September 2017; they were extensions of mutual aid relationships that had been building for years, accelerated by the crisis [1]. The Transition Towns network, active in 992 registered communities across 67 countries, has reached the same conclusion from the opposite direction — communities building resilience before a crisis — and its most successful local chapters share the same feature: years of trust-building through regular reciprocal exchange before any emergency required it [2].

The practical implication for a Midwest rural community: begin building mutual aid infrastructure now, in normal conditions, for practical everyday reasons — shared equipment, skill exchange, labor pooling — and it will be available, tested, and trusted when you need it for survival. This document provides the specific structures to build that infrastructure.

Three complementary systems are described here:
1. **Time banking** — skill matching and hour-for-hour labor exchange
2. **Reciprocal labor and cooperative production** — barn-raising, harvest sharing, shared equipment
3. **Community exchange and local currency** — formalizing and scaling non-dollar exchange

All three are designed to be functional before a crisis, more critical during one, and self-reinforcing over time.

## Section 1: Building the Social Infrastructure

### The Skills Census

Before any exchange system can function, the community needs to know who has what skills. A skills census is a simple survey (1–2 pages) distributed to all households, asking:

**Medical and health**: training level (first aid, CPR, EMT, WFR, RN, NP/PA, MD, midwifery), special skills (dental, veterinary, mental health, herbalism), availability (primary or backup role)

**Food production and preservation**: gardening (acreage, crops grown), large-scale field production, animal husbandry (dairy, poultry, pigs, cattle, bees), hunting and trapping, fishing, canning and pressure canning, dehydration, fermentation, butchering, cheesemaking

**Construction and maintenance**: carpentry (rough framing, finish), roofing, plumbing, electrical, masonry, welding, mechanics (small engine, diesel, automotive), blacksmithing

**Water and sanitation**: water system operation, plumbing repair, well maintenance, composting toilet installation and management, water testing

**Energy**: solar installation and maintenance, battery bank management, generator operation and maintenance, wood stove installation, chimney sweeping

**Communication and information**: ham radio operator (license class), teaching, translation (languages), record-keeping, conflict resolution and mediation

**Transportation and logistics**: truck driver's license, large vehicle operation, heavy equipment (tractor, backhoe), horse or mule driving

**Care and social support**: childcare, elder care, education (grade levels comfortable teaching), mental health support, pastoral care

**Craft and production**: textile (spinning, weaving, sewing), leather, soap, candle, pottery, metalworking, herbalism and medicine-making

The census is voluntary but strongly encouraged. Store results in a physical binder at the command post and a maintained spreadsheet accessible to the town clerk. Review and update annually at the community meeting.

The census transforms an anonymous neighborhood into a known resource network: when the water pump fails at 2 AM, you know who the plumber is. When a child needs to continue their schooling during an extended school closure, you know who can teach mathematics.

### Social Trust as Infrastructure

Elinor Ostrom's Nobel Prize-winning research on commons governance identified one non-negotiable prerequisite for successful shared resource management: social trust between participants. Communities that had successfully managed shared resources for generations all shared it; communities that failed to maintain shared resources typically lacked it [3]. Trust is built through repeated positive interactions, shared history, and demonstrated reliability — none of which can be rushed or manufactured during a crisis.

Regular community events — the monthly community meeting, the annual seed swap, the summer potluck, the October preservation workshop — are not social niceties. They are infrastructure maintenance. Each event where community members interact positively, exchange help, and resolve small disagreements constructively is a deposit in the community trust account that will be drawn on during a crisis.

**Recommended regular events for trust-building** (see `midwest/calendar.md` for timing):
- Monthly community meeting (first Sunday at 10 AM)
- Spring seed swap (late March, before planting season)
- Summer potluck and skills fair (July — longest days, easiest travel)
- Fall preservation workshop (September–October, coordinated with food hub season)
- Winter gathering / holiday celebration (December)
- Annual preparedness tabletop exercise (March)

## Section 2: Time Banking — The Hour Economy

### What Time Banking Is

A time bank is a mutual exchange system where one hour of any service earns one time credit, regardless of the market value of the service. A plumber, a childcare provider, and a gardener all earn the same credit per hour. Credits are spent on hours of service from other participants.

The principle is ancient — community members helping each other with an implicit expectation of future reciprocation — and time banking gives it a formal structure that scales beyond the group of people you know personally. Research consistently documents that time banks increase social cohesion, reduce social isolation, and increase reciprocal exchange across class and skill lines [4].

Time banking also provides a practical economic bridge during infrastructure failures: when dollar-based commerce breaks down (no ATMs, businesses cannot restock), the time bank continues to function. A community member who earns credits by helping with water delivery can spend those credits on medical advice, childcare, or food preservation assistance — all without dollar exchange.

### Setting Up the Time Bank

**Step 1: Designate a coordinator**
The time bank requires one person to maintain the ledger and handle disputes. The town clerk is a natural fit during normal operations. During Level 2/3 emergencies, the coordinator role passes to the designated Logistics Officer (per `01-emergency-response-infrastructure.md`).

**Step 2: Establish the ledger**
For a community of up to 150 people, a paper ledger is fully adequate and more resilient than a digital-only system:
- Each participant has an account page: name, contact, current balance (in hours)
- Transactions are recorded: date, provider, recipient, service description, hours
- Balance is updated after each transaction
- Negative balance is permitted up to -20 hours (encourages use; prevents gaming)
- Maximum balance is capped at +100 hours (prevents hoarding of credits)

Digital backup: a spreadsheet maintained by the coordinator, synced to an offline backup weekly. During grid outage, the paper ledger is authoritative.

**Step 3: Define what earns and what spends credits**

*Credits are earned by*: any service provided to another community member with their agreement — childcare, elder care, clinic support shifts, water maintenance, firewood splitting, teaching, cooking meals for others, construction assistance, transportation, interpreting, mediation, record-keeping.

*Credits are spent on*: any service from another community member — the same list. No service category is excluded except: emergency services (fire, search and rescue, emergency medical response) are provided unconditionally and do not require time credit payment.

*Credits may also be exchanged for goods during Level 2/3 events* (per the exchange protocol in Section 3): a defined rate is established at the start of each monthly resource period (e.g., 2 credits = 1 lb dry corn; rates adjusted by emergency council based on current supply).

**Step 4: Launch and promote**

Run a launch event at the community meeting: explain the system, answer questions, take initial signups. Set a critical mass target (30% of households enrolled within 6 months) — below this threshold, there are not enough potential exchanges to keep the system active.

The most common failure mode in time banks is insufficient participation: if only 10 people are enrolled, each person's potential exchange pool is too small to satisfy their needs, and participation drops. Reach 30% or more of households before calling the system established.

### Time Bank Governance

Disputes between participants are resolved by the time bank coordinator first; if unresolved, by the selectboard arbitration process (`household/06-community-scale-overview.md` Section 1 conflict resolution). Document every dispute and its resolution.

Fraud prevention: require that transactions be confirmed in writing by both parties (a simple paper receipt with date, service, hours, and both signatures). Without the receipt, the ledger is not updated.

Exit terms: participants who leave the community must bring their balance to within 5 credits of zero before leaving. Positive balances may be donated to a community time bank fund (used for community services that need labor input without a direct recipient).

## Section 3: Reciprocal Labor and Cooperative Production

### The Barn-Raising Model

The barn-raising tradition — entire communities gathering to build or rebuild a structure for a neighbor in a single day — was not charity. It was a reciprocal labor exchange sustained by the implicit understanding that each participant could call on the community in their time of need [5]. The tradition persists unchanged in Amish and Old Order Mennonite communities across Ohio, Indiana, and Pennsylvania today [5]. The Finnish *talkoot* and the Japanese *yui* system operate on the same principle: labor contributed now is labor that can be called on later, without money changing hands.

At community scale, the barn-raising model applies to any task that exceeds a single household's labor capacity in a required timeframe: building a root cellar, repairing storm damage, bringing in a harvest before a weather event, fencing a shared pasture, stacking firewood for a household that cannot do it alone. The key features that make it work:

1. **Clear task specification**: everyone knows exactly what is being done and when it will be complete
2. **Defined participation**: the household hosting the work provides meals; participants provide labor
3. **Reciprocity expectation**: explicit, not just implied — the community norm that barn-raising participants can call a barn-raising themselves
4. **Festive character**: a barn-raising is an event, not just work; community meals, children playing, socializing alongside labor

**Organizing a labor pool event**:
- Announce at least 2 weeks in advance (at community meeting, on community board, via cluster stewards)
- Specify: task, location, start time, expected end time, what tools/skills are needed, what meals will be provided
- The hosting household coordinates with the food hub for meal provision if scale exceeds household capacity
- Record participation in the time bank ledger (participants earn 1 credit/hour; the hosting household's account is debited accordingly — or alternatively, the contribution is treated as a gift with no time bank accounting, depending on community preference; agree this before launch)

### Harvest Cooperation and Agricultural Pooling

Zone 5 Midwest agriculture has narrow harvest windows — a week or two for each crop — during which weather can destroy unharvested crops. A single household cannot always harvest a large garden or field in this window alone. Harvest cooperation — rotating crews of neighbors who harvest each household's garden in turn — solves this.

**Harvest rotation schedule** (coordinate with `midwest/calendar.md`):
- Early September: sweet corn, early tomatoes, summer squash — 2–3 days per household garden
- Late September to mid-October: potatoes, winter squash, dry beans — 2–4 days per household
- October: storage crops (carrots, beets, cabbage, turnips, parsnips) — 1–3 days per household
- October–November: grain and hay (for farms with field crops)

A harvest rotation crew of 5–8 people can bring in a large household garden in one 8-hour day. A community of 50 households can rotate through 8–10 of the largest gardens in a single harvest season — prioritizing gardens of elderly or mobility-impaired households first, then households with children, then households whose primary gardener has a health limitation.

Coordination: the cluster steward maintains a garden schedule for their cluster. Cluster-level harvest rotation is coordinated by the steward; cross-cluster coordination for community fields is managed by the food hub steward.

### Shared Equipment and Tool Library

Farm and household equipment is expensive and infrequently used. A community tool library — shared ownership of high-cost, low-use equipment — reduces individual capital cost while increasing availability.

**High-value items for community-shared ownership**:

| Equipment | Individual Cost | Community Use | Shared Cost (10 households) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pressure canner (large, 30+ qt) | $200–350 | 2–4 times/year per household | $25–35/household |
| Grain mill (hand or electric) | $300–600 | Monthly | $35–60/household |
| Food dehydrator (large-capacity) | $150–300 | Seasonal | $15–30/household |
| Chainsaw (18–20" bar, professional grade) | $400–700 | As needed | $40–70/household |
| Rototiller / walk-behind tractor | $800–2,000 | Spring and fall | $80–200/household |
| Water test kit (comprehensive) | $150–300 | Quarterly | $15–30/household |
| Generator (7.5 kW, transfer-switch ready) | $1,000–2,500 | During outages | $100–250/household |
| Pressure washer | $200–500 | Seasonal | $20–50/household |
| Welding equipment | $500–1,500 | As needed | $50–150/household |
| Draft horse / mule | $2,000–5,000 | Plowing, hauling | $200–500/household |

**Tool library governance**:
- Equipment list maintained by a designated tool librarian (volunteers, rotating annually)
- Checkout system: request at least 24 hours in advance for items shared with multiple households; same-day for items in low demand
- Return condition: equipment must be returned in same or better condition than received; user is responsible for fuel used, minor consumables (chains, blades), and repair of damage caused during use
- Major repair costs (>$50) are shared proportionally among all member households unless damage was due to negligence, in which case the using household bears the cost
- Replacement fund: each household contributes $20–50/year to a tool replacement fund; managed by the tool librarian; expenditure requires approval of two members

The tool library is documented in the household cluster-level MOU (per `household/01-household-coordination-overview.md`) and scales to the full community level through the town charter.

## Section 4: Community Exchange and Local Currency

### When Dollar Commerce Breaks Down

During extended infrastructure failures, dollar-based commerce faces structural obstacles: electronic payment systems require grid power; cash is limited because ATMs and banks are non-functional; businesses cannot restock; and prices for critical goods can spike in ways that undermine community cohesion. The community needs a pre-agreed exchange framework before this happens.

Three exchange models are available; each with documented real-world precedents. The critical point is that the model must be chosen *before* a crisis, documented in the town charter, and practiced (even lightly) during normal conditions so it is not novel when it matters.

### Model 1: Time Bank Credits as Exchange Medium

The simplest extension of the time bank: during Level 2/3 events, time credits become exchangeable for essential goods at rates set by the emergency council.

**Implementation**:
- At Level 2 declaration, emergency council sets the monthly exchange rate: e.g., 1 credit = 1 lb dry corn = 1 quart canned tomatoes = 0.5 gallon propane
- Rates are posted on the status board and announced at the daily community check-in
- Exchange happens at the food hub (food) or fuel depot (fuel) on the community distribution schedule
- The Logistics Officer tracks all exchanges against the resource register

**Advantage**: no new system to learn; builds on the existing time bank infrastructure.
**Limitation**: does not address households without time credits who have urgent needs — address this with a community emergency allocation (see Section 5).

### Model 2: Commodity-Backed Community Vouchers

The selectboard issues printed vouchers backed by the community's grain and fuel reserves. Vouchers circulate as a local medium of exchange.

**Implementation**:
- Vouchers are printed in advance (a 4"×2" card with: sequential number, denomination, signature of two council members, expiration date, and community seal or stamp)
- Print enough vouchers to cover 30 days of exchange at typical community volume: roughly 3–5 vouchers per household per day at denominations of $1, $5, and $10 equivalent
- Vouchers are distributed at the start of a Level 2 event: each household receives a base allocation (equal per capita) plus additional vouchers earned through labor contributions
- Vouchers are accepted at the food hub, fuel depot, and any community member offering goods or services
- Vouchers expire at the end of the emergency period (announced by the selectboard); unexpired vouchers can be exchanged for goods at the community distribution point during a 1-week redemption window

**Precedents**: This model closely parallels the local voucher systems used during the 2010–2012 Greek economic crisis, where several communities issued locally-backed vouchers after the mainstream currency became unusable for daily commerce [6]. The Transition Towns movement in the UK documented similar systems in Totnes (Totnes Pound, 2007–2019) and Bristol (Bristol Pound), both of which operated as community-backed exchange media [7].

**Advantage**: tangible, does not require digital infrastructure, familiar as "money-like."
**Limitation**: requires pre-printing and storage of vouchers; susceptible to counterfeiting if not carefully produced (use numbered, signed, stamped cards — not plain paper).

### Model 3: Commodity Exchange with Price Controls

A simpler barter system with pre-agreed rates and price controls for essential goods. No printed currency; exchanges happen directly or through the food hub as intermediary.

**Pre-agreed exchange rates** (set by emergency council before any emergency; reviewed quarterly during normal operations and updated to reflect current resource reality):

| Item | Exchange Equivalent |
|---|---|
| 1 lb dry corn (shelled) | 1 hour general labor |
| 1 lb dry beans | 1.5 hours general labor |
| 1 quart canned tomatoes | 1 hour general labor |
| 1 gallon propane | 2 hours general labor |
| 1 kWh microgrid allocation | 1 hour general labor |
| 1 dozen eggs | 0.5 hours general labor |
| 1 lb butter | 1.5 hours general labor |
| 1 hour skilled labor (medical, electrical, plumbing) | 2 hours general labor |

Price controls: the emergency council sets a cap on essential goods at 150% of pre-emergency reference values. Hoarding of community-allocated resources triggers graduated sanctions (per `01-emergency-response-infrastructure.md` Section 3).

**Advantage**: no infrastructure required; direct and transparent.
**Limitation**: awkward for multi-party exchanges (requires coincidence of wants); less flexible than currency. Best used as a supplement to Model 1 or 2, not as a standalone system.

### Evidence from Local Currency Experiments

The research on local currencies is mixed, which is worth stating directly. An academic study of BerkShares (Southern Berkshire County, Massachusetts) using the Synthetic Control Method found no statistically significant impact on county-level economic indicators — employment, business formation, or income — over the 15 years of BerkShares' operation [8]. The implication: local currencies do not substitute for strong regional economies in normal conditions, and should not be expected to. Their value is in crisis resilience — maintaining exchange capacity when the mainstream system is temporarily unavailable — and in building community identity and trust that has non-economic value.

The Totnes Pound (UK), operational 2007–2019, demonstrated this accurately: it did not transform the local economy but it did increase local business loyalty and community identity, and created exchange infrastructure that would have been valuable during an acute crisis [7]. Its discontinuation in 2019 was driven by the general shift to cashless payment, not by failure of the concept.

The lesson: a community exchange system should be valued primarily for its social infrastructure and crisis resilience functions, not as a development strategy.

## Section 5: Equity and Care Within Mutual Aid

### The Foundational Principle: Mutual Aid Is Not Charity

The distinction between charity and mutual aid is operationally important, not just ideological. Charity creates a permanent giver and a permanent receiver; it tends to reinforce hierarchy and dependence. Mutual aid is characterized by reciprocity — the expectation that everyone both gives and receives — and it maintains dignity for all participants because no one is permanently in the role of recipient.

This distinction matters for system design. A food distribution system that categorizes some households as "people who receive" and others as "people who give" will generate resentment and ultimately fail. A system where every household contributes something — labor, knowledge, childcare, companionship, elder care — and also receives something, sustains participation over time.

**Implementation**: ensure that even households with limited physical capacity are contributors, not just recipients:
- An elderly person who cannot do field labor can teach math to children, provide childcare, cook meals for labor crews, or offer counseling and emotional support
- A household with a disability can coordinate the tool library, maintain the resource register, or operate the communication node
- A household experiencing a health crisis can contribute as soon as they recover, understanding that their contribution earlier (when they were well) built the credit that supported them during their crisis

### Emergency Allocation for Zero-Credit Households

During Level 2/3 events, households with no time credits or exchange capacity — newly arrived, recently disabled, exhausted from sustained labor — still need food, water, and warmth. The community sets aside a **solidarity reserve**:
- 5–10% of all food distribution is set aside as unconditional allocation
- Managed by the Logistics Officer
- Available to any household presenting to the food hub with documented need (self-report; no means testing beyond the request)
- Provided without expectation of repayment during the crisis; recipient is asked to contribute labor when able after the crisis resolves

This reserve is funded by the community's normal resource allocation and does not require any external mechanism. It is simply the recognition that mutual aid that abandons those in acute need defeats its own purpose.

### Children and Elder Care as Infrastructure

Child care and elder care are typically undervalued but are essential infrastructure for community resilience. If parents cannot leave their children, they cannot participate in labor pools, food hub shifts, or emergency response. If elderly community members are not cared for, they become a crisis rather than a resource.

**Child care cooperative**: a rotating schedule where 2–3 households take responsibility for caring for all cluster children (typically 4–8 children per cluster) while other households engage in labor or emergency response. The caring households earn time bank credits; the parents earn time on the labor crew. This is documented in the cluster-level coordination agreement (`household/01-household-coordination-overview.md`).

**Elder care assignment**: each elderly or mobility-impaired household is assigned a cluster buddy — a neighboring household that commits to checking in daily, delivering resources, and providing emergency assistance. This relationship is established before any crisis and maintained through regular contact.

## Section 6: Beyond the Crisis — Building Permanent Infrastructure

### From Emergency Response to Permanent System

Mutual aid networks built primarily for crisis response often dissolve when the crisis passes — the energy that sustained them was crisis-driven, not structurally built. Networks that persist beyond crises are those that serve everyday needs in ways that are genuinely better than market alternatives.

The Centros de Apoyo Mutuo in Puerto Rico were still active more than two years after Hurricane Maria because they had shifted from emergency response to ongoing community services: food programs, youth education, community governance support, and mental health services that addressed persistent community needs [1]. The Detroit Summer collective that grew from Grace Lee Boggs' vision has maintained urban farming and community education programs for 30+ years — not because a crisis sustained it but because it addressed real ongoing community needs [9].

For a Midwest rural community, the permanent mutual aid network sustains itself through:
- The seed library and annual seed swap (serves practical growing needs year-round)
- The tool library (saves real money for member households monthly)
- The harvest rotation (reliably solves a practical seasonal problem)
- The time bank (provides genuine value for care work and skilled exchange that money doesn't efficiently handle)
- Community preservation workshops (save food, build skills, create social occasion)
- The monthly community meeting (maintains governance and social connection)

None of these requires a crisis to justify them. They are simply better than their alternatives.

### Integration with Neighboring Communities

The mutual aid network does not stop at the community boundary. Neighbors towns and rural clusters within 15–30 miles are natural partners in mutual aid that a single community cannot provide alone:
- Shared skilled labor for work that exceeds local capacity (a second community's electrician for a major installation)
- Supply exchange when one community has surplus of something another community lacks
- Emergency refuge for households displaced by disaster (reciprocal hosting agreements)
- Information sharing on what is working and what is not

**Establishing inter-community mutual aid agreements**:
1. Identify 2–4 neighboring communities within 15–30 miles through existing relationships (county emergency management, agricultural cooperative membership, school district connections)
2. Propose a mutual aid compact: a 1-page agreement covering the principle of reciprocal assistance, the contact protocol (who calls whom and how), the types of assistance that can be requested (labor, supplies, refuge, equipment), and the expectation that resources will be repaid in kind when the requesting community is able
3. Conduct an annual joint exercise: a combined tabletop or communication drill that tests the inter-community contact protocol

The Transition Towns network documents numerous examples of inter-town mutual aid agreements and their effectiveness in creating regional resilience beyond what any single town can achieve [2].

## Section 7: Precedents in Detail

### Centros de Apoyo Mutuo — Puerto Rico (2017–present)

Following Hurricane Maria's September 2017 landfall, with Puerto Rico's grid offline for up to 11 months and government relief failing to reach many communities, spontaneous mutual aid centers (Centros de Apoyo Mutuo, CAMs) arose in the most affected neighborhoods. In Caguas, what began as an improvised community kitchen within weeks became a fully operational mutual aid center with: food distribution, collective healthcare, legal assistance, grief counseling, education for displaced children, and — critically — the first community-owned and -operated solar microgrid installed in the area (Barrio Mariana, May 2018) [1].

The key operational features that made the CAMs functional:
- Horizontal governance: no central authority; decisions made by collective vote at daily assemblies
- Immediate recognition of non-monetary contributions: cooking, childcare, and emotional support counted equally with construction and technical work
- Continuous documentation: the CAMs kept records of what they did and learned, enabling replication in other neighborhoods
- Connection to national and international mutual aid networks: supplies, expertise, and financial support flowed in through established mutual aid channels that pre-existed the disaster

By 2019, after most external attention had moved on, multiple CAMs were still operational and had expanded their services — demonstrating the shift from emergency response to permanent community infrastructure.

**Applicable lessons for Zone 5 Midwest**:
- Pre-crisis social network is the most important factor — no amount of physical preparation compensates for absent community trust
- Horizontal governance can function at village scale if decision-making processes are clear and trusted
- Document and share what works — neighboring communities benefit from lessons learned

### Grace Lee Boggs and Detroit Summer (1992–present)

Grace Lee Boggs and James Boggs launched Detroit Summer in 1992, modeled on the 1964 Mississippi Freedom Summer campaign, to channel youth energy into community rebuilding through urban agriculture, mural painting, and community organizing [9]. The program produced community gardens on formerly blighted lots, youth education programs, and most importantly a community ethic of self-reliance and mutual investment that shaped Detroit's remarkable post-industrial community agriculture movement.

The urban agriculture movement Boggs helped catalyze in Detroit has become a national model: by the 2010s, there were 1,400 urban gardens in Detroit, and community land trusts and cooperative farms had transformed the landscape of several neighborhoods. The Boggs Center continues to operate, sustaining mutual aid networks and community agriculture decades after the founding [9].

**Applicable lessons for Zone 5 Midwest**:
- Youth engagement in community resilience work builds the next generation's investment in community
- Urban/suburban agriculture (container gardening, small plots, community gardens) is viable even in dense settings
- Mutual aid built around tangible, productive work — growing food — sustains participation better than mutual aid built around abstract solidarity

### Transition Towns — Totnes and Global Network (2006–present)

The Transition Towns movement, founded in Totnes, UK in 2006 by Rob Hopkins, provides a structured framework for communities to build resilience before external crises force them to. The network had grown to 992 registered communities across 67 countries by 2024 [2]. The Totnes Pound (2007–2019) was one of the most visible experiments, demonstrating both the potential and the limitations of local currency as a resilience tool (see Section 4).

More durable than the local currency experiment were Totnes's community-wide skill-building programs (permaculture design, food forest establishment, local food network, energy descent planning) and its governance model: community-wide visioning processes that built shared understanding of what the town needed to become resilient, followed by working groups that implemented specific projects.

**Applicable lessons for Zone 5 Midwest**:
- Community visioning before infrastructure investment: know what you're building toward before spending money
- Working groups with defined scope and clear deliverables outperform large committees
- Skills building is always high-value mutual aid — a community with more skilled people is a more resilient community regardless of other factors

## Bullet-Point Summary (for cross-referencing)

- Mutual aid networks must pre-exist crises to function during them — build during normal conditions for practical everyday reasons
- Skills census: annual survey of who can do what; stored at command post; forms the basis of the time bank and labor pool

- Labor pool / barn-raising: rotating labor crews for harvest, construction, repair; 2-week advance notice; hosting household provides meals; participation recorded in time bank
- Tool library: shared ownership of high-cost/low-use equipment; checkout system; replacement fund at $20–50/household/year
- Three exchange models: time credits as goods exchange (simplest), commodity-backed vouchers (most tangible), direct commodity barter (simplest to start)
- Local currency evidence: no significant economic development impact in normal conditions; valuable for crisis resilience and community identity
- Equity: solidarity reserve (5–10% of food distribution) for zero-credit households; no means-testing; elder care and child care as structured community roles
- Inter-community agreements: compact with 2–4 neighbors within 15–30 miles; annual joint exercise
- Permanence: systems must serve everyday needs to survive beyond crisis; seed library, tool library, harvest rotation, and monthly meetings are the sustaining mechanisms
- Precedents: Puerto Rico CAMs (horizontal governance, solar microgrid, documentation); Detroit Summer (youth engagement, urban agriculture, multi-decade persistence); Transition Towns (community visioning, working groups, skills-building)
