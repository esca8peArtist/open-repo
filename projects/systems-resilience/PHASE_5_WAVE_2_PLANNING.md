## Most Important Finding

Wave 1 established the knowledge preservation layer (Tier 1) and the governance/logistics bridge (Tier 2 Dimension 1). Wave 2 fills the remaining Tier 2 dimensions — the specialized operational domains that the coordination infrastructure document explicitly references but does not cover — and then bridges up to Tier 3 (inter-household federation into community). The four Wave 2 documents are not optional extensions: three of them (Veterinary Care, Psychological Support, Conflict Resolution) were identified as load-bearing gaps in the Phase 4 Synthesis Framework. The fourth (Tier 3 Community Coordination) is the capstone that connects all Phase 5 Tier 2 work to the Phase 3 community-scale corpus.

The sequencing recommendation is: Psychological Support first (highest-impact gap, Zone 5 winter amplifier, requires no specialized equipment knowledge), then Conflict Resolution (builds directly on Psychological Support's de-escalation layer and completes the governance facilitation gap), then Veterinary Care (agricultural food security dependency, specialized but bounded), then Tier 3 Community Coordination (synthesis document, should be written after the other three are complete).

## Document 1: Tier 2 Veterinary Care Guide

### Gap Being Filled

Phase 4 Synthesis Framework Gap 2. The corpus currently has no document covering routine vaccination, injury management, obstetric emergencies, zoonotic disease identification, or emergency slaughter protocols for livestock at household scale. The individual agriculture document (individual/06-agriculture.md) covers livestock in caloric production terms only. Seedwarden's field manual covers basic husbandry. This document covers animal health crises — the scenarios where basic husbandry is not sufficient and professional veterinary care is unavailable.

The rural veterinary shortage makes this gap urgent beyond a resilience-planning context: as of 2025, USDA declared 243–245 rural veterinary shortage areas in 46–47 states, the highest ever recorded. In 2024, only 3,424 U.S. veterinarians (3.4% of the total workforce) worked in food animal practice. A Zone 5 household with chickens, goats, or pigs is operating with a structurally degraded professional veterinary safety net under normal conditions — not only in disruption scenarios.

### Key Sections

**Section 1: Zone 5 Livestock Profile and Disease Calendar**
The species most common in Zone 5 Midwest homestead operations: laying chickens (Newcastle disease, Marek's disease, Avian Influenza biosecurity, respiratory illness in winter), meat goats and dairy goats (CAE, CL, respiratory infections, enterotoxemia, urinary calculi in wethers), meat rabbits (GI stasis, respiratory, fly strike in summer), pigs (swine influenza, erysipelas, internal parasites). Each species has a Zone 5 disease calendar: which conditions peak in Zone 5 winter vs. summer; which are introduced by wildlife contact; which are spread between species (the pig-poultry influenza transfer risk). Sources: USDA APHIS biosecurity guides, Iowa State CFSPH Farm Biosecurity portal, Minnesota Board of Animal Health, AVMA rural veterinary shortage documentation.

**Section 2: Preventive Protocols — Vaccination and Biosecurity**
Species-specific vaccination schedules implementable without a veterinarian: goat enterotoxemia/tetanus (CD&T, annual), chicken Marek's (hatchery-administered, document at purchase), rabbit-specific (limited US vaccines, biosecurity emphasis). Quarantine protocols: 30-day isolation for new animals, 60 days for swine, dedicated equipment, traffic control. What to observe and document in a daily health check that takes under 5 minutes per species. Biosecurity failure modes specific to Zone 5: wildlife contact (raccoon, deer, waterfowl) as disease vectors; winter housing density increasing respiratory transmission; pasture management for parasite load reduction. Sources: USDA APHIS sheep/goat biosecurity guide, Iowa State extension, UMass CAFE biosecurity guidelines, AVMA advocacy publications.

**Section 3: First Response — Injury, Illness, and Obstetric Emergency**
The three most common acute crises per species, each with triage decision: is this manageable by the household, or does it require outside veterinary assistance (which may be hours away or unavailable)? Wound care for livestock (how a goat wound differs from a human wound in cleaning, closure, and antibiotics); bloat in goats and cattle (the five-minute assessment, trocar intervention decision); obstetric emergencies (dystocia recognition — when a goat or pig has been in active labor too long, how to assess presentation, when manual intervention is safe vs. when to call immediately). Emergency slaughter protocols (humane kill methods by species, food safety considerations for meat from sick animals, what conditions make meat unsafe to consume even after slaughter). The Pet Emergency Academy Livestock First Aid certification covers much of this territory and is the recommended training course. Sources: Homestead.org Barnyard Basics, The Art of Homestead Animal Care guide, Pet Emergency Academy Livestock First Aid certification, CABI farm animal medicine reference.

**Section 4: Zoonotic Disease Recognition**
The diseases that transfer between the household's livestock and its human members — the overlap between animal health management and household medical management. Zone 5-relevant zoonoses: Salmonella (chickens), ringworm (cattle, goats), Q fever (goats and cattle, particularly hazardous during birthing), Lyme disease (wildlife exposure during outdoor animal management), Cryptosporidium (goat and cattle young). For each: transmission routes specific to household farming practices, early symptom recognition in humans, initial response, and the threshold at which medical evacuation is warranted regardless of infrastructure disruption. This section requires coordination with individual/05-healthcare.md's infection management protocols. Sources: USDA APHIS, CFSPH zoonosis fact sheets, AVMA animal health resources.

**Section 5: Household Veterinary Supply Cache and Knowledge System**
What a household can realistically acquire, store, and use without prescription (many livestock medications are available OTC in farm supply stores): injectable penicillin and LA-200 (oxytetracycline), banamine (flunixin meglumine, for pain and inflammation), probiotics, electrolytes, epinephrine (for anaphylaxis), wound supplies (betadine, wound powder, staples). What requires a veterinarian relationship for prescription access. The case for establishing a relationship with the nearest large-animal vet before an emergency: many vets will provide phone consultation for established clients even when on-farm visit is not possible. The household veterinary notebook: animal records (birth date, vaccination history, illness history, breeding records), emergency contact list, and species-specific quick-reference cards. Recommended reference texts: Merck Veterinary Manual (offline reference), Pipestone's Veterinary Guide to Sheep and Goats, Gail Damerow's Storey's Guide to Raising Chickens.

## Document 2: Tier 2 Psychological Support Infrastructure

### Gap Being Filled

Phase 4 Synthesis Framework Gap 3. The healthcare document covers acute physical emergencies. No document in the corpus addresses the dominant health burden in extended disruption: depression, anxiety, grief, interpersonal trauma, and the Zone 5-specific amplifier of seasonal affective disorder in the November–March period. This is not a clinical gap requiring professional mental health training — WHO's Psychological First Aid (PFA) field guide describes lay-person-applicable frameworks designed specifically for resource-constrained environments.

The evidence base on community psychological resilience is large. A Sandy-aftermath study (PMC 4427458) found that community-level factors — social support, sense of community, collective efficacy, place attachment — predicted psychological resilience at least as strongly as individual-level factors. A SAMHSA meta-analysis on disaster behavioral health found that peer support and community-based psychosocial support are as effective as or more effective than formal clinical intervention in the acute and early recovery phases. The practical conclusion: communities do not need licensed therapists to support psychological resilience. They need trained lay practitioners, functioning mutual support structures, and grief rituals that have community legitimacy.

### Key Sections

**Section 1: Zone 5 Psychological Risk Profile**
The conditions that make Zone 5 extended disruption particularly high-risk psychologically: the five-month winter (November–March) compresses indoor time and reduces sensory variety at exactly the period when disruption fatigue accumulates; Zone 5 isolation (low population density, difficult winter travel) removes normal social escape valves; agricultural disruption — crop failure, livestock loss — has documented mental health consequences that mainstream disaster mental health frameworks developed for urban settings systematically undercount; the household at 8–25 people is large enough for significant interpersonal conflict but small enough that there is no escape from the conflict within the group. The most acute risk populations: children under 12 (cognitive frameworks for understanding loss and mortality are still forming), adults with pre-existing depression or anxiety (loss of medication access, loss of coping routines), elderly members (grief, identity loss, physical disability increasing dependency), and the household's primary decision-makers (leadership exhaustion, decision fatigue, isolation from peer support). Sources: PMC 4427458 (Sandy), SAMHSA disaster behavioral health approaches, collective trauma research.

**Section 2: Psychological First Aid — The Lay Practitioner Layer**
The WHO Psychological First Aid field guide defines PFA as eight core actions: contact and engagement, safety and comfort, stabilization, information gathering, practical assistance, connection with social supports, information on coping, and linkage with collaborative services. None of these require clinical training. All can be taught to a lay practitioner in 8–16 hours of training. This section translates the eight PFA actions into household-scale practice: how to make initial contact with a distressed household member (non-clinical language, no diagnosis framing), how to assess safety without pathologizing normal grief responses, how to stabilize without medication in an acute distress episode, how to connect a struggling person to their within-household support network. The section explicitly distinguishes between what lay PFA can address (grief, anxiety, situational depression, interpersonal conflict arising from stress) and what requires external clinical referral even if difficult to access (psychosis, suicidal ideation with plan and intent, substance dependence requiring medical detox). The evidence caveat must be present: PFA's evidence base is contested. A PMC 2023 systematic review (PMC 10624106) found that while PFA has been widely adopted, rigorous RCT evidence of effectiveness is limited. The honest framing: PFA is not proven treatment; it is a structured approach to not making things worse while providing social connection. Sources: WHO PFA Field Guide, PMC 5314921 (proliferation and evidence review), PMC 10624106 (systematic review), NCTSN PFA Field Operations Guide.

**Section 3: Community Grief Rituals and Collective Healing**
The anthropological and psychological literature on collective trauma recovery consistently identifies a finding that clinical frameworks underweight: communities recover psychologically through collective meaning-making processes, not just individual therapy. Rituals of commemoration, shared grief expression, and seasonal markers serve a recovery function that individual support cannot. The research on this is consistent: communities that maintain cultural practices and communal rituals after disaster show better long-term psychological outcomes than those that prioritize individual treatment. This section provides practical guidance for a Zone 5 household: how to structure a grief ritual after a household death or a significant loss (crop failure, livestock loss, community member departure), how seasonal events (first harvest, winter solstice, planting day) can carry psychological renewal meaning when deliberately structured, how the household meeting itself — when facilitated with emotional awareness — can serve a social cohesion function beyond its governance purpose. The Niroga Institute's work on movement-based collective trauma practices is relevant here. Rooted in Anishinaabe circle practices (directly relevant to Zone 5 cultural context), restorative circles also serve this ritual function. Sources: Routledge Collective Trauma Collective Healing (Saul), PMC 12265293 (collective narrative and healing Ethiopia), Niroga Institute, restorative justice circle research.

**Section 4: Winter Depression and Seasonal Affective Management**
Zone 5-specific. Seasonal affective disorder (SAD) affects an estimated 1–6% of the general population and 10–20% in subthreshold winter depression forms in northern climates. Under normal conditions, light therapy (10,000-lux light boxes, $30–100) and controlled outdoor time manage most cases. Under disruption conditions, pharmaceutical treatment may be unavailable, light therapy equipment may lose power, and the social support structures that buffer SAD are disrupted. This section covers: recognition (distinguishing normal winter slowing from clinically significant depression in yourself and in household members), non-pharmaceutical management approaches that require no electricity (timed outdoor activity in daylight hours, physical labor that produces early fatigue and improves sleep quality, social rhythm therapy principles that apply without professional oversight), light management using south-facing windows and interior space planning, and the role of physical work cadence in maintaining psychological structure during the househound Zone 5 winter. The section also addresses winter-specific risk elevation for suicide: Zone 5 November–March has above-baseline suicide rates; the household's lay PFA practitioners should know the warning signs and the question to ask directly (evidence consistently shows that directly asking "are you thinking about suicide?" does not increase risk and often decreases it).

**Section 5: Caregiver Burnout and Leadership Exhaustion**
Extended disruption creates an asymmetric psychological burden: the household's domain coordinators, medical coordinator, and conflict resolution facilitators are simultaneously under the greatest stress and providing the most support to others. Research on caregiver burnout and compassion fatigue (Nirvana Healthcare, ClevelandClinic, PMC 9517410 RCT on mindfulness intervention for caregiver burnout) documents a consistent pattern: caregivers delay recognizing their own depletion until it has reached the point of functional impairment, at which point they become a liability rather than an asset. This section provides: a structured self-assessment for burnout recognition (the established Maslach Burnout Inventory simplified to household-applicable language), a rotation protocol for high-burden roles that distributes emotional labor rather than concentrating it in the most resilient person, peer check-in practices that create a support layer for the household's support providers, and the specific governance structure (mandatory rest rotation, peer accountability for self-care) that prevents collapse of the household's psychological support capacity at the moment of highest need. Sources: Nirvana Healthcare compassion fatigue documentation, Caregiver Action Network, APA on compassion fatigue, PMC 9517410.

**Section 6: Implementation — Building Household Psychological Support Capacity**
Practical implementation: who takes the WHO PFA training (recommended: at least two people, ideally across age groups), what physical resources to maintain (quiet space accessible to any household member, a written resource list of what mental health supports exist in the region within travel distance), how to run a monthly household emotional check-in that is distinct from the governance meeting, what to do in the first 24 hours after a significant loss or traumatic event. Implementation checklist organized by timeline: pre-crisis (training, space designation, ritual calendar establishment), acute crisis (PFA activation, grief ritual within 72 hours of loss), sustained disruption (monthly check-in, burnout monitoring, seasonal structure).

## Document 3: Tier 2 Conflict Resolution Deep Dive

### Gap Being Filled

Phase 4 Synthesis Framework Gap 5. The Phase 3 governance document and the tier-2-household-coordination-infrastructure-guide both describe conflict resolution structures (four-step protocol, step escalation, assembly review). Neither document covers what a facilitator actually does in the room. This document is the facilitation skills layer: the specific techniques, practices, and decision frameworks that a lay mediator uses to de-escalate a contested household dispute, run a restorative circle after a harm event, and distinguish between a conflict resolvable by process and a membership crisis requiring removal.

The Phase 3 security document establishes that internal conflict — not external threat — is the dominant security burden after two weeks of extended disruption. The Household Coordination guide makes the same point for household scale. This document is therefore a security document as much as a governance document: a household with a functional conflict mediator is a safer household than one with better locks.

### Key Sections

**Section 1: Conflict Typology — Not All Conflicts Are the Same**
The most common facilitation error is applying the wrong process to the wrong type of conflict. This section establishes a working typology for household-scale conflicts, based on the interest-based negotiation literature and community mediation practice:

- **Resource conflicts**: Disputes about allocation of scarce resources (food ration, labor assignment, space). These are resolvable by policy, not by relationship repair. The correct process is domain coordinator review with clear criteria. Applying relationship-focused mediation to a resource conflict wastes time and generates false intimacy that breaks down at the next allocation dispute.
- **Relationship conflicts**: Disputes arising from interpersonal history, communication patterns, or unmet relational needs. These require a facilitated conversation with emotional space. Applying policy review to a relationship conflict suppresses the conflict rather than resolving it.
- **Values conflicts**: Disputes about what the household should prioritize — security vs. openness, conservation vs. comfort, risk-taking vs. caution. These are the hardest to resolve because neither party is wrong within their own framework. The correct process is structured dialogue to identify shared values and to find policy that expresses both sets of concerns, not to force agreement on which value is superior.
- **Structural conflicts**: Disputes that keep recurring because the household's governance structure creates them (a role assignment that systematically disadvantages one person, a decision process that consistently silences one demographic). These cannot be resolved through facilitation alone — they require structural change.

**Section 2: Nonviolent Communication (NVC) as the Household Facilitation Language**
Marshall Rosenberg's NVC framework is the most widely practiced and taught lay-accessible de-escalation language in community settings. Its four components — observation (what happened without evaluation), feeling (how it lands emotionally), need (the underlying need behind the feeling), and request (the specific actionable ask) — are learnable in a weekend workshop and applicable without professional credentials. This section teaches NVC in household facilitation terms: how to translate a complaint ("you always take more than your share") into an NVC request ("when the food allocation isn't documented, I feel anxious about whether there's enough for next week; I need a written record of each week's allocation; would you be willing to keep that record?"). The practical distinction between empathic listening and agreement — hearing someone's concern fully does not require agreeing with it, and this distinction is frequently lost under stress. NVC is not the only framework available — the interest-based negotiation literature (Fisher and Ury's Getting to Yes), the Community Mediation Basics framework, and the restorative circles model all have relevant components. This document synthesizes rather than commits to one. Sources: CNVC, Center for Nonviolent Communication; Community Mediation Basics (RSI); Racialequitytools.org conflict transformation.

**Section 3: Facilitation Skills — The Mediator's Toolkit**
Practical facilitation skills that require no formal credentials:

- **Active listening under duress**: The mediator's primary tool. Reflecting back what was said with accurate emotional temperature (not over-soothing, not minimizing). The technique of paraphrase-and-check ("It sounds like you're saying X — is that right?") as the most underused de-escalation move in lay facilitation.
- **The pre-meeting**: Running separate conversations with each party before bringing them together. The mediator's questions in the pre-meeting: what outcome would feel like resolution for you? What do you need the other person to understand that you don't think they currently do? What are you willing to offer?
- **Meeting structure**: The opening frame (why we are here, the ground rules, what the mediator's role is), the initial statements (each party speaks uninterrupted), the joint problem-identification phase, the option-generation phase, the agreement phase. Adapted for household settings where the mediator knows all parties personally.
- **Impasse tools**: When conversation stalls. The hypothetical ("if you could change one thing that would make this workable for you, what would it be?"), the cooling pause (20-minute break, not a recess that allows coalitions to form), the reframe ("both of you are saying you want the household to work — let's talk about what 'working' looks like for each of you").

**Section 4: Restorative Circles — After a Harm Event**
When a household member has caused harm — not just a governance disagreement, but an action that hurt another person — the conflict resolution process requires an additional layer: the restorative circle. Grounded in Anishinaabek and other indigenous circle traditions (directly relevant to Zone 5 cultural context), restorative circles bring together the person who caused harm, those who were harmed, and the wider household community to: acknowledge what happened, hear the impact, invite accountability, and establish repair. This is not punitive — the goal is to restore the relationships and the community functioning that the harm disrupted, while holding the person who caused harm accountable for specific repair actions. Reed College's harm process and the Center for Justice Innovation's RJ circle framework are the primary models. This section provides a step-by-step facilitation guide for a household restorative circle: preparation (separate meetings with harmed person and harm-causer), opening the circle (a talking piece, introductions, intention-setting), the story (what happened), the impact (how it affected each person present), the accountability (what the harm-causer acknowledges), the repair plan (specific, time-bounded actions), the closing. Sources: RSC mediation and restorative justice, Reed College harm process, Center for Justice Innovation RJ circle guide, Restorative Justice Colorado practices.

**Section 5: Escalation Thresholds — When Facilitation Is Not the Right Tool**
The most important skill in conflict facilitation is knowing when to stop. Conflict facilitation is not appropriate and must be stopped or redirected when:

- A household member poses a physical safety threat. This is a security matter, not a mediation matter. The security coordinator, not the mediator, manages this.
- A household member's behavior is coercive or threatening in a pattern, not a single incident. Mediation of abuse is not appropriate — it creates a false equivalence between the person causing harm and the person being harmed. The household assembly must convene on membership, not on "finding both sides."
- The conflict is a values conflict that cannot be bridged. Some values differences are real and irresolvable through facilitation. The mediator's honest framing: "I don't think we can make this workable. We may need to make a decision about household structure rather than about this particular dispute."

The threshold for membership termination, which the Household Coordination guide establishes as a two-thirds majority assembly decision with documented behavioral cause, is also the threshold at which facilitation should stop and governance should begin.

**Section 6: Building the Household's Mediation Capacity**
Who should be trained, in what, and at what depth. Minimum viable: two household members with NVC training and familiarity with the four-step conflict resolution protocol. Recommended: one designated Conflict Resolution Facilitator (a role addition to the governance framework in the Household Coordination guide) with training in NVC, restorative circle facilitation, and the typology framework from Section 1. Implementation checklist: training acquisition (online courses available from CNVC, RSI, and local community mediation centers), role designation, practice sessions (running the four-step protocol on minor disagreements before major ones), and the standing protocol for when to invoke a circle vs. when to use facilitated conversation vs. when to go directly to assembly.

## Document 4: Tier 3 Community Coordination Framework

### Structural Gap Being Filled

This is the capstone Phase 5 document. It connects the Tier 2 household layer to the Phase 3 community-scale corpus. It answers the question not addressed by any existing document: how do internally coherent households federate into a functioning 50–150 person community? Phase 3's governance document describes community governance at 100+ people. The Household Coordination guide describes internal household governance at 8–25 people. Nothing describes the transition between them — the formation of inter-household coordination structures, the delegate selection process, the creation of shared resources and common resource governance, and the application of Ostrom's design principles to the specific Phase 3 Zone 5 context.

This document is the integration synthesis for Phase 5. It should be written after the other three Tier 2 documents are complete, because it draws on all four Phase 5 Tier 2 documents (including Household Coordination) plus all five Phase 3 domain documents as its foundation.

### Key Sections

**Section 1: The Federation Problem — From Households to Community**
The Dunbar 150-person threshold (from Phase 3 scaling document) is approached household by household. The threshold is not a hard wall — it is a cognitive load inflection point where direct personal-trust-based governance begins to degrade. The problem is not reaching 150 people: it is reaching 150 people without a governance restructuring plan already in place. Communities that hit this threshold without it have documented failure modes: the original household cluster's decision-making norms no longer work for a larger body, new members lack the social history that made informal governance possible, and the community fractures into competing sub-groups. This section documents the four primary federation models with case studies: delegate council (Phase 3 governance model; Zapatista EZLN community assemblies; Rojava canton councils), working group federation (issue-specific cross-household groups with rotating leads; used in Twin Oaks and East Wind at their current 70–100 person scale), sociocratic circle organization (domain circles with representatives to a general circle; used in cohousing communities at 15–50 household scale), and Ostrom commons governance (nested tiers with polycentric authority; applicable to shared natural resource management across households). Each model is assessed for Zone 5 Midwest applicability.

**Section 2: Shared Resource Governance — The Commons Problem**
When households pool resources — a shared water system, a community root cellar, a shared bulk grain purchase, a community seed library — they create a commons: a shared resource with potential for over-exploitation or under-maintenance by individual free-riders. Elinor Ostrom's eight design principles, derived from 800+ documented cases of successfully governed commons, provide the most empirically grounded framework for designing shared resource systems that do not collapse under free-rider pressure. This section applies all eight principles to the specific resource types shared in a Zone 5 household cluster: water systems (clearly defined boundaries of who has access; usage monitoring; sanctioning mechanism for over-use), food storage (FIFO rotation as a governance requirement, not just logistics; accountability for what enters and exits the community reserve), seed library (check-out and renewal system; variety diversity requirement; accountability for seed quality), and shared equipment (maintenance responsibility rotation; replacement cost accounting; damage accountability). The Phase 3 food and water documents assume these governance structures exist; this document provides them. Sources: Ostrom 8 design principles (multiple references including ScienceDirect, Life with Alacrity revised version), PMC commons governance applications.

**Section 3: Delegate Selection and Inter-Household Communication**
How households select their representatives to inter-household coordination. Not a trivial question: the person who is best at internal household coordination is not necessarily the person who represents the household well externally, and the person who is most persuasive in an assembly may not be accountable to their household's actual positions. This section provides: delegate selection criteria (accountability to household, clear mandate, communication back to household, defined term), the delegate rotation question (fixed term vs. continuous), and the inter-household meeting structure (agenda development, quorum, decision threshold for different categories). The information flow problem: households that send a delegate but do not maintain information feedback loops between that delegate and the household produce representation in name only. The runner protocol from the Household Coordination guide and the AREDN mesh network are the physical communication infrastructure; this section is the governance protocol layer. Cross-reference: Phase 3's information infrastructure document (the community information system that inter-household coordination depends on).

**Section 4: Scaling the Phase 5 Gap-Fill Documents to Community**
The three Phase 5 Tier 2 documents each have a community-scale extension:

- **Veterinary Care → Community Veterinary Cooperative**: A community of 5–8 households can share a veterinary equipment cache (large-animal obstetric equipment, dehorning equipment, incubators for poultry), schedule rotating training with the nearest veterinary school extension program, and maintain a shared vaccination cold chain. One household cannot afford or justify a $2,000 portable ultrasound for livestock assessment; a community of 50–100 people can. The community veterinary cooperative is how veterinary care scales from household necessity to community asset.
- **Psychological Support → Community Grief Infrastructure**: Community-scale grief rituals, seasonal commemorations, and collective meaning-making practices that the household guide references but that require community participation to fully realize. The community also provides the peer support network for household-level PFA practitioners who would otherwise be isolated in their support work.
- **Conflict Resolution → Inter-Household Mediation Panel**: Three households each designating one trained mediator creates a six-to-nine-person inter-household mediation panel with no member mediating their own household's disputes. This is the structural form that makes restorative circles available for inter-household conflicts (the most destabilizing conflict type for a forming community).

**Section 5: The Phase 3 Connection — Activating Existing Community Domains**
Phase 3's five domain documents (governance, food systems, information infrastructure, security, scaling pathways) describe community-scale functions that require a federation of organized households to operate. This section maps the specific Phase 3 activation requirements onto the household-level prerequisites that Phase 5 has built:

- Phase 3 governance (delegate council) requires: households with functioning internal governance and a designated delegate (Household Coordination Guide, Section 2)
- Phase 3 food systems (community food hub, shared storage) require: households with 6-month supply rotation, procurement relationships, and a clear surplus identification process (Household Coordination Guide, Section 3)
- Phase 3 information infrastructure (Kiwix server, community skills census) requires: households with individual skill inventories and a household information binder (Tier 1 Education guide, Section 2; Household Coordination Guide, Section 4)
- Phase 3 security (mutual defense network) requires: households with established watch protocols, contact networks, and inter-household communication (Household Coordination Guide, Section 5)
- Phase 3 scaling pathways (Dunbar transition planning) require: households with documented governance charters that include the constitutional threshold procedures for integrating new members and expanding governance structure

**Section 6: Implementation — The 18-Month Federation Timeline**
A realistic timeline for federation from a cluster of 3–5 households with functioning internal governance to a 50–150 person community coordination structure. Milestones: delegate selection (Month 1), first inter-household meeting with formal agenda (Month 2), shared resource identification and Ostrom governance framework adoption (Months 3–4), community skills census (Month 4–5), first community-scale tabletop exercise (Month 6), AREDN mesh linking all households (Month 6–8), community seed library and equipment pool activation (Month 8–12), Dunbar transition planning document ratified (Month 12–18).
