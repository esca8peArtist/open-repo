# Phase 4b Options Comparison

> **Purpose**: Enable a go/no-go decision on the next systems-resilience document by June 1, 2026.
> **Context**: Phase 4a (Technology Repair) is complete — two documents, ~14,000 words, covering community repair infrastructure and equipment-by-equipment protocols. Phase 4b is the second of the optional expansion topics. Three candidates remain.
> **Decision needed**: Choose one option from Options 2, 3, and 4 below, or defer.

---

## Most Important Finding Up Front

If you can only choose one Phase 4b option, choose **Agricultural Intensification**. It is the only option that has a hard time dependency: perennial systems require years to establish, and a food forest planted in Year 1 of a disruption produces nothing until Year 3–5. Every year of delay in planting is a year of lost production during the crisis window that matters most. The other two options — Knowledge Preservation and Governance Scaling — are valuable but can be developed and deployed in parallel with crisis response. Agricultural Intensification cannot.

The second-strongest option is **Governance Scaling**, primarily because it fills a structural gap not addressed anywhere in Phase 1–3: the document space between 150 people (Phase 3's upper limit) and the township/county structures that exist in Midwest law. This gap is abstract today and urgent the moment a multi-community disruption exceeds two years.

Education and Knowledge Preservation is the most speculative option in terms of immediate actionability — it is genuinely Phase 5 territory for most communities — but it has the lowest research overhead and the clearest primary source base (Foxfire, Kiwix, NEH).

## Option 2: Agricultural Intensification

### Scope

Two documents, ~8,000–10,000 words each. Document A covers perennial systems and food forest design for Zone 5: species selection, 7-layer food forest architecture, food forest yield timelines (establishment cost vs. 20–50 year production window), and Kernza perennial grain developed at the Land Institute in Salina, Kansas (10-foot root system; 96% more nitrate absorption than corn/soy; 500–800 lbs/acre after establishment). Document B covers community seed saving: isolation distances, viability storage lifespans, heirloom variety selection for Zone 5, and community seed library governance.

The core problem this solves: Phase 1 covers a 400 sq ft individual garden. Phase 2 covers coordinated household production. Neither covers feeding 50–150 people locally across multiple years when supply chains do not restore. The qualitative shift — from annual emergency garden to multi-year perennial production system — requires planning that must begin before the disruption, not during it.

### Primary Sources

1. **Savanna Institute** — 5,200+ acres transitioned, 400 Midwest farmers directly supported, 14-farm research network across Minnesota, Iowa, Illinois, and Wisconsin in woody perennial polyculture systems. 2025–2026 field data includes Vernal Ridge Farm (Illinois, Year 3 of agroforestry), Red Fern Farm (Wapello, Iowa), and ongoing Midwest perennial grain trials. [savannainstitute.org](https://www.savannainstitute.org/)

2. **Land Institute — Kernza Perennial Grain** — peer-reviewed yield and soil health data from Salina, Kansas; the most credible source for perennial grain at field scale in the Midwest. [landinstitute.org/our-work/perennial-crops/kernza](https://landinstitute.org/our-work/perennial-crops/kernza/)

3. **Frontiers in Sustainable Food Systems (2025)** — landscape-scale agroforestry transition study for the Upper Midwest, quantifying nitrate reduction, carbon storage (43M tons CO2e/year from 5% of suitable Midwest land), and profitability pathways. [frontiersin.org](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/sustainable-food-systems/articles/10.3389/fsufs.2025.1499410/full)

4. **Seed Savers Exchange** — 14,000+ variety heritage seed collection, Heritage Farm in Decorah, Iowa (Zone 5 accessible regional source); peer-reviewed isolation distance and seed viability data; community seed library governance templates. [seedsavers.org/preservation](https://seedsavers.org/preservation/)

5. **Midwest Seed Library Network** — Minnesota and Iowa seed library case studies with working governance models (Lanesboro Public Library, Owatonna Public Library, Lawrence Public Library). Active check-in/check-out governance with open-pollinated, non-GMO focus. [midstory.org/saving-the-world-one-seed-at-a-time](https://www.midstory.org/saving-the-world-one-seed-at-a-time-how-libraries-are-tackling-modern-environmental-crises/)

### Unique Angle vs. Phase 1–3

Phase 1's `06-agriculture.md` covers regenerative principles, Three Sisters, and soil health for one person at 400–1,000 sq ft. Agricultural Intensification scales this to community production (1–10 acres), introduces the perennial food forest architecture Phase 1 does not develop, and adds community-level seed saving governance. The seed library governance section is entirely new territory — Phase 1 mentions saving seeds but does not provide the isolation distance charts, viability tables, physical storage specifications, or community governance rules needed to operate a real seed library.

The critical distinction: Phase 1's agriculture covers what to plant this year. Agricultural Intensification covers what to plant now so you can eat in Year 5.

### Integration with Phase 1–3

**Phase 1 cross-references that would be extended**:
- `individual/06-agriculture.md` — perennial layer added; Three Sisters scaled to field production; soil building given multi-year timeline
- `individual/02-food.md` — seed saving section deepened from basic to community-scale

**Phase 2 cross-references**:
- `household/03-food-coordination.md` — food preservation hub extended to community-scale storage and seed vault

**Phase 3 cross-references**:
- `community/03-mutual-aid-networks.md` — labor exchange during harvest extended; seed library as mutual aid infrastructure
- `community/02-infrastructure-interdependency.md` — food system cascade risk extended to multi-year scenario

**Phase 4a cross-references**:
- `04-technology-repair-equipment-protocols.md` — small tractor, chainsaw, and irrigation equipment maintenance is prerequisite for perennial system management at community scale

### Effort Estimate

15–22 hours total. Realistic: the primary source base is strong (Savanna Institute has the best farm-scale Zone 5 data available anywhere; Seed Savers Exchange has published isolation distance and viability tables), and the document structure maps cleanly to existing Phase 1 architecture. The main research overhead is species selection for Zone 5 hardiness and yield — this requires cross-checking USDA hardiness data against Savanna Institute farm records, which is 5–7 hours of careful work. The seed saving document is more formulaic and can be produced faster.

**Dependencies**: Phase 1's `06-agriculture.md` is the only prerequisite, and it is complete. Phase 4a Technology Repair is a practical prerequisite (you need working equipment to manage a food forest), and it is also complete. No external dependencies.

**Can it be done by June if started now?** Yes. 15–22 hours over three weeks is achievable at two to three research sessions per week.

## Option 3: Education and Knowledge Preservation

### Scope

One document, ~8,000–10,000 words. Five sections: knowledge inventory and priority triage (taxonomy of at-risk knowledge, Community Knowledge Audit worksheet); oral transmission methods (NEH oral history methodology, Foxfire apprenticeship model, Indigenous mnemonic approaches); analog documentation infrastructure (acid-free paper, pigment ink, filing systems, 3-location redundancy rule); offline digital infrastructure (Kiwix server on Raspberry Pi, ZIM file selection, power requirements, failure modes); structured learning without schools (learning pod design, curriculum sequencing, competency assessment).

The core problem: Phase 1–3 assume practitioners can read, do arithmetic, and access technical information. None of the documents address what happens when this assumption fails — when the person who knew how to run the water treatment system dies, the printed manuals are lost, and the Kiwix server runs out of power. Knowledge preservation is the most under-addressed risk in the current content, but it is also genuinely a Phase 5 activation in most disruption scenarios (it becomes critical at Year 2+, not Day 1).

### Primary Sources

1. **NEH Cultural and Community Resilience Program** — federal oral history methodology, structured to recover rural and minority community knowledge through interviewer training, documentation standards, and archiving protocols. Active program with Midwest rural community focus. [neh.gov/blog/preserving-resilience-through-oral-histories](https://www.neh.gov/blog/preserving-resilience-through-oral-histories-snapshot-nehs-cultural-and-community-resilience)

2. **Foxfire Series (Volumes 1–12, Penguin Random House)** — the canonical apprenticeship-based knowledge capture model from Appalachian elders: log cabin construction, food preservation, herbalism, blacksmithing, animal husbandry — presented through intergenerational practitioner voice. The Edutopia profile of the Foxfire method remains the clearest description of the pedagogical model. [foxfire.org](https://www.foxfire.org/)

3. **Kiwix** — offline Wikipedia, Khan Academy, iFixit, and medical reference server deployable on a Raspberry Pi 4 at ~5W. ZIM files cover Wikipedia, Wiktionary, Project Gutenberg, Khan Academy STEM, and medical references. Field-validated in rural deployment (Tanzania case study, 2024, Wikimedia Diff). [kiwix.org](https://kiwix.org/en/)

4. **UNESCO Disaster Risk Management for Documentary Heritage (2024)** — six training modules covering physical preservation standards, recovery from water damage and fire, digital migration, and distributed archiving. Directly applicable to community documentation under disruption. [unesdoc.unesco.org](https://unesdoc.unesco.org/ark:/48223/pf0000391132)

5. **Village Earth Appropriate Technology Library** — 1,050 books on self-reliance technology on a single USB drive; the most comprehensive offline practical knowledge repository available. Cross-references with Phase 4a Technology Repair's offline manual library. [villageearth.org/appropriate-technology-library](https://villageearth.org/home/appropriate-technology-library/)

### Unique Angle vs. Phase 1–3

Phase 1's `individual/06-agriculture.md` and `05-healthcare.md` both mention learning and skill-building in passing. Neither addresses the structural knowledge preservation problem: what happens when the person who holds a skill dies or leaves, or when the reference materials that enable self-directed learning are no longer accessible? The Community Knowledge Audit worksheet — identifying skills held by only one community member (single points of failure) — is the highest-value output of this domain and does not exist anywhere in Phase 1–3.

The Kiwix server section is also entirely new. Phase 4a Technology Repair covers hardware maintenance; Education/Knowledge Preservation covers what the hardware serves — the knowledge itself.

### Integration with Phase 1–3

**Phase 1 cross-references**:
- `individual/05-healthcare.md` — medical knowledge preservation extended; Kiwix medical reference ZIM files added
- `individual/06-agriculture.md` — Zone 5 agricultural knowledge audit added (tile drainage maps, elder farmer knowledge, planting timing)

**Phase 2 cross-references**:
- `household/01-household-coordination-overview.md` — household skills census tied into Community Knowledge Audit

**Phase 3 cross-references**:
- `community/03-mutual-aid-networks.md` — skills census already in Phase 3; Knowledge Preservation deepens this into systematic knowledge triage and apprenticeship pairing

**Phase 4a cross-references**:
- `04-technology-repair-community-infrastructure.md` — offline repair manual library already specified here; Knowledge Preservation provides the broader framework (what to print, how to file, how to protect, how to distribute)

### Effort Estimate

13–19 hours total. The lowest research overhead of the three options — the source base is strong (NEH, Foxfire, Kiwix documentation, UNESCO toolkit), the content is more curatorial than analytical, and the section structure is already well-defined in the Phase 4 scoping document. The main challenge is the Community Knowledge Audit worksheet, which requires original thinking about skill taxonomy and gap assessment methodology.

**Dependencies**: Phase 4a Technology Repair is a practical prerequisite (the Kiwix server hardware is covered there). Phase 1 is the only required prior content. No external dependencies.

**Can it be done by June if started now?** Yes, with room to spare. This is the fastest option to complete.

## Option 4: Governance Scaling

### Scope

One document, ~8,000–10,000 words. Five sections: the governance gap (Phase 3 ends at 150 people; the Christchurch post-earthquake lesson on top-down vs. bottom-up governance; Ostrom's polycentric framework); formal governance structures (selectboard emergency authority in Midwest states, FEMA Local Elected Officials Guide 2025, inter-municipal agreements, regional council design); resource allocation under scarcity (Ostrom's 8 Design Principles adapted for Midwest towns, rationing protocols, price controls vs. barter evidence); conflict resolution at scale (restorative vs. punitive, mediator training, written record systems); regional coordination for 500–2,000 people (radio nets, courier systems, Transition Towns case study).

The core problem: Phase 3 covers emergency governance for a single community of 25–150 people. Between 150–500 people (small town or multi-village district) and up to 2,000 (regional), formal Midwest governance structures exist (township trustees, selectboards) but are poorly designed for extended disruption. When communities must share water sources, roads, or medical facilities across jurisdictions, the Phase 3 mutual aid framework becomes insufficient without a formal inter-municipal agreement layer.

### Primary Sources

1. **Elinor Ostrom — Governing the Commons and Nobel Lecture** — the canonical framework for community resource governance without state enforcement. Eight Design Principles documented across decades of field research in fishing communities, irrigation systems, and forest commons. The polycentric governance model (nested decision-making with defined scope at each level) is the theoretical backbone of this document. [nobelprize.org](https://www.nobelprize.org/uploads/2018/06/ostrom_lecture.pdf)

2. **FEMA Local Elected Officials Guide (2025)** — federal template for Continuity of Government planning, emergency declaration authority, and inter-municipal coordination. The 2025 edition includes updated guidance for federal devolution scenarios — directly relevant given current federal emergency management retrenchment. [fema.gov](https://www.fema.gov/sites/default/files/documents/fema_npd_local-elected-officials-guide_2025.pdf)

3. **Tandfonline — Strengthening Community Resilience Through Participation (2025)** — systematic review finding that bottom-up participatory processes strengthen community resilience through agency, network building, conflict resolution, and shared decision-making. Provides quantitative evidence that consensus-oriented governance outperforms top-down models in extended disruption. [tandfonline.com](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/23251042.2025.2479666)

4. **Christchurch Post-Earthquake Bottom-Up Governance Case Study** — the Canterbury Earthquake Recovery Commission's top-down approach demonstrably slowed recovery; bottom-up organizations (Greening the Rubble, community gardens, civil society) outperformed in immediate crisis. The tension between formal authority and informal capacity is the central governance problem at Phase 4 scale. [researchgate.net](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/312192326_Bottom-Up_Governance_after_a_Natural_Disaster)

5. **All Resilience is Local — Teneo (2026)** — direct analysis of federal emergency management devolution implications for local governments; specifically addresses what happens when FEMA and state emergency management reduce capacity, and what local governments need to build now. [teneo.com](https://www.teneo.com/insights/articles/all-resilience-is-local-implications-of-federal-devolution-of-disaster-preparedness-response/)

### Unique Angle vs. Phase 1–3

Phase 3's `01-emergency-response-infrastructure.md` covers Incident Command System, communication cascades, and single-community emergency councils. Governance Scaling extends this to the inter-municipal layer — a space that Phase 3 explicitly does not address. The key unique outputs: inter-municipal agreement (IMA) templates that two Zone 5 Midwest communities could actually sign, Midwest-specific township trustee emergency authority summary (distinct from municipal government and not documented anywhere in Phase 1–3), and rationing protocol design that works across community boundaries.

This is also the only Phase 4 option that directly addresses the most current policy context: the 2025–2026 federal devolution of disaster preparedness is shifting responsibility to local governments faster than most localities can respond. The Teneo 2026 analysis and FEMA 2025 guide both document this shift explicitly.

### Integration with Phase 1–3

**Phase 3 cross-references (primary)**:
- `community/01-emergency-response-infrastructure.md` — ICS structure extended to inter-community coordination; emergency council authority extended via IMAs
- `community/03-mutual-aid-networks.md` — mutual aid network extended beyond single-community boundary; reciprocity tracking across communities

**Phase 2 cross-references**:
- `household/01-household-coordination-overview.md` — household governance charter connects upward to community and regional governance

**Phase 4a cross-references**:
- `04-technology-repair-community-infrastructure.md` — community tool library governance model serves as a template for the resource allocation governance patterns in this document

**Downstream unlocks**: Governance Scaling is the prerequisite for any Phase 5 work on regional federation, formal economic institutions, or legal frameworks. It is the capstone of the community governance stack started in Phase 3.

### Effort Estimate

12–17 hours total. The lowest time estimate of the three options. The Ostrom framework is well-documented and does not require original synthesis — the task is applying eight known design principles to a specific Zone 5 Midwest context. The FEMA guide and state-level township law research are the highest-overhead components (4–6 hours), but the material is findable in public documents. The conflict resolution section has a strong existing body of practice (Community Boards model, restorative justice literature) that is straightforward to apply.

**Dependencies**: Phase 3 complete (it is). No Phase 4 content required. FEMA and state emergency law research is the only non-trivial external research task.

**Can it be done by June if started now?** Yes, this is the fastest option after Knowledge Preservation.

## Recommendation

**Choose Option 2: Agricultural Intensification.**

The strongest argument is the irreversibility of delay. Every other Phase 4 option — Knowledge Preservation, Governance Scaling — can be developed and deployed in parallel with an active crisis response. Residents can build a Kiwix server in Month 2 of a disruption. Communities can draft inter-municipal agreements under pressure. But a hazelnut tree planted in Year 1 cannot produce meaningful yield until Year 3–5. A community seed library that does not exist before a disruption cannot be bootstrapped from hybrid seed packets and general-purpose garden centers that may no longer be operating.

The Phase 4 scoping document's own recommended sequencing confirms this: Agricultural Intensification is listed as the highest-value single addition if only one Phase 4 document is produced (after Technology Repair, which is now Phase 4a complete). The seed saving document (Document B) has the additional property that it must be established in advance — you cannot save seeds from a variety you do not already grow.

The two-document structure at 15–22 hours is also the most complete knowledge package of the three options. Option 2 produces ~17,000 words across two distinct topics (perennial systems and seed governance) that serve different Phase 1–3 documents and different community practitioners. Options 3 and 4 produce single documents that, while valuable, do not extend the project's scope as broadly.

**If you want to run two Phase 4b options**: Agricultural Intensification and Governance Scaling pair well. Agricultural Intensification addresses the food production gap; Governance Scaling addresses the inter-community coordination gap. Together they cover the two most consequential Phase 4 additions. The combined effort (27–39 hours) could be spread across June–July 2026.

**If you want the fastest completion**: Governance Scaling (12–17 hours) is the fastest path to a completed document. Choose it if the decision is made with less than three weeks of writing time available before June 1.
