# Phase 6 Democracy Tools — Research Execution Schedule

**Lead finding**: The November 4 to December 20, 2026 research window is seven weeks and 175–210 total researcher-hours. It is structured in four research zones and three synthesis weeks, with weekly checkpoint gates, explicit contingency branches for expert-contact delays and source-access blocks, and compressed-scope fallbacks if the December 20 distribution date becomes threatened. The Jan 3, 2027 Congress-seating deadline is fixed; the schedule works backward from it with no slack in December.

## Zone 1: Constitutional Vulnerabilities (Weeks 1–2, ~60h)

**Zone definition**: The structural, legal, and administrative barriers preventing eligible citizens from registering to vote or maintaining registration — with specific attention to the post-Callais legal environment and the SAVE Act state proliferation.

**Why Zone 1 first**: The Zone 1 legal landscape shapes the framing of Zones 2, 3, and 4. You cannot write accurately about what technology tools solve (Zone 2) without knowing what the current legal constraints are. You cannot assess what international models offer (Zone 3) without establishing the U.S.-specific baseline they are compared against. Zone 1 is not an introduction — it is the foundation the rest of the document stands on.

**Week 1 (November 4–10) — Zone 1, Part 1**

Day 1 (Nov 4): Source library verification before any new research begins. Check all seven sources flagged "VERIFY AT LAUNCH" in PHASE_3_DEMOCRACY_TOOLS_RESEARCH_READINESS_CHECKLIST.md. Specifically: October 2026 Brennan Center State Voting Laws Roundup (expected brennancenter.org), Ballotpedia SDR tracker (current state count), Movement Advancement Project AVR tracker (current state count), Princeton Gerrymandering Project press archive (check Wang primary outcome), FairVote Callais explainer (verify for updates), Democracy Works 2026 impact data (check for midterm post-election analysis), vTaiwan/Estonia Participedia entries. Log all verified URLs and any broken links in a launch-day source log. Time: 3h.

Days 2–5 (Nov 5–8): Zone 1, Questions 1–3. Research SDR adoption (target 26 states + D.C. baseline from March 2026; verify any new state adoptions or repeals in 2026 legislative sessions). Research AVR evidence (24 states + D.C. baseline from November 2025; verify Georgia trajectory, the primary empirical case). Research SAVE Act state equivalents post-Senate failure — at minimum five states enacted state-level citizenship documentation requirements as of June 2026 (South Dakota, Utah, Kentucky, Mississippi, Florida); verify additional state enactments through November 2026 and update the Kansas precedent figures (31,000 prevented registrations / 12% of applicants). Contact Wendy Weiser (Brennan Center) with a specific Zone 1 question about Zone 5 state VRA status. Contact Charles Stewart III (MIT MEDSL) with a specific question about Zone 5 AVR adoption data. Time: 15h.

Days 6–7 (Nov 9–10): Zone 1, Questions 4–5. Voter purge documentation: update the 19.1M purge baseline (EAC 2024 EAVS); note that the next EAC comprehensive purge data won't be published until the 2026 EAVS (expected June 2027); document the DOJ national voter database initiative through current status (six federal courts dismissed through Sixth Circuit appellate ruling June 24; verify current status November 4). Produce Zone 1 Part 1 draft: approximately 4,000–5,000 words covering Questions 1–4. Time: 12h.

**Week 1 checkpoint gate** (Day 7, November 10): Zone 1 Part 1 draft at minimum 4,000 words. Source verification log complete. Two expert outreach emails sent (Weiser, Stewart). See Checkpoint Gate criteria below.

**Week 2 (November 11–17) — Zone 1 completion + Zone 2 opening**

## Contingency Scenarios

### Scenario A: Expert contacts slow to reply (−1 week buffer)

**Trigger**: By end of Week 2 (November 17), zero expert contact replies received.

**Impact assessment**: Expert contact replies are not critical-path dependencies for Zone 1 or Zone 2 research. All five primary research questions in Zone 1 are answerable from published sources (Brennan Center, EAC, SCOTUSblog, Stanford Law, redistrictingonline.org). Expert contact adds Zone 5-specific unpublished data and quote-level authority — it does not supply the primary evidence base.

**Response**: Continue research on published sources. Send one follow-up to each non-responsive expert contact by Day 12 (November 15). If still no reply by Day 18 (November 21), mark as non-responsive and proceed without. Do not delay Zone 3 or Zone 4 research to wait for Zone 1 expert replies. Activate replacement contacts (Levitt for redistricting; Sweren-Becker for technical state voting law questions; Patrick for administrator perspective) if primary contacts remain non-responsive after follow-up. This scenario costs approximately 5 hours of expert-chasing time and loses some Zone 5-specific unpublished data — it does not threaten the December 20 deadline.

### Scenario B: Source access blocked (+2 weeks recovery path)

**Trigger**: A primary source category becomes access-gated or otherwise unavailable during the research window (e.g., Brennan Center site restructure, JSTOR institutional access required, court document paywalled).

**Probability**: Low. Brennan Center has maintained open access since 2001. All court opinions are available via supremecourt.gov, congress.gov, or everycrsreport.com. The academic sources in the source library are either on SSRN (free) or available via researcher institutional access.

**Response**: If a specific source is unavailable, do not stop research — proceed with available sources and flag the unavailable source in a running access log. For JSTOR-hosted academic sources: researcher institutional access covers the research window; if no institutional access, JSTOR JPASS ($199/year annual plan; $19.50/month) resolves the access issue within 24 hours of purchase. For court documents: SCOTUSblog, EveryCRSReport, and Harvard Law's free legal resource library cover 95% of the court-document base without specialized access. For Democracy Works proprietary data: the 2024 and 2025 published impact reports are public; if internal data is needed and unavailable, estimate from published reports and note the limitation explicitly. This scenario adds approximately 10–15 hours of access-workaround time spread across the research window. It does not require a 2-week extension unless an entire source category becomes simultaneously unavailable — a low-probability event with no historical precedent in this source library.

If multiple source categories are simultaneously affected: compress Zone 3 to two international models instead of three (drop the Canada section to a 1,000-word summary rather than a full 2,500-word analysis), which frees approximately 10 hours for additional primary source documentation. This is the scope compression option before timeline extension.

### Scenario C: Deadline slip — scope compression options

**Trigger**: End of Week 5 (December 8) assessment shows the December 20 deadline is threatened — either total word count is below 28,000 (significantly behind pace) or key sections are unstarted.

**Scope compression Option C-1 (saves 1 week)**: Reduce Section 9 (Canada's Inspire Democracy) from a full 2,500–3,500 word section to a 1,000–1,500 word comparative note appended to Section 8 (Taiwan). Reduce Section 12 (Implementation Templates) from two full templates to one (keep voter registration drive; convert ballot guidance session to a 500-word outline rather than full template). This compression saves approximately 8–10 hours and eliminates no core research question — it reduces the depth of two less-critical sections.

**Scope compression Option C-2 (saves 1.5 weeks)**: Option C-1 plus: reduce Section 11 (Zone 5 Civic Action Toolkit) from five-state individual analyses to a combined Zone 5 analysis with state-specific notes in table format (saves 4–6 hours). Eliminate the distribution templates entirely from the Week 7 scope — they are a distribution task, not a research task, and can be produced post-December 20 if needed (saves 3 hours). Under Option C-2, the research document is complete and distribution-ready by December 20; the ancillary distribution materials follow in early January.

**Scope compression Option C-3 (hard deadline slip prevention)**: If Week 6 ends (December 15) with the document still under 35,000 words and unintegrated, the December 20 deadline becomes a partial-delivery target. Deliver Zones 1–3 (Sections 1–9) in complete form by December 20; flag Zone 4 (Sections 10–12) as "in final draft" with a January 10 completion date. This partial delivery still enables distribution to Tier 1 contacts before January 3 — Sections 1–9 cover voter registration barriers, technology tools, and international models, which are the highest-priority sections for civic organization audiences. Zone 4 (movement infrastructure and Zone 5 toolkit) is added for the broader January distribution wave. This is the last resort; Options C-1 and C-2 should be exhausted first.

## Solo vs. Two-Person Timeline Differences

The seven-week schedule above is calibrated for a solo researcher at 25–30 hours per week. The following adjustments apply if a two-person team is activated (per PHASE_3_RESEARCHER_PROFILE_AND_RECRUITMENT_PLAN.md Option B criteria):

**Two-person configuration** (activated if solo recruitment does not produce a firm commitment by October 1):

Researcher A owns Zones 1 and 2 (Weeks 1–3, approximately 90 hours). Researcher B owns Zones 3 and 4 (Weeks 2–4, starting Week 2 to allow sequential onboarding, approximately 85 hours). Both researchers contribute to Synthesis weeks (Weeks 5–7) under coordinator integration direction.

The two-person configuration gains approximately one week of parallel-track capacity: Zone 2 and Zone 3 can run simultaneously in Week 3 rather than sequentially. This creates a potential Week 3 completion gate of Zone 2 (Researcher A) plus Zone 3 opening (Researcher B) simultaneously — compressing the effective research window from seven weeks to six for the zone research, leaving a full three weeks for synthesis. Against this gain, coordination overhead (shared source deconfliction, weekly sync, integration consistency pass at Synthesis A and B) costs approximately 8–10 hours total across the project.

Net timeline confidence with two-person configuration: 88–92% (vs. 82% solo), per Phase 3 Researcher Recruitment Plan Option B analysis.

**Two-person specific checkpoint additions**: Add a Wednesday sync (30 minutes, async or call) in Weeks 2–4 when both researchers are actively in different zones. Agenda: source deconfliction (same sources cited differently?), zone boundary questions (where does Zone 2 technology end and Zone 3 international models begin for Estonia's i-Voting?), cross-zone coherence early warning.

**Two-person Synthesis A change**: Synthesis A (Week 5, Days 5–7) requires a joint integration session — both researchers read each other's zone outputs and flag contradictions, terminology inconsistencies, and cross-zone connection points. This session produces the coherence memo that Week 6 writes against. Budget 12 hours for Synthesis A instead of 10 in the two-person configuration.

## Integration with Post-Phase-6 Distribution Workflow

December 20 research completion feeds directly into the January distribution window:

- December 20: Final document submitted to coordinator for review
- December 22: Coordinator review complete; any revision requests returned
- December 23–26: Researcher completes revision requests (no more than 5 hours)
- December 27: Final document approved; distribution materials finalized
- December 28–January 2: Pre-seating distribution wave (Tier 1: Brennan Center, LWV, ACLU, NAACP LDF, Democracy Works; email template pre-positioned)
- January 3: 120th Congress seated
- January 6–10: Post-seating distribution wave (Tier 2: state-level civic organizations, Zone 5 contacts, academic partners)
- January 15: Full distribution complete

**Hard constraint**: Research must be in distribution before January 3. The December 20 final gate is the critical path node. All contingency scenarios above preserve December 20 delivery of at least a partial document. If full document delivery slips to December 22–27, the pre-seating distribution wave shrinks but the January 3 hard date is preserved.
