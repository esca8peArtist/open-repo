## Section 1: Fifteen-Minute Orientation

### What This Research Is and Why Now

This is the Democracy Tools domain — Domain G — of Phase 6 of a multi-phase resistance research project. Phase 5 documented community resilience in food, water, energy, governance, and psychological support. Domain G addresses the missing piece: the civic participation infrastructure through which communities influence the laws and policies that govern those systems. It treats voter registration, ballot guidance, and civic action not as political activities but as infrastructure — the mechanisms through which communities exercise collective power.

The timing is defined by two converging deadlines. First: the U.S. civic landscape has entered a period of acute structural stress that makes a practitioner guide urgently needed right now. The Supreme Court's April 29, 2026 decision in Louisiana v. Callais (6-3) weakened Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act to the point of functional inoperability for vote dilution claims. At least five states have enacted documentary proof-of-citizenship requirements that block an estimated 21 million eligible voters who lack ready access to the required documents. Between 2020 and 2022, more than 19 million voter registrations were purged — a 21 percent increase from the 2014–16 cycle. Second: the 120th Congress seats on January 3, 2027. Research must be complete and in distribution before that date. December 20 is the final gate. There is no flexibility on January 3.

### What Domain G Is

**One-sentence scope**: How do individuals, communities, and civic organizations navigate voter registration barriers, exercise ballot guidance rights, and deploy civic action infrastructure in the current federal and state legal environment — and what international models demonstrate what is achievable?

**What it is not**: This is not a national electoral reform document. The Electoral College, campaign finance reform, and federal constitutional amendments are context, not subject matter. This is not written for election administrators — it is written for participants: voters, organizers, civic technologists, and the community organizations that serve them. That audience distinction shapes every writing decision.

**What it adds to Phase 5**: Phase 5's community resilience work does not address how communities influence the laws that govern those resilience systems. Domain G is the missing layer — civic participation as the mechanism for exercising collective power within political systems, not just adapting around them.

### The Four Research Zones

The research is organized into four zones, each answering a different dimension of the "how do we participate" question:

**Zone 1 — Constitutional Vulnerabilities** (what the current legal environment looks like and what specifically has changed). Covers voter registration barriers: same-day registration (SDR) in 26 states; automatic voter registration (AVR) in 24 states; the SAVE Act's state-level proliferation post-Senate failure; voter roll purges (19.1 million in 2020–22); and the Callais ruling's cascade effects. This is the reality map — you need it before you can assess any solution.

**Zone 2 — Technology Solutions** (what civic technology tools exist with documented outcomes). Covers TurboVote (79% registered-user turnout vs. 64% nationally in 2024), ballot guidance platforms, open-source election technology (VoteSecure, ElectionGuard), AI-assisted civic engagement, and the 2026 midterm voter protection technology infrastructure.

**Zone 3 — International Models** (what other democracies demonstrate is possible). Covers Estonia's i-Voting system (20 years of practice), Taiwan's vTaiwan platform (AI-assisted deliberation, post-2024 volunteer-driven evolution), Canada's Inspire Democracy network (293 to 900 organizations for the April 2025 election), and comparative voter registration systems (countries with automatic enrollment achieving 90%+ registration rates).

**Zone 4 — Movement Infrastructure** (what organizational capacity exists and what the 2026–2028 activation sequence looks like). Covers the democracy movement's organizational landscape (Brennan Center, LWV Unite & Rise, ACLU, NAACP LDF, Democracy Works, BISC, Protect The Vote 2026); Zone 5 ballot initiative thresholds and pathways (Iowa, Minnesota, Wisconsin, Illinois, Missouri); and the civic action toolkit evidence base.

## Section 2: The 12 Research Questions

These are the questions the research must answer. Three per zone. For each question, the evidence standard expectation is stated: what counts as an adequate answer versus what counts as insufficient.

### Zone 1 Questions

**Q1. What is the empirical record of same-day voter registration (SDR) adoption and impact?**

As of March 2026, 26 states and D.C. have some form of SDR. Verify any changes through November 2026 (2026 state legislative sessions). Document registration rate improvements and turnout impacts in states that adopted SDR after 2018, with particular attention to Delaware, Minnesota, and Pennsylvania (all adopted 2023). Document the barriers to SDR adoption in the remaining 24 states.

Evidence standard: State-by-state outcome data from MIT Election Data and Science Lab or EAC EAVS; not sufficient to cite news coverage of SDR adoption without empirical outcome data.

**Q2. What is the evidence on automatic voter registration (AVR) impact?**

As of November 2025, 24 states and D.C. enacted AVR. The Center for American Progress projects universal AVR would register more than 22 million new voters in the first year. Georgia is the primary empirical case: active registration increased from 78% to 98% of eligible citizens in the first four years of AVR. Verify current state count at launch (Movement Advancement Project AVR tracker), any 2026 new adoptions, and the Zone 5 state-by-state status.

Evidence standard: Georgia empirical data verified against primary sources (Georgia Secretary of State, Brennan Center analysis); not sufficient to cite projection figures without the underlying state data.

**Q3. What is the SAVE Act's practical impact after Senate failure and state proliferation?**

The SAVE Act failed in the Senate in March 2026 (60-vote threshold not met). Five states enacted SAVE Act-equivalent citizenship documentation requirements as of June 2026 (South Dakota, Utah, Kentucky, Mississippi, Florida). Verify the current count at November 2026 launch. Kansas's prior implementation is the primary empirical predictor: 31,000 eligible citizens (12% of all applicants) prevented from registering. Apply Kansas precedent to estimate impact of state equivalents.

Evidence standard: Kansas data from Institute for Responsive Government and Votebeat primary reporting; state equivalents documented from state legislative records and Brennan Center tracker. Not sufficient to extrapolate from the federal SAVE Act text without state-by-state implementation documentation.

### Zone 2 Questions

**Q4. What explains TurboVote's documented 15-point turnout premium?**

Democracy Works' TurboVote achieved 79% turnout among registered users in 2024, outperforming national registered voter turnout (64%) by 15 percentage points. Under-25 users: 72% vs. 56% nationally. First-time users: 86% vs. 57% nationally. The research question is the attribution question: is the premium caused by the technology, or by selection effects (people who sign up for TurboVote are more civically engaged before signing up)?

Evidence standard: Primary Democracy Works impact reports (2024 Impact Report and 2025 annual report); address the selection effects question even if it cannot be fully resolved — state the honest methodological limitation. Not sufficient to quote the 79% figure without discussing attribution.

**Q5. What is the security architecture and deployment status of open-source election technology?**

VoteSecure (Bradley Tusk Foundation) and ElectionGuard (Microsoft, open-source) represent two approaches. ElectionGuard creates an encrypted record allowing voters to confirm their ballot was counted. What are the actual deployment records — where has each been deployed, with what outcomes? What are the documented barriers to deployment at the civic organization level (as opposed to election administrator level)?

Evidence standard: Deployment records from VoteSecure and ElectionGuard documentation, plus EAC Election Technology Clearinghouse; StateScoop reporting; not sufficient to describe the technology architecture without documented deployment cases.

**Q6. What voter protection technology infrastructure exists for the 2026 midterms, and what are its coordination gaps?**

Protect The Vote 2026 (No Small Act) built a 500,000-volunteer election protection program. The ACLU committed $24.5 million to voter protection for 2026. What technology platforms coordinate this work — volunteer management, incident reporting, real-time monitoring? The coordination gaps question is as important as the infrastructure description: where are the gaps that community organizations and individuals can fill?

Evidence standard: Protect The Vote 2026 published documentation (protectthevote2026.org); ACLU press release and follow-up reporting; not sufficient to describe the infrastructure without assessing what it does not cover.

### Zone 3 Questions

**Q7. What does Estonia's 20-year i-Voting record honestly demonstrate — including what it does not prove for U.S. contexts?**

Estonia has offered internet voting since 2005. The Standing Committee on Cybersecurity identified risk scenarios in October 2024 including auditability and misinformation concerns. The Free Software Foundation Europe has criticized partial source code publication. The honest assessment task requires giving equal weight to the documented successes (high adoption, no documented manipulation in 20 years) and the documented concerns (auditability, identity infrastructure dependency, centralized-system vulnerabilities).

Evidence standard: Primary e-Estonia documentation, Estonia e-Governance Academy sources, Wikipedia article (as navigation to primary technical sources, not as primary source itself), FSFE critique; transferability paragraph must identify 3–5 specific institutional prerequisites from Estonia's model and assess their presence or absence in U.S. contexts.

**Q8. What has Taiwan's vTaiwan platform demonstrated about AI-assisted digital deliberation, and how has it evolved since losing government support in 2024?**

vTaiwan facilitated over 20 legislative reviews. The 2019 Uber regulatory consultation gathered input from 4,000+ participants using Pol.is for opinion mapping. Since 2024, vTaiwan operates without direct government support as a volunteer-driven civic laboratory. January 2025 public experiments are documented. March 2025 National Human Rights Commission consultation is documented. The evolution question matters: what does it mean for U.S. civic adoption potential that vTaiwan can operate without government funding?

Evidence standard: Participedia entry (continuously updated — verify current version at launch); digicnx.network January 2025 article; People Powered case study; Bertelsmann Stiftung shortcut; transferability paragraph must address the decentralized, volunteer-driven model specifically.

**Q9. What does Canada's Inspire Democracy program demonstrate about civic engagement infrastructure at scale, and which elements are transferable to U.S. contexts without a government program structure?**

For the 45th general election (April 28, 2025), Inspire Democracy expanded from 27 to 40 contracted organizations (+48%) while the total network grew from 293 to approximately 900 organizations. Document the organizational models, outreach methodologies, and measurement frameworks. The transferability question is explicit: what requires a government-administered program (and is therefore not directly transferable), versus what can U.S. civic organizations replicate independently?

Evidence standard: Elections Canada April 2025 stakeholder mobilization report; Elections Canada Strategic Plan 2025–28; the transferability distinction is required — insufficient to describe the program without assessing it against U.S. institutional constraints.

### Zone 4 Questions

**Q10. What is the current organizational coordination landscape of the democracy movement, and where are the capacity gaps?**

Key organizations: Brennan Center (constitutional advocacy, monthly voting law roundups), League of Women Voters (Unite & Rise 8.5 million individual initiative), ACLU ($24.5M 2026 voter protection), NAACP LDF (Voting Rights 2026 campaign), Movement Voter Project (tech platform access), Democracy Works (TurboVote, Elections API), Ballot Initiative Strategy Center (tracking 150+ anti-ballot-measure bills in 2025), Protect The Vote 2026 (500,000-volunteer program). The capacity gaps question: where is coordination failing, and what can community organizations do that these national organizations are not doing?

Evidence standard: Current organizational websites and published reports; not sufficient to describe organizations without assessing their coordination with each other.

**Q11. What are the specific ballot initiative thresholds and deadlines in Zone 5 states for the 2026–2028 window?**

Zone 5 states: Iowa, Minnesota, Wisconsin, Illinois, Missouri. Document: (a) whether each state has a citizen-initiated ballot initiative process at all; (b) if yes, the signature threshold and geographic distribution requirement; (c) the filing deadline for 2028 measures; (d) whether any Zone 5 state has active anti-ballot-initiative legislation modeled on the 150+ bills tracked by BISC.

Evidence standard: State legislative databases and secretaries of state for thresholds and deadlines; Ballotpedia ballot initiative qualification requirements by state; Ballot Initiative Strategy Center for anti-ballot-initiative bills; the per-state format must be specific enough for a community organization in each state to use directly.

**Q12. What does the civic action toolkit evidence base show about which approaches produce measurable policy impact?**

Beyond the Ballot's 50x2026 campaign (all 50 states with civics education requirements), BISC's Defend Direct Democracy campaign, Muster's Grassroots Advocacy 101, Alliance for Justice Ballot Measures Toolkit. The evidence question is harder than the description question: which civic action approaches have documented policy outcomes versus which primarily build organizational capacity?

Evidence standard: Documented outcome evidence (policy changes, ballot measure qualification rates, legislative impacts) rather than self-reported organizational activity metrics; honest about the distinction between high-quality evidence and advocacy claims.

## Section 3: Source Access Standard Operating Procedures

### Academic Source Access

**If you have current university affiliation**: Use your institution's library portal. This covers JSTOR, most law review databases, and ProQuest. It is the zero-cost, highest-access path.

**If no current affiliation**: Purchase JSTOR JPASS annual plan: $199/year (120 PDF downloads). For this project's source load — approximately 60–80 JSTOR-hosted sources — the annual plan is appropriate. Claim as a project expense. Allow 24 hours for account activation. Do not wait until the research window opens; purchase by October 25 if no institutional access is available.

**Congress.gov, CRS reports**: Free. No account required. CRS reports published after January 2019 are public at crsreports.congress.gov. Older CRS reports accessible via EveryCRSReport.com.

**PACER** (federal court documents): Create a free account at pacer.uscourts.gov before November 4. PACER charges $0.10 per page but waives fees under $30 per quarter for low-usage accounts. The primary court documents needed for this project (Louisiana v. Callais, Ohio SB 293 suit, DOJ voter database litigation) are available via SCOTUSblog, Brennan Center, or Campaign Legal Center without PACER access for most research needs; PACER is useful for any direct document access.

**Brennan Center, Campaign Legal Center, Democracy Docket, EAC**: All free at their respective websites. No account required. EAC reports are all public at eac.gov.

**SCOTUSblog**: Free at scotusblog.com. The shadow docket tracker and case archives are primary sources. Do not cite Wikipedia or news coverage of court decisions when the actual SCOTUSblog analysis and slip opinions are available.

**If a source appears access-gated**: Do not stop research. Log the inaccessible source in a running access log (maintained in the project's Nextcloud folder). Proceed with available sources. Email the project coordinator with the specific source and describe what you need from it — there is almost always a publicly accessible alternative.

### PDF Request from Academic Libraries

For paywalled journal articles not on JSTOR: Use Interlibrary Loan (ILL) via your institutional library if you have current affiliation. Allow 3–5 business days for ILL fulfillment. If no institutional affiliation: email the article's lead author directly and request a PDF — academic authors routinely provide PDFs of their own published work. A brief email: "I am a researcher working on civic participation infrastructure. Could you share a PDF of your article [title]? Our project does not have institutional database access." Response rate is approximately 60–70% within one week.

## Section 4: Writing Standards and Templates

### What "Democracy Tools" Writing Sounds Like

This research is hybrid academic-advocacy writing. It is not a law review article (no extensive footnotes, no passive voice throughout, no hedged methodology sections that bury the finding on page 8). It is not an op-ed (not 800 words, not stripped of citation). The target reader is a civic organization program director or a community organizer with a college education and no specialized legal background, who has 30 minutes to read your document and needs to understand both what the evidence shows and what to do about it.

Concrete standards:

- Lead with the most important finding, not with the historical context. "As of 2026, 26 states have same-day voter registration" is not a lead — "Same-day voter registration's documented impact across 26 states shows a consistent 3–5 percentage point turnout increase in adoption years, with Minnesota's 2023 implementation producing the largest single-year increase in state history" is a lead.
- Every section ends with a clear practitioner implication, not a summary of what was just described.
- Technical legal terms (Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act, Gingles preconditions, preclearance, proof-of-citizenship requirement) are defined briefly the first time they appear in each section. Do not assume legal background.
- Confidence levels are stated explicitly when the underlying evidence is genuinely uncertain. Use: "the available evidence suggests..." or "this claim has not been independently verified as of the research window" rather than false precision.
- Active voice throughout. Passive voice is permitted when the actor is genuinely unknown or irrelevant ("31,000 eligible citizens were prevented from registering" — the actor is the administrative system, not a specific individual).
- Acronyms spelled out on first use per section (SDR, AVR, SAVE Act, VRA, EAC, MEDSL).

### Citation Format

Parenthetical in-text citations with a full references section at the end of the document.

In-text format: (Brennan Center, "State Voting Laws Roundup," October 2026) for reports; (Louisiana v. Callais, 2026) for court decisions; (Stewart and Loffredo, 2025) for academic papers.

References section format:
- Organization. "Title." Month Year. URL. Accessed [Month Year].
- Author Last, First. "Title." Journal or Publisher, Year. URL or DOI.
- Case Name, Volume (Court Year). URL to opinion.

**One non-negotiable**: All court opinions must be cited from primary sources (supremecourt.gov slip opinions, official state court websites, or SCOTUSblog) — not from news coverage of the ruling. All bill texts from congress.gov. All statistical claims from the original publishing organization's report — not from a secondary article citing that organization.

Wikipedia is a navigation tool to find primary sources, not a citable reference. The one exception: the Wikipedia article on Electronic Voting in Estonia is the most comprehensive organized overview of the technical literature and is acceptable as a supplementary reference when the primary technical claims it makes are verified against cited sources.

### Revision Procedures

**Feedback turnaround**: Project coordinator provides written feedback within 48 hours of draft submission. You have 72 hours to incorporate feedback and resubmit.

**Feedback rounds**: Maximum two rounds per section. If a section requires a third revision round, that indicates a scope or structure issue that should be resolved in a 20-minute coordinator call rather than another written revision cycle.

**What "revision success" means**: A revised draft is successful when the specific items flagged in the previous feedback round have been addressed. The coordinator reviews the flagged items against the new draft. If all are addressed, the section moves forward regardless of whether the coordinator would have written it differently. This research is your voice synthesizing the sources — the coordinator's job is to ensure claims are supported and the audience is correctly calibrated, not to impose a particular style.

**Escalation**: If you disagree with a feedback request, say so directly in your response with the reason. "I think the Section 3 lead should stay as is because..." is welcome. Unaddressed disagreement that shows up in repeated revision cycles is not.

### Citation Quality Floor (Non-Negotiable)

These are minimum standards. A draft that does not meet these standards will be returned for revision before moving to the next zone:

- No Wikipedia as a primary citation. Wikipedia is acceptable as a navigation tool to identify primary sources; it is not citable in the research document.
- All bill texts from congress.gov. Not from advocacy organization summaries.
- All court opinions from official sources: supremecourt.gov, state court websites, SCOTUSblog with slip opinion link.
- Statistical claims cited from the publishing organization's own report — not from a news article that cited the report.
- International comparative claims from official government sources (Elections Canada, e-Estonia, g0v documentation) or peer-reviewed scholarship — not from news articles.
