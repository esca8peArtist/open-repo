## Module 1: Governance Charter & Decision Framework

### What This Module Does
Establishes formal decision-making authority for your group (individual household, community, or institution). Required before any other system (food, security, resource allocation) can function legitimately.

### Scope
- **For households (8–25 people)**: One-page charter covering meeting schedule, roles, conflict resolution, resource-sharing rules
- **For communities (100+ people)**: Two to three-page charter with assembly structure, delegate roles, decision procedures, scaling thresholds
- **For institutions**: Memorandum of understanding (MOU) defining relationship, roles, communication channels, resource-sharing boundaries

### Time Estimate
- Household charter: 8–12 hours (meetings + drafting + ratification)
- Community charter: 20–30 hours (working group + assembly + feedback + revision)
- Institutional MOU: 12–16 hours (conversations + legal review + finalization)

### Prerequisite Reading
- **Phase 3**: 01-governance-decision-making.md (complete—study the Dunbar threshold, delegate council model, failure modes)
- **Phase 3**: 05-scaling-pathways-and-thresholds.md (complete—understand 150-person and 500-person transitions)
- **Phase 4**: PHASE_4_IMPLEMENTATION_FRAMEWORK.md, Section 3 (governance sequence)

### Execution Steps

**Week 1: Drafting**
1. Form charter committee (3–5 people for households; 5–7 for communities)
2. Answer core questions:
   - Who makes decisions? (assembly? leader? consensus? delegates?)
   - How often do you meet? (weekly crisis, monthly stability)
   - What requires a vote? (resource allocation, new members, conflict resolution)
   - Who enforces decisions? (how do you hold people accountable?)
   - What happens if someone violates the charter? (removal? mediation? escalation?)
3. Draft charter (use templates from Phase 3 governance document as reference)
4. Distribute draft to all members with feedback deadline

**Week 2: Review & Revision**
1. Gather feedback in writing or in-person meetings
2. Revise draft based on feedback
3. Address conflicts that emerge during review (good sign—governance is now a real question)

**Week 3: Ratification**
1. Hold ratification assembly (all members, formal vote)
2. Document decisions (who voted, any objections, final version)
3. Print and distribute final charter to all members
4. Schedule first recurring governance meeting (assign date/time for next 6 months)

### Success Criteria
- [ ] Charter approved by 75%+ of members (or unanimous consent for small groups)
- [ ] Explicit decision procedures written (how are votes called? quorum requirements?)
- [ ] Role descriptions documented (coordinator, conflict mediator, resource manager)
- [ ] First three governance meetings scheduled and dates distributed to all members
- [ ] Backup for every key role identified (no single point of failure in governance)

### Common Pitfalls
- **Overly complex procedure**: Start simple (monthly meeting, simple majority vote). Add complexity only after you've tested the basic structure.
- **No conflict resolution mechanism**: Charter without a way to resolve disputes is useless. Include at least mediation or elder council option.
- **Secret drafting**: If charter is drafted by committee without community input, people won't ratify it. Draft in public, revise iteratively.

### Integration Points
- Module 2 (Inventory) depends on governance (inventory is approved and maintained by governance)
- Module 3 (Education) depends on governance (mentors and apprenticeships are assigned by governance)
- Module 4 (Testing) depends on governance (decision-making drills validate governance structure)

## Module 2: Resource Inventory & Information System

### What This Module Does
Creates current baseline of what your household/community has (food, tools, fuel, medical supplies, skills) and establishes system to keep that inventory updated. Prerequisite for all food, security, and energy decisions.

### Scope
- Inventory form (one-page per household, or summary per community)
- Data collection and entry (central ledger—written, spreadsheet, or simple database)
- Update schedule (weekly for food, monthly for tools/fuel, quarterly for skills)
- Access protocols (who sees what? privacy boundaries?)
- Communication system (how do coordinators share inventory status?)

### Time Estimate
- Household inventory: 6–10 hours (form design + data collection + setup)
- Community inventory: 15–20 hours (form distribution + collection + data entry + system setup)

### Prerequisite Reading
- **Phase 3**: 03-information-infrastructure.md (read sections on GMRS, data recording, inventory)
- **Phase 4**: PHASE_4_IMPLEMENTATION_FRAMEWORK.md, Section 1 & 3 (when inventory matters most)

### Execution Steps

**Week 1: Design**
1. Create one-page inventory form covering:
   - Stored food (what, where, quantity, expiration)
   - Fuel (propane, wood, kerosene—quantity on hand)
   - Medical supplies (list of medications, quantities, expiration)
   - Tools and equipment (what, working condition, location)
   - Skills (who can do what: medical, mechanical, agricultural)
2. Decide on storage format (paper ledger, spreadsheet, or simple web form)
3. Decide on update cadence (weekly for food, monthly for others is standard)
4. Draft privacy policy (some people don't want entire community knowing their food stores)

**Week 2: Collection & Entry**
1. Distribute form to all households with deadline (1 week)
2. Follow up on non-responders (1–2 conversations per non-response)
3. Enter data into central ledger
4. Create summary report (total food days, fuel quantity, skilled people by domain)

**Week 3: System Establishment**
1. Designate inventory coordinator (1–2 people)
2. Establish weekly or monthly update process
3. Train all household reps on form and data entry
4. Schedule first update (two weeks after initial collection)
5. Share summary report with all members (build trust through transparency)

### Success Criteria
- [ ] Initial inventory form completed by 85%+ of households/community members
- [ ] Summary report generated and shared with all members
- [ ] Coordinator and backup designated
- [ ] Update schedule established and first update completed
- [ ] Privacy concerns addressed (people know what information is shared with whom)

### Common Pitfalls
- **Incomplete initial data**: Follow up on missing responses. Initial inventory must be 90%+ complete to be useful.
- **No backup system**: If coordinator is incapacitated, inventory knowledge disappears. Have at least 2 people trained.
- **Privacy violations**: If people feel their personal inventory is public knowledge, they'll hide information. Establish clear privacy boundaries.
- **Outdated information**: If you don't update on schedule, people stop trusting the inventory. Schedule updates and actually do them.

### Integration Points
- Module 1 (Governance) governs how inventory is accessed and updated
- Module 4 (Testing) validates inventory accuracy (7-day food test reveals actual vs. documented quantities)
- Module 5 (Succession) documents who maintains inventory if primary coordinator leaves

## Module 3: Education Framework & Skill Transfer

### What This Module Does
Establishes mentorship and apprenticeship structures so critical knowledge (water system maintenance, medical first aid, food preservation, equipment repair) transfers to multiple people before single expert is incapacitated.

### Scope
- Skills audit (who knows what, and how well)
- Mentorship pairing (expert + apprentice for each critical skill)
- Learning plan (what will apprentice learn, over what timeline, how will competency be verified)
- Peer teaching sessions (monthly or quarterly group skill-sharing events)

### Time Estimate
- Skills audit + pairing: 8–12 hours (interviews + matching + written plans)
- Quarterly teaching sessions: 4–6 hours per session (prep + delivery)

### Prerequisite Reading
- **Phase 1**: individual/05-healthcare.md (medical skills—apprenticeship model)
- **Phase 1**: individual/02-food.md (food preservation skills)
- **Phase 4**: PHASE_4_IMPLEMENTATION_FRAMEWORK.md, Section 1 (knowledge transfer as critical)
- **Off-grid-living**: 16-skills-knowledge.md (Tier 1–4 competency framework—adopt this)

### Execution Steps

**Week 1: Skills Audit**
1. Survey all members (1 question: "What skills do you have? What skills do you want to learn?")
2. Identify critical knowledge gaps:
   - Medical: Who does first aid? Who knows MARCH protocol? Who can splint or suture?
   - Food: Who preserves food? Who knows how to can, dry, ferment, root cellar?
   - Water: Who maintains water system? Who understands filters? Who can troubleshoot?
   - Equipment: Who repairs tools? Who can work on generators or engines?
3. Identify single points of failure (knowledge held by only one person)

**Week 2: Pairing & Planning**
1. Match experts with willing apprentices (1 mentor : 1–2 apprentices per skill)
2. Write simple learning plans for each pair:
   - **Skill**: What will be learned
   - **Timeline**: 3 months? 6 months?
   - **Method**: Hands-on training during routine tasks, or formal workshops?
   - **Competency test**: How will you know learning is complete? (skill demonstration, written test, documented achievement)
3. Get commitment from both mentor and apprentice (signed or verbal with witnesses)

**Week 3: First Mentorship Round**
1. Schedule first mentoring session (hands-on training with mentor during routine work)
2. Document what was taught (1-page note: date, skill, what was covered, progress)
3. Plan next session (when will mentor and apprentice meet again?)
4. Public peer-teaching session (first volunteer mentor teaches skill to larger group)

### Success Criteria
- [ ] Skills audit completed (90%+ response rate)
- [ ] At least 5 critical skills identified with single-person risk
- [ ] Mentorship pairings established for 90%+ of gap skills
- [ ] Learning plans written for each pair
- [ ] First mentoring sessions scheduled and at least 2 completed
- [ ] Quarterly group teaching session scheduled (all members invited)

### Common Pitfalls
- **Mentor refuses to teach**: Some experts hoard knowledge (intentional or not). Frame it as governance requirement or mutual aid obligation.
- **Apprentice gives up**: Mentoring takes time; apprentices lose motivation. Schedule regular sessions and provide small incentives (shared meals, public recognition).
- **No documentation**: If you don't write down what was taught, knowledge loss happens. Create simple 1-page templates for mentors to document sessions.
- **Competency not verified**: Mentoring without assessment produces false confidence. Have apprentices demonstrate skills before considering learning complete.

### Integration Points
- Module 1 (Governance) authorizes mentorship system and handles conflicts
- Module 4 (Testing) validates that apprentices actually learned skills (emergency drills)
- Module 5 (Succession) documents mentorship networks so knowledge persists even with turnover

## Module 4: Testing & Simulation Drills

### What This Module Does
Validates that systems actually work under stress conditions (not just in theory). Tests water, food, governance, communication, and medical response.

### Scope
- **Water test**: 72 hours using only on-site capacity and stored reserves (no external input)
- **Food test**: 7 days using only stored goods and homegrown/preserved foods (no external purchase)
- **Communication test**: Monthly GMRS radio check (all coordinators test, confirm battery status, document issues)
- **Governance drill**: Tabletop exercise—present a conflict scenario (contested resource allocation or authority dispute), measure time to decision
- **Medical drill**: Medical emergency scenario (injury, illness), measure response time and triage accuracy

### Time Estimate
- Water test: 8 hours (prep) + 72 hours (observation, usually in background)
- Food test: 2 hours (prep) + 7 days (observation)
- GMRS test: 1 hour monthly
- Governance drill: 3–4 hours (setup + exercise + debrief)
- Medical drill: 3–4 hours (scenario design + exercise + debrief)
- Total per quarter: 15–20 hours

### Prerequisite Reading
- **Phase 3**: 01-governance-decision-making.md (conflict scenarios and resolution)
- **Phase 1**: individual/02-food.md (food preservation and storage)
- **Phase 1**: individual/01-water.md (water capacity and filtration)
- **Phase 4**: PHASE_4_IMPLEMENTATION_FRAMEWORK.md, Section 5 (metrics and testing)

### Execution Steps

**Month 1: Water Test**
1. Announce date 2 weeks in advance (so people can prepare)
2. Cut off all external water input (stop using city water, stop filling tanks)
3. All water for 72 hours comes from on-site storage and generation
4. Document: gallons used per day, source breakdown, any shortages or rationing
5. Debrief: Did you have enough? What adjustments needed?

**Month 2: Communication Test**
1. Schedule radio check (e.g., second Sunday of month, 10 AM)
2. All GMRS equipment users check in on designated channel
3. Document: who checked in, who missed, battery status, signal quality, issues
4. Debrief with coordinator: replace batteries, fix antennas, update contact lists

**Month 3: Governance Drill**
1. Design scenario (e.g., "We have 30 days of food left but 40 people. Distribution decision needed. Some people want to ration equally; others want to prioritize children and elderly. What does assembly decide?")
2. Run tabletop exercise with full group (or representative group if large)
3. Measure: time from problem presentation to decision, level of agreement, whether process was followed
4. Debrief: Did governance process work? Would people accept the decision?

**Month 4: Food Test**
1. Announce date (people plan meals in advance)
2. No external food purchases for 7 days
3. All food from household stores, garden, or preserved goods
4. Document: what was eaten, what was unavailable, meals missed, rationing needed
5. Debrief: Realistic food security assessment

**Ongoing: Medical Drills**
1. Quarterly scenario (injury, illness, emergency) with medical coordinator
2. Time the response (alert to treatment)
3. Debrief: What gaps in training? What equipment was missing?

### Success Criteria
- [ ] Water test completed; actual vs. theoretical capacity documented
- [ ] Food test completed; 7 days of meals prepared from stores without purchase
- [ ] GMRS monthly checks completed (80%+ check-in rate within first test)
- [ ] Governance drill completed; decision reached within 2 hours, accepted by 70%+ of group
- [ ] Medical drill completed; response time under 15 minutes, triage protocol followed
- [ ] Results documented (findings, improvements needed, schedule for next test)

### Common Pitfalls
- **Tests done in ideal conditions**: Do tests during actual disruptions (power outage, illness) if possible. Routine tests are less revealing.
- **No documentation**: If you don't write results, you forget what worked and what failed. Create simple 1-page report for each test.
- **No follow-up**: Finding problems in tests is useless without fixing them. Assign repairs/improvements and schedule follow-up test.
- **Infrequent testing**: Quarterly testing at minimum. Anything less frequent and you lose institutional memory of how systems actually work.

### Integration Points
- All modules benefit from testing (governance works? communication works? people trained?)
- Module 5 (Succession) is verified through testing (does backup person understand their role?)

## Module 5: Succession Planning & Redundancy

### What This Module Does
Documents what happens if any key coordinator is incapacitated or leaves. Prevents single-point-of-failure collapse.

### Scope
- Role documentation (what does each coordinator actually do?)
- Backup designation (who takes over if primary person is unavailable?)
- Knowledge transfer (what must the backup know before taking over?)
- Document storage (where is critical information stored? who has access?)
- Training timeline (when will backup be trained to take over full responsibilities?)

### Time Estimate
- Role documentation: 8–10 hours (interviews with coordinators + writing)
- Backup training: ongoing (1–2 hours per month per role)

### Prerequisite Reading
- **Phase 4**: PHASE_4_IMPLEMENTATION_FRAMEWORK.md, all sections (every section mentions succession)
- **Module 3**: Education Framework (mentorship is the mechanism for succession)

### Execution Steps

**Week 1: Role Documentation**
1. For each critical role (water, medical, food, communication, security, governance):
   - Interview the current coordinator
   - Document: what do they do daily? weekly? monthly? What decisions do they make?
   - Document: what skills do they have? What would someone need to learn to do their job?
   - Document: what critical information do they hold (passwords, contacts, procedures)?
2. Write 1-page role description for each position

**Week 2: Backup Designation & Planning**
1. Current coordinator nominates a backup (or governance selects one)
2. Backup accepts role (this is a real commitment, not ceremonial)
3. Write training plan: what must backup learn, over what timeline?
4. Schedule first training session between primary and backup

**Week 3: Document Storage**
1. Collect all critical information for each role (contact lists, system diagrams, decision trees, procedures)
2. Store in waterproof, accessible location (physical copies preferred—not digital only)
3. Ensure backup has access and knows where to find information
4. Update master document list and distribute to governance coordinator

**Ongoing: Training & Validation**
1. Primary coordinator trains backup monthly (1–2 hours)
2. Backup shadows primary during routine work
3. Every 6 months, have backup assume full role for 1 week while primary observes
4. Update training progress in role documentation

### Success Criteria
- [ ] Role description written for every critical coordinator (water, medical, food, communication, security, governance)
- [ ] Backup designated for 100% of roles
- [ ] Training plan written for each backup
- [ ] Critical information documented and stored accessibly
- [ ] First training session completed for each backup
- [ ] Governance knows succession plan (included in charter review)

### Common Pitfalls
- **Backups are ceremonial**: Real succession requires actual training. Backup must be able to do the job if called on tomorrow.
- **Documentation is incomplete**: Role descriptions that miss critical tasks are useless. Interview the actual coordinator; ask "what would break if you left?"
- **No regular training**: Training happens once, then stops. Schedule regular (monthly) sessions and treat them as mandatory.
- **Documents not accessible**: If backup doesn't know where information is stored or can't access it, succession fails. Test that backup can actually retrieve and use critical documents.

### Integration Points
- Module 1 (Governance) includes succession plan in charter review
- Module 3 (Education) is the mechanism—mentorship pairs are succession pipelines
- Module 4 (Testing) validates that backups are actually trained (can they step in during simulated emergency?)
