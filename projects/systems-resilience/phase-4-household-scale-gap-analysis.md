# Phase 4 Household-Scale Gap Analysis: 8–25 Person Infrastructure

**Most important finding**: The 8–25 person scale is the most structurally underserved tier in existing resilience literature. Phase 2 (household/01-household-coordination-overview.md) covers 2–5 households (8–20 people) through resource pooling and informal coordination, and Phase 3 community-scale research begins at 100 people. Between roughly 20 and 100 persons lies a zone where informal coordination has broken down but formal community governance has not yet been installed. The five dimensions below — coordination infrastructure, conflict resolution, psychological support, education/skill transfer, and equipment maintenance — each show specific failure modes unique to the 8–25 person scale that cannot be resolved by scaling either the household or community-scale research.

---

## Dimension 1: Household Coordination Infrastructure

### Preliminary Findings

**What the existing research covers**: The Phase 2 document (household/01-household-coordination-overview.md) establishes that 3-household clusters of approximately 12 people can coordinate through domain-specialist authority, pooling intermittent equipment, and sharing weekly radio check-ins. That model works up to approximately 5 households / 20 people before coordination overhead scales faster than the resilience benefit.

**The 8–25 person gap**: When a mutual aid network or resilience cluster grows past the point where everyone knows everyone's schedule and skill set, three specific failures emerge that are absent at the 3-household scale and already solved at community scale:

1. **Inventory blindness**: At 3 households, one person in one conversation can track all shared assets. At 8 households (25 people), the asset list spans two dozen items across six storage locations, and informal tracking breaks down. Physical inventories get depleted without notification. The NYC-area Bushwick Ayuda Mutua mutual aid network solved this by adopting Airtable for all resource tracking from the first month of operation, enabling any member to see current stock across multiple collection hubs without a centralized coordinator. Their workflow — request form triggers a row in Airtable, which routes to scheduled distribution installathons, tracked in a private Slack channel — applies directly to a 20-person cluster maintaining shared food, fuel, and medical supplies.

2. **Labor accounting opacity**: Phase 2 handles labor through informal household exchange ("we helped them harvest, they'll help us next month"). Above 10–12 persons, unreciprocated labor creates resentment that ends networks. Research on cooperative housing maintenance consistently identifies labor equity as the primary trigger for community dissolution in the 10–30 person range. The Foundation for Intentional Community (FIC) recommends explicit labor hour tracking — even a simple monthly spreadsheet — for any group exceeding 10 adults engaged in shared work.

3. **Supply distribution equity**: When resources become genuinely scarce (fuel shortage, harvest shortfall), a 25-person network faces a distribution problem that 6-person households do not: who gets priority? Without explicit allocation criteria, the loudest or most central members capture supply. The FEMA Mutual Aid guidance and the North Carolina community resource exchange toolkit both recommend priority tiering set in advance by group vote — not by coordinators deciding in the moment.

**What works at 8–25 person scale**:
- **Hub-and-spoke supply distribution** with a single designated collection point per resource category, maintained by one rotating household per quarter, visited by all households on a defined schedule
- **Shared digital infrastructure** using free-tier Airtable, Google Sheets, or Signal groups for real-time inventory updates — critical once the group exceeds memory-scale tracking
- **Labor token systems**: simple paper ledger or shared spreadsheet tracking hours contributed per household per month; visible to all members; settled quarterly; prevents free-rider dynamics before they calcify into resentment
- **Documented allocation criteria**: one-page written priority tiers for scarce resource distribution, agreed by the group before scarcity, stored with the governance agreement

**What does not scale from Phase 2**:
- Weekly verbal check-ins as the primary information system
- Trust-based resource sharing without written accounting
- Single-person memory as inventory management
- Emergency decisions made by informal consensus among whoever is present

**Gap not covered by Phase 3**: Phase 3 community-scale governance includes formal rotating councils, elected coordinators, and formal treasury functions — all correct for 100+ people but imposing prohibitive overhead for 25. The gap is a lightweight coordination layer: structured enough to track assets and labor, light enough that it does not require dedicated coordinators.

## Dimension 2: Conflict Resolution at Household Scale

### Preliminary Findings

**The unique challenge**: A 12–15 person household or mutual aid cluster sits at exactly the worst scale for conflict: large enough that interpersonal conflicts ripple through the whole group and cannot be handled quietly, but small enough that formal mediation procedures feel bureaucratic and alienating. The research on intentional communities consistently identifies the 10–20 person range as the highest-conflict phase of community development — after the honeymoon of initial organizing, before the development of institutional conflict norms.

**What the FIC and cooperative housing research shows**: The Foundation for Intentional Community's recent curriculum identifies four core governance questions communities must answer: how to make decisions, how to self-regulate tensions, how to make meaning together, and how to care for each other's needs. Their instructors report that communities in the 8–25 person range most often fail on the second question — self-regulation — because they import either household-scale resolution (private conversation) or community-scale resolution (formal mediation panels) without developing the intermediate form appropriate to their size.

**Sociocracy at 8–25 person scale**: The sociocratic consent model — "no significant objections" rather than unanimous agreement — has been successfully adopted by cohousing communities of 8–30 units. The Collective Spaces Farm case study documents a community that adopted sociocracy after experiencing decision stalls and members feeling unheard. The immediate results: reduced meeting length, reduced relitigating of settled decisions, and a dramatic reduction in interpersonal conflict arising from governance process frustration. The Cohousing Association of the United States now recommends sociocracy or a hybrid sociocracy/consensus model for groups of 6–20 households. The mechanics for an 8–25 person cluster:
- **Circles** of 4–8 people with domain authority (food, energy, health, coordination)
- **Consent-based decisions** within circles; cross-circle decisions require a brief plenary with structured rounds
- **Objection protocol**: objections must be reasoned, not preferential — "this will harm the group's ability to meet its aims" rather than "I don't like it"
- **Double-linking**: circles connect through two people who carry information and accountability both ways

**Restorative circles and VORP principles**: For interpersonal conflict (not governance disputes), restorative approaches outperform adjudicative approaches in small communities for a specific structural reason: the parties will continue living and working together. Victim-Offender Reconciliation Programs, originating with Mennonite community practice in Elkhart, Indiana (1978), established that voluntary face-to-face dialogue between those in conflict, facilitated by a trained neutral, produces repair agreements that hold in ongoing relationship contexts. The International Institute for Restorative Practices (IIRP) documents that restorative circles — bringing together those who acted, those directly impacted, and the wider community — work specifically for communities small enough that everyone knows everyone affected. At 25 people, that circle is exactly the right size; it would be too unwieldy at 200.

**NASCO cooperative housing protocols**: The North American Students of Cooperation (NASCO) maintains one of the most practical conflict resolution curricula for small cooperative communities. Their framework distinguishes:
1. **Direct resolution**: parties address conflict bilaterally before involving the group
2. **Supported resolution**: one or two trusted community members sit with both parties, not to judge but to facilitate
3. **Group process**: issue brought to plenary when it affects the community's functioning
4. **External mediation**: rarely needed; reserved for situations where trust in internal neutrals has broken down

The critical finding from NASCO's community surveys: groups that skip step 1 and go directly to step 3 have dramatically worse outcomes — the group becomes the arena for interpersonal conflict, which burns out members and fractures social cohesion faster than the original conflict would have.

**Authority structures under stress**: The cohousing research from Wrigleys Solicitors' cooperative housing practice documents a consistent finding: conflict resolution protocols written during calm periods hold under stress only if the authority to invoke them is clear and pre-delegated. Groups that require full consensus to begin a mediation process cannot initiate that process when one party is unwilling to participate. Effective small-community conflict protocols specify one named person (rotating quarterly) who has authority to declare a situation a community conflict requiring a supported resolution process — no veto by the parties in conflict on whether the process begins.

**What fails at this scale**:
- Pure consensus (stalls at 12+ people under stress)
- Majority voting (creates persistent minority who feel overridden; worse in small communities where the minority lives next door)
- Informal leader authority (fine for decisions, corrosive for interpersonal conflict where authority is seen as taking sides)
- No protocol at all (default to avoidance → resentment → exit)

## Dimension 3: Psychological Support Infrastructure

### Preliminary Findings

**The structural problem**: Crisis psychology research is built on two population sizes — individuals/families (1–6 people) and large-scale community/disaster response (hundreds to thousands). The 8–25 person range falls in a gap where neither framework applies cleanly. Critical Incident Stress Debriefing (CISD) teams typically use 1 trained facilitator per 5–7 participants — correct sizing for a 10–20 person group — but CISD is designed for discrete critical incidents (a firefighter crew after a fatality), not extended low-grade crisis conditions (a mutual aid cluster navigating 6 months of supply disruption and social stress).

**Pre-disaster social infrastructure is the primary protective factor**: A systematic review of post-disaster mental health outcomes across Hurricane Sandy, Australian wildfires (627 participants, 12–18 months post-disaster), and Nepal earthquake communities consistently identifies the same finding: stronger pre-existing social group connections predict lower distress and higher resilience at follow-up, independent of material losses. The wildfire study found that social group identification with the disaster-affected community was a significant predictor of both reduced PTSD symptomatology and greater resilience. This reframes psychological support infrastructure: its primary function is building cohesion before crisis, not intervening after.

**Peer support effectiveness at small scale**: A 2020 systematic review of peer support interventions for public safety and healthcare workers under crisis conditions (PMC7589693, n=18,849) found limited but favorable evidence for peer support, with "small but potentially important short-term results." The review's limitations are significant — 12 of 14 studies at high bias risk — but the qualitative evidence points consistently toward peer support being more acceptable and more sustained than professional intervention in small teams under extended crisis. The mechanism appears to be social permission: peer supporters normalize reactions ("what you're feeling is expected given what we're all dealing with") in a way that professional intervention cannot achieve without stigma.

**What works at 8–25 person scale**:

1. **Psychological First Aid (PFA) at peer level**: FEMA's PFA framework for CERT teams scales directly to 8–25 person clusters. The model is: Listen (genuine interest, non-judgmental), Protect (stabilize acute distress, connect to basic needs), and Connect (link to peer and community support). One or two PFA-trained cluster members can apply this framework without clinical authority. FEMA offers free online PFA certification (IS-360, 30 minutes).

2. **Regular structured emotional check-ins**: The Critical Incident Stress Management (CISM) literature documents that structured group check-ins — 15–30 minutes, dedicated to how people are doing rather than logistics — significantly reduce burnout rates in emergency response teams of 5–20 people. The format is simple: each person has 90 seconds uninterrupted, no problem-solving, no advice, just report. Facilitated weekly.

3. **Leadership rotation specifically for burnout prevention**: Research on leaders' mental health during crisis conditions (Frontiers in Psychology, 2023) documents that crisis leadership carries a specific dual burden — managing one's own stress while providing psychological resources (feedback, inspiration, support) to the group. This burden is unsustainable in one person over extended periods. Effective small-community crisis teams rotate the leadership-facing burden across 3–4 people on 2–4 week cycles, ensuring no single person holds the coordinator role continuously for more than a month.

4. **Exit pathway normalization**: Cohousing health research (21 studies, PMC7539375) identifies "sense of security" as one of the strongest predictors of positive outcomes — specifically, residents who knew they could leave if needed reported higher wellbeing than those who felt trapped. Psychologically, a 25-person crisis cluster must establish explicit off-ramp protocols: how can a member reduce participation or exit temporarily without stigma or social rupture? Groups that cannot answer this question generate chronic anxiety in members who are struggling.

5. **Trauma-informed maintenance of group identity**: The Australian wildfire research found that continuity of social group identity — maintaining pre-crisis group memberships and forming new ones — predicted resilience but that new groups formed "out of necessity" rather than choice were associated with both greater resilience and greater distress simultaneously. The practical implication for 8–25 person clusters: do not dissolve pre-existing subgroup identities when forming a larger resilience network. Preserve family units, friendship pairs, and household identities within the larger cluster; do not demand that the cluster become the primary identity.

**The burnout cascade**: Leadership burnout in small crisis groups follows a predictable sequence — coordinator absorbs disproportionate emotional labor → coordinator becomes irritable → group mistakes irritability for poor leadership → trust erodes → coordination quality falls → material failures → crisis worsens → coordinator burns out completely. The intervention point is early rotation and explicit acknowledgment that emotional labor is labor that must be tracked and distributed like physical labor.

**What the evidence does not support**: CISD as a one-time group debriefing after extended crisis conditions. The evidence for CISD is weakest precisely in extended, complex situations; some studies find debriefed participants report higher stress at 5-year follow-up. For acute incidents, CISD may help. For the extended low-level crisis conditions characteristic of resilience contexts, ongoing peer support and regular check-ins outperform structured debriefing.

## Dimension 4: Education and Skill Transfer

### Preliminary Findings

**The andragogy foundation**: Malcolm Knowles' adult learning theory (andragogy) identifies six core assumptions about adult learners that distinguish effective adult skill-transfer from pedagogical (child-learning) approaches: adults are self-directed, bring accumulating experience that is a primary learning resource, are ready to learn when they face a practical problem, are problem-oriented rather than subject-oriented, are internally motivated, and need to know why they are learning something before they will engage. All six assumptions are heightened in crisis contexts — adults in a 20-person resilience cluster will reject abstract skill training but respond strongly to "we need three people who can operate this well pump when I'm not available." The "why" is immediate.

**The craft guild model's relevance**: Historical guild apprenticeship combined household-scale living arrangements with structured skill progression — masters and apprentices lived and worked in shared space, with the bulk of learning informal and observational. Contemporary research on informal craft skill ecosystems (International Journal of Training and Development, 2023) finds that the tacit knowledge component of craft skills — the feel for when fermentation is ready, the sound a healthy engine makes, the visual cues of plant disease — transfers almost exclusively through close observation and supervised practice, not through documentation or formal instruction. This has direct implications: a 20-person cluster attempting to cross-train water system maintenance through written documentation alone will fail; the documentation supports but cannot replace hands-on supervised sessions.

**The 8–25 person scale advantage**: A 3-person household cannot practically maintain deep specialization and broad cross-training simultaneously — there are not enough people. A 200-person community has so many skill domains that systematic cross-training across the full group becomes impractical. The 8–25 person range is the optimal cross-training unit: enough people that some specialization is possible, few enough that each person can achieve functional competency in 4–6 of the most critical domains within one year through monthly sessions.

**What works at 8–25 person scale**:

1. **Domain rotation training**: One 2-hour hands-on session per month, covering one domain per month, rotating through 8–12 critical domains per year. Each session run by the domain specialist. Phase 2 (household/01-household-coordination-overview.md) identifies this model; the 8–25 person version requires formalizing attendance tracking and explicit skill-level assessments so the group knows its actual cross-training coverage.

2. **Three-level skill assessment**: Rate each member in each domain at intake and annually: 0 (no knowledge), 1 (can operate under supervision), 2 (can operate independently), 3 (can teach others). A 25-person cluster targeting Level 2 competency in each critical domain for at least 3 members per domain has a functioning redundant skill structure. This is the minimum threshold — no single person's absence creates an operational gap.

3. **Learning during crisis — the "teach while doing" protocol**: Conventional training assumes leisure time. Crisis conditions eliminate leisure. The substitute is embedded instruction: when performing any critical system operation, the operator narrates the procedure aloud and specifically asks a nearby watcher to practice one step. Medical training calls this "see one, do one, teach one." For resilience clusters, the adaptation is: every critical operation is performed with at least one observer, and the observer practices the operation within 48 hours under supervision.

4. **Knowledge documentation as institutional memory**: The Phase 2 document establishes the three-ring binder model. At 8–25 person scale, this must be formalized: every domain has a documented procedure, verified annually by the person who performs it (not written by someone who thinks they know how it works), stored in at least two physical locations and one offline-accessible digital location. The intentional community literature (Schiavelli & Gumprecht, 2018, on Dancing Rabbit Ecovillage) identifies undocumented knowledge as the primary operational failure point when key members leave.

5. **Apprenticeship pairing**: For skills requiring extended practice (food preservation, medical procedures, equipment repair), informal apprenticeship pairing — one senior person + one junior person working together on actual operations, not simulations — outperforms workshop-style instruction for retention and confidence. The historical craft guild model's effectiveness at knowledge transfer derived from exactly this structure.

**Gaps at this scale**: No existing framework specifically addresses skill transfer during active crisis conditions when normal training time is unavailable. The military has developed stress inoculation training (exposing trainees to realistic stress conditions before deployment) but this is not accessible or appropriate for civilian resilience clusters. The informal learning literature documents that learning under stress produces stronger retention but narrower skill acquisition — people learn exactly what they needed to solve the immediate problem and nothing adjacent. This suggests crisis-time training should focus on one specific task per session rather than any attempt at comprehensive instruction.

**Integration with formal adult education**: Community colleges and extension services (USDA Cooperative Extension, land-grant universities) offer free or low-cost hands-on training in food preservation, well system maintenance, basic electrical, and first aid that 8–25 person clusters can access as a group. The Fairfax County Adult and Community Education (ACE) apprenticeship program model is replicable in most Midwest counties through local extension services. Attending as a group of 4–6 people from the cluster produces better cross-training outcomes than individual enrollment because group members teach each other afterward.

## Dimension 5: Equipment Maintenance and Repair

### Preliminary Findings

**The maintenance gap at 8–25 person scale**: A single household managing its own equipment handles maintenance reactively — things break, someone fixes them. A Phase 3 community of 100+ can afford dedicated maintenance staff or contracted services. The 8–25 person cluster sits in between: too many assets and people to rely on reactive maintenance, not enough people to dedicate anyone to full-time equipment management. The result, in practice, is deferred maintenance that accumulates until a failure occurs at the worst possible moment.

**What community tool libraries have learned**: Portland's network of community tool libraries — Northeast Portland Tool Library (NEPTL), Southeast Portland Tool Library (SEPTL), and North Portland Tool Library (NPTL) — collectively manage thousands of items with fully volunteer staffing and annual budgets under $10,000. The NPTL reported 7,364 tool loans in a single year, saving users an estimated $447,205 (approximately $60 per loan). Their operational protocols are directly applicable to 8–25 person resilience clusters:

- **Standardize by brand**: Where possible, stock tools from one manufacturer per category. This makes maintenance simpler (one parts supplier, one repair manual, one diagnostic process) and reduces learning overhead for repair volunteers.
- **Mandatory return condition**: Items return clean, functional, and at operating condition (fuel topped up, batteries charged, filters clean). This distributes maintenance labor to the point of use rather than accumulating it.
- **Broken-item tagging**: A physical tag on any item returned with a problem, with a description of the issue. Prevents broken equipment from being checked out by the next user who will discover the problem in the field.
- **Repair role as gateway volunteer function**: Creating a specific "repair volunteer" role — no expertise required, just willingness to follow a checklist and learn — turns equipment maintenance into a community engagement opportunity rather than a burden on one or two people.

**The repair cafe / Fixit Clinic model**: The Transition Berkeley repair cafe events document a 70% repair success rate (142 of 203 items) with 20% partial repair — 90% of items brought in had their lifespans extended. The model's key innovation is co-repair: participants sit with repair coaches and work through the problem together rather than dropping off items for experts to fix. For an 8–25 person cluster, a quarterly co-repair session where the group brings all shared equipment for inspection, and at least two people learn the diagnostic and repair process for each item, achieves both maintenance and skill transfer simultaneously.

**Right-to-repair legislative context**: As of 2025–2026, Oregon (strongest law, effective January 1 2025), Colorado (effective January 1, 2026), and several other states have enacted right-to-repair legislation requiring manufacturers to provide parts, tools, and diagnostic information for electronics, appliances, and agricultural equipment. This significantly reduces parts sourcing difficulty for cluster maintenance. iFixit's community repair wiki and repair manuals database (free) covers thousands of common household items. For agricultural and generator equipment, manufacturer service manuals are now legally required to be accessible in Oregon and Colorado — Midwest states are 2–4 years behind but the legislation is advancing.

**Maintenance scheduling protocols**: The Shareable Library of Things Toolkit identifies a common failure mode in volunteer-maintained inventory: maintenance happens reactively (when something breaks visibly) rather than proactively (before failure). The toolkit recommends:
1. Each item's manufacturer service interval documented at acquisition
2. A shared maintenance calendar with item-specific check dates
3. A designated "maintenance steward" per item category (not all items — water pump steward, generator steward, food processing steward)
4. A simple condition rating system updated every use: Green (fully operational), Yellow (functional but needs attention within 30 days), Red (out of service)

**Parts and supply sourcing**: Resilience clusters operating in rural Midwest face longer supply chains than urban networks. Effective parts sourcing strategy:
- **Buffer inventory**: For every critical shared item, maintain the top 3–5 most commonly replaced components (filters, belts, spark plugs, seals) on-hand. A $150–$300 parts buffer for a generator eliminates the 4-day wait documented in the Phase 2 winter failure scenario.
- **Supplier relationships**: Establish account relationships with one regional farm supply store and one regional equipment dealer before parts are needed. Accounts receive priority fulfillment and occasional lead-time information ("we're seeing carburetor kits going fast before winter").
- **iFixit and manufacturer resources**: iFixit's repair manuals, combined with right-to-repair access to manufacturer documentation, eliminate most diagnostic uncertainty for electronics and appliances. For mechanical equipment, the owner's manual plus one service manual is a minimum reference library.

**What the maker community has learned**: The maker/hackspace movement has developed protocols for collective equipment ownership and maintenance that apply directly. Core principles from the Open Source Circular Economy and Fablabs: equipment lives where it is maintained, the person who uses equipment most frequently is the best maintainer of that equipment, and public documentation of repair history turns individual fixes into institutional knowledge. For a 25-person cluster, this means the person who runs the pressure canner maintains the canner and documents each maintenance action in a shared log.

### The Case for Household-Scale as Phase 4 Focus

The five dimensions above confirm the June 1 decision framing in PHASE_4_SCOPE_AND_DECISION_FRAMEWORK.md. The 8–25 person scale is not a minor interpolation between Phase 2 and Phase 3 — it is a distinct tier with its own failure modes, its own effective protocols, and its own literature base. The primary evidence:

**Structural distinctiveness**: Each dimension identifies at least one failure mode that is absent at 3-household scale and already solved at community scale. Inventory tracking, conflict authority structures, burnout cascade prevention, crisis-time skill transfer, and preventive maintenance scheduling all emerge as critical issues specifically in the 8–25 person range. This is not a gap that fills itself through scaling either Phase 2 or Phase 3 protocols.

**Integration value**: The household-scale protocols described in these five dimensions are not only valuable in themselves — they are the prerequisite practices for Phase 3 community governance to function. Communities that skip the 8–25 person coordination layer and attempt to jump directly to Phase 3 formal governance consistently fail in the first year on exactly the dimensions described here: inventory disputes, interpersonal conflict without resolution pathways, burnout of founding coordinators, skill single-points-of-failure, and equipment failures from deferred maintenance.

**Research accessibility**: Unlike Phase 3 community governance (which requires field research in active communities) or regional-scale federation (which requires governmental and inter-organizational case studies), the 8–25 person scale has robust accessible literature in intentional communities, cooperative housing, mutual aid networks, peer support psychology, adult education, and community tool libraries. Phase 4 research in this scope can draw on primary empirical studies and practitioner documentation of sufficient quality to meet academic/practitioner citation standards.
