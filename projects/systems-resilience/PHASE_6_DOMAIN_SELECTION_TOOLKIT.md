## Part 1: Selection Criteria

Five criteria distinguish among the three candidates. They are listed in order of decision weight — the criteria that should move your selection most are at the top.

### 1. Source Library Readiness (Weight: High)

This criterion determines how quickly production can begin after June 1. High readiness means the research agent can begin drafting within the first week; low readiness means a 7–10 day source sprint before production starts, which compresses the delivery window.

The readiness threshold matters because Phase 6 domains are large (45,000–60,000 words). An 8–10 day pre-production delay at the start translates directly into a 1–2 week slip at the back end. If you are targeting August 23 completion, a domain with low source readiness has approximately 78% on-time confidence vs. 95% for a high-readiness domain.

**Benchmark**: 75%+ source readiness = production begins June 8. Below 70% = production begins June 15 at earliest.

### 2. Scope Containment Risk (Weight: High)

Phase 6 domains are each 45,000–60,000 words with 120–150 citations. The literature for all three candidates extends well beyond those limits. The question is: how actively must the research agent manage scope to stay within the document bounds?

Scope containment risk is the most common cause of Phase 6 timeline slippage in multi-domain projects of this type. A domain that can be bounded with a simple geographic and scale filter (Zone 5, 20–200 person communities) is operationally safer than a domain where the relevant literature keeps opening into adjacent questions that are genuinely important but not within scope.

**Benchmark**: Low expansion risk = the Zone 5 + crisis-context + scale filters narrow the literature to a manageable set. High expansion risk = domain boundary requires active weekly enforcement.

### 3. Cross-Domain Leverage (Weight: Medium-High)

Some domains unlock downstream work. A domain with high leverage produces templates and frameworks that Phase 7 and later phases can build on directly. A domain with lower leverage produces useful standalone content that does not multiply.

For a 12-week research investment, high-leverage domains produce more total value to the corpus. However, leverage is subordinate to readiness and scope containment — a high-leverage domain that slips by 3 weeks does not outperform a medium-leverage domain that delivers on time.

### 4. Synergy with Phase 5 Publication (Weight: Medium)

Whichever Phase 5 publication option is selected (A, B, or C), reader feedback will arrive in June. High synergy means the Phase 6 domain answers questions that Phase 5 readers are likely to raise — it deepens reader engagement and creates a natural "Phase 6 arrives just as you need it" narrative. Low synergy means Phase 6 can proceed independently of reader response.

This criterion is secondary to the operational criteria above. Domain selection should not be driven primarily by anticipated reader feedback — Phase 6 was scoped based on corpus gaps, not audience requests.

### 5. User Interest Signals and Regional Fit (Weight: Medium)

The Phase 1–5 corpus has a consistent thematic orientation: Zone 5 Midwest, agricultural tradition, cooperative institutions, community-scale governance. Domains that fit this thematic orientation tightly will produce more regionally grounded content than domains where the regional application is a thin overlay on a generic framework.

## Part 2: Per-Candidate Scorecards

The three candidates — Community Economic Resilience (A), Skills Development and Knowledge Transmission (C), and Governance Scaling (D) — were fully analyzed in Session 1707 (`PHASE_6_DECISION_SUPPORT.md`). This section consolidates the scorecard data into structured per-candidate assessments.

---

### Candidate A: Community Economic Resilience

**What it fills**: Phase 5 documents production (food, water, power, healthcare) but says nothing about the exchange layer. When the dollar fails, who gets what? How does the farmer pay the medic? How are surpluses allocated without market price signals? Community Economic Resilience answers these questions, providing the economic operating system for the Phase 5 community.

**Target word count**: 45,000–55,000 words (10–12 sections)  
**Source library readiness**: 75–80% (Green)  
**Production start date**: June 8  
**Estimated completion**: August 9 (single-domain, 95% confidence)  
**On-time confidence (August 23, paired)**: 90%

**Core source anchors**:
- Elinor Ostrom, *Governing the Commons* (eight design principles for commons governance — directly applicable)
- David Graeber, *Debt: The First 5,000 Years* (anthropological grounding for alternative exchange systems)
- WIR Bank documentation (70+ years operational mutual credit, Switzerland; $3B+ annual transactions)
- Bristol Pound and Totnes Pound impact evaluations (UK complementary currency case studies)
- ICA cooperative movement standards; Cooperative Development Foundation Midwest data
- 2025 peer-reviewed study, *Journal of Economic Behavior and Organization* (mutual credit resilience, firm-level evidence)
- Iowa State Extension Service agricultural cooperative data; Land O'Lakes governance records

**Documented evidence gaps**: Timebanking quantitative effectiveness data is thin (qualitative case studies available, quantitative impact limited). Regional economic simulation models for 200–2,000 person communities are sparse. Neither gap blocks production — document them explicitly in the relevant sections.

**Scorecard**:

| Criterion | Rating | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Source library readiness | Green | 75–80%; core foundational sources freely accessible; no paywall bottleneck |
| Scope containment risk | Green | Three tightly bounded questions; Zone 5 + crisis-context filters narrow the literature effectively |
| Cross-domain leverage | Yellow | Directly enables Phase 7 operationalization (local currency templates, commons charters); medium multiplier |
| Synergy with Phase 5 publication | Green | Phase 5 readers will immediately identify the missing economic layer; high relevance to community playbook questions |
| Regional fit | Green | Iowa/Minnesota agricultural cooperative tradition is primary case material; strong regional grounding |
| **Overall** | **Strongest quick-start; highest delivery confidence** | |

**Unique contribution to corpus**: The resilience literature has extensive coverage of food, water, and energy systems. It has almost no rigorous synthesis of the economic operating systems of small communities. This domain fills a specific gap in the available literature and produces the most immediately actionable templates of the three candidates (local currency design, commons management charter, minimum viable exchange network).

### Candidate C: Skills Development and Knowledge Transmission

**What it fills**: Phase 5 assumes the community has the practical skills to execute its documented procedures. Most communities at formation do not. The gap between "knowing you need to rotate grazing paddocks" and "knowing how to do it, teaching it, and ensuring the skill survives when the expert dies or leaves" is the failure mode that kills communities over the medium term. Skills Development is the transmission infrastructure that makes Phase 5 usable across generations.

**Target word count**: 48,000–58,000 words (10–12 sections)  
**Source library readiness**: 70–75% (Yellow)  
**Production start date**: June 10 (requires dedicated learning curve research sprint June 8–12)  
**Estimated completion**: August 23 (single-domain, 88% confidence)  
**On-time confidence (August 23, paired)**: 90%

**Core source anchors**:
- Lave and Wenger, situated learning framework (most-cited work on knowledge transmission in practice)
- Kolb's experiential learning cycle; andragogy theory (adult learning)
- 4-H documentation from USDA land-grant universities; Iowa State Extension curricula on practical agricultural skills
- Cuba post-Soviet collapse education adaptation (well-documented academic record)
- Puerto Rico hurricane recovery education rebuilds; UNESCO non-formal education reports
- Historical guild and apprenticeship records (European and American craft traditions)
- Homeschooling network data from Midwest cooperative schools and community education initiatives

**Documented evidence gaps**: Learning curve data for specific trades (well-drilling, solar installation, medical response, hydraulics repair) in low-resource settings exists as practitioner reports but lacks rigorous academic grounding. Intergenerational teaching dynamics in North American contexts are not well-synthesized. These gaps are manageable — use best-available practitioner evidence and document limits explicitly.

**Scorecard**:

| Criterion | Rating | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Source library readiness | Yellow | 70–75%; requires 5-day learning curve data research sprint before full production |
| Scope containment risk | Yellow | Educational research is deep; hard boundary required at kickoff (scope = 12–15 critical resilience skills, not comprehensive education system design) |
| Cross-domain leverage | Green | Highest leverage by the 10–20 year horizon metric; without transmission architecture, all Phase 1–5 systems degrade when the founding generation ages out |
| Synergy with Phase 5 publication | Yellow | Phase 5 Wave 3 covers educational governance (institutions); this covers operational skill transmission — complementary, not redundant |
| Regional fit | Green | 4-H and extension service tradition throughout Zone 5 region; strong agricultural apprenticeship historical record |
| **Overall** | **Highest long-term leverage; one-week longer ramp than Domain A** | |

**Unique contribution to corpus**: The only domain that explicitly addresses the 10–20 year sustainability horizon. A community using Phase 5 water purification procedures in 2026 must be able to teach those procedures to someone 15 years younger in 2030. This domain documents how. Companion document to Phase 6 Track A (Farm Equipment Repair): repair documentation without transmission architecture is incomplete.

### Candidate D: Governance Scaling

**What it fills**: Phase 3 and Phase 5 Wave 2 document governance structures for communities of roughly 20–50 people. These work at 20 people. They fail at 100. Consensus-based decisions that functioned with 20 people create deadlock at 100. Unwritten norms break down. Authority contests emerge. Governance Scaling documents what breaks at each scale threshold and what structural interventions prevent or repair the breaks — including how institutions learn from governance failures.

**Target word count**: 50,000–60,000 words (11–13 sections)  
**Source library readiness**: 65–70% (Red — requires 8–10 day source sprint)  
**Production start date**: June 15 (source sprint June 1–10; early section drafts June 10–15)  
**Estimated completion**: August 30 (single-domain, 78% confidence with aggressive scope management)  
**On-time confidence (August 23, paired with Economic Resilience)**: 85%

**Core source anchors**:
- Elinor Ostrom and Vincent Ostrom, polycentric governance framework (directly applicable to federated community structures)
- Indigenous governance systems literature (nation-to-nation governance without central authority — extensive academic record)
- Swiss cantonal system documentation
- Post-disaster governance case studies: Christchurch earthquake recovery, Puerto Rico hurricane governance
- UNDRR international disaster governance frameworks; adaptive management literature (Holling, Folke)
- North and Williamson, institutional change theory
- Wisconsin soil-water conservation district cooperatives; Minnesota county governance; Iowa agricultural cooperative federations

**Documented evidence gaps**: Case studies of governance failure in resource-constrained settings are systematically underreported (failures are less documented than successes — known limitation of the governance literature). Consensus decision-making at 300+ person scale has limited case studies. "Optimal federation size" research is at the frontier — this is a genuine research gap, not one that will close before publication. These gaps require the author to acknowledge evidence limits and build from adjacent literature.

**Scorecard**:

| Criterion | Rating | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Source library readiness | Red | 65–70%; paywall exposure ~30% of desired sources; 8–10 day source sprint required |
| Scope containment risk | Yellow | Governance literature is vast; hard scale limit (150–500 person federation only) must be enforced; weekly scope audits recommended at weeks 3, 6, 9 |
| Cross-domain leverage | Green | Governance architecture is foundational to all other Phase 6 domains; highest strategic multiplier |
| Synergy with Phase 5 publication | Yellow | Natural extension of Phase 3 governance document and Phase 5 conflict resolution; not a response to immediate Phase 5 reader questions |
| Regional fit | Yellow | Midwest cooperative federation data exists (agricultural sector); governance failure case studies are less regionally specific |
| **Overall** | **Highest strategic multiplier; highest source risk; longest timeline** | |

**Unique contribution to corpus**: There is extensive political science literature on national and international governance. There is almost no rigorous synthesis of how 3–5 federated communities (150–500 people) make decisions together in practice. The Ostrom polycentric governance framework provides the theoretical foundation but has not been applied at this specific scale and context. The institutional learning question — how communities change their governance when it fails — is frontier research territory. Most academically novel of the three candidates.

## Part 3: Configuration Decision Framework

### Solo Configuration (One Domain)

Launch one domain June 1. All author and orchestrator resources concentrated on a single track.

**Best candidate for solo**: Community Economic Resilience.

Rationale: Highest source readiness (75–80%), lowest scope expansion risk, fastest production ramp. Author can begin production June 8. First-draft outline deliverable June 15. Final document August 9. Produces the most immediately actionable outputs. Unlocks Phase 7 operationalization directly. 95% on-time confidence.

If you select solo and your priority is long-term leverage over fast delivery, select Skills Development instead. Accept the 1-week longer ramp (June 10 start vs. June 8) and the 88% vs. 95% confidence in August delivery.

Do not select Governance Scaling as the sole domain unless you have confirmed author expertise in political science or public administration and are prepared for active scope management. The 78% on-time confidence under solo Governance selection is the lowest of any solo configuration.

### Option A+C Configuration (Economic + Skills)

Two domains, parallel tracks, lowest coordination overhead.

**Timeline**:
- June 1–8: Source library compilation (both domains in parallel); author onboarding
- June 8: Economic Resilience enters production
- June 10: Skills Development enters production (after 5-day learning curve sprint)
- July 13: Economic Resilience first draft complete
- August 10: Skills Development finalization; Economic Resilience in final editing
- August 23: Both domains complete

**Coordination requirement**: Minimal. These domains are loosely coupled — skills determine who can operate economic systems; economic systems determine which skills are viable to teach. No required synchronization milestones. Share findings asynchronously. Light integration section at document completion (500–1,000 words).

**Orchestrator load**: Each domain requires 3–4 feedback cycles (June 10–July 10 for Economic; June 10–August 10 for Skills). Two simultaneous author tracks means feedback cycles overlap for approximately 6 weeks (June 10–July 27). Combined Orchestrator author-feedback load during this overlap: 6–8 hrs/week. This is within sustainable operating range under Option A publication (post-June 5 Orchestrator demand drops sharply).

**On-time confidence**: 90% both domains complete by August 23.

**When to choose A+C**: You want fastest delivery with high confidence, you value the 10–20 year sustainability argument for Skills Development, and you are comfortable deferring Governance Scaling to September (Phase 6b). This is the lowest-risk two-domain configuration.

### Option A+D Configuration (Economic + Governance)

Two domains with tighter coupling and higher coordination overhead, but stronger strategic multiplier.

**Timeline**:
- June 1–8: Economic Resilience source library + author onboarding
- June 1–10: Governance Scaling extended source sprint (8–10 days)
- June 8: Economic Resilience enters production
- June 15: Governance Scaling enters production (delayed start is a feature — Economic domain framing reduces Governance scope expansion risk)
- July 13: Economic Resilience first draft complete; shared with Governance author for cross-domain alignment
- July 13–August 9: Governance Scaling full production with Economic domain framing available
- August 9: Economic Resilience final complete
- August 9–23: Governance Scaling finalization + cross-review
- August 23: Both domains complete

**Coordination requirement**: One synchronization milestone: July 13 (30-minute sync when Economic first draft is complete). Co-author a 3,000–5,000 word integration section (August 10–13) explaining how economic resilience and governance co-evolve. Active scope management required for Governance track (weekly scope audits at weeks 3, 6, 9 of production).

**Orchestrator load**: Higher than A+C due to Governance scope management overhead. Scope audit at Week 3 (July 6), Week 6 (July 27), Week 9 (August 17) each require 1–2 hours of Orchestrator review. Combined with standard feedback cycles: 7–10 hrs/week during July 13–August 17. This is the highest Orchestrator demand of any two-domain configuration.

**On-time confidence**: 85% both domains complete by August 23.

**When to choose A+D**: You believe the governance gap is strategically more important than the skills transmission gap, and you are willing to accept higher Orchestrator load and slightly lower delivery confidence in exchange for the governance multiplier. Also the right choice if you have confirmed author expertise in political science or governance (specialist background reduces scope expansion risk materially).

### Three-Domain Note

Do not attempt all three domains in a single 12-week window. Combined scope, three simultaneous author tracks, and Governance scope expansion risk produce 65% on-time confidence — too low for a June 1–August 23 commitment. The recommended three-domain approach is Phase 6a (June 1–August 23, two domains) plus Phase 6b (September 1–October 15, Governance Scaling standalone).

## Part 5: Go-Forward Sequencing (Phase 6b Candidates)

After Phase 6a completes (August 23), Phase 6b launches September 1. The Phase 6b candidate is determined by which domains were selected for Phase 6a.

### If Phase 6a = Solo Economic Resilience

Phase 6b options: Skills Development (September 1–October 15, 88% confidence) or Governance Scaling (September 1–October 31, 78% confidence, scope management required).

Recommended Phase 6b: Skills Development. It benefits from the Economic domain's cooperative and exchange-system framing. The apprenticeship literature and economic systems literature reinforce each other. Governance Scaling defers to Phase 6c (October–December).

### If Phase 6a = A+C (Economic + Skills)

Phase 6b: Governance Scaling (September 1–October 31). This is the natural completion. Economic and Skills domains provide the structural context that reduces Governance scope expansion risk. The Economic domain's cooperative and commons governance framing directly anchors the federation model research in Governance Scaling.

Author recommendation for Phase 6b Governance: Recruit in August (July 27 at earliest to allow Phase 6a draft review before briefing). Author profile: political science or public administration background; familiarity with comparative governance; experience writing for practitioner (not academic) audiences. See `PHASE_6_AUTHOR_HIRING_AND_ONBOARDING_FRAMEWORK.md` for profile details.

### If Phase 6a = A+D (Economic + Governance)

Phase 6b: Skills Development (September 1–October 15). Skills is the natural follow-on: Phase 6a established the economic and governance architecture; Phase 6b adds the transmission infrastructure that makes that architecture persist across generations.

Author note: Skills Development author can be recruited July 15–August 1 (before Phase 6a completes) because the Skills domain has low dependency on Economic or Governance framing at the research level.

After all three domains complete, Phase 6c options include:
- Ecosystem restoration and regenerative agriculture (Domain 63 from earlier framework — has pre-research basis in Phase 4 agricultural intensification work)

These are not decision-urgent for May 31. Log them in the Phase 6 decision file for the September check-in.

## Quick Decision Prompt (2 minutes)

Answer each question. The pattern of answers points to your configuration.

1. Is your community's acute gap resource fairness and exchange coordination when cash fails? → Economic Resilience (Domain A)
2. Is your acute gap skill concentration in 1–2 people and generational knowledge loss risk? → Skills Development (Domain C)
3. Is your acute gap governance deadlock as your community grows beyond 25–30 people? → Governance Scaling (Domain D)
4. Do you want fastest delivery with highest confidence? → Solo Economic Resilience or A+C pairing
5. Do you want maximum coverage before Phase 7 operationalization? → A+C by August 23, Governance September
6. Do you have a confirmed author with political science or governance background? → A+D pairing is viable; if not, A+C is lower risk
