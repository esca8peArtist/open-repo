## Zone 5 Baseline Data

**USDA Hardiness Zone 5**: Average annual minimum temperature -20°F to -10°F (-29°C to -23°C). Covers the bulk of the Midwest agricultural core: northern and central Illinois, Indiana, Ohio, Wisconsin, Iowa, and southern Minnesota. [USDA Plant Hardiness Zone Map](https://planthardiness.ars.usda.gov/)

| Parameter | Zone 5A | Zone 5B | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Last spring frost (50% probability) | ~April 25 | ~April 15 | Varies up to 2 weeks by microclimate |
| First fall frost (50% probability) | ~October 8 | ~October 15 | Earlier in low-lying, north-facing areas |
| Frost-free growing days | ~166 | ~170–175 | Midwest average 160–175 [Old Farmer's Almanac](https://www.almanac.com/gardening/frostdates) |
| Annual precipitation | 34–40 in | 34–40 in | Higher in southern Midwest; lower in northern tier [Midwestern Regional Climate Center, Purdue](https://mrcc.purdue.edu/) |
| Peak precipitation months | April–June | April–June | 7.5–12 in/quarter |
| Peak drought risk | July–September | July–September | Groundwater lowest in late summer [USGS Upper Midwest Water Science Center](https://www.usgs.gov/centers/upper-midwest-water-science-center) |

**Key planning number**: The Zone 5 growing season of 160–175 frost-free days is long enough for all major caloric crops (corn, beans, potatoes, sweet potatoes, squash) but tight enough that every planting delay matters — a missed May window for corn means a September harvest failure.

## Month-by-Month Calendar

---

### January

**Weather Pattern**: Coldest month in Zone 5. Average daytime highs 28–34°F; lows 14–22°F. Polar vortex events drop temperatures to -20°F or below every 2–4 years. Average snowpack 4–12 inches. Heating demand is at maximum.

**Extreme weather risk**: Highest blizzard and polar vortex risk. Ice storms possible. See [midwest/extreme-weather.md](extreme-weather.md) for full protocols.

#### Food
- No planting activity. This is the planning and seed ordering month.
- Review seed catalogs: Seed Savers Exchange, Baker Creek, Johnny's Selected Seeds.
- Inventory stored food: check root cellar for spoilage; cull any soft or diseased roots.
- Check stored dried goods (beans, corn, sunflower seeds) for moisture — re-dry any that feel soft.
- Canning jars: check lids for rust; replace any compromised seals from fall canning.
- Grind dried corn into cornmeal as needed; store flour in cool dark location.
- **Rural track**: Feed livestock daily; water troughs freeze — break ice twice daily or install tank heaters.

#### Water
- **Highest pipe freeze risk of the year**. Vulnerable pipes (crawl spaces, exterior walls, unheated garage) need heat tape or continuous slow drip to prevent freeze. [Consumer Reports Frozen Pipes](https://www.consumerreports.org/home-garden/home-maintenance-repairs/how-to-keep-pipes-from-freezing-a2277945570/)
- Well pump house: maintain minimum 40°F with heat lamp or pipe insulation.
- Surface water sources (streams, ponds) may be frozen over. Identify open water or have ice-breaking plan.
- Rainwater collection: system dormant or frozen; no collection expected. Rely on stored water.
- Groundwater at moderate level — not at seasonal low yet, but recharge minimal under frozen ground.
- **Suburban track**: Know where your main shutoff valve is. If a pipe bursts during polar vortex: shut off main, open lowest faucet to drain pressure, then address pipe.

#### Energy
- Peak heating demand. Zone 5 homes typically use 60–70% of annual heating fuel in December–February.
- Solar panel output at seasonal minimum — approximately 25% of peak summer output due to short days and low sun angle. [Solar Insure — Seasonal Performance](https://www.solarinsure.com/solar-performance-in-winter-spring-fall-summer)
- If running any generator or backup propane: ventilation is critical — carbon monoxide risk in sealed winter homes.
- Wood stove if equipped: burn through stored wood at highest rate; check chimney for creosote buildup every 4–6 weeks under heavy use.

#### Healthcare
- **Flu season at peak risk**. CDC data shows Midwest Region 5 flu peak typically falls late January through February. [CDC Flu Season](https://www.cdc.gov/flu/about/season.html)
- Limit shared indoor air space with ill contacts; handwashing primary defense.
- Vitamin D deficiency peaks — supplement 2,000–4,000 IU/day; low sunlight means zero dermal synthesis.
- SAD (Seasonal Affective Disorder) at worst period. Light box use 30–45 min each morning if symptomatic. [NIMH SAD](https://www.nimh.nih.gov/health/publications/seasonal-affective-disorder)
- Cold injury risk: frostbite occurs in under 30 minutes at -10°F with wind. Mittens (not gloves), layered synthetic base + wool outer. Any outdoor work over 30 minutes requires face covering.
- **Rural track**: Outdoor livestock work at -10°F requires buddy system; inform someone of your location before going to barn.

#### Shelter
- Monthly: check for ice dam formation on north-facing rooflines. Signs: icicles at eave edge + water stain on ceiling below.
- Ice dam mitigation: roof rake to clear snow from bottom 4 feet of roof; add attic insulation/ventilation to prevent heat escape.
- Check basement egress windows for snow blockage — emergency escape route must remain clear.
- Carbon monoxide detectors: test monthly; replace batteries.
- **Rural track**: Check outbuilding roof loads — wet snow adds 20 lbs/sq ft; old structures may be at limit.

**January cross-domain intersection**: The coldest month puts simultaneous pressure on heating, water (pipe freeze risk), and health (flu + SAD + cold injury). The three systems can fail together during a prolonged polar vortex: heating system failure → pipes freeze → no water → hypothermia risk escalates. Preparation priority: reliable heat source with manual backup.

### February

**Weather Pattern**: Cold continues. Average highs 33–38°F; lows 18–25°F. Slight increase in day length (sunset ~5:30 PM by end of month). Occasional mid-winter thaw "false spring" — temperatures can reach 50–60°F for 3–5 days before returning to winter.

**Extreme weather risk**: Blizzard and polar vortex risk continues. Ice storms peak in late February in much of the Midwest.

#### Food
- Begin seed starting indoors (6–8 weeks before last frost = February 15–28 start for Zone 5):
  - **Onion seeds** (10–12 weeks before transplant): start under lights now.
  - **Leeks** (10–12 weeks): start alongside onions.
  - **Celery** (10–12 weeks): start late February.
  - Grow lights: minimum 14–16 hours/day for seedlings started this month.
- Order seeds now if not done in January. Zone 5 popular varieties sell out by March.
- If mid-winter thaw occurs: inspect root cellar; potatoes may begin sprouting — cool them down immediately.
- **Rural track**: Maple syrup tapping window opens when daytime temperatures consistently hit 35–45°F and nights drop below 32°F. Monitor forecast. Tap trees when this pattern begins. [Lange Girl Farm — Maple Tapping Timing](https://langegirlfarm.com/maple-tapping-prep-tools-timing-for-homestead-syrup/)

#### Water
- Groundwater recharge begins slowly as snowmelt infiltrates (where ground is not frozen solid).
- Spring thaw water: first melt water is high quality but carry bacteria from animal waste on fields — treat all surface water. [USGS Upper Midwest Water Science Center](https://www.usgs.gov/centers/upper-midwest-water-science-center)
- Pipe freeze risk continues — do not relax heating protocols until sustained thaw in March.
- Check rooftop rainwater collection downspouts for ice blockage ahead of March thaw.

#### Energy
- Second coldest month. Heating fuel usage near maximum.
- Day length increases noticeably by late February — solar panels begin incremental recovery (still 30–40% of summer output).
- **Rural track**: Check propane or fuel oil tank levels — winter fuel demand may have depleted reserves faster than expected; order before roads become difficult in late-winter ice events.

#### Healthcare
- **Flu typically peaks in Midwest in late January–February**. [CDC 2024-25 Influenza Summary](https://www.cdc.gov/flu/whats-new/2025-2026-influenza-activity.html) Highest isolation precautions this month if treating any fever/respiratory illness without antibiotics.
- Continued SAD risk. Light exposure critical: maximize window time during mid-winter thaw periods.
- Scurvy risk in long-term grid-down scenarios (3–6 months without fresh produce): supplement Vitamin C 60–90 mg/day from stored foods (rosehips, canned tomatoes, dried peppers all contain meaningful Vitamin C).
- **Rural track**: Frostbite vigilance for extended livestock care in wind; check fingers and toes every 30 minutes below 0°F.

#### Shelter
- Late February: evaluate roof for ice dam damage. Check attic for condensation or frost on rafters (sign of air leakage and insulation gaps).
- Begin planning spring repairs — materials need to be ordered before ground thaws and hardware stores get busy.
- Window weatherstripping check: maximum heating load this month reveals any drafts with a candle flame test.

**February cross-domain intersection**: The mid-winter thaw is a deceptive event. Warm temperatures feel like relief but create flooding risk, ice dam water infiltration, false spring that risks early seed germination, and early tick emergence in southern Zone 5. Do not relax winter protocols during a thaw; use the window for outdoor assessment, not a reduction in stored fuel reserves.

### March

**Weather Pattern**: Transitional. Average highs 42–52°F; lows 26–34°F. Highly variable — blizzard possible in early March; 70°F possible in late March. Average 2–4 inches of precipitation (rain and snow mix). Spring thaw begins; frost depth in Zone 5 soils typically 12–24 inches through mid-month.

**Extreme weather risk**: Late-season ice storms peak. Flash flooding from snowmelt + rain possible in low-lying areas.

#### Food
- **Indoors (seed starting)**:
  - Tomatoes (6–8 weeks before last frost = March 1–15 start)
  - Peppers (8–10 weeks: start March 1–7)
  - Cabbage, broccoli, cauliflower, kale: start mid-March
  - Eggplant: March 1–15
- **Outdoors** (as soon as soil is workable — typically mid-to-late March in Zone 5):
  - Spinach, lettuce, peas, radishes can be direct-sown when soil can be worked without compacting (squeeze test: soil should crumble, not form mud ball)
  - Onion sets: plant as soon as soil is workable (can tolerate 28°F)
- **Maple syrup**: Primary collection window in Zone 5 is late February–March. Collect daily when daytime temps 35–45°F. Typical ratio: 40 gallons sap → 1 gallon syrup. [Lange Girl Farm — Maple Tapping](https://langegirlfarm.com/maple-tapping-prep-tools-timing-for-homestead-syrup/)
- Harvest any overwintered carrots or parsnips mulched in the garden before ground re-freezes.
- **Rural track**: Order bare-root fruit trees and asparagus crowns now for April planting.

#### Water
- **Spring thaw water management begins**: Ground thaw releases stored winter moisture.
- Groundwater recharge accelerates — well levels typically peak in March–April. [Epp Well Solutions — Seasonal Groundwater Variation](https://eppwellsolutions.com/blog/do-groundwater-levels-change-seasonally)
- Rainwater collection: re-activate gutters and downspout diverters as freeze risk passes. First rains may contain roof sediment from winter — allow first flush to drain before collecting.
- Surface water quality warning: snowmelt carries agricultural chemicals, road salt, animal waste from fields. Treat all surface water aggressively in March–April. [USGS — Groundwater Seasonal Correlation](https://pubs.usgs.gov/wsp/1539d/report.pdf)
- Cisterns and collection tanks: inspect seals and connections; top off storage before summer demand.
- Check sump pump operation before spring rains.

#### Energy
- Heating demand begins declining. Wood stove users: burn through lower-quality or wetter wood first; save dry hardwood for next fall.
- Solar output recovering — approximately 50–60% of summer peak by late March as day length increases.
- **Rural track**: Maple syrup evaporation is energy-intensive: 1 gallon of syrup requires 8–10 hours of sustained wood fire or equivalent propane. Plan fuel budget.

#### Healthcare
- Flu season winding down (though late-season spikes possible through April).
- **Tick activity begins in southern Zone 5 by late March** when temperatures consistently exceed 45°F. Begin tick check protocols now — full-body check after any outdoor time in grass/brush/leaf litter. [Contagion Live — Lyme Disease Spring Spread](https://www.contagionlive.com/view/tick-tick-spring-signals-the-spread-of-lyme-disease-in-the-us)
- Mud season: increased wound risk from outdoor work. Keep tetanus immunization current if available; clean all cuts immediately with copious water irrigation.
- First outdoor physical activity after winter: watch for cardiovascular overexertion, especially during heavy spring soil work. Pace first weeks.
- SAD typically begins resolving in March as light increases.

#### Shelter
- **Spring inspection priority**: roof condition assessment after winter.
  - Look for: lifted or missing shingles, damaged flashing around chimney/vents, ice dam damage (water stain on ceiling), gutter separation.
  - Schedule repairs before April rain season.
- Sump pump: test operation; replace battery backup if present.
- Downspout extensions: ensure water is directed 6 feet from foundation.
- Chimney/stovepipe: final inspection before end of heating season; clean before summer to prevent nesting and corrosion.
- **Suburban track**: Check window AC units stored in basement for mold before reinstallation season.

**March cross-domain intersection**: Spring thaw simultaneously provides the first rainwater of the year (filling cisterns) and floods the soil with moisture needed for planting — but the same runoff contaminates surface water with agricultural chemicals. March is the month where water collection and water treatment urgency are highest simultaneously.

### April

**Weather Pattern**: Spring. Average highs 56–64°F; lows 36–44°F. Zone 5 last frost risk highest in first two weeks; rapidly declining through the month. Average precipitation: 3.5–4.5 inches, primarily rain. Severe thunderstorm season begins.

**Extreme weather risk**: Tornado season opening month. Late ice storms possible early April. Flash flooding from heavy spring rains. See [midwest/extreme-weather.md](extreme-weather.md).

#### Food
- **High-intensity planting month**:
  - **Early April** (soil workable, frost risk <50%):
    - Direct sow: beets, carrots, turnips, parsnips
    - Plant seed potatoes (soil temp 45°F+): 1 oz seed piece every 12 inches, eyes up, 4 inches deep
    - Direct sow: additional spinach, lettuce, peas (succession)
    - Transplant: broccoli, cabbage, cauliflower, kale (frost-hardy transplants)
  - **Mid-April** (last frost risk below 30%):
    - Transplant: onion seedlings started in February
    - Direct sow: Swiss chard, beets (second succession)
  - **Late April** (approaching frost-free zone):
    - Harden off tomato and pepper seedlings (begin outdoor exposure for 1–2 hours/day)
- Wild food: **Morel mushrooms** begin appearing when soil temperature reaches 50°F (around April 15–May 1 in Zone 5). Check dead elm trees, south-facing slopes, old orchards. [Missouri Dept of Conservation — Wild Edibles](https://mdc.mo.gov/wild-edibles-4)
- **Ramps (wild leek)** peak in April in rich hardwood forests, north-facing slopes. Two-week window.
- **Rural track**: Asparagus crowns planted in April (1-year-old crowns; harvest begins Year 3). Fruit trees: bare-root planting before leaf-out in early April.
- **Suburban track**: Begin container growing setup — sweet potatoes in 15-gallon grow bags need setup now for June slip planting.

#### Water
- **Best rainwater collection month of the year**. Midwest April precipitation averages 3.5–4.5 inches; catchment from 1,000 sq ft roof = 2,000–2,800 gallons/month. Ensure tanks and cisterns are connected and clean.
- Groundwater at or near seasonal peak. Best time to assess well performance — note static water level.
- Check all outdoor faucets after winter: inspect for crack or leak from freeze damage.
- Irrigation infrastructure: lay out drip lines or soaker hose for garden beds; mulch paths to retain soil moisture.
- Surface water still carries spring runoff contamination — treat all non-well water. [Midwest Regional Climate Center Spring Precipitation Data](https://mrcc.purdue.edu/)

#### Energy
- Heating load declining sharply. Transition month: heating at night, ventilation during warm days.
- Solar panel output at approximately 70% of summer peak by late April (long days, moderate angle).
- Last month to assess winter heating fuel usage for future planning. If you exhausted propane or wood, adjust stockpile targets for next year.
- **Rural track**: Begin assessing spring/summer water needs for irrigation; hand pump or gravity-fed cistern system sizing should be confirmed before summer demand.

#### Healthcare
- **Tick season fully active across Zone 5 by mid-April**. Peak nymphal tick season (highest Lyme transmission risk) is May 15–July 15. Start daily tick checks now. [Truveta — Lyme Disease Seasonal Trends](https://www.truveta.com/blog/research/lyme-disease-seasonal-trends/)
- Deer tick (black-legged tick) is primary Lyme vector in Midwest. American dog tick peaks April–June and carries Rocky Mountain Spotted Fever (RMSF). RMSF is rapidly fatal without early doxycycline — never wait for lab confirmation.
- First spring outdoor work: be alert to bee/wasp emergence. Any known allergy — keep epinephrine autoinjector accessible all season.
- Wild mushroom foraging: NEVER eat foraged mushroom without 100% positive identification. Morel has no dangerous lookalikes in Zone 5 (false morel has a cottony interior vs. hollow interior of true morel — cut in half before eating). See [individual/05-healthcare.md](../individual/05-healthcare.md) for plant toxin management.

#### Shelter
- **Roof repair execution**: complete any repairs identified in March before heavy spring rains.
- Install/inspect window screens ahead of insect season.
- Check foundation drainage: water should move away from house after heavy rain. If pooling occurs, regrade or extend downspouts.
- **Rural track**: Inspect barn and outbuilding roofs after winter; address before hay/feed storage season begins.
- Tornado season begins: confirm shelter location, emergency kit, and weather radio batteries. [midwest/extreme-weather.md](extreme-weather.md)

**April cross-domain intersection**: April is the highest simultaneous-activity month of the year. Food planting begins, water collection peaks, healthcare risk escalates (ticks, tornado season), and outdoor work creates wound/exertion risk. A new gardener who falls behind in April typically does not recover enough caloric production for the year — potato planting delay of 2 weeks can cost 20% of yield in Zone 5.

### May

**Weather Pattern**: Spring peak. Average highs 68–74°F; lows 48–56°F. Last frost risk drops to below 5% by May 15 in Zone 5. Peak thunderstorm and tornado risk for northern Midwest. Average precipitation: 4–5 inches.

**Extreme weather risk**: Peak tornado month for much of the Midwest (northern tier) and the Great Plains. Derecho season opens. Always-on weather radio protocol from May through August. [midwest/extreme-weather.md](extreme-weather.md) · [NOAA SPC Tornado Statistics](https://www.spc.noaa.gov/wcm/permonth_by_state/)

#### Food
- **Critical planting month**: all frost-tender crops go in now.
  - After May 10 (Zone 5B): transplant tomatoes, peppers, eggplant, basil
  - Direct sow: bush beans, pole beans (soil temp 60°F+)
  - Direct sow: corn (soil temp 55°F+ minimum; 60°F+ for good germination)
  - Direct sow: cucumbers, zucchini, summer squash
  - Direct sow: winter squash (plant in hills; 3 seeds per hill spaced 6 ft apart)
  - Transplant or direct sow: melons (warm soil required)
  - Second succession of beets, carrots, radishes
  - **Rule**: Beans and corn planted after June 1 in Zone 5 will not reliably dry before first frost (September frost risk). Plant corn no later than May 20 for reliable dry corn harvest.
- Foraging: **Stinging nettle** (April–May), **elderflower** (late May), **garlic mustard** (invasive; eat freely), **wood sorrel** begins. [TREEfool — Midwest Foraging](https://treefool.com/midwest-foraging/)
- **Rural track**: Plant sweet potato slips when soil is consistently 65°F — typically June 1–10, but prepare beds in May. Slips need 2 weeks of preparation before planting.
- Begin observing garlic (planted last October): scapes on hardneck varieties appear in late May/early June — harvest scapes when they curl; do not leave or they reduce bulb size.

#### Water
- May is typically the wettest month in many Zone 5 locations. Continue active rainwater collection.
- Garden irrigation demand begins — mulch heavily (4 inches straw or wood chips) to reduce water need.
- **Aquifer recharge season nearing end**. If you plan any well improvement work, do it now while groundwater is high and before summer drawdown begins.
- Check rain barrel / cistern overflow management before peak rain events.

#### Energy
- Heating season effectively ended for most of Zone 5 by May 15.
- Cooling demand not yet significant. Best month for energy-neutral living.
- Solar panel output at ~90% of summer peak — high sun angle, long days.
- **Rural track**: Maple syrup season ended. Tap wounds on maple trees should be healing; avoid additional cuts.

#### Healthcare
- **Tick season at full peak**. Nymphal ticks (primary Lyme transmitters) are tiny (poppy-seed size) and easily missed. Daily full-body tick check after outdoor time is non-negotiable during this period. [CDC Tick Bite Warning 2026](https://theheartysoul.com/us-tick-bite-warning-2026-states-maps-cdc-alert/)
- Remove attached tick: grasp with fine-point tweezers at skin level, pull straight out with steady pressure. Do not twist, squeeze, or apply heat.
- If tick was attached 36+ hours (engorged), start doxycycline 200 mg single prophylactic dose if available. [individual/05-healthcare.md](../individual/05-healthcare.md)
- Bee and wasp risk increases with warm weather and outdoor activity near nests.
- First significant mosquito emergence in late May — West Nile Virus risk begins (peaks July–August). Eliminate standing water (check every container in your yard).

#### Shelter
- **Rural track**: Install window screens on all outbuildings before fly season.
- Check AC unit function before first heat wave (typically late May Zone 5 has at least one 90°F+ day).
- Inspect all exterior caulking: windows, doors, pipe penetrations — summer heat will worsen any gaps.
- Tornado shelter: do a timed walk-through drill. Time from warning to shelter location (goal: under 2 minutes). See [midwest/extreme-weather.md](extreme-weather.md) for shelter preparation.

**May cross-domain intersection**: The convergence of peak storm risk (tornadoes) with peak planting urgency creates a conflict — outdoor planting work must continue even when weather is volatile. The solution: check weather radar at the start of every outdoor work session during May. Keep a portable battery weather radio in the garden. A tornado warning is not a pause; it is an immediate stop.

### June

**Weather Pattern**: Early summer. Average highs 78–84°F; lows 58–65°F. Hot, humid. Longest days of year (summer solstice June 21). Thunderstorm and derecho risk at peak. Average precipitation: 3.5–4.5 inches, often in intense convective events.

**Extreme weather risk**: Peak derecho season (June–August). Severe thunderstorms with 70+ mph winds, large hail. [NOAA — Where and When Derechos Occur](https://www.noaa.gov/jetstream/derecho-climo) · [midwest/extreme-weather.md](extreme-weather.md)

#### Food
- **June 1–15**: Plant sweet potato slips (Zone 5 target: soil temp 65°F+). [individual/02-food.md](../individual/02-food.md) for slip preparation.
- Direct sow: fall broccoli, cabbage (will mature October); second corn planting if area allows.
- **Garlic scapes**: harvest when scape makes one full curl; cut at base. Scapes are edible (roast, pesto, pickle). This triggers the plant to put energy into bulb development.
- **Succession planting (every 3 weeks)**: beans, cucumbers, summer squash for continuous harvest.
- **First major harvest window**:
  - Spring greens, peas, radishes, turnips: harvest before heat causes bolting.
  - Garlic: leaves beginning to die back (harvest when 4–5 lower leaves are brown — typically late June to early July).
  - Fresh herbs: harvest regularly to prevent bolting.
- Begin canning infrastructure prep: test pressure canner gauge, acquire new jar lids (never reuse lids), organize jars.
- Foraging: **Elderflower** (late May–June), **mulberry** (late June–July), **wild strawberry** (June), **lamb's quarters** (throughout summer).

#### Water
- Garden irrigation demand increasing. Rule of thumb: 1 inch of rain or irrigation per week for most crops; corn and potatoes need 1.5 inches during pollination and tuber formation.
- Rain gauge: monitor weekly. If less than 1 inch, supplement with irrigation.
- **Rainwater collection**: June still productive (3.5–4.5 inches average), though intense events concentrate rainfall in short windows.
- Groundwater levels beginning to drop from spring peak as evapotranspiration exceeds recharge.
- Cattle pond / dugout / water feature: first algae blooms possible in June heat — do not use algae-bloom water without treatment.

#### Energy
- First cooling demand. Window AC units or passive cooling becomes necessary during heat waves.
- Solar panel output at seasonal maximum (longest days, high sun angle).
- Wood fire: transition to outdoor cooking fires/smoker for food preservation — keeps heat out of living space.

#### Healthcare
- **Tick season full peak** — June through mid-July is the highest-risk window for Lyme disease transmission. [Truveta — Lyme Seasonal Data](https://www.truveta.com/blog/research/lyme-disease-seasonal-trends/)
- West Nile Virus: mosquito population building. Peak transmission risk is July–August, but prevention (standing water elimination, protective clothing at dawn/dusk) must start now.
- Heat illness risk begins. During 90°F+ days with physical work: drink 1 cup water every 15–20 minutes; work during early morning (5–10 AM) and late evening (6–8 PM); avoid midday outdoor exertion. [EPA Climate Indicators: Heat-Related Illnesses](https://www.epa.gov/climate-indicators/heat-related-illnesses)
- Poison ivy/oak emerges with full leaf cover. Learn identification: "leaves of three, let it be." Urushiol (irritant oil) remains active on tools and clothing. Wash exposed skin with soap within 10 minutes of contact.

#### Shelter
- **Rural track**: Inspect well house and pump: ensure ventilation prevents overheating of pressure tank.
- Close window blinds/curtains during peak heat hours (10 AM–4 PM) — passive solar management reduces cooling load by 30–40%.
- Check attic ventilation: inadequate attic airflow accelerates shingle degradation and raises cooling load.
- Inspect chimney cap for bird nesting — June is active nesting month; capped chimney prevents obstruction.

**June cross-domain intersection**: Elderflower (medicinal and culinary) and garlic harvest overlap in June, creating a brief window where two important medicinal/food crops are simultaneously ready. Elderflower for antiviral tea/syrup must be processed within 24 hours of harvest. Garlic pulled in late June will be dry and storable by August — time the harvest right and cure immediately in well-ventilated shade.

### July

**Weather Pattern**: Peak summer heat. Average highs 83–89°F; lows 64–70°F. Midwest heat dome events (3–5 consecutive days above 95°F) occur 1–3 times per summer. Derecho risk highest. Average precipitation: 3.5–4.5 inches but highly variable — late-summer drought is common.

**Extreme weather risk**: Highest derecho risk (May–July). Heat dome events. Flash flooding from intense convective storms.

#### Food
- **Primary summer harvest window**:
  - Cucumbers, zucchini, summer squash: harvest every 2–3 days (fast growth in heat).
  - Green/snap beans: harvest when pods are pencil-thick; do not allow pods to dry or plant production stops.
  - Fresh tomatoes begin ripening (late July for most Zone 5 varieties).
  - Fresh basil: harvest before flower formation; basil flowers reduce leaf quality.
  - Garlic: cure in well-ventilated shade 3–4 weeks (hang in bundles or lay on screen); store after curing.
- **Begin canning and preservation push**:
  - Pickles (cucumbers): water bath canning — acidified brine makes these safe for water bath processing.
  - Fermented dill pickles: no canning required — 2% salt brine, 3–7 days room temp.
  - Dried herbs: harvest morning before heat; dry at 95–115°F or in sun; store in sealed jars.
- Direct sow for fall: kale, Swiss chard (will harvest through November), beets and carrots for fall harvest (plant 10–12 weeks before first frost = July 1–15 in Zone 5).
- Foraging: **Wild plum** (late July), **mulberry** (early July), **purslane** (gardens and disturbed areas), **lamb's quarters**, **wood sorrel**.
- **Rural track**: If running cattle/livestock: ensure adequate shade and water — heat stress in animals mirrors human heat illness risk.

#### Water
- **Summer water stress begins**. Groundwater at or approaching seasonal low for shallow wells. [USGS — Groundwater Seasonal Correlation](https://pubs.usgs.gov/wsp/1539d/report.pdf)
- Monitor well recovery rate: if well takes more than 1 hour to recover 10 gallons, you are drawing down a stressed aquifer — reduce consumption.
- Garden irrigation: corn during pollination (silk emergence) requires consistent moisture — water stress at silk stage causes yield loss disproportionate to other stages.
- Cistern levels: track drawdown rate vs. any rainfall input. July drought + high garden water use can deplete a 1,000-gallon cistern in 2 weeks.
- Surface ponds: avoid swimming or using water from warm-weather ponds without treatment — blue-green algae (cyanobacteria) produces toxins not removed by standard filtration.

#### Energy
- Peak cooling load. Midday heat 85–95°F+ requires ventilation or cooling for safe living.
- Passive cooling strategies: open windows 10 PM–6 AM; close and shade all windows by 8 AM; whole-house fan if available.
- Solar panel output at maximum (longest effective production days despite some heat efficiency loss).
- **Rural track**: Solar charging of batteries reaches peak reliability — best time to top off battery banks from summer solar surplus.

#### Healthcare
- **Peak heat illness risk**. CDC data shows 300+ heat-related ED visits per 100,000 total visits in July–August — nearly 50% above prior historical average. [CDC Heat-Related Emergency Department Visits](https://www.cdc.gov/mmwr/volumes/73/wr/mm7315a1.htm)
- Heat stroke (not heat exhaustion) kills: recognize by confusion + hot dry skin + absence of sweating. Treat with cold water immersion immediately — this is a life-threatening emergency. [individual/05-healthcare.md](../individual/05-healthcare.md)
- Vulnerable: elderly, children, those doing outdoor physical labor. Never leave anyone in a closed vehicle; interior car reaches 130–150°F in July sun within 20 minutes.
- Continued tick vigilance through July 15 (nymphal peak ends). Adult ticks remain active and can transmit Lyme but are more visible and easier to remove.
- Waterborne illness risk: high temperatures + outdoor water sources = higher pathogen load. Treat all surface water.
- **Rural track**: Wasp and hornet nests fully populated by July — inspect around eaves, under decks before working in those areas.

#### Shelter
- Heat management priority. Signs of heat stress in a home without cooling: indoor temperature above outdoor temperature (heat is being trapped rather than vented) — this means inadequate attic ventilation.
- Window fans: exhaust fan in highest window (pulls hot air out); intake in lowest cool-side window.
- Refrigerator and chest freezer: check door seals; run at maximum cold in July to prevent food spoilage during heat dome events.

**July cross-domain intersection**: The July heat dome is the single most dangerous weather pattern for outdoor workers in Zone 5 because it coincides with peak garden labor demands (daily harvesting, canning setup) and high tick exposure during the continued tail of tick season. The solution: compress all outdoor work into the 5–10 AM window and use evenings for preservation work indoors.

### August

**Weather Pattern**: Late summer. Average highs 81–87°F; lows 61–68°F. Days shortening noticeably. Late-season heat waves possible through mid-August. Thunderstorm frequency declining. Average precipitation: 3.5–4 inches but often in drought pattern — late August is often the driest stretch of the year.

**Extreme weather risk**: Late derecho season. Flash drought can set in quickly. [midwest/extreme-weather.md](extreme-weather.md)

#### Food
- **Peak harvest and preservation month**. This is the single most labor-intensive period of the year.
  - Tomatoes: canning push begins in earnest. Process within 24 hours of harvest.
  - Pressure can: whole tomatoes, tomato sauce, tomato juice, green beans, corn (see [individual/02-food.md](../individual/02-food.md) for USDA pressure canning times)
  - **Water bath canning**: salsa, tomato paste, pickles, jam/jelly, acidified tomato sauce
  - Dehydrate: tomatoes, peppers, herbs, apple slices (begins late August as early apples ripen)
- Corn: when kernels are milky (silk is brown, kernels squirt white liquid) = sweet corn.  For **dried corn**: leave on stalk until husks and kernels are completely dry and hard — typically September–October.
- Direct sow: fall lettuce, spinach, arugula (6 weeks before first frost = plant by August 15 in Zone 5B, August 25 in Zone 5A).
- Plant garlic beds: prepare bed in August (amend soil, add compost) for October garlic planting.
- Foraging: **Elderberries** (August–September) — MUST be cooked; raw causes nausea; excellent syrup (antiviral traditional use). **Wild plum**, **pawpaw** ripening begins August–September. **Black walnut** green hulls begin dropping in late August. [Powell Gardens — Midwest Summer Foraging](https://powellgardens.org/midwest-foraging-summer-guide/)

#### Water
- Groundwater at seasonal low in Zone 5. Shallow wells (under 25 ft) most vulnerable to drawdown. [Epp Well Solutions — Groundwater Seasonal Patterns](https://eppwellsolutions.com/blog/do-groundwater-levels-change-seasonally)
- Reduce garden irrigation as crops begin to mature and dry down (beans, corn need less water when nearing harvest).
- Rainwater: collect aggressively when storms do occur — late August storms can deliver 2–4 inches; a 1,500-gallon cistern can be substantially refilled in a single event.
- Check cistern and rain barrel filters — sediment from summer use may reduce capacity.

#### Energy
- Cooling demand still high through mid-August, then declining.
- Days shortening — solar output beginning slow decline.
- **Rural track**: Fill propane tank before fall season begins (prices typically lower in August–September than late fall; pre-season fills often available from rural co-ops).

#### Healthcare
- Heat illness risk remains high through mid-August. Outdoor work protocols same as July.
- **Fall tick season begins late August**: adult deer ticks become active again after summer dormancy — second activity peak August–November. Continue daily tick checks. [CDC Lyme Disease Case Maps](https://www.cdc.gov/lyme/data-research/facts-stats/lyme-disease-case-map.html)
- Ragweed pollen season begins mid-August — allergic rhinitis (hay fever) peaks August–October. For those with severe allergy, outdoor work during peak hours is affected.
- Elderberry harvest: if making syrup for winter respiratory illness support, August–September is the window. Process immediately — raw elderberries cause nausea; cook before consuming or making tincture. [individual/05-healthcare.md](../individual/05-healthcare.md)
- Canning safety: pressure canning in August heat raises kitchen temperatures significantly. Adequate ventilation needed; carbon monoxide risk if burning propane indoors in poorly ventilated space. Water bath canning on outdoor propane burner preferred for bulk processing.

#### Shelter
- **Fall maintenance begins mid-August**: while temperatures allow comfortable outdoor work.
- Inspect and clean gutters: tree debris accumulation through summer needs removal before fall leaf drop.
- Exterior paint and caulk: last good window for exterior painting before temperatures drop below 50°F (minimum for most exterior paints).
- **Rural track**: Inspect outbuilding roofs before harvest season; clear any debris from summer storms.

**August cross-domain intersection**: The elderberry harvest window (August–September) connects food preservation (syrup for long-term storage), healthcare (antiviral properties), and foraging skills simultaneously. A single productive elderberry shrub near a creek edge can yield 5–10 lbs of berries processed into 2–3 quarts of elderberry syrup — a significant winter health resource.

### September

**Weather Pattern**: Early fall. Average highs 72–78°F; lows 50–57°F. Frost risk returns to northern Zone 5 by late September (some years). Dry, stable weather typical mid-month. Excellent outdoor work conditions. Average precipitation: 3–4 inches.

**Extreme weather risk**: Secondary tornado peak for some Midwest regions (August–September). Flash flooding from tropical remnant systems.

#### Food
- **Harvest acceleration**: Every day matters as first frost approaches.
  - **Winter squash**: harvest before hard frost (before first 28°F night). Cure 10 days at 75–85°F to harden skin; then store at 50–55°F. Properly cured butternut or Hubbard stores 5–6 months.
  - **Dried corn**: stalk may still be drying. Test: kernel should not dent when pressed with fingernail. If mature: harvest ears; husk and lay on screen or hang in mesh bags to continue drying indoors.
  - **Dried beans**: pods should rattle; shell beans before first hard frost if pods not yet fully dry. Spread on screen for 2 additional weeks of drying indoors.
  - **Tomatoes**: green tomatoes can ripen indoors at 65–70°F (not refrigerator). Continue canning from ripe fruit.
  - **Potatoes**: dig before hard frost. Allow to cure at 50–65°F for 2 weeks; then move to root cellar at 38–40°F.
  - **Sweet potatoes**: harvest before soil temperature drops below 50°F (typically before October 1 in Zone 5). Cure at 85°F for 10 days; then store at 55–60°F.
- **Plant garlic**: Zone 5 target is October 1–20 (4–6 weeks before ground freeze). Source: [Evergreen Seeds — Garlic Zone 5 Timing](https://evergreenseeds.com/when-to-plant-garlic-in-zone-5/)
- **Cover crop planting**: sow winter rye, hairy vetch, or clover into cleared beds by September 15. Cover crops suppress weeds, prevent erosion, add organic matter.
- Foraging: **Pawpaw** (September is peak; find in river bottomland). **Black walnut** (hull removal: wear gloves; hulls stain permanently). **Hickory nuts** begin dropping. **Wild rose hips** develop (highest Vitamin C content after first light frost). **Elderberries** continue through September.

#### Water
- Groundwater beginning slow recharge from fall rains. [USGS Groundwater Seasonal Data](https://pubs.usgs.gov/wsp/1539d/report.pdf)
- Rainwater collection: September rains reliable after summer drought. Good month to top off cisterns before freeze.
- **Winterize irrigation**: drain and disconnect drip lines, soaker hoses, and outdoor hoses before first hard freeze.
- Garden water demand dropping as crops mature and temperatures cool.

#### Energy
- Heating season begins: Zone 5 furnace first use typically late September.
- **Heating system inspection window**: September is ideal — HVAC professionals not yet at peak winter demand; repairs more available and less urgent. [Midwest Air Pros — Furnace Inspection](https://midwestairpros.com/schedule-your-annual-furnace-inspection-now/)
- Order/stack firewood before October if using wood heat. Target 3–5 cords for Zone 5 winter.
- Solar output declining as days shorten — plan battery storage accordingly.

#### Healthcare
- **Fall tick season peak**: adult deer ticks active August–November. Second-highest Lyme transmission risk window. Daily tick checks essential through October. [CDC 2026 Tick Warning](https://theheartysoul.com/us-tick-bite-season-lyme-disease-alpha-gal-syndrome/)
- Ragweed pollen peaks in September. Affects outdoor workers more than indoor — schedule heavy outdoor work in afternoons when pollen counts are lower.
- Physical harvest exertion: September combines heavy lifting (potato digging, squash harvesting, firewood stacking) with declining daylight. Pace work; back strain from heavy repetitive lifting common.
- **SAD early warning**: if you experience seasonal depression, begin light therapy and Vitamin D supplementation proactively in September before symptoms worsen. [NIMH Seasonal Affective Disorder](https://www.nimh.nih.gov/health/publications/seasonal-affective-disorder)
- Flu vaccination: get flu shot in September–October before season begins. Vaccine effectiveness declines slightly if given before October as season may not start until December. [CDC Influenza Season](https://www.cdc.gov/flu/about/season.html)

#### Shelter
- **Primary fall weatherization month**. This is the most important maintenance window.
  - Check and replace weatherstripping on all doors and windows
  - Caulk exterior around windows, doors, pipe penetrations
  - Add door sweeps to any gaps at base of exterior doors
  - Inspect basement rim joists (where floor meets foundation) — major heat loss point; seal with rigid foam insulation
- Heating system: schedule or complete furnace inspection, filter change, pilot light check.
- **Rural track**: insulate well pump house before October; install heat lamp or heat tape on pump and pressure tank.
- Chimney: have professionally swept if burning wood; remove bird nests from stovepipe.
- **Suburban track**: Clean leaves from gutters by end of October to prevent ice dam formation; ensure downspouts drain away from foundation.

**September cross-domain intersection**: September is the most consequential month of the year for food security. A Zone 5 grower who loses 10 productive days in September (due to illness, injury, or poor planning) may fail to bring in all crops before frost. The risk cascade: missed sweet potato harvest (frost damage) → lost caloric crop → winter food shortage. Healthcare priority this month is injury prevention and physical capacity.

### October

**Weather Pattern**: Mid-fall. Average highs 57–63°F; lows 40–46°F. First frost typically October 8–15 in Zone 5. Hard frost (28°F) usually by October 15–25. Daylight saving time ends — sharp psychological shift. Heavy leaf fall obscures obstacles. Average precipitation: 3–4 inches, increasingly from cold rain.

**Extreme weather risk**: Late-season tornado risk (northern tier, secondary peak). Early ice storms possible. First significant freeze risk for pipes.

#### Food
- **Final harvest completion**:
  - **Carrots**: can remain in ground under 6–8 inches of mulch (straw or leaves) until December; dig as needed.
  - **Parsnips**: sweeter after frost — leave in ground, harvest November–December.
  - **Brussels sprouts and kale**: tolerates hard frost; harvest through November.
  - **Any remaining sweet potatoes**: must be out of ground before soil temp drops below 50°F.
  - Green tomatoes: bring all remaining tomatoes indoors before first frost.
- **Garlic planting**: October 1–20 is the Zone 5 target window. Hardneck varieties (Rocambole, Porcelain) superior for Zone 5. Plant 2 inches deep, 6 inches apart, pointy end up. Mulch with 4–6 inches of straw after ground cools but before hard freeze. [Evergreen Seeds — Garlic Zone 5 Timing](https://evergreenseeds.com/when-to-plant-garlic-in-zone-5/)
- **Put garden to bed**:
  - Till in cover crop residue if not overwintered
  - Add compost to beds: fall application allows 6 months of decomposition before spring planting
  - Mark bed locations — essential when snow covers garden in January planning sessions
- Foraging: **Black walnut** and **hickory nut** peak — hull walnuts before hulls blacken completely. **Wild rose hips**: harvest after first frost (highest sugar content). **Acorns**: bur oak (lowest tannin) can be eaten after one cold-water leaching. Others require 3+ leachings. [ediblewildfood.com — Midwest Foraging Calendar](https://www.ediblewildfood.com/midwest-foraging-seasons.aspx)
- **Hunting season opens**: Archery deer season open in most Midwest states September–January. Firearm season typically opens in late October or November. One deer provides 40–90 lbs venison — major caloric supplement. [individual/02-food.md](../individual/02-food.md) for field dressing and preservation.

#### Water
- **Outdoor water infrastructure winterization**:
  - Drain all garden hoses and bring indoors
  - Shut off and drain outdoor faucets (if interior shutoff available)
  - Drain underground irrigation systems if present
  - Disconnect downspout rain barrel connections; drain barrels or bring indoors
- Groundwater recovering from summer low as fall rains recharge aquifer. [Epp Well Solutions — Seasonal Patterns](https://eppwellsolutions.com/blog/do-groundwater-levels-change-seasonally)
- Fill cisterns and tanks while conditions still allow — last reliable rainwater collection before freeze.
- **Rural track**: Winterize any surface water collection systems; insulate any above-ground pipes before November.

#### Energy
- Heating system in full use by October 15. Establish thermostat set-point routine.
- Daylight shortens sharply (sunset at 6 PM early October; 5 PM by late October post-DST).
- Solar output declining — approximately 50% of summer peak.
- Firewood: final stacking and covering before freeze; dry and accessible location.
- **Rural track**: Check propane or fuel oil level; order fill before winter demand drives up delivery lead times.

#### Healthcare
- **Tick season declining but still active**: adult deer ticks remain active until sustained ground freeze. Continue tick checks through October. [Truveta Lyme Seasonal Data](https://www.truveta.com/blog/research/lyme-disease-seasonal-trends/)
- **SAD onset**: October is the month when day length crosses below the threshold triggering symptoms in susceptible individuals. If you know you have seasonal depression: begin light box use at 10,000 lux, 30–45 min, in the morning. [Mayo Clinic — SAD Light Therapy](https://www.mayoclinic.org/diseases-conditions/seasonal-affective-disorder/in-depth/seasonal-affective-disorder-treatment/art-20048298)
- Fall: early influenza cases begin appearing. Review hand hygiene; avoid touching face when out in shared spaces.
- Physical: heavy lifting season (firewood, harvest). Use proper lifting mechanics; back strain common.
- **Rural track**: Hunting accidents peak in October–November. Blaze orange required in most states; safe firearm handling; inform someone of location and expected return before hunting.

#### Shelter
- Complete all exterior caulking and weatherstripping work before temperatures drop below 50°F (adhesive won't bond below this temperature).
- Heating system: complete furnace inspection if not done in September.
- Drain and store outdoor hoses at same time as garden irrigation winterization.
- Test smoke detectors and carbon monoxide detectors as heating season begins.
- **Suburban track**: Schedule chimney sweep if burning wood this winter; chimney fire risk increases with creosote buildup from intermittent use.
- Leaf removal from gutters: must be complete before first freeze to prevent ice dam formation.

**October cross-domain intersection**: Garlic planting in October is one of the highest-leverage single acts in the entire Zone 5 growing calendar — one pound of seed garlic planted now produces 6–10 lbs of harvest in July, requires zero summer attention, and the harvest timing fits perfectly with the year's food preservation calendar. It also has recognized medicinal uses (antimicrobial properties). The 20-minute investment in October garlic planting pays dividends across food and healthcare domains.

### November

**Weather Pattern**: Early winter. Average highs 42–48°F; lows 28–34°F. Ground typically freezes by mid-November in Zone 5. Heavy frost every night. First significant snowfall usually November 15–December 1. Daylight at minimum effective hours (sunset ~4:30–5:00 PM post-DST). Average precipitation: 2.5–3.5 inches (rain and snow).

**Extreme weather risk**: Early blizzard risk. First ice storms. First polar vortex extensions possible by late November.

#### Food
- **Last outdoor harvest**:
  - Carrots and parsnips: leave in ground under heavy mulch; dig as needed through December
  - Kale and Brussels sprouts: continue through hard frosts (flavor improves after frost)
  - Any remaining dried beans or corn if missed in October: shell indoors; dry for 2 additional weeks before storage
- Root cellar management:
  - Monitor temperature weekly (goal: 32–40°F)
  - Check potatoes for soft spots; remove and use any that begin softening
  - Maintain humidity with damp sawdust or open water pan if cellar dries out
  - Apples: keep well away from potatoes (apple ethylene gas accelerates potato sprouting)
- Indoor: begin feeding sourdough starter more frequently as temperature drops; cooler temps slow fermentation
- Plan next year's garden based on current year's results: note what produced well, what failed
- Hunting: **Deer rut peak** occurs roughly November 1–15. Deer are highly active and less cautious during this period — highest success rates for hunters. Whitetail firearm season in most Zone 5 states opens November. [individual/02-food.md](../individual/02-food.md) for field dressing, processing.
- **Rabbit and squirrel season**: November–February is prime small game season. Foliage down; animals visible. Cottontail populations typically peak.

#### Water
- Rainwater collection: transition to snow collection if desired (snow can be melted; 10–12 inches of snow = approximately 1 inch of water).
- Groundwater recharge pausing as soil freezes. Any remaining infiltration from fall rains completes by mid-November.
- Protect outdoor spigots: insulated faucet covers ($5–10) on all exterior faucets.
- Well pump house: heat lamp or heat tape operational by November 1.

#### Energy
- Full winter energy regime begins. Heating dominant.
- Solar output at 30–40% of summer peak.
- **Rural track**: Firewood use increases sharply. Maintain 6–8 week advance supply in accessible dry location.

#### Healthcare
- Influenza season begins: cases rising from October base, typically reaching moderate levels by November.
- SAD worsens for susceptible individuals: light box use daily; exercise (even indoor) important for mood regulation.
- Cold exposure: first outdoor work in cold of winter; confirm that cold-weather work clothing is adequate (base layer moisture-wicking synthetic, mid-layer wool or fleece, outer wind/water-resistant shell).
- Vitamin D: supplement 2,000–4,000 IU/day through winter. Extremely limited dermal synthesis below 45° latitude (all of Zone 5 Midwest) from November through March.
- **Rural track**: Hunting accidents second peak in November (firearm season). Check in and out protocols with household members.

#### Shelter
- **Serious winterization**: all caulk, weatherstrip, and exterior work completed.
- Inspect and clear attic of any pest nesting (mice move indoors as temperatures drop — first evidence often gnawing sounds in walls or attic in November).
- Check that emergency exit paths (windows, basement egress) are accessible despite holiday storage in basements.
- Wood stove: chimney inspected and clean; damper operates freely.
- **Rural track**: Check and insulate any pipe runs in unheated outbuildings; heat tape on vulnerable lines.

**November cross-domain intersection**: The deer rut peak (November 1–15) coincides with peak firearm hunting season, creating the most productive hunting window of the year — but it also coincides with the first real cold snaps requiring full winter gear. Field dressing a deer at 20°F requires cold-weather work protocols and awareness of hypothermia risk during extended outdoor exposure. Prepare hunt bag to include insulation layer for the wait after a kill before you can get the animal cooled.

### December

**Weather Pattern**: Full winter. Average highs 32–38°F; lows 18–25°F. Ground frozen. Reliable snowpack usually established. Shortest days of year (winter solstice December 21; sunset ~4:20–4:30 PM in Zone 5). Heating demand approaching maximum.

**Extreme weather risk**: Blizzard risk increases. Ice storms. Polar vortex possible. Maximum heating failure consequence period.

#### Food
- No planting. Review year's results; order new seeds for next season.
- **Root cellar checks**: increase frequency to weekly. Cold waves can drop basement temps below freezing in poorly insulated cellars — insulate pipes and floor for any temperatures approaching 30°F.
- Dried corn: grinding corn into cornmeal for ongoing use. A hand-cranked grain mill (Country Living, WonderMill Junior) handles dried dent corn — essential non-electric equipment.
- Fermentation: sourdough, kimchi/sauerkraut in cool root cellar continues slowly. Fermented foods are a critical source of probiotics and Vitamin C when fresh vegetables are unavailable.
- Hunting: small game (rabbit, squirrel) most accessible; deer season closes in most states by December 15–31 (varies by state).
- Wild turkey: fall season through December in most Midwest states.
- Foraging: limited. Dried rosehips (Vitamin C), pine needle tea (Vitamin C), stored nuts and dried berries. This is the lean season for foraging.

#### Water
- Same as November. All outdoor infrastructure must be protected.
- Indoor water: if heating fails during polar vortex, protect pipes:
  1. Allow cold water trickle from farthest faucet
  2. Open cabinet doors under sinks (allows heated air to reach pipes)
  3. If power out: maintain minimum 55°F with any backup heat source
- **Rural track**: Check well water system weekly; pump house temperature log recommended.

#### Energy
- Peak heating demand begins. Maximum energy consumption period.
- Solar output lowest of year (~20–25% of summer peak). Plan for extended low-production winter. Battery banks sized for winter must support 3–5 days without significant solar input.
- Wood stove: most intensive use period. Chimney inspection and creosote check recommended every 4–6 weeks under heavy use.
- Carbon monoxide: any combustion heating in winter spaces (propane heaters, generators, wood fire in poorly ventilated room) requires CO detector. Zone 5 December is peak CO poisoning season nationally.

#### Healthcare
- **Flu season at full peak** (typically December–February). Highest household transmission risk.
- SAD: worst month for many. Maintain light box regimen; keep some daily outdoor light exposure even when cold.
- Carbon monoxide poisoning: second highest risk period (after January). Ensure CO detector is functioning.
- Holiday food safety: in grid-down scenarios, food prepared in large batches then stored at ambient temperature (too cold for cooking bacteria, but not cold enough to be safe). Zone 5 December garage temperature (25–35°F) is actually reasonable as a supplementary refrigerator for meat storage — but not reliable (mid-December thaws can push temps to 45°F+). Do not rely on outdoor cold without a thermometer.
- Review emergency medical supplies before deep winter: antibiotics, pain medications, wound care supplies. [individual/05-healthcare.md](../individual/05-healthcare.md) full supply list.

#### Shelter
- Ice dam monitoring: ice forms at eave when warm attic air melts roof snow that refreezes at the cold overhang. Result: water backs up under shingles. Immediate fix: roof rake to remove snow from bottom 4 feet of roof slope.
- Check exterior lighting — short days mean more activity in darkness; trip hazard risk increases.
- Final review of emergency heating backup: if primary heating fails tonight, what is your plan? Should be documented and rehearsed. See [midwest/extreme-weather.md](extreme-weather.md) polar vortex protocol.
- **Rural track**: Check roof loads on barns and large outbuildings after any heavy snowfall exceeding 12 inches.

**December cross-domain intersection**: The winter solstice (December 21) marks the turning point — days begin lengthening immediately, though cold intensifies through January. This psychological marker is important for those managing SAD. Mark December 21 as "the turn" in your household calendar. The day length increase is small (1–2 minutes/day) but accumulates meaningfully by February.

## Rural vs. Suburban Track Summary

| Activity | Rural Track | Suburban Track |
|---|---|---|
| Maple tapping | February–March — tap own trees | Not applicable unless sugar maple on lot |
| Deer hunting | Hunt own property; no travel needed | Must arrange permission or use public land; travel to location |
| Pond fishing | Catfish/bluegill pond on property — year-round | Retention ponds (HOA), city park fishing, rivers within driving range |
| Root cellar | Separate structure possible; hillside preferred | Basement corner (northeast) or buried trash can; see [individual/02-food.md](../individual/02-food.md) |
| Well | May have existing well; add hand pump | Likely on municipal water; emergency gravity storage only |
| Rain collection | Large cistern (500–2,000 gal) off barn roof | 50–200 gal barrels; check local ordinance (legal in IL and most Midwest states) |
| Garden scale | 0.5–2 acres feasible | 500–3,000 sq ft after house/garage; maximize vertical and container |
| Firewood | Self-harvest from woodlot | Must purchase or arrange delivery; store 1–2 cords minimum |
| Livestock | Chickens, pigs, goats feasible | Check HOA/city ordinance; backyard chickens legal in most Midwest cities |
