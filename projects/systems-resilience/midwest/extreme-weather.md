## 1. Tornado

### What It Is

A rotating column of air in contact with both a cumulonimbus cloud and the ground. Midwest tornadoes predominantly form in supercell thunderstorms during the collision of warm Gulf moisture and cold/dry Arctic air.

**EF Scale (Enhanced Fujita):**
| Rating | Wind Speed | Typical Damage |
|---|---|---|
| EF0 | 65–85 mph | Light: shingles off, small trees snapped |
| EF1 | 86–110 mph | Moderate: roof stripped, mobile homes overturned |
| EF2 | 111–135 mph | Considerable: roofs torn off, large trees downed |
| EF3 | 136–165 mph | Severe: well-constructed homes destroyed |
| EF4 | 166–200 mph | Devastating: leveled homes; cars airborne |
| EF5 | 200+ mph | Incredible: reinforced concrete damaged; rare |

FEMA safe rooms are rated to survive EF5 debris. Standard homes fail at EF2–EF3.

### Tornado Warning System

**Watch vs. Warning:**
- **Tornado Watch**: Conditions favorable; be alert; go about activities but monitor
- **Tornado Warning**: Tornado confirmed by radar or spotter; take shelter IMMEDIATELY
- **Emergency Alert**: Your phone will sound with Wireless Emergency Alert — distinct sound, not ignorable

**Detection sources:**
- NOAA Weather Radio (battery backup — most reliable): ~$35
- Phone: enable all Emergency Alerts in phone settings; Wireless Emergency Alert (WEA) is federal system
- Local outdoor sirens: effective in daytime; may not wake sleeping residents
- Weather apps: AccuWeather, Weather Underground, Weather Channel — all have tornado alert settings

**Lead time reality**: National average warning lead time is 13 minutes. In flat Midwest terrain with good radar coverage, lead time can extend to 20–30 minutes. But a tornado can also drop from a cell with zero warning.

**The rule**: If you are asleep in a Midwest home without a NOAA weather radio, you are at serious risk from a nighttime tornado. Buy and program a weather radio — it will wake you even if phone is on silent.

### Protocol — Tornado Watch

**Upon Watch issuance:**
1. Charge all phones and backup batteries now (power may fail with Warning)
2. Fill bathtubs and water containers (water pressure drops after tornado)
3. Know which weather app or radio you'll monitor — check it now
4. Identify shelter location and clear a path to it
5. Bring pets inside
6. Move outdoor furniture inside or secure to anchors
7. Move propane tanks away from house if possible
8. Check on elderly neighbors — give them a plan

### Protocol — Tornado Warning (Immediate Action)

Time from Warning to impact: 0–30 minutes. Move immediately.

**30 minutes out:**
1. Go to shelter location
2. Bring: shoes (essential — debris everywhere after tornado), phone (charged), medications (if ongoing), one change of clothes, flashlight, water bottle

**10 minutes out (or when warning sounds):**
1. At shelter location: get to lowest point, away from windows
2. Cover head and neck with hands or helmet
3. If in basement: get under stairs, heavy workbench, or mattress in NW corner (tornadoes typically travel SW to NE — NW corner is the upwind side)
4. If interior room no basement: pull mattress on top of yourself in interior bathroom/closet
5. Close all doors between you and outside — each closed door reduces debris velocity

**At impact:**
- Stay sheltered through impact + 10–15 minutes after winds stop
- Do NOT leave shelter to look at tornado
- Sirens stopping ≠ all clear

### Protocol — Tornado Aftermath

**Immediate (0–2 hours):**
1. Check yourself for injuries before moving — shock masks pain
2. Structural assessment before moving through building:
   - Can you smell gas? → Do NOT use electrical switches; ventilate; leave building immediately
   - Is any ceiling sagging? → Leave immediately; structural failure ongoing
   - Are exterior walls separated from floor? → Leave
3. If no structural concerns: turn off main gas valve (yellow valve on gas meter, perpendicular to pipe = off)
4. If trapped under debris: do not struggle; conserve oxygen; yell or bang periodically; use whistle
5. Text (not call) emergency contact — texts route through congested networks better than calls

**Short-term (2–72 hours):**
1. Photograph all damage before any cleanup (insurance documentation)
2. Do not enter standing water — downed power lines may energize it
3. Do not use generator, gas stove, or charcoal inside — CO risk post-storm
4. Boil or treat all tap water until utility confirms pressure is restored and water is tested
5. Wear heavy gloves and sturdy boots during cleanup — most post-tornado injuries are from puncture wounds

**Water disruption after tornado:**
Tornadoes frequently damage water mains. Boil order is standard protocol after significant events.
- Source: stored water (per `individual/01-water.md`)
- Duration: typically 1–7 days until pressure restored and tested
- Do NOT drink from tap without boiling until boil order is lifted

**Power outage after tornado:**
Duration: 1 day to several weeks depending on infrastructure damage.
- Follow generator safety (outdoors only, never garage with door closed, 20 ft from windows)
- Food safety: full refrigerator maintains 40°F for 4 hours; full freezer maintains for 48 hours

## 2. Derecho

### What It Is

A derecho (Spanish: "straight") is a widespread, fast-moving wind damage event associated with a band of rapidly moving thunderstorms forming a "bow echo" on radar. Unlike tornadoes, derechos:
- Cover 240+ miles of path (threshold for classification)
- Produce sustained winds 58–100 mph, gusts to 130 mph+
- Have no rotation — straight-line winds
- Often develop quickly and travel at 60+ mph — racing faster than some storms

**The August 10, 2020 Iowa Derecho** is the benchmark event: winds exceeded 130 mph over a 770-mile track; destroyed 37% of Iowa's corn crop (5.8 million acres); 1.6 million power outages; damage exceeded $7.5 billion. Some Iowa communities were without power for 3+ weeks.

### Radar Identification

A derecho produces a distinctive bow-echo pattern on radar:
- Classic "hockey stick" or bow shape in radar reflectivity
- Rear-inflow notch: clear area behind the bow where rear-inflow jet enters the storm
- "Book-end" vortices at north and south ends of the bow
- An RFD (rear-flank downdraft) signature similar to supercell tornado producers

**Apps for radar interpretation:**
- RadarScope: professional-grade radar; $10 one-time; shows velocity data for bow echo identification
- AlamoWeatherNet (free) or NOAA's radar.weather.gov (free) for basic radar

**Watch for:**
- Severe Thunderstorm Warning with "particularly dangerous situation" language
- Derecho watch from SPC (Storm Prediction Center)
- Wind gusts forecast above 70 mph in a line of storms moving faster than 40 mph

### Protocol — Derecho

**Upon Severe Thunderstorm Watch with high-wind language:**
Same as tornado watch — prepare shelter, charge devices, secure outdoor items.

**Upon Severe Thunderstorm Warning (especially "PDS" — Particularly Dangerous Situation):**
- Treat as equivalent to tornado warning
- Get to interior room, lowest floor
- The distinction between tornado and high-wind derecho is academically interesting but survival response is identical

**Derecho impact (may last 30–60 minutes over your location):**
- Most injuries are from falling trees and structural debris — stay interior, head and neck covered
- Flying debris is lower-altitude than tornado but at 100 mph has extreme destructive capacity

**Aftermath:**
- Power outages are the dominant consequence — can last 3 weeks in agricultural derecho zones where transmission infrastructure is rural and understaffed for rapid repair
- Same post-storm protocol as tornado: gas check, structural assessment, boil water, generator safety

**Planning for 3-week power outage post-derecho in Iowa/Illinois:**
This is not a worst-case scenario — it happened in 2020. Your food and water systems must be able to sustain 3 weeks without power. See `individual/01-water.md` and `individual/02-food.md` for infrastructure requirements.

## 3. Polar Vortex / Extreme Cold

### What It Is

The "polar vortex" is a large area of low pressure and cold air surrounding Earth's poles. When the stratospheric polar vortex weakens or splits, frigid Arctic air spills southward into the Midwest. These events:
- Send temperatures -20°F to -40°F into normally Zone 5 areas
- Produce wind chills of -50°F or lower
- Typically last 3–7 days
- Occur every 2–4 years in significant magnitude

**Recent Zone 5 polar vortex events:**
- January 2019 (Chicago): -27°F recorded; -51°F wind chill; temperature killed 22 people in Illinois alone
- February 2021 (extended): Midwest sustained -15 to -25°F for 5+ days
- Historical: -40°F has been recorded in Iowa, Wisconsin, and Minnesota

**Why polar vortex is dangerous beyond "just being cold":**
1. **Grid failure**: Heating demand exceeds grid capacity; rolling blackouts or full failure possible
2. **Pipe bursts**: Millions of pipes freeze and burst simultaneously — plumbers booked weeks out
3. **Gas supply failure**: Natural gas demand exceeds pipeline capacity in extended events
4. **Road closures**: Infrastructure maintenance impossible at -40°F; supply chains interrupt
5. **Propane shortage**: Propane suppliers run out; prices spike; delivery delayed

### Protocol — Polar Vortex (72+ Hour Advance Warning)

Polar vortex events are the most predictable of Midwest extreme weather — typically 3–5 days of advance notice from NWS.

**72 hours before (upon NWS "extreme cold warning" forecast):**

*Fuel:*
- Fill vehicle gas tanks (gas stations can run out or lose power)
- Verify propane tanks are full (top up if below 50%)
- Confirm firewood supply is accessible (can you get to it if buried in snow?)
- Buy 10–15 gallons of kerosene if kerosene heater is your backup
- Stock 5 gallons extra cooking fuel (camp stove propane or butane)

*Water:*
- Fill all water storage containers now (in case pipes freeze or pressure drops)
- Confirm hand pump or alternative water source is operable in extreme cold
- Locate pipe shut-off valve; know how to operate it

*Food:*
- 7–10 day supply easily accessible without leaving home
- Foods that don't require cooking: crackers, peanut butter, nuts, dried fruit, canned goods with pull tabs
- Foods that only need hot water: oatmeal, freeze-dried, instant soups

*Heat:*
- Test backup heater now (Mr. Heater, kerosene, wood stove)
- Verify CO detector is working and has fresh batteries
- Wood stove: clean flue if not done in fall; have fire ready to light

*Pipes:*
- Insulate all vulnerable pipes (exterior walls, garage, crawlspace)
- Disconnect and drain garden hoses from exterior spigots
- Know shut-off valve location for every zone of your plumbing

**24 hours before:**
- "Tent" your home: lay area rugs over cold floors; hang heavy curtains; close off unused rooms
- Move heat-sensitive items from unheated spaces (garage, barn, workshop)
- Fill vehicle
- Charge all devices, radios, and battery packs

### Protocol — During Polar Vortex (-20°F or Below)

**Limit outdoor exposure to absolute minimum.** At -20°F with 20 mph wind, frostbite occurs in 10 minutes on exposed skin.

**Daily home maintenance during polar vortex:**

*Morning:*
1. Check thermostats and heat source — confirm system is running
2. Check all exterior-wall pipes under cabinets — drip must be running
3. Check CO detector
4. If wood stove: load and light early (takes 30+ min to reach full heat output)

*Continuously:*
- Never let interior temperature drop below 55°F if piping is in exterior walls
- If temperature in any room drops below 50°F: begin one-room strategy (see `individual/03-shelter.md`)
- If heating system fails: enact heat failure protocol immediately (do not wait — interior temperature recovery takes time)

**Vehicle care in polar vortex:**
- Park in garage if possible; a garage maintains 10–20°F above outdoor even unheated
- Battery fails at extreme cold: maintain full charge; consider battery blanket/tender
- Do NOT leave vehicle running in garage (CO)
- Diesel fuel gels below -15°F without additives; add diesel winter blend or power service additive
- Keep emergency kit in car: wool blanket, hand warmers, candles in tin, 72-hour food/water, flares

### Protocol — Heat Failure During Polar Vortex

**Minutes 0–30 (immediate):**
1. Layer ALL clothing immediately — everyone in household
2. Set up propane heater in chosen shelter room (lowest, most interior, away from exterior walls)
3. Crack window 1/4" in shelter room for CO ventilation
4. Close all other interior doors with towels/blankets at gaps
5. Move sleeping bags, blankets to shelter room

**Hour 1–6:**
1. Take temperature readings every hour: shelter room, exterior, vulnerable pipe areas
2. If pipe areas dropping below 40°F: open cabinet doors; increase drip
3. If shelter room temp is stable above 55°F: maintain this, conserve fuel
4. Contact utility company, landlord, or repair service
5. Alert one external person of your situation (neighbor, family member)
6. Assess whether staying is viable or evacuation to heated shelter (hotel, warming center) is safer

**Decision point (heat failure extending beyond 6 hours):**
- If fuel supply is adequate and shelter room is above 50°F → stay and wait for repair
- If fuel supply limited or temperatures uncontrollable → evacuate to hotel, warming center, or friend/family with heat
- Warming centers: most Midwest cities open warming centers during extreme cold events; check city emergency management website or 211

**Night protocol during heat failure:**
- One person stays awake (rotating watch) if propane heater running
- OR: turn off propane heater at night; rely on sleeping bags rated to 0°F or below (individual body heat is your last-resort heat source)
- 0°F rated sleeping bag + wool base layer + hat + gloves + vapor barrier (emergency space blanket inside bag) = survivable to -10°F ambient air temperature in enclosed space

## 4. Ice Storm

### What It Is

An ice storm occurs when a layer of freezing rain coats surfaces with a sheet of ice. Unlike sleet (frozen rain pellets), freezing rain falls as liquid and freezes on contact. Ice accumulation categories:

| Ice Accumulation | Impact |
|---|---|
| Trace – 1/4" | Slippery surfaces; minor hazard |
| 1/4" – 1/2" | Power line loading begins; tree limb breakage |
| 1/2" – 1" | Significant power outages; major tree damage |
| 1" + | Catastrophic; widespread outages; structural damage possible |

**1 inch of ice on power lines**: ~500 lbs of additional weight per 100 ft of line — well beyond design capacity of most rural distribution lines.

**Midwest ice storm history:**
- January 2009: major ice storm across Illinois, Indiana, Kentucky — 1.3 million without power; some rural areas 2 weeks without power
- February 1994: $700M damage across Midwest; 3+ million without power
- January 2007: extensive ice storm, Kansas through Ohio — record-breaking power outages

### Protocol — Ice Storm Watch (24–48 hours before)

**Fuel and power:**
1. Fill vehicle tank
2. Fill all propane tanks and kerosene containers
3. Verify generator fuel supply
4. Charge all devices, battery packs, radios

**Food and water:**
1. Stock 7-day supply without refrigeration requirement (ice storms routinely cause multi-day outages)
2. Fill water containers (water pressure may drop if pump stations lose power)

**Outdoor preparation:**
1. Bring in all outdoor furniture, tools, propane tanks
2. **Critical**: Assess trees for immediate hazard — dead limbs over house that ice will bring down are your primary risk
3. If there are dead limbs over house that you can reach safely with a pole saw: cut them now
4. Locate and mark your driveway edges so you know where to shovel after icing
5. Calcium chloride ice melt (NOT rock salt — damages vegetation + metal): spread on walkways before freezing begins

**Vehicles:**
1. Move vehicles into garage if available
2. If no garage: put cardboard under windshield wipers; this prevents them from bonding to glass
3. Apply Rain-X Aquapel or similar product to windshield (reduces ice bonding)

### Protocol — Ice Storm During Event

**Do not travel.** Ice storm roads are more dangerous than blizzard roads because ice has no traction point — even 4WD is ineffective on solid ice.

**Home:**
1. Monitor power — is it flickering? Charge everything now before it goes out
2. Listen for tree sounds (cracking, snapping) — a large tree falling on a house can happen without warning
3. If you hear extensive cracking from the tree over your bedroom: move to a different part of the house
4. Do NOT go outside to check on things unless absolutely necessary; falling ice and branches are real hazard

**If power goes out during ice storm:**
1. Enact power-outage protocol (generator + transfer switch, or propane heater)
2. Keep refrigerator door closed; it maintains temperature 4 hours
3. Full freezer maintains 48 hours; half-full maintains 24 hours
4. Outdoor temperature in ice storm: typically 25–33°F — your garage or porch IS your refrigerator; move perishables there

### Protocol — Ice Storm Aftermath

**24–72 hours (outage response):**
1. Assess tree damage: do NOT touch or go near any tree that has downed wires entangled in it
2. Any wire on the ground = treat as energized and stay 30 feet away; call utility company
3. Ice on roof: monitor for ceiling sag (ice adds 5.6 lbs/sq ft at 1"; old/weak roofs can fail)
4. If ceiling starts to crack or show water: ice dam has formed; evacuate affected room; puncture ceiling at low point to drain water before structural failure (use an awl or ice pick — this is counterintuitive but correct)

**Tree and roof ice removal:**
- Roof rake (aluminum with extendable pole): pull snow off lower 3–4 ft of roof to reduce ice dam formation risk; NEVER use metal rake near power lines
- Do NOT chop ice off roof with ax or sharp implement — you will damage roofing material
- Calcium chloride (not rock salt) in a mesh tube or sock laid across an existing ice dam: melts channel through dam; takes several hours

**Extended outage after ice storm (1–2 weeks):**
This is the characteristic consequence of major Midwest ice storms — ice damage to distribution lines takes days to weeks to repair in rural areas.

Apply full extended-outage protocol:
- Day 1–2: propane/generator for basic heat and food
- Day 3–7: wood stove if available; begin rationing propane
- Week 2+: if utilities not restored, evaluate evacuation or extended hosting arrangement
- Coordinate with neighbors — share wood heat, generator, food

## 5. Blizzard / Winter Storm

### What It Is

A blizzard requires: sustained winds 35+ mph + 1/4 mile or less visibility from blowing snow. Winter storm is the broader category covering heavy snow without full blizzard conditions.

**Primary threats in Midwest blizzard:**
- Travel impossibility (days)
- Wind-driven infiltration making homes hard to heat
- Structural loading: 12" of wet snow = 20–25 lbs/sq ft; old or poorly maintained roofs at risk
- Isolation: rural areas can be cut off for 24–72 hours even after storm passes (roads uncleared)

### Protocol — Blizzard

**24 hours before:**
Same as polar vortex — fuel, food, water, devices charged. Add:
- Shovel: have accessible, not buried in garage
- Sand or kitty litter: for traction at doorways and critical paths
- Confirm snow blower fuel if you have one

**During:**
- Do not travel; emergency services cannot reach you in white-out conditions
- Shoveling: the #1 cause of heart attack in the US during winter storms is snow shoveling in unfit individuals; take 15-minute breaks; do not overexert in extreme cold

**After:**
- Check all vents (furnace exhaust, dryer vent, gas meter) — drifts block these and can kill
- Roof snow load: 12" wet snow = structural concern for older homes; shovel if feasible and safe (do not go on icy roof)
- Check on elderly neighbors within 24 hours of storm ending

## 6. Flash Flood (Midwest Specific)

### What It Is

Flash floods occur when intense rainfall saturates soil or overwhelms drainage systems faster than water can be absorbed. In Midwest:
- Agricultural tile drainage accelerates water movement to streams — rural stream flooding happens faster than urban models predict
- Low-water crossings on rural roads flood and kill more people than urban flash floods
- Basement flooding from sump pump failure or overwhelmed systems is the most common suburban flood event

**"Turn around, don't drown"**: 6 inches of water can knock you off your feet; 12 inches can carry a small car; 24 inches floats most vehicles. Never drive through water over a road.

### Protocol — Flash Flood Watch

1. Know your flood risk: check FEMA Flood Map (msc.fema.gov) for your property
2. If in flood zone: move valuables off ground floor to second story
3. Test sump pump; verify check valve functioning; have backup pump available
4. If sump pump runs on electricity: have battery backup sump pump ($100–200) installed

**During flooding:**
- Do not enter floodwater; assume sewage contamination
- Do not touch electrical outlets, panels, or appliances in flooded areas
- If basement flooding: do not go in if any electrical systems are energized — water + electricity kills instantly

**After flooding:**
- 4 weeks minimum before re-entering space without mold assessment
- Document all damage before cleanup
- Assume all tap water contaminated until tested

## 7. Heat Dome / Excessive Heat

### What It Is

Prolonged periods of extreme heat and humidity in Midwest, typically driven by a stationary high-pressure system. Without air conditioning, heat kills older adults, infants, and outdoor workers within hours.

**Midwest heat index thresholds:**
| Heat Index | Risk | Action |
|---|---|---|
| 91–103°F | Caution | Limit outdoor activity; hydrate |
| 103–124°F | Danger | Limit outdoor activity to coolest hours; cooling center |
| 125°F+ | Extreme danger | No outdoor exposure; cooling center immediately |

**1995 Chicago Heat Wave**: 739 people died over 5 days; primarily elderly in non-air-conditioned apartments. Single most lethal peacetime event in modern Chicago history.

### Protocol — Heat Dome

**Short-term (grid operational):**
1. Air conditioning: if available, use it; set no higher than 78°F
2. If no AC: open windows at night (coolest air); close and cover south/west windows by 8am; keep interior dark and closed during day
3. Electric fans: move air but don't cool; fan + slightly cooled room is effective; fan alone in hot room increases dehydration

**Grid-down heat dome:**
This is a serious survival scenario. A Zone 5 Midwest summer heat dome without electricity at 105°F heat index is fatal within hours for vulnerable people without intervention.

1. Water: drink 1 quart/hour during heavy heat exposure (not plain water — electrolytes)
2. Wet cloth on neck and wrists (cooling major blood vessels)
3. Move to lowest floor (cool air settles; basement is typically 55–65°F)
4. If basement available and is cool: this is your survival space in grid-down heat dome
5. Passive cooling (see `individual/03-shelter.md` passive cooling section — north-facing windows, thermal mass)
6. Community resources: public libraries, malls, and cooling centers typically run on backup power
7. Extreme heat watch neighbors: door-knock or call elderly/isolated neighbors daily during heat emergency
