## COMPONENT 1: Governance Synthesis Workshop Outline (1,200 words)

### Purpose

Create a 4-6 hour workshop outline that walks a 100+ person community through the transition from implicit norms to explicit governance. This is the load-bearing structure: everything else in Phase 4 and Phase 5 depends on legitimate decision-making authority.

### Pre-Workshop (2 hours before start)

**Facilitator prep:**
1. Print Phase 3 governance-decision-making.md (required reading, 30 min)
2. Print Phase 4 Framework Section 3 (community-scale synthesis, 20 min)
3. Prepare 5 large sheets: one for "Current Decision Method," four blank for "Governance Structure," "Information System," "Escalation Matrix," "Scaling Threshold"
4. Gather: markers (4 colors), sticky notes, timer, laptop + projector (optional)

**Participant materials:**
- Print or share access to Phase 3 governance document (20-page reference)
- Phase 4 Framework Section 3 (governance synthesis order, 5-page reference)
- Blank governance charter template (see Component 2 below)

### Workshop Flow (4-6 hours)

**SEGMENT 1: Current State Mapping (45 min)**

1. **Icebreaker / context setting** (5 min): "We're here to make our decision process explicit. Right now, who decides X [pick a real recent decision — resource allocation, conflict, work schedule]?"
2. **Current method documentation** (30 min): Facilitated group mapping on first sheet: "How do we actually make decisions now?" Expect to see: informal consensus, loudest voice, leader dominance, voting without quorum, decision avoidance. This is not wrong — it is the baseline.
3. **Current pain points** (10 min): "What decisions feel broken? What decisions have made people angry?" (usually: resource allocation, communication breakdowns, unclear authority)

**SEGMENT 2: Governance Principles (60 min)**

1. **Phase 3 governance framework introduction** (15 min): Presenter walks through: legitimacy, information infrastructure dependency, delegation vs. assembly, quorum/veto/escalation, scaling transitions.
2. **Community-specific governance design** (35 min): Break into 2-3 small groups (~15-20 people each if community is 100+). Each group gets one governance model:
   - **Model 1**: Direct assembly (weekly, all decisions, 150-person limit, Dunbar threshold)
   - **Model 2**: Delegate council (monthly assembly, weekly delegate meetings, 500-person limit, sub-committees for domains)
   - **Model 3**: Nested circles (consensus-based working groups, each group has liaison to next level, horizontal scaling)
   
   **Task per group** (20 min): "How would your model make a resource allocation decision? Who has authority? What's the escalation path if there's disagreement?"
3. **Report back and synthesis** (10 min): Each group presents their model. Facilitator highlights: "Model 1 breaks at 150+ people. Model 2 requires trained delegates. Model 3 is slow but preserves autonomy." Communities almost always choose Model 2 (delegate councils).

**SEGMENT 3: Governance Implementation (60 min)**

1. **Charter skeleton** (30 min): Facilitated completion of governance charter template:
   - Decision-making method: [assembly / delegates / hybrid]
   - Quorum: [threshold for decision-making]
   - Escalation protocol: [who arbitrates disagreements]
   - Information infrastructure: [how does governance get accurate data?] ← **CRITICAL — information is gating**
   - Scaling threshold: [at what population size do we restructure?]
   - Decision authority: [what decisions require full assembly vs. delegated permission?]
2. **First-pass authority matrix** (20 min): Simple 2×2 for each domain (governance, food, security, information, health):
   - **Axes**: Daily decisions vs. emergency decisions vs. resource allocation vs. policy change
   - **Who decides**: Group fills in for food first (highest-conflict domain), then others
   - **Triggers**: "If food is running low, who has authority to restrict consumption?"
3. **Information infrastructure spec** (10 min): "What information does governance need to make decisions?" Expected: resource inventory, population status, external threat assessment, performance metrics for each domain.

**SEGMENT 4: Household Scaling (45 min)**

1. **Household vs. community governance** (10 min): "Governance at 15 people (household) is different from 100+ (community). Same principles, different scale."
2. **Household coordinator roles** (25 min): Each household (8-15 people) designates or volunteers:
   - **Water coordinator**: Tracks daily consumption, maintenance, backup activation
   - **Food coordinator**: Manages stores, planting schedule, preservation
   - **Health coordinator**: Maintains records, first-aid response, mental health awareness
   - **Energy coordinator**: Load-shedding, backup fuel, distribution priorities
   - **Governance liaison**: Attends community meetings, brings household input, reports community decisions back
   - **Child/elder care coordinator** (if applicable): Childcare, elder support, education
   
   **Task**: Each household completes 1-page "Household Charter" (see Component 2) naming roles and decision authority within the household.
3. **Report back** (10 min): Each household reports: "Our food coordinator is X. Our governance liaison is Y." This makes accountability visible.

**SEGMENT 5: Commitment & Closure (15 min)**

1. **Draft governance charter read-aloud** (7 min): Facilitator reads the full governance charter skeleton back to the group.
2. **Amendment window** (5 min): "Does this match what we just decided? What's missing?"
3. **Commitment moment** (3 min): "This governance structure is now in effect starting June 2. Governance assembly meets [date/time]. First agenda: finalize charter and name delegates." (If delegate model chosen.)

### Post-Workshop (June 2-4)

- **June 2 morning**: Charter distributed (digital + printed). Governance liaison from each household gathers household feedback.
- **June 3 morning**: Feedback consolidated. Facilitator makes amendment pass (max 3 amendments, justification required).
- **June 3 evening**: First governance assembly OR delegate meeting (whichever model chosen) to formally adopt governance structure.
- **June 4 evening**: Governance is operational. Household coordinators are active. Information infrastructure is in startup.

### Facilitator Notes

- **Hardest moment**: When the community realizes governance is their responsibility, not a pre-made solution. Expect resistance. ("Can't you just tell us what to do?") Remind them: "Legitimate decisions are the ones people affected by them had a voice in making."
- **If community is < 25 people**: Use single workshop instead of small groups. Communities of 15-25 use simplified governance (one coordinator per 5 people, monthly assembly, no delegates).
- **If community is 200+**: Use two separate workshops (subgroups), then a synthesis meeting to integrate governance structure across sub-groups.
- **Political will is structural**: If governance fails, it's not because people are bad at meetings. It's because information infrastructure isn't working (people don't have data to decide), or authority isn't distributed clearly (people feel disrespected), or compensation is perceived as unfair (blame the allocation process, not the governor).

## COMPONENT 3: Zone 5 Adaptation Guide (1,100 words)

### Midwest-Specific Implementation Adjustments

**Why this matters**: The Phase 3 and Phase 5 documents are written at community-generic level. Zone 5 (Illinois, Michigan, Iowa, Indiana, Wisconsin, Minnesota, Missouri) has three climate and logistics constraints that change implementation order:

1. **Winter harshness** (November-March): Heating is life-or-death. Food availability is near-zero. Energy is critical.
2. **Agricultural season compression** (May-September): Short growing window means planting/harvest timing is rigid.
3. **Distance and weather volatility**: Summer storms, spring flooding, road closures in winter. Logistics planning is non-optional.

### Implementation Sequence for Zone 5 (Differs from Generic Community)

**Generic (Phase 3 order)**: Information infrastructure → Governance → Food → Security
**Zone 5 order** (phase 4 adjustment): Information infrastructure → Governance + Winter Preparation (parallel) → Food → Security

**Why the difference**: In Zone 5, winter survival (fuel, shelter, heating) is time-gating. If governance isn't operational by September, you won't have decision-making authority in place before heating season (October 1). If food preservation isn't complete by October 1, you don't have winter food supply.

**June-December implementation timeline for Zone 5 communities:**

| Month | Governance | Food | Energy | Water | Health |
|-------|-----------|------|--------|-------|--------|
| **June** | Adopt charter, establish council | Finalize June planting, plan preservation | Test solar/batteries, stock fuel | Finalize well/cistern, test pumps | First-aid training, mental health baseline |
| **July** | First decisions (resource policy), escalation testing | Intensive harvesting, start preservation | Fuel/propane delivery, load-test system | Summer maintenance window | Psychological support group established |
| **August** | Conflict protocol refined, scaling readiness | Peak preservation, root cellar prep | Winterization audit (heating capacity?) | System check before winter | Mental health monitoring |
| **September** | Delegate election (if 100+), finalize authorities | Finish preservation, full inventory | Heating system operational test, fuel stockpile final | Water backup systems tested | Winter protocol finalized |
| **October** | Governance fully operational, no surprises | Food rationing protocol active (if needed) | Heating seasonal operation begins | Winter backup ready | Mental health prevention active |
| **Nov-Mar** | Quarterly review only (minimal new decisions) | Ration monitored, spring planning begins | Heating sustained, fuel monitored | Manual backup ready (power down frozen pipes) | Daily mental health support critical |

### Zone 5 Governance Bottleneck

**The hardest decision to make in Zone 5 governance: winter heating allocation.**

Communities with >100 people will inevitably face scarcity decisions: If heating fuel is running low (January), do we:
- Reduce heating to 60°F and ask elders to stay in shared heated spaces?
- Reduce heating per household proportionally?
- Prioritize health risks (children, elders)?
- Increase work priority for fuel procurement?

**This decision MUST be made in advance** (before October). If governance tries to make it in January during an actual heating crisis, the community fractures. The Phase 3 governance document covers this conceptually. Phase 4's job is to force the community to make the specific Zone 5 decision in June, when there's no crisis pressure.

**June governance meeting agenda item (non-negotiable for Zone 5):**
- "If heating fuel reaches 30 days supply, activate winter rations: [specify temperature drop / space consolidation / rationing protocol]."
- Vote to adopt. Record the decision. Communicate to all households.
- When January arrives and heating is at 35 days supply, governance executes a pre-made decision, not a heated argument.

### Zone 5 Food Security Adjustments

**Growing season is compressed (May-September).**
- Phase 5 agriculture documents assume 6-month growing windows (southern US standard).
- Zone 5 frost dates: May 15 (last spring frost) to September 15 (first fall frost) = 4 months max.
- **Implication**: Cool-season crops (spring) + summer (tomatoes, beans, squash) + fall (brassicas) must be on a *strict calendar*. One week late on spring planting means lost crop.
- **Phase 4 adjustment**: Food coordinator must have June planting finalized by June 15 (hardline). No flexibility.

**Preservation window is short (August-October).**
- Peak harvest is August-September.
- Phase 4 responsibility: Confirm by June 1 that canning equipment, freezer capacity (backup power?), or dehydrator capacity exists. If not, emergency procurement in July. If not in July, winter food security fails.
- **Checklist item**: "Household preservation capacity audit complete by June 15. If capacity insufficient, emergency equipment procurement by July 15."

### Zone 5 Energy Reality Check

**Winter solar is nearly useless in Zone 5.**
- December-February: 8-9 hours daylight per day, but 60-70% cloudy. Panels generate ~20% of summer rating.
- Solar-only systems (common in southern communities) create an "energy cliff" in November.
- **Phase 4 adjustment**: Any household with solar-only energy systems must install backup by October 1: propane, kerosene, wood, or generator. This is not optional. It's a heating-survival prerequisite.
- **Implementation**: Phase 4 governance meeting in June must explicitly authorize household backup fuel purchases (often $800-2,000 per household). If authorization doesn't happen in June, households can't budget for July-August fuel stockpiling.

### Zone 5 Water Infrastructure Timing

**Winter freezing is real and specific.**
- Outdoor water systems freeze in October (air temp drops below 32°F consistently).
- Pipes above-ground freeze; underground water lines need minimum 4-foot depth.
- Hand pumps freeze; electric pumps draw heavy current.
- **Phase 4 adjustment**: By September 15, all water infrastructure must be winterized: buried pipes, heated pump houses, or manual-only backup systems. If winterization hasn't started by August, it won't be done by October.
- **Phase 4 responsibility**: In June, governance must allocate resources for winterization work and assign responsibility (who does the work, when, what's the budget).

### The One-Page Zone 5 Checklist (Post-Governance-Adoption)

**By September 15, verify:**
- ☐ Winter heating source is operational (temperature drop test at 50-60°F minimum)
- ☐ Fuel is stockpiled for 120 days minimum (October 1 – January 30)
- ☐ Food preservation is complete (all harvest preserved by October 1)
- ☐ Water infrastructure is winterized (pipes buried or heated, backup ready)
- ☐ Mental health support protocol is in place (winter depression is real)
- ☐ Governance is fully operational (no pending decisions that could fracture in a crisis)

If any item is not complete by September 15, winter survival is at risk.
