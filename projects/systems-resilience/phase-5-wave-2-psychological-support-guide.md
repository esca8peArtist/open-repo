# Psychological Support and Trauma Recovery
## A Tier 2 Household Guide for Zone 5 Extended Disruption

> **Region**: Midwest US (Zone 5) | **Scale**: Household (8–25 people)
> **Phase**: 5 — Gap Fill | **Tier**: 2 — Household Operational Domain
> **Prerequisite reading**: individual/05-healthcare.md, phase-5/tier-2-household-coordination-infrastructure-guide.md
> **Read alongside**: phase-5/tier-2-conflict-resolution-deep-dive.md (de-escalation layer shared between documents)

## The Most Important Finding

Mental health is the dominant health burden in extended disruption — not physical injury. Studies following Hurricane Sandy found that the prevalence of probable serious mental illness in affected communities increased from 3.5% pre-Sandy to 14.5% post-Sandy among the hardest-hit groups. [1] SAMHSA's systematic analysis of disaster behavioral health identifies depression, PTSD, grief, and substance use escalation as the primary health consequences of extended disaster exposure, affecting 30–40% of directly exposed populations in sustained disruption. [2]

This is not a clinical gap requiring professional intervention for the Zone 5 household. The World Health Organization's Psychological First Aid (PFA) field guide describes lay-practitioner-applicable frameworks designed for exactly this context: resource-constrained environments, no licensed therapists available, significant ongoing stressors. [3] The honest evidence caveat must be stated upfront: PFA is *evidence-informed*, not evidence-based. A 2023 systematic review (PMC 10624106) confirmed that while PFA is widely adopted globally, rigorous randomized controlled trial evidence remains limited. [4] The practical framing that matches the evidence: PFA is a structured approach to not making things worse while providing social connection and practical support. This is sufficient justification for adoption — its components (safety, calm, connection, efficacy, hope) align with what the disaster resilience literature identifies as the core drivers of psychological recovery regardless of delivery method. [5]

Zone 5 amplifier: the November–March winter window creates a five-month period in which outdoor access is limited, social escape valves are reduced, household density is maximum, and seasonal affective disorder risk is elevated — all simultaneously. A household that builds psychological support infrastructure in advance of the first winter is dramatically better positioned than one attempting to build it during month two of a January disruption.

## Section 1: Zone 5 Psychological Risk Profile

### Understanding the Conditions That Create Psychological Vulnerability

Zone 5 Midwest rural extended disruption is psychologically distinctive in ways that standard disaster mental health frameworks — mostly developed for urban, post-hurricane, or post-earthquake scenarios — systematically undercount.

**The winter compression factor**: Five months of Zone 5 winter (approximately November through March) concentrates multiple psychological risk factors into the same temporal window. Reduced daylight reduces serotonin and melatonin regulation. Reduced outdoor access eliminates a primary coping resource. Agricultural disruption at the end of the prior growing season means households enter winter knowing exactly how much food they have and watching it deplete. Winter creates a psychological pressure environment that summer disruption does not. [6]

**The agricultural grief factor**: The loss of a growing season, a livestock herd, or a significant portion of stored food has documented psychological consequences that mainstream disaster frameworks undercount. Agricultural communities show elevated rates of depression and suicide following crop failure, livestock disease events, and farm economic losses. [7] The loss of livestock is functionally a combination of financial loss, relationship loss (animals are known as individuals), and identity disruption (livestock keepers' self-concept is bound to their animals) — a compounded grief with no close urban analog.

**The density and no-exit factor**: A household of 8–25 people has enough people for interpersonal friction and not enough space to escape it. In an urban disruption, a person experiencing conflict with a neighbor can leave. On a Zone 5 homestead in January with 12 inches of snow on the ground, there is nowhere to go. The household's psychological environment is non-negotiable.

**High-risk populations within the household**:

| Population | Specific Risk | Zone 5 Amplifier |
|---|---|---|
| Children under 12 | Developing cognitive frameworks for loss; regression under stress; limited verbal processing capacity | Winter cabin fever + disruption of school/social structure |
| Pre-existing depression/anxiety | Medication access disruption; coping routine disruption; social support network loss | Winter SAD amplification |
| Elderly members | Identity loss (role reduction), physical dependency increase, grief accumulation, generational loss memory | Physical mobility limits in winter |
| Primary decision-makers | Leadership exhaustion, decision fatigue, isolation from peer support, no one to report to | Continuous demand for judgment under uncertainty |
| Bereaved individuals | Acute grief; survivor guilt; disrupted grief rituals | Winter social isolation compounds grief |

**Source anchors**: PMC 4427458 [1]; SAMHSA Disaster Behavioral Health [2]; PMC 3955699 [8]; PMC 11203463 [9]; Sagepub SAFE Framework [10].

## Section 2: Psychological First Aid — The Lay Practitioner Layer

### What PFA Is and Is Not

The WHO Psychological First Aid Field Guide defines PFA as consisting of eight core actions: contact and engagement, safety and comfort, stabilization, information gathering, practical assistance, connection with social supports, information on coping, and linkage with collaborative services. [3] None of these require clinical training. All can be taught to a lay practitioner in 8–16 hours of training. NCTSN offers a free 5-hour online PFA course (NCTSN PFA Online, CE credit available). [11]

What PFA is not:
- It is not debriefing (the "critical incident stress debriefing" approach — CISD — has been shown in some studies to potentially increase PTSD risk; PFA deliberately does not include forced emotional processing). [4]
- It is not therapy. PFA does not attempt to resolve underlying psychological conditions.
- It is not diagnosis. PFA practitioners do not label people.

The evidence situation, stated honestly: the 2023 PMC 10624106 systematic review found that out of 21 studies reviewed, none were RCTs that met the review's quality criteria. A 2024 integrative review in Psychological Trauma (Sage) documented significant implementation variability. [12] The adoption of PFA is based on expert consensus, theoretical alignment with recovery literature, and the absence of harm evidence — not on controlled outcome data. This is an adequate basis for household-level adoption.

### The Eight PFA Actions in Household Practice

**1. Contact and Engagement**
Make initial contact with a distressed household member in a non-intrusive, non-clinical way. Do not approach with "what's wrong?" (diagnostic framing). Do approach with presence — sit nearby, offer a practical task to do together, say "I've got a few minutes if you want company." The goal is availability, not interrogation.

**2. Safety and Comfort**
Assess the immediate physical safety of the distressed person. Are they in physical danger? Are they safe from immediate harm? Provide physical comfort: warmth, water, a quiet space away from the noise of the household.

**3. Stabilization**
For a person in acute distress (crying, hyperventilating, dissociated), stabilization before problem-solving is essential. Grounding techniques: ask the person to name five things they can see, four they can touch, three they can hear. Slow breathing (inhale 4 counts, hold 4, exhale 6) activates the parasympathetic nervous system. Do not attempt to discuss the cause of distress until the person is physiologically stable.

**4. Information Gathering**
Once stable, gather what you need to help practically: what has happened? What is the person most concerned about right now? Who do they need to connect with? Do not gather more than needed. The goal is understanding what help is needed, not processing the full experience.

**5. Practical Assistance**
Address immediate practical needs first. A person worried about whether the livestock survived the night cannot process emotional support until that worry is addressed. PFA's evidence-aligned insight: practical help is psychological help. Solving a concrete problem restores a sense of efficacy, which is one of the five documented drivers of psychological recovery. [5]

**6. Connection with Social Supports**
Connect the person with their within-household support network. Identify who in the household the person is closest to; create the conditions for that connection. Do not try to be the sole source of support — the PFA practitioner is a connector, not a therapist.

**7. Information on Coping**
Share information about normal stress responses — what is happening to the body and mind under stress is biologically normal. Normalizing the response reduces the secondary trauma of being frightened by one's own reaction. "What you're feeling is a normal response to an abnormal situation" is load-bearing language.

**8. Linkage with Collaborative Services**
Under disruption conditions, "services" may be unavailable. The household PFA practitioner should know: what is available within travel distance? Who has clinical training in the household (nurses, social workers, counselors)? What is the household's protocol for a genuine psychiatric emergency?

### What PFA Cannot Manage

Lay PFA is not appropriate and must be escalated when:
- The person is expressing suicidal ideation with a specific plan and intent (see Section 4 for the protocol)
- The person is experiencing psychosis (delusions, hallucinations, disorganized thinking)
- The person has a substance dependence that requires medical detox to manage withdrawal safely
- The person has been physically harmed and the harm-causer is within the household (this is a security matter before it is a mental health matter — see phase-5/tier-2-conflict-resolution-deep-dive.md, Section 5)

## Section 3: Community Grief Rituals and Collective Healing

### Why Individual Support Is Not Enough

The anthropological and psychological literature on collective trauma recovery identifies a finding that clinical frameworks consistently underweight: communities recover psychologically through collective meaning-making processes, not primarily through individual treatment. [17] The studies are consistent across disaster types and cultural contexts: communities that maintain collective rituals, shared grieving practices, and communal renewal events show better long-term psychological outcomes than communities that prioritize individual-only support.

For the Zone 5 homestead household, this literature provides practical guidance: the household meeting, the shared meal, the seasonal event, and the explicit acknowledgment of loss are not secondary concerns after logistics are managed — they are part of the psychological infrastructure that enables the household to maintain collective function under stress.

### Types of Collective Healing Practice

**Acknowledgment rituals after loss**: When a household member dies, significant livestock is lost, or a major setback occurs (crop failure, structure loss), a brief formal acknowledgment — distinct from logistics response — serves a recovery function. This does not require religious framing (though it can include it). Elements: gathering all household members; naming what was lost; space for each person to say something if they wish; a physical marker (planting something, placing a stone, creating a record in the household notebook). The anthropological evidence consistently shows that communities without such practices accumulate unprocessed grief that surfaces later as interpersonal conflict and psychological breakdown. [17]

**Seasonal markers as psychological structure**: The Zone 5 agricultural calendar — planting, first harvest, solstice, final harvest, winter preparation — provides natural psychological structure when deliberately maintained. These are not trivial. Research on social rhythm therapy (the clinical approach to maintaining daily/seasonal routine as a mental health intervention) shows that consistent daily and seasonal rhythms protect against depression, particularly in winter. [18] The household that marks the winter solstice with a community meal, acknowledges the longest night, and celebrates the increasing daylight from that point is using a 10,000-year-old evidence-based intervention.

**The household meeting as emotional infrastructure**: The Household Coordination guide establishes the weekly household meeting as the primary governance tool. The same meeting, when facilitated with emotional awareness, serves an additional function: it creates a regularized context in which household members see each other as full people, not just role-holders. A brief check-in at the start of each meeting (one word: how are you today?) requires 90 seconds and serves as a diagnostic for developing distress that might otherwise remain invisible until it disrupts operations.

**Circle practices and restorative grief rituals**: Restorative circle practices — drawing on Anishinaabek and other indigenous circle traditions that are culturally proximate to the Zone 5 Midwest — provide a structured format for communal grief expression and healing after significant harm or loss events. [Note for full draft: the restorative circles source material is shared with the Conflict Resolution document. Cross-reference rather than duplicate. The grief ritual application is distinct from the harm-repair application covered there.] The Foundation for Intentional Community's community grieving resources provide a starting point accessible to households without prior experience. [19]

### Practical Implementation

A Zone 5 household can establish collective grief and healing infrastructure without training or prior experience:

- Designate one person as the ritual keeper (can rotate quarterly) — their responsibility is to notice when a collective acknowledgment is needed and to convene it
- Establish four seasonal anchor events per year (planting day, first harvest, winter solstice, and one household-chosen event)
- Within 72 hours of any significant loss: convene a brief (30–60 minute) acknowledgment gathering — not a problem-solving meeting, not a grief-therapy session, just a moment of named recognition
- Include a monthly emotional check-in as a distinct agenda item in the household meeting (5–10 minutes, separate from logistics)

**Source anchors**: Saul, "Collective Trauma, Collective Healing" [17]; Foundation for Intentional Community [19]; Resilience.org grief and ritual [20]; PMC 11047467 [16]; PMC 11185912 [21].

## Section 4: Winter Depression and Seasonal Affective Management

### Zone 5-Specific Risk

Seasonal affective disorder (SAD) affects an estimated 1–6% of the U.S. general population. In northern latitudes (Zone 5 includes Minnesota, Wisconsin, Michigan, Iowa, Illinois, Indiana — all high-latitude states), subsyndromal winter depression ("winter blues" affecting energy, mood, sleep, appetite without meeting full diagnostic criteria) may affect 10–20% of the population. [22] Under extended disruption conditions — medication unavailability, disrupted coping routines, reduced social support, and the additional stressors of resource management — these baseline rates are likely to be significantly elevated.

### Non-Pharmaceutical Management Approaches

The following approaches are supported by clinical evidence and require no pharmaceutical access or electricity for their core implementation:

**Outdoor daylight exposure (primary intervention)**:
- Goal: 30–60 minutes of outdoor exposure during peak daylight hours (10 AM–2 PM in Zone 5 winter)
- Works even on overcast days (outdoor light levels, even on cloudy winter days, range from 1,000–10,000 lux, far exceeding indoor artificial light)
- The 2025 PMC systematic review and network meta-analysis on light therapy for SAD (PMC 12237333) confirms light therapy as the most effective monotherapy for SAD; outdoor exposure achieves comparable effect. [22]
- Zone 5 practical challenge: extreme cold (below −10°F with wind chill) limits outdoor exposure. Mitigate: brief outdoor exposure (10–15 minutes) with proper layering is still beneficial; work tasks that must be done outdoors (animal care, wood gathering) can be scheduled during peak daylight to serve double duty.

**Physical labor cadence**:
- Structured physical labor — not exercise for its own sake, but productive work that requires physical exertion — serves multiple psychological functions simultaneously: provides accomplishment, creates fatigue that improves sleep quality, generates endorphins, and creates social connection through shared work.
- The Zone 5 homestead has abundant physically demanding work in winter: wood splitting, animal care, building maintenance, fermentation and food processing. The psychological function is unlocked by scheduling this work during daylight and doing it with other household members.

**Social rhythm therapy principles**:
- Social rhythm therapy (SRT) is a clinical intervention developed for bipolar disorder that has documented effectiveness for seasonal depression. Its core principle is applicable without clinical oversight: regular daily rhythms (consistent wake/sleep time, meal timing, social contact timing) buffer against depressive episodes.
- Practical household application: maintain consistent meal times regardless of day of week; maintain a consistent morning routine regardless of workload; protect one social meal per day at a consistent time. These are not restrictions — they are psychological infrastructure.

**Sleep hygiene under winter disruption**:
- Winter disruption is particularly dangerous for sleep: extended darkness increases sleep drive; disruption stress disrupts sleep architecture; alcohol (which may be more available in a homestead context) suppresses REM sleep. Poor sleep is both a symptom and an accelerant of depression.
- Practical: enforce a consistent bedtime and wake time; use darkness during designated sleep hours (Zone 5 winter will provide this naturally); avoid alcohol as a sleep aid.

### Light Therapy When Power Is Available

If the household has electricity (solar, generator): 10,000-lux broad-spectrum light therapy boxes are the evidence-based intervention. [22] Commercial units are $30–100; they require 20–30 minutes of morning exposure. This is a high-value preparedness acquisition for Zone 5 households: inexpensive, effective, and low power draw (typical draw: 20–50 watts).

### Suicide Risk in the Zone 5 Winter Window

Zone 5 November–March has above-baseline suicide rates. This is well-documented in the agricultural mental health literature. The household's lay PFA practitioners should know three things:

1. **Warning signs**: withdrawal from social contact, giving away possessions, expressed hopelessness ("nothing will ever get better"), previous attempts (the strongest predictor), dramatic mood change after a period of depression (which can indicate a decision has been made).

2. **The question to ask**: The evidence is clear and consistent — directly asking "Are you thinking about suicide?" does not increase suicide risk and frequently reduces it by establishing that the topic is not forbidden. The QPR Institute ("Question, Persuade, Refer") provides the evidence-based lay-practitioner protocol for suicide risk conversations. [23] QPR training is available free online.

3. **The response**: A person expressing active suicidal ideation with plan and intent is a clinical emergency. Under normal conditions: call 988 (Suicide and Crisis Lifeline) or 911. Under disruption conditions with limited communication: do not leave the person alone; activate all household support resources; the goal is immediate human connection, safety, and getting to professional help as soon as possible regardless of infrastructure constraints.

## Section 5: Caregiver Burnout and Leadership Exhaustion

### The Asymmetric Burden Problem

The household's psychological support providers — the PFA practitioners, the Medical Coordinator, the domain coordinators — are simultaneously under the greatest stress and providing the most support to others. This is the pattern consistently documented in caregiver burnout research: caregivers delay recognizing their own depletion until it has reached the point of functional impairment. [26] At that point, the household has not just lost a functioning member — it has lost the member who was holding the others together.

The 2023 scoping review of compassion fatigue in healthcare providers (PMC 10693134) found that emotional exhaustion, depersonalization, and reduced sense of personal accomplishment follow a predictable progression that is recognizable well before functional impairment — but only if the caregiver is being observed by someone other than themselves. [27]

### Recognition

The Maslach Burnout Inventory (MBI) is the clinical standard for burnout measurement. In household-accessible language, the three MBI dimensions are:

**Emotional exhaustion**: "I feel emotionally drained at the end of the day more often than not." "I feel like I have nothing left to give." This is the leading indicator — it appears first, before the other dimensions.

**Depersonalization**: "I find myself treating household members as problems to solve rather than people I care about." "I feel detached from what's happening." This is the middle stage — the caregiver's empathy capacity is depleted.

**Reduced efficacy**: "Nothing I do seems to make a difference." "I don't know why I'm doing this." This is late-stage burnout — intervention at this point is much harder.

A monthly peer check-in between the household's PFA practitioners and domain coordinators, using these three dimensions as the check-in framework, provides early warning at the emotional exhaustion stage, before depersonalization develops.

### Prevention Protocols

**Role rotation**: No single person should hold a high-burden support role indefinitely. The Medical Coordinator, Conflict Resolution Facilitator, and designated PFA practitioners should have defined rotation schedules (quarterly is appropriate). The transition should include a handoff period, not a cold transfer.

**Mandatory rest**: The Household Coordination guide establishes the governance right to request role rotation for incapacity. The psychological support layer adds a proactive requirement: high-burden roles require a designated off-day each week — one 24-hour period in which the role holder is explicitly not responsible for their domain. This is operationally necessary, not an indulgence.

**Peer accountability for self-care**: The pattern in caregiver burnout is that the caregiver cannot accurately assess their own depletion. The household's peer support system must include explicit accountability: each high-burden role holder designates a peer check-in partner whose responsibility is to notice burnout warning signs and name them. This relationship should be formalized in the household governance charter.

**The support-for-supporters protocol**: Monthly, the full household PFA capacity (typically 2–3 trained practitioners) meets without any case content — purely to support each other. This is the peer supervision model from professional mental health practice adapted to household scale. It requires 45–60 minutes once a month and is the single most effective protection against caregiver burnout collapse.

## Section 6: Implementation — Building Household Psychological Support Capacity

### Minimum Viable Implementation

**Who gets trained**: At minimum, two household members should complete WHO PFA training (NCTSN online, 5 hours, free [11]). Recommended profile: one older adult (50+) and one younger adult (25–45) to ensure age-appropriate support for all household demographics. At least one should not be in a primary governance role — their PFA function needs to be somewhat separate from their authority role.

**Physical resources**:
- A designated quiet space accessible to any household member at any time — a specific room, a corner of a barn, an outdoor shelter — that is understood to be a place for private recovery, not for productive work
- A written resource list: nearest mental health professional within travel distance; nearest crisis line (988); nearest hospital or emergency mental health service; any household members with clinical mental health training (nurses, social workers, counselors)

**Household governance integration**:
- Monthly emotional check-in as a standing household meeting agenda item (5–10 minutes; simple format: each person shares one word or one sentence about how they are doing)
- Burnout monitoring as a standing quarterly agenda item for all domain coordinators
- Ritual calendar established in the household charter: the four seasonal anchor events, the monthly check-in, and the 72-hour acknowledgment protocol for significant losses

### Implementation Timeline

**Pre-crisis (before any disruption)**:
- [ ] Two household members complete NCTSN PFA Online
- [ ] Quiet space designated and communicated to all household members
- [ ] Resource list created and placed in household information binder
- [ ] Ritual calendar established (four seasonal events, monthly check-in format)
- [ ] Light therapy box acquired and stored (if solar/power available)
- [ ] Burnout monitoring framework discussed and agreed by all high-burden role holders

**First 72 hours of disruption**:
- [ ] PFA practitioners identified and activated (household members know who to go to)
- [ ] Safety assessment conducted for all household members
- [ ] Communal meal convened within first 24 hours (social connection signal)

**Sustained disruption (weeks 2+)**:
- [ ] Weekly household check-in maintained
- [ ] Monthly emotional check-in established
- [ ] Burnout monitoring for coordinators begins
- [ ] Seasonal events maintained as scheduled regardless of operational pressure

**After significant loss (death, major setback)**:
- [ ] Acknowledgment gathering convened within 72 hours
- [ ] PFA check-in with most affected household members within 24 hours of the event
- [ ] Normal work schedule suspended for 24–48 hours where operationally possible
