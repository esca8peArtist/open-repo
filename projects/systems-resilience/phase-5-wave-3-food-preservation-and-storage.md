# Food Preservation & Storage Systems

## Overview

A Zone 5 household that grows nothing beyond a summer garden will consume, on average, 2,000–2,500 calories per person per day — roughly 730,000–912,000 calories per person per year [1]. Commercial supply chains that deliver those calories operate on just-in-time logistics with 3–7 days of buffer stock at any given node. A single hard freeze, fuel disruption, or regional infrastructure failure can empty store shelves within 72 hours and leave rural households without resupply for days to weeks.

Zone 5's 100–140 day frost-free window (last frost approximately May 15, first frost approximately October 1–15) [2] creates a structural forcing function: nearly all caloric-dense production happens in a 4.5-month window, but consumption is continuous across 12 months. Bridging that gap without refrigeration electricity or commercial supply is the core problem food preservation solves.

This document covers six primary preservation pathways — canning, cold storage, drying, fermentation, freezing, and combination methods — with specific quantities, yield tables, failure modes, and step-by-step procedures for Zone 5 conditions. Equipment can be sourced from local farm stores, cooperative extensions, and online suppliers; no professional training or licensed infrastructure is required.

**Annual need benchmarks (per adult, moderate activity):**
- Calories: 2,000–2,500/day → 730,000–912,500/year [1]
- Preserved vegetables: 50–60 quarts canned + 50–80 lbs dried/fermented minimum [3]
- Preserved fruit: 20–30 quarts canned or equivalent dried [4]
- Grain/legume bulk storage: 300–400 lbs grain + 60–100 lbs legumes [5]

**Primary failure modes for preservation systems:**
- Power loss (freezing method dependency)
- Seal failure during canning (improper headspace or worn lids)
- Temperature fluctuation in root cellars (Zone 5 spring freeze-thaw)
- Insufficient salt concentration in fermentation (allows spoilage organisms)
- Inadequate moisture control in dried foods (rehydration and mold)

Recovery time after preservation failure varies by method: a failed canning batch can be reprocessed within 24 hours; a root cellar that floods or overheats may lose weeks of stored produce; a failed fermentation is typically unrecoverable but detectable by off-odor within 2–5 days.

## Section 1: Individual Scale — Canning and Pressure Processing

### 1.1 The Science of Safe Canning

Canning creates a vacuum-sealed, shelf-stable product by using heat to destroy spoilage organisms and expel oxygen. The single greatest safety risk in home canning is *Clostridium botulinum*, a spore-forming anaerobe that produces a neurotoxin lethal in microgram quantities. Botulinum spores are heat-resistant: they survive 212°F (100°C) boiling water for hours but are destroyed at 240°F (116°C) within 10–12 minutes [6].

This creates the fundamental rule of home canning: **high-acid foods (pH ≤ 4.6) can be safely processed in a boiling water bath; all low-acid foods must be pressure canned.** No exceptions. The division is not arbitrary — botulinum cannot produce toxin below pH 4.6, so acid itself is the safety barrier in water bath canning [7].

**High-acid (water bath canning):** Tomatoes (with added acid), all fruits, pickles, jams, jellies, chutneys, most salsas.

**Low-acid (pressure canning required):** All plain vegetables (green beans, corn, carrots, potatoes, beets, squash), meats, poultry, fish, soups, stews, dried beans.

The USDA Complete Guide to Home Canning (Agriculture Information Bulletin No. 539, 2015 revision) is the authoritative source for all tested processing times, pressures, and jar sizes [8]. Do not use untested recipes or times from non-USDA sources — many older family recipes, particularly those from before 1989, used processing times that are now known to be unsafe.

### 1.2 Equipment for Individual-Scale Canning

**Water bath canner:** A large stockpot (at minimum 12-quart capacity) with a rack to keep jars off the bottom. Cost: $20–$45 new, or improvise with a stockpot and folded jar rings as a rack.

**Pressure canner:** A weighted-gauge or dial-gauge canner, minimum 10-quart capacity. The All American (aluminum, no gasket) and Presto models are widely recommended for durability. Cost: $90–$350 new; used units are widely available. A pressure canner is not the same as a pressure cooker — do not substitute. Key difference: pressure canners have larger chambers and tested temperature uniformity [8].

**Jars:** Ball or Kerr mason jars in pint and quart sizes. Jars are reusable indefinitely if chips and cracks are absent; lids (flat metal sealing discs) must be replaced each use; bands (screw rings) are reusable many times [9].

**Other tools:** jar lifter ($8–$12), lid wand or tongs, wide-mouth funnel, bubble remover/headspace tool, clean cloths.

**Annual supply cost estimate for individual:** Starter equipment (one canner, 1 case each pints/quarts, tools) = $80–$200 first year. Subsequent years: lids ~$0.20–0.35 each, produce costs variable. University of Maine Cooperative Extension found per-jar costs ranging from $0.25–$2.00 depending on produce source and method [10].

### 1.3 Water Bath Canning — Step-by-Step

1. Inspect jars for chips, cracks, or rough sealing edges. Wash with hot soapy water; rinse well. Keep jars hot until filling.
2. Prepare lids: simmer (do not boil) in hot water to soften sealing compound.
3. Prepare produce per tested recipe. Tomatoes require acidification: add 2 tablespoons bottled lemon juice or ½ teaspoon citric acid per quart (1 tablespoon lemon juice or ¼ teaspoon citric acid per pint) [8].
4. Fill jars leaving proper headspace (typically ¼ inch for jams/jellies, ½ inch for most fruits and pickles, 1 inch for low-acid items).
5. Remove air bubbles by running a thin spatula around interior.
6. Wipe jar rims with a clean damp cloth — any food on the rim prevents sealing.
7. Apply lids and bands; tighten to "fingertip tight" — snug but not torqued.
8. Load jars into canner on rack; add boiling water to cover jars by at least 1–2 inches.
9. Bring to full rolling boil; start timing. Process for tested time (varies by food, jar size, altitude).
10. At altitude above 1,000 ft, increase processing time: 1–3,000 ft add 5 min; 3,001–6,000 ft add 10 min [8]. (Most Zone 5 Midwest sites are below 1,000 ft.)
11. Remove jars; cool undisturbed 12–24 hours on a towel.
12. Test seals: center of lid should not flex. Remove bands; store sealed jars in cool, dark location.

### 1.4 Pressure Canning — Step-by-Step

1. Follow jar preparation as above.
2. Add 2–3 quarts water to canner bottom (follow manufacturer specs).
3. Fill jars, leaving 1 inch headspace for most vegetables and meats.
4. Apply lids; load into canner; lock lid.
5. Heat on high; vent steam for 10 minutes before closing petcock or placing weight.
6. Bring to required pressure (10 lbs weighted gauge, or 11 lbs dial gauge, at altitudes below 2,000 ft).
7. Begin timing once pressure is reached and stable.
8. Maintain steady pressure for full processing time — do not let pressure drop and recover.
9. Turn off heat; allow canner to depressurize naturally (30–45 minutes). Do not force-cool.
10. Remove jars; cool and test seals as above.

**Selected pressure canning times (pints, below 2,000 ft elevation):**
- Green beans (cut): 20 minutes at 10 lbs [8]
- Carrots (sliced): 25 minutes at 10 lbs [8]
- Corn (whole kernel): 55 minutes at 10 lbs [8]
- Potatoes (cubed): 35 minutes at 10 lbs [8]
- Tomato juice: 35 minutes at 10 lbs [8]

### 1.5 Yield Calculations — Pounds of Produce to Jars

| Produce | Lbs per quart | Lbs per pint | Notes |
|---------|--------------|--------------|-------|
| Tomatoes (whole/halved) | 3 lbs | 1.5 lbs | Add acid; water bath OK [11] |
| Green beans | 2 lbs | 1 lb | Pressure can only [8] |
| Carrots | 2.5–3 lbs | 1.25–1.5 lbs | Pressure can only [8] |
| Corn (cut) | 4.5–5 lbs | 2–2.5 lbs | In husk; pressure can only [8] |
| Peaches | 2–3 lbs | 1–1.5 lbs | Water bath [8] |
| Apples (slices) | 2.5 lbs | 1.25 lbs | Water bath [8] |
| Strawberries (jam) | 1 lb berries + 0.75 lbs sugar → ~5 half-pints | — | Water bath [9] |

A bushel of tomatoes (53 lbs) yields approximately 17–18 quarts canned whole tomatoes, or 15–20 pints of sauce [11]. A bushel of green beans (30 lbs) yields 12–15 quarts [12].

## Section 2: Household Scale — Cold Storage, Drying, and Fermentation

### 2.1 Root Cellar Construction for Zone 5

Root cellars exploit the thermal mass of soil, which at 4–8 ft depth in Zone 5 Midwest maintains a relatively stable 45–55°F year-round, buffering both the summer heat and winter cold [13]. The ideal storage range for most vegetables is 32–40°F at 90–95% relative humidity; the challenge in Zone 5 is maintaining this range during the shoulder seasons when outdoor temperatures swing rapidly.

**Construction options (ascending cost and permanence):**

**Option A — Corner basement cellar (retrofit):** Convert a corner of an unheated basement into a root cellar by insulating the interior walls (not the exterior walls, which provide cold exposure) and installing a ventilation system. Cost: $200–$800 in materials. Requires two exterior foundation walls. The Michigan State University Extension provides detailed diagrams for this approach [13].

*Specifications:* Insulate interior walls with 2-inch rigid foam (R-10 minimum). Install a vent inlet near floor level (exterior, 4-inch diameter) and exhaust near ceiling (exterior, 4-inch diameter). Both vents should have closeable covers for temperature control. Add a door with weatherstripping. A thermometer/hygrometer is essential. Target: 32–40°F, 85–95% RH.

**Option B — Buried outdoor cellar (new construction):** Excavate below frost line (typically 42–48 inches in Zone 5 Illinois/Indiana/Ohio, up to 60 inches in Minnesota/Wisconsin [14]). Build walls from poured concrete or concrete block; pour concrete floor. Cover with 18–24 inches of soil over insulated roof deck.

*Specifications for a household-scale cellar (8 ft x 10 ft interior):*
- Excavation: 10 ft x 12 ft footprint, 8 ft deep (2 ft below frost line)
- Walls: 8-inch poured concrete or 12-inch block
- Roof: 6-inch concrete slab + 2-inch rigid insulation + 18-inch soil cover
- Access: insulated hatch or stairwell with double doors (airlock to reduce temperature shock)
- Ventilation: two 4-inch PVC pipes, one inlet at floor level, one exhaust at ridge
- Drainage: French drain or gravel bed around perimeter to prevent flooding
- Estimated capacity: 60–120 bushels of mixed produce
- Cost: $3,000–$8,000 depending on materials and labor; DIY significantly reduces cost

**Option C — In-ground storage (minimalist):** For individual-scale supplemental storage, bury a 55-gallon food-grade drum or wooden barrel to the frost line in a sheltered north-facing location. Pack around with straw bales for additional insulation. Store root vegetables in damp sand. Cost: $30–$80. Suitable for 50–100 lbs of carrots, beets, or turnips through a Zone 5 winter [15].

**Zone 5 specific failure modes:**
- Spring temperature spikes (March–April) can push cellar temps above 50°F, accelerating spoilage. Solution: close vents aggressively and monitor daily.
- Late freeze events (Zone 5 can see -5°F as late as April in northern areas) can freeze cellar contents if vents are open. Solution: install thermostatic vent closers or monitor nightly during transition seasons.
- Flooding from snowmelt: Zone 5 spring thaw can produce significant runoff. French drains and proper grading are non-negotiable.

### 2.2 What to Store and For How Long

**Optimal root cellar storage conditions (Zone 5 applicable varieties):**

| Crop | Temp Range | Humidity | Storage Duration |
|------|-----------|----------|-----------------|
| Carrots | 32–40°F | 90–95% | 4–6 months |
| Beets | 32–40°F | 90–95% | 3–5 months |
| Potatoes | 38–40°F | 85–90% | 4–6 months |
| Winter squash (cured) | 50–55°F | 60–70% | 3–6 months |
| Cabbage | 32–40°F | 90–95% | 3–4 months |
| Turnips/Parsnips | 32–40°F | 90–95% | 4–5 months |
| Apples (late varieties) | 32–40°F | 90–95% | 2–4 months |
| Onions/Garlic | 32–40°F | 65–70% | 6–8 months |

Note: Apples and cabbage emit ethylene gas and should be stored separately from other produce, or they will accelerate ripening and decay [16].

### 2.3 Food Drying for Zone 5

Drying removes 80–95% of moisture from food, inhibiting microbial growth without heat processing. The critical threshold is 10% moisture or less for vegetables and herbs (brittle), and 15–20% for fruits (leathery but pliable) [17]. At these moisture levels, bacteria cannot grow; mold growth is inhibited below approximately 13% moisture [18].

**Methods in order of reliability and cost:**

**Electric dehydrator (most consistent):** A fan-forced dehydrator with temperature control (Excalibur, Nesco, L'Equip are commonly cited models) maintains uniform temperature and airflow, typically drying at 125–145°F.

- Vegetables: blanch first (2–3 minutes boiling water or steam), then dry at 125°F for 4–12 hours depending on thickness and moisture content. Blanching preserves color, flavor, and extends storage life [17].
- Fruits: pre-treat with ascorbic acid (1 tsp/2 cups cold water, submerge 3 minutes) to prevent browning. Dry at 135°F for 6–36 hours. 
- Cost: $50–$400 for consumer dehydrators; $400–$900 for commercial units capable of processing a garden harvest in single batches.

**Solar dryer (no electricity):** A solar food dryer uses a glazed collector panel to concentrate heat and a separate screened drying chamber. Temperatures of 95–130°F are achievable on clear days in Zone 5. Limitations: Zone 5's short days and frequent cloud cover in September–October (peak harvest) make solar drying unreliable as a primary method. Use as supplemental or for early-season herbs [19].

*Construction schematic for household solar dryer:*
- Collector box: 2 ft x 4 ft, black-painted interior, glazed with polycarbonate or glass on top
- Drying chamber: insulated box 2 ft x 4 ft x 1 ft, screened vents at bottom and top
- Trays: 1/4-inch hardware cloth in wooden frames
- Positioning: south-facing at 35–45° tilt; collector feeds heated air upward through drying chamber
- Estimated capacity: 15–25 lbs fresh produce per clear day
- Cost: $40–$80 in materials

**Air drying (herbs and some fruits):** Hanging bundles of herbs, dried chili peppers, or apple rings in a warm, dry, well-ventilated space with low humidity. Zone 5 in summer can support this approach; autumn humidity often makes it unreliable for vegetables.

**Dried food yield and storage life:**

| Fresh product | Dried yield | Storage life (airtight, cool) |
|--------------|-------------|-------------------------------|
| 10 lbs zucchini | 0.8–1.2 lbs | 12 months [17] |
| 10 lbs tomatoes | 0.5–0.8 lbs | 12–18 months [17] |
| 10 lbs apples | 1–1.5 lbs | 12 months [17] |
| 10 lbs green beans | 1–1.5 lbs | 12 months [17] |
| 10 lbs onions | 1–1.5 lbs | 12 months [17] |
| 1 lb fresh herbs | 0.15–0.2 lbs dried | 12–24 months [17] |

Mylar bags with oxygen absorbers extend storage life by 50–100%: dried vegetables at 10% moisture in oxygen-free Mylar maintain quality for 3–5 years [5].

### 2.4 Lacto-Fermentation Systems

Lacto-fermentation is the oldest preservation method in human history and requires no heat, electricity, or specialized equipment — only salt, vegetables, and an airtight (or anaerobic-capable) vessel. The Lactobacillus bacteria present on vegetable surfaces consume sugars and produce lactic acid, which rapidly lowers pH below 4.6 (where botulinum cannot produce toxin) and then below 4.0, where nearly all pathogens are inhibited [20].

**Salt ratios — the critical variable:**
- Dry-salted ferments (sauerkraut, kimchi): 2–2.5% salt by weight of vegetable. USDA recommends 2.25–2.5% for sauerkraut [21]. Below 1.5% allows spoilage; above 3% may inhibit Lactobacillus.
- Brine ferments (cucumbers, whole vegetables): 2–3% brine by weight (2–3 tablespoons non-iodized salt per quart of non-chlorinated water).
- Use pickling salt or pure sea salt; iodized salt inhibits fermentation; chlorinated tap water may also inhibit fermentation (use filtered or stand 24 hours to off-gas chlorine).

**Vessel options:**
- Traditional crock (ceramic, 1–5 gallon): best for household scale; needs weights to submerge produce. Cost: $30–$120.
- Glass mason jars with airlock lids ($5–$15) or loosely placed standard lids (gas escapes, open daily to "burp").
- Food-grade 5-gallon plastic buckets with lids: adequate for community scale but harder to monitor.
- Do not use aluminum, reactive metals, or containers with cracks.

**Sauerkraut — step-by-step:**
1. Shred cabbage thinly (1/16–1/8 inch).
2. Weigh shredded cabbage. Add 2% salt by weight (e.g., 20g salt per 1 kg cabbage).
3. Massage and squeeze cabbage with salt for 5–10 minutes until it releases brine.
4. Pack tightly into crock or jars, pressing down until brine rises above cabbage surface.
5. Place weights to keep cabbage submerged (zip-lock bag filled with brine, or glass weights).
6. Cover loosely (not airtight) at room temperature (65–75°F optimal).
7. In Zone 5, summer temperature fermentation (70–75°F): ready in 4–7 days for mild sauerkraut, 2–4 weeks for fully soured.
8. Slower fermentation at 60–65°F: 3–6 weeks; produces more complex flavor.
9. Once desired sourness is reached, seal tightly and refrigerate or move to cold storage (35–40°F will slow but not stop fermentation).
10. Shelf stable at cool temperatures (35–40°F) for 3–6 months; in root cellar, 3–4 months minimum [20].

**Troubleshooting fermentation:**
- *White surface film (Kahm yeast):* Harmless wild yeast on oxygen-exposed surface. Skim off; ensure vegetables remain submerged.
- *Fuzzy colored mold (green, black, pink):* Discard if penetrating below brine surface. If only surface and vegetables are fully submerged with intact smell, carefully skim and assess.
- *Soft, mushy vegetables:* Usually from too-warm fermentation or insufficient salt; still safe if smell is clean and sour. Taste to verify.
- *Off/putrid smell:* Not sour but rotten — discard. This is caused by anaerobic bacteria in high-salt, high-heat, or improperly salted conditions.

**Community-scale fermentation:** A 5-gallon crock holds approximately 15–20 lbs cabbage, yielding 10–14 lbs sauerkraut. A household cabbage harvest of 50–80 heads (common in Zone 5 gardens) can produce 150–250 lbs sauerkraut per season. Virginia Tech Cooperative Extension details community-scale vegetable fermentation for multiple vessel types [22].

### 2.5 Freezer Preservation

Freezing is the most labor-efficient preservation method and maintains nutritional quality closest to fresh, but it is entirely dependent on sustained electricity. A Zone 5 preparedness strategy cannot rely on freezing as a primary method, but it is highly effective for shorter-term storage and as a parallel system.

**Freezer storage: USDA quality guidelines (at 0°F/-18°C):**

| Food | Quality window |
|------|---------------|
| Blanched vegetables (corn, beans, carrots, peas) | 8–12 months [23] |
| Tomatoes (raw or cooked) | 8–12 months [23] |
| Fruits | 8–12 months [23] |
| Bread/baked goods | 2–3 months [23] |
| Cooked beans, soups | 2–3 months [23] |
| Raw poultry | 9–12 months [23] |
| Raw ground meat | 3–4 months [23] |

Food stored at 0°F is safe indefinitely; quality degrades over time [23].

**Blanching before freezing — critical step for vegetables:** Blanching (brief boiling or steaming followed by immediate ice bath) deactivates enzymes that continue to degrade color, texture, and flavor even in frozen storage. Unblanched frozen vegetables become discolored and mushy within 2–4 months. Times: green beans 3 minutes, corn on cob 7–11 minutes, broccoli 3 minutes, carrots 2 minutes (sliced) [17].

**Zone 5 power failure planning:** An upright or chest freezer that is kept full (use water jugs to fill space) and not opened will hold temperature below 40°F for 24–48 hours during a power outage. A full chest freezer can maintain safe temperatures for 48–72 hours [23]. Have a plan: identify neighbors with generators, or have a cooler and ice plan to extend window. If temperature exceeds 40°F for more than 2 hours, meats and cooked foods must be cooked immediately or discarded; produce can often still be safely processed into canned or dried products.

## Section 3: Community Scale — Shared Infrastructure and Governance

### 3.1 Scaling Preservation Infrastructure

Community-scale food preservation enables efficiency through shared capital equipment, coordinated labor, and aggregated supply. A single All American 930 pressure canner (30-quart, $350) can process 7 quarts per batch; a community operation running 4–6 batches per day can process the equivalent of a household's annual vegetable needs in a weekend. The key constraints are coordination, sanitation, and knowledge transfer.

**Community canning kitchen requirements:**
- Commercial or large-capacity pressure canners (minimum 2–4 units for efficiency)
- Dedicated stainless or washable work surfaces
- Commercial sink with hot water
- Jar inventory (200–500 jars of mixed sizes)
- Processing logbook with dates, contents, lot identification, and processing parameters
- Trained operators: USDA Master Food Preserver certification is available through most university cooperative extension systems [24]

**Bulk food storage for community:**
A 20-person community requires approximately 14.6 million calories per year (2,000 cal/person/day × 20 people × 365 days). Grain and legume storage covers the caloric foundation:
- White rice (30-year shelf life in oxygen-free Mylar): 1,000 lbs stores approximately 1.6 million calories [5]
- Dried corn/hominy: 800 lbs → 1.3 million calories
- Dried beans (assorted): 400 lbs → 640,000 calories
- Total grain/legume foundation: ~3,540,000 calories → covers 485 person-days at 2,000 cal/day

Community root cellar (20 ft x 30 ft, properly constructed): stores 400–600 bushels of mixed produce, sufficient for 20 people through Zone 5 winter.

### 3.2 Governance and Distribution Models

Shared food preservation infrastructure requires explicit governance to function. Key decisions:
- Who has access to equipment (open community, membership, work-share contribution)?
- How are preserved supplies divided (proportional to labor/inputs contributed, equal share, need-based)?
- How are processing logs maintained and who certifies safety (trained Master Food Preserver or community-designated checker)?
- What happens to individual households' produce processed in communal space?

Recommended model for 10–30 household communities: **work-share cooperative**. Each household contributes 2–4 hours of labor per 10 lbs of produce processed. Common equipment (canners, dehydrators, fermentation vessels) is owned collectively through a community land trust or informal co-op agreement. Supplies (jars, lids, salt) are either individually owned or pooled with documented contribution. Missouri Extension's community garden program documentation provides governance templates [25].

### 3.3 Preservation Success Rates by Method

Based on USDA and university extension reported outcomes when procedures are correctly followed:

| Method | Safety failure rate (proper procedure) | Quality failure rate |
|--------|----------------------------------------|---------------------|
| Pressure canning | < 0.001% [7] | 2–5% (seal failures) |
| Water bath canning | < 0.01% (acid foods) [7] | 3–7% |
| Fermentation | < 0.1% [20] | 5–15% (Kahm, soft texture) |
| Drying (electric dehydrator) | < 0.1% [17] | 5–10% (moisture checks) |
| Root cellar (well-built) | < 1% pathogens | 10–25% seasonal losses [16] |
| Freezing (power maintained) | < 0.1% [23] | 5–15% freezer burn |

The highest quality losses occur in root cellar storage from temperature fluctuation, produce contact (spreading rot), and humidity mismatch. Regular monitoring (weekly inspections, culling damaged items) reduces quality losses to under 10% [16].

## Section 4: Implementation Roadmap

### Phase 1 — Bootstrap (First 6 Months)

**Goal:** Preserve one full growing season's Zone 5 harvest.

1. **Equipment procurement:** Water bath canner ($25–$45), pressure canner ($90–$200), 24 quart jars + 24 pint jars, standard tools. Budget: $150–$300.
2. **Skill acquisition:** Complete NCHFP free online canning course (nchfp.uga.edu) or local extension workshop. Process first batches with mentor or following USDA Guide step-by-step. Do not improvise.
3. **First fermentation trial:** 5 lbs cabbage → sauerkraut (zero equipment cost beyond a jar and salt). Establishes baseline for skill, taste preference, and troubleshooting.
4. **Baseline cold storage:** Designate the coolest, most stable-temperature space available (unheated basement, attached garage wall). Add thermometer; monitor daily for one week to characterize temperature range.
5. **Freezer baseline:** Fill chest freezer with 2–3 water jugs (extends thermal mass); process blanched vegetables for 6–8 month window. Parallel to, not replacing, shelf-stable methods.

### Phase 2 — Stabilize (Months 6–18)

**Goal:** Achieve 6-month food independence for household.

1. **Root cellar upgrade:** Retrofit existing basement corner or build in-ground option. Install ventilation; calibrate seasonal management.
2. **Acquire electric dehydrator:** Extend preservation capacity for herbs, sliced fruits, and excess garden produce.
3. **Fermentation system expansion:** 2–4 gallon crock or multiple mason jar system. Ferment through the fall harvest (cabbage, beets, turnips, green tomatoes).
4. **Document yields:** Track lbs in → jars out for all methods. This data drives garden planning for the following year.
5. **Expand grain/legume storage:** Target 6 months of grain/legume calories stored in food-grade containers with oxygen absorbers.

### Phase 3 — Scale (Months 18–36)

**Goal:** Community-scale capacity and 12-month food independence.

1. **Community canning kitchen:** Identify shared space (barn, church, community hall); acquire commercial-scale canner (All American 930 or equivalent); train 2–3 operators to USDA Master Food Preserver standard.
2. **Community root cellar:** If land is available, construct dedicated community-scale cellar per specifications in Section 2. Governance document required before first use.
3. **Combination preservation portfolio:** Implement pickled-then-dried products (dried pickle slices, fruit leathers), lacto-fermented vegetable mixes, and combination-method jams (pectin + acid + heat).
4. **Cross-domain dependencies:** Food preservation integrates with Zone 5 Seed Saving (heirloom varieties for storage quality), Water Systems (sanitation water for canning), and Community Health (food safety training and contamination response protocols).
