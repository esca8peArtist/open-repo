# Phase 4 Scope & Implementation Roadmap

> **Purpose**: Four concrete options for Phase 4 direction, with timelines, resource requirements, and a decision framework. User decision needed by June 1 for June execution to begin without delay.
>
> **Phase 1–3 Status**: Complete. ~100,000 words, 280+ citations, 35+ case studies. Phases 1–3 cover individual survival through community-scale crisis response across all five domains: governance, food systems, information, security, and scaling pathways.
>
> **Phase 4 question**: Does the project move toward implementation (pilots, training), scale geographically (other bioregions), deepen theoretically (governance specialization), or convert to practitioner-facing curriculum?

---

## Phase 3 Baseline — What Exists

Before assessing Phase 4 options, it helps to be precise about what Phase 3 produced and what gaps it leaves.

**Phase 3 produced** (across the `community/` and `phase-3/` directories):

- Five domain documents at community scale (100–1,000 people): governance/decision-making, food systems/supply chain, information infrastructure, security/defense, scaling pathways and governance transitions
- Three infrastructure deep-dives: emergency response infrastructure (ICS, radio nets, medical surge), infrastructure interdependency mapping (cascade pathways, intervention points), mutual aid networks (time banking, labor pooling, community exchange)
- 170+ citations across Zapatista autonomous communities, Rojava communes, Mondragon cooperatives, Hurricane Maria response, Christchurch earthquake recovery, Blue Lake Rancheria microgrid, Dunbar threshold research, Ostrom commons governance

**What Phase 3 does not cover** (the gaps Phase 4 can address):

- How to actually deliver this content to real communities (training, facilitation, workshops)
- What happens when the framework meets specific terrain — a rural MN township, a Pacific Northwest watershed, an Appalachian hollow
- The deeper legal, economic, and conflict-resolution layers that governance requires at scale
- A conversion from research to practitioner-usable curriculum format

Each Phase 4 option addresses one of these gaps.

## The Four Options

---

### Option A: Midwest Zone 5 Pilot Implementation

**Timeline**: 3–4 weeks  
**Effort**: High coordination, moderate research  
**Type**: Implementation-first

#### What This Is

Select 2–3 pilot communities in rural Minnesota, Wisconsin, or North Dakota. Develop a 200-hour community governance curriculum rooted in the Phase 3 frameworks. Conduct 2 in-person workshops (one per pilot site). Document lessons learned, barriers to adoption, and what the framework gets wrong at ground level. Produce a pilot report.

This is the transition from research project to deployment project. It tests the core assumption underlying Phase 1–3: that the frameworks are actionable by real communities in real conditions.

#### Scope Detail

**Pilot community selection criteria**: Population 100–400 people (within Phase 3's primary scale band). Rural Midwest geography (Zone 5 climate). Some existing social infrastructure (township government, church network, farm bureau, or similar). No requirement for pre-existing resilience interest — cold outreach is the harder and more useful test.

**200-hour curriculum structure**: The 200-hour figure maps to roughly one year of monthly 4-hour community meetings plus additional household implementation time. The curriculum is not a course — it is a structured implementation sequence: Month 1 skills census, Month 2–3 governance charter development, Month 4–5 mutual aid network setup, Month 6–8 infrastructure mapping, Month 9–12 tabletop exercises and drill. The curriculum document specifies the facilitator guide, agenda templates, and participant materials for each session.

**Two workshops**: One orientation workshop (half-day, 20–30 participants) introducing the framework and beginning the skills census. One follow-up workshop (full-day) reviewing draft governance charter and practicing tabletop scenario. Both can be facilitated by the project lead; no external expertise required.

**Pilot report**: 3,000–5,000 words. What happened, what the community adopted and rejected, which Phase 3 assumptions held and which broke, what would need to change in the framework to be more usable.

#### Timeline (3–4 weeks)

| Week | Work |
|---|---|
| Week 1 | Community identification and outreach (phone/email); Phase 3 framework review for curriculum gaps |
| Week 2 | Curriculum design — 200-hour implementation sequence, session guides, materials |
| Week 3 | Workshop 1 (orientation); document observations; revise curriculum based on feedback |
| Week 4 | Workshop 2 (governance charter + tabletop); produce pilot report |

#### Resource Requirements

- **Time**: 40–60 hours (curriculum development 20–25 hrs, workshops 8–10 hrs each including travel, documentation 10–15 hrs)
- **Travel**: 2 in-person visits to pilot sites (rural MN/WI/ND); estimate 2–4 hours drive from Twin Cities or similar Midwest hub
- **Materials**: Printed workbooks (10–20 copies per session), whiteboard or flipchart paper, laminated quick-reference cards drawn from Phase 3 docs
- **No specialized tools**: All materials producible from Phase 3 content already written

#### Feasibility Assessment

**High feasibility** if the project lead can commit 2 in-person days (travel + workshop time) within the 4-week window. Community identification is the highest-risk dependency — rural communities are not always responsive to cold outreach, and established relationships (extension office contact, farm bureau connection, existing acquaintance) significantly accelerate this. Without a warm entry point, expect Week 1 to stretch into Week 2.

**Risk**: The pilot tests the framework's real-world usability. If Phase 3 content is too abstract or jargon-heavy for a rural township audience, the pilot will reveal that — which is valuable, but means Phase 4 output becomes "framework needs revision" rather than "pilot confirmed." That is the honest risk of implementation-first approaches.

#### Impact Assessment vs. Systems-Resilience Goal

Option A directly advances the project's stated purpose — building a practical, actionable knowledge base for when systems fail. The pilot tests actionability against the hardest constraint: real people, real communities, real skepticism. A successful pilot produces the most valuable output of any Phase 4 option: evidence that the framework works outside the researcher's head. A failed pilot produces the second most valuable output: specific, documented evidence of what needs to change.

**Impact rating**: Highest long-term impact; highest short-term execution risk.

### Option B: Bioregion Expansion

**Timeline**: 6–8 weeks  
**Effort**: High research, moderate writing  
**Type**: Geographic scaling

#### What This Is

Adapt the Phase 3 frameworks to three additional US bioregions (Pacific Northwest, Great Lakes, Appalachian) and one international bioregion (Amazon basin or Southeast Asian archipelago). Each bioregion adaptation is a document set that preserves the Phase 3 structural logic while replacing Zone 5 Midwest specifics with bioregion-appropriate equivalents: climate, soil, water systems, native food species, governance traditions, and case studies.

#### Scope Detail

**Pacific Northwest adaptation**: Marine climate (USDA Zones 7–9 west of Cascades, 5–6 east). Water abundance vs. drought risk. Salmon as cultural and nutritional keystone. Tribal governance traditions (treaty rights, co-management) as Phase 3 governance input. Urban-rural split more pronounced than Midwest. Key difference from Midwest: longer growing season, higher rainfall, different food forests (Cascade Range species vs. prairie species).

**Great Lakes adaptation**: Closest to Zone 5 Midwest but distinct: freshwater access as dominant resilience asset, Great Lakes Compact governance as inter-jurisdictional model, ice fishing and lake-based food systems, Detroit urban agriculture case studies (relevant to community-scale food production). Overlaps most with existing Phase 3 content — adaptation cost is lowest of the three US bioregions.

**Appalachian adaptation**: Holler-scale community structure (50–200 people per holler), long tradition of mutual aid and informal governance, coal economy collapse as proxy for extended economic disruption, distinct food culture (Foxfire documentation as existing source), high-elevation cold-climate growing challenges. The Appalachian case is the most culturally distinct from Midwest and likely produces the most informative contrast.

**International bioregion (Amazon or Southeast Asian archipelago)**: Either the Brazilian Amazon (rubber tapper cooperative governance, quilombola communities, indigenous territorial management under deforestation pressure) or Indonesian/Filipino island communities (maritime mutual aid, typhoon resilience, community-scale food systems without refrigeration). The international case is the highest research burden but also the most intellectually generative — it tests which Phase 3 principles are universal and which are Zone 5 Midwest artifacts.

#### Timeline (6–8 weeks)

| Period | Work |
|---|---|
| Weeks 1–2 | Source research for Pacific Northwest + Great Lakes (bioregion-specific food species, governance models, case studies) |
| Weeks 3–4 | Pacific Northwest adaptation document; Great Lakes adaptation document |
| Weeks 5–6 | Appalachian research + adaptation document |
| Weeks 7–8 | International bioregion research + adaptation document; cross-bioregion synthesis (what's universal, what's regional) |

#### Resource Requirements

- **Time**: 80–120 hours (research-heavy; each bioregion requires 8–12 hours of source-finding before 10–15 hours of writing)
- **Research tools**: Web search, academic databases for bioregion-specific agricultural and governance literature; no in-person requirements
- **Output**: 4 adaptation documents (one per bioregion) + 1 cross-bioregion synthesis, estimated 40,000–60,000 words total
- **No travel required**; purely desk research and writing

#### Feasibility Assessment

**Moderate feasibility** as research work. The Pacific Northwest and Great Lakes adaptations are straightforward — strong existing literature, English-language sources, well-documented case studies. Appalachian adaptation requires sourcing Foxfire Series and regional governance literature but is also well-documented. The international bioregion is the high-risk component: quality English-language sources on Amazon indigenous governance and Southeast Asian community resilience exist but require more navigation.

**Risk**: Bioregion adaptation can drift toward superficial localization (swap out plant species, keep everything else the same) rather than genuine structural adaptation. The value is in finding where the frameworks genuinely break — where Pacific Northwest coastal communities need fundamentally different governance approaches, not just different food species lists.

#### Impact Assessment vs. Systems-Resilience Goal

Option B broadens the project's reach significantly but does not deepen its implementation. The Phase 3 framework's Zone 5 specificity is both its strength (highly applicable to Midwest communities) and its limitation (requires translation for other regions). Bioregion expansion makes the framework usable by a much wider audience and tests its universality.

**Impact rating**: High breadth impact; lower depth impact; no implementation test.

### Option C: Governance Deep-Dive

**Timeline**: 5–6 weeks  
**Effort**: High research and writing  
**Type**: Theoretical deepening

#### What This Is

Expand the Phase 3 governance findings into three specialized modules that go substantially further than the existing `phase-3/01-governance-decision-making.md` document. The three modules:

1. **Law and sovereignty for post-collapse governance** — what legal authority remains, what must be improvised, how communities establish legitimate governance without state backing
2. **Economic models** — mutual aid, gift economy, commons governance, time banking at community scale; what the Ostrom framework tells us about durable economic institutions
3. **Decision-making and conflict resolution at scale transitions** — what happens to governance structures as communities grow through the 150-person, 500-person, and 2,000-person thresholds; how conflict resolution must change at each threshold

#### Scope Detail

**Module 1: Law and Sovereignty**: The Phase 3 governance document identifies that formal authority structures matter above ~150 people. Module 1 goes deeper: what is the legal basis for community self-governance when state and federal systems are degraded? Midwest township government structure (Iowa, Minnesota, Wisconsin) as a pre-existing legal scaffold that survives most infrastructure collapse scenarios. Emergency powers statutes at state level. FEMA Continuity of Government requirements. The interface between informal community authority and surviving formal legal structures. International case studies: Zapatista autonomous governance and the Mexican state, Rojava's contested sovereignty, Christchurch community organizations and Canterbury Earthquake Recovery Commission friction.

**Module 2: Economic Models**: The Phase 3 mutual aid networks document covers time banking and reciprocal labor. Module 2 extends this to: Ostrom's 8 design principles applied to economic institutions (commons governance of food, water, energy, labor); gift economy theory (Marcel Mauss, David Graeber) and its practical limits under crisis conditions; local currency design (what works, what fails — the Berkshire example, Bristol Pound, Ithaca HOURS); cooperative economics under disruption (Mondragon's crisis-period performance data); rationing design (WWII UK and US evidence, 1970s gasoline rationing evidence, COVID-era food bank economics).

**Module 3: Decision-Making at Scale Transitions**: The Phase 3 scaling pathways document identifies the 150-person and 500-person thresholds but does not fully document what governance redesign they require. Module 3 is the detailed implementation guide for those transitions: how to move from assembly-based to delegate council-based governance without governance failure; what conflict resolution tools work at each scale; how decision speed and decision quality trade off as scale increases; specific facilitation methods for contentious community decisions (sociocracy, consent-based decision-making, modified consensus).

#### Timeline (5–6 weeks)

| Period | Work |
|---|---|
| Weeks 1–2 | Research pass on legal/sovereignty literature (Midwest township law, FEMA COG, Zapatista/Rojava legal status); Module 1 writing |
| Weeks 3–4 | Research on economic models (Ostrom, gift economy, local currency evidence); Module 2 writing |
| Week 5 | Research on scale transition decision-making; Module 3 writing |
| Week 6 | Integration, cross-references to Phase 3, editorial review |

#### Resource Requirements

- **Time**: 70–90 hours (research-heavy; legal literature and economic theory sources require careful navigation)
- **Specialized source domains**: Ostrom's Governing the Commons and Nobel lecture; Graeber's Debt; WWII rationing economics literature; Midwest state emergency powers statutes; sociocracy and consent-based governance facilitator guides
- **Output**: 3 specialized modules, estimated 24,000–30,000 words total, 80–100 citations
- **No travel, no coordination burden**; purely desk research and writing

#### Feasibility Assessment

**High feasibility** as research and writing work. The source literature is well-established and English-language. Legal research (Midwest township law) has some complexity but is navigable via state legislative websites and FEMA documentation. The main risk is scope creep — each module could expand indefinitely. Strict word-count discipline (8,000–10,000 words per module) and clear exclusion criteria ("this goes into Phase 5 governance") are required.

**Risk**: The governance deep-dive produces content that is theoretically rich but may be less immediately actionable than Options A or D. The audience for Module 1 (law and sovereignty) is relatively narrow — most communities will not need to grapple with legal authority questions during a 3–24 month disruption. Module 2 and Module 3 have broader utility.

#### Impact Assessment vs. Systems-Resilience Goal

Option C deepens the most theoretically underdeveloped area of Phase 3. The governance documents in Phase 3 are strong on case studies and structural models but relatively thin on the legal and economic dimensions that matter when governance must function without state backing. This option makes the framework significantly more robust for long-duration or high-severity disruption scenarios (2+ years, state capacity degraded).

**Impact rating**: High theoretical depth; moderate practitioner utility; no implementation test.

### Option D: Curriculum and Training Materials

**Timeline**: 4–5 weeks  
**Effort**: High conversion/design work, moderate research  
**Type**: Practitioner conversion

#### What This Is

Convert Phase 3 research into train-the-trainer modules and workshops: facilitator guides, slide decks, participant workbooks, and a community assessment template. The goal is to make Phase 3 content usable by practitioners who are not the original researcher — a community organizer who did not read all 100,000 words of Phase 1–3 but needs to run a governance workshop next Saturday.

This is distinct from Option A (which pilots the framework with specific communities) in that Option D produces reusable materials that any competent facilitator can deploy, rather than a one-time implementation test.

#### Scope Detail

**Train-the-trainer modules** (one per Phase 3 domain): Each module is a self-contained facilitator package covering one Phase 3 domain. Module 1: Community governance structures. Module 2: Food system resilience. Module 3: Information infrastructure and communications. Module 4: Security and mutual defense. Module 5: Scaling pathways and governance transitions. Each module includes: facilitator guide (how to run the session, common questions, facilitation traps), slide deck (15–20 slides per module), participant workbook (exercises, worksheets, reference tables).

**Community assessment template**: A structured 2–3 hour community self-assessment that any facilitator can run with a group of 10–30 community members. Produces: baseline skills census, infrastructure vulnerability map, governance structure audit, and prioritized action plan. This is the entry point for any community that has not yet started — the assessment identifies where to begin.

**Workshop design** (3 workshops): An introductory half-day workshop (3 hours) for community members with no prior resilience planning background. A full-day governance workshop (6 hours) focused on charter development, skills census, and mutual aid network setup. A tabletop exercise workshop (4 hours) running the three Midwest scenarios from `community/01-emergency-response-infrastructure.md` (ice storm, derecho, extended grid failure).

**Facilitator guide**: A standalone document for anyone running these workshops — how to recruit participants, set up the space, handle conflict during workshops, adapt materials to different community contexts, and document outcomes. Written for practitioners, not researchers.

#### Timeline (4–5 weeks)

| Period | Work |
|---|---|
| Week 1 | Community assessment template design; facilitator guide structure |
| Weeks 2–3 | Train-the-trainer modules (5 modules × 2–3 days each); slide decks |
| Week 4 | Participant workbooks; introductory workshop design |
| Week 5 | Governance workshop + tabletop exercise design; final review and formatting |

#### Resource Requirements

- **Time**: 60–80 hours (conversion and design work is intensive but draws on existing Phase 3 content; no new research required)
- **Design tools**: Basic slide software (PowerPoint, Google Slides, or Markdown-to-presentation conversion); word processor for workbooks
- **Output**: 5 facilitator modules, 5 slide decks, 5 participant workbooks, 3 workshop designs, 1 community assessment template, 1 master facilitator guide — estimated 30,000–40,000 words of practitioner-facing content plus visual materials
- **No travel, no new research**; conversion of existing content into new format

#### Feasibility Assessment

**Highest feasibility** of all four options. No travel, no new research required, no coordination burden. All source material already exists in Phase 3. The work is conversion — taking dense research documents and restructuring them into practitioner-friendly formats. The main risk is that the conversion process is more time-consuming than estimated; research documents resist simple reformatting and require genuine restructuring to be usable by a non-specialist facilitator.

**Risk**: Materials produced without pilot testing may not work as designed. A facilitator guide written by the researcher who knows Phase 3 deeply may assume background knowledge that a community organizer does not have. Option A (pilot) would generate the empirical feedback needed to validate the Option D materials. Option D without Option A produces theoretically complete but field-untested training materials.

#### Impact Assessment vs. Systems-Resilience Goal

Option D has the highest multiplier effect: instead of one researcher implementing the framework in one community, it enables dozens of facilitators to implement it in dozens of communities. This is the highest-leverage output if the goal is broad adoption of the framework. The Phase 3 content is research-grade; the training gap is real and large.

**Impact rating**: Highest potential reach; depends on external facilitators for actual impact; benefits significantly from Option A pilot validation first.

## Parallel Potential

Options A and D are complementary and can run partially in parallel:
- Option D curriculum design (Weeks 1–3) can begin before the Option A pilot (Weeks 3–4), using Week 1–2 for curriculum design and Week 3–4 for pilot.
- The pilot then validates and revises the curriculum before final production.
- Combined timeline: 5–6 weeks, with Option A providing field validation of Option D materials.

Options B and C are independent and can run sequentially (B then C) for a comprehensive 12–14 week Phase 4 covering geographic expansion plus governance depth. They have no parallel potential between themselves — both are full research and writing efforts.

Options A+D parallel execution is the most strategically coherent combination: implementation test + practitioner materials, mutually validating.

## Decision Framework

### Choose Option A if:

You have warm relationships with 1–3 rural Midwest communities and can commit 2 in-person days within a 4-week window. The priority is testing whether Phase 3 frameworks work in practice, not producing more research. You are willing to have the pilot reveal that some Phase 3 content needs revision.

### Choose Option B if:

The priority is making the framework usable by communities outside Zone 5 Midwest. The project's geographic scope should expand. You are comfortable with 6–8 weeks of research-heavy work without implementation feedback.

### Choose Option C if:

The priority is theoretical completeness — filling the governance gaps in Phase 3 before any deployment. The framework will face scenarios (2+ year disruption, degraded state capacity, contested legal authority) that require the deeper treatment. You are comfortable with research-and-writing work that may have narrower immediate audience than other options.

### Choose Option D if:

The priority is enabling other people to deliver the content. You want practitioners — community organizers, extension agents, emergency managers — to be able to run workshops without reading 100,000 words of Phase 1–3 research. You want the highest multiplier on Phase 3 investment.

### Choose A+D (parallel) if:

You want both implementation validation and practitioner materials, and can commit 5–6 weeks across both tracks simultaneously.
