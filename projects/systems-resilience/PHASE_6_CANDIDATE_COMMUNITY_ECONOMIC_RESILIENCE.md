# Phase 6 Candidate: Community Economic Resilience

**One-sentence definition**: How communities trade goods, store value, allocate capital, and coordinate economic activity when conventional money, banks, and supply chains fail.

**Domain ID**: Domain 64 (Community Economic Resilience)

---

## Research Scope

This domain answers three bounded questions:

**1. What economic architectures function when the dollar fails?**
Local currencies, mutual credit systems, and barter protocols that operate without central banking. Case studies include the WIR mutual credit system (Switzerland, 70+ years operational), Bristol Pounds (UK, 2009-2022), and timebanking networks in Japan and Vermont. Design principles for currency systems in communities of 200-10,000 people.

**2. How do communities allocate capital and coordinate production without market signals?**
Gift economies, commons-based resource allocation, and Elinor Ostrom's design principles for common-pool resources. Enterprise models: cooperatives, land trusts, mutual aid networks. Decision-making structures that replace price signals — consensus, participatory budgeting, ranked preference voting. Midwest case studies: agricultural cooperatives, tool-share networks, local food enterprises.

**3. What economic bridges prevent community isolation?**
Inter-community trade networks and federation models. Specialized production (comparative advantage without global supply chains) and barter protocols for non-monetary trade. Regional economic integration: how 5-10 communities coordinate surplus exchange (rural grain, urban manufacturing).

**Out of scope**: National macroeconomic policy, Federal Reserve dynamics, corporate finance structures, ancient barter history without 21st-century application.

## Cross-Domain Bridges

**Extends Phase 1-5 in the following ways:**

- **Phase 3 (Governance)**: Phase 3 documented decision-making structures. This domain adds the economic governance layer — how communities allocate resources and determine exchange value through those structures.
- **Phase 5 Wave 2 (Community Playbook + Conflict Resolution)**: Economic systems prevent the resource-allocation conflicts Phase 5 describes. Clear economic rules reduce conflict frequency; Phase 5 conflict tools handle what economic rules don't.
- **Phase 4 (Power Mapping)**: Phase 4 mapped political power. This domain extends it to economic power — how capital accumulates in small communities and how cooperative structures prevent concentration.
- **Phase 5 Wave 3 (Food Preservation, Water Systems, Fuel Production)**: Phase 5 produces outputs (food, water, fuel). This domain answers: how do community members exchange those outputs equitably when conventional currency is unavailable?

**Feeds forward to**:

- Domain 65 (Governance Scaling): Economic structures shape what governance models are feasible (economically fragile systems need different governance than economically stable ones).

## Unique Contribution

**What this adds that Phases 1-5 do not contain:**

Phase 1-5 documents are production-oriented: how to filter water, grow food, generate power, manage psychological health, resolve conflict. Every domain produces something. None of them addresses exchange. A community that grows food and generates power still faces a critical question: how does the farmer compensate the electrician? How does the medic get food if they spend all day on patient care?

This domain fills the gap between production and distribution. It is the economic operating system that makes specialized labor sustainable. Without it, communities default to barter inefficiency or interpersonal debt networks that degrade cohesion over time.

**Market gap**: The resilience literature has extensive food/water/energy coverage and thin coverage of economic coordination systems. The academic literature on complementary currencies and commons governance is rich but not synthesized for Zone 5 rural/suburban contexts. This document does that synthesis.

**Midwest specificity**: The region has a strong cooperative tradition (agricultural cooperatives, credit unions, tool libraries). Demand for local economic system guidance is active, not hypothetical. USDA extension offices are already distributing local food system economics guides.
