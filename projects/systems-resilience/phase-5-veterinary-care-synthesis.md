# Veterinary Care Integration: Household and Community-Scale Crisis Response

> **Most Important Finding**: Veterinary care resilience is a systems problem, not a stockpiling problem. The households and communities that survive extended disruption with intact animal populations are those that built multi-tier coordination structures before the crisis — not those with the largest medication inventories. Every decision tree in this document points back to the same structural prerequisite: the relationship network (veterinarian, community coordinator, neighbor with training) must be in place before it is needed.

---

## Section A: Household-Scale Veterinary Care

### A1. Pet Medical Supply Chains

The household veterinary supply cache functions at two layers: what can be obtained and stored without professional involvement, and what requires a pre-established veterinary relationship. Since FDA Guidance for Industry No. 263 (GFI 263) took effect June 11, 2023, the dividing line is sharper than most household preparedness guides reflect.

**OTC-accessible household baseline (6-month minimum, updated for GFI 263 compliance)**

| Supply Category | Items | Approximate 6-Month Cost | 12-Month Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| Wound care | Betadine (4oz), Vetrap rolls (4), sterile gauze pads (25), non-stick dressings (10) | $35 | $60 |
| Infection prevention | Chlorhexidine 2% solution (16oz), hydrogen peroxide (2 quarts) | $20 | $35 |
| Parasites | Species-appropriate flea/tick prevention, Capstar (nitenpyram, OTC for dogs and cats) | $60–120 | $110–200 |
| Thermometers | Digital rectal thermometer (2) | $20 | $20 |

| Reference | Merck Veterinary Manual (physical copy) or species-specific guide | $90 | $90 |
| **Totals** | | **$297–357** | **$442–532** |

**Prescription-required items (require veterinary relationship established before disruption)**

For dogs and cats, the following medication categories require a valid veterinary prescription and an established veterinarian-client-patient relationship (VCPR):

- Pain management (meloxicam for dogs, meloxicam and buprenorphine for cats) — no safe OTC equivalent exists; human NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen, acetaminophen) are toxic to both dogs and cats
- Antibiotics (amoxicillin, doxycycline, metronidazole) — accessible through established VCPR; many veterinarians will prescribe for household stockpile with written protocol
- Anti-anxiety/sedation medications (trazodone, gabapentin for anxious animals in disruption scenarios) — prescription only
- Ophthalmic and otic preparations

**Cross-species medication overlap (with critical limitations)**

Several medications have legitimate cross-use between dogs, cats, and humans, but the overlap requires precision:

- **Amoxicillin**: Standard human-dose oral amoxicillin is pharmacologically identical to veterinary formulations. Dosing must be species-specific; a 50 lb dog requires a different dose than a 10 lb cat. Any cross-use requires documented veterinary guidance in the household protocol notebook.
- **Doxycycline**: Used in humans, dogs, and some other species. Cats tolerate doxycycline but require specific dosing and formulation (tablets, not capsules — esophageal stricture risk). Q fever (a Zone 5 livestock-associated zoonosis from goat/cattle births) is treated with doxycycline in both humans and infected animals.
- **Diphenhydramine (Benadryl)**: OTC antihistamine with species-appropriate dosing for dogs (not cats without veterinary guidance). Used for allergic reactions and mild sedation.
- **Metronidazole**: Veterinary and human formulations are equivalent. Useful for gastrointestinal infections. Prescription-only for veterinary use.

**Capacity limits: What household owners can manage safely**

The household veterinary capacity threshold maps to three zones:

*Green zone (household-managed):* Minor wounds without deep tissue involvement, external parasite management, supportive care for self-limiting gastrointestinal illness, monitoring and documentation, end-of-life comfort care when the HHHHHMM scale indicates acceptable quality of life, preventive care (vaccinations OTC for dogs — but note rabies must be veterinarian-administered in most states).

*Yellow zone (veterinary consultation required, telemedicine viable if VCPR established):* Wound infection with early signs (increasing redness, warmth, minor discharge), behavioral changes without obvious cause, failure to eat for 24+ hours without other symptoms, minor lameness without trauma history, chronic condition monitoring adjustments.

*Red zone (immediate professional intervention, transport priority):* Urinary obstruction in male cats — complete blockage causes death within 24–72 hours, no reliable home intervention. Respiratory distress (open-mouth breathing in cats, extreme labored breathing in any species). Suspected poisoning. Seizures lasting more than 3–5 minutes. Active hemorrhage from deep wound. Suspected gastric dilatation-volvulus (GDV/bloat) in deep-chested dogs. Any animal with blue-tinged gums (cyanosis — indicates severe oxygen deprivation).

**Supply sourcing: Where household veterinary supplies come from**

Under normal conditions: veterinary clinics (highest selection, professional guidance), feed stores (Tractor Supply, Rural King — strong OTC selection, limited prescription assistance), online distributors (Chewy, Valley Vet, KV Supply — prescription processing available for established accounts, 2–5 day shipping), and pet specialty retailers.

Under disruption: feed stores show the highest supply resilience compared to veterinary clinics (which may close when staff cannot travel) and online distributors (which require functioning logistics infrastructure). A preparedness household should source OTC supplies from feed stores during normal conditions to establish the supplier relationship, and should request that the veterinarian send any standing prescriptions to local feed store pharmacies rather than only dispensing from the clinic.

### A2. Integration with Household Medical Infrastructure

The Phase 5 household healthcare module covers human medical supply caching and first-response protocols. The veterinary supply cache overlaps substantially — this section identifies the genuine integration points versus the distinct veterinary requirements.

**Equipment that serves both human and animal care:**

- Suture kits and suture material: direct cross-use. Absorbable (Vicryl) and non-absorbable (nylon, polypropylene) sutures serve both. Technique differs by tissue type, not species. Needle type and gauge vary by wound size.
- Digital thermometers: same equipment, rectal measurement for animals (normal ranges documented in household veterinary notebook by species).
- Wound closure strips (Steri-Strips): cross-use without modification.
- Elastic bandaging (Coban/Vetrap for animals, ACE wrap for humans): similar function, different products preferred by species due to hair coat.
- Saline solution: identical product, cross-use without modification for wound irrigation.
- Isopropyl alcohol: identical product for surface sterilization of equipment.
- Syringes and needles: cross-use for medication administration; note that needle gauge varies by use (20–22g for SC injection in cats, 18–20g for IM injection in medium dogs).

**Training that transfers from human medical skills to animal care:**

The household healthcare training pipeline (CPR, wound care, basic assessment) transfers to animal care with species-specific modification:

- Vital sign assessment (pulse, respiratory rate, temperature): same conceptual framework, different normal values and measurement locations
- Wound assessment and wound care: identical wound classification and care principles; the difference is hair coat management and species-specific infection flora
- Oral rehydration: same principles; electrolyte formulas differ by species but the physiology is identical
- Documentation and monitoring: identical skills; the household veterinary notebook mirrors the human medical log

**What does NOT transfer:**

- CPR technique: the mechanics differ substantially by species and body size. Human CPR training does not prepare a household member for canine or feline CPR. This requires species-specific training.
- Medication dosing: human medication doses are body-weight-calibrated but species physiology differs. Never apply human dosing tables to animals without species-specific verification.
- Surgical anesthesia: no component of human first-aid training prepares a household member for managing animal surgical procedures beyond superficial wound closure under local anesthetic.

### A4. Alternative Emergency Techniques and Supportive Care

**Ethnoveterinary practices with documented evidence base**

The research literature on ethnoveterinary medicine (PMC8228004, 2021 systematic review across Sub-Saharan Africa) confirms that traditional plant-based practices function primarily as: anti-parasite treatments, wound antiseptics, and supportive digestive aids. Several practices have received pharmacological validation:

- **Garlic (Allium sativum)** preparations: Documented ectoparasitic effects in Ethiopian pastoral practice; antimicrobial and antiparasitic properties confirmed in laboratory studies. Application: topical preparations for mange, surface wound antisepsis. Dose and formulation poorly standardized — use as adjunct to, not replacement for, evidence-based care.
- **Cucurbita pepo seeds (pumpkin seeds)**: Active compounds (cucurbitin) show anthelmintic (deworming) activity against tapeworms in multiple animal species. Not effective for roundworms or barber pole worm; cannot replace FAMACHA-guided anthelmintic protocols in Zone 5. Can supplement deworming in resource-constrained settings.
- **Aloe vera**: Wound healing properties (acemannan component) documented in peer-reviewed studies. Direct application of fresh aloe gel to superficial wounds is an evidence-supported intervention for wound soothing and moisture retention.
- **Honey (medical-grade Manuka or raw)**: Documented antimicrobial properties, particularly against Staphylococcus and Streptococcus species. Applied to wounds when standard antiseptics are exhausted. Significant limitation: not appropriate for wounds with significant dead tissue or deep-tissue infection.

**Confidence assessment on ethnoveterinary practices**: Moderate. The documentation of specific plants and preparations is extensive; the pharmacological validation is limited. These practices represent useful knowledge under genuine resource constraints, not equivalent alternatives to evidence-based veterinary care.

**Supportive care protocols (applicable without medications)**

Supportive care is undervalued in household preparedness planning. A significant fraction of animal illness is self-limiting if the animal is supported through the acute phase:

- **Hydration protocol**: Animals that stop drinking require intervention. Oral fluids via syringe (for cats and small dogs) or offered in small amounts frequently. Warm (not hot) electrolyte solution is more palatable than plain water and provides electrolyte replacement for vomiting/diarrhea cases. A dehydrated animal (skin tent test: gently pinch skin between shoulder blades — if it stands up rather than returning immediately, dehydration is present) that receives adequate fluids often recovers without other intervention.
- **Temperature management**: Febrile animals need cool (not cold) water and air circulation; hypothermic animals need warmth. These are frequently overlooked as treatment modalities.
- **Nutrition during illness**: Animals that won't eat may eat warmed food or high-palatability foods (rotisserie chicken, warm broth). Maintaining caloric intake through acute illness dramatically improves outcome.
- **Stress reduction**: Isolation from other animals, familiar bedding, reduced handling, familiar human presence. Stress hormones impair immune function; reducing environmental stressors has measurable treatment value.

**Euthanasia protocols (humane end-of-life in no-professional-vet scenarios)**

This section is handled with the gravity it requires. The AVMA Guidelines for the Euthanasia of Animals (2020 edition) define acceptable methods. For companion animals:

The honest assessment is that there is no household-accessible method for companion animal euthanasia that fully replicates the quality of pentobarbital overdose administered by a trained veterinarian. Owners facing this decision under genuine resource constraints have two paths: natural death under maximum supportive and palliative care, or the methods the AVMA accepts when controlled substances are unavailable (gunshot to the brainstem — acceptable only when genuinely no other option exists, requires firearm handling competence and anatomical knowledge).

The HHHHHMM Quality of Life Scale (Dr. Alice Villalobos, 2004) provides the assessment framework. Scores consistently below 35 (on a 70-point scale across seven domains: Hurt, Hunger, Hydration, Hygiene, Happiness, Mobility, More good days than bad) indicate quality of life is not sustainable. This is a decision tool for household members, not a trigger for automatic action — it provides language and structure for a difficult judgment.

For livestock euthanasia, penetrating captive bolt (pneumatic or powder-actuated, $50–300 from farm supply or online) and gunshot with correct species-specific anatomical placement are the household-accessible methods. AVMA Guidelines provide species-specific placement diagrams.

## Section B: Community-Scale Veterinary Care

### B1. Livestock Triage and Large-Animal Emergency Management

**Zone 5 livestock baseline (Illinois, Michigan, Iowa, Indiana, Wisconsin)**

USDA NASS 2022 Census of Agriculture data for Zone 5 core states documents the scale of the livestock population that requires veterinary care under normal conditions and that will require community-coordinated care under disruption:

- Iowa: 3.7 million cattle/calves, 23.6 million hogs/pigs (the highest single-state hog inventory in the U.S.), 215,000 sheep/lambs
- Illinois: 960,000 cattle, 4.2 million hogs
- Indiana: 820,000 cattle, 3.9 million hogs
- Michigan: 710,000 cattle, 830,000 hogs
- Wisconsin: 3.1 million cattle (primarily dairy — Wisconsin is the second-largest dairy state)

This population is served by a food-animal veterinary workforce that prior research (SYSTEMS_RESILIENCE_VETERINARY_CARE_RESEARCH.md) characterized precisely: Iowa's shortage region IA257 alone covers nine counties with 786,400+ cattle and 4.29 million swine served by only 60 food animal veterinarians — a ratio that already creates multi-week wait times for non-emergency care. This is the baseline; any disruption worsens it.

**High-frequency large-animal emergencies: What requires community response capability**

The four emergencies that constitute the majority of large-animal veterinary calls in Zone 5 and that are potentially manageable at the farm with training:

*Dystocia (difficult birth)*: Cattle, sheep, and goat dystocia is the single most common veterinary emergency on Zone 5 farms. In cattle, the intervention window is approximately 2 hours of active labor without progress before irreversible maternal fatigue and fetal compromise begin. The safe intervention techniques (vaginal examination, repositioning, gentle traction with obstetric chains) are learnable by trained lay practitioners. The critical constraints: any presentation where the calf cannot be repositioned within 30 minutes of skilled manual manipulation requires veterinary intervention (uterine torsion, ringwomb, or fetomaternal mismatch require surgical or pharmacological intervention unavailable to lay practitioners). Pulling a calf requires obstetric lubricant (OB lube, OTC), obstetric chains or a calf puller ($30–150), and trained hands. Equipment cost is low; training is the constraint.

*Bloat (ruminal tympany) in cattle and small ruminants*: Free gas bloat in cattle can be relieved by stomach tube (14–16mm nasogastric tube) within minutes in the hands of a trained farmer. Frothy bloat requires anti-foaming agents (vegetable oil or poloxalene via stomach tube — CRITICAL SAFETY NOTE: never drench oil without a stomach tube; aspiration causes fatal lipid pneumonia). Trocar insertion in the left paralumbar fossa as a last resort is a learnable emergency procedure for cattle; the technique is documented in extension publications and Pet Emergency Academy coursework.

*Choke (esophageal obstruction)*: Common in cattle that ingest root vegetables or other objects. Signs: profuse salivation, neck extension, inability to eructate (belching). If the obstruction is recent and soft, passage of a large-bore stomach tube will often dislodge it. If not, veterinary intervention is required. Duration matters: obstruction for 4+ hours causes severe esophageal damage and reduces the chance of successful non-surgical resolution.

*Foot rot*: A bacterial infection (Fusobacterium necrophorum and Bacteroides melaninogenicus) causing acute lameness and swelling between the claws. Highly responsive to antibiotics if treated early. Prior to GFI 263, this was manageable with OTC oxytetracycline; since June 2023, antibiotic treatment requires a veterinary prescription. A community with a shared protocol and pre-positioned prescription antibiotics (obtained through a community VCPR with a large-animal veterinarian) can treat foot rot within hours; without that infrastructure, the animal goes untreated until a veterinarian is reached.

**When to cull rather than treat**

The culling decision is both practical (resource allocation) and ethical (animal welfare). The framework:

- Any animal that cannot rise, has not responded to 24–48 hours of appropriate treatment, and shows progressive deterioration is a culling candidate.
- Any animal with a condition requiring surgery or specialist intervention that is genuinely inaccessible: humanitarian culling is preferable to untreated suffering.
- Any animal with a systemic infection so advanced that treatment would require sustained intensive care unavailable at community scale.
- The food-safety rule: culled animals with localized injury (broken leg, reproductive complication) may yield safe meat if processed promptly. Animals with systemic infection should not be consumed.

**Supply logistics for large-animal emergencies**

A community-scale supply hub for large-animal care requires items that individual households will rarely own but that serve multiple households when shared:

| Item | Function | Cost | Sharing factor |
|---|---|---|---|
| Calf puller (OB come-along) | Cattle/large animal dystocia | $120–200 | 1 per 10–20 cattle farms |
| OB chains (2–4 pair) | Dystocia traction | $15–25/pair | 1 set per 5–10 farms |
| Large-bore stomach tube (14–16mm, 10 ft) | Cattle bloat | $20–40 | 1 per 5–10 farms |
| Obstetric lubricant (gallon) | Dystocia | $30–50 | 1 gallon per 5–10 births |

### B2. Community-Scale Organization

**The multi-tier care model**

Community veterinary care under disruption follows a three-tier structure that mirrors the East African Community Animal Health Worker (CAHW) framework, adapted for Zone 5 conditions and the specific regulatory environment documented in the Phase 5 research base.

**Tier 1: Individual household self-care**

Every household with livestock maintains: species-appropriate vaccination protocols (OTC-accessible), basic wound care supplies, oral electrolytes, a thermometer, and the household veterinary notebook with emergency contact cascade. This is the content of the Phase 5 tier-2 household veterinary guide. At this tier, the household manages: routine vaccinations, minor wounds, parasite monitoring (FAMACHA for small ruminants), basic supportive care, and early-stage dehydration management.

**Tier 2: Community coordinator**

One trained person per 8–15 livestock-keeping households, functioning as the community veterinary coordinator. This person has:
- Completed a structured training course (Pet Emergency Academy Livestock First Aid certification, approximately 8–12 hours, covers most required skills at this tier; ideally supplemented by a 2-day in-person workshop with a large-animal extension veterinarian)
- Maintains the community supply hub (shared obstetric equipment, a shared refrigerated medication cache if standing prescriptions are available, reference texts)
- Has an established VCPR with the nearest large-animal veterinarian, and has discussed phone consultation protocols in advance
- Performs the triage function: receives calls from Tier 1 households, assesses whether a condition is Tier 1 manageable, Tier 2 treatable, or Tier 3 specialist-required
- Documents all animal health events in a community log (supports pattern recognition — if three farms have respiratory disease in the same week, a disease event may be in progress)

This is structurally equivalent to the CAHW model's most important design principle: "knowing when to refer is the most important single skill." The Tier 2 coordinator is not replacing a veterinarian; they are functioning as the triage and referral layer.

**Tier 3: Specialist (when available)**

A retired veterinarian, large-animal extension specialist, veterinary school field services team, or veterinarian reachable by phone who can: authorize prescriptions for the community cache, provide phone consultation for complex cases, and make periodic farm visits under normal conditions to maintain VCPR for the community coordinator's household accounts.

Zone 5 institutional resources at this tier: Iowa State University Lloyd Veterinary Medical Center operates four mobile veterinary trucks with eight full-time clinicians serving rural clients across Iowa. Purdue University College of Veterinary Medicine, University of Illinois CVM, Michigan State University CVM, and University of Wisconsin-Madison all operate large-animal field services with rural extension components.

**Mutual aid ratios**

From the East African CAHW evidence base (ODI Working Paper 214, Kenya CAHW), the sustainable coverage ratio is approximately 1 trained community animal health worker per 200–400 livestock owners in dispersed pastoral settings. In the denser Zone 5 rural context with better road infrastructure, 1 trained Tier 2 coordinator per 8–15 households is both achievable and sustainable. At this ratio, a community of 60 households with livestock requires 4–8 trained coordinators — enough to provide redundancy (a critical design requirement; the single-coordinator model is a single point of failure).

**Supply distribution: Community pharmacy model**

The community supply hub functions analogously to the community medical supply system described in Phase 5 household coordination documents. Key design requirements:

- Physical location: a secured outbuilding at the coordinator's farm, or a dedicated space at a community facility (church, feed cooperative, community center)
- Refrigeration for temperature-sensitive items (vaccines — many require 35–45°F storage; some injectable medications)
- Inventory tracking (a physical log, not digital-only): item, quantity, expiration date, last audit date
- Resupply protocol: designated person tracks inventory thresholds and contacts supply sources during normal conditions to maintain adequate stock
- Distribution log: records which household received which items and when (essential for prescription medication tracking and for identifying disease patterns)

The per-household cost of this shared infrastructure is dramatically lower than individual household stockpiling. Obstetric equipment that would cost $200+ per household costs $15–20 per household when amortized across 15 households.

### B3. Critical Supply Chain Vulnerabilities

**Antibiotic access post-GFI 263**

The single most significant supply chain vulnerability for community livestock care is the prescription requirement for all medically important antibiotics since June 2023. The community implications extend beyond the household model because: (1) the community coordinator who establishes a VCPR can potentially receive standing prescriptions covering multiple community households' animals; (2) this requires advance legal and logistical work — not all veterinarians will authorize multi-household prescription protocols; (3) the alternative (individual VCPR for every livestock household) requires each farm to have its own established veterinary relationship.

The 2020–2022 penicillin shortage (19% drop in U.S. penicillin sales in 2021, driven by supply chain disruption from a Chinese active pharmaceutical ingredient supplier experiencing quality failures at the same time pandemic demand diverted shared ingredient base to human amoxicillin production) demonstrated that supply chain disruption and regulatory transition can compound multiplicatively. A community with 30-day stockpiles of prescription antibiotics obtained through advance VCPR work is dramatically more resilient than one that must locate and re-supply during a crisis.

**Obstetric emergency tools**

Low-tech but life-critical: OB chains, come-alongs, lubricant, and gloves are the difference between a successful farm delivery and a lost breeding animal. These items are available from farm supply stores, feed cooperatives, and online distributors. Under extended logistics disruption, local feed stores show consistently higher resilience than online distributors — this is where community supply hubs should source.

**Zoonotic disease management as a community decision**

Brucellosis, Q fever, and leptospirosis create public health risks that extend beyond individual household animal care into community health planning. In a collapse scenario where professional public health infrastructure is reduced:

- *Brucellosis* (cattle and goats): Causes spontaneous abortion in animals and undulant fever (relapsing fever, severe) in humans. Transmission through contact with birth fluids, aborted fetuses, or unpasteurized milk. Community response: isolate affected animals, exclude from milking herd, do not handle birth products without gloves and masks, pasteurize all milk.
- *Leptospirosis* (cattle, pigs, rodents): Shed in urine; human exposure through skin contact with contaminated water or soil. Causes fever, muscle pain, jaundice, and potentially kidney/liver failure. Community response: rodent control (reduced rodent population reduces leptospirosis reservoir), avoid standing water in livestock areas, protective footwear when working in muddy livestock environments.
- *Q fever* (goats, cattle, sheep): Highest risk during birthing season (Zone 5: February–April). The pathogen (Coxiella burnetii) remains infectious in dried birth materials for months. Community response: designated attendance at births with appropriate PPE (gloves, N95 or equivalent mask, eye protection), exclusion of pregnant household members from birthing areas.

### B4. Integration with Zone 5 Food Systems

The connection between livestock health and community food security is most acute at the breeding stock level. The Phase 3 food systems document (02-food-systems-supply-chain.md) covers the structural fragility of the Zone 5 food supply chain. The veterinary dimension adds a specific vulnerability: breeding stock loss cascades across 3–5 years of recovery time.

**Why breeding stock matters more than production animals**

A finished steer or fattening hog lost to disease is a food loss — measurable in pounds of protein and months of recovery. A breeding cow or breeding doe lost to disease is a 3–5 year setback: the animal must be replaced, the replacement must reach reproductive maturity, and the herd must rebuild from the reduced base. In a prolonged disruption where replacement animals are unavailable (no markets, no transport), breeding stock loss is functionally irreversible within the planning horizon of most resilience frameworks.

**Community allocation framework: Breeding preservation vs. immediate protein**

This is the decision that communities struggle with most explicitly in historical case studies. The framework:

- Any animal with a condition that can be treated at community Tier 2 capability level: treat, prioritize preservation
- Breeding animals that are terminally ill but not infectious: emergency harvest for protein before the animal deteriorates further
- Breeding animals with infectious conditions (brucellosis, confirmed salmonellosis, suspected leptospirosis): isolation, assessment for treatment potential, cull if treatment not feasible; do not consume if systemic infection is present
- Surplus production animals (non-breeding): the community allocation decision between continued production vs. immediate harvest is a governance decision (see Phase 3/01-governance-decision-making.md) that must be made with information about feed supply availability and the likely duration of disruption

**Seasonal windows: Spring calving and kidding**

Zone 5's highest veterinary risk period (January–April) coincides with the lowest professional veterinary availability under disruption scenarios. Winter road conditions reduce vet travel capacity precisely when calving and kidding create maximum demand. This is the structural argument for pre-positioning the Tier 2 community coordinator with training and obstetric supplies before January of any year — and for ensuring the community VCPR is established with a phone consultation protocol before birthing season begins.

### B5. H5N1 and Pandemic Zoonosis Risk

**The 2024 H5N1 dairy cattle outbreak as a crisis model**

The March 2024 HPAI H5N1 detection in U.S. dairy cattle (first-ever confirmed detection in this species) and its subsequent spread to 19 states by end of 2024 — including Iowa, Michigan, Minnesota, Ohio, and Wisconsin across the Zone 5 corridor — is the most important recent case study in livestock disease emergency management. It exposed three specific vulnerabilities directly relevant to community resilience planning:

*Surveillance gap*: The outbreak circulated in U.S. dairy cattle for weeks or months before detection in March 2024. In geographies where large-animal veterinary coverage is thinnest — the same shortage areas documented throughout this research — there were insufficient practitioners conducting routine herd visits to notice the emerging pattern. Early detection of zoonotic disease emergence requires regular professional presence in herds. At the community scale, the Tier 2 coordinator's documentation function (tracking animal health events, sharing weekly status with community health network) is a partial substitute for professional surveillance.

*Human infection rate*: 41 confirmed human infections through end of 2024 in farm workers and veterinary practitioners. A CDC serosurvey of bovine veterinary practitioners at the October 2024 AABP annual conference found evidence of prior exposure in attending veterinarians performing routine care (rectal exams, milk sampling) without H5N1-specific PPE. The mortality rate in prior human H5N1 cases has been approximately 33% globally, though the dairy cattle strain appears less lethal than prior strains. Community biosecurity implication: PPE use (N95 or better respirator, eye protection, gloves) during any respiratory disease event in dairy cattle is required, not optional.

*November 2024 swine extension*: The first U.S. H5N1 detection in pigs occurred in November 2024 in an Oregon backyard farm with comingled poultry and livestock — the exact operational profile of the Zone 5 homesteads this framework addresses. Fencing that prevents contact between pigs and wild birds (waterfowl, particularly during Mississippi Flyway migration windows in March–May and September–November) is now standard biosecurity for any swine operation in Zone 5.

**Biosecurity protocols for infectious disease events**

A community detecting a possible infectious disease cluster (multiple animals from different households showing similar symptoms within a 2-week period) should activate:

1. Immediate reporting cascade: Tier 2 coordinator notifies all livestock households, USDA APHIS State Veterinarian office (reporting line for disease events), and establishes daily check-in protocol
2. Movement restrictions: no animals leave or enter the community during the assessment period
3. PPE requirement: all animal handling during the event uses gloves, mask, and eye protection
4. Exposure logging: document who handled which animals and when — critical for human health tracking if zoonotic transmission is suspected

**Community decision: When to cull infected flocks**

The culling decision for infectious disease (as distinct from individual animal illness) involves a different calculus: the question is not "can this animal be treated" but "is the continued presence of this animal a threat to the community's entire animal population and to human health."

- HPAI (avian influenza) in poultry: If confirmed, culling the affected flock is the only accepted control measure. The USDA Defend the Flock program documents the biosecurity protocols and the federal indemnity programs available for depopulated flocks. Community implications: neighboring flocks should be placed on high biosecurity alert, outdoor access should be restricted, shared equipment should be disinfected.
- Brucellosis in cattle: State-level management protocols vary; affected animals are typically removed from the herd. This is a community-level decision with 3–5 year food security implications.

### B6. Ecosystem-Scale Cascades

**Wildlife-livestock interface in Zone 5**

Zone 5's rural homesteads exist within a landscape under pressure from wildlife populations that will behave differently under extended human disruption:

*Predator pressure on livestock*: Coyote, fox, and bobcat populations in Zone 5 are constrained by hunting pressure, trapping, and access to natural prey. Under disruption, hunting pressure decreases while prey populations change. Documented pattern from historical disruptions: predators shift toward livestock targets when human activity (which normally functions as aversive conditioning) decreases. Community-level predator management (shared fencing resources, cooperative livestock guarding dog programs, organized deterrence) is more effective than individual household responses.

*Chronic Wasting Disease (CWD) in deer*: CWD is present in deer populations across all Zone 5 states. The prion disease is not directly transmissible to cattle, goats, or humans by current evidence — but deer carcasses from CWD-positive areas should not be fed to livestock. More practically: the Zone 5 community relying on deer hunting as a protein supplement during disruption (Phase 3/02 food systems document: venison as a supplemental protein source) faces CWD as a management constraint. CWD prevalence in Zone 5 counties ranges from 1–40% depending on location; testing harvested deer before consumption is standard guidance from state wildlife agencies.

*Disease surveillance as ecosystem signal*: Unusual mortality events in wild animals (multiple dead waterfowl, deer with neurological symptoms, unusual raccoon behavior) function as early warning signals for disease events that may cross to domestic livestock. A community that maintains wildlife observation logs (as part of the broader food systems monitoring documented in Phase 3) gains advance warning of potential livestock health risks.

## Section C: Household-Community Integration Bridges

### C1. Supply Chain Coordination

The relationship between household veterinary supply caches and the community supply hub is not competitive — it is designed as a cascade. Household caches cover the immediate 72-hour response window for any animal health event; the community hub provides the next-tier resources that individual households would not maintain but that serve multiple households over weeks.

**Integration design:**

Household cache (owned by individual household): consumables used frequently or urgently — wound care supplies, thermometers, electrolytes, probiotics, vaccination materials for owned species, reference texts. These are items the household restores from community hub or direct supply sources after use.

Community supply hub (shared infrastructure): obstetric equipment (rarely used by any single household but critically urgent when needed), shared refrigerated medications accessed via standing community prescriptions, diagnostic reference library, euthanasia equipment (captive bolt), community IV fluid sets, and the community veterinary coordinator's training materials and records.

**Cost amortization example (20-household community):**

|---|---|---|

| Captive bolt | $250 | $12.50 |

| Coordinator training (Pet Emergency Academy) | $200 | $10.00 (2 trained, shared cost) |

Individual household full OTC supply cache for livestock + companion animals: $300–500. Total per-household investment for full household+community system: $350–550. This is the cost basis for a veterinary resilience system covering 20 households.

### C2. Decision Cascades

**Household threshold: When to call the community coordinator**

The Tier 1 to Tier 2 escalation triggers are:
- Fever above 104°F (small ruminants, cattle) that does not respond within 4–6 hours to basic supportive care (cooling, fluids)
- Active labor without progress for 30 minutes (livestock any species)
- Animal down and unable to rise
- Suspected toxic ingestion (plant or chemical)
- Disease affecting multiple animals in the household simultaneously
- Any reproductive emergency (uterine prolapse, retained fetal membranes beyond 6 hours)
- Any wound with signs of rapidly progressing infection (heat, swelling, discharge, behavioral change) within 24 hours

**Community coordinator: Tier 2 to Tier 3 escalation triggers**

The Tier 2 to Tier 3 escalation (to veterinarian/specialist) triggers are:
- Any condition where Tier 2 intervention has not produced improvement within the expected timeline (bloat not resolving in 20 minutes, dystocia not progressing after 30 minutes of skilled manipulation)
- Any suspected reportable disease (HPAI, brucellosis, exotic Newcastle disease, foot-and-mouth disease) — reporting is legally required and activates federal response resources
- Any zoonotic disease event (household members showing concurrent illness after animal contact)
- Any population-level event (multiple unrelated households with similar animal health events in the same week)
- Any condition requiring prescription medications not covered by the community's standing protocol

### C3. Precedent Case Studies

**Case Study 1: East African Community Animal Health Worker (CAHW) Model**

The CAHW system, documented across Ethiopia, Kenya, South Sudan, Somalia, and Uganda (Fic Tufts full report; ODI Working Paper 214; PMC PubMed 15729896), provides the most extensively studied international precedent for the Zone 5 community tier system. Key outcomes from the evidence base: a CAHW project in Dollo Ado, southern Ethiopia documented significant reductions in mange, trypanosomosis, helminthosis, anthrax, and non-specific respiratory disease. Kenya CAHW programs generated approximately USD 350 per household per year in reduced livestock loss. IGAD region (Ethiopia, Sudan, South Sudan, Somalia, Djibouti, Uganda) formally recognizes CAHW services as permanent structural features of the animal health system, not emergency workarounds.

The design principle most transferable to Zone 5: the CAHW system is sustainable when it uses fee-for-service structures (charging per vaccination or treatment) rather than pure volunteerism, because volunteer-only models collapse when personal economic pressure increases. A Zone 5 community coordinator should have a recognized community role with direct compensation (food, supplies, labor credits in a barter-based system) rather than relying on goodwill alone.

**Case Study 2: India Mobile Veterinary Unit (MVU) Program**

India's national Livestock Health and Disease Control Programme (LHDCP), revised March 2025 with $460 million USD approved, provisions approximately one MVU per 100,000 livestock population. The Andhra Pradesh state model — where farmers call a single number and a mobile unit responds to the farm — is the most documented. Outcomes: on-farm diagnosis, vaccination, wound care, and education delivered without requiring farmers to transport animals to a clinic. The hybrid model now emerging in Indian rural contexts (teleconsultation for initial assessment, scheduled mobile unit visit for physical examination and medication dispensing) maps directly to the Zone 5 community coordinator + land-grant veterinary school extension model.

The transferable element: a Zone 5 community that establishes a dispatch protocol (Tier 2 coordinator as the single point of contact, with a pre-agreed response protocol with the nearest veterinary school field services team) replicates the Indian MVU's most valuable feature — farmer certainty about how to get help.

**Case Study 3: Mongolia Herding Communities and Dzud Crisis**

Mongolian herders use peer-based mutual aid systems during dzud (extreme winter events that kill large numbers of livestock) documented by FAO and IFAD. The mutual aid structure includes: sharing of winter fodder between herders, cooperative emergency slaughter to preserve meat from animals at risk of dying, cooperative animal transport, and peer training on emergency husbandry. The key design feature: informal mutual aid protocols are negotiated during normal summers, not improvised during crisis winters. The Zone 5 equivalent is the pre-crisis establishment of the community coordinator network, supply hub, and community VCPR — all developed during normal conditions.

**Case Study 4: Texas Panhandle Wildfire, February 2024**

The Smokehouse Creek Fire (February 26–March 2024) burned approximately 1.1 million acres in the Texas Panhandle — the largest wildfire in Texas history — killing an estimated 10,000–13,000 cattle and affecting hundreds of ranches. The Texas A&M VET (Veterinary Emergency Team) deployed to the region and documented specific lessons:

*Rapid triage*: Animals with severe burns (greater than 50% body surface area) were humanely euthanized on scene; animals with less severe burns received wound care and fluid support. The triage threshold for euthanasia was established in advance of deployment, allowing field personnel to make consistent decisions without case-by-case consultation.

*Community coordination gap*: The largest source of delay in reaching affected animals was the absence of pre-established communication networks identifying which ranches had livestock, their locations, and their access routes. Communities that had pre-registered livestock with county extension offices received faster response.

*Culling decision precedent*: Animals that could not be transported for care and had injuries beyond manageable thresholds were euthanized on scene using gunshot. The decision framework — animals that cannot be transported, cannot stand, and have injuries beyond manageable thresholds — was documented in the deployment after-action report and directly applies to the Zone 5 community coordinator's culling decision framework.

**Case Study 5: India's Veterinary Barefoot Doctor Model (Bihar and Rajasthan)**

The "Pashu Sakhi" (livestock friend) program in Bihar and the companion program in Rajasthan trains village-level women as community animal health workers, with structured 30-day training covering vaccination administration, basic disease recognition, and referral protocols. Each Pashu Sakhi covers 50–100 households. The program is government-subsidized and operates with a drug kit provided by the state government and replenished through a cost-recovery mechanism. By 2022, over 20,000 Pashu Sakhis had been trained across India.

The Zone 5 parallel: a rural county that trains 20–40 community veterinary coordinators using extension service resources, provides a shared equipment kit through a county farm bureau or cooperative, and establishes a cost-recovery mechanism (annual fee from participating households) has built a structurally equivalent system at comparable scale.

## Appendix: Quick Reference — Decision Trees and Supply Lists

### Decision Tree Summary

**Household Decision Tree: Animal health event response**
1. Is the animal in immediate life-threatening danger? (respiratory distress, unconscious, major hemorrhage, complete urinary obstruction) → Red Zone: transport/euthanasia
2. Is the animal stable but declining? (fever >104°F, down but responsive, suspected infection) → Call Tier 2 community coordinator
3. Is the animal stable with a specific manageable condition? (minor wound, mild GI upset, early-stage parasite burden) → Household management with monitoring protocol
4. Is the condition unclear and the animal otherwise normal? → Document, monitor every 6–8 hours, escalate if declining

**Community Coordinator Decision Tree: Escalation**
1. Is this a reportable disease? (HPAI, brucellosis, foot-and-mouth) → Report to state veterinarian, activate community biosecurity protocol
2. Is this a multiple-household event? → Aggregate reporting, consult Tier 3 specialist
3. Is this within Tier 2 scope? → Treat with community supplies, document outcome
4. Is this beyond Tier 2 scope? → Contact Tier 3 (veterinarian/specialist), transport if feasible
