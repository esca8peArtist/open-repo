# Phase 4b — Agricultural Intensification

> **Region**: Midwest US (Zone 5) | **Scale**: 50–150 people, 1–10 acres  
> **Phase**: 4b — food production at community scale, multi-year horizon

## Most Important Finding

The Zone 5 planting window for perennial food systems is **April 15 to June 15**. Any perennial tree or shrub planted in this window in 2026 begins producing supplemental yield by 2028–2029 and reaches meaningful community-scale production by 2031–2032. A decision deferred beyond June 15, 2026 forfeits the 2026 planting window entirely. Given the 3–5 year establishment timeline documented across the Savanna Institute's 5,200-acre Midwest research network and Mark Shepard's 140-acre New Forest Farm case study in southwestern Wisconsin, the compounding effect of delay is the single most important fact in this document.

Phase 1 (`individual/06-agriculture.md`) covers a 400 sq ft garden for one person using annual crops. Phase 2 (`household/03-food-coordination.md`) covers coordinated household-scale production. Neither addresses the qualitative shift that occurs when supply chains do not restore within 24 months: a community of 50–150 people must begin producing meaningful fractions of its calories locally. This requires perennial systems — food forests, perennial grains, and structured seed libraries — that cannot be bootstrapped during a disruption. They must be established before one begins.

## Section 1: The Case for Perennial Systems

### Energy Accounting — Annual vs. Perennial

The labor economics of annual versus perennial production are the foundation for every decision in this document. An established food forest requires approximately 15 labor-hours per person per year after Year 5 — compared to approximately 60 labor-hours per person per year for equivalent caloric output from an annual vegetable garden. The difference is not management style; it is structural. Annual systems require soil preparation, seeding, irrigation, weeding, and harvest every year. Perennial systems absorb this front-loaded labor into the establishment phase (Years 1–4) and return compound yields thereafter.

Savanna Institute research, drawn from a 14-farm research network across Minnesota, Iowa, Illinois, and Wisconsin, confirms that woody perennial polyculture systems produce year-over-year yield improvement through Year 10+ without proportional labor increases. The Institute's analysis of 400+ Midwest farmers directly supported since 2013 — now managing over 5,200 acres of agroforestry — shows that perennial crop income in the United States averaged an annual return of 12.2% on investment over the past decade, compared to 4.5% for annual crops. [1][2]

This is not an argument against annual crops. Three Sisters polyculture, cover crops, and annual vegetable production remain essential for Year 1 and Year 2 of any disruption scenario. The argument is that planting perennials now, in parallel with annual systems, creates the production base that sustains a community through Year 3 and beyond — when annual inputs (seeds from garden centers, fertilizers, tillage equipment fuel) may no longer be available.

### The Irreversibility Constraint

A hazelnut planted in May 2026 begins producing meaningful yields in 2029–2030. The same hazelnut planted in May 2027 begins yielding in 2030–2031. There is no way to recover that year. This is unlike almost every other resilience investment in Phase 1–4: a water filtration system can be installed in a week; a radio network can be built in a month; a mutual aid agreement can be signed in an afternoon. Perennial food systems cannot be compressed.

This constraint makes the June 1, 2026 decision deadline meaningful in a way that most project deadlines are not.

## Section 2: Perennial Species Selection for Zone 5

### Design Principle: 7-Layer Architecture

The 7-layer food forest model — canopy, sub-canopy, shrub, herbaceous, ground cover, root zone, and climbing — is not an aesthetic preference. It is a production system that maximizes yield per acre by stacking species through vertical space, extends the harvest season across April through November, and creates the structural complexity that supports soil biology and pest management without external inputs. The University of Missouri Center for Agroforestry 2024 training manual and the Savanna Institute's Perennial Pathways guide (published through UW-Madison Extension) both document this architecture as the appropriate design model for Midwest temperate conditions. [3][4]

At community scale (1–5 acres), a practical Zone 5 food forest does not need all seven layers equally developed. Priority should go to: (1) nut canopy for caloric density, (2) fruit sub-canopy for vitamin and mineral diversity, (3) berry shrub layer for early production (shrubs yield in Year 2–3 vs. Year 5+ for trees), and (4) herbaceous layer with nitrogen-fixing and medicinal species that perform ecological functions in Years 1–3 while trees establish.

### Canopy Layer: Nut Trees

**Hybrid Hazelnut (Corylus hybrid — American × European)**
The single highest-priority planting for Zone 5 community food forests. The Savanna Institute has invested substantial research capital in hazelnut, including sequencing the American hazelnut genome and deploying drone-based yield analysis to optimize cultivar selection. Hazelnuts begin producing in 3–5 years from planting (with early yields by Year 3, meaningful production by Year 5), reach full production at 7–10 years, and are hardy to Zone 4. Yield at maturity: 1,500–2,500 lbs/acre under Midwest field conditions, based on Midwest Hazelnut Development Initiative research. Protein content is 14–17%, fat content 60–65% — making hazelnuts one of the highest-calorie, highest-nutrient perennial crops available in Zone 5. [5][6]

Mark Shepard's New Forest Farm in southwestern Wisconsin (Zone 5a) demonstrates commercial-scale hazelnut integration over 20+ years. His STUN (Sheer Total Utter Neglect) management philosophy — applying selective neglect to cull weak individuals while maintaining productive stands — has produced multi-generational hazelnut groves that self-select for local adaptation and disease resistance over time. [7]

**Hybrid Chestnut (Castanea hybrid — American × Chinese)**
Hybrid chestnuts are the highest-calorie nut crop available in Zone 5, producing 2,000–4,000 lbs per acre at maturity (Year 12–15) with early yields beginning in Year 3–5 at approximately 350 lbs/acre. Tom Wahl at Red Fern Farm in Wapello, Iowa documents that 10 acres of well-managed chestnuts can produce 35,000–40,000 lbs annually at full production, at Iowa wholesale prices of $2–3/lb generating $70,000–120,000/year. Chestnut flour is 45% carbohydrate — the highest of any tree crop — making chestnuts a genuine caloric staple rather than a supplement. Blight-resistant hybrid varieties (Silverleaf, Mossycup) are hardy to Zone 5 and available from regional nurseries. [8][9]

**Black Walnut (Juglans nigra)**
Native to Zone 4–9, black walnut is an existing resource on most Midwest farmsteads and can be incorporated into food forest designs as existing canopy. Productivity varies by tree age and site; mature trees yield 25–50 lbs of nut meats per year. Black walnut does produce juglone, a compound toxic to some plants — notably apple, tomato, and blackberry — and this spacing constraint (15–30 ft clearance for juglone-sensitive species) must be incorporated into site layout. [10]

### Sub-Canopy Layer: Fruit Trees

**Pawpaw (Asimina triloba)**
The pawpaw is North America's largest native fruit and is native to Zone 5–8. It produces fruit 5–7 years from planting, requires cross-pollination (plant 2+ varieties for fruit set), and yields approximately 14,000 lbs/acre at full sun at full production. In partial shade under chestnut canopy, Red Fern Farm data shows 50–75% of full-sun yield — approximately 7,000–10,500 lbs/acre — making the chestnut-pawpaw integration one of the highest-calorie polyculture combinations documented in the Midwest. The fruit has the texture of custard and the nutritional profile of an avocado: 4g protein, 7g fat per 100g. [8]

**American Persimmon (Diospyros virginiana)**
Native to Zone 4–9, the American persimmon is more cold-hardy than Asian varieties and is adapted to Zone 5 clay-loam soils. Grafted trees begin bearing in 3 years from planting; seed-grown trees begin bearing in 4–9 years. Named varieties yield 90–100 lbs per mature tree. Unlike Asian persimmons, American persimmons are not astringent after frost exposure and can be harvested November through December — extending the fresh fruit window significantly after other crops are done. [11]

### Shrub Layer: Berries and Woody Shrubs

The shrub layer is the highest-priority planting in Years 1–3 because it produces the earliest yields while canopy trees establish. Berry shrubs reliably produce by Year 2–3, provide bird habitat that reduces insect pressure, and perform nitrogen fixation where leguminous shrubs (Siberian pea shrub, goumi) are included.

**Elderberry (Sambucus canadensis)**
Native throughout the Midwest, hardy to Zone 3. Begin bearing in Year 1–2, with full production in Year 3–5 at 12–15 lbs per plant. Yields of 7,000 lbs/acre have been documented in managed plantings. Elderberries are the fastest-yielding shrub crop for Zone 5 and have significant medicinal value (elderflower and elderberry are established antiviral and immune-support plants in traditional herbalism and are supported by a growing peer-reviewed literature). Space 8–10 ft apart in rows. [12][13]

**Serviceberry / Saskatoon (Amelanchier alnifolia)**
Hardy to Zone 2. Yields 10 lbs per plant at full production (Year 5–8), machine-harvestable, and the first tree fruit to ripen in Zone 5 (late May to early June). Savanna Institute research specifically highlights serviceberry as a high-priority species for Midwest food forest systems. The early harvest window is strategically important: serviceberry produces when the annual garden is newly planted and nothing else is yielding, filling a caloric gap in the early-season production schedule. [14]

**Currants and Gooseberries (Ribes spp.)**
Both are Zone 3–5 hardy, yield in Year 2–3 from planting, and tolerate partial shade — making them compatible with tree canopy. Black currant (*R. nigrum*) has the highest vitamin C content of any common berry. Red currant and gooseberry can be used for fresh eating, wine, and preservation. Yield: 4–8 lbs per plant at maturity. Note that Ribes has historically been restricted in some states (white pine blister rust vector) — check current regulations for your specific county before planting. [10]

**Siberian Pea Shrub (Caragana arborescens)**
Not a food crop, but one of the most valuable functional plants in a Zone 5 food forest. It is one of very few cold-hardy nitrogen-fixing shrubs, fixing 45–175 lbs of nitrogen per acre annually. It tolerates Zone 2 cold, poor soils, and drought. Plant as a hedgerow border and as inter-row N-fixer in tree alleys. Seeds are edible (8% protein) though not a primary food source. [10]

### Herbaceous Layer

**Comfrey (Symphytum officinale)** — The functional workhorse of any food forest herbaceous layer. Comfrey is a dynamic accumulator, drawing potassium, phosphorus, and calcium from deep in the subsoil and depositing them at the surface through leaf decomposition. Cut leaves 3–4 times per season and lay them as mulch around tree bases. Root system is virtually indestructible once established; plant it where you want it permanently. Hardy to Zone 3. [15]

**Asparagus (Asparagus officinalis)** — One of the few true perennial vegetables, asparagus produces for 20–30 years from a single planting. Begin harvesting lightly in Year 3, full harvest from Year 4. Zone 3–8. Plant crowns 18 inches apart in well-drained beds amended with compost; protect beds from foot traffic permanently.

**Jerusalem Artichoke (Helianthus tuberosus)** — Native to North America, Zone 3–9. Produces edible tubers at roughly 1,000–2,500 lbs/acre after establishment. Caution: once established, it is extremely aggressive and difficult to remove. Plant in a contained area or use as a food forest edge species. Requires no fertility inputs and tolerates Zone 5 winter completely. High-calorie, high-inulin tubers are an important starch crop.

**Ramps (Allium tricoccum)** — Native Midwest woodland onion, Zone 3–8, produces in deep shade under canopy trees. Ramps are among the few spring-harvest crops that require mature woodland conditions to produce well, and they begin yielding from seed in Year 3–5. They fill the early April gap before other crops are available.

### Ground Cover

**White Clover (Trifolium repens)** — Standard food forest ground cover. Fixes nitrogen, tolerates foot traffic, provides continuous ground cover that suppresses weeds. Seeded between trees at establishment. [10]

**Alpine Strawberry (Fragaria vesca)** — Produces throughout the season, tolerates partial shade, self-seeds freely. Not a commercial strawberry yield, but provides continuous fresh fruit from June through frost with zero annual care after establishment.

### Climbing Layer

**Hardy Kiwi (Actinidia arguta)** — Hardy to Zone 4–5. Self-fertile variety 'Issai' begins fruiting 1–2 years from planting; other varieties begin in 3–5 years. At full production: 40–70 lbs per mature vine. Train on a sturdy 6-post trellis (posts must support 200+ lb vine load at maturity). Fruit is grape-sized, smooth-skinned, and edible without peeling. [16]

**Grapes (Vitis hybrid)** — Cold-hardy Midwest grape varieties (Marquette, Frontenac, Itasca) are Zone 4–5 rated and produce from Year 3. Grapes are high-calorie, preserve well as raisins, and provide harvest in August–September — earlier than most nut crops. [10]

### Perennial Grain: Kernza

**Kernza (Thinopyrum intermedium — Intermediate Wheatgrass)**
Kernza, developed by the Land Institute in Salina, Kansas, is the most important perennial grain crop currently available for Zone 5 Midwest production. Key data from peer-reviewed Land Institute research and 2024 grower reports:

- Root depth: 10 feet (vs. 2–3 feet for annual wheat). This root depth anchors soil against erosion and enables the crop to access subsoil water during drought.
- Nitrate retention: 96% more nitrate absorbed compared to corn/soy rotations, measured by the Land Institute in field trials. This is the most significant water quality benefit of any Midwest perennial crop.
- Carbon sequestration: 300–1,000 lbs of carbon per acre per year, depending on management.
- Yield: 400–600 lbs/acre in Year 2 (the peak year after fall seeding). Average yields in 2021 were 400+ lbs/acre with experienced growers achieving up to 1,200 lbs/acre under optimal conditions. Yields are maximized in Year 2 following fall seeding; between-row cultivation and management extend productive life to 5–7 years. [17][18]
- Planting in Zone 5: Fall seeding (August 15–September 15 in Minnesota/Wisconsin) provides best establishment. Spring seeding is possible but will not produce grain in Year 1. Seeding rate: 10–18 lbs/acre.
- Nutritional profile: Higher in protein, carotenoids, and antioxidants than annual wheat; high in dietary fiber. Suitable as a partial flour substitute; currently used by several commercial brewers and bakeries.

For a community of 50–150 people, 5–10 acres of Kernza in combination with Three Sisters field production provides meaningful grain supplementation by Year 2 of planting. This is the fastest-to-production perennial caloric crop available in Zone 5.

## Section 3: Food Forest Design and Site Preparation

### Site Selection Criteria

The Savanna Institute's Perennial Pathways guide (UW-Madison Extension) specifies that most fruit and nut trees thrive at a soil pH of 6.0–6.5, requiring a soil test before planting. Additional site criteria for Zone 5:

- **Sun exposure**: Minimum 6 hours direct sun daily for canopy and sub-canopy trees. Shrub and herbaceous layers can function at 4–6 hours.
- **Drainage**: Avoid low spots where water pools in April–May (Zone 5 snowmelt). Waterlogging for more than 48 hours will kill most nut tree root systems.
- **Wind protection**: Zone 5 winter winds accelerate moisture loss and can cause bark splitting in young trees. Locate food forest adjacent to an existing windbreak (conifer row, berm, or building), or plant a living windbreak of Siberian pea shrub on the north and northwest edges.
- **Distance from black walnut**: Site any juglone-sensitive species (apple, cherry, tomato, blackberry) at least 50 feet from established black walnut trees.

### Spatial Layout at 1 Acre

A 1-acre food forest (approximately 210 × 210 ft) can accommodate:
- 25–30 nut/fruit trees at 20–25 ft spacing (canopy and sub-canopy)
- 60–80 shrubs at 8–10 ft spacing (berry layer)
- 100–200 herbaceous perennials (comfrey, asparagus, ramps, strawberry) in understory
- 2–4 hardy kiwi trellises on south-facing edges

At full production (Year 10+), this 1-acre design produces approximately:
- Hazelnuts: 1,500–2,000 lbs/year (2,000 calories/lb)
- Chestnuts: 500–1,000 lbs/year (600 calories/lb)
- Mixed berries and fruit: 1,000–2,000 lbs/year
- Asparagus: 50–100 lbs/year fresh

For 50 people, this is meaningful supplemental production — not full caloric sustenance from 1 acre alone, but a resilient year-round food contribution. Scaling to 5 acres of food forest + 5 acres of Kernza + annual garden production approaches meaningful caloric contribution for 50–100 people by Year 5+.

### Soil Building Before Planting

Phase 1's `individual/06-agriculture.md` covers soil building principles at homestead scale. At community food forest scale, the soil building protocol preceding planting is the single highest-leverage activity in Year 0 (the season before trees go in).

**Year 0 Soil Building Protocol (for Zone 5 clay-loam):**

1. **Soil test first.** USDA NRCS and most county extension offices provide soil testing at $15–25 per sample. Test for pH, phosphorus, potassium, organic matter percentage (%), and a full nutrient panel. Results take 2–3 weeks. Adjust pH to 6.0–6.5 before planting using agricultural lime (if below 6.0) or elemental sulfur (if above 6.5).

2. **Sheet mulch the planting footprint in fall.** Lay cardboard directly on the soil surface (overlapping by 6 inches — no gaps), wet thoroughly, then cover with 6–8 inches of wood chips. This kills existing vegetation without tillage, feeds soil fungi, and begins the decomposition process that will improve structure by planting time.

3. **Amend before planting.** Pull back mulch at each tree hole; add 2–3 gallons of finished compost mixed with biochar (3:1 compost:biochar ratio) before setting the tree. This concentrates fertility in the root zone where trees need it most in Year 1.

4. **Soil organic matter targets:** Most Zone 5 Midwest soils are at 2–3% organic matter after decades of tillage farming. Target is 4–6% for a productive perennial system. At 1% SOM increase per 3–5 years of consistent cover cropping + compost application, reaching 5–6% takes approximately 10–15 years of sustained management. Start the soil building process now — the trajectory is the important metric, not the starting point. [19]

## Section 4: Community Seed-Saving Governance

### Why Seed Saving Cannot Be Deferred

`individual/06-agriculture.md` mentions seed saving briefly. This section develops it into a community governance system, because the logic of the agricultural intensification argument applies to seeds as directly as it applies to perennial trees: you cannot save seeds from varieties you do not already grow. A seed library that does not exist before a disruption cannot be bootstrapped from hybrid seed packets — hybrids are F1 crosses that do not breed true, meaning seeds saved from a hybrid will not produce the same plant.

The practical threshold: a community seed library needs at least 2 complete growing seasons to establish working stocks, because seed viability must be tested and varieties need at least one season of local adaptation before they can be reliably shared. Starting a seed library in 2026 means working seed stocks are available for 2028 emergency distribution.

### Open-Pollinated vs. Hybrid — The Core Distinction

Every seed-saving decision flows from this distinction:

- **Open-pollinated (OP) seeds**: Produced by natural pollination (by insects, wind, or self-pollination). Seeds saved from OP varieties reliably produce plants with the same characteristics as the parent. All heirloom varieties are open-pollinated.
- **Hybrid (F1) seeds**: Produced by controlled cross-breeding of two inbred lines. Hybrid plants are vigorous but their seeds segregate in the next generation — you get unpredictable results. Hybrids cannot be reliably saved.
- **GMO seeds**: Legally restricted (Monsanto/Bayer terminator patents), ecologically unreliable for seed saving, and excluded from all community seed libraries by standard policy.

**Rule**: The community seed library collects, stores, and distributes only open-pollinated, non-GMO seed. This is the standard adopted by every functioning seed library in the Midwest, including the Omaha Public Library's Common Soil Seed Library (founded 2013), the Cleveland Seed Bank (partnering with 20 libraries), and Michigan's network of 125+ seed libraries. [20][21]

### Isolation Distances

Cross-pollination between varieties of the same species produces seeds that are genetically intermediate — they may look like either parent, or something in between. For a seed library to maintain true-to-type varieties, isolation distances must be maintained between different varieties of the same species.

**Isolation distances for Zone 5 seed saving:**

| Crop | Pollination Type | Minimum Isolation | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Corn | Wind | 1,500–2,640 ft (up to 1 mile) | Most demanding; cannot isolate adequately in small gardens |
| Brassica (cabbage, kale, broccoli) | Insect | 1,500–5,280 ft (up to 1 mile) | All Brassica oleracea cross-pollinate freely |
| Squash/Pumpkin | Insect | 1,320–2,640 ft (¼–½ mile) | Same-species crosses only; C. pepo/C. maxima/C. moschata do NOT cross |
| Pepper | Insect | 300–1,600 ft | Mostly self-fertile but insect movement causes crossing |
| Tomato | Self-fertile | 10–50 ft | Very low crossing risk; bagging effective if needed |
| Bean (pole/bush) | Self-fertile | 20 ft | Self-pollinating before flower opens |
| Lettuce | Self-fertile | 10 ft | Safest crop to save seed from |
| Cucumber | Insect | 500–1,500 ft | Crosses with other cucumbers; not with squash |

Sources: Seed Savers Exchange crop-specific chart; MSU Extension seed saving guide; Almanac.com isolation distance reference. [22][23][24]

**Practical implication**: In an urban or suburban setting, corn and brassica seed saving requires caging (hand-pollination under exclusion cover) or coordinated regional isolation — planting only one variety of each high-distance crop across an entire neighborhood. In a rural setting, these distances are achievable through spatial planning across the farm.

### Seed Viability — Storage Lifespans

Seed viability declines with time. These are standard lifespans for correctly stored seed (cool, dry, dark — ideally 40°F and less than 40% relative humidity):

| Crop | Viability (years) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Onion, parsnip | 1–2 | Must save annually; do not stockpile |
| Corn, bean, pea, carrot | 2–3 | Save every 2 seasons minimum |
| Tomato, squash, cucumber | 4–5 | Most forgiving; can hold 4 years reliably |
| Lettuce, brassica | 3–5 | Test germination before distributing old stock |
| Pepper | 2–4 | Dries out easily; seal tightly |

The 40°F / 40% RH rule of thumb: the sum of temperature in Fahrenheit and relative humidity percentage should be below 100 for seeds to store well long-term. At room temperature (70°F), humidity must be below 30%. A household refrigerator at 35–40°F with silica gel desiccant provides excellent conditions. A chest freezer with sealed containers can extend viability to 10+ years for most crops. [25]

### Physical Storage Infrastructure

**Minimum viable seed library for 50-person community:**
- 200–400 seed packets (envelopes or small paper bags, labeled with: variety name, crop species, year saved, source, isolation notes, germination rate if tested)
- 10–20 mason jars with airtight lids (for medium-term storage, 1–3 years)
- 5–10 mylar bags with oxygen absorbers (for long-term storage, 5+ years, in freezer)
- Silica gel desiccant packets (replace or recharge annually by drying in oven at 250°F for 2 hours)
- Physical catalog: one 3×5 card per variety, filed alphabetically by crop species, with: accession date, source, notes, last germination test result

**Storage locations:** Apply the 3-location redundancy rule from Phase 4a (Technology Repair): original collection + two backup sets in geographically separated locations. For a community seed library, this means: (1) primary seed vault at the community hub, (2) backup collection held by a member in a different household, (3) deposit with a regional seed library (Seed Savers Exchange Heritage Farm in Decorah, Iowa accepts seed deposits from verified community collections).

### Seed Library Governance Model

The Midwest seed library model has converged on a check-out framework based on the public library system. The governance rules below are drawn from the operational models of the Omaha Public Library Common Soil Seed Library, the Michigan Seed Library Network (125+ member libraries), and the Cleveland Seed Bank. [20][21][26]

**Checkout rules:**
1. Any community member with a library card / membership can check out up to 5 seed packets per season.
2. Seeds are checked out at no cost.
3. Members are encouraged (but not required) to return seeds at end of season. This policy flexibility is intentional — mandatory returns create compliance overhead and can deter participation. The library relies on donor seed, grown-out returns, and annual seed swap events to replenish stock.
4. Members who do return seeds must meet quality standards: seeds must be dry, labeled with variety and year, and free from pest damage. A seed steward reviews returned packets before adding to the collection.

**Annual renewal cycle:**
1. Each December–January: germination test all packets older than the crop's viability threshold. Discard packets below 50% germination rate or renew by growing out in the following season.
2. Each spring: conduct a seed swap event open to the community. Swap events are the fastest way to expand the collection and are supported by research showing they improve community social cohesion alongside food security. [27]
3. Each fall: add newly saved seeds to the collection, completing the annual cycle.

**Scientific advisory structure:**
The Lyon Township Public Library (Michigan) model uses a scientific advisory board to determine which varieties to stock based on regional climate performance. For a Zone 5 community library, this advisory role can be assigned to 2–3 community members with gardening experience, using Seed Savers Exchange's variety database (14,000+ varieties, with Zone 5 notes) and regional extension recommendations as the primary reference. [26]

### Variety Selection for Zone 5

The core criterion for Zone 5 variety selection is a frost-free growing window of 150–165 days (last frost: May 1–15; first frost: October 1–15). All varieties in the community library should have a days-to-maturity within this window, with a 10–15 day buffer to account for late spring and early fall frosts.

**Priority varieties with Zone 5 track record (Seed Savers Exchange database):**

*Tomatoes:* Brandywine (80 days), Cherokee Purple (80 days), Mortgage Lifter (80 days) — all OP, disease-resistant for Midwest late blight risk, large-fruited for caloric density.

*Corn:* Bloody Butcher (110 days — push the boundary of Zone 5; start indoors May 1 for transplant), Painted Mountain (85 days — most frost-tolerant field corn available; bred for Zone 3–5 high-altitude conditions), Minnesota Midget cantaloupe (60 days — crosses a fruit crop with the corn timeframe).

*Beans:* Dragon Tongue (55 days), Jacob's Cattle (85 days dried), Cherokee Trail of Tears (85 days — pole bean with black dried beans; heat- and cold-tolerant).

*Squash:* Delicata (100 days), Butternut (105 days — push limit for Zone 5; start indoors April 15), Red Kuri (95 days), Acorn (80 days).

*Brassica:* Lacinato kale (60 days — frost-hardy to 20°F; overwinters in Zone 5 with mulch), Red Russian kale (50 days — extremely cold-hardy; self-seeds freely), Tronchuda (60 days Portuguese kale).

*Greens:* Sorrel (perennial; harvest April–November), Good King Henry (perennial; harvest April–July), Arugula (40 days; self-seeds).

Source: [Seed Savers Exchange Heritage Farm, Decorah, Iowa](https://seedsavers.org/); [Omaha Sprouts Midwest Regional Seed Guide](https://www.omahasprouts.org/post/midwest-seed-buying-guide) [28][29]

## Section 5: Implementation Timeline

### Year 0 (Fall 2025 / Spring 2026 — Now)

**If a 2026 planting decision is made by June 1:**

- **September–October 2025 (if possible):** Sheet-mulch designated food forest footprint with cardboard and 6–8 inches of wood chips. Allow one full winter for cardboard to break down and wood chips to begin decomposing. This is the single highest-leverage pre-planting action — it creates 6 months of soil building before a single tree goes in.
- **March–April 2026:** Soil test. Order trees and shrubs from regional nurseries (bare-root stock ships March–May; availability is limited and sells out early — order by February for spring planting).
- **April 15–June 1, 2026:** Plant Zone 5 perennial trees and shrubs within the planting window. Priority order: (1) canopy nut trees (hazelnuts, chestnuts), (2) shrubs (elderberry, serviceberry), (3) sub-canopy fruit trees (pawpaw, persimmon), (4) herbaceous layer (comfrey, asparagus, ramps).
- **May 2026:** Begin seed library collection. Conduct community seed-saving workshop. Identify 3–5 community members as seed stewards. Start growing out 10–20 OP varieties from purchased Seed Savers Exchange seed.
- **August–September 2026:** Fall-seed Kernza at 10–18 lbs/acre in prepared field area. This is the critical planting window; Kernza seeded August 15–September 15 establishes the largest plants before winter and produces the highest Year 2 yield.

### Year 1 (2027)

- **Early spring:** First asparagus and ramp shoots emerge. Berry shrubs leaf out. Observe establishment success; replace any dead trees (expect 5–15% mortality in Year 1, higher in drought years).
- **May:** Plant annual Three Sisters field (corn, bean, squash) alongside perennial system. Year 1–4 annual production is the primary food source while perennials establish.
- **June:** Elderberry begins first light berry production. Serviceberry produces first significant harvest.
- **Summer:** Kernza reaches vegetative growth but does not produce grain in Year 1 if fall-seeded (Year 2 is peak grain production).
- **Fall:** First seed saving season. Save seed from at least 5 staple varieties. Test germination. Begin physical catalog.

### Year 2 (2028)

- **Kernza:** Peak grain production. Expect 400–600 lbs/acre. Harvest when grain is approximately 20% moisture, using combine or small plot harvester. Process and store.
- **Elderberries:** Full production (12–15 lbs per plant). Harvest August–September.
- **Serviceberry:** Full production. Harvest June.
- **Hazelnuts and chestnuts:** First light production (50–150 lbs per tree, variable). Not meaningful yield yet — this is the "promise" phase.
- **Seed library:** Second growing season of seed saving. Expand to 30–50 varieties. First annual seed swap event.

### Year 3–4 (2029–2030)

- **Kernza:** Year 3 yields begin declining unless managed with between-row cultivation. By Year 5–7 of the stand, plan to renovate or replant a portion of the field.
- **Hazelnuts:** Meaningful first hazelnut harvest (first significant crop; hazelnut development is notoriously variable by cultivar and site).
- **Chestnuts:** 350 lbs/acre in well-managed stands, per Red Fern Farm data. First real chestnut harvest.
- **Hardy kiwi:** Full production for 'Issai' (planted 2026); 40–70 lbs/vine.
- **Pawpaw:** First fruit (if cross-pollination was established correctly).
- **Seed library:** 50–100 variety collection, fully governed, with annual swap, renewal, and germination-test cycle established.

### Year 5+ (2031 and beyond)

Full food forest system in productive maturity. Hazelnuts, chestnuts, pawpaw, elderberry, serviceberry, kiwi all at or approaching peak production. Community seed library self-sustaining with local adaptation improving variety performance annually. Annual Three Sisters and Kernza production providing grain and vegetable base. The community's food system is no longer dependent on supply chain inputs for seed, soil fertility, or crop diversity.

## Section 6: Risks and Mitigations

| Risk | Probability | Impact | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Late frost after planting (May 10–20 frosts common in Zone 5) | High | Medium — loses 1 year if severe | Cover young trees with 5-gallon buckets during frost warnings; plant after May 15 for frost-sensitive species |
| Deer browse pressure on new plantings | High | High — a deer can kill a young tree overnight | Install tree tubes (4 ft minimum) on all canopy and sub-canopy trees at planting. Cost: $3–8/tube for 100 trees = $300–800. Non-negotiable in rural Zone 5. |
| Hazelnut Eastern Filbert Blight (Anisogramma anomala) | Medium in upper Midwest | Medium — reduces yield significantly | Select blight-resistant cultivars from Badgersett Research Farm or Savanna Institute. Do not plant European hazelnut (C. avellana) in upper Midwest — highly susceptible. |
| Kernza weed pressure in establishment year | High | Medium — reduces Year 1 stand density | Use clean seed bed; control perennial weeds before planting. Kernza's own shading suppresses weeds by Year 2. |
| Seed library dormancy (no volunteers to steward) | Medium | High — collection loses viability if not maintained | Designate 2 backup stewards; build germination testing into annual calendar so failure is visible; connect with regional seed library network for backup. |
| Drought during establishment (Years 1–2) | Medium | High — trees do not re-establish easily after failure | Mulch heavily (6–8 inches around each tree, not touching trunk); irrigate newly planted trees weekly in dry periods. See [04-technology-repair-equipment-protocols.md](04-technology-repair-equipment-protocols.md) for irrigation system maintenance. |
| Black walnut juglone damage to misplaced species | Low (if designed correctly) | High — kills established plants | Map existing black walnut trees before planting. Apply 50-ft buffer for juglone-sensitive species. |

## Section 7: Adjacent Opportunities — Silvopasture, Chestnuts at Farm Scale, and Medicinal Perennials

### Silvopasture

Silvopasture — the integration of trees with pasture and livestock — is the highest-value adjacent opportunity for any community with existing pasture land and small-scale livestock (chickens, pigs, goats). The USDA National Agroforestry Center documents silvopasture across NRCS Practice Standard 381, covering zone-specific establishment protocols and financial assistance programs (EQIP incentive payments are available for silvopasture establishment).

The chestnut-pasture integration is particularly well-suited to Zone 5 Midwest: chestnuts planted at 70–100 trees/acre in a pasture system begin producing supplemental mast for pigs and deer (and harvest for people) by Year 5–7, while maintaining the pasture's grass production in the intervening years. The Frontiers in Sustainable Food Systems (2023) silvopasture study documented a 30-year net present value for chestnut silvopasture approximately 14 times higher than conventional fodder systems in eastern US contexts, with returns of $4,000–10,000/acre after 10 years. [30][31]

For communities with any livestock component (even small-scale backyard chickens), silvopasture is the fastest path to integrating perennial tree production with existing land management — no separate food forest infrastructure is required.

### Chestnuts at Farm Scale

Red Fern Farm in Wapello, Iowa (Zone 5b) is the most detailed and credible publicly available case study of chestnut + pawpaw polyculture at farm scale in the Midwest. Tom Wahl documents 10 acres of chestnuts producing 35,000–40,000 lbs at maturity (Year 12–15), with early returns beginning at Year 5 (350 lbs/acre). The chestnut-pawpaw understory integration recovers 50–75% of full-sun pawpaw yield even in moderate shade, making the vertical stacking effective. [8]

At community scale, a dedicated 2–3 acre chestnut planting begun in 2026 reaches meaningful production (700–1,000 lbs total) by 2031 and full production (4,000–8,000 lbs) by 2038–2041. This is a 15-year investment, but chestnuts are one of the most calorie-dense tree crops available in Zone 5 (45% carbohydrate, 5% fat, 5% protein per 100g), and a mature chestnut planting produces food year after year with zero annual input cost.

### Medicinal Perennials

The integration of medicinal perennials into the food forest herbaceous layer adds pharmaceutical value with near-zero additional labor. The most important Zone 5 medicinal perennials for a community resilience context:

**Echinacea (E. purpurea and E. angustifolia)** — Hardy to Zone 3. Root and flower preparations are among the most extensively studied plant medicines for immune support and antiviral function. Harvested roots in Year 3+; flowers annually. Self-seeds readily. [32]

**Elderberry (Sambucus canadensis)** — Already listed in Section 2 as a food crop. Elder flower and berry have an established ethnobotanical and peer-reviewed track record for respiratory illness, with the berry extract showing antiviral activity in randomized controlled trials. The dual role (food + medicine) makes elderberry the highest-priority double-use species in the system.

**Valerian (Valeriana officinalis)** — Hardy to Zone 4. Root preparations used for sleep and anxiety. Harvest roots in fall of Year 2+. Widely naturalized in Zone 5 and largely self-sustaining once established.

**Comfrey (Symphytum officinale)** — Already listed as a functional plant for dynamic accumulation. Comfrey root preparations are traditional medicine for bruises, sprains, and wound healing (contains allantoin, which accelerates cell proliferation). Do not use comfrey internally — alkaloids are hepatotoxic — but topical applications are well-supported.

**Ginseng (Panax quinquefolius)** — Native American woodland species, shade-adapted, Zone 3–8. Slow-growing (roots harvested after 6–10 years) but extremely high-value medicinally and economically. Plant in deep shade beneath mature canopy. The Red Fern Farm system includes ginseng and goldenseal as understory crops in mature chestnut/pawpaw canopy. [8]
