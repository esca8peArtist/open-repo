## Section 1: Etsy Market Research — Demand by Bundle

### How the Market Is Structured

The medicinal herbs digital guide market on Etsy divides into three tiers with a gap between them that Phase 3 is positioned to occupy.

**Tier 1 (impulse-buy reference cards)**: Quick-reference sheets — herb property charts, apothecary labels, printable identification cards. Price range $3–$9. Review counts of 300–600 on established listings. These are volume plays targeting casual buyers. Seedwarden does not compete here.

**Tier 2 (PLR compilations)**: 60–180 page ebooks assembled from licensed resale content, covering 40–250 herbs. Price range $8–$18. Review counts 30–120. Buyer feedback consistently notes broad coverage, thin depth per herb. These are the price competitors that Phase 3 must be substantively differentiated from — not by adding more herbs, but by going deeper on fewer herbs with original cultivation content and sourcing ethics that PLR compilations cannot replicate.

**Tier 3 (practitioner-grade guides)**: Step-by-step herbology courses, guidebook sets, condition-specific themed bundles. Price range $18–$45. Review counts 30–150. This is the segment where Phase 3 competes. The most important finding from strategy research: no dominant review leader has emerged in this tier. The highest-reviewed practitioner-quality herb guides top out around 150–200 reviews. A well-positioned new entrant with 30–50 reviews and demonstrably superior cultivation depth can rank competitively because the gap between existing Tier 3 leaders and a well-listed new product is smaller than it is in more saturated Etsy categories.

### Bundle-Level Demand Analysis

The five planned bundles differ in demand signal strength, competition level, and seasonal timing. This affects both production priority and launch sequencing.

**Bundle 1: Women's Health Herbs Guide (SKU: MH-BUNDLE-WH-001, $22)**

Primary search terms: "women's health herbs guide," "herbal guide women," "black cohosh guide," "menopause herbs printable," "hormonal herbs guide."

Market signal: The women's health herbal category has consistent medium-high search volume year-round, with peaks in spring (hormonal balance interest after winter) and September (practitioners restocking educational resources before fall patient season). The black cohosh entry point is the strongest practitioner keyword — black cohosh is the most-sold menopausal herb in North America, yet almost no cultivation-inclusive digital guides address it. Search volume for "black cohosh guide" is lower than "elderberry guide" in absolute terms, but competition is much thinner. The practitioner buyer who searches for black cohosh is looking for something Etsy largely does not have.

Competition level: Low at the practitioner tier. Condition-specific women's health herbal bundles are nearly absent from Etsy at the $20–$25 price point with cultivation depth. Most listings are either recipe-focused (tinctures, teas) or cover 50+ herbs at PLR depth. The five-species cultivation + traditional use + conservation ethics format is uncontested.

Launch timing: September 2026 is correct. Clinical herbalists and integrative health practitioners build patient education libraries in August–September before fall clinical season. Gift buyers for wellness practitioners peak in November–December, making September the right date for review accumulation before the holiday window.

**Bundle 2: Respiratory Health Herbs Guide (SKU: MH-BUNDLE-RH-001, $20)**

Primary search terms: "respiratory herbs guide," "elderberry cultivation guide," "mullein herb guide," "echinacea growing guide," "cold season herb guide."

Market signal: Elderberry is the highest-searched medicinal herb keyword on Etsy. The vast majority of elderberry listings are physical products (dried berries, syrups, capsules) or recipe guides (how to make elderberry syrup). Cultivation-inclusive digital guides for elderberry as a shrub — how to establish it, which cultivars, coppicing for yield — are nearly absent. The Respiratory bundle leads with elderberry specifically because that keyword drives search traffic, then the guide differentiates by going where the recipe guides stop: the actual shrub.

Competition level: Very low for the cultivation framing. High for the recipe/remedy framing. The cultivation-inclusive angle is the search positioning that avoids competing with 10,000 elderberry syrup listings.

Launch timing: October 2026 is the correct window. Cold and flu season interest begins building in September; the buyer who acts in October plants in spring and is prepared the following winter. The guide sells year-round but peaks October–February.

**Bundle 3: Immunity Support Herbs Guide (SKU: MH-BUNDLE-IM-001, $22)**

Primary search terms: "immunity herbs guide," "echinacea cultivation," "ashwagandha growing guide," "adaptogen herbs pdf," "goldenseal herb guide."

Market signal: Ashwagandha is the fastest-growing adaptogen search term in the US herbal supplement market. Cultivation-inclusive ashwagandha content — how to grow it from rhizome, annual vs. perennial zone management, root harvest — is almost completely absent from Etsy digital downloads as of May 2026. This is the bundle's differentiation anchor. Echinacea drives baseline search volume; ashwagandha drives practitioner curiosity; goldenseal drives conservation-aware buyer engagement. The combination covers three distinct search entry points.

Competition level: Very low at the cultivation tier. Market signal: The sleep and nervous system support category is among the highest-growth wellness search categories in 2025–2026, driven by the burnout-recovery and anxiety management trend. ## Section 2: Grow-Guide Sourcing Strategy by Primary Species

### Research Infrastructure Available

Three tiers of sourcing authority exist for each species, and each has a distinct role in the guides.

**University extension and USDA sources**: These are the primary cite-by-name authorities for cultivation information. The forest-farming extension program (forest-farming.extension.org), NC State Extension publications, and USDA NIFA grant research provide peer-reviewed cultivation data that differentiates Seedwarden guides from PLR competitors. Key resources confirmed active as of May 2026:
- Forest Farming extension (forest-farming.extension.org): dedicated profiles for black cohosh and goldenseal with soil pH, shade, and propagation data
- NC State Extension: "Commercial Goldenseal Cultivation" and "Black Cohosh (*Actaea racemosa* L.)" both current
- USDA Forest Service: "Forest Production of Goldenseal" (agroforestry notes, public domain, citable)
- NC A&T State University: "Propagation and Forest Farming of Goldenseal and Black Cohosh" (NIFA grant research, citable)

**United Plant Savers**: The At-Risk species list (current 2025 publication), Forest Grown Verified program, and species profiles at unitedplantsavers.org are the primary cite-by-name conservation authorities. UpS species profiles are publicly available and explicitly citable in educational materials.

**Traditional medicine sources**: The German Commission E Monographs (Blumenthal et al.) and the American Botanical Council's HerbalGram are the primary evidence-based traditional medicine sources. Both are citable and are the authoritative reference for traditional use framing that avoids FTC disease claim exposure.

### Per-Species Sourcing Strategy

**Black Cohosh (Actaea racemosa) — Women's Health Bundle**

Grow-guide authority: NC State Extension "Black Cohosh (*Actaea racemosa* L.)," forest-farming.extension.org species profile, UpS At-Risk profile. Cultivation notes confirmed from these sources: pH 5–6, 70–80% shade minimum, woods-cultivated or wild-simulated systems, rhizome division propagation in fall, 3–5 year harvest timeline.

Conservation/licensing status: UpS At-Risk (not CITES, not ESA-listed nationally). Endangered in Massachusetts. No legal restriction on cultivated material anywhere in the US. The 95%+ wild-harvest rate of commercial supply is documented in UpS literature and is the basis for the conservation sidebar in the Women's Health bundle. Cultivated sources for guide citation: Prairie Moon Nursery (nursery-propagated, confirmed on product page), Strictly Medicinal Seeds (seeds and transplants), Fedco Seeds (spring catalog).

Traditional medicine sources: Cherokee traditional medicine (documented in multiple ethnobotanical sources), Appalachian Eclectic tradition, Lydia Pinkham's Vegetable Compound (historical context). German Commission E Monograph: black cohosh approved for climacteric complaints.

Sustainability note: Forest Grown Verified certification covers black cohosh; the guide should cite FGV as the gold standard while noting that any documented nursery-propagated source is adequate. Annual verification of Prairie Moon and Strictly Medicinal product pages is the maintenance task.

**Goldenseal (Hydrastis canadensis) — Immunity Support Bundle**

Grow-guide authority: NC State Extension "Commercial Goldenseal Cultivation," USDA FS "Forest Production of Goldenseal," forest-farming.extension.org goldenseal profile, NC A&T NIFA research.

Conservation/licensing status: CITES Appendix II since 1997. This is the highest legal complexity species in Phase 3. CITES restricts international trade in wild-harvested material; domestic commerce in cultivated material is entirely unrestricted. The practical guide implication: the CITES sidebar must appear in every goldenseal section, and all sourcing recommendations must specify cultivated origin. FGV participant directory (unitedplantsavers.org/forest-grown-verified) is the authoritative cultivated source directory. Prairie Moon Nursery and Strictly Medicinal Seeds both carry cultivated rhizome divisions. The guide recommends FGV-certified or nursery-propagated sources by program name and URL rather than specific seller lot numbers, which buffers against supplier change between guide publication and reader use.

Traditional medicine sources: Cherokee use for dyeing and wound treatment (documented in ethnobotany literature), 19th-century Eclectic medical tradition (extensively documented), German Commission E Monograph: goldenseal listed but with limited evidence base. Berberine content: isoquinoline alkaloid studied for antimicrobial and metabolic effects in laboratory and clinical settings — describe accurately, not as a product claim.

Sustainability note: Annual check of FGV participant list before each November Immunity bundle promotion cycle. If major supplier status changes, update the sourcing sidebar (estimated 2-hour revision). Log each verification in WORKLOG.md with date and URL.

**Elderberry (Sambucus nigra / S. canadensis) — Respiratory and Immunity Bundles**

Grow-guide authority: USDA PLANTS database, Prairie Moon Nursery cultivation notes, university extension shrub cultivation resources. Both S. nigra and S. canadensis are well-documented garden shrubs with abundant extension coverage.

Conservation/licensing status: No conservation concern. No CITES or UpS restriction. S. canadensis (native American elder) is abundant; S. nigra (European elder) is widely cultivated and naturalized. The guide content focuses on cultivar selection (Adams, Bob Gordon, York for American elder; Black Beauty, Black Lace for European) and coppicing management.

Traditional medicine sources: Hippocratic-era European use documented, Haudenosaunee and Anishinaabe traditional use documented. Sambucol randomized controlled trial (2016, Nutrients, PMID traceable via PubMed) is the primary evidence citation — describe result with qualifying language: "associated with reduced cold duration in one randomized controlled trial."

Photo note: Elderberry is among the best-photographed shrubs on Wikimedia Commons and iNaturalist. No direct contact sourcing required. See sourcing-guide.md for CC-BY-SA image catalog.

**Echinacea (E. purpurea and E. angustifolia) — Respiratory and Immunity Bundles**

Grow-guide authority: NC State Extension "Echinacea angustifolia" (Jeanine Davis, available as PDF from newcropsorganics.ces.ncsu.edu), University of Florida IFAS warm climate production guidelines, forest-farming.extension.org adjacent resources. The two-species comparison is the content angle that university extension explicitly covers — E. purpurea as the easy garden perennial and E. angustifolia as the conservation-significant prairie species.

Conservation/licensing status: UpS At-Risk (both species). No CITES restriction. E. angustifolia wild populations are depleted by commercial root harvest; cultivated sources (Prairie Moon, Strictly Medicinal Seeds) are the recommended citation. The five-year root harvest timeline for E. angustifolia versus three-year for E. purpurea is documented in extension literature and is a practitioner-relevant distinction no PLR guide includes.

Traditional medicine sources: Most heavily researched herb in the Western herbalism canon. Multiple systematic reviews and meta-analyses on immune outcomes documented in PubMed. Native American traditional use across Plains nations documented in ethnobotanical literature. German Commission E Monograph approved for supportive therapy in colds and respiratory infections.

**Valerian (Valeriana officinalis) — Sleep and Nervines Bundle**

Grow-guide authority: USDA PLANTS database, university extension perennial herb cultivation guides, Strictly Medicinal Seeds cultivation notes. Valerian is a Zone 4–9 perennial with standard extension coverage for division propagation and root harvest timing.

Conservation/licensing status: No conservation concern. Widely cultivated in North America and Europe; abundant in nursery trade. The cultivation content focus is on the second-year root harvest timing and low-temperature drying protocol — specific, actionable content not present in PLR guides.

Traditional medicine sources: European herbalism since at least the 16th century documented. German Commission E Monograph approved for restlessness and sleep disturbances. Multiple systematic reviews on sleep latency outcomes documented in PubMed — describe with qualifying language ("studied in clinical trials for sleep-related outcomes with mixed results across study populations").

Valerenic acid and GABA-related mechanism: describe as studied, not proven. The mechanism is contested in current research; accurate framing is "Contains valerenic acid, a compound studied in relation to GABA receptor activity."

**Ashwagandha (Withania somnifera) — Immunity Support Bundle**

Grow-guide authority: Strictly Medicinal Seeds (the primary US cultivation source for home growers; email info@strictlymedicinalseeds.com for educational photo and cultivation data use). USDA PLANTS database for range and classification. Springer Nature has published peer-reviewed cultivation chapters. The annual-in-northern-zones / perennial-in-zones-8–12 distinction is the content angle that no other Etsy guide covers — this is the practical cultivation knowledge gap.

Conservation/licensing status: No conservation concern. Commercially grown in India at large scale. For home cultivation guides, US-adapted seed sources (Strictly Medicinal Seeds) are the recommended citation. The grocery store rhizome caution (treated with growth inhibitors; not suitable for planting) is a specific, practical, differentiated content note.

Traditional medicine sources: Ayurvedic Rasayana tradition (approximately 3,000 years of documented use), Unani medicine, growing Western clinical trial base. Multiple RCTs on cortisol-related stress markers and sleep quality documented in PubMed. Describe as "withanolide-standardized extracts studied in randomized controlled trials for stress-related physiological markers" — not as a product claim.

Pregnancy and thyroid contraindications: both require explicit statement in the contraindications section. These are documented in the clinical literature and are mandatory inclusions.

**Passionflower (Passiflora incarnata) — Sleep and Nervines Bundle**

Grow-guide authority: USDA PLANTS database (native range eastern US, hardy to Zone 6), university extension resources for native vine cultivation. The maypop edibility note (the fruit is a documented wild food, passion-fruit flavor) is the forager cohort crossover content — buyers who identified maypop in the wild edibles catalog recognize it here.

Conservation/licensing status: No conservation concern. Native to southeastern US, naturalized to Midwest. Legal to cultivate and wild-harvest in all range states. The guide notes the underground runner spread behavior as a cultivation management consideration.

Traditional medicine sources: Cherokee and other southeastern nations traditional use documented. Appalachian folk medicine. 2011 RCT comparing passionflower to oxazepam for generalized anxiety (PubMed, PMID traceable) — describe result with qualifying language: "In one randomized controlled trial, passionflower was compared to a benzodiazepine for anxiolytic effects" — no efficacy claim.

**Lemon Balm (Melissa officinalis) — Sleep/Nervines and Digestive Bundles**

Grow-guide authority: University extension herb cultivation guides (available from multiple state extension programs). Lemon balm is among the easiest-to-source cultivation information; the content differentiation is the dual-bundle framing (nervine in the Sleep bundle, carminative in the Digestive bundle) and the harvest timing for volatile oil content.

Conservation/licensing status: No concern. Spreads freely; widely available from all herb nurseries.

Traditional medicine sources: European herbalism since the Middle Ages (Paracelsus, Gerard documented). German Commission E Monograph approved for nervous agitation and sleep disorders. Dual use (nervine and carminative) documented in historical sources — this is a teaching point about functional herb categories.

Thyroid contraindication: lemon balm may inhibit TSH binding; individuals on thyroid medication require practitioner consultation. This is documented in the clinical literature and mandatory in the contraindications section.

**Dandelion (Taraxacum officinale) — Digestive Support Bundle**

Grow-guide authority: Existing wild-edibles catalog (sourcing-guide.md notes dandelion images already in archive from Session 560). The cultivation content extension needed for Phase 3 is specifically root-focused: taproot variety selection ('Thick-Leaved'), deep loose soil requirements, and fall harvest for maximum inulin content.

Conservation/licensing status: No concern. Introduced species; cosmopolitan; not at risk. Legal to harvest from unsprayed sites everywhere.

Traditional medicine sources: German Commission E Monograph approved for stimulation of diuresis and as a digestive bitter. Pu Gong Ying in Chinese medicine documented. Taraxacin (bitter sesquiterpene lactone) and inulin (prebiotic fiber) content documented in botanical chemistry literature.

**Ginger (Zingiber officinale) — Digestive Support Bundle**

Grow-guide authority: Strictly Medicinal Seeds carries untreated planting rhizomes and provides cultivation notes. The grocery store rhizome limitation (growth inhibitors, may not sprout) is again the differentiating content note. USDA PLANTS database for zone range.

Traditional medicine sources: Documented in Chinese medicine, Ayurveda, Greek-Roman antiquity (Dioscorides), European folk medicine. Ginger has the most consistent research support for nausea outcomes of any herb in the Digestive bundle — multiple RCTs on pregnancy nausea, chemotherapy-associated nausea, and postoperative nausea documented in PubMed. Describe accurately: "studied in multiple randomized controlled trials for nausea associated with pregnancy, chemotherapy, and postoperative recovery."

**Remaining species** (Vitex, Red Clover, Calendula, Lavender, Mullein, Thyme):

All have abundant university extension coverage, no conservation concerns, and established traditional medicine documentation. The sourcing task for these species during the June 22 – July 13 window is photo sourcing and supplier verification rather than primary research. See phase-3-medicinal-herbs-sourcing-guide.md for photo sourcing paths and lead times per species.

## Section 3: Photography Requirements

### States Needed Per Species

Each guide requires photographs in multiple plant states to serve the practical cultivation reader. The priority sequence below reflects what actually informs growing decisions and what differentiates guide quality from PLR competitors.

The content-outline.md specifies distinct photo sequences per species (see Part 1: Photo Sourcing by Species in sourcing-guide.md). The states needed across the full Phase 3 species list organize into five categories:

**Seedling/juvenile state**: Required for any species where the new grower needs to recognize the plant before it reaches its mature and distinctive form. Priority species: passionflower (resembles many other vines as a seedling), ashwagandha (resembles a tomato seedling when started indoors), echinacea purpurea (first-year vegetative rosette before flowering), valerian (juvenile pinnate leaves before the tall flowering stalk).

**Mature vegetative state (pre-flower habit)**: The full-plant habit photograph used for identification and as the listing thumbnail anchor. Required for all 16 primary species. The naming convention in sourcing-guide.md is `[species-slug]-habit.jpg`. These are the Working Task photos currently at 0/18 complete.

**Flowering state**: Required for species where the flower is the primary recognition feature or has harvest significance. Priority species: passionflower (the listing thumbnail; unmistakable), calendula (bright orange is the visual anchor for two bundles), echinacea purpurea (purple coneflower; highest recognition), elderberry (elderflower for forager crossover content), black cohosh (white bottle-brush raceme distinctive and rare in guides), mullein (six-foot spike is the strongest recognition image for the Respiratory bundle).

**Drying and post-harvest state**: Required for any species where the preparation method is a harvest outcome. Priority species: valerian root (pungent dried root is the medicinal part — omitting this image is the gap most herb guides have), black cohosh root (rhizome cross-section), lavender (dried bundles hanging), mullein leaf (dried for tea preparation), dandelion root (dried for roasted root coffee preparation). Secondary: elderberries drying, calendula petals for oil infusion.

**Root/rhizome dug state**: Required for the root-harvest species where the underground portion is the medicinal part. Priority species: valerian (root dug, washed, showing the diagnostic earthy odor note in text), goldenseal (yellow rhizome interior that gives the plant its name — this is the conservation story visual), dandelion (root dug and split for bitters preparation), ginger (rhizome showing growth buds for planting guide), echinacea (root of each species side-by-side for the two-species comparison).

## Section 4: Production Timeline — Critical Path Analysis

### What the June 22 – July 13 Window Actually Is

The PHASE_3_PRODUCTION_TIMELINE.md establishes June 22 – July 13 as falling inside the Research Phase (June 1 – July 31, 8 weeks). This window is not a writing sprint — it is the research and photo sourcing acceleration that unblocks the August writing sprint. Understanding this distinction is essential for realistic capacity planning.

The 64–74 development hours estimated in the content-outline.md are writing hours for the five final PDF guides, not including research, photo sourcing, Canva production, or platform staging. The full June 22 – July 13 window task list draws from all four resource categories.

### Weekly Production Capacity

The existing production timeline assumes 10–12 available hours per week during the August–October peak writing period. For the June 22 – July 13 research window (a lighter capacity assumption of 4–5 hours per week from PHASE_3_PRODUCTION_TIMELINE.md Section 6.1), the realistic output per week is:

- Photo sourcing: 6–8 species per week at 1–2 hours per species (Wikimedia search, license confirmation, download, WORKLOG.md logging)
- Direct outreach: 2–3 outreach emails per week; responses arrive in 2–4 weeks (outreach sent in this window yields responses in time for August writing)
- Research notes: 2–3 species research compilations per week at 1–2 hours per species
- Supplier verification: 3–5 supplier page checks per week at 15–30 minutes each

At 4–5 hours per week over 3 weeks (June 22 – July 13), the realistic throughput is 12–15 hours of productive research and sourcing work. This is enough to complete photo sourcing for 8–12 species, send all required outreach emails, and compile research notes for the 5 Tier 1 priority species (black cohosh, elderberry, echinacea, valerian, dandelion).

### Bottleneck Analysis

Three bottlenecks appear consistently across the PHASE_3_PRODUCTION_TIMELINE.md analysis and the content structure established in the outlines.

**Primary bottleneck: SME review cycle.** The practitioner buyer validates guide quality partly by whether the content reflects expert review. Sourcing 3–5 herbalist reviewers for the June–August window requires outreach in early June, not late July. The PHASE_3_PRODUCTION_TIMELINE.md mitigation strategy — identify SMEs in May, pre-vet responsiveness, offer $50–100 honorarium for priority response — applies in full. For the June 22 – July 13 window, the specific action is confirming SME schedules for August 13–16 review dates and sending draft Women's Health species outlines 5 days before the first scheduled review call.

**Second bottleneck: Canva template lock.** The content-outline.md identifies a one-time 4–6 hour Canva template build from the Phase 1/Phase 2 house style, then 4–6 hours per bundle for adaptation (color palette, photography, section headers). The template must be locked before writing begins — not after — to avoid content revisions triggering design revisions. Template lock deadline: July 31. If Canva is not locked by July 31, August writing risks producing content that does not fit the template, requiring rewrites.

**Third bottleneck: Photo availability for conservation-significant species.** Black cohosh, goldenseal, and echinacea angustifolia all require either institutional outreach or iNaturalist filtering to find CC-BY images with adequate habitat context. These species have the thinnest Wikimedia coverage in the Phase 3 list. The outreach sent in the June 22 – July 13 window is the mechanism for having these images available for the August Canva production sprint. Images received after August 1 create Canva production delays.

### How Many Guides Per Week Can Production Handle

The PHASE_3_PRODUCTION_TIMELINE.md writing velocity estimates, corrected for the content-outline.md word counts:

- At 500 words/hour: 3,500–3,800 word bundle guide requires 7–7.6 hours of writing time per species section, or approximately 35–40 hours per bundle if all five species are written fresh
- Shared species (lemon balm appears in both Sleep and Digestive; lavender appears in Women's Health and Sleep; elderberry appears in both Respiratory and Immunity; echinacea appears in both Respiratory and Immunity; calendula appears in both Women's Health and Digestive): the content-outline.md specifies condensed re-use with framing shifts, saving approximately 30–40% writing time on the second bundle for each shared species

At 10 hours of writing per week (August peak): approximately 0.25 bundles per week, or one complete bundle every 4 weeks. Five bundles at this rate requires 20 weeks of sustained writing. The actual schedule compresses this because (a) shared species reduce duplication, (b) research notes are pre-compiled during the June–July window, and (c) the front matter, preparation methods, practitioner context, and seasonal timing sections are templated and reusable across bundles.

Realistic writing velocity with pre-compiled research notes and template infrastructure: 1 bundle per 3 weeks at 10–12 hours per week. Five bundles: 15 weeks. The content-outline.md 64–74 hour estimate aligns with this — 13–15 hours per bundle average, consistent with 3-week per bundle cadence.

The decision facing the user before Phase 2 launch is not whether Phase 3 is viable — the market research, sourcing strategy, content outlines, and Etsy listing templates confirm viability. The decision is about scope and timing.

Requirement: Phase 2 launches successfully May 30 and Day 16 metrics (June 15) show forager cohort >20% and conversion >1.5%.

The research effort is not wasted — it is valid regardless of Phase 2 performance — but the August writing investment (40+ hours) should not begin without the June 15 gate confirmation.

**Option B: Reduced scope, June 22 start**

Proceed with three bundles (Women's Health, Respiratory, Immunity) — the original June 15 Wave 1 targets from PHASE_3_PRODUCTION_TIMELINE.md. Defer Sleep/Nervines and Digestive Support until August gate confirmation.

Trade-off: Digestive bundle forager crossover value is lost in 2026; must be rebuilt in Q1 2027.

**Option C: Deferred start, no June 22 action**

Defer Phase 3 research window to August 1. No June 22 – July 13 activity.

Impact: September 2026 Women's Health launch is infeasible. October 2026 becomes the earliest target for the first bundle, compressing the review accumulation window before the November–December holiday peak. Revenue delay: approximately 6 weeks.

**Recommended decision path**: Authorize Option A (full scope, June 22 start) for the research window, with the August 1 writing sprint conditional on the June 15 gate. The research hours (12–15 hours in June–July) are low-cost insurance regardless of Phase 2 outcome. The writing hours (40+ hours in August) are authorized only after the June 15 gate confirms Phase 2 is performing.

This separates the low-cost, low-risk research investment (June 22 – July 13) from the higher-cost, higher-commitment writing investment (August), giving you a June 15 decision point on the writing authorization rather than committing to everything from May 30.
