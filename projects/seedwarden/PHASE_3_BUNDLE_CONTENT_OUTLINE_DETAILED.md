## Part 1: Structural Template — Shared Across All Five Bundles

Every bundle follows this eight-section template. The template exists because buyers who purchase multiple bundles should be able to navigate them without reorientation. A buyer who reads the Women's Health bundle and then purchases the Respiratory bundle already knows how to find the contraindications section.

### Section Architecture (all bundles)

```
1. Front Matter (80–100 words)
   - Copyright + disclaimer page
   - FTC educational use statement (identical language, every bundle)
   - Practitioner note (10-pack license reference)

2. Introduction (180–220 words)
   - Bundle framing (why these specific plants together)
   - Conservation thread (name the at-risk species upfront)
   - Reader orientation (how the guide is organized)
   - Seasonal hook (when this guide is most actionable)

3. Species Sections (varies by bundle — see per-bundle detail below)
   Per-species structure:
   3.1 Identification and Botanical Description
   3.2 Range and Habitat
   [CONSERVATION SIDEBAR — inserted here for at-risk species only]
   3.3 Cultivation
   3.4 Harvest and Preparation
   3.5 Active Constituents
   3.6 Traditional and Historical Use
   3.7 Contraindications

4. Preparation Methods (180–210 words)
   - 3–5 practical preparations applicable across the bundle's species
   - Process descriptions only — no dosage information

5. Practitioner Context (140–160 words)
   - Why these herbs matter to the RN/NP/clinical herbalist buyer
   - Reference to patient education use
   - Cross-reference to AHG and ABC resources

6. Seasonal Timing (90–110 words)
   - Month-by-month planting and harvest calendar
   - Zone 5 as the baseline (coldest zone in the primary audience range)
   - Note zone 3 and zone 8 variations for key species

7. Cross-Reference Notes (50–80 words)
   - Which herbs appear in other Seedwarden bundles
   - One-line description of the framing difference per bundle
   - Cross-sell prompt at natural reader decision point

8. Sources (80–120 words)
   - All cited sources listed by URL or PMID
   - No footnotes — inline citation or back-of-guide list
   - Minimum 6 sources per bundle
```

### FTC Compliance Language — Standardized Blocks

These four blocks are used verbatim across all bundles. Copy and paste; do not paraphrase.

**Block A — Disclaimer page (Front Matter)**
"This guide is for educational and informational purposes only. It is not intended as medical advice, diagnosis, or treatment. The information describes traditional and historical uses and does not constitute a health claim. Consult a qualified healthcare provider before using any herb for therapeutic purposes."

**Block B — Active constituents framing opener**
"[Plant name] contains [constituent name(s)], compound(s) that have been studied in [laboratory / clinical / in vitro] settings for [descriptive research area — not efficacy claim]."

**Block C — Traditional use framing opener**
"Traditionally used in [cultural tradition] for [application area]. This describes historical use and does not constitute a treatment claim."

**Block D — Contraindications header**
"The following section describes traditional cautions and research-identified interactions. It does not constitute medical advice. Consult a qualified healthcare provider before therapeutic use."

---

### Writing Approach — Shared Principles

**Voice**: Second person ("you") for practical sections (cultivation, harvest, preparation). Third person for historical and research sections ("Cherokee healers used," "a 2016 RCT found"). Do not mix voices within a section.

**Depth signal**: The sentence "this is more complex than most guides acknowledge" should be true for at least one claim per species. Identify the one place where standard PLR gets the species wrong or oversimplifies — that sentence earns the guide's premium positioning.

**Verification protocol**: Every constituent name, USDA zone claim, and conservation status should be verified before writing. The medicinal-herbs-candidate-list.md documents conservation status for all 12 primary species. USDA PLANTS confirms zone claims.

**Quality gate**: Before moving to the next section, the writer reads the contraindications section aloud. If it sounds like a marketing brochure, it needs to be more clinical. The contraindications section is the liability-critical section. It is never an afterthought.

## Part 2: Bundle 1 — Women's Health Herbs Guide

**SKU**: MH-BUNDLE-WH-001
**Upload target**: June 29, 2026
**Price**: $22 consumer / $120–130 practitioner 10-pack
**Word target**: 3,700–3,900 words
**Species**: Black Cohosh (700), Vitex (600), Red Clover (400), Calendula (400), Lavender (400)
**Sprint days**: D1–D7, June 22–28

### Writing Workflow — Section by Section

**Front Matter + Introduction (300 words combined — Sprint D1, first 30 min)**

Approach: Write the introduction last if necessary — the conservation thread (Black Cohosh is the hook) should reference specific details from the Black Cohosh section. But draft it first as a placeholder and revise on the D7 self-edit pass. Mandatory: name the conservation problem in the second paragraph. Readers who bought this bundle because they want to grow Black Cohosh need to understand the sourcing stakes immediately.

Quality gate: Does the introduction explain what makes this bundle different from a supplement buying guide? If not, add one sentence.

**Black Cohosh — 700 words (Sprint D1, ~2 hours)**

Section-by-section word allocation:
- Identification: 100 words — describe the bottle-brush raceme, compound leaves, three-to-six foot height in flower. Note the toxic lookalikes (Actaea pachypoda, A. rubra) explicitly.
- Conservation sidebar: 150 words — the 95% wild-harvest statistic is load-bearing. Include the Forest Grown Verified (FGV) program reference and three specific cultivated-source nurseries (Prairie Moon, Strictly Medicinal Seeds, Fedco).
- Range and Habitat: 50 words — eastern North America, Appalachian core range, mature deciduous forest understory.
- Cultivation: 150 words — shade minimum (70–80%), soil pH, rhizome division as the practical planting path, three-to-five year timeline to first harvestable root. The long-timeline detail is the one most PLR guides omit.
- Harvest and Preparation: 80 words — fall harvest after die-back, low-temperature drying, glass jar storage.
- Active Constituents: 60 words — triterpenoid glycosides (actein, cimicifugoside). The contested phytoestrogenic mechanism must be noted — do not perpetuate the misconception that Black Cohosh is estrogenic when the triterpene mechanism has stronger research support.
- Traditional Use: 80 words — Cherokee use framing, Appalachian folk medicine, Eclectic tradition, Lydia Pinkham historical context.
- Contraindications: 80 words — pregnancy, liver concerns (case reports), tamoxifen interaction.

Mandatory content that cannot be omitted: (1) the 95% wild-harvest statistic, (2) the FGV program URL, (3) the three toxic lookalike species names, (4) the three-to-five year root harvest timeline.

**Vitex — 600 words (Sprint D2, ~1.7 hours)**

Writing angle: The "Chaste Tree" naming history is the entry point. The monk story (the plant was used by monastery residents who believed it suppressed libido) is historically documented and provides a framing hook that no PLR guide develops. From there: modern mechanism (dopaminergic pituitary prolactin regulation) is what practitioners actually want to know.

Mandatory content: (1) The invasive watch list status in Tennessee and North Carolina — this is a liability issue if omitted and a buyer plants Vitex without knowing it. (2) Hardy zone range (Zone 6 perennial; Zone 5 with protection). (3) The MAOI and oral contraceptive interaction in the contraindications section.

**Red Clover — 400 words (Sprint D2, ~1.1 hours)**

Writing angle: Red Clover is the accessibility species in this bundle — it is easy to grow, edible (forager crossover point: the flowers are edible raw), and widely available. The isoflavone content connects it to the Women's Health theme with documented phytoestrogenic activity. Keep this section practical and brief.

Mandatory content: (1) Distinction from white clover (Trifolium repens) — the pink flower and larger size are the identification markers. (2) The four isoflavone names (formononetin, biochanin A, daidzein, genistein) in the active constituents section. (3) Note that Red Clover self-sows readily and can be considered semi-permanent in a garden patch.

**Calendula — 400 words (Sprint D3, ~1.1 hours)**

Writing angle: Calendula is the gateway herb for this bundle. If someone has never grown a medicinal herb before, this is where they start. Keep the identification and cultivation sections practical and direct. The salve preparation goes in the Preparation Methods section, not here.

Mandatory content: (1) The self-sowing nature — once established, Calendula largely maintains itself with minimal intervention. (2) Asteraceae allergy note in contraindications. (3) Topical vs. internal use distinction — the Women's Health bundle covers the topical anti-inflammatory applications; the Digestive bundle will cover the internal tea application.

**Lavender — 400 words (Sprint D3, ~1.1 hours)**

Writing angle: Three cultivar differentiation is the depth content. Most buyers know lavender but do not know the difference between Hidcote (compact, deep purple, best for small gardens), Munstead (hardiest English lavender, Zone 5), and Phenomenal (largest, Zone 4b, most productive). This is specific, actionable, verifiable information that no PLR guide includes.

Mandatory content: (1) The three cultivar names and their zone hardiness. (2) "Do not amend with organic matter" — lavender prefers lean, dry conditions; this is counterintuitive and buyers get it wrong. (3) Do not ingest essential oil — safety note for the aromatherapy user.

**Preparation Methods — 200 words (Sprint D3, folded into shared sections work)**

Standard infusion, cold infusion (for aromatics like lavender and calendula), tincture basics (overview only), infused oil process. Note that the infused oil is a cosmetic preparation — FTC implications differ from dietary supplement preparations.

**Cross-Reference Notes for Women's Health**

- Lavender appears in the Sleep bundle: there, the framing is aromatherapy and linalool research for sleep support. Here, the framing is topical application and the Women's Health traditional use context.
- Calendula appears in the Digestive bundle: there, the framing is internal tea for mucosal support. Here, the framing is topical salve and lymphatic support.
- Black Cohosh has a condensed appearance in the Immunity bundle (supporting species): cross-reference note points readers to this bundle for the full forest-farming cultivation guide.

## Part 3: Bundle 2 — Respiratory Health Herbs Guide

**SKU**: MH-BUNDLE-RH-001
**Upload target**: July 6–7, 2026
**Price**: $20 consumer / $120–130 practitioner 10-pack
**Word target**: 3,400–3,700 words
**Species**: Elderberry (800), Mullein (600), Echinacea (600), Thyme (500)
**Sprint days**: D4–D7 overlapping with Women's Health + D8 (June 25–July 1)

### Writing Workflow — Section by Section

**Introduction (200 words) — seasonal timing is the organizing principle**

The October–February demand window shapes the introduction framing. A buyer reading in August or September is in the optimal planning window; a buyer reading in January when they are already sick is too late for cultivation decisions but not too late to plan ahead. Both buyer moments are real and should be acknowledged. The introduction earns the guide's authority by naming the problem: the supplement aisle in October is crowded, expensive, and opaque about sourcing. Growing your own — or knowing the sourcing chain — is the alternative.

**Elderberry — 800 words (Sprint D4, ~2.3 hours)**

This is the longest species section in the Respiratory bundle because Elderberry has the most evidence, the most cultivation complexity, and the highest buyer interest of the four species.

Writing angle: The elderflower-to-elderberry lifecycle is the structural hook. Most buyers know elderberry syrup; fewer know that the same shrub produces edible flowers in June (forager crossover) and berries in August-September. The guide teaches the whole plant, not just the berry.

Section word allocation:
- Identification: 100 words — flat-topped flower clusters, dark purple berries, compound leaves with 5–9 leaflets. Red elderberry distinction (non-flat cluster, toxic raw berries) is mandatory.
- Cultivation: 200 words — bare-root establishment, coppicing method, cross-pollination requirement for yield, three cultivar recommendations (Adams, Bob Gordon, York) with zone data.
- Elderflower section: 80 words — culinary use, harvest timing for fragrance, the forager connection.
- Harvest and Processing: 150 words — sambunigrin toxicity in raw berries (mandatory: always cook), fork-stripping method, syrup basic process, dehydrator drying.
- Active Constituents: 80 words — anthocyanins (cyanidin glycosides), quercetin, rutin. The 2016 Sambucol RCT in Nutrients is the key citation.
- Traditional Use: 80 words — European history, Native American (Haudenosaunee, Anishinaabe), Appalachian folk medicine, elderflower in 19th-century pharmacopeia.
- Contraindications: 80 words — raw berry toxicity (the most important safety note in this bundle), immunosuppressive drug interaction caution for transplant patients.

Mandatory content: (1) Sambunigrin and the "always cook" rule. (2) The red elderberry distinction. (3) Cross-pollination note. (4) The 2016 Nutrients RCT citation with qualifying language.

**Mullein — 600 words (Sprint D5, ~1.7 hours)**

Writing angle: The biennial lifecycle is the depth that differentiates. Year-one rosette (flat, silvery, large soft leaves) is unambiguously identifiable. Year-two spike (four to eight feet tall) is the harvest and observation year. Most buyers plant mullein not knowing it will not flower in its first year — setting that expectation is a practical service.

Mandatory content: (1) The year-one vs. year-two distinction — most essential cultivation fact for this species. (2) Wild harvest legality: mullein is naturalized across most of North America; in states where it is a listed noxious weed, wild harvest is legal and encouraged. (3) Filter the leaf tea through fine cloth — the leaf hairs irritate mucous membranes. (4) The ear oil preparation is an oleic acid maceration, not a pharmaceutical preparation — important for FTC framing.

**Echinacea — 600 words (Sprint D5, ~1.7 hours)**

Writing angle: The two-species structure (E. purpurea vs. E. angustifolia) is the entire differentiation of the Echinacea section in this bundle. Most guides treat them as interchangeable. They are not: different growing requirements, different plant-part concentration of active compounds, different conservation status. A buyer who understands this distinction can make better sourcing decisions.

Mandatory content: (1) E. angustifolia conservation sidebar (UpS At-Risk, prairie overharvest). (2) Growing conditions comparison: E. purpurea is a garden perennial tolerating average soil; E. angustifolia requires lean, well-drained, prairie-mimicking conditions. (3) Root harvest timeline: three years for E. purpurea, five years for E. angustifolia. (4) Autoimmune contraindication.

**Thyme — 500 words (Sprint D6, ~1.4 hours)**

Writing angle: Thyme is the transition from wild-forager species to culinary-medicinal species. Most buyers already grow thyme. The guide extends their relationship with a familiar plant into the respiratory tradition — a familiar herb reframed in historical medical context.

Mandatory content: (1) Harvest timing at or just before flowering for peak volatile oil content. (2) Thymol concentration note — culinary and tea preparations are safe; concentrated essential oil is not for ingestion. (3) Pregnancy caution at medicinal doses (emmenagogue effect documented at large amounts).

**Cross-Reference Notes for Respiratory**

- Echinacea appears in the Immunity bundle: there, the framing shifts from respiratory support to general immune function and root harvest for immune applications. Full two-species comparison lives in this (Respiratory) bundle; the Immunity bundle condenses to about 40% of this section's length.
- Elderberry appears in the Immunity bundle: there, the anthocyanin and flavonoid content connects to immune modulation framing. The fermentation preservation note (elderberry vinegar, elderberry mead) appears in the Immunity bundle, not here.

## Part 4: Bundle 3 — Immunity Support Herbs Guide

**SKU**: MH-BUNDLE-IM-001
**Upload target**: July 20, 2026
**Price**: $22 consumer / $130 practitioner 10-pack
**Word target**: 3,700–3,900 words
**Species**: Echinacea condensed (600), Ashwagandha (900), Elderberry condensed (400), Goldenseal (900)
**Sprint days**: D8–D11 (June 29–July 2)

### Writing Workflow — Section by Section

**Introduction (200 words) — the most practitioner-oriented introduction of the five bundles**

The introduction should name the practitioner use case explicitly: these are the four herbs patients are most commonly self-prescribing in the immune support category. A registered nurse or nurse practitioner who sees patients using echinacea and goldenseal without adequate sourcing knowledge is the direct target reader of this introduction paragraph. Frame the guide as a cultivation and sourcing knowledge resource, not as a treatment guide.

**Echinacea condensed — 600 words (Sprint D8, ~1.7 hours)**

This section covers the same species as the Respiratory bundle but with immunity framing foregrounded. The structural difference: in the Respiratory bundle, the two-species comparison is the organizing principle throughout all subsections. In this bundle, the identification and cultivation comparison is condensed to one combined subsection. The unique content in this version is: root harvest timing for immune applications (specifically, harvesting in fall when the plant concentrates compounds in the root before dormancy) and a cross-reference note directing full two-species cultivation content to the Respiratory bundle.

Cross-reference note placement: "For the full cultivation comparison of E. purpurea and E. angustifolia, including the conservation sidebar for E. angustifolia, see the Respiratory Health Herbs Guide."

**Ashwagandha — 900 words (Sprint D9, ~2.6 hours)**

This is the highest-word-count section in the entire five-bundle set for a single species in a non-primary bundle. The allocation is justified: Ashwagandha has the strongest adaptogen evidence base of any herb in the portfolio, the largest practitioner search interest, and the least accurate cultivation content in existing Etsy guides (most of which either ignore cultivation entirely or give incorrect zone information).

Section word allocation:
- Identification and habitat: 100 words — woody perennial in zones 8–12, annual in zones 5–7. The winter cherry papery calyx around the red berries is diagnostic. Resemblance to a tomato seedling at the indoor start stage is a useful practical note.
- Ayurvedic context: 100 words — Rasayana classification (three-thousand-year tradition), what Rasayana means as a pharmacological category. Use "traditionally classified as" language throughout.
- Cultivation: 200 words — the zone 5–7 annual path (start indoors 8–10 weeks before last frost, transplant to full sun, harvest root in fall) vs. the zone 8–12 perennial path. The grocery store rhizome problem: most grocery store ginger rhizome analogy applies here — store-bought ashwagandha root is often treated and will not sprout. Source from Strictly Medicinal Seeds.
- Active Constituents: 150 words — withanolides (steroidal lactones unique to Withania genus) are the primary studied compounds. Multiple independent RCTs — not just in vitro data — on cortisol-related stress markers, thyroid function, and sleep quality. Frame each with Block B compliance language. The breadth of study (stress, sleep, thyroid) means the guide describes each research area distinctly rather than conflating them.
- Traditional Use: 100 words — Ayurvedic and Unani medicine traditions. The Unani (Islamic/Persian traditional medicine) angle is rarely included in English-language herb guides and is a genuine depth signal.
- Contraindications: 100 words — this is the most dense contraindications section in the portfolio. Four specific categories: (1) Pregnancy (absolute contraindication, traditionally and clinically). (2) Thyroid conditions and thyroid medication (ashwagandha measurably affects thyroid hormone levels). (3) Autoimmune conditions. (4) Drug interactions: sedatives, immunosuppressants, thyroid medications. All four must appear.
- Sourcing note: 80 words — domestically grown options (Strictly Medicinal Seeds), bulk dried root with USDA Organic certification (Mountain Rose Herbs).

Mandatory content: (1) All four contraindication categories. (2) The grocery store rhizome note. (3) The Unani medicine reference. (4) The thyroid effect explained — not just noted.

**Elderberry condensed — 400 words (Sprint D10, ~1.1 hours)**

Condense from Respiratory bundle with immunity framing foregrounded. New content unique to this version: the fermentation preservation note (elderberry vinegar for long-term preservation), and the immune-modulation framing of anthocyanins specifically (rather than the cold/flu duration framing in the Respiratory bundle). Cross-reference note: "For the full cultivation guide including cultivar selection and elderflower uses, see the Respiratory Health Herbs Guide."

**Goldenseal — 900 words (Sprint D10–D11, ~2.6 hours)**

This is tied with Ashwagandha as the longest single-species section in the portfolio. The CITES Appendix II sidebar is the reason: it requires 200 words of legally and historically precise text that no other species requires.

Section word allocation:
- Identification: 100 words — low woodland perennial, two large palmate leaves, single white spring flower, red raspberry-like fruit, yellow-orange diagnostic rhizome.
- CITES Legal Sidebar: 200 words (mandatory, verbatim structure):
  - CITES Appendix II since 1997
  - What this means: international trade in wild-harvested rhizome requires export/import permits
  - What it does not restrict: domestic US commerce in cultivated goldenseal is entirely legal
  - The market problem: 95%+ of commercial goldenseal raw material is still wild-harvested despite the listing
  - The action: FGV-certified, nursery-propagated, or documented cultivated-source only
  - Three specific sources: Prairie Moon Nursery, Strictly Medicinal Seeds, UpS FGV participant directory URL
- Cultivation: 200 words — the most demanding cultivation in the portfolio. Shade requirement (70–80%), rich moist humus-heavy soil, rhizome division planting, 4–5 year harvest timeline, companion planting with bloodroot and wild ginger in a forest garden.
- Active Constituents: 150 words — berberine (isoquinoline alkaloid, also found in barberry and Oregon grape). Studied in laboratory and clinical settings for antimicrobial activity and blood glucose regulation. The guide explicitly notes it makes no antimicrobial claim for goldenseal as a product — berberine content is a constituent description.
- Traditional Use: 100 words — Cherokee dye plant and wound treatment, Eclectic medical tradition (19th century), mucosal conditions framing.
- Contraindications: 100 words — pregnancy (absolute: berberine is uterotonic), newborns and infants, cytochrome P450 drug interactions (wide range of pharmaceuticals), long-term continuous use, jaundice/liver conditions.

Mandatory content: (1) Full CITES sidebar (200 words). (2) The 95% wild-harvest statistic. (3) All contraindication categories — goldenseal's contraindication list is the most extensive in the portfolio. (4) The three specific cultivated-source suppliers.

## Part 5: Bundle 4 — Sleep and Nervines Herbs Guide

**SKU**: MH-BUNDLE-SN-001
**Upload target**: July 13, 2026
**Price**: $20 consumer / $120–130 practitioner 10-pack
**Word target**: 3,300–3,600 words
**Species**: Valerian (800), Passionflower (700), Lemon Balm (500), Lavender condensed (500)
**Sprint days**: D12–D14 (July 3–5)

### Writing Workflow — Section by Section

**Introduction (200 words) — January framing, burnout recovery season**

The January upload timing shapes this introduction. The nervine category needs a brief definition — most buyers know "sleep herb" but not the word "nervine." Define it plainly: herbs traditionally used to support the nervous system, typically through calming effects. The visual anchor of the bundle (Passionflower's flower) should be earned in the introduction — one sentence that names what the plant actually is before the section dives into it.

**Valerian — 800 words (Sprint D12, ~2.3 hours)**

Writing angle: The smell contrast is the entry point. The fresh flowers smell pleasant; dried valerian root smells pungent, earthy, and unmistakably medicinal. Most buyers who have taken valerian supplement capsules have encountered the smell. The identification section uses that as a reader hook — "the flower smells nothing like the supplement."

Mandatory content: (1) Second-year root harvest only — planting valerian expecting a first-year harvest is the most common buyer mistake. (2) WWI "shell shock" historical use — this is a documented historical application that gives the nervine framing historical depth and is rarely included in herb guides. (3) The sedative drug interaction is the most important contraindication in this bundle — do not combine with prescription sedatives, benzodiazepines, or anesthetics. This warning appears twice: in the contraindications section and in the preparation methods section when tincture preparation is described. (4) The impractical-to-use-before-driving note — valerian can cause next-morning grogginess.

**Passionflower — 700 words (Sprint D13, ~2 hours)**

Writing angle: Passionflower is the thumbnail species. The flower is visually extraordinary — the complexity of the radially symmetric structure with white petals, purple filament corona, and central staminal column cannot be summarized, only described. The cultivation guide extends that visual appeal into practical gardening instruction. The maypop (the fruit) is the forager crossover moment — buyers who already forage in the southeastern US may have eaten maypop without knowing it; this is the plant.

Mandatory content: (1) The MAOI interaction warning — passionflower flavonoids have monoamine oxidase inhibitory activity; combining with MAOI antidepressants is contraindicated. This is the highest-severity drug interaction in the Sleep bundle. (2) The Zone 6–10 perennial / Zone 4–5 annual distinction. (3) The trellis requirement — passionflower is a vine; buyers who plant it without a support structure lose the harvest and break the plant. (4) Pregnancy contraindication (uterine-stimulating at high doses).

**Lemon Balm — 500 words (Sprint D14, ~1.4 hours)**

This is a condensed section that appears in both the Sleep and Digestive bundles. The Sleep bundle version foregrounds the nervine application; the Digestive bundle version foregrounds the carminative application. The overlap is a teaching point: a plant classified as a nervine (calming the nervous system) and a carminative (easing digestive tension) illustrates how herbal categories are functional rather than strictly botanical.

Mandatory content: (1) TSH binding inhibition — lemon balm may inhibit TSH binding at the thyroid receptor; individuals with thyroid conditions or on thyroid medication must consult a practitioner. This is not commonly known and is the most clinically relevant lemon balm safety note. (2) The spreading by underground runners note — lemon balm requires a contained space. Buyers who plant it in a garden bed without containment will spend subsequent years managing it.

**Lavender condensed — 500 words (Sprint D14, ~1.4 hours)**

The Sleep bundle version of the Lavender section foregrounds the linalool aromatherapy angle (distinct from the topical salve and women's health applications in Bundle 1). The clinical framing: linalool and linalyl acetate have been studied in laboratory and clinical settings for anxiolytic properties. The preparation covered in the Preparation Methods section (sleep sachet, eye pillow, sleep pillow spray) is specific to the Sleep bundle — a buyer who purchased the Women's Health bundle should encounter different practical material here.

**Cross-Reference Notes for Sleep and Nervines**

- Lavender appears in the Women's Health bundle: topical salve and lymphatic support framing there; aromatherapy and sleep framing here.
- Lemon Balm appears in the Digestive bundle: carminative and digestive bitter framing there; nervine and anxiolytic framing here.
- Passionflower's maypop fruit connects to the Wild Edibles Quick Reference (for buyers who know maypop as a wild edible before encountering it here as a cultivated nervine).

## Part 6: Bundle 5 — Digestive Support Herbs Guide

**SKU**: MH-BUNDLE-DS-001
**Upload target**: August 3, 2026
**Price**: $20 consumer / $120–130 practitioner 10-pack
**Word target**: 3,400–3,700 words
**Species**: Dandelion (900), Calendula condensed (600), Lemon Balm condensed (500), Ginger (700)
**Sprint days**: D15–D17 (July 6–8)

### Writing Workflow — Section by Section

**Introduction (200 words) — March framing, bitters and spring**

The March upload timing gives the introduction its seasonal anchor. Spring is when bitter herbs are most naturally relevant — dandelion is the first edible green of the season, and the bitters tradition across cultures aligns spring with digestive stimulation after the heavy foods of winter. Open with the forager's observation, not an herbalism lecture.

**Dandelion — 900 words (Sprint D15–D16, ~2.6 hours)**

This is the bridge species of the entire Phase 3 portfolio. The cross-sell hook to the Wild Edibles Quick Reference appears in the identification section: "If you purchased the Wild Edibles Quick Reference from Seedwarden, you have already met dandelion as a foraged green. This section extends that relationship to the roots." This is not a promotional insertion — it is genuinely useful orientation for the buyer who arrives at this guide from the forager catalog.

Section word allocation:
- Identification: 100 words — the species complex note matters here (Taraxacum is taxonomically complex; for practical purposes all common North American dandelion is T. officinale sensu lato and is interchangeable). Milky sap and hollow stems are diagnostic.
- Wild edibles cross-sell note: 50 words (placed in the identification section naturally)
- Ecology and harvest context: 100 words — dandelion is introduced from Europe (not native in North America); it is cosmopolitan; wild harvest from unsprayed sites is the practical path. The guide covers how to identify sprayed vs. unsprayed sites.
- Root cultivation: 100 words — for intentional supply, deep loose rock-free soil, the 'Thick-Leaved' variety for larger roots, fall harvest timing for highest inulin.
- Root preparation: 200 words — three preparations (roasted root coffee substitute, fresh root bitters tincture, roasted root decoction). The bitters mechanism (bitter-taste receptors in mouth and esophagus stimulate bile flow and digestive enzyme secretion) is described as physiology, not a product efficacy claim.
- Active Constituents: 100 words — taraxacin and taraxacerin (bitter sesquiterpene lactones), inulin (prebiotic fiber), potassium content context (important for the diuretic framing — the herb replaces potassium through its diuretic action).
- Traditional Use: 100 words — German Commission E monograph exists for dandelion (high-authority source), Chinese medicine (Pu Gong Ying), Native American uses, European folk medicine.
- Contraindications: 100 words — bile duct obstruction and gallstones (cholagogue action contraindicated), potassium-sparing diuretic interaction, lithium interaction, latex allergy.

Mandatory content: (1) The Wild Edibles cross-sell note. (2) The bitters mechanism explained physiologically. (3) German Commission E citation. (4) All contraindication categories.

**Calendula condensed — 600 words (Sprint D16, ~1.7 hours)**

Digestive framing differs from the Women's Health version. The Women's Health section covers the topical salve and lymphatic support applications. Here, the mucosal healing internal tea application is primary — calendula tea for gastrointestinal lining irritation. The anti-inflammatory properties that make calendula a good topical salve are the same properties that make the tea relevant to the digestive tract.

**Lemon Balm condensed — 500 words (Sprint D16, ~1.4 hours)**

Carminative framing here; nervine framing in the Sleep bundle. Cover the post-meal gas-relief application, traditional use for colic and nervous digestion, and lemon balm tea as a digestive practice. Cross-reference note to Sleep bundle for the nervine application.

**Ginger — 700 words (Sprint D17, ~2 hours)**

Writing angle: The cultivation section is where this guide earns its differentiation. Most buyers know ginger as a root from the grocery store. The cultivation guide teaches: (1) grocery store ginger is often treated with growth inhibitors — source untreated planting rhizomes, (2) the zone 5–7 container annual path vs. the zone 8–12 perennial path, (3) the young ginger vs. mature ginger distinction (first-year root is thin-skinned and mild; second-year is fibrous and pungent).

Mandatory content: (1) The grocery store rhizome growth inhibitor note. (2) The gingerol-to-shogaol conversion under heat — important for understanding why dried ginger and fresh ginger have different properties. (3) The nausea RCT evidence statement: "Gingerols and shogaols in ginger have been studied in multiple randomized controlled trials for nausea associated with pregnancy, chemotherapy, and postoperative recovery." (4) Blood-thinning caution with anticoagulant medications — the most important drug interaction for ginger.

### Cross-Reference Sentence Templates

Use these templates when noting bundle cross-references in the guide text. These appear at the end of condensed species sections and in the Practitioner Context section.

- "For the full cultivation guide for [species], including [specific deep content], see the [Bundle Name] Guide from Seedwarden."
- "The [framing angle] of [species] is covered in the [Bundle Name] Guide; this section covers [this bundle's framing angle]."
- "If you purchased the [Bundle Name] Guide, you have already met [species] in the context of [application]. This section extends that relationship to [new application]."

### Cross-Sell Placement Rules

Cross-sell references appear in exactly two places per bundle: (1) in the condensed species section where the species also appears in another bundle, and (2) in the Practitioner Context section at the end of the guide. They do not appear in the introduction, the contraindications sections, or the sources section. Inserting a cross-sell in a contraindications section is a tone error.
