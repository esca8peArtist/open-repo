# Phase 3 Medicinal Herbs — Content Outlines (5 Bundles)

**Prepared**: May 7, 2026
**Format**: Each bundle is a standalone PDF guide, 3,500–4,000 words of body text. Formatted in the Seedwarden house style (clean, no-nonsense, research-grounded, field-practical). Sections are standardized across all five bundles so a reader who buys multiple bundles can navigate them consistently.

**House style rules for these guides**:
- Write in second person ("you") for practical sections; third person for historical/research sections
- No marketing language in the body of the guide (all marketing goes in the Etsy listing)
- Cite sources inline or in a Sources section at the back; no footnotes
- Contraindications section is mandatory for every species — omitting it creates liability exposure
- FTC disclaimer appears on the copyright page and in every contraindications section header: "This section describes traditional and historical use and does not constitute medical advice."
- Active constituent descriptions: describe what is in the plant and what has been studied; do not make efficacy claims for the guide itself
- Conservation sidebars: placed immediately after the identification section for at-risk species, before cultivation content

### Species 1: Black Cohosh (*Actaea racemosa*) (700 words)

**1.1 Identification and Botanical Description** (100 words)
- Tall perennial, 3–6 feet in flower; large compound leaves with toothed leaflets; distinctive white bottle-brush flower spike (raceme) June–August
- Formerly classified as *Cimicifuga racemosa*; current accepted name *Actaea racemosa*
- Note the similar-looking species to distinguish from: *Actaea pachypoda* (white baneberry) and *Actaea rubra* (red baneberry) — toxic berries vs. the seed capsules of black cohosh
- Habitat: woodland understory, moist rich soil, partial to full shade

**CONSERVATION SIDEBAR** (150 words)
- United Plant Savers At-Risk status; endangered in Massachusetts
- The commercial overharvest problem: 95%+ of commercial black cohosh raw material is wild-harvested
- What this means for the buyer: purchasing bulk dried root without sourcing documentation almost certainly means purchasing wild-harvested material
- Forest Grown Verified (FGV) program: what it is, how to find certified sources
- Practical action: Prairie Moon Nursery, Strictly Medicinal Seeds, and Fedco all offer nursery-propagated seed and plants — the guide always recommends these over wild material

**1.2 Range and Habitat** (50 words)
- Eastern North America from Ontario south to Georgia; core range in Appalachians
- Thrives in mature deciduous forest with rich, humus-rich soil
- Threatened by habitat loss and commercial overharvest simultaneously

**1.3 Cultivation** (150 words)
- Shade requirement: 70–80% shade minimum; thrives under mature oaks, maples, tulip poplars
- Soil: rich, well-draining, high organic matter; pH 5.5–6.5
- Planting: bare-root rhizome divisions in early spring; seeds require cold stratification and take 2–3 years to reach transplant size — rhizome division is the practical path
- Water: moist but not waterlogged; mulch heavily to retain moisture and mimic forest floor
- Spacing: 18–24 inches
- Time to first harvestable root: 3–5 years minimum — this is a long-term planting
- Forest farming context: black cohosh works within an existing woodland or established shade garden; it is companion-planted with other woodland natives (bloodroot, mayapple) in established forest farming systems

**1.4 Harvest and Preparation** (80 words)
- Roots harvested in fall after aerial parts die back (3–5 years post-planting minimum)
- Excavate with a garden fork; shake off soil; rinse; split large rhizomes
- Drying: slice thin; dry at low temperature (below 100°F) until brittle
- Storage: glass jar, dark, cool; potency degrades faster at room temperature with light exposure
- Processing note: the guide does not specify dosage or therapeutic preparation — that is outside scope

**1.5 Active Constituents** (60 words)
- Triterpenoid glycosides (actein, cimicifugoside) are the primary studied compounds
- Isoflavone content is minimal — the phytoestrogenic mechanism commonly cited in popular literature is contested in current research; the triterpene mechanism is better supported
- Frame as: "Contains triterpenoid glycosides that have been studied in clinical settings for hormonal health applications in women" — not a product efficacy claim

**1.6 Traditional and Historical Use** (80 words)
- Cherokee traditional medicine: uses for women's health during menstrual and menopausal transitions; also used for rheumatic conditions
- Appalachian folk medicine: adopted by European settlers from Cherokee knowledge in the 18th century; became a staple of the Eclectic medical tradition in the 19th century
- Lydia Pinkham's Vegetable Compound (1875) is the most famous commercial use — historical context
- FTC framing: "traditionally used for women's hormonal transitions in Cherokee and Appalachian folk medicine"

**1.7 Contraindications** (80 words)
- Header: "The following section describes traditional cautions and does not constitute medical advice."
- Pregnancy: traditionally contraindicated; should not be used during pregnancy
- Liver concerns: case reports of liver toxicity (rare, causality contested) have been reported in the literature; individuals with liver conditions should consult a physician before use
- Drug interactions: possible interaction with tamoxifen and other hormone-related pharmaceuticals; advise practitioner review for patients on these medications
- Not for children

### Species 2: Vitex / Chaste Tree (*Vitex agnus-castus*) (600 words)

Follow the same 7-section structure. Key content notes:

**Identification**: Shrub to small tree, 5–15 feet; palmately compound leaves (5–7 narrow leaflets); aromatic; spikes of lavender-blue flowers in summer. Distinctive among ornamental shrubs; often used in landscape design in the South and West.

**Cultivation**: Hardy to Zone 6 (some cultivars Zone 5 with protection); drought-tolerant once established; full sun required; poor soil acceptable — actually prefers lean, well-drained soil over rich amended beds. Prune hard in early spring for compact form. Berry harvest: ripe berries in fall, dark purple-black, size of a peppercorn.

**Invasive note**: Vitex is on the invasive watch list in several southeastern states (Tennessee, North Carolina). In guide: note the regional status and recommend growing in a managed garden setting, not in a situation where seeds can escape to natural areas.

**Traditional use**: European herbalism dating to Hippocrates; used by monks (hence "Chaste Tree") as an alleged anaphrodisiac; in modern herbal practice, used for supporting regular menstrual cycles; dopaminergic mechanism studied (acts on pituitary prolactin regulation). FTC framing: "traditionally used in European herbalism for women's hormonal cycle support."

**Contraindications**: Not for use during pregnancy or while taking hormonal medications including oral contraceptives; may interfere with dopamine-related medications.

### Species 3: Red Clover (*Trifolium pratense*) (400 words)

**Identification**: Common pasture legume; distinctive three-leaflet leaves with chevron markings; pink to purple round flower heads. Often confused with white clover (*T. repens*) — the pink flower and larger size distinguish it.

**Cultivation**: Nitrogen-fixing annual in most zones; seeds directly in spring; self-sows readily. Easy to establish in a garden patch. Edibility note: flowers are edible raw or cooked (forager cross-sell).

**Active constituents**: Isoflavones (formononetin, biochanin A, daidzein, genistein) — documented phytoestrogenic activity in the literature. FTC framing: "contains isoflavones, compounds that have been studied for phytoestrogenic activity in women."

**Traditional use**: European folk medicine; used as a blood purifying tonic; isoflavone content studied in context of menopausal symptom management in clinical literature — describe the research descriptively.

**Contraindications**: Theoretical concerns about phytoestrogenic compounds and hormone-sensitive conditions; consult a practitioner before therapeutic use if on hormone medications.

### Species 4: Calendula (*Calendula officinalis*) (400 words)

**Identification**: Annual with orange to yellow daisy-like flowers; sticky stems and leaves; distinctive resinous smell. One of the most reliably self-sowing herbs.

**Cultivation**: Direct sow in early spring (frost-tolerant seedlings); successive plantings for season-long harvest; deadhead to extend bloom. Very easy — this is the practical gateway herb for the new grower.

**Preparation**: Salve and infused oil preparation are the primary content here (topical applications). Infused oil: fill a clean jar with dried petals; cover with olive oil; infuse in a warm spot for 4–6 weeks; strain. These are cosmetic preparations — FTC considerations differ from dietary supplement claims.

**Traditional use**: European folk medicine for lymphatic support, wound healing, and menstrual comfort. The topical anti-inflammatory application (dried petal salve) is the best-documented folk use.

**Contraindications**: Rare allergy in individuals sensitive to the Asteraceae (daisy) family. Avoid during pregnancy (traditionally). No significant drug interactions documented.

### Species 5: Lavender (*Lavandula angustifolia*) (400 words)

**Identification**: Woody perennial subshrub; gray-green narrow leaves; purple flower spikes on long stems. English lavender vs. French lavender (*L. dentata*) vs. lavandin hybrids — the guide distinguishes the three with cultivation notes specific to each.

**Cultivation**: Zone 5–9 perennial; container cultivation in zones 3–4 with winter protection. Full sun, excellent drainage, alkaline to neutral soil. Do not amend with organic matter — lavender prefers lean, dry conditions. Cultivar guide: Hidcote (compact, deep purple, zone 5), Munstead (most cold-hardy English variety, zone 5), Phenomenal (zone 4b, largest, most productive).

**Harvest**: Harvest when one-third of the flowers on the spike are open; dry upside-down in small bundles in a dark, airy space.

**Traditional use**: Mediterranean folk medicine for relaxation and sleep support; used in European hospitals during World War I when medical supplies were limited. Linalool and linalyl acetate (the primary volatile compounds) studied for anxiolytic properties in laboratory and clinical settings.

**Contraindications**: Generally regarded as safe for topical use. Essential oil is highly concentrated — do not ingest. Not for infants. Rare allergic reactions.

### Species 1: Elderberry (*Sambucus nigra* / *S. canadensis*) (800 words)

**Identification (100 words)**: Shrub to small tree; opposite compound leaves with 5–9 leaflets; flat-topped flower clusters (elderflowers); dark purple berry clusters. American elder (*S. canadensis*) vs. European elder (*S. nigra*): the American species is native east of the Rockies and similar in all practical respects. *S. nigra* 'Black Beauty' and 'Bob Gordon' are commercial cultivars. Note: the red elderberry (*S. eburnea*) has toxic raw berries and different flower cluster shape (not flat-topped) — distinguish clearly.

**Cultivation (200 words)**: Multi-stem shrub; establish from bare-root cuttings or potted starts; planting hole twice root ball width; moist, well-draining soil; full sun to partial shade. Coppicing method: cut all stems to 12 inches in late winter every 3–4 years to rejuvenate and maximize berry production. Cultivar selection: Adams (large berry clusters, reliable in zones 3–9), Bob Gordon (developed for high berry yield, zones 3–8), York (late-ripening, large berries). Do not plant only one — cross-pollination improves yield substantially. Spacing: 6–8 feet between plants.

**Elderflower content (80 words)**: The flowers (early summer) are edible and culinary — elderflower cordial, fritters, and infused vinegar. This is the forager cohort crossover content. Harvest elderflowers before full open for most fragrance; trim the flower clusters, remove stems. Forager connection: the same buyer who used the Wild Edibles guide will recognize elderberry from the wild edibles context — extend the relationship to cultivation.

**Harvest and Processing (150 words)**: Berry harvest timing: when clusters are uniformly dark purple-black and clusters begin to droop. Individual berries do not separate easily from green stems until ripe — use a fork to strip berries from stems. Raw elderberries contain sambunigrin, which can cause nausea — always cook before consumption (simmering 15+ minutes breaks down the compound). Syrup basic process: simmer berries with water, strain, add honey when cooled below 100°F. Drying for storage: low temperature, food dehydrator, spread thinly.

**Active Constituents (80 words)**: Anthocyanins (primarily cyanidin glycosides) from berries; flavonoids including quercetin and rutin. Evidence: Sambucol randomized controlled trials on cold and flu duration — describe results with qualifiers: "A 2016 RCT published in Nutrients found elderberry supplementation was associated with reduced cold duration and severity in air travelers." Do not write: "elderberry prevents colds."

**Traditional Use (80 words)**: Hippocratic-era European use documented; Native American use (Haudenosaunee, Anishinaabe) for fever, respiratory conditions; widely used in Appalachian folk medicine. Elderflower in 19th-century pharmacopeia as a diaphoretic (sweat-inducing febrifuge). The historical record across cultures is unusually consistent for an herbal remedy.

**Contraindications (80 words)**: Raw berries — do not consume uncooked (nausea, vomiting risk). Green elderberries — not for consumption. Allergy: rare. Drug interactions: theoretical immune-modulating interaction with immunosuppressive drugs — advise caution in transplant patients.

### Species 2: Mullein (*Verbascum thapsus*) (600 words)

Follow the 7-section structure. Key content notes:

**Identification**: The biennial lifecycle is the structural hook — year one is a flat, silvery-gray rosette of large, soft, woolly leaves (distinctive; very few plants resemble it in the rosette stage); year two sends up a flowering stalk 4–8 feet tall. No look-alike species cause serious confusion; the soft gray leaves and the distinctive habit make it unmistakable.

**Cultivation**: Direct sow in fall or spring; cold stratification helpful but not required; thin to 12 inches minimum; very poor soil tolerance — actually thrives in disturbed, rocky, or nutrient-poor ground. Do not overwater. Container growing possible in a large pot (5+ gallon for the flower stalk year).

**Wild harvest ethics**: Legal in most states (mullein is naturalized/invasive in much of North America). In states where mullein is listed as a noxious weed, harvest from wild populations is legal and may be encouraged. Note states where it is not noxious-listed — in those locations, treat wild harvest with the same caution as any common plant (harvest from clean, unsprayed sites; leave at least 50% of the population intact).

**Leaf harvest**: Harvest first-year leaves for drying; second-year leaves are acceptable but may be tougher. Dry thoroughly — thick leaves require aggressive drying to prevent mold.

**Flower preparation**: Mullein flower ear oil — macerate fresh flowers (not dried) in olive oil in a warm spot for 4–6 weeks; strain through fine cloth. This is a traditional Appalachian and European folk preparation; it is an oleic acid maceration of plant material, not a pharmaceutical preparation.

**Contraindications**: Filter all mullein leaf preparations through fine cloth before use — the leaf hairs can be irritating to mucous membranes if consumed in tea without filtering. No significant drug interactions or toxicity concerns.

### Species 3: Echinacea (*E. purpurea* and *E. angustifolia*) (600 words)

**The two-species structure is the organizing principle**: every section compares and contrasts the two species. This is the content angle that no PLR guide uses and the one that practitioner buyers specifically value.

**Conservation sidebar for *E. angustifolia***: UpS At-Risk; overharvested prairie species; wild populations depleted by commercial root harvest. Prairie Moon Nursery and Strictly Medicinal Seeds carry cultivated *E. angustifolia* — always recommend cultivated sources.

**Cultivation**: *E. purpurea* — easy, full sun, average soil, garden perennial zones 3–9; direct sow or transplant; blooms year 2. *E. angustifolia* — requires excellent drainage, lean soil, full sun; slower-growing; the prairie species needs conditions that mimic open grassland, not a rich amended garden bed.

**Root harvest**: Both species: three-year root minimum for *E. purpurea*; five-year for *E. angustifolia* (slower growth). Fall harvest after die-back. Clean, slice, dry at low heat.

**Active constituents**: Alkylamides and polysaccharides are the primary studied immune-active compounds; species and plant part vary in their relative content — another reason species identity matters.

**Evidence**: Most heavily studied herb in the Western herbalism canon; multiple systematic reviews and meta-analyses on immune function outcomes; use qualifying language throughout ("studied in randomized controlled trials for immune function parameters").

**Contraindications**: Autoimmune conditions — traditionally contraindicated (may stimulate immune function in ways that are counterproductive for autoimmune disease); individuals with active autoimmune conditions should consult a physician. Allergy: Asteraceae family sensitivity. Avoid concurrent use with immunosuppressive drugs.

### Species 4: Thyme (*Thymus vulgaris*) (500 words)

**Identification**: Low-growing woody perennial subshrub; tiny oval leaves; pink to purple flowers in summer; very aromatic. Common culinary herb — buyers already know it. The guide's job is to extend the frame to the medicinal dimension.

**Cultivation**: Zones 5–9 perennial; Zone 4 with protection; full sun; poor, well-drained soil; drought-tolerant once established. Container-hardy everywhere. Propagate by division or cuttings (easier than seed). Harvest: just before or at flowering, when volatile oil content is highest.

**Active constituents**: Thymol and carvacrol are the primary volatile compounds; studied in laboratory settings for antimicrobial properties. Frame as: "Contains thymol, a volatile compound studied in vitro for antimicrobial activity."

**Traditional use**: European herbalism (Hippocrates, Dioscorides); thyme in historical European pharmacopeia for respiratory conditions; widely used in Appalachian and German folk medicine. Thyme tea for dry coughs is documented across cultures.

**Contraindications**: Very large medicinal doses of thyme oil can cause irritation — culinary and tea preparations are not a concern. Avoid concentrated essential oil internally. Pregnancy caution at large doses (emmenagogue effect historically documented for large amounts).

### Species 2: Ashwagandha (*Withania somnifera*) (900 words)

This is the longest section in the Immunity bundle — it is the most practitioner-interest-generating and the least-covered in existing guides.

**Identification (100 words)**: Woody perennial shrub in zones 8–12; annual in zones 5–7. Dull green ovate leaves; small greenish-white flowers; distinctive red-orange berries enclosed in a papery calyx (the "winter cherry" appearance gives the genus its common name). In the annual cultivation zones, the plant is started indoors 8–10 weeks before the last frost — it resembles a tomato seedling at this stage.

**Ayurvedic context (100 words)**: Classified as a Rasayana herb (rejuvenating tonic) in Ayurvedic tradition; used for approximately 3,000 years for vitality, stress resilience, and immune function. Rasayana classification is a traditional pharmacological category, not a Western clinical diagnosis. The guide uses "traditionally classified as" language consistently — it is historically accurate and does not constitute an efficacy claim.

**Cultivation (200 words)**: Zone 8–12 (perennial): treat as a garden shrub. Zone 5–7 (annual): start indoors 8–10 weeks before last frost; transplant to full sun location after soil warms; harvest root in October of same year. Soil: well-drained, sandy to loamy; tolerates poor soil; does not tolerate waterlogging. Water: moderate; drought-tolerant once established. Root harvest: the first-year annual root is small but processable; the second-year perennial root is the commercial standard. Untreated rhizomes: grocery store ginger analogy — grocery store ashwagandha root is typically not suitable for planting; source from Strictly Medicinal Seeds or tropical plant suppliers for viable planting stock.

**Active Constituents (150 words)**: Withanolides (the primary studied phytochemicals) are steroidal lactones unique to the Withania genus. Multiple clinical trials have studied withanolide-standardized ashwagandha extracts for cortisol-related stress markers, thyroid function, and sleep quality. Frame: "Contains withanolides, compounds that have been studied in randomized controlled trials for stress-related physiological markers and sleep quality outcomes." Do not write: "reduces cortisol" or "treats stress." The distinction is between describing the research (permissible) and claiming the product achieves an outcome (requires FDA-level substantiation).

**Traditional Use (100 words)**: Ayurvedic Rasayana tradition; used in Unani medicine (Islamic/Persian traditional medicine) for similar applications; clinical trial base is stronger than most adaptogens — multiple independent RCTs, not just in vitro data.

**Contraindications (100 words)**: Pregnancy: traditionally contraindicated; do not use during pregnancy. Thyroid conditions: ashwagandha has been shown to affect thyroid hormone levels — individuals on thyroid medication should consult their physician before use. Autoimmune conditions: potential immune-stimulating effects may be counterproductive; consult a practitioner. Drug interactions: possible interaction with sedatives, immunosuppressants, and thyroid medications.

**Sourcing note (80 words)**: Commercial ashwagandha is primarily grown in India. For home cultivation guides, Strictly Medicinal Seeds carries US-grown transplants and seeds. For practitioners sourcing bulk dried root, Mountain Rose Herbs carries USDA Organic certified ashwagandha root and powder with full sourcing documentation.

### Species 4: Goldenseal (*Hydrastis canadensis*) (900 words)

This is the highest-stakes species in the guide from a conservation and legal standpoint.

**Identification (100 words)**: Low woodland perennial, 8–12 inches tall; distinctive two large lobed palmate leaves at top of stem; single small white flower in spring; red raspberry-like fruit in late summer. The yellow-orange rhizome is diagnostic (golden color gives the plant its name). Habitat: rich, moist hardwood forest understory with 70–80% shade.

**CITES LEGAL SIDEBAR (200 words)**: Separate boxed section, prominently placed.
- CITES Appendix II since 1997
- What this means: international trade in wild-harvested goldenseal root and rhizome requires CITES export and import permits
- What it does not restrict: domestic commerce in cultivated goldenseal is entirely legal in the US without any permit
- The market problem: despite the CITES listing, 95%+ of commercial goldenseal raw material is still wild-harvested (based on UpS documentation and market analysis)
- For guide readers: purchasing dried goldenseal root without explicit cultivated-source documentation almost certainly means purchasing illegally traded or legally-grey wild-harvested material
- Action: the guide recommends only FGV-certified, nursery-propagated, or documented cultivated-source goldenseal. Specific sources: Prairie Moon Nursery (rhizome divisions, cultivated), Strictly Medicinal Seeds (cultivated), UpS FGV participant directory (unitedplantsavers.org/forest-grown-verified)

**Cultivation (200 words)**: The most demanding cultivation in this bundle. Requires: 70–80% shade (mature hardwood canopy or purpose-built shade structure); rich, moist, humus-heavy soil; pH 5.5–6.5; no waterlogging. The forest farming setup — growing goldenseal under existing mature trees — is the closest analog to its native habitat. Rhizome propagation: rhizome divisions planted in fall; each division with a growth bud; plant 4–6 inches deep, 12 inches apart. Time to harvest: 4–5 years. This is a long-term investment; goldenseal should be treated as a multi-year perennial plantings, not an annual crop. The guide includes a companion planting note: bloodroot, wild ginger, and mayapple are appropriate woodland companions in a goldenseal forest garden.

**Active Constituents (150 words)**: Berberine is the primary alkaloid — an isoquinoline alkaloid present in goldenseal, barberry (*Berberis* spp.), and Oregon grape (*Mahonia* spp.). Berberine has been studied in laboratory and clinical settings for antimicrobial activity and blood glucose regulation. Frame: "Contains berberine, an alkaloid studied in vitro and in clinical settings for antimicrobial and metabolic effects." Note explicitly: the guide does not make antimicrobial claims for goldenseal as a product. The berberine content is a constituent description, not an efficacy claim.

**Traditional Use (100 words)**: Cherokee use as a dye plant and wound treatment; adopted by the Eclectic medical tradition (19th century) as one of their most valued remedies; widely used in early American herbalism for mucosal conditions. The guide frames this as: "used historically by Cherokee and in 19th-century Eclectic medicine for mucosal conditions."

**Contraindications (100 words)**: Pregnancy: contraindicated; berberine has uterine-stimulating properties. Newborns and infants: do not use. Drug interactions: berberine interacts with a wide range of pharmaceuticals including antibiotics, blood thinners, and medications processed by cytochrome P450 enzymes — this requires a practitioner review for anyone on prescription medications. Not for long-term continuous use without practitioner supervision. Individuals with jaundice or liver conditions: consult a physician.

- Visual framing: passionflower is the star. The listing thumbnail is the flower; the introduction should earn that image with a sentence about what the plant actually is.

### Species 1: Valerian (*Valeriana officinalis*) (800 words)

**Identification**: Tall perennial, 3–5 feet in flower; pinnately compound leaves; clusters of small pale pink to white flowers; the distinctive feature is the smell — dried valerian root has a pungent, earthy, somewhat unpleasant odor that is completely unlike the pleasant scent of the fresh flowers. This is useful for identification and is a notable point for anyone expecting the supplement capsule to smell like the flower.

**Cultivation**: Zones 4–9; full sun to partial shade; moist, well-draining soil; propagate by crown division (most reliable) or seed. Plant in an out-of-the-way location — the plant is large and the root smell is pervasive when harvesting. Root harvest: second-year fall only. Growing a valerian patch means committing a permanent spot to a two-year crop cycle; divide roots after harvest and replant divisions for continuous production.

**Root harvest and drying (detailed)**: Excavate entire root mass with a garden fork in fall; shake off soil; do not wash (washing introduces moisture that causes decay); split large roots; dry at below 100°F (not in a conventional oven — too hot); slice thick roots. Storage: glass jar with tight lid; the smell concentrates over time.

**Active Constituents**: Valerenic acid and isovaleric acid are the primary studied compounds. The mechanism is not fully established (GABA-related pathways are studied but contested). Frame: "Contains valerenic acid, a compound studied in relation to GABA receptor activity and sleep latency outcomes."

**Evidence**: Multiple systematic reviews and meta-analyses on sleep-related outcomes; evidence is mixed across studies (variation in preparation, dose, and study design). Use qualifying language: "has been studied in clinical trials for sleep-related outcomes with mixed results."

**Traditional Use**: European herbalism since at least the 16th century; Hippocrates referenced the plant; used in the 19th-century Eclectic tradition; WWI-era use for "shell shock" (now recognized as PTSD).

**Contraindications**: Sedative drug interactions — do not combine with prescription sedatives, benzodiazepines, or anesthetics without practitioner supervision. Avoid before driving or operating machinery. Not for use during pregnancy. Long-term use at high doses — discontinue gradually.

---

### Species 2: Passionflower (*Passiflora incarnata*) (700 words)

**Identification**: The thumbnail species. The flower is unmistakable — a complex radially symmetric structure with white to pale lavender petals, a fringe of blue-purple filaments, and a central column bearing the stamens and style. Native to the southeastern US, naturalized to the Midwest; the "maypop" fruit (a small yellow-green oval berry) is edible and was eaten by Native Americans.

**Cultivation**: Zones 6–10 perennial vine; treat as an annual in zones 4–5. Aggressive growth once established — needs a sturdy trellis, fence, or pergola. Underground runners spread; the plant will come back from the root in its perennial zones. Direct sow from seed (scarification helpful) or from cuttings. Leaf and flower harvest: just before full bloom for highest alkaloid content.

**Maypop edibility (forager crossover)**: The fruit (edible when fully ripe, golden-yellow) is a wild food with a passion-fruit flavor. Cross-sell hook: the buyer who used the Wild Edibles guide to identify maypop in the wild now learns how to cultivate the vine intentionally.

**Active Constituents**: Flavonoids (chrysin, vitexin, isovitexin) studied for anxiolytic properties. Frame: "Contains flavonoids including chrysin and vitexin, compounds studied for their interaction with GABA and serotonin receptor systems in laboratory and clinical settings."

**Evidence**: PubMed clinical trials on anxiety-related outcomes; a 2011 RCT compared passionflower to oxazepam for generalized anxiety; describe descriptively without making the anxiety treatment claim.

**Traditional Use**: Cherokee and other southeastern nations used passionflower as a calming tea and for wound treatment. Appalachian folk medicine. Adopted in 19th-century Eclectic practice.

**Contraindications**: Pregnancy: contraindicated (uterine-stimulating properties in large doses). Sedative drug interactions: same cautions as valerian. Do not combine with MAOIs. Drowsiness effect — avoid driving.

### Species 3: Lemon Balm (*Melissa officinalis*) (500 words)

**Identification**: The easiest identification in this bundle — a spreading, bushy, mounding herb with distinctively wrinkled and veined leaves that smell strongly of lemon when crushed. Small white flowers in summer. Spreads aggressively by underground runners — plant in a contained space or large pot.

**Cultivation**: Zones 4–9; partial shade to full sun; adapts to most soils; drought-tolerant once established. Container-friendly — one of the best nervine herbs for an apartment or patio garden. Harvest repeatedly before and during flowering; do not let it bolt to seed if you want to extend leaf production.

**Dual-bundle connection**: In the Digestive bundle, lemon balm appears as the carminative species. In this bundle, the nervine framing is primary. The guide notes the dual use — this is a teaching point about the difference between a nervine (calming to the nervous system) and a carminative (eases digestive tension) and why those categories overlap.

**Traditional Use**: European herbalism since the Middle Ages; Paracelsus and Gerard both wrote about it; used in Carmelite water (a 17th-century European distillation); one of the most continuously documented herbs in Western herbal tradition.

**Contraindications**: Thyroid conditions — lemon balm may inhibit TSH binding in the thyroid; individuals with thyroid conditions or on thyroid medication should consult a practitioner. Otherwise very mild safety profile.

### Species 1: Dandelion (*Taraxacum officinale*) (900 words)

**This is the longest section in the Digestive bundle — it is the bridge species and deserves the most content.**

**Identification (100 words)**: Ground-level rosette of deeply toothed leaves (the "lion's tooth" that gives the plant its French common name); single yellow composite flower per hollow stem; white seed head (pappus); exudes milky sap when broken. The species complex is large and taxonomically complex — for practical purposes, any common dandelion in North America is *T. officinale* sensu lato and can be used interchangeably.

**Wild Edibles Cross-sell Note**: "If you purchased the Wild Edibles Quick Reference from Seedwarden, you have already met dandelion as a foraged green. This section extends that relationship to the roots — the part of the plant most used in traditional medicine — and to the bitters application."

**Ecology and harvest context (100 words)**: Dandelion is non-native in most of North America (introduced from Europe by colonial settlers as a food and medicine plant); it is cosmopolitan, abundant, and not at conservation risk. Wild harvest from unsprayed sites is the primary practical path — most people do not need to cultivate dandelion, they need to learn where to find uncontaminated plants. Guide covers: identifying sprayed vs. unsprayed sites; roadside harvest cautions; best growing contexts (unsprayed lawn, disturbed garden edges, meadow).

**Root cultivation (100 words)**: For those who want to grow dandelion intentionally (e.g., for a reliable, clean root supply), the guide covers: deep, loose, rock-free soil (dandelion roots grow 12+ inches); the taproot variety 'Thick-Leaved' is bred for larger roots; thin seedlings to 6 inches. Harvest roots in fall of the first year (highest inulin content before the plant stores energy in roots for winter).

**Root preparation (200 words)**: Three preparations covered:
- Roasted root "coffee substitute": slice fresh or dried root into thin rounds; roast in a 350°F oven until dark brown and fragrant; brew as a tea. A culinary preparation with no health claim implications.
- Fresh root bitters tincture: pack a clean jar with fresh, cleaned dandelion root; cover with 100-proof vodka; infuse 4–6 weeks in a dark location; strain. The bitter taste is the preparation's mechanism of action in traditional use — bitter-taste receptors in the mouth and esophagus stimulate bile flow and digestive enzyme secretion. Frame: "Traditional bitters preparations like this one stimulate bitter taste receptors, which have been associated with increased digestive secretions in the physiological literature."
- Roasted root decoction: simmer dried, roasted root pieces in water for 20 minutes; strain; drink before or after meals.

**Active Constituents (100 words)**: Taraxacin and taraxacerin (bitter sesquiterpene lactones responsible for the bitterness), inulin (a prebiotic fiber concentrated in the root), potassium (leaves are notably potassium-rich — important context for the diuretic traditional use, as the traditional herb replaces potassium potentially lost through its diuretic effect). The inulin content makes dandelion root a documented prebiotic fiber source — describe this accurately: "Dandelion root contains inulin, a prebiotic fiber that has been studied in relation to gut microbiome diversity."

**Traditional Use (100 words)**: European folk medicine — liver tonic, bile-stimulating, diuretic; documented in German Commission E monographs; widely used in Chinese medicine (Pu Gong Ying) for heat-clearing and liver-related applications; Native American uses documented across many nations for food and medicine. One of the most cross-culturally documented medicinal plants on earth.

**Contraindications (100 words)**: Bile duct obstruction or gallstones: cholagogue (bile-stimulating) action may be contraindicated — consult a physician. Drug interactions: dandelion affects potassium levels and may interact with potassium-sparing diuretics or lithium. Latex allergy: the milky sap contains latex; individuals with latex sensitivity should test cautiously.

### Species 4: Ginger (*Zingiber officinale*) (700 words)

**Identification**: Tropical perennial rhizome plant; blade-like leaves in warm-season cultivation; the rhizome (the commercial product) is what most people recognize without having seen the plant above-ground. In cultivation contexts in zones 5–7, the plant grows to 18–24 inches as a container annual; in zones 8–12, it is a perennial reaching 3–4 feet.

**Cultivation (detailed — this is where guides fall short)**: The grocery store ginger rhizome is often treated with growth inhibitors and may not sprout. Source untreated planting rhizomes from Strictly Medicinal Seeds, a local nursery, or a tropical plant supplier. Planting: break rhizome into 1–2 inch sections with at least one growth bud each; plant 2–3 inches deep in rich, moist, well-draining soil; partial shade in hot climates, full sun in zone 5–7 summers. Water consistently — ginger does not tolerate drought. Harvest: after the above-ground portions die back in fall (in annual zones) or after 8–10 months of growth. The first-year rhizome is "young ginger" (thin-skinned, mild, no fiber); the second-year and older rhizome is mature ginger (thick-skinned, fibrous, more pungent).

**Active Constituents**: Gingerols (fresh ginger) and shogaols (dried/cooked ginger — formed when gingerols convert under heat). Both studied for anti-nausea and anti-inflammatory properties. Ginger is one of the best-supported herbs for nausea outcomes in clinical research — the evidence base is substantial and relatively consistent. Frame: "Gingerols and shogaols in ginger have been studied in multiple randomized controlled trials for nausea associated with pregnancy, chemotherapy, and postoperative recovery."

**Traditional Use**: Documented in every major traditional medical system that has access to it — Chinese medicine (sheng jiang for fresh, gan jiang for dried), Ayurvedic Rasayana tradition, Greek-Roman antiquity (documented by Dioscorides), European folk medicine. One of the oldest continuously used medicinal spices in global commerce.

**Contraindications**: Blood-thinning effects at high doses — caution with anticoagulant medications (warfarin, aspirin therapy); consult a practitioner. Pregnancy: culinary doses are considered safe; medicinal doses in pregnancy should be discussed with an obstetrician (despite the anti-nausea evidence, high doses raise caution). Gallstone disease: same cholagogue caution as dandelion at high doses.
