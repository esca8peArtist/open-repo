# Cluster C — Props Acquisition Plan

This file documents all props required for the Cluster C physical shoot (food preservation cluster). It covers three products: Harvest Preservation Field Manual, Fermented and Preserved Harvest Handbook, and Grow Your Own Hot Sauce.

Do not execute purchases until you have reviewed this plan and confirmed what you already own vs. what needs to be sourced.

## Props List

### Group 1: Mason Jars (all three products)

**What**: 3–5 mason jars, mixed sizes (quart and pint both useful). Used or slightly weathered look is preferred — pristine brand-new jars read as commercial, not homestead.

**Why**: Mason jars appear in lifestyle compositions for all three Cluster C products. They signal home preservation immediately to the target buyer. They can be reused across all three product shots by swapping contents.

**Contents per product**:
- Harvest Preservation: filled jars with dried herbs, grain, or dried beans (visible texture through glass)
- Fermented/Preserved: filled jars with visible brine (water + salt + peppercorns is sufficient for visual authenticity)
- Hot Sauce: one jar with a red or orange liquid visible (can be food coloring in water if needed for color pop)

**Source**:
1. Check your kitchen first — used canning jars are ideal
2. Grocery store canning aisle (Ball or Kerr brand, 12-pack): $10–$14 for a dozen quart jars
3. Hardware store (Ace, True Value, Home Depot): typically same price as grocery

**Estimated cost if buying new**: $10–$14 for a 12-pack. If you only buy a 4-pack: $5–$7.

**What to avoid**: Decorative Anthropologie-style jars, blue-tinted vintage jars (unless you have them — they photograph beautifully but are not required), or plastic containers.

---

### Group 2: Canning Funnel or Jar Lifter

**What**: A wide-mouth canning funnel (the stainless or plastic kind that fits inside a mason jar) or a jar lifter (the tong-like tool for lifting hot jars from a water bath).

**Why**: Props that signal the act of canning — functional tools reinforce the "this is real preserved food" aesthetic. Either item works; a funnel is more versatile as a flat-lay prop.

**Source**:
1. Check your kitchen — many households already own these
2. Hardware store canning section: $4–$8 for a basic funnel
3. Grocery store (Ball canning accessory section): same price

**Estimated cost if buying new**: $4–$8. Often sold in a multi-tool canning kit ($12–$18) that also includes a jar lifter, magnetic lid wand, and headspace tool — useful if you plan to shoot Cluster A later.

**Priority**: Medium. If you already have any canning tool, use it. Do not buy solely for this shoot if budget is constrained — a wooden spoon partially inserted into a jar reads similarly.

---

### Group 3: Fermentation Crock Weights (or substitute)

**What**: Glass or ceramic fermentation weights — small discs that sit inside a wide-mouth jar to keep contents submerged. Alternatively: a small zip-lock bag filled with brine, placed inside a jar mouth, works as a functional substitute and is invisible in most shot angles.

**Why**: Specifically required for the Fermented and Preserved Harvest Handbook shoot to signal fermentation (vs. general canning).

**Source**:
1. Kitchen/homestead stores (Sur La Table, Williams-Sonoma, local kitchen store): $8–$15 for a 2-pack
2. Amazon: $6–$12 for a 2-pack of glass weights (search "wide mouth mason jar fermentation weights")
3. Substitute: use a clean, smooth river rock if you have one — it photographs authentically

**Estimated cost if buying new**: $6–$15. Substitute (brine bag or flat stone) costs $0.

**Safety note**: If using a river stone as a weight prop, wash with soap and hot water before placing in a food-adjacent composition, even if the jar is only a photo prop.

---

### Group 4: Dried Chilis and Fresh Peppers

**What**: 
- Dried chilis (red or burgundy): 6–10 pieces. Ancho, guajillo, or New Mexico dried chilis work well — they are large enough to be prominent in frame.
- Fresh peppers (red or orange): 3–5 medium-sized. Fresno, red jalapeño, or red bell pepper work for color pop.

**Why**: Required for Grow Your Own Hot Sauce shoot. Chilis are the primary visual signal for this product. Both dried and fresh versions together tell a "process" story (from fresh to preserved sauce).

**Source**:
1. Grocery store produce section: fresh red/orange peppers, $2–$4
2. Grocery store bulk or Mexican food aisle: dried guajillo or ancho chilis, $3–$6 per small bag
3. Latin grocery or international market: wider variety, often cheaper

**Estimated cost if buying new**: $5–$10 total for enough chilis to shoot.

**Safety note — WEAR GLOVES when handling dried chilis for photo arrangement**. Dried chili oils transfer to fingers easily and can cause severe eye irritation if you touch your face during the shoot. Disposable nitrile gloves (kitchen or medical grade) are sufficient. If you have capsaicin sensitivity, consider fresh bell peppers as a visual substitute for the fresh pepper component — they read similarly in frame.

---

### Group 5: Wooden Cutting Board

**What**: A medium to large wooden cutting board (12"x18" or larger preferred). Butcher block or end-grain boards photograph best. Aged boards with use marks are more authentic than pristine ones.

**Why**: The primary flat-lay surface for the hot sauce and preservation compositions. Also used as a secondary surface under jars and tools.

**Source**:
1. Check your kitchen — any wooden board works
2. Thrift store (Goodwill, Salvation Army): $2–$8 for used wooden boards in excellent condition for photography
3. Home goods store: $15–$40 new. Not recommended to buy new for this shoot.

**Estimated cost if buying new**: $0 recommended (use existing board or thrift). If must buy: $2–$8 thrift.

**What to avoid**: Plastic or composite cutting boards, boards with heavy dark stains from use (they absorb light poorly), boards with painted decorations.

---

### Group 6: Mortar and Pestle or Empty Glass Bottle

**What**: A stone or ceramic mortar and pestle (medium size, 4–6 inch diameter), OR a clean empty glass bottle with a narrow neck (hot sauce bottle shape preferred — 5–8oz flip-top or corked).

**Why**: 
- Mortar and pestle: signals the "homemade spice blend / hand-processed" aesthetic for Harvest Preservation and Hot Sauce shots.
- Empty glass bottle: signals finished hot sauce product — props a "before and after" composition when placed next to dried chilis and fresh peppers.

**Recommendation**: The empty glass bottle is higher priority because it is unique to the hot sauce product identity. The mortar and pestle is a supporting prop that many kitchens already own.

**Source (mortar and pestle)**:
1. Check your kitchen first
2. Grocery store (often in kitchen gadget aisle): $8–$20
3. Thrift store: $3–$10 for marble or stone versions

**Source (empty glass bottle)**:
1. Free: use an empty hot sauce bottle you already own (Cholula, Tapatío, etc.) — remove label with warm water
2. Specialty bottle supplier (Fillmore Container, SKS Bottle): $0.50–$2 per bottle, usually minimum order of 12
3. Local homebrew or specialty kitchen store: may sell individual bottles

**Estimated cost if buying new**: Mortar $8–$20 (skip if budget-constrained). Glass bottle: $0 from existing hot sauce bottle or $1–$3 for a specialty bottle.

## Prep Timeline

**Before May 7 (start of Week 2 shoot window)**:

- Day 1–2 (May 1–2): Check kitchen for existing props. List what you do and do not own.
- Day 2–3 (May 2–3): Source any missing items — grocery run covers chilis, peppers, and mason jars in one trip. Hardware store for canning funnel if needed.
- Day 4–5 (May 4–5): If ordering fermentation weights or a specialty bottle online, order now to ensure delivery before May 7. Amazon Prime is typically sufficient for 2-day delivery.
- Day 6 (May 6, day before shoot): Stage all props on your shooting surface. Do a dry-run composition — arrange mason jars, chilis, board, and funnel as you plan to shoot them. Photograph with your phone to preview the arrangement. Adjust before shoot day so shoot day is execution only.

**Shoot day setup**:
1. Wipe all glass surfaces with a microfiber cloth immediately before shooting — fingerprints and water spots are visible at close focus distances.
2. Pre-fill mason jars with props contents (brine, dried herbs, etc.) the night before so they look settled and natural on shoot day.
3. Have gloves accessible before handling dried chilis.
4. Shoot the hot sauce product first while fresh peppers are at peak color, before they begin to soften.
