## Part 1: Color Palette Shift (Herbalist/Apothecary Aesthetic)

### Phase 2 vs. Phase 3 Aesthetic Positioning

**Phase 2 (Forager aesthetic)**:
- Primary color: Deep Forest Green (#2D5016) — evokes wild plants, nature, outdoor foraging
- Accent: Warm Cream (#F5EDD6) background — light, accessible, inviting
- Supporting: Burnt Sienna (#A0522D) — earthy, grounded
- Text: Dark Charcoal (#2C2C2C)

**Phase 3 (Herbalist/Apothecary aesthetic)**:
- Primary color: Deep Burgundy (#8B3E3E) — medical/wellness, clinical, premium
- Secondary: Sage Green (#6B8E6F) — herb-focused (softer than forest green)
- Accent: Gold/Amber (#D4AF37) — apothecary vintage, clinical authority, premium positioning
- Background: Cream/Ivory (#F9F5F0) — medical/clinical whitespace (slightly warmer than Phase 2)
- Text: Dark Charcoal (#2C2C2C) — unchanged
- Optional: Muted Lavender (#9B8BA0) — Sleep bundle accent

## Part 2: Stock Image Sourcing Strategy

### Source Hierarchy (by quality and licensing)

**Tier 1 (preferred)**: Original fresh herb photography from June photo staging (May 26–June 21)  
**Tier 2 (secondary)**: Wikimedia Commons CC-BY-SA or CC-BY images (verified in sourcing guide)  
**Tier 3 (backup)**: iNaturalist CC-BY observation photos  
**Tier 4 (last resort)**: Unsplash/Pixabay lifestyle images (apothecary aesthetic)

### Bundle Cover Image Sourcing Plan

Each bundle requires 1 primary hero image (for Etsy listing thumbnail) and 2–3 supporting images (for interior PDF pages).

#### Women's Health Bundle

**Hero image** (cover thumbnail):
- **Primary choice**: Black Cohosh plant in forest-floor context (live specimen June 10–16, OR Wikimedia CC-BY-SA if not available)
- **Fallback**: iNaturalist CC-BY Appalachian Black Cohosh (abundant; filter for June observations)
- **Last resort**: Wikimedia Commons search "Actaea racemosa" (multiple CC-BY-SA available)

**Supporting images** (interior pages):
- Vitex flowering shrub (search Wikimedia "Vitex agnus-castus")
- Red Clover in flowering stage (search Wikimedia "Trifolium pratense")
- Calendula flower close-up (Unsplash search "calendula flowers" — high-quality lifestyle images)
- Lavender field or garden row (Unsplash "lavender garden" — premium aesthetic)

**Styling note**: Emphasize the botanical + clinical mix — pair live specimens with apothecary props (dried flowers, mortar, glass jars) to bridge the forager/herbalist aesthetic gap.

#### Respiratory Bundle

**Hero image** (cover):
- **Primary choice**: Elderberry shrub with flower/berry clusters (live specimen June 10–16, OR Wikimedia CC-BY-SA)
- **Fallback**: iNaturalist CC-BY Sambucus nigra (excellent coverage; search for June observations)
- **Last resort**: Wikimedia Commons search "Sambucus nigra" (extensive CC-BY-SA coverage)

**Supporting images**:
- Thyme plant in herb garden (search Wikimedia "Thymus vulgaris")
- Echinacea flowering head close-up (Wikimedia "Echinacea purpurea" — abundant)
- Mullein tall flower spike (Wikimedia "Verbascum thapsus" — distinctive second-year growth)

**Styling note**: Emphasize warm, earthy tones (golden-hour lighting, warm backgrounds). Respiratory bundle appeals to seasonal preparedness; imagery should feel "late summer into fall."

#### Immunity Bundle

**Hero image** (cover):
- **Primary choice**: Goldenseal in forest-farming context (sourced from NC Botanical Garden photo contact, preferred; OR Wikimedia CC-BY-SA if contact unavailable)
- **Fallback**: iNaturalist CC-BY Hydrastis canadensis (Ohio/Indiana/Appalachian results)
- **Last resort**: Wikimedia Commons "Hydrastis canadensis" (thinner coverage than Black Cohosh, but available)

**Supporting images**:
- Echinacea root cross-section (live specimen, June; OR Wikimedia illustration if available)
- Ashwagandha plant habit in warm-climate setting (search Wikimedia "Withania somnifera")
- Elderberry dried for tincture (dried herb photography, June 17–21)

**Styling note**: Immunity bundle is the "premium" clinical tier. Imagery should emphasize precision, clinical detail (root anatomy, berry clusters, medicinal preparation stages). Forest-farming Goldenseal photo is the visual differentiator — no competitor has this image.

#### Sleep Bundle

**Hero image** (cover):
- **Primary choice**: Passionflower single flower in full bloom (live specimen on trellis June 10–16, OR Wikimedia CC-BY-SA)
- **Fallback**: iNaturalist CC-BY Passiflora incarnata (Southeast US abundant observations)
- **Last resort**: Wikimedia Commons "Passiflora incarnata" (multiple CC-BY-SA images, all high-quality)

**Supporting images**:
- Valerian tall flowering spike (live specimen OR Wikimedia "Valeriana officinalis")
- Lemon Balm bushy herb habit (local herb nursery plant OR Wikimedia)
- Lavender dried sachet or hanging bundle (dried herb photography, June; OR Unsplash "dried lavender")

**Styling note**: Sleep bundle should evoke calm, softness. Use warmer lighting, softer backgrounds (linen, cream), muted colors. The Passionflower flower is visually stunning and should be the design anchor — let the image speak.

#### Digestive Bundle

**Hero image** (cover):
- **Primary choice**: Dandelion root cross-section (live specimen dug June; OR wild dandelion from garden/yard)
- **Fallback**: Ginger rhizome (fresh OR Wikimedia "Zingiber officinale")
- **Last resort**: Unsplash search "roots spices" (lifestyle photography)

**Supporting images**:
- Ginger rhizome in hand (fresh specimen + live action shot, June)
- Calendula petals in bowl (fresh specimen June OR dried from Mountain Rose Herbs)
- Lemon Balm leaves close-up (herb garden OR Wikimedia)

**Styling note**: Digestive bundle appeals to both herbalists and culinary-minded buyers. Imagery should feel practical (kitchen/preparation context) rather than purely botanical. Root vegetables + prepared herbs (dried, in bowls) work well here.

### Stock Image Search Protocol

**Wikimedia Commons search**:
1. Go to commons.wikimedia.org
2. Search by scientific name (e.g., "Actaea racemosa")
3. Filter by license: CC-BY, CC-BY-SA, or CC0 only
4. Review 5–10 results; select 2–3 best options for variety
5. Click image > click "Use this file" > copy URL
6. Download at highest resolution available (usually 2400+ px)

**iNaturalist search**:
1. Go to inaturalist.org
2. Search by species name
3. Filter by "License" = CC-BY
4. Sort by "Faves" (highest quality, most liked by community)
5. Click observation > click "View" on photo > verify CC-BY license
6. Right-click > "Save image as"
7. Record observer name for attribution

**Unsplash search**:
1. Go to unsplash.com
2. Search by topic (e.g., "lavender field", "herbal tea", "dried herbs")
3. All Unsplash images are free for commercial use (CC0, attribution recommended)
4. Click image > "Download free"
5. Attribution: photographer name (if available) + "Unsplash"

**Image specifications for Canva**:
- Minimum 1200 px on shortest side (prefer 2400+ px for high-res PDF export)
- Format: JPG or PNG
- File size: <5 MB each

## Part 3: Zone Card Adaptation for Medicinal Plants

### Differences from Phase 2 Vegetable Zone Cards

**Phase 2 zone cards**: Seed starting calendar (when to sow indoors/outdoors, when to transplant) for vegetables by hardiness zone

**Phase 3 zone cards**: Cultivation and harvest calendar for medicinal herbs by hardiness zone

The structure and template are the same, but the content (planting/harvest timing) differs significantly.

**Template** (8.5" × 11" portrait, Canva dimensions 8.5 × 11 inches):

### Cultivation Calendar Table (Heart of Zone Card)

Instead of Phase 2's "Sow Indoors / Sow Outdoors / Transplant" columns, use:
- **Sow/Plant**: When to start seeds indoors or plant divisions
- **Grow**: When the plant is growing (vegetative stage)
- **Harvest (Leaf/Flower)**: When to harvest aerial parts (fresh)
- **Harvest (Root)**: When to harvest roots (typically fall/winter when plant dies back)

### Zone-Specific Notes Section

Include 3–4 bullet points specific to the zone and bundle. Examples:

**Zone 3 (cold climate) — Women's Health Bundle**:
- Black Cohosh requires Zone 3 winter cold for dormancy; cold stratification essential — direct sow in fall for spring germination
- Vitex is marginal in Zone 3; grow in containers (bring indoors in winter) or choose hardy Cimicifuga racemosa alternative
- Calendula and Red Clover are annuals; direct sow after last frost (May 15 typical date)
- Lavender: Choose cold-hardy cultivars ("Munstead" and "Hidcote" perform best); plant in fall for best winter survival

**Zone 8 (warm climate) — Respiratory Bundle**:
- Elderberry prefers partial shade and consistent moisture; afternoon shade is protective in August heat
- Echinacea tolerates heat well; drought-tolerant once established
- Mullein is a biennial; rosette stage in Year 1, flowering in Year 2; does not perform well in Zone 8 heat (prone to bolting) — sow in fall for spring flowers
- Thyme: Choose Mediterranean varieties (Thymus vulgaris); reduce watering in summer (root rot risk in humidity)

### Harvest Window Details

For each bundle, include a simplified harvest summary:

**Women's Health**:
- Red Clover flowers: Cut flowering tops June–August (fresh or dried)
- Calendula flowers: Harvest as petals open, mid-morning (June–October)
- Lavender flowers: Cut spike when ¾ flowers open (June–July for Zone 5)
- Vitex seeds: Collect in September–October as fruits turn black
- Black Cohosh roots: Harvest in fall (Oct–Nov) of Year 3+

### Canva Implementation for Zone Cards

**Steps**:
1. Duplicate the Phase 2 zone card template 5 times (one per bundle)
2. Update header: change zone number to [#], region name to relevant zone region, bundle name
3. Change header color to bundle color (Burgundy for Women's Health, Sage Green for Respiratory, etc.)
4. Replace table content with cultivation calendar (copy from sourcing guide or content outline, adapt to zone)
5. Replace zone-specific notes with 3–4 bullets relevant to zone + bundle
6. Update harvest windows section
7. Export to PDF
8. Test PDF rendering (no text overflow, table legible at print size)

**Time per zone card**: 25–35 minutes (fill template + verify content + export)  
**Total for 5 bundles**: 2–3 hours per bundle (design work done June 25–July 5 during sprint)

## Part 4: Practitioner Bundle Cover Design Specs

### Practitioner 10-Pack Visual Positioning

The Practitioner 10-pack ($120–$150 bundle) is the premium tier. Cover design should signal premium/clinical authority, not consumer-casual.

### Design Specifications

**Format**: 
- Size: 8.5" × 11" portrait (printable PDF document, not square Etsy thumbnail)
- DPI: 300 DPI (print quality, not screen)

**Visual Hierarchy**:
1. **Title** (largest): "Practitioner's Bundle — [Medicinal Herbs] Complete Collection"
2. **Subtitle** (medium): "Designed for clinical herbalists, Registered Herbalists-AHG, and healthcare practitioners"
3. **Visual element** (prominent): 5 small bundle cover thumbnails or botanical illustrations arranged at base
4. **Authority signal** (small): Certifications (AHG, RH-AHG) or compliance notes ("Includes FTC-compliant language and sourcing guidance")

**Color scheme**:
- Primary: Apothecary Gold (#D4AF37) — premium, clinical authority
- Secondary: Deep Burgundy (#8B3E3E) or Sage Green (#6B8E6F) — maintains bundle association
- Background: Clinical Cream (#F9F5F0) — clean, medical
- Text: Dark Charcoal (#2C2C2C)

**Typography**:
- Title: Playfair Display, 42–48pt, Bold (dominant)
- Subtitle: Montserrat, 16–18pt, Italic
- Body: Montserrat, 12pt

**Layout example**:

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                         │
│                                                         │
│    PRACTITIONER'S BUNDLE — MEDICINAL HERBS             │
│         COMPLETE COLLECTION                             │
│                                                         │
│    Designed for clinical herbalists,                    │
│    Registered Herbalists-AHG, and                       │
│    healthcare practitioners                             │
│                                                         │
│                                                         │
│    [5 small bundle cover images in a row at bottom]    │
│    [Women's Health] [Respiratory] [Immunity] [Sleep]   │
│    [Digestive]                                          │
│                                                         │
│                                                         │
│    Includes: Assessment frameworks, formulation guides, │
│    practitioner reference tools, and FTC-compliant       │

│                                                         │
│    [AHG seal or authority marker — if applicable]       │
│                                                         │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Canva Implementation

**Steps**:
1. Create new Canva document: 8.5" × 11" (8.5 × 11 inches, custom size)
2. Set background to Clinical Cream (#F9F5F0)
3. Add title text: "Practitioner's Bundle — [Name] Complete Collection"
4. Add subtitle: "Designed for clinical herbalists, Registered Herbalists-AHG, and healthcare practitioners"
5. Import 5 bundle cover images (reduced to thumbnail size, ~0.8" × 0.8" each)
6. Arrange thumbnails in a row at bottom of page
7. Add authority statement or compliance note (small text)
8. Add apothecary gold accent bar (top or bottom full-width)
9. Export as PDF at 300 DPI

**Time estimate**: 1.5–2 hours (per practitioner bundle cover)

## Part 7: Fallback Design Approach (If Canva Issues Arise)

If Canva performance is unexpectedly slow or templates don't render correctly:

**Minimum viable design alternative**: PDF export from Google Docs or LibreOffice Writer

**Steps**:
1. Create bundle PDFs in Google Docs (simple formatted document)
2. Add header image (hero photo)
3. Set margins and fonts (Playfair Display for headings, Montserrat for body)
4. Export as PDF at 300 DPI
5. Upload to Etsy alongside Canva versions

**Visual impact**: Lower than Canva designs, but content is identical. Conversion rate impact is real but not fatal — practitioner buyers prioritize content quality over design polish.

**Recommendation**: Implement Canva workflow as planned. Only use fallback if Canva is demonstrably non-functional by June 24.
