## Part 1: Priority Species for Phase 2 Photography

### How This List Was Built

These eight species were selected from the `endangered-species-candidate-list.md` Tier 1 and Tier 2 pools by applying four filters simultaneously: (1) conservation status sufficient to anchor a conservation-education narrative, (2) at least one legal commercial seed or root procurement path available in May, (3) photo sourcing path achievable before May 30 through iNaturalist CC-BY, botanical garden outreach, or procured specimens, and (4) guide content value — medicinal, culinary, or ecological significance strong enough to sell as a premium Etsy guide.

The six species already listed in `PHASE_2_SOURCING_LOGISTICS.csv` (ginseng, goldenseal, black cohosh, bloodroot, ramps, wild bergamot) form the core. Two additions — Trillium and Lady's Slipper — are included because both have May photo windows and supply paths that are specific to this calendar moment.

### Species 1: American Ginseng (*Panax quinquefolius*)

**Conservation status**: CITES Appendix II (roots and whole plants; seeds explicitly exempt from CITES export controls). Wild harvest regulated in 19 states. Not ESA-listed. The US Fish and Wildlife Service maintains a state-by-state harvest permit database. Wild populations have declined 30–50% over the last 30 years in documented Appalachian locations.

**Why this species matters for photography**: Ginseng is the anchor of the entire endangered species guide series. The visual story — forked taproot resembling a human figure, red berry cluster, distinctive palmate leaves — is immediately recognizable to buyers who know ginseng by name but have never seen it in the field. The cultivation-vs-wild-harvest distinction is the central educational argument of the guide, and photographs of nursery-propagated roots vs. a historic BHL botanical illustration convey that argument more effectively than text alone.

**Photo requirements**:
- Prop: dried whole root (purchase locally or Mountain Rose Herbs) — cross-section showing root structure
- iNaturalist CC-BY: full plant habit in hardwood forest context; red berry cluster (September timing — archive photos only available now); palmate compound leaf detail
- BHL botanical illustration: 1912 "Ginseng and Goldenseal Growers Handbook" has engraved plates at biodiversitylibrary.org/item/116021 — public domain, no attribution required

**Procurement path**: Mountain Rose Herbs dried root (primary, 2–3 day ship). Spring ginseng seeds are a fall crop — availability near zero in May; do not wait for seeds. Dried root is the stronger identification prop in any case.

### Species 2: Goldenseal (*Hydrastis canadensis*)

**Conservation status**: CITES Appendix II since 1997. State-listed endangered in Connecticut, Georgia, Maine, Massachusetts, Minnesota, New Jersey, and North Carolina. IUCN Vulnerable. An estimated 95% of commercial goldenseal raw material is still wild-harvested despite documented population declines across the Appalachian range. Wild plants and roots require CITES permits for interstate commercial movement; cultivated material is unrestricted.

**Why this species matters**: Goldenseal's defining visual feature — the vivid yellow rhizome interior — makes it one of the most photogenic at-risk medicinal plants. A cross-sectioned dried root photographed against a white card communicates the plant's identity, its antimicrobial reputation (berberine alkaloid), and the conservation stakes in a single frame. No other species on this list has that combination of visual clarity and educational weight.

**Photo requirements**:
- Prop: dried root from Mountain Rose Herbs — photograph cross-section dry, then lightly misted (moisture deepens the yellow color; photograph within 5 minutes of misting)
- Prop: seed packet from Strictly Medicinal Seeds — flat-lay identification shot
- iNaturalist CC-BY: full plant in woodland understory showing hairy stem and lobed leaves; spring flower (white stamens, April–May timing — archive photos required now)
- Institutional: NC Botanical Garden (ncbg@unc.edu) — maintains documented cultivated populations; email outreach template in `phase-3-medicinal-herbs-sourcing-guide.md` Part 4

**Procurement path**: Strictly Medicinal Seeds (seeds, 8–12 days from order) + Mountain Rose Herbs (dried root, 2–3 days). Both orders overdue — execute today.

### Species 3: Black Cohosh (*Actaea racemosa*)

**Conservation status**: United Plant Savers At-Risk roster. State-listed endangered in Massachusetts; watch-status or special concern in Michigan, Ohio, Tennessee. Not CITES-listed, not ESA-listed. No legal restriction on cultivated plants, seeds, or dried root material. An estimated 95% of commercial raw material is still wild-sourced.

**Why this species matters**: The dramatic white wand-like flower raceme (June–August, reaching 4–6 feet) is the most visually striking flowering stage of any species on this list. For the May photography window, the raceme will not yet be present at Zone 5 elevations — the guide's hero image will come from iNaturalist archive photography. What can be photographed in May: emerging compound leaf clusters (ternate, toothed margin) that confirm the species for buyers who encounter it on an early summer woodland walk.

**Photo requirements**:
- Live potted plant from Strictly Medicinal Seeds: habit photo, compound leaf detail, scale reference against hand or ruler
- iNaturalist CC-BY archive: flower raceme (June–August archive, rich CC-BY supply); seed follicles (fall); full plant in forest context
- No dried root required — root is not the guide's primary identification anchor; the flower and leaf are more visually useful

**Procurement path**: Strictly Medicinal Seeds live potted plant ($8–12, ships March–July, 7–10 days from order). Order today; arrival expected May 16–19.

### Species 4: Bloodroot (*Sanguinaria canadensis*)

**Conservation status**: United Plant Savers At-Risk. "Exploitably Vulnerable" in New York; "Special Concern" in Rhode Island; harvest monitoring status in several states. Not CITES or ESA-listed. No restriction on cultivated plants. The spring bloom (March–April) is missed for the current window; foliage and rhizome photography are the May-achievable angles.

**Why this species matters**: Bloodroot is the most visually dramatic spring ephemeral in eastern North American forests — the brilliant white bloom on bare brown forest floor in March. That bloom timing is missed for May photography, but the orange-red rhizome (which gives the plant its name, exuding a red sap when cut) is as visually distinctive as goldenseal's yellow root and can be photographed from a procured rhizome specimen. The alkaloid content (sanguinarine) adds educational complexity that distinguishes a Seedwarden guide from generic plant lists.

**Photo requirements**:
- Prop: rhizome from Prairie Moon Nursery or Strictly Medicinal Seeds — photograph intact, then cross-sectioned to show orange-red sap (photograph within 20–30 minutes of cutting; the color oxidizes and dulls)
- iNaturalist CC-BY archive: spring bloom (March–April archive photos, excellent availability); leaf (distinctive round, deeply lobed palmate form, blue-green above)
- BHL: multiple 19th-century herbal illustrations available; suitable as a heritage-context image within the guide

**Procurement path**: Prairie Moon Nursery (rhizome/bare root, 7–14 days from order — check stock first) or Strictly Medicinal Seeds (seeds, 7–10 days). Note from `PHASE_2_SOURCING_LOGISTICS.csv`: bloodroot seeds have a one-week viability window post-collection; commercial spring seed is narrow. Rhizome is the stronger prop. If neither vendor has spring rhizome stock, the guide proceeds entirely on iNaturalist CC-BY and BHL illustration — no schedule impact.

### Species 5: Ramps / Wild Leek (*Allium tricoccum*)

**Conservation status**: Threatened in Quebec (commercial harvest prohibited by provincial law). The variety *A. tricoccum var. burdickii* is listed endangered in New York. Michigan prohibits harvest on public land. West Virginia caps national forest harvest at one grocery bag per person per visit per day. No federal ESA listing. Restaurant demand (primarily high-end dining) drove a documented collapse of multiple Appalachian population clusters in the 2010s.

**Why this species matters**: Ramps have more cultural weight than any other species on this list. The Appalachian foodway story — spring festivals, Cherokee *wasdi* harvest traditions, Quebec's harvest ban, the restaurant trend that overharvested entire hillsides — gives the guide a narrative arc that a buyer will remember and share. The visual is immediately recognizable: broad green lance-shaped leaves on a reddish stem base, emerging from bare brown leaf litter in early spring. Slow regeneration (5–7 years to reproductive maturity from seed) is the conservation argument made concrete.

**Photo requirements**:
- Fresh leaves from farmers market or specialty grocery (highest-value prop, available now or within days in early May): photograph against white card, showing the reddish stem base; mist lightly just before photographing
- Bulblets from Prairie Moon Nursery if available: bulb identification shot showing the white elongated bulb with papery skin
- iNaturalist CC-BY archive: woodland carpet emergence shot (March–May archive, excellent supply); flower umbel (June–July, after leaves die back)

**Procurement path**: Local farmers market or specialty grocery for fresh leaves (purchase May 18–19 for the May 20 shoot; leaves hold 48 hours refrigerated wrapped in damp paper towel). Prairie Moon Nursery bulblets as supplementary prop if still in spring stock (check availability first; spring ramp stock often exhausted by late May). EFN seeds are sold out; Etsy small-scale Appalachian native plant sellers are a secondary bulblet source — search "ramps bulbs cultivated" and confirm "not wild-harvested" before ordering.

### Species 6: Wild Bergamot (*Monarda fistulosa*)

**Conservation status**: Not broadly endangered nationally; listed as a species of concern in specific regional contexts, particularly shortgrass prairie and certain New England habitats where prairie communities have been reduced below 1% of historical extent. Included here as an accessible bridge species: lower conservation urgency than ginseng or goldenseal, but high educational value as a dual-use medicinal and pollinator plant, and widely available from commercial sources with no procurement risk.

**Why this species matters**: The lavender flower heads in late May through August are the most accessible flowering photography target on this list within the May window. Zone 5 bloom begins in late May, meaning the May 24–25 field session in the photography logistics plan has a realistic chance of capturing bloom stage. The dual-use story — edible tea herb, respiratory medicine in Cherokee pharmacopeia, specialist plant for bumble bee queens requiring longer tongues — differentiates a Seedwarden guide from any single-use herb list.

**Photo requirements**:
- Live potted plant from local garden center (Home Depot, Lowe's, or independent nursery typically stock Monarda fistulosa in May): full plant habit, flower head closeup if blooming
- Field photography May 24–25 (see photography logistics plan): bloom-stage habitat context photo in prairie or woodland-edge setting
- iNaturalist CC-BY: full bloom habit (July peak — archive photos for this angle); leaf and stem detail

**Procurement path**: Local garden center purchase on or before May 10 (zero lead time, typically in stock). No mail-order required for bergamot. Cost: $4–8 for a 4-inch or 6-inch pot.

### Species 7: Trillium (*Trillium* spp., non-federally-listed species)

**Conservation status**: United Plant Savers Critical (genus-level). *T. persistens* and *T. reliquum* are federally listed endangered (Georgia/South Carolina) — do not attempt to source or photograph these species without institutional oversight. Non-listed species (*T. grandiflorum*, *T. erectum*, *T. luteum*, *T. sessile*) are legally cultivated and sold commercially. The genus as a whole is a keystone indicator of old-growth forest health; a picked trillium sets back 7–10 years of growth from the rhizome. That single fact is the most powerful conservation education tool on this list.

**Why this species matters**: The three-petaled symmetry of trillium is among the most recognizable of all eastern wildflower forms. Buyers who have no idea what "Actaea racemosa" is will recognize trillium immediately. Including it in the guide series makes the conservation content accessible to a broader audience while the 7–10 year regeneration story delivers the depth that conservation-focused buyers require.

**Photo requirements**:
- Prairie Moon Nursery bare root or potted plant (*T. grandiflorum* or *T. erectum* — both legal, cultivated, spring-shipped): habit photo showing the three-leaf whorl and flower; rhizome reference showing the corm
- iNaturalist CC-BY: bloom-stage habitat photo (April–May; archive photos available); forest context showing colony emergence

**Procurement path**: Prairie Moon Nursery spring catalog (bare root divisions ship mid-spring; confirm availability by calling 866-417-8156 before ordering — spring trillium stock sells quickly). If unavailable from Prairie Moon: Strictly Medicinal Seeds or a regional native plant nursery (search "native plant nursery trillium grandiflorum" for your region). Budget: $8–15 for a 1-pack or 3-pack.

**Legality note**: Do not photograph or procure *T. persistens* or *T. reliquum*. The guide must name these two federally endangered relatives in the conservation section as an educational point, but their photos must come from institutional sources (US Botanic Garden or FWS educational materials) and must be clearly attributed.

### Species 8: Lady's Slipper Orchid (*Cypripedium* spp.)

**Conservation status**: United Plant Savers Critical. Most native *Cypripedium* species are protected by state law across their range; wild collection is illegal in nearly every state. *C. reginae* (showy lady's slipper) is Minnesota's state flower and protected. The primary threat is not harvesting but the trade in wild-dug plants presented as nursery stock — they die within 1–2 years of transplant because they depend on decades-old mycorrhizal networks that cannot be transplanted.

**Why this species matters**: Lady's slipper is the most visually compelling species on this list. The pouch-shaped labellum of *C. acaule* (pink) or *C. reginae* (white with magenta) produces a photograph that drives social media sharing and gift-market purchasing. The conservation education angle is concrete and actionable: how to distinguish nursery-propagated specimens from wild-dug (a life skill for any native plant buyer). The fact that seeds require specific mycorrhizal fungi for germination — meaning they cannot be grown from seed without laboratory protocols — makes cultivation from seed impractical for home growers and strengthens the "buy propagated, not wild-dug" message.

**Photo requirements**: Hillside Nursery (hillsidenursery.biz, Northampton, Massachusetts) maintains propagated *Cypripedium* specimens and has been photographed extensively. Contact for educational photo licensing (same template as in `phase-3-medicinal-herbs-sourcing-guide.md` Part 4). Minnesota DNR maintains a photo library with documented wild-context images; the photo use policies for educational commercial PDFs require research — contact minnesota.dnr.gov media office. iNaturalist CC-BY supply is thinner for lady's slipper than for the other species; focus on the institutional path.

**Procurement path**: Lady's slipper is the only species on this list with no viable mail-order physical specimen path within the May window. Do not attempt to order for photography — the plants are extremely expensive ($35–85 per plant for tissue-cultured specimens), require careful transport, and the photo value does not justify the cost when Hillside Nursery photos are licensable by email. Flag for in-person photography if Hillside Nursery (Northampton, MA) is within driving distance. Otherwise, proceed entirely on institutional photo licensing and iNaturalist CC-BY.

**Lead time note**: Hillside Nursery photo licensing response typically takes 2–4 weeks from initial email. Send the outreach email on May 9 (today). If no response by May 23, proceed with the best available iNaturalist CC-BY frames and publish the lady's slipper guide after institutional photo is confirmed, regardless of May 30 launch date. Lady's slipper is not the lead species for the launch — it can follow the core four (ginseng, goldenseal, black cohosh, ramps) by 2–4 weeks without affecting the launch's momentum.

### iNaturalist and Wikimedia Fallback

**iNaturalist CC-BY filter**: The non-negotiable fallback for every species except lady's slipper. Filter URL format: `https://www.inaturalist.org/observations?taxon_id=[ID]&license=CC-BY&quality_grade=research&photos=true`. **Critical license reminder**: iNaturalist's default observation license is CC-BY-NC (non-commercial). Only CC-BY observations are usable in Etsy-sold guides without individual photographer permission. Filter explicitly — do not assume.

### Integration into Field Photography (May 20–25)

Once procured specimens have arrived and acclimatized, integrate them into the field photography sessions scheduled in `PHASE_2_PHOTOGRAPHY_LOGISTICS.md` Section 7.

**Which specimens to photograph first (priority order):**

1. **Goldenseal dried root cross-section** — photograph on May 12–13 immediately after Mountain Rose Herbs delivery. The yellow color is the most distinctive and memorable image in the series; getting this frame early removes it as a production dependency.

2. **Ginseng dried root** — photograph on May 12–13 alongside goldenseal. Photograph both the intact root (showing forked shape) and a cut cross-section. Natural light on a white card; no scale bar needed but include a US quarter for size reference.

3. **Wild Bergamot live plant** — photograph May 10–12 immediately after local purchase. If the plant has any bloom buds, delay the flower-head closeup until buds have opened (typically 3–5 days at room temperature with indirect light). Leaf and stem habit shots on the day of purchase.

4. **Fresh ramp leaves** — photograph within 24 hours of purchase. Refrigerate until the moment of the shoot; mist with water spray bottle just before. Do not photograph wilted leaves.

5. **Black Cohosh live plant** — photograph May 19–21 after 48 hours acclimatization. This is the most time-sensitive live prop because the plant's condition directly affects the frame quality.

## Part 5: Contingency Plan

### Scenario A: Government or Conservation Restrictions Prevent Procurement

**Reality check**: None of the eight species on this list are federally listed under the ESA in a way that would prevent commercial cultivation, sale, or use of nursery-propagated material. The relevant restrictions apply to wild harvest and wild plant trade (CITES Appendix II for ginseng and goldenseal roots, state-level protections for trillium and lady's slipper). Purchasing nursery-propagated seeds, seedlings, or dried roots from commercial suppliers like Strictly Medicinal Seeds, Prairie Moon Nursery, and Mountain Rose Herbs is fully legal in all 50 states. If a vendor's website raises any concern about whether material is wild-collected, contact them directly before ordering and log the answer in WORKLOG.md.

**If a vendor cannot confirm cultivated (not wild-harvested) origin**: Do not order. Proceed to the next vendor. Do not use any wild-harvested material as a prop or source for guide content without a clear documentation of origin.

### Scenario B: Vendor Delays — Specimens Arrive After May 20

**Trigger**: One or more physical specimen orders does not arrive by May 20.

**Response by species:**
- **Goldenseal dried root delayed**: Mountain Rose Herbs is the only vendor with a 2–3 day lead time. If this order is delayed, it is a logistics failure, not a supply failure. Contact Mountain Rose Herbs immediately. If the order has not shipped by May 12, cancel and purchase at a local health food store or herb shop (goldenseal dried root is widely stocked in specialty retail).
- **Black Cohosh live plant delayed**: Proceed to iNaturalist CC-BY for habit and leaf shots. The guide does not require the live plant if iNaturalist supply is adequate. No schedule impact.
- **Ramps bulblets delayed**: Fresh leaves from the farmers market (purchased May 18–19) are adequate. No schedule impact.
- **Trillium delayed**: Proceed on iNaturalist CC-BY. Trillium is among the most-photographed spring wildflowers in eastern North America; CC-BY supply is good.

### Scenario C: May 30 Launch Scope Reduction

**Trigger**: By May 25, fewer than 5 of the 8 species have complete photo sets (at least 3 photos covering habit/root, leaf or flower, and one contextual or archival frame).

**Revised launch scope**: Launch 4–5 species guides on May 30, with the remaining 3–4 following on a rolling basis through June 15. The four most likely to be launch-ready (because their photo sourcing paths are lowest-risk) are:

1. American Ginseng (Mountain Rose Herbs root + iNaturalist CC-BY + BHL illustration)
2. Goldenseal (Mountain Rose Herbs root + Strictly Medicinal seeds + iNaturalist CC-BY)
3. Black Cohosh (iNaturalist CC-BY is abundant; no physical prop required to launch)
4. Ramps (fresh leaf from farmers market + iNaturalist CC-BY woodland emergence)

These four together form the "Appalachian Medicinals — Core Four" bundle that was the original Wave 1 scope in `endangered-species-candidate-list.md`. A May 30 launch of these four, with Bloodroot, Trillium, Wild Bergamot, and Lady's Slipper following in June, is a coherent scope reduction that does not damage the launch's credibility.

**If the scope reduction applies**: Update Etsy listing text to say "Core Appalachian Medicinals bundle — 4 guides" rather than implying all eight are included. Do not over-promise on the listing and then deliver fewer guides.

### Wikimedia Commons and iNaturalist as Permanent Infrastructure

The Wikimedia Commons CC-BY and iNaturalist CC-BY photo archives are not a "fallback" in the sense of being second-best — they are a permanent production infrastructure. Physical specimen photography adds proprietary images that no competitor has. Digital archive photos add depth and seasonal coverage that physical specimen photography cannot achieve in a three-week window. Both belong in every guide; the physical specimens are the enhancement layer, not the only layer.
