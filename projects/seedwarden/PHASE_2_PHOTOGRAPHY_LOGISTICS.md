## Section 1: Site Selection Criteria by Ecosystem

The following criteria apply across all ecosystem types before finalizing any field session location. A site that fails two or more of these shared criteria should be dropped in favor of a backup.

**Universal access criteria**: Confirm public land status or written landowner permission at least 48 hours before a planned session. Do not photograph on posted private land or within restricted restoration zones, regardless of species presence. In national parks and most national wildlife refuges, commercial photography requires a permit — plan 14 days minimum lead time. State parks, BLM land, national forests, and county parks generally allow incidental photography without a permit as long as no tripod or equipment anchoring is involved.

**Universal safety criteria**: Check current conditions for the site (flooding, fire risk, trail closures) via agency websites or AllTrails the evening before each session. Do not enter any site alone without leaving trip details with a third party.

### Desert and Arid Ecosystems

**Target species**: Opuntia (prickly pear cactus), Yucca (edible flower clusters), Desert Marigold, Saguaro (Arizona), Ephedra (Mormon tea)

**Site selection factors**: Visit desert sites during May before heat becomes limiting — morning departures by 7am, sessions completed by 11am. Spring annual wildflower windows close fast; scout potential sites for winter annual bloom indicators (Phacelia, Eschscholzia, Lupinus) in the week before scheduling. Sandy washes and rocky slopes support different communities than flat upland — document both if accessible. Access permits are required for designated wilderness areas within national parks (Sonoran, Chihuahuan, Great Basin); BLM desert land and national forest land in the Southwest do not require photography permits for individual field work.

**Seasonal timing**: Mid-May is the tail end of Sonoran spring bloom. Opuntia buds through flower stage is a May 10–20 window at elevations below 3,500 feet. Yucca flower stalks emerge May through June depending on elevation. For central and northern Great Basin, the annual window extends later (into June) at higher elevation.

**Specific site types to prioritize**: Creosote-bursage flats for Larrea and Sphaeralcea; rocky bajada slopes for Opuntia engelmannii; sandy washes for Ephedra and Yucca baccata. Avoid steep talus where stable tripod placement is impossible and specimen damage risk is high.

**Permit requirements**: Saguaro National Park, Joshua Tree NP, Chiricahua NM — written commercial photography permits required ($50–$150 range, 14-day lead). BLM Arizona, Utah, and Nevada — no permit required for non-commercial field photography; confirm status of any planned tripod setup. Sonoran Desert National Monument — permit via BLM Arizona office, 7-day lead time.

### Riparian and Wetland Ecosystems

**Target species**: Watercress (Nasturtium officinale), Elderberry (Sambucus canadensis / S. nigra), Cattail (Typha latifolia), Water Lily (Nymphaea odorata — roots edible for indigenous communities), Smartweed (Polygonum species), Stinging Nettle (Urtica dioica — riparian margins)

**Site selection factors**: Riparian corridors are among the most productive multi-species photography sites — 3–5 target species frequently co-occur within 100 meters of a creek or pond margin. Scout sites before committing: seasonal flooding and bank erosion can make previously accessible creek margins impassable after spring snowmelt. Use satellite imagery (Google Earth) to identify stable bench areas above the active floodplain. Wader-level access (rubber boots or neoprene waders) is recommended for any site photographing emergent aquatic plants. Assess water quality before any direct contact: avoid sites downstream of agricultural runoff (visible algae bloom, turbid green water, visible nutrient load).

**Safety protocol**: Never photograph solo in deep-margin riparian zones. Flash flooding risk is real in canyon-country riparian corridors even under clear skies — check upstream weather 24 hours before any canyon creek session. Carry a dry bag for electronics. Carry a whistle.

**Harvest-safe documentation notes**: Watercress is a common edible riparian plant but can carry the liver fluke parasite (Fasciola hepatica) in areas where livestock have access to streams. Photograph and note livestock presence in adjacent fields; include a warning note in WORKLOG.md for any watercress site where upstream grazing is confirmed.

**Seasonal timing**: May is peak growth for watercress (cool water preference), early elderberry bloom, and cattail pollen head emergence. Late May into June brings more mature cattail and smartweed bloom.

**Permit requirements**: Army Corps of Engineers Section 404 wetlands — no permit for photography only (no disturbance). National Wildlife Refuge photography — check individual refuge; some require photography permits for commercial use. State fish and wildlife management areas — generally open with hunting/fishing license or access permit; check individual state agency.

### Oak Woodland and Scrub Ecosystems

**Target species**: Miner's Lettuce (Claytonia perfoliata), Chickweed (Stellaria media), Wood Sorrel (Oxalis oregana / O. acetosella), Hawthorn (Crataegus species — edible berries), Wild Onion (Allium validum, A. tricoccum), Trout Lily (Erythronium americanum), Wild Strawberry (Fragaria virginiana)

**Site selection factors**: Oak woodlands support dense understory foraging species in May, when canopy is still partially open and spring ephemerals are at peak. Mixed oak-madrone and blue oak foothill woodland in California; post oak-hickory woodland in the Ozarks; coastal live oak scrub in the mid-Atlantic — all produce different but overlapping species sets. Target the dripline edge and shaded north-facing slopes first; full sun clearings hold different species (Rubus, Sambucus, wild rose) worth documenting as secondary targets.

**Public land vs. private consideration**: Foothill oak woodland in the West is predominantly private in California valley floors but abundant on BLM land in foothills. Ozark National Forest, Mark Twain National Forest, Ouachita National Forest — all carry extensive oak woodland accessible without permits for incidental photography. Southern Appalachian oak woodland on Pisgah and Cherokee National Forests is relevant for Trout Lily, Ramps, and Wild Strawberry; no permit required for individual field work, trail access only.

**Seasonal timing**: May 10–20 is the prime window for Miner's Lettuce (cool weather annual, bolts by late May), Trout Lily (ephemeral, already senescent above Zone 5 by mid-May), Wild Strawberry (flower to fruit transition happening mid-May at Zone 5 elevations), Wood Sorrel (continuous through summer). Hawthorn bloom window is May at most oak woodland elevations.

**Permit requirements**: National forest land — no permit for incidental non-tripod photography. State natural areas within woodland — check individual state DNR. Private land — written permission required; approach through land trust organizations for access to conserved woodland parcels.

### Coastal Ecosystems

**Target species**: Sea Rocket (Cakile edentula), Glasswort / Sea Pickle (Salicornia species), Orache (Atriplex species), Beach Plum (Prunus maritima), Sea Purslane (Sesuvium portulacastrum), Wild Beach Strawberry (Fragaria chiloensis)

**Site selection factors**: Coastal sites offer compact multi-species photography for salt-tolerant edibles. Target three micro-habitats: upper dune crest (Sea Rocket, Beach Plum), upper beach / wrack zone (Orache, Cakile), salt marsh margin (Glasswort, Sea Purslane). All three can often be accessed from a single trailhead. Avoid intertidal zone photography below the high-tide line — park on dry sand and work upslope. Carry weatherproof equipment bags; salt-laden fog is corrosive over multiple sessions.

**Tidal zone awareness**: Know high tide times for the specific site. Pocket beaches with no dune escape route require exiting before high tide — check NOAA tide tables the evening before. Salt marsh photography on soft mud substrate — probe ground with a stick before committing body weight to avoid unexpected sinkhole spots.

**Harvest safety**: Document whether any salt marsh or coastal flat is within a marine reserve or state shellfish closure area. Several Northeast coastal areas have closures due to harmful algal bloom events — check the state shellfish program website before scheduling.

**Permit requirements**: National seashore land (Cape Cod, Point Reyes, Padre Island, Gulf Islands) — commercial photography permits required, 14-day lead. State beach parks — usually open for non-commercial photography without permit. Coastal strand on BLM Oregon Coast — generally open. Marine Protected Areas in California — no permit required for photography only, but no harvest documentation photos (plant in hand being pulled from substrate) in MPA zones.

**Seasonal timing**: May is early for peak beach plum bloom in the mid-Atlantic (bloom typically late May into June); Pacific coastal species peak April–June depending on latitude. Glasswort is most photogenic in fall coloration (red-orange) but structurally identifiable through the growing season — May provides the green succulent-stage ID shot.

### Temperate Forest Ecosystems

**Target species**: Ramps / Wild Leek (Allium tricoccum), Wood Nettle (Laportea canadensis), Mayapple (Podophyllum peltatum), Pawpaw (Asimina triloba), Ostrich Fern (Matteuccia struthiopteris — fiddlehead), Jack-in-the-Pulpit (Arisaema triphyllum), Wild Ginger (Asarum canadense), Bloodroot (Sanguinaria canadensis), Solomon's Seal (Polygonatum biflorum)

**Site selection factors**: Southern Appalachian cove forests and northern hardwood forest on mesic slopes are the richest temperate forest foraging environments in the US. Target north-facing slopes with deep leaf litter accumulation — these hold the highest density of spring edibles. Old-growth indicators (large-diameter trees, tip-up mounds, multiple decay stages of coarse woody debris) correlate strongly with the best ephemeral wildflower populations. Secondary-growth forest on former agricultural land is less productive for ephemerals but may support good nettle and elderberry populations.

**Spring ephemeral timing**: This is the most time-critical ecosystem in the May photography window. At Zone 5 elevations, ramp leaves begin senescing by late May. Bloodroot is already fruiting by mid-May at Zone 5; only foliage and seedpod photos are possible (bloom was March–April). Ostrich fern fiddleheads are fully open by mid-May at Zone 5 — photograph both fiddlehead stage (edible, April) and open-frond stage (habitat context).

**Mushroom habitat note**: Temperate forest sites also support morel (Morchella species) fruiting in May at elevations corresponding to Zones 4–6. If morels are found during spring ephemeral sessions, photograph them in situ and document site conditions — this is opportunistic secondary content for the mushroom/foraging content track.

**Permit requirements**: National forest land in southern Appalachians (Pisgah, Cherokee, Nantahala, Jefferson) — no permit for individual photography, trail access only. Wilderness areas within national forests — no permit for photography without tripod anchoring. Private timber company land — access typically prohibited; do not trespass.

**Trail access notes**: Many productive forest ephemeral sites are accessible only via foot trail. Plan for 30–60 minute trail approaches. Carry water for the full session duration; temperate forest hiking in May can be warm and humid. Trail conditions vary with recent rain — check trail condition reports on hiking apps before committing to muddy or flood-prone approach routes.

## Section 3: Daily Field Checklist

### Pre-Shoot (Day Before)

Equipment manifest:
- Camera body or smartphone (camera preferred; minimum iPhone 12 or Android equivalent with 12MP+ resolution)
- Macro lens or macro attachment clip (60mm equivalent minimum for specimen closeups)
- Tripod (minimum; lightweight travel tripod acceptable for forest and coastal work)
- Remote shutter release or 2-second timer mode enabled
- Two fully charged batteries; spare battery or power bank
- Scale bar (3-inch / 7.5cm rigid plastic scale bar, USDA standard; bring two in case one is lost)
- Reference objects: a US quarter coin and a standard pencil as supplementary scale references
- Field notebook (waterproof paper preferred; Rite in the Rain standard)
- Regional botanical identification key specific to planned site (not optional)
- GPS device or phone GPS with offline maps downloaded
- Red permanent marker for labeling sample bags (should not be needed unless collecting voucher)

Safety and logistics:
- Water: minimum 2 liters per person for any session over 3 hours
- First aid kit including tick remover, wound wash, and closed-toe waterproof boots
- Printed or downloaded permit confirmation if applicable
- Vehicle with current registration and spare tire check completed
- Communicate itinerary (site name, trailhead, expected return time) to one contact not present

### On-Site Protocol

For each specimen documented:
1. Locate specimen in habitat-representative context before handling
2. Photograph specimen in situ from habitat distance (3–5 meters) — this is the habitat context frame
3. Move to 1 meter for full plant (habit) frame — capture entire plant or typical clump; place scale bar in frame
4. Macro closeup: distinctive ID feature — leaf texture, flower structure, stem cross-section, root collar if visible without disturbance
5. Photograph any lookalike species present within 5 meters and label clearly in field notebook
6. Record descriptive notes (see Section 5 template) before moving to next specimen

Minimum three angles per specimen: habitat context, full plant habit, diagnostic closeup. Preferred target is five frames: habitat, full plant, leaf detail, flower or fruit, and one additional angle selected for guide section fit (harvest method demonstration, size comparison against scale bar, or root/rhizome if safe to observe without significant disturbance).

Do not uproot, cut, or harvest any specimens being photographed unless the site expressly permits harvest and the guide content requires harvest-stage documentation. Note permission status in WORKLOG.md.

### Post-Shoot (Same Day — No More Than 24 Hours After Session)

- Transfer all images to primary storage device and one backup location (external drive or cloud)
- Create session folder: `YYYY-MM-DD_[site-name]/` within `projects/seedwarden/assets/wild-edibles/`
- Apply initial metadata tags in-camera or via Lightroom import: species name (tentative), site location, date, photographer initials
- Log session in WORKLOG.md: site visited, species photographed, frame count per species, any issues (poor light, specimen condition, missing angles)
- Flag frames requiring species verification with a red label — do not incorporate any frame into the guide until species ID is confirmed against a regional botanical key or iNaturalist observation consensus

Safety debrief:
- Check for ticks (full body check immediately after field removal — do not wait until morning)
- Note any plant contact that caused skin reaction — document species, contact site, and symptom for future safety notes
- Record field confidence level for any species where identification was uncertain

### Why Habitat Context Matters for the Guide

Readers in different regions encounter the same species in meaningfully different habitat configurations. A guide that shows Elderberry only in a backyard setting fails the reader in western Oregon, where elderberry grows in riparian corridors under 40-foot alders. A guide that photographs Stinging Nettle only in shade gives no help to the reader in a mid-continental farm creek edge where full-sun nettle is the norm. The habitat assessment framework captures the conditions under which the photograph was taken, which then informs the guide text's regional range notes and habitat descriptions.

### Data Integration Pathway

The habitat assessment form data maps to the guide template in the following structure:
- **Elevation + aspect + moisture regime** → "Where to find it" subsection
- **Canopy + shrub layer species** → companion plant association text (cross-referenced to the companion planting guild tables in `native-plants-guide-expansion.md`)
- **Disturbance level + invasives present** → habitat health note and harvest ethics guidance
- **Invasive species noted** → "Lookalikes and potential confusion species" subsection where relevant (e.g., invasive Allium vineale presence complicates wild onion identification)

All habitat assessment data is stored in `projects/seedwarden/data/habitat-assessments/` with filename convention `YYYY-MM-DD_[site-slug]-habitat.csv`. The CSV structure mirrors the form fields above to allow future database integration.

## Section 6: Post-Shoot Photo Processing Workflow

### Selection and Culling

Within 72 hours of each field session, complete a first-pass cull:

1. Review all frames at 100% zoom; discard any frame with focus failure on the primary subject
2. Discard any frame where the scale bar is not visible or is partially out of frame (exception: habitat context shots where scale bar is intentionally absent)
3. For each of the five required angles, keep a maximum of 3 candidate frames for final selection — do not attempt to final-process more than 15 frames per species
4. Flag any frame where species ID confidence is below High with a yellow label; these frames require verification before moving to processing
5. Flag any frame showing a toxic lookalike that could be misread as the primary species — these require a caption note during processing and cannot be used as primary ID photographs

**Culling standard for inclusion**: A frame meets the guide standard if: (a) the subject is in sharp focus at the primary ID feature, (b) the exposure is within 2 stops of correct (recoverable in processing), (c) the background does not obscure the plant form, and (d) no foreign objects or competing brand logos are visible.

### Metadata Standardization

Apply the following metadata fields to all keeper frames using Lightroom, Capture One, or the Photos app:

- Title: `[Species-common-name]_[site-slug]_[angle-type]_[YYYYMMDD]`
- Caption: Full species name (scientific + common), site location, date, photographer name
- Keywords: species name, genus, ecosystem type, guide section (e.g., "habit", "flower", "habitat-context")
- Copyright: Seedwarden / photographer name, year
- GPS: import from camera or enter manually from field GPS record

File naming convention on disk: `[species-slug]-[angle-type]-[site-slug]-[YYYYMMDD].jpg`

Example: `stellaria-media-habit-pisgah-nf-20260515.jpg`

### Editing Guidelines

Apply the following processing parameters in Lightroom Mobile or equivalent. Save as a named preset (e.g., "Seedwarden Field — Forest") and apply per ecosystem batch:

| Parameter | Forest / Riparian | Desert / Coastal |
|---|---|---|
| Exposure | +0.2 to +0.4 | +0.1 to +0.3 |
| Contrast | +18 to +22 | +15 to +20 |
| Highlights | -30 to -45 | -20 to -35 |
| Shadows | +20 to +30 | +15 to +25 |
| White Balance | 5400–5800K (warm, forest light) | 5800–6200K (cooler, outdoor ambient) |
| Clarity | +10 to +15 (enhances leaf texture) | +8 to +12 |
| Saturation (Greens) | +10 to +15 | +5 to +10 |
| Saturation (Yellows) | +8 to +12 | +10 to +15 |
| Crop | 4:3 for habit shots; 1:1 for social variants | Same |

Do not apply vignetting, heavy grain, or any presets that flatten mid-tones — field guides require tonal accuracy, not stylized effect. Skin tones and foliage colors must be representative of real field appearance. If a frame needs more than +1 stop of exposure recovery, discard it rather than publishing a noisy result.

**Scale bar visibility check**: After any crop or rotation, confirm the scale bar is fully visible in its intended frame. If it is cropped out, either widen the crop or take a note that this frame lacks scale — acceptable only for habitat context frames.

**No excessive filters**: Do not apply any filter that changes species color beyond color accuracy (e.g., do not allow a green-boosting filter to make pale yellow-green leaves appear emerald if that is not accurate). Guide accuracy supersedes aesthetic preferences.

## Section 8: Equipment Manifest and Field Setup

### Camera and Optics

**Primary camera (preferred)**: Mirrorless APS-C or micro four-thirds body — Fujifilm X-series, Sony Alpha, or OM System OM-5 are field-appropriate. Weather sealing recommended for riparian and coastal sessions.

**Lens recommendations**:
- Macro lens (minimum 60mm equivalent, preferably 90–100mm): required for diagnostic closeup frames. Third-party options (Laowa 65mm f/2.8 2x Ultra-Macro, Tamron 90mm f/2.8 Macro) are acceptable at lower cost than OEM options.
- Wide-to-standard zoom (24–70mm equivalent): for habitat context frames. A kit lens is sufficient.
- Do not use a fisheye or ultra-wide lens for habitat context frames — perspective distortion misrepresents plant scale.

**Smartphone alternative**: iPhone 15 Pro or equivalent with macro mode and RAW capture capability. For sessions where camera weight is prohibitive (long trail approaches), the Pro iPhone macro mode is adequate for most guide applications. Use a clip-on macro lens (Moment or Xenvo) for dedicated closeup work.

**Tripod**: Lightweight carbon fiber travel tripod (Joby GorillaPod 3K or equivalent for short sessions; Vanguard Alta Pro or equivalent for sessions where macro work requires precise angle control). Bring the tripod on every session — the temptation to hand-hold macro work in field conditions consistently produces unusable frames.

**Remote shutter**: Use a Bluetooth or cable remote shutter release for all macro frames. Camera shake at 1:1 magnification at field-appropriate shutter speeds is the primary cause of soft macro images.

**Reflectors**: A 5-in-1 collapsible reflector panel (32 inch) is the most effective field lighting tool. The white side fills shadows on north-facing slope specimens; the gold side warms cool ambient light in dense canopy. Requires a second person or a reflector arm on the tripod to be useful.

### Field Tools

- Regional plant identification key (physical copy — battery-free)
- iNaturalist app (offline mode downloaded for planned region)
- Measurement scale (3-inch / 7.5cm rigid plastic scale bar, bring two)
- Soil probe or tent stake (for checking soil depth and texture without full digging)
- Waterproof field notebook and waterproof pens
- Compass (backup navigation; phone GPS fails in dense canopy)
- Hand lens (10x loupe) for in-field leaf surface and feature examination
- Small plastic bags (for temporary voucher collection if species confirmation requires lab or expert verification — not for harvest)

### Safety and Logistics

- Water: 2 liters minimum per person per session; 3 liters for desert sessions over 3 hours
- First aid kit: wound wash, non-stick bandages, tick remover card, medical tape, emergency mylar blanket, nitrile gloves for handling unknown specimens
- Vehicle: 4WD or AWD recommended for desert and forest trailhead access; check road conditions before departure
- Vehicle emergency kit: jumper cables, tow strap, shovel for soft-ground mud or sand egress
- Permits and access documentation: printed or downloaded (do not depend on cell signal at the site)
- Personal protection: long sleeves for nettle and bramble sites; tick repellent applied before entry; gaiters for tall grass sites
- Sun protection for desert and coastal sessions: hat, SPF 50+, lightweight long sleeves

### Photo Documentation Standards — Standard Angles Reference

For quick reference on-site, every species documentation set should include these five standard frames in this sequence:

1. **Habitat wide** (3–5m distance, no scale bar, includes surrounding plant community)
2. **Full plant habit** (0.5–1m distance, scale bar in frame, entire plant or representative clump visible)
3. **Key ID feature closeup** (macro, scale bar in frame or coin for reference, sharpest possible focus on the most distinctive identifying feature)
4. **Seasonal feature** (flower, fruit, or seedpod depending on what is present — if none, photograph the feature most likely to cause lookalike confusion)
5. **Field context detail** (any additional angle serving a specific guide section — harvest method, root visible at soil surface, or colony growth form)

Each set should take 8–12 minutes on-site including setup, shooting, and note-taking. The 5-frame target keeps sessions moving. Do not spend more than 20 minutes on any single specimen.
