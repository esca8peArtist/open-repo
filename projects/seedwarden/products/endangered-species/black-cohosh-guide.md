# Black Cohosh
### *Actaea racemosa*

**Know it. Grow it. Protect it.**

*Published by Seedwarden — Appalachian Medicinals Series*

---

*Black cohosh grows six feet tall and flowers with dramatic white wands in midsummer. It is impossible to miss in a forest understory. It has been harvested commercially for over 150 years. And it is still, today, almost entirely wild-harvested despite being one of the most extensively studied medicinal plants in North America. That gap between what we know and what we do is the story of black cohosh.*

---

## A Note Before You Begin

Black cohosh is not CITES-listed and not federally endangered. That designation gap does not mean it is fine. United Plant Savers classifies it At-Risk. Massachusetts lists it as endangered. Wild harvest rates remain at approximately 95% of commercial supply, and the populations most accessible to collectors have been systematically reduced over decades of commercial wildcrafting. This guide focuses on cultivated production — the ethical and increasingly economical path for anyone who works with this plant.

## 1. The Plant: Identification and Natural History

**Scientific name**: *Actaea racemosa* L. (formerly *Cimicifuga racemosa*)
**Family**: Ranunculaceae (buttercup family)
**Common names**: Black cohosh, black snakeroot, bugbane, fairy candles, squawroot (archaic, should not be used)
**Native range**: Eastern North America, from Ontario and Massachusetts south through the Appalachians to Georgia, west to Missouri and Arkansas; primarily Zones 3–8; most abundant in the central and southern Appalachian hardwood forest

### What It Looks Like

Black cohosh is one of the most recognizable medicinal plants in eastern North America once it is in flower. Its scale alone sets it apart.

**Height**: 3–8 feet in full bloom. The plant has a distinctive silhouette visible from a distance in a forest understory.

**Leaves**: Large, compound, alternating on the stem, divided into groups of three (ternately compound). Individual leaflets are 2–4 inches long, with irregular toothed margins and a pointed tip. The leaf surface is smooth, medium green. The overall leaf architecture looks somewhat like a very large, coarse wild geranium.

**Flowers**: The most distinctive identification feature — a long, wand-like raceme (spike) of small white flowers, 1–3 feet long, appearing June through September. The individual flowers have no petals; the white puffball appearance comes from dozens of white stamens per flower. Multiple racemes typically emerge from a single plant. The flowers emit a distinctive unpleasant odor (hence "bugbane") that repels flies and beetles but attracts bumble bees, the primary pollinator.

**Fruit**: Round, dry seed follicles in fall, turning dark as they mature. Seeds are dispersed by gravity and small mammals.

**Rhizome**: Thick, black, knotted, horizontal. The surface is nearly black (hence the common name); the interior is pale cream-white. The rhizome has a bitter, acrid smell and taste — a secondary identification confirmation. Mature rhizomes are 1–3 inches thick and branch extensively. This is the commercial harvest target.

### The Only Lookalike to Know

**White baneberry** (*Actaea pachypoda*) and **red baneberry** (*Actaea rubra*) — both in the same genus — grow in similar habitat. They are much shorter (1–2 feet) and produce distinctive white or red berry clusters, not the elongated wand-like racemes of black cohosh. The dramatic height and raceme of flowering black cohosh is essentially unmistakable once you have seen it once.

## 2. The Conservation Story: At-Risk Without a Federal Listing

### The Status That Does Not Tell the Full Story

Black cohosh is not on the ESA federal endangered species list. It is not CITES-listed. At the global scale, NatureServe rates it as "Apparently Secure." Those designations create a misleading picture of a plant that conservation botanists and wildcrafters on the ground both describe as significantly depleted across much of its accessible range.

United Plant Savers, whose At-Risk designation is based on field observation and commercial harvest monitoring rather than range-wide population modeling, classifies black cohosh as one of their highest-priority species for monitoring and cultivation promotion. The discrepancy between the official "apparently secure" global status and the At-Risk designation reflects a well-documented pattern: a species can have a broad overall range while losing commercially accessible populations rapidly without triggering federal listing thresholds.

### What the Harvest Data Shows

Black cohosh is the highest-volume wild-harvested medicinal plant in the eastern US after ginseng. Annual commercial harvest estimates range from 750,000 to 1,000,000 lbs fresh root per year. Approximately 95% of that harvest originates from wild populations — an unchanged proportion despite 30 years of conservation attention.

The pattern mirrors ginseng with one difference: black cohosh is not CITES-regulated, so its trade is not tracked with the same documentation rigor. The conservation community's primary data comes from buyer records, state harvest reports, and field surveys of historically documented populations — all of which show declining root size, younger average plant age, and shrinking colony footprints in accessible collecting areas.

The 2024 Massachusetts endangered listing — the first state ESA listing for black cohosh — is one concrete policy response. More may follow.

## 3. Medicinal History and Current Market

Black cohosh has been used medicinally in North America for longer than the European botanical tradition has documented it. Cherokee and Iroquois healers used rhizome preparations for a range of conditions. European botanical medicine adopted it in the 19th century, and by the mid-20th century it was an ingredient in widely sold women's health tonics.

The modern market is driven almost entirely by one application: menopausal symptom management. Clinical trials beginning in the 1980s (primarily German, under the Remifemin brand) documented statistically significant reductions in hot flashes and other vasomotor symptoms. Black cohosh became one of the best-selling botanical supplements in the US and Europe — the American Botanical Council estimated US sales at $8–12 million annually in the early 2000s, and the figure has remained significant.

**The extraction problem**: Almost every commercially sold black cohosh product uses rhizome extracted primarily from wild populations because cultivated supply has never been developed at the scale the market requires. No market failure — commercial buyers simply did not create the incentive for large-scale cultivation when wild supply remained accessible. The conservation cost of that market structure has fallen entirely on wild populations.

## 4. Growing Black Cohosh: Three Methods for Different Sites

### Method 1: Wild-Simulated Forest Farming

Plant rhizome divisions or stratified seeds directly into suitable forest habitat with minimal site preparation. This method produces roots of maximum market value (wild-simulated roots look identical to genuinely wild roots and command similar prices from informed buyers) and provides the most authentic habitat restoration function.

**Best for**: Landowners with 0.5+ acres of mature hardwood forest, north or northeast aspect, adequate shade. Patience required — commercial-size roots in 7–10 years.

### Method 2: Woods-Cultivated (Recommended for Most Growers)

Raised beds in forest shade with amended soil. Supplemental irrigation during establishment. This is the standard approach in the forest-farming extension network and the most predictable for first-time growers.

**Best for**: Home growers with a suitable garden space under deciduous trees, or forested property with available ground. Commercial-size roots in 4–6 years.

**Setup**: Clear understory competition from a 4x8 foot bed. Loosen soil to 10 inches depth. Amend with 3–4 inches of composted hardwood leaves and leaf mold. Plant rhizome divisions 12 inches apart, 2–3 inches deep. Mulch to 3 inches with shredded hardwood leaves. Water in well.

### Method 3: Shade Structure Cultivation

70–80% shade cloth over raised beds in full ground exposure. This approach does not require existing forest and is appropriate for urban and suburban growers. Root growth is faster than forest-grown material (3–4 years to harvestable size) but roots are less dense and irregularly shaped compared to forest-cultivated roots.

**Important note**: Black cohosh grown under shade structures on level ground is susceptible to root rot if drainage is not excellent. Raised beds with sharp drainage are essential. A bed mixture of 40% compost, 30% perlite, and 30% native topsoil is appropriate for shade structure cultivation.

## 5. Propagation: Rhizome Division and Seed Starting

### Rhizome Division (Preferred)

Divide established plants in early spring (before shoot emergence) or late fall (after senescence). Each division should include 2–3 inches of rhizome with visible dormant buds and at least some fiber roots.

1. Dig and expose the full rhizome mass
2. Cut divisions with a clean, sharp blade
3. Dust cut surfaces with powdered cinnamon or sulfur to prevent rot
4. Replant immediately, 2–3 inches deep, buds facing up
5. Do not allow divisions to dry out between digging and replanting

Divisions from a healthy 5-year plant can produce 6–12 propagatable divisions.

### Seed Starting

Black cohosh seed has a double dormancy. Seeds collected fresh in fall (October–November) should be sown immediately in a cold frame or outdoor nursery bed and allowed to stratify naturally over winter for spring germination. Alternatively, store seeds in slightly damp peat moss at 33–38°F for 60–90 days (cold stratification) after an initial 60-day room-temperature period.

Germination rate from fresh-stratified seed: 30–50% under good conditions. First-year seedlings produce a single small leaf and establish slowly. Seed-grown plants reach harvestable rhizome size in 6–8 years.

**Supplier-stratified seed** is available from several sources and is the most reliable starting point for growers without experience with woodland seed stratification protocols.

## 6. Site Selection: Forest Understory and Garden Adaptations

| Factor | Ideal | Acceptable | Avoid |
|---|---|---|---|
| Shade | 50–80% | 40–85% | Full sun or deep shade |
| Aspect | North, northeast, east | Any with adequate shade | South or west in low elevation |
| Soil type | Rich loam, high organic matter | Loam with compost amendment | Sandy, clay, or rocky |
| Soil pH | 5.5–6.5 | 5.0–7.0 | Below 4.8 |
| Drainage | Well-drained, moist | Moderate drainage | Wet or compacted |
| Moisture | Consistently moist through growing season | Moist with irrigation during dry spells | Drought-prone sites |

**A note on shade tolerance**: Black cohosh tolerates significantly more variation in shade than ginseng or goldenseal. A site with 40–50% shade (such as the edge of a woodland or under a young tree canopy) will produce black cohosh with faster root growth, though less shade-grown roots may show slightly different alkaloid profiles. For market roots, 60–75% shade is standard.

**Companion indicators for site suitability**:
- American ginseng (*Panax quinquefolius*) — strongest positive indicator
- Solomon's seal (*Polygonatum biflorum*)
- Wild ginger (*Asarum canadense*)
- Bloodroot (*Sanguinaria canadensis*)
- Jack-in-the-pulpit (*Arisaema triphyllum*)

## 7. Year by Year: A Three-Season Cultivation Timeline

### Year 1 (Establishment)
- Rhizome divisions produce shoots within 4–6 weeks of planting
- Plants reach 2–3 feet in their first full season with good site conditions
- Flowers uncommon in Year 1; if they appear, allow them
- **Your job**: consistent moisture, 3-inch mulch, remove competition, monitor for slug damage (black cohosh is palatably attractive to slugs in the first two years)

### Year 2–3
- Full-size plants, typical 4–6 feet in flower
- Flower racemes May/June through August depending on latitude
- Rhizomes developing mass, branching structure visible
- Allow plants to flower and seed; seeds dispersed naturally will fill in bare areas of the patch
- **Your job**: same as Year 1; begin assessing patch density — thin or transplant if spacing drops below 12 inches

### Year 4–5 (First Selective Harvest — Woods-Cultivated)
- Rhizomes 1–2 inches thick on established plants, branched
- Harvest 20–25% of the patch: select the largest, most branched specimens
- Dig, divide the rhizome, replant 30–40% of the mass in the harvest hole before removing the rest
- Dry at 100–110°F until rhizome sections snap cleanly

### Year 6+ (Sustained Production)
- Annual selective harvest of 20–25% of mature plants
- Patch has likely doubled from initial planting area through natural seeding and rhizome spread
- Consider establishing a second production site with divisions from the primary patch

## 8. Harvest Ethics and Responsible Wild-Harvest Practice

The ethical case for not wild-harvesting black cohosh is the same as for ginseng and goldenseal: the commercial supply deficit cannot be filled by further wild harvest. Wild populations are not recovering because harvest exceeds regeneration rate.

If you are in a situation where you encounter wild black cohosh and are considering harvest:

1. Do not harvest plants that have not flowered (plants under 4–5 years old)
2. Do not harvest more than 20% of any colony in a single visit
3. Divide the rhizome mass and replant 30–40% before leaving
4. If the colony shows signs of stress (small plants, declining density), walk away entirely
5. Consider instead collecting seed from ripe follicles in fall — seeds can be replanted to strengthen the colony — and grow your own supply

The legal context: black cohosh has no federal restrictions on harvest on private land. Some states have limited regulations. But legal is not the same as sustainable, and the plant's conservation status is not protected by the absence of a federal listing.

## 9. Indigenous Knowledge and Cultural Context

Black cohosh was a significant plant in the medicinal traditions of several eastern woodland peoples. The Cherokee used rhizome preparations for musculoskeletal pain and as a gynecological medicine. The Iroquois used it in treatments of kidney conditions, rheumatism, and as a tonic. The name "cohosh" itself is derived from an Algonquian word — the exact etymology is disputed, but the Algonquian-speaking peoples were among the first to document its use to European observers.

The European and later American herbal tradition adopted black cohosh in the 19th century under the name "squawroot" — a colonial-era term that is ethnographically inaccurate, offensive to Indigenous communities, and should not be used in contemporary herbal literature. The plant's current common name, black cohosh, honors the Algonquian root of the word without the pejorative colonial overlay.

The transition from "squawroot" to "black cohosh" in mainstream botanical and herbal literature over the last two decades is a small but real example of the kind of language correction that makes botanical publishing more accurate and more respectful. This guide uses black cohosh throughout.
