# Goldenseal
### *Hydrastis canadensis*

**Know it. Grow it. Protect it.**

*Published by Seedwarden — Appalachian Medicinals Series*

---

*Goldenseal's rhizome contains a vivid yellow alkaloid called berberine. For a thousand years, that color was used to dye basketry and fabric, treat eye infections, and mark ceremonial objects. For the last forty years, it has been the reason 95% of all goldenseal on the commercial market is still wild-harvested, and the reason the plant is functionally extinct in large parts of its former range.*

---

## A Note Before You Begin

Goldenseal is one of the top-selling herbal supplements in the United States. It is also CITES Appendix II-listed, state-listed endangered in seven states, and still predominantly wild-harvested despite decades of conservation pressure. This guide does not describe where to find wild goldenseal. It describes how to grow it, why that matters, and what you can do right now.

## 1. The Plant: Identification and Natural History

**Scientific name**: *Hydrastis canadensis* L.
**Family**: Ranunculaceae (buttercup family)
**Common names**: Goldenseal, yellow root, ground raspberry, turmeric root, eye balm, Indian paint
**Native range**: Eastern deciduous forests, primarily from Vermont and southern Ontario south through the Appalachians to Georgia and Alabama, west through the Ohio River Valley to Nebraska and Iowa; natural populations are now fragmented and significantly reduced from historical range

### What It Looks Like

Goldenseal is a low-growing woodland perennial, typically 6–12 inches tall, emerging from a knotty, branching rhizome. Its small size and preference for shaded understory make it easy to overlook.

**Leaves**: Two large, deeply lobed, palmate leaves with 5–7 pointed lobes, each 4–9 inches across — recognizably distinctive once you know what you are looking for. Leaves are rough-textured, dark green on top, lighter beneath, with prominent veins. A single smaller leaf lower on the stem is the other leaf. The leaf surface looks almost hand-corrugated.

**Stem**: Hairy, erect, 6–12 inches tall. The stem base wraps in a papery sheath near the soil surface. A non-flowering plant produces a single stem with one leaf; a flowering plant produces a stem with two leaves at the top.

**Flowers**: Small, solitary, white to pale greenish-white, appearing April through May. The petals fall quickly; what remains is a dense cluster of prominent yellow stamens. The flower is modest — the stamens carry the color.

**Fruit**: By late summer, a cluster of bright red berries resembling a small raspberry replaces the flower (hence the folk name "ground raspberry"). The berries are edible but not particularly tasty — their ecological function is seed dispersal.

**Rhizome**: The diagnostic feature. Cut the rhizome and it bleeds a vivid chrome-yellow juice — that color is berberine, the alkaloid that drives the entire commercial market. The rhizome is knotted, branching, 0.5–1.5 inches thick, with a strong bitter-astringent smell. This is what buyers want. It takes 4–6 years of growth to produce a rhizome of harvestable size.

### What It Does Not Look Like

Goldenseal is sufficiently distinctive once you know it — the combination of hairy stem, large lobed leaves, and intensely yellow rhizome is unlike anything else in its habitat. The only reasonable caution is mayapple (*Podophyllum peltatum*), which also grows in similar habitat with umbrella-like leaves — but mayapple is larger, produces a large pale flower beneath a single deeply lobed leaf, and its rhizome is not yellow.

## 2. The Crisis: The Berberine Market and the Wild Harvest Loop

### The Market That Created the Problem

Berberine — the primary active alkaloid in goldenseal — has been the subject of intensive clinical research in the last two decades. Human trials have documented effects on blood glucose regulation, gut microbiome balance, and antimicrobial activity. As a result, berberine is now one of the fastest-growing standalone supplement categories in North America, sold at $20–60 for a month's supply at standard dosages.

What most berberine supplement buyers do not know: synthetic berberine and berberine from other plant sources (barberry, Oregon grape root) costs a fraction of goldenseal-derived berberine to produce and is commercially substituted in most mid-market products. The genuine goldenseal-derived berberine market is real but narrower than marketing implies — it is a premium and ethnobotanical product category.

The result: actual goldenseal demand from informed buyers who specifically want *Hydrastis* is high and growing. That demand still runs 95% through wild harvest. The Sustainable Herbs Initiative's supply chain audit of the US botanical supplement industry documented this in 2020: goldenseal is among the species with the smallest proportion of commercial supply sourced from cultivated plants.

### The Wild Harvest Loop

The loop is self-reinforcing. Wild goldenseal commands higher prices than cultivated goldenseal because buyers associate "wild" with potency (evidence for this is mixed, but the price premium is real). The price premium incentivizes wildcrafting over cultivation. Cultivation does not scale fast enough to compete on price with wild-harvested material because cultivated roots take 4–6 years to reach commercial size. Wild harvest depletes populations faster than they regenerate, which reduces supply, which increases prices further.

The only way out of the loop is cultivated supply at scale. That requires growers. This guide is one step toward creating more of them.

### Population Status

Historical goldenseal range covered most of the eastern US and Canada. That range is now fragmentary. State-listed status confirms the pattern: endangered in Connecticut, Georgia, Maine, Massachusetts, Minnesota, New Jersey, and North Carolina; threatened in Maryland, Michigan, and New York; vulnerable in Pennsylvania; and IUCN-assessed as Vulnerable globally.

A 2025 study in *Ecology and Evolution* (University of Pennsylvania) confirmed that goldenseal at the edge of its range in Pennsylvania shows acute sensitivity to climate and habitat variables — a finding consistent with a population under cumulative stress from both harvest pressure and habitat degradation.

## 3. Legal Landscape: CITES, State Laws, and the Cultivation Window

### CITES Appendix II (Since 1997)

Goldenseal has been CITES Appendix II-listed since 1997. The listing covers roots and whole plants in international trade. US Fish and Wildlife Service issues export permits for documented cultivated-origin and legally harvested wild material.

**The practical implication**: Any goldenseal root sold in interstate or international commerce should be documented as either cultivated or legally state-harvested. Cultivated goldenseal grown on your property is unambiguously legal to sell domestically and exportable with standard CITES documentation.

**Seeds and rhizome propagules traded domestically**: No CITES documentation required. Purchase, plant, divide, and sell cultivated starts freely within the United States.

### State Protections

State-listed status in seven states (endangered) and additional states (threatened, vulnerable) means wild harvest in those states is prohibited or heavily restricted. Cultivated goldenseal has no harvest restrictions in any state. Your cultivated crop is yours, harvestable at any stage, saleable through any legal channel.

## 4. Growing Goldenseal: Forest Farming Methods

### The Standard Approach: Woods-Cultivated Under Hardwood Canopy

Goldenseal is not difficult to grow if the site matches its needs. The most common cultivation failures trace to one of three mistakes: too much sun, inadequate moisture, or poor soil drainage. Get those three right and the plant is relatively forgiving.

**The core method**:
1. Select a site with 70–75% shade under native hardwoods (see Section 6 for site selection criteria)
2. Prepare raised planting beds 6–8 inches deep with a mix of native topsoil, leaf mold, and composted hardwood bark — or simply work the existing forest floor soil to 6 inches depth and add 2–3 inches of composted leaves
3. Plant rhizome divisions 6–8 inches apart, 1–2 inches deep, buds facing up
4. Mulch with shredded hardwood leaves to 3 inches
5. Water during establishment and during summer dry spells (goldenseal will not tolerate drought stress)

**Expected timeline**: First harvestable rhizomes in year 4–6. Plants spread by rhizome division naturally; a well-established patch doubles in area every 3–4 years without replanting.

### Container and Raised-Bed Cultivation (Urban and Small-Space Option)

Goldenseal can be grown in containers or raised beds under 60–75% shade cloth where forest conditions are unavailable. Use a mix of coconut coir, composted bark, perlite, and a small amount of native soil for mycorrhizal inoculation. Container plants require more frequent watering and may need liquid organic fertilizer (fish emulsion diluted to half strength) monthly through the growing season. Container-grown rhizomes are typically harvestable in 4–5 years.

## 5. Propagation: Rhizome Division and Seed Starting

### Rhizome Division (Recommended)

Rhizome division is faster, more reliable, and produces plants that are genetically identical to the parent. Divide established plants in early spring before shoot emergence or in fall after senescence.

1. Dig the entire rhizome mass from an established plant
2. Use a clean, sharp knife to divide the rhizome into sections 1–2 inches long, each with at least one bud (the small pinkish protrusion on the rhizome surface) and several fiber roots
3. Replant immediately at 1–2 inches depth; do not allow rhizome divisions to dry out
4. Water in well; mulch to 3 inches

Each division produces a new plant within the same growing season.

### Seed Starting (Slower, Good for Genetic Diversity)

Goldenseal seed has a complex dormancy requiring both warm-moist and cold-moist stratification periods — similar to ginseng. Commercial seed suppliers sell stratified seed ready for fall planting or cold stratification.

Seeds germinate in 15–30% of cases under good conditions — this is normal for goldenseal. Plant densely, expect attrition, and do not assume bare soil means dead seed. Some seeds take two full years to germinate.

Seed-grown plants are more genetically diverse than rhizome-propagated plants, which matters for long-term patch resilience as climate conditions shift. For a production planting, start with purchased rhizome divisions; use seeds to diversify and expand the patch over time.

## 6. Site Selection: Matching Goldenseal's Habitat Requirements

| Factor | Ideal | Acceptable | Avoid |
|---|---|---|---|
| Canopy shade | 70–75% | 60–80% | Below 50% or full shade |
| Soil moisture | Consistently moist but not wet | Moderately moist | Dry or waterlogged |
| Soil type | Rich loam, high organic matter | Sandy loam with added compost | Heavy clay or sand |
| Soil pH | 5.5–6.5 | 5.0–7.0 | Below 4.8 |
| Slope drainage | Gentle slope with moderate drainage | Level with good drainage | Low spots, waterlogging |
| Overstory | Mixed hardwoods (sugar maple, ash, tulip poplar, basswood) | Any native hardwoods | Conifer plantation |

**Companion indicator plants** (same as ginseng habitat — these species co-occur naturally):
- Blue cohosh (*Caulophyllum thalictroides*)
- Black cohosh (*Actaea racemosa*)
- Wild ginger (*Asarum canadense*)
- Trout lily (*Erythronium americanum*)
- Bloodroot (*Sanguinaria canadensis*)

Finding two or more of these species on a potential site is a strong positive signal for goldenseal cultivation suitability.

## 7. Year by Year: A Four-Season Cultivation Timeline

### Year 1 (Establishment — Rhizome Division)
- Shoots emerge within weeks of planting
- Single leaf, 4–8 inches tall
- Focus: maintain consistent moisture; mulch; remove competition within 6 inches
- Do not harvest or disturb

### Year 2
- Larger plants, 2 leaves on some specimens
- Rhizomes beginning to branch
- Some plants may produce small flowers — allow them
- Focus: same as Year 1; begin light fertilization with fish emulsion if growth appears slow

### Year 3
- Most plants producing 2 leaves and flowers
- Berries present by late summer
- Rhizomes approaching 0.5–0.75 inches thickness
- The patch has likely expanded 30–40% in area through natural rhizome spread

### Year 4–5 (First Harvest — Woods-Cultivated)
- Select and harvest no more than 25% of plants
- Choose the largest, most established plants
- Dig carefully; divide the rhizome mass and replant a portion before removing the harvest share
- Dry rhizomes at 100°F until completely dry (they should snap cleanly)

### Year 6+ (Sustained Production)
- Annual selective harvest of 20–25% of plants
- Patch continues expanding without replanting
- Consider dividing and transplanting to a new adjacent site to expand production area

## 8. Harvest Ethics: Roots, Rhizomes, and Responsible Practice

The same principle that applies to ginseng applies here: harvest is a management activity, not extraction. The goal is a patch that produces indefinitely at a sustainable rate.

**Harvest only roots that have developed for at least 4 years.** Younger roots have lower berberine concentration and lower market value; harvesting them also removes plants before they have seeded multiple seasons.

**Replant divisions before leaving the harvest site.** Take the marketable rhizome mass; replant the fiber roots, buds, and smaller rhizome sections. A harvested plant can regenerate from a small bud and root section left in the soil.

**Never harvest more than 25% of a patch in a single year.** Goldenseal spreads slowly by rhizome — over-harvesting removes the reproductive base and the patch shrinks rather than recovers.

## 9. Indigenous Use and Cultural Significance

Goldenseal was used medicinally by numerous eastern woodland peoples including the Cherokee (who called it *nv-wa-s-ti*), Iroquois, Ojibwe, Potawatomi, and others. Documented uses include: treatment of eye infections (a berberine solution applied to the eye mucosa), wound wash, treatment of skin inflammations, and use of the root as a vivid yellow dye for basketry, fabric, and wood staining.

The Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) Confederacy used goldenseal root to dye arrows and ceremonial objects. The Cherokee used it in combination with bear grease as an insect repellent and skin protectant. Ojibwe healers used it as a topical antiseptic. These uses preceded the modern clinical interest in berberine by centuries and are consistent with berberine's documented antimicrobial properties.

The irony of goldenseal's conservation status: a plant whose value to indigenous communities was integrated with sustainable harvesting practices has been pushed toward endangerment by a commercial market that extracted the active compound without maintaining the plant populations that produce it.

## 11. Conservation Impact: The Numbers Behind Your Garden

A single mature goldenseal plant produces a rhizome mass of 20–60 grams fresh weight after 5 years. A 100-plant patch at full production yields approximately 2–6 kg fresh rhizome per selective harvest. Dried yield: 0.5–1.5 kg per harvest event.

At current cultivated goldenseal prices ($30–60/lb dried), a modest 100-plant patch generates $30–90/harvest from a fraction of the patch, sustainably, indefinitely. The patch you establish this year is producing revenue and providing conservation benefit simultaneously 5 years from now.

More importantly: every cultivated pound available to buyers is a pound that reduces the incentive to dig from wild populations. The supply gap between documented demand and cultivated production is the precise space where forest farmers operate. You are not competing with wild harvest — you are displacing it.
