# American Ginseng
### *Panax quinquefolius*

**Know it. Grow it. Protect it.**

*Published by Seedwarden — Appalachian Medicinals Series*

---

*For two hundred years, the Appalachian mountains supplied the world's ginseng demand. Collectors walked the ridgelines in September, reading the forest floor for the telltale red berry clusters. They knew where the big roots were. Then, so did everyone else. This guide is about what happened next — and what you can do about it.*

## A Note Before You Begin

American ginseng is one of the most commercially valuable medicinal plants in North America and one of the most at risk. This guide is not a foraging guide. It does not tell you where to find wild ginseng because wild populations should not be dug for personal use. This guide teaches you to identify, cultivate, and understand a plant that can be grown legally and ethically — and that becomes harder to save with every root dug from the wild.

If you want ginseng, grow it. This guide shows you how.

## 1. The Plant: Identification and Natural History

**Scientific name**: *Panax quinquefolius* L.
**Family**: Araliaceae (ginseng family)
**Common names**: American ginseng, ginseng, sang, five-leaflet ginseng
**Native range**: Eastern deciduous forests, Zones 3–7, from southern Manitoba and Quebec to northern Florida; historically most dense in the Appalachian mountain corridor from Pennsylvania to Tennessee

### What It Looks Like

American ginseng is a slow-growing woodland perennial that emerges once from a single taproot each spring. Recognizing it correctly is non-negotiable before any cultivation or harvest decision.

**Leaves**: Compound palmate — three to five leaflets arranged like a hand. Most commonly three groups of five leaflets (hence *quinquefolius*, five-leaved). Each leaflet is 2–5 inches long, oval with a pointed tip and a toothed margin. Leaflets are largest in the center, diminishing toward the outer pairs. The leaf surface is smooth on top, slightly lighter beneath. Leaves emerge bright yellow-green in spring, deepen to mid-green in summer.

**Stem**: A single unbranched stem, 8–24 inches tall. The stem is smooth, round, and green, attaching to the root at a collar. Multiple leaves emerge from a whorl at the top of the stem. A two-prong plant has two leaf whorls; a three-prong plant has three — the more prongs, the older and more productive the root.

**Flowers**: Small and inconspicuous — a tight umbel of greenish-white flowers appearing June through July at the center of the leaf whorl. Pollinated by small native bees and flies.

**Berries**: The most identifiable field mark in late summer. A cluster of 6–20 bright red, glossy berries replacing the flower cluster by August–September. Each berry contains one or two wrinkled tan seeds. The red berry cluster is the clearest visual cue for locating plants; experienced collectors mark berry-bearing plants and return when legal harvest windows open.

**Root**: The economic target. A spindle-shaped taproot, cream to tan, with a firm fibrous surface and horizontal wrinkles that accumulate with age like tree rings. A 3-year-old root is pencil-thin. A 7-year root can be 1–3 inches long and finger-thick. A mature wild root showing multiple neck rings (each ring = one year of growth) is the premium product — wild roots over $500/lb dried. The root has a faint sweet, earthy scent when cut.

### Lookalikes

The cardinal rule: do not dig anything you are not completely certain is ginseng.

**Poison ivy** (*Toxicodendron radicans*) — three leaflets, not five; no red berry cluster; severely irritating to skin on contact. Do not confuse the two.

**Virginia creeper** (*Parthenocissus quinquefolia*) — also five-leaflet, but it is a vine, not an upright plant; berries are dark blue, not red; no economic ginseng root.

**Sarsaparilla** (*Aralia nudicaulis*) — the most common confusion species in the northern range. Three compound leaves at the top of the stem; berries are black or dark purple at maturity, not red; the root is horizontal rather than a taproot. Harmless, but not ginseng.

## 2. The Crisis: Two Hundred Years of Overharvest

The American ginseng story is one of the most documented collapses of a wild plant population on record, and it is still happening.

### The Numbers

Wild American ginseng harvest data is collected by the US Fish and Wildlife Service as a condition of the CITES Appendix II listing. The trend is unambiguous:

- **Pre-2015 average**: 62,000–65,000 lbs of dried wild root harvested annually across the 19 states that permit commercial harvest
- **2020**: 32,000 lbs — a 50% drop from the pre-2015 baseline
- **2024 (preliminary)**: Under 20,000 lbs — a 68% drop from baseline

The population is not recovering. Demand has not decreased. The shortfall is met by roots dug from progressively smaller, younger, more remote, and more ecologically fragile populations.

### Why the Collapse Is Accelerating

Ginseng takes a minimum of five years to reach reproductive maturity — and wild plants often don't bloom or set seed for seven to ten years. A root dug before it has seeded multiple times is a root that leaves no offspring in the ground. In states with active wild harvest, surveys consistently document declining population sizes and younger average root age in surviving colonies. The collectors still walking the ridgelines are digging roots before they've reproduced.

The market structure makes this worse. Wild-simulated and cultivated roots sell for $20–70/lb dried. Wild roots — identifiable by their natural neck rings and irregular shape — command $300–700/lb dried and occasionally more at auction. The premium for "wild" creates an active economic incentive to dig the oldest, rarest, most reproductively important roots.

### The Supply Disruption Signal

In 2024 the American Ginseng Alliance reported the US entering its first structural supply deficit for CITES-legal exported ginseng root: domestic harvest plus legitimate cultivation no longer meets export demand. Buyers in Asia are paying a premium that wild roots cannot sustainably supply and that US field cultivation has not yet scaled to fill. This is the window for forest farming — the economic signal that makes ethical cultivation financially viable for the first time in decades.

## 3. Legal Landscape: CITES, State Laws, and What You Can Do

### CITES Appendix II

American ginseng has been listed under CITES Appendix II since 1975 — the same year the trade pressure became impossible to ignore. Appendix II means: commercial trade is permitted with documentation, not prohibited outright.

**What CITES controls**: Whole roots and root parts crossing international borders require export permits from the US Fish and Wildlife Service. Those permits require documentation of legal state harvest or cultivation origin.

**What CITES does not control**: Seeds and seed-grown starts. CITES Appendix II explicitly exempts seeds from export permit requirements. You can purchase, sell, ship, and plant American ginseng seeds freely in the United States — and in most international trade — without CITES documentation.

**Practical impact for you**: Seeds from any of the commercial suppliers listed in Section 10 are unambiguously legal to purchase and plant anywhere in USDA Zones 3–7. No permits required. Your cultivated roots, once mature, are documented as cultivated origin and exportable under CITES with standard paperwork from any licensed buyer.

### State Harvest Laws

Nineteen states permit commercial wild harvest of American ginseng. Rules vary significantly:

- **Harvest season**: Most states limit harvest to September 1 through November 30 — after berries mature and before ground freeze. Harvesting before seed maturity is prohibited in most states.
- **Minimum root age**: Several states require that dug roots show a minimum number of neck rings (typically 5+), meaning no roots under 5 years old may be legally harvested.
- **Seed planting requirement**: Some states require that harvesters plant the mature seeds from any dug root at the harvest site before leaving.
- **Reporting**: All commercial harvest must be reported to state wildlife agencies, which report to USFWS for CITES compliance.

**Cultivated roots have no state harvest restrictions.** Roots grown on private land from purchased seed are your property, harvestable at any age, and need no state permit for domestic sale.

### What This Means for Your Operation

If you plant seed this fall, your roots are:
- Legal to grow in any USDA Zone 3–7 state
- Harvestable from your own land at any time
- Marketable domestically with no permits
- Exportable with standard CITES documentation through licensed buyers at maturity (5–7 years)

## 4. Growing American Ginseng: Forest Farming Methods

Three cultivation methods are in common use. They differ primarily in labor input and the price premium the resulting roots command.

### Method 1: Wild-Simulated (Highest Premium, Lowest Labor)

Seeds are planted directly in suitable woodland habitat with minimal site preparation. Plants are left to grow without irrigation, fertilizer, or pest management. The roots develop slowly over 7–10 years and are nearly indistinguishable from genuinely wild roots in shape, size, and chemical profile.

**Price premium**: Wild-simulated roots sell for $50–150/lb dried at local buyers and up to $300+/lb at specialty buyers who can document provenance.

**The tradeoff**: 10% establishment rate is common; no irrigation means drought losses in low-rainfall years; you wait 7–10 years for your first meaningful harvest.

**Best for**: Property owners with 2+ acres of mature hardwood forest who are comfortable planting heavily and accepting natural attrition. Think of it as an investment in the forest, not a garden.

### Method 2: Woods-Cultivated (Balanced)

Site preparation includes selective understory clearing, raised planting beds with added organic matter, and mulch cover. Plants receive supplemental water in dry spells. This approach produces roots that develop significantly faster than wild-simulated (harvestable in 5–7 years) while still retaining the irregular shape and natural chemical profile that justifies a price premium over field cultivation.

**Price premium**: $30–60/lb dried at most buyers.

**Best for**: Growers with 0.25–1 acre of suitable forest who want a realistic return within a 5–7-year window. This is the recommended method for most home growers.

### Method 3: Field Cultivation (Fastest Return, Lowest Price)

Shade cloth (70–80%) replaces forest canopy. Raised beds with amended soil allow dense planting. Drip irrigation controls moisture. Fungicide applications prevent the disease pressure that makes field cultivation risky. Roots are harvestable in 3–4 years.

**Price**: $20–35/lb dried. Roots are recognizably cultivated — regular shape, dense cell structure — and do not command a premium.

**The tradeoff**: High capital cost (shade structures, irrigation) and significant disease risk. Field-cultivated ginseng is susceptible to Alternaria leaf blight and root rot. This method is not recommended for first-time growers without extension support.

## 5. Starting from Seed: Stratification, Germination, and Year One

### Understanding Ginseng's Seed Dormancy

Fresh ginseng seed has a double dormancy: a warm-moist period is required to complete embryo development, followed by a cold-moist period before germination will occur. In nature, seeds drop in September, overwinter under leaf litter, complete warm-moist conditioning through the fall, then cold-stratify through winter, and germinate the following spring or sometimes the spring after that (two full years after planting).

Most commercial seed suppliers sell stratified seed — seed that has completed the warm stratification period and is ready for cold stratification or direct fall planting. Purchase stratified seed and follow this protocol:

### Fall Planting (Recommended)

**When**: September 15 through November 15, after ground temperature drops below 55°F but before hard freeze.

**How**:
1. Prepare planting site per Section 6.
2. Plant stratified seeds 1–1.5 inches deep, 6–8 inches apart in rows 12 inches apart.
3. Cover with 2–3 inches of shredded hardwood leaf mulch.
4. Do not irrigate at planting — fall moisture is typically sufficient.
5. Mark rows. Seeds look like soil to everyone, including you, eight months from now.

**Spring emergence**: Expect germination in 15–40% of planted seeds the following spring. Some seeds will take two springs to germinate — do not assume bare soil is dead planting.

### Cold Stratification and Spring Planting (Alternative)

If you miss the fall planting window:
1. Place stratified seeds in slightly damp peat moss or vermiculite in a sealed plastic bag.
2. Store at 33–38°F (a standard refrigerator) for 90–120 days.
3. Plant in spring after soil temperature reaches 45°F.

**Note**: Spring-planted seeds have lower germination rates than fall-planted seeds. Fall planting mimics natural conditions and is strongly preferred.

### Year One: What to Expect

First-year plants produce a single three-leaflet leaf on a 2–4 inch stem. This is normal. Do not disturb them. Do not fertilize. Keep mulch consistent (2–3 inches). Remove invasive competition (garlic mustard, multiflora rose) from within 6 inches.

First-year plants are vulnerable to deer browse, slug damage, and Alternaria leaf blight. Copper-based fungicide applied in May and June reduces blight risk without compromising organic status.

## 6. Site Selection: Reading the Forest for Ginseng Habitat

Site selection is the single highest-leverage decision in ginseng forest farming. A wrong site produces poor establishment, slow growth, and disease-prone plants regardless of how carefully you manage everything else.

### The Indicator Plant Method

The most reliable site selection tool is not a soil test — it is the presence of established ginseng companion plants. American ginseng evolved in a specific forest community. Where you find these plants, you find ginseng conditions:

**Primary indicators** (all three ideally present):
- Black cohosh (*Actaea racemosa*) — a positive ginseng site indicator in 80%+ of documented wild populations
- Bloodroot (*Sanguinaria canadensis*) — indicates rich, calcium-adequate soils
- Wild ginger (*Asarum canadense*) — indicates consistent soil moisture and good organic matter depth

**Secondary indicators** (strongly favorable):
- Trout lily (*Erythronium americanum*)
- Trilliums (*Trillium* spp.)
- Blue cohosh (*Caulophyllum thalictroides*)
- Solomon's seal (*Polygonatum biflorum*)

**Negative indicators** (avoid these sites):
- Running cedar / clubmoss — indicates acidic, nutrient-poor soil
- Bracken fern — indicates disturbed soil with low organic matter
- Cinnamon fern — indicates overly wet conditions

### Site Conditions Checklist

| Factor | Ideal | Acceptable | Avoid |
|---|---|---|---|
| Slope aspect | North or northeast-facing | East-facing | South or west-facing |
| Canopy shade | 70–80% (well-established hardwoods) | 60–70% | Below 50% or above 90% |
| Soil pH | 5.5–6.0 | 5.0–6.5 | Below 4.8 or above 7.0 |
| Soil drainage | Well-drained, moist | Moderate drainage | Wet or compacted |
| Organic matter depth | 3+ inches leaf litter | 2 inches | Bare mineral soil |
| Slope | 15–35° | 5–15° | Flat (pooling risk) or over 45° |
| Dominant overstory | Mixed hardwoods (sugar maple, beech, tulip poplar) | Any native hardwoods | Conifer plantation |

### Calcium: The Hidden Key

More ginseng sites fail from calcium-poor soil than from any other single factor. American ginseng is a calciphile — it thrives in soils with adequate calcium (pH 5.5–6.0). The best diagnostic: if sugar maple is dominant in your canopy and the forest floor has deep, sweet-smelling leaf litter, calcium is almost certainly adequate. If the soil is raw and acidic under pine or hemlock, test before planting and amend with ground oyster shell (not lime, which raises pH too fast) if calcium is low.

## 7. Year by Year: A Five-Season Cultivation Timeline

### Year 1 (Establishment)
- One three-leaflet leaf per plant, 2–4 inches tall
- Shallow root, highly vulnerable
- **Your job**: mulch, remove invasive competition, copper fungicide in May–June, fence against deer if browsing pressure is present

### Year 2
- One five-leaflet leaf on most plants; some still three-leaflet
- Root developing a distinct taproot shape
- **Your job**: same as Year 1; note which plants are thriving — site quality is readable now

### Year 3–4
- Two-prong plants dominant (two leaf whorls)
- Roots are pencil-width, 3–5 inches long
- Some flower clusters may appear — remove them if you want root size to develop faster
- **Your job**: continue mulch management; begin noting plant density — thin to 8-inch spacing if crowded

### Year 5–6
- Three-prong plants developing; flowers and seed production consistent
- Roots are finger-width, developing characteristic wrinkled neck rings
- Woods-cultivated roots are approaching marketable size
- **Your job**: allow seed set; collect ripe red berries and replant the seeds in a new section of your planting area to expand the patch naturally

### Year 7–10+
- Wild-simulated roots developing premium shape and neck ring count
- Mature plants reseeding the surrounding forest floor
- **Your job**: selective harvest — dig no more than 25% of mature plants per year; replant seeds from every dug root

### A Note on Patience

A ginseng patch is a commitment measured in decades, not seasons. Forest farmers in West Virginia and Kentucky report that patches established 15–20 years ago are now producing offspring in surrounding forest without any management — the plants have naturalized. This is the goal: not extraction, but restoration. Your patch becomes self-sustaining and expands. You harvest a fraction annually and the population grows.

## 8. Harvest Ethics: If and When to Harvest

### The Cherokee Protocol

The Eastern Band of Cherokee have a harvest protocol that predates the commercial trade by centuries: never dig a ginseng root before the red berries are fully ripe and ready to plant. Harvest means planting the seeds in the same location before you leave. You take one root; you leave the next generation.

This protocol — not an abstraction, a documented harvesting practice — maps almost exactly onto the modern CITES-informed best practice:
- Harvest only after seed maturity (September in most of the range)
- Plant seeds from the harvested root at the harvest site
- Never harvest all roots from a colony; leave the majority in the ground

If you cultivate ginseng on private land, this protocol remains the most sound approach to patch management. Harvest mature roots — those with 5+ neck rings — and replant their seeds. Your patch produces and regenerates simultaneously.

### Commercial Harvest Timing

If you intend to sell roots:
- Harvest in fall after leaf senescence (September–November) when root starch content is highest and roots are at peak weight
- Wash roots gently; do not remove the small fiber roots — buyers evaluate root integrity
- Dry at 75–90°F until roots snap rather than bend (usually 3–6 days in a dehydrator or warm dry space)
- Dried roots lose 70–80% of fresh weight — plan accordingly when calculating yield
- Store dried roots in paper bags, not plastic; plastic encourages mold

## 9. Cherokee Stewardship: The Original Harvest Protocol

American ginseng is called *a-ta-li-gv* in Cherokee. The plant has been central to Cherokee medicinal practice, trade, and spiritual life for documented centuries. The relationship between Cherokee communities and ginseng is not only historical — the Eastern Band of Cherokee today maintains a Culturally Significant Plant Species program that includes ginseng among its highest-priority conservation targets.

The Cherokee understood something that took Western conservation biology two centuries to document: a harvested ginseng root that is not replaced by its own seed is a net loss to the population. This is why the harvest protocol requires seed planting at the harvest site. The harvester is not merely an extractor; the harvester is a gardener working across a generation.

The deep overlap between traditional Cherokee stewardship and contemporary forest-farming practice is not coincidence. Both systems reached the same conclusion by observing the same plant's biology: ginseng requires long-term reciprocity, not extraction. When you plant the seeds from every root you harvest, you are practicing a form of stewardship that predates CITES by several hundred years.

Wild-harvested material may be illegally dug; it is also genetically narrower than cultivated stock and may introduce pathogens from degraded wild habitats. Commercial cultivated suppliers are the appropriate source.

## 11. Conservation Impact: Your Garden as Habitat Relief

Every pound of ginseng root you produce on your property is a pound that does not need to be dug from a wild population. The math is direct.

At current wild-harvest rates (under 20,000 lbs dried/year nationally), the commercial herb market — which demands approximately 2.5 million lbs annually — is running almost entirely on Chinese and Canadian cultivated supply. American wild ginseng is no longer a meaningful supply input; it is a price signal and a prestige product. The only thing that genuine wild American ginseng populations need right now is to be left alone to regenerate.

Home cultivation does not replace wild ginseng economically — the scales are too different. But it does three things:

1. **It reduces poaching incentive** by providing legal, accessible cultivated supply at competitive prices
2. **It creates naturalized populations** on private land where seeds escape cultivation and establish in surrounding forest — every well-managed forest farm expands local ginseng range
3. **It demonstrates viability** of the forest-farming model to landowners who might otherwise allow commercial wildcrafters access to their forests

A 100-plant patch in suitable Appalachian forest, managed with care, will have 250–400 plants in 15 years without replanting. That is what the species does when it is not being dug.
