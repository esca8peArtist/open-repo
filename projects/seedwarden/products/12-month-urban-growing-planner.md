## Important Notice

This planner is educational content based on general growing principles and real-world experience. Your results will vary based on your specific climate, soil, microclimate, and growing conditions. It is not professional agricultural advice, and Seedwarden is not liable for crop outcomes. Use it as a guide, adapt it to what you observe, and learn from each season.

---

## How to Use This Planner

This planner is designed to be printed and used — not kept as a pristine PDF on your hard drive. Write in the margins, cross things off, stick it to a clipboard in your growing space.

**Before you start:** Look up your USDA Hardiness Zone and your average last frost date. Both appear in the regional customization notes at the back of this planner. These two pieces of information will tell you which month's prompts apply to your actual calendar.

The planner is written for a "standard" timeline based on a Zone 6 last frost date of approximately May 1. If you're in a warmer or colder zone, see the **Regional Adjustment Guide** on the final pages to shift the schedule forward or back.

**Each monthly page includes:**
- What to sow (direct and indoors)
- What to transplant outdoors
- What to harvest
- What to save seeds from
- Succession planting prompts
- A notes row for your own observations

**January Focus:** This is the planning month. Order seeds before varieties sell out — heirlooms especially go fast. Audit last year's saved seeds. **February Focus:** For Zone 6, start tomatoes in the last week of February. Earlier than that and they'll outgrow their cells before it's warm enough to transplant. ## Regional Adjustment Guide

This planner uses a Zone 6 baseline (last frost approximately May 1, first frost approximately October 15). Use the adjustments below to shift the whole schedule forward or back.

---

### USDA Zone Reference

| Zone | Avg Minimum Winter Temp | Examples |
|------|------------------------|---------|
| 4 | -30 to -20°F | Minneapolis MN, Burlington VT |
| 5 | -20 to -10°F | Chicago IL, Denver CO, Boston MA |
| 6 | -10 to 0°F | Philadelphia PA, Columbus OH, Kansas City MO |
| 7 | 0 to 10°F | Washington DC, Nashville TN, Albuquerque NM |
| 8 | 10 to 20°F | Seattle WA, Dallas TX, Atlanta GA |
| 9 | 20 to 30°F | Los Angeles CA, Houston TX, Portland OR |
| 10 | 30 to 40°F | Miami FL, San Diego CA |

---

### How to Adjust

**Zone 4 (colder than baseline):** Shift all outdoor activities 3–4 weeks later than written. Indoor seed starting stays on the same schedule (count from your later last frost date). Your growing season is shorter — focus on fast-maturing varieties.

**Zone 5 (slightly colder):** Shift outdoor activities 1–2 weeks later. Count backwards from your last frost date (typically May 15–June 1) for all seed starting timing.

**Zone 6 (baseline):** Use the planner as written.

**Zone 7 (slightly warmer):** Shift outdoor activities 2–3 weeks earlier. You can start cool-season crops outdoors in late February or early March. Your fall season extends into November.

**Zone 8 (warmer):** Shift outdoor activities 4–6 weeks earlier. Spring planting begins in February or even January for cool crops. You'll have a true fall growing season (September–December) for cool crops. Summer heat may force a mid-season pause on cool crops.

**Zone 9–10 (warm/hot climates):** Warm-season crops go out in February–March. Cool crops can be grown almost year-round with a summer gap. Focus your lettuce, spinach, and kale growing in October–April. Summer (June–September) is your "rest" period for many crops.
