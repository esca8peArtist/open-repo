## How to Use This Calendar

### Step 1: Find Your Zone

Look up your USDA Hardiness Zone at planthardiness.ars.usda.gov or check the zone map reference below. Your zone is based on average annual minimum winter temperature, and it determines your frost dates and growing season length.

### Step 2: Understand the Format

Each zone section gives you three categories per month:

- **Indoor Start** — Seeds to start in trays/pots under lights or in a sunny window. Listed as "X weeks before last frost" so you can adjust to your exact microclimate.
- **Transplant Outdoors** — Seedlings (started indoors or purchased) ready to move outside. Includes hardening off notes.
- **Direct Sow** — Seeds planted straight into garden soil. Includes minimum soil temperature.

### Step 3: Adjust for Your Microclimate

South-facing walls, raised beds, and urban settings run warmer. North-facing slopes, valleys, and exposed sites run colder. If you're on the edge of two zones, read both and split the difference.

### Symbols

- **(H)** — Heirloom variety
- **(OP)** — Open-pollinated (non-hybrid, seed-saveable)
- **ST:** — Minimum soil temperature for germination

# Zone 3 — Northern Plains, Mountain Interior & Upper Great Lakes

**Last frost date (average):** May 15 - June 1
**First frost date (average):** September 5 - September 20
**Growing season length:** 95-125 days
**Key challenges:** Extremely short season, late springs, early falls, deep soil freeze, intense winter, wind exposure
**Season extension tips:** Wall O' Waters for tomatoes and peppers starting 2-3 weeks before last frost. Low tunnels and cold frames extend fall harvest 3-6 weeks. Mulch heavily after soil warms. Choose short-season varieties for everything — this is non-negotiable.

# Zone 10 — South Florida, Coastal Southern California & Hawaii

**Last frost date (average):** January 31 or earlier (many areas frost-free)
**First frost date (average):** December 15 or later (many areas frost-free)
**Growing season length:** 300-365 days (essentially year-round)
**Key challenges:** Extreme heat and humidity (Florida), salt spray (coastal), nematodes, whiteflies, tropical storms, sandy/alkaline soils (Florida), poor chill hours eliminating many temperate crops, cool-season crops only in "winter"
**Season extension tips:** The concept reverses entirely. "Season extension" means keeping cool-season crops alive longer into spring using shade cloth and mulch. The summer rainy season (Florida) or dry season (SoCal) is the limiting factor, not cold.
