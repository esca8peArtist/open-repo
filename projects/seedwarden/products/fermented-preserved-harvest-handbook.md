## Important Notice

Food preservation involves real safety risks, and the most dangerous ones — like botulism — give no visible or smell warning before making you seriously ill. If you are canning, pH testing is not optional; it is the difference between safe food and a hospital visit. Follow tested recipes exactly as written, especially processing times and acid ratios. Do not improvise with canning until you thoroughly understand the science behind it. When in doubt, throw it out. That jar is not worth the risk.

## Part One: Lacto-Fermentation

### The Science in Plain Language

Lacto-fermentation is one of the oldest food preservation methods humans have ever used. It requires no equipment you don't already have, no electricity, and no special ingredients beyond salt.

Here's what's happening: vegetables are covered in naturally occurring bacteria, including *Lactobacillus* species. These bacteria love an anaerobic (oxygen-free), salty environment. When you submerge vegetables in a brine — or create brine through the salt drawing out vegetable juices — you create exactly that environment. The *Lactobacillus* bacteria proliferate rapidly. They consume sugars in the vegetable and produce lactic acid as a byproduct. That lactic acid acidifies the environment, which kills or suppresses competing bacteria and pathogens. The result is preserved food with a pleasantly sour, complex flavor.

The key safety principle: **an acidic, anaerobic environment is inhospitable to dangerous pathogens, including *Clostridium botulinum* (the bacterium responsible for botulism).** Properly fermented vegetables are not just safe — they're safer than raw vegetables in some respects, because the fermentation environment actively suppresses pathogens.

**What can go wrong and how to recognize it:**

- *White film on surface:* Usually harmless kahm yeast — a surface yeast that looks like a thin white film or scum. Not dangerous. Skim it off, make sure vegetables are submerged, continue.
- *Pink or black mold:* Discard without question. This indicates spoilage. It's usually caused by vegetables not being fully submerged.
- *Truly bad smell:* Fermented vegetables smell sour and funky, sometimes aggressively so. A spoiled ferment smells rotten in a different, unambiguous way. Trust your nose.
- *Soft, mushy vegetables from day one:* Often from starting with overripe produce. Use firm, fresh vegetables.

---

### Salt Ratios: The Foundation

Salt concentration determines the environment. Too little salt and unwanted bacteria get a foothold before the lactic acid builds up. Too much salt and you suppress even the beneficial bacteria (and create inedibly salty food).

**Standard brine for lacto-fermentation: 2% by weight**

For a 1-quart jar:
- 2 cups water + 1 teaspoon non-iodized salt = approximately 2% brine

For a 2-cup (pint) jar:
- 1 cup water + ½ teaspoon non-iodized salt

**Why non-iodized salt?** Iodine is added to table salt to prevent iodine deficiency. It also inhibits fermentation. Use kosher salt, sea salt, canning salt, or pickling salt — any salt without iodine added. Check the label.

**For vegetable rubs (where you create brine by salting the vegetable directly):**
- Use approximately 1–2% of the vegetable's weight in salt
- For practical purposes: 1 teaspoon of salt per pound of vegetable works well for most applications

---

### Equipment for Apartment Fermentation

You genuinely don't need much.

**Mandatory:**
- Glass mason jars (wide-mouth quart jars are the most versatile)
- A way to keep vegetables submerged (see below)
- Non-iodized salt
- Clean hands and clean equipment

**For keeping vegetables submerged:**
The single biggest fermentation mistake is letting vegetables bob above the brine and get exposed to air. Solutions range from:
- A small zip-lock bag filled with brine, pressed on top of the vegetables
- A smaller jar filled with water and weighted inside the mason jar
- A clean rock, boiled and cooled
- Commercial fermentation weights (ceramic or glass discs) — not necessary but convenient if you ferment often
- Fermentation lids with airlocks — nice to have, genuinely not required

**Temperature:**
Room temperature (65–75°F) is ideal. Your kitchen counter is usually fine. Cooler temperatures slow fermentation (extend timeline by a few days). Hotter temperatures speed fermentation (watch more carefully for kahm yeast and taste earlier).

---

### Troubleshooting Guide

| Problem | Likely Cause | Solution |
|---|---|---|
| Nothing happening after 3 days | Too cold, or too much salt | Move somewhere warmer; check salt ratio |
| Vegetables soft immediately | Overripe produce | Use firmer vegetables next time |
| Very fizzy/active brine | Normal! Especially in first few days | Check that jar isn't sealed too tight |
| White film on surface | Kahm yeast | Skim off, ensure vegetables submerged |
| Pink or black mold | Spoilage | Discard |
| Very sour after 3 days | Warm temperature, small vegetables | Taste and refrigerate when it's where you like it |
| Not sour after 7 days | Cold temperature, large vegetable pieces | Wait longer; keep at room temperature |

## Lacto-Fermentation Recipes (Scaled for Apartment Harvests)

### Recipe 1: Fermented Hot Sauce
*Makes approximately 1 pint | Fermentation: 5–7 days at room temperature*

This is the hot sauce recipe to make when your pepper plants hit their peak harvest. It produces a complex, rounded heat — nothing like vinegar-based bottled sauces — with depth from the fermentation process.

**Ingredients:**
- ¾ lb fresh hot peppers (any combination — red jalapeños, serranos, your homegrown Lemon Drops or Fresnos work beautifully; avoid bell peppers)
- 4 garlic cloves, peeled
- ½ teaspoon non-iodized salt per cup of brine needed (see below)
- Optional: ½ small onion, a few sprigs of fresh herbs

**Equipment:**
- Wide-mouth pint mason jar
- Blender or food processor (after fermentation)
- Fine mesh strainer (optional, for a smooth sauce)

**Process:**

1. Remove stems from peppers. Leave the seeds in for more heat; remove for milder sauce.
2. If peppers are large, roughly chop them. Small peppers (like serranos) can go in whole.
3. Pack peppers and garlic tightly into the pint jar. They should fill the jar.
4. Make brine: dissolve ½ teaspoon non-iodized salt in 1 cup of water. Pour over peppers.
5. The peppers will float. Weigh them down with a small zip-lock bag filled with extra brine, or a clean weight.
6. Cover loosely with a cloth, coffee filter, or loosely placed lid. You want gas to escape but don't want to seal it tight during active fermentation.
7. Leave at room temperature, out of direct sunlight. Check daily.
8. After 2 days, you'll see tiny bubbles — this is CO2 from the fermentation process.
9. Taste at day 5. The peppers should taste pleasantly sour and funkier than they started. If you want more ferment character, leave another 1–2 days.
10. When ready: pour peppers, garlic, and their brine into a blender. Blend until smooth. Strain for a smooth sauce or leave chunky. Taste and adjust salt.
11. Bottle in a clean jar. Refrigerates well for 3–6 months. It will continue to slowly develop in the fridge.

**Notes:** The fermentation significantly mellows the raw pepper heat. If you want it hotter, add a fresh raw pepper at the blending stage.

### Recipe 2: Pickled Green Beans (Dilly Beans)
*Makes one quart jar | Fermentation: 3–5 days at room temperature*

A great use for a small harvest of beans — that pound of Dragon Tongue or Rattlesnake beans that isn't quite enough for a full meal. These are crunchy, garlicky, and keep for months in the fridge.

**Ingredients:**
- ¾ – 1 lb fresh green beans, washed and trimmed to fit in a quart jar
- 4 garlic cloves, peeled and lightly smashed
- 1 teaspoon dill seed (or 2–3 fresh dill fronds)
- ½ teaspoon whole black peppercorns
- Optional: pinch of red pepper flakes, a small dried chile
- **Brine:** 2 cups water + 1 teaspoon non-iodized salt

**Process:**

1. Make brine by dissolving 1 teaspoon salt in 2 cups water. Set aside.
2. Pack beans vertically into the quart jar, standing upright. This is satisfying.
3. Tuck garlic, dill, and peppercorns among the beans.
4. Pour brine over the beans. Beans should be fully submerged.
5. Weigh down if needed. Cover loosely.
6. Ferment at room temperature 3–5 days. Taste at day 3.
7. When pleasantly sour and tangy, move to fridge with a lid on.

**Notes:** These beans stay crunchy for 4–6 weeks in the fridge. The crunch is the whole point — don't let them go soft by fermenting too long. When in doubt, refrigerate early.

### Recipe 3: Kimchi-Style Fermented Greens
*Makes one quart | Fermentation: 2–4 days at room temperature*

This is not traditional kimchi — that requires napa cabbage and a specific technique — but it captures the same spirit with whatever greens you're growing. Excellent with kale, bok choy, turnip greens, mizuna, or chard.

**Ingredients:**
- 1 lb fresh greens, washed and roughly chopped
- 1 teaspoon non-iodized salt
- 3 garlic cloves, minced or pressed
- 1 teaspoon fresh ginger, grated (or ½ teaspoon ground ginger)
- 1–3 tablespoons Korean red pepper flakes (gochugaru) — or substitute any dried chile flakes; adjust to taste
- 1 teaspoon fish sauce or soy sauce (optional, for umami depth)
- 2 scallions, sliced

**Process:**

1. In a large bowl, toss greens with 1 teaspoon salt. Massage the salt into the greens for 2–3 minutes until they start to wilt and release liquid. Let sit 30 minutes.
2. The greens should have released a good amount of liquid. Squeeze them firmly.
3. Mix garlic, ginger, red pepper flakes, and fish sauce into a paste in the bottom of your jar.
4. Add drained greens and scallions to the jar. Pack tightly.
5. The greens should be surrounded by their own liquid. If not, add small amount of brine (½ cup water + ¼ teaspoon salt) to cover.
6. Press everything down so it's submerged. Cover loosely.
7. Ferment 2–4 days. This one moves fast — taste after 2 days.
8. Refrigerate when it tastes right to you. It continues to develop flavor in the fridge.

**Notes:** Great on eggs, grain bowls, noodles. Better after a week in the fridge than right after fermentation finishes.

### Recipe 4: Fermented Salsa
*Makes approximately 1½ pints | Fermentation: 3–5 days*

This fermented salsa has more complexity than fresh salsa — the tomatoes and other vegetables mellow and deepen. The texture changes (it becomes less crunchy), but the flavor trades that for something rounder and more interesting.

**Ingredients:**
- 1 lb ripe tomatoes, diced (about 2 medium tomatoes)
- 2–3 jalapeños or hot peppers of your choice, diced
- ½ white or red onion, diced
- 3 garlic cloves, minced
- Juice of 1 lime
- ¼ cup fresh cilantro, roughly chopped
- 1 teaspoon non-iodized salt
- Optional: ½ teaspoon cumin

**Process:**

1. Combine all ingredients in a bowl and toss. The salt will begin drawing out liquid.
2. Pack into a quart mason jar. Press down firmly so the vegetables are submerged in their own liquid.
3. If there isn't enough liquid, add a small amount of brine.
4. Weight vegetables down, cover loosely.
5. Ferment 3–5 days at room temperature. The tomatoes will soften and the flavors will mellow and combine.
6. Refrigerate. Keeps 2–3 weeks.

**Notes:** This salsa is softer than fresh salsa. Use it where you'd use cooked salsa — in eggs, on tacos, with beans. It's not a chip-and-dip texture. Consider it a different product, not an inferior one.

## Part Two: Water Bath Canning

### Safety Overview

Home canning has a serious safety component that fermentation doesn't: improper canning creates a genuine risk of botulism. Understand the fundamentals before proceeding.

**The key rule:** Only *high-acid* foods (pH 4.6 or below) can be safely water bath canned.

High acid foods include:
- Tomatoes (with added acid)
- Pickles and pickled vegetables
- Fruits and fruit products
- Jams and jellies

**Why acid matters:** *Clostridium botulinum* cannot produce toxin in high-acid environments. In low-acid foods (vegetables, meats, beans), the only safe canning method is pressure canning, which reaches temperatures that destroy botulinum spores. Water bath canning doesn't reach these temperatures.

**The tomato asterisk:** Tomatoes are borderline — modern tomatoes vary in pH and some heirloom varieties are lower acid than the USDA tested recipes were built around. Always add acid (lemon juice or citric acid) to canned tomatoes.

**Use tested recipes:** The USDA and the National Center for Home Food Preservation (nchfp.uga.edu) publish tested recipes with established safety records. This guide provides tested-style recipes with established ratios. Don't significantly alter vinegar-to-vegetable ratios in pickling recipes — that ratio is what maintains safety.

---

### Equipment

You genuinely don't need a dedicated canning pot. What you need:
- Any large stockpot deep enough to cover jars by 1–2 inches of water
- A rack or folded kitchen towel to keep jars off the bottom of the pot (direct contact causes thermal shock cracking)
- Mason jars with new lids (bands can be reused; flat lids should be new each time for a reliable seal)
- Jar lifter (cheap, essential, don't skip this)
- Wide-mouth funnel (helpful but optional)
- Clean towels

---

### The Water Bath Canning Process

1. **Sterilize or sanitize jars:** Wash in hot soapy water and rinse, or run through dishwasher. For products processed less than 10 minutes, you need to sterilize (boil empty jars 10 minutes). For products processed 10+ minutes, sanitized jars are fine.

2. **Heat lids:** Keep flat lids in simmering (not boiling) water until use.

3. **Prepare your recipe.** Keep food hot while you work.

4. **Fill jars:** Leave appropriate headspace (usually ¼ inch for jams/jellies, ½ inch for most other products). Remove air bubbles with a thin spatula or chopstick run around the inside edge.

5. **Wipe rims:** Any food on the rim prevents a proper seal. Use a clean damp cloth.

6. **Apply lids:** Finger-tight (snug but not cranked down).

7. **Process in boiling water:** Lower jars into water that's already simmering. Bring to a full boil, start timing. Process time depends on recipe and altitude. At elevations above 1,000 feet, add processing time (see altitude adjustment table below).

8. **Remove and cool:** Remove jars to a clean towel without tilting. Don't touch for 12–24 hours.

9. **Check seals:** Press the center of each lid. If it flexes up and down, it's not sealed — refrigerate and use within a week. Sealed lids are concave and firm.

**Altitude adjustments for water bath canning:**
- 0–1,000 ft: No adjustment
- 1,001–3,000 ft: Add 5 minutes
- 3,001–6,000 ft: Add 10 minutes
- 6,001–8,000 ft: Add 15 minutes
- 8,001–10,000 ft: Add 20 minutes

## Canning Recipes

### Recipe 5: Basic Tomato Sauce
*Makes approximately 2–3 pints | Process time: 35 minutes*

This is stripped-down, no-frills tomato sauce — just tomatoes, garlic, salt, and required acid. You can add herbs and more aromatics, but this base is infinitely versatile.

**Ingredients:**
- 5–6 lbs ripe paste or sauce tomatoes (Roma, Amish Paste, San Marzano types are ideal, but any will work)
- 4 garlic cloves, minced
- 1 teaspoon olive oil
- 1 teaspoon salt
- **Required for safety:** 1 tablespoon bottled lemon juice per pint (2 tablespoons per quart), OR ¼ teaspoon citric acid per pint

**Process:**

1. Blanch and peel tomatoes: Score an X in the bottom, lower into boiling water 30–60 seconds, transfer to ice water, slip off skins.
2. Core and roughly crush peeled tomatoes into a large pot.
3. Sauté garlic in olive oil briefly, add to tomatoes.
4. Simmer tomato mixture, stirring frequently, until thickened — about 45–60 minutes. The volume will reduce by roughly a third.
5. Season with salt. Taste and adjust.
6. To each hot, clean jar: add bottled lemon juice (1 tablespoon per pint), then ladle in hot sauce leaving ½-inch headspace.
7. Remove air bubbles. Wipe rims. Apply lids.
8. Process in boiling water bath: 35 minutes for pints, 40 minutes for quarts.

**Notes:** This is where your Costoluto Genovese, Amish Paste, or Opalka tomatoes shine. High-flesh, low-water tomatoes make better sauce.

### Recipe 6: Classic Dill Pickles
*Makes one quart | Process time: 10 minutes*

A tested recipe that produces genuinely crisp refrigerator-style pickles, not the soft commercial kind.

**Ingredients:**
- 1–1½ lbs small pickling cucumbers (4-inch or under are best)
- 1 cup white vinegar (5% acidity — check the label)
- 1 cup water
- 1 tablespoon pickling salt
- 2–3 garlic cloves
- 1 teaspoon dill seed (or 2 heads fresh dill)
- ½ teaspoon whole black peppercorns
- Optional: ¼ teaspoon red pepper flakes, 1 grape leaf or oak leaf (tannins help with crispness)

**Process:**

1. Wash cucumbers thoroughly. Cut off blossom end (the opposite end from the stem — this end contains enzymes that soften pickles).
2. Slice into spears or rounds, or leave whole if small.
3. In a saucepan, combine vinegar, water, and salt. Bring to a boil, stirring to dissolve salt.
4. Pack cucumbers tightly into a hot quart jar. Add garlic, dill, and peppercorns.
5. Pour hot brine over cucumbers, leaving ½-inch headspace. Remove air bubbles. Wipe rim. Apply lid.
6. Process in water bath 10 minutes.
7. Let rest at least 1 week before opening for best flavor and texture.

---

### Recipe 7: Chunky Tomato-Pepper Sauce
*Makes approximately 3 pints | Process time: 35 minutes*

A heartier, chunkier sauce with peppers — excellent for pasta, pizza, or shakshuka.

**Ingredients:**
- 4 lbs ripe tomatoes, peeled and chopped
- 1 lb sweet peppers (any color), seeded and diced
- 1 medium onion, diced
- 4 garlic cloves, minced
- 2 tablespoons olive oil
- 1 teaspoon salt
- 1 teaspoon sugar (optional, balances acid)
- 1 tablespoon dried oregano
- **Required:** 1 tablespoon bottled lemon juice per pint

**Process:**

1. Sauté onion and garlic in olive oil until softened.
2. Add peppers; cook 5 minutes.
3. Add peeled, chopped tomatoes, salt, sugar, and oregano.
4. Simmer 30–45 minutes until sauce thickens and flavors meld.
5. Ladle into hot jars. Add bottled lemon juice. Leave ½-inch headspace. Process 35 minutes.

---

### Recipe 8: Pepper Jelly
*Makes approximately 5 half-pint jars | Process time: 10 minutes*

Sweet, spicy, and extraordinarily good with cheese, on grilled meats, or as a glaze. One of the most crowd-pleasing things you can make from a small pepper harvest.

**Ingredients:**
- 1 cup finely minced hot peppers (jalapeños, serranos, your choice), seeds and ribs included for heat
- ½ cup finely minced sweet peppers (for body and sweetness)
- 1½ cups white wine vinegar or cider vinegar (5% acidity)
- 5 cups granulated sugar
- 1 pouch (3 oz) liquid pectin

**Process:**

1. In a large heavy pot, combine peppers, vinegar, and sugar. Stir well.
2. Bring to a full rolling boil over high heat, stirring constantly.
3. Add liquid pectin all at once. Return to full rolling boil, boil hard for 1 minute, stirring constantly.
4. Remove from heat. Skim foam if needed.
5. Ladle into hot jars leaving ¼-inch headspace. Wipe rims. Apply lids.
6. Process 10 minutes.

**Notes:** The jelly may not set immediately — give it 24 hours to see the final texture. If still liquid after 24 hours, it's technically a failure as a "jelly" but an excellent hot pepper sauce.

## Part Three: Dehydrating

### What to Dehydrate and Why

Dehydrating is the most forgiving of the three preservation methods in this guide. The only real mistake is not drying things completely, which causes mold in storage. When in doubt, dry longer.

**Best candidates from an urban garden:**

| Vegetable/Fruit | Notes |
|---|---|
| Hot peppers | Dry whole if small; slice larger ones. Store dry or grind to powder |
| Sweet peppers | Slice into rings. Reconstitute well in soups, stews |
| Tomatoes | Slice ¼-inch. Dry until leathery. Outstanding in pasta and sauces |
| Herbs (basil, oregano, thyme) | Dry entire stems; strip leaves when dry |
| Greens (kale, chard) | Make chips at low temp; dry completely for crumbles in soups |
| Beans (shell beans) | Shell and dry fully for long-term storage |
| Garlic | Slice thin; dry until crisp; grind for powder |

---

### Temperature Guide

**If using a dehydrator:**

| Food | Temperature | Approximate Time |
|---|---|---|
| Fresh herbs | 95–115°F | 1–4 hours |
| Greens (kale chips) | 125°F | 4–8 hours |
| Fruits and vegetables | 125–135°F | 6–12 hours |
| Hot and sweet peppers | 125°F | 8–12 hours |
| Tomatoes | 135°F | 10–14 hours |
| Shell beans | 115°F until dry | 4–8 hours |

**If using an oven:**
Set to the lowest possible temperature (usually 170–200°F). Prop door open slightly for air circulation. Check every hour. Oven drying is less precise but works fine.

**Testing for doneness:**
- Herbs: crumble between fingers; should be dry and crisp
- Vegetables: no moisture when pressed or squeezed; leathery but not sticky
- Peppers: crisp and crackly when whole; leathery when sliced
- Tomatoes: leathery with no moisture release when pressed

---

### Storage

Completely dried food stored in airtight containers in a cool, dark place keeps:
- Dried herbs: 6–12 months
- Dried vegetables: 6–12 months
- Dried peppers (whole): 12–18 months
- Pepper powder: 12 months (starts losing pungency faster than whole dried peppers)
- Dried tomatoes (without oil): 9 months at room temperature, longer in freezer

**Moisture is the enemy.** If you're not sure something is fully dry, put it in the fridge or freezer rather than a pantry shelf.

## Part Four: Seasonal Preservation Calendar

What to preserve and when — adapted for a four-season climate. Adjust based on your zone.

---

### Spring (March – May)

**What's coming in:**
- Greens: spinach, arugula, kale, lettuce

**What to do:**
- Blanch and freeze greens (spinach, chard) for cooking later — not salads, but anything cooked
- Dehydrate kale chips from the first big harvest
- Make herb pestos and freeze in ice cube trays (basil isn't here yet, but spring herbs like chives, parsley, sorrel are)

**Note:** Spring is short and cold-weather greens bolt fast. The window from "perfect salad" to "bitter and tough" can be 2 weeks. Preserve before the bolt.

---

### Early Summer (June – July)

**What's coming in:**
- Peas, early beans, cucumbers, zucchini, garlic, herbs, early tomatoes

**What to do:**
- Ferment: dilly beans, pickled garlic scapes, kimchi-style greens
- Can: dill pickles (cucumbers are peaking)
- Dehydrate: herbs (basil, oregano, thyme — harvest before flowers)
- Dry: garlic (cure on the counter for 2–3 weeks, then store whole)

**The garlic moment:** If you grew garlic, early summer is when you harvest it. Cure it in a cool, dry, ventilated space for 2–3 weeks. It's not "preserving" per se — just curing — but it's the most important timing-sensitive task of early summer.

---

### High Summer (August – September)

**What's coming in:**
- Tomatoes (peak), peppers (peak), eggplant, beans, cucumbers continuing

**This is the busiest preservation season.** Everything comes at once.

**What to do:**
- **Tomatoes (priority one):** Sauce and can, dehydrate (sun-dried style), ferment (salsa, hot sauce)
- **Peppers:** Fermented hot sauce (when fully ripe), dehydrate whole or as powder, can pepper jelly
- **Beans:** Ferment (dilly beans), let dry on the vine for seed and storage
- **Eggplant:** Difficult to preserve — eat fresh, roast and freeze, or make and can/freeze baba ganoush

**Hot sauce moment:** Wait until peppers are fully ripe (red, orange, yellow — whatever ripe means for your variety). Unripe green peppers make inferior sauce. The flavor complexity is in the ripe fruit.

---

### Fall (October – November)

**What's coming in:**
- Roots (beets, carrots, turnips), fall greens, winter squash, last peppers

**What to do:**
- Ferment: kimchi-style greens from fall brassicas (kale, bok choy, turnip greens)
- Lacto-ferment: beets (outstanding), turnips, carrots
- Can: apple butter or fruit preserves (if you have access to fruit)
- Dehydrate: root vegetable chips, the last of the herbs
- Winter squash storage: properly cured squash keeps months in cool, dark storage

**Fermented beets sidebar:** Slice beets thin, pack into a jar with garlic and a bay leaf, cover with 2% brine. Ferment 5–7 days. One of the best ferments in this guide that isn't technically in it — consider this a bonus recipe.

---

### Winter (December – February)

**No preservation, just using what you made.**

Winter is inventory time: check your canned goods for seal integrity, use oldest preserves first, replenish as needed. It's also planning time — what sold out fastest, what you wish you had more of, what you'll grow more of next year to preserve.

---

## A Note on Small-Batch Mindset

The recipes in this guide make one quart or one pint of things, not a shelf full. This is intentional.

A single quart of fermented hot sauce is three or four months of excellent hot sauce. A pint of pepper jelly is eight to ten servings. A quart of dilly beans is six to eight servings as a condiment. These are not inadequate quantities — they are appropriate quantities for an apartment garden.

Preserve what you actually grew, in amounts that match what you'll use. There's nothing sustainable or satisfying about a shelf full of canned goods you never finish.
