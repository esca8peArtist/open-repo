**Description**

Five of the herbs most closely associated with women's hormonal health — in a single, research-grounded cultivation and traditional use guide. This is not a supplement label or a recipe list. **What's inside:**

**Black Cohosh** (*Actaea racemosa*) — The most widely sold menopausal herb in North America, and one of the most overharvested. This guide covers forest farming and shade-garden cultivation so you can grow your own from verified cultivated stock. Includes the United Plant Savers At-Risk status, why 95% of commercial black cohosh is still wild-harvested, and how to find Forest Grown Verified sources. Isoflavone content documented in the peer-reviewed literature; framed as "contains isoflavones, compounds studied for phytoestrogenic activity" — not a disease claim. Cultivation: easy, annual in most zones, self-seeding. Edibility note: flowers are edible (forager cohort crossover).

**What this is not:**

This guide is for educational purposes only. It describes traditional and historical uses and does not constitute medical advice. Consult a qualified healthcare provider before using any herb therapeutically. No disease treatment claims are made.

**Description**

The four herbs most commonly turned to for respiratory and seasonal immune support — grown, not just purchased. This guide covers cultivation from seed to harvest, traditional folk medicine use, ethical sourcing, and the conservation notes that most herb guides skip entirely.

**What's inside:**

**Elderberry** (*Sambucus nigra* and *S. canadensis*) — Native elderberry is one of the most searched medicinal herbs on Etsy, and almost every guide covers syrup recipes. This one covers the shrub itself: planting, establishment, coppicing for maximum berry yield, harvest timing, and processing from garden to syrup base. Covers both *S. nigra* (European elder) and *S. canadensis* (native American elder) with notes on cultivated varieties (Adams, Bob Gordon). Elderflower culinary use included — the forager crossover the recipe guides ignore. Evidence note: elderberry is one of the best-studied immune herbs; the guide describes the Sambucol randomized controlled trial results using appropriate qualifying language.

**Mullein** (*Verbascum thapsus*) — The roadside apothecary. Distinctive biennial with a first-year rosette and second-year flower spike reaching six feet. Legal to harvest from wild populations in most states (invasive or naturalized); seed collection from established plants is free. Cultivation: direct sow in fall; thin to 12 inches; prefers poor, well-drained soil. Traditional use for dry, irritated respiratory passages — leaf tea and the flower-in-oil ear preparation. Regional note: mullein is listed as a noxious weed in some western states; the guide covers this with state-specific wildcrafting guidance.

**Echinacea** (*Echinacea purpurea* and *E. angustifolia*) — The "two echinacea" angle is the hook: *E. purpurea* is common, widely cultivated, and easy to grow. *E. angustifolia* is the prairie species on the United Plant Savers At-Risk list — overharvested for the supplement market. Practitioners specifically want to know which species is which. The guide covers both, with cultivation guides tailored to each and an explicit sourcing ethics section for *E. angustifolia*. Evidence: most heavily researched herb in this bundle; multiple meta-analyses on cold duration cited descriptively.

**Thyme** (*Thymus vulgaris*) — The culinary herb that doubles as a serious medicinal plant. Thymol content (the active constituent studied for antimicrobial properties in laboratory settings) is documented. Cultivation: perennial in zones 5–9; container-hardy everywhere. Harvest timing (just before flowering for highest volatile oil content); drying and storage. The culinary-to-medicinal bridge species for buyers who don't think of themselves as herbalists yet.

**Who this is for:**

Anyone who reaches for elderberry syrup every winter and wants to understand what they are growing. Clinical herbalists building a home apothecary for cold and flu season. Homesteaders and permaculture gardeners adding medicinal plants to an existing food forest. Gift buyers for the natural wellness household in their life — peak demand October through February.

**What this is not:**

Educational and informational only. Traditional and historical use descriptions throughout. No disease treatment or prevention claims. Consult a qualified healthcare provider before therapeutic use.

The digestive use is the anti-inflammatory traditional application — calendula tea and tincture used in European herbalism for gastric irritation and mucosal support. The guide covers: rhizome sourcing (grocery store ginger is often treated; get untreated rhizomes from a tropical plant supplier or Strictly Medicinal Seeds); growing conditions; and multiple preparation methods (fresh root grated, dried and powdered, syrup). Evidence base: ginger has the most consistent research support for nausea of any herb in this bundle — described with appropriate qualification.
