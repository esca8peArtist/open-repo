# Goldenseal (*Hydrastis canadensis*): Conservation Status, CITES Listing, and Why Cultivated Sources Matter

Goldenseal is one of the most recognized medicinal plants in North American herbal practice. It is also one of the most over-harvested.

Before reaching for goldenseal — as a supplement, a tincture, or an ingredient in a formulation you recommend to clients — it is worth understanding exactly why this plant is under regulatory and conservation pressure, what CITES Appendix II means in practice, and what the cultivated sourcing alternatives actually look like.

---

## Where Goldenseal Grows — and Why It Is Disappearing

*Hydrastis canadensis* is a native woodland perennial of the eastern United States. It grows in rich, moist hardwood forest understory — the kind of habitat that has been declining steadily for two centuries as forests were cleared for agriculture and development. In its natural habitat, goldenseal is a slow-growing, clonal plant. A colony established from seed can take five to seven years to reach harvestable size. When a wild population is dug out, recovery is measured in decades, not seasons.

United Plant Savers (UpS) maintains an "At-Risk" list of native medicinal plants facing serious conservation pressure. Goldenseal has been on that list since UpS was founded in 1994. The organization classifies it as one of the most at-risk species in North American herbal medicine alongside American ginseng, bloodroot, and black cohosh.

The reasons are straightforward: goldenseal's roots contain berberine and other isoquinoline alkaloids that have been the subject of substantial clinical research, generating consistent consumer demand. That demand is largely met by wild-harvested material. Most commercial goldenseal sold in the United States is still collected from wild populations, despite the availability of cultivated alternatives.

---

## CITES Appendix II: What It Actually Means

Goldenseal was listed on CITES Appendix II in 1997. CITES — the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species — does not prohibit trade in Appendix II species, but it does regulate it. Any international trade in wild-harvested goldenseal requires export permits from the country of origin, and importing countries must verify that the trade is not detrimental to wild population survival.

In practice, CITES Appendix II listing for goldenseal means:

- International shipments of wild-harvested material require documentation
- Commercial importers must track sourcing and demonstrate legal harvest
- The listing signals international recognition that unregulated trade poses a threat to the species

What CITES does not do is stop domestic trade within the United States. Wildcrafting and selling goldenseal domestically remains legal in most states. State-level protections vary: goldenseal is listed as Endangered in Connecticut, Georgia, Maine, Massachusetts, Minnesota, New Jersey, and North Carolina. In those states, harvest from wild populations is prohibited or restricted. Elsewhere, legal harvest continues.

This regulatory patchwork is one reason that the sourcing question falls to practitioners and informed consumers rather than being resolved by law.

## Forest Grown Verified: The Cultivated Alternative

United Plant Savers developed the Forest Grown Verified (FGV) certification program specifically to address the demand gap created by at-risk native species. FGV-certified growers produce goldenseal using forest farming methods — cultivating plants under established woodland canopy in conditions that mimic natural habitat. The plants take longer to mature than if grown in open fields, but the resulting root quality is comparable to wild-harvested material, and the ecological impact is fundamentally different.

The FGV participant directory is publicly accessible at unitedplantsavers.org/forest-grown-verified. Retailers who carry FGV-certified goldenseal include Zack Woods Herb Farm in Vermont and Pacific Botanicals in Oregon. Some Mountain Rose Herbs goldenseal is FGV-certified — check the product page for the certification notation.

When purchasing goldenseal for your practice or recommending it to clients, asking for FGV certification is the most straightforward way to verify that the supply chain does not draw on declining wild populations.

---

## What This Means for Your Practice

The clinical evidence for goldenseal is real — berberine has been studied for antimicrobial activity, gastrointestinal function, and immune support, with a body of peer-reviewed literature that justifies its place in herbal practice. The conservation status does not change the evidence. It changes the sourcing obligation.

For practitioners who recommend goldenseal: specify cultivated, FGV-certified sources in your formulation notes. For clients who purchase goldenseal independently: point them to the FGV directory rather than leaving sourcing to chance. For product formulations that include goldenseal: require supplier documentation and make FGV certification a vendor selection criterion.
