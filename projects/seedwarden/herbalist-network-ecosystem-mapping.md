# Herbalist Network Ecosystem Mapping — Phase 3 Practitioner Targeting

**Prepared**: May 20, 2026  
**Research window**: 2026-05-20  
**Purpose**: Audience research supporting Phase 3 launch (June 22 – July 13, 2026). Maps practitioner organizations, community hubs, top voices, and distribution opportunities for Seedwarden's medicinal herb bundles ($120–$150 practitioner tier).

---

## Part 1: Network Landscape Overview

### 1.1 American Herbalists Guild (AHG)

The AHG is the primary professional body for non-licensed clinical herbalists in the United States. Founded 1989. Voluntary membership with a credentialing pathway called Registered Herbalist (RH), which requires 1,200+ hours of training and clinical practice.

**Membership**: No publicly disclosed total membership count. The RH directory lists practitioner-level members. Estimates in the PHASE_3_AUDIENCE_STRATEGY.md place RH-designated holders plus active members at 4,000–6,000; informal practitioners who identify as herbalists bring the broader community to 15,000–25,000 in the US. The AHG directory is filterable by specialty (Women's Health, Respiratory, Nervines, Digestive), making it the best cold-outreach starting point for Seedwarden.

**Chapters**: 13 active chapters as of May 2026 (down from ~20 several years ago). Chapter locations: Arizona, DMV (Washington DC area), Florida West Coast, Nebraska (Herb Nerds), Maine White Pine, New Hampshire, New Jersey North (Changewater NJ), New Jersey South, New York Long Island, North Florida, Pennsylvania Eastern, Tennessee, and one topic-specific chapter (Herbal Business Chapter). Chapter contact: chapters@americanherbalistsguild.com / coordinator Sabrena Gwin.

**Annual conference**: 36th Annual Symposium, **August 14–16, 2026**. Location TBD (registration not yet open as of May 2026). This is the single highest-value practitioner gathering of the year; it falls outside Phase 3's June 22 – July 13 sprint but is the right window for follow-up outreach to practitioners met at the event. Pre-conference digital outreach in late July (2–3 weeks before) is the correct timing.

**Publications**: The AHG produces a peer-reviewed journal; members receive it as a benefit. Journal audience = RH-level practitioners, the exact Seedwarden practitioner tier.

**Seedwarden fit**: High. AHG directory filtering by specialty is the fastest path to verified practitioners in each bundle's target domain. Estimated reachable practitioners per bundle specialty: 200–600 in each filtered search.

### 1.2 National Ayurvedic Medical Association (NAMA)

NAMA is the main professional body for Ayurvedic practitioners in the US. Founded 1998. Membership has four levels: Ayurvedic Health Counselor (AHC, $170/yr), Ayurvedic Practitioner (AP, $185/yr), Advanced Ayurvedic Practitioner (AAP, $250/yr), and Ayurvedic Vaidya (AV, $300/yr). Certification is administered by a separate body (NAMACB). Membership size is not publicly disclosed; the organization is smaller than AHG. The 2026 Annual NAMA Conference ran May 1–3, 2026 (virtual; already completed).

**Herbalist overlap**: Moderate but targeted. Ayurvedic practitioners commonly use herbs present in Seedwarden's Immunity and General Wellness bundles: Ashwagandha, Tulsi, Triphala, Shatavari, Brahmi. The Digestive bundle (ginger, fennel, licorice) also aligns with Ayurvedic formulation logic. Women's Health bundle (Shatavari overlap) has partial fit.

**Seedwarden fit**: Medium. NAMA practitioners tend to use formulas rather than single-herb guides; the practitioner bundle value proposition needs adjustment for this audience (see Part 7: Messaging Framework). Outreach timing: post-June 22. The NAMA conference has already passed; next opportunity is NAMA's regional events and newsletter.

### 1.3 Clinical Herbalism Schools — Major Programs

These schools produce the next generation of practitioners and have active alumni networks, course-completion communities, and email lists that are among the most engaged in the space.

**Herbal Academy** (online): Largest online herbalism school by enrollment. Over 100,000 cumulative students. Offers introductory through clinical-level courses. Strong Instagram presence (875K followers). Product ecosystem includes course bundles at $47–$297. Their student and alumni email list is almost certainly the largest herbalism email list in the US. Herbal Academy is not a Seedwarden distribution partner candidate — they are a direct competitor in the educational content space — but understanding their pricing ($47–$297) establishes the price ceiling for practitioner-tier audience purchasing behavior.

**Chestnut School of Herbal Medicine** (Asheville, NC — Juliet Blankespoor): Focuses on Appalachian and bioregional herbalism. Strong in-person and online programs. Instagram: 303K followers (@chestnutschoolherbs). Alumni community is active and geographically centered in the Southeast, which maps to at-risk species (Black Cohosh, Goldenseal, Ginseng) in Phase 3 bundles. Good audience for Women's Health and Immunity bundles.

**Rosemary Gladstar programs**: Science and Art of Herbalism (online correspondence course; foundational, widely taken since 1990s). California School of Herbal Studies (CSHS, Forestville CA; Gladstar founded 1978). These programs have minted tens of thousands of practitioners over decades; alumni represent a diffuse network rather than a single contact channel.

**Colorado School of Clinical Herbalism**: Three cohorts per year. More clinically focused (Vitalist tradition). Next cohorts start August 25 and September 17, 2026.

**David Winston's Center for Herbal Studies** (Chester NJ / Asheville NC): Small, intensive clinical program. Graduates become high-credential practitioners. Direct outreach to graduates via DW's own mailing list is the best path.

**Northeast School of Botanical Medicine (7Song)**: Small, community-herbalism focused. Ithaca NY. First-aid and wilderness medicine emphasis. Audience skews toward budget-conscious community-based practitioners; lower price sensitivity than clinical/RH-level.

**Bastyr University**: Only nationally accredited BS in Herbal Sciences in the US, chaired by Jenn Dazey ND, RH(AHG). Graduates become credentialed practitioners; some continue to ND programs. Bastyr also offers a clinical herbal sciences track within its ND program. Audience is credential-focused and willing to pay for practitioner-grade resources.

**Notre Dame of Maryland University**: MS Clinical Herbal Medicine — only accredited MS program of this type at an accredited university. Graduates are at the top of the practitioner credential ladder. Small cohort size but high-value audience.

**Estimated annual new graduates entering practice**: Across all programs, approximately 2,000–5,000 new practitioners complete some form of clinical herbalism training each year in the US, though the definition of "graduate" varies enormously by program rigor.

### 1.4 United Plant Savers (UpS)

Conservation organization co-founded by Rosemary Gladstar in 1994. Focus: at-risk medicinal plant species. Membership: approximately 2,000 members; 300+ botanical sanctuaries globally. Manages the Forest Grown Verified (FGV) certification program, which is directly referenced in Phase 3 species sourcing guidance.

UpS is the key bridge between practitioner networks and conservation-minded growers. Their member list skews toward experienced, ethically-focused herbalists — exactly the profile of a practitioner-tier Seedwarden buyer. UpS holds an annual botanical sanctuary conference and publishes a plant at-risk list that Seedwarden's sourcing guidance already cites.

---

### 1.5 Herb Society of America (HSA)

Founded 1933. More garden-focused than clinical. 40+ chapters (units) across the US. Membership is not publicly disclosed but the organization is in the low thousands. Audience skews older, female, garden/culinary herb focus rather than medicinal/clinical. HSA is a secondary audience — useful for forager and general wellness bundles, lower fit for clinical practitioner bundles. Business members include Mountain Rose Herbs.

---

### 1.6 International Iridology Practitioners Association (IIPA)

Founded 1982 (originally National Iridology Research Association). Iridologists often work alongside herbalists and recommend herbal protocols as part of their practice. IIPA membership size is not publicly disclosed; the organization is small (hundreds to low thousands globally). Some IIPA members sell herbal supplements and reference materials. Overlap with Seedwarden practitioner bundles is genuine but modest: the strongest fit is Digestive and General Wellness bundles. Contact: iridologyassn.org.

---

### 1.7 Academic Programs — Ethnobotany and Integrative Medicine

Key programs with herbalist-adjacent audiences:

- **University of Massachusetts Medicinal Plant Program**: Research and outreach focus; public extension audience. Christopher Hobbs teaches here.
- **Emory University** (Cassandra Quave): Ethnobotany + antimicrobial plants. Academic, not practitioner.
- **Washington State University**: Five ethnobiologists; PhD/MS programs in ethnobiology.
- **University of Wisconsin Integrative Medicine**: Clinical integrative medicine continuing education. Practitioner CME audience.
- **University of New Mexico Health Sciences**: Strong integrative medicine program; Southwest practitioner market.
- **Florida International University**: Tropical ethnobotany program.
- **University of Hawaii**: Botany + ethnobotany MS/PhD.

Academic ethnobotanists are not Seedwarden's primary commercial audience; they are expert reviewers and credibility validators. However, continuing medical education (CME) channels at integrative medicine programs (UW, UNM) are distribution pathways for practitioner-grade educational materials.

## Part 3: Community Hub Identification

### 3.1 Geographic Hubs

**Appalachian Region (Central Appalachia — WV, KY, VA, TN, NC)**: The single most concentrated herbalist region in North America. 50% of North American at-risk medicinal herbs are native here (ginseng, black cohosh, goldenseal, ramps). Schools: Chestnut School (Asheville), Northern Appalachia School (PA), Appalachian School of Holistic Herbalism. Events: Wild Indigo Herb Fest (KY), Great Lakes Herb Faire (MI). Practitioners: high density of community herbalists, wildcrafters, and small-farm herb growers. This region maps most directly to Phase 3's Women's Health (Black Cohosh, Vitex) and Immunity (Goldenseal, Echinacea) bundles.

**Pacific Northwest (OR, WA, northern CA)**: Second major hub. Characterized by bioregional herbalism (Pacific Botanicals, Wildflower School, Oregon College of Oriental Medicine). Mountain Rose Herbs is headquartered in Eugene, OR. David Winston's NC/NJ programs pull from this region too. Annual Northwest Herbal Fair draws 500–1,000 attendees. Practitioners lean toward Eclectic and Naturopathic traditions; price tolerance for quality resources is high.

**New England (VT, MA, NH, ME)**: Rosemary Gladstar's base (Vermont). United Plant Savers HQ is in Rutland, OH but Gladstar's network is New England-heavy. International Herb Symposium has historically run at Wheaton College MA. Misty Meadows Women's Herbal Conference is in Thetford VT (August 28–30, 2026). High practitioner density; urban Boston market has integrative health practitioners.

**Upper Midwest (WI, MN, MO)**: Midwest Women's Herbal Conference (Almond WI, annual). Herb Society of America regional units concentrated here. Matthew Wood Institute in MN. Less dense than Appalachian/PNW but meaningful.

**Southwest (NM, AZ, CO)**: Colorado School of Clinical Herbalism (Boulder). University of New Mexico CIIM. Tucson-based Sonoran medicine tradition. SCNM (naturopathic school). Climate supports medicinal herb cultivation year-round.

### 3.2 Online Communities

**Reddit r/herbalism**: Approximately 97,000+ members (2023 data; likely 110,000–130,000 by 2026 given growth trends). General discussion, from enthusiasts to practitioners. Low conversion for practitioner bundles; useful for Phase 3 brand awareness and free content seeding.

**Facebook groups**: Fragmented. The largest herbalism Facebook groups include private groups organized around specific schools (Herbal Academy alumni groups), specific traditions (Women's Herbalism, Traditional Western Herbalism), and regional networks. No single dominant group. A common pattern: course enrollment + private Facebook group = captive audience. Estimated aggregate membership across major herbalism Facebook groups: 50,000–100,000, but fragmented across dozens of groups.

**HerbMentor (LearningHerbs)**: Paid community platform run by LearningHerbs (John Gallagher, Rosalee de la Forêt). Monthly subscription. Members are herb enthusiasts and intermediate practitioners. Podcast audience (HerbMentor Radio: 156 episodes, 18 years running). This is one of the most engaged paid communities in the space.

**HerbRally events community**: HerbRally serves as the herbalism events directory for the US. They have a podcast (The Herbalist Hour) and an events listing that is the most comprehensive publicly available calendar. Not a paid community, but the practitioner audience that uses HerbRally is committed enough to attend in-person events.

### 3.3 Social Media Hubs

**Instagram**: Dominant platform for herbalism content. Top hashtags: #herbalism (11M+ posts), #medicinalherbs, #herbalist, #herbalremedies, #plantmedicine, #herbalmedicine. Engagement is highest on educational content (identification posts, preparation method reels, cultivation tips). The best-performing content format for practitioners: "3 uses for [herb]" carousel posts and "how to make [preparation]" reels.

**TikTok**: Growing rapidly. Carmen Adams (@black.herbalist, 220K TikTok) has led a revival of Black herbalism content. The #herbalmedicine tag has millions of posts. TikTok audience skews younger (20s–30s) and more enthusiast than practitioner, but it is the discovery channel where practitioners first hear about new resources. Viral content around fermented garlic honey, elderberry syrup, and mullein (respiratory) drives significant traffic to practitioner-adjacent purchases.

**YouTube**: Longer-format content. Mountain Rose Herbs and Chestnut School have active channels. Less important for Phase 3 launch timing but valuable for SEO.

## Part 5: Cross-Selling and Partnership Opportunities

### 5.1 Natural Distribution Partners

**Mountain Rose Herbs**: The ideal distribution partner. They sell educational content (books, guides) through their website alongside bulk herbs. Their blog audience is already buying herb guides. Partnership model: affiliate link placement in their newsletter or blog review of Seedwarden bundles. They have a precedent for featuring independent herbalism authors and educators. Contact: marketing@mountainroseherbs.com.

**LearningHerbs / HerbMentor**: They sell courses and guides to a captive subscriber base. A Seedwarden practitioner bundle could be featured in their newsletter as a "complementary resource" recommendation. Affiliate commission model is standard in this space. Rosalee de la Forêt is an RH (AHG) and actively recommends practitioner tools.

**Strictly Medicinal Seeds (Richo Cech)**: Sells cultivation guides and seed packets to the exact audience that needs Seedwarden's sourcing and cultivation sections. A cultivation-focused version of the Phase 3 guide could be cross-promoted with Richo's blog or newsletter. Low friction because there is no product competition — seeds vs. plant guides.

**United Plant Savers newsletter**: UpS members are buying conservation-focused herbalism resources. Seedwarden's sourcing guidance (FGV references, at-risk species ethics) aligns with UpS values. A mention in the UpS newsletter or at their botanical sanctuary conference would reach the most conservation-minded practitioners — the segment most likely to pay premium for ethically framed guides.

**AHG chapter newsletter or symposium vendor presence**: AHG chapters send newsletters to local members. A small paid ad or editorial mention in 3–4 chapter newsletters (e.g., New England, Appalachian, Pacific NW chapters) before the August symposium would reach exactly the RH-level practitioners Phase 3 targets.

### 5.2 Price Compatibility Analysis

The $120–$150 practitioner bundle falls squarely within existing practitioner purchasing norms:
- Clinical herbalist consultation fees: $50–$200/hour (median ~$125); practitioners who charge this are accustomed to paying similar amounts for quality reference materials
- Herbal Academy course modules: $47–$297 for a focused course
- Botanical reference books: $30–$65 (physical); practitioners purchase 5–10 per year
- Continuing education credits (AANP, AHG): $200–$500 per CE event

The $120–$150 practitioner bundle is roughly equivalent to one clinical consultation — this is a well-established anchor in the practitioner purchasing context. The strongest friction point is not price but perceived depth: practitioners will scan the first two pages of any guide for correct botanical nomenclature, evidence sourcing, and contraindication accuracy before purchasing. If those signals are present, price resistance at this level is low.
