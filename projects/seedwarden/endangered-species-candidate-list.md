# Endangered Species Candidate List — Phase 2 Content Expansion

**Prepared**: May 5, 2026  
**Research scope**: Identify 15 US native plants with federal or state endangered/threatened status that also carry culinary, medicinal, or ecological education value; assess legal documentation paths, photo access, commercial seed availability, and market positioning for a Seedwarden endangered species guide series.

**Lead finding**: The most commercially viable and legally clean candidates are not federally listed under the ESA, but are state-listed, CITES Appendix II-regulated, or on the United Plant Savers (UpS) At-Risk roster. This distinction matters enormously for guide production: federally endangered plants under the ESA trigger Section 9 take prohibitions that would restrict hands-on documentation, but UpS At-Risk / CITES Appendix II plants can be cultivated, photographed on private land or in botanical gardens, and have legally available seeds from multiple commercial suppliers. The sweet spot for Seedwarden is the middle tier — plants under enough pressure that they carry conservation weight and storytelling, but cultivated enough that guides, photos, and seed sourcing are legally and practically straightforward.

---

## Part 1: The 15-Candidate Shortlist

The candidates are ranked from most to least feasible for guide production, accounting for photo access, legal seed availability, botanical garden presence, and educational / market demand.

### Tier 1 — Highest Feasibility (Cultivated, Multiple Suppliers, Strong Education Story)

**1. American Ginseng** (*Panax quinquefolius*)  
- **Protection status**: CITES Appendix II since 1975; state-listed threatened or of special concern in Illinois, Maine, Michigan, and others; not federally listed under the ESA. Wild harvest is regulated state-by-state; cultivated plants and seeds are freely bought and sold. Notably, CITES Appendix II explicitly excludes seeds from export permit requirements — only roots and whole plants trigger CITES controls.  
- **Value angle**: Adaptogenic medicinal herb with deep Cherokee and Appalachian folk medicine history. Story arc: 200 years of overharvest, the CITES listing, the forest-farming revival, and how home cultivation directly reduces poaching pressure on wild populations.  
- **Commercial seed availability**: Prairie Moon Nursery, Strictly Medicinal Seeds, Penn State Extension, multiple Appalachian forest-farming suppliers. Seed and live root both available in quantity.  
- **Photo access**: Botanical gardens (Missouri Botanical Garden, US Botanic Garden) maintain live specimens. iNaturalist has thousands of CC-BY-NC observations. Forest-farming operations in Appalachian states are photographable with permission.  
- **Feasibility**: High. This is the anchor species for the series.

**2. Goldenseal** (*Hydrastis canadensis*)  
- **Protection status**: CITES Appendix II since 1997; state-listed endangered in Connecticut, Georgia, Maine, Massachusetts, Minnesota, New Jersey, and North Carolina; IUCN Vulnerable. Wild harvest and trade of roots require CITES permits; cultivated material is unrestricted.  
- **Value angle**: Antibiotic-property rhizome, historical Cherokee and Iroquois medicinal use, now 95%+ wild-harvested despite two decades of conservation pressure. A forest-farming guide teaches how to grow it legally and reduce wild-harvest demand.  
- **Commercial seed availability**: Johnny's Selected Seeds, Strictly Medicinal Seeds, Prairie Moon Nursery, Southern Exposure Seed Exchange. Well-supplied by cultivated sources.  
- **Photo access**: Botanical gardens, iNaturalist (abundant), forest-farming operations.  
- **Feasibility**: High. Close second to ginseng for anchor status.

**3. Black Cohosh** (*Actaea racemosa*)  
- **Protection status**: UpS At-Risk; state-listed endangered in Massachusetts; threatened watch status in several states. Not CITES-listed, not ESA-listed. No legal restriction on cultivated plants or seeds.  
- **Value angle**: Menopausal and hormonal herbal medicine with strong market demand driving 95%+ wild harvest. The conservation story (a species becoming commercially extinct in the wild) resonates with the same buyer who shops herbal guides.  
- **Commercial seed availability**: Prairie Moon Nursery, Strictly Medicinal Seeds, Vermont Wildflower Farm, Annie's Heirloom Seeds, Fedco.  
- **Photo access**: Widely photographed; full sun and partial shade garden specimens available across eastern US. iNaturalist CC-BY-NC rich.  
- **Feasibility**: High.

**4. Bloodroot** (*Sanguinaria canadensis*)  
- **Protection status**: UpS At-Risk; "Exploitably Vulnerable" in New York; "Special Concern" in Rhode Island. Not CITES or ESA-listed. No restriction on cultivated plants.  
- **Value angle**: Spring ephemeral with brilliant white bloom; historical dye and dental-care plant. Commercially harvested almost entirely from wild populations despite minimal cultivation demand. The toxicity angle (sanguinarine) adds educational depth — teach what it is and what it isn't, and how the alkaloid is extracted commercially for livestock feed export to Europe.  
- **Commercial seed availability**: Strictly Medicinal Seeds, Prairie Moon Nursery, several regional native plant nurseries. Seeds viable for 1 week post-collection; immediate sowing or moist storage required — good seed-saving content angle.  
- **Photo access**: Common in botanical garden collections; spring bloom timing makes photography straightforward.  
- **Feasibility**: High.

**5. Ramps / Wild Leek** (*Allium tricoccum*)  
- **Protection status**: Threatened in Quebec (commercial harvest prohibited); New York lists *A. tricoccum var. burdickii* as endangered; Michigan prohibits harvest on public land; West Virginia caps national forest harvest at one grocery bag per person. No federal ESA listing. Restaurant demand has devastated Appalachian populations in the past decade.  
- **Value angle**: The most culturally resonant plant on this list. Ramp festivals, Appalachian foodways, Cherokee *wasdi* harvesting traditions, Quebec's harvest ban, the restaurant trend that overharvested entire population clusters. Slow regeneration (5 years to reproductive maturity) makes every leaf you pull today worth understanding.  
- **Commercial seed availability**: Multiple native plant nurseries offer seed and bulbs. Seed germinates slowly — content-rich establishment guide opportunity.  
- **Photo access**: Widely cultivated and photographed; spring woodland setting, easy to source from iNaturalist, nature photographers, and native plant nurseries.  
- **Feasibility**: High. The strongest cultural/culinary story in the list.

**6. Wild Bergamot / Bee Balm** (*Monarda fistulosa*)  
- **Protection status**: Not ESA or CITES-listed at the national level; listed as a species of concern in specific state and regional contexts. Common in many areas but dramatically reduced in shortgrass prairie and certain New England habitats.  
- **Value angle**: Edible and medicinal (tea, respiratory uses, Cherokee pharmacopeia). Exceptional pollinator plant — bumble bee specialist. Demonstrates the connection between medicinal plant overharvest, habitat loss, and pollinator decline in a single species.  
- **Commercial seed availability**: Widely available; nearly all suppliers carry it.  
- **Feasibility**: Very high — lower "drama" than ginseng but useful as an accessible bridge plant for buyers new to conservation content.

### Tier 2 — Moderate Feasibility (State-Listed, Cultivated Sources Exist)

**7. False Unicorn / Fairy Wand** (*Chamaelirium luteum*)  
- **Protection status**: UpS Critical (near the top of their risk tier); state-listed threatened in New York, endangered in Connecticut, Indiana, and Massachusetts. Wild populations described as "nearly non-existent" in much of range. Not ESA-listed.  
- **Value angle**: Historically important women's herbal medicine (menopausal, uterine tonic); now barely available from any commercial source, cultivated or wild. The conservation crisis story is acute and under-documented.  
- **Commercial seed availability**: Very limited. Cultivation is difficult; rhizome harvest kills the plant. This limits guide scope — focus on identification, habitat, and conservation rather than cultivation how-to.  
- **Photo access**: Botanical garden collections and iNaturalist; fewer observations than ginseng or cohosh but sufficient for a guide.  
- **Feasibility**: Moderate. Best positioned as a "Species in Crisis" spotlight within a multi-species guide rather than as a standalone.

**8. Lady's Slipper Orchid** (*Cypripedium* spp.)  
- **Protection status**: UpS Critical; most states list all native *Cypripedium* species as protected. *Cypripedium reginae* (showy lady's slipper) is Minnesota's state flower and protected. Most species are not ESA-listed at the federal level, but state protections are strong. Wild collection is illegal in nearly every state.  
- **Value angle**: The most visually iconic plant on this list. Conservation hook: most lady's slippers sold online are dug from the wild and die within two years of transplant. Teaching buyers to identify nursery-propagated specimens from wild-dug ones is concrete, actionable, and builds buyer trust. Medicinal historical use (mild sedative, "American valerian").  
- **Commercial seed availability**: Hillside Nursery (Northampton, Massachusetts) offers nursery-propagated specimens. Tissue-culture propagation has enabled legal supply. Seeds require mycorrhizal fungi for germination — not cultivatable from seed without laboratory protocols.  
- **Photo access**: Botanical gardens, dedicated native orchid nurseries (Hillside Nursery has been photographed extensively), iNaturalist CC-BY-NC. Minnesota state parks have publicly documented specimens.  
- **Feasibility**: Moderate-to-high for photography; limited for seed-saving content. Best as identification + conservation story guide.

**9. Mayapple** (*Podophyllum peltatum*)  
- **Protection status**: CITES Appendix II (roots and whole plants, driven by anticancer compound podophyllotoxin demand); not ESA-listed. The pharmaceutical demand (130 tons harvested wild in 1970 alone) shifted to Himalayan species when higher podophyllotoxin concentrations were found there, but CITES listing remains.  
- **Value angle**: Edible fruit (ripe only; all other parts toxic); anti-cancer pharmaceutical history; the unusual conservation story of being saved by corporate indifference (pharmaceutical shift away from it reduced harvest pressure). Excellent educational complexity for a premium guide.  
- **Commercial seed availability**: Moderate; difficult to propagate from seed. Spreads by rhizome; live divisions more available than seed.  
- **Feasibility**: Moderate. Strong story; cultivation limitations restrict the practical guide scope.

**10. Trillium** (*Trillium* spp., multiple species)  
- **Protection status**: UpS Critical (the genus as a whole); state-listed at various levels across its range, including Minnesota's prohibition on distribution without landowner and commissioner permission. Individual species vary: *T. grandiflorum* (great white trillium) is common but locally threatened; *T. persistens* (persistent trillium) and *T. reliquum* (relict trillium) are federally listed endangered in Georgia/South Carolina.  
- **Value angle**: Spring ephemeral that takes 7–10 years to reach flowering maturity from seed — the single most powerful argument for conservation in any plant guide. Pick a trillium, you set back a decade. Medicinal historical use (Beth Root, parturition herb in Cherokee practice). The genus is also a keystone indicator of old-growth forest health.  
- **Commercial seed availability**: Available for non-ESA-listed species (e.g., *T. grandiflorum*, *T. erectum*, *T. luteum*). Prairie Moon Nursery stocks multiple species. The two federally listed species (persistent, relict) should not appear in commercial guides as cultivation targets.  
- **Photo access**: Botanical gardens, woodland nature photographers, iNaturalist — extensive.  
- **Feasibility**: High for non-listed species with a clear conservation message about the listed relatives.

### Tier 3 — Specialist / Long-Lead Candidates

**11. Oconee Bells** (*Shortia galacifolia*)  
- **Protection status**: State-listed in South Carolina (endangered) and North Carolina; CPC National Collection. Rarest spring ephemeral in the eastern US; endemic to a handful of gorges in the Southern Appalachians.  
- **Value angle**: The romantically obscure species — found by Michaux in 1788, then "lost" for nearly a century. Remarkable botanical detective story. Ecological specialist tied to specific stream-gorge microclimates.  
- **Commercial seed availability**: Wildside Garden (seed-grown propagation program); Plant Delights Nursery; South Carolina Botanical Garden. More available than false unicorn.  
- **Photo access**: South Carolina Botanical Garden maintains a documented wild-propagated population. Small but sufficient photo sourcing.  
- **Feasibility**: Low-to-moderate. Strong story for a regional Appalachian guide insert; limited standalone commercial potential.

**12. Venus Flytrap** (*Dionaea muscipula*)  
- **Protection status**: UpS Critical; state-listed endangered in North Carolina (only naturally occurring state); ESA-listed as threatened as of 2023. Wild collection is a felony in North Carolina.  
- **Value angle**: The most famous carnivorous plant in the world, with a wild population covering a roughly 100-mile radius around Wilmington, NC. The education story is strong: fire-suppressed longleaf pine savannas are the habitat, and without prescribed burns, the plants disappear as woody shrubs close the canopy. Conservation as active land management.  
- **Commercial seed availability**: Widely cultivated and sold in commerce; tissue culture has made cultivated venus flytraps universally available. The key educational point: any plant labeled "wild-harvested" should be avoided — cultivated specimens are identical and legal.  
- **Photo access**: Easy. Widely cultivated; botanical gardens, nurseries, iNaturalist.  
- **Feasibility**: Moderate. The species is well-known enough to anchor a carnivorous plants / longleaf pine ecosystem guide. Different audience than foragers but strong gift/educational market crossover.

**13. Blue Cohosh** (*Caulophyllum thalictroides*)  
- **Protection status**: UpS At-Risk; state concern or special status in several northeastern states. Not CITES or ESA-listed. Declining from overharvest for its historical use as a labor-induction herb (high toxicity to pregnant animals — the guide must be careful here).  
- **Commercial seed availability**: Prairie Moon Nursery, Strictly Medicinal Seeds.  
- **Feasibility**: Moderate. Toxicity complexity requires careful guide framing; pairs well with black cohosh as a "two cohoshes" companion piece.

**14. Wild Columbine** (*Aquilegia canadensis*)  
- **Protection status**: Not broadly endangered; localized state and regional concerns. Included here because regional variants in Texas (*A. longissima*, golden columbine) are state-listed.  
- **Value angle**: Native columbine demonstrates the "locally threatened, nationally common" pattern that is a central conservation education lesson. Hummingbird specialist plant, nectar-drinking history among indigenous peoples.  
- **Feasibility**: High for general guide content; lower conservation urgency than Tier 1–2 species.

**15. Wild Bergamot Varieties / Appalachian Monarda Species** (*Monarda clinopodia*, *M. media*, *M. russeliana*)  
- **Protection status**: Several Appalachian-endemic *Monarda* species are state-listed; *M. russeliana* is rare in Tennessee. Wild bergamot itself (*M. fistulosa*) is common, but the regional relatives provide a useful conservation contrast — same genus, very different trajectories.  
- **Feasibility**: Moderate. Best used as supporting content in an Appalachian medicinal plants guide alongside ginseng and cohosh.

**Photo sourcing reality check**: The single most reliable path for commercial-quality botanical photography of these species is direct partnership requests to three categories of institution: (1) nurseries that specialize in nursery-propagated specimens of rare plants (Hillside Nursery for orchids, Plant Delights for shortia), (2) botanical garden education departments (Missouri Botanical Garden, US Botanic Garden, NC Botanical Garden, UC Berkeley), and (3) iNaturalist CC-BY (fully open) or CC-BY-NC (non-commercial; confirm with individual users before commercial use) contributors who have documented Research Grade observations. Timeline: 4–8 weeks for nursery contact and approval; 6–12 weeks for botanical garden partnerships.

## Part 3: Educational Angles

Four frameworks produce the highest educational depth and reader engagement across the candidate list:

**Framework 1 — The Harvest Crisis Narrative**  
Species: Ginseng, Goldenseal, Black Cohosh, Ramps, Bloodroot  
The narrative arc: market demand created by a plant's medicinal reputation → commercial wildcrafting at unsustainable scale → wild populations collapse faster than they regenerate → the gap between demand and cultivated supply widens → further pressure on wild populations. This cycle is documented and citable for all five species. The guide teaches it not as abstraction but through species-specific harvest data (e.g., 95% of black cohosh raw material is still wild-harvested despite decades of conservation concern; Quebec banned commercial ramp harvest after 20% of documented populations vanished).

**Framework 2 — The Cultivation Solution**  
Species: Ginseng, Goldenseal, Black Cohosh, Ramps  
The direct action frame: "You don't have to choose between using this plant and protecting it. Forest farming, shade cultivation, and home growing are proven techniques that reduce wild-harvest pressure." Each guide section can include a cultivation overview — stratification requirements, shade percentages, establishment timeline, and where to buy nursery-propagated seeds or starts. This makes the guide actionable, not merely educational, which aligns with Seedwarden's established guide voice (see seed-saving-field-manual.md and native-plants-regional-guide.md).

**Framework 3 — Indigenous and Cultural Heritage**  
Species: Ginseng, Ramps, Goldenseal, Trillium, Wild Bergamot  
The Eastern Band of Cherokee, USDA Forest Service, and multiple universities have active Culturally Significant Plant Species initiatives. The Cherokee relationship with wasdi (ramps), ginseng harvest protocols (never dig before seeds mature; plant the red seed back), and the role of culturally significant species in language preservation are citable from peer-reviewed ethnobotany sources. These stories deepen the educational content and position guides as more than species lists — they become cultural history documents.

**Framework 4 — Habitat as the Real Story**  
Species: Venus Flytrap (longleaf pine savanna), Lady's Slipper (old-growth forest microbiome), Trillium (old-growth indicator), Oconee Bells (Appalachian gorges)  
Each of these species is ecologically specific to a habitat type that is itself threatened. The plant story is inseparable from the habitat story: venus flytraps don't just need protection from collection, they need prescribed fire. Lady's slippers don't just need soil — they need decades-old mycorrhizal networks that only intact forest floor provides. Teaching the habitat teaches why the species is declining in a way that resonates beyond botanists.

## Part 4: Market Positioning

**Premium tier rationale**  
The existing Seedwarden catalog tops out at approximately $22–28 for comprehensive guides (per phase-2-premium-taxonomy-research.md, the $25–$50 premium bundle tier is structurally vacant on Etsy). An endangered species guide series positions at the high end of that opening:

- **Single species deep-dive guides**: $18–22. Format: 25–40 pages. Scope: identification, distribution map, conservation status, cultivation method, harvest ethics, cultural significance, seed-saving window. Modeled on the existing seed-saving-field-manual.md depth and voice.
- **Multi-species thematic bundle**: $28–38. Example bundles: "Appalachian Medicinal Plants in Crisis" (ginseng + goldenseal + black cohosh + ramps, $32); "Eastern Woodland Ephemerals" (bloodroot + trillium + wild ginger + mayapple, $28).
- **Premium conservation gift bundle**: $42–48. All six core Appalachian species with full habitat and cultural heritage content. Conservation-framed gift packaging. Positioned for: birthday gift for a naturalist, teacher gift, conservation organization member gift.

**Buyer segmentation**  
Three distinct buyer types are accessible for this series, only partially overlapping with the existing forager/homesteader/prepper cohorts:

1. **Conservation-conscious naturalists**: Already buy field guides, native plant books, and attend native plant society events. This buyer skews older (35–65), higher income, and is gift-market active. They will pay $28–42 for a thorough guide that treats conservation seriously — they can tell when content is shallow.
2. **Herbalists and wild food practitioners**: The existing Seedwarden audience. Overlap is high with ginseng, goldenseal, black cohosh guides — these are plants they already know by name and are interested in growing ethically.
3. **Educators and school programs**: A smaller but high-value segment. Native plant societies, high school biology classes, and nature center programs are the buyer. A guide priced at $18–22 per PDF license (or $38 for a school-use license allowing class printing) is competitive with curriculum materials while offering regional specificity that generic materials lack.

**Positioning statement**  
The series tag line should be: *"Know it, grow it, protect it."* Three verbs, three outcomes, applicable to every species in the list. This frames Seedwarden not as a "plants are in danger, feel bad" content category, but as an empowerment brand — the buyer's knowledge and cultivation practices are the conservation mechanism. This is consistent with Seedwarden's existing voice (the native-plants-regional-guide.md and seed-saving-field-manual.md both treat the reader as a capable practitioner, not a passive student).

**Differentiation from Phase 1 content**  
Phase 1 wild edibles and medicinal guides focus on abundance and accessibility — "here is what you can find and use." The endangered species series flips to scarcity and responsibility — "here is what is disappearing and what you can do about it." The two series are complementary, not competing: a buyer who purchased the Wild Edibles Quick Reference is a natural upsell to "learn which of these plants you should grow rather than forage."

## Part 5: Photo Access Assessment — Top 5–8 Candidates

The following candidates have realistic photo sourcing paths for commercial-quality imagery within a 6–10 week timeline:

| Species | Best Photo Source | Access Method | Estimated Timeline |
|---|---|---|---|
| American Ginseng | Appalachian forest farms (WV, KY, TN, NC); Missouri Botanical Garden | Email request to 3–5 forest farms; botanical garden media contact | 4–8 weeks |
| Goldenseal | NC Botanical Garden, Missouri Botanical Garden; Strictly Medicinal Seeds growing operation | Botanical garden media partnership request; seed supplier photo exchange | 4–8 weeks |
| Ramps | Spring woodland, NY/NC/WV timing March–April; iNaturalist CC-BY contributors | Identify 5–10 iNaturalist contributors with CC-BY license; request permission for any CC-BY-NC | 2–4 weeks |
| Black Cohosh | iNaturalist (abundant, June–August bloom); Vermont Wildflower Farm growing stock | iNaturalist CC-BY filter; farm photo request | 2–4 weeks |
| Bloodroot | Spring bloom March–April; botanical gardens, state parks with photography permission | iNaturalist CC-BY; botanical garden education request | 3–5 weeks |
| Lady's Slipper | Hillside Nursery (Northampton, MA); Minnesota state parks (public photography) | Direct nursery contact for photo licensing; MN DNR photo library | 6–10 weeks |
| Trillium (common spp.) | Botanical gardens, nature photographers, iNaturalist (most photographed woodland plant in eastern US) | iNaturalist CC-BY; no special partnerships required | 1–2 weeks |
| Venus Flytrap | Nurseries (widely available); NC coastal plant photography (public land, legal) | Purchase nursery-grown specimens for tabletop botanical photography; no permissions required | 1–3 weeks |

**Key sourcing note**: iNaturalist's default license is CC-BY-NC, which prohibits commercial use. However, many contributors have set CC-BY (fully open) licenses. The iNaturalist observation map for each species can be filtered by license. For ginseng specifically, the most striking in-habitat photography requires direct relationships with forest farms because wild-habitat locations are deliberately kept private (poaching pressure is real and documented). This is itself a conservation education story worth including in the guide.

## Part 6: Phase 2 Sequential Roadmap

Prioritized by the intersection of: (a) market demand for the species as a topic, (b) photo availability right now, (c) commercial seed availability enabling cultivation content, and (d) ease of research relative to depth of the resulting guide.

**Wave 1 (Phase 2, Q3 2026 — production start by June)**  
Target launch September 2026, coinciding with fall foraging season demand peak (145–175% baseline).  
- Appalachian Medicinals bundle: **Ginseng + Goldenseal + Black Cohosh + Ramps** — the four most recognized, most commercially relevant, and most cited in conservation literature. Each has cultivated seed suppliers and sufficient photo sources. These four species together tell a complete harvest-crisis-to-cultivation-solution arc. Price: $32 bundle, $18 per individual guide.

**Wave 2 (Phase 2, Q4 2026 — production start August)**  
Target launch October–November, gift season.  
- Spring Ephemerals bundle: **Bloodroot + Trillium (common spp.) + Wild Ginger + Mayapple** — spring bloom photography needs to be sourced in March–April 2026 (already passed) or March–April 2027. This sequence requires advance photo planning unless stock iNaturalist imagery is sufficient. If iNaturalist sourcing is adequate, production can proceed in fall 2026 for spring 2027 launch. Price: $28 bundle, $18 per individual guide.

**Wave 3 (Phase 3, Q1–Q2 2027)**  
- **Lady's Slipper Orchid** standalone: The most photogenic, most gift-market-appropriate species on the list. The photo sourcing (Hillside Nursery partnership or botanical garden) is the pacing constraint. Start outreach in October 2026 for spring 2027 production.  
- **Venus Flytrap / Longleaf Pine Ecosystem** guide: Different audience (ecology enthusiasts, school programs). Can be produced year-round (cultivated specimens available). Strong science classroom angle. Price: $18–22 standalone.
- **Oconee Bells** regional insert: Southern Appalachian add-on to Wave 1 bundle for the regional guide variant. Not a standalone product — adds depth to the Appalachian bundle for buyers who want the full regional story.

**Phase 3 long-range**  
A complete "Endangered Native Plants of North America" digital library — 12–15 individual guides bundled at $55–65 — is the Phase 3 aspirational catalog anchor. This positions Seedwarden alongside physical field guides (the closest physical equivalent, *Native Plants of the Northeast* by Leopold, sells for $29–35 on Amazon) but with actionable cultivation content and conservation specificity that field guides omit. No competitor currently offers this as a digital product on Etsy.
