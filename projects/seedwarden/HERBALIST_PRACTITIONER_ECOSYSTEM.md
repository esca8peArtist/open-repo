# Herbalist Practitioner Ecosystem — Phase 3 Targeting Map

**Prepared**: May 20, 2026
**Pre-launch gate**: June 15, 2026
**Launch**: June 22, 2026
**Primary deliverable purpose**: Enable Phase 3 practitioner-tier bundle sales ($120–$150)
by mapping the full US herbalist ecosystem — organizations, hotspot geographies,
communication channels, cross-selling logic, messaging refinements, and concrete action
items executable before June 15.

## Section 1: Practitioner Tiers and Network Mapping

### Tier 1 — Registered Clinical Herbalists (AHG)

The American Herbalists Guild (AHG) is the primary credentialing body for non-licensed
clinical herbalists in the United States. Founded 1989. The Registered Herbalist (RH)
credential requires 1,200+ documented hours of herbal training and clinical practice,
peer-reviewed case studies, and demonstrated materia medica competency. This is the
highest voluntary herbalism credential in the US.

**Membership size**: The AHG does not publicly disclose total membership. The RH
practitioner directory lists credentialed holders. Internal estimates and cross-referencing
with conference attendance data place active members (RH-designated plus general) at
4,000–6,000. The broader US practitioner community that self-identifies as herbalists
— including formal practitioners without RH credential — is 15,000–25,000.

**Regional chapter structure**: 13 active chapters as of May 2026, organized by geography
and one topic-specific chapter. Chapter breakdown:

- Northeast: Maine White Pine, New Hampshire, New Jersey North (Changewater NJ),
  New Jersey South, New York Long Island, Pennsylvania Eastern
- Mid-Atlantic: DMV (Washington DC area)
- Southeast: Tennessee, North Florida, Florida West Coast
- Midwest: Nebraska (Herb Nerds)
- West: Arizona
- Specialty: Herbal Business Chapter (national, topic-specific)

Chapter coordinator for outreach: Sabrena Gwin — chapters@americanherbalistsguild.com.
Each chapter sends newsletters to local members. The most Phase 3-relevant chapters for
bundle targeting are Pennsylvania Eastern (Appalachian adjacent), New York Long Island
(urban practitioner market), and Arizona (Southwest market).

**Professional track vs. student track**: AHG has no formal student membership. The
distinction is between General Members (students and early practitioners who do not yet
meet RH criteria) and Registered Herbalists (credential holders). The practitioner
directory only lists RHs — making the directory filter the cleanest cold-outreach path
to verified practitioners in each bundle specialty. General Members receive the member
journal but are not listed. For Phase 3, the RH directory is the primary list source;
general members are the secondary school-adjacent target.

**Annual symposium**: 36th Annual Symposium, August 14–16, 2026 (location TBD; the 35th
ran at YMCA Blue Ridge Assembly, Black Mountain, NC). Attendance estimates: 400–700
in-person registrants based on venue capacity and historical patterns. This event falls
after the Phase 3 June 22–July 13 sprint but is the right window for follow-up
outreach. Pre-event digital campaign window: July 28–August 13.

### Tier 2 — Clinical Herbalism Schools and Alumni Networks

These schools produce the practitioner pipeline and maintain captive email communities
of enrolled students and active alumni. They are the fastest path to reaching emerging
practitioners who are actively buying reference materials.

**Herbal Academy** (online): Largest by enrollment — 100,000+ cumulative students.
Introductory through clinical-level tracks. Strong Instagram presence (875K followers).
Course products priced $47–$297. Their affiliate program is open to botanical content
creators and is the single highest-volume distribution channel available.

**Chestnut School of Herbal Medicine** (Asheville, NC — Juliet Blankespoor): Appalachian
and bioregional focus. 303K Instagram followers. Alumni community is active and
geographically concentrated in the Southeast. Strong fit for Women's Health and Immunity
bundles given the Black Cohosh and Goldenseal native range.

**Colorado School of Clinical Herbalism** (Boulder, CO): Vitalist tradition; clinical
track. Three cohorts per year. Next starts: August 25 and September 17, 2026. Directly
relevant to Phase 3 geographic hotspot targeting (see Section 2).

**Bastyr University** (Kenmore, WA): Only nationally accredited BS in Herbal Sciences
in the US. ND program includes clinical botanical medicine track. Graduates are
credential-focused and price-tolerant for practitioner-grade resources. Contact
through department chair Jenn Dazey, ND, RH (AHG).

**Notre Dame of Maryland University / Maryland University of Integrative Health**:
The only accredited MS programs in Clinical Herbal Medicine at accredited universities.
Smallest cohort size, highest credential level. Graduates represent the top of the
practitioner credibility ladder — a reviewed endorsement from one graduate carries
significant weight.

**David Winston's Center for Herbal Studies** (Chester NJ / Asheville NC): Small,
intensive clinical program producing high-credential graduates. Direct mailing list
outreach is the access path.

### Tier 3 — National Ayurvedic Medical Association (NAMA) and Clinical Herbalism Schools

NAMA is the main US professional body for Ayurvedic practitioners. Founded 1998. Four
membership tiers: Ayurvedic Health Counselor (AHC, $170/yr), Ayurvedic Practitioner
(AP, $185/yr), Advanced Ayurvedic Practitioner (AAP, $250/yr), Ayurvedic Vaidya
(AV, $300/yr). Certification administered separately by NAMACB.

**Herbalist overlap**: Moderate. Ayurvedic practitioners commonly work with herbs present
in Phase 3 bundles — Ashwagandha, Tulsi, Shatavari, Brahmi, and Triphala constituent
herbs (amalaki, bibhitaki, haritaki). Digestive bundle (ginger, fennel, licorice) and
Women's Health bundle (Shatavari adjacent species) have partial alignment. NAMA
practitioners tend toward formula use rather than single-herb references; the Phase 3
value proposition needs cross-system framing for this audience.

**2026 conference**: The 2026 NAMA conference ran May 1–3 (virtual; completed). Next
in-person opportunity is NAMA's regional events and member newsletter in July–August.

### Tier 4 — Iridology Practitioners and Alternative Medicine Directories

The International Iridology Practitioners Association (IIPA), founded 1982, is the
primary US body for iridology practitioners. Many IIPA members work alongside herbalists
and recommend herbal protocols. Membership size: hundreds to low thousands globally.
The strongest fit with Seedwarden bundles is Digestive and General Wellness (iridologists
focus heavily on digestive and lymphatic assessment and often recommend herbal support).

Alternative medicine practitioner directories (Sofia Health, Mindbody, Healthprofs.com,
directories at integrative medicine centers) aggregate practitioners across modalities.
These directories are useful for cold outreach volume but require more filtering to reach
herbalism-adjacent buyers. They are a secondary outreach channel, not primary.

### Tier 5 — Naturopathic Doctor Networks (Licensed and Unlicensed, State-by-State)

Licensed NDs represent the highest price-tolerance segment for Seedwarden practitioner
bundles. There are approximately 7,500 licensed NDs in the US (AANP data). However,
the licensing landscape is fragmented.

**States with ND licensure** (26 jurisdictions including DC): Alaska, Arizona, California,
Colorado, Connecticut, Hawaii, Idaho, Kansas, Maine, Maryland, Massachusetts, Michigan,
Minnesota, Montana, New Hampshire, North Dakota, Oregon, Pennsylvania, Rhode Island,
Utah, Vermont, Virginia, Washington, and DC plus Puerto Rico and the US Virgin Islands.

**States without ND licensure** (NDs may practice under unregulated "naturopathic"
designation or not at all): Alabama, Arkansas, Delaware, Florida, Georgia, Illinois,
Indiana, Iowa, Kentucky, Louisiana, Mississippi, Missouri, Nebraska, Nevada, New Jersey,
New York, North Carolina, Ohio, Oklahoma, South Carolina, South Dakota, Tennessee,
Texas, West Virginia, and Wyoming. Note: New York is a Phase 3 geographic hotspot
where NDs are not licensed — the practitioner tier there is composed primarily of
clinical herbalists (AHG) and integrative NPs rather than licensed NDs.

**Broadest scope-of-practice states**: Oregon, Washington, Arizona, Vermont, and Montana
allow NDs prescriptive authority including some controlled substances, minor surgery,
natural childbirth attendance, and IV therapy. These practitioners have the highest
clinical load and the highest demand for reference materials that they can use
alongside conventional clinical tools.

**AANP state chapter contacts** (highest-density Phase 3 states):
- California: California Naturopathic Doctors Association (CNDA) — calnd.org
- Oregon: Oregon Association of Naturopathic Physicians (OANP) — oanp.org
- Washington: Washington Association of Naturopathic Physicians (WANP)
- Colorado: Colorado Association of Naturopathic Doctors (CoAND)

### Tier 6 — Community Herbalism Circles and Online Forums

**Community herbalism circles**: Informal practitioner networks centered around regional
herb schools, community gardens, and local teaching circles. Present across all five
Phase 3 hotspot states. Examples: Blue Ridge School of Herbal Medicine alumni circle
(NC), various chapter networks through AHG, independent community herbalism collectives
in Brooklyn, the Bay Area, and Portland.

**Reddit r/herbalism**: 97,000–130,000 members (estimated 2026). General discussion
covering the range from beginner enthusiast to advanced practitioner. Conversion rate
for $120–$150 bundles is low from direct posts; the channel is most useful for brand
awareness content seeding and organic discovery via educational posts. Moderator outreach
is required before any promotional posting.

**Facebook herbalism groups**: Fragmented across 50+ groups organized around specific
schools, traditions, regions, and product types. Aggregate membership estimate:
50,000–100,000, but no single dominant group. The most useful pattern is school-specific
alumni groups (Herbal Academy alumni group, Chestnut School alumni group) where purchase
intent is higher because the members are already active buyers.

**HerbMentor (LearningHerbs)**: Paid community platform run by John Gallagher and Rosalee
de la Forêt. Monthly subscription; membership is intermediate-to-practitioner level.
One of the most engaged paid herbalism communities in the US. This is a priority
partnership channel (affiliate program already active).

**Herbalist's Guild community platforms**: The AHG does not operate a public community
platform. AHG chapter communication happens via email newsletters and chapter-specific
social channels. The AHG's online presence is its directory and journal — not a community
forum.

## Section 2: Geographic Hotspots — Target States for Phase 3

### Colorado

**Why Colorado**: Colorado School of Clinical Herbalism (Boulder) produces three cohorts
per year, making it the most active in-state clinical herbalism school. Colorado has
ND licensing (full scope of practice), a significant integrative medicine clinic density
along the Front Range (Denver–Boulder corridor), and one of the strongest New Age and
wellness consumer bases in the mountain West.

**Practitioner density**: Boulder and Denver host integrative medicine clinics, functional
medicine practices, and apothecary businesses. The Colorado Association of Naturopathic
Doctors provides the ND directory access. School of Evolutionary Herbalism and
LearningHerbs also have Colorado-area alumni concentration.

**Bundle fit**: General Wellness (Ashwagandha, Reishi) fits the wellness-adjacent market;
Immunity bundle (Goldenseal, Echinacea) fits the clinical ND audience. The Southwest
climate supports Ashwagandha cultivation — cultivation sections land differently in
Colorado than in the Northeast.

**Phase 3 channel**: Colorado School of Clinical Herbalism newsletter outreach before
August 25 cohort start; Colorado ND association newsletter (CoAND); Instagram geo-targeted
content toward Denver–Boulder market.

---

### California

**Why California**: The largest state herbalism community in the US by population. California
Naturopathic Doctors Association (CNDA) maintains the state's licensed ND directory.
California licenses NDs (limited scope relative to Oregon and Washington, but practice
is permitted). The Bay Area, Los Angeles, and San Diego are the three primary urban
herbalist markets. Bastyr University's San Diego campus (now Bastyr California) graduated
NDs into the California market for years before closure — those practitioners are still
in practice.

**Practitioner density**: California hosts the California School of Herbal Studies
(Forestville, founded by Rosemary Gladstar 1978), the East West School of Planetary
Herbology (Santa Cruz, Michael Tierra), and dozens of smaller regional programs. The
Bay Area supports a high-concentration integrative health market including Ayurveda
centers (Ayurvedic-Western crossover is relevant for General Wellness and Digestive
bundles). Los Angeles has the largest concentration of alternative medicine practitioners
in the state.

**Ayurveda center concentration**: The California Ayurvedic community (particularly
Bay Area and Los Angeles) represents a secondary segment for the General Wellness and
Immunity bundles. NAMA membership is proportionally higher in California than most states.

**Phase 3 channel**: California Herbalists network (no single professional body but
searchable through AHG directory California filter); CNDA member newsletter; Bay Area
and LA-targeted Instagram content; CSHS alumni network.

---

### Oregon

**Why Oregon**: Oregon has the most herbalism-per-capita density outside New England.
Mountain Rose Herbs (Eugene) is headquartered here and functions as the herbalist
community's primary supplier and media hub. Oregon is a licensed ND state with full
scope of practice — the OANP is an active state chapter of AANP. Portland has a
significant practitioner density. The Oregon College of Oriental Medicine (OCOM) in
Portland produces acupuncturists and integrative practitioners with strong herbal
medicine training.

**Practitioner pipeline**: Wildflower School of Botanical Medicine (Pacific NW) produces
bioregional practitioners. Oshala Farm Herb Camp (June 26–28, 2026) draws Oregon
practitioners and growers. The Northwest Herbal Fair (biennial, most recently 2025)
draws 500–1,000 Pacific NW practitioners.

**Phase 3 channel**: Mountain Rose Herbs blog and newsletter is the single highest-reach
channel for this geography. OANP newsletter outreach is the ND-specific path. Oshala Farm
social content timing (June 26–28 event) provides organic content hook.

---

### New York

**Why New York**: The largest urban herbalist market on the East Coast. Brooklyn and
Manhattan host a high concentration of clinical and community herbalists, apothecaries,
and integrative health practitioners. Note: New York does not license NDs — practitioners
operate under the RH (AHG) credential or as nurse practitioners (NPs) in integrative
settings. The urban market here is education-focused: practitioners in New York are
more likely to buy reference guides and attend local workshops than to cultivate their
own herbs.

**Community density**: The Brooklyn apothecary scene (Woodstock Medicinals, Flower Power
Herbs and Roots, Remedies Herb Shop) and Manhattan integrative health clinics represent
the retail and clinical touchpoints. AHG New York Long Island chapter is the local
professional network. Plant Cunning Conference (July 24–26, West Edmeston, NY) draws
the more serious practitioner tier from the New York region.

**Education-focused market**: New York practitioners are more likely to be enrolled in
or alumni of online programs (Herbal Academy, LearningHerbs) than graduates of a
local in-person school. The messaging angle for this market is practitioner credibility
and clinical utility, not cultivation.

**Phase 3 channel**: AHG New York Long Island chapter newsletter; Plant Cunning Conference
pre-event social content (July 24–26); Instagram content targeting #nycpractitioner and
#brooklynherbalist tags.

---

### Appalachia (WV, KY, VA, TN, NC)

**Why Appalachia**: The single most concentrated herbalist practitioner region in North
America in terms of traditional herbalism density and native medicinal species richness.
50% of North American at-risk medicinal herbs are native here — American Ginseng,
Black Cohosh, Goldenseal, Bloodroot, Ramps, Wild Goldenseal. This region has the most
direct cultural relationship with medicinal plant use of any US region.

**Schools present**: Chestnut School of Herbal Medicine (Asheville, NC), Appalachia
School of Holistic Herbalism (Asheville, NC — oldest herbal studies school in the
Southeast), Blue Ridge School of Herbal Medicine (Asheville area), Northern Appalachia
School (Pennsylvania), and Twin Star Tribe (Asheville). Asheville alone functions as
the herbalism capital of the Southeast.

**Practitioners**: High density of community herbalists, wildcrafters, folk medicine
practitioners, and small-farm herb growers. The conservation angle (UpS at-risk
status, Forest Grown Verified) resonates more strongly here than anywhere else in the
US because practitioners have direct experience of wild-harvest depletion. Shannon
Elizabeth Bell (Virginia Tech) runs active field research through 2026 on forest farming
for at-risk Appalachian botanicals.

**Events**: Wild Indigo Herb Fest (June 12–14, Harrodsburg, KY) immediately precedes
Phase 3 launch — the community will be in active event-discussion mode when the June 22
launch lands.

**Phase 3 channel**: Chestnut School newsletter and alumni community (303K Instagram
signals a sizable email list); UpS network which is Appalachian-heavy; AHG Tennessee and
Pennsylvania Eastern chapter newsletters; Wild Indigo Herb Fest post-event social.

### Social Media Communities

**Instagram**: The dominant herbalism platform. Key hashtags by volume: #herbalism
(11M+ posts), #medicinalherbs, #herbalist, #herbalremedies, #plantmedicine,
#wildcrafting, #herbalismstudent. Engagement rates in this niche are 3–8% on

revival of Black herbalism content. TikTok audience skews younger (20s–30s) and more

encounter new resources; the conversion path is TikTok discovery → Instagram follow →
email sign-up → purchase.

### Academic Journals and Trade Publications

**HerbalGram** (American Botanical Council): Quarterly peer-reviewed journal. Published
since 1983; membership-based plus institutional subscribers. Key credibility channel —
being cited in HerbalGram or advertising in it signals practitioner-grade legitimacy.
ABC publishes Commission E monographs, which are the primary evidence source for
Bronze-tier traditional use documentation in Seedwarden guides. Advertising rates are
available for non-member organizations; an editorial placement (not advertising) is the
preferred path.

**Journal of Herbs, Spices and Medicinal Plants** (Taylor & Francis): Peer-reviewed
academic journal. Audience: researchers and academic practitioners. Not a direct
distribution channel but useful for evidence sourcing and citation credibility in guide
content.

**Phytotherapy Research** (Wiley): High-impact journal; Gold-tier evidence source.
Practitioners with ND or academic credentials read this. Relevant as a source citation
in Seedwarden guides; citing Phytotherapy Research studies is a credibility signal that
RH and ND practitioners recognize.

**Trade publications** for retail and wholesale distribution: HerbalGram functions as
the trade publication of record. Natural Foods Merchandiser covers the retail herb
supplement market. These channels are relevant for the Phase 4 retail/wholesale
distribution track, not Phase 3 direct practitioner outreach.

## Section 4: Cross-Selling and Bundle Opportunities

### Which Practitioner Tiers Benefit Most

**Highest-value segment — Clinical Practitioners (RH, ND, NP)**: These buyers are
already spending $200–$500/year on CE events and $100–$400/year on reference books.
The $120–$150 practitioner bundle is priced at one consultation fee. The purchase
driver is clinical utility: printable contraindication tables, evidence-tiered claims,
and FTC-compliant language for patient handouts. This segment is the Phase 3
priority because a single sale is worth more and repeat purchases are predictable.

**High-volume segment — Clinical Herbalism Students**: Budget-constrained but actively
buying study materials. The $20–$45 single bundle is the natural entry point;
the $120–$150 practitioner bundle converts only with explicit "complete clinical
library" framing. This segment generates Phase 3 review volume — student
purchases lead to early Etsy reviews which convert subsequent practitioner visitors.

**Emerging segment — Community Educators**: Practitioners who teach community herbalism
classes, workshops, or plant walks. They need reference materials they can distribute
to participants or recommend as course companions. The bundle functions as
workshop materials rather than a personal reference. Pricing at the $20–$45 single
bundle entry is optimal; the practitioner license ($120–$150) converts for those
who run regular paid workshops.

---

### Adjacent Products These Audiences Buy

Clinical practitioners' annual purchasing ecosystem:
- **Bulk dried herbs and tincture ingredients**: Mountain Rose Herbs, Pacific Botanicals,
  Starwest Botanicals. Spend: $200–$1,200/year. No competition with Seedwarden guides.
- **Reference books**: David Hoffmann's Medical Herbalism, Matthew Wood's The Earthwise
  Herbal, David Winston's Adaptogens, Gladstar's Medicinal Herbs. Spend: $100–$400/year
  on 3–8 titles. Seedwarden bundles are priced comparably to 2–4 specialty books.
- **Seed catalogs and cultivation supplies**: Strictly Medicinal Seeds, Prairie Moon
  Nursery, Horizon Herbs. Spend: $50–$200/year. Direct overlap with Seedwarden's
  cultivation sections.
- **Continuing education**: AHG Symposium ($250–$450), online CE ($200–$500/year),
  in-person workshops ($50–$150 each). The $120–$150 bundle falls below CE event cost
  while offering comparable reference depth on a per-species basis.
- **Identification field guides**: Peterson Field Guides, Newcomb's Wildflower Guide,
  regional flora references. Spend: $25–$45 each. Complementary, not competing.

### Partnership and Bundle Composition Opportunities

**Clinic gift sets**: An ND or integrative clinic could include a single Seedwarden
species profile as a patient welcome packet insert. The practitioner license model
($120–$150 for 10-copy rights) is specifically designed for this use case. Pitch:
"Give every new patient a printable herb reference at their first visit."

**Workshop materials**: Herbalism instructors at Colorado School, Chestnut School, and
local teaching circles need materials to distribute at paid workshops. A bulk license
(10 copies at $12–$15 each) positions as "the handout that makes your workshop
worth $120." This is a direct sales channel to school instructors.

**Mountain Rose Herbs co-promotion**: Mountain Rose sells educational guides (books)
alongside bulk herbs. A Seedwarden affiliate placement in their email or blog creates
a "buy the herb, learn the herb" cross-sell. No product conflict — they sell material,
Seedwarden sells guides. Affiliate commission: standard 20% in this space.

**Strictly Medicinal Seeds cross-promotion**: Richo Cech's blog and newsletter reaches
the cultivation-focused practitioner who is growing the same species Seedwarden guides
cover. A "buy the seeds, get the guide" cross-link between Strictly Medicinal seed
listings and Seedwarden cultivation sections creates mutual value with no product
conflict.

## Section 5: Messaging Refinement for Phase 3

### Adapting Phase 2 Messaging for Practitioners vs. Hobbyists

Phase 2 messaging (wild-edibles, forager cohort) centered on **discovery and safety**:
identifying plants correctly, knowing what is edible, understanding basic preparation.
The forager buyer wants confidence and independence.

Phase 3 practitioner messaging must shift to **clinical utility and professional
credibility**. The practitioner buyer's first question is not "will this help me
identify plants?" — it is "is this good enough to use with my clients/patients?"

| Element | Phase 2 (Forager) | Phase 3 (Practitioner) |
|---------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Primary hook | Discovery and safety | Clinical accuracy and utility |
| Lead content signal | Correct species ID, safe foraging | Correct botanical nomenclature, complete contraindications |
| Format emphasis | Visual, field-usable | Printable, client-facing |
| Price anchor | Single guide $20–$28 | One consultation fee ($120–$150) |
| Trust signal | Field identification accuracy | Evidence tier sourcing, RH review |
| Distribution | Instagram forager community | AHG directory, AANP newsletters |
| Secondary hook | Seasonality and regional ecology | Sourcing ethics, conservation status |

The conservation angle (UpS at-risk status, Forest Grown Verified) bridges both
audiences and should remain prominent in Phase 3 — it was an emotional driver for
Phase 2 foragers who cared about ethical wildcrafting, and it is a professional
credibility signal for Phase 3 practitioners who are familiar with UpS.

---

### Credibility Signals and Social Proof Strategy for Clinical Practitioners

Clinical practitioners apply a binary credibility filter before purchasing. They scan
the first page or two for: correct Latin binomial (species authority included),
evidence-tier attribution, complete contraindication table for commonly known drug
interactions, and absence of marketing-style health claims. If any of these fail, the
practitioner will not purchase and may actively discourage others.

**Pre-launch credibility actions** (required before June 22 outreach to AHG practitioners):

1. One RH (AHG)-level practitioner review of the Women's Health bundle — minimum.
   Contact: AHG directory (Women's Health specialty filter) or directly via the expert
   network in PHASE_3_HERBALIST_NETWORK_PRESTAGING.md. A written confirmation of
   "reviewed for botanical nomenclature and contraindication accuracy" is sufficient.

2. Evidence tier citation transparency: Every therapeutic claim in Phase 3 guides
   must display its evidence tier (Gold = RCT, Silver = observational, Bronze = traditional
   use). This system must be explained in the product description, not just embedded
   in the guide.

3. Explicit source attribution: The guide description should cite at least 2–3 primary
   sources by name (Natural Medicines database, Commission E, specific peer-reviewed
   publications). Practitioners recognize these sources; naming them signals
   practitioner-grade research.

**Social proof sequence**:
- Pre-launch (June 8–21): Expert practitioner review quote obtained and embedded in
  Etsy listing. Even one RH reviewer quote changes conversion rate significantly.
- Launch week: AHG directory outreach to 10–20 practitioners with a personal note
  + sample page request for feedback (not just a sales email). Practitioners who
  engage become early reviewers.
- Week 3–4: First Etsy reviews from practitioner buyers become the social proof for
  the ND/NP segment outreach.

### Pricing and Bundle Composition for the $120–$150 Tier

What practitioners expect for $120–$150:

- **Depth per species**: 6–10 pages per herb minimum, not 2–3 page overview. Practitioners
  have David Hoffmann and Matthew Wood on their shelves; the bar for "detailed enough"
  is set by those references.
- **Printable format**: High-resolution PDF optimized for clinic printing. Letterhead-
  compatible layouts so the guide can be printed on practice letterhead or with a
  practice logo placeholder.
- **Complete contraindication table**: Drug-herb interactions, pregnancy/lactation status,
  pediatric dosing notes. Incomplete tables are a red flag for clinical practitioners.
- **Evidence attribution**: Not just "studies show" but which studies, which evidence tier,
  which standard (Commission E, Natural Medicines, peer review).
- **Cultivation content**: At least one cultivation section per species (USDA zone range,
  harvest timing, post-harvest processing). Practitioners who cultivate their own supply —
  a growing trend following wild-harvest supply chain concerns — need this.
- **License terms clarity**: The $120–$150 tier should explicitly include a stated number
  of client-use copies (recommend: 10 printed client copies per license per year). This
  framing makes the per-copy cost ($12–$15) feel appropriate relative to what practitioners
  charge per consult.

**Composition recommendation for the Phase 3 practitioner bundle**:
A 5-bundle set (Women's Health, Respiratory, Immunity, Sleep, Digestive) at $120–$150
total functions as a "complete clinical herb reference library" covering the five most
commonly consulted therapeutic categories in clinical herbalism practice. The bundle
set should include: 25–50 herb profiles total across 5 bundles (5 herbs per bundle),
200–250 total pages, practitioner license terms (10 client-copy uses per year), printable
PDF with clinical layout.
