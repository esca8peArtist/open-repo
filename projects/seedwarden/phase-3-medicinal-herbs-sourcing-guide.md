# Phase 3 Medicinal Herbs — Sourcing Guide

**Prepared**: May 7, 2026
**Scope**: Three sourcing domains — (1) guide photos, (2) cultivation content suppliers (seed and plant stock), and (3) FGV and conservation certification verification. All sourcing paths have been selected for license clarity, supplier reliability, and lead time compatibility with the September 2026 Women's Health bundle launch target.

## Part 1: Photo Sourcing by Species

### Protocol

All guide photos must satisfy one of:
- Wikimedia Commons with explicit CC-BY, CC-BY-SA, or CC0 license (most permissible)
- iNaturalist observation with CC-BY license (confirm per-observation, not per-species)
- Direct permission from a botanical garden, herbalist supplier, or forest farm operation (email template in Part 3)

For each species, the preferred Wikimedia Commons search URL is listed. iNaturalist fallback is listed where Wikimedia coverage is thin. Direct contact targets are listed for the three conservation-significant species (Black Cohosh, Goldenseal, Echinacea angustifolia).

---

### 1. Black Cohosh (*Actaea racemosa*)

**Wikimedia Commons search**: `https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:Search?search=Actaea+racemosa+habit`
**Known CC images**: Multiple CC-BY-SA images of the flower spike, leaf, and forest floor habit. The June–August bloom window provides the most distinctive images (white bottle-brush flower spike).
**iNaturalist fallback**: Search `Actaea racemosa` on iNaturalist; filter for CC-BY observations. Rich results in June–August from Appalachian and Great Lakes states.
**Direct contact**: Vermont Wildflower Farm (vermontbutterflyfarm.com — farm offers plant photography; email their wholesale contact for permission to use growing-stock photos in educational guides).
**Priority image sequence for the guide**:
  1. Full habit in dappled forest shade showing leaf and stem (identification)
  2. Flower spike close-up (recognition)
  3. Mature seed cluster (harvest timing cue)
  4. Root rhizome cross-section (cultivation anchor)
**Filename convention**: `black-cohosh-habit.jpg`, `black-cohosh-flower.jpg`, `black-cohosh-root.jpg`

### 2. Goldenseal (*Hydrastis canadensis*)

**Wikimedia Commons search**: `https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:Search?search=Hydrastis+canadensis+habit`
**Known CC images**: Several CC-BY-SA images; coverage thinner than black cohosh. The NC Botanical Garden and Missouri Botanical Garden both have media contacts for educational use.
**iNaturalist fallback**: Search `Hydrastis canadensis`; strong results from Ohio, Indiana, and Appalachian states. Bloom window: April–May.
**Direct contacts**:
  - NC Botanical Garden: ncbg.unc.edu — media@ncbg.unc.edu (request educational license for habitat photos)
  - Missouri Botanical Garden: missouribotanicalgarden.org — media@mobot.org (same request)
  - Forest farming operations in WV/KY: contact via UpS member directory (unitedplantsavers.org) — several FGV goldenseal farms welcome educational photo partnerships
**Priority image sequence**:
  1. Full plant habit in hardwood forest understory (conservation story visual)
  2. Flower (single white flower, early spring — distinctive and rare in most guides)
  3. Rhizome dug, showing the yellow interior that gives the plant its name
  4. Forest farming setup (rows under tree canopy) — this is the differentiation image
**Filename convention**: `goldenseal-habit.jpg`, `goldenseal-flower.jpg`, `goldenseal-rhizome.jpg`, `goldenseal-forest-farm.jpg`
**Note**: The forest farming photo is the most important single image in the Immunity bundle. It visualizes the conservation story concretely — no other Etsy competitor has this image.

### 3. Elderberry (*Sambucus nigra* / *S. canadensis*)

**Wikimedia Commons search**: `https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:Search?search=Sambucus+nigra+habit`
**Known CC images**: Excellent coverage. Multiple CC-BY-SA images of flower clusters, berry clusters, and full shrub. Also search `Sambucus canadensis` for the native American elder — visually similar but the native species content is a differentiation point.
**iNaturalist fallback**: Abundant. Filter for CC-BY observations.
**Unsplash**: Search "elderberry" — lifestyle photography of berry clusters and syrup preparation available with Unsplash license (free for commercial use, attribution recommended).
**Priority image sequence**:
  1. Full shrub habit in summer (multi-stem structure visible)
  2. Flower cluster (flat-topped, creamy-white — the elderflower)
  3. Berry cluster (dark purple, ripe)
  4. Cultivated row showing coppiced multi-stem structure (production farming angle)
**Filename convention**: `elderberry-habit.jpg`, `elderberry-flower.jpg`, `elderberry-berries.jpg`

### 4. Echinacea (*E. purpurea* and *E. angustifolia*)

**Wikimedia Commons search**: `https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:Search?search=Echinacea+purpurea+habit` and `https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:Search?search=Echinacea+angustifolia+habit`
**Known CC images**: *E. purpurea* (purple coneflower) is among the most-photographed garden plants. Abundant CC-BY-SA. *E. angustifolia* is less widely photographed; iNaturalist is the better fallback for prairie-habitat images.
**iNaturalist fallback**: Search both species; *E. angustifolia* results concentrated in Kansas, Nebraska, South Dakota.
**Priority image sequence**:
  1. *E. purpurea* full habit in garden setting (common, recognizable, visually impactful)
  2. *E. angustifolia* prairie-context habit (conservation story anchor — visually differentiated from the garden species)
  3. Root of each species side-by-side (the "two echinacea" teaching moment)
  4. Native prairie context photo for *E. angustifolia* (conservation framing)
**Filename convention**: `echinacea-purpurea-habit.jpg`, `echinacea-angustifolia-habit.jpg`, `echinacea-root.jpg`

### 5. Valerian (*Valeriana officinalis*)

**Wikimedia Commons search**: `https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:Search?search=Valeriana+officinalis+habit`
**Known CC images**: Good coverage of flower clusters and tall habit. The flowering stalk at 4–6 feet is visually distinctive and well-documented on Wikimedia.
**iNaturalist fallback**: Search `Valeriana officinalis`; results concentrated in Northeast US and Pacific Northwest.
**Priority image sequence**:
  1. Full habit showing tall flowering stalk
  2. Flower cluster close-up (pale pink to white, fragrant)
  3. Root/rhizome dug (the medicinal part — the image most herb guides omit)
  4. Dried root in storage (practical guide anchor)
**Filename convention**: `valerian-habit.jpg`, `valerian-flower.jpg`, `valerian-root.jpg`

### 6. Lemon Balm (*Melissa officinalis*)

**Wikimedia Commons search**: `https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:Search?search=Melissa+officinalis+habit`
**Known CC images**: Abundant. Common garden herb, well-photographed.
**iNaturalist fallback**: Rich results across eastern US.
**Priority image sequence**:
  1. Full bushy habit (the spreading mound growth pattern is the ID anchor)
  2. Leaf close-up showing the distinctively wrinkled texture and veining
  3. Small white flowers (easy to miss; makes a useful "this is how you know it's about to bloom" harvest timing image)
**Filename convention**: `lemon-balm-habit.jpg`, `lemon-balm-leaf.jpg`

### 7. Lavender (*Lavandula angustifolia*)

**Wikimedia Commons search**: `https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:Search?search=Lavandula+angustifolia+habit`
**Known CC images**: Best Wikimedia coverage of any species in this list. Dozens of CC-BY and CC-BY-SA images including formal garden rows, wild Mediterranean habitat, and close-up flower spikes.
**Unsplash**: Excellent lifestyle photography. Search "lavender field" and "lavender flowers."
**Priority image sequence**:
  1. Garden row habit (the classic lavender-farm visual — sets the aspirational tone)
  2. Single flower spike close-up (identification)
  3. Dried bundles hanging (harvest outcome)
  4. English lavender (Hidcote) vs. French lavender (dentata) comparison (variety differentiation — rare in guides)
**Filename convention**: `lavender-habit.jpg`, `lavender-flower.jpg`, `lavender-dried.jpg`

### 8. Mullein (*Verbascum thapsus*)

**Wikimedia Commons search**: `https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:Search?search=Verbascum+thapsus+habit`
**Known CC images**: Exceptional coverage. The six-foot flower spike is one of the most dramatic biennial plant habits in North America and is extremely well-documented. CC-BY-SA images from roadside and disturbed ground habitats.
**iNaturalist fallback**: Abundant across the full US range.
**Priority image sequence**:
  1. Full second-year habit with flower spike (the dramatic roadside visual — strongest recognition image in the Respiratory bundle)
  2. First-year rosette (flat to the ground, silvery-gray leaves — important for the cultivation guide's biennial lifecycle section)
  3. Flower spike detail (small yellow flowers progressing up the spike)
  4. Dried leaf (the preparation the guide covers)
**Filename convention**: `mullein-habit.jpg`, `mullein-rosette.jpg`, `mullein-flower.jpg`

### 9. Calendula (*Calendula officinalis*)

**Wikimedia Commons search**: `https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:Search?search=Calendula+officinalis+habit`

**Unsplash**: Lifestyle photography with calendula available in abundance.

  1. Full plant habit in garden row (self-sowing annual — productive season visual)
  2. Single flower close-up (the vivid orange is the visual anchor for both Women's Health and Digestive bundles)
  3. Infused oil in jar with fresh petals floating (preparation outcome)

**Wikimedia Commons search**: `https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:Search?search=Withania+somnifera+habit`

  3. Root dug and cleaned (the medicinal part — dried root or fresh-dug)

## Part 3: FGV and Conservation Certification Verification

### What FGV Is

Forest Grown Verified (FGV) is a third-party certification program operated by United Plant Savers (UpS) for medicinal plants grown in forest farming systems (under existing tree canopy, mimicking native habitat). FGV certification is the gold standard sourcing credential for at-risk woodland medicinal species — particularly Goldenseal and Black Cohosh.

The FGV mark tells a practitioner buyer that the material was: (a) grown in a forested setting mimicking wild conditions, (b) verified by UpS against their standards, and (c) not wild-harvested without sustainable management documentation.

**Why this matters for the guides**: A Seedwarden guide that cites FGV-certified sources for Goldenseal and Black Cohosh is not just recommending an ethical alternative — it is differentiating from every PLR compilation on Etsy that ignores sourcing entirely. The practitioner buyer will notice.

---

### Verification Steps for Goldenseal FGV

1. Visit: unitedplantsavers.org/forest-grown-verified/
2. Locate the active FGV participant directory (current as of May 2026: active projects documented in Appalachian Virginia)
3. For each supplier cited in the Goldenseal guide, confirm FGV participant status or nursery-propagated certification before print
4. If a specific supplier loses FGV status between guide publication and reader use: the guide recommends the FGV program by name and URL, not a specific seller's current lot. This buffers against supplier change.
5. Annual check: verify FGV participant list each September before the annual Immunity bundle is the live primary listing. If the list changes materially, update the sourcing sidebar in the Goldenseal guide (2-hour revision).

**FGV program contact**: unitedplantsavers.org | info@unitedplantsavers.org

---

### Verification Steps for Black Cohosh

Black Cohosh is UpS At-Risk but not CITES-listed. The FGV program covers it, but cultivated nursery stock (Prairie Moon, Strictly Medicinal Seeds, Fedco) is adequate guidance without FGV certification — because wild harvest of Black Cohosh, while legal, is documented at 95%+ of commercial supply. The guide's job is to direct buyers to any verified cultivated source. Steps:

1. Confirm Prairie Moon, Strictly Medicinal Seeds, and Fedco explicitly state propagated (not wild-collected) stock on their Black Cohosh product pages before citing them.
2. Search: site:prairiemoon.com "black cohosh" — confirm the listing description states nursery-propagated or cultivated.
3. Log confirmation in WORKLOG.md with date and URL for each supplier verified.
