# Endangered Species Photo Sourcing Pipeline — Wave 1

**Purpose**: Systematically source 3–5 production-quality photos per species for the Wave 1 guide series. This document defines sourcing priority, outreach scripts, license verification protocol, and WORKLOG logging requirements.

**Naming convention**: `[species-slug]-habit.jpg`, `[species-slug]-flower.jpg`, `[species-slug]-root.jpg`, `[species-slug]-leaf.jpg`, `[species-slug]-berry.jpg`

**License requirement**: CC-BY (preferred, fully commercial) or CC-BY-SA (acceptable with attribution). CC-BY-NC (non-commercial) is not acceptable for Etsy product use without individual permission upgrade from the contributor.

---

## Species 1: American Ginseng (*Panax quinquefolius*)

### iNaturalist — Primary Source

1. Navigate to: `https://www.inaturalist.org/observations?taxon_id=116333&license=CC-BY&quality_grade=research`
2. Filter: License = CC-BY (not CC-BY-NC); Quality Grade = Research Grade; Photos = true
3. Target observations: habit (full plant in forest context), berry cluster (red berries in September–October), root (rhizome exposed), leaf detail (palmate compound leaves)
4. For each selected photo:
   - Record: observer username, observation ID, observation URL, license type
   - Download highest-resolution available (right-click > Save Image or use iNaturalist download button)
   - Log in WORKLOG.md per template below

**Expected availability**: Ginseng is one of the most-observed medicinal plants on iNaturalist; thousands of Research Grade observations. CC-BY licensing is less common but present — filter specifically for it. Target: 5–10 CC-BY observations to select from.

**Search URL to use**: `https://www.inaturalist.org/observations?taxon_id=116333&license=CC-BY&quality_grade=research&photos=true`

### Missouri Botanical Garden — Secondary Source

**Contact**: media@mobot.org
**Request template**:
> Subject: Photo licensing request — American Ginseng (Panax quinquefolius) for educational botanical guide
>
> I am producing an educational guide on American ginseng conservation and forest farming for Seedwarden, a botanical education publisher. I am seeking 2–3 high-resolution images of *Panax quinquefolius* for commercial use in a digital PDF guide. Would the Missouri Botanical Garden be willing to license images under a commercial use agreement? I am happy to discuss a licensing fee or explore reciprocal attribution options.
>
> Images needed: habit (full plant), leaf detail, berry cluster.
>
> Thank you for your time.

**Timeline**: Allow 2–4 weeks for response. Follow up once at 2 weeks.

### Forest Farm Contact — Tertiary Source

Search "West Virginia forest farm ginseng photo permission" and "Kentucky ginseng farm photography." Appalachian forest farming operations (see americanginseng.com/member-farms) occasionally allow photography with permission. These produce the most authentic habitat shots — ginseng in mature hardwood forest, full context.

**Note on location privacy**: Wild ginseng populations are deliberately kept off public maps because of poaching pressure. Do not share or publish any habitat location information received from forest farm contacts. Photo only; no location identification in published material.

## Species 2: Goldenseal (*Hydrastis canadensis*)

### iNaturalist — Primary Source

**Search URL**: `https://www.inaturalist.org/observations?taxon_id=75842&license=CC-BY&quality_grade=research&photos=true`

Target photos: habit (full plant in bloom April–May), flower (close-up of white stamens), rhizome (yellow cut surface — the ID feature), leaf detail (hairy stem, lobed leaf).

**Seasonal note**: Goldenseal flowers April through May. If searching outside that window, habit photos of the full plant (leaves only) are still useful. The yellow rhizome can be photographed from cultivated plants year-round — contact suppliers below.

### NC Botanical Garden — Primary Institutional Source

**Contact**: ncbg@unc.edu or use the contact form at ncbg.unc.edu
**Request**: Same template as Missouri Botanical Garden above, substitute "North Carolina Botanical Garden" and specify goldenseal.

The NC Botanical Garden maintains documented cultivated populations of goldenseal and is specifically interested in educational partnerships around at-risk Appalachian species. This is the most likely institutional source for high-quality goldenseal photography.

### Strictly Medicinal Seeds — Commercial Grower Source

**Contact**: info@strictlymedicinalseeds.com
**Request**: Explain the educational guide project; ask if they are willing to share cultivation photos of goldenseal for use with attribution. As a supplier referenced in the guide, they have an indirect marketing interest in the guide's success.

## Species 3: Black Cohosh (*Actaea racemosa*)

### iNaturalist — Primary and Likely Sufficient Source

**Search URL**: `https://www.inaturalist.org/observations?taxon_id=75699&license=CC-BY&quality_grade=research&photos=true`

Black cohosh is one of the most visually distinctive and most photographed medicinal plants on iNaturalist. The dramatic white wand-like flower racemes (June–August) produce abundant, high-quality CC-BY observations each summer. This source should yield 10–20 CC-BY Research Grade photos to choose from.

Target photos:
- Habit (full plant in flower, showing height — the 4–6 foot scale is a key ID feature)
- Flower raceme close-up (white stamens, multiple wands)
- Leaf detail (compound ternate leaves, toothed margin)
- Seed follicles (fall, dark follicles on bare raceme stem)

**No institutional outreach required for black cohosh** — iNaturalist CC-BY supply is expected to be adequate. Confirm during the sourcing sprint; if CC-BY options are limited, contact Vermont Wildflower Farm for cultivation photos.

### Vermont Wildflower Farm — Backup Source

**Contact**: info@vermontflowerfarm.com
**Same request template as above, adapted for black cohosh.**

## Species 4: Ramps / Wild Leek (*Allium tricoccum*)

### iNaturalist — Primary Source

**Search URL**: `https://www.inaturalist.org/observations?taxon_id=56482&license=CC-BY&quality_grade=research&photos=true`

Ramps are heavily photographed on iNaturalist during the spring window (March–May). The visual story for ramps is the woodland carpet emergence — a hillside of broad green leaves emerging from bare forest floor. This image type is the ideal hero photo for the guide.

Target photos:
- Habit/colony (wide shot, forest floor carpet of emerging leaves)
- Leaf detail (broad lance-shaped leaves, reddish stem base)
- Flower (white umbel, June–July, after leaves die back — confirms species)
- Bulb (white bulb with reddish stem, in soil context)

**Seasonal note**: Ramp emergence photos require March–May timing. For the current guide production timeline (May 2026), this spring's emergence season has passed. Options:
1. Use iNaturalist CC-BY archive photos from previous years (fully acceptable — species appearance does not change year to year)
2. Plan for new photography in March–April 2027 for any updated edition
3. Contact woodland photography suppliers for licensed archive imagery

## Photo Quality Standards

All photos selected for guide production must meet:

| Standard | Requirement |
|---|---|
| Minimum resolution | 1500px on shortest side (prefer 2000px+) |
| Focus | Subject in sharp focus; background blur acceptable |
| Lighting | Natural light preferred; no harsh flash shadows |
| Composition | Subject fills 60%+ of frame for ID photos; context shots may be wider |
| Species accuracy | iNaturalist Research Grade confirmed, OR verified by credentialed observer |
| License | CC-BY or CC-BY-SA confirmed before download |

## Canva Integration Notes

All sourced photos are used in two ways:
1. **Inside the guide PDF**: As identification reference images within the species entry, attributed in the image caption
2. **Etsy listing mockup images**: The most visually striking habit photo becomes the listing cover image (slots 1–2 of the Etsy listing for each endangered species guide)

Photos used in Etsy listings require commercial license (CC-BY or CC-BY-SA). Photos used inside the educational PDF that is sold commercially also require commercial license — CC-BY-NC is not adequate for either use without individual permission from the contributor.
