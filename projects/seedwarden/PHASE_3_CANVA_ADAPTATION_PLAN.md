# Phase 3 Canva Adaptation Plan
## Adapting Phase 2 Native-Plant Templates for Medicinal Herbs

**Prepared**: May 26, 2026  
**Execute by**: June 21 (brand kit loaded and tested before sprint Day 1)  
**Design lock**: July 3, 2026 — no cover design changes after this date  
**Time required**: 2-3 hours total for brand kit setup + cover template adaptation (not including per-bundle cover production, which is ~1 hour per cover)

The core principle is that Phase 2 templates are the structural foundation — you are changing colors, swap images, and adjusting the framing language. You are not rebuilding from scratch.

## Part 1: Color Palette Changes

### What Is Changing and Why

Phase 2 (Forager aesthetic) uses a deep forest green that signals outdoor, wild, nature-adjacent content. Phase 3 (Herbalist/Apothecary aesthetic) uses burgundy, sage green, and gold — a clinical-wellness palette that signals medicinal authority, premium positioning, and practitioner credibility.

The practical difference: a buyer who found you via "forage wild plants" reads Deep Forest Green as "this is my guide." A buyer who found you via "medicinal herb guide" reads Deep Burgundy or Sage Green as "this is my guide." Both buyers can exist in the same store — the palette distinction makes it visually clear which catalog each belongs to.

**The two palettes are additive — not replacement.** Do not delete Phase 2 colors from the Canva Brand Kit. Add Phase 3 colors as a second group.

## Part 2: Stock Image Needs

### What Is Different About Medicinal Herb Photography vs. Foraging Photography

Phase 2 (foraging guide) images emphasize habitat and identification: the plant in its natural environment, the habitat context, the distinctive identifying feature. The buyer is learning to recognize a plant in the field.

Phase 3 (medicinal herb guide) images serve a different function: they simultaneously show the plant AND the practitioner context — the root, the preparation stage, the apothecary aesthetic. The buyer already knows what lavender is; they want to see lavender in a context that says "this is a serious herb guide, not a recipe blog."

The practical implication is that Phase 3 images should include at least one "apothecary prop" element per bundle cover: a mortar and pestle, a glass jar with dried herbs, dried bundles hanging, or a cutting board with fresh-dug roots. These elements elevate the clinical-wellness aesthetic above what foraging images alone can achieve.

## Part 3: Template Structure Differences

### Foraging Guide vs. Medicinal Guide Page Architecture

Phase 2 (foraging guide) page structure:
- Identification section (visual features, lookalikes)
- Habitat and range (where to find it)
- Edibility and harvest (what to eat, when to harvest)
- One-line conservation note

Phase 3 (medicinal herb guide) page structure:
- Botanical overview (same identification anchor, shorter)
- Cultivation method (how to grow it — this is the primary content)
- Harvest timing and preparation (root vs. leaf vs. flower; drying; storage)
- Traditional use history (one paragraph: what culture, what use, how long documented)
- Research note (one paragraph: what the science says, descriptive only)
- FTC-compliant use framing (the "traditionally used for X" statement, not a claim)
- Conservation note (required for at-risk species; brief for common species)
- Sourcing recommendation (specific supplier recommendations)

The medicinal guide is longer per species (1,400-2,000 words vs. 600-900 words per plant in the foraging guide) and has an additional "cultivation" and "research note" section that foraging guides do not have. This is the primary structural difference.

**Canva template implication**: The interior page layout needs an additional text zone for the research note and FTC framing. In the Phase 2 Zone Card layout, this is accomplished by using a smaller top-third image (rather than full-bleed) and expanding the text block. Alternatively, run the medicinal herb guides as 2-page species spreads rather than 1-page species entries — this accommodates the longer word count and gives the layout room to breathe.

### Adapting the Zone Card Template for Medicinal Herbs

Phase 2 zone cards use a 3-panel structure: top (plant image), middle (identification features), bottom (harvest and edibility notes).

Phase 3 adaptation — two options:

**Option A (minimal change, fastest execution)**: Keep the 3-panel layout. Top panel = herb image. Middle panel = cultivation requirements (zone, soil, light, water). Bottom panel = traditional use note + FTC framing statement + sourcing recommendation (2-3 lines per field). This reuses the Phase 2 layout with only content changes.

**Option B (deeper adaptation, better practitioner positioning)**: Split the middle panel into two columns: left column = cultivation requirements; right column = preparation method (tea, tincture, infused oil, poultice). The two-column layout signals clinical depth that a single-column foraging card does not.

Recommendation: Use Option A for sprint production speed. Add Option B as a v1.1 template upgrade for the practitioner 10-pack tier.

## Part 4: Typography Considerations

### No Changes Required to Core Typography

Phase 2 typography (Playfair Display headings, Lato or Source Sans 3 body, Cormorant Garamond Italic for accent) is carried forward unchanged into Phase 3. The Playfair Display heading font already reads as both botanical-classic and medicinal-authority — it works for both aesthetic positions.

The only Phase 3 typography addition is the botanical name italicization rule, which was already in the Phase 2 style guide: all genus and species names (e.g., *Echinacea purpurea*, *Hydrastis canadensis*) are always italicized in both body text and display headings.

### Clinical vs. Accessible Tone

The primary adjustment is in the headings and section labels, not the font choice itself:

| Phase 2 (Forager) | Phase 3 (Herbalist) |
|---|---|
| "Where to Find It" | "Cultivation Requirements" |
| "Harvest and Eat" | "Harvest Timing and Preparation" |
| "Watch Out For" | "Lookalikes and Safety Notes" |
| "Conservation Note" | "Conservation and Ethical Sourcing" |
| "Fun Fact" | "Traditional Use History" |

The headings shift from outdoor-activity framing to practitioner-reference framing. This is a text edit in Canva, not a layout change.

### Accessibility Consideration

Phase 3 buyers include clinical practitioners (RNs, NPs) who may print guides for patient consultation. Minimum body text size: 11pt. Line height: 140-150%. Do not drop below these specifications for layout density. A guide printed at 100% on a standard printer must be readable without magnification.
