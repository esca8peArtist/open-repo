## EMAIL 2 — Herb Spotlight: Elderberry (Send: Day 3)

**Subject line**: The one shrub every homesteader should plant this year (and most don't)

**Preview text**: Black elderberry: $40 bare-root plant → years of immune support

---

**Body:**

Most medicinal herbs are annuals or small perennials you replace every few years.

Elderberry is different.

A single elderberry shrub (Sambucus nigra or its American native cousin, S. canadensis) planted this year can produce harvestable berries in year 2. By year 5, a well-established pair of shrubs produces enough berries to supply your household's elderberry syrup for the entire winter — and then some.

And unlike most medicinal plants where the clinical evidence is preliminary, **elderberry has some of the strongest research support for a specific application of any herb in the Western herbal tradition**.

A 2019 meta-analysis of four randomized controlled trials found black elderberry supplementation significantly reduced the total duration and severity of upper respiratory symptoms. A separate 2016 trial showed travelers taking elderberry extract caught colds half as often and recovered 2 days faster when they did get sick.

This is not folk medicine. These are controlled clinical trials. And the mechanism makes sense: elderberry appears to inhibit viral neuraminidase (the enzyme influenza uses to replicate) and stimulate cytokine production.

---

**Elderberry syrup is one of the easiest preparations you'll make:**

Heat 1 cup dried elderberries in 3 cups water with a cinnamon stick and a few cloves. Simmer 30–45 minutes until the liquid reduces by half. Cool, strain, add raw honey (1 cup per 2 cups liquid). Refrigerate. 1 tablespoon daily as a preventive.

The total active work time is about 20 minutes. The syrup keeps 2–3 months refrigerated, or 12 months frozen.

**What to plant**: Look for Sambucus canadensis cultivars 'Bob Gordon' or 'Adams' for North American growers. Plant two different cultivars for cross-pollination — single plants produce significantly less fruit. Space 10–15 feet apart. They prefer moist soil and full sun. They tolerate wet areas better than most shrubs, which makes them useful for rain gardens and low spots that other plants hate.

**One important safety note**: Raw elderberries contain a compound (sambunigrin) that causes nausea and vomiting. Always cook berries before eating or preparing them. Cooking or drying eliminates this compound completely.

## EMAIL 3 — Quick Tip: How to Make a Tincture (Send: Day 7)

**Subject line**: 5 minutes of work, 5 years of shelf life

**Preview text**: Why tinctures are the most practical thing you can make from your herb garden

---

**Body:**

A tincture is just an herb extracted into alcohol.

That's it. The word sounds complicated; the process is not.

Here is what you need: a clean mason jar, dried herb, and 80-proof vodka. That's the whole list.

Here is what you do:

Fill the jar one-half full with loosely packed dried herb. Pour vodka over it until the jar is full. Screw on the lid. Shake it. Label it with the herb name and today's date. Put it in a dark cabinet.

Come back in 4–6 weeks. Strain out the plant material. Pour the liquid into a small amber glass dropper bottle. Label with the strain date and "use by: [5 years from today]."

That's a tincture.

---

**Why tinctures are worth making:**

**Shelf life**: A well-made tincture lasts 3–5 years. Dried herbs last 1–2 years; fresh herbs last days. If you grow a good harvest of valerian or echinacea this fall, a tincture captures it for use through the following 4 winters.

**Consistency**: Once you've strained and bottled a tincture, every dose is the same concentration. Tea is variable depending on how much herb you used and how long you steeped it. A tincture dose (2–4 mL in a little water) is reproducible.

**Convenience**: The dropper bottle goes in a bag. Dried herbs do not.

---

**The one herb where fresh matters more than any other:**

St. John's Wort. The active constituent (hyperforin) is unstable — it degrades significantly during drying. If you grow St. John's Wort and want the full clinical effect, make a fresh plant tincture at peak bloom (around the summer solstice, when the buds are just opening). Fill the jar tightly with freshly harvested flowers and buds. Cover with 60% alcohol (either 120-proof vodka or grain alcohol diluted with water). The finished tincture should be a deep red-purple. That color tells you the hyperforin transferred into the alcohol.

The full-guide has the detailed weight-to-volume ratios (1:2 for fresh plant, 1:5 for dried root) and the right alcohol percentage for each herb in the collection. Some herbs need 40% alcohol; some need 70%. The guide specifies which.

For now, the folk method above — fill the jar, cover with 80-proof vodka — works fine for most herbs and is how most home herbalists work.

## EMAIL 4 — Seasonal Spotlight: What to Harvest Right Now (Send: Day 14)

**Subject line**: What to harvest this week (summer edition)

**Preview text**: Calendula, elderflowers, St. John's Wort — the summer window is short

---

**Body:**

Summer is the busiest medicinal herb harvest season.

Here's what's happening in your garden or nearby fields right now, depending on where you are:

---

**Northeast and Upper Midwest (Zones 4–6)**

Elderflowers are either just finishing or at peak. If you have an elderberry planted, this is the window — cut the large flat flower clusters just as they fully open, before the petals start to drop. Elderflower cordial is one of the best things you can make from a garden. Elderflower tea has diaphoretic (fever-supporting) traditional use.

Calendula should be in full production. If you sowed it in May, it's blooming now and will keep going until frost — but only if you deadhead or harvest every 2–3 days. Leave the flowers and it stops producing. Pick them and it keeps going. Pick in the morning. The stickier the flower, the better the resin content.

St. John's Wort blooms around the summer solstice (usually the third week of June). The harvesting window for fresh tincture is short — about 10–14 days when the buds are just opening and the first flowers are fresh. After that, the hyperforin content drops significantly.

---

**Southeast (Zones 7–9)**

Your summer harvest is 4–6 weeks ahead of the Northeast. Elderberries may be ripening now in your area (look for uniformly deep purple-black clusters). If you see any green berries, wait — the green ones are toxic.

Thyme, oregano, rosemary, and lavender are at peak aromatic intensity right now. Harvest heavy. These are the most shelf-stable dried herbs and well worth harvesting in bulk for winter kitchen and medicinal use.

---

**Pacific Northwest (Zones 7–9)**

Excellent conditions for elderberry production — the mild, moist climate suits it well. If you planted elderberry last spring, you may have your first partial harvest this year; full production typically comes in year 2–3.

Nettle is past its best leaf harvest (before flower set), but if you find a patch that hasn't flowered yet, harvest now. After flowering, the leaves develop calcium carbonate crystals that can irritate the kidneys with heavy internal use.

---

**Southwest (Zones 7–10)**

This is peak harvest time for Mediterranean herbs — lavender, rosemary, thyme, oregano. All are heat-lovers that do well in dry southwestern summers. Harvest before the worst of the summer heat if you're in a hot zone; aromatic quality declines in extended 100°F+ heat.

Chamomile may have finished if temperatures climbed early. If you didn't catch it, mark the calendar for a fall sowing — chamomile is a cool-weather crop that does better in the Southwest as a fall or spring plant.
