# Photo Shoot Equipment and Location Brief
## Seedwarden Phase 2 — What You Need, Where to Set Up, and How to Use It

**Purpose**: This is the pre-shoot reference document. It consolidates equipment requirements, location specs, and setup guidance from LIFESTYLE_PHOTOGRAPHY_STRATEGY.md and PHOTO_SHOOT_PLANNING.md into a single one-stop brief that answers three questions: what to bring, where to stand, and how to set it up. Read this once before the shoot. PHOTO_SHOOT_CHECKLIST.md is what you carry during the shoot.

**Scope**: Physical photography for Clusters A, B, and C (15 products, 30 shots). Stock images will be sourced separately for Clusters D and E per LIFESTYLE_PHOTOGRAPHY_STRATEGY.md.

## Part 1: Camera and Technical Equipment

### Camera — What You Have Is Almost Certainly Sufficient

No dedicated camera is required. Any of the following qualify for all 30 shots in this project:

| Device | Qualifies? | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| iPhone 14, 14 Pro, or newer | Yes | Portrait mode + standard mode both work; 12–48 MP |
| iPhone 13 | Yes | 12 MP main sensor; adequate at 2400px output target |
| Android flagship 2022 or newer (Pixel 7, Galaxy S22, OnePlus 10) | Yes | All meet 12 MP minimum at usable quality |
| Older Android or iPhone 12 and below | Marginal | Test first: shoot a close-up flat lay at standard mode, export at 2400px, check for softness. If sharp: proceed. |
| Mirrorless or DSLR with 35mm or 50mm lens | Excellent | Overkill for this project but produces the best result if available |

**The phone is not the limiting factor. Light is the limiting factor.** A phone in good window light outperforms a DSLR in poor light.

### Camera Mode — The One Rule You Must Follow

| Shot type | Distance from subject | Mode to use | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| Flat lay (overhead) | 24 inches or more above surface | Standard photo mode | Portrait mode blurs the wrong elements at this distance — props blurred instead of background |
| Close-up / in-use detail | 18 inches or closer | Portrait mode | Creates background blur (bokeh) that isolates the subject; reads as professional |
| Scene-based (mid-distance) | 18–36 inches | Standard mode | Portrait mode inconsistent at medium distances; standard is safer |

Set the mode before beginning each shot. Do not rely on "automatic" decisions by the phone.

### White Balance — Set It Once, Do Not Touch It

Auto white balance is the single most common error in DIY product photography. It drifts between frames even in the same location and makes batch editing exponentially harder.

**Setting procedure:**
- iPhone: Install "Halide" app (free tier works) or "ProCamera" for manual WB control. Set to 5500K for Clusters A and B, 6000–6500K for Cluster C.
- Google Pixel: Camera > Settings > White Balance > Sunny (approx. 5500K)
- Samsung Galaxy: Pro mode > WB > set to 5500K
- If manual WB is not available on your device: set scene mode to "Cloudy" or "Shade" — this locks a warm bias that is better than Auto

**Do not change the WB setting between shots in the same cluster session.** Even if the light shifts slightly, a consistent WB produces a more cohesive batch that edits faster.

### Tripod / Phone Mount

A tripod is not required for flat lay or scene shots, where the phone rests on a stable surface. A tripod IS required for:

- Any shot where both hands appear in frame (Products 1, 3, 8, 12, 13)
- Any shot where a hand holds the guide while the camera must be at a specific angle

**Budget option that works**: Stack hardcover books at counter height, position phone against a cookbook propped vertically, use the 3-second self-timer. This is stable enough for the 2400px output target.

**Dedicated mount**: Any phone clip tripod adapter (~$10) on any standard tripod serves this purpose. If you have a GorillaPod or flexible tripod, that also works for counter-height mounting.

**For two-hand shots specifically**: Set the 3-second timer, press shutter, put both hands into the scene in the correct position before the timer fires. Three seconds is enough.

## Part 2: Lighting

### Natural Light — The Right Setup

Window light is the best light source for all 30 shots in this project. The key variables are direction, time of day, and diffusion.

**Direction**: Position the shooting surface so the window is to the LEFT or at a 45-degree angle to the subject. Direct front lighting (window behind the camera) flattens the image and removes texture. Side-front lighting (45 degrees) creates the soft shadows that give depth to wood grain, seed envelopes, and mason jars.

**Time of day**:
- 9am–12pm: optimal for Clusters A and B. The sun is low enough that light coming through a window is still directional (not overhead), and most windows face east or south and receive their best indirect morning light at this time.
- 12pm–2pm: acceptable for Cluster B if the window faces south. Light becomes more neutral (less warm) but is still soft.
- After 2pm: acceptable for Cluster C (kitchen shoot) — overhead kitchen light can supplement if needed. This is the only cluster where mixed light sources are tolerated.
- Avoid direct midday sun cutting across the surface: it creates harsh shadows and hot spots that are difficult to recover in editing.

**Overcast days are often better than sunny days** for flat lays and scene shots because the cloud cover acts as a giant natural diffuser. If you have the choice between a clear-sky morning and an overcast morning, the overcast day produces more even light across the surface with no harsh shadow edges.

**Testing your window**: Before the shoot, place a white piece of paper on your planned shooting surface at your planned shoot time. Look at the paper. If you see a bright rectangular patch of direct sun on it, move the surface further from the window or wait for the sun angle to shift. If the paper is evenly lit with soft shadows from any objects placed on it, you have the right light.

### When Natural Light Is Not Available

If you cannot shoot during daylight hours or if your living situation does not have a usable window (basement apartment, north-facing windows only, etc.):

**Option 1: LED softbox (~$35–60 on Amazon)**
A single 45W daylight LED softbox placed at a 45-degree angle to the subject produces results equivalent to good window light for this type of photography. Search "45W photography softbox" — any from a recognized seller works. Place it at counter height, angled down at 30–45 degrees to the surface.

Do NOT use a ring light. Ring lights create a distinctive circular catch-light in glass and reflective surfaces that reads immediately as "influencer content" rather than authentic homestead imagery. This conflicts directly with Seedwarden's brand positioning.

**Option 2: Cloudy-day outdoor shooting**
A patio, deck, or covered outdoor area under overcast sky is often the most practical alternative to a window if indoor options are limited. The even overhead diffusion from overcast sky is genuinely excellent for flat lays. Avoid direct outdoor sun (same problem as indoor direct sun: harsh shadows and blown highlights).

## Part 3: Backdrops and Surfaces

Each cluster uses a specific surface type for visual consistency. These are not expensive; most can be found at home or sourced cheaply.

### Priority Surfaces (Have at Least Two Ready Before the Shoot)

**Surface 1 — Raw wood surface (essential)**
- Use: Cluster A (all 8 products), Cluster C (all 3 products)
- Options: large raw wood cutting board (18" or wider), reclaimed wood plank from a hardware store, outdoor picnic table top section, potting bench surface
- Why: Wood grain is the single most versatile backdrop in homestead-adjacent product photography. It communicates craftsmanship and authenticity without competing with the subject.
- If you don't have one: a $15–25 cutting board from a kitchen supply store is the right investment. It will be in the background of 18 of 30 shots.
- Not acceptable substitutes: veneer-over-particle-board (reads as fake), painted wood (distracting), laminate (reflective).

**Surface 2 — Dark linen or canvas cloth (important for variety)**
- Use: Cluster A (Products 2, 6 as secondary surface), contrast option for any overhead flat lay
- Color: navy, charcoal, or forest green — not black (black is too stark) and not beige/white (too bright)
- Size: 18" x 24" minimum
- Source: ~$10–15 at a fabric store; cut a piece from any linen fabric; no hemming needed
- Why: Seed envelopes and ceramic pots pop against dark linen in a way they do not against wood. Provides tonal variety within the cluster so not every shot looks identical.

**Surface 3 — Terracotta or stone tile (Cluster B)**
- Use: Cluster B flat lays (Product 11: Apartment Plant Catalog), urban container shots
- Options: a terracotta floor tile, a slate cheese board, an outdoor flagstone step
- Source: hardware store, home goods store, or outdoors
- If unavailable: stone patio surface (outdoor step) works well and is free

**Surface 4 — Concrete or stone outdoor surface**
- Use: Any outdoor scene shot where a gritty, practical context is appropriate
- This is a fallback surface — if your outdoor space has a concrete step, patio, or stone path, that is sufficient. No purchase needed.

### What NOT to Use as a Surface

| Surface | Why Not |
|---|---|
| White seamless paper | Reads as commercial product photography; wrong brand register |
| Patterned tablecloth or placemat | Competes with the product visually; creates noise in the composition |
| Shiny marble or granite | Reflections are nearly impossible to eliminate; reads as upscale rather than practical |
| Plastic or laminate | Reads as cheap; contradicts the artisanal brand signal |
| A dining table as-is (with finish) | Usually reflective; cover with a cloth or surface if needed |

Complete these the evening before each cluster session. Takes 15–20 minutes.

### Night Before Session 1 (Cluster A)
- [ ] Phone charged to 100%

## Part 6: Common Technical Errors and How to Prevent Them

This section covers the most frequently made errors in DIY product photography for digital goods on Etsy, based on what separates high-converting listing images from generic ones.

| Error | What It Looks Like | Prevention |
|---|---|---|
| Portrait mode at flat lay distance | Props are blurred instead of the background; the product page is sharp but the seed jar next to it is soft | Switch to standard photo mode for any overhead shot from 24+ inches |
| Auto white balance shift | Two shots from the same session look different color temperatures; batch editing requires individual adjustments | Lock white balance before first shot; do not open the camera app settings mid-session |
| Product not visible in thumbnail | Etsy crops the primary image to 1:1 square; the guide cover disappears into the corner | Before each shot, mentally apply a 1:1 center crop. If the product is not in the center third, recompose. |
| Overlit background / blown highlights | The window appears as a white rectangle; the scene looks washed out | Move the shooting surface further from the window, or angle the surface so the window is not in frame |
| Every prop the same shade of green | The frame looks flat and busy simultaneously | Vary surface texture: rough clay + smooth glass + soft linen + raw wood grain. Color variety is secondary to texture variety. |
| Commercial branding visible | A seed company logo on an envelope or a brand name on a jar reads as endorsement or as clutter | Tape over commercial text or turn the branded face away from camera before shooting |
| Hands posed, not acting | A hand resting beside a product looks like a hand resting beside a product — staged and unconvincing | Hands must always be mid-task: writing, holding, comparing, packing. If there is no task, remove the hands. |
| Blurry close-up shot | Portrait mode close-ups are sharpest when the subject is 6–18 inches from the lens; beyond 18 inches, focus becomes inconsistent | For close-ups, move physically closer to the subject rather than zooming in |

## Part 7: File Transfer and Backup Protocol

**Transfer immediately after each cluster session.** Do not accumulate all 30 shots before transferring. Transferring immediately:
- Protects against phone loss or damage during the multi-day shoot
- Allows a quick review while props are still staged (catch a reshoot need before props are packed)
- Makes the batch editing session faster (you know exactly what you captured)

**Transfer method:**
- iPhone to Mac: AirDrop or USB cable > Image Capture
- iPhone to Windows: USB cable > Windows Photos import
- Android: USB cable > file manager, or Google Photos auto-backup > download to desktop

**Cloud backup immediately after transfer**: Google Photos, iCloud, or Dropbox. All 30 raw images at 4–8 MB each = ~200–240 MB total. Any cloud service handles this in under 5 minutes on a standard connection.

**Naming for raw files**: No specific naming convention is required for raw files. Name them in Lightroom Mobile or file manager only when exporting finals. The final naming convention (product-slug-slot4-lifestyle.jpg) is applied at export time, not to raw files.

**Final export destination**: `/projects/seedwarden/marketing/lifestyle-photos/etsy-ready/`

Confirm this directory exists before the export session. If it does not exist yet, create it.
