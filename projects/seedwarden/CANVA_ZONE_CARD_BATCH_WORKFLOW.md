# Canva Zone Card Batch Design Workflow
## Seedwarden Phase 2 — 8 Zone Quick-Start Cards, Per-Zone Content Reference

**Purpose**: This is the Canva operator's reference document for the zone card production run. It contains two things: (1) the precise batch workflow for building all 8 cards efficiently from a single master template, and (2) a complete per-zone content table with every word that goes into each card. ZONE_CARD_PRODUCTION_TIMELINE.md is the week-by-week schedule; ZONE_QUICKSTART_CARD_SPEC.md is the design brief. This document is the content database and production decision guide you keep open while working in Canva.

**How to use this document**: Keep it open alongside Canva. When you start a new zone card, navigate to that zone's content table, copy each text block into the correct Canva field, change the zone band color, and export. The full copy for every field is here — nothing needs to be composed or invented during the Canva session.

**Time estimates per card:**
- Zone 5 master template (first build): 150–180 minutes (includes full layout build)
- Zone 6 (first duplicate): 30 minutes
- Zones 3, 4, 7, 8, 9, 10 (subsequent duplicates): 35–45 minutes each

Total production time across all 8 cards: approximately 7.5–9 hours. Organized as described in ZONE_CARD_PRODUCTION_TIMELINE.md across Weeks 1–3 in 90-minute sessions.

## Part 1: Batch Workflow — The Right Build Order

Build in this order. The sequence prevents zone-band color errors and minimizes re-work.

| Build order | Zone | Zone band hex | Duplicate from |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 (master) | 5 | #2D5016 Temperate | (blank template) |
| 2 | 6 | #2D5016 Temperate | Zone 5 master |
| 3 | 3 | #3D6B8A Cool | Zone 5 master |
| 4 | 4 | #3D6B8A Cool | Zone 3 |
| 5 | 7 | #C9943A Warm | Zone 5 master |
| 6 | 8 | #C9943A Warm | Zone 7 |
| 7 | 9 | #A0522D Hot | Zone 5 master |
| 8 | 10 | #A0522D Hot | Zone 9 |

**Why this order matters**: Each zone-band color group is built sequentially (duplicate with the correct color already set, then update content). If you duplicate Zone 5 (green band) and forget to change the band to blue before building Zone 3 content, you ship a Zone 3 card with the wrong visual indicator. The table above makes this impossible by having you duplicate within the correct color group.

**Rule**: Always duplicate from a card with the same zone-band color or from the Zone 5 master (then immediately change the band color as the first edit, before any text).

## Part 2: Per-Session Workflow

### Building the Master Template (Zone 5 — Session 1A + 1B)

This is covered in full in ZONE_CARD_PRODUCTION_TIMELINE.md and TRACK_B_EXECUTION_KICKOFF.md. Short version:

Session 1A (90 min): Build layout only — header, three-column body, variety spotlight band, footer. No zone content yet. Verify zone number is visually dominant at 72pt.

Session 1B (60 min): Populate Zone 5 content from Part 3 of this document (Zone 5 table). Export PDF, review at 100% zoom, save as master template.

### Building Subsequent Zones (Sessions 2A, 2B, 3A)

For each duplicate card, execute in this exact sequence:

1. Duplicate the appropriate source card in Canva (see build order table above)
2. Rename the design immediately: `Seedwarden Zone [X] Quick-Start Card`
3. Change the zone number: the 72pt header number is the first and most visible change
4. Change the zone band color (the 12px horizontal rule): only needed when crossing from one color group to another (Zone 5 to Zone 3, Zone 5 to Zone 7, Zone 5 to Zone 9)
5. Update the region name line
6. Update Column 1 content: frost dates, growing season, example cities, This Month tasks
7. Update Column 2 content: three Quick-Start Crops
8. Update Column 3 content: Storage and Preservation Tips
9. Update Variety Spotlight section: three varieties with one-line descriptions
10. Review: confirm zone number correct, zone band color correct, footer placeholder links present
11. Export PDF Print (Download > PDF Print > all pages > done)
12. Name the file: `zone-[X]-quick-start-card.pdf`
13. Upload to Kit: Content > Files — copy the download URL, paste into your URL tracking log

Do steps 1–13 for each zone before moving to the next. Do not build two zones simultaneously — the zone-specific content in both header and body is dense enough that interleaving creates errors.

### Zone 3

**Header zone number**: 3
**Region line**: Northern Plains, Mountain Interior, Upper Great Lakes
**Zone band color**: Cool Slate Blue #3D6B8A

**Column 1 — Frost Dates and Season**

Last frost: May 15 – June 1
First frost: September 5 – September 20
Growing season: 95–125 days
Example cities: International Falls MN, Bismarck ND

This Month — May 2026:
1. Start cucumbers, squash, melons, and pumpkins indoors now (3–4 weeks before last frost). Zone 3's short season requires a head start.
2. Direct sow peas, spinach, and radishes as soon as soil is workable — these crops tolerate frost.
3. Finish pepper and eggplant seedlings under lights — they need 4–6 more weeks before outdoor temperatures allow transplanting.

**Column 2 — Quick-Start Crops**

1. Bush beans (Provider OP) — direct sow after last frost, 55 days, reliable short-season producer.
2. Kale (Lacinato H) — cold-hardy, harvest June through first hard freeze, frost improves flavor.
3. Stupice tomato (OP) — 52–65 days, the Zone 3 tomato standard, produces before frost.

**Column 3 — Storage and Preservation Tips**

Zone 3's short harvest window makes batch-processing essential. Learn lacto-fermentation for greens — no canning equipment, 20-minute hands-on time per batch.

Root cellar or cool basement storage (35–40 F) extends beet, carrot, and potato harvest into spring.

Bush beans all ripen within two weeks in Zone 3. Freeze or dehydrate at harvest peak — do not try to eat them fresh in that window.

**Variety Spotlight**

Stupice tomato (H): Czech heirloom, 52–65 days. The tomato that actually ripens in Zone 3. Small red fruits, reliable even in short seasons. Save seed year after year.

Waltham 29 broccoli (H): 74-day compact heads, handles Zone 3 cold, plant in March for June harvest.

Golden Bantam sweet corn (H): 75 days, the historic American heirloom corn. Grows in Zone 3 in the warmest microsites. Direct sow after last frost.

### Zone 4

**Header zone number**: 4
**Region line**: Upper Midwest, Northern New England, Mountain Valleys
**Zone band color**: Cool Slate Blue #3D6B8A

**Column 1 — Frost Dates and Season**

Last frost: May 5 – May 25
First frost: September 15 – October 5
Growing season: 120–150 days
Example cities: Minneapolis MN, Burlington VT, Madison WI

This Month — May 2026:
1. Direct sow peas, spinach, lettuce, arugula, radishes, and turnips — soil is workable and these crops need 4–6 weeks before summer heat.
2. Start cucumbers, squash, pumpkins, melons indoors in late April (3–4 weeks before last frost).
3. Plant onion sets and seed potatoes now — both handle light frost once in the ground.

**Column 2 — Quick-Start Crops**

1. Brandywine tomato (H) — 80 days, full Zone 4 season, the tomato worth growing here.
2. Sugar snap peas (OP) — direct sow April, harvest by June, done before summer heat. Best reward-to-effort ratio in Zone 4 spring.
3. Detroit Dark Red beet (H) — sow mid-April, harvest July through October, stores for months in a cool space.

**Column 3 — Storage and Preservation Tips**

Zone 4's brief canning window (August–September) means canning supplies must be ready before harvest, not purchased during peak when stores run short.

Dehydrate herbs (dill, basil, parsley) at harvest peak in July — Zone 4 herbs bolt quickly. Dehydrating takes 20 minutes of prep and preserves the season for 12 months.

Root cellar or garage: beets, carrots, potatoes, and winter squash store 4–6 months at 35–45 F. Cure squash for 2 weeks at room temperature before cool storage.

**Variety Spotlight**

Brandywine tomato (H): The benchmark heirloom, 80 days, full Zone 4 season. Pink beefsteak, rich flavor. Worth every day of the growing season.

Long Island Improved Brussels sprouts (H): 90 days — Zone 4 is long enough for it. Frost improves flavor; harvest into October.

Scarlet Nantes carrot (H): Direct sow April, reliable germinator in Zone 4 soil, 65 days. The heirloom carrot standard.

### Zone 5

**Header zone number**: 5
**Region line**: Central Corridor, Southern New England, Mid-Elevation West
**Zone band color**: Temperate Forest Green #2D5016

**Column 1 — Frost Dates and Season**

Last frost: April 15 – May 10
First frost: October 1 – October 20
Growing season: 150–180 days
Example cities: Denver CO, Des Moines IA, Boston MA suburbs

This Month — May 2026:
1. Transplant broccoli, cauliflower, cabbage, kale, and onion starts outdoors now — they are frost-tolerant and need to establish before heat arrives.
2. Direct sow beets, carrots, and succession lettuce — these are your spring harvests.
3. Start cucumbers, squash, pumpkins, and melons indoors in late April (3–4 weeks before last frost). Do not start cucurbits too early — they resent root disturbance.

**Column 2 — Quick-Start Crops**

1. Dragon Tongue bush bean (H) — direct sow after last frost, 57 days, no staking, high yield, excellent in containers.
2. Mortgage Lifter tomato (H) — 80 days, large beefsteak, forgiving for beginners, full Zone 5 season.
3. Lemon cucumber (H) — 65 days, compact vine, prolific, less bitter than slicing types, great for containers.

**Column 3 — Storage and Preservation Tips**

Zone 5 gets two natural preservation windows: early summer (peas, greens — blanch and freeze) and late summer (tomatoes, peppers, beans — can, ferment, or dehydrate).

Tomato sauce is the highest-value Zone 5 preservation project. A day of canning in August produces 18–24 quarts — 6 months of pasta sauce.

Hot sauce fermentation uses peppers that ripen just before frost. Ferment 2–3 weeks, then refrigerate — no canning needed.

**Variety Spotlight**

Mortgage Lifter tomato (H): 80 days, bred by M.C. Byles of West Virginia for home gardeners. Exceptionally large sweet pink beefsteak. Seed-saveable, true to type, a Zone 5 staple.

Dragon Tongue bean (H): Yellow with purple streaks, 57 days, no staking. The variety that converts non-bean-growers.

Chioggia beet (H): 55 days, candy-stripe interior, dual-purpose root and greens, grows in 12 inches of soil. Ideal for Zone 5 raised beds and containers.

### Zone 6

**Header zone number**: 6
**Region line**: Mid-Atlantic, Ohio Valley, Central Transition Zone
**Zone band color**: Temperate Forest Green #2D5016

**Column 1 — Frost Dates and Season**

Last frost: April 5 – April 25
First frost: October 10 – November 1
Growing season: 170–200 days
Example cities: St. Louis MO, Philadelphia PA, Kansas City MO

This Month — May 2026:
1. Tomatoes, peppers, and eggplants can go out in the ground — your last frost is early April in most of Zone 6. Harden off 7–10 days before transplanting.
2. Direct sow beans and corn now (soil temperature above 60 F).
3. Succession-sow lettuce, radishes, and arugula for a second spring round before summer heat.

**Column 2 — Quick-Start Crops**

1. Cherokee Purple tomato (H) — 72–80 days, the signature heirloom for Zone 6's long summer. Rich complex flavor; harvests July through October.
2. Rattlesnake pole bean (H) — direct sow after mid-April, 65–70 days, drought-tolerant, distinctive purple-streaked pods.
3. Clemson Spineless okra (H) — Zone 6 is warm enough for productive okra. Direct sow April, harvest July–October.

**Column 3 — Storage and Preservation Tips**

Zone 6 gardeners can run two full preservation seasons: early (peas, spring brassicas in June) and main (tomatoes, peppers, beans, corn in August–September).

Tomato canning is the anchor — Zone 6's long tomato season (July–October) means enough volume to can, ferment, and dry.

Fermented pickles (cucumbers, green beans) are a low-equipment option that peaks in Zone 6's July harvest window.

**Variety Spotlight**

Cherokee Purple tomato (H): 72–80 days, deeply flavored dark red-purple beefsteak with green shoulders. One of the most widely grown heirlooms in Zone 6–7 for good reason.

Rattlesnake pole bean (H): Named for its markings. Drought-tolerant — a virtue in Zone 6 summers. 65–70 days, continuous producer if picked regularly.

Wando pea (OP): The heat-tolerant pea. Direct sow late March or April and it keeps producing into Zone 6's early summer heat when other peas quit.

### Zone 7

**Header zone number**: 7
**Region line**: Piedmont South, Oklahoma, North Texas, Maritime Northwest
**Zone band color**: Warm Amber #C9943A

**Special note for Column 1**: Add subzone caveat after frost dates: "Zone 7a last frost April 5–15; Zone 7b last frost March 20–April 5. Check planthardiness.ars.usda.gov for your exact subzone."

**Column 1 — Frost Dates and Season**

Last frost: March 22 – April 15 (Zone 7a to 7b range)
First frost: October 20 – November 10
Growing season: 190–225 days
Example cities: Raleigh NC, Oklahoma City OK, Seattle WA inland

This Month — May 2026:
1. Tomatoes, peppers, and eggplants are already in the ground — your last frost passed in March or early April.
2. Direct sow beans, corn, cucumber, squash, zucchini, and okra throughout April and into May.
3. Harvest cool-season crops before they bolt in Zone 7's spring heat — peas, lettuce, spinach, and broccoli finish by late May. Pick now at peak.

**Column 2 — Quick-Start Crops**

1. Okra — Clemson Spineless (H) or Hill Country Red (OP) — Zone 7 is the sweet spot for okra. Direct sow April, harvest June through October.
2. Sweet potato — Beauregard (OP) — plant slips after soil reaches 65 F. Harvest October. Stores for months.
3. Amish Paste tomato (H) — 85 days, large paste type, exceptional for sauce, heat-tolerant enough for Zone 7's summer.

**Column 3 — Storage and Preservation Tips**

Zone 7's main preservation window is July–October: sweet potatoes, tomatoes, peppers, okra, and beans.

Okra freezes better than it cans — blanch 3 minutes, freeze on a sheet pan, then bag. Maintains texture better than jars.

Sweet potato curing (10 days at 85–90 F, then 55–60 F storage) extends shelf life from weeks to 6–12 months. A 10-day passive process that multiplies harvest value dramatically.

**Variety Spotlight**

Amish Paste tomato (H): 85 days, large meaty paste type, developed in Amish communities and adapted to Southeastern heat. Superior sauce tomato for Zone 7.

Clemson Spineless okra (H): The 1939 All-America Selections winner and still the standard. Productive, spineless for easy harvesting, 56 days.

Beauregard sweet potato (OP): 90 days, copper skin, orange flesh. The most reliable sweet potato for Zone 7 home gardeners. Plant slips, not seeds.

### Zone 8

**Header zone number**: 8
**Region line**: Deep South, Coastal Pacific Northwest, Central Texas
**Zone band color**: Warm Amber #C9943A

**Column 1 — Frost Dates and Season**

Last frost: March 5 – March 25
First frost: November 1 – November 25
Growing season: 225–265 days
Example cities: Portland OR, Dallas TX, Atlanta GA

This Month — May 2026:
1. Tomatoes, peppers, eggplants, and all warm-season crops are in the ground — last frost passed in March. May is full growing season in Zone 8.
2. Plant sweet potato slips now (soil above 65 F). Plant okra and southern peas for summer.
3. Harvest spring crops (lettuce, broccoli, cauliflower, peas) before they bolt in May's rising heat. Succession sow beans and corn for summer harvest.

**Column 2 — Quick-Start Crops**

1. Homestead tomato (OP) — 80 days, heat-tolerant, disease-resistant, bred for the South. Reliable where heirlooms sometimes struggle in Zone 8's heat and humidity.
2. Clemson Spineless okra (H) — zone staple. Direct sow March–April, harvest June–November. Thrives where other crops fail.
3. Seminole pumpkin (H) — heat-and-humidity-tolerant heirloom squash. Resists vine borers, stores for a year without refrigeration.

**Column 3 — Storage and Preservation Tips**

Zone 8's summer heat and humidity makes fermented vegetables more reliable than you might expect — active fermentation cultures out-compete spoilage bacteria. Kimchi, fermented green beans, and hot sauce all work well.

Sweet potato curing and long storage is the highest-ROI preservation project in Zone 8. One 4×8 ft bed can produce 50–80 lbs. Cured and stored at 55–60 F, they last 8–12 months.

Dehydration shines in Zone 8: hot peppers, okra, and tomatoes all dehydrate well and reduce to compact shelf-stable storage.

**Variety Spotlight**

Homestead tomato (OP): The tomato bred for Southern conditions. 80 days, globe-shaped, disease-resistant. Reliably productive where Brandywine fails in July humidity.

Seminole pumpkin (H): The historically grown squash of the Southeast. Pest-resistant, stores 12 months without refrigeration, grows as a sprawling vine. Culturally significant and practically superior.

Rattlesnake watermelon (H): 90 days, 25–40 lbs, thrives in Zone 8 heat and humidity. A Southern heirloom worth growing for its own history.

### Zone 9

**Header zone number**: 9
**Region line**: Gulf Coast, Southern Texas, Central Florida, SoCal Inland
**Zone band color**: Terracotta #A0522D

**Important note for Zone 9**: This zone has two growing seasons (spring and fall) with a summer dormancy or slowdown period. The "This Month" tasks reflect this — mention both the end of spring and the approach of summer management in May.

**Column 1 — Frost Dates and Season**

Last frost: February 10 – March 5
First frost: November 20 – December 15
Growing season: 260–300 days
Example cities: Houston TX, Jacksonville FL, Sacramento CA

This Month — May 2026:
1. Spring garden is in full production — harvest cucumbers and squash frequently to maximize yield before summer heat peaks.
2. Cool-season crops are done or finishing. Remove spent plants now — do not let them bolt and exhaust the soil.
3. Get okra seeds in the ground now if not already done. Okra, southern peas, and sweet potatoes are your summer workhorses.

**Column 2 — Quick-Start Crops**

1. Okra — Cow Horn (H) or Clemson Spineless (H) — the Zone 9 summer crop. Sow April through June, harvest through November.
2. Heat Wave II tomato (OP) or Solar Fire (OP) — standard Zone 9 heat-tolerant tomatoes. Set fruit at high temperatures when standard varieties drop blossoms.
3. California Blackeye cowpea (OP) — Southern pea for Zone 9's summer. Nitrogen-fixing, edible beans, direct sow April through June.

**Column 3 — Storage and Preservation Tips**

Zone 9 has two preservation windows: spring (February–April, before heat) and fall (October–December, after heat). Plan canning sessions around these, not around August.

Tomatoes: Process spring tomatoes in April–May before the crop finishes. Fall tomatoes ripen October–November for a second canning session.

If April kitchen heat is already impractical: freeze crushed spring tomatoes and process them in October when temperatures allow comfortable canning.

**Variety Spotlight**

Tropic tomato (OP): Bred at the University of Florida specifically for tropical and subtropical heat. 72 days, disease-resistant, heat-set. The workhorse Zone 9 tomato.

Cow Horn okra (H): Longer pods (8–12 inches), classic Southern heirloom, prolific in Zone 9's heat. Harvest when pods are 4–6 inches for best texture.

Zipper Cream cowpea (OP): Named for the easy-to-shell pod. Rich, creamy texture. One of the most popular Southern peas for Zone 9 home gardeners.

### Zone 10

**Header zone number**: 10
**Region line**: South Florida, Coastal Southern California, Hawaii
**Zone band color**: Terracotta #A0522D

**Important note for Zone 10**: This zone is essentially frost-free year-round in most areas. The growing calendar is inverted compared to the northern zones — summer is the fallow or low-production period; fall and winter are peak growing seasons. The "This Month" tasks and variety spotlight must reflect this.

**Column 1 — Frost Dates and Season**

Last frost: January 31 or earlier; many areas frost-free year-round
First frost: December 15 or later; many areas frost-free
Growing season: 300–365 days (essentially year-round)
Example cities: Miami FL (frost-free), San Diego CA, Honolulu HI (frost-free)

This Month — May 2026:
1. Cool-season crops are done — harvest remaining lettuce, peas, broccoli, and cauliflower now before they bolt completely.
2. Succession-sow okra through April and May for a staggered summer harvest.
3. Plan your summer strategy now: Zone 10 open gardens enter the hot season in June. Mulch heavily, solarize empty beds, and plan to start fall crops indoors in late July.

**Column 2 — Quick-Start Crops**

1. Everglades tomato (H) — the cherry tomato native to South Florida, essentially perennial in Zone 10. Small, prolific, heat-set, disease-resistant. Self-sows. The only tomato that thrives in Zone 10 summer heat year after year.
2. Seminole pumpkin (H) — native to Florida, bred for Zone 10 conditions. Resists vine borers, stores 12 months, sprawling vine.
3. Malabar spinach (OP) — not true spinach, but the vine vegetable that fills the spinach role in Zone 10's summer. Heat-loving, productive on a trellis.

**Column 3 — Storage and Preservation Tips**

Zone 10's cool season (October–April) is the primary preservation window — process spring tomatoes, citrus, and root vegetables while temperatures allow comfortable kitchen work.

Tropical fruit preservation (Florida: avocado, mango, guava; SoCal: citrus, figs, avocado) is the Zone 10-specific preservation opportunity most guides overlook. Dehydrated mango and fig preserves are high-value, long shelf life.

Fermented hot sauce is ideal for Zone 10's prolific hot pepper harvest — no canning required, and Zone 10's warm temperatures accelerate lacto-fermentation.

**Variety Spotlight**

Everglades cherry tomato (H): Native Florida heirloom. Produces prolifically through conditions that kill standard tomatoes. Self-sows. Small marble-sized fruits, intensely sweet. The one tomato worth growing in Zone 10 summer.

Seminole pumpkin (H): The historically significant Florida heirloom. Pest-resistant, stores 12 months without refrigeration, heat-adapted. Culturally significant.

Datil pepper (H): St. Augustine, Florida's locally famous hot pepper. Similar heat to habanero, fruitier flavor. The defining Zone 10 (Florida) hot pepper for sauces and ferments.

## Part 5: Quality Check Before Each Export

Complete these six checks per card before downloading the PDF. These are the six most common errors in a batch production run.

1. **Zone number is correct**: Look at the large 72pt number in the header. Read it aloud. Confirm it matches the zone you are building.

2. **Zone band color is correct**: Compare the horizontal rule color against the quick reference table above. If you duplicated from a card in a different color group and forgot to update, this is where you catch it.

3. **Region line matches the zone**: Zone 3 should say "Northern Plains, Mountain Interior, Upper Great Lakes" — not anything from another zone. This text sits directly under the large zone number. Easy to miss if you were copying text and pasted from the wrong source row.

4. **This Month tasks use May 2026** (for the initial May launch): The tasks in Part 3 of this document are written for May 2026. If you are building cards in a later month, update these tasks to the current month before exporting. Frost dates, crops, storage tips, and variety spotlight do not change monthly — only This Month tasks do.

5. **Footer placeholder links are present but NOT live Etsy URLs** (for cards being built before Etsy listings are active): The footer should read "Get the full Zone [X] Calendar — [Etsy link placeholder]" and "Free guide: seedwarden.com/zone". Do not put a broken Etsy URL in the footer. Leave the placeholder text until the Etsy listing is confirmed live.

6. **File exported as PDF Print at 300 DPI**: In Canva: Download > PDF Print (not PDF Standard — Print gives higher resolution). Confirm the resulting file is under 1.5 MB. Canva single-page designs at 300 DPI typically export between 800 KB and 1.2 MB for this content density.

## Part 8: Monthly This Month Refresh Workflow

The This Month block in Column 1 is the only element that requires monthly updates. All other content (frost dates, crops, storage tips, variety spotlight) is stable across the growing season.

**Monthly update protocol (20 minutes, all 8 zones):**

1. Open the Canva master file for Zone 5 first
2. Navigate to Column 1 — find the "This Month" task block
3. Update: change the month name in the header ("May" → "June")
4. Update: replace the three task bullets with June tasks for Zone 5 (source: the Zone-by-Zone Seed Starting Calendar product — Seedwarden's own product, already zone-verified and monthly)
5. Export: re-download the PDF
6. In Kit: Content > Files > upload the new Zone 5 PDF; Kit replaces the file at the same URL if you upload with the same filename
7. Repeat for Zones 6, 3, 4, 7, 8, 9, 10 in order (same sequence as original build)

**Total refresh time per month**: 15–25 minutes across all 8 zones.

**Set a recurring calendar reminder**: First business day of each month. Title: "Zone Card This Month refresh — 20 min."

**Why this matters**: A subscriber who downloads the June card gets June-specific tasks, not the April content they would receive from a static file. Seasonal freshness is what makes the card genuinely useful rather than archival — and it gives Seedwarden a reason to post "Zone cards updated for June" on social each month, generating one low-effort content piece per month indefinitely.
