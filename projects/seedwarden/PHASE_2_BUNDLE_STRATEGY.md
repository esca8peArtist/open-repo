# Phase 2 Bundle Strategy
## Bundle Testing, Pricing Psychology, and Cohort-Aligned Packaging

**Purpose**: This document defines the bundle combinations to test in Phase 2, analyzes pricing psychology and discount depth, and maps bundle appeal to Seedwarden's four customer cohorts. Bundles in Phase 1 already exist (Apartment Grower Bundle at $32, Food Sovereignty Bundle at $30). This document builds on those foundations with three new bundle tests and a structured A/B testing plan for Months 1–3.

**Key constraint**: All bundle pricing in this document assumes digital PDF products. No physical product bundles are in scope for Phase 2. Phase 3 may introduce print-on-demand bundles if Wave 3 economics validate.

---

## Part 1: Phase 1 Bundle Baseline and What the Data Will Tell Us

### Existing Phase 1 Bundles

Two bundles are live in Phase 1 (from bundle-listings.md):

| Bundle | Price | Individual Total | Discount | Products |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Apartment Grower Bundle | $32 | $42 | $10 (24%) | Apt. Seed Starting, Plant Catalog, Container Blueprint, 12-Month Planner |
| Food Sovereignty Bundle | $30 | $42 | $12 (29%) | Seed Saving Manual, Food Sovereignty Starter, Anti-Catalog, Heirloom Guide |

**What Month 1–2 data from these bundles will reveal**:
- Whether the 24–29% discount rate drives purchases, or whether buyers would have bought the most expensive item anyway
- Which cohorts bundle — the Apartment Grower Bundle is Cluster B; the Food Sovereignty Bundle is Cluster A
- Whether bundled order values are higher than the individual item average ($12 per the financial model)

Until Phase 1 data arrives, all Phase 2 bundle decisions are hypotheses. The A/B test structure below is designed to give Phase 1 data a direct role in validating or overriding the recommendations here.

### What We Already Know Without Data

From the financial-sustainability-model.md cohort analysis:

- **Survival Prepper cohort** places large single orders (6+ guides), already bundles by behavior, and has the highest order value ($45–100+). A pre-packaged Prepper Bundle aligns with their existing behavior and removes the cart-building friction they are already navigating.
- **Gift Buyer cohort** makes seasonal purchases (holiday, Mother's Day, graduation) and responds to "complete system" framing — they want to give something substantial, not a single guide.
- **High-Intent Forager cohort** makes repeat purchases seasonally. They are not the primary bundle audience in Phase 2 — they prefer targeted specific purchases over broad bundles. Bundle appeal is lower here.
- **Homesteader cohort** is the largest segment (30–35%) and makes thematically driven purchases. They bundle when the bundle is thematically coherent (all preservation, or all seed-saving) but resist bundles that mix themes.

## Part 2: Three Phase 2 Bundle Tests

The three bundles below are designed to test distinct hypotheses. Only one should go live per 4-week test window to isolate the variable. Launch order is in the sequence that maximizes spring-to-summer seasonal alignment.

---

### Bundle Test 1: Wild Edibles + Zone Quick-Start Card — "Forager Starter Bundle" ($25)

**Test hypothesis**: The Wild Edibles guide ($18 individually) pairs with the Zone Quick-Start Card (free lead magnet or paid add-on) as a low-friction cross-sell bundle that converts browsers who are close to buying one but not both.

**Products**: Wild Edibles Quick Reference (or Native Plants Regional Guide, $16–18) + Zone Quick-Start Card ($5–7 as a standalone product, if listed)

**Pricing logic**:
- Native Plants Regional Guide: $16
- Zone Quick-Start Card (if listed as paid): $6
- Individual total: $22
- Bundle price: $18 — saving $4 (18%)
- Rationale: A 20%+ discount threshold is the standard trigger for "bundle feels worth it." However, for low-price-point bundles, the savings amount matters more than the percentage. $4 savings on an $18 purchase is marginal. This bundle is better positioned as a convenience play ("get both at once") than a discount play.

**Alternative pricing structure**: List the Zone Quick-Start Card at $0 (free download / lead magnet only) and do not create a formal bundle listing. Instead, promote the combination via email — "Bought the Wild Edibles Guide? Here's your free zone card." This approach tests the same cross-sell without requiring a separate bundle listing.

**Cohort appeal**:
- High-Intent Forager: High appeal. This is the cohort most likely to buy the foraging guide and also find value in the zone-specific seasonal context.
- Homesteader: Moderate appeal. The zone card resonates; the wild edibles pairing may feel off-theme for homesteaders who came for seed-saving content.
- Survival Prepper: Low. They will buy the foraging guide as part of a larger order rather than as a targeted bundle.
- Gift Buyer: Low. "Forager Starter Bundle" does not read as a gift product.

**Test window**: Months 1–2 post-Phase-1 launch (May–June 2026 — prime foraging season).

**Success metric**: Bundle listing CTR above 2.5% and conversion rate above 3%. Individual guide orders should not drop during the test period (the bundle should be additive, not cannibalistic).

### Bundle Test 2: Wild Edibles Guide + Zone Quick-Start Card at $25 vs. Separate at $30 Total — "Spring Forager Bundle"

**Test hypothesis**: A $5 combined discount on a $30 hypothetical total drives more incremental revenue than the separate products listed individually, particularly in the April–June spring foraging window.

**Pricing**:
- Wild Edibles Quick Reference Guide: $18 (individual price)
- Zone-by-Zone Seed Starting Calendar (Zone-specific, individual): $7
- Combined individual total: $25
- Bundle price: $22 — $3 savings (12%)
- Note: A 12% discount is below the psychological threshold for compelling savings. Consider pricing at $20 (20% savings) to reach the trigger point, accepting $2 less per transaction in exchange for a measurably higher conversion rate.

**Why this pairing works**:
The Zone Calendar tells the buyer when to plant each month. The Wild Edibles Guide tells them what they can forage while waiting. These products solve adjacent problems in the same seasonal rhythm — the buyer who values one will predictably value the other. The pairing is not artificial; it reflects actual grower behavior.

**Cohort appeal**:
- High-Intent Forager: Very high. This bundle maps exactly to their seasonal behavior in spring.
- Homesteader: High. Homesteaders buy the Zone Calendar as an operating tool and add foraging content as supplementary.
- Survival Prepper: Moderate. This bundle does not read as "preparedness" framing — it reads as "garden planning." Preppers who are already buying the Zone Calendar may add the foraging guide, but not because of the bundle.
- Gift Buyer: Low.

**Test window**: Month 1 (May 2026 — spring peak). This is the highest seasonal relevance window for both products in the bundle.

### Bundle Test 3: Full Preservation Stack — "Harvest Season Bundle" ($38)

**Test hypothesis**: The three preservation products (Fermented and Preserved Harvest Handbook, Harvest Preservation Field Manual, Grow Your Own Hot Sauce) form a cohesive stack that homesteaders buy collectively as a single seasonal investment before the summer harvest window.

**Products and pricing**:
- Fermented and Preserved Harvest Handbook: $13
- Harvest Preservation Field Manual: $15
- Grow Your Own Hot Sauce: $10
- Individual total: $38
- Bundle price: $28 — $10 savings (26%)
- The 26% discount is at the upper end of the compelling range. This is the right depth for a $38→$28 bundle because the dollar savings ($10) is significant at the price point.

**Framing**: "Harvest Season Bundle — Everything you need to preserve your summer harvest. Fermented vegetables, water-bath canning, dehydrating, and hot sauce from your own peppers. Guides that work together, priced together."

**When to launch**: June–July 2026 (pre-harvest season). Buyers who plan their preservation setup before August are the most motivated — they are problem-aware (summer harvest is coming and they do not know how to handle the volume) and actively searching for solutions. The bundle should be live and indexed on Etsy before the August canning rush.

**Seasonal alignment note**: The Food Preservation cluster has its own seasonal rhythm distinct from the seed/garden cluster. The search peak for "canning guide" and "fermentation guide" is July–September, not March–May. Launching this bundle in June positions it to capture the rising search trend before peak.

**Cohort appeal**:
- Homesteader: Very high. This is their bundle. Homesteaders are preservation-oriented, multi-method, and buy guides as references they keep.
- Gift Buyer: High. A "Harvest Season Bundle" reads as a thoughtful gift for someone who preserves food. The $28 price point is a comfortable gift-level purchase.
- High-Intent Forager: Moderate. Foragers who harvest in quantity become preservation buyers by necessity. This bundle captures them when they realize foraging produces more than they can eat fresh.
- Survival Prepper: Moderate. Self-sufficiency framing connects, but preppers may already own one or two of these individually.

**Test window**: Month 2–3 (June–July 2026).

## Part 3: Pricing Psychology Framework

### The Three Discount Depth Zones

| Discount | Buyer Perception | Use Case |
|---|---|---|
| Under 15% | "Bundled for convenience" — minimal savings feel | Low-price bundles where the convenience argument is stronger than savings |
| 15–25% | "This is worth buying together" — the standard bundle trigger | The target zone for most Seedwarden bundles |
| 26–35% | "Real savings, limited time feel" — highest conversion but eats more margin | Seasonal anchor bundles (Harvest Season Bundle) or clearance events |
| Over 35% | Often reads as desperation or value perception problem | Avoid |

**Seedwarden's structural advantage**: Because the marginal cost per unit sold is zero (digital products), any bundle discount comes entirely out of revenue, not out of cost. A $10 discount on a $38 bundle is $10 less revenue per sale, not $10 less profit. The financial model can sustain deeper discounts than physical product sellers because there is no COGS floor.

### Charm Pricing vs. Round Number Pricing

Etsy research on digital products consistently shows:
- Charm pricing ($24.99 vs $25) has minimal impact for products priced above $15 — buyers in the homesteading/gardening niche respond to round number transparency
- Bundle savings framed as "save $10" outperform "save 26%" — buyers do the dollar math, not the percentage math, when evaluating bundles
- "Usually sold separately for $38, this bundle is $28" is the most converting bundle framing on Etsy — it makes the comparison explicit

**Recommended framing for all Seedwarden bundle listings**:
Lead with the dollar savings, not the percentage. Example: "Buying these three guides separately costs $38. This bundle is $28 — $10 off when you get the full preservation stack."

### Price Anchor Strategy

The bundle listing should always display the individual total prominently. In the Etsy listing title, include the savings: "Harvest Season Bundle — $28 (Saves $10)." This creates a price anchor effect — the buyer's reference point becomes $38 (the would-be individual cost), not $28 (the bundle price). Everything feels like a deal relative to the anchor.

## Part 4: A/B Test Plan — Months 1–3

Because Etsy does not support native A/B testing of listing variants, the "test" here is sequential — one bundle live at a time, with a 4-week observation window before the next bundle launches. This is not a true simultaneous A/B test, but it provides directional data across seasonal contexts.

### Month 1 A/B Test: Spring Forager Bundle vs. No Bundle

**Control**: Wild Edibles Guide and Zone Calendar listed individually at $18 and $7 (combined buyer potential: $25 if both purchased).

**Test**: Spring Forager Bundle at $22, both products listed individually alongside the bundle listing.

**Measurement period**: 4 weeks (first full month with lifestyle photos on listings, for clean attribution).

**What to track**:
- Bundle listing page views vs. individual listing page views
- Conversion rate of bundle vs. individual products
- Whether individual product conversions drop when the bundle is live (cannibalization signal)
- Average order value: does the bundle pull order value up (buyers who would have bought one guide now buy two via the bundle) or down (buyers who would have bought both individually are now paying the bundle price instead)?

**Decision rule**: If the bundle listing converts at 3%+ and average order value increases by $5+ vs. the control period, expand bundle strategy. If average order value does not change, the bundle is additive but not incremental — it will not hurt and can stay live, but is not a conversion driver.

### Month 2: Harvest Season Bundle Launch

Launch the Harvest Season Bundle at $28. No active A/B test needed — June is too early in the data collection period to have comparative baseline. This bundle is launched on seasonal timing, not test-driven timing.

**Track**: Total preservation category revenue in June vs. May (seasonality-adjusted). If June preservation revenue exceeds May by more than 30%, seasonal demand exists and the bundle is surfacing it. If June is flat, the bundle is not working as a volume lever and pricing should be tested ($25 vs. $28).

### Month 3: Pricing Test on Harvest Season Bundle

**Test**: Change the Harvest Season Bundle price from $28 to $25 for a 4-week period.

**What to track**:
- Conversion rate at $25 vs. $28
- Total bundle revenue at $25 (more units × lower price) vs. $28 (fewer units × higher price)
- If $25 sells 30%+ more units, the lower price captures more revenue — move to $25 permanently
- If $25 sells fewer than 20% more units than $28, $28 captures more total revenue — move back to $28

This is the most actionable price test in the first 90 days because it is testing a specific price point on a defined product with historical data available for comparison.

## Part 5: Per-Cohort Bundle Appeal Summary

| Bundle | Forager | Homesteader | Prepper | Gift Buyer | Best Launch Window |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Spring Forager Bundle ($22) | Very high | High | Moderate | Low | May–June |
| Wild Edibles + Zone Card email cross-sell (free) | High | Moderate | Low | Low | Ongoing |
| Harvest Season Bundle ($28) | Moderate | Very high | Moderate | High | June–September |
| Apartment Grower Bundle ($32) (existing) | Low | Moderate | Low | High | Year-round |
| Food Sovereignty Bundle ($30) (existing) | Low | Very high | Moderate | Low | March–May |

### Cohort-Specific Bundle Positioning

**Forager cohort**: Respond to specificity and seasonal timing. The bundle framing that works: "Everything you need for spring foraging season." Does not respond to broad homesteading bundles. Bundle the foraging guide with seasonal or regional guides, not with preservation guides.

**Homesteader cohort**: The largest segment and the best bundle buyers. They buy systems, not single products. The Harvest Season Bundle and the Food Sovereignty Bundle are both natural fits. Bundle framing that works: "Everything you need for [harvest/seed-saving/preservation] season." They are the primary target for the $28–$32 price range.

**Survival Prepper cohort**: Already bundles by behavior (buys 6+ guides in one order). They respond better to a "Complete Prepper Pack" framing than to seasonal bundles. If Phase 1 data shows preppers as a significant cohort share (targeting 15–20%), a "Prepper Essentials Bundle" combining Survival Garden, Hunting Manual, Meat/Fish Preservation, and Harvest Preservation at $45–50 (individual total ~$62) should be tested in Month 4.

**Gift Buyer cohort**: Peak season is November–December (holiday) and May (Mother's Day, graduation). The Apartment Grower Bundle and the Harvest Season Bundle are both gift-viable. Consider creating a limited "Gift Version" listing for the holidays — same content, framing adjusted to "a complete gift for the gardener in your life." No new product needed; just a listing variant with holiday-friendly title and description.
