## Site Selection Criteria

Three non-negotiable criteria govern site selection for this shoot:

**1. Commercial photography permission**: Any photograph used in a product sold on Etsy is commercial photography. This triggers permit or written-permission requirements at every public and private land. Sites that require 14+ days advance notice are closed for this timeline: NC State Parks (14-day deadline: May 6 — passed) and National Forest lands (60-day deadline: March 21 — passed). The viable sites are those with 7-day permit windows or private landowner arrangements.

**2. Species-appropriate habitat**: The guides teach readers to identify these plants in the wild. Photographs shot in a naturalistic Appalachian woodland context — leaf litter, canopy light, dark forest floor, root-visible soil — are more instructive than table-top compositions. A garden or farm setting is acceptable; a concrete patio or generic backdrop is not.

**3. Logistical feasibility for a 1–2 person crew**: The crew must be able to drive to the location, set up and shoot within a 4–6 hour window, and leave with backed-up images. Parking, cell coverage (or offline maps), restroom access, and shelter from unexpected rain are all evaluated.

### Why Asheville Botanical Garden

The Garden is the only Appalachian location meeting all three criteria with a permit window short enough to work within the May 9–30 timeline. Key advantages:

- **Species density**: 600+ Appalachian native plant species across 10 acres of naturalistic woodland, meadow, and streamside habitat. Black Cohosh, Ramps, and Goldenseal are documented Appalachian understory species present in collections of this type. American Ginseng may appear in demonstration plots or shaded garden sections. Even where these exact species are not visible in situ, the woodland floor backdrop quality is what the guide photographs require — the cultivated specimens in the photographer's prop bag are photographed against the naturalistic background.
- **Permit window**: 7-day advance notice — the only major Appalachian public botanical site with a permit window short enough for the current timeline.
- **Logistics**: Free adjacent parking; visitor restrooms on-site; 5-minute drive from Asheville downtown; full cell coverage throughout. No hike required.
- **Weather safety net**: Covered walkways provide shelter during unexpected rain; downtown Asheville is 5 minutes away, enabling a rapid retreat to the indoor backup location without significant logistics disruption.

### Seasonal Optimal Timing

May is peak Appalachian woodland photography timing. Canopy is fully leafed out but not yet blocking all light. Ground-level light is filtered, dappled, and directional — superior for plant texture photography compared to midsummer's heavy shade.

Species-specific conditions in late May:

| Species | Foliage State | Bloom Status | Best Photography Focus |
|---|---|---|---|
| American Ginseng | Compound leaves fully emerged; bright green; 5-leaflet arrangement visible | No bloom (August–September) | Full compound leaf arrangement; stem emergence; root collar at soil line |
| Goldenseal | Full foliage; paired, large deeply-veined leaves | Bloom typically past by late May (April–May) | Distinctive large paired leaves; leaf vein and margin texture; colony spreading on forest floor |
| Black Cohosh | Compound palmate leaves fully developed; toothed margins visible | No bloom (June–August) | Full leaf height (3–8 ft mature); toothed compound leaf margin (key ID feature); stem architecture |
| Ramps | Leaves still present but beginning to senesce in some populations; May 20 is near the end of the visible leaf window | Bloom past (March–April); flowers appear after leaves senesce | Broad strap leaves in colony; leaf texture; bulb base visible at soil line; act quickly — leaves may be fading by May 22 |

**Ramps timing note**: The May 20–22 window is the trailing edge of ramp leaf season in Appalachian NC. Shoot ramp leaf shots first on Day 1 (May 20) before other species. Leaves in protected north-facing slopes at garden elevation (2,134 ft) will be in better condition than lower-elevation populations. Supplement with fresh-purchased leaves (prop bag) if garden specimens are senescent.

### Photo Opportunities

**Understory habitat backdrop (north-facing paths)**: Mature mixed hardwood canopy with filtered light throughout the day; leaf-litter floor with natural color variation (brown oak, tan beech, dark sycamore); exposed tree roots as natural prop elements. Best for: full-plant-in-habitat, colony views, habitat context wide shots.

**Streamside habitat**: Small seasonal stream on garden property; moist soil and exposed root banks; ramps typically grow in stream-adjacent moist woodland (this is species-accurate backdrop for ramps habitat shots). Best for: ramps colony and habitat context; also suitable for ginseng in-situ shots (ginseng also prefers moist north-facing slopes).

**Sun angle guidance**: East-facing paths receive soft morning light 9am–11am (directional, not harsh). North-facing understory is in open shade all day — the preferred condition for botanical photography (eliminates harsh shadows on leaf texture and root color). Arrival by 8am allows the crew to walk the site before the 9am shoot window and identify the 3–4 best specific positions.

**Accessibility**: Flat to gently sloping pathways throughout; free adjacent parking (no reservation required); no trail difficulty for standard equipment load (camera bag, foam-core board, specimen case).

## Backup Location 1: Private Appalachian Forest Farm

**Access method**: Via United Plant Savers (UpS) regional member referral
**Geographic range**: Western NC, SW Virginia, Eastern TN, Southern WV — all within 30–90 minutes of Asheville
**Permit requirement**: Written landowner permission only (no public land permit process); written email from landowner is sufficient
**Cost**: $0–$100 honorarium or copy of the finished guides as a reciprocal gift

### Why This Is Photographically Superior

A working Appalachian forest farm provides what no botanical garden can replicate: the actual cultivation system in context. Ginseng and goldenseal growing under a shade canopy, with companion plantings, slope-appropriate soil visible, and natural forest floor intact. If the landowner is willing to participate in shots, in-frame hands-on cultivation photographs (demonstrating seed stratification, harvest protocols, shade structure) are the highest-value guide imagery and impossible to source from any other provider.

The cultivation story — forest farming as a conservation tool — is one of the three central arguments of the guide series (see "Cultivation Solution" framework in `endangered-species-candidate-list.md`). Photographs from a real forest farm make that argument visible in a way that a garden backdrop cannot.

### How to Contact

**United Plant Savers**: Email info@unitedplantsavers.org by May 10 with subject line "Botanical Photography Request — Appalachian Forest Farm — May 20–22." State: Seedwarden is a botanical education publisher producing digital plant guides; seeking a 1-day visit to photograph cultivated ginseng, goldenseal, black cohosh, or ramps in their growing environment; willing to share the finished guides and a $50–100 honorarium for the landowner's time.

**Western Carolina Botanical Club**: wncbotanicalclub.org — contact members who are active native plant growers or naturalists with private woodland access in western NC. Introduce the project and ask for referrals; a club newsletter mention reaches the right audience quickly.

**UpS outreach deadline**: Begin May 10; allow 10 days to identify candidates, exchange messages, and confirm date and access. Written permission must be confirmed by May 14.

### Logistics

- Driving distance from Asheville: 30–90 minutes depending on site location; confirm address before departure
- Cell coverage: variable in rural Appalachian areas — download offline maps (Maps.me or Google offline) before departure; do not rely on navigation cell service in remote forest areas
- Road conditions: confirm with landowner whether access road is passable after rain; gravel roads in the Appalachians can become impassable in sustained rain — verify with landowner on May 19 if forecast shows prior-day rain
- Parking: confirm with landowner before visit that vehicle access is available to the planting area

### Permission Documentation

The written permission must explicitly state:
- "Commercial photography for a digital educational product is authorized on these premises"
- Landowner name, farm/property name or address
- Date range covered (May 20–22, 2026)
- Signature or confirming email

A confirming email from the landowner is legally sufficient. Print a copy and carry it to the shoot.

## Backup Location 2: Indoor Window Studio (Weather Contingency)

**Location**: Any home or apartment with a large east- or south-facing window
**Permit**: None
**Cost**: $0–25 (foam-core reflector board if not already acquired)
**Always available**: This location is never at risk of being unavailable

### When to Activate

Activate the indoor mode if the May 17 forecast for Asheville shows 3 or more consecutive rain days covering the May 20–22 window. Do not wait until May 20 morning — the prop setup and equipment configuration differ between field and indoor shoots, and preparation time is required. A May 17 decision enables a smooth pivot.

### What Indoor Can and Cannot Provide

**Can provide (photographic quality equal or superior to field for these shot types)**:
- Root and rhizome close-ups (controlled lighting eliminates harsh shadows on root texture)
- Seed and seed-packet identification shots
- Dried specimen arrangements on dark board
- Botanical illustration props in-frame
- Single-leaf detail against a controlled dark or white background
- Any flat-lay composition

**Cannot provide without supplemental archival sources**:
- Full plant in naturalistic habitat context (requires iNaturalist CC-BY archive)
- Colony or patch views
- Root-in-soil in-situ shots
- Any shot requiring woodland background

**Consequence**: All guide interior photography is fully produceable with indoor + iNaturalist CC-BY archive. A field shoot produces higher-quality habitat context images that make the guides more visually compelling — it is a quality upgrade, not a functional dependency. The guides can launch on May 30 in either scenario.

### Indoor Setup

- East- or south-facing window with 3+ hours of unobstructed morning light
- White foam-core board (24×36 in) opposite the window — fill reflector: $8–12 at any craft store
- Dark wood board or black velvet cloth as surface for root and seed close-ups
- Camera or smartphone with white balance locked at 5500K (Daylight)
- All potted plants and specimens positioned 18–24 inches from window; not in direct midday sun

## Weather Contingency Decision Tree

| Decision Point | Date | Trigger | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Forecast check | May 17 | 5-day forecast for Asheville / Western NC | If 3+ consecutive rain days May 20–22: activate indoor-only mode; update all shoot prep to Cluster 2 configuration |
| Day-before check | May 19 | 48-hour hourly forecast | If morning (9am–12pm) May 20 is clear: proceed with Cluster 1 regardless of afternoon forecast |
| Day-of check | May 20, 6:00am | Hourly forecast for shoot window | If clear at 6am: depart for Garden. If active rain at 6am: execute Cluster 2 indoor instead; Cluster 1 shifts to May 22 |
| On-site decision | May 20, 8:30am | Weather at location | If rain begins during shoot: shelter under covered walkway; if sustained (>30 min) and no clearing forecast: pack out; execute Cluster 2 at accommodation |
| Day 2 check | May 21, 6:00am | Check for makeup field opportunity | If May 21 is clear and Day 1 was rained out: execute Cluster 1 on May 21; compress schedule |

**Fallback shoot dates**: May 21 and May 22 absorb one field-day postponement each without launch date impact. If all three field days are rained out, execute indoor-only shoot (Cluster 2) and source all habitat photography from iNaturalist CC-BY archive. May 30 launch is fully achievable in this worst-case scenario.
