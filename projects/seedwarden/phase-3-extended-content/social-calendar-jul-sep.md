### Post 33 — Educational: Storage and Shelf Life by Herb Form (Instagram)

**Date**: August 6, 8am ET
**Platform**: Instagram
**Content type**: Educational (preparation/preservation)
**Photo**: No contractor photo needed for this text-first format

**Copy-paste text (Instagram)**:

Your herbs have an expiration date. Here's what it actually is.

Dried leaves and flowers:
- 1–2 years if stored cool, dark, airtight
- Signs of aging: color fades from green/bright to brown/dull; smell weakens

Dried roots and barks:
- 2–3 years (denser material, slower degradation)
- Exception: valerian root IMPROVES with age (valeronic acid concentration increases)

Tinctures (alcohol extract):
- 3–5 years if 50%+ alcohol content; keeps indefinitely if higher
- Never refrigerate — keep at room temperature, dark

Infused oils (herb in carrier oil):
- 6–12 months; refrigerate
- Check for rancidity (smell the oil — not the herb)

Herbal honeys:
- Indefinite if properly sealed; honey is antimicrobial
- Garlic honey: 1 year

Tea blends (dried + pre-mixed):
- 1 year; individual herb shelf life applies to each component

Save this. Every harvest is only as good as your storage.

### Post 36 — Educational: Adaptogens vs. Nervines vs. Nootropics (LinkedIn)

**Date**: August 11, 9am ET
**Platform**: LinkedIn
**Content type**: Educational (plant science — categorization)

**Copy-paste text (LinkedIn)**:

Three categories of herb that wellness culture has blurred into one. They're not the same.

**Adaptogens**: Normalize stress response over time. Require consistent daily use (weeks to months) to show benefit. Examples: ashwagandha, astragalus, rhodiola, eleuthero. Mechanism: modulation of HPA axis and cortisol rhythm. Not appropriate for acute use — they work slowly.

**Nervines**: Tonify or calm the nervous system. Can work acutely. Subdivided: tonic nervines (rebuilding — oats milky, skullcap) vs. relaxant nervines (immediate calming — valerian, passionflower, California poppy). Use when acute support is needed.

**Nootropics**: Cognitive enhancement and neuroprotection. Traditional examples: lion's mane mushroom, bacopa monnieri, ginkgo biloba. Emerging research category with legitimate evidence and significant noise. Use with higher skepticism and more insistence on primary sources.

These categories interact: some herbs span categories (lemon balm is a nervine with mild adaptogenic effects at higher dose). The categories tell you *when* to use something and *what* outcome to expect, not just which genus to buy.

This is why Seedwarden's Immunity and Sleep bundles include the clinical mechanism alongside the cultivation instructions. Growing it and using it correctly are not the same skill.

### Post 43 — Educational: Elderberry — Cultivation vs. Wild (LinkedIn)

**Date**: August 20, 9am ET
**Platform**: LinkedIn
**Content type**: Educational (cultivation + ethics)
**Photo**: Use `elderberry-habit.jpg` (H. Zell CC BY-SA 3.0, Sambucus nigra habit)

**Copy-paste text (LinkedIn)**:

Why growing your own elderberry is better than harvesting wild.

Sambucus nigra and S. canadensis are both available as cultivated varieties. The named varieties (Adams, Nova, York, Bob Gordon) have been selected for:
- Larger berry clusters (more surface area = more efficient harvest)
- Higher antioxidant content per berry (selective breeding works for medicine plants too)
- Disease resistance specific to your region
- More predictable harvest timing

Wild elderberry is available. Wild harvesting is legal on your own land and on some public lands. But:

1. You cannot control the harvest timing precisely enough to hit peak anthocyanin content in an unmanaged shrub.
2. Roadside wild elderberry concentrates heavy metals from vehicle exhaust.
3. Wild shrubs are productive in some years and not in others, depending on water access and competition.
4. In most suburban and peri-urban settings, "wild" elder is growing in disturbed sites with soil contamination risk.

Cultivate. Get the named variety that works in your zone. Manage the harvest. Know exactly what you harvested and when.

### Post 45 — Educational: Fermentation as Preservation (Instagram)

**Date**: August 22, 8am ET
**Platform**: Instagram
**Content type**: Educational (preparation — fermentation)
**Photo**: No contractor photo needed

**Copy-paste text (Instagram)**:

Fermentation is an herb preservation method most guides skip.

Elderberry shrub (not the berry soda — the vinegar shrub): Raw apple cider vinegar + elderberries + honey. Ferment 4–6 weeks. Shelf stable. No alcohol. Bioavailability potentially higher than syrup because fermentation pre-digests some sugars.

Fire cider: Horseradish, ginger, garlic, turmeric, pepper, apple cider vinegar. Ferments as it infuses. Traditionally vinegar-extracted; the fermentation adds additional probiotic benefit.

Lacto-fermented herb paste: Herb (chive, dill, parsley, or medicinal herbs) + 2% salt. Ferments in 3–5 days. Refrigerate after. Shelf life 3–6 months.

When to grow vs. when to buy. The honest calculation.

**Always worth growing**:

### Post 60 — Educational: The Herb-Drug Interaction Tier (LinkedIn + Instagram)

**Date**: September 12, 8am ET Instagram / 9am ET LinkedIn
**Platform**: LinkedIn + Instagram
**Content type**: Educational (safety — herb-drug interactions)

**Copy-paste text (LinkedIn)**:

Herb-drug interactions are real. Here are the categories that matter most — and why most guides undersell them.

**Category 1: Herb inhibits or induces liver enzymes (CYP system)**:
Herbs that inhibit CYP3A4 (goldenseal, grapefruit, black pepper in large doses) can raise blood levels of any drug metabolized by that enzyme — including immunosuppressants, statins, and many psychiatric medications. Significant drug levels + CYP inhibition = drug toxicity risk.

**Category 2: Herb affects blood clotting**:
Garlic, ginkgo, fish oil, and vitamin E are additive anticoagulants. In patients on warfarin, aspirin, or newer anticoagulants (rivaroxaban, apixaban): unexpected bleeding risk.

**Category 3: Herb duplicates drug mechanism**:
Valerian + benzodiazepines: additive CNS depression. St. John's Wort + SSRIs: serotonin syndrome risk. These are potentiation events, not nutrient depletion events.

**Category 4: Herb depletes or interferes with absorption**:
Slippery elm + any oral drug: mucosal barrier delay. Take drugs 2+ hours separately. Calcium-rich herbs (nettle, oatstraw) + iron supplements: decreased iron absorption.

Every Seedwarden bundle includes drug interaction sections for each herb, sorted by category. Not "consult your doctor" (though you should), but the specific interaction mechanism so you understand what question to ask.
