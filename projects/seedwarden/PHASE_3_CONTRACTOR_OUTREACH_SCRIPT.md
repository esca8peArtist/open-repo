## Section 1 — Outreach Message Templates

### Template 1A — Instagram DM (Cold — Photographer Found via Hashtag Search)

Use this when you find a photographer on Instagram via hashtag search. Keep it short — DMs above 4–5 lines often go unread. The goal is to get a reply, not to explain the full project.

---

Hi [NAME] — I love your flat-lay work, especially [MENTION A SPECIFIC POST OR DETAIL]. I am working on a botanical herb guide series (medicinal herbs for Etsy) and looking for a product photographer for 3 half-day sessions in July. Commercial use license required, budget $150–300 per session. Would this be something you would want to hear more about?

---

**Why this structure works**: Specific compliment signals you are not mass-blasting. Short ask reduces friction. Budget stated upfront filters non-matches before they invest time.

**Variation for accounts with less flat-lay content but relevant style**:

---

Hi [NAME] — your [STYLE ELEMENT, e.g. "use of natural light" or "prop styling"] catches my eye. I am shooting product flat-lay for a medicinal herb guide series — 3 half-day sessions in July, $150–300 per session, commercial use. Do you take product work?

## Section 3 — Pre-Vetting Questions

Ask these questions at the end of the first message exchange (after a photographer has replied with interest) — before scheduling a discovery call. These filter out mismatches in under 2 minutes.

Do not ask all four at once. Embed them in a natural reply.

**Q1 (Availability)**: "Are you available for a half-day shoot session anytime July 1–5?"
- Target answer: Yes, confirmed date. Anything with excessive hedging ("probably," "I'll have to check with my other clients and get back to you") scores low on the availability criterion.
- If they say July 1–5 is not available: ask about July 8–12 as an alternative for Session 1. Do not accept a photographer who cannot confirm any July window.

**Q2 (Product experience)**: "Do you have any flat-lay product photography samples I can look at — even just 2–3 examples?"
- Target answer: Yes, with a link. Photographers who have done product work will have this ready. If they send portrait or event work and describe it as product, that is a mismatch signal.
- Acceptable variation: "I have not shot medicinal herbs before but here are some kitchen/food flat-lays from last year." Kitchen and food flat-lay experience transfers directly.

**Q3 (Equipment)**: "Do you have a tripod and can you shoot overhead?"
- Target answer: Yes, I have a tripod; I have shot overhead flat-lay before. A tripod is not optional for overhead flat-lay — a handheld overhead shot at 2000px cannot hold enough stability. If a photographer does not own a tripod, they cannot do this job.

**Q4 (License)**: "Does your standard work-for-hire include commercial use rights for digital distribution?"
- Target answer: Yes, standard contract includes commercial rights. Any hedging ("I'll have to think about that" or "I usually keep rights for six months") requires clarification before proceeding. The Etsy listing is a commercial digital product — the commercial license is not negotiable.

## Section 4 — Screening Rubric (Rapid Go/No-Go)

Use this rubric after the pre-vetting questions to make a rapid yes/no decision on whether to spend 20 minutes in a discovery call.

| Signal | Go | No-Go |
|---|---|---|
| Portfolio has flat-lay or product shots | Yes — any flat-lay | Only portraits/events/landscapes |
| Availability confirmed for July | Specific date named | Hedging or no July availability |
| Overhead flat-lay capability confirmed | Tripod confirmed | No tripod; never done overhead shots |
| Commercial license | Confirmed or standard | Refuses or requires separate negotiation |
| Budget alignment | Within $150–$350/session | Quotes $400+ per session on first contact |
| Response time | Replies within 24 hours | Takes 3+ days on first reply (reliability signal) |

**Decision rule**: Any single hard No-Go (portfolio, availability, or commercial license) disqualifies regardless of other signals. Two or more soft No-Go signals (budget slightly high, slightly slow response) warrant a polite decline and moving to the next candidate.

## Section 5 — Discovery Call Guide (20 Minutes)

Run this call after a candidate passes pre-vetting. The goal is not to sell them on the project — they are already interested. The goal is to confirm style alignment, confirm technical workflow, and get a verbal rate commitment before you send the bid spec.

**Opening (2 minutes)**

Introduce the project briefly: "Seedwarden is a digital guide series on medicinal herbs. We sell on Etsy. I need product flat-lay and lifestyle photography for three guide bundles in July — each is a half-day shoot with 5 edited deliverables. You will be photographing dried and fresh herb specimens, a printed guide mockup, and some simple props."

**Questions to ask (12 minutes)**

1. "Walk me through how you typically handle a product flat-lay session — from setup to delivery."
Target: They describe a consistent process (light setup, test shots, hero shots, edit workflow). A photographer with no consistent process description is less reliable than one who says "I always start with a test frame to check white balance before shooting the full set."

2. "What surface and light would you use for this kind of shoot?"
Target: Natural fiber surface (linen, canvas, wood) and natural window light or soft-box. Reject: photographer insists on a dark, high-contrast dramatic background (style mismatch); photographer says they only work with on-camera flash (hard light, incompatible with brand).

3. "If I send you 5 herb specimens and 3 props, roughly how long does it take you to set up and shoot 5 images?"
Target: 2–3 hours comfortable answer. If they say 1 hour or less, probe whether they rush edits. If they say 5+ hours, the half-day session rate does not pencil out for them.

4. "What is your standard edit turnaround?"
Target: 2–3 business days. 5 business days is acceptable but slower than preferred. More than 7 days is incompatible with the sprint delivery calendar.

5. "Have you shot for any Etsy sellers? What was that experience like?"
This is not a disqualifying question — it is a reliability and communication style signal. A photographer who has worked with Etsy sellers before understands the 5-slot listing format. A photographer who has not can still do the work, but needs slightly more briefing.

**Close (6 minutes)**

"Thanks — this sounds like a strong match. My next step is to send you the full technical spec sheet and a sample shot list for the first bundle. If you can send me a bid against the spec within 3 days, I plan to finalize the booking by June 17. Any questions before I send that over?"

Take note of any questions they ask — good candidates ask about image use, the guide content, and the plant sourcing. Candidates who ask only about payment timing are a mild flag.
