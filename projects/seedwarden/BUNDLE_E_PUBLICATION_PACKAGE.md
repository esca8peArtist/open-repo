Here's the problem: A dozen lookalike plants exist. Most foragers confuse Knotweed 
with native species.

JAPANESE KNOTWEED IDENTIFICATION (Step 1-5)

**Step 1 — The Stem Test** (most reliable)
Look at the stem. Knotweed stems are:
• Hollow (unlike most lookalikes, which are solid)
• Segmented with reddish swollen nodes every 6-8 inches
• Smooth, not fuzzy
• Bamboo-like appearance

If you cut the stem and it's solid, it's not Knotweed.

**Step 2 — The Leaf Test** (secondary confirmation)
Knotweed leaves are:
• Broadly heart-shaped
• Large (3-6 inches long) on young growth
• Arranged alternately
• Hairless and slightly waxy

**Step 3 — The Habit Test** (context)
Knotweed grows in:
• Dense stands (6-8 feet tall, monoculture thickets)
• Disturbed areas: stream banks, field edges, roadsides
• Full sun to partial shade
• Usually multiple plants touching each other

If you see only one or two plants isolated, it's probably not Knotweed.

**Step 4 — The Smell Test** (optional)
Crush a leaf. Knotweed smells vaguely sour, mineral-like. Not unpleasant, but distinctive.

**Step 5 — The Geographic Test** (reality check)
Japanese Knotweed is established in:
• Northeast US: Connecticut to Maine (dense populations)
• Mid-Atlantic: Pennsylvania, New York, New Jersey (widespread)
• Pacific Northwest: Washington, Oregon (spreading)
• Great Lakes: Michigan, Wisconsin, Minnesota (moderate)

Less established in: Southeast, South-Central, Southwest.

HARVEST WINDOW:
Late April - mid-June (early shoots, 12-18 inches tall, tender texture)
