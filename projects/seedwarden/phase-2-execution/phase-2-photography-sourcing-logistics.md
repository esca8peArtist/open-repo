# Phase 2 Photography & Plant Sourcing Logistics

**Purpose**: This document resolves the last concrete gap in Phase 2 execution: how to get physical plant specimens and production-quality photographs in hand before the May 30 launch. Everything else — product strategy, Canva templates, email automation, buyer lifecycle campaigns, Etsy listing structure — is built and documented. Photography and plant sourcing are the only open logistics items. This document closes them.

**Scope**: Wave 1 Appalachian Medicinals series — six species. Guide priority order for May 30: Ginseng, Goldenseal, Black Cohosh, Ramps (primary four), Bloodroot and Wild Bergamot (supporting pair). All six ship in the launch window; the primary four carry the initial Etsy listing momentum.

**Operating constraint**: Today is May 7, 2026. The May 30 launch is 23 days away. Every decision in this document is made with that constraint in the foreground.

## Section 1: Endangered Species Plant Sourcing

### 1.1 Wave 1 Species — Guide Sequence and Rationale

The six Wave 1 species ship in two sub-clusters. The primary four anchor the "Appalachian Medicinals: At Risk, Worth Growing" bundle. The supporting pair appear in individual single-species guides and as upsell listings cross-linked from the bundle.

**Primary Four (Etsy listings go live May 30):**

1. **American Ginseng** (*Panax quinquefolius*) — Anchor species. Most searched, highest conservation recognition, strongest story arc. CITES Appendix II; cultivated seeds are freely available and legal. This guide leads the series and all marketing copy. Photography priority: highest.

2. **Goldenseal** (*Hydrastis canadensis*) — Second anchor. CITES Appendix II, state-endangered in seven states. The "yellow rhizome" ID feature makes for visually distinctive photos. Market searches for "goldenseal identification" are well above baseline — strong SEO pull. Photography priority: high.

3. **Black Cohosh** (*Actaea racemosa*) — Third. The white wand-like flower raceme is the most photogenic plant in the set. June–August bloom means if any iNaturalist CC-BY contributor photographed it in the last two summers, the archive is available now. Photography priority: high, and most likely to be sourced entirely from iNaturalist.

4. **Ramps / Wild Leek** (*Allium tricoccum*) — Fourth. Strongest cultural resonance (Appalachian foodways, Cherokee *wasdi* practice, restaurant overharvest controversy). Spring emergence season has passed for 2026, but iNaturalist archive photos from prior years are fully adequate — species appearance does not change year to year. Photography priority: high; all from archive.

**Supporting Pair (Etsy listings go live May 30; lower initial marketing weight):**

5. **Bloodroot** (*Sanguinaria canadensis*) — Spring ephemeral. UpS At-Risk, Exploitably Vulnerable in New York. Striking white blooms make high-quality iNaturalist archive material readily available. Photography priority: moderate; likely sourced entirely from archive.

6. **Wild Bergamot** (*Monarda fistulosa*) — The accessible bridge species. Not acutely endangered at the national level, but conservation-adjacent story (pollinator specialist, habitat-dependent). Most widely available from any seed supplier; easiest to photograph. Photography priority: lowest; source last.

## Section 2: Photography Location Scouting

### 2.1 Appalachian Field Locations

For ginseng and goldenseal in particular, the most compelling guide photography shows the plant in authentic forest habitat: dappled light through a hardwood canopy, leaf litter floor, the plant emerging in context. This is the "hero" image for each guide cover and the primary listing image on Etsy.

**Primary field location option: Local deciduous forest, eastern US**

If you are located in or within driving distance of Appalachian or eastern hardwood forest — any location from western Pennsylvania south through Virginia, West Virginia, Tennessee, or North Carolina — you can photograph habitat context directly. What you need:
- A mature hardwood forest tract with significant canopy cover (70%+)
- Filtered light conditions (overcast day or morning hours before direct sun hits the forest floor)
- No permits required for photography on most public hiking trails in national forests (Pisgah, Cherokee, Monongahela, Jefferson) unless commercial use is explicitly restricted. Check the specific national forest's photography policy: most national forests allow personal and educational photography without a Special Use Permit; commercial photography for product sales is the gray area. Use iNaturalist photos for the final guide if permit requirements are unclear rather than risk a licensing issue.

**Best public land options with documented ginseng/goldenseal populations (general region):**
- Monongahela National Forest (West Virginia) — documented Appalachian medicinal plant habitat; accessible trailheads throughout the forest
- Great Smoky Mountains National Park — photography for educational purposes is generally permitted; commercial use restrictions apply to the NPS, so confirm before using NPS-location photos in a for-sale guide
- Any state forest or wildlife management area in WV, KY, VA, or TN — state land generally has more permissive photography policies than NPS

**Practical note on ginseng location privacy**: Wild ginseng populations are deliberately kept off public maps because poaching pressure is documented and severe. If a forest farm contact shares a habitat location for photography, do not include location-identifying information in the published guide. Photo only; no GPS coordinates, no named hollows, no identifiable landmarks. This is both an ethical obligation and a courtesy to the source relationship.

**Forest farm contacts for photography access:**
- United Plant Savers Botanical Sanctuary (Rutland, Ohio): upsanctuary@unitedplantsavers.org — they maintain a working ginseng and goldenseal population and have previously supported educational photography requests
- American Ginseng Group member farms: americanginsenggroup.com/member-farms — contact 2–3 member farms directly; explain educational guide purpose; offer to credit the farm in the guide
- Target contact date: May 8–9. If a farm responds favorably, coordinate a visit for May 15–17.

### 2.2 Controlled / Studio Setup

If field access is not confirmed by May 12, shift entirely to the studio approach. This is the higher-probability path given the 23-day window, and it produces photos that are fully sufficient for the guide and Etsy listing quality standard.

**Studio setup for tabletop botanical photography:**

The goal is a "naturalistic studio" aesthetic — not a clinical white background, not a full wilderness scene. The visual language is: plant specimen on a surface that evokes forest floor, with natural or simulated natural light.

Background surfaces (choose one per species):
- Dark forest-green linen or canvas cloth — the closest approximation to dappled forest floor light when paired with window light from above-left
- Weathered cedar plank or birch bark sheet — available at craft stores or lumber yards; communicates "woodland" without requiring outdoor location
- Actual forest floor substrate: gather a tray of damp leaf litter, moss, and soil from any nearby wooded area. This is the highest-authenticity option for studio photography and costs nothing

**Species-specific studio context:**
- Ginseng: Arrange roots and seeds on forest floor substrate. Single stem with characteristic five-leaflet palmately compound leaves in foreground. Berries (red) if available from any source.
- Goldenseal: Cut rhizome cross-section showing yellow interior beside intact rhizome pieces. Leaves arranged flat on dark linen behind. The yellow interior is the ID feature — make sure it's lit and in focus.
- Black Cohosh: Tall (4–6 foot) habit shots require a live plant or a plant from a botanical garden; for studio work, use the leaf detail and seed follicle shots, sourcing habit shots from iNaturalist archive.
- Ramps: Bulbs in soil context, leaves flat. A single ramp "colony" cluster (5–7 bulbs planted together) staged in a dark tray.
- Bloodroot: Spring ephemeral; rhizome showing orange-red sap cross-section is the ID-critical photo. White flower shots from iNaturalist archive.
- Wild Bergamot: Dried flower heads photograph well and are available year-round; fresh flowers from any garden center that stocks native perennials.

### 2.3 Props List

Acquire before May 12. Most items are on hand or available at a hardware store and nursery in a single run.

**Substrate and surface props:**
- Dark forest-green linen or canvas cloth (12" x 18" minimum), $8–14 at a fabric store
- One weathered cedar plank or birch bark section (craft store or lumber yard; $5–12)
- One shallow tray, 12" x 16", to hold forest floor substrate if gathered outdoors
- Small ceramic or terracotta pots (4-inch), 3–4 units — $4–8 total at a garden center

**Scale reference props:**
- US coin (quarter) and a metric ruler for scale shots — required for any ID-critical root or rhizome photo
- Small brown kraft seed envelopes (10-pack, $3–6 at a craft store)

**Lighting equipment:**
- **Primary option — natural window light**: A north or east-facing window on an overcast day produces the most flattering diffuse light for botanical photography. This is free and sufficient for all species if the shoot is timed for morning on an overcast day.
- **Secondary option — softbox or ring light**: A single LED softbox (18" or larger, $35–60 on Amazon) mounted above-left gives controllable repeatable light when window conditions are not consistent. If multiple shoot days are needed, the softbox eliminates weather dependency.
- White foam board (24" x 36", $3–5 at a craft or office supply store) — for fill light: position opposite the window to bounce light into shadows and open up the underside of leaves and root detail

**Camera equipment:**
- iPhone 14 or newer (or equivalent Android with a 12MP+ camera) is fully sufficient for all guide photography at 2400×2400px export. Do not delay the shoot while waiting for a camera upgrade.
- Flexible phone tripod or gorillapod ($12–20) — essential for any overhead flat-lay shot and any root/rhizome close-up where camera shake ruins focus
- Alternatively: a stack of books to prop the phone at the correct angle for each shot

### 2.4 Location Confirmation Milestones

- **May 9 (end of day)**: Decision made — field shoot or studio shoot (or both). If field access is being pursued, at least one forest farm or botanical garden contact has been emailed. If studio-only, props list confirmed and acquisition run scheduled.
- **May 12**: Props acquired. If field shoot: location confirmed with permission or access plan. Studio substrate gathered.
- **May 14**: Shoot logistics finalized — date, time, all props staged and confirmed. No outstanding logistics questions.

### 3.2 Three-Cluster Organization

**Cluster 1 — Tier 1 Rare Plants: Ginseng + Goldenseal (high-priority studio setup)**

These two species require the most careful photography because their guide covers will carry the most Etsy listing weight. Allocate the first 2–2.5 hours of the shoot to Cluster 1. Shoot in this sequence:
1. Ginseng root close-up (quarter for scale; dark substrate background)
2. Ginseng leaf detail (compound five-leaflet arrangement; overhead flat-lay)
3. Ginseng berries if available (red cluster; use iNaturalist archive if not)
4. Ginseng habit / full plant (iNaturalist CC-BY archive photo likely needed for this shot type)
5. Goldenseal rhizome (whole piece beside cut cross-section showing yellow interior; ruler for scale)
6. Goldenseal leaf detail (hairy stem, lobed margin; flat-lay on dark linen)
7. Goldenseal flower (April–May bloom; iNaturalist archive; do not attempt to recreate)

Per-species shot target: 8–12 raw captures to select from. Final guide needs 2–3 photos per species minimum (habit, leaf/root detail, one supplementary). Etsy listing cover needs 1 strong hero image per species.

**Cluster 2 — Specialty Plants: Black Cohosh + Ramps**

Setup transition from Cluster 1: same dark linen background; change substrate to a leafy woodland floor context for ramps. Allow 30 minutes for transition and setup.

Shoot sequence:
1. Black cohosh leaf detail (ternate compound leaves, toothed margins; flat-lay)
2. Black cohosh seed follicle (dark dried follicles on bare raceme; if available from any source)
3. Black cohosh habit (tall wand form; iNaturalist archive photo)
4. Ramps bulb in soil context (cluster of 5–7 bulbs, reddish stems visible)
5. Ramps leaf detail (broad lance-shaped leaves; overhead)
6. Ramps colony habitat (wide shot of emerging woodland carpet; iNaturalist CC-BY archive)

Per-species shot target: 6–10 raw captures.

**Cluster 3 — Reference Images: Bloodroot + Wild Bergamot**

These two species are the simplest to photograph. Allow 1–1.5 hours.

1. Bloodroot rhizome — the orange-red sap cross-section is the ID feature; photograph cut surface beside intact rhizome
2. Bloodroot leaf (distinctive lobed leaf; flat-lay)
3. Bloodroot flower (white; iNaturalist archive for the bloom shot)
4. Wild bergamot flower head (dried or fresh; overhead flat-lay with linen background)
5. Wild bergamot leaf (aromatic; simple arrangement)

Per-species shot target: 5–8 raw captures.

### 3.3 Camera and Lighting Setup

**Equipment setup (apply to all three clusters):**

Primary light: Position phone camera to the **right** of the subject. Position window or softbox light source to the **upper-left** of the subject. This creates the standard botanical photography lighting direction: main light from upper-left, shadows falling lower-right, camera capturing from the right side with clean fill from a white foam board bounce placed at lower-right.

**For root and rhizome close-ups (Cluster 1 priority):**
- Use portrait mode at a working distance of 8–12 inches
- The cut surface of a goldenseal rhizome requires macro-level focus; if portrait mode at 8 inches is not sharp enough, switch to standard photo mode and move 4–6 inches closer
- Depth of field: the entire root or rhizome piece should be in focus; background substrate may blur slightly but should not be unrecognizable
- Shoot 5–8 captures of each root angle, changing focus point and distance slightly between captures; cull during editing

**For flat-lay overhead shots (leaf details, seed arrangements):**
- Position phone directly overhead at 18–24 inches
- Use a rigid prop stack (books, a step stool, or a tripod with an overhead arm) to eliminate camera shake
- White balance: set to "Daylight" (5500K) manually, not "Auto." Auto white balance shifts between frames when photographing different colored substrates
- Shoot in square (1:1) aspect ratio if the phone allows; this eliminates the 1:1 crop step in post-processing

**30-shot sequence structure per species (use as a checklist):**
1. 5 shots: Full plant / habit context (from iNaturalist archive, or live plant from 12–18 inches distance)
2. 8 shots: Primary ID feature close-up (root/rhizome cross-section, OR distinctive leaf, OR flower)
3. 5 shots: Secondary ID feature (stem detail, seed, second leaf view)
4. 5 shots: Scale reference shot (same subject with ruler or coin)
5. 5 shots: Environmental context / substrate arrangement
6. 2 shots: "Working use" framing — seeds in hand, root beside field notes, guide page visible in background

**Editing standard adjustments** (apply as a batch preset to all images from each species cluster before culling):
- Exposure: +0.3
- Contrast: +25
- Highlights: -35 (critical for preventing window blow-out on any light-colored surface)
- Shadows: +25 (opens root and rhizome detail in shadow areas)
- White balance: 5500K (consistent warm daylight)
- Green saturation: +15
- Blue saturation: -10
- Sharpening: +40 (helps root texture read in reduced-size guide layouts)

### 4.4 Review Cycle

Each guide goes through two passes before Etsy upload:

**Pass 1 — Content review (self, same day as draft complete)**
- Is every factual claim about conservation status, range, and cultivation citable to a source already documented in `endangered-species-candidate-list.md`?
- Is the legal status callout box (CITES / ESA / UpS status) accurate to the specific species?
- Does the cultivation calendar reflect the correct stratification requirements and zone range for this species?
- Are all photos attributed with the correct attribution line in the image caption?

**Pass 2 — Format and brand review (following morning)**
- Does the guide feel visually consistent with the master template?
- Is the conservation status callout box using Conservation Red (#8B2000) and not appearing anywhere else in the document?
- Does the cover photo have sufficient contrast over the dark overlay for title text to be legible at thumbnail size (500px wide)?
- Is the guide filename following the convention: `[species-slug]-appalachian-medicinals-guide-v1.pdf`?

No external reviewer required for this phase. If a second pair of eyes is desired, the most efficient path is to post a preview page screenshot in a botanical or forager-focused Discord or Facebook group and ask for a one-line identification accuracy check. This takes 20 minutes and often catches a species name error or range inaccuracy that would otherwise reach buyers.
