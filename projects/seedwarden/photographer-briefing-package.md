## 1. Brand Overview and Visual Identity

### Who Seedwarden Is

Seedwarden is an independent digital publishing brand focused on practical food sovereignty: seed saving, home food preservation, container gardening, and small-scale homesteading skills. Products are sold as digital downloads (PDF guides and reference charts) on Etsy.

The brand's audience is urban homesteaders, apartment food growers, small-property farmers, and food-sovereignty advocates. They are practical rather than aesthetic. They value function over polish. They are skeptical of aspirational homesteading content that requires a 10-acre property and a Pottery Barn kitchen — Seedwarden's content meets them where they actually are: an apartment balcony, a kitchen counter, a shared community garden.

### Brand Tone

Earthy. Honest. Practical. Self-sufficient without being survivalist. Warm without being precious. Think: worn seed envelopes in a jar, not a curated flatlay. Working hands, not manicured ones. A potting bench in actual use, not a staged prop setup.

What Seedwarden is not: aspirational, polished, wealthy-homestead aesthetic. The Pottery Barn farmhouse look — pristine white kitchen, perfect produce arranged on marble — is the wrong reference. The right reference: documentary photography of working food growers. Real soil, real hands, real context.

### Color Palette and Visual Temperature

Seedwarden's brand colors:
- Earthy warm greens (sage, olive, forest green)
- Warm off-whites and creams (not stark white)
- Terracotta and rust tones
- Natural wood grain tones

All photography should feel warm in color temperature — aim for 5500–6000K (daylight-balanced but on the warm side of neutral). Avoid images that pull cool or blue. In editing, slightly reduce blue saturation and lift green and yellow saturation for an earthy, natural look.

### Existing Visual References

Seedwarden's current mockup images (available on request) use the Playfair Display font for product titles and a brand palette that anchors on the warm green and terracotta tones above. Lifestyle images should feel like the natural context in which someone would reach for these product guides — they do not need to match the mockups exactly, but they should feel from the same visual world.

## 2. Project Scope and Deliverables

### What We Are Shooting

**15 digital products** requiring lifestyle photography. These products are PDFs and printable guides — they are not physical products. The lifestyle images show the guides being used in real-world contexts (gardening, seed saving, food preservation), not the guides themselves as physical objects.

The product is represented in each shot as either:
- A tablet or laptop screen displaying the PDF cover in full-screen mode (most shots)
- Printed pages from the PDF laid flat on a surface (flat-lay shots, where the print is the hero)

Neither the tablet nor the printed pages need to be the focus of every image — in contextual shots, the product can be visible in the background or mid-ground while the real-world action (hands with seeds, mason jars on a counter) occupies the foreground.

### What "Done" Means for Each Product

Each product is considered complete when two images are delivered that meet the following criteria:

1. The product (tablet displaying the guide, or printed pages from the guide) is identifiable in at least one of the two images
2. The props in frame are thematically appropriate to the product's subject matter (see Section 4 for per-product guidance)
3. Lighting is soft and even — no harsh directional shadows cutting across the surface or product
4. No competing brand logos, product names, or commercial packaging visible in frame
5. The image passes the brand tone check: earthy, practical, warm — not polished or aspirational

## 3. Equipment and Technical Requirements

### Camera

The images can be captured with any of the following:

**Preferred (if available):**
- Mirrorless or DSLR with a 35mm or 50mm prime lens
- Aperture: f/5.6–f/8 for flat-lay overhead shots (keep full scene in focus); f/1.8–f/2.8 for contextual shots where background blur is appropriate
- ISO: keep below 800 to prevent visible grain in shadow areas
- Shutter: 1/100 or faster for hand-held; tripod preferred for flat-lay shots

**Also appropriate:**
- iPhone 14+ or equivalent Android flagship (Pixel 8, Samsung S24)
- Portrait mode for any shot with background blur
- Grid lines on; HDR off; maximum JPEG quality or ProRAW where available

**Do not use:**
- Lenses wider than 28mm (focal length) for flat-lays — wide-angle distortion makes overhead shots look warped
- Ring light — the circular catch-light in reflective surfaces reads as influencer content, not the earthy Seedwarden aesthetic

### Lighting

**First choice — natural window light:**
- Position the shooting surface 2–4 feet from the largest available window
- Shoot between 9am–2pm when window light is even and not low-angle
- If direct sun creates harsh diagonal shadows: hang a thin white curtain or tape white tissue paper over the lower pane
- Add a white foam board or white poster board on the far side of the subject from the window — this reflects fill light into the shadow side without requiring a second light source

**Second choice — LED softbox:**
- 45W daylight LED softbox at 45 degrees to the subject, placed at roughly subject height or slightly above
- Color temperature: 5500K (daylight balanced)
- One softbox is sufficient; a second as fill is an improvement but not required

**Do not use:**
- Ring light (see above)
- Phone flash or on-camera flash — creates flat, textureless light that eliminates depth
- Overhead room fluorescent lighting alone, without supplemental warm light

### Backgrounds and Surfaces

**Cluster A (Seeds/Garden) — wooden surface:**
- Wooden cutting board (11×15 inch or larger), reclaimed wood plank, or outdoor picnic table
- The wood grain is a feature — it adds texture and warmth that communicates Seedwarden's earthy brand
- Outdoor alternative: potting bench, garden table, garden path stones

**Cluster B (Urban/Container Growing) — window sill or balcony:**
- Genuine apartment context is essential for this cluster — the setting communicates "achievable in your space"
- Window sill with plants on the sill, balcony railing visible behind, or indoor plant shelf
- Urban architecture visible through a window is a positive — it signals "apartment," not "homestead"

**Cluster C (Food Preservation) — kitchen surface:**
- Wooden kitchen table, butcher block counter, or cutting board as embedded surface
- Kitchen context (tile backsplash visible, mason jars present) is strongly preferred

**Do not use for any cluster:**
- White seamless paper backdrop — signals product photography studio, not authentic lifestyle
- Black velvet or dark seamless — too dramatic; reads as jewelry photography
- Polished marble or granite — too aspirational; Pottery Barn aesthetic

## 4. Shot List — All 15 Products

All shots are organized by cluster. Within each cluster, all products share the same setup — same location, same surface, same lighting position. The only variables between products are: the specific product displayed on the tablet, the arrangement of cluster-appropriate props, and the specific action in contextual shots.

**Batch shooting by cluster is strongly recommended.** Set up Cluster A once, shoot all 8 products, break down and set up Cluster B, shoot all 4, then move to Cluster C. This reduces total setup time and maintains visual consistency within each cluster.

## 5. Styling Guidelines — Applied to All Shoots

### Props Protocol

**What to include:**
- Seed envelopes that look hand-labeled or genuinely used — not pristine commercial packaging
- Mason jars with visible contents (dried beans, herbs, a ferment) — empty jars look staged
- Garden tools that show use — a trowel with some soil on it is better than a clean one
- Tablets should display the PDF at maximum brightness; position the screen so it is not washed out by window light

**What to exclude or conceal:**
- Any competing brand names, logos, or product labels visible in frame — turn products label-side away or remove labels entirely
- Commercial seed packets with large brand names facing the camera (Baker Creek, Johnny's, Burpee) — turn face-down or use hand-labeled substitutes
- Branded mugs, water bottles, or any commercial product labeling
- Items that imply wealth or aspirational homesteading: polished copper tools, brand-new matching garden sets, Pottery Barn-style staging

**Hand styling:**
- When hands appear in the frame, they should look working — natural or slightly callused, no nail polish, dry-to-normal skin, mid-task rather than posed
- Use your own hands for contextual shots — one-handed photography while the other hand engages a prop is the standard technique
- Do not hire a hand model; genuine working hands are preferable to styled ones for this brand

### Composition Standards

- **Flat-lay shots (slot 4):** Shoot from directly overhead (90 degrees). Keep the frame square or crop to square. Position the tablet or printed product in the center third or upper center; props arranged around it, not competing with it for center attention.
- **Contextual shots (slot 5):** Use a 45-degree angle from horizontal as the default. This provides depth and conveys "in use" better than an overhead or fully eye-level shot. Background separation (bokeh or slight blur) is welcome for contextual shots where the foreground action is the focus.
- **Product legibility:** In at least one of the two images per product, the guide's title or cover art should be readable (tablet screen or printed page visible and in focus). The image may be lifestyle-first, but the product identity should be discernible.
- **Breathing room:** Leave some space at the edges of the frame — Etsy thumbnails crop to 1:1 and a slight outward crop (not a tight fill) prevents the most important elements from being cut in the thumbnail view.

## 6. Approval Workflow

### Submission and Review Stages

**Stage 1 — Raw selects (within 48 hours of shoot):**
The photographer submits a shared folder with 4–5 selects per product (unedited or lightly edited) for initial client review. This is not the final deliverable — it is a sanity check for composition, product visibility, and props styling before full editing begins.

The client (Seedwarden) reviews all selects and marks each as:
- Approved for editing (proceed as-is)
- Needs adjustment (note the specific issue — lighting, angle, props, product legibility)
- Reshoot required (major issue that cannot be corrected in post-processing)

Turnaround time for Stage 1 client feedback: 24 hours from submission.

**Stage 2 — Edited images (within 5 days of Stage 1 approval):**
The photographer delivers fully edited images at final specification (minimum 3000×3000px, JPEG 90%, sRGB, named by naming convention). Client reviews for final approval.

Final approval criteria:
- Technical specification met (resolution, format, color space, file naming)
- Brand tone correct (warm, earthy, practical — not aspirational or polished)
- Product identifiable in at least one of the two images per product
- No competing brands visible in frame
- Lighting is soft and even — no harsh shadows across the product

**Stage 3 — Revisions (if needed):**
Up to two rounds of minor revisions are included. Minor revisions are: color temperature adjustment, exposure lift, crop change. Major revisions (reshoot because of composition, props, or product legibility issues) are billed at the photographer's day rate.

**Stage 4 — Final delivery:**
Photographer delivers final images plus RAW files (where applicable) via shared cloud folder. Client confirms receipt and provides final sign-off.
