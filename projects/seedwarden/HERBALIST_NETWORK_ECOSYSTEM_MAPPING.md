## Part 1: Practitioner Networks — Organization Directory

### 1.1 American Herbalists Guild (AHG)

The primary credentialing body for non-licensed clinical herbalists in the United States. Founded 1989. The Registered Herbalist (RH) credential requires 1,200+ documented hours of herbal training and clinical practice, peer-reviewed case studies, and demonstrated materia medica competency. The RH is the highest voluntary herbalism credential in the US.

**Membership size**: The AHG does not publicly disclose total membership. The RH directory lists credentialed holders. Cross-referencing conference attendance data and directory size places the active community at 4,000–6,000 RH and General Members; the broader US practitioner community self-identifying as herbalists reaches 15,000–25,000.

### 1.2 National Ayurvedic Medical Association (NAMA)

NAMA is the main US professional body for Ayurvedic practitioners. Founded 1998. Four membership tiers: Ayurvedic Health Counselor (AHC, $170/yr), Ayurvedic Practitioner (AP, $185/yr), Advanced Ayurvedic Practitioner (AAP, $250/yr), Ayurvedic Vaidya (AV, $300/yr). Certification is administered separately by NAMACB.

The NAMA audience responds to cross-system literacy messaging: Western botanical evidence mapped to traditional Ayurvedic action frameworks.

**2026 conference**: Annual NAMA Conference ran May 1–3, 2026 (virtual; completed). Next in-person opportunity: NAMA regional events and member newsletter in July–August. Target: NAMA newsletter placement for Immunity and Digestive bundles in July.

### 1.5 United Plant Savers (UpS)

United Plant Savers is the primary US conservation organization for at-risk native medicinal plants. Founded by Rosemary Gladstar 1994. Membership: 5,000–10,000 active members. The UpS newsletter and member communications reach the most conservation-literate segment of the herbalist community — practitioners who know what Forest Grown Verified means and use it as a sourcing filter.

**Phase 3 relevance**: The conservation angle in Seedwarden's Phase 3 guides (UpS at-risk status for Black Cohosh, Goldenseal, and Echinacea angustifolia; FGV sourcing documentation) directly aligns with UpS values. A mention in the UpS member newsletter positions Seedwarden as a conservation ally, not a product vendor.

**Outreach angle**: "Our Phase 3 medicinal herbs guides include UpS at-risk status, FGV sourcing guidance, and a mandatory CITES Appendix II sidebar for Goldenseal. We are not selling wildcrafted herbs — we are helping practitioners source ethically." This framing is genuine (the guide content supports it) and is the only framing that earns organic mention from UpS.

## Part 5: Community Research — Practitioner Pain Points and Buying Patterns

### 5.1 Practitioner Pain Points

Understanding these pain points is the foundation of effective Phase 3 messaging. Each pain point maps to a specific Seedwarden product feature.

**Pain point 1 — Sourcing ethics uncertainty**
Many practitioners who want to recommend Black Cohosh, Goldenseal, or Echinacea angustifolia to patients do not know how to verify that the material they are sourcing is cultivated rather than wild-harvested. The at-risk species conservation crisis (95%+ of commercial Black Cohosh is wild-harvested) has created genuine uncertainty about whether recommending these herbs is ethical.

Seedwarden product response: Mandatory conservation sidebar in each at-risk species section, FGV certification path documented per species, explicit recommendation to cultivated sources only. This is not a bonus feature — it is the reason a practitioner who already owns David Hoffmann and Matthew Wood would buy an additional guide.

**Pain point 2 — Generic FTC-compliant language that loses clinical specificity**
Practitioners who write patient handouts are caught between making the information useful ("this herb supports respiratory function") and FTC-compliant ("not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease"). Most available content on the market lands one of two places: too vague to be useful, or legally risky to distribute.

Seedwarden product response: Evidence-tier framework (Gold = RCT, Silver = observational, Bronze = traditional use) with specific citations lets practitioners understand exactly what claim weight they are working with. The guide's language is crafted to be clinically accurate and FTC-compliant simultaneously — the work is done for the practitioner rather than left to them.

**Pain point 3 — Preparation method gaps in existing references**
David Hoffmann's Medical Herbalism and Matthew Wood's The Earthwise Herbal are the primary field references. Both are excellent on materia medica depth but light on modern preparation methods, cultivation guidance, and practical sourcing paths. A practitioner who wants to tell a patient how to grow Valerian for root harvest, or how to identify a cultivated Goldenseal from a wildcrafted one in a bulk herb bin, cannot get that information from standard references.

Seedwarden product response: Cultivation sections (USDA zone range, harvest timing, post-harvest processing) and preparation method coverage are embedded in every species section. This is the content gap that PLR and general wellness guides miss entirely.

**Pain point 4 — Drug-herb interaction data scattered across sources**
The most liability-sensitive area for any practitioner recommending herbs is drug-herb interactions. The reliable sources (Natural Medicines database, Commission E monographs, Memorial Sloan Kettering integrative medicine database) require subscriptions and search time. Practitioners who lack access pull from general web sources, which are unreliable.

Seedwarden product response: Contraindication tables with drug-interaction data are standard in every Phase 3 guide. The Ashwagandha thyroid warning, Valerian CNS depressant potentiation, Passionflower MAOI contraindication, and Lemon Balm TSH caution are documented with evidence tier attribution. The guide does not replace the Natural Medicines database; it provides the table a practitioner can hand to a patient at the first consult.

**Pain point 5 — Patient education materials that match practitioner knowledge level**
Most patient-facing herb literature is written for beginners. Practitioners need materials they can give to patients who have been using herbs for years and want clinical-level explanation for why their herbalist is recommending a specific species and preparation.

Seedwarden product response: The practitioner variant (printable 8.5"×11" layout) includes a patient-facing side (preparation method, safety notes) and a practitioner-facing side (evidence tier, contraindications, sourcing ethics). The format is designed for clinical distribution, not just personal reading.

### 6.2 Authority Positioning and Certification Framing

Phase 3 product descriptions must pass a 10-second credibility scan by a practitioner. The elements that pass or fail that scan:

**Pass**: Correct Latin binomial with species authority (e.g., *Actaea racemosa* L., not just "Black Cohosh"). Evidence tier citation in the description (not just in the guide). Named primary sources (Natural Medicines database, Commission E, specific named studies). Explicit contraindication table description. Peer-review confirmation from a named RH.

**Fail**: Health claims ("this bundle supports hormonal balance") without evidence tier qualifier. Generic wellness photography rather than botanical identification imagery. No mention of sources or evidence standards. Price that suggests beginner content ($5–$15 range).

**Pre-launch credibility action (required before June 22)**: One RH (AHG)-level practitioner review of Women's Health bundle — minimum. A written "reviewed for botanical nomenclature and contraindication accuracy" confirmation is sufficient and can be included in the Etsy listing description and in outreach emails. This single action changes conversion rate significantly for subsequent practitioner visitors.

**Social proof sequence**:
- Pre-launch (June 8–21): Practitioner review quote obtained; embedded in Etsy listing
- Launch week (June 22–28): AHG directory cold outreach to 10–20 practitioners with personal note and sample page request for feedback
- Week 3–4: First Etsy reviews from practitioner buyers become the social proof for ND/NP segment outreach

## Part 8: Audience Segmentation Framework and Prioritized Outreach

### 8.1 Audience Segmentation Framework

**Tier 1 — Clinical/Certified Practitioners**
- Who: AHG Registered Herbalists, licensed NDs in scope-of-practice states, clinical NPs in integrative settings
- Practice context: Patient consultations, clinical herbal prescribing, client-facing handouts
- Price sensitivity: Low — $120–$150 is one consultation fee; within annual reference book budget
- Primary channels: AHG directory, AANP state chapter newsletters
- Bundle priority: Women's Health (Black Cohosh clinical depth), Immunity (Goldenseal CITES + FGV), Respiratory (Echinacea two-species comparison)
- Conversion timeline: 2–4 weeks after first meaningful touchpoint
- LTV estimate: High — repeat buyer for each new bundle release; potential wholesale license buyer

**Tier 2 — Educational Practitioners**
- Who: Clinical herbalism school instructors, workshop leaders, CE course developers, community herbalism teachers
- Practice context: Curriculum development, handout preparation, workshop materials
- Price sensitivity: Medium — $120–$150 is reasonable for a 10-copy workshop handout license
- Primary channels: School newsletters, instructor direct outreach, AHG chapter chapters
- Bundle priority: Any bundle matching their specialty; Women's Health and Immunity for Appalachian schools
- Conversion timeline: 3–6 weeks; tied to course/workshop schedule
- LTV estimate: Medium-High — recurring purchase per course season; potential school partnership

**Tier 3 — Enthusiast Practitioners**
- Who: Community herbalists without formal credentials, herbalism students, wildcraft enthusiasts with growing practice
- Practice context: Personal use, informal community teaching, social media content
- Price sensitivity: High — $20–$45 single bundle is the natural entry point; $120–$150 needs explicit "complete library" framing to convert
- Primary channels: Instagram, Reddit r/herbalism, Facebook alumni groups
- Bundle priority: Entry guide at single-bundle price; practitioner tier only with "complete library" framing
- Conversion timeline: Variable — impulse purchases from organic content; longer cycle if discovery from cold outreach
- LTV estimate: Medium — generates review volume (critical for Phase 3 Etsy track record) even when average order value is lower

### 8.2 First-Cohort Priority Order

1. AHG-credentialed RHs in Appalachian and PNW regions — highest quality, fastest conversion, generate first reviews that unlock the broader practitioner channel. Target: 20–30 outreach contacts per bundle specialty from the AHG directory.

2. UpS conservation network and FGV-aware practitioners — Women's Health and Immunity bundles. Conservation framing is a purchase driver for this segment, not just a differentiator.

3. Clinical students via school newsletters and Herbal Academy affiliate — volume at the single-bundle price point generates early Etsy reviews that convert subsequent practitioner visitors.

4. Licensed NDs via AANP state chapters in OR, WA, CA, CO — highest LTV but longest outreach cycle (newsletter lead time 4–6 weeks). Submit by June 22 for August issue placement.
