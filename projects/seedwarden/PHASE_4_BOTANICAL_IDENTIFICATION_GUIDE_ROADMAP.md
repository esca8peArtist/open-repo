# Phase 4 Botanical Identification Guide Roadmap
## 18 North American Native Medicinals — Visual Keys, Habitat, and Harvesting Protocols

**Prepared**: June 1, 2026
**Production window**: July 14 – August 31, 2026
**Wave 1 launch**: August 1, 2026 (9 guides)
**Wave 2 launch**: August 31, 2026 (9 guides)
**Format**: Single-species PDF identification guides (4–6 pages each), compatible with Etsy digital download and ZIM offline repository packaging
**Timeline relationship**: Begins the day after Phase 3 closes (July 13). Phase 3 scope decision (A/B/C) does not affect this roadmap — botanical ID guides are an independent product line.

---

## Strategic Rationale

Phase 3 delivers clinical herb bundles organized by therapeutic theme (women's health, respiratory, sleep, immunity, digestive). Phase 4 botanical identification guides serve a different but adjacent need: the visual and habitat literacy required to find, confirm identity, and responsibly harvest the plants covered in Phase 3 and Phase 4 bundles.

The gap is real. Practitioners who have purchased Phase 3 bundles know the pharmacology but often lack field-level confidence in identifying wild populations. Wildcrafters have field experience but want evidence-backed clinical context. The identification guides bridge these audiences and unlock a third revenue vector: foragers, naturalists, and outdoor educators who do not currently purchase clinical herb bundles.

**Market signal**: Peterson Field Guides (Medicinal Plants and Herbs, Foster and Duke) retail at $27–32 and sell continuously 25 years after publication. The Audubon Society Field Guides and the National Audubon Society Wildflower Guides demonstrate that species-specific botanical identification content has durable demand. AHG curricula include botanical identification as a required competency. No current Etsy seller produces single-species identification guides with habitat keys, geographic range maps (described in text), and harvesting-season protocols in a format that is both field-usable and printable.

**ZIM integration**: The ZIM archive format (used by Kiwix for offline reading) is the standard for open botanical repositories. By structuring guide content with consistent HTML-compatible markdown, Phase 4 guides can be packaged into a downloadable ZIM file for offline use — aligning with open-knowledge principles and opening a distribution channel (OpenZIM, Kiwix library submission) beyond Etsy.

## Guide Format Specification

Each identification guide follows a 5-section format (250–400 words per guide depending on complexity). The format is deliberately leaner than Phase 3 bundles — identification guides are field references, not clinical monographs.

**Section 1 — Identity at a Glance** (40–60 words)
- Common name, accepted Latin binomial with authority citation
- Family
- USDA hardiness zone range
- UpS conservation status (At-Risk / To-Watch / Abundant)
- Key distinguishing feature in one sentence

**Section 2 — Visual Identification Key** (80–120 words)
- Leaf: shape, margin, arrangement, texture, size range
- Stem: round/square/hollow/hairy; color; height range
- Flower: color, count of petals/ray florets, bloom window
- Root: color, texture, distinctive features if medicinal part
- Fruit/seed: if relevant to identification or harvest
- Lookalike warning: one or two species that could be confused; the distinguishing feature that separates them
- Seasonal identification note: what changes between spring/summer/fall that affects identification confidence

**Section 3 — Habitat and Range** (60–80 words)
- Native range in plain language (e.g., "Appalachian Mountains from Pennsylvania to Georgia; naturalized in disturbed ground across northeastern North America")
- Soil preference: drainage, pH, forest vs. open
- Light: full shade / partial shade / full sun tolerance
- Associated plant community: what grows alongside this species in native habitat (practical cue for field ID)
- USDA range map note: direct citation to USDA PLANTS range map URL for that species

**Section 4 — Harvesting Protocol** (60–80 words)
- Part harvested: leaf / root / berry / bark / flower / aerial herb
- Optimal harvest window: month range by region (note that Pacific NW/Southeast lags Northeast by 2–4 weeks)
- Responsible wild-harvest rule: the 1-in-10 rule baseline; modifications for at-risk species (UpS guidance embedded)
- Post-harvest processing: fresh vs. dried, drying temperature, storage container
- Ethical note for UpS At-Risk species: "Do not wild-harvest; source from cultivated supply" — with one sourcing vendor named

**Section 5 — Sources and Field References** (3–5 citations minimum)
- Peterson Field Guide citation for this species (Foster and Duke, 2000 or Newcomb's Wildflower Guide)
- USDA PLANTS database URL for species
- iNaturalist species page URL
- UpS status citation (if applicable)
- One additional regional flora or academic monograph

**Guide 1: Black Cohosh** (*Actaea racemosa* L.)
- **Bundle cross-reference**: Phase 3 Women's Health bundle (primary anchor)
- **UpS status**: At-Risk
- **Habitat**: Rich deciduous forest understory; Appalachian core range
- **Lookalike risk**: HIGH — *Actaea pachypoda* (white baneberry) produces poisonous white berries vs. the dry seed clusters of black cohosh. This warning is the most clinically significant ID error in Appalachian herbalism.
- **Key ID feature**: Tall raceme of white flowers; compound leaves with toothed leaflets; May–July bloom
- **Harvest**: Root (rhizome); October–November after fruiting; cultivated sources only per UpS
- **Research hours estimated**: 2.5 hours (identification detail well-documented; lookalike section requires careful sourcing from regional flora)
- **Photo sourcing**: Wikimedia Commons CC-BY-SA (abundant bloom images June–August); iNaturalist CC-BY
- **Competitor gap**: Peterson Field Guide covers this species but does not provide the UpS conservation framing or cultivated sourcing guidance. AHG Herbal Education Library has monograph-level content but not a standalone identification guide in PDF format.
- **Priority**: 1 (Phase 3 bundle anchor; practitioner audience overlap is highest)

---

**Guide 2: Goldenseal** (*Hydrastis canadensis* L.)
- **Bundle cross-reference**: Phase 3 Immunity bundle (primary anchor)
- **UpS status**: At-Risk; CITES Appendix II
- **Habitat**: Rich mesic hardwood forest; moist slopes; Appalachian and Midwest
- **Lookalike risk**: MODERATE — young plants can be confused with bloodroot (*Sanguinaria canadensis*) before flowering. Distinguishing feature: goldenseal has a distinctive wrinkled palmate leaf; bloodroot leaf is deeply lobed but smooth and glaucous beneath.
- **Key ID feature**: Single raspberry-like red berry cluster; distinctive wrinkled large leaf; small cream-white flower in spring; yellow root (berberine staining)
- **Harvest**: Root; October–November; cultivated sources only — CITES Appendix II wild-harvest restriction noted explicitly
- **Research hours estimated**: 3.0 hours (CITES documentation, FGV sourcing sidebar, two-species comparison)
- **Photo sourcing**: NC Botanical Garden (media contact on file); Missouri Botanical Garden; Wikimedia Commons
- **Competitor gap**: CITES Appendix II status and its practical implications for wild-harvest legality are not covered in any existing Etsy ID guide
- **Priority**: 1 (regulatory complexity is unique selling point)

---

**Guide 3: Echinacea — Two Species** (*Echinacea purpurea* L. Moench; *E. angustifolia* DC.)
- **Bundle cross-reference**: Phase 3 Respiratory and Immunity bundles
- **UpS status**: *E. angustifolia* At-Risk; *E. purpurea* common
- **Habitat**: *E. purpurea*: moist open meadows, forest edges, central and eastern US; *E. angustifolia*: dry prairies, Great Plains
- **Lookalike risk**: LOW for expert; MODERATE for beginner — *E. paradoxa* (yellow coneflower) lacks purple coloration; *Rudbeckia* (black-eyed Susan) is frequently confused by beginners due to similar form
- **Key ID feature**: Drooping purple ray florets (in both medicinal species); spiny central disk; single stem from basal rosette; rough-hairy texture
- **Harvest**: Root (primary clinical part); fall of second year; aerial herb (first year or at flowering)
- **Research hours estimated**: 3.5 hours (two-species comparison is the most complex ID section in Wave 1; prairie vs. woodland habitat distinction requires separate range language)
- **Photo sourcing**: Wikimedia Commons abundant for both species; iNaturalist CC-BY rich
- **Competitor gap**: No Etsy guide differentiates the two medicinal species with distinct ID keys and separate conservation status
- **Priority**: 1 (Etsy search volume for "echinacea guide" is top-5 herb term)

---

**Guide 4: Elderberry** (*Sambucus nigra* L.; *S. canadensis* L.)
- **Bundle cross-reference**: Phase 3 Respiratory and Immunity bundles
- **UpS status**: Abundant (not at risk)
- **Habitat**: Forest edges, stream margins, disturbed ground; native across eastern North America; *S. nigra* naturalized from Europe widely
- **Lookalike risk**: HIGH and SAFETY-CRITICAL — *Sambucus racemosa* (red elderberry) produces bright red berries that are considered toxic raw; *Aralia spinosa* (devil's walking stick) can be confused as a young plant. The distinguishing feature is the berry color (blue-black for *S. canadensis*; red for *S. racemosa*) and compound vs. simple-pinnate leaf arrangement.
- **Key ID feature**: Large compound leaves; flat-topped cream flower clusters (June–July); blue-black berry clusters September–October; pithy hollow stems with warty bark
- **Harvest**: Berries September–October; flowers June–July (elderflower); leaves (topical only — not edible)
- **Research hours estimated**: 2.5 hours (lookalike warning requires careful sourcing from safety literature; dual-species coverage adds time)
- **Photo sourcing**: Wikimedia Commons exceptional; iNaturalist CC-BY rich; Unsplash lifestyle
- **Priority**: 1 (highest Etsy search volume of any herb in Phase 3 catalog)

---

**Guide 5: Valerian** (*Valeriana officinalis* L.)
- **Bundle cross-reference**: Phase 3 Sleep and Nervines bundle (primary anchor)
- **UpS status**: Not at risk (naturalized European species, widely cultivated in North America)
- **Habitat**: Moist meadows, riverbanks, disturbed ground; widely naturalized; tolerates a range of conditions
- **Lookalike risk**: MODERATE — *Valeriana edulis* (tobacco root; edible valerian) is native to western North America and is sometimes confused; the key distinction is *V. officinalis* has opposite-pinnate leaves while *V. edulis* has simple or shallowly lobed leaves. Also confused with *Sambucus* seedlings in early spring.
- **Key ID feature**: Hollow square stems; pinnately compound leaves with serrate leaflets; small pale pink-white flower clusters in summer; distinctive earthy smell of root (valerianic acid)
- **Harvest**: Root; autumn of second year (roots are harvested when at maximum valerenic acid content); strong smell intensifies on drying
- **Research hours estimated**: 2.0 hours (well-documented; distinctive smell as ID feature simplifies key)
- **Photo sourcing**: Wikimedia Commons adequate; iNaturalist CC-BY moderate; garden photography available
- **Priority**: 1 (sleep bundle anchor)

---

**Guide 6: Passionflower** (*Passiflora incarnata* L.)
- **Bundle cross-reference**: Phase 3 Sleep and Nervines bundle
- **UpS status**: Not At-Risk at species level; Vulnerable in northern range fringe (OH, PA)
- **Habitat**: Forest edges, roadsides, old fields; primarily southeastern and south-central US; naturalized north to Zone 6 with protection
- **Lookalike risk**: LOW — the flower is unmistakable. Leaflet shape and vine growth habit differentiate from other native vines. Maypop fruit (fleshy yellow-green) is distinct.
- **Key ID feature**: Complex radially symmetric purple-and-white flower with filamentous corona (unmistakable); three-lobed alternate leaves; climbing vine; distinctive egg-shaped "maypop" fruit in summer
- **Harvest**: Aerial herb (leaves, stem, tendrils); late spring through summer before full seed development; flowers for highest flavonoid concentration at peak bloom
- **Research hours estimated**: 1.5 hours (distinctive appearance reduces ID key complexity; range and habitat are the primary content focus)
- **Photo sourcing**: Wikimedia Commons excellent (flower photograph is extraordinary); iNaturalist CC-BY rich
- **Priority**: 1 (visual appeal is highest of any Wave 1 guide — thumbnail conversion driver)

---

**Guide 7: Mullein** (*Verbascum thapsus* L.)
- **Bundle cross-reference**: Phase 3 Respiratory bundle
- **UpS status**: Not at risk (common European biennial, naturalized)
- **Habitat**: Disturbed ground, roadsides, rocky slopes, clearcuts; ubiquitous across North America in open habitats; full-sun obligate
- **Lookalike risk**: LOW — the distinctive tall flower spike (up to 6 feet) and large grey-felted basal rosette are unique. *V. blattaria* (moth mullein) is a slender close relative with purple-centered flowers; not toxic but not the medicinal species.
- **Key ID feature**: Large (up to 18 inch) elliptical grey-woolly basal leaves; tall spike of packed yellow flowers in year 2; biennial (rosette year 1, flower spike year 2); roadside common
- **Harvest**: Leaves (first year rosette or lower leaves of second year); flowers (in oil maceration for ear oil, June–August); root (uncommon)
- **Research hours estimated**: 1.5 hours (simplest ID key in Wave 1 due to distinctive appearance and absence of dangerous lookalikes)
- **Photo sourcing**: Wikimedia Commons exceptional; iNaturalist CC-BY rich; roadside photography straightforward
- **Priority**: 1 (forager audience crossover; highest wild-harvest accessibility)

---

**Guide 8: Wild Ginger** (*Asarum canadense* L.)
- **Bundle cross-reference**: Phase 3 Digestive bundle (supporting); Phase 4 Nervine bundle (potential)
- **UpS status**: To-Watch (not At-Risk, but UpS monitors for overharvesting trends)
- **Habitat**: Rich mesic deciduous forest; shaded north-facing slopes; Appalachian and Great Lakes; often found with trilliums, mayapple, and bloodroot
- **Lookalike risk**: IMPORTANT SAFETY NOTE — *Hexastylis* species (heartleaf ginger) are native to southeastern US and are closely related; *Asarum europaeum* (European wild ginger) contains aristolochic acid (nephrotoxic) — this distinction MUST appear in the guide as a safety warning. Native North American *A. canadense* does not contain aristolochic acids at levels of concern, but the European species confusion is documented in the safety literature.
- **Key ID feature**: Kidney-shaped hairy leaves growing in pairs from ground level; distinctive single maroon three-lobed flower at soil surface in spring; strong ginger scent when crushed; creeping rhizome
- **Harvest**: Rhizome; small quantities only due to slow growth; spring or autumn; To-Watch status means cultivated sourcing is recommended
- **Research hours estimated**: 3.0 hours (aristolochic acid safety distinction requires careful sourcing and precise language)
- **Priority**: 2 — elevated to Wave 1 due to safety significance of the lookalike warning (this is the guide practitioners most need to get right)

---

**Guide 9: Bloodroot** (*Sanguinaria canadensis* L.)
- **Bundle cross-reference**: Phase 3 Respiratory bundle (historical; not a therapeutic recommendation guide — identification safety guide only)
- **UpS status**: To-Watch
- **Habitat**: Rich deciduous forest; moist forest floor; early spring ephemeral; blooms before canopy leaf-out; Appalachian and eastern North America
- **Lookalike risk**: MODERATE — basal leaf in spring can be confused with mayapple (*Podophyllum peltatum*) before unfurling, or with young black cohosh. The primary safety relevance: bloodroot root is toxic and the red sap is a documented tissue irritant. The guide is framed as an identification reference to avoid misidentification, not as a harvesting guide.
- **Key ID feature**: Single white 8-petaled flower emerging from a rolled grey-green leaf in early spring (March–April); leaf deeply lobed with 5–9 irregular lobes; brilliant orange-red root sap (highly distinctive); leaf wraps around stem
- **Harvest note**: SAFETY WARNING — bloodroot rhizome contains sanguinarine (escharotic); internal use is not within scope of this guide. This is an identification guide only. External use in historical herbal tradition is noted with the appropriate safety context.
- **Research hours estimated**: 2.5 hours (safety framing requires extra care; early spring ephemeral habitat description requires precision)
- **Priority**: 2 (included in Wave 1 because of safety significance and Appalachian practitioner audience need)

**Guide 10: Wild Bergamot / Bee Balm** (*Monarda fistulosa* L. / *M. didyma* L.)
- **Bundle cross-reference**: Phase 4 Respiratory bundle (supporting)
- **UpS status**: Not at risk
- **Habitat**: *M. fistulosa*: dry prairies, open woodland, roadsides across North America; *M. didyma*: moist forest edges, stream margins, eastern mountains
- **Lookalike risk**: LOW within *Monarda*; higher if confused with non-Monarda mints (family Lamiaceae is large). Square stem, opposite aromatic leaves, and distinctive tubular flower heads distinguish *Monarda* from other wild mints.
- **Research hours estimated**: 2.0 hours

**Guide 11: Skullcap** (*Scutellaria lateriflora* L.)
- **Bundle cross-reference**: Phase 4 Nervine Materia Medica bundle (primary)
- **UpS status**: Not At-Risk species-wide; some wild populations stressed
- **Habitat**: Moist open woodland, stream margins, meadow edges; eastern North America; often found with jewelweed and wild mint
- **Lookalike risk**: HIGH and SAFETY-CRITICAL — adulteration with *Teucrium* species (germander) is documented in the supplement industry and has caused liver toxicity. The wild plant ID key should note that *Teucrium canadense* (American germander) and *Scutellaria* can grow in similar habitats. Key distinguishing feature: *Scutellaria* has a distinctive "skull cap" calyx shape and blue-violet flowers; *Teucrium* has pink-purple flowers with a strongly asymmetric lower lip.
- **Research hours estimated**: 3.0 hours (adulteration safety section is complex and requires careful sourcing from safety literature)
- **Priority**: 2 (safety significance warrants depth; Phase 4 Nervine bundle anchor)

**Guide 13: Mayapple** (*Podophyllum peltatum* L.)
- **Bundle cross-reference**: Not in Phase 3/4 therapeutic bundles — identification safety guide; forager crossover
- **UpS status**: Not at risk; common
- **Habitat**: Rich deciduous forest; often colonial carpets; eastern North America
- **Lookalike risk**: CRITICAL SAFETY NOTE — the umbrella leaf is distinctive, but the fruit (the only edible part, when fully ripe and yellow) requires careful identification. The green unripe fruit and all other plant parts (root, leaves, seeds) contain podophyllotoxin (potently toxic). This guide is a safety-first identification reference.
- **Research hours estimated**: 2.5 hours (safety framing is primary content; forager relevance of edible ripe fruit is secondary)
- **Priority**: 2 (high wild-harvester demand for this species; safety content is the differentiator from any existing forager guide)

**Guide 14: Hawthorn** (*Crataegus monogyna* Jacq.; *C. laevigata* (Poir.) DC.; *C. oxyacantha* auct. sensu lato)
- **Bundle cross-reference**: Phase 4 Circulatory and Heart bundle (primary anchor)
- **UpS status**: Not at risk (widespread native and naturalized shrubs/small trees)
- **Habitat**: Forest edges, hedgerows, open rocky slopes, old fields; native Crataegus species in virtually every North American region
- **Lookalike risk**: MODERATE — the genus *Crataegus* in North America has over 200 described species (among the most taxonomically complex genera in the flora). For the medicinal user, the key is that all red-berried *Crataegus* are generally used interchangeably; the guide should note that the thorns and berry cluster shape distinguish from *Pyracantha* (firethorn, non-native, somewhat similar berry clusters) and from crabapple (*Malus* spp., larger fruit).
- **Research hours estimated**: 3.0 hours (taxonomic complexity of the genus requires careful framing; European vs. native North American species distinction in therapeutic literature requires explanation)
- **Priority**: 2 (Phase 4 Circulatory bundle anchor; ND/RH primary audience)

**Guide 15: Boneset** (*Eupatorium perfoliatum* L.)
- **Bundle cross-reference**: Phase 4 Respiratory bundle (supporting); historical use guide
- **UpS status**: Not at risk
- **Habitat**: Moist meadows, swamp edges, stream margins; perfoliate (stem appears to pierce the leaves — the most distinctive field characteristic in eastern herbalism)
- **Lookalike risk**: LOW — the perfoliate leaf arrangement is unique in the genus and immediately distinguishing
- **Research hours estimated**: 1.5 hours
- **Priority**: 2 (Appalachian herbalist community; classic boneset/echinacea combination for acute respiratory support in traditional practice)

**Guide 16: Solomon's Seal** (*Polygonatum biflorum* (Walter) Elliott)
- **Bundle cross-reference**: Phase 4 Musculoskeletal bundle (Wave 2 deferred in diversification strategy); connective tissue and joint use
- **UpS status**: Not At-Risk; locally abundant
- **Habitat**: Rich deciduous forest; partial shade; arching stems create distinctive silhouette; eastern North America
- **Lookalike risk**: MODERATE — *Smilacina racemosa* (false Solomon's seal) has similar habitat and arching stem but the flowers and fruits occur at the tip (terminal raceme) rather than hanging in pairs beneath the stem nodes. This is a commonly confused pair in the Appalachian forest.
- **Research hours estimated**: 2.0 hours
- **Priority**: 2 (traditional Appalachian and Native American use; connective tissue niche)

- **Habitat**: Open meadows, roadsides, lawns; cosmopolitan; easy to recognize

**Guide 18: Plantain** (*Plantago major* L.; *P. lanceolata* L.)
- **Bundle cross-reference**: Phase 4 Skin and Lymphatic bundle (primary)
- **UpS status**: Not at risk (cosmopolitan weed species; forager-accessible everywhere)
- **Habitat**: Compacted disturbed soils, roadsides, lawns; universal; one of the most accessible wild medicinal plants in the northern hemisphere
- **Lookalike risk**: VERY LOW — the ribbed oval leaves of *P. major* and the narrow lance-shaped leaves of *P. lanceolata* are distinctive and have no toxic lookalikes in North America
- **Key ID feature**: Prominent parallel ribs on leaves; inconspicuous flower spikes; low-growing rosette or slightly upright; "weed" of compacted ground worldwide
- **Harvest**: Leaves spring through summer before they become too fibrous; young leaves have lower tannin content; fresh plant or dried for tea/poultice
- **Research hours estimated**: 1.5 hours (simplest ID key; primary value is the cultivation and harvest section distinguishing *P. major* from *P. lanceolata* for different preparations)
- **Priority**: 2 (forager audience bridge; urban harvest accessibility)

## Competitor Analysis

**Peterson Field Guide to Medicinal Plants and Herbs** (Foster and Duke, Houghton Mifflin, 2000; updated 2014):
- Coverage: 500+ species; eastern North America volume + western North America volume
- Format: Physical book, 400+ pages, not printable as a species guide
- Price: $27–32 paperback
- Gap: No conservation sourcing guidance; no harvesting protocols specific to at-risk species; no UpS status; no connection to therapeutic bundles or clinical context
- Seedwarden advantage: Single-species PDF format with at-risk sourcing guidance and bundle cross-references; printable for field use; priced at $5–7 per guide vs. $0.05–0.07 per species in Peterson

**AHG Herbal Education Library**:
- Coverage: Monograph-level, therapeutically focused
- Format: Members-only digital access
- Gap: Not field-identification focused; behind AHG membership paywall
- Seedwarden advantage: Standalone purchase, practitioner-accessible, field-focused format

**iNaturalist species pages**:
- Coverage: Comprehensive; free
- Format: Community-generated, variable quality
- Gap: No harvesting protocols; no at-risk sourcing guidance; not designed for offline field use or printing
- Seedwarden advantage: Curated, quality-controlled, printable, harvest-protocol focused

**Etsy competitors**:
A search of "plant identification guide" on Etsy surfaces primarily wildflower identification cards (illustration-based) and regional wildflower guides (broad coverage, thin per-species detail). No current Etsy seller produces species-specific guides with visual identification keys, at-risk status, and harvesting protocols in the format specified here.

## ZIM Archive Integration

**What ZIM enables**: Kiwix-compatible offline reading. A botanist, wildcrafter, or field educator in an area without internet access can download the Seedwarden ZIM file once and have all 18 identification guides available offline indefinitely.

**Technical requirement**: Guides authored in HTML-compatible markdown with consistent heading structure (H1 = species name, H2 = section headers, inline citation links). Canva exports are not ZIM-compatible directly; guides need a companion HTML version for ZIM assembly.

**ZIM file name**: `seedwarden-botanical-id-guides-v1.zim`

**Submission**: OpenZIM library submission (library.kiwix.org) is reviewed quarterly. Submission in August 31 cycle targets November 2026 library inclusion.

**License**: ZIM content distributed under CC-BY-NC-SA 4.0. Etsy PDF versions remain copyrighted for individual purchase. The ZIM version's free availability drives practitioner community goodwill and Seedwarden brand recognition in open botanical networks without cannibalizing Etsy sales (ZIM is for offline use; Etsy buyers want printable, formatted PDFs).
