## Section 2 — Interview Question Framework

Four templated question sets. Each is designed for a 30-minute phone or video call. Send the
relevant species draft (2–3 pages per species) to the interviewee 5 business days before the
call. All templates are copy-paste ready.

---

### Template A — Contraindication and Safety Review

*Use for: Tier 2 and Tier 3 species; any guide where drug interactions or population-specific
safety concerns exist. Intended interviewee: clinical herbalist, ND, or pharmacognosist.*

**Pre-call materials to send**: Draft contraindication section for 2–3 species (species name,
therapeutic actions, drug interactions listed, pregnancy/nursing recommendation, pediatric flag).

---

**Opening (2 min)**

"Thank you for taking the time to speak with us. Seedwarden produces educational plant guides
for practitioners, homesteaders, and wellness-focused individuals. Our Phase 3 focus is
medicinal herbs. We are trying to ensure that our contraindication and safety content is
accurate, appropriately qualified, and reflects current clinical understanding. I will be
asking you to react to our draft content for [species A] and [species B]."

**Core Questions**

1. Looking at our drug interaction list for [species], are there any interactions we have
   omitted that a clinical herbalist would consider significant? Are any interactions we have
   listed overstated or understated in their clinical relevance?

2. For pregnancy and nursing safety, we have stated [quote draft text]. Is this characterization
   accurate and appropriately qualified, or would you phrase the caution differently?

3. Are there specific patient populations — beyond pregnancy, liver disease, or anticoagulant
   use — that would require additional caution with this species? We want to ensure our guide
   does not omit any high-risk population.

4. We have listed [dosage range] as the standard adult therapeutic range. Does this align with
   your clinical experience, or have you seen evidence suggesting a different range is more
   commonly used or more appropriate?

5. Our pediatric safety statement reads: [quote text]. Is this accurate, or should we
   strengthen, soften, or specify the age threshold?

6. Are there any contraindication resources — databases, monographs, or reference texts —
   that you rely on for this species that we should cross-check before publication?

**Wrap-Up (3 min)**

"Is there anything about this species' safety profile that our guide does not address and that
you would expect to see in a practitioner-quality reference? Would you be comfortable with us
crediting you for this review, or would you prefer anonymity? May we follow up by email if
clarifying questions arise during the final edit?"

---

### Template B — Efficacy Evidence Standards

*Use for: All species where therapeutic action claims require classification. Intended
interviewee: academic herbalist, naturopath, or clinician with publication record.*

**Pre-call materials to send**: Draft "Therapeutic Actions" and "Modern Research" sections for
2–3 species, with specific claims highlighted for review.

---

**Opening (2 min)**

"We want to ensure that how we classify the strength of evidence for each therapeutic claim
is consistent with how a practicing clinical herbalist or researcher would evaluate it. We
have a tiered system — we call it Gold (RCT evidence), Silver (observational or cohort
studies), Bronze (traditional use, case reports) — and I would like to check our tier
assignments against your clinical judgment."

**Core Questions**

1. We have classified the evidence for [specific claim about species X] as [Gold/Silver/Bronze].
   In your clinical assessment, does this classification accurately reflect the strength and
   quality of available evidence? If not, what tier would be more accurate, and why?

2. When you are advising a patient about [species], what level of evidence do you consider
   sufficient to recommend it for [specific application]? How do you communicate uncertainty
   to patients when the evidence is primarily traditional or observational?

3. Are there specific published studies — clinical trials, meta-analyses, or systematic reviews
   — on [species] that you consider especially reliable or foundational, that we should cite
   in our guide?

4. Our "Modern Research" section for [species] cites [X sources]. Are there studies we have
   missed that would meaningfully change the evidence picture?

5. When traditional use evidence is the primary basis for a therapeutic claim, how do you
   frame that claim in clinical documentation or patient education materials? We want to use
   language that accurately represents the traditional basis without implying RCT-level
   confirmation.

6. Are there any claims about [species] that are widely circulated but that you consider
   unsupported or overstated relative to the evidence? We would rather correct these now
   than publish them.

**Wrap-Up (3 min)**

"Based on your review, is there a therapeutic action or indication we have included that you
would remove, or one we have omitted that you would add? How should we attribute your input?"

### Template C — Cultivation Technique Validation

*Use for: All species. Intended interviewee: commercial herb grower, permaculture specialist,
or agricultural herbalist with cultivation experience.*

**Pre-call materials to send**: Draft "Growing and Sourcing" section for 2–3 species, including
USDA zone ranges, soil requirements, propagation method, harvest timing, and post-harvest notes.

---

**Opening (2 min)**

"Our guides include a cultivation section so that practitioners and homesteaders can grow
their own medicinal herbs rather than relying entirely on purchased dried material. I am
looking for feedback on whether our cultivation instructions are accurate, complete, and
practical for a grower working at the home garden or small farm scale."

**Core Questions**

1. Looking at our USDA zone range and growing conditions for [species] — we have listed
   [zones X–Y, soil pH X.X–X.X, sun/shade requirement] — does this match your field
   experience, or have you observed the species thriving or struggling outside these parameters?

2. We have listed [propagation method] as the primary propagation approach. Is this the most
   practical approach for a home-scale grower, or is there a simpler or more reliable method
   you would recommend first?

3. Our harvest timing guidance for [species] reads: [quote text]. Is this consistent with
   your harvest practice, and are there visible plant cues — beyond calendar dates — that
   a grower should watch for to optimize harvest quality?

4. For post-harvest processing, we have described [drying method, temperature, storage
   conditions]. Are there practices that meaningfully improve potency or shelf life that we
   have omitted?

5. We have noted [conservation status] and recommend cultivated sources over wild-harvested
   material. Are there specific cultivar selections or seed sources you would recommend
   for highest quality and ethical sourcing?

6. Are there common cultivation mistakes or pest/disease problems with [species] that a
   guide should warn against? We want the growing section to prevent failure, not just
   describe ideal conditions.

**Wrap-Up (3 min)**

"Is there a cultivation resource — a specific grower, organization, or publication — that
we should credit or direct readers to for more detailed growing information? Attribution
preferences for your input?"

### Template D — Traditional Use Documentation and Cultural Sensitivity

*Use for: Species with significant Indigenous, TCM, or Ayurvedic traditional use contexts.
Intended interviewee: ethnobotanist, traditional knowledge practitioner, or academic with
cultural competency in the relevant tradition.*

**Pre-call materials to send**: Draft "Traditional Uses" section for 2–3 species, including
cultural attribution and historical framing.

---

**Opening (2 min)**

"Seedwarden's guides include traditional use sections that document historical and
cross-cultural plant use. We want to ensure this content is accurate, appropriately attributed,
and handles traditional knowledge with cultural sensitivity. I am looking for your guidance
both on factual accuracy and on how to frame traditional use in a way that respects the
communities whose knowledge this reflects."

**Core Questions**

1. Our traditional use section for [species] states: [quote text]. Is this historically
   accurate? Are there specific cultures, traditions, or time periods we should more
   precisely identify, rather than using broad terms like 'Native American use' or
   'Traditional Chinese Medicine'?

2. Are there aspects of the traditional use of [species] that our current draft omits that
   you consider significant from an educational standpoint?

3. How do you recommend we attribute traditional knowledge in a consumer-facing plant guide?
   For example, should we cite specific tribal nations, specific historical herbalists, or
   historical texts, or is it more appropriate to use broader framing?

4. Are there traditional uses of [species] that the plant's communities of origin would
   consider sacred, ceremonial, or inappropriate for broad commercial publication? We want
   to identify these early and handle them differently or omit them entirely.

5. If a traditional use of [species] conflicts with the current evidence base — for example,
   a traditional use not supported by clinical studies, or vice versa — how do you recommend
   framing that discrepancy in a way that is honest but respectful of the traditional knowledge?

**Wrap-Up (3 min)**

"Are there resources — scholars, organizations, or publications — that you consider the most
responsible sources for traditional knowledge documentation in this area? How should we
credit your guidance in the guide?"

**Standard contraindication check workflow (15 min per species)**:

1. Open NatMed Pro monograph for species. Copy all listed drug interactions, pregnancy rating,
   and nursing rating into the species database field.
2. Cross-check against MSK Herbs database. Note any discrepancies.
3. PubMed search: "[species name] drug interaction" filtered to last 10 years. Scan titles;
   read abstracts for any interactions not captured in step 1–2. Log any new interactions.
4. Record: source name, access date, interaction type (pharmacokinetic / pharmacodynamic),
   severity (major / moderate / minor), evidence grade.

### 3.2 Efficacy Evidence Tiers

**Gold (RCT Standard)**
- Source types: Randomized controlled trial (RCT), meta-analysis of RCTs, systematic review
  of clinical trials
- Confidence threshold: Two or more independent RCTs with consistent direction of effect
- Language template: "Studied in randomized controlled trials for [effect]; [number] trials
  showed [result]. Results should be interpreted cautiously as [relevant limitation]."
- Example species: Elderberry (Sambucol flu trials), Echinacea (Cochrane review on cold
  duration), Valerian (RCTs on sleep latency)

**Silver (Observational and Cohort)**
- Source types: Cohort studies, open-label clinical observations, case series (n > 20),
  pharmacological mechanism studies with human data
- Confidence threshold: Two or more observational studies in the same direction, or one
  high-quality cohort study
- Language template: "Observed in clinical practice and observational research for [effect];
  controlled trial evidence is limited. Traditional use documentation supports this application."
- Example species: Motherwort (observational data on menstrual cramping), Slippery Elm
  (GI soothing observations), Mugwort (traditional use + limited pharmacological data)

**Bronze (Traditional Use)**
- Source types: Documented traditional use in recognized herbalism texts, historical medical
  literature, ethnobotanical surveys, pharmacopoeial monographs (European or German Commission E)
- Confidence threshold: Documentation in two or more independent traditional medicine sources
  (e.g., American Botanical Council, European Herbal Monographs, traditional TCM or Ayurveda)
- Language template: "Used traditionally in [tradition / time period] for [effect]. Clinical
  research on this application is limited or absent; this use has not been evaluated in
  controlled trials."
- Example species: Lady's Mantle (traditional European women's herb; limited clinical data),
  Coltsfoot (traditional respiratory use; regulatory caution noted)

Apply this 10-minute vetting check before sending any outreach email. Use for all 25 contacts

**Step 4 — Fit Assessment (2 min)**
- [ ] Does their stated expertise align with at least one Phase 3 bundle domain?
- [ ] Is there a documented willingness to engage with educational content producers (podcast appearances, prior interviews, school affiliation suggest yes)?
- [ ] Flag for escalation: if contact requires licensing fee, requires institutional review, or has stated preference for avoiding commercial partnerships — note this before outreach.
