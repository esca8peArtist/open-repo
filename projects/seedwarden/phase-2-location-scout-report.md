# Phase 2 Location Scout Report

**Shoot window**: May 20–22, 2026 (3 active days; May 23–25 float)  
**Location decision deadline**: May 13 (Asheville Botanical Garden permit confirmation)  
**Species to photograph in field context**: American Ginseng, Goldenseal, Black Cohosh, Ramps  
**What the field location provides**: naturalistic woodland backdrop for habitat context shots and full-plant-in-environment compositions; root and seed close-ups are shot indoors regardless of field location

---

## Location Selection Criteria

Three criteria govern site selection for commercial botanical guide photography:

**Criterion 1 — Species-appropriate habitat context**: Guide photographs are most instructive when they show plants in realistic habitat. A goldenseal colony on a north-facing forest slope reads completely differently from the same plant on a table. The location must provide a naturalistic Appalachian woodland understory backdrop with appropriate soil color, leaf litter, and canopy light.

**Criterion 2 — Commercial photography permissions**: Any photograph made for a product sold on Etsy is commercial photography under the permit language of every public land management agency. This triggers permit requirements. Key constraint: National Forest permits require 60-day advance notice (deadline March 21 — passed for this shoot window); NC State Parks require 14-day advance notice (deadline May 6 for May 20 — passed). These options are closed for this shoot. Use a location with a shorter permit window.

**Criterion 3 — Logistical feasibility**: Parking, drive time, shelter from weather, bathroom access, and electrical access (or battery alternatives) all determine whether a location is executable on shoot day for a 1–2 person crew.

## Primary Location: Asheville Botanical Garden (North Carolina)

**Address**: 151 W.T. Weaver Blvd, Asheville, NC 28804  
**Website**: ashevillebotanicalgarden.org  
**Permit contact**: Facility rental / events manager via the facility-rental page

### Why This Location

The Asheville Botanical Garden is the strongest available primary location for five reasons:

1. **Species presence**: 600+ Appalachian native plant species in a naturalistic 10-acre woodland setting. Black Cohosh, Ramps, and Goldenseal are all documented as Appalachian understory species that appear in collections of this type. Ginseng may appear in managed demonstration plots or shade garden sections. This is the single most species-relevant public botanical garden in the region.

2. **Permit process**: 7-day advance notice required — the only major Appalachian public site with a viable permit window for May 20. Fee is $25–75 per 2 hours.

3. **Logistics**: Free adjacent parking lot; visitor restrooms on site; 5-minute drive from Asheville center; cell signal throughout. No hike required to reach any collection area.

4. **Weather resilience**: Covered pathways provide partial shelter during unexpected rain; proximity to town allows retreat to indoor backup without significant logistics disruption.

5. **Photography approach**: Bring your own cultivated specimens (from vendors in `phase-2-plant-sourcing-vendor-list.md`) and photograph them against the garden's woodland backdrops from public pathways. This is fully compliant with access policies — photographers are limited to pathways and non-planted areas for commercial sessions.

### Permit Details and Application

- **Fee**: $25 per 2-hour session (minimal equipment, 1–2 crew); $75 for sessions with multiple equipment pieces
- **Full-day estimate**: $75–$150 (3 two-hour sessions across the day)
- **Application deadline for May 20**: Submit by May 12 (one business day buffer before May 13 confirmation)
- **Application**: via ashevillebotanicalgarden.org/facility-rental/ — include: shoot dates (May 20–21), crew size (1–2 persons), equipment list (smartphone or camera, portable reflector panel, no tripod-mounted lights), intended use (botanical guide photography for digital educational publication sold on Etsy)

### Seasonal Peak: What Is Visible in May

May is the optimal month for Appalachian woodland photography. Specific to the four core species:

| Species | May Visibility | Bloom Status | What to Photograph |
|---|---|---|---|
| American Ginseng | Leafing out; foliage emerging | No bloom (August–September) | Compound leaf arrangement, stem emergence, any visible root collar |
| Goldenseal | Full foliage; distinctive leaves | No bloom (April–May, typically past) | Distinctive large paired leaves, leaf vein texture, forest floor context |
| Black Cohosh | Full foliage; palmate compound leaves visible | No bloom (June–August) | Compound leaf arrangement (5 leaflets), stem structure, woodland setting |
| Ramps | Leaves still visible; some populations beginning to senesce | Bloom past (March–April) | Broad strap-shaped leaves in colony clusters; leaf texture; bulb base if visible |

Flower photography for all four species must be sourced from iNaturalist CC-BY archive (previous spring seasons). This is expected and fully planned for.

### Accessibility

- **Driving distance from Asheville city center**: 5 minutes
- **Parking**: Free lot directly adjacent to garden entrance; no reservation required
- **Trail difficulty**: Flat to gently sloping pathways throughout the 10-acre site; no trail difficulty for typical equipment loads
- **Setup time on arrival**: 15–20 minutes (light test shot, initial path walk to identify 3–4 backdrop locations)

### Photography Conditions

- **Soil and understory background**: Naturalistic Appalachian woodland floor with leaf litter, root systems, and mixed understory — exactly the habitat context the guides need to depict
- **Sun angle in May**: East-facing pathways receive morning light May–June (9am–11am is optimal for soft directional light); north-facing understory areas remain in open shade throughout the day (superior for botanical photography — eliminates harsh shadows)
- **Shade patterns**: Mature woodland canopy filters direct sun to dappled light by 9am; overcast days eliminate dappling entirely and produce ideal even illumination for leaf and habitat shots
- **Water features**: Small seasonal stream on the garden property provides water-adjacent habitat context for ramps photography (ramps typically grow in moist woodland near streams)

## Backup Location 1: Private Forest Farm (Appalachian Region)

**Access method**: Via United Plant Savers (UpS) regional member referral or Western Carolina Botanical Club contact  
**Geographic range**: Western North Carolina, Southwest Virginia, Eastern Tennessee, Southern West Virginia  
**Permit**: Written permission from landowner only — no public land permit process

### Why This Is Photographically Stronger

A private Appalachian forest farm provides what no botanical garden can replicate: the actual cultivation system. You can photograph ginseng or goldenseal in the shade canopy where they grow, alongside companion plantings, leaf litter, and slope aspect that define their habitat. If a landowner is willing to participate, in-frame hands-on cultivation shots (spreading seed, demonstrating harvest protocols) are the most educational imagery in the entire guide series.

### How to Identify and Contact

- **United Plant Savers**: Email info@unitedplantsavers.org by May 10 requesting a referral to a regional member who does Appalachian forest farming and may be willing to host a botanical photography session. UpS regional chapters maintain active forest farmer networks in all five Appalachian states.
- **Western Carolina Botanical Club**: wncbotanicalclub.org — members include active native plant growers and naturalists in western NC with access to private woodland plant populations
- **Civil Eats forest farming network**: The 2020 Civil Eats article on Appalachian forest farming named several regional forest farmers; direct outreach to named individuals is viable

### Permit Requirements and Cost

No public land permits required. Arrangement: written permission via email (sufficient); offer $50–100 honorarium or a copy of the finished guides as reciprocal gift; confirm access to the planting area (not just the farmhouse). Request that the permission letter explicitly cover "commercial photography for a digital educational product."

**Lead time**: Begin outreach by May 10; allow 10 days to identify candidates, exchange messages, and confirm date and access. Written permission must be confirmed by May 14.

### Accessibility

- **Driving distance**: 30–90 minutes from Asheville depending on site location
- **Parking**: Confirm with landowner before visit — most Appalachian farm properties have vehicle access
- **Cell signal**: Variable in Appalachian rural areas — download offline maps before departure; do not rely on navigation cell service in remote forest areas
- **Road conditions**: Confirm with landowner whether access road is passable after rain; gravel roads in the Appalachians can become impassable in sustained rain

## Backup Location 2: Indoor Window Studio (Weather Contingency)

**Location**: Any home or apartment with a large east- or south-facing window  
**Permit**: None required  
**Cost**: $0–25 (foam-core reflector)

### When to Activate

Activate the indoor backup if the May 17 forecast shows 3 or more consecutive days of rain covering the May 20–22 shoot window. Do not wait until May 20 to make this decision — the prop setup and equipment configuration differ between field and indoor shoots, and preparation time is required.

### What Indoor Can and Cannot Provide

**Can provide** (photographic quality equal or better than field for these shot types):
- Root and rhizome close-ups (controlled lighting eliminates shadows that complicate field close-ups)
- Seed and seed packet identification shots
- Dried specimen arrangements
- Botanical illustration props in frame
- Leaf detail against controlled dark background

**Cannot provide** (must be supplemented with iNaturalist CC-BY archive):
- Full plant in naturalistic habitat context
- Colony or patch views
- Root-in-soil in-situ shots
- Any shot requiring a woodland background

**Consequence**: All guide interior photography is fully produceable indoors + iNaturalist archive. The guides do not require a field shoot to launch on May 30. The field shoot produces higher-quality habitat context images that make the guides more visually compelling — it is an upgrade, not a dependency.

### Setup

- East- or south-facing window with 3+ hours unobstructed morning light
- White foam-core board (24×36 in) opposite the window as fill reflector — $8–12 at any craft store
- Dark wood board or black velvet cloth as background for root/seed close-ups
- Camera or smartphone with white balance locked at 5500K (Daylight preset)
- All potted plants positioned 18–24 inches from window; not in direct midday sun
