# Phase 3 Herbalist Network Ecosystem Map

**Prepared**: May 20, 2026
**Purpose**: Audience research supporting Phase 3 June 22 launch. Identifies community hubs,
top voices, cross-selling opportunities, and audience segmentation for Seedwarden's
medicinal herb practitioner bundles ($120–$150 tier). Targets herbalist networks as the
primary Phase 3 expansion cohort beyond the general wild-edibles base.

---

## 1. Professional Network Overview

### American Herbalists Guild (AHG)

The primary professional body for non-licensed clinical herbalists in the US. Founded 1989.
Voluntary membership with a credentialing pathway — Registered Herbalist (RH) — requiring
1,200+ hours of documented training and clinical practice.

**Size and reach**: No publicly disclosed total. Estimates place RH-designated holders plus
active general members at 4,000–6,000. The broader community of US practitioners who
self-identify as herbalists (informal + formal) is 15,000–25,000. The AHG directory is
filterable by specialty (Women's Health, Respiratory, Nervines, Digestive) — the fastest
path to cold outreach for any specific bundle.

**Chapters**: 13 active chapters as of May 2026. Key regional chapters:
- Appalachian/Southeast: Tennessee, North Florida, Florida West Coast
- Northeast: Maine White Pine, New Hampshire, New Jersey North (Changewater NJ), New Jersey South, New York Long Island, Pennsylvania Eastern
- Mid-Atlantic: DMV (Washington DC area)
- Midwest: Nebraska (Herb Nerds)
- West: Arizona
- Specialty: Herbal Business Chapter (national, topic-specific)

Chapter coordinator: Sabrena Gwin — chapters@americanherbalistsguild.com. Each chapter
sends newsletters to local members; a Seedwarden ad or editorial mention in 3–4 chapters
reaches exactly the RH-level practitioners Phase 3 targets.

**Annual conference**: 36th Annual Symposium, August 14–16, 2026, location TBD. The 35th
(2024) was held at YMCA Blue Ridge Assembly, Black Mountain, NC. Attendance is not
publicly disclosed; estimate 400–700 in-person registrants based on event venue capacity
and regional conference norms. Both in-person and virtual options are offered. This is the
single highest-value practitioner gathering of the year. It falls outside the June 22–July
13 Phase 3 sprint; pre-event outreach in late July (2–3 weeks ahead) is the right follow-on
timing window.

**Publications**: Peer-reviewed journal (title: Journal of the American Herbalist Guild,
commonly cited as the AHG journal). Members receive it as a membership benefit. Audience
is RH-level practitioners — the exact Seedwarden practitioner tier.

**Seedwarden fit**: High across all bundles. AHG directory cold outreach filtered by
specialty is the fastest verified-practitioner acquisition path. Estimated reachable
practitioners per specialty filter: 200–600 per bundle domain.

---

### National Ayurvedic Medical Association (NAMA)

The main professional body for Ayurvedic practitioners in the US. Founded 1998. Four
membership tiers: Ayurvedic Health Counselor (AHC, $170/yr), Ayurvedic Practitioner
(AP, $185/yr), Advanced Ayurvedic Practitioner (AAP, $250/yr), Ayurvedic Vaidya
(AV, $300/yr). Certification administered by NAMACB. Membership size not publicly
disclosed; smaller than AHG by a meaningful margin. The 2026 NAMA Conference ran
May 1–3, 2026 (virtual; already completed).

**Herbalist overlap**: Moderate and targeted. Ayurvedic practitioners commonly use herbs
in Seedwarden's Immunity and General Wellness bundles (Ashwagandha, Tulsi, Shatavari,
Brahmi, Triphala constituent herbs). Digestive and Women's Health bundles have partial
alignment. NAMA practitioners tend to use formulas rather than single-herb references;
the practitioner bundle value proposition needs a cross-system framing for this audience
("Western botanical counterpart to Ayurvedic action").

**Seedwarden fit**: Medium. Outreach timing: post-June 22 given the NAMA conference has
passed. Next opportunity is NAMA's regional events and member newsletter.

---

### National Association for Holistic Aromatherapy (NAHA)

Not a direct herbalist organization, but relevant as a practitioner-adjacent community.
NAHA recognizes herbalists as Level 2 professional members alongside massage therapists,
nurses, and other health professionals. Membership includes practitioners who also prescribe
or recommend herbal protocols alongside essential oil work. Annual conference: Beyond
Aromatics 2025 ran October 23–25, Tampa, FL; 2026 date TBD. Overlap with Seedwarden's
General Wellness and First-Aid bundles (Lavender, Calendula, Chamomile are shared
species). Lower priority than AHG and NAMA but worth a single batch outreach to NAHA
member newsletter.

---

### American Association of Naturopathic Physicians (AANP)

Licensed NDs (naturopathic doctors). The AANP directory is searchable and publicly
accessible. Estimated 7,500 licensed NDs in the US (AANP data). NDs complete botanical
medicine training as part of their ND degree at accredited schools (Bastyr, NUNM, SCNM,
CCNM). This audience has the highest price tolerance of any Phase 3 segment — they pay
$200–$500 per CE event and purchase clinical reference materials regularly. They are
Segment 2 in PHASE_3_AUDIENCE_STRATEGY.md (Integrative Health Practitioners).

**Seedwarden fit**: Very high for practitioner bulk license. The absence of unsubstantiated
health claims in Seedwarden guides is a USP for this audience, not a limitation.
Outreach channel: AANP member newsletter; state ND association newsletters (CAND, OANP,
WSANP are the highest-density state chapters).

---

### United Plant Savers (UpS)

Conservation organization co-founded by Rosemary Gladstar in 1994. Focus: at-risk
medicinal plants. Membership approximately 2,000; 300+ botanical sanctuaries globally.
Manages the Forest Grown Verified (FGV) certification program, cited in Seedwarden Phase 3
sourcing guidance. UpS membership skews toward experienced, ethically-focused herbalists
— the most conservation-motivated Seedwarden buyers. They hold an annual botanical
sanctuary conference and publish a plant at-risk list that anchors Phase 3 sourcing ethics
content. HQ: Rutland, OH. Director: Susan Leopold, PhD.

**Seedwarden fit**: High. UpS newsletter mention reaches ~5,000–10,000 high-quality
practitioners. FGV sourcing alignment is an instant credibility signal with this audience.

---

### Herb Society of America (HSA)

Founded 1933. Garden-focused rather than clinical; 40+ chapters across the US.
Membership is low thousands. Audience skews toward older, garden-focused herb enthusiasts
rather than clinical or medicinal practitioners. Relevant for the General Wellness and
culinary herb bundles; lower fit for the clinical practitioner tier. HSA is a secondary
audience for awareness, not a primary distribution target for the $120–$150 bundle.

---

### Two Most Influential Networks for Phase 3 Targeting

1. **AHG** — verified practitioners, filterable directory, 13 chapters with existing
   newsletter infrastructure, annual symposium creating a natural August follow-on window.
2. **AANP** — highest per-practitioner LTV, FTC-compliant framing aligns with clinical
   purchasing standards, state-chapter outreach is highly focused geographically.

UpS functions as the third essential network for credibility positioning even if direct
sales through that channel are lower volume — a UpS newsletter mention legitimizes the
conservation angle for all practitioner audiences.

(thousands per year) with the widest skill range. The practitioner pipeline is growing:
enrollment in online herbalism programs expanded substantially during 2020–2022 and has
maintained elevated levels since.

### What Clinical Curricula Cover — and the Gaps Seedwarden Fills

Standard clinical herbalism curriculum covers: materia medica (species identification,
traditional use, basic therapeutic actions), pharmacy (tincture-making, tea preparation,
external applications), anatomy and physiology, clinical assessment, and some programs
include herb-drug interactions and evidence evaluation.

**Consistent gaps in existing curricula**:
- Cultivation and sourcing ethics (most programs treat this lightly or not at all)
- Regional species variation and bioregional plant substitution
- At-risk species conservation protocols and sourcing alternatives (UpS content is
  rarely embedded in curricula, only externally referenced)
- FTC-compliant language for client-facing educational materials
- Evidence tiering (how to represent clinical vs. traditional evidence to clients)

These gaps are exactly what Seedwarden Phase 3 guides fill. The sourcing ethics,
conservation status, cultivation instructions, and evidence-tiered language are the
differentiators that practitioner-tier buyers cannot find in standard school curricula.

### Geographic Community Hubs

**Appalachian Region (WV, KY, VA, TN, NC)**: The densest herbalist practitioner region
in North America. 50% of North American at-risk medicinal herbs are native here. Schools
present: Chestnut School (Asheville), Appalachian School of Holistic Herbalism, Northern
Appalachia School (PA). This region maps most directly to Phase 3's Women's Health (Black
Cohosh, Vitex) and Immunity (Goldenseal, Echinacea) bundles.

**Pacific Northwest (OR, WA, northern CA)**: Second hub. Characterized by bioregional
herbalism. Mountain Rose Herbs headquartered in Eugene, OR. Wildflower School,
Oregon College of Oriental Medicine, Bastyr (WA). High price tolerance for quality
resources. Strong Naturopathic tradition (OR and WA have robust ND licensing).

**New England (VT, MA, NH, ME)**: Rosemary Gladstar's base. UpS network is New
England-heavy. International Herb Symposium has historically run at Wheaton College MA.
Misty Meadows Women's Herbal Conference (Thetford VT). High practitioner density;
urban Boston integrative health market.

**Upper Midwest (WI, MN, MO)**: Midwest Women's Herbal Conference (Almond WI, annual,
May). Matthew Wood Institute (MN). HSA chapter concentration. Less dense than
Appalachian/PNW but meaningful for Women's Health bundle.

**Southwest (NM, AZ, CO)**: Colorado School of Clinical Herbalism (Boulder). UNM CIIM.
Sonoran medicine tradition (Tucson). SCNM naturopathic school. Grows medicinal herbs
year-round; cultivation content lands differently here — species that require heat.

### Online Communities

**Reddit r/herbalism**: 97,000–130,000 members (2023 figure; growth trend continues).
General discussion, enthusiast-to-practitioner range. Lower conversion for $120–$150
bundles; useful for brand awareness and free content seeding.

**HerbMentor (LearningHerbs)**: Paid subscription community run by John Gallagher and
Rosalee de la Forêt. One of the most engaged paid communities in the space; members
are intermediate-to-practitioner level. Active podcast and course ecosystem.

**Facebook groups**: Fragmented across 50+ groups organized by school, tradition, and
region. Combined estimated membership 50,000–100,000. No single dominant group; most
useful for organic shares rather than direct distribution.

**HerbRally**: Functions as the herbalism events directory for the US. Not a community
per se, but the practitioner audience that uses HerbRally attends in-person events —
a proxy for commitment level. Podcast (The Herbalist Hour) reaches active practitioners.

## 4. Publications and Media

### Print and Digital Publications

**HerbalGram (American Botanical Council, ABC)**: Quarterly peer-reviewed journal.
Published since 1983; 40th anniversary Winter 2024. Audience: researchers, practitioners,
and supplement industry professionals. Circulation not publicly disclosed; membership-based
plus institutional subscribers. Key for credibility: being cited in or advertising in
HerbalGram signals practitioner-grade legitimacy. ABC also publishes Commission E
monographs — the primary evidence source Seedwarden uses for Bronze-tier traditional
use documentation.

**AHG Member Journal**: Practitioner-tier content. Members-only distribution. No
advertising slots for external parties but editorial mentions are possible via AHG
education committee. This is the read-and-trusted publication for RH-level buyers.

**Herbal Reality**: UK-based but internationally read herbal media platform. High
domain authority (DR 50+). Publishes practitioner-written articles, event listings,
and research summaries. Guest content opportunity for Seedwarden.

**Regional zines**: Several regional herbalist collectives produce small-circulation
print and PDF zines. Examples: various regional chapter newsletters, small press
botanical publications from Appalachian and PNW communities. Aggregate audience is
small but highly motivated.

## 6. Audience Segmentation

### How Practitioners Segment

The US herbalist community does not self-segment in a tidy way, but five functional
archetypes describe the practice patterns that determine what they buy and at what
price point.

---

**Archetype 1 — Clinical Practitioner (RH, ND, NP)**

Who: Registered Herbalists, Naturopathic Doctors, Nurse Practitioners in integrative
settings. Credentialed. Charging $75–$200/hour for consultations. Using herbs as the
primary or co-primary treatment modality.

What they need from a guide: Correct botanical nomenclature on first scan. Evidence
sourcing transparency (they know the difference between an RCT and a case report).
Complete contraindication tables. FTC-compliant framing that they can hand to patients
without professional liability concerns. Printable format that survives clinical
environments.

Price sensitivity: Low. $120–$150 is one consultation fee. They will pay this without
hesitation if the guide passes their 30-second credibility scan (nomenclature,
contraindications, sourcing notes).

Key messaging differentiator: "Practitioner-grade reference your clients can take home
— botanical nomenclature, contraindication tables, and sourcing ethics in one printable
guide." Do not lead with aesthetics; lead with accuracy signals.

Size: 4,000–6,000 credentialed RHs; 7,500 licensed NDs; larger informal practitioner
community of 15,000–25,000.

Acquisition channels: AHG directory (cold, filtered by specialty); AANP state-chapter
newsletters; UpS member network.

---

**Archetype 2 — Student / Emerging Practitioner**

Who: Enrolled in Herbal Academy, Colorado School of Clinical Herbalism, Chestnut School,
or equivalent. 1–4 years into herbalism training. May have some Phase 2 Seedwarden
content already.

What they need: Study supplement content. Evidence-tiered explanations that complement
coursework. Cultivation instructions for species they are studying. Affordable entry
point.

Price sensitivity: Moderate. Budget-constrained. Will pay $20–$50 per guide. Will buy
$120–$150 if it functions as a complete course reference covering 5–10 species they are
actively studying.

Key messaging differentiator: "The clinical reference guide that works as a study
companion — evidence tiers, dosage ranges, and contraindication tables all in one
printable bundle." Curriculum alignment is the unique hook — map Seedwarden bundles
explicitly to Herbal Academy module structure.

Size: 5,000–10,000 actively enrolled; 2,000–5,000 new graduates entering practice
per year.

Acquisition channels: Herbal Academy affiliate program; school program newsletters;
HerbMentor community; lead magnet curriculum alignment guide (Lead Magnet B in
PHASE_3_AUDIENCE_STRATEGY.md).

---

**Archetype 3 — Wilcrafter / Bioregional Practitioner**

Who: Practitioners whose primary herb supply is their own harvesting or cultivation.
Often more rural. May have AHG membership but identity is more "wildcrafter" than
"clinical herbalist." Common in Appalachian, Pacific NW, and New England regions.

What they need: Correct species identification (critical — misidentification in
wild-harvest is a safety issue). Harvest timing and post-harvest processing guidance.
At-risk species guidance (they are ethically motivated; they want to know which species
to cultivate instead of harvest). Regional bioregional variation notes.

Price sensitivity: Moderate to high. More price-conscious than clinical practitioners
but willing to pay for reference quality. The ethics angle (UpS, FGV, at-risk status)
is an emotional driver that overrides price hesitation.

Key messaging differentiator: "Grow your own instead of wild-harvesting — cultivation
guides that protect the species you love." Conservation framing is the primary purchase
driver for this archetype.

Size: Hardest to count precisely. Part of the 15,000–25,000 informal practitioner
population. Concentrated in Appalachian, PNW, and New England communities.

Acquisition channels: UpS newsletter and network; HerbRally events calendar; AHG
chapter newsletters in Appalachian and NE chapters; Strictly Medicinal Seeds blog;
Reddit r/herbalism; Mountain Rose Herbs blog audience.

---

**Archetype 4 — Culinary / Cottage Industry Herbalist**

Who: Makes herbal products for community or limited-scale commercial sale. Tinctures,
teas, salves, infused oils, herb kits sold at farmers markets or Etsy. Not formally
credentialed but 3–10 years of self-directed study. Significant overlap with Phase 2
Homesteader cohort.

What they need: Preparation method instructions. Herb identification support. Safety
information for products they sell. Upsell material to include with their own herb kits.
Sourcing reference.

Price sensitivity: Low to moderate. The $120–$150 practitioner bundle is a hard sell
unless framed as something that enhances their own product offering. Lower-priced entry
at $20–$45 is more natural; the bundle works if positioned as "what to include with
every herb kit you sell."

Key messaging differentiator: "Give your herb kit customers a practitioner-grade
reference to match every herb you sell — printable guides that make your products more
valuable."

Size: 10,000–30,000 US cottage-industry herbalists (conservative estimate).

Acquisition channels: Etsy seller communities; Instagram #apothecary #herbalmaker;
Facebook herbalism product-making groups; Midwest and Appalachian regional communities.

---

**Archetype 5 — Ceremonial / Spiritual Practitioner**

Who: Practitioners whose primary relationship with plants is ceremonial, energetic, or
spiritual. Overlaps with astrology-adjacent herbalism (Sajah Popham's audience),
folk magic traditions, curanderismo, Indigenous plant medicine. This archetype is the
most culturally distinct and the hardest to reach through conventional practitioner
channels.

What they need: Traditional use documentation that honors cultural context. Not clinical
framing. Cultivation and bioregional content is appealing; contraindication tables are
less relevant.

Price sensitivity: Variable. Willing to pay premium for culturally respectful resources;
will not pay for a guide that feels clinical or disrespectful toward traditional knowledge.

Key messaging differentiator: "Traditional use, cultivation wisdom, and cultural context
in one guide — no disease claims, just the deep history of each plant."

Size: Difficult to estimate; several thousand US practitioners primarily in this mode.

Acquisition channels: Instagram ceremonial herbalism community; TikTok (Carmen Adams
audience); Southwest and California communities; folk herbalism event circles.

Phase 3 fit: Medium. This archetype can be served by Phase 3 content but requires
a different entry point — Traditional Use sections rather than contraindication tables.
Not the primary first-cohort target; a secondary expansion opportunity.

### Credential Dimension

Credentials create real purchasing behavior differences independent of archetype:

- **RH (AHG) designated**: Highest evidence standard. Will not buy without checking
  nomenclature, sourcing attribution, and contraindication completeness. $120–$150
  is acceptable if the guide passes their scan.
- **ND or NP**: Clinical framing is essential. Lead with "patient education resource."
  The practitioner license is the primary revenue mechanism for this credential tier.
- **School-enrolled student**: Price-sensitive. Curriculum alignment is the purchase
  trigger. $20–$22 entry; $120–$150 only with library framing.
- **Self-trained, no formal credential**: Price-sensitive, but motivated by depth and
  accuracy signals. The same content signals that attract RHs (botanical nomenclature,
  UpS sourcing notes) also signal quality to this buyer — they have done enough
  self-study to recognize them.

### Geographic Dimension

Regional variation matters for Phase 3 bundle sequencing:
- **Appalachian**: Women's Health (Black Cohosh, Goldenseal native range) and Immunity
  (Goldenseal, Echinacea) are the natural entry bundles. Conservation framing resonates
  strongly — this region has seen the worst wild-harvest depletion.
- **Pacific Northwest**: General Wellness (Ashwagandha, Reishi) and Respiratory (Mullein,
  Elderberry) are strong; bioregional cultivation content is highly valued. ND density
  means the practitioner license has high conversion probability.
- **New England**: Women's Health and Sleep bundles map to the strong women's herbalism
  tradition here (Gladstar, Wise Woman, UpS network). Conservation framing resonates.
- **Southwest**: Cultivation content for warm-climate species (Ashwagandha, Tulsi) is
  a regional differentiator. General Wellness bundle leads.
- **Midwest**: Generalist entry (General Wellness or Digestive) due to less concentrated
  specialist practitioner base. Women's Health bundle is relevant given Midwest Women's
  Herbal Conference presence.

### Early Adopter Profile for Phase 3 First Cohort

Highest-probability first-cohort buyer across all dimensions:

- Is either currently enrolled in or recently graduated from a clinical herbalism program

This profile responds to the sourcing ethics and conservation angle first, the evidence-tier
depth second, and the printable clinical-reference format third.

## 7. Cross-Selling and Alliance Opportunities

### What Practitioners Currently Buy

Understanding the existing spending ecosystem is essential for positioning Phase 3
bundles within established purchasing behavior.

**Dried herbs and bulk plant material**: Mountain Rose Herbs, Pacific Botanicals, Starwest
Botanicals, Strictly Medicinal Seeds. Annual practitioner spend: $200–$1,200/year. These
are not competitors — they sell material, not guides. Cross-promotion is natural.

**Reference books**: Standard clinical herbalism library includes David Hoffmann's
Medical Herbalism, Matthew Wood's The Earthwise Herbal (2 volumes), Rosemary Gladstar's
Medicinal Herbs: A Beginner's Guide, David Winston's Adaptogens, and specialty texts.
Average spend: $100–$400/year on 3–8 titles. The $120–$150 practitioner bundle is priced
comparably to 2–4 specialty books and positions as a printable, format-updated alternative.

**Seed catalogs and cultivation materials**: Strictly Medicinal Seeds, Prairie Moon
Nursery, Horizon Herbs. Annual spend: $50–$200. Direct overlap with Seedwarden's
cultivation sections — a practitioner purchasing from these catalogs is actively growing
the same species Seedwarden guides cover.

**Continuing education**: AHG Annual Symposium registration ($250–$450), online CE
courses ($200–$500/year), in-person workshops at regional conferences ($50–$150 each).
The $120–$150 practitioner bundle falls below the cost of any formal CE event while
offering similar depth on a per-species basis.

**Practitioner tools**: Herb identification field guides ($25–$45), tincture-making
equipment, apothecary packaging. Lower overlap with Seedwarden content.

---

### Content Gaps in Existing Products

These are the gaps that Seedwarden Phase 3 is uniquely positioned to fill — not filled
by any current competitor in the space:

1. **Sourcing ethics and conservation status, species-specific and printable**: No existing
   printable herb guide systematically includes UpS at-risk status, FGV sourcing
   alternatives, and ethical wildcrafting guidance per species. This is a genuine white
   space.

2. **Evidence-tiered therapeutic claims with FTC-compliant language**: Standard herbalism
   books either use unqualified claims (regulatory liability) or dense academic language
   (not accessible for client-facing use). Seedwarden's Gold/Silver/Bronze evidence tier
   system with practitioner-appropriate language is a format innovation.

3. **Cultivation-to-use continuity**: Most books are either cultivation guides or therapeutic
   use guides. Very few cover both in sufficient depth for a practitioner who grows and uses.
   The Carpenters' Organic Medicinal Herb Farmer does cultivation well; Hoffmann's Medical
   Herbalism does therapeutics well. Neither covers both in a single species-organized format.

4. **Regional bioregional notes**: National guides treat cultivation uniformly. A practitioner
   in Zone 4 (MN) and a practitioner in Zone 8 (CA) have completely different cultivation
   contexts. Regional notes embedded in the per-species cultivation section are not available
   in any current printable format.

**For Clinical Practitioners (RH, ND, NP)**: Lead with accuracy and clinical utility.

kit customers a reference they can keep — printable guides that make every herb you sell
more valuable." Upsell positioning, not practitioner positioning.

**For Ayurvedic Practitioners (NAMA)**: Lead with cross-system translation. "Western

### Key Risk: Practitioner Credibility Threshold

The practitioner tier has a binary credibility filter. If the guide fails the 30-second
scan (wrong Latin binomial, missing contraindication for a commonly known drug interaction,
claims that sound like marketing rather than evidence documentation), the practitioner
will not purchase and will not recommend.

Mitigation: Before any outreach to AHG practitioners begins, at minimum one RH-level
reviewer (from the contact list in PHASE_3_HERBALIST_NETWORK_PRESTAGING.md) should have
cleared the Women's Health bundle content. A single credentialed confirmation
("I reviewed this guide and find the botanical nomenclature and contraindication content
accurate") unlocks the AHG practitioner channel. Without it, outreach risks converting
early-adopter practitioners into negative reviewers rather than repeat buyers.
