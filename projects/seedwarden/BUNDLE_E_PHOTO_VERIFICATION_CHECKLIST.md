## How to Use This Checklist

For each species, you need exactly 3 photo types:

- **ID photo**: Clear close-up of identifying features (leaf shape, stem detail, distinctive markings). Used in Quick ID section of guide.
- **Habitat photo**: Medium or wide shot showing the plant in its natural setting (disturbed ground, field edge, roadside). Used in Where It Grows section.
- **Harvest-ready photo**: Plant at the optimal harvest stage (young shoots, ripe berries, open petals). Used in Harvest Method section.

Work through the 5x3 matrix below. For each cell, confirm: URL found, license checked, attribution recorded, image downloaded to local working folder.

## Per-Species Photo Audit

### 1. Garlic Mustard (*Alliaria petiolata*)

**ID Photo**
- Wikimedia search URL: `https://commons.wikimedia.org/w/index.php?search=Alliaria+petiolata`
- What to look for: Heart-shaped basal rosette leaves, serrated edges, white four-petaled flowers if in bloom
- Minimum resolution: 1200px on short side
- License: Verify CC0 or CC-BY (not CC-BY-NC or CC-BY-SA without plan for compliance)
- Attribution field: [ ] Recorded in attribution log below

**Habitat Photo**
- What to look for: Dense stand in shaded or disturbed area — woodland edge, trail margin, roadside with leaf litter
- Notes: Should show multiple plants together (monoculture context makes the invasive story visual)
- Attribution field: [ ] Recorded

**Harvest-Ready Photo**
- What to look for: Young basal rosette leaves in spring, tender green, before bolt (no tall flowering stalk visible). Second option: fresh-cut leaves in basket or bowl showing pesto-ready size.
- Notes: Peak harvest (late April - May) photos are most useful. Bolted plants with tall flower stalks are less useful for this guide.
- Attribution field: [ ] Recorded

**Backup Sources (if Wikimedia unavailable)**
1. iNaturalist: `https://www.inaturalist.org/taxa/54038-Alliaria-petiolata` — filter by CC-BY license, download from observation page
2. USDA Plants Database: `https://plants.usda.gov/home/plantProfile?symbol=ALPE4` — photos are public domain
3. Canva fallback: Search "garlic mustard plant illustration" in Canva's botanical illustration category — takes 15 minutes to produce a clean ID illustration if no photos available

### 2. Japanese Knotweed (*Reynoutria japonica*)

**ID Photo**
- Wikimedia search URL: `https://commons.wikimedia.org/w/index.php?search=Reynoutria+japonica`
- What to look for: Hollow bamboo-like stem with swollen reddish nodes, heart-shaped leaf with flat base (not heart-pointed)
- Stem cross-section photo is highest value if available (confirms hollow interior)
- Attribution field: [ ] Recorded

**Habitat Photo**
- What to look for: Dense monoculture thicket, 4-8 feet tall, stream bank or roadside; multiple stalks visible
- Notes: A stand showing scale (human or object for reference) is ideal — communicates the "this is takeover-level" context from the Etsy copy
- Attribution field: [ ] Recorded

**Harvest-Ready Photo**
- What to look for: Young shoots 6-18 inches tall, reddish-pink at base, leaflets just unfurling. This is the exact window (May 13-June 15 approximately). Shoots should look tender, not fibrous.
- Priority: This is the highest-value photo in the bundle given the seasonal urgency. If Wikimedia source is low resolution, use iNaturalist.
- Attribution field: [ ] Recorded

**Backup Sources**
1. iNaturalist: `https://www.inaturalist.org/taxa/55857-Reynoutria-japonica` — many spring shoot observations available
2. Phase 1 asset library: Check `projects/seedwarden/assets/` for any knotweed photos from prior shoots
3. Canva fallback: "Japanese knotweed illustration" or "bamboo shoot illustration" — style-match to Garlic Mustard illustration for visual consistency

### 3. Autumn Olive (*Elaeagnus umbellata*)

**ID Photo**
- Wikimedia search URL: `https://commons.wikimedia.org/w/index.php?search=Elaeagnus+umbellata`
- What to look for: Silvery-underside leaves (distinctive silver scaly coating visible on leaf undersides), small yellow-white tubular flowers or red/orange dotted berries
- Notes: Leaf underside silver sheen is the primary ID feature — a photo showing both top and underside is ideal
- Attribution field: [ ] Recorded

**Habitat Photo**
- What to look for: Shrub along fence line, field edge, or disturbed land. Multi-stemmed shrub form, 6-15 feet tall. Berry-laden branches in fall context (even if guide is written in May, the berries photo sets accurate visual expectations for the July-September harvest)
- Attribution field: [ ] Recorded

**Harvest-Ready Photo**
- What to look for: Ripe berries — red/orange with silver dots, clustered on stems. Close-up showing the dotted texture is the most distinctive image. Berries should look plump, not shriveled.
- Notes: Since Autumn Olive harvest is July-September, a harvest-stage photo from Wikimedia (fall date) is acceptable even if written in May. Date accuracy in the photo is not critical — berry quality is.
- Attribution field: [ ] Recorded

**Backup Sources**
1. iNaturalist: `https://www.inaturalist.org/taxa/77989-Elaeagnus-umbellata` — berry photos common in September-October observations
2. Penn State Extension: Often publishes CC-licensed invasive species photos
3. Canva fallback: "autumn olive berries illustration" or "silver berry shrub botanical"

### 4. Purslane (*Portulaca oleracea*)

**ID Photo**
- Wikimedia search URL: `https://commons.wikimedia.org/w/index.php?search=Portulaca+oleracea`
- What to look for: Succulent paddle-shaped leaves, reddish prostrate stems, flat rosette mat form growing low to ground. Very distinctive succulent texture distinguishes from lookalikes.
- Notes: Purslane is the easiest species to photograph and the most available on Wikimedia — expect multiple high-quality options
- Attribution field: [ ] Recorded

**Habitat Photo**
- What to look for: Purslane mat growing in garden path, pavement crack, disturbed bare soil. Context shows it's a common "volunteer" in cultivated settings (reinforces message that this is accessible to anyone with a garden or sidewalk)
- Attribution field: [ ] Recorded

**Harvest-Ready Photo**
- What to look for: Tender young growth, stems 3-6 inches long, leaves bright green and plump. A handful of purslane just harvested (in hand or in bowl) communicates the harvest quantity possible from a small patch.
- Attribution field: [ ] Recorded

**Backup Sources**
1. iNaturalist: `https://www.inaturalist.org/taxa/53155-Portulaca-oleracea` — extremely common, many observations
2. USDA Plants Database: Public domain photos available
3. Canva fallback: "purslane succulent illustration" — low risk (Purslane is one of Canva's more commonly illustrated edible plants)

### 5. Multiflora Rose (*Rosa multiflora*)

**ID Photo**
- Wikimedia search URL: `https://commons.wikimedia.org/w/index.php?search=Rosa+multiflora`
- What to look for: Small white five-petaled flowers in dense clusters, stipules (fringed leaf-base structures) which are the primary ID feature distinguishing from native roses
- A photo showing the fringed stipules close-up is the single most useful ID photo for this species
- Attribution field: [ ] Recorded

**Habitat Photo**
- What to look for: Dense thorny thicket at field edge or fence line, arching canes, in full bloom (May-June) or showing hip clusters (September-November)
- Notes: A habitat photo from the May-June blooming period is available now and ideal for the guide
- Attribution field: [ ] Recorded

**Harvest-Ready Photo**
- Notes: This species has two harvests — petals (May-June) and hips (September-November). Source one photo for each if possible; if only one available, use the petal harvest since the guide launches in May.
- Petals: What to look for: Open flowers with petals accessible for picking, clearly showing the white color and cluster structure. A small bowl of harvested petals is high-value if available.
- Hips: What to look for: Red-orange small hips (smaller than native rose hips) in dense clusters. Good contrast photo for the seasonal context section.
- Attribution field: [ ] Recorded (note if 1 or 2 photos sourced for this entry)

**Backup Sources**
1. iNaturalist: `https://www.inaturalist.org/taxa/47957-Rosa-multiflora` — blooming photos common May-June
2. USDA Plants Database: Public domain
3. Canva fallback: "wild rose illustration" or "rose hip botanical illustration" — abundant options

## Attribution Templates by License Type

Record attribution in the format required before embedding in any guide. Copy-paste the correct template.

### CC0 (Public Domain)
```
Photo: [Description]. Source: Wikimedia Commons. Public domain — no attribution required.
(You may optionally credit): Photo credit: [Author name if listed], Wikimedia Commons.
```

### CC-BY (Attribution Required)
```
Photo: [Description]. Credit: [Author name], Wikimedia Commons, CC BY [version number].
Source URL: [direct URL to the specific image page, not search results]
```

### CC-BY-SA (Attribution + ShareAlike Required)
```
Photo: [Description]. Credit: [Author name], Wikimedia Commons, CC BY-SA [version number].
Note: If this photo is embedded in a guide, the guide's photo section must note the SA license.
Source URL: [direct URL]
```

**Do not use CC-BY-NC images**. NC (Non-Commercial) licenses are incompatible with commercial Etsy sales. If a Wikimedia image shows CC-BY-NC, skip it and use a backup source.

## File Organization & Optimization Specs

### Local Folder Structure
Create this folder before downloading any images:
```
projects/seedwarden/assets/bundle-e-photos/
  garlic-mustard/
    garlic-mustard-id.jpg
    garlic-mustard-habitat.jpg
    garlic-mustard-harvest.jpg
  japanese-knotweed/
  autumn-olive/
  purslane/
  multiflora-rose/
```

### Optimization Specs for Web/PDF Delivery
- **Format**: JPEG for photos, PNG only if transparency is needed
- **Maximum file size per image**: 300 KB (keeps guide PDF under 5 MB Etsy limit when all 15 images included)
- **Resolution**: 1200px on the long edge (sufficient for screen display and home printing at 4x6)
- **Color space**: sRGB (not CMYK — CMYK causes color shift in browser PDF viewers)

### How to Resize (Free Tools)
- **Squoosh** (browser-based, no download): `squoosh.app` — drag and drop, set JPEG quality to 80%, resize to 1200px long edge. Takes 30 seconds per image.
- **Preview (macOS)**: Tools > Adjust Size, enter 1200px width, export as JPEG quality 8/10
- **GIMP or Photoshop**: Image > Scale Image to 1200px, Export As JPEG quality 80

### Embedding in Guides
In the Markdown guide template, insert each photo using:
```
![Alt text describing what the photo shows](../assets/bundle-e-photos/[species]/[species]-[type].jpg)
*Caption: [Description]. Credit: [Author], Wikimedia Commons, CC BY [version].*
```

When exporting to PDF (via Canva or Pandoc), images embed automatically at the file path specified.
