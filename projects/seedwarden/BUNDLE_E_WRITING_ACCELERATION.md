### GUIDE 1: PURSLANE

**File name**: `bundle-e-purslane-guide.pdf`
**Target word count**: 1,000-1,100 words
**Seasonal urgency**: Low — available June through first frost. Write without time pressure.

---

**Quick ID** (pre-fill — expand to 100 words)

- **Scientific name**: *Portulaca oleracea*
- **Plant family**: Portulacaceae
- **What to look for**: Prostrate succulent mat; paddle-shaped fleshy leaves (1/2 inch to 1 inch long); reddish-green succulent stems; yellow five-petaled flowers (tiny, appearing at stem tips in summer). The succulent texture — thick, waxy, moisture-filled leaves — is the instant identifier. No other common weed has this combination.
- **Harvest season**: June through first frost (Zone 5-6); May through October (Zone 7-8)
- **Best for**: Raw salads (adds slight lemony crunch), stir-fry, quick-pickled stems

`[CUSTOMIZE: Add one sensory detail — what does the leaf feel like between your fingers? What does it taste like raw? One personal note here increases buyer trust significantly.]`

---

**Where It Grows / Habitat** (pre-fill — expand to 150 words)

Purslane grows in disturbed, bare, or compacted soil. It is a warm-season annual that thrives in heat. You will find it reliably in:

- Garden beds and pathways — it volunteers in cultivation, especially after watering or rain
- Pavement cracks and gravel shoulders
- Field margins and orchard floors
- Bare patches after soil disturbance (construction sites, road repair zones, tilled fields)

It requires full to partial sun and tolerates drought better than almost any edible plant. Purslane does not establish under canopy; look for open ground.

Distribution: Effectively worldwide in temperate to tropical zones. Present in all 48 contiguous US states. In North America it is treated as an introduced weed but has been part of human food systems for thousands of years across the Mediterranean, Middle East, and South Asia.

`[CUSTOMIZE: Note one specific location type in your region where you have personally found it in abundance — garden, parking lot strip, farm field.]`

---

**Seasonality and Harvest Windows** (pre-fill — use specific dates, not vague seasons)

- **Peak season**: June 15 – September 15 in Zone 5-6; May 15 – October 1 in Zone 7-8
- **Optimal harvest window**: Any 4-week period within the peak; earliest growth in June is the most tender
- **What happens after peak**: Purslane toughens and becomes stringy as stems age. By late September in northern zones, plants produce seed and stems are fibrous. Harvest the top 3-4 inches of new growth — this is always tender regardless of plant age.
- **Regional variation**: Zone 9+ (Southwest, Gulf Coast): year-round in mild winters; primary season March – November

---

**Harvest Method** (pre-fill — expand to 150 words)

Target the growing tips (top 3-4 inches of each branch) and young leaves. Whole stems can be harvested if plants are young (under 4 inches tall).

- Use scissors or fingers; stems snap cleanly from the main plant
- Harvest 30-40% of the plant and leave the rest — Purslane regrows vigorously if not fully uprooted
- A 3-square-meter patch yields 1-2 cups of tender tips in 5 minutes
- For culinary use, collect in a basket or bowl — do not compress heavily (bruises the succulent tissue)

Ethical harvest note: Purslane seeds prolifically. Harvesting before seed set (before small yellow flowers close and produce a capsule) reduces the plant's spread while providing maximum nutritional value.

`[CUSTOMIZE: Note whether you harvest whole plants or just tips, and why. A preference detail here makes the guide feel lived-in rather than theoretical.]`

---

**Safety Notes** (pre-fill — MANDATORY items starred)

**Allergies**: No known common allergic reactions. `[CUSTOMIZE: Add if you have encountered any documented sensitivity]`

**Toxic lookalike — MANDATORY**: Spurge (*Euphorbia* species) can grow in similar bare-soil habitats and superficially resembles purslane. **The definitive distinction**: Break a stem. Spurge produces a white milky sap; Purslane produces clear fluid. If any white sap appears, discard the plant immediately — spurge causes skin irritation and is toxic if ingested. This test takes three seconds and eliminates all confusion.

**Oxalic acid**: Purslane contains moderate oxalic acid. For most people this is irrelevant; individuals with a history of kidney stones should limit consumption or cook before eating (cooking reduces oxalate content significantly).

**Habitat contamination**: Purslane growing in roadsides or near industrial sites may carry soil contamination. Wash thoroughly and avoid harvesting from high-traffic areas or sites with visible industrial history.

---

**Preparation Workflow** (pre-fill)

1. **Wash**: Submerge in cold water, agitate gently, lift out (do not pour off — grit stays at bottom of bowl). Repeat once.
2. **Dry**: Shake or spin in a salad spinner. Purslane wilts quickly in heat — keep refrigerated until use.
3. **Store raw**: Wrap in a damp cloth or paper towel, refrigerate. Use within 3 days — it deteriorates faster than most greens.
4. **Cook from fresh**: Heat deactivates the slightly mucilaginous texture (similar to okra); cooking 2-3 minutes produces a tender green with no sliminess.

---

**Regional Notes** (pre-fill)

- **Northeast**: June 15 – September 30. Most abundant in warm, dry July-August periods. After cool wet summers, it may not appear until mid-July.
- **Midwest/Great Lakes**: June 1 – September 15. Thrives in hot flat terrain; garden and corn-field margins are reliable.
- **Southeast/Gulf Coast**: April 15 – November 1. Year-round in Gulf zones. Heat and humidity produce the densest stands.
- **West (California, Pacific Northwest)**: Available March – October in coastal zones; May – September in mountain zones.

---

**Why This Plant Matters / Ecological Context** (pre-fill)

Purslane is one of the most nutritionally dense plants you can forage. It contains more omega-3 fatty acids per gram than many fish — a fact well-documented in food science literature since the 1980s. For a plant growing in pavement cracks and garden margins, this is extraordinary.

From an ecological standpoint, Purslane is an introduced weed in North America (native to the Mediterranean and Persia), and harvesting before seed set is a small but genuine contribution to managing its spread. The permaculture principle applies directly: the problem — a vigorous weed in your garden paths — is also the solution.

### GUIDE 2: GARLIC MUSTARD

**File name**: `bundle-e-garlic-mustard-guide.pdf`
**Target word count**: 1,000-1,100 words
**Seasonal urgency**: HIGH — harvest window closing NOW (second-year plants bolt by late May to mid-June depending on zone). Write with urgency language.

---

**Quick ID** (pre-fill — expand to 100 words)

- **Scientific name**: *Alliaria petiolata*
- **Plant family**: Brassicaceae (mustard family)
- **What to look for**: Heart-shaped to triangular leaves with scalloped-serrated margins; crushed leaves emit a distinct garlic odor (this is the single most reliable identifier — if it smells like garlic, it is Garlic Mustard); white four-petaled flowers in spring (cross-shaped, typical of Brassicaceae); second-year plants produce a tall (1-4 foot) flowering stalk. First-year plants are a low rosette that is harvestable through winter in mild zones.
- **Harvest season**: March-June (first-year rosettes year-round in Zone 7+); April-May (second-year greens before bolt)
- **Best for**: Pesto (garlic-forward, bright), raw salad, sautéed greens, pestos and compound butters

`[CUSTOMIZE: Describe the garlic smell in your own words — is it sharp? mild? closer to wild garlic or chive? Buyers who have never encountered this plant trust sensory descriptions more than visual ones.]`

---

**Where It Grows / Habitat** (pre-fill)

Garlic Mustard colonizes forest understories, woodland edges, trail margins, and disturbed shaded to partially shaded areas. It is particularly aggressive in riparian corridors and suburban woodland patches.

Key habitat signatures: Look for dense stands — this plant grows in monoculture colonies. If you see one Garlic Mustard plant, there are usually hundreds nearby. It tolerates deep shade better than most invasive plants and can dominate forest floor areas where native spring ephemerals once grew.

Established range: Essentially all of eastern North America from Nova Scotia to Georgia, west to the Great Plains. Pacific Northwest populations are scattered but spreading. Densest in suburban and disturbed forest settings.

`[CUSTOMIZE: One specific site type where you've found it — trail edge? park margins? stream corridor? Specificity makes the habitat section genuinely useful for first-time foragers.]`

---

**Seasonality and Harvest Windows** (pre-fill)

- **Peak season**: April 1 – May 20 (Zone 5-6); March 15 – May 1 (Zone 7-8)
- **Optimal harvest window**: 2-week window from when second-year plants emerge in spring to when they produce elongated seed stalks. Target: harvest when plants are 4-12 inches tall.
- **What happens after peak**: After flowering, leaf tissue becomes bitter and fibrous. By June 1 in Zone 5-6, second-year plants are past prime. Note for buyers: First-year rosettes (small, low, no stem) are harvestable from September through winter in mild zones — this is underutilized.
- **Regional variation**:
  - Zone 5-6 (New England, Great Lakes): Peak April 15 – May 20
  - Zone 7-8 (Mid-Atlantic, Ohio Valley): Peak March 25 – May 5
  - Zone 9+ (Southeast): March 1 – April 15 (window largely closed at time of this guide's publication)

---

**Harvest Method** (pre-fill)

Harvest the top 3-4 inches of growth (young leaves and stems) before the plant bolts. Pinch or cut with scissors. For pesto production, stripping leaves from young flowering stalks also works. A single dense stand (3m²) can yield a pound of greens in 15 minutes.

Ethical harvest: Garlic Mustard produces 500-1,000 seeds per plant per year and spreads aggressively. Pulling entire plants (including root) before seed set is the most ecologically beneficial harvesting method. If you are harvesting for food production at scale, uprooting rather than cutting tops reduces regrowth. Place uprooted plants directly into a bag — do not leave them on the ground where seeds can still ripen on the cut plant.

`[CUSTOMIZE: If you have a preferred harvest method — cut tops or pull whole plants — explain your reasoning here. This positions you as an experienced practitioner, not a text summarizer.]`

---

**Safety Notes** (pre-fill)

**Allergies**: Brassicaceae family allergy possible (the same family as broccoli, cabbage, mustard). If you react to mustard greens or arugula, use caution with Garlic Mustard.

**Lookalikes**: No toxic lookalikes at the leaf stage. The garlic smell is essentially diagnostic — no toxic plant in its range produces garlic-scented heart-shaped leaves. In forest settings, it can superficially resemble Violet (*Viola* spp.) before the smell test, but Violets have no garlic odor and distinctly different leaf texture.

**Post-bolt safety**: Mature plants past flowering are bitter and unpalatable but not toxic. Avoid for quality reasons, not safety.

**Habitat contamination**: Garlic Mustard accumulates certain compounds from soil. Avoid harvesting from roadsides with heavy herbicide application history, or from urban forest patches adjacent to industrial infrastructure.

---

---

**Preparation Workflow** (pre-fill)

1. **Wash**: Cool water soak 3-4 minutes to remove insects and soil. Lift out, do not drain over the greens.
2. **Dry**: Salad spinner or dry towel. Young leaves are fragile — handle gently.
3. **Blanch if bitter**: For mature leaves, blanch 30 seconds in boiling water, shock in ice water. This removes 50-70% of bitterness and makes mature spring plants usable.
4. **Store**: Fresh leaves refrigerate 3-4 days wrapped in a damp cloth. Blanched leaves freeze for 3 months.

---

**Regional Notes** (pre-fill)

- **Northeast** (Zone 4-6): Window April 15 – May 25. Densest stands in suburban woodlands, trail margins, and riparian corridors. Common enough that you can harvest without travel — it likely grows within 2 miles of most suburban properties.
- **Mid-Atlantic / Ohio Valley** (Zone 6-7): Window March 25 – May 10. Dense populations in mid-Atlantic deciduous forest understory. This is the epicenter of Garlic Mustard invasion in North America.
- **Midwest / Great Lakes** (Zone 5-6): Window April 15 – May 30. Present but less dense than the east; spreading. Woodland edges and suburban parks most reliable.
- **Pacific Northwest** (scattered populations): Small populations in Oregon and Washington; not widespread enough for commercial harvest but can be found in disturbed urban parks.

---

**Why This Plant Matters / Ecological Context** (pre-fill)

Garlic Mustard is one of the most ecologically damaging plants in eastern North American forests. It releases allelochemicals from its roots that inhibit the mycorrhizal fungi that native trees depend on for nutrient uptake — it does not just compete with plants, it disrupts the soil web underneath them.

A single plant produces 500-1,000 seeds, viable in the soil for 5-7 years. Harvesting before seed set removes that seed load permanently. Consistent harvesting of a stand over 2-3 years significantly reduces its density.

### GUIDE 3: MULTIFLORA ROSE

**File name**: `bundle-e-multiflora-rose-guide.pdf`
**Target word count**: 1,100-1,300 words (slightly longer — two seasonal stages require extra structure)
**Seasonal urgency**: MODERATE — petals window is open NOW (late May–June); hip window is September–November

---

**Quick ID** (pre-fill)

- **Scientific name**: *Rosa multiflora*
- **Plant family**: Rosaceae
- **What to look for**: Dense arching thorny shrub; small white five-petaled flowers in large clusters (distinguishes from most native roses, which have fewer, larger flowers); distinctive **fringed stipules** at the leaf base — the fringe structure where the leaf attaches to the stem is the primary diagnostic feature separating Multiflora Rose from native roses; small red oval hips (smaller than most native rose hips) produced in dense clusters
- **Harvest season**: Petals: May–June; Hips: September–November
- **Best for**: Petals — rose tea, infused honey, syrup, baked goods; Hips — vitamin C-rich syrup, jam, powder

`[CUSTOMIZE: Describe the fringed stipule in your own words — or describe a memory of the first time you found this plant and noticed that feature. Buyers remember vivid descriptions.]`

---

**Where It Grows / Habitat** (pre-fill)

Multiflora Rose colonizes field edges, fence lines, forest margins, old pastures, and roadsides. It forms dense impenetrable thickets — stands are often 6-15 feet tall and arc outward, creating a nearly impassable barrier.

Key habitat context: It thrives in areas where land management pressure has relaxed — old pastures no longer grazed, field edges no longer mowed, abandoned hedgerows. It is the default "scrub fence plant" across much of eastern North America.

Range: Eastern US (ubiquitous from Maine to Texas), spreading in the Midwest and Pacific Northwest. Less common in the interior West.

`[CUSTOMIZE: Note whether Multiflora Rose in your region is a field-edge plant, a stream-bank plant, or something else. This regional grounding is rare in foraging guides and adds real value.]`

---

**Seasonality and Harvest Windows** (pre-fill — dual-stage structure)

**Stage 1 — Petals (May–June)**
- **Peak season**: May 15 – June 20 (Zone 5-6); May 1 – June 5 (Zone 7-8)
- **Optimal window**: 2 weeks when flowers are fully open but before they drop petals naturally. Pick fully open flowers in morning (before noon — heat causes faster petal drop).
- **What happens after peak**: Petals drop within 2-4 days of full bloom. Once petals drop, the hip forms — no more petal harvest until following spring.

**Stage 2 — Hips (September–November)**
- **Peak season**: September 15 – November 15, after first frost (frost sweetens the hips)
- **Optimal window**: October — after first light frost, when hips are fully red and slightly soft to the touch
- **What happens after peak**: Hips shrivel and dry on the plant through winter (still usable as dried product; flavor concentrates). Birds eat them by January.

---

**Harvest Method** (pre-fill)

**Petals**: Pinch flower heads gently and pull petals, leaving the yellow stamens behind (stamens contribute to bitter flavor in preparations). Or harvest whole flower heads and separate at home — faster in the field. A productive stand yields 1-2 cups of petals in 20 minutes. Harvest before noon; afternoon heat wilts petals quickly.

**Hips**: Cut hip clusters with scissors — do not pull, which stresses the canes. Hips should be fully red and slightly pliable, not hard and orange. Avoid shriveled or very soft hips.

Safety for harvest: Wear long sleeves and gloves — Multiflora Rose thorns are curved and catch fabric. Approach stands from the side, not directly.

`[CUSTOMIZE: Add a practical tip you have found useful when harvesting from dense thorny stands — a specific technique for getting into the shrub, or a tool preference.]`

---

**Safety Notes** (pre-fill — MANDATORY items starred)

**Allergies**: Rose family allergy (Rosaceae) is a known reaction type. If you react to strawberries, apples, or cherries (all Rosaceae), approach Multiflora Rose petals cautiously and test with a small amount first.

**Lookalikes**: Native roses (*Rosa carolina*, *Rosa blanda*, *Rosa palustris*) are also edible — this is not a confusion risk but a benefit. All North American native roses produce edible petals and hips. The only important distinction: make sure you are not harvesting from a cultivated ornamental rose that has been sprayed with pesticides. Cultivated ornamental roses are treated with systemic pesticides in most commercial settings — never harvest from garden roses unless you know the spray history.

**MANDATORY — Hip seed warning**: Rose hip seeds contain small hair-like fibers (achene hairs) that cause throat and intestinal irritation if consumed. All rose hip preparations must be strained through a fine-mesh sieve or cheesecloth before consuming. This includes syrup, jam, and powder. A coarse strainer is not sufficient — use a fine sieve or double cheesecloth layer.

**Spray risk**: "Avoid commercially sprayed roses" is not sufficient guidance. Know the spray history of your specific harvesting site. If in doubt about a fence-line stand adjacent to agricultural land, do not harvest.

---

**Recipe Frameworks** (pre-fill)

**Recipe 1: Rose Petal Tea**
*Why first*: Simplest preparation, immediately accessible, no cooking required
- 1/2 cup fresh petals or 2 tablespoons dried petals
- 2 cups near-boiling water (190°F — not full boil, which destroys floral aromatics)
- Honey to taste (rose + honey is a classic pairing)
- `[CUSTOMIZE: Add dried hibiscus for color, or a strip of lemon peel, or fresh mint]`
- Steps: Pour hot water over petals, steep 5-7 minutes, strain, sweeten if desired
- Storage: Drink fresh; dried petals store 6 months in an airtight jar away from light

**Recipe 2: Rose Hip Syrup**
*Why second*: Classic preserved preparation, vitamin C-rich, gift-worthy
- 2 cups rose hips (cleaned, stem ends and blossom ends removed)
- 3 cups water
- 1 cup sugar or honey
- `[CUSTOMIZE: Add cinnamon stick or cardamom pod during cooking if you want a spiced variant]`
- Steps: Simmer hips in water 20 minutes until soft, mash, strain twice through fine sieve (discard fiber), return liquid to pot, add sugar, simmer until slightly thickened (10 minutes). Cool and bottle.
- Storage: Keeps 3 weeks refrigerated; can freeze in ice cube trays for 6 months

**Recipe 3: Rose Petal Infused Honey**
*Why third*: Zero-effort preserved product, long shelf life, excellent gift item
- 1 cup fresh petals (or 1/2 cup dried)
- 2 cups raw honey
- `[CUSTOMIZE: Use as a base for salad dressing, spread on toast, or stir into yogurt — note your favorite use]`
- Steps: Gently warm honey to liquid consistency (do not boil). Pack petals into a clean jar. Pour warm honey over petals. Seal and let infuse at room temperature 1-2 weeks, shaking gently daily. Strain if desired (or leave petals in — they continue infusing).
- Storage: Honey does not spoil; infused honey keeps indefinitely at room temperature

---

**Preparation Workflow** (pre-fill)

Petals:
1. Shake gently to dislodge insects; do not wash (washing destroys aroma quickly)
2. Use same day if fresh; or dry on a clean screen in shade for 24-48 hours until papery
3. Store dried petals in a sealed jar — 6 months at room temperature

Hips:
1. Remove stem end and blossom end (small nib at tip) with scissors — 5 minutes per cup of hips
2. Rinse briefly, drain
3. Use fresh or freeze whole for later processing (freeze on a sheet pan first, then transfer to bag)
4. When making syrup or jam: always double-strain to remove all seed fibers

---

**Regional Notes** (pre-fill)

- **Northeast**: Petal peak May 20 – June 20; hip peak October 1 – November 15. One of the most abundant invasives in the region — hedgerows, trail margins, old farmland.
- **Mid-Atlantic**: Petal peak May 10 – June 10; hip peak September 20 – November 1. Extremely common; one of the target invasives for regional control programs.
- **Midwest/Great Lakes**: Petal peak May 25 – June 30; hip peak October 1 – November 20.
- **Southeast**: Petal peak April 20 – May 25; hips September – October. Less dominant than in the Northeast.

---

**Why This Plant Matters / Ecological Context** (pre-fill)

Multiflora Rose was deliberately introduced across eastern North America by USDA in the 1930s-1950s as a "living fence" and wildlife habitat plant. It is now one of the most economically damaging invasives in the region, degrading old-growth forest understories and pasture productivity across millions of acres.

The hips are consumed by birds (especially waxwings) and spread through their digestive systems — which is exactly how Multiflora Rose spreads so effectively. Regular harvesting of hips reduces seed dispersal directly. For a land manager, harvesting is land management.

**Quick ID** (pre-fill)

**Where It Grows / Habitat** (pre-fill)

Autumn Olive grows in disturbed areas, old fields, pasture edges, roadsides, and degraded forest margins. It fixes atmospheric nitrogen, which allows it to thrive in poor soils where most other shrubs cannot establish — this is precisely what makes it invasive: it modifies soil chemistry to favor its own spread.

Range: Eastern and central US, primarily from New England south to Georgia and west to the Great Plains. Densest populations in the mid-Atlantic and Ohio Valley regions. Pacific Northwest populations are scattered and limited.

**Seasonality and Harvest Windows** (pre-fill)

- **Peak season**: August 15 – October 30 (Zone 5-7)
- **Optimal harvest window**: Late September – October, after berries have fully ripened (deepest red, most aromatic, highest sugar)
- **What happens after peak**: Berries shrivel but are still usable through November in mild years. Birds (starlings, cedar waxwings) strip most stands by late October in areas with high bird pressure. Harvest does not need to wait for frost (unlike rose hips) but flavor improves after cool nights.
- **Regional variation**: Zone 8-9 (South): August – September, sometimes through November. Zone 4-5 (North): August 1 – September 30 before early frost.

**Harvest Method** (pre-fill)

Harvest berry clusters by hand or use a berry comb. Pull small clusters into a bowl; large clusters can be placed over a bucket and stripped by running fingers along the stem.

A productive mature shrub yields 10-30 lbs of berries per season. One hour of picking from a productive stand can yield 5-8 lbs.

**Important**: Taste a berry before committing to a large harvest. Autumn Olive flavor varies by individual plant — some plants produce sweet, almost cherry-like berries; others are notably tart and astringent. Find the sweeter individuals in your area and return to them year after year.

**Safety Notes** (pre-fill)

**Allergies**: Some individuals with Elaeagnus family sensitivity may react to Autumn Olive. Rare but documented. Test a small amount if you have not consumed this species before.

**Lookalikes**: Silverberry (*Elaeagnus commutata*) is the primary lookalike — and it is also edible, so confusion carries no safety risk. The berries are similar; harvest either freely. In some western regions, Buffaloberry (*Shepherdia argentea*) may be encountered — Buffaloberry berries are also edible. The silver-leaf feature differentiates all three from toxic species.

**Unripe berries**: Green (unripe) Autumn Olive berries are astringent and may cause stomach upset. Harvest only fully red or orange berries.

**Habitat**: Avoid berries from fence lines adjacent to actively treated fields. The shrub is often targeted by invasive species management programs using herbicide — know your harvesting site's land management history.

**Why This Plant Matters / Ecological Context** (pre-fill)

Autumn Olive is one of the most researched invasive shrubs in North America, in part because of an unusual data point: it has one of the highest known lycopene concentrations of any food plant — up to 18 times the lycopene content of raw tomatoes, depending on the study. This has attracted interest from food scientists and nutritionists.

From an ecological standpoint, Autumn Olive changes soil chemistry wherever it establishes (nitrogen fixation raises soil nitrogen, shifting competitive advantage to other invasives like multiflora rose). Regular harvesting removes the berry crop that birds spread to new locations. For a land manager, systematic Autumn Olive harvest is both food production and an ecological intervention.

### GUIDE 5: JAPANESE KNOTWEED

**File name**: `bundle-e-japanese-knotweed-guide.pdf`
**Target word count**: 1,100-1,200 words
**Seasonal urgency**: CRITICAL — harvest window is open RIGHT NOW (mid-May through mid-June). Write with maximum urgency. Every delay costs buyers their spring window.

---

**Quick ID** (pre-fill)

- **Scientific name**: *Reynoutria japonica* (also *Fallopia japonica*)
- **Plant family**: Polygonaceae (buckwheat family)
- **What to look for**: **Hollow stem with bamboo-like segmented structure** and reddish-pink swollen nodes at each segment — this is the primary identifier. Cut a stem and it will be hollow like bamboo. Leaves are large (4-6 inches), broadly heart-shaped with a flat base (not pointed), arranged alternately. Stems are smooth, not hairy. Grows in dense monoculture stands 6-15 feet tall at maturity. Spring shoots are reddish-pink and asparagus-like when young.
- **Harvest season**: Late April – mid-June (shoots 12-18 inches tall, tender stage); mature stems June onward (more fibrous)
- **Best for**: Spring shoots: sautéed, roasted, jam (rhubarb substitute); older stems: pickled, fermented

`[CUSTOMIZE: The hollow stem cut is the single most memorable identification technique in foraging. Describe what it feels like or sounds like to cut a fresh knotweed stem. First-person sensory description anchors this guide.]`

---

**Where It Grows / Habitat** (pre-fill)

Japanese Knotweed grows in dense monoculture stands in disturbed, moist areas. Key habitats: stream banks, roadside ditches, construction margins, and any disturbed moist area with partial to full sun. It is one of the few invasive plants that establishes directly on stream banks, which is how it spreads downstream — plant fragments dislodge in flooding events and root wherever they land.

You will never find one knotweed plant alone. The root system (rhizome network) forms interconnected clonal stands — what looks like many plants is usually one organism. Stands detected in the 1980s in the Northeast are still present and expanding today.

Range: Primary range is the Northeast US and Canada; Mid-Atlantic states; Pacific Northwest (spreading). Dense populations in Connecticut, New York, Pennsylvania, Massachusetts, Oregon, and Washington.

`[CUSTOMIZE: One specific site type where you have encountered knotweed — stream corridor, roadside, urban lot. The more specific, the better.]`

---

**Seasonality and Harvest Windows** (pre-fill)

- **Peak season**: May 1 – June 15 (Zone 5-6); April 15 – June 1 (Zone 7-8)
- **Optimal harvest window**: 2-3 weeks when shoots are 8-18 inches tall. This is the window RIGHT NOW in mid-May (Zone 5-6). After 18 inches, shoot texture becomes fibrous and flavor becomes more astringent.
- **What happens after peak**: Shoots above 18 inches are still edible but less tender. By late June, knotweed reaches 4-6 feet and the young growth is gone until next spring. The young leaves (tip clusters on mature canes) remain harvestable through June but require more preparation.
- **Regional variation**:
  - Northeast / New England (Zone 5): May 1 – June 15 (shooting window open NOW)
  - Mid-Atlantic (Zone 6): April 20 – June 5
  - Pacific Northwest (Zone 7-8, Washington/Oregon): April 10 – May 25
  - Great Lakes (Zone 5): May 5 – June 20

**This guide launches during the peak window. Buyers who purchase now can harvest within days of receiving the guide.**

---

**Harvest Method** (pre-fill)

Snap or cut shoots at ground level when 8-18 inches tall. The shoot should snap cleanly with little resistance — if it is tough or stringy, the shoot has passed its harvest stage.

Economic reality of knotweed: One productive stand (100 square meters) yields 20-40 lbs of fresh shoots per harvest. The shoot window allows 2-3 harvests over 4-6 weeks. Regular harvesting of the same stand over consecutive seasons significantly weakens the rhizome network — harvesting is control.

Do not compost knotweed roots, stems, or any plant fragments. Any fragment can regenerate a new stand. Dispose of harvested material that you are not eating in sealed bags.

---

**Safety Notes** (pre-fill — MANDATORY items starred)

**Allergies**: Buckwheat family allergy possible; if you react to buckwheat, use caution.

**MANDATORY — Oxalic acid**: Japanese Knotweed contains significant oxalic acid. Cooking (blanching, roasting, stir-frying) reduces the oxalate content substantially. Raw consumption in large quantities is not advisable. For individuals with a history of kidney stones, limit consumption to cooked preparations and moderate quantities. This is a management note, not a prohibition — cooked knotweed is safe for most people.

**Lookalikes**: Hollow bamboo-like stem with swollen reddish nodes is essentially unambiguous. Native *Polygonum* species are occasionally confused but are much smaller plants and grow singly rather than in monoculture stands. If you see a stand of bamboo-look-alike plants 6-12 feet tall in a dense colony, it is almost certainly Japanese Knotweed.

**Stream bank caution**: Knotweed growing on stream banks may have root systems exposed to upstream agricultural or industrial runoff. Harvest from the upper stems and shoots rather than the root zone, and wash thoroughly.

**Recipe Frameworks** (pre-fill)

**Recipe 1: Knotweed Compote (Rhubarb Substitute)**
*Why first*: The flavor profile is genuinely closest to rhubarb — tart, fruity, excellent in sweet preparations; demystifies the plant for skeptical buyers
- 3 cups knotweed shoots (cut into 1-inch segments)
- 1/2 cup sugar or honey
- 2 tablespoons water
- Juice of 1/2 lemon
- `[CUSTOMIZE: Add strawberries (the classic pairing), vanilla, or ginger. Note your favorite combination.]`
- Steps: Combine all in a saucepan over medium heat, cook stirring occasionally until shoots soften and break down (10-15 minutes). Adjust sweetness. Use as jam, tart filling, pancake topping, or ice cream sauce.
- Storage: Refrigerates 2 weeks; freezes 6 months

**Recipe 2: Roasted Knotweed Shoots with Olive Oil**
*Why second*: Simplest savory preparation; demonstrates the vegetable use case
- 1 lb knotweed shoots (8-12 inches, woody base trimmed)
- 3 tablespoons olive oil
- Salt, pepper
- `[CUSTOMIZE: Add lemon zest and parmesan after roasting, or miso butter, or roasted garlic — note what you have used]`
- Steps: Halve shoots lengthwise if thick, toss with oil, season, roast at 400°F for 15-20 minutes until tender and lightly caramelized. Serve as a side vegetable.
- Storage: Best fresh; keeps 2 days refrigerated

**Recipe 3: Lacto-Fermented Knotweed Pickles**
*Why third*: Preserved option; fermentation reduces oxalate content (extra safety benefit); interesting for advanced foragers and fermentation enthusiasts
- 2 cups knotweed shoots (cut into 3-4 inch lengths)
- 1 tablespoon non-iodized salt
- Water to cover
- `[CUSTOMIZE: Add garlic cloves, dill, peppercorns, or hot pepper — note your preferred combination]`
- Steps: Pack shoots tightly in a clean jar, dissolve salt in 2 cups water, pour over shoots until fully submerged, weigh down to keep submerged (ziplock bag filled with water works), cover loosely with cloth, ferment at room temperature 3-7 days until pleasantly tart. Refrigerate.
- Storage: Keeps 2-3 months refrigerated

---

**Why This Plant Matters / Ecological Context** (pre-fill)

Japanese Knotweed is widely considered the most difficult invasive plant in the world to eradicate once established. Rhizomes penetrate 6-10 feet deep and 60-90 feet wide from the visible stand. Chemical control requires 3-5 years of annual treatment with systemic herbicide. Mechanical removal without chemical follow-up causes the stand to resprout from root fragments.

Consistent aboveground shoot harvest does not eradicate Knotweed (the rhizome is largely unaffected) but significantly weakens the stand over years by denying it aboveground photosynthesis during critical spring growth. For land managers, this is meaningful: harvest as management, not just as food.

The economic argument is also real. A productive knotweed stand harvested systematically yields 200+ lbs of food per season from land that is otherwise considered degraded. The forager's math is: ecological problem + human effort = food.

**Email → Etsy click**: Divide email clicks by email sends. Target 3-7%. Below 2% means subject line or preview text is not resonating, or the audience is not matched to the product.
