# Phase 2 Photography Reference Guide

**Purpose**: This document is the visual direction reference for all Phase 2 lifestyle photography. It covers the aesthetic philosophy, mood board references with links, per-cluster styling direction, lighting and angle specifications, and licensing guidance. Use it before picking up a camera or opening a stock search. The execution checklist is in PHASE2_PHOTOGRAPHY_EXECUTION_PLAN.md; this document answers the question "what should these photos look like and feel like?"

---

## Styling Philosophy

Seedwarden's brand voice is direct, slightly political, and built for urban growers who are not romanticizing farming — they are doing it in apartments, on fire escapes, and in shared community plots. The photography must match that voice or it will feel dishonest to the audience.

**The single most important styling rule**: these photos should look like they belong to a real person, not a lifestyle brand. A seed envelope that has been handled dozens of times is better than a pristine new one. A mason jar with a hand-written fermentation label is better than one with a commercial label turned away from camera. A balcony with an actual clothesline in the background is more credible than a styled terrace with nothing but photogenic plants.

Seedwarden's audience has a highly calibrated filter for aspirational homestead content. They have seen the curated farmhouse aesthetic on every major seed company's Instagram and they distrust it. Photos that signal "real person, real constraints, real practice" will convert better than photos that signal "lifestyle."

**The four styling pillars**:

1. **Earthy and tactile**: soil-adjacent, texture-rich, natural materials. Wood, clay, linen, glass. No chrome, no marble, no white seamless.

2. **Working, not display**: products in use, hands mid-task, props that have been used rather than arranged. An open seed envelope mid-sort. A mason jar with the ring slightly rusted. A cutting board with knife marks.

3. **Warm and honest**: color temperature 5500–6200K across all shots. Shadows open but not eliminated. The warmth should read as window light, not studio warming gel.

4. **Contained and practical**: small spaces are a feature, not a limitation. A balcony with limited room, a kitchen counter with real kitchen clutter pushed to the edge, a potting bench with well-used tools. The images should make a viewer in a 600-square-foot apartment think "that's exactly my situation."

## Lighting Specifications

### Natural Light (strongly preferred for all physical photography)

**Window selection**: Find the window that receives indirect light for the longest period of day. A north-facing window gives consistent indirect light all day. East and west windows have stronger directional light that can create harsh shadows; shoot in the first or last hour of their window to get soft rather than direct light. South-facing windows in summer give bright but harsh noon light; shoot in the morning.

**Optimal shoot time**: 8–11am for most window orientations. The light is bright enough for crisp photos but not yet at the harsh midday angle.

**Light direction for flat-lays**: Position the window to the left or right of the flat-lay surface, not directly behind the camera. Side-lighting creates the shadows that give wooden surfaces, seed envelopes, and mason jars their texture. A directly-overhead window or a window behind the camera gives flat, textureless light.

**Diffusion**: If direct sunlight is hitting your surface, tape a white sheet or a thin white bedsheet over the window. This converts direct sunlight to a giant soft box. Do not wait for a cloudy day — the diffusion technique works in direct sun.

### Artificial Light (fallback for Cluster A/B/C shoots)

**Recommended**: One 45W daylight LED softbox, 5500–6000K color temperature. Position at a 45-degree angle to the surface, at approximately table height (not overhead). This replicates the angle and softness of window light from the side.

**Source**: Amazon, search "photography softbox 45W daylight LED" — budget $35–60 for a single-light kit. Godox and Neewer both produce acceptable quality at this price point.

**Do not use**: Ring lights (catch-light pattern reads as influencer content, not practical lifestyle photography). Overhead room lighting without supplemental fill (creates flat, clinical light with the wrong color temperature). Flash in auto mode (creates harsh, flat light regardless of angle).

**Color temperature check**: Take one test shot with your artificial light setup and check the white balance on a phone screen. The surface should look warm (toward yellow-orange), not cool (blue-white). If it reads cool, add a 3200K CTO gel over the softbox or use a warmer bulb (3000–3500K for a slightly warmer tone, then correct in editing to 5500K — this approach reduces editing variance).

### Editing Color Specification

These are the specific parameters for the Lightroom Mobile / Snapseed / RawTherapee edit that produces the Seedwarden look. Apply these as a preset to the first image of each cluster, then batch-apply to the rest:

| Parameter | Value | Purpose |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| Exposure | +0.25 to +0.40 | Open up shadow detail without washing out |
| Contrast | +18 to +25 | Add richness; prevents flat look |
| Highlights | -25 to -40 | Pull back overexposed window areas |
| Shadows | +15 to +25 | Open shadows; earthy not dark |
| White Balance | 5500–6200K | Warm tone; earthy, not clinical |
| Tint | +3 to +8 | Slight green-toward-yellow push; organic feel |
| Saturation (Greens) | +10 to +15 | Healthy plant color |
| Saturation (Yellows) | +8 to +12 | Warm wood tones |
| Saturation (Blues) | -8 to -15 | Reduces digital/cool cast |
| Crop | 1:1 square | Etsy primary; crop 4:5 for Pinterest variant |

**Export settings**: 2400×2400px (Etsy), JPEG quality 85–90%. Run all exports through squoosh.app to verify under 1MB before Etsy upload.

## Camera and Angle Guidelines

### Flat-Lay (Etsy Slot 4 — primary lifestyle image)

**Angle**: Directly overhead, 90 degrees to the surface. Stand on a chair or step ladder if needed; an off-angle flat-lay is obvious and reduces perceived quality.

**Composition**: The product (tablet screen or printed pages) should be in the center or upper-center third of the frame. Props arranged around it, not on top of it. The product must be readable — not obscured by props, not too small in the frame. Aim for the product occupying 35–45% of the frame's area.

**Framing discipline**: Decide before shooting what the outer boundary of the arrangement is. Overspilling props that are cut at the edge of the frame look unintentional. Deliberately placing a prop so it bleeds off one edge (compositional choice) looks intentional. The distinction is whether the center composition is complete without the bleeding prop.

### Contextual / In-Use (Etsy Slot 5)

**Angle**: 45–60 degrees from horizontal. This is a "looking down at what someone is doing" angle — it communicates human presence and use without requiring a full-body shot or a second person.

**Hands**: One hand in frame is sufficient and preferred over no hands. The hand should be doing something: holding the tablet edge, flipping through printed pages, reaching for a prop. A hand doing nothing (resting flat) looks staged.

**Depth**: Give the image some foreground and background depth. The product in the middle ground, a prop in the foreground (slightly out of focus), a background element suggesting context. This is not bokeh for bokeh's sake — it communicates "this is a real space with real depth."

**Social media variant**: When shooting contextual shots, take 5–8 frames per setup. Some will be better for the 1:1 Etsy crop; others will work better cropped 4:5 (Instagram portrait) or 9:16 (TikTok B-roll still). Keep all usable frames — do not delete until after all cropping is done.

## License and Attribution Tracking

Every image used in Etsy listings and social content must have a logged entry before publication. The log entry goes in WORKLOG.md.

**Format for each log entry**:
- Image use: which product, which Etsy slot or social use
- Source: URL where the image was obtained
- License type: CC0, CC BY, CC BY-SA, Unsplash License, Pexels License, iStock Standard
- Attribution requirement: yes/no — if yes, exact attribution text
- Image file saved to: full path in project directory

**iStock Standard License**: Covers Etsy commercial digital product use. No attribution required. Covers up to 500,000 digital impressions — sufficient for Etsy at current scale.

**Wikimedia CC BY-SA**: Attribution required in any derivative work. For Etsy PDF products, this means a photo credits page at the back of the PDF. The wild-edibles habit photos (Session 560) already have this requirement documented. Same rule applies to any new Wikimedia-sourced lifestyle images.

**Unsplash / Pexels License**: Free for commercial use. No attribution required by license, though crediting is good practice. Log the URL regardless.

**CC0**: No restrictions. Log the URL for internal record only.
