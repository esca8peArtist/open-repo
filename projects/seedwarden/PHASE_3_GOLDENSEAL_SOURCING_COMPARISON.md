## Section 1: Species and Conservation Context

Goldenseal (*Hydrastis canadensis*) is the single most legally and ecologically significant species in Phase 3. Understanding its conservation status is prerequisite to the sourcing decision — it shapes not just the photograph but the entire guide body, the listing description, and the practitioner audience's credibility assessment.

### CITES Appendix II

CITES Appendix II listing means international commercial trade in wild-harvested Goldenseal requires export permits. It does not ban domestic US cultivation, sale, or use of cultivated material. However, the listing imposes a sourcing documentation requirement: any guide or educational product referencing Goldenseal must, to satisfy the practitioner credibility filter, explicitly distinguish between cultivated and wildcrafted sources and recommend only cultivated or Forest Grown Verified (FGV) material.

This requirement exists regardless of which photo path is chosen. The CITES sidebar (verbatim text in PHASE_3_MEDICINAL_HERBS_CRITICAL_PATH.md Appendix A) is mandatory in the Immunity bundle body and must be present at launch. The photograph does not satisfy or substitute for this sidebar.

### UpS Species of Concern

The United Plant Savers lists Goldenseal as a Species of Concern — not endangered, but population-pressured by wild harvest demand. A guide recommending wildcrafted Goldenseal would fail the practitioner credibility filter immediately. Both paths must therefore source either cultivated nursery stock (Path 1) or verified cultivated photographs (Path 2). Wild-harvested photography from iNaturalist observations of wild populations is not acceptable for this species in the Seedwarden context — it signals the wrong sourcing message to practitioner buyers.

### Path 1 Photography Yield

A single rhizome division at the June 8 order date produces one plant specimen arriving mid-July. Goldenseal at that size provides three to five photographable shots: rhizome structure (primary visual), early leaf emergence, and a hand-scale comparison. Flower photographs require second-year plants — a one-season lead time ahead of the June 8 order is required for flower photography, which is not achievable within Phase 3.

For the Immunity bundle, the most compelling Goldenseal image is the root/rhizome — this is the plant part used medicinally and the part that signals authentic botanical knowledge to practitioners. A fresh rhizome from a nursery order produces a better rhizome photograph than most Wikimedia Commons images, which tend to capture flowering habit rather than root structure.

### When Path 1 Is Justified

Path 1 is justified under three conditions simultaneously:

1. Option A or B is selected (5-bundle sprint) — meaning Immunity is written during the sprint rather than post-sprint, and the Immunity bundle's visual quality matters within the sprint window.
2. The user intends to position Seedwarden as a "premium, plant-in-hand" brand rather than an "educational resource" brand — and is willing to invest in that signal at Immunity launch.
3. The June 8 deadline is confirmed with zero slip. An order placed June 9 arrives July 20–27 — after the Immunity July 20 upload, with no v1.0 photography value whatsoever.

Under Option C (recommended), none of these conditions applies: Immunity is written post-sprint, Path 2 is sufficient for a July 20 upload, and the June 8 zero-float deadline creates unnecessary pressure.

The NC Botanical Garden email path (media@ncbg.unc.edu) typically responds within 3–5 business days with educational-use permission for high-resolution versions of their archived photographs. This path costs nothing and upgrades Path 2 photo quality to near-Path 1 levels for the rhizome and habit shots.

### Path 2 Photography Yield

Three to five usable images covering: (1) full plant in natural habitat, (2) dried root or rhizome, (3) close leaf detail or flower (if available). This is sufficient for one hero/cover candidate, one guide-body identification image, and one preparation image. It does not produce the in-studio, hand-scale, cultivated-plant-in-your-garden feeling that a live nursery specimen provides.

## Section 4: Brand Perception Analysis

The choice between paths is ultimately a brand positioning question, not a cost question. The cost difference ($15–22 vs. $0) is not material. The question is what the photography signals to the practitioner buyer.

### Path 1 Signal

A studio photograph of a live *Hydrastis canadensis* rhizome — clearly nursery-propagated, clearly in-hand — signals: "I grew or sourced this plant myself. I know what it looks like in person. I can teach you to identify and cultivate it because I have done so."

This is the highest credibility signal available for the Immunity bundle. It is the difference between a guide that reads like it was written from secondary sources and a guide that reads like it was written by someone who has held the plant. For the $120–$150 practitioner tier, this distinction matters.

The downside: this signal is delivered at v1.1, not v1.0 launch, because the plant arrives post-sprint under any option. The July 20 upload (v1.0) uses Path 2 photography either way. Path 1 only affects v1.1 quality.

### Path 2 Signal

Wikimedia Commons CC-BY-SA images, properly attributed, signal: "I have done thorough research and sourced verified botanical documentation." For most buyers, this is sufficient — practitioners recognize that academic and botanical garden photographs are authoritative. The NC Botanical Garden logo in an attribution credit is itself a credibility signal.

The "open-source aesthetic" concern in the prompt framing is real but overstated for this context. Practitioners are accustomed to educational materials using attributed botanical garden photography. The credibility concern is not the source of the image — it is whether the guide body demonstrates clinical-level herbalist knowledge. A guide with Path 2 photography and a complete CITES sidebar, correct Latin binomials with species authority, evidence-tier citations, and a full contraindication table will outperform a guide with Path 1 photography and weaker clinical content.

### Competitive Differentiation

Non-exclusive use is the primary real risk of Path 2. A competitor can use the same Wikimedia image. Under Seedwarden's current market position (early mover, low direct competition in the practitioner-grade medicinal herbs Etsy segment), this risk is low in the short term. If a competing guide launches with the same hero image in 2027, Path 1 v1.1 photography resolves the differentiation gap.

### The v1.0 / v1.1 Hybrid Strategy (Recommended Under Option A)

If Option A is selected and the practitioner-tier brand investment is desired, a hybrid approach is available:

1. Confirm Path 2 by May 30 (removes June 8 pressure from the decision window).
2. Place the Path 1 order by June 8 as a parallel action after the sprint is underway and pace is confirmed.
3. Upload Immunity v1.0 on July 20 with Path 2 photography.
4. When the plant arrives (July 13–20), photograph the rhizome in the studio.
5. Update the Immunity guide to v1.1 in August — refresh the hero image and add "Now includes original botanical photography" to the Etsy listing description.

This approach captures the brand premium at v1.1 without holding the July 20 upload hostage to the specimen's arrival date.
