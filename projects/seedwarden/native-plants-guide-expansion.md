## How Native Plants Guild Together

A guild is not a garden bed — it is a functional community. The right group of native plants placed together creates its own fertility, controls its own pests, manages its own water, and attracts its own pollinators. The gardener's job is to assemble the players and then largely step back.

Each guild below is organized around one **anchor species** (typically a canopy or structural plant), a set of **support species**, and the **functional logic** — why they work together and what each member contributes.

---

### Zone 3 — Northern Great Plains, Northern Rockies, Canadian Border States
*Minnesota, North Dakota, South Dakota, Wyoming, Montana highlands, northern Michigan and Wisconsin*

**Cold and wind are the primary design constraints.** Native guilds in Zone 3 emphasize windbreak structure, deep-rooted species that tolerate freeze-thaw cycling, and warm-season plants that make the most of a short growing window.

---

**Guild 3-A: The Bur Oak Savanna Guild**

- **Anchor**: Bur Oak (*Quercus macrocarpa*) — the defining tree of the northern plains, tolerates drought, fire, and sustained cold
- **Mid-layer**: Wild Plum (*Prunus americana*) — suckering shrub provides dense wind protection and spring pollinator bloom
- **Ground layer**: Wild Bergamot (*Monarda fistulosa*) — attracts bumble bees, deters Japanese beetle through scent
- **Ground layer**: Buffalo Grass (*Bouteloua dactyloides*) — drought-adapted turf, fixes the soil surface, suppresses annual weeds
- **Ground layer**: Prairie Smoke (*Geum triflorum*) — low-growing, deep taproot, thrives in the dry zones between oaks

*Why they work together*: Bur Oak's broad canopy creates a sheltered microclimate. Wild Plum fills the wind-exposed edges. The diverse ground layer reduces bare soil and distributes drought stress across species with different root depths. Once established (typically 3 years), this guild is largely self-maintaining.

*How to establish*: Plant the oak first and allow 2 full growing seasons before installing the ground layer. Wild Plum can go in year 2 along the northern edge as a windbreak. Ground layer plugs go in year 3, after the oak has begun modifying the soil immediately around it.

---

**Guild 3-B: The Wetland Edge Guild**

- **Anchor**: Swamp White Oak (*Quercus bicolor*) or Green Ash (*Fraxinus pennsylvanica*)
- **Support**: Buttonbush (*Cephalanthus occidentalis*) — fixes nitrogen, extraordinary pollinator plant, tolerates standing water
- **Support**: Swamp Milkweed (*Asclepias incarnata*) — monarch butterfly host plant, thrives in wet margins
- **Ground layer**: Blue Flag Iris (*Iris versicolor*) — filters runoff, stabilizes pond and stream banks
- **Ground layer**: Wetland Sedges (*Carex* spp.) — soil binding, waterfowl habitat, excellent weed suppression in wet areas

*Why they work together*: This guild functions as a biological water treatment system. Each species occupies a different water-tolerance niche — from periodically flooded (sedges, iris) to moist but not saturated (buttonbush, milkweed) to well-drained upland (the oak anchor). Together they create a gradient that manages drainage while producing continuous bloom from May through August.

*How to establish*: Identify the wettest point in your site. Install sedges and iris there first. Move outward to moist soil for buttonbush and milkweed. Plant the oak anchor at the driest point of the guild footprint.

---

### Zone 4 — Upper Midwest, New England, Northern Appalachians
*Maine, Vermont, New Hampshire, northern New York, Wisconsin, Minnesota, Upper Michigan, northern Iowa*

**Zones 4 guilds must bridge late springs and compressed summers.** The most effective native guilds here leverage the spring wildflower window (before canopy closure) and multi-season interest from fall seed heads.

---

**Guild 4-A: The Sugar Maple Understory Guild**

- **Anchor**: Sugar Maple (*Acer saccharum*) or American Beech (*Fagus grandifolia*)
- **Understory**: Serviceberry (*Amelanchier* spp.) — first woody plant to bloom in spring, early berry for birds and humans
- **Spring layer**: Wild Ginger (*Asarum canadense*) — dense spreading ground cover, suppresses weeds under canopy
- **Spring layer**: Trout Lily (*Erythronium americanum*) — uses the narrow window before canopy closes, corm-based so extremely cold-hardy
- **Understory edge**: Witch Hazel (*Hamamelis virginiana*) — blooms October–December, provides structure and late-season pollinators

*Why they work together*: This guild structures itself around the canopy light calendar. The spring ephemerals (trout lily, wild ginger) take full sun in March–May before the maple leafs out. Serviceberry blooms while the maple is still bare. Witch hazel's November bloom closes the season. No single plant monopolizes any niche.

*How to establish*: Begin with the anchor. Serviceberry can be planted as a 2–3 foot whip the following spring. Wild ginger spreads slowly — purchase plugs and allow 3 years to fill in. Do not rush the spring layer; it establishes on its own timeline.

---

**Guild 4-B: The Meadow Edge Prairie Guild**

- **Anchor**: Little Bluestem (*Schizachyrium scoparium*) — structural grass, winter interest, seeds support songbirds
- **Support**: Purple Coneflower (*Echinacea purpurea*) — long bloom period, seed heads persist through winter for goldfinches
- **Support**: Wild Bergamot (*Monarda fistulosa*) — bumble bee magnet, aromatic deer deterrent
- **Support**: Black-Eyed Susan (*Rudbeckia hirta*) — gap-filler, self-seeds freely, bridges early and mid-summer bloom
- **Support**: Butterfly Weed (*Asclepias tuberosa*) — monarch host plant, drought-tolerant, orange bloom fills the July gap

*Why they work together*: A staggered bloom sequence ensures something is in flower from June through October. The grass anchor provides winter structure and prevents the guild from looking bare in cold months. None of these species require supplemental irrigation once established.

*How to establish*: Direct-seed or plug all species in fall (cold stratification naturally occurs over winter). First-year appearance is minimal — resist the urge to weed aggressively. By year 2 the guild begins knitting together. Full establishment: year 3.

---

### Zone 5 — Mid-Atlantic, Ohio Valley, Lower Great Lakes
*Ohio, Indiana, Illinois, Missouri, Kansas, southern Pennsylvania, northern Virginia, southern New England*

**Zone 5 is the crossroads zone — warm-season and cool-season natives both thrive here.** Guilds can include plants from the prairie tradition and the eastern forest tradition.

---

**Guild 5-A: The Rain Garden Guild**

- **Anchor**: Swamp Rose (*Rosa palustris*) — tolerates periodic flooding, dense thorny thicket deters deer
- **Support**: Cardinal Flower (*Lobelia cardinalis*) — hummingbird magnet, handles wet feet for 48–72 hours
- **Support**: Blue Wild Indigo (*Baptisia australis*) — deep taproot breaks hardpan, nitrogen fixing, extremely long-lived
- **Support**: Joe-Pye Weed (*Eutrochium purpureum*) — 4–7 foot structural presence, late-summer monarch and swallowtail nectar
- **Ground layer**: Golden Alexanders (*Zizia aurea*) — early-season bloom, black swallowtail host plant, handles wet to moist soil

*Why they work together*: This guild is designed around a bioswale or low-lying wet spot. The deep-rooted indigo and structural joe-pye weed handle the slope edges. Cardinal flower and golden alexanders colonize the wet bottom. The swamp rose ties the guild together structurally. The result is a biologically rich planting that captures and processes stormwater while producing four months of bloom.

*How to establish*: Map the flood extent during a rain event — plant cardinal flower and golden alexanders at the lowest point, indigo and joe-pye at the margins. Swamp rose along the upslope edge. All are available as nursery plugs and establish well in their first season.

---

**Guild 5-B: The Sunny Meadow Guild**

- **Anchor**: Eastern Red Cedar (*Juniperus virginiana*) — evergreen structure, cedar waxwing magnet, windbreak function
- **Support**: Wild Columbine (*Aquilegia canadensis*) — ruby-throated hummingbird magnet, self-seeds freely on disturbed ground
- **Support**: Pasque Flower (*Pulsatilla patens*) or Prairie Dropseed (*Sporobolus heterolepis*)
- **Support**: Tall Goldenrod (*Solidago canadensis*) — fall monarch fuel, seeds support wintering sparrows
- **Support**: New England Aster (*Symphyotrichum novae-angliae*) — September–October bloom, late-season bumble bee support

*Why they work together*: The cedar provides year-round visual anchor and wildlife cover. The surrounding meadow species occupy full sun, with bloom running from April (columbine) through October (aster). Goldenrod and aster together create the critical fall fueling station for monarch migration. Self-seeding species (columbine, goldenrod) reduce long-term maintenance.

---

### Zone 6 — Transition Zone: Mid-South, Mid-Atlantic, Central Plains
*Tennessee, Kentucky, Virginia, West Virginia, North Carolina mountains, central Missouri, eastern Kansas, northern New Mexico highlands*

**Zone 6 offers the longest guild establishment window of any northern hardiness zone.** The limiting factor shifts from cold tolerance to summer heat and drought endurance.

---

**Guild 6-A: The Woodland Edge Guild**

- **Anchor**: Redbud (*Cercis canadensis*) — first large tree to bloom in spring, extraordinary pollinator value, fixes nitrogen
- **Support**: Wild Azalea (*Rhododendron canescens* or *R. calendulaceum*) — shade-tolerant, spring bloom period bridges the gap between redbud and summer natives
- **Support**: Wild Stonecrop (*Sedum ternatum*) — dense, drought-tolerant ground cover for dry woodland edges
- **Support**: Spicebush (*Lindera benzoin*) — spicebush swallowtail host plant, edible berries, fragrant, fall color
- **Support**: Virginia Bluebells (*Mertensia virginica*) — spring ephemeral, mass blue bloom in March-April

*Why they work together*: The redbud's early bloom (March–April) jumpstarts the pollinator season before most other trees have leafed out. Spicebush fills the May gap. Wild azalea carries into June. The spring ephemeral bluebells maximize the pre-canopy window. Stonecrop covers bare ground at the base and prevents annual weed establishment year-round.

*How to establish*: Redbud transplants well as a 3–4 foot nursery tree. Spicebush from nursery plugs or bare-root stock. Virginia bluebells from bulbs planted in fall. Stonecrop from division — one plant will spread across several square feet in 3 growing seasons.

---

**Guild 6-B: The Native Fruit Guild**

- **Anchor**: Pawpaw (*Asimina triloba*) — largest native North American fruit, clonal groves provide deep shade
- **Support**: Elderberry (*Sambucus canadensis*) — fast-growing, enormous pollinator value, edible berries, tolerates wet feet
- **Support**: Wild Strawberry (*Fragaria virginiana*) — ground cover, runners spread quickly, early fruit for birds and people
- **Support**: Wild Ginger (*Asarum canadense*) — under the pawpaw canopy where little else will grow
- **Support**: Spicebush (*Lindera benzoin*) — fills the gap between pawpaw height and ground layer

*Why they work together*: This is a food forest guild — every member produces edible fruit or provides pollinator support for those that do. Pawpaw requires cross-pollination (plant at least two trees of different clones). Elderberry attracts the unusual fly pollinators that service pawpaw. The ground layer fills in and suppresses weeds across the full footprint.

*How to establish*: Pawpaw requires patience — slow first three years but then accelerates. Elderberry fills in rapidly (2–3 years to full production). Plant elderberry and groundcover layer first to keep weeds down while the pawpaw establishes.

---

### Zone 7 — Upper South, Pacific Northwest Coast, Lower Mid-Atlantic
*Georgia piedmont, Alabama, Mississippi, Arkansas, Pacific Coast from central California to British Columbia, coastal Virginia and Carolinas*

**Dual climate considerations:** East of the Rockies, Zone 7 requires heat and humidity tolerance. West of the Rockies (Pacific Northwest), Zone 7 means mild wet winters and dry summers — plants need summer drought tolerance.

---

**Guild 7-A: The Eastern Zone 7 Woodland Guild**

- **Anchor**: White Oak (*Quercus alba*) — keystone native, 500+ insect species depend on it, acorns feed deer, turkey, and squirrel
- **Support**: Native Azalea (*Rhododendron canescens*) — spectacular spring bloom, shade-tolerant
- **Support**: Oakleaf Hydrangea (*Hydrangea quercifolia*) — deep shade tolerance, extraordinary fall color, native to southeastern US
- **Support**: Wild Ginger (*Hexastylis arifolia*) — evergreen ground cover in Zone 7, handles deep dry shade under the oak
- **Support**: Beautyberry (*Callicarpa americana*) — iridescent purple berry clusters in fall, extraordinary bird food source

*Why they work together*: The white oak is the ecological anchor of the southeastern deciduous forest. Everything in this guild has co-evolved with oak canopy conditions — seasonally dry, acidic, moderately shaded. The bloom sequence runs March (azalea) through June (hydrangea). Beautyberry and oakleaf hydrangea deliver spectacular fall interest when the oak is dropping acorns.

---

**Guild 7-B: The Pacific Northwest Dry-Summer Guild**

- **Anchor**: Oregon White Oak (*Quercus garryana*) — Pacific coast keystone equivalent of white oak; acorn-based food web
- **Support**: Pacific Ninebark (*Physocarpus capitatus*) — tough, deer-resistant shrub, white spring bloom
- **Support**: Blue Wild Rye (*Elymus glaucus*) — drought-tolerant bunchgrass, seeds for birds and small mammals
- **Support**: Yarrow (*Achillea millefolium*) — pollinator magnet, soil binder, handles summer dry-down completely
- **Support**: Camas (*Camassia quamash*) — early spring bulb bloom before summer drought begins; historically significant food plant

*Why they work together*: This guild is engineered around the Pacific Northwest's Mediterranean-pattern climate: wet winters, dry summers. Every member handles summer drought without irrigation once established. Camas blooms in April–May before the dry season begins. Yarrow and wild rye carry through the drought period. Oregon White Oak frames the entire planting and supports the local food web that shaped the original oak savanna landscape.

---

### Zone 8 — Deep South, Pacific Northwest Lowlands, Central Texas
*Texas Gulf Coast, Louisiana, Mississippi, Alabama Gulf, coastal Georgia and Florida, western Oregon lowlands, Puget Sound*

**Heat, humidity (east), or summer drought (west) are the primary design constraints.** Native guilds in Zone 8 emphasize deep-rooted drought tolerance in the west and heat-adapted species in the east.

---

**Guild 8-A: The Eastern Zone 8 Heat Guild**

- **Anchor**: Live Oak (*Quercus virginiana*) — evergreen, wind-resistant, extremely long-lived canopy
- **Support**: Yaupon Holly (*Ilex vomitoria*) — native evergreen shrub, bird food plant, tolerates full shade, drought, and salt
- **Support**: Turk's Cap (*Malvaviscus arboreus*) — summer-to-fall bloom in deep shade under live oak, hummingbird plant
- **Support**: Coral Honeysuckle (*Lonicera sempervirens*) — native (non-invasive) vine for fences and arbors, hummingbird magnet
- **Ground layer**: Inland Sea Oats (*Chasmanthium latifolium*) — shade-tolerant grass, rattling seed heads, good under canopy

*Why they work together*: Live oak canopy creates permanent deep shade — most plants cannot survive here. Every species in this guild is adapted specifically to dry, shaded, warm conditions. Turk's cap and coral honeysuckle together provide nectar for hummingbirds from June through November. Yaupon's winter berries feed cedar waxwings.

---

**Guild 8-B: The Texas Hill Country Guild**

- **Anchor**: Texas Live Oak (*Quercus fusiformis*) or Texas Mountain Laurel (*Sophora secundiflora*)
- **Support**: Agarita (*Berberis trifoliolata*) — thorny evergreen shrub, early-spring yellow bloom, wildlife cover
- **Support**: Mexican Sage (*Salvia greggii*) — season-long hummingbird plant, drought-tolerant, deer-resistant
- **Support**: Prairie Verbena (*Glandularia bipinnatifida*) — prostrate ground cover, butterfly nectar plant, handles rocky alkaline soil
- **Support**: Blackfoot Daisy (*Melampodium leucanthum*) — continuous white bloom spring through frost, drought-adapted

*Why they work together*: Hill Country soils are thin, rocky, and alkaline — conditions most cultivated plants struggle in but these natives were shaped by. The guild requires zero supplemental irrigation after the first year. Mexican sage and blackfoot daisy together provide bloom from March through November. Agarita fills the winter structure role and provides cover for ground-nesting birds.

*How to establish*: Plant into dry, rocky, unimproved soil — do not amend. Compost and extra water during establishment can actually weaken Zone 8 drought-adapted natives by preventing the deep root development they need for summer survival. Water weekly for the first 8 weeks only, then stop.

## Native Plants for Specific Site Conditions and Goals

The species listings below are quick-reference guides for buyers who have a specific problem to solve. Each entry includes the common name, scientific name, and a description focused on the use-case function.

---

### Rain Gardens and Bioswales

A rain garden is a shallow depression planted to capture and infiltrate stormwater runoff. It receives periodic flooding — sometimes 24–72 hours of standing water — followed by dry conditions. The plants below tolerate this boom-bust moisture cycle.

**Cardinal Flower** (*Lobelia cardinalis*) — Brilliant red spikes from July through September. Handles 48–72 hours of standing water, then thrives in moist but not saturated conditions. The single most effective hummingbird plant for the eastern half of North America.

**Blue Flag Iris** (*Iris versicolor*) — Grows directly in shallow water or saturated margins. Rhizomatous root system binds stream banks and rain garden edges against erosion. Striking blue-violet bloom in May–June.

**Swamp Milkweed** (*Asclepias incarnata*) — Monarch butterfly host plant adapted specifically to wet conditions. Establishes quickly from plugs, spreads moderately, deep roots improve infiltration over time. Pink bloom July–August.

**Joe-Pye Weed** (*Eutrochium purpureum*) — Architectural plant reaching 5–7 feet, handling everything from wet margins to moist upland. Late-summer (August–September) bloom serves migrating monarchs and swallowtails. Seed heads persist through winter.

**Buttonbush** (*Cephalanthus occidentalis*) — Woody shrub adapted to standing water. Unique spherical white flower clusters (July–August) are extraordinary bee and butterfly attractors. Provides structure at the edge where the garden transitions to dry ground.

**Softstem Bulrush** (*Schoenoplectus tabernaemontani*) — Tall emergent plant for the wettest zones. Stabilizes pond and stream margins, provides nesting material for marsh birds, filters nitrates from agricultural runoff.

**Copper Iris** (*Iris fulva*) — Native to the lower Mississippi Valley, handles extended flooding better than any other iris. Burnt-orange bloom is striking and unusual.

---

### Drought-Tolerant Landscapes

These species are adapted to dry, often rocky or sandy conditions. Once established (typically one full growing season), they require no supplemental irrigation in their native climate regions.

**Buffalo Grass** (*Bouteloua dactyloides*) — The original lawn alternative for the Great Plains. Fine-textured, blue-green, grows 4–6 inches tall without mowing. Requires no irrigation after establishment. Dormant in winter but green and dense from April through October.

**Prairie Dropseed** (*Sporobolus heterolepis*) — Fine-textured bunchgrass with a delicate fountain shape and late-summer bloom that smells faintly of popcorn. Essentially indestructible once established. Requires no inputs of any kind.

**Butterfly Weed** (*Asclepias tuberosa*) — The most drought-tolerant milkweed species, adaptable across much of North America. Deep taproot means it survives drought that kills surrounding plants. Brilliant orange bloom from June through August.

**Blackfoot Daisy** (*Melampodium leucanthum*) — Native to the arid Southwest and Texas. Continuous white daisy bloom from March through frost with zero irrigation in its native range. Thrives in poor, rocky, alkaline soil where most plants fail.

**Deerweed** (*Acmispon glaber*) — Pacific Coast drought specialist. Nitrogen-fixing, so it builds soil as it grows. Covers disturbed ground quickly in California and Oregon.

**Aromatic Aster** (*Symphyotrichum oblongifolium*) — October bloom when most plants are finished. Handles drought, clay, and neglect equally well. Spreads slowly into a mounding ground cover 1–2 feet tall.

**Blue Grama** (*Bouteloua gracilis*) — Companion grass to buffalo grass on the shortgrass prairie. Distinctive eyelash-shaped seed heads on fine stems. Handles sandy soil and alkaline pH. Excellent for dry-climate gardens across the Mountain West.

---

### Pollinators: Monarchs, Native Bees, and Hummingbirds

**For Monarchs (host plants)**

The monarch caterpillar eats only milkweed. These species are the most widely adaptable milkweed species for gardens:

**Common Milkweed** (*Asclepias syriaca*) — Best large-scale milkweed for the Midwest and East. Spreads aggressively by rhizome — ideal for a meadow, not a tidy bed. Intensely fragrant pink-purple bloom.

**Swamp Milkweed** (*Asclepias incarnata*) — Clumping milkweed for wet to moist sites. Better-behaved in garden settings than common milkweed. Reliable monarch magnet.

**Butterfly Weed** (*Asclepias tuberosa*) — Clumping, drought-tolerant, non-aggressive. Best for dry garden situations where common milkweed would spread excessively.

**For Native Bees (nectar and pollen)**

**Wild Bergamot** (*Monarda fistulosa*) — The single most important bumble bee plant in the eastern tall-grass prairie tradition. Lavender bloom July–August. Handles drought, clay, and dry shade. Self-seeds freely.

**Purple Coneflower** (*Echinacea purpurea*) — Long-blooming (June–September), tolerant of most soil types, extremely well-studied for native bee support. Seed heads feed goldfinches through winter.

**Golden Alexanders** (*Zizia aurea*) — First significant native bee food plant of the season (April–May). Specialist bee species depend on it for early-season pollen.

**Anise Hyssop** (*Agastache foeniculum*) — Extraordinarily high nectar output, nearly constant bloom from July through frost. One of the most productive single plants per square foot for native bee support.

**For Hummingbirds**

**Cardinal Flower** (*Lobelia cardinalis*) — The primary ruby-throated hummingbird plant east of the Rockies. Flower tube shape and red color are specifically co-evolved for hummingbird pollination.

**Coral Honeysuckle** (*Lonicera sempervirens*) — Native vine (non-invasive, unlike Japanese honeysuckle). Red trumpet flowers produce nectar continuously from April through fall. Hummingbird migration plant.

**Turk's Cap** (*Malvaviscus arboreus*) — Deep-shade hummingbird plant for the southeast. Blooms in conditions where most hummingbird plants fail.

**Mexican Sage** (*Salvia greggii*) — Best hummingbird plant for the Southwest and Texas. Available in red, pink, and coral. Blooms March through frost with no irrigation needed once established.

---

### Privacy Screening: Fast-Growing, Dense Native Species

Native screening plants have a significant advantage over invasives like privet, burning bush, or barberry: they do not escape into the surrounding landscape and they actively support wildlife while providing privacy.

**Elderberry** (*Sambucus canadensis*) — Fastest-growing native shrub for screening. Reaches 8–12 feet in 2–3 years. Spreads by suckers to form a dense thicket. White flower clusters (June) produce black berries (August) loved by 35+ bird species. Not for a manicured hedge — for an informal wildlife screen.

**American Holly** (*Ilex opaca*) — Evergreen tree growing 15–30 feet over time, dense enough to block sound and sight year-round. Slow but completely reliable. Red winter berries feed cedar waxwings and mockingbirds. Dioecious — plant one male per 2–3 females for berry production.

**Inkberry** (*Ilex glabra*) — Evergreen, compact (4–6 feet), tolerates wet and dry conditions. Spreads slowly by suckers. Better for formal hedge situations than elderberry. Native to the eastern US, excellent in foundation plantings.

**Buttonbush** (*Cephalanthus occidentalis*) — Fast-growing, moisture-tolerant screening shrub for wet sites. Reaches 6–8 feet in 3 years. Not evergreen, but extremely dense in leaf. Plant alongside inkberry for a wet-site evergreen/deciduous privacy combination.

**Wax Myrtle** (*Morella cerifera*) — Semi-evergreen fast grower for Zone 7–9. Reaches 10–15 feet and can be maintained as a hedge with 1–2 annual cuttings. Bayberry-scented foliage. Tolerates salt spray — excellent coastal screen.

**Arrowwood Viburnum** (*Viburnum dentatum*) — Dense, multi-stemmed native shrub for zones 3–8. Reaches 6–10 feet. White bloom in spring, dark blue berries in fall, outstanding red-orange fall color. One of the most versatile native screening plants across climate zones.

## When to Collect Native Plant Seeds: A Month-by-Month Guide

Collecting seeds too early means they have not fully matured and will not germinate. Collecting too late means they have already dispersed, been eaten, or been damaged by weather. This calendar covers the most commonly grown native species and what to look for at harvest time.

**Storage note used throughout this section**: "Cool-dry storage" means an airtight container in a cool, dark location (40–65°F). Paper envelopes inside sealed glass jars work well. "Cold-moist stratification required" means seeds need a cold-moist period before germination — stratify in damp sand or peat in the refrigerator for the number of weeks noted, or sow outdoors in fall and let winter do the work naturally.

---

### January and February — Collect Nothing / Plan Ahead

No native seeds are ready in January or February in most of the US. These are the months for:
- Inventorying last year's stored seeds and testing germination rates
- Researching which species to add to the collection
- Ordering seed-starting supplies before spring rush

**Exception**: In Zone 8–9, a few cool-season annuals (chickweed, henbit, shepherd's purse) may have matured seed by late February on warm years. These are minor forage species — collect only if you have specific use for them.

---

### March — Spring Ephemerals Begin

**Bloodroot** (*Sanguinaria canadensis*)
- What to look for: Elongated seed pods (1–1.5 inches) split open along one side. Seeds are shiny, orange-coated (elaiosome present). Collect immediately when pods split — seeds are shed within hours.
- Storage: Sow fresh within 1 week of collection. Seeds do not store well; they require moist conditions and quickly lose viability when dried.

**Early Spring Sedges** (*Carex* spp.)
- What to look for: Seed heads turn from green to brown-tan. Individual seeds firm and dark when squeezed.
- Storage: Cool-dry storage. Viable 1–2 years. Cold-moist stratification (8 weeks) improves germination.

---

### April — Peak Spring Ephemeral Window

**Wild Columbine** (*Aquilegia canadensis*)
- What to look for: Five-part seed follicles begin upright and turn from green to dry tan-brown. Follicle tips curl back when fully ripe. Tip them upside down into a bag — seeds spill out freely when ripe.
- Storage: Cool-dry storage, 2–3 year viability. Benefits from cold stratification (4–6 weeks), but many seeds germinate without it.

**Wild Ginger** (*Asarum canadense*)
- What to look for: Seed capsules at ground level ripen in April–May. Capsule turns brown and soft. Ants disperse seeds naturally (elaiosome attracts them) — collect before ants find them.
- Storage: Sow immediately or refrigerate in moist peat for fall planting. Loses viability quickly if dried.

**Golden Alexanders** (*Zizia aurea*)
- What to look for: Umbel seed clusters turn from green to deep tan-brown. Individual seeds are ribbed, oval, and brown. Seed readily detaches when clusters are rubbed lightly.
- Storage: Cool-dry storage, 2 year viability. Cold stratification beneficial.

---

### May — Transition to Early Summer Species

**Virginia Bluebells** (*Mertensia virginica*)
- What to look for: Flowers drop; four small nutlets form in the calyx. Nutlets turn from green to wrinkled-brown. Plant dies back entirely by late May — mark plants clearly so you can find the seed before they scatter.
- Storage: Sow fresh for best germination. Cool-dry storage acceptable for short term (1 season).

**Wild Strawberry** (*Fragaria virginiana*)
- What to look for: Fruits ripen from white-green to deep red. Collect and dry the tiny achenes (true seeds) from the surface. Birds will compete with you — harvest as soon as fully red.
- Storage: Clean achenes from fruit pulp, dry completely, cool-dry storage. Viable 2–3 years. Cold stratification (4 weeks) required.

---

### June — Early Summer Natives

**Wild Bergamot** (*Monarda fistulosa*)
- What to look for: Seed heads turn from lavender-brown to gray. Individual nutlets visible in the dried calyx heads. Seed heads rattle when shaken and ready.
- Storage: Cool-dry storage, excellent viability (3–5 years). No stratification required.

**Spiderwort** (*Tradescantia ohiensis*)
- What to look for: Capsules swell from green to tan and split open along seams. Catch with a bag placed over the seed head the day before you expect splitting.
- Storage: Cool-dry storage, 2–3 year viability. Surface sow in fall for spring germination.

---

### July — Peak Summer Collection

**Purple Coneflower** (*Echinacea purpurea*)
- What to look for: Cone turns from prickly purple-brown to hard and dark gray-black. Seeds are white-tipped achenes visible at the cone surface. Finches begin eating them — this is your signal. Clip entire cone heads and dry indoors.
- Storage: Cool-dry storage, excellent viability (3–5 years). Light stratification (2–4 weeks) improves germination rates.

**Wild Bergamot** (*Monarda fistulosa*) — late flush continues through July.

**Swamp Milkweed** (*Asclepias incarnata*)
- What to look for: Seed pods are 3–4 inch elongated capsules. Pod color turns from green to gray-brown. Pod surface becomes papery. Do not wait until they split open — harvest pods when they just begin to crack. The silky floss disperses immediately on wind when pods open.
- Storage: Remove seeds from floss, cool-dry storage. Viable 3–5 years. No stratification required.

---

### August — Milkweeds and Grasses

**Common Milkweed** (*Asclepias syriaca*)
- Same timing and method as swamp milkweed above. Pods are larger (4–5 inches). Harvest when pods crack at the seam tip but before they fully open.
- Storage: Cool-dry storage, 3–5 year viability.

**Little Bluestem** (*Schizachyrium scoparium*)
- What to look for: Fluffy white seed heads fully developed and feathery. Seeds are hidden at the base of the white plumes. Rub seed heads between palms over a container to knock seeds free.
- Storage: Cool-dry storage. Cold-moist stratification (4–6 weeks) improves germination, but many seeds germinate without it.

**Prairie Dropseed** (*Sporobolus heterolepis*)
- What to look for: Seeds have ripened when the distinctive popcorn fragrance is strongest in the seed heads. Seeds drop freely when heads are stroked.
- Storage: Cool-dry storage, 2–3 year viability.

---

### September — Asters, Goldenrods, and Berries

**New England Aster** (*Symphyotrichum novae-angliae*)
- What to look for: Fluffy achenes similar to dandelion. Seed heads turn from purple bloom to white-plumed. Collect seed heads before wind strips them — the window is short (3–5 days per plant).
- Storage: Cool-dry storage, 1–2 year viability. Cold stratification (4 weeks) improves germination.

**Tall Goldenrod** (*Solidago canadensis*)
- What to look for: Plumes turn fluffy and white-tan. Individual achenes are tiny and brown with a pappus of white hairs. Rub flower heads over a paper bag.
- Storage: Cool-dry storage, 1–2 year viability. Sow on surface of growing medium — needs light for germination.

**Elderberry** (*Sambucus canadensis*)
- What to look for: Entire berry clusters turn deep purple-black (not red — red clusters are not fully ripe). Berries soft and juicy. Separate seeds from pulp by mashing through a strainer with water. Seeds sink; pulp and bad seeds float.
- Storage: Cold-moist stratification required (60–90 days). Sow in fall or stratify in refrigerator.

---

### October — Late Season Collection

**Serviceberry** (*Amelanchier* spp.)
- What to look for: Berry clusters turn from red to dark blue-black. Fruit is fully sweet and soft. Same cleaning method as elderberry — float cleaning in water.
- Storage: Cold-moist stratification required (90 days). Sow outdoors in fall for natural stratification, or stratify in refrigerator for spring planting.

**Witch Hazel** (*Hamamelis virginiana*)
- What to look for: Woody capsules harden and turn light tan. Capsules audibly and dramatically eject their seeds (up to 30 feet) when ripe — the capsule splits and fires. To collect, enclose capsule in a small paper bag and wait for the shot.
- Storage: Cool-dry storage. Cold-moist stratification (60–90 days) required.

**Pawpaw** (*Asimina triloba*)
- What to look for: Fruit falls from tree when ripe and ripe fruit smells intensely of banana-mango. Seeds are large (1 inch), dark brown, slippery.
- Storage: Do not dry — seeds must remain moist. Stratify in moist sand or peat at 35–40°F for 90–120 days before sowing. Store moist, never frozen.

---

### November and December — Final Collections

**Wild Plum** (*Prunus americana*)
- What to look for: Pits cleaned from overripe or frost-softened fallen fruit. Float-clean: viable pits sink, empties float.
- Storage: Cold-moist stratification required (90 days). Sow outdoors and let winter do the stratification naturally, or refrigerate in moist sand.

**Eastern Red Cedar** (*Juniperus virginiana*)
- What to look for: Blue-gray waxy berry-like cones fully colored. Birds begin feeding heavily — collect before cedar waxwings strip the tree.
- Storage: Clean fruit pulp from seeds, cold-moist stratification (30–60 days). Germination rate typically 30–50% even with good stratification — sow thickly.

**Summary Note**: The most reliable approach for any native species with cold stratification requirements is direct fall sowing outdoors. Nature's own freeze-thaw cycles over the winter produce germination results that artificial stratification approximates but rarely exceeds. For seeds you are not confident about, the outdoor fall sow is the lowest-effort, most reliable approach.

## 10 Common Problems and How to Solve Them

Native plants are marketed as low-maintenance once established — that qualifier matters. The establishment period (typically the first full growing season, sometimes two) is when most problems occur. These are the ten most common failures and what to do about each one.

---

**Problem 1: "My plants died over winter."**

The cause is almost always insufficient root development before the ground froze, not actual cold intolerance. A native plant rated to Zone 4 that was planted in October of the same year it was potted may not have had time to establish a root system that can survive hard freezing — the roots were still at nursery size and did not extend far enough into the soil for stable anchoring and moisture access.

*Solutions*:
- Plant at least 6 weeks before your average first frost date. This gives roots time to push into the soil while the ground is still workable.
- Mulch the root zone 2–3 inches deep before winter (but keep mulch away from the crown — never pile it against the stem). Mulch moderates freeze-thaw cycling which is more damaging than simple cold.
- Water plants deeply before the ground freezes — hydrated roots survive freezing better than dry ones.
- If the problem recurs: suspect drainage. Plants in poorly-drained soil often fail their first winter from crown rot rather than cold per se. Improve drainage or relocate to a higher spot.

---

**Problem 2: "Nothing is germinating."**

Direct-seeded native plants often require cold stratification — a period of cold-moist conditions that mimics winter — before their seeds will sprout. If you sowed in spring without stratifying, most will not germinate until the following spring at the earliest.

*Solutions*:
- Check the species' stratification requirements before sowing. Cold stratification duration varies: 4 weeks for coneflower, 8–12 weeks for trilliums and bloodroot, 90+ days for viburnums and most tree species.
- Fall sowing is the simplest solution for cold-stratification species: sow outdoors in October–November and let winter provide the stratification naturally. Mark the bed clearly so you do not disturb it.
- If you sowed in spring without stratifying, do not assume failure — some cold-stratification species will lie dormant for a full year before germinating. Resist the urge to replant over the bed for at least 12 months.
- Check seed viability. Native plant seeds from reputable sources should have collection dates. Seeds older than 2–3 years (or dried seeds that required moist storage) may have low germination rates even with correct stratification.

---

**Problem 3: "Plants are being eaten."**

There are three primary culprits and the solution differs for each.

*Deer*: They are creatures of habit. If deer are eating your plants, they will return to the same spot every time unless actively deterred. The most effective deterrents: physical fencing (the only 100% reliable option), or the combination of strong-scent repellents applied consistently and rotated regularly (deer habituate to any single scent within 2–3 weeks). Plants that deer reliably avoid: ferns, native grasses, milkweeds, native mints (monarda, mountain mint), yarrow, goldenrod, and most native asters.

*Rabbits*: Most damaging to seedlings and new transplants in spring. Protection: wire cylinder guards around individual plants for the first growing season. Once stems are woody or plants are 12+ inches tall, rabbit pressure typically decreases.

*Voles and Mice*: Underground damage to roots and bulbs, typically in late winter under snow cover. Signs: plant fails to emerge in spring despite apparent good health the previous fall; entire root system has been eaten. Prevention: hardware cloth barriers around roots of valuable specimens, or applying a granular repellent in fall before snow.

---

**Problem 4: "Plants are spreading too aggressively."**

Some natives spread by rhizome (underground runners) more vigorously than expected in garden soil. Common culprits: goldenrod, wild bergamot, common milkweed, boneset, native sunflowers, and Virginia wild rye in moist-fertile conditions.

*Solutions*:
- Rhizomatous spreaders are much better-behaved in poor, dry soil than in rich, amended garden soil. If you have a vigorous spreader, try moving it to a lower-fertility site.
- Edging: burying a rhizome barrier 8–12 inches deep around the plant's perimeter (a technique used for bamboo) is effective for aggressive spreaders like goldenrod and mountain mint.
- Annual division and editing: pulling or digging out unwanted shoots in early spring, before they establish for the season, keeps most spreading natives contained without chemical intervention.
- Reconsider placement: aggressive spreaders belong in naturalized areas, meadow plantings, or wildlife gardens — not in tight border beds with other plants they will smother. The plant is not the problem; the placement is.

---

**Problem 5: "The plant looks healthy but never blooms."**

Three causes, in order of likelihood:

1. **Too much shade.** Most native wildflowers require full sun (6+ hours direct) to bloom well. A plant that performed well the first year and then stopped blooming is often indicating that nearby trees or shrubs have closed the canopy overhead. Solution: thin the canopy or relocate the plant.
2. **Juvenile period not yet complete.** Tree and shrub species (redbud, serviceberry, witch hazel, native viburnums) require several years before first bloom — this is normal. Herbaceous perennials that fail to bloom after 3 years in good sun are likely in wrong conditions.
3. **Nutrient imbalance.** Excess nitrogen produces abundant leaves but suppresses flowering. If the plant is near a lawn that receives fertilizer applications, or in heavily amended soil, reduce nitrogen inputs. Most natives actually perform better in lean, lower-fertility soil.

---

**Problem 6: "I can't get anything to establish in my clay soil."**

Clay soil is not the death sentence it is often treated as. Many native plants prefer it. The mistake is planting natives in compacted, anaerobic clay — which is a drainage problem, not a texture problem.

*Solutions*:
- Test drainage before planting: dig a hole 12 inches deep and fill with water. If it drains in 1 hour, drainage is adequate. If water sits for 4+ hours, drainage is impaired and most plants (including many natives) will struggle.
- For impaired drainage: either install a small rain garden that embraces wet conditions (using species from the rain garden list above), or break up compaction with a broad fork or subsoiler and incorporate organic matter to open channels.
- Clay-adapted natives that establish readily in unamended heavy clay: ironweed, joe-pye weed, swamp milkweed, blue wild indigo, New England aster, cardinal flower, and most wetland sedges.

---

**Problem 7: "The deer ate everything that wasn't deer-resistant on my list."**

This is a list problem, not a plant problem. The concept of deer-resistant plants depends heavily on deer pressure, season, and available alternatives. A plant rated deer-resistant in the Midwest may be preferred browse in the Mid-Atlantic where deer density is three times higher.

*The honest rule*: In high deer pressure areas (more than 20 deer per square mile), assume nothing is fully deer-proof except thorny shrubs, ferns, and strongly aromatic herbs (mints, yarrows, native salvias). Plan accordingly: either fence the entire planting area (8-foot minimum) or focus exclusively on the genuinely resistant species and accept that everything else will need protection.

---

**Problem 8: "My seedlings looked great, then suddenly collapsed and died."**

This is almost certainly damping-off — a fungal condition caused by *Pythium*, *Fusarium*, or *Rhizoctonia* species that attacks stems at the soil surface. It moves fast and is nearly always fatal to the affected seedlings.

*Solutions*:
- Prevention is the only real answer. Once damping-off starts in a flat, the affected seedlings cannot be saved.
- Use a sterile seed-starting mix, not garden soil or compost, for starting seeds indoors.
- Do not overwater. Let the surface dry slightly between waterings.
- Ensure air circulation around seedlings. A small fan running on low helps dramatically.
- If damping-off appears, remove affected seedlings immediately and allow the flat to dry down before rewatering. Healthy seedlings may survive if air circulation is improved quickly.

---

**Problem 9: "I planted natives and now I have more weeds than before."**

New plantings with bare soil between plants are an open invitation for weed seed germination. The plants are not the problem; the bare soil is.

*Solutions*:
- Mulch bare soil between plants immediately after planting (2–3 inches of wood chip mulch is standard). This alone eliminates most weed pressure.
- Plant densely enough that the native species close the canopy over bare ground within 2 growing seasons. Sparse plantings with 3-foot gaps between plugs will be overrun by annual weeds in the first year.
- Learn to distinguish your natives from the weeds early. Many native seedlings look weedy before they mature. Photograph new plantings at installation so you know exactly what and where things are.
- Accept that the first year will require active weeding. The second year requires much less. By year 3, a well-planted native planting typically out-competes most annual weeds on its own.

---

**Problem 10: "Everything established fine, but my planting looks messy and my neighbors are complaining."**

Naturalistic native plantings look different from conventional ornamental beds — that is by design, not by accident. But "by design" is the key phrase. A planting that looks intentional reads differently than one that looks neglected.

*Solutions for keeping native plantings looking managed*:
- Define edges clearly with a crisp mow line, edging, or a low border. Even a very wild planting looks intentional when it has a clean edge.
- Include some visually structured plants (ornamental grasses, coneflowers, tall vertical species) that signal "this is a garden" rather than an oversight.
- Add a simple sign identifying the planting as a certified wildlife habitat or native garden — this shifts neighbor perception from "overgrown lot" to "intentional project."
- Cut everything down to 6–8 inches in late February rather than fall. This preserves winter structure (seed heads and standing stems support birds and overwintering beneficial insects) while making the planting look intentionally managed as it goes dormant.
- If you are in a jurisdiction with nuisance vegetation ordinances, research exemptions for certified native plantings. Many municipalities now have official exemption processes for documented native plant gardens.
