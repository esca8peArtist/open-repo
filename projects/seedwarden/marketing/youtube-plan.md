# Seedwarden YouTube Content Plan

---

## 1. Channel Strategy

### Channel Positioning

Most gardening channels on YouTube fall into two camps: cottagecore homesteaders with acres of land and a lifestyle to sell, or technical growers running through care schedules for houseplants. Seedwarden is neither.

**The positioning**: Seedwarden is the channel for people who live in cities, rent their apartments, and are starting to connect the dots between what they eat, who grows it, and what that means politically. The host is a mechanical engineer — someone who thinks in systems, asks why things are designed the way they are, and applies that same critical lens to food.

The differentiator is not just the growing content. It is the combination of:
- **Practical apartment-scale growing** — no backyard required, no assumption of land ownership
- **Honest political context** — why heirloom seeds matter, what seed patents actually do, who controls the food supply and why that is a problem
- **Community as the solution** — seed swaps, mutual aid networks, neighborhood growing projects, not just individual self-sufficiency

The brand voice is a knowledgeable friend who will help you start your first container of tomatoes and, while doing it, explain why Bayer/Monsanto owns a third of the world's seed patents. Never preachy. Just honest.

This channel is not about escaping the city. It is about making the city feed itself.

---

### Content Pillars

Every Seedwarden video should fall under one of these four pillars:

**Pillar 1: Grow It Anyway**
Practical how-to content for apartment and small-space growers. Containers, windows, grow lights, balconies, vertical space. The frame is always: you do not need land to grow food. This is the top-of-funnel content that brings in beginners through search.

**Pillar 2: Follow the Seed**
Explainer content about the food system — seed patents, corporate consolidation, hybrid vs. heirloom vs. GMO, the history of particular varieties, what seed saving actually does. This is the political and educational layer. It gives the channel a point of view that distinguishes it from generic gardening tutorials.

**Pillar 3: The Engineer's Garden**
Systems thinking applied to growing. How to build a self-watering container, how to model a grow light setup for a north-facing window, how to think about airflow in a small apartment. This leverages the host's professional background in a way that is genuinely useful and hard to fake.

**Pillar 4: Grow Together**
Community content — seed swaps, community gardens, interviews with local organizers, urban farming projects. Shows that food sovereignty is not an individual project. This pillar reinforces the brand's values and builds a community around the channel.

---

### Upload Cadence

For a solo creator with 5-10 hours per week available:

**Recommended**: One long-form video every two weeks, plus one Short per week.

Rationale: A 10-12 minute video — scripted, filmed, and edited to a reasonable standard — takes roughly 6-10 hours of solo work. Trying to publish weekly on that schedule burns out most creators within three months. Biweekly is sustainable and still gives the algorithm enough signal.

Shorts can be batched. Film three in one session, publish one per week. They can be off-cuts from long-form filming, standalone tips filmed in under 10 minutes, or quick reactions to news (seed patent ruling, recall, harvest moment). Budget 1-2 hours per week here.

**Rough weekly split**:
- Long-form weeks (alternating): 7-8 hours (scripting, filming, editing, thumbnail)
- Short-form weeks: 1-2 hours (film, quick edit, publish)
- Ongoing: 1 hour community engagement (comments, community tab)

Do not try to be everywhere. YouTube first. Build the archive before branching to other platforms.

---

### Monetization Path: YouTube to Etsy

YouTube's partner program (ad revenue) requires 1,000 subscribers and 4,000 watch hours. At the biweekly cadence with focused SEO, this is achievable in 6-12 months. But ad revenue is not the primary monetization goal — Etsy store conversion is.

**The funnel**:

1. **Discovery** — Someone searches "how to grow tomatoes in an apartment" or "heirloom seeds vs hybrid." They find a Seedwarden video. It is specific, useful, and has a voice they have not heard before.

2. **Trust** — The video is not a sales pitch. It teaches something real. The host mentions Seedwarden seeds naturally in context ("I used the Mortgage Lifter from the shop for this"). The channel trailer and About section explain what Seedwarden is.

3. **Conversion** — Every video ends with a specific call to action tied to what was just covered. "The seeds I grew in this video are in the Seedwarden shop — link in description." Not a generic plug; a specific one.

4. **Retention** — A link to a free seed-saving guide, newsletter, or Etsy announcement list keeps viewers in orbit between purchases.

**Key tactical points**:
- The Etsy store link goes in the first two lines of every video description (YouTube collapses descriptions after two lines on mobile)
- Pin a comment on every video with the relevant product link
- Use YouTube's end cards to link to a "shop" playlist or channel trailer that explains Seedwarden
- Mention specific seed varieties by name in titles when relevant — people search for specific heirlooms ("Cherokee Purple tomato seeds," "Dragon Tongue bean seeds")

YouTube is the trust engine. Etsy is where the trust converts. The goal is not to sell from the video — it is to make someone want to buy before the video ends.

## 3. YouTube Shorts Strategy

Shorts work differently than long-form. They are discovered through the Shorts feed, not search. The goal is to create strong standalone moments that make someone curious about the channel, not to summarize the long video. Three types work well here.

---

### Short 1: "Did you know this one thing about tomato seeds?"

**Type**: Curiosity/fact hook
**Concept**: Hold up a tomato. Squeeze a seed out into a jar of water. "This seed will sprout next spring. But only if I bought the right kind." Cut to showing two packets — one hybrid, one heirloom. "The word 'hybrid' on the packet means this doesn't work. Here's what to look for instead." Close with the label on an open-pollinated packet. Under 45 seconds.
**Why it works**: It is a practical tip wrapped in a surprising fact. The "only if I bought the right kind" line is the hook. It drives interest in the longer seed-saving video and in the Seedwarden shop.

---

### Short 2: "How much food I grew in my apartment this month"

**Type**: Results/proof
**Concept**: Flat lay or quick camera pan of one month's harvest on a kitchen counter. Fast cuts. Narrate in real numbers: "Two pounds of lettuce. Fourteen tomatoes. One bell pepper — that took three months, worth it. All of this from two containers and a $35 grow light." Final line: "Full setup guide on the channel." Under 50 seconds.
**Why it works**: Proof-of-concept content is highly shareable and drives subscribe intent. Apartment gardeners are skeptical that it produces anything real. Show them it does. No lecture required.

---

### Short 3: "The most important thing I look for on a seed packet"

**Type**: Quick practical tip
**Concept**: Hold up a seed packet. "Most people look at the picture. Here's what I look for." Zoom on the fine print. Find the words "open-pollinated" or "heirloom." "These two words mean you can save the seed and grow it again next year — for free, forever. Hybrid means you can't." Flip through three packets fast — one good, two not. "Start here." Under 40 seconds.
**Why it works**: It is immediately actionable, teaches something real, and positions the Seedwarden brand's entire value proposition in under a minute without saying "buy my seeds." The viewer who watches this and then visits the channel will immediately understand what Seedwarden is about.
