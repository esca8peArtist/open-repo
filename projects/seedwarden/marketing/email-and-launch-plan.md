## Deliverable 1: Lead Magnet

### Concept and Title

**"The Seedwarden Starter Pack: 5 Heirloom Varieties That Actually Thrive in Small Spaces"**

Subtitle: *A no-fluff guide to choosing, planting, and saving your first heirloom seeds — even if you've only got a balcony*

This is a 2–3 page illustrated PDF (can be created in Canva in an afternoon). It delivers immediate, actionable value — specific variety recommendations with brief growing notes — rather than generic advice someone could Google. The target reader is someone who is curious about heirloom seeds but feels slightly overwhelmed. This guide makes them feel capable before they spend a dollar.

---

### Outline

**Page 1: Introduction (half page)**
- One short paragraph from the founder: why heirloom seeds matter, what Seedwarden is, and why this guide exists
- A brief explanation of what makes a variety "small-space friendly" (compact habit, high yield per square foot, container-tolerant)

**Page 1–2: The 5 Varieties**

Each entry is a mini-profile (4–6 lines) covering:
- Variety name and what kind of plant it is
- Why it was chosen (flavor, resilience, history — lean into the story)
- Ideal container/bed size
- Days to harvest
- One seed-saving tip specific to that variety

Suggested varieties (adjust to match your actual product catalog):
1. Mortgage Lifter Tomato — the variety with a folk-hero backstory, forgiving for beginners
2. Dragon Tongue Bush Bean — no staking, fast producer, great for containers
3. Lemon Cucumber — compact vine, prolific, kids love them
4. Chioggia Beet — dual-purpose (greens + root), gorgeous, grows in 12" of soil
5. Speckled Roman Tomato — paste type, disease resistant, smaller plant than most tomatoes

**Page 2–3: Getting Started (half to full page)**
- A simple 4-step framework: Choose → Plant → Tend → Save
- A callout box: "Ready to go deeper? Browse the full Seedwarden guide collection at [your Etsy link]"
- A soft, non-pushy closing note with a reminder to reply to the delivery email if they have questions

**Design notes:**
- Keep it clean. One or two accent colors, a hand-drawn or botanical illustration element if possible
- No stock photo filler. Real, specific information earns trust faster than pretty padding
- File size under 5MB for easy delivery

---

### Email Platform Recommendation: Kit (formerly ConvertKit)

**Use Kit.** Here is why, for a creator in this exact situation:

- Free up to 10,000 subscribers — more than enough runway before you need to pay anything
- Built specifically for creators selling digital products; the workflow mental model matches what you are doing
- Visual automation builder makes the welcome sequence below easy to set up without technical skill
- Native landing page builder means you can create a lead magnet sign-up page without a website
- Integrates directly with Etsy and Gumroad if you ever want to automate purchase follow-ups
- Better deliverability than Mailchimp's free tier in practice
- Beehiiv is excellent but optimized for newsletter-first creators; Kit is the better fit here because you are product-first

**How to deliver the lead magnet:**
1. Upload the PDF to Google Drive or Dropbox and get a shareable link (anyone with link can view)
2. In Kit, create a new "Form" (landing page style) for the sign-up
3. Set up a single automation: Form submitted → Send welcome email → Welcome email contains the PDF link
4. That is the entirety of the technical setup. It takes about 45 minutes including form styling.

### Email 2 — Day 2
**Goal: Educate, build credibility, establish the "why" behind heirloom seeds**

**Subject:** The difference between an heirloom tomato and a lie

---

Hi [First Name],

I want to tell you about a tomato called Brandywine.

It's a big, ugly, slightly lopsided pink tomato that takes 90 days to produce. It bruises easily, it doesn't ship well, and grocery stores won't touch it. By every commercial metric it is a failure.

It also tastes like someone distilled the concept of summer into a piece of fruit.

Brandywine has been grown in American gardens since at least the 1880s. Nobody owns it. The seed has passed from gardener to gardener for over a century, and every person who has grown it and saved its seed has participated in something that's a little hard to name — a kind of continuity, a thread between people who never met.

This is why I care about heirloom seeds, and it's the lens through which Seedwarden was built.

The guides I create aren't just about growing instructions. They're about understanding what you're growing — the variety's history, what makes it distinctive, why it was worth saving in the first place. That context doesn't make the tomatoes taste better, but it changes the experience of growing them. You're participating in something larger than your garden bed.

A few things worth knowing about heirlooms vs. hybrids if you're newer to this:

**Heirloom:** Open-pollinated, stable genetics, seed-saving works — the plant you grow from saved seed will be the same as the parent. Usually 50+ years old by definition.

**Hybrid (F1):** Two varieties deliberately crossed for specific traits (uniformity, shelf life, disease resistance). Not bad plants — just not seed-saveable in a meaningful way. The next generation reverts to unpredictable traits.

**GMO:** A separate category entirely, involving lab-based gene modification. Not relevant to home gardening — GMO seed is not sold to home growers.

Most seed packets don't explain any of this. Most gardening content treats it as too nerdy to bother with. I think that's a mistake.

Next email I'll share something more practical — a mistake I made in my first year of seed saving that cost me an entire season of work, and what I learned from it.

[Your name]

### Email 3 — Day 5
**Goal: Vulnerability and trust-building, practical takeaway**

**Subject:** The mistake that wiped out a full season of seeds

---

Hi [First Name],

My first serious attempt at seed saving almost ended before it started.

I grew a beautiful crop of paste tomatoes — Amish Paste, which is a variety I still love — let them ripen fully on the vine, and saved the seeds using what I thought was a reasonable method: I squeezed them onto a paper towel and let them dry in the kitchen.

Seemed logical. Tomato seeds are small. Paper towel absorbs moisture. Done.

What I didn't know: tomato seeds are surrounded by a gel coating that contains germination inhibitors. That coating has to be removed through fermentation — you float the seeds in a small jar of water for 2–3 days until the viable seeds sink and the gel and mold float to the top. Then you rinse them, dry them properly on a ceramic plate (not paper — they stick), and store them in a cool, dry, dark place.

My paper towel seeds were contaminated, stuck to the towel, and had an abysmal germination rate the following spring. I got maybe 15% of what I should have.

That mistake is in the Amish Paste guide I sell on Etsy — not as a cautionary tale, but as part of the actual seed-saving instructions, because it's the kind of thing nobody puts on a seed packet and most articles skip over.

The guides I've built are heavy on this kind of detail. Not because I want to overwhelm anyone, but because the small procedural things — fermentation for tomato seeds, when exactly to harvest bean seeds (on the plant, not after), how to test old seed viability before you commit a whole bed — are what separate a frustrating first season from one that actually works.

If you want a look at what's in the shop, here's the Etsy link: [your Etsy shop URL]

## Deliverable 3: Six-Week Go-to-Market Launch Plan

### Honest Framing First

Organic growth on Etsy, Instagram, TikTok, and YouTube without an existing audience is slow. Weeks 1–6 will not produce meaningful follower counts. The realistic goal for this phase is:

- First Etsy sale within 4–6 weeks
- Email list of 50–150 subscribers by end of week 6 (if you are consistent with content)
- A small number of social followers who are genuinely interested (50–300 is realistic, not 3,000)
- Foundation in place that compounds over the next 6–12 months

The accounts that appear to "blow up fast" almost always have one of three things: a prior audience somewhere, paid ads, or a piece of content that hit an algorithm at the right moment by luck. Plan for slow and consistent. A well-built Etsy listing that ranks for the right keywords will generate sales for years. A viral video does not.

---

### Minimum Viable Launch

**The absolute minimum to get your first sale:**

1. One complete, polished Etsy listing with good photos (mockups of the PDF are fine), a thorough description, and the right keyword-rich title
2. The lead magnet PDF created and uploaded to Google Drive
3. A Kit account with the landing page live and the 5-email sequence set up
4. One piece of content — a single Instagram post, Reel, or TikTok — with a link to the sign-up page in bio
5. Post the link in 2–3 relevant Reddit communities (r/vegetablegardening, r/homesteading, r/urbangardening) with a genuine, non-spammy post

That is it. You could do all of this in a single focused weekend. Everything in the 6-week plan below is the fuller build-out.

---

### Week 1 — Foundation

**What to set up:**
- Kit account, lead magnet landing page, and welcome sequence (all 5 emails drafted and loaded)
- All 10 Etsy listings created. Do not launch yet — set them to draft. Use this week to write thorough descriptions and research keywords using Etsy's search bar autocomplete and eRank's free tier
- Create 3–5 Pinterest boards relevant to your content (heirloom seeds, urban gardening, container growing, seed saving). Pinterest drives Etsy traffic in this category and is worth setting up before launch
- Brand your Etsy shop: banner, shop icon, shop announcement, About section. These affect conversion once people arrive

**What to publish:**
- One Instagram post introducing Seedwarden (founder photo or flat-lay of seeds/notes, short honest caption about why you started this)
- Pin the lead magnet landing page link to the top of each Pinterest board as a "save this" post

**Metrics to watch:**
- Did you finish the setup? That is the only metric this week.

**Realistic expectation:** Zero sales, zero subscribers yet. That is normal.

---

### Week 2 — Soft Launch

**What to do:**
- Publish all 10 Etsy listings
- Announce the lead magnet on Instagram (post + Stories). One clear CTA: "link in bio to download"
- Post one TikTok or Reel: "5 heirloom varieties for small spaces" — short, direct, shows your face or your hands working with seeds/soil
- Share the lead magnet landing page link on Reddit in 2–3 relevant communities. Write a genuine post about heirloom seed saving or small-space growing. Drop the link naturally in the comments or at the end as "I made a free guide on this if it's useful." Do not lead with the link or it will be removed.
- Email your personal network (family, friends, anyone who knows you). Not asking for sales — asking them to sign up for the list if they have any interest in gardening, and to share with one person if they know someone who might care

**What to publish:**
- 2 Instagram posts (one Reel if possible)
- 1 TikTok
- 2–3 Pinterest pins linking to your Etsy listings

**Metrics to watch:**
- Etsy: listing views, number of favorites
- Kit: number of sign-ups, email open rate on welcome sequence
- Social: reach on the Reel/TikTok (not followers — reach is more informative early on)

**Realistic expectation:** 10–40 email sign-ups, 5–20 Etsy favorites, possibly your first sale. Do not be discouraged if no sale yet.

---

### Week 3 — Content and SEO

**What to do:**
- Audit your Etsy listings using eRank (free tier). Look at which keywords you are ranking for and which listings have zero views. Rewrite titles and tags for any listing with zero views after 5+ days
- Create a YouTube "evergreen" video: something like "How to save tomato seeds at home" or "Heirloom vs. hybrid seeds — what actually matters." Long-form YouTube content takes months to rank but becomes a permanent traffic source. Start now.
- Write a 3–5 sentence "micro-blog" Instagram caption on a specific growing topic (not promotional). These outperform promotional posts in saves and shares.

**What to publish:**
- 1 YouTube video (do not obsess over production quality — clear audio and steady shot are the only technical requirements)
- 2–3 Instagram posts
- 1 TikTok (can repurpose a clip from YouTube)
- 5 Pinterest pins

**Metrics to watch:**
- Etsy: which listings are getting views; which are getting clicks to checkout; which are getting no views at all
- YouTube: watch time percentage (aim for above 40%)

**Realistic expectation:** Maybe 1–3 sales by end of this week if your listings are well-optimized and you have had some Reddit/social traction. Email list may be at 30–80.

---

### Week 4 — Community and Conversation

**What to do:**
- Spend time genuinely participating in communities: answer questions on Reddit, engage with content from other seed and gardening accounts on Instagram (real comments, not "great post!"). This is relationship-building, not growth-hacking. The payoff is slow.
- Send your first non-welcome email to your list: a single growing tip or short story about something happening in your garden right now. No sell. Just value. This maintains the relationship between the welcome sequence and future promotional emails.
- Review your Etsy shop statistics. Which listings are converting (views → sales) and which are getting views but no purchases? Listings with views and no purchases usually have a pricing or description problem, not a traffic problem.
- Create a second YouTube video if time allows

**What to publish:**
- Email to list (non-promotional)
- 2–3 Instagram posts
- 1 TikTok
- Continue Pinterest

**Metrics to watch:**
- Etsy conversion rate per listing (views divided by orders)
- Email open rate and click rate
- Any referral sources appearing in Etsy stats (Pinterest, social, direct search)

**Realistic expectation:** 3–8 total sales, list at 50–120. You will start to see which listings get traction and which do not. That data is valuable.

---

### Week 5 — Double Down on What's Working

**What to do:**
- By now you have data. Which Etsy listing has the most views? Create a second listing in the same topic area. Which social post got the most saves? Make a variation of it.
- Record a TikTok or Reel that directly references the lead magnet and drives sign-ups. Something like: "I made a free guide to the 5 heirloom varieties I'd start with if I were starting over — it's free, link in bio"
- Plan a small "launch moment" for Week 6: a short email campaign (2 emails: a teaser and a direct offer) and a coordinated social post pushing to your best-selling Etsy listing

**What to publish:**
- 2–3 Instagram posts
- 1–2 TikToks
- 1 YouTube video (if you have bandwidth)
- Continue Pinterest

**Metrics to watch:**
- Which content is driving Etsy traffic (check Etsy's "traffic sources" in shop stats)
- TikTok: any video that breaks 500 views organically signals a topic worth repeating

**Realistic expectation:** Steady trickle of sales (1–3/week if Etsy SEO is working). Email list 80–150.

---

### Week 6 — First Real Push

**What to do:**
- Send two emails to your list:
  - **Day 1:** "I've been heads-down building — here's what's in the shop and who it's for" (honest, specific, low pressure)
  - **Day 4:** Direct offer with coupon code SEEDWARDEN15 (the same one from Email 5 in the welcome sequence — new sign-ups from this week will see it fresh; older subscribers get a second reminder with a slightly different framing)
- Post a "Shop spotlight" Reel or TikTok showing your top 3 guides, what's inside, and who they're for
- Run your first Pinterest promoted pin if you have $20–$30 to test paid. Pinterest ads in the gardening/homesteading niche are cheap and well-targeted. Not required, but highest ROI paid channel for this product type.
- Thank your first customers. If anyone has purchased, send them a personal message on Etsy thanking them and asking if they have questions. Reviews are critical on Etsy and a personal note significantly increases review rate.

**What to publish:**
- 2 emails
- 1–2 Instagram posts
- 1 Reel or TikTok
- Pinterest (organic + optional paid)

**Metrics to watch:**
- Email click-through rate on both launch emails
- Etsy: daily orders, revenue, and favorites this week vs. last week
- Total reviews received

**Realistic expectation at end of Week 6:**
- Total sales: 8–25 (highly variable; Etsy SEO takes 4–8 weeks to build)
- Email list: 100–200 subscribers
- Social: 100–500 followers across platforms (Instagram likely leads)
- Revenue: $80–$300 (at $7–$15 per guide)

This is not a big number. It is a real foundation that, if continued consistently for 6–12 more months, builds into something meaningful. The creators who succeed with this model are the ones who treat month 2 the same as month 6 — consistent content, consistent optimization, consistent engagement.

---

### After Week 6: What Matters Most

In order of impact for long-term Etsy sales:

1. **Etsy SEO** — Every view from Etsy search is free and compounds over time. Invest in understanding keywords. eRank's free tier is sufficient.
2. **Email list** — Your most valuable asset. A list of 500 engaged subscribers is worth more than 5,000 social followers.
3. **Pinterest** — Underrated for this niche. A well-pinned guide can drive Etsy traffic for years from a single pin.
4. **YouTube** — Slowest to build, highest long-term leverage. A video ranking for "how to save tomato seeds" gets watched forever.
5. **TikTok/Instagram** — Good for brand and list growth; direct Etsy conversion is lower than people expect.
