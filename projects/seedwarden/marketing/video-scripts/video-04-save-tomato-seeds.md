## SCRIPT

**[SCENE 1 — HOOK]**
VISUAL: A glass jar filled with murky liquid and seeds, sitting on a kitchen windowsill. It looks slightly gross — intentionally. Text appears: "This is supposed to look like this." Then a fast-forward animation: the liquid clears, seeds settle, they get rinsed, dried, and stored in a clean envelope. The whole process in 3 seconds.
NARRATION: Saving tomato seeds wrong means they'll rot in storage, fail to germinate, or carry disease into next year's garden. The right method involves fermentation — which sounds complicated but is genuinely one of the easiest things you can do in a kitchen. Here's the full process.
DURATION: 14 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Wrong method = seeds that rot. Right method = seeds that last years.

---

**[SCENE 2 — WHY FERMENTATION?]**
VISUAL: A cross-section of a tomato seed illustrated in detail. Around the seed, a gel coating glows and pulses — labeled "Germination-Inhibiting Gel Coat." An animation shows the gel dissolving during fermentation. Another panel: a healthy seed without the gel, next to a germination sequence.
NARRATION: Here's why tomatoes specifically need this method. A tomato seed is naturally coated in a gel-like substance that inhibits germination — it's a survival mechanism that prevents the seed from sprouting inside the fruit. In nature, a tomato rots on the ground, fermentation happens, the gel breaks down, and the seed is ready to germinate in spring. We're just replicating that process deliberately, in a jar, in about three days.
DURATION: 24 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Gel coat = natural germination inhibitor. Fermentation breaks it down. We're copying nature.

---

**[SCENE 3 — WHAT YOU NEED]**
VISUAL: A clean illustrated flat-lay of the supplies. Simple, minimal. Each item gets a label that pops in.
NARRATION: What you need: a ripe tomato from an open-pollinated or heirloom variety — this only works with non-hybrid seeds. A knife and a spoon. A small jar or cup. Water. A fine mesh strainer. Paper towels or newspaper for drying. And small envelopes or paper coin envelopes for storage. That's it. You probably have most of this already.
DURATION: 18 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: You need: Ripe heirloom tomato. Knife + spoon. Small jar. Water. Fine mesh strainer. Paper towels. Storage envelopes.

---

**[SCENE 4 — STEP 1: CHOOSE YOUR TOMATO]**
VISUAL: Character walks through a garden, examining tomatoes. They choose one that's deeply, fully ripe — past table-ripe. It's heavy, deeply colored, slightly soft. They hold it up. A checkmark appears. Then a comparison: a slightly underripe tomato gets an X.
NARRATION: Step one: choose the right tomato. You want one that's fully ripe — not just ripe enough to eat, but deeply ripe. For red varieties, that means deeply red, almost past peak. For heirlooms like Cherokee Purple or Black Krim, look for softness and full color development. The seed inside is fully mature when the fruit is fully ripe. Don't rush this.
DURATION: 20 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Choose: deeply ripe fruit. Not just table-ripe. Fully mature = fully ripe seeds.

---

**[SCENE 5 — STEP 2: EXTRACT THE SEEDS]**
VISUAL: Illustrated step-by-step hands. The tomato is sliced in half across the equator (not top to bottom). Seeds visible in the gel chambers. A spoon scoops the seeds and gel into a small jar. Clean, simple animation.
NARRATION: Step two: extract the seeds. Slice the tomato across its equator — that exposes all the seed chambers at once. Then use a spoon to scoop the seeds and the surrounding gel into a small jar or cup. Don't worry about being neat. Get it all in there — the gel is what you need.
DURATION: 18 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Slice across the equator (not top to bottom). Scoop seeds AND gel into jar.

---

**[SCENE 6 — STEP 3: ADD WATER AND WAIT]**
VISUAL: Water being added to the jar — just enough to roughly double the volume. The jar gets a loose paper towel cover fastened with a rubber band (keeps out bugs, lets it breathe). Placed on a countertop or windowsill. A small label written in marker: variety name and date. Then a calendar animates — 2 to 3 days marked off.
NARRATION: Step three: add water and wait. Add roughly the same volume of water as you have seed goop. Mix it loosely. Cover the jar with paper towel secured with a rubber band — you need airflow but you don't want bugs or debris getting in. Label it with the variety name and date. Then leave it somewhere at room temperature for two to three days.
DURATION: 22 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Add equal volume of water. Cover loosely — needs air. Label: variety + date. Wait 2–3 days at room temp.

---

**[SCENE 7 — WHAT HAPPENS DURING FERMENTATION]**
VISUAL: Time-lapse animation of the jar over three days. Day 1: murky, seeds suspended. Day 2: foam or mold starts forming on top — looks gross. Seeds start sinking. Day 3: clear liquid on top, dense layer of seeds on the bottom, mold/debris floating.
NARRATION: Here's what you'll see. A layer of mold or foam forms on the surface — that's completely normal, that's the fermentation working. The gel coating breaks down. Good viable seeds sink to the bottom. Non-viable seeds, debris, and gel float. By day two or three, you'll see a clear separation.
DURATION: 20 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Day 1: murky, seeds suspended. Day 2: foam forms on top — normal. Day 3: seeds sink, debris floats. That's what success looks like.

---

**[SCENE 8 — STEP 4: RINSE]**
VISUAL: Illustrated hands pour the jar through a fine mesh strainer. Then the strainer is held under running water. The mold and debris wash away. The seeds left in the strainer are clean and distinct.
NARRATION: Step four: rinse. Pour the jar through a fine mesh strainer. Run water over the seeds, using your fingers or a spoon to wash off any remaining gel and debris. Rinse until the water running through is clear and the seeds feel clean. This takes about a minute.
DURATION: 16 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Pour through fine mesh strainer. Rinse under water until clear. Seeds should feel clean, not slippery.

---

**[SCENE 9 — STEP 5: DRY]**
VISUAL: Clean seeds spread onto a paper towel or piece of newspaper. Then an illustration of seeds on a ceramic plate. A window with airflow illustrated behind it. Two common mistakes appear with red Xs: seeds piled in a heap (not spread out), seeds sitting on paper towel too long (they stick). A fan on low circulates air in the room.
NARRATION: Step five: dry them properly. Spread the seeds in a single layer on a ceramic plate or on newspaper. Not paper towels — seeds stick to them permanently. Spread them out so they're not touching. Let them dry at room temperature with good airflow for at least a week, two weeks is better. Don't use a dehydrator or oven — heat kills germination. Don't rush this step. Underdried seeds rot in storage.
DURATION: 24 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Spread thin on ceramic plate or newspaper. Not paper towels — seeds stick. Room temp, good airflow. 1–2 weeks to dry. No dehydrator. No oven.

---

**[SCENE 10 — HOW TO KNOW THEY'RE READY]**
VISUAL: Character picks up a seed and tries to bend it. It snaps cleanly — checkmark. Then the bad version: a seed that bends without breaking — red X, still has moisture.
NARRATION: How do you know they're fully dry? Pick up a single seed and try to bend it. A properly dried tomato seed will snap clean — like a tiny piece of dry pasta. If it bends without breaking, it still has moisture. Give it more time. This step matters. Moisture in storage is how seeds rot.
DURATION: 18 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Properly dry = snaps cleanly when bent. Bends without breaking = still wet. More time needed.

---

**[SCENE 11 — STEP 6: STORE]**
VISUAL: Illustrated storage options. Paper coin envelopes with variety name and year written on them. Then these envelopes inside a glass jar with a silica gel packet. Then the jar going into a cool, dark drawer. Optional: a jar in the fridge.
NARRATION: Step six: store correctly. Paper envelopes work better than plastic bags — paper lets the seeds breathe and reduces condensation risk. Write the variety name and year on the envelope. Store them somewhere cool, dark, and dry — a drawer works, a basement works, a refrigerator works even better. Keep them away from heat and humidity. Tomato seeds stored properly will stay viable for 4 to 6 years.
DURATION: 22 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Paper envelopes (not plastic). Label: variety + year. Cool, dark, dry = 4–6 years viability. Fridge is ideal.

---

**[SCENE 12 — VIABILITY TESTING]**
VISUAL: A damp paper towel laid flat. Ten seeds placed on it, spaced out. The towel is rolled up loosely. Placed in a plastic bag. Set aside for a week. Then unrolled: 8 of the 10 seeds have germinated — tiny white tails visible. Text: "80% germination rate — strong."
NARRATION: One more thing: viability testing. Before you plant a batch of old seeds, do a quick germination test. Place ten seeds on a damp paper towel, roll it up, keep it in a plastic bag at room temperature for a week. Count how many sprout. Eight out of ten is strong. Six out of ten is fine, just plant more densely. Below five, the batch is aging — use it this season and save fresh seeds.
DURATION: 22 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Germination test: 10 seeds on damp paper towel. Keep in bag, room temp, 1 week. 8/10 = strong. 6/10 = plant densely. Below 5/10 = use this season.

---

**[SCENE 13 — RECAP]**
VISUAL: Six illustrated steps appear in a clean grid layout, each with a small icon and label. Numbered 1–6.
NARRATION: Let's recap. One: choose a fully ripe open-pollinated tomato. Two: scoop seeds and gel into a jar with water. Three: ferment at room temp for two to three days. Four: rinse through a strainer. Five: dry for one to two weeks on a plate — no paper towels, no heat. Six: store in paper envelopes, cool and dry.
DURATION: 20 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: 1. Ripe heirloom tomato. 2. Seeds + gel into jar with water. 3. Ferment 2–3 days. 4. Rinse. 5. Dry 1–2 weeks (plate, no heat). 6. Paper envelope, cool + dry.
