**[SCENE 3 — VARIETY SELECTION: THE MOST IMPORTANT DECISION]**
VISUAL: A large tomato plant (illustrated, massive, sprawling) next to a small one. The large one has an X over it. The small one has a checkmark. Bold labels: "Indeterminate (vines forever)" and "Determinate / Compact (stays manageable)."
NARRATION: Variety selection is the most important decision you'll make, and it happens before you buy anything else. Avoid large indeterminate varieties — these are plants that vine forever, need 6-foot cages, and want 30 gallons of soil. Great for a garden bed. Not for a balcony. What you want are determinate or compact varieties.
DURATION: 20 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Indeterminate = vines forever. NOT for containers. Determinate / Compact = manageable. YES for containers.

**[SCENE 4 — VARIETY RECOMMENDATIONS]**
VISUAL: Illustrated seed packets appear one by one, each with a character portrait of the tomato — a small illustration of what the fruit looks like. Bold variety names animate in. Color-coded by type (cherry, paste, slicer).
NARRATION: For containers, these are varieties that actually work. For cherry tomatoes: Tumbling Tom — it cascades beautifully over the edge of a pot. Tiny Tim — stays under 18 inches tall. For slicers: Patio — bred specifically for container growing. Bush Early Girl — compact, productive, reliable. For something with more character: Glacier — an heirloom that produces early and handles cool weather well. All of these are also open-pollinated, which means you can save seeds from them. **[SCENE 5 — CONTAINER SIZE: DO NOT CHEAP OUT]**
VISUAL: A series of pots lined up, labeled by size: 1 gallon, 5 gallons, 10 gallons, 15 gallons, 25 gallons. A tiny sad tomato in the 1-gallon. A thriving plant in the 15-gallon. An arrow with bold text: "Go bigger than you think."
NARRATION: Container size is where most people make their first mistake. They use whatever pot they have. The root system of a tomato plant is enormous — it wants to spread. In a small container, roots get bound, water stress is constant, and fruit production tanks. For most container varieties, the minimum is 10 gallons. I use 15-gallon fabric pots for compact varieties. They're cheap, they drain well, and they air-prune the roots which actually improves plant health.
DURATION: 26 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Minimum: 10 gallons. Better: 15 gallons. Fabric pots = better drainage + healthier roots. Go bigger than you think.

---

**[SCENE 6 — SOIL]**
VISUAL: Two bags illustrated side by side. One labeled "Basic potting mix" — looking average, plain. One labeled "Container mix + amendments" — illustrated with small icons for perlite, compost, slow-release fertilizer. Character weighs them in their hands.
NARRATION: Don't use garden soil or basic cheap potting mix in containers. Garden soil compacts, drains badly, and stresses roots in a pot. You want a quality potting mix designed for containers — something with perlite for drainage. Then amend it: a slow-release granular fertilizer mixed in at planting, and worm castings or compost if you can find them. Tomatoes are heavy feeders. Feed them.
DURATION: 22 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Use: container potting mix + perlite. Add: slow-release fertilizer + compost. Tomatoes are heavy feeders.

---

**[SCENE 7 — LIGHT: THE NON-NEGOTIABLE]**
VISUAL: A sun tracker animation — showing the sun's arc across a sky. An apartment balcony shown from above with the sun's shadow mapped at different times. Hours of direct light counted: 4 hours, 6 hours, 8 hours. A plant illustrated at each level: struggling, okay, thriving.
NARRATION: Here's the non-negotiable: tomatoes need at least 6 hours of direct sunlight per day. Not bright indirect light. Direct. If your balcony gets 4 hours or fewer, tomatoes are going to disappoint you — it's physics, not technique. Before you invest in soil and containers, track how many hours of direct sun your space actually gets. Stand there with a timer. It's the most important thing you can do.
DURATION: 24 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Minimum: 6 hours direct sun per day. 4 hours or fewer = wrong crop. Track your sun first.

---

**[SCENE 8 — WHAT TO DO WITH LOW LIGHT]**
VISUAL: A small balcony with limited sun. But then: a small grow light appears above the pot. Character adjusts it. Plant perks up. Then a second option: the character moves the pot to a different spot on the balcony that gets more direct sun.
NARRATION: If your space gets under 6 hours: option one, move to the sunniest spot possible — sometimes just shifting a pot a few feet makes a real difference. Option two, supplement with a grow light. A basic full-spectrum LED panel costs about 30 dollars and can add 2-3 hours of effective light. Option three, pick a different crop — lettuce, herbs, and kale will thrive with 4 hours where tomatoes won't.
DURATION: 22 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Under 6 hours sun: Move the pot. Add a grow light (~$30). Or: grow lettuce/herbs instead.

---

**[SCENE 9 — WATERING]**
VISUAL: A moisture meter illustrated as a simple gauge — dry, moist, wet. Container soil shown drying out in layers from the top down. Then two failure modes: a wilted plant (too dry), a yellowing plant with soggy soil (too wet). Character puts a finger into the soil to check.
NARRATION: Containers dry out faster than garden beds — sometimes every single day in summer heat. Check daily. Stick your finger an inch into the soil — if it's dry, water. If it's still damp, wait. When you water, water deeply — until it drains out the bottom. That means the whole root zone is wet. Shallow watering just wets the top inch and leaves the roots dry. And don't let your containers sit in standing water — roots need air too.
DURATION: 25 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Check soil daily. Water when top inch is dry. Water deeply — until it drains. No standing water.

---

**[SCENE 10 — FERTILIZING]**
VISUAL: A tomato plant growth timeline: seedling, flowering, fruiting. At each stage, a different fertilizer icon appears. Early stage: balanced NPK (illustrated as 3 equal bars). Flowering: lower nitrogen, higher phosphorus/potassium (bar chart shifts). Fruiting: same.
NARRATION: Fertilizing matters more in containers because nutrients wash out every time you water. Early in the season when the plant is growing, a balanced fertilizer is fine. Once you see flowers, back off the nitrogen — too much nitrogen in the fruiting stage gives you a beautiful plant with almost no fruit. Shift to a fertilizer higher in phosphorus and potassium. Liquid fertilizers every two weeks work well for containers.
DURATION: 24 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Growing: balanced NPK. Flowering + fruiting: lower nitrogen, higher P+K. Liquid feed every 2 weeks.

---

**[SCENE 11 — COMMON MISTAKES, QUICK FIRE]**
VISUAL: Quick-cut illustrated cards, each showing a mistake with a red X and then the fix with a green checkmark. Fast rhythm, almost listicle energy.
NARRATION: Quick mistakes to avoid. One: planting too early. Container tomatoes still need warm nights — under 50 degrees consistently damages them. Wait until nighttime temps are reliably above 50. Two: not hardening off. If you start seeds indoors, ease the plant into outdoor conditions over a week — don't just move it outside. Three: ignoring pests. Aphids and whiteflies love container tomatoes. Check the undersides of leaves weekly. Four: overcrowding. One tomato per large container. Resist the urge to cram more in.
DURATION: 28 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Don't: Plant too early (wait for 50°F nights). Don't: Skip hardening off. Do: Check for pests weekly. One plant per large container.

Pick it, save the seeds using the fermentation method, and you've got next year's garden in a jar. We have a whole video on exactly how to do that.

ON-SCREEN TEXT: For seed saving: let one tomato go deeply ripe. Past table-ripe = peak seed viability.
