# Video 02: How 3 Companies Took Over the World's Food Supply
**Brand:** Seedwarden
**Format:** Animated explainer / cartoonish infographic
**Target Length:** 6–8 minutes
**CTA:** Food Sovereignty Starter Guide

---

## SCRIPT

---

**[SCENE 1 — HOOK]**
VISUAL: A stylized world map fills the screen. Thousands of tiny illustrated seed packets scatter across it — small, colorful, varied. Text counter in corner: "1970: ~1,000 independent seed companies." Then a slow animation: packets start vanishing, gobbled up by three growing blobs of color spreading across the map. Time-lapse feel. The counter ticks down as the blobs grow.
NARRATION: In 1970, there were thousands of independent seed companies around the world. Regional businesses. Family operations. Companies that had spent generations developing varieties for specific soils and climates. Today, three corporations control over 60% of the global seed market. Here's how that happened — and what it actually means.
DURATION: 22 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: 1970: ~1,000 independent seed companies. Today: 3 corporations = 60%+ of global seeds.

---

**[SCENE 2 — WHAT SEEDS ACTUALLY ARE]**
VISUAL: A single seed on a black background. It cracks open. A tiny plant emerges, then a full plant, then fruits, then new seeds. Simple cycle animation. Then zoom out: the same animation repeated thousands of times across the screen — a whole food system built on that one moment.
NARRATION: Seeds are the foundation of the food system. Everything that gets eaten — by humans and animals — starts with a seed. Whoever controls the seeds controls what gets grown, where it gets grown, and who can afford to grow it. That makes the seed industry one of the most strategically important industries on earth.
DURATION: 20 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Seeds = Foundation of the food system. Control seeds → control food.

---

**[SCENE 3 — THE 1970s: DIVERSITY ERA]**
VISUAL: Illustrated collage of small, regional seed companies. Mom-and-pop storefronts. Illustrated characters: an elderly woman with soil-stained hands, a farmer at a county fair, a small catalog with hundreds of variety names. Warm, nostalgic color palette — sepia tones mixed with muted greens.
NARRATION: For most of the 20th century, seeds were largely a local and regional business. Small companies developed varieties suited to local climates. Farmers saved seeds across generations. Seed catalogs were thick with options — hundreds of varieties of every vegetable, adapted to everywhere from Georgia to Minnesota to Oregon.
DURATION: 20 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Seeds were local. Diverse. Community-held.

---

**[SCENE 4 — THE CHANGE: SEED PATENTS]**
VISUAL: A courtroom-style illustrated scene. A judge's gavel comes down. A seed packet gets a patent stamp on it — like a legal document overlay. The stamp reads: "U.S. Patent No. XXXXXXX." A dollar sign floats up from it.
NARRATION: The shift began in 1980, when the U.S. Supreme Court ruled in Diamond v. Chakrabarty that living organisms — including plants and seeds — could be patented. That decision opened a door. If you could own a seed variety the way you own an invention, seeds stopped being a commons and became intellectual property.
DURATION: 22 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: 1980: Diamond v. Chakrabarty. Seeds can be patented. Seeds become intellectual property.

---

**[SCENE 5 — WHAT A SEED PATENT MEANS]**
VISUAL: A farmer's field. An illustrated farmer holds a bag of patented seeds. A large corporation logo hovers above them like a blimp. Fine print scrolls across the bottom of the screen — humorously tiny. Then two panels: Panel 1: "Can you save these seeds?" Farmer shakes head, red X. Panel 2: "Can you replant without buying again?" Red X.
NARRATION: When a seed is patented, the company that owns the patent controls how it's used. Farmers who plant patented seeds — which is most commercial seeds now — sign licensing agreements that forbid them from saving seeds to replant. Every season, they have to buy again. From the same company. It's a subscription model for food production.
DURATION: 24 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Patented seeds = buy every year. No saving. No replanting without permission. Subscription farming.

---

**[SCENE 6 — THE CONSOLIDATION TIMELINE]**
VISUAL: A horizontal timeline animates from left (1960) to right (today). Key acquisitions light up as animated dots, with company logos (stylized, not exact corporate logos — think iconic shapes) connecting via lines. The visual gets increasingly tangled as acquisitions compound. By the end it's a web, then it simplifies into three labeled nodes: Bayer/Monsanto, Corteva (Dow/DuPont), ChemChina/Syngenta.
NARRATION: Starting in the 1990s and accelerating through the 2000s, large chemical and agricultural companies began buying up seed companies. Monsanto bought DeKalb, Holden's, Asgrow, and dozens of others. DuPont and Dow merged their ag divisions. Syngenta was acquired by ChemChina. By 2018, after Bayer purchased Monsanto for 63 billion dollars, the consolidation was essentially complete. Three conglomerates now control the majority of commercial seeds on earth.
DURATION: 30 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: 1990s–2018: Rapid consolidation. Bayer acquired Monsanto for $63 billion (2018). Three players remain: Bayer/Monsanto. Corteva (Dow+DuPont). ChemChina/Syngenta.

---

**[SCENE 7 — WHAT THESE COMPANIES ACTUALLY SELL]**
VISUAL: A Venn diagram. Three overlapping circles: "Seeds," "Pesticides," "Fertilizers." In the center overlap: a dollar sign. Each circle fills in with color as the narration mentions it.
NARRATION: Here's what's important to understand about why these companies wanted seeds: they're the same companies that sell pesticides and fertilizers. When you own the seed, you can engineer it to require your herbicide. Or to perform best with your fertilizer. You're not just selling one product — you're selling a system. And the farmer who buys your seed is locked into that system.
DURATION: 22 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Seeds + Pesticides + Fertilizers = Integrated lock-in.

---

**[SCENE 8 — THE ROUNDUP READY EXAMPLE]**
VISUAL: A soybean field. Half the screen shows conventional soybeans — a farmer weeding by hand. The other half shows Roundup Ready soybeans — a tractor spraying herbicide, weeds dying. Then the reveal: both the seed and the herbicide have the same company logo. Two sales, one customer.
NARRATION: The most well-known example is Roundup Ready crops — corn, soybeans, canola — engineered to survive being sprayed with Monsanto's Roundup herbicide. The pitch to farmers was: buy our seed, spray our herbicide, skip the hand weeding. It worked. Roundup Ready crops now cover hundreds of millions of acres. And every acre is a customer for both products.
DURATION: 24 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Roundup Ready: engineered to survive Monsanto's own herbicide. Same company. Both products.

---

**[SCENE 9 — WHAT THIS MEANS FOR SMALL FARMERS]**
VISUAL: Two illustrated farmers. One: small scale, heirloom seeds, hand tools, farmer's market in background. One: large scale, surrounded by bags of branded seed and pesticide, a bank loan notice on the table. The small farmer's operation gets smaller. The large farmer's costs keep rising, illustrated as a stacking bar chart of input costs.
NARRATION: For small and mid-scale farmers, this consolidation has been brutal. Seed prices have risen dramatically — in some cases more than 300% since the early 2000s. The varieties farmers relied on for generations get discontinued if they're not commercially profitable. And farmers who want to grow outside the system have fewer and fewer options from mainstream suppliers.
DURATION: 24 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Seed prices up 300%+ since 2000. Regional varieties discontinued. Fewer options every year.

---

**[SCENE 10 — WHAT THIS MEANS FOR BIODIVERSITY]**
VISUAL: A seed catalog — thick, full of variety names — slowly loses pages until it's a thin pamphlet. Then three variety names remain, highlighted. Everything else fades out.
NARRATION: It also affects what varieties exist at all. Corporations keep varieties that sell at scale. Regionally adapted varieties, unusual heirlooms, niche crops — they get dropped. The number of commercially available vegetable varieties has collapsed. When a seed company discontinues a variety and no one is saving those seeds, that variety can be gone forever.
DURATION: 20 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Commercially available varieties: shrinking. Discontinued = gone forever.

---

**[SCENE 11 — NOT A DOOM VIDEO: WHAT PEOPLE ARE DOING]**
VISUAL: Tone shift — brighter colors, more energy. Illustrated montage: a seed library inside a public library, a community garden seed swap table, a small seed company logo on a computer screen, a gardener labeling seed envelopes.
NARRATION: Okay. Here's the part where we don't just leave you feeling terrible. Because there are real, practical things that individuals and communities are doing — and they work.
DURATION: 10 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: What you can actually do.

---

**[SCENE 12 — ACTIONABLE STEPS]**
VISUAL: Four illustrated panels animate in sequence, each with a bold header and a small character taking action.
NARRATION: One: Buy seeds from independent seed companies. There are hundreds of them — small regional operations that specialize in open-pollinated and heirloom varieties. They need your business, and they're growing varieties that corporations have abandoned. Two: Save seeds from what you grow. Every seed you save is a seed that doesn't go through the commercial system. Three: Participate in seed swaps and seed libraries. These networks preserve varieties outside the market entirely. Four: Support policy that protects farmers' rights to save and replant seeds — seed sovereignty laws are active issues in many states and countries.
DURATION: 35 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: 1. Buy from independent seed companies. 2. Save your own seeds. 3. Seed swaps and seed libraries. 4. Support seed sovereignty policy.

But the alternative food system — the one built on diversity, local adaptation, and community knowledge — it doesn't come from corporations. It comes from millions of individual choices. And it already exists. It just needs more people in it.
DURATION: 22 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: The alternative food system already exists. It needs you in it.
