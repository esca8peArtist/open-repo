# Video 05: What Is a Seed Swap and How Do You Start One?
**Brand:** Seedwarden
**Format:** Animated explainer / cartoonish infographic
**Target Length:** 5–6 minutes
**CTA:** Seed Swap Hosting Kit

---

## SCRIPT

---

**[SCENE 1 — HOOK]**
VISUAL: A public library — warm, illustrated, a little lived-in. A folding table in the corner covered in labeled seed envelopes, paper bags, little jars. A small hand-lettered sign: "SEED SWAP — TAKE ONE, LEAVE ONE." Three illustrated characters are talking, trading, writing variety names on envelopes. The scene radiates warmth and low-stakes ease.
NARRATION: A seed swap is one of the most subversive acts of community you can do. And it is also incredibly easy to run. Here's what it is, why it matters, and exactly how you can start one.
DURATION: 14 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: One of the most subversive acts of community you can do. Also easy.

---

**[SCENE 2 — WHAT A SEED SWAP ACTUALLY IS]**
VISUAL: Simple illustrated diagram. Person A has envelopes labeled "Cherokee Purple Tomato" and "Mortgage Lifter Tomato." Person B has "Dragon Tongue Beans" and "Chioggia Beet." They trade. Both leave with more than they came with — and their new seeds have a story behind them.
NARRATION: A seed swap is exactly what it sounds like. People bring seeds — usually from their own gardens, sometimes extras they've bought — and trade them with other growers. You show up with what you have, you leave with something you didn't. But the real exchange isn't just seeds. It's knowledge. Every seed envelope someone hands you usually comes with a story: where it came from, what it does, what conditions it prefers.
DURATION: 22 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Bring what you have. Leave with something new. Seeds come with stories.

---

**[SCENE 3 — WHY THEY MATTER: PRESERVATION]**
VISUAL: A timeline. On the left: a map covered in diverse regional seed varieties — hundreds of dots. The dots thin out as the timeline moves right, toward the present. Then: at seed swap events, new dots appear — not many, but persistent. Stubborn little points of light.
NARRATION: Seed swaps are one of the oldest and most effective ways to preserve agricultural biodiversity outside of corporate control. When a gardener in your city saves a tomato variety that's been in their family for three generations, and passes it to someone else at a swap, that variety survives. It exists in more hands. It becomes harder to lose. Commercial seed companies have discontinued thousands of varieties. Seed swaps are part of why some of them still exist.
DURATION: 24 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Seed swaps = distributed preservation. More hands = harder to lose. Some varieties survive only through swaps.

---

**[SCENE 4 — WHY THEY MATTER: COMMUNITY]**
VISUAL: The seed swap table again, but now the illustration is fuller. Neighbors who don't normally talk, now talking. Someone getting growing advice from an experienced gardener. A child fascinated by a weird-looking bean. A newcomer who looks relieved to be included in something.
NARRATION: There's also something that happens at seed swaps that's hard to describe in practical terms — they just feel like community. Real community. Not the kind organized by an app or sponsored by a corporation. People who grow things tend to be generous, and a seed swap concentrates that. I've met neighbors at seed swaps I'd lived next to for years without knowing.
DURATION: 20 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: The kind of community that isn't on an app.

---

**[SCENE 5 — HOW TO FIND ONE]**
VISUAL: A search screen animation — stylized, not a real UI. Search terms appear: "seed swap [your city]," "seed library [your city]," "seed saving club." Results populate: a library event, a community garden post, a local gardening group. Then alternative channels illustrated: a bulletin board at a food co-op, a community center flyer.
NARRATION: How do you find one? Search "[your city] seed swap" or "[your city] seed library." Many public libraries now host seed swaps — especially in late winter and early spring when gardeners are planning. Local community gardens, food co-ops, and gardening clubs are also common hosts. If nothing comes up for your area, that's actually useful information. It means there's a gap — and you could fill it.
DURATION: 22 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Search: "[your city] seed swap" / "[your city] seed library" / Check: public libraries, community gardens, food co-ops. Nothing nearby? That's your sign.

---

**[SCENE 6 — HOW TO START ONE: THE MINIMUM]**
VISUAL: A single folding table. A hand-lettered sign. A pile of donated seed envelopes. A character nervously setting it up — then people start showing up. The table gets more crowded with seeds. Character relaxes and starts talking with people.
NARRATION: Starting a seed swap does not require much. Here's the minimum viable version: you need a table, a location, a date, and seeds to start with. That's genuinely it. The event can grow from there.
DURATION: 14 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Minimum: Table. Location. Date. Seeds. That's it.

---

**[SCENE 7 — FIND A LOCATION]**
VISUAL: Three illustrated options. A public library (most common). A community center. A garden center or plant nursery willing to host. Each gets a label and a small checkmark.
NARRATION: For location, public libraries are your best bet. They're free, they're already community spaces, and many librarians love this kind of programming — it's exactly what libraries are for. Call your local branch and ask if they'd host a seed swap. You might be surprised how easy they say yes. Community centers and community gardens are good second options. Some independent garden centers will host one as a way to bring in foot traffic.
DURATION: 22 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Best: Public library (free, community-ready). Also: Community center. Community garden. Local garden center.

---

**[SCENE 8 — COLLECT SEEDS TO START]**
VISUAL: Character sends a simple message to their contacts: "Starting a seed swap — do you have any seeds to donate or trade?" Response bubbles pop in with enthusiastic replies. A pile of donated envelopes grows on screen. Then a small illustration of the host's own saved seeds being labeled and sorted.
NARRATION: Before the event, put out a call for seed donations. Post in your local neighborhood group, community garden email list, or local gardening Facebook group. You'll almost always get people who have extras they've been meaning to share. Then bring your own saved seeds — whatever you grew this year. Even a few varieties is enough to start.
DURATION: 20 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Ask your community for donations. Post in local groups. Bring your own saved seeds. A few varieties is enough to start.

---

**[SCENE 9 — PREPARE YOUR SEEDS]**
VISUAL: Illustrated step-by-step: small paper envelopes, a pen, seeds being scooped in. Labels written on each: "Brandywine Tomato — saved 2024 — good for containers?" Small icons: seed type, growing notes, source.
NARRATION: Prepare your seeds for sharing by putting them in small paper envelopes — coin envelopes from a dollar store or craft store work perfectly. Label each one: variety name, year saved, and anything useful you know about it — what it's good for, where it came from, any special care notes. The more information you give, the more useful the seed is to whoever takes it. Seeds with stories get taken first.
DURATION: 20 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Use coin envelopes (cheap at dollar stores). Label: variety, year, growing notes. Seeds with stories get taken first.

---

**[SCENE 10 — THE DAY-OF SETUP]**
VISUAL: A folding table being set up. Illustrated layout: seeds organized loosely by category (tomatoes, beans, greens, herbs, flowers). A small sign explaining the swap rules. A donation box for anyone who wants to bring seeds but forgot envelopes. A notebook for an email signup sheet.
NARRATION: Day-of setup is simple. Organize seeds loosely by type — tomatoes together, beans together, herbs together. Make a small sign explaining the concept for anyone who wanders in: take what you want, leave what you have, no strict accounting required. Have a few blank envelopes and a pen for people who brought seeds but didn't package them. A signup sheet for a mailing list is smart — you're building a community, not just hosting an event.
DURATION: 24 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Organize loosely by type. Sign: Take what you want. Leave what you have. Blank envelopes available. Signup sheet for future events.

---

**[SCENE 11 — MAKING IT RECURRING]**
VISUAL: A calendar. First event marked. Then three months later, another dot. Then next year, the dot is bigger — more people, more seeds. The swap grows as a recurring community event.
NARRATION: The most valuable swap is a recurring one. Even twice a year — late winter for spring planning, and late summer for fall reflection — builds a community of growers who look forward to it. Each time, more people bring saved seeds. More knowledge circulates. Varieties get preserved. You're not just hosting an event; you're building infrastructure.
DURATION: 20 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Twice a year = consistent community. Late winter + late summer. You're building infrastructure.

---

**[SCENE 12 — PARTNERING WITH SEED LIBRARIES]**
VISUAL: A public library with a small illustrated cabinet labeled "SEED LIBRARY." A character checks out seed packets the way you'd check out a book. Return date stamped on the envelope.
NARRATION: One step further than a swap is a seed library — a permanent collection, usually housed in a public library, where community members can borrow seeds, grow them out, save seeds from their harvest, and return a portion. Some public libraries already have one. If yours doesn't, you could propose starting one — the ALA and several nonprofits have established models you can adapt. It's a long-term investment in community food resilience.
DURATION: 22 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Seed library = borrow seeds, grow, save, return. Many public libraries already have one. Ask yours.

---

**[SCENE 13 — WHY THIS IS POLITICAL (WITHOUT BEING PREACHY)]**
VISUAL: A large corporation logo (generic, stylized blob) trying to sell seed packets to a community. But the community already has a table full of free, saved seeds between them. The blob shrinks. The community doesn't even notice — they're busy talking to each other.
NARRATION: Seeds passing directly between growers — no transaction, no patent, no commercial system in the middle — is genuinely threatening to an industry built on selling you the same seed every year. Not in a dramatic way. In a quiet, stubborn, deeply human way. When you run a seed swap, you're not protesting anything. You're just building something better.
DURATION: 20 seconds
ON-SCREEN TEXT: Seeds between neighbors = no patents required. Not a protest. Just something better.
