### Sub-Gate 2b: Color Palette Decision (30–60 minutes)

**Purpose**: Seedwarden visual brand needs to signal both "heirloom seeds / growing guides" AND "medicinal herbs / wellness." Three color palette paths are provided; you choose one to lock in for all Phase 2–3 materials.

**Decision is final** (changing colors mid-production breaks visual consistency). Take 30–60 minutes now to choose. If unsure, pick **Option B (Multi-Color Palette)** — it's the safest default.

**The three options**:

---

#### **Option A: Simple Greenscape** (Minimalist, refined)

**Vibe**: Clean, sophisticated, traditional apothecary  
**Best for**: Users who want minimal color palette; professional/business-focused aesthetic  
**Color priority**: Deep greens + cream only; accent colors minimal  

**Palette**:
- Primary text: Deep Forest Green (`#143b28`)
- Background/light: Warm Cream (`#F5EDD6`)
- Secondary accent: Sage (`#8FA882`) — used sparingly for badges/callouts
- Zone cards: Use zone band colors only (no brand colors on cards)
- Social graphics: Deep greens + cream + one accent color per post

**What you're saying**: "We're serious about herbs. Elegant. Trustworthy."

**Risk**: Limited color variety might feel less dynamic on social media (Instagram/TikTok). Might need more visual interest than two colors can provide for 50+ social posts.

**Use this if**: You prefer minimalism and trust that green + cream + one accent is enough.

---

#### **Option B: Multi-Color Palette** (Balanced, versatile) — **RECOMMENDED**

**Vibe**: Warm, accessible, educational  
**Best for**: Maximum flexibility across zone cards, pins, and social graphics; appeals to multiple cohorts (foragers, homesteaders, herbalists)  
**Color priority**: All 6 brand colors + zone bands; mix and match per context

**Palette**:
- Deep Forest Green (`#143b28`) + Deep Ink Green (`#1A3A2A`) — primary
- Warm Cream (`#F5EDD6`) + Parchment (`#EDE0C4`) — backgrounds
- Sage (`#8FA882`) + Burnt Sienna (`#A0522D`) — accents
- Zone band colors (Cool/Temperate/Warm/Hot) — zone cards only

**What you're saying**: "We're growing things, preserving things, learning things. Warm. Practical. Inclusive."

**Flexibility**: Each zone card uses a different accent (Sage on one, Sienna on another) without looking disjointed. Social graphics can pair Sage with greens one day, Sienna with creams the next.

**Use this if**: You want maximum design flexibility without overthinking color choices. Most versatile for a diverse audience.

---

#### **Option C: Monochrome with Accent** (Bold, distinctive)

**Vibe**: Modern, structured, premium  
**Best for**: Strong brand identity; users who want clear visual hierarchy; high-contrast graphics  
**Color priority**: One neutral monochrome (deep green only) + one bold accent (Sienna)

**Palette**:
- Primary: Deep Forest Green (`#143b28`) + black/dark gray
- Light: Warm Cream (`#F5EDD6`) only
- Single accent: Burnt Sienna (`#A0522D`) — used for highlights, CTAs, zone bands
- All type over: Green text on cream OR cream text on green (high contrast)

**What you're saying**: "We're focused. Premium. Clear. Medicinal herbs are serious business."

**Risk**: Very strict palette. If you want to experiment with Sage mid-June, you'll feel constrained. Less playful for social media.

**Use this if**: You want a strong, distinctive brand that's easy to execute at scale (less color decision-making per design piece).

---

### Color Decision Framework

**Ask yourself**:
1. **Do I want to experiment with multiple colors** as I produce zone cards and social graphics? → **Option B** (you'll love the flexibility)
2. **Do I prefer a minimal, no-decisions color palette** so I can move fast? → **Option A or C**
3. **Am I producing mostly professional/formal materials** (PDFs, guides, educational content)? → **Option A**
4. **Am I producing a mix of professional + social (pins, Reels, TikToks)**? → **Option B**

**Default recommendation**: **Option B (Multi-Color Palette)** unless you have a strong reason otherwise. It's the most forgiving and flexible for a growing visual brand.
