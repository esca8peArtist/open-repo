## Part 1: Medicinal Herb States to Photograph

### Understanding Plant Lifecycle Stages for Photography

Unlike vegetable gardening (where you photograph ripe tomatoes), medicinal herb photography needs to cover the full lifecycle for identification, cultivation, and preparation purposes.

### Detailed State Descriptions

#### Seedling Stage (June 3–9)
**What it shows**: Germination, early leaf development, identification at the smallest size
**Species to capture**:
- Calendula (direct-sown May 27, sprouting June 2–5)
- Red Clover (direct-sown May 27, sprouting June 2–5)
- Mullein (if started early; otherwise skip)
- Dandelion (if seeds are started; otherwise skip)
- Ginger (rhizome planted May 30, sprouting June 5–8)

**Camera angle**: Overhead (top-down), showing leaf shape and growth pattern emerging from soil
**Props**: Small potting tray or small pot, potting mix visible, tablet or seed packet nearby (context)
**Lighting**: North-facing window, morning light
**Image count target**: 25–35 images total seedling stage

#### Juvenile/Vegetative Stage (June 3–16)
**What it shows**: Established plant growth, leaf texture and veining, plant habit before flowering
**Species to capture**:
- Lemon Balm (potted nursery start or propagated from seed, showing bushy habit)
- Lavender (potted young start, showing upright growth and silvery-green leaves)
- Black Cohosh (if bare-root arrives early, potted and establishing; leaf complex is identifying feature)
- Passionflower (vine starting to vine on temporary trellis)
- Thyme (potted herb start from nursery)
- Ashwagandha (if available; otherwise skip)

**Camera angle**: Side angle (45°), showing full plant habit and leaf arrangement
**Props**: Potted plant on wooden surface, leaf close-up detail shot, size reference (hand, ruler)
**Lighting**: Window light at 45° angle (side-lit), shadows cast to one side (dimensional)
**Image count target**: 30–40 images total vegetative stage

#### Mature/Flowering Stage (June 10–16)
**What it shows**: Full plant habit, distinctive flowers, identifying features in mature form, seed/fruit development
**Species to capture**:
- Black Cohosh (full plant with leaf complex, flowering spike if available June bloom)
- Elderberry (mature shrub, flowering or mature leaf canopy)
- Echinacea (flowering head, both species if available)
- Passionflower (vine with flowers, close-up of distinctive flower structure)
- Valerian (tall flowering spike, 4–6 feet if mature)
- Ginger (mature plant growth, if available)
- Ashwagandha (if mature; berry clusters in papery calyx are distinctive feature)

**Camera angle**: Overhead (habit), 45° side angle (flower detail), close-up macro for flower heads
**Props**: Natural garden context, studio backgrounds (kraft paper, wood plank), small tools for scale
**Lighting**: Window light, diffused (north-facing window), no harsh shadows on flower heads
**Image count target**: 40–50 images total flowering stage

#### Dried/Prepared Stage (June 17–21)
**What it shows**: Dried harvest states, preparation outcomes, storage forms, root anatomy
**Species to capture**:
- Dried roots: Black Cohosh, Goldenseal, Valerian, Dandelion (purchased dried or dug from mature plants)
- Dried leaves: Calendula, Lemon Balm, Thyme, Mullein, Lavender
- Dried flowers: Calendula petals, Echinacea flower heads, Elderberry dried
- Root cross-sections: Dandelion (split lengthwise), Goldenseal (if available to cut), Ginger (cross-section)
- Bundled herbs: Dried lavender bundles tied with twine, herb bundles in jars

**Camera angle**: Flat-lay (overhead), close-up macro (cross-section detail), side angle (bundled herbs)
**Props**: White poster board (isolation), mortar and pestle, glass jars, wooden spoon, linen cloth, muslin bags
**Lighting**: Window light (north-facing), white reflector board opposite light source (fill shadows)
**Image count target**: 30–40 images total dried stage

## Part 2: Lighting & Styling Requirements for Apothecary Aesthetic

### Aesthetic Vision

Phase 3 medicinal herbs should evoke "herbalist's apothecary" — clinical, organized, botanical, warm, earth-toned. Not influencer-style (no bright ring light, no overly styled props), but also not sterile/medical (avoid harsh white backgrounds, clinical overhead fluorescent light).

### Lighting Setup (Primary)

**Primary light source**: North-facing window, 9 AM–2 PM shoot window
- North light is consistent, cool-toned, even (no harsh direct rays that create strong shadows)
- Avoid south/west-facing windows (too bright, creates contrasty shadows)
- If window receives afternoon sun, close curtains/blinds during shoot to diffuse

**Diffusion**:
- **Essential**: White bedsheet hung 12–18" in front of window, OR 3×3 ft diffusion panel
- **Purpose**: Softens direct light even further, eliminates subtle shadows on plant close-ups
- **Material**: White cotton bedsheet works; specialized photography diffusion panels (Amazon $30–$50) also work

**Reflector** (optional but helpful):
- White poster board (22"×28") positioned opposite light source
- "Bounces" soft light back into shadowed side of subject, reducing contrast
- Creates dimension without harsh shadows

**Backup lighting** (if window light insufficient):
- 45W daylight LED softbox ($80–$120, Amazon)
- Color temperature: 5500K–5700K (daylight temperature, matches window light)
- Position: 45° off-axis from subject (not directly overhead)
- Avoid ring light: reads as "influencer lifestyle content," not practitioner

## Part 3: Location & Backdrop Options for June 20–23 Photo Shoot

### Primary Location: Indoor Studio Setup

**Preferred**: North-facing room (bedroom, kitchen, or dedicated workspace) with large window

**Requirements**:
- Table surface: 36"×24" minimum (to arrange multiple props + backgrounds)
- North-facing window (large enough to provide 2–3 hours of direct diffused light)
- Wall space for backdrop boards (to hang kraft paper or position dark wood)
- Storage for 50+ images on camera/phone during 7-day shoot cycle
- Temperature stable (not overheated, keeps plants fresh during long photo sessions)

**Advantage of indoor studio**:
- Consistency (same lighting throughout 4-week period)
- Flexibility (weather-independent)
- Control (can position backgrounds, props, plants exactly as needed)
- Plant freshness (indoor temperature prevents rapid wilting during shoots)

**Setup time**: 2–3 hours (one-time, May 26–31)
- Clear and clean table surface
- Position light sources (window, diffusion panel, reflector board)
- Arrange backdrop boards for easy rotation
- Test camera angles and lighting

### Bonus: Outdoor Location (If Available)

Outdoor location is **optional** but valuable for "habitat shots" (plants in garden context). Only pursue if you have access and time allows.

**Ideal outdoor locations**:
- Personal garden bed (if medicinal herb planting exists)
- Botanical garden (if admission/photography permission available)
- Herb farm or local nursery (if relationships exist)

**Advantage**: Natural habitat context, green background, authentic "growing" photography

**Disadvantage**: Weather-dependent (rain disrupts schedule), less control over lighting, props appear artificial outdoors

**Recommendation**: Prioritize indoor studio. Only add outdoor shots if indoor work finishes early.
