# Location Scouting Checklist
## Phase 2 Photography — Appalachian Medicinals, May 20–25, 2026

**Shoot window**: May 20–25, 2026 (6 days; 3 active shoot days; 3 float days)
**Species scope**: American Ginseng, Goldenseal, Black Cohosh, Bloodroot, Ramps
**Plants-must-arrive-by**: May 18 (inspection and acclimatization before shoot)
**Location decision deadline**: May 12 (permits require 14-day advance notice minimum for state parks)

This checklist is the working decision document for shoot location. It covers the primary location, two backup locations, permit requirements for each, weather and seasonal suitability, and the logistics that determine whether a location is actually executable on shoot day.

---

## Part 1: Location Decision Framework

Three criteria govern location selection for botanical guide photography of these species:

**Criterion 1 — Species presence**: The most instructive guide photographs show plants in their actual habitat context — a goldenseal colony on a north-facing forest slope reads very differently from the same plant on a table. However, wild populations of these five species are under poaching pressure, and location disclosure creates real risk for wild plants. The correct approach is documented cultivated specimens on private land or in botanical garden collections, not wild populations.

**Criterion 2 — Commercial photography permissions**: The moment a photograph is produced for a product sold commercially (Etsy guide), it is "commercial photography" under the permit language of every public land management agency. This triggers permit requirements at state parks, national forests, and botanical gardens. Botanical gardens have the most streamlined commercial photography processes; national forests are the most restrictive for unmanned operations in Appalachian terrain.

**Criterion 3 — Logistical feasibility**: Parking, vehicle access, shelter from sudden weather, bathroom access, and electrical access for lighting equipment are all real constraints for a 6-hour shoot day. A beautiful forest location that requires a 45-minute hike with equipment is not feasible for a one-person or two-person crew.

## Part 2: Primary Location — Asheville Botanical Garden (North Carolina)

**Location**: Asheville Botanical Garden, 151 W.T. Weaver Blvd, Asheville, NC 28804
**Website**: ashevillebotanicalgarden.org
**Contact for permits**: Facility rental / events manager via contact form on facility-rental page
**Collection relevance**: 600+ species of Appalachian wildflowers, flowering shrubs, and trees on 10 acres. As an institution focused on Appalachian native plants, this is the single most species-relevant public botanical garden for the five Wave 1 species.

### Field Access and Collection Details

The Asheville Botanical Garden maintains documented Appalachian native plant collections in a naturalistic woodland setting. Black Cohosh, Bloodroot, and Ramps are all documented Appalachian understory species that appear in collections of this type. Ginseng and Goldenseal are more likely to appear in managed demonstration plots or shade garden sections than in open-access areas.

Access to the garden collections (planted areas) for commercial photography is restricted under standard botanical garden policy — photographers are limited to open pathways, boardwalks, and non-planted areas for sessions that require equipment setup. This means the garden's value is primarily for natural-background contextual shots taken from pathways, not hands-in-soil prop arrangements with live garden specimens.

**Practical workaround**: Bring your own cultivated specimens (purchased from vendors in `plant-sourcing-spreadsheet.csv`) to the garden location. Photograph your specimens against the garden's naturalistic woodland backdrop, from public pathways. This is fully compliant with access policies and produces the habitat-context imagery that in-studio prop shoots cannot replicate.

### Permit Requirements

Commercial photography at Asheville Botanical Garden requires a completed reservation form and fee payment submitted at least 7 days before the visit.

- Session with minimal equipment and single subject: **$25 per 2 hours**
- Session with multiple pieces of equipment and entourage: **$75 per 2 hours**
- For a 6-hour shoot day (minimal equipment, 1-2 person crew): estimated **$75–$150** total
- Permit application deadline for May 20 shoot: **May 13** (7-day advance notice)
- Application submission: via the facility-rental page at ashevillebotanicalgarden.org/facility-rental/

**Action item**: Submit commercial photography application by May 12 to allow one business day buffer. Include: shoot dates (May 20–21 or 20–22), crew size, equipment list (camera, lens, portable reflector), and intended use (botanical guide photography for digital publication).

### Weather and Seasonal Suitability

May in Asheville (elevation ~2,100 ft) is typically the best month for Appalachian wildflower photography. Average daytime temperatures: 60–72°F. Average precipitation: 3.5 inches for the month (approximately 1 day in 5 with measurable rain). Bloodroot has typically finished its bloom by mid-May; Ramps leaves are still visible through May; Black Cohosh and Goldenseal show foliage without flowers in May (flowers emerge June–August). Ginseng leafs out in May.

**Weather risk**: May 20–25 falls in a window where afternoon thunderstorms are possible (typical Appalachian late-spring pattern). Morning shoots (9am–1pm) are lowest risk. If morning rain is forecast, a 2-hour delay to late morning is usually sufficient.

**Shoot-day weather protocol**: Check 72-hour forecast starting May 17. If forecast shows sustained rain on May 20 (primary shoot day), advance the schedule to May 19 or shift to May 21. Do not attempt to shoot Cluster 1 (woodland understory species) in direct rain — wet leaves produce glare and color shifts that are difficult to correct in editing.

## Part 3: Backup Location 1 — Private Land / Forest Farm (Appalachian Region)

**Location type**: Private forest farm or homestead with cultivated medicinal plant specimens
**Geographic range**: Western North Carolina, Southwest Virginia, Eastern Tennessee, Southern West Virginia
**How to identify**: Contact United Plant Savers (unitedplantsavers.org) and ask for a referral to a regional member who does forest farming. Local Appalachian native plant societies (Western Carolina Botanical Club, Tennessee Native Plant Society) may also provide referrals. The Civil Eats article from 2020 documented several named forest farms in the region that have been interviewed publicly.

### Why This Is the Strongest Location Option

Private forest farm land offers something no botanical garden can provide: documentation of the entire cultivation system. You can photograph ginseng or goldenseal in the actual shade canopy where they grow, alongside the companion plants, leaf litter, and slope aspect that define their habitat. The landowner can demonstrate the cultivation method in-frame — hands spreading seed, demonstrating the harvest protocols, showing the planting records. This is the most educational imagery possible and directly supports the "grow it" action frame in the guide.

### Permit Requirements

No public land permits required. Standard private property: written permission from the landowner (via email or signed release). Request explicitly that the permission covers commercial photography for digital publication (guide sold on Etsy). The typical arrangement is: photographer brings a gift (a copy of the finished guide, or a small payment of $50–100 for the half-day), gets 3–4 hours of access, and leaves the land in the condition it was found.

**Lead time to secure**: Contact outreach should begin by May 10. A 10-day window (May 10–20) is needed to identify candidates, exchange messages, and confirm a date. United Plant Savers regional chapters are the fastest path — their member networks include active forest farmers in all five Appalachian states.

### Weather and Seasonal Suitability

Same regional weather window as Asheville Botanical Garden. Private forest sites typically have more natural shelter (tree canopy) during light rain events than open botanical garden pathways. Morning window (9am–1pm) is still recommended. Confirm with the landowner whether their access road is passable after rain (gravel roads in the Appalachians can wash out in heavy rain — ask specifically about road condition in wet weather).

## Part 4: Backup Location 2 — Controlled Indoor Environment (Weather Contingency)

**Location type**: Indoor studio setup with grow lights and potted cultivated specimens
**Location**: Any home or apartment with a large east- or south-facing window, or a rented photography studio

This backup is activated if both the primary and backup field locations fail due to weather or access issues. It is not a preferred option — indoor shots of potted plants on a table are informative but lack the habitat depth that field shots provide. However, indoor photographs are technically superior in consistency, color accuracy, and repeatability, and the guide interior sections (root identification, seed close-ups, leaf detail) are in many cases better photographed indoors regardless.

### Why the Indoor Backup Is Adequate for May 30 Launch

Five of the twelve shot types in the shoot plan (root, seed, leaf cross-section, scale reference, botanical illustration) can only be executed indoors with controlled lighting. The indoor backup session captures these five types at full quality. The field shots (full plant in habitat, flower on stem in natural light, understory colony view) are sourced from iNaturalist CC-BY archive photography when in-studio specimens are the only material available. The guide is fully produceable without a field shoot.

### Setup Requirements

- East- or south-facing window with 3+ hours of unobstructed morning light
- White foam-core board (24x36 in) as reflector: $8–12 at craft store
- Black velvet or dark wood board as background for root/seed close-ups
- Potted plants positioned 18–24 inches from window, not in direct midday sun
- No artificial grow lights required if window light is adequate (9am–1pm)
- Camera or smartphone: shoot at highest resolution, white balance set to Daylight or Cloudy

### Weather Trigger Decision Point

If forecast on May 17 shows 3+ consecutive days of rain covering May 20–22, switch to indoor backup immediately. Do not wait until May 20 to make this call — the prop arrangement and equipment setup for indoor versus field shooting are different, and preparation time is required.

### Primary Contingency Schedule

| May 23 | Cluster 3 | If all field sessions completed: begin indoor root/seed shots |
| May 24 | Float — additional shots or re-shoots | |
| May 25 | Hard cutoff — all shooting complete by end of day | |

### Weather Decision Checkpoints

- **May 17**: Review 5-day forecast for Asheville/Appalachian region. If 3+ consecutive days of rain forecast, activate indoor backup.
- **May 19**: Review 48-hour forecast. Confirm or revise May 20 shoot plan. Notify any crew members of schedule.
- **May 20, 6am**: Check hourly forecast. If morning (9am–1pm) is clear, proceed with field shoot regardless of afternoon forecast.
- **May 20, 8:30am**: Final go/no-go call. If current conditions are rain at shoot location, switch to indoor backup. Do not attempt to wait out weather on location with live plant specimens.

### Backup Date If All 5 Days Fail

If weather makes shooting impossible May 20–25 (sustained multi-day rain event, which occurs in the Appalachians roughly 1–2 times per month in May), the contingency is June 6–7 for the field component of the shoot. The indoor root/seed shots are executed May 20–25 regardless of weather, preserving those 5 shot types per species (25 total shots). The field component (full plant in habitat, flower, understory colony) is sourced from iNaturalist CC-BY archive. This maintains May 30 as a viable launch date. See `guide-production-timeline.md` for the June 6 contingency path details.

Use the Asheville Botanical Garden (which has an expedited 7-day process) or private land instead.
