# Phase 3 Content Authoring Template Kit

**Prepared**: May 31, 2026  
**Purpose**: Templates that reduce per-bundle authoring workload by 30–40% through structural reuse. Every section of every bundle follows the same architecture. Once one bundle is written, subsequent bundles require 60–70% of the first bundle's effort.

**How to use this document**: This is the template reference. The companion file `PHASE_3_BUNDLE_MASTER_TEMPLATE.md` contains the Women's Health bundle as a fully filled example with two complete species blocks. Copy the templates here into a new Google Doc, replace the bracketed fields, follow the quality checklist at the end of each section.

---

## 1. Per-Bundle Master Template

The master template applies to all five bundles (Women's Health, Respiratory, Sleep & Nervines, Immunity, Digestive). Structural elements are identical. The only bundle-specific elements are the herb selections, the bundle framing paragraph, and the seasonal hook.

### Front Matter Block (80–100 words)

Copy this block verbatim into every bundle. Replace `[BUNDLE NAME]` only.

```
Copyright [YEAR] Seedwarden. All rights reserved.

This guide is produced for educational purposes only. The information
in this guide does not constitute medical advice and is not a substitute
for professional healthcare consultation. Always consult a qualified
healthcare provider before using herbal preparations, particularly if
you are pregnant, nursing, taking medications, or managing a chronic
health condition.

FTC Disclosure: This guide represents original research and practitioner
consultation. No sponsored content. Botanical identification photographs
sourced from Wikimedia Commons (CC-BY-SA) and iNaturalist (CC-BY).

Practitioner License: The 10-Pack Practitioner License (sold separately)
permits distribution of this guide to up to 10 clients. Single-copy
purchase is for personal and household use only.

[BUNDLE NAME] | Seedwarden Medicinal Herb Series
```

**Quality gate**: Does the copyright year match the current year? Is the FTC disclosure accurate for this bundle's photo sources?

### Introduction Block (180–220 words)

Structure: (1) Bundle framing — why these plants together, (2) Conservation thread — name any at-risk species, (3) Reader orientation — what the guide contains, (4) Seasonal hook — when is this guide most actionable.

Template:

```
[BUNDLE FRAMING: 60–80 words. One paragraph. What connects these specific plants?
What is the common thread of use, tradition, or ecology that makes them a bundle
rather than a random list? Write from the reader's need: "If you are looking for
[reader goal], these are the plants that [specific connection]."
Avoid vague wellness language ("support your journey"). Be specific: body system,
preparation type, or foraging/growing context.]

[CONSERVATION NOTE: 30–40 words. If any bundle herb is UpS At-Risk or CITES Appendix II,
name it here and briefly state what the guide does about it (forest farm sourcing,
cultivated-only recommendation, or wild harvest guidance with population ethics).]

[READER ORIENTATION: 30–40 words. "This guide covers [number] plants. Each plant section
includes identification, range, cultivation, harvest, active constituents, traditional use,
and contraindications. Preparation methods are consolidated after the species sections."]

[SEASONAL HOOK: 20–30 words. When does this guide pay off most? Spring planting window?
Fall harvest? Year-round home apothecary? Be specific about the calendar moment.]
```

**Quality gate**: Is the bundle framing specific enough that a reader could not mistake this bundle's introduction for another bundle's? Does the conservation note appear if and only if an at-risk species is present?

### Per-Species Content Block Template

This is the single most reusable structure in the kit. All seven species sections use identical subsection headers. A completed Women's Health species block (Lavender) is in `PHASE_3_BUNDLE_MASTER_TEMPLATE.md` — use it as the calibration example.

```
## [COMMON NAME] (*[Latin binomial]*)

### Identification and Botanical Description (150–200 words)

[What does this plant look like? Write for field recognition, not taxonomy.
Cover: overall growth form (habit), leaf shape and arrangement, stem character,
flower description and color, fruit or seed if relevant. Include one
distinguishing feature that separates this species from look-alikes.
If the plant has a notable smell, taste, or texture — include it.
At-risk species: add cultivation note here (see Conservation Sidebar below).]

[PHOTO CAPTION: habit-photo filename — e.g., "lavender-habit.jpg — Lavandula angustifolia,
summer bloom, cultivated planting. Photo: [Author], CC-BY-SA 4.0, Wikimedia Commons."]

### Range and Habitat (80–120 words)

[Where does this plant grow? USDA hardiness zones + US/Canadian range by region.
Native range vs. naturalized range — distinguish clearly.
Habitat preference: forest floor, meadow, disturbed ground, riparian, cultivated.
For cultivated species (Lavender, Lemon Balm, Valerian): note which climate regions
grow it successfully outdoors vs. as an annual or container plant.
For native species (Black Cohosh, Passionflower): note ecological community and
any indicator species for habitat identification.]

[CONSERVATION SIDEBAR — AT-RISK SPECIES ONLY]
*[SPECIES NAME]* is listed [United Plant Savers monitoring list / UpS At-Risk / CITES Appendix II].
Wild harvest is discouraged. This guide recommends [forest-farm sourced / cultivated / Wikimedia CC
educational photos only]. Supplier note: [Mountain Rose Herbs FGV / Strictly Medicinal seeds / etc.].
[/CONSERVATION SIDEBAR]

### Cultivation (150–200 words)

[How does a reader grow this plant at home or on a small farm?
Cover: soil preference (pH, drainage, organic matter), sun/shade requirements,
planting method (direct seed, transplant, division, rhizome), spacing, watering,
companion plant (one or two), USDA zone range for perennial establishment.
For biennials (Mullein): describe year-one rosette vs. year-two spike behavior.
For rhizomatous plants (Valerian, Black Cohosh): describe division timing.
Include one practical tip that is not in standard sources: something a grower would
learn from the second or third growing season, not from a seed packet.]

### Harvest and Preparation (120–160 words)

[When and how is this plant harvested for the relevant use?
Be specific: which plant part, what time of year, what visual cue signals readiness,
and what size batch is practical for a home grower. Post-harvest handling: fresh use,
drying method and temperature, storage container and shelf life.
Include one common error: the most frequent harvest timing or handling mistake that
reduces potency or quality.]

### Active Constituents (100–140 words)

[List the primary constituent classes (flavonoids, volatile oils, alkaloids, etc.)
with specific compound names where well-documented. Use precise language.
Cite the strongest evidence: one or two human clinical studies where available
(with year and study description, not citation number — readable prose format).
FTC framing: "Studies have shown [specific outcome] in [specific population]"
not "This herb supports [vague wellness goal]."
For traditional-use-only plants: distinguish clearly — "Traditional use as [X];
clinical evidence is limited to [study type or animal model]."]

### Traditional and Historical Use (120–150 words)

[What have herbalists, traditional medicine systems, and historical practitioners used
this plant for? Ground this in specific traditions: Eclectic physicians, Appalachian
folk herbalism, European apothecary tradition, Ayurveda (where applicable).
Note the specific preparation that each tradition preferred (tea, tincture, poultice, etc.).
Do not flatten all traditions into generic "traditional medicine" — be specific.
Where traditional use diverges from modern evidence: note the divergence honestly.
Where traditional use anticipates modern evidence: make that connection explicitly.]

### Contraindications (80–120 words)

[This section cannot be omitted or abbreviated. Cover:
(1) Pregnancy and lactation status — specific, accurate, not blanket warnings
(2) Known drug interactions — name the drug classes (blood thinners, SSRIs, etc.)
(3) Contraindicated health conditions — specific conditions, not "consult a doctor"
(4) Dosage threshold issues where relevant (hepatotoxicity, phototoxicity, etc.)
Write at the level of a knowledgeable lay reader, not a pharmacist.
Avoid both over-alarming (listing every theoretical interaction) and under-alarming
(vague "some people should avoid this" language).]
```

**Quality gate**: Does every species section have all seven subsections? Are all contraindications accurate and specific? Is the conservation sidebar present if and only if the species has an at-risk status?

## 4. Common Pitfalls and How Templates Prevent Them

**Pitfall 1 — Inconsistent contraindication depth across bundles**
Without a template, authors write detailed contraindications for familiar herbs (Lavender) and thin contraindications for less-familiar ones (Passionflower, Elecampane). The template enforces 80–120 words minimum and four specific content requirements, preventing this asymmetry.

**Pitfall 2 — Conservation sidebar drift**
The conservation sidebar should appear for at-risk species only. Without a conditional template marker, authors either add it to every species (diluting its impact) or forget it entirely for at-risk species. The template makes it a named optional block with explicit trigger criteria.

**Pitfall 3 — Vague bundle framing**
Bundle introductions written without a template tend toward "herbal wellness support" framing that could describe any bundle. The template requires a reader-need-specific opening sentence and bans wellness platitudes.

**Pitfall 4 — Missing seasonal hooks**
Authors writing in winter frequently omit seasonal hooks, making guides feel timeless but disconnected from the reader's planning calendar. The template mandates a seasonal hook as a named section.

**Pitfall 5 — Over-relying on Latin binomials in body text**
Academic-trained authors use Latin binomials throughout body text. The template places the Latin binomial in the section header only, then uses common name throughout body text — matching how home herbalists search and read.

## 5. Quality Checklist

Run this checklist before finalizing each bundle manuscript. One reviewer pass (author self-check) is sufficient for Scenarios A and B. Peer reviewer pass required for Scenario C.

### Botanical Accuracy
- [ ] All species Latin binomials verified against USDA PLANTS database (plants.usda.gov) or iNaturalist Taxonomy
- [ ] All USDA hardiness zone ranges verified against zone map (current 2023 USDA map)
- [ ] Native vs. introduced range stated correctly for each species
- [ ] No species described as "native" if it is naturalized (e.g., Mullein is naturalized in North America, not native)

### Contraindication Completeness
- [ ] Pregnancy status stated for every species (safe, avoid, insufficient data — be specific)
- [ ] Lactation status stated for every species
- [ ] At least one drug interaction class named for species with known interactions (Valerian + CNS depressants; St. John's Wort is not in Phase 3 but the pattern applies)
- [ ] Hepatotoxic, phototoxic, or dose-threshold concerns included for any species where relevant

### Sourcing Verification
- [ ] All clinical study citations describe the study population and outcome (not just "studies show")
- [ ] All photo attributions logged in PHOTO_ATTRIBUTION_LOG.md with CC license confirmed
- [ ] Forest farm sourcing recommendations current (confirmed supplier availability as of sprint start)
- [ ] Conservation sidebar present for all UpS At-Risk species; absent for all non-at-risk species

### FTC Compliance
- [ ] No structure-function claims stated as fact ("this herb treats X") — all framed as "studied for" or "traditional use as"
- [ ] All clinical citations describe study type (RCT, systematic review, observational) in plain language
- [ ] Disclaimer block present verbatim on front matter page
- [ ] Practitioner 10-pack license terms stated correctly (up to 10 clients, no resale)

### Formatting Consistency
- [ ] All species sections contain all seven subsections in correct order
- [ ] Word counts within template ranges (spot-check 3 sections per guide)
- [ ] Practitioner section present and bundle-specific
- [ ] Photo captions present for all embedded images with filename, description, credit, license
