## Lead Finding

Flock Safety has built the largest private license plate surveillance network in American history — over 100,000 cameras across 7,000+ law enforcement networks, scanning billions of plates per month — with documented unauthorized ICE access, suppression of protest activity, targeting of abortion seekers, and repeated misrepresentation to municipal governments. More than 80 localities have canceled or deactivated their contracts since early 2025. The legal framework governing Flock cameras is weak in most states, but organized city council action has proven effective in terminating contracts, and federal constitutional litigation is advancing in the Fourth Circuit.

## Part 1: Company Profile and Corporate Structure

### 1.1 Founding and Growth

Flock Safety was founded in 2017 by Garrett Langley, Matt Feury, and Paige Todd in Atlanta, Georgia. Langley, the CEO, framed the company around a personal experience of property crime and police inability to act without license plate evidence. The company's original pitch was narrow: affordable automated license plate readers (ALPRs) for HOAs, apartment complexes, and small municipalities that could not afford traditional law enforcement camera systems.

By July 2026, Flock has raised approximately $957 million in total funding across nine rounds. The largest single round was a $275 million Series F in March 2025 led by Andreessen Horowitz, which also backed Ring (acquired by Amazon) and several other surveillance-infrastructure companies. Other major investors include Tiger Global, Founders Fund, Kleiner Perkins, Matrix Partners, and Y Combinator. Current valuation: approximately $8.4 billion.

**Key investors with policy implications**: Andreessen Horowitz and Founders Fund have both publicly supported the Trump administration's deregulatory agenda, creating potential alignment between Flock's investor base and the federal agencies seeking expanded access to Flock's network. This conflict is not publicly disclosed in Flock's marketing materials to municipalities.

Sources: [Who Owns Flock Safety](https://whoistheownerof.com/who-owns-flock-safety-complete-ownership/) | [Flock Safety Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flock_Safety) | [Series F announcement](https://www.flocksafety.com/blog/flock-safety-secures-major-funding)

### 1.2 Network Architecture and Scale

As of June 2026, Flock operates:
- **100,000+ cameras** across the United States (crossed this threshold June 2026)
- **7,000+ law enforcement networks** with access to the national database
- **10,000+ cameras in Texas alone**
- **4,800+ law enforcement agencies** able to query the national system
- **75% of Flock law enforcement customers** use the National Lookup Tool, enabling cross-jurisdiction searches

The technical design is built around a reciprocity model: agencies that share their locally-collected plate data with the national network gain access to plates captured by all other participating agencies. Company training materials describe this as "You show me yours, I'll show you mine." This design means that a small city's 20 cameras automatically become accessible to thousands of other agencies once that city enables national sharing — a fact frequently not disclosed during the municipal sales process.

Flock cameras capture: license plate numbers, vehicle make, model, color, and "vehicle fingerprint" data (unique identifiers like bumper stickers, damage, and accessories). The company has also announced integration with Amazon Ring cameras (late 2025) and has launched "Drones as Automated Security" for commercial clients, enabling drone pursuit of vehicles beyond property boundaries.

Sources: [ACLU of Massachusetts — Location Data](https://data.aclum.org/2025/10/07/flock-gives-law-enforcement-all-over-the-country-access-to-your-location/) | [Flock Massachusetts and Updates — ACLU](https://www.aclu.org/news/privacy-technology/flock-massachusetts-and-updates)

## Part 2: Government Partnerships and Mission Creep

### 2.1 Scale of Law Enforcement Contracts

Flock contracts with thousands of law enforcement agencies across every state. The company markets directly to:
- Municipal police departments
- County sheriff's offices
- State police agencies (including a $26 million Texas DPS contract signed 2024)
- School districts
- Homeowners associations
- Private businesses

The Houston City Council approved a $6.39 million sole-source contract in August 2022 covering the Houston Police Department ($3.94M), Public Works ($2.3M), and Administration and Regulatory Affairs ($150K). This five-year agreement (one year with four renewal options) was awarded without competitive bidding under a Texas Local Government Code exemption.

### 2.2 The "Pilot Programs" Problem: Federal Access Without Municipal Consent

The most consequential pattern of mission creep involves federal agency access to municipal Flock data without municipal authorization. Documented incidents include:

**The Illinois CBP Pilot**: An Illinois state audit — triggered after Texas police used Flock to track a woman who had traveled to Illinois for abortion care — found that Flock had allowed U.S. Customs and Border Protection to access Illinois license plate cameras through a "pilot program" that, according to Flock itself, "Flock leadership was unaware of." The company blamed a regional sales team. The practical effect: CBP had warrantless access to Illinois plates without any Illinois government official's knowledge or consent.

**Mountain View Unauthorized Access**: A Mountain View Voice investigation found that more than 250 unapproved law enforcement agencies conducted approximately 600,000 queries of the city's Flock cameras over 12 months ending December 2025. The city had not authorized most of those agencies.

**Dayton Immigration Enforcement**: An audit revealed that data from Dayton, Ohio's cameras had been searched more than 7,100 times for immigration enforcement purposes — searches explicitly prohibited under the city's own written policy.

**Evanston Camera Reinstallation**: When Evanston canceled its Flock contract in August 2025 and ordered cameras removed, the city later discovered that only some cameras had been taken down — and that Flock had reinstalled others without the city's permission. Full removal did not occur until early 2026.

**Syracuse**: Local councilors discovered their LPR data had been searched 4.4 million times by out-of-state police and shared with ICE despite previous assurances to residents.

Sources: [State of Surveillance — 80 Cities Pulling Out](https://stateofsurveillance.org/articles/surveillance/flock-safety-national-surveillance-database-how-it-works-2026/) | [Immigration Policy Tracking Project](https://immpolicytracking.org/policies/reported-ice-accessing-flock-automated-license-plate-reader-cameras-via-local-law-enforcement/) | [ACLU — Flock Pushback](https://www.aclu.org/news/privacy-technology/flock-pushback)

### 2.3 Terms and Conditions: The February 2026 Changes

In February 2026, Flock rewrote its standard municipal contract terms in ways that significantly shift risk to municipalities. The ACLU has flagged four critical changes:

1. **Data sale prohibition removed**: The old language stated "Flock does not own and shall not sell Customer Data." That sentence is now gone. Municipalities can no longer rely on a contractual guarantee that their surveillance data will not be sold.

2. **Flock controls data access method**: New language grants Flock the "exclusive right to determine and control the method, timing, format, and medium" of customer data access. In practice, municipalities receive degraded, low-resolution copies without metadata — not the raw data their cameras captured.

3. **Perpetual licensing**: Flock retains perpetual, non-expiring rights to use customer surveillance data for "support and improve" purposes, even after contract termination. The company keeps the data indefinitely.

4. **Dispute resolution changes**: Mandatory private arbitration under Georgia law; expanded liability shields for Flock's own gross negligence; stricter termination requirements that make it harder for cities to exit contracts, even after city council votes to do so.

Source: [ACLU — Flock's Terms and Conditions](https://www.aclu.org/news/privacy-technology/tracking-alpr-cameras/flocks-terms-and-conditions)

### 2.4 Documented Deception of City Councils

The ACLU has documented a pattern of Flock misrepresenting its capabilities and data-sharing practices to city councils during the sales process and subsequent contract review hearings. Specific documented misrepresentations include:

- Claiming federal agencies cannot access local data (contradicted by CBP pilot program)
- Claiming the company would immediately remove cameras upon contract cancellation (contradicted by Evanston incident)
- Claiming data ownership guarantees that the company's own contract language has since removed
- Claiming ICE does not have direct access while local police were conducting ICE-requested searches

Source: [ACLU — Flock Credibility Lost](https://www.aclu.org/news/privacy-technology/tracking-alpr-cameras/flock-safety-credibility-lost-as-it-repeatedly-lies-to-city-councils-police-departments-and-public-across-the-country)

## Part 3: National Legal Landscape — State-by-State Analysis

### 3.1 Federal Constitutional Framework

**Current standard**: The federal baseline is weak. A January 27, 2026 ruling in *Schmidt v. Norfolk* (E.D. Va.) granted summary judgment to the City of Norfolk, rejecting plaintiffs' Fourth Amendment challenge to 176 Flock cameras deployed in 75 clusters across the city. Plaintiffs Lee Schmidt and Crystal Arrington had been captured 475 and 325 times respectively over four months. The court held that the cameras did not capture "enough information to catalogue citizens' movements in their entirety" and therefore did not constitute a search under the Fourth Amendment.

**Critical caveat in the ruling**: The district court explicitly noted that ALPR surveillance "could become too intrusive" at "some point" as camera density increases. This density-dependent constitutional threshold is the basis for the ongoing appeal.

**Fourth Circuit appeal**: The Institute for Justice, joined by ACLU, ACLU of Virginia, and Electronic Frontier Foundation (amicus, April 20, 2026), is appealing the Norfolk ruling. The ACLU's amicus brief argues that ALPR systems give government unprecedented surveillance powers that "upset traditional expectations of privacy in violation of the Fourth Amendment." A Fourth Circuit ruling could establish binding precedent for VA, MD, NC, SC, WV, and influence other circuits.

*Carpenter v. United States* (2018, SCOTUS) — which held that government access to seven days of cell-site location data constitutes a Fourth Amendment search — provides the strongest existing precedent for challenging comprehensive ALPR surveillance. No court has yet squarely applied *Carpenter* to hold Flock-scale ALPR networks unconstitutional, but the analytical framework is available.

Sources: [ACLU — Schmidt v. Norfolk](https://www.aclu.org/cases/schmidt-v-norfolk) | [WHRO — Federal Judge Ruled](https://www.whro.org/business-growth/2026-02-11/a-federal-judge-ruled-norfolks-flock-surveillance-cameras-dont-invade-peoples-privacy-yet)

### 3.2 State-by-State Legal Status

The following analysis reflects status as of July 2026. Regulatory status is rapidly evolving.

---

**STRONG RESTRICTIONS (warrant requirements, short retention, or ban)**

**Maine**
- Only state expressly prohibiting ALPR use by private individuals
- Requires warrants for certain categories of law enforcement ALPR access
- Strict data retention limits
- Status: Most protective ALPR law in the country

**New Hampshire**
- Bans state police from using ALPR systems entirely
- Requires law enforcement to delete license plate data not connected to a crime within **three minutes**
- Status: Functional near-prohibition on law enforcement ALPR use

**Washington**
- SB 6002, effective March 30, 2026 — first comprehensive ALPR restrictions in Washington history
- Prohibited uses: immigration enforcement, tracking "gender-affirming treatment and reproductive health care services," monitoring constitutionally protected activities
- Prohibited collection locations: healthcare facilities, schools, places of worship, courts, food banks, immigration facilities
- Data retention: 21 days maximum (general); duration of case (criminal investigations)
- Federal sharing: prohibited unless sharing serves an authorized purpose or required by judicial proceeding
- Agencies must register ALPR systems with Attorney General by September 30, 2026
- Audit trails required, maintained two years
- Exclusionary rule: evidence obtained in knowing violation is inadmissible
- Status: Among the strongest ALPR laws enacted in 2026

**Oregon**
- SB 1516, effective March 31, 2026
- Caps data retention at 30 days
- Requires logs of inter-agency sharing; limits sharing purposes
- Status: Significant restrictions enacted 2026

---

**PARTIAL RESTRICTIONS (retention limits or use restrictions without warrant requirements)**

**California**
- SB 34: Highway Patrol retention capped at 60 days
- Oakland and San Francisco have enacted local ordinances banning real-time ALPR surveillance by police without oversight
- Pending 2026 legislation would bar sharing ALPR data with out-of-state or federal agencies without a warrant
- Austin-style local ordinances provide model framework
- Status: Strong local restrictions; state law moderate; pending legislation would significantly strengthen

**Vermont**
- Strict limitations on private ALPR use
- Transparency requirements for law enforcement
- Addresses authorized uses, data retention, and data sharing controls
- Status: Moderate to strong restrictions

**Illinois**
- State audit authority demonstrated in CBP pilot investigation
- No comprehensive ALPR statute, but audit findings prompted policy changes
- Status: Partial accountability mechanisms; no statutory framework

**Colorado**
- No statewide ALPR statute
- Denver voted unanimously to reject Flock proposal (2026)
- ACLU of Colorado filed demand for contract termination; coalition of civil rights organizations publicly opposed continuation
- Status: City-level action effective; no state law

---

**MINIMAL OR NO RESTRICTIONS (high risk states)**

**Texas**
- No comprehensive state ALPR privacy law
- Texas DPS signed $26 million contract with Flock in 2024
- Texas requires all county sheriffs to enter 287(g) immigration enforcement agreements with ICE (effective December 1, 2025), creating structural pipeline from Flock data to immigration enforcement
- Flock fined by Texas for operating without proper licensing (2026) — but fine applied to private operators; government contracts exempt
- Austin and Hays County terminated contracts; most Texas municipalities have no restrictions
- Status: No state protection; municipalities operate at their own discretion; high-risk for immigrant communities

**Virginia**
- No state ALPR statute
- *Schmidt v. Norfolk* held Flock cameras constitutional at district court level (under appeal)
- Richmond terminated data sharing after discovering ATF conducted immigration searches without notification
- Status: Litigation pending; no statutory protection

**Georgia**
- Flock Safety headquartered in Atlanta; company contracts governed by Georgia law (per February 2026 terms changes)
- No state ALPR law
- Multiple Atlanta-area municipalities use Flock cameras
- Status: Minimal protection; company home state

**Florida**
- No comprehensive ALPR law
- Seminole County conducted searches at May Day protests
- Status: Minimal restriction

**Most other states**
- No specific ALPR laws
- Law enforcement can query Flock networks with no legal restrictions beyond department policy
- Department policies are frequently inadequate (see Houston HPD documentation of "ASDF," "donut," and blank search justification entries)

### 3.3 The Private Deployment Gap

A critical legal gap affects all states: when cameras are installed by **private parties** (HOAs, businesses, apartment complexes) and data is shared voluntarily with police, standard Fourth Amendment analysis does not apply. No state comprehensively regulates private ALPR deployment. This means:

- A HOA camera operating without any law enforcement contract is legal in all but Maine
- When that HOA enables "National Lookup" on its Flock account, its data feeds into the same network accessed by ICE
- The individual whose plate was read has no legal recourse under current federal or most state law
- School districts (like Alvin ISD south of Houston) that purchased cameras for campus security are legally operating "private" systems that any of 4,800+ agencies can query

Source: [Simeon on Security — ALPR Surveillance 2026](https://simeononsecurity.com/articles/flock-safety-camera-surveillance-prevalence-privacy-protection-2026/)

## Part 4: Surveillance of Civic Activity — Documented Cases

### 4.1 Protest Surveillance

The Electronic Frontier Foundation documented that more than 50 federal, state, and local agencies ran hundreds of searches through Flock's national network in connection with protest activity. Officers frequently entered minimal justifications — "protest," "investigation," "suspect" — without specifying any crime.

**Documented protest events monitored via Flock ALPR:**

- **No Kings protests** (June 2025): 19 agencies ran dozens of searches. Agencies included Spokane County Sheriff's Office, Beaumont Police Department (Texas), San Bernardino County Sheriff's, and Tempe Police Department
- **February 50501 protests**: Carson City Sheriff's conducted searches across 178 networks
- **Hands Off protests** (April 2025): Multiple agencies
- **May Day rally** (Seminole County, Florida): Documented search activity
- **Pro-Palestinian protests**: Tulsa Police Department conducted at least 38 protest-related searches, including searches targeting pro-Palestinian activism
- **Jabari Peoples police shooting protests**: (Alabama)

**Tulsa PD pattern**: At least 38 protest-related searches documented, targeting deportation raid protests, pro-Palestinian activism, and No Kings demonstrations.

**Border Patrol**: Conducted 7 searches for a motorist who had extended his middle finger at Border Patrol vehicles transporting detainees — both the protest activity and the search represent First Amendment protected conduct used as the basis for surveillance.

Source: [EFF — Cops Using Flock to Surveil Protesters](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2025/11/how-cops-are-using-flock-safetys-alpr-network-surveil-protesters-and-activists)

### 4.2 Targeting of Protected Activities

Beyond protest monitoring, Flock data has been used to target:

**Abortion access**: Texas law enforcement used Flock to track a woman who had obtained abortion care in Illinois. The affidavit from that case reveals a search coded as "had an abortion, search for female." The same cross-state data flow that enabled this surveillance is structurally available to any law enforcement agency in the Flock network.

**Critical speech**: Kansas police used Flock to pursue a man who wrote a critical op-ed about the police department. Lebanon, Tennessee police searched Kyle, Texas camera data citing "Obstructing Justice – Suspicious female filming traffic stop and making comments about ICE" — both filming police and commenting on ICE are explicitly protected First Amendment activities.

**Activist organizations**: Delaware State Police conducted 9 Flock searches targeting Direct Action Everywhere (DxE) animal rights activists in March 2025. California Highway Patrol conducted dozens of searches related to DxE activity on May 27, 2025.

Source: [Texas Observer — License Plate Readers Texas](https://www.texasobserver.org/license-plate-readers-texas-privacy-advocates/) | [EFF — Cops Using Flock](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2025/11/how-cops-are-using-flock-safetys-alpr-network-surveil-protesters-and-activists)

### 4.3 Voter Intimidation and Civic Chilling Effect

No direct documented case yet exists of Flock data being used specifically to suppress voter registration or turnout. However, the chilling effect infrastructure is fully operational:

- Cameras placed near polling locations, voter registration events, or political meetings capture participant license plates without their knowledge
- That data is retained up to 30 days (or indefinitely for "investigative purposes") and accessible to thousands of agencies
- The data reveals which religious centers, healthcare clinics, political meetings, and organizing events individuals attend — precisely the information that enables targeted intimidation
- In states where ICE has access (directly or via local police proxy), surveillance of naturalization ceremony participants, immigrant civic engagement events, or immigrant neighborhood organizing creates a documented deterrence effect on civic participation

The structural effect on democratic participation mirrors the historical use of public records to identify civil rights demonstrators: the combination of lawful surveillance with political weaponization.

Source: [Daily Cal — Flock Cameras Threaten Local Democracies](https://www.dailycal.org/opinion/the_soapbox/flock-surveillance-cameras-threaten-local-democracies/article_1d5514db-aa83-4676-b50e-6f444ba9b88a.html) | [EFF — Cops Using Flock](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2025/11/how-cops-are-using-flock-safetys-alpr-network-surveil-protesters-and-activists)

## Part 5: Removal and Advocacy Pathways

### 5.1 What Has Worked: The 80-City Model

As of July 2026, more than 80 localities have canceled, deactivated, or rejected Flock contracts. The pattern of successful campaigns shares common elements:

**Information first**: Obtain camera locations, contract terms, data-sharing settings, and search logs through public records requests before organizing publicly. FOIA requests to the police department, city clerk, and city manager's office. FlockBlocker.org provides sample FOIA templates.

**Build the record**: Audit logs showing unauthorized agency access, immigration searches, or inadequate search justifications are the most powerful advocacy tools. Mountain View, Evanston, Dayton, and Syracuse all succeeded after audit data revealed unauthorized use.

**City council leverage**: Flock contracts require city council approval (or renewal). A majority of council members willing to vote against renewal, or unwilling to approve a new contract, is sufficient for termination. Contracts are time-limited and come up for renewal.

**Coalition**: Successful campaigns have combined civil liberties organizations, immigrant rights groups, reproductive rights organizations, faith communities, and neighborhood groups. The political coalition in Colorado included ACLU of Colorado, Colorado Immigrant Rights Coalition, and American Friends Service Committee.

**Media**: Local investigative journalism partnerships have been decisive in several cities. The Mountain View Voice's audit investigation, the Texas Standard investigation of Houston HPD, and The 74's school camera investigation all preceded contract cancellations.

### 5.2 Legal Pathways

**Contract termination review**: Examine whether the current contract contains exit clauses, breach of contract provisions, or misrepresentation remedies. The February 2026 terms changes retroactively affect renewals; contracts signed under prior terms may contain stronger protections. A municipal attorney's review focused on the data sale prohibition removal and perpetual licensing clauses may reveal grounds for contract termination for cause.

**State open records**: In most states, Flock search logs, contract terms, and sharing agreements are public records. Use state public records laws aggressively to obtain:
- Complete search logs with agency identification
- Data-sharing agreements with named agencies
- Internal communications about federal access
- Evidence of cameras installed or retained beyond contract termination

**Fourth Amendment litigation**: *Schmidt v. Norfolk* (4th Cir., pending) may establish that sufficiently dense ALPR networks constitute unconstitutional warrantless searches. Watch for the Fourth Circuit ruling. Organizations engaged in active Fourth Amendment Flock litigation: ACLU, EFF, Institute for Justice.

**State legislative advocacy**: The ACLU released model state ALPR legislation in February 2026. Priority provisions:
- 21-day maximum data retention (Washington model)
- Prohibition on immigration enforcement use
- Prohibition on use to monitor constitutionally protected activity
- Local-only data sharing unless judicial authorization obtained
- Required deletion upon contract cancellation
- Exclusionary rule for evidence obtained in violation

## Integration Notes

**Connection to `illegal-surveillance-camera-removal-guide.md`**: Flock cameras in public spaces operate under a different legal framework than the private surveillance cameras covered in that guide — the primary question is not reasonable expectation of privacy in a private space, but the lawfulness of mass collection of public-space movement data. However, the guide's state law framework (particularly audio recording consent laws and the two-party consent states) applies to any Flock camera that captures audio. The demand letter framework in the removal guide can be adapted for city council communications. See the Houston-specific guide for localized application.

Immigrant community members should be advised: (1) their vehicle movements are being recorded and retained, accessible to thousands of agencies; (2) disabling national sharing at the city level (if the city chooses to do so) does not prevent other agencies from accessing data already collected; (3) the 30-day retention window means that data from 30 days ago may already be in federal agency possession even if the city terminates its contract tomorrow. The Houston-specific guide covers local exposure in detail.

**Connection to litigation tracker**: *Schmidt v. Norfolk* (4th Cir.) is the controlling federal case. Monitor for ruling.
