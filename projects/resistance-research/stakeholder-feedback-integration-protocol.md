# Stakeholder Feedback Integration Protocol

**Prepared: May 5, 2026**

This document operationalizes the feedback loop between Phase 1 institutional contacts and Phase 2 domain selection. It answers three questions: (1) How do we ask Phase 1 contacts what they need from Phase 2? (2) What do specific feedback patterns tell us about the framework's fit, gaps, and Phase 2 priorities? (3) What is the timing and decision structure for converting Month 1 feedback into Month 3 research decisions?

The broader feedback infrastructure is documented in `feedback-integration-framework.md`. This protocol focuses narrowly on the structured feedback collection process during Phase 1 — what to ask, when to ask it, and how to interpret the answers.

---

## Part I: Feedback Collection Strategy

### Who Gets Feedback Requests

Feedback requests go to contacts who have already engaged substantively with Phase 1 outreach. Do not send feedback requests to contacts who have not opened the email, have not replied, or have replied only with an acknowledgment. A feedback request sent to a contact who has not yet engaged is a second cold outreach — it does not gather feedback, it generates friction.

**Feedback request cadence by engagement level**:

| Engagement Level | Definition | Feedback Request Timing |
|-----------------|------------|------------------------|
| Tier 1 Engaged — Substantive reply | Contact replied with domain-specific questions, routing confirmation, or request for materials | Week 2–3 after reply |
| Tier 1 Engaged — Confirmed routing | Contact confirms passing framework to a team or colleague | Week 3–4 after confirmation |
| Tier 2 Engaged — Brief acknowledgment | Contact replied but only with a courtesy acknowledgment | Month 2 follow-up; include feedback request in follow-up |
| No engagement | No reply, no open confirmation | No feedback request; redirect to alternative contact strategy |

## Part II: Feedback Template

The feedback template has two formats: a short async version (5–7 questions, deliverable by email) and a longer structured conversation guide (for contacts who offer a call). Most Phase 1 feedback will come via email; the conversation guide is for the highest-leverage contacts who offer direct engagement.

### Async Email Feedback Template (5 Questions)

Send this as a follow-up email 2–3 weeks after a substantive reply. Subject line: "Quick follow-up: 3 questions on framework use."

---

*[Contact first name],*

*Thank you for your earlier response — it confirmed that [specific domain] is landing as intended with your work on [specific issue they mentioned].*

*Three quick follow-up questions that would take 5 minutes to answer, and would directly shape what the framework develops next:*

*1. Which of the framework's 35+ domains has been most directly useful for your current work, and why?*

*2. What is the most significant gap you've noticed — a topic the framework should cover that it currently doesn't, or a domain that needs significantly deeper treatment?*

*3. In the next 6–12 months, what policy or litigation development do you expect to face where better analytical infrastructure would make the most difference?*

*Any answer, even a sentence on each, is valuable. If it's easier to talk briefly, I can work around your schedule.*

*[Signature]*

---

**Why these three questions**: Question 1 identifies which domains are generating active institutional use (demand signal for heatmap). Question 2 directly asks for Phase 2 domain candidates from an authoritative audience. Question 3 elicits the urgency-window information that determines Phase 2 timing — a contact who says "we expect a major Section 3 SCOTUS decision in fall 2026" is identifying an urgency window that should move Domain 41 up the priority matrix.

### Structured Conversation Guide (For Calls)

Use this if a contact offers a call or if the email feedback is substantive enough to warrant a follow-up conversation.

**Opening (2 minutes)**: "I want to understand how the framework is being used in practice — what's working, what's missing, and where the most important analytical gaps are for the work your organization does. I have about 6 questions; we can get through all of them in 15–20 minutes."

**Section A: Current Use (5 minutes)**

- "Which parts of the framework have you found yourself returning to or referencing in your work?"
- "Has any specific domain been cited or forwarded within your organization or network?"
- "Are there specific pieces of evidence or analysis in the framework that you've found particularly useful — anything that filled a gap you'd been trying to fill?"

**Section B: Gaps (5 minutes)**

- "What's the most important topic the framework doesn't adequately cover? Not just a gap you notice in the abstract — a topic where you've faced a real analytical need that the framework didn't meet."
- "Is there a domain that's in the framework but needs to go significantly deeper? A topic where the current treatment is a useful starting point but doesn't give you enough?"

**Section C: Phase 2 Priorities (5 minutes)**

- "In the next 6–12 months, what are the one or two policy or litigation developments you're most focused on where better analytical infrastructure would change your approach?"
- "If you were advising on what to research next, what would it be?"

**Closing (3 minutes)**: "Is there anyone else in your network who you think would benefit from the framework and hasn't received it yet? A colleague, a counterpart at a partner organization, or someone at a different institution who works on these issues?"

*The closing routing question is not primary feedback — it is distribution extension. But it is the most efficient mechanism for identifying warm introductions to Tier 2 contacts who were not in the original distribution list.*

## Part III: Red Flags — What Specific Feedback Means for Phase 2

Feedback from Tier 1 contacts falls into predictable categories. The following interpretation guide maps specific feedback patterns to Phase 2 planning implications.

### Red Flag 1: "The international comparisons don't hold up in practice"

**Signal**: Multiple contacts in the legal sector (AG offices, law school faculty, litigation advocacy) indicate that the international comparative evidence — foreign law precedents, comparative constitutional design examples — is not usable in their professional context.

**What it means**: The framework's comparative constitutional law dimension is a liability for the litigation and AG contact segments. AG offices and federal litigators operate in federal circuits that treat foreign law as non-persuasive; a brief that cites the German Bundesverfassungsgericht provision will be dismissed in most US circuit courts.

**Phase 2 implication**: Future domain research (particularly Domain 41 and Domain 46) should produce two versions of the comparative analysis: (a) a full comparative section with international evidence for academic and think tank audiences; and (b) a domestic-precedent-only version for AG and litigation audiences that draws comparisons from US constitutional history, US state analogues, and US comparative precedent rather than foreign law. This is a format modification, not a content modification — the comparative evidence remains in the document but is separated from the domestic legal argument structure.

### Red Flag 2: "The framework is too comprehensive — I can't find what I need quickly"

**Signal**: Contacts who engage positively with the framework's content report that the 35-domain scope is too large to navigate in a professional context with time constraints. They find the framework valuable but stop engaging because they can't quickly identify the two or three domains relevant to their work.

**What it means**: Navigation and entry-point friction is the adoption barrier, not content quality. This is the most common adoption failure mode for comprehensive policy frameworks — the Model Penal Code faced the same challenge and solved it with a detailed index and a "jurisdiction-specific quick reference" format.

**Phase 2 implication**: Before distributing new Phase 2 domains, produce sector-specific one-pagers and domain quick-reference guides. For AG offices: a two-page "top five domains for active federal litigation" brief. For think tanks: a domain index organized by institutional policy focus. This is distribution infrastructure work, not domain research — it should be prioritized over new domain production if this red flag appears in Phase 1 feedback.

### Red Flag 3: "The urgency analysis is outdated"

**Signal**: Contacts indicate that specific domain developments have moved faster than the framework documents — a domain that was described as an emerging threat has escalated to a crisis, or a policy window that was described as open has closed.

**What it means**: The framework's content currency is degrading faster than expected. This is a risk identified in `PHASE_1_EXECUTION_READINESS.md` for Domains 19f and 25, where Senate votes and executive actions were pending at time of launch.

**Phase 2 implication**: Establish a domain content update protocol before Phase 2 domains are finalized. Monthly update passes over the highest-urgency domains (Domain 37 election interference, Domain 29 prosecutorial weaponization, Domain 1 voting rights) should be built into the Phase 2 research calendar as standing work, not exceptional sessions.

### Red Flag 4: "This covers everything except [specific critical topic]"

**Signal**: Multiple contacts independently mention the same missing topic. The convergent gap identification is a high-confidence signal that a Phase 2 domain candidate has institutional demand.

**What it means**: This is the strongest possible demand signal for Phase 2 prioritization. Three independent contacts identifying the same gap scores a 5 on Criterion A (Institutional Demand Signal, weight 2.0x) in the priority matrix, which moves the corresponding domain to the top of the Phase 2 research queue regardless of its Day 0 ranking.

**Phase 2 implication**: Log the topic immediately in the domain performance heatmap as a "Strong" demand signal. If the topic corresponds to an existing Phase 2 candidate, move that candidate to Month 1–2 priority. If the topic does not correspond to any existing candidate, add it to the Phase 2 candidate list for evaluation.

### Red Flag 5: "The framework reads as partisan"

**Signal**: Contacts from center-right or institutionalist-conservative segments (Niskanen Center, R Street Institute, libertarian organizations) indicate that the framework's framing implies a partisan alignment that limits its cross-partisan credibility.

**What it means**: The framework's distribution strategy includes Tier 3 contacts from the center-right (Niskanen Center, R Street Institute, Cato Institute) precisely because cross-partisan credibility is a significant institutional adoption multiplier. If center-right contacts flag the framing as partisan, the framework cannot achieve its cross-partisan institutional credibility goal.

**Phase 2 implication**: Domain 43 (Civil Asset Forfeiture) and Domain 40 (Epistemic Infrastructure) are the highest-priority Phase 2 domains for establishing cross-partisan credibility because both have genuine bipartisan reform coalitions (libertarian-right + civil liberties left). Fast-tracking these domains is both a content priority and a credibility strategy response to this red flag.

## Part IV: Iteration Cycle — Month 1 to Month 3

### The Three-Step Cycle

**Month 1 — Collect and Log**

All feedback received in Month 1 — replies, routing confirmations, domain mentions, explicit requests — is logged in `DISTRIBUTION_EXECUTION_LOG.md` with the following fields:
- Contact name and tier
- Date of feedback
- Domain(s) mentioned
- Feedback category (positive use, gap identification, format friction, urgency update, cross-partisan signal)
- Any explicit Phase 2 request

By end of Month 1: summarize feedback in the "Month 1 Attribution Review" entry in `DISTRIBUTION_EXECUTION_LOG.md`.

**Month 2 — Analyze and Decide**

In the first week of Month 2, conduct a structured feedback analysis:

1. Review all Month 1 feedback entries.
2. Update Criterion A (Institutional Demand Signal) scores in `phase-2-domain-research-priority-matrix.md` based on observed demand signals.
3. Identify any red flags from Part III and document their Phase 2 implications.
4. Produce a "Month 2 Phase 2 Domain Selection Memo" — a single 200–300 word decision document that specifies: (a) which Phase 2 domain to begin research on immediately; (b) which domains to begin in Month 3; and (c) any distribution infrastructure work (one-pagers, sector-specific guides) that should precede domain production.

The Month 2 Phase 2 Domain Selection Memo is the primary output of the iteration cycle. It converts Phase 1 feedback data into a concrete Phase 2 research decision.

**Month 3 — Research Begins**

Research on Month 1–2 priority domains begins in Month 3, guided by the Month 2 memo. The exception is urgency-window domains with hard external deadlines (Domain 42 DEA June 29 — research begins in Month 1 regardless of feedback cycle).

By end of Month 3: at least one Phase 2 domain is in active research or completed. If the domain completes in Month 3, distribute immediately to the specific institutional contacts who expressed demand for that topic in Phase 1 feedback — targeted distribution to a pre-identified waiting audience is faster and more effective than broad wave distribution.

### What Happens If Feedback Is Insufficient

If Month 1 produces fewer than 3 substantive feedback responses from Tier 1 contacts, the iteration cycle has insufficient data to update Criterion A scores. In that case:

- Use the Day 0 priority ranking from `phase-2-domain-research-priority-matrix.md` unchanged.
- Begin research on the urgency-window domains (Domain 42, Domain 39) regardless of feedback confirmation.
- Defer Month 3–4 domains until Month 3 feedback improves data quality.

The feedback cycle is designed to sharpen Phase 2 decisions, not to block them. Insufficient feedback is information — it means Phase 1 distribution has not yet reached the activation threshold, and Phase 2 decisions should proceed on the prior probability ranking rather than on demand signal data that has not yet materialized.

## Part V: Routing the Framework Forward — Extending the Distribution Network

The feedback template's closing question — "Is there anyone else in your network who you think would benefit?" — is the single highest-leverage distribution extension action available during Phase 1. A warm introduction from a Tier 1 contact to a new institutional contact is worth 5–10 times the impact of a cold outreach email to the same contact.

**Protocol for routing leads**:
1. When a contact offers a name, follow up within 48 hours with a brief email: "I appreciate you recommending [Name]. I'm going to reach out to them and mention your introduction — is that all right?"
2. If confirmed: send a personalized outreach email to the new contact, referencing the warm introduction. This contact is treated as Tier 1 regardless of where they would have been ranked in the original contact list.
3. Log the routing chain in `DISTRIBUTION_EXECUTION_LOG.md`: "Routing event: [Contact A] → [Contact B], date, domain context." This routing chain is the second-order diffusion evidence that `attribution-measurement-plan.md` identifies as a high-confidence attribution signal.

Every routing event is both a distribution success and an attribution data point. A framework that is routing through institutional networks is exhibiting the diffusion behavior that the Rogers S-curve model predicts for the early adopter phase — it is one of the clearest signals that Phase 1 is working as designed.
