# Phase 3 Contract and Collaboration Framework

**Purpose**: This document establishes the working terms for Phase 3 research collaboration. It covers compensation structure, intellectual property, publication rights, communication cadence, exit procedures, and contingency protocols. It is designed to prevent ambiguity, not to create legal formalism. The project is built on a collaborative relationship; these terms exist so both parties have the same expectations before work begins.

Share this document with the researcher during the offer conversation (October 1) and confirm written acknowledgment before November 4.

---

## Section 1: Engagement Structure — Paid vs. Volunteer

Phase 3 research is compensated work, not volunteer work. The rationale is explicit: volunteer engagements for substantive knowledge work fail at a higher rate than paid engagements when the volunteer encounters competing demands on their time. A researcher who is not being paid will deprioritize Phase 3 obligations when a paid opportunity or academic deadline competes. Given that Phase 3 has a hard January 31, 2027 deadline tied to the January 3 Congressional window, volunteer risk is unacceptable.

**Compensation structure**: Hourly rate negotiated at time of offer, documented in the offer letter. Suggested range:
- Advanced PhD candidate (pre-defense): $35–40/hour
- Recent PhD recipient (within 2 years): $45–50/hour
- Postdoctoral researcher with 2+ publications: $50–55/hour

**Invoicing**: Researcher invoices every two weeks, submitted by the first and fifteenth of each month. Payment within 10 business days of invoice receipt. No payment is contingent on publication, distribution outcomes, or external adoption of the research — payment is for research production, not for advocacy results.

**Total budget estimate**: At 120–160 hours over 13 weeks (solo configuration), budget range is $4,200–$8,800 depending on hourly rate. At 80–100 hours each for a 2-person team, per-researcher budget is $2,800–$5,500. Include a 10% contingency buffer in the project budget for scope overages confirmed in advance by the project coordinator.

**Expense reimbursement**: Documented research expenses are reimbursed at cost with receipts. Covered expenses include: JSTOR annual subscription ($199), PACER document fees (up to $30/quarter), any academic journal articles not accessible through JSTOR or library access (per-article fees up to $10 each, with prior coordinator approval above $50 total). Not covered: general computing equipment, software, or expenses incurred without prior authorization.

## Section 2: Intellectual Property

**Ownership**: The research outputs produced under this engagement — all written documents, source databases, expert contact notes, and zone synthesis drafts — are work-for-hire owned by the project principal (the user). The researcher retains no ownership interest in the produced documents.

**What this means in practice**: The project coordinator can edit, excerpt, combine, redistribute, and publish the research outputs without the researcher's approval. The researcher cannot post full copies of the produced documents on their own website, academic page, or institutional repository without prior written authorization from the project coordinator.

**What this does not restrict**: The researcher retains full rights to the ideas and arguments they develop during the engagement — meaning they can use the analytical frameworks, arguments, and findings from their Phase 3 research as the basis for independent academic writing, provided that independent writing is substantially different from the produced documents (new structure, new framing, original synthesis) and is not a republication of the Phase 3 documents in a different venue.

This is the standard distinction between a work product (owned by the commissioning party) and the ideas and expertise developed while producing it (retained by the researcher). The researcher should consult with their institution's academic freedom office if they have questions about how this applies to their specific academic employment context.

## Section 3: Publication Rights

**Attribution in Phase 3 documents**: Phase 3 research documents will attribute research contributions in the following way: "Research assistance provided by [Researcher Name], [Institutional Affiliation if applicable]." The project coordinator will be listed as the primary author or as the producing entity. If the researcher makes intellectual contributions that go beyond research execution — developing an original analytical framework, identifying a novel line of argument not in the preliminary research — the coordinator will discuss co-authorship on a case-by-case basis.

**Researcher's independent publication rights**: The researcher may publish academic work that uses Phase 3 research materials as the basis for independent scholarship, subject to the following conditions:

1. The independent publication is submitted no earlier than six months after the Phase 3 production document is completed and in distribution (i.e., no earlier than August 1, 2027 for work completed by January 31, 2027)
2. The independent publication cites the Phase 3 research document if it uses findings directly developed in that document
3. The independent publication is a genuine academic or policy contribution that adds substantively to the Phase 3 analysis — not a repackaging of the Phase 3 document for a different venue
4. The researcher notifies the project coordinator before submission so the coordinator can confirm there is no conflict with planned distribution or future Phase 3/4 publication

The six-month window is not a restriction on the researcher's career — it is a protection against the Phase 3 distribution being preempted by a parallel publication that covers the same ground before the primary advocacy audience has seen the Phase 3 document.

**Credit for distribution outcomes**: If Phase 3 research is cited in congressional testimony, a law review article, or a major advocacy campaign, the researcher will be notified and thanked. These acknowledgments are not contractual obligations but will be honored as a matter of professional practice.

## Section 4: Communication Cadence

**Weekly check-in**: Asynchronous by default. Every Monday, the researcher sends a 3–5 sentence status update to the project coordinator: what was completed in the previous week, what is planned for the current week, and whether any blockers or decision points need coordinator input. The coordinator responds within 48 hours. This is the primary accountability mechanism.

**Synchronous calls**: One call per month (30 minutes) to review the previous month's output, discuss any substantive questions about the research argument, and confirm the coming month's zone targets. Monthly calls are scheduled in advance and are held on the first Thursday of each month (November 6, December 4, January 8). If a call must be rescheduled, it is rescheduled within the same week — not deferred to the following month.

**Real-time communication**: For time-sensitive questions (a source cannot be verified, a bill status has changed significantly, a contingency trigger has been hit), the researcher emails the project coordinator directly with a "time-sensitive" flag in the subject line. The coordinator responds within 24 hours to time-sensitive flags. For non-urgent questions, email is the primary channel and the coordinator responds within 48 hours.

**Feedback on drafts**: When the researcher submits a zone output or draft document, the coordinator returns substantive written feedback within 5 business days. Feedback will be organized by: (1) required changes before the zone is considered complete, and (2) optional suggestions for improvement. The researcher is not required to implement optional suggestions but should acknowledge having read them. Required changes are incorporated before the next zone begins.

**No unilateral scope expansion**: If the researcher identifies a research angle that was not in the onboarding kit and believes it should be added to the scope, they flag it in the weekly update. The coordinator makes a scope decision within 48 hours. The researcher does not expand scope without explicit coordinator approval — this is not a restriction on intellectual initiative but a protection against timeline overruns.

## Section 5: Exit and Contingency Procedures

### If the Researcher Exits Before Completion

A researcher exit at any point before January 31, 2027 is a contingency event that activates the following protocol:

**Exit before Week 4 (before Zone K-2 is complete)**: This is a high-impact exit. Action: immediately post to the SSN democracy governance listserv with the same researcher opportunity posting from the Recruitment Plan, noting an immediate start date. The existing Zone K-1 output is complete and usable by a replacement researcher. Onboarding for a replacement researcher requires 5–7 days; the timeline is compressed but recoverable. The 2-person configuration should be activated if recruitment takes more than 10 days.

**Exit between Week 4 and Week 8 (after Zone K-2, before Domain H begins)**: This is a medium-impact exit. Action: assess whether the completed Domain K zones are sufficient to produce a partial Domain K distribution document. If Zone K-1 through K-3 are complete, a Zone K-4 can be produced by the project coordinator with the existing materials. Domain H becomes a Phase 4 obligation rather than a Phase 3 deliverable if no replacement researcher is found by November 30.

**Exit after Week 8 (during Domain H work)**: This is a low-impact exit if Domain K is complete. Domain K can be distributed on its January schedule without Domain H. Domain H extension work is brought to whatever completion point the researcher reached, and the existing Domain H production document (June 6, 2026 version) is used as the distribution document in place of the extended version.

**Partial work product ownership**: All work produced up to the exit point is owned by the project and is usable by the project coordinator or a replacement researcher. The departing researcher is paid for all hours worked to date. Payment is not contingent on project completion.

**Resignation protocol**: The researcher provides minimum 5 business days' written notice before stopping work, delivers all in-progress drafts, source notes, and zone documents in the project's shared folder, and holds a 30-minute handoff call with the project coordinator. Researchers who complete these exit obligations are eligible for a positive professional reference regardless of why the engagement ended.

### If a Key Source or Access Is Lost

**JSTOR access failure**: If the researcher's JSTOR access is suspended or limited, notify the coordinator immediately. The coordinator will provide the specific articles needed via institutional library access or inter-library loan. Phase 3 has a documented list of the 20 most critical JSTOR-hosted sources in PHASE_3_DOMAIN_K_SOURCES_AND_CONTACTS.md and PHASE_3_DOMAIN_H_SOURCE_STRATEGY.md — these can be pulled in advance if JSTOR access is uncertain.

**Congress.gov downtime**: Congress.gov experiences periodic maintenance downtime. If the site is unavailable for more than 24 hours during Zone K-1, use GovTrack (govtrack.us) as a secondary bill tracking source. All bill texts are also available through the Senate and House committee press release archives.

**Expert contact non-response**: If a priority expert contact does not respond after two outreach attempts, document the non-response in the zone output and proceed without their input. Phase 3's research quality is based on primary sources and published scholarship, not on exclusive expert interviews. Expert contacts are supplementary verification, not the primary research base.

## Section 6: The Working Relationship

This section exists because the most common failure mode in short-term research collaborations is unspoken expectation mismatch — not bad faith. These are the explicit expectations on both sides.

**What the researcher can expect from the project coordinator**:
- Written feedback on zone outputs within 5 business days
- Clear, specific required-change requests (not vague aesthetic preferences)
- Prompt response to time-sensitive questions (within 24 hours)
- Payment on schedule, no exceptions
- Acknowledgment of good work, including public attribution where appropriate
- Honest communication about whether the project is on track

**What the project coordinator expects from the researcher**:
- Weekly status updates every Monday, without prompting
- Zone outputs that meet the writing standard described in the Onboarding Kit before submission — not draft material sent for the coordinator to complete
- Honest early communication about schedule disruptions (within 24 hours of knowing about them, not at the end of an affected week)
- Transparent communication about uncertainty in the research (stating when a source is contested is better than false confidence)
- Iteration on feedback without defensiveness — the feedback is about the document, not about the researcher's quality as a scholar

**The phase 3 research is not a test of the researcher's academic credentials**. It is a production engagement to create tools for democratic advocacy. A researcher who approaches it as a performance of academic rigor rather than a service to a concrete advocacy purpose will produce documents that are technically excellent and practically unusable. The measure of success is whether a Senate Judiciary Committee staffer reads Domain K and immediately understands what legislation to introduce, and whether a Fix the Court campaign director reads Zone K-4 and knows exactly which contacts to call.
