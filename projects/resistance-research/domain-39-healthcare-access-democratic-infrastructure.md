# Domain 39: Healthcare Access as Democratic Infrastructure
## How Medical Exclusion Functions as Civic Exclusion

*Production document — Phase 2, Session May 15, 2026*

---

## Lead Finding

Healthcare access is not a parallel concern to democratic participation — it is a prerequisite for it. The empirical record is now clear enough to state as a structural claim: when healthcare infrastructure is removed from a community, civic participation declines. When healthcare debt overwhelms a household, political engagement becomes a luxury that household cannot afford. When Medicaid enrollment offices lose capacity, voter registration infrastructure disappears from the communities most dependent on it. When maternal mortality concentrates in specific racial communities, civic voice is permanently silenced. When disability programs are defunded, the most politically marginalized Americans lose the legal and logistical assistance that makes voting possible.

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA, P.L. 119-21, signed July 4, 2025) accelerates all five pathways simultaneously. The Congressional Budget Office projects 7.5 million people will lose Medicaid or CHIP coverage by 2034, with 5.3 million of those losses driven by work reporting requirements alone. As of January 2026, 417 rural hospitals are classified as vulnerable to closure — one-third of all rural facilities in the country. The Protection and Advocacy for Voting Accessibility program that has distributed $130 million in disability voter assistance since 2003 faces proposed elimination in the Trump administration's FY2026 budget. Each of these is simultaneously a health crisis and a democracy crisis.

The dominant healthcare advocacy frame treats these harms as social welfare failures. That frame is accurate but strategically incomplete. This document provides the democratic design argument: healthcare access is civic infrastructure, and dismantling it is a mechanism of civic exclusion — one that concentrates its effects on the same communities already most structurally disadvantaged in the democratic system.

**Timing context for this document**: HHS must issue an interim final rule on OBBBA work requirement implementation by June 1, 2026. Unlike typical rulemaking, this rule is explicitly exempt from public notice and comment requirements under the OBBBA text. Organizations seeking to influence implementation must act through litigation, legislative pressure, and advocacy campaigns before January 1, 2027, when work requirements take effect. This document is designed to equip those efforts with the democratic participation argument that has been largely absent from healthcare advocacy to date.

## The Empirical Architecture: Five Causal Pathways

### Pathway 1: Rural Hospital Closures Reduce Voter Turnout

The most direct evidence in the domain: a study published in the *American Political Science Review* in August 2025 examined 10.5 million rural voters across the 2016, 2018, and 2020 elections and found that communities affected by hospital closures experienced a **3.8 percentage-point reduction in voter turnout** in the raw effect analysis. The study, by Cox, Epp, and Shepherd, used individual-level fixed effects panel regression, merging L2 voter files with University of North Carolina hospital closure data.

The effect was temporally structured: the demobilizing impact was largest 4–6 months before an election (6% turnout reduction), smaller 1–3 months before (4% reduction), and returned to baseline when closures occurred 7–12 months out. This timing pattern suggests the mechanism is not purely attitudinal — it is a capacity disruption. Hospital closures remove healthcare workers who often anchor civic infrastructure, destabilize the local economy that political participation requires, and eliminate the community anchor institution around which organizing occurs.

The affected populations are not randomly distributed. The study found the largest effects among economically disadvantaged groups — particularly individuals over 65 with lower income, who experienced nearly 3% reduced turnout even in fixed-effects models controlling for individual characteristics. These are the voters whose political engagement is most dependent on stable community infrastructure, and who have the fewest alternative resources when that infrastructure collapses.

**Current scale of the crisis**: As of January 2026, Chartis Health analytics classifies 417 rural hospitals as vulnerable to closure — one-third of all rural facilities nationally. Texas leads with 50 vulnerable facilities; Kansas has 44; Tennessee, at 61% of its rural hospitals vulnerable, has the highest proportion of any state. A 15% cut in Medicaid revenue would cost rural hospitals over $1.8 billion nationwide — "equivalent to more than 21,000 full-time hospital employee salaries," per the Chartis 2026 Rural Health State of the State report.

The SPLC's "Critical Condition" report identifies at least 99 rural hospitals at risk of closing across Alabama, Florida, Georgia, Louisiana, and Mississippi alone. Lawrence Medical Center in Alabama shuttered its emergency department in May 2025, leaving Lawrence County without emergency services. The University of Michigan School of Public Health analysis documents that OBBBA Medicaid cuts will particularly devastate hospitals in counties with high poverty and Medicaid participation — the same facilities serving communities with the thinnest civic participation margins.

The OBBBA's Rural Health Transformation Fund ($50 billion over 5 years, allocated across all 50 states) was designed as an offset, but individual state allocations range from $147 million (New Jersey) to $281 million (Texas), and in some states amount to less than $100 per rural resident. The Fund covers approximately 37% of estimated rural Medicaid cuts. The remaining 63% represents uncompensated revenue loss that will, according to the hospital finance literature, flow through to service cuts, staff reductions, and closure decisions — each carrying the 3.8 percentage-point turnout penalty documented in the *APSR* study.

**The democratic design argument**: The closures are not geographically random. They concentrate in states that declined Medicaid expansion (states that made a political choice not to extend coverage to their low-income residents), in counties that already have the lowest voter participation rates, and in communities whose political representation is most diluted by the structural barriers documented throughout this framework. A policy that removes hospital infrastructure from these communities is, empirically, a policy that reduces their voter turnout. Whether or not that is its intent, it is its documented effect.

### Pathway 2: The Medicaid/NVRA Nexus — Enrollment Offices as Voter Registration Infrastructure

The National Voter Registration Act (52 U.S.C. § 20506, originally 42 U.S.C. § 1973gg-5) requires public assistance agencies — including Medicaid offices — to offer voter registration to eligible applicants. This is not aspirational policy; it is a statutory mandate. Medicaid enrollment offices are, by law, mandatory voter registration sites. They are the primary voter registration access point for low-income Americans.

For thirty years, this mandate has been largely unmet. Since 1995, the total number of voter registration applications from public assistance agencies has never surpassed the number submitted in the first election cycle after the NVRA was enacted. The Institute for Responsive Government documents that between 2018–2020, public assistance agencies collectively produced only 1.7 million voter registration forms out of 104 million forms collected nationally — approximately 1.6% of all registrations. Voter registration rates among the populations served by Medicaid are 15–20 points lower than among wealthier citizens.

**The Automatic Voter Registration difference**: The research on Medicaid Automatic Voter Registration (AVR) demonstrates what NVRA compliance could achieve if implemented well. In Oregon, Medicaid AVR would reach 85% of the state's unregistered voter-eligible population. In Colorado, Medicaid AVR is projected to reach approximately 755,000 eligible but unregistered voters. As of 2024, eight jurisdictions have enacted AVR at Medicaid: Colorado, Nevada, Massachusetts, Washington D.C., New Mexico, Minnesota, Oregon, and Michigan. In the remaining 42 states, traditional paper-based NVRA compliance produces the documented 1.6% rate — a nearly 50-fold gap.

**What the OBBBA cuts do to this infrastructure**: The OBBBA's administrative architecture — 6-month redetermination cycles (doubled from the prior 12-month standard), compressed state Medicaid agency budgets, work requirement documentation demands — reduces the capacity of Medicaid enrollment offices to offer voter registration at precisely the moment enrollment volume increases due to coverage churn. Each 6-month redetermination is a new NVRA-mandated voter registration opportunity. States without AVR rely on paper forms with documented under-2% uptake. Staff reductions in enrollment offices make even that minimal NVRA compliance harder to sustain.

A critical framing clarification: Medicaid does not automatically register people to vote, and no administration has claimed it does. The NVRA requires offer, not automatic registration. The democratic harm is not automation — it is that Medicaid enrollment offices are the statutory voter registration access point for low-income populations, and degrading those offices degrades voter registration infrastructure for a specific constituency. A 2026 Poynter fact-check confirms this distinction.

**The litigation and political leverage**: NVRA Section 7 compliance is enforceable. The Department of Justice under prior administrations used NVRA compliance investigations to compel states to improve public assistance agency registration rates. Under the current administration, that enforcement mechanism is unavailable. But private litigation by voting rights organizations under Section 7 remains viable — and Domain 39's Medicaid-NVRA infrastructure argument provides the analytical foundation for framing such litigation in democratic participation terms rather than purely procedural ones.

### Pathway 3: Medical Debt as Civic Exclusion

An estimated 100 million people in the United States — 41% of adults — carry debt related to unpaid medical bills. The CFPB estimates total medical debt at $195–220 billion. The KFF Health Care Debt Survey documents that 14 million Americans owe $1,000 or more. The burden is disproportionately concentrated among Black Americans and low-to-middle income households.

Medical debt does not merely harm finances. It reallocates time, attention, and cognitive bandwidth. Political participation — registering to vote, traveling to a polling place, attending a community meeting, contacting an elected official — requires the same resources that medical debt depletes. For working-class voters who cannot absorb the time cost of civic engagement without material support, medical debt is a participatory tax on their democratic voice.

The specific mechanism is not attitudinal disengagement (the "why bother" argument common in civic participation research). It is resource constraint. Research on financial scarcity consistently documents that resource constraints reduce cognitive bandwidth available for non-immediate tasks — the same mechanism that explains why food insecurity reduces educational achievement. Medical debt operates the same way on civic engagement: it shifts the household's attentional resources away from activities that are important but not immediate, including political participation.

**The OBBBA coverage loss projection makes this worse**: CBO projects 1.3 million additional uninsured Americans in 2026, rising to 5.2 million by 2027 and 6.8 million by 2028. Uninsured Americans who incur medical costs carry that cost as debt — medical debt that the CFPB's 2024 rule (removing medical debt from credit reports) was designed to partially address. That rule has been blocked by the Trump administration. Premium Tax Credit expiration increases uninsured rates in marketplace coverage. The interaction of coverage loss and premium tax credit elimination creates a medical debt generation mechanism across a multi-year horizon.

Third Way's analysis documents the GOP health cuts as a "medical debt disaster" generator: "Before the OBBBA, 100 million Americans already carried medical debt. After it, millions more will join them." This is not merely a health equity argument. It is a democratic participation argument: each percentage point of Americans absorbed into the medical debt burden is a reduction in the civic engagement capacity of those households.

### Pathway 4: Maternal Mortality and Community Civic Capacity Loss

Black women in the United States die in childbirth at 50.3 deaths per 100,000 live births — 3.5 times the rate for white women (14.5 per 100,000) and over four times the rate for Asian women (10.7), per CDC 2023 data released in February 2025. This disparity did not narrow between 2022 and 2023. American Indian and Alaska Native women face similar rates of preventable maternal death. The racial gap is widening, not closing.

These are not statistical abstractions. They are the permanent removal of civic participants from communities already facing the most concentrated structural barriers to political representation. Women who die in childbirth cannot vote. They cannot run for office, organize their neighbors, or raise children who will become voters. The communities with the highest maternal mortality rates are communities whose civic engagement capacity is depleted by grief, medical trauma, economic loss, and the downstream effects of preventable family dissolution.

**The historical evidence for civic power as a health intervention**: The relationship between political power and maternal and infant health is documented with longitudinal precision. After women gained the right to vote in 1920, maternal and infant mortality rates declined significantly. When the Voting Rights Act of 1965 expanded Black political power, infant mortality rates dropped and the Black-white disparity narrowed. A 2024 study published in the *American Journal of Public Health* (Rushovich et al.) found that Black infant deaths in counties covered by the VRA decreased by an average of 11.4 deaths per county beyond unexposed areas — a 17.5% reduction — in the five years following the VRA's passage. The mechanism was documented: increased government funding to high-Black-population counties, reduced racial wage gaps, and lower arrest rates. Political power, operationalized through voting rights, improved the social conditions that determine infant survival.

This relationship runs in both directions. Maternal mortality reduces civic capacity in the same communities where political power is most constrained. When the communities whose health outcomes are worst also have the least political representation, the mortality is self-reinforcing: political exclusion produces health outcomes that further reduce political voice.

**The legislative vehicle and the HHS CDC dismantlement**: H.R. 7973 (119th Congress), the Momnibus Act, introduced March 18, 2026 by Representatives Underwood and Adams, provides $100 million annually from 2027–2031 for community-based organizations working to reduce racial bias in maternal care, and extends Medicaid and CHIP coverage from 60 days to a full year postpartum. The Trump administration has moved in the opposite direction: the March 27, 2025 HHS reorganization laid off most staff in the CDC's Division of Reproductive Health, halted community-based maternal health grants, and closed federal offices that coordinated state and local maternal health equity programs.

Congressional resolutions S.Res.67 and H.Res.119 (119th Congress) declared racism a public health crisis and specifically cited the 2-4x Black-white maternal mortality disparity. These resolutions provide the legislative acknowledgment that maternal mortality racial disparities are a structural, not biological, phenomenon — created and sustained by policy choices that can be reversed through different policy choices.

**The democratic framing shift**: The Momnibus coalition — Black Mamas Matter Alliance, Association of Maternal and Child Health Programs, National Birth Equity Collaborative — frames maternal mortality prevention primarily as a health equity demand. Domain 39 adds the democratic equity argument: preventing the preventable deaths of Black and Indigenous women is protecting the civic voice of their communities. Organizations fighting for maternal mortality reduction and organizations fighting for voting rights are fighting for the same communities. They should be coordinated as such.

### Pathway 5: Disability Disenfranchisement — Three Compounding Mechanisms

People with disabilities face civic exclusion through three distinct but compounding mechanisms: guardianship laws that directly strip voting rights; defunding of the programs that enable disabled voters to exercise the rights they retain; and work requirements that impose participation barriers on disability beneficiaries whose conditions do not meet the formal exemption definition.

**Guardianship-based disenfranchisement**: Approximately 1.3 million adults were under guardianship or conservatorship in the United States as of 2018. Nine states — Arizona, Louisiana, Mississippi, Missouri, South Carolina, Tennessee, Utah, Virginia, and West Virginia — automatically bar voting by people under guardianship, regardless of individual capacity. In practice, full guardianships remain the default even where law requires courts to consider less restrictive alternatives: Virginia data shows 96% of guardianships granted between 2015–2021 were full guardianships, with only 10 rights restorations among over 4,000 cases.

The U.S. Department of Justice issued guidance in 2024 stating that states may not categorically disqualify people under guardianship from voting based on disability status alone. But without enforcement, that guidance has produced minimal change in practice. The Center for American Progress documents that 52 bills addressing guardianship-based voting rights disenfranchisement were introduced across 20 states since 2023 — only 7 became law, and of those that addressed voting, none expanded voting rights. The bill activity shows political awareness of the problem; the lack of successful legislation shows the structural barriers to reform.

**PAVA program defunding**: Congress established the Protection and Advocacy for Voting Accessibility program under the Help America Vote Act of 2002. Since 2003, PAVA has distributed over $130 million to state protection and advocacy agencies to ensure full participation in the electoral process for people with disabilities — covering voter registration assistance, polling place accessibility monitoring, and legal representation for disabled voters facing barriers. In FY2024, $10 million was allocated, with each P&A agency receiving an average of $161,842.

The Trump administration's FY2026 budget proposed eliminating P&A program funding. The Administration for Community Living — the agency through which PAVA funds flow — had nearly half its staff laid off by Secretary Kennedy in April 2025. Without PAVA, the primary legal and technical assistance program for disabled voters at the state level disappears. This is not a marginal reduction; it is the elimination of the infrastructure that makes voting accessible for the most marginalized segment of the electorate.

The Bipartisan Policy Center's analysis of the P&A system documents that these agencies provide the only available voting assistance in many rural and under-resourced communities. They train poll workers on disability accommodations, provide absentee ballot assistance, and bring litigation when polling places are inaccessible. Their defunding removes a legal accountability mechanism for the ADA's voting rights provisions.

**Work requirements as disability exclusion**: The OBBBA work requirement exempts individuals who are "medically frail or otherwise [have] special medical needs" — but this exemption is structured to exclude people with many conditions that substantially limit work capacity without meeting the formal definition of "medically frail." Georgetown CCF's April 2025 analysis documents the specific inadequacy: two-thirds of people with disabilities enrolled in Medicaid do not receive SSI, making SSI-based disability status an unreliable exemption criterion. People with episodic conditions (multiple sclerosis, lupus, mental health conditions with variable impairment, chronic pain), those who cannot access documentation due to lack of transportation or internet, and those whose conditions are contested by providers will not qualify for exemptions even though their conditions genuinely prevent consistent 80-hour monthly work compliance.

The National Health Law Program's December 2024 analysis, "Unfit to Work? How Medicaid Work Requirements Hurt People with Disabilities," documents that the medically frail standard requires physician documentation that many disability patients cannot access — precisely because they lack the stable healthcare coverage that would give them a regular physician. The requirement traps people in a documentation catch-22: prove disability to a physician you cannot see because you lost coverage because you could not prove disability.

**The interaction effect**: The three disability pathways compound. A person under full guardianship in Virginia cannot vote; a person with episodic disability in Tennessee loses coverage under work requirements and loses PAVA assistance simultaneously; a person with mobility impairment in rural Kansas has polling place barriers that PAVA would have addressed, but no PAVA agency with resources to help. The cumulative effect is systematic exclusion of disability communities from political participation — not through any single explicit exclusion, but through the layered elimination of the infrastructure that makes their participation possible.

## The FQHC Opportunity: Underutilized Democratic Infrastructure

Federally Qualified Health Centers serve 31.5 million patients annually — approximately 1 in 11 people across the nation. Their patient populations are disproportionately low-income, uninsured, and from communities of color: the same populations with the lowest voter registration rates and the most structural barriers to civic participation.

FQHCs are approved voter registration locations under NVRA Section 7. HRSA guidance explicitly encourages FQHCs to support nonpartisan voter registration. The 2023 Healthy Democracy Kit research (PMC 10213595) documented 141 FQHCs ordering toolkit kits, with FQHC participation at 23% of institutional participants. Among all participating healthcare settings, the toolkit generated 27,317 voter registrations and 17,216 mail-in ballot requests.

Clinical research confirms the potential. A Bronx primary care initiative registered 89% of eligible voters in 2012. A youth-focused program achieved 44% turnout among registrants in 2018 midterms. PMC 10480081 (Stanicki et al., *Health Services Research*, 2023) documents that adding hospitals to DMV-based voter registration access significantly reduces the geographic barriers that prevent Black and Hispanic voters from registering: only 14% of Americans lack a driver's license, but those individuals are disproportionately people of color and low-income, making FQHCs and hospitals the most accessible registration points for these communities.

**The structural gap**: FQHC underutilization for voter registration is not attitudinal — healthcare workers in these settings consistently express support for voter registration assistance when surveyed. The barriers are structural: FQHC staffing is stretched thin, voter registration training is not integrated into standard workflows, and there is no reimbursement incentive structure for voter registration activities (unlike most clinical services).

**The reform lever**: The simplest expansion of FQHC voter registration capacity would cost less and reach more low-income voters than almost any other democracy investment currently being debated. HRSA already has authority to encourage NVRA compliance as a condition of FQHC grant funding. A CMS guidance document or condition of participation update requiring FQHC NVRA compliance documentation could reach 31.5 million patient contacts annually at the cost of a federal guidance document. Under the current administration, that regulatory path is closed — but it establishes the legislative reform target for future administrations and the current basis for organizing within the FQHC network using existing authorities.

## Evidence-Based Reform Architecture

The five pathways generate a corresponding set of reforms. These are not aspirational positions — they are the specific statutory and regulatory changes that would interrupt the documented causal mechanisms.

### 1. Statutory Minimum Coverage Floor for Rural Healthcare Infrastructure

**Mechanism addressed**: Rural hospital closure → voter turnout decline.

**Reform**: Federal legislation establishing minimum rural healthcare infrastructure standards — emergency department capacity per county population, labor and delivery unit proximity standards, minimum Medicaid reimbursement floors for rural hospitals — that cannot be cut through reconciliation processes. The 3.8pp turnout penalty documented in the *APSR* study establishes the democratic rationale for treating rural hospital infrastructure as civic infrastructure, not merely social welfare.

**Legislative vehicle**: Rural Emergency Medical Care Act framework extended to include minimum service requirements. Alternatively, a stand-alone Rural Hospital Stabilization Act with Medicaid reimbursement floors for facilities serving populations above a poverty threshold.

**Interim advocacy lever**: State-level Medicaid directed payment programs (restricted but not eliminated under OBBBA) can supplement rural hospital reimbursement. Georgetown CCF's February 2026 analysis documents these as the primary remaining tool states have to prevent closures while federal reform is unavailable.

### 2. Medicaid Automatic Voter Registration (National AVR Standard)

**Mechanism addressed**: Medicaid/NVRA infrastructure degradation → voter registration decline.

**Reform**: Federal legislation requiring all states to implement Automatic Voter Registration at Medicaid enrollment offices, with CMS enforcement authority and withholding of administrative matching funds for non-compliance. The Institute for Responsive Government's research demonstrates that AVR produces 85–90% registration rates where implemented; traditional NVRA paper-form compliance produces 1.6%.

**Legislative vehicle**: The NVRA modernization bills introduced in prior Congresses (American Voting Rights Act) provide model language. State-level: the eight states with existing Medicaid AVR provide legislative templates.

**Current advocacy lever**: NVRA Section 7 private right of action. Voting rights organizations can bring enforcement litigation in states with documented non-compliance — establishing that the NVRA mandates meaningful voter registration access, not pro forma paper form distribution.

### 3. Guardianship Reform and PAVA Funding Restoration

**Mechanism addressed**: Disability disenfranchisement through guardianship laws and P&A defunding.

**Reform (guardianship)**: Federal legislation requiring individualized capacity determination before stripping voting rights of guardianship wards, with a presumption in favor of retained voting rights. Eighteen states already provide this protection; nine states' blanket bans are the target.

**Reform (PAVA)**: Restore and expand P&A voting access funding to pre-cut levels, and designate PAVA funding as mandatory (not discretionary) to protect it from future reconciliation cuts. The $10 million annual investment that reaches 1.3 million potential beneficiaries is among the highest-leverage democracy investments available.

**Legislative vehicle**: PAVA Inclusion Act (H.R.7326, 117th Congress) provides model language. The Bipartisan Policy Center's January 2024 analysis documents bipartisan support for P&A funding that can be mobilized in appropriations negotiations.

### 4. Disability-Inclusive Work Requirement Standards

**Mechanism addressed**: Work requirements as disability exclusion mechanism.

**Reform**: If work requirements cannot be repealed, federal regulations should require (1) an individualized capacity assessment rather than SSI-based disability determination for exemptions; (2) presumptive eligibility during documentation periods; (3) an automatic exemption for individuals with episodic or fluctuating conditions documented in any medical record; (4) legal aid resources for exemption appeals funded at a ratio of 1 legal aid staff per 10,000 work-requirement-subject enrollees.

**Legislative vehicle**: OBBBA Section 1905(y) amendment; alternatively, HHS regulatory interpretation of "medically frail" to encompass the full population of individuals with work-limiting conditions.

**Current advocacy lever**: The ADA's protections against disability discrimination in Medicaid — and the DOJ's 2024 guidance on guardianship-voting rights — provide grounds for administrative complaints and litigation challenging exemption definitions that are facially neutral but have documented disparate impact on disability communities.

### 5. Civic Participation Accessibility Standards for Healthcare Settings

**Mechanism addressed**: FQHC underutilization for voter registration.

**Reform**: HHS conditions of participation or HRSA grant conditions requiring FQHC NVRA compliance as a standard of care — documented in quality reporting, staffed through workflow integration, and trained alongside other mandatory clinical staff training. This would operationalize existing NVRA obligations without requiring new legislation.

**Alternative path**: State Medicaid plan amendments requiring NVRA offer at all public assistance points of contact, with CMS approval as a condition of enhanced matching funds.

## Movement Leverage: Coalition Architecture

The unique strategic contribution of Domain 39 is the cross-coalition synthesis that no existing advocacy document achieves. Each of the five pathways corresponds to a distinct movement sector. Each sector has resources, relationships, and expertise that the others lack. The democratic infrastructure framing is the bridge concept that allows them to coordinate.

**Healthcare advocacy organizations** (Georgetown CCF, KFF, CBPP, National Health Law Program, Commonwealth Fund): These organizations have the policy analysis infrastructure and relationships with state Medicaid directors that voter access organizations lack. Domain 39's democratic framing gives healthcare advocates a new audience — election protection organizations, voting rights funders, media covering democracy — without requiring them to abandon their primary health access framing.

**Democracy and voter access organizations** (Brennan Center, Protect Democracy, National Voter Registration Project, Institute for Responsive Government): These organizations have the litigation infrastructure, legislative relationships, and NVRA enforcement expertise that healthcare organizations lack. The Medicaid-NVRA pathway (Pathway 2) and the rural hospital-turnout finding (Pathway 1) are the specific analytical contributions that make healthcare directly relevant to these organizations' missions.

**Disability rights organizations** (National Disability Rights Network, Disability Rights Advocates, Disability Belongs, National Council on Disability): These organizations have the PAVA relationships, guardianship expertise, and ADA litigation capacity that neither healthcare nor democracy organizations possess. The three disability pathways documented here provide the specific factual record that disability organizations need to frame their advocacy in democratic participation terms — shifting their work from a social services defense to a voting rights offense.

**Reproductive justice coalitions** (Black Mamas Matter Alliance, National Birth Equity Collaborative, Momnibus coalition): These organizations have the deepest relationships with communities facing highest maternal mortality rates and strongest legislative presence through the Momnibus framework. The civic capacity argument — that preventing maternal mortality is protecting democratic voice — expands their coalition to include voting rights funders and election protection organizations who do not currently see maternal health as their issue.

**FQHC and community health networks** (National Association of Community Health Centers, Healthy Democracy Healthy People, Community Health Vote): These organizations are the implementation infrastructure for Pathway 2 and the FQHC opportunity. The Healthy Democracy Kit research (PMC 10213595) has already documented their capacity. The organizational bridge-building work is connecting these networks to the democracy funding ecosystem that supports voter registration initiatives.

**Timing**: Organizations distributing Domain 39 before June 1, 2026 can use it in three specific action contexts:

1. Advocacy campaigns directed at HHS implementation choices for work requirements that remain within agency discretion (state flexibility, exemption interpretation guidance, look-back period decisions).

2. Litigation strategy development for challenges to work requirements, PAVA defunding, and NVRA non-compliance — using the democratic participation evidence base to support both standing arguments and public interest framing.

3. Pre-election voter registration initiatives coordinated across healthcare and voting rights organizations, using the FQHC network and NVRA statutory authority as operational vehicles ahead of the November 3, 2026 midterms.

Organizations with the strongest policy analysis capacity have disproportionate influence on discretionary implementation choices even without formal comment rights. **January 1, 2027 — Work requirements effective**: This is the implementation cliff. Organizations need litigation strategies, communications campaigns, and coalition infrastructure in place before January 2027. The window from June to December 2026 is the primary organizational deployment period. Domain 39 is designed to be distributed in that window.

The *APSR* finding should be in their 2026 election integrity briefings.

**The June 1 comment period nuance**: The OBBBA statute explicitly exempts the HHS work requirement rule from notice-and-comment requirements. This does not mean advocacy is pointless — it means the advocacy channel is different. The effective channels are: (1) direct outreach to HHS career staff on discretionary implementation choices; (2) congressional pressure on oversight committees; (3) pre-litigation submissions documenting the democratic participation harms as a basis for APA challenges and constitutional claims; (4) state-level advocacy where state discretion remains (states can implement less stringently than federal standards require).

## Evidence Quality Assessment

**Highest confidence findings** (direct causation, peer-reviewed evidence, large samples):
- Rural hospital closures → voter turnout decline: Cox, Epp, and Shepherd (APSR 2025), 10.5 million rural voters, individual fixed effects. Effect size: 3.8pp raw, 0.2pp in fully controlled models. Both are meaningful; the raw effect captures community-level disruption, the controlled effect captures individual-level impact net of community characteristics.
- Voting Rights Act → infant mortality decline: Rushovich et al. (AJPH 2024), 17.5% reduction in Black infant deaths in VRA-covered counties. Large sample, natural experiment design, well-documented causal mechanism.
- Procedural disenrollment racial disparities: PMC 11148781, COVID unwinding analysis. Black and Hispanic enrollees approximately 2x as likely as white enrollees to lose coverage procedurally (adjusted odds ratios 2.19 and 2.08 respectively).

**High confidence findings** (strong evidence base, some causal ambiguity):
- Medicaid AVR registration rates: Institute for Responsive Government meta-analysis. 85–90% registration in Oregon projection vs. 1.6% national NVRA compliance rate. The comparison is real but involves projection for AVR vs. documented data for traditional compliance.
- Maternal mortality racial disparities: CDC NCHS 2023 data. 50.3 vs. 14.5 deaths per 100,000 live births. Statistical fact; causal mechanisms are structural racism, not individual biology.
- FQHC voter registration effectiveness: Bronx clinic (89% registration); PMC 10213595 toolkit research. Both are real but from specific contexts; generalizability to all FQHC settings requires additional implementation research.

**Moderate confidence findings** (policy projections, model-dependent):
- CBO coverage loss projections (7.5M by 2034, 5.3M from work requirements): CBO projections are authoritative for budget purposes but carry modeling assumptions that could over- or understate effects.
- Georgetown CCF coverage loss estimates (1.2–1.8M in year one): Expert organization, consistent with CBO directional finding, but based on state-readiness assumptions that vary.
- Rural hospital closure risk (417 vulnerable): Chartis financial analysis; financial vulnerability does not equal guaranteed closure. The political and financial dynamics that determine actual closure decisions involve more variables than the financial model captures.

**Evidence gaps**:
- Direct longitudinal data connecting medical debt specifically to reduced voter participation (as opposed to general civic engagement).
- Post-OBBBA tracking of NVRA compliance rates as enrollment office capacity declines — this data will not exist until 2027, but organizations should be building the monitoring infrastructure now.
- Systematic data on guardianship-based disenfranchisement is severely limited: the 1.3 million figure is from 2018, no federal agency tracks it reliably, and the civic participation impact is largely undocumented at scale.
