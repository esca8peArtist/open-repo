# Domain 59 Research Outline
## Economic Precarity & Civic Participation Crisis

*Phase 2 — Research outline for July 1–August 15, 2026 execution window*
*Compiled: June 14, 2026*

---

## Section 1: Executive Summary

Economic precarity is the most undertheorized barrier to democratic participation in the United States. Most reform frameworks — electoral, judicial, legislative — assume that citizens have the time, cognitive bandwidth, and material stability to engage the political system. Domain 59's core thesis challenges that assumption directly: for at least 40 percent of the American electorate, the material conditions of daily life do not permit the consolidation of time and resource that sustained civic participation requires. Non-participation is not apathy. It is rational adaptation to structural scarcity.

The income-based voter turnout gap reached 42 percentage points in the 2024 presidential election — the widest documented in modern US history, expanded from 27 points in 2020 and 17 points in 2016. This widening is not explained by political alienation. It is explained by the simultaneous intensification of five material pathways: time poverty from multi-job work and caregiving obligations; housing instability that destroys voter registration and civic organizational ties; medical debt that depletes cognitive bandwidth and triggers residential mobility; gendered caregiving burdens concentrated on single mothers; and gig-economy scheduling unpredictability that makes advance civic commitments structurally impossible.

Domain 59's unique contribution to the Phase 2 framework is the bridge it provides between economic justice advocacy and democratic renewal advocacy. All other Phase 2 domains — judicial capture, civil service erosion, electoral interference, surveillance, multilateral withdrawal — document what has gone wrong with democratic design. Domain 59 explains why most Americans cannot mobilize to fix it. Material precarity is not a background condition; it is the mechanism that makes democratic failures stick.

**Why this matters now**: The policy decisions made in the June–August 2026 legislative window — on the Child Tax Credit, Medicaid work requirements, federal minimum wage, childcare, and gig worker classification — will reach their civic impact precisely at the November 2026 midterms. This outline frames those decisions as democratic infrastructure choices, not merely welfare measures.

## Section 2: Five Research Zones

### Zone 1: Quantified Time and Resource Poverty Pathways

**Core argument**: A rigorous hours-based accounting of economic precarity reveals that the consolidated discretionary time required for sustained civic participation does not exist for a substantial fraction of the electorate. The barrier is not preference — it is arithmetic.

**Preliminary data points**:

1. **Commute burden**: Average one-way US commute is 27.2 minutes (Census ACS, 2024), rising from 26.8 minutes in 2023. Workers with commutes over 60 minutes increased from 8.9 percent (2023) to 9.3 percent (2024). A 54-minute daily round-trip commute adds 4.5 hours per week of non-discretionary time.

2. **Multi-job work hours**: Approximately 8 million Americans hold two or more jobs, concentrated in sub-$35,000 income brackets. Aggregate weekly work hours for multi-job holders average 50–60 hours. At $7.25/hour (federal minimum wage, unchanged for 17 years), a 50-hour week produces $362.50 before taxes — below poverty threshold for a family of three.

3. **Gendered caregiving burden**: Full-time employed mothers spend 19.5 hours per week on combined childcare and household work versus 12.3 hours for fathers (BLS American Time Use Survey, 2024). Single-parent households, where one adult absorbs 100 percent of both income-earning and caregiving time, face the most acute consolidation failure.

4. **Childcare cost trap**: National median childcare cost was $1,230/month in 2026 (Care.com). Childcare deserts — areas where licensed slots are fewer than one-third of demand — cover 51 percent of US communities. For a single mother earning $31,000 annually (median for single mothers), childcare at median cost consumes 47 percent of gross income, foreclosing the option to reduce work hours.

5. **Discretionary time floor**: A multi-job worker with one child, no childcare subsidy, and a median commute has approximately 17–22 hours of discretionary time weekly after work, sleep, commuting, and basic caregiving. Civic participation — voting preparation, attending a meeting, contacting a representative, joining an organization — requires 4–6 consolidated hours monthly. The consolidation requirement, not the raw hours, is the structural barrier: sporadic minutes do not substitute for planned, advance-committed civic time.

6. **Voter turnout income gap**: Income quintile bottom vs. top voting rates diverge by 38–48 percentage points (Econofact; PRRI 2024 election survey). The 42-point gap observed in 2024 (Rastogi and Laurison, *Socius*, 2025) represents the largest recorded widening.

7. **Youth barrier confirmation**: 17 percent of young non-voters in 2024 cited "too busy," other commitments, or lack of childcare as direct participation barriers (CIRCLE/Tufts University, 2024).

**Research gap to close**: Cross-pathway compound modeling — estimating the combined suppression effect for households facing multiple simultaneous pathways (multi-job work + childcare desert + medical debt). Currently each pathway is documented individually; a synthetic model of compound exposure does not exist in the literature.

### Zone 2: Economic Stress Health Cascade

**Core argument**: Economic stress does not remain economic. It cascades through health, housing stability, cognitive function, and social connection — each step further compressing civic capacity. This cascade is documentable as a multi-step causal chain, not a correlation.

**Preliminary data points and study citations**:

1. **Real wage decline against cost of living**: Between April 2025 and April 2026, real average hourly earnings declined 0.3 percent (BLS Real Earnings Summary, April 2026). The national home-price-to-income ratio stood at 5.08 in 2026, nearly double the recommended 2.6 threshold. Between 2019 and 2024, median home prices rose 31 percent while household incomes rose 22 percent — homes became 1.5 times more expensive relative to income in five years.

2. **Housing cost burden**: 22.7 million renter households are cost-burdened (paying more than 30 percent of income on rent); 12.1 million are severely cost-burdened (more than 50 percent) (Harvard Joint Center for Housing Studies, *Americas Rental Housing 2026*). In Hawaii, median households spend approximately 50 percent of income on housing; in California, 43 percent.

3. **Medical debt and housing instability**: Adults with medical debt in 2024 had a 44 percent elevated risk of housing instability in 2025, and a 7 percentage-point higher probability of residential moves, compared to those without medical debt (Johns Hopkins Bloomberg School of Public Health / PMC, 2026). Medical debt outstanding: $220 billion nationally (CFPB, 2025).

4. **Residential mobility and voter suppression**: Residential moves — whether from cost burden, eviction, or medical debt — produce a 3.6 percentage-point decrease in voter registration and persistently depressed turnout lasting nearly two decades, even when the move is policy-induced and economically beneficial (PNAS Moving to Opportunity study, 2024).

5. **Eviction and civic withdrawal**: Neighborhood-level eviction rates negatively impacted voter turnout in 2016 through three mechanisms — material hardship, social isolation, and government cynicism (Slee and Desmond, *Politics & Society*, 2023). The 2008–2010 foreclosure crisis, which displaced 3.8 million homeowners, created a civic participation deficit cohort now in their mid-to-late 30s that has not fully recovered electoral participation trajectory.

6. **Cognitive bandwidth depletion**: Economic scarcity — the mental burden of managing insufficient resources — reduces effective cognitive bandwidth by the equivalent of a full standard deviation on IQ and executive function tests (Mani et al., *Science*, 2013; replicated in *Journal of Economic Psychology* meta-analysis, 2024). Medical debt management consumes an estimated 8–15 hours monthly of working time, directly competing with civic participation time.

7. **Acute financial hardship and voting**: When household bills fall immediately before an election, voter turnout among low-income citizens drops by 5 percentage points — with no measurable effect at higher income levels. The mechanism is dual: stress-induced withdrawal from the public sphere, and the salience shift from long-horizon civic activity to immediate financial management (Schaub, *American Political Science Review*, 2021).

**Key data gap**: Direct peer-reviewed research specifically linking food insecurity — which affects 13.7 percent of US households (18.4 percent of households with children) as of 2024 — to voter turnout reduction. The SNAP cuts from the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (3 million fewer participants from July 2025 to January 2026) are documented, but the causal pathway to electoral non-participation relies on inferred mechanism rather than direct study.

### Zone 3: Intersectional Patterns

**Core argument**: The five pathways documented in Zones 1–2 do not affect all Americans equally. Their intersection with race, gender, geography, and work classification concentrates maximum suppression on populations already most structurally disadvantaged in the democratic system — creating a compound exclusion that no single-pathway analysis reveals.

**Key intersectional patterns with citations**:

1. **Black women at maximum intersection**: Black women experience a simultaneous convergence of the wage gap (earning approximately 67 cents per dollar earned by white men as of 2024), disproportionate caregiving burden (Black women constitute 22 percent of all US family caregivers, AARP), healthcare access gaps that produce higher medical debt rates, and higher housing cost-burden rates. Each pathway compounds the others; the civic suppression effect of their intersection exceeds the sum of parts.

2. **Single mothers — highest compound exposure**: Single mothers in 2022 had a poverty rate of 28 percent under the official poverty measure (Urban Institute, 2025). At median single-mother income of $31,000, childcare at national median cost consumes 47–58 percent of gross income. Single mothers simultaneously face Zone 1's caregiving time burden, Zone 2's financial stress cascade, and gendered barriers to organizational participation (participation rates 18–25 percent lower for mothers of children under 5 than for childless women). This population carries the heaviest compound weight of every pathway.

3. **Rural communities — triple simultaneous exposure**: Rural communities face childcare desert exposure (the highest concentrations of childcare deserts are rural), longer commutes (above-median travel times), and Medicaid coverage gaps amplified by OBBBA work requirement implementation. Rural hospital closures — 180+ since 2005, concentrated in states that have not expanded Medicaid — compound medical debt risk by eliminating nearby care options, forcing travel costs and higher-priced regional care.

4. **Gig workers — structured scheduling exclusion**: Approximately 59 million Americans participate in gig economy work. Bae and Haselswerdt (2023, confirmed in replication) found that gig economy participation shifts workers toward expressive political participation (protests, one-time contacts) and away from sustained participation (voting, organizational membership) — not because of preference change, but because schedule unpredictability eliminates the advance commitment capability that organizational participation requires. 57 percent of gig workers report working more than 40 hours weekly with no predictable scheduling window.

5. **Communities of color and felony disenfranchisement overlay**: 4 million Americans were unable to vote in the 2024 election due to felony conviction laws (The Sentencing Project, 2024). 1 in 22 African Americans of voting age is formally disenfranchised — more than three times the rate for non-African Americans. For those not formally disenfranchised, economic precarity from criminal-legal system contact (fines, fees, wage impacts of criminal record) overlaps with Zone 2's financial stress cascade.

**Confidence note**: Intersectional compound effect modeling does not yet exist as a unified study in the political science literature. Each pairwise intersection (Black women + wage gap; single mothers + childcare; rural communities + healthcare) is separately documented, but a unified cross-pathway suppression estimate for multi-disadvantaged populations is a genuine research gap Domain 59 could help fill.

### Zone 4: June–August 2026 Policy Leverage Points

**Core argument**: The legislative windows of summer 2026 are not primarily economic policy debates — they are democratic infrastructure decisions. Each bill or rulemaking in this window will either expand or contract the civic capacity of the Americans most structurally excluded from participation. The framing shift from welfare advocacy to democracy advocacy is Domain 59's most immediate strategic contribution.

**Specific bills and initiatives with timelines**:

1. **Child Tax Credit — Senate Finance Committee markup window (June–July 2026, ACTIVE)**: The OBBBA's CTC structure, as confirmed by CBPP and ITEP tracking (May 2026), leaves the 17 million children in the lowest-income families receiving zero or reduced benefit. Neither the House nor the Senate Finance Committee proposal expands benefits for these families. The American Family Act (or analogous fully-refundable CTC amendment) is the advocacy target in the Senate Finance markup, expected June–July 2026. The democratic participation argument: CTC for the lowest-income families directly reduces the cognitive bandwidth tax of financial scarcity (Zone 2, Mani et al.) and the childcare cost trap (Zone 1). Organizations driving this: CBPP, ITEP, National Women's Law Center, MomsRising. Decision point: Senate Finance markup vote, estimated July 2026.

2. **EITC Expansion — Working Parents Tax Relief Act (April 2026, advocacy window summer 2026)**: Representative Kristen McDonald Rivet (D-MI) introduced the Working Parents Tax Relief Act (April 15, 2026), which would expand EITC by up to $5,500 per qualifying child under age 4, benefiting approximately 10 million adults and 8 million children with average cuts of $4,500. The bill has minimal near-term legislative pathway in the Republican-controlled House but is central to Democratic messaging in the 2026 election cycle. Summer 2026 is the coalition-building and framing window. Organizations: Third Way, Americans for Tax Fairness, CBPP.

3. **Medicaid Work Requirements Response — State outreach notice period (June–August 2026, ACTIVE, WORSENING)**: OBBBA Medicaid work requirements take effect December 30, 2026. States must send outreach notices to current enrollees in June, July, or August 2026, depending on state-level implementation decisions. Urban Institute projects 4.9–10.1 million fewer Medicaid enrollees by 2028. The state advocacy window — whether states implement early (Nebraska began May 2026) or seek 2028 extensions — is the active decision point. Each disenrolled person faces elevated medical debt risk (Johns Hopkins 2026), which carries the Zone 2 cascade effect to civic withdrawal. Organizations: CBPP health program, Protect Our Care, state Medicaid advocacy networks.

4. **Federal Minimum Wage — Living Wage for All Act / Raise the Wage Act (coalition-building window, legislative post-midterm)**: The Living Wage for All Act ($25/hour, introduced April 2026) and Raise the Wage Act of 2025 (H.R. 2743 / S. 1332, $17/hour) have no Senate traction under the current majority but are the pre-midterm coalition and framing priorities. The federal minimum wage has not moved from $7.25 since 2009 — 17 years. The democratic participation argument: at $25/hour, multi-job dependency decreases for 12–15 percent of affected households, directly reducing the weekly work-hour burden that compresses civic time (Zone 1). Organizations: AFL-CIO, SEIU, One Fair Wage, Fight for $15, NAACP, Black Women's Roundtable.

5. **Childcare — Child Care for Working Families Act (S. 2295 / H.R. 4418, 119th Congress) and Child Care Modernization Act (June 2026)**: Senator Tim Kaine and colleagues reintroduced the Child Care for Working Families Act (introduced every Congress since 2017), which would cap family childcare costs at 7 percent of income. In June 2026, Representatives Mackenzie, Lee, Hinson, and McDonald Rivet introduced the bipartisan Child Care Modernization Act to strengthen the Child Care and Development Block Grant. Decision point: summer markup sessions and appropriations negotiations. Organizations: Child Care Aware, MomsRising, First Five Years Fund, National Association of Counties.

6. **Gig Worker Reclassification — California rideshare unionization and state portable benefits tracks (2026 implementation period)**: California rideshare unionization law and the Alabama Portable Benefits Act represent state-level implementation apertures in 2026. Massachusetts's ABC test for worker classification continues to be tested in litigation. Federal reclassification under FLSA or NLRB rulemaking is blocked in the current Republican majority. State legislative sessions in 2026 are the active window. The democracy argument: schedule predictability is a democratic participation prerequisite, not merely a labor standard. Organizations: SEIU, National Employment Law Project (NELP), California Labor Federation, Gig Workers Collective.

### Zone 5: Movement Leverage and Organization Networks

**Core argument**: The organizations capable of converting economic precarity research into electoral and legislative impact are already assembled — but they are siloed by sector (labor, maternal justice, housing, racial justice). Domain 59's unique organizational contribution is providing the democratic participation frame that can bridge these silos and bring election protection organizations into coalition with economic justice advocates for the first time.

**Primary organizations and contact paths**:

1. **AFL-CIO** (12.5 million members, post-SEIU merger 2025): The largest pre-organized constituency for economic precarity + democratic participation advocacy. AFL-CIO explicitly connects social and economic justice to union purpose. Legislative affairs division has direct Congressional access. Strategic angle: reframe wage and childcare advocacy as "democracy infrastructure" rather than welfare. Contact: AFL-CIO Legislative Affairs Division (Washington, D.C. office); President Liz Shuler's office for endorsement coordination.

2. **SEIU** (2.2 million members, healthcare and service workers): Disproportionate representation of gig-adjacent, low-wage workers who face highest intersection of Zone 1–3 pathways. SEIU President April Verrett has been vocal on Living Wage for All Act support. Contact: SEIU Legislative and Political Department; state council offices in California, Illinois, New York (highest gig-economy exposure states).

3. **Economic Policy Institute**: Peer-reviewed wage stagnation data, income inequality research, and the foundational "strong unions, stronger democracy" research framework. EPI's wage tracker provides real-time wage-growth vs. inflation data by sector and region. Contact: EPI Policy Director; request for Domain 59 technical review of Zone 1 quantification.

4. **Center on Budget and Policy Priorities (CBPP)**: Primary institutional source on SNAP cuts, CTC structural exclusion, and Medicaid work requirement tracking. CBPP's Sharon Parrott (economic security program director) is the highest-leverage contact for CTC Senate Finance markup advocacy. Contact: Sharon Parrott (economic security); CBPP press and communications for coordinated distribution.

5. **National Domestic Workers Alliance (NDWA)**: Organizes approximately 2.5 million nannies, housecleaners, and homecare workers — among the most economically precarious and civically excluded constituencies. NDWA's Care Workers Can't Wait coalition (co-convened with SEIU, AFL-CIO, AFT, AFSCME, MomsRising) is the pre-existing coalition infrastructure for Domain 59 distribution. Civic engagement is listed as a NDWA strategic priority. Contact: NDWA policy and civic engagement teams; care.workers@domesticworkers.org (public contact).

6. **National Low Income Housing Coalition (NLIHC) — "Our Homes, Our Votes" initiative**: Direct bridge between housing instability (Zone 2) and voter access advocacy. NLIHC's annual "Out of Reach" report maps rent-to-wage gaps by county — precisely the empirical input for Zone 2's geographic sorting argument. "Our Homes, Our Votes" already frames housing stability as civic participation prerequisite. Contact: NLIHC Policy Director; Our Homes, Our Votes program staff.

7. **MomsRising** (5 million members): Direct constituency reach to single mothers — the demographic group with highest combined pathway exposure across all five zones. Executive Director Kristin Rowe-Finkbeiner has established relationships with Senate Finance offices relevant to CTC June window. Contact: kristin.rowe-finkbeiner@momsrising.org (confirmed public contact from prior research phases).

8. **Brennan Center for Justice**: Provides legal infrastructure and public communication capacity to translate economic-participation research into litigation and advocacy. The Brennan Center's voting rights and economic justice programs are the bridge organization for bringing election-protection advocates into coalition with economic justice advocates. Contact: Brennan Center Democracy and Economic Opportunity program; media contact for joint distribution.

**Coalition gap**: The convergence of labor unions, maternal justice organizations, economic research networks, and housing justice coalitions under a unified "economic precarity as democratic design failure" frame does not currently exist. The Living Wage for All Act endorsement list shows the constituent organizations are assembled for wage advocacy. What is absent is the democracy-participation argument that would bring election protection organizations (Brennan Center, Democracy Docket, Protect Democracy) into coalition with these economic justice advocates. Domain 59 provides that connective frame and is the missing analytical bridge.

## Section 3: Research Questions for the Full Domain Document

The following eight to twelve questions will drive the 10,000–12,000 word full domain document:

1. What is the precise arithmetic of discretionary time for representative household types (single-parent, dual-income with children, single adult in childcare desert, gig worker)? How many hours per week remain after work, commute, sleep, and caregiving — and does that threshold support meaningful civic participation?

2. How has the income-based voter turnout gap widened from 17 points (2016) to 42 points (2024), and what specific material changes in wage stagnation, housing costs, and caregiving burden account for this widening? (Correlational to causal inference challenge.)

3. What is the two-step causal chain from medical debt onset to voter suppression — specifically, can the Johns Hopkins 2026 finding (medical debt → housing instability, 44 percent elevated risk) be linked to the PNAS 2024 finding (residential mobility → 3.6 percentage-point voter registration decline) in a combined empirical argument?

4. For which household types does economic precarity produce political withdrawal versus political mobilization? What structural and organizational conditions tip the outcome toward mobilization?

5. What is the compound suppression estimate for households simultaneously facing multiple pathways (multi-job work + childcare desert + medical debt + housing cost burden)? Is the combined effect additive, multiplicative, or partially offsetting?

6. How do intersectional identity factors — race, gender, disability, immigration status, criminal record — modify the baseline suppression effect of each pathway? What specific populations face compound suppression exceeding any single-domain analysis?

7. What is the geographic distribution of economic precarity pathway concentration versus 2026 competitive congressional districts? How many voters in competitive districts are currently experiencing student debt default, SNAP disruption, or medical debt onset in the 6-month window before November 2026?

8. What policy interventions — CTC expansion, minimum wage increase, childcare subsidy, same-day voter registration, automatic voter re-registration — show the strongest causal evidence for civic participation recovery? What is the dose-response relationship between economic stabilization and participation recovery?

9. How does the OBBBA's structural exclusion of 17 million children from full CTC benefit function as a democratic design choice rather than a welfare policy trade-off? What is the participation-return estimate for CTC full refundability for the lowest-income quintile?

10. Where do labor union membership and civic participation correlate most strongly, and what does this imply for union density decline (35 percent to under 10 percent in private sector since 1970s) as an explanation for economic precarity becoming disenfranchising rather than mobilizing?

11. What are the legal theories — Voting Rights Act, equal protection, NVRA enforcement — that could convert economic precarity's civic suppression effects into cognizable voting rights claims? Which legal theories have the strongest post-*Loper Bright* doctrinal footing?

12. What does the historical precedent of the 1930s labor movement — converting Depression-era economic anxiety into durable political power through organizational infrastructure — teach about the organizational conditions required for economic precarity to produce democratic renewal rather than democratic exit?
