# Domain 59: Economic Precarity and Democratic Exclusion
## Research Outline with Section 1 Complete

*Prepared: May 19, 2026. Phase 2 research initiation. Full production begins June 22.*

## Central Argument (One Paragraph — Do Not Edit in Production)

Democratic participation is not a free activity. In 2024, 42 percent of Americans earning under $50,000 did not vote; among those earning over $100,000, the non-voting rate was 16 percent (PRRI, 2024 post-election survey). That 26-point gap is the largest income-based participation gap in any OECD democracy, and it did not emerge from political apathy or ideological disengagement. It emerged from a political economy in which wage stagnation, housing cost burden, medical debt, childcare inaccessibility, student loan garnishment, and gig work classification systematically deplete the time, cognitive bandwidth, residential stability, and material resources that civic participation requires. The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA, signed July 4, 2025) accelerated this exclusion: it simultaneously cut Medicaid for 7.5–10 million projected enrollees (CBO), reduced SNAP access for 2.4 million households, and removed childcare subsidies — stacking concurrent income and time shocks on households already at the margins of civic capacity. This domain documents seven mechanistically distinct causal pathways through which economic precarity converts material scarcity into democratic exclusion, identifies the specific 2026–2027 policy leverage windows in which those pathways can be interrupted, and maps the organizational infrastructure — labor unions, economic justice research networks, maternal justice coalitions, housing advocacy organizations — through which advocates can translate this analysis into democratic design reform.

### 1.1 The Income-Participation Gap: Establishing the Empirical Floor

The income-based voter participation gap is the most robust and reproducible finding in American political science. But stating the correlation is not enough. The domain's contribution is naming the mechanism.

**The headline data**: PRRI's 2024 post-election survey, the most comprehensive income-by-voting analysis of the 2024 presidential election, found that 42 percent of Americans earning under $50,000 did not vote — versus 16 percent of those earning over $100,000. Non-voters are more than twice as likely as voters to be in the under-$50,000 income band (42% of non-voters vs. 16% of voters). Nearly six in ten 2024 voters (58%) reported household income of $100,000 or higher, compared with 27% of non-voters. This is not a marginal gap; it is a structural sorting of the electorate by income.

The Census Bureau's 2024 Current Population Survey Voting and Registration Supplement, released April 30, 2025, confirms the household income gradient across registration and turnout: higher income predicts greater participation systematically at every income threshold. The complete table package with family income categories establishes this as a structural pattern, not a cycle-specific anomaly.

Rastogi and Laurison (2025, *Sociological Perspectives*) analyzed precinct-level data across the 2016 and 2020 presidential elections and found the income-participation gap at the precinct level reached 27 percentage points in 2020, up from 17 points in 2016 — a 10-point widening over a single election cycle. The direction of trend is unambiguous: the gap is growing, not closing, even as overall turnout increased.

The youth dimension is equally documented. CIRCLE/Tufts University's post-2024 election analysis found that 62 percent of young nonvoters (18-29) sometimes or often find it difficult to meet basic financial needs. Among youth who didn't vote because they were too busy or missed registration deadlines, 29 percent also often struggle to meet basic financial needs — more than double the 14 percent rate among youth overall. Youth turnout fell from over 50 percent in 2020 to approximately 42 percent in 2024. The Harvard Institute of Politics Spring 2026 Youth Poll (conducted March–April 2026, 2,018 respondents ages 18-29) found that 45 percent of young Americans are struggling to make ends meet or getting by with very little financial security — a figure rising to 57 percent among non-college-credentialed young adults. Trust in the federal government fell to 15 percent, the lowest ever recorded by the Harvard Youth Poll; only 33 percent trust the 2026 elections will be conducted fairly; and half of respondents said people like them have no real say in government — up 15 percentage points from 2017. These are not indicators of ideological disengagement. They are indicators of material exclusion producing political alienation.

**The international comparison**: OECD comparative data (*Society at a Glance 2024*) places the United States at 26th out of 32 OECD countries in voter turnout for national elections, with a 2020 turnout rate of approximately 62 percent against an OECD average of 65 percent. The Nordic contrast is instructive: Sweden maintains parliamentary election turnout above 87 percent; Denmark's 2024 European Parliament turnout exceeded the EU average by 10 points. International IDEA's Voter Turnout Database documents Nordic parliamentary election turnout consistently above 80 percent across the 2020–2024 period.

The structural explanation is economic: countries with high turnout tend to have comprehensive social welfare systems that eliminate the material barriers to participation documented in this domain. When basic material security is guaranteed by the state — healthcare, childcare, housing support, income stability in unemployment — the opportunity cost of civic engagement drops dramatically. The Nordic income-participation gap is approximately 5–10 percentage points, compared to the US gap of 26–38 points depending on measurement method. The gap is not a function of political culture; it is a function of economic architecture.

**The causal anchor — financial relief increases voter turnout**: The most direct causal evidence that financial precarity suppresses — and financial relief restores — civic participation comes from Liu, McCartney, Ramcharan, Zhang, and Zhang (Dallas Federal Reserve Bank Working Paper 2517, revised title "Household Finance Shapes Political Participation: Evidence from Mortgage Refinancing," March 2026 revision). Using quasi-experimental variation from the Home Affordable Refinance Program (HARP) eligibility cutoffs, the authors show that mortgage refinancing during the Great Recession (2009–2012) causally increased voter turnout in the 2012 general election — concentrated among households that experienced larger payment reductions and among politically independent voters. This is the domain's primary causal anchor: not a correlation between income and voting, but a documented causal chain from specific financial relief to specific increases in electoral participation. The mechanism is confirmed: reducing financial pressure increases civic engagement. Increasing financial pressure — through wage stagnation, medical debt accumulation, student loan garnishment — reduces it.

### 1.2 Wage Stagnation: The Architecture of Time Poverty

The federal minimum wage has remained at $7.25 per hour since July 2009 — 17 consecutive years without adjustment, the longest stagnation since the Fair Labor Standards Act was enacted in 1938. Twenty states remain at the federal floor in 2026 (Alabama, Georgia, Idaho, Indiana, Iowa, Kansas, Kentucky, Louisiana, Mississippi, New Hampshire, North Carolina, North Dakota, Oklahoma, Pennsylvania, South Carolina, Tennessee, Texas, Utah, Wisconsin, and Wyoming), covering approximately 60.2 million workers. For a full-time worker at that floor, gross annual income is $15,080 — below the federal poverty line for a single-parent household.

The Economic Policy Institute's analysis confirmed in 2025 that the federal minimum wage is officially a poverty wage: it has lost more than 30 percent of its purchasing power since 2009. The productivity-pay gap — EPI's Nominal Wage Tracker and productivity-pay gap analysis — shows that if median household income had tracked productivity growth since 1979, it would be approximately $30,000 higher today. That gap is not a natural economic outcome; it is a policy design choice, redistributed upward through wage suppression.

Real average hourly earnings decreased 0.3 percent from April 2025 to April 2026 (BLS Real Earnings Summary, April 2026), meaning wage growth is not keeping pace with inflation for the second consecutive year. The median full-time wage sits at approximately $33,280 per year — which, against median national rent of $1,413/month ($16,956/year per HUD data) and median infant childcare costs of $14,760/year nationally, leaves zero margin for financial stability.

Multi-job holding is the structural response to this income-expenditure gap. BLS Table A-39 (Multiple Jobholders, April 2026) documents 8+ million Americans currently holding two or more jobs, concentrated in the under-$35,000 income bracket. The average workweek for production and nonsupervisory employees is 33.8 hours — but for multi-job workers, aggregate work time commonly runs to 50–60 hours across positions. The American Community Survey documents a 27.5-minute average one-way commute, rising to 32+ minutes for lower-income workers who trade commute distance for housing cost. A multi-job worker with a 35-minute commute each way burns 52–62 hours weekly on paid work and transit alone.

The BLS Alternative Work Arrangements Supplement and the platform economy literature document 59 million Americans in gig economy participation as primary or secondary income. Gig workers who depend primarily on platform income average $18,000–20,000 per year and maintain 50+ work hours weekly to approach that figure — with no employer-provided benefits, no wage floor guarantees, and algorithmically assigned schedules that eliminate the advance planning that sustained civic engagement requires.

**The time-poverty calculus**: Functional democratic engagement at a basic level requires a minimum of 15–20 hours per month: voting logistics including transportation and wait times (45–90 minutes per election, twice in the 2026 midterm cycle), basic organizational engagement — one meeting per month (2–3 hours), civic information consumption sufficient for informed participation (5–8 hours/month), and occasional advocacy actions such as public comments or constituent contact (3–5 hours/month). Four to six consolidated hours per month is the irreducible minimum. For the multi-job worker with children, those 4–6 consolidated hours do not reliably exist. Their 17–27 hours of discretionary weekly time is fragmented across evenings and weekends under conditions of fatigue and cognitive depletion — hours subject to constant displacement by logistics emergencies (sick child, car repair, shift change at the second job) that cannot be scheduled around civic calendars.

### 1.3 Housing Cost Burden and the Residential Instability Pathway

Harvard's Joint Center for Housing Studies *America's Rental Housing 2026* report documents an affordability crisis of record depth. The national rental market cooled through 2024–2025 — asking rents for professionally managed apartments declined 0.6 percent year-over-year in Q4 2025 — but that cooling produced no affordability relief. In 2024, 22.7 million renter households spent more than 30 percent of their income on rent and utilities — 49 percent of all renters, a record high. Of these, 12.1 million — 26 percent of all renters — are severely cost-burdened, spending more than half their income on housing. Cost burdens rose in 44 states and 88 of the 100 largest metropolitan areas over the past five years.

The National Low Income Housing Coalition's *Gap Report 2025* documents a national shortage of 7.1 million affordable and available rental homes for extremely low-income (ELI) renter households, with only 35 affordable and available homes for every 100 ELI renter households. In Nevada, that ratio falls to 16 per 100; in the worst metropolitan areas — Las Vegas, Dallas, Austin, San Diego, and Houston — fewer than 20 affordable units exist per 100 lowest-income renters. Three-quarters of renters with extremely low incomes are severely cost-burdened.

The civic participation consequences are structural and causal, not inferential. Two peer-reviewed studies establish the mechanism with quasi-experimental evidence:

**Slee and Desmond (2023, *Politics and Society*)**: "Eviction and Voter Turnout: The Political Consequences of Housing Instability." Using commercial evictions as an instrumental variable for residential evictions — a methodology that addresses endogeneity — the authors find that a one percentage point increase in the residential eviction rate is associated with a 0.46 percentage point decline in voter turnout; in neighborhoods with the lowest baseline eviction rates (where eviction is most disruptive), a one percentage point increase correlates with a 5.54 percentage point decline. Their instrumental variable model estimates that a one percentage point reduction in residential evictions would have increased voter turnout by 2.73 percentage points in the 2016 presidential election. Eviction is not merely correlate with non-voting; it causes it.

**Kuk, Hajnal, and Lajevardi (PNAS, 2024)**: "Residential Mobility and Persistently Depressed Voting Among Disadvantaged Adults in a Large Housing Experiment." Using the Moving to Opportunity housing voucher lottery as a natural experiment, the authors find that induced residential mobility — even when the move was to a lower-poverty neighborhood with better housing quality — persistently depressed voter participation among low-income Black and Hispanic adults for nearly 20 years. The mechanism is not housing quality or neighborhood safety; it is social capital disruption and voter registration lapse. Moving breaks the civic ties through which participation is maintained. The finding directly challenges the assumption that housing assistance automatically enables civic participation.

The Eviction Lab's 2025 Preliminary Analysis confirms that while national eviction filing rates fell slightly overall in 2025, 13 of the 48 tracked jurisdictions saw above-baseline rates. Nashville's 2025 filing rate was 46 percent above its 2023–2024 baseline; Austin (Travis County) was 30 percent above baseline; Charleston, SC, and St. Louis, MO, also recorded significant above-baseline rates. NAACPLDF's "Forced Moves" analysis documents more than 14 million tenants reporting pressure to move due to rent increases — informal evictions that may be five times more common than formal court filings, producing the same civic consequence (registration lapse, organizational severance) without entering eviction court data.

In states without automatic voter re-registration — 26 states as of November 2025 — every move requires a new registration. In states that do not automatically match new address records to voter rolls, the registration gap between a move and the next election can be decisive. The SAVE Act's documentary proof-of-citizenship requirements (analyzed in Domain 1) fall hardest precisely on the population most likely to have moved frequently and most likely to lack specific documentation.

### 1.4 Medical Debt and Bankruptcy as Civic Suppression Mechanisms

Medical debt is the largest single source of financial precarity for American households below median income. Approximately 100 million Americans — 38 percent of the adult population — carry some form of medical or dental debt, with $220 billion in total household medical debt documented in the CFPB's 2025 analysis. The Peterson-KFF Health System Tracker estimates the real figure approaches $500 billion when hidden forms are included: credit card balances used to pay medical bills, informal family loans, deferred rent to cover medical costs.

Bankruptcy filings reached 591,850 in the 12 months ending March 31, 2026 — an 11.9 percent increase from 529,080 the prior period (Administrative Office of US Courts, April 2026). Medical debt is implicated in 60–66.5 percent of consumer bankruptcy filings — approximately 350,000–395,000 cases per year at the current filing rate, representing a direct financial collapse event for hundreds of thousands of households annually.

The OBBBA's Medicaid implications compound this crisis directly. CBO projects that OBBBA's Medicaid work requirements and eligibility restrictions will eliminate coverage for 7.5 million people in 2034, with 15 million becoming uninsured overall. Nebraska implemented Medicaid work requirements on May 1, 2026, the first state under OBBBA's framework. As Medicaid coverage is withdrawn from currently enrolled adults, medical debt accumulation will accelerate: emergency care without coverage, deferred management of chronic conditions, foregone preventive care that becomes acute crisis.

The civic suppression mechanism operates through two analytically distinct channels:

**The cognitive bandwidth channel**: Mani, Mullainathan, Shafir, and Zhao (*Science*, 2013) established that financial scarcity consumes working memory and reduces cognitive performance on tasks unrelated to the financial problem — the "bandwidth tax." The same individuals tested in periods of financial stress show reduced cognitive capacity compared to periods of financial relief, with the effect comparable in magnitude to losing 13–14 IQ points. A 2024 meta-analysis in the *Journal of Economic Psychology* confirmed these findings across multiple study designs. "Tunneling" — the phenomenon in which financial crisis directs attention exclusively to the immediate problem, crowding out awareness of less urgent concerns — directly applies to civic participation: the household managing medical debt payment negotiations, insurance appeals, and collection disputes is consuming 8–15 hours monthly and a substantial fraction of cognitive bandwidth on processes that leave less of both for civic engagement.

**The income collapse channel**: When medical creditors obtain judgments, wage garnishment averaging $200–400 per month further reduces the disposable income available for the transportation, childcare coverage, parking, and time-off-work costs associated with in-person voting and civic participation. For the household already at the margin, these are non-trivial barriers. A $30 parking fee and $40 childcare coverage represent 3–4 hours of minimum wage work.

### 1.5 Student Loan Garnishment as 2026 Crisis Accelerant

The Trump administration restarted federal student loan collections in May 2025 and wage garnishment in January 2026. As of early 2026, approximately 5.5 million borrowers are in default, with another 3.7 million 270+ days past due and 2.7 million in early delinquency — approximately 12 million borrowers in default or serious delinquency (AEI analysis of DOE data). Wage garnishment notices began the week of January 7, 2026, with the number of notices increasing monthly. The federal government can order employers to withhold up to 15 percent of a borrower's wages.

The Pressley-Booker-Warren Ending Administrative Wage Garnishment Act (introduced May 2025) would suspend garnishment as a student debt collection tool; it has not passed, and the January 2026 garnishment restart proceeded over congressional objection. The failure of this legislation establishes that the 2026 garnishment policy is a deliberate executive choice, not an oversight.

At 15 percent of wages on a $33,280 median income, wage garnishment removes $4,992 per year from the affected household's disposable income — a step-change increase in financial precarity that compounds with existing medical debt, housing cost burden, and childcare costs. The households entering garnishment in 2026 are predominantly in the bottom two income quintiles. OBBBA's simultaneous elimination of the SAVE income-driven repayment plan removed the primary mechanism through which borrowers could have avoided default; the policy sequence (SAVE elimination → mass default → wage garnishment) is a compounding design that concentrates financial precarity on the same population already most affected by Medicaid cuts, SNAP reductions, and housing cost burden.

### 1.6 Childcare Inaccessibility and the Gendered Democratic Deficit

Average infant center-based care in the United States costs $1,230 per month nationally in 2026 (Care.com 2026 Cost of Care Report, a 5–8 percent increase since 2024). This figure consumes 20 percent or more of median household income — nearly triple the HHS-defined affordability threshold of 7 percent of income. Twenty percent of families spend more than $30,000 annually on childcare; 78 percent spend 10 percent or more of household income on care; 31 percent are drawing down savings to cover childcare costs.

Fifty-one percent of Americans live in childcare deserts — communities where licensed childcare capacity falls significantly short of demand. Rural communities face the worst access gaps: 26 percent of rural families report significant difficulty finding childcare, compared to 10–11 percent in suburban and urban areas. In Utah and Nevada, 72–77 percent of families live in childcare deserts.

American Time Use Survey data (BLS, June 2025, covering 2024 activities) documents that among full-time employed parents, mothers spend 19.5 hours per week on childcare and household work combined, compared to 12.3 hours for fathers. For single mothers — 21 million in the US with average household income of $31,000 — the childcare burden is undivided. At the national median cost, childcare would consume 47–58 percent of a single mother's gross income, making formal childcare structurally unaffordable without subsidies. OBBBA's reductions to the Child Care and Development Block Grant (CCDBG) funding deepened this inaccessibility in the states most reliant on federal subsidy.

The democratic design consequence is structural and gendered: organizational meetings, union halls, public hearings, town halls, phone banks, and voter registration drives require physical presence at specific times. When a mother cannot cover childcare for those hours — and when the cost of doing so in a childcare desert is prohibitive — democratic participation is not a matter of preference or motivation. It is a matter of physical impossibility. Survey data consistently finds that single mothers cite "cannot attend meetings or voting due to childcare" as a primary participation barrier at rates exceeding any other demographic group.

### 1.7 The OBBBA's Multiplicative Compounding: Five Simultaneous Cuts to Civic Capacity

The OBBBA's democratic design significance is not in any single cut but in the simultaneity. Five concurrent income and capacity reductions operate on overlapping populations:

1. **Medicaid** (7.5–10 million projected enrollment losses by 2034; work requirements effective December 2026 for most states): Medicaid loss accelerates medical debt accumulation, which triggers the cognitive bandwidth and wage garnishment pathways.
2. **SNAP** (2.4 million households losing benefits in an average month; $106 billion reduction over 10 years): SNAP reduction increases food insecurity, forcing additional work hours to compensate, directly reducing civic time.
3. **Childcare subsidies** (CCDBG reduction): Childcare cost increase forces workforce exit for some mothers (losing income and organizational networks) and increased financial pressure for those who remain employed.
4. **Student loan protections** (SAVE plan eliminated): 6+ million borrowers enter default and wage garnishment pipeline, removing 15 percent of wages.
5. **Housing assistance** (housing voucher implications): Housing instability acceleration produces voter registration lapse.

The compounding dynamic is multiplicative: a household affected by two OBBBA cuts faces medical debt AND food insecurity simultaneously, consuming cognitive bandwidth at double the rate of either crisis alone. Add student loan garnishment, and the household is managing three concurrent financial emergencies. The civic participation capacity of this household is not reduced by 3x but by a multiplier closer to 5–7x, because each additional crisis lowers the threshold at which the tunneling effect fully crowds out civic engagement.

CBO's distributional analysis documents that OBBBA's combined effects will reduce incomes of the lowest 10 percent of earners by 3.1 percent by 2034 through program cuts, while raising incomes of the highest 10 percent by 2.7 percent through tax cuts. The Yale Budget Lab's analysis incorporating 2025 tariff effects shows the bottom 10 percent facing income reductions exceeding 6.5 percent when tariffs are combined with OBBBA spending cuts. This is a documented, quantified transfer of economic security from the population with the least civic capacity to the population with the most.

### Seven Causal Pathways from Economic Precarity to Democratic Exclusion

Each pathway requires an anchor causal or quasi-experimental study. Production phase must confirm effect sizes and identification strategies for all seven.

**Pathway 1 — Time Poverty**: Wage stagnation + multi-job work + commute burden + childcare hours = insufficient discretionary time for sustained civic engagement. **Causal anchors**: Dallas Fed WP2517 (financial relief → turnout), ATUS discretionary time analysis, Econofact/CEPR income-participation evidence. **Research question for production**: What does the latest ATUS show for discretionary hours per week for the bottom income quintile, controlling for multi-job workers and caregiving obligations?

**Pathway 2 — Housing Instability and Registration Lapse**: Cost burden → residential moves → voter registration lapse → organizational disconnection → community capital erosion → persistent non-participation. **Causal anchors**: Slee and Desmond (2023) — effect size confirmed (0.46 pp turnout decline per 1 pp eviction rate increase; 5.54 pp in low-eviction neighborhoods); PNAS 2024 housing voucher study — 20-year persistence. **Research question for production**: What percentage of registered voter dropouts are attributable to residential moves versus other causes?

**Pathway 3 — Medical Debt Cognitive Taxation**: Medical crisis → debt accumulation → cognitive bandwidth exhaustion → reduced capacity for political deliberation and engagement logistics. **Causal anchors**: Mani et al. (Science 2013) — experimental evidence; Journal of Economic Psychology 2024 meta-analysis — replication. **Research question for production**: Is there direct survey evidence linking medical debt burden specifically to reduced civic participation (Gallup, Pew, PRRI)?

**Pathway 4 — Wage Garnishment and Financial Coercion**: Student loan default + medical debt judgment → employer wage withholding (up to 15%) → income reduction below civic participation capacity threshold → chilling effect on employment-destabilizing organizing activity. **Causal anchor**: DOE/AEI default and garnishment data. **Research question for production**: What is the CFPB's data on wage garnishment prevalence by income bracket?

**Pathway 5 — Gendered Caregiving**: Women bear 72% of unpaid childcare (ATUS 2024) → additional 7+ hours per week over male counterparts in equivalent employment → single mothers face highest time-poverty exposure → civic organizing infrastructure excludes them by design. **Causal anchor**: ATUS disaggregated data; Care.com childcare economics; participation rate data for mothers vs. childless women. **Research question for production**: Is there direct comparative data on civic participation rates for single mothers vs. childless women vs. partnered mothers?

**Pathway 6 — Gig Economy Structural Unavailability**: Algorithm-driven scheduling → inability to commit to regular meetings or voting in advance → shift toward expressive (low-commitment) participation and away from sustained electoral participation. **Causal anchor**: Bae and Haselswerdt (Perspectives on Politics, 2023) — confirmed lower voting rates among gig workers despite higher expressive participation. **Research question for production**: What is the current BLS count of gig/platform workers and their income distribution?

**Pathway 7 — Social Stigma and Exclusion Signaling** *(least empirically developed — handle carefully)*: Reliance on SNAP, Medicaid, payday loans, or eviction history creates stigmatized "outsider" identity that reinforces perception that politics is for others. **Theoretical anchor**: Uslaner and Brown (Russell Sage Foundation) on inequality, trust, and civic engagement; political efficacy literature on external efficacy and financial distress. **Research question for production**: Does the political efficacy literature address financial distress as a predictor of low external political efficacy?

## Section 4: International Precedent — Economic Stabilization as Democratic Prerequisite
*[Full production draft target: July 1–5, 2026 — 4 research hours]*

### Core Comparative Cases

**West Germany 1948–1960 — Wirtschaftswunder as Democratic Foundation**

West Germany's democratic consolidation after 1949 required economic stabilization as a prerequisite. Ludwig Erhard's Sozialmarktwirtschaft (social market economy) — currency reform (June 1948), price control abolition, combined with a social safety net protecting households through transition — created the material conditions for democratic participation to become habitual rather than aspirational. Germans managing acute financial survival could not engage in democratic deliberation; the Wirtschaftswunder provided the floor from which democratic participation could be built.

Abelshauser, *Rebuilding Germany: The Creation of the Social Market Economy, 1945–1957* (Cambridge University Press), provides the primary scholarly treatment. Explaining History's 2025 analysis of the Wirtschaftswunder documents the role of economic recovery in identity reconstruction and democratic legitimacy. The Britannica treatment of the Adenauer period synthesizes the political-economic consolidation arc.

**Research questions for production**: What does Abelshauser specifically document about the causal relationship between Erhard's reforms and democratic stabilization? Are there other comparative cases in which economic floor policies demonstrably increased civic participation — Nordic welfare state construction, FDR New Deal, post-war Japan?

**Nordic Model — Economic Security as Civic Infrastructure**

Nordic democracies maintain parliamentary election turnout above 80 percent (Sweden 87 percent; Denmark's 2024 European Parliament turnout 58 percent, highest Nordic country) paired with comprehensive welfare systems that eliminate most material barriers to participation. International IDEA's Voter Turnout Database documents these turnout rates across the 2020–2024 period. The OECD *Society at a Glance 2024* chapter on voting provides the comparative civic engagement framework.

The specific claim: in Sweden, the income-participation gap is approximately 5–10 percentage points, compared to the US gap of 26–38 points. The structural explanation is economic: universal healthcare, free childcare, affordable housing supports, and free higher education remove the specific barriers this domain documents.

**Research questions for production**: What does the OECD Better Life Index show for US vs. Nordic civic engagement indicators? Is there academic literature explicitly making the economic security → civic participation causal link for Nordic countries in the comparative politics journals?

**FDR New Deal — US Domestic Precedent**

The New Deal's democratic significance was not primarily its specific programs but what they established: that the state had a constitutional responsibility to protect citizens from economic catastrophe. The Social Security Act (1935), the National Labor Relations Act (1935), and the Fair Labor Standards Act (1938) created the floor conditions for economic participation that translated, over the following decades, into expanded civic participation infrastructure. Union membership reached 35 percent of the workforce by the mid-1950s. Union halls were civic infrastructure.

**Research questions for production**: Is there political science literature explicitly connecting New Deal economic reforms to civic participation expansion in subsequent decades? What does the labor history literature show about the relationship between union density and voter turnout?

## Section 5: Reform Architecture — What Would Reverse This
*[Full production draft target: July 5–10, 2026 — 3 research hours]*

### Three-Tier Reform Architecture

**Tier 1 — Participatory Infrastructure (Direct Access Reforms)**

*Automatic Voter Registration (AVR)*: 24 states and DC have enacted AVR as of November 2025 (Brennan Center tracking). The remaining 26 states maintain opt-in registration that is acutely burdensome for geographically mobile low-income households. AVR decouples civic registration from residential stability — each move in an AVR state does not automatically sever voter registration. The Brennan Center's research documents significant gains in voter rolls wherever AVR has been implemented. MIT Election Lab's academic analysis confirms turnout impacts. The CIRCLE 2025 analysis documents that proposed registration restrictions would disproportionately harm young voters who frequently change addresses.

*Same-Day Registration (SDR)*: Rock the Registration (2022, *Journal of Politics*) establishes that SDR increases youth turnout by 3.1–7.3 percentage points — concentrated in the demographic most subject to geographic mobility.

*Automatic Voter Registration at Public Benefit Enrollment*: The NVRA reform argument (developed fully in Domain 50): requiring automatic registration at SNAP, Medicaid, and housing assistance enrollment would target exactly the population most subject to geographic instability and most excluded from civic participation. This is the bridge reform between economic policy and civic infrastructure.

*Voting Leave*: NCSL documents state employer voting leave requirements. A federal paid voting leave requirement — minimum 2 hours for hourly workers — would address the time cost of voting for multi-job workers who cannot absorb lost income.

**Tier 2 — Economic Floor Reforms (Upstream Interventions)**

*Federal minimum wage*: Federal minimum wage has been $7.25/hour since 2009. The Living Wage for All Act (introduced April 2026 by Representatives García, Ramirez, Simon, and Mejia) would phase to $25/hour — the 2026 cost-of-living equivalent. The Raise the Wage Act (H.R. 2743, S. 1332, 119th Congress) proposes a phase to $17/hour. Post-2026 midterm Senate composition determines the legislative feasibility timeline.

*CTC expansion*: ITEP's 2026 analysis documents that under OBBBA, the bottom fifth of households receive an average CTC benefit of $0 — 99 percent of children in the poorest fifth are excluded from the full credit. The American Family Act and analogous proposals would make the CTC fully refundable regardless of income, directly targeting the households with the highest precarity burden. The 2021 ARP expanded CTC reduced child poverty by 46 percent in the months it operated.

*Medicaid defense and expansion*: Post-OBBBA legislative strategy for states with Democratic governors and attorneys general: full ACA Medicaid expansion, aggressive NVRA Section 7 enforcement at Medicaid enrollment sites, legal challenges to work requirement implementation.

*Student loan reform*: The Pressley-Booker-Warren Ending Administrative Wage Garnishment Act; income-driven repayment with a genuine economic hardship exemption that provides relief before default rather than after.

*Childcare*: Child and Dependent Care Tax Credit Enhancement Act (S. 1421, 119th Congress); universal subsidized childcare federal baseline; CCDBG funding restoration.

**Tier 3 — Structural Reform (Long-Cycle)**

The democratic design argument at full scale: universal healthcare access, universal childcare, housing stability infrastructure, and a wage floor sufficient for one-job economic participation are not welfare programs. They are democracy architecture. Countries that have built these systems have achieved and maintained civic participation rates 20–25 percentage points above the US. The argument that economic policy is democratic infrastructure — not welfare adjacent to democratic reform — is the long-cycle reframe that this domain provides.

### What This Domain Adds (Not Duplicated Elsewhere)

Domain 59 provides the material-economic foundation that explains why the democratic exclusion documented in every other domain falls hardest on the populations it documents. Economic precarity is not a background condition for the harms in Domains 1, 31, 33, 39, 42, 43, 47, 48, and 50. It is the amplification mechanism:

- Domain 1 (Voting Rights / SAVE Act): SAVE Act documentation costs fall hardest on renters who have moved frequently — the OBBBA-housing-instability cohort.
- Domain 31 (Medicaid/OBBBA): Coverage loss accelerates medical debt accumulation; Domain 59 provides the causal chain from coverage loss to civic exclusion via medical debt.
- Domain 39 (Healthcare access → civic participation): Oregon Health Insurance Experiment established that healthcare coverage causally increases civic participation; Domain 59 documents the inverse mechanism (coverage loss → debt → exclusion) with equal rigor.
- Domain 47 (Housing security and democratic participation): Domain 47 documents housing crisis structure; Domain 59 provides the causal evidence (Slee/Desmond, PNAS 2024) that housing instability produces voter turnout decline.
- Domain 50 (NVRA-Medicaid nexus): Domain 50 documents the statutory mechanism (NVRA Section 7) by which Medicaid enrollment serves as voter registration infrastructure; Domain 59 explains why losing that infrastructure damages civic capacity at the household level.
- Domains 42 and 48 (Criminal justice / LFOs): Fines and legal financial obligations create economic precarity as a criminal justice output, operating through the same wage garnishment and cognitive taxation mechanisms documented in Domain 59.

**What no existing domain provides**: The time-poverty quantification and time-budget calculus; the causal evidence that financial relief (Dallas Fed WP2517) increases turnout; the gendered caregiving pathway as structural democratic exclusion; the gig economy structural unavailability analysis (Bae and Haselswerdt); and the democratic design reframe of CTC/minimum wage/housing as participation infrastructure rather than welfare.

The domain's single most important contribution is the reframe: economic precarity advocacy must become democratic infrastructure advocacy. The policy interventions it identifies — minimum wage, CTC, childcare, housing stability — are not antipoverty programs with democratic side effects. They are democratic design reforms with poverty reduction as a co-benefit. That reframe opens these policy arguments to the attention of democratic renewal funders, election protection organizations, and congressional oversight staff who currently do not receive economic justice work as directly relevant to their mission.
