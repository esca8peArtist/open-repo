# Domain 38 — Regulatory Capture in AI/Tech Regulation
## Phase 2 Research Launch Brief

---

## 1. Domain Title and Unique Research Angle

**Title**: Regulatory Capture in AI Governance: How Industry Shapes the Rules That Were Supposed to Govern It

**Unique angle (2-3 sentences)**: This domain does not survey the AI policy landscape — it identifies who controls AI policy and how. The US has no statutory AI governance framework; the only federal accountability architecture was revoked within hours of Trump's inauguration on January 20, 2025. What exists instead is a layered apparatus of voluntary standards written by industry, revolving-door placements at standards bodies, and a December 2025 executive order that weaponizes federal litigation authority to eliminate the only binding AI accountability mechanisms in existence — state-level laws.

---

## 2. Core Research Questions

- Which tech companies have operational regulatory capture apparatus in US AI governance — specifically: in-house policy shops shaping OMB guidance, revolving-door placements in NIST standards working groups, and standards-body capture through industry-led consortia like the Agentic AI Foundation?
- What are the current 2025-2026 capture instances? The xAI v. Colorado case (April 9 filing, April 24 DOJ intervention, April 27 enforcement suspension, May 9 legislative retreat) is the clearest case; what are its structural parallels in EU AI Act implementation gaps and FTC preemption theory failure?
- How does the December 2025 AI preemption executive order — directing DOJ, FTC, FCC, and Commerce to challenge state AI accountability laws — function as a governance capture tool, and what is its legal durability post-Loper Bright?
- Where does AI regulatory capture intersect with democratic and electoral infrastructure — specifically: algorithmic content moderation, voter microtargeting disclosures, and the absence of transparency requirements for AI-generated political content in US domestic political contexts while EU AI Act Article 50 requires machine-readable synthetic content marking for EU markets starting August 2, 2026?

---

## 3. Why This Domain Matters for Democratic Resistance

The regulatory capture documented here is not background policy failure — it is the mechanism explanation for why the algorithmic harms documented in Domains 36 and 40 persist. WISeR denies Medicare claims without disclosure. ImmigrationOS targets deportation through unaudited Palantir models. AI deepfakes operate as standard 2026 midterm campaign infrastructure. These harms share a common causal root: the absence of any binding federal AI accountability framework, and the active dismantlement of every state-level accountability mechanism that tried to fill the vacuum.

AI regulatory capture is also structurally distinct from conventional corporate regulatory capture: the captured institutions have no statutory authority to be captured. The capture is happening at the standard-setting layer — before binding law is enacted. NIST AI RMF is voluntary, but functions as de facto binding through federal procurement reference, state legislation incorporation, and private litigation duty-of-care standard. The capture of voluntary standards bodies, with no democratic mandate and no enforcement authority, sets the AI governance floor for the entire country.

The EU AI Act Article 50 enforcement deadline (August 2, 2026) makes the democratic stakes visible and cross-partisan: the same US tech companies that must embed machine-readable synthetic content markers for EU political markets face no equivalent obligation for US domestic political content. Regulatory arbitrage by design, operating in the least-regulated information context among all major democracies.
