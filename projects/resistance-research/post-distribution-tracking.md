# Post-Distribution Institutional Adoption Tracking Framework

**April 30, 2026**

This document provides the operational tracking architecture for the period after Phase 1 distribution begins, whichever path the user selects (A / A+37 / B). It does not duplicate the measurement-and-iteration-framework.md (which governs the feedback loop cycle) or the phase-3-institutional-playbooks-outline.md (which provides sector-specific operationalization). Instead, it provides what those documents do not: a decision-tree structure for real-time adoption signal interpretation, a domain-specific adoption performance table calibrated to the 35-domain corpus as it stands, and concrete success/failure thresholds by institutional sector and month.

The framework is path-agnostic. Path A and A+37 share the same tracking architecture; Path B adds a 14-28 day delay before Week 1 metrics are relevant.

---

## Executive Summary: 4-Week Post-Distribution Tracking Roadmap

The first four weeks after launch are the highest-signal period and the hardest to track systematically because institutional adoption happens in private. The purpose of the Week 1-4 roadmap is to distinguish early momentum (signals that indicate the framework is penetrating institutional networks) from early silence (silence that may indicate distribution failure, content barriers, or simply institutional processing lag).

**The core diagnostic question for the first 30 days is not "how many people read this" — it is "which institutional actors are doing something with it."**

### Week 1 (Days 1-7): Send monitoring and initial signal detection

- Monitor for email replies to institutional outreach — any substantive reply (not auto-acknowledgment) within 7 days signals a Tier 1 contact who should move immediately to the adoption facilitation sequence
- Set up Google Scholar alerts for: "democratic renewal proposal," domain title phrases (e.g., "35-domain diagnostic framework," "35-domain democratic"), and author-adjacent terms
- Set up Google News alerts: same search strings, daily delivery
- Log all Substack views and shares in the first 48 hours — the shape of the decay curve (how quickly views fall after initial post) indicates whether organic resharing is happening
- Monitor Just Security and Balkinization RSS feeds daily — these are the fastest institutional publishing channels for legal content
- Do NOT interpret silence in Week 1 as failure — Tier 1 institutional contacts typically take 5-14 days to process unsolicited policy material

**Week 1 target signal**: 2-4 substantive institutional replies (any tier). If zero substantive replies by Day 10, activate the "Week 1 silence response protocol" in Section 6.

### Week 2 (Days 8-14): First-mover identification and bridge node activation

- Identify which Tier 1 contacts have replied — these are the first-movers; immediately move them to the adoption facilitation sequence (Section 5 of measurement-and-iteration-framework.md)
- Begin the bridge node check for each of the seven priority bridge nodes: has any second-order signal appeared (a contact using the framework in their own published work)?
- Run the first domain performance check: which domains are mentioned in any reply, social media engagement, or citation? Record in the Domain Performance Log (Section 5 below)
- Activate the 14-day check-in for Reddit posts: have posts generated substantive discussion? Which subreddits showed the most engagement?

**Week 2 target signal**: At least 1 identified first-mover in Tier 1 or Tier 2; at least 1 domain cited or specifically requested.

### Week 3 (Days 15-21): Adoption pipeline confirmation

- Run the first adoption level assessment for any Tier 1 contacts who have replied — are they at Level 1 (reference) or progressing toward Level 2 (framework)?
- Conduct the first active check-in outreach to Tier 1 contacts who received materials but have not replied — not a follow-up asking if they read it, but a substantive prompt tied to a current event (see Section 6 for prompt templates)
- Check Overton for any new policy document citations (on a 6-18 month lag, this will not yet show framework citations, but it establishes the baseline for future comparison)
- Compile the first "domain heat map" — a simple table of which domains have generated any signal (reply, citation, question, social engagement), which have generated none

**Week 3 target signal**: Domain heat map reveals 3-5 domains with active engagement signals.

### Week 4 (Days 22-30): End-of-month synthesis

- Complete the first monthly iteration memo (per measurement-and-iteration-framework.md, Part IV, Week 2 procedure): which domains are generating pull, which are generating pushback, which bridge nodes have activated
- Assess adoption level distribution across all contacted organizations
- Confirm whether Phase 2 domain demand signals have emerged — which contacts are asking for more on specific domains
- Begin preparation for 30-day check-in outreach cycle with all Tier 1 contacts who have not yet responded

**Week 4 target**: 3-5 Level 1 adoption events confirmed; 1 Level 2 adoption event confirmed or in progress; zero bridge node cascade failures identified.

## Part I: Institutional Adoption Pathways by Sector

The 35-domain framework will encounter five distinct institutional processing pathways. Each has a characteristic timeline, decision structure, and first-adoption signal. Understanding which pathway each institution uses determines what evidence constitutes a meaningful early signal and prevents misreading institutional silence as rejection.

### Pathway 1: State Attorneys General (Democratic AGs — 24 states as of April 2026)

**Why fastest adoption is expected here**: Democratic AGs have been meeting almost daily since November 2024 preparing legal responses to the administration. As of April 2026, they have filed 71 lawsuits and won 40 of 51 resolved cases. They are operationally primed to receive new analytical frameworks because they are actively building legal theories, not merely studying them. The litigation-tracker-2026.md documents the AG network in detail — the key AGs (Bonta/California, James/New York, Ellison/Minnesota, Fetterman/Pennsylvania, Henry/Wisconsin) are already in active coordination.

**How they process policy frameworks**: AG offices have policy counsel who screen for litigation relevance first, research value second. The framework domains most likely to clear their threshold immediately are those with active or pending litigation anchors: Domain 6 (judicial independence — 350/362 federal court wins to protect), Domain 29 (prosecutorial weaponization — SPLC indictment, Nashville vindictive prosecution precedent), Domain 1 (voting rights — SAVE Act coalition, 71 AG suits already), and Domain 16 (immigration enforcement — most of the 71 suits are in this space).

**Expected timeline**: 2-4 weeks to first substantive contact; 4-8 weeks to first documented usage in a brief, press release, or coalition communication.

**First adoption signal**: An AG office's communications shop uses domain language in a press release or coalition statement. Not a citation — AGs rarely cite policy frameworks in press materials. Look for thematic alignment: if an AG's press release on a new ICE lawsuit describes the enforcement pattern using the same conceptual framework as Domain 16's Section 3 (the pattern-and-practice theory), that is an adoption signal even without attribution.

**Domain targeting for AGs**: Do not send all 35 domains. The optimal package is: Domain 1 (voting rights, especially the SAVE Act constitutional analysis), Domain 6 (judicial independence, especially the appellate capture timeline), Domain 16 (immigration enforcement, especially the warrantless arrest pattern-and-practice theory), and Domain 29 (prosecutorial weaponization, especially the SPLC indictment analysis and the Nashville vindictive prosecution standard). These four domains map to where AGs have active dockets.

**Tracking method**: Monitor AG press releases and coalition statements via each state AG website's press room (most have RSS feeds). Track CourtListener for new AG-filed suits and scan the complaint language for domain-aligned framing. Direct contact intelligence from Tier 1 contacts with AG relationships (Protect Democracy, States United Democracy Center, Democracy Docket) is more reliable than passive monitoring.

**Decision tree for AG sector**:
```
Day 30: Any AG office reply or contact-confirmed engagement?
  Yes → Advance to adoption facilitation sequence; identify specific domain need
  No → Day 45: Send targeted one-pager to AG policy counsel for top 3 AG offices;
        frame around a specific active case in their docket
        Day 60: If still no engagement, deprioritize passive monitoring;
        wait for a triggering court event (SPLC ruling, Crenshaw ruling, new Xinis development)
        to re-activate AG outreach with a timely hook
```

### Pathway 2: Law Schools — Clinics, Faculty, and Law Reviews

**Processing timeline**: 4-8 weeks for initial engagement; 8-16 weeks for confirmed academic use; 6-18 months for law review publication.

**How they process**: Law school clinic directors prioritize material that can be immediately deployed in active casework. Faculty prioritize material that advances research agendas or fills a teaching gap. Law review editors prioritize novel scholarly contributions. These three functions operate on different timelines and require different entry points.

**Clinic entry point**: The comparative constitutional law format (international precedents on the same legal question) is the highest-value offering for clinics. Domain 6 (judicial independence — comparative terms, removal standards, transparency requirements) and Domain 7 (rights protection — international covenant implementation) translate most directly to clinic use because comparative constitutional law is an accepted evidentiary standard in certain constitutional arguments. The Domain 28 (war powers) material also has clinic relevance given the Venezuela AUMF litigation ecosystem.

**Faculty entry point**: The "research agenda note" format — identifying the empirical gaps each domain leaves open that a graduate student or junior faculty could fill with original research. The domains with the most obvious academic research gaps are Domain 27 (higher education — academic freedom Index methodology disputes, brain drain measurement), Domain 35 (Supreme Court OT2026 — cert prediction under major questions doctrine), and Domain 36 (AI governance — algorithmic due process litigation theory). Send these domains with a research gap framing to political science and law school faculty contacts.

**Law review entry point**: The proposal's comparative constitutional law methodology is the most likely pathway to law review note or student comment publication. Domain 6 and Domain 28 map cleanest to existing constitutional law scholarship. A student note author at a top law review who uses the proposal's comparative evidence base represents a high-value second-order amplification event — the law review piece creates a citable academic source that subsequently legitimizes all downstream uses.

**First adoption signal for law schools**: A clinical professor requests a domain-specific brief for use in pending casework. This is the highest-value early signal because it indicates Level 3 (operational) adoption is in process, not merely Level 1 (reference). Second-order signal: a professor cites the proposal on a course syllabus or in a public lecture.

**Tracking method**: Direct contact intelligence is primary. Passive monitoring of law school clinic websites for new matter descriptions and published briefs. Google Scholar alerts for domain-title phrases appearing in law review articles or student notes. SSRN (Social Science Research Network) alerts for working papers citing the proposal.

**Decision tree for law school sector**:
```
Day 45: Any substantive reply from clinic director or faculty contact?
  Yes → Identify casework or research application; prepare tailored domain brief
  No → Day 60: Send "research agenda note" format version to faculty contacts;
        this is lower-friction than asking for immediate use
        Day 90: If still no signal, target clinics with specific active cases
        that match domain material (e.g., Domain 29 for prosecutorial defense clinic,
        Domain 6 for judicial independence litigation support)
```

### Pathway 3: Think Tanks and Policy Research Organizations

**Processing timeline**: 6-12 weeks to a published output citing the framework; 3-6 months to institutional integration.

**How they process**: Think tanks produce issue briefs, policy memos, and reports on a publication cycle that is responsive to the news environment. A think tank fellow who receives the framework does not publish about it the next week — they file it, reference it in internal work, and cite it when they write their next piece on a related topic. The publication timeline is driven by their editorial calendar and the news environment, not by the distribution date.

**The most important early signal for think tanks is not publication — it is a request for additional materials or a question about methodology.** When a Brennan Center fellow emails asking for the underlying sources for the Domain 6 comparative term-limits data, that is a stronger adoption signal than a social media share, because it indicates the fellow is doing serious engagement with the evidence.

**Domain targeting for think tanks**: By institution:
- Brennan Center: Domains 1, 2, 3 (election and voting rights), Domain 6 (judicial independence), Domain 29 (prosecutorial weaponization)
- CAP: Domains 11/31 (healthcare), Domain 17 (labor), Domain 5 (fiscal reform), Domain 23 (trade policy)
- EPI: Domain 17 (labor — co-determination data, NLRA reform proposals), Domain 5 (fiscal — carried interest, corporate tax)
- Brookings: Domains 28, 29 (war powers, DOJ capture — Quinta Jurecic/governance studies), Domain 6 (judicial reform)
- Protect Democracy: Full framework — their mission is the closest institutional analog to the proposal's scope
- Just Security: Domains 28, 29 (war powers, DOJ — their editorial focus)
- Issue One: Domain 3 (campaign finance — their primary mandate), Domain 6 (SCOTUS ethics)

**Tracking method**: Overton.io policy document citation monitoring (on lag — set alert but do not expect results before Month 3). Monitor each institution's publications page weekly. Direct contact intelligence via follow-up outreach at 60 days. Google News alerts for institution name + domain topic.

**Decision tree for think tanks**:
```
Day 60: Any citation in published think tank output?
  Yes → Log as Level 1 adoption; follow citation chain for second-order effects
  No → Day 60: Send targeted check-in to Tier 1 think tank contacts;
        frame check-in around a specific current event (e.g., new SCOTUS ruling, 
        Xinis contempt development) that makes a domain newly relevant
        Day 90: If still no signal for Brennan Center or Protect Democracy specifically,
        investigate whether the framework has been received — may be a delivery issue,
        not an adoption issue
```

**Processing timeline**: 8-16 weeks to first organizational statement citing the framework; 4-8 weeks to internal use in coalition strategy.

**How they process**: Civil rights coalitions work on immediate caseload and campaign priorities. A framework that maps to their current fight gets immediate attention; a framework that is valuable but not immediately actionable gets filed. The critical translation step is connecting each domain to a named current case, legislative battle, or community impact the organization is currently working on.

**The SPLC indictment is the entry point for Domain 29.** The Abrego Garcia case is the entry point for Domain 16. The SAVE Act failure is the entry point for Domain 1. ### Pathway 5: Legislative Sector (Congressional and State)

**Processing timeline**: Federal — 3-6 months from concept to bill introduction; 6-12 months to committee hearing; State — 4-12 weeks during active legislative session.

**How they process**: Congressional staff operate on hearing calendars and legislative vehicles. A policy framework becomes relevant to Hill staff when it provides evidence for a position already being advanced in a bill or hearing, not when it proposes new positions. The correct framing for Hill outreach is "supporting evidence for what you are already doing" rather than "a new agenda."

**State legislative engagement has a different and faster logic.** State legislatures in session right now (many will close spring sessions in May-June) are in active markup. A domain that provides model legislation language (Domain 33 on state autocratization, Domain 1 on voting rights, Domain 7 on rights protection) has the potential to be incorporated into a pending bill amendment within a single legislative session. The DLCC and SiX (State Innovation Exchange) contacts in the influencer map are the gateway to state legislative adoption.

**Federal entry points**: The proposal has built-in committee jurisdiction alignment:
- Senate Judiciary (Whitehouse, Durbin, Klobuchar): Domains 1, 6, 29
- Senate HELP (Warren): Domains 10, 17, 27
- House Judiciary Ranking (Raskin): Domains 6, 29
- Senate Armed Services (war powers subcommittee): Domain 28
- Senate Foreign Relations: Domain 28, Domain 19f

**Tracking method**: Congress.gov bill text search for domain-aligned language (imperfect but usable). Direct contact intelligence from Tier 1 legislative contacts. ProPublica Congress API for bill introduction monitoring. State legislative bill tracking via NCSL and LegiScan API.

**Decision tree for legislative sector**:
```
State (in-session):
  Day 21: Any state legislative contact confirmation?
    Yes → Prepare model legislation language brief for the domain they're working on
    No → Day 45: Check state session calendars; if session closing, wait for next session
          (most state legislatures reconvene in January — this becomes the Phase 2 window)

Federal:
  Day 60: Any Hill staff reply or engagement?
    Yes → Prepare domain-specific hearing background memo format
    No → Day 90: Re-engage when a relevant hearing is announced —
          committee hearing announcements are the natural hook for Hill outreach
```

## Part II: Impact Metrics Framework

### Measurement Philosophy (Carried Forward from measurement-and-iteration-framework.md)

Near-term signals are behavioral (who responds, who requests more). Medium-term signals are institutional (citations, organizational adoption). Long-term signals are outcome-based (legislation introduced, litigation shaped). This framework adds a fifth category absent from the prior document: **legal impact signals** — references in court filings and amicus briefs, which are distinct from policy document citations and represent the highest-value downstream use of the proposal's legal analysis sections.

### Legal Impact Metrics — Expanded

The measurement-and-iteration-framework.md does not specifically address legal citations, which are the highest-value impact signal for a proposal that grounds 18 of its 35 domains in litigation or constitutional law analysis. This section provides the specific tracking protocol.

**What to look for in court filings**:
- Verbatim or near-verbatim language from domain analysis sections (particularly the comparative constitutional law evidence in Domains 6, 7, 28, 29)
- Citation to the same underlying sources the proposal uses (e.g., a brief citing the same international judicial independence standards cited in Domain 6's Section 2.3)
- Structural argumentation that follows the domain's analytical framework without citation — the hardest to detect but most significant form of influence

**Tracking protocol**:
1. Set CourtListener full-text search alerts for proposal title and key domain phrases (e.g., "35-domain democratic renewal," "democratic renewal proposal," key analytical coinages)
2. Bloomberg Law or PACER docket search for new filings in tracked litigation cases (litigation-tracker-2026.md cases) — when a case receives new briefing, check for domain-aligned language
3. Track amicus brief dockets specifically in SCOTUS and circuit court cases touching domains 1, 6, 14, 28, 29 — amicus briefs are the most likely early vehicle for proposal citation because they are produced by advocacy organizations (ACLU, Brennan Center, Protect Democracy) that are in the Tier 1 outreach list

**Success threshold for legal impact**: A single amicus brief citation in a federal circuit court case is a high-value Phase 1 adoption event — it creates a citable precedent for all subsequent legal work, meaning the proposal's influence compounds through the citation chain.

## Part III: Domain Performance Analysis

### Domain Adoption Probability Rankings

The following rankings estimate which domains will generate institutional adoption signals earliest, based on: (1) the density of active litigation or legislative vehicles in the domain, (2) the novelty of the analytical framework relative to existing coverage, (3) the strength of Tier 1 contacts already working in the domain, and (4) the advocacy window urgency.

**Tier A — Fastest adoption expected (2-6 weeks)**

1. **Domain 29 (Prosecutorial Weaponization)**: The SPLC indictment (April 21, 2026) has not yet generated sustained analytical coverage from organizations outside the immediate civil rights community. Domain 29's Section 3 (22-case retaliatory pattern) and Section 5 (bank fraud contradiction) are novel relative to existing coverage. Just Security and Weissmann-adjacent networks will engage with this material quickly. The Nashville Crenshaw ruling, when it comes down, creates a news hook that makes this domain immediately publishable.

2. **Domain 6 (Judicial Independence)**: The domain covers the most active current institutional battlefield. 350/362 federal court wins. Xinis contempt proceedings ongoing. The appellate capture timeline (Section 8.4) is the analytical frame that most sophisticated Tier 1 contacts are lacking. Whitehouse's office and the Brennan Center judiciary team are the primary early adopters.

3. **Domain 28 (War Powers / Venezuela)**: The domain documents the most significant war powers constitutional confrontation since the Iraq AUMF. The Senate 51-50 vote record, the OLC memo's internal concession on constitutional determination, and the Seth Harp subpoena connection are all under-reported. Just Security has a direct editorial interest in this domain.

4. **Domain 16 (Immigration)**: The existing litigation network (71 AG suits, 350/362 federal court wins, the NILC/CLINIC/RAICES relationships from the cybersecurity trilogy) creates the fastest pathway to Level 3 adoption for any domain in the corpus.

**Tier B — Moderate adoption timeline (4-10 weeks)**

5. **Domain 1 (Voting Rights)**: Dense evidence base, active Brennan Center and Democracy Docket engagement, but also highly contested terrain with heavy existing coverage. The SAVE Act constitutional analysis and the BISC 155-bill count are the novel additions.

6. **Domain 27 (Higher Education)**: The academic freedom domain will be fastest to engage the academic sector directly — AAUP and Chronicle of Higher Education are the entry points. The U.S. institutional autonomy score collapse (3.3 to 1.7, faster than Hungary) is the data point that will generate academic citation.

7. **Domain 33 (State Legislative Autocratization)**: The state-level democratic erosion framing fills a genuine gap, but requires state legislative staff relationships to convert into operational adoption. Timing depends on state session calendars.

8. **Domain 23 (Trade Policy)**: The IEEPA SCOTUS ruling (Learning Resources, February 20, 2026) provides an immediately usable hook. CAP and EPI both cover trade policy; Section 301 investigation analysis fills a gap. SCOTUS ruling made this domain time-sensitive.

**Tier C — Slower adoption timeline (8-16 weeks) or dependent on triggering event**

9. **Domains 2-3 (Redistricting, Campaign Finance)**: High-quality evidence but heavily covered terrain. Adoption depends on a triggering legislative event (e.g., a new gerrymandering ruling or FEC enforcement action).

10. **Domain 35 (SCOTUS OT2026)**: Value will spike when OT2026 cert grants are announced (summer 2026). Currently in Tier C but will move to Tier A by September 2026.

11. **Domain 31x (Healthcare Tariff Collision)**: July 31 tariff effective date creates urgency, but healthcare policy is a specialized space with existing coverage. Adoption will depend on whether HHS guidance on Medicaid work requirements generates a media hook in June.

12. **Domains 5, 9, 10, 11, 14, 15, 18-22 (fiscal, federalism, education, healthcare, criminal justice, environment, labor, media, immigration, disability, data)**: The framework's structural domains. These are less likely to generate early adoption signals because they are less tied to active litigation or legislative vehicles. They will be adopted as organizations engage with the framework holistically after initial entry through a faster domain.

### Adaptation Patterns to Track

Beyond which domains generate adoption, the framework needs to track *how* domains get adapted when institutions use them. Three adaptation patterns are most significant:

**Pattern 1 — Scope extension**: An organization takes a domain's analytical framework and applies it to a problem beyond the domain's stated scope. Example: a state AG uses the Domain 29 prosecutorial weaponization framework to analyze a state-level AG political prosecution pattern not covered in the domain. This is the highest-value adaptation — it signals that the framework is analytically generative beyond its documented cases.

**Pattern 2 — Local contextualization**: An organization connects the domain's national or comparative evidence to a specific state or city context. Example: the Domain 33 state autocratization framework applied to a specific state's preemption law package. This is a predictable adaptation for Tier 3 organizations; document but do not revise the base domain in response.

**Pattern 3 — Evidence challenge or gap identification**: An organization disputes a specific evidence claim in a domain or identifies a research gap. Example: an academic challenges the Domain 27 claim that U.S. institutional autonomy score decline is faster than Hungary's. This is the most valuable feedback for domain iteration — when a Tier 1 or Tier 2 contact challenges a specific evidence claim, the response protocol is to verify immediately and update if warranted.

**Adaptation tracking log template** (maintain one entry per identified adaptation):
```
Domain: [Domain number and title]
Institution: [Organization name]
Adaptation type: [Scope extension / Local contextualization / Evidence challenge / Evidence gap]
Description: [What did they do with the domain material?]
Date detected:
Source of intelligence:
Response required?: [Yes — update domain / Yes — prepare tailored brief / No — log only]
```

| Civil rights org monitoring | Individual org press rooms | Monthly manual check of NAACP LDF, Lawyers' Committee, Indivisible resource library, FairVote, States United press rooms | Monthly | Agent review session |
| Social media amplification | Bluesky / Twitter keyword search | Weekly manual search for proposal title and domain phrases | Weekly | User |
| Contact log | Manual — maintained by user | Record every substantive outreach response immediately | Per event | User; reviewed by agent monthly |

### Monitoring Review Cadence

The monitoring infrastructure runs on two parallel cycles:

**User-managed (daily / triggered)**: News alerts arrive and are flagged if relevant. RSS feeds surface publications for quick review. Social media searches run when user has time. The user logs contacts immediately on receipt.

**Agent-managed (biweekly / monthly)**: Every two weeks, run a review session covering: Overton alerts, CourtListener search, LegiScan state bills, civil rights org press rooms. Monthly: write the synthesis memo (per measurement-and-iteration-framework.md, Part IV). This is the session where all passive signals are compiled into the domain heat map and adoption scorecard update.

## Part V: Feedback and Iteration Protocol

### What Feedback to Capture

Three types of feedback are analytically distinct and require different responses:

**Type 1 — Evidence feedback**: A contact challenges or refines a specific factual claim in a domain. Response: verify immediately; if the contact is correct, update the domain file within 72 hours and notify the contact of the update. This is the fastest response protocol because it maintains the proposal's credibility.

**Type 2 — Framing feedback**: A contact says the domain analysis is correct but is framed in a way that prevents their use of it ("too academic for our members," "sounds like advocacy not analysis," "doesn't connect to our current fight"). Response: prepare a translated version of the relevant domain section for that organization — do not revise the base document.

**Type 3 — Gap feedback**: A contact identifies a domain or sub-domain that would be valuable but is not covered. Response: log as a Phase 2/3 research candidate in EXPLORATION_QUEUE.md. Do not start research immediately — assess whether other contacts identify the same gap before investing research time.

### Iteration Triggers (Inherited from measurement-and-iteration-framework.md, refined)

| Trigger | Threshold | Action |
|---------|-----------|--------|
| Two Tier 1 contacts raise the same evidence objection within 30 days | 2 independent contacts | Verify; update domain if warranted; notify both contacts |
| A court ruling changes the legal landscape of a domain | Any ruling affecting tracked cases | Update domain section within 1 week; notify domain-specific Tier 1 contacts |
| A Tier 3 organization reports the domain does not match their operational context | 1 organization report | Prepare localized adaptation brief; do not revise base domain |
| A domain generates zero engagement signal after 60 days | 0 any-tier signals | Analyze whether distribution failure (wrong contacts) or content barrier; adjust targeting, not content |
| A Phase 2 domain scope document receives a Tier 1 request | 1 Tier 1 request | Advance to full research scheduling |
| A domain adaptation creates a novel analytical extension worth incorporating | Agent assessment | Add as a new sub-section (not a revision) in next iteration cycle |

### Synthesizing Adaptations Back Into the Framework

When institutional users adapt a domain — extending its scope, localizing it, or identifying evidence gaps — the most valuable adaptations should be integrated into the base document. The integration protocol:

1. Log the adaptation in the adaptation tracking log
2. Assess whether the adaptation represents a genuine improvement to the framework or a specialized use case
3. If improvement: add as a new subsection or evidence update in the next monthly iteration
4. If specialized use case: file as a "derived format" alongside the base domain (e.g., domain-29-ag-brief.md as a companion to domain-29-prosecutorial-weaponization.md)
5. Notify the institution whose adaptation was incorporated — this strengthens the relationship and creates a co-ownership dynamic that increases long-term adoption

## Part VI: Decision Trees — When to Do What

### Decision Tree 1: Reading Early Adoption Signals

```
Week 2-3 post-launch: What signals exist?

SIGNAL A: Tier 1 institutional reply (substantive)
  → Immediate action: Enter adoption facilitation sequence (Part V, measurement-and-iteration-framework.md)
  → Prepare tailored domain brief within 7 days
  → Log in contact log; update adoption scorecard

SIGNAL B: Just Security or Lawfare publication citing the framework
  → Immediate action: Log as Level 1 adoption; track citation chain
  → Forward to any Tier 1 contacts who have not yet engaged: "Just Security just published on this" is a credibility accelerant
  → Within 24 hours: check if the publication is shareable to Reddit or social channels for amplification

SIGNAL C: Social media engagement from named contact
  → Log if from a Tier 1 or Tier 2 contact; log for bridge node tracking if from a bridge node
  → Do not interpret Tier 3 social engagement as institutional adoption — it may be entirely separate from internal organizational use
  → If a Tier 2 academic shares or comments publicly: send a direct follow-up email within 48 hours

SIGNAL D: Domain-specific request (contact asks for more on a specific domain)
  → This is a higher-value signal than a substantive reply — it means active use is in process
  → Prepare domain brief within 72 hours
  → Ask what they are using it for — this surfaces Level 3 adoption in progress

SIGNAL E: No signal by Day 14 from any Tier 1 contact
  → Do not conclude failure — institutional processing lag is normal
  → Check that outreach emails were delivered (ask a trusted contact if they received it)
  → Prepare Week 3 active check-in outreach using the prompt templates in Section 6 of measurement-and-iteration-framework.md
  → Day 21: If still no signal, assess whether the subject line or framing in the outreach email may be creating a filter problem
```

### Decision Tree 2: Interpreting Domain Performance at 60 Days

```
60-day domain heat map review:

PATTERN 1: 3+ domains generating signals, rest silent
  → Expected outcome — concentrate adoption facilitation resources on hot domains
  → Use hot domain success as social proof for cold domain outreach ("Brennan Center is using the Domain 6 material; the Domain 17 material uses the same evidence standard")
  → Do not revise cold domains based on silence alone — silence at 60 days is not content failure

PATTERN 2: 1-2 domains generating signals, rest silent
  → Investigate whether distribution reached the right institutional contacts for cold domains
  → Prepare targeted cold-domain briefs for specific contacts who should be most receptive
  → Check whether a "current threat brief" format would make cold domains more accessible

PATTERN 3: Zero domains generating signals
  → Investigate delivery — did institutional outreach actually reach the right staff?
  → Assess whether subject line / framing is creating a filter problem
  → Consider a warm referral approach: does any existing relationship allow a person-to-person introduction that bypasses cold outreach filtering?
  → Do not revise content until delivery is confirmed — the problem is more likely distribution, not content

PATTERN 4: Only Tier 3 engagement (coalitions, general public) — no Tier 1 signals
  → Tier 3 engagement is valuable but not sufficient for institutional adoption
  → Re-evaluate Tier 1 outreach: were the right contacts identified? Were emails sent to named individuals or general inboxes?
  → Activate a warm referral approach using any Tier 3 contacts who have confirmed reading the proposal — ask them to make a direct introduction to a Tier 1 contact they know
```

### Decision Tree 3: When a Bridge Node Activates

```
Bridge node activation event (a priority bridge node uses the framework in published work):

STEP 1: Log immediately with: date, publication/venue, what they said, estimated reach
STEP 2: Within 24 hours — check for second-order signals (who is citing the bridge node's piece?)
STEP 3: Within 48 hours — forward the bridge node's publication to any Tier 1 or Tier 2 contacts who have not yet engaged; a bridge node citation is the most powerful credibility signal available
STEP 4: Assess whether the bridge node's use of the framework aligns with the intended framing or represents a reinterpretation
  → If aligned: amplify broadly
  → If reinterpreted: do not correct publicly; reach out privately to clarify the intended scope
STEP 5: If a bridge node activates a cascade (their publication generates citations from other institutions), map the cascade:
  → Use the cascade mapping template in Part III of measurement-and-iteration-framework.md
  → The depth of the cascade (A cites proposal → B cites A → C cites B) is the primary measure of network penetration
```

### Decision Tree 4: Responding to Institutional Pushback

```
A Tier 1 or Tier 2 contact pushes back on domain content:

TYPE A: "The evidence for this claim is overstated"
  → Verify immediately against primary sources
  → If they are correct: update domain within 72 hours; thank them and notify of the update
  → If they are incorrect: respond with the primary source citations; do not revise
  → Log as an evidence challenge in the adaptation tracking log

TYPE B: "This framing is too political for our use"
  → Offer to prepare a diagnostic-only version that separates crisis analysis from prescriptive proposals
  → Do not revise the base document — this is a translation issue, not a content issue
  → Log as a framing challenge; if three or more contacts raise the same framing concern within 60 days, reconsider the domain introduction structure

TYPE C: "We already have this analysis"
  → Ask to see their analysis — this is a discovery, not a rejection
  → If their analysis is compatible: explore whether their evidence base can supplement the domain
  → If their analysis contradicts: identify the disagreement specifically; this is an evidence verification opportunity
  → Log as a confirmation signal (domain analysis is correct) and a potential co-authorship opportunity

TYPE D: "We don't have capacity right now"
  → Convert to a conditional commitment: "When [specific triggering event] happens, would this be useful?"
  → Schedule a 90-day follow-up tied to that event
  → Log as "conditional" in adoption scorecard — not a failure, a future opportunity
```

### What Success and Failure Actually Look Like at 12 Months

**Success scenario**: The Brennan Center has produced a judicial independence brief that incorporates Domain 6's comparative term-limits analysis (Level 2 adoption). Just Security has published two pieces citing Domain 28 and Domain 29 (Level 1 adoption generating academic credibility). An AG coalition in 3-4 states is using the Domain 1 constitutional analysis in SAVE Act litigation (Level 3 adoption). Indivisible's training materials reference the 3.5% threshold with a footnote to the resistance meta-analysis (Level 2 adoption). One state has introduced a voting rights bill using Domain 1 model legislation language (Level 3 adoption, state legislative sector). Domain 35 (SCOTUS OT2026) has generated a law school note submission at a top law review (beginning of academic citation chain). Total: 8-10 confirmed adoption events at Level 2 or higher.

**Failure scenario**: At 12 months, all engagement is at Level 1 (citation) and mostly from Tier 3 general audience. No Tier 1 think tank has incorporated the framework into published work. No AG has used domain analysis in litigation. No law school clinic has used the material in active casework. This scenario most likely indicates a distribution problem (the right institutional contacts were not reached) rather than a content problem, and the response is a targeting recalibration, not a document revision.

**Partial success scenario** (most likely): 2-3 domains have generated genuine Tier 1 institutional adoption while the remaining 30+ domains remain at Level 0 or Level 1. This is the expected pattern — institutional adoption of policy frameworks concentrates in the domains that map to the adopting organization's current priority fight. The response is to use the high-adoption domains as entry points for relationship-building that eventually expands to other domains.
