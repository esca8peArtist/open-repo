## The Coalition Problem, Stated Precisely

Phase 1 distribution targets 150+ institutions: law schools, think tanks, civil rights organizations, labor unions, environmental advocates, fiscal watchdogs, faith coalitions, state bar associations, local newspapers, and more. Each sector has different primary constituents, different institutional cultures, different donor relationships, different political risk tolerances, and different timelines for action. A law school's highest-priority concern is academic freedom and tenure protection; a labor union's highest-priority concern is collective bargaining rights and wages; an environmental organization's highest-priority concern is regulatory protection and climate action; a fiscal watchdog's highest-priority concern is government accountability and deficit management.

These are not the same priorities. In many policy areas, they are actively in tension.

The coalition problem is not how to pretend these differences don't exist — that produces the lowest-common-denominator outcomes that plague multi-sector advocacy. The coalition problem is how to structure decision-making and resource-sharing so that institutions with genuinely different priorities can coordinate on shared objectives without requiring agreement on everything, while preventing the loudest or most-funded voices from systematically dominating the coalition's direction.

This playbook addresses four questions:
1. How does Phase 1 achieve broad buy-in without becoming lowest-common-denominator?
2. How does the coalition make decisions when sector interests conflict?
3. How are resources shared without creating dependency hierarchies?
4. How are coalition partners held accountable without replicating the tyranny-of-majority dynamics that cause coalitions to fracture?

## Part I: The Buy-In Architecture

### Why Lowest-Common-Denominator Is the Default Failure Mode

Multi-sector coalitions almost always produce lowest-common-denominator outcomes when they are organized around finding consensus before acting. The mechanism is structural: each additional sector added to a coalition reduces the action space (what everyone can agree on), while adding organizational overhead (more coordination, more meeting time, more conflict). Rational coalition managers respond by narrowing the scope of the coalition's agenda to the intersect of all member priorities — which is usually small, vague, and non-threatening to any existing power structure.

The European Council provides an extreme version of this dynamic: member states formally adopt most decisions by consensus even though qualified-majority voting is available, because the vetocracy of consensus-seeking is preferable to the political cost of outvoting a member state. [1] The result is lowest-common-denominator policy output on the most contested issues.

The solution is not to eliminate the diversity of coalition partners — that diversity is the source of the coalition's political breadth. The solution is to structure the coalition so that most decisions are made within domains (by the institutions with relevant expertise) and only a small number of decisions require cross-domain deliberation.

### The Phase 1 Buy-In Principle: Differentiated Entry Points

The Phase 1 framework's 40 domains are not equally relevant to all 150+ target institutions. Law schools care intensively about academic freedom (Domains 27, 8), civil liberties (Domain 7), and civil service independence (Domain 2). Labor unions care intensively about labor protections (Domain 17), economic policy (Domains 3, 4, 20), and campaign finance (Domain 7). Environmental organizations care intensively about climate and regulatory protection (Domain 15, 38), and intersect with labor on the BlueGreen Alliance framework (jobs + climate together). [2]

The buy-in architecture should reflect this: each institution is invited into the coalition through its highest-priority domain entry point, not through a comprehensive agenda that requires immediately signing onto all 40 domains. The Civil Marriage Collaborative model — funders joining around shared strategic logic while maintaining independent grantmaking priorities — provides the template. [3] Partners commit to shared infrastructure (research, messaging, coordination) while maintaining independent programmatic priorities.

**Concretely, differentiated entry point categories:**

**Category A: Litigation Anchor Partners** (ACLU, Brennan Center, NAACP LDF, Lambda Legal, Earthjustice, Democracy Forward, state AG offices)
These institutions join primarily around the litigation infrastructure — shared filing coordination, pre-positioned brief libraries, rapid-response capacity. Their commitment is to coordinate litigation timing and share legal research, not to adopt a comprehensive policy platform. They maintain independent case selection.

**Category B: Research and Policy Partners** (law schools, policy institutes, Brookings, Center for American Progress, Urban Institute, Economic Policy Institute, Center on Budget and Policy Priorities)
These institutions contribute research infrastructure: domain-specific policy papers, model legislation, impact analysis, and evidence synthesis. Their commitment is to publish research accessible to coalition partners and participate in domain working groups on their areas of expertise. They do not commit to advocacy positions that would compromise their research independence.

**Category C: Advocacy and Organizing Partners** (labor unions, NAACP, LWV, SEIU, UAW, faith coalitions, environmental groups, reproductive rights organizations, disability rights organizations)
These institutions contribute advocacy capacity: public communications, grassroots mobilization, donor networks, and electoral infrastructure. Their commitment is to amplify coalition messaging on shared-priority domains and to participate in cross-domain coordination where their constituencies are directly affected. They maintain independent advocacy agendas on their primary issues.

**Category D: Civic Infrastructure Partners** (local newspapers, community colleges, public libraries, bar associations, community foundations)
These institutions contribute civic infrastructure: distribution networks, credibility in specific communities, and relationship networks that national organizations cannot replicate. Their commitment is to distribute Phase 1 materials, host civic education events, and connect the coalition to local organizing networks. They do not make policy commitments.

This architecture allows a local bar association and the SEIU to both be coalition partners without requiring them to agree on labor law. Their entry points are different; their coordination requirements are minimal; their combined reach is greater than either alone.

### The Research on "Winning Without Consensus"

The Medicaid expansion case is the strongest U.S. evidence base for this principle. Diverse advocacy coalitions — combining healthcare providers, civil rights organizations, faith communities, and labor unions — succeeded in passing expansion in Republican-controlled states (Idaho, Nebraska, Oklahoma, Missouri through ballot initiative) precisely because they built coordinated mobilization around a single concrete deliverable (Medicaid expansion) rather than requiring comprehensive agreement on healthcare policy. [4]

The interest group conflict research on Medicaid expansion found that public advocates had a "surprising impact" — unexpected influence relative to their organizational resources — because they maintained coalition discipline around the single expansion question while allowing independent advocacy on related but distinct issues (insurance regulation, pharmaceutical pricing, etc.). [4] The discipline was scope-bounded, not comprehensive.

The BlueGreen Alliance demonstrates the same principle in the labor-environment tension. The Alliance took eight months and 24 drafts to produce its 2019 "Solidarity for Climate Action" platform — a process that demonstrated how much deliberation cross-sector alignment requires. But the Alliance explicitly acknowledged that on some issues (permitting reform, for example) it could not achieve internal consensus and did not take a position. [2] The scope boundaries — what the coalition does and does not coordinate on — are explicit rather than implicit.

## Part II: Decision-Making Under Coalition Diversity

### The Three-Axis Decision Matrix

The fundamental insight for coalition decision-making is that different types of decisions require different authority structures. Attempting to use the same decision process for all decisions produces either paralysis (if consensus is required for everything) or resentment (if majority rule is applied to decisions that substantially affect minority members).

The three-axis decision matrix classifies decisions by scope and impact:

**Axis 1 — Federal Priority Decisions**: Decisions primarily affecting federal-tier work (which lawsuits to support, which legislation to prioritize, which congressional relationships to develop). These decisions are made by the Category A and B partners active in federal-tier work, with Category C and D partners informed but not voting.

*Example*: Whether to file an amicus brief in a particular SCOTUS case on voting rights. Decision authority: Brennan Center, ACLU, LWV (Category A and B partners with litigation expertise). Process: majority of the relevant domain working group, with opt-out available for Category C and D partners who have conflicting commitments.

**Axis 2 — State Priority Decisions**: Decisions primarily affecting state-tier work (which ballot initiatives to support, which state legislative campaigns to coordinate). These decisions are made by state-tier partners in the relevant states, with national partners providing resources but not directing state-level choices.

*Example*: Whether to support a ranked-choice voting ballot initiative in Arizona in 2026. Decision authority: Arizona-based coalition members (SiX Arizona contacts, ACLU of Arizona, Arizona AFL-CIO), with national partners providing funding and communications support if the state coalition proceeds. Process: consensus of state-level members with national partners providing opt-in support.

**Axis 3 — Shared Priority Decisions**: Decisions that affect the coalition's overall direction, resource allocation between tiers, or public messaging framework. These decisions require multi-sector deliberation and are made by an elected steering committee with representation from all four category tiers.

*Example*: How to allocate a $5M infrastructure grant across federal, state, and local tiers. Decision authority: Coalition steering committee (3 representatives from each category, elected annually). Process: supermajority (two-thirds) with opt-out available for partners who cannot support a specific allocation.

### The Opt-Out Mechanism

The opt-out mechanism is the critical safety valve that makes supermajority governance workable across sector diversity. When the coalition makes an Axis 3 decision by supermajority, partners who cannot support that decision have two options: (1) opt out of that specific decision without leaving the coalition, or (2) publicly dissent while remaining in the coalition on other decisions.

This mechanism is derived from EU Council practice, where qualified-majority voting is technically available but consensus is usually sought because the political costs of outvoting a member state are high. The opt-out equivalent — allowing a partner to formally decline participation in a specific decision while maintaining coalition membership — reduces the political cost of being in a coalition where one specific decision does not align with institutional priorities. [1]

The critical constraint on the opt-out mechanism: partners cannot opt out of infrastructure sharing (overhead pool contributions, shared research access, joint communications) without leaving the coalition. The opt-out applies to specific action decisions, not to coalition membership terms.

### When Federal and State Infrastructure Conflict

The tension between federal infrastructure priority (Domain 34 power-of-purse fiscal reassertion) and state infrastructure priority (ballot initiatives requiring state-level education spending) is not hypothetical — it is the exact tension that has fragmented the progressive coalition on education funding, healthcare financing, and tax policy for decades.

The three-axis matrix resolves this through scope clarity: the federal fiscal reassertion work (Domain 34) is an Axis 1 federal priority decision, decided by federal-tier partners. State education ballot initiatives are Axis 2 state priority decisions, decided by state-tier partners in the relevant states. The coalition does not need to resolve the abstract tension between federal fiscal authority and state spending autonomy — it needs to operate the two tiers independently, with shared infrastructure but independent action.

Where the tension becomes a real coordination problem is in messaging: if the federal-tier partners are campaigning for congressional power-of-purse reassertion (potentially involving spending cuts to re-establish budget discipline), and the state-tier partners are campaigning for state education funding increases (requiring state spending), the public messaging will be incoherent unless there is explicit coordination.

The resolution mechanism is a messaging working group — not to force agreement, but to identify incompatibilities and develop framing that allows both campaigns to proceed without actively contradicting each other. "Federal fiscal accountability" and "state investment in education" can coexist as messages if they are targeted at different audiences, with different specific asks. The working group's function is frame-separation, not frame-unification.

### The Priority Stack Model

For decisions where the three axes are insufficient — where a decision genuinely requires choosing between federal, state, and local priorities — the coalition should use an explicit priority stack rather than attempting deliberation without a decision rule:

**Priority 1 (Non-negotiable)**: Actions that would reduce coalition partners' capacity to pursue their primary missions. No coalition decision can require a labor union to stop organizing, a law school to violate academic freedom principles, or a civil rights organization to abandon clients. These constraints are bright lines that override any coalition majority.

**Priority 2 (Supermajority required)**: Actions that require cross-domain coordination and affect multiple partners' public positioning. Coalition messaging, resource allocation, and endorsement decisions require the two-thirds steering committee supermajority, with opt-out available.

**Priority 3 (Domain working group authority)**: Actions within a specific domain that primarily affect domain-specific partners. Voting rights litigation coordination is decided by voting rights partners; labor law advocacy strategy is decided by labor partners. No cross-domain consultation required unless the decision affects other domains.

**Priority 4 (Individual partner authority)**: Actions taken independently by coalition partners in their own name, not the coalition's name. Partners are encouraged but not required to coordinate with other partners before individual public actions. They are required to notify the messaging working group of major public commitments that could affect the coalition's collective positioning.

## Part III: Conflict Resolution Architecture

### The Three Predictable Conflict Types

Coalition conflict in multi-sector democratic renewal work follows predictable patterns. Naming these patterns in advance — making them part of the coalition's governance documentation rather than waiting for them to emerge — reduces the cost of resolution when they occur.

**Type 1 — Resource Conflict**: Competition for foundation grants, donor attention, media coverage, and political relationships among coalition partners. This is the most common conflict in advocacy coalitions and the most destructive if unresolved, because it creates competitive incentives that undermine coordination.

*Prevention*: Domain-specific funding lanes (healthcare funders fund healthcare domain work; environmental funders fund environmental domain work; shared-infrastructure funders fund coalition overhead). Partners are explicitly encouraged to identify their own funding lanes and notify the coalition when pursuing grants that could affect partner organizations.

*Resolution*: When funding conflicts arise (two partners competing for the same grant), the steering committee facilitates a division of scope — one partner takes the litigation component, the other takes the advocacy component — or a joint application where both partners are named. The Democratic Attorneys General Association's model of shared litigation costs without competitive independent filings provides a template. [5]

**Type 2 — Message Conflict**: Public statements by one partner that contradict or complicate another partner's messaging. This is particularly acute when labor and environmental partners make public statements on economic policy (where their constituencies may have conflicting interests), or when civil rights and law enforcement reform partners address criminal justice policy.

*Prevention*: A rapid-communication protocol that gives the messaging working group 24 hours to review major public statements before release. Not approval authority — but a notification system that allows partners to flag potential conflicts before they go public.

*Resolution*: The messaging working group facilitates a clarification call within 48 hours of a conflict emerging. If the conflict reflects genuine disagreement on policy, the resolution is explicit scope separation (each partner speaks to its own domain) rather than forced consensus. The BlueGreen Alliance's practice of explicitly declining to take positions on issues where labor-environment consensus is impossible is the model. [2]

**Type 3 — Accountability Conflict**: Partner organizations failing to fulfill coordination commitments — not sharing research outputs, not attending domain working groups, not notifying the coalition of conflicting actions. This is the hardest type to resolve because it implicates organizational capacity and internal priorities, not just decision-making structures.

*Prevention*: Lightweight commitment tracking. Each partner specifies, at joining, a concrete commitment: "We will share two research publications per quarter with the coalition research repository" or "We will attend quarterly domain working group meetings and send a delegate when unable to attend." These commitments are tracked publicly on the coalition's internal coordination platform.

*Resolution*: Graduated accountability mechanism: (1) private inquiry from the coordination staff (month 1 of non-fulfillment); (2) steering committee review (month 3); (3) tier downgrade (the partner remains in the coalition but loses Axis 3 decision-making participation until commitments are met). Exit from the coalition (termination of data-sharing access, removal from public partner list) is a last resort, requiring two-thirds steering committee vote.

### The Interest-Based Negotiation Framework

When conflicts reach the steering committee, the resolution process follows the interest-based negotiation model documented in coalition management research: [6]

1. **Frame the conflict as a shared challenge**, not a zero-sum competition. "We both need the coalition to be effective; this conflict is reducing its effectiveness" is more productive than "your public statement undermined our campaign."

2. **Identify the underlying interests** behind each party's position. A labor union's resistance to a specific climate policy is usually about job security in fossil fuel industries, not opposition to environmental protection. Identifying the specific economic interest allows targeted resolution (just transition provisions, geographic specificity about affected communities) rather than comprehensive compromise.

3. **Generate multiple settlement options** before evaluating them. Scope separation (each party keeps independent authority on the specific issue), joint advocacy (both parties sign a joint statement on shared priorities while maintaining independent positions on contested issues), and timeline separation (one campaign proceeds now; the other proceeds after specific conditions are met) are all viable options in different conflict types.

4. **Evaluate options against coalition mission** rather than against each party's preferred outcome. The shared evaluation standard — does this option enable the coalition to achieve democratic renewal objectives more effectively? — shifts the frame from competitive bargaining to collaborative problem-solving.

### Case Study: M4BL Accountability Lessons

The Movement for Black Lives coalition provides important negative evidence on accountability structures. By 2025, demands for greater transparency and independent audits of M4BL's resource flows had intensified among stakeholders, with disclosures remaining limited to sporadic reports that did not include granular spending breakdowns. [7]

The accountability failure emerged from a governance design that prioritized coalition cohesion over accountability infrastructure — specifically, the absence of independent audit mechanisms, the concentration of fiscal sponsorship in a single organization (Borealis), and the absence of standardized reporting requirements for coalition members.

The lesson for Phase 1 coalition design: accountability mechanisms must be built into the governance structure at founding, not retrofitted after conflicts emerge. Specifically:
- Independent financial auditor review of overhead pool funds annually
- Standardized commitment tracking for all partner organizations
- Public (within coalition) reporting on each partner's coordination contributions
- Clear tier downgrade procedures for non-fulfillment, with graduated escalation

### The "No Tyranny of Majority" Design Principle

The most common critique of coalition governance is that it allows well-resourced, well-organized institutional partners to dominate decision-making even when they nominally represent a minority position. The NAACP's 115th National Convention resolution process demonstrates this: resolutions are formally passed by delegates representing member units, but the organizational infrastructure advantages of national staff are substantial. [8]

Three mechanisms prevent this in the Phase 1 coalition structure:

**Equal representation on the steering committee**: Each of the four category tiers (litigation anchors, research partners, advocacy and organizing partners, civic infrastructure partners) elects equal representation to the steering committee, regardless of budget size. A community newspaper and the ACLU have equal steering committee votes. Budget-based representation is explicitly prohibited.

**Opt-out without exit**: Partners can decline participation in specific decisions without losing coalition membership. This means smaller or less well-resourced partners can block specific decisions that substantially harm their interests without having to leave the coalition to do so.

**Domain working group autonomy**: Decisions within domains are made by domain-specific partners. The ACLU does not get votes in the labor domain working group; the AFL-CIO does not get votes in the voting rights litigation working group. Scale advantages are domain-specific, not coalition-wide.

## Part IV: Resource-Sharing Models

### The Three Resource-Sharing Structures

**Structure 1 — Overhead Pool Funding (Shared Infrastructure)**

An overhead pool is a dedicated fund, contributed to by all coalition partners at a fixed percentage of coordination-related budget, that pays for:
- Coalition coordination staff (2-3 FTE for a 150+ partner coalition)
- Shared research repository and distribution infrastructure
- Legal services coordination (not litigation — that's a Category A function)
- Messaging working group operations
- Annual all-coalition convening

The contribution formula: each partner contributes a percentage of the resources they receive from the coalition (grants facilitated, research accessed, communications infrastructure used) rather than a flat fee. This creates proportional contribution without excluding smaller organizations that benefit from coalition membership but cannot pay flat fees.

The Civil Marriage Collaborative operated a version of this through Proteus Fund: pooled funder resources administered by a neutral intermediary, with shared research and communications infrastructure available to all state campaign coalitions. The CMC awarded approximately $2M annually in shared infrastructure support. [3]

**Structure 2 — Lead-Organization Funding (Domain-Specific)**

Within each domain, a lead organization coordinates domain-specific work and serves as the primary fundraising contact for domain-specific foundations. The Brennan Center leads the voting rights and judicial reform domain (Domain 1 and Domain 6), approaching election law funders (Democracy Fund, MacArthur) as the domain coordinator. SiX leads the state legislative coordination domain. The AFL-CIO leads the labor rights domain (Domain 17). The NAACP LDF leads the racial justice litigation domain.

This prevents the funding competition (Type 1 conflict) by giving each domain a designated lead with primary foundation relationships. Other partners within the domain receive funding through the lead or maintain independent funding relationships for complementary work that does not duplicate the lead's primary function.

**Structure 3 — Federated Funding (Local Organization Self-Sufficiency)**

Local organizations (Category D civic infrastructure partners) fund their own operations through local foundations, community fundraising, and government grants. The coalition provides them with research, messaging templates, and coordination access — but does not fund their operations. This is the federated model's key feature: local organizations maintain independent viability and are not dependent on the national coalition for survival.

The Alliance for Youth Action's model demonstrates the sustainability of this structure: 21 affiliates across 19 states, each with independent funding and operations, united by shared federation infrastructure that the national Alliance funds. [9] The national Alliance's coordination function is relatively lean (~$8M annually); the aggregate organizational capacity of the 21 affiliates far exceeds what a centrally funded hierarchy could achieve at that budget level.

### The Marriage Equality Funding Precedent

Freedom to Marry's 2014 investment pattern — $2.4M across 24 states, averaging $100K per state — provides a direct benchmark for Phase 2 organizational infrastructure in the state tier. [10] At 2026 cost levels, a comparable multi-state infrastructure investment would require $150-200K per state for operational capacity (accounting for inflation and higher coordination complexity in democratic renewal vs. single-issue marriage equality campaigns).

For a 10-state priority tier in Phase 2, this implies $1.5-2M in direct state infrastructure investment beyond the overhead pool. This is consistent with the Phase 2 organizational budget in the resource analysis (Document 3).

The Marriage Equality model also demonstrates the division between shared infrastructure (provided by Freedom to Marry at no direct cost to state campaigns — research, messaging, polling) and campaign-specific funding (raised independently by each state campaign). Replicating this division in the Phase 1 coalition reduces the pressure on the overhead pool by clarifying what the coalition funds vs. what individual partners fund.

### The In-Kind Contribution Framework

The resource-sharing analysis underestimates the value of in-kind contributions from labor unions and faith coalitions — organizations with infrastructure that cannot easily be purchased:

**Labor union in-kind contributions:**
- Phone banking and door-knocking capacity (SEIU, UAW, AFSCME, UFCW have millions of trained volunteer-hours annually)
- Local political relationships in competitive districts (union political operations have decades of relationship investment)
- Communications infrastructure (union publications, email lists, social media reach)
- Training facilities and meeting space
- Research capacity (AFL-CIO research department is one of the most sophisticated advocacy research operations in the country)

At market rates, the value of labor union in-kind contributions to a coordinated democratic renewal campaign could reach $50-100M annually across all major unions — far exceeding any philanthropic commitment to the same work.

The coordination challenge is that labor union in-kind contributions flow through internal political priority processes, not through coalition direction. The framework for capturing in-kind value is alignment: ensuring that the coalition's 2026 priorities are consistent with labor's 2026 political priorities, so that labor's existing investments contribute to shared goals without requiring additional allocation.

**Faith coalition in-kind contributions:**
- Physical space (churches, synagogues, mosques provide meeting space in every congressional district)
- Trusted messenger networks (faith leaders reach communities that political organizations cannot)
- Non-partisan credibility (faith coalitions can speak to conservative and liberal audiences simultaneously)
- Civic education infrastructure (weekly services provide regular civic education touchpoints that no other institution can match)

The National Council of Churches and its member denominations, plus the Islamic Society of North America, the Union for Reform Judaism, and parallel organizations represent civic capacity that is rarely integrated into progressive democratic renewal work — partly because of historical tensions between faith communities and specific policy positions (reproductive rights, LGBTQ rights) that domain-based entry points can navigate.

## Part V: The Accountability Infrastructure

### Commitment Tracking System

Each coalition partner specifies at joining:
1. **Resource contribution**: What research, communications, or organizational capacity they commit to share with the coalition (specified quarterly)
2. **Coordination commitment**: Attendance at domain working groups and steering committee meetings (specified in tier terms)
3. **Alignment commitment**: Notification of the messaging working group within 72 hours of major public commitments that affect coalition-relevant domains

These commitments are tracked on a shared internal platform (private to coalition partners, not public). Quarterly review by coordination staff; annual review by steering committee.

**No commitment to ideological agreement.** Partners do not commit to specific policy positions. They commit to process: sharing research, attending coordination meetings, notifying on major public actions. This distinction is essential — requiring ideological agreement produces the lowest-common-denominator outcome we are specifically trying to avoid.

### The Public Partner Compact

Each coalition partner signs a one-page public compact that specifies:
- What the coalition is (a coordination infrastructure for democratic renewal, not a unified advocacy organization)
- What each partner commits to (process commitments, not policy commitments)
- What the coalition commits to each partner (research access, coordination support, shared communications infrastructure, overhead pool contributions)
- What the exit process is (30-day notice, return of shared resources, public notification to coalition members)

This compact is public — available on the coalition's website — which creates accountability to the broader public for the coalition's structure and membership.

### Handling Bad-Faith Actors

The accountability system must address the possibility that specific coalition partners will use coalition membership to extract information, credentials, or relationships while contributing nothing or actively undermining coalition work. This is not hypothetical: opposition research operations in democratic reform movements have a history of using coalition membership for intelligence gathering.

Indicators of bad-faith participation:
- Consistent non-attendance at domain working groups without delegates
- No research sharing after multiple quarters
- Public statements that explicitly contradict coalition messaging without prior notification
- Use of coalition research in funding applications without attribution or overhead pool contribution

The steering committee has authority to investigate and, on two-thirds vote, to suspend a partner's data access pending investigation or to terminate membership. This authority must exist in the governance documentation from founding — retrofitting accountability mechanisms after a bad-faith actor is identified is far less effective.

## Summary: The Coalition Design Principles

The following principles, taken together, constitute the governance architecture for a 150+ partner coalition that avoids lowest-common-denominator outcomes while maintaining the breadth that gives Phase 1 its political reach:

1. **Differentiated entry points**: Partners join through their highest-priority domain, not through comprehensive agenda agreement.

2. **Three-axis decision authority**: Federal-priority decisions are made by federal-tier partners; state-priority decisions are made by state-tier partners; shared-priority decisions require steering committee supermajority.

3. **Opt-out without exit**: Partners can decline specific decisions while maintaining coalition membership.

4. **Domain working group autonomy**: Decisions within domains are made by domain experts, not by coalition-wide vote.

5. **Overhead pool plus federated funding**: Shared infrastructure is funded collectively; operational funding is domain-specific or locally self-sufficient.

6. **Process commitment, not ideological commitment**: Partners commit to coordination processes, not policy positions.

7. **Graduated accountability**: Non-fulfillment triggers private inquiry, then steering committee review, then tier downgrade — not immediate expulsion.

8. **Equal steering committee representation across tiers**: Budget size does not determine governance influence.

9. **Explicit priority stack**: When cross-domain decisions must be made, the priority ordering is pre-agreed and written into governance documents.

10. **Public partner compact**: Coalition membership terms are public, creating external accountability for the governance structure itself.
