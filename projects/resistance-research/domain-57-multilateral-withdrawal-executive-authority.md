# Domain 57: Multilateral Withdrawal and Executive Unilateralism

**The United States has, since January 2025, executed the largest single withdrawal from international institutions in its modern democratic history. The democratic accountability argument is not primarily geopolitical — it is constitutional. International treaty obligations, ratified with Senate consent, function as constraints on executive discretion. When the executive unilaterally withdraws from them — circumventing the Senate ratification requirement and bypassing congressional authorization — it simultaneously expands presidential power and removes the accountability structures that international institutions provide. This is not foreign policy debate. It is structural democratic design failure in real time.**

---

## Section 1: The Withdrawal Mechanisms — Constitutional Framework and Legal Architecture

### The Treaty Clause and Its Circumvention

The US Constitution's Treaty Clause (Article II, Section 2) establishes the foundational democratic constraint on executive international engagement: the President may make treaties, but only "by and with the Advice and Consent of the Senate," with two-thirds of Senators concurring.[1] Once ratified, treaties become the supreme law of the land under the Supremacy Clause (Article VI).[2] The consent requirement is not procedural formality — it is the constitutional mechanism through which international commitments are given democratic legitimacy and insulated from purely presidential reversal.

The Constitution is silent, however, on treaty *withdrawal*. This silence has been exploited across presidencies but most dramatically under the second Trump administration. The Department of Justice Office of Legal Counsel issued a 2020 opinion claiming the President holds exclusive constitutional authority over treaty withdrawal, insulating executive exit decisions from congressional constraint.[3] Congress has never formally accepted this claim — and in the case of NATO, has explicitly rejected it through statute. But no court has authoritatively resolved the constitutional question.

The practical result is a constitutional gap that functions as executive power leverage. Presidents can ratify treaties only with Senate supermajority consent, but — absent specific statutory prohibition — can exit them with a signature on an executive order. The asymmetry is structurally dangerous: the democratic threshold for entering binding international obligations is high, but the executive threshold for exiting them approaches zero.

### The Youngstown Framework Applied to Treaty Withdrawal

Justice Jackson's concurrence in *Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co. v. Sawyer* (1952) establishes the standard analytical framework for assessing executive action against congressional will.[4] The framework has three zones:

- **Zone 1**: President acts pursuant to express or implied congressional authorization — power at maximum.
- **Zone 2**: Congress has been silent — the "zone of twilight" where the distribution of power depends on "imponderables."
- **Zone 3**: President acts contrary to expressed or implied congressional will — power "at its lowest ebb," sustainable only if the President holds exclusive constitutional authority.

For the Trump administration's unilateral exit from 66 international organizations — including entities established by Senate-ratified treaties — this framework has sharp implications. Where Congress has explicitly prohibited withdrawal (as with NATO through NDAA 2024 Section 1250A), presidential action falls into Zone 3: it can survive judicial review only if treaty withdrawal is an exclusively executive constitutional power.[5] The Congressional Research Service's definitive February 2026 analysis (CRS Report R48868) concludes that no court has established such exclusivity, and that the constitutional text — which makes treaty *entry* a shared power — provides strong textual grounds to question unilateral executive exclusivity over treaty exit.[6]

### Goldwater v. Carter and the Justiciability Gap

The Supreme Court's fractured 1979 decision in *Goldwater v. Carter* — where Senator Barry Goldwater challenged President Carter's unilateral termination of the Mutual Defense Treaty with Taiwan — declined to reach the merits, with plurality justices finding a nonjusticiable political question and others finding it unripe.[7] The decision left the constitutional question unresolved. For over 45 years, this gap has enabled executive unilateralism in treaty withdrawal by removing judicial accountability.

The CRS R48868 analysis indicates this calculus may have shifted post-*Goldwater*: recent Supreme Court jurisprudence, particularly *Zivotofsky v. Kerry* (2015), suggests that where a President acts contrary to a statute on the ground that it interferes with Article II authority, the constitutional question is one properly resolved by the judiciary, not left to the political branches.[6] This creates the doctrinal foundation for judicial enforcement of Section 1250A — if Congress provides standing and if a President actually invokes Article 13 of the North Atlantic Treaty.

### Executive Agreements vs. Senate-Ratified Treaties: The Two-Tier Problem

Not all international commitments are Senate-ratified treaties. Many of the 66 organizations from which the US withdrew in January 2026 were joined through executive agreements — international arrangements concluded by the executive alone, without Senate consent — or through statutory authorization.[8] Executive agreements are legally easier to exit because the President's authority to withdraw from executive agreements tracks the authority under which they were made.

The Paris Agreement exemplifies this two-tier problem. The Obama administration treated the Paris Agreement as an executive agreement rather than a Senate-ratified treaty — a structural choice that reduced the Senate supermajority consent barrier at entry, but also lowered the barrier to executive exit. The *American Journal of International Law* analysis from April 2025 documented that the Trump administration treated the Paris withdrawal as "immediately effective" despite the Agreement's own 1-year notice provision — demonstrating executive disregard for even the procedural constraints within executive agreements.[9]

The UNFCCC (UN Framework Convention on Climate Change), by contrast, *is* a Senate-ratified treaty. The Trump administration's assertion of authority to exit the UNFCCC without congressional authorization is a more significant constitutional claim — and one that congressional researchers, including CRS, flag as unresolved.[10] The distinction between executive agreements and ratified treaties matters enormously for which withdrawals are constitutionally contestable and which face higher democratic defense.

## Section 2: The 2025-2026 Case Studies — Scope and Democratic Harm

### 2.1 The January 2026 Mass Withdrawal: 66 Organizations

On January 7, 2026, President Trump signed a presidential memorandum directing withdrawal from 66 international organizations — the largest single-day institutional exit in any democracy's modern history. The memorandum covered 35 non-UN organizations and 31 UN entities, including climate bodies, economic development institutions, human rights mechanisms, and democracy-support organizations.[11]

The administration's stated rationale — that these institutions were "redundant in their scope, mismanaged, unnecessary, wasteful, poorly run, captured by the interests of actors advancing their own agendas contrary to our own, or a threat to our nation's sovereignty" — reflects the ideological underpinning of the withdrawal project: that multilateral accountability is a threat to sovereignty rather than an exercise of collective democratic governance.[12]

The democratic harm from this mass withdrawal operates at three levels. First, direct accountability loss: institutions like International IDEA (the International Institute for Democracy and Electoral Assistance) provide monitoring of electoral systems, technical assistance to democracies, and comparative data on democratic health. The US withdrawal from International IDEA — the intergovernmental body dedicated to democratic monitoring globally — at the precise moment when US electoral integrity is under documented stress, is the most symbolically resonant of the 66 withdrawals.[13] Second, information environment degradation: international monitoring generates public information about domestic practices that civil society, media, and courts use domestically. Third, normative contagion: US withdrawal licenses other states' withdrawal, eroding the universalist premise on which human rights monitoring depends.

### 2.2 NATO Withdrawal Threat: War Powers and Collective Defense Constraints

The Trump administration has repeatedly threatened NATO withdrawal without formally executing it — deploying withdrawal threats as leverage instruments against allied criticism of unilateral military action. The April 2026 *Time* report that Trump was "strongly considering" NATO exit following allied criticism of the Iran war is the most direct evidence that withdrawal threats are being instrumentalized as a punitive mechanism against allied democratic oversight.[14]

The constitutional architecture has become concrete: NDAA 2024 Section 1250A is the first statute in US history to explicitly prohibit presidential unilateral withdrawal from a ratified treaty.[5] It provides that the President "shall not suspend, terminate, denounce, or withdraw the United States from the North Atlantic Treaty" without Senate advice and consent (two-thirds majority) or an Act of Congress. The 119th Congress also saw introduction of the NATO Edge Act (H.R. 2010) and standalone NATO Act (S. 2174) — additional legislative efforts to close the constitutional gap.[15][16]

The democratic accountability harm from NATO withdrawal would not be primarily military — it would be structural. NATO's Article 5 collective defense obligation creates a consultation requirement before military action. This consultation process functions as a deliberative constraint on unilateral presidential war-making: it requires allied engagement, imposes political costs for unilateral action, and embeds US military decisions in a network of democratic allies. Withdrawal would eliminate this constraint, leaving the War Powers Resolution (1973) as the only remaining institutional check on presidential military action — and that statute's history demonstrates its weakness as a binding constraint on determined executives.

The Lawfare analysis of Section 1250A's enforceability identifies a critical gap: the original Senate version of the legislation included litigation authorization for Congress, but this was removed during the House-Senate conference. Without standing authorization, no party has clear legal capacity to challenge a presidential withdrawal in violation of Section 1250A.[17] Congress has protected the treaty through statute but not provided the enforcement mechanism to make the protection justiciable.

### 2.3 ICC Sanctions: International Accountability Under Siege

Executive Order 14203 (February 6, 2025) represents a qualitatively different species of institutional aggression: not withdrawal from a court, but financial sanctions against its judges and prosecutors.[18] By December 2025, 11 ICC officials had been designated under the order, which invokes IEEPA to declare the ICC's investigative activities an "unusual and extraordinary threat to national security."

The real-world impact has been documented. Microsoft cut off Chief Prosecutor Karim Khan's email service after the sanctions designation. Bank accounts were frozen. American lawyers trying to prosecute international crimes through ICC mechanisms found sanctions preventing them from performing their work — an unprecedented situation in which US sanctions actively suppressed a US citizen's ability to engage in international legal accountability work.[19]

PBS NewsHour reporting confirmed the sanctions are "taking a toll" on ICC operations: "The sanctions include asset freezes, travel bans, and restrictions on services from US [entities], and providing advice or services to designated individuals includes things like evidence or submission of amicus briefs."[20] The Brookings Institution analysis concluded: "ICC sanctions will hurt, not help, the United States" — because the US relies on international accountability mechanisms for its own strategic interests, including prosecution of actors whose crimes affect US personnel and allies.[21]

Hungary's April 2025 announcement of ICC withdrawal — citing political bias, immediately following Orbán's invitation to Netanyahu during an active ICC warrant — illustrates the normative contagion effect. The US sanctions preceded and effectively licensed Hungary's withdrawal. Democratic states contemplating compliance face a US government that actively suppresses the court's function; authoritarian-leaning states have been provided explicit permission to exit.[22]

### 2.4 UN Human Rights Mechanisms: The UPR Withdrawal and Domestic Information Loss

The Trump administration executed a two-stage withdrawal from UN human rights accountability. First, in February 2025, withdrawal from the UN Human Rights Council and UNRWA, with a review order for UNESCO.[23] Second — and more significant for domestic democratic accountability — the August 2025 withdrawal from the Universal Periodic Review (UPR), making the US the first UN member state to refuse to participate in its own UPR cycle in the history of the mechanism.[24]

The UPR matters for domestic democratic accountability in a way that is underappreciated in standard foreign policy analysis. Civil society organizations — ACLU, Human Rights Watch, NAACP — have used the UPR process to generate international documentation of domestic practices: racial injustice, Indigenous rights violations, immigration detention conditions. This documentation creates a public information layer that courts, legislative advocates, and journalists use domestically. The UPR process was, in effect, a subsidized civil society accountability mechanism.

The EJIL: Talk! analysis of the UPR withdrawal documented its dual harm: "The UPR's legitimacy depends on universal participation. Every state that undergoes review reinforces the norm of shared accountability; every withdrawal weakens it. When a leading democracy like the US refuses to appear before the Human Rights Council, other governments, particularly authoritarian ones, gain an easy excuse to follow."[25] The domestic harm is the information environment: civil society organizations lose the international documentation mechanism, and government agencies are relieved of the structured accountability obligation to document their own practices.

### 2.5 WTO and Trade Law: Rules-Based Order vs. Power-Based Enforcement

The Trump administration's approach to the WTO represents functional withdrawal without formal exit: suspension of financial contributions (March 2025), invocation of IEEPA to impose sweeping tariffs that violated WTO tariff bindings under international trade law, and the USTR's explicit rejection of WTO dispute resolution in favor of unilateral enforcement.[26]

The February 20, 2026 Supreme Court decision in *Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump* held — in a 6-3 ruling — that IEEPA does not authorize the president to impose tariffs, grounding the decision in Article I's vesting of the tariff power exclusively in Congress.[27] This ruling is significant for Domain 57's democratic accountability argument: the Court's reasoning directly mirrors the treaty withdrawal analysis. Just as tariff authority is a core congressional power that cannot be seized by executive emergency declaration, treaty obligations ratified with Senate consent cannot be unilaterally exited by executive memo. Both involve the same structural danger: using emergency or unilateral authority to bypass the deliberative democratic processes Congress and the Senate represent.

The WTO's institutional crisis predates the second Trump term but the administration's posture has accelerated it. The number of WTO dispute settlement cases has fallen to approximately one-third of pre-Appellate Body collapse levels — a direct consequence of WTO members concluding the dispute settlement system can no longer provide enforceable rules-based resolution.[28] When the world's largest economy withdraws from rules-based trade enforcement, it signals to all trading partners that power-based unilateralism is the operative norm — a normative shift that advantages large economies over small ones and authoritarian states over democratic ones.

### 2.6 Refugee and Asylum Regime: Non-Refoulement and the Senate-Ratified Obligation

Executive Order 14163 suspended the US Refugee Admissions Program indefinitely upon signing on January 20, 2025. The 9th Circuit upheld the suspension in a 2026 ruling, finding that Congress had provided the President with a "sweeping grant of power" over refugee admissions.[29] By March 2026, USCIS had partially lifted the adjudicative hold for asylum seekers from non-travel-ban countries, while the freeze remained for nationals of 39 designated "high-risk" countries.[30]

The constitutional dimension is precise: the 1951 Refugee Convention and its 1967 Protocol, to which the US is a party through Senate ratification, establish the principle of non-refoulement — the prohibition on returning a refugee to persecution.[31] The domestic codification of asylum rights in 8 U.S.C. § 1158 derives from this Senate-ratified treaty obligation. When the executive suspends asylum processing, it acts against the grain of a Senate-ratified obligation — precisely the pattern the Treaty Clause was designed to prevent.

The *American Journal of International Law* published a 2025-2026 analysis documenting "The Trump Administration's Unprecedented Violations of the Non-Refoulement Principle," noting that the border closure regime resulted in zero asylum seekers being given the opportunity to access the asylum system during certain months — a functional nullification of a treaty obligation without Senate consent.[32]

IRAP litigation in *Pacito v. Trump* produced a compliance order requiring the government to process and provide resettlement support to refugees who were conditionally approved before January 20, 2025.[33] But the 9th Circuit's ultimate ruling on the USRAP suspension illustrates the enforcement gap: even where international treaty obligations are clear, the executive's "sweeping grant" of authority over admissions creates a domestic statutory framework that can functionally override treaty-level obligations without the formal treaty withdrawal process.

## Section 3: Democratic Design Gaps — Where the Constitutional Architecture Failed

### 3.1 The Asymmetric Consent Problem

The most fundamental democratic design gap in the international agreements architecture is asymmetry: entry into Senate-ratified treaties requires a two-thirds supermajority, but exit — absent specific statutory prohibition — can be accomplished by executive order alone. This asymmetry was not intentional constitutional design; the Framers simply did not address treaty withdrawal. But its practical effect is a ratchet: democratic majorities can constrain executive action through treaty entry, but a determined executive can reverse those constraints unilaterally.

The OLC 2020 opinion's claim of exclusive executive treaty withdrawal authority compounds the design gap. If the OLC claim is accepted — that Congress cannot constrain presidential treaty withdrawal by statute — then the two-thirds entry threshold provides only temporary democratic constraint: it can be bypassed as soon as a new executive decides to exit. International commitments become, in effect, purely executive agreements in practice, regardless of how they were constituted. The Senate's consent becomes a procedural hurdle on entry with no binding effect on exit.

### 3.2 The Standing Problem: Laws Without Enforcement

Congress has responded to the withdrawal crisis by passing protective legislation — most significantly, NDAA 2024 Section 1250A prohibiting presidential NATO withdrawal. But the Lawfare analysis identified the critical implementation gap: Congress enacted protection without enforcement standing.[17] Without a statutory grant authorizing Congress or individual members to file suit challenging a Section 1250A violation, no party has clear legal standing to bring judicial challenge.

This pattern — protective statutes without enforcement mechanisms — is a recurring democratic design failure in the executive oversight architecture. The War Powers Resolution (1973) prohibits unauthorized military action but lacks effective enforcement standing. Section 1250A prohibits NATO withdrawal but lacks litigation authorization. The result is a pattern where Congress expresses democratic will through statute, the executive asserts exclusive constitutional authority in defiance of that will, and no court can reach the merits because no party has standing to force the issue.

### 3.3 The Executive Agreement Loophole

The Obama administration's decision to treat the Paris Agreement as an executive agreement rather than a Senate-ratified treaty was made precisely to avoid the Senate supermajority barrier. This was a calculated political trade-off: lower the entry barrier to enable participation, but accept lower stability. The Trump administration's easy exit from the Paris Agreement — twice — demonstrates the vulnerability of international commitments made as executive agreements.

The structural problem is that the executive agreement framework — developed over the 20th century to manage the practical reality that Senate two-thirds supermajority requirements block most international cooperation — has created a two-tier system. Senate-ratified treaties carry constitutional dignity and are harder to exit. Executive agreements are more flexible but also more vulnerable to unilateral reversal. The proliferation of executive agreements over ratified treaties has, paradoxically, *reduced* the democratic accountability of international commitments by making them easier to exit without democratic deliberation.

### 3.4 Emergency Powers as a Backdoor to International Disengagement

The Trump administration's invocation of IEEPA to sanction ICC judges and prosecutors — declaring the Court an "unusual and extraordinary threat to national security" — reveals a third constitutional failure: emergency powers can be weaponized against international accountability mechanisms. The same executive branch that claims unilateral withdrawal authority also claims the authority to use emergency economic powers to suppress the functioning of international judicial bodies. The Supreme Court's February 2026 IEEPA tariff ruling restricts one application of this authority, but the underlying framework — presidential declaration of emergency justifies broad executive action with limited congressional override — remains available for novel applications.

### 3.5 Congressional Oversight Atrophy in Foreign Affairs

The deeper structural failure is congressional capacity atrophy in foreign affairs oversight. The accumulation of deferences — Goldwater v. Carter's political question dismissal, decades of acquiescence to executive agreements, the Senate's consistent failure to ratify major multilateral conventions (including the Law of the Sea, the Rome Statute, the Convention on the Rights of the Child) — has produced a foreign policy architecture in which Congress's role is largely reactive rather than constitutive. Congressional foreign policy leverage is primarily appropriations-based (the power of the purse) rather than structural (treaty consent, oversight capacity, enforcement standing). That appropriations leverage has been progressively eroded as well: the WTO financial contribution suspension was accomplished without Congressional authorization, and foreign aid redirection through executive order has reduced congressional leverage over international engagement commitments.

## Section 4: International Precedent — Hungary, Turkey, and the Limits of Institutional Constraint

### 4.1 Hungary: Institutional Capture from Within, Then Exit

Hungary under Orbán provides the most instructive recent precedent for understanding how a democratic state can systematically dismantle accountability mechanisms while remaining formally within multilateral institutions — and then exit when remaining becomes inconvenient. Orbán's government captured the Constitutional Court, restricted independent media, eliminated university independence, used emergency powers to govern by decree, and systematically weakened civil society over 16 years — all while maintaining EU membership as a financial resource.[34]

The EU's accountability mechanisms largely failed. Article 7 procedures — designed to sanction members violating EU values — require unanimous consent of other member states, and Hungary and Poland blocked each other for years. ECJ rulings against Hungary on judicial independence were defied or minimally complied with. Freedom House and International IDEA both classified Hungary as a "hybrid regime" by 2023 — no longer a full democracy despite EU membership.[35]

Hungary's April 2025 ICC withdrawal — the first state to leave the Rome Statute following its founding — illustrated the cascading exit dynamic. Orbán's invitation to Israeli Prime Minister Netanyahu during an active ICC warrant created a domestic political need to formally exit the court to prevent arrest obligations, and the US sanctions had already established the precedent that ICC withdrawal was acceptable behavior for a US-aligned state.[22]

**The critical update**: Hungary's April 2026 national election resulted in a landslide victory for Péter Magyar and the Tisza party, ending Orbán's 16-year tenure. Magyar's party won more than two-thirds of parliamentary seats — a constitutional supermajority — enabling constitutional amendment and reversal of Orbán-era structural changes.[36] The lesson from Hungary's democratic recovery is precise: institutional erosion is reversible when material conditions turn against the incumbent and when opposition maintains organizational coherence. EU financial conditionality — €36 billion frozen during Orbán's rule — contributed to the economic discontent that drove the electoral reversal. International institutions that survive internal capture pressure can eventually exert leverage; institutions the US has abandoned cannot.

### 4.2 Turkey: Alliance Membership Without Democratic Accountability

Turkey under Erdogan demonstrates that NATO alliance membership is compatible with significant democratic backsliding when alliance partners prioritize strategic utility over democratic norms. Turkey has remained in NATO despite: the imprisonment of 150,000+ people following the 2016 coup attempt (including journalists, judges, and military officers); the purchase of Russian S-400 systems incompatible with NATO standards; military operations in Syria without allied consultation; and ongoing suppression of the Kurdish political opposition.[37]

NATO has responded with expressions of concern but not expulsion or formal sanction. This demonstrates the limits of alliance accountability — but also its value. Turkey's NATO membership has created ongoing strategic leverage for allied democratic pressure that does not exist outside the alliance. The consultation processes embedded in NATO governance have periodically slowed Turkish unilateral action. The contrast between Turkey *inside* NATO and a US *outside* NATO is instructive: even imperfect institutional membership provides accountability leverage that exit forecloses permanently.

The lesson for Domain 57 cuts two ways. First, the US's failure to enforce democratic norms against Turkey within NATO demonstrates that institutional membership is not a sufficient constraint on determined authoritarian drift — the accountability mechanisms have real limits. Second, and more important: US withdrawal from multilateral institutions would simultaneously remove accountability pressure on the US (parallel to Hungary's situation) and remove US leverage over other states' democratic compliance (parallel to the Turkey problem). Both effects simultaneously reduce multilateral democratic accountability.

### 4.3 The 1930s Precedent and Its Costs

American isolationism in the 1930s was driven by post-WWI revisionism, the economic disruption of the Depression, and domestic political pressure to focus on internal concerns.[38] FDR's rejection of international cooperation at the 1933 London Economic Conference — choosing unilateral monetary policy over multilateral coordination — reinforced British appeasement and French paralysis. The State Department's historical record characterizes the period as one in which the US "ran for cover as militarism and virulent nationalism began to engulf Europe and East Asia," with US withdrawal reducing the collective capacity to constrain aggressive actors.

The parallel to 2025-2026 is not precise — the US has not formally withdrawn from NATO and the geopolitical context differs significantly. But the structural dynamic is precedent: US withdrawal from multilateral coordination reduces collective capacity to constrain aggressive actors, emboldens those actors, and leaves democratic states to navigate destabilizing pressures with reduced institutional support. The Carnegie Endowment's February 2025 analysis, "The Death of the World America Made," documented how the accumulated institutional order built since 1945 — the order that provided the structural context for post-WWII democratic consolidation in Western Europe, Japan, and South Korea — is being systematically dismantled.[39]

## Section 5: Reform Pathways — Closing the Democratic Design Gaps

### 5.1 Treaty Withdrawal Veto: Requiring Senate Consent for Exit

The most structurally significant reform is the establishment of a default Senate consent requirement for withdrawal from Senate-ratified treaties, mirroring the two-thirds consent requirement for entry. The Nebraska Law Review analysis by Broeg (2020) established the legal framework for this reform: Congress can protect a ratified treaty from presidential termination through statute expressing the will of both houses, and such statutory protection moves presidential denunciation of that treaty from Youngstown's Zone 2 "twilight zone" into Zone 3, requiring demonstration of exclusive executive authority.[40]

Section 1250A is the prototype. What it demonstrated — and what its implementation gap also demonstrates — is that the political will to pass protective legislation exists in bipartisan coalitions (Kaine-Rubio), but that legislative protection requires enforcement mechanisms to be effective. The reformative architecture requires two components: the substantive rule (executive cannot exit Senate-ratified treaties without Senate consent) and the enforcement mechanism (litigation standing for Congress or designated parties to challenge violation).

**Legislation model**: A General Treaty Withdrawal Act would establish as a statutory default that withdrawal from any Senate-ratified treaty requires either: (a) Senate advice and consent with the same two-thirds threshold as ratification; or (b) an Act of Congress passed by simple majority with 180-day advance notice. Executive agreements would require 60-day congressional notification and be subject to a concurrent resolution of disapproval. The Act would include a private right of action for members of Congress to challenge violations, or designate the Senate Legal Counsel as an authorized litigant — addressing the Section 1250A standing gap.

### 5.2 Mandatory Congressional Approval for Mass Institutional Exit

The January 2026 withdrawal from 66 organizations was accomplished through a presidential memorandum with no congressional authorization, consultation, or notification requirement. Even where specific treaties did not require Senate consent to join, the scale of mass institutional exit — affecting 66 organizations through a single executive action — represents a qualitatively different exercise of executive authority than routine treaty management.

A Mass Withdrawal Notification and Review Act would establish a tiered framework: withdrawal from any organization involving more than $50 million in annual US contributions requires 90-day congressional notification; withdrawal from any organization established by or implementing a Senate-ratified treaty requires Senate consent or Act of Congress; withdrawal affecting more than 10 organizations in a 12-month period triggers mandatory Joint Committee review. The Committee review produces a resolution of approval or disapproval — subjecting large-scale institutional exit to the democratic deliberation that withdrawal from single major treaties would require under the General Treaty Withdrawal Act.

### 5.3 Standing Authorization: Making Protective Statutes Enforceable

The gap between Section 1250A's protective intent and its enforcement capacity is the most technically tractable reform. The Lawfare analysis identified three available mechanisms, in decreasing order of robustness:

**Option 1 (strongest)**: A standalone litigation authorization statute for Congress as an institution — the Congressional Standing Authorization Act — providing that Congress, acting through the Bipartisan Legal Advisory Group of the House or the Senate Legal Counsel, has standing to challenge executive violations of any statute governing treaty withdrawal, alliance membership, or mass institutional exit. This is the standing mechanism present in the War Powers Resolution's framework but not effectively implemented.

**Option 2**: Senate standing order preauthorizing litigation challenge to presidential violations of Section 1250A and its successors. A standing order persists across future congresses absent affirmative repeal, and the Senate's inherent authority to enforce its constitutional role in treaty-making provides the institutional basis for the order. The Lawfare analysis notes this signals credible commitment to enforcement, amplifying deterrent effect even before judicial enforcement is tested.[17]

**Option 3**: Individual member standing claims under the Appropriations Clause. Section 1250A prohibits use of appropriated funds for NATO withdrawal — a provision that may give members of Congress standing to challenge funding violations under existing Appropriations Clause doctrine. This is the weakest option (most likely to face justiciability barriers) but requires no new legislation.

### 5.4 International Accountability Re-engagement: Returning to Monitored Frameworks

The UPR withdrawal, UNHRC exit, and International IDEA departure created domestic accountability deficits that are tractable to executive reversal — but require legislative protection to prevent future unilateral exit. A US recommitment to international human rights monitoring — combined with a statutory framework requiring congressional consent for future exit from human rights monitoring mechanisms — would restore the information environment and diplomatic leverage that withdrawal eliminated.

The specific reform architecture for UPR re-engagement is straightforward: US participation in the Universal Periodic Review is not a treaty obligation but a political commitment. A statute requiring congressional authorization for US withdrawal from any UN monitoring mechanism with more than five-year US participation history would prevent future unilateral exit while preserving executive discretion over initial participation decisions.

### 5.5 Emergency Powers Constraints: Closing the IEEPA-as-Weapon Loophole

The Supreme Court's February 2026 ruling in *Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump* restricts IEEPA tariff authority but leaves open the broader pattern: presidential emergency declarations as instruments to suppress international accountability mechanisms. Congress should close this gap specifically for judicial and quasi-judicial bodies. A Judicial Sanctions Prohibition Act would prohibit use of IEEPA or any emergency economic powers statute to impose financial sanctions on judges, prosecutors, or court officials of any international court or tribunal to which any current or former US ally is a party, absent a specific finding — subject to congressional review — that the target has engaged in criminal conduct against the United States.

This reform is both narrow (targeted at judicial sanctions rather than all IEEPA applications) and structurally significant: it closes the loophole that allowed EO 14203 to weaponize emergency economic authority against international judicial accountability.

### 5.6 The Executive Agreement Rationalization Problem

The most structurally difficult reform is the executive agreement framework. The proliferation of executive agreements over ratified treaties reflects genuine governance constraints — the Senate two-thirds supermajority requirement blocks participation in most major multilateral frameworks. A regime that required Senate ratification for all international commitments would be worse in democratic accountability terms than the current executive agreement system: the US would be less engaged internationally, not more democratically accountable.

The reform framework should distinguish between *types* of executive agreements by their democratic implications:

- **Trade agreements** with binding tariff and dispute resolution commitments (USMCA-type) should require majority approval of both Houses under the Congressional-Executive Agreement model — already the standard practice.
- **Human rights and monitoring mechanisms** joined as executive agreements should require 60-day congressional review with concurrent resolution of disapproval as the exit mechanism, making departure politically costly without requiring affirmative supermajority consent for participation.
- **Security arrangements** with collective defense implications should require Senate advice and consent regardless of whether they are structured as treaties or executive agreements — the functional equivalence principle applied to the most consequential category.

This graduated framework does not eliminate executive agreement flexibility but imposes democratic deliberation requirements calibrated to the significance of the commitment and the stakes of exit.

## Lead Finding Restated

The United States has executed the largest withdrawal from international institutions in its modern democratic history — 66 organizations in a single January 2026 presidential memorandum, following withdrawal from WHO, UNHRC, Paris Agreement, and ICC sanction designations in 2025. The democratic accountability harm is constitutional, not merely geopolitical: treaty obligations ratified with Senate supermajority consent are being unilaterally exited by executive order, ICC judges are being sanctioned through emergency economic powers declarations, and Congress's protective legislation lacks enforcement standing.

The Supreme Court's February 2026 *Learning Resources* ruling — striking down IEEPA tariffs as an unconstitutional seizure of Congress's taxing power — provides the doctrinal foundation for the parallel argument: just as tariff authority belongs to Congress and cannot be seized by emergency declaration, treaty obligations ratified by the Senate cannot be unilaterally exited by executive memo. The same constitutional logic applies. The reform architecture to close the democratic design gap exists: treaty withdrawal veto legislation, mass institutional exit review requirements, litigation standing authorization, and emergency powers constraints for judicial sanctions. The political will to implement it requires the same bipartisan coalition that passed Section 1250A — a coalition that demonstrably existed and demonstrably lacked enforcement teeth.

Hungary's April 2026 electoral reversal of 16 years of authoritarian drift — accomplished through democratic elections once economic conditions created sufficient voter discontent — establishes the empirical case that even well-entrenched institutional erosion is reversible when international accountability mechanisms survive long enough to maintain pressure and when democratic opposition maintains organizational coherence. The institutions the US abandons cannot perform that function. The institutions it remains in — however imperfectly — preserve the conditions for democratic reversal.
