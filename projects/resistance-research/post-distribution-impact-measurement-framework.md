# Post-Distribution Impact Measurement Framework

**April 30, 2026**

This document synthesizes and extends the measurement infrastructure across the three distribution paths (A, A+37, B) into a unified, operationally focused framework. It does not duplicate the detailed decision trees in `post-distribution-tracking.md` or the monthly iteration protocol in `measurement-and-iteration-framework.md`. It addresses the four specific questions left underspecified in those documents: how each sector actually discovers and adopts policy frameworks, what tools reliably detect impact at scale, which domains get cited versus implemented versus adapted, and what misapplication looks like before it compacts into a persistent misreading.

---

## Part I: Institutional Adoption Pathways — Natural Diffusion by Sector

Institutional adoption does not follow a uniform diffusion curve. Each sector has a distinct discovery mechanism, a characteristic decision bottleneck, and a predictable first-use pattern. Designing measurement that does not account for these differences will produce false negatives at the 30- and 60-day marks — silence that looks like rejection but is actually the sector's normal processing lag.

### 1. State Attorneys General — Coalition-First Discovery

**How they find new frameworks**: AGs do not discover research through general distribution channels. They discover it through three pathways: (a) peer AG referral within the Democratic AG Association or NAAG coordination calls; (b) Tier 1 civil society partners who translate research into litigation briefs — Protect Democracy, Democracy Docket, and the Brennan Center all function as informal AG research desks; (c) the national media cycle when a specific AG lawsuit generates press coverage that is cross-referenced back to analytical work.

**Natural adoption sequence**: AG policy counsel screens for litigation utility first. A framework enters an AG's working files when it provides one of three things: a novel legal theory, a factual record the AG cannot easily recreate internally, or a cross-jurisdictional precedent map. The 35-domain framework satisfies all three for Domains 1, 6, 16, 23, and 29. Domains 17, 27, and 33 are slower to clear the litigation utility threshold.

**Decision bottleneck**: AG offices have institutional cultures that distrust unsolicited external research. The adoption catalyst is almost always a warm referral from a trusted institutional partner — not a cold email. This means the measurement framework's bridge node cascade (Brennan Center to AG staff via personal contact) is not supplementary to AG adoption: it is the primary mechanism.

**First-adoption signal**: Not a citation. AGs rarely cite policy documents in public filings; they cite cases and statutes. The first adoption signal is structural alignment — an AG press release or coalition statement that uses the domain's analytical framework without attribution, or direct contact from AG policy counsel requesting materials. The Domains 1 and 6 coalition letters are the most likely first-appearance vehicle.

**Expected timeline**: 4-8 weeks to first confirmed private use; 8-16 weeks to public signal; 6-12 months to documented litigation impact.

---

### 2. Law Schools and Legal Clinics — Evidence-Standard-First Discovery

**How they find new frameworks**: Law school clinics discover through two primary routes: faculty-to-faculty referral within scholarly networks, and the publication cascade — when Just Security, Lawfare, or a law review's online companion publishes a piece citing the framework, clinics see it in their standard reading. The academic discovery rate accelerates sharply once the first peer-reviewed or law-review citation exists; that citation functions as a credibility gate.

**Natural adoption sequence**: Clinical professors assess utility for pending active casework first. A framework earns clinical adoption when it provides comparative constitutional law evidence (the comparative law brief format in `institutional-adoption-playbooks.md`) that can be cited in active motions. Law review editors assess for scholarly contribution: novelty of analytical framework, quality of citation apparatus, and fit with current jurisprudential debates.

**Decision bottleneck**: The peer review gate. Academic institutions require one published scholarly endorsement before they will formally assign or use external research. This means the fastest path to law school adoption is a Just Security or Lawfare piece (publishing within 48-96 hours), not a direct outreach to law school deans.

**First-adoption signal**: A clinical director requests a domain-specific brief for pending casework. This signals Level 3 (operational) adoption is in process, not merely interest.

**Expected timeline**: 6-10 weeks to first clinical use; 4-8 months to first law review article citation; 12-18 months to published law review note.

---

### 3. Think Tanks and Policy Institutes — Editorial-Calendar-Driven Discovery

**How they find new frameworks**: Think tank fellows receive hundreds of documents monthly. Discovery is almost entirely relationship-mediated — a trusted colleague's recommendation, an editor's assignment, or a request for comment from a congressional staffer who has already seen the framework. The policy research ecosystem is smaller and more networked than it appears from outside; if a Brennan Center fellow mentions the framework in an internal meeting, it can reach a CAP fellow within days through their existing working relationships.

**Natural adoption sequence**: Think tanks do not adopt frameworks — they consume evidence and produce their own analysis that cites it. The adoption question for think tanks is whether the framework provides a novel analytical lens or evidence base that their next publication will cite. The process is: a fellow is assigned to write a new issue brief → they search for supporting research → the framework appears in search results or was recommended → they assess whether it meets their publication's evidentiary standard → they cite it or they don't.

**Decision bottleneck**: Publication cycle timing. A fellow who discovers the framework in May but whose next relevant publication is scheduled for August will not produce a visible citation for three months. This is not adoption failure — it is calendar lag. The measurement framework must not interpret 90-day silence from a known Tier 1 think tank contact as rejection.

**First-adoption signal**: A substantive methodology question from a fellow ("what is the primary source for the institutional autonomy score data in Domain 27?"). This signals active evidence verification, meaning the fellow is preparing to cite the work.

**Expected timeline**: 6-12 weeks to first cited publication; 3-6 months to institutional integration (when the framework appears in multiple publications from the same institution).

---

### 4. Civil Rights and Advocacy Organizations — Current-Campaign-First Discovery

**How they find new frameworks**: Advocacy organizations discover through staff recommendation within issue networks, advocacy listservs, and coalition meeting summaries. The discovery trigger is almost always a connection to a current campaign priority: a voter rights organizer sees the Domain 1 SAVE Act analysis referenced in a Democracy Docket newsletter and shares it within their organization.

**Natural adoption sequence**: Campaign directors assess whether the framework advances their current fight. This is not a broad analytical assessment — it is a narrow question: "Does this help us with the thing we are working on right now?" A 35-domain framework only converts into organizational use when the staff member who receives it has time to identify which domain is their entry point. The adoption facilitation sequence (Step 1 in Part V of `measurement-and-iteration-framework.md`) — domain triage before asking for broad engagement — is essential for this sector.

**Decision bottleneck**: Staff capacity. Civil rights organizations are perpetually at capacity. A framework that requires a staff member to read 200 pages to identify their entry point will not be read. The single highest-leverage improvement for advocacy sector adoption is producing "current threat briefs" — 2-4 page extracts from a single domain directly tied to the organization's named current case.

**First-adoption signal**: A coalition incorporates domain language into a public statement or training material, with or without attribution.

**Expected timeline**: 4-8 weeks to internal use in strategy documents; 8-16 weeks to public signal; 6-12 months to operational integration in member communications.

---

### 5. Corporate Boards and ESG-Oriented Institutions — Lowest-Priority but Worth Designing For

**How they find new frameworks**: Corporate governance teams and ESG-oriented institutional investors discover policy research through three channels: proxy advisory firms (ISS, Glass Lewis) that cite research in proxy season recommendations; general counsel networks that surface regulatory risk analysis; and peer shareholder referral. In April 2026, the corporate governance environment is politicized — ESG frameworks that were standard two years ago are now contested. This constrains direct adoption.

**Natural adoption pathway**: The framework's strongest corporate relevance is institutional resilience and rule-of-law stability — the argument that democratic backsliding creates systematic business risk (unpredictable regulatory environment, contract enforcement uncertainty, supply chain concentration risk from geopolitical instability). Domain 34 (Congressional Power of the Purse) and Domain 6 (Judicial Independence) have the most direct corporate governance relevance, framed not as advocacy but as risk analysis.

**Adoption constraint**: In 2026's environment, any corporate actor explicitly citing a "democratic renewal" framework faces political risk in ways that were not true in 2022. Adoption is more likely to be quiet and internal than cited and public. Measure through proxy season research citations rather than direct attribution.

**First-adoption signal**: An institutional investor's proxy season white paper cites the framework's judicial independence analysis as a governance risk factor.

**Expected timeline**: 12-18 months if adoption occurs at all; most likely as a secondary citation within a larger risk framework.

## Part II: Impact Metrics Framework — Tools and Methodology

### Citation Tracking Stack

The primary tools for tracking framework citations across all sectors, with honest assessments of their coverage gaps:

**Google Scholar** (free, 1-3 month lag on academic work): Set persistent alerts for the framework title and key coined phrases. Scholar is strong on academic and law review coverage; weak on policy documents (grey literature), litigation filings, and advocacy organization materials. Run alerts as the baseline layer, not the primary detection mechanism.

**Overton.io** (paid, 6-18 month lag on policy documents): Overton indexes 21 million+ documents from government agencies, think tanks, IGOs, and policy-focused sources, with automatic citation detection. It is the most comprehensive tool for think tank and policy document citations. The lag is real — do not expect to see Phase 1 citations in Overton before Month 4. Use Overton to establish a baseline and confirm trends, not for real-time detection. The SAGE partnership allows free access via institutional library at some universities; check whether any contact has this access.

**CourtListener RECAP Search Alerts** (free, near real-time for federal court filings): CourtListener launched "RECAP Search Alerts for PACER" in 2025 — essentially Google Alerts for federal court filings. Set keyword searches for framework title and key domain phrases. This is the correct tool for tracking AG litigation citations; it surfaces new federal court filings within hours of docketing. Set alerts for: "democratic renewal," key domain phrases (e.g., "ICE-at-polls," "NVRA quiet period," "35-domain"), and named litigation cases from the litigation tracker. The RECAP archive contains tens of millions of PACER documents, fully searchable. This is the primary tool for legal impact tracking.

**PACER / Bloomberg Law** (per-page fees / institutional subscription): For cases already in the litigation tracker, set docket monitoring for new filings. Bloomberg Law's Brief Analyzer provides text search across brief content, which is the most reliable way to detect verbatim or near-verbatim domain language in court filings. PACER is free for searching; per-page fees apply for document download. For a budget-conscious operation, CourtListener RECAP handles most PACER search needs for free.

**LegiScan API** (free tier: 30,000 queries/month): LegiScan indexes the full text of approximately 175,000 bills introduced annually across all 50 states and Congress, with email alerts when bills matching saved searches are introduced or updated. Set text searches for domain-aligned keywords in priority states: voting rights (Domain 1), state autocratization (Domain 33), academic freedom (Domain 27), and ICE-at-polls language (Domain 37). The free public tier covers 30,000 queries/month — sufficient for targeted monitoring of 7-10 key states. The API allows automated bill text comparison against domain language.

**Congress.gov ProPublica API** (free): ProPublica's Congress API provides bill introduction monitoring and sponsor tracking. Useful for federal bills that incorporate domain analysis language. Monitor Senate Judiciary (Domains 6, 29), HELP Committee (Domains 10, 17, 27), and Armed Services (Domain 28) for new bill introductions.

**Google News Alerts** (free, near real-time): Set persistent daily alerts for the framework title and named contacts. This is the fastest-responding monitoring tool and the only one that surfaces media coverage within hours. Limitation: generates noise; filter for named institutional contacts and substantive coverage, not every incidental mention.

**SSRN / ResearchGate** (free): Set alerts for working papers and preprints citing the framework. SSRN is the primary repository for law review working papers; a working paper citation typically precedes the published law review article by 6-12 months and represents an earlier detection point.

---

### Adoption Survey Protocol

Quarterly 5-minute survey to a stratified sample of Tier 1-3 contacts (30-40 respondents per quarter). Questions:

1. Have you seen or used the Democratic Renewal Framework? (Yes / Heard of it but not read / No)
2. If yes: How are you currently using it? (Not actively / Cited in published work / Informing our internal analysis / Integrated into our work / Foundation for our strategy)
3. Which domain or domains are most relevant to your work? (open text)
4. What is missing that would make the framework more useful? (open text)
5. Has anyone in your network cited or used it based on your referral? (Yes / No / Unsure)

Question 5 is the network diffusion probe — it detects second-order adoption that passive monitoring cannot see.

## Part III: Success Pattern Analysis — Predicted and Detectable

### Domain Adoption Variance — What Drives It

Three variables predict whether a domain generates early citation versus sits unused:

**Active litigation anchor**: Domains with a named pending case in the litigation tracker (Domain 6 — Xinis/Wilcox, Domain 1 — SAVE Act cases, Domain 29 — SPLC indictment, Domain 16 — Abrego Garcia) will be adopted faster than domains covering structural issues without a current case. Active cases create urgency and organizational priming in the institutions working on those cases.

**Novelty relative to existing coverage**: Domains that fill genuine analytical gaps generate more sustained institutional engagement than domains that provide additional evidence for already-covered ground. Domain 37's ICE-at-polls and election denier appointment documentation is novel; the general "democratic backsliding is bad" framing is not. Measure novelty effect by comparing citation rate for high-novelty domains (6, 28, 29, 37) against lower-novelty domains (2, 3, 14) in the first 90 days.

**Institutional entry point clarity**: Domains that map directly to a specific organization's named current campaign convert faster than domains requiring translation. Domain 17 maps directly to EPI's labor brief output; Domain 27 maps directly to AAUP's academic freedom campaign; Domain 37 maps directly to Democracy Docket's NVRA litigation. Domains 5, 9, 20, and 24 require more institutional translation and will show slower early adoption regardless of analytical quality.

### Expected Citation vs. Implementation vs. Adaptation Split

**Cited (Level 1)**: Expected for Domains 6, 28, 29 fastest — these have the strongest publication-pull for Just Security and Lawfare; the most novel analytical claims; the tightest connection to named current cases. Citation without implementation is the most common outcome for structural/analytical domains.

**Implemented (Level 3)**: Expected first for Domains 1, 16, 37 — these have the most directly actionable content for organizations doing real-time litigation preparation (Domain 37 to Democracy Docket, Domain 16 to NILC/RAICES, Domain 1 to Brennan Center voting rights team). Implementation in advocacy organizations means the framework language or evidence base appears in active casework materials, not just cited publications.

**Adapted (Scope extension or local contextualization)**: Expected for Domains 33, 7, 29 — these have the most analytical generativity for organizations applying the framework to their specific state context. Domain 33's state autocratization framework will be adapted by state-level organizations to document their specific state's preemption package; Domain 29's retaliatory prosecution typology will be applied to state-level AG patterns not covered in the original domain. Track adaptations explicitly because they are often invisible to citation monitoring: an advocacy org that applies Domain 29's charging-timeline analysis to a state case will not cite the domain.

## Part IV: Failure Mode Detection — Early Warning Signals and Misapplication Patterns

### Failure Mode 1: Partisan Capture

**What it looks like**: The framework circulates in progressive activist networks as a political document rather than in institutional policy networks as an analytical framework. Reddit and Substack distribution without corresponding institutional uptake is the early signal. Measure: ratio of general audience shares (Reddit upvotes, Substack forwards from non-institutional accounts) to institutional engagement events (Tier 1 reply, methodology question, material request).

**Early warning threshold**: If, at Day 60, the ratio of social media engagement to institutional engagement events exceeds 50:1, the framework is circulating as activist content rather than policy research. This is not a failure of the research — it is a distribution sequencing problem. Response: accelerate warm referral outreach to Tier 1 think tanks to establish the institutional credibility anchor before the social media distribution dominates the framework's reputation.

**Detection tool**: Compare Substack subscriber/forward count to confirmed Tier 1 engagement events weekly through Day 90.

---

### Failure Mode 2: Domain Concentration Without Systems Integration

**What it looks like**: Three or four crisis domains (1, 29, 37, 6) generate all citations while structural reform domains (2, 5, 9, 11, 17, 34) are ignored. The framework gets used as a crisis documentation tool rather than a systems change framework. This is the most likely failure mode and arguably the highest-stakes one — the whole analytical architecture of the 35-domain framework is the cross-domain feedback loop structure, and if only the crisis domains get read, that architecture is invisible.

**Early warning threshold**: If, at Day 90, more than 70% of identified citations reference four or fewer domains, the framework is being consumed selectively. This is structurally similar to Project 2025 being read as "the scary executive power section" while the regulatory rollback sections that were equally consequential went underreported.

**Detection tool**: Domain citation log updated monthly. Flag when the top-4-domains share exceeds 70% of all identified citations.

**Response**: Targeted outreach to think tanks covering the under-cited structural domains (EPI for Domain 17, CAP for Domains 11 and 5, Brookings for Domains 9 and 34) with domain-specific briefs that foreground the cross-domain synthesis. The message: "Domains 6 and 1 are getting attention; here is why the structural reform domains determine whether the litigation wins consolidate."

---

### Failure Mode 3: Framework Flattening — Reduction to a Checklist

**What it looks like**: Secondary summaries of the framework reduce it to a numbered list of domains without the cross-domain synthesis, the wave-sequencing logic, or the international evidence base. When those summaries are cited more than the primary document, the framework's analytical core has been laundered out. This is what happened to the original Sabatier Advocacy Coalition Framework in many policy school curricula — it became a checklist of "coalition attributes" rather than a dynamic theory of policy learning.

**Early warning signal**: When a citation or adaptation references the "35-domain framework" without citing any of the specific domain evidence, analytical sections, or cross-domain connections, it is citing the label, not the work. Log label-only citations separately from substantive citations.

**Detection tool**: When reviewing any citation identified through monitoring tools, classify it as: (a) substantive — cites specific evidence, analytical claim, or cross-domain connection; (b) label-only — references the framework by name without specific content; (c) mischaracterization — attributes a claim to the framework that it does not make. The ratio of substantive to label-only citations is an analytical depth metric.

---

### Failure Mode 4: Institutional Co-optation

**What it looks like**: An organization claims the framework analysis as their own in a published brief or report without attribution, or publishes a derivative that contradicts the original's evidence claims. This is not purely a credit problem — it is an evidentiary integrity problem if the derivative version cites different sources to reach different conclusions.

**Detection tool**: When passive monitoring surfaces a document that uses the framework's analytical structure or domain-specific evidence without citation, compare the evidence claims in the derivative document against the primary domain file. If the derivative reaches different conclusions from the same evidence, that is the most important kind of misapplication to address.

**Response**: Do not correct publicly. Contact the publishing organization directly with the specific discrepancy: "Your March brief documents [X]; our domain analysis documents [Y] using the same primary source. We want to understand whether you have additional evidence we are missing or whether there is a discrepancy worth resolving." This converts a potential conflict into a collaborative relationship.

---

### Failure Mode 5: Premature Election Domain Mischaracterization

**Specific to Domain 37**: The ICE-at-polls and federal employee deployment documentation is the highest-stakes content for mischaracterization. If Domain 37's documented surveillance and intimidation analysis circulates in general audiences as prediction rather than as documented pattern, it creates three problems: (a) it invites credibility attacks that would not apply to a precise claim about documented past behavior; (b) it may generate premature operational responses that reduce rather than increase election protection capacity; (c) it undermines the carefully caveated legal analysis.

**Early warning signal**: Any citation of Domain 37 that characterizes its content as "predicting" or "warning about" future interference rather than "documenting" current infrastructure and behavioral patterns.

**Detection tool**: Review all Domain 37 citations for framing. If a citation uses future-tense language to describe documented present-tense facts, flag for direct contact with the citing organization to clarify the documentary basis of the claims.

## Part V: Path-Specific Measurement Calibration

Each distribution path activates the same measurement infrastructure but generates different expected signal patterns in the first 90 days.

**Path A (Immediate 35-domain)**: Expect faster first-signal volume but lower institutional signal-to-noise ratio. All domains hit all sectors simultaneously; early citations will cluster in the highest-salience domains (1, 29, 37). Monitor for Domain 37 mischaracterization risk within the first 30 days given that election protection organizations receive it in a general distribution without specialized framing.

**Path A+37 (Hybrid, recommended)**: Phase 1 (34 domains) signal pattern will look identical to Path A at Day 30. Phase 2 Domain 37 targeted distribution to election protection organizations generates a second signal cluster at Day 45-60, which should be tracked separately: compare Domain 37 citation and adoption quality between general-distribution recipients and targeted-distribution recipients. This comparison validates the strategic sequencing logic and informs future targeted distribution decisions.

**Path B (Domain maintenance first)**: First-signal timing is compressed by approximately 14-28 days relative to the other paths. The 90-day and 180-day targets should be adjusted accordingly. Path B's primary analytical value for impact measurement is the question of whether the additional domain content (Domains 1, 6, 19f, 25, 28, 33, 35 updated through May) improves institutional adoption rates relative to the pre-update corpus. Track whether Tier 1 contacts cite the May-updated content specifically — this tests whether Path B's content investment was worth the timing cost.

## Part VI: Integration With Existing Measurement Infrastructure

This document extends rather than replaces the existing measurement architecture:

- `measurement-and-iteration-framework.md` governs the monthly iteration cycle, bridge node tracking templates, and the adoption facilitation sequence. Use it for operational execution.
- `post-distribution-tracking.md` provides the week-by-week first 30 days roadmap and domain adoption probability rankings. Use it for the first month of monitoring.
- `institutional-adoption-playbooks.md` provides sector-specific operational guidance for organizations that have reached Level 2-3 adoption. Use it once adoption events are confirmed.
- This document provides: the sector diffusion model (Part I), the tool stack with coverage analysis (Part II), the success pattern and domain variance analysis (Part III), and the failure mode detection system (Part IV). Use it to design the measurement system and interpret signals against the baseline.
