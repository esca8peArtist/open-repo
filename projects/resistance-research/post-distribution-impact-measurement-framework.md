# Post-Distribution Impact Measurement Framework

**May 5, 2026 — Phase 1 Execution Prep**

This framework governs how adoption of the 35-domain Democratic Renewal Proposal gets measured, diagnosed, and course-corrected after Phase 1 distribution launches. It is path-independent: the same framework applies whether the user selects Path A, Path A+37, or Path B. The measurement system activates on distribution day.

The framework operates on a central premise from policy diffusion research: institutional adoption is not a binary event but a staged process, and the most consequential adoptions — the ones that produce litigation wins, legislative language, and organizational strategy shifts — are often invisible to passive monitoring. A Senate Judiciary staff director who incorporates domain analysis into a hearing background memo will not appear in any Google Scholar alert. This framework builds systems to detect both the visible and invisible signals.

## Part I: Institutional Adoption Pathways by Sector

This section maps where the framework naturally lands in each sector, the realistic diffusion pattern within that sector, and the specific evidence signals to watch for.

### Sector 1: State Attorneys General

**Why this sector matters first**: The 22-AG coalition (plus DC) is already organized, funded, and in active litigation against the administration on elections, immigration enforcement, and executive authority. These AGs are not starting from scratch — they are looking for evidence, legal theory, and analytical frameworks to strengthen work already in progress. The framework's Domains 6, 28, 29, 34, and 37 map directly to their existing dockets.

**Diffusion pattern**: AGs adopt through their policy counsel staff, not through the elected AG reading the proposal. The entry point is a law clerk, policy attorney, or chief of staff who encounters the framework and routes a specific domain to the relevant litigation team. Coalition adoption (one AG's office shares with peers through NAAG channels) is the primary amplification mechanism. The Massachusetts AG's five-year strategic plan model — which tracks measurable institutional commitments over a defined timeline — is the format most compatible with how AG offices engage with external policy frameworks ([Foley Hoag State AG Insights, April 2024](https://foleyhoag.com/news-and-insights/blogs/state-ag-insights/2024/april/massachusetts-attorney-general-campbell-unveils-five-year-strategic-plan/)).

**Realistic adoption timeline**:
- Week 1-4: Framework received by policy counsel; routed to relevant litigation team
- Week 4-8: Domain analysis incorporated into pending filing or strategy memo (internal, invisible)
- Week 8-16: First external signal — amicus brief, coalition letter, or public statement using domain language
- Month 4-6: Formal incorporation into AG office policy priorities or next litigation wave

**What to look for**:
- PACER searches for amicus briefs in pending federal cases (Wilcox/Slaughter independent agency removal; SAVE Act litigation; DOJ voter roll cases) using framework language or citing its evidence
- NAAG coalition letters (naag.org) using domain-specific framing or citing international precedents from the comparative research base
- State AG press releases citing framework evidence (particularly Domains 1, 37 for election interference; Domain 29 for prosecutorial weaponization)
- Direct confirmation from AG policy counsel contacts

**Structural resistance point**: AGs cannot formally endorse external policy proposals without creating appearance concerns. The adoption will typically be evidence-citation rather than framework-endorsement. An AG brief that cites the Domain 29 comparative analysis of prosecutorial norm violations without naming the framework is still an adoption event — it indicates the evidence reached the litigation team.

### Sector 2: Law Schools and Legal Academia

**Why this sector matters**: Law schools are the training ground for the next generation of institutional actors — clerks, government attorneys, legislative drafters — and the credibility anchor for legal arguments. A proposal cited in Harvard Law Review has a different reception than the same proposal distributed via email. Law school adoption converts the framework from "activist document" to "scholarly resource," which is the prerequisite for credibility in Tier 1 institutional audiences.

**Diffusion pattern**: Legal academia adopts through course integration and law review publication. These are distinct pathways with distinct timelines. Course integration is faster but less visible; a clinical professor who assigns Domain 6 in a judicial independence seminar generates no public signal for months. Law review publication is slower (6-18 months from submission to publication) but creates a permanent, citable, searchable record.

**Realistic adoption timeline**:
- Month 1-2: Framework reaches faculty through direct outreach or bridge contact (ACS scholars network, clinic director referrals)
- Month 2-4: Course integration decisions made (syllabus preparation is typically 6-8 weeks before semester)
- Month 4-8: Student note authors and symposium editors encounter framework; submission inquiry or draft citation
- Month 8-18: Law review publication cycle (if submitted Month 4, publication likely Month 12-18)

The GPPI think tank impact study documents that significant policy citations in legislation routinely take 5-15 years from first publication — Milton Friedman's school voucher proposal took 30 years to become viable federal policy ([GPPI "Whose Bright Idea Was That," 2023](https://gppi.net/assets/Bressan_Hoxtell_2023_Whose-Bright-Idea-Was-That.pdf)). Law review scholarship operates faster in some domains (rapid-response online forums publish within weeks of major rulings) but the full academic cycle is measured in semesters, not months.

**Domains with fastest law school adoption**:
- Domain 6 (Judicial Independence): Maps directly to existing constitutional law scholarship on court-packing, removal, and institutional design; comparative law methodology is standard academic format
- Domain 28 (War Powers / Venezuela): AUMF scholarship, OLC memo analysis, and Seth Harp coverage create natural entry points for national security law faculty
- Domain 29 (Prosecutorial Weaponization): Connects to existing academic literature on DOJ independence and special counsel history; Nashville ruling provides live case study
- Domain 37 (Election Interference): Voting rights faculty have strong existing infrastructure for research (Democracy Docket, Brennan Center relationships) making citation likely once credibility is established

**Domains with slowest law school adoption**:
- Domains 23-25 (Trade Policy, Surveillance, FISA): These require specialized technical expertise in trade law and national security law respectively — fewer clinics and faculty with direct practice relevance
- Domain 34 (Congressional Fiscal Authority): Important but abstract; maps to appropriations scholarship that is niche within law schools

### Sector 3: Think Tanks and Policy Organizations

**Why this sector matters**: Think tanks are citation multipliers. A Brennan Center brief reaches 435 congressional offices; an EPI issue brief reaches AFL-CIO's 56 affiliated unions. Think tanks convert the framework from one document into dozens of documents — briefings, op-eds, testimony, reports — each of which carries the evidence downstream to audiences that would never read the original proposal.

**Diffusion pattern**: Think tanks adopt through their research directors and program officers, who assess external frameworks against their own publication needs. The adoption decision is usually framed as: "Is there something in this that fills a gap in our current work?" Adoption is fastest when the framework provides evidence or a comparative international dataset that the think tank's own research infrastructure cannot generate quickly.

The On Think Tanks assessment methodology identifies three impact levels: direct policy influence (officials cite research), research adoption (think tanks incorporate into own work), and debate influence (changes what questions are asked) ([On Think Tanks: Policy Impact, 2024](https://onthinktanks.org/articles/policy-impact-a-think-tanks-perspective/)). The measurement framework here targets all three levels but prioritizes research adoption as the most detectable.

**Realistic adoption timeline**:
- Week 1-3: Framework reaches program officer; internal routing to relevant research team
- Week 3-6: Research team reviews; determines whether specific domain fills a gap in pending work
- Week 6-10: Incorporation into pending brief or report (internal, invisible)
- Week 10-16: Published brief cites framework domain evidence or analytical framework
- Month 4+: Conference presentation, testimony, or media placement amplifies the citation

**Domain-specific think tank pathways**:
- **Brennan Center**: Domains 1, 6, 29, 37 — strongest match with their democracy program; Senate Judiciary hearing testimony is a direct cascade opportunity
- **EPI**: Domain 17 (Labor) — strongest match; Domain 5 (Fiscal Reform) secondary
- **Just Security**: Domains 28, 29 — fastest publication turnaround of any think tank in the map (48-96 hours); a Domain 28 piece citing the Venezuela operation analysis establishes immediate credibility
- **Protect Democracy**: Domains 6, 37 — their Hatch Act and election security dockets match directly
- **CAP (Center for American Progress)**: Domains 11, 17, 22, 23 — their economic and racial equity portfolios
- **Brookings**: Domain 9 (Federalism), Domain 28 (War Powers), Domain 34 — their governance and foreign policy programs

**Domain 37 as the fastest think tank entry point**: Because Domain 37 is based on current 2026 events (the DOJ voter roll lawsuits, CISA gutting, ESG non-activation), it provides evidence that think tanks cannot generate quickly from their own research. The seven quantified baselines in `domains/domain-37-baseline-metrics.md` are directly usable in think tank publications and will not age quickly. This makes Domain 37 the highest-likelihood first-adoption domain at Tier 1 think tanks.

### Sector 4: Civil Rights and Advocacy Organizations

**Why this sector matters**: Civil rights and advocacy organizations have two things that think tanks and law schools do not: members and operational campaigns. When an advocacy organization adopts a framework, it cascades to member communications, legislative testimony, coalition organizing, and — in some cases — direct litigation. The AFL-CIO model is illustrative: EPI publishes, AFL-CIO distributes to 56 affiliated unions, union legislative directors testify citing the EPI brief. This is the only sector where a single adoption event can reach millions of people.

**Diffusion pattern**: Advocacy organizations adopt through their policy directors and communications teams, who evaluate external frameworks against two questions: does this help our current campaign, and does this speak the language our members understand? The first criterion favors operational relevance; the second favors accessible framing. The framework's comparative international evidence is a strength for policy staff but a weakness for member communications — it requires translation before it reaches grassroots audiences.

**Realistic adoption timeline**:
- Month 1-2: Framework reaches policy director; assessed against current campaign priorities
- Month 2-3: Domain-specific brief requested or prepared internally
- Month 3-4: Incorporated into legislative testimony or public campaign materials (external signal)
- Month 4-6: Member communications referencing domain analysis (internal-to-external translation signal)
- Month 6-12: Operational integration (specific domain analysis shapes litigation or campaign strategy)

**Domain-specific advocacy pathways**:
- **Domain 1 (Voting Rights)**: Indivisible, Common Cause, FairVote, NAACP LDF — fastest adoption given the *Callais v. Landry* ruling and ongoing NVRA quiet period urgency
- **Domain 17 (Labor)**: AFL-CIO via EPI bridge; NEA for Domain 27 (academic freedom in schools)
- **Domain 16 (Immigration)**: NILC, RAICES, CLINIC — current Abrego Garcia and CBP One litigation connects directly
- **Domain 29 (Prosecutorial Weaponization)**: SPLC (the SPLC indictment documented in Domain 29 is their own case), NAACP LDF, Lawyers' Committee
- **Domain 37 (Election Interference)**: Democracy Docket, Campaign Legal Center, States United — these organizations are already producing the litigation this domain documents

### Sector 5: Corporate Boards and Fiduciary Duty Advisors

**Why this sector is included**: The democratic renewal proposal's framing of institutional resilience — particularly the domains covering independent agency protection, rule of law stability, and regulatory predictability — maps onto the governance and fiduciary duty frameworks that corporate boards use to assess long-term risk. This is not a primary adoption sector but it is an underexplored one with meaningful downstream effects.

**The fiduciary duty connection**: Corporate directors in all 50 states owe duties of care and loyalty to their corporations. The duty of care includes monitoring material risks to the corporation's operating environment. When the rule of law, regulatory independence, and democratic institutions face the documented threats in the framework, corporate governance advisors are professionally obligated to brief their boards on those risks. The Harvard Law School Corporate Governance Forum's 2019 analysis of stakeholder governance explicitly frames fiduciary duty as extending to institutional stability assessment — and the Journal of Business Ethics 2025 analysis of the "corporate purpose trilemma" documents the growing tension between shareholder primacy and broader democratic governance obligations ([Stakeholder Governance and Fiduciary Duties, HLS Corp Gov Forum, 2019](https://corpgov.law.harvard.edu/2019/08/24/stakeholder-governance-and-the-fiduciary-duties-of-directors/)).

**Diffusion pattern**: Corporate governance adoption occurs through general counsel offices, institutional investor advisors (Institutional Shareholder Services, Glass Lewis), and ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) risk analysts. The entry vector is typically "democratic risk" framing within ESG reporting — when the framework's documentation of judicial independence degradation and regulatory capture translates into "governance risk" language that ESG analysts can operationalize.

**Domains with corporate governance relevance**:
- Domain 6 (Judicial Independence): Regulatory risk, contract enforcement stability
- Domain 9 (Federalism): Preemption uncertainty, multistate compliance risk
- Domain 23 (Trade Policy): Tariff unilateralism as supply chain risk
- Domain 34 (Congressional Fiscal Authority): Budget uncertainty as operational risk
- Domain 19f (War Powers): National security risk, overseas operations exposure

**Realistic adoption timeline**: Long. Corporate governance is an 18-36 month adoption cycle. This sector is a 3-5 year impact target, not a Phase 1 measurement priority. Monitor through Institutional Shareholder Services publications and ESG risk reports; do not invest active outreach resources in Phase 1.

**One near-term activation opportunity**: If any of the framework's domains are cited in an ESG risk disclosure filed with the SEC, that constitutes both a corporate adoption event and a regulatory signal. Monitor SEC EDGAR for proxy statements and sustainability reports that cite democratic governance risk using framework language.

## Part II: Impact Metrics Framework

### The Measurement Stack

The framework uses five tracking layers with distinct tools, cadences, and signal types. Each layer captures a different slice of the adoption signal spectrum.

**Layer 1 — Active passive monitoring** (automated, daily/weekly):
Google News alerts, Google Scholar alerts, CourtListener RECAP alerts, SSRN new paper notifications, RSS feeds for priority publications (Just Security, Brennan Center, Lawfare, Balkinization). This layer captures signals that generate a public footprint. Setup time: 2 hours. Ongoing labor: 30 minutes/week to review alerts and log to the adoption scorecard.

**Layer 2 — Database searches** (manual, monthly):
PACER litigation keyword searches, LegiScan state bill full-text searches, Congress.gov testimony searches, Overton policy citation database (quarterly, after Month 4 due to indexing lag). This layer captures institutional citations that don't generate public news coverage. Setup time: 1 hour. Ongoing labor: 2 hours/month.

**Layer 3 — Contact intelligence** (active, ongoing):
Direct contact log — every substantive response to outreach, every request for additional materials, every piece of feedback on specific domains. This is the highest-signal layer because it captures what is actually being done with the framework inside organizations before any external signal appears. Setup time: none (starts on Day 1 of outreach). Ongoing labor: varies.

**Layer 4 — Bridge node cascade tracking** (active, per event):
For each of the seven priority bridge nodes, log every second-order signal (the node uses the framework in their own published work) and each third-order signal (downstream citation to the node's work). This is the most labor-intensive layer but captures the most consequential cascade events. The cascade depth metric — how many citation generations have occurred — is a proxy for institutional penetration.

**Layer 5 — Overton and policy citation index** (quarterly, Month 4+):
[Overton.io](https://www.overton.io/policy-impact) tracks citations of research in policy documents across 21 million+ government, think tank, and IGO sources globally. Due to a 6-18 month indexing lag, this layer is not actionable in Phase 1 but becomes the most comprehensive citation record in Year 2-3. Register at Overton; set calendar reminder at Month 4, Month 8, and Month 12 to run searches. The Overton Index methodology is documented in the MIT Press Quantitative Science Studies analysis ([Overton database methodology, MIT QSS, 2022](https://direct.mit.edu/qss/article/3/3/624/112760/Overton-A-bibliometric-database-of-policy-document)).

## Part III: Success Pattern Analysis — Which Domains Get Cited vs. Implemented vs. Adapted, and Why

This section operationalizes a domain-level variance analysis. The 35 domains will not diffuse uniformly. Understanding the expected pattern in advance allows the measurement system to distinguish expected silence from unexpected failure.

### High-Adoption Probability Domains (Year 1 Citations Expected)

**Domain 1 (Voting Rights) and Domain 37 (Election Interference)**:

These two domains benefit from the most active external validation environment. The *Callais v. Landry* ruling (April 29, 2026, gutting VRA Section 2), the DOJ voter roll litigation (24 states + DC), and the CISA election security gutting are all generating daily coverage in Democracy Docket, Brennan Center, and Votebeat. The framework's domain evidence will be cited in this ongoing coverage within weeks of distribution because it provides quantified baselines and comparative analysis that are currently unavailable in a single consolidated source.

Expected adoption pattern: Citation-dominant in Year 1 (organizational references to the evidence base); implementation-reaching by Year 2 (litigation strategy and legislative testimony using the domain recommendations).

**Domain 6 (Judicial Independence)**:

The academic infrastructure for judicial independence scholarship is dense (Balkinization, Just Security, constitutional law journals), active, and currently producing high-volume output in response to the NLRB/MSPB independent agency removal cases and the Wilcox/Slaughter Supreme Court litigation. Domain 6's comparative constitutional law methodology — international court systems, judicial appointment reform models — is the format that this academic infrastructure produces and consumes. Citation is likely within 60-90 days of distribution reaching Tier 2 academics.

Expected adoption pattern: Citation in academic publications within 90 days; framework adoption (comparative methodology) in law school courses within 6 months; operational adoption (AG litigation strategy) within 12 months.

**Domain 29 (Prosecutorial Weaponization)**:

Domain 29 is the domain with the highest "live case" density — the SPLC indictment (April 21, 2026), the Nashville vindictive prosecution ruling, the 22-case retaliatory pattern. Organizations directly affected by documented cases (SPLC, EarthJustice, NAACP LDF) are the natural first adopters, and they have direct organizational incentive to use the domain's evidence in their defense strategies. The Just Security national security law community is the academic vector.

Expected adoption pattern: Implementation-level adoption possible in Year 1 for organizations directly named in the domain's case documentation. This is the domain most likely to see Level 3 (operational) adoption before Level 1 (citation) adoption, because organizations with live cases will use the evidence before they publish about it.

### Moderate-Adoption Probability Domains (Year 1-2 Citations Expected)

**Domain 28 (War Powers / Venezuela)**:

Strong academic infrastructure (Just Security, national security law faculty) but narrower operational adoption pathway — only organizations with national security law portfolios (ACLU National Security Project, Center for Constitutional Rights, Brennan Center Liberty and National Security program) can operationalize this domain. Expect citation-level adoption quickly, implementation-level adoption slowly.

**Domain 17 (Labor)**:

Strong operational pipeline (EPI to AFL-CIO), but the pipeline is dependent on a single bridge node (EPI's decision to publish Domain 17 analysis). If EPI adopts, the cascade is reliable and fast. If EPI does not adopt in Year 1, Domain 17 remains at citation level with limited operational reach. The binary nature of this cascade makes it a high-variance domain — prepare a contingency plan for direct AFL-CIO outreach if EPI adoption stalls after 90 days.

**Domain 27 (Higher Education / Academic Freedom)**:

Strong interest from AAUP and Chronicle of Higher Education community; strong adverse interest from administrations defending against Domain 27-documented attacks. Expected citation adoption in academic media within 60-90 days. Implementation adoption — universities using the domain analysis in accreditation defense or federal funding litigation — is possible in Year 1 given the immediacy of current federal funding leverage cases (Harvard $2.2B freeze, Columbia $221M settlement).

### Low-Adoption Probability Domains (Year 2-5 Citations Expected)

**Domain 34 (Congressional Fiscal Authority / Power of the Purse)**:

Important structurally but abstract in Year 1 adoption terms. Congressional fiscal authority is not a domain that generates grassroots momentum or advocacy organization campaigning. Adoption will occur through budget committee staff, appropriations-focused policy organizations (Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget, Bipartisan Policy Center), and fiscal law academics — all of whom operate on longer timelines. This domain is likely to appear first in congressional testimony rather than organizational campaigns.

**Domain 9 (Federalism)**:

Important but diffuse — the federalism domain does not map cleanly to any single advocacy sector. State legislatures, preemption scholars, and municipal law attorneys each consume a piece of it, but no single sector "owns" federalism as a primary advocacy frame. Expect citation adoption at Brookings and similar governance think tanks; expect operational adoption only when a specific preemption dispute creates a test case for the domain's framework.

**Domains 23-25 (Trade Policy, AI Governance, Surveillance/FISA)**:

These domains require technical specialization that limits the initial audience. Trade policy scholars, privacy advocates, and FISA-specialized litigators are the natural adopters, but each community is relatively small and insular. Expect adoption within those specialized communities before any cross-sector diffusion.

### Implementation Barriers by Domain Type

The Green New Deal diffusion pattern is instructive here. The GND was proposed in 2019; by 2023, it had diffused into dozens of state and local proposals and shaped the Inflation Reduction Act's investment framework — but the most radical structural elements were dropped or significantly adapted in every legislative vehicle ([Slow Boring: How the Green New Deal became the IRA, 2023](https://www.slowboring.com/p/how-the-green-new-deal-became-the)). The pattern: crisis-adjacent domains diffuse fastest; structural-reform domains face the most adaptation pressure.

Applied to this framework:

- **Domains with high crisis adjacency** (1, 16, 28, 29, 37): Diffuse fastest; cited earliest; adapted soonest for immediate use
- **Domains with high structural reform content** (6, 13, 34): Diffuse more slowly; cited first in academic venues; implemented only in Wave 2 or 3 scenarios
- **Domains facing executive power resistance** (19f, 34, 28 executive limits section): Highest structural resistance because they require congressional action; operational adoption is dependent on congressional partners who are currently in the minority

Based on policy diffusion literature (Sabatier's Advocacy Coalition Framework; GPPI think tank impact research) and the GND case study, the following adoption curve is realistic for this framework:

## Part IV: Failure Mode Detection — Identifying Misinterpretation and Misapplication

### Framework for Detecting Misapplication

A distributed policy framework that reaches hundreds of organizations will be used incorrectly by some of them. This is not a catastrophic failure — it is a feature of distributed adoption. The relevant question is not whether any misapplication occurs, but whether misapplication is (a) systemic (a large percentage of users share the same incorrect interpretation), (b) reputationally damaging (the misapplication is public and attributable to the framework), or (c) strategically counterproductive (the misapplication actively undermines the framework's goals).

The detection system below distinguishes these categories and assigns response protocols accordingly.

### Early Warning Signal System

**Signal type 1 — Partisan capture (systemic risk)**:

*Detection*: Social media engagement (shares, comments, reactions) exceeds institutional engagement (substantive responses, material requests, methodology questions) by a ratio of 20:1 or more. The ratio of social to institutional engagement is the partisan capture indicator; a 50:1 ratio in the first 30 days indicates the framework has been absorbed into partisan media discourse without institutional adoption.

*What wrong interpretation looks like*: The framework is cited as a political campaign document — "proof that Democrats should X" — rather than a diagnostic and reform framework. Domain 29 (Prosecutorial Weaponization) is the highest-risk domain for this pattern because it documents specific acts by named officials. If Domain 29 citations appear primarily in campaign fundraising materials or partisan attack ads, partisan capture has occurred.

*Why this matters*: Partisan capture reduces the framework's credibility with exactly the institutional audiences (think tanks that must maintain nonpartisan credibility, law schools that cannot assign advocacy documents in courses, AG offices that cannot be associated with campaign materials) that are most valuable for long-term impact.

*Response trigger*: If any sustained media coverage (3+ articles over a two-week period) characterizes the framework as campaign material, activate Protocol A (institutional credibility reinforcement) within two weeks.

**Signal type 2 — Domain cherry-picking (structural risk)**:

*Detection*: More than 70% of identified citations reference fewer than 30% of domains. Typically the crisis domains (1, 16, 19f, 28, 29, 37) are cited while the structural reform domains (6, 9, 13, 34) are not.

*What wrong interpretation looks like*: Organizations treating the framework as a crisis documentation tool ("evidence that bad things are happening") rather than a diagnostic and reform framework ("here is what is broken and here is how to fix it"). This is the most common pattern in policy framework diffusion — the evidence sections get cited while the reform proposals are ignored.

*Why this matters*: The reform proposals in the framework are the highest-value content for long-term democratic recovery. Crisis documentation without reform pathway becomes activism without strategy. The framework's cross-domain synthesis — that 35 crises share structural root causes and require coordinated institutional responses — is lost when crisis domains are cited in isolation.

*Response trigger*: If citation analysis at Month 3 shows domain concentration exceeding 70/30 ratio, prepare domain-specific briefs for the under-cited structural reform domains and route them to Tier 1 think tank contacts whose portfolios match those domains.

**Signal type 3 — Factual misattribution (reputational risk)**:

*Detection*: Any citation that attributes to the framework a factual claim, statistical figure, or policy recommendation that it does not make. Monitor all identified citations for accuracy against the source documents.

*What wrong interpretation looks like*: The framework documents a SAVE database error rate of 81% (Missouri) and 95%+ (Idaho). A misattribution might be: "The Democratic Renewal Proposal claims that SAVE has a 95% error rate nationally" — when the 95% figure is specific to Idaho's state review and has not been extrapolated nationally. This is a subtle but meaningful distinction; an AG opponent could undermine a brief citing the framework by pointing to the overstated claim.

*Why this matters*: A single well-publicized factual error can be used to discredit the entire framework in institutional venues. The framework's evidence standard is its primary institutional credibility asset — one error in a high-profile citation can set back adoption by months.

*Response trigger*: Any identified factual misattribution triggers direct contact with the citing organization within one week. The response should not be adversarial — frame it as "we noticed the draft is using our data in a slightly different way than intended" and offer a corrected formulation. Never issue a public correction without first attempting a private correction with the organization.

**Signal type 4 — Domain 37-specific present-tense erasure (strategic risk)**:

*Detection*: Any citation of Domain 37 content that uses future-tense or hypothetical language ("if ICE were deployed at polling places") for actions that Domain 37 documents as already occurring or in active preparation.

*What wrong interpretation looks like*: "The Brennan Center's 2025 report warns that election deniers could be appointed to federal election positions" — when Domain 37 documents 13+ named election denier appointees already confirmed as of May 2026. This pattern degrades urgency and allows audiences to treat documented present threats as speculative future risks.

*Response trigger*: Any identified present-tense erasure triggers direct contact with the citing organization, flagged as time-sensitive. This is the highest-priority misapplication detection protocol because Domain 37's primary purpose is establishing documented present-tense threats for the August 7 NVRA quiet period and November 2026 election cycle.

### Course Correction Mechanisms

The framework distinguishes between three modes of misapplication response:

**Mode 1 — Direct private correction** (for factual errors and domain-specific mischaracterization):
Contact the citing organization privately with a specific correction framed as supportive. "We noticed your brief uses our SAVE data in a way that might be vulnerable to challenge — here's the exact formulation that reflects what the data actually shows." This maintains the relationship while correcting the error. Use for: factual misattribution, present-tense erasure, specific domain mischaracterization.

**Mode 2 — Public clarification without naming the misapplication** (for systemic patterns):
If a misapplication pattern is appearing across multiple organizations (not just one), issue a brief public clarification document — a "How to Use This Framework" one-pager that addresses the pattern without attributing it to any specific organization. Post to the distribution channel (Substack, GitHub Gist) as an update. Use for: partisan capture pattern, domain cherry-picking at scale.

**Mode 3 — No intervention** (for legitimate adaptation):
Organizations will legitimately adapt the framework for their own contexts. An advocacy organization that takes Domain 17's labor analysis and adds their own state-specific data, or a law school clinic that uses Domain 6's comparative methodology but frames it in terms of their specific litigation docket, is not misapplying the framework — they are adapting it. The distinction between misapplication (attribution error, factual error, strategic misalignment) and legitimate adaptation (framing change, scope adjustment, audience translation) is the key judgment call.

**Rule of thumb**: If the adaptation preserves the evidentiary basis and the institutional logic of the domain while changing the framing for a specific audience, it is legitimate adaptation — no intervention needed. If the adaptation changes the evidentiary claims or uses the framework to support a conclusion it does not reach, it is misapplication — private correction appropriate.

### Feedback Loop Triggers: Intervene vs. Let Organic Adoption Continue

The hardest judgment call in post-distribution impact management is knowing when to intervene in organic adoption dynamics and when to let them continue without interference. The general principle is: intervene when adoption is being undermined by detectable errors; do not intervene when adoption is proceeding organically but differently than expected.

**Intervene when**:
- A factual error in a published citation is attributable to the framework
- Partisan capture has begun to contaminate institutional reception
- A specific domain is being used to support a position the research does not support
- A bridge node has been contacted and misunderstood the framework in a way that will affect their second-order distribution

**Let organic adoption continue when**:
- Organizations are using only the crisis domains and ignoring structural reform domains (this is frustrating but it is how policy frameworks diffuse — wait for the structural domains to become urgent)
- A domain is being cited less than expected from a specific sector (sector adoption timelines vary; absence of signal at Day 60 is not failure)
- An organization is adapting the framework's framing for their audience in ways that change emphasis but not accuracy
- Academic criticism of the framework's analytical framework appears (academic engagement of any kind, including critical engagement, is adoption — it keeps the framework in the scholarly conversation)

**The 90-day passivity rule**: For the first 90 days after distribution, default to observation rather than intervention. The most common error in policy framework distribution is over-managing early adoption — contacting organizations before they have had time to engage with the material, sending follow-up materials before the initial distribution has been processed, and interpreting the absence of signal as failure before the adoption timeline has elapsed. The measurement system should be running at full capacity during Day 1-90; the intervention instinct should be restrained.

## A4: Attribution and Interpretation Logic

### A4.0 Why Attribution Is Hard Here

The pre-distribution baseline is high. The 23-state AG coalition was already formed. ProPublica had already published its major investigation. Brennan Center model legislation was already in 11 states. Positive developments after distribution would be wrong to attribute to the framework without affirmative evidence of framework-specific influence.

The framework's contribution is marginal: faster coordination, shared analytical language, mechanism depth that practitioners may not have independently developed. Attribution tests look for these specific marginal contributions, not simply positive trend continuation.

---

### A4.1 The Three Attribution Tests

Every event logged in the Events Log should be assessed against three tests.

**Test 1 — Vocabulary Marker Test (V)**

Did the policy actor use language specific to the framework that could not have been independently originated from publicly available pre-distribution sources?

Evidence required: Direct quote or paraphrase using one of the 8 tracked vocabulary markers from A1.2. The phrase must appear in a work product produced after distribution. The phrase must not appear in any of the 50 sources cited in `domain-37-baseline-metrics.md` or in any prior published work identified by the Day-0 baseline search.

Confidence notes: Verbatim appearance is the strongest marker. Paraphrases require judgment. "Federal executive interference election" is not vocabulary-marked if the actor has previously used those words independently. "SAVE false positive cascade" is vocabulary-marked because it is a specific compound construction not found in prior sources.

**Test 2 — Structural Convergence Test (S)**

Does the new work product align structurally with framework recommendations across multiple domains — not just one shared element?

Evidence required: At least two distinct structural features match framework analysis. Examples: A new AG brief both (a) targets defendant classes identified in Domain 37 (Albus nationwide jurisdiction, Honey HSGP leverage) and (b) uses the consent decree strategy structure. A new bill both (a) incorporates the 81% SAVE false positive rate as the evidentiary standard and (b) provides the state criminal statute model from the Brennan Center track.

Rationale: A single shared element may reflect parallel development or common sources. Two or more converging structural elements are less likely coincidental.

**Test 3 — Timing and Contact Test (T)**

Can you trace a plausible causal chain: (a) the actor received the framework, (b) sufficient deliberation time passed, (c) the actor produced the work product after the deliberation window?

Evidence required: (a) Actor is on the Batch 1 contact list, or the framework demonstrably reached them via documented forward from a Batch 1 contact, or they publicly referenced the distribution; (b) deliberation gap of at least 14 days; (c) work product date is after the gap.

---

### A4.2 Attribution Score and Confidence Level

| Tests Passed | Score | Confidence | Interpretation |
|-------------|-------|-----------|----------------|
| V + S + T (all three) | 3 — High | High | Strong attribution. Document prominently. Use in public reporting. |
| Any two of three | 2 — Medium | Medium | Probable contribution. Note which test failed. Include in aggregate count with uncertainty notation. |
| Any one of three | 1 — Low | Low | Possible contribution. Log but do not publicize. Detect patterns across multiple events only. |
| None | 0 — None | None | Not attributable. Baseline trend or independent development. Do not count in impact totals. |

---

### A4.3 Attribution Decision Flowchart

Work through each step in order. The first "No" that cannot be overcome determines the ceiling score.

```
START: An event occurs — bill introduced / article published / brief filed / position changed

STEP 1: Is the event post-distribution?
  NO  → Score: 0. STOP. Pre-dates the framework's possible influence.
  YES → Continue to Step 2.

STEP 2 — Vocabulary Marker Test (V):
  Does the work product use any of the 8 tracked vocabulary markers
  that are absent from the pre-distribution source corpus?
  YES → V = PASS (add 1 to score)
  NO  → V = FAIL

STEP 3 — Structural Convergence Test (S):
  Does the work product align structurally with framework recommendations
  across two or more distinct elements?
  YES → S = PASS (add 1 to score)
  NO  → S = FAIL

STEP 4 — Timing and Contact Test (T):
  Is the actor reachable via documented path from Batch 1?
  Is there at least 14 days between framework receipt and work product date?
  YES → T = PASS (add 1 to score)
  NO  → T = FAIL

RAW SCORE = count of tests passed (0–3)

COUNTERFACTUAL CHECK (apply to Score 2 or 3 only):
  Would this action likely have occurred at this scale and pace
  absent the framework, based on the pre-distribution trend rate
  documented in domain-37-baseline-metrics.md Section 8?
  YES, likely anyway → Downgrade score by 1
  NO, not likely anyway → Maintain score

FINAL SCORE → Log in Events Log. Record which tests passed.
```

---

### A4.4 Non-Causality Filters

Apply these before finalizing attribution. Any event matching a filter should be downgraded or excluded.

1. **Election-year baseline activity**: Policy actions demonstrably scheduled before distribution (e.g., appropriations subcommittee markups already calendared) are not attributable even if they move favorably. Check the markup calendar before attributing any CISA budget development.

2. **No confirmed contact**: An event by a policy actor with no documented path to the framework (not on Batch 1 list, no evidence of secondary receipt) cannot receive a T-test pass. Maximum score: 2 (only if both V and S pass).

3. **Vocabulary overlap without structural alignment**: If an actor uses framework vocabulary but proposes a structurally different solution, the V-test passes but the S-test fails. Score ceiling: 1 (unless T also passes).

4. **Common-source vocabulary**: If the vocabulary marker appears in any of the 50 sources cited in `domain-37-baseline-metrics.md`, it is not a Domain 37 attribution signal. Check every V-test claim against the baseline source list before recording.
