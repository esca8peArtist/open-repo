## Domain Candidate 1: Civil Service Politicization and Nonpartisan Governance Architecture

**Proposed number**: Domain 56 (next available after current 55-domain ceiling)

**Scope**

The Pendleton Civil Service Reform Act of 1883 is the foundational democratic design decision that ended the spoils system: that government should be administered by people hired for competence, retained for performance, and protected from political retaliation. The 143-year merit system that Pendleton inaugurated is the structural mechanism through which policy expertise persists across administrations, scientific findings remain independent, and democratic elections produce policy change rather than mass patronage replacement.

Schedule Policy/Career — the OPM final rule published February 6, 2026 and effective March 9, 2026 — converts approximately 50,000 career positions to at-will employment by executive action, stripping statutory Merit Systems Protection Board protections without congressional authorization. Nine percent of the federal workforce (approximately 300,000 positions) has been eliminated through DOGE since January 2025. The result is a civil service structurally redesigned to function as a loyalty instrument for the current president rather than a nonpartisan administrative infrastructure accountable to Congress and the public.

The democratic-design argument is specific and distinct from adjacent domains: the nonpartisan civil service is the institutional mechanism through which democratic elections produce policy change at all. When career scientists at the CDC, career lawyers at the DOJ, and career economists at the Fed are replaced by political loyalists or eliminated, the causal chain from elections to policy outcomes is severed. Elections become contests over who occupies the top of an organization chart, not over the direction of the governmental machinery that delivers programs, enforces law, and generates the empirical foundations for public deliberation.

This domain must be distinguished from the existing `domain-civil-service-resilience.md`, which documents tactics for protecting career employees under attack. This is the structural argument: why the merit system is a democratic institution, not an administrative preference; what its systematic destruction means for democratic accountability; and what statutory and constitutional reform architecture would restore it. The Hatch Act, the Saving the Civil Service Act (H.R. 1002 / S. 399), and the congressional review authority over Schedule Policy/Career under the APA and Loper Bright doctrine are the reform vectors.

**Causal pathway to democracy**

The pathway is direct and two-stage. First: Schedule Policy/Career reclassification makes career employees removable for "willful subversion of presidential directives" — language that inverts the constitutional accountability structure by treating compliance with legal congressional mandates as insubordination to the executive. Second: the replacement of merit-hired experts with political appointees at agencies that enforce civil rights law (OCR), election law (DOJ Civil Rights Division), and environmental standards (EPA) means that partisan actors directly control whether these laws are enforced against partisan allies. The nonpartisan civil service is the enforcement mechanism for every other domain in this framework. Its destruction is not a parallel crisis — it is the administrative precondition for all the others.

**Time-critical windows**

- March 9, 2026: Schedule Policy/Career rule effective — reclassifications began
- AFGE litigation amended complaint filed March 4, 2026: APA challenge to Schedule Policy/Career has a strong Loper Bright argument; preliminary injunction ruling likely Q3 2026
- Saving the Civil Service Act (H.R. 1002 / S. 399): congressional calendar Q3–Q4 2026; midterm elections create a 2027 legislative window
- Federal workforce midterm organizing: 300,000+ terminated workers are a politically mobilized constituency through 2026

**Unique contribution**

No existing domain makes the affirmative democratic-design argument for the merit system. Domain 26 (Government Accountability) covers the institutional resilience of oversight mechanisms. Domain 29 (Prosecutorial Weaponization) covers DOJ capture. The civil-service-resilience document covers protective tactics. None of them argue that the Pendleton Act is a democracy statute — that merit hiring is the mechanism through which elections translate into neutral administration of law, and that its destruction is constitutionally equivalent to capturing the enforcement machinery of every election the opposition might win. This framing is new and is the highest-leverage reframing available to the civil service advocacy community, which currently argues in terms of employee rights rather than democratic design.

**Distribution targets**: Partnership for Public Service, American Federation of Government Employees (AFGE), National Treasury Employees Union (NTEU), National Academy of Public Administration, Brookings Governance Studies, Government Executive readers, congressional Government Operations committees

## Domain Candidate 2: Multilateral Withdrawal and the Domestic Democratic Cost of International Norm Abandonment

**Proposed number**: Domain 57

**Scope**

On January 7, 2026, President Trump signed Executive Order 14199 withdrawing the United States from 66 international organizations — 35 non-UN entities and 31 UN entities including UNFCCC, IPCC, UN Women, UNFPA, and UNCTAD. This followed the January 2025 WHO withdrawal and the February 2025 sanctions against ICC judges and prosecutors (Executive Order 14203). Taken together, these actions represent the most comprehensive US withdrawal from the post-WWII multilateral architecture since its construction.

The democratic-design argument is not primarily about global governance. It is about three domestic consequences that the framework does not currently address anywhere. First, the international democratic norms that the US helped build — universal suffrage, freedom of assembly, freedom of the press, equal protection — are now formally disowned as binding commitments. This removes the external accountability mechanism (treaty bodies, UN Human Rights Council review, ILO conventions) that democratic backsliding scholars consistently identify as a key constraint on authoritarian consolidation in states that remain formally democratic. Second, withdrawal from digital governance forums (World Summit on the Information Society, Internet Governance Forum, ITU) means the standards governing AI transparency, platform liability, and election-related content moderation are being set by China, the EU, and private companies without US government input — a direct consequence for every technology-and-democracy domain in the framework. Third, the ICC sanctions signal that individual accountability for atrocity crimes by state actors is no longer a US-backed norm — a shift that reverberates domestically through its implicit message about accountability for official conduct.

The 66-organization withdrawal touches every domain's international precedent sections: the EU AI Act (Domain 38 production), the EU DSA political advertising ban (Domain 40 production), and ILO Convention 87 on freedom of association (Domain 44 labor rights). This domain synthesizes those scattered international references into a coherent argument about what democratic legitimacy requires from international engagement.

**Causal pathway to democracy**

The pathway operates through two mechanisms. The first is accountability removal: international treaty bodies, periodic review processes, and multilateral forums are the external constituencies that constrain democratic erosion. Hungary's withdrawal from the Council of Europe's parliamentary assembly was a deliberate step in its democratic backsliding; the US trajectory is structurally analogous. The second is norm abandonment: the US withdrawal from international human rights instruments does not change domestic law immediately, but it signals to state-level actors — and to federal courts — that the interpretive community which treated international norms as relevant persuasive authority in constitutional litigation has been disavowed by the executive. The long-run effect on judicial interpretation of equal protection, due process, and First Amendment doctrine is not trivial.

**Time-critical windows**

- January 22, 2026: WHO withdrawal took effect — the one-year treaty period expired
- August 2, 2026: EU AI Act Article 50 enforcement (already covered in Domain 38 production, but this domain provides the broader institutional withdrawal context)
- November 2026 midterms: electoral accountability window for multilateral abandonment messaging
- Fall 2026 UN General Assembly: diplomatic isolation becomes visible at the world stage

**Unique contribution**

The framework currently addresses international dimensions only as precedent sections within individual domains (EU AI Act, Finnish media literacy, Ireland Citizens' Assembly). No domain makes the affirmative argument that multilateral engagement is a structural requirement of democratic legitimacy — that democracies that exit the institutions through which democratic norms are enforced globally are weakening the norms' enforceability domestically. This is a significant gap: the framework makes no case for why international institutions are democratically relevant to a domestic US audience. That argument requires its own domain.

**Distribution targets**: American Society of International Law (ASIL), Council on Foreign Relations, Brookings Foreign Policy, Human Rights First, Amnesty International USA, Global Engagement Initiative, CSIS, international law clinics at Georgetown and Yale Law

## Domain Candidate 3: Tribal Sovereignty as Pre-Constitutional Democratic Design

**Proposed number**: Domain 58 (canonicalizing the existing draft research document)

**Scope**

This candidate elevates an existing research draft — `domains/domain-38-tribal-sovereignty-indigenous-democratic-design.md` (produced May 13, 2026, ~5,200 words) — to a fully numbered, distribution-integrated production domain. The research is substantially complete; what is missing is the canonical number assignment, integration into `democratic-renewal-proposal.md` Part II, executive summary table entry, and distribution template creation.

The argument: the 574 federally recognized tribal nations are not racial minorities with special preferences. They are sovereign governments whose political relationship with the United States is established by 370 ratified treaties — the supreme law of the land under Article VI. The Indian Health Service, Bureau of Indian Affairs, and tribal self-governance programs funded under ISDEAA are not welfare programs; they are contracted performance of treaty obligations. Defunding them is not a budget decision — it is treaty breach.

The 2025–2026 crisis is documented in the draft: DOGE directed closure of 25+ BIA offices; IHS lost 1,000+ employees; at least 12 IHS facilities shuttered; FY2026 budget proposes $911M (24%) cut to tribal programs; the birthright citizenship EO produced Solicitor General Sauer's April 1, 2026 oral argument statement that Native Americans are "not birthright citizens" under the 14th Amendment — contradicting the Indian Citizenship Act of 1924 and 100 years of settled law.

The Callais VRA redistricting ruling (Domain 49) — which the Supreme Court issued in April/May 2026 — has specific, acute consequences for Native American voters in North Dakota, South Dakota, and Montana, where Section 2 litigation protecting tribal voting districts is now in limbo. This is not addressed in Domain 49 because that domain focuses on Louisiana and the standard VRA Section 2 framework; the tribal government-to-government dimension is legally distinct.

**Causal pathway to democracy**

Tribal sovereignty is the pre-constitutional democratic design case study: Native nations developed sophisticated governance structures before European contact, including the Haudenosaunee Confederacy (which influenced Benjamin Franklin's Albany Plan of Union and arguably elements of the federal structure). The federal government's current breach of treaty obligations is not just a human rights violation — it is an assault on 574 governments that exercise actual democratic authority over their own citizens. The IHS defunding reduces the healthcare capacity that produces the voter registration and turnout outcomes documented in Oregon RCT studies (7% turnout increase from Medicaid access). The BIA closures eliminate the land titling, real estate, and economic development infrastructure through which tribal governments exercise sovereignty. The citizenship-status threat directly attacks the franchise.

**Time-critical windows**

- June/July 2026: *Trump v. Barbara* SCOTUS ruling on birthright citizenship — if the court adopts even partial elements of Sauer's argument, the Indian Citizenship Act of 1924's status becomes legally contested for the first time in a century
- August 2026: BIA office closure process ongoing through summer; tribal litigation coalitions filing
- Fall 2026 appropriations: FY2027 budget markup with $911M proposed cut to tribal programs
- Turtle Mountain Band of Chippewa Indians v. Howe (25-253): SCOTUS cert petition on tribal voting rights — conference distribution ongoing

**Unique contribution**

The framework currently has no domain addressing tribal nations as a distinct democratic constituency. Domain 22 (Racial Equity) handles racial disparities; Domain 49 (Callais) handles VRA redistricting; Domain 39 production (Healthcare as Democratic Infrastructure) handles rural hospital closures. None of these makes the government-to-government argument: that tribal nations are not constituencies within the US democratic system but co-equal sovereigns whose governing capacity is directly threatened by federal defunding. This is a legally and constitutionally distinct argument that opens distribution pathways to NCAI, NARF, tribal gaming authorities, federal Indian law clinics, and the 13 states with large Native American voting populations — none of which is currently reachable through Phase 1 distribution.

**Distribution targets**: National Congress of American Indians (NCAI), Native American Rights Fund (NARF), tribal gaming authorities and councils, federal Indian law clinics (Michigan, Arizona, University of New Mexico), Indigenous-serving law reviews, Native Vote Alliance, Arizona State University Indian Legal Program

## Domain Candidate 4: Economic Precarity as Civic Exclusion Architecture

**Proposed number**: Domain 59

**Scope**

The framework covers the financial sector (Domain 41), housing instability (Domain 47), healthcare access (Domains 31, 39 production, 50), and Medicaid specifically (Domain 31). What it does not have is a domain that synthesizes these into a single unified argument: that the OBBBA's simultaneous cuts to Medicaid, SNAP, housing vouchers, and childcare represent a coordinated assault on the material conditions of civic participation for approximately 80 million Americans — and that these cuts operate through the same structural logic as formal voter suppression.

The empirical foundation is strong. Voter participation increases from 48% (lowest income quintile) to 86% (highest income quintile) — a 38-point gap that dwarfs the effects of individual voter suppression tactics. Economic insecurity is three times more strongly associated with civic disengagement than political membership (GJETA 2025). Government benefits are documented to increase civic participation, strengthen institutional trust, and improve rule-of-law compliance — taking them away does the opposite (Carnegie Endowment, January 2026). The OBBBA enacted on July 4, 2025 cut Medicaid, SNAP, housing assistance, and childcare subsidies simultaneously — a policy design that compounds civic exclusion across all material dimensions at once.

SNAP alone serves 42 million Americans; the OBBBA's work requirement expansion will reduce enrollment by an estimated 3.7 million (CBO). Food insecurity is a documented predictor of political disengagement: the cognitive bandwidth depletion from resource scarcity (Mullainathan-Shafir, Science 2013) reduces the attentional capacity for civic participation. Housing voucher cuts produce residential instability that severs the continuous address required for voter registration. Childcare subsidy reductions eliminate the time availability that enables meeting attendance, candidate forums, and voting.

The democratic design argument: these are not incidental consequences of budget policy. They are the predictable, documented outcomes of cutting civic participation infrastructure for the 40% of Americans who depend on public benefits for basic material security. The communities losing these benefits are the same communities subject to concentrated voter ID requirements, polling place closures, and voter roll purges documented across Domains 1, 33, and 37. The cumulative effect is a democratic system in which the bottom income quintile has a 38-point participation gap that policy compounds rather than alleviates.

**Causal pathway to democracy**

Two documented causal mechanisms. First: material deprivation reduces civic participation through time, cognitive bandwidth, and informational access constraints. The Mullainathan-Shafir bandwidth research quantifies this: poverty consumes cognitive resources equivalent to 13 IQ points of temporary diminishment. Second: benefits receipt increases civic trust and participation — Mettler's "submerged state" research and PMC 2771575 document that welfare-state programs that make beneficiaries feel like citizens rather than supplicants produce higher civic engagement. Cutting benefits does not merely remove an economic transfer — it removes the civic participation boost that the transfer produced.

**Time-critical windows**

- OBBBA enacted July 4, 2025 — implementation timelines cascade through 2026–2028 (Medicaid work requirements January 2027, SNAP changes 2026)
- Senate Democrats' forced floor votes on CFPB rollbacks announced May 13, 2026 — legislative engagement window
- November 2026 midterms: economic precarity is a direct organizing frame for benefit-losing constituencies
- HUD CoC NOFO expected June 1, 2026: housing advocacy window opens simultaneously

**Unique contribution**

Domain 41 (Consumer Financial Architecture) covers the financial sector's predatory extraction from low-income communities. Domain 47 (Housing Security) covers displacement. Domain 31 (Healthcare Access, OBBBA/Medicaid) covers the healthcare dimension. None of them makes the synthesizing argument: that the 2025 OBBBA was a coordinated assault on all material preconditions for civic participation simultaneously, and that the income participation gap is not a cultural or motivational problem but a structural democratic design failure requiring a universal basic civic participation infrastructure response — automatic voter registration, paid voting leave, election holiday, guaranteed civic capacity baseline. This synthesizing argument is the unique contribution of Domain 59, and it is the frame most likely to resonate with antipoverty organizations, labor unions, and faith coalitions that are not currently reachable through the framework's existing policy-technocratic framing.

**Distribution targets**: Center on Budget and Policy Priorities (CBPP), Food Research and Action Center (FRAC), Center for Law and Social Policy (CLASP), National Low Income Housing Coalition, Economic Policy Institute, Greater Good Science Center at UC Berkeley, Demos, Equitable Growth, faith coalitions (NETWORK Lobby for Catholic Social Justice, Sojourners)

## Priority Ranking

**Rank 1: Domain 58 — Tribal Sovereignty (canonicalization of existing draft)**

The research is already substantially done. The gap is structural — no canonical number, no proposal integration, no distribution template. Production cost is 8–12 hours to complete and integrate, not 40–60 hours to research from scratch. The *Trump v. Barbara* ruling is expected June–July 2026, which is both the hardest near-term deadline and the highest-visibility hook for distribution. No other domain in the framework creates a distribution pathway to NCAI, NARF, and the 13 states with substantial Native American voting populations. This is lowest-cost, highest-urgency, highest-distribution-expansion.

**Rank 2: Domain 56 — Civil Service Politicization**

The Schedule Policy/Career rule is already effective and has produced litigation. The Saving the Civil Service Act is in committee with a 2027 post-midterm legislative window. The democratic-design argument for the merit system is currently absent from every civil service reform advocacy effort — they argue in terms of employee rights, not democratic architecture. That reframing, in a domain document, is the highest-leverage contribution the framework can make to an advocacy community that has 50,000+ federal workers as an organized constituency. Estimated production: 40–50 hours from research to distribution-ready.

**Rank 3: Domain 59 — Economic Precarity as Civic Exclusion**

This is the highest-reach domain by constituency size — 42 million SNAP recipients, 80 million Medicaid beneficiaries, millions of housing voucher recipients. The antipoverty and faith coalition distribution pathways open constituencies not currently reachable. The synthesizing argument across OBBBA's simultaneous cuts is genuinely not available elsewhere in the framework. However, the empirical foundation spans multiple literatures (political science, behavioral economics, welfare state research) and will require more sourcing work than Domain 58. Estimated production: 50–60 hours.

**Rank 4: Domain 57 — Multilateral Withdrawal**

Structurally important and completely absent. But the domestic democratic consequences are more diffuse and harder to operationalize for advocacy than the other three domains. Distribution pathways are also the most specialized — international law academics and foreign policy organizations are not the framework's strongest adoption constituency. Best produced after the first three, with a fall 2026 UN General Assembly hook. Estimated production: 40–50 hours.
