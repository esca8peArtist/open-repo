## Executive Summary: Top 10 Evidence-Backed Tactics for the Current Moment

Ranked by impact potential and feasibility for ordinary citizens, drawing on the full body of civil resistance research:

1. **Electoral organizing at the local level** — School boards, state legislatures, city councils, and county offices are where policy is made and where individuals can actually win. The Tea Party gained 913 state legislative seats in five years through this focus. Low turnout (5-10%) in local elections means small organized groups have disproportionate influence.

2. **Sustained constituency pressure on elected officials** — The Indivisible movement's application of Tea Party tactics saved the Affordable Care Act in 2017 and contributed to the 2018 blue wave. Constituent calls, town halls, and sustained district-level pressure work, especially when coordinated.

3. **Strategic coalition building across ideological lines** — Movements win when they expand beyond their natural base. Research consistently shows that elite defection and broad coalition formation are among the strongest predictors of success. Unity around democratic norms (rather than policy preferences) is the most achievable cross-partisan goal.

4. **Strategic litigation** — Courts remain a powerful check, especially when they retain independence. The ACLU, NAACP LDF, Brennan Center, and state attorneys general have successfully challenged executive overreach, voting restrictions, and civil liberties violations. Supporting and using these mechanisms matters.

5. **Nonviolent direct action with strategic targeting** — Sustained campaigns, not one-day marches, move outcomes. Chenoweth's research shows nonviolent campaigns succeed at roughly twice the rate of violent ones. The key is choosing targets strategically using pillars-of-support analysis.

6. **Economic pressure (boycotts and divestment)** — The Montgomery Bus Boycott caused the city to lose an estimated $3,000/day. Boycotts work when they hit genuine economic interests, are sustained, and are coordinated. Divestment campaigns work primarily through reputational and political pressure rather than direct financial impact.

7. **Civil society and institutional resistance** — Professional associations, universities, unions, bar associations, and religious institutions have historically been critical anchors of resistance. Activating and supporting these institutions — and choosing to be a member of them — is high-leverage action.

8. **Information, narrative, and media** — Movements that control their narrative win. Citizen documentation, local journalism support, disciplined messaging, and counter-disinformation work all matter. The information environment is contested; presence and consistency in it counts.

9. **Mutual aid and community resilience** — Mutual aid networks serve dual functions: providing material support to community members and building the relational infrastructure that sustains political movements. They are organizing made material.

10. **Digital security and operational security** — Not a tactic in itself, but a precondition for all others under conditions of surveillance. Signal, 2FA, and basic operational security practices protect individuals and organizations from compromise.

## Part I: The Science of Resistance — What the Evidence Says

### 1.1 Nonviolent vs. Violent Resistance

#### Chenoweth and Stephan: The Landmark Research

Political scientists Erica Chenoweth (Harvard Kennedy School) and Maria Stephan conducted the most comprehensive empirical study of civil resistance in history, examining over 300 campaigns from 1900 to 2006. Their findings, published in *Why Civil Resistance Works* (2011), overturned conventional assumptions about the relative power of violent and nonviolent campaigns.

**Key findings**:
- Nonviolent campaigns succeeded in achieving their stated goals approximately **53% of the time**, compared to approximately **26% for violent campaigns** — roughly twice the success rate
- This held across regime type, geography, and historical period
- When extended to campaigns through 2019, over half of nonviolent campaigns succeeded, while only a quarter of violent ones did
- Countries where nonviolent campaigns occurred were **10 times more likely to transition to democracy** within five years compared to countries with violent campaigns — whether the campaigns succeeded or failed
- Civil resistance movements suffered casualties at a ratio of roughly **22:1** compared to armed uprisings (armed uprisings suffered far more casualties)

**Why nonviolence outperforms violence**:
1. **Participation breadth**: Nonviolent tactics allow participation by people who cannot or will not participate in violence — elderly people, children, those with jobs and families to protect, people with moral or religious objections. This dramatically expands the potential movement size.
2. **Elite defection**: Nonviolent campaigns are far more likely to induce defection among elites, security forces, and regime insiders. A soldier who refuses to fire on peaceful protesters is more likely than one asked to fight armed insurgents.
3. **Public sympathy**: Violence by protesters typically triggers public backlash; violence by the state against nonviolent protesters typically triggers public sympathy ("backfire effect").
4. **Legitimacy**: Nonviolent campaigns are harder for regimes to delegitimize as terrorist or criminal.
5. **Better post-conflict outcomes**: Nonviolent movements that succeed are more likely to produce stable democratic governments than violent ones.

#### The 3.5% Threshold

The most cited finding from Chenoweth's research is the **"3.5% rule"**: in the historical dataset studied, every campaign that achieved active participation from at least 3.5% of the total population ultimately succeeded. No campaign that crossed this threshold failed.

**What this means**:
- In the United States, 3.5% of the population is approximately **11.5 million people** — achievable but requires serious organizing
- Many campaigns succeeded with far fewer participants — the 3.5% is a near-guarantee, not a minimum requirement
- "Active participation" does not mean full-time activism; it includes showing up to demonstrations, strikes, boycotts, and other visible actions
- The rule applies to regime-change campaigns; for more limited policy goals, the threshold may be lower

**Important caveats**:
- Chenoweth has noted that both nonviolent and violent resistance have become **less effective since 2010**, partly because authoritarian regimes have learned from history, coordinated with each other, and trained security forces specifically to discourage defection
- Some recent scholarship suggests the comparative advantage of nonviolence may be smaller than early studies indicated when additional historical cases are included
- The rule applies to campaigns; individual tactics still need to be evaluated for their specific contexts

#### Nonviolence Is Not Passivity

A common misconception is that nonviolent resistance is the same as peaceful compliance or polite petition. It is not. Gene Sharp's taxonomy identifies 198 distinct methods of nonviolent action, including:
- Strikes and work stoppages
- Economic boycotts
- Civil disobedience and deliberate law-breaking
- Sit-ins and occupations
- Blockades
- Refusing to pay taxes
- Mass non-cooperation with state systems
- Symbolic acts designed to humiliate or expose opponents

The strategic logic of nonviolence is not moral — it is operational. Nonviolent discipline is maintained because it works strategically, not because it is intrinsically superior.

### 1.2 What Makes Movements Succeed or Fail

#### Key Success Factors

Research across hundreds of cases identifies a consistent set of conditions associated with movement success:

**1. Unity and discipline**
Movements with a unified, disciplined approach consistently outperform fragmented movements. This does not mean ideological uniformity — it means strategic coordination and maintenance of nonviolent discipline under pressure.

**2. Participation breadth — reaching beyond the base**
The single most important predictor of success is the ability to recruit participants from outside the natural base of the movement. When a movement is perceived as representing only one demographic, party, or ideological faction, it is easier for opponents to frame and isolate it. Cross-cutting coalitions that include unlikely allies are far more threatening to any power structure.

**3. Elite defection**
Perhaps the most operationally important variable: when business elites, military officers, police, civil servants, or members of the opposition party withdraw support from the regime or government, that withdrawal dramatically increases the probability of movement success. Research suggests elite support/opposition accounts for up to 80% of the variance in outcomes. This is why movements should always think about what would cause key actors to defect or withdraw cooperation.

**4. Strategic planning and clear demands**
Movements with clear, specific, achievable demands consistently outperform those with diffuse grievances. The demand needs to be understandable, legitimate, and actionable — something that a specific actor can actually do or stop doing.

**5. Pillars of support analysis**
Gene Sharp's foundational insight: no regime or power structure maintains itself alone. It depends on "pillars of support" — institutions and groups whose cooperation is necessary: the military, police, civil service, business community, media, religious institutions, trade unions, professional associations. When these pillars are eroded or shift allegiance, the power structure cannot hold. Effective movements identify which pillars to target and in what sequence.

**6. Dilemma actions**
The most effective tactics put opponents in a "lose-lose" position: if they respond with repression, they create sympathy for the movement and recruit new participants; if they don't respond, the movement advances. The sit-ins of the Civil Rights Movement were classic dilemma actions: police who arrested protesters became recruiting tools for the movement; police who allowed protesters to sit unmolested conceded the ground.

#### Common Failure Modes

**Fragmentation and "diversity of tactics"**
Movements that cannot maintain strategic and tactical unity are vulnerable to marginalization, co-optation, and defeat. The "diversity of tactics" argument — that movements should embrace both violent and nonviolent approaches — has historically been used to justify violence that alienates the broader public and gives regimes justification for repression. The research is clear: tactical discipline around nonviolence significantly improves success rates.

**Co-optation**
When powerful actors offer movement leaders positions, resources, or partial concessions in exchange for demobilizing the broader movement, this is co-optation. It has defeated many promising movements. The countermeasure is distributed leadership and clear criteria for what counts as success before entering any negotiations.

**Leader capture**
Movements built around charismatic individual leaders are vulnerable to decapitation — arrest, assassination, discrediting, or buyout of the leader stops the movement. Decentralized, distributed leadership structures are more resilient, though harder to coordinate.

**Social media as substitute for organizing**
Online engagement creates the feeling of participation without the reality of power. Millions of social media shares do not translate to power unless they are connected to organizational structures capable of taking sustained action. Likes are not leverage.

**Reactive positioning**
Movements that respond to their opponent's agenda rather than setting their own agenda lose the initiative and exhaust their participants with constant emergency responses. Proactive movements set their own timeline and target selection.

**Violence by movement members**
Even when state violence provokes it, movement violence shifts public attention from the state's behavior to the movement's, reduces sympathy, gives authorities justification for repression, and filters out participants who cannot engage in violence (reducing breadth). The evidence on this point is robust.

**Lack of sustainability planning**
Protest energy is episodic; political change is gradual. Movements that cannot transition from mobilization to sustained organizational work tend to exhaust themselves and dissipate. Infrastructure — organizations, training programs, funding, institutional relationships — is what converts protest energy into durable power.

### 1.3 Modern Landscape — Unique Challenges and Opportunities

#### How the Authoritarian Playbook Has Evolved

Contemporary democratic backsliding rarely looks like 20th-century coups. Modern authoritarian consolidation typically follows a pattern of **autocratic legalism** — using legal and institutional mechanisms to dismantle democratic safeguards from within:

1. **Capture the courts**: Fill judicial appointments with loyalists; use courts to legitimize power grabs
2. **Erase independent institutions**: Convert civil service, inspectors general, independent agencies into tools of the ruling faction
3. **Weaponize law enforcement**: Prosecute opponents using selective enforcement; protect allies from accountability
4. **Control the information environment**: Buy or pressure media; accelerate propaganda through state-adjacent outlets; seed disinformation
5. **Tilt the electoral playing field**: Change voting rules, electoral maps, administration, and rules about who can participate in elections
6. **Delegitimize opposition**: Label critics as traitors, criminals, terrorists, or foreign agents
7. **Legal harassment**: Use regulatory power, tax enforcement, and litigation to exhaust and financially drain opposition organizations

This playbook has been executed in Hungary, Turkey, Venezuela, and Russia. Political scientists Anna Grzymala-Busse, Levitsky and Ziblatt (*How Democracies Die*), and Protect Democracy's "Authoritarian Playbook" document its specific features in the American context.

**The critical window**: V-Dem research shows there is a critical period before electoral processes and institutional constraints are fully captured during which resistance is significantly more likely to succeed. After capture, resistance must confront rather than prevent dictatorship — a much harder task.

#### The Changed Information Environment

The contemporary information environment presents both threats and opportunities:

**Threats**:
- Algorithmic amplification rewards outrage and extremism, making moderate, evidence-based movements harder to sustain in online spaces
- Disinformation spreads faster than correction and is specifically weaponized against resistance movements
- Social media platforms are owned by private actors with their own political and financial interests
- Filter bubbles reduce the ability to reach across ideological lines
- Movements can be infiltrated, misrepresented, or co-opted in the online space with low cost

**Opportunities**:
- Lower cost of organizing and communication than any previous era in history
- Real-time documentation and distribution of state violence and abuse
- Global solidarity networks and real-time learning across movements
- Decentralized organizing reduces dependence on formal organizations
- AI tools for translation, legal research, campaign analytics, and communication

#### Surveillance Capabilities

State surveillance capabilities have expanded dramatically:
- Cell phones are location tracking devices by default
- Social media platforms collect and share data with law enforcement
- Facial recognition technology is deployed in many cities
- Financial transaction monitoring can identify and map movement participants
- Foreign governments (and domestic actors) use commercial spyware (e.g., Pegasus) against activists
- License plate readers, CCTV networks, and stingray devices track physical movement

This does not mean organizing is impossible — the Civil Rights Movement operated under FBI surveillance throughout its most effective period — but it means threat modeling and operational security are legitimate strategic concerns, not paranoia.

## Part II: The Tactics Toolkit — Evidence and Application

### 2.1 Electoral Organizing (Highest Long-Term Impact)

#### Why Local Elections Are the Critical Battleground

The most underappreciated fact in American civic life: **turnout in local elections is typically 5-15%**. This means that small, organized groups can swing outcomes that affect millions of people with a few hundred or thousand votes.

**What local offices control**:
- School boards: curriculum, library book policies, sex education, hiring of teachers and administrators, budgets affecting tens of millions of students
- City councils: policing policy, zoning and housing, local labor law, public health, local budget priorities
- County commissions: election administration in many states, property tax rates, county-level law enforcement
- State legislatures: congressional district maps, voting rules, abortion law, labor law, gun law, education policy, the vast majority of criminal law
- Attorneys general: prosecution discretion, civil rights enforcement, consumer protection
- Secretaries of State / election officials: administration of elections in most states
- Judges: many states elect judges at multiple levels, with enormous consequences

**The Tea Party model, applicable across the spectrum**: Beginning in 2009-2010, Tea Party organizers focused on sleepy primaries — school board races, state legislative primaries — where they knew the electorate would be small and where a disciplined minority could win. Between 2010 and 2015, Republicans gained a net of 913 state legislative seats. The redistricting power gained through state legislative control in 2010 shaped congressional maps for the following decade.

**Indivisible's evidence-based approach**: The Indivisible Guide (written by former congressional staffers after the 2016 election) taught thousands of local groups to apply consistent constituent pressure on their specific representatives. The result was a major factor in saving the Affordable Care Act in 2017 and contributed to Democratic gains in 2018 that produced the largest midterm margins in history.

#### Specific Electoral Tactics

**Voter registration and turnout**
- Same-day voter registration increases youth turnout by 3.1-7.3 percentage points
- Automatic voter registration increases young voter turnout by 3.2%
- Targeted get-out-the-vote contact (door-to-door canvassing) has a well-documented turnout effect of roughly 6-8 percentage points per conversation
- Phone banking has a smaller but measurable effect; text banking is lower still
- Priority: focus registration and turnout efforts in low-turnout elections where the marginal voter has highest impact (primaries, school boards, special elections)

**Candidate recruitment**
- Run for Something has elected 637+ candidates to local office since 2017
- Emerge America has trained 7,000+ Democratic women; 1,200+ alumnae serve in elected office
- The most important question for any activist: "Who is running for my school board? My state legislature? Do I know them? Have I volunteered?"
- Many local offices run essentially uncontested — a qualified candidate filing is itself a victory

**Precinct organizing**
- The precinct is the fundamental unit of electoral politics. Becoming a precinct captain or precinct committee person gives formal standing in party decision-making (including primary candidate endorsements and platform)
- Most precincts have captain positions that go unfilled; the organizing opportunity is enormous

### 2.2 Sustained Nonviolent Protest and Direct Action

#### What the Evidence Shows About Protest Effectiveness

A 2019 study in *American Politics Research* found that protest size has a causal impact on outcomes — larger protests lead to higher probability of policy change. But there is a critical distinction between:

**One-day marches**: Effective for visibility, participant mobilization, and media coverage, but do not themselves produce policy change. The 2017 Women's March (estimated 3-5 million participants in the United States alone) is the largest single-day protest in American history. It did not, by itself, produce legislation — but it demonstrated a reservoir of mobilized sentiment and energized ongoing organizing.

**Sustained campaigns**: The Civil Rights Movement's Montgomery Bus Boycott lasted 381 days. The Solidarity movement in Poland sustained pressure for a decade. The Velvet Revolution required sustained escalation over weeks. Sustained campaigns force their targets into ongoing economic or reputational costs that eventually become untenable.

**Strategic targeting**: Effective campaigns identify which specific actor has the power to grant the specific demand, and target that actor. Marching past a non-decision-maker accomplishes less than blocking access to a decision-maker's office. The "pillars of support" framework asks: who does this target depend on? Target those dependencies.

#### Civil Disobedience: When and How

Civil disobedience — deliberate, public, nonviolent law-breaking as a form of protest — has a documented effectiveness under specific conditions:
- The specific law being broken must be connected to the injustice being protested (or the disobedience must directly disrupt the activity being protested)
- Participants must be willing to accept legal consequences — this acceptance demonstrates commitment and creates the "backfire" dynamic
- It is most effective when it reveals the moral bankruptcy of the law or the enforcement response
- It is least effective when violence occurs or when the act is perceived as purely disruptive to ordinary people rather than to power

Classic examples: Lunch counter sit-ins (Civil Rights), Salt March (India), draft card burning (Vietnam), civil disobedience at nuclear facilities.

**Legal rights during civil disobedience**: Participants in civil disobedience should understand their rights and the likely legal consequences before engaging. National Lawyers Guild provides legal observer presence at protests and operates a Legal Support Hotline: 212-679-6018 (national); local chapters have local numbers.

#### Strikes and Work Stoppages

Labor strikes remain one of the most powerful nonviolent tools, but operate in a constrained legal environment in the United States:

- The Taft-Hartley Act of 1947 prohibits strikes on political grounds and "secondary boycotts"
- General strikes (affecting multiple industries) have occurred rarely in U.S. history (Seattle 1919, Oakland 1946) but have been decisive in other countries
- Wildcat strikes (unauthorized by union leadership) carry legal risk for participants
- Unions with existing collective bargaining agreements are generally limited to striking over economic issues during contract disputes
- Nonunion workers can engage in concerted action under the NLRA but have weaker protections

**Historical power of general strikes**: The 1905 Russian general strike forced Tsar Nicholas II to issue the October Manifesto; the 1920 Kapp Putsch in Germany was defeated by a general strike called by the Social Democratic Party; Polish strikes were integral to the Solidarity movement's success.

#### Blockades, Sit-Ins, Occupations

These tactics directly impose costs on targets and create dilemma situations:
- Work when the blocked or occupied location is genuinely important to the target
- Create the "backfire" dynamic when state or employer response is disproportionate
- Require sustained participant commitment; short-term actions are more manageable for participants
- Legal risks vary significantly by jurisdiction and specific conduct

### 2.3 Economic Pressure

#### Boycotts: Evidence on Effectiveness

Boycotts work through two mechanisms: direct economic harm and reputational harm. The evidence is strongest when both are in play.

**Montgomery Bus Boycott (1955-1956)**: The African American community constituted over 70% of the city's bus ridership. The boycott was 90% effective from its first day and caused an estimated $3,000/day in lost fares (enormous in 1950s dollars). Combined with legal action (Browder v. Gayle), it forced desegregation of the Montgomery bus system after 381 days.

**Conditions for boycott effectiveness**:
1. The target genuinely depends on the boycotting population's spending (economic leverage exists)
2. The boycott is sustained and coordinated, not a one-time event
3. The demand is specific and achievable — the target can actually do what is being demanded
4. There is a path to declaring victory and ending the boycott
5. The boycott receives media coverage that amplifies reputational harm

**Conditions where boycotts underperform**:
- When the targeted company's customers are not the same as the movement's base
- When the demand is diffuse or impossible for the company to meet alone
- When consumers have no practical alternative to the boycotted product
- When the boycott fades after initial momentum without an organizational structure to sustain it

**Modern examples**: The 2013 Boy Scouts of America boycott contributed to the organization reversing its ban on gay scouts; the Komen/Planned Parenthood controversy in 2012 resulted in rapid reversal after boycott threats; Montgomery's pattern has been replicated in numerous successful civil rights era and subsequent boycotts.

#### Divestment Campaigns

Divestment campaigns target institutional investors (universities, pension funds, foundations) rather than consumers, and work primarily through reputational and political pressure rather than direct financial impact.

**Evidence of effectiveness**: Mixed. The South African divestment campaign contributed to the moral and political isolation of the apartheid regime and strengthened the internal anti-apartheid movement, even though studies show the direct financial impact on South African companies was limited. Fossil fuel divestment campaigns have committed over $15 trillion in pledged divestment from various institutions (as of recent estimates), with growing reputational impact.

**More effective approach**: Divestment campaigns are more effective as political organizing tools — forcing institutions to take public positions, creating internal debates, and building political alliances — than as direct economic levers.

#### Shareholder Activism

Institutional investors and even individual shareholders can file resolutions, speak at annual meetings, and pressure corporate boards on governance, political spending, and social policy. Organizations like As You Sow and ShareAction coordinate shareholder activism across portfolios.

### 2.4 Legal and Institutional Resistance

#### Strategic Litigation

Strategic litigation — using courts to challenge specific actions, policies, or laws — has been one of the most consistently effective tools of resistance in American history.

**How it works**: Organizations like the ACLU, NAACP Legal Defense Fund, Brennan Center for Justice, Lambda Legal, and Earthjustice identify legally vulnerable government actions, find appropriate plaintiffs, and bring carefully crafted cases designed to establish or reinforce legal principles that constrain the government.

**Key examples**:
- *Brown v. Board of Education* (1954): Result of a decades-long NAACP LDF litigation strategy beginning in the 1930s with cases designed to expose the inherent inequality of "separate but equal"
- Voting rights litigation: The ACLU and NAACP have successfully challenged dozens of voter suppression laws in federal courts
- ACLU challenges to Muslim ban, immigration orders, and civil liberties violations during the Trump administration resulted in numerous temporary restraining orders and injunctions

**How ordinary citizens contribute**:
- Donate to strategic litigation organizations
- Report civil liberties violations to relevant organizations (ACLU has intake at aclu.org)
- Document incidents that may be legally actionable
- If you are an attorney, consider pro bono work through ACLU, NAACP, or legal aid organizations

#### State Attorneys General as Resistance Actors

State attorneys general have broad power to challenge federal actions that harm state residents. During the Trump administration (2017-2021), Democratic state attorneys general filed over 100 lawsuits against federal executive actions. This "litigation resistance" produced real results — many Trump administration policies were enjoined or reversed by federal courts in response to state AG lawsuits.

**How to support**: AG campaigns (most AGs are elected); advocacy to your AG's office on specific issues; coalition letters signed by multiple AGs are more impactful than individual state actions.

#### Sanctuary Policies

State and local "sanctuary" policies limit cooperation with federal enforcement of laws that residents and governments object to. These policies operate on the constitutional principle that the federal government cannot **commandeer** state and local government officials to enforce federal law (Printz v. United States, 1997).

**Legal basis**: The 10th Amendment's anti-commandeering principle has been repeatedly upheld by federal courts, including in challenges to sanctuary policies. Local jurisdictions can refuse to honor federal immigration detainers without being compelled to cooperate.

**Scope**: Sanctuary-type policies have been used for immigration enforcement, marijuana enforcement (in states with legal marijuana), and are being developed for other contexts (e.g., states declining to cooperate with enforcement of laws that criminalize medical care provided in the state).

**Limitations**: Sanctuary policies cannot prevent federal officers from operating in the jurisdiction; they can only limit local cooperation. They are most effective when the federal government lacks the resources to enforce a policy without local assistance.

#### Administrative Law Challenges

Federal agencies must follow the Administrative Procedure Act (APA) when making rules. Challenges to rules on the grounds that they are "arbitrary and capricious" or not supported by the administrative record have been highly effective. Organizations track regulatory actions and challenge those that exceed agency authority or ignore required procedures.

### 2.5 Political Organizing and Coalition Building

#### The Science of Coalition Building

Research on social movement coalitions identifies consistent principles:

**What works**:
- Coalitions formed around concrete, shared threats (rather than shared policy preferences) are more durable
- Pre-existing relationships and organizational ties between groups dramatically increase coalition effectiveness
- Distributed leadership that represents the full breadth of the coalition — not just the founding organizations
- Explicit processes for managing internal conflict and addressing power imbalances within the coalition
- Clear shared principles alongside space for disagreement on other issues

**What doesn't work**:
- Demanding ideological conformity as a condition of coalition membership — this limits the coalition's breadth, which is its primary source of power
- Allowing the best-resourced or most prominent organizations to dominate coalition decisions
- Failing to build relationships and trust before a crisis — coalitions built under pressure are weaker than those built in relative calm

**Cross-partisan coalitions**
The most successful democratic defense coalitions unite around shared democratic norms (rule of law, free and fair elections, press freedom, checks and balances) rather than shared policy preferences. Conservative lawyers, libertarians, traditional Republicans, and former military officers have historically been crucial allies in such coalitions — they provide legitimacy that purely left or partisan coalitions lack.

Organizations doing this work include Protect Democracy, States United Democracy Center, and various Republican Voters Against Trump-type formations.

#### Precinct-Level Organizing

The precinct committee model — in which party volunteers take responsibility for voter contact in specific small geographic units — is the most durable structure for political organizing. Becoming a precinct committee person or captain provides:
- Formal standing in party decisions including primary endorsements
- A specific geographic accountability structure
- Access to voter data and campaign infrastructure
- A pipeline to local party leadership positions

Most parties have vacant precinct positions. Filling them is one of the highest-leverage individual civic actions available.

#### Issue Framing: Universal vs. Particular

Research on political messaging consistently shows:
- **Universal framing** (values everyone shares — fairness, freedom, security) attracts broader coalitions than **particular framing** (benefits to specific groups)
- **Values-based communication** that speaks to audiences' core moral frameworks is more persuasive than fact-based communication alone
- **Narrative framing** (stories about specific people) is more emotionally resonant than abstract policy argument
- **Offensive framing** (stating what you stand for) outperforms **defensive framing** (denying what you're accused of) — "going on the offensive changed the game and allowed advocates to reset the terms of debate" (Opportunity Agenda research)

### 2.6 Information, Narrative, and Media

#### Controlling the Narrative

Movements that define the conflict on their terms win more often than those that respond to the opponent's framing. Practical implications:
- Establish and repeat a clear, simple message that captures the essential moral stakes
- Choose spokespeople who embody the message (trusted messengers from the communities most affected)
- Don't let opponents set the terms of debate — reframe rather than rebut
- Maintain message discipline across the coalition: different groups can say different things, but not contradictory things

#### Local Journalism as Infrastructure

Local journalism has been devastated by economic disruption: more than 2,500 local newspapers have closed since 2005; many communities are now "news deserts" with no professional accountability journalism covering local government.

This matters for resistance because local journalism is the primary accountability mechanism for local government — city councils, school boards, county commissions, and state legislatures operate with little scrutiny in the absence of local reporters. Supporting local investigative journalism (subscribing, donating, submitting tips) is civic infrastructure work.

Organizations doing this work: Local Independent Online News (LION) Publishers, INN (Institute for Nonprofit News), Report for America.

#### Citizen Documentation

Documenting government actions — including abuses — is itself a form of resistance and accountability. Practical guidance:
- Know your state's laws on recording police and public officials (most states permit recording in public spaces; some require consent for audio recording)
- Upload and back up documentation immediately to cloud storage
- Provide documentation to journalists, legal organizations, and civil rights groups with context
- Legal observers (trained by National Lawyers Guild) serve a specialized documentation function at protests

#### Counter-Disinformation

Disinformation is not countered effectively by simply debunking individual false claims — corrections spread more slowly than the original disinformation and often reinforce the claim by repeating it. More effective approaches:
- "Prebunking" — warning audiences about specific disinformation tactics before they encounter them
- Elevating authoritative voices and trusted messengers rather than amplifying disinformation by responding to it
- Building trusted community information networks (local newsletters, community relationships) that provide alternative information infrastructure
- Reporting disinformation to platform trust and safety teams

### 2.7 Civil Society and Institutional Resistance

#### The Role of Institutions

Civil society institutions — churches, unions, universities, bar associations, medical associations, professional organizations — are critical infrastructure for democratic societies. They:
- Provide organizational capacity that is not dependent on government
- Aggregate and represent the interests of specific communities
- Provide moral and political legitimacy to positions they endorse
- Create solidarity networks that sustain individuals under pressure
- Serve as "pillars of support" that, when activated, can shift political outcomes

**Historical examples**: The Catholic Church in Poland was essential to Solidarity's ability to operate under martial law — providing meeting space, communication networks, and moral legitimacy. Labor unions were the organizational backbone of the Civil Rights Movement's economic pressure campaigns. Bar associations have stood against executive attacks on judicial independence in multiple countries.

**Practical implication**: Join and participate in civil society institutions relevant to your professional or community identity. Advocate for those institutions to take positions consistent with democratic values. Make them part of the broader coalition.

#### Professional Association Resistance

Specific professional groups have outsized influence:
- **Lawyers and bar associations**: The legal profession has particular standing to resist judicial capture and executive overreach. The American Bar Association, state bar associations, and law school faculties have historically been voices for rule-of-law norms. If you are an attorney, your voice in bar associations matters.
- **Medical and health professionals**: Played critical roles in resisting authoritarian policies in multiple countries, providing care to activists, and speaking with moral authority about the health consequences of policy.
- **Judges and former judges**: Statements from retired judges carry significant weight in rule-of-law debates.
- **Military and national security veterans**: Veterans and retired officers who speak out against authoritarian overreach carry legitimacy that civilian critics lack.
- **Academics and scientists**: Defending academic freedom and scientific integrity is both practically important (protecting climate science, public health, independent economic analysis) and symbolically significant.

#### Bureaucratic Resistance

Civil servants, many of whom are appointed or protected by civil service rules, have agency in how they execute their duties. This includes:
- **Meticulous documentation**: Creating contemporaneous records of illegal or unethical orders; preserving institutional memory; filing required reports even under pressure not to
- **Using internal processes**: Inspector general complaints, congressional notifications, administrative challenges
- **Whistleblowing**: Protected under federal law (though protections have been weakened) and state whistleblower statutes; the Government Accountability Project and National Whistleblower Center provide support
- **Slow walking**: Legitimate procedural compliance requirements can slow the implementation of policies; career professionals who understand regulatory processes can ensure legal requirements are met
- **Working with oversight bodies**: Cooperating with congressional oversight, inspectors general, and GAO investigations

Whistleblowing is high-risk. Mark Felt's disclosure contributed to Nixon's resignation; Daniel Ellsberg's Pentagon Papers disclosure changed the Vietnam War debate; Edward Snowden's NSA disclosures informed public debate. All faced severe personal consequences. Organizations like the Government Accountability Project and National Whistleblower Center provide legal support.

### 2.8 Mutual Aid and Community Resilience

#### Beyond Emergency Relief

Mutual aid — voluntary, non-hierarchical networks of community support — serves two functions simultaneously:
1. **Immediate material support**: Food, housing assistance, medical care, childcare, bail funds, transportation
2. **Organizational infrastructure**: Mutual aid networks build the trust, relationships, and capacity that sustain political organizing

**Historical examples**:
- The Black Panther Party's "survival programs" (free breakfast programs, health clinics) were explicitly political in intent — building community relationships and demonstrating that community self-organization could provide what the state refused to
- During the AIDS crisis, LGBT+ mutual aid networks provided medical and social support when the government refused to act, and also built the organizational infrastructure for political advocacy
- In Puerto Rico after Hurricane Maria, the Caguas kitchen network built during austerity protests became the infrastructure for post-hurricane survival and subsequent political organizing
- COVID-19 mutual aid networks organized in thousands of communities, providing food, medicine delivery, and social connection — and many have evolved into ongoing political organizations

**How to start or join a mutual aid network**:
- mutualaidhub.org provides a directory of existing networks
- Dean Spade's *Mutual Aid: Building Solidarity During This Crisis (and the Next)* is the definitive guide
- Start with one specific, concrete thing you can offer (a car for rides, a free grocery run) and build from there

### 2.9 Digital and Technological Resistance

#### Secure Communications

The single most important digital security step for activists is **using Signal** for sensitive communications. Signal uses end-to-end encryption (only the sender and recipient can read messages), does not store metadata in a form accessible to law enforcement, is open-source and regularly audited, and is used by journalists, activists, and security professionals worldwide.

**Why Signal specifically**:
- WhatsApp also uses end-to-end encryption but is owned by Meta (Facebook) and retains metadata
- iMessage is encrypted but only between Apple devices and is accessible to Apple under legal process
- SMS/text messages are completely unencrypted and accessible to carriers and law enforcement
- Signal's encryption "ratchets" — meaning even temporary compromise of a device does not expose past messages

**Essential setup steps**:
1. Download Signal from signal.org (avoid third-party app stores)
2. Enable disappearing messages by default (prevents long-term retention of conversation history)
3. Set a PIN or passphrase for your account
4. Use Signal's "Note to Self" function for sensitive information storage instead of standard notes apps
5. Enable screen security so Signal content doesn't appear in app switchers

**Other security essentials**:
- Enable two-factor authentication (2FA) on all important accounts; use hardware keys (YubiKey) or authenticator apps (Authy, Google Authenticator) rather than SMS-based 2FA when possible
- Full-disk encryption on laptops (FileVault on Mac, BitLocker on Windows, enabled by default on modern iPhones and Android)
- Use a password manager (Bitwarden is free and open-source) to generate and store unique passwords for every account
- Keep software updated — most successful attacks exploit known vulnerabilities for which patches exist

#### Threat Modeling

Not everyone faces the same threats. Threat modeling means identifying your specific situation:
- **Who is your threat actor?** A local activist facing stalking faces different threats than a journalist covering national security.
- **What are you trying to protect?** Your identity? Your communications? Your location history? Your financial information?
- **What is the consequence of exposure?** Job loss? Physical danger? Legal risk? Harassment?
- **What is the likelihood of each threat?**

For most activists, the threat actors are: local law enforcement, federal law enforcement, private actors (employers, political opponents), and online harassers. Pegasus-level nation-state surveillance is a real concern for a small number of high-profile targets. Basic digital hygiene protects against most threats most activists face.

#### Decentralized Organizing

Platform risk — the possibility that a platform will ban an organizing community, comply with law enforcement requests, or simply shut down — is a real operational threat. Countermeasures:
- Maintain contact information for key participants outside of any single platform
- Use federated or self-hosted communication tools (Element/Matrix, Mastodon) for critical communications
- Build email lists as a backup to social media following
- Avoid concentrating critical organizing capacity in any single platform

#### Digital Archiving

Documents, records, and websites can be deleted or disappear. Proactive archiving:
- The Internet Archive's Wayback Machine (web.archive.org) allows saving snapshots of web pages
- PACER records can be downloaded before they become inaccessible
- Government data and reports should be downloaded and backed up when identified as at risk
- Tools like HTTrack and Wget can archive entire websites
- Harvard's Library Innovation Lab and other institutions have undertaken proactive archiving of government data

### 2.10 International Solidarity and Pressure

#### How International Attention Protects Resistance

International attention is not merely symbolic — it provides real protective value:
- Journalists and monitors from international organizations are harder for governments to disappear or imprison without cost
- International economic and diplomatic relationships create leverage — governments depend on these and can lose them if their domestic behavior is sufficiently egregious
- Diaspora communities serve as advocates in their adopted countries and can mobilize foreign policy institutions

**South Africa model**: The international anti-apartheid movement (sports boycotts, divestment campaigns, sanctions) created the economic and reputational costs that made the apartheid regime's position ultimately unsustainable. International pressure did not alone end apartheid, but it strengthened internal resistance and eroded the regime's ability to present itself as legitimate.

**Documentation and reporting**:
- Report human rights abuses to UN special rapporteurs, Inter-American Commission on Human Rights, and international human rights organizations (Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International)
- Submit documentation to foreign government human rights bodies (EU institutions, foreign parliaments)
- Build relationships with foreign journalists covering the country

## Part III: Strategic Framework — Applying Tactics Today

### 3.1 Pillars of Support Analysis

Every power structure depends on a set of institutions and actors whose ongoing cooperation is necessary. Sharp identified typical pillars including:
- **Authority**: The perceived right to rule and give orders
- **Human resources**: The people who obey, cooperate, and implement decisions
- **Skills and knowledge**: Expertise that the ruler needs and that others provide
- **Intangible factors**: Habits of obedience, ideology, cultural beliefs
- **Material resources**: Property, natural resources, financial resources, economic system
- **Sanctions**: Rewards and punishments that enforce compliance

**Practical application**: Before choosing tactics, ask:
1. What specific outcome do we want?
2. Who has the power to produce that outcome?
3. What does that actor depend on to maintain power?
4. Which of those dependencies can we affect?
5. Who or what would need to shift for the target to be compelled to act?

**Sequencing**: It is rarely effective to try to erode all pillars simultaneously. Identifying the most vulnerable pillar and concentrating effort there often produces cascading effects. In many resistance scenarios, the key question is: what would cause business elites, senior civil servants, or members of the judiciary to withdraw support or take a stand?

### 3.2 Avoiding the Most Common Mistakes

#### Tactical Diversity Without Strategic Unity

The "diversity of tactics" debate — whether movements should embrace both violent and nonviolent approaches — has been one of the most damaging debates in social movement history. The research is clear: tactical diversity that includes violence reduces success rates, reduces participation breadth, and gives opponents justification for repression.

This does not mean all tactics must be identical. It means that the movement must maintain consensus discipline around the strategic approach, especially the commitment to nonviolence, because defection from that discipline has predictably negative consequences.

#### Social Media Is Not Organizing

Social media is a useful communication tool, but it is not organizing. Organizing means:
- Building relationships of trust with specific people
- Creating structures through which those people can take coordinated action
- Developing leadership capacity in those people
- Maintaining organizational infrastructure between campaigns

Social media can support these things, but it cannot substitute for them. A million social media followers with no off-platform organizational structure can be neutralized by a platform policy change or an algorithm update. A hundred deeply organized precinct committees cannot be.

#### Leader Capture

Charismatic individual leaders make movements legible to media and mobilize participants emotionally. They are also single points of failure — arrest, discrediting, assassination, or buyout of a leader can stop a movement organized around that leader. Countermeasures:
- Distributed leadership structures that do not depend on any single individual
- Leadership development programs that create depth
- Explicit succession planning
- Organizational procedures that function without specific leaders present

The Civil Rights Movement is often remembered as Martin Luther King Jr.'s movement. In reality, it had thousands of local leaders, organizational structures at the national and local level, and continued after King's assassination in 1968 (though without him it was different).

#### Reactive Positioning

Authoritarian playbooks often deliberately overwhelm defenders with volume — so many attacks, so rapidly, that defenders exhaust themselves responding to each one. The antidote is proactive positioning: choose the terrain, set the agenda, force opponents into defending rather than attacking.

This means identifying the two or three most important long-term goals and pursuing them consistently, rather than responding to every provocation. It means maintaining the initiative rather than surrendering it.

#### The Co-optation Trap

When power structures offer partial concessions or position for movement leaders in exchange for demobilizing the broader movement, this is co-optation. Tactics:
- Establish specific, public criteria for what counts as success before entering negotiations
- Maintain the ability to escalate — don't trade away leverage before receiving real concessions
- Require transparency with the broader movement about any negotiations
- Distinguish between tactical concessions (accepting less than full demand to continue building) and co-optation (accepting positions and resources in exchange for demobilization)

### 3.3 Building for the Long Term

#### The 10-Year Perspective

The right-wing infrastructure built after the 1971 Powell Memo provides the clearest model of what sustained long-term investment in political infrastructure produces. The Heritage Foundation, ALEC, the Federalist Society, and a network of state-level think tanks spent decades building:
- Legal talent pipelines (Federalist Society law school chapters, clerkship networks)
- Legislative templates (ALEC's model bills)
- Policy ideas ready to implement when political windows open (Heritage's "Mandate for Leadership")
- Communication infrastructure (think tanks, media outlets, training programs)
- Donor networks with long time horizons

The result: when political opportunities opened, the movement had the talent, ideas, legal frameworks, and legislation ready. The lesson for any movement: **infrastructure built between crises enables action during crises**.

Progressive equivalents being built:
- State Innovation Exchange (SiX): Progressive counterpart to ALEC, providing model legislation and support to progressive state legislators
- Wellstone Action: Political training for progressive candidates and organizers
- Movement School: Free online training for activists
- Blueprint: Colorado model of long-term statewide political infrastructure investment

#### Training Programs

Investment in training is investment in durable capacity:
- **Indivisible Guide** (indivisible.org/resource/guide/): Free guide to constituent advocacy
- **Wellstone Action** (wellstone.org): Progressive political training programs
- **Movement School** (movementschool.us): Free online training for organizers and activists
- **National Training Initiative** (various): Electoral and civic training programs
- **She Should Run** (sheshouldrun.org): Candidate recruitment and preparation for women

#### Progressive Federalism

When national politics is hostile, state-level victories provide both material benefits (actual policy change for actual people) and political infrastructure (state governments that can resist federal overreach). California, New York, and other blue states serve as laboratories for policy and as sanctuary jurisdictions that limit the reach of federal policy. Building durable state-level majorities is a long-term strategic priority.

### 3.4 Digital Security — Essential Practices for Modern Activists

#### Threat Modeling Steps

1. **Identify what you want to protect**: Contact lists, communications, location, finances, identity
2. **Identify who you're protecting against**: Local police, FBI, private investigators, employers, online harassers, foreign actors
3. **Identify likely attack vectors**: Phone searches at arrest, subpoenas for service provider data, phishing, social engineering, physical surveillance
4. **Assess probability and consequence**: What is actually likely to happen, and what would it cost you?
5. **Choose protections proportional to risk**: A school board activist needs less protection than a civil liberties attorney

#### Device Security

- **Full disk encryption**: Enabled by default on iPhone; enable on Android in settings; use FileVault (Mac) or BitLocker (Windows) on computers
- **Strong passcodes**: 6+ digit PIN (avoid fingerprint/face unlock when facing police — you cannot be compelled to provide a passcode but can be compelled to place your finger or face)
- **Updates**: Keep all software updated; don't run end-of-life operating systems
- **App permissions**: Audit and revoke unnecessary app access to location, contacts, microphone, camera

#### Communication Security

| Use Case | Recommended Tool | Notes |
|----------|-----------------|-------|
| Sensitive conversations | Signal (signal.org) | Enable disappearing messages |
| Internal organizational communications | Signal groups or Matrix/Element | Avoid WhatsApp for sensitive organizing |
| Email to lawyers, journalists | ProtonMail or S/MIME encrypted email | Regular email is not secure |
| Anonymous tip submission | SecureDrop (securedrop.org) | Used by major news organizations |
| General communications | Your current tools | Most communications don't require special protection |

#### Physical Security

- **At protests**: Leave personal devices at home if possible; if you must bring a phone, put it in airplane mode or use a burner; disable Bluetooth; know your lawyer's number written on your arm, not stored only in phone
- **Meeting security**: For high-security meetings, no phones in the room (even powered off, some can be remotely activated by sophisticated adversaries); assume any digital device can be compromised
- **Social media**: Don't post real-time location; don't tag others without permission; be aware that historical posts can be used against you

## Part IV: The Current Moment — Specific Threats and Responses

### 4.1 Indicators of Democratic Backsliding

V-Dem (Varieties of Democracy) and Freedom House provide the most rigorous empirical monitoring of democratic health globally. Current findings (2024-2025):

**Freedom of expression**: Deteriorating in 44 countries (a quarter of all countries globally) — the highest level ever recorded. Government censorship of media is the most common tool of autocratization, deployed in 44 countries over the past decade.

**Civil society**: Governments in 41 countries have increased repression of civil society organizations compared to 2014.

**Electoral integrity**: Multiple countries show deteriorating electoral competition and administration integrity.

**United States specifically**: The Century Foundation's Democracy Meter and other measures identify multiple indicators of democratic stress in the U.S. context, including attacks on judicial independence, civil service politicization, and press freedom concerns.

**V-Dem indicators to watch**:
- Government censorship of media (direct and indirect)
- Harassment of journalists
- Freedom of discussion (online and offline)
- CSO repression
- Electoral management body independence
- Executive attacks on the legislature and courts
- Rule of law (equal application of law regardless of political affiliation)

#### The Autocratization Sequence

Based on how democratic backsliding has unfolded in Hungary, Turkey, Venezuela, Poland, and other cases:

1. **Electoral win** by a faction willing to use democratic power to undermine democracy
2. **Rapid institutional moves**: Judiciary appointments, agency heads, civil service dismissals
3. **Media pressure**: Advertising pressure, regulatory harassment, buyout by allies
4. **Opposition weakening**: Selective prosecution of opponents; campaign finance rule changes; voter suppression
5. **Civil society pressure**: New regulations, investigations, funding threats targeting independent organizations
6. **Electoral manipulation**: Rule changes that tilt the playing field before the next election
7. **Consolidation**: By the time the next election comes, the system has been sufficiently modified that competitive democracy is no longer possible

**Critical window**: V-Dem research shows resistance is significantly more likely to succeed before steps 4-7 are complete. "Before electoral processes and institutional constraints on executives are fully captured."

### 4.2 Targeted Resistance Responses by Threat Type

#### Attacks on the Judiciary

**What it looks like**: Packing courts with loyalists; using presidential powers to pressure judges; congressional jurisdiction-stripping; defunding or ignoring court orders; impeachment threats against independent judges.

**Responses**:
- Legal professional associations (bar associations, law school faculties) must speak publicly
- Strategic litigation to establish and reinforce judicial precedents while courts remain independent
- Public education about specific judicial decisions and their stakes
- Coalition with conservative lawyers and federalists who value judicial independence as a principle
- Legislative proposals for court reform (term limits, ethics rules) that could build bipartisan coalitions
- Support for Brennan Center for Justice, Protect Democracy, and similar organizations

#### Attacks on Free Press

**What it looks like**: Retaliatory regulatory action against media companies; defamation threats designed to chill reporting; exclusion of critical journalists from access; state-adjacent purchase of media outlets; promotion of state-favorable media.

**Responses**:
- Subscribe to and financially support independent journalism
- Report press freedom violations to the Committee to Protect Journalists, Reporters Without Borders, and Freedom of the Press Foundation
- Support the Reporters Committee for Freedom of the Press (legal defense for journalists)
- Shield law advocacy in states that don't have them; support federal shield legislation
- Amplify suppressed reporting
- Document and publicize press access restrictions

#### Attacks on Civil Service

**What it looks like**: Mass dismissals of career professionals; replacement with political loyalists; using performance review and schedule changes to purge civil servants; Schedule F or equivalent reclassification; retaliation against those who resist or document.

**Responses**:
- Support civil service protection organizations (Government Accountability Project, National Whistleblower Center)
- Advocacy for legislative reinforcement of civil service protections
- Document and publicize purges and their consequences
- Support affected civil servants legally and professionally
- Coalition with public sector unions (AFGE, NTEU) fighting to protect workers

#### Attacks on Elections

**What it looks like**: Voter ID requirements designed to suppress specific populations; polling place closures in specific communities; voter roll purges; gerrymandering; changes to election administration structures; threats to certification processes.

**Responses**:
- Voter registration drives targeting affected communities
- Election protection work (Election Protection coalition, 1-866-OUR-VOTE hotline)
- Become a poll worker or election official (direct action)
- Support state-level election administration candidates who are committed to democratic administration
- Strategic litigation (Brennan Center, ACLU)
- Redistricting commissions and anti-gerrymandering initiatives
- Run for county clerk, election board, and other election administration positions

#### Attacks on Civil Liberties

**What it looks like**: Expanded surveillance programs; weakening of privacy protections; political prosecution of activists and journalists; restrictions on protest rights; weakening of due process protections.

**Responses**:
- Support ACLU, EFF (Electronic Frontier Foundation) financially and as members
- Digital security practices (see 3.4)
- Know your rights at protests and in encounters with law enforcement
- Document and report civil liberties violations
- Support state-level privacy legislation and local civil liberties ordinances
- Legal observer training (National Lawyers Guild)

#### Economic Capture and Oligarchy

**What it looks like**: Regulatory capture by concentrated industries; use of government contracts and regulatory power to reward allies and punish opponents; undermining of antitrust enforcement; financial sector deregulation benefiting political allies.

**Responses**:
- Support antitrust advocacy (American Economic Liberties Project, Open Markets Institute)
- Campaign finance reform advocacy (Campaign Legal Center, Issue One)
- Support state-level campaign finance restrictions
- Shareholder activism targeting corporate political spending
- Support journalists covering financial-political connections
- Union organizing as counterweight to corporate political power
