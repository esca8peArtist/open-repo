# ITEM10: Domain 37 Candidate Scoping

*Completed April 28, 2026. This document identifies genuine gaps in the existing 36-domain framework and defines 3–5 candidate topics that would fill them. The two highest-priority candidates receive full research roadmaps. Format follows established domain conventions: executive summary, problem framing, reform scope, sources, cross-domain connections.*

## Part I: Gap Analysis — What the 36-Domain Framework Covers and What It Misses

### The Existing Domain Map

The current 36 domains span the following areas of democratic governance:

**Electoral and Participatory Architecture** (Domains 1, 3, 37): Electoral reform, voter rights, redistricting, ranked-choice voting, citizens' assemblies, participatory budgeting. Domain 37 covers federal interference in the 2026 midterms.

**Institutional Integrity and Oversight** (Domains 2, 26, 29, 34): Civil service, independent agencies, inspectors general, anti-corruption architecture, congressional fiscal authority, prosecutorial weaponization.

**Judicial System** (Domains 6, 35): Judicial independence, SCOTUS structure, Loper Bright fallout, Humphrey's Executor collapse.

**Rights and Civil Liberties** (Domains 7, 14, 16, 21, 22): Press freedom, assembly, criminal justice and policing, immigration, data privacy, reparations and racial justice.

**Economic Architecture** (Domains 4, 5, 17, 18, 20, 23): Digital government, fiscal/tax reform, labor and employment, social safety net, antitrust, trade policy.

**Policy Domains** (Domains 10–15): Education (with pre-K), healthcare, infrastructure, housing, environment and climate.

**Information and Technology Governance** (Domains 8, 27, 36): Media ecosystem and local news, higher education and academic freedom, AI governance and algorithmic accountability.

**Comparative and Structural** (Domains 9, 13, 19, 28, 33): Federalism, constitutional amendments, national security and foreign policy, war powers, state legislative autocratization.

**Recent Expansions** (Domains 31, 33, 34, 35, 36): Healthcare/Medicaid crisis (OBBBA), state autocratization, congressional fiscal authority, SCOTUS OT2026 preview, AI governance.

---

### Identified Gaps

After mapping all 36 domains, four genuine gaps emerge — areas that are either (a) absent from the framework, (b) treated as cross-domain references without a standalone structural analysis, or (c) freshly significant due to 2026 developments that postdate the framework's core composition.

**Gap 1: Foreign and Transnational Interference in Democratic Institutions**

The framework addresses domestic interference (Domain 37: federal executive interference in elections; Domain 8: disinformation and media ecosystem) but has no domain that analyzes the foreign/transnational dimension. This includes foreign information operations, influence over US political infrastructure, foreign funding of domestic political actors, and the collapse of the US's own democracy-promotion infrastructure abroad (NED, USAID). This is a significant gap: the same administration dismantling domestic democratic accountability has also ended 97% of USAID democracy and governance grants (more than 1,600 grants worth $14 billion terminated) and proposed defunding the National Endowment for Democracy. The strategic consequence — authoritarian powers filling the vacuum — has direct feedback effects on US democratic resilience.

**Gap 2: Constitutional Architecture and Amendment Process Reform**

Domain 13 (Housing) covers constitutional amendments only as an implementation pathway for housing rights. No domain addresses the constitutional design question directly: the Article V amendment process, its vulnerabilities, and structural design alternatives for a functioning democracy. This gap is increasingly acute: (a) 28 states have called for an Article V convention, only six short of the threshold, with organized campaigns from both left (Wolf-PAC) and right (Convention of States, Balanced Budget Amendment) that have conflicting and potentially runaway implications; (b) the SCOTUS administrative law trifecta (Loper Bright, Corner Post, Jarkesy) has rendered statutory reform more fragile and elevated the need for constitutional-level protections; (c) abusive constitutionalism — using the amendment process to entrench power rather than protect rights — is the playbook documented in Hungary, Turkey, and Venezuela.

**Gap 3: Tribal Sovereignty, Indigenous Democratic Traditions, and Nation-to-Nation Relations**

The framework's reparations domain (Domain 22) covers racial equity for Black Americans and general civil rights enforcement, but has no structural analysis of tribal sovereignty, treaty rights, and Indigenous governance as distinct democratic design questions. This is not merely a justice gap — it is a structural one. Indigenous nations are the only governments within the United States that predate the Constitution and maintain independent governmental authority. Their treaty rights are being systematically dismantled (USAID defunding affected Indigenous programs globally; the Interior Department's reorganization is targeting Bureau of Indian Affairs funding). The absence of a domain that engages tribal sovereignty as a democratic design problem — rather than only a civil rights enforcement question — is a structural gap in the framework.

**Gap 4: International Democratic Alliance and US Geopolitical Role in Democratic Resilience**

Domain 19 (National Security and Foreign Policy) addresses security alliances and US foreign policy architecture but does not cover the specific question of the US role in the global democratic order: what happens when the US withdraws from its traditional role as democracy promoter, what the evidence shows about authoritarian powers filling that vacuum, and how a reformed US government would restore its international democratic leadership. This is closely related to Gap 1 but distinct: Gap 1 is about foreign interference in US democracy; Gap 4 is about US democracy's role in the world. With NED defunded, USAID effectively destroyed, and the State Department's democracy programs gutted, the framework has no domain that addresses how to rebuild US international democratic leadership as part of domestic democratic renewal.

## Part II: Domain 37 Candidates

Five candidates follow, ordered by strength of fit. Each is evaluated on: (1) gap-fill rationale, (2) connection to the framework's democracy/institutional resilience theme, (3) feasibility of a research document at domain standard, and (4) urgency given current crisis trajectory.

---

### Candidate A: Foreign and Transnational Interference in Democratic Institutions

**One-line framing**: The mechanisms by which foreign governments undermine US democratic institutions — information operations, election infrastructure attacks, financial influence over political actors, and the strategic collapse of US democracy promotion — and the structural defenses a reformed democratic government would build.

**Gap-fill rationale**: This candidate fills Gap 1. No existing domain addresses the foreign interference dimension of democratic erosion. Domain 8 (Media Ecosystem) addresses disinformation from a domestic media-design angle. Domain 37 addresses domestic federal executive interference in elections. Domain 19 addresses security alliances but not the democratic dimension of US foreign policy posture. A standalone domain on foreign interference would:

- Document the documented mechanisms: Russian information operations (2016–2026), Chinese influence operations targeting US academic and policy institutions, foreign funding of US political nonprofits (Domain 7's dark money analysis identifies the domestic loophole but not the foreign-origin funding problem), and Saudi/Gulf state influence via think tank funding and sovereign wealth vehicles.
- Analyze the collapse of US counterintelligence capacity against democratic threats: the Foreign Influence Task Force disbanded in 2025; CISA's election security division defunded (directly connected to Domain 37 but with distinct structural implications for non-election civic infrastructure); FBI's shift from counterintelligence to political prosecutions (Domain 29).
- Document the strategic consequence of ending democracy promotion: 120 countries losing US-funded civil society support; authoritarian powers expanding their governance-assistance programs into the vacuum; the democratic regression data (Freedom House 2026: the 20th consecutive year of global democratic decline, with the US no longer functioning as a counterweight).
- Propose reform architecture: a statutory foreign agent registration framework with real enforcement teeth (FARA reform — the existing law's 2019 enforcement revival has been reversed); an independent electoral foreign interference commission with mandatory public reporting; restoration of NED and democracy-promotion infrastructure; and a statutory framework for countering foreign information operations that does not compromise First Amendment protections.

**Connection to framework theme**: Direct. Foreign interference is a force multiplier for every domestic vulnerability the framework documents. A weakened civil service (Domain 2) is more easily penetrated. A captured DOJ (Domain 29) is less able to prosecute foreign agents. A defunded CISA (Domain 37) leaves election infrastructure exposed. The cross-domain synthesis loops in Section 5.4 of the proposal do not include a Foreign Interference-Democracy loop — this domain would add it.

**Urgency**: High. The 2026 midterms are the immediate pressure point. Foreign interference in the August primaries and November general election is actively being facilitated by the institutional destruction documented in Domain 37. Research produced before August 2026 would be actionable.

**Assessment**: Strongest candidate. Fills a clear gap, has abundant primary source material, connects to every existing domain in the electoral and rights clusters, and is immediately relevant to the distribution context.

---

### Candidate B: Constitutional Architecture and Article V Reform

**One-line framing**: The Article V amendment process as both structural fragility and strategic opportunity — the vulnerabilities to runaway convention scenarios, the case for lowering amendment thresholds, and the international design evidence for constitutional systems that can adapt without self-destruction.

**Gap-fill rationale**: This candidate fills Gap 2. The framework extensively documents the case for specific constitutional amendments (Citizens United reversal, civil service protection, citizens' assembly authority, etc.) but has no domain that addresses the amendment process itself as a structural design problem. The two risks are opposite and both real:

- **Runaway Convention risk**: 28 states have called for an Article V convention (threshold: 34). The three active campaigns include a right-wing Convention of States that would add a balanced budget amendment, term limits, and potentially other provisions; a left-wing Wolf-PAC campaign for a campaign finance amendment; and a bipartisan term limits campaign. Article V provides no procedural framework — no rules for delegate selection, no rules for agenda limitation, no provision limiting the convention to its stated purpose. The Brennan Center and Common Cause have both documented the runaway convention risk: a convention, once called, could potentially address any constitutional provision, not only those that motivated the state calls. Congress has never established rules for a convention, and there is no agreement on how to prevent delegates from proposing amendments far beyond their mandate.
- **Amendment rigidity risk**: The flip side is that the current 2/3-3/4 threshold makes the amendments the framework proposes — Citizens United reversal, civil service protection, Supreme Court structural reforms — functionally unavailable in the current political environment. Germany's Basic Law (Article 79) requires 2/3 majority of both chambers but no state ratification; the amendment process has been used 66 times. South Korea's constitution can be amended by 2/3 parliamentary approval plus national referendum. Ireland uses citizens' assemblies to develop amendment proposals followed by popular referendum — the most democratic design in the comparative record. A domain that analyzes these alternatives provides the reform roadmap for converting the specific constitutional amendments the proposal requires from aspirational to achievable.

**Connection to framework theme**: The constitutional amendment pathway is either the most powerful tool available for structural democratic reform (Citizens United reversal, Supreme Court reform, civil service protection) or it is a structural threat (runaway convention). A domain that maps the risk landscape and the design alternatives strengthens every domain in the proposal that proposes constitutional changes.

**Urgency**: Medium-High. The Article V convention count is at 28/34. A domain published before the 2026 midterms could reach state legislators who are the actual decision-makers on state calls and rescissions.

**Assessment**: Strong candidate. Lower distribution urgency than Candidate A (the convention risk has a longer fuse than election interference) but potentially higher long-term structural impact. This domain would be referenced by virtually every other domain that proposes a constitutional reform pathway.

### Candidate C: Tribal Sovereignty, Indigenous Democratic Traditions, and Nation-to-Nation Relations

**One-line framing**: The democratic design questions raised by Indigenous nations as co-governing entities within the US — their treaty-based governmental authority, the systematic dismantling of that authority since January 2025, and the design argument for authentic nation-to-nation relations as a democratic strengthening mechanism rather than a rights-protection add-on.

**Gap-fill rationale**: This candidate fills Gap 3. Domain 22 (Reparations and Racial Justice) addresses Black Americans' reparations claims and general federal civil rights enforcement. It does not engage tribal sovereignty as a structural governmental question. The distinction matters: a civil rights framing treats Indigenous peoples as a protected class within US legal structures; a sovereignty framing treats Indigenous nations as governmental entities whose authority preexists and is not derived from US constitutional grants. These are different legal and political frameworks with different reform implications.

The 2025–2026 context has made this gap more acute:
- Interior Department reorganization targeting Bureau of Indian Affairs is reducing the federal government's nation-to-nation governmental capacity.
- Tribal programs funded through USAID and State Department democracy assistance (especially for Indigenous communities internationally) have been terminated.
- The administration's approach to public lands (including expansion of extractive leases on lands with treaty protections) is generating new legal conflicts that the existing framework has no domain to analyze.

The democratic design argument is substantive: Indigenous governance traditions — particularly the Haudenosaunee Confederacy (Iroquois Confederacy) — influenced the design of the US Constitution itself. The participatory governance mechanisms documented in Domain 3 (citizens' assemblies) have direct precedents in Indigenous deliberative traditions. A domain that takes this seriously, rather than treating Indigenous affairs as only a civil rights enforcement question, strengthens the framework's theory of democratic design.

**Connection to framework theme**: Connects to Domain 22 (reparations), Domain 9 (federalism and the specific design of nation-to-nation relations within a federal system), Domain 15 (environment — the intersection of tribal treaty rights and public lands), and Domain 3 (participatory democracy — Indigenous deliberative traditions as precedent).

**Urgency**: Medium. No immediate crisis window comparable to the August/November 2026 election deadlines, but the Interior Department reorganization is actively underway and the window for legislative response to treaty rights rollbacks is open.

**Assessment**: Substantively important gap-filler. Lower urgency than Candidates A and B for the current distribution cycle, but significant for the framework's long-term intellectual credibility and for reaching Indigenous-rights academic and legal communities.

### Candidate D: International Democratic Alliance and US Global Role in Democratic Resilience

**One-line framing**: The strategic consequences of the US withdrawing from global democracy promotion — the authoritarian vacuum-filling, the evidence on how democratic collapse in US partner countries feeds back into domestic institutional erosion — and the structural design for restoring US international democratic leadership as part of domestic renewal.

**Gap-fill rationale**: This candidate fills Gap 4. Domain 19 (National Security and Foreign Policy) addresses security alliance architecture but treats democracy promotion as a subcategory of foreign policy, not as a structural element of democratic resilience. The evidence suggests that framing is incomplete:

- US democracy promotion infrastructure (NED, USAID governance programs, State Department's Bureau of Democracy, Human Rights and Labor) has been effectively terminated: 97% of USAID democracy grants ended; NED proposed for defunding; 1,600+ grants worth $14 billion terminated globally.
- The strategic consequence documented in the research: authoritarian powers — China, Russia, Iran, Gulf states — are expanding governance assistance programs into the vacuum. Countries that received US democracy support are experiencing accelerated democratic backsliding (Freedom House 2026 data: 20th consecutive year of global democratic decline).
- The feedback mechanism for US domestic democracy: countries that backslide toward authoritarianism become sources of the foreign interference (Candidate A) that weakens US democracy in return. The relationship is bidirectional.
- International democratic alliances — the Summit for Democracy, the Community of Democracies, the Alliance of Democracies Foundation — have lost their anchor member. The design question of how a reformed US government would re-engage these structures, and what architecture would make that re-engagement durable across administrations, is a genuine reform design problem.

**Connection to framework theme**: Connects to Domain 19 (foreign policy), Domain 8 (global information ecosystem), and to the foreign interference domain (Candidate A). Provides the "restoration" complement to the "resistance" framing: if Candidate A documents the threat from foreign erosion, this domain documents the US's role in building an international democratic bulwark.

**Urgency**: Lower for the immediate 2026 distribution cycle. The foreign policy architecture question is a medium-term recovery planning question more than an immediate crisis window question.

**Assessment**: Valuable for the framework's completeness but lower distribution urgency for the current path. Best treated as a Phase 3 candidate or as a deeper section of Domain 19 rather than a standalone Domain 37.

### Candidate E: Proportional Representation and Comparative Electoral Systems

**One-line framing**: A comparative deep-dive on proportional representation systems — single transferable vote, mixed-member proportional, party list — with detailed analysis of which variants are most compatible with US constitutional design, what the evidence shows about their resistance to autocratic capture, and what the political pathway looks like from the current FPTP system.

**Gap-fill rationale**: Domain 1 proposes proportional representation (specifically STV and MMP) and cites Ireland and Germany as precedents. But the proposal's treatment of PR is at the conceptual level — it establishes the case for the reform but does not provide the depth of comparative evidence needed to answer the objections that state-level practitioners and policy researchers will raise: What happens to minority representation in multi-member districts vs. majority-minority single-member districts under VRA? How does PR interact with the US's existing political party infrastructure? What does the transition look like procedurally? Which variant (STV vs. MMP vs. open-list PR) is most compatible with existing US constitutional provisions?

A standalone domain would fill this gap, functioning as the evidence companion for Domain 1 that Domain 4's deepening document provides for the digital government reforms.

**Connection to framework theme**: Direct extension of Domain 1. Connects to Domain 33 (gerrymandering as the primary mechanism of state-level autocratization), Domain 9 (state-level adoption pathways), and the cross-domain synthesis's representation-democracy feedback loop.

**Urgency**: Low-medium. The proportional representation conversation is gaining momentum (Fair Representation Act, Virginia RCV expansion) but there is no immediate crisis window that this domain would serve the way Domain 37 (elections) serves election-protection organizations before the 2026 midterms.

**Assessment**: Fills a real gap in the Domain 1 evidence base but is more of a domain-deepening document than a standalone new domain. If the distribution reaches electoral reform practitioners and state legislators, this would be a high-value complement. Lower priority than Candidates A and B as a standalone Domain 37.

## Part III: Top Two Candidates — Preliminary Research Roadmap

### Candidate A: Foreign and Transnational Interference in Democratic Institutions

**Proposed domain title**: Domain 37 — Foreign and Transnational Interference in US Democratic Institutions: Mechanisms, Evidence, and Structural Defense Architecture

**Scope statement**: This domain documents the foreign interference dimension of US democratic erosion — distinct from, but directly connected to, the domestic interference mechanisms in existing Domain 37 (Federal Executive Interference in the 2026 Midterms). It covers: the documented mechanisms of foreign influence operations targeting US elections and civic infrastructure; the systematic dismantling of US counterintelligence capacity against democratic threats; the strategic collapse of US democracy promotion and its feedback effects; and the statutory reform architecture for a democratic defense that does not compromise civil liberties.

**Estimated length**: 8,000–10,000 words with 50–60 citations. Consistent with Domain 36 (AI governance) and the current Domain 37 (8,857 words, 50 citations).

**Key research questions**:
1. What are the documented mechanisms of foreign information operations targeting US democratic institutions since 2020, and how has the US counterintelligence response capacity changed under the current administration?
2. What is the fiscal and programmatic scale of the US democracy promotion collapse (NED, USAID, State DRL), and what does the evidence show about authoritarian power vacuum-filling?
3. What statutory framework would give FARA real enforcement capacity without creating a political-speech chilling effect?
4. What does the comparative evidence show about how democracies build structural defenses against foreign interference while maintaining civil liberty protections? (EU case: Digital Services Act, disinformation code; Canada: Critical Election Incident Public Protocol; Germany: Bundesverfassungsschutz mandate reform.)
5. How do foreign interference threats interact with the domestic accountability gaps documented in other domains — specifically, does a captured DOJ (Domain 29) reduce FARA enforcement, and does CISA defunding (Domain 37) create the election infrastructure exposure that foreign interference exploits?

**Primary sources**:
- Senate Intelligence Committee Russia Investigation Final Report (2020) — establishes the baseline mechanisms; search for 2025-2026 update reports
- CISA Foreign Influence Task Force disbandment documentation — DHS Inspector General reports, congressional testimony
- NED defunding legislative record — proposed 2026 budget, congressional response, NED's own documentation of grant terminations
- USAID Democracy and Governance grant termination data — AidData database, GlobalDev figures (97% termination documented in search results)
- Freedom House *Freedom in the World 2026* report — global democratic regression data
- DOJ FARA Unit enforcement records — annual reports, prosecution data pre- and post-2019 revival
- Carnegie Endowment reporting on "Startling Reversal of US Global Anti-Corruption Policy" (January 2026)
- Council on Foreign Relations: "How the World Is Seeing the United States as 2026 Begins" — international perception data
- Senate Intelligence Committee and ODNI public reporting on Chinese and Iranian influence operations targeting 2024 and 2026 elections
- Brennan Center: "International Lessons on Democratic Backsliding and Recovery" — for comparative recovery evidence

**Cross-domain connections**: Domain 37 (election infrastructure the foreign interference exploits), Domain 8 (information operations as the mechanism foreign actors use in the US media ecosystem), Domain 7 (foreign funding as a subset of campaign finance dark money — the FARA loophole), Domain 29 (DOJ capture reducing FARA enforcement capacity), Domain 19 (security alliance architecture for coordinating democratic defense), Domain 21 (data privacy as a foreign intelligence collection vulnerability).

**Key reform proposals to develop**:
- FARA Enforcement Act: mandatory annual registration renewal, civil penalty schedule with real enforcement teeth, independent FARA Commission removed from DOJ partisan control, automatic criminal referral for willful violations by agents of identified adversary states
- Foreign Interference Commission: statutory body modeled on Canada's Critical Election Incident Public Protocol — independent, bipartisan, permanent, with mandatory pre-election public reporting on identified interference threats
- NED and Democracy Promotion Restoration Act: baseline funding floor indexed to 0.05% of federal discretionary spending, statutory independence from executive branch defunding authority (parallel to ICA reform in Domain 34), restored grant-making protocols
- Authoritarian Influence Registry: a public database of foreign government and sovereign wealth fund investments in US media, think tanks, universities, and political nonprofits — extending the FARA model to influence activity below the formal agent threshold

**Distribution target**: This domain reaches a specific audience — counterintelligence and national security scholars (complements but doesn't duplicate Domain 19's security community), democracy advocates who work on foreign policy (Brennan Center's Democracy Program, NED's network, Carnegie Endowment Democracy program), and election-protection organizations already targeted by existing Domain 37 who have a foreign interference dimension to their work. It also has crossover appeal to mainstream foreign policy audiences who may not have engaged the domestic democratic framework — a distribution expansion opportunity.

### Candidate B: Constitutional Architecture and Article V Reform

**Proposed domain title**: Domain 37 — Constitutional Architecture and the Amendment Process: Structural Fragilities, Runaway Convention Risks, and the Case for Deliberate Constitutional Adaptation

**Scope statement**: This domain addresses the US constitutional amendment process as both a structural vulnerability (Article V runaway convention risk, with 28 of 34 required state calls already achieved) and a strategic resource (the amendment pathway for Citizens United reversal, civil service protection, Supreme Court reform, and other constitutional changes the framework requires). It maps the design alternatives using international comparative evidence, identifies which proposed amendments in the framework are constitutionally necessary vs. achievable by statute, and provides a sequenced strategy for democratic constitutional renewal that avoids triggering a runaway convention while preserving the reform pathways the framework depends on.

**Estimated length**: 7,500–9,000 words with 45–55 citations. This is a design-intensive domain — less crisis-documentation and more comparative evidence analysis — which slightly reduces the raw citation count while increasing the analytical depth per section.

**Key research questions**:
1. What is the current Article V convention count, and what are the procedural mechanisms by which a convention could go beyond its stated purpose? What has Congress done or failed to do to establish convention rules?
2. What does the international comparative record show about amendment process design — specifically, which amendment threshold and process designs allow constitutions to adapt without either stagnation (the US problem) or abuse (the Hungary/Turkey abusive constitutionalism problem)?
3. Which constitutional amendments proposed across the framework (Citizens United reversal, civil service protection, SCOTUS structural reform, term limits) are constitutionally necessary vs. achievable through federal statute — and what is the sequencing logic?
4. What is the Article V strategic landscape: who controls the 28 state calls, what would it take to achieve or prevent the 34-state threshold, and how should reform advocates position relative to each active convention campaign?
5. What is the democratic design case for lowering amendment thresholds while adding deliberative requirements — and is there a constitutional-amendment-by-citizens'-assembly model that both increases democratic legitimacy and reduces the risk of elite capture?

**Primary sources**:
- Brennan Center: "Is a New Constitutional Convention a Good Idea?" — establishes the risk framework and runaway convention analysis
- Common Cause: Article V Convention tracking and state rescission analysis — the most current count data
- International IDEA: *Annual Review of Constitution-Building 2024* — global constitutional change tracking
- Heinonline: *Tracking Constitutional Change Around the World: Spring 2026 Updates* — current global data
- Germany Basic Law amendment record (Bundestag official records, 66 amendments) — the model for a functioning amendment process
- Ireland referendum process documentation — the citizens' assembly to referendum pathway that is the best international precedent for the US context
- Article V Project (law review scholarship) — the legal literature on convention procedure, delegate authority, and runaway convention risk
- Heritage Foundation and Convention of States Project materials — for understanding the right-wing convention campaign's procedural theory
- Wolf-PAC and Get Money Out organizational materials — for understanding the left-wing campaign's theory and status
- Florida Law Review: "Not the People: How Courts Cut Voters Out of Amendment" (2024) — for the courts-and-amendment interaction
- William & Mary Law Review: "The Article V Convention Threat Awakens" — for the legal framework

**Cross-domain connections**: Every domain in the framework that proposes a constitutional amendment pathway — Domain 1 (redistricting reform amendment, Citizens United reversal), Domain 2 (civil service constitutional protection), Domain 6 (Supreme Court structural reform, term limits), Domain 7 (First Amendment and foreign campaign finance), Domain 22 (reparations — whether HR 40 requires constitutional authorization). Also connects to Domain 3 (citizens' assemblies as a deliberative mechanism for constitutional development — Ireland's model as the best precedent for a US constitutional convention alternative).

**Key reform proposals to develop**:
- Article V Procedural Framework Act: congressional legislation establishing convention delegate apportionment, vote allocation, agenda limitation, transparency requirements, and ratification sequencing — closing the procedural vacuum that makes a runaway convention possible
- Constitutional Modernization Amendment: a single constitutional amendment proposing to lower the proposal threshold from 2/3 to 60% of Congress while adding a deliberative requirement (a citizens' assembly review period before floor vote) and maintaining the 3/4 state ratification threshold — making the amendment process harder to abuse but easier to use for legitimate reform
- Amendment Priority Sequencing Framework: identifying which of the framework's proposed constitutional amendments are highest-priority for a future reform window (Citizens United reversal, civil service protection, SCOTUS term limits) vs. which are achievable by statute and should be pursued through normal legislation — ensuring constitutional amendment political capital is spent on the changes that cannot be achieved any other way
- Anti-Abusive Constitutionalism Protocol: a doctrine — modeled on the EU's Rule of Law Mechanism and Germany's Basic Law eternity clauses (Article 79(3)) — establishing that certain constitutional provisions (fundamental rights, democratic election requirements, separation of powers) cannot be amended even by a valid supermajority, creating an unamendable constitutional core

**Distribution target**: This domain has an unusual distribution profile. It reaches constitutional law scholars (overlapping with the law school distribution in DISTRIBUTION_OUTREACH_CONTACTS.md — Baude, Metzger, Bowie are the natural primary contacts given their Article V and constitutional design work). It also reaches state legislators — the actual decision-makers on Article V convention calls and rescissions — who are not currently in the distribution list and represent a new audience channel. The Brennan Center's Constitutional Democracy Program and Common Cause's Article V tracking team are natural distribution entry points for the civil society tier.

## Part IV: Recommendation and Format Consistency Check

### Recommended Selection for Domain 37

**Primary recommendation: Candidate A (Foreign and Transnational Interference)**

Candidate A fills the most acute gap in the framework for the current distribution context. The existing Domain 37 already goes to election-protection organizations. A new Domain 37 on foreign interference extends the election-security theme to the transnational dimension that those same organizations are actively tracking. It has the clearest connection to the most urgent crisis moments (August primaries, November midterms). And it reaches a new distribution audience — national security scholars and foreign policy organizations — that the current framework does not fully engage.

**Secondary recommendation: Candidate B (Constitutional Architecture)**

Candidate B fills the deepest structural gap in the framework — the one that every constitutional amendment proposal in the proposal's reform architecture depends on. It is the highest-impact domain for long-term democratic renewal, even if its urgency for the 2026 distribution cycle is slightly lower. It also addresses an active and underreported threat (the Article V convention count at 28/34) that no other domain covers.

**Candidates C, D, and E** are genuine gap-fillers that would strengthen the framework's completeness and reach, but they are lower priority for the immediate Hybrid distribution path. They are better positioned as Phase 3 expansion candidates or as deepening documents for existing domains (Domain 22 for Candidate C, Domain 19 for Candidate D, Domain 1 for Candidate E).
