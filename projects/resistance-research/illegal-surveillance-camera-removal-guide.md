# Illegal Surveillance Camera Removal — Citizen Legal Guide

*Compiled: July 4, 2026 | Resistance Research Project*
*Status: Production-ready | Distribution: General public, legal aid orgs, tenant advocacy, immigration communities*

---

## Introduction

Surveillance cameras are now ubiquitous — in workplaces, rental properties, shared housing, and neighborhoods. The vast majority of cameras are installed lawfully and serve legitimate security purposes. But a significant and growing number are installed illegally: positioned to capture private areas, recording audio without consent, used to harass or intimidate rather than to protect property, or deployed in contexts where law prohibits monitoring entirely.

This guide is designed for people who suspect they are being surveilled illegally — and who need to understand what the law actually says, how to report violations, how to demand removal, and where to find legal help. It is organized for practical use: you can go directly to the section most relevant to your situation.

**The most important threshold question**: Does the camera capture a space where you have a reasonable expectation of privacy? If yes, the camera may be illegal regardless of where it is placed or who placed it. Every other analysis flows from this baseline.

## Section 1: What Makes a Surveillance Camera Illegal

### 1.1 The Reasonable Expectation of Privacy Standard

American privacy law — built from the Fourth Amendment but applied in civil and criminal law across all states — rests on the concept of "reasonable expectation of privacy." Courts define this as: spaces where a person would reasonably expect not to be observed by others without consent. The legal formula is two-part: (1) the person actually expected privacy, and (2) that expectation is one society recognizes as reasonable.

**Spaces with clear reasonable expectation of privacy:**
- Bathrooms, restrooms, and toilet areas
- Bedrooms and sleeping areas
- Changing rooms, locker rooms, and dressing areas
- Medical examination rooms and treatment areas
- Private offices when employee has been told the space is theirs
- Hotel rooms and short-term rental interiors
- Fenced backyards not visible from public spaces
- Areas inside a private home not viewable from a street or public vantage point

**Spaces with limited or no reasonable expectation of privacy:**
- Public sidewalks, streets, and parks
- Store sales floors and retail common areas
- Building lobbies and entrance areas
- Open-plan office areas where employees are informed of monitoring
- Driveways and front yards visible from the street
- Parking lots and garages

**The critical nuance**: A camera can be legally placed in a location but still be illegal if it is **angled** to capture a private area. A neighbor's camera mounted on their own home is legal; the same camera positioned to look through your bedroom window is not. A workplace camera in a common area is usually legal; a camera in the break room bathroom is not. Position and angle matter as much as location.

### 1.2 Audio Recording: The More Restrictive Legal Standard

Video-only surveillance is generally held to a lower standard than audio recording. Audio recording — capturing conversations — triggers consent laws that are significantly more restrictive than video recording laws in most states.

**Federal law (the Electronic Communications Privacy Act / Wiretap Act, 18 U.S.C. §§ 2510-2522)**: Prohibits intentional interception of oral, wire, or electronic communications without consent. One-party consent satisfies federal law — meaning the person recording the conversation can be that one consenting party.

**State "two-party" (all-party) consent laws**: Eleven states require the consent of **all parties** to a conversation before audio can be recorded. These are the states where covert audio recording of conversations — even in spaces that are not inherently private — is a criminal offense:

| All-Party Consent States | Key Statute |
|--------------------------|-------------|
| California | Penal Code § 632 |
| Connecticut | C.G.S. § 52-570d |
| Florida | F.S. § 934.03 |
| Illinois | 720 ILCS 5/14-2 |
| Maryland | Md. Courts § 10-402 |
| Massachusetts | M.G.L. c. 272 § 99 |
| Michigan | MCL § 750.539c |
| Montana | MCA § 45-8-213 |
| Nevada | NRS § 200.650 |
| New Hampshire | RSA § 570-A:2 |
| Oregon | ORS § 165.540 |
| Pennsylvania | 18 Pa. C.S. § 5703 |
| Washington | RCW § 9.73.030 |

A surveillance camera that also records audio in any of these states requires all-party consent. Many "smart" cameras and doorbells include audio by default — in all-party consent states, enabling audio recording without consent is a criminal act, not merely a civil violation.

### 1.3 Prohibited Locations: Per Se Illegal Cameras

Some camera placements are illegal **per se** — meaning they are never permissible regardless of consent, notice, or purpose.

**Bathrooms and toilet areas**: Every state in the U.S. prohibits video surveillance in bathrooms. Both state voyeurism statutes and federal law (Video Voyeurism Prevention Act of 2004, 18 U.S.C. § 1801) criminalize capturing images of individuals in states of undress in spaces where they expect privacy. This applies to: public restrooms, workplace bathrooms, apartment building bathrooms, hotel bathrooms, and any other toilet or bathing facility.

**Changing rooms and locker rooms**: Prohibited in all states under voyeurism statutes. The prohibition extends to fitting rooms in retail stores, athletic locker rooms, and any facility where people change clothing.

**Bedrooms in rental properties**: Landlords are prohibited in all states from placing cameras inside tenants' bedrooms. Most states extend this prohibition to all interior spaces of a rental unit.

**Medical and healthcare facilities**: HIPAA (the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act) governs camera use in healthcare settings. While HIPAA does not explicitly prohibit security cameras, it requires that covered entities protect patients' privacy and prohibits recording that captures protected health information without consent. Cameras in treatment rooms, hospital rooms, psychiatric wards, or any space where patients are examined or treated are legally problematic under HIPAA unless the facility has specific consent protocols. The HHS Office for Civil Rights enforces HIPAA violations.

**Peep holes and two-way mirrors used for covert observation**: Many states specifically criminalize the use of peepholes, two-way mirrors, or concealed openings to observe people in private spaces. These are treated as equivalents to hidden cameras under voyeurism statutes.

### 1.4 Harassment and Stalking Statutes

Even where a camera is placed in a location that does not clearly violate privacy law, its use can be illegal if it constitutes harassment or stalking. Most states have harassment and stalking statutes that prohibit conduct that causes a reasonable person to feel fear, intimidation, or emotional distress.

**Patterns of surveillance that may constitute harassment or stalking:**
- A neighbor repeatedly repositioning cameras to track your movements in your yard or through your windows
- An employer using cameras to monitor a specific employee in ways unrelated to legitimate business purposes
- A former partner installing cameras to track an ex-spouse or romantic partner
- Landlords using camera footage to intimidate tenants who have filed complaints
- Systematic recording of a specific person's comings and goings as a pattern of behavior

In these cases, the camera itself may be legal, but the surveillance use pattern is criminal under harassment or stalking laws. Document the pattern of surveillance (dates, times, what you observed, who was present) because pattern evidence is essential for harassment and stalking charges.

### 1.5 ADA Considerations

The Americans with Disabilities Act does not directly regulate surveillance camera placement, but it creates relevant obligations in some contexts:

- Accessible restrooms, changing areas, and private healthcare spaces used by people with disabilities carry the same privacy protections as any other private space
- Camera use in disability-related services (group homes, rehabilitation facilities, vocational training centers) may be subject to additional state regulations governing surveillance of vulnerable populations
- Cameras that target individuals specifically because of their disability-related activities may constitute discriminatory harassment

Contact your state's Disability Rights organization (a federally mandated protection and advocacy system) for state-specific guidance. The National Disability Rights Network maintains a directory at ndrn.org.

## Section 2: State-by-State Law Overview

The following overview covers the seven highest-risk states for surveillance violations, focusing on consent laws, harassment statutes, and specific regulations relevant to common scenarios.

### California

**Primary statutes**: Penal Code § 632 (audio consent), § 637.7 (electronic tracking), § 647(j) (voyeurism), Civil Code § 1708.8 (invasion of privacy)

**Audio consent**: All-party consent required. Recording a conversation without all parties' consent is a criminal misdemeanor on first offense, with civil liability of $5,000 per violation or actual damages.

**Video in private spaces**: Voyeurism under § 647(j) is a misdemeanor, with specific felony enhancements for recording in restrooms or changing rooms.

**Workplace surveillance**: California Labor Code § 435 requires employer notice of electronic monitoring. Employers must provide "prior written notice" of workplace surveillance. Hidden cameras in workplaces are legal only in limited circumstances (ongoing investigation of criminal activity) and require union notification in unionized workplaces.

**HOA cameras**: HOAs can install cameras in common areas. HOA cameras cannot point into private units, and HOA boards must maintain a written policy on camera use and footage storage.

**Landlord surveillance**: Civil Code § 1940.2 prohibits landlords from using cameras as a tool to interfere with tenants' quiet enjoyment. California courts have found that surveillance cameras used as weapons of harassment violate this provision.

**ICE surveillance context**: California's Immigrant Tenant Protection Act (Civil Code § 1940.35) prohibits landlords from disclosing tenant immigration status and prohibits using lease provisions or surveillance to facilitate immigration enforcement. This extends to allowing ICE or DHS access to camera footage for immigration enforcement without a judicial warrant.

**Reporting**: California AG's Privacy Unit (oag.ca.gov/privacy), California Department of Fair Employment and Housing (for workplace), local police for criminal voyeurism.

### Texas

**Primary statutes**: Penal Code § 16.02 (unlawful interception), § 21.15 (invasive visual recording), § 21.17 (voyeurism), Occupations Code § 1702 for licensed security companies

**Audio consent**: One-party consent state. You can record conversations you are a party to; covert third-party interception remains criminal.

**Video in private spaces**: Invasive visual recording under § 21.15 is a state jail felony. Texas treats filming in bathrooms, dressing rooms, and spaces where nudity is expected with significant penalties. Texas also specifically criminalizes filming a person's "intimate parts" without consent in any space, including through clothing, expanding protection beyond strictly "private" spaces.

**Workplace surveillance**: Texas has no comprehensive workplace surveillance statute, but common law privacy claims are available. Employee cameras in restrooms are criminal; cameras in open areas with posted notice are generally lawful.

**HOA cameras**: Texas Property Code § 202.022 generally permits HOAs to adopt surveillance policies. HOA cameras must comply with state privacy law. An HOA camera aimed at a fenced backyard where residents have a reasonable expectation of privacy can violate state law.

**Texas-specific note — handgun license asymmetry**: Texas accepts handgun licenses as voter ID but prohibits student IDs. This asymmetry reflects documented racial disparity: 80% of handgun licenses went to white Texans, while majority-minority students are disproportionately disenfranchised by the ID rule. This same asymmetry in how institutions treat different communities is relevant to understanding the pattern of discriminatory surveillance.

**Reporting**: Texas AG Consumer Protection (oag.texas.gov), local law enforcement for criminal violations.

### Florida

**Primary statutes**: F.S. § 934.03 (security of communications — all-party consent), § 810.145 (video voyeurism), § 784.048 (stalking)

**Audio consent**: All-party consent required. Recording a private conversation without all-party consent is a third-degree felony.

**Video voyeurism**: § 810.145 is specifically expanded in Florida — video voyeurism includes not just capturing images of genitalia or nudity but also capturing images of people in "private" contexts including private conversations or activities they expect to be unobserved, even when clothed. This is broader protection than most states.

**Rental properties**: Florida landlord-tenant law (F.S. Ch. 83) does not explicitly address cameras, but courts apply the reasonable expectation of privacy standard. Landlords cannot install cameras inside rented units without tenant consent. Florida courts have awarded damages under the tort of invasion of privacy for landlord surveillance of tenant interiors.

**Workplace**: Florida has no specific workplace surveillance notice statute. EEOC guidance applies.

**Immigrant communities**: Florida HB 991 (signed April 1, 2026) creates new documentation requirements for voter registration that also reflect a broader surveillance posture toward immigrant communities. Florida law enforcement has expanded cooperation with ICE under 287(g) agreements. Communities should be aware that Florida sheriffs' departments in many counties actively share surveillance data with federal immigration authorities.

**Reporting**: Florida Department of Law Enforcement (fdle.state.fl.us), Florida AG (myfloridalegal.com), local law enforcement.

### New York

**Primary statutes**: Penal Law § 250.05 (unlawful surveillance), § 250.45 (unlawful surveillance in second degree), § 250.50 (first degree), Civil Rights Law § 50-a (expanded 2020, limiting police surveillance disclosure)

**Audio consent**: One-party consent for audio under state wiretapping law. However, New York Penal Law § 250.00 et seq. creates broader criminal liability for unlawful surveillance than the federal one-party consent standard would suggest.

**Unlawful surveillance statute (§ 250.45)**: New York has an unusually comprehensive unlawful surveillance statute that criminalizes viewing or recording another person "dressing or undressing or the partial or full nudity of such person" without their consent in any "private" place or other place where "such person has a reasonable expectation of privacy." This is a class E felony. First-degree unlawful surveillance (§ 250.50, class D felony) applies when the offense is for profit or when recordings are disseminated.

**Workplace**: New York City Human Rights Law provides some of the strongest workplace privacy protections in the country. NYC employers must provide written notice of electronic monitoring at the start of employment. State Labor Law § 203-c (effective May 2022) requires notice to employees when employers monitor electronic communications.

**HOA and condominium**: New York Real Property Law governs HOA operations. HOA cameras in common areas are lawful; cameras in any area where residents have private expectations are not.

**Reporting**: New York AG Letitia James (ag.ny.gov), New York City Commission on Human Rights (nyc.gov/cchr), NYPD Detective Bureau for criminal surveillance violations.

### Illinois

**Primary statutes**: 720 ILCS 5/14-1 et seq. (Eavesdropping Act — all-party consent), 720 ILCS 5/26-4 (unauthorized video recording)

**Audio consent**: All-party consent required under the eavesdropping statute. Illinois's eavesdropping law was substantially revised after People v. Melongo (2014) narrowed an earlier version. The revised statute (720 ILCS 5/14-2) requires all-party consent for private conversations. Recording a private conversation without all parties' consent is a Class 4 felony.

**Video recording**: Unauthorized video recording under § 26-4 — capturing images of someone's "private areas" without consent — is a Class A misdemeanor for first offense and Class 4 felony for subsequent offenses.

**Workplace surveillance**: Illinois employers must have a legitimate business purpose for surveillance and must notify employees of electronic monitoring. The Illinois Human Rights Act prohibits discriminatory surveillance targeting employees based on protected characteristics.

**Biometric data**: Illinois has the nation's most aggressive biometric privacy law — the Biometric Information Privacy Act (BIPA, 740 ILCS 14). If surveillance cameras use facial recognition or other biometric identification, BIPA applies: collection of biometric data without informed written consent creates a private right of action with statutory damages of $1,000-$5,000 per violation. This is a powerful tool against employer and landlord biometric surveillance.

**Reporting**: Illinois AG (illinoisattorneygeneral.gov), Illinois Department of Human Rights (dhr.illinois.gov), Illinois Department of Labor for workplace violations.

### Washington

**Primary statutes**: RCW § 9.73.030 (all-party consent for audio), § 9A.44.115 (voyeurism), § 9.73.060 (civil liability for privacy violations)

**Audio consent**: All-party consent required. Washington's privacy law is among the most plaintff-friendly: RCW § 9.73.060 creates a civil cause of action for privacy violations, with recovery of actual damages plus $100 per violation day plus attorney fees. The potential for attorney fee recovery makes Washington privacy law unusually accessible for plaintiffs.

**Voyeurism**: Voyeurism in Washington (§ 9A.44.115) is a class C felony when the recording is for sexual gratification. Other unlawful recording is a gross misdemeanor.

**Workplace surveillance**: Washington state law does not specifically require notice for video surveillance (unlike audio). But under the state constitution's privacy clause (Article I, § 7), courts have found broader privacy protections than the federal Fourth Amendment standard. This heightened state constitutional protection applies to government workplace surveillance; private employer surveillance is governed by common law privacy torts.

**Tenant protections**: Washington's Residential Landlord-Tenant Act does not specifically address cameras, but the state's privacy law creates a civil cause of action. Tenant rights organizations report that landlord camera installation inside units without tenant consent is actionable under the state privacy law.

**Reporting**: Washington AG (atg.wa.gov), Washington State Human Rights Commission for employment violations, local law enforcement for criminal violations.

### Oregon

**Primary statutes**: ORS § 165.540 (all-party consent for audio), § 163.700 (invasion of privacy — visual), § 163.701 (invasion of personal privacy)

**Audio consent**: All-party consent required. Violation is a Class A misdemeanor.

**Visual privacy**: Oregon's invasion of personal privacy statute (§ 163.701) prohibits recording the "intimate areas" of a person without consent in places where they have a reasonable expectation of privacy. This is a Class A misdemeanor. Oregon courts have expanded this protection beyond strict nudity to include any body part a reasonable person would consider private.

**Workplace**: Oregon OSHA has jurisdiction over workplace safety but does not specifically regulate surveillance cameras. Oregon Revised Statutes do not require employer notice for video surveillance. Union contracts often fill this gap in unionized workplaces.

**HOA**: Oregon's Planned Communities Act governs HOA authority. HOA cameras in common areas are lawful; HOA boards cannot install cameras that capture private unit interiors.

**Immigrant communities**: Oregon has a statewide sanctuary law (ORS § 181A.820 — Oregonians' Safety Act) that prohibits state and local law enforcement from using resources to assist federal immigration enforcement. This does not prohibit ICE from using private surveillance data it lawfully obtains, but it does prevent Oregon police from sharing surveillance footage with ICE without a judicial warrant.

**Reporting**: Oregon AG (doj.state.or.us), Oregon Bureau of Labor and Industries for employment violations, local law enforcement.

## Section 3: Reporting Channels

When a camera is illegal, or when you believe surveillance has crossed from legal monitoring into harassment, stalking, or criminal voyeurism, you have multiple reporting channels available. Use more than one — reports to different agencies create a documented record and increase the likelihood of investigation.

### 3.1 Local Law Enforcement

**When to use**: Criminal voyeurism, hidden cameras in bathrooms or bedrooms, stalking, criminal harassment.

**How to report**: File a police report with your local law enforcement agency (city police or county sheriff). Bring all documentation you have: photographs or screenshots of the camera, dates and times of discovery, witness statements, and any prior communications with the camera owner.

**What to say**: "I am reporting a possible violation of [state voyeurism/privacy statute]. I discovered what appears to be a hidden camera in [location] on [date]. I believe this constitutes criminal surveillance under [statute]."

**Important**: Request a case number when you file. If local police decline to investigate, escalate to the county DA's office.

**Limitations**: Local police vary enormously in responsiveness to surveillance complaints. Urban police departments with dedicated cybercrime units are more likely to investigate; rural departments may lack resources or expertise. If local police are unresponsive, proceed to state AG.

### 3.2 City and County Code Enforcement

**When to use**: Surveillance cameras that violate local ordinances, HOA disputes that local government can mediate, cameras used to harass in violation of local anti-harassment ordinances.

**How to report**: Contact your city's code enforcement division (often within the building or planning department). Many cities have 311 systems that route complaints to the correct department.

**What city code enforcement can do**: Issue notices of violation, require removal, impose fines, escalate to municipal court. Code enforcement cannot investigate criminal violations but can compel removal of cameras that violate local ordinances.

**HOA enforcement**: If you live in an HOA community, file a formal written complaint with the HOA board as well as code enforcement. HOA boards have authority to require camera removal from common areas if the installation violates HOA governing documents.

### 3.3 Federal Trade Commission (FTC)

**When to use**: Commercial surveillance products that lack required privacy disclosures; employer use of surveillance software or cameras that violates FTC Section 5 consumer protection standards; data security failures by companies operating surveillance systems.

**How to report**: File at ReportFraud.ftc.gov or call 1-877-FTC-HELP (1-877-382-4357). The FTC enters complaints into Consumer Sentinel, shared with 2,000+ law enforcement agencies.

**Recent enforcement**: In August 2024, the FTC took action against Verkada, a security camera firm, for failing to secure video footage and violating CAN-SPAM. The FTC's surveillance enforcement authority is expanding — commercial surveillance systems that fail to protect consumer privacy are within FTC jurisdiction.

**Limitations**: The FTC does not investigate individual neighbor or landlord disputes. Its jurisdiction is commercial actors and companies, not private individuals.

### 3.4 Federal Communications Commission (FCC)

**When to use**: Wireless surveillance cameras that may violate radio frequency regulations; cameras that intercept communications.

**How to report**: File at FCC.gov/consumers/guides/filing-informal-complaint. The FCC's primary surveillance-related jurisdiction is over devices that transmit or intercept communications — its authority over standard surveillance cameras is limited.

**Realistic scope**: FCC complaints are most useful for unlicensed wireless transmitting devices or interception of wireless communications. For most camera complaints, the FTC and state AG are more directly applicable.

### 3.5 State Attorney General Offices

**When to use**: Systematic violations by businesses or landlords; violations of state consumer protection laws; widespread pattern of surveillance harassment.

**Priority states**: California (oag.ca.gov), New York (ag.ny.gov), Illinois (illinoisattorneygeneral.gov), Washington (atg.wa.gov), Oregon (doj.state.or.us), Florida (myfloridalegal.com), Texas (oag.texas.gov).

**What to request**: File a consumer protection complaint. Request that the AG investigate under the state's consumer protection statute, privacy law, or both. AG offices are more likely to investigate patterns involving multiple victims — if your building or neighborhood has multiple affected residents, coordinate a joint complaint.

**Access**: Most AG offices have online complaint forms. For patterns involving vulnerable populations (immigrants, disability community), contact the AG's Civil Rights Division directly.

### 3.6 EEOC and State Labor Agencies (Workplace Surveillance)

**When to use**: Employer surveillance that constitutes harassment based on a protected characteristic (race, sex, national origin, disability, religion, age); employer surveillance in prohibited areas; failure to provide required notice of monitoring.

**EEOC**: File at EEOC.gov or call 1-800-669-4000. You must file an EEOC charge before suing for discrimination. The filing deadline is 180 days from the discriminatory act (300 days if a state agency also has jurisdiction).

**State labor agencies**: Most states with comprehensive labor protections have their own agencies that parallel EEOC authority. California (DFEH), New York (NYSDHR), Illinois (IDHR), Washington (WSHRC).

## Section 4: The Demand Letter Process

Before or alongside legal reporting, a formal demand letter serves multiple purposes: it creates a documented record of your claim, it gives the camera owner an opportunity to comply voluntarily (often the fastest path to removal), and it establishes a basis for civil litigation if voluntary removal does not occur.

### 4.1 When to Use a Demand Letter

Use a demand letter when:
- You have identified a specific, documentable camera violation
- You know who is responsible for the camera (neighbor, landlord, employer)
- You want the camera removed or repositioned without immediately involving law enforcement
- You are preparing for potential civil litigation

Do NOT use a demand letter as your first response to criminal voyeurism (hidden cameras in bathrooms, bedrooms, or changing areas). In criminal cases, contact law enforcement first. A demand letter to a criminal actor can tip them off to destroy evidence.

### 4.2 The 30-Day Response Window

Standard demand letters request a response within 30 days. This timeframe:
- Gives the recipient time to consult legal counsel
- Is short enough to establish urgency
- Creates a documented timeline for civil litigation

If the camera is an active threat to your safety (ongoing harassment, stalking), request a response within 72 hours and simultaneously file a police report.

### 4.3 Demand Letter Template

---

**[Your Name]**
**[Your Address]**
**[City, State, ZIP]**
**[Date]**

**[Camera Owner/Responsible Party Name]**
**[Their Address]**

**RE: Formal Demand for Immediate Removal of Unlawful Surveillance Camera / Cessation of Unlawful Surveillance**

Dear [Name/Property Manager/Employer]:

I am writing to formally notify you that I have identified a surveillance camera installation at [describe location and address] that I believe violates [applicable law(s) — e.g., California Penal Code § 632, state voyeurism statute, etc.] and constitutes an unlawful invasion of my privacy.

**Description of the Violation:**
On [date], I discovered [describe camera: its location, what it appears to be capturing, any audio capabilities]. Based on the camera's position and angle, it [captures/records/monitors] [describe: my bedroom window / bathroom area / private area of my yard / private conversations in my home / etc.], a space where I have a reasonable expectation of privacy under applicable law.

**Applicable Legal Requirements:**
Under [state] law, specifically [cite statute], [describe what the law requires or prohibits]. The installation and/or operation of this camera as currently configured violates this statute.

**What I Am Requesting:**
Within **30 days** of the date of this letter, I request that you:
1. Immediately cease any recording of [the described private space]
2. Remove or permanently reposition the camera so that it no longer captures [the private area]
3. Delete all recordings made of [the private space] to date
4. Provide written confirmation that steps 1-3 have been completed

**Consequences of Non-Compliance:**
If you do not comply with this demand within 30 days, I will take the following steps without further notice:
- File a criminal complaint with [local law enforcement agency] for violation of [state statute]
- File a complaint with [state attorney general] under [consumer protection / privacy statute]
- Seek civil remedies under [state privacy law / voyeurism statute / harassment statute], which provides for [actual damages / statutory damages of $X / attorney fees]
- [If applicable: File a complaint with the EEOC for unlawful workplace surveillance]

I am preserving all evidence of this violation and this letter.

Sincerely,

**[Your Name]**
**[Signature]**
**[Phone / Email]**

*Sent via: [Certified Mail / Email with read receipt / Hand delivery with witness]*
*Copy retained by: [You]*

### 4.4 Escalation Path

**If no response within 30 days:**
1. File police report (for criminal violations)
2. File state AG complaint (for consumer protection / pattern violations)
3. Consult with attorney for civil litigation
4. Contact organizations listed in Section 5

**If camera owner disputes the violation:**
1. Document their response in writing
2. Consult attorney to evaluate your legal position
3. Consider mediation (many jurisdictions offer free or low-cost housing mediation)

**If you receive threats in response to the demand letter:**
1. Contact police immediately — threats in response to a legal demand can constitute criminal harassment or obstruction
2. Document threats with screenshots, recordings (one-party consent states), or written notes
3. Consult attorney about restraining order

## Section 6: Specific Scenarios

### 6.1 Employer Surveillance Overreach

Employer surveillance is the most common legal surveillance context — and the one most commonly extended beyond legal bounds.

**What is legal:**
- Cameras in open work areas where employees have been notified (written notice is required in several states)
- Cameras in reception, entry/exit areas, cash handling areas
- Computer and network monitoring with notice (federal law requires notice for electronic communications monitoring)

**What is illegal:**
- Cameras in restrooms, locker rooms, break rooms (when separate from the main work area), or any area where employees have privacy expectations
- Hidden cameras installed without notice in states requiring disclosure (California, New York)
- Cameras targeting a specific employee based on race, sex, religion, disability, or national origin
- Audio recording of employee conversations in all-party consent states without consent
- Biometric surveillance (facial recognition) without consent under Illinois BIPA

**What to do:**
1. If you discover a hidden camera in a prohibited area, document it with photographs and date/time stamps, then contact HR in writing
2. If HR is unresponsive or retaliates, file with the EEOC and your state labor agency
3. In Illinois, consider a BIPA claim if biometric data was collected without consent — statutory damages are $1,000-$5,000 per violation
4. Consult an employment attorney; many take workplace privacy cases on contingency

**Specific red flag**: Cameras that are installed or repositioned after an employee files a discrimination complaint, requests a disability accommodation, or engages in union organizing activity may constitute retaliatory surveillance. Document the timing carefully.

### 6.2 Landlord-Tenant Surveillance

Landlord surveillance disputes are among the most commonly reported illegal camera situations. Landlords have legitimate interests in security of common areas; they do not have authority to surveil tenants' private spaces.

**What landlords can legally do:**
- Install cameras in building entries, lobbies, hallways, parking areas, laundry rooms, and other common areas
- In most states, install cameras on building exteriors capturing public areas

**What landlords cannot do:**
- Install cameras inside individual apartment units or rented rooms without tenant consent
- Install cameras that capture bathroom, bedroom, or private living areas
- Use cameras as tools of harassment or intimidation against tenants who complain about housing conditions
- In California, use cameras or surveillance information to facilitate eviction retaliation or immigration enforcement

**What to do:**
1. Document the camera (photos, description of what it records)
2. Send a written demand letter requesting removal (Template in Section 4)
3. Contact your local tenant rights organization
4. File a housing complaint with your city's housing authority or code enforcement
5. In California, unlawful landlord surveillance in the context of tenant intimidation may violate Civil Code § 1940.2 (tenant harassment statute) — file with the city attorney's office

**Scenario: Surveillance used to harass after filing repair requests:**
Landlords sometimes use cameras — legal ones in common areas and sometimes illegal ones in private spaces — to monitor and intimidate tenants who have filed habitability complaints. If you discover that a camera was repositioned or added after you filed a complaint, document the timeline. This pattern supports a retaliation claim.

### 6.3 Neighbor Disputes

Neighbor surveillance disputes are among the most nuanced because the camera placement (on their property) is often legal, but the angle (capturing your private space) may not be.

**Common patterns:**
- Neighbor's camera aimed at your backyard from their property line
- Camera mounted to capture your front door or driveway at an angle that also captures window interiors
- Cameras installed to harass a specific neighbor during a property dispute

**Legal framework:**
The pivotal question is always whether your reasonable expectation of privacy is being violated. A camera on a neighbor's property that captures a public area is almost always legal. A camera that captures your backyard's private areas, looks through your windows, or monitors your movements in a systematic pattern may be actionable.

**What to do:**
1. Photograph the camera's position and attempt to determine its angle
2. If you can establish that it captures a private area, send a demand letter
3. If the neighbor is unresponsive, contact local code enforcement and police
4. Document any harassing pattern (repeated camera repositioning, threats)
5. Consider civil mediation — neighbor disputes are often more efficiently resolved through mediation than litigation

**Oregon and Washington note**: Both states have privacy statutes (Oregon ORS § 163.700; Washington RCW § 9.73.060) that create civil causes of action specifically for privacy violations, including invasion of private spaces through cameras. Washington's statute allows recovery of attorney fees, making civil suits economically viable even for relatively small violations.

### 6.4 ICE and DHS Cameras in Immigrant Communities

Surveillance in immigrant communities has expanded dramatically since 2025, including through:
- ICE use of privately operated surveillance networks (Flock Safety license plate readers, ShotSpotter, CCTV camera networks)
- Local law enforcement sharing surveillance data with ICE under 287(g) agreements
- Landlords allowing ICE access to building surveillance systems
- Private neighbors or businesses reporting surveillance observations to immigration authorities

**What immigrant communities need to know:**

**ICE access to private camera systems requires a warrant.** Under the Fourth Amendment, ICE cannot compel private parties (landlords, businesses, HOAs) to provide surveillance footage without a judicial warrant. An administrative immigration warrant (Form I-200 or I-205) is not a judicial warrant and does not compel private parties to provide footage. If a landlord, business owner, or HOA receives an ICE request for footage:
- They are not legally required to comply without a judicial warrant
- They should consult an attorney before complying
- They may contact a tenant rights organization or immigrant rights organization for guidance

**What you can do if cameras are used for immigration surveillance:**
1. Document visible cameras in your building or neighborhood (location, angle, operator if identifiable)
2. Contact the National Immigration Law Center (nilc.org), CLINIC (cliniclegal.org), or your local immigrant rights organization
3. In California, file a complaint if a landlord provided surveillance footage to ICE without a judicial warrant — this may violate California's Immigrant Tenant Protection Act
4. Contact your state sanctuary policy office if the surveillance involves local law enforcement sharing data with ICE

**License plate reader (LPR) cameras**: Flock Safety and other LPR networks are privately operated but widely contracted with local law enforcement. The ACLU has documented extensive ICE use of LPR data through law enforcement data sharing. Contact the ACLU's Privacy Project if you believe LPR data is being used for immigration enforcement in violation of your state's sanctuary policy.

**Red card practice**: Keep an ILRC Red Card accessible. If ICE comes to your door citing camera footage or other surveillance information, do not open the door, do not confirm your identity, and say clearly: "I am exercising my right to remain silent. I do not consent to a search. If you have a warrant, show it under the door." Contact a rapid response hotline immediately (see ICE Victim Support Networks Guide for numbers).
