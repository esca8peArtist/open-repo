## Section 1: Legislative Developments June–August 2026

### Congressional Calendar

Congress is in district work period June 11-22. The Senate floor as of June 15 includes FISA Section 702 extension (extended to July 2, 2026 by House vote), FY 2027 appropriations markups (Labor/HHS/Education and Homeland Security ordered reported as amended by Appropriations Committee), and the nomination of Walter Clayton as DNI Director.

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed July 4, 2025, has implementation timelines running through 2026-2027. The OBBBA created an unaccountable $70 billion deportation slush fund through the Senate reconciliation bill signed June 10, 2026 — a direct accountability gap. This connects to existing Domain 56 (civil service) and the proposed Domain M (government procurement) below.

No major democracy-governance legislation is on the House or Senate floor for July-August 2026. The most consequential legislative calendar item affecting Phase 3 planning is the FY 2027 appropriations process: if no FY 2027 appropriations pass before October 1, 2026, a continuing resolution locks in current democratic-institution funding cuts for another year. This is a gap in the existing Phase 3 domain coverage.

### State Legislative Sessions

38 state legislatures were in session by January 2026. Nearly half have now concluded their 2026 sessions. States still in session as of June 2026 include California, Michigan, Pennsylvania, New York, and Ohio.

**Critical developing issue — Direct Democracy under attack:**

Lawmakers in more than 15 states introduced over 100 bills targeting the ballot measure process in 2026. Supermajority requirements will appear on November 2026 ballots in Missouri, North Dakota, South Dakota, and Utah. Missouri's proposal requires citizen-initiated constitutional amendments to pass statewide AND in every single congressional district — a structural impossibility for progressive policy in a gerrymandered state. Florida's House Bill 1205 imposed requirements so extreme that zero citizen-led initiatives qualified for the 2026 ballot. This constitutes a coordinated national attack on direct democracy as a democratic bypass mechanism.

**Ballot measure deadline structure:** State signature submission deadlines for 2026 ballot measures mostly fell between February and June 2026. The active advocacy window for the November 2026 measures targeting ballot initiative processes closes in September-October 2026.

**Voter rights bills:** 26 states advanced legislation restricting mail voting; 14 states advanced proof-of-citizenship requirements; 12 states advanced bills limiting early voting. Brennan Center's May 2026 State Voting Laws Roundup documents these in detail.

### Ballot Measure Developments

The 2026 ballot cycle has 333 active measures tracked by BISC as of May 31, 2026, with 107 certified. Key measures relevant to Phase 3 planning:

- **Virginia** (November 2026): Constitutional amendment to automatically restore voting rights to formerly-incarcerated individuals — on the ballot.
- **Kansas** (August 2026): Method of judicial selection — voters decide whether to change judge selection process.
- **Illinois**: State Finance Act amendment creating the Illinois Judicial Election Democracy Trust Fund (voluntary public campaign financing for judicial elections).
- **Missouri, North Dakota, South Dakota, Utah**: Anti-ballot-initiative supermajority requirements — if these pass, direct democracy is structurally foreclosed in those states for the 2028 cycle.

The anti-ballot-initiative measures have a November 3, 2026 decision date. Their passage or failure directly shapes Phase 3 research on constitutional resilience (Domain H) and state-level reform pathways.

## Section 2: Litigation Landscape Post-Callais/Skrmetti

### Supreme Court OT2025-2026 New Developments

**Trump v. Slaughter** (FTC independent agency removal power): Decision expected by end of June 2026. All six conservative justices indicated during oral argument that a president should have absolute control over independent agencies carrying out executive functions. If decided as signaled, *Slaughter* eliminates "for-cause" removal protections for FTC commissioners and signals the same outcome for NLRB, MSPB, FEC, EEOC, SEC, and CFTC. This would be a 5-10 domain cascade event — it directly implicates Domain K (judiciary restructuring), Domain 56 (civil service), and creates a new research angle on independent agency structural reform. *Slaughter* is the most consequential pending ruling for Phase 3 domain design.

**Alabama v. Middleton** (Callais redistricting cascade): SCOTUS vacated three lower court judgments on May 11, 2026 (unsigned shadow docket order) and remanded to reconsider in light of *Callais*. A lower court then blocked Alabama's 2023 discriminatory map. The case will return to SCOTUS — likely OT2026-2027. This extends the *Callais* redistricting cascade into the Phase 3 research window and strengthens the existing Domain 49 (post-election redistricting) case.

**Bost v. Illinois State Board of Elections**: SCOTUS will consider whether Illinois's mail ballot counting scheme (postmarked by Election Day but received after) violates federal law. Decision expected OT2025-2026. A ruling against the mail ballot scheme would create a new suppression mechanism affecting Phase 3 voting rights analysis.

**V-Dem US Downgrade (March 2026)**: The US fell from 20th to 51st place on the Liberal Democracy Index in one year — the largest single-year drop in the dataset's history. The US can no longer be classified as a liberal democracy, only an "electoral democracy." Freedom House's 2026 report ("The Growing Shadow of Autocracy") separately documented executive unilateral authority escalation, conflicts of interest, and weakened anticorruption safeguards. This international reclassification creates new research leverage for Domain I (International Democratic Standards) — it is no longer a comparative framework for reform proposals but empirical documentation of US democratic collapse.

### Circuit Court Developments

**D.C. Circuit** ruled that Trump likely has authority to remove NLRB and MSPB members without cause (Wilcox/Harris removal cases), staying reinstatement orders from lower courts. SCOTUS had already issued emergency stay on the same question (September 22, 2025 shadow docket order). This line of cases, consolidated with *Trump v. Slaughter*, will determine whether any independent agency is constitutionally independent. The *Slaughter* decision expected by June 30, 2026 will resolve the circuit-level dispute.

**Redistricting Circuit Landscape**: Post-*Callais*, courts in the 5th, 6th, 9th, and 11th Circuits are applying the new intentionality standard inconsistently. Alabama v. Middleton (11th Circuit) is the clearest example of a court refusing to apply *Callais* retroactively to pre-*Callais* discriminatory decisions. Circuit splits on *Callais* application are forming and will require SCOTUS resolution — potentially OT2026-2027, within Phase 3 research window.

### State Constitutional Litigation

**Direct democracy litigation**: After Florida's HB 1205 blocked all citizen-led initiatives for 2026, litigation challenging the law's constitutionality was filed. Democracy Docket documented the coordinated attack on ballot initiatives as a constitutional-democratic-rights question, not merely a procedural one. This creates a state constitutional litigation angle that is not currently covered by any Phase 3 domain.

**Kansas judicial selection**: August 2026 ballot measure on judge selection method — if passed, will shift judicial selection toward gubernatorial appointment with reduced merit commission input. Litigation over the measure's constitutionality is anticipated.

## Section 4: New Candidate Domains

### Candidate M — Direct Democracy Infrastructure Defense

**What it is**: A research domain analyzing the coordinated legislative attack on citizen-initiated ballot initiative processes across US states — supermajority requirements, district-by-district passage thresholds, signature requirement inflation, single-subject rule weaponization, and petition process restrictions — as a mechanism for capturing state-level democratic bypass routes. Distinct from voting rights suppression (which targets individual voters) and from campaign finance (which targets funding). This domain addresses the structural elimination of the legislative bypass mechanism.

**Why it is genuinely new**: No existing Phase 3 domain addresses the citizen initiative process. Domain H (Constitutional Architecture) addresses federal constitutional amendment pathways; Domain 51 (Campaign Finance) addresses money in campaigns. This domain addresses the direct democracy mechanism itself — a distinct constitutional structure present in 26 states that the resistance research framework has not analyzed.

**External deadline**: The November 3, 2026 ballot measures (Missouri, North Dakota, South Dakota, Utah) have a decision date. Research must be complete and distributed before the measures are voted on — by September 15-30, 2026 — to serve organizations running campaigns against them. This makes Candidate M Phase 2-adjacent (deliverable before Phase 3 formal launch), but it carries into Phase 3 if the measures pass (post-election direct democracy restoration analysis).

**Movement leverage**: BISC has a coalition of 93+ nonprofits engaged in direct democracy in 2026. Brennan Center, Democracy Docket, and Common Cause are all active in ballot initiative litigation and campaigns. Existing Phase 2 contacts (Common Cause, LWV) have active California ACA 13 work (requiring same supermajority to pass a supermajority requirement). Distribution network exists.

**Urgency score: 9/10** (September 2026 campaign window; November 2026 ballot decision)
**Movement leverage score: 8/10** (BISC, Brennan Center, Democracy Docket, Common Cause network active)
**Feasibility score: 7/10** (estimated 20-25 hours; BISC has primary data; Ballotpedia state-by-state coverage is comprehensive)
**Strategic impact score: 9/10** (direct democracy is the primary legislative bypass mechanism — if it is closed in a supermajority of states, every other reform pathway relies solely on electoral majorities the existing framework documents are being suppressed)

**Estimated research hours**: 20-25 hours

**Expert contact starter list**:
- Ballot Initiative Strategy Center (ballot.org) — primary coordinating body, publishes national tracker, has campaign connections in all 4 supermajority-measure states
- Democracy Docket (democracydocket.com; Marc Elias) — already Tier 1 contact; filed direct democracy litigation
- Common Cause (commoncause.org) — already Phase 2 contact (Domain 51); active on California ACA 13

### Candidate N — Federal Whistleblower Protection Collapse and Restoration Architecture

**What it is**: A research domain analyzing the collapse of federal whistleblower protection infrastructure under the Trump second term — OPM's proposed mandatory NDA for all federal employees (May 2026, 40,000+ comments, 94% opposed), Office of Special Counsel capture, Inspectors General mass firings (20+ fired January 20, 2025), the DOGE retaliation documented in Dan Berulis/NLRB case, and the specific legislative proposals for restoration. Whistleblower protection is the information-access mechanism that makes democratic accountability possible — its collapse is a precondition for the impunity documented across Domains 29, 38, 56, and K.

**Why it is genuinely new**: No existing Phase 3 domain addresses the whistleblower infrastructure as a research subject. Domain 56 (civil service) analyzes Schedule Policy/Career and merit system erosion. Domain 29 (prosecutorial weaponization) analyzed DOJ capture. Neither analyzes the disclosure infrastructure — Inspector General independence, OSC enforcement, whistleblower retaliation prohibition, anti-gag protections — that enables accountability actors to receive and act on information about government misconduct.

**External deadline**: The OPM mandatory NDA final rule comment period closed. The rule is moving forward. Congressional Whistleblower Protection Act (Blumenthal, 8 Democratic Senate cosponsors) is the legislative counter — it needs distribution to judiciary and government oversight committee staff before January 3, 2027 (120th Congress seating). Senate Grassley (R-IA) has separately introduced two bipartisan bills extending whistleblower protections. The bipartisan angle is the distribution lever.

**Movement leverage**: Government Accountability Project (GAP), POGO, Project on Government Oversight, and the Government Accountability Office alumni network are the primary constituencies. AFGE and NTEU (already Phase 2 Domain 56 contacts) represent the federal employee population affected. The DOGE whistleblower cluster creates a new media-active population of whistleblowers who will be looking for advocacy and legal support.

**Urgency score: 8/10** (January 3, 2027 Congressional seating; OPM NDA rule advancing; active retaliation documented)
**Movement leverage score: 7/10** (GAP, POGO, AFGE, NTEU existing contacts; Grassley bipartisan angle opens Republican distribution pathway)
**Feasibility score: 8/10** (estimated 18-22 hours; legislative inventory well-documented; primary sources in Federal News Network, Government Executive, Grassley/Blumenthal bill texts)
**Strategic impact score: 8/10** (whistleblower protection is the accountability prerequisite — its collapse makes all other accountability mechanisms blind; restoring it is logically prior to using any other reform)

**Estimated research hours**: 18-22 hours

**Expert contact starter list**:
- Government Accountability Project (whistleblower.org; Tom Devine, Legal Director) — already in Phase 3 Domain 56 contact list; primary whistleblower advocacy organization
- Project on Government Oversight (pogo.org) — independent watchdog tracking IG firings and DOGE access; has published DOGE whistleblower documentation
- Sen. Grassley's office (Chuck Grassley, Senate Judiciary) — introduced two bipartisan whistleblower extension bills; creates Republican distribution pathway

### Candidate O — Government Procurement as Democratic Corruption Vector

**What it is**: A research domain analyzing federal government procurement as the mechanism through which executive capture is converted into structural advantage — Musk/SpaceX's $15.4B in government contracts while Musk runs DOGE (directing cuts that did not touch his own contracts), DOGE personnel with direct regulatory conflicts of interest (25-year-old software engineer managing CFPB access while holding $365,000 in Apple/Tesla stock), the $81 million funneled to DOGE with no accountability or transparency, and the legislative architecture (Federal Acquisition Regulation, OCI rules, Ethics in Government Act gaps) that should prevent this but does not. Procurement corruption is how private power is institutionalized within state capacity.

**Why it is genuinely new**: Domain 38 (AI regulatory capture and governance) addresses technology sector regulatory capture. Domain 51 (campaign finance) addresses money in politics. Neither addresses federal contracting as a corruption mechanism — the specific use of procurement to reward allies, disadvantage adversaries, and create financial entanglements that subordinate regulatory enforcement. The Special Government Employee loophole (Musk's classification) is a specific legal mechanism that is not analyzed anywhere in the existing framework.

**External deadline**: The Preventing Organizational Conflicts of Interest in Federal Acquisition Act (S. 3905/H.R. 7602) is pending. House Oversight Committee hearings on DOGE procurement conflicts are ongoing. January 3, 2027 Congress seating creates a Government Oversight Committee oversight window. February 2027 Federal Acquisition Regulation comment period for OCI rules (anticipated) creates a regulatory comment opportunity.

**Movement leverage**: Campaign Legal Center (already Phase 2 contact, Domain 51), POGO, Revolving Door Project, Just Security (published "Pioneering Plunder" analysis of procurement corruption), Democracy Forward (already in Phase 1 contact network via anti-DOGE litigation). Medium movement leverage — this is primarily a policy/legal audience, not a mass mobilization audience.

## Section 5: Comparison to Original Phase 3 Candidates (What Shifted in Priority)

**Domains that increased in urgency since February 2026:**

- **Domain I (International Standards)**: V-Dem's March 2026 US downgrade from "liberal democracy" to "electoral democracy" — and the largest single-year drop in their dataset history — transforms this domain from a comparative/reform document into an empirical accountability document. The research leverage is now higher than the February 2026 scorecard anticipated. Suggest upgrading urgency from 6/10 to 8/10.

- **Domain K (Judiciary Restructuring)**: *Trump v. Slaughter* (decision expected June 30, 2026) will likely eliminate for-cause removal protections for all independent agencies. This expands Domain K's scope — it is no longer only SCOTUS reform but all independent agency structural reform. Research hours estimate may need to increase from 16-20 to 22-28 to cover the full independent agency cascade.

- **Domain 37a (Post-Election Section 3)**: Alabama v. Middleton (Callais redistricting cascade returning to SCOTUS) strengthens the post-election contestation research angle documented in Domain 37a. No priority change needed — urgency remains 9/10.

**Domains whose priority is unchanged:**
Domain H, Domain 49, Domain 51, Domain 56, Domain L: external deadline structure unchanged. Research angles strengthen but no priority reordering needed.

**New candidates' fit in existing sequence:**
- Candidate M (Direct Democracy) has a September 2026 deadline — it belongs in Phase 2 as an accelerated deliverable, not Phase 3. If produced in July-August 2026, it serves the November 2026 ballot campaigns and carries into Phase 3 as a post-election analysis.
- Candidate N (Whistleblower Protection) fits in January-February 2027 alongside Domain 56 post-election update. The legislative window (Congressional Whistleblower Protection Act, Grassley bills) is the same window as Domain 56's civil service restoration push. The two domains can share distribution infrastructure.
- Candidate O (Procurement Corruption) is best scheduled for February-March 2027 alongside Domain I and Domain J. It is deferrable to Q2 2027 without loss of primary value.
