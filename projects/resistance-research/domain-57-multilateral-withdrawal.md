## 5-Section Document Outline

*The research document is complete. This outline is the structural map for future framing updates and for contact targeting — each section maps to specific audience segments.*

### Section 1: The Withdrawal Architecture — Constitutional Framework and Legal Design
*~1,400 words | Audience: International law practitioners, ASIL, Just Security, Lawfare*

- Treaty Clause (Article II) and the Senate consent requirement
- The OLC 2020 opinion claiming exclusive executive withdrawal authority
- Youngstown framework applied to treaty withdrawal (Zone 3 analysis for NATO, Zone 2 for executive agreements)
- Goldwater v. Carter justiciability gap and post-Zivotofsky shift
- Executive agreements vs. Senate-ratified treaties: the two-tier problem

**Primary audience for Section 1**: Lawfare, Just Security, ASIL — constitutional law practitioners who work the executive power / foreign affairs intersection. These are the contacts for whom the Youngstown and Goldwater analysis is the primary entry point.

### Section 2: The 2025-2026 Case Studies — Scope and Democratic Harm
*~2,200 words | Audience: Foreign policy think tanks, Carnegie, CFR, PIIE, Brookings*

- 2.1 January 7, 2026 mass withdrawal memorandum (66 organizations — the full empirical record)
- 2.2 NATO withdrawal threat: NDAA Section 1250A protection and enforcement gap
- 2.3 ICC sanctions: criminal exposure for US persons assisting accountability proceedings
- 2.4 UN Human Rights Council / UPR withdrawal: domestic information environment loss
- 2.5 WTO functional withdrawal: *Learning Resources v. Trump* (Feb 20, 2026) and the tariff-treaty parallel
- 2.6 Refugee and asylum regime: non-refoulement obligation and executive suspension

**Primary audience for Section 2**: Carnegie Endowment (International Law Program), PIIE (trade and international economics), Brookings (foreign policy and governance), CFR (International Institutions and Global Governance program). The case study catalog is the primary entry point for foreign policy researchers.

### Section 3: Global Pattern Analysis — Comparative Cases and Normative Contagion
*~1,200 words | Audience: International democracy organizations, IRAP, HIAS, Human Rights First*

- Hungary's ICC withdrawal (April 2025, following Orbán-Netanyahu meeting during active warrant) as US-licensed normative contagion
- Turkey's CoE drift and functional democratic erosion correlation
- Brexit comparison: institutional exit without democratic exit rationale
- Prior US withdrawals (UNESCO 1984, Paris Agreement Trump I, WHO Trump I) — what the Biden re-entry pattern shows about reversibility and cost
- China's GONGO strategy inside UN human rights bodies as the parallel track (capture vs. exit — both dismantle accountability from different directions)

**Primary audience for Section 3**: IRAP (refugee accountability angle), HIAS (human rights monitoring), Human Rights First (comparative authoritarian drift). The normative contagion frame — that US withdrawal licenses other states' exit — is the entry point for human rights organizations working on multilateral accountability.

### Section 4: Constitutional Asymmetry Framing — The Democratic Design Failure
*~1,100 words | Audience: Brennan Center, constitutional law scholars, domestic legal organizations*

- The asymmetric consent problem: two-thirds supermajority entry / unilateral executive exit
- OLC exclusivity claim as practical consequence: Senate consent becomes a temporary procedural hurdle
- NDAA Section 1250A as proof of concept: Congress can constrain presidential unilateralism when it chooses to act
- The *Learning Resources v. Trump* tariff ruling as analogous structural reasoning
- The justiciability gap: Section 1250A has no standing authorization; who can enforce it?
- Design failures in international agreement architecture: the two-tier problem creates predictable executive arbitrage

**Primary audience for Section 4**: Brennan Center (executive power program), constitutional law reviews, Institute for Constitutional Advocacy and Protection. This section is most useful for organizations focused on domestic constitutional constraints on executive power — the multilateral frame is a new data point for an argument they are already making.

### Section 5: Reform Pathways
*~900 words | Audience: All Tier 1 contacts; most directly actionable for ASIL and congressional-focused organizations*

- Treaty Withdrawal Notification Act: require 60-day notice to Congress before any treaty withdrawal, with opportunity for congressional review and disapproval
- Multilateral Commitment Protection Act: extend the NDAA Section 1250A NATO model to the full class of Senate-ratified treaties (require either Senate consent or an Act of Congress for withdrawal)
- ICC Sanctions Repeal: restore US lawyer capacity to cooperate with international accountability proceedings
- Litigation authorization: close the Section 1250A enforcement gap by providing Congress with standing to challenge executive withdrawal violations
- Comparative models: UK Parliamentary sovereignty as an alternative accountability model; EU institutional lock-in mechanisms

**Primary audience for Section 5**: All Tier 1 contacts benefit from the reform architecture. ASIL and Just Security are most likely to publish commentary on the specific legislative proposals. Brennan Center and Campaign Legal Center are most likely to develop model legislation or litigation theory from the standing authorization proposal.
