## Section 2: Research and Writing Templates

### 2.1 Citation-First Research Template

Every research session for Domains 57 and 59 (not yet written) should follow this protocol. Domains 56 and 58 (existing drafts) use a modification of this for the production pass.

**Before writing any paragraph:**
1. Identify the claim the paragraph will make.
2. Locate the source that confirms that claim. Write the citation first, in the bibliography-ready format below.
3. Write the paragraph around the confirmed citation. Do not write from memory and insert citations afterward.

**Citation format (consistent with existing domain documents):**
- Academic journals: `[Author surname et al., "Title," Journal Name, Vol. X, No. Y (Year), DOI or URL]`
- Government documents: `[Agency, "Document Title" (Date), URL]`
- Think tank/advocacy reports: `[Organization, "Title" (Month Year), URL]`
- News: `[Outlet, "Title" (Date), URL]`

**For each confirmed empirical claim, record:**
```
CLAIM: [one sentence statement of the finding]
SOURCE: [full citation as above]
ACCESS STATUS: [Open / Paywalled / Library required]
CONFIRMED: [yes/no — did you access the source directly?]
```

This format prevents the most common quality failure in domain documents: empirical claims that cite real sources but mischaracterize their findings.

---

### 2.2 Evidence Checklist — What Counts as "Confirmed" vs. "Preliminary"

**Confirmed** (cite in the body of the domain document):
- You have directly accessed the source and read the relevant section
- The claim you are making is explicitly stated in the source (not inferred)
- The URL works as of the research session date
- For quantitative claims: you have seen the specific table or figure, not just a secondary summary

**Preliminary** (flag in research notes, do not cite as established):
- Claim is inferred from a source that does not explicitly state it
- URL is not yet confirmed live
- Paywalled source has not been accessed — only secondary summary available
- Claim appears in only one source with no corroboration

**Verification rule for each domain's empirical backbone claims:**

| Domain | Claim requiring double-source confirmation |
|---|---|
| 56 | Number of positions affected by Schedule P/C (50,000 figure — confirm Federal Register + CRS) |
| 57 | Number of organizations in January 7 withdrawal (66 figure — confirm WH Memorandum + Al Jazeera taxonomy) |
| 58 | Eligible-but-unregistered tribal population (4.7M figure — confirm Native Americans Today + cross-check NARF) |
| 59 | Income-participation gap (42%/16% — confirm Census CPS November 2024, not just PRRI) |

**What does NOT count as a confirmed citation:**
- A citation to a citation (citing Source A's reference to Source B)
- Wikipedia as a primary source (Wikipedia for background is acceptable; always follow through to the primary source it cites)
- Press releases summarizing reports (cite the report itself, not the press release, unless the press release language is itself analytically relevant)

### 2.4 Outline-to-Draft Conversion Process

This is the process for converting a research outline (Domains 57 and 59) into a full prose section. It is adapted from the workflow that produced Domain 56's 6,800-word draft.

**Step 1 — Research pass (do not write prose).** Read all Primary sources for the section. Record confirmed claims using the Citation-First template. Build a list of: (a) claims that are confirmed, (b) claims that are preliminary only, (c) claims in the outline that cannot yet be sourced.

**Step 2 — Section outline (one paragraph per claim).** Write one sentence per paragraph you intend to write. This is your outline layer. Each sentence should be a complete claim, not a topic label. Example:
- Bad: "discuss the constitutional asymmetry"
- Good: "Article II requires a two-thirds Senate supermajority for treaty ratification but is silent on withdrawal, creating an asymmetry that CRS R48868 (February 2026) identifies as the constitutional crux of the reform problem"

**Step 3 — Write from citations.** For each sentence in your outline, write the paragraph. Place the citation at the point of the specific claim, not at the end of the paragraph. If you write three claims in one paragraph, each needs its own citation.

**Step 4 — Section boundary check.** After drafting a section, read it once for scope. Ask: does this section argue the domain's core claim, or does it drift into adjacent territory that belongs in another domain? Cut anything in the second category and note where it belongs.

**Step 5 — Word count gate.** Each section should be within 20% of its target. If a section runs long, the excess is almost always setup and context that can be compressed. The body argument is rarely the problem — the wind-up is.
