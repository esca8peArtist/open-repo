## Tier 1 — Highest Priority (Produce First: July 2026)

### Candidate 1 (Rank 1): Domain 44 — Reproductive Freedom as Democratic Architecture

**The gap it fills:** No existing domain analyzes reproductive freedom through the democratic-design lens. The rights-based framing is well-covered by existing advocacy materials. What is not covered is the structural exclusion argument: how restrictions on reproductive autonomy produce civic and economic exclusion, how the ballot initiative suppression documented in Domain 33 is being deployed specifically to override abortion access measures, and what the international comparative evidence (Ireland 2018, Poland 2021, Hungary's use of reproductive policy as a democratic backsliding instrument) shows about the relationship between reproductive autonomy and democratic resilience.

**Why it is ranked first:** The reproductive rights sector has the largest organized constituency of any Phase 2 candidate and the clearest 2026 advocacy window. Missouri, Nevada, Virginia, Idaho, and Nebraska have active 2026 ballot measures. The movement has demonstrated extraordinary direct-democracy organizing capacity since Dobbs: voters in seven states passed protective measures in 2024. The framework's distribution to Reproductive Freedom for All, NARAL state affiliates, and Planned Parenthood Action Fund would reach thousands of organizers who are not currently reachable through think-tank outreach.

**Phase 1 integration:** Primary integration with Domain 33 (State Legislative Autocratization — the same ballot initiative suppression toolkit that targets reproductive rights restoration), Domain 1 (NVRA nexus: the communities most affected by reproductive restrictions are also subject to systematic voter suppression), Domain 31 (Medicaid-reproductive care nexus: the OBBBA Medicaid cuts reduce reproductive healthcare access directly), Domain 7 (rights protection — First Amendment and privacy dimensions of government-mandated disclosure laws in abortion ban states).

**Research scope:** 5,000–6,500 words. Primary sources: KFF reproductive rights state tracker, Guttmacher Institute state law data, International IDEA comparative analysis, Ireland Citizens' Assembly case study (2016-2018), Poland Constitutional Tribunal analysis, Reproductive Freedom for All 2026 ballot measure tracking.

**Advocacy window:** Active now — 2026 ballot measures are in signature collection or final campaign phases. Category A outreach (Reproductive Freedom for All, NARAL state affiliates, Planned Parenthood Action Fund, URGE) should be ready by July 2026.

**Organizing brief topic:** "How abortion restrictions function as voter suppression — and what that means for the democracy movement."

### Candidate 2 (Rank 2): Domain 45 — Labor Rights, NLRB Capture, and Worker Power as Democratic Infrastructure

**The gap it fills:** Domain 17 (Labor and Employment) exists as a proposal-body entry but has no standalone research file. The existing NLRB analysis in Domain 6 covers the Humphrey's Executor constitutional collapse but does not analyze worker rights from the organizing angle. The corporate constitutional challenge to the NLRB — SpaceX, Amazon, Trader Joe's, and Starbucks arguing the NLRB is unconstitutional — is the most significant attack on collective bargaining rights since Taft-Hartley (1947), but it does not appear in any existing domain as a democracy crisis.

**Why it is ranked second:** AFL-CIO membership is 12.5 million. SEIU membership is 2 million. These institutions run their own research departments, legislative affairs teams, and member education programs. A domain that frames the NLRB constitutional crisis and the anti-union organizing campaign as a democratic design failure — not just a labor relations dispute — would be integrated into member education curricula for an audience that dwarfs Phase 1's combined Tier 1-3 contact list. The AFL-CIO-EPI relationship means EPI publication of analysis triggers automatic distribution into the labor federation's network.

**Phase 1 integration:** Primary integration with Domain 6 (Judicial Independence — Humphrey's Executor collapse as the constitutional foundation for NLRB incapacity), Domain 38 (Financial Sector Independence — parallel corporate capture pattern: crypto companies captured financial regulation the same way Amazon captured labor regulation), Domain 29 (Prosecutorial Weaponization — DOJ weaponization against labor organizers has historical precedent and contemporary echoes), Domain 2 (Civil Service and Executive Accountability — Schedule Policy/Career attacks on career civil servants at DOL and NLRB).

**Research scope:** 5,000–6,500 words. Primary sources: EPI labor law analysis, Georgetown Law Poverty Journal NLRB future analysis, NLRB docket data on ULP filings and processing times, SpaceX/Amazon/Trader Joe's NLRB constitutional challenge filings, Labor Notes organizing data, AFL-CIO Future of Work commission report, Janus v. AFSCME implementation data.

**Advocacy window:** Active — the NLRB constitutional challenge will likely reach the Supreme Court in the next 12-18 months. Labor organizing in the private sector has been accelerating (70%+ win rate in union elections). The framework's distribution to AFL-CIO research department, SEIU legislative affairs, and LaborNotes would reach organizers at the inflection point of this fight.

**Organizing brief topic:** "Why the NLRB constitutional attack is a democracy crisis — and what unions can do about it before the Supreme Court rules."

## Tier 2 — High Priority (Produce Second: August-September 2026)

### Candidate 3 (Rank 3): Domain 46 — Housing Instability, Eviction, and Democratic Exclusion

**The gap it fills:** No existing domain addresses housing through the democratic-exclusion lens. The research base is now robust: eviction reduces voter turnout (Slee and Desmond 2023), housing instability creates categorical barriers to voter ID, voter registration, and mail ballot delivery, and the communities most affected — Black and Latinx renters evicted at twice the rate of white renters — are the same communities subject to systematic voter suppression documented in Domain 1.

**Why it is ranked third:** The housing organizing constituency (tenant unions, homeless coalition staffers, housing court advocates, NLIHC policy network) is genuinely grassroots in a way that the labor and reproductive rights sectors are not. These are organizations working directly with people experiencing displacement in real time. They are not policy analysts — they are street-level practitioners who need a democratic-design framing to make the case for their advocacy to public officials, funders, and coalition partners. The framework's housing domain would be the first materials to reach this constituency.

**Phase 1 integration:** Domain 31 (Medicaid-housing nexus: housing-unstable Medicaid beneficiaries lose coverage when they lose address stability — a documented compounding vulnerability), Domain 1 (NVRA nexus and eviction-turnout evidence), Domain 22 (Racial Justice — racial disparity in eviction rates as documented civil rights violation), Domain 33 (State Legislative Autocratization — state preemption of local tenant protection ordinances as an anti-democratic mechanism).

**Research scope:** 4,500–6,000 words. Primary sources: Eviction Lab data (Slee and Desmond 2023 JPSP paper), National Low Income Housing Coalition state-by-state data, Urban Institute housing instability and voting barrier research, National Alliance to End Homelessness civic participation research, ABA Human Rights journal (voting rights for unhoused people with felony records, 2025).

**Advocacy window:** Evergreen (no specific legislative deadline), but the OBBBA Medicaid-housing connection creates an August-September 2026 window as Medicaid work requirement implementation moves through the comment period.

**Organizing brief topic:** "Every eviction is a suppressed vote — the data and what tenant organizers can do about it."

### Candidate 4 (Rank 4): Domain 47 — Campaign Finance, Dark Money, and Corporate Capture of Democratic Institutions

**The gap it fills:** The framework documents corporate capture sector by sector — crypto-PAC capture of financial regulation (Domain 38), pharmaceutical industry capture of drug pricing (Domain 42), anti-union corporate campaigns (Domain 45). What it lacks is the meta-analysis: Citizens United as the structural mechanism through which all of these sector-specific captures operate. Dark money ($1.3 billion in the 2024 cycle) is the connective tissue.

**Why it is ranked fourth:** The 2026 DISCLOSE Act legislative window (sponsored by all 47 Senate Democrats, 139 House Democrats) and four state-level dark money reform ballot measures (tracked by OpenSecrets) create active advocacy hooks. Campaign finance reform organizations (Common Cause, Campaign Legal Center, Issue One) have their own robust distribution infrastructure and would amplify to their networks. The domain is the most likely Phase 2 candidate to reach both the policy-maker desk (via Campaign Legal Center's existing think-tank relationships) and the organizer's hands (via Common Cause's state-level chapters and Issue One's cross-partisan coalition).

**Phase 1 integration:** Domain 38 (Financial Sector Independence — crypto PAC spending as the financial sector analog), Domain 33 (State Legislative Autocratization — dark money funding the ballot initiative suppression campaigns), Domain 42 (Drug Policy — pharmaceutical industry lobbying capture of scheduling decisions), Domain 7 (Rights Protection — Citizens United's first amendment architecture), Domain 35 (Supreme Court 2026 — likely SCOTUS review of remaining campaign finance regulations in the 2026 term preview).

**Research scope:** 4,500–5,500 words. Primary sources: OpenSecrets dark money database, Campaign Legal Center Citizens United tracker, FEC disclosure data, DISCLOSE Act of 2026 one-pager (Whitehouse.senate.gov), Center for American Progress SCOTUS campaign finance analysis, Montana Initiative 194 documentation (from Domain 33 research), California Fair Elections Act.

**Advocacy window:** Active — DISCLOSE Act is live legislation. 2026 dark money ballot measures are in campaign phase. Arizona Prop. 211 dark money challenge provides the judicial mechanism case study.

**Organizing brief topic:** "The dark money machine funding the attacks on ballot initiatives, unions, and voting rights — and the five reforms that could stop it."

## Tier 3 — Important (Produce Third: October 2026)

### Candidate 5 (Rank 5): Domain 48 — Environmental Justice and Community Democratic Participation in Environmental Decision-Making

**The gap it fills:** The NEPA formal repeal (effective January 8, 2026), closure of all ten EPA environmental justice regional offices, and cancellation of $400 million in EJ-related grants represent the most significant rollback of community participation in environmental decision-making in the law's 56-year history. No existing domain covers this as a democratic participation issue — only as an environmental policy issue.

**Research scope:** 4,000–5,000 words.
**Organizing brief topic:** "How NEPA's repeal silenced communities of color — and the legal challenges that remain open."

**The gap it fills:** The revocation of Executive Order 14112 on Tribal Sovereignty and Self-Governance (March 2025), $1.5 billion in clean energy grant terminations to tribal communities, and the Census Bureau's withdrawal from the Indigenous Data Sovereignty Symposium are the most direct attacks on tribal self-determination since the termination policy era. No existing domain covers tribal sovereignty as a democratic design question.

**Research scope:** 4,500–5,500 words.
**Organizing brief topic:** "The treaty rights the federal government is violating — and what tribal governments and their allies can do right now."

### Candidate 7 (Rank 7): Domain 50 — Disability Rights, Civic Access, and the Democratic Participation of Disabled Americans

**The gap it fills:** Domain 41b covers disability through the benefits-access lens. The civic access dimension — 57% of disabled people experiencing barriers to civic engagement, 11% reporting difficulty voting, ACL cuts reducing independent living center funding — requires distinct treatment. The Section 504 regulatory rollback under the current administration is the most significant legal development.

**Research scope:** 3,500–4,500 words.
**Organizing brief topic:** "Every voting line that is inaccessible is a suppressed vote — the disability rights agenda for democratic reform."

## Tier 4 — Medium Priority (Produce Post-October 2026 or as standalone resources)

### Candidate 8 (Rank 8): Domain 51 — Felony Disenfranchisement and Re-Enfranchisement as Democratic Repair

**The gap it fills:** Domain 1 (Electoral Reform) mentions felony disenfranchisement (4 million Americans; 1 in 22 Black adults) but does not analyze it as a democratic design failure deserving standalone treatment. Domain 42 (Drug Policy) covers the disenfranchisement-drug enforcement nexus in the regulatory capture context. A standalone domain would document the state-by-state re-enfranchisement movement, the 2025-2026 reform campaigns (Massachusetts, Washington), and the public safety research showing that voting rights restoration reduces recidivism.

**Phase 1 integration:** Domain 1, Domain 42, Domain 14 (Criminal Justice), Domain 22 (Racial Justice).
**Research scope:** 3,500–4,500 words.
**Organizing brief topic:** "4 million missing votes — and the state-by-state campaigns to restore them before 2026."

### Candidate 9 (Rank 9): Domain 52 — Civil Society Suppression and the Nonprofit Independence Crisis

**The gap it fills:** The Trump administration's use of tax threats, federal takeovers, and embedded federal agents against civil society organizations — documented in NationofChange's "No nonprofit is safe" coverage (2025) — is a systematic attack on the institutional infrastructure of democratic civil society. The IRS's failure to enforce 501(c)(4) political activity rules while selectively targeting progressive-affiliated organizations creates an uneven enforcement playing field. This is distinct from Domain 29 (Prosecutorial Weaponization), which focuses on DOJ rather than IRS and administrative mechanisms.

**Phase 1 integration:** Domain 29 (Prosecutorial Weaponization — IRS as a weaponization mechanism parallel to DOJ), Domain 26 (Government Accountability — Inspector General and accountability mechanism breakdown), Domain 2 (Civil Service — IRS career staff under pressure to apply enforcement selectively).
**Research scope:** 4,000–5,000 words.
**Organizing brief topic:** "If the IRS can threaten any nonprofit that receives federal grants, every civil society organization is at risk — including yours."

### Candidate 10 (Rank 10): Domain 53 — Mutual Aid Networks, Tax Law, and the Legal Infrastructure of Democratic Solidarity

**The gap it fills:** Mutual aid networks are the most decentralized and resilient form of democratic organization, demonstrated during COVID-19 and currently during the 2025-2026 funding freeze. The legal gray zone documented by Time (March 2026) — that mutual aid monetary transfers fall outside clear IRS guidance, exposing participants to tax liability — is a structural barrier to democratic self-organization. The policy ask (IRS guidance or a statutory exclusion for communal mutual aid transfers) is narrow and technically achievable.

**Phase 1 integration:** Domain 7 (Rights Protection — freedom of association), Domain 26 (Government Accountability — IRS as an administrative tool), Domain 5 (Fiscal Reform — tax law and its democratic effects).
**Research scope:** 3,000–4,000 words.
**Organizing brief topic:** "Why mutual aid is legally risky — and the simple rule change that could fix it."

### Candidate 11 (Rank 11): Domain 54 — Digital Organizing, Secure Communications, and the Technical Infrastructure of Democratic Resistance

**The gap it fills:** The cybersecurity hardening trilogy (`projects/cybersecurity-hardening/`) covers operational security for individuals. What it does not cover is the organizational dimension: how democratic civil society organizations secure their communications, protect organizing databases, and maintain operational capacity under sustained surveillance pressure. Domain 21 (Data Privacy) covers surveillance law; this candidate would cover the practical organizational response. The distinction matters because the audience is different: this domain is for organizing directors, not for individual activists.

**Phase 1 integration:** Domain 21 (Data Privacy and Surveillance), Domain 36 (AI Governance — algorithmic content moderation affecting organizing communications), Domain 7 (Rights — First Amendment protections for organizing communications).
**Research scope:** 3,500–4,500 words.
**Organizing brief topic:** "Securing your organizing infrastructure against state and corporate surveillance — the organizational checklist."

### Candidate 12 (Rank 12): Domain 55 — Rural Democracy and Agricultural Community Civic Participation

**The gap it fills:** The framework has a documented gap in rural organizing constituencies. Rural communities are affected by the tariff crisis (Domain 23), the healthcare Medicaid crisis (Domain 31), and the environmental rollbacks (Domain 15 and Domain 48), but none of these domains are framed for rural audiences or distributed through rural organizing channels. The agricultural community specifically — affected by farm bill cuts, tariff retaliation, and rural hospital closures — has organized civic infrastructure (Farm Bureau, National Farmers Union, rural co-ops) that is not currently reached by any Phase 1 or Phase 2 distribution target.

**Phase 1 integration:** Domain 23 (Trade Policy and Tariffs — tariff retaliation directly affects agricultural exports), Domain 31 (Healthcare — rural hospital closures are the most visible healthcare access crisis), Domain 9 (Federalism — rural-urban preemption conflicts), Domain 15 (Environment — WOTUS and agricultural land use regulation).
**Research scope:** 4,000–5,000 words.
**Organizing brief topic:** "What the tariffs, Medicaid cuts, and rural hospital closures have in common — and the democratic reform agenda that addresses all three."

### Candidate 13 (Rank 13): Domain 56 — Voting Rights Restoration: A 50-State Playbook for the 2026-2028 Window

**The gap it fills:** This is a more operationally focused companion to Domain 1 (Electoral Reform) and the proposed Domain 51 (Felony Disenfranchisement). Domain 1 documents the crisis; Domain 56 would document the remedial architecture — the state-by-state legislative, litigation, and ballot initiative strategies that are viable in the 2026-2028 window, with specific analysis of which state legislative chambers have the votes for re-enfranchisement legislation, which states have court cases that could force registration access, and which states have the ballot initiative architecture to pursue constitutional amendments.

**Phase 1 integration:** Domain 1 (Electoral Reform — primary foundation), Domain 33 (State Legislative Autocratization — the preemption and ballot initiative suppression context), Domain 37 (Federal Executive Interference in 2026 Midterms — electoral integrity context).
**Research scope:** 4,000–5,000 words.
**Organizing brief topic:** "The states where voting rights expansion is winnable before 2028 — and the campaigns that can get there."

### Candidate 14 (Rank 14): Domain 57 — Criminal Justice Reform and the Democratic Participation of Justice-Involved Communities

**The gap it fills:** Domain 14 (Criminal Justice and Policing) is a proposal-body entry. This candidate would produce a standalone research file focused specifically on the democratic participation dimension — the civic exclusion effects of mass incarceration (not just disenfranchisement, which Domain 51 covers, but jury exclusion, public employment restrictions, housing discrimination, and the debt burden that follows criminal records). The Sentencing Project, Prison Policy Initiative, and Justice Lab at Columbia have extensive research on these mechanisms.

**Phase 1 integration:** Domain 14 (Criminal Justice — proposal foundation), Domain 22 (Racial Justice — racial disparity in incarceration), Domain 42 (Drug Policy — drug enforcement as the primary driver of mass incarceration), Domain 1 (Electoral Reform — felon disenfranchisement nexus).
**Research scope:** 4,500–5,500 words.
**Organizing brief topic:** "Beyond the ballot: the full civic exclusion architecture of mass incarceration — and 10 reforms that would begin to dismantle it."

---

### Candidate 15 (Rank 15): Domain 58 — Immigrant Communities, Civic Participation, and the Surveillance Chilling Effect on Democratic Organization

**The gap it fills:** Domain 16 (Immigration and Citizenship) covers immigration enforcement and the legal rights of immigrants. What it does not cover is the civic participation chilling effect: how mass deportation threats, community surveillance, and ICE enforcement actions suppress the civic participation of both undocumented immigrants and their U.S. citizen family members and neighbors. Research from the immigration enforcement surge has documented reduced participation in public schools, healthcare, and civic institutions by people who are legally in the country but fear proximity to enforcement activity.

**Phase 1 integration:** Domain 16 (Immigration — primary foundation), Domain 21 (Data Privacy and Surveillance — ICE data tools), Domain 36 (AI Governance — ImmigrationOS and algorithmic enforcement), Domain 7 (Rights — First Amendment chilling effects on political participation), Domain 29 (Prosecutorial Weaponization — immigrant rights organizations targeted by DOJ).
**Research scope:** 4,000–5,000 words.
**Organizing brief topic:** "How immigration enforcement is silencing American communities — the chilling effect data and what civil society can do."
