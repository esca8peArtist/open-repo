# Domain 59: Economic Precarity & Democratic Exclusion
## Research Outline — Phase 2 Production Preparation

*Research outline — Phase 2 pre-production, Session May 17, 2026*

---

## Lead Finding

Economic precarity is not a circumstance that coexists with democratic exclusion — it is a mechanism of it. The empirical record across multiple methodological traditions now permits a structural claim: poverty, debt, housing instability, and wage stagnation do not merely correlate with reduced civic participation — they causally suppress it. When household finances are disrupted at the moment of an election, voter turnout among low-income citizens drops by 5 percentage points. When a family receives an eviction notice, their political engagement collapses and does not recover. When a person relocates to a low-poverty neighborhood through a housing voucher, their voter turnout remains depressed for nearly two decades — equal in magnitude to the most effective get-out-the-vote campaigns, but in the opposite direction. When 2.6 million student loan borrowers fall into default in a single quarter, their credit scores are destroyed, their housing access constrained, and their civic bandwidth shrinks accordingly.

The structural argument this domain develops is distinct from general income-inequality research. The claim is not merely that poorer people vote less (though they do, at substantially lower rates). The claim is that specific, discrete economic shocks — medical debt onset, eviction, foreclosure, food assistance disruption, debt default — function as acute disenfranchisement mechanisms that operate independently of underlying income level, strike targeted populations in concentrated geographic and demographic patterns, and are now being intensified simultaneously by the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) and associated federal policy retrenchment in 2025-2026.

The November 2026 midterms are four months from this document's production start date. The policy decisions being made now — SNAP cuts affecting 3 million recipients since July 2025, student loan default affecting 2.6 million borrowers in Q1 2026, the largest-ever drop in SNAP participation in January 2026 — will reach full civic impact precisely at the November election. This timing is not coincidental. The research document must make the democratic cost legible in time for advocacy, litigation, and electoral mobilization to respond.

## Research Architecture: Seven Causal Pathways

### Pathway 1: Acute Financial Hardship — The Election-Day Mechanism

**Core finding**: Schaub (2021, *American Political Science Review*) established the causal link between acute financial hardship and voter turnout using a natural experiment based on the sequence of bank working days. When bills are due immediately before an election — creating a discrete financial pressure point — voting intentions among the poor drop by 5 percentage points and actual voter turnout is reduced by 5 percentage points. The effect is fully concentrated at the lower end of the income distribution; no significant effect appears at higher income levels.

The mechanism is dual: increased stress and alienation from the political process, combined with material contraction into the private sphere that reduces the social embeddedness that drives turnout. This is not apathy — it is structural incapacity. The poor are disenfranchised not by law but by the material conditions of their financial lives at the precise moment when civic participation is required.

**2026 empirical extension**: With student loan defaults now running at 2.6 million per quarter (New York Federal Reserve, May 2026), with SNAP disruptions affecting 42 million households during the 2025 government shutdown, and with medical debt onset increasing housing instability risk by 44% (Johns Hopkins/Bloomberg School of Public Health, 2026), the scale of acute financial disruption entering the 2026 election cycle is historically anomalous.

**Production research needed**: Quantify the overlap between acute financial hardship populations and 2026 battleground state electoral margins. Estimate the number of voters in competitive congressional districts experiencing debt default, SNAP disruption, or medical debt onset in the 6-month window before November 2026.

### Pathway 2: Medical Debt and the Participatory Tax

**Core finding**: Medical debt functions as a participatory tax — an involuntary extraction of civic capacity from households that cannot absorb it. A 2026 study from the Johns Hopkins Bloomberg School of Public Health found that adults with medical debt in 2024 had a 7-percentage-point higher probability of housing instability in 2025, translating to a 44% elevated risk. Housing instability, in turn, is a well-documented turnout suppressor through the registration re-entry burden.

The United States carries approximately $220 billion in medical debt outstanding, concentrated in states that have not expanded Medicaid. The populations most exposed — Black, Hispanic, and low-income households — are the same populations already most structurally disadvantaged in the democratic system. Medical debt creates a cascading disenfranchisement mechanism: debt onset leads to housing instability, housing instability leads to residential moves, residential moves require voter re-registration, re-registration failure leads to turnout suppression.

**Cross-reference to Domain 39**: Domain 39 (Healthcare Access as Democratic Infrastructure) established the 3.8 percentage-point rural hospital closure turnout effect and the broader healthcare-civic framework. Domain 59 extends this by centering the financial debt mechanism — the civic cost of debt itself rather than the civic cost of care removal.

**Empirical anchors for production**:
- $220 billion medical debt outstanding (CFPB pre-2025)
- 1 in 7 households food insecure in 2024 (13.7%), rising under OBBBA SNAP cuts
- Johns Hopkins medical debt → housing instability effect, 2026 (44% elevated risk)
- OBBBA work requirements project 7.5 million Medicaid disenrollments by 2034 (CBO)

**Production research needed**: Disaggregate medical debt geographic distribution versus congressional district competitiveness. Find peer-reviewed studies specifically measuring medical debt onset → civic withdrawal (distinguish from the healthcare access literature already covered in Domain 39).

### Pathway 3: Housing Instability — Registration Destruction as Disenfranchisement

**Core finding**: The PNAS Moving to Opportunity study (2024) provides the cleanest causal identification in the domain. Adults who received housing vouchers and moved to lower-poverty neighborhoods — a positive economic intervention — experienced a 3.6 percentage-point decrease in voter registration and persistently depressed voter turnout for nearly two decades. The effect was not attitudinal; it was structural. Residential mobility disrupted the geographic-administrative link between a person and their voting registration, and the bureaucratic re-entry requirement proved a persistent barrier.

Among people experiencing involuntary housing instability — evictions, foreclosures — the effects are worse. Slee and Desmond (2023, *Politics & Society*) combined tens of millions of eviction and voting records and found that neighborhood-level eviction rates negatively impacted voter turnout in the 2016 presidential election through three neighborhood mechanisms: material hardship, social isolation/community disinvestment, and government cynicism. Individual-level foreclosure exposure is associated with reduced voter turnout both individually and for neighbors in hard-hit areas.

**2025-2026 empirical context**: The housing affordability crisis has intensified. Shelter inflation has consistently outpaced overall CPI, driven by rising rents and mortgage costs. SNAP participation drops in states like Arizona reached 50% of July 2025 pre-OBBBA levels by March 2026 — suggesting a population under simultaneous food and housing pressure. An estimated 10% of unhoused people vote in federal elections, compared to roughly 60% of the general voting-eligible population.

**The disenfranchisement mechanism**: Restrictive voter ID laws, voter roll purges (which disproportionately capture people who have moved), and the absence of automatic voter re-registration create a structural filter. When people move — voluntarily or involuntarily — the burden of democratic re-entry falls entirely on them. States without same-day registration or automatic transfer policies systematically exclude the most mobile, most economically precarious segments of the electorate.

**Production research needed**: State-by-state analysis of automatic re-registration policies vs. housing instability rates and current eviction filing rates. Brennan Center 2024 roundup data on restrictive voting legislation intersected with housing instability data from NLIHC.

### Pathway 4: Student Debt, Stop-Outs, and the Democratic Cost of Non-Completion

**Core finding**: The student debt literature divides cleanly by completion status. Macdonald (2025, *Political Research Quarterly*) found that Americans with student loan debt are significantly more likely to vote and engage electorally than non-borrowers — but this holds only for those who completed degrees. The civic harm concentrates on "stop-outs": those who took on debt but did not obtain a credential. Stop-outs are less interested in politics, feel less heard by government, and have lower civic engagement — with the debt burden functioning as a negative policy feedback signal that disaffiliates them from democratic institutions.

Separately, a 2025 ERIC-indexed study (*Review of Higher Education*) documents "bad lessons" from the student loan system: the policy feedback effect of debt on democratic citizenship. Under debt burden, students become more controllable, less likely to challenge power, and more oriented toward private financial survival than civic engagement.

**2026 crisis scale**:
- 2.6 million borrowers fell into default in Q1 2026 alone (New York Federal Reserve, May 12, 2026)
- Student loan delinquency rate: 25% as of early 2026 — nearly triple the pre-pandemic 9.2% (Protect Borrowers, 2026)
- 13 million borrowers projected to default by end of 2026 if current trends hold
- Black and Native borrowers: delinquency rates near 50%
- Pell Grant recipients: 27% delinquency vs. 15% for non-Pell borrowers
- Credit score destruction: borrowers dropping from 680 to 580 score face $64,000 in additional lifetime mortgage costs and $8,800 in auto loan costs — compounding economic exclusion

The demographic pattern is critical: the borrowers facing highest default rates are disproportionately Black, Native, first-generation, and low-income. These are the same populations with the thinnest margins of civic participation to begin with.

**Production research needed**: Map the geographic distribution of default-concentrated counties against 2026 competitive congressional districts. Find research specifically measuring the civic engagement effect of debt default (distinct from debt burden).

### Pathway 5: Food Insecurity, SNAP Cuts, and Survival Triage

**Core finding**: The CBPP and FRAC have documented the steepest post-enactment decline in SNAP participation in decades. Between July 2025 (OBBBA enactment) and January 2026, SNAP participation fell by 3 million people — an 8 percent reduction. Arizona saw participation fall to less than half of July 2025 levels by March 2026. The CBO projected that OBBBA SNAP provisions would reduce participation by 2.4 million in an average month over 2025-2034.

The democratic link is indirect but structurally legible: when households are in food insecurity survival mode, civic participation becomes a luxury that cannot be afforded. The research on survival triage — how extreme material hardship narrows cognitive bandwidth and directs attention to immediate needs at the cost of long-term engagement — provides the mechanism.

An additional structural harm: USDA has ended its annual survey on food security after 30 years, beginning with cancellation of 2025 data collection. This data suppression prevents researchers, policymakers, and advocacy organizations from measuring the democratic cost of SNAP cuts in real time. The epistemic harm compounds the material harm.

**Empirical anchors**:
- 13.7% household food insecurity rate in 2024, with 18.4% among households with children
- 42 million SNAP recipients disrupted during 40-day government shutdown in late 2025
- USDA elimination of food security data collection: measurement infrastructure for harm destroyed
- Nearly 1 in 5 households with children food insecure in 2024, pre-OBBBA

**Production research needed**: Civic engagement research specifically linking food insecurity to political disengagement. Survey data from 2024-2025 on political attitude effects of hunger stress. Geographic analysis of food insecurity concentration vs. competitive 2026 districts.

### Pathway 6: Precarious Employment, Scheduling, and the Time-Cost of Voting

**Core finding**: Precarious and gig employment creates structural barriers to voting through two mechanisms: unpredictable scheduling that makes planning voting-day absence impossible, and the absence of paid time off that makes voting costly in real income terms. The Federal Reserve's 2024 Household Economic Well-Being Survey found that 31% of prime-age women were not working for pay — partly reflecting family and childcare responsibilities. Nearly half of gig workers report wishing their pay was more consistent.

The CIRCLE (Tufts University) research on 2024 youth non-voting found that 17% of young non-voters cited being too busy, having other commitments, or lacking childcare as barriers to voting. Financially secure youth were overrepresented among 2024 voters; financially unstable youth were overrepresented among non-voters.

The political economy of this gap: Senators respond most strongly to the preferences of affluent constituents, to a lesser extent to the middle class, and not at all to the preferences of the poor (Bartels, *Unequal Democracy*). The combined effect of low-income non-participation and legislative non-responsiveness to low-income voters creates a self-reinforcing exclusion cycle: economic precarity → reduced participation → reduced legislative responsiveness → policy that deepens precarity → further reduced participation.

**2024-2026 empirical context**:
- 38% of capable voters did not vote in 2024 — with logistical barriers cited as major factor (American Association for Integrity in Media)
- Young voters identified inflation and cost of living as #1 problem (30% of under-30 voters)
- 41% of all Americans identified inflation as their top 2024 election issue (Bankrate)
- 64% of Americans worried about inability to afford healthcare, housing, and everyday expenses
- 64% turnout in 2024 — 2.5% below 2020, suggesting erosion of base participation

**Gig economy-specific vulnerability**: Approximately 16% of Americans participate in gig work. These workers lack employer-paid leave, have unpredictable day-to-day schedules that make advance voting planning difficult, and are disproportionately low-income, immigrant, and non-white — the same demographics already underrepresented in the electorate.

**Production research needed**: Research on employer-provided voting time, state-by-state policies on voting leave, and the intersection of gig economy growth with voting access. Peer-reviewed research on time-cost of voting and income-level effects.

### Pathway 7: Felony Disenfranchisement as Economic-Justice Intersection

**Core finding**: Felon disenfranchisement is the most visible legal mechanism through which economic precarity converts to formal disenfranchisement. The Sentencing Project (2024) found 4 million Americans unable to vote in the 2024 election due to felony conviction laws. The racial and economic pattern is unambiguous: 1 in 22 African Americans of voting age is disenfranchised, a rate more than three times that of non-African Americans. More than 10% of African Americans are barred in five states (Arizona, Florida, Kentucky, South Dakota, Tennessee). Approximately 496,000 Latino Americans are disenfranchised.

The economic precarity link is structural: people who are incarcerated are disproportionately poor. Employers discriminate against people with criminal histories, particularly Black applicants. Fines, fees, and predatory pricing extract resources from justice-involved individuals and their families. The result is a compounding mechanism: poverty → criminalization → incarceration → disenfranchisement → continued economic exclusion → poverty. Each step deepens the next.

The historical roots matter for the advocacy argument: disenfranchisement provisions originate in post-Civil War efforts to deny Black Americans equal citizenship. Their current operation is continuous with that design intent, even where the specific statutory language has evolved.

**Production research needed**: State-by-state analysis of criminal legal debt (fines and fees) as voting restoration barrier. Research on the economic profile of currently disenfranchised populations. Cases where felon disenfranchisement law interacts with economic conditions (e.g., Florida Amendment 4 litigation on financial obligation completion).

## Movement Leverage Analysis: Four Key Constituencies

### Constituency 1: Labor Unions and Worker Power Organizations

Labor unions are the most institutionally powerful constituency connecting economic precarity to democratic participation. AFL-CIO explicitly advocates for social and economic justice as a core function. The American Academy of Arts and Sciences has documented how the assault on labor endangers civil society writ large — union membership correlates with higher civic engagement, voter turnout, and participation in democratic institutions.

The 2025-2026 period has seen significant labor mobilization: the Chicago Teachers Union's May Day Strong coalition, the Minnesota general strike drawing 100,000 marchers to Minneapolis on January 23, 2026, and ongoing coalition-building connecting workplace and community organizing. These are the organizations with existing infrastructure, member contact, and civic mobilization capacity to convert economic precarity awareness into electoral participation.

**Distribution targets**: AFL-CIO, SEIU, UFCW, Communications Workers of America, Chicago Teachers Union, National Education Association, Teamsters. Labor Notes as media channel. Economic Policy Institute as research partner.

**Strategic angle**: The democratic argument is natural territory for labor — unions exist as counter-institutions to employer power, and the extension to political power is direct. The "No Democracy Without Unions" framework (American Bar Association, CRSJ) provides the normative bridge.

## Precedent Cases: Prior Economic Crises and Democratic Participation

### Case 1: The 2008-2012 Great Recession

The subprime mortgage crisis provides the most recent large-scale natural experiment for economic crisis effects on voting. Research on the subprime mortgage crisis (*Political Science Research and Methods*) found that individuals whose homes were foreclosed on were less likely to vote — demobilized rather than mobilized. Foreclosure exposure correlated with reduced turnout both individually and for neighbors in hard-hit areas.

The crisis's most documented democratic effect was not depressed turnout but political polarization. Research across 70 countries found that financial crises increase the percentage of radical left and right voters while decreasing the centrist/moderate share. The 2008 crisis explains approximately 35% of the surge in US polarization documented since 2007 (CaixaBank Research). This polarization-through-precarity mechanism is distinct from the direct disenfranchisement argument — but provides historical precedent for the broader democratic damage of economic crisis.

The 2008-2010 foreclosure crisis removed approximately 3.8 million homeowners from their residences — creating a residential mobility wave whose voting effects, per the PNAS Moving to Opportunity research, would have persisted for nearly two decades. The civic cohort affected by the 2008 crisis has not fully recovered its electoral participation trajectory.

**Application to 2026**: The 2025-2026 student debt default crisis (2.6 million defaults in Q1 2026 alone) is an order-of-magnitude smaller than the foreclosure crisis — but it falls on a younger, more racially concentrated population, with the same residential mobility and credit destruction mechanisms. The precedent suggests the turnout effects will be durable.

---

### Case 2: The 1930s Depression — Economic Extremity and Democratic Fragility

The New Deal period is the canonical precedent for both the political mobilization potential and the democratic fragility of severe economic crisis. The Depression-era Democratic coalition was built partly on mobilizing economically excluded voters, but the same economic conditions also created fertile ground for authoritarian and anti-democratic movements domestically and internationally.

The key lesson for Domain 59: economic precarity is politically ambiguous. It can mobilize the disadvantaged, but it can equally produce withdrawal, cynicism, and susceptibility to authoritarian populism. The question is which institutional responses — organized labor, direct economic relief, voting access expansion — can convert precarity into democratic participation rather than democratic exit.

The 1930s US precedent is also a warning: even with strong participation, legislative responsiveness to the poor depends on organizational infrastructure. The labor movement of the 1930s-40s was the mechanism that converted Depression-era economic anxiety into durable working-class political power. The attenuation of that infrastructure since the 1970s — union density declining from 35% to under 10% in the private sector — is part of the story of how economic precarity has become disenfranchising rather than mobilizing in the current period.

---

### Case 3: Childhood Poverty as Longitudinal Disenfranchisement

Recent research (ScienceDirect, 2025) establishes that both current and past economic status predict electoral participation, with childhood poverty creating lasting participation deficits. This longitudinal dimension is critical for the production document: economic precarity does not just suppress the votes of people currently in poverty — it depresses the civic participation of people who grew up in poverty, even after their economic circumstances have improved.

This finding expands the scale of the democratic cost substantially. The cohorts who experienced childhood poverty during the 2008 recession are now in their mid-to-late 20s — prime voting age. Their participation deficits are now entering the electorate.

## Confidence Assessment

**High confidence (peer-reviewed, multiple studies)**:
- Acute financial hardship → turnout reduction (Schaub, APSR 2021)
- Residential mobility → voter registration decline (PNAS 2024, Moving to Opportunity)
- Eviction → neighborhood turnout suppression (Slee/Desmond 2023)
- Foreclosure → individual turnout suppression (multiple studies, 2008-2012)
- Student debt non-completion → civic disengagement (Macdonald 2025)
- Legislative non-responsiveness to low-income preferences (Bartels, multiple studies)

**Medium confidence (strong data, causal mechanism implied but not directly established)**:
- Medical debt → housing instability → turnout (Johns Hopkins 2026 + PNAS 2024, two-step chain)
- Food insecurity → civic withdrawal (theoretical mechanism, indirect empirical support)
- SNAP cuts → democratic participation loss (policy effect established, electoral link inferred)

**Evidence gaps (noted for production)**:
- Direct causal study of food insecurity → voter turnout (does not appear to exist)
- Peer-reviewed study of gig employment → voting access gap
- Geographic overlap analysis of economic precarity metrics vs. 2026 competitive districts
