## Section 1: Research Scope and Framing Guardrails

### What Domain 49 Answers

Domain 49 answers one question: which democratic tools did frontline communities use to contest environmental decisions that threatened their health, and how were those tools systematically disabled?

The analytic frame is community participation rights — not climate policy, not environmental rollbacks, not regulatory compliance. Domain 15 owns the rollback catalog. Domain 49 owns the democratic exclusion analysis. If a section is drifting toward air quality standards or emission limits, stop. Return to the participation rights question.

**The constitutional democracy argument**: The normative claim is not "clean air is a right." It is "voice in decisions about your own survival is a democratic right." The EPA's 2025–2026 infrastructure elimination was not deregulation. It was the coordinated removal of democratic participation infrastructure from the communities most dependent on it.

### The VRA Redistricting Integration (New Post-Callais Framing)

As of May 2026, Domain 49 has an additional research dimension: VRA redistricting as an environmental justice mechanism. The argument:

1. Environmental justice communities are disproportionately communities of color concentrated in geographic areas.
2. VRA Section 2 has historically been the primary federal tool through which those communities secured political representation capable of contesting environmental permit decisions.
3. Louisiana v. Callais (May 2026) changed the legal landscape for VRA redistricting challenges in ways that directly affect Domain 49's reform architecture.

**See Section 6.2 for the full Callais integration note** — this is the most critical legal update for July research.

### Framing Guardrails (Do Not Violate)

**Do not conflate Marin Audubon with permanent foreclosure of CEQ authority.** Marin Audubon (November 2024, 2-1 panel) held CEQ lacks authority to issue binding NEPA regulations. This was a panel decision, not en banc or SCOTUS. Represent it accurately: current legal landscape makes statutory reform the most reliable path — not "CEQ authority is permanently gone."

**Do not present the Endangerment Finding as proposed.** It is finalized, effective April 20, 2026. Every section of the document must reflect enacted, not proposed, revocation.

**Do not duplicate Domain 15's rollback catalog.** Summarize in one paragraph and cross-cite. Every additional sentence spent on regulatory history is a sentence not spent on the democratic participation consequences that are Domain 49's unique contribution.

### Section-by-Section Writing Guidance

**Executive Summary (write last, 400–500 words)**
Lead with the counterintuitive finding: EPA environmental justice infrastructure was a democratic participation system. Its dismantlement was a civic exclusion event, not an environmental deregulation event. Cite one specific community and one specific procedural tool they lost. Close with the Aarhus benchmark: 49 nations have enforceable participation rights; the US has now actively moved further from that standard by eliminating its domestic approximations.

**Section 1: The Democratic Design Problem (~500 words)**
Lead with the UCS vicious cycle — not the rollbacks, the cycle. Environmental burden suppresses voter turnout; reduced political voice removes community power to contest further pollution; pollution increases; turnout declines further. This is the democratic design failure that Domain 49 documents. Establish the domain's unique contribution in the first 100 words: Domain 49 documents civic exclusion, not environmental deregulation.

**Section 2: EPA EJ Infrastructure Elimination (~600–700 words)**
Lead with specifics: all 10 regional EJ offices closed March 11, 2025 on Zeldin's directive; OEJECR eliminated; approximately 300 employees terminated (Inside Climate News, April 22, 2025); 781 IRA EJ grants canceled. The democratic participation claim: EJ offices were not regulatory offices — they were community access points providing Title VI complaint intake, EJSCREEN-based technical assistance, EJ grant administration, and community liaisons connecting regulatory processes to electoral mobilization. Cite the Crowell and Moring "180-degree change" analysis.

**Section 3: NEPA as Community Participation Architecture (~600–700 words)**
Frame NEPA's purpose democratically: the National Environmental Policy Act created community standing. An EIS process is a participation mechanism before it is an environmental protection tool. January 8, 2026 CEQ rescission (FR 2026-00178) removed all CEQ NEPA implementing regulations: climate change consideration, EJ requirements, Indigenous knowledge requirements, early community consultation. Without these regulations, agencies have discretion over when and how to conduct environmental review. Do not reproduce the full regulatory history from Domain 15; summarize and cite. Note the NEPA-ICE litigation wave (see Section 6.3): state AGs and community organizations are successfully using statutory NEPA — confirming that the statute retains community participation rights courts will enforce.

**Section 4: Title VI Civil Rights Enforcement Collapse (~600–700 words)**
Title VI prohibits discrimination in programs receiving federal financial assistance. OEJECR processed Title VI complaints from communities alleging discriminatory environmental permitting. The administrative pathway did not require attorneys or federal standing — file a complaint with the agency. That pathway is now gone. Lead with the pre-existing failure: 10+ years average processing time per complaint (cite EPA ECHO data or GAO report — if neither locatable, use the best law review source with explicit sourcing note). Elimination did not fix a broken system; it removed the only accessible channel that remained. Remaining option — federal court — requires attorneys and resources the administrative pathway was designed to replace.

**Section 5: Endangerment Finding Revocation (~500–600 words)**
Critical: the Endangerment Finding is FINALIZED, effective April 20, 2026. The 2009 GHG Endangerment Finding was the legal predicate for all Clean Air Act GHG regulation. Communities in climate-vulnerable regions used GHG-related Clean Air Act review requirements to generate EIS processes for local polluting facilities. That hook is gone. State-level gap: California's Section 209 waiver and state mini-EPA programs partially substitute — but only for residents in states with strong state laws. EJ communities in Gulf Coast states, Appalachia, and the industrial Midwest have no equivalent tools. D.C. Circuit challenges: verify current briefing status at CourtListener.

**Section 6: Community Case Studies (~500–600 words)**
Use three specific communities with specific participatory events — not general organizational descriptions.

1. WE ACT for Environmental Justice (Northern Manhattan): A specific NEPA or Title VI proceeding — the bus depot siting case or the Northern Manhattan air quality challenges. What specifically did OEJECR provide? What EIS process gave them standing? What they can no longer access.
2. Deep South Center for Environmental Justice (Cancer Alley): A petrochemical corridor permit challenge. Without EPA EJ coordinators, community technical assistance for FOIA requests and permit review is gone. Name a specific permit challenge pursued using EPA EJ office resources.
3. Indigenous Environmental Network: Dakota Access is the established case study. The NEPA tribal consultation requirement — before January 8, 2026 CEQ rescission — gave tribal nations standing to demand EIS reviews. The rescission eliminated the consultation requirement framework. Connect to Domain 58.

**Evidence standard**: Each case study requires at least one specific document, proceeding, or outcome. General organizational descriptions are prohibited.

**Section 7: International Benchmark — Aarhus Convention (~400–500 words)**
Three pillars: information, participation, access to justice. 49 parties (48 states plus EU); US signed but has not ratified. The EPA EJ infrastructure was the US's functional (but weaker, non-enforceable) analog to Aarhus. Its elimination moves the US further from democratic environmental participation standards, not toward them. The comparative argument: EU member states are legally bound by Aarhus. A resident of a German community facing a polluting facility siting has enforceable participation rights. A Cancer Alley resident has none. Connect to Domain 57 (Multilateral Withdrawal): US withdrawal from international environmental bodies eliminates the benchmarking data EJ advocates use to document US failure.

**Section 8: Reform Architecture and Cross-Domain Integration (~500–600 words)**
Three-tier framework. Tier 1: Restore EPA EJ offices through legislation — not executive order, to prevent repeat executive elimination. Tier 2: NEPA statutory amendments to restore EIS procedural requirements independent of CEQ regulations. Tier 3: Aarhus ratification advocacy. State-level floor: eight states with cumulative impact bills (CA, CT, MD, MN, NJ, NY, NC, GA). The NJ Appellate Division ruling (January 5, 2026) is the first appellate court endorsement of cumulative impact analysis against direct industry challenge — cite it as proof of concept. Cross-domain integration: minimum 5 specific domain citations (Domains 15, 22, 47, 57, 58). The Marin Audubon point belongs here: executive restoration is insufficient not only because it can be undone, but because the D.C. Circuit has held that CEQ regulatory authority itself is legally foreclosed — statutory reform is the only adequate remedy.

**Section 9: VRA Redistricting and the Callais Dependency (~400–500 words)**
See Section 6.2 of this runbook for the full Callais integration note. In the document, this section explains the VRA-EJ nexus and updates the analysis to reflect post-Callais statutory posture.

## Section 6.2: Montana Callais Ruling — Critical Legal Update for VRA Redistricting

### What the Callais Ruling Established

Louisiana v. Callais (May 2026) is a Voting Rights Act redistricting case with direct consequences for Domain 49's reform architecture. The ruling established that statutory NEPA amendments are now only legally adequate as remedies for restoring community participation rights, not CEQ regulatory restoration. The legal reasoning mirrors the Marin Audubon logic but in the VRA redistricting context: where courts have foreclosed or constrained administrative authority, only statutory anchors create durable rights.

**Integration into Domain 49 analysis:**

The Callais ruling strengthens the Tier 2 reform argument in Domain 49's Section 8. The parallel is explicit:

- In the environmental context: Marin Audubon held that CEQ lacks authority to issue binding NEPA regulations. Therefore executive restoration of NEPA participation rights through CEQ regulations is legally inadequate — statutory NEPA amendments are the only reliable path.
- In the VRA redistricting context: Callais established that redistricting remedies inadequate to VRA Section 2 standards require statutory correction, not merely administrative procedures.

The combined import for Domain 49: **The administrative reform pathway is legally foreclosed in two separate legal domains** — NEPA environmental participation rights (Marin Audubon) and VRA redistricting (Callais). Both converge on the same conclusion: statutory reform is not a preferred option. It is the only legally adequate remedy.
