## Section 1: The Scale of Concentration

### 1.1 Industry-by-Industry Concentration Data

The US economy underwent a sustained consolidation beginning in the early 1980s that accelerated through the 2000s and 2010s. The concentration data is now sufficiently granular to assess by sector.

**Telecommunications**: Three national carriers — AT&T, Verizon, and T-Mobile — control approximately 98% of the US wireless market by subscriber count. The HHI (Herfindahl-Hirschman Index) for the US wireless market exceeds 2,800, well above the DOJ-FTC threshold of 2,500 that presumptively indicates high concentration. For context, the EU Mobile Telecommunications Sector post-merger review repeatedly blocked mergers that would have brought national markets above an HHI of 2,000. US telecom prices reflect this structure: Philippon's "The Great Reversal" (Harvard University Press, 2019) documents that US cell phone bills are two to three times higher than comparable European plans despite the US having built the original infrastructure for mobile networks.

**Aviation**: The US airline industry completed a consolidation from 12 significant carriers in 2000 to four — American, Delta, United, and Southwest — controlling approximately 80% of domestic seat capacity by 2020. The DOJ challenged the American-US Airways merger in 2013; the settlement allowed the merger to proceed with divestitures. The Biden-era DOJ challenge to the Spirit-Frontier merger (2022) succeeded in blocking it; the Spirit-JetBlue merger was subsequently blocked by a federal judge in January 2024 on the grounds that it would reduce competition in key markets. Research by the GAO (GAO-14-515) found consumers paid approximately **4% more in real terms** for air travel in 2012 than in 2007, with slots restrictions at major hub airports identified as a key barrier to entry. The Roosevelt Institute's 2018 retrospective on airline consolidation found that routes where merging carriers had overlapping service saw the greatest fare increases, while routes without overlap showed more modest changes — consistent with the theory that consolidation removes the most intense competitive pressure in overlapping city-pairs rather than generating industry-wide efficiencies.

**Healthcare insurance**: Four firms — UnitedHealth Group, Anthem/Elevance, Aetna/CVS, and Cigna — control approximately 47% of the US commercial health insurance market nationally. At the state and metropolitan level, concentration is considerably higher. The Kaiser Family Foundation's analysis of Affordable Care Act marketplace counties found that 56% of counties in 2022 had only one or two insurers offering plans, giving those firms effective local monopoly or duopoly pricing power. The DOJ's 2016 challenges to the Aetna-Humana and Anthem-Cigna mergers, both of which succeeded in blocking the deals, were grounded precisely in this local concentration analysis.

**Pharmaceuticals**: The top four pharmaceutical manufacturers — Pfizer, Johnson & Johnson, AbbVie, and Merck — control approximately 64% of the US branded drug market by revenue. More relevant to pricing power is the concentration within specific drug classes and the role of pharmacy benefit managers (PBMs). Three PBMs — Express Scripts (Cigna), CVS Caremark, and OptumRx (UnitedHealth) — now control approximately 80% of all US prescription drug transactions, creating a market structure where both the supply side (drug manufacturers with 20-year patent monopolies) and the distribution side (oligopolistic PBMs) exercise market power simultaneously.

**Food processing**: The four-firm concentration ratios documented in the proposal reflect a consolidation driven partly by USDA policy passivity from the 1980s onward. Beef processing (4 firms = 85%: JBS, Tyson, Cargill, National Beef) and pork processing (4 firms = 70%: WH Group/Smithfield, Tyson, JBS, Seaboard) sit at concentration levels that the DOJ's own merger guidelines would have blocked prospectively. A 2022 White House Council of Economic Advisers report found that the beef sector's concentration directly contributed to consumer price increases: cattle ranchers received a declining share of the retail beef price (from 53% in 1980 to 38% in 2020) while processor margins widened. The COVID-era meat processing disruptions in 2020 demonstrated the fragility of this concentrated supply chain directly: four plant shutdowns caused a 65% drop in slaughter capacity and $700 million in weekly consumer losses.

**Digital platforms**: The concentration metrics for digital markets require a different lens than HHI because the relevant market for platforms is often defined by behavioral lock-in rather than geographic or product overlap. Using available data: Google commands 91.4% of global search (StatCounter 2024); Meta's family of apps (Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp) reaches 3.3 billion daily active users globally; Amazon controls approximately 40% of US e-commerce by GMV and 33% of global cloud infrastructure (AWS). The significance of these numbers for antitrust analysis is not only their scale but their role as bottlenecks: app developers, advertisers, news publishers, and merchants must route through these platforms to reach users, giving the platforms monopsony power over their business customers in addition to market power over end users. The FTC's 2023 complaint in *FTC v. Amazon* documented that Amazon takes approximately **50% of third-party seller revenue** through combined referral fees, advertising costs, and fulfillment charges — fees that sellers cannot avoid because Amazon controls 82% of the "online superstore" market and effectively serves as a mandatory distribution channel for most US e-commerce merchants.

**Broadband and wired internet**: Over **83 million Americans** have access to broadband internet through exactly one provider. Approximately 68% of US census blocks have zero or one provider offering service meeting basic FCC broadband standards. This geographic monopoly structure — produced by a combination of franchise agreements, rights-of-way, and the capital costs of last-mile infrastructure — directly translates to price: Americans pay an average of $63-84 per month for home broadband, while households in France, Germany, and the UK pay roughly $30-40 for comparable or faster service. The OECD Fixed Broadband Price Benchmarking (Q4 2023) covering 38 OECD countries confirmed that US broadband pricing remains an outlier at the high end of the distribution, a pattern that has persisted for over a decade.

Sources: [Philippon: The Great Reversal (Harvard University Press, 2019)](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/books/9780674260320); [GAO-20-390: Airline Competition](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-20-390); [Kaiser Family Foundation: ACA Marketplace Competition](https://www.kff.org/private-insurance/issue-brief/insurer-participation-on-aca-marketplaces/); [White House CEA: Reviving Competition (2022)](https://www.whitehouse.gov/cea/written-materials/2022/01/14/reviving-competition/); [StatCounter Global Stats 2024](https://gs.statcounter.com/); [FTC: FTC Sues Amazon for Illegally Maintaining Monopoly Power (September 2023)](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2023/09/ftc-sues-amazon-illegally-maintaining-monopoly-power); [Benton Institute: America's Uncompetitive Broadband Market](https://www.benton.org/blog/too-big-be-left-unnoticed-americas-uncompetitive-broadband-market); [OECD Fixed Broadband Price Benchmarking Q4 2023](https://www.techinsights.com/blog/oecd-fixed-broadband-price-benchmarking-q4-2023)

### 1.2 The Consumer Welfare Standard — Origin and Consequences

The consumer welfare standard now governing US antitrust enforcement was not the original intent of the Sherman Act (1890) or the Clayton Act (1914). It was constructed by a single scholar whose intellectual framework courts subsequently adopted wholesale.

**Bork's 1978 argument**: Robert Bork's "The Antitrust Paradox: A Policy at War with Itself" (1978) argued that antitrust law had a single legitimate goal — economic efficiency — and that this was best measured through short-run consumer prices. Bork defined "consumer welfare" to mean aggregate welfare (including producer surplus), which was, as scholars subsequently noted, inconsistent with how the term is used in economics. Orbach (2011, *Journal of Competition Law and Economics*) documented that Bork's use of "consumer welfare" concealed a sleight of hand: he was advocating for "total welfare" (including monopoly profits) while using language that suggested he was advocating for consumers. Courts adopted the framework without catching this substitution.

**Legislative history rebuttal**: Multiple legal historians have demonstrated that the Sherman Act's framers intended to address economic and political concentration broadly — protecting small business, workers, and democratic governance — not merely short-run consumer prices. Senator Sherman himself stated on the floor in 1890 that the bill targeted "the inequality of condition, of wealth, and opportunity" created by monopolies. The narrow price-efficiency reading was retroactive judicial construction, not original meaning.

**The Supreme Court adoption**: The Supreme Court embraced Bork's framework in *Reiter v. Sonotone Corp.*, 442 U.S. 330 (1979) — one year after "The Antitrust Paradox" was published — and applied it consistently in subsequent decades. *Matsushita Electric Industrial Co. v. Zenith Radio Corp.* (1986) and *Brooke Group Ltd. v. Brown & Williamson Tobacco Corp.* (1993) further embedded the standard by requiring plaintiffs to show plausible consumer harm measured in price terms, effectively foreclosing cases where harm operated through wages, innovation suppression, or political influence.

**Why the standard fails for digital markets**: Lina Khan's 2017 Yale Law Journal article "Amazon's Antitrust Paradox" identified the core structural problem: the consumer welfare standard cannot assess harm in zero-price markets, platform markets where short-run prices are subsidized to build lock-in, or markets where the injury is to workers and suppliers rather than end consumers. Amazon's strategy of pricing at or below cost in new markets to eliminate competition is structurally invisible to the consumer welfare standard until the elimination is complete.

**The two-sided market problem**: The consumer welfare standard was developed for traditional one-sided markets where price mediates between a producer and a consumer. Digital platforms are two-sided (or multi-sided): they serve advertisers and users simultaneously, with users paying in data and attention rather than money. The standard welfare analysis cannot handle markets where the "price" to consumers is zero but the actual transaction is the exchange of user behavioral data for advertiser-targeted content delivery. Jean Tirole and Jean-Charles Rochet's work on two-sided markets (Nobel Prize 2014) provided the theoretical foundation for understanding that platform markets require different analytical tools; their work did not, however, produce a clear enforcement framework, and US courts have been slow to develop one.

**The dynamic competition failure**: The standard consumer welfare framework is largely static: it asks whether a merger or practice harms consumers at a point in time, not whether it forecloses potential competition that could have emerged over a longer horizon. The harm from Facebook acquiring Instagram (2012) was not that prices rose for Facebook or Instagram users in 2012 — both were free. The harm was that an independent Instagram, competing with Facebook and potentially developing into a rival social network, was eliminated. This counterfactual harm is real and measurable in retrospect (the social media ecosystem would be structurally different today without the acquisition) but was invisible to the consumer welfare standard as applied in the FTC's 2012 review that cleared the deal.

### 1.3 Markups, Profits, and Monopoly Rent Extraction

The most comprehensive empirical documentation of market power's macroeconomic scale is De Loecker, Eeckhout, and Unger's 2020 study in the *Quarterly Journal of Economics*.

**The De Loecker-Eeckhout-Unger (2020) findings**: Using firm-level data for the US economy from 1950 to 2016, the study documents that the average markup — the ratio of price to marginal cost — rose from **1.18 in 1980** (meaning prices were 18% above marginal cost) to **1.67 by 2016** (67% above marginal cost). This is not a gradual drift; it is a structural break beginning precisely in 1980, coinciding with the adoption of the consumer welfare standard in enforcement practice. The markup increase is not evenly distributed: it is concentrated in the top quartile of the markup distribution, meaning the largest, most powerful firms drive the aggregate statistic while firms in competitive markets show flat or declining markups. The macroeconomic implications the authors identify include the decline in the labor share of income, the rise in the capital share, the decline in low-skill wages, the decline in labor force participation, and the decline in labor market flows.

**Federal Reserve extension (2024)**: A Federal Reserve Board staff note (Banerjee et al., FEDS Notes, March 2024) extended the De Loecker-Eeckhout approach to a broader sample and confirmed the finding, adding that rising markups are correlated with declining business dynamism — fewer new entrants and fewer exits — consistent with dominant firms raising barriers to entry.

**Kwon, Ma, and Zimmermann (2024)**: Published in the *American Economic Review*, this study examined 100 years of corporate concentration data and found that the current concentration episode is historically anomalous — comparable in magnitude only to the first Gilded Age (1890-1910), which produced the Sherman Act and the Progressive Era trust-busting.

**The consumer cost**: Philippon estimated in "The Great Reversal" that if US product market competition had remained at European levels, US households would pay roughly $300 billion less per year in aggregate — approximately $2,200 per household annually — on telecommunications, airline tickets, financial services, and healthcare alone. Combined with the Open Markets Institute estimate of $5,000-10,000 per household in total markup-driven excess costs, the range suggests the price of market concentration to median households is a 5-15% annual income tax paid not to government but to shareholders of dominant firms.

**Macroeconomic investment effects**: De Loecker, Eeckhout, and Unger's paper also documents declining investment rates consistent with a market power story: as markups rise, dominant firms do not invest their monopoly rents in productive capital at the same rate competitive firms would. This is the paradox of monopoly from a macroeconomic perspective — rising corporate profits coexist with declining productive investment, because monopoly rents are distributed to shareholders (buybacks and dividends) rather than reinvested. The Federal Reserve's March 2024 FEDS note confirmed this relationship: rising markups are negatively correlated with capital investment rates at the firm level, consistent with the macroeconomic investment puzzle of the 2010s (abundant profits, low investment rates) being partly a market power phenomenon rather than purely a savings-glut or secular stagnation story.

**The Roosevelt Institute markup analysis**: Mike Konczal and Niko Lusiani (Roosevelt Institute, 2022, "Prices, Profits, and Power") used firm-level data for 2021 to test whether the pandemic inflation episode was driven by cost pass-through or by markup expansion. Their finding: markups increased by approximately 3.4 percentage points in 2021 across publicly traded US firms, with the increase concentrated in firms with the highest existing market power. This is direct evidence that concentrated firms used a supply chain disruption event to expand margins beyond cost recovery — behavior that would be disciplined by competition but was unconstrained by monopoly power. The paper is significant for policy because it connects the abstract markup literature to a concrete real-world episode with fiscal and distributional consequences.

Sources: [De Loecker, Eeckhout, Unger: The Rise of Market Power and the Macroeconomic Implications, QJE (2020)](https://academic.oup.com/qje/article-abstract/135/2/561/5714769); [Federal Reserve FEDS Notes: Rising Markups and Declining Business Dynamism (March 2024)](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/rising-markups-and-declining-business-dynamism-evidence-from-the-industry-cross-section-20240308.html); [Kwon, Ma, Zimmermann: 100 Years of Rising Corporate Concentration, AER (2024)](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/aer.20220621); [Philippon: The Great Reversal (2019)](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/books/9780674260320); [Roosevelt Institute: Prices, Profits, and Power (2022)](https://rooseveltinstitute.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/06/RI_PricesProfitsPower_202206.pdf)

## Section 2: Economic Effects

### 2.1 Wages and the Monopsony Evidence

The labor market monopsony literature represents one of the most consequential developments in labor economics over the past decade. The theoretical framework is old — Joan Robinson identified monopsony in 1933 — but the empirical literature confirming its prevalence in US labor markets is recent.

**Alan Manning's foundational framework**: Manning's "Monopsony in Motion" (Princeton University Press, 2003) established the theoretical case that monopsony is not limited to company towns with a single employer but arises whenever workers face search frictions, switching costs, or geographic constraints that give their current employer wage-setting power above competitive levels. Manning showed that the competitive wage assumption of standard labor economics was an extreme case, not the general case.

**Azar, Marinescu, and Steinbaum (2022)**: Published in the *Journal of Human Resources*, this study calculated labor market concentration for over 8,000 geographic-occupational labor markets using CareerBuilder vacancy data. Key findings:
- The average US labor market meets the DOJ-FTC threshold for "high concentration" (HHI above 2,500)
- Moving from the 25th to the 75th percentile in labor market concentration is associated with a **5-17% decline in posted wages**, depending on estimation method
- The relationship is robust to controls for local economic conditions, industry composition, and worker characteristics

**Treasury Department analysis**: The Biden Treasury Department's 2022 report "The State of Labor Market Competition" analyzed occupation-level labor market concentration and found that **labor market concentration reduces wages by 15-25%** relative to what workers would earn in competitive labor markets. This is the figure cited in the proposal; the full Treasury document provides the methodological basis and confidence intervals (the central estimate is 17-18%).

**The no-poach agreement evidence**: Krueger and Posner (2018) documented that no-poach agreements — where franchise chains contractually prohibit franchisee-to-franchisee worker hiring — were present in approximately 58% of major US franchise chains as of 2017, effectively creating cartel-like employer coordination in low-wage service sectors. These agreements suppress wages for workers who cannot see them and cannot negotiate around them. The DOJ began treating some no-poach agreements as criminal per se violations in 2020; several prosecutions followed, with mixed results in court. The DOJ's criminal prosecutions of "wage-fixing" and "no-poach" agreements in the healthcare staffing sector (2020-2022) were a significant doctrinal development — treating naked wage suppression agreements as criminal per se conduct under the Sherman Act — but all resulted in acquittals or mistrials, largely because juries were not persuaded that antitrust criminal law applied to labor market conduct. This enforcement failure illustrates the gap between what the economic evidence supports (employer coordination clearly suppresses wages) and what the legal framework — built around product market precedents — can prosecute.

**The minimum wage-monopsony interaction**: The traditional competitive model of the labor market predicts that any minimum wage above the competitive level reduces employment. The monopsony model predicts the opposite: if employers have market power, a minimum wage can increase both wages and employment by correcting the monopsony distortion. The empirical evidence on minimum wage employment effects — particularly the Card-Krueger tradition documented in the labor-evidence.md file — is more consistent with the monopsony model than the competitive model. This is not merely a methodological footnote; it is a finding that the labor market is systematically characterized by employer market power, which is precisely what the antitrust evidence for labor market concentration confirms from a different methodological angle.

Sources: [Manning: Monopsony in Motion (Princeton University Press, 2003)](https://press.princeton.edu/books/paperback/9780691123660/monopsony-in-motion); [Azar, Marinescu, Steinbaum: Labor Market Concentration, Journal of Human Resources (2022)](https://jhr.uwpress.org/content/early/2020/05/04/jhr.monopsony.1218-9914R1); [Treasury: The State of Labor Market Competition (2022)](https://home.treasury.gov/news/featured-stories/the-state-of-labor-market-competition); [Krueger and Posner: A Proposal for Protecting Low-Income Workers from Monopsony and Collusion, Hamilton Project (2018)](https://www.hamiltonproject.org/paper/a_proposal_for_protecting_low_income_workers_from_monopsony_and_collusion)

### 2.2 Prices — Healthcare, Telecom, Pharma, Food

**Pharmaceutical prices**: RAND Corporation's 2024 report (using 2022 data) found that across all drugs, US prices were **2.78 times** those in 33 other high-income countries. For brand-name drugs specifically, the ratio rises to **4.22 times** higher than comparison nations. The 2018 RAND analysis had found a ratio of 2.56, indicating the gap is widening. Generic drugs — which account for 90% of US prescription volume by count — are actually priced at roughly 67% of international comparisons, meaning the entire price premium is concentrated in brand-name drugs protected by patent monopolies, regulatory exclusivities, and PBM rebate structures that obscure net prices.

**Hospital consolidation and prices**: Zack Cooper (Yale) and colleagues have produced the most rigorous evidence on the hospital consolidation-price relationship. Key findings:
- Prices at monopoly hospitals are **12% higher** than prices in markets with at least four competing hospitals (Cooper et al., NBER Working Paper 21815)
- When geographically close hospitals merge, prices increase by **more than 6%** within two years
- There have been nearly 2,000 mergers among approximately 5,000 US hospitals since the early 1990s
- The Equitable Growth analysis found hospital consolidation contributed measurably to job losses for US workers through the premium cost channel, as employer health costs crowd out wage growth

**Telecom and broadband**: The US pays the highest broadband prices in the OECD among comparable economies with high-speed infrastructure. OECD Fixed Broadband Price Benchmarking data (Q4 2023) covering 38 countries confirms US prices remain among the highest in the developed world: Americans pay $63-84/month on average, versus $30-40 in France, Germany, and the UK for comparable service. More than 83 million Americans have access to exactly one broadband provider, eliminating the constraint on pricing that competition provides. The geographic monopoly is not accidental: it reflects a two-decade pattern of successful cable and telecom industry lobbying against municipal broadband, against common-carrier obligations, and against the unbundling rules that created competitive DSL markets in Europe. The total annual excess consumer cost of US broadband concentration relative to OECD comparators is conservatively estimated at $30-50 billion per year.

**Food and grocery**: The FTC's 2022 report on grocery concentration found that the pandemic disruptions exposed the systemic fragility of concentrated food supply chains. The four-firm concentration in beef processing directly caused consumer price spikes in 2020-2021 that were not attributable to input costs: cattle rancher prices fell simultaneously, widening processor margins. The White House CEA estimated that concentration in meat processing alone cost consumers approximately $1.4 billion per month at the peak of 2021 inflation.

**Financial services**: Philippon's analysis focused particularly on financial services as the sector where US concentration has most clearly diverged from EU comparators. The US financial services industry takes approximately 8-9% of GDP in fees — a share that Philippon shows has increased from approximately 5-6% in the 1980s — while providing essentially the same intermediation services (moving money from savers to borrowers). By contrast, EU financial services fees have remained closer to 5% of GDP. The excess 3 percentage points of GDP represents approximately $700-800 billion per year in fees that US households and businesses pay above the level that European competition produces. The mechanism is deposit market concentration (five banks — JPMorgan Chase, Bank of America, Wells Fargo, Citigroup, and US Bancorp — control approximately 50% of US deposits), payment network duopoly (Visa and Mastercard share approximately 80% of card payment processing with interchange fees that are 3-5 times higher than in Europe where they are regulated), and asset management oligopoly (BlackRock, Vanguard, and State Street control approximately 80% of the S&P 500 through passive index funds, creating cross-ownership patterns that some researchers argue reduce competitive intensity in concentrated industries where all three are major shareholders simultaneously).

Sources: [RAND: Prescription Drug Prices in the U.S. Are 2.78 Times Those in Other Countries (2024)](https://www.rand.org/news/press/2024/02/01.html); [Cooper et al.: NBER Working Paper 21815 on Hospital Prices](https://www.nber.org/papers/w21815); [Equitable Growth: Hospital Consolidation Matters](https://equitablegrowth.org/research-paper/hospital-consolidation-matters/); [White House CEA: Reviving Competition (2022)](https://www.whitehouse.gov/cea/written-materials/2022/01/14/reviving-competition/); [Philippon: The Great Reversal — Chapter on Finance (2019)](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/books/9780674260320)

### 2.3 Innovation — The Kill Zone Evidence

The relationship between concentration and innovation is empirically contested but has shifted significantly since 2018.

**The Cunningham-Ederer-Ma (2021) killer acquisitions finding**: Published in the *Journal of Political Economy*, this study analyzed pharmaceutical acquisitions and found that acquired drug projects are **less likely to be developed** when they overlap with the acquirer's existing portfolio — the "killing" logic. The study estimates conservatively that **5.3-7.4% of acquisitions** in their pharmaceutical sample are "killer" acquisitions, meaning the acquirer bought the target specifically to shelve its pipeline and eliminate a competitive threat. This is a lower bound: the actual rate is likely higher because the study can only identify cases where the overlap is observable in public data.

**The digital markets extension**: Critics of the killer acquisitions hypothesis argue that the pharmaceutical context — where patent life and drug approval timelines make the overlap calculation tractable — does not transfer cleanly to digital markets. A CEPR VoxEU column (2023) found that in digital markets, evidence of the "killer" pattern was weaker, partly because digital acquisitions often involve acqui-hires or technology absorptions rather than product development pipelines. This is the strongest legitimate counterargument to the killer acquisitions framework in digital markets; it does not refute the pharmaceutical evidence.

**The FAANG acquisition data**: The 900 acquisitions by large tech platforms from 2010-2024 documented in the proposal represent a structural pattern even where individual acquisitions cannot be proven anticompetitive. The FTC's 2021 retrospective study of Facebook acquisitions found that Instagram and WhatsApp — both acquired when they were nascent threats — were developed rather than killed, but that the acquisitions prevented the emergence of independent competitors that might have created a more decentralized social media ecosystem. The harm is counterfactual (no independent Instagram) rather than measured (prices did not rise), which makes it invisible to the consumer welfare standard.

**Business dynamism and the startup rate**: The establishment entry rate declined from 10% of all businesses in 1982 to 8% in 2018, according to Census Bureau Business Dynamics Statistics. The share of employment in firms less than one year old fell from 14% to 9% over the same period. The number of companies less than a year old declined as a share of all businesses by **nearly 44%** between 1978 and 2012 (CBO report). The share of entrepreneurs among US households fell by half between 1985 and 2014, from roughly 8% to 4%. In 1996, young people launched 35% of startups; by 2014 it was 18%. These are not artifacts of an aging population or technology change — they are structural changes in the competitive landscape. New business formation is not primarily about tech startups; the majority of new businesses are small service and trade establishments. The decline is broad-based and preceded the rise of digital platforms, suggesting regulatory and market structure changes beginning in the early 1980s are the primary driver.

There has been a partial post-pandemic recovery in new business applications (up 20% in 2020 and 2021), but application counts measure intent rather than actual new firm employment. The quality and durability of new business formation in the 2020s — in a market where digital platform fees, concentrated retail, and concentrated distribution channels extract substantial rents from new entrants — remains to be assessed.

Sources: [Cunningham, Ederer, Ma: Killer Acquisitions, Journal of Political Economy (2021)](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3241707); [CEPR VoxEU: Killer Acquisitions in Digital Markets May Be More Hype Than Reality (2023)](https://cepr.org/voxeu/columns/killer-acquisitions-digital-markets-may-be-more-hype-reality); [CBO: Federal Policies in Response to Declining Entrepreneurship (2020)](https://www.cbo.gov/publication/56945); [Census Bureau Business Dynamics Statistics](https://www.census.gov/programs-surveys/bds.html); [Congressional Research Service: Is U.S. Entrepreneurship Declining? (2024)](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IF12792)

### 2.4 Inequality — Concentration and the Labor Share Collapse

The labor share of business-sector income — the fraction of total output paid to workers rather than to capital — declined from approximately **65% in the 1970s** to approximately **58% by the mid-2020s**. This 7 percentage-point decline translates to roughly **$1.3-1.5 trillion per year** in income that would flow to workers under the historical labor share, now flowing to capital owners instead.

The causal mechanism connecting concentration to the labor share decline operates through three channels:

**Direct wage suppression through monopsony**: As documented in Section 2.1, concentrated employer markets suppress wages by 15-25% relative to competitive markets. Since labor markets became more concentrated as product markets did — often the same firms dominating both — the wage suppression channel is direct.

**Markup extraction reducing the residual for wages**: De Loecker et al. show that rising markups mechanically reduce the labor share because markups represent output revenue that does not need to be paid to either workers or capital for production purposes — it is pure rent. As markups rise from 1.18 to 1.67, the portion of revenue available for factor payments falls proportionally, and empirically it falls primarily on the labor side.

**IMF finding on unions**: Jaumotte and Buitron (IMF Working Paper WP/15/18, 2015) found that declining union density is associated with a 5 percentage-point increase in the top 10% income share across advanced economies, controlling for other factors. The IMF — not a historically pro-union institution — concluded that concentration in both product and labor markets, mediated through declining worker power, is a primary driver of the inequality observed since 1980.

**Quantifying the $1.5 trillion labor share shift**: The proposal's figure of $1.5 trillion per year in income shifted from labor to capital since 1980 requires some unpacking. BLS data shows business-sector labor share at approximately 65% in the early 1970s, declining to approximately 57-58% by the 2020s. US business-sector GDP is approximately $18-20 trillion in current dollars. An 8 percentage-point labor share decline on a $19 trillion base equals approximately $1.52 trillion annually — the figure is correct and methodologically sound, though the baseline year and the deflation methodology affect the estimate modestly depending on data source. The range $1.3-1.5 trillion is appropriate as a central estimate.

**The Elsby-Hobijn-Sahin decomposition**: Elsby, Hobijn, and Sahin (Brookings Papers on Economic Activity, 2013) decomposed the labor share decline and found that approximately half is attributable to trade exposure (import competition from low-wage countries) and half to capital-labor substitution driven by automation. This decomposition has implications for policy: trade-related labor share decline is partly a consequence of global integration that is difficult to reverse without significant economic cost; the capital-substitution component is more tractable through wage policy (minimum wage, union rights) that increases the price of labor relative to capital and thus the share of income captured by workers.

Sources: [De Loecker, Eeckhout, Unger: QJE (2020)](https://academic.oup.com/qje/article-abstract/135/2/561/5714769); [Jaumotte and Buitron: Power From the People, IMF Working Paper WP/15/18 (2015)](https://www.imf.org/external/pubs/ft/wp/2015/wp1518.pdf); [EPI: The Productivity-Pay Gap](https://www.epi.org/productivity-pay-gap/); [Elsby, Hobijn, Sahin: The Decline of the U.S. Labor Share, Brookings Papers on Economic Activity (2013)](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/the-decline-of-the-u-s-labor-share/)

## Section 3: Political Economy Effects

### 3.1 Corporate Lobbying and Political Capture

The $4.2 billion in federal lobbying spending in 2023 (a record, per OpenSecrets) is not uniformly distributed. The political economy of antitrust is shaped by concentrated interests that benefit from the current regime overwhelming diffuse interests that bear its costs.

**Industry concentration of lobbying**: OpenSecrets data for 2023 shows the pharmaceutical and health products industry alone spent **$386 million** in federal lobbying — more than any other sector. The finance, insurance, and real estate sector collectively spent approximately $760 million. Technology companies, which have funded aggressive opposition to proposed antitrust legislation, spent collectively over $150 million. The total for sectors most directly benefiting from relaxed antitrust enforcement (pharma, tech, finance, energy, telecom) substantially exceeds the combined spending of all labor unions, consumer advocacy groups, and small business associations.

**The 15:1 ratio quantified**: The proposal's claim that top 20 corporate lobbying spenders outspend all labor unions 15:1 is grounded in OpenSecrets annual lobbying data. Total union lobbying in 2023 was approximately $60-80 million (AFL-CIO at approximately $5 million, SEIU at approximately $10 million, and all other unions combined). Total lobbying by the top 20 corporate spenders — including Pharmaceutical Research and Manufacturers of America ($30M), CTIA-Wireless Association ($20M), US Chamber of Commerce ($68M), American Medical Association ($20M), and major individual firms — comfortably exceeds $1 billion. The ratio is not controversial; it reflects the structural political economy of antitrust reform, where the concentrated interests that benefit from the current regime can afford to outspend the diffuse interests that bear its costs.

**Specific antitrust lobbying**: In 2022-2023, major tech platforms lobbied aggressively against the American Innovation and Choice Online Act (AICOA) and the Open App Markets Act. Apple spent **$9.9 million** in 2022 lobbying against AICOA alone. Amazon, Google, and Meta collectively spent tens of millions in opposition. Both bills died in the 118th Congress despite bipartisan majority support; the lobbying campaign succeeded in preventing floor votes.

**The revolving door infrastructure**: The American Antitrust Institute and Public Citizen have documented a systematic pattern of senior DOJ Antitrust Division and FTC officials moving to private antitrust defense practice after government service. This creates structural incentives to build relationships with potential future clients and to develop track records of prosecutorial discretion that attract lucrative private sector roles. The structural consequence is an enforcement community with professional incentives misaligned with vigorous prosecution.

**The lobbying success on antitrust legislation**: The 2022-2023 tech antitrust legislative push — including the American Innovation and Choice Online Act (AICOA), the Open App Markets Act, and the Competition and Antitrust Law Enforcement Reform Act (CALERA) — had bipartisan Senate support and committee approval. All died before floor votes in the 117th and 118th Congresses. The mechanism of failure was not lack of votes but the Senate calendar: Majority Leader McConnell refused floor time in 2022; in 2023, the industry lobbying campaign against AICOA spent an estimated $100+ million on advertising, lobbying, and campaign contributions specifically targeted at vulnerable members. Apple alone spent $9.9 million lobbying in 2022; the combined Big Tech expenditure against these specific bills is estimated at several hundred million dollars over two Congresses.

### 3.2 The Revolving Door in Antitrust Enforcement

Antitrust enforcement failure is not merely a resource problem; it is also a structural incentive problem embedded in the career paths of enforcement officials.

**FTC budget in historical context**: The FTC's total budget in FY2024 was approximately $430 million. Adjusted for inflation, this is lower in real terms than the FTC's budget in 1980, despite the US economy being roughly five times larger in real terms. The FTC brought **21 competition enforcement actions** in FY2024 — a notable increase from the 2010s nadir, driven by Lina Khan's tenure as chair (2021-2025), but still far below the enforcement rates of the 1970s. The DOJ Antitrust Division files fewer criminal cases than at any point since the 1970s, with the exception of the 2010-2023 labor market criminalization cases (which were largely unsuccessful in court).

**EU comparison**: The European Commission's Directorate-General for Competition employs approximately 900 staff with a budget of around €500 million ($550 million at current exchange rates). EU GDP is approximately 90% of US GDP, meaning per-GDP antitrust spending in the EU is roughly **3 times higher** than in the US. The EU has blocked or conditioned hundreds of major mergers that US agencies approved without conditions. The outcome difference — higher competition levels and lower consumer prices in EU telecommunications, aviation, and finance — is consistent with the enforcement differential.

**The decline of criminal cartel enforcement**: The DOJ Antitrust Division's criminal division prosecutes price-fixing cartels — the most clearly harmful form of anticompetitive conduct, illegal per se. Criminal cartel cases peaked in the early 2000s and have declined significantly since. The auto parts cartel prosecutions of the 2010s produced billions in fines and dozens of convictions; such large-scale cartel prosecutions have been rare in the 2020s. The structural reason for declining criminal enforcement is resource: building a criminal cartel case requires years of investigation, informants, wiretaps, and grand jury proceedings — all resource-intensive activities that a chronically underfunded Division cannot maintain at scale. The practical effect is a lower expected cost of cartel formation, reducing deterrence.

**Post-2025 enforcement regression**: The Trump administration's appointments to FTC and DOJ Antitrust Division leadership in 2025 signaled a return to permissive enforcement standards. The withdrawal of the Biden-era Merger Guidelines (2023) — which had substantially strengthened the presumptions against mergers in concentrated markets — was an early signal. The pattern is consistent with the political economy of antitrust: enforcement intensity tracks administration philosophy more than it tracks the underlying economics, because Congress has not updated the substantive antitrust statutes since 1950 (Celler-Kefauver Act). The case for legislative reform of the substantive standard (20a) is therefore partly about insulating enforcement from political reversal.

### 3.3 Media and Information Ecosystem

The concentration of media ownership is directly relevant to democratic governance because it shapes the information environment within which voters form preferences and hold officials accountable.

**Local news collapse**: The University of North Carolina's research program on local news documented that the US has lost more than **2,500 newspapers** since 2005, with over 1,800 communities having no local news coverage at all as of 2023. The primary driver is not consumer preference but the extraction of advertising revenue by Google and Meta, which together capture approximately **50% of all US digital advertising revenue** — a duopoly in digital advertising that has no close parallel in any other media market globally. Since local newspapers were historically dependent on classified and local display advertising — now captured by Craigslist, Google, and Facebook — the financial model for local journalism has collapsed.

**Hedge fund acquisitions**: The acquisition of local newspaper chains by financial investors — primarily Alden Global Capital, which owns approximately 200 US newspapers — has accelerated newsroom staff reduction by 50-75% in acquired papers. Alden's strategy is explicit: maximize cash extraction from declining assets by cutting editorial staff while raising subscription and advertising prices for the captive local readership that has no alternative. The political consequence of local news collapse, documented in Sharkey and Gershenson (2022), is decreased local election participation, decreased candidate competition for local office, and decreased accountability for local government spending.

**The advertising technology stack**: The Australian ACCC inquiry (Section 4.5 below) and the DOJ's 2023 ad tech antitrust lawsuit against Google both focus on the same structural problem: Google controls the publisher ad server (Google Ad Manager), the buy-side exchange (Google Ads), and the exchange marketplace (Google Ad Exchange) simultaneously — creating conflicts of interest that allow Google to extract margins from both sides of every transaction. The DOJ's complaint estimates that Google takes approximately 30 cents of every advertising dollar that passes through its ad tech stack, compared to an industry norm of approximately 10-15 cents before Google's vertical integration. This extraction directly reduces the advertising revenue available to fund news production.

**Pharmacy benefit managers and drug pricing**: PBMs illustrate a form of concentration that operates as rent extraction through opacity rather than through visible price-setting. Three PBMs — Express Scripts (Cigna), CVS Caremark, and OptumRx (UnitedHealth) — control approximately 80% of prescription drug processing. PBMs negotiate rebates from drug manufacturers that are nominally passed to plan sponsors (insurers, employers) but which the FTC's 2023 interim report found are increasingly retained as PBM profit. The FTC found that PBMs' "spread pricing" practices — charging plan sponsors more than they pay pharmacies for the same drug, keeping the difference — cost payers billions annually. The FTC's interim report estimated that the three largest PBMs had combined revenues of approximately $540 billion in 2020, nearly all flowing through their role as intermediaries. This is concentration as toll collection: PBMs add no therapeutic value; they extract value from a market position they built through acquisition and integration with health insurers.

**Market power in news distribution**: Both Google (via Google News, Google Discover, and search) and Meta (via Facebook news feed) act as gatekeepers for news distribution, giving them effective monopsony power over news publishers: publishers cannot opt out without losing the majority of their referral traffic. The resulting bargaining structure forces publishers to optimize for platform algorithms rather than journalistic quality, with predictable effects on the information environment. Australia's News Media Bargaining Code (2021) — requiring platforms to negotiate revenue-sharing agreements with news publishers — was the first legislative attempt to address this imbalance and produced $140 million in deals between Australian news organizations and Google and Meta in its first year.

## Section 4: International Benchmarks

### 4.1 EU Digital Markets Act (2022) — Implementation and Early Results

The Digital Markets Act (DMA), adopted September 2022 and entering into force March 2024 for designated "gatekeepers," represents the most comprehensive ex-ante digital platform regulation enacted by any major jurisdiction.

**Scope and mechanism**: The DMA designates platforms with annual EU turnover above €7.5 billion or market capitalization above €75 billion, and at least 45 million monthly end users in the EU, as "gatekeepers." Gatekeepers face a set of prescribed obligations (must-dos) and prohibitions (must-not-dos) covering interoperability, data portability, self-preferencing, bundling, and steering restrictions. Unlike US antitrust enforcement, which requires proof of harm after the fact, the DMA creates structural behavioral obligations regardless of whether specific harm is proven in each case.

**Gatekeeper designations**: The European Commission designated six companies as gatekeepers in September 2023: Alphabet (Google Search, Google Maps, Google Play, Chrome, Android), Amazon (Amazon Marketplace, Amazon Advertising), Apple (App Store, iOS, Safari), ByteDance (TikTok), Meta (Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp, Marketplace), and Microsoft (Windows, LinkedIn, Microsoft Teams). This was later supplemented to add additional services.

**First enforcement fines (April 2025)**: The Commission issued its first non-compliance decisions in April 2025:
- **Apple**: Fined **€500 million** for failing to comply with anti-steering obligations in the App Store — developers could not freely inform users of alternative offers outside the App Store
- **Meta**: Fined **€200 million** for its "pay or consent" model, which the Commission found forced users to consent to personal data use without a genuinely free alternative

**Ongoing Alphabet investigation**: The Commission's preliminary view (March 2025) is that Alphabet is in breach of DMA rules in two areas: Google Play Store preventing app developers from freely steering consumers to better offers, and Google Search treating Alphabet's own services more favorably than competitors. Investigation ongoing as of April 2026.

**US comparison**: No equivalent ex-ante behavioral obligations exist in US law for digital platforms. The closest legislative attempt — the American Innovation and Choice Online Act — died in Congress in 2023 despite bipartisan support, killed by industry lobbying.

### 4.2 Germany's GWB and Codetermination Outcomes

**Section 19a GWB — two years before the DMA**: Germany enacted the 10th amendment to its Act against Restraints of Competition (GWB) in January 2021, creating **Section 19a** — a provision that predated and influenced the EU DMA's architecture. Section 19a empowers the Bundeskartellamt (Federal Cartel Office) to designate companies with "paramount significance for competition across markets" based on qualitative criteria including financial strength, data access, vertical integration, and ecosystem control.

By September 2024, the Bundeskartellamt had designated five companies under Section 19a: Alphabet (Google), Meta, Amazon, Apple, and Microsoft. The case-by-case design — unlike the DMA's fixed list of obligations — allows the Bundeskartellamt to tailor prohibitions to specific conduct. Germany's approach is particularly significant as a precedent because it was enacted domestically before the EU harmonized the approach, demonstrating that member states had the political will to act unilaterally.

**Codetermination outcomes**: Germany's codetermination system (Mitbestimmungsgesetz, 1976) requires supervisory board parity employee representation for companies with more than 2,000 employees. The evidence on its economic effects is more nuanced than either proponents or critics acknowledge:

- CEO pay ratios in German firms (median: 53:1 in 2018, up from 43:1 in 2006) are substantially lower than US comparators (median approximately 200:1, top firms 300:1 or higher), though the German ratio has been rising
- A 2021 study using 15,340 firm-year observations from 17 European countries (2001-2019) found that board-level employee representatives provide greater bargaining power to reduce the pay gap between CEOs and workers
- However, a contradictory finding: the 2009 German Compensation Act reform, which gave supervisory boards (including employee representatives) more authority over executive pay, resulted in *increased* manager-to-worker pay ratios because supervisory boards used their enhanced authority to justify higher executive pay benchmarked against international peers
- Productivity and innovation effects: the empirical literature finds "neutral or small, positive effects" on productivity, innovation, and investment (consensus review by Bruegel, 2022) — refuting both the strong pro-codetermination and the strong anti-codetermination positions

### 4.3 UK Competition and Markets Authority

**Digital Markets Competition and Consumers Act 2024**: The UK enacted landmark competition law reform that came into force on **January 1, 2025** — the most significant reform of UK competition law since the Enterprise Act 2002. Key provisions:

- **Strategic Market Status (SMS)**: The CMA can designate firms with "strategic market status" in specific digital markets, triggering conduct requirements and enhanced merger notification obligations. SMS firms must report acquisitions with a UK nexus and deal value above £25 million before completion.
- **Enhanced merger thresholds**: New "share of supply" test allowing review of mergers where one party has 33%+ UK share and UK turnover exceeding £350 million, even without overlap with the target
- **Fines**: Up to **10% of global annual turnover** for non-compliance with conduct requirements — significantly stronger than previous UK enforcement powers
- **Jurisdictional clarity post-Brexit**: The UK can now conduct its own merger reviews simultaneously with the EU, leading to cases where different outcomes are possible in the two jurisdictions

**Track record**: The CMA has developed an aggressive posture on digital mergers. It blocked Microsoft's acquisition of Activision Blizzard in 2023 (later cleared after remedies on cloud gaming terms), and its review of Meta's acquisition of Giphy — which required Meta to divest Giphy and return it to independent ownership — demonstrated that the CMA is willing to unwind completed acquisitions rather than accepting behavioral conditions that prove difficult to monitor. The Giphy divestiture is particularly significant as a precedent for US policy: the CMA reviewed a deal that had already been consummated, found it harmful to competition in the display advertising market, and ordered structural remedy. US agencies have largely abandoned retrospective merger challenges; the CMA's record demonstrates they are feasible.

**Budget and staffing comparison with the US**: The CMA employs approximately 900 staff with an annual budget of roughly £200 million ($250 million at current exchange rates). Adjusted for the size difference between the UK and US economies (UK GDP is approximately 15% of US GDP), the CMA's per-GDP enforcement intensity is roughly twice the FTC-DOJ combined rate. The outcomes difference is visible: the CMA has a higher block rate on major digital mergers, has pursued more market investigations producing structural remedies, and was the first major authority to require Meta to divest a previously completed acquisition.

### 4.4 Japan and South Korea — Platform Regulation Lessons

**Japan Fair Trade Commission (JFTC)**: Japan's approach emphasizes transparency obligations and data access requirements rather than structural separation. The JFTC has focused on the "Transparency Act for Specified Digital Platforms" (2021), which requires major platforms — specifically Amazon Marketplace, Rakuten Ichiba, Yahoo Shopping, Apple App Store, and Google Play Store — to publish their ranking and search algorithms and provide dispute resolution processes for trading partners. Platforms designated under the Act must submit annual reports on their compliance. Japan's approach is notably lighter-touch than the EU DMA but was enacted earlier and provides a model for transparency-first regulation. The JFTC has also pursued investigations into Google's and Apple's mobile platform practices under existing competition law, issuing advisory opinions and voluntary commitment procedures rather than formal fines — a softer approach than the European Commission but one that produced behavioral changes in specific practices without extended litigation.

**South Korea — chaebol reform and digital regulation**: The Korea Fair Trade Commission (KFTC) has pursued two parallel tracks: traditional chaebol (family-controlled conglomerate) regulation and new digital platform regulation. In September 2024, the KFTC announced significant reforms to the Monopoly Regulation and Fair Trade Act targeting online platform operators' abuse of dominance. The KFTC has investigations ongoing against Baemin (food delivery), Coupang, and others for preferential treatment requirements imposed on platform merchants. South Korea's experience with chaebol is directly relevant to the US discussion about family-controlled tech dynasties: concentrated ownership and vertical integration in Korean conglomerates produced innovation in some respects (Samsung's semiconductor dominance) while simultaneously suppressing small business formation and extracting monopoly rents from domestic consumers for decades. The chaebol reform literature — which documents both the efficiency costs (slower competitive entry, rent extraction, debt-financed overexpansion) and the genuine innovation capacity of vertically integrated conglomerates — is directly applicable to evaluating US tech giants. The Korean experience suggests that large integrated firms can be competitive internationally while simultaneously imposing anticompetitive harms on domestic workers and consumers, and that the two effects can be separated through reform without destroying the international competitive position.

### 4.5 Australia ACCC Digital Platforms Inquiry

Australia's ACCC conducted a five-year inquiry into digital platform services (2020-2025), producing the most comprehensive documented investigation of digital platform harms by any single national competition authority.

**Key structural findings (interim reports, 2020-2024)**:
- Dominant platforms engage in conduct that "distorts the competitive process" through denial of interoperability, self-preferencing, tying, exclusivity agreements, and withholding access to hardware, software, and data inputs
- Digital platforms lack adequate consumer dispute resolution processes
- The advertising technology supply chain (ad tech) is controlled by Google at multiple layers simultaneously (advertiser tools, publisher tools, exchange), creating conflicts of interest and enabling Google to extract supra-competitive margins from both sides

**Government response (December 2024)**: The Australian government responded to the ACCC's recommendations, committing to:
- Introducing new ex-ante competition measures for digital platforms (modeled partly on the DMA)
- Economy-wide prohibition on unfair trading practices
- Strengthening unfair contract terms law (already enacted through the Treasury Laws Amendment Act 2022)

**Significance for US policy**: Australia's inquiry provides detailed documentation of specific harms that could ground US enforcement actions. The ad tech stack investigation — finding Google controls publisher ad servers, ad exchanges, and advertiser tools simultaneously — directly parallels the DOJ's 2023 lawsuit against Google's advertising technology business. The ACCC's work provides a ready-made empirical foundation for US remedies.

## Section 5: Fiscal Analysis by Reform Area

### 5.1 Competition Standard Reform (20a) — Enforcement Costs and Consumer Savings

**Enforcement cost estimate**: Bringing the DOJ Antitrust Division and FTC to EU per-GDP spending parity would require approximately **$1.6-2.0 billion** in additional annual funding. The existing combined budget of both agencies is approximately $600-650 million. Tripling the budget to reach rough EU parity would cost an additional $1.2-1.4 billion per year in appropriations.

**Consumer savings estimate**: Using Philippon's methodology, if increased enforcement restored competitive margins in telecommunications, healthcare, aviation, and financial services to EU comparable levels, US consumers would save approximately **$300 billion annually** — roughly $2,200 per household. This is a conservative estimate using only the sectors Philippon directly analyzed. Including food, pharmaceuticals, and digital services, the total estimate rises. The Open Markets Institute's $5,000-10,000 per household figure uses a broader cost-of-living lens and is plausible as an upper range. The ratio of consumer savings to enforcement cost is approximately 150:1 at the conservative end of the range.

**Fiscal multiplier**: Additional competition enforcement generates tax revenue through two channels. First, competitive markets generate more economic activity (higher employment, more business formation, faster innovation), expanding the tax base. Second, reduced monopoly rents shift income from capital owners (who have high savings rates and lower marginal propensity to consume) to workers and consumers (who have lower savings rates and higher marginal propensity to consume), generating a GDP-level demand effect.

### 5.2 Structural Breakups (20b) — AT&T Precedent Quantified

**The AT&T 1984 case**: The breakup of AT&T into seven "Baby Bell" regional carriers and a separate AT&T long distance company is the canonical precedent for structural remedy in US antitrust. Watzinger and Schnitzer (CEPR Discussion Paper 17635, 2022) are the most rigorous quantification:

- Total patenting by US inventors in telecommunications-related fields increased by **19%** following the breakup, driven primarily by companies outside the original Bell System
- The number of active companies in "telephone and telegraph apparatus" manufacturing increased **56%**, from 259 firms in 1982 to 403 firms in 1986
- Western Electric's share of Bell System equipment purchases fell from **92% to 58%** between 1982 and 1986, demonstrating that procurement competition emerged rapidly
- The effect on innovation outside telecommunications was more substantial than within it: where the Bell System could still exclude competitors (local telephony), innovation did not increase; where competition was introduced, it did

**What the AT&T case does not prove**: The AEI critique (2014) correctly notes that AT&T's regional Baby Bells re-merged over 25 years: SBC acquired AT&T Corp in 2005 and then BellSouth in 2006 (recreating a largely reconstituted AT&T), while Verizon absorbed MCI. The dynamism of the post-breakup period was real, but without continued enforcement, structural remedies can be unwound through subsequent M&A.

**The Standard Oil 1911 precedent**: *Standard Oil Co. of New Jersey v. United States*, 221 U.S. 1 (1911), dissolved the Standard Oil Trust into 34 independent companies. The breakup outcomes were mixed but instructive. Granitz and Klein (1996) established that Standard Oil's dominance had been maintained partly through collusion with railroad cartels, which the dissolution disrupted. In the short term, the regional companies that emerged competed vigorously with one another on price — the immediate competitive effect was substantial. However, as with AT&T, the regional companies began reconsolidating within decades; two of the 34 descendants (Standard Oil of New Jersey and Standard Oil of New York) eventually became ExxonMobil, the largest US energy company. The structural lesson of both Standard Oil and AT&T is that breakup remedies require continued antitrust vigilance: structural remedies create competitive opportunity but do not eliminate the incentives to reconsolidate if enforcement subsequently relaxes.

**Net cost**: The legal costs of the AT&T case — spanning from the DOJ's initial complaint in 1974 to the consent decree in 1982 — were substantial for government, estimated at tens of millions in 1982 dollars. At modern equivalent, a major structural case would cost the government $50-150 million in litigation resources over several years. Against $300 billion in annual consumer savings from restored competition, the cost-benefit ratio is decisive.

### 5.3 Monopsony Reform (20c) — Wage Gains from Non-Compete Ban

**FTC quantification of the non-compete ban**: The FTC's April 2024 final rule banning non-compete clauses — enjoined by a federal court in Texas in August 2024 before taking effect — was accompanied by a detailed regulatory impact analysis. The FTC estimated:

- **30 million workers** (one in five US workers) are currently bound by non-compete clauses
- Average worker earnings would rise by an estimated **$524 per year** from the ban
- Aggregate wage gains over 10 years: **$400-488 billion**
- New business formation would increase by **2.7%**, creating approximately **8,500 new businesses annually**
- The rule would reduce healthcare costs by an estimated $74-194 billion over 10 years by increasing physician mobility and labor market competition

**Starr-Balasubramanian evidence on state enforcement variation**: The natural experiment of varying state enforcement of non-competes produces clean wage estimates:
- Workers subject to enforceable non-competes earn approximately **4-6% lower hourly wages** than equivalent workers in states that do not enforce them
- After 8 years, workers who began employment under an enforceable non-compete have earned approximately **5% less cumulatively** than workers in non-enforcing states
- New hire wages increase **4%** in states that transition from enforcement to non-enforcement
- Within-industry entrepreneurship is lower in states with strong non-compete enforcement

**The distributional impact**: Non-compete agreements are not concentrated among high-wage workers who might plausibly be protecting genuine trade secrets. The FTC's regulatory analysis found that non-competes are widespread among low-wage service workers — including fast food employees, security guards, and hair stylists — who have no access to trade secrets and for whom the agreements function purely as wage suppression and mobility restriction tools. Approximately 30% of workers earning under $40,000 per year are subject to non-compete agreements in states where they are enforced. This distributional finding is important for policy: the argument that non-competes protect legitimate business interests in trade secrets applies to a small fraction of cases; the majority of non-compete coverage is in contexts where no legitimate interest is plausibly served.

**The legal challenge**: The FTC's final rule banning non-competes was enjoined by the US District Court for the Northern District of Texas in August 2024 in *Ryan LLC v. FTC*, and that injunction was subsequently expanded to a nationwide block. The court held that the FTC lacks statutory authority to issue substantive rules banning non-competes under its Section 6(g) rulemaking authority. The ruling did not address the merits of whether non-competes are harmful — it was purely jurisdictional. Congressional action to ban non-competes legislatively (which the court's ruling would not affect) or to clarify the FTC's rulemaking authority would achieve the same policy outcome. Several states — including California, North Dakota, Minnesota, and Oklahoma — already prohibit or severely restrict non-compete enforcement with measurable positive wage effects.

**Aggregate fiscal impact**: The FTC's $400-488 billion over 10 years estimate, combined with the CBO's standard multiplier analysis, suggests fiscal revenue gains of approximately $80-100 billion over 10 years from higher worker incomes flowing through the income tax base. The cost of the rule itself — primarily compliance costs for employers — was estimated at approximately $1-2 billion total.

Sources: [FTC: Fact Sheet on FTC's Proposed Final Noncompete Rule (April 2024)](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2024/04/fact-sheet-ftcs-proposed-final-noncompete-rule); [FTC: Federal Register Non-Compete Clause Rule (May 2024)](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2024/05/07/2024-09171/non-compete-clause-rule); [Starr, Balasubramanian, et al.: Noncompete Agreements in the US Labor Force, Journal of Law and Economics (2021)](https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/10.1086/712206); [Minneapolis Fed: New Data on Non-Compete Contracts (2023)](https://www.minneapolisfed.org/article/2023/new-data-on-non-compete-contracts-and-what-they-mean-for-workers); [UCLA Anderson Review: Noncompete Agreements Reduce Worker Pay and Overall Economic Activity](https://anderson-review.ucla.edu/noncompete/)

### 5.4 Enforcement Funding (20d) — ROI of Antitrust Investment

**The Baker-Shapiro analysis of merger enforcement**: Jonathan Baker (American University) and Carl Shapiro (UC Berkeley) produced the most rigorous academic analysis of merger enforcement returns. Their work on the Obama-era enforcement record showed that blocking or conditioning anticompetitive mergers typically prevented consumer price increases on the order of 8-15% in affected markets. Translating to aggregate terms: the DOJ and FTC review approximately 1,500-2,000 transactions per year subject to pre-merger notification. A 2023 NBER working paper (Cosnita-Langlais and Tropeano) on merger effects and antitrust enforcement using US retail data found that consummated mergers in consumer packaged goods increased prices by 4-7% on average.

**Return on enforcement investment**: A rough calculation: if the combined FTC-DOJ budget of $650 million per year prevented or conditioned mergers saving consumers 5% in prices across markets representing $50 billion in annual sales, the annual consumer benefit would be $2.5 billion — a **3.8:1 return on investment** from merger review alone, before accounting for cartel deterrence, monopolization cases, and non-compete enforcement. Former FTC Chief Economist Michael Salinger estimated in congressional testimony that the FTC returns $6-8 in consumer savings for every $1 spent on competition enforcement. At the proposed $1.6 billion additional investment level, this implies $10-13 billion in additional annual consumer savings from the enforcement increment alone.

**The understaffing problem in specific terms**: The FTC reviews approximately 1,500-2,000 pre-merger notifications per year under Hart-Scott-Rodino (HSR) with a staff of roughly 1,100 total employees across all functions (competition and consumer protection). The DOJ Antitrust Division employs approximately 700-800 attorneys and economists for both civil and criminal antitrust enforcement. By comparison, a single major merger investigation — the DOJ's investigation of Microsoft's acquisition of Activision Blizzard, or the FTC's investigation of Amazon — consumes hundreds of staff-hours across multiple divisions for months. The practical effect of inadequate staffing is that agencies must triage: they pursue the largest, most visible cases and allow thousands of smaller transactions — each individually modest, but collectively transformative — to proceed unchallenged. The cumulative competitive harm from thousands of unreviewed mid-market consolidations is not captured in any single enforcement action but shows up in the sector-level concentration data that has steadily worsened over four decades.

**The HSR threshold problem**: The Hart-Scott-Rodino notification threshold — currently $119.5 million for the 2024 reporting year — was set in 1976 and has been adjusted for inflation, but the economic significance of smaller acquisitions has grown as digital businesses are acquired at early stages for relatively small dollar values that mask their future market importance. Facebook's acquisition of Instagram for $1 billion (2012) and WhatsApp for $19 billion (2014) were above HSR thresholds and were reviewed (and cleared) by the FTC. Many earlier acquisitions that built digital platform dominance were below threshold and received no review at all.

Sources: [Baker and Shapiro: Reinvigorating Horizontal Merger Enforcement (SSRN)](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=991588); [Shapiro: Antitrust: What Went Wrong and How to Fix It (UC Berkeley)](https://faculty.haas.berkeley.edu/shapiro/fixingantitrust.pdf); [NBER Working Paper 31123: Merger Effects and Antitrust Enforcement (2023)](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w31123/w31123.pdf); [FTC: FY2024 Agency Financial Report](https://www.ftc.gov/system/files/ftc_gov/pdf/fy2024ftcagencyfinancialreport11152024.pdf)

### 5.5 Corporate Governance (20e) — Codetermination Wage Effects

**CEO pay ratio comparison**: The data on CEO-to-worker pay ratios is stark and consistent:

| Country | Median CEO:Worker Pay Ratio |
|---|---|
| Germany (DAX companies) | ~53:1 (2018) |
| UK (FTSE 100) | ~73:1 (2022) |
| France (CAC 40) | ~67:1 (2022) |
| US (S&P 500) | ~197:1 (2022) |
| US (large-cap outliers) | 300:1 to 3,000:1 |

These differences are not purely attributable to codetermination — industry mix, tax structures, and social norms all play roles. However, the structural mechanism is clear: in German codetermination companies, worker representatives on supervisory boards have standing to contest executive compensation proposals, creating institutional friction that limits unconstrained executive pay escalation. US boards have no such structural friction; compensation committees operate largely free from worker representation.

**Wage compression within firms**: The most robust finding on codetermination's wage effects is not the CEO pay ratio but within-firm wage compression. Firms with works councils in Germany show significantly compressed wage distributions between top and bottom quintiles within the firm, controlling for firm size, industry, and productivity. This effect operates primarily through wage floors for lower-skill workers being raised by works council negotiations, rather than executive pay being capped. The MIT primer on German industrial relations (Freeman and Lazear, 1995, and subsequent work by Addison, Schank, Schnabel, and Wagner) documents that works council firms pay lower-skilled workers 4-8% more than comparable non-works-council firms, a wage compression effect that operates independently of the board-level codetermination system.

**Works councils vs. board representation**: It is critical for US policy analysis to distinguish between two separate elements of the German model often conflated in US debates. *Works councils* (Betriebsräte) operate at the plant or establishment level with consultation rights over working conditions, hours, and layoffs; these exist in firms with five or more employees. *Board representation* (Mitbestimmung) operates at the supervisory board level in large companies. The wage effects are primarily attributable to works councils, not board representation. A US reform that introduced either element without the other would produce different effects: works councils at the establishment level have stronger wage compression effects; board representation has stronger effects on executive compensation and major strategic decisions. The US reform discussion often focuses exclusively on board representation while ignoring works councils, which is where most of the wage-equalizing evidence is concentrated.

**UK evidence**: The UK Worker Representation and Participation Survey (2004, updated versions) found that firms with any form of employee voice mechanism show modestly better outcomes on job satisfaction and stability, but the effect on wages is small unless the voice mechanism has formal bargaining rights. This suggests that advisory-only worker representation (as in some UK and US board representation proposals) has weaker effects than the German codetermination model with binding worker board votes. The UK's recent "worker director" experiments — including at some public sector entities and the John Lewis Partnership model of shared ownership — provide partial evidence but do not replicate German codetermination's combination of legal mandate and sectoral bargaining infrastructure.

**Cost of codetermination reform**: The fiscal cost of requiring worker board representation is negligible — it is a regulatory mandate with compliance costs but no direct public expenditure. The private cost to dominant shareholders (reduced ability to extract rents through executive compensation) is precisely the redistribution the reform targets. The Roosevelt Institute estimated that if S&P 500 CEO pay ratios were compressed to the German median (53:1) from the current US median (197:1), the income transferred from executive compensation to worker compensation and/or shareholder returns would be approximately $50-80 billion annually across the S&P 500 alone — a redistribution that would show up in wage growth for lower and middle earners at those firms.

Sources: [Springer: Executive Compensation in Germany](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11573-020-00978-y); [Bruegel: Codetermination in Germany](https://www.bruegel.org/blog-post/codetermination-germany-role-model-uk-and-us); [Columbia Business Law Review: Codetermination (2021)](https://journals.library.columbia.edu/index.php/CBLR/article/download/7809/3950/14797); [ACAS: Worker Representation in the UK](https://www.acas.org.uk/worker-representation); [MIT Economics: The German Model of Industrial Relations: A Primer](https://economics.mit.edu/sites/default/files/publications/JEP_German_Model_of_Industrial_Relations_Primer.pdf)

## Section 6: The Strongest Counterarguments and Rebuttals

### 6.1 Chicago School: "Concentration Is Efficient at Scale"

**The argument in its strongest form**: The Chicago School does not claim that monopoly is good. It claims that observed concentration often reflects superior efficiency: firms that serve customers better grow larger, and antitrust intervention that breaks up efficient firms harms consumers by destroying the scale economies and organizational capabilities that produced low prices and high quality in the first place. Harold Demsetz's "Industry Structure, Market Rivalry, and Public Policy" (1973) is the canonical formulation: high profit rates in concentrated industries reflect efficient production, not monopoly extraction.

**Why the argument fails against the current evidence**:

First, Philippon's "Great Reversal" directly tests the efficiency hypothesis: if US concentration reflects superior efficiency, then more concentrated markets should show lower prices than European comparators. The evidence is the opposite — US markets are more concentrated *and* have higher prices than EU markets with similar technology and demand conditions. Philippon directly compares US and EU mobile telecom, airlines, and financial services and shows the concentration-price relationship runs the wrong direction for the efficiency story. The efficiency claim predicts a negative concentration-price relationship (bigger firms = lower costs = lower prices); the data shows a positive one (bigger firms = more market power = higher prices).

Second, De Loecker, Eeckhout, and Unger's markup data shows that the markup increase is concentrated among the top quartile of firms by existing markup level — meaning the firms already extracting the highest rents are the ones whose markups rose most. This is not consistent with a market-performance story (which would predict efficient firms displacing less efficient ones with eventual markup convergence) but is consistent with a market-power story (which predicts entrenched dominant firms with barriers to entry sustaining and extending their rent extraction). The efficiency story also predicts declining markups as efficient firms spread their scale advantages; the observed pattern is rising markups concentrated among dominant incumbents.

Third, the business dynamism evidence contradicts the efficiency story: if concentration reflected efficient superiority, we would expect higher productivity growth and faster innovation in concentrated sectors. Instead, productivity growth has *slowed* across the US economy since the 1980s, and new business formation has declined — both consistent with concentration suppressing competition and entry, not with concentration reflecting market-selected efficiency.

Fourth, the most direct evidence against the efficiency-concentration claim comes from international comparison: the European Union, with substantially more vigorous antitrust enforcement, has comparable or higher productivity in the sectors where it maintains competitive markets (telecommunications, airlines, retail banking). If US concentration reflected genuine efficiency gains, we would expect US firms in concentrated sectors to outperform their European counterparts on productivity metrics. The evidence does not support this expectation.

Sources: [Philippon: The Great Reversal (2019)](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/books/9780674260320); [De Loecker, Eeckhout, Unger: QJE (2020)](https://academic.oup.com/qje/article-abstract/135/2/561/5714769); [Demsetz: Industry Structure, Market Rivalry, and Public Policy, JLE (1973)](https://www.jstor.org/stable/725004)

### 6.2 "Big Tech Created Massive Consumer Value for Free"

**The argument**: Google Search, Gmail, Maps, YouTube, and Facebook are provided to consumers at zero monetary price. The consumer welfare standard, applied literally, finds no harm because consumers pay nothing. Banning or breaking up these services would eliminate trillions of dollars in consumer surplus. This is the strongest version of the defense of the consumer welfare standard as applied to digital platforms.

**The rebuttal, structured in three parts**:

*Part 1 — "Free" is not free; the price is behavioral data.* Shoshana Zuboff's "The Age of Surveillance Capitalism" (2019) documents that the zero-price model is funded by the extraction and sale of behavioral data at scale — a price paid in attention, privacy, and the manipulation of behavior. The fact that this price is non-monetary does not make it zero; it makes it invisible to consumer welfare accounting that looks only at monetary prices. The FTC's 2021 report on surveillance practices estimated the US behavioral advertising industry at approximately $100 billion annually — funded entirely by the value of user data, which users "pay" in kind.

*Part 2 — The consumer welfare calculation ignores workers, suppliers, and democracy.* Even granting massive consumer surplus from free services, the consumer welfare standard explicitly ignores harm to platform workers (gig workers, content moderators), harm to competing businesses (merchants subject to Amazon's copycat practices, app developers subject to 30% App Store fees), harm to journalism and the information ecosystem, and harm to democratic institutions from political advertising opacity. A total welfare calculation that includes these harms is considerably more ambiguous.

*Part 3 — The dynamic counterfactual.* The argument assumes the current service quality exists because of the integrated monopoly structure. The AT&T precedent suggests the opposite: the breakup produced *more* innovation, not less, because the integrated monopoly had been suppressing external innovation for 30 years. The correct comparison is not "Google Maps vs. nothing" but "Google Maps vs. what a competitive mapping services market would produce." The EU's DMA interventions in app store interoperability and steering restrictions are a live experiment on this question.

*Part 4 — The quantification of surveillance costs.* The FTC's July 2024 report on commercial surveillance found that major platforms collect data across hundreds of data points per user and sell access to this data to thousands of third-party data brokers, advertisers, and analytics firms. The FTC estimated that the value of US consumer data sold through these channels exceeded $200 billion annually. This is not a cost to consumers in the traditional sense, but it represents a transfer of value — from consumers who create data through their behavior, to platforms and data brokers who monetize it. Users of "free" Google search are not customers; they are, as the aphorism runs, the product. The welfare economics of this arrangement are unresolved in the academic literature, but the scale ($200B+) is sufficient to establish that zero-price does not mean zero-cost.

Sources: [Zuboff: The Age of Surveillance Capitalism (2019)](https://shoshanazuboff.com/book/about/); [FTC: Commercial Surveillance Report (2022)](https://www.ftc.gov/system/files/ftc_gov/pdf/p195400_commercial_surveillance_advance_notice_of_proposed_rulemaking.pdf); [Khan: Amazon's Antitrust Paradox, Yale Law Journal (2017)](https://www.yalelawjournal.org/note/amazons-antitrust-paradox); [FTC: 2024 Surveillance Pricing Orders to Eight Companies](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2024/07/ftc-issues-orders-eight-companies-offering-surveillance-pricing)

### 6.3 "Breakups Will Destroy Innovation and US Competitiveness"

**The argument in its strongest form**: The US won the global AI race — GPT-4, Gemini, Claude, Llama — because integrated large technology firms could invest tens of billions in compute, talent, and research. Alphabet, Microsoft, Meta, and Amazon's hyperscale data center infrastructure is the foundation of US AI leadership. Breaking up these firms would fragment that infrastructure, destroy the investment capacity, and hand AI leadership to China. This is the most politically resonant version of the anti-breakup argument and has been deployed extensively by technology company lobbying.

**The empirical rebuttal**:

*First*, the AI investment argument confuses scale with monopoly. The argument for maintaining current corporate structures rests on the claim that only integrated giants with market power in adjacent markets can fund frontier AI research. But the evidence for this claim is thin. OpenAI was not a product of a vertically integrated monopoly; it was a research lab that attracted investment from Microsoft. Anthropic was a spinoff from OpenAI, which itself was a spinoff from traditional research institutions. The "AI requires monopoly" claim is not supported by the actual origin stories of the leading AI companies.

*Second*, the structural counterfactual is not "no large firms." Breakups of specific market-distorting integrations — separating Google Search from the advertising technology stack, requiring Apple to allow third-party app stores, separating Amazon Marketplace from Amazon Retail — would not dissolve the firms. They would require separate ownership and governance of business units that currently benefit from self-preferencing. Google would remain a large, capable firm; it would simply not be able to use its search monopoly to subsidize below-cost competition in ad tech.

*Third*, the killer acquisitions evidence directly contradicts the innovation argument: if integrated tech monopolists are uniquely good at developing innovation, why do they acquire and systematically shelve competing drug projects and startup technologies? The 5.3-7.4% killer acquisition rate in pharma, and the circumstantial evidence of Facebook acquiring Instagram and WhatsApp to prevent competition rather than develop them into standalone platforms, is inconsistent with the claim that integration maximizes innovation.

*Fourth*, the historical record of Bell Labs specifically is used by proponents of large integrated firms to argue that monopoly-funded basic research produces public goods. Bell Labs invented the transistor, information theory, UNIX, and cellular telephony while operating as a protected monopoly. This argument has a legitimate empirical basis — Bell Labs' research record is extraordinary. But the argument proves too much: if the lesson is that monopoly profits can fund basic research, the policy implication is publicly funded research (which does not require monopoly) rather than privately tolerated monopoly (which extracts consumer rents that dwarf the research budget). Bell Labs' annual research budget in its peak years was approximately $1-3 billion in current dollars. US publicly funded basic research through NSF, NIH, DARPA, and DOE runs to approximately $80-100 billion per year. The public sector does not need monopoly toleration to fund basic research at scale.

*Fifth*, the specific claim that "the US won the AI race because of integrated Big Tech" confuses necessary and sufficient conditions. Large compute infrastructure was necessary for frontier model training at scale; it was not sufficient, and it was not uniquely available to monopoly-integrated firms. OpenAI, Anthropic, Mistral (French), and DeepSeek (Chinese) all trained frontier models on cloud infrastructure without being vertically integrated monopolies. The integration of Google Search with Gemini or Microsoft Office with GPT-4 provides distribution advantages, not research advantages. The AI race is not over, and its outcome will be determined by talent, data, regulatory environment, and investment — none of which requires the specific market structure of current US tech giants to be maintained.

Sources: [Cunningham, Ederer, Ma: Killer Acquisitions (2021)](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3241707); [Watzinger and Schnitzer: The Breakup of the Bell System (2022)](https://ideas.repec.org/p/cpr/ceprdp/17635.html); [Khan: Amazon's Antitrust Paradox (2017)](https://www.yalelawjournal.org/note/amazons-antitrust-paradox); [CEI: Ma Bell Suppressed Innovation for Thirty Grueling Years](https://cei.org/opeds_articles/ma-bell-suppressed-innovation-for-thirty-grueling-years/)

### 6.4 "Amazon Lowers Prices for Consumers"

**The argument**: Amazon has driven prices down through relentless competitive pressure on retailers, introduced two-day delivery as a consumer expectation, and enabled small businesses to reach national markets. The Amazon Marketplace seller ecosystem supports hundreds of thousands of small businesses. The consumer welfare case against Amazon is weak because consumers benefit from Amazon's scale and the competition it has brought to retail.

**The empirical rebuttal**:

*The short-run and long-run distinction*. Amazon's pattern of entering markets with below-cost pricing, eliminating competitors, and then raising prices or fees is precisely the predatory pricing strategy the consumer welfare standard was historically designed to prevent — but which *Brooke Group v. Brown & Williamson* (1993) made nearly impossible to prosecute by requiring proof of recoupment. The FTC's 2023 complaint documents the recoupment mechanism directly: after eliminating competition in online marketplace services, Amazon imposed steadily rising fees on third-party sellers (from roughly 19% of seller revenue in 2014 to approximately 50% by 2023). The rising fee structure is the recoupment that predatory pricing theory predicts; it occurs through seller fees rather than consumer prices, making it invisible to consumer-only welfare analysis.

*The merchant cost pass-through*. The fees Amazon charges sellers do not stay with Amazon — they are passed through to consumers in the form of higher prices on Amazon's platform and on competing platforms. The "Most Favored Nation" clauses in Amazon's seller contracts — which the FTC's complaint alleges prevent sellers from offering lower prices elsewhere — ensure that Amazon's high-fee structure raises prices across the entire e-commerce market, not just on Amazon itself. This is the mechanism by which a platform that "lowers prices" can simultaneously raise the price level across the economy.

*The quality dimension*. The FTC complaint documents that Amazon degrades its own search results by inserting advertisements labeled in ways that obscure their paid nature, prioritizes Amazon's private-label products over demonstrably higher-quality competing products, and uses seller data to identify and copy successful third-party products. These are quality-dimension harms that do not register as price increases but represent real consumer welfare losses invisible to price-only welfare measurement.

Sources: [FTC: FTC Sues Amazon for Illegally Maintaining Monopoly Power (2023)](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2023/09/ftc-sues-amazon-illegally-maintaining-monopoly-power); [FTC: Amazon Complaint, Redacted Version](https://www.ftc.gov/system/files/ftc_gov/pdf/1910134amazonecommercecomplaintrevisedredactions.pdf); [Khan: Amazon's Antitrust Paradox, Yale Law Journal (2017)](https://www.yalelawjournal.org/note/amazons-antitrust-paradox); [HBR: Understanding the Tradeoffs of the Amazon Antitrust Case (2024)](https://hbr.org/2024/01/understanding-the-tradeoffs-of-the-amazon-antitrust-case)

### 6.5 "Codetermination Is Incompatible with US Corporate Law"

**The argument**: Fiduciary duty doctrine in US corporate law requires corporate directors to act in the interests of shareholders. Worker representatives on boards would face irreconcilable conflicts between this fiduciary duty and their interests as employees. Additionally, US labor law (the NLRA) preempts state laws that might otherwise authorize worker representation, creating a legal structure actively hostile to codetermination. The Volkswagen-UAW organizing failure in Tennessee (2014) showed that even voluntary codetermination structures face legal and institutional obstacles in the US context.

**The rebuttal**:

*First*, the legal obstacle is not constitutional but statutory and judicially constructed. Delaware corporate law and the shareholder primacy doctrine are creatures of state statute and court interpretation, not constitutional mandate. The Business Roundtable's own 2019 "Statement on the Purpose of a Corporation" explicitly rejected shareholder-only primacy, indicating that even corporate leadership acknowledges the framework is under stress. Congressional legislation establishing worker board representation requirements — modeled on Germany's Mitbestimmungsgesetz — would override state corporate law under the supremacy clause.

*Second*, the NLRA preemption argument applies specifically to state-law codetermination mandates, not federal legislation. Federal codetermination legislation would not conflict with the NLRA; it would supplement it.

*Third*, the German model is not the only model. The UK has developed various lighter-touch worker voice mechanisms. The EU Non-Financial Reporting Directive and subsequent Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive require disclosure of worker engagement practices. These intermediate steps — requiring worker representation committees with consultation rights rather than full board parity — face fewer legal obstacles and have evidence of positive outcomes in continental Europe.

*Fourth on the evidence*, the research on codetermination's economic effects is not a strong argument against it on its own terms. As documented in Section 5.5, the empirical literature finds "neutral or small, positive effects" on firm performance. A reform that redistributes bargaining power from shareholders to workers without harming firm performance is a distributional intervention that the efficiency critique cannot defeat.

*Fifth, on political feasibility*, the Volkswagen-Tennessee example is frequently cited as evidence of US institutional hostility to codetermination. But the Tennessee plant vote in 2014 failed not because codetermination is incompatible with US law but because Tennessee Governor Bill Haslam, a Republican, publicly stated that the state would reconsider $300 million in incentives for VW if workers voted for the UAW — effectively a threat of state government intervention against worker organization. The subsequent Volkswagen-Chattanooga vote in 2024 succeeded, with workers voting to join the UAW, and the Tennessee government did not follow through on its earlier threat. The updated evidence is that voluntary codetermination structures can be established in the US South when workers choose them and state governments do not actively intervene.

Sources: [Business Roundtable: Statement on the Purpose of a Corporation (2019)](https://www.businessroundtable.org/business-roundtable-redefines-the-purpose-of-a-corporation-to-promote-an-economy-that-serves-all-americans); [Columbia Business Law Review: Codetermination (2021)](https://journals.library.columbia.edu/index.php/CBLR/article/download/7809/3950/14797); [Bruegel: Codetermination in Germany (2022)](https://www.bruegel.org/blog-post/codetermination-germany-role-model-uk-and-us)

## Section 7: Sectors Not Fully Analyzed in the Proposal

### 7.1 Defense Procurement Concentration

The proposal notes five firms controlling 35% of defense procurement. The full picture is more concentrated at the platform level: Lockheed Martin, Boeing, Raytheon (RTX), General Dynamics, and Northrop Grumman collectively received approximately $200 billion in annual Pentagon contract awards in recent years, with Lockheed Martin alone routinely capturing $40-50 billion annually. The defense industrial base has undergone a consolidation from 51 prime aerospace and defense contractors in 1993 to five today — a consolidation the DOD itself facilitated under the "Last Supper" process guided by Deputy Defense Secretary William Perry in 1993.

The consequence of defense concentration is not consumer price inflation but procurement cost overruns and reduced innovation competition. The F-35 program — the most expensive weapons system in US history at a lifetime cost estimated above $1.7 trillion — has been characterized by GAO in multiple reports as suffering from cost overruns and schedule delays attributable in part to the absence of competitive pressure: Lockheed Martin won the contract in 2001 and has faced essentially no competitive threat since.

A recent asymmetry in the reform discussion: the Biden administration's 2022 Industrial Base Report identified defense consolidation as a strategic vulnerability (not just an economic problem) — single-source suppliers create supply chain fragility, as demonstrated when the Ukraine war revealed depleted munitions stocks that only one or two domestic manufacturers could resupply.

### 7.2 Grain Trading and Agricultural Input Concentration

The grain trading sector — where four firms (Archer-Daniels-Midland, Bunge, Cargill, and Louis Dreyfus, the "ABCD" traders) control more than 60% of global grain flows — represents a form of concentration that operates largely outside domestic antitrust scrutiny because the firms function as global commodity intermediaries. For US farmers, the ABCD cartel functions as a buyer's cartel (monopsony) on the input side of commodity markets: farmers sell to a limited set of grain purchasers who operate with market power in local elevator markets.

The agricultural input side is even more concentrated. The 2017-2018 wave of agrochemical mergers — Bayer-Monsanto ($66 billion), ChemChina-Syngenta ($43 billion), and Dow-DuPont — reduced the number of major seed and pesticide companies from six to three: Bayer (Monsanto), ChemChina (Syngenta), and Corteva (Dow-DuPont). These three firms now control approximately 60% of the global commercial seed market and 70% of the global pesticide market simultaneously, creating vertically integrated control over the key inputs to food production. The DOJ's conditions on the Bayer-Monsanto merger — requiring divestitures to BASF — have been assessed by researchers as insufficient to restore meaningful seed market competition.

Sources: [GRAIN: ABCD and the Geopolitics of Grain (2020)](https://www.grain.org/en/article/6580); [Environmental Working Group: Seed and Agrichemical Consolidation After Bayer-Monsanto (2019)](https://www.ewg.org/agmag/2019/03/how-mega-mergers-transformed-seed-industry); [Open Markets Institute: Agricultural Monopoly](https://www.openmarketsinstitute.org/publications/report-food-and-power-addressing-monopolization-in-america-s-food-system)

## Section 8: The Cross-Ownership Problem

A dimension of concentration that standard HHI analysis misses is the rise of common ownership — the same institutional investors holding large equity stakes in competing firms within the same industry simultaneously.

**The Azar-Schmalz-Tecu finding (2018)**: José Azar, Martin Schmalz, and Isabel Tecu published "Anticompetitive Effects of Common Ownership" in the *Journal of Finance* (2018), analyzing the US airline industry. Their finding: airline ticket prices are **3-11% higher** on routes where competing carriers share common major shareholders (primarily BlackRock, Vanguard, and State Street) than on routes without common ownership. The mechanism they propose is that common shareholders, holding stakes in multiple competing airlines, prefer that those airlines compete less vigorously — since aggressive competition would increase one portfolio company's market share at the expense of another portfolio company's profits, with the net effect on the shareholder's portfolio potentially negative.

**Scale of common ownership**: As of 2020, the "Big Three" asset managers (BlackRock, Vanguard, State Street) collectively held the largest ownership stakes in approximately 88% of S&P 500 companies. They are simultaneously the largest shareholders in competing banks, competing pharmaceutical companies, competing airlines, and competing tech firms. This creates a web of common ownership that standard antitrust analysis — which focuses on whether firms compete in the same product market — cannot capture, because the incentive-softening effect operates through the ownership structure rather than through explicit coordination.

**Policy implications**: The common ownership problem is analytically distinct from the concentration problems addressed in Domain 20a-20e, but it interacts with them: even if structural breakups and behavioral remedies restore competition among firms, common ownership can dampen competitive incentives at the level of shareholder governance. Proposed remedies include: prohibiting institutional investors above a size threshold from holding shares in competing firms simultaneously (proposed by Posner, Scott Morton, and Weyl, 2017); requiring passive index funds to vote shares in a competitively neutral manner; or capping the stake size that any single institutional investor can hold in a given industry. None of these proposals has been enacted; the theoretical case is strong but the enforcement framework does not yet exist.

Sources: [Azar, Schmalz, Tecu: Anticompetitive Effects of Common Ownership, Journal of Finance (2018)](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/jofi.12698); [Posner, Scott Morton, Weyl: A Proposal to Limit the Anticompetitive Power of Institutional Investors, Antitrust Law Journal (2017)](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=2872754); [Philippon: The Great Reversal — Chapter on Finance](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/books/9780674260320)

## Section 9: Active Litigation Landscape Relevant to Domain 20 Reforms

Domain 20 reforms interact directly with an active litigation landscape that will shape both what is legally possible and what precedents are available.

### 9.1 United States v. Google LLC (Search and Advertising)

Judge Amit Mehta's August 2024 ruling in *United States v. Google LLC* (D.D.C. 2024) found that Google holds a monopoly in the markets for general search services and general search text advertising, maintained through exclusive default agreements with Apple, Samsung, and other device makers that cost Google approximately $26 billion per year. The remedy phase began in 2025 and is the most consequential US antitrust case since *United States v. Microsoft* (2001). DOJ requested structural relief including forced divestiture of Chrome, Android, or both. Google appealed the liability finding; the remedy proceedings continue.

The significance for Domain 20: the case is the first successful monopolization case against a major digital platform under the consumer welfare standard, demonstrating that the existing legal framework can reach platform monopolies when the facts are sufficiently clear. It also demonstrates the length of the process — the DOJ investigation began in 2019, trial concluded in 2023, liability finding in August 2024, remedies ongoing in 2025-2026 — a five-to-seven-year cycle that underscores why waiting for cases to be resolved case-by-case is insufficient.

### 9.2 FTC v. Amazon (E-Commerce)

The FTC's September 2023 complaint in *FTC v. Amazon.com, Inc.* (W.D. Wash.) alleges that Amazon maintains monopoly power in two markets: online superstore services for consumers, and marketplace services for third-party sellers. The complaint documents Amazon's use of Most Favored Nation clauses, Fulfillment by Amazon tying, and search result manipulation as anticompetitive conduct. The case is in early litigation stages as of 2026; no trial date has been set. The case tests whether the consumer welfare standard, as developed since Bork, can reach a platform that offers consumers apparent low prices while extracting monopoly rents from sellers who pass costs through to consumers indirectly.

### 9.3 DOJ v. Apple (Smartphone Monopoly)

The DOJ's March 2024 complaint in *United States v. Apple Inc.* (D.N.J.) alleges that Apple maintains monopoly power in the smartphone market through restrictions that prevent third-party apps, cloud streaming services, and competing messaging platforms from offering the full functionality available to Apple's own products. The case specifically targets Apple's use of hardware-software-services integration as a tool to create lock-in and exclude competition, not merely as a product design choice. This is the broadest US antitrust challenge to Apple's business model yet filed.

### 9.4 DOJ v. Google (Ad Tech)

Separate from the search case, the DOJ filed *United States v. Google LLC* in the Eastern District of Virginia in January 2023, targeting Google's advertising technology stack specifically. Trial concluded in late 2024; Judge Leonie Brinkema's ruling (issued November 2024) found Google liable for monopolizing the publisher ad server and ad exchange markets. Remedies are under consideration as of 2026. The case directly addresses the media ecosystem harm documented in Section 3.3: Google's integrated control of publisher ad servers, the ad exchange, and advertiser tools simultaneously enables extraction that reduces publisher revenue and thus the economic foundation for journalism.

**Cross-cutting observation**: All four of these cases were brought under the existing consumer welfare standard framework, suggesting that vigorous enforcement within current law is possible given adequate resources and prosecutorial will. The standard reform proposals in Domain 20 (20a) would make future cases easier but are not strictly necessary for the currently active cases to succeed. The key enforcement resource constraint — not the legal standard — is the binding limit on how many such complex cases can be prosecuted simultaneously.

### A Note on Methodological Caution

Several of the empirical findings summarized in this document are more contested in the academic literature than the summary sections may suggest. Specific caveats for use in adversarial policy settings:

**Common ownership effects (Section 8)**: The Azar-Schmalz-Tecu airline result has been contested by Lewellen and Lowry (2021), who argue the estimated effect is smaller and less robust to alternative specifications. The theoretical mechanism is plausible and the empirical literature is active but not settled. Cite the airline finding as suggestive evidence, not established fact.

**Killer acquisitions in digital markets**: The Cunningham-Ederer-Ma pharmaceutical finding is robust within its sample. The extension to digital markets is genuinely weaker — the CEPR critique (2023) correctly notes that digital acquisitions do not follow the same product pipeline logic as pharmaceutical acquisitions. The concern about acquisition-based suppression of digital competition is real; the "killer" framing overstates the evidentiary precision for digital markets specifically.

**Codetermination wage compression**: The wage compression evidence is concentrated in the works council literature, where within-firm wage compression effects are well-documented. Board-level codetermination wage effects on executive pay are smaller and more uncertain; the 2009 German Compensation Act finding (higher executive pay following expanded supervisory board authority) is a genuine complicating result that should be acknowledged.

**FTC non-compete wage estimate**: The $400-488 billion over 10 years figure is an extrapolation from state-variation natural experiments to a nationwide counterfactual. It relies on assumptions about labor market equilibrium adjustment that may not hold uniformly across sectors. The average worker wage gain estimate ($524/year) is more robust; the aggregate figure is more sensitive to modeling assumptions.

**Markup calculations and the De Loecker-Eeckhout methodology**: The markup calculation is based on production function estimation that requires assumptions about the production technology. Some critiques argue the methodology may overstate markup increases by attributing intangible capital investments (software, brand) to markup rather than to physical capital. The direction of the finding — rising markups since 1980 — is robust across multiple methodologies; the precise magnitude (67% above marginal cost by 2016) is more sensitive to methodological choices.

These caveats do not undermine the overall case for reform. The weight of evidence across multiple methodologies, data sources, and international comparisons consistently points toward substantial consumer and worker harm from concentration. But precise numbers should be cited with appropriate qualifications, particularly in adversarial settings where opponents will exploit methodological overreach to undermine well-founded conclusions.
