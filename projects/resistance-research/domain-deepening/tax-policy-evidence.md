## Gaps This Document Fills

The Fiscal Reform domain (Domain 5) correctly identifies the tax gap, regressive incidence, the buy-borrow-die strategy, and corporate tax erosion as the central structural problems. What it is thinner on:

- Precise international comparisons placing the US in OECD context — particularly the counterintuitive finding that the US is not a high-tax country by any measure
- The full Saez/Zucman billionaire effective rate finding and the methodological dispute it generated — both the finding and the challenge need to be stated clearly
- The "starve the beast" theory — the proposal implies lower taxes starve public funding, but doesn't cite the empirical literature showing tax cuts actually increase long-run spending
- The elasticity of taxable income literature and what it implies about revenue-maximizing rates — the technical core of supply-side objections
- The European wealth tax failure evidence in full (France's exit effect, Norway's 2022 migration wave) and why this does not defeat the reform case
- The TCJA corporate tax incidence dispute in detail — who bears the corporate tax? — and why the $4,000 wage promise failed
- Tax expenditures as a parallel and underappreciated problem: the $1.8 trillion annual figure and the distributional reality
- Financial transaction taxes: Sweden/UK design divergence — a revenue and market-stability tool the proposal doesn't address
- EITC and negative income tax evidence for the bottom-up distributive case

## Section 1: The US in International Context — A Low-Tax, High-Inequality Outlier

### Total Tax Revenue as a Share of GDP

The United States is among the lowest-taxed wealthy nations in the OECD. The claim that the US is overtaxed relative to peer countries is empirically false.

| Country | Tax-to-GDP Ratio (2024) |
|---|---|
| Denmark | 45.2% |
| France | 43.5% |
| Austria | 43.4% |
| Germany | ~39% |
| United Kingdom | ~35% |
| Canada | ~34% |
| OECD average | 34.1% |
| Australia | ~30% |
| United States | 25.6% |
| Mexico | 18.3% |

The US at 25.6% of GDP sits approximately 8.5 percentage points below the OECD average — a gap worth roughly $2.3 trillion annually at current GDP levels. Only six OECD countries collect less tax as a share of GDP than the US: Chile, Colombia, Costa Rica, Ireland, Mexico, and Türkiye. Among G7 nations alone, the US has the lowest tax-to-GDP ratio.

The US tax-to-GDP ratio has remained near 25–26% for decades, while peer nations at 33–45% fund universal healthcare, subsidized childcare, paid parental leave, active labor market programs, robust pension systems, and free or heavily subsidized public higher education. When out-of-pocket healthcare costs, higher education tuition, and other "private" expenditures for social goods are added to the US tax burden, the total household obligation of a median US family is comparable to or exceeds that of equivalent families in most peer nations — with inferior outcomes.

### Corporate Tax Revenue: A Wider Divergence

The corporate tax comparison is even more stark than the personal income comparison, and it has steepened over time.

| Metric | United States | OECD Average (ex-US) |
|---|---|---|
| Corporate income tax revenue as % of GDP (2021) | 1.6% | 3.2% |
| Statutory corporate rate (post-TCJA 2017) | 21% | ~23% weighted avg |
| OECD average effective tax rate (2024) | ~9–12% for large firms | ~20–22% |

Corporate income tax revenue as a share of US GDP has declined from approximately 4% in the 1960s to 1.6% in 2021 — a halving in relative terms over sixty years. The Tax Policy Center confirms the US raises less corporate tax revenue as a share of GDP than any other G7 country and almost all other OECD members. A 2024 Government Accountability Office analysis found that profitable large US corporations paid an average effective federal tax rate of approximately 9% — less than half the 21% statutory rate. The Institute on Taxation and Economic Policy found that 55 of the largest US corporations paid zero federal income tax on approximately $40 billion in combined profits in at least one recent year.

**The OECD global minimum tax (Pillar Two)**: The OECD Pillar Two framework — a 15% global minimum corporate tax on multinationals with revenues above €750 million — began taking effect in 2024 across approximately 90% of covered multinationals. The US has not implemented the GloBE rules into domestic law. Instead, in 2026 the OECD agreed to a "side-by-side" arrangement extending a safe harbor that effectively exempts US-headquartered multinationals from foreign minimum top-up taxes — a significant retreat from the original framework under US pressure.

### The Compliance Burden as a Regressive Tax

One underappreciated dimension of US tax policy: the compliance burden itself functions as a regressive impost. The US tax code extends to 6,871 pages plus approximately 70,000 pages of regulations, rulings, and guidance. Americans spend an estimated 6.5 billion hours and $400 billion per year on tax compliance (National Taxpayers Union Foundation, 2024) — more than the GDP of 80% of the world's economies. This expenditure produces no goods, no services, and no public benefit.

The burden is regressive in structure: wealthy individuals and large corporations hire specialists to exploit complexity and reduce their effective tax rates; middle-income taxpayers bear the highest compliance costs relative to income; low-income filers entitled to refundable credits (EITC, CTC) face the most complex filing requirements and among the highest audit rates. The IRS audits EITC recipients at roughly the same rate as taxpayers earning over $500,000.

**The pre-populated return comparison**: Sweden's Skatteverket pre-fills most returns using employer and bank data — 74% of Swedes file by SMS or app, average completion time: 3 minutes. Estonia achieves 98% digital filing averaging 3–5 minutes. Denmark's pre-filled returns have a 99% acceptance rate. The IRS's 2024 Direct File pilot — 140,000 taxpayers in 12 states — demonstrated that the technical capability exists for the US to replicate this. The barrier is the tax preparation industry's lobbying, not technical limitation.

Sources: [NTUF: Americans Spend 6.5 Billion Hours on Taxes](https://www.ntu.org/foundation/detail/americans-spend-6-5-billion-hours-on-federal-taxes); [Tax Policy Center: How US Taxes Compare](https://taxpolicycenter.org/briefing-book/how-do-us-taxes-compare-internationally); [IRS: OECD Revenue Statistics Country Note](https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/topics/policy-sub-issues/global-tax-revenues/revenue-statistics-united-states.pdf)

## Section 2: The Saez/Zucman Billionaire Rate Finding — The Evidence and the Dispute

### The Core Finding

Economists Emmanuel Saez (UC Berkeley) and Gabriel Zucman (UC Berkeley) published an analysis showing that in 2018 — the first full year after TCJA — the 400 wealthiest American families paid a combined effective all-in tax rate of approximately 23%, including federal income taxes, state/local taxes, and corporate taxes attributable to their ownership. The bottom half of US households paid approximately 24.2% — a thin margin higher. This was the first time in the data (going back to 1960) that the ultra-wealthy paid a lower effective rate than the working class.

The longer trend line: the effective rate on the top 400 families fell from approximately 56% in 1960, to 47% in 1980, to 30% in 2017, to 23% in 2018. The post-TCJA inflection reflects the corporate rate cut from 35% to 21% and its concentrated benefit to wealthy equity owners.

**The ProPublica IRS data confirmation**: ProPublica's June 2021 analysis using leaked IRS data for the 25 wealthiest Americans calculated a "true tax rate" — taxes paid as a fraction of wealth increase — of 3.4% over 2014–2018 ($13.6 billion in taxes on $401 billion in wealth growth). Elon Musk: 3.27% true tax rate. Jeff Bezos: 0.98% in two years. Carl Icahn: paid zero federal income tax in 2016 and 2017 despite reporting $544 million in adjusted gross income — using loans of approximately $1.2 billion secured against his appreciated assets (the buy-borrow-die strategy in direct form).

These figures are lower than the Saez/Zucman 23% because they use wealth increase — including unrealized appreciation — as the denominator. The traditional income tax system taxes only realized income; the ProPublica "true rate" captures the economic income that goes untaxed.

**Wealth concentration as the underlying structural problem**: Federal Reserve distributional data as of Q1 2024 shows the top 1% hold 30.9% of total household wealth (up from 23% in 1989), while the bottom 50% hold 2.5% (down from 3.4%). The Piketty/Saez/Zucman framework documents that top 1% income share rose from 10% in 1979 to 17.6% in 2019 (excluding capital gains) and to 21.1% including capital gains. Gilens and Page's 2014 study found that policy outcomes in the US correlate strongly with economic elite preferences and show no statistically significant correlation with median voter preferences — establishing the political economy mechanism by which fiscal policy tracks concentrated wealth.

### The Methodological Dispute

The Saez/Zucman finding is contested on specific grounds worth stating plainly:

**The EITC/CTC omission**: Harvard economist Jason Furman and others note that Saez/Zucman's analysis of lower-income households does not include refundable tax credits — the EITC and Child Tax Credit — which represent real income and real tax relief for lower-income working families. Including these would reduce the effective rate of lower-income households and widen the gap in their analysis. This is a genuine methodological point, though it strengthens rather than weakens the finding that the wealthy pay less.

**The Auten/Splinter counter-analysis**: Gerald Auten (Treasury) and David Splinter (JCT) published a 2024 paper arguing that properly accounting for government transfers and adjusting for changing household composition, US income inequality has not risen as dramatically as Piketty, Saez, and Zucman find. Their analysis uses different assumptions about how to attribute undistributed corporate profits and government transfers. The debate is unresolved in the academic literature; most analysts accept that the directional PSZ findings are correct even if precise magnitudes are contested.

**The corporate tax incidence issue**: The 23% effective rate includes corporate income taxes attributed to wealthy families' ownership stakes. This requires assumptions about who bears the corporate tax (see Section 5). Under different incidence assumptions, the calculation changes — but the directional result (effective rates fall sharply at the top) is robust across incidence assumptions.

**Bottom line**: The specific numbers are contested; the directional finding is not. No serious analyst disputes that the tax system is less progressive in practice than its nominal rate structure suggests, that unrealized gains constitute the dominant form of elite wealth accumulation and go untaxed indefinitely, or that the buy-borrow-die strategy eliminates tax on a substantial fraction of billionaire fortunes.

## Section 3: The Buy-Borrow-Die Strategy and Mark-to-Market Reform

### How the Strategy Works

The buy-borrow-die mechanism is not aggressive tax planning — it is the US tax code functioning as written. The three steps:

**1. Buy**: Accumulate wealth in appreciating assets — equity stakes in private companies, publicly traded stocks, real estate, art. Unrealized appreciation is not taxable income under US law. A founder whose startup appreciates from $1 million to $10 billion owes no federal income tax on the $9.999 billion gain until and unless they sell.

**2. Borrow**: Use appreciated assets as collateral for loans — Securities-Backed Lines of Credit (SBLOCs) from brokerage firms, or direct portfolio loans from wealth managers. Loan proceeds are not income; they are debt. The borrower pays single-digit interest (often 1–3% for very large loans), which may be partially deductible. Living expenses — houses, jets, staff, travel — are funded from borrowed money, with no tax event. Carl Icahn's documented $1.2 billion loan from Bank of America, secured against his equity positions while paying zero income tax on $544 million in reported AGI, is the strategy in concrete form.

**3. Die**: Heirs receive appreciated assets with a "stepped-up basis" — cost basis is reset to the asset's fair market value at death, permanently erasing the embedded capital gain. The $9.999 billion appreciation on the hypothetical founder's stake disappears from the tax base at death, never taxed.

**> Evidence callout**: The stepped-up basis costs approximately $40–50 billion per year in foregone federal revenue (CRS estimate; NBC News/Biden Treasury analysis). Over 75% of this benefit accrues to the wealthiest estates. Combined with the unlimited deferral of unrealized gains, the buy-borrow-die strategy effectively creates a zero-capital-gains-tax system for those wealthy enough to sustain themselves on borrowed money secured against perpetually appreciating assets.

### Mark-to-Market and Billionaire Minimum Tax Options

**Mark-to-market taxation**: Treat unrealized appreciation in publicly traded securities as taxable income in the year it accrues, as is already the treatment for futures contracts, certain dealer inventory, and foreign currency positions. For publicly traded assets — which constitute the majority of ultra-high-net-worth portfolios by value — mark-to-market is administratively trivial. Prices are public, tracked daily, and already reported to the IRS on 1099-B forms. No new reporting infrastructure is required.

The harder case — closely held businesses, private equity, real estate — requires valuation. Options: (1) carryover of accruals with interest until sale or death (pay the deferred mark-to-market tax at eventual realization); (2) apply mark-to-market only to publicly traded holdings and liquid assets above a threshold; (3) a deemed-realization trigger at death with an installment payment option for illiquid assets.

**The Biden Billionaire Minimum Income Tax approach**: Rather than pure mark-to-market, the Biden proposal required a 25% minimum tax on "total income" — traditional taxable income plus unrealized capital gains — for taxpayers with net worth above $100 million (phased in between $100M–$200M). Treasury estimated this would raise $361 billion over 10 years (approximately $36B/year). OMB estimates ranged to $517 billion.

**Congressional Research Service framing**: The CRS notes that mark-to-market addresses the two fundamental privileges of capital income: deferral (taxing gains only at realization, not accrual) and the forgiveness at death (stepped-up basis). Eliminating only the stepped-up basis (without mark-to-market) raises $40–50B/year but leaves the deferral privilege intact. A full mark-to-market regime raises substantially more and eliminates the buy-borrow-die strategy structurally.

## Section 4: The Wealth Tax Debate — European Evidence Assessed

### The Revenue Case

The Piketty/Saez/Zucman wealth concentration data establishes the scale of the target. Federal Reserve data shows the top 1% holding 30.9% of total household wealth as of Q1 2024, up from 23% in 1989. The top 0.1% holds approximately 22% (Saez/Zucman 2014 estimates). The bottom 50% holds 2.5%.

The Saez/Zucman 2021 revenue estimate for a graduated wealth tax (2% on wealth above $50 million, 3% above $1 billion): approximately $200–220 billion per year. A 2% annual levy on the estimated $4.5 trillion in wealth held above the $50 million threshold would raise the lower end of that range. This exceeds the entire federal education budget.

Importantly, a wealth tax addresses a gap that income taxes structurally cannot fill: accumulated wealth held in appreciating assets that generates returns (in the form of access to borrowing, social capital, and eventual inheritance) but whose underlying principal faces no annual tax obligation. A billionaire who earns zero taxable income in a year by living off borrowed money secured against a $10 billion equity stake is completely outside the income tax base.

### The European Counter-Evidence

The European experience with wealth taxes deserves full engagement rather than dismissal:

**France's ISF** (*Impôt de Solidarité sur la Fortune*, 1988–2017): Applied to net wealth above approximately €1.3 million at rates of 0.5–1.5%. By 2015, approximately 10,000 millionaires were leaving France annually — 7,000 from Paris alone, approximately 6% of the city's millionaires. GDP growth was estimated to be reduced by 0.2% per year attributable to the tax. The tax was generating approximately €4 billion annually but losing substantial income tax, corporate tax, and consumption tax revenue from departing taxpayers. It was repealed in 2017 by Macron and replaced with a narrower tax on real estate wealth only.

**Norway's 2022 experience**: Norway raised its wealth tax rate from 0.85% to 1.1% in 2022. In the subsequent 12 months, approximately 30 large taxpayers — representing an estimated $54 billion in combined net worth — emigrated, primarily to Switzerland. The associated loss in tax revenue was estimated at $594 million — exceeding the incremental revenue the rate increase was projected to raise. Prior to 2022, emigration by large taxpayers had been extremely rare: the 2022 figure exceeded the total for the prior 13 years combined.

**The broader European retreat**: More than a dozen European countries adopted and then repealed wealth taxes across the 1990s–2010s, including Austria, Denmark, Finland, France, Germany, Iceland, Ireland, Italy, the Netherlands, Luxembourg, and Sweden. As of 2025, only Norway, Spain, and Switzerland levy a net wealth tax among OECD members.

Sources: [Tax Foundation: Wealth Tax Impact](https://taxfoundation.org/research/all/eu/wealth-tax-impact/); [Brussels Report: Failure of Norway's Wealth Tax as Warning](https://www.brusselsreport.eu/2024/09/11/the-failure-of-norways-wealth-tax-hike-as-a-warning-signal/); [Tax Foundation: Wealth Taxes in Europe 2025](https://taxfoundation.org/data/all/eu/wealth-taxes-europe/); [Common Dreams: Norway Isn't Bowing to Threats of Capital Flight](https://www.commondreams.org/opinion/norway-exit-tax); [AInvest: Impact of Wealth Taxes on Capital Flight — Norway](https://www.ainvest.com/news/impact-wealth-taxes-capital-flight-entrepreneurship-lessons-norway-experience-2512/)

---

### Why the European Evidence Doesn't Defeat the Reform Case

**> Evidence callout**: The European wealth tax failures share design features that a well-structured US proposal can avoid. France's ISF and most of the repealed European taxes: (1) applied at low thresholds (€1–2 million), capturing upper-middle-class professionals, small business owners, and retirees alongside the ultra-wealthy; (2) excluded financial assets held through certain corporate structures while taxing operating businesses, creating perverse incentives to restructure; (3) operated in the EU/Schengen area, where emigration requires no new visa, language is shared across borders, and Switzerland offers ready sanctuary with bilateral agreements; (4) lacked complementary exit taxes or deemed-disposition rules to prevent untaxed departure.

The US context differs structurally:
- **Worldwide taxation**: US citizens and permanent residents pay US tax on worldwide income regardless of residency. This is legally unique among major economies. Moving to Switzerland does not exempt a US citizen from US taxes.
- **Exit tax (Section 877A)**: Covered expatriates — those renouncing citizenship or long-term residency with net worth above $2 million or average annual net income tax above $190,000 — are subject to a mark-to-market deemed-disposition tax on all assets at the time of expatriation. This provides a foundation for extending deemed disposition to wealth tax avoidance.
- **Threshold design**: The Saez/Zucman $50 million threshold captures a few thousand families — not millions of professionals and small business owners. Norway's 1.1% rate applied starting at a net worth of approximately $171,000 (2022 conversion), capturing a fundamentally different population.

The honest assessment: design matters enormously. A US wealth tax should include: high thresholds, exit taxes on unrealized gains at expatriation, coordination with FATCA/CRS reporting, and possibly exclusions for illiquid operating businesses below a liquidity threshold. None of this is insurmountable. The European evidence proves that bad designs fail; it does not prove that good designs fail.

## Section 5: Corporate Tax Incidence — Who Bears the Burden?

### The Official Assumptions vs. the Empirical Literature

The question of who ultimately bears the economic burden of the corporate income tax — capital owners or workers — is technically contested and politically consequential. It determines how distributional analyses assign TCJA's benefits and whether the $4,000 wage claim had any theoretical foundation.

**Official agency assumptions**:
- Congressional Budget Office: ~75% on capital (shareholders), ~25% on labor (workers through lower wages)
- Treasury Department: 82% on capital, 18% on labor
- Joint Committee on Taxation: 75% capital, 25% labor

These assumptions derive largely from the closed-economy Harberger (1962) model, where capital cannot relocate internationally.

**The open-economy empirical challenge**: More recent empirical work using state- and country-level variation finds that labor may bear substantially more of the corporate tax burden. In an open economy where capital is internationally mobile, a corporate tax in one jurisdiction reduces investment there, reducing labor demand and wages — capital flows out, and workers absorb the incidence. Estimates from open-economy models range from 50% to 73%+ of the corporate tax burden falling on labor. The Tax Foundation's preferred estimates assign the majority of burden to labor. The Mercatus Center finds similar results.

**The resolution**: The debate turns on how mobile capital actually is. For very large multinationals with complex profit-shifting capabilities, capital is highly mobile and labor bears more of the burden. For domestic corporations without offshore options, capital mobility is lower and the traditional CBO assumption holds better. The empirical literature has not converged on a single estimate, and the question is genuinely unresolved. A 50/50 split is probably as defensible as 75/25 for the aggregate corporate sector.

Sources: [CBO: How CBO Projects Corporate Income Tax Revenues (October 2023)](https://www.cbo.gov/system/files/2023-10/59436-Corporate-Tax-Revenues.pdf); [Tax Policy Center: Who Bears the Burden of Corporate Income Tax?](https://taxpolicycenter.org/briefing-book/who-bears-burden-corporate-income-tax); [Mercatus Center: Who Really Pays? Reevaluating the Corporate Tax Burden](https://www.mercatus.org/research/policy-briefs/who-really-pays-reevaluating-corporate-tax-burden); [CRS: Overview of the Corporate Income Tax System (2023)](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R47519)

### The $4,000 Wage Promise vs. the Evidence

The Trump Council of Economic Advisers projected in October 2017 that TCJA's corporate provisions would raise average household wages by a "conservative" $4,000–9,000 annually. The theoretical mechanism: corporate tax cuts raise the return on capital, attract additional investment, increase the capital-to-labor ratio, and ultimately raise labor productivity and wages. Even on the labor-incidence framework, this is a long-run effect requiring capital accumulation — not an immediate result.

**What happened**:

In 2018, US corporations spent approximately $800 billion–$1 trillion on stock buybacks — roughly double the pre-TCJA historical average. The S&P 500's buyback yield reached record levels. By contrast, capital investment growth in 2018 was modest: large firms spent less than 20% of their TCJA tax savings on new capital investment and R&D. Between 2018 and 2022, 280 large US corporations spent a combined $2.7 trillion on buybacks — 98% more than in the comparable pre-TCJA period.

On wages:
- A 2022 study by Joint Committee on Taxation and Federal Reserve researchers found that earnings for workers in the bottom 90% of the within-firm income distribution did not change following TCJA. Wage increases concentrated in the top 10% of earners within firms, and particularly sharply among executives and managers.
- A 2024 study by Patrick J. Kennedy et al. (JCT) reached the same conclusion: bottom-90% workers saw no wage gains from the corporate rate cut; top earners did.
- Median wages showed nominal increases in 2018–2019 that were largely offset by healthcare and energy cost inflation.

**The honest middle ground**: TCJA's immediate expensing provision did accelerate business equipment investment in 2018–2019, consistent with standard economic predictions. Some firm-level wage effects are real at the top. But the broad-based $4,000 wage gain for working families did not materialize, consistent with the CBO/Treasury assumption that capital, not labor, captures most of the incidence.

## Section 6: Tax Expenditures — The Hidden Budget

### Scope and Scale

Tax expenditures — revenue forgone through deductions, exclusions, credits, and preferential rates — are economically equivalent to direct spending but escape annual appropriations scrutiny almost entirely. The Joint Committee on Taxation estimates federal tax expenditures for fiscal year 2024 in the range of $1.8 trillion annually — exceeding combined domestic discretionary spending and approaching the entire defense budget ($860 billion discretionary).

The largest categories (approximate FY2024):

### Distributional Reality: Who Gets What

**Retirement savings ($397B)**: CBO analysis found that 77% of individuals in the highest income quintile benefit from retirement-related tax expenditures, compared to only 19% of those in the bottom quintile. Of $201.6 billion in retirement tax expenditures analyzed, the top quintile received 63% of the benefit ($126.7 billion); the bottom quintile received 1% ($1.4 billion). The mechanism is structural: higher earners are more likely to have employers offering plans, more likely to reach contribution limits, and face higher marginal rates that increase the deduction's value. Lower-income workers often cannot spare the income to contribute.

**> Evidence callout**: The 401k/IRA tax expenditure is the single largest item in the federal tax code at $397B/year. Its benefits are concentrated at the top: the wealthiest fifth of earners receives 63% of the subsidy. The bottom fifth receives 1%. This is not a middle-class program — it is a savings subsidy for those already able to save substantially, structured in a way that amplifies advantage for those with higher marginal rates and more disposable income.

**Mortgage interest deduction ($25B)**: Taxpayers earning above $200,000 in AGI represent 49% of all claimants but capture approximately 71% of total benefit. Most lower-income households take the standard deduction and receive no benefit from the mortgage deduction. Research consistently finds little effect on homeownership rates from the deduction — its primary effect is to raise house prices and purchase amounts for buyers who would buy regardless, providing windfall gains to existing homeowners.

**SALT deduction**: Pre-TCJA, 71% of SALT deduction benefits accrued to households earning above $200,000. The TCJA cap at $10,000 (2017) reduced upward skew but created new political conflict — the "One Big Beautiful Bill" 2025 proposals to raise or eliminate the SALT cap would primarily benefit households earning $200,000–$500,000 in high-tax states like New York, New Jersey, and California.

**The EITC and CTC as contrast**: The Earned Income Tax Credit ($64B) and Child Tax Credit (partially refundable) are strongly progressive — cash transfers to working and lower-middle-income families. In 2024, the EITC lifted approximately 4.4 million people above the poverty line, including 2.3 million children. Together, the EITC and CTC lifted approximately 6.4 million people out of poverty in 2023. This is a different category of tax expenditure: genuinely redistributive in design and effect.

## Section 7: The Tax Gap — $688 Billion and the Enforcement Reversal

### Scale and Distribution

The IRS estimates the gross tax gap — taxes legally owed but not voluntarily paid — at $688 billion for tax year 2021. The net gap (after enforcement and late payments) is approximately $606 billion. This figure has nearly doubled from the 2001 estimate of $345 billion, outpacing inflation and GDP growth.

The distributional pattern reflects deliberate system design:

| Income Type | Compliance Rate | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| Wages and salaries | >99% | Mandatory third-party withholding and W-2 reporting |
| Investment income (dividends, interest) | ~95% | Mandatory third-party 1099 reporting |
| Self-employment income | ~68% | No third-party verification |
| Partnership and S-Corp income | ~60–70% | Complex pass-through structures, limited IRS capacity |
| Offshore accounts (pre-FATCA) | Variable/unknown | No automatic domestic reporting |

Workers' incomes are collected at source before they see a paycheck. Wealthy taxpayers' incomes from partnerships, S-corporations, closely held businesses, and offshore accounts have compliance rates of 45–83% — a gap that represents both genuine evasion and legal-but-aggressive avoidance through complex structures.

**The Guyton et al. finding**: John Guyton and colleagues (2021, NBER Working Paper 28542, *Tax Evasion at the Top of the Income Distribution*) estimated that standard IRS audit methods systematically miss evasion by high-income filers because random audits don't detect offshore accounts and complex pass-through structures. Applying targeted corrections, they found that the top 1% fail to report approximately 21% of their income, accounting for roughly $160–175 billion in annual unpaid taxes. The top 0.1%'s underreported income is multiplied by 1.8 when these adjustments are applied.

### IRS Funding: The Investment Made and Then Reversed

The Inflation Reduction Act (2022) allocated $80 billion in supplemental IRS funding over ten years: $45.6 billion for enforcement, $25.3 billion for operations support, $4.8 billion for systems modernization, and $3.2 billion for taxpayer services. Treasury's projected return: $561 billion in additional revenue over 2024–2034, an approximately 7:1 ROI. If renewed after the initial period, estimated revenues would reach $851 billion.

**Early results before reversal**: By fall 2024, the Biden IRS had announced collection of $1.3 billion from high-income, high-wealth individuals: $172 million from 21,000 individual filers, $520 million from a large partnerships initiative.

**The funding rescissions**: Three successive congressional acts clawed back the IRA enforcement appropriation:
- Fiscal Responsibility Act of 2023: rescinded $1.4 billion
- Further Consolidated Appropriations Act (2024): rescinded $20.2 billion
- Full-Year Continuing Appropriations and Extensions Act (2025): rescinded another $20.2 billion

Total rescissions: approximately $41.8 billion — more than half the original IRA allocation, before enforcement work could scale to its projected capacity.

**The ROI dispute**: House Ways and Means Republicans testified in 2025 that actual FY2024 enforcement ROI was below 1:1 for some programs. The Yale Budget Lab's analysis supports substantially positive long-run ROI. The dispute is partly a time-horizon argument: enforcement actions against large partnerships and offshore accounts typically take 3–7 years from initiation to collection. Evaluating FY2024 ROI on enforcement funding appropriated in FY2022 compares early costs against early returns while the full multi-year return remains uncollected. Long-run CBO/Treasury estimates remain consistently positive at $5–12 per dollar invested.

**> Evidence callout**: The $41.8 billion in IRS funding rescissions represent a deliberate choice to allow $600+ billion per year in unpaid taxes to go uncollected. At the conservative CBO/Treasury ROI of $5:1, the rescinded enforcement funding would have generated approximately $200 billion in additional annual revenue at scale. This is not a spending reduction — it is a transfer of $200B/year from general taxpayers (who will need to fund the deficit through future taxes or debt) to the high-income non-compliers who disproportionately avoid payment.

## Section 8: The "Starve the Beast" Theory — Empirical Failure

### What the Theory Claims

The "starve the beast" argument — prominent in conservative fiscal policy since Reagan — holds that cutting taxes reduces government revenue, which creates fiscal pressure that eventually forces Congress to cut spending as deficits become politically untenable. On this theory, tax cuts are a mechanism for reducing the size of government; deficit spending is a transitional pain before the spending squeeze forces restraint.

This theory has justified major tax cuts without offsetting spending reductions: the Reagan cuts (1981), the Bush cuts (2001 and 2003), and TCJA (2017). In each case, proponents argued that the deficit created would be "taken care of" by eventual spending discipline.

### What the Evidence Shows

The empirical literature is unambiguous in rejecting the prediction.

William Niskanen's 2006 Cato Institute paper — a critic of the theory from within the libertarian tradition — found the opposite of the predicted relationship. Controlling for the unemployment rate, federal spending from 1981 to 2000 increased by approximately 0.5% of GDP for every 1 percentage point decline in tax revenue as a share of GDP. Tax cuts were followed by higher spending, not lower. Niskanen identified the mechanism: when taxes are cut without reducing spending, the perceived cost of government falls — voters experience the benefit of programs but attribute less of the cost to current taxation, reducing political pressure to cut spending.

Romer and Romer's Berkeley analysis (*Do Tax Cuts Starve the Beast?*, examining tax changes 1945–2007) found "no evidence that tax cuts lead to reductions in government spending."

**The post-TCJA confirmation**: Federal spending as a share of GDP in FY2019 — the last pre-COVID year — was higher than in any year of the Obama administration. The TCJA added approximately $1.5–1.9 trillion to the national debt over its first decade without producing commensurate spending reductions.

**The long-run US record**: Since 1981, top marginal income tax rates have fallen from 70% to 37%, the corporate rate from 48% to 21%, and capital gains rates from 28% (1986) to 15–20%. Over this same period, federal debt held by the public grew from roughly 25% of GDP to over 95% of GDP. The supply-side prediction — that lower taxes would generate growth sufficient to reduce deficits — has not materialized at the aggregate level.

Sources: [Niskanen (2006): Limiting Government — The Failure of Starve the Beast (Cato)](https://www.cato.org/sites/cato.org/files/serials/files/cato-journal/2006/11/cj26n3-8.pdf); [Berkeley (Romer): Do Tax Cuts Starve the Beast?](https://eml.berkeley.edu/~dromer/papers/draft509.pdf); [Tax Policy Center: Starving the Beast — TCJA Is Feeding It](https://taxpolicycenter.org/taxvox/starving-beast-tax-cuts-and-jobs-act-seems-be-feeding-it); [Ballotpedia: Starve-the-Beast Theory](https://ballotpedia.org/Starve-the-beast)

## Section 9: The Elasticity of Taxable Income and Revenue-Maximizing Rates

### The Technical Framework

Supply-side objections to high marginal income taxes have a technically respectable form in the elasticity of taxable income (ETI). ETI measures how responsive reported taxable income is to changes in marginal rates: if ETI is 0.5, a 1% increase in the net-of-tax rate (1 minus the marginal rate) raises reported income by 0.5%. High ETI means raising rates reduces the tax base substantially; low ETI means raising rates captures most of the mechanical revenue.

Saez's 2001 paper (*Using Elasticities to Derive Optimal Income Tax Rates*, Review of Economic Studies) established the key formula for the revenue-maximizing top rate: t* = 1 / (1 + a*e), where a describes the shape of the top-income distribution and e is the ETI. For typical top-income distribution parameters (a ≈ 2) and ETI = 0.25: t* = 1 / (1 + 2×0.25) = 73%. At ETI = 0.5: t* ≈ 56%.

**The ETI literature**: The comprehensive review by Saez, Slemrod, and Giertz (2012, *Journal of Economic Literature*) found ETI estimates ranging from 0.1 to 0.5, with a central estimate for broad income of approximately 0.25 for higher-income taxpayers. Critically, the authors distinguish between real behavioral responses (working fewer hours, reducing effort) and avoidance responses (income shifting, tax sheltering). Most of the measured ETI at high incomes reflects avoidance behavior — behavior that base-broadening reform can largely eliminate, not fundamental changes in labor supply.

**The policy implication**: An income tax reform that simultaneously broadens the base (eliminates sheltering opportunities) and raises rates can achieve higher revenue with a lower behavioral cost than the ETI literature suggests under current law. This is exactly what the 1986 Tax Reform Act demonstrated: eliminating deductions and loopholes while reducing the statutory top rate to 28% — a revenue-neutral simplification that reduced avoidance responses.

Sources: [Saez (2001): Using Elasticities to Derive Optimal Income Tax Rates](https://eml.berkeley.edu/~saez/derive.pdf); [Saez, Slemrod, Giertz (2012): ETI — A Critical Review (JEL)](https://eml.berkeley.edu/~saez/saez-slemrod-giertzJEL12.pdf); [NBER Working Paper 17616: Optimal Taxation of Top Labor Incomes](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w17616/w17616.pdf); [IMF: Are Elasticities of Taxable Income Rising?](https://www.imf.org/-/media/Files/Publications/WP/2018/wp18132.ashx)

### Historical Evidence: High Rates and Growth

The top US marginal income tax rate was 91% from 1951 to 1963, fell to 70% from 1965 to 1981, was cut to 50% in 1981 (Reagan), and to 28% in 1986 (Tax Reform Act), before rising to 39.6% in 1993. Current top rate: 37%.

The 1950s evidence is frequently invoked in both directions. Key facts:

- Real GDP growth averaged 4.2% annually in the 1950s, alongside a 91% top marginal rate.
- The top 1% of taxpayers paid approximately 42% of their income in effective federal income taxes in the 1950s — not 91%, because the statutory rate applied only to income above approximately $200,000 (roughly $2 million in 2024 dollars), and substantial deduction and sheltering opportunities existed.
- A Congressional Research Service analysis found that the statistical association between top marginal income tax rates and real GDP growth rates is not statistically significant across the postwar period.
- Countries with significantly higher total tax burdens — Sweden (42%), Denmark (45%), Germany (39%) — have maintained comparable or stronger productivity growth, human development indices, and median living standards relative to the US in recent decades.

The honest reading: the 1950s don't prove that 91% rates cause growth; they prove such rates are compatible with strong growth. The ETI literature, correctly applied, suggests revenue-maximizing rates well above current US levels (56–73%), with base-broadening as the complementary tool that lowers behavioral costs.

**The interstate migration evidence (state-level)**: A related argument is that high state income taxes drive wealthy residents to zero-income-tax states. The academic literature is more measured than the anecdotal evidence. Kleven et al.'s research on top-earner migration finds larger behavioral responses in Europe (where international migration requires no visa or language change) than in the US. US state-to-state migration of high earners in response to tax differentials is real but modest — overwhelmingly dominated by retirement, family, and lifestyle factors rather than tax optimization. A study of New Jersey's millionaire surtax found fewer than 100 high earners departed in the year of enactment, with revenue gains substantially exceeding projected migration costs.

**The Reagan cut: what actually explains the 1980s expansion**: The 1981 Economic Recovery Tax Act is frequently cited as proof that cutting top rates from 70% to 50% produces growth. The academic evidence does not support monocausal attribution: the 1983–1989 expansion was driven primarily by Federal Reserve monetary easing after the Volcker disinflation, a recovery from the 1981–82 recession (the deepest since WWII), and increased defense spending. The tax cuts were partially reversed in 1982 (TEFRA — one of the largest peacetime tax increases in history) as deficits widened, suggesting supply-side revenue feedback did not materialize as claimed.

Sources: [Roosevelt Institute: Effective Progressive Tax Rates in the 1950s](https://rooseveltinstitute.org/blog/effective-progressive-tax-rates-in-the-1950s/); [CRS: Comparison of Historical Top Tax Rates With GDP Growth](https://www.taxnotes.com/research/federal/legislative-documents/congressional-research-service-reports/crs-updates-comparison-of-historical-top-tax-rates-with-rate/vpl8); [EconLib: How Did We Get Good Growth in the 1950s?](https://www.econlib.org/how-did-we-get-good-growth-in-the-1950s-despite-high-marginal-tax-rates/); [Manhattan Institute: Revisiting High Tax Rates of the 1950s](https://media4.manhattan-institute.org/pdf/ib_19.pdf); [AEI: Why We Can't Go Back to 1950s Tax Rates](https://www.aei.org/economics/public-economics/why-we-cant-go-back-to-sky-high-1950s-tax-rates/)

## Section 10: The EITC as Evidence for Refundable Credits

### What the EITC Demonstrates

The Earned Income Tax Credit is the most evidence-supported anti-poverty intervention in the US federal toolkit. It approximates Milton Friedman's proposed negative income tax: a refundable credit that phases in with earned income (creating work incentives at the low end), reaches a maximum, and then phases out. Key 2024 metrics:

- 23 million filers claimed $64 billion in EITC benefits in the 2023 tax year. Average credit: $2,743.
- The EITC lifted approximately 4.4 million people above the poverty line in 2024 (2.3 million children) using the Supplemental Poverty Measure.
- Together with the Child Tax Credit, the EITC lifted approximately 6.4 million people out of poverty in 2023.
- Studies link $1,000 increases in EITC to a 7.3 percentage point increase in employment and a 9.4 percentage point reduction in poverty rates for eligible families.
- Downstream outcomes: children in EITC-eligible families show improved health outcomes, higher educational attainment, and higher adult earnings — persistent effects across the life course.

The EITC has been expanded under every president since Carter (including Reagan, Bush, and Clinton) and enjoys cross-partisan support that almost no other poverty-reduction program achieves. Its political durability reflects the work-incentive framing: benefits increase with employment and wages, creating a constituency that frames recipients as workers rather than beneficiaries.

**The negative income tax lineage**: Milton Friedman proposed a negative income tax (NIT) in 1962 as a replacement for the entire welfare system — a universal floor below which no citizen's income could fall, delivered through the tax system. The EITC is a partial implementation of this idea for working families with children. The contrast between the EITC's bipartisan success and the broader NIT's political failure illustrates the work-conditionality constraint in US policy: unconditional income support remains politically difficult despite strong evidence from pilot programs (Finland's 2017–2018 UBI experiment found improved wellbeing and modest employment effects; Stockton SEED's 2019–2021 guaranteed income pilot found reduced income volatility and improved full-time employment outcomes).

**State EITC programs**: 31 states and the District of Columbia have enacted their own EITC programs that supplement the federal credit, ranging from 3% (Montana) to 100% (California for some filers) of the federal credit. State EITCs provide natural experiments in credit generosity. ITEP's 2024 analysis finds state EITCs collectively provide an additional $6.6 billion annually to working families, with strongest impact in states with higher supplemental rates.

**Limitations**: The EITC phase-out creates high marginal effective tax rates for earners moving from the peak to the phase-out range (effectively 21–36% above the normal rate). Adults without children receive a substantially smaller credit ($649 maximum in 2024 for childless workers). The American Rescue Plan's 2021 temporary expansion of the childless worker credit — raising the maximum to $1,502 — was not made permanent. The program has no benefit for those outside the labor market entirely — it is an in-work subsidy, not a floor for non-workers.

Sources: [CBPP: The Earned Income Tax Credit](https://www.cbpp.org/research/federal-tax/the-earned-income-tax-credit); [SRCD: EITC Reduces Poverty (2025 brief)](https://www.srcd.org/sites/default/files/2025-06/Child%20Policy%20Brief_EITC_06302025.pdf); [CRS: The EITC — An Economic Analysis](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R44057); [NBER: EITC and the Distribution of Income](https://www.nber.org/papers/w21340); [ITEP: State EITCs 2024](https://itep.org/state-earned-income-tax-credits-2024/)

## Section 11: Financial Transaction Taxes — Design Determines Outcome

### The Revenue Potential and the Design Problem

A financial transaction tax (FTT) — a small percentage levy on trades of stocks, bonds, or derivatives — has been proposed as both a revenue source ($50–200B+ annually at 0.1% on equities) and a mechanism for reducing high-frequency trading (HFT) externalities. The design question is dispositive: the same tax at the same rate can either raise billions annually with minimal market impact, or cause most taxable trading to migrate offshore.

### Sweden's Failure vs. the UK's Success

The Sweden/UK contrast is the most instructive natural experiment in FTT design:

**Sweden (1984–1991)**: Applied a 0.5% tax on purchases and sales of equity securities, payable only on transactions executed through registered Swedish brokerage houses. The result was immediate and catastrophic: within two years, more than 50% of Swedish equity trading had migrated to London. By 1990, approximately 60% of Swedish equity volume was executed offshore. Revenue from the equity FTT: approximately 50 million Swedish kronor annually — less than 4% of the 1,500 million projected. The fixed-income securities FTT raised less than 80 million SEK in any year against a 1,500 million projection. The tax was repealed in 1990. The lesson: a tax that applies only to transactions through domestic brokers is trivially avoidable.

**UK stamp duty (1986–present)**: Levied at 0.5% on equity purchases. The critical design difference: the UK tax exempts intermediaries — market makers and dealers — from the tax on their purchasing transactions. Only the final investor's purchase from an intermediary is taxed. This exempts market-making activity from double-taxation and prevents the wholesale migration of liquidity provision to offshore venues. The UK stamp duty has raised approximately £3–4 billion annually with reasonable consistency (£3.52 billion in FY2017-18) and has not produced significant trading migration.

**Design principles from the comparison**: (1) Apply the tax to the investor, not to every transaction in the chain — this preserves market-making liquidity; (2) include non-equity instruments (derivatives, futures, options) proportionally to prevent substitution; (3) implement as a residence-based tax on US investors regardless of execution venue, to prevent offshore migration. A US FTT modeled on the UK structure, extended to derivatives at lower rates (0.005–0.01% on notional value), is estimated by urban institute analysts to raise $50–100 billion annually with manageable market impact.

## Section 12: Carbon Taxation — Revenue, Incidence, and Political Economy

### The British Columbia and Sweden Evidence

British Columbia's carbon tax — operational since 2008 — is the most studied carbon pricing system in North America. Starting at $10 CAD per tonne, it rose to $65 CAD/tonne by 2020. Studies examining BC through 2015 found 5–15% reductions in fuel consumption and GHG emissions compared to counterfactual emissions. Metcalf (2019) preferred estimates cluster around 5–8% aggregate emissions reduction. The BC economy's GDP growth matched or exceeded the Canadian average during the tax period.

**Limitation**: Pretis (2019) found that Metcalf's aggregate emissions estimates are sensitive to specification and may not be statistically significant at conventional levels. The honest characterization: BC demonstrates that a modest carbon price reduces fuel consumption and is compatible with economic growth, but strong causal identification of the aggregate emissions effect remains contested.

**Sweden's stronger evidence**: Sweden's carbon tax at approximately $130/ton — among the world's highest — has been operational since 1991. Sweden's emissions fell approximately 27% between 1991 and 2018 while the economy grew 83%. Sweden's carbon tax provides the strongest evidence that carbon pricing at meaningful rates is compatible with strong long-run economic performance.

### Household Incidence and the Dividend

Carbon taxes are regressive in their direct incidence — lower-income households spend a higher fraction of income on energy. The correction is the per-capita dividend: returning revenue equally per person makes the net policy effect progressive.

Under Canada's federal carbon pricing backstop (implemented 2019), provinces without their own pricing system receive the Canada Carbon Rebate — approximately 90% of revenue returned to households equally per capita. Households earning below $40,000 receive approximately CAD $1,000–1,600 net positive per year. The bottom 60% of households are net beneficiaries.

**Political economy**: Carbon tax opposition has been mobilized effectively around energy price impacts on working-class households — a real concern under a non-dividend design. The dividend model neutralizes this concern in aggregate while requiring political communication infrastructure to make the rebate check visible. Alaska's Permanent Fund (oil revenue dividends since 1982, averaging approximately $1,600/person/year) demonstrates the political durability of per-capita dividends: they create a constituency for continuation that has survived multiple gubernatorial administrations.

At $100/ton on approximately 5 billion metric tonnes of US CO2 emissions, gross revenue would be approximately $500 billion annually. With 100% per-capita dividend: approximately $1,500/person ($6,000 for a family of four) at initial rates. As emissions decline (the policy's intended effect), revenue and dividends decline proportionally — but avoided climate damage far exceeds declining revenue.

## Section 13: The Estate Tax — Concentrated Wealth and the "Death Tax" Framing

### Scale and Erosion

The federal estate tax applies to estates valued above a threshold at death. At its most progressive (1970s), the estate tax applied to estates above approximately $60,000 (equivalent to roughly $500,000 today) at graduated rates up to 77%. The current (post-TCJA) exemption is $13.61 million per individual ($27.22 million per married couple) — the highest in the tax's history. The top rate is 40%.

The practical result: the estate tax now affects fewer than 1 in 1,000 estates. The Tax Policy Center estimates that in 2023, approximately 3,800 estates were taxable — roughly 0.12% of all deaths. The estate tax raised approximately $31 billion in FY2023 — a meaningful sum but a thin fraction of annual wealth transfer, estimated at $70–100 trillion over the next three decades as Baby Boomers pass assets.

**The "death tax" framing**: The estate tax was successfully rebranded as a "death tax" in the 1990s, with particular focus on family farms and small businesses. The Tax Policy Center's data shows that fewer than 80 estates per year nationwide face estate tax liability while also holding closely held farms or small businesses insufficient to cover the tax liability without forcing a sale — the specific harm the "family farm" argument invokes. The vast majority of estate tax liability falls on large investment portfolios, real estate holdings, and business interests where the heirs are not active operators.

**Revenue potential at reform parameters**: Restoring the estate tax to 2009 parameters ($3.5 million exemption, 45% top rate) would affect approximately 0.3% of estates and raise an estimated $85–100 billion over a decade. A more progressive reform with graduated rates above $10 million (the Sanders "For the 99.8%" proposal) would raise substantially more while affecting fewer than 0.2% of estates.

**The stepped-up basis connection**: The estate tax and the stepped-up basis interact: the estate tax theoretically captures some of the unrealized appreciation at death, but with a $13.61 million exemption, most of the wealth that benefits from stepped-up basis (estates between $1 million and $13 million in appreciated assets) faces no estate tax at all. Eliminating the stepped-up basis would capture the tax that the eroded estate tax no longer reaches.

## Section 14: International Profit Shifting and Corporate Tax Avoidance

### Scope of the Problem

US multinational corporations shift substantial profits to low-tax jurisdictions — a practice that is legal, technically complex, and directly reduces federal corporate tax revenue without reducing US economic activity.

The core mechanisms:
- **Transfer pricing**: Setting prices for intra-company transactions (intellectual property licenses, management fees, intercompany loans) to move profits to low-tax subsidiaries
- **Inversions**: Restructuring corporate domicile to a foreign jurisdiction while maintaining US operations
- **IP holding companies**: Locating patents and trademarks in low-tax jurisdictions (Ireland, Netherlands, Luxembourg, Caribbean), then licensing them to US operations at high royalty rates that strip profits from the high-tax US entity

The Tax Justice Network estimates that US companies shift approximately $900 billion in profits offshore annually, representing approximately $200–250 billion in annual revenue loss to the US Treasury. The OECD's broader estimate puts global corporate profit shifting at $100–240 billion annually across all countries.

**Ireland as the dominant vehicle**: Apple's 2014 Irish tax arrangement — revealed in EU investigations — involved routing profits through entities that were tax residents of no country, paying effective tax rates below 1% on European sales. The EU's 2016 ruling ordered Ireland to collect €13 billion in back taxes from Apple (a case that reached the European Court of Justice in 2024, upheld against Apple). Google, Facebook, Microsoft, and Pfizer have used similar Irish structures.

**TCJA's partial response — and its gaps**: TCJA introduced the Global Intangible Low-Taxed Income (GILTI) tax — a minimum tax on foreign intangible income of US multinationals. GILTI's design was criticized from the start: it used an 80% foreign tax credit that effectively set the minimum rate at 10.5% (half the 21% domestic rate), creating an incentive to shift profits to jurisdictions taxing above 10.5% rather than eliminating shifting. The Biden administration's proposed GILTI reform (raising the effective rate to 15%) passed the House but not the Senate.

Sources: [Tax Justice Network: Corporate Tax Avoidance](https://taxjustice.net/); [Tax Policy Center: OECD Pillar 1 and Pillar 2 Reforms](https://taxpolicycenter.org/briefing-book/what-are-oecd-pillar-1-and-pillar-2-international-taxation-reforms); [Tax Foundation: Global Tax Agreement Analysis](https://taxfoundation.org/blog/global-tax-agreement/); [FACT Coalition: OECD Side-by-Side Allowing Tax Abuse](https://thefactcoalition.org/oecd-side-by-side-agreement/); [Yale Budget Lab: International Tax in the Age of Pillar 2](https://budgetlab.yale.edu/research/international-tax-age-pillar-2)

### The Tax Gap's Offshore Component

The IRS's $688 billion annual tax gap estimate includes offshore tax evasion, but the offshore component is separately estimated by academic researchers and NGOs to be substantially higher than official IRS figures capture — precisely because offshore structures are the hardest to audit.

The Tax Justice Network's methodology (2020 estimate) found that wealthy individuals and corporations together lose approximately $427 billion globally in annual tax revenue to offshore abuse, with the US accounting for approximately $60–100 billion of that figure.

**FATCA's documented success**: The Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act (2010) required foreign financial institutions to report US account holders to the IRS. In the five years after FATCA implementation, over 100,000 US taxpayers disclosed previously unreported offshore accounts through associated amnesty programs (the Offshore Voluntary Disclosure Programs). Before FATCA, UBS alone facilitated approximately 52,000 secret accounts for wealthy US clients — a practice the bank paid $780 million to resolve.

**The Corporate Transparency Act and beneficial ownership**: The CTA (2021) required domestic companies to report beneficial ownership information to FinCEN. The Trump administration suspended enforcement in 2025. This reversal directly undermines one of the primary anti-avoidance tools enacted to complement FATCA — allowing shell companies to continue masking the ultimate owners of US-registered entities used for tax avoidance and evasion.

## Section 15: Fiscal Policy and Democratic Legitimacy

### The Gilens/Page Connection

The proposal's framing of fiscal reform as a democracy issue — not merely an economic policy choice — is supported by the most systematic study of policy responsiveness in the American political science literature.

Gilens and Page (2014, *Perspectives on Politics*) analyzed 1,779 policy outcomes from 1981 to 2002 against the preferences of three groups: average-income citizens, economic elites, and organized interest groups. Finding: policy outcomes correlate strongly with economic elite preferences and with the positions of organized business interest groups. They show no statistically significant correlation with the preferences of average-income citizens when elite preferences are controlled for.

Specifically: a proposed policy change with low support among economically elite Americans is adopted only about 18% of the time even when supported by a majority of average citizens. A change with high elite support is adopted roughly 45% of the time regardless of public opinion. "Mass-based interest groups and average citizens have little or no independent influence" on policy outcomes.

**The methodological challenge**: Bashir (2015) and others argued the Gilens/Page analysis is prone to underestimating median-income citizen influence due to measurement issues when elite and average citizen preferences are highly correlated. The debate is unresolved. But it does not change the directional finding: economic elites have demonstrably greater policy influence than the scale of their numbers would suggest in a one-person-one-vote system.

**The fiscal feedback loop**: Fiscal policy is particularly susceptible to elite capture because: (1) tax law complexity is opaque to most voters; (2) benefits of complexity (loopholes, preferences) are concentrated and visible to their recipients; (3) costs of complexity (revenue foregone, distributional distortion) are diffuse and invisible; (4) the lobbying ROI on tax policy is exceptionally high — H&R Block and Intuit/TurboTax spent $39 million lobbying against IRS free filing between 2006 and 2023, preserving an industry that captures approximately $10–20 billion annually from Americans who would file for free under a pre-populated return system.

**> Evidence callout**: The tax preparation industry's lobbying against IRS free filing is one of the clearest documented cases of concentrated-interest capture of fiscal policy. At approximately $39 million in lobbying expenditure, the industry has blocked a reform that would save Americans an estimated $200–300 billion in annual compliance costs. The return on lobbying investment — roughly 5,000:1 — illustrates why fiscal policy, absent democratic accountability mechanisms, will continue to be shaped by those with the most to gain from complexity.

Sources: [Gilens and Page (2014) — Internet Archive](https://archive.org/details/gilens_and_page_2014_-testing_theories_of_american_politics.doc); [Semantic Scholar: Testing Theories of American Politics](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Testing-Theories-of-American-Politics:-Elites,-and-Gilens-Page/e8a906e3330e9d222634b6bd7063d6d0598daece); [Journalist's Resource: Influence of Elites, Interest Groups, and Average Voters](https://journalistsresource.org/politics-and-government/the-influence-of-elites-interest-groups-and-average-voters-on-american-politics/); [Sunlight Foundation: American Oligarchy](https://sunlightfoundation.com/2014/04/17/american-oligarchy-how-the-preferences-of-elites-shape-policy-outcomes/)

## Section 14b: Flat Tax Arguments — Fairness and Efficiency Claims Assessed

The flat tax proposal — a single rate on all income above a personal exemption, with no deductions — resurfaces regularly in US fiscal debates (Forbes 1996, various Republican budget proposals). Its claims:

**The fairness argument**: Everyone pays the "same" percentage — no bracket penalties for success. This argument conflates horizontal equity (equal treatment of equals) with vertical equity (proportional sacrifice). A 20% rate takes $2,000 from a $10,000 income and $200,000 from a $1 million income — the same percentage but not the same marginal sacrifice, given declining marginal utility of income (the 200th thousand dollars buys less welfare improvement than the 10th thousand).

**The efficiency argument**: Eliminating bracket variation reduces the ETI for high earners by reducing avoidance incentives. This is partially correct but overstated: most ETI avoidance occurs at the top (complex sheltering, income shifting), and a flat tax eliminates brackets without eliminating the deductions and exclusions that drive most avoidance. A flat tax with a broad base eliminates more avoidance than a flat tax that preserves major tax expenditures.

**Distributional reality**: Every serious analysis of flat tax proposals finds they reduce taxes for the wealthy and raise them for the middle class or reduce progressivity significantly. The Tax Policy Center analyzed flat tax proposals consistently finding that the top quintile receives the majority of the benefit. A pure flat tax without a substantial personal exemption is regressive by construction. Even with a generous exemption, the elimination of the EITC (not compatible with a flat tax structure) would harm working families.

**The 1986 precedent as a better model**: The 1986 Tax Reform Act achieved genuine simplification — collapsing multiple brackets to two rates (15% and 28%), eliminating many shelters and loopholes, and broadening the base while lowering rates. It was broadly revenue-neutral, had bipartisan support, and reduced avoidance substantially. It demonstrates that base-broadening-with-rate-reduction is achievable; it also demonstrates that the political gains from simplification are temporary — the code re-complexified rapidly in subsequent years as concentrated interests lobbied for restored preferences.

## Section 16: Counterargument Consolidation

The strongest objections to the Domain 5 reform agenda, and their evidence-based responses:

**"High taxes cause capital flight and harm growth."**

The supply-side claim has its strongest support in: specific wealth tax designs with easy exit and no deemed-disposition rules, and corporate taxes in highly open economies with internationally mobile capital. These are real effects under specific conditions. They do not generalize to the proposed reforms: (1) the US has structural barriers to capital flight that European countries lack — worldwide taxation and strong exit taxes; (2) the ETI literature supports revenue-maximizing top marginal rates of 56–73% at current ETI estimates; (3) the OECD data shows no statistically significant correlation between total tax burden and GDP growth rates across member countries; (4) TCJA's corporate rate cut demonstrably did not produce the promised wage gains.

**"Cutting IRS enforcement saves money — ROI is poor."**

Early-year ROI comparisons are methodologically invalid for multi-year enforcement work. Enforcement actions against large partnerships and offshore accounts take 3–7 years; evaluating FY2024 returns on FY2022 appropriations captures costs without the bulk of returns. Long-run CBO/Treasury estimates of $5–12 return per enforcement dollar are consistent and positive. The $41.8 billion in IRS funding rescissions represent a choice to allow $600B/year in unpaid taxes to go uncollected.

**"Wealth taxes are administratively unworkable."**

Valuation complexity is real for closely held businesses and private assets, but concentrated in a minority of portfolios. The majority of billionaire wealth is in publicly traded securities — trivially valued. The estate tax system already navigates annual valuation of complex assets. The honest objection is "more burdensome than mark-to-market" — which is true and supports mark-to-market as the preferred reform vehicle for the unrealized gains problem.

**"Carbon taxes hurt working people."**

Under a dividend design, the bottom 60% of households are net beneficiaries. Canada's Carbon Rebate confirms this. The harm claim is valid only for a non-dividend design — a policy choice, not a structural inevitability.

**"Tax expenditures like the mortgage deduction support homeownership."**

The evidence does not support this. Research consistently finds little effect on homeownership rates — the primary effect is raising house prices and purchase amounts for buyers who would buy regardless. 71% of the mortgage deduction's benefit accrues to households earning above $200,000. It is a windfall to existing high-income homeowners, not a homeownership incentive.

**"The US needs low corporate rates to attract investment."**

Pre-TCJA, the US effective corporate tax rate — after deductions, credits, and profit-shifting — was already broadly comparable to OECD peers despite a higher statutory rate. Post-TCJA, the effective rate is below most peers. The OECD Pillar Two 15% minimum establishes a floor that substantially changes the competitive dynamics of further rate cutting: under Pillar Two, if the US raises its corporate rate to 25%, the revenue stays in the US; if it stays at 21%, other countries collect the top-up taxes and the competitive "benefit" of the lower rate is shared with foreign treasuries rather than US workers.

**"Tax reform is too complex to achieve politically."**

The 1986 Tax Reform Act passed with bipartisan support, 97–3 in the Senate, combining rate reductions with substantial base-broadening against intense lobbying by affected industries. It demonstrates that comprehensive reform is politically achievable when the distributional argument is made clearly. The base-broadening case — that loopholes primarily benefit the wealthy and their closing pays for rate reductions that benefit everyone — has cross-ideological appeal. The failure of subsequent reform efforts reflects the re-concentration of lobbying power after 1986, not a structural impossibility.

**"Progressive taxation punishes success and reduces investment incentives."**

The investment incentive argument is empirically weak. Post-TCJA corporate investment growth was modest despite the largest corporate tax cut in decades. Pre-1986, when top marginal rates were 50–70%, the US had higher rates of GDP growth, higher wage growth for the bottom quintile, and comparable or higher business investment rates than the post-Reagan era. The claim that high marginal rates reduce investment does not survive empirical scrutiny at the levels of taxation under discussion. High marginal rates reduce avoidance-based sheltering and income-shifting — which are not productive investments — more than they reduce genuine capital formation.
