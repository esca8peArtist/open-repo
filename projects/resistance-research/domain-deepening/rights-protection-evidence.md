## 7a. First Amendment Rights Under Pressure

### The Anti-Protest Legislative Wave: Scope and Pattern

Since 2017, more than 100 anti-protest bills have been introduced across 34+ states. The legislative surge follows a recognizable pattern: a wave of significant protest activity — Standing Rock (2016), BLM/racial justice (2020), campus Palestine solidarity (2024) — triggers a wave of bills that expand criminal liability, elevate misdemeanor offenses to felonies, create new tort claims against organizations, and impose civil liability on third parties funding protests.

Key patterns in the legislation:

**Felonization of assembly**: Georgia House Bill 1322 (2026) would reclassify riot from a misdemeanor to a felony punishable by one to twenty years in prison. Georgia's existing riot definition is broad enough to encompass peaceful protests where any participant causes property damage — meaning a single actor's conduct can expose every participant to felony liability. The ACLU of Georgia has testified this would make organizations legally rational to avoid mass mobilization entirely.

**Pipeline and infrastructure protest criminalization**: At least sixteen states have enacted enhanced criminal penalties for protests at "critical infrastructure" — typically oil pipelines, power plants, and similar facilities. Penalties in some states reach fifteen years for simple trespass. North Dakota's 2017 version, enacted after Standing Rock, was the template. By 2025, versions have passed in Texas, Louisiana, Minnesota, Tennessee, West Virginia, and elsewhere.

**Permit architecture as prior restraint**: The First Amendment prohibits prior restraints on speech and assembly. However, permit requirements for protests on public property create a de facto prior restraint when: (a) permit denial is discretionary; (b) timeframes require advance notice incompatible with spontaneous assembly; (c) fees are imposed. The Supreme Court established the basic permit-as-prior-restraint doctrine in *Shuttlesworth v. Birmingham* (1969), but the doctrine has been applied inconsistently, and permit requirement abuse has proliferated.

**Organizational liability**: Several legislative proposals would impose civil liability on organizations that "fund" protests where property damage occurs — a mechanism that would make labor unions, civil rights organizations, and advocacy groups financially liable for any participant's conduct at events they financially supported.

**Chilling effect documented**: The ACLU's Gary Daniels (Ohio chapter) testified directly that the effect of these provisions is singular — chilling protest by making organizations risk-averse about mobilization. An ACLU of North Carolina staff attorney documented the mechanism: "Over the last decade, the responses to those protests have become increasingly more severe, and without speaking to everyone who's participated, I would imagine that has had the effect of preventing some students and just North Carolinians from participating."

### Ag-Gag Laws and SLAPP Suits

"Ag-gag" statutes criminalize undercover investigations of agricultural facilities — documentary journalism that has historically exposed animal cruelty, food safety violations, and labor abuses. As of 2025, ag-gag laws are in force in approximately eight states. Federal courts have struck down versions in Iowa and North Carolina as unconstitutional First Amendment violations, but legislative re-drafting continues.

Strategic Litigation Against Public Participation (SLAPP) suits — civil lawsuits filed against individuals, journalists, and advocacy organizations for the purpose of suppressing speech through litigation cost rather than merit — impose a structural chilling effect. A defendant who wins a SLAPP suit may nonetheless be bankrupted by legal fees. Only thirty-two states have enacted anti-SLAPP statutes as of 2025; federal legislation has not passed. The ACLU has documented SLAPP suits filed against journalists covering immigration enforcement, environmental monitors, and pipeline protesters.

### Surveillance of Political Movements

The FBI's Joint Terrorism Task Forces (JTTFs) have conducted documented surveillance of Black Lives Matter organizations, climate activists, and environmental groups. A 2020 document obtained under FOIA revealed that the FBI's Counterterrorism Division circulated an assessment designating "Black Identity Extremists" — a term with no legal definition and no prior usage in law enforcement — as a domestic terrorism threat, a designation critics identified as updating COINTELPRO's racial surveillance logic for the social media era.

In August 2017, the FBI formally designated "Black Identity Extremists Likely Motivated to Target Law Enforcement Officers" as a terrorism threat category — a designation that justified enhanced surveillance and infiltration of racial justice organizations, applied prospectively without prior violent conduct by designated organizations.

**Informant operations**: An "Alphabet Boys" podcast series (2023) documented that the FBI paid an informant at least $20,000 to infiltrate and surveil activist groups in Denver, Colorado following the 2020 George Floyd protests. The informant, operating as an FBI asset, encouraged activists to acquire firearms and contemplate violence — a behavioral pattern that mirrors documented COINTELPRO tactics from the 1960s.

The ACLU filed a FOIA lawsuit after the FBI refused to release surveillance records related to its Black Identity Extremism investigations. The FBI's response did not dispute the surveillance occurred; it contested the disclosure obligation.

### International Contrast: Assembly Rights Architecture

**Germany — Article 8, Basic Law**: Germany's Basic Law (Grundgesetz) Article 8 guarantees the right of all Germans to assemble without prior notification. Outdoor assemblies require 48 hours' advance notice under the 1953 Federal Assembly Law (Versammlungsgesetz) — but ad-hoc assemblies responding to breaking events are exempt from the notification requirement. The standard for dissolving a lawful assembly is high: authorities must demonstrate concrete imminent danger. The German Federal Constitutional Court has consistently held that the state must demonstrate actual disruption, not theoretical risk, before restricting assembly. The German Federal Ministry of the Interior website states explicitly: the right of assembly "also covers demonstrations the authorities consider unwanted." In February 2026, Germany's Constitutional Court ruled that even a counter-protester fined for disruptive conduct retains core assembly rights, underscoring how seriously the framework takes any incursion.

**France — Loi Avia Lessons**: France's 2019 Loi Avia (named after the MP who authored it) would have required social media platforms to remove "hateful" content within 24 hours. France's Constitutional Council struck it down in June 2020, holding that the 24-hour removal mandate would inevitably lead platforms to over-remove protected speech to avoid liability — a textbook chilling effect analysis that could be applied directly to the US anti-protest legislative architecture now restricting assembly through similar over-broad liability threat.

## 7b. Government Data Weaponization

### DOGE Access to Federal Databases: The 2025–2026 Arc

The Department of Government Efficiency (DOGE) accessed federal databases at multiple agencies without statutory authority, without standard data governance protocols, and in documented violation of at least one court order. The pattern is the most significant unauthorized access to civilian government data since the creation of the modern federal privacy framework.

**Treasury and IRS**: DOGE personnel accessed Treasury Department payment systems and IRS databases. An IRS CEO appearing before Congress in March 2026 repeatedly declined to answer questions about data sharing between the IRS and Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE). A federal judge later found that the IRS had broken the law by erroneously sharing thousands of people's tax information with ICE under an agreement between the Treasury Department and the Department of Homeland Security — an agreement that had no statutory basis. (Nextgov/FCW, March 2026)

**Social Security Administration**: DOGE accessed SSA systems containing records on approximately 70 million Americans. Key documented facts in the litigation record:

- A DOGE employee signed an agreement to share SSA data with an unnamed political advocacy group that wanted to overturn election results in specific states — the government itself disclosed this in a correction to the court record.
- DOGE used an unauthorized server; SSA still does not know the full scope of what data was accessed or transmitted as of April 2026.
- A federal judge blocked DOGE access; the Fourth Circuit reversed the block in April 2026 — over the objection of circuit judges who called the most recent whistleblower report "even more alarming" — ruling on standing and justiciability grounds rather than on the merits of the data access.
- The Supreme Court allowed DOGE to access SSA data and to avoid FOIA disclosure obligations, at least temporarily, pending further proceedings.
- The Tax Law Center identified that SSA data access "raises important areas for further investigation" regarding both the Privacy Act and the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act.

**HHS and VA**: DOGE access to HHS and VA databases — containing medical records and benefits data — has been reported but is less fully documented in court filings as of April 2026.

Coalitions including AFSCME, the American Federation of Teachers, and the Alliance for Retired Americans have filed legal challenges through Democracy Forward. The ACLU filed a FOIA demand and sought a preliminary injunction on SSA data disclosure.

### ICE Database Access: DMV, Utility, and Benefits Records

ICE now routinely accesses state DMV records, utility customer records, child welfare records, credit headers, employment records, geolocation data, healthcare records, housing records, and social media posts for civil immigration enforcement — without warrants, without judicial authorization, and in many states without legislative authorization.

**DMV access at scale**: ICE has warrantless, self-service access to driver database records in all but five states. Five states — Illinois, New York, Massachusetts, Minnesota, and Washington — have taken legislative or technical steps to block Immigration and Customs Enforcement from querying their DMV records through the Nlets national law-enforcement network. In at least six of the seventeen jurisdictions that allow undocumented people to obtain driver's licenses, ICE has used facial recognition to scan driver's license photographs for deportation identification.

**Washington state case study**: Between January 1, 2025 and November 30, 2025, federal agencies queried Washington State Department of Licensing data via Nlets a total of 2,671,776 times — in a state that had enacted a law restricting such access. A University of Washington Center for Human Rights report documented that DOL allowed ICE agencies to access the data during this period, resulting in at least nine immigration arrests from the access to personal driver data. The report was released in January 2026.

**Expansion scope**: The Migration Policy Institute documented in 2025 that the Trump administration was "granting ICE access to a swath of government databases that were previously off limits," including sensitive tax and Social Security records — the same IRS-ICE data sharing agreement that a federal court later found was unlawful.

### Fusion Centers: 80+ Nationwide, Documented Civil Liberties Audit Failures

The national network of approximately 80 fusion centers — information-sharing hubs connecting federal, state, and local law enforcement — was established post-9/11 to improve counterterrorism intelligence. A bipartisan Senate Permanent Subcommittee on Investigations (PSI) report in October 2012 found the enterprise had produced substantially the opposite of its stated mission.

**Senate PSI 2012 key findings**:

- DHS had spent approximately $1.4 billion funding fusion centers over the prior decade.
- Fusion center intelligence was "of uneven quality — oftentimes shoddy, rarely timely, sometimes endangering citizens' civil liberties."
- The Senate investigation identified 40 fusion center reports drafted by DHS officials that were later rejected by Washington reviewers who raised civil liberties concerns — reports that the panel concluded "should not have been drafted at all."
- The subcommittee "could identify no reporting which uncovered a terrorist threat, nor could it identify a contribution such fusion center reporting made to disrupt an active terrorist plot."

**Documented civil liberties violations**: Fusion centers have repeatedly targeted minority communities and protest movements. Reports circulated by fusion centers have included information on Ron Paul supporters, the ACLU, war protesters, gun rights advocates, and activists on both sides of the abortion debate — all First Amendment-protected activities. The Brennan Center for Justice has documented ongoing fusion center misuse of their authorities, including reports from as recently as 2023, when Maine's fusion center was caught surveilling peaceful residents with no criminal nexus. (Reason, January 2023)

**Fiscal note**: $1.4 billion in documented fusion center expenditure over approximately a decade produced zero documented counterterrorism disruptions. The Brennan Center's analysis concludes the civil liberties costs are concrete; the security benefits remain undemonstrated.

### International Contrast: EU Data Protection Architecture

**Germany's DSGVO (GDPR implementation)**: Germany's implementation of the EU General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) through the Federal Data Protection Act (BDSG) creates affirmative obligations that are structurally incompatible with DOGE-style database access. Data controllers must maintain records of processing activities; purposes must be specified at the time of collection; data minimization principles prohibit collecting more than necessary; and secondary uses require a new legal basis. DOGE's transfer of SSA data to a political advocacy organization would constitute a clear Article 5 GDPR violation — unlawful processing for a purpose incompatible with the original collection purpose — triggering penalties up to 4% of global annual turnover.

**EU AI Act Article 5 (effective February 2, 2025)**: The EU AI Act's prohibited practices article explicitly bans AI-enabled social scoring systems by public or private actors — the kind of cross-database profiling that ICE's integration of DMV, utility, benefits, and tax data approaches. The Article also prohibits real-time remote biometric identification in public spaces for law enforcement purposes, with narrow exceptions. Penalties for violations reach €35 million or 7% of global annual turnover. The US has no comparable prohibition.

**France's CNIL**: France's Commission nationale de l'informatique et des libertés (CNIL) operates as an independent administrative authority with investigation, enforcement, and sanction authority over both public and private actors. It has issued substantial fines against government agencies for improper data use. No equivalent independent enforcement authority with civil society complaint intake exists at the US federal level.

## 7c. Surveillance and Digital Rights

### Section 702 FISA: Warrantless Surveillance of Americans

Section 702 of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act authorizes collection of foreign intelligence from domestic electronic communications systems — targeting non-US persons abroad — but inevitably sweeps in large volumes of Americans' phone calls, texts, and emails in what is termed "incidental collection."

**Reauthorization history**: Congress reauthorized Section 702 on April 20, 2024, via the Reforming Intelligence and Securing America Act (RISAA), extending it two years to April 20, 2026. In the House debate, an amendment requiring a warrant before querying Section 702-collected data to find communications involving Americans tied 212–212 — falling two votes short of passing. Had it passed, it would have imposed the most significant civil liberties reform on the program since its creation.

**Documented FBI abuses using 702 data**: The FBI conducted warrantless searches of Section 702-acquired information to access communications of:
- Black Lives Matter protesters
- US government officials
- Journalists and political commentators
- 19,000 donors to a single congressional campaign

**The query accounting problem**: The FBI conducted 5,518 known "US person queries" of Section 702 data in 2024 — a claimed 90%+ decline from the prior year. However, the Brennan Center's analysis documents that FBI personnel used an "advanced filter" accounting method that may have substantially undercounted actual queries. A Department of Justice review ordered by the FISA Court was unable to determine how many queries actually occurred. The FISA Court itself declared that "compliance problems with the FBI's querying of Section 702 information have proven to be persistent and widespread." The 5,518 figure may be a lower bound for actual query volume, not the true count.

**The 2026 sunset**: Section 702 expires April 20, 2026. In February 2026, Senators Durbin and Lee introduced the bipartisan SAFE Act to reform 702 ahead of expiration. Whether reauthorization will include a warrant requirement for US person queries remains unresolved as of April 2026.

**The FISA Court structural problem**: The FISC operates in secret, hears only the government's case, and has historically approved the overwhelming majority of government applications. The court has been described as operating with a "rubber stamp" dynamic by former NSA analysts and civil liberties scholars. In the Section 702 context, the court has found the government's compliance program adequate despite acknowledging persistent and widespread noncompliance — a logical tension the court has not resolved.

### Stingray/IMSI Catcher Deployment Without Warrants

IMSI catchers ("Stingrays") impersonate cell phone towers, collecting the unique identifier of nearby cell phone SIM cards and enabling law enforcement to request subscriber identity, address, and location history from carriers. The devices capture communications data from every phone in range — not just the target — with no prior judicial authorization in most deployments.

Local law enforcement agencies across the United States have purchased and deployed IMSI catchers without publicly disclosing the capability to the communities they serve, without adopting use policies, and without obtaining warrants. The Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF) has documented widespread deployment. In 2025, the EFF's Rayhunter project — an open-source tool for detecting IMSI catcher presence — documented active deployment in multiple cities during the research period.

The Fourth Amendment should apply to IMSI catcher use under *Carpenter v. United States* (2018), which held that long-term cell site location data requires a warrant. However, no federal statute directly regulates IMSI catcher deployment, and most state laws have not been updated to address the technology.

### Facial Recognition: Clearview AI, ICE Contracts, and Wrongful Arrests

Clearview AI operates a facial recognition system with a database of more than 20 billion images collected from the internet, including social media applications, without consent from the individuals depicted. Law enforcement agencies, federal agencies, and private actors have used the tool for identification purposes.

**ICE contract**: In 2025, Clearview AI signed a $9.2 million contract with ICE. Illinois law bans Clearview for police use within the state; ICE uses the tool in Illinois without oversight through the federal contract, circumventing the state prohibition.

**Wrongful arrests**: As of 2026, at least eight people have been wrongfully arrested due to false positive identifications from facial recognition systems — all documented cases involve people of color, consistent with academic research showing substantially higher error rates for darker-skinned individuals and women in commercially deployed facial recognition systems.

**Local surveillance infrastructure**: The Marshall Project documented in November 2024 that law enforcement agencies ran over 20 billion monthly scans through automated license plate reader (ALPR) networks, deployed IMSI catchers in unmarked vehicles, and used facial recognition capable of identifying individuals in real-time crowds — all in the context of protest surveillance.

**ICE surveillance arsenal**: A State of Surveillance analysis (2025) assessed ICE's technology procurement at approximately $45 million and documented the full stack: Clearview AI facial recognition, ALPR networks, cell phone tracking, social media monitoring, and cross-database profiling.

### International Contrast: EU Surveillance Limits

**Germany's Bundesverfassungsgericht**: The German Federal Constitutional Court has repeatedly struck down surveillance programs that lack proportionality to the investigative purpose, issuing landmark rulings on online surveillance of computers, mass telecommunications metadata retention, and automated vehicle tracking. The German framework requires that surveillance measures be targeted, proportionate, and subject to independent judicial authorization — a structural contrast with Section 702's bulk collection-then-query model.

**UK Investigatory Powers Tribunal**: The UK's Investigatory Powers Tribunal adjudicates complaints about unlawful surveillance by UK intelligence services. Although the UK has its own bulk surveillance controversies (the Investigatory Powers Act 2016 permits bulk collection), the Tribunal has issued enforceable rulings against intelligence agencies for unlawful surveillance — a mechanism that does not exist for Section 702 targets in the United States.

**EU GDPR Article 9**: Special categories of personal data — political opinions, racial or ethnic origin, religious beliefs, health data, sexual orientation — receive heightened protection under GDPR Article 9, prohibiting processing absent specific legal bases. BLM protest surveillance using social media monitoring to identify attendees' racial or ethnic origin would be presumptively unlawful under this framework.

## 7d. Criminalization of Poverty and Dissent

### Grants Pass v. Johnson (2024): Criminalizing Homelessness

On June 28, 2024, the Supreme Court issued a 6-3 decision in *City of Grants Pass v. Johnson* holding that municipal enforcement of public camping ordinances against people experiencing homelessness does not violate the Eighth Amendment's prohibition on cruel and unusual punishment. The Court's majority expressly rejected the Ninth Circuit's *Martin v. City of Boise* (2019) framework, which had held that enforcing camping bans when no shelter was available criminalized the status of homelessness.

**Practical impact**: Within six months of the ruling, more than 100 cities across the United States enacted or enforced public camping bans, including in jurisdictions where no alternative shelter was available. The ruling permits fines and jail time for sleeping outdoors — even where the individual has no other lawful option. Justice Sonia Sotomayor, joined by Justices Kagan and Jackson in dissent, wrote: "Sleep is a biological necessity, not a choice... For people with no access to shelter, that means punishing them for being homeless."

**Fiscal analysis**: The cost of criminally prosecuting and incarcerating homeless individuals for camping violations substantially exceeds the cost of providing supportive housing. The National Alliance to End Homelessness and multiple city-level analyses have found that chronic homelessness costs approximately $30,000–$50,000 per person per year in emergency services, medical care, and criminal justice, while permanent supportive housing costs approximately $8,000–$20,000 per person per year — a cost ratio that makes criminalization fiscally irrational as well as constitutionally troubling under the dissent's framework.

### Modern Debtors' Prisons: Driver's License Suspension and Court Fines

Forty-three states permit driver's license suspension for failure to pay court fines and fees — a practice that generates a cascading poverty trap. Suspending a license for nonpayment removes the primary mechanism by which most Americans travel to work, reducing earning capacity and making repayment less likely. Only four states require a determination of "willfulness" or ability to pay before license suspension — meaning in 39 states, inability to pay is legally indistinguishable from refusal to pay.

**The attorney cost problem**: In 24 states surveyed by the Institute for Justice, the median civil forfeiture seizure was less than $1,678. The estimated cost to hire an attorney to contest it is approximately $3,300. The mathematics of the attorney-cost barrier ensures that most seizures go uncontested — generating an adverse selection dynamic where the government retains the most meritless seizures because they are least likely to be challenged.

**2024 reform progress**: Ohio and Illinois both passed bills in 2024 ending debt-based license suspensions. Maryland eliminated $13 million in outstanding fines and fees. Since 2019, approximately half of states have enacted some reform of the debt-based suspension practice — but the practice remains legal in most states and affects an estimated 7 million Americans who have suspended licenses due to court debt.

**Ferguson, Missouri template**: The Department of Justice's 2015 Ferguson report documented a municipal court system designed primarily as a revenue extraction mechanism — with fines, fees, and warrants generating the majority of municipal revenue, imposed disproportionately on Black residents. Ferguson was not an outlier; it was a more documented example of a widely replicated model.

### Civil Asset Forfeiture: $82 Billion Since 2000

Civil asset forfeiture permits law enforcement to seize property suspected of being connected to criminal activity — without charging or convicting the property owner. The owner bears the burden of contesting the seizure in civil proceedings that many states structure to make successful challenge prohibitively expensive.

**Scale**: The Institute for Justice's fourth edition of *Policing for Profit* (2025 update) documents that forfeiture has generated at least $82 billion nationwide since 2000. In the federal system, civil forfeiture generates approximately $2 billion in annual revenue. The DOJ's Equitable Sharing program — which allows state and local agencies to seize property locally, transfer it to federal prosecutors, and receive back up to 80% of proceeds regardless of state law restrictions — paid out more than $8.8 billion to state and local law enforcement agencies between 2000 and 2019.

**The judicial bypass problem**: 71% of DOJ forfeitures are completed administratively — bypassing any judicial review entirely. The owner receives a notice with a short contestation deadline; if they miss it or cannot afford an attorney, the government retains the property.

**The small-seizure inequity**: Across 24 states, half of currency forfeitures involve amounts less than $1,678. The estimated attorney cost to contest a forfeiture is approximately $3,300. This arithmetic makes challenging most small forfeitures economically irrational — even if the seizure is legally baseless.

**2024 SCOTUS ruling**: In *Culley v. Marshall* (2024), the Supreme Court reaffirmed that the Constitution guarantees a timely hearing when property is seized for civil forfeiture. The Institute for Justice's updated report found that, despite this constitutional guarantee, civil forfeiture procedures "frequently fail to deliver on this promise" — with many states offering hearings only months after seizure.

**Racial disparities**: A Stanford Law Review analysis (2025) — "Asset Forfeiture and Inequality" — documents that civil forfeiture falls disproportionately on Black and Brown communities, consistent with the underlying policing disparities that generate the encounters in which property is seized.

### Prosecutorial Overreach: RICO and the Cop City Template

The RICO (Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations) statute, enacted in 1970 to target organized crime, has been applied with increasing frequency to protest movements. The Georgia "Cop City" prosecutions established the most significant recent template.

**The Cop City indictment**: In September 2023, Georgia prosecutors indicted 61 activists under Georgia's RICO statute in connection with opposition to the Atlanta Public Safety Training Center ("Cop City"). The indictment encompassed:
- Peaceful protest activities
- Writing and distributing zines (political pamphlets)
- "Mutual aid" (providing food, legal support, and other assistance to protesters)
- "Collectivism" as alleged evidence of criminal conspiracy
- Raising money for protest signs (charged as money laundering)

Legal scholars across the political spectrum characterized the indictment as a gross overreach. The ACLU wrote: "RICO and domestic terrorism charges against Cop City activists send a chilling message" that routine social justice organizing — fundraising, communication, logistical support — can be charged as criminal conspiracy if prosecutors choose to apply the statute to political opponents.

**Continuing litigation**: In 2025, a Georgia appeals court declined to dismiss the RICO charges, and at least one activist filed a civil lawsuit alleging the Atlanta Police Department adopted "a policy of systemic arrests and pretextual charges" against Stop Cop City protesters. The case continues to have chilling effects on protest organizing in Georgia and beyond — the statutory theory, if accepted, would make any protest movement's support infrastructure potentially criminal.

**Legislative amplification**: Georgia Republicans in January 2024 proposed expanding RICO to cover "loitering" protesters — a proposal that would have allowed RICO charges for presence at a protest site, without any further conduct.

### International Contrast: Social Floor Protections

**Scotland's Fairer Scotland Duty**: The Fairer Scotland Duty (2018) requires public bodies in Scotland to actively consider how they can reduce inequality of outcome caused by socioeconomic disadvantage when making strategic decisions. It creates an affirmative obligation to assess the poverty impact of public policy — directly contrary to the US approach of treating poverty-correlated enforcement outcomes as constitutionally neutral.

**Germany's Sozialstaatsprinzip**: Article 20(1) of Germany's Basic Law establishes Germany as a "social federal state" (sozialer Bundesstaat). The Federal Constitutional Court has interpreted this as requiring the state to guarantee a "dignified minimum" of subsistence — meaning the state cannot simply criminalize the condition of poverty without providing an alternative. The *Grants Pass* majority's reasoning that camping bans are facially neutral would encounter direct constitutional challenge under this framework.

## 7e. Reproductive and Bodily Autonomy Rights

### Post-Dobbs Landscape: 14+ State Bans as of 2026

*Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization* (June 24, 2022) eliminated the federal constitutional right to abortion established in *Roe v. Wade* (1973). As of 2026, the landscape is:

- More than 31 million women of reproductive age live in the 22 states where abortion is banned or under severe restriction — approximately 41% of all reproductive-age women in the United States. (National Partnership for Women and Families, 2025)
- Republican state lawmakers in 13 states introduced bills in 2025 legislative sessions that would bring homicide charges against people who have abortions, with several states' proposals potentially subjecting pregnant people to the death penalty for abortion-related conduct.
- The Dobbs majority opinion's analytical logic — that rights not "deeply rooted in this Nation's history and tradition" are not constitutionally protected — is the doctrinal foundation for Justice Thomas's concurrence explicitly inviting reconsideration of *Griswold v. Connecticut* (contraceptive access), *Lawrence v. Texas* (same-sex relationships), and *Obergefell v. Hodges* (marriage equality).

**Gallup approval data correlation**: The sustained Supreme Court approval collapse documented in the Domain 6 evidence deepening document correlates directly with *Dobbs*. The June 2022 decision drove a structural legitimacy shift that predates the current administration's intensification of pressure.

Sources: [National Partnership: Three Years Post-Dobbs Report](https://nationalpartnership.org/report/abortion-bans-and-criminalization-three-years-post-dobbs/) | [KFF: Abortion Trends Before and After Dobbs](https://www.kff.org/womens-health-policy/abortion-trends-before-and-after-dobbs/)

### Digital Surveillance of Reproductive Health

The same commercial surveillance infrastructure that enables targeted advertising has become a law enforcement tool for abortion criminalization. Key documented vectors:

**Menstrual tracking and health apps**: Seventy-nine percent of health-related smartphone applications share data with third parties. Menstrual tracking apps collect information about menstrual cycles, fertility windows, and pregnancy status. This data is not protected by HIPAA because the apps are not healthcare providers — it is commercially available to data brokers and, through data broker purchases, to law enforcement.

**Location data**: Cell phone location data revealing visits to abortion providers, pharmacies dispensing abortion medication, or out-of-state reproductive health clinics is commercially available through data brokers. The same data used to verify advertising effectiveness can be subpoenaed by or purchased by state prosecutors.

**Search and browsing history**: Browser search history for "abortion pills," "medication abortion," "miscarriage care," or clinic locations creates a potentially subpoenable evidentiary record available to prosecutors in states with abortion bans.

**The data broker loophole**: The National Partnership for Women and Families (2025) documented that digital surveillance "supercharges abortion criminalization" specifically because the data broker loophole — the absence of federal regulation on commercial data sale to law enforcement — means that prosecutors can purchase data that would require a warrant to obtain directly from a cell phone carrier.

### International Contrast: Constitutional Abortion Protections

**France — World's First Constitutional Right to Abortion (March 8, 2024)**: On International Women's Day 2024, France became the first country in the world to explicitly guarantee abortion as a constitutional right. The joint session of the French parliament at the Palace of Versailles approved the measure 780 votes to 72. The constitutional amendment adds to Article 34 of the French Constitution: the law "determines the conditions by which is exercised the freedom of women to have recourse to a termination of pregnancy." President Macron's government pursued the amendment directly in response to the American *Dobbs* decision — the government stated explicitly that it acted to prevent the possibility of a future French government restricting abortion access through ordinary legislation. France thus used the US constitutional regression as a catalyst to entrench a right that had previously been protected only by statute.

**Germany — Section 218 Expert Commission (April 2024)**: Germany's abortion law has historically been among Europe's most restrictive — abortions are technically prohibited under Section 218 of the Penal Code but exempt from punishment in the first twelve weeks under Section 218a when preceded by mandatory counseling. In April 2024, a government-appointed expert commission presented its final report recommending that the legislature legalize abortion in the early stages of pregnancy and remove the criminal prohibition framework entirely. The reform remains pending in the German legislature as of April 2026. The commission also recommended legalizing egg donation and regulated surrogacy — a broad reproductive rights modernization package.

**Canada — Morgentaler Precedent**: The Supreme Court of Canada's 1988 *R v. Morgentaler* decision struck down Canada's criminal abortion restrictions as a violation of Section 7 of the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms (right to life, liberty, and security of the person). Canada has had no federal abortion law since. The contrast with the US underscores that constitutional rights framing — rather than legislative consensus framing — provides more durable protection.

Sources: [NPR: France Makes History on Abortion Rights](https://www.npr.org/2024/03/04/1235217454/france-abortion-rights-constitution) | [Human Rights Watch: France Protects Abortion as Guaranteed Freedom](https://www.hrw.org/news/2024/03/05/france-protects-abortion-guaranteed-freedom-constitution) | [Center for Reproductive Rights: France First Constitutional Right](https://reproductiverights.org/news/france-guarantees-constitutional-right-abortion/) | [Library of Congress: Germany Expert Commission on Abortion Reform](https://www.loc.gov/item/global-legal-monitor/2024-05-09/germany-expert-commission-recommends-reform-of-laws-on-abortion-egg-donation-and-surrogacy/) | [Verfassungsblog: Enshrining Abortion in French Constitution](https://verfassungsblog.de/enshrining-abortion-rights-in-the-french-constitution/)

## 7f. Right to Protest and Peaceful Assembly

### Police Use of Force at Protests: 2020 Incident Documentation

The summer of 2020 — following the police murder of George Floyd on May 25 — produced the most widespread protest activity in modern US history, with documented protests in all 50 states and more than 2,000 cities. It also produced the most extensively documented instance of police use of force against civil rights protesters since the 1960s.

**Portland documentation (most extensively recorded)**: Portland sustained nightly protests for more than 100 consecutive days. Portland police documented more than 6,000 uses of force during the protest period. The city of Portland has paid over $2.8 million resolving police use-of-force claims and lawsuits stemming from the 2020 protests.

**Federal agent deployment**: The Department of Homeland Security deployed more than 750 federal officers to Portland — many without protest crowd control training. The DHS Office of Inspector General found that of 63 officers examined, only 7 had received riot and crowd control training. Federal agents in military-style unmarked uniforms detained protesters without probable cause in unmarked vehicles — conduct that ACLU Oregon sued over and the federal government ultimately settled.

**Specific documented injuries**:
- Donavan LaBella, 26, was shot in the head by a US Marshal, suffering a frontal lobe skull fracture.
- Navy veteran Christopher David was struck with a baton and pepper-sprayed by federal agents, suffering two broken bones in his hand (the encounter was recorded on video).
- Documented injuries across Portland protests included bone fractures, lacerated scalps and necks, and multiple traumatic brain injuries.

**Settlement**: In 2024, the federal government settled with Portland protesters over excessive force allegations by federal agents. Street Roots documented in January 2026 that federal agents are again deploying tear gas in Portland in connection with immigration enforcement protests.

### Kettling and Mass Arrest: NYC 2020

New York City police employed "kettling" — the practice of encircling protesters in a tight area with no exit, holding them without formal arrest — against hundreds of protesters during the 2020 uprisings. The NYPD kettled approximately 300 protesters and bystanders in the Bronx during a June 4, 2020 incident. Charges were eventually dismissed or vacated against most individuals. The New York Civil Liberties Union documented that the tactic was used against people who were exercising First Amendment rights and had no prior notice they would be detained.

New York subsequently passed legislation limiting NYPD use of crowd control techniques, but kettling and mass arrest remain available tactics in most US jurisdictions.

### Infiltration, Informants, and the COINTELPRO Legacy

The FBI's COINTELPRO program (1956–1971) systematically targeted civil rights organizations, antiwar groups, and socialist organizations through infiltration, planted evidence, forged correspondence, and coordinated harassment — including actions that contributed to violence against movement leaders. Although COINTELPRO was officially terminated when its existence was exposed through a 1971 burglary of an FBI field office, its tactics and organizational logic have recurred in subsequent decades.

**2017 Black Identity Extremism designation**: In August 2017, the FBI's counterterrorism division circulated an assessment designating "Black Identity Extremists Likely Motivated to Target Law Enforcement Officers" as a terrorism threat — creating a domestic terrorism category for racial justice activism without any statutory basis and without documented predicate conduct by named organizations. Civil liberties organizations identified the designation as functional COINTELPRO: it authorized enhanced surveillance and disruption of Black activist organizations under a terrorism label.

**2020 BLM infiltration**: FBI infiltration of racial justice organizing following the 2020 Floyd protests has been documented through the "Alphabet Boys" podcast investigation. The FBI paid an informant at least $20,000 to infiltrate Denver activist networks; the informant encouraged violent conduct — a classic COINTELPRO entrapment pattern.

**The JTTF monitoring framework**: Joint Terrorism Task Forces across the country have documented monitoring of environmental groups, anti-war organizations, animal rights activists, and racial justice organizations. The FBI designated Earth Liberation Front and Animal Liberation Front as domestic terrorism threats in the early 2000s, a designation that justified ongoing surveillance of affiliated organizations well beyond anyone who engaged in property damage.

### International Contrast: Assembly Rights in Practice

**Germany's Versammlungsgesetz (Assembly Law)**: Under Germany's federal assembly law and its state-level analogues, outdoor assemblies require 48 hours' advance notice — but spontaneous assemblies responding to immediate events are explicitly exempt from this requirement. German authorities must actively protect counter-demonstrators as well as demonstrators. The German Constitutional Court has held consistently that the state must maintain a very high evidentiary threshold before dissolving or restricting an assembly. In 2020, during the COVID-19 pandemic, the Constitutional Court upheld the right of assembly even under emergency restrictions — requiring that the state demonstrate specific, concrete danger rather than general theoretical risk. This is structurally incompatible with the broad public-order discretion US police agencies have exercised to disperse protests.

**South Korea's Candlelight Revolution (2016–2017)**: South Korea's Constitutional Court in 2009 had struck down a blanket ban on nighttime assembly as unconstitutional, holding that the right to peaceful assembly must be available at all hours. This precedent enabled the Candlelight Protests of 2016–2017 — where millions of South Koreans assembled weekly in central Seoul for eighteen weeks, ultimately compelling the National Assembly to impeach President Park Geun-hye and the Constitutional Court to remove her from office. The movement is studied as one of the most successful examples of constitutional protest leading to democratic accountability in recent democratic history. The nighttime assembly right the Constitutional Court protected was load-bearing for the movement's success.

**South Africa's Constitutional Framework**: South Africa's Constitution (Section 17) guarantees the right to assemble, demonstrate, picket, and present petitions — framed as positive rights rather than negative liberties. South Africa's Constitutional Court has interpreted these provisions as requiring the state to take active steps to enable protest, including protecting protesters from counter-protesters. This positive obligation framing contrasts with the US model, where the state's only obligation is to refrain from suppressing protected assembly.

## International Benchmarks and Reform Roadmap

### Where the US Stands in Global Civil Liberties Rankings

**V-Dem Civil Liberties Index (2025)**: V-Dem's 2025 Democracy Report identified the United States as among the countries experiencing the most significant civil liberties erosion — placing it in the autocratizing country category for the first time. V-Dem specifically cited the judiciary's partisanship, attacks on the press and academia, and erosion of civil liberties as contributing factors.

**Freedom House (2025)**: The US received a score of 79 out of 100 on Freedom House's Freedom in the World index in 2025 — a marked decline from scores above 90 in previous decades, and lower than the scores of all peer liberal democracies in Western Europe and the Anglosphere.

**World Justice Project Civil Justice Ranking**: The US ranks 112th of 143 countries on accessibility and affordability of civil justice — meaning the formal legal system for vindicating civil liberties claims is inaccessible to most people who need it. Civil liberties are only meaningful if they are enforceable; 111 countries have more accessible enforcement systems.

### Reform Priorities and Fiscal Estimates

The following reform priorities emerge from this evidence base, with preliminary fiscal framing:

**1. Federal Anti-SLAPP Statute**: A federal anti-SLAPP law would protect journalists, activists, and civil society organizations from retaliatory litigation. Fiscal cost: minimal — primarily court administration. Current absence costs more through suppressed public interest litigation.

**2. Warrant Requirement for Section 702 US Person Queries**: Requiring a warrant before querying Section 702 data for communications involving Americans costs no net government expenditure — it adds judicial review. The 2024 vote was 212-212; passage would require shifting two House votes.

**3. Data Broker Regulation for Reproductive and Civil Rights Data**: Prohibiting the sale of location data, search history, and health app data to law enforcement would close the post-*Dobbs* digital surveillance gap. Estimated federal administration cost: $50–100 million annually for FTC enforcement capacity. The cost of the absence is borne by approximately 31 million women living under abortion restrictions.

**4. Civil Forfeiture Reform — Conviction Required**: Requiring criminal conviction before civil asset forfeiture fundamentally restructures the incentive — agencies must prove wrongdoing, not merely assert suspicion. IJ has calculated that requiring conviction and raising the evidentiary standard would eliminate the majority of the $2 billion in annual federal forfeiture revenue, redirecting law enforcement incentives toward prosecution rather than seizure.

**5. Fusion Center Audit and Restructuring**: A mandatory GAO audit of all 80+ fusion centers, modeled on the 2012 PSI report, with civil liberties impact assessments and binding use-restriction policies, would cost approximately $10–20 million in audit resources against a $1.4 billion decadal investment that the Senate found produced no documented counterterrorism benefit.

**6. IMSI Catcher Warrant Requirement**: Federal legislation extending *Carpenter*'s logic to IMSI catcher deployment would require warrants for device use — eliminating the most significant legal gap in cell phone surveillance regulation. Administrative cost: minimal.

**7. Debt-Based License Suspension Abolition**: Federal legislation conditioning highway funding on elimination of debt-based driver's license suspension (the model used effectively in other transportation policy areas) would eliminate a poverty trap affecting an estimated 7 million Americans. Revenue impact on states: recouped through reduced criminal justice costs and increased economic participation.

### The International Benchmark Gap

France has constitutionally protected abortion. Germany has a constitutional minimum subsistence guarantee that cannot be satisfied by criminalizing homelessness. The EU prohibits AI-enabled social scoring and real-time biometric surveillance in public spaces. Germany's Constitutional Court has protected assembly rights even under pandemic emergency conditions. South Korea's Constitutional Court protected nighttime assembly, enabling the movement that removed a corrupt president.

Each of these protections exists because democratic societies recognized a rights gap and chose to close it through constitutional architecture rather than legislative discretion. The US civil liberties deficit documented in this evidence base is not primarily a resource problem or an administrative problem. It is an architectural problem: rights that rest on statutory protection or judicial interpretation can be revoked by the same power that granted them. The international comparison suggests the solution is entrenchment — constitutional amendment, treaty obligations, independent enforcement bodies, and structural separation of civil liberties review from the political branches.
