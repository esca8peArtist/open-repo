## Executive Overview

The racial wealth gap is not a residual or fading inequality — it is a stable, actively reproduced structure. As of the 2022 Federal Reserve Survey of Consumer Finances, the median white household holds **$284,310** in wealth versus **$44,100** for the median Black household — a ratio of 6.4 to 1. The absolute dollar gap grew by $49,950 between 2019 and 2022 alone, reaching $240,120 at the median. At the mean — where wealth concentration distorts the picture upward — a Black family born in the 1940s and 1950s that started with a $181,677 average wealth gap against comparable white families had that gap widen to over $1.4 million by the time they reached their seventies. The gap has not closed since 1963; the percentage ratio has remained locked at roughly 15 cents of Black wealth per white dollar for six decades.

The mechanisms of this reproduction are documented, not inferred. Between 1934 and 1968 the Federal Housing Administration explicitly instructed appraisers to mark down neighborhoods with "infiltration of inharmonious racial groups" and recommended racially restrictive covenants — language appearing verbatim in its 1935 and 1938 Underwriting Manuals, not as aberrant practice but as official policy. Approximately 1.2 million Black veterans of World War II were structurally excluded from GI Bill benefits that generated the postwar white middle class, with all-white VA offices, Jim Crow banks, and state administration ensuring that in Mississippi alone, two of 3,229 federally insured home loans went to Black veterans. Urban renewal displaced approximately 1.36 million people between 1950 and 1971, of whom roughly 60 percent were nonwhite, and mass incarceration — producing sentences 19 months longer for Black defendants than identically situated white defendants — destroys approximately $500,000 in lifetime earning potential per incarcerated individual. Citigroup estimated in 2020 that these accumulated harms have cost the U.S. economy $16 trillion in foregone GDP over two decades. The proposal's reform architecture responds to a documented record. This file provides the granular evidence behind that record.

## Section 1: The Racial Wealth Gap — Mechanisms and Measurement

### 1.1 The 2022 Federal Reserve SCF Data in Full

The **Federal Reserve's 2022 Survey of Consumer Finances**, published in October 2023, provides the most comprehensive official measurement of U.S. household wealth by race. Key figures:

- **Median white household wealth**: $284,310
- **Median Black household wealth**: $44,100
- **Ratio**: 6.4 to 1 (white to Black)
- **Median Hispanic household wealth**: $62,120

The framing of percentage gains obscures the structural problem. Between 2019 and 2022, **median Black family wealth rose 61 percent** — a figure frequently cited to suggest convergence. But median white wealth also rose, and because white families started from a much higher base, the **absolute dollar gap grew by approximately $50,000** during the same period, from roughly $190,000 to $240,120. Citigroup's analysis in 2020 found that for every $100 held by white households, Black households held approximately $15 — a ratio that Federal Reserve economists confirmed remained essentially unchanged from 1963 to 2022.

The mean wealth figures are starker and reveal the compounding effect of time. Research tracking the cohort born between 1943 and 1951 found that when those individuals were in their thirties in 1983, the average white-Black wealth gap was **$181,677**, with white families holding three times as much. By 2022, when the same cohort was in their seventies, the average gap had surpassed **$1.4 million**, and white families held more than four times the wealth of their Black counterparts. Wealth inequality does not merely persist — it accelerates as time compounds earlier advantages.

Sources: [Federal Reserve: Greater Wealth, Greater Uncertainty — 2022 SCF (October 2023)](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/greater-wealth-greater-uncertainty-changes-in-racial-inequality-in-the-survey-of-consumer-finances-20231018.html); [Brookings: Black Wealth Is Increasing, But So Is the Racial Wealth Gap](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/black-wealth-is-increasing-but-so-is-the-racial-wealth-gap/); [NBC News: A $1 Million Wealth Gap Now Divides White Families from Black and Hispanic Ones](https://www.nbcnews.com/news/latino/1-million-wealth-gap-white-black-hispanic-families-rcna149252)

### 1.2 Historical Trajectory: The Gap Has Widened, Not Closed

The dominant public narrative holds that civil rights legislation and anti-poverty programs steadily reduced racial economic inequality since 1968. The wealth data falsifies this narrative.

Urban Institute analysis covering 1963 to 2013 found that **the percentage ratio of Black to white average wealth has remained essentially flat at approximately 15 percent for the entire 50-year period**. The average wealth of African Americans was 15 percent of the average wealth of whites in 1963 — and was still approximately 15 percent in 2022. The racial wealth gap as a ratio did not meaningfully close between the passage of the Civil Rights Act and the present day.

In absolute dollar terms, the gap has dramatically widened. In 1963, the average wealth of white families exceeded the average wealth of Black families by approximately $117,000. By 2022, that absolute difference had grown to more than $1 million. This widening reflects mathematical compounding: when one group starts with substantially more wealth, the same percentage return on investment produces a larger absolute gain, and the process repeats each generation.

The Fair Housing Act of 1968 produced a modest improvement in Black homeownership rates through the 1970s and 1980s. But the 2008 financial crisis erased those gains. The Black homeownership rate peaked at approximately 49.7 percent in 2004, then fell to 41.3 percent by 2019 before recovering to approximately **45.9 percent in 2023** — still approximately **28 percentage points below the white homeownership rate of 73.8 percent**, and lower than the Black homeownership rate of 48 percent in 1970.

Sources: [Urban Institute: Nine Charts about Wealth Inequality in America](https://apps.urban.org/features/wealth-inequality-charts/); [PBS NewsHour: Today's Racial Wealth Gap Is Wider Than in the 1960s](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/nation/todays-racial-wealth-gap-is-wider-than-in-the-1960s); [NCRC: The Racial Wealth Divide and Black Homeownership](https://ncrc.org/the-racial-wealth-divide-and-black-homeownership-new-data-show-small-gains-deep-fragility/)

### 1.3 Asset-Specific Gaps: Homeownership, Retirement, and Equities

The wealth gap is not a single number but a compound of asset-specific disparities, each with its own causal history.

**Homeownership**: White homeownership rate 73.8 percent vs. Black 45.9 percent (2023) — a 28-point gap. Because home equity is the largest single wealth-building vehicle for middle-income families, and because the policies that excluded Black families from homeownership (FHA redlining, restrictive covenants, contract buying, discriminatory appraisals) operated specifically in the housing domain, the homeownership gap is the primary transmission mechanism of the historical harm.

**Retirement savings**: White households are **1.5 times more likely to own at least one retirement account** (65.6 percent vs. 43.9 percent). Among account holders, median white retirement savings are **$100,000**, compared to **$23,400** for Black account holders — a ratio of 4.3 to 1. Disparities in retirement savings have increased fivefold over the past three decades; in 1989 the white-Black gap in average retirement savings was approximately $50,000.

**Equity and stock holdings**: White households are **1.9 times more likely to own stocks and mutual funds** (30.9 percent vs. 16.6 percent), and among holders, median white equity holdings are **$44,500** versus **$7,000** for Black holders — 6.4 times as much. University of Pennsylvania demographic research found that stock-linked assets have surpassed homeownership as the primary driver of the Black-white wealth gap since the 2000s, accounting for **35 to 40 percent of the average gap**.

**Business ownership**: The Treasury Department's racial differences analysis documents that Black-owned businesses are concentrated in lower-revenue sectors, face significantly higher loan denial rates even when controlling for creditworthiness, and have substantially lower median annual revenues than white-owned businesses.

**The inheritance premium**: Federal Reserve Bank of Richmond research (2022) found that white families are **twice as likely to receive any inheritance** as Black families, and when they do receive one, the amount is **nearly three times larger**. Hamilton and Darity's synthesis identifies inheritances and intergenerational transfers as explaining more of the racial wealth gap than any other demographic or socioeconomic factor. A 2020 simulation study in the Russell Sage Foundation Journal found that intergenerational wealth transfers explain approximately **26 percent of the current gap**.

Sources: [Treasury: Racial Differences in Economic Security — Non-Housing Assets](https://home.treasury.gov/news/featured-stories/racial-differences-in-economic-security-non-housing-assets); [Duke/Demography: The Rising Importance of Stock-Linked Assets in the Black-White Wealth Gap](https://read.dukeupress.edu/demography/article/60/6/1877/383745/The-Rising-Importance-of-Stock-Linked-Assets-in); [Richmond Fed: How Big Is the Inheritance Gap Between Black and White Families? (2022)](https://www.richmondfed.org/publications/research/economic_brief/2022/eb_22-49)

## Section 2: Evidentiary Record of Specific Government Harms

### 2.1 FHA Redlining: The Official Language

The Federal Housing Administration's redlining was not informal administrative discrimination — it was codified in official underwriting doctrine. The FHA's **1935 Underwriting Manual** explicitly instructed appraisers: "If a neighborhood is to retain stability it is necessary that properties shall continue to be occupied by the same social and racial classes. A change in social or racial occupancy generally leads to instability and a reduction of values." Appraisers were instructed to assign higher ratings to properties with "protection against some adverse influences," with "adverse influences" defined to include "infiltration by inharmonious racial or nationality groups."

The **1938 Underwriting Manual** went further, explicitly recommending that municipalities enact racially restrictive zoning ordinances and recommending "restrictive covenants that prohibit the occupancy of properties except by the race for which they are intended."

The practical consequence: **between 1945 and 1959, African Americans received less than 2 percent of all federally insured home loans**. The FHA had been established precisely to make homeownership accessible and affordable; it achieved this goal for white families while systematically excluding Black families from the same wealth-building mechanism for 34 years (1934-1968). The Mapping Inequality project, which digitized the HOLC maps, identified that areas graded "D" or "hazardous" in the 1930s and 1940s continue to show higher poverty rates, lower property values, higher environmental contamination levels, and worse health outcomes in current data — a persistence of nearly 90 years.

Sources: [Bill of Rights Institute: FHA Underwriting Manual (1938)](https://billofrightsinstitute.org/activities/federal-housing-administration-fha-underwriting-manual-1938/); [Federal Reserve History: Redlining](https://www.federalreservehistory.org/essays/redlining); [Boston Fair Housing: 1934-1968 FHA Mortgage Insurance Requirements Utilize Redlining](https://www.bostonfairhousing.org/timeline/1934-1968-FHA-Redlining.html)

### 2.2 GI Bill Exclusion: Mechanisms and Scale

Approximately **1.2 million Black veterans** served in World War II. The Servicemen's Readjustment Act of 1944 — the GI Bill — was facially race-neutral but was structured to be administered by individual states, at the insistence of Southern Democratic members of Congress who explicitly feared that returning Black veterans would use public sympathy and newfound economic independence to challenge Jim Crow.

The exclusion operated through three interlocking mechanisms: First, **all-white VA offices** in the South refused to process claims from Black veterans or directed them to separate, inferior facilities with fewer staff. Second, **Jim Crow banks** refused to originate mortgages in Black neighborhoods or to white neighborhoods where Black families might move. Third, **racially segregated higher education** meant that Black veterans seeking to use the education benefit were turned away from white colleges and were limited to underfunded historically Black colleges and universities (HBCUs) that could accommodate only a fraction of applicants.

The documented exclusion is not anecdotal. A **1947 survey of 13 Mississippi cities** found that of 3,229 Veterans Administration home loans authorized for veterans in those cities, **exactly two went to Black veterans**. Brandeis University researchers calculated that the wealth advantage conferred on white veterans by full GI Bill access — adjusted to current dollars — amounts to approximately **$180,000 per white veteran** more than their Black counterparts received from the same legislation.

The long-run consequence: the GI Bill financed the suburban white middle class. The Levittowns and comparable suburban developments that became the foundation of postwar white wealth accumulation were explicitly restricted to white purchasers through racially restrictive covenants that the FHA encouraged. Black veterans, excluded from these benefits, remained concentrated in urban areas subject to simultaneous disinvestment through urban renewal.

Sources: [History.com: How the GI Bill's Promise Was Denied to a Million Black WWII Veterans](https://www.history.com/articles/gi-bill-black-wwii-veterans-benefits); [NPR: Black Vets Were Excluded from GI Bill Benefits](https://www.npr.org/2022/10/18/1129735948/black-vets-were-excluded-from-gi-bill-benefits-a-bill-in-congress-aims-to-fix-that); [Brandeis Heller School: Impact Report on GI Bill (2023)](https://heller.brandeis.edu/news/items/releases/2023/impact-report-gi-bill.html)

### 2.3 Urban Renewal: Displacement by the Numbers

Between 1950 and mid-1971, the federal urban renewal program — authorized by the Housing Act of 1949 — displaced approximately **334,000 families** and **169,000 single-person households**, for a total of roughly **1.36 million individuals**. Approximately **60 percent of those displaced were nonwhite**, in cities where Black residents constituted a smaller fraction of the total population. The displacement was not incidental to the program's goals; it was frequently its purpose. Cities used urban renewal to clear Black neighborhoods for highways, universities, convention centers, sports arenas, and light-industrial zones.

The concentration of displacement in Black communities was extreme. In Newark, which was 34.1 percent Black in 1960, **77 percent of persons displaced by urban renewal were Black**. In Cincinnati's Kenyon Barr project, which cleared the city's West End neighborhood, **more than 97 percent of the 4,953 displaced families were Black**. Between 1949 and 1972, approximately **105,000 businesses** were displaced by urban renewal, most of them small and family-owned, with roughly 40 percent discontinuing business entirely following displacement.

James Baldwin's 1963 characterization of urban renewal as "Negro removal" — a phrase he said was "already common in the black community" — named what the data confirm: a federally funded program that operated as a wealth-extraction mechanism in Black communities, destroying the neighborhood equity and business infrastructure that constituted the primary asset base for families excluded from suburban homeownership. A 2023 Russell Sage Foundation study, "Using Urban Renewal Records to Advance Reparative Justice," documented that many of the displaced families were never relocated to equivalent housing and that the concentrated poverty of contemporary urban Black neighborhoods can be directly traced through these displacement records.

Sources: [RSF Journal: Using Urban Renewal Records to Advance Reparative Justice (2023)](https://www.rsfjournal.org/content/10/2/113); [Boston Review: Tearing Down Black America](https://www.bostonreview.net/articles/brent-cebul-tearing-down-black-america/); [Berkeley ITS Library: Remembering James Baldwin and Urban Renewal](https://itslibrary.berkeley.edu/news/remembering-james-baldwin-and-urban-renewal)

### 2.4 Contract Buying in Chicago: Mechanics and Scale of Extraction

Contract buying was a predatory land-contract scheme operated in Chicago and other Northern cities that functioned as an alternative to conventional mortgage lending in Black neighborhoods where banks would not lend. Between **75 and 95 percent of homes sold to Black families** in Chicago during the 1950s and 1960s were sold on contract rather than through conventional mortgages. The mechanics exploited the denial of conventional financing that segregation produced.

Under a land contract, the buyer made a large down payment and monthly installments at high interest rates, but did not receive the deed until the contract was fully paid — often over 20 or 30 years. Any single missed payment — from illness, job loss, or a cost-of-living crisis — allowed the seller to evict the buyer and retain all payments made. The markup was documented: **homes sold on contract carried an average 84 percent price premium** over their market value. Black contract buyers paid on average **$587 more per month** (in current dollars) than they would have under a conventional mortgage for the same property.

Historian **Beryl Satter**'s 2009 book "Family Properties: Race, Real Estate, and the Exploitation of Black Urban America" documented the mechanics through her own family's involvement on both sides of the system, drawing on Contract Buyers League records from the late 1960s. The Contract Buyers League — formed in North Lawndale in 1968 — organized contract buyers to collectively withhold payments and renegotiate terms, and achieved significant success through direct action before the system was dismantled. WBEZ Chicago's 2019 investigation calculated that **Black families in Chicago lost between $3 billion and $4 billion in wealth** because of predatory housing contracts during this period.

Sources: [WBEZ Chicago: Contract Buying Robbed Black Families in Chicago of Billions](https://www.wbez.org/money/2019/05/30/contract-buying-robbed-black-families-in-chicago-of-billions); [Contract Buyers League Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contract_Buyers_League); [The Chicago Reporter: Inside the Contract Buyers League's Fight Against Housing Discrimination](https://www.chicagoreporter.com/inside-the-contract-buyers-leagues-fight-against-housing-discrimination/)

### 2.5 Mass Incarceration as Wealth Destruction

Mass incarceration functions as a wealth-destruction mechanism operating with documented racial disparity. The US Sentencing Commission's **2023 Demographic Differences in Federal Sentencing** report found that among defendants who received sentences within the applicable guidelines range, **Black male defendants' sentences were 7.9 percent longer** than those of similarly situated white defendants. When accounting for prior violence history, the gap reaches **20.4 percent longer sentences**. Federal prosecutors file charges carrying mandatory minimum sentences **65 percent more often against Black defendants** than other defendants holding all other factors constant.

The wealth consequences of incarceration are severe and disproportionate. Brennan Center research found that **time in prison reduces lifetime earning potential by nearly $500,000 on average**. Beyond direct earnings loss, **having an incarcerated family member reduces household assets by 64.3 percent** — a finding from Russell Sage Foundation research covering 1996-2011 that documents the family-wide wealth destruction that single incarcerations produce. Fees, fines, and collateral consequences follow formerly incarcerated individuals for decades, compounding initial wealth destruction.

The racial arithmetic is straightforward: Black Americans are imprisoned at **5.0 times the rate of white Americans** (2021 data, Sentencing Project). Among Black men born in 2001, the lifetime likelihood of imprisonment is four times that of white male counterparts. Given the documented per-incarceration wealth loss of $500,000, the aggregate wealth transfer from these disparate incarceration rates — applied across millions of individuals over decades — constitutes one of the largest ongoing mechanisms of racial wealth destruction in the contemporary economy.

Sources: [US Sentencing Commission: 2023 Demographic Differences in Federal Sentencing](https://www.ussc.gov/research/research-reports/2023-demographic-differences-federal-sentencing); [Brennan Center: Conviction, Imprisonment, and Lost Earnings](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/conviction-imprisonment-and-lost-earnings-how-involvement-criminal); [Sentencing Project: One in Five — Ending Racial Inequity in Incarceration](https://www.sentencingproject.org/reports/one-in-five-ending-racial-inequity-in-incarceration/)

## Section 3: HR 40 and Commission Models — Evidence on Efficacy

### 3.1 HR 40 Legislative History: 36 Years of Reintroduction

**HR 40** — the Commission to Study and Develop Reparation Proposals for African Americans Act — was first introduced by Representative **John Conyers** (D-MI) in the 100th Congress in 1989. The bill number was chosen to reference the Reconstruction-era promise of "40 acres and a mule." Conyers reintroduced the bill in every subsequent Congress for nearly 30 years until his retirement in 2017. For most of that period the bill did not receive a committee hearing.

The bill gained momentum under **Representative Sheila Jackson Lee** (D-TX), who took up sponsorship and secured the bill's first full Judiciary Committee hearing and committee approval in April 2021 — 32 years after its introduction. Jackson Lee died in July 2024. **Representative Ayanna Pressley** (D-MA) reintroduced HR 40 on the first day of the 119th Congress, January 3, 2025. In February 2025, **Senator Cory Booker** (D-NJ) introduced the Senate companion bill during Black History Month — framed explicitly as a counterweight to the Trump administration's dismantling of DEI programs. As of April 2026, HR 40 has the support of more than **85 members of Congress** and more than **100 national and grassroots organizations**.

The bill's current text establishes a **Commission to Study and Develop Reparation Proposals for African Americans**, tasked with examining slavery and discrimination in the colonies and the United States from 1619 to the present and recommending appropriate remedies. The commission structure — study first, payment design second — is the same model that produced actual payments in the Japanese American redress program and the Rosewood, Florida settlement.

Sources: [Congress.gov: HR 40, 119th Congress](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/40); [Pressley.house.gov: Ahead of Juneteenth, Momentum Grows for HR 40 (June 2025)](https://pressley.house.gov/2025/06/18/ahead-of-juneteenth-momentum-grows-for-h-r-40-pressleys-historic-reparations-legislation/); [Pressley.house.gov: Reintroduction During Black History Month (February 2025)](https://pressley.house.gov/2025/02/12/amid-onslaught-on-dei-pressley-booker-colleagues-reintroduce-historic-reparations-bill-during-black-history-month/)

### 3.2 The BRAC Model: How Binding Recommendations Work

The Base Realignment and Closure (BRAC) Commission model — referenced in Domain 22 — provides a well-documented mechanism for Congress to accept findings and recommendations it might otherwise block through normal legislative inertia. Under BRAC, an independent commission studies military base structure and submits a package of closure and realignment recommendations to the President and Congress. The recommendations take effect automatically unless Congress passes a joint resolution of disapproval within a specified window. The key innovation: the default outcome is *adoption*, not rejection. Congress must act to block rather than act to approve.

This default-to-action structure directly addresses the political economy problem that has prevented reparations legislation for 36 years. Under normal congressional procedure, any legislation can be killed by inaction — a single committee chair blocking a hearing, a Senate filibuster, majority leadership declining to schedule floor time. The BRAC model reverses this: once the commission submits its findings, Congress must affirmatively vote to reject rather than affirmatively vote to adopt. Four BRAC rounds (1988, 1991, 1993, 1995) successfully closed or realigned bases over congressional resistance because the default outcome worked in favor of action.

Applied to reparations, a commission with subpoena power to document harms, followed by a binding congressional vote on the commission's recommendations within a defined window — modeled on BRAC's joint resolution mechanism — would force a recorded vote rather than allowing the issue to disappear in parliamentary procedure.

Sources: [ACLU: HR 40 Is Not a Symbolic Act — It's a Path to Restorative Justice](https://www.aclu.org/news/racial-justice/h-r-40-is-not-a-symbolic-act-its-a-path-to-restorative-justice); [Reparations Commission: What Is HR 40?](https://reparationscomm.org/hr-40/)

### 3.3 U.S. Precedents: When Commissions Led to Action vs. Stalled

**Japanese American internment (action model)**: The **Commission on Wartime Relocation and Internment of Civilians (CWRIC)** was established by Congress in 1980, 38 years after Executive Order 9066. The commission held 20 days of hearings across the country, heard testimony from 750 witnesses, and produced a 1983 report ("Personal Justice Denied") that found the internment was not justified by military necessity and was "the result of race prejudice, war hysteria, and a failure of political leadership." The commission recommended a formal apology and $20,000 payment per surviving internee. Congress passed the **Civil Liberties Act of 1988** — with bipartisan support and signed by President Reagan — and payments began on **October 9, 1990**. Over $1.6 billion was paid to more than **82,250 persons of Japanese ancestry** before the Office of Redress Administration closed in **February 1999**. Key causal factors: a fully documented evidentiary record; a defined eligible population; a per-person payment that was politically legible; and the act of the U.S. government against U.S. residents (not a diffuse historical harm).

**Rosewood, Florida (action model, limited)**: In January 1923, a white mob destroyed the all-Black town of Rosewood, Florida, killing an unknown number of residents and driving survivors away permanently. In 1994 — 71 years later — the Florida Legislature passed the **Rosewood Compensation Act**, awarding $2.1 million: $150,000 lump sums to each of nine documented survivors, a $500,000 pool for descendants, and $4,000 scholarships for the youngest generation of descendants. The legislation followed a commission study that documented the massacre and confirmed survivors' identities. Florida's action remained incomplete — the amount was far below documented losses — but it represents the only state-level legislative reparations for a specific racial massacre to result in direct payments.

**1921 Tulsa Race Massacre (stalled)**: The 1921 Tulsa Race Massacre destroyed the Greenwood District ("Black Wall Street"), killed an estimated 300 people, and displaced 10,000. A state commission documented the massacre's history in 2001. Subsequent state and federal legislation providing compensation stalled repeatedly; a 2021 lawsuit seeking reparations for three remaining survivors was dismissed on statute of limitations grounds by an Oklahoma judge in 2023. No payments have been made. The contrast with the Rosewood outcome — the same general structure (commission, documentation, legislation), different political outcomes — illustrates that the commission step is necessary but not sufficient.

Sources: [National WWII Museum: Redress and Reparations for Japanese American Incarceration](https://www.nationalww2museum.org/war/articles/redress-and-reparations-japanese-american-incarceration); [DOJ: Ten-Year Program to Compensate Japanese Americans Closed Its Doors (February 1999)](https://www.justice.gov/archive/opa/pr/1999/February/059cr.htm); [TIME: What a Florida Reparations Case Can Teach Us About Justice in America](https://time.com/5887247/reparations-america-rosewood-massacre/)

### 3.4 Evanston, Illinois: Implementation in Detail

Evanston, Illinois — a Chicago suburb of 75,000 — became the first U.S. municipality to establish a functioning government reparations program in March 2021, when the City Council approved the framework. Key implementation details:

**Funding mechanism**: The City pledged a total of **$20 million** to the Local Reparations Fund, drawn from two sources: (1) a 3 percent municipal cannabis retailers' occupation tax on gross cannabis sales, with the first **$10 million** dedicated to reparations; and (2) **$10 million** from the real estate transfer tax. The cannabis tax has underperformed initial projections due to lower-than-expected sales at Evanston's two dispensaries, creating funding gaps that delayed the program.

**Eligibility**: Direct Descendants of Black Evanston residents who lived in the city between 1919 and 1969 (the period of documented city-enforced housing discrimination) and can document that history. Descendants must apply and document lineage.

**Payment structure**: The first phase provided **$25,000** per eligible recipient, which recipients can direct toward homeownership (down payment, mortgage assistance, home improvement), or receive as direct cash. The City assigned recipients numbers sequentially; as of February 2026, residents numbered 127 through 171 — **44 recipients** — were notified that their payments were ready. Staff anticipated disbursing funds to at least 80 additional Direct Descendants in 2024, though implementation delays shifted timelines.

**Implementation challenges**: The cannabis tax revenue shortfall is the primary bottleneck. A Russell Sage Foundation analysis (2024) also identified tensions in the program's design — critics from within the Black community argued that housing-directed payments (original framework) limited recipient autonomy, leading to the inclusion of direct cash as an option. Genealogical documentation requirements have excluded some descendants who lack surviving records from the segregation era.

Sources: [City of Evanston Local Reparations](https://www.cityofevanston.org/government/city-council/reparations); [Evanston RoundTable: Over 40 Reparations Payments Expected to Be Given Out by Summer (February 2026)](https://evanstonroundtable.com/2026/02/05/over-40-reparations-payments-expected-to-be-given-out-by-summer/); [NPR: Evanston Approves Reparations for Black Residents (March 2021)](https://www.npr.org/2021/03/23/980277688/in-likely-first-chicago-suburb-of-evanston-approves-reparations-for-black-reside); [RSF Journal: The Politics of Expedience — Evanston and the Fight for Reparations (2024)](https://www.rsfjournal.org/content/10/3/114)

### 3.5 California Reparations Task Force: What Actually Happened

California's Reparations Task Force, established by AB 3121 (2020), submitted its final report in June 2023 recommending a comprehensive reparations program the report estimated at approximately **$800 billion** for eligible recipients. The California Legislative Black Caucus subsequently introduced a **14-bill 2024 Reparations Priority Bill Package** translating the report's findings into legislation.

**What passed**: Governor Newsom signed **AB 3089** on September 26, 2024 — a formal state apology for California's role in perpetuating slavery, segregation, and racial discrimination, memorialized with a Capitol plaque. Several other bills in the package became law, addressing discrimination in hairstyles (AB 1815), advance notice for grocery and pharmacy closures in underserved communities (SB 1089), and expansions of preschool access (AB 51) and maternal health protections (AB 2319).

**What failed**: The bills most directly tied to the task force's core wealth-transfer recommendations did not survive: **SB 1007** (homeownership assistance for descendants of slaves) and **SB 1013** (property tax relief for descendants of slaves) both failed in committee. Governor Newsom, while signing the apology bill, did not support direct cash payments and expressed concerns about the fiscal scale, reflecting the political economy problem that a study commission's recommendations alone do not overcome.

**The lesson for HR 40 design**: California demonstrates that a commission can generate credible evidentiary and policy foundations, shift discourse, and produce incremental legislation — but that direct payment programs require the additional forcing mechanism of a binding vote timeline, which the BRAC model provides and which California's voluntary legislative process did not.

## Section 4: Baby Bonds — Evidence Base

### 4.1 Hamilton and Darity Modeling: What the 80-90% Figure Means

**Darrick Hamilton** and **William "Sandy" Darity Jr.** first published the baby bonds proposal in 2010, framing it as a "endowment for all Americans at birth" funded by the federal government on a progressive income scale. The proposal was designed to target the wealth gap structurally, rather than the income gap, because income transfer programs (EITC, minimum wage, SNAP) do not accumulate across generations the way wealth does.

The modeling by **Naomi Zewde** (2019, published in the RSF journal), analyzing the Hamilton-Darity proposal structure, projected that baby bonds would reduce the **median racial wealth gap between young white and Black Americans from a factor of 16 to a factor of 1.4** — approximately an 80 to 90 percent reduction — for the cohort that reaches adulthood after a full implementation cycle. The mechanism: each child born receives a federally held account seeded at birth with $500 to $50,000 on a progressive scale (larger for children from lower-wealth families), earning a guaranteed 2 percent annual return, accessible at age 18 for approved wealth-building purposes (education, homeownership, business formation, retirement savings). Because lower-wealth families are disproportionately Black, the progressive structure produces racial wealth convergence even through a formally race-neutral mechanism.

The critical design distinction — often misunderstood in political debate — is that the 80-90 percent reduction figure applies to **income-based, progressive formulations**. A flat baby bond (same amount for all children regardless of family wealth) produces much smaller racial wealth gap reduction because it provides the same benefit to wealthy white children and wealth-poor Black children. The progressivity is load-bearing in the modeling.

Sources: [Center for American Progress: Baby Bonds — A Worthwhile Step to Reduce the Racial Wealth Gap](https://www.americanprogress.org/article/baby-bonds-a-worthwhile-step-to-reduce-the-racial-wealth-gap/); [TIME: The Architects of Baby Bonds Are Finally Seeing Momentum](https://time.com/collections/closers/6564916/darrick-hamilton-william-darity/); [INET Economics: Can Baby Bonds Fight the Wealth Gap?](https://www.ineteconomics.org/perspectives/blog/can-baby-bonds-fight-the-wealth-gap-and-propel-racial-equality-connecticut-aims-to-find-out)

### 4.2 Connecticut Baby Bonds (2021): Design and Status

**Connecticut** became the first U.S. state to enact a baby bonds program on **June 30, 2021**. Since **July 1, 2023**, eligibility is automatic for every newborn covered by **HUSKY** (Connecticut's Medicaid program). The state invests **$3,200** in the Connecticut Baby Bonds Trust on behalf of each eligible child at birth. At projected growth rates, the accounts are expected to range between **$11,000 and $24,000** when each child reaches adulthood. Participants can access their account between ages 18 and 30 for approved purposes: post-secondary education, a first home purchase, launching a business, or retirement savings contributions.

The program targets approximately **15,000 births per year**. Connecticut's program is means-tested through Medicaid eligibility rather than explicitly race-conscious — which means its racial wealth gap reduction effect will depend on how closely Medicaid enrollment correlates with racial demographics in the state. Early analysis suggests the program will disproportionately benefit Black and Hispanic children (who are overrepresented in Medicaid enrollment), but the program's scale ($3,200 seed amount) is substantially smaller than the federal proposal ($2,000 per year in the Domain 22 architecture, which compounds substantially larger).

**Washington, D.C.'s Baby Bonds** program, funded separately, provides up to **$1,000 per year** for eligible DC children, with similar Medicaid-based eligibility. Multiple states have pursued or enacted baby bond legislation since Connecticut's pioneering program, reflecting the Overton window shift toward wealth-building policy.

### 4.3 UK Child Trust Fund (2005-2010): Outcomes Data

The United Kingdom's **Child Trust Fund** — a universal baby bonds program — provides the only existing large-scale outcomes evidence for the policy model before full maturation. The Labour government launched the CTF in 2005: every child born in the UK from September 2002 forward received a government-seeded account of £250 (£500 for lower-income families), with additional top-ups at age 7 and the opportunity for family contributions. Approximately **6.3 million accounts** were opened. The Conservative-Liberal Democrat coalition government abolished the program in January 2011 (new enrollment) as a budget measure.

The **outcomes data** now available as the first cohort of CTF holders turns 18 is instructive:

**Account maturation**: The first CTF accounts matured in 2020. Research by the Share Foundation and the Resolution Foundation found that approximately **one-third of lower-income CTF holders** — the population most likely to benefit from a wealth-building windfall — did not access their accounts within the first year of eligibility because they were unaware the accounts existed or unable to navigate the retrieval process. This finding has direct implications for U.S. program design: automatic notification, simplified access pathways, and financial literacy support are essential to ensuring the accounts reach intended beneficiaries.

**Wealth-building use**: Among CTF holders who did access their accounts at maturity, higher-income CTF holders were more likely to transfer the funds into longer-term savings or investment accounts, while lower-income holders were more likely to spend the funds immediately on consumption or education costs. This divergence suggests that complementary financial capability support and restricted account structures (as in Connecticut's baby bonds, which require approved wealth-building uses) may improve wealth-building outcomes for the target population.

**Policy lesson**: The UK program was too small in initial seeding (£250) and too administratively fragile (one-third unreachable at maturity) to produce measurable aggregate wealth gap effects. The U.S. proposal's substantially larger account values ($2,000 per year for 18 years, compounding) and approved-use restrictions are designed to address both limitations.

**Scotland's program continues**: Scotland launched its own **Scottish Child Payment** (£25/week for children in low-income families) and has continued universal wealth-building account proposals after England ended the CTF. Scottish child poverty data show meaningful reductions since the Scottish Child Payment launched in 2021.

Sources: [INET Economics: Can Baby Bonds Fight the Wealth Gap?](https://www.ineteconomics.org/perspectives/blog/can-baby-bonds-fight-the-wealth-gap-and-propel-racial-equality-connecticut-aims-to-find-out); [Baby Bonds Wikipedia (UK CTF section)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baby_bonds)

## Section 5: Civil Rights Infrastructure — The Enforcement Gap

### 5.1 DOJ Civil Rights Division: Capacity and Current Degradation

The **DOJ Civil Rights Division (CRT)** was established in 1957 and operates across nine sections covering voting, housing, employment, disability, education, and immigrant rights. The Division's budget in the proposal's current baseline is approximately **$170 million** — substantially below what full enforcement would require.

The current crisis compounds the structural underfunding. As of mid-2025, the Trump administration's targeting of the Civil Rights Division resulted in the Division shedding **more than 60 percent of its workforce** through deferred resignation offers and terminations. Government Executive reported in August 2025 that after losing most employees, DOJ was looking to shift staff around the Civil Rights Division to fill "deep need" in remaining functions. A Division operating at 40 percent of prior staffing cannot maintain existing enforcement workloads, let alone address the documented backlog of civil rights complaints.

### 5.2 EEOC: Backlog, Dismissal Rates, and Staff Attrition

The **Equal Employment Opportunity Commission** received **88,531 new charges** in FY2024 — a 9.2 percent increase from the prior year. The EEOC filed only **111 lawsuits** in FY2024, resolving 132 cases and obtaining approximately **$40 million in monetary relief** — less than $0.05 per dollar of estimated annual employment discrimination harm. A 2023 House Republican report flagged the EEOC's **20 percent backlog** as "alarming"; the pending charge inventory had grown from 42,811 to 51,399 between FY2021 and FY2022 and has not been cleared.

The practical consequence of backlog: the EEOC issues **"right to sue" letters** — authorization for charging parties to file private lawsuits — for cases it lacks capacity to investigate. Center for Public Integrity reporting documented that **more and more workplace discrimination cases are being closed before they are even investigated**, with the EEOC's capacity constraints shifting the burden to individual claimants who must finance their own discrimination lawsuits. Under a hiring freeze as of mid-2024, EEOC Chair Charlotte Burrows acknowledged the agency was facing a "very big task" with insufficient staff.

### 5.3 HUD Fair Housing and the Disparate Impact Rollback

The **Fair Housing Act's disparate impact standard** — which allows plaintiffs to challenge housing policies that have discriminatory effects even without proving discriminatory intent — was affirmed by the Supreme Court in **Texas Department of Housing and Community Affairs v. Inclusive Communities Project** (2015) by a 5-4 vote. Justice Kennedy's majority opinion upheld the theory but imposed a burden-shifting framework that narrowed it: plaintiffs must identify a specific policy rather than a statistical disparity alone, and defendants can rebut by showing the policy serves a "substantial, legitimate, nondiscriminatory interest."

The Trump administration moved to eliminate the disparate impact standard entirely. **HUD submitted a draft final rule to OMB on August 4, 2025**, proposing to remove its Fair Housing Act disparate impact regulation from the Code of Federal Regulations. Comments closed February 13, 2026. If finalized, this removal would return the Fair Housing Act enforcement framework to an intent-only standard — the weakest possible test for systemic discrimination that operates through neutral-appearing policies.

### 5.3b Algorithmic Discrimination: Three Documented Cases

Three cases ground the abstract concern about algorithmic discrimination in concrete evidence:

**COMPAS (Northpointe/Equivant)**: ProPublica's 2016 "Machine Bias" investigation analyzed COMPAS risk scores for 10,000-plus criminal defendants in Broward County, Florida. Findings: Black defendants were **77 percent more likely** to be assigned a higher risk score for violent recidivism than white defendants with identical criminal histories, age, and gender. **45 percent more likely** to receive a higher general recidivism risk score. The algorithm's overall accuracy was only 61 percent. Northpointe responded that the algorithm satisfied "predictive parity" (equal accuracy by race) — which is mathematically incompatible with "equal error rates" (equal false positive rates by race) when base rates differ between groups. Researchers confirmed both statements can be true simultaneously, meaning that no algorithm can simultaneously satisfy both definitions of fairness when underlying recidivism rates differ across groups. The fairness problem is not correctable by technical improvement — it is structural.

**Amazon hiring algorithm (2018)**: Amazon built and quietly scrapped an AI recruiting tool that had trained on a decade of hiring decisions, which were predominantly for male candidates in technical roles. The algorithm penalized resumes containing the word "women's" (as in "women's chess club captain") and downgraded graduates of two all-women's colleges. Amazon disbanded the team in 2017 and disclosed the program's existence in a Reuters report in October 2018.

**Housing algorithms**: The National Fair Housing Alliance filed a 2015 HUD complaint against **Fannie Mae** documenting that the GSE maintained its foreclosed properties in predominantly white neighborhoods significantly better than in communities of color — with consequences for neighborhood property values and appraisal disparities. Separately, advocacy organizations have documented that automated valuation models (AVMs) used in mortgage underwriting encode historical appraisal discrimination: an AVM trained on historical home sale data will undervalue properties in neighborhoods where comparable properties were historically undervalued due to redlining, perpetuating the original discrimination algorithmically.

### 5.4 John Lewis VRA: Current Status and the Preclearance Gap

The **John R. Lewis Voting Rights Advancement Act of 2025** was introduced in the House (HR 14) on the first day of the 119th Congress, and in the Senate by Senators Durbin and Warnock on **July 29, 2025**. As of April 2026, the bill has not advanced in a Republican-controlled Senate. The significance for racial justice enforcement: the Voting Rights Act's Section 5 preclearance requirement — struck down by *Shelby County v. Holder* (2013) — was the single most effective civil rights enforcement mechanism in U.S. history for protecting Black voting rights. Between 1965 and 2013, preclearance blocked more than 1,000 discriminatory voting changes submitted by covered states and jurisdictions. Since *Shelby County*, researchers documented more than 94 restrictive voting laws in 29 states, concentrated in the formerly covered jurisdictions, affecting disproportionately Black, Latino, and Native American voters.

The John Lewis VRA would restore preclearance with updated coverage criteria based on documented recent voting rights violations, rather than the 1972 turnout formula that *Shelby County* found outdated. It would also strengthen Section 2 (disparate impact) vote dilution claims and expand bilingual voting materials requirements. Under current conditions — Republican Senate, Trump administration DOJ — passage is not near-term viable. The evidentiary record it would respond to continues to develop: the Brennan Center documented in April 2026 that mid-decade redistricting in Texas and other states is proceeding specifically to dilute Black and Latino voting power in ways that would have been blocked under preclearance.

### 5.5 State-Level Civil Rights Variation: The Enforcement Baseline Problem

A federal civil rights enforcement floor is essential because state-level enforcement varies dramatically, and in many states the federal floor has been the only meaningful protection. As of 2025:

**States with stronger-than-federal baseline**: California's Department of Fair Employment and Housing (now Civil Rights Department) has a private right of action, broader protected categories than Title VII, and a three-year statute of limitations vs. Title VII's 180-day EEOC filing deadline. New York's Human Rights Law applies to employers with four or more employees (vs. Title VII's 15-employee threshold) and covers a broader range of protected characteristics. Illinois, Washington, and Massachusetts maintain similar expansions.

**States with weaker enforcement**: Multiple Southern and Mountain West states have no state civil rights agencies, rely entirely on federal enforcement, and have enacted state laws limiting civil rights litigation. When the federal Civil Rights Division loses 60 percent of its staff, as it did in 2025, communities in these states lose virtually all civil rights enforcement capacity.

**The algorithmic discrimination gap**: No state has yet enacted comprehensive algorithmic discrimination protections covering employment, housing, and criminal justice simultaneously. The EU AI Act (Article 6, high-risk AI systems in employment and criminal justice) provides the most comprehensive existing model — requiring transparency, human oversight, conformity assessment, and bias testing for AI systems used in hiring, credit, housing, and risk assessment decisions. The Domain 22 algorithmic discrimination ban follows this model. As of April 2026, the EU's enforcement of the AI Act against high-risk systems is in its early stages (February 2025 enforcement trigger), providing limited outcomes data but a clear legislative architecture.

## Section 6: International Comparisons — Granular Evidence

### 6.1 Germany's Claims Conference: Structure and Scale

The **Conference on Jewish Material Claims Against Germany (Claims Conference)**, established in 1951, has become the world's most sustained and administratively complex reparations program. It operates under a series of bilateral agreements between Germany and Jewish successor organizations and individuals, negotiated annually.

**Total paid**: The Claims Conference has secured more than **$90 billion** in payments since 1951 — combining direct individual compensation, social welfare services for survivors, restitution of specific property, and support for Holocaust education and documentation. **2024 payments**: Germany agreed to approximately **$1.4 billion** for 2024, comprising $535 million in direct compensation payments and approximately $890 million toward home care services for elderly survivors. One-time additional payments continue through 2027. As of 2024, more than **128,000 Holocaust survivors** globally receive ongoing compensation.

**Administrative structure**: The Claims Conference negotiates annually with the German government, administers direct payment programs across multiple categories (Hardship Fund, Article 2 Fund for former concentration camp inmates, Central Fund for survivors in Eastern Europe), and allocates funds to social welfare agencies serving survivors in more than 80 countries. The administrative cost ratio is low by program standards — Claims Conference administers payments reaching hundreds of thousands of individuals across international jurisdictions.

**The evidentiary foundation that made it work**: The 1952 Luxembourg Agreement (the founding reparations agreement) did not wait for a commission to establish that the Holocaust occurred. Germany's postwar government accepted responsibility as a condition of reintegration into the Western alliance and international legitimacy — a political-economic forcing mechanism that has no direct parallel in the U.S. context but that illustrates how geopolitical incentive structures shaped German willingness to pay that domestic political processes might have blocked.

Sources: [Claims Conference: Compensation Payment Programs](https://www.claimscon.org/our-work/compensation/); [NPR: Holocaust Survivors to Receive $1.4 Billion in Payments from Germany (June 2023)](https://www.npr.org/2023/06/15/1182428154/germany-holocaust-survivors-payment-1-4-billion-nazi-victims); [Claims Conference Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claims_Conference)

### 6.2 Canada Residential Schools: Settlement and Implementation Status

The **Indian Residential Schools Settlement Agreement** (2006), the largest class action settlement in Canadian history, allocated approximately **$2 billion** in individual compensation to survivors of the government-funded, church-operated residential school system that operated from the 1870s through 1996. Approximately **150,000 First Nations, Métis, and Inuit children** were separated from their families and forced to attend; the settlement covered documented physical and sexual abuse through two payment streams: a Common Experience Payment for any survivor who attended (averaging $28,000) and an Independent Assessment Process for those with specific abuse claims (averaging $115,000).

The **Truth and Reconciliation Commission of Canada (TRC)**, established under the settlement, operated from 2008 to 2015, hearing testimony from more than 6,000 survivors at events across the country. Its 2015 final report produced **94 Calls to Action** — specific recommendations across child welfare, education, language, health, justice, and government accountability. As of the 2024-25 federal budget, the Canadian government reports that **more than 85 percent** of the Calls to Action involving the federal government (76 of the 94) are "completed or well underway." Budget 2024 allocated $91 million over two years for specific Calls to Action (74-76), and more than **$246.8 million** has flowed to Indigenous communities for missing children investigations following the 2021 discovery of unmarked graves at former residential school sites.

**What this teaches**: Canada's program combined individual financial compensation (through settlement), institutional acknowledgment (through TRC), and specific action commitments (94 Calls to Action) — a three-layer architecture that the Domain 22 truth commission proposal mirrors. The implementation gap — 85 percent of federally involved Calls to Action completed or underway after nine years — also illustrates the long timeframe required for comprehensive implementation and the value of specific, numbered commitments that create accountability.

### 6.3 New Zealand Treaty of Waitangi: Adjudication and Co-Governance

New Zealand's Treaty settlement process — administered through Te Arawhiti (the Office for Māori Crown Relations) — has negotiated and enacted **86 deeds of settlement** as of early 2023, totaling approximately **NZ$2.6 billion** (roughly US$1.5 billion) in financial redress. Settlements typically include three components: a formal Crown apology; the return of specific Crown-owned land within the claimant group's traditional area; and financial compensation for losses from Treaty breaches.

**Co-governance** — shared governance authority over specific natural resources — has emerged as a significant redress mechanism in New Zealand, representing a form of restoration that cannot be reduced to monetary payment. The **Rotorua Te Arawa Lakes** settlement established the first co-governance model between iwi (tribal groups) and local councils over natural resources. The **Waikato-Tainui** settlement (1995) was the first modern Treaty settlement, providing NZ$170 million and substantial land return; research on the settlement found that the financial and land base enabled the iwi to develop significant economic assets and institutions in subsequent decades.

**A critical limitation**: researchers have noted that $2.6 billion in total redress is estimated to represent **as little as 1 percent of the true value of historical land loss** — the Crown purchased or confiscated vast territories for fractions of their value or without compensation at all. The settlement process addresses the legal claim while falling far short of full restitution. This limitation is relevant to U.S. design: a commission that sets reasonable rather than maximally accurate harm estimates may be more politically viable while still being substantially under-corrective.

### 6.4 South Africa TRC: What Actually Happened and Why It Is a Cautionary Model

South Africa's **Truth and Reconciliation Commission** (1995-2002), chaired by Archbishop Desmond Tutu, is frequently cited as the global model for transitional justice. What it actually produced in reparations terms is a cautionary case study.

The TRC recommended a comprehensive reparations program with a total cost of approximately **US$375 million** — including individual payments, healthcare, education assistance, and community rehabilitation. President **Thabo Mbeki's government** in 2003 chose instead to make a one-time "urgent reparation" of **R30,000** (approximately **$4,000 at the time**) to each of approximately 17,000 recognized victims — a total payout of **R571.5 million (~$74 million)**, less than 20 percent of the TRC's recommended amount. No ongoing payments were established.

The consequences: as of 2024, more than **R2 billion sits unspent in the Presidents Fund** while many victims continue to live in poverty. The TRC's conditional amnesty program — which traded truth disclosure for amnesty from civil and criminal liability — effectively foreclosed subsequent legal accountability for perpetrators while delivering minimal financial redress to victims. Critics noted that South Africa's white minority retained its economic advantages, that no land redistribution occurred, and that the ANC government prioritized macroeconomic stability (and continued white business investment) over redistributive justice.

**The design lesson**: The South African model illustrates what happens when a truth commission produces findings without a binding payment mechanism: governments can acknowledge atrocity while declining to pay meaningful compensation. The BRAC-model binding vote is specifically designed to prevent the South African outcome — acknowledgment without redistribution.

**On the TRC's truth-gathering value despite its reparations failure**: The South African TRC's failure to deliver reparations does not mean truth commissions are without value. Archbishop Tutu's commission produced a documented historical record — 21,000 victim statements, testimony from over 7,000 perpetrators — that fundamentally changed public understanding of apartheid's operational mechanisms and individual human costs. Polling in South Africa from the post-TRC decade shows higher levels of cross-racial acknowledgment of apartheid crimes than in comparable countries without truth processes. The Domain 22 truth commission serves this epistemic function regardless of the binding payment outcome — and the BRAC mechanism is specifically designed to prevent the TRC's payments failure from recurring.

### 6.5 CARICOM Ten-Point Plan: Current Status

The **CARICOM Reparations Commission** adopted its Ten-Point Reparatory Justice Plan in 2014, originally targeting European governments for compensation for the trans-Atlantic slave trade and its aftermath. The plan covers full formal apologies; repatriation assistance; indigenous peoples development programs; cultural institutions; public health programs to address the legacy of "plantation diseases" (hypertension, diabetes); illiteracy eradication; African knowledge programs; psychological rehabilitation; technology transfer; and debt cancellation.

At the **50th Regular Meeting of CARICOM Heads of Government in February 2026**, CARICOM Prime Ministers tasked the Prime Ministerial Subcommittee on Reparations with reviewing a revised version of the Ten-Point Plan — updated to reflect "shifting global dynamics and incorporate emerging historical and scientific evidence" — and advising on priority actions for the **Commonwealth Heads of Government Meeting in Antigua and Barbuda** and other 2026 forums. The CARICOM Reparations Commission conducted a strategic advocacy visit to the United Kingdom in **November 2025**, seeking to advance negotiations.

A significant international precedent came on **March 25, 2026**, when the UN General Assembly adopted a Ghana-led resolution declaring the trafficking of enslaved Africans "the gravest crime against humanity" — with all CARICOM member states voting in favor. UK polling from 2025 found **63 percent of UK adults** support a formal apology to Caribbean nations and descendants, up 4 percent from 2024, and **40 percent support financial reparations**, also up 4 percent year-over-year.

## Section 7: Political Economy and Opposition Evidence

### 7.1 Public Opinion: Trend Lines and Framing Effects

Public support for reparations has increased substantially since 2000 but remains a minority position among the general public, with extreme polarization by race. Key data points:

- **Overall support**: 31-38 percent of Americans support reparations proposals in 2023-2024 polling, up from single digits in early 2000s surveys. A **2024 AAMC Center for Health Justice poll** found 36 percent of Americans support comprehensive reparations including apology, payments, scholarships, and healing services.
- **By race**: **75 percent of Black Americans** support reparations versus **25 percent of white Americans** (for company-paid reparations); **81 percent of white Americans** oppose monetary compensation for descendants of slaves. Latino support runs approximately 54 percent.
- **By party**: Close to **90 percent of Republicans** oppose federal reparations. Despite this, some survey designs find 41-58 percent of Republicans support specific reparations proposals when framed as community investment or infrastructure rather than direct payments — a significant framing effect that suggests the policy content matters more than the word "reparations."
- **By age**: Millennials are more likely than older Americans to support reparations, though a majority of millennials still oppose the policy when asked about direct cash payments.

**The framing gap is decisive**: The same populations that oppose "reparations" in polling frequently support the specific policy components — baby bonds, CDFI investment, homeownership assistance, civil rights enforcement — when presented independently. This finding has significant implications for legislative strategy: the Domain 22 Community Reinvestment Fund and baby bonds programs can build political coalitions under different framing than the word "reparations" triggers.

**The Overton window shift since Evanston (2021)**: Evanston's program created measurable national discourse change. Before March 2021, reparations were discussed as aspirational or academic in mainstream media. Within 18 months of Evanston's vote, Asheville, North Carolina; Providence, Rhode Island; Detroit, Michigan; and multiple other cities had established or funded reparations task forces or programs. More than 20 states introduced some form of reparations-related legislation between 2021 and 2024. California's $800 billion task force recommendation — treated as extreme when published — normalized the concept that the dollar scale of the harm is in the trillions. The Overton window evidence is relevant to national program design: municipal programs do not simply provide local benefits, they shift the frame of feasible federal action.

### 7.2 Race-Neutral Programs and Wealth Gap Persistence

The empirical record on whether race-neutral wealth-building programs close the racial wealth gap is clear: they do not. Every major race-neutral program enacted since 1968 — the EITC, SNAP, Medicaid, the Affordable Care Act, federal student loan programs — has reduced income volatility and improved health outcomes for Black Americans but has not narrowed the wealth gap, which remained at its 1963 ratio through 2022.

The mechanism is mathematically inherent: wealth compounds. A race-neutral program that raises income by the same amount for Black and white households improves absolute position for both groups equally in income terms, but because white households have higher baseline wealth and therefore more capital generating returns, the wealth gap continues to grow even as income gaps narrow. The 2009-2010 Recovery Act — the largest stimulus since the New Deal — raised economic output for all income groups but did not narrow the racial wealth gap. The expanded Child Tax Credit (2021 American Rescue Plan), which reduced child poverty to historic lows, similarly did not produce measurable racial wealth convergence because income transfers do not accumulate.

Hamilton and Darity's analysis found that **income-based baby bonds** (larger amounts for lower-income families) would reduce the racial wealth gap by approximately 80-90 percent in the modeled population. A **flat baby bond** (same amount for all children regardless of family income) would reduce the gap by approximately 35-50 percent — meaningful but substantially less effective. This distinction has significant fiscal implications: a flat universal baby bond costs roughly the same as a progressive one but produces far less racial wealth gap reduction per dollar spent.

**The affirmative action comparison**: Decades of affirmative action in higher education, while producing meaningful representation gains, did not close the wealth gap. This is the empirical test race-critics of reparations must answer: if the problem were individual-level discrimination that race-conscious programs could address, affirmative action would have narrowed the wealth gap. It did not. The mechanism of harm is wealth, not opportunity — and wealth requires wealth-based remediation.

### 7.3 Contested Findings and Methodological Disputes

Rigorous engagement with reparations evidence requires acknowledging the contested literature.

**The causation debate**: Not all researchers accept that government policies are the primary cause of the racial wealth gap. Some economists, including economists associated with the Cato Institute and Manhattan Institute, attribute a significant portion of the gap to differences in educational attainment, family structure, and geographic concentration in lower-wage labor markets. The counterargument — supported by the longitudinal SCF data — is that these factors are themselves products of the historical harms (redlining restricted educational opportunity, incarceration destroyed family units, geographic concentration resulted from displacement), making them intermediate mechanisms of the original government harm rather than independent explanatory variables.

**The fungibility problem in Evanston**: Some researchers analyzing Evanston's program have questioned whether $25,000 housing-directed grants close wealth gaps or simply increase asset prices in the targeted neighborhoods, with gains captured by sellers rather than recipients. The program's shift to allow direct cash payments partly addresses this concern by giving recipients flexibility to deploy funds in higher-return ways.

**Who counts as a descendant**: Defining the eligible population for a federal reparations program is a genuine technical and moral challenge. The California task force recommended limiting eligibility to descendants of enslaved persons in the United States as of the 13th Amendment (1865), rather than all Black Americans. This distinction would exclude more recent Black immigrants from Africa and the Caribbean who did not experience the specific government harms documented here. The commission model allows this definitional work to occur through documented evidence rather than legislative assumption.

**Germany's success vs. South Africa's failure — what variable is decisive**: Comparative analysis of Germany and South Africa identifies the key variable as not moral will but political economy. Germany paid because Western alliance integration required it — an external geopolitical incentive structure. South Africa's government declined to pay the TRC's recommended amount because white economic power was structurally entrenched in the post-apartheid economy and the ANC government needed that economic cooperation for stability. For U.S. reparations, this analysis suggests that a binding congressional vote mechanism (the BRAC model) is more important than moral acknowledgment — because moral acknowledgment (California's formal apology) has already been demonstrated to be insufficient without a payment obligation.

## Section 8: Fiscal Architecture — Mechanisms and Revenue

### 8.1 The Cost of the Racial Wealth Gap: What Inaction Costs

**Citigroup (2020)**: Racial discrimination has cost the U.S. economy **$16 trillion in foregone GDP over the past two decades**. The components: $13 trillion in potential business revenue lost due to discriminatory lending to Black entrepreneurs; $2.7 trillion in income lost from wage disparities; $218 billion from housing credit discrimination; and $90-113 billion from discrimination in higher education access. If racial inequality gaps were fully addressed, Citigroup projected **$5 trillion could be added to GDP over five years**.

**McKinsey (2019)**: The racial wealth gap and associated human capital underinvestment will cost the U.S. economy between **$1 trillion and $1.5 trillion in cumulative GDP losses between 2019 and 2028** — 4 to 6 percent of projected 2028 GDP.

These figures are not advocacy estimates — they are calculations from major financial institutions using standard macroeconomic modeling, motivated by investor interest in identifying sources of growth. They reframe reparations politically: not as a redistribution from white taxpayers to Black recipients, but as a partial correction of a decades-long drag on overall economic output.

### 8.2 Revenue Mechanisms: What Has Worked

**Estate tax to 2009 parameters**: Restoring the estate tax to its 2009 parameters — a $3.5 million per-spouse exemption, 45 percent top rate — would raise substantial revenue relative to current law. Under current law (post-2025 TCJA extension, with a $15 million exemption per individual after the One Big Beautiful Bill Act), the estate tax applies to an extraordinarily small fraction of estates. The Tax Policy Center's modeling found that restoring 2009 parameters would affect only 0.23 percent of estates while generating meaningful revenue — TPC and CBO estimates from 2022 Penn Wharton Budget Model analysis suggest a reformed estate tax restoring pre-TCJA parameters could yield $160-200 billion over ten years. With a specifically higher rate for very large estates (the Darity-Hamilton "wealth tax" framing), revenue potential is substantially higher.

**Dedicated revenue streams**: Evanston's cannabis tax model provides proof of concept for dedicated revenue streams — though its revenue shortfall illustrates the risk of depending on a single volatile source. Germany's approach used specifically negotiated bilateral transfer payments; Canada's residential schools settlement used general appropriations. A federal reparations trust fund combining estate tax reform, a small financial transaction tax, and a dedicated appropriation stream would distribute revenue risk across multiple sources — consistent with the Domain 22 fiscal architecture.

**Administrative cost ratios**: The Japanese American redress program's Office of Redress Administration operated from 1988 to 1999, disbursing $1.6 billion to 82,250 recipients. Given the documented administrative costs, the program ran at roughly 2-3 percent overhead — a benchmark for efficient direct-payment administration. Germany's Claims Conference operates at comparable low overhead across a much larger and more complex program. A well-designed federal commission with clear eligibility criteria and streamlined documentation requirements could achieve similar administrative efficiency.

### 8.3 Multiplier Effects: Community Development Investment Evidence

The Domain 22 Community Reinvestment and Restoration Fund's $200 billion targeting of formerly redlined HOLC-D neighborhoods is not only a reparative investment — it is an economically productive one. The Federal Reserve Bank's research on Community Development Financial Institution (CDFI) lending shows that CDFI-financed businesses have higher survival rates and employment growth than comparable businesses financed through conventional channels, partly because CDFIs operate with lower collateral requirements in communities where collateral scarcity was itself produced by redlining.

The economic multiplier for targeted community investment in underserved areas is documented at **1.5 to 2.0x** in local GDP terms — meaning $200 billion invested over 10 years in formerly redlined tracts would generate $300-400 billion in local economic activity. This compares favorably with the 1.1-1.3x multiplier typically estimated for tax cuts concentrated in upper-income households.

**Homeownership as wealth-building mechanism — the data**: Urban Institute research finds that the **average homeowner has 40 times the wealth of the average renter** across all races and income levels. The $25,000-$50,000 down payment grant component of the Domain 22 architecture targets precisely this mechanism: for a family purchasing a $300,000 home with a $25,000 grant as down payment, the leveraged return over 20 years (assuming 3 percent annual appreciation) would be approximately $240,000 in home equity — a roughly 960 percent nominal return on the original $25,000 grant. This is the mechanism the GI Bill used to build the white middle class; applying it specifically to the descendants of those excluded is both reparative and economically rational.

### 8.4 Total Fiscal Estimate for Full Domain 22 Package

The Domain 22 components and their approximate 10-year fiscal magnitudes:

- **Federal Reparations Commission** ($50M budget, 12-month window): $50 million — de minimis in federal budget terms
- **Community Reinvestment and Restoration Fund** ($200B over 10 years): $20 billion per year — comparable to the annual Earned Income Tax Credit (~$66B), significant but not unprecedented as federal program scale
- **Baby bonds** ($60-80B per year at full implementation): The largest single line item, comparable in magnitude to the child tax credit at its expanded scale
- **DOJ Civil Rights tripling** ($170M → $500M annually): $330 million incremental per year — trivial in federal budget terms, substantial in enforcement capacity
- **Federal Office of Racial Equity and enforcement infrastructure**: Estimated $1-2 billion per year for office operations, EEOC expansion, and HUD fair housing expansion
- **Truth Commission**: $200-500 million for a multi-year commission with subpoena infrastructure and public hearing support

**Total 10-year investment**: Approximately $800 billion to $1 trillion, concentrated in the baby bonds program and the Community Reinvestment Fund. Against the Citigroup estimate that racial inequality has cost the economy $16 trillion over 20 years ($800 billion per year), this 10-year investment represents approximately one year of ongoing GDP cost — a substantially favorable return on investment even under conservative macroeconomic assumptions. McKinsey's estimate of $1-1.5 trillion in GDP losses per decade means the baby bonds and community investment programs alone could be economically self-financing within a generation if the wealth gap reduction projections hold.

## Section 9: HOLC Maps — What the Data Shows in Formerly Redlined Neighborhoods Today

### 9.1 Persistent Structural Disadvantage in Grade-D Tracts

The Home Owners' Loan Corporation produced its residential security maps for 239 U.S. cities between 1935 and 1940. The maps graded neighborhoods A (green, "best"), B (blue, "still desirable"), C (yellow, "declining"), and D (red, "hazardous"). Grade-D designations were applied overwhelmingly to neighborhoods with Black, immigrant, or lower-income residents. The maps were shared with the FHA and private lenders and directly informed mortgage underwriting decisions for decades.

The **Mapping Inequality** project at the University of Richmond digitized all surviving HOLC maps, enabling contemporary researchers to link 1930s-1940s neighborhood grades to current census tract data. The findings are unambiguous: **formerly redlined neighborhoods (HOLC grade D) continue to show substantially worse outcomes on virtually every measurable dimension** compared to contemporaneous green-graded neighborhoods, nearly 90 years after the maps were drawn.

**Poverty and economic outcomes**: Redlined census tracts have consistently higher poverty rates, lower median household incomes, lower homeownership rates, and higher unemployment than non-redlined tracts within the same metropolitan areas. University of Wisconsin Population Health Institute analysis found that residents of formerly redlined tracts have **life expectancies 3.6 years shorter** on average than residents of formerly green-graded tracts in the same cities.

**Health outcomes**: A 2022 study in the American Journal of Public Health examined the Detroit metropolitan area and found that historical redlining significantly predicted contemporary social determinants of health — poverty, housing quality, access to green space — and that these variables in turn predicted worse health outcomes including higher rates of preterm birth, respiratory disease, and cardiovascular disease. A scoping review in Environmental Health Perspectives (2022) found consistent associations between historical redlining and: higher urban heat island effect (from lack of tree canopy), higher PM10 air pollution concentrations, worse mental health outcomes, and higher rates of emergency department use.

**Environmental burden**: Environmental Protection Agency environmental justice screening data consistently show higher concentrations of industrial facilities, waste sites, and air pollution sources in formerly redlined tracts — a pattern explained by the lower property values that redlining produced, which made those tracts cheaper for industrial siting decisions over subsequent decades.

**The mechanism of persistence**: The question is not merely whether redlining correlates with current disadvantage (it does) but how the correlation reproduces over generations. The mechanism is documented: lower property values in redlined tracts generated lower property tax bases, which produced lower-quality public schools, which reduced human capital formation, which reduced residents' earning potential and ability to build financial assets, which kept property values low, completing the cycle. Federal Community Reinvestment Act (CRA) data shows that lending in formerly redlined tracts remains below comparable non-redlined tracts even today, meaning the original lending discrimination continues to echo in contemporary credit markets.

Sources: [NCRC: Redlining and Neighborhood Health](https://ncrc.org/holc-health/); [Urban Institute: Assessing the Legacies of Historical Redlining (January 2023)](https://www.urban.org/sites/default/files/2023-01/Addressing%20the%20Legacies%20of%20Historical%20Redlining.pdf); [AJPH: Historic Redlining Practices and Contemporary Determinants of Health in the Detroit Metropolitan Area (2022)](https://ajph.aphapublications.org/doi/full/10.2105/AJPH.2022.307162); [PMC: The Relationship of Historical Redlining with Present-Day Neighborhood Environmental and Health Outcomes (2022)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9342590/)

### 9.2 Appraisal Bias: The Continuing Transmission of Redlining

Historical redlining's most direct contemporary transmission mechanism is **appraisal discrimination** — the systematic undervaluation of homes in Black neighborhoods and of homes owned by Black families. The Brookings Institution's 2018 analysis of Zillow data found that **homes in majority-Black neighborhoods are undervalued by an average of $48,000** compared to comparable homes in majority-white neighborhoods — representing approximately $156 billion in total undervaluation of owner-occupied homes in majority-Black neighborhoods nationwide.

A 2022 Freddie Mac study analyzing 12 million appraisals found that **Black and Latino borrowers were significantly more likely to receive appraisals below the contract price**, forcing them to either renegotiate purchases, make larger down payments, or abandon transactions. Appraisals below contract price occur at approximately **twice the rate** for Black and Latino borrowers as for white borrowers in comparable transactions.

The mechanism: the "sales comparison approach" used in residential appraisals requires appraisers to compare the subject property to recent nearby sales. In historically redlined, majority-Black neighborhoods, comparable sales reflect decades of underinvestment and disinvestment — meaning each new appraisal is calibrated against a depressed baseline that itself reflects historical discrimination. The discrimination is algorithmic before automation: it is embedded in the methodology.

**Automated valuation models (AVMs)** compound this problem. When machine learning models train on historical appraisal data — which already encodes decades of appraisal bias — they replicate the bias systematically and at scale. A National Fair Housing Alliance analysis found that AVMs used in refinancing and home equity decisions undervalued homes in majority-Black neighborhoods consistent with historical appraisal patterns. HUD and the GSEs (Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac) issued **Reconsideration of Value guidance in May 2024** allowing borrowers to contest biased appraisals — an incremental step that does not address the baseline undervaluation embedded in training data.

The Domain 22 anti-displacement protections and homeownership grants are directly responsive to this appraisal bias problem: grants that enable purchase despite below-contract appraisals, and community land trust structures that prevent undervalued properties from being acquired by investors rather than community members, address both ends of the mechanism.

Sources: [Brookings: Devaluation of Assets in Black Neighborhoods (2018)](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/devaluing-black-owned-homes/); [HUD, Fannie Mae, and Freddie Mac: Reconsideration of Value Guidance (May 2024)](https://www.consumerfinancemonitor.com/2024/05/09/hud-fannie-mae-and-freddie-mac-issue-reconsideration-of-value-guidance-to-combat-appraisal-bias/); [NAACPLDF: Fighting Appraisal Bias — How the Government and Housing Industry Can Better Address This Discriminatory Practice](https://www.naacpldf.org/appraisal-algorithmic-bias-racial-discrimination/)

### 9.3 The Community Reinvestment Fund — CDFI Targeting Logic

The Domain 22 Community Reinvestment and Restoration Fund's $200 billion over 10 years is specifically targeted at **HOLC-D neighborhoods** through CDFIs and anti-displacement protections. This targeting logic rests on documented evidence that general-purpose investment in these tracts generates displacement rather than community benefit unless anti-displacement protections are embedded simultaneously.

The pattern documented in post-2000 gentrification research is consistent: when investment flows into formerly redlined, low-income, predominantly Black neighborhoods without tenant protections and community land trust structures, property values rise and incumbent residents — who mostly rent rather than own — are displaced by higher-income, predominantly white new residents who capture the value appreciation. The original residents bear the disruption costs while receiving no wealth benefit. This is the "right to return" problem that **Evanston's** program attempts to address by directing grants specifically to Black residents who can document family presence during the redlining era.

The $200B fund's combination of **CDFI capitalization** (lending to Black-owned businesses and first-time homebuyers in redlined tracts), **anti-displacement protections** (tenant protections, community land trusts, right of first refusal for long-term residents in tax lien sales), and **infrastructure investment** is designed to build wealth for incumbent communities rather than displace them. This three-part structure is documented as necessary — any single element in isolation produces incomplete outcomes.

## Section 10: Truth Commission Design — Evidence on What Produces Durable Outcomes

### 10.1 The Evidence Base for Truth Commissions as Instruments of Harm Documentation

The **UN Declaration of Basic Principles of Justice for Victims of Crime and Abuse of Power (1985)** and the subsequent **UN Basic Principles and Guidelines on the Right to a Remedy and Reparation for Victims of Gross Violations of International Human Rights Law (2005)** establish four elements of redress: restitution, compensation, rehabilitation, and guarantees of non-repetition. Domain 22's truth commission addresses all four: the evidentiary record supports the financial and programmatic dimensions; congressional acknowledgment is the "guarantee of non-repetition" commitment; permanent monitoring creates ongoing accountability.

The International Center for Transitional Justice's comparative study of 40 truth commissions worldwide (1974-2015) identifies the factors that distinguish commissions producing durable outcomes from those that produce documented history without institutional change:

1. **Subpoena and investigative authority**: Commissions with compulsory process — ability to require document production and testimony — produce more complete and less contested records than advisory bodies relying on voluntary participation. The South Africa TRC had this authority; the Tulsa Race Massacre Commission (2001) did not, limiting its ability to compel corporate and insurance records.

2. **Protected victim testimony with victim-centered design**: Commissions that center victim testimony in public hearings, rather than treating victims as evidentiary inputs, produce higher survivor participation and greater public understanding. Canada's TRC held 238 community hearings across the country — the geographic scope itself signaling that the commission was coming to survivors, not summoning them.

3. **Binding follow-through mechanism**: No truth commission in the ICTJ's study produced full reparations payment without either external political pressure (Germany — Cold War alliance building) or a binding domestic legal mechanism (Rosewood — statutory payment provision embedded in the same legislation as the study commission authorization). Advisory commissions recommending voluntary legislative action have a poor record.

4. **Education integration requirements**: Commissions that produce curriculum mandates alongside their findings — Canada's 2015 TRC specifically called for mandatory K-12 residential schools education — generate longer-term public understanding than commissions whose reports become archival documents. Germany's mandatory Holocaust education in public schools (Bundesgesetze) is consistently cited as the reason German public memory of the Holocaust remains active across generations.

**The Domain 22 design against these benchmarks**: The proposal's truth commission has subpoena authority (passes criterion 1), but the proposal does not specify the geographic hearing structure (criterion 2 requires attention in implementation design). The BRAC-model binding vote directly addresses criterion 3. The education integration component — mandating residential school curriculum — addresses criterion 4. The proposed permanent monitoring office addresses the "guarantees of non-repetition" element that most commissions fail to institutionalize.

### 10.2 Congressional Acknowledgment: Precedents and Political Economy

Congressional acknowledgment of racial injustice has occurred in isolated instances without triggering comprehensive reparations. The Senate passed **Senate Resolution 26 in 2009** — a formal apology for slavery and Jim Crow — by unanimous consent. House Concurrent Resolution 194 (2008) apologized specifically for slavery and segregation. California enacted its formal apology via AB 3089 in September 2024.

These acknowledgment actions share a common feature: they were designed to avoid creating legal liability. The Congressional apology resolutions included explicit language disclaiming any basis for legal claims or funding obligations. This is the political economy problem the BRAC model addresses: voluntary acknowledgment can be structured to be cheap (symbolic apology with liability waiver), while a binding vote on commission recommendations forces Congress to choose between acting on the evidence or voting explicitly to reject it — a much higher political cost.

The Japanese American redress precedent is instructive on congressional acknowledgment working as a precursor to payment. The CWRIC's 1983 report produced several years of congressional inaction before the Civil Liberties Act of 1988 passed. The mechanism that finally moved the legislation was intensive lobbying by Japanese American veterans' organizations, a sympathetic political window (Reagan administration Cold War outreach to Japanese-American communities), and the actuarial pressure of aging survivors. The Domain 22 design does not rely on these accidental factors — the BRAC binding mechanism forces the vote.
