# Immigration Policy — Evidence Deepening

*Prepared April 2026. Supplements Domain 16 in the Democratic Renewal Proposal. Does not restate the reform architecture — deepens the evidentiary foundation with specific data, incident-level documentation, the international benchmarks that reform arguments depend on, and the fiscal arithmetic that should anchor any policy debate.*

## Gaps This Document Fills

The Immigration and Citizenship domain correctly identifies the structural failures: enforcement without accountability, courts without independence, a legal system that manufactures the undocumented population it then punishes, and a detention apparatus that enriches private industry through human captivity. What it is thinner on:

- The precise per-day and per-deportation fiscal arithmetic — the actual numbers that make alternatives-to-detention cost comparisons concrete
- The economic contribution data — GDP, tax, Social Security, entrepreneurship — that answers the "fiscal burden" framing dominant in public debate
- Granular documentation of 2025–2026 enforcement actions, including wrongful deportation cases involving US citizens and legal permanent residents with federal court orders
- Family separation: the updated reunification status, including how many children remain separated six years later
- ICE detention death data for 2024–2025, with the documented decline in oversight inspections
- The Card-Borjas wage debate — what the research actually shows vs. how it is misrepresented
- Visa overstay data — the overlooked fact that 40%+ of the undocumented population entered legally
- International benchmarks with outcome numbers, not just system descriptions: Canada's Express Entry employment rates, Germany's first-year Skilled Immigration Act figures, Australia's reform arithmetic
- The mass deportation fiscal case — Penn Wharton, American Immigration Council, and CBO projections from 2025

## Section 1: The Fiscal Arithmetic of the Current System

### 1.1 ICE Detention: Costs Per Day, Per Year, Per Deportation

The proposal states that detention costs $150–$300/day versus $4.50/day for community-based alternatives. The specific verified figures:

**Per-day detention cost**: ICE's own FY2025 bed rate is **$164.65/day per detainee**. The 2024 average across facility types was approximately **$152/day**, producing an annual detention bill of roughly **$3.4 billion** for a daily average population of approximately 41,500.

**All-in deportation cost**: The complete lifecycle cost — from arrest through detention to removal — averages **$17,121 per person**, according to the American Immigration Council's October 2024 analysis of ICE operational data.

**Annual ICE budget trajectory**: The One Big Beautiful Bill Act, enacted July 2025, includes **$45 billion** for expanding detention capacity over four years and approved **$29.9 billion** toward ICE enforcement and deportation operations — tripling ICE's annual budget. ICE's pre-OBBBA budget was approximately $8 billion/year.

**Alternative to detention**: ICE's Intensive Supervision Appearance Program (ISAP) costs **$4.20/day per participant**. Community-based case management through nonprofit organizations costs approximately **$14/day per family member** based on 2018 pilot data. The ratio is not 1/30th (as the proposal states) — it is more conservatively 1/10th to 1/40th depending on the specific program, but compliance outcomes are equivalent or superior.

Sources: [American Immigration Council: Cost of Immigration Enforcement Fact Sheet 2024](https://www.americanimmigrationcouncil.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/cost_of_immigration_enforcement_factsheet_2024.pdf) | [National Immigration Forum: Immigration Detention Costs in a Time of Mass Deportation](https://forumtogether.org/article/immigration-detention-costs-in-a-time-of-mass-deportation/) | [American Immigration Council: Congress Approves Unprecedented Funding for Mass Detention/Deportation](https://www.americanimmigrationcouncil.org/press-release/congress-approves-unprecedented-funding-mass-detention-deportation-2025/)

### 1.2 Alternatives to Detention: Compliance Rate Evidence

The 99% court appearance rate stated in the proposal requires the specific data behind it:

- ICE's own ISAP contractor (BI, Inc.) reported a **99.6% appearance rate** for participants in its "Full Service" program (Contract Year 2013 data, the most granular public figure available).
- Two national alternatives-to-detention programs more recently reported **97% appearance rates**.
- The FEMA/CRCL Case Management Pilot Program received **$15 million** in FY2024 funding and was operating in five cities before being terminated in January 2025.

The 99%+ appearance rate for ATD participants compares to lower rates for detained persons released on bond without case management support — the relevant comparison is not detention vs. no supervision but detention vs. supervised community alternatives. The evidence supports supervised community alternatives as equally or more effective at ensuring court appearances at a fraction of the cost.

### 1.3 The Mass Deportation Fiscal Case

**American Immigration Council (October 2024)**: A one-time mass deportation of the estimated 11+ million undocumented population would cost at minimum **$315 billion**, broken down as:
- $89.3 billion for arrests
- $167.8 billion for detention
- $34.1 billion for legal processing
- $24.1 billion for removals

Sustaining one million deportations per year would cost **$88 billion annually**, the majority in detention infrastructure.

**Penn Wharton Budget Model (July 2025)**: The most rigorous independent fiscal modeling found:
- A 4-year mass deportation policy reduces GDP by **1.0% by 2034** and requires $82.7 billion in additional outlays over the 10-year budget window
- A 10-year sustained policy reduces GDP by **3.3% by 2034** and **4.9% by 2054**, with total fiscal cost of **$987 billion** over ten years after economic feedback effects
- Per-capita wages fall **0.5% (4-year) to 1.7% (10-year)** — meaning mass deportation makes the average American worker poorer, not richer
- The federal deficit **increases** under mass deportation, directly contradicting the fiscal argument made for it

**American Immigration Council extended analysis**: Long-term mass deportation (until no undocumented population remains) would produce a **4.2% to 6.8% GDP loss**, totaling $1.1 to $1.7 trillion in lost output.

Sources: [American Immigration Council: Mass Deportation Special Report, October 2024](https://www.americanimmigrationcouncil.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/mass_deportation_report_2024.pdf) | [Penn Wharton Budget Model: Mass Deportation of Unauthorized Immigrants: Fiscal and Economic Effects, July 2025](https://budgetmodel.wharton.upenn.edu/issues/2025/7/28/mass-deportation-of-unauthorized-immigrants-fiscal-and-economic-effects) | [Joint Economic Committee: Mass Deportations Would Deliver a Catastrophic Blow to the U.S. Economy (December 2024)](https://www.jec.senate.gov/public/index.cfm/democrats/2024/12/mass-deportations-would-deliver-a-catastrophic-blow-to-the-u-s-economy)

## Section 2: The Economic Contribution of Immigrants

### 2.1 Tax Contributions: Undocumented Immigrants

The "fiscal burden" framing in public debate systematically omits the tax contribution side of the ledger. The Institute on Taxation and Economic Policy (ITEP) data:

**Undocumented immigrants pay significant taxes**:
- In 2022, the 10.9 million undocumented immigrants in the United States paid **$96.7 billion in total taxes**, including $19.5 billion in federal income taxes and $32.3 billion in federal payroll taxes.
- In 2023, undocumented immigrant households paid **$89.8 billion** in combined federal, state, and local taxes.
- Undocumented immigrants pay **$32.1 billion into Social Security and Medicare annually** — programs from which they are categorically excluded from receiving benefits.
- More specifically: **$25.6 billion to Social Security** and **$6.4 billion to Medicare** per year, contributing to programs that pay retirement and health benefits to American citizens, not to themselves.

**The Social Security subsidy**: The Congressional Budget Office's 2024 analysis of the immigration surge found that the surge population is expected to contribute **$348 billion in Social Security revenues** between 2024 and 2034, while collecting only approximately **$1 billion in Social Security benefits** over the same period. This is not a burden — it is a structural subsidy to the retirement security of native-born Americans.

### 2.2 GDP and Labor Market Contributions

**CBO 10-year projection**: The congressional budget office projects that immigration — including the recent surge — will boost total nominal GDP by **$1.3 trillion (3.2%) in 2034** and **$8.9 trillion over the 2024–2034 period**. Federal tax revenue increases by **$1.2 trillion** over the same period. The federal deficit decreases by **$900 billion** over the 10-year window. These are not advocacy estimates — they are from the non-partisan budget scorer used by Congress for all fiscal legislation.

**All immigrant households**: Immigrant households (legal and undocumented combined) pay **$16.80 of every $100 in federal, state, and local taxes collected** — roughly in proportion to their population share, and in some categories above it.

**Entrepreneurship**: In 2025, the American Immigration Council documented that **46% of Fortune 500 companies** (230 companies) were founded by immigrants or their children — the highest share in the report's history. These companies collectively generated **$8.6 trillion in revenue in FY2023** and employ more than **15.5 million people worldwide**. The manufacturing sector has the highest concentration: more than two-thirds of Fortune 500 manufacturers were founded by immigrants or their children.

**Agricultural dependency**: Approximately **35% of hired agricultural workers** — an estimated 680,000 of 1.93 million workers — lack legal work authorization. This is not a policy failure that enforcement can solve: the legal pathway (H-2A visa) is chronically under-resourced relative to demand, and the agricultural sector's structural dependence on unauthorized labor is the direct consequence of a legal system that has never provided sufficient supply for actual demand.

### 2.3 The Card-Borjas Wage Debate — What the Research Actually Shows

Immigration's effect on native worker wages is the most contested empirical question in immigration economics. The debate is frequently misrepresented in both directions.

**The Card finding (1990, updated)**: David Card's foundational Mariel Boatlift study found that a 7% increase in Miami's labor force from Cuban immigrants produced no measurable wage effect on native workers. Card's subsequent work consistently found that immigration's wage effects on native workers are negligible to slightly positive, because immigrants and native workers are imperfect substitutes — they do not directly compete for the same jobs.

**The Borjas finding**: Harvard economist George Borjas, analyzing the 1980–2000 period using a "skill-cell" methodology, found that immigration reduced the average wage of similarly-educated native workers by 3–4%, and by 9% for workers without a high school diploma. Borjas's work is the most-cited empirical case that immigration reduces wages for low-wage native workers.

**The methodological disagreement**: The substantial difference in findings turns on methodology. The "spatial" approach (Card) compares cities with different immigrant concentrations and finds minimal effects. The "skill-cell" approach (Borjas) compares education-experience cohorts nationally and finds larger negative effects. A 2025 meta-analysis in the Journal of Development Economics reviewing multiple studies concluded that "the consensus is very strong that there are not significant costs to U.S.-born workers from immigration" at current levels — but that low-wage native workers without high school diplomas face the most direct competition in Borjas-framework analyses.

**The practical takeaway for reform design**: The research supports a focus on labor protections for low-wage workers — including both immigrants and native workers — rather than restriction. When all workers have enforceable rights (Domain 17), the wage suppression mechanism that Borjas identifies (exploitable undocumented workers bidding down wages) disappears. Labor reform and immigration reform are functionally interdependent.

Sources: [EPI: Immigration and Wages — Methodological advancements confirm modest gains for native workers](https://www.epi.org/publication/bp255/) | [CNBC: Are immigrants taking jobs from 'native' U.S. workers? Here's what economists say (September 2024)](https://www.cnbc.com/amp/2024/09/28/are-immigrants-taking-jobs-from-us-workers-heres-what-economists-say.html) | [Cato Institute: Does Immigration Reduce Wages?](https://www.cato.org/cato-journal/fall-2017/does-immigration-reduce-wages)

## Section 3: Who the Undocumented Population Actually Is

### 3.1 Visa Overstays: The Border-Centric Frame Is Wrong

Public debate treats the undocumented population as primarily composed of unauthorized border crossers. The data contradicts this.

**40%+ entered legally**: The DHS FY2024 Entry/Exit Overstay Report documents that in FY2023, approximately **510,000 people** overstayed their visas — representing nearly **40% of new undocumented arrivals** that year, alongside an estimated 860,000 unauthorized border crossings. Over the accumulated undocumented population (built up over decades), more than **40% are individuals who entered legally** and remained beyond their authorized period.

**The implication**: Border wall and enforcement-at-the-border spending — which constitutes the bulk of enforcement expenditure — addresses at most 60% of the mechanism creating the undocumented population. A policy framework that treats all undocumented presence as a border security problem misdiagnoses 40% of its own subject.

**Overstay rates are low by percentage**: The FY2024 overall suspected in-country overstay rate was **0.92%** — meaning over 99% of nonimmigrant visa holders departed or adjusted status as required. The problem is not a dysfunctional overstay detection system; it is the absence of a legal mechanism for people who were here, integrated, and did not leave.

Sources: [DHS: Entry/Exit Overstay Report Fiscal Year 2024](https://www.dhs.gov/sites/default/files/2025-09/25_0912_cbp_entry-exit-overstay-report-fiscal-year-2024.pdf) | [Statista: Visa Overstays Make up Almost 40 Percent of Illegal U.S. Entries](https://www.statista.com/chart/16701/visa-overstays-outnumber-illegal-crossings/)

### 3.2 DACA Recipients: The Specific Profile

**Active recipients**: As of 2024, approximately **525,210 active DACA recipients** live in the United States. More than half are female; 81% were born in Mexico, 4% in El Salvador, 3% in Guatemala.

**Average time in the US**: DACA recipients arrived at an average age of **6.6 years old** and have spent an average of **25.2 years** in the country. They are Americans in every practical sense — educated in American schools, speaking English as a primary language, with family, careers, and community ties in the United States.

**US citizen family members**: If DACA were terminated, approximately **254,000 US-born children** with at least one DACA parent would face family separation. An additional **110,000 US citizen spouses** and **300,000 other US citizen family members** would be affected.

**Economic contribution**: 92.4% of DACA recipients are currently employed. Their average hourly wage has increased from **$11.88 before DACA** to **$33.55 in 2024** — a 182% increase. They contribute collectively **$2.1 billion annually to Social Security and Medicare**. After receiving DACA, 60% moved to better-paying jobs and 48.7% to jobs with better working conditions.

**The wage growth tells a specific story**: DACA recipients earning $33.55/hour pay substantially more in taxes than they would have at $11.88/hour. The wage increase is not a gift — it reflects the economic value unlocked when a person can legally work in the job for which they are actually qualified, rather than the underpaid shadow-economy job they could access without documentation.

Sources: [Center for American Progress: 10th Annual DACA Survey 2024](https://www.americanprogress.org/article/10th-annual-daca-survey-2024-findings-reveal-whats-at-stake-for-recipients-and-the-united-states/) | [American Immigration Council: DACA Overview July 2024](https://www.americanimmigrationcouncil.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/deferred_action_for_childhood_arrivals_daca_an_overview.pdf)

## Section 4: The Asylum System Dysfunction — Numbers

### 4.1 The Backlog

**3.8 million pending cases**: As of Q3 2025, the US immigration court system has a backlog of approximately **3.8 million pending cases** — up from 3.7 million at the end of 2024. Of these, approximately **2.4 million** are asylum seekers' cases.

**Average wait time**: For all cases, the average pending case has been waiting approximately **636 days** as of early 2026. For asylum cases specifically, the National Immigration Forum reported an estimated wait of **4.3 years** in 2024. These waits are not a feature — they are a system failure with direct human consequences: people cannot work legally, cannot make permanent life plans, and are subject to removal at any time for years while awaiting a hearing.

**Caseload per judge**: By 2024, the average immigration judge carried **5,286 pending cases** — double the caseload of a decade prior. By March 2026, some measures show approximately 3,300+ cases per judge as early terminations and dismissals have reduced the count, but the NAIJ's recommended maximum caseload is approximately 700 cases per judge. The system is running at 5–7x recommended capacity.

**Judge hiring has collapsed**: New judge hires fell to **46 in 2024**, the lowest since 2015, and through Q3 of 2025, only **14 new judges** have been hired. The administration has simultaneously dismissed large numbers of cases categorically — a maneuver that reduces the backlog count without adjudicating claims on the merits.

**The backlog as a structural tool**: The 3.8 million case backlog is not an administrative failure — it is the predictable output of a system chronically underfunded relative to its caseload, subject to constant political interference in case categories and priority, and structured so that immigration judges answer to the Attorney General (who is simultaneously prosecuting the cases they hear).

Sources: [TRAC Immigration: Immigration Court Backlog](https://tracreports.org/phptools/immigration/backlog/) | [National Immigration Forum: Immigration Detention Costs](https://forumtogether.org/article/immigration-detention-costs-in-a-time-of-mass-deportation/) | [GAO: U.S. Immigration Courts See a Significant and Growing Backlog](https://www.gao.gov/blog/u.s.-immigration-courts-see-significant-and-growing-backlog)

## Section 5: Detention — Deaths, Oversight Collapse, and Specific Cases

### 5.1 Deaths in Custody: 2024–2025 Data

**2024 baseline**: 11 deaths in ICE detention were reported for 2024.

**2025 — the deadliest year in two decades**: **32 people died** in ICE custody in 2025 — nearly **three times the 2024 rate** and the highest annual total since 2004. NPR's October 2025 reporting documented 2025 as "the deadliest year to be in ICE custody in decades."

**Oversight inspections collapsed**: ICE detention facility inspections declined by **36.25%** in 2025 even as the detained population surged to record highs. The Project on Government Oversight documented this inverse relationship: more detainees, fewer inspections, more deaths.

**Preventability finding**: A June 2024 ACLU report found that the **overwhelming majority** of deaths in ICE detention could have been prevented if ICE had provided clinically appropriate medical care. The DHS Office of Inspector General found in 2023 that **at least 1 in every 5 deaths** at ICE and CBP facilities in FY2021 occurred because detainees were not given timely or adequate medical care.

**The structural cause**: The DHS OIG's 2021 report identified the specific mechanism — "remote locations, a cumbersome hiring process, and competing opportunities" — that prevents adequate medical staffing at private detention facilities. Private facilities have a financial interest in minimizing operating costs (including medical staff costs) while maximizing bed occupancy. This is not a bug in the private detention model — it is a predictable output of aligning financial incentives with captivity rather than care.

## Section 6: Family Separation — The Documented Record

### 6.1 Zero Tolerance: Scale and Ongoing Failure

**The program**: The "Zero Tolerance" policy was officially adopted across the entire US-Mexico border from April to June 2018, prosecuting all adults crossing without authorization and automatically separating accompanying children.

**Total separated**: More than **5,500 children**, including infants, were separated from their families. The practice continued for at least 18 months after the policy's nominal end — an estimated **1,100 additional families** were separated between June 2018 and the end of 2019.

**Reunification as of 2025–2026**: Six years after the policy ended:
- Approximately **1,360 children** — nearly **30%** of all known separated children — may remain separated from their parents as of 2024.
- There is no current plan or initiative for reunification of the remaining separated families.
- Human Rights Watch's December 2024 report, titled "We Need to Take Away Children: Zero Accountability Six Years After Zero Tolerance," documented that no federal official has faced criminal accountability for the policy and that the reunification effort has effectively been abandoned.

**The Inspector General findings**: DHS OIG documented systemic record-keeping failures that made tracking and reuniting families difficult by design — the government separated children from parents without creating adequate records linking them. This is not administrative negligence; court-filed documents from the ACLU's Ms. L. v. ICE litigation revealed that the administration anticipated the reunification difficulty and proceeded anyway.

**Second-term acceleration**: The Trump administration's second term has initiated new family separation through structural means — detaining parents while removing children (or vice versa) through operations that avoid the specific "Zero Tolerance" label while producing equivalent results. The February 2025 case of a **2-year-old US citizen deported to Honduras** with her pregnant mother "with no meaningful process" represents a direct continuation of the separation paradigm.

Sources: [HRW: "We Need to Take Away Children": Zero Accountability Six Years After Zero Tolerance (December 2024)](https://www.hrw.org/report/2024/12/16/we-need-take-away-children/zero-accountability-six-years-after-zero-tolerance) | [University of Cincinnati IHRLR: Forcible Family Separation at the U.S.-Mexico Border (March 2025)](https://lawblogs.uc.edu/ihrlr/2025/03/14/forcible-family-separation-at-the-u-s-mexico-border-continued-impact-years-after-zero-tolerance/) | [American Immigration Council: A Look Back at the Family Separation Policy](https://www.americanimmigrationcouncil.org/report/family-separation-policy/)

## Section 7: 2025–2026 Specific Documented Enforcement Actions

### 7.1 Wrongful Deportation: Documented Cases

The Trump administration's second term has produced a documented pattern of deportations in violation of federal court orders and the removal of individuals with legal status, including US citizens and legal residents. Courts have ruled more than **4,400 times** that the Trump administration is detaining people illegally.

**Kilmar Abrego Garcia (Maryland)**: A 29-year-old with a court-issued "withholding of removal" order — which legally barred deportation to El Salvador — was arrested and deported to CECOT in March 2025. The US Supreme Court unanimously ruled that Judge Xinis "properly requires the Government to 'facilitate' Abrego Garcia's release." The administration refused to comply. As of April 2026, Garcia remains imprisoned at CECOT in violation of Supreme Court order.

**Chanthila Souvannarath (Louisiana)**: ICE deported a man with a 20-year substantive claim to US citizenship to Laos in direct violation of a federal court ruling recognizing his citizenship claim.

**Jordin Melgar-Salmeron (Virginia)**: Deported to El Salvador on May 7, 2025, **thirty minutes** after a federal court issued a stay of removal. Courts directed the administration to facilitate his return.

**2-year-old US citizen**: Deported to Honduras in April 2025 "with no meaningful process" while her pregnant mother was removed. A US citizen child was removed from the country of her citizenship without due process.

**Britania Uriostegui Rios**: A Mexican transgender woman deported to Mexico in November 2025 in violation of an immigration court order, discovered only after her attorneys made inquiry.

These are not isolated errors — they represent a systemic pattern of non-compliance with judicial orders. Federal courts have directed the Trump administration to return wrongfully deported individuals in at least **four documented cases** in the first six months of the second term, with compliance consistently resisted.

### 7.2 Alien Enemies Act Invocation: The 1798 Statute in 2025

In March 2025, the Trump administration invoked the **Alien Enemies Act of 1798** — a wartime statute last used to intern Japanese Americans in World War II — to deport **238 Venezuelans** to El Salvador, including **137 under the statute** itself and 101 under regular immigration law. They were transferred to CECOT, El Salvador's maximum-security terrorism prison, under an agreement paying El Salvador to hold US deportees.

**The judicial confrontation**: District Judge James Boasberg imposed a temporary restraining order. The planes flew anyway. The Justice Department argued Boasberg had no jurisdiction over foreign policy. The Supreme Court ruled on April 8, 2025 that Boasberg was without jurisdiction to issue his order — but in December 2025, a federal judge ruled that the deportations violated due process, were conducted in defiance of court orders, and ordered the administration to provide due process to the deported class.

The September 2025 federal appeals court ruling **blocked further use** of the Alien Enemies Act for deportations, finding it was being applied outside any recognized wartime context.

**The historical precedent being invoked**: The Alien Enemies Act was used to intern 120,000 Japanese Americans (70,000 of whom were US citizens) during World War II. Its invocation in peacetime against a specific national-origin group for mass removal represents a use of the statute without precedent in its two-century history outside of declared wars.

### 7.3 Operation Aurora: Documented Execution vs. Announced Ambition

In late January 2025, the Trump administration announced "Operation Aurora," describing Aurora, Colorado as "ground zero" for mass deportations targeting Tren de Aragua gang members. The announced operation involved military preparation at Buckley Space Force Base for use as a detention center.

**Documented execution**: On February 5, 2025, ICE and federal law enforcement conducted multi-location operations in Denver and Aurora at four apartment complexes. More than 100 armed federal agents in 30–40 vehicles, including armored vehicles, surrounded the Cedar Run Apartments at 4 a.m., using smoke grenades and forced entry. Despite the military-scale deployment, approximately **50 people were detained** — a significant gap between the operation's announced scope and documented result.

**The documentation gap**: ICE accused Colorado law enforcement of leaking the operation's details, then deleted the accusation from social media. The gap between announced mass deportation operations and their documented scale reflects a pattern throughout the second term: enforcement operations are announced at a scale designed to generate fear throughout immigrant communities, while actual detention numbers are substantially lower — but the chilling effect on community behavior (withdrawing children from schools, not reporting crimes, avoiding medical care) is achieved regardless.

### 7.4 Birthright Citizenship: The Constitutional Challenge

On January 20, 2025, President Trump signed an executive order purporting to end birthright citizenship for children born to undocumented or temporary visa holders. Multiple federal judges found the order "blatantly unconstitutional" and issued injunctions.

**Supreme Court intervention on injunctions**: On June 27, 2025, the Supreme Court ruled 6–3 that federal district courts generally cannot issue nationwide injunctions — but made no ruling on birthright citizenship's underlying constitutionality.

**Ninth Circuit ruling**: In July 2025, the Ninth Circuit became the first appeals court to rule that the executive order was unconstitutional on the merits.

**Current Supreme Court case**: On December 5, 2025, the Supreme Court granted certiorari in *Trump v. Barbara*. The case is expected to be argued in spring 2026, with a ruling by June–July 2026. The Supreme Court's oral argument (April 2026) showed justices appearing likely to rule against the administration — but the case remains pending as of this writing.

**What a ruling would mean**: If the Supreme Court upholds birthright citizenship (the expected outcome based on April 2026 oral argument), the executive order becomes void and the Fourteenth Amendment's text is judicially reaffirmed. If the Court restricts birthright citizenship, it would create a permanent class of stateless persons born on US soil — individuals who are citizens of no country, ineligible for US citizenship despite birth here, and ineligible for citizenship of their parents' country in most cases.

## Section 8: Employer-Side Enforcement — The Missing Half

### 8.1 E-Verify Coverage and the Agricultural Exemption

The logical counterpart to interior enforcement is employer-side enforcement — ensuring that undocumented workers cannot find employment. The US has never seriously pursued this approach.

**E-Verify is not mandatory**: E-Verify — the federal electronic employment eligibility verification system — is mandatory only for federal contractors and in a minority of states. For the majority of private-sector employers, it remains optional. Participation in agriculture increased from 1,225 employers in 2008 to 8,383 in 2022 — a growth from an extremely low base, not comprehensive coverage.

**Agricultural exemptions are structural**: E-Verify proposals consistently include agricultural exemptions because the agricultural labor market cannot function without undocumented labor at current wage rates and under current legal pathways. Approximately **35% of hired agricultural workers have no legal work authorization** — roughly 680,000 workers. Mandatory E-Verify without a functional agricultural guest worker program would collapse the US food supply.

**The contradiction**: The United States simultaneously uses aggressive interior enforcement to expel undocumented workers while maintaining an agricultural and food processing system structurally dependent on their presence. This is not an accident of policy design — it reflects the preference of agricultural and food industry employers for a workforce that cannot organize, report violations, or demand higher wages.

**E-Verify error rates**: E-Verify produces false positive "no match" results for legal workers — particularly for workers with name discrepancies (common among naturalized citizens, workers with hyphenated names, and workers whose legal names differ from commonly used names). A 2011 Westat report found that approximately **0.8% of authorized workers** received erroneous "no match" results — a small percentage that translates to hundreds of thousands of workers when applied to the full workforce.

## Section 9: International Benchmarks — What Works, With Numbers

### 9.1 Canada: Express Entry and Points-Based Outcomes

Canada's Express Entry system, operational since January 2015, uses a Comprehensive Ranking System (CRS) to score candidates on language proficiency, education, work experience, and adaptability, selecting candidates above a score threshold for permanent residence.

**Employment outcomes**: IRCC data shows that immigrants who entered through Express Entry in 2015–2016 were:
- **9 percentage points more likely to be employed** than those entering through other pathways
- **18 percentage points more likely to work in professions requiring university education**
- Earned **20% more on average** within one year of arrival

**Efficiency as a selection mechanism**: The points system selects for demonstrated economic integration capacity rather than employer sponsorship alone. This produces better early employment outcomes but does not fully solve the credential recognition problem — by 2016, approximately 40% of immigrants (including points-based arrivals) were employed below their skill level. By 2021, that "overeducation rate" had declined to **27%**.

**Admission scale**: Canada admitted **483,591 permanent residents in 2024**, the highest number since 1972. Facing housing and infrastructure pressure, the government announced a reduction to a 2025 target of **395,000** — a 21% decrease. Canada's immigration system is not without political tension, but it demonstrates that a functional points-based system producing positive employment outcomes at scale is achievable.

**What the US could learn**: The US employment-based system requires an employer-specific offer as the primary pathway. Canada's model allows qualified individuals to enter and find work — not requiring a specific employer before arrival. This produces more flexible labor market matching and better outcomes for skilled immigrants whose credentials may not align precisely with a single employer's needs.

### 9.2 Germany: Skilled Immigration Act 2023 — First-Year Outcomes

Germany's Fachkräfteeinwanderungsgesetz (Skilled Immigration Act) was substantially reformed in November 2023 (Phase 1) and completed with the "opportunity card" (Chancenkarte) in June 2024. The reform opened Germany's labor market to third-country skilled workers through three pathways: qualification recognition, potential (without pre-arranged employment), and capacity.

**First-year results** (announced by the German Federal Interior Ministry, November 2024):
- Approximately **200,000 visas for gainful employment** were issued in the first year — an increase of more than **10% over the previous year** (177,578).
- Student visas from outside the EU increased by **20%**.
- Trainee visas increased by **two-thirds**.
- Recognition-measure visas increased by **nearly 50%**.

**Projected impact**: The German federal government estimated the act would allow an additional **60,000 qualified third-country nationals** to enter annually for employment, qualification, or job search — plus **30,000** for the new Chancenkarte.

**The policy problem Germany was solving**: Germany has a projected shortage of **7 million skilled workers by 2035** due to demographic aging and low birth rates — the same demographic pressure the United States faces but in more acute form. Germany's solution was to build a transparent, skills-based admission pathway that does not depend on a single employer sponsorship.

### 9.3 Australia: Points System — The Reform Debate

Australia has operated a points-tested skilled migration program since the 1970s. Current analysis reveals both its strengths and structural limitations — useful for proposal design.

**The $84 billion question**: Research commissioned by the Australian government found that **tweaking the allocation of points** to better predict lifetime earnings would increase government revenues by **$84 billion over 30 years**. The current system allocates points for characteristics that are poor predictors of actual labor market success — a correctable design flaw, not an inherent limit of points-based selection.

**The overeducation problem**: More than 50% of graduate visa holders with bachelor's degrees or higher are working significantly below their skill level, even when studying in shortage occupations. This reflects a credential recognition problem and labor market integration failure, not a selection failure — Australia selects qualified people who then cannot get their qualifications recognized by Australian employers.

**Program scale**: Australia brought net overseas migration of **528,000** in 2022–23, recognized as unsustainable, and is reducing to a projected **260,000** in 2024–25 — a halving within two years in response to housing and infrastructure pressure.

**Lesson for reform design**: A points-based system is a selection tool, not a complete immigration system. It performs best when combined with: (1) a functioning credential recognition system, (2) English language settlement support, and (3) housing and public service capacity scaled to intake volume. The proposal's legalization pathway and asylum reform must be paired with integration infrastructure to produce the economic outcomes that make the fiscal case for reform.

Sources: [The Conversation: How should the skilled migration points test be reformed? ### 9.4 Canada's Immigration and Refugee Board: The Independence Model

Canada's Immigration and Refugee Board (IRB) is the direct institutional precedent for Domain 16b's Article I immigration court proposal. The IRB is:

- An independent quasi-judicial tribunal, not part of any ministry or law enforcement agency
- Governed by fixed-term Governor-in-Council appointments
- Structured with four specialized divisions (Refugee Protection, Refugee Appeal, Immigration, Immigration Appeal)
- Insulated from the direction of the Minister of Immigration on individual case decisions

The IRB processes approximately 72,000 refugee protection hearings annually. Its grant rate varies by claimant country — but importantly, it is governed by law and evidence, not by Attorney General direction. Canadian refugee claimants appear before adjudicators who have no institutional stake in the outcome and no supervisory accountability to the agency presenting the case.

This is precisely what current US immigration judges lack. The current Executive Office for Immigration Review (EOIR) is a component of the Department of Justice — meaning immigration judges answer to an Attorney General who is simultaneously the chief prosecutor of the cases they hear. This structural conflict is not a matter of individual judicial bias; it is an institutional design defect.

## Section 10: Cross-Cutting Fiscal Summary

**The core fiscal proposition**: The current system — $3.4 billion/year in detention, $17,121/deportation unit cost, 3.8 million case backlog, systemic wrongful deportations generating federal court litigation — is not fiscally conservative. It is enormously expensive, economically destructive, and counterproductive to the labor market outcomes it purports to protect.

**The fiscal alternative**:
- Alternatives to detention: $4.20–$14/day vs. $164.65/day = $1.5–2.5 billion/year in savings at current detention populations
- Legalization of the undocumented population: The 2013 Senate CBO score showed **$197 billion in deficit reduction over 10 years and $700 billion over 20 years**
- Maintaining DACA and extending it to the full eligible population: $2.1 billion/year in current Social Security/Medicare contributions, rising as the population ages into higher-wage careers
- Avoiding mass deportation: Penn Wharton projects a **$987 billion 10-year fiscal cost** for sustained mass deportation — the most expensive approach available
- Immigration surge fiscal impact: CBO projects the recent immigration surge **reduces the federal deficit by $900 billion** over 2024–2034

The proposal's reforms are not costly — they are the least expensive option available. The current system's cruelty is funded by the same taxpayers who bear its costs.
