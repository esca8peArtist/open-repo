# Housing Policy — Evidence Deepening

*Prepared April 2026. Supplements the Democratic Renewal Proposal (democratic-renewal-proposal.md), Domain 13: Housing. This document does not restate the proposal's reform architecture — it deepens the evidentiary foundation with specific data, contested findings, and the strongest counterarguments.*

## Gaps This Document Fills

The Housing domain in the proposal correctly identifies the supply shortage, exclusionary zoning, the racial homeownership gap, and homelessness as the central structural problems. What it is thinner on:

- Precise supply-shortage numbers across competing methodologies (Freddie Mac vs. NLIHC vs. NAHB) and why estimates diverge
- The Hsieh-Moretti GDP drag finding and its correction — an important result that needs both the number and the caveat
- The causal mechanism linking exclusionary zoning to racial wealth stratification (the legal history of Euclid, HOLC, FHA as a connected system)
- The filtering evidence: whether building luxury housing actually helps lower-income renters, and on what timeline
- The Diamond et al. 2019 San Francisco rent control study — the most rigorous evidence on rent regulation, showing a 15% long-run supply reduction
- Moving to Opportunity: what the long-run evidence shows for children vs. adults, and why it changes the policy design argument
- Community Land Trusts as a third model between private ownership and public housing
- The Pennsylvania land value tax experiments — the closest US analog to the Henry George proposal
- Vienna and Singapore as social housing comparators — what each demonstrates and what each cannot be transplanted
- LIHTC cost-efficiency concerns — why the dominant affordable housing subsidy vehicle may be producing fewer units at higher cost than alternatives

## Section 1: The Scale of the Housing Crisis

### The Supply Shortage — Competing Estimates

The US housing market is in chronic undersupply. The estimates of magnitude vary by methodology but all point to the same structural problem.

**Realtor.com / Up For Growth (2025–2026)**: The housing supply gap reached 4.03 million homes in 2025, up from 3.8 million in 2024. Under an optimistic scenario — construction 50% above 2025 pace — eliminating the current deficit would take approximately seven years.

**Freddie Mac**: Freddie Mac's own research documents the collapse of starter-home construction. Single-family homes under 1,400 square feet fell from over 400,000 per year (more than 35% of all new single-family homes) in 1976 to approximately 65,000 (7% of completions) in 2020. By 2024 this recovered slightly to 101,000 — 10% of completions — but remains far below the long-run baseline.

**NLIHC (2025)**: The National Low Income Housing Coalition tracks the gap specifically for the lowest-income renters. For extremely low-income households (incomes at or below 30% of area median income or the federal poverty guideline), there is a shortage of 7.2 million affordable and available rental homes. Only 35 affordable and available homes exist for every 100 extremely low-income renter households.

**NAHB (conservative)**: The National Association of Home Builders estimates that approximately 1.2 million additional units would restore vacancy rates to historical norms — a more conservative figure because it measures only the cyclical shortfall, not the secular structural deficit.

**Why the estimates diverge**: The gap between NAHB's 1.2 million and Realtor.com's 4 million reflects different reference periods and what "shortage" means. NAHB measures the units needed to return to pre-2008 vacancy norms. Realtor.com and Up For Growth measure cumulative underbuilding relative to household formation. NLIHC measures adequacy for the lowest-income households specifically. All three are capturing real phenomena; none is wrong. The policy implication is that supply reform addresses the market shortage but does not, alone, close the affordability gap for the bottom quintile.

Sources: [RealtyTimes: Housing Supply Gap Surpasses 4 Million 2025](https://realtytimes.com/real-industry-news-articles/housing-supply-gap-surpasses-4-million-homes-in-2025-as-construction-fails-to-keep-pace-with-demand); [NAHB Eye on Housing: Size of Housing Shortage 2024](https://eyeonhousing.org/2026/02/the-size-of-the-housing-shortage-2024-data/); [NLIHC: The Gap 2025](https://nlihc.org/news/nlihc-releases-gap-2025-shortage-affordable-homes); [Freddie Mac on Starter Home Construction](https://privatebank.jpmorgan.com/nam/en/insights/markets-and-investing/tmt/a-shortage-of-supply-the-housing-market-explained)

### Cost Burden — Record Highs

A household is cost-burdened when it spends more than 30% of pre-tax income on housing costs; severely cost-burdened when it spends more than 50%.

**Harvard JCHS (2024)**: The number of cost-burdened renter households rose by 2 million in three years to a record-high 22.4 million — half of all US renter households. Of those, 12.1 million face severe burdens (50%+ of income), also an all-time high. Rental assistance currently serves 5.1 million households against 22.4 million cost-burdened households — a 4.4:1 unmet-need ratio.

**Lower-income renters (JCHS/NLIHC)**: Renters earning under $30,000 annually have an 83% cost-burden rate; 65% are severely burdened. Eighty-seven percent of extremely low-income renters face some cost burden; 75% are severely burdened.

**Middle-income deterioration**: Since 2019, cost-burden shares have risen most sharply for middle-income renter households earning $30,000–$74,999 — meaning the affordability crisis has migrated up the income distribution, no longer confined to the bottom quintile.

**The Housing Wage (2025)**: NLIHC calculates the hourly wage a full-time worker must earn to afford a modest two-bedroom apartment at 30% of income. The 2025 national Housing Wage is $33.63/hour — more than 4.6 times the federal minimum wage of $7.25. In no state can a full-time minimum-wage worker afford a two-bedroom rental home.

Sources: [Harvard JCHS: New Report Shows Rent Unaffordable for Half of Renters](https://www.jchs.harvard.edu/press-releases/new-report-shows-rent-unaffordable-half-renters-cost-burdens-surge-record-levels); [Harvard JCHS: Americas Rental Housing 2024](https://www.jchs.harvard.edu/americas-rental-housing-2024); [NLIHC Out of Reach 2025](https://nlihc.org/news/nlihc-releases-out-reach-2025-high-cost-housing); [NLIHC: The Gap 2025](https://nlihc.org/gap)

### Homelessness — Record Levels

**HUD Point-in-Time Count (January 2024)**: 770,000 people were experiencing homelessness on a single night — the highest number in the history of HUD's Annual Homeless Assessment Report (AHAR). This represents an 18% increase from 2023.

| Population | 2024 Count | Change from 2023 |
|---|---|---|
| All people experiencing homelessness | 770,000+ | +18% |
| Families with children | 259,000+ | +39% |
| Chronic homelessness | 153,000 | +7% |
| Unaccompanied youth (under 24) | ~38,000 | +10% |
| Veterans | 32,882 | -8% (bright spot) |

**Geography**: California accounts for approximately 187,000 of the national total (24%); New York accounts for 158,000 (20%). Both reflect acute supply constraints in high-cost coastal markets. The veteran decrease — 8% year-over-year, the only population to decline — reflects targeted voucher investments and demonstrates that purpose-specific interventions can move the needle when resources are committed.

**Contributing factors**: The 2024 record reflects rising housing costs, the expiration of pandemic-era emergency rental assistance (the Emergency Rental Assistance Program ended without a successor), and migration placing pressure on shelter systems. HUD's own analysis ties the surge primarily to rent increases that outpaced income growth, not to changes in population demographics or substance use patterns.

### The Racial Homeownership Gap

**Current statistics (Q4 2024)**: White homeownership rate — 74%+. Black homeownership rate — 46.4%. A gap of approximately 27.6 percentage points. This gap is larger than it was at the time the Fair Housing Act passed in 1968.

| Group | Homeownership Rate (2024) |
|---|---|
| White (non-Hispanic) | ~74% |
| Asian American | ~63% |
| Hispanic/Latino | ~49% |
| Black | ~46% |

**Wealth implications**: Because homeownership is the primary wealth-building mechanism for middle-class families, the homeownership gap translates directly into a wealth gap. Black families have $23 in wealth for every $100 of white family wealth (National Community Reinvestment Coalition, drawing on Federal Reserve data). Homes owned by white families appreciate approximately $28,000 more than homes owned by Black families, largely due to persistent residential segregation — homes in neighborhoods more than 10% Black lose approximately 16% of value (Shapiro, *The Hidden Cost of Being African American*, 2004). African-American families accumulate a dime for every dollar of White family wealth.

The mechanism is not income disparity alone. Even after controlling for income, Black families face a larger homeownership gap — reflecting the accumulated effects of historical policy exclusion and present-day discrimination in lending, appraisals, and neighborhood investment.

## Section 2: Why Housing Is Expensive — Zoning as the Causal Factor

### The Glaeser-Gyourko Finding: Zoning Inflates Prices Above Construction Costs

Edward Glaeser (Harvard) and Joseph Gyourko (Wharton) established in their foundational 2002 NBER paper *The Impact of Zoning on Housing Affordability* that in competitive markets without land-use constraints, housing prices are close to the marginal physical cost of construction. Price divergence from construction cost is therefore attributable to regulatory constraints, not to underlying materials or labor costs per se.

Their methodology: comparing housing prices to construction costs from R.S. Means data (the industry-standard cost estimator), using American Housing Survey and Census data. Finding: in most of America, prices track construction costs closely. In places where they diverge significantly — New York City, coastal California — the divergence is correlated with regulatory restrictiveness, not superior amenities alone.

**The 2005 follow-up** (Glaeser, Gyourko, Saks, *Why Have Housing Prices Gone Up?*): Housing prices increased much faster than construction costs from the 1970s onward, but only in specific markets. The key differentiator is not demand — all US metros experienced demand growth — but supply constraints. Supply-constrained cities (San Francisco, New York, Boston) saw prices skyrocket; supply-elastic cities (Houston, Phoenix, Atlanta) saw prices track construction costs.

Sources: [Glaeser/Gyourko NBER 2002: The Impact of Zoning on Housing Affordability](https://www.nber.org/papers/w8835); [Glaeser/Gyourko: The Economic Implications of Housing Supply](https://realestate.wharton.upenn.edu/wp-content/uploads/2017/03/802.pdf); [Glaeser/Gyourko/Saks 2005: Why Have Housing Prices Gone Up?](https://inequality.stanford.edu/sites/default/files/media/_media/pdf/Reference%20Media/Glaeser_Gyourko_Saks_2005.pdf)

### Hsieh-Moretti: Zoning as a Macroeconomic Tax

Chang-Tai Hsieh (Chicago Booth) and Enrico Moretti (UC Berkeley) quantified what housing supply restrictions cost the entire US economy — not just the households who can't afford to live in high-productivity cities.

**The argument**: High-productivity cities — New York, San Francisco, San Jose — generate substantially above-average wages for workers of all skill levels. Workers who would move to those cities for higher wages are prevented from doing so by housing costs that have been inflated by zoning constraints. The result is spatial misallocation of labor: workers remain in lower-productivity cities and produce less than they could. The total economy pays a productivity penalty.

**The finding**: If land use regulations in high-productivity cities were relaxed to the level of the median US metro, US productivity would increase by 9.7% and GDP would be roughly $1.4 trillion higher. Workers' wages would be an estimated 9.5% higher nationally.

**The correction**: The original Hsieh-Moretti (2015) paper reported an 8.9% GDP gain. A subsequent reanalysis identified a calculation error; the corrected figure is that GDP would be approximately 36% higher — a dramatically larger estimate. A later published version (2019, *American Economic Journal: Macroeconomics*) uses more conservative assumptions and finds GDP would have been 13.5% higher in 2009 (roughly $2 trillion) if geographic wage dispersion had remained at 1964 levels. The key point is that even the most conservative version of the finding represents a multi-trillion dollar economic cost of exclusionary zoning.

**The policy implication**: Zoning reform is not just a housing affordability measure. It is a macroeconomic growth intervention with distributional benefits — wages would rise most for lower-skilled workers who currently cannot afford to live near high-productivity employers.

Sources: [Hsieh-Moretti: Housing Constraints and Spatial Misallocation (AEJ: Macro 2019)](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257%2Fmac.20170388); [NBER: Housing Constraints and Spatial Misallocation](https://www.nber.org/papers/w21154); [Econlib on the correction](https://www.econlib.org/a-correction-on-housing-regulation/); [CityLab: Urban Housing Crunch Costs $1.6 Trillion](https://www.citylab.com/equity/2015/05/the-urban-housing-crunch-costs-the-us-economy-about-16-trillion-a-year/393515/)

### The Racial History of Exclusionary Zoning

Single-family zoning is not a politically neutral technical planning choice. Its origins are inseparable from racial exclusion.

**Before Euclid**: The first racial zoning ordinance was adopted by Baltimore in 1910, requiring Black and white residents to live in separate blocks. Louisville's 1914 ordinance was struck down by the Supreme Court in *Buchanan v. Warley* (1917), which established that race-based zoning violated the Fourteenth Amendment.

**The Euclid workaround**: After *Buchanan* invalidated explicit racial zoning, planners developed single-family zoning as a facially neutral substitute. Because "apartment houses" were culturally coded as housing for immigrants, low-income workers, and racial minorities, single-family exclusivity served the same exclusionary function without naming race. The Supreme Court upheld Euclid, Ohio's comprehensive zoning ordinance in *Village of Euclid v. Ambler Realty Co.* (272 U.S. 365, 1926). Justice Sutherland's majority opinion compared apartment buildings to "a pig in the parlor instead of the barnyard" — language whose social subtext was understood at the time. The decision opened the floodgates: within years, single-family zoning spread to municipalities across the country.

**The HOLC-FHA system**: The Home Owners' Loan Corporation (1933) drew "Residential Security Maps" color-coding neighborhoods from "A" (green, best) to "D" (red, hazardous). Neighborhoods with Black residents were systematically graded D. The Federal Housing Administration (1934) then used these maps — explicitly incorporating them into its underwriting standards — to determine which neighborhoods qualified for FHA-insured mortgages. Only 2% of the $120 billion in federally backed housing loans between 1934 and 1962 went to nonwhite borrowers (Rothstein, *The Color of Law*, 2017). The FHA additionally recommended — in its Underwriting Manual — that developers include racially restrictive covenants in deeds.

**Restrictive covenants**: Racial covenants barring sale to non-white buyers were declared unenforceable in *Shelley v. Kraemer* (1948) but remained recorded on deeds for decades. Minnesota's Mapping Prejudice project has identified over 53,000 properties in the Twin Cities with recorded racial covenants.

**Present-day legacy**: NCRC (2024) analysis finds that areas designated "hazardous" by HOLC in the 1930s are still less likely to have residents with mortgages today. The long-run mechanism: exclusion from home purchase during the period of maximum appreciation (1945–1975) permanently foreclosed Black families from the primary wealth-building channel available to white middle-class families.

### Construction Cost Drivers Beyond Zoning

Zoning is the dominant but not sole cause of high housing costs. The full picture includes:

**Materials and supply chain**: The COVID-era lumber price spike (lumber futures rose more than 400% from April 2020 to May 2021) demonstrated the vulnerability of construction supply chains. Although lumber prices subsequently declined, other materials — drywall, copper, concrete — remained elevated. The National Association of Home Builders tracks that building material costs increased approximately 35–40% between 2019 and 2024.

**Labor shortages**: Construction employment has not recovered proportionally from the 2008–2012 trough, which drove experienced tradespeople permanently out of the industry. The construction sector in 2024 had an estimated 400,000+ unfilled job openings. Apprenticeship pipelines have not rebuilt to pre-crisis levels.

**Parking minimums**: Parking requirements — typically 1–2 spaces per unit — add $25,000–$75,000 per unit to construction costs in urban areas (structured parking) and consume land that could otherwise be used for housing. A 2020 study found that cities with eliminated or reduced parking minimums saw significantly more multifamily development than comparable cities that retained them.

**Permitting delays**: From permit application to certificate of occupancy averages 6–24 months in supply-constrained cities, representing carrying costs at prevailing interest rates (typically 5–7% on construction loans) for the entire period. In California, entitlement processes can extend to 3–5 years for contested projects.

**The LIHTC premium**: Research finds that housing financed through the Low-Income Housing Tax Credit costs 20% more per square foot than comparable market-rate construction, due to added compliance costs, transaction costs from the tax equity structure, and the multiple intermediaries involved. This is a subsidy vehicle problem, addressed below.

Sources: [NBER: Impact of Zoning on Housing Affordability](https://www.nber.org/papers/w8835); [NAHB: Housing Shortage Tracker Q4 2024](https://www.nar.realtor/blogs/economists-outlook/housing-shortage-tracker-insights-from-the-final-quarter-of-2024); [NowBAM: Housing Shortage 41 Years to Fix](https://nowbam.com/housing-shortage-could-take-41-years-to-fix-in-some-regions-heres-why/)

## Section 3: Supply-Side Interventions — Evidence Base

### Minneapolis: The First City to Eliminate Single-Family Zoning

Minneapolis adopted its 2040 Comprehensive Plan in 2018, with core provisions taking effect in 2020. The plan eliminated single-family-only zoning citywide — allowing triplexes by right on all residential lots — and relaxed parking minimums near transit and commercial corridors.

**Results**: Rents in Minneapolis rose just 1% from 2017 to 2022, while rents in the rest of Minnesota rose 14% over the same period. The city's housing stock grew by approximately 12%. Minneapolis significantly outperformed comparable Midwestern cities in housing production and affordability.

**The key nuance (Governing/Reason analysis)**: Eliminating single-family zoning and allowing 2–4 unit buildings by right accounted for only 1% of new permits. The dominant drivers of new supply were policies around parking minimums (eliminated near commercial areas and transit) and streamlined permitting near commercial corridors — which together produced nearly 21,000 new permitted units. This finding matters for federal policy design: the headline reform (single-family zoning elimination) was symbolically important and set the legal framework, but the proximate production drivers were parking and process reform.

Sources: [Pew: Minneapolis Land Use Reforms Blueprint 2024](https://www.pew.org/en/research-and-analysis/articles/2024/01/04/minneapolis-land-use-reforms-offer-a-blueprint-for-housing-affordability); [TCF: How Minneapolis Ended Single-Family Zoning](https://tcf.org/content/report/minneapolis-ended-single-family-zoning/); [Governing: How Important Was the Single-Family Zoning Ban?](https://www.governing.com/community/how-important-was-the-single-family-housing-ban-in-minneapolis); [Planetizen: Zoning Reform Working in Minneapolis 2024](https://www.planetizen.com/news/2024/01/126989-zoning-reform-working-minneapolis)

### Oregon: First State to Legalize Missing Middle Statewide

Oregon's House Bill 2001 (2019) made Oregon the first state to comprehensively bar local governments from imposing exclusive single-family residential zoning, requiring cities above 25,000 population to permit duplexes and cities above 10,000 to permit duplexes at minimum, with larger cities required to allow triplexes, fourplexes, and cottage clusters.

**Results by 2024**: Over 1,400 middle-housing units were built in Portland's single-dwelling zones through the city's Residential Infill Project. However, permit numbers remain modest relative to need — builders have been cautious, and cost-of-construction barriers continue to dampen production. The supply response has been real but smaller than advocates projected.

**The constraint**: HB 2001 changed what can be built, but not necessarily what pencils out financially. In lower-cost markets (eastern Oregon), upzoning has produced more; in high-cost Portland, the construction cost/rental income math remains difficult for small infill projects without additional subsidy or process reform.

Sources: [City Observatory: In Oregon Middle Isn't Missing Any More](https://cityobservatory.org/in-oregon-the-middle-isnt-missing-any-more/); [1000 Friends of Oregon: HB 2001 Gets Us Back on Track 2023](https://friends.org/news/2023/3/hb-2001-gets-us-back-housing-track/); [Oregon DAS: Housing Needs Analysis 2024](https://www.oregon.gov/das/oea/Documents/OHNA-Methodology-Report-2024.pdf)

### California ADUs: The Most Productive Infill Reform

California's progressive liberalization of accessory dwelling unit (ADU) regulations — through a series of legislative packages from 2017 to 2024 — represents the most successful example of infill housing production in the US.

**Key legislative milestones**: AB 2299 and SB 1069 (2016) required ministerial approval and removed owner-occupancy requirements; AB 68 (2019) eliminated setback requirements for certain ADUs; SB 9 (2021) legalized duplexes and lot splits on single-family parcels statewide; AB 976 (2023) permanently eliminated owner-occupancy requirements; AB 68 exempted ADUs under 750 sq ft from impact fees.

**Production results**: ADU permit issuance in California surged 334% from 2016 to 2022, totaling 83,865 permitted ADUs over that period. Los Angeles County alone permitted more than 45,000 ADUs in 2023. ADUs now account for approximately 21% of all new housing units permitted in California.

**Limitations**: SB 9 (duplex by right statewide) has produced fewer units than advocates hoped — local implementation resistance, financing complexity for small developers, and construction costs remain barriers. The ADU success reflects that simplifying the regulatory path for homeowners to add units on existing lots outperforms the statewide upzoning approach in near-term production. ADUs are also predominantly market-rate and serve the moderate-income rather than extremely low-income market.

Sources: [California YIMBY ADU Report 2024](https://cayimby.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/CAY-ADU_Report-2024-v4.pdf); [Brookings: California's ADU Lessons for Other States](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/californias-decade-long-effort-to-legalize-adus-offers-lessons-for-other-us-states-and-regions/); [HCD California ADU Page](https://www.hcd.ca.gov/policy-and-research/accessory-dwelling-units)

### The Filtering Mechanism: Does Building Luxury Housing Help Low-Income Renters?

The "filtering" critique holds that market-rate (especially luxury) housing construction doesn't help low-income households because the initial occupants are wealthy. The empirical literature substantially refutes this framing.

**Evan Mast (2021, Journal of Urban Economics)**: Mast tracked 686 market-rate multifamily buildings built 2009–2017 in 12 large US cities. Building a market-rate building housing 100 people ultimately leads 45–70 people to move out of below-median-income neighborhoods — through a chain of moves that propagates through the housing market within 3–5 years. The mechanism: new buildings draw higher-income residents from existing housing, which frees up older units for lower-income renters. Of movers into new luxury buildings, 67% came from elsewhere in the same metro; by the sixth link in the moving chain, 40% of movers came from below-average-income neighborhoods.

**The timeline**: Essentially all the across-market filtering happens within 2–5 years of new construction — faster than critics assumed. Research finds that new housing units in a market lead to lower rents within one year for comparable nearby units.

**The scale constraint**: Filtering is a real mechanism that operates across the market but it cannot close the affordability gap for households at the extreme low end (0–30% AMI). A household earning $15,000/year cannot afford even a fully filtered 40-year-old apartment in San Francisco. Supply reform helps the market broadly but leaves the bottom quintile requiring targeted subsidy. The policy implication is not supply *or* subsidy but supply *and* subsidy at different points in the income distribution.

Sources: [Mast 2021: Effect of New Market-Rate Housing on Low-Income Market (JUE)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0094119021000656); [Upjohn Institute: Mast Working Paper](https://research.upjohn.org/up_workingpapers/307/); [Planetizen: Market-Rate Development Filters Into Affordable Housing 2021](https://www.planetizen.com/news/2021/09/114685-study-market-rate-development-filters-naturally-occurring-affordable-housing)

## Section 4: Demand-Side Interventions

### Housing Choice Vouchers — Evidence and Scale Problem

The Housing Choice Voucher (HCV) program — Section 8 — is the federal government's primary housing assistance tool for low-income renters. It provides tenant-based subsidies: voucher holders pay 30% of their adjusted income toward rent; the program pays the difference up to a local payment standard.

**Current scale**: Approximately 2.3 million vouchers are in circulation, serving roughly 5 million people. The program operates through approximately 2,200 Public Housing Authorities.

**Effectiveness**: Vouchers work as described — they substantially reduce the housing cost burden for recipients. CBPP research finds that families with vouchers spend a manageable share of income on rent, have greater housing stability, and show improved outcomes for children compared to non-assisted families in similar income categories.

**The scale problem**: Due to inadequate funding, only 1 in 4 households eligible for a voucher receives any form of federal rental assistance. CBPP estimates that approximately 22.4 million cost-burdened renter households exist against roughly 5.1 million assisted households — leaving approximately 17 million eligible households without assistance. President Biden proposed extending the program to all eligible households; CBPP did not publish a specific total-universalization cost estimate but the order of magnitude is $150–200 billion annually in additional federal outlay, based on average per-voucher cost (approximately $12,000–$15,000/year) scaled to the unserved eligible population.

**The wait-list problem**: Because demand vastly exceeds supply, PHAs operate lotteries and waiting lists. New York City's 2024 Section 8 lottery received applications from 200,000 households for a waiting list that will issue approximately 1,000 vouchers per month. At that rate, a newly listed household waits 17 years for assistance. Many PHAs have closed their waiting lists entirely because the queue exceeds any foreseeable capacity.

**Landlord acceptance**: Vouchers are only as effective as the rental units available to voucher holders. Evidence from large cities shows significant landlord rejection of vouchers, concentrating voucher holders in lower-opportunity neighborhoods and undercutting the mobility benefit. Source of income (SOI) anti-discrimination laws at the state level — now in effect in 17 states and the District of Columbia — have modestly improved acceptance rates, but the problem persists.

Sources: [CBPP: Housing Choice Voucher Program](https://www.cbpp.org/research/housing/the-housing-choice-voucher-program); [HUD.gov: Housing Choice Voucher Program](https://www.hud.gov/topics/housing_choice_voucher_program_section_8); [Harvard JCHS: Americas Rental Housing 2024](https://www.jchs.harvard.edu/americas-rental-housing-2024); [Terner Center: Who is Served by the HCV Program?](https://ternercenter.berkeley.edu/research-and-policy/who-is-served-by-the-housing-choice-voucher-program-a-state-and-county-level-look/)

### Moving to Opportunity — The Long-Run Evidence

The Moving to Opportunity (MTO) experiment is the most rigorous evaluation of housing voucher policy ever conducted. HUD randomly assigned approximately 4,600 families in high-poverty public housing projects in five cities to three groups: an experimental group that received vouchers restricted to low-poverty (under 10% poverty rate) neighborhoods, a comparison group that received standard unrestricted vouchers, and a control group that received no vouchers.

**The initial (2002) adult findings**: For adults, the experimental voucher group showed no statistically significant improvement in economic outcomes — employment rates, earnings, public benefit receipt. The initial evaluation was widely read as a failure of the mobility hypothesis.

**The Chetty-Hendren-Katz reversal (2016, *American Economic Review*)**: Raj Chetty (Harvard), Nathaniel Hendren (Harvard), and Lawrence Katz (Harvard) returned to the MTO data with two decades of tax records. The adult null result held. But for children who moved before age 13, the results were striking:

- College attendance rates increased significantly
- Earnings at age 26 were measurably higher
- Rates of single parenthood declined

The exposure-to-neighborhood-quality mechanism operates cumulatively and early: children who moved at age 8 benefited more than those who moved at age 12. Children who moved as adolescents showed slightly negative effects, perhaps due to social disruption.

**The policy design implication**: MTO demonstrates that housing vouchers paired with mobility assistance (counseling, landlord recruitment in low-poverty areas) can produce significant intergenerational benefits when deployed early in childhood. The adult null result suggests that mobility programs should be targeted to families with young children. The Opportunity Atlas (Chetty, Friedman, Hendren, Jones, Porter 2018) extends this finding, showing that childhood neighborhood quality predicts adult income with remarkable precision at the census tract level.

**The mechanism**: Children gain from exposure to lower-poverty neighborhoods through peer effects, school quality, and reduced exposure to violence and stress. The mechanism does not change adult behavior because adult employment networks, habits, and skills are already formed — but childhood outcomes are malleable.

Sources: [Chetty/Hendren/Katz 2016 (AER): Effects of Exposure to Better Neighborhoods](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/aer.20150572); [Equality of Opportunity Project: MTO Executive Summary](http://www.equality-of-opportunity.org/images/mto_exec_summary.pdf); [NBER Working Paper 21156](https://www.nber.org/papers/w21156); [Census/Chetty: The Opportunity Atlas](https://www.census.gov/content/dam/Census/programs-surveys/center-for-economic-studies/opportunity_atlas_paper.pdf)

### LIHTC — Effectiveness and Cost Concerns

The Low-Income Housing Tax Credit (1986) is the primary federal vehicle for financing affordable rental housing. It operates by allocating tax credits to states, which award them to developers; developers sell the credits to corporate investors (primarily banks meeting CRA obligations), generating equity for construction. In exchange, units must remain affordable (at 60% or 50% of area median income) for a minimum of 30 years.

**Scale**: Since 1987, LIHTC has financed approximately 3.7 million affordable housing units across 54,000+ projects. It finances roughly 50,000 units per year. The program costs the federal government approximately $13.5 billion annually in foregone tax revenue (2023).

**The cost problem**: Median per-unit production costs range from approximately $126,000 (Texas) to $326,000 (California), with high-end outliers reaching $606,000/unit in California. Research by the Government Accountability Office (2018) found that LIHTC developers produce units approximately 20% more expensive per square foot than comparable market-rate construction. The premium reflects:
- Transaction costs from the tax equity structure (multiple attorneys, syndicators, and institutional intermediaries each taking fees)
- Compliance costs (annual LIHTC monitoring, income certifications, set-aside tracking)
- The mismatch between CRA-motivated investor appetite and geographic housing need

**The efficiency debate**: Critics argue the LIHTC dollar-per-affordable-unit cost is higher than it needs to be, and that direct subsidy (vouchers, public housing capital grants) would produce more units for the same federal expenditure. Defenders argue that LIHTC leverages private capital and the private development system, keeping government out of construction management. The honest assessment: LIHTC produces real units but at a premium, and the program's political durability (bipartisan support from both affordable housing advocates and corporate investors) has preserved it against reform.

**LIHTC expansion proposals**: The Affordable Housing Credit Improvement Act (introduced in multiple Congresses, most recently 2023) would increase the annual LIHTC allocation by 50%. CBO estimates this would produce an additional 200,000+ affordable units over 10 years.

Sources: [Tax Foundation: LIHTC Details and Analysis](https://taxfoundation.org/research/all/federal/low-income-housing-tax-credit-lihtc/); [Tax Policy Center: What is LIHTC and How Does It Work?](https://taxpolicycenter.org/briefing-book/what-low-income-housing-tax-credit-and-how-does-it-work); [GAO: LIHTC Cost Assessment and Fraud Risk](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-18-637); [HUD USER: LIHTC Property Data](https://www.huduser.gov/portal/datasets/lihtc.html)

## Section 5: Homelessness — What Works and What Doesn't

### Housing First — The Evidence Base

Housing First is an approach that provides immediate permanent housing to homeless individuals without preconditions — sobriety, treatment compliance, psychiatric stability — that are required under the older linear "treatment first" or "continuum of care" model.

**Sam Tsemberis and Pathways to Housing (1992)**: Tsemberis founded Pathways to Housing in New York City after observing that the linear model systematically excluded people with active mental illness and substance use disorders — exactly the population with the highest need. Pathways provided immediate independent apartments with Assertive Community Treatment (ACT) support.

**The original RCT (1992–1997)**: In a randomized controlled trial published in American Psychiatry, 88% of Pathways Housing First participants remained stably housed over the study period, compared to 47% in the standard residential treatment system. The control group required sobriety and treatment engagement as preconditions; the treatment group received housing first, with services offered but not required.

**The Canadian At Home/Chez Soi RCT (2009–2013)**: The largest Housing First RCT ever conducted, spanning five Canadian cities with 2,148 participants randomly assigned to Housing First with ACT or to treatment as usual. At one-year follow-up, 73% of Housing First participants resided in stable housing versus 31% of treatment-as-usual participants. Housing First produced greater improvements in housing stability, quality of life, and community functioning. Crucially, there was no evidence that Housing First without preconditions increased substance use disorder rates — the most common objection.

**HUD's Housing First review (2023)**: HUD's Homelessness Research Institute synthesized the evidence base: Housing First consistently outperforms linear residential treatment for housing stability. The evidence for additional outcomes (psychiatric functioning, substance use) is more mixed — housing alone does not cure addiction or mental illness — but it is a necessary condition for engagement with treatment.

**Cost evidence**: A 2017 analysis in *Health Affairs* found that Housing First reduced emergency service use (ER visits, hospitalizations, incarcerations) by approximately 60% compared to shelter or street. In Seattle/King County, the 1811 Eastlake Housing First project found cost savings of approximately $2,500/month per participant from reduced emergency service use — nearly enough to offset the housing subsidy cost. Permanent supportive housing costs $25,000–$45,000 per year per unit; emergency service utilization for chronically homeless individuals without housing averages $30,000–$100,000 per year.

Sources: [Pathways Housing First](https://www.pathwayshousingfirst.org/); [HUD User: Housing First Review (2023)](https://www.huduser.gov/archives/portal/periodicals/em/spring-summer-23/highlight2.html); [At Home/Chez Soi RCT — Psychiatric Services 2015](https://psychiatryonline.org/doi/10.1176/appi.ps.201400167); [Tsemberis Street Roots 2020](https://www.streetroots.org/news/2020/11/11/sam-tsemberis-has-revolutionized-way-we-think-about-homelessness)

---

### What Doesn't Work: Shelters, Criminalization, and the Linear Model

**Emergency shelter limitations**: Shelter provides a count off the street in PIT surveys but does not end homelessness. In most jurisdictions, shelter is overcrowded, high-disorder, and functions as a temporary warehouse rather than a transitional step toward permanent housing. Research on shelter-to-permanent-housing transitions shows that the majority of shelter users cycle through the shelter system repeatedly without obtaining stable housing.

**Criminalization**: Sweeps, anti-camping ordinances, and anti-panhandling laws have proliferated as cities face political pressure to reduce visible homelessness. The evidence is consistent: criminalization disperses homeless individuals and destroys possessions, community connections, and documentation required for housing applications. *Martin v. City of Boise* (9th Cir. 2018, upheld in modified form) held that the Eighth Amendment bars criminally punishing homeless individuals for sleeping outside when no adequate shelter is available. Subsequent litigation has continued as cities seek to narrow the ruling. The empirical literature finds no evidence that criminalization reduces homelessness; the only documented effect is displacement and barrier creation.

**The linear model**: The "continuum of care" model required sobriety, medication compliance, and step-by-step progression through shelter → transitional housing → permanent housing. Evidence from multiple evaluations finds that individuals with the most severe disabilities — the chronic homeless with co-occurring mental health and substance use disorders — cannot reliably meet these preconditions and therefore cycle through the system without reaching permanent housing. This population accounts for disproportionate service utilization and benefits most strongly from Housing First.

Sources: [National Alliance to End Homelessness: State of Homelessness 2025](https://endhomelessness.org/state-of-homelessness/); [HUD: 2024 AHAR Part 1](https://www.huduser.gov/portal/datasets/ahar/2024-ahar-part-1-pit-estimates-of-homelessness-in-the-us.html); [HUD User: Housing First Review](https://www.huduser.gov/archives/portal/periodicals/em/spring-summer-23/highlight2.html)

## Section 6: The Rent Control Evidence — A Contested Debate

### The Diamond et al. Finding

The most rigorous study of rent control in the US is Rebecca Diamond, Tim McQuade, and Franklin Qian's 2019 paper published in the *American Economic Review*, exploiting a 1994 San Francisco law change that extended rent control to buildings with four or fewer units built before 1980.

**The benefit finding**: Rent control reduced tenant displacement significantly. Covered tenants were 19% more likely to remain at their address over the following decade than comparable tenants in non-covered buildings. For the covered tenants, rent stabilization worked as intended.

**The supply finding**: Landlords responded to rent control by reducing their rental housing supply by **15%**. Buildings subject to rent control were 8 percentage points more likely to be converted to condos or tenancies-in-common. Some landlords redeveloped rent-controlled buildings entirely (demolition allowed under Ellis Act).

**The market-wide effect**: The 15% supply reduction drove up market rents city-wide by approximately 5.1% — a substantial spillover that fell on non-covered tenants, including future residents and lower-income renters who could not obtain covered units. Rent control, by protecting incumbents while reducing supply, effectively transferred costs from covered tenants to non-covered tenants — disproportionately younger and lower-income households trying to enter the market.

**Heterogeneous landlord response**: Corporate landlords were more likely to remove units from the rental market than individual landlords. Diamond et al. note that rent control "raises revenue for rental subsidies from landlords" but in a regressive way — corporations with capital access could escape the burden through conversion or redevelopment while smaller landlords were more constrained.

**What the study does and doesn't show**: The San Francisco study is specific to a jurisdiction with a dominant condo conversion option, California's Ellis Act (which makes building exit from rental housing relatively easy), and the nation's most extreme housing scarcity. The supply reduction effect depends on the availability of exit options. The study is the strongest causal identification of rent control's supply effects in the US literature; it cannot be generalized without attention to local context.

Sources: [Diamond/McQuade/Qian 2019 (AER): Effects of Rent Control Expansion](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/aer.20181289); [Diamond/Qian 2019 (AEA Papers and Proceedings): Who Pays for Rent Control?](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/pandp.20191021); [Microeconomic Insights: Who Benefits from Rent Control?](https://microeconomicinsights.org/who-benefits-from-rent-control-evidence-from-san-francisco/)

---

### The Counterarguments and the Weight of Evidence

**The incumbent protection case**: Rent control advocates correctly observe that without protection, rent-burdened tenants — particularly elderly, disabled, and low-income residents — face displacement when landlords raise rents to market level. The Diamond study confirms the benefit for existing tenants. The question is whether the aggregate welfare effect (benefits to incumbents minus costs to non-covered tenants and the market-wide supply reduction) is positive.

**Jurisdictional variation**: The Diamond finding is strongest where exit options (condo conversion, redevelopment) are easy. In jurisdictions with strong just-cause eviction requirements and conversion restrictions, the supply reduction from rent control may be smaller. European rent regulation in Sweden (with broad coverage and restrictions on conversion) shows smaller supply effects than the US literature.

**The New York evidence**: New York City's rent stabilization system covers approximately 1 million units. Studies consistently find misallocation — apartments held by higher-income tenants below market rent — but evidence on supply effects is mixed, in part because New York's regulatory environment restricts conversion more than San Francisco's.

**The consensus position**: The mainstream economics literature — including economists who support robust housing assistance — finds that rent control reduces housing supply when landlords have exit options, creates misallocation (keeping high-income tenants in below-market apartments), and fails to benefit the lowest-income households most in need. The policy case for rent control as a substitute for supply reform and direct subsidy is weak. As a short-term anti-displacement measure in specific communities facing acute gentrification, it may be defensible as a bridge while supply reform scales.

Sources: [Diamond/McQuade/Qian 2019 (AER)](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/aer.20181289); [ScienceDirect: Rent Control and Supply of Affordable Housing 2025](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1051137725000221)

## Section 7: Homeownership, Wealth, and Racial Justice

### The Oliver-Shapiro Wealth Studies

Melvin Oliver (UCLA) and Thomas Shapiro (Brandeis) documented in *Black Wealth/White Wealth* (1995, updated 2006) that income equality — when achieved — does not produce wealth equality, because wealth transfers intergenerationally through inheritance and asset appreciation, and the policy system systematically excluded Black families from asset-building during the period when the white middle class was building wealth through federally subsidized homeownership.

**Key findings**:
- African-American families have a dime for every dollar of white family wealth — a 10:1 ratio
- Over half of Black families fell below the asset poverty line at the century's turn
- Even Black families who reached equivalent incomes to white families had dramatically less wealth, because income does not inherit and does not compound

**Shapiro's *Hidden Cost of Being African American* (2004)**: Based on interviews with 200 families and national survey data from 10,000 households, Shapiro documented that residential segregation systematically depresses the appreciation rate of Black-owned homes. Homes in neighborhoods more than 10% Black lose approximately 16% of value; white-owned homes appreciate approximately $28,000 more than Black-owned homes on average. The mechanism is not the quality of the home but the racial composition of the neighborhood — a direct legacy of HOLC and FHA policies that channeled investment away from Black neighborhoods.

**The wealth-building trap**: Homeownership builds wealth through forced savings (mortgage principal paydown) and appreciation. When appreciation is depressed by segregation, the wealth-building mechanism is impaired even when homeownership is achieved. Black families caught in this trap experience homeownership's costs (property taxes, maintenance, illiquidity) without proportionate appreciation gains.

Sources: [Shapiro: Hidden Cost of Being African American (2004)](https://www.racepowerofanillusion.org/articles/hidden-cost-being-african-american/); [Oliver/Shapiro: Disrupting the Racial Wealth Gap (2019)](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1536504219830672); [Washington University Law: Race, Homeownership and Wealth](https://openscholarship.wustl.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1242&context=law_journal_law_policy)

---

### Down Payment Assistance and First-Generation Buyer Programs

Down payment is the dominant barrier to homeownership for first-generation buyers. Median down payment for first-time buyers is approximately 8% of purchase price (National Association of Realtors, 2024) — for a median-priced home ($410,000 in Q1 2024), approximately $33,000, which takes the median renter household with no inherited wealth roughly 8–10 years to accumulate.

**State DAP programs**: State housing finance agencies (HFAs) in 49 states operate down payment assistance programs, typically providing 2–5% of purchase price as a second lien or grant. Evidence shows these programs reach first-generation and minority buyers at high rates. However, program scale is small relative to need.

**The Biden first-generation homebuyer credit proposal**: Would have provided up to $25,000 in down payment assistance to first-generation homebuyers. Not enacted, but CBO-scored at approximately $100 billion over 10 years.

**The appreciation compounding effect**: Because homeownership wealth compounds through appreciation, every year of delayed access to homeownership reduces lifetime wealth. A Black family delayed from homeownership by 5 years due to discrimination, exclusion, or income barriers loses not just 5 years of appreciation but the compounding of that appreciation over the remaining decades of ownership — and the inherited wealth that would have transferred to children.

Sources: [Mass. Budget: History of Racist Federal Housing Policies](https://massbudget.org/2021/08/06/a-history-of-racist-federal-housing-policies/); [Housing Affordability Institute: History of Exclusion](https://www.housingaffordabilityinstitute.org/history-of-exclusion-in-americas-housing-policies/)

## Section 8: Land Value Tax — Theory and Evidence

### The Henry George Argument

Henry George's *Progress and Poverty* (1879) argued that the economic value of land is created not by landowners but by surrounding community investment — public infrastructure, density of economic activity, proximity to employers and amenities. Therefore, taxing the land value (not the improvements on it) captures a value that belongs to the community and imposes no disincentive on productive activity, since the supply of land is fixed and taxing it cannot reduce the quantity of land available.

**The policy implication**: A land value tax (LVT) or split-rate property tax (taxing land at a higher rate than buildings) encourages intensive use of well-located land, discourages land banking and speculative holding, and does not penalize investment in buildings. Standard property taxes, which tax both land and improvements equally, penalize density and development (building more triggers higher taxes) while rewarding underutilization (holding land vacant is subsidized relative to building on it).

---

### Pennsylvania Split-Rate Tax Experiments

Nearly 20 Pennsylvania cities have employed split-rate property taxation — taxing land at a higher rate than improvements — since 1913. This represents the largest and longest-running US laboratory for LVT policy.

**Harrisburg**: Adopted a two-rate system in the 1970s under Mayor Stephen Reed. By the mid-1990s, Harrisburg had reversed severe urban decline — vacancy rates fell, building permits rose, and the city went from one of the most distressed in Pennsylvania to a modest revival. A two-rate analysis found that over 90% of Harrisburg property owners paid less under the two-rate system than they would under a uniform single-rate property tax.

**Altoona**: Adopted a pure land value tax (100% on land, 0% on improvements) from 2011 to 2016 — the only US city to rely solely on LVT. Repealed in 2016, not because it failed but because (1) the county and school district continued standard property taxes, limiting the incentive effect; (2) residents were unfamiliar with the rationale. Empirical studies of the Altoona period found increased building activity.

**Georgetown/Lincoln Institute research**: Econometric studies of Pennsylvania's split-rate cities find positive correlations between land tax intensity and building activity, urban densification, and lower vacancy rates, without negative effects on overall property values or economic output.

**The Singapore model**: Singapore's Housing Development Board (HDB) directly controls land supply — the state owns most land and leases 99-year leaseholds. This allows the government to capture land value appreciation directly and recycle it into infrastructure and social housing. In 2023, approximately 77% of Singapore's resident population lived in HDB housing, with homeownership rates of ~90%. A typical first-time HDB buyer spends 5–25% of monthly income on mortgage payments (often covered entirely by mandatory retirement savings contributions). Singapore's model is not directly transferable — it required state land ownership assembled before rapid development — but it demonstrates that housing affordability is achievable at scale when land value is treated as a public rather than private asset.

Sources: [FHWA: Land Value Tax](https://www.fhwa.dot.gov/ipd/value_capture/defined/land_value_tax.aspx); [Lincoln Institute: Split-Rate Taxation](https://www.lincolninst.edu/app/uploads/legacy-files/pubfiles/yang_wp21zy1.pdf); [Wikipedia: LVT in the United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Land_value_tax_in_the_United_States); [Washington Examiner: Short Life of Pennsylvania's Radical Tax Reform](https://www.washingtonexaminer.com/the-short-life-of-pennsylvanias-radical-tax-reform); [Singapore HDB: Public Housing Icon](https://www.hdb.gov.sg/about-us/our-role/public-housing-a-singapore-icon); [SDG16: Singapore HDB 80%](https://www.sdg16.plus/policies/singapores-housing-development-board-provides-housing-to-80-percent-of-residents/)

## Section 9: Public and Social Housing

### Vienna — The Gemeindebau Model

Vienna is the most cited example of large-scale municipal social housing in the developed world. The city directly owns approximately 220,000 housing units (the Gemeindebau), housing about 500,000 residents — approximately 22% of Vienna's total housing stock. An additional 22% of residents live in non-profit limited-dividend housing associations (Wiener Wohnen). In total, approximately 60% of Vienna's residents live in subsidized housing.

**Outcomes**: Renters in Austria on average spend 21% of household income on rent — compared to roughly 30–35% in comparable US cities. Austrian studies estimate that the subsidized sector depresses private market rents by 5–20% depending on local conditions, through competition with the private market.

**The financial constraint**: Vienna stopped building new Gemeindebau in 2005 because it had become a financial burden — accumulated debt of $1.3 billion from sticky rents (legally constrained from rising to maintain affordability) against high maintenance and renovation costs of aging stock. Approximately one-third of municipally owned units lack modern amenities such as central heating or private bathrooms. New social housing production has shifted to the non-profit association model, which is financially more sustainable.

**The income mixing feature**: Vienna's social housing is not means-tested at the income floor — residents across a wider income range are eligible. This creates income-mixed communities rather than concentrated poverty, which is credited with better outcomes on social integration and lower stigma than US public housing.

**The US transferability question**: Vienna's model depends on land costs that were set when the city acquired sites at 20th century prices, a non-profit association legal structure that differs from US non-profit law, and a political culture comfortable with permanent public ownership. The model cannot be transplanted wholesale, but specific elements — income mixing, non-profit operator model, long-term public subsidy commitment — are applicable.

Sources: [Climate and Community Institute: Green Social Housing Vienna](https://climateandcommunity.org/research/vienna-green-social-housing/); [Shelterforce: How to Bring Vienna's Model to the US](https://shelterforce.org/2023/12/19/how-we-can-bring-viennas-housing-model-to-the-u-s/); [Policy Options: Does Vienna Have the Answer? 2024](https://policyoptions.irpp.org/2024/03/vienna-housing-lessons/); [AEI: Setting the Record Straight on Vienna Social Housing](https://www.aei.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/Setting-the-record-straight-on-the-Vienna-Social-Housing-Model-final.pdf)

### US Public Housing — Failure Factors and HOPE VI

**The original failure mode**: US public housing was built as concentrated, racially segregated, high-rise developments, systematically underinvested, managed by patronage-ridden local housing authorities, and stripped of capital funding through annual appropriations battles. By the 1980s, developments like Pruitt-Igoe (St. Louis, demolished 1972–1976), Cabrini-Green (Chicago, demolished 1995–2011), and the Robert Taylor Homes (Chicago, demolished 1998–2007) had become symbols of concentrated poverty, crime, and disinvestment. The failure reflected political choices about who deserved quality housing, not an inherent impossibility of public housing.

**HOPE VI (1992–2010)**: HUD's response was to demolish "severely distressed" public housing and replace it with mixed-income developments through HOPE VI grants. Scale: 98,592 public housing units demolished; 97,389 mixed-income units produced (57% replacement public housing, 30% affordable, 13% market-rate).

**HOPE VI outcomes — mixed**:
- Physical environments improved substantially
- Poverty concentration fell in redeveloped sites
- Most original public housing residents did not return: approximately 80% of HOPE VI-redeveloped units were occupied by new rather than original residents, meaning the program's benefits flowed largely to a new population
- Adult economic outcomes for displaced residents: minimal improvement in employment or earnings
- Children's outcomes: children who resided in HOPE VI projects earned 15% more at age 26 than children in comparison projects (recent Chetty et al. analysis), with the largest gains for demolitions in high-poverty neighborhoods in large cities

**The net public housing unit loss**: The 100,000-unit demolition with partial replacement reduced the absolute stock of deeply subsidized public housing, worsening the gap at the bottom of the income distribution.

Sources: [HUD User: HOPE VI Mixed-Income Redevelopment](https://www.huduser.gov/portal/periodicals/cityscpe/vol21num2/ch11.pdf); [NPR/Chetty: HOPE VI Public Housing Research 2026](https://www.npr.org/2026/01/28/nx-s1-5691692/hope-vi-public-housing-opportunity-insights-raj-chetty); [ScienceDirect: Children of HOPE VI Demolitions 2024](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0047272724001245); [Reimagine RPE: HOPE VI Displaces Poor](https://www.reimaginerpe.org/node/1811)

### Community Land Trusts — The Hybrid Model

Community Land Trusts (CLTs) separate ownership of land (retained by the non-profit CLT in perpetuity) from ownership of the buildings (sold to individual homeowners). The CLT permanently removes land from speculative markets; homeowners build equity but through a resale formula that keeps the home affordable for subsequent buyers.

**Scale**: Over 300 CLTs operate in the US, representing 40,000+ affordable housing units. Growth accelerated: CLT numbers increased 30% from approximately 225 to over 300 between 2011 and 2022.

**Evidence on outcomes (Lincoln Institute, 2021)**:
- 60% of CLT homeowners subsequently bought a market-rate home — the CLT functioned as a stepping-stone to conventional homeownership
- CLT homeowners typically invest under $2,000 at purchase and accumulate approximately $14,000 in equity on exit — modest but real wealth-building
- In a pre-post study of 4,108 sales across 58 shared equity programs, 95% of homes remained affordable (priced at or below 80% AMI) at first sale to a new buyer
- Buying a shared-equity home was associated with a $36,346 reduction in mortgage debt compared to market-rate purchases

**Foreclosure resistance**: Research finds CLT homeowners have lower foreclosure and mortgage delinquency rates than comparable conventional homeowners — partly because price appreciation is limited (reducing the incentive to over-leverage), and partly because CLTs actively monitor and support homeowners facing financial distress.

**The trade-off**: CLT homeowners forgo full market appreciation in exchange for affordable purchase prices. They build wealth, but less than conventional homeowners in appreciating markets. This is the design: the CLT captures the appreciation that would otherwise benefit only the homeowner and recycles it as permanent affordability for future residents.

Sources: [TIME: Community Land Trusts 2025](https://time.com/7212194/community-land-trusts-how-they-combat-affordable-housing-crisis/); [Results for America: CLT Evidence Catalog](https://catalog.results4america.org/programs/community-land-trusts); [NLC: Community Land Trusts Guide for Local Governments](https://www.nlc.org/wp-content/uploads/2021/08/Community-Land-Trusts_A-Guide-for-Local-Governments_Report-1.pdf); [Journal of Housing Economics: CLT Units Study 2024](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/10511482.2024.2362643)

## Section 10: Manufactured and Modular Housing

### Cost Advantage

Manufactured housing — built in factories under the federal HUD Code (established 1976) and transported to sites on a steel chassis — offers a significant cost advantage over site-built housing.

**Harvard JCHS (2024)**: Excluding land costs, a manufactured home can be **27% to 65% less expensive** than a comparable site-built home. The per-square-foot production cost is less than half that of comparable site-built single-family homes. Savings come from factory efficiency (stable workforce, controlled supply chain, no weather delays), design standardization, and reduced on-site labor.

**2024 HUD Code update**: HUD issued its most extensive update in three decades in September 2024 — 87 changes, including expansion to cover multi-residential structures (duplex, triplex, quadplex). This opens manufactured construction to multifamily formats that were previously unavailable under HUD Code.

---

### Regulatory Barriers

**Zoning exclusion**: In rural areas, local regulations often confine manufactured housing to designated mobile home parks while limiting the zoning of new parks. In urban areas, outright prohibitions or onerous design requirements are common. These restrictions are often driven by NIMBY opposition to perceived quality concerns — concerns the industry argues are outdated given the HUD Code and quality improvements since the 1970s.

**Personal property status**: Many states still treat manufactured homes as personal property rather than real property, making traditional mortgage financing unavailable. FHA and VA loan programs for manufactured housing exist but are less favorable than site-built financing. The inability to access conventional mortgage products raises effective borrowing costs and limits the wealth-building potential of manufactured homeownership.

**The steel chassis requirement**: The HUD Code's requirement that manufactured homes be built on a permanent steel chassis (which defines them as manufactured rather than modular) adds $5,000–$10,000 to each unit's cost and constrains design flexibility. The ROAD to Housing Act (proposed) would eliminate this requirement, but doing so requires legislative action because the chassis requirement is in the authorizing statute, not changeable by rulemaking alone.

Sources: [Harvard JCHS: Barriers to Manufactured Housing 2024](https://www.jchs.harvard.edu/sites/default/files/research/files/harvard_jchs_barriers_manufactured_housing_2024.pdf); [HUD 2024 Code Update](https://archives.hud.gov/news/2024/pr24-233.cfm); [Niskanen Center: Manufactured Housing Reform 2024](https://www.niskanencenter.org/two-big-developments-in-manufactured-housing-reform-new-rules-new-bill/); [Housing Wire: HUD Extensive Manufactured Housing Update](https://www.housingwire.com/articles/hud-issues-extensive-update-to-manufactured-housing-safety-standards/); [Urban Institute: Manufactured Housing Can Fill Affordable Gaps](https://housingmatters.urban.org/articles/how-manufactured-housing-can-fill-affordable-housing-gaps)

## Section 11: The Federal Legislative Landscape

### National Zoning Atlas — Documenting the Problem

The National Zoning Atlas project is digitizing and analyzing zoning codes across all 33,000+ US jurisdictions. Early state analyses document the scale of exclusionary zoning:

- Connecticut: 90.6% of state land is zoned for single-family housing by right; 2.2% is zoned for four-or-more-unit housing by right
- The Montana Zoning Atlas directly informed transformative statewide land-use reforms in 2023–2024, demonstrating the atlas's policy utility
- California, Colorado, New York, and other high-cost-state analyses are underway

The Atlas provides a factual foundation for federal legislation: without a systematic map of zoning across the country, federal policy targeted at exclusionary zoning lacks the data to identify where the problem is worst.

Sources: [National Zoning Atlas](https://www.zoningatlas.org/); [NZA Analysis](https://www.zoningatlas.org/analysis); [HUD User: National Zoning Atlas to Inform Housing Research](https://www.huduser.gov/portal/periodicals/cityscape/vol25num3/ch3.pdf)

---

### Federal Legislative Proposals

**The YIMBY Act (Yes In My Backyard Act)**: Bipartisan legislation (Rep. Derek Kilmer D-WA, Sen. Todd Young R-IN in recent Congresses; 45 House co-sponsors). Would require Community Development Block Grant (CDBG) recipients to report every five years on their exclusionary land-use policies and document plans for reform. Passed House Financial Services Committee 48-0. Advocates acknowledge it is narrow — it requires reporting and planning, not reform — and is designed to be politically achievable by avoiding federal override of local zoning. As a first step in a federal engagement with zoning restrictiveness, it is a starting point rather than a solution.

**The Housing Accountability Act (federal, S.581, 118th Congress)**: Would tie federal transportation funding to state and local compliance with housing production targets — a significantly stronger lever than YIMBY Act reporting requirements. This approach (conditioning federal infrastructure dollars on housing production) has bipartisan conceptual support but faces significant resistance from local governments and their congressional delegations who view it as federal overreach.

**The ROAD to Housing Act (21st Century)**: Introduced in the Senate by the Banking, Housing and Urban Affairs Committee majority in 2025. Contains multiple provisions: eliminates the manufactured housing steel chassis mandate, reduces zoning barriers, streamlines permitting for factory-built construction, and restores accountability in HUD programs.

**Voucher universalization**: No enacted legislation, but both parties have introduced proposals to expand voucher funding. CBPP analysis finds that serving all eligible households would cost on the order of $150–200 billion annually — achievable through a phased expansion funded by the revenue reforms in Domain 5.

**The Fair Housing Act enforcement gap**: HUD's Office of Fair Housing and Equal Opportunity (FHEO) is systematically underfunded, with a backlog of thousands of unresolved complaints. AFFH (Affirmatively Furthering Fair Housing) regulations, requiring communities receiving HUD funds to assess and address patterns of segregation, were suspended by the Trump administration in 2020, partially restored by Biden in 2023, and face continued litigation. Restoring and funding AFFH is a prerequisite for enforcing the integration commitments made in 1968.

Sources: [YIMBY Action: Federal YIMBY Bills 2025](https://yimbyaction.org/endorsements/federal-yimby-bills-2025/); [Semafor: Is Congress Having its YIMBY Moment? 2024](https://www.semafor.com/article/05/23/2024/is-congress-having-its-yimby-moment); [Senate Banking: ROAD to Housing Act 2025](https://www.banking.senate.gov/newsroom/majority/the-facts-the-21st-century-road-to-housing-act-cuts-red-tape-builds-more-homes-and-restores-accountability); [Congress.gov: S.581 Housing Accountability Act 2023](https://www.congress.gov/bill/118th-congress/senate-bill/581); [CBPP: Housing Choice Voucher Program](https://www.cbpp.org/research/housing/the-housing-choice-voucher-program)

### The Policy Architecture

Taken together, the evidence supports a tiered federal housing agenda:

**Tier 1 — Remove supply barriers (market-led, bipartisan potential)**

Mandate zoning reform as a condition of federal transportation and infrastructure funding (Housing Accountability Act model). Prohibit single-family-exclusive zoning in jurisdictions receiving CDBG. Require ministerial approval (no discretionary review) for housing projects that comply with objective standards. Eliminate parking minimums on federal-aid corridors. Fund the National Zoning Atlas to full national coverage.

*Evidentiary basis*: Glaeser-Gyourko, Hsieh-Moretti, Minneapolis, Oregon. GDP gain estimate: 1–10% over 20 years depending on reform depth.

**Tier 2 — Expand direct subsidy for low-income households**

Phase voucher expansion toward universalization. Estimated cost: $150–200 billion/year to cover all eligible households. Prioritize families with children under 13 to capture MTO children's outcome benefits. Fund mobility counseling and landlord recruitment in high-opportunity neighborhoods.

Reform LIHTC to reduce transaction costs and increase units per federal dollar — either through direct subsidy (capital grants for non-profit construction), program simplification, or requiring below-cost land from federal land banks.

*Evidentiary basis*: Chetty/Hendren/Katz MTO 2016; CBPP voucher analysis; LIHTC GAO cost study.

**Tier 3 — Address homelessness through Housing First**

Fund Housing First as the national standard for chronic homelessness. Permanent supportive housing costs $25,000–$45,000/year and reduces emergency service costs by $2,500+/month. Cost-effectiveness compared to shelter cycling and criminalization is favorable. This requires federal capital funding for permanent supportive housing units and operating subsidies.

*Evidentiary basis*: Tsemberis RCT, At Home/Chez Soi, HUD review.

**Tier 4 — Repair the racial homeownership gap**

Fund down payment assistance targeted to first-generation buyers (whose parents were excluded from federally subsidized homeownership). Enforce Fair Housing Act and AFFH with adequate FHEO funding. Expand CLT seed capital for community land trusts in historically redlined neighborhoods. Reform appraisal methodologies to reduce racial bias.

*Evidentiary basis*: Oliver-Shapiro, HOLC legacy studies, NCRC 2024.

**Tier 5 — Land reform for the long term**

Pilot split-rate property taxation in cities willing to adopt it, using federal incentive funding. Reform federal tax treatment of land (currently identical to building improvements) to reduce land speculation. Expand manufactured housing eligibility and financing.

*Evidentiary basis*: Pennsylvania experiments, Henry George theory, Harvard JCHS manufactured housing analysis.

### What Rent Control Cannot Do

The reform synthesis must include an honest assessment of rent control: it protects individual incumbents in the short run but reduces the supply available to future renters, creates misallocation (high-income tenants in below-market apartments), and functions as a tax whose burden falls on non-covered tenants and incoming residents — disproportionately younger and lower-income households. The Diamond et al. finding (15% supply reduction in San Francisco) is the strongest causal evidence available. The policy conclusion: deploy rent control narrowly as an anti-displacement bridge where displacement is acute and immediate, not as a substitute for supply reform and direct subsidy.

### Cost Estimates and Fiscal Framework

The housing reform agenda is expensive at full scale. A rough order-of-magnitude for the federal investment:

| Component | Annual Federal Cost (steady state) |
|---|---|
| Voucher near-universalization (phased over 10 years) | $50–150B/year |
| LIHTC expansion (50% increase) | $7B/year additional |
| Permanent supportive housing (Housing First) | $10–20B/year |
| Down payment assistance programs | $10B/year |
| CLT seed capital fund | $2–5B/year |
| Fair housing enforcement and AFFH | $1–2B/year |
| **Total** | **~$80–200B/year** |

For context: the US currently spends approximately $50 billion annually on direct federal housing programs (HUD budget). The proposed expansion represents 1.5–4x the current commitment. Against the $400 billion in annual housing cost burden borne by 22.4 million cost-burdened households, and against the estimated $150–200 billion in annual emergency service costs associated with homelessness and housing instability, the investment case is favorable. The Hsieh-Moretti GDP gain from supply reform alone — worth roughly $1.4 trillion annually in sustained productivity — dwarfs the direct subsidy cost.

## Counterargument Consolidation

**"We can't build our way out of the affordability crisis."**

Partially true for the bottom quintile, false for the market overall. The Mast filtering evidence and the Minneapolis result demonstrate that supply growth broadly reduces rents. For the extremely low-income households facing a 7.2 million unit shortage of affordable units, supply reform alone is insufficient — the economics of market construction cannot serve households at 0–30% AMI without subsidy. The correct position is supply *and* subsidy, not supply *or* subsidy.

**"Rent control protects vulnerable tenants."**

It protects incumbent tenants in covered units. Diamond et al. demonstrate it does so at the cost of a 15% long-run supply reduction and a 5.1% city-wide rent increase, imposed on non-covered tenants who are often younger and lower-income. As a short-run anti-displacement measure in a gentrification emergency, it may be defensible. As a long-run affordability strategy, the evidence is negative.

**"Public housing failed — we shouldn't repeat the experiment."**

The US public housing failure reflected specific design choices: racial segregation, concentrated poverty, annual appropriations vulnerability, political disinvestment. Vienna, Singapore, and successful US scattered-site and mixed-income models demonstrate that public housing works when those failure modes are avoided. The HOPE VI experience shows that mixed-income redevelopment improves physical conditions but displaces most original residents. The policy lesson is not to abandon public housing but to design it correctly — income-mixed, adequately capitalized, managed by non-profit operators with long-term commitment.

**"Zoning is a local matter — federal government has no role."**

Zoning's exclusionary effects are not local in their consequences: they suppress national GDP by $1.4+ trillion annually (Hsieh-Moretti), enforce racial segregation that is a federal civil rights violation, and impose housing costs on workers in other states who cannot access high-productivity cities. The federal government has a constitutional basis (Spending Clause, Commerce Clause, Fair Housing Act) to condition federal funding on zoning reform. The current voluntary approach (YIMBY Act reporting) is a starting point; stronger tools (transportation funding conditioning) are warranted by the scale of the problem.
