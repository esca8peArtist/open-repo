# Criminal Justice & Policing — Evidence Deepening

*Prepared April 2026. Supplements the Democratic Renewal Proposal (democratic-renewal-proposal.md), Criminal Justice Domain. This document does not restate the proposal's reform architecture — it deepens the evidentiary foundation with specific data, contested findings, and the strongest counterarguments.*

## Gaps This Document Fills

The criminal justice domain in the proposal identifies structural reforms but is thin on:
- Specific cross-national incarceration comparisons that place the US in stark relief
- Evidence on *what actually reduces crime* vs. what the US currently funds
- The body camera null-result literature (the proposal implies cameras are a proven fix)
- The full Fryer/Knox-Lowe-Mummolo dispute on racial bias in police shootings — two incompatible findings, neither suppressible
- The Portugal/Oregon contrast: what successful vs. failed drug decriminalization looks like

- The incapacitation vs. marginal deterrence question: does incarcerating more people prevent more crime at the margin?

### Racial Disparities

**> Evidence callout**: Black Americans comprise 13.6% of the US population but 32% of sentenced state and federal prisoners. The national Black incarceration rate is approximately six times the white rate. Jail incarceration rates in 2023 were roughly 3.6 times higher for Black residents (552 per 100,000) than for white residents (155 per 100,000). The lifetime likelihood of imprisonment for Black men born in 2001 is approximately four times that of their white counterparts.

Hispanic Americans are incarcerated at approximately 1.3x the white rate in prisons; in jails the ratio is higher.

The racial disparity is not uniform across states. In some states, the Black-to-white incarceration ratio exceeds 10:1 (Iowa, Wisconsin, New Jersey). In no state does the Black incarceration rate fall below the white rate.

**Structural driver note**: The Sentencing Project's 2024 report *One in Five* identifies that even controlling for offense type and criminal history, Black defendants receive longer sentences than white defendants for equivalent conduct — a finding replicated across multiple federal sentencing datasets.

Sources: [Sentencing Project: One in Five (2024)](https://www.sentencingproject.org/app/uploads/2024/02/One-in-Five-Ending-Racial-Inequity-in-Incarceration.pdf); [Prison Policy Initiative: Updated Charts 2024](https://www.prisonpolicy.org/blog/2024/04/01/updated-charts/); [factually.co: Incarceration Rates Black vs. White 2024](https://factually.co/fact-checks/justice/incarceration-rates-black-vs-white-americans-2022-2024-1a0bbb)

**Total system cost**: The US spends more than $80 billion annually on prisons alone. When jails, probation, parole, policing, and courts are included, the total criminal justice spend exceeds $300 billion per year. A separate analysis quantifying costs imposed on families of the incarcerated (lost wages, legal fees, child welfare costs) estimates an additional ~$350 billion in annual societal costs.

### Historical Trend: 1970 to Present

The US incarceration rate was stable at approximately 110 per 100,000 from 1925 to 1972. The explosion in mass incarceration is entirely a product of policy choices made between 1972 and 2008:

- **1972**: ~330,000 incarcerated; rate ~161 per 100,000
- **1980**: ~503,000; rate ~220
- **1990**: ~1.14 million; rate ~458
- **2000**: ~1.94 million; rate ~683 (jail + prison)
- **2008**: ~2.31 million — the peak
- **2020**: ~1.7 million (accelerated by COVID-19 releases)
- **2023**: ~1.9 million (partial rebound post-COVID)

The imprisonment rate grew 6–8% annually from 1972 through 2000 — the longest and largest sustained prison expansion in any democracy in recorded history. The growth was driven by: (1) mandatory minimum sentencing for drug offenses; (2) truth-in-sentencing requirements eliminating early release; (3) three-strikes laws; (4) the War on Drugs intensification under Reagan and Clinton.

**Post-2008 plateau and partial decline**: The population peaked in 2008 and declined modestly through 2019, a period when state fiscal pressures (not reform conviction) drove some decarceration. The decline was not accompanied by crime increases — homicide rates reached 50-year lows in 2014.

Sources: [Sentencing Project: Mass Incarceration Trends](https://www.sentencingproject.org/reports/mass-incarceration-trends/); [Brennan Center: History of Mass Incarceration](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/analysis-opinion/history-mass-incarceration); [National Academies: Growth of Incarceration in the US](https://www.nationalacademies.org/read/18613/chapter/4)

### Pretrial Detention and the Cash Bail System

**> Evidence callout**: In midyear 2023, approximately 70% of the US jail population (467,600 people at any given moment) were unconvicted — held pretrial, not serving a sentence. The pretrial detention rate has increased more than 470% over the last four decades. The US pretrial detention rate (approximately 150 per 100,000) is 50% higher than Russia, making the US the world's leading jailer of unconvicted people.

More than 60% of pretrial defendants are detained solely because they cannot afford to post bail — not because a judge determined they posed a flight or safety risk.

**The Bail Project's counter-evidence**: Data from The Bail Project shows that 92% of people released without cash bail returned to court — challenging the core premise used to justify expanding pretrial detention. Studies consistently show that pretrial detention itself increases the probability of conviction (independent of evidence), increases sentence length, and increases post-release recidivism. Holding someone pretrial for even 2–3 days measurably increases the probability of losing employment, housing, and custody of children.

**What the US does vs. what evidence supports**: The US uses money-based detention (cash bail), which selects for poverty rather than risk. Evidence-based alternatives — risk assessment at arraignment, supervised release, court date reminders — achieve equivalent court return rates at a fraction of the cost and without the documented collateral harm of pretrial detention.

Sources: [Prison Policy Initiative: Pretrial Detention Research](https://www.prisonpolicy.org/research/pretrial_detention/); [Vera Institute: Justice Denied](https://www.vera.org/publications/for-the-record-justice-denied-pretrial-detention); [MacArthur Justice: Pretrial Detention](https://www.macarthurjustice.org/issue/ending-the-punishment-of-poverty/pretrial-detention/); [Davis Vanguard: Bail Project 92% Return Rate 2025](https://davisvanguard.org/2025/12/pretrial-release-data-bail-project/)

## Section 2: What Actually Reduces Crime

*This section is the most policy-critical in the document. The US invests overwhelmingly in incarceration and prosecution. The interventions with the strongest evidence are primarily in public health, economic support, and targeted community programs.*

---

### Lead Abatement and the Environmental Crime Reduction Evidence

**> Evidence callout**: Two independent 2007 studies — Rick Nevin and Jessica Wolpaw Reyes — found that leaded gasoline phase-out explains a substantial portion of the 1990s US crime decline.

**Nevin (2007)**, analyzing data from nine countries, found that gasoline lead use from 1941–1975 explained 90% of variation in US violent crime from 1964–1998, with best-fit lags of 18–23 years. Countries that phased out leaded gasoline earlier saw earlier crime declines.

**Reyes (2007)**, using a state-level natural experiment, found that the phase-out of leaded gasoline was responsible for approximately a **56% decline in violent crime** between 1992 and 2002. A 10% reduction in lead exposure corresponded to an average 8% reduction in violent crime two decades later.

**Lead paint application**: Cost-benefit analyses suggest lead paint hazard control yields $17–$221 in societal returns for every dollar invested, primarily through reduced healthcare, special education, and criminal justice costs, plus increased lifetime earnings and tax revenue.

**Status of the evidence**: The lead-crime hypothesis is now mainstream economics. The mechanism (lead as a neurotoxin causing impulsivity and reduced executive function during critical developmental windows) is biologically well-supported. The policy implication — that environmental remediation is crime prevention — remains dramatically underrepresented in criminal justice budgets.

Sources: [Nevin (2007) in Environmental Research](https://pic.plover.com/Nevin/Nevin2007.pdf); [Reyes (2007) NBER Working Paper w13097](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w13097/w13097.pdf); [PMC: Lead-Crime Hypothesis Systematic Review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10393136/); [County Health Rankings: Lead Paint Abatement](https://www.countyhealthrankings.org/strategies-and-solutions/what-works-for-health/strategies/lead-paint-abatement-programs)

### Summer Youth Employment Programs

**> Evidence callout**: Sara Heller's 2014 RCT, published in *Science*, randomly assigned 1,634 students from 13 high-violence Chicago neighborhoods to Chicago's One Summer Plus program (8 weeks, minimum wage, adult mentor) or a control group. Results: **43% reduction in violent crime arrests over the following 16 months**. The reduction occurred largely after the summer program ended — indicating behavioral change, not just displacement of time.

The program cost approximately $1,100 per participant. Given that a single violent crime arrest costs the system roughly $10,000–$20,000 in processing alone (before incarceration costs), the ROI is substantial.

**Gelber, Isen, and Kessler (2016)** studied the New York City summer jobs lottery and found that male lottery winners had significantly lower felony arrest rates — a 16% reduction — over the following year.

**Mechanism**: Summer employment programs appear to reduce crime primarily through changes in non-cognitive skills (impulsivity, conflict resolution, engagement with legitimate institutions) rather than through income effects alone. The persistence of effects after employment ends supports a behavioral change interpretation.

**What the US does vs. what evidence supports**: Summer youth employment programs are perennially underfunded and disproportionately cut in budget cycles. The evidence base for their crime reduction effects is among the strongest for any short-term intervention.

Sources: [Heller (2014) in Science](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1257809); [J-PAL: Summer Jobs Reduce Violence](https://www.povertyactionlab.org/evaluation/summer-jobs-reduce-violence-among-youth-facing-barriers-opportunity-united-states); [Chicago Crime Lab: Summer Jobs Study](https://crimelab.uchicago.edu/resources/summer-jobs-reduce-violence-among-disadvantaged-youth/)

### SNAP/Food Assistance and Crime

**> Evidence callout**: Cody Tuttle's 2019 study (*American Economic Journal: Economic Policy*) used a regression discontinuity design exploiting Florida's 1996 modification of the federal lifetime food stamp ban for drug felons. Finding: the ban **increases recidivism** among drug traffickers. The increase is driven by financially motivated crimes, indicating that SNAP access reduces crime by providing financial support to individuals at risk of reoffending.

A related 2019 study by Carr and Packham found that staggering SNAP disbursement schedules leads to large reductions in crime and theft at grocery stores — 17.5% reduction in overall theft, 20.9% in grocery theft — in the days immediately following disbursement.

**Implication**: Programs restricting SNAP access for people with criminal records — still in effect in many states — are criminogenic. Removing these restrictions has direct public safety benefits.

Sources: [Tuttle (2019) in AEJ: Economic Policy](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/pol.20170490); [Snapping Back full paper](https://codytuttle.github.io/tuttle_snap_recidivism.pdf); [Newsweek: Restricting Food Stamps Is Soft on Crime](https://www.newsweek.com/restricting-food-stamps-soft-crime-opinion-1803694)

### Certainty vs. Severity of Punishment

**> Evidence callout**: Chalfin and McCrary's 2017 comprehensive review of the deterrence literature (*Journal of Economic Literature*, 55(1): 5–48) is the definitive statement of the field's consensus:

- Evidence that crime responds to **certainty** of punishment (i.e., probability of arrest) is robust.
- Evidence that crime responds to **severity** of punishment (i.e., sentence length) is weak and inconsistent.
- Elasticity of crime with respect to police: −0.2 (burglary) to −0.7 (murder)
- Elasticity of crime with respect to incarceration: −0.1 to −0.7, with most recent estimates at the low end

**Practical implication**: Investing in police presence and investigative capacity reduces crime more reliably per dollar than investing in longer sentences. Mandatory minimums, which increase severity, have weak deterrence evidence but very high incarceration costs.

**What the US does vs. what evidence supports**: The US has historically invested in sentence length (severity) as the primary deterrence mechanism. The evidence says the opposite lever — certainty, including clearance rates for violent crimes — is more effective. Currently, only about 50% of violent crimes in the US result in an arrest; only about 25% result in conviction. Increasing clearance rates would increase deterrence without increasing incarceration.

Sources: [Chalfin & McCrary (2017), Journal of Economic Literature](https://eml.berkeley.edu/~jmccrary/chalfin_mccrary2017.pdf); [AEA: Criminal Deterrence Review](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/jel.20141147)

### Hot Spots Policing

**> Evidence callout**: Weisburd (2015) documented a "law of crime concentration at places": across every city studied, approximately 1% of street segments account for approximately 50% of crime. This spatial concentration is stable over decades.

Meta-analyses of randomized controlled trials of hot spots policing — directed patrol at these high-crime micro-locations — find **consistent, statistically significant crime reductions** at treated places, with no evidence of spatial displacement into surrounding areas. Rather, crime control benefits "diffuse" into proximate areas (the so-called "diffusion of benefits" effect).

The National Academies of Sciences (2018) report on proactive policing concluded that hot spots policing is one of the most evidence-supported approaches to short-term crime reduction.

**Limitation**: Hot spots policing reduces crime at specific locations, but does not address the underlying social conditions producing that crime concentration. The evidence supports it as a short-term supplement to social investment, not a replacement.

Sources: [Weisburd (2015) on crime concentration](https://cina.gmu.edu/wp-content/uploads/2020/11/Braga-Weisburd2020_Article_DoesHotSpotsPolicingHaveMeanin.pdf); [Journal of Experimental Criminology: Hot Spots Meta-Analysis](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11292-019-09372-3)

### READI Chicago — Community Violence Intervention

**> Evidence callout**: READI Chicago (Rapid Employment and Development Initiative) is the most rigorously evaluated individual-level gun violence intervention to date. The evaluation was a randomized controlled trial of 2,456 men at the highest estimated risk of shooting or being shot in Chicago. Results over 20 months:

- **63% fewer arrests** for shootings and homicides
- **19% fewer victimizations** for shootings and homicides
- For participants referred by outreach workers (pre-specified subgroup): **79% reduction in arrests**, **43% reduction in victimizations**
- Estimated social savings: **$182,000–$916,000 per participant**
- Benefit-cost ratio: **4:1 to 18:1**

The program model: paid transitional employment + cognitive behavioral therapy + case management, targeting men identified by gunshot wound records and outreach worker nomination as highest-risk.

**Scale context**: In the 20 months post-randomization, 11 per 100 men in the control group were shot or killed — 52 times the rate for average Chicagoans. READI is not a general crime prevention program; it is a targeted intervention for a very high-risk population, and it works.

Sources: [READI Chicago Experimental Evaluation — NBER w30852](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w30852/w30852.pdf); [PMC: READI Chicago Evaluation](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10898100/); [UChicago Crime Lab: READI](https://crimelab.uchicago.edu/projects/readi/)

## Section 3: Policing Reform Evidence

### Body Cameras — The Null Result

**> Evidence callout — contested finding**: Yokum, Ravishankar, and Coppock (2019) published the largest RCT of police body-worn cameras to date, involving 2,224 Metropolitan Police Department officers in Washington DC, randomly assigned to receive cameras or not. Result: **cameras had no statistically significant effect on use of force, civilian complaints, or other policing outcomes**.

This null result challenged a widespread assumption that cameras would constrain officer behavior. Previous smaller studies had found positive effects; the DC RCT was large enough to detect effects of practical significance and found none.

**Why it may not generalize**: The DC Metro PD has a relatively high baseline accountability; the null result may not apply to departments with weaker internal discipline. Cameras may also affect behavior when officers know recordings will be reviewed — a resource-intensive practice many departments do not implement.

**Policy implication**: Body cameras are not a simple accountability mechanism. The evidence suggests cameras matter when paired with systematic review and consequences — not as a standalone intervention. The proposal should not treat camera adoption as a meaningful reform absent policies requiring regular footage review and clear consequences for non-activation.

Sources: [Yokum et al. (2019) in PNAS](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1814773116); [PMC: BWC RCT](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6535014/)

### Traffic Stop Disparities — Stanford Open Policing Project

**> Evidence callout**: The Stanford Open Policing Project analyzed over 200 million traffic stops from 21 state patrol agencies and 29 municipal police departments. Key findings:

- Black drivers were approximately **20% more likely to be stopped** than white drivers relative to their share of the residential population
- Black drivers made up a **smaller share of those stopped at night**, when driver race is harder to determine — suggesting racial bias (not differential driving behavior) influences daytime stop decisions ("veil of darkness" test)
- In every state analyzed, both Black and Hispanic drivers are searched at **significantly higher rates** than white drivers
- Despite higher search rates, **contraband was found at equal or lower rates** for Black and Hispanic drivers than for white drivers — indicating police require less suspicion to search Black and Latino drivers
- After stops, Black and Hispanic drivers were more likely to be cited, searched, and arrested

**What the US does vs. what evidence supports**: The traffic stop data is among the most comprehensive evidence of racial disparate impact in routine law enforcement. The evidence supports: (1) ending consent searches (studies show they are nearly always racially disparate); (2) redirecting traffic enforcement for equipment/license violations to civil rather than criminal processes; (3) publishing stop and search data by race in real time.

Sources: [Stanford Open Policing Project: Findings](https://openpolicing.stanford.edu/findings/); [Stanford: Large-Scale Analysis of Racial Disparities](https://5harad.com/papers/traffic-stops.pdf)

### Use-of-Force Disparities — The Fryer/Knox-Lowe-Mummolo Dispute

This is one of the most contested empirical debates in criminal justice research. It is directly relevant to the proposal's claims.

**Fryer (2016, 2019 in Journal of Political Economy)**: In the most cited study on this question, Roland Fryer found that on non-lethal use of force, Black and Hispanic individuals are more than **50% more likely** to experience force in police interactions, even controlling for circumstances. However, he found **no racial bias in police shootings** — a finding he called "the most surprising result of my career."

**Fryer's methodological approach**: He used administrative data from police stops to assess whether race predicts officer-involved shootings, conditional on an interaction occurring. This "conditional on encounter" analysis is the source of the primary critique.

**Knox, Lowe, and Mummolo (2020, American Political Science Review)**: Using different data and methodology, they found Fryer's approach introduces post-treatment bias. If officers are racially biased in *initiating* stops and arrests (i.e., they stop Black people who would not have been stopped if white), then conditioning on the stop erases evidence of discrimination. Knox et al. found that even in the NYPD's stop-and-frisk data, accounting for this bias leads to estimates showing **substantial racial bias** in officers' decisions to draw weapons.

**The state of the science**: Both studies are peer-reviewed and published in top journals. They use different data and make different identification assumptions. The Fryer result — no bias in shootings — depends on assumptions about encounter-selection that Knox et al. show are likely violated. The methodological literature, as summarized by RAND (2020), concludes there is no reliable method to cleanly identify racial bias in police shootings from administrative data. Neither finding should be presented as settled.

**The non-contested finding**: The racial disparity in *all other* uses of force (restraints, tasing, strikes) is consistently found across multiple studies, is large, and is not explained by differences in encounter circumstances.

Sources: [Fryer (2019) in Journal of Political Economy](https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/abs/10.1086/701423); [NBER w22399](https://www.nber.org/papers/w22399); [RAND: Methodological Challenges](https://www.rand.org/research/gun-policy/analysis/essays/methodological-challenges-research-racial-bias-police-shootings.html); [Boston Review: Race, Policing, and the Limits of Social Science](https://www.bostonreview.net/articles/race-policing-and-the-limits-of-social-science/)

### Mental Health Co-Response Programs — Denver STAR

**> Evidence callout**: Denver's Support Team Assisted Response (STAR) program launched in June 2020, deploying teams of mental health clinicians and emergency medical technicians to low-risk mental health and substance abuse calls in place of police. Results:

- First 6 months: responded to ~750 mental health calls with **zero arrests, zero injuries, zero police assistance required**
- June 2020 through December 2024: over 12,000 clinical encounters; only **3% required a mandatory psychiatric hold**
- ~68% of people contacted were experiencing homelessness; ~61% had active mental health diagnoses

**Limitation noted in Urban Institute evaluation**: Housing is the biggest unmet need for STAR clients; the program's clinical success is limited by the absence of housing resources. STAR changes how crises are responded to but does not address what creates the crises.

**Applicability**: The Denver model is one of several; Eugene, Oregon's CAHOOTS program (running since 1989) handles approximately 24% of Eugene's 911 call volume without police. The evidence across programs is consistent: a significant share of calls dispatched to armed officers involve no safety threat and are better managed by unarmed health workers.

Sources: [Urban Institute: Denver STAR Evaluation](https://www.urban.org/research/publication/evaluating-alternative-crisis-response-denvers-support-team-assisted-response); [Denver Gov: STAR Program](https://www.denvergov.org/Government/Agencies-Departments-Offices/Agencies-Departments-Offices-Directory/Public-Health-Environment/Community-Behavioral-Health/Behavioral-Health-Support/Behavioral-Health-Outreach/Support-Team-Assisted-Response-STAR-Program); [CBS News: Denver Health Workers Replace Cops](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/denver-health-professionals-replaced-cops-in-handling-hundreds-of-low-level-incidents-for-6-months-and-successfully-did-so-with-no-arrests/)

### Civilian Oversight — What Structures Matter

The evidence on civilian oversight effectiveness is candid: most oversight boards do not produce meaningful accountability. Why:

- Only **6 of the 50 largest police agencies** have civilian oversight entities with any **disciplinary authority** — the rest can only make non-binding recommendations to the police chief
- Average time from investigation to resolution: **2–3 years**
- When discipline is recommended, consequences are typically limited to loss of vacation time or mandatory training
- Washington Post investigative reporting found that police departments and political leaders routinely undermine board findings

**What structures are associated with better outcomes**: Research identifies the following features of effective oversight: (1) statutory independence (not established by police department or city manager); (2) subpoena power and unfettered access to personnel and records; (3) authority to impose or recommend discipline with appeal to civil service board, not just the chief; (4) adequate funding; (5) community representation in board composition.

One study linked jurisdictions with **strong oversight entities** to **reductions in police homicides of community members** — though the sample size is small and causation is difficult to establish.

Sources: [Council on Criminal Justice: Civilian Oversight Evidence](https://policing.counciloncj.org/assessing-the-evidence/xi-civilian-oversight/); [Governing: Do Civilian Review Boards Work?](https://www.governing.com/workforce/do-civilian-review-boards-work); [Washington Post: How Police Undermine Civilian Oversight](https://www.washingtonpost.com/investigations/interactive/2021/civilian-oversight-police-accountability/); [NACOLE: Benefits of Oversight](https://www.nacole.org/benefits)

## Section 4: Sentencing and Mandatory Minimums

### Does Sentence Severity Deter Crime?

**Marvell and Moody (1996)** found that the probability of arrest (certainty) was strongly associated with crime reductions — consistent with deterrence theory. Their findings on severity were less consistent; mandatory minimums showed limited general deterrent effects in their modeling.

**Helland and Tabarrok (2007)** is the most rigorous study of California's three strikes law. Using a natural experiment comparing those with two strikeable offenses (facing extremely severe penalties for a third) to those with one strikeable offense, they found approximately a **20% lower rearrest rate** among the two-strike group. However, they then conducted a cost-benefit analysis and concluded: **"the crime-saving benefits are so small relative to the increased costs of incarceration that the lengthy prison sentences mandated by the third-strike provision cannot be justified on the basis of their effectiveness."**

**The Chalfin-McCrary synthesis** (see Section 2) concludes: the elasticity of crime to incarceration is between −0.1 and −0.3 at the current US incarceration rate. This means we are well into the range of diminishing returns — each additional incarceration produces progressively smaller crime-reduction effects.

**National Academies (2014) consensus**: The National Academies' comprehensive review *The Growth of Incarceration in the United States* concluded that the marginal incarceration of low-level offenders at current US incarceration levels has minimal crime-reduction effect. The US has incarcerated so many people that those remaining have lower propensity for violence than earlier cohorts sent to prison.

Sources: [Helland & Tabarrok (2007)](https://www.justice.gc.ca/eng/rp-pr/csj-sjc/ccs-ajc/rr02_1/p4_1.html); [National Academies: Growth of Incarceration](https://www.nationalacademies.org/read/18613/chapter/7); [Chalfin & McCrary (2017)](https://eml.berkeley.edu/~jmccrary/chalfin_mccrary2017.pdf)

### Fair Sentencing Act — The Crack/Powder Disparity

**Historical disparity**: The Anti-Drug Abuse Act of 1986 set a 100:1 crack-to-powder cocaine sentencing ratio. Five grams of crack cocaine triggered a 5-year mandatory minimum; 500 grams of powder cocaine triggered the same sentence. The racial impact was severe: by 2009, 79% of sentenced crack cocaine offenders were Black; only 10% were white.

**Fair Sentencing Act (2010)**: Reduced the disparity from 100:1 to 18:1. Eliminated the 5-year mandatory minimum for simple crack possession. Result: **substantial decrease in the number of crack cocaine offenders sentenced federally and in average sentence length**.

**First Step Act (2018)**: Made the FSA's provisions retroactive. FAMM research shows the retroactivity provision led to sentence reductions for over 3,000 crack cocaine cases, by an average of **six years per case**. Approximately 85% of those benefiting were Black.

**Remaining gap**: The 18:1 ratio is better than 100:1 but has no scientific or empirical basis. The pharmacological effects of crack and powder cocaine are nearly identical; the disparity reflects 1980s political dynamics, not evidence. The EQUAL Act (passed House in 2021, stalled in Senate) would eliminate the remaining disparity entirely.

Sources: [USSC: Report to Congress on Fair Sentencing Act (2015)](https://www.ussc.gov/sites/default/files/pdf/news/congressional-testimony-and-reports/drug-topics/201507_RtC_Fair-Sentencing-Act.pdf); [Fair Sentencing Act Fact Sheet 2023](https://legislativeanalysis.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/11/Fair-Sentencing-Act-Fact-Sheet-FINAL.pdf); [Brennan Center: Thousands Eligible for Fairer Sentences](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/analysis-opinion/thousands-prisoners-now-eligible-receive-fairer-sentences)

### Three Strikes: California Proposition 36 (2012) Outcomes

California's Prop 36 (2012) modified the three-strikes law to require that the third strike also be a serious or violent felony to trigger a 25-years-to-life sentence. It also created a resentencing pathway for people already serving life sentences for non-violent third strikes.

**Results** (California Policy Lab, 2025 evaluation):

- **2,217 people** were resentenced and released earlier under Prop 36
- 3-year recidivism rate for Prop 36 releases: **25%** convicted of a new offense within 3 years
- 3-year recidivism rate for total prison releases over same period: **42%**
- The majority of the 25% recidivism involved **misdemeanors**, not violent felonies
- Early data showed a 1.3% rearrest rate for Prop 36 releases in the first year vs. ~30% for all other releases over the same period
- Released population was predominantly **Black men aged 50+** at time of release

**Savings**: The Prop 36 population would have served, on average, many more years at California's ~$128,000 annual per-prisoner cost. At scale, the reform has generated hundreds of millions in savings with crime outcomes substantially better than the general prison population.

Sources: [California Policy Lab: Three Strikes Resentencing 2025](https://capolicylab.org/three-strikes-resentencing-under-proposition-36/); [Reason Foundation: Three-Strikers Recidivate Below Average](https://reason.org/commentary/three-strikes-recidivism/); [NAACPLDF: Progress Report Prop 36](https://www.naacpldf.org/publication/progress-report-three-strikes-reform-proposition-36)

### Pretrial Risk Assessment Tools — PSA and the Racial Bias Critique

The Arnold Foundation's Public Safety Assessment (PSA) tool is the most widely deployed pretrial risk assessment in the US. It uses 9 factors (prior convictions, prior failure to appear, age at first arrest, etc.) to predict flight risk and new criminal activity. It explicitly does not use race, gender, or socioeconomic status as inputs.

**The case for PSA**: Arnold Ventures' research shows PSA-guided decisions increase pretrial release rates while maintaining court appearance rates — reducing unnecessary pretrial detention.

**Dressel and Farid (2018)** in *Science Advances*: Found that the widely-used COMPAS recidivism prediction algorithm is **no more accurate than predictions made by people with no criminal justice expertise**. Moreover, a simple two-variable model matches COMPAS's full 137-variable model. This challenges the assumption that algorithmic complexity improves on human judgment.

**The racial bias critique of PSA and similar tools**: Even race-neutral inputs can produce racially disparate outputs if the underlying data reflects racialized enforcement. "Prior arrests" as an input means communities that have historically been over-policed will produce higher risk scores — not because of individual behavior, but because of differential enforcement. This is a structural, not a technical, problem.

**Evidence summary**: Risk assessment tools are better than cash bail at calibrating detention to actual risk. They are not race-neutral in effect because they encode histories of racially disparate enforcement. The proposal should treat them as a transitional improvement, not a permanent solution.

Sources: [Dressel & Farid (2018) in Science Advances](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/sciadv.aao5580); [Arnold Ventures: Risk Assessments and Pretrial Reform](https://www.arnoldventures.org/newsroom/risk-assessments-when-paired-with-appropriate-policies-can-contribute-significantly-to-pretrial-reform/); [MIT SERC: Dangers of Risk Prediction in CJ](https://mit-serc.pubpub.org/pub/risk-prediction-in-cj)

## Section 5: Reentry and Recidivism

### National Recidivism Rates

**> Evidence callout**: BJS's 2018 update on prisoner recidivism tracked state prisoners released in 2005 over 9 years:

- **68% rearrested within 3 years** of release
- **79% rearrested within 6 years**
- **83% rearrested within 9 years**

The primary offenses driving rearrest are property crimes and drug offenses — not violent crimes. Violent offenders have lower rearrest rates than property and drug offenders.

**The framing caveat**: Rearrest is not reconviction, and reconviction is not reimprisonment. The 83% 9-year rearrest figure is frequently misrepresented as a recidivism rate; the 9-year reconviction rate is substantially lower, and the reimprisonment rate lower still. The BJS data shows high system contact but does not show that two-thirds of people commit serious new crimes after release.

Sources: [BJS: 2018 Update on Prisoner Recidivism — 9-year follow-up](https://bjs.ojp.gov/content/pub/pdf/18upr9yfup0514.pdf); [Council on Criminal Justice: New National Recidivism Report](https://counciloncj.org/recidivism_report/)

### Ban the Box — Unintended Consequences for Black Men

**> Evidence callout — contested and nuanced**: Ban the Box (BTB) policies prevent employers from asking about criminal records until late in the hiring process. The intent is to give returning citizens a fair chance at employment.

**Doleac and Hansen (2020)** (*Journal of Labor Economics*) found that BTB policies **decrease the probability of employment by 3.4 percentage points (5.1%) for young, low-skilled Black men**. The mechanism: when employers cannot ask about criminal history, they engage in statistical discrimination — assuming that young Black men without degrees are more likely to have records, and avoiding hiring them preemptively.

**Important update**: A 2025 reanalysis by Burton and Wasser (*Census Bureau Working Paper CES-WP-25-58*) found that after correcting miscoded laws, the original Doleac-Hansen results are less robust than previously reported — results vary depending on data source (CPS vs. ACS). The negative employment effect for Black men may be smaller than originally estimated.

**What this means for the proposal**: The BTB evidence illustrates that well-intentioned reforms can produce negative unintended consequences when not paired with anti-discrimination enforcement and direct hiring support. BTB works best when combined with: (1) fair chance hiring requirements for government contractors; (2) individualized assessment requirements (not blanket bans); (3) active employer engagement and wage subsidy programs.

Sources: [Doleac & Hansen (2020) in Journal of Labor Economics](https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/full/10.1086/705880); [CLEAR: Doleac & Hansen Study Summary](https://clear.dol.gov/Study/unintended-consequences-ban-box-Statistical-discrimination-and-employment-outcomes-when); [Burton & Wasser (2025): Revisiting Unintended Consequences](https://www2.census.gov/library/working-papers/2025/adrm/ces/CES-WP-25-58.pdf)

### Occupational Licensing Restrictions

The American Bar Association's inventory documents **27,254 state occupational licensing restrictions** triggered by criminal records. Nationwide, approximately **32,000 laws** related to licensing include criminal record considerations. Effects:

- Healthcare alone accounts for nearly 7,500 restrictions, with ~44% being mandatory and ~83% lifetime
- The economy loses an estimated **$78–$87 billion annually** in foregone productivity from people with criminal records who are unemployed or underemployed due to licensing barriers
- 40 states have eased or eliminated some licensing barriers since 2015, but most changes are incremental
- Only 20 states bar licensing boards from denying licenses unless the offense is "directly related" to the licensed practice

**What the evidence supports**: The relevant question is whether a specific prior offense creates a substantive safety risk in a specific licensed role. Blanket bans — a prior drug conviction bars someone from becoming a barber, or a 20-year-old theft conviction bars someone from becoming a home health aide — are not defensible on public safety grounds and are criminogenic in their net effect.

Sources: [NELP: Unlicensed & Untapped](https://www.nelp.org/insights-research/unlicensed-untapped-removing-barriers-to-state-occupational-licenses/); [Institute for Justice: Barred From Working](https://ij.org/report/barred-from-working/); [NCSL: Barriers to Work](https://www.ncsl.org/labor-and-employment/barriers-to-work-improving-employment-in-licensed-occupations-for-individuals-with-criminal-records)

### Prison Education — The Rand PIACC Study

**> Evidence callout**: RAND's 2013 meta-analysis (*Evaluating the Effectiveness of Correctional Education*, Davis et al.) is the most comprehensive evaluation of prison education programs in the US:

- Inmates who participate in correctional education have **43% lower odds of recidivating** than those who do not (13 percentage point reduction in recidivism risk)
- Employment after release is **13% higher** for participants in academic or vocational education programs
- Cost-benefit: for every dollar invested in correctional education, **$4–$5 is saved** in reincarceration costs within 3 years of release

The 1994 federal ban on Pell Grants for incarcerated students — which eliminated most prison college programs within a decade — is directly implicated in the US's persistently high recidivism rates.

**Second Chance Pell Grant restoration (2023)**: As of July 1, 2023, Congress restored Pell Grant eligibility for incarcerated students. The Department of Education estimates **760,000 people** are newly eligible. By mid-2024:
- At least **45,000 incarcerated students** enrolled in college programs
- Those students earned **more than 18,000 credentials**
- Research from the pilot phase indicates that even **one college course** while incarcerated cuts reimprisonment probability by approximately half

**Caveat**: Infrastructure is still being built. As of 2024, program approval processes were in place in all states, but only one new program had received full federal approval. Scale-up will take years.

Sources: [RAND: Education and Vocational Training in Prisons (2013)](https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_reports/RR266.html); [RAND: How Effective is Correctional Education?](https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_briefs/RB9728.html); [Inside Higher Ed: State of Prison Higher Ed One Year After Pell Restoration (2024)](https://www.insidehighered.com/news/students/financial-aid/2024/07/03/state-prison-higher-ed-year-after-pell-restoration); [Marshall Project: Prison Education and Pell Grants (2023)](https://www.themarshallproject.org/2023/07/08/prison-education-college-financial-aid-pell-grant)

## Section 6: Drug Policy

### Portugal's Decriminalization — A 20-Year Evaluation

In 1999, Portugal had the highest HIV infection rate in the EU among people who inject drugs and was described as the "heroin capital of Europe." In 2001, Portugal enacted the most significant drug policy reform in democratic history: personal drug use and possession for all substances decriminalized (not legalized). Drug trafficking remained a criminal offense. Decriminalization was paired with investment in harm reduction services.

**Outcomes at 5 years (2006)**:
- Drug use did not increase; in some categories it declined
- HIV infections among people who inject drugs fell sharply

**Outcomes at 20 years (2021)**:
- HIV diagnoses among people who inject drugs: fell from **1,016 cases in 2001 to fewer than 16 per year** by 2020 — a 98% reduction
- Overdose death rate: fell from **80 deaths per million** in 2001 to **6 per million** by 2021 — a 93% reduction
- Drug use rates: Portugal continues to have among the **lowest drug use rates in the EU** despite decriminalization
- However: lifetime drug use prevalence among adults rose from 7.8% in 2001 to 12.8% by 2022 — suggesting some normalization effect, though this is within European averages

**Recent reversal of some gains**: Overdose rates reached a 12-year high in Portugal around 2022–2023, and drug deaths have roughly doubled in Lisbon since 2019. Analysts attribute this partly to austerity cuts to social services that were the backbone of Portugal's model, not to the decriminalization policy itself.

**The critical lesson**: Portugal's success was not decriminalization alone — it was decriminalization **plus** investment in treatment, housing, and harm reduction. Oregon's failure (below) demonstrates what decriminalization without the services infrastructure produces.

Sources: [Transform: Drug Decriminalisation in Portugal](https://transformdrugs.org/blog/drug-decriminalisation-in-portugal-setting-the-record-straight); [Drug Policy Alliance: Drug Decriminalization in Portugal](https://drugpolicy.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/dpa-drug-decriminalization-portugal-health-human-centered-approach_0.pdf); [NPR: Portugal's Overdose Death Rates](https://www.npr.org/2024/02/24/1230188789/portugal-drug-overdose-opioid-treatment); [Scientific American: Portugal 5 Years After](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/portugal-drug-decriminalization/)

### Oregon Measure 110 — What Went Wrong

Oregon's Measure 110 (2020) decriminalized possession of small amounts of all drugs and directed cannabis tax revenue toward treatment funding. It was presented as the US equivalent of Portugal's model.

**Implementation failure**:

- **Decriminalization was enacted immediately** (February 2021); **treatment infrastructure was funded and built slowly**, with the first treatment funds not distributed until late 2022
- The $100 citation-or-hotline model for diverting people to treatment was almost never used: only a few hundred calls were made to the treatment hotline statewide in the first year
- Law enforcement and courts were given neither training nor incentive to implement the deflection model; police largely stopped making drug arrests without implementing any alternative pathway to services
- State legislators failed to fund the online training course for police ($50,000), declined to redesign citations to make the treatment hotline more visible, and did not mandate coordination between law enforcement and treatment providers

**Outcome**: Between 2021 and 2024, Oregon saw increases in visible drug use, particularly fentanyl, in public spaces. Fatal overdose deaths continued to rise (a national trend, but Oregon's rose faster than peer states). Homelessness in Portland increased substantially, though attributing this to M110 vs. housing costs and COVID-19 recovery is contested.

**Recriminalization (2024)**: In March 2024, the Oregon Legislature passed House Bill 4002, recriminalizing possession of small amounts of drugs as a misdemeanor. The new law took effect September 1, 2024.

**The lesson for the proposal**: Oregon demonstrates that decriminalization without concurrent service investment is not the Portugal model — it is the removal of criminal penalties without providing any alternative pathway. The Drug Policy Alliance's analysis argues M110 was not a failure of decriminalization as a concept but a failure of implementation and political will to fund the treatment side.

Sources: [OPB: Oregon's Drug Decriminalization Experiment Appears Dead (2024)](https://www.opb.org/article/2024/03/01/oregon-legislature-passes-bill-recriminalizing-drug-possession-sends-to-governor-measure-110/); [Drug Policy Alliance: What Really Happened with M110](https://drugpolicy.org/resource/oregons-measure-110-what-really-happened/); [OPB: Leadership Failures in M110 Implementation](https://www.opb.org/article/2024/02/14/oregon-drug-decriminalization-plan-measure-110-leadership-failures/); [Prison Policy Initiative: Oregon Shouldn't Go Backwards](https://www.prisonpolicy.org/blog/2024/02/15/oregon-110/)

### Cannabis Legalization and Crime — Dragone et al. (2019)

**> Evidence callout**: Dragone et al. (2019) exploited the staggered legalization of recreational cannabis in Washington (end of 2012) and Oregon (end of 2014) using county-level difference-in-differences and spatial regression discontinuity designs. Finding: legalization **caused a significant reduction in rapes and property crimes** on the Washington side of the border in 2013–2014 relative to the Oregon side and pre-legalization period.

**Proposed mechanisms**: (1) direct psychotropic effects of cannabis substituting for more violence-inducing substances; (2) substitution away from alcohol; (3) reallocation of police attention away from cannabis enforcement; (4) reduced role of violent criminal organizations in the cannabis market.

**Spillover caveat**: Some evidence shows marijuana trafficking arrests in neighboring (non-legalizing) states increased following Colorado and Washington legalization, as legal-state cannabis moved into neighboring black markets. This is an argument for federal coordination, not against state legalization.

**Broader evidence synthesis**: Multiple subsequent studies (Oregon 2020; Colorado 2023) find no consistent increase in crime following cannabis legalization, and several find modest crime reductions.

Sources: [Dragone et al. 2019 — NBER w23426](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w23426/w23426.pdf); [ScienceDirect: Crime and the Legalization of Recreational Marijuana](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0167268118300386)

### Harm Reduction — Needle Exchange and Naloxone

**> Evidence callout**: Syringe services programs (SSPs) are associated with approximately a **50% reduction in HIV and hepatitis C virus (HCV) incidence** among people who inject drugs. This is the strongest single harm-reduction finding in the public health literature.

SSPs do not increase drug use or crime in the communities where they operate — a finding replicated across dozens of studies. Additional evidence:
- Frequent SSP participation significantly decreases HCV risk (HCV can survive on drug equipment surfaces for several days)
- SSPs that provide naloxone distribution and training reduce overdose deaths among their client populations
- SSPs serve as bridges to HIV/HCV testing, linkage to care, and medication-assisted treatment for opioid use disorder

**The US context**: Federal funding for SSPs was restricted or banned from 1988 to 2016. The 2016 budget allowed federal funding for SSP operational costs (not needle purchase) in jurisdictions with documented need. Many states still ban or restrict SSPs through drug paraphernalia laws, despite CDC and NIH support for their evidence base.

**Naloxone access laws**: As of 2024, all 50 states have enacted some form of naloxone standing order or pharmacy dispensing law. Evidence shows naloxone distribution programs reduce opioid overdose deaths at the population level; one study found each additional naloxone kit distributed was associated with approximately 0.5 overdose deaths averted.

Sources: [PMC: Harm Reduction Services and Infectious Diseases](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7596878/); [PMC: Role of Needle Exchange Programs in HIV Prevention](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1307729/); [Harm Reduction Journal: SSPs and HCV Meta-Analysis](https://harmreductionjournal.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12954-017-0156-z); [HIV.gov: Syringe Services Programs](https://www.hiv.gov/federal-response/other-topics/syringe-services-programs)

## Section 7: Key Objections — Evidence-Based Responses

### "Defund the Police" Framing Damage

**The polling data is unambiguous**: The "defund the police" slogan — prominent after George Floyd's murder in May 2020 — generated a rapid and durable backlash that damaged criminal justice reform more broadly.

- June 2020 (peak of protests): **57% of Black Americans** supported "defund the police" in one ABC/Ipsos poll
- April–May 2021: Support among Black Americans dropped to **41%** (Axios/Ipsos)
- Late 2022: **Only 17% of Black voters** supported defunding the police (Washington Examiner/Rasmussen)
- 2020 overall: 64% of Americans opposed the "defund the police" movement and its key goals (ABC News)
- 2022 Gallup: Only **35% of all adults** wanted to reduce police budgets and shift money to social services

The 2020 elections showed that Democratic candidates who distanced themselves from "defund" in competitive races outperformed those who did not. Republicans used the framing effectively through 2022.

**The substantive vs. rhetorical gap**: When poll respondents are asked about specific reforms — more mental health co-response, better training, accountability for misconduct — support is high across partisan and racial lines. The problem is the slogan, which communicated something (abolition, eliminating police) that the majority of even Black Americans in high-crime neighborhoods do not support.

**What the proposal should do**: Retire the "defund" frame entirely. Use "reinvest in public safety" — distinguishing between police functions that require armed response and those that do not.

Sources: [ABC News: 64% Oppose Defund the Police Movement](https://abcnews.go.com/Politics/64-americans-oppose-defund-police-movement-key-goals/story?id=71202300); [NPR: Few Want to Defund Police](https://www.npr.org/sections/live-updates-protests-for-racial-justice/2020/07/09/889618702/police-viewed-less-favorably-but-few-want-to-defund-them-survey-finds); [FiveThirtyEight: Americans Like Ideas Behind Defunding More Than the Slogan](https://fivethirtyeight.com/features/americans-like-the-ideas-behind-defunding-the-police-more-than-the-slogan-itself/)

### "Soft on Crime" — Evidence-Based Rebuttal

**New York City as the primary evidence**:

- NYC reduced its most serious crimes by approximately **80% from 1990 to 2019** — the largest sustained urban crime decline in US history
- From 2001 to 2012, NYC's incarceration rate fell **36%** while the national rate grew 3%
- In 2008, NYC's incarceration rate was **28% below its 1990 level**; nationally, incarceration was up 65% over the same period
- Murder dropped from 2,000+ per year in 1990 to 313 in 2019 — a per-capita decline of ~87%

**The Brennan Center synthesis**: Increased incarceration accounted for only **6% of the crime decline** in New York from the 1990s onward. The larger factors: policing strategy (CompStat/hot spots), economic improvement, demographic changes, lead removal.

**The counterargument (conservative critics)**: The 2019 bail reforms in New York led to the release of 2,000+ defendants from city jails by January 2020, followed by a period of rising crime. Critics attribute the post-2020 crime increase directly to these reforms. This is a live and contested debate — the post-2020 crime increase was national (including in states that made no bail reforms), suggesting the COVID-19 disruption is a significant confound.

**Evidence summary for the proposal**: The claim that decarceration necessarily increases crime is false at the levels the US has experienced since 2008. The US has reduced its prison population by ~400,000 since 2008 without a corresponding national crime increase (pre-COVID). The "soft on crime" frame conflates declining incarceration with declining enforcement — they are different.

Sources: [Brennan Center: New York — Increased Incarceration Had Limited Effect](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/analysis-opinion/new-york-increased-incarceration-had-limited-effect-reducing-crime-over-two); [Harvard Kennedy School: NYC's Remarkable Reversal of Mass Incarceration](https://www.hks.harvard.edu/research-insights/policy-topics/human-rights/study-reveals-new-york-citys-remarkable); [Brennan Center: How NYC Reduced Mass Incarceration (2013)](https://www.brennancenter.org/sites/default/files/publications/How_NYC_Reduced_Mass_Incarceration.pdf)

### "Prisons Keep Dangerous People Off the Streets"

The incapacitation argument for mass incarceration has two versions: (1) keeping any violent person incarcerated prevents crimes; (2) the current scale of US incarceration is justified by incapacitation benefits. Version (1) is clearly true for genuinely dangerous people. Version (2) is where the evidence cuts against current policy.

**The marginal incarceration problem**: At the current scale of US incarceration, the marginal prisoner — the person who would be released by modest reform — is not the violent predator of political rhetoric. Studies of released prisoners after policy changes (Prop 36 in California; COVID-19 emergency releases in 2020; New Jersey bail reform) consistently find that the marginal release does not produce proportional crime increases.

**The National Academies conclusion** (2014): At current US incarceration levels, the crime-reduction benefit of additional incarceration is close to zero for marginal offenders. The prison population has expanded far past the point where incapacitation produces meaningful safety benefits.

**The abolition vs. reform question**: The evidence does not support abolition — there is clear evidence that incarcerating genuinely violent individuals prevents crimes they would otherwise commit. The evidence strongly supports reform: reducing incarceration for drug offenses, non-violent property crimes, and technical probation/parole violations (which account for a substantial fraction of new prison admissions) would not meaningfully increase violent crime and would free substantial resources for evidence-based interventions.

Sources: [National Academies: Growth of Incarceration](https://www.nationalacademies.org/read/18613/chapter/7); [Roodman (2020): Impacts of Incarceration on Crime](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2007.10268); [Brennan Center: History of Mass Incarceration](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/analysis-opinion/history-mass-incarceration)

## Key Proposal Updates — Evidence-Based Recommendations

Based on this evidence review, three specific changes to the democratic renewal proposal's criminal justice domain are warranted:

**1. Add environmental crime prevention as a core pillar.**
The lead-crime evidence (Nevin 2007, Reyes 2007) demonstrates that environmental policy is among the highest-leverage crime prevention mechanisms available. The proposal should explicitly include lead abatement (housing and soil), summer youth employment funding, and SNAP access restoration for people with criminal records as criminal justice investments — not just as social policy. These interventions have better evidence bases than most policing reforms and are dramatically underfunded relative to the incarceration budget.

**2. Revise body camera and civilian oversight sections to reflect the null results.**
The current proposal implies cameras and oversight boards are proven accountability mechanisms. The Yokum et al. (2019) RCT finding — no effect on use of force in the largest study ever conducted — requires that the proposal specify the conditions under which cameras produce accountability: mandatory review, clear policy on activation, and consequences for non-compliance. The civilian oversight section should specify that boards without subpoena power, disciplinary authority, and independent funding are unlikely to change officer behavior, based on the structural findings in the Council on Criminal Justice evidence review.

**3. Reframe drug policy language to use the Portugal model explicitly, and integrate Oregon's failure as a cautionary evidence case.**
The proposal should clearly state that decriminalization without concurrent treatment investment is not the reform model the evidence supports. Portugal's 20-year results are among the strongest in public health policy; Oregon's 3-year failure demonstrates exactly what happens when the enforcement side of the reform is implemented without the services side. The proposal should call for decriminalization-plus-investment as a package, not decriminalization alone — and should explicitly commit to treatment funding benchmarks before or concurrent with any decriminalization provision.
