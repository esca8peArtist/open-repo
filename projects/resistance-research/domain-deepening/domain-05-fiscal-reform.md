## Section 1: The Core Problem — Empirics

### 1.1 Effective Tax Rates by Wealth Percentile

The US income tax is nominally progressive. In practice, effective rates invert at the top.

The CBO's most recent distributional analysis (2021 data, published 2024) places the average federal income tax rate for the middle quintile at approximately 8–9%, rising to 22% for the top 5%, and 24% for the top 1% — a pattern that looks progressive at the top of the income distribution.

That picture collapses when wealth, not income, is the denominator. Saez and Zucman's 2019 analysis (published in the *Brookings Papers on Economic Activity*) calculated effective all-in tax rates — federal income tax, state/local taxes, and corporate taxes attributed to ownership — across the full wealth distribution. Their headline finding: in 2018, the 400 wealthiest American families paid a combined effective rate of approximately 23%. The bottom 50% of households paid approximately 24.2%. For the first time in the dataset — which extends to 1960 — the ultra-wealthy paid a lower effective rate than the working class. The top rate in 1960 was approximately 56%; the trend line since runs continuously downward.

ProPublica's June 2021 analysis of leaked IRS microdata for the 25 wealthiest Americans calculated a different metric: taxes paid as a fraction of wealth increase over 2014–2018. This "true tax rate" ran at 3.4% — $13.6 billion in taxes on $401 billion in wealth growth. Bezos: 0.98% in two years. Musk: 3.27%. Carl Icahn: zero federal income tax in 2016 and 2017 on $544 million in reported adjusted gross income, made possible by $1.2 billion in loans secured against his appreciated equity positions.

The Auten/Splinter counter-analysis (2024) challenges the magnitude of the PSZ distributional findings by using different assumptions about undistributed corporate profits and government transfers. The methodological debate is genuine. What is not in dispute: unrealized appreciation constitutes the dominant form of ultra-high-net-worth wealth accumulation, goes entirely untaxed until realization, and may never be taxed at all under the stepped-up basis rule.

Sources: [CBO Distributional Analysis FY2021](https://www.cbo.gov/publication/59907); [Saez/Zucman BPEA 2019](https://gabriel-zucman.eu/files/SaezZucman2019BPEA.pdf); [ProPublica: The Secret IRS Files](https://www.propublica.org/article/the-secret-irs-files-trove-of-never-before-seen-records-reveal-how-the-wealthiest-avoid-income-tax); [Auten/Splinter Reply to PSZ (2024)](https://www.davidsplinter.com/AutenSplinter-2024-ReplyToPSZ.pdf)

### 1.2 The Buy-Borrow-Die Strategy — Mechanics and Revenue Cost

The mechanism exploits three structural features of the US code that interact to eliminate capital gains taxation on a large fraction of ultra-high-net-worth wealth accumulation.

**Step 1 — Buy**: Accumulate wealth in appreciating assets. Unrealized appreciation is not taxable income under US law. A founder whose startup rises from $1 million to $10 billion owes zero federal income tax on the $9.999 billion gain until realization.

**Step 2 — Borrow**: Use appreciated assets as collateral for Securities-Backed Lines of Credit (SBLOCs) at brokerage firms, or portfolio loans from wealth managers. Loan proceeds are not income — they are debt. Single-digit interest (often 1–3% for very large facilities) may be partially deductible. Living expenses — property, aircraft, staff — are funded from borrowed money. No tax event occurs.

**Step 3 — Die**: Heirs receive appreciated assets with a "stepped-up" cost basis — reset to fair market value at date of death, permanently erasing the embedded capital gain. The $9.999 billion accumulated gain is extinguished from the tax base at the moment of transfer.

The stepped-up basis costs approximately $40–50 billion per year in foregone federal revenue (Congressional Research Service estimate). Over 75% of this benefit accrues to the largest estates. The strategy is not aggressive tax planning; it is the code functioning as written.

Sources: [ProPublica: The Secret IRS Files](https://www.propublica.org/article/the-secret-irs-files-trove-of-never-before-seen-records-reveal-how-the-wealthiest-avoid-income-tax); [Yale Budget Lab: Buy-Borrow-Die Reform Options](https://budgetlab.yale.edu/research/buy-borrow-die-options-reforming-tax-treatment-borrowing-against-appreciated-assets); [NBC News: Inside the $40B Stepped-Up Basis Loophole](https://www.nbcnews.com/politics/politics-news/inside-40-billion-year-tax-loophole-biden-s-plan-would-n1267481); [CRFB: Closing the Stepped-Up Basis Loophole](https://www.crfb.org/blogs/closing-stepped-basis-loophole)

### 1.3 Corporate Tax Avoidance: Offshore Profit Shifting and GILTI's Weakness

US multinational corporations shift substantial profits to low-tax jurisdictions through transfer pricing, IP holding company structures, and intra-company royalty arrangements. The Tax Justice Network estimates approximately $900 billion in US corporate profits shifted offshore annually, representing $200–250 billion in annual revenue loss to the US Treasury. The OECD's broader estimate puts global profit shifting at $100–240 billion annually across all countries.

Apple's pre-2016 Irish arrangement — in which profits were routed through entities tax-resident in no country, producing effective rates below 1% on European sales — remains the canonical illustration. The EU's 2016 ruling ordered Ireland to collect €13 billion in back taxes from Apple; the European Court of Justice upheld the ruling in 2024.

The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (2017) introduced the Global Intangible Low-Taxed Income (GILTI) regime as a minimum tax on foreign intangible income of US multinationals. Its design was compromised from the start: an 80% foreign tax credit effectively set the minimum rate at 10.5% (half the 21% domestic rate), creating an incentive to shift profits to jurisdictions taxing above 10.5% rather than eliminating shifting structurally. Biden's proposed GILTI reform — raising the effective minimum to 15%, aligned with OECD Pillar Two — passed the House but not the Senate.

As of January 2026, the OECD released a "side-by-side" package under which US-headquartered multinationals are effectively excluded from the Under-Taxed Profits Rule (UTPR) and Income Inclusion Rule (IIR) in exchange for the US removing the Section 899 retaliatory tax measures from the One Big Beautiful Bill. Under OBBB, GILTI is replaced with "net CFC tested income" (NCTI) at an effective rate of approximately 12.6% — below the Pillar Two 15% floor. US companies therefore remain below-compliant with the global minimum in 2026 while foreign companies operating in the US face effective minimum taxation.

Sources: [Tax Justice Network: Corporate Tax Avoidance](https://taxjustice.net/); [Tax Policy Center: OECD Pillar 1 and Pillar 2 Reforms](https://taxpolicycenter.org/briefing-book/what-are-oecd-pillar-1-and-pillar-2-international-taxation-reforms); [FACT Coalition: OECD Side-by-Side Allowing Tax Abuse](https://thefactcoalition.org/oecd-side-by-side-agreement/); [Grant Thornton: OECD Side-by-Side Pillar 2 Deal](https://www.grantthornton.com/insights/alerts/tax/2026/flash/oecd-side-by-side-pillar-2); [Bloomberg Tax: Aligning GILTI With Pillar Two](https://news.bloombergtax.com/tax-insights-and-commentary/aligning-gilti-with-pillar-two-is-a-task-the-us-shouldnt-delay)

### 1.4 The $688 Billion Tax Gap — Who Owes It

The IRS estimates the gross annual tax gap at $688 billion for tax year 2021 — taxes legally owed but not voluntarily paid. The net gap (after enforcement and late payments) is approximately $606 billion. This figure has roughly doubled from the 2001 estimate of $345 billion.

The compliance pattern is not random — it directly reflects the structure of third-party reporting:

| Income Type | Compliance Rate | Enforcement Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| Wages and salaries | >99% | Mandatory employer withholding and W-2 reporting |
| Dividends and interest | ~95% | Mandatory 1099 reporting by financial institutions |
| Self-employment income | ~68% | No third-party verification |
| Partnership and S-Corp income | ~60–70% | Complex pass-through structures; limited IRS capacity |
| Offshore accounts | Variable; systematically understated | FATCA post-2010 only |

Workers' incomes are collected at source. Wealthy taxpayers' incomes from partnerships, closely held businesses, and offshore structures have compliance rates of 45–83% — a gap reflecting both evasion and legal-but-aggressive avoidance through structures the IRS lacks capacity to audit.

The Guyton et al. NBER Working Paper (2021) applied targeted corrections for offshore accounts and partnership structures undetected by random audit programs. Result: the top 1% fails to report approximately 21% of its income, accounting for roughly $160–175 billion in annual unpaid taxes — more than the entire IRS enforcement budget multiple times over.

Sources: [IRS: The Tax Gap (official)](https://www.irs.gov/statistics/irs-the-tax-gap); [Treasury: The Case for a Robust Attack on the Tax Gap](https://home.treasury.gov/news/featured-stories/the-case-for-a-robust-attack-on-the-tax-gap); [NBER WP 28542: Tax Evasion at the Top](https://www.nber.org/papers/w28542); [CRS: The Federal Tax Gap (R47858)](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R47858)

### 1.5 Section 199A Pass-Through Deduction — TCJA's Hidden Wealth Transfer

Section 199A, enacted by TCJA (2017), allows owners of pass-through businesses — sole proprietorships, partnerships, S-corporations, and some LLCs — to deduct 20% of "qualified business income" from their taxable income. The stated rationale: align the effective tax rate on pass-through income with the reduced 21% corporate rate.

The distributional reality diverges sharply from this framing. The Joint Committee on Taxation estimates that 61% of §199A's benefit in 2024 accrues to the top 1% of income earners. Annual revenue cost: $57.6 billion in 2024, projected at $60.9 billion in 2025. CBO estimates that extending §199A permanently (as proposed in OBBB) would reduce federal revenues by approximately $700 billion over ten years.

The deduction is not available to high-income earners in "specified service trade or business" categories (law, medicine, consulting, finance) above threshold income levels — creating an arbitrary distinction between "good" and "bad" professional businesses that reflects lobbying power rather than economic logic. Real estate investors and manufacturing firms receive the full deduction regardless of income level.

Penn Wharton Budget Model's 2025 analysis found that eliminating the §199A deduction for high-income earners — those above the existing threshold — while preserving it for small businesses would reduce its revenue cost by approximately $350 billion over a decade while eliminating the largest windfall for wealthy pass-through owners.

Sources: [Penn Wharton: Eliminating Excess 199A Benefits](https://budgetmodel.wharton.upenn.edu/issues/2025/1/27/eliminating-excess-benefits-from-section-199a-deduction); [JCT: Section 199A Revenue Cost](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IF12838); [Brookings: 199A's Sunset](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/199as-sunset/); [Tax Law Center: Ways and Means 199A Proposals](https://taxlawcenter.org/blog/ways-and-means-proposes-making-costly-199a-pass-through-deduction-more-generous-and-valuable-to-high-income-earners)

## Section 2: Revenue Potential

### 2.1 Wealth Tax: Saez-Zucman Estimates and Evasion Concerns

The Saez/Zucman wealth tax proposal — 2% on net wealth above $50 million, 3% above $1 billion — was originally estimated at $200–220 billion annually. Their 2021 updated estimate, reflecting growth in ultra-high-net-worth concentration, raised this to $300 billion annually for the Warren iteration of the proposal. Competing estimates from Smith, Zidar, and Zwick range to $117 billion — a methodological gap driven principally by different assumptions about valuation difficulty and avoidance behavior.

The evasion concern is real and must be stated directly: European wealth taxes that failed largely failed because they lacked the three features the US uniquely has: (a) worldwide taxation — US citizens pay US taxes regardless of where they reside; (b) the Section 877A exit tax — covered expatriates face a mark-to-market deemed-disposition tax at departure; (c) FATCA's comprehensive foreign account reporting infrastructure. A well-designed US wealth tax with high thresholds ($50M+), enhanced exit taxation, and FATCA-based reporting is a different instrument from France's ISF or Norway's 1.1% levy applied starting at $150,000 in net worth.

Zucman's 2024 advocacy before the G20 for a coordinated 2% tax on billionaires (approximately 3,000 individuals globally) projected $200–250 billion annually worldwide, with the US share in the range of $100 billion.

Sources: [Saez/Zucman Wealth Tax Revenue Estimates (2021)](https://www.warren.senate.gov/imo/media/doc/Wealth%20Tax%20Revenue%20Estimates%20by%20Saez%20and%20Zucman%20-%20Feb%2024%2020211.pdf); [ICIJ: Zucman G20 Billionaire Tax Pitch](https://www.icij.org/investigations/pandora-papers/top-economist-pitches-global-billionaire-tax-to-g20-finance-leaders/); [Saez/Zucman 2019 BPEA](https://gabriel-zucman.eu/files/SaezZucman2019BPEA.pdf)

### 2.2 Mark-to-Market on Unrealized Gains — Mechanics, Revenue, Legal Challenge

The Wyden mark-to-market proposal would treat annual unrealized appreciation in publicly traded securities as taxable income in the year it accrues — the same treatment already applied to futures contracts, dealer inventory, and foreign currency positions. For publicly traded assets, the administrative mechanism is trivial: prices are public, tracked daily, and already reported on 1099-B forms. No new infrastructure is required.

The harder cases — closely held businesses, private real estate, illiquid partnership interests — require either (a) carryover of imputed tax with a deferral charge until actual realization; (b) a deemed-realization trigger at death with installment payment options; or (c) mark-to-market confined to liquid assets above a threshold.

The Biden Billionaire Minimum Income Tax took a hybrid approach: a 25% minimum tax on "total income" (taxable income plus unrealized gains) for taxpayers with net worth above $100 million. Treasury estimated $361 billion over ten years ($36B/year); OMB projections ranged to $517 billion.

**The constitutional challenge**: There is a credible Article I argument that taxing unrealized gains constitutes a "direct tax" required to be apportioned by state population — a requirement that would make a nationwide wealth or mark-to-market tax unworkable. The Supreme Court's 2024 decision in *Moore v. United States* (upholding the TCJA's mandatory repatriation tax on previously accumulated foreign earnings) addressed this directly, though narrowly. The majority opinion preserved the "realization" requirement as a constitutional matter for most income taxes while finding Moore's tax on accumulated earnings constitutionally distinct. The long-term resolution remains contested in the legal literature. A constitutional amendment authorizing income taxation without apportionment — analogous to the Sixteenth Amendment — would permanently resolve the question for mark-to-market but is not a near-term legislative strategy.

Sources: [CRS: Mark-to-Market Taxation of Capital Gains](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IF11957); [ITEP: Biden Billionaires' Minimum Income Tax](https://itep.org/president-bidens-proposed-billionaires-minimum-income-tax-would-ensure-the-wealthiest-pay-a-reasonable-amount-of-income-tax/); [CRFB: Closing the Stepped-Up Basis Loophole](https://www.crfb.org/blogs/closing-stepped-basis-loophole)

### 2.3 Estate Tax — Current Exemption, Stepped-Up Basis, Revenue if Reformed

The federal estate tax currently applies to estates above $13.61 million per individual ($27.22 million per married couple) — the highest exemption threshold in the tax's history, a TCJA provision scheduled to sunset in 2025 before the One Big Beautiful Bill extended it. The top rate is 40%. The practical effect: the Tax Policy Center estimates approximately 3,800 estates were taxable in 2023 — roughly 0.12% of all deaths. Revenue: approximately $31 billion in FY2023.

The stepped-up basis rule interacts with the estate tax in a way that eliminates tax on most wealth transfers. The estate tax (theoretically) captures appreciation at death — but with a $13.61 million exemption, the vast majority of estates holding appreciated assets (valued between $1 million and $13 million) face no estate tax and benefit from the stepped-up basis with no offsetting levy.

Reform parameters and revenue:
- **Restore 2009 parameters** ($3.5 million exemption, 45% top rate): approximately 0.3% of estates affected; estimated $85–100 billion over a decade.
- **Sanders "For the 99.8%" graduated structure** (45% above $3.5M, rising to 77% above $1B): substantially higher revenue; fewer than 0.2% of estates affected.
- **Eliminate stepped-up basis alone** (without estate tax reform): $40–50 billion per year in additional revenue, capturing the forgiven gains at death that the eroded estate tax no longer reaches.

The "family farm and small business" objection is quantitatively narrow: the Tax Policy Center finds fewer than 80 estates per year face estate tax liability while holding closely held farms or small businesses insufficient to cover the tax without a forced sale.

Sources: [Tax Policy Center: How Many People Pay the Estate Tax?](https://taxpolicycenter.org/briefing-book/how-many-people-pay-estate-tax); [CRS: Estate and Gift Tax Issues](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IF12846); [Americans for Tax Fairness: Stepped-Up Basis Fact Sheet](https://americansfortaxfairness.org/wp-content/uploads/ATF-Stepped-Up-Basis-Fact-Sheet-FINAL-6-24-21.pdf)

### 2.4 Corporate Alternative Minimum Tax — Design Flaw and Revenue Underperformance

The Inflation Reduction Act (2022) enacted a 15% Corporate Alternative Minimum Tax (CAMT) on the "adjusted financial statement income" (AFSI — book income) of corporations with average annual book profits above $1 billion. The projected revenue: approximately $35 billion annually.

Actual revenue in 2024: approximately $572 million — less than 2% of projections.

The gap is largely attributable to a structural design choice: accelerated depreciation deductions permitted under the regular corporate tax also reduce AFSI for CAMT purposes. Corporations with heavy capital investment — utilities, manufacturers, infrastructure operators — can eliminate most of their CAMT liability through accelerated bonus depreciation claimed against book income. The IRS waived CAMT penalties entirely in 2023 because the rules were too complex to comply with.

Fixing the CAMT requires either: (a) disallowing accelerated depreciation in the AFSI calculation — a straightforward technical amendment that would dramatically increase effective CAMT collections; or (b) replacing book-income CAMT with a cash-flow or modified-accelerated basis minimum tax. The Brookings Institution's 2023 analysis concluded that without the accelerated depreciation carve-out, the CAMT would raise revenue more closely aligned with its original projections.

Sources: [Brookings: The Limits of the New Corporate AMT](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/the-limits-of-the-new-corporate-amt-why-little-has-changed/); [Tax Foundation: CAMT Repeal and Reform](https://taxfoundation.org/blog/corporate-alternative-minimum-tax-camt-repeal/); [CRS: The 15% Corporate Alternative Minimum Tax](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R47328)

### 2.5 GILTI Reform to Pillar Two 15% — Revenue and Coordination Challenge

Aligning GILTI (or its OBBB successor NCTI) with the OECD Pillar Two 15% global minimum would require raising the effective US minimum rate from ~12.6% to 15% — a 2.4 percentage point increase. The Joint Committee on Taxation's estimates for the Biden GILTI reform, which proposed precisely this alignment, projected approximately $300–350 billion over a decade at 15%.

The coordination challenge has become more acute after the 2026 OECD side-by-side package. By agreeing to exempt US multinationals from UTPR/IIR top-up taxes applied by foreign jurisdictions, the US traded multilateral enforcement for domestic non-compliance. The practical consequence: US multinationals can shift profits to 12–14% jurisdictions without facing either US GILTI top-up taxes or foreign jurisdiction top-up taxes — a gap that partially restores the pre-TCJA incentive structure for offshore profit shifting.

Sources: [Tax Policy Center: Pillar 1 and Pillar 2 Reforms](https://taxpolicycenter.org/briefing-book/what-are-oecd-pillar-1-and-pillar-2-international-taxation-reforms); [Bipartisan Policy Center: International Taxes Under Pillars 1 and 2](https://bipartisanpolicy.org/explainer/the-2025-tax-debate-international-taxes-under-pillars-1-and-2/); [Yale Budget Lab: International Tax in the Age of Pillar 2](https://budgetlab.yale.edu/research/international-tax-age-pillar-2)

### 2.6 Financial Transaction Tax — Revenue, UK Stamp Duty, and Design Principles

A financial transaction tax (FTT) at 0.1% on equity trades and 0.01% on derivative notional values is estimated to raise $50–100 billion annually under a well-designed residence-based structure (Urban Institute/Tax Policy Center analysis). The design determines whether this revenue materializes or evaporates offshore.

Sweden's 1984–1990 FTT applied to transactions through registered Swedish brokers; within two years, more than 50% of Swedish equity volume had migrated to London. Revenue: less than 4% of projections. The tax was repealed in 1990.

The UK stamp duty (0.5% on equity purchases, 1986–present) has raised approximately £3–4 billion annually consistently, with no significant trading migration. The design difference is decisive: UK stamp duty exempts market makers and dealers from the tax on their purchasing transactions — only the final investor's purchase is taxed. This preserves market-making liquidity without creating double-taxation of intermediary activity.

A US FTT modeled on UK stamp duty with these modifications — applied to the final investor, extended proportionally to derivatives, and structured as a residence-based tax on US investors regardless of execution venue — would address the Swedish migration problem while capturing revenue from the full range of financial instruments.

Sources: [Urban Institute: Financial Transaction Taxes in Theory and Practice](https://www.urban.org/sites/default/files/publication/78181/2000634-financial-transaction-taxes-in-theory-and-practice.pdf); [NBER: International Experiences with Securities Transaction Taxes](https://www.nber.org/system/files/chapters/c6276/c6276.pdf)

### 2.7 Carbon Pricing — Social Cost of Carbon, British Columbia Model, Revenue Potential

The social cost of carbon (SCC) — the present value of economic damage from one additional ton of CO2 emissions — was estimated at $51/ton under the Obama administration. The Biden administration's interim SCC was $51/ton pending updated analysis; independent estimates using modern integrated assessment models range from $80 to $185/ton or higher.

At $50/ton on approximately 4.5 billion metric tonnes of net US CO2 emissions, gross revenue is approximately $225 billion annually. At $100/ton: approximately $450 billion. As emissions decline in response to the price signal, revenue declines proportionally — this is the tax working as designed.

British Columbia's carbon tax — $10 CAD/tonne in 2008, raised to $65 CAD by 2020 — is the most studied North American carbon pricing system. Metcalf (2022) estimates 5–8% aggregate emissions reductions compared to counterfactual, with BC GDP growth matching or exceeding the Canadian average during the tax period. Sweden's carbon tax at approximately $130/ton (since 1991) provides stronger evidence: Swedish emissions fell approximately 27% from 1991 to 2018 while the economy grew 83%.

Carbon taxes are regressive in direct incidence — lower-income households spend a higher fraction of income on energy. The correction is the per-capita dividend: Canada's Carbon Rebate returns approximately 90% of revenue equally per capita; households earning below $40,000 receive approximately CAD $1,000–1,600 net positive per year. At $50/ton with a 100% per-capita dividend, a family of four would receive approximately $3,000 annually — a structural income supplement for the bottom 60% that offsets the regressive fuel cost effect.

Sources: [Duke Nicholas Institute: Review of BC Carbon Tax](https://nicholasinstitute.duke.edu/project/review-british-columbia-carbon-tax); [Metcalf/Springer: Does a Carbon Tax Reduce CO2 Emissions?](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10640-022-00679-w); [LSE Grantham: Carbon Pricing with Regressive Co-Benefits](https://www.lse.ac.uk/granthaminstitute/publication/carbon-pricing-with-regressive-co-benefits-evidence-from-british-columbias-carbon-tax/)

## Section 3: International Comparisons

### 3.1 OECD Tax-to-GDP Context — The US Is a Low-Tax Outlier

The US at 25.6% of GDP collects taxes at a rate approximately 8.5 percentage points below the OECD average of 34.1% — a gap worth roughly $2.3 trillion annually at current GDP levels. Among G7 nations, the US has the lowest tax-to-GDP ratio. Only Chile, Colombia, Costa Rica, Ireland, Mexico, and Turkey collect less tax as a share of GDP among OECD members.

Corporate income tax revenue as a share of US GDP declined from approximately 4% in the 1960s to 1.6% in 2021. The GAO's 2024 analysis found that profitable large US corporations paid an average effective federal rate of approximately 9% — less than half the 21% statutory rate. ITEP found that 55 of the largest US corporations paid zero federal income tax on approximately $40 billion in combined profits in at least one recent year.

### 3.2 Nordic Countries — High Tax, Strong Growth

The Nordic countries — Denmark (45.2%), Sweden (43%), Norway (43%), Finland (42%) — fund universal healthcare, heavily subsidized higher education, paid parental leave, and active labor market programs through tax burdens roughly 17–20 percentage points above the US. Their median living standards, human development indices, social mobility rates, and infant mortality outcomes equal or exceed the US on almost all metrics.

The mechanism is not mystery: broad consumption taxation (VAT at 20–25%), substantial payroll contributions, and progressive income taxes generate revenue used for universal services that replace what US households pay out-of-pocket. When US out-of-pocket healthcare costs, tuition, and childcare are added to the tax burden of a median US family, the total household obligation is comparable to that of equivalent Nordic families — with inferior service outcomes.

Sweden abolished its wealth tax in 2007 — the most frequently cited point for wealth tax critics. Sweden's abolition was preceded by decades of documented capital flight to Switzerland and Liechtenstein, design features that taxed operating businesses at full value (while other countries didn't), and threshold levels that captured middle-class homeowners rather than the ultra-wealthy. The Swedish abolition was followed by a capital repatriation — a design-failure lesson, not a principle-of-wealth-taxation lesson.

Sources: [OECD Revenue Statistics 2025](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/12/revenue-statistics-2025_07ca0a8e.html); [OECD: Average Tax Revenues Remain Steady](https://www.oecd.org/en/about/news/press-releases/2024/11/average-tax-revenues-in-the-oecd-remain-steady-as-spending-pressures-grow.html)

### 3.3 Wealth Taxes That Failed vs. Norway's Ongoing Tax

More than a dozen OECD countries adopted and then repealed wealth taxes from the 1990s to 2010s: Austria, Denmark, Finland, France, Germany, Iceland, Ireland, Italy, Luxembourg, Netherlands, and Sweden. As of 2025, only Norway, Spain, and Switzerland levy a net wealth tax.

The French ISF (1988–2017) applied at 0.5–1.5% on net wealth above approximately €1.3 million. By 2015, approximately 10,000 millionaires per year were leaving France (approximately 6% of Paris's millionaires annually). The tax was generating ~€4 billion annually while losing significant income, corporate, and consumption tax from departing taxpayers. Macron repealed it in 2017, replacing it with a tax on real estate wealth only.

Norway raised its wealth tax rate from 0.85% to 1.1% in 2022 (applied starting at approximately $150,000 in net worth). In the 12 months after the increase, approximately 30 large taxpayers — representing ~$54 billion in combined net worth — emigrated, primarily to Switzerland. The associated tax revenue loss was estimated at $594 million — exceeding the incremental revenue from the rate increase. **However**: Norway's broader wealth tax revenue continues to rise. Estimated 2025 wealth tax collections: 34 billion kroner, up from 27 billion in 2022, because the broad base (reaching hundreds of thousands of taxpayers) grows faster than the high-end emigration depletes it.

Norway subsequently closed the exit deferral loophole in 2024 — emigrants now face an immediate exit tax on unrealized gains rather than an indefinite deferral. The 2024 household behavior study found that households facing higher wealth tax bills responded by saving more and working longer rather than depleting assets — a finding inconsistent with the predicted investment-suppression effect.

**What drove the pattern**: European failures share features a US proposal must avoid — (1) very low thresholds capturing upper-middle-class households; (2) exclusion of financial assets through corporate structures while taxing operating businesses; (3) EU/Schengen portability where emigration requires no visa and Switzerland provides instant sanctuary; (4) absence of exit tax on unrealized gains. None of these features applies to a properly designed US wealth tax.

Sources: [Tax Foundation: Wealth Taxes in Europe 2025](https://taxfoundation.org/data/all/eu/wealth-taxes-europe/); [Canadian Affairs: Norway's Wealth Tax Drove Out the Rich Without Breaking the Bank](https://www.canadianaffairs.news/2025/10/24/norways-wealth-tax-drove-out-the-rich-without-breaking-the-bank/); [AInvest: Impact of Wealth Taxes on Capital Flight — Norway](https://www.ainvest.com/news/impact-wealth-taxes-capital-flight-entrepreneurship-lessons-norway-experience-2512/); [Brussels Report: Norway Wealth Tax Warning](https://www.brusselsreport.eu/2024/09/11/the-failure-of-norways-wealth-tax-hike-as-a-warning-signal/)

### 3.4 VAT — What the US Could Learn and the Regressivity Problem

The United States is the only OECD member country without a national value-added tax. All 37 other members use VAT as a primary revenue instrument, at rates ranging from 5% (Canada's GST on a narrow base) to 27% (Hungary). The OECD average standard VAT rate is approximately 19%.

The revenue efficiency of VAT is well established: it is self-enforcing through the invoice chain (each business in the supply chain claims credits for VAT paid, creating an audit trail), has low compliance costs relative to income taxation, and raises substantial revenue on broad consumption bases. A US VAT at 10% on a broad base (comparable to the EU standard) would raise approximately $700–800 billion annually, more than enough to fund a substantial EITC expansion, universal health coverage, or deficit reduction.

The regressivity problem is real: consumption taxes take a larger fraction of low-income households' budgets than high-income households' budgets. The correction mechanism is direct: pair VAT with a per-capita demogrant (a flat annual payment to every household regardless of income). At $700 billion in revenue with a $200 billion per-capita demogrant (approximately $600/person), the combined VAT-plus-demogrant system would be progressive for the bottom 60% of households and modestly regressive only at the top — where income taxes can compensate. Canada's GST/HST Credit achieves this structure at smaller scale.

The political barrier is the concentrated perception of a new tax on consumption rather than the diffuse reality of the compensating demogrant — a framing challenge, not a policy design challenge.

Sources: [Tax Policy Center: How Do US Taxes Compare Internationally?](https://taxpolicycenter.org/briefing-book/how-do-us-taxes-compare-internationally); [OECD: Revenue Statistics 2024](https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/topics/policy-sub-issues/global-tax-revenues/revenue-statistics-united-states.pdf)

## Section 4: IRS Enforcement

### 4.1 Audit Rate Collapse — The 2010–2024 Trend

The IRS audit rate for individual returns peaked in 2010 and has since declined across all income tiers — but the collapse has been concentrated at high-income levels while EITC recipient audit rates have remained elevated relative to the general population.

Key data from GAO (2022) and IRS statistics:

- **Overall individual audit rate**: declined from ~0.9% in 2010 to approximately 0.2% by 2019–2021 — a 78% reduction.
- **Top 1% audit rate**: declined from approximately 8.4% in 2010 to under 2% by 2019. ProPublica's analysis found the top 1% and EITC recipients were audited at comparable rates by 2021 — approximately 1.4–1.6% each.
- **EITC recipient audit rate**: In FY2022, returns claiming EITC were audited at 0.9% compared to 0.2% for all individual returns — EITC recipients audited at roughly four times the general rate. This is driven by IRS reliance on automated correspondence audits, which are cheap to run but disproportionately burden low-income filers who cannot easily respond.
- **Large corporations**: Audit rate for corporations with over $20 billion in assets fell from approximately 93% in 2012 to less than 50% by 2019.

The disproportion is a structural consequence of IRS understaffing: correspondence audits of EITC claims are automated and resource-light; audits of complex partnership and offshore structures require senior revenue agents and sustained multi-year investigation. IRS staffing for complex audit work has declined by approximately 17% since 2010.

FY2024 showed improvement under IRA-funded enforcement: EITC correspondence audits fell 53% while planned audits of taxpayers earning above $400,000 were 2.5 times the historical average. This progress reversed as IRA enforcement funding was rescinded.

### 4.2 IRA $80 Billion IRS Funding — What It Funded, What Was Rescinded

The Inflation Reduction Act (2022) appropriated $80 billion in supplemental IRS funding over ten years:
- Enforcement: $45.6 billion
- Operations support: $25.3 billion
- Systems modernization: $4.8 billion
- Taxpayer services: $3.2 billion

Treasury's projected return on the enforcement component: $561 billion in additional revenue over 2024–2034 — approximately a 7:1 ROI on enforcement dollars. Extended projections reached $851 billion.

**Early enforcement results before rescissions** (Biden IRS, through fall 2024):
- $1.3 billion collected from high-income, high-wealth individuals
- $172 million from 21,000 individual filers
- $520 million from large partnership initiative
- Partnership audit initiative launched examining 75 of the largest US partnerships

**Congressional rescissions** stripped more than half the allocation before enforcement could scale:
- Fiscal Responsibility Act of 2023: $1.4 billion rescinded
- Further Consolidated Appropriations Act (2024): $20.2 billion rescinded
- Full-Year Continuing Appropriations Act (2025): $20.2 billion rescinded
- **Total rescinded**: approximately $41.8 billion

At the conservative CBO/Treasury 5:1 ROI, the rescinded enforcement funding would have generated approximately $200 billion in additional annual revenue at operational scale. The rescissions represent a deliberate legislative choice to allow $200 billion per year in high-income non-compliance to go uncollected — funded by general taxpayers through deficit spending or future tax increases.

### 4.3 Direct File — Rise and Shutdown

The IRS Direct File pilot (2024) allowed taxpayers in 12 states with simple returns to file directly with the IRS at no cost. Approximately 140,000 taxpayers used the pilot in 2024; 94% rated their experience "excellent" or "above average." The 2025 expansion covered 25 states and processed 296,531 accepted returns.

In November 2025, the IRS notified states that Direct File would not be available for the 2026 filing season and that no future launch date was set. Treasury cited "low participation and relatively high costs," estimating the 2024 program cost $41 million ($138 per return). The Trump administration moved to terminate the program; Republican lawmakers had written multiple letters urging its dismantling. The tax preparation industry — Intuit (TurboTax), H&R Block — had lobbied against Direct File since its inception.

The international comparison is instructive: Sweden's Skatteverket pre-fills 74% of returns, average completion time 3 minutes. Estonia achieves 98% digital filing in 3–5 minutes. Denmark's pre-filled returns have a 99% acceptance rate. The technical infrastructure to replicate this in the US demonstrably exists; the barrier is industry capture of the legislative and regulatory process.

Democrats introduced legislation in February 2026 to restore Direct File. No vote has been scheduled.

### 4.4 Revenue Per Dollar of IRS Enforcement Spending

The CBO/Treasury long-run estimate: $5–12 returned per dollar of IRS enforcement investment, with the higher returns applying to complex partnership and offshore audit programs where cases typically take 3–7 years from initiation to collection.

The GAO's FY2022 analysis found that IRS examination of individual returns produces an average of approximately $5 per $1 spent on audit activity for the top income tiers. EITC correspondence audits — the cheapest audits to conduct — produce minimal net revenue because most EITC credits are legitimately claimed and denied credits are often reinstated on appeal.

The time-horizon dispute: Republican House members in 2025 testified that actual FY2024 enforcement ROI was below 1:1 for some programs. This compares FY2024 enforcement costs (high, as the IRS hired and trained agents) against FY2024 collections (low, because complex audits initiated in 2023–2024 have not yet collected). Evaluating a 7-year enforcement program at year 2 understates eventual returns by design.

## Section 5: Counterarguments Engaged Honestly

### 5.1 Capital Flight — How Real?

The capital flight concern is the most important empirical objection to wealth taxes and high income tax rates. It deserves honest engagement rather than dismissal.

**The Saez/Zucman position**: The US is structurally different from Europe because (a) the US taxes worldwide income regardless of residence — moving abroad does not exempt a US citizen from US taxes; (b) Section 877A imposes a mark-to-market exit tax on covered expatriates; (c) renouncing US citizenship to avoid a wealth tax creates a permanent and very high personal cost. These structural features make the France/Norway capital flight experience largely non-transferable to the US.

**The Summers counter-position**: Lawrence Summers and other economists have argued that even within the US, a wealth tax would produce substantial avoidance through entity structuring, inter-family transfers, and under-valuation of closely held assets; that the valuation problem for illiquid assets is more severe than Saez/Zucman acknowledge; and that evasion rates of 15–30% would substantially reduce actual revenue below headline estimates. Summers's Brookings analysis estimated effective revenue at the low end of the $117–300B range.

**The resolution**: Both positions have merit. Capital flight from the US is structurally more difficult than from Europe due to worldwide taxation and exit taxes. Avoidance through entity structuring — not flight — is the real concern, and it requires strong third-party reporting, anti-abuse rules, and robust IRS valuation capacity. The honest assessment: a well-designed US wealth tax with comprehensive reporting would likely raise $100–200 billion annually with behavioral responses, not the $300 billion top-line estimate and not zero.

---

### 5.2 Wealth Tax Valuation and Evasion

The valuation problem is serious for closely held businesses, private equity, real estate, and art — assets without liquid public markets. A founder with a $500 million stake in a private company faces genuine uncertainty about annual mark-to-market value, and the IRS faces corresponding difficulty verifying claimed values.

Options that reduce but do not eliminate the problem: (a) apply the wealth tax only above a very high threshold, limiting the affected population to those with sufficient liquid assets or professional advisors to handle valuation; (b) accept actuarial valuation methods with safe harbors for illiquid assets; (c) offer an illiquid-asset election that allows annual imputed wealth tax to accrue with interest, payable at realization or death. None of these solutions is frictionless; all are preferable to allowing unlimited accumulation without any annual tax.

The FATCA framework — which has generated over 100,000 voluntary offshore disclosures and extensive third-party financial institution reporting — demonstrates that the IRS can build information systems to capture previously untaxed wealth flows. Wealth tax enforcement would require significant expansion of this infrastructure.

---

### 5.3 The Laffer Curve — What the Evidence Actually Says

The Laffer curve — the theoretical claim that tax revenue first rises then falls as rates increase, reaching a maximum at some revenue-maximizing rate — is empirically correct in its general form and empirically contested in its application to current US tax rates.

The ETI-based revenue-maximizing rate formula (Saez 2001): t* = 1/(1 + a×e), where a describes the top-income distribution (~2 for the US) and e is the elasticity of taxable income. At the empirically central ETI estimate of 0.25 (Saez, Slemrod, and Giertz 2012 *Journal of Economic Literature* comprehensive review): t* = 73%. At ETI = 0.5: t* = 56%.

Current US top marginal income tax rate: 37%. This places current policy well below the estimated revenue-maximizing rate even under supply-side-friendly assumptions. The supply-side claim that US tax rates are near or above the revenue-maximizing level has no empirical support in the ETI literature.

The critical qualifier: most of the measured ETI at high incomes reflects avoidance behavior (income shifting, sheltering) rather than real behavioral changes (working fewer hours, reducing effort). Base-broadening — eliminating shelters and avoidance opportunities — lowers the effective ETI and raises the revenue-maximizing rate further. This is what the 1986 Tax Reform Act demonstrated: eliminating deductions while reducing statutory rates preserved revenue neutrality while reducing avoidance responses.

The Reagan 1981 tax cuts (70% to 50% top rate) did not produce measurable revenue feedback sufficient to offset revenue losses. Federal debt held by the public grew from 25% of GDP in 1981 to 40% by 1988. The 1983–1989 expansion is attributable primarily to Federal Reserve monetary easing after the Volcker disinflation and recovery from the 1981–82 recession — not to supply-side tax feedback.

Sources: [Saez (2001): Elasticities to Derive Optimal Income Tax Rates](https://eml.berkeley.edu/~saez/derive.pdf); [Saez, Slemrod, Giertz (2012): ETI — A Critical Review](https://eml.berkeley.edu/~saez/saez-slemrod-giertzJEL12.pdf); [EPI: Tax Reform Should Broaden the Base and Raise Top Rates](https://www.epi.org/press/tax-reform-broaden-base-raise-top-rates/)

### 5.4 Complexity — Compliance Costs and Small Business Burden

Americans spend an estimated 6.5 billion hours and $400 billion per year on federal tax compliance (National Taxpayers Union Foundation, 2024) — a real economic cost that produces no goods or services. The code extends to 6,871 pages plus approximately 70,000 pages of regulations and guidance.

The complexity burden is regressive: wealthy individuals and large corporations hire specialists to exploit complexity and reduce effective rates; middle-income taxpayers bear the highest compliance cost relative to their benefit; small business owners face genuine administrative burden. This is a legitimate concern, and it argues for simplification alongside revenue-raising reform — not against raising revenue.

The CAMT's near-zero revenue performance in 2024 ($572 million against $35 billion projected) reflects complexity that defeated its own purpose: the IRS waived CAMT penalties in 2023 because the rules were too complex to comply with. Revenue-raising reform must be administratively implementable. Mark-to-market on liquid assets is administratively simple — it requires no new infrastructure. Wealth tax on privately held assets is administratively hard. Good tax reform sequencing starts with the administratively simpler instruments.

---

### 5.5 Growth Effects — Investment, Depreciation, and Cost of Capital

The cost-of-capital argument against higher corporate and capital income taxes has a legitimate empirical foundation: capital taxes reduce the after-tax return on investment, raising the hurdle rate for new investment projects and reducing equilibrium capital formation. The Tax Foundation's general equilibrium models regularly find negative long-run GDP effects from higher corporate taxes.

The honest response:

1. Accelerated depreciation and investment tax credits offset cost-of-capital effects from higher statutory rates — the policy can raise statutory rates while protecting investment incentives through targeted credits. This is not inconsistent; it is what most OECD countries do (high statutory rates with investment-oriented deductions).

2. The empirical evidence from TCJA does not support the large investment response predicted by cost-of-capital models: 280 large US corporations spent $2.7 trillion on stock buybacks in the 2018–2022 period — 98% more than in the comparable pre-TCJA period. Capital investment growth in 2018 was modest; most TCJA savings were distributed to shareholders. The JCT/Federal Reserve (2022) study found no wage gains for bottom-90% workers from the corporate rate cut.

3. A 2024 Kennedy et al. JCT study reached the same conclusion: bottom-90% workers saw no wage gains from the TCJA corporate cut; top earners did.

The growth-effects concern is more credible for small, credit-constrained firms than for large publicly traded corporations that fund investment from retained earnings or low-cost debt. The reform design should protect investment by small and growing firms while raising effective rates on large corporations and ultra-high-net-worth individuals.

Sources: [Center for American Progress: TCJA 2 Years Later](https://www.americanprogress.org/article/tcja-2-years-later-corporations-not-workers-big-winners/); [EPI: TCJA Overwhelmingly Benefited the Rich](https://www.epi.org/press/the-tcja-overwhelmingly-benefited-the-rich-and-corporations-while-overlooking-working-families/)

## Section 6: Reform Proposals — Concrete and Sequenced

### Near-Term (No Constitutional Amendment Required; Achievable Through Ordinary Legislation)

**1. Close the stepped-up basis loophole.** Eliminate the cost-basis reset at death; treat transfer of appreciated assets at death as a realization event, with an installment payment option for illiquid assets above a threshold and an exemption for estates below $2 million. Revenue: $40–50 billion annually. No constitutional issues; requires only statutory amendment to Section 1014.

**2. Mark-to-market for liquid assets above $100 million.** Apply mark-to-market taxation to publicly traded securities held by taxpayers with net worth above $100 million. Revenue: $30–50 billion annually (Yale Budget Lab estimate range). Administratively straightforward — prices are already tracked by financial institutions and reported to the IRS. The *Moore* decision does not foreclose this; unrealized gains on liquid, publicly traded securities are more readily distinguishable from the private business income at issue in *Moore*.

**3. Restore GILTI minimum rate to 15%.** Amend the GILTI/NCTI provisions to produce an effective minimum rate of 15%, aligned with Pillar Two. Revenue: $30–35 billion annually (JCT estimates, Biden reform basis). Addresses the offshore profit-shifting incentive without requiring new multilateral coordination.

**4. Fix the CAMT accelerated depreciation carve-out.** Amend the AFSI calculation to disallow accelerated depreciation in computing book income for CAMT purposes — or replace book-income CAMT with a cash-flow minimum tax. Revenue potential: $30–35 billion annually (closing the gap between $572 million actual and $35 billion projected).

**5. Restore IRS enforcement funding.** Restore the $41.8 billion in rescinded IRA enforcement appropriations. At 5:1 long-run ROI, generates approximately $200 billion in additional revenue over the enforcement build-out period. Requires annual appropriations action.

**6. Restore and expand Direct File.** Re-legislate Direct File as a permanent program; require employer and financial institution pre-population of returns (replicating the Swedish/Danish model). This is not revenue-raising directly but reduces the $400 billion annual compliance burden, eliminates a commercial tax-preparation industry that lobbies against enforcement improvements, and increases voluntary compliance by simplifying the filing process.

**7. Phase out §199A for high-income earners.** Eliminate the 20% pass-through deduction for taxpayers above the current income thresholds ($383,900 for joint filers). Revenue: approximately $350 billion over a decade (Penn Wharton). Preserve the deduction for genuinely small businesses below the income threshold.

---

### Medium-Term (Achievable in a Favorable Legislative Environment)

**8. Progressive wealth tax.** 2% on net wealth above $50 million, 3% above $1 billion, with a high threshold, enhanced exit tax on unrealized gains at expatriation, comprehensive FATCA-integrated reporting, and anti-avoidance rules for entity structuring. Revenue: $100–200 billion annually (conservative behavioral-response-adjusted estimate). Requires robust IRS valuation capacity and sustained enforcement investment.

**9. Estate tax reform.** Restore the 2009 exemption ($3.5 million individual, $7 million couple) with graduated rates to 55% above $10 million. Revenue: approximately $100 billion over a decade. Pair with stepped-up basis elimination (reform 1 above) to close the double-benefit gap.

**10. Financial Transaction Tax.** 0.1% on equity purchases, 0.01% on derivative notional value, structured as a residence-based tax on US investors regardless of execution venue, with market-maker exemptions following UK stamp duty design. Revenue: $50–100 billion annually. Requires anti-avoidance rules for offshore execution.

**11. Carbon pricing with per-capita dividend.** $50/ton on CO2 emissions, rising by $5/ton annually, with 100% per-capita dividend return to all residents. Initial annual revenue: approximately $225 billion. Net effect for the bottom 60% of households: positive (dividend exceeds fuel cost increase). Pair with border carbon adjustment for imports from non-pricing jurisdictions (consistent with WTO rules and EU CBAM precedent).

---

### Long-Term Structural

**12. VAT-plus-demogrant hybrid.** Enact a 10% federal VAT on a broad base, paired with a $600/person annual per-capita demogrant to offset regressivity for the bottom 60%. Revenue after demogrant: approximately $400–500 billion annually. Requires Congressional action and would likely require a Senate rules change to overcome filibuster on a revenue-raising bill.

**13. Constitutional resolution of mark-to-market.** A targeted constitutional amendment clarifying that Congress may tax economic income — including accretion of wealth — without the realization requirement and without apportionment would permanently resolve the legal uncertainty surrounding mark-to-market and wealth taxes. Historical precedent: the Sixteenth Amendment's direct resolution of the pre-existing constitutional ambiguity around income taxes. A long-term objective requiring supermajority thresholds.

**Revenue range from the full portfolio**: Closing buy-borrow-die + GILTI reform + CAMT fix + IRS enforcement restoration + §199A reform + FTT + carbon tax: approximately $400–600 billion annually near-term. Medium-term wealth tax and estate reform: additional $150–250 billion. Total reform potential in a favorable legislative environment: $600–900 billion annually — enough to fund universal healthcare, free public higher education, and substantial deficit reduction simultaneously.

## Section 7: Political Economy

### 7.1 Why Tax Reform Is So Hard

The donor-class capture mechanism is documented rather than asserted. Gilens and Page (2014, *Perspectives on Politics*) analyzed 1,779 policy outcomes from 1981 to 2002 against the preferences of economic elites, organized interest groups, and average-income citizens. Economic elite preferences show strong correlation with policy outcomes. Average-income citizen preferences show no statistically significant correlation when elite preferences are controlled for. Tax policy is precisely the domain where economic elite preferences diverge most sharply from median voter preferences — and where the Gilens/Page asymmetry most directly predicts outcomes.

Complexity is not an accidental feature of the US tax code. It is a product of the lobbying process: each specific deduction, exemption, and preference reflects a successful lobbying campaign to carve out favorable treatment. The $1.8 trillion in annual tax expenditures (JCT FY2024 estimate) is the aggregate revenue cost of those successful campaigns. Complexity serves the interests of the firms that exploit it and the consulting industry that navigates it; it is directly against the interest of the median taxpayer who cannot exploit it.

The IRS is a uniquely politically vulnerable agency: its appropriations are annual, its enforcement activities affect powerful constituents, and its capacity to audit complex returns requires sustained multi-year funding that can be rescinded at any point. The 2022–2025 IRS funding cycle — appropriated by IRA, rescinded by three successive continuing resolutions — demonstrated that high-income enforcement investment is subject to rapid reversal whenever the political coalition supporting it loses legislative power.

### 7.2 The 1986 Tax Reform Act — Lessons for Coalition-Building

The Tax Reform Act of 1986 — which broadened the base substantially while reducing the statutory top rate from 50% to 28% — remains the most significant US tax reform since 1954. It passed with bipartisan support in a Democratic-controlled House and Republican-controlled Senate under a Republican president, for a narrow set of reasons that constrain its relevance as a model:

1. It was explicitly revenue-neutral — no net tax increase, which neutralized opposition from anti-tax Republicans.
2. It eliminated loopholes used by the wealthy and corporations while lowering rates — giving each side a political win.
3. Reagan provided sustained personal leadership at moments when the coalition threatened to collapse.
4. The reform did not address the incidence problem — it remained a rate-and-base reform of existing income taxation, not a new instrument (wealth tax, FTT, carbon tax).

The 1986 lesson for contemporary reform: base-broadening creates room for coalition-building that rate-increase-alone does not. Eliminating §199A's high-income windfall, closing the stepped-up basis, and fixing CAMT's accelerated depreciation carve-out can be framed as base-broadening in the 1986 tradition — closing loopholes that distort capital allocation — even when the net revenue effect is large. This framing is more durable politically than an explicit "tax increase on the wealthy" frame, though the distributive effect is identical.

### 7.3 Coalitions That Could Move Reform

The coalition for fiscal reform must connect three constituencies that rarely operate together:

**Deficit hawks and fiscal responsibility advocates**: The $606 billion annual net tax gap and the $200 billion in foregone enforcement returns from rescinded IRS funding provide a deficit-reduction case for tax enforcement that can attract Republican support when framed as collecting taxes already owed rather than raising new taxes.

**Climate and healthcare coalitions**: Carbon pricing revenue can fund universal health coverage and green infrastructure; making this connection explicit creates a constituency for the revenue instrument that extends far beyond tax-focused advocacy.

**Small business and competitive-market advocates**: The largest beneficiaries of §199A, corporate rate cuts, and GILTI loopholes are very large corporations and ultra-high-net-worth individuals. Small businesses generally face the statutory rates with fewer avoidance options. A reform that closes avoidance mechanisms while simplifying compliance for small businesses can be framed as leveling the playing field — which it is.

The 2024 election cycle demonstrated that donors contributing to political campaigns at the $2.6 billion level among 100 families expect and receive policy responsiveness. Tax reform at the scale described in this chapter requires either (a) campaign finance reform sufficient to reduce donor-class capture, or (b) a political moment — economic crisis, populist mandate, or cross-partisan convergence — that temporarily overrides the normal donor-capture equilibrium. The 1986 Act was such a moment. Identifying the conditions for the next one is the central political economy challenge.
