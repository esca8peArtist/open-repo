## Part II: Five Subsections — Structure, Reform Mechanisms, and Key Questions

### 26a. Federal Statutory Abortion Access

**The structural problem**: Dobbs eliminated the constitutional right but did not create federal statutory protection. No federal law currently protects the right to provide or obtain abortion services. State bans are the entire legal architecture. The result is a two-tier country where geographic accident determines whether a pregnant person can access emergency care.

**Primary reform mechanism**: The Women's Health Protection Act (WHPA). The bill would:
- Establish a federal statutory right to provide abortion services, protecting providers against state interference
- Establish a federal statutory right to access abortion services, creating a cause of action against state laws that impose restrictions "more burdensome than comparable restrictions on other medical procedures"
- Prohibit requirements designed to delay, deter, or preclude access: mandatory waiting periods, biased counseling mandates, gestational limits pre-viability
- Restore EMTALA emergency abortion mandate that the Biden administration issued and the current administration rescinded
- Block federal funding leverage against states that protect abortion access

**Legislative status (April 2026)**: Senate-blocked. All Senate Democrats have cosponsored; no Republican support. Requires 60-vote threshold or filibuster reform. Passage pathway is tied to Domain 1 (electoral reform) and Domain 2 (Senate procedural reform).

**Secondary mechanisms**:
- FDA protection for mifepristone approval — the current administration has not moved to revoke but has signaled willingness to do so
- Medication abortion access by mail: telemedicine prescribing + mail delivery protections at federal level
- Safe harbor for providers who see patients from ban states

**Constitutional basis**: Commerce Clause (abortion as interstate commerce — documented by out-of-state travel data); Section 5 of the 14th Amendment (equal protection argument — abortion bans disproportionately harm people of lower income and fewer resources); Spending Clause (federal funding conditions)

**Key questions for research**:
1. What is the current constitutional authority argument for WHPA under post-Loper Bright administrative law doctrine?
2. What specific EMTALA language was rescinded in April 2026 and what is the clinical evidence on deaths caused by the rescission?
3. What is the current status of mifepristone litigation (FDA v. Alliance for Hippocratic Medicine was decided 9-0 in favor of FDA in June 2024 — what has happened since)?
4. How many providers have been criminally prosecuted under state bans as of April 2026?
5. What are the documented rates of out-of-state abortion travel and the economic barrier it creates for lower-income patients?

### 26b. Contraceptive Access and Dobbs Doctrinal Limits

**The structural problem**: Justice Thomas's Dobbs concurrence explicitly invites reconsideration of Griswold v. Connecticut (1965), which established the right to contraception for married couples, and Eisenstadt v. Baird (1972), which extended it to unmarried people. The Dobbs majority's analytical framework — "deeply rooted in this Nation's history and tradition" — does not protect contraception under a historical analysis either, because the history Thomas cites predates the progressive expansion of bodily autonomy rights. Several Republican state legislators have proposed bills targeting IUD and emergency contraception access by defining them as abortifacients (a contested medical claim).

**Primary reform mechanism**: The Right to Contraception Act (introduced in both House and Senate). Would establish a federal statutory right to:
- Obtain and use contraceptive methods approved by the FDA
- Receive contraceptive counseling from healthcare providers
- Be free from interference by state or local governments in contraceptive decisions

**Secondary mechanisms**:
- Codify Griswold explicitly by statute — the Right to Contraception Act largely does this
- FDA protection for emergency contraception approval (Plan B, ella) as a statutory, not merely administrative, protection
- Prohibit state criminalization of travel for contraceptive access
- Prohibit state data collection programs targeting contraceptive use

**Constitutional basis**: Commerce Clause, 14th Amendment Equal Protection (the gender dimension — contraceptive restrictions fall disproportionately on people who can become pregnant)

**Key questions for research**:
1. What is the current status of state-level legislation targeting IUDs or emergency contraception as "abortifacients" (April 2026 legislative tracker)?
2. What is the text of the Right to Contraception Act and its legislative history?
3. What would a Griswold overruling actually mean in practice — what state laws would spring back into effect?
4. What is the contraceptive access gap by income and geography pre- and post-Dobbs?
5. Has any state since Dobbs passed legislation restricting contraceptive access?

### 26c. Gender-Affirming Care Protection

**The structural problem**: As of April 2026, at least 26 states have banned or severely restricted gender-affirming care for minors. Several states have extended bans to adults. The federal government has acted through Medicaid and Medicare rules to withdraw coverage for gender-affirming care, in effect defunding it for low-income individuals. The evidence base on gender-affirming care outcomes is substantial: the American Academy of Pediatrics, American Medical Association, American Psychological Association, and World Professional Association for Transgender Health all affirm gender-affirming care as evidence-based treatment for gender dysphoria. Major systematic reviews from Sweden, Finland, and the UK (Cass Review) have been selectively cited by legislators while the same reviews' overall conclusions support individualized, evidence-based care.

**Primary reform mechanisms**:
- Federal standard prohibiting state bans on evidence-based gender-affirming care for both minors and adults where the care is prescribed by a licensed provider following established clinical protocols
- Medicare/Medicaid coverage parity for gender-affirming care — same coverage as other prescribed treatments for the same diagnosis
- Strengthen Section 1557 of the ACA (anti-discrimination in healthcare) to prohibit discrimination on the basis of gender identity, including denial of care
- Prohibit state criminalization of providers who deliver gender-affirming care in compliance with federal standards

**Constitutional basis**: Section 1557 of the ACA (existing framework); Commerce Clause; Equal Protection Clause (sex discrimination — the Supreme Court's Bostock decision extended Title VII sex discrimination protection to LGBTQ+ people, and the same logic applies in healthcare contexts)

**Key questions for research**:
1. What is the current legal status of state gender-affirming care bans — are they being enjoined by federal courts or upheld? (Key cases: United States v. Skrmetti, argued before Supreme Court in late 2024, decision expected 2025)
2. What are the documented mental health outcomes of gender-affirming care for minors vs. denial of care? (Peer-reviewed evidence from Swedish and Finnish studies)
3. What exactly did the April 2026 Medicare/Medicaid rule changes prohibit?
4. How many providers have been prosecuted or faced license revocation for providing gender-affirming care?
5. What is the population of trans people affected by state bans, and what are the documented harms of care denial (suicidality data)?

**Note on Cass Review**: The Cass Review (UK, April 2024) is frequently cited in legislation and court filings. Research should assess what it actually concluded vs. how it has been cited — systematic reviews by WPATH and others have found methodological problems with the studies Cass reviewed, and the Cass Review itself does not recommend blanket prohibitions on gender-affirming care.

### 26d. Maternal Health Equity

**The structural problem**: The United States has the highest maternal mortality rate of any wealthy democracy: 32.9 deaths per 100,000 live births (CDC, 2021 data — the most complete available). Peer nations range from 3 to 8 per 100,000. Black maternal mortality is 69.9 per 100,000 — 2.1 times the overall rate — and is the clearest measurable racial health justice gap in any OECD country. Native American maternal mortality is similarly elevated. Abortion bans compound maternal mortality: states with abortion bans have documented higher rates of complications, preventable deaths, and obstetric emergencies resulting from delayed or denied emergency care under EMTALA rescission. The Medicaid postpartum coverage gap (60 days in many states vs. the clinical standard of 12 months) is a documented contributor to late maternal deaths.

**Primary reform mechanisms**:
- Momnibus Act (Black Maternal Health Momnibus Act of 2021, re-introduced 2023) — a legislative package covering:
  - Data collection mandates with racial/ethnic disaggregation
  - Grants for community-based doula programs
  - Funding for implicit bias training in obstetric care
  - Expansion of Maternal Mortality Review Committees to all states with federal data sharing requirements
  - Coverage of doula services through Medicaid
- Federal mandate for Medicaid postpartum coverage extension to 12 months (this was included in the ARP as a state option — full mandate is the reform)
- Federal funding for maternal health equity initiatives in under-resourced areas
- EMTALA restoration and enforcement (overlaps with 26a but critical here — documented deaths from denial of emergency obstetric care)
- Maternal mortality review as a condition for hospital participation in Medicare/Medicaid

**Fiscal analysis starting point**: The U.S. spends approximately $111 billion annually on maternal healthcare. Each maternal death costs an estimated $1.2 million in lost productivity and healthcare costs (AHRQ data). Reducing the maternal mortality rate to peer-nation levels (from 32.9 to 8/100k) would prevent approximately 1,000 deaths per year. Net fiscal calculation: meaningful positive ROI. The racial disparity is particularly costly — Black maternal deaths are disproportionately concentrated in the working-age population.

**Key questions for research**:
1. What is the most current CDC maternal mortality data (2022 or 2023 — 2021 is the figure currently in the archive)?
2. What is the documented causal mechanism linking abortion bans to maternal mortality — which specific clinical conditions are being affected?
3. What is the current status of Medicaid postpartum coverage extensions by state (how many states have opted in vs. still at 60 days)?
4. What is the evidence base for doula program effectiveness in reducing maternal mortality?
5. What are the documented racial disparities in obstetric care that are independent of socioeconomic factors?

### 26e. Reproductive Surveillance Prohibition

**The structural problem**: The commercial data ecosystem built for advertising has become an enforcement infrastructure for abortion criminalization. This is the most technically novel dimension of the post-Dobbs landscape. Documented surveillance vectors (from rights-protection-evidence.md Section 7e):
- Health and menstrual tracking apps: 79% share data with third parties; not covered by HIPAA
- Location data: clinic visits commercially purchasable from data brokers
- Search and browsing history: subpoenable
- Payment records: prescription purchases, out-of-state travel bookings

The data broker loophole is the mechanism: law enforcement can purchase data commercially that would require a warrant to obtain directly. The Fourth Amendment's third-party doctrine further weakens protection — data voluntarily shared with a third party (like an app) carries reduced constitutional protection.

**Primary reform mechanisms**:
- Extend the data broker prohibition framework (covered in Domain 21) specifically to reproductive health data categories — this requires a more targeted statutory approach because the general ADPPA (American Data Privacy and Protection Act) has not passed
- My Health My Data Act (Washington State, passed 2023) as a state model for federal legislation — prohibits collection, sharing, and use of reproductive health data without consent; creates a private right of action
- Require warrant for purchase of reproductive health data by law enforcement (closes the commercial-purchase-as-warrant-workaround)
- Prohibit app stores from hosting apps that collect reproductive health data without explicit informed consent and opt-in
- HIPAA expansion: extend HIPAA coverage to health-adjacent apps that collect reproductive health data

**Cross-domain links**: This subsection is deliberately the thinnest — it serves as an anchor for the cross-domain connection to Domain 21 (Data Privacy and Digital Surveillance). The research here should be precise about what is NOT already covered in Domain 21 and what is unique to the reproductive context.

**Key questions for research**:
1. What is the current legislative status of the My Health My Data Act model at the federal level?
2. Have any prosecutions under state abortion bans used commercially purchased data as evidence (as of April 2026)?
3. What does the legal landscape of data broker regulation look like in April 2026 — has any federal action narrowed the loophole?
4. What is the technical architecture of how location data broker sales to law enforcement actually work?
5. Which data types are most dangerous: location data, search history, payment records, or health app data?

## Part IV: International Benchmarks — Full Architecture

### France (2024) — Constitutional Entrenchment Model

France's April 2024 constitutional amendment is the strongest international precedent for any domain in the framework. It is the only country in the world to have explicitly constitutionalized abortion as a "guaranteed freedom" (as distinct from a right — the word "freedom" was chosen because French constitutional jurisprudence treats freedoms as more strongly protected than rights). The mechanism: joint parliamentary session at Versailles (780-72 vote). The catalyst: explicit response to Dobbs.

**What Domain 26 research should extract**:
- The precise constitutional text and its drafting history (why "freedom" vs. "right")
- The French government's stated rationale (Dobbs as direct trigger)
- Whether the constitutional status changes anything practically given France already had statutory protection (Veil Law, 1975)
- The political coalition that made it possible (Macron as centrist champion)

### Ireland (2018) — Citizens' Assembly Constitutional Reform Model

Ireland's 2018 referendum (66.4% Yes to repeal the Eighth Amendment and legalize abortion) is already cited in the main proposal as a citizens' assembly case study. For Domain 26, the specific reproductive rights dimension needs deeper treatment:
- The Eighth Amendment's history (1983 — Ireland constitutionalized anti-abortion provisions amid Catholic Church political dominance)
- The Citizens' Assembly 2016-2018 design (99 citizens, six months, expert testimony, deliberation — produced 64% supermajority recommendation for abortion access)
- The referendum result and implementation (Health (Regulation of Termination of Pregnancy) Act 2018)
- The political conditions: Fine Gael and Fianna Fail both supported Yes campaign despite decades of opposition

**What makes this model transferable**: The citizens' assembly mechanism created political cover that legislative action alone could not. The same dynamic exists in the US — abortion deadlocks in legislatures but wins in referendums (11 of 11 state ballot initiatives since Dobbs have protected or expanded abortion access).

### Argentina (2020) — Legislative Legalization After Decades of Failure

Argentina legalized abortion in December 2020 (38-29 Senate vote) after decades of failed attempts. This is notable because it was achieved through ordinary legislation — not constitutional change or referendum — by a left-center government. The law permits abortion on request until 14 weeks; after that, in cases of rape or risk to health.

**What Domain 26 research should extract**:
- The political coalition: Peronist center-left + feminist movement "green wave" (La Marea Verde)
- Why 2020 succeeded when 2018 failed (same bill, different president)
- The comparative lesson for WHPA: legislative passage is achievable in favorable political conditions even after long failure

### Canada (1988) — Rights-Based Constitutional Framework Without Gestational Limits

Canada's Morgentaler decision is already cited in the archive. The deep research question: Canada has had NO federal abortion law since 1988 — the Supreme Court struck down the existing law without the legislature ever replacing it. The result is that abortion is treated as any other medical procedure. This is the most permissive framework in any major democracy and has produced no documented negative public health outcomes.

**What Domain 26 research should extract**:
- The Morgentaler reasoning (Section 7 of the Charter — life, liberty, security of the person)
- Why Canada has never passed a replacement law (multiple failed attempts 1988-1992)
- The clinical outcomes: abortion rate in Canada vs. comparable countries; maternal mortality outcomes

### WHO Essential Medicines List

The WHO added mifepristone to its Essential Medicines List in 2005 (updated 2019). This is the strongest international health authority endorsement: a drug considered essential to basic healthcare worldwide. The current administration's signals toward mifepristone approval create a direct conflict with WHO's global health standard.

## Part V: Movement Landscape and Political Context

### State Ballot Initiative Record Post-Dobbs

This is one of the strongest evidence categories for Domain 26 — and one that is largely absent from the existing archive. Since June 2022, states have held abortion-related ballot measures. The record:

- 2022: Michigan, Vermont, California, Montana — all protective measures passed
- 2023: Ohio (Issue 1 — constitutional protection for abortion) passed 57-43 despite Republican supermajority legislature
- 2024: Multiple states passed protective constitutional amendments (specific count and states needed for research)
- 2025-2026: Republican state legislatures are now introducing legislation to make citizen initiatives harder in direct response to this ballot success — this is a direct democracy attack (Domain 1 connection)

**Key insight**: Abortion has won every ballot initiative since Dobbs, including in Republican-leaning states. This is the clearest evidence that reproductive rights has majority public support in practice — not just in polling — and that the state-level legislative bans represent minority rule enabled by gerrymandering and supermajority requirements.

### The Coalition Landscape

Pro-choice coalition organizations that will be essential sources and context:
- Reproductive Freedom for All (formerly NARAL) — national advocacy
- Planned Parenthood Federation of America — largest service provider
- Center for Reproductive Rights — primary litigation organization
- ACLU Reproductive Freedom Project
- National Women's Law Center
- SisterSong Women of Color Reproductive Justice Collective — racial justice framing
- National Asian Pacific American Women's Forum
- Indigenous Women Rising

**Note**: The reproductive justice framework (developed by Black women and women of color, 1994) is broader than "pro-choice" — it encompasses the right to have children, not have children, and parent in safe and supportive communities. Domain 26 should use this framing rather than purely the "choice" frame, which tends to center the experiences of wealthier white women.

**Lower-confidence areas requiring new research**:

**Evidence quality gradient**: The abortion section (26a, 26b) will have the highest-quality evidence — years of litigation, academic study, and policy analysis. The gender-affirming care section (26c) will require careful navigation of contested clinical evidence, particularly the Cass Review controversy. The maternal mortality section (26d) will have strong epidemiological evidence. The surveillance section (26e) will have good advocacy documentation but may lack quantitative prosecution data.
