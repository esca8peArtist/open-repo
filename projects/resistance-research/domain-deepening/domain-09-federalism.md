## Section 1: The Shelby County Damage — Full Legal and Empirical Record

### 1.1 What the Court Actually Held and Why It Matters

The proposal accurately states that Shelby County v. Holder (2013) "weakened federal voting rights oversight." What it does not state is the precise legal mechanism — which matters for understanding both the damage and what is required to repair it.

**The Voting Rights Act's two-track structure.** The VRA's original design rested on two interlocking provisions. Section 5 — preclearance — required covered jurisdictions to obtain advance approval from the DOJ or the D.C. District Court before implementing any change to voting laws or procedures. Section 4(b) — the coverage formula — defined which jurisdictions were "covered" based on whether they used voting tests or devices as prerequisites to voting and had voter registration or turnout below 50% in the 1964, 1968, or 1972 presidential elections. The original formula was targeted at the South's specific mechanisms for disenfranchisement as they existed in the 1960s.

**What the Court struck.** Chief Justice Roberts, writing for a 5-4 majority, held that Section 4(b) was unconstitutional as applied in 2013 — not because preclearance was inherently impermissible, but because the coverage formula was based on decades-old data that no longer reflected current conditions. The Court applied a doctrine it called "equal sovereignty of the states" — the principle that Congress must have current evidence justifying differential treatment of states — and found that Congress had reauthorized the VRA in 2006 using the same 1965-era formula without updating it to reflect contemporary discrimination patterns. Section 5 (preclearance itself) was not struck; it was rendered inoperative because there is no valid coverage formula to trigger it. Congress could, in theory, restore preclearance tomorrow by enacting a new coverage formula supported by current evidence — a task the John Lewis Voting Rights Advancement Act (introduced multiple times, never enacted) was designed to accomplish.

**The practical consequence.** The day after the decision, Texas announced it would immediately implement a strict photo ID law — Senate Bill 14 — that had been blocked by DOJ preclearance review. Within weeks, North Carolina enacted H.B. 589, the most sweeping voting restrictions in its modern history: eliminating same-day registration, shortening the early voting period, prohibiting out-of-precinct voting, and imposing a strict photo ID requirement. These laws could not have passed DOJ preclearance; without preclearance, DOJ's only remedy was Section 2 litigation — a slower, more expensive process that cannot stop a law before it takes effect in a single election.

Sources: [Shelby County v. Holder, 570 U.S. 529 (2013) — Justia](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/570/529/); [Effects of Shelby County v. Holder — Brennan Center](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/effects-shelby-county-v-holder); [About Section 5 of the Voting Rights Act — DOJ Civil Rights Division](https://www.justice.gov/crt/about-section-5-voting-rights-act)

### 1.2 Polling Place Closures — State-by-State Evidence

The proposal cites Texas's 750 polling place closures. The full picture across formerly covered states is substantially larger.

**The 2016 Leadership Conference count.** A Leadership Conference on Civil and Human Rights report published in November 2016 — "Democracy Diverted: Polling Place Closures and the Right to Vote" — documented 868 fewer polling places in formerly covered jurisdictions compared to the 2012 election. State-by-state breakdown from that report:
- **Texas**: 403 fewer polling places (the 750 figure the proposal cites reflects a longer time horizon through 2020)
- **Arizona**: 320 fewer polling places — nearly every county reduced locations, with Maricopa County, the state's largest (4 million residents), reducing from 200 locations in 2012 to 60 in 2016
- **Georgia**: 214 fewer polling places
- **Louisiana**: 61% of parishes reduced polling locations

**The longer trend.** The Brennan Center's analysis of polling place data from 2014 to 2018 found 1,173 fewer polling places in previously covered states, despite a significant increase in voter turnout that would ordinarily require more, not fewer, locations to manage lines. The closures were concentrated in majority-minority counties. A 2019 SSRN study by Cachon and Kaaua on Georgia specifically found that polling place closures in the 2018 gubernatorial election increased average wait times substantially in majority-Black precincts compared to majority-white precincts — a direct measurable effect on the practical exercise of the franchise.

Sources: [Democracy Diverted: Polling Place Closures and the Right to Vote — Leadership Conference on Civil Rights](https://civilrights.org/democracy-diverted/); [New Report Documents 868 Polling Place Closures in Former VRA Preclearance States — Leadership Conference](https://civilrights.org/2016/11/04/new-report-documents-868-polling-place-closures-in-former-vra-preclearance-states/); [Democracy on the Line: Polling Place Closures in Georgia — SSRN](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4329036)

### 1.3 North Carolina — The "Surgical Precision" Case

North Carolina H.B. 589 represents the most fully litigated example of what Shelby County enabled. The Fourth Circuit's 2016 ruling in North Carolina State Conference of the NAACP v. McCrory — which struck the law — provides the clearest judicial articulation of post-Shelby targeting.

The court found that the legislature had "requested data on the use, by race, of a number of voting practices" — and then enacted restrictions that targeted "with almost surgical precision" the specific practices African Americans used at higher rates than white voters. Specifically: African Americans used same-day registration at twice the rate of white voters — eliminated. African Americans used out-of-precinct voting at higher rates — eliminated. African Americans used early voting, particularly the first week, at higher rates — early voting cut from 17 days to 10 days with the first week removed. Photo IDs that African Americans were less likely to possess were specified as acceptable; IDs African Americans were more likely to possess (public assistance cards, student IDs) were excluded.

The North Carolina case is significant not only for the finding but for the evidence trail: the legislature's own records showed they had sought racial data before enacting restrictions. This is the pre-clearance mechanism in reverse — the state had conducted its own racially targeted analysis to ensure the law would achieve its intended demographic effect.

Sources: [Fourth Circuit Strikes North Carolina Voter ID Law, Finding 'Discriminatory Intent' — Constitutional Accountability Center](https://www.theusconstitution.org/news/fourth-circuit-strikes-north-carolina-voter-id-law-finding-discriminatory-intent/); [Battered by the Storm, 10 Years Since Shelby County in North Carolina — Democracy Docket](https://www.democracydocket.com/analysis/battered-by-the-storm-10-years-since-shelby-county-in-north-carolina/)

### 1.4 Peer-Reviewed Turnout Data — Specific Findings

The proposal's citation of a "7% turnout decline" and the Hajnal, Lajevardi, and Nielson (2017) study is accurate but requires contextualization, because that study's findings have been methodologically contested.

**Hajnal, Lajevardi, and Nielson (2017).** Published in the Journal of Politics, this study used Cooperative Congressional Election Study (CCES) survey data from 2006 to 2014 to estimate the turnout effects of strict voter ID laws. The study found that strict ID requirements reduced minority turnout by 2-3 percentage points in general elections and up to 7 percentage points in primaries. The primary finding is a differential impact: the laws reduced minority turnout substantially more than white turnout.

**The Grimmer et al. (2018) methodological challenge.** A subsequent paper by Grimmer and colleagues (Stanford, Princeton) identified modeling issues and replicated the study with corrections, finding no statistically discernible average effect on turnout. The dispute is over modeling choices, not over the underlying data. The Hajnal et al. authors replied that their central claim — differential impact on racial minorities — was not disputed even by Grimmer et al. Both papers agree that if effects exist, they are concentrated among minority voters.

**Billings, Braun, Jones, and Shi (2024) — the more durable study.** Published in the Journal of Public Economics (2024, volume 230C), this study examines the Shelby County decision specifically rather than voter ID laws generally. Using the universe of registered voters matched to Census block sociodemographic data, the researchers compare turnout in Census blocks with higher shares of Black residents in formerly covered jurisdictions before and after 2013, using comparable blocks in non-covered counties as the control. The finding: turnout for Black voters relative to white voters declined measurably in previously covered blocks after Shelby, concentrated in counties with larger Black and Hispanic populations. This is a well-designed natural experiment — the treatment is the loss of preclearance, not a single law, and the comparison is within the same counties over time.

**The Brennan Center's aggregate finding.** The Brennan Center's 2024 report on the racial turnout gap found that between 2012 and 2022, the turnout gap between white and nonwhite voters in counties once covered under Section 5 grew by 9 percentage points, while the gap in comparable non-covered counties grew by only 5 points. The gap between white and Black voters specifically in Section 5 areas grew by 11 points.

Sources: [Voter Identification Laws and the Suppression of Minority Votes — Hajnal, Lajevardi, Nielson (UCSD)](https://pages.ucsd.edu/~zhajnal/page5/documents/voterIDhajnaletal.pdf); [Disparate Racial Impacts of Shelby County v. Holder on Voter Turnout — Billings et al., SSRN](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4310237); [The Racial Turnout Gap 11 Years After SCOTUS Diminished the Voting Rights Act — Brennan Center](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/racial-turnout-gap-11-years-after-scotus-diminished-voting-rights-act); [Supreme Court's Shelby County ruling widened racial gap in voter turnout — Votebeat](https://www.votebeat.org/2024/03/18/shelby-county-racial-turnout-gap-voting-rights-act/)

## Section 2: State Preemption — The Full Evidence Base

### 2.1 Birmingham, Alabama — Anatomy of the Canonical Case

The Birmingham minimum wage preemption case is the most thoroughly litigated instance of racial preemption in the post-2013 era. The full arc matters because it demonstrates both the depth of the constitutional problem and the limits of litigation as a remedy.

**The ordinance.** In August 2015, the Birmingham City Council unanimously passed an ordinance raising the city's minimum wage from the federal floor of $7.25/hour to $8.50 in 2016 and $10.10 in 2017. Birmingham is approximately 70% Black; the minimum wage increase was designed to address the city's substantial poverty rate, concentrated in its majority-Black workforce.

**The preemption law.** In February 2016 — six months after the ordinance passed but before the first raise took effect in March 2016 — the Alabama state legislature passed Act 2016-18, which retroactively prohibited any Alabama municipality from enacting minimum wages above the state minimum. The law was passed in a matter of days in a legislative session characterized by multiple observers as "rushed." The state legislature is overwhelmingly white; at the time, the legislature's Republican caucus, which controlled both chambers, was majority-white in a state where the minority population is approximately 30%. Governor Robert Bentley signed the bill immediately.

**The litigation.** The Alabama NAACP and a coalition of Birmingham workers filed suit in federal court in May 2016, arguing that the preemption law violated the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment by targeting a policy whose primary beneficiaries were Black workers. The federal district court dismissed all claims in February 2017.

**The Eleventh Circuit panel reversal.** A three-judge panel of the Eleventh Circuit reversed the dismissal in 2018, finding the workers' complaint stated a plausible claim. The panel's opinion used language that directly addressed the racial dimensions: it described the legislative process as "rushed, reactionary, and racially polarized," and found that the allegations — that the legislature had specifically targeted a Birmingham ordinance whose primary beneficiaries were Black workers — were sufficient to survive a motion to dismiss.

**The full Eleventh Circuit's dismissal.** The case was reheard by the full Eleventh Circuit (en banc), which reversed the panel and dismissed the suit. The full court found that the plaintiffs had not sufficiently pleaded discriminatory intent — the legal standard for Equal Protection challenges requiring proof that race was a motivating factor in the legislature's decision, not merely that the law's effects were racially disparate. The minimum wage in Birmingham remained at $7.25/hour. The wage raise never took effect.

**The structural lesson.** The case illustrates the core problem: in Dillon's Rule states (Alabama operates under Dillon's Rule), municipalities have only the powers explicitly granted by the state. A city's unanimous democratic decision can be voided retroactively by a state legislature that disagrees with the policy outcome. The primary legal recourse — Equal Protection challenge — requires proof of discriminatory intent, a demanding standard that courts have made progressively harder to meet since Washington v. Davis (1976). The preemption mechanism is structurally effective and legally largely immune to challenge.

Sources: [In Battle Pitting Cities vs. States Over Minimum Wage, Birmingham Scores a Win — NPR](https://www.npr.org/2018/07/27/632723920/in-battle-pitting-cities-vs-states-over-minimum-wage-birmingham-scores-a-win); [Full 11th Circuit Tosses Fight Over Alabama Wage Limits — Courthouse News](https://www.courthousenews.com/full-11th-circuit-tosses-fight-over-alabama-wage-limits/); [Birmingham and the Impact of Race on State Preemption Laws — OnLabor](https://onlabor.org/birmingham-the-impact-of-race-on-state-preemption-laws/); [Legal Action to Undo Alabama's Preemption of Birmingham Minimum Wage Law — NELP](https://www.nelp.org/legal-action-to-undo-alabamas-preemption-of-birmingham-minimum-wage-law/)

### 2.2 The Most Aggressive Preemption States — Pattern and Data

The proposal describes a general preemption trend. The specific state record is more damning.

**EPI's preemption tracker.** The Economic Policy Institute maintains a regularly updated preemption map documenting workers' rights preemption across all 50 states. As of 2024, 25 states preempt local minimum wages; 20 states preempt local paid sick leave requirements; 15 states preempt local scheduling ordinances; and 43 states preempt some form of local gun regulation.

**Southern states as the most aggressive preemptors.** EPI's 2022 report "Preempting Progress: State Interference in Local Policymaking in the South" documents that Southern states — including Alabama, Florida, Georgia, Mississippi, South Carolina, Tennessee, and Texas — are more likely than states in other regions to preempt local labor standards, and that preemption laws in these states are passed by majority-white legislatures and disproportionately target policies enacted by cities whose residents are majority people of color.

**The accelerating pace.** Governing magazine's analysis documents that more than 115 state laws limiting local worker protections passed between 2011 and 2019, compared to just 15 such laws between 1984 and 2010. The acceleration corresponds directly with the Fight for $15 movement: as cities began enacting local wage floors, state legislatures moved preemptively.

**The gerrymandering connection.** The structural mechanism enabling urban preemption is legislative gerrymandering. State legislatures that over-represent rural constituencies — drawn under maps that were themselves often the product of racially discriminatory intent, as found in North Carolina, Texas, and Virginia redistricting litigation — systematically have incentives to override urban policy decisions. The cities that enact progressive labor, environmental, and civil rights policies tend to be majority Democratic and often majority-minority; the state legislatures that override them tend to be majority Republican and majority-white. This is not incidental. The Grassroots Change Preemption Watch project documents that the pace of preemption law enactment accelerated sharply after 2010 — precisely when post-census redistricting, combined with Republicans' coordinated REDMAP state legislative strategy, produced the most gerrymandered state legislative maps in modern American history.

Sources: [Workers' Rights Preemption in the U.S. — EPI Preemption Map](https://www.epi.org/preemption-map/); [Preempting Progress: State Interference in Local Policymaking in the South — EPI](https://www.epi.org/publication/preemption-in-the-south/); [Less Politics Is Local: States Get Increasingly Aggressive About Pre-Emption — Governing](https://www.governing.com/politics/less-politics-is-local-states-get-increasingly-aggressive-about-pre-emption); [Updated EPI tracker shows more states obstructing workers' rights — EPI](https://www.epi.org/blog/updated-epi-preemption-tracker/)

### 2.3 Granular Wage-Loss Data — NELP Estimates

The proposal states the $1.5 billion annual wage-loss figure from EPI without grounding it in specific cities or worker counts. The NELP's detailed analysis provides the granular evidence.

**NELP's city-level accounting.** NELP's report "Fighting Wage Preemption: How Workers Have Lost Billions in Wages and How We Can Restore Local Democracy" examined 12 cities and counties that enacted local minimum wage ordinances between 2000 and 2019, only to have them preempted by state law. The combined annual wage loss for workers in those 12 jurisdictions: approximately $1.5 billion. Average individual loss per affected worker: approximately $4,100 per year. Total workers losing raises: approximately 346,000 directly affected by ordinances that were already enacted and then retroactively voided.

**St. Louis as a case study.** Missouri's state legislature preempted St. Louis's local minimum wage ordinance in 2017, just weeks after it took effect. Approximately 31,000 workers had already received a raise when the city's ordinance took effect in May 2017 — those raises were reversed. An additional 7,000 workers who would have received subsequent scheduled increases lost those raises as well. Total: 38,000 workers, primarily in low-wage service sector jobs concentrated in majority-Black neighborhoods.

**The structural pattern.** Seventeen of the 21 states that remain at the federal minimum of $7.25/hour have enacted preemption laws blocking local wage increases. This is not a coincidence of political geography — it is a deliberate coordination of state legislative authority to prevent local democratic majorities from setting labor standards above the federal floor.

Sources: [Fighting Wage Preemption: How Workers Have Lost Billions in Wages — NELP](https://www.nelp.org/insights-research/fighting-wage-preemption/); [Report: Workers Lose Billions Thanks to Corporate Campaign to Block Local Raises — NELP](https://www.nelp.org/report-workers-lose-billions-wages-thanks-corporate-campaign-block-local-minimum-wage-increases/); [City Governments Are Raising Standards for Working People — and State Legislators Are Lowering Them Back Down — EPI](https://www.epi.org/publication/city-governments-are-raising-standards-for-working-people-and-state-legislators-are-lowering-them-back-down/)

## Section 3: Fragmented Governance — Fiscal Costs and Consolidation Evidence

### 3.1 Illinois as the Extreme Case

The proposal notes that the U.S. has approximately 90,000 units of local government. Illinois alone has 6,963 — more than any other state in the nation, beating its nearest competitor by more than 1,800 units.

**What this produces in practice.** According to the Illinois Policy Institute's analysis and the Civic Federation of Chicago's inventory, Illinois is the only state in the United States where a majority of residents pay property taxes to three layers of general-purpose local government simultaneously: county, township, and municipality. The median Illinois resident is subject to a county board, a township government, a municipal government, a school district (often multiple: elementary, high school, and community college), a fire protection district, a library district, a park district, a sanitary district, and potentially a mosquito abatement district, a soil and water conservation district, and others. The Civic Federation has documented that Illinois has 890 school districts — more than all but one or two states — plus over 1,400 townships.

**The fiscal cost.** Illinois residents pay the second-highest median effective property tax rates for residential property in the nation. The Illinois Policy Institute and the Chicago Metropolitan Agency for Planning (CMAP) have both documented a direct link between government fragmentation and duplicated administrative overhead. A 2024 CMAP analysis notes that across the six-county Chicago metropolitan area, many overlapping special districts maintain separate administrative offices, separate payrolls, separate health benefit programs, and separate procurement systems for functions that could be shared — a direct fiscal cost that falls on property taxpayers. The overall cost of this administrative duplication has been estimated at hundreds of millions of dollars annually in the Chicago metropolitan area alone, though precise figures are difficult to isolate because jurisdictions do not report consolidated costs.

**The political consequence of fragmentation.** Illinois's fragmented governance is also a democratic accountability failure. Most special districts hold separate elections, typically with turnout below 15%, in off-cycle elections that appear on ballots that most voters never see. Decisions about school financing, park maintenance, and library operating hours are made by boards that most citizens cannot name and most citizens never vote for. This is not a theoretical accountability gap — it is a documented phenomenon in Illinois civic research.

Sources: [Illinois Has 6,963 Units of Local Government — Illinois Policy Institute](https://www.illinoispolicy.org/press-releases/illinois-has-6963-units-of-local-government-more-than-any-other-state-in-the-nation-illinois-policy-institute-report/); [Too Much Government: Illinois's Thousands of Local Governments — Illinois Policy Institute](https://www.illinoispolicy.org/reports/too-much-government-illinois-thousands-of-local-governments/); [Considerations and Practices of Local Government Consolidation — CMAP](https://cmap.illinois.gov/news-updates/considerations-and-practices-of-local-government-consolidation/); [Local Government Fragmentation: Potential Solutions from the GFOA — UIC Government Finance Research Center](https://gfrc.uic.edu/local-government-fragmentation-potential-solutions-from-the-gfoa/)

### 3.2 Consolidation Evidence — What the Cases Show

The three major American city-county consolidations provide a nuanced and honest picture: consolidation can work, but is not a cure-all, and political conditions matter more than technical design.

**Indianapolis Unigov (1970).** In 1970, Indiana's Republican-controlled state legislature enacted Unigov, consolidating the city of Indianapolis with Marion County. The consolidation was enacted by the state legislature without a referendum — a point critics note as democratically problematic. Overnight, Indianapolis grew from 82 to 402 square miles and from 480,000 to 740,000 in population. The Abell Foundation's 40-year retrospective found that Unigov's economic vitality transformation was real — Indianapolis became a Midwest regional hub and major convention destination — but came with complications. Because the consolidation excluded independent school districts, fire departments, and several municipalities, it was a partial rather than complete consolidation. Majority-Black Indianapolis city residents found themselves in a larger consolidated jurisdiction that initially diluted their political representation, a pattern documented by political scientists as a deliberate design choice by the Republican-controlled legislature that enacted Unigov.

**Nashville-Davidson County (1962).** Nashville merged with Davidson County in 1962, becoming the first major city-county consolidation in the modern era. The outer areas of Davidson County that were consolidated into the new Metro Nashville still lack basic infrastructure — sidewalks, stormwater drainage — more than 60 years later, a function of the difficulty of equalizing service levels across a newly consolidated jurisdiction where pre-existing service inequities persist. The merger produced genuine efficiencies in core city functions but did not automatically solve the service-level disparity problem.

**Louisville-Jefferson County (2003).** Louisville's merger with Jefferson County is the most recent and most studied major consolidation. The Abell Foundation's 10-year retrospective found: the size of the government workforce did not increase; property tax rates for both former city and county residents declined slightly; and the city jumped from 65th to 18th largest in the U.S. by 2010 Census measures, improving its competitive position for business attraction and convention tourism. The Louisville merger is widely considered the most clearly successful of the three — largely because it was enacted through a democratic referendum after years of public deliberation, not legislatively imposed.

**What the literature says about consolidation generally.** The Government Finance Research Center at the University of Illinois Chicago and the Government Finance Officers Association have both published analyses noting that consolidation's fiscal benefits are frequently overstated in political campaigns for merger and understated retrospectively once the administrative transition costs are accounted for. The research consensus is approximately: consolidation reduces administrative duplication at moderate cost, but rarely produces dramatic per-capita fiscal savings, and the political costs — both of achieving consolidation and of managing the transition — are substantial. The hidden downside documented in Governing magazine's analysis is that consolidated governments can lose local accountability: the city council member who was accessible to a neighborhood becomes a metro-wide representative covering a much larger constituency, reducing the practical responsiveness that made local government valuable in the first place.

Sources: [40 Years After Unigov: Indianapolis and Marion County's Experience with Consolidated Government — Abell Foundation](https://abell.org/publication/40-years-after-unigov/); [A 10-Year Perspective of the Merger of Louisville and Jefferson County — Abell Foundation](https://abell.org/publication/a-10-year-perspective-of-the-merger-of-louisville-and-jefferson-county-ky/); [The Hidden Downsides of City-County Mergers — Governing](https://www.governing.com/urban/the-hidden-downsides-of-city-county-mergers); [In 1970, Indianapolis and Marion County Consolidated. Here's What St. Louis Can Learn — St. Louis Magazine](https://www.stlmag.com/news/politics/indianapolis-unigov-merger-what-st-louis-can-learn/)

## Section 4: Interstate Compact Anatomy — Successes, Failures, and Design Lessons

### 4.1 The Nurse Licensure Compact — A Success Story with Lessons

The Nurse Licensure Compact (NLC) is the strongest contemporary evidence that interstate compacts can achieve broad adoption when the coordination benefits are clear and the compact is well-designed for political sustainability.

**Origins.** The National Council of State Boards of Nursing (NCSBN) proposed the mutual recognition licensure model in 1997. The first states joined in 2000. The original compact grew steadily but plateaus appeared as concerns emerged from nursing associations about regulatory race-to-the-bottom dynamics — states with lower licensure standards joining and allowing their nurses to practice in states with stronger standards.

**The redesign.** Between 2014 and 2015, the NCSBN revised the framework — producing the Enhanced Nurse Licensure Compact (eNLC), which implemented 11 uniform licensure requirements including mandatory federal criminal background checks and standardized disciplinary provisions. The redesign directly addressed the race-to-the-bottom concern: the minimum standards floor was embedded in the compact architecture itself, not left to individual state discretion. As of January 2026, 40 states are full eNLC members — the largest interstate professional licensing compact in American history.

**Why it succeeded.** The NLC success factors identified by the Council of State Governments' National Center for Interstate Compacts: (1) a clear coordination benefit that individual states cannot achieve alone — nurses crossing state lines during emergencies and disasters; (2) a uniform minimum floor that addressed the quality-dilution concern rather than leaving it unresolved; (3) a central administrative body (the NCSBN) that managed implementation; and (4) decades of patient development before mass adoption, rather than rushed enactment. The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated adoption dramatically — states that had not joined discovered the NLC's emergency deployment value in 2020.

Sources: [Nurse Licensure Compact — National Center for Interstate Compacts, CSG](https://compacts.csg.org/compact/nurse-licensure-compact/); [Nurse Licensure Compact — NCSBN](https://www.ncsbn.org/compacts.page); [The National Center for Interstate Compacts: A Network Helping States Solve Big Problems — Council of State Governments](https://www.csg.org/2025/12/03/the-national-center-for-interstate-compacts-a-network-helping-states-solve-big-problems/)

### 4.2 The Multistate Tax Compact — A Cautionary Tale of Corporate Capture

The Multistate Tax Compact (MTC) was drafted in 1966 and became effective in 1967, with the explicit goal of establishing uniform rules for state taxation of multistate businesses — preventing double taxation while preserving state revenue. It is a cautionary case of what happens when a compact lacks binding enforcement and when corporate interests successfully lobby states to defect.

**The constitutional challenge.** Large multistate corporations and the Committee on State Taxation (COST) spent years challenging the MTC's constitutionality, arguing it required congressional consent under the Compact Clause. The Supreme Court upheld the MTC's validity as a voluntary association in United States Steel Corp. v. Multistate Tax Commission (1978), finding it did not increase state power at federal expense. The Court's key distinction: the MTC was permissible because it imposed no binding obligation on states.

**The defection problem.** That distinction — voluntary, not binding — proved to be the MTC's structural weakness. Because the compact imposed no penalty for defection, states could join as "associate members" (receiving technical assistance without being bound by apportionment formulas) or simply withdraw. California, Minnesota, Oregon, and other states withdrew from or refused to adopt the compact's binding apportionment provisions. As of today, only 15 states and the District of Columbia are full Compact Members; most of the remaining states are associate or project members with no binding obligation. The original goal of uniform multistate tax apportionment has not been achieved.

**The lesson.** The MTC demonstrates that compacts without binding enforcement mechanisms, minimum participation thresholds, and penalties for defection will tend toward lowest-common-denominator participation or collapse. The NLC's success partially reflects its opposite design: the eNLC requires states to meet minimum standards to join, and there is no "associate membership" that allows free-riding.

Multistate Tax Comm'n — Justia](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/434/452/); [MTC History — Multistate Tax Commission](https://www.mtc.gov/the-commission/mtc-history/)

### 4.3 The National Popular Vote Interstate Compact — Current Legal Position

The National Popular Vote Interstate Compact (NPVIC) is the proposal's central example of how compacts can achieve structural reform. As of April 2026:

**Current status.** The compact has been adopted by 17 states and the District of Columbia, representing 209 electoral votes — 77% of the 270 needed to trigger activation. Maine was the most recent to join, enacting legislation in June 2024. No Republican governor has signed NPVIC legislation, making this a partisan compact — a structural vulnerability that distinguishes it from the NLC (which has bipartisan membership) and is likely to generate constitutional challenges on grounds of partisan manipulation of federal elections.

**States that have not joined but where one chamber has passed legislation.** Arizona, Arkansas, Michigan, North Carolina, and Oklahoma. Nevada's legislature passed a resolution in 2023 but failed the required second passage to amend the state constitution in 2025.

**Constitutional challenges.** Courts have consistently held that challenges to the NPVIC are not ripe until the compact takes effect (i.e., until states totaling 270 electoral votes enact it). Multiple legal scholars have identified the key constitutional questions: (1) whether the compact requires congressional consent under the Compact Clause; (2) whether states have plenary authority to award electoral votes to any basis they choose under Article II, Section 1; and (3) whether the compact violates the Equal Protection Clause by effectively making some voters' votes (in large-population states) more determinate. Legal opinion is genuinely divided; the Supreme Court has not addressed the question.

### 4.4 The Colorado River Compact — A Cautionary Tale for Climate Compacts

The Colorado River Compact (1922) is the longest-running major interstate water compact in American history — and its current failure is the most important design lesson for the interstate compact framework proposed in 9e.

**The original flaw: overallocation based on wrong hydrology.** The 1922 compact allocated 16.4 million acre-feet of water annually between seven states. This figure was based on flow measurements taken during an unusually wet period in the early 20th century. Hydrologists now estimate the long-term average annual flow of the Colorado River at approximately 13 million acre-feet — 20% below the allocated total, meaning the compact was overallocated from its inception. The 2020s have accelerated the crisis: climate change has reduced average flows further, and in 2022 Lake Powell reached its lowest level ever recorded, approximately 20% of total capacity.

**The design failure.** The compact contained no mechanism for renegotiation in response to changed conditions. Water rights were allocated under a "prior appropriation" doctrine that creates vested legal rights and strong political resistance to any reduction. The compact's three-words dispute — the phrase "in perpetuity" in the upper basin states' allocation — has made negotiations in 2024 politically toxic. Upper basin states (Colorado, Utah, Wyoming, New Mexico) resist any cuts, arguing that without upstream reservoirs, they have already borne disproportionate drought impacts. Lower basin states (California, Arizona, Nevada) committed to reduce use by 1.25 million acre-feet per year through 2026 but contested who bears further cuts.

**The design lesson for 9e.** Compacts that are designed without adaptive management mechanisms — formal processes for renegotiating terms as conditions change — will eventually fail when the conditions that justified the original terms no longer obtain. The RGGI compact, by contrast, has a built-in Program Review mechanism that allows states to adjust the emissions cap and other program parameters at regular intervals. Any compact addressing water, climate, or other resource allocation should include mandatory adaptive review, explicit renegotiation triggers, and dispute resolution mechanisms with teeth.

Sources: [Colorado River Compact: Rethinking Southwest Water Management Amid a Changing Climate — LA Waterkeeper](https://www.lawaterkeeper.org/our-stories/colorado-river-compact-rethinking-southwest-water-management-amid-a-changing-climate/); [Fixing the Flawed Colorado River Compact — Eos.org / American Geophysical Union](https://eos.org/features/fixing-the-flawed-colorado-river-compact); [Climate Crisis Is Straining the Colorado River's Complex Policy Architecture — Choices Magazine](https://www.choicesmagazine.org/choices-magazine/theme-articles/colorado-river-basin/climate-crisis-is-straining-the-colorado-rivers-complex-policy-architecture); [As a Deadline Approaches, Colorado River States Are Still Far Apart on Water Sharing — NPR](https://www.npr.org/2024/03/08/1237056819/as-a-deadline-approaches-colorado-river-states-are-still-far-apart-on-water-shar)

## Section 5: International Benchmarks in Granular Detail

### 5.1 Switzerland — Fiscal Equalization and Rights Enforcement in Practice

The proposal identifies Switzerland as the primary model for 9a's subsidiarity-with-rights-floor architecture. The specifics are important for understanding what "enforcement" actually means in practice.

**The Finanzausgleich system.** Switzerland's national fiscal equalization system — the Finanzausgleich — underwent a major reform that came into effect in 2008 and was further adjusted in 2020. The system has two components: (1) resource equalization (Ressourcenausgleich), which transfers funds from resource-rich to resource-poor cantons based on their "resource potential" (a standardized measure of taxable capacity); and (2) needs-based equalization (Lastenausgleich), which compensates cantons that face extraordinary costs due to their geographic or demographic characteristics — mountainous terrain, urban centers serving as regional hubs, etc. The critical floor: after equalization, every canton must have fiscal resources at least equal to 85% of the national cantonal average. The Swiss Federal Administration publishes annual reports on equalization flows; as of 2020, the net transfers between cantons totaled approximately CHF 5.2 billion annually, with Uri, Valais, and Jura among the main recipient cantons and Zug, Zurich, and Geneva among the main contributing cantons.

**Federal Supreme Court enforcement of cantonal rights floors.** The Federal Supreme Court of Switzerland (Bundesgericht) reviews cantonal legislation for conformity with the Federal Constitution's rights provisions. The Court has established a substantial body of jurisprudence holding that cantonal tax laws, residency requirements, and social assistance rules must conform to the constitutional principles of equality, prohibition of arbitrariness, and basic rights provisions (Articles 7-36 of the 1999 Federal Constitution). Unlike the U.S. Supreme Court, the Swiss Federal Supreme Court can directly review and annul cantonal legislation — there is no equivalent of the political question doctrine that removes federal challenges to state action from judicial review. The Court has struck cantonal laws on cantonal direct democracy procedures (finding certain supermajority requirements for naturalization votes unconstitutional), cantonal tax laws that violated the constitutional prohibition on double taxation between cantons, and cantonal residency requirements that violated free movement rights.

Sources: [National Fiscal Equalization — Swiss Federal Finance Administration](https://www.efv.admin.ch/efv/en/home/themen/finanzausgleich/aktuell.html); [The Swiss System of Fiscal Equalization — Forum of Federations](https://www.forumfed.org/pubs/Interview_Christian_Wanner_final.pdf); [Federal Supreme Court of Switzerland — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_Supreme_Court_of_Switzerland); [Switzerland's Federalism Relies Upon Fiscal Competition, But the Financially Weakest Are Not Left Alone — Avenir Suisse](https://www.avenir-suisse.ch/en/switzerlands-federalism-relies-upon-fiscal-competition-financially-weakest-not-left-alone/)

### 5.2 Germany — Horizontal Equalization and Constitutional Court Enforcement

Germany's Länderfinanzausgleich (inter-state fiscal equalization) is the world's most developed horizontal equalization system among federal states — and its constitutional history contains a specific, instructive episode in which wealthy states used the Federal Constitutional Court to challenge the system.

**The architecture.** Article 107 of the Basic Law establishes the constitutional requirement of "reasonable equalization of the disparate financial capacities of the Länder." The system works in two stages: vertical transfers from the federal government to the financially weaker Länder (Bundesergänzungszuweisungen), and horizontal transfers directly between Länder (the Länderfinanzausgleich proper). The system has historically aimed to bring all Länder to at least 95% of the national average fiscal capacity after equalization — a substantially higher floor than Switzerland's 85%.

**The wealthy-state challenge.** Bavaria and Hesse — Germany's two wealthiest Länder — filed suits at the Federal Constitutional Court in 1999 arguing that the equalization burden placed on them was constitutionally excessive. The Court's ruling (BVerfGE 101, 158, 1999) upheld the equalization system's constitutional basis but found that some aspects of the formula exceeded reasonable limits and imposed binding requirements on the legislature to reform the system. Subsequent litigation by Baden-Württemberg, Bavaria, and Hesse in 2013 challenged the reformed system again. The Court's jurisprudence in these cases established two competing constitutional principles: horizontal solidarity (the duty of wealthy states to contribute to the fiscal capacity of poorer ones) and the federalist competition principle (that equalization should not be so complete as to eliminate all incentives for fiscal efficiency and policy innovation). The Court has never struck the equalization system, but has used these cases to impose proportionality limits on the transfer burden.

**Article 28 — the local democracy guarantee.** Article 28 of the Basic Law requires that all state (Länder) constitutions conform to "principles of a republican, democratic, and social state governed by the rule of law," and further guarantees that municipalities "shall be guaranteed the right to regulate all local affairs on their own responsibility, within the limits prescribed by the laws." This is a constitutional guarantee of local self-governance — the closest German equivalent to a home rule principle. The Federal Constitutional Court has enforced Article 28 primarily by reviewing Länder laws that eliminated municipal fiscal autonomy or transferred municipal functions to state agencies without adequate compensation, finding that some such transfers violated the municipal self-governance guarantee.

Sources: [Equalization Payments in Germany — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equalization_payments_in_germany); [Territorial Justice in Unified Germany: Financial Equalization, the Länder and the Federal Constitutional Court — ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0143622897000258); [Consensus Democracy and Interjurisdictional Fiscal Solidarity in Germany — IMF](https://www.imf.org/external/pubs/ft/seminar/2000/fiscal/spahn.pdf)

### 5.3 Spain's Asymmetric Federalism — Success Modes and Breaking Points

Spain is the most instructive international case for the American federalism reform discussion because it demonstrates both the promise of asymmetric accommodation and the specific conditions under which it fails. The proposal mentions Spain but does not explore its trajectory.

**The initial success (1978–2006).** Spain's 1978 Constitution established the "State of Autonomies" — a system of asymmetric decentralization that allowed different regions to assume different levels of self-governance, with the historical nationalities (Catalonia, the Basque Country, and Galicia) receiving greater autonomy than other regions. The Basque Country and Navarra retained their pre-constitutional fiscal arrangements (the "Concierto Económico"), allowing them to collect their own taxes and transfer a negotiated amount to the central government — the most asymmetric arrangement in any democratic federation. By the 1990s and early 2000s, the autonomy system had successfully accommodated substantial regional diversity, and secessionist sentiment in Catalonia, while always present, was manageable.

**The 2010 Constitutional Court ruling and the breakdown.** In 2006, the Catalan parliament approved a new Statute of Autonomy that would have formalized Catalonia's status as a "nation" (a symbolic designation) and expanded its fiscal autonomy. After the statute was significantly amended by the Spanish parliament (over Catalan objections), and after the Constitutional Court struck key provisions in 2010, Catalan independence sentiment surged from approximately 15% of the Catalan population in 2006 to over 40% by 2012 and close to 50% by 2017. Political scientists studying the case (including Miquel Caminal, Montserrat Guibernau, and the researchers in the Publius journal symposium on Catalan independence) identify the Court's 2010 ruling as the proximate trigger — it delegitimized the reform path and left independence as the only viable option for Catalan nationalists who wanted fiscal autonomy.

**The 2017 independence referendum.** The Catalan government organized a unilateral independence referendum on October 1, 2017, over the central government's explicit legal objection. The Spanish government sent police to prevent voting; images of police violence against voters were broadcast globally. The turnout was approximately 43% (suppressed by police action), with 90% voting for independence. The Catalan government declared independence on October 27, 2017; the Spanish government invoked Article 155 of the Constitution (direct federal administration of an autonomous community) and removed the Catalan government. The episode ended in criminal prosecutions of Catalan leaders, political deadlock, and no resolution of the underlying constitutional dispute.

**The lesson for 9e.** Spain's experience demonstrates that asymmetric federalism works when the center and peripheral regions share a commitment to accommodation and when constitutional review is perceived as legitimate by both sides. When central governments use constitutional review to block accommodation rather than to enforce a rights floor, and when the constitutional amendment mechanism is too rigid to allow renegotiation, the asymmetric system can produce radicalization rather than stability. The design lesson is that adaptive federalism — with regular renegotiation processes, constitutional amendment pathways that do not require supermajorities that are impossible to achieve — is more durable than a static autonomy architecture.

Sources: [Catalonia and Spain's Constitutional Crisis: Time for a Federal Solution? — 50 Shades of Federalism](http://50shadesoffederalism.com/case-studies/catalonia-and-spains-constitutional-crisis-time-for-a-federal-solution/); [Constitutional Conflict and Asymmetric Decentralization: The Crisis of the Catalan Statute in Spain — ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/403095433_CONSTITUTIONAL_CONFLICT_AND_ASYMMETRIC_DECENTRALIZATION_THE_CRISIS_OF_THE_CATALAN_STATUTE_IN_SPAIN); [Federalism versus Nationalism: The Case of Catalonia — The Federalist Debate](https://www.federalist-debate.org/archive/year-xxxi-number-1-march-2018/borderless-debate/federalism-versus-nationalism-the-case-of-catalonia)

### 5.4 Canada — The Notwithstanding Clause and Asymmetric Federalism in Practice

Canada's Charter of Rights and Freedoms (1982) established a rights floor over provincial legislation — but with a critical exception mechanism that the proposal notes but does not detail.

**How the notwithstanding clause works.** Section 33 of the Charter allows Parliament or provincial legislatures to override sections 2 (fundamental freedoms) and 7-15 (legal rights and equality rights) by expressly declaring that legislation applies "notwithstanding" the Charter. The override is valid for five years and can be renewed. Critically, Section 33 cannot override the democratic rights provisions (sections 3-5, guaranteeing the right to vote and to regular elections) or mobility rights (section 6) — the core of what is actually necessary to maintain democratic self-government cannot be suspended.

**How it has been used.** The clause was invoked by Quebec under a blanket declaration from 1982 to 1985, applying to every law passed during that period. Quebec invoked it again in 1988 for a French-only sign law (after the Supreme Court found the law violated freedom of expression), in 2019 for Bill 21 (banning public-sector employees from wearing religious symbols), and in 2022 to strengthen French language requirements. Ontario used it in 2018 under Doug Ford to enact a law reducing Toronto's city council from 47 to 25 seats mid-election — a law the court had already found unconstitutional as a violation of freedom of expression. As of 2025, six provinces publicly defended the notwithstanding clause against proposed federal restrictions, and the Supreme Court of Canada announced it would hear a case (Hak et al v. Attorney General of Québec) in 2026 to address whether courts can provide "declaratory relief" when the clause is invoked.

**Quebec's asymmetric arrangements — the genuine accomplishments.** Canada's constitution accommodates Quebec's distinct position through several mechanisms that represent genuine asymmetric federalism: Quebec administers its own pension plan (the Quebec Pension Plan, as distinct from the Canada Pension Plan); Quebec controls its own immigration selection for economic immigrants (the Quebec Experience Program); and Quebec administers its own income tax collection separately from the Canada Revenue Agency — the only province to do so. These arrangements represent real fiscal and administrative autonomy on matters Quebec identified as central to its distinct society, negotiated within the federal constitutional framework rather than requiring constitutional amendment.

**The federal-provincial fiscal arrangements.** Canada's fiscal federalism includes a constitutionalized equalization formula (Section 36 of the Constitution Act, 1982) that commits the federal government to making equalization payments to ensure provinces have sufficient revenues "to provide reasonably comparable levels of public services at reasonably comparable levels of taxation." As of fiscal year 2024-25, the federal government's equalization payments totaled approximately CAD $24.9 billion, with Quebec receiving the largest share (approximately CAD $14 billion) despite being Canada's second-largest province by GDP — a function of the formula's treatment of resource revenues and per-capita fiscal capacity.

Sources: [Section 33 of the Canadian Charter — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Section_33_of_the_Canadian_Charter_of_Rights_and_Freedoms); [Charterpedia — Section 33 Notwithstanding Clause — Department of Justice Canada](https://www.justice.gc.ca/eng/csj-sjc/rfc-dlc/ccrf-ccdl/check/art33.html); [Notwithstanding Clause — CCLA](https://ccla.org/major-cases-and-reports/notwithstanding-clause/); [No Charter Without Section 33: How Six Provinces Are Defending the Notwithstanding Clause — The Hub](https://thehub.ca/2025/09/23/no-charter-without-section-33-how-six-provinces-are-defending-the-notwithstanding-clause/)

## Section 6: Counterarguments and Rebuttals

### 6.1 "Federal Rights Floors Violate State Sovereignty" — The Anti-Commandeering Doctrine

**The objection.** The Supreme Court's anti-commandeering doctrine — established in New York v. United States (1992) and expanded in Printz v. United States (1997) — prohibits Congress from conscripting state legislative or executive officials to administer federal regulatory programs. A federal rights floor that required states to enact specific minimum wage laws, voting access guarantees, or environmental standards would, the argument goes, compel state legislative action and violate the Tenth Amendment's reservation of powers to the states.

**What the doctrine actually forbids.** New York v. United States struck a provision of the Low-Level Radioactive Waste Policy Amendments Act that gave states a choice between enacting a federally prescribed regulatory program or having their state liable for private parties' disposal costs. Justice O'Connor held that Congress could not "commandeer the legislative processes of the States by directly compelling them to enact and enforce a federal regulatory program." The doctrine, properly understood, forbids Congress from compelling states to legislate or to use their own officials to administer federal law. It does not forbid Congress from: (1) attaching conditions to federal spending (the Spending Clause model — used in the Voting Rights Act's original preclearance system, where federal funding was tied to compliance); (2) preempting state law in areas where Congress has authority (the Supremacy Clause model — used in civil rights, environmental, and labor law); or (3) creating private rights of action that individuals can enforce directly against state actors under Section 5 of the Fourteenth Amendment.

**NFIB v. Sebelius's complication.** The ACA Medicaid expansion case (2012) introduced a limit on the Spending Clause model: the Court held that threatening to withhold all existing Medicaid funding unless states accepted the expansion was unconstitutionally coercive — the equivalent of "a gun to the head." Chief Justice Roberts held that conditions on new federal funds are constitutional; conditions that retroactively threatened existing funds cross the line into coercion. The rights floor proposal must be structured to avoid this: new federal minimum standards attached to new federal funds (the Race to the Top model) are constitutional; conditioning existing block grants on compliance with new minimum standards is constitutionally vulnerable.

**The Fourteenth Amendment's independent authority.** The most legally secure pathway for a federal rights floor is through Section 5 of the Fourteenth Amendment — Congress's explicit power to enforce the Equal Protection and Due Process clauses "by appropriate legislation." The original Voting Rights Act preclearance mechanism was upheld under Section 5 authority in South Carolina v. Katzenbach (1966). A new coverage formula based on contemporary discrimination evidence, enforcing a minimum voting access standard under Section 5, would be on firmer constitutional ground than the Court's Shelby County decision suggested — provided Congress developed a comprehensive record of current state-level discrimination as the evidentiary foundation.

### 6.2 "State Preemption of Local Ordinances Is Just Dillon's Rule in Action"

**The objection.** Municipalities are "creatures of the state" under established constitutional law (Hunter v. Pittsburgh, 1907). States that preempt local ordinances are exercising the sovereign authority they have always had over their subdivisions. Cities do not have inherent rights; they have only the powers states grant them. The Birmingham minimum wage case was not wrongly decided — it correctly reflects the constitutional relationship between states and cities.

**What Dillon's Rule actually means and what it does not mean.** Judge John Forrest Dillon articulated the rule in his 1868 treatise: municipalities have only the powers explicitly granted by the state legislature, those necessarily implied by those granted powers, and those essential to the municipality's declared purposes. The rule is a default rule of statutory construction, not an immutable constitutional command. As of 2025, 39 states apply Dillon's Rule to at least some local governments — but 31 states also have Home Rule provisions in their state constitutions that grant municipalities broader authority, partially inverting Dillon's presumption.

**The democratic accountability problem with Dillon's Rule preemption.** Even if Dillon's Rule is legally defensible as a matter of state constitutional law, using it to override local democratic decisions raises a distinct democratic legitimacy question: the Birmingham City Council that was preempted had 9 seats; it voted 9-0 for the minimum wage ordinance. The state legislature that overrode it had members who did not represent Birmingham's voters and who had no accountability to the communities whose democratic decision they voided. Dillon's Rule describes what states can do legally; it does not address whether doing so is compatible with the democratic principle that affected communities should govern themselves on matters affecting primarily themselves.

**The Home Rule alternative and its limits.** Home Rule provisions — which grant cities authority to legislate on matters of "local concern" without state authorization — exist in most states but have been progressively narrowed by courts that find state legislatures have "occupied the field" on a matter even absent explicit preemption language. A 2024 Harvard Law Review note ("Home Rule Reinforcement: Constitutional Local Autonomy Guarantees") documents the gap between the promise of Home Rule and its practice — courts applying Home Rule provisions have consistently resolved ambiguous "local vs. state" questions in favor of state authority, particularly in conservative jurisdictions that have structural political incentives to do so.

Sources: [Dillon's Rule, Home Rule, and Preemption — Public Health Law Center](https://www.publichealthlawcenter.org/sites/default/files/resources/Dillons-Rule-Home-Rule-Preemption.pdf); [Dillon's Rule — Brookings Institution](https://www.brookings.edu/wp-content/uploads/2016/06/dillonsrule.pdf); [Home Rule Reinforcement: Constitutional Local Autonomy Guarantees — Harvard Law Review](https://harvardlawreview.org/print/vol-139/home-rule-reinforcement-constitutional-local-autonomy-guarantees/); [State Preemption and the Fracturing of America — Harvard ALI Social Impact Review](https://www.sir.advancedleadership.harvard.edu/articles/state-preemption-and-the-fracturing-of-america)

### 6.3 "Interstate Compacts Bind Future Legislatures Undemocratically"

**The objection.** Interstate compacts, once enacted, bind participating states even if subsequent legislatures would prefer different policies. A compact that creates a carbon market, sets a minimum wage floor, or establishes water allocation rules cannot be exited unilaterally — or if it can, exit creates significant costs for the remaining members. This is an anti-democratic entrenchment of policy preferences from one legislative generation over subsequent ones.

**The honest response.** The entrenchment objection is real and not dismissible. The question is whether it applies with different force than other forms of entrenchment. All long-term governmental commitments constrain future legislatures to some degree: bond covenants, long-term contracts, pension obligations, and constitutional provisions all bind successors. The policy question is whether the coordination benefits of a compact — achieving outcomes that no individual state could achieve alone — justify the entrenchment cost.

**What the research shows about compact durability and adaptability.** The Council of State Governments' National Center for Interstate Compacts documents that compacts with built-in adaptive mechanisms — periodic review, renegotiation processes, procedures for amendment — are both more durable and more democratic than compacts written as permanent, static agreements. The RGGI compact has undergone multiple Program Reviews that have adjusted the cap, the price floor, and other parameters in response to changing conditions. The NLC's redesign from the original compact to the eNLC demonstrates that compacts can be substantially reformed without dissolution. The Colorado River Compact's failure is partly attributable to its lack of any such adaptive mechanism.

**The exit issue.** The standard objection assumes compacts cannot be exited. In practice, most interstate compacts include withdrawal provisions — though often with advance notice requirements (typically 1-3 years) that prevent precipitous exit. This is functionally similar to the advance notice requirements in treaties and trade agreements, which are also binding on successor governments. The democratic accountability question is therefore not "can this be undone" (it can, with process) but "is the process of undoing appropriate to the importance of the commitment."

### 6.4 "Fragmentation Enables Experimentation — Consolidation Loses Local Accountability"

**The honest objection.** This is the strongest counterargument to the consolidation-favoring strand of the proposal, and it deserves a direct and honest response. Local government fragmentation does enable policy experimentation: communities with distinct preferences can choose distinct service levels, tax rates, and regulatory environments. A small town that wants a volunteer fire department and no zoning law has the governance structure to make that choice. Consolidation eliminates this optionality. Moreover, small-scale government has genuine accountability advantages — a city council member in a 10,000-person town is genuinely accessible to constituents in a way that a metro-wide representative covering 500,000 residents is not.

**What the evidence actually supports — and what it does not.** The proposal does not advocate for consolidating all 90,000 local government units into regional mega-governments. What the evidence supports is more limited: (1) consolidating overlapping special districts with duplicate administrative overhead in dense metropolitan areas — the Illinois example — can reduce fiscal costs without eliminating community-level governance; (2) regional coordination bodies with specific functional mandates (transit, water, climate) can address coordination gaps without eliminating local governments; (3) the fragmentation problem is most acute for *service delivery* in metropolitan areas where scale economies are large — not for *democratic representation* in small communities where local accountability is genuine.

**The genuine tension.** The proposal's framing — 90,000 units of local government as simply "fragmentation" — understates the diversity of governance scales and functions involved. A metropolitan transit authority that covers a 10-county region has very different accountability dynamics than a rural school district covering a 200-person community. The reform pathways in 9c (municipal reform networks) and 9e (interstate compact frameworks) are both more targeted and more defensible than a general consolidation argument, and the evidence deepening in this document should be understood as supporting those targeted interventions rather than wholesale consolidation.
