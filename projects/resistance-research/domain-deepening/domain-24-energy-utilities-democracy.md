# Domain 24: Energy Access, Utility Democracy, and Regulatory Capture

*Research document for democratic-renewal-proposal.md Part I (Diagnostic Framework). April 2026.*

---

## The Core Problem

Energy access is infrastructure for democratic participation in the same way that clean water, internet access, or a functioning road network is. A household without heat in winter or cooling in summer — or one facing the financial crisis of disconnection — is a household with reduced capacity for civic engagement, political organizing, or the kind of sustained action that democratic resistance requires. The U.S. energy system has two structural failures operating simultaneously: (1) regulatory capture of the state utility commissions and FERC that are supposed to protect public interest in energy markets, and (2) energy poverty at a scale that constitutes a hidden tax on low-income and minority households. These failures are not engineering problems; they are governance and power distribution problems.

Domain 12 of the existing proposal addresses broadband and grid modernization. It notes that "grid reliability is not a climate issue alone — it is a national security and public safety issue" and proposes grid modernization investment. But it does not address the governance architecture that produces the grid's failures: the regulatory capture of state public utility commissions, the institutional design of FERC, the energy poverty crisis as a democratic participation problem, or the structural conflict between investor-owned utility profit maximization and public interest energy access. This domain fills that gap.

## 1. The Scale of Energy Poverty: 2024-2026 Data

### 1.1 Shutoffs as Public Policy Failure

In 2024, U.S. utilities disconnected residential electric service 13.5 million times and disconnected gas service 1.6 million times for unpaid bills. In the same year, utilities issued approximately 94.9 million final disconnect notices — warning letters that create fear of service loss even when disconnection does not follow. (U.S. Energy Information Administration, 2024 data; Energy and Policy Institute, April 2026)

These numbers represent a policy failure of scale. The same investor-owned utilities that disconnected service 13.5 million times in 2024 earned over $52 billion in profits that year — a $3.5 billion increase from the prior year. This is not an underfunded sector that cannot afford to serve low-income customers. It is a sector that is extracting record profits from captive customers while disconnecting the most vulnerable at record rates.

The geographic and racial distribution of disconnections is not random:

- **Southern concentration**: The South accounts for approximately 71% of electricity disconnections nationally, driven by a combination of weaker consumer protection laws, higher summer cooling loads (making energy essential for survival, not comfort), and lower median incomes. Texas, Florida, Oklahoma, and Georgia lead in shutoff volumes.
- **Florida Power and Light**: FPL alone disconnected nearly 1.3 million homes in 2024 — the same year state regulators approved what was then the largest utility rate increase in Florida's history. This is the classic regulatory capture dynamic: the regulated entity captures the regulator, extracts rate increases, and uses disconnections to enforce collection rather than making cost-effective affordability investments.
- **Racial disparities**: Available state-level data consistently shows that Black and Latino households face disconnection at 2-4 times the rate of white households with comparable incomes, controlling for payment capacity. The Center for Biological Diversity analysis of 2024 EIA data found that counties with higher Black population shares had significantly higher per-household shutoff rates even after controlling for income levels. (CBD, April 2026)
- **Data suppression**: Twenty-two states do not require utilities to report disconnection data to state regulators at all — meaning the 13.5 million figure is an undercount. States without reporting requirements are predominantly the same states with the weakest consumer protections and the highest documented disconnection rates.

### 1.2 The Cost Burden and Its Democratic Consequences

Low-income households spend an average of 8.6% of their annual income on energy — nearly triple the 3% threshold that energy economists define as the affordability standard. By comparison, higher-income households spend approximately 2% of income on energy. This is a regressive allocation that functions as an inverted progressive tax: the less income you have, the higher your effective energy tax rate.

The 2025 electricity bill increase compounds this. Average monthly residential electricity bills rose from approximately $121 in 2021 to $156 in 2025 — a 29% increase in four years. For a household spending 8.6% of income on energy, this increase represents a significant share of any income gains from a period of wage growth, effectively reversing the material gains of low-wage workers in the 2021-2024 labor market.

An estimated 21.5 million U.S. households — roughly 1 in 6 — are currently behind on their energy bills. (EIA, 2025; RMI Affordability Hub)

**The democratic consequence** is direct and measurable. Research on voter participation consistently finds that material precarity — housing instability, food insecurity, energy disconnection — reduces civic participation. A household that spends three hours on the phone each month managing utility payment arrangements is a household with three fewer hours for political engagement. A household whose electricity was shut off in July does not have the bandwidth to attend a public hearing on redistricting. Energy poverty is not merely a poverty issue; it is a democratic participation issue that operates through the same mechanism as the other material deprivations documented in Domain 18 (Social Safety Net) and Domain 13 (Housing).

### 1.3 LIHEAP Under Attack

The Low Income Home Energy Assistance Program (LIHEAP) is the primary federal tool for addressing energy poverty. In April 2025, the Trump administration fired the entire LIHEAP program staff at HHS — the administrators responsible for distributing approximately $4 billion per year to states for energy assistance. While Congress has continued to fund the program ($4.13 billion for FY2026, rejecting the administration's proposal to eliminate it entirely), the dismissal of the federal program staff has left states unable to access guidance, technical assistance, or federal support for program administration. (NPR, May 2025; PBS NewsHour, 2025; Roll Call, April 2026)

The administration has proposed eliminating LIHEAP in every one of its budget submissions — this is its sixth such proposal. The program serves approximately 6 million low-income households annually, with an average benefit of approximately $500 per household. The elimination would be the largest single reduction in energy assistance in American history, eliminating the primary mechanism by which low-income households avoid the disconnection cycle.

The LIHEAP attack is consistent with the broader pattern of social safety net dismantling documented in Domain 18 — but it is specific to energy access in a way that deserves explicit treatment because energy shutoffs, unlike food insecurity or housing instability, carry direct mortality risks in extreme heat and cold.

## 2. Regulatory Capture: How Utilities Own Their Regulators

### 2.1 The Structure of State Utility Regulation

Investor-owned utilities are natural monopolies — a customer cannot choose to buy electricity from a different provider. The policy response to natural monopoly is rate-of-return regulation: a state public utility commission sets the prices the utility can charge based on its costs plus a regulated rate of return on invested capital. This system depends entirely on the regulator being genuinely independent of the regulated entity. In most states, it is not.

The mechanism of capture is documented and structural:

**Revolving door**: Former utility executives and lobbyists serve on public utility commissions. Former commissioners join utility companies and law firms representing utilities. The 2024 Energy and Policy Institute analysis found that approximately 30% of active state utility commissioners had previously worked for a regulated utility or utility trade association.

**Campaign finance**: State utility commissioners are elected in 12 states — and in those states, utilities are among the largest contributors to commissioner campaigns. In states with appointed commissioners, utilities lobby the governors and legislators who make appointments. Georgia Power (a Southern Company subsidiary) spent $7.5 million in a single legislative session on lobbying and campaign contributions to Georgia state elected officials, who then appointed and confirmed a Public Service Commission majority that approved a $2.7 billion cost overrun on the Vogtle nuclear plant — passed entirely to ratepayers.

**"Regulatory economics"**: The utility regulatory model creates a structural incentive for over-investment. Utilities earn their regulated rate of return on rate base — the value of their physical assets. This means a utility earns more by spending more on infrastructure, whether or not that spending is cost-effective. The $2.5 billion Federal Reserve renovation that became a pretext for investigating Powell is small compared to the systematic capital cost inflation that utility rate-of-return regulation produces. The Georgia Vogtle case is the clearest example: a nuclear plant originally budgeted at $14 billion was ultimately completed at $35 billion — every dollar of overrun becoming a permanent addition to the rate base on which Georgia Power earns its regulated return.

**Industry-drafted legislation**: ALEC (the American Legislative Exchange Council), heavily funded by utility industry members including Duke Energy and Southern Company, has drafted model state legislation that preempts local energy regulation, restricts municipal broadband (documented in Domain 12), bans community solar programs that compete with utility monopolies, and limits net metering for rooftop solar — all policies that protect monopoly utility revenue at the expense of consumer choice and clean energy transition. At least 14 states have passed ALEC-model utility protection legislation.

### 2.2 FERC: Federal Regulatory Capture

The Federal Energy Regulatory Commission, which regulates wholesale electricity and natural gas markets, faces its own capture dynamics at the federal level:

**AI and data center power demand as political priority**: The Trump administration has directed FERC to prioritize the power needs of AI data centers — which are major Republican donor industries — over consumer affordability concerns. FERC is setting a June 2026 deadline to rewrite large-load interconnection rules specifically to accommodate data center demands, while wholesale electricity prices are projected to rise through 2026 due to supply-demand imbalances driven partly by AI load growth. (Utility Dive, 2026)

**Deregulation pressure**: FERC has finalized a "Sunset Rule" imposing expiration dates on regulations, part of a broader deregulation push. Independent analysts note that rate-of-return utility regulation — which FERC oversees at the federal level for interstate transmission — is itself a form of guaranteed profit extraction, and its "deregulation" often means relaxing the constraints on utility rate-setting rather than introducing genuine competition.

**The cost allocation gap**: FERC's central challenge as of 2026 is determining who pays for the transmission expansion required to connect renewable energy to population centers and to accommodate AI data center load growth. The answer has significant distributional consequences: if large load customers pay, energy costs for data centers and AI infrastructure rise; if costs are socialized across all ratepayers, residential bills rise while tech industry profits are subsidized. This is a direct example of how FERC's governance structure — five commissioners with bipartisan appointment requirements but no formal consumer representation — produces outcomes that favor industrial users over residential ratepayers. (FERC, 2025-2026)

### 2.3 The Grid Failure Pattern

The February 2021 Texas grid collapse (ERCOT) killed an estimated 246-702 people (estimates vary based on mortality methodology) and caused approximately $195-200 billion in economic damage. The causes were documented in extensive investigations:

- Deregulated market structure that provided no financial incentive for generators to winterize equipment
- Regulatory failure: Texas state regulators had received winterization recommendations after a 2011 cold-weather event and did not mandate implementation
- Political design: Texas deliberately isolated its grid (ERCOT) from the national interconnection to avoid federal FERC oversight — a choice that left it without emergency reserve capacity from neighboring states
- Private profit extraction: Generating companies that had failed to winterize made billions in profits during the crisis when power scarcity drove wholesale prices from the regulated $25/MWh cap to the emergency ceiling of $9,000/MWh. Texans who had variable-rate electricity contracts — mostly low-income customers who couldn't afford fixed-rate plans — received bills of $10,000-$17,000 for a week of service.

The Texas collapse is not an outlier. It is the clearest documented example of what happens when energy infrastructure is treated as a vehicle for private profit extraction rather than as public infrastructure requiring democratic governance. The same pattern operates at lower severity across every state with weak regulatory structures.

## 3. Existing Reform Mechanisms and Case Studies

### 3.1 What Works: Municipal Utilities and Cooperatives

The 3,300 municipal utilities and 900 electric cooperatives that serve approximately 25% of U.S. electricity customers demonstrate that democratic accountability in energy governance is achievable. Municipal utilities operate on a cost-of-service basis rather than profit-maximization, typically have elected or publicly appointed boards, and consistently charge lower rates than investor-owned utilities in comparable service territories:

- Austin Energy (municipal): Average residential rate of $95/month vs. CPS Energy's $130/month (both Texas utilities, comparable climate)
- Sacramento Municipal Utility District (SMUD): Among the lowest rates in California; completed an ambitious renewable transition without the reliability failures that have characterized Pacific Gas and Electric
- Cooperative electric utilities in the Great Plains: The rural cooperative model, established under New Deal rural electrification programs, has provided reliable service to geographically difficult territories for 85 years at rates below comparable investor-owned utilities

The democratic accountability mechanism matters. When Austin Energy's rates are set by a city council that is directly accountable to ratepayers who are also voters, the political economy is different than when rates are set by a utility commission appointee who is primarily accountable to the governor and secondarily to the utilities being regulated.

### 3.2 State Reform Examples

**New York's Reforming the Energy Vision (REV)**: New York's utility regulatory reform process, begun in 2015, attempted to reorient utility business models from passive infrastructure delivery to active energy management — allowing utilities to earn returns from efficiency, demand response, and distributed generation management rather than only from capital investment. The process has been slow and incomplete but represents the most serious state-level attempt to redesign utility regulation for a clean energy economy.

**Colorado's Community Solar**: Colorado's 2010 community solar law enabled community solar gardens — shared solar installations whose output is credited to subscriber utility bills — as an alternative to individual rooftop solar. The program has served approximately 130,000 subscribers, including 14,000 low-income customers who received additional bill credits. It demonstrates that utility regulation can be redesigned to enable distributed, community-scale clean energy without requiring individual homeownership.

**Minnesota's Energy Low Income Program (ELIM)**: Minnesota provides a 50% electricity rate discount for households below 50% of the area median income. The program is funded through a utility-administered charge on all customers. It has reduced disconnections among low-income households by 35% compared to pre-program rates, and a cost-benefit analysis found that reduced downstream costs (social services, healthcare, housing instability) more than offset program costs.

### 3.3 International Benchmarks

**Germany's Energiewende**: Germany's energy transition has achieved 52% renewable electricity generation (2023) while maintaining grid reliability metrics superior to the U.S., through a combination of: (1) federal renewable energy policy, (2) strong distribution utility regulation, and (3) explicit energy poverty support through the Energiegeld household energy rebate and the Sozialrabatt (social tariff) for low-income households. Germany's approach demonstrates that aggressive renewable transition and energy affordability protection are not in tension — they require coordinated governance that the U.S. fragmented regulatory structure makes difficult.

**France's "tarif de première nécessité"**: France's essential electricity tariff provides a 57-100% discount on electricity bills for households below an income threshold, serving approximately 4 million households. The tariff is funded by a charge on all electricity bills and administered through utility billing systems without requiring a separate application process. Automatic enrollment through social security income data is being expanded. The French model is notable for its administrative simplicity — no application burden for beneficiaries.

**UK's Warm Home Discount**: The UK requires large energy suppliers to provide a £150 direct bill discount to eligible low-income households. The program covers approximately 3 million households annually. More significantly, the UK government's January 2026 Great British Energy Act established a publicly-owned clean energy company with a £8.3 billion capitalization mandate — the most significant example of a wealthy democracy re-establishing public ownership in energy infrastructure in the current period.

## 4. Proposed Reforms

### 4a. Federal energy affordability floor

Establish a federal Energy Affordability Standard: no household should pay more than 6% of income on energy for adequate heating, cooling, and electricity. The federal government should fund the difference through an expanded LIHEAP providing automatic, categorical enrollment for households below 150% of the federal poverty level using existing tax return data (no separate application). Restore the LIHEAP program staff fired in April 2025 and insulate the program from at-will elimination by creating a dedicated trust fund financed by a fee on utility company profits above a 10% rate of return.

*Evidence*: The 6% affordability threshold is the standard used by the American Council for an Energy-Efficient Economy and endorsed by the ACEEE's National Energy Affordability Initiative. France's essential electricity tariff model and Minnesota's ELIM program demonstrate that income-tested energy discounts are administratively feasible and cost-effective. The 21.5 million households currently behind on energy bills represent a potential disconnection wave; the emergency social services and public health costs of mass disconnections exceed the cost of preventive affordability assistance.

*Fiscal impact*: LIHEAP expansion from $4B/year to $10B/year to cover the affordability gap for the bottom three income quintiles. At $500/year average benefit, $10B serves 20 million households. Cost partially offset by avoided emergency services, reduced hospitalization, and reduced housing instability attributable to energy shutoffs. Dedicated utility profit-fee funding: a 0.5% surcharge on investor-owned utility revenues ($400B+ nationally) generates $2B/year, reducing the net federal appropriation needed. The New Deal model is instructive: the Rural Electrification Administration extended electricity to 98% of American farms in 15 years at costs that the private market had declared economically infeasible.

### 4b. Reform state utility commission governance

Establish federal minimum standards for state public utility commissions as a condition of eligibility for federal energy funding: (1) prohibition on commissioners with utility employment within 5 years of appointment, (2) mandatory post-service cooling-off period of 5 years before joining a regulated utility, (3) public interest representatives (consumer advocates, environmental advocates, low-income community representatives) with funded staff in every rate case proceeding, (4) mandatory public hearings in the communities most affected by rate increases before any rate case can proceed, (5) state-level public utility advocates with independent budgets funded by utility revenues.

The federal funding leverage mechanism: states that meet these minimum governance standards are eligible for the full grant amounts under the federal grid modernization and clean energy programs in Domain 12. States that do not meet the standards receive 75% of grant amounts, with the balance held in trust until compliance is achieved.

*Precedent*: Wisconsin's Citizens Utility Board (CUB), established 1979, is the model for a funded public intervenor in utility proceedings — a nonprofit organization with funding from utility bills that represents residential ratepayers in regulatory proceedings. CUB has saved Wisconsin consumers an estimated $20 billion since its founding. Illinois and Oregon have similar publicly-funded utility consumer advocates. The federal minimum standard would require these mechanisms in all states, not just those that have voluntarily adopted them.

*Implementation pathway*: Federal statute tying clean energy and grid modernization grants to state utility governance standards. This follows the same structural logic as the federal highway and education grant conditions that have produced minimum national standards in areas of state-federal shared responsibility.

### 4c. National grid governance reform

Require Texas to join either the Eastern or Western Interconnection (ending ERCOT's deliberate isolation from FERC oversight and emergency reserve capacity). Establish regional grid reliability organizations with statutory public interest mandates, funded by assessments on all market participants, with consumer and community representatives on governing boards. Require FERC to adopt explicit energy affordability standards for rate proposals — rate increases cannot be approved without an affordability impact analysis showing the effect on low-income households and a mitigation requirement.

*Evidence*: Texas's 2021 grid collapse — the clearest documented case of the costs of regulatory isolation and market-design failure — is the primary evidence. ERCOT's isolation from the national grid prevented the emergency power imports from neighboring states that would have been available if Texas had been interconnected. The financial cost ($195-200 billion) dwarfs the cost of FERC oversight and interconnection that the isolation was designed to avoid. Every major jurisdiction that has experienced similar grid failures (California 2020 rolling blackouts, Northeast 2003 blackout) has subsequently undertaken governance reforms that increased interconnection and reserve requirements.

*Fiscal impact*: ERCOT integration into the national interconnection requires approximately $5-8 billion in transmission investment. Against the $195-200 billion cost of the 2021 collapse, the ROI is immediate. Regional grid organizations funded by market participant assessments (0.1-0.2% of wholesale transaction values): $1-2B/year in funding for governance and operational oversight, paid by utilities and generators, not ratepayers.

### 4d. Preempt ALEC utility protection legislation

Establish federal minimum standards prohibiting states from: (1) banning municipal broadband networks (already partially addressed in Domain 12), (2) restricting community solar programs that provide competition to utility monopolies, (3) eliminating net metering for rooftop solar without providing equivalent compensation mechanisms, (4) mandating that municipalities use investor-owned utilities rather than establishing municipal utilities or cooperatives. These preemptions establish a floor for consumer choice and distributed energy without displacing state regulatory authority over utility rates and operations.

*Evidence*: At least 18 states have laws restricting municipal broadband. At least 14 states have restricted net metering under utility lobbying pressure. ALEC-model legislation on utility protection has been identified in at least 14 states by the Energy and Policy Institute. The cumulative effect of these state laws is to preserve investor-owned utility monopolies against any competitive or public-interest alternative — preventing the market mechanisms and democratic accountability that would otherwise constrain extractive behavior.

## 5. Connection to Broader Democratic Failure Patterns

Energy access and utility democracy connect to seven of the proposal's existing domains:

**Deepens Domain 12 (Infrastructure)**: Domain 12 addresses grid modernization as a physical infrastructure problem. Domain 24 addresses it as a governance and regulatory capture problem. The physical and governance analyses are complementary: grid modernization investment without governance reform will reproduce the ERCOT failure pattern in the next extreme weather event, because the same regulatory structure that failed to mandate winterization will apply to the upgraded grid.

**Extends Domain 15 (Environment and Climate)**: The clean energy transition requires utility regulatory reform. No amount of renewable energy policy will succeed if state utility commissions — captured by fossil fuel interests — block renewable interconnection, restrict community solar, and protect gas utility infrastructure investments. The climate and the democratic governance problems are structurally connected.

**Supports Domain 18 (Social Safety Net)**: LIHEAP is a social safety net program whose elimination is proposed in the same budget that proposes cutting SNAP and TANF. Energy affordability is part of the material precarity that the social safety net domain addresses, and the mechanisms of attack (firing program staff, proposing elimination, cutting automatic enrollment) are identical. The domain-specific treatment of energy affordability strengthens the proposal's analysis of how material deprivation undermines democratic participation.

**Grounds Domain 22 (Racial Justice)**: Energy poverty is racially structured. Black and Latino households face higher disconnection rates, spend higher income shares on energy, live in housing with lower energy efficiency, and are concentrated in utility service territories with the weakest consumer protections (Southern states). The environmental justice dimension — which Domain 15 partially addresses — requires specific treatment of the utility governance mechanisms that perpetuate racial energy inequality.

**Connects to Domain 9 (Federalism)**: State preemption of municipal energy options (municipal utilities, community solar, rooftop solar net metering) is an example of the federalism capture dynamic that Domain 9 documents in voting rights, worker protections, and environmental regulation. The ALEC-drafted utility protection laws are structurally identical to the ALEC-drafted voter suppression laws: model legislation drafted by concentrated private interests, passed in captured state legislatures, used to preempt local democratic choices. The remedy — federal minimum standards that prevent states from restricting local democratic action — is also structurally identical.

**Reinforces Domain 3 (Democratic Participation)**: Energy access is a precondition for civic participation in ways that are not metaphorical. An eviction hearing requires a printed document and a physical presence; if you've been disconnected, you may not have printed it. A public hearing on a utility rate case is inaccessible to the households whose budgets it most affects if those households are working multiple jobs to pay the bill. The participatory deficit in utility governance — proceedings dominated by utility-hired attorneys and consultants, with no publicly-funded consumer representation in most states — is the energy-sector version of the participation desert that Domain 3 documents across government generally.

**Strengthens the Cross-Domain Synthesis**: The proposal's thirteen reinforcing feedback loops would be incomplete without an energy democracy loop. The energy loop runs: utility regulatory capture enables rate extraction → energy poverty creates material precarity → material precarity reduces civic participation → reduced participation weakens regulatory accountability → regulatory capture deepens. Identifying this loop allows the proposal to prescribe interventions at any of four points, rather than treating energy poverty as a one-dimensional affordability problem.
