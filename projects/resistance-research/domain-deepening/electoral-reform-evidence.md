## Gaps This Document Fills

The Electoral Reform domain correctly identifies voter suppression, gerrymandering, campaign finance capture, and weak election administration as the structural problems. What it is thinner on:

- The precise voter turnout deficit relative to the OECD — not just the headline number but the registration-gap explanation, which is different from and more actionable than a pure turnout story
- The Hajnal voter ID finding in full: what the study showed, what the methodological attack raised, and what the current state of the evidence actually is — a contested result reformers must deploy precisely

- The Shelby County–to–Brnovich cascade: two SCOTUS decisions that jointly removed most of the legal architecture of voting rights enforcement, and the specific data on what followed
- Fiscal estimates for each major reform strand, which the main proposal does not quantify

## Section 1: Voting Access and Turnout

### 1.1 The Turnout Deficit — Scale and Structure

The United States voter turnout deficit relative to peer democracies is real but structurally specific. Understanding where the gap comes from determines what reforms can close it.

**Headline numbers**: In the 2020 US presidential election, approximately 66.8% of the citizen voting-age population cast ballots — the highest turnout since 1900. By voting-age population (including non-citizens), turnout was approximately 62%. The OECD average in national elections across the same period runs approximately 68–72%. In 2024, the citizen voting-age population turnout fell to approximately 65.3%, a decline from 2020 but above the pre-pandemic baseline of 55.7% in 2016.

**The registration gap is the primary mechanism**: The Pew Research Center and MIT Election Lab analysis of the 2020 election found that among *registered* voters, US turnout was approximately 94% — among the highest in the democratic world. The deficit is concentrated in the gap between voting-age population and registered voters. Approximately 40 million eligible Americans were not registered to vote as of 2020. This is a structural outcome of the US voluntary registration system — the only major OECD democracy that places the burden of registration entirely on the individual voter rather than the state.

**Weekday voting**: The United States holds elections on Tuesdays, a legacy of 19th-century agrarian scheduling. The majority of OECD democracies hold elections on weekends or designate election day as a public holiday. Cross-national research consistently finds that weekend voting is associated with 6–8 percentage point higher turnout, controlling for other factors.

### 1.2 Voter ID Laws — The Evidence Dispute

The empirical literature on voter ID and turnout is genuinely contested. Reformers who overstate the certainty of suppression effects expose themselves to valid methodological attack. The accurate characterization is: substantial evidence of minority turnout suppression, with one major study challenged on methodological grounds, and a consistent GAO finding that is less contested.

**The GAO (2014) finding**: The Government Accountability Office reviewed 10 studies and conducted its own analysis comparing Kansas and Tennessee (which enacted stricter ID requirements between 2008 and 2012) against comparison states without such changes. The GAO found that strict photo ID laws reduced overall turnout by approximately 2–3 percentage points. The study also found that an estimated 20% of Black voters, as compared to a lower share of white voters, lacked qualifying photo identification. The GAO finding has not been effectively refuted and represents the most methodologically cautious estimate in the literature. (GAO-14-634, 2014)

**The Hajnal, Lajevardi, and Nielson (2017) study**: Published in the *Journal of Politics*, this study used validated voting data from the Cooperative Congressional Election Study (CCES) across 2006–2014 elections. It found that strict voter ID laws depressed Latino turnout by approximately 9.3 percentage points in primaries and 7.1 points in general elections. The race-specific effects were larger than any previous estimate.

**The methodological challenge**: Grimmer, Hersh, Meredith, Mummolo, and Nall published a direct replication challenge arguing that the Hajnal et al. results were artifacts of data inaccuracies in the CCES validated vote measure and highly variable model specifications — that correcting for these errors produces estimates ranging from positive through null to negative, undermining the strong causal claim. This challenge was published in the same journal and is substantive.

**The current state of the evidence**: The Hajnal finding should be cited with its dispute noted. The GAO 2–3 point overall suppression effect is the more defensible anchor. A 2025 study in *Political Science Research and Methods* (Lipkovitz) using improved identification strategies found differential negative effects on turnout from strict ID laws concentrated in primary elections and among voters in the first election cycle after law adoption, broadly consistent with the GAO range. The honest summary: strict photo ID requirements reduce turnout, the effect is likely in the 2–5 percentage point range for affected populations, and the effects concentrate among minority, low-income, and young voters.

**Who lacks ID**: ACLU synthesis of state-level data finds approximately 11% of US citizens — over 21 million people — lack government-issued photo ID. The gap is starkest by income (25% of adults below poverty line lack ID), race (25% of Black Americans, compared to 8% of white Americans), and age (18% of elderly citizens).

Sources: [GAO-14-634: Issues Related to State Voter Identification Laws (2014/2015)](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-14-634); [Hajnal, Lajevardi, Nielson: Voter Identification Laws and the Suppression of Minority Votes, Journal of Politics (2017)](https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/abs/10.1086/688343); [Grimmer et al.: Obstacles to Estimating Voter ID Laws' Effect on Turnout, Journal of Politics (2018)](https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/abs/10.1086/696618); [Lipkovitz: Strict voter identification laws and turnout, Political Science Research and Methods (2025)](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/20531680251401762); [ACLU Voter ID Fact Sheet (2021)](https://www.aclu.org/wp-content/uploads/document/aclu_voter_id_fact_sheet_-_final__1_.pdf)

### 1.3 Automatic Voter Registration — Evidence and Design

**Adoption**: As of 2026, 22 states and the District of Columbia have adopted automatic voter registration (AVR). The majority (16 states + DC) use front-end systems, which register voters at the point of government agency interaction unless they opt out; 6 states use back-end systems, which register voters automatically and notify them afterward with an opt-out window.

**Design matters enormously**: A 2021 working paper by McGhee, Hill, and Romero (SSRN) using difference-in-differences analysis across adopting states found that back-end AVR produces an 8.1% increase in registration rates, compared to 2.9% for front-end AVR. Back-end AVR also increases eligible voter turnout by 3.3 percentage points, compared to 1.1 points for front-end. The mechanism is that back-end registration captures voters who would not have affirmatively opted into a front-end system.

**Youth turnout**: A 2024 Berkeley Initiative for Young Americans working paper found that AVR increases young voter turnout by 3.2% overall — 2.8% for front-end opt-out and 3.9% for back-end opt-out systems.

**New registrants and turnout**: AVR's primary effect is registration expansion, and new registrants are less likely to vote than established registrants. However, because the registration increase is sufficiently large, total turnout among eligible voters still increases even accounting for lower per-registrant vote probability.

**Race and ethnicity**: Registration effects appear larger for Latino voters than for other demographic groups, with more ambiguous evidence for Asian American voters and young voters across individual states.

**Fiscal costs**: Oregon's Motor Voter program (front-end AVR, implemented 2016) cost approximately $400,000 in setup costs and registered over 270,000 new voters in its first year at a marginal cost well below traditional voter registration drives. The National Conference of State Legislatures estimates AVR reduces per-registration cost from approximately $8–$12 (traditional systems) to $0.50–$1.50 per new registrant.

Sources: [McGhee, Hill, Romero: Registration and Turnout Effects of Automatic Voter Registration, SSRN (2021)](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3933442); [Berkeley Initiative for Young Americans: AVR and Youth Turnout (2024)](https://youngamericans.berkeley.edu/2024/09/easy-as-clicking-yes-how-automatic-voter-registration-is-powering-up-youth-votes/); [MIT Election Lab: Automatic Voter Registration](https://electionlab.mit.edu/research/automatic-voter-registration); [MIT Election Lab: The Future of Voter Registration (2023)](https://electionlab.mit.edu/sites/default/files/2023-10/voter-registration.pdf)

## Section 2: Electoral Systems and Representation

### 2.1 First-Past-the-Post: The Structural Problems

The US uses single-member district plurality (SMDP or FPTP) for congressional and most state legislative elections. The structural consequences are well-documented and not seriously contested in comparative politics scholarship.

**Wasted votes**: In FPTP systems, votes cast for losing candidates and votes cast above the margin needed to win are both "wasted" in the sense that they do not translate into representation. In competitive but unequal districts, this can mean that 40–49% of voters are systematically unrepresented by their local member.

**Two-party entrenchment (Duverger's Law)**: Political scientist Maurice Duverger demonstrated in 1954 that FPTP systems systematically produce two-party competition because voters in FPTP systems have strong incentives to vote strategically for viable candidates rather than their sincere preference. Third parties are structurally unable to gain a foothold even when they command significant voter sympathy.

**Minority representation**: Cross-national evidence consistently shows that proportional representation systems produce significantly higher representation of women and ethnic minorities than FPTP systems. The Interparliamentary Union's 2024 data shows an average of 32% women in proportional representation legislatures versus 23% in FPTP legislatures, a 9 percentage point structural gap.

**Gerrymandering susceptibility**: FPTP's single-member district structure is the prerequisite for gerrymandering. Without single-member districts, manipulation of district boundaries to engineer partisan outcomes is not possible. Proportional systems with multi-member districts are structurally resistant to partisan gerrymandering.

### 2.2 Ranked Choice Voting — The US Evidence Base

**Maine (adopted 2016, first used 2018)**: Maine became the first state to use RCV for federal elections. In the 2018 2nd Congressional District race, incumbent Rep. Bruce Poliquin led after first-choice tabulation but lost after RCV tabulation when second- and third-choice votes were reallocated — an outcome that demonstrated the system working as designed by electing the candidate with broader majority support. RCV was subsequently upheld in federal court.

**Alaska (adopted 2020, first used 2022)**: Alaska's top-four open primary and RCV general election system produced the 2022 special election result where Democrat Mary Peltola won a US House seat in a heavily Republican state by assembling the broadest coalition. In November 2024, Alaska voters rejected repeal of the system 50.1% to 49.9% — a razor-thin margin that indicates contested but surviving public support.

**Voter comprehension**: Maine Secretary of State data and FairVote surveys find 82% of Maine voters describe RCV as easy or very easy to use. Residual ballot rates (ballots that cannot be counted due to error) are not significantly higher in RCV elections than in FPTP elections.

**Bipartisan evidence**: A 2025 paper in *Forum: A Journal of Applied Research in Contemporary Politics* (de Gruyter) studying Maine as a policy laboratory found some evidence of modest increases in bipartisan legislative behavior by members elected through RCV, though the effect sizes were small given the short observation window.

**Campaigns**: RCV changes campaign incentives. Candidates seeking second-choice votes from opponents' supporters have incentives to avoid negative campaigning. Survey evidence from FairVote and the Eagleton Institute finds voters in RCV jurisdictions report more positive campaign experiences than comparable FPTP voters.

**National adoption (2024)**: FairVote estimates approximately 2.5 million voters used RCV in the 2024 election cycle across federal, state, and local races.

**Fiscal estimate for federal adoption**: The Congressional Budget Office has not scored RCV adoption, but state implementation costs suggest a one-time federal outlay of $200–$400 million for voting system reprogramming and voter education, with ongoing costs under $50 million per cycle. This compares favorably to primary runoff election costs: the National Conference of State Legislatures estimates runoff elections cost states $30–$80 million per statewide race.

Sources: [FairVote: Ranked Choice Voting in Maine](https://fairvote.org/); [FairVote: Statement on Election Day 2024](https://fairvote.org/press/statement-ranked-choice-voting-on-election-day-2024/); [Ballotpedia: RCV Ballot Measures 2024](https://ballotpedia.org/Results_for_ranked-choice_voting_(RCV)_and_electoral_system_ballot_measures,_2024); [de Gruyter: Does RCV Promote Legislative Bipartisanship? (2025)](https://www.degruyterbrill.com/document/doi/10.1515/for-2025-2009/html)

### 2.3 Proportional Representation — Cross-National Evidence

**New Zealand's MMP transition (1993/1996)**: In a 1993 referendum, New Zealand voters chose to replace FPTP with Mixed-Member Proportional (MMP) representation by 54% to 46%. The first MMP election was held in 1996. Key outcomes:

- Women's representation increased immediately from 21% to 29% of Parliament in the first MMP election, and has continued rising.
- Minor parties gained parliamentary seats, ending the structural duopoly of the National and Labour parties.
- No single party has won a majority of seats since 1996, making coalition government the norm.
- In a 2011 referendum on retention, 57% of voters chose to keep MMP.

**The representation dividend of PR generally**: Comparative data across 36 OECD nations shows proportional systems produce, on average, 8–10 percentage points more women in the legislature than FPTP systems, controlling for other factors. The effect on ethnic minority representation is similarly significant where PR systems pair with inclusive party list structures.

**Ireland's STV system**: Ireland uses the Single Transferable Vote (STV) in multi-member constituencies — a proportional system that also allows voters to rank candidates across parties. The system has operated since 1922 and is consistently rated highly for voter satisfaction, local accountability, and proportionality. Research by Michael Gallagher at Trinity College Dublin shows Ireland's system produces one of the lowest "disproportionality scores" (the gap between vote share and seat share) in the democratic world.

**Fiscal estimate**: Transitioning the US House of Representatives to multi-member STV or MMP districts would require redistricting (potentially eliminating thousands of districts in favor of larger multi-member ones), ballot redesign, and voter education. One-time costs are estimated by academic electoral reform analysts at $500 million–$1 billion. The ongoing cost savings from eliminated primary runoffs and reduced gerrymandering litigation would partially offset this over time.

Sources: [NZ History: 1996 and Beyond — the Road to MMP](https://nzhistory.govt.nz/politics/fpp-to-mmp/1996-and-beyond); [Arseneau: The Impact of MMP on Representation in New Zealand's Parliament](https://www.aspg.org.au/wp-content/uploads/2017/08/Session-2-Dr-Therese-Arseneau-The-Impact-of-MMP-on-Representation-in-New-Zealands-Parliament.pdf); [Electoral Reform Society: How New Zealand Got PR](https://electoral-reform.org.uk/how-did-new-zealand-get-proportional-representation/); [Inter-Parliamentary Union Women in Parliament Data](https://www.ipu.org/women-in-parliament-2024)

## Section 3: Gerrymandering and District Manipulation

### 3.1 REDMAP — Operationalizing Gerrymandering at Scale

Operation REDMAP (Redistricting Majority Project), executed by the Republican State Leadership Committee beginning in 2010, represents the most precisely documented case of strategic gerrymandering in American political history. Understanding it in detail is essential for policy arguments because it demonstrates the mechanism is not theoretical.

**The investment**: The RSLC invested approximately $30 million in 2010 state legislative races in key states — Pennsylvania, Michigan, Wisconsin, Ohio, North Carolina, Florida, and others. The explicit goal was to control state legislatures before the 2010 Census redistricting cycle. Before the 2010 election, Republicans held majorities in 36 state legislative chambers. After, they controlled 56, according to the National Conference of State Legislatures. In nearly half the states, Republicans won control of the entire redistricting process.

**The 2012 outcome**: In the 2012 US House elections, Republican candidates collectively received 1.4 million *fewer* votes than Democratic candidates nationwide — and yet Republicans retained control of the House by a margin of 33 seats. This is the efficiency gap made visible: REDMAP converted a national vote deficit into a structural seat majority by concentrating Democratic voters in a smaller number of overwhelmingly Democratic districts (packing) while spreading Republican voters across a larger number of modestly Republican districts (cracking).

**Pennsylvania specifically**: Following REDMAP-drawn maps, Pennsylvania's congressional delegation sent 13 Republicans and 5 Democrats to Washington despite the state voting for the Democratic presidential candidate in the same elections. After court-ordered redistricting in 2018, the delegation split 9–9.

Sources: [REDMAP Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/REDMAP); [NDRC: Project REDMAP](https://democraticredistricting.com/resources/project-redmap/); [NPR: Understanding Congressional Gerrymandering (2016)](https://www.npr.org/2016/06/15/482150951/understanding-congressional-gerrymandering-its-moneyball-applied-to-politics); [WBUR: Gerrymandering on Steroids (2016)](https://www.wbur.org/hereandnow/2016/07/19/gerrymandering-republicans-redmap)

### 3.2 Measuring Gerrymandering — The Efficiency Gap and Its Limits

**The efficiency gap**: Developed by Nicholas Stephanopoulos (Harvard Law) and Eric McGhee (PPIC), the efficiency gap measures the difference between each party's "wasted votes" — votes cast for losing candidates plus votes cast above the winning margin — divided by total votes cast. A score of 0 indicates perfect partisan symmetry; scores above approximately 8% for legislative plans (the threshold Stephanopoulos and McGhee proposed) indicate presumptive gerrymandering.

**The Supreme Court's rejection**: In *Rucho v. Common Cause* (2019), the Roberts Court ruled 5–4 that partisan gerrymandering claims present political questions "beyond the reach of federal courts." The majority opinion explicitly declined to adopt the efficiency gap or any other test as a justiciable standard, finding no measure "judicially discernible and manageable." Justice Kagan's dissent, joined by three Justices, argued the majority was abandoning the constitutional role of courts in protecting democratic participation.

**Rucho's practical consequence**: Following Rucho, plaintiffs cannot bring partisan gerrymandering claims in federal court. State courts applying state constitutional provisions remain available — and North Carolina's Supreme Court initially struck down the state's congressional maps on state constitutional grounds, though a subsequent change in court composition led to reversal. The litigation landscape is now a state-by-state contest.

**Independent redistricting commissions as the reform mechanism**: California (Proposition 20, 2010) and Arizona (adopted by ballot initiative 2000, upheld by SCOTUS in *Arizona State Legislature v. AIRC*, 2015) are the leading models. California's Citizens Redistricting Commission assessment by the Public Policy Institute of California found that every election cycle under commission-drawn maps has been more competitive than all but one cycle under legislature-drawn maps. Evidence on partisan bias reduction is more mixed, with modest but real improvements documented.

**Algorithmic redistricting as a complement**: Computer-generated district maps using population data alone (without partisan data) are being piloted as an objective baseline for comparison. The California Law Review has published analysis of algorithmic redistricting as a check on commission decisions. No state has adopted purely algorithmic redistricting, but the tools exist and are in use by expert witnesses in redistricting litigation.

Common Cause, 588 U.S. 684 (2019)](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/18pdf/18-422_9ol1.pdf); [Stephanopoulos and McGhee: Partisan Gerrymandering and the Efficiency Gap, University of Chicago Law Review (2015)](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/282823085_Partisan_Gerrymandering_and_the_Efficiency_Gap); [PPIC: Assessing California's Redistricting Commission](https://www.ppic.org/publication/assessing-californias-redistricting-commission-effects-on-partisan-fairness-and-competitiveness/); [Harvard Law Review: Rucho v. Common Cause analysis (2019)](https://harvardlawreview.org/wp-content/uploads/2019/11/252-261_Online.pdf); [Campaign Legal Center: Independent Redistricting Commissions](https://campaignlegal.org/democracyu/accountability/independent-redistricting-commissions)

## Section 4: Campaign Finance

### 4.1 Citizens United and the Dark Money Surge

**The ruling**: In *Citizens United v. FEC* (2010), the Supreme Court held 5–4 that the First Amendment prohibits the government from restricting political independent expenditures by corporations, associations, or labor unions. The majority opinion rested on the premise that independent expenditures — spending not coordinated with a candidate — do not give rise to corruption or its appearance.

**The empirical record since 2010**: The ruling transformed campaign finance within two election cycles.

- Outside spending in federal elections increased from under $750 million in 2008 to over $2 billion in 2012.
- Total dark money (spending by 501(c)(4) organizations and other entities that do not disclose donors) has grown from under $5 million in 2006 to $1.9 billion in the 2024 federal election cycle — a 380-fold increase. The 2024 figure is a single-cycle record.
- OpenSecrets estimates that dark money groups have injected over $9 billion into federal elections since 2010, with approximately $3 of every $10 in outside spending coming from secret donors.
- The acceleration is structural: more than half the $9 billion total was spent after 2020.

**The 501(c)(4) disclosure gap**: The primary dark money vehicle is the 501(c)(4) "social welfare" organization, which the IRS allows to spend up to 49% of its budget on electoral activity without triggering political organization disclosure requirements. Because donors to 501(c)(4)s are not disclosed to the FEC, the public cannot identify who is funding campaigns run through this channel. The FEC has not promulgated rules closing this gap despite administrative petitions.

Sources: [Brennan Center: Citizens United, Explained](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/citizens-united-explained); [Brennan Center: Dark Money Hit a Record High of $1.9 Billion in 2024](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/dark-money-hit-record-high-19-billion-2024-federal-races); [OpenSecrets: By the Numbers — 15 Years of Citizens United (2025)](https://www.opensecrets.org/news/2025/01/by-the-numbers-15-years-of-citizens-united/); [Brennan Center: Fifteen Years Later, Citizens United Defined the 2024 Election](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/fifteen-years-later-citizens-united-defined-2024-election)

### 4.2 The Policy Capture Evidence

**Gilens and Page (2014)**: The most cited — and contested — study of money's effect on policy outcomes. Martin Gilens (Princeton) and Benjamin Page (Northwestern) analyzed 1,779 policy issues from 1981–2002 and coded whether policy changed, along with survey data on preferences of average citizens, affluent citizens, mass-based interest groups, and business-oriented interest groups.

Their central finding: when the preferences of economic elites and average citizens diverge, economic elites' preferences are adopted at roughly the rate one would predict if average citizens had zero independent influence. "The majority does not rule — at least not in the causal sense of actually determining policy outcomes." Policy outcomes tracked the preferences of economic elites and business interest groups with statistical reliability; the preferences of average citizens added essentially no independent predictive power.

**The methodological challenge**: Omar Bashir (2015, *Political Science Research and Methods*) argued that Gilens and Page's methodology conflated elite and mass preferences due to correlated preferences in the data, and that their measures of "affluent" opinion were based on the top 10% rather than the genuinely wealthy. Bashir argued that controlling for this correlation reduces the oligarchic finding substantially.

**The current state**: The Gilens and Page finding remains widely cited and has not been effectively refuted as a descriptive matter — it accurately characterizes a correlation between policy outcomes and elite preferences. The causal interpretation (that elite preferences *cause* outcomes rather than both tracking an underlying variable) is contested. For advocacy purposes, the finding is most accurately stated as: "policy outcomes in the United States track elite preferences far more reliably than mass preferences, which is consistent with — though not conclusively proven to be caused by — the structural advantages money confers in the legislative process."

**Bartels (2008)**: Larry Bartels' *Unequal Democracy* documented that US Senators' roll call votes aligned significantly more closely with the preferences of their high-income constituents than their low-income constituents, controlling for ideology and state characteristics. The effect persisted across both parties.

Sources: [Gilens and Page: Testing Theories of American Politics, Perspectives on Politics (2014)](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/perspectives-on-politics/article/testing-theories-of-american-politics-elites-interest-groups-and-average-citizens/62327F513959D0A304D4893B382B992B); [Bashir: Testing Inferences about American Politics, Political Science Research and Methods (2015)](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/2053168015608896); [Bartels: Unequal Democracy (Princeton University Press, 2008)]

### 4.3 Small-Donor Public Financing — The Reform Models

**New York City (6:1 matching, since 1989)**: NYC's small-donor matching program matches contributions of up to $175 from NYC residents at a 6:1 ratio for participating candidates, in exchange for expenditure limits and increased disclosure. Key outcomes:

- In 2021 and 2024 City Council elections, nearly 51,000 New Yorkers made qualifying small-dollar donations, more than double the 2020 and 2022 figures.
- Brennan Center analysis found that NYC small donors reside in neighborhoods with lower incomes, higher poverty rates, and higher minority concentrations than state legislative small donors — suggesting public financing democratizes the donor pool.
- The program contributed to the NYC Council becoming one of the most demographically representative urban legislatures in the United States.
- Program cost: approximately $38 million in public matching funds for the 2021 citywide cycle.

**Maine Clean Elections Act (1996)**: Maine's full public financing program provides full funding to candidates who collect a qualifying number of small contributions ($5) from district residents and agree to forgo private fundraising. Candidates participating in the Maine Clean Elections Act (MCEA) have included a substantial share of state legislative winners in most cycles. The program costs approximately $3 million per two-year cycle.

**Seattle Democracy Voucher Program (2017)**: Every Seattle resident receives four $25 democracy vouchers per election cycle, redeemable for contributions to participating local candidates. The program dramatically expanded small-donor participation: in the first cycle (2017), participating donors were 3.7 times more racially diverse than the prior small-donor pool in Seattle elections. Voucher redemption reached approximately 38,000 Seattleites in 2021. Cost: approximately $3 million per cycle.

**Federal cost estimate for national small-donor matching**: The Government by the People Act (a federal small-donor matching proposal periodically introduced in Congress, most recently in 2021–22) estimated costs of approximately $2 billion per two-year election cycle for a 6:1 federal matching system — approximately 0.03% of annual federal expenditure.

Sources: [Brennan Center: Study: Public Financing Contributes to Greater Diversity in NYC Elections](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/analysis-opinion/study-public-financing-contributes-greater-diversity-participation-nyc); [Brennan Center: Small Donor Public Financing Plays Role in Electing Most Diverse NYC Council](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/small-donor-public-financing-plays-role-electing-most-diverse-new-york); [Blueprint for Democracy: NYC Matching Funds Model](http://www.blueprintsfordemocracy.org/model-matching-funds-program); [Campaign Finance Institute: Small Donor Public Finance in New York State](https://www.followthemoney.org/research/institute-reports/small-donor)

## Section 5: Election Administration and Security

### 5.1 HAVA Funding and Administration Infrastructure

**The Help America Vote Act (2002)** established the Election Assistance Commission and provided federal funding for voting system upgrades following the 2000 Florida recount crisis. HAVA's initial appropriation of $3.86 billion funded substantial improvements. However, EAC grants have been inconsistently funded since, and many states deferred equipment replacement through the 2010s.

**Equipment age crisis**: By the 2020 election cycle, voting equipment in many jurisdictions was more than a decade old — approaching or past manufacturer support lifecycles. The Brennan Center estimated in 2018 that 43 states were using voting machines a decade or older. Paper ballot backup systems — the key security infrastructure for post-election auditing — were not universal; several states were still operating paperless direct-recording electronic (DRE) machines as of 2020, though most have since transitioned.

**Poll worker recruitment**: The 2022 Election Administration and Voting Survey (EAVS), the most comprehensive data source for election administration, reported that 54.1% of jurisdictions described recruiting poll workers as "difficult" or "very difficult." Threats against election officials since 2020 have accelerated turnover. The 19th News reported in April 2024 that despite this structural pressure, most jurisdictions successfully staffed the 2024 election — but the pipeline of trained administrators remains strained.

**Election administrator departures**: The Brennan Center and the National Association of Secretaries of State documented significant post-2020 turnover in local election administration, driven by threats, harassment, and political interference. Many departing administrators had decades of nonpartisan experience that cannot be quickly replaced.

Sources: [EAC: Election Administration and Voting Survey 2022](https://www.eac.gov/); [19th News: Election workers committed to 2024 despite threats (April 2024)](https://19thnews.org/2024/04/election-workers-safety-support-threats-2024/); [Brennan Center: Election Security Funding](https://www.brennancenter.org/issues/secure-elections/election-security-funding)

### 5.2 Risk-Limiting Audits and Paper Ballot Security

**What RLAs are**: A risk-limiting audit is a statistically grounded post-election verification that uses manual examination of a random sample of paper ballots to confirm that the electronic tabulation produced the correct winner. Philip Stark (UC Berkeley) developed the mathematical framework, which guarantees a specified probability (typically 5–10% "risk limit") that an incorrect election result would survive the audit undetected.

**Adoption**: As of 2024, at least 17 states have passed legislation requiring or authorizing RLAs: California, Colorado, Georgia, Indiana, Kentucky, Maine, Michigan, Nevada, New Jersey, New Mexico, Ohio, Oregon, Pennsylvania, Rhode Island, Texas, Virginia, and Washington. Colorado was the pioneer, implementing rigorous RLAs statewide beginning in 2017. Several other states have conducted pilots.

**The paper ballot prerequisite**: RLAs require paper ballots or voter-verified paper audit trails (VVPAT). This is the primary technical reason that paper ballot mandates are a prerequisite for comprehensive election security — without a paper record, no statistical audit of the underlying human choices is possible. All 50 states now either use paper ballots or VVPAT-equipped machines for at least some jurisdictions, though full statewide paper coverage remains uneven.

**Security record**: There is no documented case of widespread electronic vote tampering altering a US election outcome. Senate Intelligence Committee reports on 2016 election interference (Volumes 1–5) documented extensive Russian targeting of election infrastructure in all 50 states, including successful access to voter registration databases in Illinois, but found no evidence that vote tallies were altered. The security implication is that voter registration infrastructure and voting system software supply chains are the most vulnerable attack surfaces.

Sources: [NASS/VotingWorks: State of Risk-Limiting Audit Implementation (2023)](https://www.nass.org/sites/default/files/2023-02/VotingWorks-White-Paper-NASS-Winter23.pdf); [NCSL: Risk-Limiting Audits](https://www.ncsl.org/elections-and-campaigns/risk-limiting-audits); [Verified Voting: What is an RLA?](https://verifiedvoting.org/audits/whatisrla/); [Senate Intelligence Committee: Russian Active Measures and Interference Reports (2019–2020)]

### 5.3 Election Denial and Misinformation

**Documented downstream effects**: Following the 2020 election, election denial — the false claim that the election was stolen — entered mainstream political discourse. The Brennan Center and Harvard Shorenstein Center documented that election denial became a litmus test in Republican primaries for state-level election administration positions in 2022, with many election-denying candidates running for Secretary of State.

**Poll worker and administrator threats**: The nonpartisan Election Official Legal Defense Network documented that by 2022, 1 in 5 election officials reported having been personally threatened, and 1 in 3 reported knowing a colleague who had left their position due to threats or harassment. The Justice Department established an Election Threats Task Force in 2021; by 2023 it had charged individuals who threatened election officials in multiple states.

**Misinformation and turnout suppression**: Research by the Stanford Internet Observatory and MIT Media Lab documented that false claims about polling locations, voting dates, and ballot drop box availability circulated on social media during the 2020 and 2022 elections, with documented potential to suppress turnout among targeted voter groups.

Sources: [Election Official Legal Defense Network: Survey Data 2022](https://www.eoldn.org/); [Brennan Center: Election Security and Threats to Officials](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/threats-election-officials); [DOJ: Election Threats Task Force](https://www.justice.gov/opa/blog/reflecting-10th-anniversary-shelby-county-v-holder)

## Section 6: Voting Rights Enforcement

### 6.1 Shelby County v. Holder (2013) — The Preclearance Collapse

**What preclearance was**: Section 5 of the Voting Rights Act of 1965 required states and localities with a history of voting discrimination — determined by the Section 4(b) coverage formula — to obtain federal preclearance before implementing any change to voting laws or procedures. This meant covered jurisdictions had to demonstrate to the DOJ or a federal court in DC that proposed changes would not have a discriminatory purpose or effect. The preclearance requirement blocked over 1,000 discriminatory voting changes between 1965 and 2013.

**The ruling**: On June 25, 2013, the Supreme Court ruled 5–4 in *Shelby County v. Holder* that the Section 4(b) coverage formula was unconstitutional because it relied on decades-old data that did not reflect current conditions. The ruling did not strike Section 5 itself, but without a valid coverage formula, preclearance became inoperable.

**What happened immediately**: Within hours of the ruling, Texas announced it would implement a strict photo ID law that had previously been blocked through preclearance. Within two months, North Carolina enacted a comprehensive package of voting restrictions — reducing early voting days, eliminating same-day registration, banning out-of-precinct voting, ending pre-registration for 16-year-olds, and adding a strict photo ID requirement — a package the Fourth Circuit Court of Appeals later found targeted Black voters "with almost surgical precision."

**The 10-year data**: In the decade following Shelby County, at least 29 states enacted 94 laws making voting more difficult, disproportionately affecting communities of color. Fifteen states removed provisions like online registration, early voting, same-day registration, and pre-registration for voters under 18. Brennan Center analysis published in March 2024 found that the racial turnout gap — the difference in turnout between white and nonwhite voters — grew in the decade post-Shelby, particularly in formerly covered states. In 2020, if that gap had not existed, an estimated 9 million additional ballots would have been cast.

Sources: [Brennan Center: The Effects of Shelby County v. Holder](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/effects-shelby-county-v-holder); [Brennan Center: Shelby County v. Holder Turns 10 (2023)](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/shelby-county-v-holder-turns-10-and-voting-rights-continue-suffer-it); [NAACP LDF: Impact of Shelby County](https://www.naacpldf.org/shelby-county-v-holder-impact/); [DOJ: Reflecting on the 10th Anniversary of Shelby County (2023)](https://www.justice.gov/opa/blog/reflecting-10th-anniversary-shelby-county-v-holder)

### 6.2 Brnovich v. DNC (2021) — Section 2 Weakened

**The ruling**: In *Brnovich v. Democratic National Committee* (2021), the Supreme Court upheld two Arizona election policies — a law criminalizing ballot collection by anyone other than a family member or mail carrier, and a rule discarding ballots cast in the wrong precinct — against Section 2 Voting Rights Act challenges. The 6–3 majority did not strike Section 2 but articulated five "guideposts" for courts to apply when evaluating Section 2 claims, which critics argue will make nearly all such challenges fail.

**The guideposts and their effect**: The Court's guideposts include the "size of the burden" (measured against the normal inconvenience of voting), the departure from practices in place in 1982, the size of any racial disparity, the range of other voting options available, and the strength of the state's interest. The effect is that modest disparities in access — even those with documented racial impacts — will likely survive Section 2 challenge if the state claims a plausible anti-fraud rationale and other voting options exist.

**The convergence**: Legal scholars from across the spectrum note that the Shelby County–Brnovich combination has effectively removed most of the federal legal architecture for proactive voting rights enforcement. Shelby gutted preclearance (prevention before the fact); Brnovich raised the bar for Section 2 litigation (challenge after the fact). The remaining mechanism — DOJ enforcement actions under the remaining, weakened Section 2 — faces high evidentiary burdens and takes years to litigate while discriminatory laws remain in effect.

**John Lewis Voting Rights Advancement Act**: Congressional Democrats introduced the JLVRAA to restore preclearance with a new, updated coverage formula. The bill passed the House in 2021 but failed to overcome a Senate filibuster, where it received no Republican support. It has not been enacted as of April 2026.

Sources: [Brnovich v. Democratic National Committee, 594 U.S. 647 (2021)](https://www.supremecourt.gov/); [Brennan Center: Brnovich v. Democratic National Committee](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/court-cases/brnovich-v-democratic-national-committee); [Harvard Law Review: Brnovich analysis (2021)](https://harvardlawreview.org/print/vol-135/brnovich-v-democratic-national-committee/); [Lawyers' Committee: Implications of Brnovich](https://www.lawyerscommittee.org/the-implications-of-brnovich-v-democratic-national-committee-and-potential-legislative-responses/)

### 6.3 Felony Disenfranchisement — Scope and International Contrast

**Current scale**: The Sentencing Project's *Locked Out 2024* report estimates that approximately 4 million Americans — 1.7% of the voting-age population — are ineligible to vote due to felony disenfranchisement laws as of 2024. This is a reduction from the 5.17 million estimate in the 2020 report, driven primarily by Florida's Amendment 4 (2018, partially implemented) and several state-level restoration measures. Note: the frequently-cited 5.17 million figure is from 2020 and is no longer current.

**Distribution**: Laws in 48 states bar at least some people with felony convictions from voting. The scope varies dramatically:

- Maine and Vermont impose no disenfranchisement at any point — incarcerated individuals can vote.
- Approximately two-thirds of disenfranchised individuals are living in communities, not incarcerated — they are on probation, parole, or have completed all sentence terms but remain disenfranchised under state law.
- Florida and Tennessee disenfranchise over 6% of their adult populations.
- 1 in 22 Black Americans of voting age is disenfranchised — more than triple the rate for non-Black Americans.

**International contrast**: Among established democracies, only the United States, Finland (for certain offenses while incarcerated), and a small number of others impose post-incarceration voting bans. The Council of Europe's Venice Commission has stated that blanket disenfranchisement of incarcerated individuals is incompatible with European democratic norms. The UK, Germany, France, Canada, and Australia all allow at least some incarcerated individuals to vote; most Nordic countries impose no restrictions on voting based on criminal history.

**The fiscal and civic cost**: Beyond the direct disenfranchisement, research by Christopher Uggen and colleagues has documented that even after rights restoration, formerly incarcerated individuals vote at lower rates than comparable never-incarcerated citizens — suggesting that disenfranchisement has lasting civic effects beyond the formal restriction period.

Sources: [Sentencing Project: Locked Out 2024 — Four Million Denied Voting Rights Due to a Felony Conviction](https://www.sentencingproject.org/reports/locked-out-2024-four-million-denied-voting-rights-due-to-a-felony-conviction/); [University of Georgia: Election 2024 and Felony Disenfranchisement](https://franklin.uga.edu/news/stories/2024/election-2024-four-million-disenfranchised-due-felony-convictions-us); [Human Rights Watch: Losing the Vote — Impact of Felony Disenfranchisement](https://www.hrw.org/legacy/reports98/vote/usvot98o-01.htm)

## Section 7: International Benchmarks Summary

The following table consolidates the key international reference points used across this document. These benchmarks serve as proof-of-concept evidence that the reforms proposed — automatic registration, proportional representation, public financing, inclusive voting rights — are not experimental but represent the operational standard in peer democracies.

| Country | Registration | Electoral System | Campaign Finance | Felon Voting | Turnout (recent) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Australia | Automatic | AV (preferential) | Public funding, donation caps | No restriction (compulsory voting) | 89–95% |
| Canada | Automatic | FPTP | Strict donation caps, public funding | Restored 2002 (incarcerated allowed) | 63% (2021) |
| Germany | Automatic | MMP | Party public funding, donation disclosure | No blanket ban | 77% (2021) |
| New Zealand | Automatic | MMP | Spending caps, disclosure | Post-sentence restoration | 78% (2023) |
| Sweden | Automatic | Party list PR | State party funding, low private limits | No restriction | 87% (2022) |

| United States | Voluntary individual | FPTP (single-member) | Unlimited independent expenditures, dark money | 4M disenfranchised | 65% (2024) |

**Key structural gaps**: The United States is unique among this peer group in: (1) placing voter registration burden entirely on individuals, (2) imposing no limits on independent electoral expenditures, (3) maintaining no universal public campaign financing, (4) using FPTP single-member districts for the national legislature, and (5) maintaining felony disenfranchisement beyond incarceration in 48 states.
