# Phase 3 Playbook: Civil Service Unions (AFGE, NTEU, AFSCME)

**Audience**: AFGE, NTEU, and AFSCME legislative and legal departments; AFL-CIO political department; Federal Workforce Caucus staff; Senate Homeland Security and Governmental Affairs Committee staff

---

## Leverage Analysis

Civil service unions are the institutional backbone of Wave 1 resistance — not because they are the most visible actors, but because they know where the damage is being done. AFGE's 1.3 million members operate the federal agencies being hollowed out. NTEU's 150,000+ members are the tax collectors, customs officers, securities regulators, and FDA scientists whose institutional knowledge cannot be recovered when it walks out the door. AFSCME's 1.6 million state and local government workers operate the cooperative programs that transmit federal policy into state-level delivery.

Their leverage operates through four mechanisms that no other constituency combines:

**Direct injury standing.** Civil service unions are principal plaintiffs, not amici. AFGE's January 20, 2025 inauguration-night filing against Schedule Policy/Career demonstrates the operational model: legal theory pre-drafted before the executive order was signed; facts inserted after signing; filing complete within the inaugural day. This is the filing speed standard for every constituency in the coalition.

**Documented harm.** When civil service unions testify, they have specific facts. The 385,000 federal civilian employees who have departed since January 2025 are identifiable by name, position, and agency function. The 2,100% MSPB case backlog is a documented number. The 70% reduction in DOJ Civil Rights Division attorneys — from approximately 250 to about 75 — is in OPM records. This documentation is credible in courts, before congressional committees, and in media.

**Electoral mobilization infrastructure.** AFL-CIO's 8-million-household voter contact program is the largest organized electoral operation in American politics. Civil service unions, as AFL-CIO affiliates (AFGE, AFSCME) and as AFL-CIO coordination partners (NTEU), can direct that infrastructure toward civil service defense framing in 2026 midterm voter contact.

**Legislative bipartisan foothold.** The Saving the Civil Service Act (H.R. 492/S. 134) has Republican co-sponsors — Representatives Fitzpatrick and Bacon — and the House has already passed the Protect America's Workforce Act (HR 2550) restoring collective bargaining rights by bipartisan majority. This bipartisan foothold distinguishes civil service from partisan labor fights.

The structural constraint is unprecedented: civil service unions are fighting for democratic institutions in a degraded legal environment for their own organizing. MSPB has been without quorum since May 2025 — federal employee appeals have no administrative tribunal. NLRB union elections fell 30% in 2025 as the Board lost quorum following the *Trump v. Wilcox* shadow docket stay (May 22, 2025). Federal workforce union membership declined approximately 28% since 2024 as the workforce contracted. Dues revenue is declining. The playbook must account for this resource constraint from the first month.

## Actionable Domains: Five Highest-Leverage Areas

### Domain 2 (Civil Service) — Primary Lead

The Schedule Policy/Career framework is the central threat and the anchor litigation. The OPM Final Rule (February 6, 2026, Federal Register Vol. 91, No. 25, pp. 8432-8498) converts approximately 50,000 career civil service positions to at-will employment by reclassifying them as "policy-influencing" — stripping Chapter 75 removal protections, Chapter 43 performance standards, Section 2302 whistleblower protections, and MSPB jurisdiction simultaneously.

The democratic-design argument is load-bearing: the Pendleton Civil Service Reform Act of 1883 was not primarily an employment protection statute. It was the legislative response to the spoils system's conversion of law enforcement, tax collection, and public order into instruments serving only those who supported the governing party. Schedule Policy/Career replicates the functional structure of the spoils system with 21st-century mechanisms.

The AFGE-AFSCME-AFL-CIO Second Amended Complaint (March 4, 2026) runs four counts: statutory authority (5 U.S.C. § 3302 requires a "necessary" finding that blanket reclassification cannot satisfy); due process (vested property interests cannot be stripped without adequate process); APA (rescission of prior regulations without notice-and-comment); First Amendment (targeting employees for speech on civil service matters). NTEU adds a fifth count: the OPM implementing rule is itself an APA substantive rulemaking without notice-and-comment. A government motion to dismiss was heard May 12, 2026; ruling is pending.

Post-*Loper Bright*, courts no longer defer to OPM's statutory interpretation of § 3302. This strengthens the statutory count significantly: the court must independently determine whether "policy-influencing" career positions fall within the historical meaning of the "confidential, policy-determining, policy-making, or policy-advocating" language Congress wrote for political appointees.

### Domain 6 (Judicial Independence) — NLRB Quorum Track

The NLRB quorum loss is the direct union operational consequence of *Trump v. Wilcox* (May 22, 2025, shadow docket). Justice Kagan's dissent was direct: the majority had "effectively repealed *Humphrey's Executor* by fiat" — without briefing, argument, or reasoned opinion. The pending *Trump v. Slaughter* decision (expected June 2026) will convert this functional reality into formal constitutional doctrine.

NLRB quorum loss means civil service unions cannot certify new shops, cannot process unfair labor practice complaints, and cannot enforce existing orders. The organizing pipeline is suspended. State labor boards in New York, California, Illinois, Massachusetts, and Colorado are the immediate alternative; state public employee labor laws — independent of NLRB — continue to function.

The structural fix is the NLRB Independence Act: model legislation converting the NLRB to an Article I court (like the Tax Court in 1969) with supermajority Senate confirmation and for-cause removal protection that survives *Slaughter* for genuinely adjudicatory functions. The constitutional argument: after *Slaughter*, removal protection survives only for Article I tribunals whose functions are adjudicatory rather than executive. NLRB's primary function — adjudicating unfair labor practice complaints — meets this standard.

### Domain 17 (Labor Rights) — PRO Act and Sectoral Bargaining

The PRO Act remains the flagship labor rights legislation, though civil service unions are not its primary constituency. Their electoral mobilization is essential to any PRO Act majority. AFL-CIO's 2026 GOTV political capital should explicitly deploy toward PRO Act passage post-midterms.

Sectoral bargaining pilots in three to five willing states (California, Colorado, New Mexico, New York, Massachusetts) are the medium-term legislative target. California's Fast Food Council (AB 1228, 2023) is the current model: a Wage Board for a specific industry sector whose determinations apply sector-wide, achieving wage compression without individual-shop organizing. AFSCME's state affiliates should push sectoral bargaining pilots as a 2027 session priority in every trifecta state.

Federal minimum wage legislation (not increased since 2009) is the symbolic legislative priority; state minimum wage campaigns are the active track. Civil service unions should support state minimum wage campaigns as an economic justice coalition issue even where it does not affect their members directly.

### Domain 26 (Government Accountability) — Inspector General Defense

Inspectors General are the accountability mechanism for the agencies where AFGE members work. The mass IG firing (January 24, 2025 — at least 17 simultaneous firings by email, violating the 1978 IG Act's 30-day advance notice requirement) was declared unlawful by Judge Ana C. Reyes on September 24, 2025. The administration re-fired the same officials after providing retroactive notice, achieving the same result.

Over 75% of presidentially appointed IG positions are now vacant. The FY2027 budget proposes 12% average IG budget cuts, with Interior and Justice at 28% each. Every dollar invested in IG oversight in 2024 yielded $18 in recovered funds; the proposed cuts eliminate the infrastructure that makes that return possible.

The IG Protection Act — requiring Senate confirmation for IG removal and 30-day written notice — has bipartisan precedent (2008 IG Protection Act). Civil service unions should formalize documentation partnerships with surviving IGs: union representatives should know which IGs are still functioning, how to refer cases to them, and which cases have the strongest factual records for referral.

### Domain 34 (Congressional Power of the Purse) — DOGE Impoundment

DOGE's mass impoundment of appropriated funds is the direct cause of many of the position eliminations civil service unions litigate. $425+ billion in congressional appropriations have been withheld through the four mechanisms documented in Domain 34. GAO opened 39 separate ICA investigation tracks.

The union litigation theory: DOGE impoundment decisions are final agency actions subject to APA arbitrary and capricious review. When an impoundment eliminates a bargaining unit position, it is a de facto reduction in force without the required 60-day notice and retention register rights (5 U.S.C. §§ 3502-3504). The administration's characterization of position elimination as a "funding contingency" rather than a RIF should be contested in every instance.

Three RIF rights class actions should be filed in the first quarter on behalf of employees affected by DOGE impoundment. The APA challenge to specific DOGE impoundment decisions runs in parallel: each impoundment (a) lacked statutory authority under the Impoundment Control Act; (b) was arbitrary and capricious for failure to provide reasoned explanation; (c) violated RIF statutory procedures as a de facto workforce action.

## Year 1 Sequencing

### Months 1-3: Litigation Consolidation and Congressional Mobilization

Month 1: Convene AFGE, NTEU, and AFSCME legal teams for a full-day litigation coordination session. Assign brief responsibility by count: AFGE leads constitutional counts; NTEU leads statutory count; AFSCME leads APA counts. Review *Loper Bright* implications for § 3302 interpretation. Produce joint coordination memo shared with outside counsel and academic partners.

Month 2: Congressional testimony mobilization. AFGE's legislative conference is the primary vehicle. Prepare 10-15 member case studies for House and Senate Judiciary Committee distribution: real federal employees in swing districts whose positions were reclassified, their specific job descriptions, and the agency functions they performed. Package for Republican members representing districts with large federal agency workforces (DoD civilians in Virginia Beach, USDA extension staff in Iowa, VA medical staff in Pennsylvania swing districts). Testimony from AFGE and NTEU presidents should frame around agency capacity loss and public-service delivery failure, not only worker rights.

Month 3: AFSCME state affiliate mobilization. Priority states: Democratic trifectas and states where 2026 midterms could produce trifectas (Michigan, Minnesota, New Mexico). State affiliate objectives: (a) pressure state legislators to enact state civil service protection statutes; (b) AFSCME members as witnesses in state hearings on federal workforce consequences in state programs; (c) state affiliate networks activated for 2026 midterm candidate support.

### Months 3-6: Electoral Integration and MSPB Architecture

Month 4: Electoral integration. AFL-CIO's 2026 GOTV program should explicitly frame the midterms as a civil service defense election. Field-tested voter contact messages for union households in swing states: specific agency functions (benefit processing, tax assistance, veterans healthcare) that are threatened by Schedule Policy/Career reclassification. Avoid abstract democratic language; use concrete agency functions that affect members' families directly.

Month 5: MSPB Reconstruction Act drafting. The MSPB without quorum means 2,100%+ case backlog and no administrative appeal mechanism for wrongfully terminated federal employees. Model legislation: converts MSPB to an Article I tribunal with 14-year staggered terms, 60-vote supermajority Senate confirmation, and expanded jurisdiction covering Schedule Policy/Career reclassification challenges and DOGE impoundment RIF claims.

Month 6: Mid-year review. Which Schedule Policy/Career cases have reached circuit courts? What is the outlook for SCOTUS cert? What is the Senate floor vote timeline for Saving the Civil Service Act?

### Months 7-12: Electoral Mobilization and Post-*Slaughter* Response

Months 7-8: Pre-election mobilization. AFL-CIO household voter contact in full operation. AFGE and NTEU members receive agency-function-based talking points identifying the 30 House districts where federal agency workforces are largest and races are competitive.

Month 9: Post-midterm assessment. Which states achieved Democratic trifectas? Activate state civil service protection legislation for 2027 session in those states immediately. Which Republican members voted against Saving the Civil Service Act? Document for accountability.

Month 10: Senate push on Saving the Civil Service Act. Post-midterm, if Senate composition improved, move to floor. Strategic argument for Republican co-sponsors: merit-based civil service is a bipartisan institution supported by every Republican president from Eisenhower through George W. Bush. Schedule Policy/Career is a radical departure that converts the administrative state into a spoils system.

Month 11: State civil service protection omnibus pre-drafted for any state achieving trifecta in November 2026: state civil service protection statute, state NLRB analogue with constitutional independence protections, state whistleblower protection strengthening.

## Year 2-3 Action

Year 2 priorities, in sequence: (1) Saving the Civil Service Act passage — best vehicle is NDAA attachment since defense civilians are directly affected and NDAA must pass annually; (2) MSPB Reconstruction Act introduction with bipartisan Senate sponsors from states with large federal workforces and federal law enforcement communities; (3) NLRB Independence Act drafted and circulated after *Slaughter* ruling establishes the constitutional architecture need; (4) state civil service protection omnibus enacted in at least two trifecta states.

Year 3 is the constitutional campaign. A constitutional amendment protecting merit-based civil service would: establish that federal employees in positions requiring specialized expertise serve on the basis of merit; provide removal-for-cause rights with appeal to an independent Article I tribunal; vest Congress with authority to define covered positions, preventing executive redefinition by EO. Civil service unions provide the 34-state ratification campaign infrastructure: AFGE state legislative contacts, AFSCME state affiliate networks, AFL-CIO state federation government — the same infrastructure used for electoral GOTV, repurposed for state legislative lobbying.

MSPB backlog clearance is Year 3 operational work: the 2,100%+ backlog will require temporary ALJ appointments funded through a specific MSPB Reconstruction Act provision. Year 3 is also the implementation year for sectoral bargaining pilots in willing states through AFSCME affiliate Wage Board representation.

## Case Studies

**Case Study 1: AFGE Inauguration-Night Litigation Strategy (January 20, 2025)**

AFGE filed suit against Schedule Policy/Career within hours of Trump's inauguration — before the day ended. Legal theory was drafted before the executive order was signed; the filing team inserted facts after signing. This is the operational standard that pre-staged litigation makes possible: the legal argument is not researched after the trigger; it is ready before the trigger, waiting for facts.

The lesson for every constituency: standing memos per domain, scenario decision trees, and brief-quality legal analysis should be complete before the anticipated executive action occurs. The month of time that normal litigation preparation requires is the month in which the administration achieves facts on the ground that courts treat as settled.

**Case Study 2: AFGE-AFSCME-AFL-CIO Second Amended Complaint (March 4, 2026)**

The Second Amended Complaint consolidated AFGE, AFSCME, and AFL-CIO as co-plaintiffs with Democracy Forward, running five independent legal theories across constitutional, statutory, and APA counts. The multi-plaintiff, multi-count structure is deliberately redundant: if one count fails at the circuit court, three others survive for SCOTUS. The legal team — O'Donoghue & O'Brien (AFGE), Bredhoff & Kaiser (AFSCME), and Cohen, Weiss and Simon (AFL-CIO) — represent the top federal labor law firms in the country, ensuring depth of institutional knowledge that outside counsel in commercial practice cannot match.

Government motion to dismiss was heard May 12, 2026. The post-*Loper Bright* statutory count is the strongest: courts must independently interpret § 3302's "necessary" requirement without deference to OPM's self-serving reading.

**Case Study 3: AFL-CIO Bipartisan Federal Worker Vote — Protect America's Workforce Act (2026)**

The AFL-CIO campaign supporting the Protect America's Workforce Act (HR 2550) achieved a bipartisan House majority and a floor vote — the first legislation directly overturning a Trump administration executive order to receive majority support. Model: identify one piece of legislation with documented bipartisan appeal; mobilize the full labor ground game around it; achieve a floor vote; use the vote record for 2026 electoral accountability in affected districts. The PAWA vote demonstrated that federal worker protection can attract Republican co-sponsors when framed around institutional merit-system values and constituent agency employment rather than around partisan labor politics.

## Natural Allies and Conflicts

**Natural allies**: State AGs (parens patriae standing in cooperative-program civil service challenges — the *Maryland v. Noem* template; AGs join as amici or co-plaintiffs on cooperative program standing theory); state legislators (state civil service protection statutes in trifecta states); religious coalitions (economic dignity frame connecting Domains 3, 11, 17; faith hospital Medicaid standing); labor unions broadly (AFL-CIO GOTV and legislative deployment).

**Key conflicts**:

*Cross-union trade policy tension*: Building trades unions historically supported tariffs; civil service unions include customs enforcement officers who administer tariff programs. The *Learning Resources* win eliminated IEEPA tariff authority that some building trades supported. Resolution: reframe as rule-of-law argument, not policy argument. "Congress, not the President, has the power to set tariffs. We support Congress exercising that power to protect manufacturing jobs. We oppose unilateral executive tariff authority — because that same authority could be used to eliminate the labor protections workers depend on."

*NLRB quorum loss affects private sector more than civil service*: Federal employee unions operate under Title 5 and the Federal Labor Relations Authority, not the NLRB. Civil service unions may not prioritize NLRB restoration as urgently as private-sector unions. Resolution: one fight — if the administration can break MSPB quorum, they can break NLRB quorum. Civil service unions should support NLRB Independence Act as a coalition solidarity matter explicitly grounded in the shared constitutional argument about independent adjudicatory authority.

*Competitive civil servants vs. politically exposed members*: AFGE members who testify publicly about Schedule Policy/Career implementation are vulnerable to informal retaliation. Resolution: protect witnesses through a secure nomination process, provide legal counsel through the union's legal program, and have a retaliation response team ready. When any member faces retaliation for union activity or congressional testimony, file an FLRA unfair labor practice charge and an OSC complaint within 48 hours.

## Key Constraints

**Post-*Slaughter* legal environment.** MSPB without quorum means civil service appeals have nowhere to go administratively. Federal employees must go directly to federal district court — slower, more expensive, and beyond most employees' individual resources. This makes the union's collective litigation function more essential, not less, but it also concentrates resource requirements.

**Resource depletion.** Federal workforce union membership declined 28% since 2024. Dues revenue is declining. The total incremental litigation and advocacy costs beyond existing programs — for SCOTUS preparation, member case study research, MSPB Reconstruction Act drafting, and civil service electoral messaging — should run $500,000-$750,000 annually and should be funded through the AFL-CIO solidarity fund and coordination with Protect Democracy.

**Cross-union coordination structure.** Civil service unions must maintain a separate joint structure from broader AFL-CIO on federal-specific issues, particularly Schedule Policy/Career litigation where the legal theory is specific to Title 5 and federal employment law. At the same time, electoral mobilization runs through the unified AFL-CIO structure. The joint litigation coordination memo (Month 1) is the mechanism that keeps these tracks aligned without collapsing them.

**Degraded organizing environment.** NLRB quorum loss means new shop organizing is effectively suspended at the federal level. State labor boards in priority states should be identified as the immediate organizing pathway. Any new organizing campaigns in the 2026-2027 window should be channeled to state public employee organizing — which operates under state law, not NLRB — until federal board capacity is restored.
