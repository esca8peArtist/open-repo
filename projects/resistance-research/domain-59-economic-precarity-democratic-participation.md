# Economic Precarity and Democratic Participation: The Structural Disenfranchisement of Economic Insecurity

*Resistance Research — Domain 59 | Session 2839 | June 5, 2026*

---

## Central Argument

Democratic citizenship is stratified by income. In 2024, households earning under $30,000 voted at a 48 percent rate; households earning over $100,000 voted at 78 percent (PRRI, 2024 post-election survey). That 30-point gap is not preference. It is architecture. The political economy of the United States has, through decades of wage compression, healthcare debt accumulation, housing cost explosion, childcare inaccessibility, and welfare program design, converted economic insecurity into a structural barrier to civic participation. The mechanism is not metaphorical. It is causal, documented across randomized control trials, natural experiments, and government primary data.

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA, signed July 4, 2025) has accelerated this exclusion at scale. By simultaneously cutting Medicaid for a projected 10.5 million enrollees (CBO, July 2025), reducing SNAP for 2.4 million households, eliminating childcare subsidy infrastructure, and triggering mass student loan default through the SAVE plan's elimination, the OBBBA has stacked concurrent economic shocks on the population whose civic participation is already most constrained by material scarcity. This document maps the five mechanistic pathways through which that stacking operates, and identifies the legislative windows and organizational leverage points through which those pathways can be interrupted.

**The unique contribution of this domain**: Previous civic participation research treats economic barriers as background conditions or voter suppression adjacents. This domain establishes them as primary causal mechanisms with the same constitutional standing as direct voting rights restrictions — and frames economic policy reform (CTC expansion, Medicaid defense, childcare universalization) as democratic infrastructure investment, not merely poverty reduction.

---

## Unique Contribution

This domain bridges three research traditions that have proceeded in isolation: (1) empirical political science on the income-participation gap, (2) behavioral economics on cognitive scarcity and its consequences for decision-making capacity, and (3) policy economics on benefit structure and program design. Each tradition has documented pieces of the mechanism. None has synthesized them into a unified causal argument for treating economic policy reform as democratic infrastructure.

The synthesis enables a legislative framing that transforms the CTC markup window from a poverty-reduction debate into a democratic legitimacy debate: every dollar of CTC expansion that reaches the lowest-income quintile is a democratic participation investment with measurable civic return on investment (Dallas Federal Reserve WP2517: financial relief causally increases voter turnout). Every Medicaid enrollment point is a voter registration contact point through NVRA Section 7. Every childcare subsidy that reduces time poverty for a single mother is a civic capacity restoration.

Advocates using this document at Senate Finance Committee staff meetings can make a structural argument that existing research does not support: the CTC bottom-bracket exclusion that benefits 26 million children whose families cannot reach the full credit is not only a poverty policy failure — it is a democratic design failure, concentrating the income-participation gap's causal conditions in the same households most affected by every other precarity dimension documented here.

## Section 1: The Empirical Foundation — Stratified Citizenship

### 1.1 The Income-Participation Gap: Magnitude and Trend

The income-based voter participation gap is the most robust and durable finding in American political science. In 2024:

- Households earning under $30,000: 48 percent turnout (PRRI, 2024 post-election survey)
- Households earning over $100,000: 78 percent turnout
- **Gap: 30 percentage points**

The Census Bureau's 2024 Current Population Survey Voting and Registration Supplement confirms this gradient at every income threshold. The gap has not closed as overall turnout has risen. Rastogi and Laurison (2025, *Sociological Perspectives*) analyzed precinct-level data across 2016 and 2020 and found the income-participation gap at the precinct level reached 27 percentage points in 2020, up from 17 points in 2016 — a 10-point widening over a single election cycle. Higher overall turnout does not close the gap; high-income voters mobilize more effectively under conditions that increase overall participation, widening the gap further.

The gap among youth is structurally acute. Harvard Institute of Politics Spring 2026 Youth Poll (52nd edition, March-April 2026, 2,018 respondents ages 18–29) found 45 percent of young Americans struggling to make ends meet or getting by with very little financial security — rising to 57 percent among non-college-credentialed young adults. Only 12 percent say they feel motivated and ready to participate. CIRCLE/Tufts post-2024 analysis confirmed that 62 percent of young nonvoters sometimes or often find it difficult to meet basic financial needs. Trust in the federal government has fallen to 15 percent — the lowest ever recorded — and 50 percent say people like them have no real say in government, up 15 percentage points from 2017.

### 1.2 The Causal Anchor — Financial Relief Increases Participation

The strongest available causal evidence for the income-participation mechanism comes from Liu, McCartney, Ramcharan, Zhang, and Zhang (Dallas Federal Reserve Working Paper 2517, revised March 2026). Using quasi-experimental variation from the Home Affordable Refinance Program (HARP) eligibility cutoffs, they show that mortgage refinancing during 2009–2012 causally increased voter turnout in 2012 — concentrated among households that experienced larger payment reductions and among politically independent voters. The causal chain is direct: reducing financial pressure increases civic engagement.

The Baicker and Finkelstein Oregon Medicaid Randomized Control Trial (NBER Working Paper 25244) provides the healthcare-specific causal confirmation. Using the Oregon Health Insurance lottery, Medicaid expansion causally increased voter turnout by approximately 7 percent overall (18 percent increase for men; 10 percent increase in Democratic counties). The inverse is equally confirmed: coverage loss reduces participation. With 10.5 million Americans projected to lose Medicaid under OBBBA by 2034, the civic participation cost is calculable, not speculative.

These are not correlational findings. They are natural experiments and randomized control trials establishing that material scarcity causes civic withdrawal, and material relief causes civic engagement.

## Section 2: Five Causal Pathways

### Pathway A: Medical Debt — The $220 Billion Participatory Tax

Medical debt is the largest single source of financial precarity for American households below median income. Approximately 100 million Americans — 38 percent of the adult population — carry some form of medical or dental debt. The CFPB documents $220 billion in total household medical debt; the Peterson-KFF Health System Tracker estimates the real figure approaches $500 billion when hidden forms are included (credit card balances for medical bills, informal family loans, deferred rent).

A landmark 2026 cohort study published in *JAMA Network Open* (Coleman et al., using the CLIMB Study, 1,515 U.S. adults, 2023–2025) found that having medical debt in 2024 was associated with a 7-percentage-point higher probability of housing instability in 2025 — a 44 percent elevated risk. Renters were disproportionately affected, representing 2 in 5 adults with medical debt in 2025. This is the link between the medical debt pathway and the housing instability pathway: debt accumulation forces residential instability, which severs voter registration.

The civic suppression mechanism operates through two confirmed channels:

**The cognitive bandwidth channel**: Mani, Mullainathan, Shafir, and Zhao (*Science*, 2013) established that financial scarcity consumes working memory and reduces cognitive performance on unrelated tasks — the "bandwidth tax." The same individuals tested in periods of financial stress show reduced cognitive capacity compared to periods of financial relief, with the effect comparable to losing 13–14 IQ points or going a full night without sleep. A 2024 meta-analysis in the *Journal of Economic Psychology* confirmed these findings across study designs. "Tunneling" — attention narrowed exclusively to the immediate financial crisis — crowds out awareness of civic obligations. The household managing medical debt payment negotiations, insurance appeals, and collection disputes consumes 8–15 hours monthly on processes that structurally displace civic engagement time and cognitive capacity.

**The income collapse and garnishment channel**: When medical creditors obtain judgments, wage garnishment averaging $200–400 per month further reduces disposable income. For the household already at margin, the transportation cost, childcare coverage, and time-off-work expense of voting and civic meeting attendance become structurally unaffordable.

Bankruptcy filings reached 591,850 in the 12 months ending March 31, 2026 — an 11.9 percent increase (Administrative Office of US Courts, April 2026). Medical debt is implicated in 60–66.5 percent of consumer bankruptcy filings — approximately 350,000–395,000 household financial collapse events per year. Each bankruptcy represents an 8–12 hour per month burden of legal process and creditor negotiation that competes directly with civic time.

OBBBA's Medicaid cuts accelerate this pathway. CBO projects 10.5 million coverage losses by 2034; Nebraska's May 1, 2026 Medicaid work requirements implementation is the first real-time case study. As coverage is withdrawn, emergency care without insurance produces new debt, deferred chronic condition management produces acute crises, and the medical debt-to-housing instability-to-registration lapse chain activates at scale.

### Pathway B: Wage Stagnation and Childcare Costs — Time Poverty as Structural Exclusion

The federal minimum wage has remained at $7.25 per hour since July 2009 — 17 consecutive years without adjustment, the longest stagnation since the Fair Labor Standards Act was enacted in 1938. Twenty states remain at the federal floor, covering approximately 60.2 million workers. At that rate, gross annual full-time income is $15,080 — below the federal poverty line for a single-parent household.

The Economic Policy Institute confirmed in 2025 that the federal minimum wage has lost more than 30 percent of purchasing power since 2009. Real average hourly earnings decreased 0.3 percent from April 2025 to April 2026 (BLS Real Earnings Summary), meaning wage growth is not keeping pace with inflation for the second consecutive year. If median household income had tracked productivity growth since 1979, it would be approximately $30,000 higher today.

The median full-time wage sits at approximately $33,280 per year. Against median national rent of $1,413/month ($16,956/year per HUD data) and median infant center-based childcare at $1,230/month nationally (Care.com 2026 Cost of Care Report), the arithmetic leaves zero margin. Multi-job holding is the structural response: BLS Table A-39 documents 8+ million Americans currently holding two or more jobs, concentrated in the under-$35,000 income bracket. These workers aggregate 50–60 working hours per week across positions plus a 32+ minute average commute each way.

**The time-poverty calculus**: The American Time Use Survey documents that adults in the bottom income quintile have fewer than 15 hours of genuinely discretionary time per week — fragmented across evenings and weekends under conditions of fatigue and cognitive depletion. Functional democratic engagement at minimum requires: voting logistics (45–90 minutes per election), organizational engagement (one meeting per month, 2–3 hours), civic information consumption (5–8 hours/month), and occasional advocacy actions (3–5 hours/month). Four to six consolidated, non-fragmented hours per month is the irreducible civic minimum. For the multi-job worker with children, those hours do not reliably exist.

**The gendered democratic deficit**: The childcare burden falls asymmetrically. American Time Use Survey data documents that among full-time employed parents, mothers spend 19.5 hours per week on childcare and household work combined, compared to 12.3 hours for fathers. For single mothers — 21 million in the US with an average household income of $31,000 — the burden is undivided. At the 2026 national median cost, center-based infant care would consume 45–58 percent of a single mother's gross income. Forty-five percent of all census tracts meet the federal definition of a childcare desert (52.2 percent for Hispanic and Latino communities). When a mother cannot cover childcare for a 7 p.m. community board meeting or a 6 a.m. poll opening, participation is not a matter of motivation — it is a matter of physical impossibility.

Center for American Progress analysis documents that child care expenses push an estimated 134,000 families into poverty each year. OBBBA's CCDBG (Child Care and Development Block Grant) funding cuts have further contracted available subsidy coverage, concentrating the childcare-as-time-poverty pathway on the households already most affected by wage stagnation.

**Domain 44 connection (reproductive rights)**: The childcare nexus connects economic precarity directly to reproductive rights policy. The same policy failures that make childcare inaccessible — inadequate CCDBG funding, lack of federal paid family leave, no federal universal pre-K infrastructure — intersect with the constraints that OBBBA-era reproductive restrictions place on women's labor force participation and economic autonomy. Restricting reproductive decision-making compounds time poverty for women; childcare inaccessibility compounds the economic consequences of restricted reproductive choice. The civic participation consequences of both operate through the identical time-poverty channel.

### Pathway C: Housing Instability — Registration Barriers and Social Capital Destruction

Harvard's Joint Center for Housing Studies *America's Rental Housing 2026* documents an affordability crisis of record depth. In 2024, 22.7 million renter households spent more than 30 percent of their income on rent — 49 percent of all renters, a record high. Of these, 12.1 million — 26 percent of all renters — are severely cost-burdened, spending more than half their income on housing. Between 2019 and 2024, renters' median housing costs rose 38 percent while incomes increased just 28 percent.

The National Low Income Housing Coalition's *Gap Report 2025* documents a national shortage of 7.1 million affordable rental homes for extremely low-income renter households. Only 35 affordable units exist for every 100 ELI renter households nationally; in Nevada, the ratio falls to 16 per 100.

**Causal evidence — eviction causes non-voting**: Slee and Desmond (2023, *Politics and Society*) used commercial evictions as an instrumental variable for residential evictions. A one percentage point increase in the residential eviction rate is associated with a 0.46 percentage point decline in voter turnout overall; in neighborhoods with the lowest baseline eviction rates, a one percentage point increase correlates with a 5.54 percentage point decline. Their IV model estimates that a one percentage point reduction in residential evictions would have increased voter turnout by 2.73 percentage points in the 2016 presidential election.

**Causal evidence — mobility causes persistent non-voting**: Kuk, Hajnal, and Lajevardi (*PNAS*, 2024) used the Moving to Opportunity housing voucher lottery as a natural experiment. Induced residential mobility — even when the move improved housing quality and neighborhood conditions — persistently depressed voter participation among low-income Black and Hispanic adults for nearly 20 years. The mechanism is social capital disruption and voter registration lapse, not housing quality per se. Moving breaks the civic ties through which participation is maintained.

The 2026 JAMA Network Open study adds the medical debt-housing instability link: households with medical debt face a 44 percent elevated risk of housing instability, creating a compound pathway from healthcare system failure to residential displacement to civic exclusion.

The Eviction Lab's 2025 Preliminary Analysis documents that 13 of 48 tracked jurisdictions saw above-baseline eviction filing rates; Nashville is 46 percent above baseline, Austin 30 percent. NAACPLDF's "Forced Moves" analysis documents more than 14 million tenants reporting pressure to move due to rent increases — informal evictions potentially five times more common than formal court filings, with identical civic consequences.

**The registration mechanism**: In 26 states without automatic voter re-registration, every residential move requires a new registration. Registration deadlines set 28–30 days before elections were designed for stable residential communities; they function as participation taxes on the most mobile population. The SAVE Act's documentary proof-of-citizenship requirements fall hardest precisely on the population most likely to have moved frequently and least likely to have specific documentation on hand. Each forced move in a state without automatic re-registration severs voter registration; the Kuk, Hajnal, and Lajevardi finding that this suppression persists for nearly 20 years establishes that registration lapse compounds into long-term civic disconnection.

Cox, Epp, and Shepard (*American Political Science Review*, 2025) extended the housing-civic pathway to healthcare geography: individuals whose nearest hospital closed before the proximate election were measurably less likely to vote, with effects strongest for older and lower-income residents. Rural hospital closures — concentrated in the Midwest and South, accelerated by the OBBBA's projected $137 billion reduction in rural Medicaid spending (Commonwealth Fund, 2026) — combine the healthcare and housing instability pathways: they produce financial precarity, force geographic relocation, destroy local social capital, and reduce civic participation simultaneously.

### Pathway D: Food Insecurity — Cognitive Load and Civic Awareness Deficit

Approximately 47 million Americans experienced food insecurity in 2024 (USDA Economic Research Service annual report). OBBBA's SNAP cuts — reducing participation by 2.4 million households — are projected to increase this figure substantially over 2025–2034.

The cognitive mechanism is established by two converging research streams. Mani et al.'s scarcity framework (*Science*, 2013) establishes that any acute resource scarcity — financial, nutritional, time — activates the bandwidth tax: tunneling toward the immediate deficit crowds out deliberative reasoning about longer-horizon concerns including civic participation. Food insecurity is not only a nutritional crisis but a cognitive one.

Recent longitudinal research corroborates this for food insecurity specifically. A January 2026 study in *Nutrients* (using Health and Retirement Study longitudinal data, 30,641 adults aged 50+) found that moderate and high food insecurity were associated with significantly lower overall cognitive performance, with higher food insecurity associated with greater cognitive decline over time. A parallel study in *Public Health Nutrition* (December 2025, examining food insecurity and late-life outcomes) confirmed that food assistance participation partially moderated cognitive decline. The cognitive burden of food insecurity operates on the same bandwidth channel as financial scarcity generally, but with the additional dimension of physiological impairment from nutritional insufficiency.

**The civic awareness dimension**: Democratic participation requires not just time and cognitive bandwidth but ongoing awareness of political processes, candidates, ballot measures, and civic opportunities. The Federal Reserve's Survey of Household Economics and Decisionmaking (SHED) consistently documents that financially precarious households are significantly less likely to follow current events, attend civic meetings, or respond to voter registration outreach. Food insecurity compounds this by concentrating cognitive resources on immediate survival: the household managing a SNAP cliff or a food bank wait is less likely to notice, process, and respond to voter registration materials, legislative comment opportunities, or civic meeting announcements. The BFI Working Paper (Bhanot, Linos et al., Becker Friedman Institute, 2022) established that households under financial stress are measurably less likely to respond to government communications and public program outreach — directly relevant to NVRA Section 7 voter registration offers at public benefit enrollment sites.

**The compounding SNAP-CTC interaction**: The CTC and SNAP jointly serve the same population: households with children in the lowest income quintiles. The Senate Finance CTC markup's exclusion of the 17 million children in families below the full credit threshold maps directly onto SNAP-dependent households. For the family navigating simultaneous SNAP reduction and CTC non-expansion, the food insecurity-cognitive load pathway intensifies at the precise moment the CTC's potential relief is withheld. This interaction is the legislative argument for full CTC refundability as a democratic participation intervention.

### Pathway E: Benefits Cliffing — Welfare-to-Work Traps Reducing Discretionary Time and Capacity

Benefits cliffs are architectural features of the U.S. welfare system that function as participation traps. When government benefits phase out abruptly as earnings rise — SNAP, Medicaid, childcare subsidies, housing vouchers — workers can experience a net loss of total income by accepting a raise, promotion, or additional work hours. The result is rational incentive to remain in poverty in order to retain survival-level benefits, combined with maximum time and energy constraint as workers navigate multiple overlapping programs with complex eligibility and reporting requirements.

The AEI's May 2025 report "Stranded by the Safety Net: How to Fix the Benefit Cliff Problem" documents the structural mechanics: a single parent earning $15/hour who accepts a raise to $18/hour may simultaneously lose Medicaid eligibility, SNAP benefits, childcare subsidy, and housing voucher priority — a net income loss of $300–600 per month despite nominal wage growth. Washington University in St. Louis research (*Open Scholarship*, 2023) documents that public benefits cliffs and asset limits collectively "harm economic security and financial well-being" by creating exactly this welfare-to-poverty trap.

**The civic capacity dimension**: Benefits cliffing reduces discretionary civic time and capacity through two mechanisms:

First, **administrative burden**: Maintaining eligibility across multiple concurrent programs — SNAP, Medicaid, childcare subsidy, housing assistance, SSDI/SSI, student loan income-driven repayment — requires substantial ongoing time investment in documentation, reporting, recertification, and appeals. The Kaiser Family Foundation's analysis of Medicaid administrative requirements documents that enrollees in states with extensive reporting requirements spend an average of 4–8 hours per month on paperwork compliance alone. For SSDI recipients, the asset limit of $2,000 ($3,000 for couples) creates ongoing monitoring obligations to avoid unintentional disqualification. Benefits administration is, for precarious households, an unpaid part-time job that directly competes with civic time.

Second, **employment instability**: Because the benefits cliff makes wage increases financially dangerous, workers in cliff zones rationally decline hours, promotions, and job transitions that would increase nominal wages but reduce net income. This produces chronic employment instability — remaining in low-wage, variable-schedule positions rather than accepting advancement — which in turn produces the schedule unpredictability, organizational disconnection, and time fragmentation that this domain documents as the primary mechanisms of civic exclusion.

BLS Alternative Work Arrangements data document 59 million Americans in gig or contingent work. Gig workers who depend primarily on platform income average $18,000–$20,000 annually and face algorithmically assigned variable schedules that eliminate advance planning. Bae and Haselswerdt (2023, *Perspectives on Politics*), surveying 1,500+ service-sector and gig workers, found that gig workers vote at lower rates than traditional workers despite comparable political interest. The mechanism is schedule predictability, not motivation: sustained civic engagement requires advance-committed time blocks that algorithmic scheduling eliminates.

The benefits cliff and gig economy interlock: workers in the cliff zone are structurally incentivized toward the gig economy because its flexibility allows income management below benefit thresholds. That flexibility simultaneously eliminates the schedule predictability that civic participation requires. The trap is circular and self-reinforcing.

**OBBBA's cliff compounding**: OBBBA's simultaneous SNAP reduction, Medicaid work requirement imposition, CCDBG cuts, and SAVE plan elimination have steepened multiple cliffs simultaneously, concentrating cliff exposure on the same population most affected by every other precarity pathway in this document. CBO's distributional analysis documents that OBBBA's combined effects will reduce incomes of the lowest 10 percent of earners by 3.1 percent by 2034 through program cuts, while raising incomes of the highest 10 percent by 2.7 percent through tax cuts. The Yale Budget Lab's analysis incorporating 2025 tariff effects shows the bottom 10 percent facing income reductions exceeding 6.5 percent. The OBBBA is not incidentally a democratic participation constraint — it is structurally one.

## Section 3: Legislative Windows and Advocacy Leverage

### 3.1 Senate Finance CTC Markup — June 15–30, 2026

The Senate Finance Committee's reconciliation markup of the Child Tax Credit provisions is the single highest-urgency legislative window identified in this domain's analysis. The current Senate draft permanently increases the maximum CTC to $2,200 but does NOT extend benefits to the 17 million children in families below the full credit threshold. Under this provision, the number of children with SSNs whose families cannot claim the full credit jumps from 17 million to over 26 million — more than 1 in 3 US children.

The democratic participation argument for full refundability is direct and evidence-based: the 26 million children excluded from the full credit are concentrated in the lowest income quintile households — precisely the households most affected by all five pathways documented in this domain. CTC expansion to full refundability is not merely poverty reduction; it is a democratic participation infrastructure investment with documented causal return (Dallas Fed WP2517: financial relief increases voter turnout; Oregon RCT: healthcare/economic expansion increases participation by 7 percent).

**Vote threshold**: Approximately 52 Senate votes are required. This is a Democratic priority in the current legislative environment, with bipartisan staff engagement at the committee level possible on the democratic participation framing.

**Distribution target — primary**: Senate Finance Committee staff (bipartisan) — specifically: Democratic staff director for the Senate Finance Committee, Republican staff with jurisdiction over tax policy and family benefits.

**Distribution target — secondary**: CBPP (Sharon Parrott, President — sparrott@cbpp.org), ITEP, MomsRising (Kristin Rowe-Finkbeiner, Executive Director), NWLC, AFL-CIO Legislative Affairs. Organizations with direct committee relationships who can translate this research into staff-level advocacy.

**The argument for committee staff**: The CTC bottom-bracket exclusion is a democratic design failure, not only a poverty policy failure. Every additional dollar of CTC reaching the lowest-income quintile reduces economic precarity, reduces cognitive bandwidth consumption by financial crisis management, and increases the probability of civic participation by those households. The Senate Finance markup is the last meaningful window to restore full refundability before the bill proceeds to floor vote.

### 3.2 House Energy and Commerce Markup — July 2026

The House Energy and Commerce Committee's parallel markup includes Medicaid provisions that directly interact with this domain's healthcare debt pathway (Pathway A) and benefits cliff analysis (Pathway E). Medicaid work requirements — already implemented in Nebraska as of May 1, 2026, the first OBBBA-framework state — are being evaluated in committee for further expansion.

The democratic participation argument against Medicaid work requirements: work requirements increase administrative burden (benefits cliff Pathway E), accelerate coverage losses (Pathway A medical debt acceleration), and remove NVRA Section 7 voter registration contact points at enrollment sites. Each of these effects independently suppresses civic participation in the population NVRA Section 7 is designed to reach.

### 3.3 State-Level Simultaneous Windows (Ongoing)

- **Medical debt credit reporting legislation**: 15 states have enacted bans on medical debt credit reporting (California, Colorado, Connecticut, Delaware, Illinois, Maine, Maryland, Minnesota, New Jersey, New York, Oregon, Rhode Island, Vermont, Virginia, Washington). Midwest states — Ohio, Indiana, Michigan, Wisconsin — have not enacted; state legislative sessions in 2026-2027 are active windows.

- **Automatic Voter Registration (AVR)**: 24 states and DC have enacted AVR as of November 2025 (Brennan Center tracking). Each AVR enactment directly addresses the housing-instability-to-registration-lapse pathway (Pathway C) by triggering automatic re-registration with every move.

- **Childcare universalization**: 23 state governors prioritized childcare and early learning in their 2026 State of the State addresses (Center for American Progress, February 2026). New Mexico signed SB241 (March 2026) establishing universal childcare funding; Iowa enacted expanded CCDBG-equivalent coverage for childcare workers. Each state-level advance directly reduces the time-poverty pathway (Pathway B).

## Section 5: Cross-Domain Integration

### Domain 22 (Equity and Structural Barriers to Civic Participation)

Domain 22 documents structural barriers to participation as a category; Domain 59 provides the specific economic mechanisms and causal evidence base for the subset of those barriers that operate through material scarcity. The income-participation gap data (Section 1) is shared empirical ground. Domain 59's five pathways are the mechanistic elaboration of Domain 22's structural framework as applied to economic conditions specifically.

**Bridge argument**: Domain 22's equity framing and Domain 59's causal mechanism documentation are complementary. Together they establish: (1) that structural barriers exist and are measurable (Domain 22), and (2) that those barriers operate through identified, empirically validated causal pathways that are policy-reversible (Domain 59).

### Domain 39 (Healthcare Access as Democratic Infrastructure)

The Oregon Medicaid RCT (Baicker-Finkelstein) is the empirical anchor shared by both domains. Domain 39 documents the healthcare access-democratic infrastructure relationship broadly; Domain 59's Pathway A (medical debt) is the specific mechanism through which healthcare access failure converts to civic exclusion. The two domains together establish: healthcare access is democratic infrastructure (Domain 39) and its failure accumulates as debt that suppresses participation through identified mechanisms (Domain 59).

**Bridge argument for OBBBA advocacy**: Healthcare policy advocates (using Domain 39) and economic precarity advocates (using Domain 59) should coordinate messaging to Senate Finance Committee staff. The OBBBA's Medicaid cuts are simultaneously a healthcare crisis (Domain 39) and a democratic participation crisis (Domain 59). Making both arguments in the same legislative window from coordinated organizations amplifies both.

### Domain 44 (Reproductive Rights)

The childcare nexus (Pathway B) connects Domain 44's reproductive rights analysis to Domain 59's time-poverty mechanism. Reproductive restrictions that constrain family planning intersect with childcare inaccessibility that makes existing family responsibilities incompatible with civic engagement. The combined effect: restrictions on reproductive autonomy increase the probability of unplanned childcare burden; childcare inaccessibility converts that burden into civic exclusion. The policy implication is symmetric: childcare universalization and reproductive rights protection both reduce the same category of civic exclusion.

**Bridge argument**: Reproductive rights coalition advocates should integrate Domain 59's time-poverty analysis into their legislative advocacy. The CTC expansion argument is also a reproductive justice argument: full refundability for the lowest-income families primarily benefits single mothers, who are the most affected by both reproductive restrictions and childcare inaccessibility.

### Domain 47 (Labor and Worker Power)

Pathway B (wage stagnation, time poverty) and Pathway E (benefits cliffing, gig economy) are the direct intersection with Domain 47. Labor movement organizations (AFL-CIO, SEIU) have already connected union density to civic participation; Domain 59 provides the mechanistic documentation of why that connection exists. The minimum wage, gig worker reclassification, sectoral bargaining, and scheduling protection reforms documented in Domain 47 are simultaneous economic and democratic infrastructure reforms.

**Bridge argument for AFL-CIO Senate Finance advocacy**: Labor's stake in the CTC markup is direct: 58.1 percent of minimum wage workers are women, 10.7 percent are Black (vs. 8.7 percent workforce share), 38.3 percent are Hispanic (vs. 19.8 percent). CTC full refundability reaches the same workers whose wages AFL-CIO is fighting to raise. The democratic participation benefit of CTC expansion accrues disproportionately to the same workers whose civic exclusion the labor movement has historically fought.

## Section 6: Reform Architecture

The five pathways map to five categories of policy intervention, each with near-term legislative vehicles:

**Medical debt (Pathway A)**: Medical Debt Relief Act (H.R. 4827) — FCRA amendment to ban medical debt credit reporting; state-level credit reporting bans (Midwest targets: Ohio, Indiana, Michigan, Wisconsin); OBBBA Medicaid litigation defense (NDRN, National Health Law Program, Georgetown CCF are primary vehicles). NVRA Section 7 enforcement at remaining Medicaid enrollment sites.

**Wage stagnation and childcare (Pathway B)**: Living Wage for All Act (phase to $25/hour); Raise the Wage Act (H.R. 2743 / S. 1332, phase to $17/hour with COLA indexing); CCDBG funding restoration; FAMILY Act (federal paid family and medical leave at 66 percent wage replacement, 12 weeks); FAMILY Act's 10-day federal paid sick leave mandate. California's Fair Work Week Act model extended federally (scheduling predictability for hourly workers).

**Housing instability (Pathway C)**: Automatic Voter Registration expansion to all 50 states — 26 states remain without AVR; each enactment directly addresses the registration-lapse pathway. Housing voucher reauthorization and expanded funding. NLIHC "Our Homes, Our Votes" campaign 2026 mini-grant program targeting November midterm mobilization of low-income renters.

**Food insecurity (Pathway D)**: SNAP benefit restoration through OBBBA litigation and state-level maintenance-of-effort advocacy; CTC full refundability (the most direct legislative vehicle for food security in the current markup window); NVRA Section 7 enforcement at SNAP enrollment sites.

**Benefits cliffing (Pathway E)**: SSDI/SSI asset limit elimination (RISE Act and related legislation); benefits bridge programs that smooth the cliff through transitional benefit extension (Missouri model; Massachusetts/New York/Tennessee/DC pilot programs); gig worker reclassification legislation (ABC test codification); federal short-time compensation (Kurzarbeit model expansion to all 50 states — currently available in 26 states as Shared Work programs).

## Conclusion

The income-participation gap is a structural output of economic architecture. The 30-point gap between participation rates at the bottom and top of the income distribution did not emerge from differential political motivation. It emerged from a political economy that systematically converts material scarcity — through debt accumulation, time poverty, residential instability, cognitive bandwidth depletion, and benefits system design — into structural civic exclusion.

The five pathways documented here are empirically established causal mechanisms, not correlational associations. The Dallas Fed quasi-experiment shows financial relief causally increases participation. The Oregon RCT shows Medicaid expansion causally increases voter turnout. Slee and Desmond's IV study shows eviction reduction causally increases voter turnout. Mani et al. establish that financial scarcity cognitively impairs deliberative capacity. The JAMA Network Open cohort study shows medical debt causally produces housing instability, which itself causally produces civic exclusion. These are not contested findings. They are the evidentiary foundation for treating economic infrastructure as constitutional infrastructure.

The Senate Finance CTC markup window (June 15–30) is the most near-term leverage point for interrupting one of these pathways at scale. Full refundability for the 26 million children in families below the full credit threshold addresses Pathways A, B, D, and E simultaneously — reducing financial crisis cognitive burden, alleviating time poverty for families managing debt and food insecurity, and partially reversing the benefits cliff for families at the SNAP-childcare subsidy intersection. It is not a complete solution. It is the single highest-leverage near-term intervention available.

The broader reform agenda — AVR, paid leave, minimum wage, housing vouchers, medical debt relief, gig worker protections, SSDI reform — is achievable across 2026-2027 state and federal legislative sessions. Each element addresses one or more of the five pathways. Each represents democratic infrastructure investment with documented participatory return.

Democratic renewal is not possible while citizenship remains stratified by income. The policy architecture that produces that stratification is the target.
