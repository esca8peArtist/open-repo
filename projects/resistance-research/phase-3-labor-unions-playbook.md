# Phase 3 Playbook: Labor Unions

**Audience**: AFL-CIO political directors, SEIU organizing staff, state federation presidents, building trades political action coordinators, union voting rights committee leads

---

## Executive Summary

The January 2025 SEIU reaffiliation reunified labor for the first time since 2005, creating an organizational structure that can assign domain-specific lead responsibility across the full democratic defense framework. The combined AFL-CIO/SEIU infrastructure reaches 16+ million members and 8 million household voters in swing states. The May 2026 leverage windows are immediate: the "Labor 2026" campaign launched by the South Florida AFL-CIO on May 16 demonstrates that local affiliates are already moving toward an integrated affordable housing and voter power model that should be replicated in every competitive district. The ICE/CBP reconciliation markup (week of May 19) with its June 1 Senate passage target is the most consequential funding event for the November 2026 midterm — the 287(g) expansion it funds is the primary voter intimidation mechanism documented in the litigation tracker. Labor's $38.2 billion reconciliation opposition window closes when the bill passes the Senate floor. On voting rights, the Callais cascade has eliminated the federal VRA as a reliable protection for minority union members in redistricted states; labor's voting rights committees must pivot from federal litigation support to state constitutional amendment campaigns (the Illinois model) within the next 60 days. The NLRB quorum loss is simultaneously labor's direct operational problem and its most compelling witness testimony for democratic erosion hearings.

## Ecosystem Map

**National structure**: AFL-CIO ([aflcio.org](https://aflcio.org/)) — 12.5 million members across 57 national and international unions. SEIU ([seiu.org](https://www.seiu.org/)) — 3.8 million members, reaffiliated January 2025. Combined reach: approximately 20+ million union members, 8 million household voters in electoral swing states.

**Political infrastructure**: AFL-CIO's political program mobilized 8 million voters in 2022; the 2026 program is targeting unprecedented ground game with SEIU's community-based organizing capacity now integrated. South Florida AFL-CIO's "Labor 2026" campaign (launched May 16, 2026) — framing affordable housing anger into voter power — is the organizational model for the coordinated local-national structure.

**Building trades**: North America's Building Trades Unions (NABTU) — 3 million construction workers. Historically aligned with infrastructure investment and tariff protections; diverges from AFL-CIO on climate transition speed and immigration enforcement. The just transition provisions in Domain 15 (environmental justice) are the bridge mechanism. Building trades are the politically persuadable constituency within labor.

**Key unions by domain**:
- Voting rights: AFSCME (1.6 million state/local government workers who administer elections), CWA (communications workers documenting information infrastructure), UAW (manufacturing states redistricted by Callais cascade).
- Healthcare: SEIU Healthcare, 1199 SEIU United Healthcare Workers East — directly affected by OBBBA Medicaid cuts.
- Federal workforce: AFGE (1.3 million federal workers), NTEU (150,000+ Treasury/IRS/CBP workers) — civil service unions acting as operational partners.

**State federation infrastructure**: Every state has an AFL-CIO federation; in swing states (Pennsylvania, Michigan, Wisconsin, Nevada, Arizona, Georgia, North Carolina), state federation presidents are among the most influential political actors in Democratic coalition management.

## Strategic Opportunities

**Opportunity 1: ICE/CBP reconciliation Senate opposition (window closes June 1)**

The reconciliation bill funding ICE at $38.2 billion with 287(g) expansion passes the Senate on approximately June 1 unless floor opposition materializes. Labor has three specific leverage points: (1) AFGE's customs enforcement members administer the very systems being expanded; AFGE testimony that the expansion threatens worker safety and professional standards is the most credible internal voice against the bill. (2) SEIU Healthcare members in border communities are the direct recipients of the enforcement surge the reconciliation funds. (3) AFL-CIO Resolution on Voting Rights (passed with SEIU integrated) commits the federation to opposing measures that function as voter intimidation. The 287(g) expansion is exactly that, as documented in multiple federal court cases. Labor should be in the Senate galleries with member witnesses ready for floor debate. Target: at minimum, force recorded votes on three amendments limiting 287(g)'s electoral proximity restrictions.

**Opportunity 2: State constitutional redistricting campaigns post-*Callais***

The Illinois state constitutional amendment requiring race-conscious redistricting — initiated specifically in anticipation of the SCOTUS gutting the VRA — is the model for the six to eight states where labor has sufficient legislative leverage. UAW has significant membership in Michigan, Illinois, and Ohio. AFSCME represents state workers who administer elections in most of these states. AFL-CIO's political infrastructure in Illinois, Michigan, and Pennsylvania can provide the petition signature infrastructure and legislative lobby capacity for state constitutional amendment campaigns. Target: labor should be the lead organizational force behind constitutional amendment campaigns in Michigan and Pennsylvania within 90 days of *Callais*, with Illinois as the model.

**Opportunity 3: "Labor 2026" GOTV as democratic defense framing**

The South Florida AFL-CIO's May 16 launch is the right model at the wrong scale. The affordable housing frame works because it is concrete and local. Every state federation should launch a "Labor 2026" campaign by June 30 with a specific local economic grievance tied to the midterm accountability frame. The national AFL-CIO's role: provide the national infrastructure (web, voter contact tools, media) while state federations own the local frame. This is the electoral inversion of what happened in 2024, when national messaging overwhelmed local organizing capacity.

**Opportunity 4: NLRB quorum restoration as the single-issue legislative priority**

The NLRB has been without quorum since May 2025. Union elections fell 30% in 2025. This is the most direct operational consequence of democratic erosion for the labor movement and the most compelling testimony labor can offer about why the 2026 midterm matters. AFGE and AFL-CIO should designate the NLRB Independence Act as the single legislative priority requiring bipartisan support — the ask that makes the electoral accountability test clearest. If a Republican senator votes to restore NLRB quorum, they have demonstrated minimum functionality. If not, the election accountability case writes itself.

## Implementation Pathway

**Weeks 1-2 (May 18-31): ICE/CBP reconciliation mobilization**

Activate AFL-CIO's congressional contact network in the Senate. AFGE leads testimony before Senate Judiciary and Homeland Security markup sessions (week of May 19) on the 287(g) expansion's impact on federal workers and professional standards. SEIU Healthcare deploys member witnesses from border communities with documented experiences of enforcement surge impacts on healthcare access. AFL-CIO political staff coordinate with three Democratic senators on amendment language limiting 287(g) electoral proximity (prohibiting enforcement operations within 1 mile of polling sites or registration drives). Force recorded votes on each amendment — this is the electoral accountability record for November 2026.

**Weeks 3-6 (June 1-30): State federation "Labor 2026" launch and constitutional amendment activation**

Every state federation president receives the constitutional amendment campaign template by June 15. Priority states: Illinois (already in progress — coordinate and provide resources), Michigan (UAW + AFSCME infrastructure), Pennsylvania (building trades + SEIU Healthcare), Wisconsin (AFSCME state workers). Each campaign needs: petition infrastructure, a legislative champion in the state legislature, and a public launch event with labor and civil rights co-signatories.

Simultaneously: AFL-CIO political department launches "Labor 2026" national framework — a unified digital and field infrastructure that state federations plug into with local framing. The national frame is democratic renewal; the local frame is whatever the state federation identifies as the concrete economic grievance.

**Weeks 7-16 (July-September): GOTV infrastructure build and NLRB legislative push**

The 2022 AFL-CIO mobilization model (8 million voter contacts) scales to 2026 with SEIU integrated. The targeting list should be built from: union households in competitive districts, with flag for whether they live in a district affected by the Callais redistricting cascade. Redistricted district voters have the most direct personal stake in the 2026 outcome and should receive the most intensive contact.

NLRB Independence Act legislative push: AFL-CIO president to testify before Senate HELP Committee (summer 2026) with four NLRB-election statistics: elections filed (down 30%), elections conducted (down 30%), union wins (down), median time to certification (up). Frame as public testimony about the operational consequences of democratic erosion. Republican senators in states with high union density (Pennsylvania, Michigan, Ohio, Wisconsin) are the targets.

**Months 4-12: PRO Act and sectoral bargaining pilots**

Post-midterm: if Democrats gain seats in the House or improve position in the Senate, the PRO Act is the first legislative priority. But the labor movement should not wait for federal action. Sectoral bargaining pilots in California, Colorado, New Mexico, New York, and Massachusetts — state legislation authorizing industry-level bargaining — operate independently of federal labor law. AFL-CIO's state federations in these five states should have sectoral bargaining pilot legislation drafted by January 2027 for Day 1 of the new legislative session.

## Risks and Contingencies

**Risk 1: Building trades divergence on climate and immigration.** NABTU's membership includes workers in fossil fuel industries (pipelines, petrochemical) who resist rapid climate transition. Immigration enforcement has historically divided building trades (labor market competition concerns) from service sector unions (immigrant member base). Mitigation: no playbook requires unified labor positions on climate transition speed or immigration enforcement. Domain 15 just transition provisions (green jobs expansion, infrastructure investment) are the economic bridge. Building trades can support democracy measures independently of their positions on climate. Do not require universal labor solidarity on every domain; accept domain-specific coalition composition.

**Risk 2: NLRB capture limits organizing capacity.** Labor's own organizing rights are degraded precisely when organizing capacity is most needed. State-level organizing (state labor boards, public sector unions, right-to-organize state statutes) is the short-term substitute. AFSCME's public sector membership is the most protected because public sector unions operate under different legal frameworks. Prioritize public sector organizing until NLRB quorum is restored.

**Risk 3: Economic messaging discipline.** The democratic renewal frame is abstract; the economic grievance frame is concrete. Every campaign that leads with "democracy is at stake" without a specific economic consequence loses the swing voter. The South Florida AFL-CIO model (affordable housing anger → voter power) is correct. Require every state federation "Labor 2026" launch to lead with a specific local economic grievance before moving to the democratic accountability frame.

**Risk 4: Electoral liability in Rust Belt.** AFL-CIO's 2024 mobilization produced disappointing results in Pennsylvania and Michigan. The lesson: partisan alignment alone does not motivate union voters who have diverged on trade. The NLRB, healthcare, and civil service frames are less partisan and more directly tied to union members' daily work lives. These are the correct frames for the Rust Belt states where electoral liability is highest.

## Key Contacts and Resources

**AFL-CIO**: [aflcio.org](https://aflcio.org/) — 57-union federation. Political department leads "Labor 2026" national infrastructure. Voting rights committee coordinates with Democracy Docket and Brennan Center.

**SEIU**: [seiu.org](https://www.seiu.org/) — 3.8 million members. Community-based organizing capacity in immigrant and healthcare worker communities most directly affected by reconciliation bill. SEIU Healthcare (1199 SEIU) is the witness coalition for reconciliation markup testimony.

**AFSCME**: [afscme.org](https://www.afscme.org/) — 1.6 million state and local workers. Election administration workers provide the most direct testimony about voting rights and redistricting impacts. State federation structure in Michigan, Pennsylvania, and Wisconsin is essential for constitutional amendment campaigns.

**AFGE**: [afge.org](https://www.afge.org/) — 1.3 million federal workers. Lead witness for ICE/CBP reconciliation markup testimony. First-person documentation of 287(g) expansion operational consequences.

**UAW**: [uaw.org](https://www.uaw.org/) — critical constituency in Michigan, Illinois, and Ohio for state constitutional redistricting amendment campaigns. Post-2024 organizing (electric vehicle transition) creates just-transition narrative bridge with building trades.

**Voting Rights Lab**: [votingrightslab.org](https://votingrightslab.org/) — tracks state election legislation in real time. Essential resource for state federation advocacy on election bills.

**South Florida AFL-CIO "Labor 2026"**: [wlrn.org coverage](https://www.wlrn.org/government-politics/2026-05-16/south-florida-afl-cio-launches-labor-2026-to-channel-affordable-housing-anger-into-voter-power) — organizational model for state federation campaigns. Contact South Florida AFL-CIO for the campaign template before launching state-level analogs.

**Roosevelt Institute**: [rooseveltinstitute.org](https://rooseveltinstitute.org/) — SEIU-AFL-CIO unified front analysis; academic framing for the sectoral bargaining pilot case.
