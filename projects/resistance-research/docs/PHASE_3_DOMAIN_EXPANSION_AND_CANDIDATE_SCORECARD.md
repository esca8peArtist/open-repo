## Scoring Methodology

Inherited from PHASE_3_DOMAIN_CANDIDATE_SCORECARD.md (Item 93):

- **Urgency (1-10)**: Presence of immovable legislative, litigation, or organizational deadlines between Nov 4, 2026 and March 31, 2027. 10 = hard deadline that collapses the advocacy window if missed. 5 = meaningful but deferrable. 2 = evergreen.
- **Movement Leverage (1-10)**: Coalition activation potential -- breadth and density of organized constituencies.
- **Feasibility (1-10)**: Estimated research hours relative to Phase 2 baseline (28.5 hrs/domain). 10 = under 20 hours. 5 = 25-35 hours. 2 = 45+ hours.
- **Strategic Impact (1-10)**: Multiplier effect -- does completing this domain enable or amplify other Phase 3 domains or Phase 2 coalitions?

Composite score = (Urgency x 1.5) + (Movement Leverage x 1.2) + Feasibility + (Strategic Impact x 1.3) / 5

### Expansion Domain 1: Domain G -- Press Freedom and Information Ecosystem Integrity

**Background**: Domain G was scoped in Phase 2 as a press freedom domain with a target completion date of June 15, 2026. PHASE_2_RESOURCE_CONTENTION_TRIGGERS.md (Item 107) explicitly flags Domain G as a Phase 2 carryover risk if not completed by August 31. PHASE_3_EXECUTION_TIMELINE.md notes: "Domain G must close before Phase 3 can formally announce the knowledge commons initiative." Domain G sits at the intersection of Phase 2 production obligations and Phase 3 distribution strategy.

**The case for Phase 3 inclusion**: Press freedom is not covered by any completed Phase 1-2 domain at the domain-level (mentions appear in Domains 43, 29, 7). The 2026 developments are acute: NPR Federal Court victory on Executive Order 14290 (March 31, 2026 -- U.S. District Judge Randolph Moss ruled the order violated the First Amendment); AP White House access dispute is before a three-judge DC Circuit panel; New York Times lawsuit against DOD's press restriction policy is active. These 2026 rulings generate a post-election update window that makes Domain G a Phase 3 production candidate regardless of Phase 2 completion status.

**Urgency: 7/10** -- The Freedom House "Freedom of the Press" and RSF World Press Freedom Index publish annually in April-May. Distribution of Domain G analysis to Knight First Amendment Institute, Committee to Protect Journalists, PEN America, and Reporters Committee for Freedom of the Press before March 2027 positions it for citation in both annual indices. The AP access case ruling (expected Q1 2027 from the DC Circuit) creates a distribution trigger. If Democrats recapture either chamber, a Shield Law revival and public media funding restoration are early legislative agenda items for incoming Senate Commerce Committee members (FCC and public broadcasting jurisdiction).

**Movement Leverage: 8/10** -- Knight First Amendment Institute (Columbia University), Reporters Committee for Freedom of the Press, PEN America, Committee to Protect Journalists, First Amendment Coalition, and SPJ are all active organizations with policy programs, litigation units, and large distribution networks. The NPR Federal Court victory on EO 14290 is a Phase 3 distribution anchor -- the decision vindicates the framework's Phase 2 analysis and creates immediate credibility for First Amendment contacts. Domain G's coalition provides the most direct path to the general public: press organizations have mass-audience distribution networks that no other Phase 3 coalition has.

**Feasibility: 6/10** -- Estimated 22-28 research hours. The domain is partially researched (Phase 2 scoping work and domain-g-press-freedom-information-ecosystem.md file exist). The production gap is the synthesis document: connecting the 2026 press freedom cases (EO 14290, AP access, NYT/DOD, journalist arrests in Minnesota) to the structural constitutional architecture for a free press, with comparative international evidence (Sweden's Freedom of the Press Act, Finland's constitutional media protection, UK's Leveson Inquiry aftermath). The post-election update layer adds 4-6 hours.

**Strategic Impact: 8/10** -- Press freedom is the information infrastructure prerequisite for every other Phase 3 domain's effectiveness. Research distributed through press organizations reaches journalists covering judicial reform (Domain K), civil service (Domain 56), and campaign finance (Domain 51) directly. Domain G also provides Phase 3's most direct path to the general public through press organization distribution networks.

**Composite Score: 7.72** (Priority 9 in the expanded 10-domain scorecard)

**Estimated research hours**: 22-28 hours (2-3 sessions; some hours may carry forward from Phase 2 production)

### Expansion Domain 2: Domain 50-Update -- OBBBA Medicaid Implementation Impact on Civic Participation

**Background**: Domain 50 in Phase 2 covered the "Healthcare Access and Democracy Nexus" -- specifically the OBBBA Medicaid work requirements effective January 1, 2027. PHASE_3_ELECTION_SPECIFIC_RESEARCH_ANGLES.md (Item 96) flags a Domain 50 post-election update as a November 15 - December 15 window obligation. Item 89 elevates this to a named Phase 3 expansion candidate because the January 1, 2027 effective date creates a coverage-loss cascade that Phase 3 research must address as a new structural democratic participation problem, not merely an update to an existing domain.

**The new evidence**: Urban Institute projections (2026) find that with both six-month redeterminations and work requirements, 4.9 to 10.1 million fewer people will be enrolled in Medicaid expansion coverage in an average month in 2028 -- a decline of 27 to 55 percent among those subject to work requirements. Three-in-10 young adults (18-26) vulnerable to losing coverage means the Medicaid collapse directly affects youth civic participation documented in Domain 54. Georgetown Center for Children and Families confirms that even people meeting work requirements will lose coverage due to administrative barriers -- the civic participation suppression mechanism is structural, not behavioral. OBBBA conditions coverage on 80 hours per month of work, volunteering, or school enrollment with exemptions for pregnancy, medical frailty, caregiving, and parents of children under 14.

**Urgency: 9/10** -- The January 1, 2027 effective date is the most time-specific external deadline in Phase 3 outside the November election. Organizations providing navigator and enrollment assistance need advocacy materials in the weeks before and after January 1. If Democrats recapture either chamber, the lame-duck window for OBBBA work requirement amendment is November 4 - January 3 -- Phase 3's exact opening window.

**Movement Leverage: 8/10** -- The Medicaid advocacy constituency is Phase 2-warm (Domain 50 distributed to NHeLP, Families USA, American Hospital Association, Georgetown CCF). Post-January 1, the constituency expands to every Federally Qualified Health Center (1,400+ nationally), every hospital association, and every state-level consumer health advocacy organization. AFL-CIO and SEIU have members directly affected by work requirement compliance (gig workers, part-time workers, caregivers) and are running Medicaid protection campaigns.

**Feasibility: 7/10** -- Estimated 18-24 research hours. Domain 50 (Phase 2-complete) is the base. The update requires: (a) state implementation plan review (which states have submitted compliance plans per HHS database); (b) legal challenge status (any pre-implementation TROs); (c) NVRA implications of coverage loss (does coverage termination trigger de-registration?); (d) legislative window analysis for lame-duck amendment; (e) Urban Institute and Georgetown CCF state-by-state projection data.

**Strategic Impact: 8/10** -- The Medicaid-civic participation nexus connects to Domain 59 (economic precarity -- Medicaid loss compounds directly), Domain K (HHS implementation litigation), Domain 34 (appropriations riders to defund work requirement implementation), and Domain 56 (Schedule Policy/Career-reclassified HHS career staff administering the requirements). No other Phase 3 domain has this many second-order connections.

**Composite Score: 8.04** (Priority 5.5 -- tied with Item 93's Domain 56 at 8.04; distinct identity and different deployment window)

**Estimated research hours**: 18-24 hours (2 sessions; begins November 15; Jan 1 hard ceiling for lame-duck advocacy)

**Key expert contacts**:
- National Health Law Program (NHeLP): healthlaw.org; Jane Perkins (Director) -- primary legal organization for Medicaid beneficiary rights; active OBBBA litigation; Phase 2 warm contact
- Georgetown Center for Children and Families: ccf.georgetown.edu; Joan Alker (Executive Director) -- publishes state-by-state OBBBA implementation tracking; primary data source and distribution channel
- Families USA: familiesusa.org; Frederick Isasi (Executive Director) -- national consumer health advocacy; actively running OBBBA repeal campaign; large member network

### Expansion Domain 3: Domain N -- State Attorney General Multistate Coalition Architecture

**Background**: The state AG multistate coalition function is not covered by any existing Phase 1-2 domain. Democratic AGs filed their 100th lawsuit against the Trump administration in April 2026, with a reported win rate of approximately 55 out of 67 ruled cases (Stateline, April 2026). This is the most active legal resistance infrastructure in the United States. The coalition appears in Phase 2 distribution contacts and Domain 6/29 analyses, but the framework has never mapped the AG coalition as a democratic resistance architecture with its own strategic logic, legal capacity, and Phase 3 amplification potential.

**Why a dedicated domain is warranted**: The AG coalition is not simply a collection of individual litigation cases. It is a constitutional sovereignty architecture: AGs assert state standing under the Tenth Amendment and parens patriae doctrine to challenge federal actions that harm state residents -- even when no individual plaintiff has Article III standing. This doctrine was established by Republican AGs challenging the Obama administration (2009-2017) and is now wielded at similar scale by Democratic AGs. Domain N would analyze: (a) constitutional foundations of multistate AG standing; (b) the 100-lawsuit inventory categorized by democratic-protection value vs. state-interest value; (c) coordination infrastructure (Democratic Attorneys General Association); (d) resource constraints; (e) Phase 3 legislative agenda -- which AG-won precedents need to be codified before the next administration reversal?

**Urgency: 7/10** -- The AG coalition's strategic window is the entire 120th Congress. The most time-sensitive analysis is the post-election case status review. If Democrats recapture either chamber, incoming Judiciary Committee members will want the AG litigation inventory as a briefing document for January-February 2027 oversight hearings. The new AGs elected in November 2026 will be setting their 2027 litigation priorities in January-February 2027 -- the domain lands at exactly this planning moment. No single immovable deadline, but the January 15 - February 28 window is the primary deployment target.

**Movement Leverage: 9/10** -- The Democratic Attorneys General Association coordinates 25+ state AGs. Each AG has their own legal department (thousands of career attorneys), press operation, and public communication infrastructure. A domain distributed through DAGA and directly to the AG offices of California (Bonta), New York (James), Illinois (Raoul), Pennsylvania (Henry), and Michigan reaches the most powerful legal resistance infrastructure in the country. Unlike advocacy organizations, AGs have direct legal authority -- they can file cases, issue opinions, and negotiate settlements. Domain N analysis distributed to AG offices goes directly into legal strategy briefings. The AG coalition's public communication reaches millions of state residents through state AG press releases.

**Feasibility: 5/10** -- Estimated 28-35 research hours. New-from-scratch domain with substantial primary source density: 100+ lawsuits require categorization; constitutional standing doctrine is technically complex; the coordination architecture requires review of DAGA materials and academic literature. The domain's research challenge is filtering 100 cases into a 20-30 case "load-bearing" subset. Primary sources: DAGA litigation tracker (dems.ag), state AG press release databases, Multistate Lawsuits database (attorneysgeneral.org), Harvard Law Review AG-federal resistance analysis, Marquette University multistate coalition academic literature.

**Strategic Impact: 9/10** -- Domain N is the Phase 3 domain that most directly converts research into legal strategy. When Brennan Center litigation staff and CLC attorneys receive Domain N's analysis of which AG cases are closest to dispositive rulings, they can immediately act: file amicus briefs, schedule oversight hearings, identify which state court precedents need federal legislation to codify. The AG coalition is also the clearest bridge between Phase 3's federal reform advocacy and state-level resistance infrastructure -- the 38-state ratification threshold for constitutional amendments (Domain H) requires organized state-level champions, and multistate AG coalitions provide the natural organizational architecture for that work.

**Composite Score: 7.80** (Priority 8.5 -- between Item 93's Domain L and Domain I in expanded scorecard)

**Estimated research hours**: 28-35 hours (3 sessions)

**Key expert contacts**:
- Democratic Attorneys General Association (DAGA): dems.ag -- coordinates Democratic AG legal strategy; publishes litigation summaries; primary distribution target
- California AG Robert Bonta: oag.ca.gov -- leads most multistate coalition filings; AG office policy staff are primary audience
- New York AG Letitia James: ag.ny.gov -- co-leads most coalition actions; impact litigation unit is secondary distribution target
- Harvard Law Review Blog -- State AG coverage: academic analysis of multistate AG litigation and federal overreach doctrine; potential Level 4 adoption target
- National Association of Attorneys General (NAAG): naag.org -- non-partisan umbrella; provides bipartisan comparison data (Republican AG precedents from 2009-2017 that established multistate standing doctrine)
