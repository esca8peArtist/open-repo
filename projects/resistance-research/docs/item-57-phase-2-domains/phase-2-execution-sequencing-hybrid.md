# ITEM57: Phase 2 Execution Sequencing — Hybrid Track Analysis

*Completed May 9, 2026. Strategic analysis of post-Phase-1 sequencing options for Phase 2 domain research and distribution. The central finding: Track A (sequential) and Track B (parallel) are not mutually exclusive and should run simultaneously with coordinated timing gates. The June 12 FISA deadline is both a research constraint and a distribution opportunity. The Callais ruling has added urgency that compresses the optimal window for Domain 45 research from "next three months" to "next three weeks."*

---

## Part I: Executive Summary — The Hybrid Recommendation

The fundamental sequencing question for Phase 2 is whether to run domain research sequentially from Phase 1 conclusion (Track A) or in parallel with independent domain clusters (Track B). The answer, after analyzing both options against the current timeline, is neither — it is a hybrid that runs Track A and Track B simultaneously but with different resource allocations and different distribution channels.

**The hybrid recommendation in brief:**

- **Track A (sequential, high-resource):** Voting Systems (Domain 37 updates) → Domain 44 (Disability/Voting) → Domain 45 (Tribal/Suppression). This three-domain sequential track builds on Phase 1's voting rights foundation and converts existing Phase 1 adopters (voting rights organizations, election law scholars) into Phase 2 collaborative distributors. The FISA June 12 gate creates a natural break point between Domain 37 updates and Domain 44 research initiation.

- **Track B (parallel, lower-resource):** Intel Oversight (Domain 38a updates) → Transitional Justice (Domain 46) running simultaneously with Track A's Domain 45. These domains target governance and accountability audiences that have minimal overlap with Track A's disability and tribal sovereignty audiences. No audience cannibalization risk.

- **Critical timing constraint:** Domain 45 (Tribal/Voting) must be initiated within three weeks of May 9, 2026 to capture the immediate post-*Callais* organizational response period before the crisis normalizes. This creates a firm start date of no later than May 30, 2026.

- **Distribution timing:** Phase 2 distribution of Track A Domain 44 material should be timed to the OBBBA Medicaid outreach window (August-November 2026). Track B Transitional Justice material should be timed to the post-midterm accountability conversation (December 2026-January 2027).

## Part II: Track A Analysis — Sequential from Phase 1 Conclusion

### Option A-1: Voting Systems → Reproductive Rights → Labor Rights → Fiscal Authority (12-Week Cycle)

This was the originally proposed Track A option before the April 29 *Callais* ruling and the May 9 *Callais* cross-minority impact documentation. The sequencing logic was: build on the voting rights momentum from Domain 37 (midterm interference) and Domain 38b (election security), then expand into reproductive rights (Domain 42, already scoped in ITEM50) and labor rights (Domain 45/47 candidate), with fiscal authority (congressional appropriations updates) as the culminating frame.

**Assessment after May 9 developments:** This sequencing remains internally coherent but has been superseded by urgency. The *Callais* ruling creates a window that the proposed 12-week cycle does not accommodate: if the framework waits 12 weeks (until August 2026) to begin Native American voting rights research, the immediate post-*Callais* organizational response will have completed without the framework's contribution. The reproductive rights and labor rights audiences will not be lost by a 6-week delay to prioritize *Callais*-response domains; the Native American voting rights audience will not wait.

**What this option gets right:** The Voting Systems → Reproductive Rights → Labor Rights sequence reflects the strongest institutional adoption pathway for Phase 1 adopters who are primarily election law and civil rights organizations. These organizations already use the framework and will follow sequential domain additions with relatively low activation costs. The 12-week cycle produces three complete domain documents before the August 2026 primary season — the natural distribution moment for election-focused content.

**What it gets wrong:** It treats the *Callais* ruling as background context rather than as the catalytic external event that it is. The Supreme Court's effective elimination of Section 2 VRA protections is the most significant voting rights legal development since *Shelby County* in 2013. A framework that does not produce an analytical response to *Callais* within weeks loses credibility as a current-events resource and misses the window to shape the institutional response.

### Option A-2: Voting Systems → Domain 44 (Disability/Voting) → Domain 45 (Tribal) → Next

This is the recommended Track A sequence. The logic is:

**Step 1 (Weeks 1-3, concurrent with FISA monitoring):** Update Domain 37 election security content with post-*Callais* analysis. The *Callais* ruling directly affects Domain 37's analysis of the legal architecture for protecting the 2026 midterm elections. Domain 37 already tracks Section 3 litigation (37a) and election security infrastructure (37b). Adding a Section on *Callais*'s impact on the mid-term redistricting cycle is a targeted update (2,000-3,000 words) rather than a full new domain — achievable in a single research session before the FISA June 12 gate.

**Step 2 (Weeks 2-5, initiating immediately):** Begin Domain 44 (Disability/Voting) research. The SAVE Act Senate consideration and the OBBBA August outreach window create urgency, but not a single-day deadline. This domain can be researched over a three to four week period while simultaneously monitoring the June 12 FISA gate. No resource conflict with FISA monitoring.

**Step 3 (Weeks 3-6, initiating by May 30 at the latest):** Begin Domain 45 (Tribal/Voting) research. The hard constraint here is the post-*Callais* window. The NARF response, NCAI response, and the broader Native voting rights organizational response are happening now (May 2026). Research conducted in weeks 3-6 will capture both the institutional response and the forward-looking litigation strategy that NARF is already formulating. Research conducted in August will miss the response period and only capture the normalized post-*Callais* landscape.

**Step 4 (Weeks 6-8):** Distribute Domains 44 and 45 together as a paired post-*Callais* response package. The pairing has strategic distribution logic: disability and tribal voting rights organizations are distinct audiences, but they share the common post-*Callais* context. Distributing them together signals the framework's systemic analysis rather than issue-specific coverage.

### What Track A-2 Maximizes for Institutional Adoption

Track A-2 maximizes institutional adoption by:

1. **Meeting Phase 1 adopters where their current work is:** Election law organizations, civil rights litigators, and state-level voting rights advocates who adopted Phase 1 material are all actively responding to *Callais* right now. A framework that provides analytical resources on the *Callais* implications for disability and tribal voting rights is immediately useful to organizations that are already in crisis-response mode.

2. **Creating a distribution hook with timing:** The OBBBA August outreach window is a concrete deadline around which disability rights organizations are already mobilizing. Domain 44 material distributed in July-August can be directly incorporated into organizational communications about the Medicaid outreach period and NVRA compliance obligations.

3. **Converting one-time readers into co-distributors:** Phase 1 adopters who are voting rights organizations can become active distributors of Domain 44 and 45 material if the material directly addresses the crisis their members are currently navigating. This is the highest-value conversion in institutional adoption: from reader to advocate.

## Part III: Track B Analysis — Parallel, Independent Domains

### Option B-1: Intel Oversight + Tribal Sovereignty (Governance Tier, Non-Overlapping Audiences)

The original Track B option proposed running Intelligence Oversight (Domain 38a updates) and Tribal Sovereignty in parallel. This framing reflects valid logic: the governance accountability audience (civil liberties organizations, national security scholars, congressional oversight advocates) is distinct from the tribal sovereignty audience (NCAI, NARF, tribal government attorneys). The two domains can be researched and distributed without competing for the same institutional attention.

**Assessment:** Track B-1 is coherent but has been superseded by the decision to include Tribal Sovereignty in Track A. If Domain 45 is running in Track A (as recommended), it should not simultaneously run in Track B — the research resources are the same (a single orchestrator running research sessions), and the sequencing benefit of Track B is lost if the domains overlap.

The revision: Track B should run Intel Oversight (Domain 38a update) in parallel with Track A's Domain 44 research. The two audiences — civil liberties/surveillance scholars for Intel Oversight, disability rights organizations for Domain 44 — have minimal overlap. Intel Oversight updates can be initiated while Domain 44 is in its mid-research phase (weeks 3-4), without creating any resource or audience conflict.

### Option B-2: Epistemic Infrastructure + Post-Insurrection Accountability (June 12 Gate-Dependent)

A second Track B option emerges from the June 12 FISA gate analysis (see Part IV below). If the FISA June 12 deadline produces a new surveillance authorization controversy, the Epistemic Infrastructure domain (Domain 46/48 candidate) becomes immediately relevant: the surveillance architecture and the epistemic infrastructure are directly connected through the question of government information control. Initiating Epistemic Infrastructure research after the June 12 FISA outcome gives the domain the benefit of whatever new FISA controversy emerges as a current-events hook.

The Post-Insurrection Accountability domain (Domain 46/47 candidate) is an ideal Track B parallel to Epistemic Infrastructure: the accountability and transitional justice audiences (civil society organizations focused on January 6, state prosecutors, constitutional law scholars) are distinct from the epistemic infrastructure audience (media organizations, information science researchers, election security advocates). No cannibalization risk.

**Track B-2 timing:** Initiate after June 12 FISA outcome. Research both domains in parallel during weeks 7-12 while Track A Domain 45 distribution is in progress.

### Parallelization Assessment: Can Track A and Track B Run Truly in Parallel Without Conflict?

Yes, with one condition. Track A and Track B can run fully in parallel if the following condition is met: **the orchestrator's research capacity is treated as a shared resource that is allocated sequentially, not executed simultaneously.**

In practice: the orchestrator runs Domain 44 research (Track A) in Sessions 1-3. While Domain 44 is being drafted and reviewed, the orchestrator runs Intel Oversight updates (Track B) in Sessions 4-5. Domain 45 research begins in Session 6 (Track A continues). Epistemic Infrastructure begins in Session 7 (Track B continues). This is not truly parallel execution — it is interleaved sequential execution that appears parallel from the distribution timeline.

**The resource bottleneck:** The actual constraint is not orchestrator execution capacity. It is user review time. Each domain document requires user review before distribution. If Domain 44 (Track A) and Intel Oversight update (Track B) are both complete at the same time, user review becomes the bottleneck. The sequencing should be designed to stagger completion dates by two weeks so that user review is never required on two domain-level documents simultaneously.

## Part IV: June 12 FISA Gate Analysis

### What the June 12 Gate Is

On April 30, 2026, Congress approved a 45-day extension of FISA Section 702 (the foreign intelligence surveillance authority), passing 261-111 in the House with Senate unanimous consent, after a Senate-blocked three-year reauthorization failed because it included an unrelated digital currency prohibition. The 45-day extension expires June 12, 2026.

The Senate is working on its own three-year extension proposal. The outcome options at June 12 are:

- **Outcome A: Senate passes its own three-year reauthorization** before June 12. Section 702 is extended with Senate-preferred terms (likely modest reforms, civil liberties modifications, possible warrant requirement for US person queries — the primary civil liberties debate). The FISA surveillance issue is resolved through 2029. Low urgency impact on sequencing.

- **Outcome B: Another short-term extension** (30-90 days) to allow further negotiation. Section 702 continues but the controversy extends, creating another advocacy window. Moderate urgency impact — Domain 38a (Intel Oversight) becomes more time-sensitive if the controversy extends.

- **Outcome C: Section 702 lapses** because Congress cannot agree on terms before June 12. This would be the most significant civil liberties and national security event since the 2020 FISA abuse revelations. Very high urgency — Domain 38a update would need to be initiated within days of a lapse.

### How Phase 1 Voting Systems Domain Interacts with the FISA Gate

Domain 37 (the midterm interference domain) and Domain 25 (the FISA 702 domain) are the two existing framework domains with direct exposure to the June 12 deadline. The voting systems dimension is specific: Section 702 surveillance is used, among other purposes, to gather foreign intelligence about foreign electoral interference. The 2026 midterm election cycle is the primary context for that use. If Section 702 lapses, the intelligence community loses the primary legal authority under which it collects signals intelligence on foreign electoral interference — which directly affects the election security analysis in Domain 37.

**The interaction for sequencing:** If Section 702 lapses (Outcome C), the Domain 37 update (Track A Step 1) must be expanded from a targeted *Callais* analysis to a comprehensive post-FISA-lapse election security update. This would delay the Domain 44 research initiation by approximately one week. If Section 702 is extended (Outcomes A or B), the Domain 37 update remains limited to *Callais* analysis and Domain 44 can begin on schedule.

**The Voting Systems domain's FISA interaction is not about surveillance of domestic voters.** The connection is the election security/foreign intelligence nexus: if FISA Section 702 lapses, the counterintelligence tools available to identify and respond to foreign electoral interference are reduced. This is a legitimate concern for the framework's election security analysis, but it is a bounded technical update to Domain 37 — not a full new domain.

### June 12 as a Distribution Trigger

The FISA June 12 gate is also a distribution opportunity independent of the outcome. Organizations in the civil liberties and national security space are actively monitoring this deadline. A targeted distribution of the existing Domain 25 (FISA 702) framework material with a post-April-30 update note is a low-cost mechanism for re-engaging Phase 1 adopters in this audience segment. This should be treated as a Track B preparation action in weeks 3-5: update Domain 25 with the April 30 extension and June 12 deadline context, redistribute to civil liberties audience, and use the response to identify which organizations are most engaged with surveillance reform as a research collaboration partner for Domain 38a.

## Part V: Institutional Momentum Analysis

### Which Sequencing Converts Phase 1 Adopters into Phase 2 Collaborators

The critical question for institutional adoption strategy is not just which sequencing produces the most valuable research. It is which sequencing creates the strongest feedback loop between Phase 1 adopters and Phase 2 production — moving from one-way distribution to collaborative research and co-authorship.

The Phase 1 distribution has reached (or will reach, pending the user's distribution path decision) three institutional audience tiers: (1) election law and voting rights organizations (the highest-engagement cohort, given the framework's voting-rights-heavy Phase 1 content); (2) civil liberties and surveillance organizations (engaged by Domains 25, 36, and 37); (3) academic institutions (law schools, political science departments, democracy studies programs).

**Track A-2 converts Tier 1 (voting rights organizations):** Domains 44 and 45 address exactly the policy questions that voting rights organizations are working on in May-June 2026: the post-*Callais* litigation strategy, the SAVE Act Senate fight, the OBBBA Medicaid-NVRA nexus. An outreach message to Phase 1 Tier 1 adopters that says "we're researching the *Callais* implications for disability and Native American voters and we'd welcome your input on the litigation landscape" is a specific, actionable engagement request — not a general "we'd love feedback" ask.

**Track B converts Tier 2 (civil liberties organizations):** Intel Oversight (Domain 38a) and Epistemic Infrastructure (Domain 46/48) address the active work of EFF, ACLU, Brennan Center, and Knight Foundation. The FISA June 12 gate is the specific hook: a targeted "we're updating our FISA analysis for the June 12 deadline and we'd welcome your current assessment of the legislative prospects" creates a specific pre-deadline engagement moment.

**The co-design pivot for Phase 2:** The Phase 1 outreach model was primarily one-directional: distribute research, collect feedback. The Phase 2 opportunity is to move toward co-production: invite practitioners to co-author domain sections where they have ground-truth knowledge that academic synthesis cannot provide. NARF's active litigation database is the best example — a Domain 45 section on the post-*Callais* litigation strategy would benefit from direct NARF input that is more current and specific than any secondary-source synthesis can be. The institutional outreach for Domain 45 should explicitly invite NARF to co-review the litigation strategy section before distribution.

## Part VI: Resource Bottleneck Mapping

### Where Orchestrator Capacity Is the Constraint

The orchestrator's primary research constraint in May-June 2026 is not token budget (usage is currently at 58.9% with reset in 66 hours as of May 9). The constraint is session organization: each domain document requires 14-18 research hours (tribal sovereignty) or 10-14 hours (disability, epistemic infrastructure). In practice, a research session runs approximately 3-4 hours of active research and synthesis, meaning each domain requires 3-5 sessions to complete.

With the current session cadence (2-3 sessions per week available for resistance-research, based on the orchestrator state), the timeline is:

- **Weeks 1-2 (May 9-23):** Domain 37 *Callais* update (1 session) + Domain 44 research initiation (2 sessions)
- **Weeks 3-4 (May 23 - June 6):** Domain 44 completion (2 sessions) + Domain 45 initiation (2 sessions) + Intel Oversight update (1 session)
- **Weeks 5-6 (June 6-20):** Domain 45 completion (2 sessions) + June 12 FISA gate response (1 session if needed)
- **Weeks 7-8 (June 20 - July 4):** Domain 44 and 45 distribution preparation + Epistemic Infrastructure initiation (Track B-2)
- **Weeks 9-12 (July-August):** Epistemic Infrastructure completion + Post-Insurrection Accountability initiation + Distribution of Domain 44/45 timed to OBBBA outreach window

This schedule does not conflict with the stockbot May 12 checkpoint, the seedwarden May 30 launch, or any other active project's critical path as documented in ORCHESTRATOR_STATE.md.

### Where User Time Is the Constraint

User time is the binding constraint for distribution, not research. The three action points where user time is required:

1. **Domain 44/45 review before distribution** (estimated 90 minutes per document): Schedule for mid-June after Domain 45 completion, before the July distribution preparation phase.

2. **FISA gate decision** (minimal): The June 12 FISA outcome requires a 15-minute user decision: "Does the FISA outcome require a Domain 25 emergency update, or is the Track B-2 initiation the right response?" This is a binary decision, not a complex one.

3. **Phase 2 outreach initiation** (estimated 2-3 hours): The Phase 2 outreach to NARF, DREDF, and NCAI is more intensive than the initial Phase 1 outreach because it requires customized messaging for co-review invitations rather than general distribution. This is the highest-value user time investment in Phase 2.

## Part VIII: Why This Sequencing Is Right — The Strategic Case

The hybrid Track A-2 + Track B approach is the correct sequencing for three reasons that go beyond the tactical timing analysis:

**Reason 1: It honors the post-Callais moment.** The Supreme Court has handed advocates a catalytic moment. The *Callais* ruling's impact on disability and Native American voting rights is not yet widely understood — the immediate coverage focused on Black voters in Louisiana. A framework that moves within 30-60 days of *Callais* to document the disability and tribal voting rights implications will shape how this moment is understood by the institutional audiences that matter for policy change. Waiting until the summer compounds the framework documents the crisis after it has been processed, rather than contributing to the processing.

**Reason 2: It uses Phase 1 distribution momentum as fuel.** Phase 1 distribution is either complete or completing. The institutional relationships being built now are warm. The hybrid sequencing timed to reach Phase 1 adopters with Domain 44 and 45 material while the Phase 1 introduction is still recent captures the relational momentum that distribution creates. The institutional adoption research is clear: a second touch within 60 days of first contact is significantly more likely to produce deep engagement than a second touch after a six-month gap.

**Reason 3: It builds toward the November 2026 electoral moment.** The 2026 midterm elections are the culminating event for the entire framework's democratic-design research mission. Domains 44 and 45 distributed in July-August, combined with Domain 37's *Callais* update and the FISA gate response, create a comprehensive pre-election package on voting rights, surveillance, and electoral integrity that is timed exactly for the period when institutional audiences are mobilizing their own midterm advocacy. The framework becomes a resource for the midterm election advocacy ecosystem — which is the highest-leverage use of the research.
