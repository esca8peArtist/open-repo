# ITEM57: Phase 2 Domains 44–45 Candidate Scoping

*Completed May 9, 2026. Detailed candidate analysis of two Priority Phase 2 domains: Disability Rights & Voting Access (Domain 44 candidate) and Tribal Sovereignty & Native American Voter Suppression (Domain 45 candidate). The April 29, 2026 Supreme Court ruling in Louisiana v. Callais — which gutted Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act in a 6-3 decision — elevates both candidates to urgent status and fundamentally reshapes the legal landscape for both domains. Alternative candidates and a four-dimension scoring matrix complete the analysis.*

---

## Part I: The April 29 Callais Rupture — Updated Context for Both Domains

Before analyzing the individual candidates, a threshold observation: the Supreme Court's April 29, 2026 ruling in *Louisiana v. Callais* changed the legal terrain for both Domain 44 and Domain 45 in ways that were not fully predictable when earlier scoping documents were written.

The 6-3 majority in *Callais* held that redistricting maps drawn with race as the predominant factor violate the Equal Protection Clause even when drawn to comply with Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act. The majority's logic — that partisan gerrymandering and racial gerrymandering are distinct, and that a map drawn to create Black-majority districts cannot be defended as merely reflecting partisan geography — effectively eliminates the affirmative use of Section 2 to create or preserve majority-minority districts in most circumstances.

The Native American Rights Fund responded the same day: "Today's decision cruelly undercuts the Voting Rights Act, the foundational tool for Native voters and other voters of color to protect themselves from racial discrimination when voting." The Campaign Legal Center stated the court has "eviscerated the Voting Rights Act." The Brookings Institution analysis (May 2026) projected that *Callais* would stall or reverse diversity gains in the House that took a decade to build.

For both Domain 44 and Domain 45, the post-*Callais* environment is the operative one. Section 5 (preclearance) was gutted by *Shelby County v. Holder* in 2013. Section 2 (results test) has now been rendered largely non-operational by *Callais*. The result: the primary federal statutory tools for challenging discriminatory voting laws as applied to both disabled and Native American voters have been simultaneously eliminated. This convergence elevates both candidates from "urgent" to "critical."

## Part II: Domain 44 Candidate — Disability Rights & Voting Access

### Section 2.1: The Democratic Design Problem

Disabled Americans are not a protected class with a single shared political interest. The disability community is one of the most heterogeneous political constituencies in American life — it spans every race, income level, partisan affiliation, and geographic context. What unites disabled voters for purposes of democratic design analysis is a structural exclusion pattern: the architecture of American voting systems was built around assumptions of physical and cognitive capability that systematically exclude an estimated 35 million disabled Americans of voting age.

The democratic design problem is not simply that disabled people face barriers. It is that the systems that would ordinarily correct for such barriers — administrative enforcement (DOJ Civil Rights Division), judicial review (ADA and HAVA litigation), and legislative remedy (the SAVE Act accessibility provision) — have each been degraded or weaponized in ways that compound the original exclusion rather than correcting it.

### Section 2.2: Olmstead Enforcement Gaps

The 1999 Supreme Court decision in *Olmstead v. L.C.* established that the ADA requires states to provide services and supports to disabled people in the most integrated setting appropriate to their needs. The integration mandate is, among other things, a civic participation mandate: people institutionalized in nursing homes and psychiatric facilities are systematically excluded from electoral participation through residential instability, restricted access to mail-in ballots, inadequate assistance to register and vote, and the disenfranchisement of people under guardianship orders.

**Current enforcement collapse:** The Trump administration's DOJ Civil Rights Division has explicitly reoriented enforcement priorities away from Olmstead compliance. States that were under active investigation or consent decree monitoring for Olmstead violations before January 2025 have lost their enforcement pressure in the absence of active DOJ pursuit. The American Bar Association's 2025 report on the Olmstead decision at 25 years documented that the majority of states with integration mandate compliance obligations under settlement agreements are not meeting their timelines and face no active enforcement consequence.

The practical democratic participation implications are direct. States like Georgia, Texas, and Florida — which have large nursing home and institutional populations and have historically faced Olmstead compliance challenges — maintain institutional populations that are functionally disenfranchised. The elimination of DOJ enforcement pressure removes the primary mechanism by which states are compelled to develop integrated community-living infrastructure that would, as a secondary benefit, reconnect institutionalized disabled people to civic participation.

**The guardianship disenfranchisement dimension:** An estimated 1.3 million Americans are under plenary guardianship arrangements that in many states legally strip them of the right to vote. The National Disability Rights Network's survey of state guardianship laws found that 29 states automatically revoke voting rights upon appointment of a guardian, without any individualized determination of the person's capacity to vote. This is a structural democratic exclusion mechanism that the Olmstead enforcement framework indirectly addresses: community integration, which reduces guardianship rates, would restore the democratic participation of a class of citizens currently excluded without hearing.

### Section 2.3: Work-Incentive Policy Collisions

The Social Security Disability Insurance program's Substantial Gainful Activity (SGA) earnings limit — $1,690 per month for non-blind recipients in 2026, $2,830 for statutorily blind — creates what policy analysts call the "benefits cliff." A disabled worker who exceeds the SGA limit risks losing not only their cash benefit but also their Medicare eligibility (which attaches to SSDI) after a 24-month trial work period. The consequence is that millions of disabled Americans who could work more cannot do so without risking catastrophic loss of healthcare coverage.

The democratic participation dimension of the benefits cliff is indirect but real. The communities with the highest rates of SSDI enrollment — rural areas, former industrial communities, communities of color with high rates of occupational injury — are also the communities most targeted by the administration's fiscal consolidation program. SSDI recipients who cannot transition to employment are more dependent on Medicaid for healthcare and more vulnerable to the work requirement implementation timeline.

The OBBBA's Medicaid work requirements, effective December 30, 2026, create a specific new collision: SSDI recipients who are enrolled in Medicaid as a secondary payer face administrative complexity in demonstrating their SSDI-based exemption from work requirements during the August-November 2026 outreach window. The Centers for Health Care Strategies has documented that the exemption notification process is not automated — it requires affirmative action by the enrollee or their representative. For disabled people with cognitive disabilities, communication barriers, or lack of case management support, the procedural complexity of proving an exemption that should apply automatically is itself a disenrollment mechanism.

### Section 2.4: ABLE Accounts as Democratic Infrastructure

The Achieving a Better Life Experience (ABLE) Act of 2014 created tax-advantaged savings accounts for disabled people, modeled on 529 college savings accounts. The democratic infrastructure dimension of ABLE accounts is underappreciated: ABLE accounts allow disabled people to save money without losing means-tested benefit eligibility that would otherwise be triggered by asset accumulation.

The political participation relevance is structural. People who are asset-poor are less likely to be civically engaged, less likely to contribute to political campaigns, and less likely to participate in civic organizations that charge dues or expect financial contributions. ABLE accounts, by allowing disabled people to accumulate modest financial reserves, create the material preconditions for civic participation that the political science literature documents as correlated with political engagement.

Current restrictions remain significant. ABLE accounts are limited to $100,000 before triggering SSI asset limits; the annual contribution limit is $18,000 in 2026; the account must be opened in the state of the account holder's residence, creating geographic barriers; and the account holder must have a disability that began before age 26 (a limit that the ABLE Age Adjustment Act, passed in 2022, expanded to age 46, but only effective January 2026). The age 46 expansion is operationally new and many eligible disabled people are not yet aware of it.

The democratic infrastructure framing of ABLE accounts is a contribution the domain can make that no current disability rights advocacy document makes: connecting financial inclusion policy to civic participation architecture.

### Section 2.5: Voter Registration Capacity in Medicaid Offices

Section 7 of the National Voter Registration Act requires states to offer voter registration at all offices providing public assistance, including Medicaid enrollment offices. The NVRA-Medicaid nexus is one of the highest-leverage voter registration mechanisms in the United States: Medicaid serves approximately 85 million people, disproportionately including disabled people, low-income people, and people of color who face other barriers to voter registration.

The OBBBA's work requirement implementation creates a specific NVRA threat. The August-November 2026 outreach period, during which states must contact all Medicaid enrollees to determine work requirement compliance, involves mandatory contact with 85 million people. Under the NVRA, each of these contacts is a covered transaction that requires a voter registration offer. The Institute for Responsive Government documented in March 2026 that fewer than 30 states have automatic voter registration systems that integrate with Medicaid databases. The remaining states must manually trigger voter registration offers — a process that is administratively burdensome and frequently skipped during high-volume enrollment changes.

The threat mechanism is compounded: the OBBBA's six-month renewal requirement will generate approximately 170 million Medicaid transaction events per year (assuming 85 million enrollees × 2 renewals), each of which should trigger a NVRA voter registration offer. States that do not comply with NVRA at this scale will suppress voter registration at previously unprecedented levels. DOJ NVRA enforcement, which was the primary mechanism for compelling state compliance, has been deprioritized under the current administration's Civil Rights Division orientation.

**The disability-specific NVRA gap:** State agencies that provide services specifically to disabled people — vocational rehabilitation agencies, developmental disability agencies, mental health agencies — are also covered NVRA agencies. The National Disability Rights Network's 2024 NVRA compliance survey found that disability agencies have the lowest NVRA compliance rates of any covered agency category, with many states unable to document when voter registration was last offered at disability service agencies.

### Section 2.6: The SAVE Act — Active Threat Vector

The House passed the SAVE America Act (H.R. 7296) on February 11, 2026. As of May 9, 2026, the bill is pending in the Senate. The SAVE Act would require documentary proof of citizenship for voter registration — a passport, birth certificate, or naturalization certificate — and would complicate or eliminate vote-by-mail access by requiring photo ID submission with mail ballots.

The disability community has documented the specific SAVE Act impact with precision. The Arc Rhode Island (March 2026) documented that approximately 20% of people with disabilities do not have a driver's license or state ID. The Autistic Self Advocacy Network (February 2026) demonstrated that the proposed requirements would effectively disenfranchise disabled people who cannot obtain ID due to cognitive barriers, economic barriers, or transportation barriers to DMV offices. Democracy Docket's analysis (April 2026) estimated that the SAVE Act, if enacted, would remove approximately 21 million voters from the registration rolls nationally — with disabled voters disproportionately affected both through the documentary proof requirement and through the mail-voting complications.

**The enforcement gap post-Callais:** Even if SAVE Act challengers prevail on disability discrimination grounds under the ADA and Rehabilitation Act, the *Callais* ruling removes Section 2 of the VRA as a backup avenue for disabled voters of color who face compounded discrimination (disability status and racial status). The intersection is explicit: Native American disabled voters, Black disabled voters, and Latino disabled voters face SAVE Act burdens that cannot be challenged under Section 2 after *Callais*.

## Part III: Domain 45 Candidate — Tribal Sovereignty & Native American Voter Suppression

### Section 3.1: The Democratic Design Problem

Native American voters occupy a unique position in US democratic theory: they are simultaneously citizens of the United States, members of sovereign tribal nations, and residents of geographic communities whose political and legal relationship to state governments is governed by a sui generis body of federal Indian law that is not reducible to either the state-federal relationships in ordinary federalism doctrine or the individual-rights frameworks of the civil rights tradition.

The democratic design problem for Domain 45 is not simply voter suppression. It is that the legal architecture for protecting Native American voting rights — constructed primarily through Section 5 (preclearance) and Section 2 (results test) of the VRA — has been dismantled in two stages (2013: *Shelby County*; 2026: *Callais*) and has not been replaced with any alternative protection mechanism. The consequence is that states with large Native American populations and histories of discriminatory voting laws are now free to enact restrictions that would have been blocked under either Section 5 or Section 2.

### Section 3.2: Section 5 Enforcement Elimination — The Shelby County Legacy

*Shelby County v. Holder* (2013) eliminated the preclearance requirement that had required states and counties with documented histories of voting discrimination to obtain federal approval before changing voting laws. Of the jurisdictions that were previously covered by Section 5, a disproportionate share contained tribal lands where Native American voting rights had historically been most systematically violated.

The Brennan Center's analysis (2023) documented that in the decade after *Shelby County*, states that had been removed from coverage enacted 94 restrictive voting laws. In states with significant Native American populations — Montana, South Dakota, North Dakota, New Mexico, Alaska — the post-*Shelby* period produced legislation specifically targeted at barriers that Native voters face disproportionately: rural voting barriers (Montana SB 490, restricting Election Day voter registration, challenged by NARF in June 2025), ID requirements that do not accept tribal IDs (North Dakota's ID law, struck down by NARF in federal court before its 2018 application, reinstated in modified form), and polling place consolidation in counties with reservation populations.

**The two-stage dismantlement:** The combination of *Shelby County* and *Callais* has produced a legal environment in which there is no federal statutory tool available for affirmative redistricting protection of Native American communities. Section 5 is gone. Section 2 is effectively gone for racial minority district protection. Section 3 of the VRA (the "bail-in" provision that allows courts to impose preclearance on jurisdictions with recent intentional discrimination findings) remains technically available but requires an intentional discrimination finding — the highest evidentiary standard in equal protection law — before protection kicks in. The result is a structural legal vacuum.

### Section 3.3: BIA Institutional Capture and Tribal Election Interference

The Bureau of Indian Affairs has lost 13% of its total workforce since January 2025, with the Office of the Assistant Secretary for Indian Affairs losing 27% of its staff. The reorganization was conducted without tribal consultation, in violation of the government-to-government consultation protocol required by Executive Order 13175 (2000) and by numerous statutory provisions in the Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act.

The democratic governance dimension of BIA workforce collapse is distinct from the service delivery dimension. The BIA administers the constitutional election process for "IRA-72 tribes" — the approximately 60 tribal nations that organized under the 1934 Indian Reorganization Act and whose governing documents require BIA oversight of constitutional amendments and elections. When BIA staffing collapses, the administrative capacity to conduct tribally mandated election processes degrades. NARF's voting rights documentation has noted cases where BIA's failure to timely certify election results or process constitutional amendments has created governance crises within tribal nations.

The "tribal election interference potential" framing in the task description requires precision. The mechanism is not direct interference by the BIA in tribal election outcomes. It is: (a) BIA non-functionality creates governance vacuums that create opportunities for factional capture of tribal election machinery; (b) BIA reorganization without consultation reshapes the relationship between federal trust responsibility and tribal self-determination in ways that tribes did not consent to; (c) federal Medicaid and Indian Health Service funding cuts — which the domain must analyze alongside BIA restructuring — create economic pressures that compromise tribal governments' political independence.

**The trust responsibility dimension:** The federal trust responsibility to tribal nations is a constitutional doctrine (grounded in the treaty-making power, the Commerce Clause, and the Property Clause) requiring the federal government to protect tribal lands, resources, and governmental capacities. The wholesale reduction of BIA capacity without tribal consent is a potential breach of this constitutional obligation — a democratic governance problem that no existing framework domain addresses.

### Section 3.4: Reservation Healthcare System and Medicaid Disenrollment

The Indian Health Service is chronically underfunded at approximately 55 cents on the dollar relative to need, as documented by the National Indian Health Board. Medicaid plays an essential supplementary role: approximately 40% of IHS spending is reimbursed through Medicaid, making Medicaid the primary federal healthcare financing mechanism for tribal communities beyond the IHS baseline.

**The OBBBA Medicaid-tribal nexus:** The One Big Beautiful Bill Act's work requirements include an exemption for federally recognized tribal members — a provision that was included in the final legislation after tribal advocacy. However, as documented by KFF Health News (March 2026), the exemption applies only to members of the 574 federally recognized tribes. Approximately 24,000 Native Americans in Louisiana alone are members of state-recognized tribes that are not federally recognized and are therefore subject to full work requirements.

The procedural disenrollment risk is the more acute near-term threat. Tribal health leaders, as documented by KFF Health News (March 2026), fear that their communities will experience disproportionate procedural disenrollment during the August-November 2026 outreach period because: (a) reservation addresses are frequently not recognized by state Medicaid mailing databases; (b) internet access on reservations is limited and notice-by-website is inadequate; (c) tribal community health programs do not have the administrative capacity to assist all members in completing complex exemption documentation. The COVID-era Medicaid unwinding process produced evidence that Native American enrollees experienced procedural disenrollment at higher rates than any other population category.

**The democratic participation nexus:** Medicaid disenrollment on reservations is not merely a healthcare access problem. Through the NVRA, it is simultaneously a voter registration suppression mechanism. Every Medicaid disenrollment that does not trigger a NVRA voter registration transaction is a missed voter registration opportunity in a community already facing acute registration barriers. The compounding of Medicaid disenrollment with NVRA non-compliance creates a structural voter suppression mechanism that operates entirely through administrative dysfunction rather than through discriminatory intent — making it largely immune to the remaining intentional discrimination standard required for VRA Section 3 protection.

### Section 3.5: Post-Callais Litigation Architecture for Native Voting Rights

The *Callais* ruling's Native American impact is direct and documented. The American Democracy Minute analysis (May 4, 2026) explicitly stated that the decision affects "Hispanic, Native, and Asian-American districts" in addition to the Black-majority Louisiana district at issue. The NARF response (April 29, 2026) stated that the ruling "cruelly undercuts" the foundational tool for Native voters to protect themselves from racial discrimination.

**What remains:** NARF has stated that it has never lost on the merits of any redistricting lawsuit it has brought on behalf of Native people. However, the *Callais* ruling changes the legal framework within which those cases are litigated. Section 2 claims that would previously have been analyzed under the Gingles framework (showing that a minority community is sufficiently numerous, geographically compact, and politically cohesive to constitute a majority in a properly drawn district) now face an additional Equal Protection challenge if the drawn district looks "too racial." This is not just an academic difficulty: it creates a litigation environment in which states can simultaneously make it harder for Native voters to elect representatives of their choice (through dilution) and make it legally impossible to correct the dilution (through *Callais*-based Equal Protection challenges to remedial districts).

**VAIA enforcement gaps:** The Native American Voting Rights Act (sometimes referred to as NAVRA), introduced in various forms since 2019, would address several of the most acute Native voting barriers: tribal ID acceptance, polling place location requirements on or near reservations, and federal administrative capacity to assist tribal communities with voter registration. The bill has not passed the Senate in any Congress. In the post-*Callais* environment, the NAVRA becomes the primary legislative vehicle for restoring effective Native voting rights protection — but it faces a Senate pathway that is no clearer than in previous Congresses.

The Watson v. Republican National Committee SCOTUS case (January 2026 tribal amicus brief) addressed ballot receipt deadlines — specifically whether states can extend deadlines for ballots cast by rural voters, including Native American voters, who face mail delivery delays due to postal infrastructure gaps on reservations. The National Congress of American Indians, Alaska Federation of Natives, and Native Vote Washington filed a joint amicus brief explaining the unique postal infrastructure barriers. The case's outcome is directly relevant to the VAIA enforcement analysis.

### Section 3.6: Domain 45 Research Roadmap

**Proposed domain title:** Domain 45 — Tribal Sovereignty, Native American Voter Suppression, and the Post-Callais Legal Vacuum: How the Systematic Dismantlement of Voting Rights Protection in Indian Country Reveals a Democratic Design Failure That Predates the Current Crisis

**Priority research questions:**

1. What is the full scope of post-*Shelby County* restrictive voting legislation that has directly targeted Native American communities, state by state, and what is the documented impact on Native voter registration and turnout in jurisdictions with significant tribal populations?

2. How does the *Callais* ruling change the litigation strategy available to NARF and other Native voting rights organizations, and what is the strongest remaining legal pathway — Section 3 bail-in, constitutional equal protection, Fifteenth Amendment direct claims — for challenging state voting restrictions targeting tribal communities?

3. What is the mechanism by which BIA workforce collapse and reorganization without tribal consultation degrades tribal democratic governance capacity, and how does this interact with the federal government's trust responsibility obligations?

4. How does the OBBBA Medicaid work requirement implementation — particularly the procedural disenrollment risk documented during the 2023-2024 COVID unwinding — affect Native American voter registration through the NVRA nexus?

5. What is the NAVRA's specific legislative content, what has prevented its passage in previous Congresses, and what is the realistic pathway to enactment given the current Senate composition?

6. What does the comparative evidence from New Zealand's Maori electoral rolls, Canada's Indian Act electoral provisions, and the Nordic countries' Sami Parliament electoral systems tell us about how democracies with Indigenous populations can design electoral infrastructure that respects both civic equality and tribal sovereignty?

**Primary sources:** NARF (Native American Voting Rights Project — active litigation tracker); NCAI (Native Vote initiative); KFF Health News (Medicaid-tribal nexus documentation); Alaska Beacon (BIA reorganization reporting, March 2026); Brennan Center (post-Shelby state legislation tracker); NARF *Callais* response; American Democracy Minute *Callais* cross-minority analysis; Harvard Law Review "Securing Indian Voting Rights" (2016, precedent analysis); Yale Law Journal "Enhancing Tribal Sovereignty in Federal Elections"; National Indian Health Board FY2026 budget analysis.

**Estimated scope:** 10,000–12,000 words, 65–75 citations. The research complexity (14–18 hours) reflects the specialized federal Indian law framework and the comparative dimension. ## Part IV: Alternative Candidates — Additional Strong Phase 2 Options

The two priority candidates above (Domains 44 and 45) are recommended as the highest-urgency additions to the framework following the *Callais* ruling. The following three alternative candidates represent additional strong Phase 2 options that should be sequenced in Domains 46–48.

### Alternative Candidate 1: Post-Insurrection Accountability and Transitional Democratic Justice (Domain 46)

**Why elevated to high priority in May 2026:** The May 2026 context has sharpened this candidate's urgency in ways not fully captured in ITEM50's initial assessment. The DOJ's ongoing vacating of January 6 convictions — including the seditious conspiracy convictions of Oath Keepers and Proud Boys leaders — has proceeded further than anticipated in early May. The Fulton County prosecution remains active but faces significant political pressure. The 14th Amendment Section 3 litigation that ITEM50 identified as the primary near-term accountability pathway is now generating circuit court precedent that will shape SCOTUS consideration.

What makes this candidate distinctly strong as a Phase 2 domain: the transitional justice framing — analyzing how democracies restore institutional legitimacy after autocratic ruptures — is unique to this domain and has the most direct cross-domain synthesis value of any candidate in the Phase 2 set. Every domain in the framework that documents democratic backsliding ultimately points toward the question this candidate answers: what institutional architecture enables democratic accountability when the normal accountability mechanisms have been captured?

**Estimated research hours:** 12–16. Coalition strength: medium-high (strong among rule-of-law conservatives, civil liberties community, and January 6 victim advocacy network). Urgency window: Section 3 litigation circuit splits create a 2026–2027 SCOTUS petition window.

### Alternative Candidate 2: Labor Power, Collective Bargaining, and Democratic Participation (Domain 47)

**Why elevated in May 2026:** The NLRB has been non-functional since early 2025, with multiple Board members removed. The Emergency Workplace Organizing Committee has documented an extraordinary surge in alternative organizing outside the formal NLRB process. The May Day 2026 labor mobilization — which coincided with existing Domain 29 updates documenting the administration's FACE Act enforcement and protest suppression targeting — demonstrates the convergence of the labor organizing surge with the first amendment suppression architecture the framework already tracks.

The union density–democratic participation nexus is the strongest empirical connection between economic policy and political participation in the social science literature, and no existing domain addresses it. The PRO Act's Senate pathway if Democrats gain seats in November 2026 creates a specific legislative window that the framework should be positioned to inform.

**Estimated research hours:** 10–14. Coalition strength: high (AFL-CIO, SEIU, UAW, Starbucks Workers United, Amazon Labor Union). Urgency window: NLRB board restoration as a 2027 legislative priority.

### Alternative Candidate 3: Epistemic Infrastructure and the Disinformation Crisis (Domain 48)

**Why elevated in May 2026:** The WEF March 2026 report identifying AI-enabled cognitive manipulation as the primary information environment risk for the 2026 election cycle was not yet available when ITEM50 was written. The industrial-scale deployment of AI deepfakes in Arizona, Georgia, and Pennsylvania primary races has now been documented by multiple sources. The EU AI Act's deepfake labeling requirements became enforceable in August 2026 — creating a transatlantic regulatory divergence that is itself an advocacy window.

This candidate's cross-domain synthesis value is exceptionally high: every domain in the framework that addresses democratic deliberation (voting rights, media ecosystem, higher education, AI governance) ultimately depends on the epistemic foundation that this domain would analyze and propose to protect. The June 12 FISA gate also creates a direct connection: surveillance architecture and epistemic infrastructure are the two most acute democratic design problems with concrete 2026 deadlines.

**Estimated research hours:** 10–14. Coalition strength: high (Stanford Internet Observatory, Brennan Center, Knight Foundation, EFF/ACLU with tension requiring navigation). Urgency window: August 2026 primaries as first deepfakes-in-elections stress test.

The framing problem — how administrative architecture functions as a democratic exclusion mechanism — has a direct and compelling answer that institutional audiences can act on. **Domain 44 (4/5):** The disability rights institutional infrastructure (NDRN, DREDF, CCD) is highly developed and already engaged on SAVE Act and OBBBA implementation. The academic disability law programs at major law schools provide additional institutional audience capacity.

### Dimension 4: Cross-Domain Synthesis Value

**Domain 44 (4/5):** Strong connections to Domain 1 (voting rights), Domain 31 (Medicaid crisis), Domain 41 (domestic intelligence and disability), Domain 22 (racial justice — disabled voters of color as a convergent exclusion category). The NVRA-Medicaid-disability nexus is the strongest single cross-domain synthesis point: it connects healthcare access, civic participation, and administrative accountability in a single analytical frame.

**Domain 45 (4/5):** Strong connections to Domain 40 (constitutional architecture and tribal sovereignty — deepening the existing coverage), Domain 9 (federalism — nation-to-nation relations as a distinct federal design question), Domain 1 (voting rights — the legal architecture question), Domain 22 (racial justice — though distinct from civil rights framing).

**Alt 1 Transitional Justice (5/5):** The highest cross-domain synthesis value of any candidate: every domain that documents democratic backsliding ultimately points toward this domain's question about recovery architecture.

## Part VI: Summary Recommendations

**Highest priority — immediate research authorization:** Domain 44 (Disability Rights & Voting Access) and Domain 45 (Tribal Sovereignty & Native American Voter Suppression). Both were elevated to critical status by the April 29 *Callais* ruling and both have concrete 2026 deadline windows (OBBBA Medicaid outreach period, SAVE Act Senate consideration, post-*Callais* litigation architecture).

**Second-tier — ready for research after Domains 44-45 complete:** Alt 3 (Epistemic Infrastructure, Domain 46 candidate) given its June 12 FISA gate connection and August 2026 primaries urgency. Alt 1 (Transitional Justice, Domain 47 candidate) for its cross-domain synthesis value and 2026 Section 3 litigation timeline. Alt 2 (Labor Power, Domain 48 candidate) for its institutional depth and PRO Act 2027 legislative window.

**Critical observation for research sequencing:** The post-*Callais* moment (April 29, 2026) has created a narrow window in which the framework can address the VRA's effective elimination as a new structural crisis — rather than as historical background. Research on both Domains 44 and 45 should be initiated within the next two to three weeks to ensure the domain documents capture the immediate post-*Callais* organizational and legal response before the institutional response period concludes and the crisis normalizes.
