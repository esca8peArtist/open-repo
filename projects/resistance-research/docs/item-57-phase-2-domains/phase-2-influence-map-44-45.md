# ITEM57: Phase 2 Influence Map — Domains 44 and 45

*Completed May 9, 2026. Influencer ecosystem mapping and outreach strategy for Phase 2 Domains 44 (Disability Rights & Voting Access) and 45 (Tribal Sovereignty & Native American Voter Suppression). The April 29 Callais ruling creates an immediate engagement window with both ecosystems. The co-design strategy outlined in Part IV represents a deliberate evolution beyond Phase 1's one-directional distribution model.*

---

## Part I: The Disability Rights Ecosystem

### Section 1.1: NDRN — National Disability Rights Network

**Organizational overview:** NDRN is the nonprofit membership organization for the 57 federally mandated Protection and Advocacy (P&A) Systems and Client Assistance Programs (CAP) — the largest provider of legally based advocacy services to people with disabilities in the United States. The P&A/CAP network operates in all 50 states, the District of Columbia, Puerto Rico, and the US territories. Each P&A is an independent organization that receives federal funding under the Developmental Disabilities Assistance and Bill of Rights Act, the Protection and Advocacy for Individuals with Mental Illness Act, and several other federal mandates. NDRN serves as the national membership organization that coordinates federal policy positions and provides technical assistance to the 57 members.

**Why NDRN is the highest-priority Domain 44 partner:** NDRN has statutory authority over Olmstead enforcement at the state level — each P&A member has federal mandate to investigate and litigate denial of rights in institutional settings. NDRN's federal policy work directly addresses both the SAVE Act (the Arc Rhode Island's SAVE Act analysis, which NDRN circulated as a member network resource, is one of the most precise disability-specific SAVE Act analyses available) and the OBBBA's Medicaid work requirements. NDRN's 2024 NVRA compliance survey — finding that disability agencies have the lowest NVRA compliance rates of any covered agency category — is exactly the type of empirical documentation that Domain 44 needs.

**Engagement approach:** Contact NDRN's federal policy director with a specific ask: Does NDRN have an internal analysis of the NVRA compliance implications of the OBBBA August 2026 outreach period? If so, permission to cite it; if not, would NDRN be interested in co-developing the analysis for Domain 44? This is a co-production invitation, not a distribution request. NDRN's policy team produces advocacy documents that are similar in structure to the framework domain documents; co-authorship or formal citation creates a distribution pathway through all 57 P&A members.

**Contact pathway:** NDRN's website (ndrn.org) lists federal policy staff. The digital democracy project has also indexed NDRN as a member organization. The most efficient entry point is through the Arc or CCD (see below), both of which have existing relationships with NDRN's policy team.

**Estimated distribution multiplier:** 57 P&A members × average 2-3 key contacts per member = 114-171 direct distribution recipients within the disability rights legal advocacy network. NDRN distribution also reaches the network of disability rights attorneys in each state who receive P&A bulletins.

### Section 1.2: DREDF — Disability Rights Education and Defense Fund

**Organizational overview:** DREDF is the oldest national disability rights legal organization in the United States, founded in 1979, and has shaped the majority of federal disability rights laws enacted in the past four decades. DREDF's legal strategy work encompasses the ADA, the Rehabilitation Act, the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act, and international disability rights frameworks (CRPD). DREDF maintains one of the most developed litigation strategy libraries in the disability rights movement.

**Why DREDF is the highest-priority Domain 44 legal strategy partner:** DREDF has published the most comprehensive analysis available of the ADA-HAVA nexus — the intersection of accessibility law and Help America Vote Act implementation. Their 2025 "Navigating the ADA-HAVA Nexus" analysis is the precise source Domain 44 needs for the polling place accessibility and voting technology accessibility subsections. DREDF has also published on the comparative international disability rights framework, specifically the CRPD Article 29 (voting rights) analysis, which Domain 44 will use for its comparative section.

**Post-Callais relevance:** DREDF's litigation strategy expertise is directly relevant to the post-*Callais* legal architecture question in Domain 44's Section 2.6. The domain's analysis of which legal claims survive the VRA Section 2 gutting — specifically whether ADA Section 504, Rehabilitation Act, and constitutional claims can replace Section 2 as the basis for disability voting rights challenges — requires input from DREDF's litigation team that cannot be obtained from secondary sources alone.

**Engagement approach:** DREDF is a natural co-reviewer for Domain 44's litigation strategy section. The engagement ask: "We're developing a domain-level analysis of disability voting rights in the post-*Callais* environment and we want to make sure the litigation architecture section is accurate. Would DREDF's legal team be willing to review and comment on that section before publication?" This is a review invitation, not a distribution request — the kind of professional engagement that policy lawyers regularly provide.

**Demos Voting Rights Initiative connection:** DREDF has an existing collaboration with Demos on voting rights — the Demos Voting Rights Initiative has published jointly with DREDF on accessible voting access. The Demos connection provides a second-tier distribution pathway: Demos' network of progressive policy researchers and state-level advocates reaches audiences beyond the pure disability rights community into the broader voting rights space.

### Section 1.3: CCD — Consortium for Citizens with Disabilities (also known as Consortium for Constituents with Disabilities)

**Organizational overview:** The CCD is a coalition of more than 100 national disability organizations that advocates for federal policy enabling self-determination, independence, and full inclusion of people with disabilities. CCD operates through task forces organized by policy area (health, education, employment, housing, long-term services and supports, civil rights and voting). The Civil Rights and Voting task force is the most directly relevant to Domain 44.

**Why CCD is the highest-priority Domain 44 coalition distribution channel:** CCD's 100+ member organizations constitute the full breadth of the disability rights institutional infrastructure. A document distributed through CCD reaches organizations across the spectrum: large national organizations (Arc, AAPD, Autism Society of America, National Federation of the Blind), legal advocacy organizations (NDRN, DREDF, Disability Rights Advocates), policy research organizations (ANCOR, Bazelon Center for Mental Health Law), and single-disability-focus organizations with large membership bases. CCD distribution is the single highest-leverage mechanism for reaching the disability rights institutional audience.

**Engagement approach:** CCD does not typically co-author external research documents, but it regularly circulates policy analyses that align with its legislative agenda. The engagement pathway is through the CCD Civil Rights and Voting task force coordinator. A request that CCD circulate Domain 44 as part of its SAVE Act Senate advocacy toolkit (timing: when the Senate takes up the SAVE Act) is a specific, actionable ask with a clear distribution rationale.

**Legislative agenda alignment:** CCD's current legislative priorities include: opposing the SAVE Act, advocating for NVRA enforcement at disability agencies, and engaging on the OBBBA's Medicaid work requirement implementation. Domain 44 directly advances all three priorities. This alignment is the basis for the distribution ask: not "please share our research" but "this analysis addresses your three top legislative priorities and can strengthen your Senate advocacy."

### Section 1.4: Academic Partners — Disability Law and Voting Rights

The academic disability rights community provides three distinct contributions to Phase 2: (1) peer review of the domain document's legal analysis before distribution; (2) distribution through law school clinics and policy journals that reach practitioner audiences; (3) co-authorship opportunities for the comparative international section.

**Priority academic contacts:**

- **Georgetown Law Center on Poverty and Inequality:** The Georgetown Journal on Law and Public Policy published "Navigating the ADA-HAVA Nexus" (2025), the most technically precise academic analysis of disability-voting law intersection. The Georgetown law faculty who produced this analysis are the natural peer reviewers for Domain 44's polling-place and voting-technology accessibility sections.

- **Rutgers School of Management and Labor Relations, Program for Disability Research:** Published "Accessible Democracy: Reducing Voting Obstacles for People with Disabilities" — the most comprehensive empirical study of disability voting barriers available. The Rutgers research team has data on voting difficulties that Domain 44 can use for its structural analysis.

- **Stanford Policy Clinic and Harvard Kennedy School Shorenstein Center:** Both institutions produce policy-oriented democracy research that overlaps with the epistemic and electoral dimensions of Domain 44. These are secondary contacts for the post-*Callais* legal architecture analysis rather than primary co-production partners.

**Practitioner network:** State-level disability voting rights advocates — primarily P&A attorneys and civil rights litigators who work in states with significant SAVE Act implementation battles — are the Domain 44 practitioner network. The most active state-level networks in 2026 are in Wisconsin (SAVE Act pilot state litigation), South Carolina (ACLU-SC and NDRN member challenging voter assistance restrictions), and Arizona (active ADA polling place accessibility litigation). These practitioners are target co-reviewers for the state-specific sections of Domain 44.

## Part II: The Tribal Sovereignty Ecosystem

### Section 2.1: NCAI — National Congress of American Indians

**Organizational overview:** NCAI is the oldest, largest, and most representative American Indian and Alaska Native organization in the United States, founded in 1944. NCAI represents 575 tribal nation members and serves as the principal forum for tribal governments to communicate, build consensus, and collectively address issues that affect their communities. NCAI's Native Vote initiative is its primary electoral participation program; the initiative coordinates voter registration drives, poll worker training, polling place access advocacy, and legislative work on Native voting rights.

**Why NCAI is the highest-priority Domain 45 distribution partner:** NCAI represents tribal governments directly — not individual tribal members, but the tribal government entities themselves. A Domain 45 framework document distributed through NCAI is distributed to sovereign government bodies, not just to advocacy organizations. This is categorically different from the Phase 1 distribution model and reflects the unique status of tribal nations as co-governing entities rather than interest groups.

**Post-Callais urgency:** NCAI filed the Watson v. RNC amicus brief (with Alaska Federation of Natives and Native Vote Washington) in January 2026, demonstrating that NCAI's legal team is actively engaged in Supreme Court litigation on Native voting rights. The NCAI response to *Callais* (filed April 29, 2026) explicitly stated that the ruling "cruelly undercuts the Voting Rights Act, the foundational tool for Native voters." A Domain 45 analysis that provides the detailed structural analysis behind this statement is directly useful to NCAI's ongoing advocacy and litigation planning.

**Engagement approach:** NCAI's policy team and the Native Vote initiative director are the primary contacts. The engagement ask has two components: (1) Would NCAI be willing to review and comment on the federal Indian law framework sections of Domain 45 before publication? (2) Would NCAI include Domain 45 in its post-*Callais* tribal government briefing materials? The second ask requires that the document be complete and reviewed before NCAI's next major communication to tribal governments — timing the outreach for the June-July period maximizes relevance.

### Section 2.2: NARF — Native American Rights Fund

**Organizational overview:** NARF is the oldest and largest nonprofit law firm dedicated to asserting and defending the rights of Indian tribes, organizations, and individuals. NARF handles approximately 50 cases at any given time, covering tribal sovereignty, voting rights, trust responsibility, and treaty rights. NARF has established the dedicated Native American Voting Rights Project (vote.narf.org) as its voting rights litigation clearinghouse.

**Why NARF is the highest-priority Domain 45 legal strategy partner:** NARF has never lost on the merits of any redistricting lawsuit it has brought on behalf of Native people. NARF's post-*Callais* strategy — how it intends to continue litigating Native voting rights in the new Section 2 legal environment — is the most important single piece of information for Domain 45's litigation architecture section. No secondary source can provide this analysis with the accuracy that NARF's own attorneys can.

**NARF's active 2026 cases:** NARF filed a Motion to Intervene in June 2025 in the Montana SB 490 case (restricting Election Day voter registration for Native voters). NARF filed the Watson amicus brief in January 2026. NARF's response to the *Callais* ruling explicitly addressed the impact on Native redistricting cases NARF is currently litigating in Montana, South Dakota, Nebraska, and North Dakota. The domain document should cite specific NARF cases with case numbers and current status — material that requires NARF's own litigation tracking data.

**Engagement approach:** The engagement ask to NARF is explicit about the co-review dimension: "We are developing a domain-level analysis of Native American voting rights in the post-*Callais* environment. The litigation strategy section is the core of the document, and we want to ensure it accurately reflects the current state of Native voting rights litigation and the legal pathways NARF is pursuing. We would welcome a review of that section before publication and would cite NARF's input with attribution." This is a professional co-review request, not a general endorsement ask.

**Distribution through NARF:** NARF publishes a Native American Voting Rights Project bulletin and maintains vote.narf.org as a practitioner resource. A Domain 45 document that NARF has reviewed and cited as a resource would appear on vote.narf.org — the primary practitioner resource for tribal voting rights attorneys in all 50 states.

### Section 2.3: Tribal Attorneys — IRA-72 Legal Representatives and Tribal Election Commissioners

The "IRA-72" tribal nations — those organized under the 1934 Indian Reorganization Act with BIA-supervised constitutional election procedures — have a specific democratic governance interest that goes beyond voting rights to include the internal election architecture of tribal governments themselves. The BIA workforce collapse documented in the Alaska Beacon (March 2026) directly affects the capacity of IRA-72 tribes to conduct constitutional amendments and certified elections.

**Why tribal attorneys are essential as a distinct contact tier:** NCAI represents tribal governments at the federal policy level. NARF represents tribal governments in federal litigation. But the attorneys who advise individual tribal nations on their internal governance — the election commissioners, constitutional law advisers, and government attorneys for specific tribes — have ground-truth knowledge of BIA non-functionality that is not visible in NCAI or NARF public communications.

**Engagement approach:** The engagement pathway for tribal government attorneys is through the National Native American Bar Association (NNABA) and through the tribal law programs at University of New Mexico, Colorado, University of Montana, and Arizona State's Indian Legal Program. The ask is a targeted distribution of Domain 45 with an invitation for practitioner comment — specifically on the BIA restructuring section, which is the most likely section to have inaccuracies that practitioners can identify.

**IRA-72 election interference specifics:** The BIA maintains a list of tribal constitutions that include BIA election supervision requirements. A Freedom of Information Act request to BIA for the list of tribes whose constitutions require BIA election certification could produce a document that Domain 45 can cite as evidence of the scope of potential governance disruption if BIA staffing continues to collapse. This is a research action, not just an outreach action — but it is one that a tribal attorney contact might be able to identify as already available through existing tribal government channels.

### Section 2.4: Native American Civil Rights Organizations

**Indian Law Resource Center:** The Indian Law Resource Center (ILRC) is an independent nonprofit law organization that assists Native nations in the United States and internationally to protect human rights, lands, resources, and cultures. ILRC's particular relevance to Domain 45 is its international human rights framework work: ILRC has been central in advocating for US implementation of the UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP), which provides the international reform architecture anchor for Domain 45's comparative section. UNDRIP Article 5 (the right to maintain and strengthen distinct political institutions), Article 18 (the right to participate in decision-making in matters that affect rights), and Article 20 (the right to maintain and develop political, economic, and social systems) are the international framework parallels to the domestic democratic design analysis.

**Seventh Generation Institute:** The Seventh Generation Institute (SGI) focuses on Native youth leadership and civic education — a dimension of the tribal democracy problem that neither NCAI nor NARF specifically addresses. Native youth voter registration and civic education are the long-term capacity-building dimension of the voting rights problem; SGI's programs are the practitioner infrastructure for that work. The engagement with SGI is secondary — distribution rather than co-production — but reaching SGI's network adds a youth civic education audience that the framework has not previously targeted.

**Native Vote Washington and Alaska Federation of Natives:** Both organizations filed the Watson amicus brief with NCAI, demonstrating their engagement with federal litigation on Native voting barriers. Both are geographically concentrated (Pacific Northwest and Alaska) and represent regional organizations that can reach tribal communities in states where NCAI and NARF do not have strong practitioner-level relationships with specific tribes.

## Part III: Cross-Domain Connections

### Section 3.1: Disability + Voting — The DREDF/Demos Collaboration Model

The existing DREDF/Demos Voting Rights Initiative collaboration is the most developed cross-domain institutional relationship in the Domain 44 ecosystem. Demos is a progressive policy organization that bridges between electoral democracy research (voting rights, campaign finance, elections administration) and economic democracy research (economic inequality, labor rights, racial wealth gap). DREDF's collaboration with Demos on voting access creates a pre-existing institutional bridge that Phase 2 can leverage.

**The collaboration model in practice:** DREDF-Demos joint publications have reached distribution networks in both the disability rights space (through DREDF's network) and the progressive policy space (through Demos' network of state policy organizations, the Economic Policy Institute affiliate network, and the National Conference of State Legislatures contacts). A Domain 44 analysis that explicitly builds on the DREDF-Demos collaboration model — citing their joint work and engaging both organizations as co-reviewers — can access both distribution networks simultaneously.

**What makes this collaboration replicable:** The DREDF-Demos model works because both organizations share a commitment to democratic participation as the organizing frame for their respective policy areas. This is exactly the democratic-design frame that the Phase 2 domains are designed to provide. The collaboration is not just a distribution mechanism; it is an organizational model for how disability rights organizations and voting rights organizations can jointly advocate for structural changes that serve both communities.

### Section 3.2: Disability + Tribal — The Native American Disability Rights Intersection

The intersection of Native American identity and disability is one of the most understudied dimensions of both communities' civil rights advocacy. Native Americans have the highest disability rate of any racial/ethnic group in the United States — approximately 26.5% of American Indians and Alaska Natives report having a disability, compared to 13% of the overall US population (2021 American Community Survey). The disproportionate disability rate reflects documented occupational hazards (mining, agriculture, construction), environmental factors (proximity to extraction industries, contaminated water), and the chronic underfunding of IHS preventive care.

**The compound exclusion mechanism:** A Native American disabled person in a reservation community faces the SAVE Act's photo ID barrier, the Olmstead integration mandate's non-enforcement, the BIA's workforce collapse, the OBBBA's work requirement exemption documentation complexity, and the *Callais* ruling's elimination of Section 2 redistricting protection — simultaneously. No existing domain, organization, or advocacy infrastructure analyzes this intersection as a coherent structural problem. Domain 44 and Domain 45 together create the analytical frame; the cross-domain connections section of each domain should explicitly document the compound exclusion experienced by Native American disabled voters.

**Organizational gap:** Neither NDRN/DREDF nor NCAI/NARF has a specific program addressing the intersection of Native American identity and disability. This gap is itself an organizational development opportunity: a Phase 2 domain that names this intersection and provides structural analysis creates the impetus for organizational infrastructure that does not yet exist. The academic programs that address both disability law and tribal law (University of New Mexico's combined Indigenous Peoples Law and Policy / Disability Rights programs; Arizona State's Indian Legal Program / disability law faculty) are the most likely institutional homes for developing this intersection analysis.

### Section 3.3: Common Ground — Administrative Dysfunction as a Democratic Suppression Mechanism

Both Domain 44 and Domain 45 share a structural analytical insight that is more powerful stated jointly than in either domain alone: administrative dysfunction — the deliberate or negligent failure of administrative systems to function as designed — is a primary mechanism of democratic suppression in 2026 America. The SAVE Act is a legislative threat; the OBBBA's work requirements are a policy threat. But the NVRA non-compliance at disability agencies, the BIA workforce collapse, the Medicaid procedural disenrollment, and the Olmstead enforcement deprioritization are all administrative dysfunction mechanisms. They suppress democratic participation not through discriminatory intent (which is increasingly difficult to prove after *Callais*) but through the systematic failure of systems that would, if functioning, connect marginalized communities to civic participation.

This shared analytical frame creates a distribution strategy: a joint outreach communication to both the disability rights and tribal sovereignty ecosystems, framed around administrative dysfunction as a democratic suppression mechanism, creates a cross-ecosystem moment that neither domain alone creates. Organizations in both ecosystems are experiencing the same administrative dysfunction — they are just experiencing it through different programmatic systems. The framework provides the analytical synthesis that connects their experiences.

## Part IV: Phase 2 Co-Design Strategy

### Section 4.1: Moving Beyond Phase 1 Outreach

Phase 1's outreach model was distribution-centric: produce research, identify institutional audiences, distribute through email and digital channels, collect feedback. This model is appropriate for Phase 1, when the framework is establishing credibility and demonstrating the value of the democratic-design analytical frame to organizations that have not previously encountered it.

Phase 2 requires a different model for two reasons. First, institutional audiences that adopted Phase 1 material already understand the framework and its analytical approach. They do not need to be convinced that democratic-design framing is valuable; they are already using it. Second, the complexity of Domain 44 (disability law, NVRA, Olmstead, ABLE accounts) and Domain 45 (federal Indian law, tribal sovereignty, BIA trust responsibility, IRA-72 election procedures) exceeds what secondary-source synthesis alone can accurately capture. Practitioner input at the drafting stage produces more accurate and more useful documents than post-hoc review.

**The co-design model:** For Phase 2 domains, the production process should include a practitioner consultation phase before the domain document is distributed publicly. This means:

1. Complete a research draft (approximately 70% complete, with the legal architecture and litigation strategy sections clearly flagged as requiring practitioner input)
2. Send the draft to two to three named practitioner contacts with a specific, bounded review request (30-60 minutes, focused on 2-3 specific sections)
3. Incorporate practitioner feedback into the final document, with attribution as appropriate
4. Distribute the final document with a co-reviewer acknowledgment that creates institutional credibility

This is not a full co-authorship model (which would require organizational buy-in and review processes that would delay publication by months). It is a targeted practitioner consultation model that takes 2-3 weeks additional time in exchange for significantly higher document accuracy and institutional credibility.

### Section 4.2: Priority Co-Design Partners

**For Domain 44 (Disability/Voting):**

- **Primary practitioner consultation:** NDRN federal policy director (NVRA at disability agencies section) and DREDF legal team (post-*Callais* litigation architecture section)
- **Secondary review:** One state-level P&A attorney from a SAVE Act battleground state (Wisconsin, South Carolina, or Arizona) for the state-specific barriers section
- **Academic review:** Georgetown Law faculty (ADA-HAVA nexus section)

**For Domain 45 (Tribal/Voting):**

- **Primary practitioner consultation:** NARF Native American Voting Rights Project attorney (post-*Callais* litigation strategy section and active cases citation check)
- **Secondary review:** NCAI policy team (BIA restructuring and trust responsibility section)
- **Tribal government attorney review:** One IRA-72 tribal government attorney or election commissioner for the BIA election supervision section (through NNABA contact)
- **Academic review:** Harvard Law Review "Securing Indian Voting Rights" authors (the 2016 analysis remains the most authoritative academic treatment and the authors are likely still active in this field)

### Section 4.3: The Co-Design Outreach Protocol

The co-design outreach should follow a specific protocol that distinguishes it from Phase 1 distribution:

**Step 1 — Relationship-first outreach (before sending material):** Contact each practitioner with a brief introductory message that: (a) describes the framework and provides a link to the most relevant Phase 1 domain; (b) describes the Phase 2 domain under development; (c) states specifically which section needs practitioner review and why that practitioner is the right person; (d) asks for 30-60 minutes of their time, with a clearly specified deadline.

**Step 2 — Targeted draft delivery:** Send only the specific section requiring review, plus a one-page context document explaining the domain's overall framing. Do not send the full draft — practitioners are busy professionals and a full 10,000-word draft is a deterrent to review. A 2,000-3,000 word section with specific questions is an actionable review request.

**Step 3 — Attribution and acknowledgment:** Communicate clearly how the practitioner's input will be acknowledged. Options: (a) formal co-reviewer credit in the document footer; (b) citation of specific organizational publication the practitioner directs the framework to; (c) personal acknowledgment in a distribution cover email. Most practitioners will prefer (b) — it promotes their organization's work while providing the framework the accuracy it needs.

**Step 4 — Distribution notification:** When the domain document is publicly distributed, send the practitioner a personal notification with the final document and a note on how their input shaped the analysis. This closes the loop and creates the relational precedent for Phase 3 co-design engagement.

### Section 4.4: Measuring Phase 2 Co-Design Success

Phase 2 co-design success is measured differently from Phase 1 distribution success. Phase 1 metrics: open rates, click-through rates, citation counts, adoption by institutional audiences. Phase 2 metrics:

1. **Co-reviewer count:** How many named practitioners reviewed drafts before publication? Target: 2-4 per domain.
2. **Accuracy citations:** Are the domain documents cited by practitioner organizations in their own advocacy materials? Target: at least 2 organizational citations per domain within 90 days.
3. **Collaboration conversion rate:** What percentage of Phase 1 adopters who receive Domain 44 and 45 material respond with engagement (questions, comment, request for further information) rather than passive receipt? Target: 25% (significantly higher than Phase 1 passive distribution rate).
4. **Cross-domain synthesis uptake:** Are domain documents from the disability and tribal sovereignty ecosystems citing each other, and is the framework playing a role in those cross-citations? Target: at least one cross-citation (disability org citing tribal voting rights material or vice versa) within 6 months.

These metrics are more difficult to track than open rates and click-throughs, but they measure the kind of institutional impact — changed analytical frames, modified advocacy strategies, new cross-domain collaborations — that Phase 2 is designed to produce.
