# Advocacy Implementation Guide
## For Advocacy Coalitions and NGOs

**Who this is for**: Campaign directors, coalition coordinators, communications directors, policy advocates, and organizing directors at advocacy organizations working on any of the 35 domains. Also relevant for state-level affiliate staff of national organizations.

**The core task**: Use the 35-domain framework to build a coherent multi-issue campaign that sequences advocacy actions for maximum political impact rather than running parallel siloed campaigns on each issue.

---

## The Monday Morning Question

You are running a coalition of 12 organizations. Each has a different issue priority. You have a shared campaign to manage, a media budget, and a six-month window before a legislative session. What do you lead with, what order do you move through issues, and how do you keep the coalition together while doing it?

Answer: use the framework's cross-domain synthesis to find the gravitational issue that connects your coalition, lead with it, and cascade from there.

---

## Five-Step Decision Tree

**Step 1: Map your coalition to the 35 domains.**

List every coalition member organization and assign them to their primary domain in the framework. A union is Domain 17 (Labor). A housing advocacy group is Domain 13. An immigration legal organization is Domain 16. A civil liberties group is Domain 7. An environmental group is Domain 15. Now look at the cross-domain synthesis in Section 5.4 of the proposal. Which domains have the most reinforcing connections to your coalition's composition? That intersection is your campaign's gravitational center.

For a coalition that spans labor, housing, and immigration: the gravitational center is the economic justice cluster (Domains 3, 13, 16, 17) with the electoral accountability frame (Domain 1) as the connective tissue. The campaign argument becomes: these policies persist because electoral systems give donor-class interests disproportionate power over median voter interests.

**Step 2: Identify your 12-week messaging sequence.**

The proposal's theory of change (Section 3) is explicit about sequencing: you cannot run all issues at once and win any of them. Choose one issue from your gravitational center to lead with for 12 weeks. Lead with the issue that has (a) the highest polling support in your target geography, (b) a specific near-term decision point (a vote, a regulatory deadline, a court hearing), and (c) an existing organized base that can turn people out. The framework's implementation roadmap identifies Wave 1 decision points — those are your targeting calendar.

After 12 weeks, transition to the adjacent domain in the cross-domain synthesis. Frame it as connected: "we won X because of Y structural problem; now we address Y." This is how the ACLU's 2025 "Defeat, Delay, Dilute" strategy worked — First Amendment protection (Domain 7) led, with Fourth Amendment surveillance (Domain 21) as the adjacent issue, framed as two faces of the same executive power overreach.

**Step 3: Run the influencer map against the framework.**

The proposal's policy-influencer-mapping.md identifies the key nodes in the policy advocacy network for each domain. Before launching a campaign on any domain, identify: (a) who are the two or three think tank fellows or researchers who have published on this issue in the last 18 months and whose work the framework cites? Engage them as validators. (b) Which congressional offices have staff who have requested additional framework materials? Those are your inside champions. (c) Which journalists have covered the specific domain in the last six months? Those are your earned media targets. The framework's evidentiary base is designed to be journalist-accessible — domain sections cite primary sources, not just secondary analyses, which means reporters can go directly to the source.

**Step 4: Sequence legal and legislative actions together.**

The ACLU's 2025 model demonstrated that litigation and advocacy are most powerful when sequenced, not run in parallel independently. For each domain in your campaign: identify whether there is an active or potential lawsuit that will generate a court ruling during your campaign window. If yes, sequence your advocacy crescendo to coincide with the ruling — either amplifying a favorable ruling or generating political cost for an unfavorable one. The litigation-tracker-2026.md in this research archive tracks the cases with the most direct domain relevance.

For example: if your campaign targets Domain 14 (Criminal Justice), identify the nearest relevant circuit court ruling on use of force standards or bail reform. Time your legislative hearing, your media push, and your coalition actions around that ruling window.

**Step 5: Build the alliance map before you need it.**

The most common coalition failure is learning that a potential partner organization is already engaged on your issue — and discovering it as a competitor rather than an ally. Before launching, map the full alliance landscape for your lead domain. Who is already working on this? Are they running a complementary campaign that you can amplify rather than duplicate? The proposal's domain sections name the organizations active on each issue — use that as your starting alliance inventory.

Identify one organization per domain in your coalition that will serve as the "domain lead" — responsible for maintaining the relationship with allied organizations on that domain, briefing coalition members on developments, and coordinating action. This prevents the coordination overhead from diffusing across everyone.

## Three Concrete Domain Applications

**Application 1: Using Domain 8 (Media and Information) for earned media strategy.**

Domain 8 documents the collapse of local news (2,900+ newspaper closures, 57% decline in newsroom employment) and its direct effect on democratic accountability — communities without local news have measurably higher municipal corruption rates and lower voter turnout. The advocacy application: when pitching a story to a local outlet, lead with the local news collapse data specific to your state or city. Reporters covering democracy issues are already primed to connect the dots. The domain's international benchmarks (BBC model, Finland's media literacy program, Nordic public media funding) give you a comparative frame that most U.S. political reporters have not seen.

**Application 2: Using Domain 3 (Democratic Participation) for participatory campaign tactics.**

Domain 3 documents the evidence base for participatory budgeting, citizens' assemblies, and digital deliberation platforms. These are not just policy proposals — they are campaign tactics. If your coalition is running a campaign on any domain, consider embedding a mini-citizens' assembly format in your campaign: convene a representative group of community members, give them the framework's domain analysis (in accessible language), and let them deliberate on priorities and recommendations. The output becomes your campaign's grassroots mandate — more compelling than polling because it demonstrates deliberation, not just preference. Barcelona's Decidim platform and Ireland's citizens' assembly process are documented in Domain 3 with enough detail to replicate the model at scale.

**Application 3: Using the 3.5% threshold analysis (Section 1.3) for campaign scale benchmarks.**

Erica Chenoweth's finding — no nonviolent campaign with active participation exceeding 3.5% of the population has ever failed — is in Section 1.3. In a congressional district, 3.5% is approximately 25,000 people. In a state of 5 million, it is 175,000. Use these numbers as explicit campaign benchmarks. Set a goal: we need X sustained active participants (not just petition signers — people who show up repeatedly). Track against it publicly. This reframes your campaign from "build support" to "reach a proven threshold" — a goal that has historical validity and motivates the last mile of participation that most campaigns never achieve.

## Common Mistakes in Advocacy Settings

**Issue siloing defeats the cross-domain argument.** Running a housing campaign and an immigration campaign as separate tracks, staffed by separate teams with separate messaging, leaves the framework's most powerful insight on the table: these issues reinforce each other, and the root cause is the same (electoral systems unresponsive to median voter preferences, Domains 1 and 7). The fix: create a shared messaging framework at the beginning of the campaign cycle that every team uses when introducing their specific issue. The shared frame is the proposal's Section 1.2 structural failures analysis.

**Mobilization without organizational depth.** The proposal's Section 1.3 is explicit: digital organizing lowers the cost of getting big fast, but organizations that grow fast without building organizational infrastructure collapse at the first defeat. For every mobilization push your coalition does, commit to an equal investment in infrastructure: chapter formation, leadership development, legal defense, and data systems. The ACLU's pre-2025 infrastructure investment is the model.

**Treating the research as a talking points source, not a strategy tool.** Coalition communications staff often excerpt findings from the framework for public messaging — "the U.S. scored 57/100 on the Democracy Meter" — without using the analytical structure to sequence the campaign. The research is strategy, not talking points. If you are using it only for quotes, you are underusing it by 80%.

## Implementation Tracking Checklist

- [ ] Coalition organizations mapped to 35 domains
- [ ] Cross-domain synthesis reviewed; gravitational issue identified
- [ ] 12-week messaging sequence drafted with decision-point calendar
- [ ] Influencer map completed for lead domain: researchers, congressional champions, journalists
- [ ] Litigation calendar integrated into advocacy sequence
- [ ] Alliance map completed; domain leads assigned
- [ ] 3.5% threshold calculated for your target geography; benchmark made public
- [ ] Participatory tactic (mini-assembly or equivalent) embedded in at least one campaign phase
- [ ] Organizational depth investment scheduled alongside mobilization pushes
- [ ] Quarterly review scheduled against implementation tracking template
