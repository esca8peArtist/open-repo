# Post-Distribution Feedback Integration Roadmap

**April 29, 2026**

This roadmap governs the post-distribution phase of the 35-domain Democratic Renewal Proposal. It is path-agnostic: the adaptation patterns, feedback mechanisms, success metrics, and decision trees documented here apply regardless of whether the user selects Path A (immediate full distribution), Path A+37 (hybrid phased deployment), or Path B (maintenance-first). The only path-specific notation appears in the feedback mechanism section, where Path A+37's phased structure creates an additional early signal loop not available in Path A.

The central thesis: institutional sectors do not adopt policy frameworks wholesale. They extract, adapt, and selectively deploy the components that fit their immediate operational context. This is not framework dilution — it is the mechanism through which a research document becomes embedded in institutional practice. Expecting holistic adoption is a measurement error that causes organizations to misread successful partial adoption as failure. This roadmap operationalizes selective adoption as the expected pattern and builds feedback loops that capture it.

## Part I: Adaptation Patterns by Sector

The five sectors receiving the 35-domain framework will adapt it through fundamentally different institutional logics. Each sector has a primary pressure point — the factor that determines whether the framework gets extracted and used or filed and forgotten. Understanding the pressure point is the prerequisite for both predicting adaptation behavior and designing feedback mechanisms that capture it.

### 1.1 State Legislatures

**Primary pressure point**: The session calendar.

State legislatures operate under severe time constraints with binary outcomes — bills pass or die within fixed windows, and the political cost of introducing legislation that fails without a fight is different from introducing legislation that generates a record even in defeat. The framework's most immediate value to legislative staff is not its 35-domain architecture; it is the pre-packaged legislative findings sections, cost-benefit estimates, and cross-jurisdictional precedent tables that reduce bill-drafting time.

**Expected adaptation pattern**: Selective extraction by domain cluster, driven by caucus composition. A state legislative caucus with strong labor alignment will pull Domain 17 (labor rights) and the fiscal impact evidence; a caucus with immigration-focused members will pull Domain 16; a redistricting-focused caucus will pull Domain 1f. The probability of holistic 35-domain adoption at the legislative level is near zero — no bill is titled "the 35-Domain Democratic Renewal Act." The probability of three to five domain-specific bill introductions citing the framework's evidence sections is substantial within a single two-year cycle if the caucus has received the framework before the session opens.

**Asymmetric cherry-picking risk**: Legislatures will also cherry-pick in ways that selectively omit the framework's structural analysis. A state caucus might cite the Domain 1 voting access evidence while ignoring the Domain 6 judicial independence analysis that the implementation roadmap identifies as the prerequisite domain. This is a structurally incomplete adoption — it extracts the politically easiest content while leaving the foundational content unused. The feedback mechanism needs to detect this pattern and flag it as shallow adoption, not deep adoption.

**The New Deal precedent applies directly**: During 1933-1935, 24 states adopted general sales taxes based on federal New Deal fiscal logic, but the same states often resisted federal cooperative federalism arrangements on labor and agriculture because those arrangements required surrendering local patronage control. Michigan, Pennsylvania, and New York enacted "Little New Deals" — comprehensive state-level analogs — while Southern states extracted only the fiscal transfer components. The 35-domain framework should expect an analogous geographic bifurcation: blue-state trifecta legislatures adopting comprehensive domain clusters; purple and red-minority-caucus contexts extracting only politically defensible components.

**What "successful" state legislative adoption looks like**: A Domain 1 bill citing the framework's Maine/Alaska/Australia RCV evidence section in its legislative findings. A Domain 33 bill using the framework's six-state simultaneous action map as the basis for emergency legislative procedure protection. A Domain 9 bill language that is explicitly coordinated with peer states through the GSD network, using aligned bill language across three or more states. The minimum viable success signal is one enacted statute or committee hearing where framework domain language appears verbatim or near-verbatim in the official record.

### 1.2 Federal Circuit Courts — Appellate Litigators

**Primary pressure point**: Doctrinal timing and circuit venue.

Appellate litigators do not adopt research frameworks — they mine them for citable propositions, circuit-specific factual patterns, and academic record components. The framework's value to this sector is its pre-assembled evidentiary architecture: the 22-case prosecutorial weaponization dataset (Domain 29), the shadow docket pattern data (Domain 6), the SAVE Act error rate documentation (Domain 1), and the cross-domain reinforcing pair structure that maps simultaneous litigation strategy.

**Expected adaptation pattern**: Hostile judges will attempt to weaponize the framework's own comparative analysis against it. If a Federalist Society-aligned circuit judge receives amicus briefing that cites the framework's Germany or Canada comparison, the predictable response is "foreign law is not persuasive authority." This is the known vulnerability of comparative constitutional argument in U.S. courts — and it is the same pattern that emerged when NAACP LDF tried international law arguments in the 1950s before the Supreme Court shut the door. The framework's U.S. domestic precedent sections are the durable citables; the international comparisons are the persuasive background that informs the overall analytical frame but should not anchor the doctrinal argument.

**Favorable judges** will use the framework differently: they will use its domain dependency analysis (Domain 6 must precede Domains 1, 2, 29) to understand why a case before them is not a standalone dispute but a piece of a systematic pattern. The Xinis/Abrego Garcia contempt proceedings are a case in point — a judge who understands the framework's Domain 29 and Domain 6 analysis will be more receptive to amicus briefs that document the pattern of executive defiance as a systematic posture, not an isolated incident.

**The Corner Post and Loper Bright openings are the framework's biggest near-term litigation value**: Both rulings created offensive legal opportunities in domains the framework covers — environmental rollbacks (Domain 15), surveillance reauthorizations (Domain 21), labor enforcement (Domain 17) — that litigators who have not read the framework have not yet fully exploited. The framework's Appendix on post-Loper Bright offensive strategy should be the primary extraction target for this sector.

**What "successful" court sector adoption looks like**: An amicus brief in a Domain 6 or Domain 29 case that cites the framework by name. A law school working paper published within 30 days of a major ruling (Slaughter, Wilcox en banc) that uses the framework's domain analysis as its analytical frame. A circuit split created in a domain the framework identified as a priority litigation venue. Domain 37 NVRA quiet period analysis cited in a Democracy Docket pre-filed emergency injunction template.

### 1.3 Law Schools and Universities

**Primary pressure point**: Publication venue and research agenda legitimacy.

Academic institutions adopt frameworks by converting them into research objects — things to be analyzed, critiqued, extended, and cited. The SDG model is instructive here: the UN's 2015 17-goal framework was not adopted by universities as a governance blueprint. It was adopted as a research organizing schema — 2,100+ member institutions in the SDSN network incorporated SDG goals into their research priorities, generating thousands of publications that cited the SDG framework and, through those citations, embedded the framework in academic discourse far more durably than any policy adoption would have.

The 35-domain framework should expect a similar pattern. The most adoptable domains for academic institutions are those with the clearest empirical research gaps that graduate students and junior faculty can fill. Domain 6 (judicial independence — shadow docket empirical coding), Domain 29 (prosecutorial weaponization — charging decision procedural pattern analysis), Domain 1 (RCV adoption effects on candidate recruitment), and the proposed AI governance domain are all tractable empirical projects. The framework's value to this sector is not its conclusions but its research agenda implications.

**Nudge theory's adoption trajectory is the relevant precedent**: Thaler and Sunstein's choice architecture framework (2008) was adopted by government within two years — the UK Behavioural Insights Team (2010), U.S. OIRA appointment of Sunstein (2008), Australia BETA (2016) — but the adoption pathway ran through academic publication and government advisory roles, not direct distribution to institutions. The mechanism was: peer-reviewed publications → professional network credibility → government advisory appointment → institutional policy. The 35-domain framework should expect a longer timeline (three to five years for deep academic integration) but can accelerate it by targeting the specific publication venues (Harvard Law Review Forum, Yale Law Journal online companion, Just Security, Lawfare) that have rapid-response capacity.

**Clinics are the fastest academic adoption path**: Harvard Law's Democracy and the Rule of Law Clinic is already partnered with Protect Democracy. NYU's Regulatory Policy Clinic uses case studies from real regulatory proceedings. Boston University's Legislative Policy and Drafting Clinic does statutory oversight work. These clinics need case material, and the framework's domains are pre-organized case material. Domain 27 (academic freedom) is specifically suited to Harvard's current context — the $2.2B funding freeze is a live clinic case, not a hypothetical. The framework's Domain 27 four-track threat analysis should be introduced to these clinics as a diagnostic tool for evaluating their own institutional situation, not just as curriculum content.

**What "successful" academic adoption looks like**: A working paper published on Just Security or Lawfare within 72 hours of a major ruling that uses the framework's domain analysis. A JD clinic at any of the top 20 law schools assigns a domain section as primary case material. A political science or law review article cites the framework in a peer-reviewed publication. A capstone project at a policy school uses the framework's wave-filter logic as its analytical methodology. A law school rapid-response team is pre-assigned and publishes within 48 hours of the Trump v. Slaughter ruling.

### 1.4 Think Tanks

**Primary pressure point**: The publication pipeline and donor/funder alignment.

Think tanks operate on a time-to-impact logic that the framework needs to align with. A Brennan Center researcher who receives the framework will assess it against three questions: (1) does it fill a gap in our current research or duplicate it? (2) can we build on it and still claim analytical ownership? (3) does it align with our current donor-funded research agenda? All three questions favor framework adoption as a source, not as a blueprint — the think tank researcher wants to cite the framework in their own publication, not republish the framework under their byline.

**Expected distillation pattern**: Think tanks will distill the framework's multi-domain analysis into single-issue policy briefs. The Brennan Center will extract the Domain 1 evidence for a voter suppression brief. EPI will extract Domain 17 labor analysis for an issue brief. The Urban Institute will extract Domain 11 healthcare access analysis. Just Security will extract Domain 28/29 for national security analysis. None of these briefs will cite "all 35 domains" — they will cite specific sections and present them as part of their own analytical framework.

**The risk of attribution dilution**: When think tanks extract and republish framework analysis without attribution, the framework loses its identity as a coherent whole. This is the standard fate of policy research — the underlying research becomes invisible while the derivative publication becomes the citable source. The GDPR adoption experience is instructive: the original European Data Protection Supervisor's technical analyses were largely subsumed into member-state guidance documents that cited the regulation but not the underlying supervisory analysis. To prevent this, the framework should have a consistent citation identifier that survives extraction — "Democratic Renewal Project, domain-specific brief, April 2026" — that appears in the domain files and in all derived materials.

**The op-ed pipeline**: Think tanks generate op-eds that reach general audiences and political actors simultaneously. The framework's most op-ed-ready content: the 13 reinforcing feedback loops (the structural analysis that makes democratic backsliding self-reinforcing), the 3.5% threshold applied to current mobilization numbers, the Hungary opposition victory as the contemporaneous comparator, and the Domain 37 electoral timeline with its specific advocacy deadlines. These are the analytical propositions most likely to appear in a Times or Atlantic column.

**What "successful" think tank adoption looks like**: A Brennan Center research report that cites the framework in a footnote and incorporates its comparative evidence. An EPI issue brief on Domain 17 labor analysis that the AFL-CIO distributes. A Just Security piece on Domain 28 or 29 that uses the framework's analytical framework. An op-ed in a major outlet by a named think tank fellow that introduces the framework to a general audience. A Senate Judiciary Committee hearing memo that draws on a Brennan Center brief that cites the framework.

### 1.5 Civil Rights Organizations

**Primary pressure point**: The current crisis and the member communication need.

Civil rights organizations face a specific tension the framework must address: their members need actionable guidance for immediate threats, while the framework's strength is its long-arc structural analysis. An NAACP LDF attorney dealing with a specific ICE detention case does not need a 35-domain framework — they need the specific Domain 16 evidence on warrantless arrests, the relevant litigation tracker case law, and the constitutional arguments. The civil rights sector will extract horizontally (across domains for a specific current crisis) rather than vertically (deep into a single domain).

**The 1960s civil rights movement's multi-organizational adoption pattern is directly applicable**: The NAACP LDF pursued litigation strategy; SCLC pursued direct action and political mobilization; SNCC pursued grassroots organizing and voter registration; CORE pursued direct action in the North. All four organizations shared a common analytical framework (the civil rights legal theory developed by Thurgood Marshall) but adapted it to their specific institutional constraints and tactical approaches. The 1962 Voter Education Project brought all four together on voter registration precisely because voter registration was a domain where all four organizations' tactics reinforced each other. The 35-domain framework should identify the equivalent of the Voter Education Project — the domain(s) where the different civil rights sector organizations' tactical approaches reinforce each other and can be organized around a common framework.

**Domain 29 (prosecutorial weaponization) and Domain 7 (First Amendment) are the current Voter Education Project equivalents**: The SPLC indictment has already catalyzed a mutual defense pact across 100+ organizations. The framework's Domain 29 analysis — specifically the 22-case retaliatory prosecution pattern and the bank fraud Thompson contradiction — is the analytical foundation for that defense pact's legal strategy. Civil rights organizations will extract this content for member communications, mobilization messaging, and legal defense coordination. The framework should facilitate that extraction by making the Domain 29 "current threat brief" format (as described in the institutional adoption playbooks) available immediately.

**Grassroots messaging angle**: The 3.5% participation threshold is the civil rights sector's most powerful mobilization tool from the framework. Civil rights organizations can convert this into concrete geographic targets: "We need 25,000 sustained participants in this congressional district." The framework's resistance case studies (160 international movement cases) provide the evidence that this threshold is achievable and decisive. The messaging translation: "Every nonviolent movement that reached 3.5% active participation has achieved its primary demand. The October 2025 protests reached 7 million — we are 60% of the way to threshold."

**What "successful" civil rights sector adoption looks like**: The mutual defense network activation template from the framework is used verbatim in a real crisis response. The Domain 29 current threat brief is circulated to all members of the SPLC mutual defense pact. The 3.5% threshold appears in a national civil rights campaign as an explicit benchmark. Domain 16 analysis is cited in an NAACP LDF legal filing. Coalition training materials at Indivisible or M4BL reference the framework's international resistance case studies.

## Part II: Feedback Mechanisms

### 2.1 Distinguishing Real Adoption from Shallow Acknowledgment

The central measurement problem in policy framework distribution is that acknowledgment signals and adoption signals look identical in the first 30 days. An organization that sends a polite reply thanking you for the framework and one that has assigned a staff member to extract Domain 1 for a voter purge brief both produce the same behavioral signal: one response email. The distinction only becomes visible at the 60-90 day mark, when the organization with real adoption has produced a deliverable that cites the framework and the organization with shallow acknowledgment has not followed up.

**Shallow acknowledgment signals** (do not interpret as adoption):
- Reply email expressing appreciation without a specific question or request
- Social media post sharing the framework without institutional context
- "We'll circulate this internally" without follow-up timeline
- A junior staff member acknowledging receipt without senior engagement

**Real adoption signals**:
- A request for a domain-specific one-pager (indicates operational intent, not just reading interest)
- A question about a specific evidence claim (indicates the contact is reading actively, not passively)
- An introduction to a colleague with a specific role ("I'm forwarding this to our policy director who is working on X")
- A request for a call or meeting to discuss application to their current work
- A domain section excerpt sent back with annotation or questions

### 2.2 Tracking Framework Diffusion

**Citations**: Google Scholar alerts set for the framework's title, for each domain's title, and for distinctive phrases ("35-domain framework," "democratic renewal proposal," "wave filter legislative sequence"). Any citation event generates a contact log entry and triggers a review of the citing document for downstream citation trails. The Overton Policy Citation Index (tracks citations across 30,000+ government and IGO documents) should be checked monthly — it operates on 6-18 month lag, so early results will not appear until fall 2026.

**Policy proposals**: State legislative database searches (LegiScan, NCSL bill tracking) for bill language that matches domain-specific reform proposals. The Domain 1 RCV provisions, the Domain 9 interstate compact language, and the Domain 29 whistleblower protection architecture are the most distinctive text-searchable content. A bill filed in Michigan that uses language from the Domain 1f redistricting protection section is a diffusion event even if it does not cite the framework by name.

**Litigation**: Democracy Docket, ACLU case tracker, and National Immigration Litigation Alliance filings tracked for language patterns that match the framework's Domain 37 NVRA analysis, Domain 1 voter purge arguments, or Domain 29 retaliatory prosecution doctrine. These filings rarely cite academic frameworks by name, but the analytical framework appears in how the cases are constructed.

**Academic publication**: Google Scholar, SSRN, and specific journal RSS feeds (Harvard Law Review Forum, Yale LJ online, Just Security, Lawfare) for articles using the framework's analytical structure even without explicit citation.

### 2.3 Feedback Loop Architecture

The existing `measurement-and-iteration-framework.md` establishes four feedback mechanisms (direct intake, passive monitoring, periodic check-ins, coalition input protocol). This roadmap adds three post-distribution-specific loops not covered by the existing framework:

**Loop 1 — Sector-divergence tracking**: When two sectors adapt the same domain in contradictory ways, log the divergence and assess its source. Example: if state legislatures extract Domain 33's ballot initiative suppression evidence as a basis for protective legislation while think tanks use the same domain's evidence to argue for a federal pre-emption strategy, the divergence reveals a genuine strategic question: which approach is more likely to succeed given current federal and state political conditions? The answer should feed back into domain revision — specifically, adding a "sector-specific application" subsection that acknowledges the divergent strategic logics.

**Loop 2 — Quality degradation detection**: When a domain section is extracted and republished without its evidentiary caveats, the republished version may overstate the evidence or mischaracterize the comparative precedents. The Domain 29 bank fraud Thompson contradiction is a live example: if a civil rights organization's press statement cites the SPLC indictment analysis but strips the "Weissmann assessment" framing and presents the legal conclusion as settled rather than contested, that is quality degradation. The feedback loop: any domain section that is publicly cited in a way that misrepresents the evidence triggers a "clarification note" — a short supplemental document distributed to contacts who received that domain, correcting the misrepresentation.

**Loop 3 (Path A+37-specific) — Phase 1 to Phase 2 signal feed**: Under Path A+37, the 10-day window between Phase 1 activation (April 30) and Phase 2 deployment (est. May 12) is the optimal window to detect which Tier 1 organizations are actively engaging with the framework before Domain 37 reaches election protection organizations. If Brennan Center staff request Domain 37 materials during Phase 1 monitoring, that is a signal to accelerate Phase 2 deployment and lead the Domain 37 outreach with a reference to the Brennan Center engagement.

## Part III: Success Metrics

### 3.1 Domain Popularity Metrics

The existing measurement framework tracks adoption events at the organization level. This roadmap adds domain-level popularity tracking — which domains are generating the most pull and which are being ignored — because domain-level patterns reveal institutional demand that organization-level tracking obscures.

**Domain pull ranking** (what to track): For every inbound request, question, citation, or feedback event, tag it to the domain(s) involved. At the 30-day, 60-day, and 90-day marks, rank domains by their pull count. The ranking will reveal:
- **High-pull domains**: Domains with immediate institutional relevance — likely Domain 1, Domain 6, Domain 29, Domain 37 in the current environment. These are the domains to deepen first in Phase 2 research.
- **Low-pull domains**: Domains that are not generating institutional interest despite being well-evidenced — possibly Domain 5 (fiscal policy), Domain 8 (housing), Domain 13 (education policy). Low pull does not necessarily indicate a domain is less important; it may indicate the target audience for that domain has not yet been reached.
- **Surprise domains**: Domains generating more pull than expected — these reveal unanticipated institutional demand and are the highest-priority signals for Phase 2 research investment.

**Domain citation quality**: Not all citations are equal. A domain cited in a Senate Judiciary Committee memo is a higher-quality citation than a domain cited in a Reddit post. The quality weighting from the existing measurement framework (Tier 1 citation weighted higher than Tier 3) should apply to domain-level tracking as well.

### 3.2 Implementation Conversion Rate

The four-stage conversion pipeline:
1. Framework reference (organization receives and acknowledges)
2. Policy proposal (organization produces a deliverable that incorporates domain analysis)
3. Legislative introduction or litigation filing (deliverable reaches a formal institutional process)
4. Enactment or favorable ruling (formal process produces a concrete outcome)

Track conversion rates between each stage. Historical benchmarks from comparable frameworks: the GDPR's Brussels Effect produced 30+ countries adopting GDPR-equivalent legislation within five years of enforcement — but the "reference to enactment" conversion rate for any single jurisdiction within the first 12 months was typically 5-15%. Sunstein's nudge framework moved from academic publication (2008) to government institution (UK BIT, 2010) in 24 months — a faster conversion rate, but operating in a more receptive institutional environment.

For the 35-domain framework, realistic 12-month conversion targets:
- Stage 1 to Stage 2: 25-35% of contacted organizations produce a domain-incorporating deliverable
- Stage 2 to Stage 3: 15-25% of deliverables reach a formal institutional process (bill, filing, testimony)
- Stage 3 to Stage 4: 10-20% of formal processes produce a concrete outcome in the first 12 months

These targets are not aspirational — they are calibrated against the Advocacy Coalition Framework's finding that policy change typically requires 10+ years for deep structural change but can produce identifiable policy outputs within 2-3 years when the political environment is in flux. The current U.S. political environment (courts blocking approximately 350 of 362 challenged executive actions; 22-state AG coalition at 79% win rate) is highly receptive to framework-informed legal challenges, which accelerates Stage 3 to Stage 4 conversion for litigation pathways relative to legislative pathways.

### 3.3 Velocity Metrics

**Within-sector spread**: How fast does adoption spread once one organization in a sector has adopted? The ILO convention peer-effects research found that "the probability of adopting an international standard depends on how many other countries in a peer group have already adopted that standard" — peer effects increase adoption speed nonlinearly. The same dynamic operates within institutional sectors: if Brennan Center adopts the framework, the probability that ACS Scholars Network and Protect Democracy adopt it increases. Track first-adopter dates within each sector and model whether subsequent adoption is accelerating.

**Cross-sector cascade timing**: The bridge node model predicts specific cascade pathways (Just Security cite → Senate Judiciary staff; EPI brief → AFL-CIO newsletter). Track whether these cascades are activating on predicted timelines. A cascade that takes 90 days where the model predicted 30 days indicates either a relationship failure (the bridge node didn't receive the framework) or an institutional resistance factor (the receiving organization has a structural reason not to adopt even after the bridge node has). Both are actionable intelligence.

### 3.4 Quality Metrics

**Constructive adaptation vs. dilutive adaptation**: Adaptations are constructive when they extend the framework's analytical logic to contexts the original framework did not cover. They are dilutive when they strip the framework's structural analysis (the cross-domain synthesis, the wave logic, the feedback loop architecture) and republish only the politically convenient surface-level claims.

Constructive adaptation example: A law school clinic uses the Domain 6 shadow docket analysis as the foundation for an empirical research project that codes 500+ emergency SCOTUS applications and finds the pattern the framework describes, then publishes this in a law review with the framework as an analytical anchor. The framework's thesis is tested, confirmed, and extended.

Dilutive adaptation example: A political advocacy organization publishes a "fact sheet" that excerpts the most alarming statistics from Domain 37 (ICE-at-polls, election denier appointments, Bannon quote) without the framework's analytical caveat structure, presenting contested projections as certainties. This creates factual exposure — the fact sheet will attract fact-checkers who will find the unqualified claims overstate the evidence, and the result is reputational damage to the framework's credibility in the institutional audiences that matter most.

**Quality assessment protocol**: For every published adoption event (citation in a deliverable), read the citation in context and assess: (a) does the claim match what the framework actually says? (b) are the caveats preserved? (c) is the comparative evidence correctly characterized? Flag any misrepresentation for a clarification note. This is not about controlling how the framework is used — it is about maintaining the evidentiary standard that makes the framework credible to institutional audiences.

## Part IV: Decision Trees for Feedback Response

### 4.1 Unexpected Sector-Domain Adoption

When domain X is adopted by sector Y in unexpected way Z, use this decision tree:

```
Unexpected adoption detected
  → Is it constructive or dilutive?
    CONSTRUCTIVE (extends framework logic):
      → Log as a Phase 2 demand signal for that domain
      → Reach out to confirm the adaptation is accurate
      → If accurate, integrate the adaptation's insights as a domain annotation
      → If it fills a genuine gap: consider a domain update or companion brief
    DILUTIVE (strips analytical caveats or misrepresents evidence):
      → Assess severity: does it create factual exposure?
        SEVERE (misstatement of evidence that fact-checkers will catch):
          → Issue a clarification note to all contacts who received that domain
          → Contact the publishing organization directly to offer corrective version
          → Do not issue a public correction unless the misstatement reaches major media
        MODERATE (framing diverges but evidence is not misrepresented):
          → Log for messaging iteration review
          → Wait for a second independent instance before issuing a domain update
        MINOR (stylistic divergence from original framing):
          → Log; no action required
```

### 4.2 When to Recommend Domain Updates vs. Accept Adaptation

| Signal | Update or Accept? | Rationale |
|--------|-------------------|-----------|
| Two or more Tier 1 contacts identify same evidentiary gap | Update: add evidence to domain | Multiple independent experts identifying the same gap = genuine gap, not preference |
| Single Tier 1 contact disputes a domain's policy conclusion | Accept divergence: add a "counterargument" subsection | Policy conclusions are legitimately contested; evidence is not |
| Sector adaptation produces a more effective framing for one audience | Accept and create a sector-specific brief | The base domain retains its analytical structure; derived formats serve specialized audiences |
| New court ruling, executive action, or legislative outcome updates a domain's factual predicate | Update: add a Section [X] update notation with new facts | Factual currency is a credibility requirement; stale facts undermine institutional adoption |
| Phase 2 domain has two Tier 1 demand signals | Update: initiate full research for that domain | Demand-driven Phase 2 research is the operating rule |
| Low pull on a domain after 90 days | Do not update; reassess target audience | Low pull may mean wrong audience, not wrong content |

### 4.3 Handling Contradictory Feedback from Different Sectors

The most predictable source of contradictory feedback is the legislative sector vs. the litigation sector on Domain 6 (Judicial Independence). State legislators will want reform proposals that address judicial independence through legislative action (court expansion legislation, transparency requirements, jurisdiction-stripping prevention). Appellate litigators will resist any language that suggests judicial independence can be secured through legislation, because legislative fixes to judicial capture are themselves subject to judicial review by the captured courts — a circularity the framework acknowledges.

**Resolution protocol for contradictory sector feedback**:

1. Identify whether the contradiction is over evidence (one sector disputes a factual claim the other accepts) or strategy (both sectors accept the evidence but draw different action conclusions).

2. Evidence contradictions require investigation — one sector may have access to data the other does not. Treat as a research trigger.

3. Strategic contradictions do not require resolution in the base document. The base document's role is diagnosis and reform proposal, not strategic prescription for any single sector. Add a "sector-specific considerations" note in the relevant domain acknowledging the strategic divergence.

4. If the contradiction is between a sector that represents a marginalized community's interests (civil rights organizations, immigration advocacy) and a sector representing institutional actors (think tanks, courts), default to the marginalized community's framing in the base document. The framework's evidentiary standard determines what facts are included; the community most affected by the domain's subject matter determines how those facts are framed. This is not a departure from analytical rigor — it is an acknowledgment that "who bears the cost" is itself an evidentiary question.

### 5.1 Phase 2 Deepening Domains — Demand-Activated Sequencing

The phase-2-expansion-roadmap.md identifies five candidates for full research: Domain 31x (healthcare tariff collision), Domains 37a and 37b (post-election Section 3 litigation and state election security), Domain A (trade policy), and Domain B (healthcare access — OBBBA). The feedback integration roadmap adds a demand-activation trigger to this sequencing:

- **Domain 31x**: Urgency override applies — July 31, 2026 tariff effective date is 93 days from today. Begin research regardless of Tier 1 demand signal.
- **Domain 37b**: Urgency override — April-July advocacy window is open now. Begin research.
- **Domain 37a**: Hold pending September 2026 advocacy window. Monitor for Tier 1 demand signals from election protection organizations receiving Phase 2 distribution.
- **Domain A (Trade Policy)**: Monitor for Tier 1 demand signals from economic policy think tanks (EPI, Brookings economic studies) and Senate Finance Committee staff.
- **Domain B (Healthcare Access)**: Partially covered by 31x. Assess after 31x is complete.

**Cryptographic democracy and remote voting**: The existing EXPLORATION_QUEUE.md flags this as a standing research thread with strong connection to resistance work. Post-distribution, this thread becomes relevant to the law school sector specifically — it is an area where law reviews (particularly at Yale, Harvard, and Columbia with strong election law programs) are more likely to engage than policy-focused think tanks. The academic publication pathway is the appropriate entry point, not a standalone domain.

### 5.2 Sector-Specific Expansion

**For economists and trade policy institutes**: Domain A (trade policy) requires a sector-specific brief that translates the constitutional sovereignty analysis into the economic terminology that trade economists use. The framework's existing analysis is constitutional-law-centric (IEEPA authority, Section 232). Economists will want the welfare and distributional analysis — which sectors bear the cost of 145% China tariffs, what the GDP drag quantification looks like, how the small business impact (61% negative, per the domain scope document) maps to specific congressional districts. A trade-policy-specific variant of Domain A should be scoped when Tier 1 demand signals from economic institutions materialize.

**For worker cooperatives, CLTs, and mutual aid networks**: The framework's parallel institutions analysis (parallel-institutions.md) identifies worker cooperatives, community land trusts, and mutual aid networks as the institutional layer operating outside formal democratic structures. These institutions are not in the primary distribution target list — they lack the institutional infrastructure to absorb a 35-domain framework and the relationships with electoral politics that make the framework immediately actionable. The appropriate integration pathway is through Tier 3 labor organizations (Domain 17 pathway via EPI/AFL-CIO) and through community organizing networks (Indivisible, M4BL) that already have relationships with these parallel institutions. Direct distribution to parallel institutions should wait until Tier 3 organizational adoption is confirmed.

### 5.3 Long-Arc Institutional Embedding

The framework's long-arc goal is not to be cited in policy briefs — it is to function as shared infrastructure across a coalition of organizations resisting democratic backsliding. The distinction matters. Shared infrastructure means the framework's analytical categories become the shared vocabulary that different organizations use when they coordinate. When Democracy Docket and the ACLU coordinate on NVRA quiet period strategy, they both understand "Domain 37, Section II.A" as shorthand for a body of analysis, not as a document they are choosing to cite. That level of embeddedness requires 18-36 months of sustained institutional engagement — it is not achievable through distribution alone.

The feedback integration roadmap's role in achieving long-arc embedding is to ensure that the framework's vocabulary (domain numbers, wave structure, dependency hierarchy) becomes the vocabulary that coalition coordination uses. This is only possible if the framework is consistently updated to reflect current developments — stale frameworks are abandoned; living frameworks become shared infrastructure.

**The GDPR Brussels Effect as the long-arc model**: The GDPR was published in 2016 and enforced from 2018. By 2024 — six years after enforcement — it had been adopted or closely modeled by 30+ countries, and its vocabulary (data controller, data subject, processor, DPA) was the shared vocabulary of global privacy law. The mechanism was not Brussels compelling adoption; it was the GDPR being the most complete, most rigorously enforced, and most frequently litigated framework in its domain — which made it the natural reference point for any organization navigating data protection law regardless of jurisdiction. The 35-domain framework should aim for the same status in democratic resilience analysis: the most complete, most evidence-dense, and most current framework in its domain, making it the natural reference point for any organization navigating democratic backsliding regardless of institutional sector.

## Appendix: Path-Specific Notes

**Path A (Immediate Full Distribution)**: All five sectors receive the full corpus simultaneously. The feedback mechanisms in Part II apply immediately from the April 30 launch date. The domain pull ranking (Part III.3.1) will be most useful in the first 30 days, when the simultaneous distribution creates a natural experiment — you can observe which domains generate pull from which sectors without the confounding effect of phased deployment. The Loop 3 signal feed described under Path A+37 is not applicable.

**Path A+37 (Recommended Hybrid)**: The 10-day Phase 1 monitoring window creates a first-signal dataset that is not available under Path A. Use this window specifically to identify which sectors and organizations are engaging most actively with election-adjacent domains (Domains 1, 6, 33, 37 predecessor content) before Domain 37 deploys. This informs the Phase 2 targeted outreach framing: organizations that have already engaged with election-adjacent content in Phase 1 are higher-priority Phase 2 targets and should receive a Phase 2 framing that references their Phase 1 engagement explicitly.

**Path B (Maintenance First)**: The feedback integration roadmap applies identically to a May 12+ launch. The practical effect of Path B on feedback mechanisms is that the spring legislative session advocacy windows described in Part I.1 are partially or fully closed before the first adoption signals can convert into bill introductions. The feedback loop will consequently show lower legislative sector conversion rates in the first 90 days under Path B than under Paths A or A+37 — not because of framework quality but because of timing.
