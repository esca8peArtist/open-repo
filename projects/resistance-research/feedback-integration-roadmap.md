# Post-Distribution Feedback Integration Roadmap

**April 30, 2026 — Version 2.0**

This roadmap governs the post-distribution phase of the 35-domain Democratic Renewal Proposal. It is path-agnostic: the adaptation patterns, feedback mechanisms, success metrics, and decision trees documented here apply regardless of whether the user selects Path A (immediate full distribution), Path A+37 (hybrid phased deployment), or Path B (maintenance-first). The only path-specific notation appears in the feedback mechanism section, where Path A+37's phased structure creates an additional early signal loop not available in Path A.

The central thesis: institutional sectors do not adopt policy frameworks wholesale. They extract, adapt, and selectively deploy the components that fit their immediate operational context. This is not framework dilution — it is the mechanism through which a research document becomes embedded in institutional practice. Expecting holistic adoption is a measurement error that causes organizations to misread successful partial adoption as failure. This roadmap operationalizes selective adoption as the expected pattern and builds feedback loops that capture it.

## Part I: Seeding Priority — Which Institutions First, Which Institutions Second

**Lead with the institutions that generate cascades, not the institutions that have the most followers.**

The bridge node model (from `measurement-and-iteration-framework.md`) identifies cascade pathways. The following table implements that model as a concrete seeding sequence. Institutions in Tier 1 are seeded first because they have the shortest path to institutional output — a Just Security piece, a Senate hearing memo, a Democracy Docket filing — that then reaches Tier 2 institutions through existing professional networks without requiring additional outreach.

| Tier | Institutions | Why First | Primary Domain Extract |
|------|-------------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1A — Seed first (Day 1-3) | Just Security, Lawfare, Democracy Docket, Brennan Center Democracy Program | Fastest output cycle (24-72 hrs); citations reach Senate Judiciary staff and federal litigators automatically | Domains 6, 29, 1, 37 |
| 1B — Seed first (Day 1-5) | State AG offices (MN, MA, NY, CA, IL, WA, DE coalition leads) | Active election EO litigation now; Domain 33/37 analysis immediately operational for their dockets | Domains 33, 34, 37, 1 |
| 1C — Seed first (Day 3-7) | Senate Judiciary Committee staff (Whitehouse, Klobuchar, Merkley) | Pre-positioned for hearing record; legislation-adjacent work requires longer lead time than litigation | Domains 6, 29, 2, 34 |
| 2A — Seed second (Day 7-14) | EPI, Urban Institute, NLRB-adjacent labor orgs | EPI-AFL-CIO pipeline auto-amplifies to Tier 3; use Tier 1B output as social proof | Domains 17, 3, 20 |
| 2B — Seed second (Day 7-14) | Law school clinics (Harvard DROL, NYU Brennan, BU Legislative) | Academic record takes 6-8 weeks to mature; start early; use Tier 1A publications as entrée | Domains 27, 6, 29, 35 |
| 2C — Seed second (Day 10-21) | ACLU state affiliates, NAACP LDF, SPLC mutual defense network | Domain 29 mutual defense network is pre-activated; seed Domain 37 after AG coalition framing is established | Domains 29, 7, 16, 37 |
| 3 — Seed when Tier 1 output exists | Think tank fellows (Brookings, CAP), op-ed networks, state legislature caucus directors | Tier 3 adoption is most credible when it can reference existing Tier 1 citations — wait for those to exist | Domains 1, 9, 17, 22 |

**The sequencing logic in plain terms**: State AGs and Just Security editors are seeded first not because they are the most prestigious but because they operate on the shortest feedback cycles. An AG who receives the framework can file a supplemental brief using Domain 33 analysis within days. A Just Security editor can publish a piece within 72 hours. Both events create a publicly verifiable citation that makes every subsequent outreach email easier to send: "Our analysis is already being used by [AG coalition / Just Security]."

## Part II: Adaptation Patterns by Sector

The six sectors receiving the 35-domain framework will adapt it through fundamentally different institutional logics. Each sector has a primary pressure point — the factor that determines whether the framework gets extracted and used or filed and forgotten.

### 2.1 State Attorneys General

**Primary pressure point**: The active docket and the coalition lead state.

State AGs adapt policy frameworks within a specific constraint: they need arguments that will survive a preliminary injunction standard in federal court, not proposals that are analytically elegant but legally untested. The 35-domain framework's most immediate value to AGs is its pre-assembled constitutional argument structure — Domain 34 (congressional power of the purse), Domain 33 (ballot initiative suppression and state sovereignty over elections), Domain 6 (judicial independence and shadow docket patterns), and Domain 37 (federal election interference) all contain citeable propositions that map directly onto the AG coalition's active dockets.

**The current coalition's structure shapes adaptation directly**: The April 2026 24-state AG coalition challenging Trump's election executive order — filed in D. Mass., led by AG Ellison (MN) and AG Jennings (DE) — is the live operational context for Domains 33 and 37. The AG coalition's existing filings use constitutional state sovereignty arguments (Article II elections clause, Tenth Amendment, anti-commandeering doctrine) that align exactly with the framework's Domain 33 six-state simultaneous action map. The framework's most productive adaptation in this sector is not wholesale adoption — it is the Domain 33 and Domain 37 constitutional arguments being cited in supplemental briefs and appellate filings.

**Expected modification points**: AGs will strip international law comparisons (German Federal Constitutional Court citations) from any argument they use because most federal circuits treat foreign law as non-persuasive. They will retain: U.S. state precedent comparisons, statutory text analysis, and empirical evidence on administrative effects. Domain 37, Section 8.4 (appellate capture timeline synchronized with midterm litigation timeline) is the highest-value extraction for this sector — it provides a strategic map for when to file, in which circuits, and how to construct the record for SCOTUS review.

**Feedback priority for AGs**: The AG sector provides the fastest-converting feedback because they produce publicly verifiable outputs (court filings, press releases). Monitor Democracy Docket case tracker and state AG press release feeds daily during the first 60 days. Any AG filing that cites the framework — even in a footnote — is a Tier 1 citation event.

**What successful AG sector adoption looks like**: Domain 33 constitutional analysis cited in a supplemental brief in the D. Mass. election EO case. Domain 37 Section 702/FISA expiration analysis used in AG amicus brief to an election protection case. A DAGA coordination call where the framework's Domain 34 impoundment analysis is distributed as a shared litigation resource across the coalition.

### 2.2 State Legislatures

**Primary pressure point**: The session calendar.

State legislatures operate under severe time constraints with binary outcomes. The framework's most immediate value to legislative staff is not its 35-domain architecture but its pre-packaged legislative findings, cost-benefit estimates, and cross-jurisdictional precedent tables that reduce bill-drafting time.

**Expected adaptation pattern**: Selective extraction by domain cluster, driven by caucus composition. A caucus with strong labor alignment extracts Domain 17; immigration-focused members extract Domain 16; redistricting-focused members extract Domain 1f. The probability of holistic 35-domain adoption is near zero. The probability of three to five domain-specific bill introductions citing the framework's evidence sections is substantial within a single two-year cycle if the caucus received the framework before the session opened.

**Asymmetric cherry-picking risk**: Legislatures will extract the politically easiest content while leaving the foundational content unused — Domain 1 voting access evidence without the Domain 6 judicial independence analysis the implementation roadmap identifies as a prerequisite. The feedback mechanism must flag this as shallow adoption.

**The New Deal precedent**: During 1933-1935, 24 states adopted general sales taxes based on New Deal fiscal logic while Southern states extracted only the fiscal transfer components. Michigan, Pennsylvania, and New York enacted comprehensive "Little New Deals." The framework should expect an analogous geographic bifurcation: blue-state trifecta legislatures adopting comprehensive domain clusters; purple and red-minority-caucus contexts extracting only politically defensible components.

**What successful legislative adoption looks like**: A Domain 1 bill citing Maine/Alaska/Australia RCV evidence in its legislative findings. A Domain 33 bill using the framework's six-state simultaneous action map. A Domain 9 bill coordinated across three or more states using the GSD network.

### 2.3 Federal Circuit Courts — Appellate Litigators

**Primary pressure point**: Doctrinal timing and circuit venue.

Appellate litigators mine the framework for citable propositions and pre-assembled evidentiary architecture: the 22-case prosecutorial weaponization dataset (Domain 29), the shadow docket pattern data (Domain 6), the SAVE Act error rate documentation (Domain 1), and the cross-domain reinforcing pair structure.

**Expected adaptation pattern**: Hostile judges will attempt to weaponize the framework's own comparative analysis against it — foreign law is not persuasive authority in U.S. courts. The domestic precedent sections are the durable citables; the international comparisons inform the analytical frame but should not anchor doctrinal argument.

**Post-Loper Bright openings**: Corner Post and Loper Bright created offensive legal opportunities in domains the framework covers — environmental rollbacks (Domain 15), surveillance reauthorizations (Domain 21), labor enforcement (Domain 17) — that litigators who have not read the framework have not yet fully exploited. Domain 37's NVRA quiet period analysis and Domain 34's impoundment control act analysis are the highest-priority extractions for this sector.

**What successful court sector adoption looks like**: An amicus brief in a Domain 6 or Domain 29 case citing the framework by name. A law school working paper published within 30 days of a major ruling using the framework's domain analysis as its analytical frame. A circuit split created in a domain the framework identified as a priority litigation venue.

### 2.4 Law Schools and Universities

**Primary pressure point**: Publication venue and research agenda legitimacy.

Academic institutions adopt frameworks by converting them into research objects — things to be analyzed, critiqued, extended, and cited. The SDG model is instructive: the UN's 2015 17-goal framework was adopted by universities as a research organizing schema; 2,100+ SDSN member institutions incorporated SDG goals into their research priorities, generating publications that embedded the framework more durably than any policy adoption would have.

Post-2015 SDG-aligned think tank funding increased over 140% as organizations co-financed research addressing SDG priorities to gain legitimacy through peer review. The 35-domain framework should expect a similar dynamic: think tanks and law schools will co-author and co-publish work using the framework's analytical categories, which embeds those categories in the academic literature regardless of whether the original document is cited.

**Clinics are the fastest academic adoption path**: Harvard Law's Democracy and the Rule of Law Clinic, NYU's Brennan Center Clinic, and BU's Legislative Policy and Drafting Clinic need case material. The framework's domains are pre-organized case material. Domain 27 (academic freedom) is specifically suited to Harvard's current context — the $2.2B funding freeze is a live clinic case, not a hypothetical.

**What successful academic adoption looks like**: A working paper on Just Security or Lawfare within 72 hours of a major ruling using the framework's domain analysis. A JD clinic at any top-20 law school assigns a domain section as primary case material. A law review article cites the framework in a peer-reviewed publication.

### 2.5 Think Tanks

**Primary pressure point**: The publication pipeline and donor/funder alignment.

Think tanks will distill the framework's multi-domain analysis into single-issue policy briefs. The Brennan Center extracts Domain 1 for a voter suppression brief. EPI extracts Domain 17 for a labor brief. Just Security extracts Domain 28/29 for national security analysis. None of these briefs will cite "all 35 domains" — they will cite specific sections.

**The risk of attribution dilution**: When think tanks extract and republish without attribution, the framework loses its identity as a coherent whole. This is the standard fate of policy research — the GDPR's original supervisory analyses were subsumed into member-state guidance documents. To prevent this, the framework should have a consistent citation identifier that survives extraction: "Democratic Renewal Project, domain-specific brief, April 2026."

**What successful think tank adoption looks like**: A Brennan Center research report citing the framework in a footnote with domain-specific evidence incorporated. An EPI issue brief on Domain 17 labor analysis that the AFL-CIO distributes. A Senate Judiciary Committee hearing memo drawing on a Brennan Center brief that cites the framework.

### 2.6 Civil Rights Organizations

**Primary pressure point**: The current crisis and the member communication need.

Civil rights organizations will extract horizontally (across domains for a specific current crisis) rather than vertically (deep into a single domain). The 1960s civil rights movement's multi-organizational adoption pattern applies: NAACP LDF pursued litigation strategy, SCLC pursued direct action, SNCC pursued organizing, CORE pursued direct action in the North — all sharing a common analytical framework adapted to different institutional constraints.

**Domain 29 and Domain 7 are the current Voter Education Project equivalents**: The SPLC mutual defense pact across 100+ organizations is the coordination mechanism. The framework's Domain 29 analysis — specifically the 22-case retaliatory prosecution pattern and the bank fraud Thompson contradiction — is the analytical foundation for that defense pact's legal strategy.

**What successful civil rights sector adoption looks like**: The mutual defense network activation template used verbatim in a real crisis response. The Domain 29 current threat brief circulated to all SPLC mutual defense pact members. The 3.5% threshold appearing in a national civil rights campaign as an explicit benchmark.

## Part III: Feedback Mechanisms

### 3.1 Distinguishing Real Adoption from Shallow Acknowledgment

**Shallow acknowledgment signals** (do not interpret as adoption):
- Reply email expressing appreciation without a specific question or request
- Social media post sharing the framework without institutional context
- "We'll circulate this internally" without follow-up timeline
- A junior staff member acknowledging receipt without senior engagement

**Real adoption signals**:
- A request for a domain-specific one-pager (indicates operational intent)
- A question about a specific evidence claim (indicates active reading)
- An introduction to a colleague with a specific role ("I'm forwarding this to our policy director working on X")
- A request for a call or meeting to discuss application to current work
- A domain section sent back with annotation or questions

### 3.2 Tracking Framework Diffusion

**Citations**: Google Scholar alerts for the framework's title, each domain's title, and distinctive phrases ("35-domain framework," "democratic renewal proposal," "wave filter legislative sequence"). The Overton Policy Citation Index operates on 6-18 month lag — early results will not appear until fall 2026.

**Policy proposals**: State legislative database searches (LegiScan, NCSL bill tracking) for bill language matching domain-specific reform proposals. Domain 1 RCV provisions, Domain 9 interstate compact language, and Domain 29 whistleblower protection architecture are the most distinctive text-searchable content.

**Litigation**: Democracy Docket, ACLU case tracker, DAGA press release feeds, and National Immigration Litigation Alliance filings for language patterns matching the framework's Domain 37 NVRA analysis, Domain 33 constitutional elections clause arguments, Domain 1 voter purge arguments, or Domain 29 retaliatory prosecution doctrine.

**Academic publication**: Google Scholar, SSRN, and journal RSS feeds (Harvard Law Review Forum, Yale LJ online, Just Security, Lawfare) for articles using the framework's analytical structure even without explicit citation.

### 3.3 Feedback Loop Architecture

**Loop 1 — Sector-divergence tracking**: When two sectors adapt the same domain in contradictory ways, log the divergence and assess its source. Example: state legislatures extract Domain 33's ballot initiative suppression evidence for protective legislation; think tanks use the same evidence to argue for federal pre-emption strategy. The divergence reveals a genuine strategic question. The answer feeds back into domain revision — specifically, adding a "sector-specific application" subsection acknowledging the divergent strategic logics.

**Loop 2 — Quality degradation detection**: When a domain section is extracted and republished without its evidentiary caveats, the republished version may overstate the evidence. The Domain 29 bank fraud Thompson contradiction is the live example: if a civil rights organization's press statement cites the SPLC indictment analysis but strips the "Weissmann assessment" framing and presents the legal conclusion as settled rather than contested, that is quality degradation. Any domain section publicly cited in a way that misrepresents the evidence triggers a "clarification note" distributed to contacts who received that domain.

**Loop 3 (Path A+37-specific) — Phase 1 to Phase 2 signal feed**: Under Path A+37, the 10-day window between Phase 1 activation (April 30) and Phase 2 deployment (est. May 12) is optimal for detecting which Tier 1 organizations are actively engaging with election-adjacent domains (Domains 1, 6, 33, 37 predecessor content) before Domain 37 reaches election protection organizations. Brennan Center staff requesting Domain 37 materials during Phase 1 monitoring is a signal to accelerate Phase 2 deployment and lead with a reference to the Brennan Center engagement.

**Loop 4 — AG coalition real-time tracking**: The 24-state AG coalition's active election EO litigation provides a uniquely fast feedback loop unavailable in other sectors. Monitor PACER filings, state AG press releases, and Democracy Docket docket updates for any citation to or use of the framework's Domain 33/34/37 analysis. This feedback loop operates on a weekly cycle, not the 60-90 day cycle typical of academic or legislative feedback.

## Part IV: Measurement Framework

### 4.1 Domain Popularity Metrics

For every inbound request, question, citation, or feedback event, tag it to the domain(s) involved. At 30-day, 60-day, and 90-day marks, rank domains by pull count:

- **High-pull domains**: Domains with immediate institutional relevance — likely Domains 1, 6, 29, 33, 37 in the current environment. Deepen these first in Phase 2 research.
- **Low-pull domains**: Domains not generating institutional interest despite being well-evidenced — possibly Domains 5, 8, 13. Low pull may indicate wrong audience, not wrong content. Reassess target audience before updating.
- **Surprise domains**: Domains generating more pull than expected. These reveal unanticipated institutional demand and are highest-priority signals for Phase 2 research investment.

### 4.2 Implementation Conversion Rate

The four-stage conversion pipeline:
1. Framework reference (organization receives and acknowledges)
2. Policy proposal (organization produces a deliverable incorporating domain analysis)
3. Legislative introduction or litigation filing (deliverable reaches a formal institutional process)
4. Enactment or favorable ruling (formal process produces a concrete outcome)

Realistic 12-month conversion targets, calibrated against the Advocacy Coalition Framework's finding that policy change requires 10+ years for deep structural change but can produce identifiable outputs within 2-3 years when the political environment is in flux:

- Stage 1 to Stage 2: 25-35% of contacted organizations produce a domain-incorporating deliverable
- Stage 2 to Stage 3: 15-25% of deliverables reach a formal institutional process
- Stage 3 to Stage 4: 10-20% of formal processes produce a concrete outcome in the first 12 months

The current U.S. political environment (courts blocking approximately 350 of 362 challenged executive actions; 24-state AG coalition active on election EO) is highly receptive to framework-informed legal challenges, which accelerates Stage 3 to Stage 4 conversion for litigation pathways.

### 4.4 Velocity Metrics

**Within-sector spread**: The ILO convention peer-effects research found that "the probability of adopting an international standard depends on how many other countries in a peer group have already adopted that standard" — peer effects increase adoption speed nonlinearly. The same dynamic operates within institutional sectors: if Brennan Center adopts the framework, the probability that ACS Scholars Network and Protect Democracy adopt it increases. Track first-adopter dates within each sector and model whether subsequent adoption is accelerating.

**Cross-sector cascade timing**: The bridge node model predicts specific cascade pathways (Just Security cite → Senate Judiciary staff; EPI brief → AFL-CIO newsletter). Track whether these cascades are activating on predicted timelines. A cascade that takes 90 days where the model predicted 30 days indicates either a relationship failure or an institutional resistance factor — both are actionable intelligence.

## Part V: Productive vs. Destructive Adaptations

### 5.1 The Core Distinction

Adaptations are **constructive** when they extend the framework's analytical logic to contexts the original framework did not cover — when a law school clinic uses the Domain 6 shadow docket analysis as the foundation for an empirical research project that codes 500+ emergency SCOTUS applications and publishes the result in a law review with the framework as the analytical anchor.

Adaptations are **dilutive** when they strip the framework's structural analysis and republish only the politically convenient surface-level claims. A political advocacy organization publishing a "fact sheet" that excerpts the most alarming statistics from Domain 37 (ICE-at-polls, election denier appointments, Bannon quote) without the framework's analytical caveat structure — presenting contested projections as certainties — creates factual exposure. That fact sheet will attract fact-checkers who will find the unqualified claims overstate the evidence, and the result is reputational damage to the framework's credibility in the institutional audiences that matter most.

**State-specific constitutional amendments are the clearest constructive adaptation**: When a state AG coalition uses the framework's Domain 33 analysis and adds state-specific constitutional text analysis (their own state's elections clause, anti-commandeering precedents) to produce a filing that is analytically richer than the base document, that is productive adaptation — it strengthens the framework by grounding it in specific legal contexts the base document could not anticipate.

**Narrow carve-outs are the clearest destructive adaptation**: When a think tank extracts the RCV evidence from Domain 1 and publishes it as a standalone policy brief that cites RCV's benefits without the framework's analysis of how electoral reform interacts with judicial independence (Domain 6) and redistricting protection (Domain 1f), the extracted brief is easier to refute. Advocates relying on the carve-out brief face sophisticated opponents who know the connection points the carve-out omitted.

### 5.2 Decision Tree for Feedback Response

```
Unexpected adoption detected
  → Is it constructive or dilutive?
    CONSTRUCTIVE (extends framework logic):
      → Log as Phase 2 demand signal for that domain
      → Reach out to confirm the adaptation is accurate
      → If accurate, integrate the adaptation's insights as a domain annotation
      → If it fills a genuine gap: consider a domain update or companion brief
    DILUTIVE (strips analytical caveats or misrepresents evidence):
      → Assess severity: does it create factual exposure?
        SEVERE (misstatement of evidence that fact-checkers will catch):
          → Issue clarification note to all contacts who received that domain
          → Contact the publishing organization directly to offer corrective version
          → Do not issue a public correction unless the misstatement reaches major media
        MODERATE (framing diverges but evidence is not misrepresented):
          → Log for messaging iteration review
          → Wait for second independent instance before issuing domain update
        MINOR (stylistic divergence from original framing):
          → Log; no action required
```

### 5.3 Domain Update vs. Accept Adaptation Decision Table

| Signal | Update or Accept? | Rationale |
|--------|-------------------|-----------|
| Two or more Tier 1 contacts identify same evidentiary gap | Update: add evidence to domain | Multiple independent experts identifying the same gap = genuine gap |
| Single Tier 1 contact disputes a domain's policy conclusion | Accept divergence: add "counterargument" subsection | Policy conclusions are legitimately contested; evidence is not |
| Sector adaptation produces more effective framing for one audience | Accept and create sector-specific brief | Base domain retains analytical structure; derived formats serve specialized audiences |
| New court ruling, executive action, or legislative outcome updates a domain's factual predicate | Update: add Section [X] update notation with new facts | Factual currency is a credibility requirement |
| Phase 2 domain has two Tier 1 demand signals | Update: initiate full research for that domain | Demand-driven Phase 2 research is the operating rule |
| AG coalition filing cites domain analysis with state-specific additions | Accept and log as constructive adaptation; reach out to confirm accuracy | State-specific legal grounding strengthens the base framework |
| Low pull on a domain after 90 days | Do not update; reassess target audience | Low pull may mean wrong audience, not wrong content |

## Appendix: Path-Specific Notes

**Path A (Immediate Full Distribution)**: All six sectors receive the full corpus simultaneously. The domain pull ranking (Part IV.3.1) will be most useful in the first 30 days — simultaneous distribution creates a natural experiment that identifies which domains generate pull from which sectors. The AG sector's Loop 4 real-time tracking applies immediately.

**Path A+37 (Recommended Hybrid)**: The 10-day Phase 1 monitoring window activates Loop 3 — a first-signal dataset identifying which organizations are actively engaging with election-adjacent domains before Domain 37 deploys. Organizations that engaged with Domains 1, 6, 33 in Phase 1 are higher-priority Phase 2 targets and should receive Phase 2 framing that references their Phase 1 engagement explicitly. AG offices that have filed on the election EO should receive Domain 37 in Phase 2 as a direct supplement to their active litigation rather than as new research.

**Path B (Maintenance First)**: The spring legislative session advocacy windows described in Part II.2 are partially or fully closed before the first adoption signals can convert into bill introductions. The AG coalition feedback loop (Loop 4) is still operative — court filings are not session-dependent. The Phase 2 trigger table urgency overrides (Domain 37b, Domain 31x) apply on the same timeline regardless of Path B's delayed general distribution.
