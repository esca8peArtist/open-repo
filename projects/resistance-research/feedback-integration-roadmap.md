# Post-Distribution Feedback Integration Roadmap

**May 6, 2026 — Version 3.0**

**What changed from v2.0 (April 30)**: Version 3.0 incorporates findings from `post-phase-1-institutional-adaptation-roadmap.md` and `stakeholder-feedback-integration-protocol.md` (both May 2026). V2.0 established the seeding sequence, sector adaptation patterns, and loop topology. This version adds: (1) explicit decision trees for each loop; (2) sector-specific modification risk classification with protocol for each risk tier; (3) adoption velocity benchmarks with realistic Phase 1→Phase 2 transition timelines by sector; (4) a fragmentation-prevention protocol for iterating without producing incompatible derivative versions. The sector adaptation analysis in Part I is materially deepened; the feedback loop architecture in Part III adds decision trees not present in v2.0.

---

## Part I: Sector-Specific Adaptation Patterns and Modification Risk

Each sector's adaptation of the 35-domain framework follows a predictable institutional logic. The modification risk classification below determines the level of feedback scrutiny warranted for each sector's outputs.

### 1.1 State Attorneys General — Extraction and Litigation Redeployment

**Adaptation logic**: AGs mine the framework for constitutional argument structures that survive a preliminary injunction standard in a federal circuit. They do not adopt frameworks; they extract citeable propositions and pre-assembled evidentiary architecture and embed those in filings without formal attribution.

**What AGs will modify**: Strip all foreign-court citations (German Federal Constitutional Court, Hungarian Constitutional Court) — these are non-persuasive in U.S. federal circuits and create framing vulnerability in hostile tribunals. Add state-specific constitutional text: each AG office will supplement Domain 33 and Domain 34 with their state's elections clause language, anti-commandeering precedents, and Tenth Amendment standing arguments. Convert analytical propositions into litigation-ready conclusions supported by the framework's evidence.

**What AGs will keep**: U.S. state precedent comparisons; statutory text analysis; empirical evidence on administrative effects; the cross-domain reinforcing pair structure (D1+D37 for election EO cases, D6+D29 for judicial independence arguments).

**Modification risk classification: LOW**. AG modifications are additive and jurisdiction-specific. The core constitutional argument structures are load-bearing but are not typically stripped — they are supplemented with local law. The single high-risk extraction pattern: press releases that use framework language while the underlying brief does not reference domain analysis. This is awareness, not integration; log separately.

**Feedback channel**: Democracy Docket case tracker, state AG press release feeds, PACER full-text search for distinctive framework phrases. Weekly review in Days 1–60; monthly thereafter.

---

### 1.2 Law Schools — Three-Track Adoption at Different Velocities

**Clinic adoption (6–12 weeks)**: Clinic directors use domain sections as primary case material for active student work. Adaptation pattern: simplify analytical structure, focus on the most actionable doctrinal sections, generate student-facing worksheets from evidence tables.

**Faculty adoption (4–16 weeks, semester-dependent)**: Professors incorporate domain sections into seminar reading lists or use the framework's analytical structure in their own research. Adaptation pattern: reframe advocacy-adjacent language into neutral academic register, add doctrinal precision to policy claims, cite as "analytical scaffolding."

**Law review adoption (12–24 months from submission to publication)**: This is a lagging indicator, not a leading one. The editing process strips politically charged framing and demands stronger citation standards — the published version is analytically tighter but narrower.

**Modification risk classification: LOW-CONSTRUCTIVE**. The academic editing process is constructive, not dilutive. The primary risk is attribution drift: a law review article that uses the framework's analytical structure extensively while attributing conclusions to other sources. This is standard academic practice, not misrepresentation. No response needed; log as framework impact.

**Feedback channel**: SSRN working papers, Google Scholar alerts, direct outreach to clinic directors at Harvard DROL, NYU Brennan, BU Legislative. Do not wait for law review publication to measure law school adoption — clinics generate signals 12–18 months earlier.

---

### 1.3 Think Tanks — Decomposition into Single-Issue Briefs

**Adaptation logic**: Think tanks extract individual domains, reformat in house style, add supplementary data, and publish without always crediting the original framework. This is the ALEC model-legislation pattern applied to research: between 2010–2018, ALEC model legislation was introduced in state legislatures approximately 2,900 times with over 600 bills enacted, with ALEC's effectiveness depending on individual bill customizability rather than holistic agenda adoption.

**Modification risk classification: MODERATE**. The attribution dilution dynamic is predictable and largely unavoidable. When Brennan Center extracts Domain 1's voter purge evidence and publishes it as a Brennan Center report, the framework's analytical categories are embedded in policy discourse regardless of attribution. The risk is narrow carve-outs that strip the cross-domain connective tissue: a think tank that extracts RCV evidence from Domain 1 without the Domain 6 judicial independence analysis makes the extracted brief more vulnerable to sophisticated rebuttal.

**Response protocol for narrow carve-outs**: Issue a companion brief connecting the extracted domain to its reinforcing partner domains. Do not contact the think tank to request a correction — that creates friction. Distribute the companion brief to the same audience the think tank's publication reached.

**Feedback channel**: Overton Policy Citation Index (6–18 month lag; set alerts now), direct monitoring of Brennan Center, CAP, EPI, Just Security, Lawfare publication pages. When a think tank publication is identified, conduct a structured artifact analysis (see Section 2.5 of `post-phase-1-institutional-adaptation-roadmap.md`).

---

### 1.4 Journalists — Vocabulary Migration and Background Research

**Adaptation logic**: Journalists use frameworks as interpretive lenses, not citeable sources. Adoption is non-linear and often untraceable through standard citation methods. The most common pattern: framework analysis shapes which questions a reporter asks and which sources they contact, without the framework appearing in the published story.

**Modification risk classification: VERY LOW**. Journalists do not modify frameworks; they internalize vocabulary and analytical frames. Attribution is not the goal. The risk is a journalist who misapplies a framework concept in reporting — an uncommon failure mode, and when it occurs, it is typically corrected through normal editorial processes.

**Do not send structured feedback requests to journalists**. Journalists are not institutional partners — they are amplifiers and investigators. Feedback comes through story coverage and interview requests. Make framework materials available for background research on request; do not treat journalistic engagement as a feedback channel requiring active management.

**Feedback channel**: Factiva, Google News alerts, and ProPublica searches for distinctive framework phrases (not common political vocabulary). If "wave filter legislative sequence" or domain-specific coinages appear in news reporting, that is a direct vocabulary adoption event; log it.

---

### 1.5 Unions and Civil Society — Horizontal Extraction Across Current Crises

**Adaptation logic**: Grassroots organizations extract horizontally (multiple domains mapped to one current crisis) rather than vertically (deep into one domain). Training integration is the primary adoption pathway: domain analysis is compressed into member-facing two-page issue briefs, workshop outlines, talking points.

**Modification risk classification: MODERATE-HIGH**. The highest risk is evidentiary stripping: civil rights organizations may extract the most alarming statistics from Domain 37 (election denier appointments, CISA cuts, Bannon quotes) without the analytical caveat structure, presenting contested projections as certainties. This creates factual exposure — those fact sheets will attract fact-checkers.

**Response protocol for evidentiary stripping**: Issue a clarification note to all contacts who received that domain. Contact the publishing organization directly to offer a corrective version. Do not issue a public correction unless the misstatement reaches major media. The Domain 29 Weissmann assessment framing is a specific load-bearing element: the "contested legal status" qualifier in that section must survive any adaptation. If it is stripped, treat as Moderate severity regardless of other factors.

**Feedback channel**: Request training materials that organizations develop using the framework; the adaptation artifacts (issue briefs, workshop outlines, action alerts) are the most direct evidence of how analysis has been operationalized. Quarterly check-in with Tier 3 confirmed adopters.

## Part II: Feedback Collection Decision Tree

The following decision tree governs which feedback events require active response and which are logged-only:

```
Feedback signal received
  │
  ├─ Is it an adoption signal (citation, use, derivative work)?
  │    YES → Log domain(s), sector, adoption level (1–4), date
  │          → Is adoption level 3 or 4?
  │               YES → High-value event: initiate institutional interview (Template 2)
  │               NO  → Log and monitor; check for cascade from this contact
  │
  ├─ Is it a gap or critique signal (missing evidence, missing domain, wrong framing)?
  │    YES → Is this the SECOND independent Tier 1 signal identifying the SAME gap?
  │               YES → Trigger domain update: add evidence, qualify claim, or
  │                     initiate Phase 2 research as appropriate
  │               NO  → Log; hold pending corroboration
  │
  ├─ Is it a modification artifact (derivative work exists)?
  │    YES → Is the modification constructive or dilutive?
  │               CONSTRUCTIVE (extends analytical logic, adds jurisdiction-specific data):
  │                 → Log as Phase 2 demand signal
  │                 → Reach out to confirm accuracy
  │                 → If accurate, annotate base domain with the extension
  │               DILUTIVE — SEVERE (misrepresents evidence; fact-checker exposure):
  │                 → Clarification note to all contacts who received that domain
  │                 → Direct contact with publishing organization
  │                 → Public correction only if reaches major media
  │               DILUTIVE — MODERATE (framing divergence, evidence preserved):
  │                 → Log; wait for second instance before domain update
  │               DILUTIVE — MINOR (stylistic divergence):
  │                 → Log; no action
  │
  └─ Is it a Phase 2 demand signal (explicit request for deeper research)?
       YES → What tier is the contact?
                Tier 1 → Log; if second independent Tier 1 request, advance to scheduling
                Tier 2 → Log; three signals equal one Tier 1 for threshold purposes
                Tier 3 → Log; three signals equal one Tier 1 for threshold purposes
             Has urgency override applied (advocacy window within 60 days)?
                YES → Advance to scheduling regardless of demand signal count
                NO  → Apply threshold rules above
```

## Part III: Iterative Refinement — What to Update, What to Accept

The triage principle: volume of independent agreement matters more than intensity of any individual opinion. One Tier 1 contact with strong views is less informative than three Tier 2 contacts independently reaching the same conclusion. No single contact's preference triggers a framework update; the two-signal threshold from independent sources is the floor.

| Feedback signal | Action | Rationale |
|----------------|--------|-----------|
| Two or more Tier 1 contacts identify same evidentiary gap in a domain | Update: add evidence to domain section | Multiple independent experts identifying the same gap = genuine gap |
| Multiple AGs request domain section + statutory language | Phase 2 candidate: full research on statutory companion brief | AG demand for statutory text = framework ready for diagnosis-to-remedy extension |
| Single think tank disputes a domain's policy conclusion | Accept divergence: add "contested interpretations" subsection | Policy conclusions are legitimately contested; evidence is not |
| Sector adaptation produces more effective framing for one audience | Accept: create sector-specific brief; base domain retains analytical structure | Both serve the framework's purpose without requiring base document revision |
| New court ruling updates a domain's factual predicate | Update: add Section [X] update notation with date and new facts | Factual currency is a credibility requirement |
| Law school faculty identify doctrinal imprecision | Update: add doctrinal qualification; do not change analytical conclusion | Faculty feedback on legal precision is the highest-quality content signal available |
| Single organization requests full domain rewrite | No action | Difference in editorial preference, not a content deficiency |
| Low pull on a domain after 90 days | No update; reassess target audience | Wrong audience, not wrong content — audience recalibration before content revision |
| Quality degradation: Weissmann assessment qualifiers stripped from Domain 29 | Contact publisher; issue clarification note | Load-bearing analytical qualifier; stripping creates factual misrepresentation |
| Quality degradation: Domain 37 statistics published without methodological sourcing | Contact publisher; issue clarification note | CISA cuts and election denier appointment counts require their derivation methodology |

### Fragmentation Prevention

The ABA's Ethics 2000 update (2002) created a patchwork where "the Model Rules" meant materially different documents across jurisdictions. The framework avoids this through additive versioning — updates supplement rather than replace:

- **Version tagging**: Base framework is Version 1.0 (distribution date). Any substantive domain update increments the minor version (1.1, 1.2). Phase 2 expansion is Version 2.0.
- **Update communication**: One-paragraph change notification to every contact who received that domain: what changed, why, what the update does not change. Frame as additive ("this supplements the analysis you already have"), not corrective, unless there is a genuine factual error.
- **No pull-and-replace**: Institutions that have already incorporated a domain section should not receive a correction framing that implies their work is now outdated. The additive frame preserves their citation's validity.
- **Sector-specific briefs do not fragment the base**: When a think tank needs a one-pager or a union needs a training packet, produce those as derived formats. The base domain remains canonical. Derived formats cite the base domain version number so the relationship is explicit.

## Part IV: Adoption Velocity Benchmarks and Phase 1→Phase 2 Decision Gates

### 4.1 Realistic Timelines by Sector

These benchmarks are drawn from three historical precedents: Model Penal Code (1962 ALI publication through state adoption wave), ABA Model Rules (1983 through 2009 full adoption), and Brennan Center's Automatic Voter Registration campaign (2015–2025).

| Sector | First visible signal | Level 2 adoption (reference) | Level 3 adoption (integration) | Level 4 adoption (propagation) |
|--------|---------------------|------------------------------|-------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| State AGs | Days 3–14 (docket monitoring) | 4–8 weeks | 2–4 months | 3–6 months |
| Think tanks | Days 7–21 (publication monitoring) | 2–6 weeks | 2–3 months | 4–6 months |
| Law school clinics | 6–12 weeks | 8–16 weeks | 4–8 months | Not applicable |
| Law school faculty | 4–16 weeks (semester-dependent) | 12–24 months (law review lag) | 24+ months | Not applicable |
| Journalists | Days 3–30 | Not applicable (no attribution norm) | Not applicable | Not applicable |
| Unions / civil society | 6–10 weeks | 8–16 weeks | 3–6 months | 4–8 months |

**Calibration note on law review adoption**: Law review citation is the last adoption event to measure, not the first. It is a lagging indicator confirming adoption that already occurred at clinic and faculty level 12–18 months earlier. Do not misread law review silence at Month 6 as law school non-adoption.

**Calibration note on AGs**: The 24-state AG coalition's active election EO litigation means Loop 4 (AG real-time tracking) is already operating before Phase 1 launches. AG adoption velocity in this environment will be faster than the historical baseline — the framework arrives into an active operational context, not a neutral monitoring context.

### 4.2 Quantitative Phase 1 Adoption Thresholds

These are conservative benchmarks. The AVR precedent (Oregon's adoption in March 2015 initiated a 2.4-state/year adoption wave) demonstrates that once a credible initiating institution adopts, the curve accelerates nonlinearly.

- **Minimum viable**: 5 or more Tier 1 institutional contacts producing domain-citing output within 90 days
- **Target**: 3 or more state AG offices citing framework analysis in active litigation within 6 months
- **Stretch**: 5 or more law school programs incorporating domain sections into curriculum or clinic casework within 12 months

**Below-benchmark diagnosis decision tree**:
```
Phase 1 adoption below benchmark at Month 3 check
  │
  ├─ Are high-pull domains (Domains 1, 6, 29, 33, 37) also low-pull?
  │    YES → Content gap diagnosis: framework may lack sector-specific application guidance
  │          → Produce sector-specific one-pagers; do not revise base documents
  │    NO  → Domain-specific low pull: wrong audience for that domain
  │          → Reassess which sector should be primary target for that domain
  │
  ├─ Are all sectors showing low pull, or only some?
  │    ALL SECTORS → Messaging failure: framework framing not resonating
  │                 → Revise outreach framing; do not revise base documents
  │    SOME SECTORS → Audience selection problem for low-pull sectors
  │                 → Recalibrate outreach list; do not revise base documents
  │
  └─ Is response rate itself low (below 15% of outreach)?
       YES → Distribution mechanics failure: emails not reaching decision-makers
             → Switch to secondary outreach channels; use bridge node introductions
       NO  → Response without adoption: contacts reading but not deploying
             → Implement Step 2 of adoption facilitation (tailored brief delivery)
```

### 4.3 Phase 2 Decision Gates

**Gate 1 (Month 3)**: Minimum viable benchmark met? Any domain receiving two or more Tier 1 demand signals for statutory implementation language? Any AG requesting domain deepening for active litigation? If yes to any: initiate the applicable Phase 2 research. If no: extend Phase 1 active outreach before broadening audience.

**Gate 2 (Month 6)**: Target benchmark met (3+ AG offices citing framework in active litigation)? If yes: Phase 2 distribution to broader institutional networks supported. Begin Phase 2 domain research on highest-demand candidates. If no: diagnose cause using the decision tree above; apply correction before Phase 2 expansion.

**Gate 3 (Month 9)**: Are three or more sectors showing Level 3 or Level 4 adoption evidence? If yes: Phase 2 distribution to adjacent networks; consider academic companion pieces and sector-specific curriculum materials. If no: extend Phase 1 targeted re-engagement before broadening.

**Gate 4 (Month 12)**: Phase 1 retrospective. Which domains achieved deepest institutional integration? Which sectors were most and least responsive? What does the domain pull ranking reveal about Phase 2 priorities? Results feed directly into Phase 2 domain selection and Phase 2 outreach sequencing.

## Part V: Monthly Synthesis Cadence

**Month 1 (post-distribution)**: Collection only. Aggregate all direct feedback signals. Tally domain pull counts by sector. Identify red flags (quality degradation events, framing mismatches, scope objections — see `stakeholder-feedback-integration-protocol.md` for five-flag taxonomy). No base domain updates in Month 1.

**Month 2**: Analyze Month 1 dataset against the feedback triage table in Part III. Run the gap-signal threshold check: any domain at 2 or more independent Tier 1 signals? If yes, schedule the domain update. Identify any Phase 2 demand signal accumulation. Issue clarification notes for quality degradation events logged in Month 1.

**Quarterly (Months 3, 6, 9, 12)**: Full synthesis cycle. Apply all triage rules. Conduct institutional interviews with 5–10 highest-leverage contacts using Template 2 in `adoption-feedback-template.md`. Produce one-page internal memo: which domains are generating pull, which bridge nodes have activated, what cascades are in motion. Compare adoption velocity against benchmarks in Part IV. Apply decision gate assessment.

**Monthly synthesis template location**: `adoption-feedback-template.md`, Template 4. Complete the Adoption Level Distribution table, Domain Pull Rankings, Quality Degradation Log, Phase 2 Demand Signal Tracker, and Secondary Distribution Map at each monthly interval.

## Appendix: Path-Specific Notes

**Path A (Immediate Full Distribution)**: All six sectors receive the full corpus simultaneously. The domain pull ranking (Part IV) will be most informative in the first 30 days — simultaneous distribution creates a natural experiment identifying which domains generate pull from which sectors. AG sector Loop 4 real-time tracking applies immediately.

**Path A+37 (Recommended Hybrid)**: The 10-day Phase 1 monitoring window activates an additional signal loop: which organizations are actively engaging with election-adjacent domains (Domains 1, 6, 33) before Domain 37 deploys. Organizations that engaged in Phase 1 should receive Domain 37 framing that references their Phase 1 engagement explicitly — it is a supplement to their active work, not new research. AG offices with active election EO filings are the highest-priority Phase 2 targets under this path.

**Path B (Maintenance First)**: Spring legislative session advocacy windows described for state legislature sector are partially closed before first adoption signals convert into bill introductions. AG coalition feedback Loop 4 remains fully operative — court filings are not session-dependent. Phase 2 urgency overrides (Domain 37b April–July window, Domain 31x July 31 tariff date) apply on the same timeline regardless of Path B's delayed general distribution.
