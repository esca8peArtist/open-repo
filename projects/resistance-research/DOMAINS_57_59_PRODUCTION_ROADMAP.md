## 1. Domain 57 Specification Summary — Multilateral Withdrawal and Democratic Norm Erosion

### Core Argument (one paragraph)

When the United States withdrew from 66 international organizations in a single January 7, 2026 Presidential Memorandum — including the UN Democracy Fund, International IDEA, IPCC, UNFCCC, and UNFPA — it did not merely retreat from foreign policy commitments. It dismantled the accountability infrastructure that constrains authoritarian drift domestically: the benchmarking data civil society uses to hold government accountable, the treaty compliance obligations that courts and Congress can enforce, and the reputational peer pressure that raises the cost of extreme norm violations. The ICC sanctions (February 2025 onward, extended and expanded through 2026) are the sharpest illustration: any US person who provides material support to ICC investigations now faces up to 20 years in prison, severing US civil society's capacity to invoke international accountability mechanisms against domestic abuses. Domain 57 provides the missing link between foreign policy and domestic democratic architecture — a reframing that opens the framework to international law, global democracy, and foreign policy reform constituencies currently outside Phase 1's reach.

### Research Scope and Boundaries

**In scope:**
- The January 7, 2026 withdrawal memorandum and all 66 organizations (categorized by accountability function)
- Constitutional asymmetry in treaty entry vs. exit (Article II and the Senate's ratification power)
- Three domestic accountability pathways removed by withdrawal: benchmarking, treaty compliance, and reputational cost
- ICC sanctions as a case study in domestic civil society capacity destruction
- Alliance fragmentation (EU Article 42.7 as response to NATO Article 5 credibility loss) as a democratic coalition consequence
- Comparative cases: Brexit, Turkey-CoE drift, prior US withdrawals (UNESCO 1984, Paris Agreement, WHO Trump I)
- Reform pathways: Treaty Withdrawal Notification Act, Multilateral Commitment Protection Act, ICC Sanctions Repeal

**Out of scope:**
- Foreign policy consequences as such (trade effects, defense positioning, bilateral relationships) — these are covered in Domain 23 and Domain 28
- The domestic politics of any individual foreign policy decision — this domain argues the structural mechanism, not partisan attribution
- UN reform advocacy — the domain does not argue for preserving the status quo of any institution; it argues for preserving the accountability functions those institutions serve

## 2. Domain 59 Specification Summary — Economic Precarity as Democratic Constraint

### Core Argument (one paragraph)

Democratic participation is not a free activity. The 2024 income-participation gap — 48% voter turnout among the lowest income quintile versus 86% among the highest — is not an accident of preference: it is the predictable output of a political economy that concentrates time, money, cognitive bandwidth, and residential stability in already-powerful populations while systematically depleting them in the populations most affected by policy decisions. Three peer-reviewed studies establish the causal structure: Dallas Fed wp2517 (revised March 2026) shows mortgage payment relief directly increased voter turnout; Slee and Desmond (Politics and Society, 2023) shows neighborhood eviction rates causally reduce turnout; PNAS 2024 shows induced residential mobility persistently depresses voting among low-income Black and Hispanic adults. Domain 59's central novel contribution is naming the OBBBA's simultaneous cuts to Medicaid, SNAP, housing assistance, childcare subsidies, and student loan protections as a coordinated reduction in civic participation capacity for 80 million Americans — a compounding effect that is multiplicative, not additive, and that functions as democratic design failure, not merely poverty policy failure.

**Post-outline update**: The OBBBA was signed into law July 4, 2025, and passed the Senate July 1 with a 51-50 vote. Domain 59 no longer argues about projected consequences — it documents enacted law with confirmed implementation timelines. Work requirements take effect December 2026; SNAP cuts are scheduled for 2027. The urgency argument is stronger than when the outline was written.

### Research Scope and Boundaries

**In scope:**
- Income-participation gap (empirical baseline)
- Mullainathan/Shafir scarcity framework applied to democratic participation
- Housing instability as voter registration disruption (Slee/Desmond anchor)
- Gig economy, time poverty, and temporal civic exclusion
- OBBBA's enacted cuts as coordinated civic capacity reduction
- Comparative analysis: OECD democracies with higher economic security and higher participation
- Reform architecture: Same-Day Registration, gig worker voting rights, automatic voter registration for public benefit recipients

**Out of scope:**
- Poverty policy as such — the framing is democratic participation, not economic justice per se; economic justice arguments that don't connect to civic participation are better housed in Domain 22 or Domain 47
- Electoral system reform (proportional representation, ranked-choice) — these belong in Domain 1 and Domain 33; note but do not develop
- OBBBA's fiscal or health policy consequences beyond their civic participation effects — these are the province of Domain 31 (Medicaid) and Domain 47 (housing); cross-reference, do not duplicate

## 4. Quality Gate Procedure

### What Makes a Domain "Complete"

A domain document passes the quality gate when all of the following are true:

1. **Evidence sufficiency**: All items on the evidence requirements checklist (Sections 1 and 2 above) are confirmed with working citations.
2. **Argument integrity**: The document's core novel contribution (Domain 57: withdrawal as domestic accountability dismantlement; Domain 59: OBBBA cuts as multiplicative civic capacity reduction) is stated in the introduction and fully demonstrated by the evidence in the body — not asserted and left to the reader.
3. **Cross-reference accuracy**: Every cross-reference to another domain (57 bridges: Domains 23, 26, 28, 33, 51; 59 bridges: Domains 1, 31, 47, 48/54, 50) is confirmed against the actual content of those domain files. No cross-reference describes something a domain file does not actually contain.
4. **Citation integrity**: All URLs are live at time of publication-ready status. Any URL that has gone dark since the outline was written is replaced or removed.
5. **Word count in range**: Domain 57 target 6,500–7,500 words. Domain 59 target 6,800–8,000 words (more data-dense).
6. **No duplication of existing domains**: The researcher has specifically confirmed that Domain 59 Sections 5's OBBBA material does not duplicate Domain 31, that Section 3's housing material does not duplicate Domain 47, and that Section 2's healthcare material does not duplicate Domain 50.

### Self-Check Validation (Researcher-Led)

Before the document moves to peer review, the researcher runs the following self-check:

- [ ] Read the document aloud (or have it read back): identifies unclear argument progression
- [ ] Verify the single-paragraph core argument (introduction) matches what the evidence sections actually prove
- [ ] Confirm all empirical findings are reported with full source attribution in the section where they first appear, and abbreviated citation on subsequent appearances
- [ ] Run a cross-reference audit: for each domain mentioned in the "Cross-Domain Bridges" section, open that domain file and confirm the connection is real
- [ ] Check that reform pathways in the final section are grounded in named legislative models or proposals, not generic recommendations
- [ ] Confirm word count per section is within 20% of target (prevents any single section from consuming the domain's analytical space at the expense of others)
