# Environmental Justice as Democratic Infrastructure
## How the EPA's 15-Month Dismantlement Removed Frontline Communities from the Civic Body

*Domain 49 — Resistance Research Framework, Phase 2*
*June 14, 2026*

---

## Executive Summary

The most important finding for reform advocates: *Marin Audubon Society v. FAA* (D.C. Circuit, November 12, 2024) held that the Council on Environmental Quality never had authority to issue binding NEPA regulations. This ruling, issued before the Trump administration's January 8, 2026 rescission, means that a future favorable administration cannot restore NEPA's community participation rights by executive action alone — not because the action would be politically reversible, but because the D.C. Circuit has held the regulatory pathway is legally foreclosed. Statutory NEPA amendments are not a preferred alternative to executive restoration. They are the only legally adequate remedy.

On April 20, 2026, the EPA's rescission of the 2009 Greenhouse Gas Endangerment Finding took legal effect. Between February 2025 and April 2026, EPA eliminated every formal mechanism through which frontline communities could assert procedural rights in federal environmental decision-making: EJSCREEN removed (February 5, 2025); OEJECR closed (March 12, 2025); 781 IRA EJ grants terminated (throughout 2025); all NEPA implementing regulations rescinded (January 8, 2026); the Endangerment Finding rescinded (effective April 20, 2026).

This document argues what no existing environmental advocacy document argues in full: environmental justice communities are democratic participants who have been systematically stripped of the legal and procedural tools through which they exercised civic power. The 2025–2026 elimination of EPA's EJ infrastructure is not deregulation. It is a coordinated removal of democratic participation infrastructure from the communities most dependent on it — a demolition sequence designed so that each successive step was made easier by the one before.

## Section 1: The Democratic Design Argument — Why This Is Not an Environmental Document

### 1.1 Reframing the Stakes

The conventional framing of EPA's 2025–2026 actions is environmental: rollbacks of regulations, elimination of offices, cancellation of grants. That framing is accurate but insufficient. It captures what was removed but not *who* lost the capacity to act democratically.

For three decades, the EPA's environmental justice infrastructure gave frontline communities formal channels to contest decisions about their own health, land, and survival. These channels were not charity. They were legal rights — grounded in Title VI of the Civil Rights Act, operationalized through NEPA's Environmental Impact Statement process, screened through EJSCREEN data, and administered by the Office of Environmental Justice and External Civil Rights. When a majority-Black community in Louisiana's Cancer Alley contested a permit for a chloroprene-emitting chemical plant, it used these channels. When a tribal nation contested a pipeline through sacred land, it used the NEPA consultation requirement. When West Harlem residents challenged the MTA's siting of diesel bus depots in their neighborhood, they used Title VI.

Those channels are now closed or functionally destroyed. The Union of Concerned Scientists has documented what happens when they are removed: environmental burden concentrates in communities already deprived of political power; that burden — through chronic illness, economic instability, and displacement — suppresses civic engagement; suppressed civic engagement allows further environmental degradation without political accountability; which further suppresses health and civic capacity [UCS 2018a]. The EPA's 2025–2026 actions did not create this cycle. They removed the formal mechanisms that partially interrupted it.

### 1.2 The Architecture of the Demolition Sequence

Between February 2025 and April 2026, the following EPA infrastructure was eliminated or rendered nonfunctional:

**EJSCREEN removed** (February 5, 2025): EPA removed from its website EJSCREEN, the environmental justice mapping and screening tool that communities, regulators, and advocates used to document disproportionate pollution burdens, generate data for permit challenges, and demonstrate eligibility for EJ grant programs [EDGI 2025a; Harvard EELP 2025b]. EPA simultaneously removed the Biden-era Climate and Economic Justice Screening Tool (CEJST). Both were taken down simultaneously — the complete elimination of federal EJ data infrastructure in a single action [Harvard EELP 2025d].

**OEJECR closure order** (March 12, 2025): Administrator Zeldin ordered closure of the Office of Environmental Justice and External Civil Rights and all 10 regional EPA EJ offices. EPA's press release framed the action as "terminat[ing] Biden's Environmental Justice, DEI Arms of Agency" [EPA 2025]. The administrative framing is analytically important: by characterizing Title VI civil rights enforcement as "DEI," the administration enabled the elimination of a civil rights enforcement office without engaging the statutory question of whether Congress authorized that elimination.

**Employee terminations** (April–May 2025): EPA announced plans to terminate nearly 300 employees from its EJ and civil rights programs [Inside Climate News 2025]. Public Employees for Environmental Responsibility filed a complaint with the U.S. Office of Special Counsel.

**781 EJ grants terminated** (throughout 2025): EPA moved to cancel 781 environmental justice grants — almost all funded through the Inflation Reduction Act's $3 billion Environmental and Climate Justice Block Grant Program. Communities that had received award notices — including a majority-Latino community in San Diego expecting $12.7 million for zero-emission transportation — found funding suspended or terminated [Eos 2025a]. The Environmental Data and Governance Initiative's grant cancellation map documents the geographic concentration in frontline communities [EDGI 2025b].

**NEPA regulations rescinded** (January 8, 2026): CEQ issued Federal Register rule 2026-00178, removing all NEPA implementing regulations effective immediately. The Biden Phase 2 rule's requirements to consider Indigenous knowledge, account for cumulative impacts on EJ communities, and conduct early consultation with affected populations were all eliminated [Federal Register 2026a; Harvard EELP 2026a; Orrick 2026]. A coalition of 228 organizations had warned in August 2025 that the approach would "cut the people out of the people's environmental law" [Tribal Business News 2025].

**Endangerment Finding rescinded** (effective April 20, 2026): EPA finalized rescission of the 2009 GHG Endangerment Finding (Federal Register 2026-03157), eliminating the scientific and legal predicate for all federal GHG emissions regulation under the Clean Air Act, and simultaneously repealing all GHG emission standards for light-, medium-, and heavy-duty vehicles [Federal Register 2026b].

The architecture of this sequence matters: it began with data removal (EJSCREEN), continued with personnel elimination (OEJECR), then procedural rights removal (NEPA), and concluded with foundational legal authority elimination (Endangerment Finding). Each step removed a layer of the participation infrastructure, making each successive step easier by establishing the precedent that EJ infrastructure is administrative discretion, not statutory right.

*New in June 2026*: A sixth threat layer has emerged. A federal EO issued April 8, 2025 directs the Attorney General to identify and challenge state environmental justice laws as unconstitutional, preempted, or unenforceable. The demolition does not stop at the federal level — it pursues the state-level replacement infrastructure that EJ advocates have built over 30 years [Marten Law 2025; Center for Progressive Reform 2026].

## Section 2: Five Causal Pathways from Environmental Burden to Democratic Exclusion

### Pathway 1: Health Burden as Civic Suppression

The most foundational causal pathway runs from environmental pollution through chronic illness to depressed civic participation. In Louisiana's Cancer Alley — the 85-mile petrochemical corridor between Baton Rouge and New Orleans — communities of color face 7 to 21 times the pollution levels of industrialized white communities, with children attending elementary school adjacent to facilities emitting chloroprene at 8,000 times EPA's acceptable threshold [PRRAC 2024].

The civic consequence is documented by UCS's Center for Science and Democracy: "health problems in a community tend to depress voter turnout, further limiting the community's ability to use the democratic process to protect itself" [UCS 2018a]. This is a structurally reinforcing loop. States with more restrictive voting policies also tend to have worse health outcomes. Legislatures in states with extreme gerrymandering show less willingness to expand Medicaid or provide healthcare services [UCS 2018a].

The 2026 Civic Information Index (Listening Post Collective), which for the first time integrates environmental resilience data alongside voter registration and civic health metrics, finds lower civic health scores consistently associated with communities with higher percentages of Black, Hispanic/Latino, and Native American residents — and documents that more than 40% of registered voters in Appalachian and Black South regions did not vote in the last presidential election [Listening Post Collective 2026]. These are precisely the regions where EPA EJ infrastructure was most actively used and where its elimination falls hardest.

### Pathway 2: NEPA Participation Rights as Procedural Gateway — Now Closed

NEPA created a formal mechanism for community standing in federal decision-making. The Environmental Impact Statement process required federal agencies to solicit public comment before approving projects affecting their communities. For EJ communities, NEPA EIS comment periods were frequently the *primary* democratic channel available — the only formal forum where residents' objections to industrial facility siting, highway construction through residential neighborhoods, or pipeline routing through tribal lands were legally required to be considered.

CEQ's January 8, 2026 rescission of all NEPA implementing regulations eliminated this procedural standing. The prior Phase 2 NEPA regulations had specifically required agencies to consider Indigenous knowledge, account for cumulative impacts on EJ communities, and conduct early consultation with potentially impacted communities. The rescinded regulations replaced all of these requirements with guidance that "agencies have discretion over whether and how to conduct NEPA reviews" [Harvard EELP 2026a].

Two judicial developments compound the damage. First, the D.C. Circuit's November 2024 *Marin Audubon* ruling held CEQ never had authority to issue binding NEPA regulations — meaning the administrative restoration pathway is judicially foreclosed independent of any executive action. Second, the Supreme Court's *Seven County Infrastructure Coalition v. Eagle County* (May 29, 2025, 8-0) held federal agencies are entitled to "substantial deference" on EIS scope, specifically excluding downstream Gulf Coast EJ communities ("already overburdened by pollution from refining") from the NEPA analysis. The CRS analysis of this decision identifies a "Deference Squared" effect: agencies get deference on what effects to analyze and on whether their analysis is sufficient — a compounding removal of community participation capacity [CRS LSB11333 2025].

Despite this, NEPA statutory rights retain force. State AGs and community organizations are successfully using statutory NEPA — the text of the law, not the CEQ regulations — to challenge ICE detention center conversions. Maryland's AG obtained a preliminary injunction. Salt Lake City and Salt Lake County filed suit. The persistence of NEPA litigation in 2026 demonstrates that statutory NEPA retains community participation capacity that courts are willing to enforce [Bloomberg Law 2026; KUER 2026].

### Pathway 3: Title VI Administrative Pathways — Dismantled in Two Stages

Title VI of the Civil Rights Act prohibits discrimination in programs receiving federal financial assistance. The EPA's OEJECR was the administrative body that processed Title VI complaints from communities alleging discriminatory environmental permitting.

**Stage 1 — Louisiana court ruling (August 2024)**: A federal court in the Western District of Louisiana issued a permanent injunction blocking EPA from enforcing Title VI disparate-impact regulations in Louisiana — with explicit invitation for "copycat suits" nationally [Earthjustice 2024]. In Cancer Alley, Denka Performance Elastomer and Formosa Plastics had been permitted near elementary schools in majority-Black communities. The court held that EPA's disparate impact standard exceeded statutory authority, functionally preempting the administrative Title VI pathway before OEJECR was closed.

**Stage 2 — OEJECR closure (March 2025)**: With the office eliminated, EPA has not accepted any new Title VI complaints since January 2025. The agency moved to reject five existing complaints while 13 remain in the queue with no processing timeline. Nine active investigations are frozen — including complaints in North Carolina and Alabama whose communities have received no EPA communication since Trump took office [Sentient Media 2025].

The combined effect is not administrative backlog. It is the structural closure of the only accessible civil rights pathway for communities that lack resources for federal litigation.

### Pathway 4: Endangerment Finding Rescission as Regulatory Standing Loss

The 2009 Greenhouse Gas Endangerment Finding was more than an environmental protection — it was the legal predicate that generated federal regulatory review of emissions-increasing facilities. For EJ communities, this regulatory hook had a specific democratic function: it attached Clean Air Act review requirements to facilities whose GHG emissions triggered federal oversight, generating EIS processes and public comment periods.

With the Finding rescinded effective April 20, 2026, that chain is broken. Dr. Beverly Wright of the Deep South Center for Environmental Justice frames the consequence directly: "Without federal oversight, facilities will operate without greenhouse gas limits, compounding existing pollution from petrochemical operations concentrated in Black and low-income neighborhoods along the Mississippi River Chemical Corridor" [DSCEJ 2026].

The Harvard Salata Institute identifies the core legal vulnerability in EPA's rationale: the 2007 Supreme Court decision in *Massachusetts v. EPA* directly contradicts EPA's current position, holding that "greenhouse gases are unambiguously air pollutants under the Clean Air Act" and requiring EPA to exercise scientific judgment without considering policy or cost considerations [Harvard Salata Institute 2026]. The Georgetown Environmental Law Review documents how the "major questions doctrine" also poses a threat — the question of whether EPA can use the major questions doctrine to justify escaping its own prior scientific determination is unresolved [Georgetown Environmental Law Review 2026].

### Pathway 5: Environmental Organizing as Electoral Infrastructure — Connection Severed

The Environmental Voter Project has documented that environmental priorities are among the highest motivators for previously low-turnout voters in EJ communities. EPA EJ infrastructure destruction removes the connective tissue. EPA community liaisons and EJ coordinators often served as contact points through which community organizers identified affected residents, documented permit proceedings, and mobilized participation in both regulatory processes and electoral campaigns.

A 2026 peer-reviewed study (PMC 12779674) found that "resistant capital" — understanding systems of oppression — was the strongest predictor of environmental civic participation for Black, Asian, and Latine/Hispanic individuals, and that "social capital" through community organizations was a key enabler of continued participation [PMC 2026]. The elimination of EPA community liaisons removes a category of organizational infrastructure that supported this social capital. Ben Jealous has stated the connection directly: "There is no green vote without the Black and Brown vote" [WRI 2021].

*New in June 2026 — Sixth Pathway*: The EJSCREEN data gap as information access suppression. The Aarhus Convention's first pillar (access to information) is violated by EJSCREEN removal independently of the participation and justice pillars. Third-party reconstruction efforts — Yale Energy History EJScreen Mirror, Harvard HELD data at the Dataverse, PEDP reconstruction — demonstrate what the data removal means in practice: communities cannot document their own pollution burden without accessing mirrors operated outside EPA [EDGI 2025b].

## Section 3: The Marin Audubon Problem — Why Executive Restoration Is Insufficient

The most important legal development for reform advocates is *Marin Audubon Society v. Federal Aviation Administration* (D.C. Circuit, November 12, 2024, 2-1). The majority held that CEQ never had statutory authority to issue binding NEPA regulations — that its regulatory authority was limited to advisory functions, not legally enforceable rules.

The implications for the reform architecture are direct:

1. A future favorable administration that reinstates CEQ's NEPA regulations would face immediate legal challenge under *Marin Audubon*'s holding. The D.C. Circuit would likely find the new regulations invalid under the same reasoning.

2. The en banc petition filed by the Biden administration (December 5, 2024) was effectively mooted when the new administration took office. The en banc pathway for overturning *Marin Audubon* is closed absent a new petition by parties with standing.

3. Congress's 2023 NEPA amendments codified some long-standing CEQ framework elements into statute — demonstrating that the statutory amendment pathway is viable and has precedent. But the 2023 amendments were partial; the full EJ participation rights framework requires additional statutory anchoring.

The correct framing for reform advocacy: "Executive restoration is insufficient not only because it can be undone by the next administration, but because the D.C. Circuit has held that CEQ regulatory authority itself is legally foreclosed. The only reliable pathway to restore environmental participation rights is statutory NEPA amendments that do not depend on CEQ authority."

## Section 4: Live Litigation — D.C. Circuit and State-Level Challenges

### 4.1 D.C. Circuit: Endangerment Finding Challenges

Four petitions for review were filed in the D.C. Circuit challenging the Endangerment Finding rescission. As of June 14, 2026, all four remain in active briefing; oral arguments have not been scheduled.

**APHA v. U.S. EPA** (No. 26-1037): Filed February 18, 2026 by Earthjustice representing 15 nonprofits and Alaskan tribes. The Alaskan tribes provide the most direct EJ community standing argument: these are communities experiencing immediate, documented harm from climate change — permafrost collapse, coastal erosion, inability to practice subsistence livelihoods — that cannot be separated from the GHG emissions the Endangerment Finding regulated [Earthjustice 2026a; Clearinghouse 2026].

**Venner v. U.S. EPA** (No. 26-1038): Filed by Our Children's Trust on behalf of 18 youth from 10 states — raising both Clean Air Act and constitutional claims [Our Children's Trust 2026].

**Zero Emission Transportation Ass'n v. EPA** (No. 26-1039): Filed February 20, 2026.

**Massachusetts v. U.S. EPA** (No. 26-1061): Filed March 19, 2026 by 24 states plus 12 counties and cities. Led by AGs from California, Connecticut, New York, and Massachusetts, with an additional 20 states and territories, plus city parties including Denver, Boston, Chicago, Los Angeles, and New York [Smart Cities Dive 2026].

In April 2026, petitioners filed a petition for reconsideration arguing the final rule was not a "logical outgrowth" of the proposed rule — a procedural argument that could void the rescission independently of its merits. A group of 25 states led by West Virginia and Kentucky filed a motion to intervene to defend the rescission [Harvard EELP 2026b].

**Key legal question**: Both Harvard Salata Institute and Georgetown Environmental Law Review identify *Massachusetts v. EPA* (2007, SCOTUS) as the primary obstacle to the rescission's survival [Harvard Salata Institute 2026; Georgetown Environmental Law Review 2026]. EPA's current rationale directly contradicts the 2007 holding. The D.C. Circuit's decision — expected to take months or years — will determine whether *Massachusetts v. EPA* controls or whether the agency is permitted to exercise new policy discretion under *Loper Bright* or a major questions theory.

### 4.2 Grant Termination Litigation

Courts granted summary judgment for plaintiffs and set aside EPA terminations in at least three grant cases [Earthjustice 2025b]. New York AG James took action to block termination of the Environmental Justice Block Grant Program [NY AG 2025]. California AG Bonta filed a challenge to the IRA EJ grant terminations as unlawful [California AG 2025]. A D.C. Circuit appeal on IRA EJ Block Grants heard oral arguments in March 2026; a decision is pending.

### 4.3 NEPA-ICE Litigation — New EJ Community Application

The most significant Domain 49 development since June 2026 is the wave of NEPA-based litigation against ICE detention center conversions. Maryland's AG obtained a preliminary injunction blocking construction of an ICE detention facility in Washington County [Maryland AG 2026]. Salt Lake City, Salt Lake County, and community organization Uproar Utah (Refugee Justice League) are challenging ICE's proposed 13,000-person detention facility that would use 1–2 million gallons of water per day — impacting the imperiled Great Salt Lake ecosystem [KUER 2026; Bloomberg Law 2026].

This litigation wave confirms the central statutory reform argument: communities are successfully using statutory NEPA — the text of the law, not CEQ regulations — to halt federal decisions in 2026. The D.C. Circuit's *Marin Audubon* ruling invalidated CEQ regulatory authority; it did not eliminate NEPA's statutory rights. Statutory NEPA reform remains the most reliable pathway to restore community participation rights.

### 4.4 State-Level Responses

Thirteen state AGs issued joint guidance in June 2025 affirming state EJ programs operate on independent legal authority [Harvard EELP 2025c]. Eight states have cumulative impact bills: California, Connecticut, Georgia, Maryland, Minnesota, New Jersey, New York, and North Carolina [EJStatebyState.org 2025].

**New in January 2026 — NJ Appellate Division**: The New Jersey Appellate Division issued a consolidated decision on January 5, 2026 affirming NJDEP's Environmental Justice Rules against industry challenges on all grounds — statutory authority, constitutional vagueness, and arbitrary-and-capricious review [Lowenstein Sandler 2026; Mondaq 2026a]. The operative holding: "economic benefits cannot be used to allow new sources of pollution in overburdened communities." This is the first appellate court ruling affirming the constitutionality of a state cumulative impact framework in a direct industry challenge — a decisive judicial floor for the eight-state model. Critically, this victory came 10 days before CEQ NEPA regulations were rescinded federally; state courts are moving pro-EJ as federal law moves anti-EJ.

**Federal threat to state floors**: The Trump EO of April 8, 2025 directs the AG to identify and challenge state EJ laws. The Center for Progressive Reform documents that this creates a live preemption threat to the NJ ruling and analogous state laws in Colorado, Connecticut, Minnesota, New York, and Vermont [Center for Progressive Reform 2026]. The NJ Appellate Division ruling will be the primary precedent when those federal preemption challenges are filed.

## Section 5: Three Community Case Studies

### 5.1 WE ACT for Environmental Justice — Northern Manhattan

WE ACT's 2000 Title VI complaint against the Metropolitan Transportation Authority charged that the MTA had sited six of its then-eight diesel bus depots in Harlem and Washington Heights, exposing majority-minority districts to disproportionate diesel exhaust [WE ACT 2000]. The complaint exemplifies how the administrative Title VI pathway functioned as a civic participation tool after years of failed negotiation. The FTA's 2004 review found the MTA had failed to comply with required federal environmental impact analysis.

Under the current framework: the Title VI disparate impact standard has been permanently enjoined in Louisiana and challenged nationally. The OEJECR that processed such complaints is closed. EJSCREEN is removed. New York's state EJ law (S8830) partially compensates for the federal void — but only for state-regulated facilities [Columbia Sabin Center 2022; WE ACT 2022]. Federal transit authority siting decisions remain federal matters with no federal administrative forum in which community health interests are legally required to be considered.

### 5.2 Deep South Center for Environmental Justice — Cancer Alley

The Deep South Center for Environmental Justice has documented the Mississippi River Chemical Corridor where petrochemical facilities are concentrated in majority-Black communities. In St. John the Baptist Parish, children attend elementary school adjacent to the Denka Performance Elastomer facility emitting chloroprene at 8,000 times EPA's acceptable threshold. In St. James Parish, over 80% of industrial plants locate in the majority-Black districts [PRRAC 2024]. DSCEJ's advocacy relied on EPA EJ infrastructure at multiple levels: EJSCREEN data, EPA EJ coordinators for technical assistance, Title VI complaint mechanisms, and IRA EJ Block Grants.

DSCEJ's response to the Endangerment Finding rescission: "Without federal oversight, facilities will operate without greenhouse gas limits, compounding existing pollution from petrochemical operations concentrated in Black and low-income neighborhoods along the Mississippi River Chemical Corridor" [DSCEJ 2026]. This is the democratic harm: without EPA oversight, with Title VI enforcement enjoined, with NEPA community participation eliminated, Cancer Alley communities have no federal administrative forum in which their health interests are legally required to be considered.

### 5.3 Indigenous Environmental Network — Tribal Land Permitting and the DAPL Case

The CEQ NEPA regulations created a specific protection for tribal nations: a requirement that federal agencies consult with tribal governments before approving projects on or near tribal lands. This requirement is why the Standing Rock Sioux Tribe mounted a sustained legal challenge to the Dakota Access Pipeline — NEPA created the procedural hook generating both legal standing and a public comment record [Harvard EELP DAPL 2025]. The Army Corps released the final DAPL EIS on December 19, 2025; the Standing Rock Sioux Tribe's appeal continues [Harvard EELP DAPL 2025].

Interior's shift of tribal consultation procedures to an internal handbook — "can be revised without formal rulemaking" — converts the legal enforceability of tribal consultation into discretionary administrative guidance [Tribal Business News 2025]. When a regulation is in place, its violation creates a legal cause of action. When a procedure is internal guidance, its violation is a discretionary administrative matter. For the 228-organization coalition that opposed the NEPA changes, this is the core grievance: rules that gave communities formal standing to participate in decisions about their lands have been converted to suggestions.

## Section 6: The International Standard — The Aarhus Convention

The Convention on Access to Information, Public Participation in Decision-making, and Access to Justice in Environmental Matters (the Aarhus Convention) establishes three pillars of environmental democracy:

1. **Access to information**: The public's right to receive environmental information held by public authorities
2. **Public participation**: The public's right to participate in environmental decision-making before final decisions are taken
3. **Access to justice**: The public's right to challenge decisions through a court or independent body

The Aarhus Convention has 49 parties — 48 states and the European Union. The United States signed but has never ratified [UN Treaty Collection 2023]. EU member states are legally bound through EU law [CIEL 2021]. Communities in Germany, France, the Netherlands, or Poland have enforceable rights — before agencies reach final decisions — to information, participation, and judicial review. Communities in Louisiana's Cancer Alley or West Virginia's coal country have no equivalent federal procedural protections after April 2026.

These three pillars map precisely onto what EPA's infrastructure elimination dismantled: EJSCREEN's removal reduces access to information; NEPA's rescission eliminates structured public participation; OEJECR's closure and the Louisiana Title VI ruling reduce access to administrative justice. The US not only refused to adopt international environmental democracy standards — it actively dismantled the domestic approximations it had built over thirty years.

## Section 7: Reform Architecture — What Restoration Requires

### The Marin Audubon Constraint

The *Marin Audubon* ruling changes the reform architecture in a decisive way. Even a future favorable administration that reinstates CEQ NEPA regulations would face immediate legal challenge under the D.C. Circuit's holding. Executive restoration is insufficient not merely because it is politically reversible, but because the judicial pathway to restore it through CEQ rulemaking is legally foreclosed absent either en banc reversal of *Marin Audubon* or Supreme Court review on NEPA authority. Congress's 2023 NEPA amendments demonstrated that the statutory pathway is viable and has precedent [Perkins Coie 2024].

### Tier 1: Immediate Executive Actions (Next Administration)

- Reinstate OEJECR and regional EJ offices with direct-hire authority
- Restore EJSCREEN and CEJST from EPA's servers and end third-party mirror dependency
- Restore the IRA EJ Block Grant program and rescind termination notices
- Direct EPA to resume Title VI complaint intake and processing
- Issue executive order restoring climate and EJ consideration requirements

### Tier 2: Legislative Statutory Anchors (Congress — Priority)

- **Statutory NEPA amendments**: Codify Environmental Impact Statement procedural requirements — EJ considerations, Indigenous knowledge requirements, early community consultation — in statute, without relying on CEQ authority. This is the only legally adequate remedy under *Marin Audubon*'s holding.
- **EPA EJ Offices — statutory authorization**: Authorize OEJECR by statute with defined functions, staffing floors, and reporting requirements that cannot be eliminated by executive order
- **IRA EJ Block Grant entrenchment**: Clarify in statute that appropriated EJ funds cannot be impounded; establish direct disbursement mechanisms that reduce executive discretion
- **Title VI disparate impact codification**: Codify EPA's disparate impact regulations in statute to insulate them from administrative rescission and the Louisiana court challenge
- **SPEED Act opposition**: If enacted, the SPEED Act would permanently codify NEPA's elimination — eliminating the statutory reform pathway that Tier 2 relies on. Congressional advocacy against the SPEED Act is itself a Tier 2 reform priority [Earthjustice Action 2025]

### Tier 3: Long-Term International Framework

- **Aarhus Convention ratification**: Establish binding international law obligations for US environmental participation rights
- **Regional EJ compacts**: Enable interstate compacts (California, New York, Illinois as anchors) to establish regional EJ participation standards

### State Floors (Active Now)

The NJ Appellate Division ruling (January 5, 2026) provides the judicial precedent floor for state EJ law enforcement. The WE ACT–Sabin Center model EJ bills provide the legislative template for other states [Columbia Sabin Center 2023]. For EJ communities in the 42 states without cumulative impact bills — Gulf Coast, Appalachia, industrial Midwest — federal statutory restoration is the only adequate remedy; state floors are a supplement, not a substitute.

Environmental law organizations should track this litigation for potential application to EJ program restoration.

## Section 9: Advocacy Windows and Activation Triggers

**Trigger 1 — D.C. Circuit Oral Argument Scheduled**: When the D.C. Circuit schedules oral arguments in *APHA v. EPA* (No. 26-1037) or *Massachusetts v. EPA* (No. 26-1061), distribute Domain 49 immediately to Earthjustice, amicus coalition, and state AG contacts. The advocacy window before oral argument is when amicus support has maximum impact.

**Trigger 2 — SPEED Act Senate Floor Vote**: If the SPEED Act advances to Senate floor, distribute Domain 49 immediately to congressional contacts. The Act would permanently eliminate the Tier 2 statutory reform pathway.

**Trigger 3 — State EJ Law Federal Challenge Filed**: If the Trump administration files a federal challenge to a state EJ law (New York S8830, California cumulative impact provisions), distribute Domain 49 immediately to the relevant state AG and EJ coalition. The federal challenge to state EJ laws is the point at which the state floor reform architecture collapses.

**Trigger 4 — IRA EJ Grant Appeal D.C. Circuit Decision**: When the D.C. Circuit decides the IRA EJ Block Grant appeal, distribute to all grant termination-affected community organizations regardless of outcome.

**Pre-midterm voter mobilization (August–November 2026)**: The connection between environmental burden and civic disengagement documented by the Environmental Voter Project and UCS is directly actionable. Connecting environmental advocacy to electoral mobilization is not a messaging strategy; it is the mechanism through which EJ communities regain the political power to contest environmental harm.

**EJSCREEN data access**: Harvard and Public Environmental Data Partners have reconstructed EJSCREEN versions [EDGI 2025b]. Advocates should use these mirrors and note in advocacy materials that the need for third-party reconstruction is itself evidence of the infrastructure destruction this domain documents.
