# Direct Democracy Under Supermajority Attack

## How Four Simultaneous 2026 Ballot Measures Would End the Citizen Initiative as a Democratic Bypass Mechanism

**Domain M Research Brief — July 2026**

## Introduction: The Infrastructure Playbook

On November 3, 2026, voters in Missouri, North Dakota, South Dakota, and Utah will simultaneously decide ballot measures designed to permanently restrict or eliminate the citizen initiative as a tool of democratic governance. These are not isolated state policy debates. They are the most consequential front in a decade-long, coordinated campaign to dismantle the one democratic mechanism that functions when legislatures fail — and the evidence that they are coordinated is now documented in legislative databases, national advocacy reports, and the fingerprints of model legislation distributed through national networks.

The November 3 deadline is not abstract. Campaign organizations are making voter communication and coalition framing decisions right now, in the 90–120-day window before Election Day. The advocacy infrastructure that can defeat these measures — or at minimum build the legal record for the fights that follow — needs the structural argument today, not in October. This brief exists to provide that argument.

The four measures share a common logic: make it mathematically impossible for popular majorities to use ballot initiatives to override captured legislatures. Missouri accomplishes this through a geographic trap — requiring majority approval in every single congressional district drawn by a Republican legislature. North Dakota, South Dakota, and Utah accomplish the same goal through supermajority thresholds that place the bar deliberately above what popular measures actually receive at the polls. The framing varies by state. The mechanism is identical.

What makes this authoritarian infrastructure — rather than merely bad policy — is the structural context in which it operates. The states enacting these restrictions are precisely the states where gerrymandered legislatures have produced outcomes most divorced from majority preferences, and where voter suppression measures have systematically narrowed who can participate in elections. In that context, the citizen initiative is not a supplementary democratic tool. It is the last functioning bypass mechanism. Eliminating it, or raising its threshold to a level that effectively eliminates it, is not raising the bar for democracy. It is closing the last door.

## What the Supermajority Requirements Do

### Missouri Amendment 4: The Congressional District Trap

Missouri Amendment 4 is the most structurally extreme of the four measures and the most important to understand in detail, because its mechanism is not a simple threshold change — it is a geographic veto system designed to produce failure regardless of statewide sentiment.

Under Amendment 4, a citizen-initiated constitutional amendment would need to achieve majority approval in every single congressional district in addition to a simple statewide majority. Missouri has eight congressional districts. Six are currently controlled by Republicans; two reflect the urban concentration of Democratic voters in St. Louis and Kansas City. The districts are drawn by the Republican-controlled state legislature.

The arithmetic consequence is direct: any ballot measure that draws its support heavily from urban populations — which describes most progressive ballot measures in Missouri's recent history — can be blocked by the rural districts even when it wins a comfortable statewide majority. A measure winning 55% statewide, with strong margins in St. Louis, Kansas City, and the suburban I-70 corridor, can be defeated if it falls short in any one of the six Republican-drawn rural districts.

Ballotpedia's analysis is blunt: "Rejection in one district, by as few as 5% of all voters, would be enough to defeat citizen-sponsored amendments instead of requiring a majority of all votes cast statewide." [1] This means a geographically concentrated minority can veto a statewide majority. That is not a higher standard for constitutional change. It is a structural impossibility for the kinds of measures Missouri citizens have actually been passing.

Amendment 4 appears on the August 2026 primary ballot — not the November general — which creates an additional suppression effect, since primary turnout is dramatically lower than general election turnout and tilts toward the partisan base that favors the restriction. [2] The Missourians for Fair Governance campaign is running opposition, and a parallel protective measure ("Respect Missouri Voters") attempted to qualify for the same ballot.

Amendment 4 also includes additional provisions prohibiting foreign contributions to ballot campaigns and imposing criminal penalties for petition fraud — provisions already addressed in state law — appended to what is fundamentally a structural restriction on citizen initiative power. These additions allow supporters to describe the measure as an "election integrity" bill while its core function is geographic veto of popular will.

### North Dakota Constitutional Measure 2: The 60% Threshold

North Dakota Constitutional Measure 2 would raise the approval threshold for all constitutional amendments — both citizen-initiated and legislature-referred — from a simple majority to 60%. It is certified for the November 3, 2026 ballot.

The 60% threshold is a studied choice, not an arbitrary number. BISC's national landscape analysis documents that two states — Florida and Illinois — already impose 60% thresholds for constitutional amendments. The selection of 60% as the coordinated target in North Dakota, South Dakota, and Utah reflects awareness that it sits above the vote share that most popular progressive measures actually receive, while remaining low enough not to appear facially extreme to voters evaluating the threshold in the abstract.

North Dakota's Measure 2 is distinctive in that it applies to legislature-referred amendments as well as citizen-initiated ones — a design choice that provides political cover, since it appears to apply equally to both legislative and citizen processes. In practice, the legislature can simply pass policy changes as statutes rather than constitutional amendments, preserving its own freedom of action while raising the bar only for citizens seeking constitutional protection for voter-approved policies. [3]

### South Dakota Constitutional Amendment L: The Repeated Attempt

South Dakota Constitutional Amendment L would require a 60% supermajority for all constitutional amendments, covering both initiated and legislature-referred measures. It is certified for November 3, 2026.

South Dakota has tried this before. Amendment C in 2022 would have required a 60% supermajority for ballot initiatives that increase state revenue or expenditures; voters rejected it. Amendment X in 2018 was a similar attempt; it was also rejected. [4] The 2026 Amendment L is the third attempt by the South Dakota legislature to impose a supermajority threshold on citizen initiatives. The legislature is not seeking voter approval of this change because voters have approved it — it is asking again because it has failed twice and is attempting to wear down the opposition by cycling through election cycles.

The legislature-refers-to-voters mechanism is itself an exercise in asymmetric power: the legislature can refer constitutional changes to the ballot without restrictions, while this particular change, if passed, would impose restrictions that apply exclusively to future citizen initiative activity. The legislature retains the power to refer constitutional amendments at simple majority; citizens would need 60%.

### Utah: The Tax Initiative Targeted Restriction

Utah's amendment is narrower in scope but identically motivated. It would require that citizen-initiated ballot measures imposing new taxes, expanding the application of existing taxes, or increasing existing tax rates receive 60% approval to pass. [5] Legislature-referred tax changes would remain subject to simple majority.

Utah's 2018 cycle illustrates exactly why this threshold was chosen. Three citizen initiatives passed in 2018: Medicaid expansion, medical marijuana legalization, and an anti-partisan-gerrymandering measure. The Medicaid initiative passed at approximately 53%; medical marijuana passed at approximately 53% as well. Under the proposed 60% threshold, both of those measures — both of which included tax elements — would have failed. [6]

The pattern is consistent. The threshold is set precisely at the level that would have prevented the measures that already passed. This is not accidental. It is reverse-engineered from the empirical record.

## What Direct Democracy Has Actually Delivered

The abstract argument for direct democracy is less persuasive than the empirical record. In Missouri, North Dakota, South Dakota, and Utah, citizens have used ballot initiatives in the last decade to accomplish what their legislatures would never have permitted. The following measures passed through citizen initiative in states where the legislation would not have cleared the captured legislature:

**Missouri: The Full Record Since 2020**

Ballotpedia News conducted the definitive analysis: under Missouri Amendment 4's congressional district requirement, every Missouri ballot initiative since 2020 would have failed. [7] That record includes:

- **Amendment 2, Medicaid Expansion (August 2020)**: Passed statewide at 53.3%, expanding Medicaid eligibility to approximately 275,000 low-income Missourians. [8] The Republican-controlled legislature had refused to expand Medicaid for a decade after the Affordable Care Act made it available.

- **Amendment 3, Marijuana Legalization (November 2022)**: Passed statewide at 53.1%, legalizing adult-use cannabis and providing for automatic expungement of prior marijuana convictions. [9] Missouri voters enacted a policy that legislators had blocked for years.

- **Amendment 3, Right to Reproductive Freedom (November 2024)**: Passed statewide, overturning Missouri's near-total abortion ban — one of the most restrictive in the country — and establishing constitutional protection for abortion rights up to fetal viability. [10] The Republican legislature had enacted the abortion ban; citizens used the initiative process to reverse it.

- **Proposition A, Minimum Wage and Paid Sick Leave (November 2024)**: Passed at approximately 58% of the vote, raising the minimum wage to $15 per hour and establishing paid sick leave requirements for employers. [11] The legislature subsequently repealed the paid sick leave provisions and the inflation-indexing mechanism for the minimum wage — a legislative override of a direct democracy outcome. This is precisely the pattern the supermajority requirements are designed to prevent in reverse: making the citizen initiative so difficult to use that the question of legislative override never arises.

- **Sports Wagering Legalization (2024)**: Also passed as a citizen initiative, legalizing sports betting in a state where the legislature had declined to act.

All five measures would have failed under Amendment 4's congressional district requirement. Combined, they represent healthcare access for 275,000 low-income Missourians, legal cannabis and expungement for tens of thousands of Missourians with marijuana convictions, constitutional abortion rights for millions of Missouri women, a $15 minimum wage for Missouri's lowest-wage workers, and legal sports wagering. This is the empirical content of what the supermajority requirement is designed to prevent.

**South Dakota: Medicaid Expansion**

South Dakota Constitutional Amendment D, the Medicaid Expansion Initiative, passed in November 2022 at 56% of the vote — expanding Medicaid coverage to approximately 40,000 low-income South Dakotans. [12] The measure passed over the opposition of Republican Governor Kristi Noem, who had refused to expand Medicaid through executive or legislative action for years. Citizens used the initiative process to accomplish what their governor and legislature blocked.

Under proposed Amendment L's 60% threshold, South Dakota's 2022 Medicaid expansion would have failed at 56%. Forty thousand people would have lost access to healthcare coverage because their vote totals fell four points below the legislature's selected threshold.

**Utah: Medicaid, Medical Marijuana, and Anti-Gerrymandering**

Utah's 2018 citizen initiative cycle produced three landmark measures: Proposition 2 (medical marijuana), Proposition 3 (Medicaid expansion to approximately 150,000 low-income Utahns), and Proposition 4 (an independent redistricting commission to end partisan gerrymandering). All three passed. The Medicaid expansion passed at approximately 53%; medical marijuana at approximately 53%. [6] Both would have failed under the proposed 60% threshold, since both involved tax implications.

The legislature subsequently modified or repealed elements of all three initiatives — another example of the legislative override pattern. The 60% threshold amendment would have prevented citizens from raising these issues in the first place.

## The Structural Argument

The empirical record answers the "what" — here are the specific measures that would have failed. The structural argument answers the "why" — why this matters beyond the individual policy outcomes, and why these restrictions constitute a threat to democratic governance itself.

The argument has three interlocking components.

**First: Gerrymandered legislatures produce outcomes that majorities oppose.** In heavily gerrymandered states, a legislative majority or supermajority representing a minority of voters can block policy change or enact policies that majorities would not support. The National Redistricting Foundation's 2026 Policy Lab Report documented the mechanism directly: gerrymandered legislatures are undermining citizen-led ballot initiatives precisely because those initiatives exist as a structural correction to the distorted outcomes that gerrymandering produces. [13] In Missouri, the Republican legislature that designed congressional districts also blocked Medicaid expansion for a decade. In South Dakota, the legislature refused to expand Medicaid while the governor vetoed the concept outright. In Utah, the legislature repeatedly overrode or modified citizen initiative outcomes after they passed. The gerrymandered legislature and the anti-initiative campaign are not separate phenomena. They are two phases of the same project.

**Second: Voter suppression measures further narrow who participates in elections.** Across the states implementing supermajority restrictions, documented voter suppression measures — stricter ID requirements, polling place closures, voter registration restrictions, and the documented disproportionate impact of post-2020 election law changes on minority voters — have narrowed the electorate in ways that benefit the same legislative majorities promoting the supermajority requirements. When Domains 37, 49, and 51 in this research framework document the systematic narrowing of who can vote in these states, those documents are describing the same democratic erosion project of which the supermajority ballot measures are the final phase.

**Third: In this context, citizen initiative is not a supplementary mechanism — it is the bypass.** When legislatures are structurally anti-majoritarian due to gerrymandering, and when electoral access is systematically restricted to protect those majorities, the citizen initiative is the only remaining mechanism by which majority preferences can translate into law. The Redistricting Foundation analysis is explicit: "Ballot initiatives enable citizens to overcome structural impediments to true representative democracy — for example, by translating voters' preferences into policy when gerrymandered legislative districts otherwise stymie the popular will." [13] The Brennan Center reached the same conclusion in its direct democracy analysis: when legislatures become too insulated, too captured, too gerrymandered, or too frightened of their own primaries to do what majorities want, ballot initiatives function as the democratic safeguard. [14]

The supermajority requirements eliminate that safeguard in states where it is most needed. This is what distinguishes the 2026 ballot measures from ordinary policy debates about constitutional amendment procedures. These are not being enacted in states where direct democracy is a minor supplement to otherwise functional representative government. They are being enacted in states where the citizen initiative has become the primary mechanism for translating popular will into law — and where the legislature is now using a self-referral mechanism to put the question of restricting that mechanism to the voters, with the specific threshold designed to produce failure for the outcomes citizens have already approved.

## The Coordinated Nature

The four-state simultaneous attack on direct democracy in 2026 is not a coincidence of independent state policy choices. It is the 2026 front of a multi-year, coordinated national campaign.

**The legislative data.** As of April 30, 2026, the Ballot Initiative Strategy Center is monitoring 165 bills related to direct democracy filed in legislatures across 30 states and Washington D.C. [15] In 2025 alone, state lawmakers introduced or passed more than 150 bills restricting the ballot measure process — more than double the average of 35 bills per year from 2018 to 2024, and surpassing the previous high of 41 in 2024. [16] Ballotpedia News reported that state lawmakers enacted 72 bills on ballot measures and direct democracy in 2025, the highest total since at least 2017. [17]

**The 2026 ballot escalation.** In 2025, legislatures in Idaho, North Dakota, South Dakota, and Utah referred six constitutional amendments to the 2026 ballot that would change ballot measure processes — a concentration of legislative self-referral on this single issue with no precedent in recent history. [16] This is the mechanism by which the campaign advances: rather than directly restricting citizen initiatives through legislation (which could itself face citizen initiative challenge), legislatures ask voters to restrict their own initiative power, using self-referral to place the question on the ballot with legislative control over the framing.

**ALEC's model legislation.** The American Legislative Exchange Council, which produces model legislation for distribution to conservative state legislators, has documented history of opposing citizen ballot initiatives and has circulated model bills targeting the initiative process. [18] The coordinated nature of the restrictions — similar supermajority thresholds in multiple states, similar framing as "election integrity" measures, simultaneous referral to 2026 ballots — reflects the distribution of model legislation and coordinating infrastructure through networks like ALEC. BISC's March 2026 report frames this explicitly: the attacks "rarely present as anti-democratic and instead are framed using the language of election integrity, administrative efficiency, or voter protection — masking the real-world impact: suppressing participation, raising costs, increasing legal risk, and deterring grassroots organizing." [15]

**The common playbook.** The categories of attack documented by BISC are consistent across states regardless of which specific bills appear: supermajority thresholds, stricter signature requirements, shorter campaign timelines, geographic distribution requirements, single-subject rules, and confusing legal standards that increase the cost and risk of initiative campaigns. These are not independently derived responses to state-specific policy problems. They are a menu of options from a coordinated national campaign, with different states adopting different elements based on local political feasibility.

**The timing logic.** The 2026 simultaneous vote in four states reflects a specific strategic calculation: if all four measures pass on November 3, 2026, the precedent-setting impact is immediate and national. Five states (adding to Florida and Illinois, which already have 60% thresholds) would have supermajority requirements for constitutional amendments, and more states would have facing pending legislation to adopt similar rules. The coordination is designed to create a cascade — each state that adopts a supermajority threshold makes it easier for the next state to frame its own restriction as merely conforming to an emerging national standard.

The Ballotpedia News finding that rejection by as few as 5% of all voters in a single congressional district could block an amendment that 55% of all Missouri voters support is the voter communication argument. It is not about raising the bar for constitutional change. It is about giving the rural congressional districts drawn by a Republican legislature a permanent veto over the urban populations that the legislature cannot otherwise suppress.

**Common Cause state chapters** in Missouri, North Dakota, and South Dakota are positioned to route a national research brief to the campaign-level advocates. **BISC's Defend Direct Democracy coalition** coordinates 93+ nonprofits across the 26 initiative states. Fundraising framing, coalition outreach, and voter education all benefit from the synthesis document that places the four state battles in the national context of 165+ monitored bills, 30+ states, and a decade-long coordinated campaign.

Research that arrives in July arrives when the decisions are still in formation. Research that arrives in September arrives to validate plans already locked. [15] BISC is actively coordinating the Defend Direct Democracy campaign across the 93+ nonprofits in its coalition. The research brief this document provides is intended to complement BISC's coordination work by providing the integrated constitutional argument in a form directly usable by coalition partners.

**What Democracy Docket is doing.** Democracy Docket has documented the constitutional rights dimension of initiative restriction and has filed litigation on direct democracy attacks in other states. [21] Their analysis of the Callais ruling noted that "direct democracy is more crucial than ever" in contexts where representative mechanisms are failing. [22] A research brief specifically on the four November 3 ballot measures provides Democracy Docket with a synthesis document for their public education work and, where applicable, litigation framing. The bypass mechanism argument — that citizen initiatives in gerrymandered states are not merely convenient but constitutionally essential as a corrective mechanism — is the argument that connects the November 3 measures to Democracy Docket's existing litigation framework.

**What Common Cause is doing.** Common Cause's direct democracy programs operate nationally and through state chapters in Missouri, North Dakota, and South Dakota. Common Cause's voter education mandate means that a research brief becomes a voter guide source — a different adoption pathway than organizational coalition coordination, with broader community reach. [19]

**What the Brennan Center is doing.** The Brennan Center's direct democracy series — "Politicians Take Aim at Ballot Initiatives" — is the academic citation infrastructure that opposition campaigns draw on for credibility. [14] The Brennan Center's reach into academic, legal, and policy communities means that research that reaches their direct democracy program has a citation amplification trajectory: from the brief, to a Brennan Center publication, to media coverage that reaches voters who are evaluating the abstract "higher bar for constitutional change" framing that supermajority proponents use.

**The litigation record.** Even if the four November 3 measures pass, the legal record built during the advocacy window matters. Democracy Docket's litigation on Florida's 60% threshold and other direct democracy restrictions provides the framework for legal challenges. The bypass mechanism argument — that supermajority requirements in gerrymandered states violate the equal protection rights of voters whose initiative power is effectively nullified — is not a settled legal question, and the advocacy record created during the 2026 campaign season becomes evidence in subsequent litigation. Building that record now is part of the resistance pathway regardless of November 3 outcomes.

## Conclusion: The July-October Window

November 3, 2026 is not inevitable. All four measures are legislature-referred, which means voters are deciding whether to restrict their own power at the legislature's request. Voters in South Dakota rejected similar supermajority requirements in 2022 and 2018. The measures can be defeated. The question is whether the opposition campaigns have the structural framing, the empirical record, and the coalition coordination they need to make the case to persuadable voters in the July-October advocacy window.

The argument is available. Missouri voters approved Medicaid expansion, marijuana legalization, abortion rights, and minimum wage increases through citizen initiatives that every one of the proposed supermajority requirements would have blocked. South Dakota voters expanded Medicaid at 56% — four points below the 60% threshold that would now eliminate that option. Utah voters passed medical marijuana and Medicaid expansion through initiatives that the proposed threshold would have defeated. The empirical record is concrete, local, and directly connected to the lives of voters in each state.

The structural argument is clear. In states with gerrymandered legislatures and systematic voter suppression, the citizen initiative is the only remaining democratic bypass mechanism. Making it impossible is not raising the bar for democracy. It is closing the last door.

The urgency is now. Campaign organizations are making decisions in July. Coalition partners are allocating resources in July. Voter education programs are setting their 2026 curriculum in July. This brief is designed to provide the integrated argument — the synthesis that connects the four November 3 measures to the coordinated national campaign and to the empirical record of what direct democracy has delivered in each state — in a form that campaign staff, coalition partners, voter educators, and litigation teams can deploy immediately.

The November window is July through October. The work is now.
