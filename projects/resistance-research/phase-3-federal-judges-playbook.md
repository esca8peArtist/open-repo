# Phase 3 Playbook: Federal Judicial Independence Defense

**Audience**: Federal judicial bar associations, legal advocacy organizations (ACS, Federalist Society dissidents, bipartisan judicial independence coalitions), Senate Judiciary Committee minority staff, law school judicial independence centers, Administrative Office of the U.S. Courts liaison offices

**Critical constraint**: Federal judges cannot be organized like other constituencies. The Code of Conduct for United States Judges prohibits public advocacy, partisan affiliation, or coordinated political action. This playbook does not ask judges to do any of those things. The playbook addresses the external advocacy ecosystem that supports judicial independence and the structural reforms achievable through legislation and through judicial education that operates within ethical constraints.

---

## Executive Summary

The federal judiciary is under the most sustained institutional pressure since Roosevelt's court-packing threat in 1937 — and this time the tools are more varied and the threat is more diffuse. Republican senators are calling for impeachment of judges who rule against the administration; the DOJ is considering referring federal judges to Congress for impeachment after soliciting "examples of judicial activism" from U.S. attorneys; Deputy Attorney General Todd Blanche publicly described a "war" against the judiciary; in fiscal year 2025 there were 564 threats against judges, with 131 additional threats in the first three months of fiscal 2026. The Judicial Conference of the United States discussed security threats at its biannual meeting; two senior judges publicly raised the alarm. The leverage available to outside advocates is specific: the Judicial Conference's failure to issue a formal defense statement is a governance failure addressable through advocacy with the Administrative Office; the Senate Judiciary Committee minority can hold hearings on judicial threats that force Republicans to vote on record; the ABA and American College of Trial Lawyers can issue formal censure statements on the DOJ's court-packing escalation; and the law school academic community can produce the empirical record (shadow docket abuse, impeachment threat patterns, security threat data) that makes the institutional crisis legible to the public. The single most important action in the next 30 days: convene an emergency session of the Judicial Conference's security committee and push for a formal Conference statement condemning impeachment threats as constitutional intimidation.

## Ecosystem Map

**The Judicial Conference of the United States**: Policy-making body for the federal court system, comprising the Chief Justice and 26 circuit and district court judges. Meets twice annually. The Judicial Conference is the one institution that can speak with collective authority for the federal judiciary without individual judges engaging in prohibited political advocacy. Its silence on the impeachment threat campaign is the gap this playbook addresses.

**Administrative Office of the U.S. Courts**: Implements Judicial Conference policy and manages federal court operations. Contact point for advocates seeking to influence Conference agenda items (security protocols, response to impeachment threats, judicial education programming).

**Federal Judicial Center**: Educational arm of the federal judiciary. The FJC designs and delivers continuing education for federal judges. It is the legitimate channel through which substantive legal education — including analysis of *Humphrey's Executor*, *Callais*, and the SCOTUS 2026 term pipeline — reaches sitting judges. The FJC's faculty selection and curriculum development are the advocacy leverage points within the judicial system.

**Circuit Chief Judges**: Each of the 13 circuits has a Chief Judge who can convene emergency in banc procedures, issue administrative orders, and speak on behalf of the circuit. After the Boasberg and Boardman impeachment campaigns, Chief Judges in the D.C., Fourth, and Second Circuits should be the primary addressees for bar association statements of judicial independence support.

**Key cases driving the crisis**:
- Judge James Boasberg (D.D.C.) — targeted by impeachment calls after issuing orders in immigration and civil rights cases. The D.C. Circuit is the most exposed to executive branch pressure given its jurisdiction over federal agency actions.
- Judge Deborah Boardman (D. Md.) — targeted for immigration-related rulings.
- *Trump v. Slaughter* (expected June 2026) — will formalize what the shadow docket already accomplished functionally: elimination of independent agency removal protections. If the Court rules broadly, it also weakens the structural argument for judicial independence itself (independent Article III courts depend on the same "independence from political removal" principle).

**Bar associations with institutional voice**: American Bar Association (ABA) Standing Committee on Federal Judiciary. American College of Trial Lawyers. Federal Bar Association. National Bar Association. American Constitution Society (ACS). Alliance for Justice (judicial nominations).

## Strategic Opportunities

**Opportunity 1: Judicial Conference emergency statement on impeachment threats (deadline: before June Judicial Conference meeting)**

The Judicial Conference's biannual meeting is the structural opportunity. Two senior judges already raised the alarm publicly after a Judicial Conference security committee meeting. The internal conversation is happening. The external advocacy task: the ABA, Federal Bar Association, and American College of Trial Lawyers should collectively petition the Judicial Conference's Executive Committee to place a formal statement on the agenda — specifically, a statement that impeaching judges for their rulings constitutes an unconstitutional attack on the separation of powers. The statement does not require any individual judge to say anything; it is an institutional governance body speaking in its official capacity. Historical precedent: the Judicial Conference has issued statements on judicial security before. This is an extension of that established practice.

**Opportunity 2: Senate Judiciary Committee minority hearings on judicial threats**

Senate Judiciary Committee minority staff (Democratic senators on the committee) can convene hearings on judicial independence threats even without majority cooperation. The empirical record is documented: 564 threats in fiscal 2025, 131 in the first three months of fiscal 2026, DOJ soliciting "judicial activism" examples from U.S. attorneys, Todd Blanche's "war" statement. A minority-convened hearing with testimony from retired federal judges, current ABA leadership, law school constitutional scholars, and judicial security experts creates a congressional record and forces Republicans to either participate or be visibly absent. Target: hearing scheduled before September 1, 2026.

**Opportunity 3: Shadow docket empirical record — law school and bar association coordination**

The Supreme Court's shadow docket abuse is the structural mechanism that makes individual impeachment threats unnecessary: the administration can get favorable outcomes from the 6-3 Court on emergency applications without waiting for lower court rulings to develop a full record. The empirical record of shadow docket use (applications, grants, votes, reversals of lower court injunctions) is the foundation for legislative proposals to regulate the shadow docket. Georgetown Law's Institute for Constitutional Advocacy, Yale's Rule of Law Clinic, and the Brennan Center should coordinate the systematic coding project: every emergency application from January 2025 to October 2026, outcomes by category, frequency, and whether the underlying lower court ruling was ever reviewed on the merits. The resulting dataset is both a law review article and a legislative hearing exhibit.

**Opportunity 4: Judicial Ethics Enforcement Act advocacy**

The proposed Judicial Ethics Enforcement Act (bipartisan Senate sponsors) would create an independent body to investigate judicial ethics misconduct with binding referral authority, replacing the current self-regulation model under which no Supreme Court Justice has ever been sanctioned. The Thomas gift disclosures and Alito flag incidents documented the gap. After *Callais* and the coming *Slaughter* decision, both of which split 6-3 along appointment lines, the argument that the current ethics system is adequate is no longer tenable. ABA, Alliance for Justice, and ACS should coordinate a unified public statement endorsing the Act before the Senate Judiciary Committee vote. The key Republican co-sponsor relationships: Lisa Murkowski and Susan Collins have both expressed interest; their constituent-level ask is judicial accountability, not partisan politics.

## Implementation Pathway

**Weeks 1-2 (May 18-31): ABA and bar association coordination**

ABA Standing Committee on Federal Judiciary convenes an emergency session on the DOJ judicial activism referral threat and the 564/131 threat statistics. The output: a formal ABA statement condemning executive branch threats against the judiciary as an institutional attack on the separation of powers. The statement should be signed by the ABA, Federal Bar Association, American College of Trial Lawyers, and National Bar Association — bipartisan bar association consensus. This statement becomes the document that Senate Judiciary minority uses to call the hearing.

Simultaneously: the ABA's Government and Public Sector Lawyers Division drafts the Judicial Conference petition for a formal statement on impeachment threats. The petition circulates among bar association members in the week of May 18-25; it is submitted to the Administrative Office of the U.S. Courts by June 1 with a request for placement on the biannual meeting agenda.

**Weeks 3-8 (June 1-30): Slaughter response architecture and shadow docket project**

*Trump v. Slaughter* is expected in June. If the Court broadly overrules *Humphrey's Executor*, the ruling threatens not just independent agencies but the conceptual foundation of independent Article III courts. Law school clinic teams (pre-assigned per the Law Schools playbook) publish the 72-hour response. The judicial independence angle of the *Slaughter* analysis — connecting the removal protection issue to the broader principle of judicial independence — should be one of the three pre-written templates.

Georgetown ICAP, Yale Rule of Law Clinic, and Brennan Center begin the shadow docket coding project. Coordinate to avoid duplicative work: assign circuits or time periods. Target: preliminary findings published by September 1; full dataset by December 1.

**Weeks 9-16 (July-September): Senate hearing and Judicial Ethics Act push**

Senate Judiciary Committee minority calls the hearing. Witness list: two retired circuit court judges willing to testify (they are not constrained by the Judicial Code of Conduct after taking senior status or retirement); ABA president; one law school constitutional scholar; one judicial security expert from the Federal Judicial Center's security staff; one Republican former U.S. attorney willing to break with the administration's judicial targeting posture. The hearing format: factual, statistics-heavy, focused on institutional rather than partisan framing.

Judicial Ethics Enforcement Act: ACS and Alliance for Justice coordinate the letter campaign to Murkowski and Collins. The ask is specific: co-sponsor the bill and commit to a committee vote before the end of the 2026 session. The argument: judicial ethics enforcement is a rule-of-law issue, not a partisan issue. The political moment (Thomas, Alito, Boasberg threats) creates the bipartisan rationale.

**Months 4-12: Federal Judicial Center curriculum and long-term architecture**

Federal Judicial Center continuing education programming: the FJC develops curriculum on the legal issues that will come before federal courts in 2026-27 (independent agency law post-*Slaughter*, redistricting post-*Callais*, APA vacatur doctrine post-*Trump v. CASA*). Advocates with FJC faculty relationships should propose programming sessions on these topics. The FJC's independence from political pressure makes it the most defensible channel for substantive judicial education.

Long-term: SCOTUS term limits legislation (18-year staggered terms) and expanded Court size legislation are both in the legislative pipeline. The academic and institutional record built through 2026 — shadow docket empirical analysis, judicial ethics enforcement gap documentation, judicial independence threat statistics — is the foundation for the Year 3+ constitutional architecture.

## Risks and Contingencies

**Risk 1: Judicial Code of Conduct limits coordination.** Active federal judges cannot participate in advocacy, sign petitions, or publicly oppose administration actions. This is a fundamental constraint that cannot be worked around. The entire playbook operates through external advocates (bar associations, law schools, former judges on senior status or retired) rather than active judges. Any effort to involve active judges in advocacy risks compromising their ethical standing and undermining the independence argument itself.

**Risk 2: Republican bar association members resist institutional statements.** The ABA and Federal Bar Association include members across the political spectrum. Statements condemning executive branch judicial threats may be resisted by members who support the administration. Mitigation: frame the statements as institutional, not partisan. The ABA has historically taken positions on judicial independence threats regardless of which party is making them. The 1937 Roosevelt court-packing framing is available: the argument is constitutional, not partisan.

**Risk 3: Slaughter ruling weakens the independence argument structurally.** If the Court broadly overrules *Humphrey's Executor*, it may be read as endorsing a constitutional theory under which strong executive control of all subordinate officers is legitimate. The pre-written response templates must include a version that addresses this argument directly: Article III judges are independently protected by the Good Behavior Clause and are not subject to the removal analysis in *Slaughter* regardless of how broadly the Court rules.

**Risk 4: Impeachment threat escalation after hearing.** Calling a Senate Judiciary hearing on judicial threats may provoke retaliatory rhetoric from the administration and Republican senators. Mitigation: the hearing is most effective if it is framed around institutional rule-of-law concerns (564 security threats, DOJ solicitation of "activism" examples) rather than individual cases. Statistical framing is less vulnerable to "the judges are wrong" counter-attacks than case-specific framing.

## Key Contacts and Resources

**American Bar Association Standing Committee on Federal Judiciary**: [americanbar.org](https://www.americanbar.org/groups/committees/federal_judiciary/) — primary institutional voice for the federal judiciary from the bar. Convenes emergency meetings; issues formal ratings and statements.

**Federal Judicial Center**: [fjc.gov](https://www.fjc.gov/) — educational and research arm of the federal judiciary. Curriculum proposals for *Slaughter*, *Callais*, and APA doctrine update sessions should be submitted here.

**Administrative Office of the U.S. Courts**: [uscourts.gov](https://www.uscourts.gov/) — implements Judicial Conference policy. Submit the bar association petition for a formal independence statement through the Administrative Office's Public Affairs office.

**American Constitution Society**: [acslaw.org](https://www.acslaw.org/) — progressive counterpart to Federalist Society; coordinates law school chapter networks and judicial nominations advocacy. Lead organization for Judicial Ethics Enforcement Act campaign.

**Alliance for Justice**: [afj.org](https://www.afj.org/) — judicial nominations and judicial independence advocacy. Brennan Award for Public Service nominee process surfaces judges with strong independence records.

**Democracy Docket**: [democracydocket.com](https://www.democracydocket.com/) — tracks court challenges and documents judicial decisions relevant to democratic institutions. The source for the "350 of 362 cases won" statistics that document what is actually at stake.

**Just Security**: [justsecurity.org](https://www.justsecurity.org/) — rapid publication outlet for judicial independence analysis. Pre-submit the *Slaughter* templates; 72-hour publication turnaround achievable.

**Slate — "Threats Against the Judiciary" (May 2026)**: [slate.com](https://slate.com/news-and-politics/2026/05/supreme-court-threats-two-judges-trump-doj-blanche.html) — documents the current threat landscape with named judges; essential source document for hearing exhibits.

**Senate Judiciary Committee Democratic minority staff**: Contact via Sen. Dick Durbin (Ranking Member) office for hearing coordination. The minority has the authority to hold forums and briefings; framing as a formal "hearing" is aspirational pending possible chair cooperation.
