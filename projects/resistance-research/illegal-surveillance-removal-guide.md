# Illegal Surveillance Camera Removal — Citizen Action Guide

**Bottom line**: Most surveillance cameras in the US are legal in public and semi-public spaces. But several categories are definitively illegal, and citizens have real legal tools to force removal. The legal landscape is fragmented by state — Illinois and California have the strongest protections; most other states rely on the common-law "reasonable expectation of privacy" standard. Government CCTV in public spaces is the hardest to challenge; private cameras invading private spaces are the easiest.

---

## What Makes a Camera Illegal

### The Core Standard: Reasonable Expectation of Privacy

Federal law (4th Amendment and Electronic Communications Privacy Act) and state tort law share a baseline: recording is illegal when it captures people in spaces where they have a reasonable expectation of privacy. This is the deciding legal test in most cases.

**Definitively illegal everywhere:**
- Cameras in bathrooms, locker rooms, changing rooms, or bedrooms
- Cameras installed without consent in private spaces you do not own (rental properties, hotel rooms, Airbnbs)
- Cameras aimed specifically at spaces blocked from public view — a fenced backyard, enclosed patio, bedroom window visible only through a lens
- Hidden cameras placed by someone other than the property owner inside a private space
- Audio recording without consent (this is a higher bar than video — 12 states require all-party consent for audio)

**Contextually illegal (depends on state law and specifics):**
- A neighbor's camera capturing significant portions of your private backyard
- Commercial cameras with no posted notice in states requiring CCTV signage
- Employer cameras in break rooms or private offices (many states have specific protections)
- Cameras operated by government without a warrant for targeted, extended surveillance of a private residence

**Generally legal despite your discomfort:**
- Cameras capturing public streets, sidewalks, and driveways
- A neighbor's doorbell camera that incidentally captures part of your front yard
- Business security cameras covering storefronts and public-facing areas
- Government CCTV on public streets (this is the weakest area for legal challenge — see below)

## State-by-State Highlights

### California — Strongest Protections

California has the strongest baseline privacy protections in the US. The California Constitution explicitly includes a right to privacy (Article I, Section 1) that applies to private actors, not just government. California Penal Code § 647(j) prohibits "invasion of privacy" including use of any device to observe, photograph, or record another person in a private space without consent.

**Key California rules:**
- Cameras cannot be aimed at areas where individuals have a reasonable expectation of privacy, including fenced yards, private patios, and interiors visible only through magnification
- Commercial entities that use CCTV must post conspicuous notices
- Biometric data (facial geometry captured by cameras) is regulated under the California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA/CPRA) — businesses collecting facial recognition data from California residents face heightened obligations and must disclose collection, allow deletion requests, and limit use
- The California Privacy Protection Agency (cppa.ca.gov) is the enforcement authority and takes complaints

**File complaints with**: California Privacy Protection Agency (cppa.ca.gov), your local police department, or the California Attorney General (oag.ca.gov).

### Illinois — Strongest Biometric Protections

Illinois's Biometric Information Privacy Act (BIPA, 740 ILCS 14) is the most powerful biometric privacy law in the US and the only one that allows private citizens to sue directly without proving actual harm.

**Key Illinois rules:**
- Any entity collecting biometric identifiers (including facial geometry captured by cameras) must provide written notice, obtain written consent, and publish a data retention policy
- Violation penalties: $1,000 per negligent violation, $5,000 per intentional or reckless violation — per person, per violation. Class action lawsuits have produced multi-million dollar settlements
- BIPA applies to any camera system using facial recognition in Illinois — employers, retailers, landlords
- The Illinois Attorney General enforces BIPA

**File complaints with**: Illinois Attorney General (illinoisattorneygeneral.gov), or hire a private attorney for BIPA litigation (many take cases on contingency given the statutory damages).

### New York

New York City requires commercial establishments to register CCTV systems with police and post notice. New York State does not have a comprehensive biometric privacy law as of 2026, but New York City enacted the Biometric Identifier Information Law (Local Law 1399-A) requiring commercial establishments to post conspicuous notice if they collect biometric information. Several bills for statewide biometric protection have been introduced.

### Texas

Texas has the Capture or Use of Biometric Identifier Act (CUBI), which requires consent before collecting biometric data. However, CUBI is only enforceable by the Texas Attorney General, not private individuals — you cannot sue directly as you can in Illinois.

### Most Other States

Most states rely on the general tort of "intrusion upon seclusion" — you can sue someone whose camera intrudes on your reasonable expectation of privacy, but you must prove actual harm (embarrassment, damages, distress) to recover. This is harder to win than BIPA's statutory damages model. Criminal voyeurism statutes in most states also provide a law enforcement pathway when cameras are clearly placed for prurient purposes.

## Specific Scenario: Your Neighbor's Camera

This is the most common complaint. The rules:

1. **If it captures your front yard, driveway, or public-facing spaces**: Almost certainly legal. These are not private spaces.

2. **If it captures your fenced backyard or enclosed patio**: This is where you have a claim. A camera aimed specifically at a fenced yard where you have a reasonable expectation of privacy may constitute invasion of privacy under your state's tort law.

3. **If it captures your bedroom or bathroom windows**: Clearly illegal in all 50 states.

**Action steps for neighbor cameras:**

- Document the camera's position and angle with your own photos
- Talk to your neighbor directly — many disputes resolve here
- Check local ordinances: some cities have specific CCTV restrictions that go beyond state law
- File a police report if the camera is pointed at a clearly private space (bathroom, bedroom window, enclosed yard)
- Send a demand letter (or have an attorney send one) requesting adjustment or removal
- File a civil lawsuit for invasion of privacy if the camera is causing documented harm and your neighbor refuses to engage

## Specific Scenario: Government CCTV in Public Spaces

This is the hardest challenge legally. The 4th Amendment generally does not protect what you expose to public view, and courts have been reluctant to limit government cameras on public streets.

**Current legal landscape:**
- The ACLU has challenged long-term, warrantless pole-camera surveillance targeting a private residence (Moore v. United States) — arguing that sustained 24/7 monitoring of a home crosses a 4th Amendment line even if each individual observation is public. The Supreme Court has not yet resolved this.
- Cities and municipalities generally have no obligation to post notice of CCTV locations, though many do
- No federal statute restricts government CCTV in public spaces — the field is governed by local policy and constitutional litigation
- The DHS published CCTV privacy guidelines in 2007, but these are not legally binding

**Advocacy and challenge options:**
- Request records under FOIA/your state's open records law about camera locations, retention policies, and data sharing agreements
- Attend city council meetings to advocate for local CCTV policy (camera limits, mandatory notice, data retention restrictions, prohibitions on facial recognition use)
- Contact the ACLU's State Smart Justice program or your state ACLU chapter — they track government surveillance programs and may have litigation interest
- Support legislation: several states have enacted facial recognition moratoriums for government use (Massachusetts, Virginia, Portland OR) — this is the most effective policy lever

## Organizations That Handle These Issues

**National advocacy and legal organizations:**
- **ACLU** (aclu.org/issues/privacy-technology/surveillance-technologies/video-surveillance): Litigation on government surveillance, advocacy on surveillance technology policy. File a complaint via your state ACLU chapter for cases with broader public interest
- **Electronic Frontier Foundation** (eff.org): Tracks surveillance technology, publishes legal guides, has street-level atlas of surveillance technologies in US cities. Particularly active on government facial recognition
- **Electronic Privacy Information Center (EPIC)** (epic.org): Federal agency advocacy on surveillance, CCTV guidelines, biometric privacy. Submits comments to FTC and CPPA

**State enforcement:**
- California: California Privacy Protection Agency (cppa.ca.gov/enforcement/)
- Illinois: Illinois Attorney General (illinoisattorneygeneral.gov/consumer-protection)
- All states: State Attorney General consumer protection offices

**Federal:**
- FTC: ftc.gov/complaint — for corporate camera systems (security camera manufacturers, commercial CCTV companies) that mishandle or expose your footage. The FTC does not regulate the camera placements themselves but does act against companies that misrepresent privacy practices
- For government CCTV specifically: your state's ACLU chapter, EPIC, EFF

## Legal Process: Demanding Removal or Filing Complaints

### Step 1: Document Everything

Before taking any legal action:
- Photograph the camera(s) — position, angle, field of view
- Note the date, time, and your observations about what the camera is capturing
- Keep a log of any incidents (when you observed the camera, whether anyone appeared to be watching the feed)
- Save any communications with the camera owner

### Step 2: Demand Letter

A demand letter (or cease-and-desist) from you or an attorney puts the camera owner on notice and creates a paper trail. A demand letter should:
- Identify the specific camera(s) and their location
- Identify the specific privacy violation (what private space is being captured)
- Cite applicable state law (California Penal Code § 647(j), Illinois BIPA, etc.)
- Demand specific action (removal, adjustment, or deletion of footage)
- Set a reasonable deadline (14-30 days)
- State your intent to pursue legal remedies if not resolved

Many disputes resolve at this stage, especially when an attorney's letterhead signals serious intent.

### Step 3: Law Enforcement Complaint

For cameras in clearly illegal positions (bathroom, bedroom, hidden cameras in private spaces), file a police report. This is criminal conduct in most states (criminal voyeurism, stalking, wiretapping statutes). The police may not act quickly, but the report creates an official record.

### Step 4: State Agency Complaint

File with your state Attorney General (consumer protection division) or the California Privacy Protection Agency if in California. For biometric data violations in Illinois, the AG actively pursues BIPA complaints. These agencies have subpoena power you do not.

### Step 5: Civil Litigation

Options depending on your state:
- **Small claims court**: For documented damages up to your state's small claims limit (usually $5,000–$15,000). Useful if you have evidence of tangible harm
- **Civil lawsuit**: For privacy invasion torts, especially in states with statutory damages (Illinois BIPA: $1,000–$5,000 per violation). Many privacy attorneys take BIPA cases on contingency
- **Class action**: If a business or landlord is using biometric surveillance on many people without consent (common in retail or residential property contexts), class actions under BIPA have produced large settlements

## Practical Citizen Action Steps — Summary

1. **Identify what you're dealing with**: Private neighbor camera, private business camera, or government CCTV? The legal tools differ significantly.

2. **Document the camera position** with photos before doing anything else.

3. **Know your state law**: Illinois BIPA and California Privacy Constitution give you the most powerful tools. Other states rely on tort law (harder to win without proven damages).

4. **Start with direct conversation**: For neighbor cameras, this resolves most disputes.

5. **Build your own privacy**: A 6-foot fence within local ordinance requirements blocks cameras without requiring your neighbor's cooperation.

6. **Escalate systematically**: Demand letter → police report → state agency complaint → civil lawsuit.

7. **For government CCTV**: The advocacy tools are different — FOIA, city council advocacy, state legislation on facial recognition moratoriums.

8. **For biometric data violations** (facial recognition cameras): Illinois BIPA gives you the most powerful private right of action. In other states, contact your AG.
