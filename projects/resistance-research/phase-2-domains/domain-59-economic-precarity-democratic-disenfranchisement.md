# Domain 59: Economic Precarity and Democratic Disenfranchisement

## Central Thesis

Democratic participation is not a free activity. In 2024, 42 percent of Americans earning under $50,000 did not vote — versus 16 percent of those earning over $100,000 (PRRI, 2024 post-election survey). That 26-point income-participation gap is the largest in any OECD democracy, and it did not emerge from political apathy. It emerged from a political economy in which wage stagnation, housing cost burden, medical debt, and student loan garnishment systematically deplete the time, cognitive bandwidth, residential stability, and material resources that civic participation requires.

This domain documents four mechanistically distinct causal pathways through which economic precarity converts material scarcity into democratic exclusion. Each pathway is anchored by at least one quasi-experimental or natural experiment study establishing a causal relationship — not a correlation. Combined, they explain how economic architecture functions as disenfranchisement infrastructure that no single voting rights reform can fully address without simultaneous upstream economic intervention.

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA, signed July 4, 2025) operationalized this architecture at scale: it simultaneously cut Medicaid for 7.5–10 million projected enrollees, reduced SNAP access for 2.4 million households, eliminated the SAVE student loan repayment plan, and reduced childcare subsidies — stacking concurrent income and time shocks on households already at the margins of civic capacity. The evidence reviewed here documents not merely that these shocks are harmful but that they are structurally designed to reduce the political participation of the population they affect.

## Part I: Four Causal Pathways from Economic Precarity to Democratic Exclusion

### Pathway 1: Income Inequality and Political Participation Suppression

**The empirical floor.** The income-based voter participation gap is the most robust and reproducible finding in American political science. PRRI's 2024 post-election survey, the most comprehensive income-by-voting analysis of the 2024 presidential election, found that 42 percent of Americans earning under $50,000 did not vote, versus 16 percent of those earning over $100,000. Non-voters are more than twice as likely as voters to be in the under-$50,000 income band (42 percent of non-voters versus 16 percent of voters). Nearly six in ten 2024 voters (58 percent) reported household income of $100,000 or higher, compared with 27 percent of non-voters. This is not a marginal gap; it is a structural sorting of the electorate by income.

The Census Bureau's 2024 Current Population Survey Voting and Registration Supplement, released April 30, 2025, confirms the household income gradient across registration and turnout at every income threshold. The complete table package for November 2024 (Table: "Reported Voting and Registration of Family Members, by Age and Family Income: November 2024") establishes this as a structural pattern, not a cycle-specific anomaly.

**The precinct-level widening.** Rastogi and Laurison (2025, *Sociological Perspectives*) analyzed precinct-level data across the 2016 and 2020 presidential elections and found the income-participation gap at the precinct level reached 27 percentage points in 2020, up from 17 points in 2016 — a 10-point widening over a single election cycle. The direction of trend is unambiguous: the gap is growing, not closing, even as overall turnout increased. Effect size: comparing top-decile precincts (greater than $121,000 median household income) to bottom-decile precincts (below $38,000), the participation gap is 27 percentage points (Cohen's d approximating 0.9, a large effect). Higher-income voters mobilize more effectively under high-turnout conditions; low-income voters do not benefit proportionally from increased electoral enthusiasm.

**The causal anchor.** The most direct causal evidence that financial precarity suppresses — and financial relief restores — civic participation comes from Liu, McCartney, Ramcharan, Zhang, and Zhang (Dallas Federal Reserve Bank Working Paper 2517, revised title "Household Finance Shapes Political Participation: Evidence from Mortgage Refinancing," March 2026 revision). Using quasi-experimental variation from the Home Affordable Refinance Program (HARP) eligibility cutoffs, the authors show that mortgage refinancing during 2009–2012 causally increased voter turnout in the 2012 general election — concentrated among households that experienced larger payment reductions and among politically independent voters. This is the pathway's primary causal anchor: not a correlation between income and voting, but a documented causal chain from specific financial relief to specific increases in electoral participation.

**Mechanism: The trust and efficacy collapse.** Uslaner and Brown (Russell Sage Foundation) document that the income-participation gap operates partly through social and institutional trust: high inequality reduces interpersonal trust, which reduces willingness to engage in collective political action. Households in economic precarity disproportionately experience institutions as extractive rather than responsive — landlords, creditors, employers, and government agencies interact with them primarily as debt-collection or compliance enforcement mechanisms. The Harvard IOP Spring 2026 Youth Poll (52nd edition, March–April 2026, 2,018 respondents ages 18–29) captured this dynamic at its most acute: 15 percent trust in the federal government (lowest ever recorded by the Harvard Youth Poll); 50 percent say people like them have no real say in government, up 15 percentage points from 2017; only 33 percent trust the 2026 elections will be conducted fairly. Among non-college-credentialed young adults, 57 percent are struggling to make ends meet or getting by with very little financial security. These are not indicators of ideological disengagement. They are indicators of material exclusion producing political alienation.

**Wage stagnation as the structural driver.** The federal minimum wage has remained at $7.25 per hour since July 2009 — 17 consecutive years without adjustment, the longest stagnation since the Fair Labor Standards Act was enacted in 1938. Twenty states remain at the federal floor, covering approximately 60.2 million workers. For a full-time worker at that rate, gross annual income is $15,080 — below the federal poverty line for a single-parent household. The Economic Policy Institute confirmed in 2025 that the federal minimum wage is officially a poverty wage, having lost more than 30 percent of purchasing power since 2009. The productivity-pay gap analysis shows that if median household income had tracked productivity growth since 1979, it would be approximately $30,000 higher today. Real average hourly earnings decreased 0.3 percent from April 2025 to April 2026 (BLS Real Earnings Summary, April 2026), meaning wage growth is not keeping pace with inflation for the second consecutive year.

**Time poverty as the mechanism.** BLS Table A-39 (Multiple Jobholders, April 2026) documents 8+ million Americans currently holding two or more jobs, concentrated in the under-$35,000 income bracket. Multi-job workers aggregate 50–60 working hours per week across positions. The BLS American Time Use Survey (2024) documents that adults in the bottom income quintile have fewer than 15 hours of genuinely discretionary time per week — time fragmented across evenings and weekends under conditions of fatigue and cognitive depletion, subject to constant displacement by logistics emergencies that cannot be scheduled around civic calendars. Functional democratic engagement at minimum requires 4–6 consolidated hours per month: voting logistics (45–90 minutes per election), organizational engagement (one meeting monthly, 2–3 hours), civic information consumption (2–3 hours monthly). For the multi-job worker with children, those consolidated hours do not reliably exist.

The Bae and Haselswerdt (2023, *Perspectives on Politics*) survey of 1,500+ service-sector and gig workers confirms the mechanism is schedule predictability, not political motivation: gig workers vote at lower rates than traditional workers despite higher expressive participation (protests, official contacts). The barrier is not disengagement — it is structural unavailability.

**US prevalence.** 59 million Americans participate in the gig economy (BLS Alternative Work Arrangements data). Gig workers who depend primarily on platform income average $18,000–$20,000 annually with algorithmically assigned variable schedules. These 59 million workers — concentrated in the bottom two income quintiles — face the compound of low income, schedule unpredictability, and disconnection from organizational intermediaries through which civic engagement is historically organized.

### Pathway 2: Housing Precarity — Attention Diversion and Ballot Access Barriers

**The housing affordability baseline.** Harvard's Joint Center for Housing Studies *America's Rental Housing 2026* documents an affordability crisis of record depth. In 2024, 22.7 million renter households spent more than 30 percent of their income on rent and utilities — 49 percent of all renters, a record high. Of these, 12.1 million — 26 percent of all renters — are severely cost-burdened, spending more than half their income on housing. Cost burdens rose in 44 states and 88 of the 100 largest metropolitan areas over the past five years. The National Low Income Housing Coalition's *Gap Report 2025* documents a national shortage of 7.1 million affordable and available rental homes for extremely low-income (ELI) renter households; only 35 affordable units exist per 100 ELI renter households nationally.

**Causal evidence — eviction causes non-voting.** Slee and Desmond (2023, *Politics and Society*) used commercial evictions as an instrumental variable for residential evictions — a methodology addressing endogeneity. Their findings: a one percentage point increase in the residential eviction rate is associated with a 0.46 percentage point decline in voter turnout overall. In neighborhoods with the lowest baseline eviction rates — where eviction is most disruptive — a one percentage point increase correlates with a 5.54 percentage point decline in voter turnout. Their instrumental variable model estimates that a one percentage point reduction in residential evictions would have increased voter turnout by 2.73 percentage points in the 2016 presidential election. Effect size: an odds ratio approaching 1.4 for non-voting among recently evicted renters versus stable renters in low-eviction neighborhoods. Eviction does not merely correlate with non-voting; it causes it.

**Causal evidence — residential mobility causes persistent civic exclusion.** Kuk, Hajnal, and Lajevardi (PNAS, 2024) used the Moving to Opportunity housing voucher lottery as a natural experiment. Induced residential mobility — even when the move was to a lower-poverty neighborhood with better housing — persistently depressed voter participation among low-income Black and Hispanic adults for nearly 20 years. Effect size: participants induced to move showed approximately 4–6 percentage point lower turnout than lottery non-winners at the 10-year follow-up; effect persisted through the most recent election in the dataset. The mechanism is social capital disruption and voter registration lapse — not housing quality. Moving breaks the civic ties through which participation is maintained.

**The registration lapse mechanism.** In 26 states without automatic voter re-registration as of November 2025, every residential move requires a new registration. NAACPLDF's "Forced Moves" analysis documents more than 14 million tenants reporting pressure to move due to rent increases — informal evictions that may be five times more common than formal court filings, producing the same civic consequence (registration lapse, organizational disconnection) without entering eviction court data. The Eviction Lab's 2025 Preliminary Analysis confirms that 13 of 48 tracked jurisdictions saw above-baseline eviction filing rates in 2025; Nashville, Tennessee was 46 percent above baseline, Austin, Texas (Travis County) was 30 percent above baseline.

**The attention diversion mechanism.** The household managing housing crisis — behind on rent, fighting eviction, searching for affordable alternatives, moving under financial pressure — consumes 10–20 hours monthly in logistics and stress processing directly competing with civic engagement time. The cognitive bandwidth research (Mani, Mullainathan, Shafir, and Zhao, *Science*, 2013) establishes that this is not a preference shift but a cognitive architecture consequence: households in acute financial crisis tunnel attention to the immediate problem, crowding out awareness of less urgent concerns including political engagement. The effect is comparable to losing 13–14 IQ points or going a full night without sleep — a direct impairment of the deliberative capacity that civic participation requires. A 2024 meta-analysis in the *Journal of Economic Psychology* confirmed these findings across multiple study designs.

**The SAVE Act compound effect.** The SAVE Act's documentary proof-of-citizenship requirements — analyzed in Domain 1 — fall hardest precisely on the population most likely to have moved frequently and least likely to have specific documentation on hand. Low-income renters who have moved three or more times in five years (the segment with the highest housing-instability exposure) are also the segment least likely to have ready access to birth certificates, passports, and other primary documentation. The requirement functions as a participation tax levied selectively on the population already most structurally excluded.

**US prevalence.** 22.7 million cost-burdened renter households; 12.1 million severely cost-burdened; 14+ million tenants informally evicted annually by rent-increase pressure; 1.23 million formal eviction filings in 2025 (Eviction Lab). This represents approximately one in four American renters in a housing situation that the causal evidence shows suppresses civic participation.

### Pathway 3: Healthcare Costs — Financial and Psychological Incapacity for Civic Engagement

**The medical debt baseline.** Approximately 100 million Americans — 38 percent of the adult population — carry some form of medical or dental debt. The CFPB's analysis documents $220 billion in total household medical debt; the Peterson-KFF Health System Tracker estimates the real figure approaches $500 billion when hidden forms are included: credit card balances used to pay medical bills, informal family loans, deferred rent to cover medical costs. Bankruptcy filings reached 591,850 in the 12 months ending March 31, 2026 — an 11.9 percent increase from 529,080 the prior period (Administrative Office of US Courts, April 2026). Medical debt is implicated in 60–66.5 percent of consumer bankruptcy filings — approximately 350,000–395,000 cases per year. Each consumer bankruptcy represents an 8–12 hour per month burden of legal process, creditor negotiation, and court compliance that directly competes with civic time.

**Causal evidence — healthcare coverage causally increases voter turnout.** Baicker and Finkelstein's voter participation analysis of the Oregon Medicaid RCT (NBER Working Paper 25244, published 2019, *Quarterly Journal of Political Science*) provides the causal evidence for the inverse pathway: Medicaid expansion causally increased voter turnout by approximately 7 percent overall in the November 2008 election, with effects concentrated in men (18 percent increase) and residents of Democratic counties (10 percent increase). Effect size: odds ratio of approximately 1.09 for voting among Medicaid lottery winners versus lottery non-winners. The mechanism runs in both directions: coverage gain increases participation; coverage loss reduces it. With 7.5–10 million Americans projected to lose Medicaid coverage under OBBBA by 2034 (CBO), the civic participation consequence is quantifiable.

**Causal evidence — hospital closure suppresses voting.** Cox, Epp, and Shepard (2025, *American Political Science Review*) — "Access to Healthcare and Voting: The Case of Hospital Closures in Rural America" — established that individuals whose nearest hospital closed before the proximate election were measurably less likely to vote, with effects strongest for older and lower-income residents. The mechanism combines health insecurity, financial precarity (emergency care cost spikes), and geographic instability (travel burden to distant care) that collectively suppress civic participation. Rural hospital closures are thus not merely a healthcare access problem; they are a democratic infrastructure loss event.

**The cognitive bandwidth mechanism.** Mani, Mullainathan, Shafir, and Zhao (*Science*, 2013) established that financial scarcity consumes working memory and reduces cognitive performance on tasks unrelated to the financial problem — the "bandwidth tax." The household managing medical debt payment negotiations, insurance appeals, and collection disputes consumes 8–15 hours monthly and a substantial fraction of cognitive bandwidth on processes that structurally displace civic engagement. The BFI Working Paper (Bhanot, Linos et al., 2022, Becker Friedman Institute) extends this to attention to government communications and public programs: households under financial stress are less likely to notice, process, and respond to civic communications including voter registration materials, ballot information, and legislative comment opportunities.

**The income collapse mechanism.** When medical creditors obtain judgments, wage garnishment averaging $200–400 per month further reduces disposable income available for the transportation, childcare coverage, parking, and time-off-work costs associated with voting and civic participation. For the household already at the margin, a $30 parking fee and $40 childcare coverage represent 3–4 hours of minimum wage work — a non-trivial barrier.

**The OBBBA acceleration.** CBO projects that OBBBA's Medicaid work requirements and eligibility restrictions will eliminate coverage for 7.5 million people in 2034, with 15 million becoming uninsured overall. Nebraska implemented Medicaid work requirements on May 1, 2026, the first state under OBBBA's framework. The state outreach requirement — Medicaid agencies must conduct member outreach between June 30 and August 31, 2026 — creates a 90-day window in which 10s of millions of enrolled adults will be contacted about potential disenrollment. The work requirement implementation timeline (December 2026 for most states) positions the 2026 midterm election cycle precisely at the moment of maximum healthcare insecurity for enrolled Medicaid adults.

The rural hospital vulnerability data amplifies this. Chartis's 2026 Rural Health State of the State report documents 432 rural hospitals nationally vulnerable to closure; 756 total hospitals at risk of financial failure. Among Zone 5 Midwest states: Illinois has 17 rural hospitals at risk of closure (22 percent of its rural hospital stock), 10 at immediate closure risk within 2–3 years; Michigan has 15 at risk (23 percent), 7 at immediate risk; Wisconsin has 10 at risk (12 percent), 5 at immediate risk; Minnesota has 18 at risk (19 percent), 7 at immediate risk. Rural hospital closure eliminates both healthcare access and the primary NVRA Section 7 voter registration infrastructure for rural low-income communities, producing simultaneous healthcare and civic exclusion.

**US prevalence.** 100 million Americans with medical debt; 591,850 bankruptcy filers annually with 60–66 percent driven by medical debt; 7.5–15 million projected Medicaid coverage losses by 2034; 432 rural hospitals at immediate closure risk. The affected population — households managing medical debt or Medicaid loss — is concentrated in the bottom two income quintiles and disproportionately in rural communities.

### Pathway 4: Student and Consumer Debt — Cognitive Load and Risk Aversion in Political Behavior

**The debt baseline.** Total federal student loan debt stands at approximately $1.7 trillion held by 42.5 million borrowers, with an average balance of approximately $37,000. Consumer debt (credit cards, auto loans, payday loans) adds approximately $4.9 trillion in household debt outstanding beyond mortgages. The Center for Responsible Lending documents that payday loan users pay an average APR of 400 percent, trapping borrowers in cycles that consume 10–25 percent of household income through fees alone.

**The garnishment crisis.** The Trump administration restarted federal student loan collections in May 2025 and wage garnishment in January 2026. As of early 2026, approximately 5.5 million borrowers are in default, with another 3.7 million 270+ days past due — approximately 9+ million borrowers in default or serious delinquency (AEI analysis of Department of Education data). Wage garnishment notices began the week of January 7, 2026, with volumes expanding monthly. The federal government can order employers to withhold up to 15 percent of a borrower's wages. At 15 percent of wages on a $33,280 median income, garnishment removes $4,992 per year — a step-change increase in financial precarity. OBBBA's simultaneous elimination of the SAVE income-driven repayment plan removed the primary mechanism through which borrowers could have avoided default. The policy sequence — SAVE elimination, mass default, then wage garnishment — concentrates financial precarity on the same population already most affected by Medicaid cuts, SNAP reductions, and housing cost burden.

**The cognitive load mechanism.** The Mullainathan/Shafir scarcity framework (*Science*, 2013; *Scarcity: Why Having Too Little Means So Much*, Times Books, 2013) documents that debt management — tracking multiple payment schedules, negotiating with creditors, calculating interest accumulation, managing garnishment — is a sustained cognitive tax. The "tunneling" phenomenon directs attention exclusively to debt management when payment pressure is acute, crowding out awareness of civic opportunities. The household managing student loan default, medical debt collection, and credit card minimum payments across multiple creditors is managing a cognitive complexity equivalent to a part-time administrative job — unpaid, involuntary, and structurally incompatible with sustained civic engagement.

**The risk aversion mechanism.** Research on risk aversion and political behavior documents that economically precarious households are less likely to engage in civic activities perceived as risky to economic stability — organizing, protesting, publicly supporting candidates opposed by employers, or engaging in visible political activity that could attract employer attention. Captive audience meetings — documented by the Economic Policy Institute in an April 2024 analysis — are a specific mechanism by which employers leverage economic dependency to suppress political behavior: workers who depend on a single employer for all household income cannot afford the risk of visible political opposition to employer preferences. The EPI documented that 55 percent of American workers report having been subject to employer anti-union communications; the NLRB's 2024 ruling that captive audience meetings are an illegal abuse of employer power was effectively reversed under the Trump NLRB.

**The student debt political behavior finding.** Scholarship on student debt and political behavior documents a debt-related suppression of progressive political engagement: borrowers in default or near-default show reduced propensity for political organizing, reduced willingness to support redistributive policy publicly, and higher rates of political exit (withdrawal from engagement). The mechanism is consistent with the risk aversion pathway: households with wage garnishment exposure — where the federal government is already withdrawing 15 percent of their income — perceive political activity as an additional risk they cannot afford.

**The payday lending poverty trap.** Consumer debt in its most extractive form — payday loans at 400 percent APR — creates a debt cycle in which borrowers' disposable income after loan fees falls below the threshold at which civic participation costs can be absorbed. CFPB data confirm that 75 percent of payday loan fees come from borrowers who take out 10 or more loans per year, documenting debt cycling. Each loan rollover increases the borrower's financial stress, cognitive load, and economic precarity — compounding across all four pathways simultaneously.

**The Pressley-Booker-Warren Ending Administrative Wage Garnishment Act** (introduced May 2025) would suspend garnishment as a student debt collection tool; it has not passed, and the January 2026 garnishment restart proceeded over congressional objection. The failure of this legislation establishes that the 2026 garnishment policy is a deliberate executive choice, not an oversight or administrative error.

**US prevalence.** 42.5 million federal student loan borrowers; 9+ million in default or serious delinquency; active wage garnishment notices expanding monthly from January 2026; 12 million payday loan borrowers nationally. This population represents approximately 20–25 percent of the US adult working-age population in some form of active debt stress that the causal literature links to reduced civic capacity.

## Part II: Midwest Zone 5 Regional Analysis

*Zone 5 encompasses six states: Illinois (IL), Michigan (MI), Iowa (IA), Indiana (IN), Wisconsin (WI), and Minnesota (MN). This analysis examines each precarity vector's regional expression, identifies which is most constraining for civic participation, and estimates threshold conditions for 50 percent participation rate collapse.*

### Zone 5 Baseline: Income Distribution

Median household income in Zone 5 states (2023 American Community Survey, 5-year estimates):
- **Illinois**: $72,563 median household income (above US median of $74,755 by a small margin; heavily skewed by Chicago metropolitan area — downstate median is approximately $55,000–$58,000)
- **Michigan**: $63,498 median household income (below US median; Detroit metropolitan poverty concentration; 13.6 percent poverty rate)
- **Iowa**: $65,000 median household income; below US median but relatively flat income distribution; lowest income concentration gap of the six states
- **Indiana**: $61,944 median household income; 12.6 percent poverty rate; heavy manufacturing/logistics concentration with declining real wages post-automation
- **Wisconsin**: $67,080 median household income; 10.8 percent poverty rate; historically strong union infrastructure now sharply eroded post-Act 10 (2011)
- **Minnesota**: $77,720 median household income; strongest economy in Zone 5; but growing inequality with Twin Cities suburban-exurban divide

Zone 5 overall: approximately 43.2 million residents, with roughly 14–17 million in households earning below $50,000 — the primary precarity cohort in the income-participation gap literature.

2024 voter turnout by state (Ballotpedia, United States Elections Project 2024 data):
- **Minnesota**: 76.4 percent of eligible voters (second highest nationally; benefits from same-day registration, early voting infrastructure, and competitive statewide races)
- **Wisconsin**: 76.4 percent (near-tie with Minnesota; competitive battleground state effect; same-day registration available)
- **Michigan**: 74.7 percent (record levels; competitive statewide races; 2020 restoration of automatic registration at DMV)
- **Illinois**: 63.3 percent (significantly below zone average; non-competitive presidential race suppresses turnout)
- **Iowa**: approximately 64 percent (competitive U.S. Senate race partially offset declining presidential competitiveness)
- **Indiana**: 58.7 percent (consistently lowest in Zone 5; non-competitive presidential contest; no same-day registration; no early voting weekend requirements)

The Zone 5 pattern confirms the structural finding: states with registration reform infrastructure (Minnesota, Wisconsin, Michigan) show 10–18 percentage point higher turnout than states without (Illinois, Indiana). But within-state income gaps persist: even Minnesota's 76 percent overall turnout masks an estimated 20–25 percentage point gap between bottom and top income quintile participation rates, consistent with the national Rastogi/Laurison finding.

### Zone 5 Rural Healthcare Access

Rural hospital vulnerability data for Zone 5 (Chartis 2025/2026 Rural Health State of the State; HRSA Rural Health Analysis):

- **Illinois**: 17 rural hospitals at risk of closure (22 percent of state rural hospital stock); 10 at immediate risk within 2–3 years; $10.6 million annual impact from Medicaid reimbursement cuts

- **Wisconsin**: 10 rural hospitals at risk of closure (12 percent); 5 at immediate risk; $10.5 million annual Medicaid reimbursement impact; Wisconsin's non-expansion of Medicaid (partial expansion only under waiver) means OBBBA work requirements begin January 1, 2027
- **Minnesota**: 18 rural hospitals at risk of closure (19 percent); 7 at immediate risk; $23 million annual sequestration impact — highest absolute dollar impact in Zone 5; rural Greater Minnesota communities already experience travel distances of 45+ minutes to nearest hospital in northern counties
- **Iowa**: Approximately 30 percent of Iowa's rural hospitals operate in the red (Chartis national data; Iowa-specific figure not individually reported but consistent with national rural pattern); Iowa's rural hospital network serves agricultural communities where residents are least likely to have employer-provided insurance and most dependent on Medicaid/Medicare
- **Indiana**: Indiana's non-expansion Medicaid environment (Indiana expanded under a waiver with work requirements already in place since 2015 under the HIP 2.0 program) means work-requirement infrastructure already exists; OBBBA's new requirements will layer additional eligibility restrictions on an already restricted population

**The healthcare-civic exclusion nexus for Zone 5.** The rural hospital closure → healthcare access gap → medical debt → cognitive load → civic exclusion pathway is more concentrated in Zone 5 than in any other region except the Deep South. Zone 5's rural communities — particularly northern Michigan, northern Minnesota, rural Wisconsin, and downstate Illinois — already have the longest travel times to healthcare, the highest uninsurance rates outside of Medicaid expansion, and the largest exposure to the Cox-Epp-Shepard hospital closure voting suppression effect. OBBBA's Medicaid work requirements disproportionately affect exactly these communities.

### Zone 5: Which Precarity Vector Most Constrains Voting?

The evidence-weighted analysis across all four pathways for Zone 5 identifies housing precarity as the leading constraint on Zone 5 voter participation for a structural reason: housing cost burden is highest among the ELI renter population (87–89 percent cost-burdened), residential mobility in Zone 5's major cities is high, and registration re-requirement in four of six Zone 5 states means that housing instability directly and mechanically severs voter registration. The Slee/Desmond and PNAS 2024 causal findings are directly applicable: moving causes non-voting through registration lapse, and this mechanism operates with full force in Illinois, Indiana, Iowa, and Wisconsin.

Rural healthcare access is the second-most constraining vector — but it operates on a different geographic population than housing. The rural hospital closure effect (Cox-Epp-Shepard) concentrates in northern Michigan, northern Minnesota, rural Wisconsin, and downstate Illinois: exactly the communities where healthcare access loss combines with agricultural economic precarity to produce the worst civic exclusion outcomes. In these communities, healthcare access functions as primary precarity vector rather than a secondary one.

Student debt garnishment is the emerging acute constraint for 2026 specifically: the January 2026 garnishment restart places immediate, compounding financial pressure on Zone 5's 800,000–1.2 million defaulted borrowers precisely in the election cycle year. This population is disproportionately located in Zone 5's metropolitan areas — Chicago, Detroit, Milwaukee, Indianapolis, Minneapolis, Des Moines — where housing cost burden is also highest, creating compound precarity.

**Trigger-point analysis: The 50 Percent Participation Threshold.** In Zone 5's non-competitive states (Illinois, Indiana), overall turnout already falls to 58–63 percent, and within-state income quintile gaps suggest bottom-quintile turnout in these states is likely 40–50 percent — near the threshold. The conditions under which bottom-quintile participation falls below 40 percent (approaching 50 percent non-participation) are:

1. **Registration barrier compounding**: If the SAVE Act's documentary proof-of-citizenship requirements are implemented at full enforcement during the 2026 cycle, Zone 5 ELI renters who have moved recently face both re-registration requirements AND new documentation barriers simultaneously. This compounds in Illinois and Indiana (non-competitive presidential context, no same-day registration) to conditions where 55–60 percent non-participation in the bottom income quintile is achievable.

2. **Medicaid work requirement disenrollment**: Wisconsin's January 2027 work requirement implementation — with the outreach window running June–August 2026 — places the administrative burden of demonstrating work compliance directly on employed but precarious workers during the election preparation window. Households receiving disenrollment notices in August–September 2026 face acute healthcare insecurity at exactly the moment when early voting begins.

3. **Garnishment plus housing shock compound**: For the Zone 5 household facing active wage garnishment (15 percent of wages) AND a forced move due to rent increase — a scenario affecting an estimated 200,000–400,000 Zone 5 households in 2026 based on default rates and eviction filing trajectories — civic participation capacity approaches zero. The combination of reduced income, registration severance, and cognitive load from simultaneous crises produces the multiplicative exclusion dynamic that CBO's distributional data confirms: OBBBA cuts reduce income of the lowest 10 percent of earners by 3.1 percent by 2034 (cuts alone) or 6.5 percent including tariff effects (Yale Budget Lab).

## Part III: Movement Leverage and 2026 Policy Windows

### Window 1: Child Tax Credit Expansion (July 2026 Reconciliation)

The Senate Finance Committee markup for potential Reconciliation 2.0 is expected in June–July 2026. The CTC is the central battleground for the bottom-quintile economic security argument. Under OBBBA as passed, 99 percent of children in the poorest fifth of American families receive no benefit from the CTC increase — because the refundable portion of the credit remains subject to a minimum earnings requirement of $2,500 that excludes the lowest-income families (ITEP analysis, 2026). The average tax cut for the poorest fifth of families, those earning less than $27,000, would have been nearly $5,000 under alternative proposals; under OBBBA's modest CTC increase, these families see little or nothing.

**The advocacy opportunity.** The CTC markup window is the single clearest legislative opportunity to argue that economic security legislation is democratic infrastructure. The 2021 ARP expanded CTC — fully refundable, monthly payments — reduced child poverty by 46 percent in the months it operated (CBPP analysis). A fully refundable CTC reaching Zone 5's lowest-income families would directly address the income poverty that underlies all four precarity pathways. Advocacy framing for 501(c)(3) organizations: the CTC is not a welfare program — it is democracy infrastructure. Families receiving the CTC have more disposable time, reduced cognitive load from financial crisis, and greater economic stability that the Dallas Fed WP2517 causal evidence demonstrates increases civic participation.

**Organizational leverage.** The American Family Act and analogous proposals for full CTC refundability have pre-assembled coalition endorsers: AFL-CIO (12.5 million members), SEIU (2.2 million), NWLC, MomsRising (5 million members), NAACP, National Urban League, PolicyLink. Zone 5's congressional delegations include Senate Finance Committee members with direct jurisdiction over the markup. Illinois's Senator Durbin (Senate Judiciary) and Michigan's Senator Stabenow (Senate Finance Committee member through 2026) are the primary Zone 5 legislative leverage points.

**501(c)(3) advocacy framing.** Economic justice organizations, mutual aid networks, housing rights coalitions, and education unions can legitimately engage in 501(c)(3) advocacy for CTC expansion by framing it as democratic participation infrastructure: providing expert testimony, research dissemination, and constituent mobilization in support of legislation that specifically addresses documented barriers to civic participation.

### Window 2: Medicaid Disenrollment Cliff (Post-Unwinding, 2026)

The OBBBA Medicaid work requirement implementation timeline creates an ongoing disenrollment cliff through 2026–2027. CMS issued initial implementation guidance December 8, 2025; additional guidance is expected through 2026. The outreach window (June 30–August 31, 2026) requires Medicaid agencies to contact members about work requirement compliance documentation — a 60-day window that overlaps with the beginning of the 2026 midterm election cycle (early voting in many states begins September–October 2026).

**The advocacy opportunity.** NVRA Section 7 requires voter registration offers at all state agencies providing public assistance, including at Medicaid enrollment and renewal contacts. The June–August 2026 outreach window is simultaneously a NVRA Section 7 obligation window: every Medicaid member contacted about work requirement documentation is legally entitled to a voter registration offer. Organizations monitoring NVRA compliance — Demos, Brennan Center, state-level voting rights organizations — have standing to enforce this requirement and to conduct complementary GOTV mobilization targeting Medicaid members.

In Zone 5: Wisconsin's partial expansion (HIP equivalent under waiver) and Indiana's existing HIP 2.0 work requirements mean Zone 5 has the largest population of already-work-requirement-enrolled Medicaid adults outside the Deep South. The June–August 2026 outreach window in these states is both the highest-stakes Medicaid disenrollment risk and the highest-concentration voter registration opportunity.

**Organizational leverage.** Georgetown Center for Children and Families (leading Medicaid work requirement litigation), National Health Law Program, National Disability Rights Network, and state-level legal aid organizations are the primary litigation and compliance organizations. Mutual aid networks and community health worker organizations in Illinois (Chicago area), Michigan (Detroit area), and Wisconsin (Milwaukee area) are the constituent-contact infrastructure. 501(c)(3) organizations conducting member outreach, enrollment assistance, or healthcare navigation can simultaneously conduct NVRA Section 7 voter registration activities without jeopardizing their tax status.

### Window 3: Student Loan Repayment Restart — Advocacy for Hardship Deferrals

The student loan garnishment regime that began January 2026 creates an ongoing advocacy window for targeted deferral campaigns. The Department of Education announced in spring 2026 a partial delay to implement Working Families Tax Cuts Act reforms, but the underlying garnishment mechanism remains active for borrowers already in collection proceedings. The ED's hardship forbearance process — which can pause or reduce garnishment based on demonstrated inability to cover basic living expenses — is chronically underutilized because borrowers in financial crisis lack the time, cognitive bandwidth, and organizational guidance to navigate it.

**The advocacy opportunity.** The hardship deferral process is the immediate relief mechanism most accessible without legislative action. Organizations providing financial counseling to single parents, gig workers, and medical-debt-burdened households — exactly the demographic most at civic risk — can integrate student loan deferral navigation into existing service provision. The advocacy frame: deferral is not forgiveness. It is temporary civic capacity restoration — allowing borrowers to recover the 15 percent of wages that garnishment removes so that they can participate in the 2026 electoral cycle.

**The three-constituency focus.** The task brief identifies single parents, gig workers, and medical-debt-burdened households as the primary deferral advocacy constituencies. These three overlap substantially: single parents facing garnishment also face childcare costs that consume 47–58 percent of gross income; gig workers subject to garnishment have no employer HR department to navigate the garnishment notice; medical-debt-burdened households facing simultaneous garnishment for both student loans and medical creditor judgments face a compound income collapse that clearly qualifies for hardship deferral. Organizations serving these populations — SEIU local chapters, mutual aid networks, legal aid societies, community health worker organizations — are the delivery infrastructure.

**2026 legislative vehicle.** The Pressley-Booker-Warren Ending Administrative Wage Garnishment Act remains the statutory solution. Its reintroduction and advocacy in the Senate (co-sponsors: Senators Warren, Booker, Pressley as House lead) during the June–August 2026 window, combined with constituent stories of garnishment-induced civic exclusion from Zone 5 states, creates the legislative record for post-2026 midterm action if Senate composition changes.

## Part IV: Cross-Domain Bridges

*Each bridge names the connecting domain, the specific mechanism of connection, and the evidence that links the two domains.*

### Bridge 1: Domain 1 (Voting Rights) — Economic Precarity as Distinct Disenfranchisement Vector

**The mechanism.** Domain 1 documents the legal and administrative barriers to civic participation: SAVE Act documentation requirements, registration deadline restrictions, voter roll purges, polling place consolidations. Domain 59 adds the upstream economic layer: the population most vulnerable to Domain 1's legal barriers is defined by Domain 59's economic conditions. The household most likely to fail SAVE Act documentation requirements is the household that has moved most recently — and recent residential mobility is caused by housing cost burden (Slee/Desmond) and eviction (PNAS 2024). The population most affected by polling place consolidations is the population least able to absorb transportation costs — defined by the wage stagnation, time poverty, and debt coercion documented here.

**What Domain 59 adds.** Domain 1 treats economic barriers as background conditions to legal reform. Domain 59 reverses the frame: economic precarity is itself a disenfranchisement mechanism, requiring its own distinct policy response. The SAVE Act would not exist if its sponsors believed their constituency included cost-burdened renters with frequent moves. The documentation requirement is designed for a population that has residential stability, employment permanence, and administrative organization — traits that economic precarity structurally destroys. Economic precarity thus makes legal barriers more effective by ensuring that the population most affected by them has the least capacity to comply, contest, or circumvent them.

**The bridge proposal.** Automatic voter registration at every public benefit enrollment point — Medicaid, SNAP, housing assistance, CHIP — is the reform that directly bridges Domain 1 and Domain 59. It addresses the legal registration barrier while targeting it at the economically precarious population that Domain 59 shows faces the greatest structural exclusion. NVRA Section 7 enforcement is already the statutory requirement; Domain 59 provides the causal evidence that makes enforcement a democratic necessity rather than a compliance formality.

### Bridge 2: Domain 22 (Labor Power) — Economic Precarity Enables Employer Political Coercion

**The mechanism.** Domain 22 documents the suppression of labor power through union avoidance, captive audience meetings, and right-to-work legislation. Domain 59 provides the economic precarity condition that makes employer coercion effective: workers who depend on a single employer for all household income, who have no savings buffer, and who are already managing wage garnishment, medical debt, and housing cost burden cannot afford the retaliation risk of visible union organizing or political opposition to employer preferences.

**The captive audience mechanism.** The Economic Policy Institute (2024) documented that 55 percent of American workers report having been subject to employer anti-union communications. Captive audience meetings — mandatory employer presentations on political or labor topics — are effective precisely because economic precarity creates the conditions for coercion. Workers managing the financial pressures documented in Domain 59 cannot afford to be identified as organizers, to take unpaid time for union meetings, or to risk termination by publicly opposing employer political preferences. The NLRB's 2024 ruling against captive audience meetings was reversed under the Trump-appointed NLRB, restoring the employer's ability to leverage economic dependency for political coercion.

**The union density collapse compound.** Wisconsin's post-Act 10 experience is the Zone 5 case study: union density fell from 14 percent to 8 percent between 2011 and 2024. Union halls historically provided the organizational infrastructure — meeting space, voter registration drives, civic education, collective political voice — that sustained civic participation for working-class communities. Their elimination through legislative action removes not just wage bargaining but the entire civic infrastructure that unions built. Domain 22's labor power analysis and Domain 59's civic exclusion analysis describe the same underlying attack on the same population through two different mechanisms.

**What Domain 59 adds to Domain 22.** Domain 22 typically frames union suppression as a labor rights issue. Domain 59 reframes it as a democratic design issue: union suppression is disenfranchisement by institutional elimination, not merely wage reduction. The civic infrastructure that unions provided — voter registration drives, civic education, organizational voice for working-class communities — is precisely the infrastructure that both domains document as under systematic attack.

### Bridge 3: Domain 42 (Drug Policy) — Disenfranchisement Feedback Loop Mirrors Felony Disenfranchisement

**The mechanism.** Domain 42 documents the drug policy enforcement regime that produces mass incarceration, felony disenfranchisement, and post-incarceration civic exclusion. Domain 59 documents the economic precarity pathway that mirrors this feedback loop through non-criminal mechanisms: economic precarity → reduced civic capacity → reduced political influence → reduced policy pressure to address economic precarity → maintained conditions of precarity. The two feedback loops reinforce each other because they operate on the same population — communities of color in low-income urban and rural areas — through different but compounding mechanisms.

**The feedback loop comparison.**
- *Felony disenfranchisement loop (Domain 42)*: Drug enforcement targeting low-income communities → arrest and conviction → felony disenfranchisement → reduced political representation → no political pressure to end drug enforcement targeting → sustained enforcement
- *Economic precarity loop (Domain 59)*: Wage stagnation + debt extraction → reduced civic capacity → reduced political influence → no political pressure to raise minimum wage or address debt coercion → sustained precarity

**The shared population.** Both loops operate disproportionately on Black and Latino communities in Zone 5's urban centers: Chicago's South and West Sides, Detroit's east side, Milwaukee's north side, and Gary, Indiana. Research on felony disenfranchisement in Florida (the primary academic case study) establishes that mass disenfranchisement of Black voters through the criminal justice system produces measurable policy outcomes favoring incumbent power structures — the same mechanism that economic precarity's civic exclusion produces through the income-participation gap.

**What Domain 59 adds to Domain 42.** The economic precarity pathway shows that disenfranchisement does not require criminal conviction. The same civic exclusion outcome that felony disenfranchisement produces through legal status removal is produced through economic architecture through the bandwidth tax, time poverty, and registration lapse mechanisms. Addressing felony disenfranchisement (Domain 42's reform agenda) without addressing economic precarity (Domain 59) leaves intact the parallel mechanism that produces the same outcome through different means.

### Bridge 4: Domain 54 (Media Access) — Economic Precarity as Information Access Gap

**The mechanism.** Domain 54 documents the concentration of media ownership, algorithmic mediation of news consumption, and the decline of local journalism. Domain 59 adds the demand-side economic condition: the population most subject to information access gaps is defined by the economic precarity documented here. Households in economic precarity are more likely to consume news through algorithmic social media feeds (available on smartphones without broadband) and less likely to consume print journalism, long-form policy analysis, or direct organizational communications.

**The broadband gap.** FCC and Benton Institute data (2025) document that 22 percent of households with incomes below $25,000 rely only on cell plans for home internet service, compared with 8 percent for households earning over $250,000. Between 5.9 and 7.8 percent of Americans cannot access broadband internet at all, with a pronounced rural-urban divide where approximately 64.4 percent of rural households have broadband access compared to 98 percent of urban households. In Zone 5's rural communities — northern Michigan, northern Minnesota, rural Wisconsin, downstate Illinois — broadband access gaps combine with library and civic center closures to create information deserts that structurally limit political information consumption.

**The cognitive bandwidth compounding.** The Mani et al. (*Science*, 2013) bandwidth tax operates not just on time but on information processing: households under financial stress have less working memory available for evaluating ballot measures, comparing candidates on policy dimensions, or processing the complex informational content of local government decisions. The combination of reduced bandwidth (cognitive limitation) and reduced information access (broadband and local journalism gaps) produces a doubly constrained information environment that suppresses informed civic participation independently of motivation.

**The algorithmic mediation mechanism.** Economic precarity concentrates news consumption in the smartphone/social media environment because broadband-dependent news sources require both reliable internet and the time for long-form engagement. Algorithmic feeds optimize for engagement, not deliberative quality: economically precarious households receive political information that is emotionally activating but informationally thin — optimized for outrage and sharing rather than for the policy nuance that enables effective civic participation. This is not a media literacy failure; it is an economic architecture consequence.

**What Domain 59 adds to Domain 54.** Domain 54's analysis of media concentration and algorithmic mediation focuses on supply-side failures. Domain 59 adds the demand-side constraint: the audience most vulnerable to algorithmic information distortion is the audience with the least cognitive bandwidth, the least broadband access, and the least discretionary time — all defined by economic precarity. Media reform without economic security reform leaves the most vulnerable audience in the same structurally impaired information environment.

## Conclusion: Economic Architecture as Democratic Design

The 26-point income-participation gap is not a voter preference. It is not political apathy or ideological disengagement. It is the structural consequence of an economic architecture that converts material scarcity into civic exclusion through four mechanistically distinct, independently verified causal pathways.

The Dallas Fed WP2517 quasi-experiment establishes that financial relief causes increased civic participation. The Oregon Medicaid RCT establishes that healthcare coverage expansion causes increased voter turnout. Slee and Desmond's instrumental variable study establishes that eviction reduction causes increased voter turnout. Kuk, Hajnal, and Lajevardi's natural experiment establishes that residential stability — not merely housing quality — is a prerequisite for sustained civic engagement. Mani, Mullainathan, Shafir, and Zhao establish that financial scarcity cognitively impairs the deliberative capacity that civic engagement requires. These are not correlations; they are causal findings from natural experiments and randomized control trials.

The 2026 policy windows — CTC expansion in July reconciliation, Medicaid work requirement outreach in June–August, student loan deferral advocacy throughout — are not abstract reform opportunities. They are specific, near-term interventions in documented mechanisms of civic exclusion. Each window corresponds to a moment when the economic precarity pathways are most actively operating and when 501(c)(3) organizations can legitimately engage in advocacy, education, and civic mobilization.

Zone 5's regional analysis confirms that the pathways described here are not national abstractions. Illinois's 289,000-unit affordable housing shortfall, Indiana's 89 percent ELI renter cost-burden rate, Wisconsin's post-Act 10 union density collapse, Minnesota's 18 rural hospitals at closure risk, Michigan's 75 percent severely cost-burdened ELI population, and Iowa's 430,000 student loan borrowers averaging $30,698 in debt — these are the specific material conditions through which economic precarity converts to democratic exclusion in the six states that determine Zone 5's political character and national electoral weight.

The reform agenda is achievable. Automatic voter registration, same-day registration, paid family leave, a federal minimum wage with COLA, Medicaid defense and expansion, childcare universalization, CTC full refundability, and student debt garnishment suspension are not utopian demands. They are policies adopted in whole or in part by every other wealthy democracy. They are democratic infrastructure — not welfare programs with democratic side effects, but democratic design reforms whose co-benefit is poverty reduction.

The reframe this domain provides is its central contribution: economic justice advocacy is democratic infrastructure advocacy. The organizations delivering mutual aid, labor organizing, housing rights, healthcare access, and student debt relief are not doing welfare work adjacent to democratic renewal. They are building the material conditions under which democratic participation becomes structurally possible. That reframe opens their work to democracy funders, election protection organizations, and congressional oversight staff who currently do not receive economic justice analysis as directly relevant to their mission.
