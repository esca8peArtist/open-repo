## Section 1: Domain K and H Scope Overview

### What Phase 3 Is Researching and Why

Phase 3 is the third cycle of a multi-year resistance research project. Phase 1 built monitoring infrastructure and Phase 2 produced litigation-tracking and advocacy research in seven domains. Phase 3 focuses on structural reform — what institutional changes would make US democratic institutions more durable. The January 3, 2027 seating of the 120th Congress is the primary external deadline: research must be complete and in distribution before Congress seats its judiciary committees and begins the first substantive legislative weeks.

Two domains anchor Phase 3:

**Domain K: Federal Judiciary Restructuring and Supreme Court Reform**
Domain K documents the structural crisis in the federal judiciary — lifetime tenure that enables ideological capture, an unenforced ethics code, and a shadow docket operating as a primary policymaking vehicle — and maps the full legislative inventory of reform proposals currently in Congress. The domain has four research zones (detailed in Section 2) covering legislation, constitutional feasibility, international precedent, and coalition activation strategy. Domain K is production-ready in preliminary form (DOMAIN_K_RESEARCH_REPORT.md exists at approximately 12,700 lines of accumulated research), but Phase 3 requires a complete, distribution-quality synthesis document of approximately 15,000–20,000 words that integrates all four zones into a single advocacy-research deliverable. The preliminary research is your source base and scaffold — you are not starting from zero.

**Domain H: Constitutional Resilience Architecture**
Domain H examines how the Constitution's design gaps enable democratic failure and what reform architecture — constitutional amendments, statutory reclamation, and doctrinal application — can close those gaps. A production-quality Domain H research document (approximately 6,800 words, 50+ citations) was completed June 6, 2026. Your task for Domain H is not to rewrite it but to extend it: Section 5 (Reform Architecture) requires deeper treatment of the international case studies (Germany's Basic Law, Ireland's Citizens' Assembly model, New Zealand's electoral reform pathway), and Section 6 (Movement Infrastructure) requires updating with post-June 2026 legislative and litigation developments. The target extension is approximately 4,000–6,000 additional words, bringing Domain H to approximately 10,000–12,000 words total.

### How the Domains Interrelate

Domain K and H are companion documents designed to be distributed together. They share six primary sources (Levinson's *Our Undemocratic Constitution*, the 2021 Presidential Commission on the Supreme Court, the Germany Basic Law comparative materials, the Brennan Center constitutional reform analyses, the NCC Constitution Drafting Project, and the Fix the Court legislative proposals). They answer different but adjacent questions: Domain K is "here is what Congress can do right now about the judiciary"; Domain H is "here is the structural constitutional context within which those reforms operate and what longer-horizon reform architecture looks like."

The two documents will be distributed as a linked pair to the same Tier 1 audience: Senate Judiciary Committee staff, House Judiciary Democratic caucus contacts, constitutional law scholars (ACS, CAC, Brennan Center judiciary program), and Fix the Court. A researcher reading Domain K's term limits analysis should be able to turn to Domain H's amendment process analysis and see how they connect. Your job is to ensure that connection is explicit in both documents — each should cross-reference the other where the subject matter overlaps.

## Section 4: Writing Standards and Deliverable Templates

### Expected Writing Style

Phase 3 research documents are hybrid academic-advocacy writing. They are not law review articles (no extensive footnotes, no hedged methodological framing, no passive voice constructions) and they are not op-eds (not 800 words, not stripped of citation, not purely argumentative). The target reader is a Senate Judiciary Committee staffer or a constitutional law scholar at ACS who has 30 minutes to read your document and needs to understand both the evidence base and the actionable conclusion.

**Concrete standards**:
- Lead with the most important finding, not with background context
- Every section ends with a clear implication, not a summary of what was just described
- Citations are inline hyperlinks where possible, footnotes where hyperlinks are not practical; the reader should be able to verify any claim without leaving the document
- Confidence levels are stated explicitly when the underlying evidence is genuinely uncertain (use formulations like "the dominant scholarly view holds..." or "this interpretation is contested — three constitutional scholars argue X while Fix the Court argues Y")
- Active voice throughout; passive voice is permitted only when the actor is genuinely unknown or irrelevant
- Acronyms are spelled out on first use
- Technical legal terms (shadow docket, writ of certiorari, circuit split) are defined briefly the first time they appear in each section; do not assume the reader holds a law degree

**Length targets**:
- Domain K complete synthesis: 15,000–20,000 words
- Domain H complete document (existing 6,800 words plus extensions): 10,000–12,000 words
- Zone K-4 one-page executive summary: 500 words
- Zone K-4 two-page policy brief: 1,200 words

### Deliverable Template: Research Zone Output

Each research zone produces a zone output document structured as follows. This structure makes it easy to integrate zone outputs into the final domain synthesis.

---

**[Zone Name] — [Domain K or H] — [Date Completed]**

**Zone summary** (100–150 words): What does this zone cover and what is the one most important finding?

**Evidence section** (body of zone analysis): structured with named subsections (e.g., "1.1 H.R. 1074 — Bill Status and Constitutional Analysis"), inline citations, confidence level notes where relevant.

**Cross-domain connections** (3–5 bullets): Where does this zone's findings connect to Domain H, Phase 2 domains (list specific domain numbers), or other Phase 3 zones? These bullets become cross-reference links in the final synthesis document.

**Sources used in this zone** (formatted as: Author/Organization. "Title." Source/URL. Accessed [month year]. Classification: PRIMARY or SECONDARY.)

**Open questions flagged for next zone** (if any): Questions this zone's research raised that the next zone must address.

## Section 6: Success Criteria and Measurement

### How Phase 3 Research Is On Track

The following checkpoints are used to assess research quality and trajectory. The project coordinator will conduct a brief weekly check-in (15–20 minutes, async or synchronous) to assess each checkpoint.

**Week 1 check**: Zone K-1 output document received. Spot-check: 3 bills' status has been verified against Congress.gov primary source (not secondary summaries). Citation format is consistent with Domain K Research Report exemplar.

**Week 3 check**: Combined Zone K-1 and K-2 output is at least 7,000 words. Zone K-2 constitutional feasibility argument has been cross-checked against at least one dissenting scholarly view (not just the Fix the Court and Brennan Center position). Confidence levels are stated where the constitutional debate is genuinely unresolved.

**Week 6 check**: Zone K-4 executive summary can be read by a non-specialist and clearly states (a) what the structural problem is, (b) what the three most achievable legislative reforms are, and (c) why now (the January 2027 congressional window). If the executive summary requires a legal background to understand, it is not ready.

**Week 7 check**: Domain K is submitted in production-ready condition — no placeholder sections, no [VERIFY THIS] flags, no broken links. All cited bill texts link to Congress.gov or equivalent primary source.

**Week 10 check**: Domain H integrated draft reads as a unified document, not as three separate extensions attached to the base. Tone and citation standard are consistent throughout.

**Week 12 check**: Both Domain K and Domain H are submitted. The two-page research summary identifies: (a) 3 specific findings that were not anticipated in the preliminary research, (b) 2 open questions the research could not resolve and why, (c) the recommended first distribution contact for each domain.

### Source Quality Standards

These are non-negotiable minimum standards:

- No Wikipedia as a primary source. Wikipedia is acceptable as a navigation tool to find primary sources, not as a citeable reference
- All bill texts cited from Congress.gov primary source, not summaries
- All court opinions cited from official court, SCOTUSblog, or slip opinion PDFs — not news coverage of the ruling
- Statistical claims (polling numbers, citation counts, approval ratings) cited from original polling organization or institutional publisher — not third-party recitations
- International comparative claims cited from official government sources (Germany Basic Law: official translation at gesetze-im-internet.de; Canada Supreme Court Act: justice.gc.ca) or peer-reviewed scholarship — not Wikipedia or news articles

### What Makes Research Distribution-Ready

A zone output is distribution-ready when a Tier 1 contact (Senate Judiciary staffer, constitutional law scholar) can read it without needing a guide to the preliminary research. It does not require familiarity with Phase 1 or Phase 2 context. It does not require knowledge of the research project's history. It begins with a finding, builds through evidence, and ends with an actionable conclusion. Its citations are verifiable. Its language is clear to a non-specialist. It is internally consistent (no claims in one section that contradict claims in another).
