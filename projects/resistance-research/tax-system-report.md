# Tax System Design: A Comprehensive Reform Report
## How to Build a Tax System That Is Effective, Equitable, Fraud-Resistant, Loophole-Free, and Citizen-Friendly

*Evidence-based analysis | Research conducted March 2026*

---

## Executive Summary

The United States tax system is losing approximately $696 billion per year in taxes that are legally owed but never collected (the gross tax gap, tax year 2022 IRS projection). Over a decade, that represents roughly $7 trillion in foregone revenue — more than enough to fund universal pre-K, eliminate medical debt, rebuild infrastructure, and still reduce the deficit. This is not primarily a problem of law-breaking. Most of the gap is legal: the system is riddled with provisions that treat identical economic outcomes differently depending on how income is labeled, how assets are held, or who your accountant is.

Meanwhile, $1.9 trillion per year in additional revenue is forgone through tax expenditures — deductions, exclusions, and credits that primarily benefit those at the top. The combined cost of the tax gap and tax expenditures exceeds $2.6 trillion annually in revenue that could be collected under a coherent system.

**The 10 Most Impactful Reforms (ranked by revenue and equity impact):**

| Rank | Reform | Estimated Revenue / Impact |
|------|--------|---------------------------|
| 1 | IRS funding fully restored and expanded | $395B+ over 10 years in gross collections |
| 2 | Eliminate stepped-up basis at death | $500B+ over 10 years |
| 3 | Treat capital gains as ordinary income above $1M | $250B+ over 10 years |
| 4 | Mark-to-market / Billionaire Minimum Income Tax (25%) | $360B+ over 10 years |
| 5 | Universal pre-filled tax returns (Direct File restored) | Saves $5.6B/year in compliance costs; closes filing gap |
| 6 | Close carried interest loophole | $12–18B/year |
| 7 | Corporate rate to 25–28% + country-by-country reporting | $500–886B over 10 years |

| 9 | Strengthen GAAR + economic substance doctrine | Systemic, prevents $100B+ annually in avoidance |
| 10 | Beneficial ownership transparency restored and enforced | Systemic; enables enforcement of existing law |

**Key Tenets of a Well-Designed Tax System:** Every dollar of economic income — regardless of source, form, or holder — should be treated consistently. The system must be simple enough that citizens can comply without professional help, enforced vigorously enough that evasion is unprofitable, and designed without artificial distinctions that create planning opportunities.

## Part I: The Core Tenets of a Just and Effective Tax System

These tenets constitute the foundational principles by which every feature of a tax system should be evaluated. A provision that violates multiple tenets simultaneously — as many current US provisions do — has no legitimate policy justification.

### Tenet 1: Equity

**The Tenet:** Tax burdens must be distributed according to ability to pay, with equal treatment of equals (horizontal equity) and progressive rates on unequals (vertical equity).

**What it means:** Two people with the same economic income should pay the same tax, regardless of how that income is earned. A person earning $500,000 in wages should not pay more in taxes than a person earning $500,000 in capital gains, interest, or pass-through business income. Vertical equity requires that as income rises, the effective tax rate rises — those with more capacity to contribute do so.

**What violates it in the current system:** Long-term capital gains are taxed at a top federal rate of 23.8% (20% + 3.8% net investment income tax), while ordinary labor income is taxed at up to 40.8% (37% + 3.8% payroll contributions). A hedge fund manager earning $50 million in carried interest pays capital gains rates on what is economically compensation for services. The Section 199A deduction gives pass-through business owners a 20% deduction unavailable to wage earners. The stepped-up basis rule means that a billionaire's entire lifetime of capital appreciation escapes income tax entirely at death.

**How to uphold it:** Apply a uniform rate schedule to all income regardless of source. Eliminate provisions that distinguish based on income type rather than economic capacity. Apply the Haig-Simons definition of income: consumption plus change in net worth, regardless of realization. Ensure audit rates increase, not decrease, with income level.

---

### Tenet 2: Simplicity

**The Tenet:** Citizens must be able to understand their tax obligations and comply without professional assistance.

**What it means:** A tax system that requires an accountant to navigate is not a neutral system — it is one that advantages those who can afford professional help and punishes those who cannot. Complexity is not a neutral byproduct of sophistication; it is itself a policy choice with distributional consequences.

**What violates it in the current system:** Americans spend an estimated 6 billion hours per year on tax compliance — more than any comparable nation. The Internal Revenue Code exceeds 4 million words. The tax preparation industry (Intuit, H&R Block, Jackson Hewitt) generates over $14 billion in annual revenues precisely because compliance is too complex for most citizens to handle alone. Countries like Estonia complete tax filing in under 5 minutes via pre-filled digital returns. The US government already possesses most of the data needed to pre-fill returns but has been lobbied out of doing so.

**How to uphold it:** Universal pre-filled returns (the government sends you what it knows; you correct errors). Eliminate most itemized deductions in favor of a large standard deduction and targeted refundable credits. Consolidate the multiple layers of minimum tax, phase-outs, and carve-outs into a single coherent rate schedule. Write tax-facing materials in plain language.

---

### Tenet 3: Transparency

**The Tenet:** Every citizen must be able to see where their tax money goes and how the tax burden is distributed.

**What it means:** Tax expenditures — the $1.9 trillion per year in foregone revenue from deductions, exclusions, and credits — are a "hidden budget" that subsidizes particular activities but receives almost none of the scrutiny that direct spending receives. Citizens who demand accountability for welfare spending have no equivalent information about the preferential treatment of capital gains or carried interest.

**What violates it in the current system:** Tax expenditure analysis is buried in technical reports by the Joint Committee on Taxation and Treasury. There is no requirement that tax expenditures be reauthorized periodically (unlike discretionary spending). Corporate tax payments are not publicly disclosed. Countries in scope of Pillar Two now report country-by-country to tax authorities, but the US has not required public disclosure.

**How to uphold it:** Annual public tax expenditure report with plain-language explanation and distributional analysis. Public country-by-country reporting for large multinationals. Publication of effective tax rates paid by major corporations. Sunset all tax expenditures on a 5-year cycle, requiring affirmative reauthorization.

---

### Tenet 4: Efficiency

**The Tenet:** Taxes should raise revenue with minimum distortion of economic decisions, minimizing deadweight loss.

**What it means:** Every tax creates some behavioral change — that is unavoidable. But when tax differences are large enough to drive economic decisions (the "lock-in effect" of capital gains tax, the choice of organizational form based on pass-through deductions, the "borrow don't sell" strategy), the economy operates below its potential. The goal is taxes that are as neutral as possible across economic choices.

**What violates it in the current system:** The capital gains preference creates lock-in — investors hold appreciated assets longer than economically optimal to defer taxation, reducing market liquidity. The corporate interest deduction creates a debt bias, artificially incentivizing debt financing over equity. The pass-through deduction creates incentives to reclassify wages as business income. The Section 1031 like-kind exchange allows real estate investors to defer capital gains indefinitely through a daisy-chain of exchanges.

**How to uphold it:** Comprehensive income taxation reduces the distortions created by differential treatment. A broad-based low-rate system (the classic Ramsey principle: tax inelastic bases at higher rates, elastic bases at lower rates) minimizes deadweight loss. Eliminate interest deductibility or limit it (thin capitalization rules). Mark-to-market for liquid assets eliminates the lock-in effect entirely.

---

### Tenet 5: Adequacy

**The Tenet:** The tax system must raise sufficient revenue to fund the public services a democratic society chooses to provide.

**What it means:** A tax system that chronically underfunds public needs forces a choice between debt, service cuts, or both. The US collects taxes at about 27% of GDP — the lowest among wealthy OECD nations, which average 34%. This "adequacy gap" is structural: the tax base has been narrowed by decades of tax expenditures, rate cuts, and enforcement defunding.

**What violates it in the current system:** The $696 billion annual tax gap. The $1.9 trillion in tax expenditures. The TCJA's corporate rate cut from 35% to 21%, which costs approximately $100 billion per year in revenue. The defunding of the IRS from 2010 to 2020, which reduced its enforcement capacity by a third. The elimination of Direct File in 2025, which had 94% user satisfaction and was reaching underserved populations.

**How to uphold it:** Revenue-neutral reform that broadens the base while keeping rates competitive. IRS funding restoration (the Inflation Reduction Act appropriated $80 billion for IRS enforcement; Congress subsequently clawed much of it back). Close the tax gap through enforcement. Restore and expand Direct File.

---

### Tenet 6: Zero Tolerance for Avoidance

**The Tenet:** No legal distinction should arbitrarily favor certain income types, asset classes, or organizational structures over economically equivalent alternatives.

**What it means:** When two economically identical transactions are taxed at dramatically different rates based on legal form, the tax code is not merely failing to achieve equity — it is actively rewarding those who can engineer transactions to exploit the difference. The existence of large planning opportunities is evidence of a poorly designed code.

**What violates it in the current system:** Carried interest (income from investment management taxed as capital gains rather than compensation). The stepped-up basis (capital gains that existed during life escape taxation entirely at death). The "Buy, Borrow, Die" strategy (borrow against appreciated assets to avoid realization; receive stepped-up basis at death; cycle repeats). The pass-through deduction (reduces effective rates for business owners but not employees). Check-the-box rules for foreign entities (allow multinationals to create "tax nothing" structures).

**How to uphold it:** A General Anti-Avoidance Rule (GAAR) that captures transactions whose principal purpose is tax avoidance, regardless of technical compliance. Economic substance doctrine codified and enforced. Carry back the definition of income to economic reality rather than legal form. Eliminate the stepped-up basis. Treat carried interest as compensation.

---

### Tenet 7: Universality

**The Tenet:** All income, from all sources, regardless of who earns it, must be included in the tax base unless there is an explicit, justified, and time-limited exception.

**What it means:** The Haig-Simons definition of income — annual consumption plus change in net worth — is the correct baseline. Every departure from that definition must be justified on policy grounds. In the absence of justification, all income is income.

**What violates it in the current system:** Unrealized capital gains ($8.5 trillion held by the top 1%) are not taxed until realization — and may never be taxed if assets pass through an estate. Employer-sponsored health insurance premiums ($5.9 trillion in forgone revenue over 10 years) are excluded from income. Municipal bond interest is excluded. Life insurance inside build-up is excluded. The tax code is riddled with exclusions that have accumulated over decades without systematic review.

**How to uphold it:** Start from a comprehensive income base and defend each exclusion on policy grounds in a regular review cycle. Mark-to-market for the wealthiest taxpayers as a first step toward universality. Treat death as a realization event (or eliminate stepped-up basis). Regular sunset review of all exclusions.

---

### Tenet 8: Enforceability

**The Tenet:** The system must be designed from the ground up to be auditable, with minimal opportunities for undisclosed transactions.

**What it means:** Third-party reporting — where employers, banks, brokers, and others report income to the IRS — is the single most powerful enforcement tool available. When third-party reporting exists, compliance is above 95%. When it does not (cash businesses, self-employment, cryptocurrency), compliance drops dramatically. The system should be designed to maximize the ratio of income subject to third-party reporting.

**What violates it in the current system:** Cash economy transactions are largely invisible to the IRS. Cryptocurrency, until recent broker reporting rules, had essentially no systematic third-party reporting. Offshore accounts, while subject to FATCA reporting, remain difficult to enforce against non-US institutions. The IRS audit rate for millionaires dropped 71% between 2010 and 2019. The beneficial ownership transparency system established by the Corporate Transparency Act was gutted in March 2025 for domestic companies. The IRS had 9,510 auditors in 2024, down a third from 2010.

**How to uphold it:** Expand third-party reporting to cryptocurrency (already begun with Form 1099-DA for 2025), cash-intensive businesses, and pass-through entities. Restore and expand IRS enforcement budget. Fix the perverse audit pattern — the IRS audits the poor (EITC claimants) at higher rates than the wealthy because poor taxpayers cannot fight back; shift audit resources to high-income and high-wealth individuals. Restore beneficial ownership transparency requirements.

---

### Tenet 9: Stability

**The Tenet:** Tax law should be predictable and not subject to constant manipulation for short-term political purposes.

**What it means:** Economic decisions — investment, hiring, saving — are made over multi-year horizons. Frequent, unpredictable changes to tax law increase the cost of planning and tend to generate windfall gains and losses for those who happened to be positioned correctly at the moment of change. Stability also reduces the lobbying incentive: if tax rates were permanent and base erosion impossible, there would be less reason to spend millions on favorable carve-outs.

**What violates it in the current system:** The Bush tax cuts (2001–2003) were made "temporary" and extended repeatedly before being partially made permanent. The TCJA of 2017 made corporate rate changes permanent but individual rate changes temporary (expiring 2025). Tax policy has become a constant lobbying battleground, with major changes passed through reconciliation to avoid bipartisan negotiation. The "tax extenders" — dozens of provisions temporarily renewed each year — create planning uncertainty and lobbying dependency.

**How to uphold it:** Sunset all provisions on a regular cycle requiring affirmative reauthorization. Make major structural features of the code permanent. Separate temporary emergency measures (stimulus credits) from permanent structural tax policy. Establish a nonpartisan tax expenditure review commission modeled on the UK's Office of Tax Simplification.

---

### Tenet 10: Democratic Accountability

**The Tenet:** Tax law must be made transparently, with genuine public deliberation, representing the will of the governed rather than the will of major donors and special interests.

**What it means:** The tax code reflects the accumulated victories of well-funded lobbying operations. Provisions like carried interest, the real estate industry's preservation of 1031 exchanges, the life insurance industry's exemptions, and the oil and gas industry's percentage depletion allowance exist not because they represent sound policy but because those industries invested in having them written into law. A democratically legitimate tax code would be developed through open deliberation with full distributional analysis.

**What violates it in the current system:** The TCJA was passed in weeks with minimal public hearings. The private equity industry has repeatedly killed legislation to close the carried interest loophole, most recently seeing the loophole narrowly survive the Inflation Reduction Act negotiations in 2022 after Joe Manchin dropped it under pressure. The tax preparation industry spent more than $90 million lobbying against IRS free filing. Citizens cannot see, in any accessible form, who benefits from tax expenditures.

**How to uphold it:** Mandatory distributional analysis for all tax legislation, published in accessible form before any vote. Multi-year public deliberation process for major code changes. Strict limits on tax lobbyist-to-lawmaker revolving door. Public country-by-country reporting. Annual publication of effective tax rates by income percentile.

## Part II: Diagnosis — What Is Broken and Why

### 2.1 The Tax Gap

**The Numbers:** The IRS's most recent projection estimates the gross tax gap at $696 billion for tax year 2022. The agency expects to recover approximately $90 billion of that through enforcement, leaving a net tax gap of $606 billion. This represents the single largest source of potential new revenue available without any rate increases.

For context: $606 billion is more than the US spends on Medicaid ($616 billion in FY2023). The tax gap has almost doubled since 2001 (from $345 billion) in nominal dollars, outpacing inflation.

**Components of the tax gap:**
- **Nonfiling** ($63 billion): Taxpayers who are required to file but do not
- **Underreporting** ($539 billion, 77% of total): Taxpayers who file but understate income or overstate deductions
- **Underpayment** ($94 billion): Taxpayers who report correctly but do not pay

**Who underpays:** The gap is not evenly distributed. Self-employed individuals, business owners, and those with income not subject to third-party reporting drive the vast majority of underreporting. The IRS estimates that when third-party reporting covers income (wages, salaries, dividends), compliance rates exceed 95%. When income is self-reported with no third-party confirmation (self-employment, cash businesses, pass-through income), compliance rates drop to 45% or below.

**Enforcement asymmetry:** Between 2010 and 2019, audit rates for millionaires dropped 71%. For large corporations, audit rates fell 54%. Meanwhile, EITC claimants — among the lowest-income Americans — are audited at rates comparable to or exceeding wealthy taxpayers, because their returns are simple enough to audit by correspondence and they lack the legal resources to contest findings. This is the enforcement status quo: the system audits those least able to cheat and ignores those most able to.

**The IRS budget crisis:** Between 2010 and 2018, IRS enforcement staff declined by 30%, from approximately 50,000 to 35,000 employees. By 2024, the IRS had 9,510 auditors, down a third from 2010. The Congressional Budget Office estimated that cuts to IRS enforcement translate to $395 billion in reduced gross tax collections over a decade. For every additional dollar spent auditing top-decile taxpayers, the government recovers approximately $12 in revenue.

The Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 appropriated $80 billion for IRS modernization and enforcement over 10 years. The IRS expected this to yield $561 billion in additional revenue collections from 2024 to 2034. Congress subsequently clawed back significant portions of this funding. The Trump administration's IRS cuts in 2025 further decimated the agency's enforcement capacity.

### 2.2 Structural Inequities

**Carried interest:** Investment managers at hedge funds, private equity firms, and venture capital firms receive a share of their funds' profits (typically 20%) called "carried interest." This is economically compensation for services rendered. However, by characterizing it as a capital gain rather than ordinary income, these managers pay a top rate of 23.8% rather than 40.8%. The CBO estimates closing this loophole would raise $12–14 billion over 10 years. Other estimates put the annual cost at $18 billion. The provision benefits a small number of extremely wealthy individuals. Despite bipartisan rhetorical opposition — both Trump and Obama promised to close it — the private equity lobby has successfully defended it for over two decades. It nearly died in the Inflation Reduction Act negotiations in 2022 but survived due to a single senator's veto.

**Capital gains preference:** Long-term capital gains (assets held over one year) are taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on total income — far below the 37% top rate on ordinary income. The equity argument against this preference is straightforward: a household earning $500,000 in wages pays 37% in federal income tax; a household earning $500,000 in long-term capital gains pays 20%. The typical American household receives only 0.5% of its income from capital gains. Those in the top 0.01% receive more than 44% of their income from capital gains. The capital gains preference is thus a tax preference for the ultra-wealthy, not for ordinary investors. The cost: the reduced rate on capital gains and dividends costs approximately $237 billion per year in foregone revenue.

**Section 199A pass-through deduction:** The TCJA created a 20% deduction for "qualified business income" from pass-through entities (S-corporations, partnerships, sole proprietors). The stated rationale was to level the playing field with C-corporations after the corporate rate cut. The actual result: 61% of the benefit (per JCT estimates for 2024) goes to the top 1% of income earners; 26% goes to the top 0.1%. Research found "little evidence of changes in real economic activity" — investment, employment, or wages did not increase measurably. The deduction costs between $700 billion and $980 billion over 10 years if extended.

**Depreciation acceleration and real estate preferences:** Real estate receives a cascade of favorable tax treatments that bear no relationship to policy rationale:
- Accelerated depreciation allows real estate investors to write off assets faster than they actually decline in value, generating artificial "losses" that offset other income
- Like-kind exchanges (Section 1031) allow indefinite deferral of capital gains as long as sale proceeds are reinvested in similar property; the JCT estimates eliminating this provision would raise $30.5 billion over 10 years
- The combination of depreciation, interest deductions, and 1031 exchanges means sophisticated real estate investors can hold and grow substantial wealth while reporting low or negative taxable income

**Corporate tax rate erosion:** The US corporate rate was 35% from 1993 to 2017 — among the highest statutory rates in the developed world, though the effective rate was substantially lower due to deductions, credits, and offshore structuring. The TCJA cut the rate to 21%, costing approximately $919 billion over 10 years (FY2018–2027 projection). The effective rate for all active corporations dropped from 26.4% in 2017 to 12.5% in 2018. While some reduction from the outlier 35% rate was economically defensible (most OECD nations had already reduced their rates), the magnitude of the cut and the failure to compensate by base-broadening measures resulted in a permanent fiscal hole.

**The "Buy, Borrow, Die" strategy:** This is not a loophole in the colloquial sense — it is the interaction of multiple deliberate code features that allows the ultra-wealthy to avoid income taxation on their entire lifetime of wealth accumulation:

1. **Buy**: Accumulate appreciating assets (stocks, real estate, art, private company stakes). The appreciation is not income until realized (sold).
2. **Borrow**: Rather than sell assets (which would trigger tax), borrow against them. Loans are not income. Interest paid may be deductible. The borrowed cash funds consumption.
3. **Die**: Assets pass to heirs with a stepped-up basis — the heir's cost basis is the fair market value at death, not the original purchase price. All lifetime capital appreciation vanishes untaxed.

The strategy is entirely legal. It is the product of three deliberate policy choices: (1) the realization principle (unrealized gains are not taxed), (2) the stepped-up basis rule, and (3) the relative weakness of the estate tax. The top 400 American wealth-holders pay an average effective tax rate that is lower than the macroeconomic average tax rate of 29%, per Saez and Zucman's research.

### 2.3 Corporate Tax Avoidance

**Transfer pricing:** Multinational corporations must set prices for transactions between their subsidiaries in different countries. These "transfer prices" should in theory reflect arm's-length market rates — what would be charged between unrelated parties. In practice, companies systematically manipulate transfer prices to shift profits to low-tax jurisdictions and costs to high-tax jurisdictions. Common methods:
- Charge a subsidiary in a high-tax country an inflated royalty for use of intellectual property held in Ireland or Cayman Islands
- Sell goods at artificially low prices from a high-tax affiliate to a low-tax affiliate, which then sells to customers at market price
- Concentrate financial functions (treasury, insurance, financing) in low-tax jurisdictions

BEPS (Base Erosion and Profit Shifting) is the OECD term for this phenomenon. The OECD estimates that transfer pricing manipulation and other BEPS strategies cost countries $100–240 billion in lost corporate tax revenue annually — equivalent to 4–10% of global corporate income tax revenue. Developing countries are disproportionately harmed.

**Corporate inversions and treaty shopping:** An "inversion" occurs when a US company reincorporates in a low-tax jurisdiction (historically Ireland, Bermuda, UK) while maintaining its operational headquarters in the US. The transaction is often structured as a "merger" with a foreign company to satisfy regulatory requirements. The result is that future foreign profits — and, in some cases, US profits through complex intercompany structuring — are taxed at the lower foreign rate. The Treasury has issued regulations limiting inversions, but the technique persists in modified forms. "Treaty shopping" involves routing transactions through intermediary countries that have favorable tax treaties with the country where income is ultimately earned.

**Domestic tax havens:** Delaware is incorporated by approximately 68% of Fortune 500 companies primarily because Delaware corporate law (not primarily its tax law) is favorable. But more significant domestic structures include:
- Nevada and Wyoming LLCs with no income tax and minimal reporting requirements
- Real estate investment trusts (REITs) structured to minimize corporate tax through dividend deductions

**The Global Minimum Tax (Pillar Two):** The OECD's Pillar Two framework, agreed to in 2021 by over 140 countries, establishes a 15% global minimum corporate income tax for multinationals with revenue over €750 million. The key mechanism is the "income inclusion rule" (IIR): if a subsidiary is taxed below 15% in its home country, the parent company's country can impose a "top-up tax" to bring the effective rate to 15%. As of August 2025, 65 countries have adopted final or draft legislation implementing Pillar Two. EU member states were required to implement by December 2023. Approximately 90% of in-scope multinationals will be subject to the 15% minimum by 2025 per OECD projections.

**Limitations of Pillar Two:** The 15% minimum is well below the 21–25% rates most economists consider optimal for a well-functioning corporate tax. It applies only to very large multinationals (€750M revenue threshold). The US has not enacted domestic implementing legislation as of early 2026. Without US participation, US-parented multinationals face a patchwork of foreign minimum taxes without a coordinated US response.

### 2.4 Complexity and Compliance Burden

Americans spend approximately 6 billion hours per year on federal tax compliance. This is more than comparable OECD nations by a significant margin. Estonia's average filing time: under 5 minutes. Sweden and Denmark have pre-filled return systems that eliminate most filing burden for ordinary taxpayers.

The US compliance industry generates over $14 billion in annual revenues. This is an economic deadweight loss — it represents skilled labor and capital deployed not to create goods or services but to navigate an unnecessarily complex regulatory system.

**The political economy of complexity:** The tax preparation industry (Intuit, H&R Block) has a direct financial interest in preventing simplification. Intuit spent $3.7 million on federal lobbying in 2024, its highest-ever total, to oppose the IRS Direct File program. The two companies have spent over $35 million combined since 2006 lobbying against free filing options. The IRS Direct File program — launched in 2024, expanded to 25 states in 2025 with 94% user satisfaction — was terminated by the Trump administration in November 2025, following intense industry pressure.

**The cost of complexity beyond compliance time:** Complexity generates errors, both inadvertent and deliberate. It creates a premium for tax professionals who can navigate ambiguity in ways favorable to clients. It creates multiple opportunities for aggressive positions that may or may not be sustained on audit. It burdens small businesses disproportionately, since they cannot afford the same level of tax advisory services as large corporations. It depresses compliance among those who find the process overwhelming.

### 2.5 Tax Expenditures — The Hidden Budget

Tax expenditures are provisions in the tax code that reduce tax liability for specific activities, industries, or taxpayers. They include deductions (mortgage interest, charitable contributions, SALT), exclusions (employer health insurance, municipal bond interest, life insurance buildup), and credits (EITC, child tax credit, R&D credit). The total cost of federal tax expenditures was approximately $1.9 trillion in 2024 — more than the US spends on Social Security, Medicare, or defense individually.

**Individual vs. corporate distribution:** Individual income tax expenditures: $1.7 trillion. Corporate tax expenditures: $188 billion. Of the individual expenditures, 82% of the benefits from the most commonly claimed deductions go to taxpayers with incomes over $200,000 per JCT estimates.

**The largest tax expenditures (selected):**
- Employer-sponsored health insurance exclusion: $5.9 trillion over 10 years (largest single expenditure)
- Capital gains preferential rates: ~$237 billion/year
- State and local tax deduction: $1.5 trillion over 10 years
- Mortgage interest deduction: $904 billion over 10 years
- Section 199A pass-through deduction: $700–980 billion over 10 years if extended
- Charitable contribution deduction: ~$65 billion/year

**Distributional analysis of key expenditures:**

*Mortgage interest deduction (MID):* The MID was originally justified as an incentive for homeownership. The evidence does not support this rationale. Countries without the deduction (Canada, UK, Australia) have homeownership rates similar to or higher than the US. The deduction benefits only those who itemize (a minority since the TCJA doubled the standard deduction) and is worth more in absolute terms to those in higher tax brackets. Eliminating or converting it to a uniform credit would improve equity.

*Employer health insurance exclusion:* This exclusion has the perverse effect of tying health insurance to employment, creating "job lock" (workers staying in jobs they would otherwise leave to keep coverage). It provides larger benefits to higher-income workers (in higher tax brackets). And it inflates healthcare consumption by making employer-provided coverage effectively cheaper than out-of-pocket spending, contributing to cost growth. Reform should move toward individual tax-neutral coverage options.

*Charitable deduction:* Benefits only those who itemize; worth more to those in higher brackets. Evidence on whether it actually increases charitable giving is mixed — economists estimate a "price elasticity of charitable giving" of roughly -1 (a 1% tax increase raises the after-tax cost and reduces giving by about 1%), suggesting the tax benefit roughly pays for itself in increased donations. But the primary beneficiaries are wealthy individuals funding foundations that may serve their interests as much as the public.

**How to evaluate tax expenditures:** Apply a simple test: if this were a spending program, would it be funded? Does the evidence show it achieves its stated policy goal? Who benefits? If the answers are "no," "mixed," and "primarily the wealthy," the expenditure should be reformed or eliminated.

### 2.6 Wealth Concentration and Tax Policy

**The relationship between tax policy and inequality:** Research by Emmanuel Saez and Gabriel Zucman (UC Berkeley) documents that the share of pre-tax income earned by the top 1% rose from 10% in 1978 to approximately 19% in 2018. The share of wealth owned by the top 0.1% rose from 7% in the late 1970s to approximately 18–20% in recent years. The bottom 90%'s wealth share fell from 35% in the early 1980s to approximately 25% today. Tax policy has contributed to this divergence through rate cuts on capital income and inheritance, reduced enforcement, and the accumulation of preferences for capital over labor.

**Estate tax erosion:** The estate tax was once a major backstop against multi-generational wealth concentration. The top estate tax rate was 77% in 1976. Through successive legislative increases in the exemption threshold, the estate tax has been progressively defanged:
- 2001: Estate tax exemption $675,000; top rate 55%
- 2011: Exemption $5 million, indexed to inflation; top rate 35%
- 2017 (TCJA): Exemption doubled to $10 million+ (inflation-adjusted; approximately $13.6 million per person in 2024)
- Current regime: Only about 0.2% of estates pay any estate tax

**The stepped-up basis:** This is arguably the single most expensive loophole in the tax code. When a person dies, their heirs receive a "stepped-up" cost basis equal to the fair market value at death — not the original purchase price. This means that any capital gains that accrued during the decedent's lifetime are permanently untaxed. The JCT estimates this costs $58 billion in forgone revenue in 2024 alone. Over 10 years, eliminating stepped-up basis would raise $500 billion or more. The benefit is concentrated: 56% of the stepped-up basis benefit in 2019 went to the top 20% of estates; the top 1% received $7 billion.

**Wealth taxes: evidence from Europe:** Several European countries experimented with net wealth taxes in the 1990s and 2000s, and most abolished them. France's solidarity tax on wealth (ISF) was abolished in 2018, replaced by a tax on real estate wealth only. The French experience suggested capital flight was relatively modest (representing 0.3–0.5% of revenues collected). The primary reasons for failure were: administrative complexity in valuing illiquid assets, capital mobility (wealthy individuals moved assets offshore or emigrated), and political opposition.

Norway's wealth tax has survived and generates meaningful revenue: NOK 1.7 million exemption (approximately USD 153,000), 1% rate, generating approximately €2.7 billion (1.5% of tax revenues). Only 0.01% of the richest households relocated after wealth tax reforms in Norway, Sweden, and Denmark — substantially less capital flight than critics predicted.

The consensus from the international evidence: a well-designed wealth tax can work but requires strong administrative capacity, information exchange with other countries, and a realistic assessment of the valuation challenges for illiquid assets. The Elizabeth Warren-style annual wealth tax (2% above $50 million, 3% above $1 billion) was analyzed by Saez and Zucman as potentially raising $200–300 billion per year; critics noted administrative and constitutional challenges.

## Part III: International Models — What Works

### 3.1 Top-Performing Tax Systems

**Nordic Model (Denmark, Sweden, Norway)**

The Nordic countries consistently deliver high revenue (tax-to-GDP ratios of 40–46%), high compliance, reasonable simplicity for most taxpayers, and strong social outcomes. Key features:

*Structure:* The "dual income tax" system separates labor income (taxed progressively) from capital income (taxed at a flat rate equal to or near the corporate rate). This reduces the incentive to convert labor income to capital income — a major source of US complexity and avoidance.

*Broad base:* Nordic countries rely heavily on broad-based taxes: VAT (25% in Denmark and Norway, 25% in Sweden), payroll/social contribution taxes, and property taxes. The broad base allows lower rates than would otherwise be needed.

*Compliance:* High compliance rates stem from: (1) pre-filled returns reducing filing friction; (2) high levels of third-party reporting; (3) strong social trust and norm of compliance; (4) vigorous enforcement. Citizens perceive a direct connection between taxes paid and services received, reinforcing compliance motivation.

*Pre-filled returns:* Sweden, Denmark, and Norway all offer pre-filled tax returns. Most taxpayers receive a completed return and need only confirm or correct the information. In practice, most citizens do nothing, or review digitally in minutes.

*Lesson for the US:* The dual income tax approach could resolve many US horizontal equity problems. Pre-filled returns are technically feasible (the IRS already has the data). Broad-based consumption taxes with equity offsets could supplement income taxes.

---

**Estonia: Digital-First Tax Administration**

Estonia tops the Tax Foundation's International Tax Competitiveness Index for the 11th consecutive year. Its tax features:

- 20% flat income tax rate (both personal and corporate)
- Corporate tax not levied on retained earnings — only on distributed profits (rewards reinvestment)
- e-Tax system: pre-filled returns, digital submission, typically 3–5 minutes to file (one-click confirmation available in many cases)
- 98%+ of tax filings submitted online
- Near-universal digital identity system enables seamless data integration

Estonia's approach is relevant not primarily for its rate structure (flat tax is not necessarily the right model for a country with greater inequality) but for its administrative innovation: the government collects the data, pre-fills the return, and makes compliance the path of least resistance.

---

**Germany: Anti-Avoidance Architecture**

Germany employs several robust anti-avoidance tools:
- General Anti-Avoidance Rule (Section 42, General Tax Code): applies to abusive tax structures
- Controlled Foreign Corporation (CFC) rules prevent passive income sheltering in low-tax subsidiaries
- Transfer pricing rules with strong documentation requirements
- Anti-treaty shopping rules deny treaty benefits where the principal purpose of an arrangement is obtaining treaty advantages
- Anti-hybrid rules (implemented 2014 onward) prevent mismatches from hybrid instruments

Germany's corporate tax system combines a federal corporate tax (15%) with a solidarity surcharge and trade tax, yielding an effective combined rate of approximately 30%. While this rate is on the higher end, Germany maintains strong enforcement capacity and significant anti-avoidance architecture.

---

**New Zealand: Clean Code, Simple Structure**

New Zealand is notable for having one of the world's cleanest tax codes — minimal expenditures, a comprehensive goods and services tax (GST) at 15%, no general capital gains tax (though some gains on property sold within 2 years are taxed), and relatively simple administration. The Inland Revenue Department (IRD) has implemented an "auto-calc" system where most employees and pension recipients receive pre-populated returns requiring no action.

New Zealand's absence of a capital gains tax is sometimes cited as evidence that such a tax is unnecessary; however, economists have long argued for its introduction, and various reform proposals have been advanced. The absence creates its own distortions (overinvestment in property relative to other assets).

The key lesson from New Zealand is that a tax code can be clean, have very few expenditures, and still adequately fund public services — if the few taxes it levies are broad-based and well-administered.

---

**Canada: Provincial Integration, Functional Administration**

Canada's federal tax system, administered by the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA), is more complex than the Nordic systems but substantially simpler than the US system. Key features:
- Federal-provincial tax integration reduces the compliance burden from multiple separate tax regimes
- Relatively fewer "extravagant" tax expenditures than the US
- GAAR (General Anti-Avoidance Rule) since 1988 under Section 245 of the Income Tax Act
- CRA administers both federal and most provincial income taxes through a single return

Canada represents a plausible intermediate model for the US: federal-state coordination of tax administration (currently US states maintain entirely separate tax systems), a functional GAAR, and moderate complexity.

---

### 3.2 Pre-Filled Returns: The Single Biggest UX Reform

**How it works:** The government already receives most of the data it needs to compute most taxpayers' liability. Employers report wages via W-2s. Banks report interest via 1099-INT. Brokers report dividends and sales via 1099-B and 1099-DIV. The IRS collects all of this information before the filing deadline. In a pre-filled system, the government simply computes the return from its own data, sends it to the taxpayer for review, and the taxpayer confirms, corrects, or supplements it.

**Countries that do it:**
- **Estonia**: 3–5 minutes average; one-click confirmation available; 98%+ digital filing
- **Sweden**: Most taxpayers receive a completed return; confirm via SMS or app
- **Denmark**: Pre-filled return mailed or available digitally; most taxpayers review in minutes
- **Spain**: "Borrador" (draft return) available for many taxpayers
- **UK (PAYE)**: Wage earners receive tax computed automatically through employer withholding; no annual return required for most workers

**Why the US hasn't done it:** The IRS has had the technical capability to offer pre-filled returns for decades. The barrier is not technical — it is political. The tax preparation industry (Intuit, H&R Block, TaxAct) spends tens of millions lobbying against any IRS filing capability. Their business model depends on complexity and friction. In 2002, the IRS struck a deal with the industry (the "Free File Alliance") under which the IRS agreed not to develop its own free filing option in exchange for industry commitments to offer free services to low-income taxpayers. The industry systematically undermined this commitment, steering eligible users to paid products.

**IRS Direct File:** Launched as a pilot in 12 states in 2024, Direct File allowed eligible taxpayers to file directly with the IRS for free. Over 140,000 users filed in 2024, saving an estimated $5.6 million in preparation fees. Expanded to 25 states in 2025 with broader income situations covered. Customer satisfaction: 94% "excellent" or "above average." The Trump administration terminated the program in November 2025 following intense industry lobbying. The program should be restored and expanded to all 50 states.

---

### 3.3 VAT and Consumption Taxes

**How VAT works:** A value-added tax is collected at each stage of production on the "value added" — the difference between a business's sales and its purchases from other businesses. Each business charges VAT on its sales and receives a credit for VAT paid on its inputs. This "invoice matching" system creates a paper trail that makes evasion difficult: each business in the chain has an incentive to demand invoices from suppliers (to claim the credit) and to report sales accurately.

**VAT vs. retail sales tax:** The US relies on retail sales taxes at the state level, which are collected only at the final sale. This creates significant evasion opportunities — there is only one point of enforcement. VAT, by contrast, enforces at every transaction in the supply chain, dramatically reducing evasion. This is one reason VAT evasion rates in Europe (the "VAT gap") are substantially lower than US sales tax evasion rates.

**The regressivity problem:** VAT is regressive when measured against current income: lower-income households spend a higher share of their income on consumption and thus bear a higher VAT burden as a share of income. However, when measured against lifetime income, the regressivity is less pronounced (since high-income individuals eventually spend their savings). Several mechanisms can address regressivity:
- Exempt essential goods (food, medicine, children's clothing)
- Provide a universal prebate or refundable credit equal to estimated VAT paid on essential consumption
- Pair with progressive income tax reductions

**Should the US adopt a VAT?** A 5% broad-based US federal VAT could raise approximately $160 billion per year. Arguments for: significant revenue, efficient, self-enforcing. Arguments against: adds a new tax layer rather than fixing existing problems, requires new administrative infrastructure, regressive without compensating measures. The strongest case for US VAT adoption is as a complement to a reformed income tax that closes existing loopholes — not as an alternative to income tax reform.

## Part IV: The Reformed System — Design Specifications

### 4.1 Individual Income Tax Reform

**Rate structure:** A reformed income tax should maintain progressivity while eliminating the distinction between income types. A single rate schedule applying to all income — wages, salaries, capital gains, dividends, carried interest, pass-through income, lottery winnings, inheritance — would eliminate the vast majority of planning opportunities that currently exist.

Evidence on optimal top rates: Saez (2001) estimated the revenue-maximizing top marginal rate at approximately 70%, though this estimate is sensitive to assumptions about behavioral responses. More conservative estimates place the revenue-maximizing rate at 50–60%. The current 37% top rate is not at the peak of the Laffer curve — there is room to raise top rates without reducing revenue, according to most econometric estimates.

A practical reformed structure: rates of 15%, 25%, 35%, and 45% on income above $500,000, with all income types taxed uniformly. The rates themselves are less important than the uniformity: eliminating the capital gains preference is more valuable than any specific rate change.

**Eliminating the capital gains preference:**
- Treat all long-term capital gains as ordinary income
- Address the lock-in effect through mark-to-market for liquid assets (publicly traded securities), which eliminates the incentive to hold appreciated positions to avoid realization
- Allow a modest inflation adjustment to cost basis to avoid taxing purely inflationary returns
- Phase in over 3–5 years to avoid sharp market adjustments

**Eliminating stepped-up basis:**
- Option A: Treat death as a realization event — the decedent is treated as having sold all assets at death, triggering capital gains tax. Allow generous exemptions for small estates, family farms, and closely held businesses
- Option B: Carryover basis — heirs inherit the decedent's original cost basis (no step-up); capital gains are taxed when the heir eventually sells
- Option A is more progressive and raises more revenue; Option B is simpler to administer
- Either option should include a generous exemption ($2–5 million) to protect small farmers and family businesses

**Simplifying deductions:**
- Dramatically expand the standard deduction (already doubled by TCJA — this is one TCJA provision that improved simplicity)
- Convert itemized deductions to uniform credits (equal value to all taxpayers regardless of bracket)
- Eliminate or cap the SALT deduction, which primarily benefits high-income residents of high-tax states
- Retain a simplified mortgage interest credit (capped, on primary residence only)
- Retain the EITC and Child Tax Credit as refundable credits, expanded

**Mark-to-market taxation:**
- Apply to taxpayers with publicly traded assets exceeding $100 million in value (the "Wyden proposal" threshold)
- Annual inclusion of unrealized gains on publicly traded securities
- Deferred treatment for illiquid assets (private companies, real estate) with an interest charge on the deferral to approximate mark-to-market economics
- Revenue estimate: the Wharton Budget Model estimated the Wyden proposal would raise approximately $360 billion over 10 years

**Universal pre-filled returns:** Restore and expand IRS Direct File to all 50 states. Invest in IRS technology to pre-populate returns from all available third-party data. For the approximately 70–80% of filers with straightforward situations (wages, standard deduction, a few common credits), pre-filled returns would eliminate the compliance burden entirely.

---

### 4.2 Corporate Tax Reform

**Optimal rate:** The worldwide average statutory corporate rate is 23.51% across 181 jurisdictions. Most analysis suggests a US rate of 25–28% balances revenue generation with international competitiveness. The current 21% rate is below the OECD average; restoring it to 25–28% would generate $500–886 billion over 10 years while remaining competitive with major trading partners.

**Corporate Alternative Minimum Tax (CAMT):** The Inflation Reduction Act enacted a 15% minimum tax on corporate "book income" (accounting profits reported to shareholders) for corporations with over $1 billion in average annual income. This partially closes the gap between reported taxable income and economic income. It should be maintained and strengthened — ideally with the threshold lowered and the rate increased to 20%.

**Country-by-country reporting:** Require large multinationals to publicly report revenues, profits, taxes paid, employees, and assets by country. This is the most powerful anti-avoidance tool that can be implemented without changing tax rates: it creates public accountability and enables journalists, civil society, and governments to identify profit-shifting arrangements. The EU has moved toward public country-by-country reporting; the US should match it.

**Anti-inversion rules:** Strengthen Treasury's existing regulatory authority to prevent inversions. An inversion should be treated as a taxable event to the extent assets are relocated offshore.

**Interest deductibility:** The current system allows unlimited deduction of interest expense, creating a tax bias toward debt financing. Limit interest deductibility to a percentage of earnings (EBITDA-based limitations, as implemented in Germany and as partially implemented in TCJA Section 163(j)) to reduce the debt bias and limit "earnings stripping" by foreign multinationals.

---

### 4.3 Wealth and Estate Tax Reform

**Estate tax reform:** The current exemption ($13.6 million per person in 2024, $27.2 million per couple) ensures that fewer than 0.2% of estates pay any estate tax. A reformed estate tax should:
- Reduce the exemption to $3.5 million per person ($7 million per couple), protecting genuine family wealth while taxing dynastic fortunes
- Raise rates from the current 40% to a progressive schedule reaching 55–65% above $50 million
- Eliminate stepped-up basis (as described above) — this is more important than the estate tax rate because it closes the realization escape hatch

**Annual wealth tax:** The evidence from Europe suggests that an annual wealth tax can work if well-designed but faces challenges: valuation of illiquid assets, potential capital flight, administrative complexity. A US wealth tax (Warren proposal: 2% above $50 million, 3% above $1 billion) is estimated to raise $200–300 billion annually. Constitutional questions about whether an unapportioned wealth tax is a "direct tax" requiring constitutional amendment remain contested; the Saez-Zucman analysis argues it is constitutional under existing doctrine; others disagree.

**Billionaire Minimum Income Tax:** The Biden proposal (25% minimum tax on income including unrealized gains for those with $100M+ in wealth) is a more politically defensible alternative that does not face the same constitutional questions as an annual wealth tax. It applies existing income tax principles (treating appreciation as income) to an extraordinarily wealthy class.

**Real estate reform:**
- Limit 1031 exchanges to deferral of up to $500,000 per taxpayer per year (Biden proposal); eliminate for gains above $1 million
- Reform depreciation rules to match actual economic depreciation rather than accelerated schedules
- REIT reform to close passive income sheltering structures

---

### 4.4 Anti-Avoidance Architecture

**General Anti-Avoidance Rule (GAAR):** The US lacks a statutory GAAR. The existing "economic substance doctrine" was codified in 2010 (Section 7701(o)) and the "substance over form" principle has been applied by courts, but neither provides the comprehensive protection of a true GAAR.

A GAAR for the US should:
- Apply when the principal purpose of a transaction is obtaining a tax benefit
- Allow the IRS to recharacterize the transaction to deny the benefit, regardless of technical compliance with specific statutory provisions
- Include meaningful penalties for transactions that fail GAAR scrutiny to deter aggressive planning
- Be coupled with a disclosure regime requiring reportable transactions to be disclosed upfront

Australia (since 1981), Canada (since 1988), and the UK (since 2013) all have GAARs. The UK GAAR applies to arrangements that "cannot reasonably be regarded as a reasonable course of action in relation to the relevant tax provisions." These rules have been effective at deterring the most aggressive avoidance structures.

**Beneficial ownership transparency:** The Corporate Transparency Act (2021) required most US companies to report their beneficial owners to FinCEN's database. In March 2025, the Trump administration gutted this requirement for domestic companies, exempting them entirely. This leaves anonymous shell companies available for tax fraud, money laundering, and illicit finance. Congress should restore the CTA's full scope and ensure public access to beneficial ownership information (as the EU's Anti-Money Laundering Directives require).

**Global coordination:** US participation in OECD BEPS 2.0 (including Pillar Two implementation through domestic legislation) is essential to prevent profit shifting. The US should enact the income inclusion rule domestically to ensure US-parented multinationals pay at least 15% globally, and should advocate for raising the minimum rate to 21% or higher.

---

### 4.5 Enforcement Reform

**IRS funding:** The IRS needs sustained, predictable funding of at least $14–15 billion per year (current appropriations are approximately $12 billion) to restore enforcement capacity. The Inflation Reduction Act appropriation of $80 billion over 10 years was a step in the right direction; its effective rollback by Congress and the Trump administration has set enforcement capacity back significantly. Every additional dollar in IRS enforcement funding generates approximately $5–12 in additional revenue depending on where it is directed.

**Audit rate rebalancing:** The current audit pattern is perverse — EITC recipients (low-income workers) are audited at rates comparable to or exceeding millionaires. A reformed audit program should:
- Dramatically increase audit rates for high-income individuals and large business returns
- Use data analytics to target returns with high audit potential
- Reduce correspondence audits of low-income filers that generate small recoveries and create hardship
- Rebuild the "Global High Wealth Industry" group that audits individuals with $10M+ in assets

**Third-party reporting expansion:**
- Cryptocurrency broker reporting (Form 1099-DA, effective 2025) — essential step but needs enforcement
- Lower the Form 1099-K threshold for payment app reporting (currently set at $5,000 for 2024, better than the previous $600 controversy)
- Require reporting for gig economy income, rental income (Airbnb-style platforms already required to report in many contexts)
- Strengthen requirements for professional service payments

**Cryptocurrency enforcement:** The IRS's identification of crypto compliance as a major challenge is well-founded. The mandatory broker reporting rules effective for 2025 are a start. Additional measures needed: wash sale rules applied to crypto (currently exempt), clarity on staking/DeFi transaction taxation, enhanced blockchain analytics capability.

**Offshore account reporting:** FATCA (Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act) requires foreign financial institutions to report US account holders to the IRS or face withholding penalties. The system has improved offshore transparency but has implementation gaps, particularly with smaller financial institutions and in countries that do not participate. Strengthening FATCA and automatic information exchange under the OECD Common Reporting Standard (CRS) is needed.

**Whistleblower program:** The IRS whistleblower program pays 15–30% of collected proceeds from information provided. The program has been criticized for slow processing times (cases take years to resolve) and inadequate processing of submissions. Reform should accelerate case processing, ensure consistent award payments, and provide legal protections against retaliation for whistleblowers.

---

### 4.6 Simplification

**Universal pre-filled returns:** As described above — the single most impactful simplification available. For the approximately 70% of filers with straightforward situations, pre-filled returns would reduce compliance time from hours to minutes.

**Consolidate business entity taxation:** The current system has a bewildering variety of business tax regimes: C-corporations (subject to corporate tax), S-corporations, partnerships (two types: general and limited), LLCs (taxed as corporations, partnerships, or disregarded entities depending on elections), sole proprietorships. Each regime has different rates, deduction rules, and compliance requirements. A simplified regime would:
- Apply a single rate to all business income at the entity level
- Pass through remaining income to individuals at ordinary rates
- Eliminate the Section 199A deduction and the complexity it creates

**Eliminate the Alternative Minimum Tax:** The individual AMT was designed to ensure wealthy taxpayers paid some minimum amount of tax. After TCJA dramatically raised the AMT exemption amounts, the AMT now hits primarily upper-middle-income taxpayers (those with many dependents or high state taxes) rather than the truly wealthy it was designed for. Replace it with a minimum tax on economic income (the CAMT for corporations and the Billionaire Minimum Income Tax for individuals are steps in this direction).

**Large standard deduction:** Maintain and increase the standard deduction to the point that almost no ordinary filer needs to itemize. This simplifies compliance enormously and reduces the premium on having sophisticated tax help.

## Part V: Action Items — What Citizens and Legislators Can Do

### Legislative Actions (Require Congress)

**1. Eliminate stepped-up basis at death**
- **What it is:** Remove the rule that resets an asset's cost basis to fair market value at death, eliminating all accumulated unrealized gains from taxation
- **Revenue/equity impact:** $500+ billion over 10 years; primarily affects the top 1% of estates
- **Who opposes:** Estate planning industry, wealthy family interests, real estate industry
- **Current status:** Proposed repeatedly; failed to advance in Build Back Better (2021). No current legislative vehicle.

**2. Treat capital gains as ordinary income above $1M**
- **What it is:** Apply regular income tax rates to capital gains for taxpayers earning over $1 million in total income
- **Revenue/equity impact:** ~$250B+ over 10 years; affects top 0.1%
- **Who opposes:** Investment industry, private equity, venture capital
- **Current status:** Various proposals; no current vehicle

**3. IRS funding — fully restore and expand enforcement budget**
- **What it is:** Provide sustained, inflation-adjusted funding to the IRS at $14-15B/year to restore pre-2010 enforcement capacity
- **Revenue/equity impact:** $395B+ over 10 years in recovered gross taxes; positive ROI of approximately 5:1 to 12:1 depending on enforcement target
- **Who opposes:** Anti-government spending groups; those who benefit from non-enforcement
- **Current status:** IRA appropriation gutted; current enforcement capacity severely diminished

**4. Implement universal pre-filled returns (restore and expand IRS Direct File)**
- **What it is:** Restore the Direct File program, expand to all 50 states, and build pre-populated returns from existing third-party data
- **Revenue/equity impact:** Reduces non-filing gap; saves $5.6B+ in compliance costs; improves equity (reduces advantage of tax professionals)
- **Who opposes:** Intuit, H&R Block, TaxAct, and their lobbying allies
- **Current status:** Program terminated November 2025; would require legislative mandate to insulate from future executive action

**5. Close carried interest loophole (~$12–18B/year)**
- **What it is:** Require investment fund managers to pay ordinary income tax rates on carried interest compensation, not capital gains rates
- **Revenue/equity impact:** $12–18 billion per year; entirely concentrated on very high earners
- **Who opposes:** Private equity, hedge funds, venture capital — have spent over a decade killing this proposal
- **Current status:** Has died in Congress repeatedly; survived IRA negotiations only because Manchin dropped it

**6. Implement mark-to-market / Billionaire Minimum Income Tax (25%)**
- **What it is:** Require taxpayers with $100M+ in wealth to pay a minimum 25% rate on income inclusive of unrealized capital gains
- **Revenue/equity impact:** ~$360B over 10 years (Wharton estimate)
- **Who opposes:** Ultra-wealthy individuals, investment industry
- **Current status:** Biden proposed; never enacted

**7. Lower estate tax exemption, raise rates**
- **What it is:** Reduce the per-person exemption from ~$13.6M to $3.5M; raise top rates to 55–65% on very large estates
- **Revenue/equity impact:** Significant; primarily affects the top 0.2% of estates
- **Who opposes:** Wealthy families, estate planning industry, farm/family business lobbies (though most actual farms and family businesses would be unaffected by a $3.5M exemption)
- **Current status:** No current vehicle; TCJA exemption is scheduled to revert to lower levels in 2026 absent legislative extension

**8. Country-by-country public reporting for large corporations**
- **What it is:** Require corporations over a revenue threshold to publicly disclose revenues, profits, taxes paid, employees, and assets by country
- **Revenue/equity impact:** Systemic; creates transparency that deters avoidance and enables enforcement
- **Who opposes:** Large multinationals, Chamber of Commerce
- **Current status:** EU has moved toward public CbCR; US lags

**9. Strengthen GAAR / economic substance doctrine**
- **What it is:** Enact a statutory General Anti-Avoidance Rule giving IRS authority to deny tax benefits from transactions whose principal purpose is tax avoidance
- **Revenue/equity impact:** Systemic; prevents category of avoidance schemes; difficult to quantify but substantial
- **Who opposes:** Tax planning industry, corporate tax departments
- **Current status:** Economic substance doctrine codified 2010; no comprehensive GAAR

**10. Mandatory beneficial ownership disclosure with enforcement**
- **What it is:** Restore and strengthen the Corporate Transparency Act's requirements for domestic entities to report beneficial owners; ensure public access
- **Revenue/equity impact:** Enables enforcement of existing law; systemic anti-avoidance and anti-money-laundering effect
- **Who opposes:** Privacy advocates, anonymous ownership interests
- **Current status:** CTA gutted for domestic companies in March 2025; needs restoration

### Administrative Actions (President/IRS Can Do Without Congress)

**1. Restore and expand IRS Direct File to all 50 states immediately**
The prior administration terminated Direct File via administrative action. The next administration committed to tax simplification can restore it the same way. The technical infrastructure exists. The program had 94% user satisfaction.

**2. Audit rate rebalancing: shift toward high-income and high-wealth audits**
The IRS Commissioner has significant discretion over how audit resources are allocated. Explicitly directing resources toward high-income, high-wealth, and large corporate returns — rather than correspondence audits of EITC claimants — does not require legislation.

**3. Increase whistleblower awards and processing speed**
IRS whistleblower program management is largely administrative. Setting target processing times, ensuring consistent award payment, and publicizing the program to potential informants can be accomplished without legislative change.

**4. Cryptocurrency reporting enforcement with existing rules**
The broker reporting rules now in effect for 2025 provide a legal basis for enforcement. The IRS should actively audit cryptocurrency traders using existing blockchain analytics tools and the new 1099-DA data.

**5. Enforce existing economic substance rules more aggressively**
The codified economic substance doctrine (Section 7701(o)) has been underused. Aggressive application to identified tax shelter transactions, combined with the associated penalties, would deter planning that currently proceeds because the IRS rarely invokes the doctrine.

### Citizen Actions

**1. Use IRS Direct File or Free File (if restored) — not commercial tax software**
If and when Direct File is restored, using it directly serves your interest, saves money, and builds political support for the program. Even under the current regime, the IRS Free File program (through the Free File Alliance) remains available for those under the income threshold — though industry has systematically made it hard to find.

**2. Support organizations fighting for tax fairness**
Key organizations doing credible research and advocacy:
- **Institute on Taxation and Economic Policy (ITEP)**: Analysis of federal and state tax policy with distributional focus
- **Tax Policy Center (Urban-Brookings)**: Nonpartisan analysis; the gold standard for tax modeling
- **Americans for Tax Fairness**: Advocacy organization focused on corporate and wealth tax reform
- **Tax Justice Network**: International focus on offshore tax avoidance
- **ProPublica**: Investigative journalism that has broken major tax stories (the "Billionaires' Tax Returns" series)

**3. Demand public disclosure of large political donors' tax compliance**
Citizens and advocacy organizations can pressure Congress to require disclosure of effective tax rates for major political donors — creating accountability that currently does not exist.

**4. Contact representatives specifically about carried interest and stepped-up basis**
These are the two provisions with the clearest case for reform, the highest equity impact, and the strongest legislative history of near-success. Constituent contact specifically naming these provisions sends a signal that the public understands and cares about them.

**5. Support public country-by-country reporting at state level**
State legislatures can require corporations doing business in the state to file country-by-country reports. State pension funds can use shareholder power to demand voluntary public CbCR from portfolio companies. California and other large states have used this approach effectively on environmental disclosure.

**6. Demand state-level transparency laws for LLC beneficial ownership**
Many states (Delaware, Wyoming, Nevada) allow LLCs to be formed with no public record of ownership. State-level beneficial ownership requirements would complement federal reforms and close domestic anonymity gaps.

### Economic Growth Effects

The evidence on tax reform and economic growth shows:

- **Progressive rates do not strongly suppress growth.** Research using OECD data finds no clear relationship between top marginal rates and GDP growth rates. The US grew faster in the 1950s–1960s (when top rates were 70–91%) than in the 1980s–2000s (after rate cuts). Natural experiments (comparing states with and without income taxes, periods before and after rate changes) show effects are real but small.

- **Base-broadening is growth-neutral or positive.** Eliminating the lock-in effect (through mark-to-market), reducing the debt bias (through interest limitation), and closing planning opportunities (through GAAR) improve resource allocation without suppressing aggregate activity.

- **Enforcement spending has highly positive returns.** Every additional dollar of IRS enforcement spending yields $5–12 in revenue; there is no evidence it suppresses growth by affecting legitimate economic activity.

- **Complexity suppresses growth.** The 6 billion hours spent on tax compliance is a real economic cost. Simplification (pre-filled returns, fewer deductions) releases resources for productive use.

### International Competitiveness

The most frequent objection to corporate tax increases is that they will drive investment and headquarters to lower-tax jurisdictions. The evidence:

- **Pillar Two significantly reduces this concern.** With 90% of in-scope multinationals subject to a 15% floor, the range of competition has narrowed. Companies cannot escape to zero-tax jurisdictions as easily as before.
- **The US rate of 21% is already below the OECD average of 23.51%.** Raising to 25–28% would bring it into the middle of the range, not above it.
- **Effective rates matter more than statutory rates.** With strong anti-avoidance rules, a 25% statutory rate with a broad base may be more competitive (in terms of actual tax paid) than a 21% rate with Swiss-cheese deductions.

### Dynamic Scoring

"Dynamic scoring" attempts to account for behavioral and macroeconomic responses to tax changes when estimating revenue. The evidence on dynamic effects:

- **Rate cuts rarely "pay for themselves."** The TCJA's corporate rate cut was projected to lose $919 billion; even optimistic dynamic estimates reduced this only modestly. The promised investment boom did not materialize as predicted.
- **Enforcement spending does "pay for itself."** Every dollar of enforcement spending recovers multiple dollars.
- **Base-broadening reforms have near-neutral dynamic effects.** They raise the same revenue regardless of behavioral change because they tax a broader set of economic activity.

### Glossary of Tax Avoidance Techniques

**Stepped-up basis:** The rule that resets an asset's cost basis to fair market value at death, permanently eliminating tax on capital gains accrued during the decedent's lifetime.

**Carried interest:** The share of investment fund profits (typically 20%) paid to general partners as compensation, currently taxed at capital gains rates rather than ordinary income rates.

**Transfer pricing:** The prices charged between related entities (e.g., subsidiaries of the same multinational) for goods, services, and intellectual property. Manipulation of transfer prices shifts profits to low-tax jurisdictions.

**GAAR (General Anti-Avoidance Rule):** A statutory provision allowing tax authorities to disregard or recharacterize transactions whose principal purpose is obtaining a tax benefit, regardless of technical compliance with specific rules.

**BEPS (Base Erosion and Profit Shifting):** The OECD's term for multinational tax avoidance strategies that exploit mismatches in international tax rules to shift profits to low-tax jurisdictions.

**CFC rules (Controlled Foreign Corporation):** Rules that treat passive income earned by a foreign subsidiary as currently taxable to the parent company, preventing sheltering of passive income offshore.

**Mark-to-market:** Taxation of unrealized gains on an annual basis, treating appreciated assets as if sold at year-end. Currently applied to securities dealers and proposed for ultra-wealthy taxpayers.

**Economic substance doctrine:** The principle that a transaction must have economic substance beyond its tax consequences to be respected for tax purposes. Codified in Section 7701(o) of the Internal Revenue Code in 2010.

**Check-the-box regulations:** IRS rules allowing entities to elect how they are classified for tax purposes. Multinationals use these rules to create "tax nothing" structures where subsidiary income is treated as earned directly by a parent while local source rules do not recognize the income there.

**Inversion:** A transaction in which a US corporation reincorporates in a foreign jurisdiction while maintaining its operational headquarters in the US, to access lower foreign tax rates.

**Treaty shopping:** Routing transactions through a country with favorable tax treaties to access treaty benefits not otherwise available.

**Pillar Two / Global Minimum Tax:** The OECD/G20 agreement establishing a 15% minimum effective corporate income tax rate for multinationals with revenue over €750 million, with "top-up taxes" applicable in the parent company's country if subsidiaries are taxed below 15%.

**FATCA (Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act):** US law requiring foreign financial institutions to report US account holders to the IRS or face withholding penalties, designed to combat offshore tax evasion.

**Thin capitalization:** A technique where a multinational funds its subsidiaries in high-tax countries through debt (from a low-tax affiliate) rather than equity, generating interest deductions in the high-tax country and interest income in the low-tax country.
