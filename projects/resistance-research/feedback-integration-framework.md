## Overview

This document maps expected institutional adaptation patterns, builds sector-specific feedback loops, and establishes version control protocols for the 35-domain Democratic Renewal Framework post-distribution. It assumes Phase 1 distribution is complete or imminent and that the user needs operational infrastructure — not a monitoring wish list — to sustain the framework's institutional life after distribution.

The central finding from the historical record is direct: frameworks that survive are frameworks with active feedback loops. The Model Penal Code was actively maintained through ALI annual meetings where practitioners reported modifications and the chief reporter adjudicated them. The ABA Model Rules are updated through continuous commentary cycles. The Brennan Center's automatic voter registration advocacy succeeded because the Center ran annual adoption surveys, updated state-by-state scorecards, and fed those results back into state advocacy targeting. The 35-domain framework requires equivalent infrastructure.

This document does not duplicate the operational tracking machinery in existing adoption frameworks. It provides the connective architecture: how adaptation data flows from institutions back into framework development, what counts as feedback worth responding to versus noise to filter, and how version control prevents the framework from fragmenting as adoption scales.

## Section 1: Institutional Adaptation Patterns by Sector

### 1.1 State Attorneys General

State AGs are the fastest-converting institutional sector — they produce publicly verifiable outputs (court filings, press releases, coalition memos) on timelines measured in days to weeks, not months. AGs adapt frameworks by extracting constitutional argument structures they can cite at the preliminary injunction standard, not by endorsing a framework's analytical architecture.

**Expected adaptation pattern**: Selective extraction from Domains 33 (state constitutional elections clause analysis), 34 (Impoundment Control Act enforcement), 37 (federal election interference documentation), and 6 (circuit filing strategy for judicial independence claims). AGs will strip international law comparisons because most federal circuits treat foreign law as non-persuasive. The domestic precedent sections, statutory text analysis, and empirical evidence on administrative effects are the durable citables.

State-specific constitutional provisions drive divergence. Colorado's Taxpayer Bill of Rights creates different fiscal authority arguments than Arizona's legislature. Michigan AGs will modify Domain 33's ballot initiative suppression analysis using Article II, Section 9 of the Michigan Constitution.

**Constraint**: AGs rarely operate outside 1-3 core litigation areas per session. The realistic adoption unit is a domain cluster, not the full framework.

**Feedback signal priority**: Democracy Docket case tracker, state AG press release feeds, PACER full-text search for framework terminology. Each AG filing using Domain 33/34/37 analysis — even in footnotes — is adoption regardless of explicit citation.

### 1.2 Law Schools and Legal Clinics

Law schools operate on semester-cycle logic: clinical adoption is fastest (6-12 weeks), faculty adoption is medium (4-16 weeks), law review publication is slowest (12-24 months).

**Expected adaptation pattern**: Domain selection per clinic focus area. Immigration clinics extract Domains 16 and 29. Voting rights clinics extract Domains 1, 33, and 37. Constitutional seminars extract Domains 6 and 35.

**Timing constraint**: May distribution reaches curriculum decisions for Fall 2026 only if it reaches instructors by mid-June — a hard six-week window.

**Feedback signal priority**: Google Scholar alerts for domain-specific terminology; SSRN new uploads in constitutional law; annual December survey to clinic directors.

### 1.3 Think Tanks and Policy Organizations

Think tanks decompose the 35-domain framework into sector-specific briefs in 2-6 weeks. The cross-domain synthesis — the framework's claim that multiple domains constitute a unified ecosystem — will rarely survive decomposition.

**Constraint**: Publication pipeline (4-6 weeks), existing donor agenda, and political feasibility filter compress options.

**Feedback signal priority**: Subscribe to publication mailing lists; Overton.io citation database check at Month 7, 12, and annually; congressional testimony monitoring.

### 1.4 Civil Rights and Advocacy Organizations

Advocacy organizations adapt frameworks horizontally — extracting across domains for a specific current crisis — rather than adopting comprehensively.

**Expected adaptation pattern**: Domain prioritization per campaign ladder. Common Cause prioritizes Domain 34. Protect Democracy prioritizes Domain 37. Budget and staff capacity set realistic scope.

**Feedback signal priority**: Campaign launch press releases, member education webinars, social media monitoring for framework terminology.

### 1.5 Media and Investigative Journalists

Journalists adopt analytical frameworks as interpretive lenses without citation. The adoption signal is vocabulary migration: when journalists start using framework analytical categories in their reporting.

**Expected adaptation pattern**: Use as reporting checklist, source map for expert contacts, and cross-domain storytelling framework.

**Feedback signal priority**: Google Alerts for coined terms; Muck Rack journalist byline monitoring; conference announcement tracking.

## Section 2: Sector-Specific Feedback Mechanisms

**State AGs (weekly)**: PACER full-text search alerts; Democracy Docket monitoring; state AG press release feeds. Query: "state elections clause" AND "federal interference"; "impoundment control act" AND "appropriations."

**Law schools (semester)**: Annual December curriculum survey (5 questions) to clinic directors at Tier 1 contacts. Target 50% response. Supplement with SSRN and Google Scholar monitoring.

**Think tanks (monthly)**: Subscribe to publication mailing lists. Overton.io check at Month 7, 12+. Congressional testimony monitoring via Congress.gov.

**Advocacy organizations (event-driven)**: Monitor campaign launches (press releases, funding appeals). Quarterly check-in calls with rotating 20% sample of contacts.

**Journalists (alert-based)**: Google Alerts (daily digest). Muck Rack daily monitoring. Monthly manual review of investigative pieces.

**Academic researchers (quarterly)**: SSRN new uploads in relevant fields; academic conference announcements (AALS, APSA, Law and Society).

## Section 3: Post-Distribution Adaptation Timeline

**Days 1-7**: Distribution complete. Week 1 success signal: unprompted secondary distribution (contact sharing with peer network without being asked). In Rogers' diffusion framework, this is the innovator-to-early-adopter transition.

**Weeks 2-4**: Early adopters engage. EFF, ACLU, Just Security, state AG policy counsel already in litigation coalitions will engage within 72 hours. Vocabulary migration signals appear in public outputs within 2-4 weeks if adoption is real.

**Month 2-3**: Secondary diffusion. Labor research departments operate on 60-90 day cycles. Academic programs on semester cycle: May distribution → Fall 2026 syllabi if received by mid-June. Month 2-3 threshold: 2+ think tank publications citing framework + 1 law school clinic confirming use + 5+ organizations using domain language publicly.

**Month 4-6**: Institutional integration. Clinical case use appears. Formal policy proposals incorporating framework language in bill findings sections. Month 4-6 threshold: 1 confirmed litigation reference + 2 formal policy proposals + 15 organizations integrating domain materials into standing research.

**Month 7-12**: Full ecosystem adaptation. NGO campaigns informed by framework (3-4 month design window). Academic courses (Fall 2027 semester). Investigative series (6-12 month production). Month 7-12 threshold: see Section 9.

**Year 2+**: Long-tail adoption. State legislatures. Local governments. International adaptation.

## Section 4: Feedback Loop Architecture

Three feedback tiers operate simultaneously at different frequencies and depths.

**Tier 1 — Immediate, low-friction**: 1-question form ("Which domain was most valuable?") on GitHub/Gist. Email reply tracking. Social media monitoring via daily Google Alerts for coined terms. Expected response: 5-10% of recipients. Easy to implement, high volume, low depth.

**Tier 2 — Structured, moderate-friction**: Monthly rotating contact check-in with 20% sample (10-15 organizations/month). Quarterly sectoral surveys. Annual impact assessment. Medium implementation cost (4-6 hours/month). Medium volume, medium depth.

**Tier 3 — Research-grade, high-depth**: Semi-structured interviews with 5-10 adopters at Month 6 and 12. Case study narratives (1-2/year). Attribution analysis (Month 12). High implementation cost (15-20 hours per interview set; 8-12 hours per case study). Low volume, very high depth.

## Section 5: Adaptation Quality Assessment

Not all adaptation is equal. High-quality adaptations preserve the framework's cross-domain interconnectedness logic.

**High-quality signals**: References specific domains by number and structure. Preserves cross-domain logic (Domain 33 + Domain 37 as connected constitutional argument). Cites framework with specificity. Identifies a gap or error (critique = deep reading).

**Medium-quality signals**: References framework conceptually without domain precision. Reduces analytical depth for sector audience. Internal-only adoption (staff training, memos). Genuine adoption, less visible impact.

**Low-quality signals**: Cherry-picks single domain without preserving logic. Uses terminology as labels without analytical content. Most dangerous: extracting Domain 37 (election interference) while ignoring Domains 1, 33, 34 that provide analytical context.

**Measurement**: At Month 6, code 20-item sample of public outputs from recipients. Two coders independently rate on 3-point quality scale. Indicators: domain specificity, structural preservation, analytical depth. Track trend Month 6→12. Sharp quality decline signals the core analytical logic is not surviving decomposition and requires companion explainer document.

## Section 6: Feedback to Version Control Protocol

**Domain currency marking**: Each domain carries "current through [date]" stamp. Major developments trigger "Domain Update" supplement rather than full rewrite. Supplement appended with dated notation: "Section [X] updated [date] for [development]." Preserves analytical architecture while keeping facts current.

**Change request tracking**: Sector-specific request log. "Law School Requests: Expand Domain 3 curriculum (3 requests); rural clinic options (2 requests). AG Requests: Colorado ballot initiative constitutional analysis (1 request)." Track by sector and domain. Requests with 2+ Tier 1 contacts trigger Phase 2 research.

**Release cadence**: Rolling updates within 30 days of feedback clustering (2+ contacts identifying same gap). Version naming: Domain-1-v1 (original), Domain-1-v1.1 (minor), Domain-1-v2 (major). Version included in header.

**Backward compatibility**: Older versions permanently archived. Institution citing Domain-1-v1 can still access in December when v2 is released. Change log explains what changed and why.

## Section 7: Attribution Methodology

The core attribution problem: the framework distributes into space where hundreds of organizations were already working on every domain. Attribution is contribution analysis, not binary causation.

**Test 1 — Vocabulary marker**: Count framework-specific terminology in post-distribution outputs. Coined terms distinctive to framework ("appellate capture timeline," not "voter suppression"). Baseline: pre-distribution vocabulary analysis to confirm terms did not exist before.

**Test 2 — Structural convergence**: Institution uses framework's specific analytical structure (Domain 34's four-mechanism taxonomy in same taxonomic sequence) without citing it. Captures adoptions that vocabulary alone misses.

**Test 3 — Timing-and-contact**: Output appears after documented contact, within plausible processing window? 2-8 weeks practitioners, 4-16 weeks academia, 6-12 weeks think tanks. Pre-distribution output rules out framework. Output outside window (6+ months) may represent independent development.

**Counterfactual baseline**: Model Penal Code achieved 2.4 states/year; Brennan Center AVR achieved 1.9 states/year. Realistic 6-month target: 15-25% of recipients showing adoption signals. 30%+ at 6 months would represent anomalously fast adoption warranting intensive Phase 2 response.

## Section 8: Risks — Misadaptation and Drift

**Risk 1**: Cherry-picking Domain 37 without Domains 1, 33, 34. **Mitigation**: Tier 2 survey asks "Which domains prioritized?" Single-domain adoption triggers outreach with interconnectedness brief before campaign launch.

**Risk 2**: Feedback clustering drives premature major version. **Mitigation**: Release curriculum guide as companion document, not domain modification. Preserve citation infrastructure.

**Risk 3**: Plain-language gap at Month 2-3. **Mitigation**: Tier 2 Month 3 survey includes accessibility question. 3+ organizations reporting same problem → 1-page plain language summary (companion doc, not core modification).

**Risk 4**: Viral distribution creates overwhelming feedback volume. **Mitigation**: Implement automated tiering if volume exceeds 200/month. Institutional adopters → Tier 2; individuals → public feedback board. Top 5 requests released monthly.

**Risk 5**: Quality degradation through secondary citation. **Mitigation**: When domain misrepresented in citations, clarification note distributed to contacts: "For users of Domain 29's SPLC analysis, note Section 4.2 characterizes bank fraud/Thompson as contested legal argument with strong merit, not settled conclusion."

## Section 9: Success Criteria — 6-Month and 12-Month Checkpoints

**6-month post-distribution** (target: ~November 2026)

- Tier 1 feedback: ≥10% of recipients provide feedback
- Institutional adoption: ≥20% of institutional recipients demonstrate adoption via public output
- Policy outputs: ≥2 policy briefs citing domain analysis; ≥1 law school clinic using materials; ≥3 legal briefs citing framework; vocabulary marker test ≥15% in coded sample
- Attribution: ≥2 high-confidence attribution events; ≥1 case study narrative

**12-month post-distribution** (target: ~May 2027)

- Tier 1 feedback: ≥20% of recipients
- Institutional adoption: ≥40% of institutional recipients
- Policy outputs: ≥5 policy briefs; ≥5 law school courses/clinics; ≥10 legal briefs; ≥2 major campaigns
- Structural convergence: ≥25% of coded sample aligns with framework logic
- Attribution: Counterfactual comparison to MPC baseline (2.4-state/year historical). If adoption rate below baseline, triggers Tier 3 investigation of barriers (not failure conclusion).
