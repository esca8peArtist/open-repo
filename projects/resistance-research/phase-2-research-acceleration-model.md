# Phase 2 Research Acceleration Model

**Prepared: May 5, 2026**

This document answers the capacity and throughput questions that the priority matrix in `phase-2-domain-research-priority-matrix.md` cannot: How fast can domain research be completed? Can multiple domains run in parallel? Which domains need deep subject-matter expertise vs. general research capacity? When does it make sense to outsource to external researchers? What happens to Phase 2 research if Phase 1 moves slower than expected?

---

## Part I: Research Capacity Assessment

### Baseline Production Capacity

The orchestrator's demonstrated research capacity, based on completed domain documents:

- **Standard production domain**: 8,000–10,000 words with 40–60 citations, fully sourced to primary materials, with international comparative evidence and a tiered reform architecture. Examples: Domain 29 (644 lines, 16 sections, April 29 completion), Domain 37 (543 lines, full sourcing, Hungary comparative evidence integrated).
- **Estimated research hours per domain**: 10–16 hours, depending on complexity.
- **Practical production pace**: One full domain per research session at standard complexity; two domains per session only if they share significant source infrastructure (e.g., Domain 37 and Domain 37b draw on the same CISA and PACER source base).

**Current session output rate**: The ITEM50 analysis (nine candidates, three full roadmaps) was completed in a single session. Full domain research documents (the equivalent of a production domain) require approximately one full session each at medium complexity. At the high end of the complexity range (Domain 46, Tribal Sovereignty — 14–18 hours), a full domain requires 1–2 sessions.

### Token Budget Per Domain

Token budget estimates are approximate and depend on source accessibility and synthesis complexity.

| Domain Complexity Level | Representative Candidates | Estimated Token Investment | Sessions Required |
|------------------------|---------------------------|---------------------------|-------------------|
| Low (8–12 hours) | Domain 43 (Civil Asset Forfeiture) | ~15,000–20,000 tokens | 1 session |
| Medium (10–14 hours) | Domain 40 (Epistemic), Domain 44 (Labor) | ~20,000–28,000 tokens | 1 session |
| Medium-High (12–16 hours) | Domain 39 (Reproductive), Domain 41 (Transitional Justice) | ~25,000–35,000 tokens | 1–2 sessions |
| High (14–18 hours) | Domain 46 (Tribal Sovereignty) | ~30,000–42,000 tokens | 2 sessions |

**Parallel research cost**: Running two medium-complexity domains in a single session increases token investment by approximately 60–80% relative to one domain (not 100%, because setup, framing, and source-gathering work overlaps). Two domains can share a session only when: (a) they draw on overlapping source infrastructure; or (b) they serve the same institutional distribution audience and the research agenda benefits from cross-domain consistency.

**Practical limit**: Two domains per session maximum, and only at low-to-medium complexity. High-complexity domains should always occupy their own dedicated session.

## Part II: Parallel Research Strategy

### When Parallel Domains Make Sense

Parallel domain research is justified when two conditions are both true:
1. The two domains share institutional audience or source infrastructure, creating efficiency gains.
2. Phase 1 demand signals from multiple domains are converging — meaning waiting to sequence them serially would delay multiple audiences simultaneously.

**Candidate parallel pairings** (based on shared sources and audiences):

**Pairing A — Domain 39 + Domain 41 (Reproductive Rights + Transitional Justice)**
- Shared audience: Civil rights litigation community, congressional Judiciary Democrats, law school constitutional law clinics.
- Shared source infrastructure: Brennan Center, Center for Reproductive Rights, Lawyers' Committee, Democracy Docket.
- Shared analytical frame: Democratic responsiveness failure — both domains analyze situations where majoritarian democratic mechanisms were defeated by institutional capture.
- Efficiency gain: International comparative work (Ireland's citizens' assembly for Domain 39; South Africa TRC for Domain 41) can be researched in a single sourcing session.
- **Recommendation**: If both are in the Month 1–2 priority tier, run them in parallel. If only one is demanded by Phase 1 signals, run serially.

**Pairing B — Domain 40 + Domain 44 (Epistemic Infrastructure + Labor Power)**
- Shared audience: Think tanks (Brennan Center, EPI, Knight Foundation), academic contacts.
- Shared source infrastructure: EPI's research on union density and economic inequality; Knight Foundation's journalism and democracy work.
- Analytical connection: Both are democratic participation erosion domains — epistemic infrastructure collapse and union density collapse are parallel mechanisms by which working-class political voice has been structurally suppressed.
- Efficiency gain: Limited; source overlap is real but not extensive.
- **Recommendation**: Run serially unless Phase 1 generates simultaneous demand signals for both.

**Pairing C — Domain 42 + Domain 43 (Drug Policy + Civil Asset Forfeiture)**
- Shared audience: Criminal justice reform community (ACLU Criminal Law, Brennan Center, Sentencing Project, Institute for Justice).
- Shared source infrastructure: Prison Policy Initiative, ACLU criminal law research, sentencing and disenfranchisement data.
- Analytical connection: Both address carceral state as a democratic legitimacy problem; both have bipartisan reform coalitions.
- Efficiency gain: Significant — the democratic legitimacy frame and the disenfranchisement evidence base overlap substantially.
- **Recommendation**: If the DEA hearing window (June 29) demands Domain 42 be completed first, produce Domain 43 in the same research wave (different session, but within the same 2–3 week window) to build out the criminal justice reform cluster.

### When Parallel Research Fails

Parallel research produces quality failures when:
- Domains require specialized literatures with no overlap (Domain 46 Tribal Sovereignty should never be paired with anything — it requires deep immersion in federal Indian law that is not transferable).
- The token budget per domain is compressed below what the domain actually requires (a rushed Domain 41 that omits the South Africa TRC comparative analysis or the Georgia prosecution status is not production-ready).
- Phase 1 demand signals have not confirmed both domains — running parallel research on domains without demand signal confirmation wastes production capacity that could be directed at domains where audiences are waiting.

**Rule**: Never parallelize a high-complexity domain. Never parallelize more than two domains in any session. Always require Phase 1 demand confirmation before a second parallel domain is added to a research session.

## Part III: Subject-Matter Expert Requirements

Not all Phase 2 domains can be researched effectively with general policy research capacity. Some require engagement with specialized legal or academic literatures where the failure mode is not incomplete sourcing but mischaracterization of specialized doctrine or evidence.

### General Research Capacity (Sufficient)

The following domains can be researched effectively with general policy research capacity (the orchestrator's current demonstrated capability):

| Domain | Why General Research Suffices |
|--------|------------------------------|
| Domain 39 — Reproductive Rights | Primary sources (KFF dashboard, Center for Reproductive Rights tracker, WHPA text) are well-organized and accessible. Constitutional law synthesis is at standard appellate level, not specialized academic subfield. |
| Domain 40 — Epistemic Infrastructure | Primary crisis documentation is recent and publicly accessible. First Amendment analysis is at standard constitutional level. EU comparative work is well-documented. |
| Domain 42 — Drug Policy | Primary sources (DOJ rescheduling order, MPP tracker, Sentencing Project data) are organized and accessible. Constitutional law (Gonzales v. Raich) is established precedent. |
| Domain 43 — Civil Asset Forfeiture | Institute for Justice has organized the primary research comprehensively. Timbs v. Indiana SCOTUS precedent is recent and accessible. This is the lowest-complexity domain in the set. |
| Domain 44 — Labor Power | EPI, OnLabor, and Labor Notes provide organized primary sources. Constitutional analysis (NLRA commerce clause, Taft-Hartley) is established doctrine. |
| Domain 45 — Housing | Primary sources are organized (NLIHC, Democracy Journal, Duke JHPPL). Constitutional dimension is limited (primarily state preemption law). |

### Specialized Expertise Required

The following domains require engagement with specialized literatures where general research produces systematic gaps or mischaracterization risks:

| Domain | Specialized Expertise Required | Risk If Absent |
|--------|-------------------------------|---------------|
| Domain 41 — Post-Insurrection Accountability (Transitional Justice) | International transitional justice — the ICTJ literature, Spain's Pact of Forgetting, South Africa TRC process mechanics | Without expertise in comparative transitional justice, the domain may over-rely on South Africa TRC as a positive model without engaging the critical literature on its limitations and failures; the recommendations could be naive about what is achievable in US context |
| Domain 46 — Tribal Sovereignty | Federal Indian law — a specialized subfield with its own doctrine, treaties, and a different constitutional framework than the rest of the project | Federal Indian law is the most siloed specialized legal subfield in US constitutional law. Errors in tribal sovereignty framing are not merely incomplete — they can actively misrepresent the nation-to-nation relationship in ways that undermine the domain's credibility with NCAI and NARF audiences |
| Domain 42 — Drug Policy (CSA/Federalism dimension) | Controlled Substances Act administrative law, DEA administrative procedure, Gonzales v. Raich federalism implications | The rescheduling process operates through specialized administrative law procedures; mischaracterizing the DEA hearing process could produce a domain document that is inaccurate about what the advocacy window actually permits |

**Mitigation for specialized domains**: The accelerated production model for domains requiring specialized expertise is: (a) complete the primary source research and structural analysis with general research capacity; (b) flag sections requiring specialized validation; (c) route the draft to a subject-matter expert for targeted review of flagged sections before distribution. This "draft-then-validate" model preserves production speed while managing accuracy risk.

## Part IV: Outsourcing Criteria — External Researcher Partnerships

### Which Domains Are Candidates for External Research Partnerships

External researcher partnerships — academic collaborators, policy think tank co-authors, specialized practitioners — are most valuable when: (a) the domain requires specialized expertise the orchestrator cannot reliably provide; and (b) an external researcher adds institutional credibility to the output that the framework cannot self-generate.

**Strong outsourcing candidates**:

| Domain | Outsourcing Case | Potential Partners |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Domain 41 — Transitional Justice | International transitional justice is a specialized academic subfield; an ICTJ or Carnegie Endowment researcher as co-author adds authoritative credibility | ICTJ researchers, Carnegie Democracy fellow, Harvard Ash Center faculty |
| Domain 46 — Tribal Sovereignty | Federal Indian law requires tribal nation institutional endorsement, not just accurate research; a domain co-authored with NCAI or NARF staff carries different institutional credibility | NCAI policy counsel, NARF legal staff, law school federal Indian law clinics (UNM, Stanford) |
| Domain 40 — Epistemic Infrastructure (technical dimension) | Content Credentials / C2PA technical provenance work requires familiarity with technical standards | Stanford Internet Observatory researcher, Mozilla Foundation tech policy team |

**Weak outsourcing candidates** (can be produced internally without quality gap):
- Domain 39, 42, 43, 44, 45 — these domains are well-served by general policy research and accessible primary sources. The time cost of coordinating with external researchers exceeds the quality gain relative to internal production.

### Outsourcing Process

If an external research partnership is pursued for Domain 41 or Domain 46:

1. **Identify partner**: Reach out through existing Phase 1 distribution contacts. If a Tier 1 or Tier 2 contact has engaged substantively with the framework, they are the first candidate for a co-authorship or research partnership conversation.
2. **Define contribution**: The partnership model should be: orchestrator produces the initial draft structure, evidence compilation, and reform architecture; partner provides expert review and targeted additions to specialized sections. This preserves production speed while integrating expertise.
3. **Attribution**: Co-authored domains or formally reviewed domains should acknowledge the expert reviewer in the domain's frontmatter. This adds institutional credibility without creating a complex co-authorship process.
4. **Timeline constraint**: External partnerships add 2–6 weeks to production timelines (reviewer response time). Do not pursue external partnerships for urgency-window domains (Domain 42 DEA deadline, Domain 39 ballot measure window).

## Part V: Contingency Planning — If Phase 1 Moves Slower Than Expected

### Phase 1 Underperformance Scenarios and Phase 2 Implications

**Scenario 1: Phase 1 is structurally failing (email deliverability problem)**

Defined as: Fewer than 2 confirmed engaged contacts (replies or routing) from the first distribution wave at Day 30. Per Metric 5 in `phase-1-baseline-metrics.md`, this triggers an immediate tactical review.

Phase 2 research impact: **Pause all Phase 2 research until Phase 1 is fixed.** Producing additional domain research while Phase 1 distribution is not functioning creates a growing library with no delivery infrastructure. The correct response is to fix distribution before expanding content.

Exception: If the DEA June 29 window (Domain 42) creates a hard deadline that overlaps with the Phase 1 fix window, Domain 42 research can proceed in parallel with the Phase 1 infrastructure correction — but only if distribution to criminal justice reform organizations (ACLU, Sentencing Project) can occur through an alternative channel (Substack direct pitch, warm introduction) independent of the primary Phase 1 outreach system.

**Scenario 2: Phase 1 has low engagement but functioning delivery (content problem)**

Defined as: Email delivery is confirmed (contacts are receiving emails) but engagement rate is below 50% of target at Day 30 — fewer than 5 engaged contacts vs. the 10-contact target.

Phase 2 research impact: **Defer new domains; focus on content improvement for existing domains.** If Phase 1 content is not engaging audiences, producing new domains will not fix the problem. The likely diagnosis (per `phase-1-baseline-metrics.md` Metric 5.4) is content framing mismatch — the domain documents are not matching audience need in their current form.

Tactical response before Phase 2 research: Produce shorter domain one-pagers (1–2 pages per domain, vs. the 8,000–10,000 word full documents) for the three lowest-engagement domains. Test whether concise executive-summary format generates more response from Tier 1 contacts. If it does, that is both a distribution fix and a Phase 2 format signal.

**Scenario 3: Phase 1 is slow but not failing (below-target but above-failure-threshold engagement)**

Defined as: 2–8 engaged contacts at Day 30 vs. the 10-contact target. Underperformance, not failure.

Phase 2 research impact: **Proceed with Month 1–2 priority domains (Domain 42 DEA window, Domain 39 ballot measure window) on schedule, but defer Month 3–4 domains until Phase 1 momentum improves.**

Rationale: The urgency-window domains (42, 39) have hard external deadlines that override Phase 1 performance concerns. These domains will be distributable regardless of Phase 1 engagement rate because they have alternative distribution pathways (Substack, Reddit, direct pitch to domain-specific organizations). Deferring them because of Phase 1 underperformance sacrifices real policy windows for administrative tidiness.

**Scenario 4: Phase 1 is exceeding targets**

Defined as: 15+ engaged contacts at Day 30 (above the 10–15 target range), or any Tier A citation within 45 days, or an explicit Tier 1 institutional adoption event (syllabus, brief, report) before Month 2.

Phase 2 research impact: **Accelerate Phase 2 research immediately.** Phase 1 exceeding targets means audiences are primed and waiting. Every week of delay in producing Phase 2 domains forfeits adoption momentum.

Tactical response: Move Month 3–4 domains to Month 2 if production capacity permits. Consider parallel research on one medium-complexity pairing (Domain 39 + Domain 41 or Domain 40 + Domain 42).

## Part VI: Maximum Throughput Strategy

The maximum sustainable Phase 2 research throughput, given single-operator orchestration and the quality standards required for institutional distribution, is:

- **Months 1–2**: 1–2 domains (one immediate-urgency domain, one if demand signals permit parallelism).
- **Months 3–4**: 2–3 domains (building production momentum as Phase 1 demand signals clarify which domains have waiting audiences).
- **Months 5–6**: 2–3 domains (peak Phase 2 production period if Phase 1 is performing on track).
- **Months 7–12**: 1–2 domains per two-month period (declining production intensity as Phase 1 diffuses and Phase 3 planning begins).

**Total Phase 2 production target**: 8–10 domains (Domains 39–48) over 12 months, plus completion of Domain 37b if Path A+37 is selected. This is achievable at the demonstrated production capacity without quality compromise.

The binding constraint is not research speed but audience readiness — Phase 2 domains completed before Phase 1 has created an engaged institutional audience for them have reduced impact. The sequencing discipline in `phase-2-domain-research-priority-matrix.md` is designed to match production timing to audience readiness. Maximum throughput is not always optimal throughput.
