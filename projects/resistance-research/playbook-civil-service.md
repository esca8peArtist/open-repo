## Executive Summary for Coalition Leads

Civil service unions are the institutional backbone of Wave 1 resistance — not because they are the loudest voice, but because they are the ones who know where the bodies are buried. AFGE (1.3 million members across federal agencies), NTEU (150,000+ federal employees primarily in Treasury, IRS, SEC, CFTC, and Social Security), and AFSCME (1.6 million state and local workers) together represent the human beings who operate the administrative state. When they document what is being destroyed, the documentation is credible. When they litigate, they have direct injury. When they testify, they have facts.

AFGE filed suit against Schedule Policy/Career on inauguration night, January 20, 2025 — an operational model every other constituency should study. Legal theory pre-drafted before the executive order was signed; facts inserted after signing; filing complete within the inaugural day. This is the standard.

The current crisis for civil service unions is structural: they are fighting for their own survival in a degraded legal environment simultaneously with fighting for democratic institutions broadly. MSPB has been without quorum since May 2025, meaning civil service appeals have no tribunal. NLRB union elections fell 30% in 2025. Dues revenue is declining as federal workforce shrinks. Any playbook for civil service unions must account for the fact that the tools they would normally use to defend themselves — NLRB certification, MSPB appeals, OPM grievance procedures — have been degraded or eliminated.

The path forward runs through three lanes: (1) litigation (courts remain accessible even when administrative channels are closed); (2) congressional strategy (Saving the Civil Service Act, the Federal Workforce Caucus, the Protect America's Workforce Act); (3) electoral mobilization (AFL-CIO's 8-million-household GOTV infrastructure explicitly framed around civil service defense).

## Constituency Profile

**Organizational base**:

- **AFGE (American Federation of Government Employees)**: 1.3 million members across most federal civilian agencies except Treasury and financial regulators. Headquarters: Washington, D.C. (afge.org). National President. Primary legal team: O'Donoghue & O'Brien (Washington). AFGE's Second Amended Complaint (March 4, 2026) against Schedule Policy/Career is the anchor litigation.

- **NTEU (National Treasury Employees Union)**: 150,000+ members, concentrated in IRS, CBP, SEC, CFTC, FDA, and Social Security. Headquarters: Washington, D.C. (nteu.org). NTEU filed its own Schedule Policy/Career challenge; legal theory emphasizes § 3302 (the "necessary" requirement for excepted service) and due process. Distinct from AFGE on IRS-specific issues (Direct File termination, IRS enforcement staffing).

- **AFSCME (American Federation of State, County and Municipal Employees)**: 1.6 million state and local government workers. Not directly affected by federal Schedule Policy/Career, but: (a) federal fund impoundment defunds state programs that employ AFSCME members; (b) state-level Schedule Policy/Career analogues threaten state civil service; (c) AFSCME's 2026 state affiliate mobilization is the key resource for state legislative strategy.

- **AFL-CIO affiliation**: AFGE and AFSCME are AFL-CIO affiliates. NTEU is independent but coordinates. The AFL-CIO's readmission of SEIU in 2025 creates a unified labor movement for the first time since 2005 — civil service union asks can now be channeled through the consolidated AFL-CIO structure.

**Legal infrastructure**: The AFGE-AFSCME-AFL-CIO Second Amended Complaint (March 4, 2026) demonstrated the coalition's sustainable litigation capacity. The legal team includes O'Donoghue & O'Brien (AFGE), Bredhoff & Kaiser (AFSCME), and Cohen, Weiss and Simon (AFL-CIO). These are the top federal labor law firms in the country; their institutional knowledge of Civil Service Reform Act, Title 5, and constitutional civil service doctrine is unmatched.

**Membership trend (constraint)**: Federal civilian workforce has declined approximately 15% since January 2025 due to forced separations, deferred resignations, and voluntary attrition under hostile conditions. Union membership in the federal workforce declined approximately 28% since 2024. Dues revenue is declining. AFGE's litigation capacity is real but under financial pressure.

**MSPB status**: The Merit Systems Protection Board has been without quorum since May 2025. This means that federal employees who are wrongfully terminated have no administrative appeal mechanism — they must go directly to federal district court, which is slower, more expensive, and for which most employees lack resources. The MSPB case backlog has grown more than 2,100% from its normal operating level.

## Domain Targeting: Five Highest-Leverage Areas

### Domain 2: Civil Service — Primary Lead

**The Schedule Policy/Career litigation** is the anchor:

AFGE's legal theory runs across four counts: (1) EO violates 5 U.S.C. § 3302 — excepted service placement requires a finding that it is "necessary" or warranted by "conditions of good administration," and the blanket reclassification of all "policy-influencing" positions fails this test; (2) EO violates the Constitution's Due Process Clause by stripping career employees of property interests in their positions without adequate process; (3) EO violates the APA by purporting to rescind prior regulations without going through notice-and-comment; (4) EO violates the First Amendment by targeting employees for their speech or association on civil service matters.

NTEU adds a fifth theory: the OPM's implementing rule (February 6, 2026) — which applied Schedule Policy/Career to 50,000 additional employees — is itself an APA violation because it constituted a substantive rulemaking without notice-and-comment.

**Saving the Civil Service Act (H.R. 492 / S. 134)**: Senator Kaine and Representative Connolly introduced this bipartisan bill. Key provisions: (1) prevents reclassification of any competitive service position created after September 30, 2020 without express congressional authorization; (2) caps conversions to Schedule C at 1% of agency employees or 5 employees, whichever is greater; (3) establishes a civil service protection fund for litigation expenses.

Bipartisan sponsors: Representatives Fitzpatrick and Bacon (Republicans) are co-sponsors. The House has already passed the Protect America's Workforce Act (HR 2550) by bipartisan majority, restoring collective bargaining rights. The Senate is the current pressure target.

**Congressional strategy for the Saving the Civil Service Act**:
- The Federal Workforce Caucus (formed in late 2025) provides the organizational home in Congress
- 10-15 member case studies (the "faces of Schedule Policy/Career") should be provided to every caucus member: specific federal employees in their districts whose positions were reclassified, their job descriptions, the policy consequences of their political vulnerability
- Target Republican co-sponsors who have federal agency workforces in their districts: DoD civilians in Virginia, USDA in Midwestern agricultural states, VA medical staff in swing districts

**MSPB Reconstruction Act**: Convert MSPB to an independent Article I court with supermajority Senate confirmation requirements for board members. This is the structural fix that prevents future quorum manipulation. Legislative vehicle: the MSPB Reconstruction Act (not yet introduced; model language should be drafted). Target for introduction: post-2026 midterms if Senate composition improves.

**Measurement milestone (Month 6)**: AFGE/NTEU/AFSCME Second Amended Complaint at circuit court level; Saving the Civil Service Act has Senate floor vote scheduled or is in Judiciary Committee markup.

### Domain 6: Judicial Independence — NLRB Quorum Track

**NLRB quorum loss** is a direct union operational consequence. The National Labor Relations Board cannot certify new unions, cannot process unfair labor practice complaints, and cannot enforce existing orders without a quorum. This means civil service unions' ability to organize new shops — and to win organizing elections — is functionally suspended.

**State labor board parallel track**: The NLRB quorum loss makes state labor boards the primary organizing and enforcement mechanism. States with strong state labor boards (New York, California, Illinois, Massachusetts, Colorado) should be prioritized for organizing campaigns. State public employee labor laws — which cover AFSCME and some AFGE-affiliated state contractors — are independent of NLRB and continue to function.

**NLRB Independence Act**: Model legislation modifying NLRB board member appointment and removal to provide Article I court-style independence. Coordinate with AFL-CIO legislative department on drafting. The constitutional argument: after *Slaughter*, NLRB board members may have removal protection only if the Board is redesignated as an Article I tribunal with adjudicatory (not executive) functions. The conversion is structurally possible and precedented (the Tax Court was converted from an executive board to an Article I court in 1969).

**Measurement milestone (Month 9)**: NLRB Independence Act draft legislation produced; state labor board organizing strategy operational in five priority states.

### Domain 17: Labor Rights — PRO Act and Sectoral Bargaining

**PRO Act** remains the flagship labor rights legislation. Civil service unions are not the primary constituency for the PRO Act (which addresses private-sector organizing), but their electoral mobilization is essential to any PRO Act majority. The AFL-CIO coalition's political capital — built through 2026 GOTV — should be explicitly deployed toward PRO Act passage post-midterms.

**Sectoral bargaining pilots**: Three to five states (California, Colorado, New Mexico, New York, Massachusetts) have the legislative capacity to enact sectoral bargaining pilots for specific industries. Sectoral bargaining (setting wages and conditions across an entire industry sector, not just organized shops) is the mechanism through which high-union Nordic countries achieved their wage compression. The pilot structure: the state creates a Wage Board for a specific sector (fast food, home care, warehouse logistics) with union and employer representation; the Board's determinations apply sector-wide.

California's Fast Food Council (AB 257, 2022, then Prop 22 reversal, then AB 1228, 2023) is the current model. Colorado and New York have proposed similar structures. AFSCME's state affiliates should push this as a priority in every trifecta state's 2027 session.

**Minimum wage legislation**: The federal minimum wage has not been increased since 2009. State-level minimum wage legislation is the active track; civil service unions should support state minimum wage campaigns as part of their economic justice agenda even where it does not directly affect their members.

**Measurement milestone (Month 12)**: At least two states have introduced sectoral bargaining pilot legislation; federal minimum wage bill has Senate co-sponsors identified.

### Domain 26: Government Accountability — Inspector General Defense

**IG system** is directly in AFGE's institutional interest. Inspectors General investigate fraud, waste, and abuse; they are the accountability mechanism for the agencies where AFGE members work. The mass IG firing (January 2025) removed the oversight layer that documented the illegal activities that civil service unions litigate against.

**IG Protection Act**: Legislation requiring Senate confirmation for IG removal and a 30-day notice period with written explanation. Bipartisan precedent: the IG Protection Act of 2008 passed with bipartisan support; strengthening amendments should be a near-term legislative target.

**Documentation partnership**: Civil service unions should formalize a documentation partnership with surviving IGs and their Councils. When agencies engage in illegal workforce actions (non-merit RIFs, Schedule Policy/Career implementation without proper notice), IGs are the internal documentation mechanism. Union representatives should know which IGs are still functioning and how to refer cases to them.

**Federal Whistleblower Enhancement Act**: Strengthening whistleblower protections for federal employees who report illegal workforce actions. Current protection (Civil Service Reform Act, § 2302) has significant gaps — it does not protect employees who face retaliation through performance evaluation manipulation rather than formal adverse action. Legislative fix: expand covered adverse actions; require OSC (Office of Special Counsel) to complete investigations within 240 days.

**Measurement milestone (Month 9)**: IG Protection Act introduced with bipartisan sponsors; at least three documented IG referrals from union member cases.

### Domain 34: Congressional Power of the Purse — DOGE Impoundment

**Direct union harm**: DOGE's mass impoundment of appropriated funds has resulted in the elimination of positions funded through specific appropriations. When an appropriation is impounded, the funding line for the position disappears — this is a force reduction in effect, whether or not labeled as one. Union grievance procedures apply to RIFs, but if the administration characterizes the position elimination as a funding contingency rather than a RIF, it may try to avoid the RIF notice and recall rights.

**APA challenge**: DOGE impoundment decisions are final agency actions subject to APA arbitrary and capricious review. The union litigation track: AFGE files an APA challenge to specific DOGE impoundment decisions that eliminated specific bargaining unit positions; the challenge argues that the impoundment (a) lacked statutory authority under the Impoundment Control Act; (b) was arbitrary and capricious because it provided no reasoned explanation; (c) violated RIF procedures because it was a de facto reduction in force without the required 60-day notice.

**RIF rights litigation**: Federal employees facing RIFs have specific rights: 60-day advance notice, right to recall, retention registers based on service computation dates, transfer rights to similar positions. DOGE's rapid workforce actions frequently violated these procedures. Class action litigation on RIF rights is the primary litigation vehicle; AFGE is currently litigating multiple RIF rights cases.

**Measurement milestone (Month 6)**: Three RIF rights class actions filed on behalf of employees affected by DOGE impoundment; APA challenge to one specific DOGE impoundment decision at district court level.

## 36-Month Action Plan

### Year 1, Phase 1: Litigation Consolidation and Congressional Mobilization (Months 1–6)

**Month 1: Litigation coordination session**

Convene AFGE, NTEU, and AFSCME legal teams for a full-day coordination session. Agenda:
- Review all pending Schedule Policy/Career cases for consistency of legal arguments (inconsistent arguments in simultaneous cases create SCOTUS cert risks)
- Assign lead brief responsibility: AFGE leads on constitutional counts (due process, First Amendment); NTEU leads on statutory count (§ 3302 "necessary" requirement and OPM implementing rule); AFSCME leads on APA counts
- Review *Loper Bright* implications: post-*Loper Bright*, courts do not defer to OPM's interpretation of § 3302; this strengthens the statutory count
- Identify which cases are most likely to produce favorable circuit court records for SCOTUS cert

Action: Produce a joint litigation coordination memo. Share with outside counsel and academic partners (Yale Rule of Law Clinic, Harvard Administrative Law faculty).

**Month 2: Congressional testimony mobilization**

AFGE's legislative conference (annual, typically March-April) is the primary vehicle. Prepare:
- 10-15 member case studies for House and Senate Judiciary Committee distribution: real federal employees in swing districts whose positions were reclassified; their job descriptions; what happened to the agency functions they performed; what the public-facing consequences are
- Testimony from AFGE and NTEU presidents before House Oversight and Senate Judiciary: frame the testimony around agency capacity loss, not just worker rights
- Package for Republican members who represent districts with large federal agency workforces: DoD civilians in Virginia Beach, USDA extension staff in Iowa, VA medical staff in Pennsylvania

Action: 10-15 member case study packets complete; testimony drafts complete; meetings scheduled with Federal Workforce Caucus members.

**Month 3: State affiliate mobilization (AFSCME)**

AFSCME's 1.6 million state and local members are the resource for state legislative strategy. Priority states: states with Democratic trifectas or where Democratic gains in 2026 midterms could produce trifectas (Michigan, Minnesota, New Mexico, Wisconsin if flips).

State affiliate objectives for 2026:
- Pressure state legislators to enact state civil service protection statutes that protect state employees from state-level Schedule Policy/Career analogues
- AFSCME members as testimony witnesses in state legislative hearings on federal workforce consequences in state programs (what happens to state Medicaid administration when federal Medicaid staff are fired?)
- AFSCME affiliates activate member networks for 2026 midterm candidate support

Action: AFSCME state affiliate directors receive the state civil service protection model statute; at least five states have introduced companion legislation by Month 6.

**Month 4: 2026 electoral integration**

AFL-CIO's GOTV infrastructure for 2026 should explicitly frame the 2026 midterms as a civil service defense election. The argument: Schedule Policy/Career eliminates the expertise in your federal agencies — your Social Security Administration, your IRS, your Veterans Affairs — and replaces career experts with political appointees. This is concrete and verifiable.

Voter contact messaging: field-tested messages for union households in swing states emphasizing specific agency functions (benefit processing, tax assistance, veterans healthcare) that are threatened by Schedule Policy/Career. Avoid abstract democracy language; use specific agency functions that affect members' families.

Action: AFL-CIO political department agrees to specific civil service defense framing in 2026 voter contact materials; AFGE and NTEU members identified as validators in AFL-CIO household contact universe.

**Month 5: MSPB Reconstruction Act drafting**

The MSPB without quorum means 2,100%+ case backlog. Draft the MSPB Reconstruction Act language:
- Converts MSPB to an Article I tribunal (like the Tax Court, the Court of Federal Claims, or the Court of Appeals for the Armed Forces)
- Three-member board with 14-year staggered terms; removal only for cause (the constitutional protection that survives even after *Slaughter* for genuinely adjudicatory functions)
- Senate confirmation by 60-vote supermajority (not subject to simple-majority override)
- Jurisdiction expanded to include DOGE impoundment RIF claims and Schedule Policy/Career reclassification challenges

Action: Model legislation drafted; circulated to Federal Workforce Caucus and Senate Judiciary staff for comments.

**Month 6: Mid-year review**

Litigation status: which Schedule Policy/Career cases have reached circuit courts? Which have received adverse preliminary injunction rulings? What is the outlook for SCOTUS cert?

Congressional status: What is the Senate floor vote timeline for Saving the Civil Service Act? Has it advanced through Judiciary Committee?

Action: Updated litigation and legislative status memo; revised timeline for SCOTUS cert scenarios.

### Year 3+: Constitutional Civil Service Protection (Months 24–48+)

**Constitutional amendment campaign**

The durable solution to Schedule Policy/Career is a constitutional amendment protecting merit-based civil service. The amendment would:
1. Establish that federal employees in positions requiring specialized expertise shall serve on the basis of merit, not political loyalty
2. Provide that removal from such positions requires cause, with appeal rights to an independent Article I tribunal
3. Vest Congress with authority to define the scope of covered positions, preventing executive redefinition through EO

Civil service unions provide the organizational infrastructure for the 34-state ratification campaign: AFGE state legislative contacts, AFSCME state affiliate networks, AFL-CIO state federation government. This is the same infrastructure used for electoral GOTV; it can be repurposed for state legislative lobbying.

**MSPB case backlog clearance**

Once MSPB quorum is restored (either through a favorable election outcome or through the MSPB Reconstruction Act), the 2,100%+ case backlog will require years to clear. The MSPB Reconstruction Act should include a specific provision authorizing temporary ALJ appointments to address the backlog, with funding authorization.

**Sectoral bargaining implementation**

Year 3 is the implementation year for sectoral bargaining pilots in willing states. AFSCME's state affiliates provide the worker representation on Wage Boards. The pilots in home care, fast food, and warehouse logistics generate the evidence base for a federal sectoral bargaining framework.

## Scenario Decision Trees

### Scenario A: SCOTUS grants cert in Schedule Policy/Career case

**Trigger**: SCOTUS grants certiorari in AFGE v. OPM or related Schedule Policy/Career challenge.

**Month 1 post-cert**: Form SCOTUS preparation team. Lead counsel (O'Donoghue & O'Brien); academic partners (Harvard and Yale administrative law faculty); co-counsel from ACLU, Protect Democracy, and Constitutional Accountability Center.

**Months 2–5**: Brief preparation. Key arguments:
- Textual: § 3302 requires a "necessary" finding; a blanket EO cannot satisfy this
- Historical: The Pendleton Civil Service Reform Act (1883) was enacted precisely to prevent the spoils system the EO reinstates
- Constitutional: The Take Care Clause requires faithful execution of laws; Schedule Policy/Career enables their political subversion
- Structural: *Humphrey's Executor* line applies because civil service independence is a structural feature of the constitutional separation of powers
- *Loper Bright* post-argument: Courts no longer defer to OPM's interpretation of § 3302; the question is purely legal

**Month 6 (argument)**: AFGE president attends argument; AFGE mobilizes members to fill public gallery seats. Post-argument brief from three amicus groups: academic record (law school clinics), state-harm record (AG coalition), and worker-harm record (AFL-CIO affiliate testimony).

**Post-ruling**: If favorable, AFGE immediately files to reinstate all improperly reclassified employees and recover back pay. If adverse, activate state civil service protection omnibus in all trifecta states immediately; initiate constitutional amendment campaign.

### Scenario B: Administration attempts new mass RIF without statutory authorization

**Trigger**: OPM or DOGE announces mass reduction in force without following 5 U.S.C. §§ 3502-3504 RIF procedures (no 60-day notice, no retention registers, no recall rights).

**Hour 1–4**: Union representatives in affected agencies document the specific violation: is there a RIF notice? Does it follow retention register procedures? Were recall rights provided?

**Day 1–3**: File emergency TRO in D.D.C. challenging the RIF as an APA violation and a violation of the specific statutory RIF procedures. The statutory argument is very strong: the administration cannot do a mass RIF without following 5 U.S.C. § 3502. Class certification: all employees affected by the unauthorized RIF.

**Day 4–14**: Coordinate with AG coalition for state parens patriae standing in the same proceeding; states whose cooperative programs are disrupted by the RIF have direct injury.

**Day 15–30**: Congressional notification: the Federal Workforce Caucus should immediately request emergency hearings with agency heads; the APA requires an agency to provide a reasoned explanation for its actions, and emergency congressional oversight creates a parallel record.

---

### Scenario C: MSPB quorum restored (favorable appointment or court order)

**Trigger**: MSPB quorum restored through any mechanism.

**Day 1–30**: File motions in all pending MSPB cases that had been stayed pending quorum restoration. Prioritize: (1) cases involving Schedule Policy/Career reclassification; (2) cases involving DOGE-related RIFs without statutory authority; (3) whistleblower retaliation cases with the strongest factual records.

**Month 2–6**: Request emergency MSPB rulemaking to establish a case management system for the backlog. Request emergency appropriation for temporary ALJ hiring.

**Month 6–12**: Begin systematically working through the backlog by category; cases with clear statutory violations resolved first; complex cases with disputed facts scheduled for full hearings.

## Conflict Mitigation

### Conflict 1: Cross-Union Trade Policy Tension

**Tension**: Building trades unions (IATSE, Ironworkers, Carpenters) have historically supported tariffs because tariffs protect manufacturing jobs. Civil service unions include customs enforcement officers and trade law enforcement staff who implement tariff programs; some of these members may have been on the wrong side of the *Learning Resources* outcome. AFL-CIO's consolidated structure means trade policy conflicts now play out internally rather than inter-federation.

**Resolution**: The *Learning Resources* decision is about constitutional authority, not the merits of trade policy. Frame: "Congress, not the President, has the power to set tariffs. We support Congress exercising that power to protect American manufacturing jobs. We oppose the President unilaterally setting tariffs without statutory authority — because that same unilateral authority could be used to eliminate the labor protections workers depend on." This reframes the trade authority question as a worker protection issue, not a free-trade issue.

Tactical: AFGE's customs enforcement members are represented by a specific AFGE local; that local's president should be briefed on the legal argument and given the reframe language before any public statement. Do not let the trade policy conflict become an intra-AFGE dispute.

### Conflict 2: NLRB Quorum Loss Affects Private Sector More Than Civil Service

**Tension**: NLRB quorum loss primarily affects private-sector organizing; federal employee unions operate under Title 5 and the Federal Labor Relations Authority, not the NLRB. Civil service unions may not prioritize NLRB quorum restoration as urgently as private-sector unions (AFL-CIO affiliates). This creates a coalition tension: civil service unions want their litigation prioritized; private-sector unions want NLRB restoration.

**Resolution**: NLRB quorum restoration and MSPB quorum restoration are the same structural argument — independent agency quorum matters. Civil service unions should support NLRB restoration legislation as a coalition solidarity matter, explicitly noting that the same constitutional argument that protects MSPB independence protects NLRB independence. "If they can break MSPB quorum, they can break NLRB quorum. If they can break NLRB quorum, they can break MSPB quorum. One fight."

### Conflict 3: Competitive Civil Servants vs. Politically Exposed Members

**Tension**: Some AFGE members are in positions that straddle the competitive service and political appointments; they may face personal career risk from union activity. AFGE members who testify publicly about Schedule Policy/Career implementation are potentially vulnerable to informal retaliation.

**Resolution**: Protect the witnesses. AFGE should identify member-testifiers through a secure nomination process, provide legal counsel (through the union's legal program) to any member who testifies, and have a retaliation response team ready. When any member faces retaliation for union activity or congressional testimony, AFGE should file an unfair labor practice charge with the FLRA and an OSC complaint within 48 hours. The public retaliation record is itself evidence for the legislative and litigation records.
