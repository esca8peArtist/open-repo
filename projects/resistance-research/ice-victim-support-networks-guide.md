## Introduction: The Scale of the Crisis

Since January 2025, the Trump administration has executed the most aggressive domestic immigration enforcement campaign in American history. ICE announced a 120% workforce expansion in early 2026, adding more than 12,000 officers and agents through a nationwide recruitment campaign. ICE's budget reached approximately $9 billion, with an additional $30 billion in multi-year funding through the Secure America Act (enacted 2026) to expand detention, deportation, and enforcement through 2029.

By mid-2026, enforcement operations have expanded from border communities to interior cities with large established immigrant populations, targeting workplaces, schools, churches, and homes. Federal funding threats have pressured hundreds of localities to reduce sanctuary protections, and 287(g) agreements have been expanded in dozens of new counties, deepening local law enforcement's entanglement with federal immigration enforcement.

In this environment, community resilience depends on knowing your rights, knowing who to call, and knowing how to protect each other. This guide is designed for three audiences:

1. **Individuals and families** who want to know their rights and prepare
2. **Rapid response volunteers** who want to organize community defense
3. **Community organizations** building long-term support infrastructure

This is not a legal advice document. It is a resource guide that can help you access legal advice, community support, and rapid defense when you need it. Everything in this guide is accurate as of July 2026; laws and policies change rapidly, and you should verify current information with the organizations listed here.

## Section 1: Know Your Rights — Foundational Organizations

### 1.1 ACLU Immigrants' Rights Project

**Website**: aclu.org/immigrants-rights  
**National hotline**: 212-549-2500  
**Immigration rights guide (updated March 2026)**: aclu.org/know-your-rights/immigrants-rights

The ACLU Immigrants' Rights Project is the largest national legal organization focused on protecting the civil rights and civil liberties of immigrants through impact litigation, advocacy, and public education. The project has challenged key Trump administration enforcement policies including targeted community raids, family separation protocols, and expansion of expedited removal.

**Most important resources:**
- Know Your Rights: If ICE Confronts You — multilingual, covers home raids, workplace raids, stops on the street
- Know Your Rights card (downloadable, printable, multiple languages): aclu.org/know-your-rights/immigrants-rights
- State chapter contacts for local legal referrals: aclu.org/about/affiliates

**Critical rights the ACLU documents:**
- You have the right to remain silent. Do not answer questions about your immigration status, country of birth, or how you entered the country.
- You do not have to open your door. ICE agents need a **judicial warrant** signed by a judge to enter your home without your consent. An **administrative warrant** (ICE Form I-200 or I-205) does not give them authority to enter.
- You do not have to consent to a search. Say clearly: "I do not consent to a search."
- You have the right to speak to an attorney before answering any questions. Say: "I want to speak to a lawyer."
- Do not sign anything without consulting an attorney. Signing documents can waive your rights or constitute an agreement to voluntary departure.

## Section 2: Rapid Response Networks

### 2.1 What Rapid Response Networks Do

Rapid response networks are volunteer organizations that respond to ICE enforcement actions in real time. When a community member reports ICE activity, rapid response volunteers:

1. Verify the report (confirm an actual ICE action is occurring)
2. Dispatch observers to document the action with video and photographs
3. Connect affected individuals with attorneys
4. Support families left behind
5. Help coordinate media coverage where appropriate

Rapid response is not direct interference with law enforcement — that is illegal. It is documentation, witness presence, and immediate connection to legal resources.

### 2.2 How to Report ICE Activity

**National hotline**: **1-800-RAID-REP (1-800-724-3737)**
This hotline, operated by Rapid Response networks, triggers verification and rapid response deployment. Calling it initiates efforts by rapid response volunteers to verify and document the situation.

### 2.3 How to Join a Rapid Response Network

Most rapid response networks recruit volunteers through their websites and through local faith communities, immigrant rights organizations, and labor unions. Standard volunteer roles include:

- **Observer/Documentarian**: Witnesses and films ICE actions from public spaces (legal; do not interfere with agents)
- **Hotline responder**: Receives calls from community members reporting ICE activity, verifies, and dispatches observers
- **Family support**: Connects family members of detained individuals with legal resources, childcare, financial assistance
- **Translator**: Provides language support for affected individuals

**Before joining**: Take a Know Your Rights training specific to observers. The ACLU of Oregon's rapid response guide (aclu-or.org) includes a model protocol. CLINIC's toolkit covers the full volunteer training curriculum.

### 2.4 Documentation Protocol During a Raid

If you witness ICE activity in your community:

1. **Stay in a public space.** You have the right to observe and film law enforcement activity from public spaces. You do not have the right to interfere with enforcement actions.

2. **Film continuously.** Capture: ICE agents' badge numbers and vehicle numbers if visible; what agents tell individuals; whether individuals are informed of their rights; whether a warrant is produced; the names of any individuals being detained (ask if you can do so safely).

3. **Document consent.** If ICE enters a home, note whether agents produced a judicial warrant. An administrative warrant (Form I-200 or I-205, signed by an ICE officer, not a judge) does not authorize entry without consent.

4. **Record witness information.** Collect names and contact information of witnesses who are willing to testify about what they observed.

5. **Immediately report to the rapid response hotline and to local legal organizations** (ACLU, CLINIC affiliate, Legal Aid).

6. **Do not post immediately to social media** if posting could identify individuals at risk. Coordinate with legal organizations first on release of documentation.

### 2.5 Text and Call Tree Protocols

Effective rapid response networks operate through text trees — networks where each person is responsible for contacting a defined list of others. For your neighborhood or community:

- Identify a central coordinator (who receives incoming reports)
- Each community member should have a list of 5-10 neighbors to contact
- Use encrypted messaging (Signal, WhatsApp with encryption enabled) for coordination
- Establish a pre-agreed signal word that means "ICE is active nearby — implement family safety plan"
- Establish who will serve as emergency contact for children at school or childcare

**Signal app** is the recommended communication tool for rapid response coordination because it encrypts messages end-to-end and messages can be set to disappear.

### 3.1 Immigration Attorneys and Pro Bono Legal Services

**Immediate need — detained individual**: If someone you know has been detained by ICE, they have the right to contact an attorney. ICE must provide a list of free or low-cost legal services at detention facilities. Federal law requires this list to be current and accurate, though advocates report significant noncompliance.

**EOIR Pro Bono List**: The Executive Office for Immigration Review maintains a list of pro bono legal service providers by state at justice.gov/eoir/list-pro-bono-legal-service-providers. This is the official government directory — use it to find free legal representation in immigration court.

### 3.2 Law School Immigration Clinics

Law school immigration clinics provide free or supervised legal representation and are often more accessible than overwhelmed nonprofit providers. Clinics operate in most major metropolitan areas with law schools. To find a clinic near you:

- Contact the nearest accredited law school and ask for their immigration law clinic
- The Law School Consortium Project (lawschoolconsortium.com) coordinates pro bono immigration services through law schools
- Ask a CLINIC-affiliated organization to refer you to the nearest clinic

**Clinic limitations**: Law school clinics are staffed by students under attorney supervision. They handle a defined caseload and may have waitlists. They are most effective for non-emergency asylum applications, DACA renewals, and complex immigration status questions. For emergency detention situations, contact CLINIC affiliates or ACLU first.

### 3.3 Immigration Bond Funds

Immigration bonds allow detained individuals to be released while their cases are heard in immigration court. Bonds average $8,500-$10,000. Most families cannot pay these amounts without help.

**RAICES Immigration Bond Fund**: raicestexas.org/bond
RAICES has paid bonds ranging up to $80,000 and has freed more than 2,000 individuals from ICE detention in partnership with the National Bail Fund Network. Apply online; RAICES evaluates bond requests on a rolling basis.

**National Bail Fund Network / Community Justice Exchange**: communityjusticeexchange.org/nbfn-bondreferral
The National Bail Fund Network coordinates 90+ community bail and bond funds across the country, posting both criminal bail and immigration bonds. This is the central referral point for connecting to local bond funds.

**Regional bond funds:**

| Fund | Coverage |
|------|----------|
| Bay Area Immigration Bond Fund | bayareaimmigrationbondfund.org |
| Midwest Immigration Bond Fund | mibfc.org |
| Washington (state) WAISN Fair Fight Bond Fund | waisn.org/what-we-do/deportation-defense/fair-fight-bond-fund/ |

**How bond funds work**: When a bond fund pays your bond, you are released to pursue your immigration case from outside detention. Most bond funds require you to remain in contact during your case. If you fail to appear for your immigration court hearing, the bond is forfeited.

**Note on bond refunds**: RAICES and other advocates have documented that less than 50% of bonds paid under the current administration have been properly refunded after cases conclude, even when individuals win their cases. Advocate for proper bond return through your legal representative.

Legal aid societies provide civil legal assistance to low-income individuals and families. Most legal aid societies handle some immigration cases. Find your local legal aid society:

### 3.5 Consular Access

If someone you know is detained by ICE, they have the right under the Vienna Convention on Consular Relations to contact their country's consulate or to have ICE notify the consulate of their detention. This is a legal right — ICE is required to inform detainees of it.

**How to exercise consular access:**
1. The detained person should inform the ICE officer: "I want to contact my consulate"
2. If ICE fails to notify the consulate, a family member can contact the consulate directly
3. Consulates can provide legal referrals, document replacement (for deportation or other proceedings), emergency contact with family in home country, and in some cases financial assistance

**To find your country's consulate in the US**: US Department of State directory at travel.state.gov/content/travel/en/consularnotification/ConsularNotificationandAccess.html

## Section 4: Sanctuary Policies — What They Protect and What They Don't

### 4.1 What "Sanctuary" Means

There is no single legal definition of "sanctuary city." The term refers to a range of policies adopted by state and local governments to limit their participation in federal immigration enforcement. These policies exist on a spectrum:

**Welcoming resolutions**: Non-binding declarations that the city or county will not use public resources to enforce federal immigration law. These provide a political signal but no enforceable legal protection.

**Non-cooperation policies**: Binding policies that prohibit local law enforcement from honoring ICE detainer requests (requests to hold someone in local jail beyond their release date so ICE can pick them up). Local governments can refuse to honor detainers because they are not mandatory — nothing in federal law requires localities to enforce federal immigration law.

**Data protection policies**: Policies that restrict sharing of local government data (arrest records, DMV records, school enrollment, social services records) with federal immigration authorities without a judicial warrant.

**ICE-free zone policies**: Local designations of certain spaces (schools, hospitals, churches, courthouses) as off-limits for immigration enforcement. These are policy commitments, not federal legal bars — ICE retains legal authority to conduct enforcement anywhere, but these zones create political and operational barriers.

**Defunding/divestment from ICE cooperation**: More than 70 jurisdictions have coupled sanctuary policies with public investments in deportation defense — funding immigration legal services, bond funds, and community support programs.

### 4.2 What Sanctuary Policies Actually Protect

**They protect against:**
- Local law enforcement arresting or detaining people solely for immigration status (not criminal offense)
- Local police honoring ICE detainers for people who have completed their criminal sentence
- Local government agencies sharing immigration data with ICE without a judicial warrant
- ICE conducting enforcement in designated sensitive locations (schools, hospitals, churches) in localities with active ICE-free zone policies

**They do NOT protect against:**
- Direct ICE enforcement action — ICE can operate in sanctuary jurisdictions and arrest individuals for immigration violations
- Home raids with judicial warrants
- Workplace raids
- Arrests in public spaces
- Federal funding conditions tied to cooperation with ICE (currently being contested in courts — courts have repeatedly ruled the federal government cannot coerce states through funding conditions)

### 4.3 Current Sanctuary Status (July 2026)

The Trump administration named more than 500 cities, counties, and states as sanctuary jurisdictions in early 2026, threatening federal funding cuts. Courts have repeatedly blocked these funding cuts — federal courts have ruled consistently that the federal government cannot use funding as coercion for states and localities to enforce federal immigration law.

### 4.4 The 287(g) Warning: Where Local Police ARE Federal Immigration Agents

287(g) agreements deputize local law enforcement officers to perform immigration enforcement functions. Where 287(g) agreements are in effect, local police may arrest, detain, and initiate deportation proceedings independently of ICE.

**States/localities with significant 287(g) expansion in 2025-2026**: Florida, Texas, Alabama, Tennessee, Georgia, Arizona, and dozens of individual counties in other states. The Trump executive order in 2025 directed DHS to expand 287(g) agreements nationally.

**How to verify**: The ACLU maintains a map of current 287(g) agreements. Ask your local immigrant rights organization whether your county law enforcement has a 287(g) agreement.

**In 287(g) jurisdictions, local police encounters — traffic stops, arrests for any reason — can initiate immigration enforcement.** This changes the community safety calculus significantly: ordinary police interaction carries immigration enforcement risk.

## Section 5: Practical Immediate Actions

### 5.1 If ICE Comes to Your Door

**Step 1: Do not open the door.** You are not required to open your door for ICE agents unless they present a judicial warrant. Say clearly through the door or a window:

*"I am exercising my right to remain silent. I do not consent to entry. If you have a warrant, please slide it under the door."*

**Step 2: Check the warrant.** A judicial warrant is signed by a federal judge or magistrate, will have the court's seal, and will be addressed to a specific individual at your address. An ICE administrative warrant (Form I-200 or I-205) is signed by an ICE officer — it is NOT a judicial warrant and does not authorize entry without consent.

**Step 3: If no judicial warrant is presented.** Do not open the door. Say you will not open the door without a judicial warrant. Agents may continue to knock — do not open.

**Step 4: If ICE enters by force.** Do not physically resist. Say clearly: "I do not consent to this search. I am exercising my right to remain silent." Document everything you can observe.

**Step 5: Call immediately.** Use the 1-800-RAID-REP hotline. Contact your designated emergency attorney (every family should have this number posted before any emergency). Contact your local rapid response network.

**Step 6: Do not sign anything.** Do not sign any document presented by ICE without consulting an attorney. Signing documents can waive your rights or constitute agreement to voluntary departure, which can permanently bar re-entry.

### 5.2 If ICE Detains Someone You Know

**Immediate steps (first 24 hours):**

1. **Try to learn where they were taken.** Call the ICE locator: 888-351-4024. Use the ICE detainee locator at locator.ice.gov.

2. **Contact an immigration attorney immediately.** A person has no right to court-appointed counsel in immigration proceedings — they must find their own attorney. Use CLINIC, ACLU, RAICES, or the EOIR pro bono list.

3. **Contact the relevant consulate.** Under the Vienna Convention, the detained person has the right to consular notification and access. Call the consulate and provide the person's full name, detention location, and any case numbers.

4. **Gather documents.** Collect: the person's A-number (Alien Registration Number) if known; any prior immigration paperwork; birth certificates for any US-citizen children; documentation of ties to the community.

5. **Assess bond.** Ask the attorney about bond eligibility. Not everyone is bond-eligible — individuals in expedited removal are not entitled to bond hearings. If bond is set, contact bond funds immediately.

6. **Do not cancel DACA or pending applications.** If the detained person has pending immigration applications, do not withdraw them. Withdrawing an application can create additional legal complications.

### 5.3 How to Document a Deportation

If someone you know is being deported, documentation is essential for:
- Immigration court proceedings (evidence of presence, community ties, family relationships)
- Family reunification after deportation
- Post-deportation legal challenges (wrongful deportation, US citizen children's rights)

**Document before deportation:**
- Photograph of the individual with full legal name, date of birth, A-number, and date visible
- Signed authorization for a family member or attorney to act on their behalf
- List of US-citizen family members with their names and contact information
- List of assets and financial accounts
- Copy of all immigration documents

**Document the deportation itself:**
- Date and time of deportation
- Which ICE field office was responsible
- Destination country and city (if provided)
- Any statements made by ICE agents during processing
- Any rights violations (denial of consular access, failure to provide interpreter, failure to inform of rights)

**Post-deportation rights:**
- US-citizen children retain citizenship regardless of parent's deportation
- Individuals who were wrongfully deported (US citizens, individuals with valid status) have rights to return and may have legal claims
- Consular assistance from the home country consulate continues after deportation

### 5.4 Family Reunification Resources

**If a parent is detained or deported:**

1. **Children's emergency contacts**: Every family should designate emergency contacts for children in writing — school, childcare, neighbors. Carry these contacts.

2. **Power of attorney**: A power of attorney designating a trusted adult to make decisions for your children is critical. It does not terminate parental rights. Download a template from your state's legal aid organization or from ILRC at ilrc.org.

3. **Federal Parent Locator Service**: If children are placed in government care after a parent's detention, the Office of Refugee Resettlement (ORR) manages unaccompanied minors. Contact information: acf.hhs.gov/orr.

4. **RAICES Family Reunification**: RAICES has specific programs for family reunification after deportation. Contact raicestexas.org.

5. **CLINIC refugee and immigration services**: Many CLINIC affiliates run family reunification programs. cliniclegal.org/find-a-legal-service-provider.

## Section 6: Community Infrastructure

### 6.1 Mutual Aid Networks

Mutual aid networks provide direct community support — food, childcare, financial assistance, transportation, language access — that government programs do not provide. They are particularly critical for families left behind after a detention or deportation, who may face immediate financial crises.

**National mutual aid resources:**

- **Win Without War Immigration Mutual Aid Directory**: winwithoutwar.org/policy/immigration-mutual-aid — curated list of national and local mutual aid resources
- **Rashida Tlaib Resource Page**: rashidaforcongress.com/2025-resources-to-defend-immigrants-and-other-neighbors — community-organized national and local resources
- **LA Local Support Guide** (model for other cities): thelalocal.org/neighborhoods/boyle-heights/support-donate-volunteer-los-angeles-immigration-raid-protests/

**Building local mutual aid capacity:**

For communities without an existing mutual aid network, the core components to establish are:

1. **Emergency fund** (even small amounts per family): A community pool to cover first-week expenses for families after a detention
2. **Food provision network**: Coordinate families or local restaurants to provide meals
3. **Childcare rotation**: Neighbor families who commit to short-term emergency childcare
4. **Transportation**: Community members who can drive to ICE detention centers or immigration court
5. **Language access**: Bilingual community members available for emergencies
6. **Housing**: Identify families willing to provide short-term housing if a family member is deported

### 6.3 Childcare Networks

Children of detained or deported parents face acute childcare crises — especially if both parents are affected simultaneously or if a sole parent is detained. Needs include:

- **Immediate (first 24-72 hours)**: School pickup, safe supervision during and after a raid
- **Short-term (first week)**: Stable housing, meals, routine
- **Medium-term (weeks to months)**: If the parent's case is pending, stable childcare arrangements

**Resources:**
- CLINIC-affiliated family services providers can coordinate emergency childcare referrals
- Local child welfare agencies (call 211) can provide emergency placements — but note that child welfare involvement can in some cases create additional complications for families. Always consult an immigration attorney before allowing child welfare agency involvement if a parent is detained.
- Faith communities are often the fastest source of emergency childcare coordination

**Legal document to prepare NOW**: An emergency authorization/power of attorney letter giving a named trusted adult authority to care for your children and make medical decisions. This should be notarized and copies held by: you, the designated caregiver, your child's school, and your attorney.

### 6.4 Food and Shelter Resources

**National food banks and emergency food:**
- Feeding America network: feedingamerica.org/find-your-local-foodbank
- Food pantry locator: 211.org or call 2-1-1
- Mutual aid food programs vary by city — ask your local CLINIC affiliate

**Emergency shelter:**
- Domestic violence shelters can provide emergency shelter regardless of immigration status (tell them you need shelter because of an emergency, not specifically DV)
- Many faith communities have emergency housing programs
- Catholic Charities (associated with CLINIC) provides emergency housing and food assistance: catholiccharitiesusa.org/find-help

**For DACA recipients and authorized workers**: You retain your employment authorization during any pending renewal application. Do not stop working or assume your authorization has ended based on news coverage alone — verify your current status with an attorney.

## Section 8: Wallet-Card Summary

*Print and laminate — keep in your wallet and post near your door.*

---

### IF ICE COMES TO YOUR DOOR

**DO NOT OPEN THE DOOR**

Say through the door or window:
*"I am exercising my right to remain silent. I do not consent to entry. If you have a judicial warrant, slide it under the door."*

A **judicial warrant** has a court seal and a judge's signature.
An ICE Form I-200 or I-205 (signed by an ICE officer) is NOT a judicial warrant and does not authorize entry.

---

### YOUR RIGHTS — EVERYWHERE

- **Right to remain silent** — do not answer immigration status questions
- **Right to refuse consent** to any search
- **Right to an attorney** — say "I want a lawyer" before answering questions
- **Do not sign anything** without a lawyer
