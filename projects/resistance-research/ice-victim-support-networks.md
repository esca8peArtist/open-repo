# ICE Victim Support Networks — Community Resource Guide

**Bottom line**: The community infrastructure for supporting people affected by ICE enforcement has matured significantly since January 2025. Rapid response networks now operate in most major metros, Know Your Rights materials are widely available in multiple languages, and legal support organizations have scaled to meet increased need. This guide covers the organizational landscape, how to find or start a rapid response network, what observers can legally do during enforcement actions, and safe reporting channels.

---

## The Current Enforcement Context (2026)

The Trump administration dramatically escalated immigration enforcement beginning January 2025. Workplace raids, community enforcement operations, courthouse arrests, and "sensitive location" violations (schools, churches, hospitals) have all increased substantially. ICE enforcement has expanded into sanctuary jurisdictions through federal pressure and federal law. Communities face:

- Increased workplace I-9 audits and physical enforcement operations
- Enforcement in areas previously considered sensitive locations
- "Collateral arrests" of bystanders without criminal records during targeted operations
- Pressure on local law enforcement to cooperate through 287(g) agreements and detainer compliance

The response infrastructure has also scaled. Rapid response networks, Know Your Rights programs, and legal support systems are more developed in 2026 than at any prior point.

## Know Your Rights Resources

### For Immigrants Directly

Every person in the US, regardless of immigration status, has constitutional rights during ICE encounters.

**Core rights in any encounter:**
- The right to remain silent — you are not required to answer questions about your immigration status, country of origin, or how you entered the US
- The right to refuse consent to a search of your home, car, or belongings (though agents can proceed if they have a warrant)
- The right to speak with a lawyer before answering questions
- The right not to sign anything without first speaking to a lawyer
- The right to make a phone call after arrest

**If ICE comes to your home:**
- You do NOT have to open the door unless they have a judicial warrant (signed by a judge). An administrative warrant signed by an ICE officer is not sufficient to require entry.
- Ask to see the warrant through the window or under the door. Check whether it is signed by a federal judge and has your correct name and address.
- Remain calm and do not lie or provide false documents.

**If stopped in public:**
- You do not have to answer questions. You can say: "I am exercising my right to remain silent."
- Do not run. Do not resist physically.
- Ask if you are free to go. If yes, calmly walk away.

**Key organizations providing Know Your Rights training:**
- **ACLU** — State-specific KYR cards available at aclu.org/know-your-rights/immigrants-rights. Most state ACLU chapters have Spanish-language versions and can provide community training sessions.
- **National Immigration Law Center (NILC)** — nilc.org — KYR guides, policy research, training for advocates
- **CLINIC (Catholic Legal Immigration Network, Inc.)** — cliniclegal.org — KYR rapid response toolkit, training for faith communities and nonprofits
- **United We Dream** — unitedwedream.org — youth-focused KYR resources, immigrant rights activism network
- **ILRC (Immigrant Legal Resource Center)** — ilrc.org — Know Your Rights materials, Red Cards for immigrant rights in multiple languages

### Know Your Rights Cards (Wallet-Sized)

Several organizations produce wallet-sized or phone-sized KYR cards that immigrants can carry:
- ILRC Red Card: Available at ilrc.org/know-your-rights (printable, free, available in Spanish)
- ACLU KYR Cards: aclu.org — state-specific versions

## Rapid Response Networks

### What They Are and How They Work

Rapid response networks are community organizations that activate when ICE enforcement is reported in their area. The core model:

1. **Alert network**: Community members call or text a hotline when they observe ICE activity (vehicles, operations, arrests in progress)
2. **Dispatch**: Trained legal observers and community defenders are dispatched to the location
3. **Documentation**: Observers document the enforcement action (video, agent identification, detention circumstances)
4. **Legal support**: Attorneys or legal aid organizations are notified to begin working on the case
5. **Family support**: The network connects detained individuals' families with legal aid and support services

The most sophisticated networks (Twin Cities model, now spreading nationally) maintain:
- License plate databases of known ICE vehicles
- Counter-surveillance at federal facilities and known staging locations
- Real-time alerts via Signal and encrypted channels
- Roving community patrols that follow and document agents through neighborhoods

### How to Join a Rapid Response Network

1. **Find your local network**: Search "[your city/state] Rapid Response Network" or "[your city] ICE hotline"
2. **Contact by email rather than calling the hotline** — hotlines are for active emergencies. Email organizers to ask about training and volunteering
3. **Attend legal observer training**: Most networks require training before dispatching observers. Training is typically 2-4 hours and covers legal rights, documentation protocols, and safety
4. **Get on the alert list**: Once trained, you receive alerts via Signal or WhatsApp and can choose when to respond

### How to Start a Rapid Response Network

If no network exists in your area, CLINIC's Rapid Response Toolkit (cliniclegal.org/toolkits/rapid-response-toolkit) is the standard resource. It covers:
- Organizational structure and roles (hotline coordinator, legal observer coordinator, family support coordinator)
- Model notebook for rapid response team documentation
- Legal framework for observers
- Community outreach and training protocols
- Relationships with legal aid organizations

Key elements of a functioning network:
- A reliable hotline number with 24-hour coverage (rotating volunteer shifts or call forwarding)
- A relationship with at least one immigration attorney or legal aid organization
- A trained core of legal observers
- Encrypted communication channel (Signal is the standard)
- A Know Your Rights training curriculum for the broader community

Starting resources: CLINIC toolkit, CrimethInc guide to Twin Cities model (crimethinc.com/2026/01/15/rapid-response-networks-in-the-twin-cities-a-guide-to-an-updated-model), State of Surveillance documentation of counter-surveillance methods (stateofsurveillance.org).

## Sanctuary City Policies and How to Support Them

### What Sanctuary Policies Do

Sanctuary policies are local or state laws limiting cooperation between local law enforcement and federal immigration enforcement. The most common protections:

- **Detainer refusal**: Refusing to hold individuals in local jails beyond their scheduled release solely based on an ICE administrative detainer (which is a request, not a court order). Courts have repeatedly held that compliance with detainers is voluntary and that the federal government cannot compel local jails to honor them.
- **Information sharing limits**: Restrictions on sharing immigration status information with federal agencies
- **Sensitive location policies**: Prohibiting local police from assisting ICE enforcement near schools, hospitals, and churches
- **Warrant requirement**: Requiring a judicial warrant (signed by a federal judge) rather than an administrative warrant before cooperating with ICE detainer requests

### Current Legal Status (2026)

The Trump administration has pursued multiple strategies to override sanctuary policies:
- **End Sanctuary Cities Act** (introduced February 2026): Would impose criminal penalties on local officials who ignore DHS requests to detain immigrants
- **Shut Down Sanctuary Policies Act**: Targets funding to cities limiting ICE detainer compliance
- Courts have largely continued to uphold local authority to decline federal detainer requests

The legal principle (established in multiple circuit court decisions) is that the 10th Amendment anti-commandeering doctrine prohibits the federal government from forcing local governments to enforce federal immigration law. This is the same principle that protects state marijuana laws, sanctuary policies, and state refusal to implement other federal mandates.

### How to Support Sanctuary Policies

- Attend city council meetings when sanctuary policies are under debate
- Contact your local officials to express support for detainer refusal policies
- Support organizations tracking sanctuary policy challenges: American Immigration Council (americanimmigrationcouncil.org/fact-sheet/sanctuary-policies-overview/)
- If your jurisdiction does not have sanctuary policies, advocate for adoption: ILRC and CLINIC both have toolkits for local advocacy campaigns

## What Observers Can Legally Do During Enforcement Actions

### First Amendment Rights for Witnesses and Bystanders

The First Amendment protects the right to:
- **Film ICE agents in public**: You have the right to record ICE and other federal agents in public spaces as long as you do not physically interfere with or obstruct their operations. This right is established by multiple federal circuit courts.
- **Provide verbal support**: You can speak with a person being detained, remind them of their rights, and provide verbal support — as long as you are not physically interfering with the agents
- **Document what you witness**: Write down everything you observe, including agent identifications, vehicle license plates, timing, and what statements were made

**The critical line**: You cannot physically obstruct the agents. Even verbal obstruction that delays or impedes enforcement can cross the line into interference, which is a federal crime. The test courts use is whether your conduct materially impeded the enforcement action, not simply whether it was inconvenient or uncomfortable for the agents.

### What Legal Observers Do

Trained legal observers (distinct from community bystanders) follow specific protocols:
1. Identify themselves as legal observers if asked (they do not have to, but doing so often reduces confrontation)
2. Document on video: agents present, badge numbers if visible, vehicle plates, location, exact time
3. Document the identity and circumstances of the person being detained if possible (name, how long detained, where being taken)
4. Do not speak with agents beyond identifying themselves if directly addressed
5. Record everything in writing immediately after the encounter
6. Report to their network coordinator and share footage via secure channels

### Safe Reporting Channels for Witnessing Enforcement

**If you witness what appears to be an ICE operation:**
1. **Call your local rapid response hotline** — This is the primary channel. The hotline dispatches trained observers and triggers the legal support network.
2. **ICIRR 24-Hour Hotline** (Illinois): 1-855-435-7693
3. **Your local ACLU**: State ACLU chapters track enforcement patterns and may want documentation of significant incidents
4. **Detention Watch Network** (detentionwatchnetwork.org): Tracks detention conditions and enforcement abuses
5. **Human Rights First**: Documents enforcement violations and tracks patterns of abuse

**Secure video storage**: If you filmed an enforcement action, do not upload directly to social media without considering the privacy of individuals who may be documented. Contact your local rapid response network or ACLU chapter for guidance on secure submission.

## Practical Action Checklist

### For Individuals and Families

- [ ] Download and store KYR information on your phone (ILRC Red Card, ACLU state-specific KYR)
- [ ] Know the name and number of an immigration attorney you can call if detained
- [ ] Create a family safety plan: who to call, where important documents are stored, who has power of attorney for children
- [ ] Know your local rapid response hotline number

### For Community Members Who Want to Help

- [ ] Find your local rapid response network and attend a training session
- [ ] Learn your state's KYR rules so you can support immigrants in your community
- [ ] Know the difference between administrative warrants (do NOT have to comply) and judicial warrants
- [ ] Practice filming on your phone so you can document quickly under stress

### For Organizers

- [ ] Connect with CLINIC's rapid response toolkit
- [ ] Build a relationship with at least one immigration legal aid organization
- [ ] Establish a Signal channel for your network
- [ ] Conduct regular community KYR trainings
- [ ] Map ICE presence in your area: vehicle descriptions, staging locations, known operation patterns
