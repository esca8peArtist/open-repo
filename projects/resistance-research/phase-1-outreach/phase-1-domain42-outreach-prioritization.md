## 1. Domain 42 Strategic Context

### Why the May 28 DEA Hearing Matters

The DEA's administrative hearing on marijuana rescheduling (Docket No. DEA-1362) begins June 29, 2026. The written participation notice deadline is May 28 — 21 days from this document's creation date. This is not a comment deadline. It is a deadline to notify DEA that your organization intends to appear, testify, or submit formal written evidence. Organizations that miss May 28 are excluded from the record.

The April 23, 2026 Acting AG Todd Blanche order moved state-licensed medical cannabis and FDA-approved cannabis products to Schedule III. Recreational cannabis remained in Schedule I. The hearing will determine whether marijuana as a whole moves to Schedule III — a question that affects the $30B+ legal cannabis industry, 4 million felony-disenfranchised Americans whose convictions remain on record, and the structural legitimacy of a scheduling process that has insulated enforcement priorities from public health expertise for 56 years.

The strategic opportunity is compressed and non-repeating. If the participation window closes without a coordinated filing wave, the next comparable opportunity is 2027 at the earliest. If Section 591 (the House Appropriations rider to freeze DEA rescheduling implementation) survives the May 13 markup and is enacted in conference, the hearing's output loses binding effect entirely. Organizations that file before May 13 establish standing in the administrative record regardless of the Section 591 outcome — organizations that have not filed lose all standing and cannot contest a freeze through APA challenge.

### What Outcomes Are Possible

The hearing can produce four distinct outcomes (documented in Domain 42, Section 2.4):

- **Scenario A (full Schedule III)**: DEA extends Schedule III to all marijuana. This removes the most severe federal penalties and triggers 280E relief, but does not resolve the federal-state conflict for existing state-legal businesses, does not expunge prior convictions, and does not fix the structural DEA regulatory capture problem. A step forward, not a resolution.
- **Scenario B (narrow medical track)**: The April 23 two-track framework is formalized permanently. Recreational cannabis businesses continue paying 280E-effective federal tax rates of 70-80% on state-legal operations. The democratic deadlock persists.
- **Scenario C (Section 591 freeze)**: Congressional appropriations rider blocks implementation. The hearing is moot. Reform reverts to 2024 status.
- **Scenario D (APA litigation)**: Any outcome is challenged in federal court. Timeline extends 12-24 months.

The hearing cannot produce legalization. What it can produce — if civil liberties, drug policy reform, and administrative law voices participate in the record — is a well-developed evidentiary foundation for the democratic accountability argument: that a law enforcement agency has been adjudicating a public health question for 56 years in structural defiance of the statute that created it.

### Why Institutional Participation from the Resistance-Research Framework Matters

Domain 42's distinct contribution is not drug policy analysis. It is a democratic exclusion architecture argument: the communities most subjected to drug enforcement are systematically excluded from the political processes that set drug policy, through felony disenfranchisement and regulatory capture of the scheduling mechanism. Organizations that engage the DEA hearing using this framing — rather than the standard drug policy reform framing — shift the evidentiary record in a direction that enables two things: (1) stronger APA challenges to any restrictive outcome, grounded in the structural independence argument about DEA ALJs adjudicating their own scheduling authority; and (2) a Congressional lobbying argument based on democratic legitimacy rather than drug policy liberalization, which is more persuasive to swing-district legislators.

The 35-domain framework has spent months building the research infrastructure for this argument. The DEA hearing is the first external docket in that framework's history where that research can be entered into a federal administrative record with legal effect.

These five organizations have the institutional standing, speed, and direct relevance to make participation decisive for the hearing record. They should receive the Category A email on May 8, with the Section 591 urgency hook explicit in the message.

**1. Law Enforcement Action Partnership (LEAP) — Diane Goldstein, Executive Director**
*diane@lawenforcementaction.org*

Highest-credibility participant category in a DEA proceeding, bar none. DEA ALJs give disproportionate weight to law enforcement reform voices because they neutralize the institutional capture critique — LEAP participation signals that the argument is not simply from reform advocates but from experienced law enforcement professionals. Goldstein has testified before Congress and in prior DEA proceedings. LEAP's participation notice will likely be designated for oral testimony, giving the democratic exclusion framing a hearing-room presence that written submissions cannot replicate.

**2. Drug Policy Alliance (DPA) — Kassandra Frederique, Executive Director**
*media@drugpolicy.org*

DPA is the institutional anchor of the drug policy reform field. They file in every major DEA proceeding and have standing as a matter of practice. Frederique's leadership connects DPA's existing racial disparity work to Domain 42's democratic exclusion framing in a natural extension rather than a pivot. DPA's participation notice will attract coalition partners — organizations that have not yet decided to file will see DPA's notice and follow. Network multiplier effect is highest among all Tier 1 contacts.

**3. Mason Marks — Yale Law School / Harvard Law Petrie-Flom Center**
*marks@law.yale.edu*

Not an advocacy organization but the single most important academic contact. Domain 42's regulatory capture argument (Section 2.2-2.3) is built on Marks's Yale Law Journal analysis ("Separation of Drug Scheduling Powers," 2024) and his February 2026 Regulatory Review piece. His academic engagement with this document before May 28 authenticates the statutory-historical argument for DEA ALJs and creates the possibility of an amicus brief or formal academic submission to the record. Academic evidence from the statute's leading scholarly interpreter carries different institutional weight than organizational advocacy.

**4. ACLU Criminal Law Reform Project — Brandon Buskey, Project Director**
*criminallaw@aclu.org*

The ACLU's framing of the DEA hearing as a voting rights issue — through the felony disenfranchisement feedback loop (Domain 42, Section 3) — creates a civil liberties record distinct from the drug policy reform record. ACLU participation notices, when accompanied by the democratic exclusion argument, frame the hearing for potential APA litigation: any hearing outcome that does not address the structural independence of DEA ALJs has a civil liberties challenge waiting. The ACLU also has the legal infrastructure to execute on that challenge if the outcome warrants it.

**5. The Sentencing Project — Kara Gotsch, Executive Director**
*staff@sentencingproject.org*

The empirical anchor for the disenfranchisement section of Domain 42 is the Sentencing Project's Locked Out 2022 data (4 million Americans currently disenfranchised due to felony conviction). Gotsch's organization engaging the DEA hearing connects the disenfranchisement research directly to the rescheduling record, which no prior DEA proceeding has included. This is a gap in the existing evidentiary record that Domain 42 specifically addresses. The Sentencing Project's participation strengthens the structural exclusion argument by providing primary-source data in the record itself.

## 4. Success Metrics

Success for this outreach campaign is measured against five benchmarks, in descending order of importance.

**Benchmark 1 — Participation Filing Rate (Primary)**

Target: 5 or more organizations from the 12-contact priority list file a DEA-1362 participation notice by May 28.

Rationale: The 2024 DEA rescheduling hearing had approximately 160 participation requests, of which 25 were designated. Five organizations from the Domain 42 wave represents a 3% share of the total filer pool — modest, but sufficient to introduce the democratic exclusion framing into the record. Fewer than 3 filings from the outreach wave means the deadline approach was insufficiently organized or the framing did not resonate. More than 7 means the wave generated secondary referrals beyond the initial contact list.

**Benchmark 2 — Democratic Exclusion Framing Adoption**

Target: At least 2 organizations cite regulatory capture, felony disenfranchisement, or democratic legitimacy (not merely drug policy reform) in their participation notices or public communications about the hearing.

Rationale: The hearing record is shaped by the framing of participation notices. If the record contains the democratic exclusion argument — specifically, the structural independence question about DEA ALJs adjudicating their own scheduling authority — it strengthens any subsequent APA challenge. A record that contains only "we support Schedule III" is a weaker foundation than a record that contains "the hearing structure itself raises APA independence concerns."

**Benchmark 3 — Academic Engagement**

Target: Mason Marks or at least one additional administrative law faculty member submits a formal comment, academic brief, or public statement on the DEA-1362 hearing before July 15.

Rationale: Academic engagement with the APA structural argument elevates it from advocacy position to legal analysis. A Yale Law professor's public engagement with the hearing — citing the same statutory argument that Domain 42 builds from — creates the citation infrastructure for future litigation.

**Benchmark 4 — Media Coverage**

Target: At least 1 specialist outlet (WeedPress, Marijuana Herald, Filter, Democracy Docket) uses the democratic exclusion framing in their hearing coverage, citing Domain 42 or the organizations that filed using that framing.

Rationale: Media coverage of the hearing shapes how Congressional staff, policy researchers, and advocacy organizations understand the stakes. Coverage that frames the hearing as a democratic accountability story — rather than a drug legalization story — broadens the coalition that cares about the outcome.

**Benchmark 5 — Secondary Referrals**

Target: At least 1 organization that was not on the original 24-contact list files a participation notice or contacts the framework author, having received the Gist from a Category A contact.

Rationale: Network multiplier effect. If DPA forwards the Gist to its partner organizations or NORML emails its coalition about the deadline, the reach extends beyond the original contact list. A single secondary referral that converts to a filing means the outreach generated its own amplification.

## 5. Contingency Paths

### If Phase 1 Launch Is Delayed to May 9-12

**Impact**: Low to moderate. Organizations contacted with 16-19 days remaining have adequate time to prepare and file a three-sentence participation notice. The Section 591 urgency hook weakens as the May 13 markup approaches, but the overall deadline pressure is sufficient.

**What changes**: Update the Section 591 language in Category A emails if markup outcome is known. Compress the Category B and C waves slightly (see `DOMAIN_42_DISTRIBUTION_RISK_MITIGATION.md`, Scenario B for day-by-day recalibration).

**What does not change**: The five Tier 1 contacts remain the priority. Mason Marks should still receive an individualized email on Day 1 of the actual launch, regardless of when Day 0 falls.

### If Phase 1 Launch Is Delayed to May 13-17

**Impact**: Moderate. Organizations contacted with 11-15 days remaining can still file, but the policy review and internal decision-making window is compressed. Some organizations with slower approval processes (state AG offices, LDF) may not be able to act in time.

**Revised timeline for May 13-17 launch:**

Collapse Category A and B into a single three-day window (May 13-15). Move Category C to May 16-19. Skip state AG press office outreach if launch is May 15 or later — they require more lead time and unlikely to act with fewer than 10 days notice. Focus the compressed window on the five Tier 1 contacts plus MPP and NORML.

**Mail submission is foreclosed.** The May 20 postal transit cutoff to reach DEA's Springfield VA address cannot be met. All outreach directs organizations to email: nprm@dea.gov, Docket No. DEA-1362.

### If Phase 1 Launch Is Delayed Past May 21

**Impact**: High for participation filing rate; lower for long-term document utility.

The hearing participation window effectively closes for organizations that have not been contacted by May 21. Policy teams at institutional organizations require 5-7 business days to draft and approve an administrative filing. With 7 days remaining, only the most nimble organizations (DPA, SSDP) can act.

**Minimum viable push for May 21-24:** Send only three emails (Drug Policy Alliance, LEAP, Mason Marks — the minimum viable wave from Section 2.2 of the risk playbook). These three are the highest-probability participants and have the widest network effect.

**What does not fail permanently:** The Gist remains live. The Domain 42 document remains the authoritative analysis. Organizations that missed the participation window can still submit written comments to the administrative record after the hearing (typically 30 days post-close), brief reporters covering the June 29-July 15 hearing, and engage with testimony submitted by organizations that did file participation notices. The post-May 28 engagement path is documented in `DOMAIN_42_DISTRIBUTION_RISK_MITIGATION.md`, Section 1, Scenario C.

### If Section 591 Survives Markup and Is Enacted in Conference

**Impact**: Significant for hearing utility; does not eliminate all value.

A Section 591 freeze would block DEA from implementing the April 23 order and freeze the hearing's output. The administrative record — including any participation notices and comments filed before May 28 — remains in existence and provides a foundation for APA litigation challenging the freeze. Organizations that filed before May 28 have standing to challenge a Section 591 application that conflicts with prior DEA rulemaking commitments.

**Domain 42 remains useful:** The democratic exclusion framing does not depend on the hearing producing a favorable outcome. A Section 591 freeze is itself evidence of the democratic accountability failure that Domain 42 documents — Congress overriding a federal agency rulemaking process on behalf of enforcement interests despite 68% public support for the underlying policy change.
