# Domain 57: Multilateral Withdrawal & Regional Destabilization
## Research Outline — Phase 2 Production Preparation

*Research outline — Phase 2 pre-production, Session May 17, 2026*

---

## Lead Finding

The United States has, since January 2025, executed the largest single withdrawal from international institutions in its history — and arguably in any democratic nation's modern history. On January 7, 2026, the Trump administration formally announced withdrawal from 66 international organizations, including 31 UN entities. This followed withdrawals from the World Health Organization, the UN Human Rights Council, and the Paris Agreement in the first weeks of the second term. Combined with the imposition of financial sanctions on International Criminal Court judges and prosecutors, the blocking of WTO Appellate Body appointments (creating an existential budget crisis), and active threats to withdraw from NATO, the US has systematically dismantled the multilateral institutional framework that has functioned — however imperfectly — as a constraint on unilateral executive power in US foreign and domestic policy for 80 years.

The democratic accountability argument is precise and requires careful articulation: the harm from multilateral withdrawal is not primarily geopolitical (though it is that too). It is constitutional. International treaty obligations, ratified with Senate consent, function as constraints on executive discretion. When the executive unilaterally withdraws from them — circumventing the Senate ratification requirement — it simultaneously expands presidential power and removes the accountability structures that international institutions provide. The withdrawal from the International Institute for Democracy and Electoral Assistance (International IDEA) is a direct example: the US has withdrawn from the primary intergovernmental body dedicated to monitoring the health of electoral systems globally, at the precise moment when US electoral integrity is under stress.

This document maps the causal pathways from multilateral withdrawal to democratic accountability loss, documents the current empirical scope of US disengagement, draws on precedent cases (Hungary, Turkey, 1930s US isolationism), and identifies the movement leverage available to resist or reverse the withdrawal trajectory.

## The Constitutional Framework: Treaty Obligations as Democratic Constraint

Before mapping the specific pathways, the document must establish the constitutional argument that makes multilateral withdrawal a democracy issue rather than merely a foreign policy issue.

**The Senate consent requirement and its circumvention**: The US Constitution's treaty clause (Article II, Section 2) requires Senate advice and consent (two-thirds majority) to ratify international treaties. Once ratified, treaties become the supreme law of the land (Article VI, Supremacy Clause). Presidential withdrawal from ratified treaties without Senate consent is constitutionally contested: the Department of Justice Office of Legal Counsel issued a 2020 opinion claiming exclusive presidential power over treaty withdrawal, but Congress has explicitly rejected this claim — most recently in the 2024 National Defense Authorization Act, which prohibited presidential withdrawal from NATO without Senate consent or Congressional action.

**The Youngstown framework**: The Supreme Court's *Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co. v. Sawyer* (1952) provides the applicable framework for assessing unilateral executive action against congressional prohibition. Where the president acts contrary to congressional prohibition, presidential authority is "at its lowest ebb" and can be sustained only if the president has exclusive constitutional authority — a claim the Youngstown framework instructs courts to scrutinize with caution. The Trump administration's unilateral withdrawal from 66 international organizations, including those established by ratified treaties, is squarely in Youngstown's third category.

**The accountability gap from withdrawal**: International institutions create accountability mechanisms that function domestically. UN Human Rights Council review processes create reporting obligations that generate public information about US practices. WTO dispute resolution creates enforceable standards that constrain unilateral trade actions. The ICC creates potential accountability for international crimes by US officials and armed forces. NATO Article 5 collective defense obligations constrain unilateral military adventurism. When these constraints are removed, executive discretion expands in precisely the domains where democratic accountability is most difficult to maintain: war powers, trade policy, human rights, and international law compliance.

## Causal Pathways: Six Mechanisms from Withdrawal to Democratic Harm

### Pathway 1: NATO Withdrawal — War Powers and Collective Defense Constraints

**Current status**: The Trump administration has repeatedly threatened NATO withdrawal without executing it, while systematically weakening the alliance's credibility. The NDAA 2024 Section 1250A prohibits presidential withdrawal without Senate consent or Congressional action — the first statute in US history to explicitly constrain presidential unilateral treaty withdrawal. Trump has threatened withdrawal over NATO spending disputes, over Greenland, and following the Iran conflict (April 2026 *Time* report). The administration has yet to formally invoke Article 13 (the one-year withdrawal notice provision), but has used withdrawal threats as leverage.

**The democratic accountability harm**: NATO Article 5 creates a collective defense obligation that requires allied consultation before military action. This consultation mechanism functions as a constraint on unilateral presidential war-making. If the US withdraws from NATO (or functionally withdraws by rendering Article 5 non-credible), the president gains the ability to conduct military operations without the allied consultation process that has historically — however imperfectly — created deliberative pause before force deployment.

The Congressional Research Service's February 2026 analysis of "Separation of Powers and NATO Withdrawal" (R48868) documents the constitutional tensions directly: the President cannot unilaterally withdraw under Section 1250A, but the OLC 2020 opinion claims exclusive executive authority. No court has resolved this conflict.

**The war powers dimension**: The War Powers Resolution (1973) already provides only weak constraints on presidential military action. NATO withdrawal would remove one of the few remaining institutional checks on unilateral force deployment by: eliminating the allied consultation requirement embedded in the North Atlantic Treaty; removing the political cost of acting without allied support; and potentially creating an environment where the president claims a foreign policy emergency justifies bypassing Congressional authorization.

**The Iran conflict as test case**: The April 2026 *Time* report that Trump is "strongly considering pulling the U.S. out of NATO" following allied criticism of the Iran war demonstrates that the NATO withdrawal threat is being deployed as a punitive mechanism against allied democratic oversight — a means of insulating executive military action from allied accountability.

### Pathway 2: International Courts and Accountability Structures — ICC Sanctions and Impunity Signals

**Current status**: The US imposed sanctions on ICC judges and prosecutors via Executive Order 14203 (February 2025), invoking IEEPA. By December 2025, 11 ICC judges and prosecutors had been designated. The administration's stated rationale is that the ICC has "no legitimacy or jurisdiction" over the US and Israel. These sanctions are unprecedented: no democratic government has previously imposed financial sanctions on international court judges.

**The constitutional basis and its implications**: EO 14203 invokes IEEPA to declare the ICC's investigative activities an "unusual and extraordinary threat to national security." This framing — using emergency economic powers to neutralize an international judicial body — expands the scope of presidential emergency power into new territory: the suppression of international legal accountability mechanisms. The UN Special Rapporteur called for withdrawal of the sanctions, stating they violated US obligations under international law.

**The democratic accountability harm**: The ICC functions as an accountability mechanism of last resort for atrocity crimes. Its existence signals that state actors — including potentially US officials and military personnel — face potential accountability for international crimes. The US's non-membership in the Rome Statute has historically been rationalized by alternative accountability mechanisms. The imposition of sanctions on ICC prosecutors goes further: it actively suppresses the court's ability to function, extending US non-accountability beyond US borders.

The impunity signal matters for domestic democratic accountability: the same executive branch that sanctioned ICC prosecutors has also withdrawn from UN Human Rights Council review mechanisms and eliminated data collection on food security. The pattern is consistent — reducing the external accountability inputs that constrain executive action.

**Hungary precedent**: Hungary announced withdrawal from the ICC in April 2025 following Orbán's invitation to Netanyahu during an active ICC arrest warrant. The US sanctions preceded and arguably enabled Hungary's ICC withdrawal. Democratic Erosion (2026) documented that neither international isolation nor EU sanctions have reversed Hungary's democratic decline — raising the empirical question of whether international accountability mechanisms are effective even when they exist.

### Pathway 3: UN Human Rights Mechanisms — Reporting Obligations and Domestic Accountability

**Current status**: The US withdrew from the UN Human Rights Council in February 2025 (second withdrawal — first was under Trump's first term in 2018). The US also withdrew from UNRWA and ordered review of UNESCO involvement. The January 2026 withdrawal from 66 organizations included the International Institute for Democracy and Electoral Assistance — the intergovernmental body dedicated to monitoring electoral systems globally.

**The accountability mechanism**: UN Human Rights Council Universal Periodic Review (UPR) requires all UN member states to report on their human rights situation periodically. While non-binding and criticized for insufficient enforcement, UPR creates: a structured reporting obligation that requires government documentation of domestic rights practices; a public record that civil society and media can use to press for accountability; and diplomatic pressure through peer review.

Withdrawal from UNHRC removes the US from UPR participation (as a Council member) and eliminates the formal reporting obligations that create government documentation. It also reduces the US's diplomatic leverage in shaping UNHRC resolutions on other countries' human rights practices — a strategic self-weakening in addition to the accountability harm.

**International IDEA withdrawal**: The withdrawal from International IDEA — established in 1995 to support democracy and electoral assistance globally — is particularly symbolic. IDEA monitors electoral systems, provides technical assistance to emerging democracies, and produces comparative data on democratic health. The US withdrawal eliminates its observer status and funding, and signals to the 37 member states that the US no longer supports the multilateral democratic assistance infrastructure.

**The domestic implication**: International human rights monitoring generates public information about domestic practices that civil society, media, and courts use domestically. Withdrawal reduces this information flow. Combined with the elimination of USDA food security data collection, the DOJ's reduced civil rights enforcement capacity (documented in Domain 56), and the suppression of internal agency reporting under Schedule F, the withdrawal from international monitoring contributes to a broader pattern of information environment degradation.

**Production research needed**: ASIL Insight on Trump's UNHRC withdrawal. Focus 2030 analysis of the 66-organization withdrawal. IDDRI blog post on what US withdrawal means for international system reform. Al Jazeera reporting on the full list of withdrawals.

### Pathway 4: WTO and Trade Law — Rules-Based Order vs. Power-Based Enforcement

**Current status**: The US has not formally withdrawn from the WTO but has suspended financial contributions (March 2025), creating an existential budget crisis. The April 2, 2025 "Liberation Day" tariffs and April 9 flat 10% tariffs violate WTO tariff bindings under international trade law. The US Trade Representative has explicitly rejected WTO dispute resolution in favor of unilateral enforcement: USTR Greer announced the US will "closely monitor implementation and swiftly reimpose higher tariffs for noncompliance" — wholesale abandonment of rules-based enforcement for power-based enforcement.

The US has been responsible for the largest number of "appeals into the void" — 9 of 24 total — through its blocking of Appellate Body appointments that has paralyzed WTO dispute settlement since 2019. The WTO's 14th Ministerial Conference (MC14, March 2026, Yaoundé) will have to grapple with the future of the organization without meaningful US engagement.

**The democratic accountability harm**: WTO membership creates binding trade obligations that constrain executive discretion over tariffs and trade policy. The Constitution gives Congress the power to regulate commerce with foreign nations — presidential tariff authority derives from statutory delegation (Section 232, Section 301, IEEPA). When the executive bypasses WTO dispute resolution, it: removes the international constraint on unilateral presidential tariff action; concentrates trade policy power in the executive without corresponding international accountability; and uses the WTO contribution freeze as leverage without Congressional authorization.

The IEEPA-based tariffs are particularly significant: the Trump administration has invoked IEEPA — a national emergency statute — to impose sweeping tariffs without Congressional authorization, arguing that trade deficits constitute a national emergency. WTO non-participation reduces the international legal constraint on this executive power expansion.

**The trade-war domestic democratic cost**: Tariff shocks create economic winners and losers in ways that are politically opaque to most voters. When trade policy is made through executive emergency declarations rather than Congressional action, democratic deliberation about trade-offs is bypassed. The communities most exposed to retaliatory tariffs (agricultural, export-dependent manufacturing) lose both the economic benefit and the political voice that WTO dispute resolution would provide.

### Pathway 5: Asylum Law and Refugee Protections — Non-Refoulement and International Refugee Regime

**Current status**: The US Refugee Admissions Program (USRAP) has been suspended indefinitely, with the 9th Circuit upholding the indefinite refugee ban in 2026, keeping admissions capped at 7,500. The administration imposed a sweeping pause on asylum decisions in late 2025 affecting nearly 4 million pending cases. A freeze remains in place for applicants from approximately 40 countries. On May 18, 2026 — the day before this document is being drafted — DHS issued a memo stating intent to arrest refugees in the US for more than one year who have not applied for permanent residence.

The 1951 Refugee Convention and its 1967 Protocol — to which the US is a party — establish the principle of non-refoulement: the prohibition on returning a refugee to a place where they face serious risk of persecution. The 8 USC 1158 domestic codification of asylum rights derives from this international obligation.

**The multilateral accountability mechanism**: UNHCR monitoring and reporting on US compliance with refugee obligations creates an international accountability layer. US withdrawal from UNRWA (the UN agency for Palestinian refugees) and from the UNHRC reduces the institutional context in which US refugee policy is subject to multilateral scrutiny.

**The democratic harm pathway**: Asylum and refugee protection represents one of the domains where international law is most clearly superior — in terms of both substantive protection and democratic legitimacy — to unilateral executive discretion. The Refugee Convention's non-refoulement obligation was ratified by the Senate. The Trump administration's suspension of USRAP and asylum processing, its bilateral agreements to remove asylum seekers to third countries before full hearing, and its May 2026 memo threatening refugees with arrest — all represent executive action taken against the grain of Senate-ratified treaty obligations.

When the US withdraws from international refugee oversight mechanisms, it removes the accountability layer that allows civil society and international organizations to document violations. The immigration legal aid community (IRAP, HIAS, USCRI) provides the domestic civil society infrastructure that has been partially substituting for international accountability — but faces capacity constraints.

**Production research needed**: IRAP explainer on Trump USCIS policies impacting refugees. HIAS deep dive on international refugee law obligations. USCRI summary of January 20 executive orders on refugees. ASAP Together resource on law changes for asylum seekers.

### Pathway 6: Regional Destabilization — Security Void and Authoritarian Opportunity

**Current status**: The broader consequence of US multilateral withdrawal is the creation of institutional voids that authoritarian states and sub-state actors fill. This pathway operates at the systemic level: as the US withdraws from multilateral frameworks that provide both normative constraints and practical coordination, the relative power of actors operating outside those frameworks increases.

**The Hungary-Turkey-Russia precedent cluster**:

*Hungary*: Orbán's government has used its EU membership while systematically dismantling democratic institutions internally. The international accountability mechanisms that exist — EU Article 7 procedures, ECJ rulings, European Parliament resolutions — have been ineffective in reversing the democratic decline. Democratic Erosion (2026) documents that neither isolation nor EU fund suspension reversed Hungarian backsliding. The lesson for Domain 57 is sobering: international institutions can document and express concern about democratic erosion, but they struggle to reverse it when the eroding state can exploit institutional exit mechanisms.

Orbán's Hungary has benefited from US alignment under Trump: the Trump-Orbán relationship provides political cover for Hungary's domestic authoritarianism while reducing the pressure from the transatlantic alliance to conform to democratic norms. This is the bilateral dimension of US multilateral withdrawal — withdrawing from multilateral accountability creates space for bilateral deals that further undermine the multilateral framework.

*Turkey*: Under Erdogan, Turkey represents a case of a NATO member state drifting toward authoritarianism while remaining inside the alliance — demonstrating that alliance membership is not a sufficient constraint on democratic backsliding. Turkey's purchase of Russian S-400 systems, its military operations in Syria without allied consultation, and its imprisonment of journalists and opposition leaders all occurred within its NATO membership. This suggests that even with multilateral membership, the accountability mechanism has limits — and further suggests that withdrawal removes even those limited constraints.

*1930s US precedent*: American isolationism in the 1930s was driven by post-WWI revisionism (the Nye Committee's finding that the US had been manipulated into WWI by financial interests) and the economic crisis of the Depression. FDR's rejection of international cooperation at the 1933 London Economic Conference — choosing unilateral action over multilateral coordination — reinforced British appeasement and French paralysis. The historical record shows that US withdrawal from international coordination in the 1930s did not prevent eventual war; it reduced the multilateral architecture's capacity to deter or respond to it.

The parallel to 2025-2026 is not precise — the US has not formally withdrawn from NATO, and the geopolitical context differs. But the structural logic is similar: US withdrawal from multilateral coordination reduces the collective capacity to constrain aggressive actors, emboldens those actors, and leaves democratic states to navigate destabilizing pressures with reduced institutional support.

**The democratic harm in the US context**: Regional destabilization creates refugee flows, economic disruption, and security anxieties that domestic actors use to justify further executive power concentration. The Iran conflict (referenced in April 2026 NATO reporting) is the current most prominent example: military action taken with reduced allied consultation, used as justification for threats to withdraw from NATO when allies expressed concern. The cycle is self-reinforcing: withdrawal → unilateral action → destabilization → security emergency → further executive power concentration.

## Precedent Cases: Historical and Comparative

### Case 1: Hungary — Institutional Capture from Within

Hungary under Orbán provides the most instructive precedent for understanding how a democratic state can systematically dismantle accountability mechanisms while remaining formally within multilateral institutions. Orbán's government has: captured the judicial system, weakened the independent media, eliminated university independence (CEU), used emergency powers to govern by decree, withdrawn from the ICC, and maintained EU membership as a financial resource while blocking EU democratic enforcement mechanisms.

The international accountability mechanisms have been largely ineffective. The EU's Article 7 procedure (suspended fundamental rights) has been activated against Hungary but has not reversed democratic decline because it requires unanimity of other member states, and Poland (until 2024) blocked consensus. ECJ rulings against Hungary on judicial independence have been defied or minimally complied with. International IDEA and Freedom House both classify Hungary as a "hybrid regime" — no longer a full democracy.

**The lesson for Domain 57**: International institutions create accountability pressure but cannot compel compliance without enforcement mechanisms that democratic states collectively choose not to use. The US's situation is different — it is withdrawing from institutions rather than subverting them from within — but the lesson holds: institutional exit reduces the accountability pressure available to civil society and international partners without eliminating the domestic political struggle entirely.

The Tandfonline (2026) article on democratic decline and illiberal citizenship in Hungary and the Springer/Comparative European Politics article on opposition strategies under Orbán provide the most current academic analysis.

---

### Case 2: Turkey — Alliance Membership Without Accountability

Turkey's trajectory under Erdogan demonstrates that alliance membership (NATO) is compatible with significant democratic backsliding when alliance partners prioritize strategic utility over democratic norms. Turkey has remained in NATO despite: imprisonment of 150,000+ people following the 2016 coup attempt (including journalists, judges, and military officers); purchase of Russian S-400 systems incompatible with NATO standards; military operations in Syria conducted without allied consultation; and ongoing suppression of the Kurdish political opposition.

NATO has responded with expressions of concern but not expulsion or formal sanction. The alliance's Article 5 collective defense commitment has been maintained. This demonstrates both the limits of alliance accountability and the strategic durability of alliance membership even under conditions of democratic erosion.

**The lesson for Domain 57**: US withdrawal from multilateral institutions would remove accountability pressure on the US (the parallel to Hungary), but also remove US leverage over other states' democratic compliance (the parallel to NATO's Turkey problem). Both effects simultaneously reduce multilateral democratic accountability.

---

### Case 3: 1930s US — The Isolationist Precedent and Its Costs

American isolationism in the 1930s was a policy choice made under conditions somewhat parallel to today: post-war revisionism, economic crisis, and domestic political pressure to focus on internal concerns. The Nye Committee's 1934-36 hearings on munitions manufacturers ("merchants of death") created a revisionist narrative about US WWI entry that strengthened isolationism. The Neutrality Acts of 1935-37 legally constrained executive ability to support European democracies.

FDR's rejection of the 1933 London Economic Conference — choosing unilateral monetary policy over international coordination — is the clearest precedent for the current administration's preference for bilateral power-based enforcement over multilateral rules-based engagement. The State Department's Office of the Historian characterizes the 1930s as a period when the US "ran for cover as militarism and virulent nationalism began to engulf Europe and East Asia" — with the US withdrawal reinforcing British appeasement and French paralysis.

**The lesson for Domain 57**: Historical isolationism did not protect US security or democracy — it delayed American engagement while allowing the conditions for conflict to deteriorate. The current withdrawal does not directly parallel the 1930s (the US faces different threats and different institutional architecture), but the structural dynamic — US withdrawal reduces collective capacity to constrain aggressive actors, which eventually produces worse outcomes requiring larger US response — is precedent for the democratic cost of institutional exit.

## Research Gaps and Production Priorities

**Highest priority gaps for July research phase**:

1. **Enforcement mechanisms for Section 1250A** — No court has ruled on the constitutionality of the NDAA's prohibition on presidential NATO withdrawal. Scholarly analysis of enforceability is needed. CRS Report R48868 is the starting point; legal commentary from Just Security and Lawfare extends it.

2. **Full list of treaties and treaty obligations affected by the 66-organization withdrawal** — The White House list names organizations, but the specific treaty obligations (and their ratification history) need to be mapped. Some withdrawals may be from executive agreements (easier to exit) vs. Senate-ratified treaties (more constitutionally protected).

3. **Empirical case for democratic consequences of specific institution withdrawals** — The normative argument (withdrawals reduce constraints on executive power) is strong; the empirical documentation of specific democratic harms from specific withdrawals needs deeper development. What specific actions has the executive taken that international institution membership would have constrained?

4. **Asylum and refugee litigation docket** — What cases are currently active? What are the legal theories? Which organizations have filed amicus briefs arguing from the Refugee Convention and Protocol obligations?

5. **The ICC sanctions enforcement record** — Has any ICC work been visibly disrupted by the sanctions? Has any sanctioned prosecutor or judge been unable to perform their work? Documentation of real-world impact of sanctions on court functioning.
