### 1.2 South Korea 1987 — June Democratic Struggle

**Claim supported**: Democratic transition is achievable in concentrated windows under mass mobilization + elite defection + international constraints. South Korea transitioned from military-backed authoritarian state to democracy in under six months of active political movement after years of civil society groundwork. The June 29 Declaration (direct elections concession) came 19 days after the mass marches began.

**Citations**:
- Global Nonviolent Action Database, Swarthmore College: "South Koreans Win Mass Campaign for Democracy, 1986-87." Available: https://nvdatabase.swarthmore.edu/content/south-koreans-win-mass-campaign-democracy-1986-87
- Chatham House: "South Korea's Democratic Evolution" (2022). Available: https://www.chathamhouse.org/2022/07/contested-politics-south-korea/2-south-koreas-democratic-evolution
- Wilson Center / 38North documentation on US pressure and Olympic constraint. Available: https://www.38north.org/2018/02/aoneill020818/
- Wikipedia, "June 29 Declaration." Available: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/June_29_Declaration

**Internal cross-reference**: `phase-4-comparative-democratic-recovery.md` (Case 1: South Korea); `democratic-renewal-proposal.md` (Part I, Section 1.3)

**Deployment**: Objection II ("politically impossible"), Objection III.1 ("no political majority"), Objection V.1 ("incrementalism better")

**Key data points**:
- 85.7% of middle class (May 1987 survey) believed human rights more important than economic growth — the threshold for middle-class defection
- US pressure (Assistant Secretary Sigur; IOC threat) prevented military force deployment — external constraints as ceiling on repression
- December 1987 elections under new constitution: completed in under 7 months from June 29 Declaration

**Citations**:
- Friedrich Naumann Foundation: "Remembering Adolfo Suárez" (analysis of reforma pactada mechanism). Available: https://www.freiheit.org/spain-italy-portugal-and-mediterranean-dialogue/remembering-adolfo-suarez

**Internal cross-reference**: `phase-4-comparative-democratic-recovery.md` (Case 2: Spain)

**Deployment**: Objection III.4 ("future administration will undo everything"), Objection V.1 ("incrementalism better")

**Key data points**:

- November 1976: Francoist Cortes voted for its own dissolution — structural reform using existing legal procedures

### 1.4 Uruguay 1980-1985 — Plebiscite Test and Recovery

**Claim supported**: Institutional resilience under sustained authoritarian pressure. Uruguay recovered from military dictatorship beginning with a plebiscite rejection of the military's constitutional framework (57.2% against, November 1980) — under conditions of severe media restriction and opposition suppression.

**Citations**:
- Library of Congress Country Studies, Uruguay: https://countrystudies.us/uruguay/25.htm
- Linz and Stepan, *Problems of Democratic Transition and Consolidation: Southern Europe, South America, and Post-Communist Europe* (Johns Hopkins University Press, 1996) — canonical comparative democratization text. Available: https://www.press.jhu.edu/books/title/2194/problems-democratic-transition-and-consolidation

**Internal cross-reference**: `phase-4-comparative-democratic-recovery.md` (Case 3: Uruguay)

**Deployment**: Objection III.3 ("already captured"), Objection V.1 ("incrementalism better")

**Key data points**:
- Military controlled media and severely restricted opposition — yet the plebiscite failed 57.2% against
- Political parties "nearly unanimous in opposition" demonstrated capacity to reorganize on short notice
- 12-year transition arc (1973-1985) — organizational persistence through repression as the enabling factor

## EVIDENCE CLUSTER 2: Loper Bright and Post-Chevron Legal Landscape (Rebuttals for Objection II)

### 2.1 Loper Bright Administrative Law Trifecta — Full Documentation

**Claim supported**: Loper Bright overruled Chevron deference; Corner Post eliminated APA statute of limitations; Jarkesy imposed Seventh Amendment jury trial requirement for agency monetary penalties. Together: the most comprehensive administrative law restructuring since the New Deal.

**Citations**:
- American Bar Association: "The End of Chevron Deference: What Does It Mean, and What Comes Next?" (August 2024). Available: https://www.americanbar.org/groups/business_law/resources/business-law-today/2024-august/end-chevron-deference-what-does-it-mean-what-comes-next/
- Harvard Journal on Legislation: "Two Takes on Administrative Change from the Roberts Court" (2024). Available: https://journals.law.harvard.edu/jol/2024/09/24/two-takes-on-administrative-change-from-the-roberts-court/
- Policy Integrity: "Loper Bright and the End of the Chevron Doctrine." Available: https://policyintegrity.org/publications/detail/loper-bright-and-the-end-of-the-chevron-doctrine
- Florida Bar: "The Death of Chevron: What Loper Bright Means for the Future of Administrative Law." Available: https://www.floridabar.org/the-florida-bar-journal/the-death-of-chevron-what-loper-bright-means-for-the-future-of-administrative-law/
- Congress.gov CRS Report R48320: "Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo and the Future of Agency Interpretations of Law." Available: https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R48320

**Internal cross-reference**: `domain-35-supreme-court-2026-term-preview-post-loper-landscape.md` (Sections 1.1–1.4)

**Key data points**:
- Loper Bright holding: APA Section 706 directs courts to "decide all relevant questions of law" — incompatible with Chevron deference instruction
- Corner Post: APA statute of limitations runs from plaintiff's first injury, not rule promulgation — perpetual challengeability for all existing agency rules
- Jarkesy: Seventh Amendment jury trial requirement for "legal" remedies (civil penalties) — increases enforcement cost for NLRB, FTC, CFPB, OSHA
- Statutory preservation clause in Loper Bright: prior Chevron-era holdings remain stare decisis — but Corner Post reopens them from new plaintiffs

**Citations**:
- New Zealand Electoral Commission, 2011 Referendum Results. Available: https://www.electoral.govt.nz
- FairVote: "Ranked Choice Voting and Proportional Representation in Practice" (2025)

**Internal cross-reference**: `democratic-renewal-proposal.md` (Domain 1a); `research-notes.md` (New Zealand section)

**Key data points**:
- MMP adopted via referendum 1993; first used 1996
- 2011 referendum: offered choice between MMP, FPTP, preferential, STV, Supplementary Member — MMP won 57.8%
- Post-MMP outcomes: higher women's representation, more competitive elections, maintained government stability

## EVIDENCE CLUSTER 6: Civil Resistance and Movement Effectiveness (Supporting Context)

### 6.1 Chenoweth — 3.5% Threshold and Nonviolent Effectiveness

**Claim supported**: Nonviolent campaigns with active participation exceeding 3.5% of the population have not failed historically. U.S. October 2025 protests reached 2.1% (7 million) — below threshold but within mobilization range.

**Citations**:
- Erica Chenoweth and Maria Stephan: *Why Civil Resistance Works* (Columbia University Press, 2011)
- Chenoweth updated research through 2019: "Questions, Answers, and Some Cautionary Updates Regarding the 3.5% Rule"

**Internal cross-reference**: `research-notes.md` (Resistance Research Notes, Chenoweth section); `democratic-renewal-proposal.md` (Part I, Section 1.3)

**Key data points**:
- Original dataset: 323+ campaigns 1900–2006; nonviolent success rate ~53% vs. violent ~26%
- 3.5% threshold: no campaign exceeding this participation level has ultimately failed in the dataset
- Post-2019 update: both violent and nonviolent campaigns have become less effective since 2010; authoritarian regimes have learned

---

### 6.2 Civil Rights Movement — Strategic Litigation + Mass Organizing Model

**Claim supported**: Durable victories required the combination of legal strategy (NAACP litigation) and mass organizing (SCLC, SNCC). Neither alone was sufficient.

**Citations**:
- Doug McAdam: *Political Process and the Development of Black Insurgency* (University of Chicago Press, 1982)
- Charles Payne: *I've Got the Light of Freedom: The Organizing Tradition and the Mississippi Freedom Struggle* (University of California Press, 1995)

**Internal cross-reference**: `research-notes.md` (Movement Case Studies, Civil Rights Movement section)

**Key data points**:
- NAACP strategic litigation began 1909; mass civil rights movement began 1955 — 46-year legal foundation before mass action
- Birmingham campaign (1963): Project C's children's marches created national media coverage via Bull Connor's response — classic dilemma action
- Selma (1965): Bloody Sunday televised violence broke Senate filibuster on Voting Rights Act in under two months
