## OVERVIEW AND DESIGN LOGIC

This protocol is built for one specific purpose: producing expert-validated analysis that fills the remaining analytical gaps in the Domain H production document and confirms or challenges the document's existing conclusions. The production document (7,500 words, 90+ citations, completed June 6, 2026) is the starting point — this framework is not a from-scratch interview guide, it is a precision instrument for obtaining the expert engagement the existing research cannot provide through secondary sources alone.

Four design principles:

**Principle 1: Every question maps to a specific Zone gap.** The questions are not generic constitutional law questions. Each maps to a specific section of the Domain H Zone architecture where the existing research either (a) acknowledges uncertainty about the scholarly consensus, (b) makes a claim that expert validation would substantially strengthen, or (c) identifies a research thread that would extend the argument beyond its current boundary.

**Principle 2: Open-ended leads, specific closes.** The questions open broadly (to surface what the expert thinks is most important, which may not match what the researcher thinks matters most) and close specifically (to get usable, citable answers on the targeted analytical question). The open-ended opening also produces the unpredictable insights — the expert's most important claims are often not the ones the researcher expected to ask about.

**Principle 3: Stress-testing over confirmation.** The strongest interview questions ask experts to identify where the existing analysis is wrong, incomplete, or overstated — not to confirm what the researcher already believes. Questions 3, 5, and 6 are designed for this purpose. A "that's right" answer to a stress-test question is more valuable than a "that's right" answer to a leading question.

**Principle 4: Snowball sampling built in.** Question 8 is the systematic snowball sampling mechanism. Every interview should produce at least one new expert contact recommendation. Over 25-30 interviews, this surfaces a second tier of specialists (often more specific to niche areas of the research) that would not have appeared in the initial contact mapping.

## THE 8-QUESTION PROTOCOL

### Pre-Interview Setup (5 minutes before the call)

Send the expert a one-page context document 48 hours before the interview. The context document should include:
- One paragraph identifying the research project (Phase 3 Domain H: Constitutional Resilience Architecture — a research brief on the constitutional design gaps that enable democratic backsliding, international precedents for resilience mechanisms, and the amendment strategy for the January 2027 congressional window)
- A single sentence identifying the Zone most relevant to their expertise
- A note that the interview is 30 minutes, semi-structured, and will be synthesized into a research brief for distribution to constitutional law scholars and congressional staff

Do NOT send the full Domain H document before the interview. Sending the full document risks anchoring the expert to the researcher's existing analysis rather than eliciting their independent judgment. Send after the interview, with a request for accuracy review.

---

### Question 1: Establishing Priorities (Open-Ended)
**"What's the most critical gap in how courts — and constitutional scholars generally — currently understand the relationship between executive power and democratic resilience? Where is the field getting it most wrong, or where is there the most important unresolved disagreement?"**

Zone mapping: Zone 1 (Constitutional Vulnerabilities, all four sections). This is the opening question for all interviews regardless of the expert's specialty, because it surfaces their priority ordering. An emergency powers specialist will lead with Zone 1.1; a judicial reform scholar will lead with Zone 1.2; a state constitutional law scholar with Zone 1.3. The answer to Q1 determines which subsequent questions to emphasize in the remaining 25 minutes.

Why this framing works: "What's getting it most wrong" is more intellectually provocative than "What's most important" — it invites genuine disagreement and forces the expert to take a position rather than survey the field neutrally.

Synthesis extraction: Note the expert's priority framework, not just their specific claim. An expert who leads with "the problem is the courts have abdicated responsibility for maintaining democratic structure" is operating from a different theoretical frame than one who leads with "the problem is scholars have no theory of when constitutional norms matter versus just politics." The frame shapes how their other answers should be interpreted.

Follow-up probe (if the answer is abstract): "Can you give me a specific recent case or ruling where you think this gap produced a concrete failure?"

---

### Question 2: Prescriptive Constitutional Design (The Expert's Model)
**"If you were designing a constitutional amendment to constrain executive power and protect the independence of democratic institutions, what would be the three essential structural guardrails? What does the scholarship suggest are the non-negotiable design elements for resilience?"**

Zone mapping: Zone 3 (Amendment Strategy), specifically Zone 3.2 (Five Priority Amendment Pathways). The question asks experts to generate a design model independent of political feasibility — which surfaces the analytically ideal structure against which the feasible proposals can be compared.

Why this framing works: The "three essential guardrails" framing forces prioritization and comparability across interviews. If 20 experts each name "independent ethics enforcement" as Guardrail 1, that's a strong signal for Zone 3 amendment prioritization. If there's wide disagreement, that's a signal about the field's internal uncertainty that the research should surface.

Synthesis extraction: Record the specific guardrails named, but more importantly record the *reasoning* behind each. The reasoning is usually more analytically useful than the conclusion — an expert who names "term limits" as a guardrail because "predictable appointment schedules prevent partisan capture over time" is making a different argument from one who names term limits because "lifetime tenure produces bad institutional incentives for individual justices." These are different claims that require different evidence.

Follow-up probe: "Of those three guardrails, which one is most undervalued in the current reform conversation? Which one does the political system seem most likely to actually implement?"

---

### Question 3: Threat Assessment from SCOTUS History
**"What SCOTUS ruling or constitutional development from the past 10 years do you think has had the most dangerous precedential impact on the structural relationship between the branches — the one that has done the most damage to the constitutional architecture of separation of powers? And is the damage reversible?"**

Zone mapping: Zone 1.2 (Judicial Capture Post-Dobbs/Loper Bright) and Zone 3 (Amendment Strategy). The "past 10 years" window captures Shelby County (2013), Dobbs (2022), Loper Bright (2024), and Trump v. United States (2024) — the four most structurally significant SCOTUS decisions in Domain H's Zone 1 analysis. The expert's choice among these (or identification of a case the research has not focused on) is a direct test of the Zone 1.2 analysis.

Why this framing works: The question asks for a *threat assessment* rather than a policy critique, which invites analytical rather than ideological answers. "Most dangerous precedential impact" is more specific than "most consequential" — it forces a structural rather than outcome-based judgment.

The "reversible" sub-question is critical: it distinguishes between experts who believe the damage can be corrected through statutory means (Zone 3.3 statutory reclamation) and those who believe constitutional amendment is required (Zone 3.2). This directly informs Zone 3's sequencing argument.

Synthesis extraction: Record the specific ruling named and the reversal assessment. Cross-tabulate across interviews to identify whether there is consensus on the most dangerous ruling or significant disagreement. The distribution of answers directly maps to Zone 3's priority amendment sequence.

Follow-up probe (if the expert names a case outside the Zone 1.2 four): "Do you think Trump v. United States (the 2024 presidential immunity ruling) or Loper Bright (the Chevron overruling) poses a greater structural threat than [the case they named]? Why?"

### Question 4: Current-Awareness Check
**"What case or legal development are you watching right now that you think could reshape executive accountability or judicial independence in the next 12-24 months? What would you need to see — which direction could the ruling go — for it to become the most important constitutional development in recent memory?"**

Zone mapping: Zone 1 (pending developments), Zone 3 (amendment strategy timing). This question performs the current-intelligence function that secondary research cannot: experts who are actively litigating cases or writing amicus briefs know what's in the pipeline before it's publicly visible. Their answer may surface monitoring items that the Phase 3 preliminary research (PHASE_3_DOMAIN_H_PRELIMINARY_RESEARCH.md Section 4) has not yet identified.

Why this framing works: "What would you need to see for it to become the most important constitutional development" is an unusually precise frame that forces the expert to think about the ruling's range of outcomes rather than just predicting one outcome. This produces more analytically useful information than "what do you think will happen."

For constitutional scholars who may not follow specific pending cases: substitute "What legislative development or political development in 2027 could most change the constitutional landscape for the reform agenda?"

Synthesis extraction: Record the specific cases or developments named. Add any new monitoring items to DOMAIN_H_RESEARCH_WORKFLOW_DESIGN.md's pending developments section. Cross-tabulate: if multiple experts name the same pending case or legislative development, it should be added to the Phase 3 critical path monitoring.

Note on Trump v. Slaughter: If the ruling has not yet been issued when interviews begin (expected June-July 2026), directly ask about the expert's read on Trump v. Slaughter. If it has been issued, ask for the expert's assessment of its impact: "The Slaughter ruling came down [X way]. Does that change your threat assessment for Zone 1.2's judicial-executive capture mechanism?"

### Question 5: Stress-Testing the Comparative Framework
**"Domain H draws heavily on the Germany December 2024 constitutional amendment as a model of pre-crisis constitutional hardening — Germany moved to protect the Federal Constitutional Court's structure in Basic Law specifically because the AfD had not yet reached governing power. How robust is the US analogy? What's most likely to be wrong or misleading about applying the German model to the US context?"**

Zone mapping: Zone 2.1 (Germany) — the most important single comparative case in Domain H. This is explicitly a stress-test question: the researcher presents the existing analysis and asks the expert to break it.

Why this framing works: The question reveals the limits of the German analogy that the research may have underweighted. Experts who know German constitutional law will immediately identify the features of the German system (parliamentary government enabling faster supermajority formation; BVerfG's 75-year institutional track record; the EU backstop) that the US lacks and that make the December 2024 amendment transferable only with significant modification. These limits are as important to document as the analogical strengths.

Synthesis extraction: Record the specific limitations named. For each limitation, assess whether it undermines the Zone 2.1 analysis or merely qualifies it. If multiple experts independently identify the same limitation (likely: "Germany has parliamentary government, the US doesn't"), add a dedicated paragraph to Zone 2.1 acknowledging the constraint.

Variant for specialists in other Zone 2 cases:
- South Africa specialists (Heinz Klug, Contact 27): "What's most likely to be wrong about applying the South African Constitutional Court's 30-year resilience record as a positive precedent for US courts?" 
- Israel specialists (Roznai, Contact 23): "The Domain H Israel analysis uses the January 2024 Supreme Court ruling as evidence that courts can assert independence against legislative attack. What does the post-ruling continuation of Netanyahu's judicial restructuring efforts tell us about the limits of judicial self-assertion?"
- Canada specialists (Dodek, Contact 26): "What's most likely to be wrong about using Canada's mandatory retirement at 75 as a reform model? Has it produced the political independence it was designed to produce, or are there problems the US reform conversation isn't seeing?"

### Question 6: Correctness Challenge
**"What does the legal community — constitutional scholars, reform advocates, or litigators — most commonly get wrong about [a specific constitutional doctrine central to this expert's work]? Where is the conventional wisdom most at odds with what the evidence shows?"**

Zone mapping: This question is adaptive by expert specialty. For emergency powers experts, the doctrine is IEEPA's legislative intent. For judicial reform scholars, the doctrine is term limits constitutionality. For amendment theorists, the doctrine is the amendment success rate's political preconditions. For comparative constitutionalists, the doctrine is the transferability of European resilience mechanisms.

Why this framing works: "Where is the conventional wisdom most at odds with what the evidence shows" is the most productive intellectual framing for producing citable, distinctive analysis. The expert's answer to this question is often the most quotable material in the interview — it is the expert's genuine intellectual position rather than a summary of the field.

Specific framings by expert specialty (select the appropriate one based on the expert's work):
- To emergency powers specialists: "What does the constitutional reform community get wrong about IEEPA's limits? Is the statutory fix (Kaine-Beyer IEEPA reform) as protective as its proponents claim, or does it leave constitutional gaps the statutory approach can't close?"
- To judicial independence specialists: "What does the term limits coalition get wrong about the constitutional feasibility argument? Is the active/senior justice model as solid as Fix the Court argues, or are there standing and Article III problems that have been underweighted?"
- To amendment specialists: "What do constitutional reformers get wrong about the amendment feasibility analysis? Domain H scores SCOTUS term limits at 10/12 on the feasibility matrix — is that too optimistic or too pessimistic, and why?"
- To comparative constitutionalists: "What do US scholars get wrong when they look at European constitutional resilience mechanisms? What's lost in translation that the reform conversation doesn't see?"

Synthesis extraction: Record the specific conventional wisdom identified and the expert's critique. These are typically the most directly citable passages from the interview. Flag for potential use in Zone 1-4 sections where the conventional wisdom appears.

### Question 7: Resource Prioritization (Prioritization Signal)
**"If you had six months, a small research team, and modest funding to research one constitutional problem that you think is underdeveloped in the literature — something that would most change the reform conversation if it were better understood — what would you choose to research, and why?"**

Zone mapping: Cross-domain — this question surfaces research gaps that may not be in any of the four Zones and that could form the basis for Domain I or subsequent research sprints. It is explicitly future-oriented.

Why this framing works: The "hypothetical resource allocation" framing bypasses professional caution and produces genuine intellectual priority signals. An expert who says "I'd research the Guarantee Clause's justiciability doctrine" or "I'd research the state ratification math for a Right to Vote Amendment under different political alignment scenarios" is revealing where they think the most important underdeveloped work lies.

This question is particularly valuable for the Domain I/J/K sequencing beyond Phase 3. If multiple experts independently identify the same research gap, that is strong evidence that gap should be a Phase 4 research priority.

Synthesis extraction: Record the research question identified, the rationale, and whether it maps to an existing Domain H Zone or represents new territory. Cross-tabulate across interviews for repeated themes.

Follow-up probe: "Is there a specific paper or approach you'd use as the methodological model? Who's doing adjacent work that would inform the research?"

### Question 8: Snowball Sampling
**"Who else should we talk to? Who is doing the most important or underrecognized work on these questions that isn't on the obvious list of constitutional law names — particularly anyone who might be coming at this from a different perspective or discipline?"**

Zone mapping: Contact database expansion. Every interview should produce at least 1-2 new names not currently in the PHASE_3_DOMAIN_H_SOURCE_STRATEGY.md contact database.

Why this framing works: "Not on the obvious list" and "different perspective or discipline" are the key framings. Asking for anyone generates the familiar names already in the database. Asking specifically for underrecognized work and cross-disciplinary perspectives surfaces the second-tier specialists — often the most analytically valuable for specific niche questions — and the non-legal scholars (political scientists, historians, sociologists) whose work Domain H has not yet incorporated.

Synthesis extraction: Record every name provided. Cross-reference against the existing 50-contact database. New names go into a "Second Wave" contact list. If the same name appears in 3+ interviews, that contact should be promoted to the primary database and outreach initiated immediately.

## TIMING AND LOGISTICS

**Call length:** Target 30 minutes; prepare for 45 minutes. Have all 8 questions ready but expect to cover only 5-6 in 30 minutes. Questions 1, 3, and 5 are highest priority — if time runs short, skip 4 and 7.

**Format options:**
- Phone interview (preferred for most academic contacts — lower friction than video)
- Written Q&A (acceptable for high-demand experts like Tribe and Chemerinsky who may prefer to respond in writing; produces more citable text but fewer follow-up opportunities)
- Email thread (lowest friction, lowest analytical yield — use only if phone/video is not available)

**Recording:** Do NOT record without explicit consent. Take notes during the call; write synthesis within 2 hours after the call while memory is fresh. Use the synthesis template below.

**Disclosure:** Be direct about the research purpose at the start of each call: "I'm conducting expert interviews for a research brief on constitutional resilience — specifically the constitutional design gaps that enable democratic backsliding and the international precedents for institutional protection. The brief will be distributed to constitutional law scholars and congressional staff in November 2026 through January 2027. With your permission, I'd like to use your insights in the brief, with attribution."

Attribution policy: Always ask. Default to attributed quotes with review opportunity. For contacts who prefer anonymity, use "constitutional law scholar at [institution]" or "senior practitioner in federal judiciary reform" — maintaining the expertise signal without individual attribution.

## QUALITY CONTROLS

**Confirmation bias check:** After each interview, assess whether the synthesis disproportionately records claims that confirm existing Domain H analysis and underrecords challenges to it. The stress-test questions (3, 5, 6) are specifically designed to surface challenges. If the synthesis from 10 interviews shows 90% confirmation and 10% challenge, that is a yellow flag — either the analysis is genuinely solid, or confirmation bias is operating.

**Inter-expert consistency check:** After 10 interviews, compile a cross-tabulation of Q3 answers (threat assessment) and Q2 answers (design model). High variance = the field is genuinely unsettled on these questions, which should be reflected in Zone 3's confidence ratings. High consistency = expert consensus exists, which strengthens the Domain H claims being validated.

**Attribution verification:** Before using any citable quote, confirm with the expert (email follow-up within 48 hours of interview) that they are comfortable with the attribution and phrasing. Even "on the record" interviews benefit from quote confirmation for academic sources who will want to review specific language before publication.
