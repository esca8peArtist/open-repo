Dear NELP team / Rebecca Dixon,

I am writing to share research that provides a democratic participation frame for NELP's gig economy and worker classification advocacy, timed to the OBBBA implementation period.

"Economic Precarity and the Civic Participation Crisis" documents that gig economy structure eliminates the institutional intermediaries through which civic engagement is historically organized — and does so with documented precision. BLS data shows 59 million Americans in gig or contingent work arrangements. Gig workers who depend primarily on platform income face algorithmically assigned, variable schedules that eliminate advance planning and predictable time blocks. Bae and Haselswerdt (Perspectives on Politics, 2023) document that gig workers shift toward expressive participation (signing petitions, social media) and away from sustained electoral participation specifically because the scheduling unpredictability of gig work prevents the advance commitments that voting and organizational membership require.

This is NELP's terrain: the same worker classification and schedule predictability policies NELP advances on labor rights grounds are simultaneously the policies that address the most severe civic participation barrier for the gig economy workforce. Worker reclassification restores not only employee benefits but the predictable schedules that sustained electoral engagement requires. Portable benefits legislation reduces the financial precarity that depletes civic bandwidth. The democratic participation argument for NELP's core legislative agenda strengthens its reach to democracy funders and election protection organizations that do not currently engage with worker classification as within their mandate.

The OBBBA context is acute: OBBBA reduced SNAP access for 2.4 million households, the population most likely to be in contingent and gig work, while ITEP confirms the CTC increases deliver nothing to the poorest fifth of households. The convergence of reduced food assistance, no CTC improvement, and continued gig precarity is a compounding civic exclusion mechanism — and NELP is the organization documenting all three.
