## SECTION 1: Response Classification Criteria

### Strong Response — Definition

**Threshold**: 4 or more of the 5 Batch 1 contacts meet the engagement criteria within 48–72 hours of send, OR 2–3 contacts reply within 24 hours with substantive content (score 3+).

**Specific indicators that qualify as Strong**:

- Any reply that references a specific domain by number (not just "interesting work")
- Any reply that asks about case application, names a pending docket, or mentions a brief or testimony in progress
- Any reply asking how the framework could be reformatted for a specific use (clinic, coalition brief, public comment)
- A referral signal: the contact forwards to a named colleague or says "you should talk to [name/org]"
- Two or more contacts from the same organization reply independently (cascade signal)

**Score threshold**: At least one reply scores 4 or 5 on the 0–5 scale (Integration or Implementation signal). A cluster of three score-3 replies with no score-4/5 is Moderate, not Strong.

**By constituency**:
- Policy orgs (Weiser/Brennan Center, Bassin/Protect Democracy): reply within 5 days, score 3+, references a specific domain or policy window
- Immigration legal (Elias/Democracy Docket): reply within 48 hours, references a case, docket, or specific legal theory
- Law schools (Goodman/Just Security-NYU, Chenoweth/Harvard Kennedy): reply within 10 days is within sector norms; a reply within 48 hours from either law school contact is an anomaly indicating very high salience

---

### Weak Response — Definition

**Threshold**: 0–1 substantive replies by 18:00 UTC May 18 (end of Wave 1 day), with no Gist clicks visible in Bitly by 48 hours post-send; OR 2 or more declines.

**Specific indicators that qualify as Weak**:

- Zero replies from any contact by end of day 3 (May 20)
- All replies are score 1 only ("Thanks, I'll take a look") with no follow-up content or questions
- Two or more contacts explicitly decline or opt out
- Zero Bitly clicks on the Gist URLs by 72 hours post-send (suggests delivery failure or spam filtering, not genuine disinterest — diagnose before concluding)
- Generic "in queue" or auto-response language from all policy org contacts

**Diagnostic first step if Weak indicators appear**: Check Bitly and email delivery before concluding the framing failed. Zero click and zero reply by 48 hours is more likely a delivery problem than a content problem. Run the self-test (send to yourself from same account). If self-test lands in spam, the problem is technical; do not revise messaging until delivery is confirmed.

**Weak is not failure — it is a pivot signal**: A Weak Wave 1 result redirects bandwidth to Domains 38–40, which are faster-turn research items with external deadlines (D39 June 1, D38 August 2, D40 November 3) that are completely independent of Phase 1 adoption performance.

---

### Moderate Response — Definition

**Threshold**: Everything not classified as Strong or Weak. Specifically: 1–3 substantive replies with at least one scoring 3+, or strong Gist click signal without replies yet, or strong performance from one constituency and silence from others.

**Most likely real-world result**: Given the Wave 1 constituency mix (2 law schools with 5–10 day cycles, 2 policy orgs with 2–5 day cycles, 1 immigration legal contact with a 48-hour potential), a Moderate classification at 10:00 UTC May 18 simply reflects that it is too early for law school responses. The Phase 2 decision-making should primarily respond to the policy org and immigration legal signals in the first 48–72 hours.

**Moderate is the default classification at 10:00 UTC May 18** unless Strong or Weak criteria are already clearly met.

## SECTION 3: Why Wave 1 Domain Selection Predicts Phase 2 Response Strength

This section documents the strategic logic behind the Wave 1 domain-contact pairings — why the specific domains sent to each contact predict what Phase 2 response patterns mean.

### Why Goodman (Just Security/NYU Law) received Domains 28/29 framing

Domain 28 (War Powers/Venezuela) and Domain 29 (Prosecutorial Weaponization) are the two domains with the most precise intersection with Goodman's work. Just Security is the primary venue for national security law and executive power analysis among working practitioners. The war powers analysis (Operation Absolute Resolve, the "arrest operation" legal theory, the Senate 51-50 vote) and the DOJ weaponization analysis (22-case retaliatory pattern, SPLC indictment, Nashville vindictive prosecution) are precisely the terrain Goodman covers. A reply from Goodman signals the framework's constitutional and prosecutorial analysis passes the scrutiny of the practitioner most likely to find specific errors.

**Phase 2 implication**: A Goodman reply referencing Domains 28/29 specifically suggests the constitutional law analysis in Domain 57 (particularly Section 2's constitutional asymmetry argument on treaty withdrawal) will land with the same audience. Domain 57 Section 2 is the intellectually adjacent analysis to Domains 28/29's war powers argument — they all address the same underlying question of executive unilateralism in the constitutional design. Goodman engagement is the clearest predictor of Domain 57 law school constituency traction.

### Why Weiser (Brennan Center) received Domain 1/37 framing

Domain 1 (Voting Rights) and Domain 37 (Federal Executive Interference in 2026 Midterms) are the Brennan Center's home territory. The Callais cascade, the CISA funding cuts, the SAVE Act error rate, and the election denier appointments are all issues where the Brennan Center has primary documentation and ongoing litigation. Sending Weiser this framing is not an introduction — it is a peer-to-peer signal that the framework synthesizes the same body of evidence they work with.

**Phase 2 implication**: A reply from Weiser signals that the framework's methodology is compatible with Brennan Center standards of evidence and framing. That compatibility is the entry point for Domain 39 (Healthcare as Democratic Infrastructure, with its NVRA-Medicaid voter registration nexus) and Domain 59 (Economic Precarity, with its participation gap data). Weiser engagement predicts that the framework will find its natural home in the Brennan Center's orbit — which is the most direct pathway to congressional testimony and judicial filing use.

### Why Chenoweth (Harvard Kennedy School) received methodology framing

Chenoweth's research is on civil resistance effectiveness — the comparative analysis of when and why nonviolent civic resistance succeeds. The methodology email asked whether the framework's analytical structure holds under comparative scrutiny. This is a different ask than "please read and endorse"; it is an invitation to engage as a methodological peer, which Chenoweth is uniquely positioned to do.

**Phase 2 implication**: A Chenoweth reply signals that the framework's comparative analysis (Domains 5, 8, 10 — historical case studies; Domain 57's comparative section on Brexit, Turkey, and historical US withdrawals) meets academic standards. That validation is decisive for the think tank constituency (Brookings, Roosevelt, CBPP) which responds to peer credibility signals more than to practitioner endorsement. A Chenoweth reply also opens the Harvard Kennedy School door for Domain 59 distribution — the Harvard IOP Spring 2026 Youth Poll data is cited directly in Domain 59 Section 1, and a Chenoweth connection would provide a natural bridge to the IOP.

### Why Bassin (Protect Democracy) received implementation framing

Protect Democracy is an operational organization, not a research organization. Its staff are lawyers, investigators, and communications professionals who use analytical frameworks to build cases and advocacy campaigns. The implementation framing asked whether the framework is usable as an operational resource.

**Phase 2 implication**: A Bassin reply focused on implementation signals that the framework meets the usability threshold for the advocacy-organizational constituency — the organizations that will distribute it to practitioners without needing a law school or think tank to validate it first. Protect Democracy's network includes state election protection organizations, civil liberties coalitions, and congressional staff — the exact Tier 2 constituency for Domain 37b (State Election Security) and Domain 40 (Surveillance Capitalism and Electoral Manipulation). Bassin engagement is the most direct predictor of Wave 2 adoption speed.

### Why Elias (Democracy Docket) received litigation framing

Marc Elias's organization is in active litigation on election law cases, several of which intersect directly with the framework's domain analyses. The litigation email specifically referenced Callais v. Landry (decided April 29, 2026) and Watson v. RNC — cases where the framework's Domain 37 analysis connects to pending legal strategy. This framing asks for operational feedback from a practitioner who has immediate use for the analysis.

**Phase 2 implication**: A reply from Elias, if it references a specific case or legal theory rather than generically acknowledging the research, is the highest-value single engagement signal of all five Batch 1 contacts. It means the framework is being tested against the hardest standard — litigation utility — and passed. That opens the Tier 2 immigration legal aid and election protection constituencies immediately. It also validates Domain 37b's election security and certification analysis as litigation-ready, which is the document Domain 57's Section 2 (constitutional asymmetry on treaty withdrawal) most needs to borrow credibility from.
