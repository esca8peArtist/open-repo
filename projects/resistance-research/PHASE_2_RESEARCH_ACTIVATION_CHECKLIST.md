### Peer Review Cycles

Both Domain 57 and Domain 59 require one full external peer review cycle. The standard protocol:

1. **Week 5 of production (draft complete)**: Send complete draft to peer reviewer with a structured review brief identifying the three categories of feedback needed: (a) empirical overclaiming — are causal claims stated at appropriate confidence levels? (b) cross-reference accuracy — do citations from other domains hold up? (c) domain-specific expertise — are the constitutional law (Domain 57) or quantitative social science (Domain 59) claims accurate?
2. **Reviewer has 5–7 business days**: Provide 2–4 hour async review; return structured comments
3. **Researcher incorporates**: 2–3 days for P0 (blocking errors) and P1 (significant improvements) feedback
4. **Final citation verification**: Browser-test all citations; confirm no URL rot since research phase

Domain 56 and Domain 58 have already completed or will complete peer review as part of their execution checklists. No additional peer review cycle needed for either.

### Revision Loop Architecture

Three revision categories for all domains:

1. **Empirical overclaiming**: Causal claims stated stronger than underlying evidence. Catch during peer review. Fix by hedging claim language to match source confidence levels. (Most common risk in Domain 59 Section 5, where multiplicative compounding argument requires precise quantification.)
2. **Cross-reference inaccuracy**: A domain cited for a claim that domain does not make. Catch during researcher self-check before peer review. Fix by reading cited domain's actual text and adjusting claim.
3. **URL rot**: Citations that were live during research but have died before publication. Catch during final citation verification pass (browser-tested, not memory-confirmed). Fix by finding archived version or identifying substitute source.

No domain enters distribution until all three categories are cleared.
