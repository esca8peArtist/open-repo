# Post-Phase-1 Community Adaptation & Feedback Integration Deep Dive

**May 6, 2026 — Session 817**

**Scope**: Five-section deep dive covering (1) sectoral modification risk assessment, (2) three-tiered feedback collection system, (3) versioning and attribution framework, (4) adaptation-to-Phase-2 pipeline design, and (5) per-sector adoption velocity monitoring. Each section grounds its recommendations in real institutional precedents, identifies which framework domains are load-bearing versus malleable per sector, and delivers procedures ready for orchestrator execution post-Phase-1-launch.

## Section 1: Sectoral Modification Risk Assessment by Domain

### The Baseline Analogy: Model Penal Code Domain Fragmentation (1962-1985)

The American Law Institute published the Model Penal Code in 1962 after eight years of drafting. Over the following 23 years, 36 states adopted revised criminal codes — but not a single state adopted the MPC wholesale. The mental state hierarchy (purpose/knowledge/recklessness/negligence) survived essentially intact across all adopting jurisdictions because it was load-bearing — strip it and the entire sentencing architecture collapses. The obscenity and drug offense provisions were rejected or radically modified in most states because they were malleable: they served a specific policy position but did not support other structural elements. The ALI did not anticipate this bifurcation in advance. It learned it retrospectively by tracking which MPC provisions appeared in enacted state codes and which did not.

This document does the ALI's retrospective work in advance, by domain and sector.

A second precedent is the ABA Model Rules of Professional Conduct (1983). When states adopted the Model Rules, the ABA's Center for Professional Responsibility tracked not just which states adopted but which provisions each state modified. By the mid-1990s this produced a "Jurisdictional Rules Comparison Chart" — a table of every Model Rule against every state's version — which became the primary tool for identifying which provisions were load-bearing across all jurisdictions (Rule 1.6 confidentiality) versus which were contested variations (Rule 7.2 advertising). The chart is updated annually and is publicly available. This document proposes analogous comparative tracking infrastructure for the 35-domain framework.

### Domain Classification Framework

**Load-bearing domain**: A domain section whose evidentiary logic, doctrinal structure, or cross-domain connection is required for the framework's analytical claims to hold. Stripping or substantially modifying a load-bearing element weakens not just that domain but connected domains. These are the sections that cannot be simplified without distorting the argument.

**Malleable domain**: A domain section that can be adapted for sector-specific framing, simplified for non-specialist audiences, supplemented with locally relevant data, or reordered without affecting the framework's core analytical claims. Adaptation here is expected and beneficial.

### State Attorneys General: Domain Risk Assessment

AGs adapt frameworks by converting them into litigation-grade argument structures. The extraction mechanism is selective: AGs mine for constitutional argument constructions that can survive the preliminary injunction standard in federal court and strip everything that cannot be cited as persuasive authority.

**Load-bearing for AGs (do not modify)**:
- **Domain 33 (Ballot Initiative Suppression)** — The six-state simultaneous action map is the operational core. Extractions that take the legal argument without the strategic sequencing recommendation miss most of the domain's value. Risk level: HIGH if extracted without sequencing context.
- **Domain 37 (Election Interference Diagnostic)** — The CISA funding cut figures and election denier appointment counts are load-bearing because they constitute the evidentiary predicate for the constitutional claims. Stripping the methodological sourcing notes creates factual exposure. Risk level: HIGH if statistics are extracted without sourcing context.
- **Domain 29 (Prosecutorial Weaponization)** — The "contested legal status" qualifier in the Weissmann assessment section is structurally necessary. Any adaptation presenting contested propositions as settled law creates credibility exposure in litigation. Risk level: HIGH if qualifier is removed.

**Malleable for AGs (expected adaptation)**:
- **Domain 1 (Voting Rights)** — AGs will supplement with state-specific constitutional text and strip international comparisons that are non-persuasive in U.S. federal circuits. Modification is expected and appropriate. Risk level: LOW.
- **Domain 6 (Judicial Independence)** — Circuit-specific filing strategy sections will be adapted to each AG's circuit. Base analysis survives. Risk level: LOW.
- **Domain 16 (Immigration Enforcement Authority)** — AGs will add state-specific anti-commandeering precedents. Risk level: LOW.

**Primary modification driver**: Legal persuasiveness in federal court — AGs will remove anything that creates framing vulnerability (foreign law citations, advocacy-adjacent language) and add anything that strengthens the local doctrinal case (state constitutional text, circuit-specific precedents).

**Phase 2 feedback trigger**: If 3+ AGs modify Domain 29 in the same way (removing the contested qualifier), the trigger is not to update the domain but to issue a supplementary brief explaining why the qualifier is analytically necessary.

### Law Schools: Domain Risk Assessment

Law schools adopt frameworks through three tracks: clinic adoption (6-12 weeks), faculty adoption (4-16 weeks), and law review publication (12-24 months). The Harvard Law School Democracy and Rule of Law Clinic, NYU Law's Brennan Center Clinic, and comparable programs are the primary early-adopter institutions. Their modification patterns are the most analytically constructive of any sector.

**Load-bearing for law schools (do not modify)**:
- **Domain 6 (Judicial Independence)** — The separation-of-powers doctrinal structure is load-bearing for any constitutional law seminar using this domain. Faculty adaptation typically adds doctrinal precision but preserves the analytical structure. If faculty strip the cross-domain connection to Domain 29 (prosecutorial capture), the doctrinal analysis loses its most important contemporary application. Risk level: MEDIUM.
- **Domain 2 (Executive Power)** — The historical precedent sections (Youngstown, Morrison v. Olson, Seila Law) are load-bearing anchors. Adaptations that forward-cite without the historical grounding lose the doctrinal depth that makes the domain classroom-viable. Risk level: MEDIUM.

**Malleable for law schools (expected adaptation)**:
- **Domain 27 (Academic Freedom)** — Primarily a descriptive domain at this stage. University and law school adaptations are expected to add their specific institutional context (each school's own litigation exposure, specific federal funding threat). Adaptation risk is low. Risk level: LOW.
- **Domain 22 (Racial Justice)** — Civil rights law programs will reframe through an intersectional equity lens. This reframing is analytically complementary, not dilutive. Risk level: LOW.
- **Domain 9 (Interstate Compacts)** — The compact model language is explicitly designed for state-specific adaptation. Risk level: LOW.

**Primary modification driver**: Academic register and pedagogical clarity. Faculty adaptation strips advocacy-adjacent language, adds doctrinal precision, and reformats for seminar discussion. This produces a more analytically rigorous version that serves the framework's purpose better in academic contexts.

**Attribution drift risk**: The most significant law school modification risk is not distortion but attribution drift — a law review article that adopts the framework's analytical structure in footnotes while attributing the conclusions to other sources, effectively incorporating the framework without crediting it. This is standard academic practice and reflects deep adoption, not misuse. It should be tracked (via Google Scholar structural convergence analysis) but not corrected.

**Phase 2 feedback trigger**: Two or more law school clinics assigning Domain 33 or Domain 37 as case material triggers Phase 2 research to produce an academic companion piece with full doctrinal apparatus.

### Federal Agencies and Think Tanks: Domain Risk Assessment

Think tanks decompose frameworks into sector-specific publications in 2-6 weeks. The Brennan Center, EPI, Center on Budget and Policy Priorities, and Protect Democracy are the primary institutional adopters. The ALEC model legislation precedent is directly applicable: between 2010 and 2018, ALEC model legislation was introduced in state legislatures approximately 2,900 times, with over 600 bills enacted — but no legislature adopted ALEC's agenda wholesale. Individual model bills were extracted, adapted for local context, and introduced independently. The 35-domain framework should expect identical dynamics.

**Load-bearing for think tanks (do not modify)**:
- **Domain 37 (Election Interference)** — Attribution dilution risk is highest here. When think tanks extract Domain 37's election denier appointment data or CISA funding cut figures and publish them without the analytical context that explains their democratic-accountability significance, the numbers become partisan assertions rather than evidence. Risk level: HIGH if cross-domain analytical context is stripped.
- **Domain 35 (Democratic Backsliding Comparative Analysis)** — The cross-national comparison is the domain's distinctive contribution. Think tanks with domestic U.S. policy focus will strip the international cases that make the analysis non-obvious. If they do, the domain loses its primary analytical claim (that the U.S. trajectory matches documented backsliding patterns). Risk level: HIGH if international cases are removed.

**Malleable for think tanks (expected adaptation)**:
- **Domain 4 (Economic Policy)** — EPI and CBPP will add labor market and fiscal policy data from their own research databases. These additions strengthen rather than dilute the domain. Risk level: LOW.
- **Domain 17 (Labor Rights)** — Labor research departments will substitute their own sector-specific enforcement data. Risk level: LOW.
- **Domain 20 (Environmental Federalism)** — Environmental organizations will add agency-specific data and current rulemaking status. Risk level: LOW.

**Primary modification driver**: Donor agenda and publication pipeline constraints. Think tanks publish in their house style, on their own timeline, and within their existing funder-determined research agenda. The framework's cross-domain synthesis will rarely survive decomposition into house-style publications — individual domains will appear as discrete briefs.

**Phase 2 feedback trigger**: If a major think tank (Brennan Center, Protect Democracy) publishes a report that structurally reorganizes its research agenda around the framework's domain map, this is a strategic adoption signal that triggers Phase 2 domain deepening in whichever domains they prioritize.

### Civil Rights Organizations: Domain Risk Assessment

Civil rights organizations adopt frameworks horizontally — extracting across multiple domains for a specific current crisis rather than adopting any single domain comprehensively. Common Cause, Protect Democracy, NAACP LDF, ACLU, and the Leadership Conference on Civil Rights are the primary sector institutions. The Leadership Conference coalition model (nearly 200 member organizations coordinated on shared frameworks since 1950) provides the most direct historical precedent for understanding how civil rights organizations share analytical frameworks: each member organization applies the shared framework to its own program area, producing sector-specific variations that are analytically complementary rather than fragmented.

**Load-bearing for civil rights organizations (do not modify)**:
- **Domain 1 (Voting Rights)** — The highest-risk domain for dilutive modification in this sector. Organizations focused on racial equity will appropriately reframe procedural disenfranchisement evidence through a racial justice lens. This reframing is constructive. The risk is organizations that extract only the racial equity sections without the Domain 6 judicial independence analysis — which is the mechanism through which voting rights claims are currently defensible in federal court. Organizations doing this produce campaigns that are morally resonant but legally fragile. Risk level: HIGH if Domain 6 judicial connection is severed.
- **Domain 29 (Prosecutorial Weaponization)** — Organizations in the mutual defense pact network (SPLC and 100+ partners) activating the mutual defense template need the contested-legal-status qualifier preserved for the same reason AGs do. Risk level: HIGH if qualifier is removed.

**Malleable for civil rights organizations (expected adaptation)**:
- **Domain 22 (Racial Justice and Equity)** — This domain is designed for adaptation by organizations with racial justice as their primary frame. Any adaptation adding sector-specific data or intersectional analysis strengthens the domain. Risk level: LOW.
- **Domain 7 (Rights Protection)** — Organizations will adapt to their specific rights portfolio (LGBTQ+ organizations, disability rights organizations, religious freedom organizations). This sector-specific framing is appropriate. Risk level: LOW.

**Primary modification driver**: Campaign resonance with member base and current crisis alignment. Civil rights organizations will prioritize domains aligned with their active campaigns and adapt the framework's analytical language to match their organizational voice.

**Phase 2 feedback trigger**: If civil rights organizations consistently modify Domain 5 (congressional reform) to emphasize voting rights implications (rather than structural reform), this signals that the domain's current emphasis underserves their audience and triggers a targeted domain supplement.

## Section 2: Feedback Collection System Design

### The Operational Constraint

The feedback system must run on under 4 hours per week of operator time. This is non-negotiable — a feedback system that demands 10+ hours weekly will be abandoned within 60 days. The design priority is automation, with manual intervention reserved for signals that automated systems cannot interpret.

### Best Practice Grounding

Three institutional precedents inform the system design:

**Uniform Law Commission adoption tracking** (established 1892, current): For every uniform act, ULC maintains a state-by-state adoption map updated monthly, a legislative activity tracker (adoptions by act and by state), and a Westlaw-integrated variation analysis (documenting state-specific modifications). The ULC system succeeds because it tracks the artifact (enacted legislation) rather than self-reported behavior, is maintained by a dedicated staff researcher (approximately 4 hours/week for the tracking function alone), and is publicly available, which incentivizes state legislative staff to report adoptions proactively.

**ABA Center for Professional Responsibility Jurisdictional Comparison Charts**: The ABA tracks every state's version of every Model Rule against the national model, updated annually through a network of state bar reporters who file modification notices. The critical design insight: the tracking burden is distributed to the institutional adopters themselves (state bars file the notices) rather than centralized in a single researcher. The ABA provides the template (comparison chart format) and the adopters fill it in.

**Brennan Center AVR State Tracker**: The Brennan Center tracks automatic voter registration adoption, implementation dates, and registration impact by state in a publicly visible scorecard updated approximately quarterly. The scorecard serves three functions simultaneously: internal monitoring tool, public accountability mechanism, and external advocacy tool. State legislators who want credit for adoption have an incentive to report it to the Brennan Center. This creates self-reporting incentives that reduce the monitoring burden.

### Tier 1 — Automated Monitoring (under 1 hour/week)

**What it captures**: High-volume, low-depth signals. Citation events, vocabulary migration, secondary distribution spikes, legislative text matches.

**Implementation**:
- Google Alerts (15-minute setup, daily email digest): Configure 12-15 coined terms distinctive to the framework — terms specific enough that their appearance in any public document almost certainly reflects framework adoption. Target terms: "wave filter legislative sequence," "appellate capture timeline," "Domain 33 six-state simultaneous action," "election denier appointment diagnostic," "state elections clause suppression pattern." Check digest takes 5-10 minutes daily.
- GitHub Gist view analytics: Weekly 5-minute check of view counts, view velocity, and referral sources. Spikes indicate secondary distribution events worth investigating.
- PACER full-text alert (Democracy Docket-integrated): Set up court filing alerts for Domain 37 and Domain 33 terminology in new federal court filings. Democracy Docket's existing monitoring infrastructure can be leveraged — they already track most relevant federal election cases. Weekly 10-minute review of new filings.
- LegiScan bill text monitoring: Configure for Domain 1 RCV provisions, Domain 9 interstate compact language, and Domain 29 whistleblower protection language. Monthly 15-minute check.

**Operator time**: Approximately 45 minutes per week.

**What it cannot capture**: Whether organizations are using the framework internally (staff training, memos) without producing public outputs. Oral adoption in meetings, phone briefings, conference presentations. The quality of the adaptation. These require Tier 2.

### Tier 2 — Semi-Automated Structured Collection (under 2 hours/week)

**What it captures**: Medium-depth signals from direct engagement. Domain-specific pull data, modification patterns, gap identification.

**Implementation**:
- Rotating check-in contact list (20% sample per month): Maintain a contact list of ~75 Phase 1 institutional contacts. Each month, rotate through 15 contacts for a 4-question email survey — not a formal survey, an email from the orchestrator asking four specific questions. Questions: (1) Which domain sections have you engaged with most? (2) Have you modified or adapted any sections for your own use? If so, which and how? (3) What is missing that would make the framework more useful for your specific work? (4) Would you be willing to share any documents you've produced using the framework? Total: 15 emails per month, 5-10 minutes each, 1.5-2.5 hours per month. Responses trigger artifact analysis (see below).
- Monthly synthesis email to Phase 1 contacts: One monthly email to all contacts with: (a) a one-paragraph summary of the most significant development in any domain since last contact, (b) one question ("Which domains are you seeing most used in your network right now?"), and (c) a link to any domain updates. Time investment: 30-45 minutes to draft and send.
- Artifact registration: When any Phase 1 contact produces a publicly available document that references or uses the framework, log it in the Artifacts Register (format in `adoption-feedback-template.md`). Monthly 20-minute review of all artifacts received in the prior month.

**Operator time**: Approximately 2.5-3 hours per week averaged over a month.

**Negative signal detection**: The following patterns in Tier 2 data are red flags requiring Tier 3 investigation:
- Three or more contacts in the same sector report that the same domain is "not useful" for their work — diagnose: wrong audience or genuine content gap?
- A domain's vocabulary is appearing in politically partisan contexts (Fox News, partisan political advertising, campaign materials) without the analytical framework — indicates misappropriation.
- An organization publicly attributes a claim to the framework that the framework does not make — requires correction outreach.
- Framework language appearing in litigation against democratic institutions or in support of voter suppression — fundamental contradiction requiring public clarification statement.

### Tier 3 — Manual Deep Investigation (under 1 hour/week average)

**What it captures**: High-depth, low-frequency signals. Root cause analysis of adoption failures, case study narratives, attribution analysis.

**Implementation**:
- Semi-structured interviews: At Month 6 and Month 12, conduct 20-minute structured interviews with 5-10 highest-engagement contacts. Use the institutional interview script in `adoption-feedback-template.md`. These are not status checks — they are diagnostic conversations about why adoption patterns developed as they did. Time: approximately 8-10 hours per interview set (scheduling, conducting, note-taking, synthesis). This is 4-5 hours per month averaged over the year, but concentrated at the 6-month and 12-month marks.
- Case study narratives: When an institution produces a high-quality adoption artifact — an AG brief citing Domain 33 analysis in a winning preliminary injunction motion, a law review article building on Domain 6's judicial independence framework — conduct a 3-5 page case study analyzing what the institution did with the framework, why it worked, and what other institutions should replicate. Time: 6-8 hours per case study; target 2-3 per year.
- Attribution analysis (Month 12): Code a 30-item sample of public outputs from Phase 1 recipients using the three-test attribution methodology from `feedback-integration-framework.md` (vocabulary marker, structural convergence, timing-and-contact). Two independent coders; inter-rater reliability check. Time: approximately 12-15 hours for the full attribution analysis; a single concentrated investment.

**Operator time**: Under 1 hour per week when averaged over the year, with concentrated investments at Month 6 and Month 12.

**Total system operator time**: Tier 1 (45 min/week) + Tier 2 (2.5-3 hours/week) + Tier 3 (under 1 hour/week average) = approximately 3.75-4.75 hours per week. This is at the ceiling of the 4-hour constraint. To remain at or under 4 hours, reduce Tier 2 rotating check-ins from 15 to 12 contacts per month (saving approximately 30-45 minutes).

## Section 3: Versioning and Attribution Framework

### The Core Tension

The framework must simultaneously allow institutions to innovate (adaptation is how frameworks achieve institutional embedding) and maintain coherence (if every institution produces an incompatible variant, the framework has fragmented into noise). The ABA's 2002 Ethics 2000 Commission update illustrates the failure mode: when the ABA updated the Model Rules, states that had already adopted earlier versions were left holding a different document than states that adopted the updated version. For several years, "the Model Rules" referred to materially different documents in different jurisdictions, complicating inter-jurisdictional practice.

The solution is the open-source governance model: a clear distinction between the upstream canonical version (which the project maintainer controls) and downstream forks/variants (which institutional adopters control), with documented relationships between them.

### Version Naming Protocol

**Canonical framework versions** (controlled by orchestrator):
- Version 1.0: Initial Phase 1 distribution (current)
- Version 1.x: Minor updates — evidentiary additions, factual corrections, new case law supplementation. Does not change the analytical structure or policy conclusions.
- Version 2.0: Major revision — new domains added, analytical framework substantially updated, significant policy conclusions changed. Requires communicating the change to all Phase 1 contacts.

**Institutional variant naming** (for use when institutions publish derivative works):
- Format: "[Institution/Sector] [Domain(s)] Variant [date]" — e.g., "NAAG Coalition Domain-33 Variant May 2026" or "HLS Democracy Clinic Domain-6 Adapted Fall 2026 Semester."
- The variant name communicates: (1) the originating institution or sector, (2) which domain(s) were adapted, (3) the date of the adaptation. This is sufficient for traceability without requiring complex version numbering that institutions will not maintain.

**When does institutional modification become a new "flavor" versus a minor variant?**

A minor variant adds locally relevant data, simplifies for audience, or reframes for sector context — but preserves the framework's analytical conclusions and evidentiary base. Example: An AG office supplements Domain 33 with their state's specific ballot initiative constitutional text. This is a variant.

A new "flavor" substantially modifies analytical conclusions, reverses policy positions, or removes load-bearing evidentiary elements. Example: A think tank reframes Domain 37's election denier appointment analysis to argue that executive appointments to election positions are constitutionally unremarkable. This changes the analytical conclusion. The producing institution should not call this an adaptation of the framework — it is a competing analysis.

The practical rule: if a reader of the institutional variant and a reader of the canonical framework would reach the same policy conclusions, it is a variant. If they would reach materially different conclusions, it is a new document.

### Attribution Requirements

**Minimum citation standard** (what the orchestrator should request of all institutional adopters):

> "This analysis draws on the Democratic Renewal Framework, [Domain X], Version [X.X], [date]. The original framework is available at [link]. Modifications to the original have been made to adapt it for [institution name]'s specific context."

This three-element citation standard (framework identification, domain and version, adaptation notice) is modeled on the Creative Commons attribution requirement (BY license): credit the original, identify the version, indicate if changes were made. Creative Commons has established this as the minimum viable attribution standard for policy frameworks that want to track derivatives without preventing adaptation.

**Attribution incentive design**: Attribution happens voluntarily when it serves the adopting institution's interests. Three incentive mechanisms:

1. Quality signal: Being associated with the canonical framework signals research quality to the institution's own audience. Law school clinics and think tanks both benefit from citing high-quality analytical sources. Make the citation easy (pre-formatted citation text at the top of each domain section).
2. Update notification: Institutions that cite the framework can opt into domain update notifications. This is a service the orchestrator provides in exchange for the attribution — institutions get automatic notification when the domains they have cited are updated.
3. Public scorecard: A publicly maintained "institutional adopters" list (like the Brennan Center's AVR state tracker) gives institutions public credit for adoption. This creates proactive reporting incentives — institutions want to be on the list.

**What not to require**: Approval before publication of derivative works. The framework is a research document, not a model code with official sanction. Requiring approval would slow adoption, create adversarial dynamics with institutions that feel their analytical independence is being constrained, and require administrative infrastructure the orchestrator does not have. Recommendation: attribution on request, correction outreach when material misrepresentation occurs, never approval gates.

### Fork Guidelines

When an institution produces a variant that differs substantially from the canonical framework (a new flavor), recommend (do not require) the following:

1. Name it distinctively — do not call it "the Democratic Renewal Framework" without qualification.
2. Identify what changed and why — a one-paragraph change log in the variant document.
3. Maintain access to the canonical version — link to the original so readers can access both.

This is the Debian derivatives model: Debian maintains a "Derivatives" wiki page documenting all known Debian-based distributions, their relationship to Debian, and the areas where they diverge. Ubuntu is the most prominent Debian derivative — it credits Debian extensively, maintains upstream compatibility where possible, and contributes patches back when appropriate. The relationship is cooperative rather than competitive because both projects have clear identities and clear areas of divergence.

## Section 4: Adaptation-to-Phase-2 Pipeline

### The Core Question

Should institutional modifications to Phase 1 domains feed back into Phase 2 domain research, or should Phase 2 proceed independently based on the pre-established research queue? The answer is: both mechanisms operate on different timelines and for different triggers.

**Demand-pull Phase 2** (driven by adaptation feedback): If law schools consistently modify Domain 5 (judicial reform) to emphasize environmental law implications — systematically connecting the appointment process analysis to EPA deference doctrine — this reveals a research demand that the original framework did not anticipate. The orchestrator should treat this as a Phase 2 trigger, initiating research on the environmental protection and democratic accountability nexus. The adaptation feedback is the evidence of demand; Phase 2 research provides the supply.

**Calendar-driven Phase 2** (driven by external deadlines): Domain 42 (DEA scheduling review), Domain 39 (November 2026 ballot measures), and any domains with hard legislative or litigation deadlines proceed to Phase 2 research regardless of Phase 1 feedback. Waiting for adaptation feedback in these cases would mean missing the advocacy window.

### Decision Gates

**Gate A (Month 2): Vocabulary signal gate**
- Trigger condition: A distinctive framework term appears in 5+ public documents from 3+ different institutional sectors within 60 days of distribution.
- Interpretation: The framework's analytical vocabulary is achieving cross-sector penetration. Phase 2 research should prioritize domains whose vocabulary is generating the most adoption.
- Gate failure (vocabulary not penetrating): The framework's terminology is too technical. Produce a plain-language companion document — do not modify the base framework.

**Gate B (Month 3): Demand-pull gate**
- Trigger condition: Two or more Tier 1 contacts from the same sector request the same type of domain deepening (e.g., three AG offices independently request statutory implementation language for Domain 34).
- Interpretation: A domain is generating operational demand that the current framework does not satisfy. Initiate Phase 2 research on that domain.
- Gate action: Add triggered domain to Phase 2 research queue above existing candidates, unless a hard-deadline domain is already queued. Document the triggering contacts and their specific requests.

**Gate C (Month 6): Sectoral adoption gate**
- Trigger condition: One or more sectors shows adoption rate below 20% of contacts producing verifiable output.
- Interpretation: The under-performing sector is not converting initial distribution into institutional use. Diagnose: content mismatch, wrong distribution channel, or too little time?
- Gate action (if content mismatch): Produce sector-specific companion brief addressing the specific mismatch (not a domain rewrite). For example, if AGs are not using Domain 37, the problem is likely insufficient PACER-ready citation format — produce a citation-formatted AG companion brief for Domain 37.
- Gate action (if wrong channel): Re-distribute through sector's primary information network (NAAG/NAGTRI for AGs, AALS Section on Clinical Legal Education for law schools, NCSL for think tanks with state legislature focus).

**Gate D (Month 6): Quality degradation gate**
- Trigger condition: Coded artifact sample (30 items) shows 30%+ of adaptations have removed load-bearing elements.
- Interpretation: Framework drift is occurring at scale. Clarification briefs are necessary.
- Gate action: Issue domain-specific clarification briefs — one-page documents that explain the load-bearing elements and why they should survive adaptation. Distribute to all Phase 1 contacts who received the affected domains.

### Adaptation-to-Phase-2 Decision Tree

**Adaptation type → Phase 2 research implication:**

| Adaptation pattern observed | Phase 2 implication | Action |
|-----------------------------|---------------------|--------|
| Multiple sectors adding similar new data to a domain (e.g., healthcare data in Domain 11) | Domain expansion needed | Phase 2: Domain 11b (Healthcare Access Collapse candidate) |
| Multiple organizations using a domain in a way the framework didn't anticipate (e.g., Domain 6 applied to state court capture) | New domain candidate identified | Phase 2: Add State Court Capture as Domain 9b candidate |
| A domain consistently modified to include international comparison the framework omitted | Evidence gap | Domain update (minor revision), not new Phase 2 research |
| Organizations applying a domain to a geographic or demographic context not covered | Geographic/sectoral companion needed | Companion brief, not Phase 2 domain |
| Consistent removal of a cross-domain connection | Load-bearing element unrecognized by adopters | Clarification brief + modify domain to make connection more explicit |
| An entire domain consistently underused across all sectors | Audience mismatch | Re-target distribution, do not modify domain |

**Case Study (illustrative)**: Law schools modify Domain 5 to emphasize environmental law implications. This pattern, if observed in 3+ law school adaptations within 6 months, triggers the following pipeline:
1. Identify the specific connection law schools are making (environmental deference doctrine + judicial appointment process).
2. Assess whether this connection is fully addressable within an existing domain (partial coverage in Domain 6 and Domain 20) or requires a new domain.
3. If partial coverage: issue a Domain 6/Domain 20 cross-reference note identifying the environmental deference angle.
4. If new domain: add "Environmental Protection and Democratic Accountability" as a Phase 2 candidate with higher priority than its current position (Candidate in exploration queue). The school-driven modification is the demand signal that justifies the priority elevation.

## Section 5: Institutional Velocity Variance and Monitoring Framework

### Empirical Velocity Baselines

Institutional adoption velocity is not uniform. The empirical record from analogous framework distributions provides sector-specific baselines:

**Think tanks** (2-4 weeks to first output): The Heritage Foundation's Mandate for Leadership provides the upper bound — it was delivered to the Reagan transition team 30 days before inauguration and 60% of its 2,000+ recommendations were adopted within two terms. This is an extreme case. A more realistic benchmark is the Brennan Center's publication pipeline: 4-6 weeks from framework receipt to published brief, assuming the framework aligns with existing research priorities. Target: 2+ think tank publications citing framework analysis within 45 days of distribution.

**State Attorneys General** (6-12 weeks to first verifiable output): The NAAG/NAGTRI coordination infrastructure (130+ training programs per year, multistate working groups) creates a well-established information diffusion network. AG adoption speed is constrained by litigation timelines — an AG will integrate a framework domain when they have an active case that the domain supports, not on a fixed calendar. Target: 2+ AG offices citing framework analysis in active litigation within 90 days of distribution.

**Law schools** (4-8 weeks for clinic adoption, 4-16 weeks for faculty, 12-24 months for law review): The binding constraint for law school clinic adoption is the semester calendar. May distribution reaches Fall 2026 curriculum decisions if it reaches clinic directors by mid-June — a hard six-week window. Faculty who receive the framework after the June curriculum decision point will not integrate it until Spring 2027 at the earliest. Target: 3+ law school clinics confirming framework integration by August 2026 (before Fall semester begins).

**Civil rights organizations** (8-12 weeks): Civil rights organizations operate on campaign cycles, not research cycles. Framework adoption occurs when an active campaign aligns with a domain's analytical content. The Leadership Conference on Civil Rights coalition (200 member organizations) provides the best adoption network — if the Lead Conference's own research team adopts the framework, it is effectively adopted by the entire coalition network. Target: 1+ major civil rights coalition (LCCR, NAACP LDF, ACLU) confirming integration into an active campaign by 90 days.

### Per-Sector Monitoring Protocol

**Think tanks — Weekly monitoring (15 minutes)**:
- Tier 1: Google Alerts daily digest for framework terms appearing in think tank publications.
- Tier 2: Month 1 check-in email to 5 highest-priority think tank contacts (Brennan Center, Protect Democracy, EPI, CBPP, Bipartisan Policy Center).
- Escalation trigger at 6 weeks: If no think tank output observed, re-distribute with sector-specific cover note targeting current legislative calendar alignment (e.g., OBBBA Medicaid cuts if distributing to CBPP).
- Escalation trigger at 12 weeks: If still no output, conduct 15-minute diagnostic call with highest-priority think tank contact. Diagnose: content mismatch or distribution failure?

**State AGs — Democracy Docket integration (30 minutes/week)**:
- Tier 1: PACER alert + Democracy Docket weekly digest for new filings in election and constitutional law cases citing framework terminology.
- Tier 2: Month 2 email to 10 AG policy counsel contacts with specific domain recommendations aligned to each AG's current litigation portfolio.
- Escalation trigger at 6 weeks (<20% integration): 20% threshold = at least 2 of 10 priority AG contacts showing verifiable framework engagement. Below threshold: contact the NAAG/NAGTRI coordinators directly to request inclusion of the framework in the next multistate working group briefing materials. NAGTRI's training infrastructure is the natural propagation channel for AG adoption.
- Escalation trigger at 12 weeks: If AG adoption below 40%, diagnose whether the distribution reached AG policy counsel (who handles framework-type research) or AG press offices (who do not). Wrong recipient is the most common cause of AG non-adoption.

**Law schools — Semester-calendar monitoring (monthly)**:
- Tier 1: SSRN and Google Scholar weekly alerts for any academic paper citing framework domains.
- Tier 2: June 15 email to clinic directors (hard deadline for Fall 2026 curriculum decisions) with targeted domain recommendations per clinic focus area.
- Escalation trigger at August 1 (<20% clinic confirmation): If fewer than 3 of 15 priority law school clinics have confirmed Fall integration, contact AALS Section on Clinical Legal Education for inclusion in the section's Fall newsletter and conference recommendations. This is the clinical education information network.
- Escalation trigger at December 1: If law review adoption signals (SSRN working papers, conference presentations) are absent, the framework has not penetrated academic research culture. Diagnosis: the framework may need a more academic-register companion document. Produce a 2,000-word annotated bibliography mapping the framework's empirical claims to peer-reviewed sources — this is the artifact academic researchers need to cite the framework in law review articles.

**Civil rights organizations — Campaign-calendar monitoring (monthly)**:
- Tier 1: Campaign launch monitoring — press releases, funding appeals, social media announcements from priority organizations (LCCR, NAACP LDF, ACLU, Protect Democracy).
- Tier 2: Rotating check-in calls with 20% sample of civil rights organization contacts each month.
- Escalation trigger at 8 weeks (<20% integration): Contact the Leadership Conference on Civil Rights policy staff directly and request a 30-minute briefing on the framework. If LCCR adopts, the coalition network follows. If LCCR does not respond, escalate to the 2-3 LCCR member organizations with the strongest alignment to current framework domains.
- Escalation trigger at 16 weeks: If civil rights sector adoption remains below 30%, the framework's language may not resonate with the sector's communication culture. Commission a 1-page "Framework in Plain Language" translation from an organization already using the framework. Community-facing language, not research language.

### Cross-Sector Adoption Stall Protocol

If two or more sectors simultaneously show adoption below velocity benchmarks at the 6-week mark, this is a cross-sector signal — not individual sector failure but a systemic issue with distribution or framing. At this point:

1. **Re-diagnose distribution**: Confirm that the framework reached the correct institutional contacts (policy counsel, clinic directors, research directors — not communications or development staff).
2. **Emergency re-distribution**: Issue a 2-page executive brief with the framework's five most immediately actionable findings, targeted to each sector's current crisis. This is not a modification of the framework — it is a crisis-specific entry point.
3. **Peer endorsement activation**: Identify the 1-2 contacts who showed the strongest early engagement across any sector. Ask them to make a direct introduction of the framework to their peer networks. Peer-to-peer recommendation is the strongest adoption driver in every Rogers diffusion study — it accelerates the Early Adopter to Early Majority transition by an average of 30-40% compared to direct outreach.

## Appendix: Key Institutional Precedents Summary

| Precedent | Relevance | Key Lesson |
|-----------|-----------|------------|
| Model Penal Code (ALI, 1962-1985) | Framework adaptation by state legislatures | Mental state hierarchy (load-bearing) survived; obscenity provisions (malleable) did not. Retroactive analysis could have predicted this. |
| ABA Model Rules (1983-present) | Jurisdictional comparison tracking | Annual comparison chart distributed tracking burden to state bar adopters; creates accountability without centralized surveillance. |
| Brennan Center AVR Tracker (2015-present) | State-by-state adoption scorecard | Public scorecard creates proactive self-reporting incentives; adoption accelerates after critical mass (Oregon/California) |
| ALEC Model Legislation (2010-2018) | Think tank framework decomposition | Individual bills extracted and introduced independently 2,900 times; holistic adoption never occurred. Extraction is the mechanism. |
| Leadership Conference on Civil Rights (1950-present) | NGO coalition framework coordination | 200 member organizations share framework while adapting to sector — complementary, not fragmented. |
| Linux Kernel / Debian Derivatives | Open-source fork governance | Canonical upstream (Linus/Debian) distinguished from downstream distributions (141 active Debian derivatives); fork attribution encouraged, not required. |
| Creative Commons BY License | Attribution standard for derivative works | Three-element attribution (credit original, identify version, indicate changes) is minimum viable standard without requiring approval. |
