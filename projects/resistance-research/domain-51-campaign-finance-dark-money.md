## Executive Summary

Citizens United v. Federal Election Commission (2010) is the most cited Supreme Court case in discussions of money in politics. What is less understood is that Citizens United was not a single decision creating a single problem — it was the removal of the last structural barrier preventing corporations and wealthy individuals from deploying unlimited funds through opaque nonprofit vehicles to directly shape the outcome of elections, legislative agendas, and regulatory proceedings. The framework documented across Domains 1–50 shows how different industries have captured different regulatory and legislative targets: cryptocurrency companies captured financial regulation (Domain 38), pharmaceutical companies captured drug scheduling (Domain 42), fossil fuel companies shape environmental enforcement (Domain 15), the anti-abortion movement funds ballot initiative suppression (Domains 33 and 44). What connects these captures is not ideology — it is architecture. The Citizens United decision, and the regulatory vacuum it created, is the structural mechanism that makes every sector-specific capture possible at this scale.

The 2024 election was the most financially opaque in American history. Dark money — election spending where donors are not publicly disclosed — hit a record $1.9 billion in federal races alone, a dramatic jump from the prior record of $1 billion in 2020. Shell companies and 501(c)(4) nonprofits that did not disclose their funding sources gave $1.3 billion to super PACs — more than in the prior two election cycles combined. Before Citizens United, billionaires accounted for 0.3% of federal election spending; by 2024, 300 billionaires and their families contributed more than $3 billion, representing 19% of all federal election donations. Total outside spending reached $4.5 billion in federal races, with more than half coming from groups that do not fully disclose their funding sources.

The 2026 crisis has three acute dimensions. First, the Federal Election Commission has lost its enforcement quorum for more than 200 consecutive days: Trump fired Democratic appointee Ellen Weintraub in February 2026, and with the prior resignation of the last Republican commissioner, the commission has been reduced to two of six seats — well below the four required for a quorum. The FEC has 195+ pending enforcement matters, some dating to 2022, that it cannot resolve. It cannot enforce existing campaign finance laws, audit spending, or impose penalties — a situation in the runup to what OpenSecrets projects will be the most expensive midterm election in history. Second, the crypto industry has already deployed $288 million toward 2026 midterm races — more than double its 2024 deployment of $130 million — with Fairshake alone now the fifth-most-funded PAC in the country at $193 million, demonstrating that the Citizens United architecture enables any sufficiently capitalized industry to directly purchase legislative outcomes. Third, the state-level reform window (California, Missouri, Montana, Hawaii) provides the most immediate actionable advocacy anchor, because state disclosure requirements and corporate charter modifications survive under Citizens United in ways that federal spending prohibitions do not.

The reform architecture is three-tiered: state disclosure requirements and the Hawaii corporate charter workaround (constitutional, actionable now), small-donor public matching programs (near-term), and constitutional amendment (long cycle, required for durability). The November 2026 California and Missouri ballot measures and the June 19 Montana signature deadline are the immediate organizing anchors.

## 1. The Constitutional Architecture: How Citizens United Created the Dark Money System

### 1.1 The Pre-Citizens United Framework

Before 2010, the campaign finance system had three structural elements that are critical to understanding what Citizens United destroyed.

The first element was the corporation-expenditure prohibition. Section 203 of the Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act of 2002 (BCRA, also known as McCain-Feingold) prohibited corporations and labor unions from using general treasury funds to pay for "electioneering communications" — broadcast ads referring to a federal candidate within 30 days of a primary or 60 days of a general election. This prohibition built on the Federal Corrupt Practices Act of 1907, which had originally banned direct corporate contributions to federal candidates. The combined framework meant that corporations could not spend unlimited sums from their general treasuries to influence federal elections.

The second element was the hard distinction between political committees (which disclosed donors) and general advocacy (which did not require disclosure). The FEC's "express advocacy" doctrine drew a line: communications that explicitly urged voters to vote for or against a candidate required disclosure; communications that discussed political issues without explicit electoral advocacy did not. This line was porous, but it maintained a basic transparency structure.

The third element was the public financing system for presidential elections, established by the Federal Election Campaign Act Amendments in 1974 after Watergate. In 2000, total outside spending from all sources was approximately $400 million and largely disclosed. By 2024, total outside spending from non-party groups reached $4.5 billion — more than a tenfold increase — with more than half from groups that do not fully disclose their funding sources.

### 1.2 Citizens United: The Three Holdings That Created the Dark Money System

Citizens United v. FEC (2010) is best understood as three rulings operating in sequence.

The narrowest holding: the government cannot prohibit corporations from spending unlimited amounts from their general treasuries on independent expenditures — political communications not coordinated with a candidate's campaign. Corporations have First Amendment rights in the political speech domain, and limits on their independent spending violate those rights.

The accompanying doctrinal shift: Citizens United abandoned the long-standing distinction between corporate and individual speech rights in the political domain. The Court held, 5-4, that the First Amendment's protections do not distinguish between speakers based on corporate status. Not just business corporations, but nonprofit advocacy organizations, trade associations, and shell companies formed for political purposes all received the same unlimited independent expenditure rights.

The simultaneous procedural consequences: SpeechNow.org v. FEC (D.C. Circuit, 2010), decided two months after Citizens United, held that contribution limits to independent-expenditure-only groups — what became known as super PACs — were also unconstitutional. The combination of Citizens United (unlimited corporate spending) and SpeechNow.org (unlimited contributions to super PACs) created the modern dark money architecture: corporations give unlimited sums to 501(c)(4) nonprofit organizations that do not disclose their donors; those 501(c)(4)s give to super PACs; and super PACs spend in elections disclosing only that their funds came from the opaque nonprofit. The shell within a shell within a shell collapses donor identity entirely.

The Brennan Center's analysis of the 15-year post-Citizens United era is definitive: dark money groups have poured over $2.6 billion into federal elections since 2010, and the 2024 cycle's $1.9 billion in dark money alone dwarfs the entire pre-2020 Citizens United era. OpenSecrets documents the trajectory: $300 million in outside spending in 2008 (the last pre-Citizens United cycle) → $1 billion in 2012 → $1.4 billion in 2016 → $1 billion in dark money alone in 2020 → $1.9 billion in dark money in 2024.

### 1.3 The 501(c)(4) Loophole and Shell Company Architecture

The 501(c)(4) provision of the tax code — which allows social welfare organizations to avoid paying taxes and, crucially, to avoid disclosing their donors — was not designed as a campaign finance vehicle. It was designed for civic associations, neighborhood improvement leagues, and volunteer fire departments. After Citizens United, it became the primary vehicle for unlimited anonymous political spending.

The operational architecture is straightforward. A wealthy donor or corporation wants to spend $50 million influencing a Senate race without disclosure. They donate to a 501(c)(4) "social welfare" nonprofit, which lists its "primary purpose" as issue advocacy rather than electoral activity (the IRS standard is that the 501(c)(4) must not have political activity as its "primary" purpose — a standard so vague it has been consistently exploited). The 501(c)(4) donates to a super PAC, which discloses the donation from "XYZ Social Welfare Fund" — a name that reveals nothing about the original donor. The super PAC spends on ads disclosing only the opaque shell as the funding source.

The Cedar Innovation Foundation — a 501(c)(4) organized in early 2023 to promote crypto industry interests without donor disclosure — is the 2026-cycle exemplar of this architecture. The Solana Policy Institute operates similarly. These vehicles allow Coinbase, a16z, Ripple, and other crypto companies to fund political spending that does not disclose the original corporate source, even as their corporate-funded super PAC, Fairshake, makes coordinated independent expenditures against specific legislators.

McCutcheon v. FEC (2014) further dismantled the post-Watergate structure by striking down aggregate contribution limits — the caps on how much a single donor could give in total across all federal candidates and committees. While Citizens United addressed corporate independent expenditures, McCutcheon eliminated the ceiling on wealthy individuals' total political giving, accelerating the billionaire concentration documented in the 2024 cycle data.

## 2. The 2026 FEC Collapse: Enforcement Vacuum at the Worst Possible Moment

### 2.1 The Commission's Structural Disablement

The Federal Election Commission was designed as a bipartisan institution specifically to prevent any party from weaponizing it: six commissioners, no more than three from any party, requiring four votes for most actions — which means any meaningful enforcement requires bipartisan agreement. This design produced chronic deadlock but at least prevented directional weaponization.

The Trump administration's 2025-2026 actions destroyed even that protection through structural rather than directional attack. First, the last remaining Republican commissioner, Trey Trainor, resigned effective October 3, 2025. Second, Trump fired FEC Chair Ellen Weintraub — a Democratic appointee — in February 2026, claiming authority under his interpretation of the Supreme Court's Seila Law and Collins rulings on independent agency removal. This firing was immediately challenged in federal court. The combination left the FEC with only two commissioners — Shana Broussard and Dara Lindenbaum — and four vacancies. The commission crossed the 200-day mark without a quorum on approximately June 1, 2026 — the longest enforcement shutdown in the agency's history.

The practical consequences are precise and severe. The FEC cannot: hold hearings; issue rules; vote on outcomes of investigations; conduct audits; issue advisory opinions; or impose fines. The case backlog stands at 195+ pending enforcement matters as of early 2026 — up from 190 in December 2025 — with some cases dating to 2022 and 2023 now at risk of exceeding the FEC's five-year statute of limitations. The Brennan Center described this as the FEC losing its enforcement powers "in the runup to the most consequential midterm election in a generation." NOTUS reporting confirms: if the shutdown continues long enough, the agency risks "jeopardizing these long-term investigations" permanently.

On February 11, 2026, Trump nominated Ashley Stow and Andrew Woodson — both Republicans — to fill two of the four vacancies. If confirmed, the commission would have a 3-2 Republican majority quorum capable of enforcement actions in Republican-favorable directions while continuing to deadlock on dark money disclosure requirements that Republican commissioners have historically opposed. As of June 1, 2026, the nominations have not been confirmed. Historical median confirmation time for FEC nominees is 91 days — meaning the earliest realistic quorum restoration is late May or June 2026, but Senate scheduling could extend this well into the fall election season.

The constitutional question — whether the president can fire FEC commissioners at will — remains unresolved in federal court. If the Supreme Court extends Seila Law's logic (which upheld presidential removal authority over single-director agencies) to multi-member independent commissions, the bipartisan design of the FEC will be definitively dismantled. This connects directly to Domain 56's analysis of the OPM final rule removing civil service protections: the same independent-agency removal doctrine being litigated over the FEC is being applied simultaneously across multiple regulatory agencies.

### 2.2 Enforcement Deadlock: A Structural History

The FEC's current total disablement is the extreme end of a structural history of deliberate enforcement failure. Since 2010, the commission's bipartisan design has been used to block enforcement rather than ensure it: Republican commissioners have systematically voted to deadlock enforcement actions against dark money organizations, using the four-vote requirement as a veto against disclosure enforcement.

The FEC's annual reports document the deadlock pattern. From 2010-2024, the commission deadlocked on high-profile enforcement actions at a rate that increased dramatically: in the 2008-2012 period, enforcement deadlocks occurred in approximately 15-20% of contested cases; by 2018-2022, that rate exceeded 50% in matters involving 501(c)(4) dark money organizations. NOTUS's investigative reporting documents how what began as occasional deadlock became systematic enforcement paralysis even before the 2026 quorum collapse.

The structural design failure this exposes: the FEC was designed to be bipartisan to prevent partisan weaponization, but it can also be weaponized through the opposite mechanism — using the four-vote requirement not to force bipartisan agreement but to guarantee that enforcement against allied dark money vehicles can be blocked by a two- or three-commissioner minority. The current quorum collapse takes this structural failure to its logical endpoint: not partisan selective enforcement but no enforcement at all, for the first time in the agency's history, entering the most expensive midterm cycle ever projected.

## 3. Sector-Specific Capture: How Dark Money Connects the Framework

### 3.1 The Crypto PAC Model: Fairshake and Legislative Purchase

The cryptocurrency industry's political spending trajectory is the most data-rich 2026 example of how the Citizens United architecture enables direct regulatory capture through electoral spending rather than through indirect lobbying.

In 2024, pro-crypto super PAC Fairshake spent $119 million in the election cycle — nearly one-third of all corporate political spending — targeting specific legislators who took critical positions on crypto regulation. The legislative payoff: the GENIUS Act (which provided a regulatory framework for stablecoins favorable to the crypto industry) and the CLARITY Act (providing a broader crypto regulatory structure). Domain 38 documents the financial regulatory consequences; Domain 51 documents the mechanism: industry-identified legislative obstacles → super PAC-funded primary challenges or general election opposition → legislative obstacle removed → industry-aligned replacement installed.

For 2026, Fairshake has already raised $193 million (as of January 2026), making it the fifth-most-funded PAC in the country. Coinbase, a16z, and Ripple alone have contributed $49 million in new 2026 contributions. Total cryptocurrency industry spending toward the 2026 midterms has reached $288 million — more than double the entire 2024 deployment. CNBC reports that "the pro-crypto super PACs are sitting on $221 million in unspent cash, waiting to deploy it against candidates who might otherwise be tempted to stand up to the crypto industry's deregulatory demands."

The dark money component: the Cedar Innovation Foundation (501(c)(4)) and the Solana Policy Institute operate as the dark money layer beneath Fairshake's disclosed super PAC spending. Donors to these vehicles are not disclosed. The operational picture is: crypto companies → Cedar Innovation Foundation (no disclosure) → Fairshake (discloses "Cedar Innovation Foundation" as source, concealing original corporate identity) → legislative advertising in specific targeted races.

The Intercept's May 2026 investigation extends this picture into AI industry PAC proliferation. OpenAI executives back "Leading the Future" super PAC. The Citizens United architecture is now being adopted across the technology sector as a template, not just by the crypto industry that pioneered it in 2022-2024.

## 4. Historical Precedent and Comparative Evidence

### 4.1 The Pre-1976 Era and the Watergate Reform Moment

Campaign finance reform in the United States has a cyclical history: a scandal reveals the extent of corrupting money in politics, reform legislation passes, courts whittle it down, a new scandal reveals new gaps. The Citizens United era breaks this cycle because no reform legislation has successfully closed the gaps opened by the 2010 decision.

The 1972 election — in which Nixon's Committee to Re-Elect the President raised $60 million (approximately $440 million in 2024 dollars) through largely unregulated channels — produced the 1974 Federal Election Campaign Act Amendments, which created the FEC, established contribution limits, created public financing for presidential campaigns, and required disclosure of all political contributions above $100. Buckley v. Valeo (1976) immediately struck down spending limits on individual campaigns and independent expenditures as First Amendment violations but upheld contribution limits and disclosure requirements as serving the anti-corruption interest.

The post-Buckley system — contribution limits, disclosure, and public financing for presidential races — was structurally stable because the Supreme Court upheld the government's anti-corruption interest as a compelling rationale for these constraints. Citizens United's doctrinal innovation was narrowing what counts as "corruption" to only explicit quid pro quos, eliminating the broader concept of undue influence that Congress had relied on to justify contribution limits on independent expenditure groups. Time magazine's April 2026 analysis documents the range of reform options available within the current constitutional framework, including state-level approaches that do not require overturning Citizens United directly.

### 4.2 International Comparative Evidence

The international comparison on campaign finance is among the sharpest in comparative democratic design. The United States stands nearly alone among wealthy democracies in permitting unlimited anonymous corporate spending in elections.

**Canada**: Canada banned corporate and union contributions to political parties in 2004. Only individuals who are Canadian citizens or permanent residents may make contributions to federal parties, capped at $1,750 per year per party. There are no super PAC equivalents; independent spending by corporations is prohibited. Canada's Elections Act requires full disclosure of all political advertising spend and prohibits third-party advertising from foreign sources.

**Germany**: Germany's political finance system combines party public funding with strict disclosure requirements. Donations from corporations are permitted but fully disclosed and subject to parliamentary review; there is no equivalent to the 501(c)(4) anonymous giving vehicle. The Federal Constitutional Court has repeatedly ruled that political finance transparency is a constitutional requirement — precisely inverting the American constitutional framework.

**France**: France prohibits corporate donations to political parties entirely and limits individual donations to €7,500 per party per year. The French Constitutional Council has upheld both spending limits and corporate donation bans as constitutionally required to protect democratic equality.

**UK**: The Political Parties, Elections and Referendums Act (PPERA) requires disclosure of all political donations above £500 and all political advertising expenditure. Foreign donations are prohibited. The UK focuses primarily on spending limits during campaign periods. The Information Commissioner's Office has fined companies for unlawful data use in political targeting.

The comparative lesson: every other wealthy democracy has found a constitutionally consistent way to limit the conversion of private wealth into political power. The contrast with the US is not a First Amendment compulsion — it is a judicial interpretation choice that other democracies with comparable free speech traditions have not made.

### 4.3 The Montana Model and Arizona Proof-of-Concept

Montana's Corrupt Practices Act (1912) banned corporate election spending after mining companies and copper trusts had purchased Montana's legislature wholesale. The Montana Supreme Court upheld the law in American Tradition Partnership, Inc. v. Bullock (2012); the US Supreme Court reversed summarily, applying Citizens United to the states.

The Montana precedent matters for reform strategy: it created the template for Arizona's Proposition 211 (2022), which required dark money organizations to disclose their donors at the state level. Arizona Prop. 211 — which passed by 72% — has since survived multiple legal challenges, with the Arizona Supreme Court upholding it in 2024 as a valid state disclosure requirement. The distinction is critical: Citizens United struck down spending limits; it did not address state disclosure requirements, which remain an open reform avenue. The Koch network's Americans for Prosperity subsequently sued to strike down Prop. 211 in Arizona — documenting that the dark money industry treats disclosure as an existential threat and will invest resources to defeat it.

## 5. 2026 Legislative and Advocacy Windows

### 5.1 The DISCLOSE Act of 2026 and the Filibuster Barrier

The DISCLOSE Act of 2026 (S.3991), sponsored by Senator Sheldon Whitehouse and Representative Chris Pappas, was introduced in March 2026 with all 47 Democratic senators and 139 House Democrats as co-sponsors — 182 total. The bill would require organizations spending money in federal elections — including super PACs and 501(c)(4) dark money groups — to promptly disclose donors who have given $10,000 or more during an election cycle. The 2026 update specifically:

- Captures payments to social media influencers to promote or oppose a candidate as disclosable political spending
- Narrows and specifies what constitutes threats and harassment to qualify for a disclosure exemption
- Allows more flexibility for disclaimers for short political ads
- Includes a measure to prevent foreign governments from interfering in US elections through state and local ballot measure campaigns

The bill cannot pass. It does not have and will not receive the 60 Senate votes needed to overcome filibuster. Senate Republicans filibustered the DISCLOSE Act in September 2022, January 2022, and July 2012 — every time Democrats have brought it to a floor vote, every Republican present has voted to block it.

Despite being blocked legislatively, the DISCLOSE Act has three strategic functions: (1) it maintains the legislative record that disclosure requirements are constitutional and have majority support; (2) it provides the specific statutory language for state-level DISCLOSE equivalents; and (3) it provides the organizing frame for state ballot measure campaigns. The forced floor vote — likely before the November 2026 midterms as a political accountability measure — will produce a clean disclosure record for use in targeted Senate races.

### 5.2 The 2026 State Ballot Measures: The Near-Term Reform Window

## 6. Reform Pathways

### 6.1 Tier 1 — State Disclosure Requirements (Constitutional and Actionable Now)

The most immediately achievable campaign finance reform pathway exploits the gap between Citizens United (which struck down spending limits) and the doctrine that disclosure requirements remain constitutional. The Supreme Court upheld disclosure requirements as serving anti-corruption and informational interests in Doe v. Reed (2010, 8-1) — the most recent affirmation that disclosure law survives Citizens United.

State-level disclosure laws modeled on Arizona's Proposition 211 represent the template. The "look-through" disclosure requirement — if a 501(c)(4) donates to a super PAC that spends $50,000+ in Arizona elections, the 501(c)(4) must trace and disclose its own donors who funded that contribution — is the structural innovation that Arizona Prop. 211 demonstrated was both constitutional and practically effective. The 72% passage rate demonstrates that disclosure enjoys cross-partisan voter support that the legislative filibuster obscures.

States with existing ballot initiative architectures — California, Colorado, Massachusetts, Nevada, Oregon, Washington — are the primary near-term targets for state DISCLOSE legislation modeled on Arizona's approach.

**Sources**: [Doe v. Reed, 561 U.S. 186 (2010)](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/561/186/); [Arizona Proposition 211 — Ballotpedia](https://ballotpedia.org/Arizona_Proposition_211,_Political_Contributions_Disclosure_Requirement_(2022))

### 6.2 Tier 2 — Small Donor Public Matching Programs

Small donor public matching programs provide a structural alternative to dark money fundraising without conflicting with Citizens United. They do not restrict spending; they amplify the voices of small donors to create competitive parity with large dark money donors.

New York City's public matching program (6:1 match for contributions up to $250) has consistently produced a more diverse donor pool and a more diverse candidate pool. New York State adopted a statewide public matching program in 2020. Maryland, Connecticut, and Washington D.C. have implemented variants. The Federal Government By the People Act would provide a 6:1 federal match for contributions up to $200 from constituents, funded by a small surcharge on federal procurement contracts over $100,000.

### 6.3 Tier 3 — The Hawaii/Montana Corporate Charter Model (New in 2026)

Hawaii SB 2471 and Montana I-194 represent a structurally new reform pathway that operates "upstream" of Citizens United. The theory: Citizens United held corporations have First Amendment rights to spend on politics. But states grant corporations their existence through charter law. If a state removes the charter authority to spend on politics, corporations have no charter-derived power to exercise — and no First Amendment right attaches to a power that was never granted.

The theory faces a significant counterargument (which Hawaii's own Attorney General raised): Citizens United did not hold that corporations have political speech rights because they have charter authority. It held they have those rights because they are "associations of citizens" who retain speech rights when they organize collectively. If the Court agrees with that framing, the charter-revocation theory fails. If the Court treats corporate political spending as a state-derived right that states can rescind, the theory succeeds. This is untested constitutional territory that the Hawaii and Montana litigation will resolve before the 2027 and 2028 election cycles.

### 6.4 Tier 4 — Constitutional Amendment (Long Cycle, Required for Durability)

At least 22 states and hundreds of cities have voted to support an amendment overturning Citizens United. Without a constitutional change, every statutory disclosure requirement and every public matching program is subject to reversal through courts extending Citizens United's logic, through FEC enforcement dysfunction, or through legislative repeal. Constitutional amendment is the only mechanism that makes the reform durable across election cycles and judicial appointments.

**Sources**: [Campaign Finance Reform Amendment — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campaign_finance_reform_amendment); [Government By the People Act — Congress.gov](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/264)
