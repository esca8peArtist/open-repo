# Fiscal Architecture and Post-Capture Fiscal Recovery

*Phase 3 Candidate 5 — Democratic Renewal Proposal Research Supplement*
*April 2026 — Structural analysis of how democracies rebuild fiscal capacity after capture, with US application*

---

## Executive Summary

Fiscal policy is not a secondary concern in democratic recovery — it is the enabling condition. A government that cannot reliably collect revenue cannot fund the civil service, judiciary, regulatory agencies, or social programs that constitute democratic governance in practice. A government whose fiscal institutions are captured by executive fiat, criminal networks, or ideological monoculture cannot credibly commit to any policy because its enforcement apparatus is compromised at the root.

The four case studies in this document — Brazil (2022–2026), Argentina (2023–2026), Peru (2022–2026), and Mexico (2024–2026) — establish three findings that bear directly on post-capture fiscal recovery in the US context.

**Finding 1: Fiscal discipline achieved by eroding institutional checks is structurally fragile.** Argentina's 2024 fiscal surplus — the first in 14 years — was achieved through real-terms cuts to retirement benefits, frozen public investment, and deferred intergovernmental payments. ([IMF Country Report Argentina, February 2024](https://www.imf.org/en/publications/cr/issues/2024/02/23/argentina-2023-article-iv-consultation-press-release-and-staff-report-544866)) These are real gains, but they are not self-enforcing and do not withstand electoral cycling without institutional infrastructure that Milei's deregulation agenda has deliberately weakened. Peru's "islands of effectiveness" — the central bank and finance ministry — maintained credibility through repeated political crises precisely because they were constitutionally insulated. The fiscal gain and the institutional protection are not the same thing and must not be conflated.

**Finding 2: Judicial capture is a direct tax enforcement liability.** Mexico's 2024 judicial reform, which replaced appointment-based federal judges with popularly elected ones, did not merely reduce the judiciary's independence as an abstract governance concern. It structurally degraded the amparo mechanism that Mexican taxpayers use to challenge improper tax assessments, introduced political-loyalty pressure into tax dispute resolution, and triggered a Moody's negative outlook downgrade that explicitly cited institutional weakness as a fiscal risk factor. ([Moody's via Mexico News Daily, November 2024](https://mexiconewsdaily.com/news/moodys-mexicos-outlook-negative-judicial-reform-debt/)) The feedback loop from judicial independence to fiscal capacity is not theoretical — it has a quantifiable cost in sovereign risk premiums and foreign investment flight.

**Finding 3: Some fiscal mechanisms are genuinely self-enforcing once enacted; others require ongoing constituency pressure to survive.** The design choice between these categories is among the most consequential a reform coalition can make. Brazil's arcabouço fiscal (fiscal framework law) incorporated automatic spending brake triggers intended to survive political cycling — but the Lula administration's progressive departure from the framework's targets shows that spending rules without independent enforcement institutions degrade under political pressure. The IRS's Inflation Reduction Act funding — $79.4 billion appropriated in 2022 — represents the clearest US example of a constituency-dependent enforcement mechanism: it required ongoing political protection and was systematically dismantled once political protection was withdrawn.

**The core implication for US fiscal recovery**: Rebuilding from the current dysfunction requires distinguishing clearly between three categories of fiscal lever — (1) self-enforcing mechanisms that activate without requiring subsequent political decisions; (2) constituency-dependent mechanisms that require organized beneficiary pressure to survive; and (3) externally-anchored mechanisms that derive their durability from international commitments or treaty structures. Each category has a different appropriate use. A recovery coalition that treats all three as equivalent will miscalculate which reforms need front-loading, which need constituency organization, and which need international coordination.

The first-100-day window is the highest-leverage period for each category. This document maps the mechanisms, the case evidence, and the implementation sequence.

## Case Study 1: Brazil 2022–2026 — The Arcabouço Fiscal and the Limits of Statutory Rules

### The Fiscal Crisis Lula Inherited

When Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva took office in January 2023, he inherited a fiscal situation distorted by his predecessor's deliberate pre-election spending expansion. Jair Bolsonaro's administration had repeatedly amended Brazil's constitutional spending cap (Emenda Constitucional 95, known as the "teto de gastos"), creating a precedent of norm-erosion that markets registered immediately. The spending cap had been Brazil's primary fiscal anchor since 2016. Bolsonaro's team had made seven significant exceptions and amendments to the cap between 2020 and 2022 — including the R$41.25 billion "PEC das Bondades" in the months before the 2022 election — that progressively eroded market confidence in the rule's enforceability. ([Global Policy Watch, January 2025, Brazil Under Lula: The Second Year](https://www.globalpolicywatch.com/2025/01/brazil-under-lula-the-second-year/))

Lula's campaign commitment to abolish the spending cap was partially fulfilled by replacing it with a new framework rather than eliminating fiscal discipline entirely.

### The Arcabouço Fiscal: Design and Passage

Finance Minister Fernando Haddad presented the new fiscal framework (officially, the "arcabouço fiscal" or fiscal architecture) to Congress on March 30, 2023. The Brazilian National Congress approved it in August 2023 with 379 votes in favor and 64 against in the Chamber of Deputies — a substantial margin that reflected Speaker Arthur Lira's coalition management capacity. President Lula signed it into law on August 30, 2023. ([Covington & Burling, August 2023](https://www.cov.com/en/news-and-insights/insights/2023/08/brazils-new-fiscal-framework-approved-by-congress))

The framework's core architecture:

- **Primary surplus target path**: From -0.5% of GDP in 2023 to +1.0% of GDP in 2026, growing 0.5 percentage points annually, with a tolerance band of ±0.25% of GDP
- **Spending growth rule**: Real spending growth capped at 70% of revenue growth, with a floor of 0.6% and ceiling of 2.5% of revenue growth
- **Brake mechanism**: If the surplus target is missed, the expenditure growth cap contracts from 70% to 50% of revenue growth
- **Social floor protection**: Minimum spending on health and education is explicitly exempted from the cap, incorporating the poorest into the budget's structural logic

The design was deliberately more flexible than the abandoned spending cap, incorporating automatic correction triggers rather than hard ceilings. The congressional adjustment that reduced the bill's vulnerability was a provision reducing over-reliance on revenue projections as the anchor. ([Global Policy Watch, April 2023](https://www.globalpolicywatch.com/2023/04/brazils-lula-administration-presents-new-fiscal-framework/))

### What the Framework Achieved — and Where It Failed

The framework's passage was a genuine institutional achievement: it was debated, amended, and passed through normal legislative procedures within five months, establishing a new fiscal anchor after a year of investor anxiety about the incoming administration's fiscal intentions.

The 2024 implementation tells a more complicated story. Market projections shifted sharply during the year. By November 2024, GDP growth projections had risen from 1.59% to 3.17% — but inflation projections had climbed from 3.90% to 4.63%, the benchmark interest rate from 9.00% to 11.75%, and the real had depreciated from 5.00 to 5.70 per dollar. Market analysts attributed part of the positive growth figures to fiscal spending exceeding the framework's targets. ([Global Policy Watch, December 2024](https://www.globalpolicywatch.com/2024/12/brazils-fiscal-framework-in-peril-impact-on-businesses/))

The mechanism of degradation was not formal amendment but administrative evasion. The administration used definitional maneuvers to classify certain expenditures outside the spending rule's scope. Parallel to this, Lula publicly questioned the Central Bank's independence in December 2024, calling interest rate hikes "irresponsible" and suggesting they would be "taken care of." This created a market signal that the institutional separation between fiscal and monetary policy was under political pressure. ([Business Standard, December 2024](https://www.business-standard.com/world-news/brazilian-currency-faces-volatility-as-prez-lula-criticises-rates-level-124121601242_1.html))

The central bank, however, operated with genuine independence under its 2021 autonomy law. Incoming governor Gabriel Galipolo, a Lula appointee, proceeded with rate hikes anyway — a 1.75 percentage point increase from 10.50% to 12.25% — demonstrating that statutory independence, once enacted, creates institutional facts that individual executive criticism cannot easily reverse. ([Bloomberg, January 2025](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-01-29/brazil-will-extend-rate-hikes-with-galipolo-in-charge))

### Analytical Takeaway for Fiscal Mechanism Design

Brazil's experience from 2022 to 2026 illustrates the distinction between a fiscal rule and a fiscal institution. The arcabouço is a rule — a statutory structure specifying how spending can grow and what happens when targets are missed. The Central Bank's autonomy law is an institution — a statutory structure that creates an independent actor with a mandate insulated from political override.

Rules without institutions to enforce them are constituency-dependent: they last as long as political actors who benefit from them are strong enough to resist those who benefit from evading them. Institutions with independent mandates are closer to self-enforcing: they create bureaucratic actors whose formal authority to resist executive pressure is itself statutory.

The lesson is not that fiscal rules are useless. The arcabouço's passage created market credibility that would not otherwise have existed. The lesson is that fiscal rules require enforcement institutions to survive political cycling — and that those enforcement institutions must be designed with the same attention to independence as judicial bodies.

## Case Study 2: Argentina 2023–2026 — Fiscal Discipline Without Institutional Reconstruction

### The Crisis Context

Argentina under Alberto Fernández (2019–2023) ran a non-financial public sector deficit of 4.4% of GDP in 2023, annual inflation reaching 25.5% per month by December 2023, and foreign exchange reserves at critically low levels. Javier Milei, a self-described "anarcho-capitalist," won the November 2023 election and took office on December 10, 2023, with a radical agenda of spending reduction, deregulation, and — originally — dollarization. ([Michigan Journal of Economics, March 2025](https://sites.lsa.umich.edu/mje/2025/03/31/inflation-and-economic-health-a-case-study-of-javier-mileis-plan-for-economic-recovery-2/))

### The Fiscal Adjustment: Mechanisms and Costs

Milei's fiscal shock therapy achieved a headline result unprecedented in recent Argentine history: Argentina recorded its first fiscal surplus in 14 years in 2024, at approximately 1.8% of GDP. Monthly inflation fell from 25.5% in December 2023 to 2.4% in November 2024. ([EconLib, January 2025](https://www.econlib.org/one-year-of-milei-stabilization-a-balanced-budget-and-deregulation-in-argentina/))

The mechanisms of adjustment reveal its structural fragility:

- **Primary expenditure reduction**: Real cuts of 35% in Q1 2024 versus Q1 2023, concentrated in retirement benefits (the most politically costly category), wages, capital expenditure, transfers to provinces, and energy subsidies
- **BCRA money supply freeze**: Milei prohibited the central bank from printing new pesos — an administratively implemented version of dollarization's monetary discipline without dollarization's permanence
- **Devaluation plus crawling peg**: A 50% peso devaluation in December 2023 followed by a managed 2% monthly depreciation, which by 2024 had allowed inflation to outpace the planned devaluation rate and created peso overvaluation
- **Deregulation**: The December 2023 DNU 70/2023 (executive decree) contained more than 300 deregulatory provisions, followed by the Ley Bases (Law 27,742) passed June 2024 after intensive congressional negotiation

The Ley Bases required stripping the original 664-article bill to 232 articles through coalition negotiations with opposition parties who collectively controlled the legislature (Milei's La Libertad Avanza held only 38 of 257 lower-house seats). The law authorized emergency economic powers for one year, privatization of state entities, investment promotion frameworks with 30-year tax stability guarantees, and labor market deregulation. ([Mayer Brown, July 2024](https://www.mayerbrown.com/en/insights/publications/2024/07/argentina-passes-sweeping-economic-reforms-deregulation-privatizations-and-promotion-of-private-investments-in-large-projects))

### The Structural Fragility Problem

Argentina's 2024 surplus is real but not yet structural. Several factors limit its durability:

**Cyclical versus structural adjustment**: The fiscal surplus was achieved primarily through large real cuts to existing programs rather than fundamental restructuring of the state's revenue and expenditure architecture. IMF analysis distinguishes between cyclical surpluses (achieved through temporary measures that can be reversed) and structural balance (where the present value of projected future results is non-negative). The Milei adjustment looks more like the former than the latter when the composition of cuts is examined. ([IMF Country Report Argentina, 2024](https://www.imf.org/en/publications/cr/issues/2024/02/23/argentina-2023-article-iv-consultation-press-release-and-staff-report-544866))

**Exchange rate vulnerability**: The peso, despite the initial 50% devaluation, had become overvalued again by mid-2024 because 118% annual inflation outpaced the 28% planned devaluation. The 2% monthly crawling peg had slowed to 1% by early 2025 while inflation remained above 2% monthly. Argentina's economic history contains multiple instances of a fiscal surplus achieved while maintaining an unsustainable exchange rate peg that eventually collapsed and reversed the fiscal gains. ([Americas Quarterly, 2025](https://www.americasquarterly.org/article/a-key-pending-challenge-for-mileis-argentina/))

**Institutional erosion risk**: The mechanism of fiscal adjustment relied heavily on executive decrees rather than legislative architecture. When the primary legislative vehicle (the original omnibus bill) was substantially stripped by Congress, the executive relied on emergency powers delegated by the Ley Bases. This is procedurally legal but institutionally fragile — emergency powers have defined lifespans, and their extension requires renewed congressional delegation. Meanwhile, the deregulatory agenda reduced the administrative state's capacity to enforce rules, creating a regulatory vacuum that fiscal recovery programs eventually need to fill. ([CELS analysis, January 2024](https://www.cels.org.ar/web/en/2024/01/on-mileis-omnibus-bill/))

### The Comparative Lesson

Argentina illustrates the mirror-image failure mode to Brazil's. Brazil's Lula government created institutional architecture (the arcabouço, the central bank autonomy law) but failed to maintain political discipline within it. Argentina's Milei government achieved short-term fiscal discipline through spending shock and executive action but without building institutional architecture — no new independent fiscal council, no restructured revenue collection authority, no reformed audit bureau — that would anchor the adjustment through electoral cycling.

Both cases point toward the same conclusion: sustainable fiscal recovery requires the simultaneous construction of the institutional enforcement apparatus, not just the fiscal rule or the spending cut.

## Case Study 3: Peru 2022–2026 — Fiscal "Islands of Effectiveness" Under Political Siege

### The Structural Context

Peru experienced a structural political collapse that began with Pedro Castillo's December 7, 2022, announcement dissolving Congress (a constitutionally impermissible act), his arrest the same day, and Vice President Dina Boluarte's assumption of the presidency. By 2025, Boluarte had become Peru's fifth president since 2020 and had cycled through 61 ministers in her first 24 months, including 16 changes in the finance minister role. ([Frontiers in Political Science, 2025](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/political-science/articles/10.3389/fpos.2025.1451195/full); [IMF, 2024 Article IV](https://www.imf.org/en/News/Articles/2024/03/21/cs32124-peru-concluding-statement-2024-art-iv-mission))

The fiscal result was predictable: the non-financial public sector deficit widened from 1.7% of GDP in 2022 to 2.7% in 2023 and 3.5% in 2024, the highest level since 1992 except during the pandemic. Peru breached its own fiscal rule targets in both 2023 and 2024. ([OECD Economic Survey Peru, 2025](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/oecd-economic-surveys-peru-2025_76f6eb73-en/full-report/achieving-strong-growth-and-safeguarding-fiscal-sustainability_000545c6.html)) Tax revenues remained at only 17% of GDP, among the lowest in Latin America given Peru's income level, while Transparency International ranked Peru 127th out of 180 countries in its 2024 Corruption Perceptions Index, falling six places in a single year. ([State Department 2025 Investment Climate Statements: Peru](https://www.state.gov/reports/2025-investment-climate-statements/peru))

### What Held: The Institutional Exceptions

Against this backdrop of executive instability, two institutions functioned as genuine "islands of effectiveness" — a term used by World Bank analysts to describe functional agencies that maintain performance standards even as the surrounding institutional environment degrades. The IMF's 2024 Article IV commended the BCRP (Banco Central de Reserva del Perú) for "decisive monetary policy tightening" that successfully brought inflation back toward target despite the political chaos above it. The Ministry of Economy and Finance maintained basic budget execution and avoided the kind of fiscal catastrophe — uncontrolled deficit expansion, debt default, currency crisis — that Peru's political instability might easily have precipitated.

The mechanisms that protected these institutions are analytically important:

**Constitutional insulation**: Both the BCRP and the core finance ministry functions have explicit constitutional anchoring that makes removal of their key personnel require formal legal process, creating friction that political actors cycling through office did not have the capacity to overcome in the available timeframe.

**Technical staff continuity**: Unlike line ministries, which turned over political appointees at the minister level, technical staff in the revenue administration (SUNAT) and central bank maintained institutional continuity. Peru's SUNAT had invested in IT systems and professional culture that reduced its dependence on political appointees for day-to-day function. ([IMF, Chapter 8, Peru Fiscal Analysis](https://www.elibrary.imf.org/display/book/9781513599748/ch008.xml))

**International surveillance**: Peru's fiscal and monetary policies are subject to continuous IMF Article IV consultation, creating an external audience whose formal assessments are available to domestic investors, bond markets, and rating agencies. This external surveillance functions as a reputational anchor — degrading these institutions has immediate and legible costs.

### What Failed: Fiscal Anti-Corruption Capacity

The contrast with the BCRP's institutional resilience is Peru's anti-corruption prosecution capacity. Specialized fiscal units investigating cases of alleged illicit financing were reduced from 18 to 4 members in the October 2023 period — specifically during the Boluarte presidency that the units were investigating. ([Infobae Peru, October 2023](https://www.infobae.com/peru/2023/10/22/caso-dina-boluarte-por-lavado-de-activos-en-riesgo-equipo-fiscal-paso-de-18-a-4-funcionarios/)) This is the textbook pattern of institutional capture through administrative attrition: rather than formally abolishing the prosecutorial unit, the executive allowed staffing to fall to a level that made effective investigation impossible.

### The Island Architecture Problem

Peru's case demonstrates both the value and the vulnerability of "islands of effectiveness." The islands — the central bank, parts of the finance ministry, SUNAT's technical core — provided sufficient fiscal infrastructure that Peru did not collapse economically despite five presidents in four years. But the islands are isolated. They cannot enforce fiscal discipline across the economy without functional anti-corruption prosecution capacity. They cannot prosecute tax evasion without a judiciary willing to apply tax law impartially. They cannot mobilize political support for fiscal consolidation when the political leadership is actively degrading adjacent institutions.

The analytical implication: building more islands of effectiveness is insufficient. Islands need bridges — structural connections between fiscal administration, judicial review, and anti-corruption prosecution that allow the functional institutions to reach beyond their immediate perimeters.

## Case Study 4: Mexico 2024–2026 — The Judicial-Fiscal Feedback Loop

### The Reform and Its Fiscal Mechanics

Mexico's 2024 judicial reform, signed into law by outgoing President López Obrador on September 15, 2024, and continued by President Claudia Sheinbaum, made Mexico the first country in the world to elect its federal judiciary — including Supreme Court justices — by popular vote. All federal and state judges were to be replaced within three years through a popular voting process in which Congress pre-selected candidates. ([Wikipedia, 2024 Mexican Judicial Reform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2024_Mexican_judicial_reform); [Mayer Brown, October 2024](https://www.mayerbrown.com/en/insights/publications/2024/10/mexicos-controversial-judicial-reform-takes-effect-assessing-its-impact))

The reform's direct fiscal mechanism operates through the amparo system — Mexico's primary constitutional relief mechanism, which allows individuals and corporations to challenge government actions including tax assessments. The reform introduced several specific constraints on amparo in fiscal matters:

- **No general effects**: Amparo rulings can no longer have erga omnes effect (protecting third parties beyond the named claimant), meaning a successful challenge to an unconstitutional tax provision protects only the petitioner, not the industry
- **Six-month resolution requirement**: Tax matters must be resolved within six months of formal admission, potentially forcing hasty decisions in complex matters
- **Indirect amparo limitations**: Restricted admissibility of indirect amparo in tax credit collection proceedings
- **Alternative Dispute Resolution**: A new ADR law (January 2024) created mediation and arbitration alternatives, but with authorities retaining discretion whether to participate

([International Tax Review, 2024](https://www.internationaltaxreview.com/article/2df85o16h58os28rqps00/sponsored/the-impact-of-mexican-judicial-reforms-on-tax-dispute-resolution); [White & Case, 2024](https://www.whitecase.com/insight-alert/reform-amparo-law-federal-fiscal-code-and-organic-law-federal-administrative-justice))

### The Fiscal Cost of Judicial Capture

The aggregate fiscal signal from markets was immediate and concrete. Moody's changed Mexico's sovereign credit outlook from "stable" to "negative" in November 2024, explicitly citing judicial reform and rising debt. The Mexican government's debt burden was projected to rise to over 45% of GDP in 2025 from 40% in 2023, with further increases to 50% projected for 2027–2028 absent fiscal consolidation. Federal budget deficits reached approximately 6% of GDP in 2024. ([Mexico News Daily, November 2024](https://mexiconewsdaily.com/news/moodys-mexicos-outlook-negative-judicial-reform-debt/); [Fortune, November 2024](https://fortune.com/2024/11/15/mexico-credit-downgrade-negative-judiciary/))

The investment freeze was equally legible: as of September 2024, foreign firms were reported to be withholding approximately $35 billion in investment projects. Foreign investment dropped to $644.9 million in Q1 2024 — lower than Q2 2020, the pandemic nadir. ([CSIS, September 2024](https://www.csis.org/analysis/no-checks-power-effects-mexicos-judicial-reform-foreign-investment-and-usmca))

The mechanism connecting judicial independence to tax enforcement is not merely about investor confidence. Independent courts are the enforcement mechanism for the tax code. When judicial impartiality is structurally compromised, the entire tax enforcement apparatus is undermined:

- Tax disputes that should be resolved on legal merits become susceptible to political influence
- Large corporations with the capacity to fund judicial election campaigns gain structural advantages over ordinary taxpayers
- Criminal organizations that can influence judicial elections gain immunity pathways through the court system — a concern explicitly raised by the Inter-American Commission on Human Rights regarding cartel interference in upcoming Mexican judicial elections
- The deterrence effect of audit enforcement degrades when taxpayers observe that judicial review of tax authority decisions is unreliable

Mexico also enacted a new constitutional framework in December 2024 adding financial crimes including smuggling and false tax receipts to the list of offenses requiring mandatory pretrial detention — a significant enforcement measure. ([Stratego-ST, 2024](https://www.stratego-st.com/newsletters/serious-tax-offenses-in-mexico-smuggling-and-fake-invoices-under-the-new-constitutional-framework/)) But enforcement capacity without judicial integrity is a partial measure: the new criminal sanctions are administered by the same judicial system that the reform has compromised.

### The 2026 USMCA Connection

Mexico's judicial reform intersects with the 2026 USMCA review in ways that have direct fiscal implications. USMCA arbitration mechanisms depend on independent judicial review of investor-state disputes. If Mexican courts are perceived as politically influenced, USMCA partners can challenge whether Mexico is meeting its obligations under the agreement's rule-of-law provisions. This creates a potential external leverage mechanism — the same kind of externally-anchored fiscal pressure that the EU used to extract institutional commitments from Hungary and Poland under its conditionality regulation. ([Wilson Center, 2024](https://www.wilsoncenter.org/article/judicial-reform-implications-rule-law-and-bilateral-relationship-between-mexico-and-united-states))

## Fiscal Mechanisms Taxonomy: Self-Enforcing, Constituency-Dependent, Externally-Anchored

### Category 1: Self-Enforcing Fiscal Mechanisms

These mechanisms, once enacted, activate without requiring a subsequent political decision. They create institutional facts that are difficult to reverse without a visible, politically costly affirmative act.

**Central bank statutory independence with fixed-term mandates.** Brazil's 2021 central bank autonomy law (LC 179/2021) established fixed four-year terms for the governor and directors, staggered with presidential terms, with removal only for cause through a defined process. Once enacted, the law creates a structural obstacle to executive interference: removing a central bank governor requires public action through a defined legal process, making the interference legible and costly. Brazil's experience in 2025 — where Lula's public criticism of rate hikes moved markets but did not change monetary policy — demonstrates the mechanism's durability under political pressure. The key design element is that the central bank does not need political protection each cycle; it needs only protection at the moment of initial enactment.

**Electronic invoicing and real-time tax reporting.** Peru's SUNAT and Mexico's SAT have both implemented mandatory electronic invoicing (e-Comprobante and CFDI, respectively) that creates real-time third-party reporting to the tax authority. Once the infrastructure is built and the requirement is statutory, compliance triggers automatically — every transaction creates a tax record without requiring a subsequent administrative decision. The self-enforcing character derives from the bilateral obligation: a seller cannot provide an invoice the buyer needs without simultaneously reporting the transaction to the tax authority. Mexico's SAT in 2025 recorded a historic year for tax collection ($5.351 trillion pesos, a 4% increase over 2024), with aggressive digital audit selection contributing significantly. ([Mexico News Daily, 2025](https://mexiconewsdaily.com/news/mexico-cracks-down-on-tax-evasion/)) The mechanism continues operating even during periods of political turbulence because it is embedded in the transaction infrastructure itself.

**Automatic spending triggers in fiscal framework law.** The arcabouço's brake mechanism — reducing expenditure growth from 70% to 50% of revenue growth when targets are missed — is formally self-enforcing in the sense that it activates without requiring a new political decision. However, as Brazil's experience showed, the self-enforcing character depends on the administrative integrity of the definitions: if the executive can reclassify expenditures to place them outside the rule's scope, the trigger becomes effectively manual. The design implication is that self-enforcing spending triggers require both clear definitional boundaries and an independent body with authority to make definitional determinations.

**Constitutional debt ceilings with enforcement institutions.** Germany's Schuldenbremse (debt brake), enacted as an unamendable feature of the Basic Law by a two-thirds majority in 2009, prohibited the federal government from running structural deficits exceeding 0.35% of GDP from 2016 onward. The Federal Constitutional Court's 2023 ruling that a €60 billion COVID fund reallocation violated the debt brake — and was therefore unconstitutional — demonstrates that constitutionalized fiscal rules can function as genuine self-enforcing mechanisms when backed by an independent Constitutional Court with jurisdiction over fiscal legislation. The rule's self-enforcing character derives from its constitutional status: changing it requires a formal constitutional amendment procedure that is far more difficult than ordinary legislation.

**Beneficial ownership registries and automatic information exchange.** The OECD's Automatic Exchange of Financial Account Information (AEOI) Standard, once implemented through bilateral agreements, creates automatic third-party reporting of offshore accounts. Wealthy individuals cannot easily evade this by simply not filing because financial institutions are separately required to report. The Inflation Reduction Act's cryptocurrency reporting provisions, had they survived implementation, would have created a similar automatic mechanism for digital assets. The self-enforcing character requires both the legislative mandate and the administrative infrastructure to collect and match reports.

### Category 2: Constituency-Dependent Fiscal Mechanisms

These mechanisms require active, ongoing constituency pressure to survive political cycling. They can be highly effective while their constituency is mobilized but are vulnerable when political attention shifts.

**High-income audit programs.** The IRS's Inflation Reduction Act funding represented the clearest recent US example. The $79.4 billion appropriation was specifically targeted at rebuilding high-income enforcement capacity that had been systematically defunded since 2010. ([Tax Policy Center](https://taxpolicycenter.org/briefing-book/how-did-inflation-reduction-act-2022-affect-irss-budget)) Between 2010 and 2022, the IRS lost approximately one-third of its employees, and audit rates for high-income taxpayers fell by roughly 70%. The IRA funding reversed this trend. But the funding required annual congressional protection. The Fiscal Responsibility Act of 2023 immediately rescinded $1.4 billion. Subsequent appropriations bills rescinded $40.4 billion more. DOGE-related actions by 2025 had eliminated approximately 28,000 IRS employees and 3,600 revenue agents — 31% of the auditing staff. The Budget Lab at Yale projected this would add $643 billion to the federal deficit over ten years net of savings, a concrete demonstration of the fiscal cost of constituency-dependent enforcement mechanisms when the constituency loses power. ([Yale Budget Lab, 2025](https://budgetlab.yale.edu/research/weakened-irs-has-substantial-consequences); [CNBC, May 2025](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/05/07/irs-budget-cuts-doge.html))

**Fiscal rules without independent enforcement institutions.** Spending caps, balanced budget rules, and debt targets enacted only as ordinary statutory provisions — not constitutionalized and not backed by independent fiscal councils with enforcement authority — are constituency-dependent mechanisms. They last as long as the coalition that enacted them is in a position to defend them against the coalition that benefits from exceeding them. Brazil's teto de gastos (spending cap) was amended seven times in six years before being abandoned in 2022. Peru's fiscal rule has been breached in consecutive years without formal legal consequence because there is no independent institution with authority to enforce it. The mechanism's vulnerability is precisely that it requires ongoing political will that the actors who benefit from fiscal expansion have strong incentives to erode.

**Anti-corruption prosecution capacity.** Peru's specialized fiscal units were staffed down from 18 to 4 members through administrative attrition — no law was passed removing them, no public decision was made. The unit's constituency (civil society anti-corruption organizations, international transparency advocates) was not strong enough to prevent the administrative starvation. This is the defining feature of constituency-dependent mechanisms: they depend on a mobilized constituency monitoring compliance in real time, and their degradation can be engineered slowly enough that the political cost at each individual step is too low to trigger mobilization.

**Participatory budget mechanisms.** Participatory budgeting — where community members directly decide how to spend a portion of the public budget — creates a constituency-based enforcement mechanism. Porto Alegre's participatory budget, initiated in 1989 and studied extensively, demonstrated that direct fiscal participation creates an informed constituency that monitors implementation and imposes political costs on non-compliance. ([Harvard Ash Center, Participatory Budgeting, 2024](https://ash.harvard.edu/wp-content/uploads/2024/02/participatory-budgeting-paper.pdf)) But the mechanism is constituency-dependent: Porto Alegre's participatory budget was progressively weakened after the Workers' Party lost municipal control, and the engaged constituency was insufficient to prevent reduction in the program's scope.

**Progressive tax enforcement focused on high-wealth individuals.** Enforcement of wealth and capital income taxes depends heavily on audit capacity that must be funded annually. The structural asymmetry is that the targets of such enforcement — high-income and high-wealth individuals — have the greatest capacity to fund political opposition to the enforcement mechanism. This makes high-income enforcement the most persistently constituency-dependent fiscal mechanism: it requires ongoing political protection against a well-resourced opposition.

### Category 3: Externally-Anchored Fiscal Mechanisms

These mechanisms derive their durability from international commitments, treaty structures, or conditionality frameworks that impose costs on evasion legible to external audiences.

**IMF Article IV consultation and conditionality.** Peru's fiscal "islands of effectiveness" — the central bank and finance ministry — maintained credibility through multiple presidential transitions in part because they were subject to continuous external surveillance. The IMF's 2024 Article IV assessment of Peru commended the BCRP's independence and identified specific weaknesses in fiscal rule compliance. This external legibility creates reputational costs for institutional degradation that are observable to bond markets and rating agencies independently of domestic political signaling. The mechanism is not a binding constraint — Peru breached its fiscal rule without IMF enforcement action — but it creates a continuous information environment in which evasion is legible and costly.

**Credit rating agency sovereign assessments.** The Moody's negative outlook assigned to Mexico in November 2024 explicitly cited the judicial reform's institutional damage as a fiscal risk factor. This created an immediate financial cost — the peso fell 0.3% on the announcement, and the outlook contributed to ongoing currency weakness — that quantified the fiscal value of institutional independence for domestic political audiences who might otherwise treat it as an abstract governance concern. ([Newsweek, November 2024](https://www.newsweek.com/mexico-credit-outlook-moodys-debt-1986536)) The rating agency mechanism is externally anchored because no domestic political decision can reverse it without demonstrating substantive institutional improvement that satisfies external assessors.

**USMCA and other trade agreement rule-of-law provisions.** Mexico's pending 2026 USMCA review creates an external leverage mechanism for fiscal and institutional governance. USMCA's labor and investment provisions require effective legal systems capable of enforcing arbitration decisions. If Mexico's judicial reform creates a judiciary that US and Canadian investors perceive as politically compromised, the USMCA review process creates a structured external pressure mechanism. This is analogous to the EU's conditionality regulation, which suspended funds to Hungary and Poland in proportion to documented rule-of-law violations — making fiscal dependence on external funding a structural incentive for institutional compliance.

**Bilateral tax information exchange agreements (TIEAs) and AEOI.** Once an OECD country has implemented AEOI, its financial institutions automatically report account information for foreign account holders to their home jurisdictions. This creates an externally-anchored enforcement mechanism for offshore tax evasion that does not depend on domestic political will at the enforcement stage: foreign governments are separately required to collect and share information. The mechanism's limitations are in its technical scope (it covers financial accounts but not all forms of wealth) and in the reciprocity requirement (countries must participate in the exchange to benefit from it).

**World Bank and regional development bank conditionality.** Infrastructure lending from the World Bank and IDB frequently includes governance conditions — fiscal transparency requirements, audit institution independence criteria, anti-corruption certification standards — that create externally-anchored incentives for institutional maintenance. Peru's World Bank relationship created an external benchmark against which its fiscal governance could be measured. The mechanism is partial — sovereign states can decline the lending — but for smaller economies dependent on multilateral financing, it creates genuine institutional pressure.

## Integration Map: Fiscal Architecture Connects to Eight Other Domains

The 35-domain diagnostic framework treats fiscal policy as one domain among many. The case evidence shows that fiscal architecture is not a standalone domain — it is the most heavily interconnected domain in the framework, with primary integration points in at least eight others.

### Integration 1: Judicial Independence (Domain 3) — The Revenue Enforcement Multiplier

Mexico's case quantified what had previously been analyzed in theoretical terms: judicial independence is a direct determinant of tax enforcement capability. An independent judiciary is required for:

- Impartial resolution of tax disputes that deters both over-collection by tax authorities and under-compliance by taxpayers
- Criminal prosecution of tax evasion that requires evidentiary standards enforced by courts
- Protection of whistleblowers and anti-corruption prosecutors from political retaliation
- Enforcement of anti-money-laundering orders that disrupt the financial infrastructure of tax evasion networks

The integration point for recovery design is sequencing: judicial independence restoration must precede or coincide with high-income tax enforcement programs, not follow them. An aggressive audit program deployed against a politicized judiciary is worse than no audit program at all — it creates the apparatus of enforcement without the legitimacy, and creates a playbook for selective enforcement by subsequent administrations.

### Integration 2: Civil Service Independence (Domain 5) — Revenue Administration as Technical Core

Peru's "islands of effectiveness" demonstrate that fiscal administration quality is a function of civil service protection for technical staff, not political appointment quality at the top. When SUNAT's technical staff maintained continuity through five presidents in four years, it was because their positions were insulated from the political cycling above them through civil service protections and professional culture. The IRS's loss of 28,000 employees through DOGE attrition is the mirror image: what was constructed as a technical civil service position became precarious when political protection was removed.

The integration point: civil service protection for revenue administration staff must be treated as a precondition for fiscal recovery, not a consequence of it. If the first reform is to fund the IRS enforcement program but the second (civil service protection) is deferred, the funding is vulnerable to the same mechanism that destroyed it.

### Integration 3: Independent Oversight and Audit Institutions (Domain 4 equivalent — GAO/CBO) — Fiscal Transparency as Democratic Function

DOGE's May 2025 attempt to embed personnel in the GAO — and GAO's successful resistance, citing its status as a legislative branch agency — demonstrates that independent fiscal oversight is an active political target, not a passive institution. ([NPR, May 2025](https://www.npr.org/2025/05/16/nx-s1-5401008/doge-gao-congress)) The GAO's $123:1 return on investment (six-year average) makes it the highest-returning oversight mechanism in the federal government.

The International Budget Partnership's Open Budget Survey tracks fiscal transparency in over 120 countries. SAI (Supreme Audit Institution) independence correlates strongly with overall fiscal performance. The World Bank's InSAI index documents a third consecutive edition of declining SAI independence globally — a trend that has accelerated in countries experiencing democratic backsliding. ([INTOSAI, Independence Strengthening](https://intosaijournal.org/strengthening-the-independence-of-supreme-audit-institutions-a-global-commitment/)) The independence conditions the INTOSAI Mexico Declaration identifies — constitutional or legal acknowledgment, financial autonomy, secure tenure, freedom to publish findings — map directly to the reform design requirements for a recovered GAO and CBO.

### Integration 4: Media Freedom and Fiscal Accountability (Domains 21–22) — Publicness as Enforcement Mechanism

Fiscal rules and budget documents are publicly legible only when investigative media has the capacity to analyze and publicize them. The arcabouço's progressive weakening was tracked in real time by Brazilian financial journalists and international media — creating public pressure that partially constrained the administration's evasion. Peru's anti-corruption prosecution staffing was documented by Infobae after the staffing drop — but the documentation came too late to reverse it, in part because media coverage of fiscal administration lacks the immediacy of coverage of political events.

The integration implication: fiscal transparency measures (open budget portals, machine-readable expenditure data, real-time budget execution reporting) create the raw material for accountability journalism, but they require functioning independent media to convert data into political accountability. Sequencing fiscal transparency before media freedom restoration produces data without accountability. The optimal sequence is parallel: open budget data and independent media must be built simultaneously.

### Integration 5: State Capacity and Federalism (Domain 6 equivalent) — Subnational Fiscal Capture

Argentina's Ley Bases reduced transfers to provinces by 35% in real terms in Q1 2024. This achieved federal deficit reduction but at the cost of subnational fiscal stress that creates long-term political pressure for reversal. The integration point is that fiscal recovery at the federal level that is achieved by transferring costs to subnational governments creates a constituency for reversal within the federal system — provincial governments become organized opponents of the fiscal discipline they were forced to absorb.

The IMF's principle that redistribution should occur at the national level to prevent interjurisdictional competition applies in reverse: fiscal consolidation achieved by pushing costs down the federal system creates the same constituency pressure that redistribution programs face.

### Integration 6: Anti-Corruption Architecture (Domain 9 equivalent) — The Fiscal-Corruption Interface

Peru's experience is the clearest demonstration. The BCRP maintained institutional integrity. SUNAT maintained technical capacity. But the anti-corruption prosecution system — which is the mechanism for converting tax enforcement into criminal accountability for politically connected tax evaders — was administratively captured. This created a fiscal enforcement architecture with one missing link: the criminal accountability mechanism. Without it, the deterrence effect of audit enforcement is severely limited for high-net-worth actors with political connections.

### Integration 7: Democracy and Elections (Domain 1) — Fiscal Capture Prevention Through Electoral Mechanics

The timing of fiscal degradation in all four case studies correlated with electoral cycles. Bolsonaro's pre-election PEC das Bondades expanded spending by R$41.25 billion in the months before his November 2022 election. Castillo's populist spending commitments created fiscal pressures that his government lacked the administrative capacity to manage. Milei's deregulatory agenda was legitimized by a genuine electoral mandate but implemented through emergency executive powers that bypassed the legislative process.

Electoral systems that shorten accountability cycles (more frequent elections, fixed-term non-renewable mandates) reduce the window for pre-election fiscal manipulation. But the primary mechanism here is fiscal transparency: if citizens can observe in real time that fiscal discipline is being abandoned for electoral purposes, they can price this into their electoral decision-making. The integration point is that electoral reform and fiscal transparency are not separate agendas — they are complementary mechanisms in a single accountability system.

### Integration 8: International Democratic Cooperation (Phase 3 Candidate 4 connection)

The USMCA mechanism and the EU conditionality regulation represent models for externally-anchored fiscal discipline that the US currently lacks on the receiving end but could engage with as the international rules-based order provides external leverage. For the US context, the primary externally-anchored mechanisms are: bond market pricing (which responds to institutional quality signals), IMF Article IV surveillance (which creates public assessments of fiscal governance), and OECD AEOI agreements (which create automatic enforcement of cross-border tax obligations).

The lesson from international democratic recovery timelines (Phase 3 Candidate 4) that is most directly applicable here: the EU's conditionality regulation was more effective at extracting institutional commitments from Hungary than any domestic mechanism because it created a concrete, legible, quantifiable cost (suspended EU funds) for institutional degradation. Designing US fiscal recovery to maximize the leverage of external anchors — especially the OECD AEOI framework and the USMCA rule-of-law provisions — is a structural choice that strengthens the durability of domestic fiscal reform.

## First-100-Day Implementation Signals

The historical record from comparative democratic recovery (see Phase 3 Candidate 4) establishes that the first two years after an anti-democratic government falls are the highest-leverage period for institutional reform. Within that window, the first 100 days generate the most durable reforms because political capital is highest, opponent adaptation is incomplete, and institutional momentum has not yet shifted to defense.

For fiscal architecture, the first-100-day window should prioritize the three categories in the following sequence:

### Days 1–30: Signal Independence and Restore Institutional Facts

**Action 1: Reinstate IRS enforcement staffing and restore rescinded IRA funding.** This is a constituency-dependent mechanism, but restoration in the first 30 days while political capital is highest creates a foundation for the structural protections that make it durable. The concrete signal: the projected revenue recovery from full IRS enforcement restoration ($597.8 billion in net deficit reduction over 10 years, per Yale Budget Lab) frames this as a fiscal responsibility action, not merely a regulatory one.

**Action 2: Affirm GAO independence through statutory clarification.** The DOGE attempt to embed in the GAO demonstrated that GAO's legislative branch status, while legally clear, is politically contested. A statutory provision explicitly barring executive branch personnel from GAO operations, enacted in the first 30 days, converts a contested norm into an enforced rule — the resilience architecture principle of converting conventions into statutes.

**Action 3: Restore inspector general positions.** The mass removal of inspectors general in early 2025 directly degraded the fiscal oversight infrastructure. Restoration and statutory protection against at-will removal (requiring Senate confirmation for IG removal, as was standard before executive erosion) is the most direct fiscal transparency action available in the first 30 days.

### Days 31–60: Build Self-Enforcing Mechanisms

**Action 4: Enact an independent fiscal council.** The OECD's principles for independent fiscal institutions (OECD 2014, updated 2024) establish that effective fiscal councils require: independent mandate, adequate resources, access to budget information, freedom to publish findings, and secure tenure for leadership. The OECD's 2024 working paper documents that fiscal councils significantly enhance GDP growth and reduce debt and inflation volatility in countries with strong institutional frameworks. Enacting a statutory Congressional Budget Office augmentation — or a separate Fiscal Responsibility Council with independent staff and mandate — before the political window narrows creates an enforcement institution for the fiscal rules enacted in the first 30 days.

**Action 5: Implement mandatory beneficial ownership disclosure with automatic enforcement.** The Corporate Transparency Act's beneficial ownership registry, which the Trump administration attempted to delay and undermine, creates the infrastructure for automatic tax enforcement against shell company tax evasion. Restoring and strengthening the registry converts a constituency-dependent enforcement program into a self-enforcing mechanism by making the information automatically available to the IRS, FinCEN, and enforcement agencies without requiring a subsequent political decision for each audit.

**Action 6: Mandate open budget data and real-time expenditure reporting.** The DATA Act framework already exists; its enforcement and extension creates a budget transparency layer that enables civil society monitoring without requiring a separate agency. Machine-readable budget execution data, updated quarterly, converts fiscal accountability from an expert function into a civil society function — expanding the constituency monitoring fiscal compliance.

### Days 61–100: Anchor Externally and Build Constituency Infrastructure

**Action 7: Commit to OECD AEOI reciprocity and aggressive implementation.** The US's role as both a major user and major source of offshore financial infrastructure makes its AEOI implementation status a critical external anchor. Full AEOI implementation creates automatic enforcement of international tax obligations, reducing the constituency-dependence of the enforcement mechanism.

**Action 8: Signal USMCA rule-of-law compliance actively.** If the Mexican case demonstrates that judicial independence degradation triggers immediate fiscal costs through sovereign credit and investment, the US restoration of judicial independence should be actively signaled to international financial audiences through the same channels. This is not merely symbolic — it creates external anchors (restored investment confidence, reduced risk premiums) that strengthen the domestic political coalition for institutional recovery.

**Action 9: Enact civil service protection for IRS and revenue administration staff.** The conversion of IRS technical staff from schedule F (administrative dismissal) back to career civil service protection is the structural change that converts the constituency-dependent enforcement mechanism into a self-enforcing one. Civil service protection for revenue administration staff — analogous to Peru's BCRP technical staff continuity — creates the institutional infrastructure that survives electoral cycling.

## Realistic Recovery Timeline from Current Fiscal Dysfunction

Drawing from the four case studies and the Phase 3 Candidate 4 timeline analysis, a realistic fiscal recovery timeline for the US context involves three phases:

**Phase 1 (Year 1): Institutional Restoration (Months 1–12)**

The immediate priority is converting existing fiscal agencies from politically captured to institutionally protected. The IRS, GAO, CBO, and inspector general network need statutory protection that converts their function from politically vulnerable to self-enforcing. The arcabouço and BCRP cases show that this phase can be completed within a single legislative year when political capital is concentrated.

Realistic outcome: enforcement staffing restored to 2022 levels, independent fiscal oversight mechanisms restored, civil service protection enacted.

**Phase 2 (Year 2–3): Enforcement Restoration and Trust Rebuilding (Months 12–36)**

Rebuilt enforcement capacity needs 12–24 months to generate results that rebuild taxpayer confidence in the system's fairness. Brazil's experience shows that fiscal framework credibility requires at least two compliance cycles before markets fully price it into sovereign assessments. Peru's BCRP rebuilt credibility through consistent independent action over approximately 18 months after major political disruption.

Realistic outcome: high-income audit rates restored to 2010 levels, tax gap reduction from $696 billion toward $550 billion annually visible in enforcement data, credit rating outlooks stabilizing.

**Phase 3 (Year 3–5): Structural Reform and Anti-Capture Design (Months 36–60)**

The self-enforcing mechanisms that prevent future capture — constitutionalized fiscal rules, automatic stabilizers with independent enforcement, beneficial ownership registries with real-time IRS access, permanent AEOI implementation — require a legislative window that typically opens only after Phase 1 restoration has demonstrated competence. Germany's design-in-advance approach (Basic Law fiscal provisions) is aspirational; the realistic US trajectory is restoration first, then anti-capture design.

Realistic outcome: fiscal architecture that makes a future administration's deliberate degradation more visible and more costly than the current cycle's relatively low-friction capture.
