## Tier 1 — Power-builders doing structural work (highest alignment)

### West Street Recovery + Northeast Action Collective
**Website**: weststreetrecovery.org / weststreetrecovery.org/northeast-action-collective
**Location**: Northeast Houston
**Model**: Disaster recovery as organizing tool → permanent political base. Home repair is the entry point; the output is organized community members with demonstrated wins at city hall.
**Track record**: Won $28M for drainage infrastructure; secured $100M in Hurricane Beryl housing repair funding; overturned a 22-year-old city ordinance limiting drainage maintenance in underserved neighborhoods.
**Structural alignment**: Parallel institution building — demonstrates that material service + base-building translates into durable political capacity. WSR is the most convincing local proof-of-concept for this model.
**Notes**: 12 staff, 400+ volunteers. Northeast Action Collective (the political arm) sends members in yellow shirts to city hall. This is not a protest organization — it's an ongoing governance-pressure organization.

### Texas Organizing Project (TOP)
**Website**: organizetexas.org
**Model**: Year-round relational door-knocking in Black and Latino communities, connected to a PAC running independent electoral campaigns. Operates 365 days a year, not just in election cycles.
**Track record**: 69,000 door knocks in 2023 Houston municipal cycle; helped flip Harris County DA to progressive candidate (2024); co-led $100M housing repair coalition win; co-produced bail reform data reports with Texas Criminal Justice Coalition.
**Structural alignment**: Electoral power-building, accountability. The most important single organization in the Houston structural landscape. Operates at scale with year-round infrastructure.
**Notes**: Also active in Dallas, Bexar, Fort Bend counties. Has a PAC (TOP PAC) for independent electoral work.

### t.e.j.a.s. (Texas Environmental Justice Advocacy Services)
**Website**: tejasbarrios.org
**Location**: East End / Manchester neighborhood
**Model**: Teaches community members to use environmental law themselves — citizen science, regulatory filings, enforcement advocacy. Combines legal capacity-building with organizing.
**Structural alignment**: Regulatory accountability, challenging industry capture of TCEQ. One of the clearest examples in Houston of building community capacity to engage regulatory systems directly rather than just petitioning authorities.
**Notes**: Manchester is among the most industrially contaminated zip codes in the US. TEJAS named Furr High School the "First Environmental Justice School in the Nation."

### Bayou City Waterkeeper
**Website**: bayoucitywaterkeeper.org
**Model**: Clean Water Act litigation + citizen science to hold polluters and city accountable.
**Track record**: Secured a **$2 billion federal consent decree** requiring Houston to upgrade failing wastewater infrastructure — among the most significant accountability wins in the city's history. Established a $20M Private Sewer Lateral Fund.
**Structural alignment**: Legal enforcement of regulatory law, accountability. Consent decrees are one of the few mechanisms that can force a municipality to act against developer/neglect interests.

### Houston Community Land Trust (HCLT)
**Website**: houstonclt.org
**Model**: Permanently removes land from speculative markets. Founded post-Harvey to block displacement of communities of color. CLT model means affordability is permanent, not time-limited.
**Track record**: 200+ households served; expanding into tiny homes, ADUs, repair programs. Partnership with City of Houston and Houston Land Bank, primarily in Acres Homes.
**Structural alignment**: Housing sovereignty, anti-displacement. The CLT model is a structural intervention — the opposite of policy reform that can be reversed.

### Fe y Justicia Worker Center
**Website**: houstonworkers.org
**Model**: Worker center for domestic workers (housecleaners, nannies, caregivers) outside traditional union structures. Legal assistance on wage theft, labor trafficking, workplace injuries.
**Structural alignment**: Parallel institution for workers in non-union sectors. Worker centers are structurally what unions were before union-busting — collective organization for workers who lack formal labor protections.

### Air Alliance Houston
**Website**: airalliancehouston.org
**Model**: Science-based advocacy challenging TCEQ permits, regulations, and enforcement patterns.
**Track record**: Published "Dirty Dozen" (top 12 industrial air polluters in Harris County). Has sued the EPA over Risk Management Program rules. Monitors permit applications and intervenes in hearings.
**Structural alignment**: Direct challenge to regulatory capture by the petrochemical industry — one of the most entrenched industry-government capture relationships in the country.

## Tier 2 — Coalition infrastructure and legal leverage

### Houston in Action
**Website**: houstoninaction.org
**Model**: Convenes Black-led, Latinx-led, and AAPI-led organizing groups across the region. Coalition infrastructure rather than a campaigning org.
**Track record**: Produced "Unity Maps" of 43 communities of interest in Harris County during redistricting. Holds community consensus-building meetings on political priorities.
**Structural alignment**: Power mapping, coalition coordination. Functions as connective tissue between organizations doing structural work — the most important infrastructure role.

### MALDEF
**Website**: maldef.org
**Model**: Strategic litigation on Latino civil rights, voting rights, immigration.
**Track record**: Primary litigant challenging Texas's federal redistricting maps (Houston-area Latino communities as named plaintiffs). Currently pursuing Supreme Court review of Texas voting rights cases. Blocked Ken Paxton's attempt to revoke FIEL Houston's nonprofit charter (federal injunction, though appeals court cleared the state to proceed Oct 2025).
**Structural alignment**: Structural voting rights litigation — the highest-leverage legal tool available.

### Texas Criminal Justice Coalition
**Website**: texascjc.org
**Model**: Data-driven accountability on bail reform, pretrial detention, youth justice. Weekly reports documenting illegal pretrial jailing in Harris County (co-produced with TOP).
**Structural alignment**: Criminal justice structural reform, judicial accountability.

### Common Cause Texas
**Website**: commoncause.org/texas
**Model**: Anti-gerrymandering, redistricting reform, campaign finance, voting rights.
**Track record**: Currently campaigning against Abbott's mid-decade redistricting targeting Houston's Black and Latino neighborhoods.
**Structural alignment**: One of few orgs explicitly working on democratic structural infrastructure (as opposed to issue-based campaigns).

### FIEL Houston
**Website**: fielhouston.org
**Model**: Immigrant-led civil rights org. Know Your Rights training, DACA/TPS support, higher education access, political advocacy.
**Notable signal**: Ken Paxton is attempting to revoke their nonprofit charter for political speech. Appeals court cleared the state to proceed (October 2025). An organization the state is actively trying to destroy for political speech is clearly threatening to captured power.
**Structural alignment**: Civil liberties, anti-repression, immigrant rights.

### Bullard Center for Environmental and Climate Justice (Texas Southern University)
**Website**: bullardcenter.org
**Model**: Academic research center at HBCU. Named for Robert Bullard, "father of environmental justice." Produces the intellectual framework other organizations use. Explicitly frames environmental inequity as structural racism.
**Structural alignment**: Research infrastructure for movement work. The scholarly analog to what the resistance-research project is doing — analysis that enables others to act.

## Gaps in the Houston ecosystem

Three areas where no aligned organization currently exists:

1. **FARA/foreign lobbying accountability** — No Houston org is doing foreign influence tracking, disclosure gap research, or accountability work on the FARA exemptions that allow foreign lobbying networks to operate without disclosure. Common Cause is the closest (campaign finance), but they don't cover the foreign influence angle.

2. **Worker cooperative infrastructure** — Fe y Justicia is the closest analog for alternative labor institution-building. The statewide Cooperation Texas network is Austin-based. Houston has almost no infrastructure for the cooperative economy model.

3. **Food sovereignty governance** — Urban Harvest, Plant It Forward, Ivy Leaf Farms exist but are project-oriented rather than politically organized. None are building the governance structures a community food system requires.

## Honest assessment: fit for the resistance-research project's specific niche

**Short answer: none of them fully fit. The project's goal is more radical than any of these organizations.**

Most of these organizations are doing structural work *within* the existing system — challenging regulatory capture, building electoral power, winning legal cases. That's important and necessary. But the resistance-research project is asking a different question: what happens when the system fails completely — when regulatory capture is total, when courts are stacked, when elections are structured to produce predetermined outcomes — and what do we build to replace it?

That question — comprehensive democratic system design from a clean slate, theory of change for post-captured-system recovery, intellectual blueprint for replacement — has no organizational home in Houston, and arguably no organizational home nationally.

The closest analog to what the project is building is think-tank/academic work (like the Bullard Center's relationship to environmental justice organizing) combined with the base-building model of West Street Recovery. The research produces the blueprint; the base-building creates the capacity to implement it.
