## Category A: Drug Policy (Domain 42)

### CJS Anti-Rescheduling Rider — Updated Status

**What the prior scan documented**: The FY2027 House Appropriations Committee markup (May 13, 2026) advanced Section 591, a rider barring use of any appropriated funds to reschedule marijuana, by 8-6 vote.

**New finding from May 19 scan**: This rider has twice been dropped in bicameral negotiations:
- For FY2026: A House committee advanced a rescheduling-block rider; bipartisan House and Senate leaders dropped the language in the January 2026 final spending deal, while preserving the longstanding medical marijuana state-protection rider.
- The House also subsequently passed a standalone CJS bill protecting state medical marijuana laws while explicitly rejecting the attempt to block Trump's rescheduling move.

**Revised threat assessment**: The FY2027 committee-level rider is real. The bicameral history makes Senate passage of the anti-rescheduling language uncertain. The administration's own policy position (Trump's December 2025 EO directing expeditious rescheduling) creates a cross-pressure that the FY2026 pattern already resolved in favor of dropping the block. The most likely FY2027 outcome given available evidence: the rider does not survive to final enactment — but it could, and the committee vote shows the House faction remains active.

**Action implication for Domain 42**: The existing research documentation should be updated to reflect the FY2026 precedent, and the FY2027 rider threat should be characterized as "committee-passed, historically stripped in conference; outcome uncertain pending FY2027 full appropriations process."

## Category B: Civil Rights / Criminal Justice

### National Voter Database Lawsuit (Common Cause / ACLU / Protect Democracy) — Filed April 21, Now Active

**Status as of May 19**: The April 21, 2026 lawsuit filed by Common Cause, ACLU, Protect Democracy, CREW, and the Harvard Law Democracy and Rule of Law Clinic to block DOJ's national voter database is now the operative legal challenge to the March 31 EO's voter-list component — concurrent with the Judge Nichols case.

**What it adds beyond the Nichols case**: While the DNC/NAACP/LULAC case before Judge Nichols challenges the EO as a whole, the Common Cause lawsuit specifically targets the SAVE program data cross-matching — the mechanism by which confidential voter registration data gets run through DHS immigration databases. It adds factual basis for standing (the SAVE cross-match itself is potentially complete before any data is "transmitted" to states — which cuts against Nichols's "no harm until transmission" reasoning if Common Cause can get to a different court or present additional evidence).

At least 12 states have voluntarily complied with DOJ data demands (Alaska, Arkansas, Indiana, Louisiana, Mississippi, Nebraska, Ohio, Oklahoma, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Wyoming). Five district courts have dismissed DOJ suits on the merits (CA, OR, MI, MA, RI). Arizona dismissal potentially the sixth.

**Phase 2 implication**: Domain 37 Section V needs the Common Cause lawsuit added as a parallel track to the Nichols case. The SAVE-program-cross-match framing is a new legal theory beyond what the Nichols case presents. The 12-state voluntary compliance list should be documented as a suppression-capacity map.

## Category C: Academic / Administrative Law

### SCOTUS CASA Decision — Nationwide Injunctions Landscape (June 2025, Still Reverberating)

The Trump v. CASA decision (June 27, 2025) limited nationwide injunctions from district courts. The practical impact as of May 2026 has been more limited than initially predicted: courts have continued to issue functionally universal relief via class action certification (Trump's birthright citizenship EO remains blocked nationwide via the CASA class action itself). The key for litigation strategy going forward: relief needs to be framed through class action, not universal injunction.

This is not a new development for May 19, but it is confirmed as the operative litigation architecture for all ongoing challenges. The existing strategy of coordinated multi-circuit cases (CASA warrantless arrest cases in DC, Colorado, Ohio, NC) achieves geographic coverage without relying on nationwide injunctions.

**Source**: [Democracy Docket: Where SCOTUS' Nationwide Injunctions Ruling Leaves the Cases Against Trump](https://www.democracydocket.com/analysis/supreme-court-nationwide-injunctions-trump-cases/)

### Corner Post / APA Statute of Limitations — New Plaintiff Opportunities

The *Corner Post* decision (2024, recently confirmed in practice) extends the APA's statute of limitations to run from when a plaintiff is first injured, not from when a regulation was promulgated. This opens new windows to challenge older federal regulations. This is an existing doctrine confirmed current — not a new May 19 development, but relevant for administrative law organizations in the Domain 42 contacts who may be filing at the DEA hearing.
