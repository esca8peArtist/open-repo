## Section 1: Research Session Structure (Time Budget)

### Session 1 — Source Compilation and Outline (5–6 hours)

This is the longest source-compilation session in Phase 2. Domain 48 has the largest empirical literature. Do not rush; the quality gate requires 45+ citations.

Tasks in order:

**1. Disenfranchisement scale (2 hours)**
- Sentencing Project 2025 annual survey: download and record current figure (confirm whether 4.4M is still accurate or has been updated). Record racial breakdown: the 1-in-22 Black Americans figure is the most cited; verify this is current.
- Bureau of Justice Statistics collateral consequences database: access at niccc.csgjusticecenter.org (National Inventory of Collateral Consequences). Record the employment restriction count (40,000+ job categories is the reference figure; update if the database has been revised).
- Vera Institute 2025 incarceration data: vera.org — download current incarceration population and racial breakdown.

**2. Legal foundation documents (1.5 hours)**
- Florida Hand v. Scott: search Florida courts docket or ACLU Florida litigation page for current status. This is the Amendment 4 debt condition challenge — the most documented recent case of LFO poll tax in operation.
- OT2025 SCOTUS docket: scotusblog.com — search for any cases with disenfranchisement or voting rights implications. If none directly on point, note the absence.
- Manza and Uggen "Locked Out" (2006): locate via Google Scholar. The original book is the empirical anchor; identify any 2020–2024 updates to their electoral effects calculations.
- Worth Rises 2025 LFO report: worthrises.org — confirm 83% of formerly incarcerated people carry court debt and $13,607 average.

**3. Reform landscape (1 hour)**
- NCSL voting rights restoration tracker: ncsl.org — identify current state-level automatic restoration legislation active in 2026 (Minnesota enacted 2023; Maryland enacted 2022; Michigan, Pennsylvania, Louisiana have 2026 proposals — verify or update this list).
- State disenfranchisement comparison: sentencingproject.org or brennancenter.org — download current state-by-state map.

**4. Outline construction (30 minutes)**
Build the 9-section skeleton with 3-sentence summaries per section. See Section 3 for recommended structure.

**Session 1 output**: Complete outline + 25+ confirmed sources in bibliography.

### Session 2 — Draft Sections 1–4 (5–6 hours)

**Goal**: 2,500–3,500 words covering the core civic exclusion architecture.

1. **Executive Summary** — placeholder only; draft last.
2. **Section 1: The Democratic Design Problem** (~500 words): The civic exclusion architecture as an unlegislated voting restriction law operating in parallel with the formal electoral system. Lead with the 4.4M figure. Frame the feedback loop: disenfranchisement reduces political accountability for the very officials who set disenfranchisement policy.
3. **Section 2: Felony Disenfranchisement — Scale and Demographics** (~700–800 words): Current count, racial breakdown (1 in 22 Black Americans), state-by-state variation (Maine and Vermont allow incarcerated voting; 11 states impose permanent or long-term bars). Sentencing Project as primary source. Historical trajectory: 1.17M disenfranchised in 1976 → 4.4M today.
4. **Section 3: The Feedback Loop Mechanism** (~700–800 words): The causal chain. Communities with highest disenfranchisement elect the prosecutors and legislators who determine disenfranchisement policy — but disenfranchised residents cannot vote in those elections. Document the Manza and Uggen electoral effects research. The causal mechanism is the analytical core of this domain.
5. **Section 4: The Debt Burden as Poll Tax** (~700–800 words): Legal financial obligations — fines, fees, court costs, restitution. Worth Rises 2025 data. The Florida Amendment 4 case study: voters approved automatic restoration in 2018; the legislature conditioned it on debt payment in 2019; the debt condition created permanent disenfranchisement for those unable to pay. Hand v. Scott litigation. This is the most directly actionable section for state advocacy.

## Section 6: Contingency Procedures

### If Worth Rises 2025 LFO report has not been published
Use the most recent available Worth Rises report (2023 or 2024 edition). Note the gap in the draft: "2025 LFO data pending publication; 2024 figures used." The $13,607 average is from the 2023 report; if it has not been updated, it remains the best available figure.

### If Sentencing Project 2025 survey has not been published by research date
Use 2024 edition (4.4M figure). Note explicitly: "Sentencing Project 2025 data not yet available as of [research date]; 2024 figure used." The domain will still be factually sound — the 2025 update is unlikely to change the structural argument, only the precise count.

### If the feedback loop claim cannot be supported with 2020–2024 electoral data
The Manza and Uggen "Locked Out" (2006) foundational analysis documented the pre-2006 electoral effects. If no 2020–2024 update exists, make the argument from the structural logic (disenfranchisement in communities → reduced electoral accountability → no incentive to reform disenfranchisement) and cite any available empirical proxies. The absence of updated empirical data on the feedback loop is itself a finding worth noting in Section 3.

### If the Domain 54 pre-check reveals partial coverage
If domain-54 covers 3 of 5 mechanisms substantially, identify the gaps and write Domain 48 to cover the gaps as a supplemental domain — cross-reference domain-54 explicitly in the introduction and focus the new research only on what domain-54 does not cover.
