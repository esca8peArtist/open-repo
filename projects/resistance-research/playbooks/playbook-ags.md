# AG Coalition Playbook: Year 1-3 Implementation Guide for State Attorneys General

**Constituency**: State attorneys general — 24 Democratic AGs (including DC), operating through NAAG infrastructure and informal coalition coordination  
**Phase**: Phase 3 Candidate 2 — Institutional Playbooks  
**Leverage tier**: Tier A — highest leverage, clearest action steps  
**Status**: Ready for distribution alongside democratic renewal proposal  

---

## Sector Analysis

There are 56 attorneys general in the United States: one per state, plus the District of Columbia, Puerto Rico, Guam, U.S. Virgin Islands, American Samoa, and Northern Mariana Islands. Following 2024 elections, Republicans hold 27 AG offices and Democrats hold 24 (including DC). The remaining offices are nonpartisan or held by independent officials.

The 24 Democratic AGs are not a single voting bloc. They differ on risk tolerance, political circumstances, state constitutional authority, and electoral vulnerability. Arizona AG Kris Mayes and Minnesota AG Keith Ellison face competitive 2026 reelection races and must calibrate their litigation exposure. Maryland, California, New York, and Massachusetts AGs have more political latitude. This asymmetry is not a weakness — it is a feature. It allows the coalition to put forward the least electorally exposed offices on the most politically charged actions while preserving broader coalition cohesion.

Current coordination level is high by historical standards but operationally fragmented. One year into the Trump second term, the 22-state Democratic AG coalition had filed 71 lawsuits — an average of more than one per week. In the first four months of 2025 alone, 30 multistate actions were filed. If that pace held, nearly 500 such lawsuits would have been filed by end of term. The actual pace moderated but remains extraordinary. The coordination mechanism is primarily informal: AG offices call each other when identifying a litigation target, with NAAG providing a convening and administrative function but not a command structure.

The structural limitation is the Supreme Court's 6-3 conservative supermajority and its aggressive shadow docket. Even a well-won district court preliminary injunction can be stayed by the Supreme Court within days without explanation. The Rhode Island permanent injunction blocking immigration conditions on emergency services funding (one of the coalition's most complete wins) survived multiple shadow docket challenges, but this required sustained litigation effort over months. The coalition has learned to combine state-court tracks, APA vacatur theories, and class action structures to reduce the single point of failure created by federal injunction vulnerability.

The NAAG's Multistate Antitrust Task Force and its 11 specialized working groups provide the institutional infrastructure through which multistate coordination happens most smoothly. For democratic renewal work, the relevant coordination mechanism is the Democratic Attorneys General Association (DAGA), which provides political coordination the bipartisan NAAG cannot.

**2026 AG elections to watch**: Arizona (Mayes, D — competitive), Minnesota (Ellison, D — competitive), Nevada (D-held, open race), Wisconsin (D-held, open race), Ohio (open). Net shifts in 2026 could add or subtract 2-4 offices from the coalition.

## Leverage Within the Democratic Renewal Proposal

The AG coalition's leverage is concentrated in five domains where parens patriae standing, federalism standing theory, or direct harm to state-administered programs creates structural litigation authority.

**Domain 6 — Judicial Independence**: AGs have parens patriae standing for amicus briefs in judicial independence cases. The Maryland AG's office litigated DOGE data access as state harm — a model for translating executive overreach into state-injury standing theory. When the administration targets specific law firms through executive orders (as in the Perkins Coie EO), state AGs who retain those firms have concrete standing arguments. The Yale Rule of Law Clinic's amicus work on behalf of 27 former government officials in Perkins Coie v. DOJ illustrates the clinic-AG coordination model.

**Domain 1 — Electoral Reform**: AGs have their clearest standing theory for election interference challenges under Article I, Section 4. State election administration is a core state function. Federal interference — through the SAVE Act's documentary ID requirements, voter roll purges, or executive orders affecting federal election administration — all create direct state injury. The Democracy Docket has pre-established the procedural infrastructure for rapid TRO filings in election cases; AG offices should integrate with that infrastructure rather than duplicate it.

**Domain 23 — Trade and Tariffs**: The 24-state AG challenge to IEEPA tariffs, culminating in the Supreme Court's Learning Resources v. Trump ruling (February 20, 2026, 6-3 striking down IEEPA authority), is the clearest model case for Phase 3 deployment. AGs filed simultaneously with private plaintiffs in the Court of International Trade; the academic record was pre-staged before argument. The resulting refund order demonstrates that even a 6-3 court will limit executive overreach when the constitutional theory is clear and the record is strong.

**Domain 34 — Congressional Power of the Purse**: DOGE's impoundment of congressionally appropriated funds, when those funds flow through state-administered cooperative programs (Medicaid, Title I education, SNAP, emergency management), creates direct state standing. Rhode Island's permanent injunction blocking immigration conditions on federal emergency services funding is the model. The theory is clean: Congress appropriated the money; the executive cannot impose conditions Congress did not authorize; states that administer the programs are injured when funds are withheld or conditioned.

**Domain 29 — Prosecutorial Weaponization**: State AGs can file civil rights actions in state courts against the same conduct targeted by federal weaponized prosecutions, using state civil rights statutes. This creates parallel litigation that cannot be removed to federal court and is not vulnerable to federal shadow docket stays. For high-profile retaliatory prosecution cases, state AG intervention creates legal cost and narrative disruption for the administration without requiring federal standing.

## Year 1-3 Sequencing

### Month 1-3: Trust-Building and Operational Preparation

The first task is not a lawsuit. It is a structured conversation among AG offices about the democratic renewal framework and how it maps to their existing litigation portfolio. This conversation should happen at DAGA level — a working session at the next DAGA convening, or a purpose-built roundtable with 8-10 AG offices most active in constitutional litigation.

The output of that conversation is a **domain assignment map**: each of the five priority domains gets a lead AG office responsible for tracking developments, convening co-plaintiffs, and maintaining the litigation record. California and New York are natural leads for Domain 6 (judicial independence) given their existing capacity. Massachusetts has led on Domain 34 impoundment. Arizona and Michigan have capacity on Domain 1 electoral challenges.

Simultaneously, AG offices should complete a **litigation triage**: map each active case in the 71-case portfolio to the domain framework. Cases often implicate multiple domains simultaneously. The mapping exercise serves two purposes: it identifies gaps (domains with litigation exposure but no filed cases), and it surfaces cases where the factual record, if properly developed, creates precedent useful for later domains.

The third Month 1-3 task is pre-staging: working with law school clinic partners (Yale Rule of Law Clinic, Harvard Democracy Clinic) to pre-write standing memos for anticipated future executive actions. When the administration issues a new executive order, the AG offices should be able to assess their standing and file a TRO within 48 hours. The NTEU and AFGE model — filing lawsuit against Schedule Policy/Career on inauguration night 2025 with legal theory pre-drafted — is the standard.

### Month 4-9: Institutional Action

The core litigation work in this phase is post-Learning Resources consolidation. The IEEPA tariff ruling validates the constitutional theory that executive overreach of statutory authority, even in national security and economic emergency contexts, has limits. AG offices should use the months immediately following that ruling to file follow-on challenges to analogous statutory overreaches — emergency declarations used to redirect congressional appropriations, executive orders purporting to override statutory civil service protections, and agency rulemaking that departs from statutory text post-Loper Bright.

The second action line in this phase is **state-law floor legislation**. AG offices should coordinate with state legislative allies (see playbook-state-legislators.md when available) to push state civil service protections, state voting rights acts, and state data privacy floors through friendly state legislatures. The AG's role is not sponsorship — it is legal drafting support, constitutional analysis of state measures, and enforcement infrastructure. A state civil service protection law enacted through a state legislature survives federal rollback by definition; the AG enforces it.

The third action line is 2026 midterm pre-litigation. By Month 6 at the latest, AG offices should have completed venue analysis for anticipated election interference scenarios and pre-written TRO templates for the most likely fact patterns: SAVE Act enforcement creating documentary burden on minority voters, federal executive action affecting voter roll maintenance, interference with mail ballot processing. This pre-staging work is pure preparation — no filing until a fact pattern arises — but having the motion ready means the response time can be measured in hours, not days.

### Month 10-24: Legislative and Constitutional Advocacy

Post-midterm, the AG coalition's role expands from litigation-only to active legislative advocacy. If midterms produce House Democratic majority or expanded Senate minority, AG coalition testimony and coalition letters become key inputs into legislative debates on the Saving the Civil Service Act, PRESS Act, and NLRB Independence Act.

The longer-term project in Year 2 is **interstate compact architecture**. AG offices are the legal drafting resource for interstate compacts. Compacts do not require federal legislative approval if they do not affect federal power. A multi-state compact on election administration standards, voter registration interoperability, or digital identity for voting creates a structural floor independent of federal political cycles. AG offices should identify 2-3 compact proposals with clear legal authority and begin drafting in Year 2 for ratification campaigns in Year 3.

Constitutional amendment ratification begins in Year 3. Four amendments have been identified in the broader democratic renewal framework (proportional representation for House, Senate reform, SCOTUS term limits, civil service constitutional protection). AG coalition's role at ratification stage is legal technical assistance to state legislatures and coordination of state-level public interest litigation when ratification campaigns face legal challenges.

## Case Study: The IEEPA Tariff Coalition (2025-2026)

The 24-state AG challenge to IEEPA tariffs is the operational model for Phase 3 AG deployment. The architecture had three simultaneous elements: AG offices filed in the Court of International Trade alongside private plaintiffs (Learning Resources, a small educational toy company with clear concrete harm); law school clinic teams at Yale Rule of Law Clinic and other institutions pre-staged academic analysis that became amicus briefs; and the factual record was built on concrete business harm plus constitutional theory.

The Supreme Court's February 20, 2026 decision in Learning Resources v. Trump struck down IEEPA tariff authority 6-3. This outcome — a conservative supermajority limiting executive power — validated the theory that clear statutory text and concrete harm can overcome ideological composition effects. The resulting CIT refund order created immediate, tangible relief.

**What made it work**: Simultaneous multistate filing created political weight and cost for the administration. Private plaintiff standing (small businesses, not state governments) reduced the political target size. Academic record was pre-staged, not assembled under pressure. Parallel tracks meant the case survived shadow docket intervention at the circuit level.

**What to replicate**: Every major AG coalition action should have (1) private plaintiff parallel track, (2) academic record pre-staged before argument, (3) state-court supplementary track insulated from federal shadow docket, (4) lead AG office with dedicated staff who own the docket.

## Constraints and Allies

**Primary constraint: shadow docket vulnerability.** Every favorable federal injunction is subject to immediate stay by the 6-3 Supreme Court on the shadow docket without explanation. The coalition's response must be structural: APA vacatur theories (which produce nationwide vacatur without an injunction, and which have different shadow docket treatment), class action certification (which creates individual member protection not subject to universal injunction limitations post-Trump v. CASA), and state-court parallel tracks (which are beyond federal shadow docket jurisdiction).

**Secondary constraint: electoral exposure of competitive-state AGs.** Kris Mayes in Arizona, Keith Ellison in Minnesota, and potentially Nevada and Wisconsin AGs face competitive 2026 reelections. Coalition organizers should not pressure these offices into leading actions that create electoral liability. Instead, use them in coalition-signing roles (lower visibility) while California, New York, Massachusetts, and Connecticut lead.

**Funding constraint**: AG offices are state-funded institutions. Litigation costs for major multistate actions can strain smaller state AG office budgets. The coalition should build a cost-sharing mechanism, possibly through DAGA or a purpose-built litigation fund, to spread costs so smaller-state offices can join coalitions without budget strain.

**Natural allies**: Civil service unions (AFGE, NTEU) provide factual record for civil service domain actions; law school clinics provide amicus production and academic record; civil rights coalitions bring additional standing and community mobilization; Democracy Docket provides specialized election litigation infrastructure; Brennan Center provides legal research support.

**Cross-sector coordination point**: AG offices and civil service unions have a tactical conflict risk in Domain 23 (trade): federal workers administer tariff programs that AGs are challenging. A no-targeting agreement — AGs challenge executive authority, not workers implementing that authority — prevents this from becoming an intra-coalition problem.

## Communication Strategy

The pitch to an AG's chief of staff or solicitor general is not ideological. It is institutional. The democratic renewal framework provides what AG offices actually need: a **systematic domain map** that allows their litigation portfolio to be understood as a coherent constitutional defense rather than a series of disconnected emergency responses. AG offices are already doing this work. The framework gives them a shared vocabulary, a prioritization logic, and a cross-state coordination structure that reduces duplicative effort.

The specific ask at initial engagement is modest: review the domain assignment proposal, identify which domains the office has existing capacity to lead, and participate in a cross-AG working session on pre-staging for anticipated executive actions. This is a coordination offer, not an advocacy ask. It does not require the AG to take any new legal position or file any new case.

For public-facing communication, the AG coalition's democratic renewal work should be framed as **constitutional defense of state authority** — protection of federalism, not partisan opposition. This framing is accurate: the cases being filed are about whether the executive can override congressional appropriations, whether federal agencies can unilaterally discard decades of administrative law, whether the president can remove independent commissioners. These are federalism and separation-of-powers questions with genuine bipartisan constituencies, as the Learning Resources ruling demonstrated.

## Success Metrics

**Year 1 metrics (operational)**:
- Domain assignment map completed with lead AG office for each of 5 priority domains
- Pre-staged standing memos for 3 anticipated executive action scenarios
- At least 15 AG offices participating in cross-coalition working session
- 2026 midterm pre-litigation TRO templates complete for 3 election interference scenarios
- Active cases in existing 71-case portfolio mapped to domain framework

**Year 2 metrics (institutional)**:
- At least 2 state-law floor measures drafted with AG legal support enacted in friendly state legislatures
- At least 1 interstate compact draft completed and circulating for ratification
- AG coalition testimony submitted in at least 3 federal legislative hearings on priority reform bills
- Shadow docket survival rate for coalition injunctions: at least 40% (baseline to establish)

**Year 3 metrics (structural)**:
- At least 1 interstate compact ratified by 5+ states
- Constitutional amendment ratification support infrastructure operational in 10+ states
- Established cost-sharing mechanism allowing 20+ state offices to join major coalition actions without budget strain
