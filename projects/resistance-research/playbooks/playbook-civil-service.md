# Civil Service Union Playbook: Year 1-3 Implementation Guide for AFGE, NTEU, and Allied Federal Employee Unions

**Constituency**: Federal civil service unions — AFGE (820,000 members), NTEU (150,000+ members), with allied participation from AFSCME, NFFE, NAGE, SEIU, AFT, and IFPTE  
**Phase**: Phase 3 Candidate 2 — Institutional Playbooks  
**Leverage tier**: Tier A — direct leverage on institutional capture reversal  
**Status**: Ready for distribution alongside democratic renewal proposal  

---

## Sector Analysis

Civil service unions represent the institutional backbone of resistance to executive branch workforce capture. Their leverage is distinct from career staff's leverage: unions operate through collective action, litigation, electoral mobilization, and congressional lobbying. Individual civil servants can document wrongdoing and refuse to implement illegal orders; unions can sue, strike, lobby, mobilize voters, and draft legislation.

**Membership landscape**: AFGE, the largest federal employee union, represents approximately 820,000 federal and DC government workers across more than 1,000 local chapters. NTEU represents approximately 150,000 employees primarily in IRS, Customs, and financial regulatory agencies. AFSCME's 1.6 million state and local members provide a parallel mobilization infrastructure for state-level civil service issues. An expanded coalition — AFGE, AFSCME, NFFE, NAGE, SEIU, NTEU, AFT, and IFPTE — has been acting in unified legal coordination since at least the March 2026 Second Amended Complaint in the shutdown firings case.

**Organizational stress**: The 2025 workforce reductions created severe organizational strain. Approximately 317,000 employees left the federal workforce by end of 2025 — a 13.7% reduction from September 2024 baseline. AFGE itself, operating as a union representing laid-off members, announced layoffs of its own staff: from 355 employees to approximately 150, a more than 55% reduction in union staff capacity. The administration's elimination of elective membership dues through executive order directly reduced AFGE dues revenue, compounding the financial pressure. Dues-paying membership is declining as the membership the union represents shrinks.

This creates a paradox: the moment of greatest institutional attack is also the moment of greatest organizational capacity reduction. The playbook must account for this by concentrating effort on actions that use existing legal infrastructure (filed lawsuits, pre-written briefs, statutory vehicles in Congress) rather than requiring large new organizing investments.

**Legal infrastructure**: NTEU filed the first Schedule Policy/Career lawsuit on inauguration night 2025 — within hours of the executive order. AFGE followed within days. The multi-union Second Amended Complaint (March 2026) added NFFE, NAGE, SEIU, NTEU, AFT, and IFPTE to the unified litigation. The Rise Up: Federal Workers Legal Defense Network maintains a litigation tracker covering all active cases. This pre-existing infrastructure is the asset. The democratic renewal framework builds on it; it does not duplicate it.

**MSPB gap**: Since May 2025, the Merit Systems Protection Board has operated without a quorum following the administration's removal of board members in the wake of Trump v. Slaughter proceedings. Civil service appeals have nowhere to go. The MSPB case backlog, estimated at 2,100% of normal volume in some accounts, represents a justice gap that unions cannot solve through their own litigation but must make visible in congressional advocacy.

## Leverage Within the Democratic Renewal Proposal

Five domains map directly to civil service union interests and capacity.

**Domain 2 — Civil Service**: This is the principal domain. Civil service unions are primary plaintiffs in Schedule Policy/Career litigation (the renamed Schedule F that would convert tens of thousands of career employees to at-will status). The Saving the Civil Service Act (H.R. 492/S. 134) — sponsored by Sen. Tim Kaine, now co-sponsored by Rep. James Walkinshaw, with 78 House co-sponsors including 2 Republicans — is the primary legislative vehicle. The democratic renewal framework's Domain 2 analysis should be integrated into AFGE and NTEU congressional testimony on the bill. Pre-staged standing memos covering Schedule Policy/Career implementation scenarios across agencies give union litigators immediate filing capacity when new conversion lists emerge.

**Domain 6 — Judicial Independence and Independent Agency Structure**: NLRB quorum loss is a direct consequence of the broader independent agency attack being litigated in Trump v. Slaughter. If the Supreme Court overrules Humphrey's Executor in June 2026 (or functionally narrows it), every independent board and commission — including NLRB — loses removal protection. This affects union organizing rights fundamentally: without NLRB independence, union elections can be stayed, dismissed, or reversed by a captured NLRB. Civil service unions have an institutional interest in Domain 6 that aligns them with a much broader coalition.

**Domain 17 — Labor Rights**: The PRO Act, sectoral bargaining pilots, and anti-union-busting statutory reform fall here. Civil service unions have a specific interest in the NLRB Independence Act, which would reconstitute NLRB as an independent Article I court with supermajority confirmation requirements. This structural reform, not just a quorum-restoration bill, is the long-term legislative priority.

**Domain 26 — Government Accountability**: The IG system has been systematically dismantled: multiple IGs fired without statutory notice, IG independence norms violated. Civil service unions lose a key accountability mechanism when IG offices are captured or eliminated. AFGE members who report wrongdoing through IG channels need those channels to be independent. Domain 26 is thus both a policy priority and a membership protection issue.

**Domain 34 — Congressional Power of the Purse**: DOGE's impoundment of congressionally appropriated funds eliminated jobs for union members who administered those programs. The constitutional theory — that executive branch cannot impound funds Congress has directed to be spent — is the same theory that underpins civil service unions' interest in MSPB restoration, IG independence, and agency budget protection. These are all facets of the same structural argument about who controls the administrative state.

## Year 1-3 Sequencing

### Month 1-3: Litigation Consolidation and Legislative Pre-Staging

The immediate priority is ensuring that the multi-union litigation coalition — AFGE, NTEU, AFSCME, NFFE, NAGE, SEIU, AFT, IFPTE — is operating with coordinated legal arguments across all active cases. Inconsistent legal theories in parallel cases create risk of adverse circuit splits. The Rise Up: Federal Workers Legal Defense Network litigation tracker provides the baseline; AFGE and NTEU general counsels should convene with all coalition partners to identify any inconsistencies.

The legislative pre-staging task: the Saving the Civil Service Act currently has 78 House co-sponsors (76 Democrats, 2 Republicans). Before any floor consideration is possible, the coalition needs either a Democratic House majority (possible but not certain after 2026 midterms) or a significantly larger Republican co-sponsor list. The Month 1-3 task is identifying the 30-40 House Republicans in swing districts with large federal employee constituencies — primarily in Virginia, Maryland, and suburban districts around military bases and federal installations — and running targeted constituent contact campaigns through local AFGE chapters. This is not a 2025 floor-vote campaign; it is building the 2027 co-sponsor list.

At the Senate level, S. 134 needs mapping of sympathetic Republicans in states with large federal workforces: Alaska (Lisa Murkowski has been the most reliable Republican partner on federal employee issues), Maine (Susan Collins), and potentially Georgia and North Carolina, where federal military and civilian employment is substantial.

### Month 4-9: Congressional Testimony and State Affiliate Mobilization

AFGE's legislative conference — typically held in March — provides the annual mobilization moment for member constituent visits to congressional offices. The 2026 legislative conference should be organized around three unified asks: (1) co-sponsor Saving the Civil Service Act, (2) support MSPB Reconstruction Act (or equivalent), (3) support NLRB Independence Act. Ten to fifteen member case studies — specific federal workers from specific districts who lost appeal rights because MSPB has no quorum — should be prepared for each targeted congressional office visit. Case studies from the member's district, featuring workers in programs the member supports (VA benefits, Social Security, IRS taxpayer services) are more persuasive than abstract policy arguments.

AFSCME's state and local membership of 1.6 million provides a parallel mobilization track through state legislatures. In states considering state civil service reform — either strengthening state merit systems as a model for federal action, or weakening them in imitation of federal rollbacks — AFSCME state chapters provide the organized opposition. States that enact strong civil service protections into state law create both a model for federal legislation and a policy floor for state employees that survives federal rollback.

The legal track in this phase: post-Slaughter rapid-response (if June 2026 Supreme Court ruling goes against independent agency authority). AFGE and NTEU should pre-assign legal teams to three scenarios: full Humphrey's Executor overruling, functional narrowing, and prospective-only ruling. Each scenario has different implications for NLRB, MSPB, and Merit Systems Protection Board authority. Having the legal analysis pre-drafted means the union can respond to members and Congress within 24 hours of the ruling, not 2 weeks.

### Month 10-24: Legislative Campaign and MSPB Reconstruction

Post-2026 midterms, the legislative priority shifts from co-sponsor building to floor consideration. If Democrats gain House seats (even without a majority), the coalition needs a specific floor-vote strategy: identify the one bill with the broadest bipartisan appeal and push it through discharge petition if necessary. The Saving the Civil Service Act's two Republican co-sponsors in the current Congress suggest bipartisan coalition potential. A discharge petition requires 218 signatures — achievable in a narrowly divided House if the bill is framed as merit-based governance reform rather than union protection.

The MSPB Reconstruction Act is a medium-term legislative priority that has not yet been introduced. The concept — converting MSPB from a quasi-judicial executive branch board vulnerable to removal to an independent Article I court with supermajority confirmation requirements — requires legislative drafting. AFGE and NTEU should commission a legislative draft from a law school clinic or academic partner (Columbia Law's Administrative Law clinic, or Michigan Law's Federal Courts clinic) so the bill text is ready when a legislative vehicle appears.

The constitutional amendment campaign for civil service protection begins in Year 2 pre-staging. The 35-domain framework identifies civil service constitutional protection as one of four plausible amendments. Civil service unions have the organizational infrastructure — 1,000+ local chapters, established member communication systems — to run a state-level ratification campaign. The Year 2 task is commissioning the amendment text, identifying state legislative champions in 10+ states, and building the education infrastructure so that union members understand what a constitutional civil service protection would and would not cover.

## Case Study: NTEU Inauguration-Night Filing Strategy (January 20, 2025)

NTEU filed its lawsuit against Schedule Policy/Career within hours of Trump's inauguration. The legal theory had been drafted before the executive order was signed; the factual allegations were inserted after. The filing was complete within inauguration day.

This is the standard. Not an emergency scramble — a pre-staged response executed on a known timeline.

How it worked: NTEU's legal team had reviewed the publicly available Project 2025 blueprint, which described Schedule F reinstatement in detail. They drafted the constitutional theory (Administrative Procedure Act violation, civil service statute violation, due process for affected employees) months in advance. When the EO text was finalized and signed, the team inserted specific EO language into pre-drafted complaint paragraphs and filed.

**What to replicate**: Every anticipated executive action should have a pre-staged legal response. For civil service unions, the predictable action categories include: new Schedule Policy/Career conversion lists naming specific agency positions, executive orders eliminating collective bargaining rights in specific agencies, IG dismissals without statutory notice, and MSPB board member replacements that maintain the quorum denial. Each category has a different legal theory; each legal theory should be pre-drafted and assigned to a specific legal team with filing authority.

The lesson extends beyond litigation: AFGE's post-midterm legislative strategy should similarly be pre-staged. Draft the Saving the Civil Service Act floor amendment now. Draft the MSPB Reconstruction Act now. When the political window opens, the coalition should be filing documents, not drafting them.

## Constraints and Allies

**Post-Slaughter legal environment**: With MSPB operating without quorum since May 2025, civil service appeals have no venue. Litigation is defensive — challenging illegal actions without the administrative appeal mechanism that normally provides an intermediate step. This means union litigation resources are stretched across both defensive fire-fighting (stopping illegal actions) and affirmative record-building (documenting patterns for eventual legislative and judicial remedies). The resource scarcity is real.

**Dues revenue decline**: The administration's executive order eliminating elective membership dues, combined with a 13.7% federal workforce reduction, has directly reduced AFGE's revenue base. The AFGE staff reduction from 355 to 150 reflects this. The coalition's playbook should account for diminished AFGE organizing capacity by concentrating effort on actions that use existing legal infrastructure (filed cases, legislative co-sponsors already in place) rather than requiring large new staff investments.

**Cross-union trade policy tension**: Building trades unions and civil service unions have historically diverged on trade enforcement. Civil service trade enforcement staff (Customs, CBP, Commerce enforcement divisions) administer tariff programs that other coalition members are challenging. A no-targeting agreement — coordinated through AFL-CIO's political staff — prevents this from becoming an active coalition fracture.

**Natural allies**: State attorneys general (parens patriae standing for state harm from federal workforce disruption), law school clinics (academic record for civil service litigation and MSPB Reconstruction Act drafting), AFSCME and state labor unions (state civil service protection advocacy), congressional civil service caucus (bipartisan identification of members with large federal employee constituencies), NARFE (the National Active and Retired Federal Employees Association, which brings a retiree constituency that extends the political coalition beyond active members).

## Communication Strategy

The pitch to AFGE or NTEU leadership is operational, not ideological. Civil service unions are already doing all the work the democratic renewal framework describes. The framework offers three things they do not currently have:

First, **cross-domain coordination**: AFGE's Domain 2 litigation and its Domain 26 IG advocacy are currently handled by different staff teams with different coalition partners. The framework provides a structure for coordinating these as a unified strategy rather than parallel campaigns.

Second, **pre-staging architecture**: The NTEU inauguration-night model works. The framework extends it: pre-staged standing memos for every anticipated executive action category, pre-drafted legislative texts ready for insertion when political windows open, pre-assigned rapid-response legal teams for Supreme Court rulings.

Third, **constitutional amendment path**: Civil service unions know that statutory protections can be reversed by the next administration. The framework offers a pathway to constitutional entrenchment — a civil service protection amendment that would require 2/3 congressional vote and 3/4 state ratification to undo. Union members, who have lived through two Schedule F cycles, understand why this matters.

For member communication, the frame is **merit over loyalty**: career civil servants serve the public, not the president. The democratic renewal framework's civil service domain is not about union power — it is about whether the government that regulates food safety, processes Social Security claims, and staffs VA hospitals is run on merit or political patronage. That distinction has bipartisan appeal and is the frame for any public-facing messaging.

## Success Metrics

**Year 1 metrics (organizational)**:
- Litigation coordination protocol established across 8-union coalition, with coordinated legal theories in all active cases
- Saving the Civil Service Act Republican co-sponsor list expanded to 10+ (from current 2)
- Pre-staged legal responses drafted for 5 anticipated executive action scenarios
- 2026 legislative conference organized around 3 unified asks with district-specific case studies for 30+ targeted offices

**Year 2 metrics (legislative)**:
- MSPB Reconstruction Act text drafted and introduced in at least one chamber
- NLRB Independence Act introduced with at least 20 co-sponsors
- Saving the Civil Service Act: floor consideration achieved in at least one chamber (discharge petition if necessary)
- State civil service protection legislation enacted in at least 3 states through AFSCME affiliate mobilization

**Year 3 metrics (structural)**:
- MSPB quorum restored (through either litigation victory or legislative action)
- Constitutional civil service protection amendment introduced in Congress
- Amendment ratification education infrastructure active in 10+ states through AFGE local chapter network
- NLRB election certification rate restored to within 20% of 2020 baseline
