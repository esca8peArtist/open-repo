# Law School Clinic Playbook: Year 1-3 Implementation Guide for Constitutional Law Clinics and Rule of Law Programs

**Constituency**: Law school clinical programs — including rule of law clinics, constitutional litigation clinics, democracy clinics, immigration clinics, and administrative law clinics at U.S. accredited law schools  
**Phase**: Phase 3 Candidate 2 — Institutional Playbooks  
**Leverage tier**: Tier A — implementation through legal action and attorney pipeline  
**Status**: Ready for distribution alongside democratic renewal proposal  

---

## Sector Analysis

Law school clinics are the connective tissue between strategic litigation (AGs, appellate litigators) and academic knowledge production (rapid-response publications, domain research, legislative record). They occupy a structural position no other constituency does: they are simultaneously legal service providers (accepting cases commercial firms cannot), academic institutions (producing publishable analysis), training programs (creating the next generation of constitutional litigators), and institutional actors (with their own First Amendment and academic freedom interests directly at stake in the current crisis).

**Scale**: More than 830 legal educators attended the 2025 AALS Conference on Clinical Legal Education — the largest such conference in history. The meeting's theme was explicitly "Reexamining the Role of Lawyers and the Responsibilities of Clinical Teachers in Society," reflecting that the legal education sector is already engaged in exactly the self-examination this playbook calls for. There are approximately 200 accredited U.S. law schools, each with multiple clinical programs. The AALS Clinical Section and the Clinical Legal Education Association (CLEA) provide coordination infrastructure.

**Key clinics engaged in active 2025-2026 constitutional defense work**:

Yale's Peter Gruber Rule of Law Clinic has been the most active in 2025-2026 constitutional litigation support. Its work includes: amicus brief for 27 former senior government officials in Perkins Coie v. DOJ (challenging the executive order sanctioning the law firm); amicus brief in Learning Resources v. Trump (IEEPA tariff challenge at the Supreme Court); amicus brief supporting Harvard in the international student ban challenge; amicus brief on behalf of 28 former Republican-appointed officials challenging birthright citizenship restriction; amicus brief with 31 former senior government officials challenging TPS termination for Haiti and Syria; amicus brief for flag-rank military officers and 86 former ambassadors challenging USIP dismantling.

Harvard Law School's Democracy and the Rule of Law Clinic works with Protect Democracy on impact litigation, policy advocacy, FOIA requests, and election protection. It focuses on identifying plaintiffs, drafting complaints, and producing preliminary injunction motions.

NYU's Constitutional Litigation Clinic, while not offered in 2025-26, provides the organizational template for carceral and policing system challenges through impact litigation that complements rule-of-law defense work at other institutions.

**Threat environment**: Law school clinics are under direct institutional pressure in ways that directly implicate their advocacy capacity. The Trump administration's freeze and threatened elimination of $2.2 billion in Harvard federal grants (partially blocked by district court on September 3, 2025), the new March 2026 Title VI lawsuit against Harvard, and the pattern of executive orders against law firms that represent political opponents all create chilling pressure on clinic advocacy. A federal district court has found that the Harvard funding freeze was unlawful retaliation against protected First Amendment activity — establishing that clinic advocacy is constitutionally protected and that funding threats against it are legally vulnerable. But the chilling effect operates regardless of ultimate legal outcome.

**The pipeline function**: Every student who clerks after working in a rule-of-law clinic carries constitutional defense training into a federal courthouse, a state AG office, a public defender's office, or a civil rights organization. Clinics are not just actors in the current crisis; they are the infrastructure factory for the next decade of democratic defense legal work.

## Leverage Within the Democratic Renewal Proposal

Law school clinics have the most diverse cross-domain leverage of any constituency in the framework. Four domains are especially actionable.

**Domain 6 — Judicial Independence**: The most valuable clinic contribution to Domain 6 is empirical research. A systematic, rigorous coding project covering the Supreme Court shadow docket from 2022 to present — tracking stays granted, legal theories accepted, gap between oral argument and decision, and outcomes — would be publishable in a top law review, cited in circuit court briefs, and useful in congressional testimony. This project requires 3-4 research assistants, faculty supervision, and 6-8 months. The output goes beyond current Brennan Center tracking (which is excellent but advocacy-oriented) by providing peer-reviewed empirical analysis courts can cite. Yale and Harvard clinics are natural leads; Columbia Law Review or Harvard Law Review are natural publication venues.

**Domain 2 — Civil Service and Administrative Law**: The Schedule Policy/Career executive order creates a major administrative law question post-Loper Bright: without Chevron deference, how much does the administration's interpretation of its own civil service authority control? Law review analysis of the intersection of Schedule Policy/Career and post-Loper Bright administrative law — examining how courts should review OPM's determination of which positions are "policy-related" under a now more stringent major questions doctrine — would directly inform AFGE and NTEU litigation. The academic analysis becomes the foundation for the plaintiffs' administrative record argument.

**Domain 36 — AI Governance and Administrative Due Process**: The EFF's FOIA litigation against CMS for WISeR Medicare AI denial records is the model for clinic partnership. Clinical teams can take FOIA cases that commercial firms will not — the damages potential is too small and the litigation too slow. But FOIA cases produce the documentary record that informs congressional testimony, regulatory comment filings, and impact litigation. A law school clinic partnering with EFF on Medicare AI transparency litigation produces both student training and the factual record that Domain 36 statutory reform needs.

**Domain 29 — Prosecutorial Weaponization**: Twenty-two documented cases of retaliatory prosecution are described in the broader democratic renewal framework. An empirical analysis of these cases — examining whether the standard indicia of retaliatory prosecution (timing relative to protected activity, prosecutorial discretion departures from norms, selective enforcement patterns) are present — would be publishable and would provide the analytical foundation for pattern-or-practice arguments in both litigation and congressional testimony. This is precisely the kind of dataset that commercial litigators lack the time to build and that advocacy organizations lack the perceived neutrality to publish.

**Domain 34 — Impoundment and the Appropriations Power**: The constitutional doctrine of impoundment has not been litigated seriously since the 1970s Impoundment Control Act. DOGE's actions have put the question back on the table. A comprehensive academic treatment of impoundment doctrine, tracing the statutory history, the 1974-75 Watergate-era precedents, and the current executive claims, would fill a genuine gap in the legal literature and would be cited in ongoing AG coalition litigation on fund impoundment.

## Year 1-3 Sequencing

### Month 1-3: Trust-Building and Coordination Infrastructure

The immediate task is building a coordination mechanism among rule-of-law and constitutional clinics across institutions. This does not require creating a new organization. The AALS Clinical Section already exists; the 2025 conference's explicit theme of lawyers' societal responsibilities established the space for exactly this conversation. The proposal is a standing working group — call it the Rule of Law Clinics Coordination Network — that meets quarterly, shares research projects to avoid duplication, and maintains a shared rapid-response roster.

Rapid-response roster design: assign each participating clinic one or two anticipated legal events (specific Supreme Court rulings, anticipated executive orders, regulatory changes) for which it will produce the first-out analysis. For the June 2026 term — which may include Trump v. Slaughter (Humphrey's Executor) and Watson v. RNC (mail ballots) — Yale's Rule of Law Clinic and Harvard's Democracy Clinic should have pre-assigned faculty teams and pre-written templates for each of three outcome scenarios. The Just Security/Lawfare/Balkinization publication channels are pre-established. The commitment is 72-hour publication — the standard Harvard demonstrated after Loper Bright in June 2024.

The second Month 1-3 task is identifying the three highest-priority empirical projects from the domain list above (shadow docket analysis, Schedule Policy/Career post-Loper Bright analysis, retaliatory prosecution dataset) and assigning faculty leads at interested institutions. These are multi-semester projects; starting in Month 1-3 means they are ready for publication or congressional testimony use in Month 10-18.

### Month 4-9: Amicus Production and Legislative Record Building

The core work in this phase is amicus brief production. The Yale Rule of Law Clinic model — assembling bipartisan groups of former government officials, military officers, and senior public servants to file amicus briefs on structural constitutional questions — is both effective and replicable. The brief in Learning Resources v. Trump, combined with the private plaintiff litigation track, contributed to a 6-3 ruling against IEEPA tariff authority. The brief in Perkins Coie v. DOJ provided the record on separation of powers and bill of attainder that lower courts needed.

The specific ask to other clinic directors: identify 2-3 Supreme Court cases in the June 2026 term where your clinic has the capacity to produce an amicus brief on structural constitutional questions. Coordinate with Yale and Harvard to avoid duplicating the former-officials recruitment effort (there are only so many former flag-rank officers and ambassadors, and multiple competing asks for their participation creates coordination costs). The AALS coordination network manages this.

For legislative record building: the three priority statutory bills — Saving the Civil Service Act, NLRB Independence Act, PRESS Act — each need legislative findings that can withstand judicial scrutiny under post-Sebelius (NFIB) standards. Courts increasingly scrutinize whether Congress made adequate factual findings when passing legislation. Clinic teams should produce law review articles that function simultaneously as scholarship and as legislative record — establishing, through rigorous empirical analysis, the factual basis for each statute's constitutional grounding.

### Month 10-24: Constitutional Amendment Academic Architecture

The long-term project for law school clinics is pre-staging the academic record for constitutional amendment campaigns. Four amendments are identified in the democratic renewal framework: proportional representation for the House, Senate reform (apportionment), SCOTUS term limits or expansion, and civil service constitutional protection.

Each amendment needs legal academic work at three levels: (1) a comprehensive law review article establishing the constitutional authority for Congress to propose or states to ratify the amendment, addressing the most serious legal objections; (2) a practitioner-facing analysis of the amendment's likely legal interpretive questions — what litigation will follow ratification; and (3) a policy analysis comparing the amendment to international constitutional comparators (Germany's Bundestag mixed-member system, Canada's Senate reform attempts, New Zealand's term limit structures).

The SCOTUS term limits article is particularly time-sensitive. Several constitutional scholars have argued that SCOTUS term limits can be achieved through statute without constitutional amendment, by appointing justices to regular active service on lower courts after an 18-year Supreme Court term. If this statutory route is viable, it changes the legislative strategy entirely. A law review article rigorously analyzing the statutory route — addressing the "during good behavior" tenure question — would either validate or close that path, clarifying the legislative strategy for the democracy reform coalition.

The Year 3 publication goal: at least one law review article per proposed amendment, published in a top-15 law review, with explicit reference to the legislative and ratification campaign applications of the analysis.

## Case Study: Harvard Law School Post-Loper Bright Rapid-Response (June 2024)

When the Supreme Court issued Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo on June 28, 2024 — overruling Chevron deference — Harvard Law School produced four substantive analyses within 72 hours. The analyses appeared in Lawfare, Just Security, and Harvard Law Review Online. They covered: the immediate implications for ongoing agency rulemaking, the impact on specific regulatory programs (immigration, environmental, labor), the relationship to Relentless v. Department of Commerce (the companion case), and the long-term implications for administrative law doctrine.

This was not improvised. Faculty teams had been pre-assigned to specific aspects of the anticipated ruling months in advance. Each team had pre-written analysis for three scenarios: (1) full Chevron overruling (which occurred), (2) significant narrowing without formal overruling, (3) minimal change with doctrinal restatement. When the opinion dropped, the team for scenario (1) inserted the specific holding language into their pre-written analysis and edited for accuracy. Publication followed within 24-48 hours per piece.

**Why it matters**: The first legal analysis frame that emerges after a major Supreme Court ruling has outsized influence on how practitioners, lower courts, and legislators initially understand the case. A 72-hour publication window positions clinic-affiliated academics as authoritative interpreters before political advocates or commercial law firm client alerts dominate the interpretive field. Lower courts, writing opinions in the months after a ruling, disproportionately cite contemporaneous academic analysis from credible institutions.

**What to replicate**: Pre-assign faculty teams to June 2026 Supreme Court cases now — specifically Trump v. Slaughter (Humphrey's Executor, independent agency removal protection), Watson v. RNC (mail ballot signature cure), and any Trump administration emergency certiorari grants in the final weeks of October Term 2025. Each team needs: a faculty lead with a writing commitment, 2-3 research assistant hours on standby, a pre-identified publication venue with editorial contact, and pre-written analysis for each likely outcome scenario. The pre-written analysis should be 90% complete before the opinion drops — only the specific holding language is missing.

## Constraints and Allies

**Funding threat chilling effect**: The Harvard precedent — executive branch threatening $2.2 billion in grants in response to institutional First Amendment activity — is a genuine constraint on clinic advocacy at institutions with large federal research funding. The district court's September 2025 finding that the Harvard freeze was unconstitutional retaliation helps, but the threat calculation is asymmetric: the legal defense costs money and time even when you win. Private universities with large endowments (Yale, Harvard, Stanford) can absorb this; institutions with smaller endowments and larger federal funding dependency face a harder calculation.

Mitigation: clinics at institutions under funding pressure can participate in coordination networks, share research, and contribute to amicus briefs without being lead institutional actors. The coordination network model allows contribution at different risk levels.

**Judicial code of conduct for law clerks and student supervision**: Clinical work directly involving former law clerks or students clerking simultaneously at federal courts raises conflict questions. Clinics should develop clear conflict screening protocols, particularly for cases involving specific judges where students may have upcoming clerk positions.

**Publication timing conflicts with litigation**: Academic publications describing the legal theory the clinic is simultaneously litigating create disclosure questions. Clinic directors should establish clear protocols about when clinic litigation positions can be pre-published in law review form (typically after filing but before argument) and when publication should wait.

**Institutional pressure from law firm advisory boards**: Several law schools have significant law firm alumni relationships, and some law firm clients may be on the opposite side of clinic litigation. Clinical programs need explicit institutional independence policies protecting clinic case selection from advisory board pressure.

**Natural allies**: State attorneys general (who use clinic amicus briefs and welcome academic record support), civil service unions (who need legislative record analysis and litigation support on Schedule Policy/Career post-Loper Bright questions), civil rights coalitions (who provide factual case development support for empirical projects), Protect Democracy and Democracy Docket (who provide litigation partnership), and the Brennan Center (whose tracking work provides the foundation for the empirical research projects).

## Communication Strategy

The pitch to a clinic director or law school dean is not a political ask. It is a professional responsibility argument and an academic opportunity.

Professional responsibility: the organized bar's obligations in moments of rule-of-law stress are well-established. The AALS 2025 conference theme made explicit that legal educators are examining these obligations. The democratic renewal framework offers specific, tractable projects — empirical analyses, amicus brief production, rapid-response publication — that fulfill professional responsibility in the area of specific academic competency, rather than through political advocacy.

Academic opportunity: the current constitutional crisis is the richest environment for constitutional law scholarship in decades. Every domain in the framework is also a live law review article opportunity. The empirical shadow docket project, the Schedule Policy/Career post-Loper Bright analysis, the retaliatory prosecution dataset — each is both useful and academically original. Clinic faculty doing this work are not diverting from their scholarly agenda; they are accelerating it.

For students: clinical work on active constitutional cases is training that no simulation can replicate. The students who worked on the Yale clinic's Perkins Coie amicus brief have briefed at the Supreme Court level before graduating. The students who worked on the Harvard post-Loper Bright analysis understand administrative law doctrine at the level of practitioners who have spent years in it. This is the educational argument for investing clinic capacity in democratic renewal work.

## Success Metrics

**Year 1 metrics (coordination and pre-staging)**:
- AALS coordination working group established with at least 15 participating clinics
- Pre-assigned faculty teams for at least 5 anticipated June 2026 Supreme Court rulings, with pre-written analysis templates
- Three empirical research projects initiated (shadow docket analysis, Schedule Policy/Career post-Loper Bright, retaliatory prosecution dataset)
- At least 8 amicus briefs produced in active constitutional cases, coordinated to avoid duplication

**Year 2 metrics (knowledge production and legislative record)**:
- Shadow docket empirical analysis published in top-15 law review
- Legislative findings analysis published for Saving the Civil Service Act, NLRB Independence Act, and PRESS Act
- Clinic attorneys pipeline: at least 50 clinic graduates placed in AG offices, public defender offices, or civil rights organizations in the 2025-2027 class years (baseline measurement)

**Year 3 metrics (constitutional amendment architecture)**:
- At least one law review article per proposed constitutional amendment (4 articles total), published in top-15 law reviews
- SCOTUS term limits statutory route analysis published and cited by at least one member of Congress in legislative record
- Rapid-response publication protocol tested and validated: 72-hour publication achieved for at least 3 major Supreme Court rulings
