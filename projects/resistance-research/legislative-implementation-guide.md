# Legislative Implementation Guide
## For State Legislatures and Congressional Offices

**Who this is for**: State legislative staff drafting bills, committee counsel, Democratic caucus policy directors, and congressional office legislative directors. Also relevant for legislative liaisons at advocacy organizations.

**The core task**: Use the 35-domain framework to identify which bills to introduce, in what order, with what evidence base, and how to coordinate them across states.

---

## The Monday Morning Question

You have a new legislative session opening. Your caucus has capacity for 8-10 serious bills. The framework gives you 35 domains of reform — how do you select and sequence?

Answer: apply the wave filter, then the feasibility filter, then coordinate with peer states.

---

## Five-Step Decision Tree

**Step 1: Run the wave filter.**

Identify which domains your institution can act on within Wave 1 (restore guardrails) without constitutional amendment, without a federal prerequisite, and without a supermajority. These are your first-session priorities. At the state level, Wave 1 domains typically include: Domain 1 (Electoral Reform — RCV, automatic voter registration, redistricting commission), Domain 7 (Rights Protection — shield laws, anti-surveillance ordinances, anti-protest law repeal), Domain 9 (Federalism — state preemption reform, interstate compact authorization), and Domain 14 (Criminal Justice — bail reform, sentencing, use of force standards). Domain 27 (Higher Education) is immediately actionable for states with public university systems.

Wave 2 domains — those that require building new institutions — include Domain 3 (Economic Justice — sectoral bargaining pilot), Domain 11 (Healthcare — state public option), Domain 15 (Environment — state climate bank). Flag these for year two or three.

**Step 2: Apply the feasibility filter.**

For each Wave 1 domain, score three factors on a 1-3 scale: (1) Do you have the votes? (2) Does the Governor sign? (3) Is there an organized constituency that will turn people out for hearings and generate political cost if the bill dies? Bills that score 3/3 are session-one priorities. Bills that score 2/3 are session-one introduction targets even if passage is uncertain — the debate itself builds the case. Bills that score 1/3 are year-two investments: build the constituency now, introduce next session.

**Step 3: Use the framework's evidence section for the bill record.**

Each domain in the proposal contains: the specific problem, international precedents, U.S. state precedents, and fiscal estimates. This is bill record material, not background reading. Committee counsel should excerpt the relevant domain subsection directly into the bill's fiscal note, legislative findings, and the statement of purpose. The framework's evidentiary standard is designed to withstand committee challenge — it cites peer-reviewed research, federal agency data, and directly comparable state statutes.

For example: a state RCV bill's findings section should cite Maine's four-cycle evidence base (Domain 1g), Australia's 108-year uninterrupted record, and Alaska's 2022 spoiler-effect elimination demonstration. These are all in the proposal; you do not need to recreate the research.

**Step 4: Coordinate the bill with peer states.**

The framework's Domain 9 (Federalism) identifies the interstate compact as the most powerful tool for coordinating state-level action without federal legislation. Identify two or three states advancing the same domain reform in the same session. Contact their legislative counsel. Align bill language where possible — courts give more weight to consistent multi-state statutory language, and aligned language makes future compact formation easier. The Governors Safeguarding Democracy network (launched November 2024, co-chaired by Pritzker/Polis) has developed coordination infrastructure: legislative staff in GSD states have access to shared bill drafting resources and coordination calls.

At the congressional level: identify the existing federal bill vehicle for each domain (e.g., the Fair Representation Act for Domain 1a, the Redistricting Reform Act of 2025 for Domain 1f, the DISCLOSE Act for Domain 1d, H.R.6589/S.3425 for RCV) and focus committee staff energy on strengthening the evidentiary record for those bills rather than starting from scratch.

**Step 5: Sequence for cross-domain reinforcement.**

The proposal's Section 5.4 documents 13 reinforcing feedback loops between domains. The most important for legislative sequencing: Domain 1 (Electoral Reform) wins make every other domain easier by producing a legislature more responsive to median voter preferences. Domain 2 (Institutional Integrity) wins protect the civil service capacity that implements every other reform. Sequence these first. Do not spend Wave 1 legislative capital on structurally ambitious reforms (constitutional amendments, new agencies) that require institutional capacity you have not yet secured.

## Three Concrete Domain Applications

**Application 1: Using Domain 1g (RCV Momentum and Backlash) to draft a state RCV protection bill.**

Domain 1g documents that RCV gains are most durable when protected by state constitutional amendment rather than simple statute — because state statutes have been preempted in Florida, Tennessee, and Indiana. It also documents the Maine Supreme Court's April 2026 ruling that a state constitutional plurality requirement blocked RCV expansion. The legislative implication: introduce both (a) a statutory RCV bill and (b) a constitutional amendment authorizing RCV, on the same day. The statutory bill sets the policy; the constitutional amendment protects it from future legislative repeal. The Maine precedent tells you to check your state constitution's winner language before drafting either bill.

**Application 2: Using Domain 1f (Mid-Decade Redistricting) to draft a state redistricting protection statute.**

Domain 1f documents the specific mechanism by which Texas and North Carolina executed mid-decade gerrymanders in 2025 — state legislative action followed by Supreme Court stay of federal court remedies. The state-level defensive bill based on this domain: (1) prohibit mid-decade redistricting except by court order; (2) specify that any court order finding a racial gerrymander triggers immediate mandatory remediation before the next election, with no stay available; (3) establish criminal penalties for legislators who knowingly approve maps subsequently found to be racial gerrymanders. This directly closes the loophole the Texas playbook exploited.

**Application 3: Using Domain 29 (Prosecutorial Weaponization) to draft a state shield for organizations facing federal targeting.**

Domain 29 documents the legal vulnerabilities in the SPLC indictment (Thompson v. United States bank fraud defect; no identified donor-victims; state bar interference rule as a secondary suppression mechanism). The state legislative response: a state law prohibiting state bar cooperation with federal demands for attorney discipline based on federal charging decisions that lack identified victim standing. Six states were considering such shield provisions in early 2026. Domain 29's analysis of the McDade-Murtha Amendment provides the federal precedent that makes the state analog legally defensible.

## Common Mistakes in Legislative Settings

**Introducing bills without a hearing strategy.** Legislation without hearings is legislation that dies in committee and generates no political record. For each bill derived from the framework, identify the expert witnesses who can testify from the international and comparative evidence base. Domain sections contain the names of the researchers and the institutions (Brennan Center, Democracy Docket, EPI, ACLU state affiliates) who can provide expert testimony.

**Failing to build the vote count before introduction.** The framework is an analytical tool, not a vote generator. Before any bill introduction, know your vote count. If you do not have the votes, introduce anyway — but explicitly frame the bill as a record-building exercise and use the committee hearings to generate evidence for the next session.

**Ignoring the fiscal note.** Every bill requires a fiscal note. The proposal's Section 5.5 (International Benchmarks and Fiscal Analysis) provides the cost estimates and return-on-investment evidence. Use them. A bill with a well-documented fiscal note is harder to kill on "we can't afford it" grounds.

**Coordination without follow-through.** Coordinating bill language with peer states only produces durable results if there is a mechanism for ongoing alignment. Establish a standing coordination call with peer-state legislative staff at session open. Set a shared calendar for bill introduction timing, hearing scheduling, and floor vote timing.

## Implementation Tracking Checklist

- [ ] Wave 1 domain bills identified and scored on feasibility filter
- [ ] Framework evidence sections extracted into bill records for each priority bill
- [ ] Peer state coordination call established
- [ ] Expert witness list developed for each bill's committee hearing
- [ ] Fiscal note drafted using Section 5.5 estimates
- [ ] Cross-domain reinforcement sequence mapped (Domain 1 and 2 first)
- [ ] Constitutional amendment companion bills identified where statutory bills are vulnerable to preemption
- [ ] Coalition partners engaged for constituent outreach during hearing periods
- [ ] Quarterly review scheduled against implementation tracking template
