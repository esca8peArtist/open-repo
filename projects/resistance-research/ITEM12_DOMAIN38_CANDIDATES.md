## Candidate 1: Voting Systems Architecture

### One-Line Framing

A comparative structural analysis of proportional representation, ranked-choice voting, and consensus-based electoral models — with evidence on which variants are most compatible with US constitutional design, most resistant to autocratic capture, and most achievable through identified legislative and state-level pathways.

### Gap-Fill Rationale

Domain 1 (Voting Rights and Elections) proposes proportional representation and cites Ireland and Germany as precedents, but the treatment is conceptual rather than architectural. It does not resolve the contested design questions that practitioners, state legislators, and civil rights advocates will raise when the proposal reaches them: Which PR variant (STV, MMP, open-list) performs best under the Voting Rights Act's majority-minority district requirements? How does the transition procedurally unfold? What does the evidence show about PR's resistance to the specific autocratization playbooks documented in Domain 33 (gerrymandering as autocratization)? This candidate fills that evidentiary gap and upgrades Domain 1 from a proposal to an implementation architecture.

### Current Implementation Landscape

As of March 2026, 49 American jurisdictions use ranked-choice voting in public elections, reaching nearly 14 million voters across 22 states and Washington, D.C. The District of Columbia will implement RCV for its June 2026 primary — the first major citywide implementation cycle — following a 73-percent voter approval of Initiative 83 in November 2024. The DC Board of Elections has conducted at least 30 voter education sessions since January 2026, making it the most carefully documented RCV rollout in US history to date.

The backlash has been simultaneously significant. Between 2024 and early 2025, twelve states enacted statewide bans on RCV: Alabama, Arkansas, Idaho, Kansas, Kentucky, Louisiana, Mississippi, Missouri, Montana, North Dakota, Oklahoma, West Virginia, and Wyoming. This is the most aggressive multi-state suppression of electoral reform since the post-Reconstruction rollback of Black suffrage. The political geography of the bans maps almost perfectly onto single-party trifecta states, demonstrating the mechanism by which RCV threatens incumbent-party advantage.

At the federal level, the Fair Representation Act (H.R. 4632, 119th Congress) was reintroduced by Rep. Don Beyer (D-VA) in 2025. It would implement STV in multi-member House districts of 3–5 seats with nonpartisan redistricting, eliminating gerrymandering structurally rather than procedurally. The bill was referred to the House Judiciary Committee but has no prospect of floor consideration under the current majority.

### International Precedent

The comparative evidence is strong. Nine of the ten countries ranked "full democracies" in the 2023 Economist Intelligence Unit Democracy Index use proportional representation systems; PR is associated with a 5.2 percentage-point boost in voter turnout, higher economic growth, and better environmental policy outcomes. Germany's Mixed-Member Proportional system has produced stable coalition governance for 75 years — coalition formation is a feature, not a bug, because it prevents single-party capture of the legislature. New Zealand adopted MMP in 1996 after a Royal Commission process and two national referenda; the 30-year implementation record shows that coalition governance did not produce instability but did produce more representative policy outcomes. Ireland's Single Transferable Vote system, combined with citizens' assemblies for constitutional amendment proposals, produced the 2018 abortion referendum and the 2015 same-sex marriage referendum — both representing genuine constitutional change through deliberative democratic processes rather than legislative capture. The Irish model is particularly relevant for the US because STV is compatible with geographic representation (single or multi-member constituencies) and does not require a party-list system that would conflict with American political culture.

### Implementation Barriers

The primary barriers are statutory, political, and constitutional. The Uniform Congressional Districts Act of 1967 requires single-member House districts; the Fair Representation Act would repeal this. That repeal requires a House majority that does not currently exist and will not exist until at least the 2026 midterms. State-level PR and RCV adoption does not face this barrier for state legislative elections. The VRA interaction is the most complex legal question: multi-member STV districts can preserve minority representation in majority-minority communities — the evidence from Australia (STV since 1948) and Ireland supports this — but DOJ pre-clearance requirements and Section 2 litigation risk create legal uncertainty that requires advance analysis. The emerging state-level ban trend is a political feasibility constraint: twelve states have banned RCV, suggesting that implementation will be geographically clustered in blue-trifecta states for the near term, with federal adoption requiring a Democratic House and Senate majority plus presidential support.

## Candidate 2: Energy Infrastructure and Democratic Decentralization

### One-Line Framing

How the architecture of energy infrastructure — centralized vs. distributed, utility-owned vs. cooperatively governed — determines the distribution of political and economic power, and what a democratically resilient grid would look like in practice.

### Gap-Fill Rationale

The framework covers infrastructure (Domain 12) and environment/climate (Domain 15) but neither addresses energy governance as a democratic design question. Energy is treated as a policy outcome (clean energy targets, grid modernization funding) rather than as an infrastructure system whose ownership and governance structure shapes democratic capacity. This distinction matters: a grid controlled by investor-owned utilities whose lobbying power shapes state and federal regulation is a different democratic environment than a grid composed of rural electric cooperatives with member-owned governance and broadband co-deployment capacity. The question is not only whether the grid is green, but who controls it.

### Energy Democracy: The Governance Question

Energy democracy pairs the renewable energy transition with democratization of energy production and management — social ownership of infrastructure, decentralized systems, and expanded public participation in energy policy. The concept has concrete institutional expressions: Europe holds registrations for over 2,250 energy cooperatives as of 2024. The EU's Clean Energy for All Europeans package requires member states to enable energy communities and protect their rights to self-generate, distribute locally, and govern democratically. This regulatory architecture — energy communities with democratic internal governance, independence from investor-owned utilities, and community-scale economic benefit capture — does not have a US statutory equivalent.

The US rural electric cooperative sector is the closest institutional precedent: 900 electric cooperatives serve 42 million people across 56 percent of US land area. These cooperatives are member-governed, not-for-profit, and historically oriented toward rural service that investor-owned utilities found unprofitable. They are also, in many cases, the delivery infrastructure for rural broadband — the connection point between grid democratization and digital democratic participation. The Biden-era USDA Broadband Technical Assistance Program awarded $5.2 million in 2024 cooperative agreements for rural broadband deployment; cooperative broadband is now the fastest-growing rural internet provider category. A domain that connects cooperative energy governance to rural civic engagement provides the infrastructure-democracy synthesis that neither Domain 12 nor Domain 15 currently offers.

### The Civic Participation Link

The Brennan Center for Justice has documented that broadband connectivity enables voters to access accurate information about candidates and voting procedures, with rural broadband deprivation directly suppressing civic engagement. Research from the National Telecommunications and Information Administration confirms that BIP awardees made high-speed internet available to over 40,000 previously unserved households and more than 130 community anchor institutions by May 2024. Grid resilience and broadband infrastructure are not merely economic assets — they are preconditions for informed civic participation in geographically isolated communities that are also disproportionately targeted by voter suppression efforts.

Germany offers the most substantive municipal-level implementation record. A 2025 study from Freiburg documents 20 years of participatory governance in a municipally controlled grid transition — the "Energiewende" at the community scale — with findings that sustained political participation in energy governance correlates with sustained participation in other civic institutions. Denmark's wind energy cooperative sector, with over 100,000 household members in collectively owned turbines, provides the economic democracy model: cooperative members receive direct financial benefit from energy transition while retaining governance rights over deployment decisions.

### Implementation Barriers

The primary barriers are structural (US energy regulation is primarily state-level, creating a 50-state landscape rather than a national one), financial (cooperative formation requires upfront capital that low-income rural communities often lack), and political (investor-owned utility lobbying has blocked cooperative competition in most states). The Trump administration's withdrawal from IRA implementation poses an additional threat: the IRA's rural energy cooperative provisions (over $10 billion in rural energy loans and grants) are being defunded or delayed, removing the most significant federal funding stream for democratic energy infrastructure. The academic literature on energy democracy notes a pattern of "rise and fall" — five comparative case studies document that cooperative governance succeeds during transition periods but is often captured or marginalized once the grid becomes economically valuable to larger actors.

## Candidate 3: Intelligence Oversight and Accountability

### One-Line Framing

The structural mechanisms by which intelligence agencies are democratically overseen — FISA court architecture, Inspector General independence, whistleblower protection channels, and FOIA accessibility — and the reform architecture needed to restore genuine accountability after systematic dismantling in 2025–2026.

### Gap-Fill Rationale

Domain 2 (Executive Power) addresses civil service independence and independent agencies generally. Domain 29 (DOJ Capture) addresses prosecutorial weaponization. Neither covers the specific problem of intelligence oversight: the parallel court system (FISC), the Inspector General network, and the whistleblower pipeline that are the only accountability mechanisms that function at the classified-information boundary. The 2025–2026 crisis has exposed these mechanisms' fragility in ways that require standalone structural analysis.

### The Current Accountability Collapse

On January 24, 2025, the Trump administration fired 17 Inspectors General across cabinet departments and agencies without the 30-day Congressional notification required by the Inspector General Act. Judge Ana Reyes ruled in September 2025 that the firings violated the Act, but declined to order reinstatement, recognizing that Trump would simply re-fire the IGs with proper notice. In April 2025, the Trump administration fired NSA Director Gen. Timothy Haugh and his deputy — a dismissal that bipartisan lawmakers from both chambers described as setting back US military and intelligence operations and making America "less safe." These actions, taken together, represent a systematic decapitation of the oversight infrastructure that sits between intelligence collection authorities and democratic accountability.

The FISA court dimension is separately alarming. Section 702 of FISA — which authorizes warrantless collection of foreign intelligence from non-US persons abroad, with US-person communications swept in as "incidental" collection — was set to sunset on April 20, 2026. The Privacy and Civil Liberties Oversight Board's 2026 report revealed that government queries of Section 702 data concerning political, religious, and media organizations surged from 227 in 2024 to 839 in 2025 — a 270-percent increase coinciding precisely with the change of administration. The FISA Accountability and Extension Act of 2026 (S. 3696) would require a warrant before government agencies access the content of US persons' communications collected under Section 702 — a reform that failed in 2024 by a 212-212 House tie vote. The Brennan Center's 2026 analysis documents the structural problem: 18 years of RISAA-style incremental oversight has produced a pattern of temporary compliance followed by new systemic violations; without structural reform to the FISC's architecture — including a genuine adversarial amicus presence and warrant requirements — no amount of reporting mandates will produce durable accountability.

The Intelligence Community whistleblower channel is also under direct attack. A classified disclosure filed with the IG for the Intelligence Community in May 2025 has been reportedly held from Congress by Director of National Intelligence Tulsi Gabbard for political reasons, according to the whistleblower's attorney. The chilling effect on internal disclosures is documented: under the current administration, internal whistleblowers have been fired or punished, deterring new disclosures and allowing wrongdoing to persist.

### Structural Reform Pathways

The reform architecture has both legislative and structural design dimensions. On the legislative side, bipartisan proposals in both the Senate and House would reauthorize Section 702 with a warrant requirement for US-person content queries — the reform that came within a single tie-breaking vote in 2024. The FISA Accountability and Extension Act would also expand the amici system, requiring congressional input into the pool of FISA amicus designees. On the structural design side, academic proposals include an office of the FISA Special Advocate — a permanent adversarial presence at the FISC with access to all FISA applications, not merely the subset where the court appoints amici. Yale Law Journal and University of Pennsylvania Law Review analyses document that the current amicus model provides insufficient access to documents and insufficient participation in routine applications — the systemic violations occur in the routine applications, not the novel legal questions where amici are currently appointed.

The IG network reform requires statutory changes to the 30-day notification requirement (currently insufficient as a protection mechanism, as Judge Reyes' ruling demonstrated) — including a legislative veto of IG firings by committee vote, or a supermajority Senate vote requirement modeled on the Federal Reserve's Board appointment structure.

### International Precedent

The UK's Investigatory Powers Tribunal offers the most relevant international comparison: it is a statutory court with jurisdiction over intelligence agency conduct, able to hear complaints from individuals whose communications may have been intercepted, with an Investigatory Powers Commissioner conducting independent oversight of bulk collection programs. Germany's G10 Commission — a parliamentary body with clearance-level access to intercept authorizations — offers the legislative oversight model. Neither system prevents intelligence collection; both create genuine adversarial oversight that the current US system's secret FISC proceedings do not.

### Implementation Barriers

Coalition-building on surveillance reform is unusual: the most significant bipartisan voting coalition in Congress crosses the standard party-line divide, with libertarian-right Republicans (the Rand Paul/Thomas Massie bloc) joining progressive Democrats on warrant requirements. The 212-212 2024 House tie vote illustrates this fragile arithmetic — it fails when national-security hawks in both parties defect. The current majority has no interest in restricting surveillance authorities; meaningful reform requires either a changed House majority after November 2026 or a bipartisan Senate coalition with 60 votes. The presidential-authority question is also salient: the executive branch has consistently argued that warrant requirements for US-person queries would impair national security, and the current administration has weaponized that argument to entrench intelligence collection against domestic political targets rather than to protect legitimate intelligence operations.

## Candidate 4: Property Rights and Economic Democratization

### One-Line Framing

How land ownership concentration, speculative real estate markets, and the erosion of cooperative and community ownership models suppress democratic participation — and what a democratic political economy of property would look like structurally.

### Gap-Fill Rationale

Domain 4 (Digital Government and Economic Policy), Domain 20 (Antitrust), and Domain 22 (Reparations and Racial Justice) each touch property and economic democracy from one direction, but no domain analyzes the property-democracy relationship as a structural design problem. This gap is significant: the connection between housing instability and reduced civic participation is now well-documented in empirical literature, and the connection between wealth concentration and political capture is the empirical foundation of the framework's democracy-renewal argument. A standalone domain that synthesizes property law reform, cooperative economics, and community land trust governance would connect the economic architecture cluster to the electoral and civic participation clusters in a way the existing domain map does not.

### Housing Instability and Civic Participation

The evidentiary base is substantial. Research published in *Sociology of Education* (2023) and confirmed in PNAS (2024) documents that induced residential mobility produces a persistent decrease in voter participation among low-income Black and Hispanic adults — a negative impact equal to or exceeding the effect magnitude of the most effective get-out-the-vote campaigns. The *Urban Institute* has documented that housing instability creates specific registration barriers: proof of address requirements for voter registration mean that the 40 million Americans experiencing housing insecurity face a structural disenfranchisement mechanism that is legally invisible but empirically powerful. Voter turnout in the US is strongly correlated with income — richer people vote at significantly higher rates — and research confirms that the income-turnout gap has widened as housing costs have risen relative to income.

The Lincoln Institute of Land Policy's 2024 survey identifies more than 308 community land trusts (CLTs) across 48 states, Puerto Rico, and the District of Columbia — up from 289 in 2021. CLTs structurally decouple land ownership from housing affordability by holding land in perpetual community trust while allowing residents to own housing: this model provides housing security without speculative displacement, directly addressing the housing-instability-civic-participation pathway. The Burlington Community Land Trust, founded in 1984 under then-Mayor Bernie Sanders, is the oldest and most studied US example; comparable models exist in London (Community Land Trust), Brussels, and throughout the Netherlands.

### The Cooperative Ownership Dimension

The United Nations declared 2025 the International Year of Cooperatives. The US worker cooperative sector has tripled in the number of co-ops over the past decade, according to the 2025 US Federation of Worker Cooperatives State of the Sector Report, though the growth rate has slowed amid funding losses and economic uncertainty. Stanford SIEPR research finds that worker cooperatives show higher worker satisfaction, more equitable pay ratios, and comparable or superior productivity to conventional firms — but their scale remains marginal relative to investor-owned enterprise. The political economy argument, made by the Democracy Collaborative and supported in a Nonprofit Quarterly essay, is that worker co-ops build grassroots democratic capacity: organizations where people practice democratic governance at work are more likely to participate in political democracy.

The antitrust dimension connects wealth concentration to political capture. Roosevelt Institute analysis documents that the Chicago School's evisceration of structural antitrust enforcement — price effects as the sole metric of anticompetitive harm — enabled the concentration of economic power that now translates directly into political power through campaign finance, lobbying, and revolving door mechanisms. The OECD's 2024 competition report examines the democracy-antitrust nexus, noting that antitrust laws were originally enacted to prevent corporations wielding monopolistic power from using it to "coerce, dominate, or cheat" — a political concern, not merely an economic one. Restoring structural antitrust enforcement (market share limits, breakup authority, democratic-harm analysis) is a precondition for preventing economic concentration from defeating democratic accountability.

### Land Value Tax and Georgist Reform

A 2024 Oxford Review of Economic Policy paper provides updated empirical analysis of Henry George's land value tax — a 19th-century proposal with contemporary academic support from across the political spectrum. The core argument: land value captures publicly created wealth (infrastructure investment, zoning decisions, civic improvement) that accrues to landowners without productive effort. Taxing land value rather than improvements would discourage speculative holding of unused urban land, reduce housing costs, and redistribute captured public value to community benefit. Baden-Württemberg, Germany began implementing a land value tax in 2025 — the most significant contemporary implementation. The 2024 scholarly analysis by Laura Wolf-Powers examines the dilemmas of land value capture in a new Gilded Age, noting that the policy instrument is available but requires political conditions — specifically, majorities that are not structurally dependent on real estate industry financing — that are difficult to achieve under current campaign finance architecture.

### Implementation Barriers

The primary barrier is political economy: the real estate industry is the single largest sector of US campaign finance at the state level, and land value reform directly threatens the speculative model that generates that wealth. Community land trusts require sustained public subsidy and institutional support; the administrative infrastructure (land trust formation, stewardship, resale formulas) is not self-funding at scale without public investment. Cooperative economics face capital access problems: worker-owned enterprises typically cannot access the same equity capital as investor-owned firms, limiting scale even when the governance model is preferable. The antitrust reform pathway requires a changed judiciary (Domain 35 work is relevant here) and a changed consumer welfare standard — either through Supreme Court shift or through statutory redesign that the current Congress will not produce.

## Comparative Impact Assessment

### Ranking by Four Criteria

**Criterion 1: Leverage on Existing Democratic Structures**

Intelligence Oversight ranks highest. The FISC architecture, IG network, and whistleblower pipeline are the accountability mechanisms that operate at the boundary between classified power and democratic accountability — the specific boundary that is being erased in 2025–2026. Dismantling these structures removes the only oversight mechanisms that can survive executive capture of the visible institutions. Property Rights ranks second because the housing-stability-voter-turnout causal chain is the most direct documented pathway between economic structure and electoral outcomes. Voting Systems Architecture ranks third because PR reform is structural rather than crisis-reactive. Energy Infrastructure ranks fourth as most long-cycle in its democratic impacts.

**Criterion 2: Urgency Given 2026 Election Context**

Intelligence Oversight ranks first: Section 702 expired April 20, 2026; the IG network has been decapitated; NSA leadership has been fired; the documented 270-percent surge in FISA queries of political organizations is an active crisis. The 2026 midterms will take place under an intelligence community whose oversight architecture has been systematically dismantled. Voting Systems Architecture ranks second: DC's June 2026 primary is the immediate test case; the Fair Representation Act's 2026 Senate provision is directly relevant to midterm architecture. Property Rights ranks third: housing instability is a chronic suppressor of turnout that will affect 2026 results but does not have a specific crisis window. Energy Infrastructure ranks fourth: the democratic implications are medium-cycle.

**Criterion 3: International Feasibility Proof**

Voting Systems Architecture ranks first: Germany (MMP, 75 years), New Zealand (MMP, 30 years), Ireland (STV, citizens' assemblies) are the strongest and most extensively studied comparative cases in the framework. The evidence is mature and specifically addresses implementation in developed democracies with adversarial political cultures. Energy Infrastructure ranks second: Germany's municipal Energiewende, Denmark's cooperative wind, and the EU's energy communities regulatory architecture constitute a substantial comparative record. Intelligence Oversight ranks third: the UK's Investigatory Powers Tribunal and Germany's G10 Commission are relevant but less directly analogous to US structural needs. Property Rights ranks fourth: CLTs and land value tax have international precedents but the US property law context is sufficiently distinct to limit direct applicability.

**Criterion 4: Coalition-Building Potential**

Intelligence Oversight ranks first by far. The bipartisan coalition on surveillance reform — libertarian Republicans and progressive Democrats — is the most durable cross-partisan coalition in US politics. The Rand Paul-Ron Wyden axis has survived two administrations, two parties' control of the Senate, and multiple FISA reauthorization cycles. This coalition can deliver floor votes even in a hostile majority environment. Voting Systems Architecture ranks second: electoral reform has active constituencies in both parties (Republicans facing gerrymandering disadvantage in some states, Democrats seeking structural majority protection), though the party-position asymmetry is real. Property Rights ranks third: CLT and cooperative advocacy has labor-aligned, racial-justice-aligned, and rural-development-aligned constituencies. Energy Infrastructure ranks fourth: cooperative energy has rural Republican and progressive urban constituencies but they rarely work together.

## Research Roadmap: Candidate 1 — Intelligence Oversight and Accountability

**Estimated research time**: 12–16 hours of dedicated research. This is higher than the comparative estimate for Voting Systems Architecture because the core primary sources are partially classified or hidden behind FOIA requests, and the legislative landscape is changing rapidly as Section 702 reauthorization negotiations proceed.

**Priority Time Allocation**:
- 4 hours: Brennan Center 2026 FISA resource page + CDT analysis + PCLOB report synthesis
