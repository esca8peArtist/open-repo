# Environmental Participation Rights as Democratic Rights
## How the EPA's EJ Infrastructure Elimination Removed Frontline Communities from the Civic Body

*Domain 49 — Resistance Research Framework, Phase 2*
*June 1, 2026*

---

## Executive Summary

On April 20, 2026, the EPA's rescission of the 2009 Greenhouse Gas Endangerment Finding took legal effect — completing what EPA Administrator Lee Zeldin called "the single largest deregulatory action in U.S. history." That framing is accurate, but it understates what actually happened. The rescission did not merely deregulate an industry. It removed the regulatory hook through which frontline communities — disproportionately Black, Indigenous, and low-income — generated the procedural standing to contest industrial facility siting in their neighborhoods.

This document makes an argument no existing environmental advocacy document makes in full: environmental justice communities are not merely environmental policy victims. They are democratic participants who have been systematically stripped of the legal and procedural tools through which they exercised civic power. The 2025–2026 elimination of EPA's EJ infrastructure — OEJECR closed March 12, 2025; NEPA regulations rescinded January 8, 2026; Endangerment Finding effective April 20, 2026; EJSCREEN removed February 5, 2025; 781 EJ grants terminated — is not a policy rollback. It is a coordinated removal of democratic participation infrastructure from the communities most dependent on it.

The D.C. Circuit cases challenging the Endangerment Finding rescission (Nos. 26-1037, 26-1038, 26-1039, 26-1061) are the live litigation window. Organizations receiving this document can support amicus filings, coalition communications, and community education while litigation proceeds. The pre-midterm advocacy window (through August 2026) adds urgency: environmental burden and civic disengagement are co-located, and the communities being stripped of federal EJ protection are the same communities targeted by voter suppression infrastructure documented elsewhere in this framework.

## Section 1: The Democratic Design Argument — Why This Is Not Just an Environmental Document

### 1.1 Reframing the Stakes

The conventional framing of EPA's 2025–2026 actions is environmental: rollbacks of regulations, elimination of offices, cancellation of grants. That framing is accurate but insufficient. It captures what was removed but not *who* lost the capacity to act democratically.

Consider the mechanism: for three decades, the EPA's environmental justice infrastructure gave frontline communities formal channels to contest decisions about their own health, land, and survival. These channels were not charity. They were legal rights — grounded in Title VI of the Civil Rights Act, operationalized through NEPA's Environmental Impact Statement process, screened through EJSCREEN data, and administered by the Office of Environmental Justice and External Civil Rights. When a majority-Black community in Louisiana's Cancer Alley contested a permit for a chloroprene-emitting chemical plant, it used these channels. When a tribal nation contested a pipeline through sacred land, it used the NEPA consultation requirement. When West Harlem residents challenged the MTA's siting of diesel bus depots in their neighborhood, they used Title VI.

Those channels are now closed or functionally destroyed. The question this document asks is: what happens to democratic participation when you remove the participation infrastructure from the communities most dependent on it?

The answer the Union of Concerned Scientists has documented is a reinforcing cycle: environmental burden concentrates in communities already deprived of political power; that burden — through chronic illness, economic instability, and displacement — suppresses civic engagement; suppressed civic engagement allows further environmental degradation without political accountability; which further suppresses health and civic capacity [UCS 2018a]. The EPA's 2025–2026 actions did not create this cycle. They removed the formal mechanisms that partially interrupted it.

### 1.2 The Scale of What Was Removed

Between February 2025 and April 2026, the following EPA infrastructure was eliminated or rendered nonfunctional:

- **OEJECR eliminated** (March 12, 2025): The Office of Environmental Justice and External Civil Rights, along with all 10 regional EPA environmental justice offices, was ordered closed by Administrator Zeldin. Approximately 171 EPA employees were placed on administrative leave in February 2025; by April 22, 2025, EPA announced plans to terminate nearly 300 employees from its environmental and civil rights workforce [Inside Climate News 2025].

- **EJ grants terminated** (throughout 2025): The EPA moved to cancel 781 environmental justice grants — almost all funded through the Inflation Reduction Act's $3 billion Environmental and Climate Justice Block Grant Program and the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law. Communities that had received award notices — including a majority-Latino community in San Diego expecting $12.7 million for zero-emission transportation — found funding suspended or terminated [Eos 2025].

- **EJSCREEN removed** (February 5, 2025): EPA removed from its website EJSCREEN, the environmental justice mapping and screening tool that communities, regulators, and advocates used to document disproportionate pollution burdens, generate data for permit challenges, and demonstrate eligibility for EJ grant programs [EDGI 2025; Harvard EELP 2025b].

- **NEPA regulations rescinded** (January 8, 2026): The Council on Environmental Quality rescinded all of its NEPA implementing regulations through Federal Register rule 2026-00178, effective immediately. The rescission eliminated the Phase 2 rule's requirements to consider Indigenous knowledge, account for cumulative impacts on environmental justice communities, and conduct early consultation with affected populations [Federal Register 2026a; Harvard EELP 2026a].

- **Endangerment Finding rescinded** (effective April 20, 2026): The 2009 Greenhouse Gas Endangerment Finding — the scientific and legal predicate for all federal GHG emissions regulation under the Clean Air Act — was rescinded through Federal Register rule 2026-03157, finalized February 12, 2026, effective April 20 [Federal Register 2026b].

The aggregate effect is the elimination of every formal mechanism through which environmental justice communities could assert procedural rights at the federal level.

### 1.3 What Domain 15 Does Not Cover

Domain 15 of this framework catalogs environmental regulatory rollbacks. This document does not duplicate that catalog. Domain 49's distinct contribution is the democratic participation analysis: why the rollbacks disable democratic voice in addition to environmental protection; how administrative channels functioned as civic participation tools before they were closed; and what the communities who used those channels have now lost beyond the environmental protections themselves.

## Section 2: Five Causal Pathways from Environmental Burden to Democratic Exclusion

### Pathway 1: Health Burden as Civic Suppression

The most foundational causal pathway runs from environmental pollution through chronic illness to depressed civic participation. Black Americans are 79% more likely than white Americans to live in neighborhoods where industrial pollution is heavily concentrated [Johnson et al. 2008, cited in environmental racism literature]. Black Americans are 75% more likely than white Americans to live near commercial facilities that produce noise, odor, traffic, or emissions [WRI 2021]. In Louisiana's Cancer Alley — the 85-mile petrochemical corridor between Baton Rouge and New Orleans — communities of color face 7 to 21 times the pollution levels of industrialized white communities, with children attending elementary school adjacent to facilities emitting chloroprene at 8,000 times EPA's acceptable threshold [PRRAC 2024].

The civic consequence is documented by UCS's Center for Science and Democracy: "health problems in a community tend to depress voter turnout, further limiting the community's ability to use the democratic process to protect itself" [UCS 2018a]. This is a structurally reinforcing loop. States with more restrictive voting policies also tend to have worse health outcomes. Legislatures in states with extreme gerrymandering show less willingness to expand Medicaid or provide healthcare services [UCS 2018a]. The geographic co-location of pollution burden, voting restriction, and healthcare deprivation is not coincidental — it is the operating logic of political disempowerment through environmental design.

The 2026 Civic Information Index (Listening Post Collective), which for the first time integrates environmental resilience data alongside voter registration and civic health metrics, finds lower civic health scores consistently associated with communities with higher percentages of Black, Hispanic/Latino, and Native American residents — explicitly documenting the overlap between environmental vulnerability and civic disengagement [Listening Post Collective 2026]. The Appalachia and Black South gaps documented in the Index are precisely the regions where EPA EJ infrastructure has been eliminated without state-level replacement.

### Pathway 2: NEPA Participation Rights as Procedural Gateway — Now Closed

NEPA created a formal mechanism for community standing in federal decision-making. The Environmental Impact Statement process required federal agencies to solicit public comment before approving projects affecting their communities. For EJ communities, NEPA EIS comment periods were frequently the *primary* democratic channel available — the only formal forum where residents' objections to industrial facility siting, highway construction through residential neighborhoods, or pipeline routing through tribal lands were legally required to be considered.

CEQ's January 8, 2026 rescission of all NEPA implementing regulations (Federal Register 2026-00178) eliminated this procedural standing. The prior Phase 2 NEPA regulations, finalized during the Biden administration, had specifically required agencies to:
- Consider Indigenous knowledge in environmental reviews
- Account for cumulative impacts on communities with environmental justice concerns
- Conduct early consultation with potentially impacted communities

The rescinded regulations replaced all of these requirements with guidance that "agencies have discretion over whether and how to conduct NEPA reviews" [CEQ 2026 guidance, per Harvard EELP 2026a]. For EJ advocates who had built litigation strategies around procedural predictability, this discretion is not flexibility — it is the elimination of the legal hook. As a coalition of 228 organizations warned in August 2025, the approach would "cut the people out of the people's environmental law" [Tribal Business News 2025].

The loss falls hardest on Indigenous nations. The NEPA consultation requirement was the primary tool tribal governments used to assert standing in federal project decisions affecting their lands — the Standing Rock Sioux Tribe used NEPA to challenge the Dakota Access Pipeline; the San Carlos Apache Tribe used it to contest a copper mine at Oak Flat. Interior's shift of consultation procedures to an internal handbook — "can be revised without formal rulemaking" — removes both the legal enforceability and the public comment opportunity that gave these challenges substance [Tribal Business News 2025].

### Pathway 3: Title VI Administrative Pathways — Dismantled in Two Stages

Title VI of the Civil Rights Act prohibits discrimination in programs receiving federal financial assistance. The EPA's OEJECR was the administrative body that processed Title VI complaints from communities alleging discriminatory environmental permitting. Two compounding events have now effectively closed this pathway:

**Stage 1 — Louisiana court ruling (August 2024)**: A federal court in the Western District of Louisiana issued a permanent injunction blocking EPA and DOJ from enforcing Title VI disparate-impact regulations in Louisiana — with explicit invitation for "copycat suits" nationally [Earthjustice 2024]. The ruling was issued in response to Louisiana's challenge to EPA's investigation of Cancer Alley permit decisions, where Denka Performance Elastomer and Formosa Plastics had been permitted near elementary schools in majority-Black communities. The court held that EPA's disparate impact standard exceeded statutory authority. This decision preceded the OEJECR closure — it meant that even before the office was shut down, the primary legal theory underlying Title VI environmental justice complaints was under judicial attack.

**Stage 2 — OEJECR closure (March 2025)**: With the office eliminated, EPA has not accepted any new Title VI complaints since January 2025. The agency has moved to reject five existing complaints while 13 remain in the queue with no processing timeline [Sentient Media 2025]. Nine active investigations are frozen. Specific cases abandoned include a North Carolina complaint challenging permits for hog and poultry farms affecting Black and Indigenous communities, and a Dothan, Alabama complaint regarding landfill expansion in a predominantly Black neighborhood — both with no EPA communication since Trump took office [Sentient Media 2025].

The combined effect is not administrative backlog. It is the structural closure of the only accessible civil rights pathway for communities that lack resources for federal litigation. As Earthjustice noted at the time of closure: "Without this office, there is no one at EPA responsible for ensuring that environmental programs do not discriminate on the basis of race, national origin, and other characteristics" [Earthjustice 2025].

### Pathway 4: Endangerment Finding Rescission as Regulatory Standing Loss

The 2009 Greenhouse Gas Endangerment Finding was more than an environmental protection — it was the legal predicate that generated federal regulatory review of emissions-increasing facilities. For EJ communities, this regulatory hook had a specific democratic function: it attached Clean Air Act review requirements to facilities whose GHG emissions were sufficient to trigger federal oversight, which in turn generated EIS processes and public comment periods.

When a proposed industrial facility would increase GHG emissions above regulatory thresholds, the Endangerment Finding meant:
1. The facility required GHG emissions review
2. That review created an Environmental Impact Statement process
3. The EIS process gave the community procedural standing and a formal comment period
4. Those comment periods created a legal record that could support litigation or negotiation

With the Finding rescinded effective April 20, 2026, that chain is broken. Facilities that would previously have triggered GHG review — and the community participation opportunities attached to it — no longer face federal scrutiny. Dr. Beverly Wright of the Deep South Center for Environmental Justice frames the consequence directly: "Without federal oversight, facilities will operate without greenhouse gas limits, compounding existing pollution from petrochemical operations concentrated in Black and low-income neighborhoods along the Mississippi River Chemical Corridor" [DSCEJ 2026].

The Harvard Salata Institute identifies the core legal vulnerability in EPA's rationale: the 2007 Supreme Court decision in *Massachusetts v. EPA* directly contradicts EPA's current position, holding that "greenhouse gases are unambiguously air pollutants under the Clean Air Act" and that EPA must apply "scientific judgment" without considering policy or cost considerations [Harvard Salata Institute 2026]. The D.C. Circuit challenges will test whether the rescission survives *Massachusetts v. EPA*'s precedent — but the process takes years, during which communities have no GHG-derived regulatory standing.

### Pathway 5: Environmental Organizing as Electoral Infrastructure — Connection Severed

The Environmental Voter Project has documented that environmental priorities are among the highest motivators for previously low-turnout voters in EJ communities — demonstrating the connection between environmental advocacy and electoral mobilization. Ben Jealous, civil rights leader and environmental advocate, has stated directly: "There is no green vote without the Black and Brown vote" [WRI 2021]. Communities facing acute pollution — particularly those contesting specific local sources — organize voter registration drives and civic engagement campaigns as direct extensions of environmental advocacy.

The EPA EJ infrastructure destruction removes this connective tissue. EPA community liaisons and EJ coordinators often served as the contact points through which community organizers identified affected residents, documented permit proceedings, and mobilized participation in both regulatory processes and electoral campaigns. The Environmental Voter Project identifies that "environmental priorities motivate previously low-turnout voters in EJ communities" — and the institutional relationships that connected environmental advocacy to electoral mobilization were built through the EPA regional offices that now no longer exist.

A 2026 peer-reviewed study (PMC 12779674) specifically investigated pathways to environmental civic engagement for diverse communities, finding that "resistant capital" — understanding systems of oppression and being motivated to challenge them — was the strongest predictor of environmental civic participation for Black, Asian, and Latine/Hispanic individuals [PMC 2026]. The study also found that "social capital" through organizations and community institutions was a key enabler of continued participation. The elimination of EPA community liaisons removes a category of organizational infrastructure that supported this social capital.

## Section 3: The Coordinated Dismantlement — Timeline and Architecture

The actions eliminating EPA's EJ infrastructure were not independent decisions. They formed a coordinated sequence executed over 15 months.

**January 20, 2025 — Executive Orders**: President Trump signed Executive Order 14173 ("Ending Illegal Discrimination and Restoring Merit-Based Opportunity") and Executive Order 14158 ("Ending Radical And Wasteful Government DEI Programs And Preferencing"), which provided the administrative rationale for OEJECR's elimination.

**February 5, 2025 — EJSCREEN removed**: EPA removed the EJSCREEN mapping tool from its public website, eliminating community access to the primary data tool used to document disproportionate pollution burdens, screen EJ grant eligibility, and support permit challenges [EDGI 2025].

**February 2025 — Administrative leave**: Approximately 171 EPA employees working on environmental justice or DEIA initiatives were placed on administrative leave — the effective start of the OEJECR shutdown before the formal order [Harvard EELP 2025a].

**March 12, 2025 — OEJECR closure order**: Administrator Zeldin ordered closure of OEJECR and all 10 regional EPA EJ offices. EPA's press release framed the action as "terminat[ing] Biden's Environmental Justice, DEI Arms of Agency" [EPA 2025]. Public Employees for Environmental Responsibility filed a complaint with the U.S. Office of Special Counsel seeking investigation and reinstatement.

**April–May 2025 — Employee terminations**: EPA announced plans to terminate nearly 300 employees from its EJ and civil rights programs [Inside Climate News 2025]. The IRA-funded EJ grants program — $3 billion allocated by Congress specifically to help disadvantaged communities — saw 781 grants targeted for termination [Eos 2025].

**August 2024 [precursor] — Louisiana Title VI ruling**: The Western District of Louisiana issued a permanent injunction blocking EPA from enforcing disparate impact regulations statewide — a ruling that functionally preempted the administrative Title VI pathway before OEJECR was closed [Earthjustice 2024]. The court's explicit invitation for copycat suits nationalized the effect.

**January 8, 2026 — NEPA regulations rescinded**: CEQ issued Federal Register rule 2026-00178, removing all NEPA implementing regulations effective immediately. The White House described this as "fix[ing] decades-long permitting failure through deregulation" [White House 2026]. Environmental law practitioners identified the loss of EJ-specific requirements: climate change and environmental justice were no longer required considerations; early community consultation was no longer required; Indigenous knowledge was no longer required in environmental reviews [Harvard EELP 2026a; Orrick 2026].

**February 12 / April 20, 2026 — Endangerment Finding rescinded**: EPA finalized rescission of the 2009 GHG Endangerment Finding (Federal Register 2026-03157), effective April 20. EPA simultaneously repealed all GHG emission standards for light-, medium-, and heavy-duty vehicles — eliminating the largest single application of the Finding's regulatory authority.

The architecture of this sequence is significant: it began with data removal (EJSCREEN), continued with personnel elimination (OEJECR), then procedural rights removal (NEPA), and concluded with the foundational scientific-legal authority elimination (Endangerment Finding). Each step removed a layer of the participation infrastructure, with each successive step made easier by the preceding one.

## Section 4: Live Litigation — D.C. Circuit and State-Level Challenges

### 4.1 D.C. Circuit: Endangerment Finding Challenges

Four petitions for review were filed in the D.C. Circuit challenging the Endangerment Finding rescission:

- **American Public Health Ass'n v. U.S. EPA** (No. 26-1037): Filed February 18, 2026 by Earthjustice representing 15 nonprofits and Alaskan tribes. The named defendant is EPA Administrator Lee Zeldin. This is the primary nonprofit/health organization challenge. Earthjustice previously defended the Endangerment Finding against industry challenges in *Coalition for Responsible Regulation v. EPA* (2012) [Earthjustice 2026].

- **Venner v. U.S. EPA** (No. 26-1038): Filed by Our Children's Trust on behalf of 18 youth from Alaska, California, Colorado, Hawaii, Montana, New York, Pennsylvania, Tennessee, Washington, and Wisconsin — raising both Clean Air Act and constitutional claims [Our Children's Trust 2026].

- **Zero Emission Transportation Ass'n v. EPA** (No. 26-1039): Filed February 20, 2026.

- **Massachusetts v. U.S. EPA** (No. 26-1061): Filed March 19, 2026 by 24 states plus 12 counties and cities. Led by AGs from California, Connecticut, New York, and Massachusetts, with Arizona, Colorado, Delaware, Hawaii, Illinois, Maine, Maryland, Michigan, Minnesota, Nevada, New Jersey, New Mexico, North Carolina, Oregon, Rhode Island, Vermont, Virginia, Washington, and Wisconsin, plus the District of Columbia, U.S. Virgin Islands, and Governor Shapiro of Pennsylvania. The city parties include Denver, Albuquerque, Boston, Chicago, Cleveland, Columbus, Los Angeles, New York, and Harris County, Texas [Smart Cities Dive 2026].

In April 2026, petitioners including environmental organizations and state AGs filed a petition for reconsideration, arguing the final rule was not a "logical outgrowth" of the proposed rule — a procedural argument that could void the rescission independently of its merits [Harvard EELP 2026b].

**On the defense side**: A group of 25 states led by West Virginia and Kentucky filed a motion to intervene to defend the rescission, framing it as a return to appropriate regulatory boundaries [Harvard EELP 2026b].

**Key legal question**: The Harvard Salata Institute and Georgetown Environmental Law Review both identify *Massachusetts v. EPA* (2007, SCOTUS) as the primary obstacle to the rescission's survival. That decision held greenhouse gases are "unambiguously air pollutants" under the Clean Air Act and required EPA to exercise scientific judgment in the endangerment analysis without policy or cost considerations. EPA's current rationale — that GHGs are not air pollutants because they lack local or regional effects — directly contradicts *Massachusetts v. EPA*. Whether the D.C. Circuit applies this precedent or defers to the agency under *Skidmore* or *Loper Bright* frameworks will determine the cases' outcome [Harvard Salata Institute 2026; Georgetown Environmental Law Review 2026].

**The EJ participation angle**: The Alaskan tribes in *APHA v. EPA* provide the most direct EJ community standing argument: these are communities experiencing immediate, documented harm from climate change — permafrost collapse, coastal erosion, inability to practice subsistence livelihoods — that cannot be separated from the GHG emissions the Endangerment Finding regulated. Their inclusion in the case is not symbolic.

### 4.2 Grant Termination Litigation

Separately, nonprofits, tribes, and local governments sued the Trump administration over termination of EPA grant programs. A federal court granted plaintiffs' motion for summary judgment and set aside EPA's terminations in at least three cases [Earthjustice 2025b]. The New York Attorney General took action to block termination of the Environmental Justice Block Grant Program [NY AG 2025]. California AG Bonta filed a challenge to EPA's termination of IRA EJ grants as unlawful [California AG 2025].

These cases are distinct from the Endangerment Finding litigation but reinforce the same argument: Congress appropriated $3 billion for EJ communities specifically, and the executive branch cannot unilaterally impound those funds.

### 4.3 State-Level Responses

Thirteen state AGs issued joint guidance in June 2025 affirming the importance and legality of state environmental justice initiatives — asserting that state EJ programs operate on independent legal authority and are not preempted by the federal rollback [Harvard EELP 2025c]. Eight states now have cumulative impact bills in place: California, Connecticut, Georgia, Maryland, Minnesota, New Jersey, New York, and North Carolina [National Law Review 2025].

New York's law — enacted under S8830 — is the strongest: it prohibits issuance of applicable permits for new projects that would cause or contribute more than a de minimis amount of pollution to a disadvantaged community already bearing disproportionate pollution burdens [Columbia Sabin Center 2022]. Connecticut's 2023 update (PA 23-202) directed cumulative impact assessments for EJ communities. Maryland Governor Moore's 2025 VOICE Order created an interagency EJ council and requires use of Maryland's EJScreen equivalent (MDEnviroScreen) in permit and investment decisions [National Law Review 2025].

The state-level developments are significant but geographically uneven: they provide meaningful protection for EJ communities in California, New York, Connecticut, Maryland, New Jersey, Minnesota, and North Carolina. They do not provide equivalent protection for communities in Louisiana, Texas, Mississippi, Alabama, Georgia, West Virginia, or the industrial Midwest — precisely the regions where the EPA EJ infrastructure was most actively used and where its elimination falls hardest.

## Section 5: Three Community Case Studies — What Participation Rights Looked Like Before They Were Removed

### 5.1 WE ACT for Environmental Justice — Northern Manhattan and the South Bronx

WE ACT for Environmental Justice has operated in Northern Manhattan and the South Bronx since 1988, representing communities with some of the highest asthma rates in the country. Their most documented case: a 2000 Title VI complaint filed against the Metropolitan Transportation Authority charging that the MTA had sited six of its then-eight diesel bus depots in Harlem and Washington Heights, exposing majority-minority districts to disproportionate diesel exhaust [WE ACT 2000].

The complaint exemplifies how the administrative Title VI pathway functioned as a civic participation tool. WE ACT used it after years of failed negotiation — it was not their first resort but their formal legal channel when informal process failed. The Federal Transit Administration's 2004 review found the MTA had failed to comply with required federal environmental impact analysis in constructing and rehabilitating bus depots — a partial vindication that also documented how federal EJ review created accountability for transit agencies [WE ACT 2000; NEPA Alliance].

Under the current framework: the Title VI disparate impact standard that WE ACT's complaint relied on has been permanently enjoined in Louisiana and challenged nationally. The OEJECR that processed such complaints is closed. EJSCREEN that WE ACT would use to document the disproportionate burden is removed from EPA's website. New York's state EJ law partially compensates for the federal void, but only for state-regulated facilities — federal transit authority siting decisions remain federal matters.

### 5.2 Deep South Center for Environmental Justice — Cancer Alley

The Deep South Center for Environmental Justice has documented the Mississippi River Chemical Corridor — "Cancer Alley" — where petrochemical facilities are concentrated in majority-Black communities between Baton Rouge and New Orleans. In St. John the Baptist Parish, children attend elementary school adjacent to the Denka Performance Elastomer facility emitting chloroprene at 8,000 times EPA's acceptable threshold. In St. James Parish, over 80% of industrial plants locate in the majority-Black districts, and a proposed $9.4 billion Formosa petrochemical complex would sit approximately one mile from a majority-Black elementary school [PRRAC 2024].

DSCEJ's advocacy work relied on EPA EJ infrastructure at multiple levels: EJSCREEN data to document the disproportionate burden; EPA EJ coordinators for technical assistance in permit proceedings; Title VI complaint mechanisms to challenge discriminatory permitting; and the IRA EJ Block Grants — DSCEJ received a $13 million grant from EPA for capacity-building work in Gulf Coast communities, and EPA announced a $2.4 million Gulf Coast Grantmaking Project grant in October 2024 [EPA 2024]. Both of these grants are now subject to termination.

DSCEJ's response to the Endangerment Finding rescission: "Without federal oversight, facilities will operate without greenhouse gas limits, compounding existing pollution from petrochemical operations concentrated in Black and low-income neighborhoods along the Mississippi River Chemical Corridor. The administration has chosen to prioritize industry over evidence and legal precedent" [DSCEJ 2026]. This is the regulatory gap that creates direct democratic harm: without EPA oversight, and with Title VI enforcement enjoined, and with NEPA community participation eliminated, Cancer Alley communities have no federal administrative forum in which their health interests are legally required to be considered.

### 5.3 Indigenous Environmental Network — Tribal Land Pipeline Permitting

The CEQ NEPA regulations created a specific protection for tribal nations: a requirement that federal agencies consult with tribal governments before approving projects on or near tribal lands. This requirement is why the Standing Rock Sioux Tribe was able to mount a sustained legal and advocacy challenge to the Dakota Access Pipeline — NEPA created the procedural hook for the challenge, generating both legal standing and a public comment record that became the evidentiary foundation for ongoing litigation [Harvard EELP DAPL tracker].

The Army Corps of Engineers released the final Dakota Access Pipeline environmental impact statement as recently as December 19, 2025, and the Standing Rock Sioux Tribe's appeal of the D.D.C.'s March 28, 2025 dismissal remains ongoing — demonstrating that NEPA-based participation rights in pipeline disputes are not historical artifacts but live legal tools [Harvard EELP DAPL tracker].

The January 8, 2026 CEQ rescission moved tribal consultation from binding regulation to Interior Department internal handbook — "can be revised without formal rulemaking" [Tribal Business News 2025]. The practical consequence: tribal nations lose the procedural predictability that NEPA's binding regulations provided. When a regulation is in place, its violation creates a legal cause of action. When a procedure is internal guidance, its violation is a discretionary administrative matter. For the 228-organization coalition that opposed the NEPA changes — including tribal nations — this is the core grievance: the rules that gave communities formal standing to participate in decisions about their lands have been converted to suggestions [Tribal Business News 2025].

## Section 6: The International Standard the U.S. Is Abandoning — The Aarhus Convention

The Convention on Access to Information, Public Participation in Decision-making, and Access to Justice in Environmental Matters (the "Aarhus Convention") was adopted by the United Nations Economic Commission for Europe in 1998 and entered into force in 2001 [UNECE 2001]. It now has 49 parties — 48 states and the European Union. The United States signed but has never ratified the Convention [UN Treaty Status 2023].

The Aarhus Convention establishes three pillars of environmental democracy:
1. **Access to information**: The public's right to receive environmental information held by public authorities
2. **Public participation**: The public's right to participate in environmental decision-making before final decisions are taken
3. **Access to justice**: The public's right to challenge decisions through a court or independent body

These three pillars are precisely what EPA's infrastructure elimination dismantled: EJSCREEN's removal reduced access to information; NEPA's rescission eliminated structured public participation; OEJECR's closure and the Louisiana Title VI ruling reduced access to administrative justice.

EU member states are legally bound by Aarhus through EU law [EUR-Lex 2005]. Communities in equivalent industrial situations in Germany, France, the Netherlands, or Poland have enforceable rights — before agencies reach final decisions — to information, participation, and judicial review. Communities in Louisiana's Cancer Alley or West Virginia's coal country have no equivalent federal procedural protections after April 2026.

The US failure to ratify Aarhus has always represented a democratic deficit. The 2025–2026 actions made that deficit acute: the US not only refused to adopt international environmental democracy standards, it actively dismantled the domestic approximations it had built over thirty years. This is the reform argument: Aarhus ratification as a long-term statutory commitment to environmental democracy that cannot be eliminated by a single administrator's order.

## Section 7: Reform Architecture — What Restoration Requires

The destruction of EPA's EJ infrastructure was accomplished primarily through executive action — which means it was accomplished without congressional authorization and can theoretically be reversed by a subsequent administration. But executive reversal is insufficient protection, because what was undone by executive order can be undone again. The reform architecture this domain advocates requires statutory entrenchment.

### Tier 1: Immediate Executive Actions (Next Administration)

- Reinstate OEJECR and regional EJ offices with direct-hire authority to rebuild staff
- Restore EJSCREEN and CEQ's Climate and Economic Justice Screening Tool (CEJST), both removed in February 2025
- Restore the IRA EJ Block Grant program and rescind termination notices on 781 grants
- Direct EPA to resume Title VI complaint intake and processing
- Issue executive order restoring climate and EJ consideration requirements in NEPA reviews

### Tier 2: Legislative Statutory Anchors

- **EPA EJ Offices — statutory authorization**: Congress should authorize OEJECR by statute with defined functions, staffing floors, and reporting requirements that cannot be eliminated by executive order
- **NEPA statutory amendments**: Restore Environmental Impact Statement procedural requirements — including EJ and climate considerations — in statute, independent of CEQ regulatory authority. The current CEQ regulations rescission worked because NEPA's statutory text does not specify EIS content requirements in detail; statutory amendments would correct that gap
- **IRA EJ Block Grant entrenchment**: Clarify in statute that appropriated EJ funds cannot be impounded and establish direct grant disbursement mechanisms that reduce executive discretion
- **Title VI disparate impact regulations**: Codify EPA's disparate impact regulations in statute to insulate them from administrative rescission and the Louisiana court challenge

### Tier 3: Long-Term International Framework

- **Aarhus Convention ratification**: Submit the Convention to the Senate for ratification, establishing binding international law obligations for US environmental participation rights that survive administration changes
- **Regional EJ compacts**: Enable interstate compacts (California, New York, Illinois as anchors) to establish regional EJ participation standards equivalent to Aarhus for communities in those states

### State Floors (Immediate)

Eight states have cumulative impact bills; the remaining 42 do not. The WE ACT–Sabin Center model environmental justice legislation [Columbia Sabin Center model bills] provides a template for state-level adoption. For EJ communities in the unprotected states — Gulf Coast, Appalachia, industrial Midwest — federal restoration is the only adequate remedy; state floors are a supplement, not a substitute.

## Section 8: Cross-Domain Integration

**Domain 15 (Environmental Rollbacks Tracker)**: Domain 15 catalogs the specific regulatory actions. Domain 49 provides the democratic participation analysis — the same actions from the civic exclusion angle rather than the environmental policy angle. These documents are complementary, not redundant.

**Domain 22 (Racial Justice)**: Environmental racism is the primary mechanism through which pollution concentrates in communities of color. Domain 22's racial equity framework explains the structural history; Domain 49's participation rights frame explains the current civic consequence. The cancer alley data (7-21x pollution levels) and the Black neighborhood industrial siting data (79% more likely) connect both analyses.

**Domain 47 (Housing Security)**: Industrial facility siting near low-income neighborhoods drives residential displacement; displacement concentrates vulnerable populations into environmentally burdened areas. The feedback loop between housing instability and environmental burden compounds civic exclusion: displaced residents lose local voting eligibility, community ties, and the institutional relationships that supported advocacy.

**Domain 57 (Multilateral Withdrawal)**: US withdrawal from international environmental agreements eliminates the international benchmarking data that EJ advocates use to document US policy failures relative to democratic peers. The Aarhus comparison works alongside Domain 57's multilateral withdrawal analysis: the US is not only withdrawing from international environmental commitments, it is simultaneously dismantling domestic equivalents of the participation rights those commitments establish.

**Domain 58 (Tribal Sovereignty)**: Domain 58 covers tribal sovereignty and federal trust responsibility. Domain 49 overlaps in the NEPA consultation context: tribal nations' procedural standing in pipeline and mining permit decisions depended on CEQ's binding consultation regulations. The January 8, 2026 rescission affects both domains' core arguments.

**Domain 31 (OBBBA / Medicaid)**: Medicaid cuts disproportionately affecting low-income and minority communities compound the health burden that suppresses civic participation. Domain 31's healthcare access analysis and Domain 49's health-burden-to-civic-suppression pathway are directly connected: communities that lose Medicaid coverage for environmental illness (asthma, lead poisoning, cardiovascular disease attributable to air quality) face compounded civic capacity reduction.

## Section 9: Advocacy Windows and Recommended Actions

**D.C. Circuit — Active through 2026 and beyond**: Organizations receiving this document can support amicus filings in *APHA v. EPA* (No. 26-1037) and *Massachusetts v. EPA* (No. 26-1061). The EJ standing argument — that Alaskan tribes and cancer alley communities are among those with the most direct and immediate interest in GHG regulation — strengthens the petitioners' case. Organizations with documented community impacts should connect with Earthjustice and state AG offices coordinating the challenge.

**Grant termination litigation — Active**: The summary judgment victories in EPA grant termination cases (court set aside EPA terminations) demonstrate that judicial review of these terminations is viable. Community organizations that received IRA EJ grant awards should document their specific losses and connect with Earthjustice and state AG offices pursuing these cases [Earthjustice 2025b].

**State legislative sessions (2026)**: Eight states have cumulative impact bills; 42 do not. The pre-midterm legislative session period through June–July 2026 is the advocacy window for state-level EJ statute campaigns, prioritizing states with favorable legislative composition in the industrial Midwest and South.

**Pre-midterm voter mobilization (August–November 2026)**: The connection between environmental burden, civic disengagement, and electoral mobilization documented by the Environmental Voter Project and UCS is directly actionable. Organizations conducting voter registration in EJ communities should use the democratic design argument: the communities being stripped of federal EJ protection are the same communities the administrative framework has historically depressed civic participation in. Connecting environmental advocacy to electoral mobilization is not a messaging strategy; it is the mechanism through which EJ communities regain the political power to contest environmental harm.

**EJSCREEN data access**: Harvard and Public Environmental Data Partners have reconstructed EJSCREEN versions after EPA removed the original [EDGI 2025]. Advocates should use these mirrors for permit challenge documentation and make clear in advocacy materials that the need for third-party data reconstruction is itself evidence of the infrastructure destruction this domain documents.
