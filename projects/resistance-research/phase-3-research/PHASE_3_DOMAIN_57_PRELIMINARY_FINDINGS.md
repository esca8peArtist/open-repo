## Finding 1: The Constitutional Asymmetry Is the Central Design Failure

**Confidence: 95%**

The US Constitution requires two-thirds Senate consent to ratify treaties (Article II, Section 2) but is completely silent on treaty withdrawal. This asymmetry is not an oversight — the Framers debated foreign affairs power extensively — but the historical record does not resolve whether the silence reflects a judgment that withdrawal requires the same supermajority or a judgment that withdrawal is an executive prerogative.

The practical consequence is a structure in which the most democratically demanding process in American governance (two-thirds supermajority in the Senate) creates binding international commitments, while the least democratically demanding mechanism (a single presidential executive order) destroys them. The entire history of US treaty-making assumes that Senate ratification creates durable, reversible-only-by-equivalent-democratic-process commitments. The executive-withdrawal practice demolishes this assumption.

The one concrete legislative fix — NDAA Section 1250A (2024), restricting presidential NATO withdrawal to Senate consent or an act of Congress — establishes that Congress has the constitutional authority to impose this requirement when it chooses to act. The significance of NDAA Section 1250A is not merely NATO-specific: it is the proof-of-concept that the asymmetry is a statutory problem with a statutory solution, not a constitutional one requiring amendment.

**What Phase 3 research must add**: The Koh mirror principle provides the constitutional theory behind the statutory fix. Research must establish whether any court has applied the mirror principle in a live case, and whether the *Zivotofsky* narrowing of the political question doctrine creates a realistic litigation pathway if the Trump administration defies NDAA Section 1250A's constraint through de facto NATO obligation reduction short of formal withdrawal.

## Finding 2: The ICC Sanctions Are a Domestic First Amendment Problem, Not Only an International Law Problem

**Confidence: 90%** (preliminary injunction establishes the legal theory; merits outcome remains open)

Executive Order 14203 (February 2025) imposed civil and criminal penalties on US persons providing "material support or services" to ICC investigations, designated individuals, and organizations associated with ICC proceedings. The maximum criminal penalty is 20 years federal imprisonment. This is a direct prohibition on speech-based services — legal advocacy, expert testimony, academic research, journalistic reporting — connected to ICC proceedings.

*Smith v. Trump* (ACLU litigation, D. Maine, Case 1:25-cv-00158) established the First Amendment theory: EO 14203 "likely violates" the First Amendment rights of Matthew Smith and Akila Radhakrishnan, who were forced to suspend work with the ICC Office of the Prosecutor. Judge Torresen's preliminary injunction (July 18, 2025) enjoined civil or criminal penalties on the plaintiffs "based on the Plaintiffs' provision of speech-based services to the ICC."

The strategic significance of this framing is substantial: it converts the ICC sanctions from an abstract international law concern (US should engage with the ICC) into a concrete domestic civil liberties claim (the US government is prohibiting American citizens from speaking). This is the frame that resonates with domestic civil society audiences who are not primarily focused on international accountability.

**What Phase 3 research must add**: Current status of *Smith v. Trump* post-preliminary injunction. Whether the government has appealed to the First Circuit. Whether the injunction's scope covers organizations beyond the two named plaintiffs (if not, systematic relief requires legislative action — the ICC Sanctions Repeal bill). Whether HRW, Amnesty, and CICC are using the *Smith v. Trump* frame in current advocacy or whether they have reverted to the international law frame.

**Confidence gap**: 75% on the Knight First Amendment Institute finding that the government "conceded that US researchers may engage with sanctioned UN officials." The scope of this concession — whether it extends beyond researchers to advocacy organizations — requires primary verification from the relevant OFAC guidance or DOJ position statement.

## Finding 3: Hungary's Reversal Is the Strongest Available Proof-of-Concept for Reversibility

**Confidence: 95%**

The Hungarian parliament voted 133-37 (with 5 abstentions) on May 27, 2026 to repeal the Orbán-era ICC withdrawal legislation, before the June 2, 2026 Rome Statute exit date. Hungary remains a party to the Rome Statute without interruption. Prime Minister-elect Péter Magyar has stated that Hungary will enforce ICC arrest warrants, including for Netanyahu, if a warranted person enters Hungarian territory.

This reversal establishes three empirical propositions that Phase 3 should build on:

**Proposition A**: The one-year waiting period in Article 127 of the Rome Statute (from formal notification to exit) is a genuine window for democratic intervention, not merely a procedural formality. Hungary announced withdrawal in April 2025 and reversed it by May 27, 2026 — before the formal exit took legal effect. Democratic electoral change (Tisza Party's April 12 landslide) produced treaty reversal within the one-year window.

**Proposition B**: Electoral accountability can reverse institutional withdrawal when the withdrawal was politically motivated and the new government has a legislative majority sufficient to repeal the withdrawal legislation. Magyar had 133 of 199 parliamentary seats — well above the simple majority needed for legislative repeal. The democratic accountability mechanism functioned precisely as institutional designers intended.

**Proposition C**: ICC reversal is internationally welcomed and diplomatically rewarded. The ICC's governing body formally welcomed Hungary's decision. EU partners signaled improved relations. Netanyahu's planned visit complications (now requiring Hungary to enforce ICC arrest warrant) demonstrate that ICC membership has concrete diplomatic consequences — reversing withdrawal is not merely symbolic.

**What Phase 3 research must add**: The legal mechanics of the reversal. The ASIL Insight (May 27, 2026) is the most authoritative analysis available. Research must confirm: was the Rome Statute notification to the UN Secretary-General formally withdrawn, or did the domestic legislative repeal operate to nullify the notification's domestic legal force while the international notification remains outstanding? This distinction matters for the "reversibility" claim — a notification formally withdrawn before the exit date is a stronger clean reversal than a domestic repeal that may leave the international notification formally outstanding.

## Finding 4: The Scale of the January 2026 Withdrawal Is Historically Unprecedented

**Confidence: 90%** (pending confirmation from the Brill Global Governance 945-withdrawal dataset)

Institut Montaigne, Just Security, and multiple analytical sources characterize the January 7, 2026 withdrawal from 66 international organizations as "the most extensive U.S. disengagement from multilateral governance since 1945." The Brill Global Governance Vol. 31(2) 2025 dataset of 945 unilateral withdrawals from 206 treaties (1945–2024) is the peer-reviewed empirical source that would establish whether this characterization holds quantitatively.

The January 2026 action is qualitatively unprecedented in several dimensions even before the quantitative dataset is consulted:

- **Simultaneity**: 66 organizations exited in a single executive memorandum. No prior US withdrawal action has approached this scale.
- **Breadth of institutional categories**: Criminal accountability (ICC via sanctions), human rights monitoring, electoral integrity (International IDEA, UN Democracy Fund), climate science, development, labor standards, and digital governance — all five functional categories simultaneously.
- **Democratic governance-specific targeting**: The withdrawal from International IDEA and the UN Democracy Fund is uniquely self-undermining for a government claiming democratic legitimacy — both institutions were created specifically to monitor and support democratic electoral systems. No authoritarian government has exited these institutions without acknowledging the democratic accountability implications; the Trump administration exited them as a routine foreign assistance cost-cutting measure.

**What Phase 3 research must add**: Confirm the Brill dataset finding on simultaneity. Establish whether any other state has exited more than 10 multilateral organizations in a single action. The quantitative anchor is essential for the "historically unprecedented" claim in the Phase 3 executive summary.

## Finding 5: The Subnational Resistance Architecture Is Underbuilt and Underanalyzed

**Confidence: 75%** (framework established; specific actions post-January 2026 require primary research)

The Just Security analysis "Washington's Multilateral Retreat Creates an Opening for State and Local Leaders" (2025) establishes the theoretical and historical framework: after the 2017 Paris Agreement withdrawal, 400+ US cities and counties adopted ambitious climate action goals; subnational actors joined international compacts directly. The Truman Center City and State Diplomacy Task Force documented the governance framework for subnational international engagement.

The preliminary finding is that the subnational resistance architecture is present but not yet systematically organized around the January 2026 withdrawal scope. Climate subnational diplomacy (America's Pledge, US Climate Alliance) has a decade of institutional infrastructure; human rights and democratic accountability subnational diplomacy does not.

The specific opportunity Phase 3 identifies: state attorneys general have existing institutional authority to file amicus briefs in federal constitutional litigation, to join multi-state coalitions challenging executive authority, and to use state procurement and investment authority to create financial pressure on entities that comply with multilateral withdrawal. Several state AGs (California, New York, Massachusetts, Illinois) have been active on treaty-adjacent issues (IEEPA tariffs, executive emergency powers). Whether any have specifically addressed the multilateral withdrawal executive memorandum of January 7, 2026 requires primary research.

**What Phase 3 research must add**: A targeted search for state AG formal positions, amicus filings, or coalition letters addressing the January 7, 2026 executive memorandum specifically. The Lawfare database, National Association of Attorneys General, and individual state AG websites are the primary search locations.

## Finding 6: The Movement Coalition Is Siloed and Needs a Bridging Frame

**Confidence: 90%**

The current advocacy landscape on multilateral withdrawal is divided across organizational mandates that reflect the functional categories of withdrawal rather than a unified democratic accountability frame:

- **ICC/international justice community** (CICC, HRW, Amnesty): Focused on ICC sanctions and criminal accountability. Primary frame is international law and victim rights.
- **Climate community** (America's Pledge, US Climate Alliance): Focused on UNFCCC/Paris Agreement exit. Primary frame is environmental science and economic competitiveness.
- **Democracy advocacy community** (National Democratic Institute, International Republican Institute, Freedom House): Focused on International IDEA and UN Democracy Fund withdrawal. Primary frame is global democratic backsliding.
- **Trade/labor community** (AFL-CIO, Public Citizen): Focused on WTO, ILO. Primary frame is worker rights and fair trade.

No organization is currently running a unified campaign that bridges these communities around the central argument Phase 3 makes: that international accountability institutions are domestic democratic infrastructure, and that withdrawal from them destroys accountability mechanisms that domestic advocates depend on in all of these domains simultaneously.

The Phase 3 intellectual contribution is precisely this bridge: the argument that IPCC withdrawal is an ICC sanctions problem is an International IDEA withdrawal problem, all viewed as attacks on the same accountability architecture. The research must verify that no existing advocacy document has made this argument explicitly — which would either confirm Phase 3's novelty claim or identify a predecessor that Phase 3 should cite and deepen.

**What Phase 3 research must add**: A systematic search for existing unified-frame advocacy documents on multilateral withdrawal. The Stimson Center "United Nations Was Built to Deliver Accountability" (2026) and the Just Security "United States in Retreat" piece come closest; confirm whether either makes the domestic democratic accountability bridge argument explicitly.

## Finding 7: Institutional Response to Withdrawal Is Moving Toward the "Forceful" Type

**Confidence: 75%** (based on ISQ typology; requires full article reading to confirm classification)

The ISQ 2025 article ("Responding to Unilateral Challenges to International Institutions") identifies three institutional response types: accommodation (compromise with the challenging state), inaction (do nothing), and forceful response. Based on publicly available information:

- **ICC response to US sanctions**: Closer to the forceful type. The ICC Prosecutor's Office has continued issuing arrest warrants and conducting investigations despite sanctions. The "Safeguard Operations" strategic goal in the 2026–2029 plan specifically addresses continuity under political threat. The ICC's governing body (Assembly of States Parties) has not accommodated the US sanctions in any formal institutional modification.

- **ICC response to Hungary withdrawal**: Successfully prevented through the Article 127 one-year waiting period mechanism — which functioned as the ISQ's "forceful but procedurally contained" response. The ICC did not accommodate Hungary's withdrawal request; it maintained full membership obligations during the waiting period, which allowed the democratic electoral reversal to function.

- **Council of Europe response to Turkey's non-compliance**: The ECHR has initiated infringement proceedings against Turkey — only the second time in the institution's history — which is the clearest available example of the "forceful response" type in a high-stakes defiance context.

- **ECHR response to Russia**: Russia was expelled (formally: Russia initiated pre-emptive exit, but the Committee of Ministers voted for expulsion). This is an ambiguous case between the forceful and inaction types — the institution did not accommodate Russia, but the practical enforcement consequence (Russia's exit) is the same whether the institution expelled or Russia exited.

**What Phase 3 research must add**: Full reading of the ISQ article to confirm whether its typology and the Phase 3 classification match. Specifically: does the ISQ article address the ICC specifically, or does Phase 3 need to apply its typology to the ICC case independently?

## Finding 8: The WTO Question Is a Structural Accountability Gap Distinct from the ICC

**Confidence: 85%**

The WTO is a congressional-executive agreement (the Uruguay Round Agreements Act, 1994) rather than a Senate-ratified treaty. This distinction matters for Phase 3's constitutional analysis:

- Senate-ratified treaties: Strongest protection argument (Koh's mirror principle applies directly)
- Congressional-executive agreements: Protected by the implementing statute (URAA) and its Section 125 five-year review mechanism; Congress would need to vote to withdraw
- Pure executive agreements: Unilaterally exitable by the president without any congressional action requirement

The WTO's status as a congressional-executive agreement means that a president who wishes to exit the WTO must navigate Section 125 of the URAA — either wait for the five-year review cycle and allow Congress to vote, or argue that the president has unilateral authority to terminate a congressional-executive agreement. No legal authority supports the latter argument in a context where Congress has specifically established a withdrawal procedure.

The 2025 WTO five-year review cycle (if a resolution was introduced) represents either a legislative leverage window (Congress could pass a resolution opposing withdrawal) or a threat (a resolution supporting withdrawal introduced by the withdrawal-facilitation caucus). Research must confirm which resolution, if any, was introduced.

**What Phase 3 research must add**: Confirmation from Congress.gov of whether any URAA Section 125 resolution was introduced in the 119th Congress and its committee status.

## Phase 3 Original Argument Summary

The following three claims constitute Phase 3's original analytical contribution beyond Phase 2:

**Claim A — Non-Delegation by Exit**: When the president exits a Senate-ratified treaty, he is exercising authority that the constitutional treaty clause deliberately placed with the Senate as co-sovereign. This is a non-delegation violation: the Senate cannot be the "silent partner" in ratification and then excluded from termination. Koh's mirror principle provides the constitutional theory; NDAA Section 1250A provides the legislative model; *Zivotofsky* provides the justiciability theory if the president defies the statute.

*Confidence this argument can be made rigorously*: 85%. Requires full reading of Koh, Galbraith, and the relevant CRS reports. The argument has not been made in exactly this form in published legal scholarship, making it a Phase 3 original contribution.

**Claim B — First Amendment Domestic Hook**: EO 14203 ICC sanctions are a domestic First Amendment violation currently enjoined in federal court (*Smith v. Trump*, D. Maine). This converts the ICC advocacy frame from international law to domestic civil liberties, dramatically widening the potential coalition. American civil liberties organizations (ACLU, Knight First Amendment Institute) are already litigating the case; the Phase 3 contribution is integrating this litigation into the multilateral withdrawal democratic accountability frame.

*Confidence this argument can be made rigorously*: 90%. *Smith v. Trump* preliminary injunction is a confirmed primary source. The argument's strength depends on the First Circuit's treatment of the appeal, if any.

**Claim C — Subnational Resistance Architecture**: Federal multilateral retreat creates both a vacuum (accountability architecture is removed) and an opportunity (subnational actors can fill some vacuum functions). State AGs have existing legal authority to challenge executive actions that violate treaty obligations with domestic legal consequences. City and state governments can maintain international compacts and organization memberships independently of federal withdrawal. The democratic accountability argument implies that subnational governments should be targeted as both advocacy audiences and operational partners.

*Confidence this argument can be made rigorously*: 75%. The framework exists (Truman Center, America's Pledge precedents); the specific post-January 2026 state AG and city government actions require primary research to document.
