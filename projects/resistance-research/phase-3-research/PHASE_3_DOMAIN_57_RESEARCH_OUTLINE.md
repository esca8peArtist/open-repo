## Core Claim (Phase 3 Formulation)

**The Phase 3 thesis extends the Phase 2 reframe**: Multilateral withdrawal is not merely democratic dismantlement — it is a structural attack on the constitutional architecture of shared foreign affairs power. The Constitution's treaty clause (Article II, Section 2) vests treaty-making authority jointly in the President and the Senate through advice and consent. When the President withdraws unilaterally from Senate-ratified treaties, he exercises authority the Constitution did not give him alone. This is not primarily a foreign policy question. It is a domestic constitutional design question: who has the power to un-make international commitments that required supermajority democratic legitimacy to create?

The Phase 3 argument adds three claims Phase 2 did not fully make:

**Claim A (Non-delegation by exit)**: The Senate's treaty ratification function is not merely a procedural formality — it is a substantive non-delegation of foreign affairs authority to the President. When the President exits a ratified treaty without Senate consent, he reclaims authority that the constitutional structure deliberately split. This is structurally analogous to the non-delegation violation the Court identified in *Schechter Poultry* — an executive arrogation of authority not granted — adapted to the foreign affairs context where *Curtiss-Wright* has historically insulated executive action.

**Claim B (First Amendment domestic hook)**: The ICC sanctions executive order (EO 14203, February 2025) is not only an international accountability question — it is a domestic First Amendment violation against US citizens engaged in advocacy, research, and legal representation connected to ICC proceedings. *Smith v. Trump* (D. Maine, preliminary injunction July 18, 2025) held that EO 14203 "likely violated" the plaintiffs' First Amendment rights. This litigation is the domestic constitutional hook for a domain that otherwise risks being perceived as a foreign policy concern rather than a civil liberties one.

**Claim C (Subnational resistance architecture)**: When federal executive multilateralism collapses, subnational governments — state AGs, city governments, regional diplomatic networks — become the structural backstop. The Just Security analysis of Washington's multilateral retreat (2025) documents that governors, mayors, and state attorneys general are actively filling multilateral institutional vacuums in climate, trade, and human rights domains. This subnational resistance architecture is underanalyzed in the Phase 2 document and represents a novel Phase 3 contribution.

## Zone 1: Legislative Inventory

### Research Questions

**Q1.** What bills in the 119th Congress (2025–2026) address presidential treaty withdrawal authority, and what is the constitutional theory underlying each?

The following bills require full text and committee status analysis:
- **S.2174 / H.R.6508** — NATO Act (Lee/Massie): Requires president to give withdrawal notice from NATO. This is the libertarian version — mandating withdrawal, not constraining it. Establishes that Congress can regulate the treaty relationship procedurally in either direction.
- **H.R.2010** — NATO Edge Act (Panetta): Prevents president from unilaterally withdrawing from NATO. The affirmative constraint model following NDAA Section 1250A.
- **S.669** — DEFUND Act (119th Congress): UN withdrawal facilitation bill. The anti-multilateral legislative vehicle. Important to map as the adversary legislative architecture.
- Any Treaty Withdrawal Notification Act introduced or anticipated: CRS R48868 identifies the concept but notes no general treaty withdrawal notification statute exists as of February 2026.

**Q2.** Does the NDAA Section 1250A (2024) NATO protection hold under the Youngstown framework if the Trump administration defies it?

The CRS R48868 analysis (February 27, 2026) suggests defiance would trigger the "lowest ebb" of presidential power under *Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co. v. Sawyer* (1952), and that post-*Zivotofsky* courts are less likely to invoke the political question doctrine to avoid reviewing presidential action contrary to a statute. The research question is whether any litigation has been threatened or initiated based on the executive's troop-reduction program (confirmed as a "multi-year ongoing process" by SACEUR Gen. Grynkewich, May 19, 2026).

**Q3.** What is the ICC Sanctions Repeal legislative pathway, and which members of Congress have introduced or co-sponsored it?

The coalition for ICC sanctions repeal has documented chilling effects on US civil society through the CICC "Criminalising Accountability" report. *Smith v. Trump*'s preliminary injunction (July 18, 2025) covers two named plaintiffs; a legislative repeal is needed for systematic relief. Identify the bill vehicle, sponsors, and the Senate Foreign Relations Committee members most active on this issue.

**Q4.** What is the WTO-specific withdrawal question, and does the URAA Section 125 five-year review mechanism (2025 cycle) represent a legislative leverage window or threat?

Section 125 of the Uruguay Round Agreements Act allows Congress to vote on WTO withdrawal every five years; the 2025 cycle coincides with the current administration. Map which members have used this mechanism as leverage, and whether any bills have been introduced conditioning continued WTO membership on reciprocal trade conduct.

**Q5.** What appropriations-based mechanisms exist for constraining defunding-as-withdrawal, and have any been invoked since January 2026?

The distinction between formal treaty withdrawal (requires waiting period, creates legal clarity) and defunding-as-withdrawal (immediate, financially devastating, harder to challenge) is one of Phase 2's key contributions. The Millennium Challenge Corporation's democratic governance conditionality, and appropriations conditions on international organization funding, are the legislative tools available short of treaty withdrawal legislation.

## Zone 2: Constitutional Feasibility

### Research Questions

**Q6.** Does Koh's "mirror principle" (Yale Law Journal) provide constitutionally sound grounds for requiring Senate consent to treaty exit, and how does it interact with *Curtiss-Wright*'s plenary executive foreign affairs power doctrine?

Harold Koh argues in "Presidential Power to Terminate International Agreements" (Yale L.J.F., 2018, updated in *The National Security Constitution in the 21st Century*, 2024) that the degree of congressional participation required to exit an agreement should mirror the degree required to enter. The mirror principle, if correct, means that treaties ratified by two-thirds Senate vote require the same supermajority to exit — invalidating every Trump administration withdrawal from Senate-ratified treaties since January 2025. The research must engage Jean Galbraith's "Rejoining Treaties" (U. Penn Scholarship, 2021) for the exit-entry symmetry argument, and Oona Hathaway and Scott Shapiro's broader work on international law compliance for the structural theory.

**Q7.** Has the *Goldwater v. Carter* (1979) political question bar on treaty withdrawal challenges been sufficiently narrowed by *Zivotofsky v. Clinton* (2012) and *Zivotofsky v. Kerry* (2015) to permit a court to reach the merits of a congressional challenge to presidential treaty withdrawal in violation of NDAA Section 1250A?

*Goldwater* produced a fractured plurality — only four justices voted for the political question doctrine; Powell concurred separately on ripeness grounds. The CRS notes that more recent jurisprudence suggests courts may be less likely to dismiss on political question grounds where a president acts contrary to a statute. *Zivotofsky v. Clinton* held that where Congress has spoken, the political question doctrine does not bar judicial review of whether the president complied. If Congress has enacted NDAA Section 1250A constraining NATO withdrawal, and the president is engaged in a "multi-year ongoing process" of de facto withdrawal, the *Zivotofsky* line provides the standing and justiciability theory for a legal challenge.

**Q8.** What is the constitutional basis for extending NDAA Section 1250A's model to non-NATO treaties, and which treaties are analytically strongest candidates for statutory protection?

The Phase 2 document identifies three candidates — Treaty Withdrawal Notification Act, Multilateral Commitment Protection Act, ICC Sanctions Repeal — without analyzing the constitutional theory distinguishing NATO from other treaties. The research must distinguish: (a) treaties ratified with two-thirds Senate consent (strongest protection argument); (b) executive agreements implemented by statute (congressional-executive agreements — the WTO falls here, protected by URAA Section 125); (c) pure executive agreements (president can exit unilaterally without constitutional challenge). The ICC Rome Statute is in category (c) for the US because the US never ratified — the ICC sanctions EO is therefore not a treaty withdrawal but a positive act of sanctions, which is what makes *Smith v. Trump*'s First Amendment framing essential.

**Q9.** Does the non-delegation doctrine, as applied in *West Virginia v. EPA* (2022) and the major questions doctrine generally, provide any grounds for challenging executive withdrawal from international organizations that Congress has funded by statute?

The IEEPA-based tariff strikes (*Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump*, February 20, 2026, struck down on major questions grounds) establish that courts will apply major questions scrutiny to executive actions of vast economic and political significance that lack clear congressional authorization. Congress has funded US membership in international organizations by statute; the executive's unilateral defunding and withdrawal from 66 organizations might be challenged as lacking statutory authorization under the major questions framework if the action is characterized as regulatory (not merely diplomatic). This is an underexplored theory requiring original constitutional analysis.

**Q10.** What is the First Amendment theory in *Smith v. Trump*, and how does it translate into a movement advocacy frame for domestic civil society?

The preliminary injunction held that EO 14203 likely violated the First Amendment by prohibiting speech-based services to the ICC (legal advocacy, research, testimony). The court enjoined civil and criminal penalties based on "speech-based services." The research must: (a) track the case's current status post-injunction and determine whether the government has appealed to the First Circuit; (b) analyze whether the injunction's scope covers organizations beyond the two named plaintiffs; (c) identify how civil society organizations (HRW, Amnesty, CICC network) are using the litigation as an advocacy frame in their sanctuary-from-sanctions communications.

## Zone 3: International Precedent

### Research Questions

**Q11.** What does the peer-reviewed comparative literature (Patberg 2025, Global Governance Brill 2025, International Studies Quarterly 2025) establish about the pattern of multilateral withdrawal, and how does the US 2026 withdrawal fit within the 1945-2024 dataset?

Three peer-reviewed 2025 publications provide the academic grounding for Phase 3's comparative claims:
- Brill/Global Governance Vol. 31(2): "Unilateral Withdrawals from Multilateral International Treaties, 1945–2024" — 945 withdrawals from 206 treaties. Research questions: Is the US January 2026 action (66 organizations simultaneously) historically unprecedented in scale? What issue areas attract most withdrawals? Does the dataset show the acceleration pattern Phase 2 claims?
- ISQ: "Responding to Unilateral Challenges to International Institutions" (2025) — three institutional response types: accommodate, do nothing, or respond forcefully. Research: Which response type is the ICC, ECHR, and other bodies applying to the US and Hungary withdrawals?
- Patberg (Journal of International Political Theory, 2025): "Treaty Withdrawal and Institutional Regression" — what democratic theory says about legitimate exit from international treaties. Research: Does Patberg's "democratic underlabourer" concept provide a normative framework for Phase 3's claim that treaty withdrawal without Senate consent violates democratic legitimacy?

**Q12.** Hungary's confirmed reversal (May 27, 2026, 133-37 parliamentary vote) is now the strongest available proof-of-concept for reversibility. What are the legal mechanics of the reversal, and what does it establish for the US context?

Hungary's withdrawal was halted before the June 2 formal exit date under Article 127 of the Rome Statute. The parliament voted to repeal the withdrawal notice legislation. This means Hungary remains a party to the Rome Statute without interruption. The research must establish: (a) the exact legal procedure used — was it a withdrawal of the notification to the UN Secretary-General, or a domestic repeal of the authorizing legislation that leaves the international notice in place but legally nullified domestically? (b) What does Magyar's government's public statement about enforcing ICC arrest warrants (including Netanyahu) mean for the domestic-international accountability link? (c) How does this compare with the Philippines' 2019 withdrawal and 2023 re-accession under Marcos — are there meaningful differences in the mechanisms?

**Q13.** What is the current status of the Duterte trial at the ICC (as of July 2026 production start), and what does the trial's continuation despite US sanctions establish about ICC institutional resilience?

The May 27 ICC status conference was the gate for the Duterte fitness dispute. Research must track: whether the court ordered a fresh fitness assessment or allowed the November 30 trial start to stand; and whether the prosecution's framing of this as "among the court's fastest to reach trial" has held. The Duterte trial proceeding against a former head of state despite US sanctions is the Phase 3 institutional resilience data point.

**Q14.** What is the subnational diplomacy response to federal multilateral retreat, and which state and local actors have taken concrete institutional action since January 2026?

The Just Security analysis "Washington's Multilateral Retreat Creates an Opening for State and Local Leaders" (2025) identifies governors, mayors, and state AGs as the subnational resistance architecture. Research must document: (a) which state AGs have filed amicus briefs or taken formal positions on treaty withdrawal issues; (b) which city governments have joined international compacts or organizations post-federal withdrawal (Paris Agreement equivalent post-2026?); (c) whether any US state or city has established a formal relationship with international organizations from which the US federal government has withdrawn. The Truman Center City & State Diplomacy Task Force report provides the framework document; the research must update it with January–July 2026 developments.

## Constitutional and Fiscal Implications

### Constitutional Implications (Novel vs. Domain 19f War Powers)

Domain 19f (War Powers) addresses the constitutional allocation of force-authorization power between Congress and the president. Phase 3 Domain 57 addresses the analogous but distinct question of treaty-making and withdrawal power. The constitutional novelty claims are:

**Novelty 1 (Non-Delegation by Exit)**: War Powers analysis focuses on *authorization* — did Congress authorize the use of force? Treaty withdrawal analysis focuses on *de-authorization* — does the president have unilateral power to undo commitments that required supermajority Senate consent to create? This is the constitutional inverse of the War Powers question, and no court has resolved it on the merits. The *Goldwater* plurality left the question open; the *Zivotofsky* line has narrowed the political question bar; and NDAA Section 1250A's existence means there is now a concrete statutory conflict waiting for a concrete presidential defiance to trigger litigation.

**Novelty 2 (First Amendment/ICC Sanctions)**: Domain 19f addresses executive war-making power in the international arena. Phase 3 Domain 57 addresses the domestic First Amendment consequences of international sanctions: US citizens prohibited from speech-based services to an international court. This is a novel domestic constitutional question untouched by War Powers analysis. *Smith v. Trump* is the live case; Phase 3 must provide the most current analysis of its status and implications.

**Novelty 3 (Major Questions/Defunding)**: The IEEPA major questions analysis (*Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump*, February 2026) established that executive economic actions of vast political significance require clear statutory authorization. Whether defunding 66 international organizations via executive memorandum — without specific congressional authorization for each withdrawal — constitutes a major questions violation is a novel constitutional question. This is closer to Domain 23 (Trade/Tariff Unilateralism) than Domain 19f, but the mechanism is the same: executive action of vast political significance in an area (international treaty obligations) where Congress has historically been joint sovereign.

### Fiscal Implications

The fiscal analysis is straightforward but underdeveloped in Phase 2:

**US Budget Impact**: The US contributed approximately $622M to the UN regular budget (22% share) and additional amounts to specialized agencies. Withdrawal from 66 organizations reduces these assessed contributions. The immediate fiscal effect is favorable to the administration's stated goal of reducing multilateral spending — which is precisely why defunding is used as a withdrawal mechanism before formal treaty withdrawal triggers political costs.

**Allied Democracy Counter-Defunding**: The EU has committed to increased multilateral institutional contributions in response to US withdrawal. Germany, France, Norway, and Japan have each separately announced increased contributions to the ICC Trust Fund for Victims and to International IDEA. The aggregate counter-defunding commitment from allied democracies represents a measurable institutional resilience response. Research must quantify the EU and bilateral commitments to establish whether they offset US withdrawal.

**Domestic Economic Consequences of Alliance Abandonment**: The Perry World House analysis documents that Japan, South Korea, and Australia are accelerating defense spending and indigenous military technology development to reduce dependency on US security guarantees. This sovereign insurance spending — forced on allied democracies by US withdrawal reliability questions — represents a fiscal consequence of US multilateral withdrawal that appears nowhere in the administration's cost-benefit framing. The Stimson Center analysis provides the framework for this argument.
