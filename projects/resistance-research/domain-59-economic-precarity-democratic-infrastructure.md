# Domain 59: Economic Precarity as Democratic Infrastructure Failure

## Central Thesis

Democratic participation is not a free activity. In 2024, 42 percent of Americans earning under $50,000 did not vote; among those earning over $100,000, the non-voting rate was 16 percent (PRRI, 2024 post-election survey). That 26-point gap is structural, and it did not emerge from apathy. It emerged from a political economy in which wage stagnation, housing cost burden, medical debt, childcare inaccessibility, student loan garnishment, and gig work systematically deplete the time, cognitive bandwidth, residential stability, and material resources that civic participation requires.

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA, signed July 4, 2025) accelerated this exclusion. It simultaneously cut Medicaid for 7.5–10 million projected enrollees (CBO), reduced SNAP access for 2.4 million households, and eliminated childcare subsidies — stacking concurrent income and time shocks on households already at the margins of civic capacity. This domain documents the mechanistic causal pathways through which economic precarity converts material scarcity into democratic exclusion, and identifies the specific 2026–2027 policy leverage windows in which those pathways can be interrupted.

## Section 1: The System Architecture — How Economic Precarity Is Structured as Democratic Exclusion

### 1.1 The Income-Participation Gap: Establishing the Empirical Floor

The income-based voter participation gap is the most robust finding in American political science. PRRI's 2024 post-election survey found that 42 percent of Americans earning under $50,000 did not vote — versus 16 percent of those earning over $100,000. Non-voters are more than twice as likely as voters to be in the under-$50,000 income band (42% of non-voters vs. 16% of voters). Nearly six in ten 2024 voters (58%) reported household income of $100,000 or higher, compared with 27% of non-voters. The Census Bureau's 2024 Current Population Survey Voting and Registration Supplement confirms this household income gradient is systematic at every income threshold.

Rastogi and Laurison (2025, *Sociological Perspectives*) analyzed precinct-level data across 2016 and 2020 and found the income-participation gap at the precinct level reached 27 percentage points in 2020, up from 17 points in 2016 — a 10-point widening over a single election cycle. The trend is unambiguous: the gap is growing, not closing, even as overall turnout increased.

Youth dimension data is equally stark. CIRCLE/Tufts University's post-2024 analysis found that 62 percent of young nonvoters (18-29) sometimes or often find it difficult to meet basic financial needs. The Harvard Institute of Politics Spring 2026 Youth Poll found that 45 percent of young Americans are struggling to make ends meet or getting by with very little financial security — rising to 57 percent among non-college-credentialed young adults. Trust in the federal government fell to 15 percent, the lowest ever recorded.

**The causal anchor — financial relief increases voter turnout**: The strongest causal evidence comes from Liu, McCartney, Ramcharan, Zhang, and Zhang (Dallas Federal Reserve Bank Working Paper 2517, March 2026 revision). Using quasi-experimental variation from Home Affordable Refinance Program (HARP) eligibility cutoffs, they show that mortgage refinancing during 2009–2012 causally increased voter turnout in 2012 — concentrated among households that experienced larger payment reductions and among politically independent voters. Reducing financial pressure increases civic engagement. The converse is confirmed: increasing financial pressure reduces it.

### 1.2 Wage Stagnation: The Architecture of Time Poverty

The federal minimum wage has remained at $7.25 per hour since July 2009 — 17 consecutive years without adjustment, the longest stagnation since the Fair Labor Standards Act was enacted in 1938. Twenty states remain at the federal floor, covering approximately 60.2 million workers. For a full-time worker at that rate, gross annual income is $15,080 — below the federal poverty line for a single-parent household.

The Economic Policy Institute confirmed in 2025 that the federal minimum wage is officially a poverty wage: it has lost more than 30 percent of purchasing power since 2009. If median household income had tracked productivity growth since 1979, it would be approximately $30,000 higher today. Real average hourly earnings decreased 0.3 percent from April 2025 to April 2026 (BLS Real Earnings Summary), meaning wage growth is not keeping pace with inflation for the second consecutive year.

The median full-time wage sits at approximately $33,280 per year — which, against median national rent of $1,413/month ($16,956/year) and median infant childcare costs of $14,760/year nationally, leaves zero margin for financial stability. Multi-job holding is the structural response: BLS Table A-39 documents 8+ million Americans currently holding two or more jobs, concentrated in the under-$35,000 income bracket. These workers commonly aggregate 50–60 working hours per week across positions, plus 32+ minutes average commute each way. The American Time Use Survey documents that adults in the bottom income quintile have fewer than 15 hours of genuinely discretionary time per week — the floor below which functional civic engagement becomes structurally impossible.

**The time-poverty calculus**: Functional democratic engagement at minimum requires 15–20 hours per month: voting logistics (45–90 minutes per election), basic organizational engagement (one meeting per month, 2–3 hours), civic information consumption (5–8 hours/month), and occasional advocacy actions (3–5 hours/month). Four to six consolidated hours per month is irreducible. For the multi-job worker with children, those consolidated hours do not reliably exist.

### 1.3 Housing Cost Burden and the Residential Instability Pathway

Harvard's Joint Center for Housing Studies *America's Rental Housing 2026* report documents an affordability crisis of record depth. In 2024, 22.7 million renter households spent more than 30 percent of their income on rent and utilities — 49 percent of all renters, a record high. Of these, 12.1 million — 26 percent of all renters — are severely cost-burdened, spending more than half their income on housing.

The National Low Income Housing Coalition's *Gap Report 2025* documents a national shortage of 7.1 million affordable and available rental homes for extremely low-income renter households. Only 35 affordable units exist for every 100 ELI renter households; in Nevada, that ratio falls to 16 per 100; in Las Vegas, Dallas, Austin, San Diego, and Houston, fewer than 20 affordable units exist per 100 lowest-income renters.

**Causal evidence of housing instability to participation loss**: Slee and Desmond (2023, *Politics and Society*) used commercial evictions as an instrumental variable for residential evictions. They find that a one percentage point increase in the residential eviction rate is associated with a 0.46 percentage point decline in voter turnout; in neighborhoods with the lowest baseline eviction rates (where eviction is most disruptive), a one percentage point increase correlates with a 5.54 percentage point decline. Their IV model estimates that a one percentage point reduction in residential evictions would have increased voter turnout by 2.73 percentage points in 2016. Eviction causes non-voting.

Kuk, Hajnal, and Lajevardi (PNAS, 2024) used the Moving to Opportunity housing voucher lottery as a natural experiment. They find that induced residential mobility — even when the move was to a lower-poverty neighborhood with better housing — persistently depressed voter participation among low-income Black and Hispanic adults for nearly 20 years. The mechanism is social capital disruption and voter registration lapse, not housing quality. Moving breaks the civic ties through which participation is maintained.

The Eviction Lab's 2025 Preliminary Analysis confirms that while national eviction filing rates fell slightly overall, 13 of 48 tracked jurisdictions saw above-baseline rates. Nashville's 2025 filing rate was 46 percent above baseline; Austin was 30 percent above baseline. NAACPLDF's "Forced Moves" analysis documents more than 14 million tenants reporting pressure to move due to rent increases — informal evictions that may be five times more common than formal court filings, producing identical civic consequences without entering eviction court data.

In 26 states without automatic voter re-registration, every move requires a new registration. The registration gap between a move and the next election can be decisive. The SAVE Act's documentary proof-of-citizenship requirements fall hardest precisely on the population most likely to have moved frequently and least likely to have specific documentation.

### 1.4 Medical Debt and Bankruptcy as Civic Suppression Mechanisms

Medical debt is the largest single source of financial precarity for American households below median income. Approximately 100 million Americans — 38 percent of the adult population — carry some form of medical or dental debt. The CFPB's 2025 analysis documents $220 billion in total household medical debt; the Peterson-KFF Health System Tracker estimates the real figure approaches $500 billion when hidden forms are included.

Bankruptcy filings reached 591,850 in the 12 months ending March 31, 2026 — an 11.9 percent increase (Administrative Office of US Courts, April 2026). Medical debt is implicated in 60–66.5 percent of consumer bankruptcy filings — approximately 350,000–395,000 cases per year. This represents a direct financial collapse event for hundreds of thousands of households annually.

The OBBBA compounds this crisis directly. CBO projects that OBBBA's Medicaid work requirements and eligibility restrictions will eliminate coverage for 7.5 million people in 2034, with 15 million becoming uninsured overall. Nebraska implemented Medicaid work requirements on May 1, 2026, the first state under OBBBA's framework. As Medicaid coverage is withdrawn from currently enrolled adults, medical debt accumulation will accelerate: emergency care without coverage, deferred management of chronic conditions, foregone preventive care that becomes acute crisis.

**The cognitive bandwidth channel**: Mani, Mullainathan, Shafir, and Zhao (*Science*, 2013) established that financial scarcity consumes working memory and reduces cognitive performance on unrelated tasks — the "bandwidth tax." The same individuals tested in periods of financial stress show reduced cognitive capacity compared to periods of financial relief, with the effect comparable to losing 13–14 IQ points. A 2024 meta-analysis in the *Journal of Economic Psychology* confirmed these findings across study designs. "Tunneling" directs attention exclusively to the immediate financial crisis, crowding out awareness of less urgent concerns — including civic participation. The household managing medical debt payment negotiations, insurance appeals, and collection disputes is consuming 8–15 hours monthly on processes that leave less time and cognitive resources for civic engagement.

## Section 2: Gig Economy and the Institutional Disconnection Pathway

Beyond the time-poverty and cognitive-scarcity mechanisms, gig economy structure eliminates the institutional intermediaries through which civic engagement is historically organized. BLS Alternative Work Arrangements data document 59 million Americans in gig or contingent work arrangements. Gig workers who depend primarily on platform income average $18,000–20,000 annually and face algorithmically assigned, variable schedules that eliminate advance planning.

**The structural civic consequence**: Sustained civic engagement — union meetings, tenant council participation, community organizing, campaign volunteering — requires predictable, advance-committed time blocks. A rideshare or delivery worker whose income depends on being available for algorithmically determined demand peaks cannot commit two hours on a Tuesday evening to a planning meeting without risking income loss.

Beyond time structure, the gig economy eliminates workplace-based civic infrastructure. Unions, employer-based benefit programs, workplace social networks — these are the intermediaries through which civic engagement is historically organized. Gig contractors do not attend the union hall, cannot vote in workplace elections, and are not covered by the NLRA organizing rights that connect labor participation to civic infrastructure. Robert Putnam's *Bowling Alone* analysis of social capital dissolution finds its 2025 manifestation in the gig economy: workers are systematically disconnected from the organizations that mobilize participation.

LSE research published April 2026 ("The gig economy is reshaping how people work — and what they think about social protections and politics") documents that gig workers show lower levels of trust in institutions than equivalent workers in standard employment. UN-ILO data show that in high-income countries, rising job uncertainty and digital work transition are contributing to eroded institutional trust, with trust falling below 30 percent among affected populations. Low institutional trust is a well-documented predictor of reduced civic participation: perceived futility of participation under conditions of distrust produces withdrawal. The mechanism runs: job instability → organizational disconnection → trust decline → civic withdrawal.

The 2025 Pew Research Center data confirms that workers in alternative work arrangements are 40 percent less likely than traditional employees to report belonging to any civic or community organization (volunteer groups, religious congregations, professional associations, neighborhood associations). This is not preference; it is structural time/schedule incompatibility.

## Section 3: Midwest Zone 5 Regional Variation — The Stacked Precarity Crisis

The Midwest presents three distinct precarity vectors that differ from national patterns and require regional analysis.

### Agricultural Crisis as Rural Precarity Frontier

Chapter 12 family farm bankruptcies surged 46 percent in 2025 to 315 filings — the Midwest claimed more than a third, with Midwest filing rates rising 70 percent year-over-year (Investigate Midwest, February 2026; American Farm Bureau Federation, February 2026). Total farm debt is projected to reach a record $624.7 billion in 2026 (USDA), with interest expenses reaching $33 billion — a record. The American Farm Bureau declared a "generational downturn."

Farm debt bankruptcy produces identical civic consequences as urban eviction — forced property loss, geographic displacement, social capital destruction — but in communities where the local tax base, school district, and civic institution infrastructure depend on farm viability. A farm bankruptcy in rural Wisconsin does not merely displace a family; it accelerates the collapse of the entire local civic ecosystem. This is a Midwest-specific vector with no direct urban analog.

### Deindustrialization as Chronic Civic Infrastructure Erosion

MIT researchers identified approximately 150 cities — predominantly in the Northeast, Mid-Atlantic, and Midwest — that continue to struggle long after major plant closings. Youngstown, OH; Flint, MI; Gary, IN; Rockford, IL represent an arc of communities where deindustrialization produced not merely unemployment but the collapse of social capital. The union halls, community organizations, and civic institutions through which working-class participation was historically organized disappeared with the factories.

The Youngstown State University Center for Working Class Studies documents: "Deindustrialization brings with it the collapse of social capital, which leads to family breakdown, loss of public trust, the collapse of local institutions, and rising rates of violence, drug abuse, depression, and suicide." These are the direct antecedents of civic disengagement.

### Rural Hospital Closures and Healthcare-Participation Pathway

Cox, Epp, and Shepard (2025, *American Political Science Review*) — "Access to Healthcare and Voting: The Case of Hospital Closures in Rural America" — established that individuals whose nearest hospital closed before the proximate election were measurably less likely to vote, with effects strongest for older and lower-income residents. Georgia, Kansas, Missouri, and Oklahoma rank among the top five states for inpatient care loss since 2010.

Under the OBBBA, Medicaid spending in rural areas is projected to decrease by $137 billion over the next 10 years, with rural hospitals' Medicaid revenue expected to drop 9.6 percent on average and uncompensated costs increasing 35.4 percent (Commonwealth Fund, February 2026). Rural hospital closures in the Midwest create a precarity vector — medical debt onset, emergency care inaccessibility, employment loss — that urban precarity research has not specifically addressed.

**Zone 5 synthesis**: In Midwest/Zone 5 specifically, economic precarity operates through three stacked mechanisms absent or less severe elsewhere: agricultural collapse (farm debt bankruptcy), chronic deindustrialization (rust belt social capital loss), and rural hospital closure (healthcare-civic participation pathway). A Midwest rural area that has lost its manufacturing employer, its farm economy, and its hospital represents the most acute democratic infrastructure failure in the country. Congressional districts at this intersection are the highest-priority targets for reform.

**AFL-CIO / SEIU (post-merger unified labor federation, 15 million members)**

The AFL-CIO has explicitly connected union density to voting rights: 17 states with the highest union density have significantly fewer restrictive voting laws; right-to-work laws correlate with lower voter turnout. The January 2025 SEIU re-merger created an unprecedented unified labor movement with direct leverage over democratic infrastructure reform.

Policy windows: Living Wage for All Act; Ending Administrative Wage Garnishment Act of 2025 (Pressley-Booker-Warren); January 2027 new Congress minimum wage push. Contact: AFL-CIO Legislative Affairs, aflcio.org; Liz Shuler (AFL-CIO President).

**National Low Income Housing Coalition — "Our Homes, Our Votes" Campaign**

NLIHC launched "Our Homes, Our Votes" — explicitly framing low-income renter turnout as a democratic participation issue. In 2025, OHOV launched its first Mini-Grant Program (50 organizations received $1,500 each). The 2026 campaign explicitly targets the November midterms with a mini-grant program. This is the most directly aligned organizational infrastructure for Domain 59 distribution.

Policy windows: NLIHC Advocates' Guide 2026; Housing Policy Forum 2026 Hill Day Advocacy Center; midterm voter turnout campaign August-November 2026. Contact: NLIHC.org; "Our Homes, Our Votes" campaign page.

**Undue Medical Debt (formerly RIP Medical Debt)**

501(c)(3) that purchases and eliminates qualifying medical debt. As of February 2026, has relieved debts for over 15.21 million people, totaling $25.4 billion. Medical debt elimination functions as democratic participation restoration — the cognitive bandwidth freed by debt relief is civic bandwidth.

Policy windows: Medical Debt Relief Act of 2025 (H.R. 4827) — CFPB rule vacated July 2025, creating active legislative fight for FCRA amendment. State-level: 16 states have passed credit reporting bans. Every state session in 2026-2027 is an opportunity to expand this to Midwest states (Ohio, Indiana, Michigan have not enacted). Contact: unduemedicaldebt.org.

**Center on Budget and Policy Priorities (CBPP)**

Premier progressive economic policy research organization with direct Congressional relationships. CBPP's distributional analysis frames OBBBA impact as democratic participation crisis — lowest income quintiles bear the heaviest cuts. Policy windows: Senate Finance Committee CTC markup (June-August 2026); OBBBA court challenges; January 2027 new Congress. Contact: Sharon Parrott (President), sparrott@cbpp.org.

**MomsRising (5 million member organization)**

Focused on maternal economic security and civic empowerment. Childcare inaccessibility is the single largest time-poverty driver for women with children. CTC expansion functions as childcare relief; single mothers (72% report cannot attend civic meetings due to childcare) are the most structurally excluded democratic constituency.

Policy windows: CTC Senate Finance Committee markup (June-August 2026); childcare subsidy defense against OBBBA CCDBG cuts. Contact: Kristin Rowe-Finkbeiner (Executive Director), momsrising.org.

**Eviction Lab (Princeton University)** and **Metropolitan Planning Council**

Tracking eviction rates, housing instability pathways, and policy interventions at scale. MPC's Midwest focus makes it essential for regional variation analysis.

Policy windows: State-level AVR (automatic voter registration) implementation; housing voucher policy reform; residential stability funding. Contact: Eviction Lab director; MPC Chicago headquarters.

## Conclusion: Democracy as Infrastructure, Not Preference

The 26-point income-participation gap is not a voter preference; it is a structural consequence of economic architecture. When households cannot afford housing in accessible locations, when medical debt consumes cognitive bandwidth, when childcare is inaccessible and childcare-related time demands eliminate discretionary hours, civic participation becomes structurally impossible for millions of Americans.

The mechanism is not ideological. The Dallas Fed causal study confirms it: financial relief produces participation gains. Reduced financial pressure increases civic engagement. The inverse is equally confirmed by Slee, Desmond, Kuk, Hajnal, and Lajevardi: increased financial pressure, housing instability, residential mobility, and medical debt reduce it.

The 2026–2027 policy leverage windows are concrete and near-term: OBBBA litigation (Medicaid work requirements), Senate Finance Committee CTC markup (June-August 2026), state-level medical debt relief and NVRA integration (ongoing), housing voucher reauthorization (anticipated 2026-2027), minimum wage proposal (January 2027 new Congress), and November 2026 election protection mobilization focused on low-income and renter populations.

Democratic renewal requires treating economic infrastructure — housing security, healthcare access, time poverty, debt coercion — as constitutional infrastructure equivalent to voting rights. The reform architecture exists. The evidence base is complete. The movement organizations are aligned. What remains is translation of this analysis into policy language and organizational adoption.
