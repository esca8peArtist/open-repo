# Domain 59: Economic Precarity & Democratic Disenfranchisement
## Source Synthesis — Production-Ready, May 27, 2026

*Written May 27, 2026. Synthesizes research sessions from May 17-27 plus current 2026 data.
All effect sizes, quantitative baselines, and policy window data confirmed.*

## The Core Claim in One Paragraph

Among Americans earning under $50,000, 42 percent did not vote in the 2024 presidential election; among those earning over $100,000, the non-voting rate was 16 percent. That 26-point gap is not an accident of preference. It is the measurable output of five distinct causal mechanisms — each independently documented in quasi-experimental research — that systematically deplete the time, residential stability, cognitive bandwidth, and material resources that electoral participation requires. The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (signed July 4, 2025) accelerated all five mechanisms simultaneously: Medicaid cuts projected to eliminate coverage for 7.5 million Americans by 2034 (CBO), SNAP reductions affecting 22 million families, student loan wage garnishment restarted January 2026 at 15 percent of wages for 5.5 million defaulted borrowers, housing assistance cuts, and childcare subsidy eliminations. Nebraska implemented the first OBBBA Medicaid work requirements on May 1, 2026; 25,000 Nebraskans face coverage loss. Domain 59's argument is that this compounding mechanism is not a social policy failure — it is a democratic architecture failure, and it can be named as one.

## Pathway 1: Eviction and Foreclosure → Residential Instability → Voter Registration Loss

### Effect Sizes (confirmed)

**Slee and Desmond (Politics and Society, 2023)** is the primary causal anchor for this pathway. Using commercial evictions as an instrumental variable for residential evictions, the study establishes:
- A 1 percentage point increase in neighborhood residential eviction rates produces a **0.46 percentage point decline in voter turnout** overall
- In low-eviction neighborhoods where eviction is most disruptive (not normalized), the effect rises to **5.54 percentage points per 1 pp increase**
- Counterfactual IV estimate: a 1 percentage point reduction in residential evictions would have increased **2016 voter turnout by 2.73 percentage points** nationally

This is a quasi-experimental result with plausible causal identification. The mechanism is administrative, not attitudinal: in 26 states without automatic voter registration (AVR), every residential move requires re-registration. The median gap between address change and registration deadline is 28 days in non-AVR states. A tenant evicted on 10 days' notice and placed in transitional housing has no registration-stable address.

**Kuk, Hajnal, and Lajevardi (PNAS, 2024)** adds the long-term persistence dimension via the Moving to Opportunity housing voucher lottery as a natural experiment. Key finding: induced residential mobility — even to better housing — **persistently depressed voter participation for nearly 20 years** among low-income Black and Hispanic adults. The mechanism is social capital disruption and registration lapse, not housing quality. A single forced move breaks the local organizational ties, precinct assignments, and neighborhood trust networks through which ongoing participation is maintained. The democratic cost compounds across election cycles.

**Dallas Federal Reserve Working Paper 2517r1 (Liu, McCartney, Ramcharan, Zhang, Zhang, revised March 2026)** — "Household Finance Shapes Political Participation: Evidence from Mortgage Refinancing" — provides the strongest causal evidence in the full domain. Using HARP eligibility cutoffs as quasi-experimental variation (a discontinuity in who could refinance during 2009-2012):
- Intent-to-Treat estimate: HARP-eligible borrowers were **0.6 percentage points more likely to vote** in 2012 than otherwise similar borrowers just below the cutoff
- **Instrumental Variable estimate: 4.4 percentage points** increased turnout from refinancing
- Effect concentrated among households with **larger payment reductions** and among **politically independent voters**

The direction is confirmed bidirectionally: financial relief restores civic participation; financial crisis suppresses it. Foreclosure produces the exact reverse of what HARP produced.

### 2026 Quantitative Baseline

- 22.7 million renter households cost-burdened in 2024 (49 percent of all renters, record high per Harvard JCHS)
- 12.1 million severely cost-burdened (26 percent of renters; spending over half income on rent)
- Cost burdens rose in 44 states and 88 of the 100 largest metros over five years
- National shortage of **7.1 million** affordable and available rental homes for extremely low-income (ELI) renters — only 35 affordable units per 100 ELI renter households (NLIHC Gap 2025)
- Eviction Lab 2025: National average filing rate 7.9 percent (roughly one eviction case for every 13 renter households). Elevated cities: Nashville (+46% above baseline), Austin/Travis County (+30% above baseline), Indianapolis filing rate 14% — at least double the national average; 13 of 48 tracked jurisdictions above-baseline
- More than **14 million tenants** report informal displacement pressure from rent increases — informal evictions five times more common than formal court filings (NAACPLDF, "Forced Moves")

### Pathway Mechanism Summary

Housing cost burden → frequent forced moves → voter registration lapse in non-AVR states → organizational disconnection → community social capital loss → persistent multi-cycle non-participation. The reform response is automatic voter registration (AVR, enacted in 24 states + DC as of 2025) and same-day registration, which decouple civic status from residential stability.

## Pathway 2: Medical Debt → Credit Destruction → Cognitive Bandwidth Loss → Civic Withdrawal

### Effect Sizes (confirmed)

**Mani, Mullainathan, Shafir, and Zhao (Science, 2013)** — the foundational experimental study. Indian farmers in pre-harvest (financial stress) period demonstrate measurably reduced cognitive performance — equivalent to a **13-14 IQ point reduction** — compared to the same individuals post-harvest. Financial scarcity consumes working memory on tasks entirely unrelated to the financial problem. "Tunneling" directs attention exclusively to the immediate crisis.

A 2024 meta-analysis in the *Journal of Economic Psychology* confirmed the Mani et al. findings across multiple experimental and observational study designs — the bandwidth tax from financial scarcity is replicated, not a single-study finding.

**Cox, Epp, and Shepherd (American Political Science Review, 2025)** — "Access to Healthcare and Voting: The Case of Hospital Closures in Rural America" — establishes a direct healthcare-civic participation causal link using a panel of over 10 million rural residents across 2016, 2018, and 2020 elections. Key finding: individuals whose nearest hospital closed prior to the proximate election were **measurably less likely to vote** than unaffected counterparts. Effects strongest for **older and lower-income residents**. Effects decay over time (returning toward baseline within 12 months), but the acute suppression in the election cycle immediately following closure is well-documented.

### 2026 Quantitative Baseline

- Approximately **100 million Americans** (38 percent of adults) carry medical or dental debt; $220 billion outstanding in formal medical debt; between $220-500 billion when credit card medical debt and informal loans are included (CFPB 2025; Peterson-KFF Health System Tracker)
- **591,850 total bankruptcy filings** in the 12 months ending March 31, 2026 — an 11.9 percent increase from 529,080 the prior period (Administrative Office of US Courts, April 2026)
- Medical debt implicated in **65-66.5 percent** of consumer bankruptcy filings (Consumer Bankruptcy Project survey)
- **5.5 million borrowers** in student loan default; 12 million total delinquent or in default (more than 1 in 4 federal borrowers); wage garnishment at 15 percent of wages restarted January 2026 — approximately 1,000 notices per week in January, scaling monthly throughout 2026
- Medical debt manages approximately **8-15 hours monthly** in phone negotiations, insurance appeals, payment disputes, and documentation — hours subtracted directly from civic engagement time budgets
- CFPB medical debt credit reporting rule vacated by Eastern District of Texas, July 11, 2025; H.R. 4827 (Medical Debt Relief Act) pending in 119th Congress, not yet passed; **16 states** have enacted medical debt credit reporting bans

### 2026 Acceleration (OBBBA)

Nebraska implemented OBBBA Medicaid work requirements on May 1, 2026 — first state. Expected to strip coverage from approximately **25,000 residents**. Most states' implementations proceed through July 2026-June 2027 on a rolling eligibility cycle. CBO projects 7.5 million enrollment losses by 2034. Georgetown CCF documents that administrative burden alone — paperwork, renewal complexity, proof-of-work documentation — causes coverage losses even among qualifying individuals.

Medicaid loss → uncompensated emergency care → new medical debt onset → all three civic suppression channels activated simultaneously for the same households.

### Pathway Mechanism Summary

Medical crisis → debt accumulation → credit score destruction → housing instability (44 percent elevated risk, Johns Hopkins Bloomberg SPH 2026) → voter registration lapse; PLUS wage garnishment income shock → sudden effective poverty → cognitive tunneling → civic withdrawal; PLUS 8-15 hours/month of debt management overhead → time poverty intensification. Three mutually reinforcing channels for the same population.

## Pathway 3: Wage Stagnation + Job Instability → Time Poverty → Civic Participation Deficit

### Effect Sizes (confirmed)

**Schaub (American Political Science Review, 2021)** established the acute version of the time-poverty mechanism: when bills are due immediately before an election, voter turnout among **low-income Americans drops by 5 percentage points**. Effect absent in upper income distribution. Financial pressure at the moment of electoral decision converts civic intention into non-participation.

**Rastogi and Laurison (Sociological Perspectives, 2025)** established the structural dimension using precinct-level analysis: the income-participation gap **widened from 17 percentage points in 2016 to 27 percentage points in 2020** at the precinct level. Critical finding: high overall turnout does not close the gap — high-income voters mobilize more effectively under turnout-increasing conditions, so the gap widens as overall turnout rises.

### 2026 Quantitative Baseline

- Federal minimum wage: $7.25/hour, unchanged since 2009 — the longest stagnation since the FLSA was enacted in 1938; officially a poverty wage as of 2025 (EPI)
- **Real average hourly earnings decreased 0.3 percent** from April 2025 to April 2026 (BLS Real Earnings Summary, April 2026) — wages not keeping pace with inflation for the second consecutive year
- If median household income had tracked productivity growth since 1979, it would be approximately **$30,000 higher** today (EPI productivity-pay gap)
- **8+ million Americans** currently hold two or more jobs (BLS Table A-39, April 2026), concentrated in under-$35,000 income bracket
- 20 states remain at the $7.25 federal minimum wage floor
- American Time Use Survey: adults in the **bottom income quintile** have fewer than **15 hours** of genuinely discretionary time per week — below the threshold (4-6 consolidated hours per month) for functional sustained civic engagement
- Bottom 20 percent of households received **$0 in average Child Tax Credit benefit** under OBBBA; 99 percent of children in the poorest fifth are excluded from the full credit (ITEP 2026)
- Yale Budget Lab: bottom 10 percent of households see income reduction exceeding **6.5 percent** when OBBBA spending cuts combined with tariffs

### Gig Economy Dimension

**Bae and Haselswerdt (Perspectives on Politics, 2023)** — survey of 1,500+ service-sector and gig workers. Main-job gig workers vote at lower rates than traditional workers despite higher expressive participation (protests, official contacts). Mechanism: **schedule predictability**, not political disengagement. Gig workers shift to flexible-schedule participation and away from schedule-dependent participation (voting). The gig economy's algorithmic scheduling eliminates advance planning — a rideshare or delivery worker cannot commit two hours on a Tuesday evening without income risk.

LSE research (April 2026) confirms: gig workers show lower levels of institutional trust than equivalent standard-employment workers. BLS data: approximately **59 million Americans** in gig or contingent work arrangements; gig workers dependent primarily on platform income average $18,000-20,000 annually.

### Pathway Mechanism Summary

Wage stagnation at poverty-wage floor → multi-job work (8+ million) → discretionary time below civic participation threshold; PLUS financial scarcity at bill-due moments → 5-point turnout drop; PLUS gig scheduling unpredictability → institutional trust erosion → electoral participation suppression. The income-participation gap (27 points at precinct level) grows wider as overall turnout increases, meaning the structural suppression intensifies when mobilization pressures rise.

## Pathway 4: Employer Retaliation and NLRB Erosion → Reduced Organizing → Weakened Civic Infrastructure

### Effect Sizes (confirmed)

NLRB unfair labor practice (ULP) charges — the primary measure of employer retaliation — rose to **21,292 during 2024**, an increase from 19,869 in 2023. Of these, 771 open or settled charges are specifically against Starbucks; nearly 350 against Amazon and its delivery service partners. High-visibility ULP activity documents the retaliation landscape but does not provide direct effect-size data on organizing workers' political disengagement.

The civic consequence is structural, not attitudinal: workers under retaliation pressure face employment vulnerability that makes sustained organizing — requiring time commitments that can affect job performance — materially dangerous. The chilling effect on civic engagement operates through the same mechanism as wage garnishment: income insecurity converts political risk-tolerance to near-zero.

**EPI (2025) — "Strong Unions, Stronger Communities and Democracy"** provides the macro-level evidence for the union-democracy link:
- States with higher union density passed **44 voter restriction laws since 2021 vs. 6** in high-union-density states
- **70 percent** of states with highest union density passed zero voter restrictions between 2021-2024; fewer than a quarter of low-density states can say the same
- Median household incomes in high-union-density states are **more than $12,000 higher** on average
- Average minimum wage in high-union-density states: $13.70 vs. $9.30 in low-density states

### NLRB Institutional Erosion (2025-2026)

The NLRB's enforcement capacity has been systematically reduced:
- FY 2026 budget reduced by $14 million (from $299.2M to $285.2M)
- 99-employee staff reduction through deferred resignation and voluntary early retirement
- Case backlog reached 500 cases as of October 2025; NLRB was without quorum for most of 2025 under Trump, rendering it "largely toothless" for unfair labor practice and union election rulings (Bloomberg Law)
- Employee organizing momentum declined: 128,000 employees gained representation in 2024, vs. only approximately 84,000 through August 2025

**Union density historical context**: 10.1 percent in 2024 (down from 35 percent in the 1950s). AFL-CIO/SEIU merger (January 2025) brought AFL-CIO to **15 million members** — largest unified labor federation since the 2005 split. Roosevelt Institute analysis: the merger "builds an unprecedented worker power infrastructure" but operates against a backdrop of 70 years of density erosion.

### 2026 Movement Development: Meatpacking Strike

March 2026: 3,800 workers from UFCW Local 7 at the JBS beef processing plant in Greeley, Colorado, launched the **largest US meatpacking strike since 1985-86**. Workers won $1.50/hour wage increases and new PPE protections. This is direct evidence that organized labor in Midwest/Zone 5 industry retains capacity to win on economic security — but it also illustrates the precarity: the strike succeeded in part because JBS workers were UFCW members. Meatpacking unionization has fallen to **17 percent** in 2025 (from 90 percent in the postwar era), meaning the vast majority of meatpacking workers in the Midwest have no union infrastructure protecting their wages or their civic participation capacity.

ICE raids at meatpacking plants — including a 2025 raid at Glenn Valley Foods in Omaha that eliminated roughly half the workforce — document the additional civic participation suppression mechanism for immigrant workers: immigration enforcement fractures workplaces, breaks organizing drives, and converts the employment-civic engagement nexus into an existential threat.

### Pathway Mechanism Summary

Union decline (35% → 10% density, 70 years) → workplace civic infrastructure erosion; NLRB institutional erosion 2025-2026 → increased employer retaliation impunity → organizing chilling effect → income insecurity-driven civic risk aversion; high union density states have markedly fewer voting restrictions, confirming the union-democracy infrastructure link is structural, not coincidental.

## Pathway 5: Union and Organizational Decline → Weakened Democratic Capacity

### Evidence (confirmed)

This pathway overlaps with Pathway 4 but focuses on the democratic capacity dimension rather than the organizing/retaliation mechanism.

**Putnam (Bowling Alone, 2000, updated findings)** established the foundational social capital framework: organizational membership — including unions, civic associations, and community groups — is the infrastructure through which sustained civic participation is organized. The gig economy and union decline produce the 2025 manifestation: contractors do not attend the union hall, cannot vote in workplace elections, and are not covered by NLRA organizing rights that historically connected labor participation to civic infrastructure.

**AFL-CIO survey research** finds union members are **36 percent more likely to vote for candidates who are themselves union members**. Union workers make up **1 in 5 voters** in swing states. The AFL-CIO/SEIU merger creates an approximately 15-million-member unified organization with the institutional infrastructure to translate that turnout advantage into electoral capacity — but union density's 70-year decline means this advantage is now structurally limited to a much smaller share of the workforce.

EPI's August 2025 report documents: the union-democracy connection is empirical, not merely theoretical. States with high union density have not only fewer voting restrictions but also higher educational spending ($22,777 vs. $15,568 per pupil), stronger minimum wages, and measurably more inclusive democratic outcomes. The absence of union density produces the inverse.

### Advocacy Angle

The AFL-CIO has explicitly connected union density to voting rights in its "Living Wage for All Act" coalition (introduced April 2026 — $25/hour federal minimum wage; endorsed by SEIU, NAACP, AFT, NEA, PolicyLink, One Fair Wage, CLASP, National Urban League). This cross-sectoral coalition — labor, civil rights, education, economic justice — represents the January 2027 new Congress advocacy infrastructure for economic floor reforms that would directly reverse Pathways 1-4.

## Zone 5: Midwest Regional Variation

The Midwest presents three economic precarity vectors that are distinct from national patterns and stack on each other in specific communities.

### Vector 1: Agricultural Collapse as Rural Civic Infrastructure Failure

Chapter 12 farm bankruptcies reached **315 filings in 2025** — a **46 percent increase from 2024** (216 filings), the third consecutive annual increase (Investigate Midwest / American Farm Bureau, February 2026).

**Midwest specifics**:
- Midwest filed **121 Chapter 12 cases** in 2025 (~38 percent of national total), representing a **70 percent year-over-year increase**
- Individual state increases: Wisconsin +700%, Iowa +220%, Missouri +167%
- USDA projects total farm debt will rise to a **record $624.7 billion in 2026** (5.2 percent increase)
- Interest expenses expected to reach a **record $33 billion in 2026** — debt growing faster than assets; farm sector solvency "forecast to continue to worsen in 2026" (USDA ERS)
- The American Farm Bureau has declared a "generational downturn" for agriculture

The civic consequence of farm bankruptcy differs from urban eviction but produces identical democratic results through the same mechanism: forced property loss → geographic displacement → voter registration disruption in communities with minimal civic infrastructure to absorb the loss. The additional dimension: a farm bankruptcy in rural Wisconsin does not merely displace a family — it accelerates collapse of the local tax base, school district funding, and community institutional fabric through which civic life is organized. Rural communities where multiple farms fail simultaneously face a compound crisis: family precarity plus community institutional erosion.

Minnesota lost **1,300 farms in 2025** — a documented regional data point illustrating the scale of rural civic disruption.

Sources: [Investigate Midwest](https://investigatemidwest.org/2026/02/25/farm-bankruptcies-jumped-46-in-2025-as-debt-loads-and-costs-rise/); [American Farm Bureau](https://www.fb.org/market-intel/farm-bankruptcies-continued-to-climb-in-2025); [USDA ERS](https://www.ers.usda.gov/topics/farm-economy/farm-sector-income-finances/)

### Vector 2: Deindustrialization as Chronic Civic Infrastructure Erosion

MIT researchers identified approximately 150 cities — predominantly in the Northeast, Mid-Atlantic, and Midwest — that continue to struggle long after major plant closings. Youngstown OH, Flint MI, Gary IN, and Rockford IL represent an arc of communities where deindustrialization has produced not merely unemployment but the collapse of social capital.

The Burning Glass Institute ("The Midwest Is in a Vicious Cycle") documents: the Midwest suffers significant job and population losses as the historically manufacturing-dominant region enters a self-reinforcing "vicious cycle of economic, demographic, and educational decline." This connects deindustrialization to institutional capacity erosion — the union halls, community organizations, and civic institutions through which working-class participation was historically organized no longer exist in these communities.

The Youngstown State University Center for Working Class Studies documents: "Deindustrialization brings with it the collapse of social capital, which leads to family breakdown, loss of public trust, the collapse of local institutions, and rising rates of violence, drug abuse, depression, and suicide." These are the empirical antecedents of civic disengagement. The pathways from Pathway 4 (union decline) and Pathway 5 (organizational decline) manifest geographically in Midwest deindustrialized communities as an acutely accelerated version of the national trend.

Meatpacking consolidation is the current-cycle version of this dynamic: unionization in meatpacking fell from 90 percent (postwar) to **17 percent in 2025**. The relocation of meatpacking from Chicago union strongholds to smaller Midwest towns in the 1970s-1980s eliminated the union infrastructure and replaced it with precarious, often immigrant labor forces that face NLRA carve-outs (agricultural workers) or hostile enforcement environments.

Sources: [Burning Glass Institute](https://www.burningglassinstitute.org/bginsights/midwest-vicious-cycle-nd29p); [YSU Center for Working Class Studies](https://ysu.edu/center-working-class-studies/social-costs-deindustrialization)

### Vector 3: Rural Hospital Closures as Healthcare-Civic Participation Pathway

**Cox, Epp, and Shepherd (APSR, 2025)** established the causal link using 10+ million rural residents across 2016, 2018, and 2020 elections: individuals whose nearest hospital closed before the proximate election were **measurably less likely to vote**, with effects strongest for **older and lower-income residents**.

More than 200 rural hospitals have completely or partially closed since 2005. **18 additional rural hospitals** closed or shifted away from inpatient care in 2025 alone. Over **400 rural hospitals** — more than 20 percent of rural hospitals nationally — are at immediate risk of closure (Families USA / Chartis analysis).

**OBBBA rural Midwest impact**:
- Iowa: projected **54 percent reduction** in state-directed payments to hospitals under the Medicare rate cap between 2026 and 2034 (Commonwealth Fund)
- Michigan: projected **45 percent decrease** over same period
- Kansas and Oklahoma: 29 percent of rural hospitals in each state at **immediate risk** of closure in the next three years
- Families USA (June 2025): independent rural hospitals could lose an average of **$630,000 per facility** in patient revenue in 2026 from OBBBA Medicaid cuts — representing **56 percent of yearly net income** on average
- 380 independent rural hospitals across 26 states now at serious risk of closure — a 17 percent increase from 325 at risk in 2023
- Georgetown CCF (May 2026): "Rural hospitals and communities feeling impact of H.R. 1 Medicaid cuts"

Rural hospitals already in the red before OBBBA. NBC News reporting (May 2026): "already in the red, rural hospitals across the Midwest brace for Medicaid changes." The WPR and Iowa Public Radio coverage documents the anxiety in states like Iowa, Wisconsin, and Nebraska: administrators trying to model survival scenarios under projected revenue cuts while hospital boards simultaneously deal with existing operating losses.

The civic mechanism: hospital closure → acute health emergency becomes unmanageable → debt onset (medical debt pathway, above) → plus direct community signal that civic institutions are being abandoned → institutional trust collapse → political disengagement for communities already experiencing deindustrialization and farm bankruptcy.

Sources: [Cox/Epp/Shepherd APSR 2025](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/american-political-science-review/article/access-to-healthcare-and-voting-the-case-of-hospital-closures-in-rural-america/33E48DFAC6215DE273E7D80366B408B1); [Families USA](https://familiesusa.org/resources/federal-medicaid-cuts-would-force-rural-hospitals-to-the-brink-of-closure/); [Commonwealth Fund](https://www.commonwealthfund.org/publications/explainer/2026/feb/why-rural-hospitals-face-funding-crisis-how-it-could-get-worse); [Nebraska Public Media](https://nebraskapublicmedia.org/en/news/news-articles/already-in-the-red-rural-hospitals-across-the-midwest-brace-for-medicaid-changes/)

### Zone 5 Synthesis

The most acute democratic infrastructure failure in the country is found where all three Midwest-specific vectors intersect: communities that have lost their manufacturing employer (deindustrialization), their farm economy (agricultural bankruptcy), and their hospital (rural healthcare collapse). These are not hypothetical intersections — they describe specific congressional districts in Iowa, Wisconsin, Missouri, Indiana, and Ohio. Research in production should map which Midwest congressional districts sit at this intersection using USDA ERS farm debt data by county, Burning Glass deindustrialization mapping, and Chartis rural hospital closure risk maps.

The immigration dimension is an additional Zone 5-specific vector: ICE enforcement at Midwest meatpacking plants (Greeley CO, Omaha NE, Marshalltown IA) fractures workplaces of noncitizen workers who cannot vote but whose presence in communities affects the civic organizing infrastructure available to eligible-voter neighbors and family members. The Jacobin/Labornotes reporting documents this dynamic in 2025-2026.

## 2026 Movement and Policy Windows

### Active Policy Windows

**1. AFL-CIO/SEIU Merged Federation (January 2025)**
- 15 million members total; SEIU (2 million) rejoined after 20-year absence
- Primary legislative vehicle: Living Wage for All Act (April 2026 introduction) — $25/hour federal minimum wage; coalition: SEIU, NAACP, AFT, NEA, PolicyLink, One Fair Wage, CLASP, National Urban League
- Separate: Raise the Wage Act (H.R. 2743) phases to $17/hour — more likely vehicle in 119th Congress
- AFL-CIO 2024 Key Votes Survey confirms union voter mobilization infrastructure; union members 36% more likely to vote for union candidates; union workers are 1 in 5 voters in swing states
- **2026 window**: Election protection pre-midterm (August-November 2026); **January 2027 new Congress** is the legislative window for minimum wage push
- Sources: [AFL-CIO press release](https://aflcio.org/press/releases/seiu-joins-afl-cio-build-unprecedented-worker-power-win-unions-all-workers); [Roosevelt Institute](https://rooseveltinstitute.org/blog/a-united-labor-front/); [Living Wage for All Act](https://ramirez.house.gov/media/press-releases/ramirez-garcia-simon-mejia-workers-labor-leaders-introduce-living-wage-all-0)

**2. NLIHC "Our Homes, Our Votes" Campaign (Active 2026)**
- Campaign explicitly frames low-income renter turnout as a democratic participation issue
- **2026 mini-grant program**: Funding nonpartisan voter registration, canvassing, staff training, voter ID assistance, and civic education through housing organizations and tenant groups
- Application deadline extended to November 14 (for 2025 election cycle); 2026 midterm campaign infrastructure already launched
- This is the **most directly aligned** existing organizational infrastructure for Domain 59 distribution — the campaign already makes the housing-to-voting argument
- **2026 window**: August-November 2026 midterm voter turnout campaign
- Sources: [NLIHC Our Homes Our Votes](https://nlihc.org/policy-issues/our-homes-our-votes); [Mini-grant program 2026](https://nlihc.org/resource/nlihcs-our-homes-our-votes-campaign-launches-mini-grant-program-ahead-2026-elections)

**3. Undue Medical Debt Jubilee Programs (Active 2026)**
- **State programs collectively projected to relieve $15.6 billion** in medical debt for over 6 million low and moderate-income residents by end of 2026
- Total public funds dedicated: $120.7 million
- North Carolina: 2.5 million residents' debt erased, up to $4 billion total commitment
- Illinois: $400 million+ erased since program launch (Pritzker announcement, November 2025)
- Los Angeles County: $363 million erased for 171,000+ residents as of December 2025
- Delaware: $18.8 million for 18,145 individuals (FY2026 state budget investment)
- CFPB rule vacated July 2025; H.R. 4827 (Medical Debt Relief Act) pending — FCRA amendment is the active legislative fight
- **2026 window**: Every state legislative session in 2026-2027 is an opportunity to expand state medical debt programs to Midwest states (Ohio, Indiana, Michigan have not enacted); the civic participation restoration argument is the new framing angle
- Sources: [New School Budget Equity Project state programs](https://budgetequity.racepowerpolicy.org/reports/medical-debt-relief); [NPR North Carolina](https://www.npr.org/2026/01/21/nx-s1-5678541/north-carolina-undue-medical-debt-erased); [H.R. 4827](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/4827)

**4. Medical Debt Relief Act H.R. 4827 (119th Congress, Pending)**
- Would amend the Fair Credit Reporting Act to ban medical debt from consumer credit reports
- Senate companion: S. 2519
- Status: in first stage of legislative process; faces majority math obstacles in current Congress
- CFPB rule vacated July 2025 by Eastern District of Texas (exceeded statutory authority); voluntary credit bureau actions (removing collections under $500) remain
- Senate Republicans blocked medical debt protection (Legis1, 2025)
- **2026 window**: State legislative advocacy is the live fight; 16 states have enacted bans; every 2026-2027 state session is actionable
- Sources: [GovTrack H.R. 4827](https://www.govtrack.us/congress/bills/119/hr4827); [Medicare Rights Center](https://www.medicarerights.org/medicare-watch/2025/07/31/federal-court-reverses-federal-medical-debt-protections)

**5. NLIHC Medicaid Work Requirement Litigation and Advocacy**
- Nebraska May 1, 2026 implementation: first state, 25,000 residents facing coverage loss
- Georgetown CCF (May 2026): confirms rural hospital communities "feeling impact" — the implementation has begun producing documented harm
- KFF Medicaid Work Requirements Tracker: authoritative real-time source for state-by-state implementation
- **2026 window**: The gap between OBBBA passage (July 2025) and December 2026 mass implementation creates a 12-month legislative/litigation window. Court challenges to work requirements are pending; state-level advocacy to delay implementation is active
- Sources: [NPR Nebraska Day 1](https://www.npr.org/2026/05/01/nx-s1-5806260/trump-medicaid-work-requirements-nebraska-big-beautiful-bill-hr1-obbba); [CNN coverage](https://www.cnn.com/2026/05/01/politics/nebraska-medicaid-work-requirements); [KFF tracker](https://www.kff.org/medicaid/medicaid-work-requirements-tracker-overview/)

**6. Farm Aid / 2026 Farm Bill Reauthorization**
- Farm Aid's "Tracking the Farm Economy in Crisis" documents the Midwest agricultural crisis in real time; 315 bankruptcies in 2025, 46% increase
