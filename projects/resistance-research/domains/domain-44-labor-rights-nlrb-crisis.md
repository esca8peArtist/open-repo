# Domain 44: Labor Rights, Worker Organizing, and the NLRB Crisis

## The Central Argument

The right to organize is a democratic right. Workers without collective bargaining have no effective political voice in the economic decisions that most shape their daily lives — wages, working conditions, benefits, safety, scheduling. The National Labor Relations Board, created by the Wagner Act in 1935, is the institutional infrastructure through which that democratic right becomes legally enforceable. When the NLRB is disabled, restructured, or made politically captive, the result is not merely a labor policy disagreement — it is the destruction of a democratic participation mechanism for approximately 130 million private-sector workers.

The 2025–2026 attack on the NLRB follows the same pattern documented in Domain 39 (immigration enforcement), Domain 27 (higher education), Domain 43 (epistemic infrastructure), and Domain 42 (drug scheduling): identify an independent institution with a democratic accountability function, disable it through procedural means (quorum destruction, leadership removal, institutional restructuring), and use the resulting vacuum to shift power to the executive while eliminating the institutional check. In the NLRB case, the procedural mechanism was elegant and unprecedented: fire a sitting Board member during her term to eliminate the quorum requirement, paralyzing the Board for 345 days without any formal repeal of the statute.

This domain documents the NLRB as a democratic institution, the anatomy of its 2025 quorum crisis, the *Trump v. Slaughter* constitutional threat that would extend quorum destruction into a permanent structural feature, the state-level preemption architecture that prevents democratic majorities in cities from adopting labor standards, and a reform architecture for restoring the institutional infrastructure of labor democracy.

## Section A: NLRB Institutional Design and Democratic Accountability Function

### A.1 The Wagner Act Design: Labor Democracy as a Democratic Right

The National Labor Relations Act (NLRA), enacted in 1935 and commonly called the Wagner Act, was premised on an explicit democratic theory: that the concentration of economic power in employers, without countervailing worker organization, produces political inequality as well as economic inequality. Workers who lack bargaining power are not simply economically disadvantaged — they are politically disadvantaged. They lack the organizational infrastructure through which to translate their numerical majority into political power.

Senator Robert Wagner's floor statement in 1935 articulated this directly: the purpose of the Act was not merely to improve wages but to "extend the rule of democracy into the industrial realm." This language has been treated as rhetorical by subsequent labor law scholars, but it was operationally precise. The Act's findings section states that denial of collective bargaining rights burdens interstate commerce by "preventing stabilization of competitive wage rates and working conditions" and by "impairing the efficiency, safety, or operation of the instrumentalities of commerce." But the Act's deeper logic was democratic: workers who cannot organize cannot participate effectively in the economic decisions that govern their lives.

The NLRA created a tripartite structure:

**The Board** (five members, staggered five-year terms, appointed by the President and confirmed by the Senate, removable only for cause): A quasi-judicial body that adjudicates unfair labor practice charges, certifies union elections, and issues binding orders. The five-member structure with staggered terms was designed to insulate the Board from single-election political capture — a new president cannot immediately reconstitute the Board.

**The General Counsel** (four-year term, appointed by the President and confirmed by the Senate, removable at will): A separately constituted prosecutorial function that investigates and prosecutes unfair labor practice charges before the Board. The GC is formally independent of the Board — the prosecutorial and adjudicative functions are separated.

**Regional Offices** (26 regional and sub-regional offices): The front-line enforcement infrastructure. Regional offices receive and investigate charges, conduct elections, and can seek injunctive relief from federal courts. Regional offices can continue operating during a Board quorum crisis; they cannot issue final decisions on unfair labor practice charges.

The NLRA's removal protections for Board members — "for cause" removal standard — were designed to prevent exactly the situation that occurred in January 2025: a president using the threat of removal or actual removal to reshape the Board's ideological composition mid-term, outside the regular appointment cycle.

### A.2 What the NLRB Actually Does: The Democratic Participation Mechanism

The NLRB's day-to-day operations are less dramatic than its constitutional significance suggests. Its core functions:

**Union election administration**: The NLRB conducts secret ballot elections in which workers decide whether to be represented by a union. In a normal year, this involves approximately 2,000 elections covering 100,000–150,000 workers. Without NLRB elections, workers have no enforceable mechanism to establish union representation with the right to bargain.

**Unfair labor practice adjudication**: When employers illegally interfere with organizing (firing organizers, threatening workers, refusing to bargain in good faith), the NLRB processes charges, investigates, and can order reinstatement, back pay, and bargaining. In FY2024, the NLRB processed approximately 17,000 unfair labor practice charges.

**Collective bargaining enforcement**: Once a union is certified, the NLRB can order employers to bargain in good faith and can enforce first-contract obligations. Without this enforcement mechanism, employer delays and bad-faith bargaining can indefinitely defer meaningful bargaining even after workers vote for a union.

**The democratic participation function**: These operational functions are, in aggregate, the mechanism by which workers exercise democratic voice in economic governance. A union contract is not merely an economic instrument — it is a democratic agreement setting the rules under which work is governed. Collective bargaining extends democratic governance into the workplace. The NLRB is the institution that makes that extension legally enforceable.

## Section B: The Quorum Crisis — Timeline, Case Backlog, and Worker Impacts

### B.1 The Anatomy of the Crisis

**January 20, 2025**: President Trump is inaugurated. On the same day, Trump fires NLRB General Counsel Jennifer Abruzzo, who had served in the role since 2021. Abruzzo had significantly expanded the GC's enforcement priorities, including efforts to secure first contracts, organize graduate workers, and challenge employer captive-audience meetings. Her firing was within presidential authority — the GC is removable at will.

**January 27, 2025**: Trump fires NLRB Board Member Gwynne Wilcox. This was unprecedented. In the NLRB's 90-year history, no sitting Board member had ever been removed by a president before the end of their term. Wilcox was a Democrat appointed by Biden in 2021, with her term running through August 2028. Her firing immediately reduced the five-member Board (which had been operating with four members) to three members.

**February–March 2025**: One additional Board vacancy exists from a prior Republican appointment (Chairman Marvin Kaplan's term was set to expire August 27, 2025). With Wilcox gone, the Board now had three members: Kaplan, Republican member David Prouty (term through August 27, 2026), and one other. But the three-member quorum was technically met — the crisis was the ideological shift, not yet a quorum loss.

**August 27, 2025**: Chair Kaplan's term expires. The Board drops to two members — Prouty (Democrat) and one Republican. Two members is below the three-member quorum required for the Board to issue final decisions. The NLRB enters a formal quorum crisis.

**August 2025–December 2025**: The Board lacks a quorum for 345 days. During this period, the Board cannot issue final decisions on unfair labor practice charges. Regional offices continue to operate — they investigate charges, conduct elections, and file injunctive relief actions in federal court — but cases that require Board-level decision-making are stalled. Workers who have been illegally fired for organizing activity are left in legal limbo, their reinstatement orders unsigned.

**December 18, 2025**: The Senate confirms two Trump nominees to the NLRB: Scott Mayer (formerly Boeing's chief labor counsel) and James D. Murphy. This restores the three-member quorum.

**January 7, 2026**: Mayer and Murphy are sworn in. The Board formally resumes operations.

**Current status**: The restored Board operates through **August 27, 2026**, when member Prouty's term expires. At that point, unless the Trump administration appoints a replacement (and the Senate confirms), the Board will once again drop below quorum. The August 2026 quorum cliff is the next structural crisis point.

### B.2 Case Backlog and Worker Impacts

The quantitative impact of 345 days without a Board quorum:

**Union elections**: The CAP analysis found that NLRB-overseen union elections fell by **30%** in 2025 — from 2,124 elections in 2024 to 1,498 in 2025. Voter participation dropped **42%**, from approximately 142,000 workers in 2024 to approximately 83,000 in 2025. This is not solely a quorum effect — the Trump administration also restructured election procedures, replaced pro-worker regional directors, and created institutional uncertainty that chilled organizing. But the quorum crisis was the most dramatic single contributor: without a Board, election certifications were delayed and ULP charges involving election interference could not receive final resolution.

**Unfair labor practice backlog**: The Acting General Counsel issued a memo in January 2026 specifically addressing the "significant backlog of cases" resulting from the quorum lapse. The memo changed charge processing procedures to accelerate resolution of the highest-priority cases — particularly cases involving illegal firings of union organizers. This change acknowledges that the backlog is real and that workers whose reinstatement cases were pending waited nearly a year for Board-level action.

**Individual worker impacts**: Workers who are illegally fired for union organizing and who prevail in ULP proceedings are entitled to reinstatement with back pay. During the quorum crisis, those remedies were deferred. Workers who had been organizing, were fired in retaliation, won their Regional Office investigations, and had their cases forwarded to the Board for final decision — waited an additional 345 days, unpaid, while the Board could not act. For workers living paycheck-to-paycheck, this deferral was not merely a procedural inconvenience. It was an effective nullification of their statutory rights.

**The deterrence effect**: The effect of the quorum crisis was not limited to pending cases. Union organizers and their campaigns make rational calculations about risk: if you get fired for organizing, will the NLRB be able to remedy it in a reasonable timeframe? During 2025, the answer was effectively no. The chilling effect on organizing activity — campaigns abandoned before filing, workers declining to participate — cannot be quantified but is structurally certain. Employer interference with organizing is rational precisely when enforcement is known to be absent.

### B.3 The Staffing Crisis Compounding the Quorum Crisis

The quorum crisis was compounded by a parallel staffing crisis that reduced the NLRB's regional capacity. The 43-day federal government shutdown in early 2025 closed regional offices, preventing election certification during that period. NLRB regional staff — who investigate charges, conduct elections, and support Board proceedings — departed at an elevated rate throughout 2025, with staffing levels described as "low and getting lower" by agency sources. By August 2025, the NLRB had closed approximately 1,600 more cases than in the same period of the prior year, but many of these closures reflected case dispositions that did not serve workers' interests — withdrawal, dismissal, or deferred resolution — rather than successful enforcement.

The CAP analysis documents that the combination of the quorum crisis, the shutdown, the leadership change (new GC with management-side priorities), and the staffing degradation produced a structural enforcement collapse, not merely a slowdown.

## Section C: Trump v. Slaughter and the Constitutional Threat to Independent Agency Independence

### C.1 The Case: Humphrey's Executor at Risk

*Trump v. Slaughter* (No. 25-332), argued before the Supreme Court on December 8, 2025, presents the most significant constitutional threat to the NLRB's institutional independence since the Wagner Act's enactment.

The case originates in Trump's firing of two Federal Trade Commission Commissioners — Rebecca Kelly Slaughter and Alvaro Bedoya — in March 2025. Like NLRB Board members, FTC Commissioners are protected by a "for cause" removal standard: they can only be removed for "inefficiency, neglect of duty, or malfeasance in office," not simply at the president's pleasure. This protection derives from *Humphrey's Executor v. United States* (1935), in which the Supreme Court unanimously upheld the constitutionality of such removal protections for members of "quasi-legislative or quasi-judicial" agencies like the FTC.

*Humphrey's Executor* has been the constitutional bedrock for independent agency design for 90 years. It permitted Congress to create the NLRB, the SEC, the FTC, the FERC, the MSPB, and dozens of other agencies with multi-member boards protected from at-will presidential removal. If it is overruled, Congress cannot constitutionally create independent agencies of this type — every agency with removal protections would be subject to immediate presidential capture through at-will firing.

The legal question in *Trump v. Slaughter*: Do statutory removal protections for FTC Commissioners violate the separation of powers? And if so, should *Humphrey's Executor* be overruled?

### C.2 Oral Argument: The Conservative Majority's Signaled Position

The December 8, 2025 oral argument was widely described as proceeding badly for the independent agencies' position. Multiple indicators:

**Justice Gorsuch** called *Humphrey's Executor* "poorly reasoned" — the sharpest criticism, signaling he views the precedent as straightforwardly wrong and endorses overruling it.

**Chief Justice Roberts** described the precedent as "a dried husk of whatever people used to think it was" — acknowledging that the Court has already substantially narrowed *Humphrey's Executor* in *Seila Law LLC v. CFPB* (2020) and *Collins v. Yellen* (2021), suggesting further narrowing or outright overruling is consistent with the Court's trajectory.

**The SCOTUS stay**: Before oral argument, the Supreme Court had already stayed a district court order enjoining Wilcox's removal from the NLRB, concluding that the government was "likely to show that both the NLRB and MSPB exercise considerable executive power" — a signals-laden determination that the administration was likely to prevail on the merits.

Most post-argument analysis characterized the government as in a strong position, with the conservative majority likely to rule for the government, either by overruling *Humphrey's Executor* directly or by so narrowing it as to eliminate meaningful protection.

**Decision expected June 2026.** If the Court overrules or substantially narrows *Humphrey's Executor*, the consequences for the NLRB are direct and immediate:

1. NLRB Board members could be fired at the president's pleasure, at any time, for any reason. The staggered five-year term design becomes meaningless — a president can simply fire incumbent members and replace them immediately.

2. The quorum-destruction tactic used in January 2025 would no longer be a constitutional gamble (subject to challenge as Wilcox's case shows). It would be explicitly authorized.

3. Future presidents would have permanent structural control over the NLRB's composition, turning the quasi-judicial body into an extension of executive labor policy rather than an independent adjudicatory institution.

4. The democratic accountability function of the NLRB — its insulation from the political cycle to ensure consistent enforcement of workers' rights across administrations — would be eliminated by constitutional design.

### C.3 The NLRB Is Not the FTC: Why the Distinction Matters and Why Courts Are Ignoring It

The government's argument in *Trump v. Slaughter* focuses on the FTC, which the administration characterizes as exercising primarily "executive" power and therefore subject to full presidential control. The argument is that *Humphrey's Executor*'s distinction between "quasi-legislative/quasi-judicial" and "executive" agencies cannot be sustained, and that all agencies with significant policy-making authority are subject to the Vesting Clause's grant of executive power to the President.

**The NLRB distinction**: The NLRB is more clearly a quasi-judicial body than the FTC. The Board does not set policy through rulemaking — it adjudicates individual disputes, applying statutory standards to specific facts, in a manner directly analogous to a court. NLRB decisions have the procedural character of judicial decisions: they involve formal proceedings, examination of evidence, and findings of fact and law. This is precisely the kind of quasi-judicial function *Humphrey's Executor* was designed to protect.

Multiple legal scholars have argued that even if the FTC's removal protections are struck down, the NLRB's adjudicatory function is sufficiently distinct to survive under a narrowed *Humphrey's Executor*. But the SCOTUS stay in the Wilcox case — specifically finding that the NLRB "exercises considerable executive power" — suggests the Court is not inclined to draw this distinction.

## Section D: State Preemption of Local Labor Standards as Democratic Exclusion

### D.1 The Preemption Architecture: 44 States and Counting

One of the most consequential and least-discussed forms of democratic exclusion operates through state laws that prevent cities and counties from enacting their own labor standards. The Economic Policy Institute tracks this architecture through its Workers' Rights Preemption Map: as of 2026, **44 states have enacted laws preempting local minimum wage or labor standards legislation**.

The scope of preemption covers:
- **Minimum wage**: The most common form — states bar cities from setting minimum wages higher than the state minimum. In states like Alabama, Georgia, North Carolina, and Tennessee, cities cannot enact local minimum wage ordinances at all.
- **Paid leave**: Preemption of local paid sick leave and paid family leave ordinances.
- **Fair scheduling**: Preemption of local predictive scheduling requirements (advance notice of work schedules, penalty pay for last-minute schedule changes).
- **Prevailing wages and project labor agreements**: Preemption of local construction wage standards and union labor requirements on public projects.
- **Benefit standards**: Preemption of local mandates for retirement savings programs, healthcare contributions, and other workplace benefits.

### D.2 The Democratic Exclusion Mechanism

The preemption architecture is a democratic design problem, not merely a labor policy debate. Cities routinely have majority support for higher minimum wages and stronger labor standards — and many cities have enacted such standards when given the opportunity. Preemption laws override those democratic decisions.

The mechanism operates in three steps:

**Step 1**: Workers in a city with a majority favoring a $15 minimum wage exercise democratic voice through their city council or by ballot initiative, enacting the ordinance.

**Step 2**: The state legislature — representing a statewide constituency that is geographically and demographically distinct from the urban workforce that benefited — enacts a preemption law nullifying the city ordinance.

**Step 3**: Workers in the city find that their democratic decision has been overridden by a different democratic body representing a different constituency with different interests.

This is democratic exclusion in the formal sense: a majority's preference in a legitimate democratic process has been negated through a higher-level legal maneuver. The EPI analysis documents that preemption laws have "a disproportionate effect on workers of color, women, and immigrants" — the communities most concentrated in urban areas where local labor standards would apply, and the communities least represented in state legislatures.

### D.3 Who Drives Preemption and Why

The preemption architecture did not emerge organically. EPI and Public Citizen research documents that the American Legislative Exchange Council (ALEC) — a corporate-funded bill mill that provides template legislation to state legislators — has been the primary architect of preemption laws. ALEC's model preemption bills have been adopted in multiple states with minimal modification, using standardized language designed to comprehensively foreclose local experimentation.

The political economy is straightforward: corporations operating in multiple states prefer a uniform, low regulatory floor over a patchwork of local standards. Preemption converts local labor standards from a floor (cities can set higher standards) into a ceiling (cities cannot exceed state law). Franchise businesses — McDonald's, Walmart, Amazon's delivery network — are the primary beneficiaries. Their operational model depends on wage uniformity across locations; local minimum wage variation creates operational complexity and competitive pressure from local competitors who face the same wage requirements.

The ALEC preemption strategy is a direct response to the success of urban labor movements. Birmingham, Alabama enacted a $10.10 minimum wage ordinance in 2015; the Alabama legislature passed a preemption law within days, nullifying it before it could take effect. Seattle's $15 minimum wage movement catalyzed similar organizing in other cities; ALEC-affiliated legislators in multiple states moved to preempt the wave before it arrived.

### D.4 Preemption and the NLRA: The Federal Layer

State preemption of local labor standards operates in a complex relationship with federal NLRA preemption. The NLRA itself preempts state and local laws that regulate "the same conduct" that the NLRA protects or prohibits — a broad preemption doctrine (called *Garmon* preemption) that has historically prevented states and cities from creating parallel labor relations systems.

California's AB 288 (signed September 30, 2025, effective January 1, 2026) was an attempt to work around NLRA preemption during the Board's quorum crisis: it authorized the California Public Employment Relations Board (PERB) to enforce NLRA rights for private-sector workers when the NLRB "cannot or will not act." The NLRB immediately sued to block the law (October 2025), a federal district court preliminarily enjoined it (December 2025), finding that NLRA preemption likely precluded California's assertion of jurisdiction over private-sector labor relations, and the case remains on appeal.

The AB 288 episode illustrates both the depth of the problem (California was driven to create a shadow labor board because the federal one was disabled) and the preemption ceiling that limits the state response (the NLRA's own preemption doctrine constrains how far states can go to fill the federal vacuum).

## Section E: The Democratic Design Framework — Why This Is a Democracy Problem, Not Just a Labor Policy Problem

### E.1 The Analytical Parallel to Domain 42 (Drug Scheduling) and Domain 27 (Higher Education)

Domain 42 argues that the DEA's capture of the drug scheduling process is a democratic design failure: the institutional structure that should translate scientific evidence and democratic will into policy has been captured by an agency with enforcement-biased incentives. Domain 27 documents the attack on higher education as an attack on the institutional infrastructure of democratic deliberation: universities are where democratic societies produce the evidence base and the informed citizenry on which democratic governance depends.

Domain 44 adds a third instance of the same pattern: the attack on the NLRB is an attack on the institutional infrastructure of economic democracy. The right to organize is a democratic right. The NLRB is the institution that makes it enforceable. When the NLRB is disabled by quorum destruction, its independence is eliminated by *Humphrey's Executor* overruling, or its enforcement is undermined by a hostile GC and management-side Board, the democratic right to organize becomes formally available but practically inoperative — the same relationship between formal rights and practical access documented in voter suppression (Domain 1) and felony disenfranchisement (Domain 42, Section 3).

### E.2 The Feedback Loop: Labor Disorganization and Political Disorganization

The democratic theory of labor rights rests on a documented feedback loop: workers with unions vote at higher rates, engage more in civic life, and generate political pressure for policies that reflect majority economic interests. The political science literature on union density and political participation is extensive:

- Unionized workers vote at rates 5–7 percentage points higher than comparable non-union workers (Leighley and Nagler, 2007; Kerrissey and Schofer, 2013).
- Union membership is associated with higher civic participation broadly — volunteering, local political engagement, community organizational membership.
- Union political spending — through PACs and member mobilization — represents the primary countervailing force to corporate political spending in most legislative battles. As union density declines from its 1954 peak of 35% to its 2025 level of approximately 10%, this countervailing force diminishes proportionally.

The consequence: as NLRB enforcement weakens, union density declines; as union density declines, the political coalition for NLRB enforcement weakens; the feedback loop is self-reinforcing. This is the democratic design failure in systemic form.

The 30% decline in NLRB-overseen union elections in 2025 is not merely a labor market statistic. It represents 624 fewer opportunities for workers to exercise democratic voice in their workplaces. The 59,000 fewer workers who participated in elections in 2025 versus 2024 are workers who did not get to decide, through a democratic process, whether they wanted collective representation.

## Section F: Reform Architecture — Restoring the Institutional Infrastructure of Labor Democracy

### F.1 Independent NLRB Appointment Process

**The problem**: The current appointment process for NLRB Board members is the same as for any executive branch official — presidential nomination, Senate confirmation. This gives each presidential administration significant control over the Board's composition, even within the statutory five-year term constraints. A president who fires members midterm (as Trump did with Wilcox) can reshape the Board more rapidly than the staggered term design intended.

**Reform proposal**: Independent, bipartisan appointment commission for NLRB Board members, modeled on the merit-based judicial selection commissions used in multiple states. The commission would screen nominees for relevant expertise in labor law and adjudication, present a list of qualified nominees to the president, and require presidential selection from the list within a specified timeframe. This is not a novel design — it has been used successfully for independent redistricting commissions, merit selection judicial commissions, and equivalent bodies.

The appointment commission reform requires statutory change and cannot survive an adverse *Humphrey's Executor* ruling. It must be paired with the constitutional protection described below.

**Interim measure**: Statutory quorum requirement with automatic interim appointment mechanism. If the Board falls below quorum due to vacancy, mid-term firing, or term expiration without a confirmed replacement, the statute automatically extends the most recently departed member's term for up to 180 days, maintaining quorum continuity while the appointment process proceeds. This is the structural fix that would have prevented the 345-day quorum crisis.

### F.2 Constitutional Protection for Independent Agency Independence

**The problem**: *Trump v. Slaughter* may establish that removal protections for agency officials are unconstitutional, which would eliminate the existing "for cause" protection for NLRB Board members and make any statutory quorum fix unenforceable.

**Reform proposal**: Congressional statute explicitly grounding NLRB independence in Congress's Commerce Clause and Necessary and Proper Clause authority, rather than relying solely on *Humphrey's Executor*. The argument: Congress has plenary authority under Article I to establish the rules under which interstate commerce is regulated, including the institutional design of the enforcement mechanism. An NLRB whose members are subject to at-will presidential removal is, in Congress's judgment, an inadequate enforcement mechanism for a statutory scheme designed to protect interstate commerce from labor unrest.

This argument does not rely on *Humphrey's Executor* — it rests on Congress's own authority to design enforcement mechanisms it deems adequate. The Supreme Court would still have to rule on whether congressional design choices about enforcement mechanisms are constitutionally permissible. The case law on this is not favorable to independence (see *Seila Law*, *Collins*), but the argument is legally distinct from the *Humphrey's Executor* theory.

**The practical floor**: At minimum, Congress should pass a statute explicitly stating that NLRB Board members can only be removed for cause as defined in the original Act, that removal during term without cause is a violation of congressional design authority, and that in the event of an adverse Supreme Court ruling, the statue will be deemed severable such that other Board reforms remain operative. This creates a clear legislative record for future constitutional litigation.

### F.3 Sectoral Bargaining Framework

**The problem**: The NLRA's enterprise-level bargaining model was designed for a 1930s economy dominated by large manufacturing establishments with stable workforces. It does not function effectively in:
- Fragmented industries (home care, gig work, franchise restaurants) where workers are employed by many small employers in the same sector
- Industries with high turnover (retail, fast food) where workers leave before a certification election can be organized and won
- Industries where employers can credibly threaten to close, relocate, or franchise in response to organizing

In these sectors — which employ disproportionately low-wage workers, workers of color, and immigrant workers — the NLRA model produces near-zero union density not because workers don't want representation but because the enterprise model doesn't function.

**Reform proposal**: Sectoral bargaining framework, modeled on California's Fast Food Council (established by AB 257, the FAST Recovery Act) and Minnesota's Nursing Home Workforce Standards Board. Tripartite boards — composed of worker representatives, employer representatives, and a neutral government chair — set minimum standards (wages, scheduling, safety, benefits) for an entire industry. Standards apply to all employers in the sector, eliminating the competitive disadvantage that enterprise-level bargaining creates for individual unionized firms.

**What sectoral bargaining accomplishes that enterprise bargaining cannot**:
- Workers who move between employers within a sector retain their standards — portability of labor protections
- Small employers compete on a level playing field — no competitive advantage to the employer who keeps wages lowest
- Standards can be set above the legislative minimum but below what any individual employer would voluntarily accept, representing the genuine democratic preference of workers in the sector

**The NLRA preemption problem**: The NLRA's *Garmon* preemption doctrine limits state and local governments from creating labor relations schemes that regulate "the same conduct" the NLRA regulates. Sectoral standards boards that set minimum wages and working conditions without creating collective bargaining rights are generally found not to be preempted (*Chamber of Commerce v. Brown*). California's Fast Food Council survived initial preemption challenges. The Roosevelt Institute's "Labor Law Breaks Free" analysis (2024) identifies the conditions under which sectoral boards can be designed to avoid preemption — focusing on minimum standards rather than bargaining rights, and using public regulatory authority rather than private enforcement mechanisms.

**Federal reform**: The Richard L. Trumka Protecting the Right to Organize (PRO) Act (H.R.20, 119th Congress), reintroduced in 2025, would address NLRB enforcement failures through expanded remedies, faster election procedures, and first-contract arbitration. It would not create a sectoral bargaining framework. The PRO Act is the primary labor law reform vehicle in Congress and establishes the legislative record for future reform even absent current passage prospects.

## Section G: Comparative Analysis — Why International Evidence Matters

The United States is an outlier among wealthy democracies in its enterprise-level-only bargaining model. OECD data:

- **Germany**: Sectoral collective agreements (Flächentarifverträge) cover approximately 56% of workers through industry-wide negotiations between employer associations and trade unions. Even workers not formally covered by a union contract receive sectoral standards through extension mechanisms.

- **Denmark**: Approximately 84% of workers covered by collective agreements through a sectoral bargaining system. The Danish "flexicurity" model combines high flexibility for employers with strong income security for workers precisely because sectoral agreements set comprehensive standards.

- **Canada**: Provincial labor boards with independent appointment processes and statutory quorum protections have maintained significantly higher union density (28% in Canada vs. 10% in the US). Canadian provinces with strong labor board independence have higher union density, higher wages, and lower income inequality than Canadian provinces that have weakened labor board independence.

- **France**: The Grenelle system of national wage agreements, supplemented by sectoral agreements, covers nearly all workers regardless of union membership. Extension by decree makes negotiated standards universally applicable.

The international evidence converges on a finding that enterprise-level bargaining, without sectoral or national standards, produces declining union density and increasing inequality as economies fragment into smaller units and gig arrangements. The NLRB model is not merely failing — it is failing predictably, in the way that models without sectoral mechanisms predictably fail.
