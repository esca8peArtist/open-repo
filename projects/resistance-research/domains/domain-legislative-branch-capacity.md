## Executive Summary

The United States Congress was designed as the first branch of government — the repository of democratic legitimacy, the source of law, and the institutional check on executive power. What exists in 2025-2026 is a legislature that has been systematically hollowed from within and encircled from without. Four decades of staff expertise erosion have left committees dependent on executive branch information or industry lobbyists. Procedural manipulation by the current majority has produced the highest rate of closed rules in congressional history, blocking the minority from any meaningful role in shaping legislation. Support agencies — the Government Accountability Office, the Congressional Research Service, the Office of Technology Assessment (defunded in 1995 and never restored) — have been cut, threatened with cuts, and constrained from performing their oversight functions. And a new theory of executive supremacy, backed by a compliant House Republican majority, treats congressional oversight as optional and congressional appropriations as advisory.

The consequences are structural, not merely political. A Congress that cannot conduct independent analysis, that cannot access information from executive agencies, that cannot assemble expert staff to evaluate legislative proposals, and that cannot protect its minority members' basic rights is not a functioning legislative branch. It is a ratification machine for executive preferences — precisely the model that democratic backsliding theorists identify as the penultimate stage before institutional capture is complete.

**Key structural vulnerabilities:**
1. **Staff expertise collapse**: The congressional workforce has lost more than a third of its specialized capacity since 1979, while the complexity of federal governance has multiplied. Congress is outgunned by the executive it is supposed to oversee.
2. **Support agency assault**: GAO faces a proposed 49 percent budget cut paired with an explicit prohibition on impoundment enforcement — a provision designed to eliminate the only agency with standing to force spending of appropriated funds.
3. **Procedural capture**: The House majority issued closed rules blocking floor amendments on 84 percent of legislation in the first half of the 119th Congress. The five-month "single legislative day" declaration froze the minority's constitutional right to force floor votes on resolutions of inquiry.
4. **Oversight obstruction**: The administration has refused access to documents, denied agency visits to members, and shielded DOGE from congressional testimony — while the majority refused even to bring resolutions of inquiry to a vote.
5. **Ethics enforcement collapse**: The Office of Congressional Conduct was effectively shut down for months at the start of the 119th Congress through a deliberate refusal to appoint board members.

**Key finding**: Congressional institutional capacity is not a process question. It is a democracy question. A legislature stripped of expert staff, independent analytical support, procedural minority rights, and oversight access cannot perform the constitutional role that separates separation-of-powers government from executive rule. Rebuilding legislative capacity is a prerequisite for the recovery of every other democratic institution documented in this framework.

The reforms available are neither speculative nor unprecedented: mandatory staffing floors, restored support agency funding, minority rights statutory protection, and procedural rules anchored in standing rules rather than majority will have all been used in the United States or in comparable democracies. The political condition for enacting them is a congressional majority willing to invest in its own institutional power against the short-term interests of whoever controls that majority — the same willingness that made the Budget Act of 1974, the Legislative Reorganization Acts, and the creation of the CBO possible.

## 1. The Staffing Expertise Crisis: Congress Outgunned

### 1.1 Four Decades of Institutional Thinning

The decline of congressional expert capacity is documented across every relevant institutional metric and spans both parties. It is not partisan in origin; it is institutional in consequence.

Between 1979 and 2015, the Congressional Research Service lost 28 percent of its staff — 238 positions eliminated. In the same period, full-time standing committee staff shrank by 38 percent — a loss of 1,361 positions. GAO, the legislature's primary investigative arm, had its staff cut by 44 percent — 2,314 positions eliminated. The Office of Technology Assessment, which provided Congress with independent scientific and technical assessments, was defunded entirely in 1995 during the Gingrich revolution, at a moment when technology governance was becoming more complex, not less. These numbers come from the Foundation for American Innovation's analysis of congressional capacity, drawing on data published by the Congressional Management Foundation and academic studies of legislative staffing.

The CRS's operating budget in 2017 was $5 million less in real terms than its budget in 2010, despite a decade of expanding research demands. Total CRS staff fell from approximately 730 employees to around 600 over that period. The result, as a former CRS staffer documented in Washington Monthly, is "fewer analysts doing more work" — a production model that reduces the depth, independence, and quality of analysis available to legislators. Staff turnover has compounded the problem: the majority of congressional staffers come in and leave after two to three years, with 56.6 percent of House staff and 49.9 percent of Senate staff under 30 years old. Institutional memory — the accumulated understanding of legislative history, precedent, and agency behavior that makes oversight effective — is not being built.

The political economy of staff thinning is perverse. The more Congress reduces its own analytical capacity, the more it depends on two alternative information sources: the executive branch (which has its own interests) and K Street (whose clients have their own interests). The revolving door's scale confirms this dependence: 866 former members of Congress and congressional staffers moved from Capitol Hill to lobbying or related private sector roles in 2025 alone, a 60 percent increase over 2024 and a record that exceeded the prior high of 777 set in 2007. Connections that departing Hill staff maintain to their former coworkers "primarily drive their higher relative value as lobbyists" — meaning the expertise Congress fails to retain becomes the asset that industry uses to influence Congress. The "big lobotomy," as some researchers have called this pattern, is self-reinforcing: reduced capacity increases dependence on outside expertise, which accelerates the departure of inside expertise.

This analysis was systematized in the book *Congress Overwhelmed: The Decline in Congressional Capacity and Prospects for Reform* (LaPira, Drutman, and Kosar, University of Chicago Press), which documents how the collapse of internal expertise has systematically shifted power to the executive branch and well-resourced outside interests. As the Fulcrum's analysis summarizes the consequence: "legislative paralysis invites presidents to backfill governing functions through executive orders, waivers, reinterpretations, and expansive emergency authorities" — and each presidential expansion of authority further reduces legislators' subject matter competency, deepening the structural asymmetry.

### 1.2 The GAO Assault: Defunding the Watchdog

The Government Accountability Office performs a specific, quantifiable function: it investigates executive branch compliance with law, identifies waste and fraud, and — critically for congressional fiscal authority — enforces the Impoundment Control Act by bringing civil actions when the executive withholds appropriated funds. Domain 34 of this framework documents the ICA enforcement crisis in detail. This section focuses on what happened to the institution that performs that function.

In June 2025, the House Appropriations Committee, on a party-line vote of 34-28, approved an FY2026 Legislative Branch bill that would cut GAO's budget by 49 percent — from $828 million to $415.4 million — and simultaneously prohibit GAO from bringing civil enforcement actions under the ICA unless Congress first adopted a concurrent resolution authorizing such action. Library of Congress funding, which includes CRS, would fall by approximately 10 percent under the House bill.

The math of the proposed cut was devastating. GAO employs approximately 3,500 people. A 49 percent budget reduction would eliminate more than 2,200 positions — the largest single-event destruction of federal audit capacity in American history. In 2024, GAO identified $67.5 billion in efficiency recommendations and savings, returning approximately $76 for every $1 invested. The proposed cut would eliminate that return while the 39 open impoundment investigations — into whether the Trump administration had illegally withheld $425+ billion in congressional appropriations — were explicitly targeted for termination through the ICA enforcement prohibition.

Ranking Member Rosa DeLauro (D-CT) identified the political dynamic with precision: "It is astonishing that for all the talk about finding and rooting out waste, fraud, and abuse, that House Republicans would defund the watchdog that is tasked with precisely that role." The cut was, as the First Branch Forecast newsletter documented in detail, a direct response to GAO's impoundment investigations: the 49 percent cut was specifically calibrated to eliminate the investigative capacity that had been generating political embarrassment and potential legal liability for the administration.

Senate appropriators pushed back, with the Senate-proposed FY2026 bill maintaining GAO funding at $811.9 million — flat compared to the FY2025 enacted level. The ICA enforcement restriction was not included in the Senate draft. The divergence between House and Senate positions created negotiating pressure that, as of April 2026, had not yet produced a final enacted FY2026 Legislative Branch appropriations package. The uncertainty itself represented a governance failure: months into the fiscal year, the agencies responsible for congressional oversight operated without confirmed funding.

The H.R. 4229 provision — requiring a concurrent resolution before GAO could pursue ICA civil enforcement — was designed to transform an existing judicial enforcement mechanism into a supermajority political requirement. Under current law, GAO's Comptroller General has standing to bring civil actions to force agencies to comply with the ICA without seeking congressional permission. Under H.R. 4229, every individual enforcement decision would require House and Senate majorities — in a political environment in which one chamber is controlled by the same party protecting the executive from oversight. The effect would be to convert a legal enforcement mechanism into a political veto by the majority.

A coalition of research organizations, including the R Street Institute, warned in formal letters to Congress that the proposed cuts "would dramatically and irrevocably harm congressional capacity" — using language specifically calibrated to address conservative audiences concerned about institutional permanence.

### 1.3 The Office of Technology Assessment: A Defunding That Aged Badly

The Office of Technology Assessment was created by Congress in 1972 precisely to address the problem this domain analyzes: Congress needed independent scientific and technical expertise that was not controlled by the executive branch and not provided by industry. OTA produced 741 studies over 23 years on subjects including nuclear weapons policy, climate change, health technology assessment, and information security. Its budget in its final year was $22 million — roughly $0.06 per American.

In 1995, the new Republican House majority under Newt Gingrich defunded OTA as part of a broader package of self-inflicted congressional capacity cuts. The rationale was fiscal efficiency; the effect was structural disarmament. As the Foundation for American Innovation documented in its 2025 fact sheet, every attempt since to restore OTA — and there have been measures introduced in the 107th, 108th, 110th, 112th, 113th, 114th, 115th, 116th, 117th, and 118th Congresses — has failed.

In June 2025, when the House Appropriations Committee proposed its 49 percent GAO cut, a coalition led by Representatives Mark Takano, Sean Casten, Bill Foster, and Don Beyer reintroduced the Office of Technology Assessment Improvement and Enhancement Act, and 34 members signed a letter requesting $6 million for OTA restoration in the FY2026 appropriations bill. The Center for Study of Responsive Law, in a pointed July 2025 analysis, noted the bitter irony: "Cuts to GAO, Library of Congress underscore the need for Office of Technology Assessment" — the tools for independent technical analysis were being cut precisely when the complexity of AI governance, autonomous systems, and digital infrastructure made them most essential.

The absence of OTA means Congress routinely considers legislation on artificial intelligence, biotechnology, cybersecurity, and climate policy without access to independent technical analysis. Domain 36 of this framework documents the AI governance gap. The institutional analysis here is that the AI governance gap is not primarily a knowledge gap — it is a structural gap. The knowledge exists; what is missing is an institutional home for that knowledge that is accountable to Congress rather than to executive agencies or industry.

## 2. Committee Independence: The Architecture of Deliberation Under Assault

### 2.1 How Committee Power Works — and How It Has Been Dismantled

The standing committee system is the structural mechanism by which Congress exercises its core legislative and oversight functions. Committees hold hearings, subpoena witnesses, review agency programs, draft legislation, and mark up bills before they reach the floor. The autonomy of committees from floor leadership — the extent to which committees can initiate and conclude investigations independently of the Speaker or Majority Leader — determines whether legislative deliberation is genuine or performed.

That autonomy has been systematically eroded. As the Bipartisan Policy Center's analysis documents, the committee system has been weakened by the centralization of legislative power in the office of the Speaker, who controls the Rules Committee and therefore controls which bills reach the floor, under what conditions, with what amendment possibilities, and on what timeline. The number of subcommittees has been constrained by House Rules, which limit each full committee to five subcommittees (with specific exceptions for Appropriations, Armed Services, Foreign Affairs, and Transportation). In practice, this limitation reduces the granularity of committee expertise and concentrates decision-making power in full committee chairs who are themselves creatures of the majority leadership.

Committee staff resources have followed the same asymmetric pattern as overall congressional staffing. Majority committee staff receive substantially greater resources than minority staff, and the differential has widened. The Senate model, which provides a 60/40 majority-to-minority staff split as a floor, is more protective of minority capacity than the House model, where the majority controls committee staff allocations entirely. Bipartisan Policy Center reforms proposed embedding nonpartisan committee staff — analysts who would not face partisan turnover at the start of each Congress — as a structural protection for investigative continuity. No such protection currently exists.

The 119th Congress has seen these dynamics intensify. The Rules Committee — operating as the "speaker's committee," with the Speaker appointing every majority member — prohibited consideration of any amendments on approximately 84 percent of measures it sent to the floor in the first half of the 119th Congress. This represented 95 closed rules barring floor amendments, on pace to shatter the record of 115 closed rules set in the 118th Congress. Representative James McGovern, the top Democrat on the Rules Committee, stated that Speaker Johnson was "using the Rules Committee to limit congressional power and carry out President Donald Trump's agenda."

The consequence of blanket closed rules is not merely procedural. When members cannot offer amendments on the floor, the deliberative function of the full chamber is eliminated. Legislation passes in the form the leadership negotiated with the executive branch, without the opportunity for the full membership — including committee chairs with substantive expertise in the relevant area — to modify it. The floor becomes a ratification mechanism, not a legislative chamber.

### 2.2 Minority Party Rights: The Accelerating Assault

The Harvard Journal on Legislation documented in November 2025 what it described as "the accelerating assault on minority rights in Congress" — a systematic pattern of procedural changes that has eliminated or constrained the tools the minority party needs to force accountability, conduct oversight, and participate in legislative deliberation. The pattern spans both chambers and constitutes the most comprehensive restriction of minority rights in recent congressional history.

**Resolutions of Inquiry.** A resolution of inquiry is a traditional House tool by which any member can demand that the administration provide specific documents or information to Congress. Under House rules, a referred resolution of inquiry must receive a committee vote within 14 legislative days, and if the committee fails to act, the full House must consider it. This procedural mechanism creates a forced accountability window.

In April 2025, House Republicans blocked dozens of Democratic resolutions of inquiry, including resolutions seeking information about Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth's use of a commercial messaging application to transmit classified information about military strikes. Rather than simply voting these down, the Republican majority deployed a structural workaround: declaring the entire five-month period from April 29 to September 30 a single "legislative day," effectively freezing the 14-day clock for all resolutions of inquiry and preventing any forced floor vote. When this prohibition expired in October, the majority quietly extended it through spring 2026.

**Amendment Restrictions.** The record-setting use of closed rules has been accompanied by additional procedural restrictions. The current majority has moved to eliminate open and modified-open rules that allowed members — including majority members with committee expertise — to offer amendments germane to the bill. The effect is to transfer legislative drafting authority entirely to leadership, excluding the deliberative expertise embedded in the committee system.

**Senate Minority Erosion.** Senate Majority Leader John Thune invoked the nuclear option in September 2025 to allow sub-Cabinet nominees to be confirmed in groups rather than individually, eliminating the extended debate opportunity that had provided the minority with a check on rapid personnel changes in the executive branch. Senate Judiciary Committee Chairman Chuck Grassley refused to hold a recorded vote when Senator Cory Booker moved to postpone a circuit court nominee vote under Committee Rule 4 — a procedural departure from established bipartisan practice that converted a minority right into majority discretion.

The budget process provided additional procedural manipulation. In passing the One Big Beautiful Bill Act through budget reconciliation in 2025, Republicans adopted a "current policy baseline" that disregarded trillions in projected deficits when evaluating whether the bill complied with Byrd Rule requirements — effectively removing the Byrd Rule's budget scoring constraint from the reconciliation process through a methodological sleight of hand. The One Big Beautiful Bill Act passed the Senate 51-50, with Vice President Vance casting the tiebreaker, containing the $911 billion in Medicaid cuts documented in Domain 31 of this framework.

**The Seven-Member Rule Restriction.** House rules now require that committee chair approval accompany any request under the 1928 "seven-member rule," which previously allowed any seven members to request access to agency records. The chair approval requirement converts a member-level right into a majority-controlled gate, eliminating the rule's function as a minority-accessible oversight tool.

**OCC Shutdown.** At the start of the 119th Congress, Republican leadership delayed appointing members to the Office of Congressional Conduct's board and limited hiring of OCC staff to the first 30 days of the legislative session. The result was that the OCC lacked a quorum to approve investigations for months and was effectively shut down as an enforcement mechanism. Ethics advocates documented the pattern in Roll Call: "Nothing but a pattern: Ethics advocates alarmed by delay for House watchdog." The OCC's lack of subpoena power was compounded by its operational paralysis, leaving ethics enforcement to the House Ethics Committee — which operates as a political body under majority control.

### 2.3 The Joint Committee on Taxation: An Institutional Anchor Under Pressure

The Joint Committee on Taxation represents one of the most effective models of institutionalized nonpartisan expertise in Congress. The JCT staff — described in its organizational materials as "independent, tax-focused, and involved in all stages of the tax legislative process" — provides official revenue estimates for all tax legislation and interacts with members and staff on a confidential basis that enables it to maintain nonpartisan credibility across partisan boundaries.

The JCT's institutional design reflects lessons learned from earlier political manipulation of fiscal scoring. Its staff works across House Ways and Means and Senate Finance — both tax-writing committees — ensuring that the same technical analysis is available to both chambers and both parties. Its revenue estimates cannot be overridden or replaced by partisan alternatives, which constrains the ability of either majority to claim fiscal benefits for legislation that the JCT scores as costly. The JCT's 2025 work on the reconciliation bill's tax provisions — scoring the extension of the TCJA tax cuts and the SALT deduction cap modifications — provided the technical basis for cost-benefit analysis that the congressional debate could not have proceeded without.

The JCT model is important not because it is currently under assault (the research found no specific threats to JCT independence in the 2025-2026 period), but because it demonstrates what nonpartisan legislative expertise looks like when it is institutionally protected. The contrast with the GAO's vulnerability to budget retaliation, the CRS's funding erosion, and the OTA's decades-long defunding is instructive: JCT's protection derives from the fact that both parties' tax-writing members depend on it and have a direct institutional interest in maintaining its neutrality. The lesson for institutional design is that congressional support agencies are most durable when they are structurally integrated into the committees that need them, with both majority and minority members having direct stakes in their independence.

## 3. Legislative Process Protections: The Infrastructure of Democratic Lawmaking

### 3.1 The Rules Committee as Governing Instrument

The House Committee on Rules is constitutionally a regular standing committee but operationally the Speaker's instrument for controlling the legislative calendar. As the Congressional Research Service documents, the Rules Committee "determines the procedures under which legislation is debated and voted on in the House," with authority to create special rules that dictate how bills can be amended, how long they can be debated, and which amendments are germane.

The historical norm was a mix of open rules (allowing any germane amendment), modified-open rules (limiting amendments by category or subject), structured rules (allowing specific named amendments), and closed rules (prohibiting all amendments). Closed rules were historically used for tax legislation and other sensitive measures where public amendment processes created disclosure risks. Their use has now become the default posture.

The Foundation for American Innovation's Senate rules reform package, published in 2025, documented the parallel problem in the upper chamber: senators have fewer opportunities to participate in debate, offer amendments, and cast up-or-down votes than at any point in the Senate's modern history. The combination of leadership-controlled amendment trees — which senators can "fill" to prevent others from offering amendments — and the decline of the open-amendment deliberative process has reduced the Senate floor to a confirmation chamber for leadership-negotiated packages.

The budget reconciliation process has become the primary legislative vehicle for major legislation precisely because it bypasses these procedural constraints. Reconciliation requires only a simple majority in the Senate (evading the filibuster's 60-vote threshold), limits debate time, and restricts the amendment process through Byrd Rule scoring requirements. The One Big Beautiful Bill Act was the most consequential use of reconciliation in US history, enacting sweeping changes to Medicaid, taxation, immigration, and environmental policy with a 51-50 Senate majority, no floor amendments, and limited committee deliberation. The reconciliation vehicle's procedural constraints — passed via a budget resolution that itself required only majority support — became the mechanism for the most comprehensive partisan legislation in decades.

### 3.2 The Filibuster and Democratic Outcomes

The legislative filibuster's role in the current crisis is complicated. The filibuster has historically served as both a minority protection mechanism and an obstruction tool. As the Brennan Center's analysis documents, the filibuster has been used strategically to protect and to obstruct democratic governance — its use against civil rights legislation in the 1950s and 1960s represents its most historically damaging application.

In the current context, the filibuster's 60-vote threshold has been the primary structural constraint preventing the Senate majority from enacting additional partisan legislation beyond what can be moved through reconciliation. Senate Majority Leader Thune has explicitly committed to preserving the legislative filibuster, and multiple Republican senators — including John Curtis (R-UT) and Thom Tillis (R-NC) — have stated their opposition to its elimination. President Trump pressured Republicans to invoke the nuclear option to end the government shutdown in October 2025; Thune refused.

The filibuster reform debate illustrates the dual nature of procedural protections in a democracy: norms and rules that protect minority rights when one party holds the majority become obstacles to democratic governance when the other party holds the majority. The Brennan Center's analysis of filibuster mechanics identifies a key distinction: the *talking* filibuster (requiring sustained floor presence and debate) functions differently as a minority protection than the *procedural* filibuster (which requires only a threat and a cloture vote that fails). The current Senate operates primarily through the procedural form — senators need not physically hold the floor to block legislation.

Any comprehensive legislative capacity reform must address the filibuster's structural role without ignoring the minority protection function it serves. The international precedents (section 4 below) are instructive: effective parliamentary systems provide strong minority rights through committee structures, proportional representation in committee assignments, mandatory minority day requirements, and formal investigation powers — not through supermajority voting thresholds on all legislation. The filibuster substitutes an undifferentiated threshold for the kind of targeted minority protections that would actually serve deliberative democracy.

### 3.3 Oversight Power and Executive Non-Compliance

Congressional oversight authority — the power to investigate executive branch conduct, require testimony, obtain documents, and enforce legal compliance — has been comprehensively resisted by the Trump administration in ways that expose the structural weakness of congressional oversight mechanisms.

The documented pattern in 2025:
- Members of Congress were denied access to ICE immigration facilities, prompting a lawsuit against the agency (resolved against the administration).
- Senior officials refused to appear before committees or comply with subpoenas.
- DOGE refused to comply with FOIA requests, claiming not to be an "agency" subject to FOIA — a claim rejected by one district court but still pending appeal.
- Agency representatives refused meetings with congressional staff conducting oversight.
- The House Ways and Means Committee — the committee with jurisdiction over Treasury and Social Security Administration operations — voted down Democratic resolutions seeking documents on DOGE's access to those agencies' IT systems.
- The administration withheld apportionment data from GAO investigators reviewing ICA compliance — the data that would have confirmed or denied whether the $425 billion in withheld appropriations constituted illegal impoundment.

The structural weakness the pattern exposes: congressional oversight enforcement ultimately depends on the executive branch's own Department of Justice. Criminal contempt of Congress requires a DOJ prosecution; civil enforcement requires DOJ cooperation or a separate standing mechanism. Domain 29 of this framework documents DOJ capture as a standing crisis. The oversight problem and the DOJ problem are structurally linked: Congress cannot enforce its investigative authority against an executive that controls the prosecutors who would bring contempt charges.

The available structural fix — conferring standing directly on congressional officers (the Comptroller General, the Sergeant at Arms, or a newly created Congressional Oversight Officer) to bring civil enforcement actions in federal court without DOJ intermediation — is legally achievable but has not been enacted. Post-*Loper Bright*, if Congress specifies with precision the documents and information to which it is entitled, courts applying de novo review rather than executive-branch deference are more likely to enforce specific statutory mandates.

## 4. International Precedent: What Legislative Capacity Looks Like When It Works

### 4.1 UK Parliamentary Committees: Cross-Party Authority and Elected Chairs

The United Kingdom's select committee system provides the most directly comparable precedent for strengthening US congressional committee independence. Following recommendations from the Reform of the House of Commons Committee, the majority of UK select committee chairs are now elected by fellow MPs through a secret ballot — not appointed by party leadership. This structural change transformed committees from leadership instruments into cross-party accountability mechanisms.

UK departmental select committees exist in a one-to-one relationship with government departments and have broad mandates to scrutinize their departments' expenditures, administration, and policy. The Institute for Government's analysis documents that committees can "call any person, including government ministers and civil servants, to give oral evidence or provide written evidence in the form of documents and memoranda." While committees cannot legally compel ministers to appear — and some, notably Mark Zuckerberg before the DCMS Committee, have declined — the cross-party election of chairs creates a political dynamic in which chair authority derives from parliamentary legitimacy rather than leadership patronage.

The UK model's primary limitation is that committees "have no legal power to compel people to appear before them," and governments are not required to act on committee recommendations. The select committee system has strong deliberative authority but weak enforcement authority — a different pathology than the US system, where committees have formal subpoena power but majority control over when that power is used.

**Lessons for the US**: The election of committee chairs by the full chamber membership — rather than appointment by party leadership — would create chairs whose authority derives from broad legislative legitimacy rather than majority-leadership favor. This would not require constitutional amendment; it would require a rules change (as the UK reform was a parliamentary standing order change). Cross-party elected chairs would have both the mandate and the political incentive to conduct genuine oversight rather than protect administration allies.

### 4.2 German Bundestag: Wissenschaftliche Dienste and Proportional Committee Structure

The German Bundestag's institutional design addresses the expertise problem through two mechanisms: a professional parliamentary research service (the Wissenschaftliche Dienste) and a proportional committee assignment system that gives all Fraktionen (parliamentary groups) representation on committees in proportion to their parliamentary share.

The Wissenschaftliche Dienste (Scientific Services) are organized into eight specialized departments corresponding to the Bundestag's 25 standing committees. As of 2018, the service had approximately 100 employees, of whom about 65 were research analysts. Each year, the service processes approximately 4,300 requests from members of parliament, providing politically neutral analyses, status reports, and expert opinions. The critical institutional feature is that the service provides independent expert analysis to *all* members, regardless of party — not through a majority-controlled body. After a four-week exclusivity period for the requesting member, analyses are published on the Bundestag's website, creating a public record of parliamentary inquiry.

The Bundestag's committee system provides that committees can "take up matters on their own initiative and may deliberate on issues that fall within their terms of reference without referral from the plenary." This self-initiation power — absent in the US system, where committees act primarily on referrals — gives German committees genuine investigative independence. The Enquete-Kommission (inquiry commission) mechanism allows the Bundestag to create cross-party investigative bodies on complex policy questions, staffed with outside experts, that operate for multiple years and produce comprehensive reports. Notable examples include commissions on artificial intelligence (2018-2021), on climate change, and on cultural and media policy.

**Lessons for the US**: The Wissenschaftliche Dienste model provides a blueprint for reforming the CRS toward more universal nonpartisan access — a reform the Modernization Committee recommended and the OTA Improvement and Enhancement Act partially incorporated. The Enquete-Kommission structure provides a model for a standing congressional science and technology assessment mechanism that could replace the defunded OTA with a more flexible, committee-driven structure.

### 4.3 Canadian Parliamentary Budget Office: Independent Analysis With Legislative Accountability

Canada's Parliamentary Budget Officer (PBO) was established in 2006 under the Federal Accountability Act, creating an independent officer of Parliament with a mandate to provide independent economic and financial analysis to Parliament. The PBO provides what CBO provides in the United States — independent cost estimates of proposed legislation — but with a critical structural difference: the PBO is formally accountable to Parliament as a whole, not to the executive, and can independently initiate analyses of fiscal policy questions without waiting for a legislative request.

The PBO's 2019 independence enhancement legislation clarified that the officer could release reports publicly even when parliamentary leaders preferred otherwise — a self-executing transparency mechanism that prevents political suppression. The PBO's published analyses of COVID-19 relief expenditures, pharmacare cost scenarios, and Indigenous infrastructure gaps have established it as an authoritative independent voice in Canadian budget debates.

The Canadian model is more parliamentarized than the US CBO — operating in a Westminster-adjacent system where the executive can govern with legislative majorities rather than separated branches — but the design principle is transferable: an independent fiscal institution with self-initiation powers, public reporting mandates, and insulation from political direction provides more durable and more credible analysis than an agency that reports to majority leadership.

**Lessons for the US**: The Domain 34 analysis proposes a US Fiscal Council modeled on international IFI precedents. The Canadian PBO adds a legislative-accountability dimension to that proposal: independent fiscal analysis should be constitutionally and institutionally accountable to the *legislature as a whole*, not to the majority party, with public reporting requirements that prevent executive or majority suppression of inconvenient findings.

### 4.4 European Parliament: Institutional Research Capacity and Committee Sovereignty

The European Parliament's institutional design offers the most ambitious model of parliamentary research capacity. The European Parliamentary Research Service (EPRS) provides comprehensive analysis to MEPs and committees across all 85 policy areas in which the ordinary legislative procedure applies. The EPRS employs over 200 professional staff producing briefings, in-depth analyses, comparative studies, and legislative observatory reports on pending legislation.

The EP's committee structure is proportional across 20 standing committees and three subcommittees, with committee assignments reflecting the proportional strength of the seven political groups in Parliament. No political group can dominate committee assignments in the way the US Speaker's party dominates House committee assignments. The EP's co-decision procedure requires Parliamentary approval for the vast majority of EU legislation — making EP committees genuine legislative actors whose modifications to Commission proposals must be negotiated, not simply accepted or rejected.

The EP's oversight powers include the ability to elect and censure the European Commission, to scrutinize the EU budget through the discharge procedure, and to constitute committees of inquiry into violations of EU law. The inquiry mechanism is particularly notable: any EU citizen can submit a petition to Parliament about EU law compliance, and Parliament can create a formal inquiry committee that investigates violations by member states. This external accountability mechanism — connecting citizen concerns directly to parliamentary investigation — provides a model for broadening congressional oversight access beyond member-initiated requests.

The EP's 2025 study on oversight powers documented the Parliament's "range of supervisory and control powers" as a framework for accountability across the EU institutional architecture. The study's analysis of the relationship between formal powers and practical enforcement — committees have strong formal powers but variable practical capacity to compel compliance — mirrors the US structural problem: formal authority without enforcement mechanisms produces oversight theater, not accountability.

**Lessons for the US**: The EPRS model demonstrates that a professional parliamentary research service can operate at scale (200+ staff, 85 policy areas) while maintaining analytical independence. The EP's proportional committee model provides a benchmark for what minority representation in the US committee system could look like if structured around deliberative function rather than partisan gatekeeping.

## 5. Recovery Pathways: Structural Reforms for Congressional Institutional Independence

The recovery pathways below are organized by the distinction between *self-enforcing* and *constituency-dependent* mechanisms developed in Phase 3 Candidate 5's taxonomy of fiscal architecture. Self-enforcing mechanisms — mandatory funding floors, automatic publication requirements, statutory minimum staffing levels — do not depend on sustained political will to function once enacted. Constituency-dependent mechanisms — voluntary bipartisan agreements on committee procedures, informal norms of minority respect — can function when good will is present but fail predictably when it is not.

### 5.1 Immediate Priority: Legislative Branch Support Agency Protection Act (0-18 months)

**Problem**: GAO, CRS, and the Library of Congress face budget threats driven by the majority's interest in eliminating oversight capacity that creates political costs. No statutory minimum funding protection exists; annual appropriations create recurring vulnerability.

**Proposed mechanism**: A Legislative Branch Support Agency Protection Act would:
- Establish a mandatory minimum annual appropriation for GAO at no less than 0.005 percent of total federal outlays (approximately $300 million at current spending levels), automatically adjusted for inflation, with mandatory sequestration of OMB operating budget funds as a remedy if the statutory floor is not appropriated
- Establish a mandatory minimum appropriation for CRS at no less than $600 million annually (roughly equal to current funding), similarly inflation-adjusted and protected by sequestration remedy
- Affirm the Comptroller General's ICA civil enforcement authority explicitly, preempting any requirement for concurrent resolution authorization, and conferring standing in 28 U.S.C. § 1331 courts without DOJ intermediation
- Protect the tenure of the Comptroller General (currently 15-year single term) by requiring a two-thirds majority vote of both chambers for removal, rather than the current simple majority via joint resolution

**Self-enforcing elements**: The mandatory funding floor with automatic sequestration remedy creates a structural incentive for compliance that does not depend on majority goodwill. The Comptroller General removal threshold converts a simple-majority vulnerability into a supermajority protection.

**Political economy**: This bill's revenue framing — GAO returns $76 for every $1 invested; IRS enforcement cuts cost $861 billion in deficits — provides a bipartisan deficit-reduction argument that reaches conservative fiscal hawks. The R Street Institute coalition letter opposing the House cuts, signed by cross-ideological organizations, demonstrates that this framing has real constituency support.

**Legislative vehicle**: The ICA has already been the subject of reform proposals (Domain 34, Section 5.2). This act would complement ICA standing reform with institutional protection for the agency that enforces it.

### 5.2 Congressional Staff Capacity Restoration Act (0-24 months)

**Problem**: Congressional staff expertise has declined by 28-44 percent across support agencies and committee staff since 1979, while the complexity of federal governance has multiplied. Turnover is endemic. The revolving door extracts expertise from Congress and monetizes it against Congress's interests.

**Proposed mechanism**: A Congressional Staff Capacity Restoration Act would:
- Establish mandatory minimum committee staff levels: each standing committee must maintain no fewer than 20 full-time professional staff (currently many operate with far fewer), with a 60/40 majority-to-minority staff split as a floor (the Senate model)
- Create a Congressional Career Staff Program: a nonpartisan career civil service track for committee staff, providing GS-scale salaries, full federal benefits, and professional development pathways, modeled on the UK House of Commons committee staff model. Career staff would not rotate at the start of each Congress.
- Restore OTA funding: appropriate no less than $50 million annually for a reconstituted Office of Technology Assessment, governed by a bipartisan Technology Assessment Board with appointment authority divided between majority and minority leadership in both chambers, and with CRS-style member access to analyses (without requiring full Board review for shorter briefings)
- Address the revolving door through a cooling-off period: a three-year period during which former committee staff above GS-13 equivalent cannot represent clients before the committee on which they served, closing the informational arbitrage that drives the revolving-door premium

**Self-enforcing elements**: Mandatory minimum staffing levels with automatic appropriations adjustments and a career track that is structurally insulated from partisan rotation.

**Legislative precedent**: The 116th Congress's Select Committee on the Modernization of Congress produced 97 unanimous recommendations across 12 categories of congressional operations, including human resources reforms, technology improvements, and CRS access expansion. The 2020 final report provides a bipartisan legislative foundation. The Close the Revolving Door Act of 2025 (H.R. 3554) introduced in the 119th Congress addresses the staffing-to-lobbying pipeline specifically.

**Timeline feasibility**: Staff restoration requires immediate funding and hiring; the expertise rebuilding takes 2-5 years. The bipartisan nature of the 2019-2022 Modernization Committee recommendations provides a basis for enactment in a period of divided government.

### 5.3 Committee Independence and Minority Rights Act (12-36 months)

**Problem**: House majority leadership exercises near-total control over the legislative calendar, committee proceedings, and floor amendment processes, eliminating minority participation in governance and converting committees into ratification bodies for leadership-negotiated packages.

**Proposed mechanism**: A Committee Independence and Minority Rights Act would codify procedural protections in statute rather than leaving them to majority-controlled rules packages:
- **Elected committee chairs**: Require that standing committee chairs be elected by the full chamber membership through secret ballot rather than appointed by majority leadership, implementing the UK select committee model within the US partisan framework (chairs would still be nominated by the relevant party caucus but confirmed by full chamber vote)
- **Open rules as default**: Establish a statutory presumption that all legislation reported from committee be considered under open or modified-open rules, with closed rules requiring a separate affirmative vote of the full chamber. This creates political visibility for procedural manipulation that currently occurs invisibly within the Rules Committee
- **Minority day mandate**: Require that for every hearing convened by a committee chair, minority members receive a corresponding minority day with their own witnesses, within 21 days of the majority hearing — giving the minority day requirement the force of statute rather than the fragile protection of chamber rules
- **Resolution of inquiry protection**: Prohibit manipulation of legislative day counting to freeze resolution of inquiry clocks; any period during which the House is in session shall count toward the 14-day referral period, closing the "single legislative day" manipulation documented in the 119th Congress
- **Seven-member rule restoration**: Restore the seven-member rule's access to agency records as a member-level right, removing the chair approval requirement imposed in the 119th Congress

**Self-enforcing elements**: Statutory codification converts majority-controlled procedural determinations into legally enforceable rights. Courts can enforce specific statutory requirements with de novo review post-*Loper Bright*; the challenge will be establishing congressional standing to bring enforcement actions.

**Standing solution**: Conferring enforcement standing on individual members (for minority-day and resolution-of-inquiry violations), or on a newly created House Minority Ombudsman, would route enforcement through the courts without requiring DOJ cooperation. The *Raines v. Byrd* problem (congressional member standing) can be addressed by designating a specific officer with institutional standing rather than individual members.

### 5.4 Oversight Enforcement Reform (12-48 months)

**Problem**: Congressional oversight enforcement depends on DOJ cooperation for criminal contempt and on executive goodwill for civil compliance. Both mechanisms have failed in the current environment.

**Proposed mechanism**: An Oversight Enforcement Act would create independent enforcement pathways:
- **Congressional Oversight Officer**: A new officer of Congress, appointed by bipartisan leadership of both chambers (requiring majority vote in each), with independent standing to bring civil actions in federal court to enforce congressional subpoenas and document requests, without DOJ intermediation. Modeled on the UK Parliamentary Commissioner for Standards
- **Automatic contempt referral**: Where an executive branch official fails to comply with a properly issued committee subpoena within 30 days, an automatic civil enforcement action is initiated by the Congressional Oversight Officer in the D.C. Circuit, without requiring a committee vote to authorize it
- **Agency access mandate**: Codifying a member's statutory right to access federal facilities for oversight purposes, with a private right of action for members denied access, closing the ICE access dispute as a structural matter
- **Public disclosure requirement**: Requiring executive branch agencies to publish within 72 hours any refusal to comply with a congressional document request or testimony request, creating automatic transparency that cannot be suppressed

**Self-enforcing elements**: Automatic civil referral, mandatory public disclosure, and the Congressional Oversight Officer's independent standing create enforcement mechanisms that do not depend on majority committee chairs authorizing subpoenas or DOJ prosecuting contempt.

**Post-*Loper Bright* advantage**: More specific statutory language mandating document production would be directly enforceable by courts applying the "best reading" standard. Courts reviewing agency refusals to comply with precisely specified congressional requests would no longer defer to executive branch characterizations of its obligations.

### 5.5 Ethics Enforcement Independence (0-18 months)

**Problem**: The Office of Congressional Conduct was effectively shut down through a deliberate refusal to appoint board members at the start of the 119th Congress. The OCC lacks subpoena power. The House Ethics Committee, which is the final enforcement body, operates as a political committee under majority control.

**Proposed mechanism**: An Ethics Enforcement Independence Act would:
- Require OCC board appointments within 30 days of the start of each Congress, with appointments divided equally between majority and minority leadership designees, and automatic triggering of an appointment commission if appointments are not made within the deadline
- Confer independent subpoena authority on the OCC, with enforcement through the federal courts rather than requiring Ethics Committee referral
- Create automatic publication of any investigation that is open for more than 6 months, with quarterly status reports, preventing the majority from using inaction to protect favored members from ethics exposure
- Establish Senate ethics committee independence through a bipartisan supermajority appointment requirement analogous to the OCC structure, replacing the current system in which the Senate Ethics Committee is controlled by members appointed by leadership of both parties without structural independence requirements

**Self-enforcing elements**: Automatic publication mandates, time-triggered appointment commissions, and independent subpoena authority convert majority-controlled discretion into statutory obligations. The Campaign Legal Center's analysis confirms that strengthening ethics enforcement law through statutory rather than rules-based mechanisms is both constitutional and achievable.

### 5.6 The Office of Technology Assessment Restoration Act (0-18 months)

**Problem**: Congress has been without independent scientific and technical analysis for 30 years, operating on AI governance, biotechnology, climate policy, cybersecurity, and nuclear weapons without an institutional mechanism for independent expert assessment.

**Proposed mechanism**: A reconstituted OTA Act would:
- Restore OTA with a $75 million annual appropriation (the original $22 million budget, inflation-adjusted)
- Govern OTA through a bipartisan Technology Assessment Board with members appointed by majority and minority leadership in both chambers (ensuring neither party can capture the institution)
- Create a CRS-style member access model: any member can request technical briefings, analysis, and assessments; Board review is required only for multi-year major assessments; shorter products are available on request
- Mandate public availability of all OTA products within 30 days of delivery to the requesting member, creating a public knowledge commons
- Establish formal relationships with AAAS, the National Academies, and the Congressional Budget Office for joint technical expertise on cross-domain issues

**Evidence base**: The 116th Congress Modernization Committee unanimously recommended OTA restoration as one of its 10 technology recommendations. The Office of Technology Assessment Improvement and Enhancement Act has been introduced in the 118th and 119th Congresses with bipartisan cosponsors. The $22 million annual cost — inflation-adjusted to $75 million — remains the most cost-effective investment in congressional institutional capacity available.

## 6. Implementation Dependencies and Sequencing

### 6.1 The Political Condition

All five reform pathways require a congressional majority willing to invest in institutional capacity against the short-term political interest of the majority party — a form of constitutional self-restraint that is historically rare but not unprecedented. The Congressional Budget and Impoundment Control Act of 1974 (which created CBO and established the appropriations enforcement framework), the Legislative Reorganization Acts of 1946 and 1970, and the creation of the GAO were all enacted by majorities that recognized institutional capacity as a common good worth protecting.

The political economy argument is strongest on staff capacity and support agency funding: these reforms have bipartisan supporters (the Modernization Committee's unanimous recommendations demonstrate it), clear ROI arguments (GAO's $76 return per dollar), and cross-institutional support (the R Street Institute coalition letter). The minority rights reforms are more contested, as they directly constrain majority prerogatives. But the long-term incentive structure is favorable: every reform that strengthens institutional capacity constrains *all* future majorities — including future Democratic majorities who might otherwise exploit the same procedural shortcuts being exploited now.

### 6.2 Sequencing

**Immediate window (0-18 months)**: OTA restoration and GAO protection are the highest-impact, lowest-controversy reforms. Both have bipartisan support, clear evidence bases, and immediate practical effect. The OTA funding ask ($75 million) is smaller than the annual savings from a single GAO investigation. The GAO protection package addresses an ongoing active threat.

**Medium term (12-36 months)**: Staff capacity restoration requires appropriations action and structural HR changes. The career committee staff program requires administrative build-out. The revolving-door cooling-off period requires clean legislative drafting that survives constitutional challenge.

**Structural reforms (24-60 months)**: Committee chair election, open-rules default, and minority rights codification require rules changes or statute that the current majority would oppose. These reforms are the recovery agenda for the next congressional majority — they must be designed and drafted in advance so that when a governing window opens, the legislative blueprint is ready.

### 6.3 Connection to the Democracy Recovery Framework

Congressional capacity is not a standalone issue. It is the institutional infrastructure for every other reform in this 38-domain framework.

- Without a GAO that can investigate ICA violations, Domain 34's fiscal authority restoration proposals have no enforcement mechanism.
- Without a CRS that can analyze proposed judicial nominations, Domain 6's judicial independence analysis is unavailable to the members who would vote on confirmations.
- Without committee staff that can evaluate OLC memos, Domain 28's war powers oversight is practically impossible regardless of the formal authority that exists.
- Without minority rights to force hearings and investigation, Domain 29's DOJ capture accountability proposals have no legislative pathway.
- Without an OTA providing technical analysis, Domain 36's AI governance reform proposals will be written by industry lobbyists rather than independent experts.

The structural vulnerability is not that Congress lacks authority — it has extensive statutory and constitutional authority to conduct oversight, appropriate funds, and legislate. The vulnerability is that Congress lacks the institutional capacity to *exercise* that authority effectively. Rebuilding capacity is the prerequisite for exercising authority. That is the logic of this domain's placement as Phase 3 Candidate 5: it addresses the enabling conditions for the entire resistance framework.
