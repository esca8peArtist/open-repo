## Executive Summary

The Trump administration has mounted the most systematic federal encroachment on election administration infrastructure in American history, operating across five simultaneous vectors: (1) a March 31, 2026 executive order directing the Postal Service to restrict mail ballot delivery to a DHS-verified citizenship list — constitutionally unauthorized and facing injunctions in at least 15 states; (2) the wholesale installation of approximately two dozen election deniers into DHS, CISA, DOJ, and White House positions overseeing election security — with the 36-person CISA election security workforce gutted and replaced with officials whose prior public record is challenging the legitimacy of the 2020 result; (3) a parallel DOJ voter file litigation campaign against 30+ states using the SAVE system — documented at an 81 percent false positive rate — to flag naturalized citizens as potential non-citizens for expedited removal from voter rolls under 45-day agreements that violate NVRA's two-election-cycle notice requirement; (4) a Supreme Court case (*Watson v. RNC*, argued March 23, 2026) that appears poised to eliminate grace periods for late-arriving mail ballots across 14 states plus D.C., with a ruling expected by late June that will give state officials months, not years, to reconstruct mail voting infrastructure before November; and (5) a record wave of election official turnover — 50 percent of chief local election officials in Western states have left their positions since 2020, and 4 in 10 local offices will be run by people who have never administered a national election.

The operational consequence is a fractured federal-state trust relationship with no near-term repair pathway. Arizona Secretary of State Adrian Fontes declined to notify CISA when a suspected Iranian cyberattack compromised his state's election website in summer 2025, stating he "just can't trust that this administration takes election security seriously." Maine Secretary of State Shenna Bellows describes the trust between the Trump administration and states as "absolutely demolished." Colorado added dedicated legal counsel to fight administration lawsuits. Georgia now routes election security functions through the state Emergency Management Agency rather than CISA. Pennsylvania relies on private contractors. The national coordination function that CISA provided since 2017 — real-time threat sharing, the Election Day situation room, the 1,300 annual physical security assessments — has not been replaced.

The vulnerability matrix is not uniform. The states most at risk — those combining electoral competitiveness with degraded election security infrastructure, election-denying candidates running for certification authority, and restrictive legislation enacted in 2025 — are the same states whose results will determine House and Senate control. Arizona, Georgia, Michigan, Nevada, North Carolina, and Wisconsin face compounded risks across multiple vectors simultaneously. The six months between now and November 2026 contain five critical legal and operational deadlines, each of which creates either a threat or a window for defensive action.

The international comparison is instructive on both failure and success: Hungary's April 2026 election demonstrated that sophisticated electoral manipulation can be overcome by sufficient mobilization — Orbán lost his 16-year hold on power to a 53.6 percent opposition landslide — but also documented that structural dismantlement of independent election administration (media ownership concentration, electoral map manipulation, resource asymmetry) creates durable disadvantage that mobilization must actively overcome, not merely match.

The legal resistance is organized, resourced, and winning the majority of its cases. Courts have permanently blocked at least six provisions of the two Trump elections executive orders. Twenty-three state AGs have filed coordinated suits. Democratic secretaries of state are conducting tabletop exercises simulating ballot seizure, military deployment to polls, and mail system disruption. The legal framework is clear: the Elections Clause (Article I, Section 4) reserves election administration to states and Congress, not the president.

But legal wins and operational defense are not the same thing. The advocacy windows identified in this analysis — the May 30 voter roll consent decree deadline, the June/July *Watson* ruling, the August 7 NVRA quiet period, and the September–October certification season — each require advance preparation to be effective. The organizations and officials best positioned to act on these windows are identified in Section VI.

## Part 1: Threat Diagnosis — The Architecture of Federal Seizure

### 1.1 The March 31, 2026 Executive Order: Unprecedented Presidential Claim Over Election Administration

On March 31, 2026, President Trump signed an executive order titled "Ensuring Citizenship Verification and Integrity in Federal Elections" — the second major elections EO of his second term, following the March 2025 order that courts had partially blocked. The new order makes three structurally unprecedented claims:

**The Citizenship List.** The order directs the Secretary of Homeland Security, within 90 days, to "establish the infrastructure necessary to compile, maintain, and transmit the State Citizenship List" — a federally constructed registry of verified U.S. citizens in each state, cross-referenced against SSA citizenship and identity data. The USPS would then be directed to deliver mail ballots only to voters appearing on that list.

**The USPS Mail Ballot Restriction.** The order directs the Postal Service to provide states with a "Mail-In and Absentee Participation List" — individuals enrolled for mail ballots — with unique ballot envelope identifiers. The constitutional problem is foundational: Congress, not the President, controls the Postal Service (Article I, Section 8), and the president has no authority to direct USPS operational decisions regarding mail delivery. Federal law explicitly prohibits USPS from selectively refusing mail delivery.

**Prosecution Threats Against Election Officials.** The order instructs the DOJ to prosecute election officials who count ballots not verified through the federal citizenship process — a direct threat to state election administrators who apply their own state law verification procedures.

On April 3, 2026, New York AG Letitia James, joined by 22 other attorneys general and Pennsylvania's governor, filed suit to block the order. As of early May 2026, federal agencies have not begun implementation, citing ongoing litigation. The Postal Service acknowledged it "has not yet published a proposed rule, nor reached any final decisions" on implementation. Courts have ordered the administration to file its response by May 7, 2026.

**What has already been permanently blocked.** The Brennan Center's litigation tracker documents that courts have permanently blocked at least six provisions of the two Trump election executive orders:

- Section 2(a) of the 2025 order: "Show your papers" citizenship documentation requirement for voter registration — permanently blocked in three separate courts
- Section 2(d): Federal agency assessment of citizenship before providing registration forms — permanently blocked for DOD, VA, Interior, and SBA
- Section 3(d): Passport requirement for overseas and military voters — permanently blocked (LULAC court)
- Section 4(b): Voting machine guidelines revision and rescission of certifications — blocked; court found the president lacks authority over election administration standards
- Sections 7(a) and 7(b): AG enforcement actions and funding denial to states with mail ballot receipt grace periods — blocked in 15 states including Arizona, California, Colorado, Michigan, Pennsylvania, and Wisconsin

What is not yet fully resolved: the DHS SAVE database expansion (proceeding in 12 compliant states), the EAC funding conditioning (blocked only in Oregon and Washington), and the entire architecture of the March 31, 2026 order.

### 1.2 The SAVE System and the 45-Day Removal Trap

The DOJ voter file litigation strategy — demanding unredacted voter files from all 50 states, suing approximately 30 who refused — is documented in detail in Domain 37. What is operationally critical for election administration defense is the mechanism through which the data is used.

The DOJ has offered states a "confidential memorandum of understanding" under which the state agrees to remove within 45 days any voter that the DOJ, using SAVE, flags as a potential non-citizen. Twelve states have fully complied: Alaska, Arkansas, Indiana, Louisiana, Mississippi, Nebraska, Ohio, Oklahoma, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, and Wyoming.

The NVRA problem is foundational: the National Voter Registration Act forbids states from removing voters without providing notice and waiting two federal election cycles — a timeline measured in years, not 45 days. The administration's counter-argument — that the 45-day removal timeline applies only to voters who "should never have been registered" — is legally untested. The NVRA's text does not contain a non-citizen exception to the quiet period or the two-cycle notice requirement.

The documented error rate makes this operationally catastrophic. When Missouri ran St. Louis County's voter list through SAVE in November 2025, it flagged 691 registered voters as potential non-citizens. Thirty-five percent were immediately confirmed as naturalized citizens. After passport cross-referencing, the list fell to 133 — an 81 percent false positive rate. DHS has flagged 21,000 registered voters across 24 states with sharing agreements. A Campaign Legal Center analysis warns: "It sets up a situation where the federal government itself is the actor trying to purge voters from the rolls in the days before the election, which is clearly illegal."

### 1.3 The *Watson v. RNC* Mail Ballot Threat: A June/July Decision That Changes Everything

*Watson v. Republican National Committee* (argued at the Supreme Court March 23, 2026) is a challenge to Mississippi's five-day grace period for mail ballots postmarked by Election Day. The Court's conservative majority appeared strongly skeptical during oral argument. Justice Thomas, joined by Justices Barrett and Gorsuch, questioned Mississippi's solicitor general in ways that suggested they view state-law grace periods as inconsistent with the federal statutory scheme establishing a single Election Day.

A decision is expected by late June or early July 2026 — far enough from November to allow some administrative adjustment, but close enough to primary season that it will create immediate implementation chaos. If the Court adopts the Fifth Circuit's reasoning, the ruling would:

- Eliminate grace periods for mail ballots in 14 states plus Washington, D.C.
- Put at risk ballots from nearly 4 million servicemembers and U.S. citizens living abroad who depend on mail delivery
- Require states to implement new voter education campaigns and administrative procedures within weeks
- Create the "split-ballot problem" Onondaga County's election commissioner Dustin Czarny describes: "How am I going to communicate that part of your ballot will count and part of it will not?"

Fourteen states plus D.C. count ballots postmarked on Election Day but received later — Washington State's grace period runs 21 days, the longest in the country. Election officials are already preparing for what they call "akin to a natural disaster" — a ruling that arrives too late for comprehensive voter re-education before the midterms.

### 2.2 The CISA Dismantlement: What Was Lost and What Cannot Be Replaced in Six Months

CISA's election security program, built after the 2016 Russian interference campaign, provided:

- 1,300 physical security assessments of election offices annually since 2023
- 700 cybersecurity assessments per year
- 500 training sessions
- Funding and operational management for the Election Infrastructure Information Sharing and Analysis Center (EI-ISAC) — the national real-time threat-sharing network
- Funding for the Multi-State ISAC (MS-ISAC) cooperative agreement
- An Election Day situation room for centralized national threat monitoring
- A workforce of approximately 36 specialists embedded in election security as their primary function

All of this is now gone. CISA halted its $10 million annual funding for EI-ISAC and MS-ISAC. CISA did not establish its Election Day situation room for the November 2025 elections. The FY27 budget proposal eliminates the election security program in its entirety — $39.6 million and 14 remaining staff. CISA's total workforce has contracted from approximately 3,400 to roughly 2,400 employees over the course of fiscal year 2025, a reduction of more than a third.

What replaced it: "A patchwork of informal phone calls, email lists, and association meetings" (Votebeat). The National Association of Secretaries of State (NASS) wrote to DHS Secretary Kristi Noem in February 2026 warning that the changes "could endanger future elections." Noem has not substantively responded.

State-level replacements are highly uneven:
- **California, Texas, New York**: National Guard cyber units with real capacity
- **Pennsylvania**: Private contractors and nonprofit security firms replacing CISA scans
- **Georgia**: State Emergency Management Agency routes security work
- **Arizona**: $650,000 state appropriation, explicitly acknowledged as "not going to go anywhere near" its needs; suffered a suspected Iranian cyberattack in summer 2025 and did not notify CISA
- **Wyoming, Vermont, New Mexico**: Minimal National Guard cyber capacity; no meaningful CISA replacement

The coordination gap is the most critical loss. CISA's function was not any single state's security posture — it was the national warning network. When adversaries probe one state's systems, CISA's real-time intelligence sharing allows all states to harden before a broader attack. That function has collapsed.

Former Connecticut Secretary of State Denise Merrill: "It took us years. It's like so many things that are being torn down — it will take us generations to replace it, if at all."

## Part 4: Institutional Fracture — The Federal-State Trust Relationship

### 4.1 What "Shattered" Means Operationally

The broken trust between federal election security agencies and state election officials is not merely a diplomatic or emotional problem — it has concrete operational consequences for the November 2026 election.

**Threat intelligence gap**: When CISA identified foreign adversary reconnaissance activities against election infrastructure in 2020 and 2024, it could alert all 50 states within hours. That early warning function is now absent. Arizona did not report the Iranian cyberattack to CISA — meaning no other states were warned. This is not a hypothetical future risk; it is documented retroactive failure from summer 2025.

**Vendor vulnerability sharing gap**: Matt Crane, of the Colorado County Clerks Association, identified the second consequence: "If you share information, you don't know if it's going to stay confidential. Why would a vendor ever share a vulnerability?" Election technology vendors — who discover security flaws in their products through normal testing — have historically shared vulnerability information confidentially with CISA, enabling coordinated disclosure and patching across states. That pipeline is broken. Vendors now face the choice between disclosing to a potentially hostile federal actor or keeping vulnerabilities private, leaving other states unknowingly exposed.

**Coordination vacuum**: The NASS letter to Secretary Noem (February 2026) warning that changes "could endanger future elections" received no substantive response. State officials are now coordinating through the NASS and informal networks — phone calls, email lists, bilateral conversations at the January 2026 secretaries of state conference. This is not a substitute for a centralized real-time information system.

**The adversary awareness problem**: Foreign adversaries — Russia, China, Iran — are aware that the coordination infrastructure has collapsed. The summer 2025 Iranian attack on Arizona occurred during CISA's operational paralysis. Intelligence community reporting on foreign interference for 2026 (to whatever extent it exists without the Foreign Malign Influence Center or NSC election security group) has no operational delivery mechanism to reach state election officials.

### 4.2 Election Official Turnover and the Institutional Memory Deficit

The Issue One "Turning the Tide on Turnover" report (February 2026) documents a crisis in election administration capacity independent of federal interference: 50 percent of chief local election officials in Western states have left their positions since November 2020. In 4 out of 10 local offices nationwide, the 2026 midterms will be administered by people who have never run a national election.

The consequences compound with the federal interference threat:
- Officials who have never experienced a federal court order blocking a state voter removal program do not know how to respond when they receive one
- Officials who have never dealt with a DOJ information demand do not have the administrative procedures to handle them lawfully
- Officials who have never managed an ICE-adjacent incident near polling places do not have the training the scenario requires
- Officials who have never navigated a post-election certification dispute do not know their legal obligations or the organizations that can help them enforce them

The intimidation and burnout dynamics driving turnover are themselves a product of the democratic erosion environment. In 2024, 33 percent of local election officials knew at least one colleague who had resigned at least in part due to fear for their safety. In 2025, 59 percent reported fear of political interference in their ability to do their jobs, and 46 percent were very or somewhat concerned about politically motivated investigations. The officials most experienced in managing hostile federal attention are precisely the ones most likely to have left.

### 4.3 The 2025 Legislative Wave: 31 Restrictive Laws as a Pre-conditioning Operation

Brennan Center's 2025-in-review report documents that at least 17 states enacted 32 restrictive voting laws in 2025 — the second highest total since the Brennan Center began tracking in 2011. Breaking a five-year trend, restrictive laws outnumbered expansive laws for the first time in recent memory.

The 16 states enacting 31 of those laws (30 of which will be fully in effect for the 2026 midterms): Arkansas, Florida, Idaho, Indiana, Kansas, Kentucky, Montana, North Dakota, Ohio, Oklahoma, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Utah, West Virginia, and Wyoming.

The most operationally significant cluster is the **election interference laws** — 8 laws in 7 states (Indiana, Iowa, Kansas, Louisiana, New Hampshire, Texas, and Utah) that grant partisan state officials new powers over local election administration:
- Iowa: Secretary of state discretion to take over county-level recounts
- Iowa, Kansas, Louisiana, Texas, Utah: New authority for state partisan officials over local election processes
- Indiana, New Hampshire: New ballot-counting requirements critics call structurally flawed

The pattern is not merely restrictive access (voter ID, mail voting receipt deadlines) — it is a structural transfer of local election administration authority to state-level partisan officials. This creates the architecture for top-down override of local election results, independent of any federal action.

## Part 5: International Precedent — What Hungary and Turkey Document

### 5.1 Hungary: Structural Manipulation Can Be Overcome, But Only With Structural Awareness

Hungary's April 2026 parliamentary election is the most directly relevant international case study for 2026 U.S. election defense — not because it proves manipulation is harmless, but because it demonstrates both the ceiling and the floor.

Orbán's Fidesz party had systematically reconfigured Hungary's electoral infrastructure over 16 years: single-member district maps drawn to concentrate opposition votes, a compensatory proportional system that amplified the governing party's plurality advantage, captured media, concentrated private funding in administration-aligned interests, and an election commission stacked with ruling-party loyalists. The opposition entered April 2026 facing the most comprehensively engineered electoral disadvantage of any EU member state.

They won anyway — 53.6 percent for Peter Magyar's Tisza party, ending Orbán's 16-year hold on power.

The lessons are two:

**First**: Democratic mobilization can overcome structural manipulation when economic conditions and political discontent motivate sufficient participation. Hungary's opposition benefited from an economic crisis, documented corruption, and years of civil society organizing that built coalition capacity outside the formal political structures Orbán controlled.

**Second**: The structural manipulation did permanent damage even in defeat. Hungary's road back to independent media, independent judiciary, independent election commission, and non-partisan public broadcasting is measured in years, not months. The Freedom House analysis identifies specific sequencing challenges in unwinding captured institutions that resist democratic restoration even after a democratic electoral victory.

The U.S. lesson is direct: winning the 2026 midterms under compromised election infrastructure does not restore the election infrastructure. It creates a new Congress that can legislate to restore it — if the margin is large enough to overcome procedural obstacles. The administrative dismantlement proceeds regardless of who wins in November, unless affirmative legislative action reverses it.

### 5.2 Turkey: The Captured Election Management Body

Turkey's transformation of the Supreme Election Council (YSK) from a nominally independent judicial body into a functionally government-controlled institution provides the closest institutional parallel to what is happening to CISA and the EAC. The YSK's 2017 decision to validate ballot envelopes without official seals — mid-count, reversing an announced rule — is the terminal example of what happens when an election management body's independence is eliminated. The institutional form remained; the substance of independent adjudication did not.

The U.S. does not have a single national election management body, which provides resilience against Turkey's specific failure mode. But CISA played the coordinative function that the YSK plays in centralized systems. The installation of Marci McCarthy (Republican activist) as CISA's public affairs director and David Harvilicz (election conspiracy theory network alumnus) as DHS's election infrastructure chief represents the same structural capture even if the formal organizational labels are different.

## Part 6: Advocacy Windows and Reform Pathways

### 6.1 The Five Critical Deadlines Between Now and Election Day

The six-month window to November 2026 contains five operational decision points that determine whether the election administration crisis is contained or escalates:

**Window 1: May 30, 2026 — DOJ Consent Decree Deadline**

This is the practical last date by which DOJ consent agreements with new compliant states can be finalized and the 45-day voter removal clock activated before it becomes entangled with the NVRA quiet period. Any state that signs a consent decree agreeing to 45-day removals after May 30 risks its removal program overlapping with the August 7 quiet period cutoff — creating an NVRA violation. The window for defensive action: state AGs should seek emergency injunctions against any consent agreements signed after May 15 that purport to authorize removal within 90 days of the November election.

**Window 2: Late June/Early July 2026 — *Watson v. RNC* Decision**

The most consequential near-term ruling for operational election administration. If the Court eliminates mail ballot grace periods, states with affected laws must immediately:

- Determine whether federal and state ballot rules can be split without creating dual-ballot administration nightmares
- Launch voter education campaigns about changed mail ballot receipt deadlines
- Revise materials for overseas and military voters
- Potentially file for stays or emergency relief based on the administrative impossibility timeline

Election law organizations should have draft emergency-relief briefs ready before the ruling drops. State elections directors should have their own administrative protocols pre-drafted for a range of ruling scenarios.

**Window 3: August 7, 2026 — NVRA 90-Day Quiet Period Begins**

After August 7, any systematic voter removal program is a per se NVRA violation. The Trump administration's counter-argument — that non-citizen removals fall outside the quiet period — is legally untested. Any removal notices sent after August 7 based on SAVE flags should be immediately challenged. The Campaign Legal Center and Brennan Center have the capacity to bring these challenges; the organizations that can refer cases to them include NAACP, League of Women Voters affiliates, and local election protection networks.

**Window 4: September–October 2026 — Certification Season and Pre-Election Litigation**

This is when certification refusal planning materializes. Local canvassing boards that are planning to refuse certification will begin telegraphing their intent through procedural moves. State-level preparation for certification enforcement should include:
- Pre-filed motions to compel certification, ready to be filed on election night if necessary
- Communication protocols that put every canvassing board member on formal written notice of their non-discretionary legal obligation to certify
- Relationships with state AG offices capable of seeking emergency certification orders within 24 hours

**Window 5: November 4, 2026 — Election Night**

The Fulton County seizure precedent (January 2026, covered in Domain 37) documents that the administration has built infrastructure for election-night or post-election-night ballot and equipment seizure. Chain-of-custody protocols that document ballot handling continuously — with independent observers and video records — are the primary defense against seizure claims that materials were compromised.

### 6.2 Structural Reform Pathways

**Immediate (May–July 2026):**

- **Expanded state ISAC funding**: Any state that has not joined or retained MS-ISAC membership should be funded to do so through state legislative appropriations or emergency reserves. The paid membership model that MS-ISAC was forced into when federal funding was cut creates a financial barrier at precisely the wrong moment.
- **Bilateral intelligence-sharing agreements**: The CDT recommends formal bilateral agreements between state election security agencies and the private sector (election technology vendors, major internet platforms) to replace the CISA-mediated sharing function. CIS (Center for Internet Security) has offered to absorb some EI-ISAC functions.
- **Pre-drafted legal responses to mail-ballot ruling scenarios**: The American Bar Association's Section on Civil Rights and Social Justice, the Election Law Clinic network, and the Campaign Legal Center should have scenario-specific response briefs drafted before the *Watson* ruling.
- **Voter file litigation intervention**: In states where DOJ voter file litigation is still pending, amicus briefs from election law academics, county clerks, and non-party state officials can strengthen the legal record on SAVE's documented error rate.

**Medium-term (July–November 2026):**

- **Certification protection legislation**: States that have not already enacted mandatory certification timelines with specific legal penalties for refusal should pass them before primary season. The Brennan Center's election certification recommendations provide a legislative template.
- **Election official support structures**: Expanded access to legal counsel for local election officials (the Committee for Safe and Secure Elections model), clear communication protocols for responding to DOJ information demands, and whistleblower protections for election officials who report improper federal pressure.
- **ICE proximity legislation**: More than half a dozen states are advancing legislation to bar federal immigration agents from operating at or near polling places. These bills should be prioritized and enacted before primary season.
- **National Guard cyber capacity deployment**: High-competition states should formally activate National Guard cyber units for election support through memoranda of agreement with state election agencies before October.

**Legislative (contingent on 2026 midterm outcomes):**

- **CISA election security restoration**: Statutory requirement to maintain a minimum CISA election security workforce and funding level, modeled on the CISA budget bill pending in Congress that would require "sufficient staffing levels"
- **NVRA clarification**: Statutory amendment confirming that the 90-day quiet period applies to all systematic removal programs regardless of the citizenship-status basis for the removal
- **Mail voting federal floor**: Federal statute establishing minimum mail ballot procedures that states must honor, providing a floor that executive orders cannot override — analogous to the Help America Vote Act's minimum standards for voting equipment
- **EAC independence**: Statutory insulation for EAC commissioners analogous to FTC or FEC commissioner protections, preventing executive removal except for cause

## Summary: The Decisive Variables

The 2026 midterms will be determined in part by voter preferences and candidate quality. But the election administration layer — which determines whether eligible voters can cast ballots, whether those ballots are counted, and whether results are certified — is under the most comprehensive federal assault in modern American history.

The decisive variables are:

1. **Watson v. RNC**: A ruling that eliminates grace periods in 14+ states changes mail voting infrastructure nationwide with months to spare, not years. The administrative chaos this creates is manageable with preparation; without preparation it is not.

2. **The NVRA quiet period**: Whether the administration attempts SAVE-based voter removals after August 7 — and whether courts move fast enough to block them before November — is the highest-stakes legal question in the immediate calendar.

3. **Arizona, Georgia, Michigan, Nevada**: The four states with open secretary of state elections in 2026 will determine whether election deniers gain state-level certification authority in the states that matter most. Each of these races is within the range of normal competitive variance.

4. **CISA replacement architecture**: Whether the informal interstate coordination networks that have emerged in place of CISA have sufficient real-time intelligence-sharing capacity to detect and respond to a foreign adversary operation against voting infrastructure is unknown. The answer depends on decisions made in the next 90 days.

5. **Certification infrastructure**: Whether local canvassing boards in high-risk counties — Cochise County, AZ; Washoe County, NV; contested counties in GA and MI — attempt certification refusal, and whether state-level legal infrastructure can compel certification within the statutory deadline, will determine whether court-determined winners are actually seated.

The legal resistance is organized, resourced, and winning the majority of its filed cases. The organizational infrastructure — Democracy Docket, Campaign Legal Center, Brennan Center, the 23+ state AG coalition, secretaries of state coordination networks — is functional and active. The question is whether legal victories translate to operational protection before November, given the six-month window and the complexity of the threats running simultaneously.

The international evidence from Hungary suggests they can. But it also documents that structural dismantlement persists even through an electoral win. The first priority is protecting the election. The second is using its outcome to begin the legislative work of restoring what has been destroyed.
