# Domain 46: Federal Research Policy and the Destruction of Epistemic Infrastructure

**Research completed**: May 9, 2026
**Crisis window**: Active — 5,844 NIH grants and 1,996 NSF grants cancelled or suspended through 2025; 1,752 NSF grants terminated by DOGE through late May 2025 alone; NSF's Social, Behavioral and Economic Sciences Directorate targeted for elimination in FY2027 budget; NIH restructuring reducing 27 institutes to 8 proposed; APHA v. NIH at First Circuit
**Priority designation**: Phase 2 — Medium-High; epistemic infrastructure upstream of all other democratic accountability mechanisms
**Cross-domain connections**: Domain 27 (Higher Education — the delivery vehicle; brain drain data originates here), Domain 43 (Epistemic Infrastructure — distribution layer of the same epistemic crisis), Domain 42 (Drug Policy — regulatory capture at the policy translation layer), Domain 45 (Climate Policy — parallel attack on scientific determination infrastructure)

## The Central Finding

The federal research funding apparatus — NIH, NSF, ARPA-H, and the DOE national laboratories — is the infrastructure through which democratic societies produce the evidence base for policy deliberation. It is not a discretionary budget item separate from democratic governance. It is the foundational layer of a three-part epistemic architecture: knowledge is produced in federally funded research institutions, validated through peer review, transmitted through universities and journals (Domain 27), and distributed through media and public discourse (Domain 43). When that production layer is politically captured — not merely underfunded, but redirected to eliminate findings that challenge current power arrangements — the downstream consequence is that democratic deliberation operates without the evidence those decisions require.

What distinguishes the 2025–2026 assault on federal research funding from ordinary budget politics is the content-based character of the terminations. Grants were not cancelled because the funded research was methodologically flawed, failed to achieve results, or duplicated other work. They were cancelled because they addressed subjects — vaccine safety, health disparities, transgender health, DEI, COVID-19 sequelae, climate change impacts — that produced findings the administration found politically inconvenient. This is epistemic infrastructure destruction at the production level, distinct from the disinformation campaigns that corrupt the distribution level (Domain 43), and analytically parallel to the regulatory capture that prevents evidence from being translated into policy (Domain 42).

The combined quantitative record as of mid-2026: more than 7,800 grants cancelled or suspended across NIH and NSF; approximately $2.45 billion in cancelled NIH awards corresponding to an estimated $6.29 billion in unrealized economic output; 68,000 jobs projected lost from the full proposed cuts; ERC early-career grant applications from US researchers nearly tripled over two years; 36 of Germany's Global Minds Initiative fellows were from the United States. The brain drain documented in Domain 27 is not primarily a visa story — it is a research funding story.

## Section A: NIH Grant Terminations — Content-Based Suppression of Research

### A.1 The Pattern and Scale

Beginning in February 2025, the National Institutes of Health launched what federal courts subsequently characterized as "a reckless purge of federal grants." NIH staff were directed to eliminate research on disfavored topics and populations without clear guidance or scientific justification. The mechanism was explicit and documented: grants were screened for keyword triggers including "diversity, equity and inclusion," "gender," "LGBT," "racism," "climate change," "vaccine acceptance," "minority," "latinx," "equity," "lived experience," and "social determinants of health." Grants flagged by keyword scan were deemed ineligible for continued funding. An NIH employee who publicized the mandate described it as requiring that grants be "clean" before receiving further funding — with "clean" defined by ideological content, not scientific merit.

Nature reported that DOGE had broad control over the world's largest public biomedical funder, with court documents and internal correspondence showing the cost-cutting force directly ordered grant terminations. By June 4, 2025, approximately 2,300 NIH grants totaling nearly $3.8 billion had been terminated, including at least 160 clinical trials in cancer, HIV/AIDS, and chronic disease research. Over the full course of 2025, a total of 5,844 NIH grants were cancelled or suspended. The FY2026 budget had proposed approximately $18 billion in NIH cuts — nearly 40% of the agency's budget.

This is not a budget reduction in the ordinary sense. Peer-reviewed, competitively awarded grants — grants that survived NIH's own multi-stage scientific review process before receiving funding — were cancelled for their subject matter. The termination criterion was content, not scientific quality.

### A.2 Specific Research Areas Eliminated

**Vaccine Safety and mRNA Research**

NIH asked each of its institutes to supply lists of grants involving vaccine hesitancy. Dozens of grants were terminated across the agency. A $548,002 project analyzing electronic health records to study COVID-19 vaccine safety in pregnant and breastfeeding individuals was cancelled; the principal investigator described receiving termination notice without prior warning. Beyond vaccine hesitancy research, HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. directed the termination of 22 BARDA contracts totaling $500 million to develop new applications for mRNA technology — halting vaccine development for diseases including influenza and HIV. Kennedy justified the cancellations using claims that health experts characterized as factually inverted: a review of Kennedy's stated evidence found that it supported expanding mRNA research, not ending it. NPR reported public health experts were "dismayed" by the defunding, which one described as sacrificing future medical breakthroughs on political grounds.

The termination of mRNA vaccine safety research is a particular epistemic infrastructure failure because it eliminates the evidence base on which future vaccine safety determinations depend. When the government cancels research into whether vaccines are safe for specific populations, it ensures those safety questions cannot be answered with the rigor that democratic accountability of pharmaceutical policy requires.

**Long COVID and Epidemiology Research**

On March 25, 2025, principal investigators of multiple COVID-19 studies received abrupt termination notices. The NIH's RECOVER (Researching COVID to Enhance Recovery) program — a $1.7 billion initiative representing the single largest federal funding mechanism for long COVID research — had all grants for pathobiology studies cancelled. This amounted to 45 separate studies designed to identify the biological mechanisms driving long COVID's varied manifestations. The administration simultaneously closed the Office for Long COVID Research and Practice. A Lancet Microbe analysis characterized the action as the US government cutting funding for research into COVID-19 sequelae at the precise moment when long COVID's chronic disease burden was becoming quantifiable.

Some RECOVER grants were partially restored following pressure from senators and early lawsuit filings — but the pattern of targeting research that would produce politically inconvenient findings about COVID-19 was established.

**Health Disparities and Minority Health Research**

The DOGE-directed NIH restructuring proposed eliminating four complete NIH institutes and centers: the National Institute on Minority Health and Health Disparities (NIMHD), the National Institute of Nursing Research (NINR), the Fogarty International Center (FIC), and the National Center for Complementary and Integrative Health (NCCIH). The proposed reduction — from 27 institutes and centers to 8 — eliminates precisely those units whose research focus is politically disfavored. The NIMHD, in particular, was the primary federal infrastructure for producing the evidence base on racial and socioeconomic health gaps — the research that would support health equity policies the administration opposes. Eliminating the institute eliminates the evidence.

A PNAS analysis of 2025 NIH grant terminations documented that the cancellations disproportionately affected women and early-career investigators, intensifying risks to research continuity and biomedical pipeline development.

At the National Institute of Allergy and Infectious Diseases, 29 grant termination letters went to principal investigators, including nine grants from the Antiviral Drug Discovery Centers for Pathogens of Pandemic Concern — a program that had committed $577 million to develop new drugs to treat COVID-19 and prevent future pandemics. ### A.3 The APHA v. NIH Litigation — Constitutional Dimensions

In April 2025, the American Public Health Association, the UAW, Ibis Reproductive Health, and individual researchers filed suit in the US District Court for the District of Massachusetts challenging the grant terminations as violations of the Administrative Procedure Act and the Fifth Amendment's due process protections. The ACLU litigated the case.

The district court struck down NIH's directives in June 2025, ruling that the terminations were arbitrary, capricious, and unlawful. The court found that NIH "offered only conclusory statements" to justify cancelling grants related to vaccines, transgender issues, DEI, COVID-19, and climate change. In August 2025, the Supreme Court issued a partial stay. The First Circuit heard oral argument on the government's appeal in early 2026. The case remains active at the appellate level.

A parallel settlement was reached under which NIH agreed to individually evaluate grant applications that had been arbitrarily frozen, denied, or withdrawn — covering applications for HIV prevention, Alzheimer's research, LGBTQ+ health, and sexual violence research. However, NIH Director Jay Bhattacharya stated publicly that DEI-related grants restored under court order would not be renewed when their terms expired, establishing a two-track outcome: restored by court order, eliminated on expiration.

The constitutional dimension is significant. The ACLU's framing characterized the terminations as content-based suppression of research — a First Amendment concern when the government conditions the receipt of public funding on the ideological content of the funded speech. While the Supreme Court's precedent on government funding and speech is complex (the government generally can choose what to fund), the mass cancellation of previously approved peer-reviewed grants based on keyword triggers, rather than merit review, introduces an arbitrary element that the district court found unlawful on APA grounds — leaving the First Amendment question partially unresolved but live in appellate briefing.

## Section B: NSF and ARPA-H Restructuring as Politically Directed Capacity Destruction

### B.1 NSF — From Broad Science Mission to Ideological Narrowing

The National Science Foundation funded approximately $9 billion in research annually before 2025, supporting basic science across disciplines including social science, behavioral research, environmental science, and STEM education. The 2025–2026 restructuring has been qualitatively different from budget reductions: it has targeted specific research categories for elimination based on their subject matter and has restructured the agency's review processes to enable political filtering of grant decisions.

**Grant Terminations**

By late November 2025, NSF had terminated over 1,300 grants totaling approximately $700 million in unspent funds. By late May 2025, the Consortium of Social Science Associations tracked 1,752 terminated NSF grants with a combined value of approximately $1.4 billion. NSF's April 18, 2025 statement explicitly cited DEI research and studies related to "misinformation and disinformation" as categories being eliminated — which meant that research into the very epistemic threats analyzed in Domain 43 was being cancelled by the government simultaneously with the proliferation of those threats. The total number of new grants funded by NSF in 2025 dropped 25% relative to the ten-year average.

**Structural Elimination: The Social, Behavioral, and Economic Sciences Directorate**

The FY2027 budget request, published April 3, 2026, proposed the complete dissolution of NSF's Social, Behavioral, and Economic Sciences (SBE) Directorate. SBE funds 63% of all academic research in social sciences and psychological sciences in the United States, and serves as the primary source of basic research funding for political science. Eliminating SBE would not merely reduce social science funding — it would eliminate the federal infrastructure for independently produced knowledge about democratic participation, voter behavior, social inequality, and political institutions. NSF leadership announced preemptive steps to begin dissolving the directorate in alignment with the proposed budget before congressional action was taken.

The political logic is transparent: SBE-funded research produces findings about racial inequality, democratic participation gaps, and institutional performance that are the empirical foundation for progressive policy arguments. Eliminating SBE eliminates that evidentiary foundation.

**Reorganization as Filtering Mechanism**

Beyond the SBE dissolution, NSF's agency restructuring replaced the previous directorate division structure with "newly formed sections" while eliminating all institute- and center-run review panels. Scientists overseeing those panels were laid off. This centralizes grant review authority in ways that remove specialized scientific judgment from funding decisions and make it easier to impose political priorities from above. The "American Competitiveness" framing that now governs NSF priorities — directing funding toward applied research and away from basic science — has been documented by American Physical Society analysis as a narrowing of eligible research in ways that exclude both social science and basic science from the agency's mission.

### B.2 ARPA-H — Mission Redirection and Leadership Purge

The Advanced Research Projects Agency for Health was established in 2022 specifically to pursue high-risk, high-reward biomedical research that conventional NIH grant-making does not fund — the DARPA model applied to public health. Its founding director, Renee Wegrzyn, was fired by the Trump administration in February 2025. Interim director Jason Roos was replaced by Alicia Jackson, appointed in late 2025, with a background in synthetic biology and unconventional electronics rather than public health research.

In February 2026, ARPA-H laid off approximately 20 employees and contractors, including the staff responsible for commercializing new biomedical technologies. The HEROES Program — designed to implement behavioral interventions for preventative healthcare — was cancelled. The agency's FY2025 appropriation of $1.5 billion was maintained for FY2026, but the organizational changes — leadership replacement, staff reductions in commercialization, cancellation of behavioral health programs — constitute a mission redirection away from the foundational health research that ARPA-H was created to pursue and toward a narrower scope that eliminates behavioral and preventative health inquiry.

The combined NIH-NSF-ARPA-H pattern is a single coherent policy: eliminate research that produces evidence supporting public health intervention, health equity, vaccine effectiveness, and climate impact; maintain research that is either politically neutral or supportive of the administration's narrative. The effect on democratic governance is structural: the executive branch acquires control over what facts are available to hold that same executive branch accountable.

### B.3 The Economic Destruction — Research Output and Innovation Capacity

The economic consequences of the grant terminations are not merely academic. The $2.45 billion in cancelled NIH awards corresponds to an estimated $6.29 billion in unrealized economic output. The Science and Community Impacts Mapping Project (SCIMaP) at Penn estimated that the proposed full NIH funding cuts would result in a $16 billion reduction in economic activity and 68,000 jobs lost nationwide. The more specific indirect cost cap proposal alone was estimated to cause a $6.1 billion GDP decrease and $4.6 billion reduction in labor income. Despite these impacts, NIH grants generated an estimated 250% return on a $36.6 billion investment in 2025 — meaning each dollar cancelled destroyed approximately $2.50 in economic value.

The research pipeline effects operate on a longer horizon. Graduate students defunded mid-degree, clinical trials interrupted, laboratory equipment contracts terminated — these represent capacity destruction that does not recover in a budget cycle. Rebuilding interrupted biomedical research programs requires not just funding restoration but reconstitution of the human capital (researchers, graduate students, laboratory staff) that departed when programs were cancelled.

## Section C: Brain Drain and International Comparative Analysis

### C.1 The Migration Signal — Quantified

The data from Domain 27's brain drain section requires extension here, because the mechanisms driving it are primarily research funding, not only visa policy. US applications to European Research Council early-career grants nearly tripled over two years: 60 for the 2024 call, 116 for the 2025 call, 169 for the 2026 call. For senior ERC grants, applications from US researchers rose from 23 in 2024 to 114 in 2026. These are applications from researchers who have not yet left — they represent intent to leave, conditional on receiving European funding.

The domestic data confirms the pattern from the other direction: one in ten faculty at research institutions report having at least one federal grant cancelled in 2025; 40% report overall research funding declines. Agriculture researchers report 57% rates of funding decline; allied health, medicine, and engineering researchers report 50% rates. Faculty in states restricting academic freedom show one in ten actively seeking jobs out of state.

Brain drain in research differs from other professional migration because it is irreversible on short timescales. A scientist who establishes a laboratory in Germany, builds a research team, and secures multi-year European funding is not returning to the US within a budget cycle — regardless of what domestic policy changes. The five-to-ten-year timescale on which research programs develop means the talent currently leaving will define the comparative position of US and European science for a decade.

### C.2 European Responses — Deliberate Recruitment Architecture

The European response to US research policy disruption has been systematic and is expanding. The European Commission launched the "Choose Europe for Science" initiative in May 2025 with a €500 million commitment for 2025–27 specifically to recruit American scientific talent. By January 2026, the initiative had attracted over 100 national and regional programs and grown to approximately €900 million in total EU funding — €400 million more than the original announcement. The ERC plans to double the funding available for grantees relocating to Europe, with individual grants of up to $4.8 million available for US researchers willing to move.

Germany operates the Global Minds Initiative, a national-level program coordinated through the DFG (German Research Foundation), Alexander von Humboldt Foundation, DAAD, and Federal Ministry of Research. In the second half of 2025 alone, the program selected 166 researchers for fellowships and grants. Of those, 36 came from the United States — making the US the second-largest source country after China. The DFG approved €12 million in initial funding in the first phase, with a "Global Minds in DFG Consortia" track specifically designed to integrate US researchers into existing German research programs.

France's Aix-Marseille University announced a "Safe Place for Science" program, committing between $10 million and $16 million to support approximately 15 researchers. The Netherlands, Norway, Denmark, Canada, Australia, and the UK have all launched targeted recruitment programs. The European Commission is considering a special scientist passport to ease relocation.

This is not a secondary effect of US policy — it is an active, explicitly targeted campaign to convert US research capacity destruction into European research capacity gain.

### C.3 The Structural Consequence for US Global Research Position

After adjusting for inflation, the Trump administration's proposed FY2027 decrease in non-defense research spending would be unprecedented over the past half century and would reduce spending to 1991 levels. The US share of global R&D spending — which has been declining relative to China for two decades — is accelerating its decline. The administration's FY2027 budget proposes deep cuts to science across NIH ($18 billion reduction), NSF (22.7% cut already enacted), EPA, and FDA.

The long-term strategic implication is straightforward: the United States built its post-World War II technological and economic leadership substantially on federally funded basic research. The transistor, the internet, GPS, mRNA vaccines — all trace to federally funded science. Dismantling that foundation in a three-to-five year window does not merely reduce current research output; it eliminates the pipeline of knowledge that becomes commercial technology a decade hence, and it exports the human capital that would have generated it. The countries currently building recruitment infrastructure for US scientists are building not just research capacity but the competitive advantage that follows from it.

## Section D: Democratic Design Failure — Epistemic Infrastructure Destruction at the Production Level

### D.1 The Three-Layer Epistemic Architecture

The attack on federal research funding is best understood in relation to the other epistemic attacks this framework has documented, because each targets a different layer of the same architecture.

**Layer 1 — Production**: Federal research institutions (NIH, NSF, ARPA-H, DOE labs) produce the knowledge — the original studies, clinical trials, epidemiological analyses, social science findings, and policy research that constitute the factual basis for democratic deliberation. This is Domain 46's focus. When the government cancels vaccine safety research, the fact of vaccine safety is not merely disputed — it becomes empirically undetermined by the standard methods democratic accountability depends on.

**Layer 2 — Transmission**: Universities (Domain 27) validate, teach, and refine the produced knowledge. They train the doctors, lawyers, public administrators, and scientists who carry that knowledge into democratic institutions. When universities are forced to self-censor, rename programs, and eliminate DEI-related courses, the transmission of produced knowledge is disrupted — but the knowledge still exists in the published literature.

**Layer 3 — Distribution**: Media, journalism, and public discourse (Domain 43) carry knowledge from research and universities to the democratic public. When local news collapses, deepfakes proliferate, and the CPB is defunded, the distribution of knowledge is corrupted even if the knowledge itself was accurately produced and transmitted.

The 2025–2026 assault operates on all three layers simultaneously. What makes the production-layer attack (Domain 46) analytically distinct is its irreversibility: a finding that was never produced does not exist to be retrieved. A clinical trial that was cancelled does not generate data that can later be recovered. When vaccine safety research is cancelled in 2025, the safety data that would have existed in 2028 does not exist — regardless of what funding is restored. The damage at Layer 1 is uniquely permanent.

### D.2 Epistemic Infrastructure and Democratic Deliberation

The purpose of federally funded research — articulated in the National Science Foundation Act, the NIH Organic Act, and the legislative histories of the major research agencies — is to produce knowledge that serves the public interest in ways the private market will not. This includes research whose findings are unprofitable (basic science), research whose findings create political inconvenience for powerful actors (pharmaceutical safety, health disparities, climate impacts), and research whose findings build the factual foundation for policy accountability.

When the government eliminates funding for vaccine safety research, the democratic process faces decisions about vaccine policy without the evidence that would inform those decisions. When it eliminates health disparities research, the democratic process cannot assess whether healthcare policy is achieving equitable outcomes. When it eliminates climate impact research, the democratic process cannot evaluate whether environmental policy is protecting public health. In each case, the mechanism is the same: a gap is created between the policy decision and the factual foundation the decision requires. Democratic governance does not end — it continues in a degraded epistemic environment in which evidence-based accountability is structurally impaired.

This is analytically parallel to Domain 42's analysis of DEA regulatory capture. In the drug scheduling context, the capture operated at Layer 2 (translation): the evidence existed but the regulatory mechanism through which evidence was supposed to be translated into scheduling decisions was captured, preventing the translation. In the federal research funding context, the capture operates at Layer 1: the evidence is prevented from being produced in the first place. The effect on democratic accountability is similar — policy operates without the evidence base it requires — but the mechanism of capture and the appropriate remediation are different.

### D.3 The Content-Based Character as the Critical Distinction

The most important structural fact about the 2025–2026 NIH and NSF grant terminations is that they are content-based, not resource-based. This distinction is legally significant (the ACLU's constitutional challenge turns on it), politically significant (it identifies the motivation), and democratically significant (it reveals the mechanism of harm).

A government that reduces research funding for fiscal reasons produces a different research portfolio — smaller, focused on the priorities that survive competitive peer review within a tighter budget. The democratic harm from fiscal constraint is real but indirect: less research overall.

A government that terminates peer-reviewed, merit-selected grants for their subject matter is doing something categorically different: it is using the funding mechanism to suppress specific fields of inquiry, ensuring that certain questions cannot be answered by the scientific methods that democratic accountability depends on. The keyword-trigger system documented at NIH — scanning grants for "equity," "climate change," "vaccine acceptance," "lived experience" — is not a budget mechanism. It is a content filter. The courts that have reviewed it have said so.

The content-based character also means the harm is durable beyond the immediate funding cycle. A researcher whose vaccine safety study is cancelled does not simply reapply next year — the study is disrupted, the research team disperses, the clinical enrollment terminates, and the institutional partners disengage. The suppression of one grant cycle compounds into a multi-year research gap.

### D.4 Cross-Domain Connections — The Integrated Epistemic Attack

This domain's analysis completes a picture that appears across the framework:

**Domain 42** (DEA regulatory capture) shows the same evidence-suppression logic operating at the policy-translation layer: the DEA's scheduling hearing process was designed to be insulated from political pressure precisely because scheduling decisions should turn on scientific evidence, not electoral politics. When that insulation is removed, policy is made without the evidence constraint.

**Domain 43** (epistemic infrastructure, disinformation) shows the distribution layer: the deliberate proliferation of disinformation, the defunding of CPB, the collapse of local journalism — these corrupt the channel through which facts reach the democratic public.

**Domain 45** (climate policy rollback) shows a parallel production-layer attack: the EPA's rescission of the Greenhouse Gas Endangerment Finding — which the National Academy of Sciences disputed and a federal court found was prepared by a group formed in violation of federal law — is the administrative erasure of a scientific determination that was legally valid and scientifically reaffirmed. It is the regulatory equivalent of cancelling the research.

What unites all four domains is the same democratic design failure: the executive branch acquiring control over the facts available to hold the executive branch accountable. The solution architecture must address this structural feature — not merely restore funding, but redesign the institutional relationship between political authority and epistemic authority so that the executive cannot weaponize funding and regulatory power against the evidence base of its own accountability.

## Section E: Reform Architecture — Statutory Protection for Epistemic Infrastructure

### E.1 Content-Neutrality Mandate for Federal Research Funding

The primary structural reform required is a statutory content-neutrality mandate for research funding decisions at NIH, NSF, ARPA-H, and DOE. Under such a mandate, the sole permissible criteria for grant termination after award would be: failure to meet scientific milestones, financial mismanagement by the recipient institution, or documented research misconduct. Subject matter, keywords, policy implications, and ideological content would be explicitly prohibited as grounds for termination.

This reform would not prevent the government from setting research priorities in advance through its peer review criteria — the government retains legitimate authority to decide what research areas to fund. What it prohibits is the retrospective cancellation of grants that have already passed merit review, based on content filters applied after award. The keyword-trigger system that drove the 2025 NIH terminations would be per se unlawful under this standard.

Implementation requires: (a) a statutory prohibition on content-based termination criteria, codified in the NIH Organic Act and NSF Act; (b) a requirement that any grant termination state specific scientific or financial grounds subject to administrative appeal; (c) an Inspector General audit function specifically covering grant termination patterns for content-based triggers; and (d) a private right of action for grant recipients whose awards are terminated without stated scientific or financial grounds.

### E.2 Statutory Independence for Research Funding Architecture

The DOGE involvement in NIH grant terminations — documented in court records showing the cost-cutting force had "broad control" over the world's largest public biomedical funder — illustrates the structural vulnerability: research funding decisions are not currently insulated from political direction in any legally enforceable way. A reform parallel to the Federal Reserve's operational independence would establish a statutory firewall between political officials and individual grant-funding decisions.

This would not require removing NIH from HHS or NSF from executive branch authority — agencies remain accountable to Congress and the president for their overall missions. What it would require is that individual grant decisions be made by scientific peer review panels whose composition and decisions are protected from override by political appointees. The 2025 elimination of institute-level peer review panels — which centralized review authority in ways that enabled political filtering — would be reversed and protected against recurrence.

### E.3 Anti-Keyword Statute

Congress should enact a targeted prohibition on the use of textual content screening for research funding decisions. An "Anti-Keyword Statute" would prohibit any federal research funding agency from: using keyword scanning tools to identify grants for termination review; establishing lists of prohibited terms whose presence in a grant application or project report triggers adverse action; or directing agency staff to flag, review, or terminate grants based on the presence of terms associated with scientific fields or subject areas rather than with specific violations of law or grant conditions.

This reform is technically precise: it targets the specific mechanism that drove the 2025 NIH terminations while leaving legitimate program management functions intact.

### E.4 Congressional Audit Rights — Political Bias in Grant Termination

The existing Inspector General function at NIH and NSF lacks specific authority to audit grant termination patterns for political bias. Legislation establishing this authority would require the IGs to: (a) maintain a public database of all grant terminations within 30 days of termination, including stated grounds; (b) conduct an annual analysis of whether termination patterns correlate with subject matter, researcher demographics, or institutional affiliation in ways that suggest content-based filtering; (c) report annually to the relevant congressional committees; and (d) refer to DOJ any termination pattern that evidence suggests resulted from direction by political appointees rather than scientific merit review.

Congressional audit authority of this kind would not prevent executive action in the near term, but it would create a contemporaneous evidentiary record that supports judicial review and congressional oversight — filling the gap that currently allows administrative content filtering to operate without transparent documentation.

### E.5 International Research Cooperation Frameworks — Insulating US Research from Domestic Political Disruption

The brain drain data establishes that the US research community is already integrating with European and Canadian research infrastructure as a response to domestic instability. A reform architecture that formalizes this integration would insulate US research capacity from future domestic disruption. Specific mechanisms:

**Bilateral research continuity agreements** with the EU (via Horizon Europe), UK Research and Innovation (UKRI), the DFG, and Canada's NSERC would establish protocols for joint funding of research programs when US funding is disrupted. Under such agreements, a grant cancelled by NIH for non-scientific reasons could be continued through a partner-agency mechanism while US litigation resolves, preserving research continuity and preventing research team dispersal.

**Joint peer review standards** would establish common criteria for research merit that insulate funded projects from single-country political disruption. A project that has passed joint US-EU peer review would be documented as meeting a scientifically validated standard — strengthening the evidentiary record for legal challenges to politically motivated terminations.

**Researcher mobility frameworks** analogous to the EU's Blue Card but designed for research continuity — allowing US researchers whose grants are cancelled to continue work in partner countries under their US institutional affiliation, without terminating US ties — would reduce the conversion of temporary disruption into permanent brain drain.

### E.6 Statutory Minimum Funding Levels for Foundational Research

The FY2027 budget's proposed elimination of NSF's Social, Behavioral and Economic Sciences Directorate — which funds 63% of all US academic social science research — illustrates the vulnerability of entire research disciplines to administrative elimination within a single budget cycle. Statutory minimum funding floors for categories of foundational research would require affirmative congressional action to eliminate rather than allowing administrative discretion to zero out entire research disciplines.

Categories warranting statutory minimums include: basic science (non-applied research without immediate commercial application), public health surveillance and epidemiology, health disparities research, behavioral and social science, and environmental health research. The floor levels could be set at a percentage of total agency appropriations (e.g., NSF must allocate no less than X% of its appropriation to basic research across designated categories), preventing the internal reallocation from basic to applied research that the "American Competitiveness" directive has pursued.

## Connections to Existing Framework — Positioning Notes

This domain fills a gap that is analytically distinct from both Domain 27 and Domain 43, though all three are components of the same epistemic attack.

**Domain 27 (Higher Education)** covers the attack on universities as institutions: funding leverage against specific universities, DEI prohibition, student visa revocations, and accreditation weaponization. The federal research funding infrastructure appears in Domain 27 as context for why the Harvard and Columbia funding leverages are so damaging — federal grants represent 55% of university research revenue. Domain 46 takes the research funding architecture as its primary subject, not its context.

**Domain 43 (Epistemic Infrastructure)** covers the distribution layer: deepfakes, CPB defunding, local news collapse, and algorithmic amplification of disinformation. Domain 46 covers the production layer: the institutions that generate the original knowledge that journalism, universities, and public discourse distribute and debate. Both domains analyze epistemic infrastructure destruction, but at different layers of the same architecture.

**Domain 45 (Climate Policy)** covers the Endangerment Finding rescission as an example of administrative erasure of scientific determination at the regulatory translation layer. Domain 46 covers cancellation of the underlying research — the studies that would produce the scientific findings that regulatory determinations are supposed to translate. The two attacks are complementary: cancel the research (Domain 46) and you prevent findings from being produced; rescind the regulatory determination (Domain 45) and you prevent findings from being translated into policy. Both are required for full epistemic infrastructure destruction.
