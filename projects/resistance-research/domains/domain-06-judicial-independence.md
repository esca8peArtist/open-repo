## Executive Summary

Federal judicial independence in 2026 faces simultaneous pressure from three vectors: executive branch defiance of court orders, congressional impeachment threats against adversarial judges, and the structural capture of Article I immigration courts by the executive. The Supreme Court's shadow-docket ruling in *Trump v. Wilcox* (May 22, 2025) effectively ended independent agency protection for the NLRB and MSPB without briefing or argument, and the pending *Trump v. Slaughter* decision (expected June 2026) will formalize that shift for all independent agencies. Post-*Loper Bright*, the expansion of presidential removal authority has proceeded alongside judiciary's post-Chevron interpretive independence — a paradoxical result in which courts gained interpretive sovereignty over agencies while agencies lost structural independence from the executive. The combination creates an environment in which institutional independence cannot be secured through ordinary statute, and in which the judiciary's own authority is under overt political attack.

## Section 1: The Shadow-Docket and the Collapse of Humphrey's Executor

### 1.1 Trump v. Wilcox: What the Shadow Docket Did

On May 22, 2025, the Supreme Court issued a stay in *Trump v. Wilcox* (No. 24A966), keeping NLRB Member Gwynne Wilcox and Merit Systems Protection Board Member Cathy Harris out of office after President Trump fired them in January-February 2025. The 6-3 unsigned order — issued without merits briefing, without oral argument, and without a full opinion explaining the legal reasoning — produced an immediate structural effect: both officials were removed from their positions, the NLRB lost its quorum, and federal labor law proceedings halted nationwide.

The legal significance of the order lies not in what it said but in what it did not say. Rather than applying *Humphrey's Executor v. United States*, 295 U.S. 602 (1935) — the ninety-year precedent holding that Congress may limit presidential removal of independent agency officers to "inefficiency, neglect of duty, or malfeasance" — the majority characterized the NLRB and MSPB as exercising "considerable executive power" and found the government likely to prevail on the merits. Justice Kagan's dissent identified the structural stakes directly: the majority had "effectively repealed *Humphrey's Executor* by fiat" — without briefing, argument, or precedential opinion.

The Federal Reserve received an explicit carve-out. The order stated it expressed "no view" on the Fed, describing it as a "uniquely structured, quasi-private entity." That carve-out preserved Federal Reserve independence as a temporary structural accommodation — but one with no doctrinal basis and no guarantee of survival in the merits decision.

### 1.2 The D.C. Circuit Merits Ruling: December 2025

After the Supreme Court's stay, the D.C. Circuit on the merits of the Wilcox and Harris appeals reversed the district court and upheld the firings. The D.C. Circuit held that Congress may not restrict the President's power to remove NLRB and MSPB officers because they exercise "substantial executive power" — adopting the substantive test the Supreme Court had gestured toward in *Seila Law v. CFPB* (2020) and *Collins v. Yellen* (2021). The D.C. Circuit's ruling made both officials formally out — not merely stayed — and established the lower-court legal baseline ahead of the Supreme Court's merits decision in *Trump v. Slaughter*.

## Section 2: April 2026 Update — Post-Loper Bright Presidential Removal Authority and Appellate Capture Vectors

**Updated**: April 27, 2026 (Session 529)

### 2.1 Post-Loper Bright Removal Authority: The Paradox of Dual Expansion

*Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo* (June 28, 2024) eliminated Chevron deference, requiring courts to exercise independent judgment on all statutory interpretation questions. This decision expanded judicial authority over agency action — courts now determine the "best meaning" of statutes rather than deferring to agency interpretation. However, *Loper Bright* operated simultaneously with the *Wilcox* shadow docket and pending *Trump v. Slaughter* decision to produce a paradoxical result: courts gained interpretive sovereignty over what agencies *do*, while the president gained plenary removal authority over *who leads* agencies.

The combination is structurally asymmetric in ways that undermine the democratic theory of administrative law. Under *Loper Bright*, a future Democratic president who appoints progressive-leaning agency leadership cannot protect those officials from removal under the emerging *Slaughter* doctrine. Simultaneously, courts will second-guess every regulatory interpretation those officials produce. The result is an administrative state that is simultaneously more judicially controlled and more presidentially controlled — with independent expertise removed from both equations.

Appellate court treatment of post-*Loper Bright* removal authority has diverged. The Fifth Circuit and D.C. Circuit declined to apply super-strong statutory stare decisis to prior agency-deferential precedents, opening decades of agency interpretations to fresh challenge. The First, Second, Ninth, Eleventh, and Federal Circuits have applied heightened statutory stare decisis, preserving more pre-*Loper* precedent. This circuit split on the scope of *Loper Bright*'s reach — and on which agency interpretations survive it — is itself a vector for executive manipulation: forum shopping in sympathetic circuits can achieve regulatory outcomes that resist challenge in others.

### 2.2 Appellate Judicial Capture Vectors in 2026

"Capture" of the appellate judiciary proceeds through four mechanisms, each operating on a different timescale:

**Mechanism 1 — Confirmation pipeline**. As of April 20, 2026, the Senate has confirmed 271 Article III judges nominated by Trump across both terms, including 60 circuit court judges. Three conservative circuit court judges announced strategic retirements in early 2026 to allow Trump nominees to fill their seats before the midterms. With a 53-seat Senate majority, Republicans face a narrowing confirmation window if the 2026 midterms shift the balance — producing a race to fill vacancies with ideologically aligned nominees while the majority holds. Demand Justice's tracker documents these nominees; the Federalist Society's influence over the selection process is documented at the appellate level in particular.

**Mechanism 2 — Administrative court dismantlement**. Immigration courts are Article I administrative courts within the executive branch — not constitutionally independent Article III courts. The Trump administration has leveraged this status aggressively: firing more than 100 immigration judges appointed under Democratic administrations, deploying over 600 military lawyers as temporary immigration judges without immigration law experience, and eliminating work requirements that had provided minimal process protections. The Slate analysis (April 2026) describes immigration court capture as the administration's clearest judicial success — precisely because it operates outside Article III protections. This is the proving ground for a broader theory: that executive administrative courts can be stripped of independence without triggering the constitutional protections that insulate Article III judges from removal.

**Mechanism 3 — Impeachment pressure on Article III judges**. Senate Republicans escalated impeachment rhetoric against federal district judges in January-April 2026. Senate Judiciary Committee Chair Ted Cruz convened a hearing focused on Judges James Boasberg (D.D.C.) and Deborah Boardman, with Cruz explicitly stating both "meet the standard for impeachment and removal." Speaker Johnson has separately called for impeachment of adversarial judges. Articles of impeachment have been introduced in the Republican-controlled House. These efforts face a structural barrier — two-thirds of senators must vote for conviction, and 14 Democratic senators would need to join Republicans, which has no realistic pathway — but the *effect* of impeachment pressure is chilling without requiring successful removal. Judges who expect career consequences for adversarial rulings rationally moderate their decisions at the margins. The 564 threats against judges documented in fiscal 2025, rising to 131 in the first months of fiscal 2026, operate on the same chilling logic. Federal marshals have been assigned protective details to multiple judges handling high-profile Trump administration cases.

**Mechanism 4 — Shadow docket normalization**. The *Wilcox* stay is the paradigmatic case of shadow docket use to achieve merits outcomes without merits process. Shadow docket orders — typically stays and emergency injunctions issued without full briefing, argument, or reasoned opinions — now routinely shape major constitutional questions. The Brennan Center's analysis documents that the Supreme Court has issued more emergency shadow docket orders favorable to the Trump administration in a shorter period than in any prior administration's tenure. Each shadow docket order that produces an irreversible structural effect (officials fired, agencies without quorums, policies in effect pending lower court review) shifts the baseline against which the ultimate merits ruling operates. By the time *Trump v. Slaughter* is decided in June 2026, the practical consequences of the May 2025 shadow docket stay will have been in effect for thirteen months.

### 2.3 Trump v. Slaughter: The Pending Formal Ruling

*Trump v. Slaughter* (argued December 8, 2025, decision expected late June 2026) will determine the formal constitutional status of independent agency removal protections. The case consolidates the FTC commissioner firing with the Wilcox/Harris litigation. Solicitor General Sauer argued that "*Humphrey's Executor* was always egregiously wrong." The Republican-appointed majority appeared at oral argument to lean toward limiting or overruling *Humphrey's Executor*.

Three options remain:

**Option 1 — Full overruling**: All independent agency officers are removable at will by the president. NLRB, FTC, FEC, CFPB, MSPB, EEOC removal protections eliminated. Federal Reserve independence threatened, though the May 2025 shadow order's explicit carve-out suggests a majority may preserve Fed independence separately.

**Option 2 — Functional narrowing**: *Humphrey's Executor* limited to agencies with quasi-judicial functions, eliminating removal protection for FTC and NLRB commissioners while preserving it for entities closer to the classic administrative court model.

**Option 3 — Prospective limitation only**: Existing commissioners protected; future legislation prohibited from creating comparable protections. Least likely given the administration's posture and the D.C. Circuit ruling already in effect.

The functional reality as of April 2026 is that *Humphrey's Executor* is already effectively overruled through the shadow docket pathway. The NLRB has been without a quorum since May 2025. Federal labor law proceedings have halted. *Slaughter* will convert this functional reality into formal constitutional doctrine.

## Section 3: Reform Architecture — What Survives the Current Landscape

### 3.1 What Cannot Be Secured Through Ordinary Statute

The *Wilcox*-to-*Slaughter* sequence establishes a structural finding with direct implications for the democratic recovery proposal: independent agency protection cannot be secured through statute alone in the current constitutional interpretation environment. Every statutory "for cause" removal protection the administrative state has built since 1935 is now subject to presidential override, with the Supreme Court providing shadow-docket authorization in advance of formal merits rulings.

This finding should be integrated into Domain 2 (executive accountability), Domain 13 (constitutional amendments), and Domain 34 (congressional power of the purse) as a baseline constraint: any reform mechanism that depends on independent agency authority — NLRB labor protections, FTC competition enforcement, FEC campaign finance oversight, CFPB consumer protection — is structurally vulnerable absent constitutional amendment.

### 3.2 Mechanisms That Remain Viable

**Private rights of action**: Explicitly creating citizen-suit provisions in every reform statute, allowing private enforcement of the same standards that agencies enforce, removes dependence on agency initiative and creates a distributed enforcement model resilient to political agency capture. If the NLRB cannot enforce the NLRA because it lacks a quorum, workers with explicit private rights of action can sue directly.

**State enforcement pathways**: State attorneys general retain full standing and authority. Multi-state AG coalitions have proven durable through *CASA* and the immunity doctrine. Strengthening state enforcement mechanisms — state analog laws mirroring federal protections, state court jurisdiction over federal officials acting in their states — remains viable architecture.

**Constitutional amendment for independent agency protection**: An amendment explicitly protecting independent agencies from at-will removal — analogous to how Article III's "during good behavior" tenure clause protects federal judges — may be the only durable architecture. Domain 13's amendment priorities should include this explicitly.

**Protecting Article III courts from jurisdiction stripping**: Congress retains authority under Article III to define and limit the appellate jurisdiction of federal courts — a power the Republican majority has considered using to strip district courts of nationwide injunction authority, and which some conservative legal scholars have proposed for other subject-matter areas. Statutory drafting should create explicit Article III jurisdiction for reform enforcement to resist future jurisdiction-stripping attempts.

## **April 2026 Update: Removal Power Operationalized — NLRB, CFPB, and State Enforcement Architecture**

### Trump v. Slaughter: Decision Still Pending, Practical Reality Already in Force

As of late April 2026, the Supreme Court has not yet issued its formal ruling in *Trump v. Slaughter* — the decision is expected in late June 2026. But the practical consequences of the May 2025 shadow docket stay have now been in effect for nearly thirteen months. The constitutional doctrine is therefore being written by the post-*Wilcox* factual reality, not by the forthcoming opinion. This is the shadow docket's most consequential institutional feature: by the time the Court issues a formal ruling, the disruption to independent agency operations is complete, the case law is settled in the lower courts (the D.C. Circuit's December 2025 ruling on the merits already holds that NLRB and MSPB removal protections are unconstitutional), and the only question for the formal opinion is how broadly the constitutional principle is stated and whether any carve-outs survive.

The four plausible outcomes and their practical significance as of April 2026:

**Full overruling of *Humphrey's Executor***: All independent agency commissioners — FTC, FEC, FCC, NLRB, MSPB, EEOC, NRC — lose statutory removal protection. The Federal Reserve's carve-out (explicitly preserved in the *Wilcox* shadow order) would be tested in subsequent litigation. The CFPB, whose single-director structure was already partially addressed in *Seila Law* (2020), would see its removal protections definitively eliminated.

**Functional narrowing**: *Humphrey's Executor* is limited to agencies performing quasi-judicial functions with no enforcement authority. Under this framing, the FTC and NLRB — which have substantial enforcement authority — lose protection, while entities like the Merit Systems Protection Board and Administrative Conference of the United States might retain it. The practical effect is similar to full overruling for the most consequential regulatory agencies.

**Prospective-only limitation**: The least likely outcome, now that the D.C. Circuit has already upheld the firings on the merits.

**Federal Reserve carve-out preserved**: The strongest judicial signals from oral argument indicated that the majority may preserve the Federal Reserve's removal protection, characterizing it as "uniquely structured." The political economy of this carve-out is significant: financial markets priced in Federal Reserve independence as a baseline for dollar stability, and the conservative justices who are most sympathetic to executive power over regulatory agencies have obvious institutional incentives to preserve the carve-out. Treasury Secretary and Wall Street have historically been more protective of Fed independence than of labor board or consumer protection agency independence.

### NLRB in April 2026: What a Captured Board Looks Like in Operation

The NLRB's April 2026 status is the clearest available case study of what the *Wilcox*-to-*Slaughter* trajectory means for regulatory agency function in practice. The Board has a quorum of three members: Chairman James R. Murphy (Trump-appointed), Member Scott A. Mayer (Trump-appointed), and Member David M. Prouty (Biden-appointed). The 2-1 Republican majority means:

- **The Board is issuing decisions** — but under a Republican majority that will reverse Biden-era precedents where it can. Under longstanding Board practice, reversal of precedent generally requires three aligned members. The 2-1 composition means overruling major Biden-era decisions (joint employer standard, expanded protected concerted activity doctrine) requires one additional Republican member — two of five seats remain vacant.
- **The General Counsel is independent from the Board**. Crystal Stowe Carey, Biden's GC appointee (sworn in January 2026), issued case-handling guidance directing regional offices to "focus on priority investigations and the protection of employees' rights of free association." The GC controls prosecution — which cases the NLRB prosecutes as Unfair Labor Practices. The Republican-majority Board controls adjudication — how those cases are decided on the merits. This split creates a structural tension: the GC can bring pro-worker cases; the Board can decide them against workers.
- **The removal power ruling creates ongoing structural instability**. The D.C. Circuit's December 2025 ruling that the president can remove NLRB members without cause means that any of the current members — including Prouty and Carey — can be removed by executive action before *Slaughter* is even decided. The Board is operating on a foundation that could be pulled at any time.

**The quorum-less period's legacy**: From May 2025 (when the Supreme Court's *Wilcox* shadow order took effect) through January 2026 (when the new members were sworn in), the NLRB could not issue binding decisions for approximately eight months. During that period, the Poster Compliance Center estimated that thousands of unfair labor practice cases and union election proceedings were delayed or dismissed for administrative reasons related to the quorum gap. This is the most direct democratic accountability impact of the removal power expansion: not the formal legal ruling, but the operational disruption to the agency's enforcement function while the constitutional question was being litigated.

### CFPB Operational Collapse and the D.C. Circuit Standoff

The CFPB's April 2026 status completes the independent agency capture picture. The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau has withdrawn over a dozen final and proposed rules under its deregulatory agenda, closed 76% of its supervisory actions, and reduced its workforce to approximately 556 employees (per DOJ workforce reduction plan). A D.C. Circuit showdown over the CFPB's structure was active as of April 22, 2026 — plaintiffs were opposing efforts to fast-track agency downsizing while also "surprisingly support[ing] remand to district court." The D.C. Circuit proceeding is the last live judicial contest over the CFPB's institutional survival.

The Consumer Financial Protection Act gives state attorneys general direct authority to enforce the CFPA's federal consumer protection provisions. This is the statutory state enforcement pathway the reform section documents as "viable architecture." Multiple state AGs — New York (Leticia James), California (Rob Bonta), Oregon (Dan Rayfield) — have explicitly positioned themselves as the enforcement substitute for the CFPB's federal role. Skadden's March 2026 analysis identifies the specific states where AG consumer financial enforcement is most active, with New York and California leading in staffing expansion with attorneys who have prior federal enforcement experience.

The practical limitation of state AG enforcement as a CFPB substitute: state AG enforcement reaches companies that do business in the state, but cannot establish national precedent, cannot access CFPB's supervisory authority over large bank holding companies, and cannot initiate the type of systematic market monitoring that the CFPB's examination function performed. A state AG enforcement action against a single predatory lender in one state does not provide the systemic deterrence that CFPB's market-wide supervision created. The state enforcement substitute is better than nothing; it is not equivalent.

### Revised Reform Architecture: What April 2026 Confirms

The reform assessment in Section 3 remains accurate but requires two updates based on April 2026 developments:

**Private rights of action are now the most time-sensitive priority**. With the NLRB operating under a partisan majority, the CFPB operationally collapsed, and *Slaughter* pending, the window for preserving any worker and consumer protection depends on whether private enforcement rights are explicitly created before agencies lose their enforcement capacity permanently. The pattern in current NLRB operation is instructive: General Counsel Carey can file cases, but the Board can decide them against workers. Private rights of action bypass the Board's adjudicative function entirely. State legislatures in California, New York, Illinois, Washington, and Minnesota have jurisdiction to create private rights of action under state labor and consumer protection law that track federal standards — and should do so before the *Slaughter* ruling creates a formal constitutional precedent that weakens the argument for federal agency enforcement in courts.

**The state AG enforcement architecture is more developed than the reform section suggests**. The pattern identified in Skadden (March 2026) and Morgan Lewis (May 2025) — state AGs explicitly repositioning as enforcement substitutes for federal agencies — represents a genuine systemic adaptation, not merely individual enforcement actions. The advocacy implication: organizations doing state-level work should prioritize supporting AG budget requests for dedicated federal-enforcement-substitute units, and should facilitate information sharing between AG offices about enforcement strategies against national companies that operate in multiple states.

### 4.4 Appellate Capture Mechanism Four: Coordinated Multi-Circuit Docket Control

The most significant dimension of the May-June confirmation wave is that it enables coordinated multi-circuit strategy on specific dockets. The Trump administration's Justice Department can file cert-oppositions in circuits with Trump-appointed majorities, knowing those circuits will reliably decline to certify questions to SCOTUS. This is doctrinal capture rather than individual case capture: by controlling enough circuits, the administration can prevent legal questions from reaching the Supreme Court at all, because there is no circuit split to trigger certiorari jurisdiction.

Examples that are already operative:

- **Immigration law**: The Fifth Circuit's Trump-appointed majority (8 of 14) can dominate immigration appeals; hostile circuits like the Ninth (where Democratic appointees still hold majority) cannot generate circuit splits because the Fifth controls the most immigration appeals by caseload.
- **Labor law**: A Seventh Circuit with 6+ Trump appointees will reliably reach pro-business outcomes on NLRA questions, preventing the circuit splits that would necessitate Supreme Court intervention.
- **Voting rights**: The Fifth Circuit's structurally Trump-appointed majority can uphold voting restriction policies knowing that other circuits (Second, Ninth) may strike them down, but the caseload concentration in the Fifth allows administration appeals to dominate the narrative.

This circuit-by-circuit capture mechanism is less visible than individual judge replacement, but it is more consequential: it shifts the baseline of what outcomes are considered "normal" across entire fields of law.

### 4.5 The State Attorney General Counter-Vector

State attorneys general have emerged as the most reliable check on appellate capture at the circuit level. The Seventh and Ninth Circuits remain homes to Democratic-appointee-majority panels that can be reliably assigned to voting rights, environmental, and labor cases through strategic amici curation and briefing priorities.

New York Attorney General Leticia James has been particularly active: as of April 2026, she had filed interventions in 23 circuit court cases involving voting rights, environmental deregulation, and labor law. The strategic logic is defensive: by maintaining state AG standing to appeal, she preserves the option to relitigate adverse Fifth Circuit decisions in other circuits or to create circuit splits that force Supreme Court review.

The limitation of this approach: it works only when there is a genuine inter-circuit disagreement on a federal law question. If the Fifth Circuit and Seventh Circuit reach the same outcome (even via different reasoning), there is no circuit split and no Supreme Court jurisdiction. This is why the May-June confirmation wave's timing is strategically critical for the state AG coalition: if they can identify circuit splits before Trump's new appointees are on the bench, they can lock in competing precedents. After June 2026, the window for creating productive circuit splits narrows substantially.
