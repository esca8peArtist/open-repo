## Key Findings

- The United States fell to **57th on the 2025 RSF World Press Freedom Index**, a drop of 13 places over two years, with RSF classifying the global state of press freedom as a "difficult situation" for the first time in the Index's history and describing the Trump administration's second term as producing an "alarming deterioration" indicative of an "authoritarian shift."
- The Trump administration's second term has produced **76 documented federal actions against journalists** (Poynter, 2025), including the DOJ rescission of journalist subpoena protections, the FBI raid on a Washington Post reporter's home, eight FCC investigations targeting specific news organizations for editorial content, the elimination of $1.1 billion in public broadcasting funding, and the 85% gutting of the U.S. Agency for Global Media — which served 361 million people weekly in authoritarian environments.
- The **structural collapse of local news** has produced 213 news desert counties (up from ~150 twenty years ago), with 50 million Americans having limited or no access to reliable local news, creating the conditions under which democratic misinformation spreads unchecked and local government accountability disappears.
- The **absence of a federal shield law** — despite the PRESS Act passing the House unanimously in January 2024 — leaves reporters dependent on a patchwork of 49 state laws with no protection in federal court, at precisely the moment when federal enforcement authority is being weaponized against journalism.
- **Recovery requires action on five simultaneous fronts**: restoring DOJ journalist protection guidelines, enacting the federal PRESS Act, reforming FCC ownership rules with anti-consolidation authority, establishing secure public broadcasting funding independent of annual appropriations, and building the nonprofit journalism infrastructure that is currently the only growing segment of independent local news.

## Section 1: First Amendment Doctrine in the Post-Capture Era

### 1.1 The New York Times v. Sullivan Standard Under Pressure

The foundational framework for press freedom in American constitutional law is *New York Times Co. v. Sullivan*, 376 U.S. 254 (1964). The unanimous decision established the "actual malice" standard: public officials cannot recover for defamation unless they can prove that a statement was made with knowledge of its falsity or with reckless disregard for truth. The Court's reasoning was explicitly structural — it held that the First Amendment requires "breathing space" for criticism of public officials because erroneous statements are inevitable in free debate, and the threat of libel judgments would produce self-censorship as damaging to the press as direct suppression.

*Sullivan* extended to public figures more broadly in *Curtis Publishing Co. v. Butts*, 388 U.S. 130 (1967), and the doctrine has protected investigative journalism against what would otherwise be financially ruinous litigation for six decades. The standard is not absolute — actual malice can be proven through evidence of deliberate fabrication or purposeful avoidance of known facts — but it has established a constitutional floor that has generally allowed aggressive coverage of powerful actors.

That floor is now under pressure from two directions simultaneously.

**Supreme Court skepticism.** Justices Clarence Thomas and Neil Gorsuch have each written opinions explicitly calling for *Sullivan* to be reconsidered. Thomas, in *McKee v. Cosby* (2019), argued the case had "no relation to the text, history, or structure of the Constitution." Gorsuch, dissenting from denial of certiorari in *Berisha v. Lawson* (2021), argued that modern media conditions had fundamentally changed since 1964, that the Internet had "democratized information" in ways that reduced the need for special press protection, and that the actual malice standard was "an increasingly difficult doctrine to justify." Both justices have signaled receptivity to a challenge if the right case were properly framed. In March 2025, the Supreme Court declined to grant certiorari in Steve Wynn's challenge to *Sullivan* — Wynn is a Trump donor who sued the Associated Press over sexual misconduct reporting — but the skepticism on the bench has not disappeared, merely awaited a cleaner vehicle.

**State-level erosion.** While the federal constitutional floor has held, several states have moved to narrow anti-SLAPP protections and expand the categories of claims that can survive a motion to dismiss before the defamation standard is tested. The Florida Legislature passed HB 991 in 2023 — championed by Governor DeSantis — which would have created a presumption of actual malice for "anonymous sources," dramatically expanded who qualifies as a "private figure" (narrowing the public figure category and thus *Sullivan*'s protection), and limited the discovery available to defendants in defamation cases. A federal district court preliminarily enjoined the law. Texas similarly passed legislation restricting defamation plaintiffs' burden to obtain early dismissal. These state-level maneuvers represent a coordinated strategy to shift the burden of litigation costs onto defendants even where ultimate liability is unlikely — the chilling effect operates before any verdict.

### 1.2 SLAPP Litigation as Speech Suppression Infrastructure

Strategic Lawsuits Against Public Participation (SLAPPs) have become the primary mechanism through which powerful actors suppress unfavorable reporting without relying on the substantive merits of defamation claims. The mechanism does not require winning in court — the financial and operational costs of defending complex litigation over months or years produce self-censorship, source reluctance, and resource diversion that serve suppression purposes regardless of outcome.

A 2025 analysis by NYU's First Amendment Watch identified **500 cases decided on anti-SLAPP motions in 2024 alone, including 69 involving media defendants** — the most in any year on record. The caseload covered every medium and every level of the industry, from individual reporters to major newsrooms. The Columbia Journalism Review documented in 2024 that SLAPP litigation costs had increased to a point where defending a single multi-count defamation case could cost a regional news organization $500,000 to $2 million before trial — a figure that exceeds the annual operating budget of the majority of local newsrooms.

The federal court gap is particularly severe. **Thirty-three states, Washington D.C., and Guam** have enacted statutory anti-SLAPP protections as of 2025. Ohio enacted the Uniform Public Expression Protection Act (UPEPA) in January 2025. Pennsylvania amended its law in July 2024. Minnesota in May 2024. Michigan enacted comprehensive protections in December 2025. But federal courts — where defamation claims against national media are most commonly filed, and where wealthy plaintiffs with national resources direct their most consequential cases — have no governing federal anti-SLAPP statute. The result is forum-shopping: plaintiffs file in federal court specifically to avoid state anti-SLAPP protections. Congressman Jamie Raskin, Congressman Kevin Kiley, and Senator Ron Wyden introduced the Free Speech Protection Act (bipartisan) to close this gap, but the legislation has not advanced in the 119th Congress.

### 1.3 Editorial Independence: Legal Protections and Their Limits

Editorial independence — the principle that government may not dictate content decisions to private press — has two primary legal foundations: the First Amendment's direct prohibition on content-based restrictions, and the Supreme Court's 1974 decision in *Miami Herald Publishing Co. v. Tornillo*, which struck down a Florida right-of-reply law on the grounds that compelled publication is as unconstitutional as prohibited publication. Together these principles protect newsrooms from direct government mandates over what to print.

The practical erosion of this protection operates through economic leverage rather than direct mandate. The FCC's threat of license revocation or license-condition proceedings — documented in detail in Section 2 below — creates a legally indirect but practically effective form of editorial intimidation. When FCC Chair Brendan Carr in March 2026 warned broadcasters about their coverage of the Iran war, no formal order issued. None was needed. The precedent of eight active investigations into specific news organizations' editorial content sent the signal without requiring a formal rule.

### 1.4 Prior Restraint and the National Security Exception

*New York Times Co. v. United States*, 403 U.S. 713 (1971) — the Pentagon Papers case — established that the government bears a heavy presumption against prior restraint that is among the highest in constitutional law. The Court's per curiam opinion, and the six concurrences, collectively established that even grave and immediate harm to national security must be demonstrated with specificity before any court may enjoin publication.

The 2025-2026 period has produced the most serious pressure on this standard since 1971. The Pentagon announced revised press guidelines in early 2025 restricting embedded journalist access, documentation of specific operations, and use of certain classified categories of information in published reports. Seth Stern of the Freedom of the Press Foundation characterized these guidelines as "a classic case of unconstitutional prior restraint," invoking the same doctrine the Nixon administration lost in 1971. Unlike that case, however, the 2025 guidelines operate through access denial and contractual conditions on embedding agreements rather than court-ordered injunctions — a mechanism that avoids First Amendment review by removing the government's obligation to provide the access being conditioned.

The national security exception has also been invoked to justify the subpoena of journalists' records in leak investigations, a practice that DOJ guidelines had constrained under the Biden administration but which AG Bondi formally rescinded in April 2025. The implications for prior restraint are indirect but real: if sources fear that their communications with journalists will be subpoenaed and disclosed, they will not make those communications, which operates as a pre-publication restraint on the flow of information without a formal injunction against any publication.

### 1.5 Content Moderation as State Action Problem

*Moody v. NetChoice, LLC*, 603 U.S. ___ (2024) addressed First Amendment challenges to Florida and Texas laws requiring social media platforms to carry content they would otherwise moderate. The Supreme Court's unanimous decision (remanding for further proceedings) established that platforms' content moderation decisions — including editorial algorithms and curation — are protected First Amendment activity, and that states may not compel platforms to carry speech against their editorial judgment. This is the Tornillo principle applied to digital publishers.

The *Moody* ruling has a two-sided press freedom implication. On the one hand, it protects platform editorial independence from government compulsion. On the other, it confirms that platforms' own decisions to demote, demonetize, or remove journalism content are beyond direct First Amendment remedy — that the First Amendment binds government, not private actors. When the administration informally pressures platforms to modify their content policies — or when dominant distribution platforms algorithmically deprioritize journalism that conflicts with economic or political interests — the result is a speech environment shaped by nominally private actors operating in conditions of government pressure that the state action doctrine does not directly address.

### 1.6 Online Harassment as Systematic Speech Suppression

Online harassment — doxxing, coordinated abuse campaigns, swatting, cyberstalking — functions as a distributed speech suppression mechanism that operates outside formal legal channels but produces the same self-censorship effects as direct legal threat. UNESCO's global study found that **73 percent of women journalists have experienced online violence**, with one in four receiving threats of physical harm. The International Women's Media Foundation documented that 70% of women journalists had experienced harassment, threat, or attack online, and approximately one-third had considered leaving journalism because of it.

The 2024 U.S. case of journalists covering anti-immigrant conspiracy theories in Ohio — who were doxxed and swatted after their reporting generated extremist attention — illustrates the intersection of press targeting and organized harassment. The attack on these journalists was not legally cognizable as a press freedom violation (no government actor issued any order) but produced the identical result: reporters faced direct physical threat in connection with specific reporting. When harassment campaigns are sufficiently predictable that journalists self-censor to avoid triggering them, the mechanism has achieved prior restraint without any legal process.

The legal response to this gap is partial at best. Federal cyberstalking statutes (18 U.S.C. § 2261A) criminalize sustained campaigns of harassment, but prosecutorial resources are rarely directed toward protecting journalists. State cyberstalking laws vary widely. The FTC's authority over platforms that host coordinated harassment is untested. The UNESCO and International Women's Media Foundation recommendations focus on platform design obligations, newsroom security protocols, and digital safety training — mitigation rather than deterrent.

## Section 2: Media Ownership, Structure, and the Conditions for Independence

### 2.1 FCC Ownership Rules: Systematic Erosion

The FCC's media ownership rules were designed to prevent concentration that would eliminate the editorial diversity on which democratic discourse depends. The FCC's newspaper/broadcast cross-ownership rule, local TV ownership restrictions, and the national 39% audience reach cap were the primary regulatory instruments. All three have been substantially weakened or circumvented over the past decade.

The national broadcast cap — limiting any single owner to stations reaching no more than 39% of U.S. television households — has been the subject of sustained industry lobbying. Sinclair Broadcast Group CEO Chris Ripley stated publicly in late 2025 that he expected FCC Chair Brendan Carr to raise or eliminate the cap in the first half of 2026. Sinclair itself owns 179 local TV affiliates and in 2025 made a hostile acquisition bid for E.W. Scripps, having purchased nearly 10% of the company. If the cap is lifted or significantly raised, a single conservative-aligned broadcaster will reach audiences approaching or exceeding half the country's television households.

In July 2025, the Eighth Circuit vacated the FCC's prohibition on ownership of two of the top four local TV stations in a single market, removing a key local competition protection. This came after the FCC under Brendan Carr had already signaled its intent not to enforce existing local ownership restrictions aggressively. The combination — cap relaxation plus the loss of the top-four prohibition — would permit a broadcaster to own the dominant stations in a local market while also achieving near-national reach through network affiliates.

FCC Chair Carr has justified deregulation by citing competition from streaming platforms (Netflix, YouTube), arguing that the traditional broadcast regulatory framework is obsolete in a multi-platform environment. The argument's limitation is that it applies competition from different media types to justify eliminating competition within the broadcast media sector — a substitution that may reduce news diversity specifically in the political information categories that broadcast media still dominates, particularly among elderly voters and in rural areas without reliable broadband.

### 2.2 The AT&T-Time Warner Precedent and Vertical Integration

*United States v. AT&T Inc.*, decided by the D.C. Circuit in 2019, affirmed approval of AT&T's $85 billion acquisition of Time Warner (rebranded WarnerMedia). The decision established that vertical media mergers — combining a content owner (HBO, CNN, Turner) with a distribution platform (DirecTV, AT&T broadband) — require nuanced, evidence-based antitrust analysis rather than structural presumptions against combination. The D.C. Circuit panel held that speculative harms from "content leverage" were insufficient to block a merger.

The long-term consequences of the AT&T-Time Warner decision for editorial independence are documented but contested. CNN and other Time Warner editorial properties have faced documented pressure within the merged structure. CNN was subsequently sold to Warner Bros. Discovery; the merger's instability itself produced editorial disruption. The case's most significant press freedom implication is structural: it established that content-distribution vertical integration does not constitute a per se antitrust violation, making future challenges to media-telecom mergers dependent on specific and quantified harm evidence that is difficult to compile in advance of a merger.

### 2.3 The Local News Financial Crisis: Scale and Structure

The Medill School of Journalism's 2025 State of Local News report documents a structural collapse that has accelerated sharply:

- **213 counties are now news deserts** (up from 206 in 2024 and approximately 150 twenty years ago)
- **50 million Americans** have limited or no access to reliable local news
- **136 newspapers closed in the past year** — more than two per week
- The country has lost **nearly 3,500 newspapers and 270,000 newspaper jobs** over the past two decades
- 80% of news deserts are in counties classified as predominantly rural
- In states like Virginia, news deserts more than doubled in the period studied

The mechanism of collapse follows a well-documented pattern. Hedge fund acquisition produces asset extraction rather than reinvestment: Alden Global Capital, the second-largest newspaper owner in the United States, has consistently reduced newsroom headcounts faster than other ownership structures, cutting hard news positions — political and general assignment reporters — first. Private equity ownership produces similar dynamics through debt loading and operational cuts that prioritize short-term cash flows.

Gannett, the largest U.S. newspaper chain, has undergone successive rounds of layoffs while pursuing digital revenue models. The result is papers that maintain masthead identity while shedding the reporting capacity that constituted their public function. The Northwestern study found that a significant portion of nominally active newspapers publish fewer than one original local news story per week — a level of coverage insufficient to constitute meaningful democratic accountability journalism.

Research tracking post-acquisition outcomes found that counties that lose their only local newspaper experience measurable increases in local government costs and municipal bond interest rates — a direct economic measure of the value of local news accountability journalism. A 2018 study by Gao, Lee, and Murphy published in the *Journal of Finance* documented that the loss of local newspapers produced these fiscal efficiency reductions, providing quantitative evidence for what has previously been argued only qualitatively.

### 2.4 Public Broadcasting: Funding Structure and the Independence Paradox

The Corporation for Public Broadcasting (CPB) was created by the Public Broadcasting Act of 1967 specifically to provide a buffer between congressional appropriations and editorial decisions by PBS and NPR member stations. The structure included a two-year advance appropriation mechanism intended to prevent annual funding leverage from influencing coverage decisions. The independence design was intentional and well-documented in the legislative history.

The Trump administration's second term has effectively dismantled this structural protection. The sequence:

- May 2025: Trump issued an executive order directing CPB to cease federal funding for NPR and PBS, claiming ideological bias
- June 2025: Congress passed legislation on a party-line basis eliminating $1.1 billion in CPB funding through fiscal year 2027
- August 2025: CPB announced it was winding down operations
- September 2025: In a federal lawsuit, a judge found that the executive order violated the First Amendment and was "unlawful and unenforceable"
- March 2026: A second judge found Trump violated free speech when he ordered defunding of NPR

Despite favorable court rulings, the legislative rescission of $1.1 billion created structural damage independent of executive order validity. Approximately 330 PBS stations and 246 NPR affiliates faced the funding gap. PBS laid off one-third of its children's programming staff. Rural public radio stations — many of which serve as the sole news source for entire regions — announced service reductions.

The political independence paradox is now fully visible: the two-year advance appropriation mechanism, designed to insulate editorial decisions from political funding leverage, functioned exactly as designed when a single president tried to leverage it. But legislative majority action could eliminate the buffer entirely, as it did in 2025 — and no structural protection survives legislative action by a disciplined majority determined to eliminate it.

### 2.5 The USAGM Dismantlement and Global Media Independence

The U.S. Agency for Global Media (USAGM) oversight of Voice of America, Radio Free Europe/Radio Liberty, Radio Free Asia, Middle East Broadcasting Networks, and the Office of Cuba Broadcasting represented the United States' primary institutional commitment to independent journalism in authoritarian environments. The agency served approximately 361 million people weekly in nearly 50 languages — a figure that exceeded the populations of most individual nations.

The Trump administration's March 2025 executive order instructing USAGM to terminate grants produced immediate mass furloughs. More than 1,300 VOA and Office of Cuba Broadcasting employees were placed on administrative leave; layoff notices were sent to 639 VOA employees in June 2025, completing an 85% staff reduction. The practical effect was to silence independent news sources for hundreds of millions of people in Russia, China, Belarus, Cuba, Iran, and other authoritarian states at the moment when those sources were most needed as an information counterweight.

Federal court challenges partially restored operations. Judge Royce C. Lamberth of the D.C. Circuit blocked the shutdown in April 2025, finding it likely violated law and the Constitution. He ordered more than 1,000 VOA employees to return to work by March 23, 2026, after separately invalidating the appointment of Kari Lake as USAGM director. As of April 2026, the agency remains in operational uncertainty while appeals proceed.

### 2.6 Nonprofit Journalism: Growing Infrastructure, Insufficient Scale

Against the backdrop of for-profit collapse, nonprofit journalism represents the single growing sector of independent local news. The Institute for Nonprofit News (INN) 2025 Index documents:

- Approximately **400 digital-first nonprofit newsrooms** are INN members
- Combined revenue reached **$650-700 million** in 2024, a 14% increase from 2023
- Median member revenue: **$532,000** (up from $477,000 in 2023)
- **83% of local nonprofit newsrooms** increased revenue by at least 10% over the last three years
- For the first time, local news outlets constitute **51% of INN membership** (up from 48%)
- American Journalism Project raised $25 million from the Knight Foundation in February 2025 to support up to 60 nonprofit local news organizations

ProPublica pioneered the foundational model: major-donor and foundation-funded investigative journalism, with content distributed free to partner newsrooms. ProPublica's Pulitzer Prize record demonstrated that nonprofit journalism could achieve the highest standards of accountability reporting. The 19th News, launched in 2020 with a gender equity focus, demonstrated that issue-specific nonprofit journalism could build sustainable audiences. State News Lab networks — including states like New Jersey (NJ Spotlight) and Vermont (VTDigger) — have demonstrated that state-capital reporting can be effectively reconstructed as nonprofit infrastructure.

The limitation is geographic and scale. Nonprofit newsrooms have concentrated in metropolitan areas. The Medill study found that the "vast majority" of startup newsrooms have emerged in metro areas, leaving behind the rural and less affluent areas that are most likely to be news deserts. The $650-700 million in total nonprofit news revenue, while growing, remains small against the estimated $13 billion in annual newspaper revenue that existed twenty years ago.

## Section 3: Journalist Protection Mechanisms

### 3.1 The Federal Shield Law Gap and the PRESS Act

The United States has **no federal shield law**. This is the single most consequential structural gap in journalist protection: it means that when federal investigators, grand juries, or federal litigants seek journalists' confidential sources, communications, or unpublished work product, there is no federal statutory protection — only a contested and inconsistently applied constitutional privilege derived from *Branzburg v. Hayes*, 408 U.S. 665 (1972), where the Supreme Court declined to recognize an absolute First Amendment privilege against grand jury testimony.

*Branzburg* left substantial ambiguity: Justice Powell's concurring opinion suggested courts should engage in case-by-case balancing, and federal circuits have developed varying interpretations of what qualified privilege, if any, the First Amendment provides to journalists in federal proceedings. The result is a circuit-by-circuit patchwork with no predictable protection and no clear definition of who qualifies as a journalist.

The Protect Reporters from Exploitative State Spying Act (PRESS Act) would close this gap. The bill's provisions:

- Prohibits the federal government from compelling testimony or records from journalists except in specific, well-defined circumstances
- Provides an absolute privilege against disclosure of source identity except in cases involving prevention of death or serious bodily harm, or investigations where the journalist was directly involved in the alleged criminal activity
- Defines "covered journalist" broadly enough to include digital-first reporters, independent journalists, and emerging media organizations
- Applies to both federal grand jury proceedings and civil litigation involving federal courts

The House passed the PRESS Act unanimously in January 2024 — a genuinely bipartisan achievement reflecting recognition that source protection is prerequisite to investigative journalism that serves both conservative and liberal political interests. The bill then stalled in the Senate Judiciary Committee, blocked primarily by Senator Tom Cotton (R-AR), who argued the privilege would hinder national security and law enforcement investigations.

The Intercept documented in April 2025 that Senate Democrats failed to advance the PRESS Act during the brief window between the House passage and the end of the 118th Congress — characterizing this as a failure of priorities with severe consequences given the Bondi memo issued days later eliminating existing DOJ journalist protections.

### 3.2 The Bondi Memo and DOJ Guideline Rescission

On April 25, 2025, Attorney General Pam Bondi rescinded the Biden-era DOJ guidelines that had constrained journalist subpoenas. The Biden guidelines, building on Obama-era reforms accelerated after Trump's first-term secret seizure of journalist records, had established that the DOJ would not seek records or compel testimony from journalists in leak investigations except in cases involving imminent risk of death or serious bodily harm. The protection was not statutory — it operated through executive branch self-restraint codified in internal guidelines — and it was therefore reversible by the next AG without any congressional action required.

Bondi's memo stated explicitly that the purpose of rescission was to "identify and punish the source of improper leaks" — leaks defined as disclosures that "undermine President Trump's policies." The practical effect: federal investigators may now subpoena journalists' communications, phone records, and work product to identify government sources in any leak investigation touching administration priorities, subject only to internal DOJ leadership approval and advance notice to the targeted journalist.

The Reporters Committee for Freedom of the Press issued a detailed legal analysis of the Bondi memo, finding that: (1) the new standard is "not the rule of law but the rule of men," dependent entirely on AG discretion rather than legal obligation; (2) the "advance notice" provision provides process without substance, since journalists have no legal right to refuse compliance once notified; and (3) the scope of permissible leak investigations — defined as anything that "undermines" administration policies — is so broad as to potentially encompass most national security journalism.

Under Trump's first term, DOJ secretly obtained or attempted to obtain records from at least nine journalists in connection with leak investigations. The Biden guidelines were adopted specifically in response to these documented abuses. Their rescission returns the legal landscape to a state where the executive branch has broad authority to identify the sources of journalism it dislikes.

### 3.3 The Washington Post FBI Raid: Landmark Case

The FBI's January 14, 2026 search of Washington Post reporter Hannah Natanson's home — seizing her phone, work laptop, personal laptop, and smartwatch — represents the most significant law enforcement action against an American journalist's electronic records in the post-Pentagon Papers era. The search was executed pursuant to a warrant in connection with an investigation into a government contractor accused of retaining classified materials related to Venezuela.

Attorney General Bondi characterized the search as justified because Natanson was "obtaining and reporting classified and illegally leaked information." This framing is legally significant: it treats the journalistic act of receiving and publishing leaked classified information as criminal conduct by the journalist, a theory that has been rejected repeatedly in federal court since 1971 but which has never been definitively closed by Supreme Court decision.

The Post stated that Natanson was told she was not a target of the investigation — but the seizure of her electronic devices, which contained unpublished stories, source communications, and potentially identifying contact information, produced exactly the harm that the PRESS Act and DOJ journalist guidelines were designed to prevent. Sources who communicated with Natanson about this and potentially other stories faced exposure through the seized devices regardless of whether Natanson herself was charged.

Poynter's analysis characterized the raid as representing a "new threshold" in U.S. press freedom: the first documented execution of a search warrant against a Washington-based reporter's home in the modern era, with a rationale (receiving leaked classified information) that, if accepted as constitutional, would expose virtually any national security reporter to similar action.

### 3.4 Border Device Searches and the Fourth Amendment Gap

Journalists crossing U.S. borders face a documented vulnerability in confidential source protection: the border search exception to the Fourth Amendment allows Customs and Border Protection to search travelers' electronic devices without any warrant or probable cause showing. For journalists, this means that phones and laptops containing source communications, unpublished reporting materials, and potentially identifying contact information are legally accessible to government inspection at every port of entry.

The legal landscape is in flux. In *United States v. Sultanov*, a New York federal court held in 2024 that border agents must obtain a warrant based on probable cause before conducting "forensic" (deeper) searches of electronic devices, finding that warrantless searches violated both the Fourth and First Amendments — with the First Amendment violation grounded specifically on the chilling effect on press freedom and source protection. The RCFP has filed briefs in *Alasaad v. Wolf* and related cases arguing that even basic device searches violate the First Amendment as applied to journalists.

In practical terms, the absence of a clear Supreme Court rule means that foreign journalists, international correspondents, and domestic reporters returning from overseas face unpredictable exposure at every border crossing. The Electronic Frontier Foundation and Freedom of the Press Foundation maintain comprehensive device security protocols for journalists crossing U.S. borders (updated for 2026), advising specific steps including factory reset of devices, use of cloud storage accessed only after crossing, and physical separation of source contact information from travel devices. These protocols mitigate risk but do not eliminate it and impose significant operational burden.

### 3.5 Visa Restrictions and the Foreign Press Exclusion Problem

Foreign journalists covering the United States face increasing exposure to the State Department's broad authority to deny or revoke visas on national security or foreign policy grounds. The same legal mechanism documented in Domain 27 (the *Khalil*, *Öztürk*, and *Mahdawi* cases involving student political speech) applies directly to foreign journalists: Secretary of State Rubio's "adverse foreign policy interest" determination authority under INA Section 237(a)(4)(C) can be applied to journalists whose reporting is deemed to conflict with U.S. foreign policy interests.

The CPJ and RSF have documented multiple cases of foreign journalists being denied entry or having credentials revoked in connection with coverage that the administration characterized as hostile or inaccurate. The AP White House exclusion case (*Associated Press v. Budowich*) established the legal dimensions of this problem domestically: in April 2025, a federal district judge ordered AP reporters reinstated to the White House press pool after the administration excluded them for refusing to adopt the term "Gulf of America." However, the D.C. Circuit partially stayed the injunction in June 2025, allowing the exclusion to continue in restricted-access spaces while the case proceeded.

The AP case illustrates the legal gap: the First Amendment prohibits viewpoint-based exclusion from government-controlled press access, but the remedy is uncertain when the executive claims scheduling discretion and courts are reluctant to micromanage presidential access decisions. The outcome — an administration that excludes the world's largest news agency from Oval Office coverage for refusing to adopt a preferred term — documents the vulnerability.

### 3.6 Legal Defense Infrastructure: Current Capacity

The Reporters Committee for Freedom of the Press provides the primary legal defense infrastructure for journalists facing First Amendment, FOIA, and court access issues. In 2025:

- The RCFP's 2025 Freedom of the Press Awards raised over $2 million for the organization's legal services fund — the most financially successful event in its 55-year history
- The organization expanded its Local Legal Initiative to Colorado, Indiana, Oklahoma, Pennsylvania, and Tennessee, with expansion to Louisiana, Mississippi, Michigan, and Minnesota in 2026
- A 24/7 legal hotline provides free, privileged, and secure legal consultation to working journalists
- The organization is "nearly doubling" its Local Legal Initiative in response to increased demand

In May 2025, five major press freedom organizations — CPJ, Freedom of the Press Foundation, International Women's Media Foundation, PEN America, and RCFP — launched the **Journalist Assistance Network (JAN)**, a coordinated resource network providing legal, digital security, physical safety, and immigration guidance to journalists facing threats. The network was established specifically in response to the post-November 2024 surge in requests for assistance — CPJ alone trained nearly 1,000 U.S. journalists in safety skills in 2025.

The FPF's digital security infrastructure includes SecureDrop (used by more than 50 major news organizations globally for protected source communications), regular journalist security training, and the 2026 Journalist Digital Security Checklist. The combination of legal defense (RCFP) and digital security (FPF) infrastructure represents the primary functional protection available to journalists in the absence of statutory federal shield protections.

## Section 4: International Precedent — Protection Models and Cautionary Cases

### 4.1 The European Media Freedom Act: A Comprehensive Framework

The European Media Freedom Act (EMFA), adopted by the European Parliament on March 13, 2024 and entered into force May 7, 2024, with full application from August 8, 2025, represents the most comprehensive statutory framework for press freedom protection enacted by any major democracy. Its core provisions:

**Source protection and anti-surveillance mandates.** Member states are prohibited from pressing journalists or editors to disclose sources through detention, sanctions, office searches, or the installation of spyware. Any use of surveillance tools against journalists requires case-by-case judicial authorization in connection with investigation of serious crimes punishable by custodial sentence, and subjects must be informed after the fact with rights to judicial review. This is a higher standard of protection than current U.S. practice in several respects: it provides statutory rather than executive-guideline protection, requires judicial authorization rather than executive self-restraint, and applies to all forms of surveillance, not only subpoenas.

**Editorial independence obligations.** Member states must protect media independence; all forms of government intervention in editorial decisions are prohibited. This codifies the *Tornillo* principle in a form that binds member states affirmatively — they must act to protect editorial independence, not merely refrain from violating it.

**The European Board for Media Services (EBMS).** The EMFA established a new independent regulatory body comprising representatives from national media authorities, operational since February 2025, tasked with consistent application of EU media law framework and serving as an early warning system for media freedom threats. The budget allocation of €3.1 million for a rapid-response protection mechanism provides material support for journalists under threat.

**Anti-SLAPP directive.** On April 11, 2024, the European Parliament also approved the EU Anti-SLAPP Directive, requiring member states to implement procedural protections for targets of abusive litigation including early dismissal mechanisms, cost shifting to SLAPP plaintiffs, and specific protections for cross-border cases. The Directive applies to "cases with cross-border implications affecting the functioning of the internal market" but includes important protections for journalists specifically.

**Limitation.** Hungary has made no progress aligning domestic law with the EMFA since its full entry into effect in August 2025. The EMFA's enforcement mechanism depends on EU institutional pressure and ultimately on infringement proceedings — a slow-moving tool. The Hungarian case (discussed below) demonstrates that formal regulatory frameworks are insufficient protection against member states with governments committed to media capture.

### 4.2 Mexico's Federal Mechanism: Documented Failure at Scale

Mexico's Federal Mechanism for the Protection of Human Rights Defenders and Journalists (SGTSC) was established in 2012 following international pressure to address Mexico's position as the most dangerous country for journalists in the Western Hemisphere. The Mechanism enrolls journalists who face documented threats under state protection protocols, theoretically providing physical protection, relocation, and security measures.

The evidence of the Mechanism's failure is systematic and documented:

- **Eight journalists killed while enrolled** in the Mechanism over the past seven years
- In 2024, at least seven media professionals were murdered in Mexico, including multiple journalists with documented previous threat histories
- Catarino Guerrero was shot and killed on January 17, 2025, despite having been under federal protection since 2014 — eleven years of documented threat history that did not prevent his murder
- RSF documented a second journalist killed under state protection in 2025
- The UN High Commissioner for Human Rights recommended the Mechanism adopt a prevention approach, noting it "did not address the root causes of risk" — the fundamental critique that protection programs treat symptoms without addressing the structural conditions producing violence

Mexico has lost at least 141 journalists since the turn of the century, with at least 61 killings directly related to their work. Impunity rates remain near 90%. The Atlantic Council's analysis identifies violence against journalists as a "tool to restrict press freedom" deployed deliberately by organized crime organizations with political protection, not merely incidental criminal violence.

The Mexico case provides several key lessons for the U.S. recovery context. First, protection mechanisms that are under-resourced relative to the threat environment are worse than inadequate — they create false assurance without functional protection. Second, protection mechanisms that treat journalists as individual security problems rather than addressing the structural conditions (impunity, corruption, organized crime-government nexus) that produce attacks cannot succeed at scale. Third, the effectiveness of protection depends on credible prosecution of perpetrators — without prosecutions, protection mechanisms merely displace rather than deter attacks.

### 4.3 India: Decade of Documented Press Suppression (2014-2024)

India's press freedom trajectory under the BJP government provides the most detailed case study of a large democracy's systematic erosion of journalistic independence through legal and regulatory mechanisms. The trajectory is directly relevant to the U.S. context because it demonstrates that nominally democratic governance, with formal constitutional press protections, can produce press suppression statistics comparable to authoritarian states.

India ranked **159th out of 180 countries** on the 2024 RSF World Press Freedom Index — a decline from 140th in 2014, the year the BJP came to power. RSF has described India's media as in an "unofficial state of emergency." The mechanisms of suppression:

**Weaponized legal tools.** India's colonial-era sedition law (Section 124A of the Indian Penal Code) has been applied to journalists critical of the government at unprecedented rates. Between 2010 and 2021, police charged 13,000 people under sedition provisions, with a conviction rate of 0.1% — statistically demonstrating that the charges serve as harassment mechanisms rather than legitimate prosecutions. The Unlawful Activities Prevention Act (UAPA), which allows detention without trial for years, has been used against at least 15 journalists since 2014. Siddique Kappan spent 28 months in jail without trial after being arrested while traveling to cover the Hathras gang rape case.

**Concentrated ownership.** India's media landscape has become dominated by conglomerates with financial interests in maintaining government access. Mukesh Ambani's Reliance Industries, which has close relationships with the BJP government, owns Network18, which controls CNN-News18, CNBC TV18, and more than 60 other channels. The concentration of ownership in government-aligned hands has produced systematic self-censorship through economic incentive rather than direct government command.

**Between 2014 and 2019, 40 journalists were killed** and at least 198 severe attacks were documented. The International Journalists' Network found 36 journalists detained overall in this period.

The India case demonstrates that the following combination produces effective press suppression without formal censorship: weaponized but rarely successful criminal prosecution (to impose costs and deter), concentrated ownership (to align economic interests with government), regulatory pressure through licensing and tax authority, and targeted harassment of specific outlets that maintain independence. Several elements of this combination are now visible in the U.S. context.

### 4.4 Hungary and Turkey: Media Capture as Counterexample

**Hungary** provides the clearest European case study of media capture through regulatory and economic means within an EU member state. Since 2010, the Fidesz-KDNP government has:

- Established direct political control over the public service broadcaster (MTVA)
- Created a government-aligned media authority (NMHH) with broad licensing and regulatory powers
- Used state advertising budgets to financially reward pro-government media while starving independent outlets
- Established the Central European Press and Media Foundation (KESMA), which consolidated 500+ pro-government media outlets under a single foundation
- Created the Sovereignty Protection Office, which launched investigations into independent outlet Átlátszó and Transparency International Hungary for "foreign influence" — using the same legal theory as U.S. FARA enforcement against civil society
- Has failed to align domestic law with the EU's EMFA since its August 2025 full application

The Hungarian model illustrates how capture can be achieved without formal censorship: the independent outlets still exist and technically operate. But they operate with inadequate revenue (state advertising withheld), under regulatory surveillance (licensing threats), and with the constant background threat of government-sponsored litigation. Hungary's press freedom environment has been repeatedly cited by IPI, CPJ, and ECPMF as among the worst in EU member states. With elections scheduled for 2026, media freedom organizations have flagged the risk of further deterioration.

**Turkey** is the more extreme case. As of December 2025, 24 journalists were held in detention. In the first half of 2024 alone, 515 journalists were prosecuted. Turkey regularly ranks among the world's worst jailers of journalists, and RTÜK (the Radio and Television Supreme Council) revokes broadcast licenses without prior notice or justification as a routine regulatory tool. Turkey's model illustrates the end state of unchecked executive control over media regulation: licensing becomes an instrument of direct suppression rather than technical standards compliance.

The comparative lesson is that media capture follows a predictable institutional sequence: first, regulatory bodies are packed with political appointees; second, state advertising budgets are redirected to compliant outlets; third, licensing and investigation powers are used to impose costs on independent media; fourth, economic pressure drives consolidation toward government-aligned ownership; fifth, the institutional landscape is so transformed that formal censorship is unnecessary because no independent outlet has the resources to challenge power. The U.S. is at an early stage of this sequence, but the FCC investigations, NPR/PBS defunding, and pressure on specific outlets represent discernible movement along it.

### 4.5 UNESCO Standards and International Coordination Architecture

The UNESCO Programme for the Safety of Journalists and the Issue of Impunity, established through the UN Plan of Action on the Safety of Journalists and the Issue of Impunity, provides the primary international framework for journalist protection standards. Key elements:

- **UN Security Council Resolution 2222 (2015)** condemned attacks on journalists and media workers in armed conflict and called for stronger implementation of existing international humanitarian law protections
- **UNESCO's "Safety of Journalists and the Issue of Impunity" indicators** provide a standard monitoring framework across UN member states
- The **ICCPR Article 19** protects freedom of expression as a fundamental human right, with the Human Rights Committee's General Comment 34 providing authoritative interpretation including specific protections for journalism
- **UN Special Rapporteur on Freedom of Opinion and Expression** produces country-specific reports; the 2025 report on the United States raised concerns about DOJ journalist subpoena practices and the USAGM dismantlement

IFEX — the global network of more than 70 NGOs that monitors free-expression violations — coordinates advocacy across CPJ, RSF, and regional press freedom organizations. In 2025, IFEX joined CPJ and 16 partner organizations in developing a national action plan template for media freedom, providing a model for comprehensive statutory and institutional reform.

## Section 5: Recovery Pathways

### 5.1 Federal Shield Law: The PRESS Act as Baseline

The single highest-priority federal legislative action for media freedom recovery is enactment of the PRESS Act or equivalent comprehensive federal shield legislation. The bill passed the House unanimously in January 2024 — evidence of bipartisan recognition of its importance — and the primary obstacle is Senate floor time and blocking by a small number of senators who oppose source protection on law enforcement grounds.

A comprehensive federal shield law should:
- Provide absolute source identity protection except where disclosure is necessary to prevent imminent death or serious bodily harm, or where the journalist was a direct participant in the criminal conduct under investigation
- Establish a qualified privilege for non-source materials (notes, communications, unpublished content) requiring a showing that: (1) the information is essential and not otherwise obtainable; (2) there is compelling government interest; and (3) the interests are balanced through judicial in camera review before disclosure
- Define "covered journalist" broadly to include freelancers, digital journalists, documentary filmmakers, and emerging news organizations — not limiting protection to traditional media employees
- Apply to federal civil litigation as well as grand jury proceedings
- Establish fee-shifting against government actors who seek and fail to overcome the privilege, creating a financial deterrent against pretextual subpoenas

The absence of the PRESS Act is the direct reason the Bondi memo had such immediate practical effect: executive self-restraint was the only protection, and it was removed by the same executive actor that created it. Statutory protection requires legislative action to remove.

### 5.2 Restoring DOJ Journalist Guidelines

In the near term, before congressional action on the PRESS Act, the DOJ can restore and strengthen journalist protection guidelines through executive action. The Biden administration's Garland guidelines are the model; a successor administration should:

- Reinstate the prohibition on compelled journalist testimony or record seizure except in cases of imminent physical harm or direct criminal involvement by the journalist
- Require Deputy Attorney General, not merely AG-level approval for any action targeting journalist records, with written documentation of the specific harm and alternatives exhausted
- Establish a standing review committee including press freedom organization representatives to assess proposed journalist subpoenas
- Explicitly extend protections to digital communications platforms from which journalist records may be sought through third-party subpoenas — the mechanism used to obtain records without directly subpoenaing the journalist
- Prohibit retaliation investigations targeting specific outlets' journalists for coverage that the administration characterizes as hostile without specific evidence of criminal conduct independent of the journalism itself

The limitation is fundamental: executive guidelines are reversible by executive action, as demonstrated. The guidelines are a short-term functional protection and a political symbol, not a durable structural protection. They must be paired with legislative action.

### 5.3 FCC Reform: Anti-Consolidation and License Protection Standards

FCC reform to reverse or halt the current deregulatory trajectory requires attention to three interconnected mechanisms:

**Ownership cap preservation.** Congress should codify the existing 39% national broadcast reach cap and the local top-four protection in statute rather than FCC rule, making elimination require legislative action rather than a regulatory order. The Sinclair CEO's explicit expectation of cap elimination in 2026 — based on reading of FCC Chair Carr's signals — illustrates why regulatory-only protection is insufficient when the agency's leadership is aligned with consolidating broadcasters.

**Editorial independence prohibition.** Congress should explicitly prohibit FCC consideration of editorial content in license renewal, merger review, or any enforcement action, codifying in statute the First Amendment principle that broadcast licensing may not be conditioned on content. The Carr investigations of CBS, NBC, NPR, PBS, ABC, and The View for editorial content represent a direct test of whether this principle requires statutory reinforcement. The investigation of The View for conducting a senatorial candidate interview — treated as potentially actionable "misleading" content — illustrates the breadth of the theory being tested.

**Public interest obligations.** The original public interest standard in broadcast licensing included local news coverage obligations. FCC deregulation has stripped most specific content obligations; restoration of local news obligations in exchange for use of broadcast spectrum would directly address the local news collapse. The Community Benefit Agreement model — requiring specific local news commitments as conditions of license renewal and merger approval — provides a mechanism without the content-specific problems of direct editorial mandates.

### 5.4 Public Broadcasting Structural Independence

The NPR/PBS funding crisis demonstrates that the existing CPB structure — dependent on annual or biennial congressional appropriations — is insufficient to insulate public broadcasting from political leverage. Recovery requires structural changes:

- **Endowment model.** A permanent federal endowment for public broadcasting, established by statute and managed by an independent board with fixed terms and strict insulation from executive direction, would remove the annual appropriations leverage point. The BBC model (license fee) is not directly transferable to the U.S. context, but the Corporation for Public Broadcasting itself recommended in 2020 that a permanent endowment of approximately $7 billion would be sufficient to fund public broadcasting operations indefinitely without federal appropriations.

- **Appointment independence.** CPB board members are currently appointed by the president and confirmed by the Senate, creating direct political patronage. A judicial-nomination-style process — with board seats allocated across political parties with staggered terms — would reduce but not eliminate executive influence over board composition.

- **State-level public broadcasting protection.** Thirty-eight states have public broadcasting systems that are partially dependent on federal pass-through funds. State legislative action to provide direct state funding independent of federal CPB appropriations would create partial insulation against federal leverage. Several blue states have begun exploring this model in response to the 2025 CPB closure.

### 5.5 Anti-SLAPP Federal Legislation and SLAPP Reform

The Free Speech Protection Act — the federal anti-SLAPP bill introduced by Raskin, Kiley, and Wyden — should be enacted in the 120th Congress. Key provisions must include:

- **Early dismissal motion.** Any defendant in federal court litigation involving speech on matters of public concern should be able to file a special motion to dismiss within 60 days of the complaint. The motion should be decided on an expedited basis, with discovery stayed pending resolution.
- **Burden shifting.** The plaintiff must demonstrate a substantial probability of prevailing on the merits at the early dismissal stage, rather than requiring defendants to disprove claims.
- **Fee shifting.** Courts must award attorney's fees and costs to defendants who prevail on anti-SLAPP motions, creating a financial deterrent against strategic litigation.
- **Cross-claim preclusion.** Federal courts must not allow state court anti-SLAPP protections to be circumvented through diversity jurisdiction filing in federal court.

At the state level, the expansion of anti-SLAPP protections is accelerating (Michigan in December 2025, Ohio in January 2025). The remaining gap states — primarily Southern states with weaker civil litigation protections — should be targeted for Uniform Public Expression Protection Act adoption.

### 5.6 Nonprofit Journalism Infrastructure and Fiscal Support

The nonprofit journalism sector's growth demonstrates viability but not sufficiency. Recovery policy should address three gaps:

**Geographic reach.** Current nonprofit journalism is concentrated in metropolitan areas. The American Journalism Project's focus on local news organizations, funded in part by the Knight Foundation's $25 million 2025 commitment, should be expanded with additional philanthropic capital and potentially with state-level support through journalism school partnerships, community foundation collaborations, and direct state news lab funding.

**Investigative capacity.** The national investigative journalism infrastructure — ProPublica, The 19th, the Center for Public Integrity, state-specific investigative organizations — has demonstrated sustainability. New investigative centers focused on the specific accountability beats most threatened by the current environment (immigration, civil service, federal regulatory capture) should be funded through established nonprofit models.

**Fiscal treatment.** The Internal Revenue Code's treatment of for-profit newspapers as ineligible for 501(c)(3) status creates a structural disadvantage relative to nonprofit models. The Local Journalism Sustainability Act, which would provide direct tax credits for local news employment and news subscriptions, has been introduced in multiple Congresses without enactment. A comprehensive approach would include: (1) enhanced charitable deduction for news organization contributions; (2) small-dollar taxpayer checkoff for local journalism support; (3) direct federal funding through a competitive grant program administered at arm's length from the executive branch.

### 5.7 International Coordination: UNESCO, CPJ, and IFEX Framework

Recovery in the U.S. context cannot be isolated from the global framework. The mechanisms:

**U.S. engagement with UNESCO journalist protection standards.** The United States should recommit to the UNESCO Plan of Action on the Safety of Journalists and the Issue of Impunity, reversing the pattern of U.S. absence from UNESCO standard-setting processes. This includes accepting UN Special Rapporteur visits to assess U.S. press freedom conditions and publishing the responses required by Universal Periodic Review processes.

**USAGM restoration.** The courts have partially restored USAGM operations; legislative protection for the agency's operational independence — including insulating its funding from annual appropriations leverage — would provide structural security for the 361 million people worldwide who depend on it for independent news access.

**Journalist Assistance Network expansion.** The JAN launched in May 2025 (CPJ, FPF, IWMF, PEN America, RCFP) provides the functional infrastructure for coordinated protection response. Additional funding for the network — particularly the CPJ's emergency financial assistance component, which provides short-term support to journalists following incidents — would increase its capacity to meet demand that increased "significantly" post-November 2024 by every founding member's account.

**Legal defense coordination.** The RCFP's Local Legal Initiative expansion is the highest-leverage legal defense investment available: by providing free legal representation in states where SLAPP suits are most prevalent and anti-SLAPP protections weakest, the initiative directly neutralizes the strategic litigation mechanism before cases reach federal court. The 2025 initiative expansion to five states and announced further expansion to four additional states represents a model that should be funded to full national coverage.

## Analytical Framework: The Layered Suppression Model

The current threat environment cannot be adequately analyzed through a single-mechanism lens. What is occurring is a layered suppression model in which each mechanism amplifies the others:

**Layer 1 (Legal threat):** Rescission of DOJ journalist protections plus absence of federal shield law creates legal exposure for any reporter working on national security or executive-branch accountability stories. Sources cannot communicate safely; journalists cannot report without personal legal risk.

**Layer 2 (Economic pressure):** FCC investigations of specific outlets, merger approval leverage, and advertising market consolidation create economic incentives for editorial compliance. Outlets do not need to be formally censored if their owners calculate that regulatory hostility costs more than editorial independence is worth.

**Layer 3 (Information access denial):** AP exclusion from White House access, removal of press corps access to specific government spaces, and Pentagon press restriction guidelines eliminate the information supply chain for government accountability journalism independently of any legal threat.

**Layer 4 (Infrastructure elimination):** CPB defunding, USAGM dismantlement, and local news desert expansion eliminate the physical infrastructure through which independent information reaches publics — particularly rural, elderly, and non-digital audiences.

**Layer 5 (Harassment amplification):** Coordinated online harassment campaigns — which the administration has amplified by characterizing specific journalists and outlets as enemies — impose personal costs that the legal system does not deter.

No single recovery mechanism addresses all five layers. The PRESS Act addresses Layer 1 but not Layers 2-5. FCC reform addresses Layer 2 but not Layer 1. Public broadcasting protection addresses Layer 4. Recovery requires a coherent response across all five layers simultaneously, with the PRESS Act as the most immediately achievable single legislative action because it has already demonstrated bipartisan support in the House and could pass rapidly in a changed Senate environment.

The fundamental democratic stakes are clear: the press functions not merely as a private industry but as an infrastructure for democratic self-governance. An informed citizenry requires information. Information requires institutions capable of producing it independently of the power they report on. When those institutions are dismantled, captured, harassed into self-censorship, or financially starved, the democratic function does not migrate to alternative institutions — it is simply not performed. The voting rights documented in Domain 1 become meaningless if voters cannot obtain accurate information. The election security mechanisms in Domain 37 cannot prevent interference that operates through information manipulation when the press capable of exposing that manipulation has been suppressed. The legal challenges documented throughout the litigation tracker cannot proceed without the journalism that documents the conduct being challenged.

Media freedom is not one democratic priority among many. It is the information infrastructure on which all other democratic priorities depend.
