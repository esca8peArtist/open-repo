## Executive Summary

The attack on American civil society in 2025–2026 is not a collection of isolated incidents. It is a coordinated multi-vector campaign using the IRS, FBI, DOJ, and state-level mechanisms to designate, investigate, defund, and ultimately suppress the nonprofit organizations that form the connective tissue of democratic participation. Three operational mechanisms are now running simultaneously.

First, National Security Presidential Memorandum 7 (NSPM-7), signed September 25, 2025, directed federal agencies including the IRS and Treasury Department to investigate nonprofits for "domestic terrorism" links using definitions so vague they encompass "anti-capitalism," "opposition to law and immigration enforcement," and "radical gender ideology" — categories of protected First Amendment speech. By March 2026, NSPM-7 had been operationalized into a joint FBI-IRS "mission control command center," housed at the FBI, with IRS Criminal Investigation agents on one-year rotating assignments, 130 new special agents, and a $166 million budget request. The December 4, 2025 AG Bondi directive defined domestic terrorism to include organizations with "extreme viewpoints on immigration" and "anti-American sentiment," functionally authorizing investigation of any advocacy organization that criticizes administration policy.

Second, Florida HB 1471, signed April 6, 2026 and effective July 1, 2026, creates a state-level domestic terrorist designation system parallel to the federal infrastructure. The Florida Department of Law Enforcement's Chief of Domestic Security may recommend designation; the Governor and Cabinet approve; designated organizations face public funding bans, corporate dissolution authority, and second-degree felony criminal penalties for providing "material support." A companion bill, HB 1473, exempts designation documentation from public records — making evidentiary challenges effectively impossible. This is not a hypothetical: Florida used executive order to designate CAIR as a "terrorist organization" in December 2025, triggering a First Amendment lawsuit that obtained a preliminary injunction in March 2026. HB 1471 codifies the executive order mechanism into statute and expands its reach.

Third, the April 21, 2026 federal indictment of the Southern Poverty Law Center — 11 counts of wire fraud, bank fraud, and conspiracy to commit money laundering for the organization's confidential informant program — is the clearest articulation of the DOJ-to-IRS pipeline in action. Whistleblower reports document that a senior DOJ official ordered prosecutors to rush the indictment despite concerns about the case's strength. Former federal prosecutors characterize the legal theory as insufficient to establish fraud. The case is best understood not as a criminal prosecution with a realistic expectation of conviction, but as a template for using the indictment process itself as a suppression tool: reputational damage, donor flight, legal cost accumulation, and organizational paralysis occurring before any adjudication.

The structural context that enables all three mechanisms is the 2025 federal grant freeze, which Urban Institute documented disrupted the funding of 33 percent of American nonprofits in the first half of 2025 — with 21 percent losing grants or contracts outright, 29 percent reducing staff, and 21 percent already serving fewer people six months into the freeze. The grant freeze functions as precondition: financially destabilized organizations are less equipped to litigate designation challenges, less able to absorb a chilling effect on donors, and more likely to engage in self-censorship rather than continued advocacy.

The constitutional defense framework is robust but requires advance filing. The relevant doctrines — unconstitutional conditions, prior restraint, vagueness, First Amendment retaliation, and the *NRA v. Vullo* (2024) coercion standard — are all available. The CAIR preliminary injunction demonstrates courts are willing to block designation mechanisms on First Amendment grounds. The advocacy window before July 1, 2026 (Florida HB 1471 effective date) is 57 days. The litigation window for NSPM-7 challenges is open now. The sector-wide coalition necessary to mount effective pre-enforcement challenges is identifiable and ready for coordination.

CIVICUS Monitor downgraded the United States from "narrowed" to "obstructed" in December 2025 — placing it in the same tier as Hungary, Brazil, and South Africa. That comparison is not rhetorical. Hungary's playbook — labeling civil society organizations as foreign agents, using vague definitions to create designator discretion, combining financial pressure with reputational stigmatization — has direct operational parallels in the mechanisms documented in this domain. The EU experience also provides the counter-model: the Court of Justice of the EU twice ruled Hungary's NGO suppression laws violated fundamental rights, establishing that targeted associations must receive procedural protections before designations take effect.

The 1.5 million nonprofits in the United States are not merely service providers. They are, in the language of *NAACP v. Alabama* (1958), vehicles for the exercise of the constitutional right to association "for the advancement of beliefs and ideas." Suppressing that infrastructure is a democratic infrastructure attack in the same analytical category as election administration seizure or judicial independence erosion. The threat requires a commensurate response: coordinated pre-enforcement litigation, sector-wide coalition building, funding model transformation, and legislative resistance to state copycat bills.

## I. Threat Landscape: The Three Active Mechanisms

### 1.1 NSPM-7 and the Federal Designation Pipeline

On September 25, 2025, President Trump signed National Security Presidential Memorandum 7, titled "Countering Domestic Terrorism and Organized Political Violence." The memo's operative definitions are the document's most consequential feature. NSPM-7 characterizes as "domestic terrorism" activity sharing "common threads" including "anti-Americanism, anti-capitalism, and anti-Christianity; support for the overthrow of the United States Government; extremism on migration, race, and gender; and hostility towards those who hold traditional American views on family, religion, and morality."

These are not criminal law definitions. They are ideological categories that encompass a substantial fraction of normal civic advocacy: organizations that favor immigration reform, oppose Christian nationalism, challenge traditional gender roles, advocate for economic justice, or criticize the administration's border enforcement policy all fall within the definitional scope of NSPM-7.

The operational mechanism is a direct IRS authorization. NSPM-7 directed the Treasury Department and IRS to take actions to ensure that no tax-exempt entities are "directly or indirectly financing political violence or domestic terrorism" and to refer such organizations, and their employees and officers, to the Department of Justice for investigation and prosecution.

On December 4, 2025, Attorney General Pam Bondi issued a DOJ implementation memo that operationalized NSPM-7 by defining targeted groups as those with "anti-fascist" views and those with "extreme viewpoints on immigration, radical gender ideology, and anti-American sentiment." The memo directed law enforcement to investigate groups with "anti-fascist" views, characterizing such groups as animated by an ideology that portrays "support for law enforcement and border control as 'fascist'" — a definition that, if taken seriously, would encompass a wide range of civil liberties organizations.

By March 2026, the implementation had moved from directive to operational infrastructure. Reporting by the Charity & Security Network and CBS News documented the creation of a joint FBI-IRS "mission control command center" housed at the FBI, with IRS Criminal Investigation agents working on one-year rotating assignments. The FBI's FY 2027 budget request, first reported by journalist Ken Klippenstein, described the creation of the "NSPM-7 Joint Mission Center," including representatives from ten agencies, tasked to counter NSPM-7 targets by "integrating intelligence, operational support, and financial analysis." The FBI requested $166 million and 328 positions to implement the initiative, including 130 new special agents.

This is the complete DOJ-IRS pipeline as of May 2026: NSPM-7 defines targets using ideological categories; the AG Bondi memo directs law enforcement; the FBI-IRS Joint Mission Center investigates; and the IRS has authorization to refer organizations for prosecution. The question is not whether the pipeline will be used — it is already operational — but which organizations will be targeted first, and whether pre-enforcement litigation can establish constitutional constraints before the first referral is made.

### 1.2 Florida HB 1471: The State-Level Designation Mechanism

Florida HB 1471, signed by Governor DeSantis on April 6, 2026, and effective July 1, 2026, creates a parallel state-level domestic terrorist designation system. The bill passed the House 80–25 and the Senate 25–11.

The designation mechanism works as follows: the Chief of Domestic Security within the Florida Department of Law Enforcement (FDLE) may recommend that an organization operating in Florida be designated a "domestic terrorist organization" upon a determination that the entity is "engaged in terrorist activity." The Governor and Cabinet must approve the determination. Once designated, the consequences cascade:

- The state and all subdivisions are prohibited from expending any funds to support the designated organization or accepting any funds from it — a public funding ban that extends to any entity receiving state contracts or regulation.
- The Florida Secretary of State acquires authority to dissolve corporations bearing the designation.
- Individuals who "willfully become a member" of a designated organization with intent to further its illegal acts commit a second-degree felony.
- Private schools receiving state funding may not allow students to "promote" designated organizations — a provision creating First Amendment concerns around student expression.

Companion bill HB 1473 exempts from public records "any documentation related to a domestic terrorist organization determination" if disclosure "would reveal information critical to state or national security." This public records exemption is the provision most threatening to judicial review: organizations challenging their designation cannot obtain the evidentiary basis for the government's determination through public records requests, limiting their ability to contest the factual predicate.

The bill does not specify what procedural protections — notice, hearing, opportunity to respond — precede designation. The ACLU of Florida characterized the bill as creating "guilt by designation" with "vague and overbroad language opening the door to arbitrary enforcement and political targeting."

A critical feature is the bill's timing relationship to existing executive action. Governor DeSantis issued an executive order in December 2025 designating CAIR as a "terrorist organization" and directing all state agencies to ban the organization from contracts, employment, funds, and benefits. CAIR-Florida filed suit in federal court in December 2025; U.S. District Judge Mark Walker issued a temporary injunction blocking the order in March 2026, finding substantial First Amendment and preemption claims. HB 1471 is designed to move the designation authority from executive order to statutory footing — potentially mooting the preemption argument by creating an explicit state statutory framework rather than relying on executive discretion. The July 1 effective date is the trigger for a new First Amendment challenge under the statutory framework.

### 1.3 The SPLC Indictment: Template for Organizational Suppression

On April 21, 2026, a federal grand jury in Montgomery, Alabama returned an 11-count indictment against the Southern Poverty Law Center. The charges consist of six counts of wire fraud, four counts of false statements to a federally insured bank, and one count of conspiracy to commit concealment money laundering. The DOJ press release, amplified by FBI Director Kash Patel in a video statement, alleged that between 2014 and 2023 the SPLC "secretly funneled more than $3 million in donated funds to individuals who were associated with various violent extremist groups including the Ku Klux Klan, Aryan Nations, and National Socialist Party of America" through fictitious entities including "Fox Photography" and "Rare Books Warehouse."

The SPLC's response is critical to understanding the legal reality: the organization described the payment recipients as "confidential sources who risked their lives to infiltrate and inform on the activities of our nation's most radical and violent extremist groups" and stated that information from the program was regularly shared with the FBI and local law enforcement. The New York Times and The Intercept confirmed that multiple SPLC donors, contacted after the indictment, reported no sense of being deceived or defrauded — the opposite of what wire fraud requires.

Legal analysts' assessment of the case is nearly uniform in identifying it as legally deficient. Just Security analysis by former DOJ prosecutor Andrew Weissmann identified three structural problems: (1) the indictment contains no allegation that the SPLC told donors it would not use funds for an informant program, which is required to establish a false representation to donors; (2) the entire fraud theory rests on $13,905 charged in a single day in 2023 despite describing a scheme spanning nine years; and (3) the bank fraud counts use "false and misleading" language when the relevant statute (18 U.S.C. § 1014) only criminalizes "false" statements, not "misleading" ones — a precision error that goes to the legal sufficiency of the charges. Former federal prosecutors quoted by CBS News stated they would not expect any prosecutor with white-collar experience to view the indictment as establishing the elements of a crime.

What the indictment does establish, regardless of its ultimate legal merit, is the DOJ-to-IRS pipeline's organizational impact template. CNN reported on May 1, 2026 that lawmakers accused DOJ of "rushing" the indictment with a senior Trump administration official ordering federal prosecutors to proceed despite internal concerns about the case's strength — consistent with a charging decision made for reasons other than prosecutorial merit. The National Urban League characterized the indictment as "a deeply troubling escalation" designed for "intimidation" and "silencing organizations that have spent decades confronting hate."

The pipeline mechanism is: DOJ indictment creates immediate reputational damage; donors reduce contributions out of reputational risk aversion; organizational leadership diverts time and resources to legal defense; staff morale is impacted; adversaries use the designation to argue the organization lacks legitimacy; and all of this occurs before any adjudication, potentially producing organizational incapacitation without conviction.

## II. The 2025 Federal Grant Freeze: Structural Destabilization

The three active suppression mechanisms described above operate against a backdrop of prior financial destabilization created by the 2025 federal grant freeze. Understanding the grant freeze is essential to understanding why designation and investigation threats are more dangerous now than they would be in a normal operating environment.

On January 27, 2025, the Office of Management and Budget ordered a pause on the disbursement of federal grants and loans. The Urban Institute's October 2025 nationally representative survey of 2,737 nonprofits documented the sectoral impact:

- **33 percent** of U.S. nonprofit service providers experienced some type of government funding disruption in the first half of 2025.
- **21 percent** lost at least some government grant or contract funding outright.
- **27 percent** faced delays, pauses, or freezes in government funding.
- **6 percent** received stop-work orders.
- Of those hit by funding cuts, **29 percent had reduced their staff** and **21 percent were already serving fewer people** by the time the survey was conducted in April–June 2025.
- Government funding constituted **42 percent of total revenue** for service-providing nonprofits that experienced disruptions — meaning the freeze was not a marginal revenue event but a structural crisis for affected organizations.

The sectoral targeting was not uniform. Executive orders issued on January 20, 2025 specifically blocked, rescinded, and terminated federal funding "relating to undocumented immigrants; diversity, equity and inclusion; green energy projects; foreign aid; and support for gender affirming care and abortion." The National Family Planning and Reproductive Health Association and the ACLU sued HHS over the withholding of $65.8 million in Title X federal family planning grants — the administration withheld 22 of the total 25 percent of Title X network grants, leaving approximately 842,000 people without access to Title X services. Civil rights and immigration services groups sought emergency relief to halt an unlawful DHS grant freeze. Organizations serving domestic violence survivors began self-editing public-facing media out of fear of losing federal grants.

The self-censorship effect documented by the 19th News and the Nonprofit Quarterly is the mechanism that links the grant freeze to the designation and investigation threats: organizations that depend on federal funding and fear grant termination are already inclined toward compliance over advocacy. Adding a terrorist designation threat on top of financial vulnerability produces a compounding chilling effect that does not require actual designation to suppress advocacy.

The Nonprofit Finance Fund's 2025 State of the Sector survey found that 84 percent of respondents with government funding expect cuts to that funding, and 52 percent have three months or less cash on hand. An organization with a three-month cash runway cannot absorb a twelve-month litigation campaign to contest a wrongful designation. The financial fragility created by the grant freeze is a structural precondition for the effectiveness of the designation mechanisms.

## III. Constitutional Vulnerabilities and Doctrine

The designation and investigation mechanisms described above are vulnerable to constitutional challenge under at least four doctrines. This section identifies the doctrinal framework for pre-enforcement litigation.

### 3.1 First Amendment Retaliation: NRA v. Vullo (2024)

In May 2024, a unanimous Supreme Court held in *NRA v. Vullo* that "government officials cannot attempt to coerce private parties in order to punish or suppress views that the government disfavors." The case arose when a New York DFS superintendent pressured insurance companies to end relationships with the NRA following Sandy Hook. The Court held that government officials cross into impermissible coercion when they engage in conduct "that, viewed in context, could be reasonably understood to convey a threat of adverse government action in order to punish or suppress speech."

*Vullo* is directly applicable to the NSPM-7 implementation. The FBI-IRS Joint Mission Center investigation of nonprofits whose "extreme viewpoints on immigration" or "radical gender ideology" place them within NSPM-7's scope is precisely the kind of government coercion through intermediate mechanisms — here, IRS audits and criminal referrals rather than insurance regulation — that *Vullo* prohibits. The Protect Democracy project is already demanding transparency from the administration on its nonprofit "enemies list," and a *Vullo* theory is the natural foundation for a First Amendment retaliation claim.

### 3.2 Unconstitutional Conditions Doctrine

The unconstitutional conditions doctrine prohibits the government from conditioning a benefit on the waiver of a constitutional right. The Nonprofit Association of Washington, the Leadership Conference on Civil and Human Rights, and the NAACP Legal Defense Fund all identified the unconstitutional conditions problem in their analysis of HR 9495 and its successor provisions: conditioning tax-exempt status on the organization's speech and associational choices is suspect under the doctrine developed in *Rust v. Sullivan* (1991) and subsequent cases.

The doctrine's application is clearest when the government revokes a benefit — tax exemption — that the organization previously held, based on the organization's expressive activity, without narrowly tailored justification. IRS revocation of a civil rights organization's 501(c)(3) status in retaliation for advocacy positions it has held for decades would implicate both the unconstitutional conditions doctrine and *NAACP v. Alabama's* protection of associational privacy.

### 3.3 Vagueness and Overbreadth

NSPM-7's definitional framework is constitutionally vulnerable under the void-for-vagueness doctrine, which requires that regulations affecting First Amendment activity provide fair notice of what is prohibited and contain sufficient standards to prevent arbitrary enforcement. Definitions reaching "anti-capitalism," "opposition to law and immigration enforcement," and "hostility towards traditional American views on family, religion, and morality" fail both requirements.

The vagueness problem is compounded by Florida HB 1471's companion public records exemption (HB 1473). An organization that cannot obtain the evidentiary basis for its designation cannot meaningfully contest that designation — a procedural deficiency that independently raises due process concerns.

### 3.4 Prior Restraint

The pre-designation investigation itself — rather than just the ultimate revocation — can constitute a prior restraint on speech. Announcing a criminal investigation of a civil society organization before charges are filed, or initiating an IRS audit as part of a politically motivated targeting scheme, chills ongoing speech and advocacy. The Supreme Court has established that prior restraints on speech carry a "heavy presumption against constitutional validity" (*New York Times v. United States*, 1971). An investigation announced through public channels — as the SPLC indictment was announced with an FBI Director video statement — is distinguishable from a traditional confidential audit.

### 3.5 Due Process and the 501(p) Framework

Under existing law, the IRS cannot revoke a nonprofit's tax-exempt status through "the stroke of a pen." The Minnesota Council of Nonprofits' analysis confirmed that neither the President, the Treasury Department, nor the IRS can revoke tax-exempt status through executive order alone; individual case-by-case audits with opportunities for the organization to defend itself are required by statute.

Section 501(p) of the Internal Revenue Code provides a mechanism for suspension of tax-exempt status for organizations designated as terrorist organizations under the IEEPA or Immigration and Nationality Act — but this provision applies only to organizations specifically designated under those federal statutes by the Secretary of State or under IEEPA authority, not to organizations merely characterized as "supporting" terrorism by the Treasury Secretary. The legislative effort to change this through HR 6408 and HR 9495 — which would have given the Treasury Secretary unilateral designation authority without prior due process — twice passed the House and twice died in the Senate before Section 112209, a nearly identical provision, was stripped from the OBBBA before its July 4, 2025 enactment.

The failure to pass the statutory authority means the administration is attempting to achieve the functional equivalent of HR 9495 through executive mechanism — NSPM-7 and the FBI-IRS Joint Mission Center — without the statutory authority that Congress repeatedly declined to grant.

### 3.6 The Material Support Boundary: Holder v. Humanitarian Law Project (2010)

*Holder v. Humanitarian Law Project*, 561 U.S. 1 (2010), remains the key precedent governing the intersection of First Amendment rights and material support prohibitions. The Court upheld the material support statute (18 U.S.C. § 2339B) as applied to training members of a designated foreign terrorist organization in peaceful conflict resolution, but crucially held that the statute does not penalize "mere association" with a foreign terrorist organization or "independent advocacy" of a group's political goals. Coordination with a designated organization can be prohibited; independent advocacy cannot.

The SPLC indictment's theory — that a civil rights organization engaged in donor fraud by funding a confidential informant program that infiltrated violent extremist groups and shared intelligence with the FBI — does not fit the *Humanitarian Law Project* framework for prohibited material support. The payments were to individuals who were informants, not to designated terrorist organizations as beneficiaries. The theory is legally distinct from material support doctrine.

More importantly, *Humanitarian Law Project's* protection of independent advocacy directly limits NSPM-7's operational reach: an organization that independently advocates against immigration enforcement, or independently criticizes the administration's approach to "anti-Americanism," is engaged in activity that NSPM-7's definitional framework encompasses but that First Amendment doctrine protects.

## IV. State-Level Designation Mechanisms: Authoritarianism from Below

### 4.1 The Pre-HB-1471 Executive Order Phase

Before HB 1471 was enacted, Florida and Texas used executive orders to designate CAIR as a terrorist organization. DeSantis signed his executive order on December 8, 2025; Texas Governor Abbott signed a proclamation designating both CAIR and the Muslim Brotherhood as "foreign terrorist organizations" and "transnational criminal organizations" under S.B. 1900 and S.B. 17.

The executive orders triggered immediate constitutional litigation. CAIR-Florida filed suit in the Northern District of Florida in December 2025, arguing federal preemption (the power to designate foreign terrorist organizations is exclusively assigned to the U.S. Secretary of State under 8 U.S.C. § 1189, and state designations conflict with that exclusive federal authority under *Crosby v. National Foreign Trade Council*), First Amendment retaliation, and due process violations. U.S. District Judge Mark Walker granted a temporary injunction in March 2026, finding CAIR had demonstrated substantial likelihood of success on the merits on both the preemption and First Amendment claims. Texas CAIR chapters filed parallel lawsuits against Governor Abbott and AG Paxton.

Stanford Law's Legal Aggregate analysis, published February 2026, characterized the Texas and Florida designations as "authoritarianism from below" and warned: "The domestic targeting of dissent starts with groups that those in power deem to be attractive political targets, but it never ends there." The analysis identified CAIR as the selected initial target precisely because of its political vulnerability, and predicted — accurately — that HB 1471 would follow to codify the executive order mechanism on statutory footing.

### 4.2 HB 1471 as Statutory Consolidation

HB 1471 converts Florida's executive order designation mechanism into a statutory one. This shift has constitutional significance: the executive order was vulnerable to preemption because it purported to designate an organization as a "foreign terrorist organization," a power exclusively assigned to the federal government. HB 1471 creates a distinct state category — "domestic terrorist organization" — that sidesteps the federal preemption argument by not claiming to operate within the federal FTO designation framework.

The July 1 implementation creates an urgent window for pre-enforcement constitutional challenge. The combination of First Amendment free association claims (the bill imposes penalties on "membership" in and "material support" for designated organizations, chilling advocacy), vagueness concerns (the designation criteria are broad and the evidentiary basis is shielded from public records disclosure by HB 1473), and due process deficiencies (no specified notice or hearing before designation) collectively support a pre-enforcement injunction application filed before the July 1 effective date.

ACLU of Florida has already urged DeSantis to veto HB 1471, calling it a "guilt by designation" bill that "threatens constitutional safeguards." A challenge in the Northern District of Florida, building on the preliminary injunction already issued against the executive order, is the most direct pathway.

### 4.3 Other States

Florida is not isolated. ICNL's analysis documented that since April 2025, Florida, Nebraska, and Arkansas have passed laws targeting nonprofits through "foreign influence registration schemes and restrictions on charitable solicitation." New bills in other states seek to expand state designation powers. Existing state terrorism laws in multiple states contain "far broader, vaguer, and more draconian provisions than commonly understood," per the Stanford Law analysis — creating latent designation authority that could be activated without new legislation.

The pattern matches the "model legislation" dynamic observed in other areas of state legislative autocratization: one state tests a mechanism; a court challenge determines its constitutional limits; other states adopt modified versions designed to survive the challenge. HB 1471's companion public records exemption is exactly this kind of challenge-resistant modification.

## V. The SPLC Case Study: DOJ-IRS Pipeline Mechanics

### 5.1 Indictment Mechanics

The April 21, 2026 SPLC indictment demonstrates how DOJ investigation becomes organizational suppression without requiring conviction or even a legally sufficient charge. The operational sequence is:

1. DOJ initiates grand jury investigation of a prominent civil rights organization, using a theory that characterizes standard investigative journalism and counterterrorism informant tradecraft as wire fraud.
2. An 11-count indictment is returned; FBI Director Patel announces it publicly via video statement — an unusual public profile for what would otherwise be a white-collar fraud case.
3. Adverse media coverage is immediate and follows DOJ framing; subsequent reporting documents that SPLC's informants provided intelligence that the FBI itself acknowledged was valuable.
4. Whistleblower reports emerge documenting that the indictment was rushed over internal DOJ objections about case strength — published by CNN on May 1, 2026.
5. Civil rights organizations respond: the National Urban League condemns the indictment as "intimidation"; the Lawyers' Committee for Civil Rights issues a statement; but the SPLC is already managing reputational damage, donor inquiries, and legal defense costs.
6. The IRS referral mechanism embedded in NSPM-7 means the indictment's existence — regardless of outcome — can trigger an IRS review of the SPLC's tax-exempt status.

This sequence does not require conviction to be effective as a suppression mechanism. The combination of reputational damage, donor risk aversion, legal cost, and organizational distraction produces organizational incapacitation as an interim effect that persists through any eventual acquittal.

### 5.2 Why the SPLC Was Selected

The SPLC is not a randomly selected target. It is the most prominent organization in the country that tracks and publicly names white nationalist, neo-Nazi, and domestic hate groups. Its Intelligence Reports and Hate Map are used by law enforcement, researchers, and journalists. Its designation of groups as "hate organizations" has been a persistent grievance of the American far right for decades.

The selection of SPLC as a test case follows the same political logic as the selection of CAIR in Florida: choose an organization whose designation will be applauded by the political coalition that benefits from the suppression mechanism, and whose defense will require expenditure of political capital by its allies. The indictment creates a precedent template that can be applied to a wider range of organizations.

### 5.3 The DOJ-to-IRS Referral Pathway

NSPM-7 explicitly directs IRS to refer "tax-exempt entities" suspected of "financing political violence or domestic terrorism" to DOJ, and directs DOJ to investigate and prosecute. The SPLC indictment runs the pipeline in the reverse direction — DOJ prosecution triggers IRS review — but the underlying mechanism is bidirectional: a DOJ indictment justifies an IRS examination of tax-exempt status; an IRS finding of financial irregularity supports a DOJ criminal referral.

The DOJ prosecution establishes a predicate for IRS inquiry, and an IRS revocation proceeding adds financial pressure during the legal defense period. Neither mechanism requires the other to proceed to completion for the combined effect — legal cost, donor flight, reputational damage, organizational distraction — to disable the targeted organization.

## VI. International Precedent and the Hungary Comparison

### 6.1 Hungary's Playbook

The comparison between Hungary's civil society suppression and the current U.S. situation is not rhetorical. CIVICUS Monitor's December 2025 rating of the United States as "Obstructed" — placing it in the same tier as Hungary, Brazil, and South Africa — reflects a substantive assessment that the mechanisms match.

Hungary's playbook unfolded in three phases across eight years. Phase 1 (2017): the Law on the Transparency of Organisations Supported from Abroad required civil society organizations receiving more than approximately 24,000 euros annually from foreign sources to register as "organisations supported from abroad" and label themselves as such in all publications. The EU Court of Justice ruled in June 2020 that the law violated both freedom of movement of capital and fundamental rights including freedom of association, finding that Hungary had "not submitted any argument proving the existence of such a threat" to public security that would justify the restriction. Phase 2 (2023): the Protection of National Sovereignty Act established the Sovereignty Protection Office with sweeping investigative powers and criminalized the use of foreign funds for "political purposes" — a vague standard enabling persecution of any organization the government disfavored. Phase 3 (proposed 2024): the Act on Transparency and Public Life proposed giving the Sovereignty Protection Office discretionary authority to list any organization as "under foreign influence" — the closest structural parallel to NSPM-7's ideological targeting scope.

The structural parallel to the U.S. trajectory is: executive order designation (DeSantis December 2025, Abbott November 2025) → statutory codification (Florida HB 1471, April 2026) → federal investigative infrastructure (NSPM-7 Joint Mission Center, March 2026) — all within a six-month period. Hungary took eight years to traverse this territory. The U.S. is moving faster.

### 6.2 EU Institutional Protections That the U.S. Lacks

The EU's response to Hungary's NGO suppression provides the clearest international counter-model. The ECJ's June 2020 ruling established that civil society designation mechanisms must meet a standard requiring proof of "a genuine, present and sufficiently serious threat to a fundamental interest of society" — not a presumption of suspicion based on funding source or ideological orientation. The Court found that Hungary's law imposed restrictions based on "a presumption made on principle and indiscriminately."

The EU enforcement mechanism — infringement proceedings followed by ECJ rulings enforceable against member states — has no U.S. analog. The U.S. constitutional protection must come from courts enforcing the First Amendment and due process provisions of the Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments. The ECJ's substantive standard — that restrictions on civil society require specific evidence of genuine threat, not ideological inference — is the functional equivalent of the First Amendment vagueness doctrine applied to the NSPM-7 definitions.

The October 2024 EU referral of Hungary to the ECJ over the Sovereignty Protection Act provides current legal precedent for the proposition that designation mechanisms with vague standards, broad investigative authority, and inadequate procedural protections violate fundamental rights. That reasoning translates directly into U.S. constitutional doctrine: NSPM-7's definitions are not "sufficiently precise" under the vagueness standard; the absence of notice and hearing before designation violates due process; and the use of ideological categories to trigger investigation constitutes impermissible viewpoint discrimination.

### 6.3 Canada and Germany: Positive Models

Canada's Charities Act requires evidence of actual prohibited activities, not ideological characterization, before revocation proceedings commence. The Canada Revenue Agency must follow structured administrative procedures including notice, opportunity to respond, and independent appeal before any revocation. Germany's equivalent protection under the Vereinigungsgesetz (Association Law) requires a formal prohibition proceeding with judicial review before an organization can be banned, and prohibitions based on advocacy positions alone — as distinct from actual criminal activity — are subject to immediate constitutional challenge.

Both models share the feature absent from the current U.S. executive approach: a procedural due process requirement that precedes rather than follows designation. The congressional refusal to enact HR 9495's post-designation process (where organizations have 90 days to "prove innocence" after designation, with no prior hearing) indicates that the legislative branch recognizes the constitutional requirement for pre-designation process. The executive branch's attempt to achieve the same outcome through NSPM-7 without the congressional due process structure is a separation of powers problem as well as a First Amendment one.

## VII. Nonprofit Sector Organizational Responses

### 7.1 Legal Strategy Coalitions

The civil rights and nonprofit legal community has mounted a coordinated response across three lines of action.

First, direct litigation: CAIR-Florida v. DeSantis (Northern District of Florida, filed December 2025) obtained a preliminary injunction against the executive order designation mechanism in March 2026. This litigation is the direct precedent for a pre-enforcement challenge to HB 1471, building on the same First Amendment and preemption grounds. Texas CAIR chapters filed parallel litigation. Both cases are active.

Second, sector-wide coalition opposition: the Leadership Conference on Civil and Human Rights, NAACP Legal Defense Fund, Lawyers' Committee for Civil Rights Under Law, ACLU, and over 100 other organizations opposed HR 9495 and Section 112209 as vehicles for the designation authority the administration is now pursuing through NSPM-7. That opposition coalition is positioned for pre-enforcement NSPM-7 challenges.

Third, IRS process defense: the Minnesota Council of Nonprofits and Tenenbaum Law Group's public analysis of the limits on IRS revocation authority creates the legal grounding for organizations that receive IRS inquiries to contest the predicate for review. Under existing statutory law, the IRS cannot revoke tax-exempt status through executive fiat; each case requires individual administrative proceedings. Organizations that receive IRS inquiries predicated on NSPM-7-style characterizations should treat them as First Amendment retaliation cases from the initial inquiry, not only at the revocation stage.

### 7.2 Financial Resilience

Urban Institute and Nonprofit Finance Fund data from 2025 document the financial precondition for the suppression mechanisms to be effective. The adaptive organizational response has two components: funding diversification and structural financial cushioning.

On funding diversification: organizations that derived more than 15–20 percent of revenue from federal grants are most vulnerable. The 2026 playbook from KLR, Instrumentl, and sector advisers emphasizes developing private grant pipelines, building individual donor bases, and piloting fee-for-service or social enterprise models to replace federal dependency. The 82 percent of affected organizations pursuing private grants as their primary adaptive response indicates widespread recognition of federal funding as a structural vulnerability.

On financial cushioning: the Nonprofit Finance Fund's finding that 52 percent of organizations have three months or less of cash on hand creates acute vulnerability to designation threats that generate legal costs. Legal defense fund structures — pooled legal defense contributions managed at the sector level — are one mechanism for spreading the cost of designation challenges across the broader nonprofit community.

### 7.3 Self-Censorship as the Primary Threat

The Nonprofit Quarterly and 19th News documentation of widespread self-censorship is the under-appreciated dimension of the suppression campaign. Domestic violence organizations self-editing public communications; immigration advocacy organizations reducing public visibility; reproductive health organizations declining media interviews — these are not dramatic organizational collapses but quiet contractions in the advocacy capacity of organizations that have not been designated, investigated, or defunded.

The chilling effect operates prospectively: organizations that observe what happened to SPLC after its indictment, or to CAIR after its designation, adjust their behavior without requiring direct government action targeting them. This is the core mechanism of political suppression through civil society attack: the threat is more effective than execution because it produces compliance at scale without requiring the government to actually proceed against each individual organization.

The counter-mechanism is information: sector-wide briefing about the actual constitutional limitations on government designation authority, the availability of pre-enforcement challenges, and the demonstrated willingness of courts to issue preliminary injunctions in the CAIR case. Organizations that understand the legal landscape are less susceptible to chilling effects than organizations operating in uncertainty.

## VIII. Litigation Pathways and Constitutional Challenges

### 8.1 Pre-Enforcement Challenge to Florida HB 1471 (Urgency: July 1, 2026)

The 57-day advocacy window before Florida HB 1471's effective date is the most urgent litigation opportunity in this domain. A pre-enforcement challenge should be filed in the Northern District of Florida, building on the CAIR v. DeSantis preliminary injunction as precedent. The filing organization has two options: (a) a renewed motion in the CAIR litigation arguing that HB 1471 reinstates the unconstitutional mechanism the preliminary injunction blocked; or (b) a new action by a broader coalition of organizations that face designation risk — civil rights groups, immigration advocacy organizations, reproductive health organizations, and progressive advocacy groups that fall within HB 1471's definitional scope.

The constitutional grounds are: First Amendment (freedom of association and speech; overbreadth; vagueness); due process (no pre-designation notice or hearing specified in the statute; evidence shielded from public records by HB 1473); Supremacy Clause/preemption as applied to any attempt to designate organizations that coordinate with federally recognized entities; and Equal Protection as applied if designations systematically target organizations associated with protected class advocacy.

**Key organizations for this challenge**: ACLU of Florida (already opposing HB 1471 publicly), CAIR-Florida (active plaintiff in the existing litigation), Lawyers' Committee for Civil Rights Under Law, NAACP LDF, and Florida-based civil rights and immigrant advocacy organizations.

### 8.2 NSPM-7 Pre-Enforcement Challenge

The FBI-IRS Joint Mission Center's operational status as of March 2026 creates standing for organizations that can demonstrate the Center's investigation scope encompasses their advocacy activities. The *Vullo* (2024) coercion standard — government conduct that could "reasonably be understood to convey a threat of adverse government action to punish or suppress speech" — encompasses an FBI-IRS Joint Mission Center explicitly tasked to investigate organizations whose ideological positions fall within NSPM-7's definitions.

The challenge would need to establish: (1) plaintiffs engage in advocacy that falls within NSPM-7's definitional scope (organizations advocating for immigration reform, reproductive rights, labor rights, or opposition to law enforcement practices); (2) the Joint Mission Center's mandate encompasses their activities; (3) the chilling effect on advocacy is concrete and documented (self-censorship, reduced advocacy activity); and (4) the government's definitions fail constitutional standards of precision required when regulating First Amendment activity.

**Key organizations**: ACLU (national), EFF (especially for digital advocacy organizations subject to data collection under NSPM-7), Brennan Center for Justice, and sector-wide coalitions of affected organizations. The Protect Democracy project's transparency demand regarding the "nonprofit enemies list" is a natural companion to a First Amendment challenge.

### 8.3 SPLC Defense and the Informant Program Precedent

The SPLC's "vigorous defense" against the April 21 indictment will be a bellwether for the DOJ-IRS pipeline's operational scope. The legal theory most favorable to SPLC is a motion to dismiss for failure to state an offense: the indictment's failure to allege a false representation to donors about the use of funds is a legal sufficiency deficiency that can be raised before trial. The whistleblower evidence of prosecutorial rushing over internal objections supports a selective prosecution defense.

The civil rights community's interest in the SPLC defense exceeds the SPLC's individual organizational interests. A successful motion to dismiss — or a verdict of acquittal — establishes that informant programs operating in coordination with law enforcement are not wire fraud, closing a legal theory that could otherwise be applied to any civil rights organization that has ever paid for investigative research or maintained confidential sources.

**Organizations with direct interests in the SPLC defense outcome**: Every civil rights organization that uses confidential sources, investigative researchers, or undercover methodologies in its work. The Lawyers' Committee's public statement of support is the beginning of a coordinated legal defense fund campaign.

### 8.4 Congressional Oversight and the Section 6103 Felony

Internal Revenue Code Section 6103 makes it a felony for government officials to use the IRS for politically motivated investigations. The House Democrats' inquiry into weaponization of the IRS, announced in April 2025, is the oversight mechanism. As the FBI-IRS Joint Mission Center becomes operational, establishing the factual record of its targeting criteria through congressional oversight creates both accountability documentation and potential Section 6103 criminal referral grounds.

The critical documentation goal is demonstrating that the targeting criteria are ideological (NSPM-7's definitional categories) rather than evidence-based (specific criminal activity). If the Joint Mission Center's target list is based on NSPM-7's ideological categories rather than particularized evidence of financing terrorism, the investigation itself violates Section 6103.

## IX. Implementation Timeline and Advocacy Windows

### Window 1: Now — June 30, 2026 (Florida HB 1471 Pre-Enforcement Period)

The 57 days before HB 1471's effective date are the primary advocacy window. Actions required:

- **Pre-enforcement challenge filing**: Target late May to mid-June 2026 to ensure injunction briefing is complete before July 1. The ACLU of Florida, CAIR-Florida counsel, and the Lawyers' Committee are the filing organizations.
- **Coalition coordination**: Organizations that face designation risk need to decide whether to join the challenge as named plaintiffs or amicus supporters. Broader plaintiff coalitions generate stronger standing arguments and demonstrate the law's sector-wide chilling effect.
- **Public records strategy**: Before HB 1473's companion exemption takes full effect, organizations should submit public records requests for any documentation of the designation recommendation process that has already begun under existing FDLE authority.
- **Legislative resistance**: State legislators in other states should be briefed on Florida HB 1471 as a model legislation threat and provided with the constitutional analysis for use in opposing copycat bills.

### Window 2: March 2026 Onward (NSPM-7 Joint Mission Center — Active Now)

The Joint Mission Center is operational. The advocacy window for pre-enforcement challenge is open but closing as the Center develops its target list.

- **First-mover litigation advantage**: An organization that files a First Amendment challenge before the Center makes its first referral is in a stronger position than one that challenges after receiving an IRS notice. Pre-enforcement standing requires demonstrating credible threat of enforcement — NSPM-7's text, the AG Bondi memo, and the Joint Mission Center's operational existence are sufficient.
- **Congressional oversight**: Requests for the Joint Mission Center's targeting criteria, operational guidelines, and list of organizations under review — through FOIA and formal congressional requests — are essential to building the evidentiary record for Section 6103 claims.
- **Sector-wide communication**: The National Council of Nonprofits, Independent Sector, and Charity & Security Network are positioned to issue sector-wide guidance on organizations' rights when contacted by the Joint Mission Center, and to coordinate legal referral networks for organizations that receive inquiries.

### Window 3: Active — SPLC Defense (Indictment dated April 21, 2026)

- **Legal defense coordination**: The civil rights community should treat the SPLC defense as sector-wide litigation with implications beyond SPLC's specific case. A coordinated amicus strategy for any appellate proceedings, and a legal defense fund that ensures SPLC can sustain full defense through trial, are the immediate organizational tasks.
- **Motion to dismiss briefing**: The strongest near-term pathway is a motion to dismiss for legal insufficiency, targeting the missing false-representation-to-donors allegation. Filing within 60–90 days of indictment is standard timing.
- **Congressional document preservation**: The whistleblower evidence of prosecutorial rushing should be documented through congressional oversight channels before potential retaliatory action against the whistleblower.

### Window 4: Ongoing — State Copycat Bill Monitoring

The Florida-Texas pattern indicates that HB 1471 will generate model legislation adoption attempts in other states. Priority monitoring targets: states that have already enacted foreign influence legislation (Nebraska, Arkansas) and competitive states with conservative legislative majorities where the political incentives for nonprofit designation tools are present (Texas, Georgia, Arizona, Wisconsin).

Early intervention — before a bill exits committee — is substantially more effective than post-enactment litigation. The constitutional analysis in this document is designed to support state legislative opposition to copycat bills.

## X. Sector-Level Impact Assessment

The 1.5 million nonprofits in the United States span healthcare, education, housing, civil rights, immigrant services, environmental protection, reproductive health, legal aid, arts and culture, and civic participation. The mechanisms documented in this domain do not threaten all 1.5 million organizations equally; they concentrate risk on organizations whose advocacy positions fall within NSPM-7's ideological definitions and whose financial structures are most vulnerable to the combined effects of funding disruption and reputational damage.

The highest-risk sector characteristics are: (a) federal grant dependence above 15 percent of revenue; (b) advocacy positions on immigration, reproductive rights, racial justice, LGBTQ+ rights, or law enforcement accountability; (c) organizational infrastructure dependent on confidential source relationships or investigative methodologies; and (d) organizational identity as a "civil rights" or "social justice" organization — categories that attract both political targeting and donor mobilization in their defense.

The sectors that fall in all four categories simultaneously — civil rights organizations, immigrant advocacy organizations, reproductive health organizations, and First Amendment legal organizations — are the primary targets of the threat landscape documented in this domain. They are also the organizations best positioned to mount pre-enforcement challenges, because their direct advocacy activity gives them the clearest standing to argue that NSPM-7's definitions encompass their work.

The counter-resource the sector has that Hungary's civil society did not have at the equivalent stage in Orbán's consolidation is a fully developed constitutional framework with favorable recent precedent (*Vullo*, *NAACP v. Alabama*, vagueness doctrine) and a federal judiciary that, despite significant pressure, continues to issue preliminary injunctions against mechanisms that fail constitutional review on the merits. The CAIR preliminary injunction is the proof of concept. The advocacy windows identified above are the implementation pathway.
