## Executive Summary

The constitutional power of the purse — Congress's exclusive authority to appropriate federal funds — is the single most important structural check on executive power. Without it, impeachment is symbolic, oversight is performative, and independent agencies are subject to fiscal strangulation regardless of their statutory mandates. Since January 2025, the Trump administration has mounted the most systematic assault on congressional fiscal authority since Richard Nixon's impoundment campaign in the early 1970s. The assault is neither accidental nor improvised: it was planned in Project 2025, executed through OMB Director Russell Vought's aggressive apportionment theory, and supplemented by four distinct operational mechanisms — agency-level fund holds, OMB apportionment manipulation, Treasury payment system interference, and post-disbursement fund clawback.

The scale is without modern precedent. Congressional Democrats documented more than $425 billion in withheld appropriations by late 2025. GAO launched 39 separate investigations into potential Impoundment Control Act violations and confirmed violations across at least eight programs — NIH grants, Head Start, electric vehicle charging infrastructure, school energy efficiency grants, and others — while the Trump administration systematically declined to provide GAO with the apportionment data needed to complete its reviews. Republicans in the House then proposed cutting GAO's budget by 50 percent and explicitly prohibiting it from pursuing ICA civil enforcement without a concurrent congressional resolution — a provision designed to declaw the only agency with legal authority to enforce the spending laws.

The legal landscape has been partially favorable. Courts have repeatedly blocked the administration's broadest freezes — the First Circuit in *New York v. Trump* (March 2026) upheld a preliminary injunction covering trillions in frozen assistance — but litigation is slow, standing is contested, and the administration has treated court orders as negotiations rather than commands. Meanwhile, the administration has developed a new theory — "pocket rescissions" — that would allow any president to effectively veto spending bills through the back door by transmitting rescission requests timed to expire before Congress can act. GAO has found this unconstitutional; the courts have not yet definitively resolved it.

The stakes extend beyond budget policy. A Congress stripped of fiscal leverage cannot credibly threaten impeachment, cannot defund unauthorized military operations, and cannot protect independent agencies from executive strangulation. Fiscal authority is the infrastructure of constitutional democracy.

**Key finding**: The mechanisms enabling executive fiscal circumvention are structural, not merely behavioral. Restoring congressional power of the purse requires statutory reforms that close the apportionment manipulation loophole, create enforceable standing for congressional appropriations challenges, reform emergency authority spending triggers, and protect the watchdog capacity (GAO, CBO, Inspectors General) that makes enforcement possible.

## 1. The Current Crisis: A Systematic Assault on Appropriations Law

### 1.1 The Four Mechanisms of Fiscal Circumvention

The Trump administration's assault on congressional appropriations authority since January 2025 has operated through four identifiable and increasingly sophisticated mechanisms, documented by the Center for American Progress, the Congressional Budget Office, and House Appropriations Committee Democrats:

**Mechanism 1: Agency-Level Fund Holds.** The most common tactic. Agencies, directed by political appointees, simply stop obligating funds Congress has appropriated, citing vague "policy review" requirements. Early examples in 2025 included pausing USAID operations, halting NIH grants for cancer and Alzheimer's research, freezing wildfire prevention funding, and suspending the Electric Vehicle Charging (NEVI) program authorized under the 2021 Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act. The EPA paused more than 30 grant programs. FEMA clawed back $80 million intended to help New York City house migrants — one week after disbursing it.

**Mechanism 2: OMB Apportionment Manipulation.** The most legally sophisticated tactic. Congress appropriates money, but agencies cannot obligate or spend it until the Office of Management and Budget "apportions" it — allocates it to specific time periods and purposes. OMB under Director Vought has weaponized this technical step through what Lawfare documented as the "Category C" mechanism: funds placed in Category C are set aside for "future fiscal years," effectively withholding them from agencies even after Congress has appropriated and the fiscal year has begun. By March 2026, OMB had issued 345 Category C apportionments, comprising 8.64 percent of all apportionments — compared to 3.94 percent under the Biden administration. OMB expanded Category C to at least 64 previously unused budgetary accounts. The D.C. Circuit established in *City of New Haven* (1985) that presidential policy preferences cannot justify fund withholding under the Antideficiency Act; the Trump administration has ignored this precedent.

**Mechanism 3: Treasury Payment System Interference.** In early 2025, DOGE personnel gained access to the Treasury's payment processing systems, creating a third potential control point at which payment orders could be blocked after obligation. Judge Paul Engelmayer in the Southern District of New York issued a temporary restraining order in February 2025 blocking DOGE from accessing these systems. The administration's claimed DOGE "savings" — ranging from $135 billion to the implausible $206 billion on DOGE's "wall of receipts" — have never been independently verified; independent analysis estimated DOGE cuts will cost taxpayers $135 billion when second-order effects are counted, and the IRS projected more than $500 billion in lost revenue from enforcement cuts.

**Mechanism 4: Pocket Rescissions.** The most constitutionally aggressive tactic. While the Rescissions Act of 2025 (P.L. 119-28, signed July 24, 2025) followed the legal ICA process — rescinding $7.9 billion from international assistance and $1.1 billion from the Corporation for Public Broadcasting, marking the first successful presidential rescission since 1992 — the administration simultaneously asserted a second theory: "pocket rescissions," in which rescission requests are transmitted so late in the fiscal year that funds expire before Congress can act. On August 28, 2025, the White House proposed 15 rescissions totaling nearly $5 billion in foreign assistance, for accounts expiring September 30 — meaning the 45-day congressional review period would run past the expiration date. GAO concluded this violates the ICA and likened it to the Line Item Veto Act that the Supreme Court struck down in *Clinton v. City of New York* (1998). A D.C. district court entered a preliminary injunction on September 3, 2025, requiring the administration to obligate the $10 billion in expiring foreign assistance funds.

### 1.2 The Scale of Violation

The aggregate documented by House Appropriations Committee Democrats Rosa DeLauro and Senate Appropriations Committee ranking member Patty Murray: more than $425 billion in federal spending frozen by the Trump administration as of late 2025. GAO opened 39 separate ICA investigation tracks. Confirmed violations include:

- **NIH research grants**: Withheld funding for cancer, Alzheimer's, and other medical research programs
- **Head Start**: GAO confirmed violation when administration restricted program funds
- **NEVI (electric vehicle charging)**: Transportation's Federal Highway Administration confirmed violation when it refused to award infrastructure funding to states
- **Department of Energy Renew America's Schools**: Suspended grants for public school energy efficiency upgrades, citing need to confirm alignment with "priorities and policies"
- **Institute of Museum and Library Services**: Withheld funds for library and museum grants
- **USAID/foreign assistance**: Comprehensive program shutdown; OMB declined to provide GAO with apportionment data to verify compliance

In each GAO investigation, the administraiton's typical response was to decline to provide apportionment schedules or respond to GAO inquiries — rendering independent verification impossible and turning the watchdog into a documentation agency with no enforcement bite.

### 1.3 The Venezuela Fiscal Nexus

Operation Absolute Resolve (January 3, 2026) is documented in Domain 28 of this framework. The fiscal dimension is distinct and significant. The operation deployed 150+ aircraft, Delta Force ground troops, and at least 200 U.S. personnel in an operation characterized by the administration as "law enforcement with military support" — a characterization designed to evade the War Powers Resolution's 48-hour reporting requirement and the Appropriations Clause's requirement that expenditures be authorized. The administration invoked the "arrest operation" theory specifically to avoid triggering congressional appropriations requirements that would apply to a declared military operation.

The legal theory allowed the administration to draw on existing DoD and DEA operational funds without seeking supplemental appropriations — an end-run around both war powers and appropriations oversight in a single operational decision. Critically, the two existing authorizations that might have been invoked (from 2001 and 1991) had been repealed in the FY2026 National Defense Authorization Act signed by Trump himself, approximately one week before the operation. The administration thus conducted a major foreign operation without available statutory authorization and without seeking new appropriations, relying solely on discretionary executive spending authority.

### 1.4 The Watchdog Dismantlement

The administration's fiscal circumvention has been accompanied by a parallel campaign to eliminate the oversight capacity that could detect and sanction violations. House Republicans proposed cutting GAO's budget by approximately 50 percent — from $828 million to $415 million — which would eliminate more than 2,200 expert staff and cancel 39 ongoing impoundment investigations. In 2024 alone, GAO identified $67.5 billion in savings or efficiency recommendations, returning approximately $76 for every dollar invested in the agency. The proposed 50 percent cut would also explicitly prohibit GAO from pursuing civil ICA enforcement action "unless Congress has adopted a concurrent resolution authorizing" such action — a provision that would require a supermajority approval process for every individual enforcement decision, effectively nullifying the agency's watchdog function.

The Library of Congress and CBO have also faced proposed funding reductions, though both survived a November 2025 legislative branch bill. The pattern is clear: eliminate the informational infrastructure that makes congressional oversight meaningful.

## 2. Root Causes: Why Executive Fiscal Overreach Is Structurally Enabled

### 2.1 The Constitutional Architecture and Its Gaps

The Appropriations Clause (Art. I, § 9, cl. 7) is unambiguous: "No Money shall be drawn from the Treasury, but in Consequence of Appropriations made by Law." The Founders designed this as the ultimate legislative check on executive ambition. Madison in Federalist 58 called the power of the purse "the most complete and effectual weapon...for obtaining a redress of every grievance."

Yet the Clause contains structural gaps that have been exploited in every era of executive expansion:

The Clause requires appropriations but does not specify timing, granularity, or enforcement mechanisms. Congress can appropriate in lump sums (giving executives discretion) or in detail (constraining them), but appropriations language has grown less specific over time as Congress has increasingly delegated operational discretion. "Transfer authorities" allow DoD to move up to $6 billion between appropriations accounts with only notification (not approval) requirements. "Reprogramming" allows smaller reallocations with even lighter oversight — the Army's request in 2026 to raise the notification threshold from $15 million to $50 million for procurement programs was rejected, but the request itself illustrates the consistent direction of executive preference.

Emergency declarations compound the gap. The National Emergencies Act (NEA) and International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) create separate spending streams that can be activated by presidential declaration without legislative appropriation. While the Supreme Court's February 20, 2026 decision in the IEEPA tariff case invoked the major questions doctrine to limit presidential tariff authority, the broader question of emergency-triggered spending authority — as distinct from economic regulation — remains contested. DoD emergency authorities allow significant reallocations once an emergency is declared; the administration declared multiple concurrent national emergencies in 2025 (border, fentanyl, energy) each activating separate spending streams.

### 2.2 The Apportionment System as a Structural Vulnerability

The Antideficiency Act (31 U.S.C. §§ 1341-1351) prohibits agencies from spending beyond what Congress appropriated but simultaneously grants OMB broad apportionment authority — the power to allocate funds in time, purpose, or amount. This dual structure creates an inherent tension: OMB can technically "comply" with the ADA by apportioning funds into future periods while functionally defeating congressional intent.

The Project 2025 blueprint, written in significant part by OMB Director Vought, explicitly called for aggressive use of apportionment authority to align spending with executive priorities. The Category C expansion documented above is the direct operational implementation of that blueprint. The fundamental vulnerability is enforcement asymmetry: while it is illegal to not spend appropriated funding, the criminal penalties under the ADA (18 U.S.C. § 1349: fines, imprisonment up to two years for willful violations) have never been prosecuted against senior executive officials. Enforcement depends on the same DOJ that reports to the president — and whose leadership is documented in Domain 29 as having been captured for political use.

### 2.3 The Post-Loper Bright Landscape

The Supreme Court's June 2024 decision in *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo* (overruling *Chevron U.S.A. v. Natural Resources Defense Council*) has indirect but significant implications for appropriations disputes. Under Chevron, courts deferred to executive agencies' interpretations of ambiguous statutes — including budget statutes. Post-*Loper Bright*, courts must independently determine the meaning of statutory provisions rather than deferring to executive interpretation.

For appropriations law, this is potentially favorable: courts reviewing whether OMB's Category C apportionments comply with the Antideficiency Act's reserve provision, or whether "pocket rescissions" comply with the Impoundment Control Act, are now required to interpret those statutes themselves rather than deferring to OMB's self-serving interpretations. The First Circuit's *New York v. Trump* ruling (March 2026) reflects this shift — the court did not defer to the administration's characterization of its own freeze as compliant with appropriations law, but applied its own statutory analysis to find the freeze "arbitrary and capricious."

The limitation of this shift is standing. Congressional members and even the institutions of Congress face substantial standing barriers under *Raines v. Byrd* (1997), which held that individual members of Congress lack Article III standing to challenge laws affecting Congress as an institution. State attorneys general, as in *New York v. Trump*, have had more success establishing standing through harm to their states' programs. But institutional congressional standing to enforce the Appropriations Clause against executive impoundment — the most direct enforcement mechanism — remains unresolved.

### 2.4 The Historical Pattern: Nixon to Trump

The current crisis is the third major impoundment confrontation in American history. Nixon's impoundment campaign (1971-74) led directly to the Congressional Budget and Impoundment Control Act of 1974, which created the modern appropriations enforcement framework. The ICA requires the president to notify Congress of any proposed rescission or deferral; Congress has 45 days to act on rescissions. The Supreme Court in *Train v. City of New York* (1975) unanimously held that even absent the ICA, the president lacked unilateral authority to impound Clean Water Act funds.

The 1974 framework was adequate for a half-century because no administration tested its limits aggressively. The 2025-26 campaign is the first systematic attempt to exploit every structural gap in the ICA simultaneously — agency holds (not formally notified as deferrals), OMB apportionment manipulation (claimed as Antideficiency Act compliance rather than impoundment), pocket rescissions (using ICA form to achieve substantive veto), and Treasury payment interference. The ICA's framework was written to address the Nixon pattern; it was not designed to address a coordinated multi-mechanism assault.

## 3. Structural Vulnerabilities: Where Executive Action Can Bypass Appropriations

### 3.1 Emergency Authority Spending Triggers

At least 136 statutory provisions across dozens of titles of the U.S. Code are activated by a presidential declaration of national emergency under the NEA. While IEEPA is primarily an economic regulation statute (its tariff application was constrained by the February 2026 Supreme Court ruling), other emergency-triggered authorities include direct spending authorities: DoD construction funds can be redirected, military personnel can be deployed, resource-sharing agreements can be executed, and foreign assistance can be redirected — all outside the normal appropriations cycle once an emergency is declared.

The Trump administration declared at least five simultaneous national emergencies in early 2025, some of which activated spending authorities that permitted reallocation of previously appropriated funds to the border wall and immigration enforcement without supplemental appropriations. The DoD's emergency construction authority (10 U.S.C. § 2808) allows the Secretary of Defense to undertake construction projects from existing military construction funds once a national emergency requiring armed forces has been declared — authority used under the first Trump administration for border wall construction and not reversed despite court challenges.

### 3.2 Transfer and Reprogramming Authorities

The FY2026 Defense Appropriations Act authorizes the Secretary of Defense to transfer up to $6 billion between appropriations accounts without congressional approval, requiring only notification "promptly." Below the notification threshold of $15 million per program, reprogramming can occur without any congressional notice. These authorities, designed for legitimate operational flexibility, create a fiscal shadow zone in which significant spending reallocation is legally permissible.

The Venezuela operation illustrates the cumulative effect: the administration used DoD's existing operational and contingency funds (already appropriated under DoD's broad discretionary accounts), DEA's law enforcement budget, and the "inherent executive authority" theory to fund a 150-aircraft, multi-carrier strike group deployment without seeking supplemental appropriations or formally triggering war powers review.

### 3.3 Independent Agency Funding Structures

The CFPB litigation resolved a separate structural question: the Supreme Court held 7-2 in *CFPB v. Community Financial Services Association* (May 2024) that Congress may constitutionally fund agencies through mechanisms other than annual appropriations — in the CFPB's case, through Federal Reserve earnings. This ruling confirmed that independent agency funding structures outside the appropriations cycle are constitutionally permissible, but it simultaneously illustrated how executive strangulation can operate without touching appropriations: the Trump administration sought to dismantle the CFPB through personnel action and operational direction rather than through defunding, precisely because the funding stream was insulated.

The reverse dynamic — where agencies are funded through appropriations but operationally strangled through apportionment holds — is the more common current threat. Independent agencies with appropriated funding (EPA, FTC, NLRB, others) are vulnerable to the Category C mechanism regardless of their statutory independence.

### 3.4 Congressional Enforcement Gaps

The ICA's enforcement mechanism is GAO referral — GAO can refer impoundment violations to the Comptroller General, who can file civil suit to compel obligation. But the ICA's criminal provision (fines and two-year imprisonment for willful violations) has never been invoked. No senior OMB or agency official has been prosecuted for an impoundment violation in the Act's 51-year history. The civil enforcement mechanism requires either state AG standing (as in *New York v. Trump*) or GAO civil action — which the House Republican FY2026 legislative branch bill would require congressional authorization to pursue, effectively creating a partisan veto over enforcement.

The standing problem is deeper. Congressional plaintiffs are barred by *Raines v. Byrd* from suing as individual members. The full institution of Congress — through its officers (Clerk of the House, Secretary of the Senate) — might be able to establish standing in some circumstances, but this theory has not been litigated successfully. The practical result is that the branch with the clearest constitutional interest in enforcing the Appropriations Clause is the branch least able to enforce it through litigation.

## 4. International Precedent: How Other Democracies Enforce Parliamentary Budget Authority

### 4.1 United Kingdom: Parliamentary Supply and the Public Accounts Committee

The UK's parliamentary system resolves the appropriations enforcement problem through constitutional structure rather than litigation. Government spending requires annual parliamentary authorization through the Supply Estimates cycle: departments submit Main Estimates, Parliament debates on designated Estimates Days (two days of debate annually, required before August 5), and the estimates form the basis of Supply and Appropriation Bills that are formally passed and receive Royal Assent before the funds have legal effect.

The enforcement mechanism operates at multiple levels. Departments cannot legally spend outside their approved "ambit" (the stated purpose of their estimate) — doing so constitutes an "excess vote," which requires retrospective authorization by the Public Accounts Committee (PAC) and a Consolidated Fund Act. The PAC is a permanent select committee of the House of Commons, chaired by convention by the leader of the official opposition, specifically designed to review government spending and follow up on excess votes and audit findings. The National Audit Office (NAO), analogous to GAO, provides independent audit support to the PAC and has no equivalent vulnerability to executive budget manipulation — its independence is secured by statute and parliamentary appointment procedures outside ministerial control.

The key difference from the U.S. system: in the UK, failure to authorize spending means the government literally cannot pay its bills, triggering a confidence crisis that would force an election. The parliamentary system resolves the enforcement problem by making fiscal authority and governmental survival coterminous.

### 4.2 Canada: Confidence Votes and Supply

Canada's parliamentary system ties the supply process even more directly to governmental survival. Budget motions and votes on supply estimates are explicitly "confidence matters" — a government that loses a supply vote falls. The Carney government in 2025 survived a budget confidence vote by 170-168, with two NDP abstentions — a margin that illustrates how direct the fiscal accountability mechanism is. Prime Minister Joe Clark's government fell in 1979 when it lost a budget vote.

Canada's Parliamentary Budget Officer (PBO), established in 2006, provides independent fiscal analysis insulated from executive manipulation by direct reporting to Parliament rather than to government. The PBO can request financial data from government departments and publish findings over government objection — a model that addresses the informational asymmetry between executive and legislative branches that enables appropriations manipulation in the U.S. context.

### 4.3 Germany: Constitutional Court Enforcement

Germany's Federal Constitutional Court enforces parliamentary budget authority directly. When the Scholz government in 2023 attempted to reallocate €60 billion in unused pandemic debt to a Climate and Transformation Fund — a transfer that bypassed the normal appropriations process — the Constitutional Court struck it down as incompatible with the Basic Law's "debt brake" provisions (Articles 109(3), 110(2), and 115(2)), which require spending to be authorized in advance, for specific fiscal years, and within constitutional deficit limits.

The constitutional principles the Court enforced — yearly budgeting, the annuality principle (authorizations may only be used in the fiscal year for which they were granted), and the current-year principle (only revenue and expenditure actually effected in the same year may be budgeted) — are structurally equivalent to the requirements of the U.S. Appropriations Clause and the Antideficiency Act. The difference is enforcement: Germany has a constitutional court with the institutional standing and precedent to strike down executive fiscal maneuvers directly. The U.S. does not have a court that has reliably exercised this authority.

### 4.4 Australia: The 1975 Crisis as Negative Model

Australia's 1975 constitutional crisis demonstrates that budget authority can be weaponized in the opposite direction — by a legislature against an executive. When the Senate under Malcolm Fraser refused to pass supply for the Whitlam government in October-November 1975, the resulting deadlock triggered the Governor-General's unprecedented dismissal of a sitting prime minister. The crisis resulted in formal institutional reforms: the Senate retains its legal power to block supply but the convention against exercising it has been reinforced. The lesson for U.S. reform is structural: the resolution of the 1975 crisis was ultimately political and norm-based rather than legal. Absent clear legal enforcement mechanisms, fiscal standoffs are resolved by whoever is willing to escalate furthest.

### 4.5 New Zealand: Select Committee Scrutiny

New Zealand's select committee model provides a third enforcement mechanism — mandatory pre-authorization scrutiny rather than post-hoc audit. Estimates hearings before parliamentary select committees are the primary mechanism for examining departmental spending plans before they are authorized. Recent reforms (2024) have doubled the time allocated to Estimates hearings and require all public sector organizations to face full parliamentary scrutiny at least once per three-year parliamentary term. The Auditor-General's Office provides independent audit support directly to select committees, analogous to GAO's role but with clearer institutional independence.

The relevant lesson for U.S. reform: New Zealand's system creates accountability through transparency and mandatory executive disclosure rather than litigation — reducing the informational asymmetry that allows the U.S. executive to manipulate apportionments without detection.

## 5. Reform Pathways

### Reform Pathway 1: The Congressional Power of the Purse Act — Immediate Statutory Reinforcement

**Problem addressed**: ICA enforcement is toothless; ADA criminal penalties have never been used; GAO's enforcement authority is vulnerable to defunding and procedural restriction.

**Reform**: Enact the Congressional Power of the Purse Act (H.R. 5220, 119th Congress; companion Senate bill co-introduced by Sen. Van Hollen) as introduced. Key provisions:
- Require the executive branch to report to Congress on all ICA and ADA violations identified by GAO within 30 days of a GAO finding
- Authorize discipline up to termination, fines, and imprisonment for officials found to have willfully violated the ICA
- Strengthen and expedite GAO's ability to obtain apportionment data from agencies — eliminating OMB's de facto ability to refuse data requests
- Create mandatory apportionment transparency: require OMB to publish all apportionments (including Category C designations) in a public database within 72 hours of issuance, with justification statements for any withholding
- Authorize GAO civil enforcement without requiring a concurrent congressional resolution (reversing the restriction proposed in the FY2026 legislative branch bill)

**Implementation**: Can be enacted through normal legislative process; does not require constitutional amendment. Could be attached to an appropriations bill as a rider, reducing filibuster exposure in the Senate if structured as a reconciliation-eligible provision.

**Timeline**: Achievable in the first year of any Congress willing to prioritize it. The Act has existing legislative text and committee support.

**Connection to Domain 2**: Addresses the structural asymmetry documented in Domain 2's analysis of executive power expansion — specifically the absence of enforceable accountability mechanisms that do not depend on DOJ cooperation.

### Reform Pathway 2: Mandatory Apportionment Reporting and Category C Reform

**Problem addressed**: OMB's Category C mechanism allows policy-driven impoundment disguised as technical fiscal management; current law does not require public disclosure of withholding rationales in time for congressional response.

**Reform**: Amend the Antideficiency Act (31 U.S.C. § 1512) to:
- Require OMB to provide written justification for any apportionment placing funds in Category C beyond a de minimis threshold, with justification tied to one of the three permissible grounds (contingencies, efficiency savings, or specific statutory authorization)
- Create a presumption of illegality for any Category C apportionment that cannot be traced to one of the three permissible grounds, subject to automatic judicial review on an expedited schedule
- Require simultaneous notification to the relevant congressional appropriations committees of any Category C apportionment exceeding $50 million, with a 30-day congressional review window
- Prohibit Category C apportionments for funds within 45 days of expiration (closing the pocket rescission mechanism at the apportionment level)

**Implementation**: Statutory; requires simple majority in both chambers and presidential signature. In an adverse executive environment, could be attached to must-pass appropriations legislation as a condition. The provision is narrow enough that it could attract bipartisan support among appropriators concerned about executive encroachment on their institutional prerogatives.

**Timeline**: Achievable within a single legislative session with targeted appropriations committee leadership.

### Reform Pathway 3: Congressional Standing Legislation

**Problem addressed**: *Raines v. Byrd* (1997) bars individual members of Congress from establishing Article III standing to challenge executive violations of the Appropriations Clause; institutional standing is unclear; enforcement depends on state AGs (who lack general appropriations enforcement authority) or GAO civil action (which can be defunded or procedurally blocked).

**Reform**: Enact legislation creating explicit statutory standing for:
- The Comptroller General (on behalf of Congress) to file civil actions seeking obligating orders when GAO determines an impoundment has occurred, without requiring concurrent congressional authorization
- Congressional committees with appropriations jurisdiction to seek injunctive relief when appropriated funds in their jurisdiction have been withheld in violation of the ICA
- A compressed judicial review schedule (30-day resolution requirement) for impoundment challenges, analogous to the expedited review procedures in FISA or the War Powers Resolution

Constitutional foundation: The major questions doctrine (*West Virginia v. EPA*, 2022; IEEPA tariff case, February 2026) and the post-*Loper Bright* interpretive framework provide favorable judicial terrain for this legislation. Courts post-*Loper Bright* are required to independently assess statutory compliance rather than deferring to executive interpretation, making explicit congressional standing legislation more likely to be effective.

**Implementation**: This pathway may require carefully drafted legislation to survive a *Raines v. Byrd* challenge. The strongest approach is to grant standing to the institution through its officers (Clerk of the House, Comptroller General) rather than to individual members — consistent with how *Coleman v. Miller* (1939) created standing for a state legislature as an institution. This approach has been analyzed favorably in scholarly literature (including the 2018 Yale Law Journal note on Appropriations Clause litigation) but has not been enacted.

**Timeline**: Medium-term (1-2 Congresses); requires sustained committee attention and likely Supreme Court clarification of institutional standing doctrine.

**Connection to Domain 6**: This pathway is dependent on judicial independence and judicial willingness to adjudicate separation-of-powers questions — the core concern documented in Domain 6. A court that invokes the political question doctrine to avoid impoundment cases would nullify this reform.

### Reform Pathway 4: Emergency Authority Sunset and Appropriations Nexus Requirements

**Problem addressed**: Emergency declarations activate hundreds of statutory spending authorities outside normal appropriations review; IEEPA, NEA, and DoD emergency construction authorities allow significant fiscal action without congressional authorization.

**Reform**: 
- Amend the National Emergencies Act (50 U.S.C. § 1601 et seq.) to require automatic expiration of spending authorities activated by emergency declaration after 60 days unless affirmatively reauthorized by concurrent resolution (replacing the current mechanism, which requires affirmative congressional action to terminate rather than to continue)
- Require OMB to publish an inventory of all spending authorities activated by each national emergency declaration within 30 days of the declaration, with dollar estimates
- Require appropriations committee review of any emergency spending exceeding $1 billion — creating a trigger for mandatory oversight hearings rather than discretionary committee action
- Amend 10 U.S.C. § 2808 (DoD emergency construction) to require a joint resolution of authorization for any emergency construction project exceeding $500 million
- Establish a statutory definition distinguishing "law enforcement operations" from "military operations" for purposes of both War Powers and appropriations review, closing the Venezuela "arrest operation" loophole (see Domain 28 for the specific drafting proposal)

**Implementation**: The shift from affirmative termination to affirmative continuation for emergency powers is the single most structurally important reform and is analogous to the change IEEPA reform proposals have called for. It can be enacted as a standalone statute or as part of a comprehensive emergency powers reform package. The Venezuela operation's specific fiscal mechanism (DoD discretionary accounts used for a law enforcement operation without supplemental appropriations) requires the additional statutory definition provision.

**Timeline**: Medium-term; IEEPA reform has bipartisan support in the aftermath of the February 2026 Supreme Court tariff decision. The emergency authority sunset reform is structurally linked to war powers reform (Domain 19f) and should be coordinated with that legislative effort.

### Reform Pathway 5: Congressional Capacity and Watchdog Protection

**Problem addressed**: Congress's informational capacity to exercise fiscal oversight has been systematically degraded through the defunding of GAO, CBO, and other support agencies; the executive branch has 120 times more funding than the legislative branch for policy analysis and oversight capacity.

**Reform**:
- Protect GAO's budget at no less than $1 billion (compared to the proposed $415 million cut) with mandatory funding floors embedded in statute, insulated from single-year appropriations manipulation
- Restore and expand CBO's budget and mandate with specific direction to score all emergency spending authorities activated by presidential declaration within 60 days
- Establish a Congressional Intelligence Support Unit, analogous to the UK's National Audit Office, with independent authority to access executive apportionment data and classified budget materials relevant to congressional appropriations oversight
- Create a digital appropriations transparency dashboard — requiring real-time publication of all apportionment actions, obligation rates by program, and Category C withholdings — accessible to congressional staff, GAO, and the public
- Increase congressional staff salaries to competitive levels with executive branch equivalents, addressing the documented brain drain that has shifted expertise to the executive and lobbying sectors

**Implementation**: The funding floor and transparency dashboard can be enacted as appropriations provisions or standalone legislation. The Congressional Intelligence Support Unit requires a more substantial organizational change but addresses a genuine informational asymmetry that cannot be resolved without dedicated institutional capacity. The Brennan Center and Project on Government Oversight have both published detailed proposals in this area.

**Timeline**: The transparency dashboard is achievable in a single session; the institutional capacity reforms require 2-3 Congresses and sustained political will.

**Connection to Domain 5**: Domain 5's analysis of congressional structure and dysfunction is directly relevant here — the capacity reforms in this pathway are prerequisites for meaningful fiscal oversight, not supplements to it.

## 6. Implementation Timeline for Democratic Recovery

**Immediate (first 100 days of a reform-aligned Congress)**:
- Protect GAO and CBO from further funding cuts; reverse the 50 percent GAO reduction proposal
- Enact transparency provisions: mandatory apportionment publication within 72 hours, public dashboard
- Authorize GAO civil enforcement without concurrent resolution requirement
- Open committee hearings on all active GAO impoundment investigations, requiring OMB and agency officials to testify

**Short-term (first year)**:
- Enact the Congressional Power of the Purse Act (H.R. 5220 framework) with strengthened criminal enforcement provisions
- Enact Category C reform amendments to the Antideficiency Act
- Enact mandatory reporting requirements for emergency-triggered spending authorities

**Medium-term (first two Congresses)**:
- Enact institutional standing legislation for Comptroller General and committee chairs
- Enact emergency authority sunset reforms (requiring affirmative reauthorization rather than affirmative termination)
- Enact statutory definition of "law enforcement operation" vs. "military operation" closing the Venezuela loophole
- Rebuild congressional staff capacity and salary competitiveness

**Structural/long-term (beyond two Congresses)**:
- Pursue constitutional clarification of institutional congressional standing through test litigation under the new standing statute
- Evaluate structural reforms to the budget process (biennial appropriations, mandatory omnibus deadlines with sequestration enforcement) to reduce reliance on continuing resolutions that reduce fiscal precision
- Consider constitutional amendment proposals for automatic appropriations enforcement mechanisms insulated from DOJ non-enforcement — analogous to the automatic sequestration trigger in the Budget Enforcement Act of 1990

**Sequencing logic**: The transparency and watchdog protection reforms (immediate phase) are prerequisites for everything else — without apportionment data and a funded GAO, the standing legislation and category C reforms have no enforcement infrastructure. The standing legislation (medium-term) can only be effective after *Loper Bright*'s post-Chevron interpretive framework has been applied in enough lower court decisions to create a favorable precedent landscape. The structural reforms are longer-term because they require not just legislative majorities but sustained inter-branch coordination that is not achievable in the crisis environment.

The foundational insight is that fiscal authority and democratic recovery are not separate tracks. No democratic renewal scenario is credible if the legislative branch cannot enforce its own appropriations decisions. Restoring congressional power of the purse is the precondition for making every other institutional reform durable.
