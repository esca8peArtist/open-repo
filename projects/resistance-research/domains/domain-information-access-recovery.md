**Crisis window**: Active — systemic dismantlement of transparency infrastructure is accelerating across all three access pillars simultaneously
**Priority designation**: Phase 3 Candidate 6 — structural deepening for journalists, open-government advocates, congressional oversight staff, civil society accountability organizations
**Cross-domain connections**: Domain 5 (Administrative Law and Regulatory Capture), Domain 16 (Transparency and Accountability), Domain 26 (Government Accountability and Institutional Resilience), Domain 29 (DOJ Capture and Prosecutorial Weaponization), Phase 3 Candidate 5 (Legislative Branch Capacity)

## Executive Summary

Public information is the oxygen of democratic accountability. A government that can hide its actions from citizens, journalists, and Congress is a government that cannot be held accountable, cannot be voted out of office on an informed basis, and cannot be checked by the institutions theoretically designed to constrain it. The Freedom of Information Act, the federal classification system, congressional investigative authority, the inspector general corps, whistleblower protections, and the National Archives are not bureaucratic technicalities. They are the foundational infrastructure of democratic oversight — the mechanisms by which the governed know what their government is actually doing.

That infrastructure is being dismantled with precision in 2025-2026, and the dismantlement is structurally coherent: the administration is simultaneously degrading each of the three independent information channels through which the public learns about government action. FOIA requests face record backlogs, politically retaliatory staffing cuts, and administrative closure policies designed to sidestep legal processing obligations. The classification system is expanding — with 75 to 90 percent of classified material estimated as overclassified — while NARA, the independent institutional guardian of government records, has had its archivist fired without legal cause and replaced by a sitting Cabinet secretary in a maneuver that appears to violate the statute governing the position. Congressional investigative capacity, documented in detail by Phase 3 Candidate 5 (Legislative Branch Capacity), is paired with a parallel problem: even when Congress has the authority to demand information, agencies are refusing to comply, whistleblowers face retaliation at nine times the historical rate, and the DOJ that would enforce contempt citations is itself captured.

The critical finding of this domain is not that any single information channel has been weakened. It is that all three channels are being degraded simultaneously, in ways that are structurally coordinated and mutually reinforcing. When FOIA backlogs grow, journalists turn to congressional investigations. When congressional investigations are blocked, advocates turn to whistleblowers. When whistleblowers face retaliation and IGs are fired, the information system has no functional redundancy. The cumulative effect is not merely inconvenience for open-government advocates. It is the construction of an information moat around executive power — a condition in which democratic accountability becomes structurally impossible, not merely politically difficult.

**Key structural vulnerabilities:**
1. **FOIA infrastructure collapse**: Government-wide FOIA backlogs increased 42 percent in one year. The CDC's entire 22-person FOIA office was eliminated. DOE announced administrative closure of all pre-October 2024 requests unless requestors re-confirmed. FOIA litigation has nearly doubled compared to the equivalent Biden-era period.
2. **Classification system capture**: The archivist — the officer statutorily required to be independent and non-political — was fired and replaced by the Secretary of State. NARA faces a $60 million budget cut. The classification system costs $18 billion annually to administer, with 75-90 percent of classified material estimated as not meeting classification standards.
3. **Whistleblower retaliation surge**: Energy Department IG whistleblower retaliation investigations jumped from 5 to 45 in a single fiscal year — a 900 percent increase. The administration moved to strip senior civil servants of whistleblower protections entirely. CIGIE was illegally defunded before Republican senators forced partial restoration.
4. **Records integrity under assault**: DOGE used Signal and personal devices for official communications, likely destroying federal records. The administration claims Presidential Records Act protection for DOGE records, potentially keeping them from the public until 2034. The Signalgate exposure revealed that unsecured commercial messaging apps were used to discuss Yemen military strikes.
5. **Congressional oversight obstruction**: The pattern documented in Candidate 5 — refused documents, blocked witnesses, denied agency access — has a specific information dimension: over 1,000 FOIA lawsuits filed in 15 months compared to 591 in the equivalent Biden-era period represent a litigation surge driven by blanket non-compliance that cannot be resolved administratively.

**Central thesis**: Information access is not one domain among many. It is the prerequisite for every other domain in this framework. Without accurate information about what the government is doing, voting is uninformed, litigation lacks evidence, journalism cannot investigate, and reform cannot be targeted. The dismantlement of information access infrastructure is therefore the precondition for the dismantlement of every other accountability mechanism. Rebuilding it is not merely a transparency priority — it is the foundational requirement for democratic recovery.

## 1. FOIA Mechanics and Degradation: The Architecture of Systematic Obstruction

### 1.1 FOIA's Constitutional Role and Structural Design

The Freedom of Information Act, enacted in 1966 and significantly expanded in 1974 following Watergate, operates on a constitutional premise: in a democracy, the people who pay for government and live under its authority are entitled to know what their government is doing. The Act creates a presumption of disclosure — agencies must release records unless they can demonstrate that one of nine specific exemptions applies. The burden falls on the government to justify secrecy, not on the citizen to justify access.

That presumption is operationally undone when agencies have structural incentives and political direction to maximize delay, deny fee waivers, invoke exemptions expansively, and eliminate the staff who would otherwise process requests. The data from FY 2024 and 2025 documents exactly this pattern.

The government received 1,501,432 FOIA requests in FY 2024 — a 25 percent increase over the previous year. Processing performance was mixed: agencies processed 1,499,265 requests, with approximately 251 million records posted online proactively. These numbers suggest functional volume. But the backlog trend was already deteriorating before the 2025 staff cuts: the agencies with the highest volumes — DHS, DOJ, VA, Defense, and HHS — accounted for 84 percent of all requests and 85 percent of all processing, creating concentrated bottlenecks where small staff reductions generate outsized backlogs.

The 2025-2026 data reveals what happened when the administration systematically eliminated those processing staff:

- **Defense Department**: Pending requests rose 20 percent; backlogged requests rose 42 percent in a single year, to more than 30,000 cases.
- **State Department**: Backlog grew from 21,000 to 27,619 — a 31 percent increase.
- **Transportation Department**: Backlog grew from 8,000 to 11,250 while the department lost 10 percent of its FOIA staff and received 11 percent more requests.
- **Energy Department**: Backlogged requests grew from 1,629 to 2,277. DOE then announced a policy of administratively closing all requests filed before October 2024 unless requestors sent confirmation emails — creating a procedural trap designed to eliminate the backlog on paper without actually processing the underlying records.
- **Simple requests**: Average response time rose from 39 to 44 days governmentwide.

The FOIA litigation surge is the structural response to this obstruction: over 1,000 FOIA lawsuits were filed between January 20, 2026 and April 2026 — compared to 591 during the equivalent period of the Biden administration. This nearly 70 percent increase does not reflect a sudden increase in journalistic or advocacy interest in government records. It reflects the replacement of administrative compliance with litigation as the only functional channel for obtaining records.

### 1.2 Political Weaponization of FOIA Administration

The staffing cuts are not neutral budget decisions. The pattern of who was cut, who was hired, and which offices were targeted reveals a deliberate strategy to convert FOIA administration from a transparency function into a political instrument.

**Targeted eliminations:**

The CDC's entire 22-person FOIA office was placed on administrative leave pending termination on June 2, 2025. This came after HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. had publicly promised "radical transparency" — a description that a former NARA director of litigation called a "lie" after the firings. Simultaneously, Kennedy's HHS eliminated FOIA staff at the FDA and the NIH. HHS described the action as "streamlining siloed offices," while the practical result was that health agencies conducting some of the most consequential public health actions in the country lost their capacity to respond to records requests about those actions.

The Justice Department's Office of Information Policy — the central policy body coordinating FOIA compliance across the federal government — had its director, Bobak Talebian, fired two months into his term. The OIP director position sets the tone for the government's interpretation of FOIA exemptions and its guidance to agency FOIA officers. Removing the director created a policy vacuum that agencies filled by defaulting to the most restrictive interpretations available.

The Bureau of Land Management's Arizona division was reduced to a single person handling all FOIA requests — a reduction that translates directly into backlogs for any journalistic or advocacy inquiry about land management decisions in a state with extensive federal land.

At Customs and Border Protection, at least three FOIA office employees, including the director, were fired after one employee objected to mislabeling completed assessments as drafts — a specifically targeted retaliation against FOIA compliance rather than against budget inefficiency.

**Politically motivated appointments:**

Roman Jankowski was appointed DHS Chief FOIA Officer despite — or because of — his prior record: as a Heritage Foundation employee, he had submitted thousands of FOIA requests specifically targeting Biden-era employees who discussed climate change or diversity initiatives. Appointing a FOIA requester who weaponized the Act to identify political opponents as the chief FOIA enforcement officer at the largest single-agency FOIA processor in the government converts the Act's administration into an adversarial function.

**USCIS processing obstruction:**

A whistleblower revealed that the USCIS FOIA office was "rejecting or throwing out" immigration proceeding requests — precisely the records that immigration attorneys, detained individuals, and their families need to understand what the government is doing. This is FOIA obstruction at the point of maximum democratic urgency: requests about the government's use of coercive power against people in custody.

**Fee waiver denial as a structural barrier:**

Of 2,502 fee waiver requests submitted to federal agencies in a recent reporting period, only 152 were granted while 1,101 remained under review — a grant rate of approximately 6 percent, with nearly 44 percent held in limbo. Fee waivers are the mechanism by which news organizations, nonprofits, and researchers can access records without paying the commercial rate for extensive searches and document production. Denying them converts FOIA into a pay-to-know system that excludes small organizations and individual journalists from meaningful access.

### 1.3 Exemption Abuse: The "Withhold It Because You Want To" Dynamic

The legal framework for FOIA exemptions creates nine categories of information the government may withhold — ranging from national security (Exemption 1) to personal privacy (Exemption 6) to internal agency communications (Exemption 5). The administrative history of FOIA is substantially the history of agencies pushing the boundaries of these exemptions to withhold information that is politically inconvenient rather than genuinely sensitive.

Exemption 5 — the "deliberative process privilege" — is the most abused. It protects predecisional communications among agency personnel to enable frank internal deliberation. The DOJ's Office of Legal Counsel has weaponized Exemption 5 to withhold its own final, binding legal opinions — the legal interpretations that have governed executive branch action for decades and that Congress and the public have no way to evaluate or challenge without access. As POGO's investigation documented, OLC's use of Exemption 5 to conceal "secret law" — final legal interpretations controlling executive action that are never made public — enables the administration to make consequential legal arguments without public scrutiny or democratic accountability.

Congress recognized this problem explicitly when passing the 2016 FOIA Improvement Act, which added a "foreseeability" requirement: agencies must show that disclosure would foreseeably harm the interests protected by the exemption, not merely that the exemption could conceivably apply. The Act also placed a 25-year sunset on deliberative process privilege claims, so that documents 25 years or older cannot be withheld on that basis. But these constraints depend on good-faith application by an administration committed to transparency. When the administration's policy is to maximize withholding, foreseeability becomes a formality rather than a genuine barrier.

The ACLU's analysis of OLC opinions secrecy describes the practical consequence: the Trump administration's "dangerously expansive views on the president's powers" — the legal theories that have driven executive action on immigration enforcement, spending authority, war powers, and agency restructuring — are not publicly available. Citizens, courts, and Congress are asked to evaluate the legality of executive action based on arguments the administration will not disclose.

## 2. Classification System Abuse: Overclassification, NARA Capture, and the Secrecy Infrastructure

### 2.1 The Scale of the Overclassification Problem

The federal classification system was designed to protect genuinely sensitive national security information from disclosure. What it has become is a $18 billion-per-year administrative apparatus that classifies, by expert estimates, 75 to 90 percent of its volume incorrectly.

The Information Security Oversight Office — the office within NARA responsible for overseeing the classification system — published its FY 2024 Annual Report to the President in September 2025. ISOO has documented that it stopped reporting government-wide classification decision counts because the data it received from agencies "was so poor and inconsistent" that publishing it would be "unreliable." The classification system's own oversight office cannot measure what it oversees.

What can be measured is scale and cost. Estimates suggest 50 million new classified records are created every year. The annual cost of administering the classification system — security clearances, secure facilities, document management, classification review — is approximately $18 billion. If 75 to 90 percent of classified material does not meet classification standards, the government is spending approximately $13.5 to $16 billion annually to maintain secrecy that does not serve any legitimate security purpose.

Overclassification functions as an information suppression mechanism with particular effectiveness precisely because it has legal authority behind it. FOIA Exemption 1 — which covers properly classified information — is an absolute bar to disclosure when properly invoked. An overclassified document cannot be obtained through FOIA, cannot be subpoenaed by Congress without a classified hearing, and cannot be analyzed by the press. Overclassification is therefore not merely waste; it is a structural extension of the executive's information monopoly.

The Federation of American Scientists' analysis of classification costs identifies the perverse incentive structure: classification is cheap for the classifier (no individual bureaucrat pays the $18 billion tab) and functionally free as a liability shield (classified information cannot be disclosed in litigation without going through the classification review system). The incentive is always to classify more, not less. Without external enforcement mechanisms that create costs for overclassification, the volume expands without check.

### 2.2 Declassification Politics: The NARA Capture

The National Archives and Records Administration occupies a unique constitutional position: it is the institutional guardian of the federal government's documentary record, the repository of executive and legislative documents, the overseer of presidential libraries, and the home of ISOO — the office that regulates the classification system. For NARA to perform these functions with integrity, its independence from political influence is not merely preferable; it is structurally required.

The Archivist of the United States statute specifically provides that the archivist "shall be appointed without regard to political affiliations and solely on the basis of the professional qualifications required to perform the duties and responsibilities of the office." The statute requires the president to notify Congress of the reasons for removal. These provisions exist precisely because every administration has an incentive to control, suppress, or destroy records of its own conduct.

On February 19, 2025, President Trump fired Colleen Shogan — a professional archivist with the requisite qualifications — without stating a cause, in apparent violation of the removal notification requirement. The firing was the first removal of a sitting Archivist in nearly a century. Deputy Archivist William J. Bosanko, a career civil servant who by law was entitled to assume the acting archivist position, was reportedly given a choice to resign or be fired by Jim Byron, a newly-appointed political appointee designated as "Senior Archivist."

Secretary of State Marco Rubio was then designated as Acting Archivist — a choice that was not publicly announced but discovered when Rubio's photograph appeared on NARA's website. By March 2026, the Boston Globe was reporting that Senator Ron Wyden had written to both Rubio and the administration questioning whether Rubio had violated federal law by overstaying his designated acting-archivist term beyond the period permitted by the Federal Vacancies Reform Act. A Substack analysis by historian Paul Musgrave characterized the situation as a "hostile takeover of the Archives" — not hyperbole, but an accurate description of placing the nation's records custodian under the authority of a political official who simultaneously serves as a senior member of the administration whose records NARA is supposed to preserve independently.

The budget dimension compounds the structural problem. The administration's FY2026 budget request set NARA's budget at $414.7 million — nearly $60 million less than projected FY2025 spending and $93 million less than FY2024 — while proposing elimination of 136 full-time employees. NARA's budget request eliminates the Office of Innovation (50 staff managing the agency's digital access and online catalog), zeros out funding for the Electronic Records Initiative (the system for receiving and preserving the billions of electronic records created by federal agencies), and eliminates all funding for the National Historical Publications and Records Commission (the grant program that funds state and local archives).

The Society of American Archivists, responding to these cuts, issued a formal statement underscoring "the critical importance of strong, independent, and non-partisan federal archives and records administration." Former NARA director of litigation Jason R. Baron stated that "NARA leadership's submission of a reduced budget for their own agency is difficult to fathom, given the enormous resource challenges NARA currently faces" — including FOIA backlogs at presidential libraries and the challenge of preserving billions of electronic records.

The practical consequence: NARA under political control, with a gutted staff and eliminated innovation capacity, cannot function as the independent institutional guardian that validates the integrity of the government's documentary record. A government that controls its own archivist controls its own history.

### 2.3 DOGE Records: The Presidential Records Act Gambit

The DOGE records dispute illustrates how the architecture of records law can be weaponized to achieve structural opacity. DOGE began operating under OMB — where it was subject to the Federal Records Act (FRA) — but was subsequently moved to the Executive Office of the President. The administration then claimed that only the Presidential Records Act (PRA) applies to DOGE records.

The distinction is consequential. Under the FRA, federal records are government property, must be preserved according to mandatory schedules, and are accessible through FOIA following appropriate review periods. Under the PRA, a president has wide discretion over the disposal of "personal" records, and the public has no right of access until five years after the president leaves office — with the incumbent president (Trump's successor in 2029) having a say in how those records are handled before public release. Under the PRA claim, the earliest DOGE records might be accessible to the public is 2034.

DOGE members used Signal, Slack, and other commercial messaging platforms for official communications — likely destroying federal records under both the FRA and the PRA, depending on which regime applies. American Oversight's lawsuit eventually prompted DOGE to implement a records retention policy requiring employees to capture work-related messages from personal devices onto official devices. But the messages sent before that court-ordered policy change are gone.

U.S. District Judge Christopher Cooper concluded in 2025 that DOGE "is likely exercising substantial independent authority much greater than" typical EOP components, making it subject to FOIA. That ruling — which was appealed — demonstrates the stakes: courts are being asked to determine whether the most consequential restructuring operation in federal government history has any public accountability obligations at all. The administration's position is that it does not.

## 3. Investigative Capacity: Congressional Authority, Inspector General Independence, and Whistleblower Protections

### 3.1 Congressional Investigative Authority and the Information Gap

Phase 3 Candidate 5 (Legislative Branch Capacity) documents the structural collapse of Congress's institutional capacity to conduct oversight in detail. This section focuses on the specific information dimension of that collapse: the systematic obstruction of congressional access to executive branch records, and the structural gap created when Congressional requests go unfulfilled without legal consequence.

Congress's investigative authority derives from Article I's "legislative Powers" clause — an implied power to inquire into matters relevant to legislating. The authority is broad: Congress can issue subpoenas for documents and testimony, hold hearings, and refer non-compliant witnesses for contempt. But the enforcement mechanism has a fatal structural dependency: criminal contempt requires DOJ prosecution, and civil enforcement requires either DOJ cooperation or a standing mechanism that routes around it.

The documented 2025 pattern:

Members of Congress were denied physical access to ICE immigration detention facilities — an access that members use to conduct oversight of conditions, procedures, and constitutional compliance. The access denial prompted litigation, which was eventually resolved against the administration. But the litigation took months; oversight was not occurring during that period.

DOGE refused to comply with FOIA requests and congressional document demands, claiming not to be an "agency" subject to disclosure obligations. One district court rejected this claim, but the appeal consumed time and resources while DOGE continued to operate without accountability.

The House Ways and Means Committee voted down Democratic resolutions seeking documents about DOGE's access to Treasury and Social Security Administration IT systems — meaning the majority used its committee control to prevent the minority from even requesting oversight information.

The administration withheld apportionment data from GAO investigators reviewing Impoundment Control Act compliance — the specific data that would have confirmed whether the administration was illegally withholding appropriated funds. Without that data, GAO cannot complete its investigation. Without a completed investigation, there is no legal referral. Without a legal referral, the withheld funds stay withheld.

Over 1,000 FOIA lawsuits in 15 months represent a systemic breakdown in the administrative relationship between the government and the public it is supposed to serve. At the congressional level, the minority filed resolutions of inquiry, demanded document production, and requested testimony — actions that the majority blocked, delayed, or converted into legally unresolvable situations through the "single legislative day" manipulation documented in Candidate 5.

### 3.2 The Inspector General System: Personnel, Budget, and Retaliation

Domain 26 (Government Accountability and Institutional Resilience) documents the mass IG firing of January 24, 2025 — at least 17 IGs terminated simultaneously by email, in violation of the Inspector General Act's 30-day notice requirement, in what Judge Ana C. Reyes declared unlawful. This domain focuses on the follow-on effects on the information infrastructure those IGs represented.

Inspector generals are not primarily punishment mechanisms. They are the federal government's internal audit and investigation infrastructure — the bodies that produce the reports, data audits, and program reviews that give Congress, the press, and the public meaningful information about what agencies are actually doing rather than what agencies report they are doing. When IGs are eliminated, the information stops flowing.

The retaliation data is now specific and alarming. At the Department of Energy, the Inspector General's office opened 45 investigations into whistleblower retaliation allegations in FY 2025 — compared to 5 investigations in the previous fiscal year. This 900 percent increase is not a statistical anomaly. It reflects a systematic pattern in which employees who disclosed wrongdoing through official channels were then subjected to adverse employment action. The IG office that was supposed to investigate that retaliation was itself operating with a fired director and diminished capacity.

The administration's move toward a rule eliminating whistleblower protections for senior federal employees — a class that includes the most senior career officials most likely to have information about high-level wrongdoing — represents a structural endpoint: a federal government in which the officials with the most information about executive branch conduct have the least legal protection for disclosing it.

CIGIE — the Council of the Inspectors General on Integrity and Efficiency, the umbrella body that provides coordination, training, and infrastructure support for IG offices — was illegally defunded, temporarily shutting down whistleblower reporting portals. Republican Senators Chuck Grassley and Susan Collins warned that "important functions that help prevent and detect waste, fraud, and abuse throughout the government" were being terminated, and ultimately succeeded in pressuring the administration to restore funding. The episode illustrates both the vulnerability of oversight infrastructure (a single budget decision can shut down a government-wide whistleblower system) and the conditions under which internal Republican pressure provides partial constraint.

USAID's Inspector General was fired specifically after his office released a report warning that administration actions had "significantly impacted USAID's capacity to disburse and safeguard its humanitarian assistance programming." This is the accountability mechanism working precisely as designed — an IG report contradicting administration claims — and the administration's response was to fire the IG. The message to remaining IGs and their staff about the consequences of candid reporting is structural.

### 3.3 Whistleblower Protections: The Last Line and Its Erosion

Whistleblowers represent the information channel of last resort: when FOIA is blocked, when congressional requests are denied, when IG offices are captured or eliminated, individual employees who witness wrongdoing remain as potential information sources. The legal protection for those employees — principally the Whistleblower Protection Act (federal employees), the False Claims Act (government contractors), and Dodd-Frank (financial sector) — determines whether that last channel functions.

The WPA covers most federal employees and prohibits adverse employment action for disclosures of violations of law, gross mismanagement, gross waste of funds, abuse of authority, or substantial danger to public health or safety. The protections route through the Office of Special Counsel for prohibited personnel practice complaints and through the Merit Systems Protection Board for appeals. Domain 26 documents the MSPB's structural compromise; this domain focuses on the WPA's specific information function.

The Trump administration's proposed rule eliminating WPA coverage for senior career employees — those in Senior Executive Service and other senior positions — would remove protections from exactly the employees with access to high-level information about executive decision-making. The practical effect would be to create an information quarantine at the most consequential levels of government: senior officials would have no legal protection for disclosing wrongdoing, while the IG infrastructure that is supposed to receive and act on those disclosures is simultaneously being dismantled.

The Energy Department's 900 percent increase in whistleblower retaliation investigations reflects the actual behavioral response to this environment: employees who attempted to use the disclosure infrastructure faced retaliation at historically unprecedented rates. The Senate response — Senator Grassley's bipartisan legislation (S. 4100 and S. 4099) to expand WPA coverage to investigative civil servants and require anti-gag notice in nondisclosure agreements — represents a legislative effort to close specific gaps rather than a comprehensive structural response.

The False Claims Act data provides a counterpoint worth noting: DOJ reported a record $6.8 billion in FCA settlements and judgments in FY 2025, with 1,297 qui tam (whistleblower-initiated) actions filed. The FCA's financial incentive structure — whistleblowers receive a percentage of recovered funds — has proven more durable than the WPA's administrative protection model. This data point has structural implications for reform design: whistleblower protection mechanisms that create financial incentives are more resilient to administrative suppression than mechanisms that depend solely on adverse-employment-action prohibitions.

## 4. International Precedent: What Functional Transparency Infrastructure Looks Like

### 4.1 EU Regulation 1049/2001: Transparency as the Default

The European Union's access to documents framework — governed primarily by Regulation 1049/2001 regarding access to Parliament, Council, and Commission documents — operates from a foundational principle that contrasts sharply with the US regime's current operation: transparency is the rule, and secrecy is the exception.

Under EU Regulation 1049/2001, any citizen or resident of an EU member state can request documents from the European Parliament, the Council, or the European Commission in any of the 24 official EU languages, and the institution must respond within 15 working days. When exceptions apply, the institution is not merely permitted to withhold — it is obligated to provide partial access, blanking out the sensitive portions, rather than withholding the document in full. The burden of justifying each specific withholding is on the institution, and the justification must be specific, not generic.

This "partial access" requirement addresses one of FOIA's specific failure modes: agencies routinely invoke exemptions to withhold entire documents when only portions contain potentially exempt information, and the burden of challenging each specific withholding falls on requesters who cannot see what is being withheld. The EU's partial disclosure obligation requires agencies to do the work of separating exempt from non-exempt content rather than imposing that burden through litigation.

The Aarhus Directive (Directive 2003/4/EC) provides an additional layer for environmental information specifically — including access rights that run against private entities holding environmental information, not merely public bodies. This extension is relevant to the EPA context in which pollution exemption records have been withheld through FOIA Exemption 5, and in which environmental agency FOIA staff have been cut while environmental enforcement records are most consequential.

Structural lesson for US reform: mandatory partial disclosure with specific written justification for each withheld element would close the blanket-exemption loophole that currently allows agencies to withhold entire documents when only specific portions require protection.

### 4.2 Canada's Access to Information Act: Independent Commissioner and Mandatory Review

Canada's Access to Information Act, enacted in 1983 and significantly reformed in 2019 through Bill C-58, provides a model for information rights that includes one structural element absent from FOIA: an independent Information Commissioner with the authority to order disclosure.

The Canadian Information Commissioner is an Officer of Parliament — not an executive branch officer — appointed by Parliament with a mandate to investigate complaints about access denials and, following the 2019 reforms, to issue binding orders directing agencies to release records. Prior to 2019, the Commissioner could only recommend disclosure; agencies could ignore the recommendation and force requesters into court. The binding order authority converts the Commissioner from an advisory body into an enforcement mechanism.

The 2025 review of the ATIA launched by the Treasury Board of Canada Secretariat focuses specifically on bringing the Act "up to the needs of Canadians and Indigenous peoples in the digital age" — including proposals for time-based presumptive declassification of older records. Canada's ATI practice has been evaluated as superior to US FOIA practice in key respects, including more consistent application of the public interest override that allows disclosure of information that would otherwise be exempt when the public interest in disclosure outweighs the interest in protection.

The 2023-24 annual report data shows that Canada received approximately 100,000 ATI requests in that period, with varying performance across departments. The Information Commissioner's binding order authority has been exercised in cases involving national security information, cabinet confidences, and law enforcement records — the categories where US FOIA is most often completely blocked.

Structural lesson: an independent ombudsperson with binding order authority — not merely advisory power — transforms access requests from proceedings that can be ignored (if litigation is too expensive) into enforceable rights. The US FOIA system's dependence on litigation as its primary enforcement mechanism disadvantages exactly the requesters (small news organizations, individual researchers, civil society groups) who most need access.

### 4.3 UK Freedom of Information Act 2000: Volume, Compliance Data, and the Section 35 Problem

The UK's Freedom of Information Act 2000 entered full force in 2005 and has generated a substantial empirical record of operational performance. The Q3 2025 statistics bulletin provides a current compliance baseline: there were 24,216 FOI requests received across all monitored bodies — the largest quarterly volume since records began. From April to June 2025, 23,547 requests were received — a 15 percent increase over the same quarter of 2024. Crucially, 90 percent of requests were responded to in time (within the 20-working-day statutory deadline) in Q3 2025, up from 75 percent in Q3 2024.

The UK's compliance rate — 90 percent in-time responses — provides a specific benchmark against which US federal performance can be evaluated. US simple FOIA requests averaged 44 days for a response in the most recent data, compared to the 20-working-day (approximately 28-calendar-day) UK standard. US agencies consistently miss the 20-day statutory response deadline, with litigation providing the primary consequence for failure.

The UK model is not without structural problems. Section 35 of the FOIA 2000 exempts "information relating to the formulation or development of government policy" — a broad exemption that functions similarly to FOIA's Exemption 5 deliberative process privilege and has been used by UK governments to withhold Cabinet records and ministerial advice. The Information Commissioner's Office (ICO) provides independent review of withholding decisions, with appeals available to the First-tier Tribunal and Upper Tribunal.

The comparison also reveals a specific UK vulnerability: the "neither confirm nor deny" (NCND) response, which allows agencies to avoid even acknowledging whether they hold responsive records, has been invoked in national security contexts in ways that make it impossible for requesters to know whether information exists. The US Glomar response serves a similar function; both represent a category of structural opacity that access to information reforms should address.

Structural lesson: statutory response deadlines with meaningful enforcement consequences (automatic fee waivers for late responses is one mechanism; litigation costs shifting is another) create behavioral incentives for compliance that do not depend on administrative culture or political commitment to transparency.

### 4.4 Global Context: Right to Information as a Democratic Indicator

The comparative democracy literature provides structural context for the information access crisis. A 2023 study in Democratization — "Information, doubt, and democracy: how digitization spurs democratic decay" — documents how the information environment shapes democratic quality: access to accurate government information supports accountability, while information opacity enables the kind of "democratic decay" visible in Hungary, Turkey, and Venezuela alongside US patterns.

The 1997 Moynihan Commission Report on government secrecy — chaired by Senator Daniel Patrick Moynihan — remains the definitive statement of the problem: "Excessive secrecy has significant consequences for the national interest when it obscures history, distorts current intelligence assessments, and impedes effective checks on the power of the executive branch." The Commission's core finding was that overclassification created a culture of secrecy that persisted even when it served no genuine national security purpose.

UNCAC Coalition analysis frames information access as a prerequisite for anti-corruption accountability: without access to government information, civil society cannot verify whether public procurement is legitimate, whether regulatory decisions reflect public interest or private capture, or whether enforcement is applied equally. This framing is directly relevant to the current US context: DOGE's restructuring of federal contracts and personnel, conducted without public disclosure, cannot be evaluated for corruption risk without records access.

## 5. Recovery Pathways: FOIA Reform, Classification Limits, Whistleblower Expansion, and NARA Independence

The recovery pathways below distinguish between reforms that can function under a reform-committed administration (administrative implementation reforms), reforms that require legislation (statutory reforms), and reforms that require structural institutional redesign (constitutional or quasi-constitutional reforms). The distinction matters because some current failures stem from bad administrative choices that could be reversed immediately; others are structural vulnerabilities that will recur under any administration without statutory protection.

### 5.1 FOIA Statutory Reform: The Transparency Enforcement Act (0-24 months)

**Problem**: FOIA's enforcement mechanism is litigation, which creates delays measured in years, costs measured in tens of thousands of dollars, and access outcomes that are beyond the means of small news organizations, researchers, and individual requesters. The presumption of openness created by the 2016 FOIA Improvement Act has no teeth when agencies can simply delay indefinitely.

**Proposed mechanism**: A Transparency Enforcement Act would:

- **Mandatory response fee waiver for late responses**: Any FOIA response that exceeds the 20-working-day statutory deadline would automatically trigger a waiver of all duplication and search fees for the request — converting the statutory deadline from an aspiration into an agency cost item. Currently, agencies routinely miss deadlines without consequence while charging requesters for delayed processing.

- **FOIA ombudsperson with binding order authority**: Establish an independent FOIA Ombudsperson as an Officer of Congress (not an executive branch officer), appointed by bipartisan leadership of both chambers, with authority to investigate complaints about FOIA delays and denials and to issue binding orders directing disclosure, subject to appeal to the D.C. Circuit. Modeled on the Canadian Information Commissioner's post-2019 authority, this mechanism would provide a non-litigation path for requester remedies.

- **Partial disclosure mandate**: Codify a requirement that agencies provide partial access to documents when only portions are exempt — segregating exempt from non-exempt content within individual documents rather than withholding entire documents when any portion is claimed as exempt. The EU Regulation 1049/2001 model.

- **Minimum staffing floor for FOIA offices**: Establish statutory minimum FOIA processing staff levels for agencies receiving more than 10,000 requests annually, calibrated to the volume-to-staff ratio necessary to maintain 20-day compliance. Prohibit FOIA staff reductions that would bring staffing below the minimum without concurrent appropriations for equivalent processing technology.

- **Exemption 5 abuse limitation**: Require that any invocation of Exemption 5's deliberative process privilege for records more than five years old include a specific, written, non-conclusory explanation of the foreseeable harm that disclosure would cause — with that explanation itself disclosable — converting blanket privilege invocations into specific, challengeable justifications.

**Legislative vehicle**: The bipartisan lineage is strong. The 2016 FOIA Improvement Act passed with unanimous Senate consent; the OPEN Government Act of 2007 was signed by President Bush. The enforcement gap has been a recurring criticism across partisan lines. The litigation-surge data (1,000 FOIA lawsuits in 15 months) provides a bipartisan efficiency argument: courts are being burdened by cases that an ombudsperson with binding order authority would resolve administratively.

### 5.2 NARA Independence Act: Statutory Insulation of the National Archives (0-18 months)

**Problem**: The NARA archivist position lacks effective tenure protections, enabling presidential removal for political reasons without the statutory constraints that protect other independent officers. A Secretary of State serving as Acting Archivist represents a structural conflict of interest: the Acting Archivist oversees preservation of records from the department the Acting Archivist leads.

**Proposed mechanism**: A NARA Independence Act would:

- **Tenure protection**: Provide that the Archivist of the United States may be removed only for cause, with cause defined as neglect of duty, malfeasance in office, or conduct unbecoming the position — not policy disagreement with the incumbent administration. Require Senate confirmation of any cause determination by a two-thirds majority, modeled on the Comptroller General's protections.

- **Acting archivist restriction**: Prohibit any current Cabinet officer, presidential appointee, or officer who serves at the pleasure of the president from serving as Acting Archivist, ensuring that the acting position passes to the senior career Deputy Archivist as provided in existing statute — and that political appointees cannot be inserted to run the Archives.

- **Budget floor**: Establish a mandatory minimum appropriation for NARA at its FY2024 enacted level, adjusted for inflation annually, protecting the agency's baseline operating capacity from budget retaliation while records management demands are increasing rather than decreasing.

- **Records management independence**: Codify NARA's authority to determine the records management schedules for all federal agencies, including DOGE and the Executive Office of the President components, with a specific statement that the Presidential Records Act exemption does not apply to units exercising substantial independent authority over federal programs — closing the DOGE records gambit.

**Evidence base**: Every president since Nixon has faced some form of records dispute. The legislative precedent for independent officer protections (Comptroller General, FEC members, MSPB members) demonstrates constitutional viability. The Senate's 2025 rejection of the administration's position that it was not required to state reasons for the Shogan firing suggests bipartisan support for at least the removal-cause requirement.

### 5.3 Classification Reform for Transparency Act: Mandatory Declassification Triggers (12-48 months)

**Problem**: The classification system has no self-correcting mechanism. Overclassification creates no cost for classifiers, no benefit for declassification, and no external check on the expansion of classified volume. The PIDB has made recommendations that have been ignored; ISOO stopped publishing classification statistics because the data was too unreliable; bipartisan Senate reform legislation (the Classification Reform for Transparency Act of 2024, introduced by Peters and Cornyn; the Warner-Cornyn-Wyden-Moran bill) has not been enacted.

**Proposed mechanism**: A Classification Reform for Transparency Act (building on the bipartisan bills already introduced) would:

- **Mandatory declassification review trigger**: Any document classified for more than 25 years must undergo mandatory declassification review — not merely be eligible for review — within 18 months of reaching that threshold, with automatic declassification if review is not completed on time and no specific harm finding documented.

- **Classification cost accountability**: Require ISOO to publish annually the specific cost per document of maintaining each classification level (Confidential, Secret, Top Secret), the number of documents at each level, and the percentage reviewed for downgrade in the prior year. Publishing this data creates public accountability for the $18 billion annual cost.

- **Overcalssification penalty**: Implement a process by which an ISOO review finding that a document was overclassified results in mandatory training for the classifying officer and a note in that officer's performance record. The current system has no individual accountability for overclassification; penalty mechanisms create the asymmetry currently missing.

- **Expanded PIDB authority**: Give the Public Interest Declassification Board binding recommendation authority — the ability to order declassification review rather than merely recommend it — and require the administration to either accept PIDB recommendations or publish specific, document-by-document harm findings explaining why each challenged classification is maintained.

- **Congressional notification for classification extensions**: Require that any extension of a classification beyond 25 years be reported to the intelligence committee chairs with specific harm findings, converting classification extension from a bureaucratic decision into a reported and reviewable one.

**Political economy**: The most powerful argument for classification reform cuts across partisan lines: overclassification conceals government failures and prevents the public from evaluating government competence. 9/11 Commission members, Biden administration officials, and Republican senators (Cornyn, Moran) have all endorsed classification reform. The $18 billion annual cost of the current system — with 75 to 90 percent of classified material not meeting standards — is a fiscal waste argument that reaches fiscal conservatives.

### 5.4 Whistleblower Comprehensive Protection Act (0-36 months)

**Problem**: The federal whistleblower protection system has multiple structural gaps: (1) senior employees face proposed elimination of protections; (2) national security whistleblowers have weaker statutory protections than other federal employees; (3) the enforcement mechanism (OSC + MSPB) is slow, underfunded, and subject to political influence; (4) private-sector contractors working on federal programs have weaker protections than direct federal employees doing the same work.

**Proposed mechanism**: A Whistleblower Comprehensive Protection Act would:

- **Universal coverage**: Extend WPA coverage to all employees exercising significant authority over federal programs, including Senior Executive Service employees (whose protections the administration proposed to eliminate), Schedule C political appointees who make disclosures that would be protected for career employees, and employees of private contractors, grantees, and state agencies implementing federal programs.

- **Financial incentive structure**: Model a federal government-wide whistleblower financial incentive on the False Claims Act qui tam structure — a percentage recovery for disclosures that result in documented savings, fraud recovery, or waste elimination. The FCA record ($6.8 billion in FY2025) demonstrates that financial incentives are more durable than administrative-protection models against politically-motivated retaliation.

- **Independent enforcement**: Create an independent Whistleblower Protection Court — a specialized Article III court with jurisdiction over all WPA matters — removing whistleblower enforcement from the MSPB (subject to political compromise) and DOJ (which has a direct conflict when the protected disclosure concerns DOJ conduct). Modeled on the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court's specialized jurisdiction but with full adversarial proceedings and public reporting on outcomes.

- **Anti-gag provision universalization**: Require that all federal employment agreements, nondisclosure agreements, and security clearance agreements include a notice that whistleblower disclosures to Congress, IGs, and OSC are protected regardless of any other confidentiality provision — closing the gap documented by the administration's use of NDAs that omit this notice.

**Legislative vehicle**: Senator Grassley's bipartisan bills (S. 4099 and S. 4100, introduced in 2025-2026) provide a starting point. The FCA track record provides the evidence base for the financial incentive model. The Expanding Whistleblower Protections for Contractors Act of 2025 (S. 874) addresses the contractor gap. These bills can be packaged into a comprehensive act rather than pursued separately.

### 5.5 Congressional Records Access Act: Routing Around DOJ (12-36 months)

**Problem**: Congressional requests for executive branch records depend on executive compliance or DOJ enforcement — neither of which is available when the executive is the subject of the investigation and DOJ has been captured. The information asymmetry between a Congress that has oversight authority and an executive that controls the records being sought is the structural gap that enables blanket non-compliance.

**Proposed mechanism**: A Congressional Records Access Act would:

- **Direct congressional standing**: Grant the House Minority Leader, the Senate Minority Leader, and chairs of committees with oversight jurisdiction standing to bring civil enforcement actions in the D.C. Circuit to compel compliance with validly issued document requests and subpoenas — without requiring DOJ involvement. This routes enforcement through Article III courts rather than through the executive branch.

- **Automatic disclosure trigger**: Provide that when an executive agency fails to produce documents responsive to a validly issued committee request within 60 days, and the committee vote (by any majority, majority or minority leader request, or automatic triggering) ratifies the request, the agency's failure triggers automatic discovery in the D.C. Circuit proceeding, allowing the court to order production directly.

- **Access to facilities**: Codify a statutory right of members and staff conducting committee-authorized oversight to access federal facilities, with a cause of action for denial that routes through the same Article III standing mechanism — closing the ICE facility access dispute as a structural matter.

- **DOGE and EOP transparency**: Provide statutory clarification that any component of the Executive Office of the President that (a) exercises authority over federal programs, (b) makes binding recommendations affecting federal employees or spending, or (c) accesses agency databases or personnel systems is subject to FOIA and the Federal Records Act regardless of Presidential Records Act claims — reversing the administration's DOGE records gambit by statute.

**Post-Loper Bright advantage**: Courts applying de novo review to specific statutory mandates governing congressional access are more likely to enforce precise statutory language than to defer to executive characterizations. Drafting the access mandate with specificity — naming the types of documents, the timeframes, and the civil remedy — maximizes the probability of judicial enforcement.

## 6. Implementation Sequencing and the Democratic Recovery Logic

### 6.1 The Information-Access-First Argument

This domain's most important structural argument is a sequencing claim: information access reforms must precede, not accompany, the other reforms in this framework.

The logic is direct. Every other domain in this 38-domain framework depends on accurate information about what the government is doing:

- Domain 29 (DOJ Capture) cannot be litigated without FOIA records establishing which prosecutions were politically directed.
- Domain 34 (Congressional Power-of-the-Purse) cannot be enforced without GAO access to apportionment data.
- Domain 26 (Government Accountability) cannot function without IG offices that can actually investigate and report.
- Domain 28 (War Powers) cannot be exercised by Congress if OLC memos authorizing operations are classified as deliberative process.
- Domain 37 (Federal Interference in 2026 Midterms) cannot be documented without FOIA access to election interference planning records.
- Domain 27 (Academic Freedom) cannot be defended if the classification of federal research funding decisions prevents any investigation of political interference in grant decisions.

Without information, the governed cannot evaluate whether their government is complying with the law. Without evaluation, accountability is impossible. Without accountability, the other domains degrade whether or not their specific reforms are enacted.

This is why the administration's simultaneous attack on all three information channels — FOIA, classification, and whistleblower protections — is structurally coherent rather than coincidental. Information access is the keystone. Pull it out and the other accountability arches begin to collapse.

### 6.2 The Window for Immediate Administrative Action

Several of the most serious information access failures require only administrative action to begin reversing — they do not require legislation. A reform-committed administration could, within its first 100 days:

- Reinstate the FOIA policy guidance removed by the Trump administration, explicitly restoring the presumption of openness and the foreseeability standard.
- Restore the CDC's FOIA office and the HHS agency FOIA staff using existing hiring authority.
- Appoint an appropriately qualified archivist and reverse the political conversion of NARA leadership.
- Issue an executive order directing all EOP components, including DOGE equivalents, to comply with FOIA and FRA requirements.
- Restore CIGIE funding and appoint qualified IG candidates to the 75 percent of positions currently vacant.
- Direct the OLC to publish, within a defined period, all OLC opinions more than five years old that are currently withheld under Exemption 5's deliberative process privilege.

These administrative reversals are valuable as immediate actions precisely because they require only political will. Their limitation is that they can be reversed by the next administration without legislation. The statutory reforms (FOIA Enforcement Act, NARA Independence Act, Classification Reform for Transparency Act, Whistleblower Comprehensive Protection Act) are required to make the administrative reversals durable.

### 6.3 The Sequencing of Structural Reforms

**Immediate window (0-18 months)**: NARA Independence Act is the highest constitutional priority — it addresses a violation of existing statute (the removal notice requirement and the qualification requirement were both violated) that has an active factual record. The bipartisan case is strongest where the administration's conduct was most clearly unlawful. CIGIE funding restoration and IG appointment can be moved through appropriations as riders.

**Medium term (12-36 months)**: FOIA Transparency Enforcement Act requires new institutional design (the ombudsperson) and new cost structures (mandatory fee waivers for late responses). These require legislative negotiation but have strong bipartisan foundations. The Classification Reform for Transparency Act builds on bipartisan legislation already introduced; the Peters-Cornyn-Warner-Wyden-Moran coalition demonstrates the political base exists.

**Structural reforms (24-60 months)**: The Whistleblower Comprehensive Protection Act and Congressional Records Access Act require more significant institutional redesign. The Whistleblower Court requires Article III funding and jurisdiction legislation. The congressional standing mechanism requires resolution of the Raines v. Byrd standing problem through careful legislative drafting of institutional (rather than individual member) standing.

## 7. Cross-Domain Links

- **Domain 5 (Administrative Law and Regulatory Capture)**: FOIA exemption abuse is the administrative mechanism by which regulatory capture is concealed. Exemption 5 shields agency decision-making from the public scrutiny that would reveal whether regulatory decisions reflect public interest or regulated industry preferences. FOIA reform is a necessary complement to regulatory capture prevention.

- **Domain 16 (Transparency and Accountability)**: This domain deepens Phase 2 Domain 16's analysis of the accountability infrastructure, focusing specifically on the information-access mechanisms (FOIA, classification, whistleblower protection) rather than the post-disclosure accountability mechanisms (IGs, oversight investigations). The two domains together describe the full transparency-accountability pipeline.

- **Domain 26 (Government Accountability and Institutional Resilience)**: Domain 26 documents the IG system's dismantlement. This domain frames that dismantlement as an information access failure — IGs produce the audit reports and investigation findings that are the primary source of accountability information about executive branch conduct. IG independence and FOIA access are complementary structural requirements.

- **Domain 29 (DOJ Capture and Prosecutorial Weaponization)**: The information access crisis has a DOJ-specific dimension: DOJ controls FOIA litigation, houses the Office of Information Policy that sets government-wide FOIA policy, and prosecutes criminal contempt for congressional obstruction. A captured DOJ converts all three information channels — FOIA, IG investigations, and congressional subpoenas — from enforcement mechanisms into procedural theater.

- **Phase 3 Candidate 5 (Legislative Branch Capacity)**: These two Phase 3 candidates form a structural pair. Candidate 5 documents Congress's loss of institutional capacity to exercise its oversight authority. Candidate 6 (this domain) documents the executive branch's systematic elimination of the information flows that make oversight meaningful. Together, they describe a complete accountability collapse: Congress cannot get the information (this domain), and Congress lacks the institutional capacity to act on information even when it gets it (Candidate 5). Both structural failures must be addressed together.

- **Domain 34 (Congressional Power-of-the-Purse)**: GAO's capacity to enforce the Impoundment Control Act depends on access to executive branch apportionment data. The administration's withholding of that data from GAO investigators — documented in the Legislative Branch Capacity domain and in Domain 34 — is a direct application of information access suppression to undermine fiscal oversight. FOIA reform alone does not address this gap; the Congressional Records Access Act's direct-access mechanism is the complementary fix.

- **Domain 35 (Supreme Court / Post-Loper Landscape)**: Post-Loper Bright's de novo review standard creates a specific advantage for precisely drafted information-access statutes. Courts are more likely to enforce specific statutory mandates for document production, FOIA processing timelines, and NARA independence than to defer to executive characterizations of their own obligations. The reform drafting strategy should exploit this doctrinal shift.
