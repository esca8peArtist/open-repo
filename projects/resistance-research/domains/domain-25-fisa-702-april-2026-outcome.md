# Domain 25: FISA Section 702 — April 2026 Deadline Outcome

*Written: April 29, 2026 (Session 652)*
*Status: Pre-vote confirmed / post-vote pending Senate action*
*Research integrity note: An earlier session (Session 573) erroneously wrote a "confirmed" 2026 outcome that was actually the 2024 RISAA (60-34 Senate, two-year extension). Session 608 corrected the record. This document reflects the actual April 2026 legislative trajectory based on confirmed reporting through April 29, 2026.*

## Summary Finding (Lead)

After three consecutive legislative failures across April 15-17, 2026, the House appears positioned to pass a three-year warrantless renewal of FISA Section 702 authority (S. 1318, the Foreign Intelligence Accountability Act) on or before April 30. The procedural rule for floor debate passed 215-210 on April 29 — a vote that required the Speaker to hold the chamber open for more than two hours while flipping Republican holdouts. Leadership would not advance a procedural rule unless final passage was secured. The three-year renewal contains no warrant requirement for U.S. person queries, leaves the commercial data broker loophole intact, and attaches a Central Bank Digital Currency (CBDC) ban as a sweetener to break Freedom Caucus opposition.

If the House passes S. 1318, Section 702 authority will not expire at midnight April 30. Senate passage is still required — but Senate Majority Leader Thune had filed cloture for a companion clean three-year extension, signaling Senate leadership alignment. Presidential signature is expected: Trump had demanded an 18-month or longer extension and dropped his SAVE Act attachment condition before the floor vote.

The civil liberties community's position is that this outcome — three-year warrantless renewal — represents a structural defeat. The next reform window will not open until April 2029, beyond the 2026 and 2028 election cycles. EFF, CDT, and ACLU have indicated they will pursue judicial challenges to the warrantless U.S. person query authority regardless of the legislative outcome.

## Section 1: Legislative History — The Four Attempts

### Attempt 1: Five-Year Clean Reauthorization (April 15-17)
Speaker Johnson's primary proposal — a five-year renewal with minimal reforms — failed when 20 House Republicans joined most Democrats. The bipartisan opposition bloc was cross-ideological: Freedom Caucus members opposing on Fourth Amendment grounds (FISA was used against Trump associates in 2016-2017, providing political cover) and moderates seeking constituent accountability for a program with documented abuse history. Roll call: approximately 197-228 against.

### Attempt 2: 18-Month Trump-Preferred Extension (April 17)
Failed the same session. Attracted no additional Democratic votes and insufficient Republican support to overcome the civil liberties bloc. Roll call: also failed.

### Attempt 3: 10-Day Stopgap (April 17-18)
Congress passed a 10-day emergency extension through April 30 by unanimous consent in the House and voice vote in the Senate. Trump signed it on April 18. This was the only vehicle with consensus — delaying, not resolving, the legislative standoff.

### Attempt 4: Foreign Intelligence Accountability Act / S. 1318 (April 24 — present)
Speaker Johnson introduced a revised three-year proposal with the following architecture:

**What the bill contains (confirmed):**
- Three-year extension of Section 702 authority through April 30, 2029
- Monthly Civil Liberties Protection Officer (ODNI) reviews of all FBI U.S. person query justifications
- Attorney-level approval required for all FBI queries using U.S. person query terms (replacing field supervisor approval — a procedural upgrade, not a warrant requirement)
- Expanded GAO audit authority over the program
- Strengthened criminal penalties for FBI employees who misuse Section 702
- Expanded congressional and staff access to FISC proceedings
- CBDC ban attached as an amendment to secure Freedom Caucus votes
- No warrant requirement for U.S. person queries
- Commercial data broker loophole remains open

**What the bill does not contain (civil liberties gap):**
- Warrant requirement for back-end searches of Americans' communications collected incidentally during foreign targeting — the core Fourth Amendment objection
- Restrictions on commercial data broker purchases (the route through which ICE and DHS access location data without a court order)
- Geographic limitations on collection
- Private right of action for Fourth Amendment violations

**Just Security assessment ("Fool's Gold"):** The attorney-level approval requirement is procedurally similar to what FBI already required internally after prior compliance failures. The warrant language in the bill addresses front-end targeting (already prohibited) rather than back-end queries of Americans' communications — the actual civil liberties problem. Eighteen years of "compliance whack-a-mole" (Julian Sanchez's framing) is not addressed by adding more internal review layers.

## Section 2: The April 28-29 Procedural Sequence

### April 28 — Rules Committee Collapse
The House Rules Committee scheduled a meeting Monday to advance S. 1318 to the floor. The committee recessed and never returned — a procedural collapse publicly attributed to inadequate vote counts. More than a dozen Republicans had publicly signaled opposition on Fourth Amendment warrant grounds. Representative Tim Burchett (R-TN): "They keep coming back with the same stuff." Representative Ralph Norman (R-SC): "Still no warrants, still no CBDC, still no penalties to amount to anything." Representative Keith Self (R-TX): "The United States Constitution is NOT a suggestion." Representatives Massie and Boebert introduced the Surveillance Accountability Act as an alternative (warrant requirement plus private right of action).

The American Prospect reported April 28 that the FISA bill had "imploded" — characterizing it as a final collapse.

### April 29 — Rules Committee Reversal
Within 24 hours, the House Rules Committee revived the bill in a 9-4 vote, advancing a procedural rule for floor consideration. The overnight reversal indicates leadership made vote-count commitments to previously opposed members that are not fully documented in public reporting. The CBDC ban addition was the publicly identified sweetener; whether additional commitments were made is unknown.

### April 29 — Procedural Rule Floor Vote (215-210)
The House voted 215-210 on the procedural rule opening floor debate — a party-line vote that Speaker Johnson held open for more than two hours while flipping Republican holdouts. In the House, a successful procedural rule vote almost always precedes successful final passage, because leadership does not advance rules without locking final votes first. As of 1:32 PM April 29 (the latest confirmed timestamp in available reporting), the final passage vote was pending "later in the day."

## Section 4: Cross-Domain Implications

### Domain 1 (Voting Rights) — Election Organizer Surveillance Window

A three-year warrantless renewal means FBI retains authority to query the Section 702 database for U.S. person communications without a warrant through April 2029. This directly affects:

- **Election security organizers**: Civil society groups working on 2026 midterm election protection — voter registration drives, poll monitoring, legal observer networks — communicate with foreign-based partners (international human rights organizations, diaspora communities, academic researchers). Those communications are incidentally collected under Section 702's foreign targeting authority, and the FBI can query the resulting database for U.S. persons without a warrant.
- **Protest organizers**: May Day 2026 and ongoing protest organizing involves communication with foreign-based support organizations. Domain 1 Section 6.2 (election security cross-reference) should be updated to reflect the three-year window of warrantless query authority.
- **Immigrant community organizations**: Section 702's incidental U.S. person collection is used in conjunction with — not instead of — the commercial data broker surveillance documented in surveillance-tracking.md. The two surveillance vectors compound each other.

### Domain 6 (Judicial Independence) — EFF/ACLU Legal Challenge Pipeline

The civil liberties community has explicitly stated it will pursue judicial challenges regardless of the legislative outcome. The most likely challenge vectors:

1. **FISC adversarial proceedings**: Amicus briefs challenging the warrantless U.S. person query authority at the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court — the only judicial venue with direct jurisdiction
2. **Fourth Amendment challenge in Article III courts**: Based on Carpenter v. United States (2018) doctrine — the reasonable expectation of privacy analysis could be extended to incidentally collected domestic communications. This challenge has been repeatedly dismissed at lower courts on standing grounds; the civil liberties community is building a case that may survive standing after documented FBI query abuses
3. **Commercial data broker separation**: The commercial data purchase pathway (Venntel/Babel Street/Accurint — documented in surveillance-tracking.md Parts 1-3) operates entirely outside Section 702 and is unaffected by any reauthorization outcome. The Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act, which would close this loophole, has not advanced in either chamber

### Domain 35 (Supreme Court 2026 Term) — Carpenter Doctrine Expansion

EFF's indicated judicial challenge strategy will likely seek to expand Carpenter v. United States (2018) — which held that cell-site location data requires a warrant despite third-party doctrine — to the incidental collection context. This is a long-cycle litigation strategy; a cert petition, if one is ultimately filed, would arrive at the Supreme Court no earlier than OT2027. The civil liberties community's pre-litigation groundwork should be monitored in surveillance-tracking.md for new case filings.

## Section 6: The Unaddressed Structural Problem — Commercial Data Broker Loophole

This is the surveillance accountability gap that no 2026 proposal closes and that determines the practical surveillance capacity of ICE, DHS, and the FBI regardless of Section 702 outcome:

**The parallel surveillance architecture**: Intelligence agencies and federal law enforcement are currently permitted to purchase location data from commercial data brokers — Venntel/Gravy Analytics, Babel Street, Accurint (LexisNexis) — without a warrant, even when a warrant would be required to compel the same data directly. This is the warrant-end-run documented in surveillance-tracking.md Part 2.

**Why Section 702 reauthorization does not address this**: Section 702 governs foreign-targeted communications collection that incidentally captures U.S. person communications. The commercial data broker pathway operates entirely outside this framework — it involves domestic commercial data purchased by agencies from private companies, not foreign-targeted collection. A warrant requirement amendment to Section 702 would have addressed back-end queries of the 702 database; it would not have touched the commercial data broker pathway.

**Statutory gap**: The Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act (Wyden-Davidson) would close the commercial data broker loophole. It has not advanced in either chamber in the 119th Congress. Senator Wyden sent a March 3, 2026 letter to the DHS OIG demanding investigation into whether ICE continues purchasing location data in violation of the FTC's Venntel/Gravy Analytics order (January 2025). That investigation is pending.

**Practical consequence for 2026-2029**: If S. 1318 passes as described, the surveillance architecture affecting immigrant communities, protest organizers, and civil society groups will consist of:
1. Section 702 warrantless U.S. person query authority — extended through April 2029, no new restrictions
2. Commercial data broker location data purchases — completely unaffected, no new restrictions, Venntel FTC enforcement gap still open
3. Palantir ImmigrationOS ($30M ICE contract, through September 2027) — integrating IRS, SSA, license plate reader, and passport data
4. Clearview AI facial recognition (ICE $9.2M contract, CBP $225K contract) — no restrictions
5. ICE "Ad Tech and Big Data" RFI (January 2026) — next surveillance frontier, completely unaddressed

The combined architecture means the Section 702 outcome — whether warrantless renewal or warrant-protected renewal — addresses only one lane of a five-lane surveillance highway. This is the core civil liberties community message for the 2026-2029 period.

## Section 9: Post-April 29 — What the Final House Vote Confirms

*Added April 29, 2026 (Session 658)*

### 9.1 Final House Vote: 235-191

The House passed S.1318 (the Foreign Intelligence Accountability Act, three-year warrantless renewal) on April 29, 2026 by a **235-191 bipartisan vote**. The bipartisan character of the vote is inverted from the normal pattern: bipartisan means bipartisan opposition, not bipartisan support. More than 20 GOP privacy hawks voted no on constitutional grounds; most House Democrats voted no on civil liberties grounds. The 235 "yes" votes came predominantly from House Republicans who accepted the attorney-level approval compromise as sufficient reform.

The 235-191 margin is materially wider than the 215-210 procedural rule vote — which means some members who voted yes on procedure voted yes on final passage after leadership extracted additional commitments in the two-hour hold. The gap (20 votes) is consistent with vote-trading on the CBDC ban attachment, committee assignment commitments, or other considerations not reflected in public reporting.

### 9.2 The CBDC Problem and Senate Resolution Path

Speaker Johnson attached a permanent CBDC ban to the House bill as a Freedom Caucus sweetener. Senate Majority Leader Thune immediately warned this provision is "dead on arrival" in the Senate, where Democrats oppose it unanimously and it cannot reach 60 votes for cloture.

The expected Senate resolution path: the Senate passes S.4344 (the companion clean three-year extension without the CBDC ban) under Thune's unanimous consent agreement (cloture vote by May 1). The House then faces a choice: accept the Senate's CBDC-free version (losing the Freedom Caucus sweetener) or insist on conference (risking lapse beyond April 30). Given the intelligence community's stated urgency, House acceptance of the Senate version is the most probable outcome. This means the enacted law will likely:
- Extend Section 702 for three years (through April 30, 2029)
- Contain the attorney-level approval and monthly ODNI CLPO review provisions
- Not contain a CBDC ban
- Not contain a warrant requirement
- Not address the commercial data broker loophole

### 9.3 The Civil Liberties Community's Post-Passage Position

The Freedom Caucus-progressive Democrat coalition that nearly blocked reauthorization (holding together long enough to defeat two proposals before splitting) represents the largest bipartisan surveillance reform bloc since RISAA (2024). The 2029 reform window is the next legislative moment. Organizations to watch:

- **EFF**: Will file FOIA requests for FBI U.S. person query justifications under the new monthly ODNI CLPO reporting requirement. These constitute the empirical record for the 2029 reform fight and potential Carpenter-doctrine Fourth Amendment challenge.
- **ACLU**: U.S. v. Hasbajrami (Second Circuit) is the active judicial challenge to warrantless Section 702 back-end queries. This case is the most likely vehicle for a warrant requirement ruling short of a statutory fix.
- **Just Security / Brennan Center**: Will publish post-enactment legal analysis within 48-72 hours of signing. Monitor for specific challenge vectors.
- **Wyden-Davidson Government Surveillance Reform Act**: This remains the legislative vehicle for the 2029 reauthorization cycle. The coalition that voted no in 2026 is the political nucleus of the 2029 reform push.

The April 30 Senate vote outcome cannot be confirmed as a completed fact as of this writing — reporting through the end of April 29 cuts off before the Senate's scheduled May 1 cloture vote on S.4344. What is confirmed from the legislative record:

- Thune publicly stated a 45-day short-term extension was the fallback: "the Senate is likely to send back a 45-day extension." Details described as "still fluid" in April 29 reporting.

**The two most probable April 30 outcomes, in order of likelihood:**

1. **Senate passes a short-term extension (45 days, approximately to June 12-14)** by unanimous consent or voice vote — the same procedural mechanism used for the April 17 10-day extension. This avoids the 60-vote cloture threshold for the three-year bill, prevents a lapse, and allows continued House-Senate negotiation on the long-term extension. Under this scenario, Section 702 authority did not lapse.

2. **Senate passes S.4344 (clean three-year extension without CBDC ban)** via cloture, then final passage. The House would then need to vote on the Senate-amended version. If the House agreed, the enacted law would be: three-year extension through April 30, 2029; attorney-level approval for FBI U.S. person queries; monthly ODNI CLPO review; no warrant requirement; no CBDC ban; commercial data broker loophole unchanged. 3. **FISA 702 lapsed at midnight April 30** — possible if Senate Republicans objected to unanimous consent on either vehicle. Under this scenario, existing FISC collection orders reportedly remain in effect through a classified court ruling (reported to extend authority through approximately 2027 independent of statutory expiration), but technology companies compelled to assist collection gain grounds to legally challenge that compulsion.

### 10.5 War Powers / FISA Parallel: The April 30 — May 1 Surveillance-Security Nexus

These two simultaneous events — FISA 702 expiration and the War Powers Resolution Iran deadline — represent a compressed constitutional stress test with a specific connection that surveillance accountability research should document:

The Iran war has driven intelligence community urgency around FISA 702 renewal. CNN's April 13 reporting ("US intel officials scramble to keep surveillance law running amid Iran war tensions") confirmed that the intelligence community publicly argued that a FISA lapse during an active conflict would cause an immediate "intelligence gap" in collection against Iranian military and diplomatic communications. This argument — national security emergency during wartime requires surveillance authority — is the strongest possible executive branch framing for renewal without civil liberties constraints.

The practical effect: the Iran war gave the intelligence community and administration the political leverage to resist warrant amendment demands. "Reform FISA during an active hot war" is a harder political argument than "reform FISA during peacetime." The Freedom Caucus members who might have held for warrant language faced a direct political cost: opposing FISA renewal during the Iran conflict could be framed as weakening national security in wartime. The CBDC sweetener worked partly because it gave Freedom Caucus members a face-saving exit from the warrant position that the Iran war context had made politically costly to hold.

This nexus — executive branch uses military conflict to suppress surveillance accountability demands — is a structural dynamic that will recur. Domain 25 and Domain 19f should be read together as a case study in how war powers overreach and surveillance accountability failures compound each other.
