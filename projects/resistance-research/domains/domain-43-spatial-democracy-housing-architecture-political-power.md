# Domain 43: Spatial Democracy — Housing Architecture and Political Power

## Lead Finding

The most consequential political geography mechanism in the United States is not gerrymandering. It is zoning.

Where people can afford to live determines which polling places they use, which community organizations they build over decades, which school boards they control, which city council districts they populate, and whether they have the time, stability, and cognitive resources for civic engagement at all. The American housing system — specifically the exclusionary zoning architecture developed between 1920 and 1970, maintained with ALEC model legislation through the present, and accelerated by gentrification and cost-burden in recent decades — functions as a spatial democratic suppression mechanism that targets low-income, Black, and Hispanic constituencies with documented precision.

This is not an analogy to political suppression. It is political suppression. It operates through private markets rather than voter ID laws, but it produces measurable reductions in voter registration, turnout, community organizational capacity, and elected representation that rival any statutory disenfranchisement mechanism in the contemporary United States.

The key findings:

1. Exclusionary zoning was designed as a racial and economic segregation tool after explicit racial deed restrictions were struck down in 1917 (Buchanan v. Warley), and its racial motivation has been confirmed empirically in peer-reviewed political science literature as recently as 2025.

2. Gentrification has displaced 261,000 Black residents from majority-Black neighborhoods since 1980, erasing neighborhood political infrastructure — church networks, ward political organizations, community institutions — that took generations to build and cannot be reconstructed by dispersed former residents.

3. Housing cost burden creates a measurable civic participation tax: PNAS research (2024) shows that residential mobility induced by housing cost pressure depresses voter registration by 3.6–4.9 percentage points and suppresses federal election participation for nearly two decades — an effect magnitude comparable to or exceeding the most effective get-out-the-vote campaigns.

4. State preemption of local tenant protections, driven by ALEC model legislation adopted in 36 states, is a structural bypass of local democratic outcomes: when urban tenant majorities pass rent stabilization, state legislatures controlled by rural and suburban homeowner majorities routinely override them.

5. The HUD Continuum of Care June 1, 2026 NOFO creates an immediate advocacy window: the Trump administration's twice-blocked attempt to gut permanent supportive housing funding is being reattempted, and the comment and litigation window is open now.

## Section 1: Exclusionary Zoning as Designed Political Segregation

### The Constitutional Moment

The architecture of American residential segregation has a precise origin point. In 1917, the Supreme Court ruled in *Buchanan v. Warley* that racially explicit zoning — ordinances designating blocks as "white" or "colored" — violated the Fourteenth Amendment's prohibition on property taking. The response from municipalities, real estate professionals, and federal housing administrators was not to abandon segregation but to re-engineer it through facially neutral tools that produced the same results.

Single-family-only zoning was the primary vehicle. As Pacific Legal Foundation's historical analysis documents, cities adopted single-family zoning "to achieve the same exclusionary result" as racial deed restrictions, with "apartment house" understood in the social context of the 1920s as coded language for "poorer residents, immigrants, and racial minorities." The Supreme Court's 1926 decision in *Village of Euclid v. Ambler Realty Co.* codified this system nationally. Justice Sutherland's majority opinion described apartment buildings as a "parasite" on residential districts — language that encoded class and racial hierarchy into constitutional doctrine and "opened the floodgates to bigoted exclusionary zoning laws around the United States for decades."

### The Political Science Evidence

The racial motivation of exclusionary zoning is not merely historical interpretation. A 2025 study published in the *Journal of Politics* (Alexander Sahn, Vol. 87, No. 4) provides direct empirical evidence using the natural experiment of the Great Migration. Analyzing zoning patterns in Northern cities where Black migration from 1940–1970 was exogenous to local housing policy decisions, Sahn found that increasing racial diversity causes cities to zone less land for multifamily housing. Critically, analysis of public opinion surveys showed that white urban voters in areas affected by Black migration were more racially conservative on housing policy — confirming that exclusionary zoning was adopted not merely for economic property-value preservation but as a response to racial threat.

This finding matters enormously for the democracy analysis. Exclusionary zoning is not merely a well-intentioned land use policy with unintentional segregating effects. It is, in documented political science terms, a racial segregation mechanism embedded in facially neutral land use law.

### The Current Landscape

The result of a century of this system is structural: approximately 75% of residential land in most U.S. cities is zoned exclusively for single-family housing, prohibiting by law the construction of multifamily housing that would enable lower-income residents to live in high-opportunity areas. Brookings Institution research confirms that in major metro areas, low-density zoning that prohibits apartment construction accounts for roughly three-quarters of residential land area.

The National Low Income Housing Coalition's *Out of Reach 2025* report quantifies the consequences. The national Housing Wage — the hourly rate a full-time worker must earn to afford a modest two-bedroom apartment without spending more than 30% of income on housing — is $33.63 in 2025. This is more than four times the federal minimum wage. A record-high 22.6 million renter households were cost-burdened in 2023. Eighty-seven percent of extremely low-income renters face cost burden, with 75% experiencing severe cost burden (more than 50% of income on housing). Among the 25 most common occupations in the U.S., 18 pay median wages below the two-bedroom Housing Wage — covering approximately 74 million workers, nearly half the entire U.S. workforce.

### The Political Geography Effect

The downstream political effect of this spatial architecture is democratic constituency segregation operating through private market mechanisms rather than legislative line-drawing. Exclusionary zoning concentrates low-income, predominantly Black and Hispanic residents in specific geographic zones. In single-member district systems (which govern city council, county commission, state legislative, and congressional elections), this concentration determines political representation patterns. It places voters with the highest barriers to political participation — those most likely to hold multiple jobs, face transportation constraints, have volatile work schedules, and face civic engagement costs most acutely — in zones that are often simultaneously underserved by public infrastructure.

This is spatial gerrymandering without a map-drawing committee. The effect — concentration of Democratic-leaning, low-income, and minority voters in fewer districts with less political leverage — is produced not by legislative intent but by a housing market architecture whose racial intent was embedded in its design a century ago and has never been dismantled.

## Section 2: Gentrification as Neighborhood Political Infrastructure Destruction

### The Scale of Displacement

Gentrification is commonly analyzed as an economic phenomenon — rising housing costs displacing lower-income residents. The democracy analysis requires recognizing it as something more specific: the destruction of neighborhood political infrastructure that has no equivalent in the receiving communities to which displaced residents relocate.

The National Community Reinvestment Coalition's 2024 report *Displaced by Design: Fifty Years of Gentrification and Black Cultural Displacement in US Cities* provides the definitive quantitative account. Between 1980 and 2020, gentrification affected 523 majority-Black neighborhoods across the United States. Of those, 155 underwent full racial turnover — shifting from majority-Black to majority-white. The measurable demographic consequence: 261,000 fewer Black people now live in gentrifying neighborhoods that were majority-Black in 1980. Total Black population loss across all gentrifying neighborhoods could be as high as 500,000.

The most impacted cities are not marginal markets. Washington, D.C., New York City, Philadelphia, New Orleans, Atlanta, and the San Francisco Bay Area are among the cities with the highest rates of neighborhood racial turnover — exactly the cities that serve as organizing hubs, political headquarters, and electoral power centers for Black civic and political life.

### The Political Infrastructure Loss Mechanism

The democratic cost of this displacement is not simply the loss of housing for individual families. It is the destruction of neighborhood political infrastructure that took decades to build.

African American urban political power is a constructed infrastructure, not a demographic accident. It consists of:
- Church networks that function as political mobilization platforms, voter registration infrastructure, and community information systems
- Ward-level political clubs and party precinct organizations embedded in specific neighborhoods
- HBCU alumni networks anchored to specific institutional geographies
- Community development organizations with longstanding relationships to local government institutions
- Block associations and neighborhood civic organizations with accumulated institutional memory
- Small businesses that serve as gathering spaces, economic anchors, and community meeting points

When a neighborhood gentrifies, displaced residents do not relocate as a community to a new neighborhood where they reconstruct this infrastructure. They scatter to dispersed suburban locations, exurban counties, or distant cities. The political infrastructure — which took generations to build and is non-portable — is destroyed. The receiving communities they arrive in have no equivalent infrastructure and no mechanism through which recently arrived dispersed residents can rapidly build one.

The American University School of Public Affairs study "Gentrification and Racial Representation: A Comparative Analysis" documents this mechanism at the level of elected officials. In the DC Shaw/U Street corridor and Atlanta neighborhoods, gentrification produced measurable reduction in Black elected officials, a diminished role in municipal decision-making, and erosion of Black cultural representation in local government. The researchers confirmed the phenomenon of "political displacement" as distinct from physical displacement — the loss of political voice among long-term residents due to neighborhood demographic change, even before those residents have been physically removed.

### The Irreversibility Problem

The critical feature of this mechanism from a democracy analysis perspective is irreversibility within politically relevant time horizons. Once a majority-Black neighborhood gentrifies and loses its political infrastructure — loses the ward political club, loses the church's electoral mobilization function, loses the HBCU alumni network that used the neighborhood as its base — these cannot be reconstructed in the short term. Electoral cycles occur every two years. Neighborhood political infrastructure takes a generation to build.

This means gentrification's democracy effects are structurally path-dependent: early displacement locks in political representation losses that compound over time. A congressional district or city council ward that loses its Black majority through gentrification in 2010 will have different electoral outcomes, different legislative representation, and different policy responsiveness for at minimum the next decade — the period needed to establish new political infrastructure in whatever communities receive the displaced population.

Census Bureau working paper CES-WP-24-29 (2024), "Whose Neighborhood Now? Gentrification and Community Life in Low-Income Urban Neighborhoods," confirms the racialized nature of gentrification's community effects using a nationally representative sample of 17,500 respondents. White residents and newcomers report higher social cohesion as gentrification increases; racial minority groups and longer-term residents show no equivalent benefit. Gentrification-related civic amenities serve newly arrived populations rather than incumbent communities.

### The Philadelphia Dimension

Research published in the *Journal of Race, Ethnicity and the City* (2024) documents the mechanism with particular precision in Philadelphia, examining how gentrification produces "the political and ideological displacement of the Philadelphia Black Worker tradition" — a documented case where the physical displacement of Black working-class residents from Center City neighborhoods destroyed a distinct political-ideological tradition embedded in labor organizing, civil rights infrastructure, and community political organizations. What was destroyed was not merely housing but an organized political capacity that could not follow its residents into dispersal.

## Section 3: Housing Cost Burden as Civic Participation Tax

### The Mechanism

Political participation is not free. It requires time, transportation, cognitive bandwidth, and freedom from material crisis urgent enough to preempt participation. Housing cost burden imposes a direct tax on all four.

The mechanism is not about motivation or political disengagement. It is about structural resource depletion. A family paying 50% of income on housing is not politically unmotivated; it is politically resource-constrained. The cognitive bandwidth research (Mullainathan and Shafir, *Scarcity*, 2013) documents that financial precarity — including housing cost burden — actively depletes the cognitive resources needed for complex information-processing tasks, including voter research, legislative tracking, and civic organizing. This is not a metaphor. It is a documented psychological mechanism.

### The Quantitative Evidence

The PNAS study by Knight and Zhang (2024), "Residential Mobility and Persistently Depressed Voting Among Disadvantaged Adults in a Large Housing Experiment," provides the most rigorous available evidence. Using data from Moving to Opportunity — a multi-city housing mobility experiment involving over 4,600 families (nearly 16,000 participants, 93% Black and Hispanic) matched against nationwide voter data — the study found:

- Receiving a housing voucher requiring relocation to a low-poverty neighborhood decreased voter registration by 3.6 percentage points
- Section 8 vouchers permitting relocation to any neighborhood decreased voter registration by 4.9 percentage points
- MTO participation decreased participation in federal elections: experimental vouchers decreased the average percentage of federal elections in which adult participants voted by 4.0 percentage points; Section 8 vouchers decreased this percentage by 3.3 percentage points
- The negative impact on voter participation persisted for nearly two decades

This is among the strongest available evidence that housing instability functions as a direct democratic participation suppressor. The magnitude — comparable to or exceeding the most effective get-out-the-vote campaigns in absolute terms — means that housing policy is de facto electoral policy.

Slee and Desmond's 2023 study in *Politics and Society* ("Eviction and Voter Turnout: The Political Consequences of Housing Instability") confirms the mechanism from the eviction end. Combining tens of millions of eviction and voting records, the study found that increases in neighborhood eviction rates produced substantial declines in voter turnout in the 2016 presidential election. Because eviction disproportionately affects low-income, Black, and Hispanic neighborhoods, the researchers concluded that "the results have troubling implications for participatory inequality" — a measured formulation of what is in fact a documented democratic suppression effect.

The Journal of Politics research (Holbein and Konisky, 2022) documents the homeowner participation premium: buying a home causes individuals to participate substantially more in local elections on average. Renters are systematically underrepresented in local elections. As renters participate less, local politicians design policies to serve homeowners — producing self-reinforcing exclusionary zoning, opposition to multifamily development, and continued cost burden for renters who have not yet achieved the homeownership-participation threshold.

### The Structural Disadvantage in Planning Processes

The civic participation tax is not only a time and cognitive resource problem. It is also an organizational asymmetry problem in the specific contexts where housing policy is actually made.

Local planning hearings — where zoning decisions are made, where development applications are approved or denied, where affordable housing projects are approved or blocked — have structural features that systematically disadvantage renters and cost-burdened residents relative to incumbent homeowners:

1. Hearings are held on weekday evenings, a format that requires workers with stable 9-to-5 schedules and no childcare constraints to attend
2. NIMBY (Not In My Backyard) opposition to new housing is geographically concentrated among existing homeowners who live near proposed development, are easily mobilized by immediate self-interest (property values, neighborhood character), and have established channels for local political influence
3. The beneficiaries of affordable housing production are diffuse future residents who do not yet live in the jurisdiction and have no formal standing in planning processes
4. Tenant organizing is itself labor-intensive in ways that cost-burdened renters are structurally less able to sustain

This is the collective action problem in housing policy: the costs of exclusionary zoning are diffuse and borne by renters who face high barriers to participation; the benefits of exclusion are concentrated among incumbent homeowners who face low barriers. The political equilibrium produces systematic underproduction of affordable housing regardless of community-wide preferences.

The Tenant Union Federation, founded in August 2024 as the first major national tenant organizing body in 40 years, is an explicit response to this structural disadvantage — a recognition that individual tenant organizing within single buildings or single cities is insufficient against a coordination problem that spans state and national jurisdictions.

## Section 4: State Preemption as Spatial Democracy Suppression

### The Architecture of Preemption

When cities and counties adopt tenant protections — rent stabilization, just-cause eviction requirements, source-of-income discrimination bans, notice requirements, right to counsel in housing court — state legislatures frequently preempt those protections, nullifying local democratic outcomes by legislative override.

As of 2026, 36 states preempt some form of local rent control. The ALEC Rent Control Preemption Act, introduced as model legislation in 1995, has been adopted verbatim or with minor modifications across multiple states — Illinois' preemption law replicates ALEC language directly. The American Legislative Exchange Council is a corporate-funded legislative drafting organization; its model preemption legislation is written by and for real estate industry interests, introduced into state legislatures by member legislators, and framed using virtually identical talking points across states: warnings about "unelected special interests overriding representative government," concern about "out-of-state interests," claims that rent control "destroys housing supply."

This framing is the preemption of democratic outcomes using the language of democracy. When tenants in a city vote for rent stabilization through ballot initiative — as happened in multiple jurisdictions — and the state legislature preempts that outcome, this is not a policy correction. It is a structural democratic bypass: rural and suburban homeowner majorities overriding urban tenant majorities on a policy question whose costs fall entirely on urban tenants.

### The 2026 Preemption Battle

The 2026 legislative session has produced an active preemption repeal front across multiple states. The National Apartment Association is tracking 191 state rent control-related bills, with 71 introduced since the start of 2026. Active preemption repeal efforts include:

- **Virginia**: HB 278 would allow localities to adopt anti-rent-gouging ordinances capping annual rent increases at 3%
- **Illinois**: SB 2884 would permit municipalities to enact rent control ordinances through voter referendum — blocked again in committee in 2026
- **Florida**: SB 1726/HB 1493 would repeal local preemption
- **New Mexico**: SB 138 would repeal local preemption
- **California**: Additional tenant protection legislation building on 2025's historic reform package
- **Massachusetts**: Rent control ballot question circulating, with legislative compromise discussions ongoing

The trend is significant: after a decade of ALEC-coordinated preemption expansion, tenant advocates in multiple states are mounting coordinated legislative and ballot-initiative responses simultaneously. The Tenant Union Federation's explicitly multi-state structure is designed to match the organizational geography of the preemption campaigns it opposes.

### The HUD CoC Preemption Crisis: June 1 Window

A parallel preemption dynamic is operating at the federal-local level through the HUD Continuum of Care program. The Trump administration has twice attempted to restructure CoC funding in ways that would defund permanent supportive housing — the evidence-based intervention for chronic homelessness — in favor of a transitional-housing and enforcement-first model aligned with the policy logic of *Grants Pass v. Johnson* (the 2024 Supreme Court decision authorizing municipal anti-camping enforcement against unhoused persons when no shelter alternatives exist).

Timeline:
- November 2025: HUD releases a revised CoC NOFO gutting permanent supportive housing, capping PSH renewal funds at 30% from a baseline of 87% of spending, and requiring CoCs to demonstrate law enforcement cooperation, anti-camping enforcement, and involuntary commitment capabilities as scoring factors
- December 19, 2025: A Rhode Island federal court issues a preliminary injunction blocking the NOFO
- January 9, 2026: HUD reinstates the original Biden-era NOFO
- May 4, 2026: HUD announces it will "try again" with a revised 2026 CoC NOFO, expected June 1, 2026, with awards expected December 2026
- The administration states the June 2026 CoC's goal is to "optimize self-sufficiency" and increase investment in transitional housing, street outreach, and addiction treatment — the same policy direction that was blocked by the court in December 2025

The advocacy and litigation window is open now. Organizations tracking the June 1 NOFO include the National Alliance to End Homelessness (which has sued HUD over grant restrictions), Community Solutions, the National Low Income Housing Coalition, and state and local CoC administrators. Shelterforce's April 2026 investigation ("A Grim March Toward Death") documented ground-level consequences of the 2025 NOFO's design, providing evidentiary record for ongoing litigation.

The connection to democracy analysis: permanent supportive housing is the intervention that stabilizes the most housing-insecure individuals, enabling voter registration maintenance, participation in services, and any form of community integration. Defunding it — in a context where *Grants Pass* has authorized anti-camping enforcement, and where HUD's own NOFO required law enforcement cooperation as a scoring factor — is the terminal end of the spatial displacement chain that Domain 43 traces: exclusionary zoning → gentrification → cost burden → eviction → homelessness → criminalization.

## Section 5: Reform Architecture

### Reform 1: Federal Zoning Conditionality Framework

The most powerful federal leverage over local exclusionary zoning is not a mandate — which would face constitutional anti-commandeering challenges — but a conditionality framework tying federal infrastructure and community development funding to municipal zoning reform.

This is not a novel proposal. The Biden administration pioneered competitive HUD grants rewarding municipalities that adopted pro-housing zoning reform. California's Housing Element law, which requires municipalities to produce state-certified housing plans showing sites adequate to accommodate assigned Regional Housing Needs Allocation (RHNA) numbers and authorizes state enforcement against noncompliant jurisdictions, demonstrates the enforceability of mandated housing planning at scale. By March 2026, California had issued "final warning" notices to 15 city and county entities that had not submitted compliant housing elements.

The federal framework should:
- Codify competitive zoning reform grants as a statutory framework under the Community Development Block Grant program, conditioning CDBG funding on demonstrated progress toward multifamily zoning permitting
- Establish a federal Zoning Impact Scorecard, modeled on California's Housing Element methodology, that municipalities must complete to maintain eligibility for federal transportation, infrastructure, and community development funding
- Create a technical assistance program enabling smaller municipalities that want to reform but lack planning capacity to access federal expertise

The conditionality approach aligns federal infrastructure investment — which benefits homeowners and businesses within jurisdictions — with the democratic access implications of those jurisdictions' zoning decisions.

### Reform 2: AFFH Restoration with Civic Participation Mandate

The Affirmatively Furthering Fair Housing (AFFH) rule, requiring recipients of HUD funding to analyze and address housing segregation, was rescinded by executive order in January 2025 and replaced with an "interim final rule" in March 2025. In response, both the Senate (S.1621) and House (H.R.3086) have introduced the Restoring Fair Housing Protections Eliminated by Trump Act of 2025.

Restoration of AFFH should include an expansion that has never existed in any previous iteration: an explicit Civic Participation Impact Assessment requirement. HUD funding recipients should be required to analyze not only housing segregation effects but also the civic participation effects of segregation patterns — voter registration rates, community organizational capacity, turnout disparities, and access to planning processes — in their AFFH assessments.

This would be the first time federal housing policy explicitly recognized democratic design as a dimension of fair housing analysis. It would create a legal and evidentiary hook for organizing and litigation connecting housing segregation to participatory inequality.

### Reform 3: Federal Anti-Displacement and Anti-Preemption Framework

Federal protection should be established for local tenant protections adopted through democratic processes — specifically, preemption by state legislatures of local rent stabilization, just-cause eviction, or source-of-income discrimination protections that were enacted through ballot initiative or local legislative process.

The constitutional mechanism is conditionality: municipalities that have enacted tenant protections through democratic processes should qualify for enhanced HUD funding and community development grant priority. State legislatures that preempt such protections after democratic enactment should trigger a HUD review of the state's Fair Housing Act compliance.

This does not create a new federal right against state preemption (which would face constitutional obstacles) but creates federal policy incentives that align with local democratic self-governance. It is the housing-specific application of the anti-preemption principle identified in Domain 33 (State Legislative Autocratization).

### Reform 4: Tenant Organizing Infrastructure Funding

The structural disadvantage of tenant interests in planning and legislative processes — diffuse beneficiaries, high barriers to civic participation, geographic dispersal — cannot be overcome without explicit organizing infrastructure investment.

The Community Services Block Grant program should be expanded to include explicit funding for tenant organizing capacity in cost-burdened communities. The labor movement model is instructive: the National Labor Relations Act did not merely guarantee workers the right to organize; it established the institutional infrastructure within which organizing could occur. Housing tenant organizing requires analogous infrastructure investment.

The Tenant Union Federation, founded August 2024 with initial member unions in Kansas City, Connecticut, Louisville, Bozeman, and elsewhere, has built explicitly multi-state infrastructure designed to coordinate against preemption at the state level. Its National Tenant Policy Agenda includes national rent caps, anti-eviction protections, habitability standards, and antitrust action against landlord consolidation and algorithmic rent collusion. Federal organizing infrastructure funding would allow TUF and affiliated organizations to scale operations to match the geography of ALEC's preemption campaigns.

### Reform 5: Civic Participation Impact Assessments for Large Displacement Projects

Any development project receiving federal subsidy, federal approvals, or HUD Community Development funding that will displace 50 or more households should be required to conduct a Civic Participation Impact Assessment (CPIA) documenting:

- Projected voter registration effects on displaced households
- Effects on established community organizational networks (church-based civic organizations, community development organizations, tenant associations, block clubs)
- Effects on neighborhood political representation (city council districts, state legislative districts, school boards)
- Proposed mitigation: community land trust creation, right-to-return provisions, community benefit agreements

The CPIA framework is analogous to the Environmental Impact Statement process under NEPA. Like the EIS, it does not prohibit displacement but requires that democracy consequences be visible in the approval record — creating a legal hook for organizing and documentation that makes the full cost of displacement legible to approving agencies.

This is the institutional mechanism for converting the research findings in this document — that displacement destroys political infrastructure — into regulatory accountability.

## Section 6: International Precedents

### Vienna: Social Housing as Democratic Infrastructure

Vienna is the definitive case study for housing policy as democratic infrastructure design. Approximately 50% of all Viennese residents live in subsidized dwellings — either in 220,000 municipal units (Gemeindebau) or in 200,000 cooperative flats built with municipal subsidies. In 2023, Vienna ranked first in the Economist Intelligence Unit's Global Liveability Index, with a perfect score for stability, healthcare, education, and infrastructure.

The democratic dimension is embedded in the design philosophy. Vienna engages residents in planning and managing social housing, building participatory mechanisms that foster community ownership and trust. The average rent per square meter in Vienna in 2023 was €10.50 — approximately one-third of London's equivalent cost. The income distribution across Vienna's neighborhoods is far more equitable than comparable American cities, with cross-neighborhood income diversity that prevents the spatial concentration of both poverty and political disenfranchisement.

Vienna's model relies on what political scientists call a "network of supportive political institutions": labor unions, tenant associations, neighborhood groups, the Chamber of Labor, and historically the Social Democratic Party. These are not supplementary amenities; they are the institutional infrastructure that makes the housing model politically sustainable and that links housing stability to civic engagement infrastructure.

The democratic lesson: when housing is treated as public infrastructure rather than private commodity, the result is income-integrated neighborhoods with high civic participation across economic strata. The alternative — the American model — produces spatial concentration of poverty, political disenfranchisement, and the civic participation tax documented in Section 3.

### Singapore: Mandated Integration as Democratic Design

Singapore's Housing Development Board (HDB) provides housing to approximately 80% of residents, with an 88.9% owner-occupancy rate among public flats (2021). The Ethnic Integration Policy, implemented in 1989, sets maximum allowable proportions for each ethnic group in each HDB neighborhood and block, explicitly preventing the formation of racial enclaves.

The civic integration dimension is deliberate. HDB neighborhoods organize civic participation through grassroots institutions under the People's Association: Citizens' Consultative Committees and Residents' Committees in each electoral constituency. The integration policy has succeeded by measurable metrics: one in every three HDB blocks has achieved demographic balance among Chinese, Malay, Indian, and other residents — up from one in four in 1989, despite continued construction.

Singapore's model demonstrates that housing policy can be explicitly designed to produce civic integration rather than spatial segregation. The democratic applications in the U.S. context are constrained by different political economies, but the underlying principle — that housing location policy is civic participation policy — is directly applicable.

### Community Land Trusts: Permanent Affordability as Democratic Anchor

Community land trusts (CLTs) separate ownership of land from ownership of housing structures. The land trust retains ownership of the land in perpetuity, selling or leasing housing units to residents at prices pegged to local incomes rather than market speculation. When residents sell, equity gains are shared between the seller and the trust, maintaining permanent affordability for subsequent occupants.

CLTs directly address the gentrification-as-political-infrastructure-destruction mechanism by creating geographically anchored, permanently affordable housing that prevents the demographic turnover that destroys neighborhood political infrastructure. The Champlain Housing Trust in Burlington, Vermont (founded 1984) is the oldest and largest U.S. model, serving over 600 families. Amsterdam's CLT H-Buurt, developed from a community organization active in the H-Buurt neighborhood for over fifteen years, demonstrates European adoption of the model.

The CLT mechanism is the anti-displacement intervention most directly targeted at the democracy dimension of gentrification: it anchors community members geographically, preventing the dispersal that destroys civic infrastructure, while maintaining long-term resident ownership interest in neighborhood governance.

Montreal's cooperative housing sector, which represents a substantial portion of the city's affordable housing stock through the network of housing cooperatives and community land trusts supported by the Fonds québécois d'habitation communautaire, provides the Canadian model for scaled CLT and cooperative housing deployment as permanent community stabilization infrastructure.

## Section 7: Advocacy Windows and Coalition Architecture

### Window 1: HUD CoC June 1, 2026 NOFO

The most immediate advocacy window in the housing democracy space is the June 1 HUD CoC NOFO release. Based on the November 2025 NOFO that was twice blocked by federal courts, the June 2026 iteration will almost certainly attempt to reintroduce the same structural changes: redirecting funding from permanent supportive housing toward transitional housing, embedding anti-camping enforcement as a scoring criterion, and operationalizing Grants Pass's logic throughout the CoC system.

Advocacy priorities:
- Submit public comments documenting the democratic participation consequences of permanent supportive housing defunding (the Slee/Desmond eviction research and Knight/Zhang housing mobility research provide the empirical base)
- Support litigation by the National Alliance to End Homelessness and state CoC networks challenging the NOFO if it repeats the blocked provisions
- Brief state and local CoC administrators on the comment period and documentation requirements
- Connect CoC litigation to the broader AFFH litigation (the NOFO's enforcement-first orientation appears inconsistent with fair housing obligations)

**Deadline: June 1, 2026 NOFO release; comment period typically 30–60 days following release**

### Window 2: State Preemption Repeal Campaigns, 2026

The 2026 legislative session is the highest-activity moment in decades for preemption repeal campaigns. Active bills in Virginia, Illinois, Florida, New Mexico, and Massachusetts provide multiple entry points. The Massachusetts ballot initiative in circulation, if it qualifies, will be decided in November 2026 and provides a high-visibility campaign opportunity.

Coalition strategy: The Tenant Union Federation's multi-state structure is the primary vehicle for coordinating across preemption battles. NLIHC state networks, NAACP state conferences, and AFL-CIO state affiliates provide additional coalition infrastructure. State attorneys general in blue states with Republican-controlled legislatures (where preemption was imposed from above) may be available to support preemption challenges on home-rule and democratic accountability grounds.

**Deadline: 2026 legislative session calendars vary by state; most close May–June 2026**

### Window 3: Social Housing Legislative Wave

California, Colorado, Hawaii, Massachusetts, New York, Rhode Island, and municipalities including Atlanta and Seattle have active social housing proposals as of 2026. California's 2025 housing reform package — including SB 79 (transit-oriented density) signed by Newsom in October 2025, and ongoing enforcement of Housing Element compliance — represents the most advanced state-level framework.

Minnesota's 2026 legislative session passed House File 3425, directing $9 million from the Strengthen Supportive Housing Program to CoC recipients — a direct state-level response to federal CoC funding uncertainty. Oregon is considering rent control expansion for mobile home parks and cooling requirements. These legislative campaigns benefit from shared democratic framing: social housing is not charity; it is civic infrastructure enabling political participation on equal terms.

**Coalition targets**: NLIHC, Tenant Union Federation, National Housing Law Project, PolicyLink, NAACP housing justice program, Enterprise Community Partners. State legislators in states with active social housing bills and preemption repeal campaigns are the primary legislative contacts.

## Section 8: Cross-Domain Synthesis

Domain 43 sits at the intersection of several research threads that, read together, describe a comprehensive system of democratic exclusion operating through the built environment:

**Domain 47 (Housing Security, Displacement, Civic Exclusion)** covers the acute end: eviction, homelessness, shelter system collapse. Domain 43 covers the upstream architectural layer that produces Domain 47's crisis — the zoning system, gentrification mechanism, and cost-burden structure that creates housing instability as a systemic condition rather than a series of individual failures.

**Domain 33 (State Legislative Autocratization)** documents how state legislatures are used to override local democratic outcomes across multiple policy domains. State preemption of local tenant protections is the housing-specific instantiation of this broader pattern. Domain 43 provides the housing case study for Domain 33's theory of autocratization.

**Domain 22 (Racial Justice)** documents structural racism across domains. Exclusionary zoning is among the most durable and consequential institutional expressions of structural racism in American civic life — more durable than any of the civil rights laws that have been formally repealed, because it was never framed as race law and therefore never struck down as such.

**Domain 1 (Voting Rights)** documents the statutory mechanisms of voter suppression. Domain 43 documents the economic mechanism of the same suppression: residential instability as voter registration barrier, cost burden as civic participation tax. The domains are cumulative, not separate.

**Domain 41 (Consumer Financial Architecture)** connects through the predatory mortgage lending and 2008 housing wealth transfer ($25 billion in housing wealth from Black and Hispanic families to financial institutions — see Domain 41's data). Domain 41 shows the financial mechanism of wealth extraction; Domain 43 shows the spatial and political consequences of that extraction.

**Domain 54 (Criminal Justice and Civic Exclusion)** is the terminal point of the spatial displacement chain. *Grants Pass v. Johnson* authorized anti-camping enforcement; HUD's CoC redesign is attempting to make that enforcement logic the organizing principle of federal homelessness policy. Domain 54's analysis of criminal justice as civic exclusion mechanism is the endpoint of the pathway Domain 43 traces from exclusionary zoning to homelessness to criminalization.

---

## Conclusion: Housing as Democratic Design

The central finding of Domain 43 is that housing policy is democracy policy. This is not a progressive framing choice or an analytical convenience. It is an empirical description of how the American political system operates.

Where people can afford to live determines whether they can maintain voter registration, attend planning hearings, sustain civic organizations, participate in neighborhood governance, and contribute to the political infrastructure that produces elected representation. The housing system's design — exclusionary zoning, gentrification, cost burden, preemption — systematically allocates these civic resources away from low-income, Black, and Hispanic constituencies and toward high-income, predominantly white constituencies. The effect is measurable in voter turnout data, community organizational capacity, and electoral representation patterns.

Addressing housing as democratic infrastructure requires analytic and advocacy frameworks that connect housing policy directly to democratic participation policy. The reforms outlined in this domain — zoning conditionality, AFFH expansion with civic participation mandates, anti-preemption protections, tenant organizing funding, civic participation impact assessments — are not solely housing policy proposals. They are democratic design proposals. They address the upstream architecture that produces the downstream participation deficits that voting rights advocates are attempting to remedy at the ballot level.

The advocacy windows are real and time-limited. The June 1 HUD CoC NOFO, the 2026 state preemption battles, and the social housing legislative campaigns represent concrete opportunities to advance housing as democratic infrastructure — not only as economic relief, but as the spatial foundation of political equality.
