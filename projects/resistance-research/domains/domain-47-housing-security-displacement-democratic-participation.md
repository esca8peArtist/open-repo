# Domain 47: Housing Security, Displacement, and Democratic Participation

**Research completed**: May 9, 2026
**Crisis window**: ACTIVE across three simultaneous tracks — (1) Emergency Housing Voucher program ending mid-2026 (~47,000 households), impacting midterm voter communities; (2) HUD proposed work requirements (comments due May 1, 2026, finalization expected before January 2027) threatening 3.3 million households; (3) Continuum of Care funding overhaul blocked by courts twice but HUD attempting regulatory restart (new NOFO expected June 1, 2026). The Grants Pass v. Johnson ruling (June 2024) has accelerated anti-camping enforcement that compounds every other track.
**Priority designation**: Phase 2 Core — housing insecurity is a structural mechanism of political exclusion with peer-reviewed causal evidence, not a hypothetical; the current administration's housing policy actions are systematically removing the residential stability that makes political participation possible for the lowest-income constituencies

## Executive Summary

The relationship between housing security and democratic participation is not a political argument — it is a peer-reviewed empirical finding backed by one of the largest natural experiments in social science. A 2024 Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences study, drawing on data from the federal Moving to Opportunity experiment (hundreds of thousands of participants), found that residential mobility — forced relocation from one neighborhood to another — depressed voter registration by 3.6 to 4.9 percentage points for nearly two decades. A 2023 study combining tens of millions of eviction records with voting records, published in *Politics and Society* (Slee and Desmond, Princeton University), found that a one percentage point increase in neighborhood eviction rates correlates with a 0.46 percentage point decline in voter turnout — and a 5.54 percentage point decline in the lowest-eviction-rate neighborhoods, where the disruption effect is most concentrated. People who lose their homes do not vote. People who move frequently cannot maintain voter registration. People criminalized for sleeping in public are barred from civic life.

The Trump administration's housing policy agenda in 2025–2026 is, in its documented effects and structural design, a systematic removal of housing stability from the lowest-income communities in the United States — communities that skew heavily toward Black, Hispanic, Indigenous, and disability-connected constituencies — at precisely the moment when those communities' political participation is most consequential for the 2026 midterms. Three simultaneous policy tracks are converging: the expiration of Emergency Housing Vouchers serving 47,000 households; the proposed HUD work requirements and term limits that CBPP projects would destabilize 3.3 million households (including 1.7 million children) within two years; and the Continuum of Care funding overhaul that twice required court intervention to block (blocked as "immediately destabilizing and disastrous" by a federal appeals court on April 1, 2026) and that HUD is attempting to restart through a new regulatory process with a June 1 NOFO publication target.

Layered over these administrative actions: the June 2024 Supreme Court ruling in *City of Grants Pass v. Johnson* (6-3) eliminated the Eighth Amendment constraint on criminalizing outdoor sleeping, enabling a wave of anti-camping enforcement that is pushing chronically homeless people further from social services, shelter, and — where they had it — political infrastructure. The 30-day eviction notice rollback (February 2026 interim rule, subsequently delayed by litigation) would have eliminated the buffer period that allows low-income renters to access emergency assistance and prepare for displacement.

The Affirmatively Furthering Fair Housing (AFFH) rule, rescinded in January 2025, was the only federal mechanism requiring communities receiving HUD funds to actively analyze and address residential segregation. Its elimination removes the one structural tool that could have begun to undo the documented connection between residential segregation, concentrated poverty, and political exclusion.

This domain's claim is structural: housing policy in 2025–2026 is being redesigned as a mechanism of political exclusion, whether or not that is its explicit purpose. The communities most affected are exactly the communities whose political participation is most unwelcome to the current political coalition. The reform agenda — restoration of stable housing assistance, protection of tenant due process, reimposition of AFFH requirements, decriminalization of homelessness, and proactive voter registration at housing assistance points of contact — is the structural complement to the voting rights reforms documented in Domain 1.

## 1. The Empirical Foundation: Housing Instability as Political Exclusion

### 1.1 The Causal Evidence

The connection between housing instability and reduced political participation is among the most robustly documented in political science and sociology. Four distinct bodies of evidence converge on the same finding:

**The Moving to Opportunity natural experiment (PNAS, 2024)**: The U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development's Moving to Opportunity study was among the largest social science experiments ever conducted, providing housing vouchers to randomly selected public housing residents in five cities. A 2024 PNAS study (Sajid et al., *Residential Mobility and Persistently Depressed Voting Among Disadvantaged Adults in a Large Housing Experiment*) matched MTO data with nationwide individual voter files and found:

- Experimental vouchers (restricted to low-poverty areas) decreased voter registration by **3.6 percentage points**
- Section 8 vouchers (unrestricted) decreased voter registration by **4.9 percentage points**
- The depression in voter participation lasted for **nearly two decades** — not just the period immediately following the move
- The effect operated through residential mobility itself, not through characteristics of destination neighborhoods

Nearly all MTO participants were Black and Hispanic families from high-poverty public housing. The study's finding — that even well-designed housing mobility programs depress voting for two decades — is not an argument against housing assistance. It is evidence that residential disruption, regardless of cause or intent, has massive and sustained democratic participation costs. Forced disruption (eviction, subsidy termination) would be expected to produce substantially larger effects than voluntary mobility program participation.

**The Slee-Desmond eviction-turnout study (Politics & Society, 2023)**: Princeton researchers Gillian Slee and Matthew Desmond (author of *Evicted*) combined tens of millions of eviction records with voting records and found:
- A 1 percentage point increase in neighborhood eviction rates correlates with a **0.46 percentage point** decline in voter turnout
- In neighborhoods with the lowest baseline eviction rates, the same 1 percentage point increase correlates with a **5.54 percentage point** decline
- The effect operates through three documented mechanisms: (1) finding shelter consumes the time and cognitive resources required for political participation; (2) eviction breaks the neighborhood networks that serve as civic mobilization infrastructure; (3) eviction requires re-registration to vote, a bureaucratic barrier that is systematically not completed during residential crisis

**The Moving to Opportunity APSR study (American Political Science Review, 2024)**: Separately published in APSR, a study of long-term neighborhood effects from MTO found that children who moved to lower-poverty neighborhoods as children had higher civic participation as adults — but adult movers, whose civic networks were formed in their original communities, experienced a different pattern. The study confirms that housing instability's political effects operate differently depending on whether disruption occurs during civic formation (childhood) or after civic roots are established.

**National Health Care for the Homeless Council**: Surveys of homeless individuals consistently find that fewer than 1 in 10 unhoused people vote. The barriers are structural, not attitudinal: lack of stable address for voter registration, lack of identification documentation, limited access to election information, and the domination of survival needs over civic engagement.

### 1.2 The Mechanism of Democratic Exclusion

These studies identify the same set of mechanisms through which housing instability suppresses political participation:

**Address-based voter registration barriers**: The U.S. voter registration system is built on permanent residential addresses. People without stable addresses cannot register. People who move — whether by choice, eviction, or subsidy termination — are removed from voter rolls if they fail to re-register, a failure rate that is predictably high during residential crisis. The NVRA's automatic voter registration provisions, which Domain 1 documents as functioning poorly in many states, are structurally incapable of reaching people who do not interact with the qualifying agencies while stable.

**Network disruption**: Political mobilization operates through social networks. Canvassers know which neighborhoods to target based on registration density. Neighbors remind each other to vote. Civic organizations anchor voter mobilization to specific communities. When eviction disperses a community, the mobilization infrastructure disperses with it. The research finding that eviction's biggest turnout effect is in low-eviction-rate neighborhoods likely reflects this mechanism: those neighborhoods have active civic mobilization networks that eviction disrupts.

**Cognitive and material resource depletion**: Research on scarcity (Mullainathan and Shafir, *Scarcity*, 2013) documents that material deprivation consumes cognitive bandwidth in ways that reduce performance across unrelated domains. The research is directly applicable to political participation: people managing eviction, housing loss, or the first weeks in a shelter are not tracking candidate positions or primary dates. This is not an argument about voter motivation — it is a finding about cognitive capacity under extreme stress.

**Criminalization producing legal barriers**: Grants Pass v. Johnson's authorization of anti-camping enforcement has enabled enforcement practices that directly preclude political participation. In multiple jurisdictions, people have lost voter registration cards, identification documents, and ballot materials through shelter confiscations and encampment sweeps. The National Law Center on Homelessness and Poverty has documented specific instances of ID documents and mail — including election mail — destroyed in sweeps. People who have been cited or arrested under anti-camping ordinances face court dates and potentially fines that further consume the time and resources required for civic engagement.

### 1.3 The Political Geography

The communities most affected by housing instability and eviction are not randomly distributed. They are:

- Disproportionately Black and Hispanic: Black renters are evicted at twice the rate of white renters with similar economic profiles (Eviction Lab, Princeton); Hispanic renters are evicted at 1.5 times the white rate
- Concentrated in competitive congressional districts: The housing crisis is most acute in metropolitan areas with large low-income renter populations — many of which are in swing districts that determine House majority control
- Overlapping with immigrant communities: Noncitizen family members of citizen voters face disproportionate housing insecurity, and eviction in these households suppresses citizen members' political participation through the network and cognitive mechanisms described above
- Overlapping with formerly incarcerated populations: The ABA's Human Rights analysis documents "double disenfranchisement" — formerly incarcerated people are nearly 10 times as likely to be homeless, and homelessness itself creates barriers to exercising restored voting rights

The political geography conclusion: the communities most exposed to housing instability-driven political exclusion are among the constituencies whose turnout most affects 2026 House majority outcomes.

## 2. The 2025–2026 Policy Architecture: Three Converging Tracks

### 2.1 Track 1: Emergency Housing Voucher Program Ending

The Emergency Housing Voucher (EHV) program was created by the American Rescue Plan Act in 2021 to provide 70,000 housing vouchers specifically for people experiencing homelessness, those fleeing domestic violence or trafficking, and people exiting foster care or incarceration. EHVs differed from regular Housing Choice Vouchers in that they came with dedicated supportive services funding and a targeted focus on the hardest-to-house populations.

**Current status (May 2026)**:
- HUD announced in March 2025 that EHV program funding would run out in late 2026, several years ahead of original projections, due to funding structure decisions made at the program's creation
- As of April 2026, approximately **47,000 EHVs remain actively leased** (down from ~59,000 in April 2025) — the decline reflects both voluntary exits and households losing vouchers as local housing authority funding runs dry
- Multiple cities — including Washington, D.C. — have reported that EHV expiration is forcing them to begin turning away newly homeless people from shelter rosters, as permanent housing placements that formerly absorbed shelter capacity are no longer being renewed
- Stateline (April 27, 2026) documented "cities and renters scrambling" as local housing authorities face the program's accelerating end

**Democratic participation implications**: EHV recipients are overwhelmingly the most politically marginalized Americans — people formerly incarcerated, formerly in foster care, formerly homeless. These are precisely the populations who have been systematically excluded from political participation through felony disenfranchisement, lack of stable address, and civic network disruption. EHV provided housing stability that, per the peer-reviewed evidence, is the prerequisite for voter registration and sustained participation. The program's ending will return 47,000 households to housing instability just as the 2026 midterm voter registration cycle peaks (August-September 2026 registration deadlines in most states).

**Sources**: [Stateline — emergency housing vouchers ending early (April 27, 2026)](https://stateline.org/2026/04/27/emergency-housing-vouchers-are-ending-early-leaving-cities-and-renters-scrambling/); [HUD EHV announcement (March 2025)](https://www.hud.gov/press/press_releases_media_advisories/HUD_No_25_132); [Streets Sense Media — DC housing voucher cuts (2026)](https://streetsensemedia.org/article/dc-housing-voucher-cuts-2026/)

### 2.2 Track 2: Work Requirements and Term Limits — The 3.3 Million Household Threat

**The proposed rule**: On March 2, 2026, HUD published a Notice of Proposed Rulemaking in the Federal Register (*Establishing Flexibility for Implementation of Work Requirements and Term Limits*) that would give public housing authorities and HUD-assisted multifamily property owners voluntary authority to:

1. **Work requirements**: Require "work-eligible" adults (defined as ages 18–61, not disabled, not pregnant, not enrolled in higher education) to engage in work activities for up to **40 hours per week** as a condition of housing assistance
2. **Term limits**: Impose minimum 2-year time limits on rental assistance for non-elderly, non-disabled households — meaning households that do not find employment or exit assistance within the term limit would lose housing

**Scale of impact (CBPP analysis)**:
- If a 2-year time limit were universally applied, an estimated **3.3 million people** would lose rental assistance
- Of those, **1.7 million are children**
- The rule is nominally voluntary for housing authorities, but federal political pressure and funding leverage mechanisms can effectively make "voluntary" into mandatory

**The Arkansas natural experiment analogy**: As documented in Domain 31 (Healthcare Access), the 2018 Arkansas Medicaid work requirement pilot — the most directly comparable policy — disenrolled 18,000 beneficiaries in 6 months with **zero measurable employment effect**. The peer-reviewed post-program analysis found that the mechanism operated as eligibility contraction, not behavior change. The people who lost Medicaid coverage did not get jobs; they lost healthcare. The structural prediction for housing work requirements is identical: the mechanism will function as assisted household contraction, removing people from housing assistance without producing the employment outcomes the policy claims to target.

**Comment period status**: The public comment period for the proposed rule closed **May 1, 2026**. The administration can finalize the rule following the comment period. Advocacy organizations have signaled readiness to litigate APA compliance and statutory authority.

**Disability rights intersection** (cross-reference Domain 41B): The rule explicitly defines "work-eligible" to exclude disabled people — but the disability determination is contested. Many people with disabilities do not receive formal disability determinations and would therefore be classified as work-eligible despite inability to work at the required level. AAHD (American Association on Health and Disability) documented specific harm to people with disabilities from the proposed rule's definitional gaps.

**Sources**: [Federal Register — proposed work requirements and term limits (March 2, 2026)](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/03/02/2026-04095/establishing-flexibility-for-implementation-of-work-requirements-and-term-limits); [CBPP — 3.3 million would lose rental assistance](https://www.cbpp.org/research/housing/house-bill-would-leave-over-400000-more-people-without-stable-affordable-housing); [NLIHC — HUD publishes NPRM, comments due May 1](https://nlihc.org/resource/hud-publishes-proposed-rulemaking-regarding-time-limits-and-work-requirements-new-nlihc); [CLASP — work requirements will destabilize millions](https://www.clasp.org/blog/hud-work-requirements-cut-destabilize-millions/); [AAHD — harm to people with disabilities](https://aahd.us/2026/05/hud-proposed-rental-assistance-work-requirements-and-term-limits-2-harm-to-people-with-disabilities/)

### 2.3 Track 3: Continuum of Care Overhaul — "Immediately Destabilizing and Disastrous"

**What the Continuum of Care program is**: The CoC program provides approximately $4 billion annually in federal grants to local "continuums" — nonprofit-government partnerships — that fund permanent supportive housing, transitional housing, and related services for people experiencing homelessness. The program's "Housing First" orientation — placing people in stable housing before requiring sobriety or treatment participation — has been validated by two decades of research as the most effective intervention for chronic homelessness.

**The November 2025 overhaul attempt**: HUD Secretary Scott Turner, acting under President Trump's executive order "Ending Crime and Disorder on American Streets," published a new CoC Notice of Funding Opportunity in November 2025 that would have:
- Cut the share of CoC grants available for permanent supportive housing from approximately 90% to a maximum of 30%
- Required sobriety, mental health treatment compliance, and other conditions as prerequisites for permanent housing placement
- Effectively ended the Housing First model by defunding its primary funding stream

**Two court interventions**:
- **January 9, 2026**: Following a December 23, 2025 court order, HUD reinstated the original Biden-era NOFO, acknowledging the court had found the overhaul's immediate implementation would cause irreversible harm
- **April 1, 2026**: A federal appeals court upheld a preliminary injunction blocking the overhaul, with the court writing that implementation "would be immediately destabilizing and disastrous." The ruling documented that multiple local homeless services providers had already stopped accepting new clients and stopped making new housing referrals because of uncertainty created by the funding change — demonstrating "serious real-world harm" from the threat alone, before a single dollar had been cut

**HUD's restart attempt**: Despite losing twice in court, HUD announced in May 2026 (Roll Call, May 4) that it intends to issue a new CoC Notice of Funding Opportunity by **June 1, 2026** and make awards by December 1, 2026. The new NOFO will presumably attempt to shift CoC funding conditions in a manner the administration believes will survive APA and statutory scrutiny. This is an active regulatory threat with a 23-day advocacy window for monitoring the NOFO terms.

**The 170,000 figure**: Advocates have consistently cited 170,000 people in federally subsidized housing at acute risk of losing their supportive housing if the CoC overhaul proceeds — a number drawn from the NLIHC's analysis of households currently in permanent supportive housing funded primarily through CoC grants.

**Sources**: [NPR — appeals court rejects HUD homelessness overhaul (April 1, 2026)](https://www.npr.org/2026/04/01/nx-s1-5770629/appeals-court-rejects-hud-homelessness-overhaul); [Shelterforce — judge blocks HUD overhaul (January 2026)](https://shelterforce.org/2026/01/23/judge-blocks-hud-overhaul-federal-funding-for-homelessness-services/); [Roll Call — HUD to try again on homelessness assistance (May 4, 2026)](https://rollcall.com/2026/05/04/hud-to-try-again-to-change-homelessness-assistance-program/); [Shelterforce — "a grim march toward death" (April 2026)](https://shelterforce.org/2026/04/20/what-huds-new-homeless-policy-looks-like-on-the-ground/); [National Alliance to End Homelessness — state and local impacts of HUD cuts (data visualization)](https://endhomelessness.org/resources/research-and-analysis/housingcuts/)

## 3. The Legal Architecture of Displacement: Four Structural Rollbacks

### 3.1 Grants Pass v. Johnson: Criminalization of Housing Instability

**The ruling (June 28, 2024)**: In a 6-3 decision authored by Justice Gorsuch, the Supreme Court held that local ordinances imposing criminal penalties on public sleeping and camping do not violate the Eighth Amendment's prohibition on cruel and unusual punishment — even in cities where no adequate shelter alternatives exist. The majority held that the Eighth Amendment regulates what penalties may be imposed for criminal acts, but does not determine which acts may be criminalized. The ordinances at issue in Grants Pass (Oregon) carried escalating penalties culminating in criminal trespass prosecution for repeat violators.

**Immediate enforcement acceleration**: The ruling's practical effect was rapid. California Governor Gavin Newsom issued an executive order within 30 days requiring state agencies to clear encampments. Cities across the country that had previously declined to enforce anti-camping ordinances out of legal concern now enforced them. The ACLU and National Law Center on Homelessness and Poverty documented a "sweeping wave of criminalization" in the months following the ruling.

**Democratic participation effects of anti-camping enforcement**: The connection between anti-camping enforcement and political exclusion operates through three documented mechanisms:
1. **Documentation destruction**: Encampment sweeps frequently destroy personal property including identification documents, voter registration cards, and election mail. Without ID, voter registration in most states is impossible.
2. **Community dispersal**: Established encampment communities develop informal civic organization, mutual aid networks, and occasional connections to voter registration outreach. Sweeps disperse these communities, destroying the social infrastructure that makes outreach possible.
3. **Criminal record creation**: Citations and arrests under anti-camping ordinances create criminal records that, in many states, restrict voting rights — directly extending the democratic exclusion architecture documented in Domain 7 (Rights and Civil Liberties) and Domain 42 (Drug Policy) to housing status.

**The local preemption pattern**: Several states have enacted laws prohibiting cities from adopting more protective homeless encampment policies — the same preemption dynamic documented in Domain 33 (State Legislative Autocratization). Texas, Florida, and Georgia have passed legislation mandating enforcement of anti-camping ordinances, eliminating the discretion that had previously allowed some cities to decline enforcement.

**Sources**: [SCOTUS opinion — City of Grants Pass v. Johnson, 23-175 (June 28, 2024)](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/23pdf/23-175_19m2.pdf); [CalMatters — Supreme Court gives cities more power to crack down (June 2024)](https://calmatters.org/housing/2024/06/california-homeless-camps-grants-pass-ruling/); [National Law Center on Homelessness and Poverty — criminalization tracker](https://nlihc.org/resource/15-2-voting-while-experiencing-homelessness-making-unhoused-voices-heard-in-our-democracy); [ABA Human Rights — double disenfranchisement (2025)](https://www.americanbar.org/groups/crsj/resources/human-rights/2025-march/voting-rights-for-unhoused-felony-records/)

### 3.2 AFFH Rescission: Eliminating the Fair Housing Structural Architecture

**The Affirmatively Furthering Fair Housing rule** required jurisdictions receiving HUD Community Development Block Grants and other federal housing funds to: (1) conduct an Assessment of Fair Housing analyzing local patterns of residential segregation, (2) identify impediments to fair housing choice, and (3) take affirmative steps to address documented segregation.

**Rescission timeline**:
- January 16, 2025: HUD published withdrawal of the proposed AFFH rule (Federal Register)
- February 2025: HUD terminated the existing AFFH rule
- June 3, 2025: HUD published a proposed rule to rescind Affirmative Fair Housing Marketing regulations
- September 2025: HUD rescinded guidance related to disparate impact liability and redlining under FHA

**What the rescission eliminated**: The AFFH rule was the only federal mechanism creating an affirmative obligation on localities to address residential segregation — rather than merely prohibiting individual discriminatory acts. Its rescission does not make discrimination legal; the Fair Housing Act's prohibitions remain. But it removes the structured policy process through which localities with documented segregation patterns were required to take action. The result is that the same patterns of concentrated poverty that the peer-reviewed evidence shows depress political participation — patterns documented in the MTO experiment, the Slee-Desmond research, and decades of fair housing scholarship — now have no federal mechanism requiring their mitigation.

**Sources**: [Federal Register — AFFH withdrawal (January 16, 2025)](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2025/01/16/2025-00981/affirmatively-furthering-fair-housing-withdrawal); [Federal Register — Affirmative Fair Housing Marketing rescission (June 3, 2025)](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2025/06/03/2025-09991/rescission-of-affirmative-fair-housing-marketing-regulations); [NAHB — HUD terminates AFFH rule (February 2025)](https://www.nahb.org/blog/2025/02/hud-affh-rule); [NCRC comment letter on AFFH rescission (2025)](https://ncrc.org/ncrc-submits-comment-letter-on-huds-2025-affirmatively-furthering-fair-housing-interim-final-rule/)

### 3.3 The 30-Day Eviction Notice Rollback

**The rule**: On February 26, 2026, HUD published an interim final rule revoking the requirement that property owners receiving HUD funding provide **30 days' written notice before initiating eviction proceedings for nonpayment of rent**. The rule would have affected more than **2 million households** receiving HUD-assisted housing.

**Why 30 days matters**: In the 30-day window, tenants can: apply for emergency rental assistance; access legal aid referrals; challenge incorrect charges; arrange alternative housing; complete voter registration at current address before move; and connect with social services that might prevent the eviction entirely. This is the "triage window" — the buffer that determines whether a payment shortfall becomes a brief crisis or a housing loss with all of its downstream political and civic consequences.

**Legal challenge and delay**: Three renter advocacy groups immediately filed suit, and HUD subsequently issued an "indefinite delay" of the rule's effective date pending consideration of public comments. As of May 2026, the 30-day notice requirement remains technically in place. But HUD is expected to finalize the rollback after the comment period, and the mere pendency of the rule has created chilling effects: some housing authorities began informing tenants of the coming change before it took effect.

**Sources**: [Federal Register — 30-day notice revocation interim rule (February 26, 2026)](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/02/26/2026-03921/revocation-of-the-30-day-notification-requirement-prior-to-termination-of-lease-for-nonpayment-of); [Federal Register — indefinite delay (March 13, 2026)](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/03/13/2026-04990/revocation-of-the-30-day-notification-requirement-prior-to-termination-of-lease-for-nonpayment-of); [Stateline — HUD seeks to reduce eviction notice time (March 2026)](https://stateline.org/2026/03/03/hud-seeks-to-reduce-time-allowed-for-tenants-to-receive-notice-before-evictions-for-nonpayment/); [Ballard Spahr — following lawsuit, HUD delays (March 2026)](https://www.ballardspahr.com/insights/alerts-and-articles/2026/03/following-lawsuit-hud-indefinitely-delays-revocation-of-30-day-notice-rule-for-nonpayment-lease-termination)

### 3.4 Budget Architecture: The $33 Billion Proposed Cut

**The FY2026 and FY2027 budget requests**: The Trump administration's FY2026 "skinny budget" proposed cutting HUD by $33.6 billion, consolidating Section 8 rental assistance, public housing, and housing for seniors and disabled people into a single block grant while eliminating $27 billion in dedicated funding. The FY2027 request has continued this direction. Key projected impacts from advocacy organization analyses:

- **181,900 fewer households served** under the House FY2026 HUD funding proposal
- **400,000+ people at increased homelessness risk** under CBPP's analysis of the House bill
- **Section 8 Housing Choice Vouchers**: Flat funding in a rising rental market is a real-terms cut; only 1 in 4 eligible households receives rental assistance under current funding levels

**What actually passed**: Congress ultimately passed an FY2026 spending bill that included enough funding to renew Emergency Housing Vouchers created during COVID — a partial preservation. But the EHV program's structural funding expiration remains and the FY2027 budget process is underway with HUD cuts as a continuing administration priority.

**The block grant conversion risk**: Converting specific housing assistance programs into a single block grant — the recurring HUD budget proposal — eliminates categorical program protections, enables states to redirect funds toward alternative uses, and removes the statutory requirements that create litigation standing for housing advocates. Block grant conversion is the same mechanism the OBBBA used for Medicaid (analyzed in Domain 31) — it converts enforceable individual entitlements into discretionary state allocations, eliminating the legal architecture through which courts have protected housing access.

**Sources**: [Multifamily Dive — Trump proposes cutting $33B in HUD funding (2025)](https://www.multifamilydive.com/news/trump-budget-housing-HUD-cuts/747789/); [CBPP — House bill would leave 400,000+ without affordable housing](https://www.cbpp.org/research/housing/house-bill-would-leave-over-400000-more-people-without-stable-affordable-housing); [Housing Finance — budget proposal slashes HUD programs](https://www.housingfinance.com/policy-legislation/budget-proposal-slashes-hud-programs_o); [NLIHC — FY26 HUD spending bill](https://nlihc.org/resource/federal-government-reopened-congress-must-now-pass-fy26-hud-spending-bill-protect-vouchers)

## 4. The Racial Disparity Structure

The housing crisis, like the drug enforcement crisis (Domain 42) and the criminal justice system analyzed in Domain 16, does not fall equally on all communities. The racial disparity structure is documented at each layer:

**Eviction**: Black renters are evicted at **twice the rate** of white renters with similar economic profiles, controlling for income, rent burden, and housing quality (Eviction Lab, Princeton University). Hispanic renters are evicted at approximately 1.5 times the white rate. These disparities are not explained by income alone — they reflect the ongoing operation of discriminatory practices in rental housing markets that the AFFH rule was designed to address.

**Housing voucher access**: HUD's own data documents that Black and Hispanic renters are disproportionately represented in Housing Choice Voucher (Section 8) households. Budget cuts that reduce voucher availability fall disproportionately on these communities.

**Homelessness**: Unsheltered homelessness disproportionately affects Black Americans: Black people constitute 13% of the general population but 37% of the homeless population and 48% of people in shelters (HUD Annual Homeless Assessment Report, 2023). Native American and Alaska Native people are similarly overrepresented at 2-3x their population share.

**Post-Grants Pass enforcement**: Anti-camping enforcement following the Grants Pass ruling has been disproportionately directed at encampments in communities of color. The National Homelessness Law Center has documented that sweeps in cities including Phoenix, Denver, and Los Angeles have disproportionately targeted encampments in neighborhoods of color, raising Equal Protection questions beyond the Eighth Amendment question that Grants Pass resolved.

**The democratic participation feedback loop**: The racial disparity structure creates a reinforcing cycle that is analytically distinct from individual discrimination. Racially concentrated poverty (itself the product of historical discrimination in housing, credit, and public investment) produces higher eviction rates → eviction depresses voter turnout → lower voter turnout reduces political representation → political underrepresentation reduces policy investment in community stability → higher eviction rates persist. The AFFH rule was the only federal mechanism designed to interrupt this cycle at the structural level rather than the individual-complaint level. Its rescission removes the structural intervention.

## 5. International Comparative Evidence

Democratic peer countries have pursued fundamentally different housing security architectures with measurable effects on civic participation:

**Finland (Housing First at national scale)**: Since 2008, Finland has implemented a national Housing First policy that provides permanent housing without preconditions to all chronically homeless people. Finland achieved near-elimination of long-term homelessness by 2023. The civic participation implications are secondary benefits: when people have stable addresses, they vote, they access services, and they participate in local civic life. Finland's approach demonstrates that the Housing First model rejected by the Trump administration's CoC overhaul is not a failed experiment — it is the only empirically validated approach to ending chronic homelessness.

**Canada (Universal basic services framing)**: Canada's 2019 National Housing Strategy explicitly frames housing as a human right and deploys housing investment as both a social service and a democratic participation infrastructure. The National League of Cities in the US has cited Canada's approach in advocacy for stabilizing the U.S. HCV system.

**United Kingdom (Social housing as civic infrastructure)**: The UK's social housing system, despite severe capacity limitations following austerity-era cuts, maintains address-based voter registration that automatically updates when tenants move between social housing — a design choice that treats housing mobility as a voter registration event rather than a voter registration barrier. The UK system's explicit connection between social housing tenancy and civic enrollment is the design architecture that the U.S. NVRA was intended to approximate but has not achieved.

**Germany (Tenant protection law as constitutional norm)**: Germany's Basic Law includes social rights protections that have been interpreted to require government action on housing security. The *Bundesverfassungsgericht* (Federal Constitutional Court) has ruled that the state has positive obligations to protect people from homelessness, a legal framework with no analog in U.S. constitutional law. German tenants facing eviction have substantially longer notice periods, right to counsel, and court review as a matter of statutory right.

## 6. Reform Architecture

### 6.1 Immediate Reforms (2026 Advocacy Window)

**EHV program extension**: Congress must appropriate funds to extend the Emergency Housing Voucher program through at least 2028. The midterm election context makes this a direct voting-rights issue: EHV expiration during the peak 2026 voter registration window will deprive 47,000 households of the stable address that is the prerequisite for voter registration. The advocacy argument is not "housing is good" — it is "housing stability is a voting rights precondition."

**CoC NOFO monitoring (June 1, 2026 target date)**: The new CoC NOFO expected by June 1 must be reviewed immediately upon publication for condition changes that would reimpose the "disastrous" overhaul blocked by courts in January and April 2026. If the new NOFO contains the same substantive conditions under a different procedural vehicle, immediate litigation by the same coalition that prevailed twice is the appropriate response.

**HUD work requirements rule opposition**: The comment period closed May 1, 2026. Advocacy organizations should prepare for rulemaking finalization challenges under the APA and under statutory authority analysis. The Arkansas Medicaid precedent (18,000 people lost coverage with zero employment effect) is directly applicable and should be the centerpiece of any legal challenge.

**30-day notice restoration**: Litigation to permanently restore the 30-day eviction notice requirement is warranted under the APA. The existing indefinite delay is not a permanent victory. The comment period created a record that, if HUD finalizes the rollback, supports APA challenge on both procedural and substantive grounds.

### 6.2 Structural Reforms (Medium-Term)

**AFFH restoration**: Congressional reinstatement of the AFFH rule with statutory backing would prevent administrative rescission in a future administration. The statutory language could explicitly connect AFFH compliance to voter registration access — requiring jurisdictions to demonstrate not only fair housing planning but also voter registration infrastructure accessible to housing-assisted populations.

**NVRA expansion at housing assistance points of contact**: The National Voter Registration Act already requires certain agencies to offer voter registration when providing social services. Explicit extension of NVRA requirements to housing authorities and HUD-funded agencies — including automatic re-registration upon address change for housing assistance recipients — would directly address the voter registration gap that the MTO research documented.

**Civil right to counsel in eviction proceedings**: Eleven jurisdictions (New York City, San Francisco, Philadelphia, and others) have enacted local right-to-counsel programs for eviction proceedings. Federal legislation establishing a civil right to counsel in eviction proceedings would reduce the eviction rate (studies show right-to-counsel reduces eviction outcomes by 70-80% in cases where tenants are represented), directly reducing the political participation suppression documented by Slee and Desmond.

**Decriminalization of homelessness**: Federal legislation establishing that camping on public property without adequate shelter alternatives cannot be criminalized — effectively codifying the constitutional standard that the pre-Grants Pass Ninth Circuit had applied — would require legislative action given the current Supreme Court majority's interpretation. A federal minimum floor preventing criminalization where shelter capacity is documented as inadequate would preserve democratic participation pathways for the most politically excluded population.

**Federal housing as a human right**: Several nations have enacted constitutional or statutory housing rights that create enforceable claims to adequate housing. Without constitutional amendment, the most achievable U.S. version is a statutory framework establishing housing assistance as an entitlement for households at or below 30% of area median income — converting the current discretionary voucher system into a mandatory program similar to Medicaid's structure.

### 6.3 Connection to Voting Rights Reform

The democracy-housing nexus is most precisely articulated as a voting rights enforcement question. Domain 1 (Voting Rights) documents the SAVE Act, proof-of-citizenship requirements, and voter roll purges. Domain 47's structural finding is that these formal barriers to voting operate on a population that has already been rendered unable to vote by the practical barriers of housing instability — making formal voting rights reforms insufficient without simultaneous housing stability reforms. The communities facing discriminatory voter roll purges are disproportionately the same communities facing discriminatory eviction enforcement.

The coherent reform package treats housing stability and voting rights as jointly necessary: neither domain's reforms produce full democratic participation without the other.
