## Executive Summary

The immigration enforcement and detention infrastructure assembled by the Trump administration since January 2025 is not simply a policy choice within the normal range of executive discretion over immigration. It is a structural transformation of state power that has four interlocking democratic accountability failures. First, it has built a detention apparatus — now targeting 100,000 beds at a cost of $38 billion — that operates in conditions of systematic impunity: 18 deaths in the first four months of 2026, the detention ombudsman permanently closed, congressional inspection rights that required a court order to restore, and a private contractor ecosystem generating over $2 billion in annual revenue from the captive population. Second, it has deployed enforcement mechanisms — 1,783 287(g) agreements with local police, a recalibrated enforcement approach following public backlash, and the documented use of immigration enforcement near protest sites — whose primary documented democratic effect is suppressing political participation by immigrant and citizen communities simultaneously. Third, it has engaged in systematic defiance of judicial authority at a scale Chief Judge Patrick Schiltz characterized as unprecedented in American history — 96 court order violations in January 2026 alone, criminal contempt proceedings threatened, and the Supreme Court's unanimous ruling in the Abrego Garcia case functionally defied while the administration was technically "complying" with a different order in the same case. Fourth, it has used the immigration enforcement apparatus as an instrument of political retaliation — most explicitly in the Abrego Garcia vindictive prosecution, which a federal judge found may have been triggered by Abrego Garcia's legal success in challenging his wrongful deportation, and in the broader pattern documented by ProPublica of individuals targeted for their political activity or advocacy work.

The democratic design problem this domain identifies is distinct from the policy debate about immigration levels, enforcement priorities, or border security. This domain's claim is structural: when a government constructs an enforcement apparatus capable of detaining 100,000 people, with minimal judicial oversight, operating outside normal accountability mechanisms, funded by legislation passed on a single-vote margin, and deployed in ways that demonstrably suppress political participation by communities the administration views as opposition constituencies — that is no longer a policy dispute. It is the construction of an instrument of authoritarian state power, even if the individuals detained are noncitizens who have no formal democratic standing.

The international comparative record is unambiguous: every democracy that has built large-scale administrative detention infrastructure with reduced judicial oversight has experienced that infrastructure being turned against politically disfavored groups within 10–15 years of its construction, often well before the demographic target that justified its construction expanded to include citizens. The reform agenda this domain proposes — not abolition of immigration enforcement but structural redesign of the accountability architecture — is grounded in that comparative evidence and in the core democratic design principle that no enforcement infrastructure should exist outside meaningful judicial oversight, independent accountability, and legislative control.

## 1. The Democratic Accountability Problem — Four Structural Failures

### 1.1 What Makes This a Democratic Design Problem, Not a Policy Disagreement

Immigration enforcement level and priority are legitimate subjects of democratic disagreement. The United States has, at various points, prioritized enforcement at the border, interior enforcement of criminal records, employer sanctions, and visa overstay enforcement. Different administrations have made different choices within the framework of congressional authorization. That is normal democratic variation.

The current situation is structurally different in four ways that convert a policy disagreement into a democratic design crisis:

**Scale of discretionary executive power**: The One Big Beautiful Bill Act, signed July 4, 2025, appropriated $45 billion for ICE detention — a 308% annual increase over FY2024 — and $170 billion total for immigration enforcement over four years. It did this through budget reconciliation, passing on single-vote margins in both chambers, without the deliberation required for normal authorization legislation. The resulting funding creates a resource base that insulates ICE from normal appropriations oversight: the agency is now on track to obligate 75% of its $191 billion OBBBA allocation by September 2026, far ahead of pace, building physical infrastructure and contractual obligations that no future administration can easily reverse.

**Systematic judicial accountability evasion**: In January 2026 alone, a federal judge documented 96 violations of court orders by ICE — with a subsequent finding of 113 additional violations. Chief Judge Schiltz stated he was "not aware of another occasion in the history of the United States in which a federal court has had to threaten contempt — again and again and again — to force the United States government to comply with court orders." The Brennan Center noted the last comparable pattern of executive defiance of court orders was Southern governors refusing to integrate schools after *Brown v. Board of Education*. This is not a dispute about the scope of executive discretion — it is systematic defiance of judicial orders that courts have already issued.

**Destruction of oversight infrastructure**: OIDO — the congressionally-created independent watchdog for immigration detention — was reduced from 100+ staff to 5 through DOGE cuts, then permanently closed May 5, 2026, as the detention system was simultaneously expanding to 58,000–72,000 people and recording 18 deaths in four months. Congressional inspection rights were blocked until a court ordered DHS to restore them. The pattern is not administrative efficiency — it is the systematic removal of the accountability infrastructure that makes democratic oversight of executive enforcement possible.

**Deployment for political effect**: The documented use of 287(g) agreements to create a chilling effect on immigrant community political participation, the administration's framing of ICE deployment near protests as "training" for the 2026 midterms, the Abrego Garcia vindictive prosecution, and the broader pattern of targeting immigration enforcement at politically active communities — documented in the American Immigration Council's report on "indiscriminate arrests and propaganda" — constitute evidence that the enforcement apparatus is being deployed as a tool of political power, not merely law enforcement.

### 1.2 The Democratic Stakes Beyond Immigration Policy

The detention infrastructure being built in 2025–2026 will outlast its current demographic purpose. This is the central comparative lesson of 20th-century democratic history: administrative detention infrastructure, once built, does not remain confined to its original target population. Japanese American internment began with "national security" grounds and expanded to 120,000 people on the basis of ancestry. Post-9/11 material witness detention was designed for terrorism cases and was applied to 70+ individuals, most of whom were never charged. The PATRIOT Act's surveillance provisions, designed for foreign terrorist monitoring, became the architecture for mass domestic surveillance revealed by Snowden in 2013.

The 100,000-bed detention infrastructure currently under construction — with weakened judicial oversight, closed watchdog institutions, and a demonstrated pattern of defying court orders — is a permanent capability. The political coalition that builds it cannot control who governs the country in 2029 or 2033, and cannot control what that future government decides to do with 100,000 detention beds, 1,783 local police partnerships, and $38 billion in physical infrastructure.

## 2. The Detention Infrastructure: Scale, Architecture, and Accountability Gaps

### 2.1 The $38 Billion Detention Reengineering Initiative

The ICE Detention Reengineering Initiative (DRI), announced February 2026, aims to achieve 92,600 detention beds by November 30, 2026 — up from approximately 40,000 at the start of 2025. The initiative allocates $38 billion toward a new detention center model with two facility types:

**Large-Scale Detention Facilities (Mega-Centers)**: Designed to hold 7,000 to 10,000 people for periods averaging under 60 days. ICE initially planned to buy 8 such facilities. By May 2026, ICE had spent $690 million acquiring at least seven industrial buildings — warehouses — in Maryland, Arizona, Georgia, Texas, Pennsylvania, and Michigan. The pivot from new construction to warehouse acquisition reflects a deliberate strategy: industrial buildings can be acquired under commercial purchase authorities that avoid the public input requirements of the Federal Acquisition Regulation and normal environmental review. The Marketplace investigation (May 5, 2026) documented that ICE is using military contracting tools specifically designed to bypass public input processes.

**Strategy Pivot — May 2026**: By May 7, 2026, Axios reported that ICE was recalibrating away from the mega-center model after significant backlash — not primarily from Democrats but from Republican elected officials in affected states who opposed facility siting in their districts. ICE is now exploring purchasing existing detention facilities directly from its two largest vendors, GeoGroup and CoreCivic — both of which reported over $2 billion in revenue in 2025, with CoreCivic up 18% year-over-year.

**Legal challenges**: Multiple states have filed suits under the National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) challenging the warehouse conversions. Maryland won a temporary restraining order blocking renovation of a Washington County facility; the federal district judge found Maryland likely to succeed on the merits of its APA/NEPA claim that the project was improperly categorically excluded from environmental review. Similar suits are pending in New Jersey, Michigan, and Arizona.

### 2.2 The Private Contractor Ecosystem

ICE's detention system is almost entirely operated by private companies. GeoGroup and CoreCivic together operate the majority of ICE detention beds. The OBBBA's $45 billion in detention funding represents a windfall for these contractors: CoreCivic's 18% revenue growth in 2025 tracks directly to the detention expansion.

The accountability problem is not that private companies operate detention facilities — that is a governance choice that can be regulated. The accountability problem is that private operation combined with weakened oversight creates a specific failure mode: contractors' incentive is to reduce per-bed costs (which means reducing medical care, food quality, staffing ratios) while the government's oversight capacity has been simultaneously eliminated. OIDO's closure removes the one institution specifically designed to investigate contractor compliance with detention standards. The pattern documented in the ACLU's detention deaths report — 95% of ICE detention deaths between 2017–2021 were preventable with adequate medical care — reflects this structural failure, not isolated incidents.

The Brennan Center's May 2026 budget analysis documented the accountability gap at the institutional level: ICE is now the highest-funded federal law enforcement agency, with its budget having grown faster than any other federal agency, while simultaneously facing the weakest oversight architecture of any major law enforcement agency.

### 2.3 Detention Conditions and Death Rates

**Mortality**: 32 people died in ICE custody in 2025 — the deadliest year since 2004. Through the first four months of 2026, 18 deaths have been reported, putting 2026 on track for a record. With OIDO closed, every death review that would previously have been conducted by an independent watchdog is now handled by DHS's own Inspector General — an office that itself lost substantial staff through the 17+ IG firings of January 2025 and subsequent DOGE cuts.

**Conditions documentation**: Between January 20, 2025 and January 12, 2026, credible reports included: 206 cases of medical neglect, 88 reports of physical and sexual abuse, 139 reports of denial of adequate food or water. Senator Ossoff's January 2026 report to Congress documented these patterns in detail. After OIDO's closure, these reports now go directly to DHS, the same agency being reported on.

**Medical care**: The ACLU's analysis found that 95% of detention deaths between 2017–2021 were preventable with adequate medical care. ICE detention facilities are not required to meet the same medical standards as federal prisons — they operate under a voluntary Performance-Based National Detention Standards (PBNDS) framework that ICE itself administers. The inspection regime collapsed alongside OIDO.

### 2.4 The Congressional Oversight Fight

In January 2026, DHS began blocking members of Congress from conducting unannounced oversight inspections of ICE detention facilities — a practice Congress had exercised for decades under its constitutional oversight authority. Democracy Forward filed suit on behalf of 13 members of Congress; a federal court ordered DHS to restore unannounced congressional inspection rights. DHS complied with the court order.

The episode documents a pattern: the administration's default posture is to eliminate oversight until a court orders its restoration. This is not a dispute about whether Congress has oversight authority — that is settled constitutional law. It is a testing strategy: push the boundary, restore when ordered, and monitor whether the pattern generates political cost.

## 3. The 287(g) Expansion — Local Police as Immigration Agents

### 3.1 Scale of Expansion

287(g) agreements — named for the section of the Immigration and Nationality Act that authorizes them — allow state and local law enforcement to exercise immigration enforcement functions with ICE training and supervision. Before January 2025, 135 agencies in 20 states had 287(g) agreements. As of May 8, 2026, ICE has signed 1,783 Memoranda of Agreement covering 39 states and 2 U.S. territories — a 1,200% increase in 16 months.

The program now includes three models: jail enforcement (immigration screening of individuals already in custody), task force (joint patrol operations with ICE agents), and a new "warrant service officer" model in which local officers can conduct immigration enforcement on their own initiative. ICE is providing substantial financial incentives: the FWD.us analysis estimated total 287(g) payments to local agencies could reach $2 billion in 2026.

### 3.2 The Democratic Effect — Chilling Political Participation

The documented democratic effect of 287(g) expansion is a chilling effect on political participation in immigrant-present communities. This is not speculative:

**Direct evidence**: The American Immigration Council's analysis of the chilling effect documented that "the threat itself of aggressive immigration enforcement imposes fear on immigrant and minority communities," reducing participation in activities — including going to work, attending school, and attending protests — that serve as foundational civic activities even for citizen family members of immigration targets.

**Word in Black analysis**: A February 2026 analysis documented the explicit connection between ICE enforcement tactics and voter suppression, noting that "a show of force by any combination of federal law enforcement, the National Guard, or active-duty military would have an immediate and chilling effect on turnout" — and that chilling effects on voter turnout cannot be remedied after the fact.

**The Domain 37 connection**: Domain 37 (Federal Executive Interference in 2026 Midterms) documents Steve Bannon's explicit framing of ICE airport deployments as "perfect training" for November 2026. The 1,783 287(g) agreements create a nationwide infrastructure through which ICE can coordinate local law enforcement presence near polling places, even without federal agents directly. The DHS position, as of May 2026, does not foreclose election-day ICE deployment.

**State resistance**: As of May 2026, nine states have enacted legislation banning 287(g) participation: California, Connecticut, Illinois, Oregon, Vermont, Washington, New Mexico, Maine, and Maryland. This represents the primary effective resistance strategy at the state level — matching the constitutional principle that states can refuse to conscript local law enforcement for federal purposes.

### 3.3 The Racial Profiling Record

The 287(g) program was partially discontinued in 2012 after the Justice Department found participating agencies had engaged in what appeared to be racial profiling. The expansion of the program under the current administration, to 1,783 agreements, has occurred without the due process safeguards that led to the partial discontinuation in 2012. The ACLU has documented "dragnet immigration enforcement" in some cities with 287(g) agreements — enforcement that targets individuals based on national origin, ethnicity, or Spanish-language use rather than specific enforcement indicators.

## 4. Court Order Defiance — The Rule-of-Law Crisis

### 4.1 The January 2026 Contempt Crisis

The most consequential rule-of-law development in the immigration enforcement sphere was Chief Judge Patrick Schiltz's January–February 2026 findings in the District of Minnesota. The sequence:

- ICE began large-scale enforcement operations in Minnesota in January 2026, during which Renee Macklin Good (a U.S. citizen) and Alex Pretti (a U.S. citizen) were killed, and Julio Cesar Sosa-Celis was wounded, in separate incidents.
- Federal courts issued multiple orders restraining specific enforcement tactics. ICE violated 96 of these orders in January 2026.
- Judge Schiltz found 113 additional violations "above and beyond" the initial count.
- Schiltz threatened criminal contempt proceedings — something no federal court had done against the federal government in modern immigration enforcement history. He stated he was "not aware of another occasion in the history of the United States in which a federal court has had to threaten contempt — again and again and again — to force the United States government to comply with court orders."
- ICE agents testified in proceedings that they were "receiving conflicting guidance" about whether to follow injunctions — establishing a pattern in which the administration's internal communications were functionally overriding judicial orders.

The Brennan Center's comparative framework is the appropriate analytical lens: the last time the United States saw this pattern of open executive defiance of judicial authority was Southern governors blocking school integration after *Brown v. Board of Education*. In that case, President Eisenhower ultimately dispatched the 101st Airborne to enforce the court's order. In the current situation, the president is the party directing defiance of the court orders.

### 4.2 The Abrego Garcia Case — Vindictive Prosecution as Enforcement Instrument

The Kilmar Abrego Garcia case demonstrates the most direct evidence that the immigration enforcement apparatus is being used as a tool of retaliation against individuals who successfully challenge executive authority.

**The facts**: Abrego Garcia was granted "withholding of removal" in 2019 — a judicial determination that he could not be deported to El Salvador due to gang threats against his family. He was deported anyway on March 12–15, 2025, to the CECOT maximum-security facility. The DHS admitted the deportation was an "administrative error." The Supreme Court, in a unanimous ruling authored by Chief Justice Roberts, affirmed the lawlessness of the removal and ordered the administration to "facilitate and effectuate" his return. Abrego Garcia was eventually returned to the US in June 2025.

**The prosecution**: Upon his return, the government charged Abrego Garcia with federal smuggling offenses. The charges were filed after his successful legal challenge to the deportation. A DOJ email labeled the prosecution a "top priority" in explicit reference to the court case. Deputy Attorney General Todd Blanche made public statements about the case that U.S. District Judge Waverly Crenshaw characterized as exhibiting grounds for "vindictiveness" claims.

**The ruling**: On or around May 2026, Judge Crenshaw found there is a "realistic likelihood of vindictiveness" — the legal standard for dismissal of a prosecution initiated to retaliate against the defendant for exercising legal rights. The court ordered discovery and an evidentiary hearing. A former federal prosecutor stated publicly that the DOJ knew the charges would likely be seen as vindictive, and proceeded anyway.

**The democratic design implication**: A government that uses criminal prosecution as a deterrent against individuals who successfully challenge its authority in court has crossed a categorical line. This is not a dispute about the merits of any immigration enforcement action — it is the use of prosecutorial power to punish the successful invocation of constitutional rights. Every individual who observes the Abrego Garcia pattern and decides not to challenge an enforcement action has been effectively deterred from constitutional protection — which is precisely the intended effect.

### 4.3 The Pattern of Supreme Court Defiance

The administration's defiance of court orders is not limited to immigration — Domain 6 (Judicial Independence) and the litigation tracker document the broader pattern. But immigration has been the primary theater because: (1) immigration detainees have the weakest political standing to resist; (2) the speed of deportation flights means defiance can be completed before courts intervene; and (3) the administration has specifically constructed a legal theory that AUMF authority and the Alien Enemies Act remove immigration enforcement from ordinary judicial jurisdiction.

The Supreme Court's March 2025 ruling blocking the use of the Alien Enemies Act for deportation flights — and the administration's subsequent efforts to relitigate the same issue through alternative procedural channels — establishes the administration's method: obtain each ruling by the narrowest possible procedural means, then find an alternative pathway that was not specifically foreclosed. This is not good-faith compliance with judicial authority; it is a strategy of finding the gaps.

## 5. The Oversight Architecture — What Has Been Destroyed

### 5.1 Office of Immigration Detention Ombudsman (OIDO)

OIDO was created by Congress in the 2019 National Defense Authorization Act specifically because the ordinary DHS oversight architecture — the Inspector General, the Office for Civil Rights and Civil Liberties — had proven insufficient for detention-specific oversight. OIDO's specific mandate: investigate detainee deaths, investigate claims of medical neglect and physical abuse, and provide a direct reporting pathway for detained individuals who face language barriers, limited legal representation, and restricted communication.

**What the closure means in practice**:

Every person who dies in ICE detention will now be investigated by the same agency responsible for their detention, using internal review processes that OIDO was created to supplement because those processes were already documented as inadequate.

Every person who experiences medical neglect in one of the 58,000+ detention beds now has one fewer independent reporting pathway.

The 206 medical neglect cases, 88 abuse cases, and 139 denial-of-food-or-water cases documented in the 2025 fiscal year will have no institutional equivalent in 2026 because OIDO does not exist.

DHS's stated justification — a "funding lapse" — is disputed by the legislative record. The appropriations bill did not mandate the closure; DHS reduced OIDO's staff from 100+ to 5 through DOGE cuts that preceded the "funding lapse," then cited the funding lapse as a justification for permanent closure. This is the same pattern as the IG firing: use administrative tools to reduce an oversight office to functional incapacity, then claim the capacity gap as a justification for eliminating it permanently.

### 5.2 Congressional Oversight

Congress's constitutional oversight authority over executive agencies, including DHS and ICE, is clear under both the Constitution (Article I, Section 8) and statutory law. The administration's default posture — blocking congressional inspections of detention facilities until a court ordered restoration — is not a legal dispute about the scope of congressional authority; it is operational defiance pending a court order.

The House Oversight Committee has requested numerous ICE accountability documents — inspection reports, use-of-force data, internal communications about court order compliance. Responses have been systematically incomplete or delayed. Democracy Forward's successful litigation to restore unannounced congressional inspections provides a model: when Congress is willing to file suit to enforce its own oversight authority, courts will order compliance.

### 5.3 The Inspector General Degradation

The broader IG context (Domain 26) compounds the OIDO closure. Seventeen or more IGs were fired in January 2025. The CIGIE — the Council of the Inspectors General on Integrity and Efficiency, which coordinates IG standards — was subject to an attempted dismantlement. By early 2026, IG offices across federal government had lost 16.6% of their workforce, with replacements often carrying administration ties. DHS's IG — the entity that would nominally inherit OIDO's functions — is operating at reduced capacity and credibility simultaneously with record-high detention death rates.

## 6. State Resistance and Federal-State Conflict

### 6.1 Sanctuary Jurisdictions — What Works and What Doesn't

Sanctuary policies — under which local law enforcement does not share information with ICE or honor immigration detainer requests beyond their legal obligation — operate as the primary effective state resistance tool against the 287(g) expansion. Nine states have banned 287(g) participation. Approximately 500 jurisdictions maintain some form of sanctuary policy.

**Legal basis**: The Tenth Amendment anti-commandeering doctrine, established in *Printz v. United States* (1997), holds that the federal government cannot compel state and local law enforcement to carry out federal programs. This is the legal foundation for sanctuary policies. Federal courts have repeatedly upheld the right of jurisdictions to decline voluntary 287(g) participation.

**What the administration is doing in response**: The DOJ has sued sanctuary jurisdictions and published a "list of sanctuary jurisdictions" as a naming-and-shaming mechanism. Congress is considering the End Sanctuary Cities Act of 2026 and the Shut Down Sanctuary Policies Act of 2026, which would impose criminal penalties on officials who refuse to cooperate with ICE. These bills have not yet passed the Senate. The "incentivization" approach — paying local agencies to adopt 287(g) — is the more likely durable strategy, because it works with Tenth Amendment constraints rather than against them.

**Evidence on effectiveness**: States with sanctuary policies have consistently shown that enforcement cooperation does not meaningfully improve public safety outcomes while it does demonstrably damage immigrant community trust in local law enforcement. The 2012 discontinuation of part of the 287(g) program — following DOJ findings of racial profiling — established that the enforcement-public safety tradeoff is unfavorable in contexts where profiling operates. The 2026 expansion has occurred without reinstating the safeguards that led to the partial 2012 discontinuation.

### 6.2 State-Level Legal Challenges to Detention Infrastructure

The NEPA litigation by Maryland, New Jersey, Michigan, and Arizona against ICE warehouse purchases is the most direct legal challenge to the Detention Reengineering Initiative. The legal theory — that ICE's categorical exclusion claims for warehouse conversions are arbitrary and capricious under the APA — has already obtained a temporary restraining order in Maryland.

State AGs have broader authority here than in most federal enforcement contexts: NEPA's environmental review requirements apply to federal actions with significant environmental effects, and state AGs can argue that the federal government failed to comply with federal law — a classic APA claim that avoids Supremacy Clause preemption because the states are not trying to substitute their judgment for federal enforcement but rather to enforce the federal environmental review requirements the agency itself is required to follow.

## 7. The Immigration Court System — Due Process Failure at Scale

### 7.1 The 3.3 Million Case Backlog

The immigration court system — operated by the Executive Office for Immigration Review (EOIR), which is part of the Department of Justice — had a backlog of 3,318,099 active cases as of February 2026. This is not merely an administrative inefficiency; it is a structural due process failure:

- 81.7% of completed deportation cases in March 2026 resulted in a removal order. This high rate reflects in part the fact that many individuals are unrepresented: only 32.8% of immigrants in removal proceedings have legal representation, and immigrants without attorneys are removed at substantially higher rates than those with counsel.
- Average wait time for a merits hearing is now measured in years in most jurisdictions. This creates a legal limbo for millions of people: not legally authorized to be removed (they have pending cases) but not legally authorized to remain (their final status has not been determined). 
- The Trump administration's use of "voluntary departure" notices — orders telling individuals to report for removal — combined with administrative closure of cases in which individuals have pending appeals has created a situation in which people are told to report for deportation before their legal options have been exhausted.

### 7.2 The Role of Legal Representation

The data on legal representation is the single most actionable finding in immigration court system reform. Immigrants with legal representation are removed at dramatically lower rates than those without. Yet only 32.8% of individuals in removal proceedings have an attorney, and the immigration legal services ecosystem has been targeted by the administration:

- The Trump DOJ terminated contracts with nonprofit organizations providing immigration legal assistance in detention facilities — removing legal aid from individuals who have the least ability to access alternative representation.
- The administration's funding cuts to Legal Services Corporation (LSC) have reduced the civil legal aid capacity that immigrants could access through LSC-funded organizations.
- Several law school immigration clinics have faced administrative pressure and in some cases donor pressure as a result of the administration's targeting of higher education institutions (Domain 27).

The Vera Institute of Justice's recommendation — targeted legal representation funding for represented individuals in removal proceedings — has the strongest evidence base of any immigration court reform proposal. Every dollar spent on representation saves multiple dollars in unnecessary detention and improper removal.

## 8. Reform Architecture — Democratic Accountability Redesign

### 8.1 Core Reform Principles

Any reform of the immigration enforcement and detention architecture that restores democratic accountability must operate on four principles, all of which are consistent with strong enforcement of immigration law:

**Principle 1 — Judicial independence from enforcement speed**: The current system's principal accountability failure is that deportation can be completed faster than courts can intervene. The solution is not simply enjoining individual flights but creating structural barriers to judicial evasion: mandatory advance notice to courts before deportation of individuals with pending cases, a 48-hour stay right for anyone who files a habeas petition, and criminal contempt authority that is exercised — not threatened — when court orders are defied.

**Principle 2 — Oversight infrastructure proportionate to enforcement scale**: The OBBBA appropriated $45 billion for detention. Effective oversight of a 100,000-bed detention system requires OIDO (or equivalent) staffed at hundreds of investigators, not five. The oversight infrastructure must scale with the enforcement infrastructure. Congressional creation of a mandatory Inspector General function for immigration detention, with independent funding stream, is the structural fix.

**Principle 3 — Independent accountability from private operators**: The private contractor model — GeoGroup and CoreCivic operating the majority of beds — creates an inherent conflict between the profit motive and the constitutional obligation to provide adequate conditions. Independent third-party inspection of detention conditions, by organizations with genuine adversarial authority rather than contractor-managed access, is the minimum requirement for accountability.

**Principle 4 — Prohibition on enforcement use for political effect**: The documented pattern of 287(g) deployment as voter suppression infrastructure, ICE deployment near polling places framed as "training" for elections, and vindictive prosecution of individuals who succeed in legal challenges against enforcement actions requires a structural prohibition. Congressional legislation establishing that immigration enforcement cannot be coordinated with electoral outcomes, and that prosecutorial decisions cannot be made on the basis of an individual's legal challenges to enforcement, provides the statutory floor.

### 8.2 Specific Structural Reforms

**Judicial oversight mechanisms**:
- Mandatory 48-hour judicial notice before removal of any individual with a pending court proceeding
- Automatic temporary stay of removal upon filing of habeas petition
- Criminal contempt authority specifically applicable to immigration enforcement court order violations, with mandatory reporting to Congress of each contempt proceeding
- Restoration of nationwide injunction authority to district courts in immigration cases (reversed by the *Trump v. Hawaii* line; would require legislative action)

**Detention oversight**:
- Restore and adequately fund OIDO with mandatory staffing minimums (congressional mandate, not executive discretion)
- Independent third-party medical care inspection, modeled on the Prison Rape Elimination Act's inspection regime
- Mandatory death review by independent body within 30 days of any in-custody death, with public report within 90 days
- Prohibition on private operator contracts at facilities without third-party compliance certification
- State and local governments' right to inspect detention facilities within their jurisdictions, independent of federal permission

**287(g) reform**:
- Reinstate the 2012 safeguards against racial profiling as a condition of any 287(g) agreement
- Require annual civil rights audits of participating agencies, published publicly
- Ban 287(g) deployment within 1,000 feet of voting locations on election days and the 72-hour window preceding them
- Restore and codify state and local authority to refuse 287(g) participation (anti-commandeering clarity)

**Immigration court system**:
- Fund legal representation for all individuals in detention-based removal proceedings, through a Legal Representation Fund modeled on the public defender system
- Remove EOIR from DOJ control; establish as an independent Article I immigration court under congressional authority (as proposed in the Immigration Court Efficiency and Independence Act)
- Mandatory case management hearings within 60 days for all detained individuals, preventing indefinite pre-hearing detention

**Anti-retaliation**:
- Prohibition on prosecution of any individual within two years of that individual's successful legal challenge to immigration enforcement action — creating a bright-line rule that prevents vindictive prosecution like the Abrego Garcia case
- Mandatory recusal of any prosecutor who was a party to the enforcement action being challenged from any subsequent prosecution of the challenger

### 8.3 State-Level Actions Available Now

For states and localities that cannot wait for federal legislative reform:

**Ban 287(g) participation**: Nine states have done this; the Tenth Amendment legal framework is clear. State AGs in states without existing bans should file declaratory judgment actions establishing their authority to opt out.

**File NEPA challenges to detention infrastructure**: Maryland's TRO model is replicable. Any state in which ICE is purchasing or converting warehouses for detention can challenge the adequacy of environmental review. The absence of categorical exclusion justification is a viable APA claim.

**State-funded legal representation**: New York, Illinois, California, and New Jersey have established state-funded legal representation programs for individuals in immigration removal proceedings. The evidence shows that legal representation dramatically reduces wrongful removal rates and reduces total system cost through faster case resolution.

**State immigrant detention standards**: California, Illinois, and Washington have enacted state-level detention standards that apply to any detention facility within their borders, creating minimum conditions requirements that are independently enforceable under state law. This approach survives preemption challenges because it regulates conditions within the state, not the federal immigration enforcement program itself.

## 9. Comparative Evidence — What Democratic Accountability of Immigration Enforcement Looks Like

### 9.1 Canada

Canada's immigration detention system operates under a framework with fundamentally different accountability architecture. The 2019 Immigration Refugee and Citizens Canada reforms established: a mandatory 48-hour initial detention review; regular judicial review of continued detention at 7 days, then 30 days; a presumption of release (rather than detention) absent specific statutory grounds; an independent Detention Review Board with power to order release; and independent inspection of detention conditions by the Office of the Correctional Investigator.

The result: Canada's average daily detention population is approximately 300–400 individuals, compared to the United States' 58,000+. Canada removes individuals with removal orders at similar rates to the United States. The accountability architecture does not prevent enforcement — it prevents the construction of a detention apparatus operating outside judicial oversight.

### 9.2 Germany

Germany's Residenzpflicht (residency requirement) and detention regime for asylum seekers and removable individuals operates under direct judicial supervision: every detention decision is subject to automatic judicial review within 48 hours by an independent judge, who must find specific statutory grounds for detention and may only authorize detention for the minimum period necessary. Germany's detention population is under 1,000 individuals at any given time. The constitutional basis — Article 104 of the Basic Law, which requires judicial authorization for any deprivation of liberty beyond 24 hours — is the structural protection that makes this architecture durable across changing governments.

### 9.3 Australia (Negative Case)

Australia provides the cautionary comparative case. Following the 1992 Migration Reform Act, Australia introduced mandatory immigration detention with no judicial review requirement — the first time a liberal democracy had legislated away habeas corpus in the immigration context. The result over 30 years: systematic abuse at offshore detention facilities (Nauru, Manus Island), including the deaths of Reza Berati (beaten to death by guards, 2014) and Hamid Kehazaei (died of septicemia from an untreated wound), the detention of hundreds of children for years at a time, and multiple findings by the UN Human Rights Committee that Australia's regime violated international obligations against torture and arbitrary detention. In 2023, the Australian High Court ruled that indefinite immigration detention violates the Australian Constitution — a ruling that came 30 years after the system was built.

The Australian case establishes the precise timeline risk: 30 years from construction of the mandatory detention system to constitutional correction. The correction required a High Court ruling; legislative reform never achieved a majority during those 30 years despite sustained advocacy. The U.S. infrastructure being built now will have a similar inertia.
