## Executive Summary

The United States intelligence oversight architecture has experienced simultaneous institutional collapse across every accountability layer that the post-Church Committee framework established. The Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court continues to approve surveillance under Section 702 of FISA while its own March 2026 opinion — not yet public — documents compliance violations that extend beyond the FBI to the NSA and CIA. The Privacy and Civil Liberties Oversight Board was dismantled by presidential firing in January 2025, deprived of quorum, and had its Democratic members' terminations ruled unlawful by a federal district court — yet remains inoperable. The FBI Director has personally directed a purge of experienced counterterrorism and counterintelligence agents, gutted the Domestic Terrorism Operations Section, severed intelligence-sharing with civil rights organizations that previously fed the bureau, and declined to commit to seeking warrants before purchasing Americans' location data from commercial brokers. A federal district court ruled in January 2025 that the FBI's "backdoor searches" of Section 702 data violated the Fourth Amendment — the first ruling of its kind in the program's 18-year history — and Congress has now delayed acting on that ruling twice.

The June 12 deadline for Section 702 reauthorization is not merely a legislative deadline. It is a structural stress test: whether Congress can attach warrant requirements, close the commercial data broker loophole, restore PCLOB independence, and mandate FISC opinion transparency against an intelligence community and an executive branch that have demonstrated both the capability and the willingness to surveil Americans without constitutional authorization. The Hasbajrami ruling, the March 2026 FISC compliance opinion, the Wyden-Lee Government Surveillance Reform Act, and the Five Eyes comparative models together identify a coherent reform pathway. The obstacles are institutional capture, not statutory complexity: the same Congress that must pass these reforms contains members whose communications have been queried under the program they are asked to restrict.

## Section 1: The Architecture of Surveillance Authority — What Section 702 Does

### 1.1 Statutory Structure

Section 702 of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act, enacted in 2008 as part of the FISA Amendments Act, authorizes the NSA, CIA, and FBI to conduct surveillance of "foreign persons located outside the United States" for foreign intelligence purposes, without obtaining individual warrants from the FISA Court. The FISC instead approves "certifications" — programmatic frameworks describing categories of targets (international terrorism, cybersecurity, weapons proliferation, foreign governments) — and approves the "targeting procedures" and "minimization procedures" that govern how collection operates.

The statute contains what its drafters described as a categorical prohibition on targeting U.S. persons or persons located inside the United States. That prohibition, however, contains the architecture of what critics call the "backdoor search" problem: while the government cannot target an American under Section 702, it can and routinely does query databases of Section 702-collected communications — which inevitably contain American communications gathered as a result of surveillance of foreign targets — using Americans' names, email addresses, and phone numbers. These queries, called "U.S. person queries," do not require a warrant. They require only internal approval procedures whose adequacy the March 2026 FISC opinion has now called into question.

**Statutory citations**: 50 U.S.C. § 1881a (Section 702, FISA Amendments Act of 2008); 50 U.S.C. § 1881b-1881e (accompanying provisions); Pub. L. No. 118-49 (Reforming Intelligence and Securing America Act, April 20, 2024); Pub. L. No. 119-___ (45-day extension, April 30, 2026, signed same night).

### 1.2 Scale of Collection

The scale of Section 702 collection — and the query problem — is not hypothetical. The ODNI reported in 2024 that the FBI conducted 5,518 U.S. person queries, a figure that represented a purported 90% decline from prior years following compliance pressure. But the March 2026 FISC opinion, the contents of which have not yet been released under the Cotton-Warner declassification deal, found that the FBI's "filtering tools" — used to conduct queries outside the formal query reporting system — were never counted or audited. The FISC found that the same off-books querying problem disclosed regarding the FBI's 2024 conduct extends to the NSA and CIA. The true number of U.S. person queries under Section 702 in 2024 and 2025 is unknown and, under current reporting frameworks, unknowable.

The history of known abuse is extensive. The government's own filings have documented: warrantless queries of Black Lives Matter protesters in 2020; warrantless searches for the communications of members of Congress; warrantless queries of journalists and political commentators; and — most strikingly — warrantless queries of 19,000 donors to a single congressional campaign. In March 2022, the government itself reported more than 278,000 noncompliant Section 702 searches. These are not edge cases in a generally compliant program. They are systemic features of a program in which the internal compliance architecture has never matched the scale of collection.

### 1.3 The 2026 Legislative Context: Four Failed Votes and a 45-Day Escape

The April 2026 reauthorization fight produced four consecutive legislative failures before a procedural escape hatch was found. The first attempt — a five-year clean reauthorization — failed 197-228 on April 15-17. The second — an 18-month Trump-preferred extension — failed the same session. The third — a 10-day stopgap — passed by unanimous consent on April 17-18 as a holding measure. The fourth attempt, Speaker Johnson's Foreign Intelligence Accountability Act (S. 1318), passed the House 235-191 on April 29 with a CBDC ban attached as a Freedom Caucus sweetener — and was declared dead on arrival in the Senate by Majority Leader Thune, who opposed the CBDC provision.

The Senate then passed a 45-day extension by unanimous voice vote on April 30. The House followed with 261-111. Trump signed the same night. Section 702 authority is continuous through **June 12, 2026**. No warrant requirement was included. The commercial data broker loophole remains open. The CBDC ban was stripped.

The sole substantive consequence of the April 30 deal was the Cotton-Warner declassification commitment: Senators Cotton and Warner, the lead Republican and Democrat on the Senate Intelligence Committee respectively, committed to declassification of the March 17, 2026 FISC opinion within 15 days of the extension's enactment. That 15-day window runs to approximately May 15, 2026 — making the opinion's release a material event for the Senate's June 12 debate.

## Section 2: The Constitutional Crisis — Hasbajrami and the Fourth Amendment Ruling

### 2.1 United States v. Hasbajrami: The Ruling

On December 2, 2024 — released publicly January 21, 2025 — Judge LaShann DeArcy Hall of the Eastern District of New York issued the first federal court ruling in the 18-year history of Section 702 holding that the FBI's warrantless backdoor searches of Section 702 databases ordinarily violate the Fourth Amendment. The ruling came in a criminal case after more than a decade of litigation, and following a 2021 Second Circuit panel decision that backdoor searches constitute "separate Fourth Amendment events" and directed the district court to determine whether a warrant was required.

Judge Hall's holding is significant precisely in what it refuses to accept. The government argued that backdoor searches are permissible under a "foreign intelligence exception" to the Fourth Amendment's warrant requirement — the same exception the intelligence community has invoked since FISA's enactment. The district court found that exception inapplicable to back-end queries of databases that have already been collected: the surveillance of the foreign target may be justified; the separate act of searching Americans' communications within that collected data is a new Fourth Amendment event requiring new authorization.

The ACLU is now pressing that ruling before the Second Circuit, seeking to transform the district court decision — which applies only to the Hasbajrami case — into circuit precedent. If the Second Circuit affirms, the government's position that warrantless backdoor searches are constitutional becomes unsustainable in one of the primary circuits hearing national security cases.

**Sources**: [EFF: Federal Court Rules Backdoor Searches Unconstitutional](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2025/01/victory-federal-court-finally-rules-backdoor-searches-702-data-unconstitutional); [ACLU: Court Rules Warrantless Section 702 Searches Violated Fourth Amendment](https://www.aclu.org/press-releases/court-rules-warrantless-section-702-searches-violated-the-fourth-amendment); [Cato Institute: Federal Court Rules Section 702 Backdoor Searches Unconstitutional](https://www.cato.org/blog/federal-court-rules-fisa-section-702-back-door-searches-unconstitutional); [ACLU: Urges Appeals Court to Uphold Fourth Amendment](https://www.aclu.org/press-releases/aclu-urges-appeals-court-to-uphold-fourth-amendment-rights-against-warrantless-government-surveillance)

### 2.2 Carpenter v. United States and Its Logical Extension

The Supreme Court's 2018 decision in Carpenter v. United States, 585 U.S. 296, established that the government must obtain a warrant before accessing historical cell-site location information, despite the third-party doctrine that had previously allowed warrantless access to records held by telephone companies. Chief Justice Roberts' majority opinion for a 5-4 Court created a "digital age" exception to the third-party doctrine, reasoning that the "deeply revealing nature" of CSLI — its "depth, breadth, and comprehensive reach, and the inescapable and automatic nature of its collection" — placed it outside the historical logic of the third-party doctrine.

The Carpenter reasoning has direct application to Section 702's mass collection architecture. The "incidental" collection of Americans' communications from surveillance of foreign targets is structurally identical to the CSLI problem: neither category of data was voluntarily shared with the government in any meaningful Fourth Amendment sense, yet both are accessed by government agencies as if the absence of a direct warrant instruction constitutes authorization. The Hasbajrami ruling's first-principles analysis effectively applies Carpenter's digital-age exception logic to the Section 702 context.

United States, 585 U.S. 296 (2018)](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/585/16-402/); [American Bar Association: Carpenter Analysis](https://www.americanbar.org/groups/litigation/committees/commercial-business/practice/2018/carpenter-v-united-states-cell-phone-location-data-requires-warrant/); [Constitution Center: Carpenter](https://constitutioncenter.org/the-constitution/supreme-court-case-library/carpenter-v-united-states)

### 2.3 Loper Bright's Post-Chevron Impact on FISA Authority

The Supreme Court's 2024 decision in Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo overruled Chevron deference and replaced it with the requirement that courts exercise "independent judgment" to determine the "best reading" of statutory text. The implications for surveillance authority are contested but consequential.

Prior to Loper Bright, the intelligence community's interpretations of ambiguous FISA provisions — including the scope of permissible "incidental collection," the definition of "targeting," and the boundaries of "minimization procedures" — received Chevron deference. Courts gave substantial weight to ODNI and DOJ's reading of statutory terms precisely because those agencies had institutional expertise and access to classified context. Post-Loper Bright, that deference is gone. What remains is Skidmore weight: courts may still consider the executive branch's well-reasoned, consistently-held interpretations as persuasive authority, but they are not bound by them and must reach their own independent statutory conclusions.

The surveillance context has a complicating layer: courts have long given "foreign affairs deference" to executive branch views in foreign policy and national security matters, based on functional justifications (executive expertise, access to classified information) that are consistent with — and in some respects independent of — the Chevron framework. Post-Loper Bright, that functional deference survives, but it is narrower than Chevron was: it applies to genuinely foreign affairs decisions, not to domestic application of surveillance statutes to American citizens. The Hasbajrami ruling's Fourth Amendment analysis does not depend on statutory interpretation — it depends on constitutional principles that no amount of executive deference can override.

The practical consequence: the FBI's position that Section 702 "minimization procedures" authorized by the FISC satisfy the Fourth Amendment warrant requirement is now litigable on independent statutory and constitutional grounds that courts must evaluate without deferring to the intelligence community's self-serving interpretations.

## Section 3: Oversight Architecture Collapse — PCLOB, OIG, and the FISC

### 3.1 The PCLOB Dismantling

The Privacy and Civil Liberties Oversight Board was established by the Intelligence Reform and Terrorism Prevention Act of 2004 and substantially strengthened after the Church Committee by the 9/11 Commission Act of 2007. Its statutory mandate is to analyze and review actions the executive branch takes to protect the nation from terrorism, and to ensure that need is balanced with the protection of privacy and civil liberties. PCLOB members serve fixed terms and can be removed only for cause — a structure Congress deliberately chose to insulate the board from political pressure.

On January 27, 2025, President Trump fired the three Democratic members — Sharon Bradford Franklin, Edward Felten, and Travis LeBlanc — without cause. The firings left the Board with one active member (Republican appointee Beth Williams), depriving it of the quorum required for official action. Trump's position: PCLOB members are executive officers removable at will.

On May 21, 2025, the U.S. District Court for the District of Columbia ruled the firings unlawful, holding that Congress intended to restrict presidential removal power over PCLOB members and that the restriction is constitutional as applied. The administration did not immediately reinstate the fired members. As of the date of this research, PCLOB remains inoperable — neither confirming nor functioning — despite the court ruling, representing a direct parallel to the Lisa Cook/Federal Reserve removal saga documented in Domain 38.

The consequences for FISA oversight are material. PCLOB had conducted the most substantive public reviews of Section 702's legal basis and compliance record, including its 2014 report on the Section 702 program that identified the "incidental collection" problem and its 2019 report that documented FBI misuse of backdoor search authority. With PCLOB inoperable, the only remaining oversight mechanisms are congressional committees (subject to partisan control), the DOJ Inspector General (described by civil liberties advocates as having been "cowed into inactivity" by the current administration), and the FISC itself — a court whose proceedings are secret and whose opinions are classified by default.

### 3.2 The FISC: Structural Independence and Appointment Risks

The Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court consists of 11 federal district judges designated by the Chief Justice of the United States for staggered seven-year terms. This appointment mechanism — sole authority in the Chief Justice, without Senate confirmation or presidential nomination — was designed to insulate FISC membership from political pressure. It has two structural vulnerabilities.

First, because FISC judges serve only as an additional assignment to their primary district court duties, and because designation authority is solely in the Chief Justice, any effort by the current administration to pressure or replace the Chief Justice — or to advance a set of district court nominees whose judicial philosophy systematically favors executive surveillance authority — indirectly shapes the FISC's composition over time. As of May 2026, there is one confirmed vacancy on the FISC's 11-member bench. With the Trump administration's judicial appointment pipeline and the current political dynamics, district court nominees aligned with a maximalist executive surveillance position are plausible future designees.

Second, and more immediately pressing, the FISC's proceedings are structurally one-sided: the government presents its surveillance applications and the court reviews them, but there is no adversary. The USA FREEDOM Act of 2015 created a pool of "amici curiae" — outside legal experts with appropriate clearances who can be appointed by FISC judges to present privacy and civil liberties arguments in cases presenting "novel or significant" legal questions. But amici appointment is discretionary; amici cannot independently petition for appellate review; and the amicus role, by design, addresses only legal issues, not the factual and operational questions about surveillance impacts on civil liberties. The result is a court that has improved from its pre-2015 baseline but still lacks the adversarial dynamic that constitutional due process normally requires.

The March 2026 FISC opinion — due for public release under the Cotton-Warner deal by approximately May 15, 2026 — is the first opportunity since RISAA's passage to assess whether the court's compliance review function is functioning. Prior declassified FISC opinions released by ODNI (September 2024, February 2025, March 2025) have consistently documented ongoing compliance failures in the FBI's querying practices despite successive rounds of remediation commitments.

### 3.3 Inspector General Independence and the DOJ OIG Problem

The Intelligence Community Inspector General (ICIG), established by the Inspector General Act Amendments of 2010, has jurisdiction over the intelligence community's compliance with law and regulations. The DOJ Inspector General has jurisdiction over FBI compliance with surveillance authorities. Both offices have statutory protections against political removal — protections that the current administration has tested across multiple agencies.

The PCLOB's effective incapacitation combined with the Brennan Center's characterization of the DOJ OIG as having been "cowed into inactivity" points to a systemic pattern: the administration has sequentially neutralized each external oversight mechanism while leaving intact the formal statutory structure that makes those mechanisms appear to be functioning. The appearance of oversight — an OIG that still formally exists, a PCLOB that technically has one member, a FISC that continues to issue orders — masks the operational reality that the substantive accountability functions of each body have been impaired.

The intelligence community's own oversight bodies — the National Security Agency's Office of Inspector General, the CIA's IG, and the FBI's IG — are internal and subject to the political pressures that attach to their parent agencies. The Hasbajrami ruling's factual predicate — that the FBI was using undisclosed filtering tools to conduct queries that bypassed statutory requirements — was discovered not through these internal oversight mechanisms but through litigation-driven disclosure in criminal proceedings. This is not an accident of institutional design; it is the predictable consequence of relying on agency self-policing for accountability in a program that operates entirely in classified space.

## Section 4: The Commercial Data Broker Loophole — Fourth Amendment Through the Back Door

### 4.1 The Warrantless Surveillance Market

The government does not need to invoke Section 702 to access Americans' location data, browsing history, app telemetry, and communication metadata. It can purchase it. Federal agencies including ICE, DHS, the FBI, and the DEA have active contracts with commercial data broker companies including Babel Street, Venntel, and Penlink to access bulk commercial location data derived from smartphone applications. The FBI signed a contract worth up to $27 million with Babel Street for 5,000 licenses to its "Locate X" product, which aggregates location history from mobile apps. ICE uses Penlink's "Webloc" tool to track mobile phone locations and identify devices that have visited specific locations — including immigration court buildings, detention facilities, and protest sites.

When Senator Ron Wyden asked FBI Director Kash Patel whether he would commit to not purchasing Americans' location data without a warrant, Patel declined, saying the FBI "uses all tools" and purchases commercially available information "consistent with the Constitution." That answer is legally defensible only if the third-party doctrine still applies — but after Carpenter, its application to aggregated commercial location data is constitutionally contested. The Hasbajrami ruling's logic, applied to purchased data, would suggest that warrantless acquisition of the same information through commercial intermediaries does not cure the Fourth Amendment problem.

**Sources**: [NPR: Your Data Is Everywhere. The Government Is Buying It Without a Warrant.](https://www.npr.org/2026/03/25/nx-s1-5752369/ice-surveillance-data-brokers-congress-anthropic); [State of Surveillance: The Data Broker Loophole](https://stateofsurveillance.org/news/data-broker-loophole-explainer-government-purchases-your-data-2026/); [State of Surveillance: ICE Tracks Your Phone Without a Warrant](https://stateofsurveillance.org/news/ice-webloc-warrantless-phone-tracking/)

### 4.2 Congressional Inaction and the Bipartisan Reform Coalition

A coalition of 130+ civil society organizations has urged Congress to close the commercial data broker loophole as a condition of any FISA reauthorization. The legislative reform vehicle is the Government Surveillance Reform Act of 2026 (S. 4082), introduced by Senators Wyden (D-OR) and Lee (R-UT) with bipartisan cosponsors Senators Lummis (R-WY) and Warren (D-MA), and a House companion from Representatives Davidson (R-OH) and Lofgren (D-CA). This is a genuinely cross-partisan bill: the Freedom Caucus libertarian wing aligns with the progressive civil liberties wing, producing a coalition that has repeatedly come within a handful of votes of blocking the warrantless renewal.

The GSRA's data broker provisions are straightforward: it would prohibit federal law enforcement agencies from purchasing data that they would be required to obtain a warrant to collect directly. The constitutional logic is the mirror image of the Hasbajrami/Carpenter analysis — if the Fourth Amendment requires a warrant for government collection of comprehensive location data, the Fourth Amendment cannot be evaded by adding a commercial intermediary in the chain of acquisition. The bill's sponsor framing articulates this as closing an "end run" around the Constitution, not as a novel restriction on law enforcement.

## Section 5: FBI Restructuring and Domestic Intelligence Accountability

### 5.1 The Patel Purge and DTOS Dismantlement

FBI Director Kash Patel's restructuring of the FBI between February 2025 and May 2026 has systematically dismantled the institutional capacity that the post-Church Committee framework constructed. The Domestic Terrorism Operations Section (DTOS) — which provided operational support to all 55 FBI field offices on domestic terrorism investigations — was gutted in early 2025. In April 2025, Senate Democratic leaders pressed Patel to reverse course on his deprioritization of domestic terrorism prevention, noting that DTOS staff had been reassigned or terminated. As of May 2026, the administration's position is that domestic terrorism investigation has been replaced by a "multiagency mission center focused on politically motivated violence" — a framing that effectively redefines domestic terrorism to exclude right-wing violence while encompassing protest activity.

The firing pattern tracks political exposure. Analysis documented four instances in which Patel fired FBI personnel within hours or days of unflattering public accounts of his competency or conduct. A group of Executive Assistant Directors and supervisors was told on January 29, 2025 — nine days after the inauguration — that they must resign or be fired. Counterespionage section leadership was fired after House Democrats' March 4, 2026 letter publicized their identities. Steven Palmer, head of the Critical Incident Response Group and a 27-year FBI veteran, was fired within days of reporting on Patel's use of an FBI jet for personal travel.

Senator Durbin formally alleged that "Kash Patel has been personally directing the ongoing purge of FBI officials" and asked the DOJ Inspector General to investigate. As of the date of this research, no Inspector General report has been published on that investigation.

### 5.2 The SPLC Parallel: Intelligence-Sharing Inversion

The connection between domestic intelligence architecture and the SPLC indictment (documented in Domain 29) is structural, not coincidental. The SPLC had operated an informant program that shared intelligence on extremist organizations with the FBI for decades — precisely the kind of civil society / law enforcement intelligence cooperation that the post-Church Committee framework was designed to allow while limiting the FBI's internal COINTELPRO-style programs. Kash Patel severed the FBI's intelligence-sharing relationship with the SPLC in October 2025, calling it a "partisan smear machine." Six months later, the SPLC was indicted for the same intelligence-sharing that the FBI had previously relied on and encouraged.

The inversion is complete: the domestic intelligence architecture now treats civil rights organizations' intelligence cooperation with law enforcement as criminal fraud, while simultaneously gutting the FBI units responsible for monitoring the violent extremist organizations those civil rights groups had been tracking. The DTOS dismantlement and the SPLC prosecution are not separate policy decisions; they are complementary moves in a single strategic restructuring of who the domestic intelligence apparatus considers a threat.

## Section 6: The FISC Opinion Crisis — June 12 and the 15-Day Declassification Window

### 6.1 What the March 17, 2026 FISC Opinion Contains

The Department of Justice delivered to Congress a classified Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court opinion and order dated March 17, 2026, with a letter cosigned by Intelligence Committee Chairman Tom Cotton and Vice Chairman Mark Warner. The opinion's full contents remain classified, but its significance is established by the Brennan Center's characterization of it as revealing "continuing violations by the FBI" and by the FISC's documented finding — reported in public sources — that the off-books querying problem the DOJ claimed to have fixed in early 2025 remains ongoing and extends beyond the FBI to the NSA and CIA.

Senator Wyden negotiated the Cotton-Warner deal specifically because he believed the opinion would materially change the Senate's calculus on whether to include warrant requirements in a three-year reauthorization. His letter stated: "We expect that this declassification review will be completed, and the FISC opinion released publicly, within 15 days." The 15-day window runs from April 30 to approximately May 15, 2026. If the declassification is completed on schedule, the Senate will have four weeks between the opinion's release and the June 12 deadline to digest its findings.

The specific legal question the March 2026 opinion likely addresses is whether the government's compliance remediation under RISAA — including the attorney-level approval requirement and the DOJ's claimed shutdown of the unauthorized querying tool in early 2025 — actually satisfied the court's prior compliance orders. The FISC's documented finding that the problem "extends beyond the FBI" to the NSA and CIA is significant: it means the compliance failure is not an FBI-specific cultural or procedural problem but a community-wide pattern of using technical workarounds to circumvent court-mandated query requirements.

**Sources**: [Nextgov/FCW: 45-Day Extension Declassification Deal](https://www.nextgov.com/policy/2026/04/house-passes-45-day-fisa-extension-after-senators-secure-declassification-deal/413250/); [Brennan Center: Section 702 2026 Resource Page](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/section-702-foreign-intelligence-surveillance-act-fisa-2026-resource-page); [CyberScoop: Congress Extends Section 702 Surveillance](https://cyberscoop.com/congress-extends-section-702-surveillance-45-days/)

### 6.2 The Cotton-Warner Dynamic and Senate Reform Pathway

The bipartisan posture of Cotton (R-AR) and Warner (D-VA) as the Senate Intelligence Committee's Republican and Democratic leads is the central variable in the June 12 legislative outcome. Cotton is a FISA maximalist: he has consistently argued that any warrant requirement for backdoor searches would "cripple" the program. Warner has been more receptive to civil liberties arguments but has historically prioritized program continuity over reform conditions.

The declassification commitment is the first time the Senate Intelligence Committee leadership has formally acknowledged that the program's compliance record warrants public disclosure before reauthorization. Whether this represents a genuine shift in Warner's reform calculus — and whether Cotton's support for declassification translates into openness to compliance-driven warrant requirements — will determine whether the June 12 bill is a three-year warrantless renewal (the House's April 29 position), a three-year renewal with meaningful warrant and data broker provisions (the GSRA's position), or another short-term extension.

The Wyden-Lee GSRA's bipartisan architecture is politically significant: it demonstrates that the warrant requirement is not a Democratic-only demand. Mike Lee and Warren Davidson (R-OH) represent the libertarian wing of the Republican Party that has blocked prior warrantless renewals. Senator Rand Paul's record of placing individual holds on FISA extensions reflects the same cross-partisan dynamic. The June 12 fight is not left vs. right — it is the surveillance state vs. the constitutional coalition, a framing that this framework's distribution to both progressive and libertarian audiences can reinforce.

## Section 7: International Precedent — Five Eyes Oversight Models

### 7.1 United Kingdom: The Double-Lock Model

The Investigatory Powers Act 2016 (IPA) created the most structurally robust judicial authorization mechanism among the Five Eyes nations. The "double-lock" system requires that surveillance warrants receive both executive authorization (Secretary of State) and independent judicial approval (a Judicial Commissioner from the Investigatory Powers Commission). Neither alone is sufficient — the Secretary of State cannot issue a warrant without judicial approval, and a Judicial Commissioner cannot issue one without executive authorization. The Investigatory Powers Commissioner's Office (IPCO) oversees more than 600 public authorities' use of covert investigatory powers, with a full-time inspectorate staffed by former judges and senior lawyers.

The IPA's structure addresses precisely the failure mode visible in Section 702: front-end collection authorization combined with an independent backend access requirement. A UK agency collecting bulk communications data under a bulk warrant must obtain a separate Judicial Commissioner authorization before querying that data for a UK person — creating the structural analog of the warrant requirement that the Wyden-Lee GSRA proposes for Section 702 backdoor searches.

The IPA is not a civil liberties utopia — its 2024 amendments expanded government surveillance powers in ways that privacy advocates contested. But its authorization architecture demonstrates that a democratic country can maintain robust foreign intelligence collection while creating genuine judicial checkpoints on domestic access.

**Sources**: [Investigatory Powers Act 2016](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2016/25/contents); [IPCO: The Double-Lock Authorization Process](https://www.ipco.org.uk/what-we-do/the-double-lock/); [CSIS: New Investigatory Powers Act in the UK Enhances Surveillance Powers](https://www.csis.org/analysis/new-investigatory-powers-act-united-kingdom-enhances-government-surveillance-powers); [Wikipedia: Investigatory Powers Act 2016](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Investigatory_Powers_Act_2016)

### 7.2 Canada: NSIRA and Whole-of-Government Review

Canada's National Security and Intelligence Review Agency (NSIRA), established in June 2019 under the National Security Intelligence Review Agency Act, represents the most significant Five Eyes reform model since FISA's 1978 enactment. NSIRA replaced the Security Intelligence Review Committee (SIRC), which was limited to reviewing CSIS activities, with an independent review body that can examine any national security or intelligence activity conducted by any federal department or agency. This cross-agency mandate directly addresses the fragmented oversight problem that characterizes the U.S. system, where PCLOB has limited jurisdiction, congressional intelligence committees oversee specific programs, and no body has comprehensive review authority across all intelligence activities.

NSIRA's structure combines statutory independence (members serve fixed terms, removable only for cause), full access to classified information, and an annual public reporting requirement that presents unclassified summaries of its review findings. The annual report model provides public accountability without compromising sources and methods — a design that is directly applicable to PCLOB reform and to the question of how frequently FISC opinions should be made public in redacted form.

Canada also requires ministerial authorizations for CSIS and CSE collection activities, with an Intelligence Commissioner reviewing authorizations for legality before they take effect — a parallel to the FISC authorization function but with a broader mandate covering collection activities that might not rise to the level of targeted surveillance.

**Sources**: [NSIRA: Annual Report 2019](https://nsira-ossnr.gc.ca/en/annual-reports/national-security-and-intelligence-review-agency-annual-report-2019/); [NSIRA: Official Website](https://nsira-ossnr.gc.ca/); [Parliament of Canada: Oversight of Intelligence Agencies — Five Eyes Comparison](https://lop.parl.ca/sites/PublicWebsite/default/en_CA/ResearchPublications/22035249E); [Wikipedia: National Security and Intelligence Review Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Security_and_Intelligence_Review_Agency); [Tandfonline: Five Years of Canada's NSIRA](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/02684527.2026.2637865)

### 7.3 Australia: IGIS and Independent Statutory Review

Australia's Inspector-General of Intelligence and Security (IGIS) is an independent statutory office holder appointed by the Governor-General under the Inspector-General of Intelligence and Security Act 1986. The IGIS reviews all six Australian intelligence agencies for legality, propriety, and consistency with human rights — not just the primary collection agency — and is explicitly not subject to ministerial direction in how it conducts its review responsibilities. The independence guarantee is structural: unlike a presidential appointee, the IGIS's independence from political direction is a statutory function of the appointment mechanism (Governor-General appointment, fixed term, for-cause removal only).

The IGIS operates through proactive inspections — not merely reactive complaint handling — and can initiate inquiries on its own motion, without waiting for a ministerial referral or a public complaint. This self-initiation authority is the critical structural difference from the PCLOB model, which relies heavily on formal referrals and lacks the resources for comprehensive programmatic inspection. The IGIS's 2024-25 annual report documents active inspections across all six agencies, providing a model of how an independent oversight body can generate accountability information without compromising operational security.

**Sources**: [IGIS Australia: About IGIS](https://www.igis.gov.au/about); [IGIS Australia: The Role of the Inspector-General — A Primer](https://www.igis.gov.au/sites/default/files/2024-05/The%20Role%20of%20the%20Inspector-General%20of%20Intelligence%20and%20Security%20-%20A%20Primer.pdf); [IGIS Annual Report 2024-25](https://www.igis.gov.au/sites/default/files/2025-10/IGIS%20Annual%20Report%202024-25.pdf); [Wikipedia: Inspector-General of Intelligence and Security (Australia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inspector-General_of_Intelligence_and_Security_(Australia))

### 7.4 New Zealand: Parliamentary Committee and Inspector-General Independence

New Zealand's Intelligence and Security Act 2017 established a two-track oversight model: an Inspector-General of Intelligence and Security with review authority over the NZSIS and GCSB, and an Intelligence and Security Committee of Parliament with 5-7 members including representation from opposition parties. Critically, both the Inspector-General and the parliamentary committee have complementary but distinct mandates: the IG reviews legality and propriety (backward-looking accountability), while the parliamentary committee examines policy, administration, and expenditure (forward-looking governance).

The parliamentary committee's bipartisan design — the Prime Minister must consult the Leader of the Opposition before nominating members, with representation weighted to reflect party proportions — directly addresses the U.S. problem of intelligence committee capture by the majority party. The Senate and House Intelligence Committees have experienced significant partisan divergence since 2017, with the Intelligence Committee's oversight function compromised by the presence of members whose executive branch loyalties (or whose own communications may have been queried under Section 702) create structural conflicts of interest.

**Sources**: [New Zealand IGIS: Home](https://igis.govt.nz/); [NZSIS: Oversight](https://www.nzsis.govt.nz/about-us/oversight); [DPMC: Strengthening Oversight of NZSIS and GCSB](https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/our-programmes/national-security-and-intelligence/intelligence-and-security-act-2017/strengthening-oversight-of-nzsis-and-gcsb); [Wikipedia: Inspector-General of Intelligence and Security (New Zealand)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inspector-General_of_Intelligence_and_Security_(New_Zealand))

### 7.5 The Five Eyes Intelligence Oversight and Review Council (FIORC)

The Five Eyes Intelligence Oversight and Review Council (FIORC), coordinated by ODNI's Intelligence Community Inspector General, provides a multilateral oversight coordination mechanism — meaning that the US, UK, Canada, Australia, and New Zealand share oversight methodologies, findings, and best practices through their respective oversight bodies. FIORC's existence is institutionally significant: it means that the US intelligence oversight collapse has international dimensions. When PCLOB is non-functional, the US's FIORC representative has no institutional base from which to engage in multilateral oversight exchange. Allied oversight bodies in the UK, Canada, Australia, and New Zealand are conducting substantive annual reviews and coordinated inspections while PCLOB sits inoperable due to a presidential firing that a federal court declared unlawful.

**Sources**: [DNI: FIORC Overview](https://www.dni.gov/index.php/ncsc-how-we-work/217-about/organization/icig-pages/2660-icig-fiorc); [IPCO: Five Eyes Oversight Organisations Meet in London](https://www.ipco.org.uk/news/five-eyes-oversight-organisations-meet-in-london/); [DNI: 2023 Annual FIORC Meeting Executive Summary](https://www.dni.gov/files/ICIG/Documents/Partnerships/FIORC/2023_Annual_FIORC_Meeting_Executive_Summary_Final.pdf)

## Section 8: The Church Committee Precedent and What It Actually Established

### 8.1 1975-1978: What the Framework Was Designed to Prevent

The Senate Select Committee to Study Governmental Operations with Respect to Intelligence Activities, chaired by Senator Frank Church of Idaho, conducted a 16-month investigation in 1975-1976 that documented systemic intelligence abuses including COINTELPRO (FBI programs targeting civil rights, anti-war, and socialist organizations), NSA's Projects SHAMROCK and MINARET (warrantless monitoring of international wire communications), Operation MKULTRA (CIA human experimentation on mind control), and the FBI's targeting of Martin Luther King, Jr. for surveillance and blackmail. The committee conducted 126 meetings, 40 subcommittee hearings, 150 staff members, and 800 witness interviews.

The committee's final report identified 96 legislative and regulatory recommendations. The two most consequential statutory outputs were: (1) Senate Resolution 400 (1976) establishing the Senate Select Committee on Intelligence to provide "vigilant legislative oversight" of intelligence activities; and (2) the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act of 1978, establishing the FISA Court as a warrant-issuing mechanism for foreign intelligence surveillance — the first time a court had been interposed between the executive branch's intelligence collection programs and their targets.

The Church Committee's fundamental premise was that intelligence agencies operating in secrecy, without external oversight, will routinely abuse their authority — not because they are staffed by bad actors, but because the structure of secrecy, the incentive to collect, and the absence of accountability produce systematic drift toward abuse. FISA was designed as a structural correction to that drift.

**Sources**: [U.S. Senate: Church Committee](https://www.senate.gov/about/powers-procedures/investigations/church-committee.htm); [Levin Center: Frank Church and the Church Committee](https://levin-center.org/frank-church-and-the-church-committee/); [Church Committee Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Church_Committee); [Brookings: 40 Years Ago, Church Committee Investigated Americans Spying on Americans](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/40-years-ago-church-committee-investigated-americans-spying-on-americans/)

### 8.2 The Snowden Disclosures and the 2014 Reset

The 2013 Snowden disclosures revealed that the post-Church Committee framework had been systematically hollowed out. NSA's PRISM program collected internet communications from major U.S. tech companies under Section 702; NSA's bulk phone metadata collection operated under Section 215 of the PATRIOT Act; and the XKeyscore program provided analysts with near-real-time access to communications worldwide. The FISC had approved these programs, but its opinions — secret and one-sided — had developed an interpretive tradition that the statutory text could not bear in public daylight.

The USA FREEDOM Act of 2015 made marginal adjustments: it ended bulk Section 215 collection (replacing it with provider-held data with court-ordered queries), created the FISC amicus curiae pool, and required some FISC opinion declassification. The 2024 RISAA made further adjustments including the attorney-level approval requirement for FBI queries. None of these reforms addressed the structural problem that the Church Committee identified in 1975 and that the current FISC compliance failures confirm: intelligence agencies operating in secrecy will find technical workarounds to any procedural requirement, and will do so unless genuinely independent external review can detect and document those workarounds.

The March 2026 FISC opinion — finding that the "filtering tool" problem extends to NSA and CIA, and that the 2025 DOJ remediation claimed to Congress did not actually solve the problem — is the most direct confirmation since the Snowden disclosures that the reform framework continues to fail at its core function.

## Section 9: Democratic Design — Structuring Surveillance Oversight to Resist Capture

### 9.1 The Structural Problem: Executive Control Over Its Own Surveillance

The fundamental democratic design failure in the current U.S. surveillance architecture is that the executive branch controls both the collection apparatus and the primary oversight apparatus. The FISC can say no — but it hears only the government's arguments. PCLOB can investigate — but it requires executive appointment to function. Congressional committees can oversight — but they depend on executive branch disclosure to know what to oversight, and they are populated by members of the majority party who may have partisan incentives to enable rather than constrain executive surveillance authority.

This is the "captured oversight" failure mode that the framework's cross-domain analysis identifies across sectors: courts that see only government applications, oversight boards that require executive appointments to function, inspector generals who can be defunded or fired, congressional committees whose oversight function is controlled by the majority. Surveillance oversight is the paradigmatic case of this failure mode because the secrecy requirement — which is legitimate — provides an institutional cover for capture that does not exist in other policy domains.

### 9.2 Democratic Design Principles for Surveillance Oversight

Four structural principles emerge from the comparative Five Eyes analysis and the Church Committee's historical record:

**Principle 1: Mandatory adversarial proceedings at the authorization stage.** The FISC's amicus framework is a step toward adversarial proceedings, but discretionary appointment by the same judges who approve surveillance applications does not create genuine adversarial dynamics. The UK's double-lock model provides the template: executive authorization plus independent judicial review, with the latter genuinely independent of the government's case. The GSRA's amicus enhancement provisions — requiring amicus appointment for all Section 702 certifications, with appeal rights and full information access — move in this direction.

**Principle 2: Independent proactive inspection authority, not reactive complaint handling.** The PCLOB's reform model should look like Australia's IGIS or Canada's NSIRA: a body with proactive inspection authority across all intelligence collection activities, not just specific programs brought to its attention by executive referral. This means fixed-term appointments, for-cause removal only, congressional rather than executive appointment authority (or an appointment mechanism similar to the FISC's Chief Justice designation), and adequate staffing for programmatic inspection rather than just legal review.

**Principle 3: Mandatory transparency with structured redaction.** FISC opinions should be presumptively declassified — with redactions for sources and methods — rather than presumptively classified with discretionary declassification. The Cotton-Warner deal's 15-day clock is an improvement but should be a floor rather than a ceiling: annual programmatic certifications should automatically trigger redacted public opinion release within 30 days of issuance.

**Principle 4: Statutory data broker parity.** The commercial data broker loophole is not primarily a surveillance problem — it is a constitutional architecture problem. Allowing the government to purchase what it cannot warrantlessly collect directly inverts the constitutional framework that Carpenter established. Closing the loophole requires only statutory clarity: the GSRA's provision prohibiting purchase of data that would require a warrant to collect is technically straightforward and constitutionally required by Carpenter's logic.

## Section 10: The Reform Pathway — What Must Be in the June 12 Bill

### 10.1 The Government Surveillance Reform Act: Core Provisions

The Government Surveillance Reform Act of 2026 (S. 4082, introduced March 12, 2026) represents the comprehensive reform bill against which any June 12 outcome will be measured. Its key provisions:

**Warrant requirement**: The GSRA requires the government to obtain a warrant or a FISA Title I order before querying Section 702 databases using U.S. person identifiers. Emergency exceptions apply for imminent threats to life or serious bodily harm, with after-the-fact court notification. The warrant requirement does not apply to foreign-to-foreign communications in the databases or to searches targeting identified foreign intelligence subjects.

**Data broker prohibition**: The GSRA prohibits federal law enforcement agencies from purchasing data that they would be required to obtain a warrant to collect directly. This provision closes the Locate X, Webloc, and similar commercial surveillance contracts that currently operate outside any constitutional authorization framework.

**PCLOB reform**: The GSRA reinstates PCLOB with confirmed members, requires bipartisan composition, and bans politically motivated firings. This provision directly responds to the January 2025 firings and the D.C. District Court's ruling that those firings were unlawful.

**FISC transparency**: The GSRA requires the FISC to publish redacted opinions in all cases and allows court-appointed amici to appeal controversial rulings to the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court of Review. This provision closes the one-way ratchet that currently limits appeal rights to the government.

**Provider definition rollback**: The GSRA repeals the 2024 RISAA expansion of the "electronic communication service provider" definition — an expansion that the FISC's own amicus warned could encompass landlords, data centers, and essentially any business with a Wi-Fi network.

**Sources**: [S.4082 Text: Congress.gov](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/4082/text); [Wyden Senate: Section-by-Section Summary of GSRA](https://www.wyden.senate.gov/imo/media/doc/section-by-section_for_government_surveillance_reform_act_of_2026pdf.pdf); [FAI: GSRA as Lever Against AI Power Concentration](https://www.thefai.org/posts/the-government-surveillance-reform-act-a-practical-lever-for-avoiding-ai-power-concentration)

### 10.2 Minimum Acceptable Provisions in a Three-Year Reauthorization

If Congress passes a three-year reauthorization rather than the GSRA, the following provisions represent the minimum civil liberties floor that would distinguish a meaningful reform from the "Fool's Gold" (Just Security's characterization) of S. 1318:

1. **Warrant requirement for U.S. person queries** with genuine emergency exceptions (not the broad "foreign intelligence purpose" exception that renders the requirement meaningless).
2. **Data broker loophole closure** covering at minimum cell-site location data, precise geolocation, and communication metadata.
3. **PCLOB restoration** with statutory independence protections equivalent to those applicable to Fed Board governors — fixed terms, for-cause removal, bipartisan composition, congressional appointment authority or confirmation requirement.
4. **March 2026 FISC opinion declassification** and a mandatory annual opinion release framework going forward.
5. **GSRA amicus enhancement** provisions: mandatory amicus appointment for all certifications, appeal rights, and full information access.

A three-year reauthorization without these provisions is not a reform outcome — it is a three-year runway during which every structural failure documented in this section will compound.

## Section 11: Congressional Committee Reform Pathway

### 11.1 Senate Intelligence Committee: Structural Conflicts of Interest

The Senate Select Committee on Intelligence (SSCI) was established by Senate Resolution 400 in 1976 specifically to provide the "vigilant legislative oversight" that the Church Committee found was absent. Its current composition includes members who have been subjects of retaliatory investigation by the administration (though not under FISA specifically) and members with strong pro-surveillance records. The SSCI's classified briefing access creates an oversight mechanism but also a co-optation risk: members who receive classified intelligence are bound by secrecy obligations that limit their ability to disclose problems publicly.

Senate Intelligence Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 2026 (enacted December 18, 2025, as Division F of the NDAA) included provisions enhancing whistleblower protections and Salt Typhoon-related security improvements, but did not include the warrant requirement, data broker, or PCLOB reform provisions that the GSRA proposes. The IAA's passage on a bipartisan Warner-Cotton basis through December 2025 demonstrates the Intelligence Committee's capacity for bipartisan action on intelligence community funding and authorities — but also its historical resistance to the civil liberties reforms that civil society advocates and the cross-partisan Wyden-Lee coalition demand.

**Sources**: [Senate Intelligence Committee: IAA FY2026 as Reported](https://www.intelligence.senate.gov/2025/07/17/intelligence-authorization-act-for-fiscal-year-2026-as-reported-on-july-17-2025/); [Warner: Senate Intelligence Committee Passes Intelligence Authorization Act](https://www.warner.senate.gov/newsroom/press-releases/senate-intelligence-committee-passes-intelligence-authorization-act/); [Cotton: IAA Passage Statement](https://www.cotton.senate.gov/news/press-releases/cotton-warner-statements-on-passage-of-intelligence-authorization-act)

### 11.2 OIG Access and Budget Authority Reform

The most underappreciated element of congressional intelligence oversight reform is the Inspector General access problem. Congressional intelligence committees depend on the DOJ OIG, ICIG, and agency-specific inspectors general to surface compliance failures — but these offices are funded through executive branch budget lines and subject to the political pressures their parent agencies can bring. The CNAS analysis of congressional intelligence committee effectiveness identified the absence of independent investigative resources as the primary constraint on effective oversight: committees that can compel testimony but cannot independently access classified operational records are dependent on executive branch goodwill to discharge their oversight function.

The reform pathway includes: (1) direct congressional funding lines for the ICIG and NSA/CIA IGs that cannot be reduced below inflation-adjusted FY2024 baselines without a supermajority Senate vote; (2) a statutory right for congressional intelligence committee staff to access FISC docket information in-camera without executive branch filtering; and (3) a dedicated GAO national security audit function with appropriate clearances to conduct financial and operational audits of intelligence collection programs.

## Section 12: Cross-Domain Connections and Enabling Functions

### 12.1 Domain 1 (Constitutional Architecture) and Domain 6 (Judicial Independence)

The FISC's institutional independence depends on the same structural protections that Domain 6 documents for Article III courts generally. Chief Justice Roberts' sole appointment authority for FISC designees creates a vulnerability analogous to the FISC's pre-RISAA amicus problem: a structurally sound design that depends on a single actor's integrity. Domain 1's framework for constitutional recovery — requiring that institutional protections be statutory and structural, not dependent on individual actors — applies directly to the FISC appointment mechanism. Reform proposals should include Senate confirmation of FISC designees (analogous to the UK's parliamentary confirmation hearing for Judicial Commissioners) or at minimum a bipartisan Senate Intelligence Committee role in the designation process.

The Hasbajrami Fourth Amendment ruling is directly relevant to Domain 6's litigation tracker: the Second Circuit appeal is the most significant pending surveillance constitutionality case and will determine whether a circuit court precedent establishes that warrantless Section 702 backdoor searches violate the Fourth Amendment. A favorable Second Circuit ruling before the June 12 deadline would materially strengthen the warrant-requirement argument in the Senate.

### 12.2 Domain 25 (FISA 702 April 2026 Outcome) — Section 14 Update Trigger

Domain 25, Section 14, documents the April 30 extension outcome and identifies the June 12 deadline as the next reauthorization crisis. This document serves as the underlying research base for Domain 25's Section 14 update, which should record the June 12 outcome when available. The specific items to be updated in Domain 25 upon June 12 resolution are: (a) whether a three-year reauthorization passed and on what terms; (b) whether the GSRA's warrant requirement, data broker, or PCLOB provisions were included; (c) the content of the March 2026 FISC opinion once declassified; and (d) the Second Circuit's Hasbajrami posture.

### 12.3 Domain 29 (DOJ Capture) and Domain 27 (Academic Freedom)

The SPLC prosecution's nexus to the domestic intelligence restructuring (Section 5.2 above) is a direct cross-domain connection between Domain 29 and this document. The practical implication: civil society organizations that conduct any form of undercover or informant-adjacent research on extremist organizations — including academic researchers, investigative journalists, and nonprofit watchdog groups — now operate under a legal theory that their intelligence-sharing with law enforcement could be recharacterized as wire fraud by a future administration. Domain 27's chilling effects analysis should incorporate this legal risk as a specific constraint on academic research into domestic extremism, white nationalist networks, and organized violence.

### 12.4 Domain 36 (AI Governance) — Algorithmic Surveillance Intersection

Section 702's commercial data broker loophole has a direct AI governance dimension. The FBI's contract with Babel Street for Locate X — 5,000 licenses at up to $27 million — purchases algorithmic aggregation products that combine location signals from hundreds of smartphone applications into dossiers on individual movement patterns. The algorithmic nature of these products means that what is purchased is not raw location data but a processed intelligence product — one that can associate individuals with protest attendance, immigration court visits, mosque attendance, and medical facility visits through machine-learning pattern analysis. Domain 36's framework for algorithmic accountability should extend to government procurement of algorithmic surveillance products, which currently operates outside both the FISA framework and the general AI governance proposals under discussion.

## Section 13: Implementation Timeline — When Each Reform Must Happen

### June 12, 2026 — The Hard Deadline

The June 12, 2026 deadline is the primary window for legislative action. A three-year reauthorization enacted at this deadline runs through approximately June 2029 — beyond the 2026 and 2028 election cycles. If warrant requirements, data broker closure, and PCLOB reform are not in the June 12 bill, they will not be legislative priorities again until at minimum 2029. The window for using the reform moment created by the Hasbajrami ruling, the March 2026 FISC compliance opinion, and the cross-partisan Wyden-Lee coalition is the six weeks between April 30 and June 12, 2026.

**Immediate actions required by May 15, 2026**: Declassification of March 17, 2026 FISC opinion under Cotton-Warner deal. Senate Judiciary and Intelligence Committee staff briefing on opinion contents. Distribution of this framework analysis to Tier 1 Senate contacts (Wyden, Lee, Warner, and their staff).

**June 12, 2026 minimum reform outcome**: Warrant requirement for U.S. person queries; data broker loophole closure; PCLOB restoration with statutory independence protections.

### 2026 Post-Midterm Congressional Session

The Intelligence Authorization Act for FY2027 will be the vehicle for structural committee reform: OIG budget independence, GAO national security audit authority, FISC amicus enhancements. The midterm election (November 3, 2026) may change the Senate majority, opening additional reform windows.

### 2027-2028 — FISC Reform and Structural Independence

FISC appointment reform — Senate confirmation of designees or bipartisan Intelligence Committee role — requires either statute or Senate Rules amendment. This is a longer-term structural change that the June 12 fight should frame as the next-cycle priority. Australia's IGIS model is the implementation blueprint for PCLOB structural reform; Canada's NSIRA model is the blueprint for cross-agency review authority. Both require 118th or 119th Congress legislative action.

**EU Data Transfer Dimension**: The PCLOB's inoperability has already triggered EU Parliamentary questions about the Trans-Atlantic Data Privacy Framework (TADPF). If PCLOB remains non-functional, the EU's adequacy finding — which underlies $7.1 trillion in annual trans-Atlantic data flows — is legally vulnerable. Restoring PCLOB is not just a civil liberties priority; it is an economic and diplomatic imperative that gives the June 12 reform fight a trade-policy argument independent of the constitutional analysis.
