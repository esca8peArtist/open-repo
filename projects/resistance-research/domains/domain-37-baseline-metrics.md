# Domain 37: Pre-Distribution Baseline Metrics

**Purpose**: Establish quantified baselines across six interference vectors before Phase 1 distribution so that post-distribution impact can be measured against a fixed reference point. These numbers represent the state of federal executive interference in the 2026 midterm elections as of early May 2026. Updated May 5 with April 30 – May 5 2026 developments.

## Metric 1 — DOJ Voter Roll Lawsuits: Active Case Count

**Baseline figure: 24 active lawsuits + DC (as of May 4, 2026)**

**UPDATED (May 5, 2026)**: The Justice Department has filed voter roll lawsuits against 30 states plus the District of Columbia. Current status breakdown:

- **Dismissed on merits** (6 states): California, Massachusetts, Michigan, Oregon, Rhode Island, and Arizona (April 28, 2026). Arizona's dismissal is the most recent, with Judge Brnovich ruling the DOJ "failed to state the basis for the demand." ([Votebeat](https://www.votebeat.org/arizona/2026/04/28/department-justice-trump-adrian-fontes-unredacted-voter-rolls-lawsuit-dismissed-brnovich-ruling/)) Most significantly, U.S. District Judge Mustafa T. Kasubhai issued a ruling in Oregon finding the DOJ "can no longer be presumed to act in good faith" and revealing an "improper purpose" — linking voter data collection to immigration enforcement rather than election integrity — directly undermining the legal rationale across all active cases. ([Democracy Docket](https://www.democracydocket.com/news-alerts/federal-judge-rules-doj-can-no-longer-be-trusted-in-voter-roll-crusade/))
- **Settled — data surrendered** (1 state): Oklahoma agreed to provide full voter files in exchange for dismissal, triggering a 45-day mandatory removal window for SAVE-flagged names. ([Brennan Center tracker](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/tracker-justice-department-requests-voter-information))
- **Dismissed on venue, refiled** (1 state): Georgia — initial filing in wrong district; DOJ refiled.
- **Active/pending** (24 states + DC): Colorado, Connecticut, Delaware, DC, Hawaii, Idaho, Illinois, Kentucky, Maine, Maryland, Minnesota, Nevada, New Hampshire, New Jersey, New Mexico, New York, Pennsylvania, Utah, Vermont, Virginia, Washington, West Virginia, Wisconsin, and the refiled Georgia case. ([University of Wisconsin State Democracy tracker](https://statedemocracy.law.wisc.edu/tracker-doj-lawsuits-states-voter-data/))
- **Appeals pending**: DOJ has appealed Michigan (Sixth Circuit), Oregon, and California (Ninth Circuit) dismissals.
- **Voluntary compliance** (12 states): Alaska, Arkansas, Indiana, Louisiana, Mississippi, Nebraska, Ohio, Oklahoma, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Wyoming have provided voter files without litigation.

**Legal trend line**: Every district court to rule on the merits has rejected DOJ's authority. No court has yet found for the government. The surviving legal theory (Civil Rights Act of 1960, Section 303 "voting records" argument) is being actively litigated in nine states with completed briefing. A Sixth Circuit ruling on the Michigan appeal is the highest-stakes pending outcome. ([Ohio Capital Journal explainer](https://ohiocapitaljournal.com/2026/04/06/the-department-of-justice-is-suing-states-for-sensitive-voter-data-%E2%88%92-an-election-law-scholar-explains/))

**NEW (May 2026 — post-May 4 baseline)**: The DOJ has filed motions requesting amended complaints ("do-overs") in approximately 13 of its active cases, signaling that the original legal theory is not holding in court. Additionally, a coalition of voting rights organizations (ACLU, Protect Democracy, CREW, Common Cause) filed suit in April 2026 — **Common Cause v. DOJ** — challenging the constitutional legality of building a national voter surveillance database from the collected state voter files. This is the first lawsuit challenging the *destination* of the data (not just the collection process) and represents a separate legal vector attacking the infrastructure of the voter roll seizure program. ([Democracy Docket](https://www.democracydocket.com/news-alerts/new-lawsuit-aims-to-block-trump-doj-from-building-national-voter-database-via-state-voter-roll-grab/)) | ([ACLU](https://www.aclu.org/press-releases/voting-rights-groups-sue-doj-to-block-national-voter-surveil-and-purge-database))

**Post-distribution re-measure**: Track dismissed vs. active count monthly. Metric improves if: appellate courts affirm dismissals; additional district courts dismiss on merits; DOJ drops cases in response to legal pressure. Metric worsens if: Sixth or Ninth Circuit reverses; settlement agreements proliferate; more states voluntarily surrender data.

## Metric 2 — CISA Election Security Budget: Confirmed vs. Proposed Cuts

**Baseline figure: $39.6M / 14 positions eliminated from CISA election security (confirmed, FY2026); $707M total CISA cut proposed for FY2027 including full election security program elimination**

Two distinct cuts must be tracked separately:

### FY2026 Confirmed (Already Executed)

- CISA's election security workforce was placed on administrative leave in early 2025, with election and disinformation officials among the first affected. ([CyberScoop](https://cyberscoop.com/cisa-misinformation-disinformation-administrative-leave/))
- CISA halted all support to states on election security as of March 2025. ([Votebeat](https://www.votebeat.org/2025/03/11/cisa-ends-support-election-security-nass-nased/))
- CISA's total workforce fell from approximately 3,300 employees (January 2025) to approximately 2,400 by late 2025 — a confirmed loss of roughly 900 people, representing more than 27% of total staff. ([Axios](https://www.axios.com/2025/06/03/cisa-staff-layoffs-resignations-trump-cuts))
- The Elections Infrastructure Information Sharing and Analysis Center (EI-ISAC) — the primary threat intelligence sharing hub for state and local election officials — has had its federal support suspended.

### FY2027 Proposed (Not Yet Confirmed)

- The Trump administration's FY2027 budget proposes eliminating CISA's Election Security Program entirely: 14 dedicated positions and $39.6 million in funding. ([Nextgov/FCW](https://www.nextgov.com/cybersecurity/2026/04/trump-proposes-cutting-cisa-election-security-program-fy27-budget/412672/))
- The proposal would also cut $707 million from CISA overall, eliminating 867 positions across the agency. ([AFCEA/Signal Media](https://www.afcea.org/signal-media/us-administration-proposes-707-million-cut-cisa-programs))
- Congress pushed back on the FY2026 proposal, reducing the cut from approximately $500 million to $135 million after negotiations. The FY2027 proposal is likely to face similar resistance — but the $707M figure is the administration's stated floor.
- The proposal would end CISA's vulnerability scanning program, field support to state election offices, and all election security advisors stationed across the country. ([Cybersecurity Dive](https://www.cybersecuritydive.com/news/cisa-trump-budget-fy2027-details/816855/))

**Critical distinction**: The FY2026 operational gutting (already executed via DOGE and administrative leave) is not contingent on congressional approval. The agency's practical election security capacity has already collapsed regardless of budget outcomes. The FY2027 budget proposal would codify and deepen that collapse.

**Election Security Group (ESG) — Binary Infrastructure Indicator** (NEW, May 5, 2026): The Election Security Group — the NSA and U.S. Cyber Command joint task force activated for every election since 2020 — **has not been activated for the 2026 midterms** as of April 30, 2026. General Josh Rudd, director of Cyber Command and NSA, testified to Congress: "I don't know that an ESG has been established yet." ([CNN](https://www.cnn.com/2026/04/30/politics/cyber-team-midterm-elections-foreign-meddling)) This is the single most precise measurable indicator of federal election security capability degradation: **ESG operational status = 0 as of May 5, 2026** (binary metric: either activated or not). With six months remaining before the November 2026 election, the absence of ESG activation is unprecedented.

**State-level resource crisis** (NEW, May 5, 2026): A survey of state and local election officials found that **75% report their governments lack sufficient resources to compensate for the CISA funding cuts**. This is the first quantified measure of downstream state capacity shortfall directly attributable to federal funding elimination. ([Nextgov/FCW](https://www.nextgov.com/cybersecurity/2026/04/federal-drawdown-election-support-destroyed-ongoing-relationships-experts-say/413181/))

**Post-distribution re-measure**: (1) ESG operational status: activated or not (binary check at 30-day, 60-day, 90-day marks and pre-election). (2) Congressional appropriations response to FY2027 CISA request. (3) EI-ISAC operational status. (4) Number of states with independent election cybersecurity contracts replacing CISA advisor support. (5) Any emergency supplemental appropriations for election security in pre-election period.

## Metric 4 — Section 3 Insurrection Clause Litigation Readiness

**Baseline figure: Post-Trump v. Anderson constraint in effect; Griswold-to-AG transition as advocacy vector; 0 active Section 3 disqualification proceedings for 2026 candidates as of May 4, 2026**

The Section 3 landscape as of this baseline:

**Governing legal constraint**: *Trump v. Anderson*, 601 U.S. 100 (2024), unanimously held that states have no power to enforce Section 3 against federal office candidates; enforcement requires congressional legislation. This forecloses the 2020-era state ballot exclusion strategy and requires a federal legislative or judicial pathway for any Section 3 proceeding against a candidate in the 2026 midterms. ([Law Cornell annotation](https://www.law.cornell.edu/constitution-conan/amendment-14/trump-v-anderson-and-enforcement-of-the-insurrection-clause-disqualification-clause))

**State-level officer applications**: *Trump v. Anderson* explicitly preserved state authority to enforce Section 3 against state office candidates. This creates a potential application window for state-level candidates in the 2026 midterms who participated in January 6 or related efforts, though no active proceedings of this type are confirmed as of this baseline.

**Griswold's transition**: Colorado Secretary of State Jena Griswold — the respondent in *Anderson v. Griswold* and nationally recognized election security figure — announced her candidacy for Colorado Attorney General in April 2025. ([Colorado Sun](https://coloradosun.com/2025/04/07/jena-griswold-colorado-attorney-general/)) If elected in November 2026, Griswold would hold state enforcement authority with demonstrated institutional will and direct litigation experience. Her Democratic primary is June 30, 2026. This transition is a structural development in state-level Section 3 enforcement capacity — she moves from the election administration position where she was constrained by *Trump v. Anderson* to a law enforcement position where she would hold independent enforcement authority over state officers.

**March 31, 2026 Election Executive Order** (NEW, May 5, 2026): Trump signed a sweeping election integrity executive order on March 31, 2026. The order restricts mail balloting, adds new documentation requirements, and threatens millions of eligible voters including military and overseas voters. A coalition of voting rights organizations (ACLU, Protect Democracy, Campaign Legal Center, Common Cause) filed suit in federal court challenging it as illegal interference with federal elections. ([ACLU](https://www.aclu.org/press-releases/voting-rights-groups-challenge-executive-order-on-mail-in-ballots-as-illegal-interference-in-elections)) This is a new election interference vector created post-May 4 baseline.

**State AG coalition**: 22 Democratic state AGs plus DC have adopted coordinated legal strategy on election protection, with over 75 lawsuits filed against the Trump administration in 2025. Washington AG Bob Ferguson has filed suit to block the March 31 elections executive order. ([Washington State AG](https://www.atg.wa.gov/news/news-releases/ag-brown-sues-block-executive-order-undermines-voting-rights)) The coalition is functionally operative as an election protection infrastructure; Section 3 enforcement against state candidates would fit within its mandate.

**Legislative gap**: No bill to create the federal Section 5 "appropriate legislation" required by *Trump v. Anderson* has advanced in the current Congress. The enforcement gap remains open.

**Post-distribution re-measure**: Track Griswold primary outcome (June 30, 2026); any new Section 3 filings in state courts against 2026 candidates; congressional bill introductions under Section 5; state AG coordinated enforcement statements citing Section 3 authority over state-level candidates.

## Metric 5 — State-Level Election Interference Legislation (2025–2026)

**Baseline figure: 32 restrictive voting laws enacted in 2025 across 17 states; 8 election interference laws enacted across 7 states; 31 of the 32 restrictive laws fully in effect for the 2026 midterms**

The Brennan Center's 2025 full-year review documents:

**Restrictive voting laws**: 32 enacted (17 states), matching the highest annual total since Brennan Center tracking began in 2011. ([Brennan Center 2025 Annual Review](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/state-voting-laws-roundup-2025-review))

States with enacted restrictions: Arkansas, Florida, Idaho, Indiana, Kansas, Kentucky, Montana, North Dakota, Ohio, Oklahoma, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Utah, West Virginia, and Wyoming.

Primary restriction types:
- **Mail ballot restrictions** (8 laws, 7 states): Kansas, North Dakota, Ohio, and Utah passed laws barring mail ballots received after Election Day — a direct attack on the election administration flexibility that protected close-margin states in 2020.
- **Voter purge expansions**: Expanded use of unreliable data sources (including SAVE) to trigger removal proceedings; Oklahoma's legislature specifically authorized SAVE-based purges.
- **ID requirement tightening**: Multiple states tightened documentation requirements for registration and voting.

**Election interference laws** (power-transfer provisions): 8 enacted across 7 states — Indiana, Iowa, Kansas, Louisiana, New Hampshire, Texas, and Utah. These laws transfer authority from nonpartisan county clerks and election administrators to partisan state legislative actors, creating a framework in which result certification can be delayed or contested by political appointees. ([Voting Rights Lab 2025 analysis](https://votingrightslab.org/report/2025-legislative-sessions-to-date-key-election-policy-trends/))

**Legislative activity pipeline**: Lawmakers in at least 47 states considered approximately 486 restrictive voting bills in 2025. The bills that failed in 2025 are candidates for re-introduction in 2026 legislative sessions still underway.

**Countervailing expansive laws**: 25 states enacted 30 expansive voting laws — the defensive half of the legislative landscape. However, the expansive laws are concentrated in blue states that are not the competitive midterm battlegrounds.

**Post-distribution re-measure**: Track 2026 legislative session outcomes (Q1-Q2 2026 sessions currently active); new bills passing; any federal preemption litigation arising from state power-transfer laws; Voting Rights Lab and Brennan Center tracker updates.

## Metric 6 — SAVE Database Error Rate and Threat Level

**Baseline figure: 24,000+ voters flagged as potential noncitizens across 24 states; 81% false positive rate documented in Missouri; 21,000 flagged in states with sharing agreements**

The SAVE database interference vector is measurable across three dimensions:

**Scale**: DHS has flagged more than 24,000 names on U.S. voter rolls as potentially registered noncitizens, as of April 23, 2026. ([Washington Times](https://www.washingtontimes.com/news/2026/apr/23/dhs-identified-24000-potential-noncitizens-voter-rolls/)) This figure covers states with voluntary sharing agreements; the DOJ litigation is designed to expand this to all 30+ defendant states.

**Error rate**: Two states now have documented SAVE false-positive rates:

- **Missouri** (November 2025): SAVE flagged 691 registered voters as potential noncitizens. After immediate verification, 35% (approximately 242) were confirmed naturalized citizens. After passport cross-referencing, the list fell to 133 — an **81% false positive rate**. Missouri county clerks (both Republican and Democrat) formally warned state leadership that SAVE "repeatedly flags individuals we know to be U.S. citizens." ([ProPublica SAVE analysis](https://www.propublica.org/article/save-voter-citizenship-tool-mistakes-confusion))

- **Idaho** (April 2026 — NEW): Idaho's state review using SAVE initially flagged 760 potential noncitizens among 1.1 million voters. After investigation, only approximately 36 were referred to law enforcement — a **95%+ false positive rate**, significantly higher than Missouri's 81%. ([Popular Information](https://popular.info/p/the-broken-database-that-could-upend)) This two-state evidence pattern (Missouri 81%, Idaho 95%) is more resistant to the "outlier" rebuttal and strengthens the baseline documentation of systemic SAVE unreliability.

**Nationwide scale applied to error rate**: If the 81% Missouri error rate holds at the 24,000 figure, approximately 19,440 of the flagged individuals are eligible citizens. Under the Oklahoma consent decree model, states have 45 days to remove flagged voters. A wrongful removal at this scale, executed in the 90-day NVRA quiet period (which begins August 7, 2026), would be a per se federal law violation.

**Threat level compared to 2020**: In 2020, there was no federal mechanism compelling state voter file sharing with DHS, no Election Integrity Network appointees in CISA or the DHS elections position, and no nationwide-jurisdiction DOJ election prosecutor. The SAVE database was not deployed as a voter roll verification tool. The construction of this infrastructure between 2025 and the present represents a qualitatively new interference architecture with no 2020 analogue.

## Threat Level Summary: Compared to 2020 Baseline

The 2020 election interference threat operated primarily at the post-election stage — legal challenges, state legislature pressure, and the January 6 certification contest. The administration's 2020 interference apparatus was improvised, understaffed, and depended on persuading officials to defy established processes.

The 2026 interference architecture differs structurally:

1. **Pre-election infrastructure is in place**: Voter roll lawsuits, SAVE database deployment, and data-sharing agreements are operational, not planned. The DOJ can begin systematic voter removal proceedings in compliant states without any additional legal action.

2. **Institutional capture is complete, not attempted**: In 2020, election security officials (Krebs, Barr on fraud claims) pushed back against the administration and kept agencies functioning. In 2026, election deniers *lead* CISA-equivalent policy, DHS elections, DOJ Civil Rights, and hold a nationwide-jurisdiction election prosecutor. There is no internal resistance infrastructure at the department level.

3. **Legal theory for election-night intervention exists**: The Fulton County warrant (cross-district, signed by a Missouri prosecutor, executed by the FBI) is documented precedent for the Albus playbook on election night. In 2020 there was no named prosecutor with nationwide jurisdiction and documented plans for election materials seizure.

4. **State legislative power-transfer laws are newly in place**: Eight election interference laws in seven states, all fully in effect for November 2026, create a certification delay or alteration architecture that did not exist in 2020.

The one meaningful structural constraint that has strengthened since 2020: coordinated state AG resistance (22 states + DC), Democratic secretaries of state in key battleground states, and democracy litigation organizations (Democracy Docket, Campaign Legal Center, ACLU) are more prepared, better funded, and more coordinated than in 2020. Every district court to rule on the merits has rejected DOJ's authority. The legal resistance is winning, but it is running against an operational timeline — the NVRA 90-day quiet period (August 7, 2026) and election-night itself.

## Measurement Protocol: Post-Distribution Re-Measurement

To assess whether Domain 37 distribution (through any of Paths A, A+37, or B) has contributed to measurable resistance outcomes, re-measure the following on the schedule indicated:

**Monthly re-measure (June, July, August, September 2026)**:
- DOJ lawsuit active count vs. dismissed count (source: Wisconsin SDRI tracker + Brennan Center tracker)
- SAVE false-positive incidents — new state reports of erroneous flagging (source: state AG press releases, Votebeat, ProPublica)
- Congressional appropriations — any emergency election security funding passed (source: Congress.gov)

**Pre-NVRA quiet period (before August 7, 2026)**:
- Number of states complying with DOJ voter roll demands (12 voluntary + Oklahoma = 13 baseline)
- Volume of wrongful removal notices sent under SAVE-based proceedings
- Count of emergency TRO filings by state AGs or civil rights organizations blocking removals

**Election cycle milestones**:
- Griswold Democratic primary outcome (June 30, 2026)
- Any Section 3 proceedings against state-level 2026 candidates
- Number of states that have retained independent election security advisors as CISA replacement
- Any new Election Integrity Network federal appointments

**Post-election (November–December 2026)**:
- Whether Thomas Albus or any nationwide-jurisdiction DOJ prosecutor took any action on election night or post-election
- Whether any state complied with Albus/DOJ document or voting machine access demands
- Number of election interference law certifications delayed or contested in the 8-state framework

**Impact attribution note**: Direct causal attribution between Domain 37 distribution and any of these outcomes is not possible. The measurement protocol tracks whether the baseline metrics moved in the direction of increased or decreased resistance capability during the distribution period. Correlation with distribution timing, distribution channel reach, and coalition adoption provides the evidentiary basis for impact assessment.
