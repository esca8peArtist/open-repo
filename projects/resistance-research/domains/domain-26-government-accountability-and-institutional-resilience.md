# Domain 26: Government Accountability and Institutional Resilience

**Created**: Session 503 (2026-04-27)
**Priority designation**: Core domain — democratic renewal proposal infrastructure
**Cross-domain connections**: Domain 19f (War Powers Reform), Domain 2 (Civil Service/Executive Accountability), Domain 6 (Judicial Independence), Domain 18 (Defense/Military Spending), Domain 4 (Economic Policy)

---

## The Central Finding

The accountability architecture of American democratic government is not merely weakened in 2026 — it is being systematically dismantled faster than it can be reconstructed. The key finding from this research is not that individual mechanisms have failed; it is that the failures are coordinated across every layer of the oversight system simultaneously. Federal inspectors general fired in mass, their replacement positions vacant, their budgets now targeted for further cuts in FY2027. The CDC surveillance infrastructure for maternal mortality gutted by layoffs, data collection paused indefinitely, and state committees politically pressured into omitting cause-of-death analysis that implicates abortion bans. EMTALA enforcement guidance rescinded, leaving 9.3 million women of reproductive age in states with no health exception for abortion without federal protection in emergency rooms. And at the state level, the ballot initiative process — the last democratic instrument that has not yet been captured by partisan executives — is now under legislative siege in 43 states simultaneously.

The question this domain must answer is not whether accountability mechanisms are failing. They demonstrably are. The question is: which mechanisms retain enough structural integrity to be rebuilt, and which require new institutional design from scratch?

## Section 1: The 2026 Accountability Crisis — A Composite Snapshot

### 1.1 The Inspector General System: Mass Firing, Vacancy, and Now Defunding

The most structurally direct attack on executive accountability has been the systematic removal of federal inspectors general. On January 24, 2025, Trump fired at least 17 inspectors general simultaneously by email — a violation of the 1978 Inspector General Act, which requires 30 days' advance notice to Congress with substantive case-specific reasons for removal. The firings were declared unlawful by federal judge Ana C. Reyes on September 24, 2025, but she declined to reinstate the fired officials, noting that the administration would simply re-fire them after providing the legally required notice.

The immediate effect: over 75% of presidentially appointed inspector general positions are now vacant, either through firing, forced resignation, or pre-existing vacancies from the Biden administration that were never filled. The Council of the Inspectors General on Integrity and Efficiency — the umbrella office supporting the IG community — has had its appropriated funds unilaterally blocked by the White House.

In October 2025, the administration took the oversight gutting a step further: at least 15 IG office websites went dark after OMB withheld funds, with the administration characterizing the action as punishment for IGs who "lied to the public." The targeting was specific: the IG at HHS, overseeing the largest domestic discretionary budget, and the IG at Defense, overseeing a department that has now failed its seventh consecutive audit covering $3.8 trillion in assets.

The FY2027 budget proposal, released in April 2026, extends the attack from personnel to budgets. Cabinet department IGs would receive on average 12% less than fiscal 2024 levels — with the most extreme cuts targeting Interior (28%) and Justice (28%). Overall IG staffing is projected to fall an additional 9% from current levels, compounding a 10% workforce reduction in 2025. The Partnership for Public Service's assessment is direct: "A 28% budget reduction does not simply mean doing the same work more efficiently. It means doing less of it." Every dollar spent on inspectors general in fiscal 2024 yielded $18 in recovered funds through fraud detection and waste elimination. The administration is proposing to spend fewer of those dollars.

The constitutional mechanism these actions are dismantling is horizontal accountability — the checks within the executive branch itself. IGs are not external checks like Congress or courts; they are intra-executive accountability mechanisms created by Congress to provide independence from political interference within the departments they oversee. Their systematic elimination does not just reduce oversight of individual programs. It removes the infrastructure that makes oversight possible at all.

### 1.2 EMTALA and the Dismantling of Emergency Healthcare Accountability

The Emergency Medical Treatment and Labor Act (EMTALA) is a 1986 federal statute requiring hospitals accepting Medicare payments — which is nearly all hospitals — to provide stabilizing care to any patient who presents with an emergency medical condition, regardless of their ability to pay. It is one of the last federal mandates of universal minimum care. The Trump administration's treatment of EMTALA in 2025 illustrates a specific accountability failure mode: not repealing a law, but withdrawing the guidance and enforcement framework that gives it operational meaning.

On June 3, 2025, CMS rescinded the Biden administration's July 2022 EMTALA guidance, which had clarified that EMTALA requires emergency abortion care when that is the necessary stabilizing treatment, regardless of state abortion bans. The rescission was timed to the same day an anti-abortion medical association dismissed its challenge to the guidance — a coordination that eliminated any need for the administration to defend its policy change in court.

The legal status post-rescission is deliberately ambiguous. CMS announced it would "continue to enforce EMTALA" for emergencies, but simultaneously withdrew the guidance that told hospitals what EMTALA required of them when state abortion bans conflicted with emergency care obligations. The practical result: hospital administrators in ban states now make conservative legal decisions based on state criminal law rather than federal civil regulatory guidance, because the federal guidance is gone and the criminal penalty is present.

The scale of affected population is specific. KFF analysis identifies six states with health-exception-free abortion bans covering approximately 9.3 million women of reproductive age. In Idaho specifically, the only entity with current court protection from EMTALA preemption of the state abortion ban is St. Luke's Health System — a single hospital network whose district court protection does not extend to any other Idaho provider.

The accountability failure here is structural, not merely legal. The 1986 EMTALA statute still exists. But a statute without enforcement guidance in an area where state law creates criminal liability for compliance operates, in practice, as if the statute does not exist. The rescission demonstrates how accountability mechanisms can be functionally eliminated without legislative repeal — through withdrawal of the administrative infrastructure that makes them operative.

Sources: [KFF: Trump administration's dismissal of EMTALA litigation](https://www.kff.org/quick-take/what-does-the-trump-administrations-dismissal-of-emtala-litigation-mean-for-emergency-abortion-care/); [ACLU: EMTALA guidance rescission](https://www.aclu.org/press-releases/trump-administration-rescinds-emtala-guidance-and-sends-clear-signal-emergency-abortion-care-remains-at-risk); [Foley Hoag: CMS Rescinds Post-Dobbs EMTALA Guidance](https://foleyhoag.com/news-and-insights/publications/alerts-and-updates/2025/june/cms-rescinds-post-dobbs-emtala-guidance-raising-new-questions-for-emergency-departments/)

### 1.3 The CDC Maternal Mortality Surveillance Collapse

The accountability failure in maternal mortality data represents a different model: not active rescission, but the quiet elimination of the surveillance capacity that makes accountability possible. If you cannot measure it, you cannot govern it.

The CDC's Division of Reproductive Health (DRH) was the primary federal infrastructure for tracking maternal mortality, abortion trends, fertility treatment outcomes, and pregnancy risk factors. Under Health Secretary Kennedy's 2025 restructuring of HHS, two of the three branches within DRH were eliminated entirely, including their leadership. The full six-person Assisted Reproductive Technology surveillance team was terminated and their work paused "indefinitely." Most critically, the entire staff overseeing the Pregnancy Risk Assessment Monitoring System (PRAMS) — the CDC's primary data source on experiences around the time of pregnancy — was placed on administrative leave, halting data collection.

The institutional accountability failure predated these cuts. ProPublica's investigation found that despite the CDC funding approximately $90 million in state maternal mortality review committees, the agency issued no guidance to those committees directing them to assess how abortion bans factor into deaths. No checkbox exists on CDC review forms for abortion-ban-related contributing factors. A 73-page HHS maternal mortality report to Congress contained "not a single reference to abortion access." The CDC spokesperson's response — that existing processes were "sufficient" — was directly contradicted by researchers who called it "pushing it under the rug."

State-level political interference compounded the federal gap. In Texas, the state delayed case reviews and eventually stopped sharing maternal death data with the CDC entirely, forgoing federal funding to avoid accountability. In Georgia, political pressure following investigative reporting on preventable deaths linked to abortion restrictions led the state to disband its Maternal Mortality Review Committee. In Idaho and Georgia, documented cases of preventable maternal deaths — Amber Thurman and Candi Miller — show the gap between what happened to patients and what was officially recorded as contributing to their deaths.

The GEPI analysis of 2019-2023 CDC data found that maternal mortality rates in states with abortion bans were twice as high as in states with protective policies, as of 2023 — the last year for which reliable annual data is publicly available. The surveillance gaps created in 2025 mean 2024 and 2025 data will be incomplete or absent when researchers attempt to measure the full impact of abortion bans on maternal mortality.

This is the accountability failure in its starkest form: the information infrastructure that would allow democratic governance to respond to a policy's consequences has been deliberately dismantled during the window when those consequences are most acute.

Sources: [ProPublica: The CDC Hasn't Asked States to Track Deaths Linked to Abortion Bans](https://www.propublica.org/article/abortion-ban-deaths-cdc-maternal-health-care); [STAT News: PRAMS maternal mortality database in limbo](https://www.statnews.com/2025/04/01/prams-maternal-mortality-cdc-layoffs/); [Washington Post: CDC cuts hamstring maternal health data](https://www.washingtonpost.com/health/2025/04/19/cdc-cuts-maternal-mortality-fertility/); [GEPI: Maternal Mortality in the United States After Abortion Bans](https://thegepi.org/maternal-mortality-abortion-bans/)

## Section 2: Structural Vulnerabilities — Why the System Breaks

### 2.1 The Three Failure Modes

*[Updated May 7, 2026]* Two accountability data points that sharpen the structural picture documented in this section: First, the DOJ Public Integrity Section — historically the primary federal body prosecuting corruption by public officials — has been reduced from 36 career prosecutors to 2 as of early May 2026. The 94% staffing reduction eliminates the institutional capacity for federal corruption prosecution regardless of any individual case decision, converting the reduction into a structural accountability gap independent of prosecutorial discretion. Second, GAO's May 2026 annual improper payments report found $186 billion in federal improper payments in fiscal 2025 — a 23% increase over fiscal 2024 — with the increase directly attributed to the dismantlement of inspector general oversight capacity that previously generated recovery actions. The $186B figure represents funds lost to fraud, waste, and error that the hollowed-out IG system can no longer detect or recover.

The 2026 accountability crisis is not a single failure; it is the convergence of three distinct failure modes that reinforce each other.

**Administrative capture without legislative change**: EMTALA, maternal mortality surveillance, IG oversight — none of these have been repealed. The underlying statutes exist. What has been dismantled is the administrative infrastructure that gives the statutes operational meaning: guidance documents, enforcement policies, trained staff, data systems, interagency reporting requirements. This mode of institutional attack is harder to litigate than outright statutory repeal, because it does not create a clean legal question for courts to decide. The administrative state is designed to have discretion. When that discretion is exercised systematically to hollow out accountability functions, the legal remedies are slow and uncertain.

**The oversight vacuum problem**: Accountability systems depend on being able to see what is happening before deciding how to respond. IG offices investigate wrongdoing after the fact but also produce the audit findings that make oversight possible. CDC surveillance systems produce the mortality data that would allow Congress, states, and the public to assess whether policies are working. When these systems are dismantled, the accountability failure is not merely that bad actors go unpunished — it is that the information needed to identify bad actors in the first place disappears. The vacuum is self-reinforcing: without data, there is nothing to investigate; without investigations, there is no pressure to restore data collection.

**Norm violation without sanction**: The IG firings violated the Inspector General Act. They were declared unlawful by a federal court. The administration fired the IGs anyway, provided the required notice retroactively after the court ruling, and the net result was the same: the IGs are gone. This pattern — violate a norm, absorb the legal consequence (if any), achieve the political result regardless — has been documented across accountability domains in this research archive. The structural implication is that accountability mechanisms that depend on voluntary compliance from the executive they are designed to oversee are inherently fragile. Courts can declare violations unlawful; they cannot unfire watchdogs or un-destroy data.

### 2.2 The Skrmetti Precedent and the Rational Basis Ratchet

The Supreme Court's June 2025 decision in United States v. Skrmetti — upholding Tennessee's ban on gender-affirming care for minors by a 6-3 margin — represents a structural vulnerability of a different kind: the judicial narrowing of the equal protection review framework in ways that limit courts' capacity to constrain policy-driven accountability failures.

The Skrmetti majority held that Tennessee's law did not classify based on sex or transgender status and was therefore subject only to rational basis review — the most deferential standard in constitutional law, which requires courts to uphold any law that could conceivably serve a legitimate government purpose. The downstream impact extends beyond gender-affirming care. Harvard Law Review analysis identifies Skrmetti as potentially restructuring the equal protection framework in ways that remove "heightened scrutiny" from a range of medical regulation contexts. When courts apply rational basis review, the accountability mechanism that the equal protection clause provides becomes largely unavailable for medical regulation challenges.

Crucially, Skrmetti validated the pattern of states using medical regulation as a vehicle for policies that earlier equal protection doctrine might have reached differently. The decision cleared the path for states to impose mandatory reporting requirements on trans healthcare providers, restrict telehealth prescribing across state lines, and create civil and criminal penalties for out-of-state care facilitation — all of which expand state surveillance and accountability burdens on patients and providers, while simultaneously contracting patients' ability to seek legal accountability for harms.

The institutional resilience implication is that the judicial oversight mechanism — historically the check of last resort when executive and legislative action fails — is operating under a reduced-scope equal protection framework at the same time the executive branch is dismantling administrative accountability systems.

Skrmetti (23-477)](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/24pdf/23-477_2cp3.pdf); [Harvard Law Review: Skrmetti Beyond Scrutiny](https://harvardlawreview.org/print/vol-139/skrmetti-beyond-scrutiny/); [PMC: Is Our Post-Skrmetti World Like a Post-Dobbs World?](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12333851/)

## Section 3: Institutional Resilience Mechanisms — What Is Working

### 3.1 State-Level Shield Laws and the Dual Federalism Effect

The most functional accountability mechanisms operating in 2026 are state-level, not federal. The dismantling of federal oversight infrastructure has — as a structural byproduct — elevated the importance of state-level mechanisms that would have been considered redundant under normal federal governance.

The clearest example is the dual-track system created by state shield laws in response to Dobbs and the EMTALA rescission. Approximately 17 states have enacted "shield laws" protecting access to gender-affirming care, and a comparable number have enacted healthcare privacy protections that function as counterweights to federal enforcement rollbacks. Washington State's My Health My Data Act, which came into full enforcement effect in March 2024 and has been actively litigated through 2025, represents the most legally sophisticated version of this model.

The MHMDA is structurally designed as an accountability mechanism, not merely a privacy protection. Any violation of the Act is a per se violation of the Washington Consumer Protection Act — a design choice that routes enforcement through the Attorney General's office and through private right of action simultaneously. This dual-track enforcement means accountability does not depend on federal regulatory will. Washington residents can sue companies directly for unauthorized collection or sharing of health data; the AG can pursue injunctions and civil penalties independently.

The EFF's analysis of the MHMDA's first full year of enforcement identifies a key structural feature: the law applies to any entity that collects health data from Washington State residents, regardless of where that entity is physically located. This extraterritorial scope was designed to prevent the accountability gap created when data flows across state lines to jurisdictions with weaker protections. The 2030 statutory review committee requirement — mandating a report to the governor and legislature on enforcement actions — creates a built-in institutional accountability loop that is absent from most federal regulatory frameworks.

Sources: [Washington State AG: Protecting Washingtonians' Health Data](https://www.atg.wa.gov/protecting-washingtonians-personal-health-data-and-privacy); [EFF: How to Build on Washington's My Health My Data Act](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2025/06/how-build-washingtons-my-health-my-data-act); [EPIC: MHMDA Goes Into Effect](https://epic.org/alive-and-kicking-washington-states-my-health-my-data-act-goes-into-effect-today/)

### 3.2 Sanctuary Jurisdictions and Anticommandeering

The sanctuary jurisdiction litigation represents a different resilience mechanism: states and localities using the Constitution's anticommandeering doctrine to limit the federal government's ability to conscript state resources for federal enforcement.

After Trump's January 2025 executive orders targeting sanctuary jurisdictions, courts imposed a preliminary injunction blocking the administration from withholding or freezing federal funding from 50 jurisdictions representing more than 30 million residents. The constitutional basis — that the federal government cannot force state and local governments to use their resources for federal purposes — was established in the first Trump administration and has been consistently reaffirmed.

The DOJ's August 2025 publication of a list of "sanctuary" jurisdictions including 13 states, 18 cities, and 4 counties represents an attempt to use public naming and funding threats as leverage where direct legal commandeering is unavailable. But the constitutional protection has held through litigation: the Brennan Center and Public Rights Project have documented the court record of successful challenges to funding conditions. As of early 2026, the injunction protecting these jurisdictions remains in effect.

The resilience lesson is structural: federalism — specifically, the constitutional principle that states are not subsidiaries of the federal government — provides institutional resistance that does not depend on federal executive goodwill. States that have built infrastructure to resist commandeering are exercising a constitutionally grounded accountability mechanism that constrains executive overreach.

Sources: [Brennan Center: Federal efforts to punish sanctuary cities are unconstitutional](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/analysis-opinion/federal-efforts-punish-sanctuary-cities-are-unconstitutional); [Public Rights Project: Court rules against funding freeze for sanctuary jurisdictions](https://www.publicrightsproject.org/news-insights/press-releases/court-rules-trump-administration-cannot-withhold-or-freeze-federal-funding-to-cities-and-counties-it-labels-as-sanctuary-jurisdictions/)

### 3.3 Direct Democracy Under Siege — But Still Functioning

The ballot initiative process represents the accountability mechanism that most directly bypasses captured legislatures and executive branches. The 2025-2026 data presents a deeply mixed picture: the mechanism is operating, producing results, and surviving. But it is simultaneously under coordinated legislative attack across 43 states in ways that threaten its structural integrity.

The scale of the 2026 ballot initiative landscape is substantial. As of March 31, 2026, the Ballot Initiative Strategy Center tracks 421 measures including 83 certified for 2026 ballots. Of those certified measures, 31 are democracy-related — covering voting rules, redistricting, and election power — with another 87 democracy-related proposals in the pipeline. The Stateline reporting from April 2026 identifies ballots becoming the primary terrain for fundamental democratic structure questions that state legislatures have proven unwilling to resolve in the public interest.

But the coexistence finding is the most important for this domain: voter-approved measures are no longer reliably implemented. The Ballot Initiative Strategy Center's December 2025 analysis of trends identifies "the migration of conflict after voter approval" as the defining pattern of 2025. Nebraska delayed and restricted voter-approved medical cannabis rollout. Missouri maintained legal resistance to voter-approved reproductive rights after three separate statewide approvals. The pattern is documented across multiple states: legislative or executive bodies are using post-election litigation, budget restriction, implementation delays, and rulemaking to neutralize voter-approved policy changes.

The structural attack on the initiative process itself compounds this. In 2025 legislative sessions, 295 bills were introduced in 43 states targeting the ballot initiative process; at least 156 sought to restrict or undermine it. Four states placed supermajority requirements for constitutional amendments on their 2026 ballots — Missouri requiring majority approval in each congressional district (a geographic veto that maps districts designed for legislative races onto a statewide direct democracy process), and North Dakota, South Dakota, and Utah requiring 60% supermajority thresholds. Courts have become central arbiters, with judges enforcing voter-approved reforms facing political backlash, impeachment threats, and public intimidation.

Sources: [Ballot Initiative Strategy Center Hot Sheet](https://ballot.org/the-hot-sheet/); [Stateline: Ballots become battlegrounds](https://stateline.org/2026/04/16/ballots-become-battlegrounds-for-voting-rules-redistricting-election-power/); [BISC: Rising authoritarian tactics against citizen-led initiatives](https://ballot.org/news/december-2025-hot-sheet-the-rising-authoritarian-tactics-against-citizen-led-initiatives/)

## Section 4: Reform Pathways — Specific, Actionable Solutions with International Precedent

### 4.1 Protecting the Administrative Infrastructure

The most actionable near-term reforms address the administrative infrastructure that has been hollowed out. Statutory action can restore what executive action dismantled — though this requires congressional majorities that currently do not exist. The value of specifying these reforms now is to have a ready program for the next window of legislative opportunity.

**Mandatory IG staffing floors**: Amend the Inspector General Act to establish minimum staffing levels for each IG office, with a prohibition on presidential reduction below those levels without congressional approval. The current statute protects IGs from removal without notice but does not protect their budgets. The FY2027 proposal to cut IG funding by 12-28% exploits this gap.

**Appropriations earmarks for surveillance systems**: Specific, line-item appropriations for CDC's PRAMS data system, the Maternal Mortality Review Committee infrastructure, and DRH staffing would prevent administrative elimination through budget manipulation. The current system of block appropriations to HHS creates discretion that has been exercised to eliminate specific programs without statutory authorization.

**EMTALA enforcement guidance codification**: Convert the 2022 EMTALA guidance — that EMTALA preempts state abortion bans in emergency medical contexts — into statutory language, removing the ability of any administration to rescind it by executive action. The statute itself already arguably requires this result; codification removes the legal uncertainty that currently operates in favor of the most restrictive interpretation.

**The Canadian model**: Canada's Office of the Auditor General is a fully independent Parliamentary officer with statutory authority to audit any government entity, guaranteed funding, and a 10-year non-renewable term that prevents political pressure through reappointment. The U.S. Government Accountability Office has similar structural independence — but unlike IGs, it is a congressional rather than executive-branch entity. Expanding GAO's mandate and resources to cover program evaluation across domains where IG capacity has been eliminated would partially substitute for the hollowed-out IG system.

### 4.2 Data Transparency as Accountability Infrastructure

The CDC surveillance collapse demonstrates that data collection is itself an accountability mechanism, not merely a precondition for one. Several states have demonstrated effective models.

**Mandatory state-level maternal mortality reporting with federal uniform standards**: California's Maternal Quality Care Collaborative has produced some of the most reliable state-level maternal mortality data in the country, using standardized case review forms and protected reporting structures. A federal statute establishing uniform reporting standards — with CDC funding conditioned on compliance and independent of state discretion about what to include — would address the political interference problem documented in Texas, Georgia, and Idaho.

**My Health My Data model expansion**: The Washington MHMDA's extraterritorial scope, private right of action, and mandatory review cycle provides a template for federal health data privacy legislation. The EU's General Data Protection Regulation contains a similar accountability structure at a larger scale, with mandatory breach notification, data protection impact assessments, and an independent Data Protection Authority with guaranteed funding and enforcement authority. The GDPR's institutional design was specifically developed to ensure that privacy protection does not depend on political will — the accountability mechanism is built into the compliance structure.

**Abortion care data collection restoration**: The specific gaps identified in the ProPublica investigation — no checkbox on CDC review forms for abortion-ban-related contributing factors, no guidance to state committees — are technically simple to fix and structurally important. Restoring them requires CDC administrative action that any future administration could prioritize on day one.

### 4.3 Structural Reforms to Ballot Initiative Integrity

The coordination of ballot initiative attacks across 43 states is not organic; it reflects model legislation and coordinated strategy. Defending the mechanism requires comparable coordination and, ultimately, constitutional protection.

**Federal floor for direct democracy rights**: The U.S. Constitution does not explicitly protect the right to initiative or referendum. Courts have treated these as state-law questions. A federal statute establishing minimum procedural protections for voter-approved measures — including prohibitions on legislative supermajority requirements retroactively applied to citizen initiatives, and timelines for implementation — would create federal ground to stand on against post-election nullification. The Guarantee Clause of Article IV (guaranteeing "a republican form of government" to each state) has been held non-justiciable, but Congress has authority to legislate under Section 5 of the Fourteenth Amendment to protect voter rights in ways that could cover post-election nullification of voter-approved measures.

**Independent implementation commissions**: Several states — notably Colorado's Independent Redistricting Commissions and Arizona's counterpart — have shown that removing implementation authority from legislative bodies that opposed a voter-approved measure can protect against nullification. Generalizing this model to voter-approved measures that require legislative implementation would address the "migration of conflict after voter approval" pattern. The Swiss model of binding referendum with automatic implementation deadlines — if a measure passes, specific legal timelines for implementation are automatically triggered — is the clearest international precedent.

**Judicial protection for implementation**: Post-Skrmetti, courts applying deferential review standards will be less available as backstops against implementation resistance. This makes building implementation protection into the initiative text itself — including specified remedies, automatic funding triggers, and contempt provisions for implementation delay — increasingly necessary as a design feature.

### 4.4 Strengthening Independent Oversight Institutions

The international comparative literature on institutional resilience identifies a common feature of systems that have survived executive overreach: accountability mechanisms with built-in independence that does not depend on executive cooperation.

**Germany's Federal Audit Office (Bundesrechnungshof)**: A constitutionally established institution with guaranteed funding, independent of executive control, with authority to audit all federal expenditures and report directly to the Bundestag. The U.S. GAO has comparable constitutional grounding but lacks the express constitutional guarantee that protects the German institution from budget manipulation. A constitutional amendment explicitly protecting the GAO and IG system from executive defunding would address the vulnerability the FY2027 budget proposal exploits.

**New Zealand's Parliamentary Commissioner model**: New Zealand's Ombudsman, Controller and Auditor-General, and Privacy Commissioner are independent parliamentary officers with guaranteed funding and operational independence from the executive. Their jurisdictions overlap — creating redundancy that ensures no single institutional attack can eliminate oversight of a domain. The overlap is a feature, not a bug.

**Fixed-term, non-renewable IG appointments**: The current IG appointment model — presidentially appointed, subject to removal with 30-day notice — creates structural vulnerability to political pressure over reappointment. Fixed-term, non-renewable appointments with removal only for cause (defined specifically in statute, not at presidential discretion) would protect IGs from both the threat of firing and the incentive to self-censor to preserve their positions.

## Section 5: Timeline and Conditions for Implementation

### 5.1 Immediate Actions (Crisis Window 2026)

The most urgent accountability actions do not require new legislation — they require using existing legal tools at state and local level while federal mechanisms are compromised.

State AGs with existing consumer protection authority can and should pursue EMTALA violations under state law, parallel to federal enforcement gaps. Washington's MHMDA enforcement infrastructure provides a model. The sanctuary jurisdiction preliminary injunction should be actively supported in appellate proceedings — the constitutional principle it protects (anticommandeering) is more valuable than any individual funding dispute it covers.

At the federal level, the Senate confirmation process for IG nominees — including acting officials — is one of the few legislative leverage points available. Holds on agency nominees in exchange for IG appointment confirmations are a historically used tool that does not require new legislation.

Congress should use its oversight hearing authority to document the accountability failures catalogued in this domain on the public record, creating both political pressure and an evidentiary record for future litigation and legislative action.

### 5.2 Near-Term (6-18 months, post-2026 election)

A Democratic House majority in the 2026 midterms would restore subpoena authority and appropriations leverage. The specific reforms most achievable in a changed House:

- Appropriations language conditioning HHS funding on restoration of DRH surveillance programs
- A concurrent resolution establishing that EMTALA preempts state abortion bans in emergency contexts (not legally binding as statute, but creates record and political pressure)
- Senate hearings on the IG vacancy crisis building toward a bipartisan IG protection bill — one of the few oversight issues with genuine Republican constituency given the $18 return per dollar figure

State-level: the 2026 ballot initiative cycle should be used to enact MHMDA-style legislation in additional states, including California (which has a pending initiative cycle) and Colorado. Initiatives that embed their own implementation protection — specifying contempt sanctions for implementation delay — are more resilient against post-approval legislative nullification.

### 5.3 Long-Term (Constitutional and Structural Reform)

The long-term reform program must address the structural vulnerabilities that made the 2025-2026 accountability crisis possible. Three constitutional provisions deserve particular attention:

**Independent agency protection**: The Supreme Court's current jurisprudence on independent agencies (Seila Law v. CFPB, 2020) has narrowed the constitutional basis for executive branch independence. A constitutional amendment explicitly authorizing Congress to create independent oversight institutions with guaranteed funding and removal-for-cause protections would protect against the IG-defunding trajectory.

**Data sovereignty for public health surveillance**: Congress has broad authority to mandate data collection and reporting that it has not fully used. A National Public Health Data Act establishing minimum federal surveillance infrastructure — funded by direct congressional appropriation, not subject to executive impoundment — would close the surveillance gap the CDC cuts created.

**Ballot initiative integrity amendment**: An Article IV amendment codifying the right of citizens in states with initiative processes to have voter-approved measures implemented within specified timelines, with federal courts empowered to enforce implementation schedules, would address the post-election nullification pattern. The amendment need not create a federal initiative process — it would only protect state processes that already exist.

## Section 6: The Core Question — Can Democratic Institutions Constrain Executive Power?

The evidence in this domain produces a qualified answer to the core question: some mechanisms can constrain executive power, but none of the mechanisms currently functioning are adequate to the scale of the executive overreach documented in 2026. The gap is structural, not merely circumstantial.

The mechanisms that are working share a common feature: they do not depend on executive cooperation. Federal court injunctions protecting sanctuary jurisdictions, state AG enforcement of MHMDA, ballot initiatives bypassing captured legislatures — each operates through a channel that the executive cannot unilaterally close. The mechanisms that are failing also share a common feature: they were designed with the assumption of good-faith executive participation. IG oversight assumes presidents will not simply fire all the IGs. EMTALA enforcement assumes CMS will issue guidance consistent with the statute's purpose. CDC surveillance assumes HHS will prioritize data collection over political convenience. Those assumptions have been falsified.

This is the theory of change for the democratic renewal proposal: rebuild accountability infrastructure specifically around the assumption that future executives will not cooperate with it voluntarily. The technical design requirements follow from that assumption. Independent funding that cannot be impounded. Fixed-term appointments that cannot be politically pressured through reappointment. Data systems that cannot be paused by administrative leave. Enforcement rights that run directly to citizens and state AGs, not through federal executive discretion.

The international models — Germany's constitutionally protected Federal Audit Office, New Zealand's parliamentary commissioner system, Washington State's MHMDA private right of action architecture — all share this design orientation. They are not optimistic about executive goodwill. They are engineered to function when goodwill is absent.

The ballot initiative mechanism matters in this analysis not just as a policy tool but as a democratic legitimacy signal. In 2025-2026, the most reliable expression of public accountability preferences — what voters actually want when directly asked — is the initiative process. The coordinated legislative attack on that process, in 43 states simultaneously, is itself a form of pre-emptive accountability suppression. Protecting and strengthening it is not merely good democratic governance. It is the precondition for all other reform.

## Cross-Domain Connections

**Domain 19f (War Powers Reform)**: The IG firings at the Defense Department and the Pentagon's seventh consecutive audit failure are the same accountability infrastructure collapse in a military context. An executive branch willing to fire oversight officials is equally willing to conduct unauthorized wars without fiscal transparency — both are accountability abdications.

**Domain 2 (Civil Service/Executive Accountability)**: The civil service analysis identifies the Pendleton Act tradition of merit-based, protected employment as the backbone of institutional accountability.
