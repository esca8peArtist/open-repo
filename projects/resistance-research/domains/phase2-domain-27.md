# Domain 27 Phase 2: Higher Education and Academic Freedom as Democratic Infrastructure

## Executive Summary

The assault on American universities has escalated across all tracks documented in the Phase 1 research (Session 505). As of May 2026, five developments have materially worsened the crisis: (1) the First Circuit appeal of the Harvard funding injunction is now fully briefed, with the government arguing agencies can revoke federal support based on "shifting agency priorities" without formal Title VI procedures; (2) a parallel DOJ admissions lawsuit — expanded May 6, 2026 to include Department of Education investigation — has added a second litigation front against Harvard with no factual basis beyond alleged document production delays; (3) the DHS Duration of Status rulemaking proposes replacing the longstanding program-length visa framework with fixed 4-year caps and mandatory federal extension applications, which would structurally convert F-1 status from a program-linked authorization into a federal government-controlled permission renewed at executive discretion; (4) the NIH has spent only 15 percent of its research budget halfway through FY2026, and NSF only 20 percent of its normal pace — not from budget cuts but from an administrative bottleneck that is functionally equivalent to impoundment; and (5) the OBBBA endowment excise tax, effective January 1, 2026, imposed tiered rates up to 8 percent on the net investment income of institutions with the largest endowments — eliminating the financial cushion that enables institutions to resist federal coercion through litigation.

The compound effect has crossed a threshold documented in the academic literature on autocratization: self-censorship now exceeds levels recorded during the McCarthy era, with 35 percent of faculty self-censoring across professional contexts — four times the 1954 rate. The United States' institutional autonomy score on the Academic Freedom Index fell from 2.4 in 2024 to 1.7 in 2025, the steepest single-year decline of any major democracy in the index's history. In a 2025 Nature poll of 1,600 scientists, 75 percent reported considering leaving the United States; Canada's $1.7 billion Canada Global Impact+ Research Talent Initiative and the EU's €500 million "Choose Europe for Science" program have converted that consideration into an accelerating structural departure.

The key finding that advances the Phase 2 analysis beyond the Phase 1 document: the administration is deploying a three-lever fiscal architecture against universities — research grant freezes targeting named institutions, administrative bottlenecks starving all institutions simultaneously, and endowment excise tax erosion targeting the financial reserves that enable litigation resistance. These three levers operate in combination and cannot be addressed through institutional resistance alone. The structural reform agenda must include congressional restoration of research funding flows, statutory protection for accreditation independence, legislative revision of immigration rules for international students, and endowment tax reform that removes the punitive tiers targeting compliance resistors.

## Part 1: The Escalating Litigation Front — May 2026

### 1.1 Harvard Funding Appeal — What the Government's 160-Page Brief Actually Argues

The Trump administration filed its appeal brief with the First Circuit in April 2026, seeking reinstatement of the $2.2 billion funding freeze that U.S. District Judge Allison Burroughs ruled unconstitutional in September 2025. The brief is notable for the breadth of the government's claimed authority, not just for the specific arguments.

**The three core claims:**

First, the government argues that Judge Burroughs lacked subject matter jurisdiction to hear the case — that because federal funding decisions are committed to agency discretion, federal courts cannot review them except in the most extreme circumstances. This is the administrative law analog of the political question doctrine: an argument that would, if accepted, largely insulate executive funding decisions from judicial review. The Solicitor General's brief characterizes federal grant terminations as "programmatic decisions" rather than enforcement actions under the civil rights statutes, attempting to place them in a category where courts give maximum deference.

Second, the government argues that Title VI of the Civil Rights Act — which prohibits discrimination in federally funded programs — does not provide the exclusive pathway for revoking federal funding based on discrimination concerns. The administration's theory is that agencies retain inherent authority to terminate grants based on "shifting agency priorities" even absent a completed Title VI administrative proceeding. This theory has no clear precedent: the federal courts that have addressed Title VI funding conditions have uniformly treated the procedural requirements of Section 602 (notice, opportunity to comply, hearing, and congressional notification) as mandatory prerequisites for fund termination, not optional ones that agencies can bypass by characterizing their decision as a priority shift.

Third, the government argues the administration did not violate the First Amendment because the April 2025 demand letter — which required Harvard to reorganize admissions, hiring, and curriculum on the government's terms — was a legitimate exercise of the government's authority as a funding condition-setter. This is the most legally ambitious claim: the argument that content-based conditions on federal funding do not implicate the First Amendment because the government is merely declining to fund activity, not prohibiting it. The unconstitutional conditions doctrine — which the Supreme Court has applied in multiple funding contexts — holds that government may not condition federal benefits on the surrender of constitutional rights. Judge Burroughs applied this doctrine in her September ruling; the government's appeal asks the First Circuit to narrow it substantially.

**Why the First Circuit outcome matters beyond Harvard:** If the First Circuit accepts even one of the three government claims, it creates a template for extending funding leverage to any institution receiving federal money. A ruling that agencies can terminate grants based on "shifting priorities" without following Title VI procedures would eliminate the procedural protections that currently constrain the speed and breadth of funding freezes. A ruling that federal funding conditions do not implicate the First Amendment would enable ideological conditions on any federal transfer — not just university grants but Medicare hospital payments, Title I school funding, and state and local government assistance.

### 1.2 The DOJ Admissions Lawsuit — Retaliatory Prosecution Pattern

On February 13, 2026, the DOJ sued Harvard in the District of Massachusetts for allegedly withholding admissions records requested during a Title VI compliance review. On May 6, 2026 — the same day as this document — the DOJ expanded the lawsuit to include a Department of Education investigation, adding a second agency's claimed interest in Harvard's document production. Harvard described the suit as "ill-conceived" and "retaliatory," noting it had produced more than 2,000 pages of records and that the DOJ was mischaracterizing the pace of production.

The timing and targeting pattern of this lawsuit fits the retaliatory prosecution analysis documented in Domain 29 (Prosecutorial Weaponization). Harvard rejected the administration's April 2025 demands and then litigated the funding freeze. The admissions lawsuit was filed approximately 10 months later. The specific allegation — that Harvard slow-walked document production rather than that it is currently discriminating in violation of SFFA — means the suit does not need to prove underlying discrimination; it only needs to make Harvard spend legal resources responding to discovery demands and compliance reviews across multiple pending matters simultaneously.

The multi-front litigation strategy is structural: with the First Circuit funding appeal, the admissions DOJ suit, and the ongoing Department of Education compliance review all running simultaneously, Harvard must maintain multiple litigation teams, multiple compliance responses, and ongoing uncertainty about which front will produce an adverse ruling first. This resource drain on an institution with a $53 billion endowment illustrates the scalability of the approach — smaller institutions with smaller endowments and fewer legal resources would face proportionally greater strain.

### 1.3 The AAUP/University of California Coalition Victory — And What It Shows

In September 2025, the AAUP and a coalition of unions won a preliminary injunction blocking the Trump administration's attempt to coerce the University of California through funding threats — a $584 million freeze tied to demands for ideological compliance. The coalition — assembled by AAUP General Counsel Veena Dubal — included faculty, academic staff, postdoctoral scholars, nurses, graduate student workers, and other campus employees across multiple UC campuses.

This case established two actionable precedents. First, coalition litigation — uniting faculty organizations, labor unions, and affected individual plaintiffs — is more difficult to dismiss on standing grounds than single-institution litigation. The government's threshold defenses (lack of standing, agency discretion, political question) are harder to sustain when multiple organizational plaintiffs with distinct injuries join a single action. Second, the coalition approach distributes litigation costs and legal risk: no single institution bears the full burden of confronting the federal government's litigation resources.

The AAUP's Winter 2026 Academe issue (Legal Watch section) documents that coalition litigation has strengthened the defense of academic freedom in ways that individual institutional lawsuits have not achieved. This is not merely a legal observation — it is a tactical blueprint for the resistance infrastructure that the reform agenda needs to institutionalize.

## Part 2: The Fiscal Architecture — Three Simultaneous Levers

### 2.1 Administrative Bottleneck — The Soft Impoundment of Research Funding

The most significant new development between the Phase 1 document (April 27) and this analysis is the documented collapse of the research funding pipeline. NIH has spent only 15 percent of its research budget halfway through FY2026; NSF is at 20 percent of its normal pace. This is not primarily the result of enacted budget cuts — Congress rejected the most severe proposed reductions. It is the result of an administrative bottleneck that functions as impoundment without the statutory process the Impoundment Control Act requires.

The mechanism: agencies must approve grants through multiple administrative layers, each of which involves political appointees with authority to request additional review, require program-specific compliance certifications, or simply fail to process applications. NIH awarded only about 30 percent as many new research grants during this fiscal year as at the same point in each of the past six years. Through the end of February 2026, NIH had awarded $5.8 billion in external research obligations — compared to nearly $9 billion at the same point in FY2025. The total disruption in 2025 alone: $29.86 billion across NIH, NSF, and EPA combined.

The effects are cascading through universities in ways that the funded-institution-level disruption (Harvard, Columbia) obscures. Graduate students whose multi-year NIH fellowships are delayed cannot plan their academic careers. Postdoctoral researchers whose grants are not renewed in time take positions elsewhere — often abroad. Early-stage investigators who would have launched independent research programs this year instead remain dependent on senior colleagues' grants, delaying the development of new research leadership. Institutions facing funding uncertainty pull back internal bridge funding, slowing faculty hiring and research program launches. These are structural losses that do not reverse when the bottleneck eventually clears.

### 2.2 OBBBA Endowment Excise Tax — Targeting the Reserves That Enable Resistance

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act's modification of the university endowment excise tax (effective January 1, 2026) restructured the Section 4968 excise on net investment income from a flat 1.4 percent to a tiered structure targeting institutions with the largest endowments per student:

- **$500,000 to $750,000 per student**: 1.4 percent (unchanged)
- **$750,000 to $2 million per student**: 4 percent (nearly 3x increase)
- **Above $2 million per student**: 8 percent (nearly 6x increase)

The tiered structure is targeted with precision at the institutions most capable of resisting federal coercion through litigation: Harvard ($53 billion endowment, approximately 22,000 students), Princeton, Yale, MIT, and a small number of other wealthy private universities. These are precisely the institutions that have the financial reserves to fund multi-year litigation against federal funding freezes. The excise tax reduces those reserves directly — Harvard estimates the new rate will cost it hundreds of millions annually in additional tax liability — and it expands the definition of taxable income to include royalties from intellectual property, compounding the financial impact.

The democratic accountability dimension: the OBBBA endowment tax was enacted through the same legislative vehicle as the Medicaid work requirements (Domain 31) and the congressional power-of-the-purse circumvention (Domain 34). The political economy is consistent — financial pressure on the institutions capable of mounting effective resistance is deployed simultaneously with ideological demands on those same institutions.

### 2.3 Duration of Status Rule — Converting F-1 Visas into Federal Permission Slips

The DHS proposed rule to replace the Duration of Status framework for F-1 and J-1 visa holders represents the third fiscal-control lever — operational rather than financial, but structurally significant.

**Current system**: F-1 international students are admitted for "Duration of Status" (D/S) — they may remain in the United States as long as they are enrolled in their authorized academic program and maintain status. Program extensions are managed by Designated School Officials at the host university, not by federal agencies. The student's I-94 reads "D/S" rather than a specific date.

**Proposed system**: Replace D/S with fixed admission periods tied to program end dates, capped at four years maximum. Students must file formal Extension of Stay (EOS) applications with USCIS — requiring fees and federal processing — each time they extend their program. Transfer restrictions would prevent undergraduates from changing schools or programs within the first year, and graduate students from doing so at any point. The grace period after program completion is reduced from 60 to 30 days.

**Why this is an academic freedom issue, not merely an immigration matter:** Under the current system, a university DSO is the gatekeeper for student status maintenance. Under the proposed system, the federal executive branch becomes the gatekeeper. A student whose politics are disfavored cannot be de-enrolled by the university alone; under the proposed system, USCIS has the authority to deny an extension application, effectively terminating their academic career in the United States without any action by the university. Combined with the visa revocation authority already being used against protest organizers, the Duration of Status rule change would give the executive branch dual leverage over international students: revocation of the existing visa through State Department authority, and denial of extension through DHS authority.

The scale of the population affected: approximately 1.1 million F-1 students enrolled in U.S. universities, contributing $44 billion annually to the U.S. economy (NAFSA 2025 estimate). The proposed rule is in the post-comment phase as of late April 2026; no final rule has been issued.

## Part 3: Accreditation Rulemaking — The Institutional Chokepoint

### 3.1 The Negotiated Rulemaking Process — What the Administration Is Actually Building

The Department of Education held its first in-person rulemaking session April 13-17, 2026, with a second session scheduled for May 18-22, 2026. The substance of the sessions reveals what the administration intends to install in permanent regulatory form.

The proposed revisions would require recognized accreditors to: enforce DEI prohibition standards as conditions of institutional accreditation; require institutions to demonstrate "viewpoint diversity" in faculty and curriculum; apply accreditation standards consistently regardless of "race or other immutable characteristics, viewpoint, or ideology" — language that committee members argued threatens institutional autonomy; and submit to more aggressive federal oversight of their recognition processes.

Inside Higher Ed reporting from April 20, 2026, documented that ED negotiators dismissed concerns from committee members during the sessions — the standard negotiated rulemaking consensus requirement was not met, meaning the Department retained authority to issue final rules unilaterally. This is the critical procedural fact: the negotiated rulemaking process is designed to produce consensus; when consensus fails, the agency proceeds with its own rule. ED can now issue final accreditation rules without the higher education community's agreement.

The Boston Globe's April 17, 2026 analysis of New England universities characterized the accreditation rulemaking as "Trump's strongest tool yet" in remaking higher education — stronger than specific grant freezes because it is systemic and permanent, affecting all accredited institutions simultaneously rather than targeting named resistors.

**Why this is qualitatively different from targeted grant freezes**: A grant freeze against Harvard affects one institution's research operation. An accreditation rule requiring all accreditors to enforce DEI prohibitions as conditions of recognition affects every accredited institution in the United States simultaneously — all 6,000+ accredited colleges and universities whose students receive federal aid. The mechanism of control is not financial pressure on specific institutions but institutional infrastructure capture: whoever defines what accreditation requires defines what universities must do to remain in operation.

## Part 4: The Brain Drain — From Indicator to Structural Fact

### 4.1 The Quantitative Picture

The brain drain from U.S. universities has moved from leading indicator to documented structural trend. The numbers:

- **75 percent** of 1,600 scientists surveyed by Nature in 2025 reported considering leaving the United States, with Europe and Canada as top destinations
- **European Research Council early-career grants**: US applications tripled in two years (60 for 2024 call → 116 for 2025 → 169 for 2026); senior researcher applications rose from 23 in 2024 to 114 in 2026
- **EU "Choose Europe for Science"**: €500 million for 2025-27 specifically targeting American scientific talent
- **Canada Global Impact+ Research Talent Initiative**: $1.7 billion Canadian dollars announced December 2025, linking programs for early-stage and world-leading researchers
- **Germany's GAIN network, Australia's Global Talent Visa, UK's High Potential Individual visa, South Korea's "Brain Return 500"**: All actively recruiting American scientists with funded labs, relocation packages, and streamlined immigration

The Wiley-published Cancer Cytopathology 2026 analysis of the medical research brain drain found that junior faculty and early-career investigators are the most mobile — the cohort whose departure produces the longest-term research capacity loss. A senior scientist who departs takes funded projects with them; a junior scientist who does not accept a U.S. position never arrives. The latter category is harder to measure but may represent the larger long-run capacity loss.

### 4.2 The State-Level Acceleration of Departure

Faculty movement data at the state level quantifies the geographic distribution of the departure signal. One in 10 faculty members in states with the most restrictive academic speech environments are actively seeking jobs out of state. In Florida — which has enacted the most aggressive state-level academic freedom restrictions — 84.9 percent of faculty said they would not recommend the state as a place to work in academia. Approximately one-third of Georgia and Texas respondents said they do not plan to remain in higher education long-term.

The Ithaka S+R analysis of state and federal policy impact identifies the compounding effect: when federal funding cuts coincide with state-level academic freedom restrictions, institutions in those states become less competitive for the remaining federal funding because their best faculty leave, weakening the research enterprise that federal funding rewards. This is a documented downward spiral: restriction drives departure, departure weakens institutional quality, weakened quality reduces federal funding, reduced funding accelerates departure.

The H-1B hiring freeze enacted by Florida's Board of Governors (14-2 vote, January 2026, effective through January 2027) and the Texas governor's parallel freeze (through May 2027) demonstrate how state-level action compounds the federal visa revocation pattern. Florida and Texas public universities — which together enroll millions of students and conduct tens of billions in research — cannot hire international researchers on H-1B visas for 1-2 years. For research programs dependent on specialized international expertise, this is a functional hiring prohibition.

## Part 5: International Comparative Analysis — How Peer Democracies Protect Academic Institutions

### 5.1 Germany — Constitutional Autonomy and Federal Research Investment

Germany's Basic Law (Grundgesetz) Article 5(3) protects "art and scholarship, research and teaching" as explicitly free. The Federal Constitutional Court has interpreted this protection as covering both individual scholarly freedom and institutional autonomy from state direction. Universities are state institutions (Länder-governed under German federalism) but constitutionally protected from ideological direction by either state or federal governments.

The German Research Foundation (Deutsche Forschungsgemeinschaft, DFG) distributes federal research funding through an arm's-length peer review system institutionally independent from the federal ministry. The DFG's governing board includes university rectors, senior researchers, and external stakeholders; its grant decisions are not subject to ministerial reversal. This institutional design means no individual minister or political executive can freeze a specific university's research funding as a political tool — the decision would require overriding the DFG's statutory independence, which would require constitutional amendment given Article 5(3)'s protection.

Germany's response to the American brain drain has been active: the Alexander von Humboldt Foundation launched an emergency acceleration of its existing fellowship programs; the DFG created fast-track funding pathways for American researchers transferring ongoing projects to German institutions; and multiple Länder governments offered targeted recruitment packages. The institutional architecture that makes Germany capable of absorbing American talent at scale is the same architecture that protects German universities from the federal funding leverage used against American universities.

### 5.2 Canada — Provincial Jurisdiction and Arm's-Length Research Councils

Canadian universities are provincially chartered and funded through a combination of provincial transfers and federal research council grants. The three federal research councils — the Natural Sciences and Engineering Research Council (NSERC), the Social Sciences and Humanities Research Council (SSHRC), and the Canadian Institutes of Health Research (CIHR) — distribute federal research funding through arm's-length processes governed by independent councils that include university representatives, business sector members, and external stakeholders.

The federal government cannot freeze a specific university's research funding through the research councils without restructuring the councils' governing legislation — which would require Act of Parliament. The political constraints on doing so are substantial: universities have strong cross-partisan constituencies (provincial governments of all parties depend on university research for economic development), and the research councils are regarded as nonpartisan science-delivery infrastructure rather than political tools.

Canada's $1.7 billion Canada Global Impact+ Research Talent Initiative is funded through the research council structure rather than direct ministerial discretion, meaning its distribution is governed by peer review and arm's-length criteria rather than political priorities. This is both good governance and a competitive advantage in the global competition for scientific talent: American researchers evaluating Canadian offers need to assess not just the current terms but the institutional durability of the funding commitment. A research council grant is more durable than a U.S. discretionary grant that can be administratively frozen.

### 5.3 United Kingdom — REF and Statutory Independence

The UK's Research Excellence Framework (REF) distributes research funding through a competitive assessment of institutional research quality, conducted by Research England (an arm's-length body), the Scottish Funding Council, the Higher Education Funding Council for Wales, and the Department for the Economy Northern Ireland. The UK government cannot direct Research England to freeze funding to specific institutions for political reasons without legislative change to the Higher Education and Research Act 2017, which established Research England's statutory independence.

The UK's Higher Education Freedom of Speech Act 2023 — passed under a Conservative government — establishes statutory duties on universities to protect free speech and academic freedom, creating an Office for Students enforcement mechanism. The law is imperfect and controversial (the Freshfields legal analysis identified potential conflicts between the free speech duty and other legal obligations), but its existence establishes that academic freedom protection can be given statutory form rather than relying on constitutional doctrine alone. This is a model for U.S. legislative reform: a federal Academic Freedom Protection Act modeled on the UK statute would create enforceable duties against government interference with university speech and research decisions.

### 5.4 The Key Structural Difference

The common design feature across Germany, Canada, and the UK is arm's-length research funding distribution: federal research grants flow through institutionally independent bodies that cannot be directed by individual ministers to freeze specific institutions' funding for political reasons. The United States' research funding structure is the outlier: NIH, NSF, and DOE research divisions are executive agencies whose political leadership can, and does, delay, redirect, and freeze research funding at individual institutional levels without going through an independent review body.

This is not a gap that requires constitutional amendment to fix. Congress can establish research councils structured like NSERC, SSHRC, or CIHR with statutory independence and governance requirements that prevent ministerial direction of individual grant decisions. The Federal Reserve model — an independent body with statutory mandate, presidentially appointed leadership subject to Senate confirmation, and cause-only removal — provides the U.S. legal framework for creating a National Research Foundation with genuine independence from executive political direction.

## Part 6: The Resistance Infrastructure — What Is Already Working

### 6.1 Coalition Litigation — The AAUP/AFT Model

The most effective resistance mechanism documented to date is coalition litigation: multiple organizations with distinct legal standing and shared interests joining single actions, distributing both litigation costs and institutional risk. The AAUP/UC coalition injunction is the model. The AAUP's Legal Watch Winter 2026 analysis identifies the specific mechanisms by which coalition litigation outperforms individual institution litigation:

- **Standing diversification**: When a faculty union, a student association, a postdoctoral researcher organization, and an administrative staff union all have independent standing claims, the government's threshold defenses fail more reliably than when a single institution is the sole plaintiff
- **Cost distribution**: Legal fees shared across organizations reduce the per-organization burden; institutions with smaller legal budgets can participate in coalition actions they could not fund alone
- **Precedent scope**: A ruling in a coalition case extends to all plaintiffs; a ruling in an individual institutional case technically binds only that institution

The AAUP/AFT "Blueprint for Strengthening and Transforming Higher Education" — launched in April 2026 ahead of the 2026 midterms — provides the policy-level companion to coalition litigation. The blueprint's four pillars: students' right to learn; affordability and accessibility; community investment; and labor protections for academic workers. The legislative agenda includes making public higher education debt-free, expanding Pell Grants indexed to inflation, ensuring full funding for HBCUs and minority-serving institutions, establishing AI guardrails for academic workers, and creating new statutory academic freedom protections.

Sources: [A Blueprint for Strengthening and Transforming Higher Education | AAUP](https://www.aaup.org/blueprint-strengthening-and-transforming-higher-education); [AAUP and AFT Launch National Higher Education Platform Ahead of 2026 Midterms | AFT](https://www.aft.org/press-release/aaup-and-aft-launch-national-higher-education-platform-ahead-2026-midterms); [Legal Watch: Coalition Litigation Strengthens the Defense of Academic Freedom | AAUP](https://www.aaup.org/issue/winter-2026/legal-watch-coalition-litigation-strengthens-defense-academic-freedom)

### 6.2 The May 1, 2026 Day of Action and Midterm Mobilization

On May 1, 2026, AAUP members, chapters, and allies mobilized with labor unions and student groups in coordinated action in defense of higher education and democracy. The Coalition for Action in Higher Education (CAHE) organized a National Day of Action on April 16-17, 2026. These mobilizations are the political-organizing complement to coalition litigation — building the constituency needed to win legislative battles in the 2026 midterms.

The midterm connection is explicit in the AAUP/AFT blueprint framing. The 2026 elections will determine the composition of the 120th Congress. A Senate majority shift and sufficient House gain would create a legislative window for: restoring NIH and NSF funding to at least FY2025 levels; establishing statutory independence for research funding distribution; modifying the OBBBA endowment excise tax tiers; legislating Duration of Status protections into statute rather than leaving them to DHS regulation; and creating a federal Academic Freedom Protection Act.

The midterm advocacy calendar is tight. The 2026 primary season begins in March; general election campaigns are underway by June. The window for legislative-focused advocacy (as distinct from election-focused organizing) is narrowing. The AAUP blueprint's midterm orientation is strategically correct: research funding levels, accreditation rules, and visa frameworks are all policy questions that elected Congress members control or can constrain. But that legislative leverage requires electing different Congress members.

## Part 7: Structural Reform Pathways — Updated for May 2026

### 7.1 Immediate (0–6 months): Litigation, Resistance Infrastructure, and Rulemaking Comment

**Accreditation rulemaking comment period (May 18-22, second session)**: The second negotiated rulemaking session on accreditation rules provides the last organized opportunity for the higher education community to build a formal administrative record against the proposed rules. Even when consensus is not reached and the Department issues rules unilaterally, a comprehensive adverse administrative record provides the evidentiary foundation for subsequent judicial review under the Administrative Procedure Act's "arbitrary and capricious" standard. Every accreditor, university association, faculty organization, and student group should participate in the May 18-22 session and subsequent comment periods.

**Duration of Status rulemaking opposition**: The DHS proposed rule is in the post-comment phase. Organizations representing international students (NAFSA, AIUSA, ISSS offices at major universities) should coordinate amicus submissions and prepare litigation briefs challenging the rule's consistency with the Immigration and Nationality Act's delegation to DSOs. The statutory question — whether DHS has authority to replace program-linked status with time-limited federal permission — is close enough to warrant pre-rule litigation preparation.

**Coalition litigation expansion**: Replicate the AAUP/UC model in additional geographic circuits and against additional adverse agency actions. The First Circuit Harvard case is the most immediate priority for amicus coalitions; a ruling that expands government authority to condition grants on "shifting priorities" needs to be contested at the circuit level before it reaches the Supreme Court.

**Congressional emergency research funding supplemental**: Pursue emergency appropriations to restore NIH and NSF funding pipeline to FY2025 pacing levels. The bottleneck is not primarily a budget shortfall — it is an administrative processing failure. Congressional directive to process grants within 90 days of award notification, with Inspector General reporting on compliance, would restore the pipeline without requiring additional appropriations.

### 7.2 Medium-Term (6–24 months): Structural Reform

**National Research Independence Act**: Model legislation establishing an independent National Research Foundation with statutory governance requirements preventing ministerial direction of individual grant decisions. Governance: 15-member board appointed by the President with Senate confirmation, staggered terms, removal only for cause, majority of members required to be active research scientists. Distribution: peer review by panels independent of political appointees; no agency political leadership authority to freeze, redirect, or delay grant awards to specific institutions.

**Academic Freedom Protection Act**: Statutory protection for academic freedom modeled on the UK Higher Education Freedom of Speech Act 2023, but adapted to the U.S. constitutional context. Key provisions: prohibit federal agencies from conditioning grants on ideological hiring, admissions, or curriculum requirements; create a private right of action for institutions and individuals whose academic freedom is violated by federal government coercion; establish an independent Academic Freedom Monitor within the Department of Education with authority to investigate and report (but not prosecute) violations.

**OBBBA Endowment Tax Reform**: Repeal the 4 percent and 8 percent tiers enacted in OBBBA and restore the flat 1.4 percent excise rate for all institutions. Alternative: cap the tax rate at 2 percent but broaden application to all institutions with endowments above $100,000 per student, removing the punitive concentration on the small number of institutions most capable of litigating federal overreach.

**Duration of Status Statutory Protection**: Enact the current Duration of Status framework into statute, preventing DHS from replacing it through administrative rulemaking. Modeled on the existing statutory protection for Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals beneficiaries post-Regents of the University of California v. DHS: Congress can lock in the program-linked status framework so that any change requires Act of Congress rather than rulemaking.

### 7.3 Long-Term (24+ months): Institutional Architecture

**Arm's-length research council structure**: Move NIH, NSF, and DOE research program administration into a Research Councils USA structure with statutory independence from the executive agencies in which they are currently housed. This does not require eliminating NIH or NSF as agencies — it requires separating the grant distribution function from the agency's political leadership through governance structures modeled on NSERC, SSHRC, the DFG, or Research England.

**Accreditation independence restoration**: After the current negotiated rulemaking is resolved (by litigation or administrative withdrawal), establish statutory independence for recognized accreditors from Department of Education direction. Accreditors should be required to meet minimum institutional standards (financial stability, student outcome transparency, academic quality) but should be prohibited by statute from applying ideological conditions as requirements for institutional accreditation.

**International student integration**: Long-term structural engagement with the countries and institutions building research ecosystems to absorb American brain drain. The EU "Choose Europe for Science" and Canada Global Impact+ programs are multi-year commitments creating durable competitive alternatives to U.S. research positions. U.S. policy should recognize the competition and respond with stabilization commitments: statutory assurance of Duration of Status, diplomatic commitment to welcoming international students as economic and intellectual contributors, and research funding levels that make U.S. positions genuinely competitive.

## Summary of Phase 2 Findings

1. The Harvard First Circuit appeal brief argues for virtually unlimited agency discretion to terminate grants based on "shifting priorities," with no First Amendment constraint on ideological conditions and minimal judicial reviewability — if accepted, this would extend funding leverage to all federal grant recipients, not just universities.

2. The DOJ admissions lawsuit, expanded May 6, 2026 to include Department of Education investigation, is a documented retaliatory prosecution pattern: multi-front litigation designed to drain institutional resources across simultaneous matters rather than establish a specific legal violation.

3. NIH at 15 percent and NSF at 20 percent of normal research funding pace halfway through FY2026 represents a soft impoundment through administrative bottleneck — $29.86 billion in total 2025 grant disruption — that Congress has not formally authorized and that the Impoundment Control Act's procedures did not govern.

4. The OBBBA endowment excise tax increase to 4-8 percent (effective January 1, 2026) directly targets the financial reserves of the institutions most capable of litigating federal coercion — eliminating the financial cushion that makes resistance sustainable over multi-year litigation timelines.

5. The DHS Duration of Status rule would convert F-1 visa status from a program-linked authorization managed by university DSOs into a federal government permission subject to discretionary executive renewal — adding a second coercive lever over international students in addition to the existing visa revocation authority.

6. The accreditation rulemaking's second session (May 18-22) is the last organized administrative opportunity to build an adverse record before the Department issues final rules unilaterally, potentially imposing DEI prohibition and viewpoint diversity requirements on all 6,000+ accredited institutions simultaneously.

7. Coalition litigation (AAUP/UC model) outperforms individual institution litigation on standing, cost distribution, and precedent scope — the blueprint for the resistance infrastructure.

8. Peer democracies (Germany, Canada, UK) protect academic institutions through arm's-length research funding distribution and statutory academic freedom protections that prevent individual political executives from freezing specific institutions' grants — models directly applicable to U.S. legislative reform.

9. The brain drain has become a structural fact: 75 percent of surveyed U.S. scientists considering departure, European Research Council applications from the U.S. tripled, Canada's $1.7 billion recruitment initiative, EU's €500 million "Choose Europe" program — these are not signals of possible future loss but documented beginning of a talent transfer.

10. The midterm electoral window (2026) is the primary legislative opportunity to reverse the fiscal architecture: research funding restoration, endowment tax reform, Duration of Status protection, and Academic Freedom Protection Act — all require a changed Congress.
