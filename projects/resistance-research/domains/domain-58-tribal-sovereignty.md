## Executive Summary

The 574 federally recognized tribal nations are not a minority population seeking civil rights protections. They are sovereign governments — democracies — whose authority derives from 370 ratified treaties that constitute the supreme law of the land under Article VI of the Constitution. The federal government's 2025–2026 assault on tribal program infrastructure is therefore not a budget adjustment. It is the dismantling of governmental capacity in 574 functioning democracies.

This domain's core argument, and its unique contribution to the democratic renewal framework, is a framing shift that no existing tribal advocacy organization has made: tribal sovereignty must be understood as democratic infrastructure design. The United States is not merely violating the rights of a minority community or breaching the autonomy of distant sovereign governments. It is destroying the physical infrastructure, economic baseline, and governmental institutions through which 574 democracies deliver services, enforce law, hold elections, and translate their members' political preferences into policy — and then labeling that destruction "government efficiency."

The 2026 crisis has three simultaneous tracks. First, administrative defunding: the Bureau of Indian Affairs has lost 13% of its workforce since January 2025, with a formal reorganization plan announced in March 2026 proposing further cuts; 25+ BIA offices have been shuttered; the Indian Health Service has lost 1,000+ employees and 12 facilities; the FY2026 budget enacted IHS at $8.05 billion against a documented need of $63 billion. Second, the SCOTUS citizenship threat: *Trump v. Barbara* (argued April 1, 2026, ruling expected June/July 2026), in which Solicitor General Sauer explicitly argued that children of tribal Indians are "not birthright citizens" — reopening a question Congress settled in 1924. Third, voting rights erosion: the *Callais* ruling (April 29, 2026) eliminated VRA Section 2's discriminatory-effects standard, directly imperiling every pending tribal redistricting case; *Turtle Mountain Band of Chippewa Indians v. Howe* (No. 25-253) received a GVR (Grant, Vacate, Remand) on May 18, 2026 — this is worse than cert denial: the Court remanded to the Eighth Circuit for reconsideration under post-*Callais* standards, a hostile framework, with Justice Jackson dissenting because *Callais* did not address the private right of action question at issue; a companion GVR in *Board of Election Commissioners v. NAACP* (Mississippi, same day) confirms a deliberate multi-circuit erosion cascade is now underway across the Fifth and Eighth Circuits simultaneously. Status: Uncertain — GVR signals hostile court; lower-court reconsideration outcome unpredictable; no relief imminent.

The most urgent immediate finding: Montana's First Judicial District Court enjoined Senate Bill 490 on May 11, 2026 — blocking a law that would have eliminated eight hours of Election Day registration and disproportionately suppressed Native American turnout. This is an active legal victory that offers the post-*Callais* doctrinal model: state constitutional challenges to voting access barriers survive even where federal VRA Section 2 enforcement has been gutted.

The domain's distribution advantage is architectural. By framing tribal sovereignty as democratic infrastructure rather than as rights protection or sovereignty assertion, these materials reach democratic reform organizations, voting rights groups focused on Black and Latino voters, and congressional oversight staff who have not historically engaged with tribal advocacy but who immediately understand the democratic design argument.

## 1. The Legal Foundation: Treaty Rights, Trust Obligations, and the Government-to-Government Relationship

### 1.1 The Treaty Architecture

Between 1778 and 1871, the United States entered into 370 ratified treaties with tribal nations. Congress ended the treaty-making era in 1871 but explicitly preserved existing treaties. Those treaties — under the Supremacy Clause — are the supreme law of the land, binding on every department of the federal government. They are not subject to administrative waiver, executive discretion, or appropriations impoundment without direct congressional action.

The core exchange documented in treaty after treaty was land for governmental services: tribes ceded territories; the United States committed to protect remaining territory, provide educational and health services, support tribal governance, and recognize tribes' right to self-governance. The Supreme Court has consistently interpreted these treaties liberally in favor of tribal nations — the "canons of construction" governing federal Indian law. In *Washington v. Washington State Commercial Passenger Fishing Vessel Ass'n* (1979), the Court construed treaty fishing rights by what parties would have understood at signing. In *McGirt v. Oklahoma* (2020), the Court ruled Congress had never formally disestablished the Muscogee (Creek) Nation reservation, vindicating a sovereignty claim federal officials assumed had been extinguished — a ruling whose aftermath the Trump administration's DOJ has sought to narrow through selective non-enforcement.

The trust relationship is the fiduciary dimension of this sovereignty. The United States holds over 56 million acres in trust for tribal nations. The BIA manages trust land, natural resources, and financial accounts. IHS provides healthcare. The Bureau of Indian Education administers 183 schools. These are not program choices. They are trust obligations whose breach is cognizable in federal court.

### 1.2 The Federal Self-Determination Framework

The Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act of 1975 (ISDEAA, Pub. L. 93-638) restructured federal-tribal relations from direct federal service delivery to tribal self-governance. Under ISDEAA Section 638 contracts and Title V compacts, tribes assume control of federal programs serving their communities, administering federal dollars directly with reduced overhead. Over 400 of the 574 federally recognized tribes now operate IHS programs under self-determination compacts.

The ISDEAA compact model has two democratic design implications. First, it places governmental authority where it belongs — in the sovereign entity accountable to the community it serves. Second, it creates a direct dependency: if compact funding is cut or impounded, tribes' ability to self-govern is directly curtailed, not because they lack capacity but because the federal government has unilaterally reduced their treaty entitlement. ISDEAA Section 110 provides a direct statutory cause of action when compact funding falls below the Secretarial Amount — what it would cost the federal government to administer the program directly.

This is the DOGE trap. When DOGE terminates BIA office leases and eliminates IHS staff, it is not merely reducing federal overhead. It is defunding the governmental infrastructure of sovereign nations that have transitioned to self-determination models under congressional authorization. The federal government cannot simultaneously require tribes to assume governmental functions under ISDEAA and then defund those programs through executive action. As of May 2026, no tribe has yet filed an ISDEAA Section 110 compact impoundment claim for FY2026 reductions — but the statutory cause of action is available and should be considered the primary legal remedy for tribal compact holders whose funding has been reduced.

### 1.3 The Termination Era Precedent and Its Repudiation

The termination era of the 1950s–1960s — in which Congress legislatively abolished federal recognition of 109 tribal nations — provides the direct historical parallel to the current defunding campaign. Termination reduced tribal land holdings by 1.4 million acres, destroyed governmental authority, and forced mass relocation to urban areas. Congress formally repudiated termination in President Nixon's 1970 Special Message to Congress on Indian Affairs, which established the self-determination framework: "The special relationship between Indians and the Federal government ... is the result ... of solemn obligations which have been entered into by the United States Government. ... The removal of this relationship may be desired by some Indians, but it should not be done without their consent."

The current DOGE-directed defunding is not formal termination — it lacks even the procedural form of congressional authorization that the 1950s termination statutes had. This makes the current defunding more clearly unconstitutional, not less: it attempts to accomplish through administrative action what the Constitution requires Congress to authorize.

## 2. The 2025–2026 Assault: DOGE, Budget Cuts, and Structural Defunding

### 2.1 BIA Office Closures and the March 2026 Reorganization Plan

The Department of Government Efficiency directed GSA to terminate leases for 25 or more Bureau of Indian Affairs offices beginning in early 2025. The BIA office in Ashland, Wisconsin — serving all 11 federally recognized Wisconsin tribes — is scheduled for closure in August 2026. BIA regional and agency offices are the physical infrastructure through which the trust relationship operates: land-to-trust applications, real estate management, law enforcement support, and probate determination for intestate Indian land estates. When the BIA's Office of Hearings and Appeals cannot function, trust land title goes unresolved — making development, sale, or lease impossible for years or decades.

As of March 2026, the situation escalated: tribal leaders reported at a congressional hearing that BIA is preparing a formal reorganization plan involving further significant staff cuts to positions critical to administering programs and distributing funding to tribal nations. This plan was drafted without consultation with tribal nations — a violation of the federal government's tribal consultation obligations under Executive Order 13175 (which the Trump administration has not formally revoked). The workforce losses already documented are severe: BIA has lost 13% of its workforce since January 2025; the Office of the Assistant Secretary for Indian Affairs lost 27%; the Pacific Regional Office lost 29%; the Southern Plains office 26%; the Alaska Regional Office 22%. The FY2026 budget proposed a 31% reduction in BIA funding from FY2025 appropriations.

The probate function deserves specific attention. When an Indian land owner dies intestate, the BIA's probate process determines heirship. Without this function, trust land titles become legally frozen — an effect that compounds across generations as parcels become subject to multiple unresolved heirship claims (the phenomenon known as "fractionation"). DOGE-directed probate office closures are creating a property rights crisis that will persist for decades after the offices reopen.

### 2.2 Indian Health Service: Dismantling the Healthcare Trust Obligation

The Indian Health Service provides healthcare to approximately 2.6 million Native Americans through 46 hospitals, 347 health centers, and 6 school health centers — either directly or through ISDEAA compact arrangements. The legal basis is not programmatic: IHS exists to fulfill the federal government's treaty and trust obligation in exchange for the land cessions that made the United States possible.

The FY2026 enacted IHS appropriation was $8.05 billion — compared to the $63.04 billion recommended by the National Tribal Budget Formulation Working Group to fully fund the agency. This represents a structural underfunding gap of approximately $55 billion. The initial Trump FY2026 budget proposal would have slashed nearly 30% from the IHS base, ended advance appropriations, halted healthcare and sanitation facility construction, and restricted tribal self-governance opportunities. The enacted level avoided the most severe cuts but remains a $15 billion shortfall against the $23 billion the National Indian Health Board identified as the minimum adequate level.

IHS-served patients already face tuberculosis rates 5.4 times the national average, diabetes rates 3.2 times higher, and maternal mortality rates exceeding comparable demographic groups in every region. In 2025, DOGE-directed early retirement and forced attrition eliminated more than 1,000 IHS employees; at least 12 IHS facilities were shuttered. The constitutional dimension: impounding IHS funds or eliminating IHS staff through administrative action, without congressional authorization, constitutes a breach of fiduciary duty cognizable in the United States Court of Federal Claims, which has concurrent jurisdiction over treaty breach claims.

### 2.3 The FY2026 Budget: Structural Defunding Across All Trust Programs

The Trump administration's FY2026 budget proposed cutting core tribal programs by $911 million — 24% — from an already inadequate funding base. The enacted FY2026 level avoided the worst cuts but delivered no meaningful restoration:

**Bureau of Indian Affairs — proposed 31% cut**: Encompasses self-governance program funding, road maintenance, housing assistance, economic development, land management, and trust administration. Contract Support Costs proposed at $205 million against the $342 million FY2025 notional level — a 40% cut to the reimbursement mechanism that makes ISDEAA self-governance economically viable for tribes.

**Tribal Law Enforcement — proposed $107 million cut (20%)**: BIA Public Safety and Justice programs fund tribal police, detention facilities, and tribal courts in jurisdictions where BIA has law enforcement authority. In communities where there is no municipal or county police department serving tribal lands, BIA is the police.

**Bureau of Indian Education construction — proposed $187 million cut**: The BIE operates 183 schools serving approximately 40,000 K-12 students on 64 reservations across 23 states, including 55 BIE-operated and 128 tribally-controlled facilities. The construction cut targets deferred maintenance that federal underfunding has accumulated for decades. The OMB justification cites "poor management, cost overruns, and delays" — without noting those conditions are a direct result of chronic federal underfunding.

**Indian Guaranteed Loan Program — proposed elimination**: The primary federal tool for tribal business financing in Indian Country, where reservation-located businesses cannot use trust land as conventional collateral. Its elimination removes the economic development infrastructure that tribal governments depend on to operate outside the appropriations stream.

## 3. *Trump v. Barbara*: The Birthright Citizenship Threat to Tribal Status

### 3.1 The Constitutional Question

*Trump v. Barbara* (No. 25-365), argued before the Supreme Court on April 1, 2026 — with President Trump personally attending, the first time a sitting president has attended oral argument in modern memory — presents the question whether Executive Order 14160 violates the Fourteenth Amendment's Citizenship Clause. The case directly implicates tribal sovereign status because Solicitor General D. John Sauer's oral argument response to Justice Gorsuch explicitly stated that children of tribal Indians are "not birthright citizens" under the Fourteenth Amendment.

This position directly contradicts the Indian Citizenship Act of 1924, which Congress enacted in response to *Elk v. Wilkins* (1884) — the Supreme Court's ruling that Native Americans born to tribal member parents did not qualify for Fourteenth Amendment citizenship because they remained "subject to the jurisdiction" of their tribe. Congress's 1924 response was unambiguous: "all non-citizen Indians born within the territorial limits of the United States" are citizens. That settled question has governed for 102 years. Sauer's argument potentially reopens it.

At oral argument, Chief Justice Roberts described the government's argument as "quirky" — signaling skepticism about the administration's reading of the Citizenship Clause. The Court appeared likely to reject the administration's position on the central birthright citizenship question. **As of May 15, 2026, the ruling has not issued.** SCOTUS typically completes its docket by the end of June. The ruling in *Trump v. Barbara* is the most consequential Supreme Court decision for tribal citizenship in a generation.

### 3.2 The Structural Stakes: Sovereignty Without Citizenship Protection

The democratic design concern that *Trump v. Barbara* raises is not primarily about the birthright citizenship of any specific class of children. It is about the structural relationship between tribal sovereignty and federal citizenship protection. If the ruling or future legislation creates ambiguity about whether tribal membership confers citizenship in the same manner as birth to documented residents, tribal members become a population whose political status is more contingent and more vulnerable to future executive action.

The Native News Online editorial board characterized the Solicitor General's position as "a dangerous misreading at the heart of *Trump v. Barbara*" — a framing that inverts the purpose of the Indian Citizenship Act. The Act was passed to include, not to exclude. Any dicta validating Sauer's argument would require tribal members to prove they meet some separate citizenship standard beyond the Indian Citizenship Act — a documentation burden falling disproportionately on tribal members in rural reservation communities where administrative records may be incomplete.

## 4. The Seven Causal Pathways: How Federal Policy Undermines Tribal Democratic Capacity

Democratic participation requires three things that federal policy systematically denies to tribal communities: the physical infrastructure to register and vote, the economic baseline that makes civic engagement possible, and the governmental institutions through which community preferences are translated into political power. The seven pathways below document how current federal policy attacks each of these requirements simultaneously and by design.

### Pathway 1: Voter Registration Suppression via Jurisdictional and Structural Barriers

The most direct suppression of tribal democratic capacity operates through voting access barriers that federal law was structurally designed to miss. Of the approximately 4.7 million American Indians and Alaska Natives eligible to vote, only 66% are registered — leaving approximately 1.5 million eligible Native voters unregistered. Only 36.4% of the eligible Native American population actually votes, compared to turnout rates in the mid-to-high 50s for the general population. In states with significant tribal populations, this gap is determinative in federal elections.

The barriers are documented and structural, not incidental. The National Voter Registration Act mandates voter registration at government agencies serving low-income populations — but tribal enrollment offices, which issue the primary IDs that most tribal members carry, are not designated NVRA voter registration agencies in most states. Tribal IDs are not universally accepted as valid voter identification. Polling places are routinely located dozens of miles from reservations across unpaved or impassable roads. Voter registration deadlines do not align with tribal enrollment ID issuance timelines. The 911 addressing system on reservations is often incomplete and error-prone, making the residential address requirements in many states' voter registration systems impossible to satisfy.

These are not abstract legal gaps. In Montana — where tribes comprise about 5% of the eligible electorate — 35,000 of the 65,000 eligible Native voters were not registered heading into the 2024 Senate race. The 2018 race, in which Jon Tester survived by 18,000 votes with a surge in Native voter turnout attributable to tribal mobilization, demonstrated that Native voter registration is not merely a civil rights issue — it is the difference-making variable in federal elections. Montana's First Judicial District Court's May 11, 2026 injunction blocking Senate Bill 490 — which would have eliminated eight hours of Election Day registration that Native voters disproportionately rely on — is the most recent confirmation that these barriers are deliberate rather than incidental.

The *Callais* ruling made federal Section 2 challenges to these barriers functionally unavailable by eliminating the discriminatory-effects standard. *Northern Cheyenne Tribe v. Montana* — challenging Montana's failure to designate tribal enrollment offices as NVRA registration agencies — now proceeds under a more hostile doctrinal environment. The Native American Voting Rights Act, which would provide universal tribal ID acceptance, on-reservation polling places, same-day registration, and residential address accommodation, has not passed Congress.

### Pathway 2: IHS Defunding and the Health-Participation Nexus

Healthcare access is a documented predictor of voter participation — and its destruction is a documented suppressor of civic capacity. The Oregon Health Insurance Experiment (Finkelstein et al., 2012) provided randomized controlled trial evidence that Medicaid coverage increases political participation — voting, attending meetings, contacting officials — because health security reduces the cognitive bandwidth depletion that illness and financial precarity impose on civic engagement. The mechanism is not ideological preference for beneficent government; it is the documented cognitive science of scarcity: when basic needs are insecure, bandwidth available for civic engagement is reduced. See Domain 39 for the full healthcare-democracy research architecture.

The IHS is the treaty-obligated healthcare system serving 2.6 million Native Americans. When IHS facilities close, the health-participation nexus is severed by treaty breach. IHS-served patients already face tuberculosis incidence rates 5.4 times the national average, diabetes rates 3.2 times higher, and maternal mortality rates above comparable demographic groups in every region. American Indian and Alaska Native communities also face the highest drug overdose death rates of any racial or ethnic group in the United States — 65.2 deaths per 100,000 persons in 2022, a rate that increased 15% from 2021 to 2022 — driven by inadequate IHS mental health and substance use treatment infrastructure. These health disparities are not background conditions. They are direct consequences of the chronic IHS underfunding gap between the $8.05 billion enacted level and the $63 billion documented need.

DOGE's elimination of 1,000+ IHS employees and 12 facilities in 2025, combined with the proposed FY2026 cuts (largely sustained in the enacted level), compounds health crises that already suppress the cognitive bandwidth available for civic participation. This pathway connects to Domain 42 (drug policy and criminal justice) and Domain 39 (healthcare as democratic infrastructure) — applying the Oregon RCT framework specifically to IHS as a treaty-obligated system, making the constitutional argument rather than merely the policy argument.

### Pathway 3: BIA Infrastructure Closures and Economic Sovereignty

The BIA manages 56 million acres of trust land — the economic base of tribal sovereignty. BIA regional and agency offices process land-to-trust applications (restoring land to tribal ownership), manage trust land leases (the primary revenue source for many tribal governments), and administer probate for intestate Indian land estates. When DOGE closes BIA offices, these functions do not transfer online or to state governments. They stop.

The economic consequences compound. Tribal businesses cannot access trust-land financing without land-to-trust clearance. Tribal governments lose revenue from disrupted trust-land leases. Intestate tribal land parcels become legally frozen. The BIA's March 2026 reorganization plan — announced without tribal consultation — proposes further cuts to staff who administer programs and distribute funding to tribal nations, on top of the 13% workforce loss already sustained. The Pacific Regional Office (serving tribes in California, Oregon, Washington, Idaho, and Nevada) has lost 29% of its staff; Alaska 22%; Southern Plains 26%.

The democratic connection is direct. Tribal governments' ability to fund their own operations — courts, police, schools, economic development — depends on the revenue streams that trust-land management generates. A tribal government that cannot maintain its courts cannot administer justice. A tribal government that cannot maintain its police cannot protect its citizens. The DOGE-directed BIA closure program is an attack on the fiscal foundation of 574 democratic governments.

### Pathway 4: Criminal Justice Jurisdictional Complexity Suppresses Civic Trust

Tribal criminal jurisdiction operates across three overlapping legal regimes — tribal, federal, and state — depending on the offense, the parties' status, and whether the land is trust land, fee land, or reservation land. This is among the most complicated jurisdictional landscapes in American law. The complexity produces documented enforcement gaps: crimes on tribal lands go unprosecuted because no single jurisdiction has both authority and capacity. The Violence Against Women Act's tribal jurisdiction provision (2013 amendment) is inadequately enforced. DOJ's post-*McGirt* resistance to tribal criminal jurisdiction expansion creates enforcement vacuums in Oklahoma where tribal governments theoretically have authority but lack resources to exercise it.

Civic participation requires institutional trust. Communities that experience inadequate criminal justice — where violence goes unprosecuted and perpetrators face no accountability — disengage from civic institutions that have demonstrably failed them. American Indian and Alaska Native communities report the highest rates of violent crime victimization of any demographic group in the United States. The criminal justice jurisdictional gap is not a collateral legal technicality. It is a direct suppressor of civic trust and political engagement in tribal communities.

The Trump administration's DOJ has accelerated the post-*McGirt* resistance pattern, declining to support tribal criminal jurisdiction expansion even in cases where the legal authority is clear. DOJ's Civil Rights Division has been among the agencies most severely affected by DOGE attrition — the same division that previously enforced tribal VAWA jurisdiction provisions. This fits the broader weaponized non-enforcement pattern documented in Domain 29.

### Pathway 6: Federal Trust Responsibility Failures as Structural Economic Disenfranchisement

Tribal governments cannot levy property taxes — the primary revenue source for local governments — because the trust-land status that protects tribal land from state taxation also prevents tribal government taxation. This is not a tribal governance failure. It is a structural consequence of the federal trust relationship: the United States holds tribal land in trust, meaning neither the tribe nor the state can tax it as fee property. The result is that tribal governments are uniquely dependent on federal appropriations, gaming revenue (where applicable), and natural-resource royalties — three funding streams the current administration is simultaneously cutting (appropriations), threatening (gaming regulation), and redirecting (resource extraction royalties that historically underpay tribal nations).

The economic consequences are severe. Wage and salary income per Crow Reservation resident is just over $10,000 — compared to the national average of over $20,000 per person. The Minneapolis Federal Reserve's Center for Indian Country Development has documented a White-to-Native net worth ratio of 32-to-1 among reservation communities. Median Native incomes have hovered at approximately 87% of median non-Native incomes since 2001 — a persistent gap reflecting structural factors, not cultural ones: the property tax prohibition, the Indian Guaranteed Loan Program elimination, the trust-land financing gap, and the natural resource extraction regime that historically enriched federal and state governments rather than tribal communities.

The proposed elimination of the Indian Guaranteed Loan Program removes the primary federal tool for bridging the credit gap in Indian Country. Reservation-located businesses cannot use trust land as conventional collateral — the same trust protection that shields tribal land from state taxation also prevents its use as loan security. The IGLP was the designed workaround. Its elimination is not a cost-savings measure. It is the removal of the economic development infrastructure that tribal governments depend on to fund operations outside the appropriations stream.

Economic precarity is the most powerful predictor of civic disengagement (see Domain 59's income-participation gap documentation: 48% turnout in the lowest quintile vs. 86% in the highest). The federal trust responsibility failure is not incidental to the democratic participation gap — it is among its primary structural causes.

## 5. Democratic Design Analysis: Tribal Sovereignty as Pre-Constitutional Democracy

### 5.1 The Oldest Democratic Governments in North America

Tribal nations are not democratic institutions created by the United States. They are sovereign governments whose democratic traditions predate the republic. The Haudenosaunee Confederacy — the Iroquois Confederation of six nations — operated a democratic governance structure that historians and political theorists have documented as a direct influence on the framers of the US Constitution, particularly through Benjamin Franklin's Albany Plan of Union (1754). The confederacy's Great Law of Peace established representative governance, consensus-based decision-making, and the principle of considering the impact of decisions on seven generations — a governance philosophy with no equivalent in contemporary US federal policy design.

The 574 federally recognized tribal nations today operate under diverse governance structures — from traditional clan-based councils to constitutions modeled on US frameworks (often because the Indian Reorganization Act of 1934 required constitutional adoption as a condition of federal recognition). That diversity reflects genuine democratic self-determination: each tribal nation governs itself according to its own structure, so long as that structure reflects the sovereign authority the tribe exercises.

The democratic resilience argument: if the United States is experiencing a crisis of democratic institutions, the most durable models of democratic resilience are the tribal nations that have maintained sovereign governance through 500 years of colonial, federal, and administrative pressure. The Cherokee Nation rebuilt its governmental capacity after forced removal. The Haudenosaunee Confederacy has maintained its Great Law of Peace governance structure through two centuries of federal pressure. The Muscogee (Creek) Nation's legal vindication in *McGirt* was the product of 20 years of sustained legal advocacy asserting a sovereignty claim that federal officials assumed had long since been extinguished.

### 5.2 The Unique Contribution: Democratic Infrastructure Framing

The existing body of tribal advocacy work operates within one of two frames: (1) rights protection — tribal members as a minority community entitled to equal treatment; or (2) sovereignty assertion — tribal nations as governments entitled to self-determination. Both frames are correct but neither captures the democratic infrastructure argument.

Domain 58's unique contribution is a third frame: tribal sovereignty as democratic infrastructure design. This frame has specific distribution advantages. It reaches democratic reform organizations that do not engage with tribal sovereignty framing. It reaches voting rights organizations focused on Black and Latino voters that have not systematically incorporated tribal voting rights. It reaches congressional oversight staff working on democratic accountability who have no tribal sovereignty background but immediately understand the democratic design argument. And it provides tribal nations with a new rhetorical resource that positions their advocacy within the mainstream democratic renewal debate rather than as a separate specialized constituency.

## 6. The Post-*Callais* Voting Rights Landscape

### 6.1 What *Callais* Did to Tribal Voting Rights — And What the May 18 GVRs Signal

**[EMERGENCY UPDATE — May 19, 2026: This section was fully rewritten following the May 18 SCOTUS GVR in *Turtle Mountain v. Howe*. The prior framing treated the GVR optimistically; that reading was incorrect. Accurate analysis follows.]**

The Supreme Court's ruling in *Louisiana v. Callais* (April 29, 2026) eliminated the discriminatory-effects standard under VRA Section 2 — the legal tool that Native American voter protection litigators have used most effectively. NARF has never lost on the merits of any redistricting lawsuit it has brought on behalf of Native people, winning cases in Montana, South Dakota, Nebraska, and North Dakota in the five years preceding *Callais*. Every one of those victories relied on the discriminatory-effects standard that *Callais* eliminated. Tribal redistricting challenges in North Dakota, South Dakota, Montana, and Oklahoma are now in limbo or must be refiled as intentional discrimination claims — a dramatically harder evidentiary standard.

**The May 18 GVR in *Turtle Mountain v. Howe*: Worse Than Denial**

On May 18, 2026, the Supreme Court issued a GVR — Grant, Vacate, and Remand — in *Turtle Mountain Band of Chippewa Indians v. Howe* (No. 25-253). The Court vacated the Eighth Circuit's ruling and remanded for reconsideration in light of *Louisiana v. Callais*. This outcome must be read carefully: it is not a win and it is not neutral. A GVR is worse than cert denial.

When the Court denies cert, the lower-court ruling stands — with all its flaws, but also with finality. Tribes retain whatever legal posture they held. A GVR is an instruction to the lower court to reconsider under a standard the Supreme Court has flagged — and the standard flagged here is *Callais*, which is hostile to broad Section 2 enforcement. The Eighth Circuit, on remand, must now re-examine whether private plaintiffs can enforce Section 2 through Section 1983, but must do so in light of a ruling (*Callais*) that substantially narrowed Section 2's scope and eliminated its discriminatory-effects mechanism. The question the Eighth Circuit must answer has become harder to answer favorably, not easier.

**The doctrinal mismatch — and what it reveals**

This is where the May 18 GVR becomes diagnostically significant. *Callais* addressed the discriminatory-effects standard — whether Section 2 violations can be established by showing statistical evidence of disparate racial impact without proving intentional discrimination. It did not address whether private parties can sue under Section 2 at all. That question — the private right of action question — is what the Eighth Circuit had ruled on, and what *Turtle Mountain* put before the Supreme Court.

Justice Ketanji Brown Jackson dissented from the GVR order in three sentences: "*Callais* did not address" the issue of "Section 2's private enforceability." Because the Supreme Court's stated reason for the GVR (reconsider in light of *Callais*) does not match the actual question presented (private right of action), the GVR is legally strained on its face. Jackson would have summarily affirmed — the correct disposition when a Supreme Court precedent does not touch the question presented. The majority's willingness to GVR anyway, using a doctrinally inapposite precedent as the vehicle, signals something more concerning than simple error: it signals that the Court's conservative majority may be engineering an environment in which these cases return to lower courts, fail on practical grounds (states redistrict, maps change, cases approach mootness), and never force the Court to rule on the private right of action question directly.

The strategic implication: the Court may not need to formally eliminate the private right of action under Section 2. By repeatedly GVRing Section 2 enforcement cases back to lower courts under an evolving and hostile doctrinal environment, the majority may achieve the functional elimination of private enforcement while maintaining formal deniability. Campaign Legal Center senior director Mark Gaber argued the GVR means "courts cannot slam the courthouse doors on plaintiffs" — but legal scholars including the Volokh Conspiracy's analysis note that the GVR more likely allows the doors to close by attrition, case by case, circuit by circuit, rather than through a single definitive ruling.

**What the Eighth Circuit now faces**

The Eighth Circuit covers the Dakotas, Minnesota, Iowa, Nebraska, Missouri, and Arkansas — states with some of the largest concentrations of tribal voters in the United States. Its panel had already held that private plaintiffs cannot enforce Section 2. That holding was vacated, but the panel members remain and the circuit's general posture remains skeptical. On remand, the Eighth Circuit must grapple with whether *Callais* requires applying an intentional-discrimination (hostile-intent) standard to the private right of action question — or whether *Callais* simply reset the substantive merits standard while leaving private enforcement intact. Neither answer is obvious; both are dangerous for tribal plaintiffs.

If the Eighth Circuit reinstates its holding that private enforcement is unavailable — now dressed in *Callais* reasoning — that ruling will again be cert-eligible, and the Supreme Court will again have the option to GVR it rather than grant cert and rule definitively. The cycle continues. Tribal voting rights advocates in Eighth Circuit states must plan for a litigation environment in which resolution is deliberately deferred.

**Status (as of May 19, 2026)**: Uncertain — GVR on May 18 signals a hostile court environment, but lower-court reconsideration outcome is unpredictable. No relief is imminent; no definitive ruling on private right of action is expected before 2027 at earliest. Do not characterize this as a win for tribal plaintiffs.

### 6.2 Post-*Callais* Doctrinal Alternatives

Three alternative legal frameworks survive *Callais* and are available for tribal voting rights litigation:

**State constitutional equal protection challenges**: Ten states have enacted their own voting rights acts; state constitutions themselves can protect against racial discrimination. The Montana Supreme Court's injunction of SB 490 on May 11, 2026 — blocking a law that would have eliminated Election Day registration hours disproportionately used by Native voters — is the first post-*Callais* proof of concept for state constitutional tribal voting rights protection. This is the model to replicate in South Dakota, North Dakota, Arizona, and Oklahoma.

**APA challenges to state election administration rules**: Administrative Procedure Act challenges to state election administration rules that create tribal voting barriers do not depend on the discriminatory-effects standard. A state election rule that fails rational basis review because its practical effect cannot be justified by any legitimate state interest — as North Dakota's address requirement fails for reservation residents without conventional addresses — is challengeable under APA doctrine.

**VRA Section 11(b) voter intimidation provision**: Section 11(b) prohibits voter intimidation and remains fully enforceable after *Callais*. Documented patterns of voter suppression — polling place location decisions that place voting sites inside county sheriff offices adjacent to reservation boundaries, as occurred in Keshena, Wisconsin — may be challengeable under Section 11(b) as constructive intimidation.

**Nevada SB 421 positive model**: Nevada Governor Lombardo signed SB 421 in May 2025, allowing tribes to run their own polling locations with Secretary of State support, guaranteeing trained staff, and requiring on-reservation training. Before SB 421, members of the Walker River Paiute Tribe had to travel over 60 miles to cast a ballot. This state-level model — enacted through normal legislative process, not federal enforcement — is the post-*Callais* reform template.

## 8. Reform Pathways

### 8.1 Immediate (Legislative, 2026–2027)

**Treaty Obligation Budget Baseline Act**: Establish a statutory appropriations floor for BIA and IHS at the prior year's enacted level, with inflation adjustment, making treaty-obligation programs immune from impoundment or administrative reduction without specific statutory authorization. Modeled on the IHS advance appropriations authority enacted in 2020. This is the legislative response to DOGE-directed compact impoundment.

**ISDEAA Compact Funding Protection**: Amend ISDEAA to explicitly prohibit reducing compact funding below the Secretarial Amount on any basis other than tribal request or demonstrated tribal mismanagement proven through formal hearing. Cross-reference Domain 40's ICA enforcement architecture: tribes whose compact funding is impounded without an ICA-compliant special message have a direct statutory cause of action. ISDEAA Section 110 claims should be filed for any FY2026 compact reduction — the statutory mechanism is ready; tribes need documentation of the reduction and the Secretarial Amount calculation.

**BIA Field Office Preservation Act**: Declare BIA regional and agency offices essential facilities for discharge of federal trust obligations; require congressional notification 180 days before any closure; require tribally-consulted impact assessment showing alternative service delivery before closure can proceed. The August 2026 Ashland, Wisconsin closure creates a specific advocacy hook: notification requirements should be triggered before August.

**Native American Voting Rights Act (NAVRA)**: The Frank Harrison, Elizabeth Peratrovich, and Miguel Trujillo Native American Voting Rights Act — originally co-authored by Sen. Tom Udall (D-N.M.) and Rep. Ben Ray Luján (D-N.M.) — requires states to accept tribal IDs as valid voter registration identification; mandates on-reservation polling places and drop boxes where requested by tribes; recognizes non-traditional addresses for registration and mail voting; restores preclearance for election law changes disproportionately affecting tribes; and strengthens language-access obligations. NAVRA has not passed Congress. The post-*Callais* environment makes NAVRA's tribal preclearance provision more urgent — and its passage the primary federal legislative remedy for the *Callais* gap.

### 8.2 Litigation Remedies (Available Now)

**ISDEAA Section 110 compact impoundment claims**: The statutory cause of action for compact funding reduced below the Secretarial Amount. Tribes should inventory their FY2026 compact funding levels against the Secretarial Amount calculation. If funding has been reduced, the claim is ripe. A coordinated multi-tribe filing in the Court of Federal Claims is the recommended approach for FY2027 cuts.

**State constitutional voting rights challenges (post-*Callais* model)**: Following the Montana SB 490 injunction model, tribal voting rights organizations should identify state constitutional provisions in South Dakota, North Dakota, Arizona, and Oklahoma that can support tribal voting access challenges independent of federal VRA Section 2 enforcement.

**Breach of trust fiduciary duty**: BIA office closures preventing land-to-trust processing, probate determination, or real estate management constitute actionable breach of trust in the Court of Federal Claims. Tribes whose trust services have been interrupted by BIA closures or staffing reductions should document the specific service failure and the specific trust obligation it breaches.

### 8.3 Medium-Term (Constitutional and Structural)

**Indian Citizenship Act Reaffirmation**: Amend 8 U.S.C. § 1401(b) to confirm that US citizenship attaches at birth within US territory regardless of tribal membership, eliminating any ambiguity created by *Trump v. Barbara* dicta. Pre-drafted legislation should be ready for introduction within 72 hours of the ruling's release.

**Federal Tribal Trust Commission**: An independent commission with audit authority over all agencies with trust responsibilities (BIA, IHS, BIE), modeled on the Commission on Civil Rights, with authority to investigate trust violations and report to Congress, tribal nations, and the public. The GAO's January 2026 report documenting BIA workforce losses without analysis of cost savings or service impact illustrates the institutional gap this commission would fill.

- **Breach of trust fiduciary duty**: BIA office closures that prevent land-to-trust processing, probate determinations, or real estate management may constitute actionable breach of trust.
- **Treaty-specific claims**: Tribes with treaty health, education, or law enforcement commitments should inventory those treaty provisions, assess whether specific DOGE cuts reach treaty-obligated services, and file treaty-based claims in the Court of Federal Claims.
- **Winnemucca Indian Colony v. United States (No. 25-1170)**: This breach-of-trust case — petition filed April 8, 2026, distributed for conference May 28, 2026 — is the most recent SCOTUS-level trust obligation litigation. If cert is granted, it will give the Court another term to define the scope of federal trust obligations — a direct hook for IHS/BIA defunding litigation.

### 9.3 Appropriations Advocacy Window

The fall 2026 congressional appropriations cycle is the primary legislative advocacy window. Required actions:

- **Tribe-by-tribe district mapping**: Every congressional district contains tribal communities or tribal treaty territory. Mapping specific BIA and IHS cuts to specific congressional districts — and presenting impacts to the member representing those districts — is the tool that moves appropriations votes.
- **Treaty obligation framing**: The government-to-government frame (tribal programs as treaty obligations, not discretionary spending) changes the political economy of the appropriations argument.
- **BIA closure testimony**: The August 2026 scheduled closure of the Ashland, Wisconsin office serving 11 tribes creates a specific advocacy hook with Wisconsin members before August.

### 9.4 Voter Mobilization in Tribal Communities

In the 10 states with significant tribal populations — Oklahoma, Arizona, New Mexico, Montana, Alaska, South Dakota, North Dakota, Washington, Michigan, Wisconsin — tribal voter turnout is often the difference-making variable in close federal elections. The infrastructure needed: on-reservation polling places (Nevada SB 421 model), same-day registration compatible with tribal ID, and voter registration outreach to tribal citizens not on state voter rolls due to address documentation gaps.

**Tactic**: Partner with the Native American Voting Rights Coalition and tribal election departments in the 10-state priority list to conduct a pre-midterm voter registration audit — identifying tribal citizens of voting age who are not registered and running targeted registration drives before state deadlines.

### What the Oral Argument Established

The April 1 argument produced three findings relevant to rapid-response preparation:

**The Sauer stumble**: When Justice Gorsuch asked whether newly born Native American babies would still be entitled to U.S. citizenship under the government's proposed test, Solicitor General Sauer initially responded "I think so. On our test, yeah, if they're lawfully domiciled here" — then immediately reconsidered: "I'm not sure. I have to think through that." After further pressing from Gorsuch, Sauer settled on the position that "the children of tribal Indians are not birthright citizens" under the Fourteenth Amendment, grounding their citizenship solely in the Indian Citizenship Act of 1924. The fact that Sauer had to talk himself into this position — and initially wavered — is itself significant: it signals that the government's lawyers had not fully thought through the tribal implications before argument, and that the tribal citizenship question was a pressure point the Court identified independently.

**Roberts's "quirky" remark**: Chief Justice Roberts described the government's argument as "quirky" during argument — a characterization that signals not merely skepticism but a degree of doctrinal disdain. Roberts's authorial fingerprints on a future majority opinion are likely to reflect this skepticism; his tendency to write narrow majorities suggests the ruling will reject the EO on *Wong Kim Ark* grounds without extensively engaging the tribal analogy.

**The bench was hostile across ideological lines**: Court observers noted that questioning came from across the bench — not only from the liberal justices but from Gorsuch and, implicitly, Roberts. The government faced skepticism from justices whose votes it needed. The probability assessment for a government loss remains high (assessed at 80%+ based on oral argument read).

### Coalition Positions (Confirmed as of May 20, 2026)

**NCAI General Counsel Lennie Fineday** (the organization's official legal position): "We believe the reliance on Elk to deny birthright citizenship to children of undocumented immigrants is misplaced. It's a misreading and a misunderstanding." The NCAI has specifically characterized the *Elk v. Wilkins* invocation as inappropriate — the case "rests solely on the specific nature of 'quasi-sovereign tribal government'" and cannot be generalized as the government attempts.

**NARF Tribal Supreme Court Project**: Filed analysis characterizing the government's position as an attempt to use the history of Native exclusion as a weapon rather than as a cautionary precedent. NARF monitors the case through its Tribal Supreme Court Project (sct.narf.org), which tracks every pending SCOTUS case affecting tribal interests.

**Berger/Ablavsky amicus brief** (University of Iowa Law / Stanford Law): Bethany Berger (Iowa) and Gregory Ablavsky (Stanford) filed a joint amicus brief arguing that the government misinterprets the historical record — Native Americans' unique legal status as members of sovereign nations is not analogous to the status of undocumented immigrants, and using federal Indian law to justify restricting birthright citizenship inverts the purpose of that body of law. The brief received national media coverage (NBC News, New York Times, Indianz.com). This is the strongest academic rebuttal to Sauer's historical analogy.

**Historians' amicus brief** (Brennan Center): A separate historians' brief, documented by the Brennan Center, addressed the historical framing of the Citizenship Clause debates and the role of Native exclusion in that history — arguing that the framers understood the tribal-Indian exception narrowly and did not intend it to serve as a general template for limiting birthright citizenship.

No independent amicus brief has been confirmed, but the NAVRC's member organizations (NARF, NCAI, ACLU VRP, Lawyers' Committee, Fair Elections Center, Western Native Voice, Four Directions) are the first-call distribution targets upon ruling.
