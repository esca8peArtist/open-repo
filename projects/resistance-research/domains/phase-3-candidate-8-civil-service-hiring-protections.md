## Executive Summary

The United States federal civil service in 2025-2026 is undergoing its most consequential structural transformation since the Progressive Era. Three simultaneous processes — mass workforce reduction, systematic reclassification of career positions to at-will political appointments, and the dismantling of the independent bodies that adjudicate civil service disputes — are converging to produce what a generation of public administration scholars would classify as the capture of the bureaucratic state. The immediate quantitative markers are stark: 386,826 federal employees departed in 2025; career Senior Executive Service (SES) membership fell 28 percent from 8,127 to 5,837 (a 25-year low); non-Senate-confirmed political appointees reached their highest level in four decades; and a new employment classification — Schedule Policy/Career — was finalized in February 2026 to strip civil service protections from approximately 50,000 positions that were previously held under merit-system rules.

**The central finding of this report is that political weaponization of the civil service is not reversed by electing different officials — it is reversed by building structural protections that survive changes in administration.** The international evidence across five democratic systems demonstrates a consistent pattern: where civil service protections are embedded in statute or constitutional provision with automatic enforcement consequences, they survive authoritarian pressure. Where they rest on convention, norm, or executive discretion alone, they do not.

Germany's post-1949 reconstruction embedded civil service principles directly in Article 33(5) of the Basic Law, creating constitutional guarantees that survived five changes of federal government without erosion. The United Kingdom's permanent secretary system creates bureaucratic continuity through Civil Service Commission independence, though a 2015 change giving prime ministers direct appointment power over permanent secretaries introduced structural vulnerability that the Lords Constitution Committee identified in 2023 and partially addressed. Canada's Public Service Commission model, anchored in a 2003 statute with independent complaint-handling and merit-based appointment authority, resisted Harper government pressures in 2006-2015 through procedural and statutory insulation. Japan's 2014 centralization of civil service appointment authority in the Cabinet Bureau represents the cautionary counterexample: a reform that consolidated political control and produced measurable increases in bureaucratic risk-aversion, scandal concealment, and institutional morale collapse.

The US reform pathway requires a layered statutory architecture with four interlocking components: (1) a statutory prohibition on executive order reclassification of merit-system positions, enacted with a supermajority requirement for amendment; (2) independent civil service commission authority with explicit adjudicative independence from DOJ; (3) a reconstructed MSPB with removal protections that survive *Trump v. Slaughter*'s anticipated narrowing of *Humphrey's Executor*; and (4) a Senior Executive Service reformed to prevent the conversion of career-reserved positions to political appointments. None of these requires constitutional amendment in Phase 1 — all are achievable through ordinary legislation. The constitutional track (Phase 4-5) runs through a civil service article modeled on Article 33 of the German Basic Law, providing a durable anchor that election cycles cannot erode.

## 1. The Architecture of Capture: How Political Weaponization of the Civil Service Works

### 1.1 The Spoils System's DNA and the Progressive Reform That Contained It

The United States federal civil service has a structural vulnerability baked into its constitutional origin: the Constitution makes no mention of a professional civil service. Executive branch appointments are vested in the president. The spoils system — the practice of distributing government jobs to political supporters after an election — was the operational norm from the Jackson administration through the 1880s. By 1884, the federal workforce had grown to 130,000 employees, virtually all of whom owed their positions to political patronage.

President Garfield's assassination by Charles Guiteau — a disappointed office seeker who believed Garfield had denied him an appointment for factional reasons — provided the political shock that made reform possible. The Pendleton Civil Service Reform Act of 1883 created the first merit-based federal employment system, establishing competitive examinations as the basis for appointment and prohibiting dismissal for political reasons within covered positions. The Pendleton Act was deliberately incremental: it initially covered only about 10 percent of the federal workforce, with competitive service expansion delegated to future presidential discretion.

The Civil Service Reform Act of 1978 — the Carter administration's most consequential domestic achievement — completed what Pendleton began. The 1978 Act codified nine Merit System Principles (5 U.S.C. § 2301(b)) covering recruitment, selection, advancement, and treatment of employees; established twelve categories of Prohibited Personnel Practices (5 U.S.C. § 2302) including dismissal for political reasons; created the Merit Systems Protection Board (MSPB) as an independent adjudicative body; and established the Senior Executive Service as a cadre of top career managers with a statutory 10 percent cap on non-career (political) appointments. The Act's institutional architecture was explicitly designed to separate the political management of human resources (vested in OPM) from the independent adjudication of civil service violations (vested in MSPB).

**The 1978 architecture's structural flaw was that it remained entirely statutory.** Every protection it created could be revoked, reinterpreted, or circumvented by executive action targeting the same agencies — OPM and MSPB — that were supposed to enforce it. This is the vulnerability the current administration has exploited.

### 1.2 The Three-Layer Capture Architecture (2025-2026)

The current administration has deployed a three-layer approach to civil service capture that is architecturally more sophisticated than the spoils system it partially resembles.

**Layer 1: Workforce reduction as political cleansing.** DOGE-directed reductions resulted in 386,826 departures from the federal workforce in 2025 — the largest peacetime workforce cut in US history. The reductions were not random: they targeted agency functions most associated with regulatory oversight, enforcement, and institutional independence. At the IRS, 3,623 revenue agents (31 percent of the audit corps) departed, with criminal investigation referrals dropping 63 percent. At FDA, technical staff specializing in food safety, nutrition, and chemical safety were eliminated. At NOAA, climate scientists with multi-decade institutional knowledge left. At CDC, epidemiologists managing pandemic surveillance infrastructure departed. The expertise lost was disproportionately concentrated in STEM PhD-holders, who accounted for 14 percent of departing employees despite representing only 3 percent of the workforce. GAO analysis found that layoffs in the Education Department's civil rights division cost $38 million in productivity — employees were paid months after termination because agencies lacked institutional knowledge to process separations correctly.

**Layer 2: Reclassification as structural conversion.** Three sequential executive orders created a new architecture for at-will political employment:

- *Schedule Policy/Career* (EO January 20, 2025; OPM final rule February 5, 2026, effective March 9, 2026): The successor to Schedule F, this designation reclassifies positions of a "confidential, policy-determining, policy-making, or policy-advocating character" from merit-protected to at-will employment. OPM estimates approximately 50,000 positions are eligible. Critics, including the AFGE-led litigation coalition, argue that the EO's definition of "policy-influencing" explicitly conflicts with Congress's usage of these terms in four separate sections of the U.S. Code to refer exclusively to political appointees — meaning the rule is not just bad policy but illegal on its own definitional terms.

- *Schedule G* (EO July 2025): A new category of political appointees focused on policy-making and policy-advocating roles who do not require Senate confirmation. Schedule G employees can be terminated at will and "are expected to leave when the President who appointed them leaves office." The non-Senate-confirmed political appointee workforce reached its highest level in 40 years by the time Schedule G was implemented. The Partnership for Public Service reported that the administration had placed 1,835 Schedule C appointees — a level no prior modern president approached until well into their tenure.

- *Performance management weaponization*: In February 2025, OPM made "adherence to the president's priorities" the "most critical element" of SES performance reviews. In September 2025, OPM capped the number of career SES members who could receive top performance ratings. Career SES fell from 8,127 to 5,837 — a 28 percent reduction — producing the smallest career senior leadership corps since at least 1998.

**Layer 3: Adjudicative body capture.** The MSPB — the independent body that adjudicates civil service violations — has been simultaneously flooded and defanged. MSPB received a 2,100 percent increase in cases from February 2025, with over 5,000 petitions in a single two-week period (the prior baseline was roughly 200 per week). Trump fired MSPB member Cathy Harris in February 2025; a federal judge ruled the action illegal. The MSPB firings are directly connected to *Trump v. Slaughter* (argued December 8, 2025) — the Supreme Court case that will determine whether *Humphrey's Executor v. United States* (1935), the 90-year precedent protecting independent agency members from at-will presidential removal, survives. Post-argument reporting uniformly characterizes the government as likely to prevail, meaning the constitutional protection for MSPB's independence as an adjudicative body may be eliminated before the end of the 2026 SCOTUS term.

The three layers interact in a specific sequence: workforce reduction removes experienced personnel who understand the legal boundaries of administrative action; reclassification converts remaining career positions to at-will employment; adjudicative body capture eliminates the forum in which violations could be challenged. Each layer makes the next more effective.

### 1.3 The Scale of Institutional Damage: Current Metrics

The Partnership for Public Service's April 2026 report on political appointee surges and career SES decline provides the clearest quantitative snapshot of where the civil service stands as of this research date:

- Career SES: 5,837 (lowest since 1998; down 28% from Biden-era 8,127)
- Non-career (political) SES: Exceeds the 10 percent statutory cap at 11.7 percent of filled positions (statutory cap was not self-enforcing)
- Schedule C non-confirmed political appointees: 1,835 (highest in 40 years)
- Total 2025 departures: 386,826 (including ~17,000 from reductions in force; remainder resignations, retirements, buyouts)
- MSPB case backlog: 2,100% above baseline; no near-term resolution mechanism
- Inspector General community: 17 IGs fired January 24, 2025; federal court ruled action unlawful but declined to reinstate; additional IGs fired through October 2025

The OPM's DOGE-linked access to federal employee personnel data — including personally identifiable information for millions of federal employees, without standard security clearances for the DOGE staff accessing it — adds a fourth layer: the capacity to identify and target specific employees for political reasons using data the employees themselves had no reason to believe would be accessible to political operators.

## 2. Comparative Case Studies: What Protects Civil Service Independence

### 2.1 Germany: Constitutional Entrenchment as Structural Firewall

The German post-1949 civil service architecture is the gold standard for meritocratic protection under democratic reconstruction. The architects of the Basic Law faced a specific design problem: the Nazi state had captured the civil service using the Berufsbeamtentum tradition — the concept of the professional civil servant — as an instrument of politicization rather than a check on it. The *Gesetz zur Wiederherstellung des Berufsbeamtentums* (Law for the Restoration of the Professional Civil Service, April 7, 1933) had used the language of professional civil service standards to purge Jews, political opponents, and democratic loyalists from government employment.

The 1949 constitutional response was not to eliminate the Berufsbeamtentum tradition but to elevate it above ordinary legislation. Article 33(5) of the Basic Law states: "Law regarding the public service shall be regulated with due regard to the established principles concerning the legal status of professional officials." The Federal Constitutional Court has interpreted Article 33(5) as guaranteeing a cluster of constitutional civil service rights: career stability (Lebenszeitelement), adequate compensation (Alimentationsprinzip), loyalty (Treuepflicht) running in both directions between civil servant and state, political neutrality, and merit-based appointment. These principles cannot be overridden by ordinary legislation — they require constitutional amendment (two-thirds majority in both Bundestag and Bundesrat) to modify.

The practical consequences are measurable. The German civil service has maintained consistent career stability metrics through five changes of federal government coalition since reunification, including the 2005 transition to a CDU-led grand coalition after 16 years of SPD influence, the 2013 and 2017 transitions that saw new ministers arrive at already-staffed agencies, and the 2021-2025 Scholz coalition that inherited CDU-structured agencies. In none of these transitions did wholesale replacement of career civil servants occur — not because politicians lacked the desire, but because Article 33(5) made it constitutionally untenable.

The failure mode is equally instructive. Law 131 (1951), which permitted former Nazi civil servants categorized as "Lesser Offenders" to return to civil service, passed with near-unanimity and effectively ended denazification for the civil service by 1951. The constitutional protection cuts both ways: the same framework that prevents political purges also limits the speed of legitimate democratic cleansing of politicized appointees. This is the tension that Poland's Tusk government is living through now (Section 2.4 below).

**Transferable design principle**: Constitutional protection for civil service principles is more durable than statutory protection, but only when the protection covers the *framework* of meritocracy rather than the *particular personnel* holding positions. Article 33(5) protects principles, not incumbents. This is the correct design — it prevents purges without preventing accountability for genuine misconduct.

### 2.2 United Kingdom: The Permanent Secretary System — Functional Independence Through Institutional Convention

The UK civil service operates on a fundamentally different model from either Germany or the US: rather than statutory or constitutional protection for individual career employees, it relies on institutional convention, Civil Service Commission independence, and the distinctive role of the Permanent Secretary as simultaneously the minister's principal policy adviser and the career civil service's senior representative within a department.

The Northcote-Trevelyan Report of 1854 established the foundational principle: the UK civil service should be recruited by open competition on the basis of merit, with permanent tenure. The principle of "permanent" civil servants — who survive changes of minister and provide institutional continuity across governments — creates an accountability relationship that differs from the US model. In the UK, the Permanent Secretary reports to the minister but also has direct accountability to Parliament (appearing before select committees, signing departmental accounts, serving as Accounting Officer). This dual accountability creates structural tension with pure political control: a Permanent Secretary who facilitates illegal ministerial action faces personal liability.

The Civil Service Commission, established in 1855 and now a statutory body, maintains independence over senior appointments. Recruitment must be on "merit on the basis of fair and open competition" — the Commission enforces this standard and can refuse to certify appointments that fail the test. For the most senior positions (Permanent Secretaries), the Commission designs and chairs the selection process, with the Prime Minister retaining final decision-making authority from a Commission-certified shortlist.

The 2015 change — shifting the PM from veto power over the Commission's preferred candidate to direct selection from a certified list — introduced a structural vulnerability that the House of Lords Constitution Committee identified in its October 2023 report. The Committee found that the relationship between ministers and senior civil servants "had become more exposed and controversial," with departures of senior officials raising questions about political motivation. The Committee recommended updating the Senior Appointments Protocol, the Civil Service Commission Recruitment Principles, and the Cabinet Manual to reflect current practice and to strengthen transparency over the Senior Leadership Committee's role.

The Lords Constitution Committee's concern is directly relevant to US reform design: **transparency requirements for appointment and removal decisions are an underrated protection mechanism.** When the rationale for a senior appointment or removal must be documented and disclosed, the political cost of purely motivated decisions rises. The UK's "exception" process for appointments outside open competition requires justification and departmental approval — not a foolproof mechanism, but one that creates friction that pure executive discretion does not.

The Reform UK party's proposed "civil service purge" plan — widely reported in 2024 — demonstrates that UK conventions remain vulnerable to sufficiently determined political opposition. The Institute for Government published an analysis in 2024 arguing that such a purge would be "a big mistake" on efficiency grounds, but noting that convention rather than statute prevents its implementation. This is the structural gap that distinguishes the UK model from Germany's constitutional protection.

**Transferable design principle**: Dual accountability (to minister AND to Parliament) creates friction against purely political removal. The Permanent Secretary's direct relationship with parliamentary committees — appearing in person, signing accounts, bearing personal liability — means dismissal carries institutional costs that pure at-will employment does not. The equivalent US mechanism would be requiring career SES members to appear directly before congressional committees rather than channeling all oversight through political appointees.

### 2.3 Canada: The Public Service Commission Model — Independent Merit Authority With Statutory Teeth

Canada's federal civil service reform has a distinct architecture: the Public Service Commission (PSC) of Canada, established in 1908 and substantially reformed by the 2003 Public Service Employment Act (PSEA), holds independent statutory authority over merit-based appointments and operates entirely outside the executive department structure.

The 1908 establishment of the Civil Service Commission was itself a response to patronage abuse in the same political generation that produced the US Pendleton Act. Canada's reforms lagged slightly behind the US on initial meritocratization, but the 2003 PSEA introduced features that outpace the current US framework in several respects:

- **First legislative definition of merit**: The 2003 PSEA created the first statutory definition of merit in Canadian federal employment law, anchoring the standard against which appointment challenges are assessed.
- **Transferred hiring authority with accountability**: The PSEA transferred hiring authority from the Commission to department deputy heads, but paired this with the Commission's independent audit and complaint-investigation authority. The Commission can investigate any appointment, conduct systemic audits, and recommend corrective action — creating a separation between operational hiring and independent oversight.
- **Independent complaint handling**: Under the PSEA, any person appointed or not appointed can file a complaint with the Public Service Staffing Tribunal (PSST). The Tribunal has independent adjudicative authority over merit complaints — an exact functional analog to the MSPB, but with explicit statutory insulation from political control.
- **Non-partisanship as an explicit statutory value**: The PSEA makes political non-partisanship an explicit legislative principle binding on all appointments, not just a merit-system aspiration.

The Harper government (2006-2015) tested the PSC model's resilience in several respects. Harper's cabinet initially harbored expectations of displacing career civil servants with Conservative sympathizers, a phenomenon documented at the time as reflecting the belief that "senior civil servants had been Liberal sympathizers or allies after the expansion of government during the postwar decades." The PSC's independent appointment authority and complaint mechanism created procedural obstacles: any politically motivated appointment could be challenged before the PSST, requiring a public hearing and documented merit justification. The combination of procedural friction and political cost from litigation deterred wholesale patronage, even as the Harper government achieved some reorientation through legitimate appointment channels over its decade in power.

The Canadian model's limitation is that it relies on the PSC's own independence, which is statutory rather than constitutional. A government with sufficient legislative majority could amend the PSEA to reduce PSC authority. The current US analog would be requiring any Schedule Policy/Career reclassification to be reviewed by an independent merit authority with litigation standing — a mechanism that does not currently exist.

**Transferable design principle**: Independent merit authority with explicit complaint-investigation powers and adjudicative standing is more durable than either self-enforcement (OPM investigating its own political decisions) or pure judicial review (too slow and too expensive for individual employees to pursue routinely). The PSST model — a specialized independent tribunal for merit complaints — is the design gap in the current US system.

### 2.4 Poland: The De-PiSification Lesson — Structural Reform Is Harder Than Purge

Poland's experience from January 2024 forward is the most directly applicable case study for post-capture civil service recovery, because it is happening in real time and its difficulties illuminate the design constraints that any US recovery effort will face.

The Law and Justice (PiS) government (2015-2023) politicized the Polish civil service through a mechanism that closely parallels the US Schedule Policy/Career approach: appointing loyalists to nominally "merit-based" senior positions while using informal pressure and contrived performance reviews to remove career officials who did not align with PiS priorities. The result was a civil service that was formally merit-based but functionally loyal to PiS by the time the October 2023 election transferred power to the Tusk coalition.

The Tusk government's response revealed the fundamental tension in post-capture recovery. Each coalition party had strong incentives to remove PiS loyalists from key institutions — but the mechanisms available for rapid removal were the same legally questionable tools PiS had used. Shortly before Christmas 2023, Poland's state media outlets were overhauled: managers, supervisors, and journalists hired during PiS's tenure were sacked. Adam Bodnar, the new justice minister, announced plans to restore prosecutorial independence by removing PiS-appointed prosecutors — but faced the reality that PiS had amended civil service law to make removal difficult.

The Journal of Democracy's 2025 analysis "Democracy After Illiberalism: A Warning from Poland" identified the core structural problem: Tusk's coalition was concentrating on purges rather than legislating structural reforms, with critics noting the approach mirrored PiS's own methods. The German Marshall Fund analysis on Poland's rule-of-law repair described the situation as "institutional paralysis" — the coalition lacked the three-fifths parliamentary majority needed to overcome presidential vetoes on key reform legislation, while simultaneously needing to act quickly against still-functioning PiS institutional networks.

The specific lesson for US design: **post-capture recovery requires distinguishing between legitimate removal of politically appointed loyalists (acceptable) and wholesale career civil service replacement (unacceptable).** The design mechanism that makes this distinction operationally possible is an independent merit authority that can review removal decisions and certify whether they meet merit-system standards. Poland lacked this mechanism. Its absence meant that any removal could be challenged as a politicized purge — which slowed legitimate reform while leaving actual PiS appointees entrenched.

**Transferable design principle**: Post-capture recovery legislation must include a "merit audit" mechanism that can distinguish political loyalty appointments from legitimate career appointments. Without this, the restoration government faces a dilemma: either restore merit broadly (which entrenches some bad actors) or purge broadly (which mirrors the original wrong).

### 2.5 Japan: The 2014 Cautionary Counterexample

Japan's 2014 civil service reform — the establishment of the Cabinet Bureau of Personnel Affairs (Naikakujinjikyoku) — provides the negative case: what happens when political control over senior civil service appointments is consolidated without independent oversight.

The 2014 reform created a centralized personnel management body that placed all senior civil service appointments under Cabinet Office control, with the Prime Minister's lieutenants holding effective appointment authority. The stated justification was responsiveness — making the bureaucracy more reactive to elected political leadership. The actual consequence, documented in academic research through the Abe period (2012-2020), was the emergence of a new class of bureaucrat known as "kantei kanryo" — officials who optimized for pleasing the PM's office rather than providing independent professional advice.

The most documented failure mode was the Moritomo and Kake scandals (2017-2018), in which Finance Ministry officials altered official documents to conceal information unfavorable to the administration, and in which an administrative decision on veterinary school licensing appeared to benefit a personal associate of Abe. Research published in the Tokyo Foundation and Tandfonline's Asian Journal of Political Science documented increased bureaucratic risk-aversion, document alteration, and the "lowering of civil servants' morale" as direct consequences of the 2014 appointment centralization.

Nippon.com's analysis "Japan's Problematic Administrative Reform" put the design flaw directly: the system gives ministers "discretion to select from a list of hundreds of candidates, leaving ample room for decisions that cannot be justified on the basis of merit alone." The opacity of the selection process prevented independent verification of whether appointments were merit-based. This is the same structural problem that the UK Lords Constitution Committee identified with the UK's Senior Leadership Committee — opacity without accountability enables politicization regardless of stated merit principles.

**Transferable design principle**: Transparent scoring requirements for appointment decisions, combined with independent audit authority, are the minimum accountability mechanism required to prevent appointive authority from becoming political patronage. The US has neither for SES appointments.

## 3. The Current US Litigation Landscape and Its Structural Implications

### 3.1 Trump v. Slaughter and the End of Humphrey's Executor

*Trump v. Slaughter* (Supreme Court, argued December 8, 2025) is the constitutional linchpin of the civil service protection crisis. The case involves Trump's firing of Rebecca Kelly Slaughter from the Federal Trade Commission without cause, in direct violation of the statutory requirement that FTC commissioners can only be removed for "inefficiency, neglect of duty, or malfeasance in office." The question presented is whether this "for cause" removal protection violates separation of powers — and whether *Humphrey's Executor v. United States* (1935), which upheld such protections for 90 years, should be overruled.

Post-argument reporting consistently characterized the government as likely to prevail. SCOTUSblog's analysis noted that most justices appeared skeptical of *Humphrey's Executor*'s continued viability given the modern FTC's expanded regulatory authority. Lawfare's Slaughter analysis ("'Slaughter'-ing Humphrey's Executor") described the constitutional theory that would likely prevail: because independent agency heads exercise "considerable executive power" (the standard from *Trump v. Wilcox*, which stayed lower court orders protecting MSPB and NLRB board members), the president must retain removal authority over them.

The MSPB-specific stakes are severe. The MSPB's independence from presidential removal was the structural guarantee that civil service adjudication remained outside political control. If *Trump v. Slaughter* overrules *Humphrey's Executor*, a subsequent administration could fire MSPB members at will — converting the adjudicative body for civil service disputes into an agency under direct presidential control. Federal courts already ruled that Trump violated the Inspector General Act in firing 17 IGs in January 2025, but declined to reinstate them, noting the president could simply re-fire them after providing the statutory 30-day notice.

The combination of *Slaughter* eliminating MSPB removal protections and Schedule Policy/Career stripping adjudicative rights from 50,000 positions would leave the bulk of the career civil service with neither substantive protection (merit-system rules would not apply to Schedule Policy/Career employees) nor procedural protection (the body that enforces merit-system rules would be under direct presidential control for the remainder of the workforce).

### 3.2 Current Litigation: AFGE et al. v. Trump

The AFGE-led litigation coalition filed its original complaint in January 2025 and a Second Amended Complaint on March 4, 2026, in the U.S. District Court for Maryland, through Democracy Forward, the Law Office of Jonathan Weissglass, and CREW. The amended complaint expanded the challenge to target the entire Schedule Policy/Career framework: the underlying executive order, OPM's final rule, and the broader reclassification effort.

The amended complaint's primary legal arguments are:

1. **Statutory definition conflict**: The EO's definition of "confidential, policy-determining, policy-making, or policy-advocating" positions conflicts with Congress's use of these terms in four sections of the U.S. Code, which consistently reserve them to political appointees rather than career employees. Under the administrative law framework post-*Loper Bright*, courts owe no deference to OPM's interpretation — they must independently determine whether the rule is consistent with the statute.

2. **Civil Service Reform Act violation**: The 1978 Act's merit system principles and prohibited personnel practices framework precludes executive reclassification of merit positions to at-will employment without congressional authorization. The EO is not a valid exercise of appointment authority but a circumvention of congressionally mandated merit protections.

3. **Separation of powers**: The President cannot unilaterally strip statutory protections from positions Congress has designated for merit-based competitive service.

The *Loper Bright* argument is double-edged: while it eliminates deference to OPM's interpretation of its own authority, it also means courts must engage substantively with the statutory text — and the statutory text is favorable to the plaintiffs' position. This is one of the clearest cases of *Loper Bright* being deployed as a restraint on executive rather than regulatory overreach.

### 3.3 The Inspector General Ecosystem

The January 24, 2025 firing of 17 inspectors general — without the 30-day notice to Congress and "substantive rationale" required by the Inspector General Reform Act of 2022 — provides the clearest case study of institutional capture through adjudicative body targeting. The IG community was the primary internal oversight mechanism for the civil service: IGs investigated prohibited personnel practices, documented fraud and waste, and reported to both agency heads and Congress independently.

Federal Judge Ana Reyes ruled that Trump violated the 1978 Inspector General Act, but declined to reinstate the fired IGs, noting they could be re-fired after proper notice. Subsequent firings continued through October 2025. The combined consequence: agencies operating without independent internal oversight, in the same period when DOGE-directed workforce reductions were occurring and political appointments were accelerating. The $75.7 billion per year in accountability value the IG community provided — documented in the IG community's own impact reports — is now largely inaccessible.

New whistleblower protection bills (Senator Grassley's Whistleblower Anti-Gag Act of 2026; the bipartisan S. 4100) have been introduced to partially address the coverage gap created by IG elimination, but these are measures at the margins rather than structural restoration.

## 4. Reform Pathways: What Works and What Doesn't

### 4.1 What Doesn't Work: Norm-Based and Convention-Based Protections

The core lesson from both the US experience and international comparisons is that protections that rely on norms, conventions, or executive good faith do not survive determined political opposition. The spoils system survived for 50 years against reform advocacy because there was no structural mechanism that made patronage costly. The current Schedule Policy/Career reclassification is possible because the statutory merit-system framework was built on the assumption that presidents would exercise appointment authority in good faith.

Norm-based protections that have failed in the current context:
- The convention against firing IGs without cause (violated January 2025)
- The convention against using executive orders to reclassify merit positions (violated January 2025)
- The convention limiting political appointments to below 10% of SES (exceeded in 2025-2026)
- The convention against SES performance management being tied to political loyalty (violated February 2025)
- The convention against granting unvetted political staff access to federal employee personnel data (violated with DOGE OPM access, February 2025)

Each of these failures followed a common pattern: the norm existed, was known to all parties, was violated, and the institutional mechanism for enforcing it either lacked authority, was itself captured, or was too slow to prevent immediate harm. This pattern will repeat for any reform that relies on institutional good faith without structural enforcement.

### 4.2 Statutory Reform Package: The Four-Component Architecture

A durable civil service protection framework requires four interlocking statutory components, each addressing a distinct vulnerability identified in the current crisis.

**Component 1: Saving the Civil Service Act (H.R. 492 / S. 134, 119th Congress)**

The Saving the Civil Service Act, introduced by Rep. Connolly and Sen. Kaine, prohibits executive agencies from placing competitive service positions in any excepted service schedule not in effect as of September 30, 2020. The bill has 58 House cosponsors, including bipartisan support from Reps. Fitzpatrick and Bacon. Its core mechanism is a categorical prohibition: not a process requirement, not a procedural hurdle, but a flat statutory ban on the executive action the current administration has taken.

The bill's design should be strengthened in three respects for Phase 3 and beyond:
- Add a supermajority requirement (two-thirds of both chambers) for any future legislative modification, preventing simple majority rollback;
- Include a private right of action for individual employees reclassified in violation of the statute, enabling direct judicial challenge without MSPB mediation;
- Extend the prohibition to cover performance evaluation criteria, preventing the February 2025 "political loyalty as primary performance standard" mechanism even for employees who retain competitive service status.

**Component 2: Independent Civil Service Commission Establishment**

The UK and Canadian models both demonstrate that independent commission authority — with its own adjudicative and investigative powers, separated from both OPM and MSPB — creates durable protection against political capture. The proposed US Civil Service Independence Commission Act (not yet introduced; design element for Phase 2 legislation) would establish:

- An independent commission with five members appointed by the president to staggered six-year terms, confirmed by the Senate, removable only for cause with 30-day congressional notice and substantive rationale (mirroring the IG Reform Act's current standard);
- Exclusive jurisdiction over merit-system complaints from Schedule C, SES, and any newly created excepted service category;
- Mandatory public reporting of all appointment "exceptions" (positions filled outside open competition), including the justification and approving official's name;
- Independent budget line in the Treasury appropriations account, preventing OMB apportionment manipulation;
- Direct accountability to Congress through biannual testimony requirements without executive intermediary.

The commission's independence from DOJ is structural: unlike the current MSPB model where appeals eventually route through DOJ enforcement, the Commission would have independent enforcement authority through referral to federal courts with mandatory standing.

**Component 3: MSPB Reconstruction Post-Slaughter**

The anticipated outcome of *Trump v. Slaughter* will reduce or eliminate the constitutional protection for MSPB board member independence. The legislative response must compensate for this with statutory mechanisms that do not depend on *Humphrey's Executor*:

- Convert MSPB into an Article I court, similar to the Tax Court or Court of Claims. Article I courts' judges retain independence through explicit statutory provision rather than the constitutional removal protection framework. This insulates MSPB from the *Slaughter* ruling's application.
- Establish a fixed eight-year term for MSPB members, with staggered expiration dates, and a supermajority requirement (60 Senate votes) for Senate confirmation. Supermajority confirmation creates bipartisan buy-in that makes pure political removal costlier.
- Grant MSPB independent litigation authority — the power to file suit in its own name to enforce its orders — rather than routing enforcement through DOJ, which reports to the president.

The MSPB's current case backlog (2,100 percent above baseline as of March 2025) requires an emergency funding mechanism: a dedicated appropriation for case resolution with a statutory maximum processing time of 90 days for urgent appeals. Without backlog resolution, MSPB protection is nominal even if structural independence is restored.

**Component 4: Senior Executive Service Structural Reform**

The SES structural vulnerabilities — the 10 percent cap on non-career positions, the conversion of career-reserved positions to political appointments, and performance evaluation manipulation — require targeted fixes:

- Reduce the statutory cap on non-career SES from 10 percent to 7 percent, with automatic enforcement: any appointment that would exceed the cap is void by operation of law, without requiring a separate administrative finding;
- Classify all SES positions currently designated "Career Reserved" as permanently career-reserved, with no executive order authority to convert them to non-career status;
- Establish a statutory prohibition on using "adherence to the president's agenda" or equivalent language as a performance evaluation criterion for career SES — SES performance evaluations must be based on measurable program outcomes, not ideological alignment;
- Require that all career-to-political SES position conversions be reported to Congress within 10 days, with a 30-day congressional review period during which Congress can pass a joint resolution of disapproval.

### 4.3 Post-Capture Recovery: The Merit Audit Mechanism

Poland's experience demonstrates that post-capture recovery requires a mechanism that can distinguish legitimate political appointments from merit-based career appointments — without subjecting every incumbent to a fresh merit competition that would effectively convert career positions to at-will status.

The proposed design is a one-time Merit Restoration Audit, modeled on the Australian Public Service Commission's independent auditing authority:

- Within 90 days of legislation, an independent audit commission (five members appointed by the Senate Majority and Minority leaders jointly, with a tie-breaker selected by the Civil Service Commission) reviews all appointments made to SES positions after January 20, 2025;
- Appointments that can be documented as merit-based through contemporaneous records are confirmed; appointments that lack merit documentation or were made under manifestly political criteria (SES positions converted from career-reserved status, positions reclassified under Schedule G or Policy/Career and then back-filled with political candidates) are subject to merit competition;
- The audit commission's determinations are subject to judicial review but are not self-executing for removal — they provide the factual record that enables Congress and courts to act.

This mechanism allows recovery governments to demonstrate good-faith commitment to meritocracy while providing a procedure-based basis for distinguishing legitimate recovery from counter-purge.

### 4.4 Whistleblower Protection Strengthening

The IG elimination created a structural gap in internal oversight that whistleblower protections cannot fully fill — but strengthening those protections is the minimum required response while IG restoration is pursued:

- Extend Whistleblower Protection Act coverage explicitly to Schedule Policy/Career and Schedule G employees, overriding any exclusion that might otherwise apply to excepted service employees;
- Create a dedicated whistleblower complaint channel outside OSC and MSPB for claims against political appointees — routed directly to the GAO and the relevant congressional oversight committees;
- Restore the IG community through the Inspector General Independence and Restoration Act (not yet introduced), which would: reinstate all 17 IGs fired without substantive rationale; require Senate confirmation for future IG appointments; and establish a two-thirds Senate majority requirement for removal (creating stronger protection than the current 30-day notice requirement, which has proven insufficient).

## 5. International Implementation Timelines: How Long Recovery Actually Takes

### 5.1 Germany's Reconstruction Timeline (1949-1960)

The German civil service reconstruction was not instantaneous. The Basic Law's Article 33(5) protections took effect in 1949, but the actual transformation of the civil service — from a body that had administered the Nazi state to one that could credibly claim democratic legitimacy — required approximately a decade of parallel processes:

- 1949-1951: Allied-supervised denazification (imperfect and inconsistently applied) followed by Law 131's liberal reintegration of "minor" Nazi officials
- 1953-1957: Internal disciplinary proceedings against the most deeply implicated civil servants, conducted within the civil service itself
- 1958-1965: Gradual replacement of Nazi-era senior officials through normal retirement, with merit-based replacements who had been trained in the new democratic civil service culture

The timeline illustrates a key design constraint: **constitutional and statutory architecture sets the framework, but personnel change happens at the speed of normal attrition unless supplemental mechanisms accelerate it**. The German case produced a functioning democratic civil service within a decade but tolerated significant institutional continuity from the Nazi era in the interim.

### 5.2 South Korea's Post-1987 Meritocratization

South Korea's 1987 democratic transition produced measurable civil service meritocratization within approximately five years. The military's special recruitment system for ex-military officers was abolished in 1988, and by 1993 the proportion of civil service exam recruits at Grade III-B level exceeded 70 percent — up from approximately 40 percent under authoritarian rule. The Meritocratic Paradox article in journals.sagepub.com (2025) documents this as a genuine improvement in procedural legitimacy while noting an emerging crisis of substantive legitimacy: the merit system became so exam-focused that it excluded practical expertise and created a civil service that was formally meritocratic but insufficiently responsive to contemporary governance challenges.

The South Korean five-year timeline for basic meritocratic restoration is plausible for a US recovery scenario, with one important caveat: Korea was rebuilding a merit system from near-zero, while the US is recovering a merit system from partial capture. Recovery from partial capture may be faster (more institutional continuity) or slower (more entrenched interests in the hybrid system) depending on which positions were most heavily politicized.

### 5.3 Canada's PSEA Implementation (2003-2008)

Canada's 2003 PSEA required a five-year implementation period for full operation. The Public Service Commission needed to:
- Redesign complaint-handling procedures under the new merit framework;
- Train agency HR offices in the new appointment authority framework;
- Build the PSST's adjudicative capacity from near-zero;
- Conduct the first round of systemic audits under the new authority.

By 2008 — five years after enactment — the Commission assessed the framework as operationally functional. The lesson for US reform: statutory reform is not self-executing. Implementation resources, institutional capacity-building, and transition management require explicit planning and funding from the first year of legislation.

## 6. Legislative Reform Roadmap and Implementation Schedule

### 6.1 Phase 1 (Now — May 2026): Non-Legislative Actions

**Litigation support**: File amicus briefs in *AFGE v. Trump* (Schedule Policy/Career) from law school administrative law clinics, retired OPM career officials, and public administration professional associations. **MSPB capacity**: Advocate for emergency congressional appropriation to reduce MSPB case backlog. MSPB's 2,100 percent backlog creates a de facto enforcement gap regardless of whether the body retains formal independence. A specific appropriation request — $150 million for two-year case backlog resolution — is both affordable and politically defensible.

**Inspector General testimony**: Support the 17 fired IGs' public testimony before congressional committees, creating a congressional record of institutional damage. This serves both accountability and legislative advocacy purposes.

**Documentation of Schedule G and Policy/Career implementation**: Beginning March 9, 2026, agencies began identifying positions for reclassification. A systematic documentation project — tracking which agencies are reclassifying how many positions, on what timeline, using what criteria — provides the factual record for both litigation and future legislative findings.

**Endorsement coordination**: Circulate this document to: American Society for Public Administration (ASPA), National Academy of Public Administration (NAPA), Partnership for Public Service, Government Accountability Project, Project on Government Oversight (POGO), and the academic civil service research community.

### 6.2 Phase 2 (June-September 2026): Legislative Introduction

**Saving the Civil Service Act amendment and reintroduction**: The existing H.R. 492 / S. 134 framework should be strengthened (supermajority requirement, private right of action, performance evaluation prohibition) and reintroduced with the strongest possible bipartisan coalition. The existing 58 House cosponsors provide a base; the goal is 45+ Senate cosponsors.

**Civil Service Independence Commission Act**: New legislation establishing the independent commission described in Section 4.2. This should be introduced by the Senate Homeland Security and Governmental Affairs Committee, with markup in September 2026.

**Inspector General Independence and Restoration Act**: Legislation reinstating the 17 fired IGs, establishing Senate confirmation for future IG appointments, and creating a two-thirds removal requirement. Congressional record support already exists through Judge Reyes's ruling that the firings were unlawful.

**MSPB Reconstruction Act**: Legislation converting MSPB to an Article I court with independent litigation authority and emergency backlog-reduction appropriation.

### 6.3 Phase 3 (2027): Institutional Rebuilding

**Phase 3 success criteria** (following a 2026 midterm shift in at least one chamber):
- Saving the Civil Service Act enacted or in Senate committee with 45+ cosponsors;
- Civil Service Independence Commission members confirmed;
- MSPB case backlog under 200 percent of baseline (emergency appropriation operational);
- Merit Restoration Audit commission convened;
- IG community restored to at least 12 of 17 agencies;
- Schedule G and Policy/Career reclassifications stayed by injunction or reversed by legislation.

**Institutional capacity investment**: The civil service requires a parallel investment in recruitment, training, and retention. The 386,826 departures of 2025 created expertise gaps that take years to fill. A "Federal Service Scholars" program — modeled on the UK's Fast Stream and funded at $500 million per year for five years — would accelerate the rebuilding of institutional expertise in critical agencies (IRS, FDA, NOAA, CDC, EPA enforcement, SSA).

### 6.4 Phase 4-5 (2029-2036): Constitutional Consolidation

**Civil Service Article proposal**: A constitutional amendment to the US Constitution establishing civil service principles analogous to Germany's Article 33(5). The amendment would:
- Guarantee merit-based appointment as a constitutional principle for executive branch positions;
- Prohibit reclassification of competitive service positions to excepted service by executive order;
- Establish independent civil service adjudication as a constitutional structural element;
- Create civil service protection as an individual constitutional right, enforceable by federal courts without legislative intermediary.

**Ten-year success metric**: By 2036, the US civil service should demonstrate institutional performance comparable to Germany, Canada, and the UK on the following indicators: career personnel retention (at least 85% of STEM PhD-holders retained through administration changes); SES political appointment ratio (below 7%, self-enforcing); merit complaint resolution time (below 60 days at the independent commission); and Inspector General coverage (all 73 agencies with confirmed IGs operating independently).
