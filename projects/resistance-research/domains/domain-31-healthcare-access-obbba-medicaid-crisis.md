## Executive Summary

The One Big Beautiful Budget Act (OBBBA, P.L. 119-21, signed July 4, 2025) authorizes cuts to federal Medicaid funding exceeding $900 billion over 10 years, with immediate impacts beginning January 2026 and escalating through January 2027. The Congressional Budget Office projects **11.8 million individuals will lose health insurance coverage**, driven by three policy mechanisms: (1) elimination of enhanced federal matching for Medicaid expansion in new states starting January 2026, (2) mandatory work requirements (80 hours/month) effective January 1, 2027 across all states, and (3) increased eligibility verification frequency (6-month recertification vs. 12-month baseline), creating administrative barriers that disproportionately affect low-income individuals, people of color, and rural populations.

**Why this is a democracy crisis**: Medicaid enrollment offices serve as voter registration sites under the National Voter Registration Act (NVRA, 42 U.S.C. § 1973gg-5). Cuts to enrollment capacity and processing reduce voter registration opportunities in specific communities (typically low-income, majority-minority, and rural areas). The work requirement is designed to function as an enrollment barrier: the Arkansas 2018 pilot disenrolled 18,000 beneficiaries in 6 months with zero measurable employment effect, demonstrating that the mechanism operates as eligibility contraction, not behavior change. Combined with Medicaid expansion disenrollment (11.8 million projected), this constitutes a coordinated removal of healthcare coverage AND simultaneous contraction of voter registration infrastructure in the same population strata. The democracy-health nexus is the argument: health disparities are not incidental to political exclusion but structural mechanisms of it.

**June 2026 deadline**: HHS must issue implementation guidance for work requirements by June 1, 2026 (currently slated for June per KFF tracking). The comment period will likely open April-May 2026, creating a discrete advocacy window before implementation locks in for January 2027.

## Current Crisis: OBBBA Medicaid Architecture

### Immediate Impacts (Effective January 2026)

**Elimination of Medicaid Expansion Enhanced Federal Matching**

Medicaid expansion (under ACA Section 1115) received a 90% federal match rate for newly eligible adults under the OBBBA modifications. OBBBA eliminates this enhanced match for any state that did not already have expansion in place by January 1, 2026. This effectively blocks further Medicaid expansion — states cannot afford to absorb 10% matching costs for entirely new populations. 

**Impact**: 12 non-expansion states (Alabama, Florida, Georgia, Idaho, Kansas, Louisiana, Mississippi, Missouri, North Carolina, South Carolina, Tennessee, Texas) will not expand, leaving 3.6 million low-income adults (mostly in the South) without a pathway to coverage even if federal matching improved. These 12 states are disproportionately majority-Black in their low-income populations (Mississippi 37%, Louisiana 32%, Alabama 27% Black Medicaid-eligible), creating a racial health equity gap that locks in over the decade.

**Voter registration impact**: 37 states with active Medicaid expansion retain enrollment offices with NVRA-mandated voter registration. The 12 non-expansion states now have reduced incentive to expand enrollment infrastructure, and existing enrollment office staffing reductions are underway in multiple states (documented in State Health and Value Strategies tracking).

### Implementation Phase (January–December 2026)

**More Frequent Eligibility Recertification**

Beginning January 2027 (but states can choose to accelerate), Medicaid expansion enrollees will be required to recertify eligibility every 6 months instead of 12 months. This doubles the frequency of administrative contact, documentation submission, and enrollment capacity requirements.

**Impact**: KFF Medicaid Enrollment Tracker shows that during the prior continuous enrollment unwinding (April 2023–June 2024), 69% of disenrollments were procedural (paperwork, address change, failed to respond to verification notice) rather than based on actual ineligibility. Procedural disenrollment rates differ by race: Black individuals accounted for 22% of procedural disenrollments despite being 16% of the Medicaid population; Hispanic individuals accounted for 34% despite being 23% of enrollment. Doubling verification frequency will mechanically increase procedural disenrollment, with documented racial disparities in completion rates.

**Voter registration impact**: Each 6-month recertification is a missed NVRA voter registration opportunity if states do not proactively implement automatic voter registration (AVR). Only 5 states have AVR in Medicaid currently (Colorado, Oregon, California, Rhode Island); the others rely on paper forms with <2.5% uptake rates. Cuts to enrollment office staffing (documented in rural hospital finance models showing state Medicaid agency budget compression) reduce capacity to offer forms.

### Full Implementation (January 2027+)

**Mandatory Work Requirements**

All states must implement 80-hour/month work requirements or equivalent community engagement by January 1, 2027, unless HHS grants an extension to December 31, 2028 for "good faith effort" demonstration. Eligible populations: Medicaid non-disabled, non-elderly, non-caregiver adults (roughly 45% of Medicaid enrollment in expansion states).

**Requirement definition** (per OBBBA Section 1905(y)):
- 80 hours per month of one or more of: employment, work programs/job training, educational enrollment (½ time), community service, or combination
- Exceptions: pregnant individuals, caregivers, students in higher education full-time, individuals with work incapacity

**Implementation funding**: $200 million to CMS (FY2026) + $200 million distributed to states ($100M equally distributed, $100M by Medicaid population). This is insufficient for 50-state implementation at scale; roughly $2-4 million per state for setup, or $10-20 per enrollee for the first year.

**Impact projection**: Arkansas 2018 provides the empirical precedent. When Arkansas implemented work requirements on June 1, 2018, it disenrolled 18,000 beneficiaries in 6 months (approximately 20% of the non-elderly, non-disabled population in the state at that time). The court found that "work reporting requirements do not advance the statutory purpose of Medicaid," and the D.C. Circuit upheld this ruling in 2020 (Gresham v. Azar, 950 F.3d 615). However, the ruling was specific to Arkansas and did not create national precedent.

**Updated projection** (Georgetown CCF March 2026): Under OBBBA work requirements, 1.2–1.8 million beneficiaries are projected to lose coverage over the first year of implementation, with the figure rising to 2–3 million annually by 2028. This assumes: (1) state implementation rates improve past Arkansas (most states will have better infrastructure), (2) some workforce participation increases from baseline (Arkansas effect was net zero on employment outcomes), (3) but procedural barriers remain high due to documentation requirements and administrative burden.

**Racial equity impact**: Work requirements function as regressive barriers that disproportionately affect populations with documented employment barriers:
- Black workers have 8.6% unemployment vs. 4.1% white unemployment (BLS 2026 data)
- Hispanic workers in rural areas face seasonal employment patterns and documentation barriers (undocumented status, valid DLs)
- Indigenous populations on or near reservations have chronic unemployment (23–45% depending on reservation) and documented CMS data failures in processing tribal ID documentation
- Single mothers (85% of Medicaid caregivers excluded from requirements, but the category is under-implemented) face childcare barriers

## Root Causes: Three Legislative and Administrative Mechanisms

### 1. Budgetary Pressure and Score Gimmicks

**CBO scoring of work requirements**: Work requirements are scored by CBO as generating federal savings through disenrollment (fewer Medicaid beneficiaries = lower federal matching costs). The OBBBA budget reconciliation process (passed under the Byrd Rule, 50-vote Senate threshold) required that every provision "pay for itself" on a 10-year CBO score. Work requirements were scored at $145 billion in 10-year savings primarily through coverage loss, not behavioral change.

**Perverse incentive**: The reconciliation scoring mechanism creates a direct financial incentive for policymakers to design policies that maximize disenrollment, regardless of employment outcomes. Arkansas achieved zero measurable employment effect, yet still generated $150 million in federal Medicaid savings. The policy mechanism is budget-driven, not employment-driven.

### 2. Medicaid's Vulnerable Legal Status Post-Loper Light

The Chevron deference doctrine (overruled by Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo, 603 U.S. ___ (2024)) previously protected agency statutory interpretations of ambiguous terms. Medicaid is a statute defined by Congress's intent to "furnish medical assistance" (42 U.S.C. § 1396). Courts interpreting work requirements must now ask whether the statute text affirmatively authorizes agency rulemaking on work requirements, not whether the agency's interpretation is reasonable.

**Current legal status**: The federal government already appealed the Arkansas decision (Gresham) to the D.C. Circuit, lost, and did not seek Supreme Court review. However, states implementing work requirements will face new litigation. Post-Loper, courts will apply textualist analysis: Does the Medicaid statute (42 U.S.C. § 1905) affirmatively authorize work requirements? The text does not; work requirements were added by OBBBA as a new condition. Courts applying textualism to the statute could find that OBBBA's work requirement provision (statutory, not regulatory) is unambiguously clear and constrains agency implementation. However, courts could also find the statute unambiguous in authorizing state flexibility, creating a circuit split until SCOTUS review.

**Litigation timeline**: The first work-requirement challenge will likely be filed within 6 months of HHS guidance (expected June 2026). State implementation begins January 2027. If litigation is decided by Q1 2027, states face 9-month implementation gap (January–August 2027) before knowing whether requirements are legal. Many states will implement provisionally, creating temporary disenrollment of 1–2 million people before legal clarity.

### 3. Federal-State Medicaid Matching Rate Leverage

The federal-state match (FMAP) is the lever by which Congress controls state Medicaid policy. States cannot opt out of Medicaid but can vary benefits, administrative standards, and eligibility. By conditioning federal matching on work requirements, Congress uses the federal funding threat to impose state-level enrollment barriers. States facing elimination of enhanced matching for expansion (like the 12 non-expansion states) cannot afford to refuse; they must implement or lose federal dollars.

**Federal leverage example**: Texas, which covers 6.1 million Medicaid beneficiaries (25% expansion-eligible), faces losing ~$18 billion in 10-year federal matching if it exits Medicaid entirely. Yet eliminating expansion federal matching (one element of OBBBA) reduces the incentive to expand within Texas's existing structure. Paradoxically, the FMAP mechanism allows Congress to use federal dollars as both carrot (enhanced matching) and stick (withdrawal of enhanced matching) to achieve policy goals that states might not independently choose.

## Structural Vulnerabilities

### Vulnerability 1: Voter Registration as a Dependent Infrastructure

The NVRA (42 U.S.C. § 1973gg-5) mandates voter registration opportunities at Medicaid offices as a condition of federal funding. However, the statute does not require states to maintain Medicaid office staffing levels or to provide adequate resources for voter registration. The Act imposes the requirement but not the funding.

**Current uptake rates**: Less than 2.5% of Medicaid enrollees register to vote or update registration at the point-of-service, despite NVRA requirements. The reason is straightforward: paper forms require training, oversight, and staff time. Busy Medicaid offices deprioritize voter registration when enrollment processing is resource-constrained.

**Disenrollment effect**: When Medicaid disenrollment occurs (1.2–1.8 million projected in 2027 alone), the voter registration infrastructure those offices represented is also lost. If 1.5 million people disenroll due to work requirements, and only 2.5% had registered at the point of service, still 37,500 people were registered through that infrastructure. But more importantly, the *existence* of that office contact point enabled state election officials to conduct NVRA outreach. Disenrollment reduces the contact vector.

**Procedural racism mechanism**: States with higher-minority Medicaid populations (Alabama, Louisiana, Mississippi, South Carolina, Georgia) are concentrated in the non-expansion South, where work requirement disenrollment will hit hardest. These are the same states where historical purges of voter rolls have been documented at higher rates (Brennan Center voter purge data shows Southern states purge at 2x the national average, often citing Medicaid status as a disqualifying factor). Removing Medicaid enrollment infrastructure removes a registration channel in exactly the states where voter removal is highest.

### Vulnerability 2: Administrative Capacity Collapse in Rural and Majority-Minority Areas

Rural hospital closures are directly linked to Medicaid funding. Hospitals that are primary employers in rural areas close due to Medicaid payment cuts, which reduces rural tax base, which reduces state Medicaid administrative budgets, which reduces enrollment office staffing.

**Rural hospital vulnerability** (Chartis/National Rural Health Association 2026):
- 380 independent rural hospitals at risk of negative operating income in 2026 vs. 325 in 2023 (17% increase)
- 417 rural facilities vulnerable to closure
- Average Medicaid revenue loss: $630,665 per hospital per year
- Concentration: 55 additional rural hospitals at risk are in Ohio (5), Missouri (4), Iowa (2), North Carolina (2), Maine (2), West Virginia (1), plus others in Alabama, Georgia, Tennessee, Louisiana, Mississippi

**State Medicaid admin budget impact**: States typically fund Medicaid office operations from general revenue. When hospitals (the largest employers in rural areas) close or reduce operations, property tax and sales tax revenue decline, reducing state general funds, which reduces state Medicaid office budgets. The causal chain is: OBBBA cuts → hospital closures → state revenue decline → Medicaid office budget cuts → reduced enrollment capacity → procedural disenrollment.

**Racial equity impact**: The 12 non-expansion states are disproportionately concentrated in the South and have higher Black population percentages in Medicaid-eligible populations. Rural hospitals in these states are already stressed from not having expansion funding. Black Americans disproportionately live in rural areas of the South (30–40% of rural population in AL, MS, LA, GA). Rural hospital closure in these states simultaneously reduces healthcare access AND employment opportunities (hospitals are major employers) AND Medicaid office staffing.

### Vulnerability 3: Litigation Paralysis and Conditional Implementation

Work requirement litigation will unfold in parallel with state implementation (January 2027). States face three risk scenarios:

1. **Litigation wins in early states** (disenrollment blocked): States that have already disenrolled beneficiaries must re-enroll them; Medicaid covering people not in the system creates coverage gaps and medical care interruptions. Example: Arkansas disenrolled 18,000; if litigation had succeeded mid-implementation, those 18,000 would have been without coverage for 3–6 months before re-enrollment.

2. **Litigation loses nationally**: Massive enrollment contraction proceeds with confirmed legal authority. 1.2–1.8 million people lose coverage, with cascading effects (hospital closures from lost Medicaid volume, insurance markets tighten).

3. **Circuit split**: 6th, 8th, 10th circuits rule differently; some states' work requirements blocked, others proceed. Creates perverse geography (same policy legal in Oklahoma, illegal in Kansas due to circuit assignment).

States implementing work requirements are essentially making a bet on litigation outcomes while processing real disenrollment. The administrative architecture is locked in before legal clarity. This is a deliberate choice to place the burden of legal risk on beneficiaries, not the state.

## International Precedent: Medicaid as Counter-Precedent

**No other developed democracy ties healthcare access to work requirements.** This is a distinctly American policy.

**EU precedent**: European Union healthcare systems are funded through general taxation and/or mandatory insurance pooling, not conditional Medicaid-style programs. Work requirements do not exist; coverage is universal or near-universal. Countries that face rising healthcare costs (Germany, France) address them through price controls and efficiency measures, not coverage reduction. The OECD health database shows no peer nation implements "work requirements" for healthcare eligibility.

**Precedent that is relevant**: Other democracies facing entrenchment threats have used administrative capacity reductions as a mechanism. Peru's Fujimori regime (1990s, before eventual collapse) reduced vote-counting capacity in rural areas to suppress turnout. Hungary's Orbán has reduced funding for municipal elections to create administrative burdens. The mechanism — reducing state capacity in opposition-majority areas to create procedural barriers — is a recognized autocratization vector in comparative democracy research (International IDEA, V-Dem Institute).

**Precedent that matters for recovery**: South Korea's 1987 recovery (from dictatorship) included rapid expansion of healthcare access to rural and working-class areas as part of legitimating democratic institutions. The theory was: functional delivery of basic services (healthcare, education, infrastructure) is necessary for democratic legitimacy. Uruguay's recovery (post-dictatorship 1985) prioritized healthcare access to marginalized populations as equity repair. The precedent suggests that recovery from democratic erosion requires expansion of state capacity in underserved areas, not reduction.

## Reform Pathways

### Pathway 1: Congressional Action — OBBBA Work Requirement Repeal or Modification

**Direct repeal**: Congress could repeal the work requirement provision of OBBBA (Section 1905(y) additions) via budget reconciliation (50-vote threshold). Requires: (1) House majority, (2) Senate 50 votes + VP tiebreaker. Current political conditions (Republican trifecta) make repeal infeasible in 2026-2027.

**Modification via waiver authority**: Congress could explicitly authorize HHS to waive work requirements for states meeting criteria (e.g., "states with above-median procedural disenrollment rates can waive implementation"). This requires legislative change and is politically contingent.

**Expansion funding restoration**: Congress could restore enhanced Medicaid matching for new-expansion states by amending the FMAP formula (42 U.S.C. § 1396d). This would allow 12 non-expansion states to access expanded coverage without state-level political decision. Requires legislative change; feasible if health-equity messaging dominates.

**Implementation constraint**: Congress could impose conditions on HHS guidance (e.g., "states must achieve >95% correct eligibility determinations before implementing work requirements"). This is legislative instruction and does not require rule changes. Would delay implementation to 2028+ while states build capacity.

### Pathway 2: Administrative/Regulatory — HHS Guidance and Agency Restraint

**Guidance language on exceptions**: HHS guidance (due June 2026) will define which populations qualify for exceptions (pregnant individuals, caregivers, incapacity standard). Broad interpretation of exceptions narrows the eligible population for work requirements. Narrow interpretation exposes more people to disenrollment.

**"Good faith effort" standard for extensions**: The statute allows HHS to grant 2-year extensions (to 12/31/2028) for states demonstrating "good faith effort." HHS guidance will define this standard. A stringent standard (independent audit, low error rates) makes extensions difficult. A permissive standard makes most states eligible for extension.

**Automatic voter registration via guidance**: HHS cannot mandate AVR (that's state-level), but CMS could condition Medicaid administrative matching on evidence that states are implementing AVR or equivalent outreach. This is regulatory leverage without statutory requirement.

**Procedural safeguards via guidance**: HHS could require states to implement pre-disenrollment hearings, notice and opportunity to cure, or continuation of coverage pending administrative appeal. These are administrative due process mechanisms within HHS authority.

### Pathway 3: Litigation — Administrative Procedure Act (APA) Challenges

**Arbitrary and capricious standard**: The Arkansas litigation (Gresham v. Azar) established that CMS approval of work requirements as advancing the "Medicaid purpose" is subject to APA review. If courts find that (1) HHS guidance in June 2026 fails to articulate a rational basis for work requirements, OR (2) data show work requirements do not advance health outcomes (Arkansas precedent), courts can block implementation.

**Chevron vacancy**: Post-Loper Bright, agencies no longer receive deference for statutory interpretations of ambiguous terms. If HHS must show that OBBBA unambiguously authorizes work requirements (textualist reading), courts will examine the statute text directly. The text does not explicitly mention work requirements; it authorizes states to implement "any permissible activity" as defined by HHS. Courts could find this delegation sufficiently clear, or could find it an invalid non-delegation.

**Injunction mechanics**: A district court blocking work requirements implementation would issue a preliminary injunction preventing HHS from approving state work requirement waivers. This would prevent January 2027 implementation. The government would appeal; the case would reach the D.C. Circuit by Q2 2027. If the D.C. Circuit affirms the block (as happened in Arkansas), the Supreme Court would likely grant cert due to the conflict between circuit law and new OBBBA statutory language. SCOTUS review would take 12–18 months (2028–2029).

**Outcome uncertainty**: SCOTUS is 6-3 conservative. Work requirements are aligned with conservative policy preferences (work-as-condition, benefit restriction). Courts may uphold work requirements even if the Arkansas precedent (blocking them) stands. However, the addition of statutory language (OBBBA) vs. regulatory authority (prior work requirement waivers) strengthens the case for work requirements by grounding them in Congress's explicit intent, not just agency discretion.

### Pathway 4: Electoral — 2026 and 2028 Leverage

**2026 midterms**: Congressional control in 2026 will determine whether repeals/modifications are feasible. If Democrats gain the House, repeal becomes straightforward (budget reconciliation). If Republicans maintain control, repeal is infeasible but modification (waiver authority expansion) remains possible if vulnerable incumbents face electoral pressure.

**2028 presidential election**: A change in presidential administration (2024→2025 happened with a Republican victory) determines whether HHS enforces OBBBA strictly or grants waivers liberally. A future Democratic administration could grant waivers to all states, effectively suspending work requirements without repealing them. However, this strategy risks litigation (states and Congress could challenge the legality of executive non-enforcement) and is contingent on electoral outcomes.

**State ballot initiatives**: A small number of states (Arizona, Colorado, Nevada, Montana, Ohio) have ballot initiative processes that could pass Medicaid protection measures or Medicaid expansion amendments (independent of state legislatures). These are state-level workarounds but do not address the federal work requirement mandate.

## Proposal Integration and Reform Architecture

### Immediate Actions (2026)

1. **Comment on HHS guidance** (April–June 2026 comment period):
   - Advocate for broad exceptions (expand "incapacity" definition, include underemployment situations)
   - Require 100% accurate eligibility determination before implementation (audit mandate)
   - Condition implementation on simultaneous AVR rollout in each state
   - Request 2-year extension for states with high disenrollment during prior unwinding

2. **Litigation coordination**:
   - Identify lead plaintiffs (people who will lose coverage due to work requirements in high-enrollment states: Texas, California, Florida, New York)
   - File APA challenges pre-emptively (before January 2027) challenging HHS guidance as arbitrary and capricious
   - Focus on racial equity and voter registration nexus (NVRA standing argument)

3. **State-level advocacy**:
   - 12 non-expansion states: Push for proactive expansion to unlock enhanced federal matching and avoid work requirement complications
   - Expansion states: Advocate for AVR implementation before 6-month recertification begins
   - Introduce state-level legislation requiring Medicaid office voter registration training and resources

### Medium-term (2027–2028)

1. **Support litigation through appellate process**:
   - If district court blocks work requirements, support appeal defense
   - Prepare circuit-level briefs on constitutional and administrative law grounds
   - Build record for SCOTUS cert petition (if needed)

2. **Protect voter registration infrastructure**:
   - Federal legislation (requires congressional change): Amend NVRA to require minimum Medicaid office staffing levels for voter registration
   - Alternatively, condition Medicaid federal funding on evidence of AVR implementation
   - Help states implement AVR to decouple voter registration from enrollment office capacity

3. **Medicaid expansion repair**:
   - If new administration takes office, issue waiver for 12 non-expansion states to access enhanced federal matching retroactively
   - Restore Medicaid expansion funding through executive action or congressional budget reconciliation (if Congress changes hands)

### Long-term (2028–2030)

1. **Structural reform**:
   - Decouple healthcare access from employment (move to universal healthcare or expanded Medicaid with no work conditions)
   - Establish baseline voter registration infrastructure independent of Medicaid enrollment offices (state voter registration agencies, automatic registration at all public interactions)
   - Fund rural hospital stabilization through federal infrastructure investments (as part of economic development, not Medicaid)

2. **Accountability**:
   - Document and publish data on work requirement disenrollment by race, state, and outcome
   - Establish federal standards for Medicaid eligibility accuracy (error rate caps, audit frequencies)
   - Require annual voting participation impact assessments from states implementing work requirements

## NVRA Section 7 Enforcement Angle — Callais Litigation Opportunity (added May 13, 2026)

The Callais redistricting cascade (Domain 49) creates a new and underutilized litigation pathway that intersects directly with this domain's core analysis: NVRA Section 7 enforcement in the Medicaid disenrollment context.

**Statutory background**: Under 52 U.S.C. § 20506 (NVRA Section 7), Medicaid enrollment offices are designated voter registration agencies. States are federally required to offer voter registration at every in-person Medicaid enrollment interaction, including disenrollment proceedings. This requirement is not conditional on staffing levels or administrative convenience — it is a federal mandate attached to OBBBA Medicaid funding.

**The disenrollment-as-disenfranchisement theory**: When OBBBA work requirements disenroll Medicaid beneficiaries through procedural failures (the primary disenrollment mechanism — 69% of prior unwinding disenrollments were procedural, not substantive), states are conducting mass administrative interactions that trigger Section 7 voter registration obligations. If states are NOT offering voter registration at disenrollment hearings, notices, or administrative appeals, they are violating NVRA Section 7 in those interactions — creating a federal cause of action independent of the healthcare question.

**The Callais connection**: Six states now in post-Callais redistricting special sessions (Alabama, Tennessee, Louisiana, South Carolina, Mississippi, Florida) are among the 12 OBBBA non-expansion states with reduced Medicaid enrollment infrastructure and the states with the highest documented voter roll purge rates (2x the national average, per Brennan Center). These are simultaneously: (1) states eliminating majority-Black congressional districts under Callais, (2) states with the highest Medicaid disenrollment impact from OBBBA work requirements, and (3) states with the worst records of NVRA Section 7 compliance. The geographic overlap is not coincidental — it reflects the same population being targeted by multiple simultaneous mechanisms.

**State AG coalition litigation opportunity**: State attorneys general (particularly in the 23-state coalition already litigating the mail voting executive order) have standing to enforce NVRA Section 7 against non-compliant states. A Section 7 enforcement action would:
- Require states to document their NVRA compliance at every Medicaid disenrollment interaction
- Create a discovery record of how many disenrollment proceedings occurred without voter registration offers
- Provide injunctive relief requiring remedial voter registration outreach to all people disenrolled since January 2027 without proper Section 7 compliance
- Expose the systematic relationship between Medicaid disenrollment and voter roll contraction in the same demographic groups

**The existing precedent**: The U.S. Supreme Court in Husted v. A. Philip Randolph Institute (2018) upheld Ohio's voter purge process but specifically noted that states must comply with NVRA notification requirements before removing voters. NVRA Section 7 compliance at public assistance agencies has historically been among the least-enforced provisions of the statute. The U.S. DOJ under prior administrations periodically brought Section 7 enforcement actions; current DOJ under the Trump administration has no incentive to enforce. State AG enforcement is the primary available mechanism.

**Recommended action for state AG coalition**: The coalition coordinating on the mail voting EO challenge should add an NVRA Section 7 enforcement track targeting states where Medicaid disenrollment and majority-minority district elimination are coinciding. This creates a federal question angle (NVRA enforcement) that is independent of APA Medicaid challenges and independent of VRA Section 2 redistricting claims — a third litigation pathway available in the same jurisdictions.

**June 1 HHS rule as the critical documentary anchor**: The HHS interim final rule due June 1, 2026 on work requirement implementation procedures will specify the documentation burden for the 80-hour/month requirement. This is the triggering document for NVRA enforcement litigation: does the rule include provisions maintaining Section 7 voter registration offer requirements during work requirement administrative transitions? Organizations with NVRA enforcement standing should audit the June 1 rule within 30 days of release and document any Section 7 compliance gaps. The June 30 – August 31 state outreach period (when states must contact Medicaid expansion adults about work requirements) is the next NVRA compliance window — state outreach materials must include Section 7 voter registration offers or the outreach itself is a NVRA violation.
