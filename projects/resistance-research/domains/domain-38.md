## Executive Summary

The United States federal government's legal relationship with the 574 federally recognized tribal nations is not a civil rights program, an affirmative action scheme, or a discretionary spending commitment. It is a government-to-government treaty obligation — the product of more than 370 ratified treaties in which Indigenous nations ceded land, resources, and sovereign territory in exchange for specific, enumerated federal commitments: healthcare, education, law enforcement, land management, and political self-governance. Those treaty obligations are the supreme law of the land under Article VI of the Constitution. They cannot be impounded, rescinded by executive order, or administratively defunded by an efficiency task force. Their violation is not a budget decision — it is a constitutional breach.

The Trump administration's 2025–2026 actions against tribal program infrastructure constitute the most comprehensive federal breach of treaty obligations since the termination era of the 1950s — a period that Congress formally repudiated in 1970, and whose harms continue to shape Indian Country today. The Department of Government Efficiency has directed the closure of 25 or more Bureau of Indian Affairs offices — more than one in four BIA locations nationwide — severing access to land titling, real estate management, law enforcement support, and economic development services that tribes are legally entitled to receive. The Indian Health Service has lost more than 1,000 employees and had at least 12 facilities shuttered, despite the fact that IHS provides healthcare to 2.6 million Native Americans under a treaty relationship that the Supreme Court recognized in *McGirt v. Oklahoma* (2020) as "one of the most robust collections of federal promises ever made." The presidential budget for FY2026 proposes $911 million in cuts — 24% — from core tribal programs, including a $617 million reduction in BIA self-governance funding and a $107 million cut to tribal law enforcement.

Simultaneously, the Supreme Court's pending decision in *Trump v. Barbara* (argued April 1, 2026) has raised the constitutional stakes to their maximum. The Trump administration's birthright citizenship executive order, if upheld even in part, could create destabilizing ambiguity about the citizenship status of children born to tribal members — reopening a question that Congress conclusively answered in 1924 with the Indian Citizenship Act. The government's own brief in *Trump v. Barbara* acknowledges "the exception for children of tribal Indians" as a recognized exception to birthright citizenship, potentially confirming a constitutional architecture in which tribal membership functions as something other than US citizenship — and where tribal members' status depends on the political branch that controls citizenship recognition.

The democratic design argument of this domain is structural: the assault on tribal program infrastructure is an assault on governmental authority — on the institutional capacity of 574 sovereign governments to deliver services, enforce law, and exercise the self-determination rights that 50 years of federal Indian policy, from the Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act (1975) to ISDEAA amendments through 2022, has progressively expanded. Defunding tribal governments is not analogous to cutting a social services program. It is analogous to defunding state governments. The constitutional and treaty framework treats tribal nations as sovereigns — not as beneficiaries.

## 1. The Legal Foundation: Treaty Rights, Trust Obligations, and the Government-to-Government Relationship

### 1.1 The Treaty Architecture

Between 1778 and 1871, the United States entered into 370 ratified treaties with tribal nations. Congress ended the treaty-making era in 1871 by statute, but existing treaties were explicitly preserved. Those treaties — in the terms of the Supremacy Clause — are the supreme law of the land, binding on every department of the federal government. They are not subject to administrative waiver, executive discretion, or appropriations impoundment without direct congressional action.

The core exchange documented in treaty after treaty was land for governmental services. Tribes ceded territories. The United States committed to protect the remaining territory, provide educational and health services, support tribal governance, and recognize tribes' right to self-governance within their territories. The specifics varied by treaty and era, but the underlying structure was consistent: government-to-government negotiation producing legal obligations on both parties, enforceable in federal court.

The Supreme Court has consistently interpreted these treaties as living documents that must be interpreted liberally in favor of tribal nations — the "canons of construction" that govern federal Indian law. In *Washington v. Washington State Commercial Passenger Fishing Vessel Ass'n* (1979), the Court found that treaty fishing rights reserved "in common with" other citizens entitled tribes to up to 50% of the harvestable fish, construing the treaty by what the parties would have understood at the time of signing. In *McGirt v. Oklahoma* (2020), the Court ruled that Congress had never formally disestablished the Muscogee (Creek) Nation reservation, meaning millions of acres of eastern Oklahoma remained Indian Country — a ruling with profound implications for criminal jurisdiction, land management, and governmental authority that the Trump administration's DOJ has sought to narrow.

The trust relationship is the fiduciary dimension of this sovereignty. The United States holds land in trust for tribal nations — over 56 million acres — and manages those trust assets with a legally enforceable fiduciary duty. The BIA manages trust land, natural resources, and financial accounts. The Indian Health Service provides healthcare. The Bureau of Indian Education administers 183 schools. These are not program choices. They are trust obligations whose breach is cognizable in federal court.

### 1.2 The Federal Self-Determination Framework

The Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act of 1975 (ISDEAA, Pub. L. 93-638) restructured the federal-tribal relationship from direct federal service delivery to tribal self-governance. Under ISDEAA Section 638 contracts and Title V compacts, tribes can assume control of federal programs that serve their communities — taking the federal dollars appropriated for those programs and administering them directly, with reduced federal administrative overhead. Over 400 of the 574 federally recognized tribes now operate IHS programs under self-determination compacts. Tribes administer schools, courts, law enforcement, water systems, and economic development programs under 638 contracts.

The ISDEAA compact model has two critical democratic design implications. First, it places governmental authority where it belongs — in the sovereign entity responsible for the community. Tribes that self-govern are better positioned than federally-administered programs to meet community needs because they are accountable to their members, not to a federal bureaucracy in Washington. Second, it creates a direct dependency: if the federal funds appropriated for these programs are cut or impounded, the tribes' ability to self-govern is directly curtailed — not because they lack the capacity but because the federal government has unilaterally reduced the funding stream that is their treaty entitlement.

This is the DOGE trap. When DOGE terminates BIA office leases and reduces IHS staffing, it is not merely reducing federal overhead — it is defunding the governmental infrastructure of sovereign nations that have transitioned to self-determination models under congressional authorization. The federal government cannot simultaneously require tribes to assume governmental functions under ISDEAA and then defund the programs through which those functions are funded.

## 2. The 2025–2026 Assault: DOGE, Budget Cuts, and Structural Defunding

### 2.1 BIA Office Closures: The Infrastructure Attack

The Department of Government Efficiency directed GSA to terminate leases for 25 or more Bureau of Indian Affairs offices beginning in early 2025. These are not redundant administrative facilities. BIA regional and agency offices are the physical infrastructure through which the trust relationship operates — the location where tribal members and tribal governments access the land management, real estate, law enforcement, and probate services that treaty and statute require the federal government to provide.

The BIA office in Ashland, Wisconsin — serving all 11 federally recognized Wisconsin tribes — is scheduled for closure in August 2026. Its functions include land-to-trust applications (the legal process by which tribal nations restore land to trust status), real estate management (overseeing the 56 million acres held in federal trust), and law enforcement support (coordinating with tribal police in jurisdictions where BIA agents have concurrent authority). These are legal functions, not administrative overhead. "Much of that work happens in those offices in ways that are virtually impossible to do remotely," Oneida Nation Chairman Tehassi Hill told Fox 11. In Seminole County, seven employees lost their jobs when the local BIA office was shuttered; the phone line is now disconnected.

DOGE's plan encompasses 41 offices across BIA, IHS, the National Indian Gaming Commission (NIGC), and the DOI Office of Hearings and Appeals — Probate Hearings Division. The probate function is particularly consequential: when an Indian land owner dies intestate, the BIA's Office of Hearings and Appeals is responsible for determining heirship. Without this function, trust land title goes unresolved — making development, sale, or lease impossible. The result is not administrative delay. It is a direct impairment of tribal property rights that flows from the trust obligation itself.

### 2.2 Indian Health Service: Dismantling the Healthcare Trust Obligation

The Indian Health Service provides healthcare to approximately 2.6 million Native Americans through 46 hospitals, 347 health centers, and 6 school health centers — either directly or through ISDEAA compact arrangements with tribes. The legal basis is not programmatic: IHS was established to fulfill the federal government's treaty and trust obligation to provide healthcare to tribal communities in exchange for the land cessions that made the United States possible.

The FY2025 IHS budget was approximately $8 billion — already far below the Indian health system's documented need. The National Tribal Budget Formulation Workgroup, which aggregates tribal input on IHS funding priorities, recommended $73 billion for FY2025 — nine times the appropriated amount — reflecting decades of deferred infrastructure maintenance, persistent funding gaps, and the documented health disparities that result. IHS-served patients face tuberculosis incidence rates 5.4 times higher than the general US population, diabetes rates 3.2 times higher, and maternal mortality rates that exceed comparable demographic groups in every region.

In 2025, DOGE-directed early retirement offers and forced attrition eliminated more than 1,000 IHS employees. A subsequent analysis found an additional 2,500 employees were at imminent risk of departure. DOGE shut down at least 12 IHS offices and facilities, including offices providing water and sewage inspection and kitchen safety inspection at tribal nutrition programs. The FY2026 presidential budget proposes $900 million in additional IHS cuts. Senators Ron Wyden and Patty Murray wrote to HHS Secretary Robert Kennedy Jr. in March 2026 demanding a halt to the cuts, describing them as "wreaking havoc on Tribal communities." The administration has not responded.

The constitutional dimension is direct. The federal government's obligation to provide healthcare to tribal communities is a treaty obligation, not a discretionary program. Impounding IHS funds or eliminating IHS staff through DOGE administrative action — without congressional authorization — is an impoundment in violation of the treaty obligations and, if it results in healthcare deprivation, potentially cognizable as a breach of fiduciary duty in federal court.

### 2.3 The FY2026 Budget: A 24% Structural Defunding

The Trump administration's FY2026 presidential budget proposes cutting core tribal programs by $911 million — 24% — from a funding base that tribal nations and their advocates have documented as already severely inadequate. The specific reductions include:

**Bureau of Indian Affairs — $617 million cut**: Encompasses self-governance program funding, road maintenance, housing assistance, economic development, land management, and the administrative infrastructure for trust management. The proposed cut targets the programs through which ISDEAA's self-determination framework operates — the tribal 638 contracts and Title V compacts. A 24% cut to BIA self-governance funding is a 24% cut to the governmental capacity of every tribe operating under the self-determination framework.

**Tribal Law Enforcement — $107 million cut (20%)**: BIA Public Safety and Justice programs fund tribal police, detention facilities, and tribal courts in jurisdictions where BIA has law enforcement authority. In tribal communities where there is no municipal or county police department serving tribal lands, BIA is the police. A 20% cut to tribal law enforcement is not a budget line item. It is a safety crisis.

**Bureau of Indian Education construction — $187 million cut**: The BIE operates 183 schools on tribal lands, including 55 residential schools. The construction cut targets deferred maintenance and capital improvements for schools the federal government is legally obligated to maintain. The OMB justification cites "poor management, cost overruns, and delays" — without noting that those conditions are a direct result of chronic federal underfunding.

**Indian Guaranteed Loan Program — elimination**: The Indian Guaranteed Loan Program provides tribal businesses with access to guaranteed loans from commercial lenders, addressing the persistent credit gap in Indian Country. Its elimination removes the primary federal tool for tribal economic development financing.

NCAI — the National Congress of American Indians, representing 574 tribal nations — has documented that the proposed cuts "break foundational trust and treaty obligations" and will "undermine tribal self-determination, public safety, health, and economic development." The proposed cuts are being litigated as violations of specific treaty commitments by NARF and tribal legal coalitions.

## 3. Trump v. Barbara: The Birthright Citizenship Threat to Tribal Status

### 3.1 The Constitutional Question

*Trump v. Barbara* (No. 25-365), argued before the Supreme Court on April 1, 2026, presents the question whether Executive Order 14160 — which eliminates birthright citizenship for children of parents without lawful US status — violates the Fourteenth Amendment's Citizenship Clause. The case directly involves tribal sovereign status because the government's own brief acknowledges "the exception for children of tribal Indians" as a recognized historical exception to birthright citizenship.

The legal history is significant. In *Elk v. Wilkins* (1884), the Supreme Court held that a Native American born to tribal member parents did not qualify for Fourteenth Amendment citizenship because he remained "subject to the jurisdiction" of his tribe, not the United States. Congress responded by enacting the Indian Citizenship Act of 1924, conferring US citizenship on "all non-citizen Indians born within the territorial limits of the United States" — conclusively overriding *Elk* and establishing that tribal membership and US citizenship are not mutually exclusive.

The government's framing in *Trump v. Barbara* potentially reopens what 1924 settled. If the Court upholds the executive order's narrowing of "subject to the jurisdiction" language and endorses "the exception for children of tribal Indians" as constitutional grounding, it creates a doctrinal architecture in which tribal sovereignty and US citizenship are again in tension — a reading that NARF and tribal advocates have identified as potentially destabilizing for the citizenship status of tribal members born to parents who lack status documentation.

At oral argument on April 1, 2026, Chief Justice Roberts described the government's argument as "quirky" — signaling skepticism about the administration's reading of the Citizenship Clause. The Court appeared likely to reject the administration's position on the central birthright citizenship question. But the scope of the ruling — and whether it addresses the tribal exception dicta — will determine whether tribal advocates need to seek legislative confirmation of the Indian Citizenship Act's continued force.

### 3.2 The Structural Stakes: Sovereignty Without Citizenship Protection

The democratic design concern that *Trump v. Barbara* raises for tribal nations is not primarily about the birthright citizenship of any specific class of children. It is about the structural relationship between tribal sovereignty and federal citizenship protection. If the Court's ruling — or future legislation — creates ambiguity about whether tribal membership confers citizenship in the same manner as birth to documented residents, tribal members become a population whose political status is more contingent and more vulnerable to future executive action.

The Indian Citizenship Act should not need reaffirmation. But NARF's guidance to tribal communities as of 2025-2026 includes specific advice about citizenship documentation for tribal members — an advisory that would be unnecessary if the Act's protections were unambiguous. The combination of the executive order, the *Trump v. Barbara* litigation, and the broader "not subject to jurisdiction" doctrinal movement creates a legal environment in which tribal members' citizenship documentation has become a practical concern.

## 4. Democratic Design Analysis: Tribal Sovereignty as Pre-Constitutional Democracy

### 4.1 The Oldest Democratic Governments in North America

Tribal nations are not democratic institutions created by the United States. They are sovereign governments whose democratic traditions predate the republic. The Haudenosaunee Confederacy — the Iroquois Confederation of six nations — operated a democratic governance structure that historians and political theorists have documented as a direct influence on the framers of the US Constitution, particularly through Benjamin Franklin's Albany Plan of Union (1754). The confederacy's Great Law of Peace established representative governance, consensus-based decision-making, and the principle of considering the impact of decisions on seven generations — a governance philosophy with no equivalent in contemporary US federal policy design.

The 574 federally recognized tribal nations today operate under diverse governance structures — from traditional clan-based councils to constitutions modeled on US governance frameworks (often because the Indian Reorganization Act of 1934 required tribal constitutional adoption as a condition of federal recognition). That diversity reflects genuine democratic self-determination: each tribal nation has the right to govern itself according to its own structure, so long as that structure reflects the sovereign authority the tribe exercises.

The democratic design relevance for the broader framework: if the United States is experiencing a crisis of democratic institutions, the most durable models of democratic resilience are the tribal nations that have maintained sovereign governance through 500 years of colonial, federal, and administrative pressure. The Cherokee Nation rebuilt its governmental capacity after forced removal. The Haudenosaunee Confederacy has maintained its Great Law of Peace governance structure through two centuries of federal pressure to dissolve. The Muscogee (Creek) Nation's legal vindication in *McGirt* was the product of 20 years of sustained legal advocacy asserting a sovereignty claim that federal officials assumed had long since been extinguished.

### 4.2 Tribal Elections and Civic Infrastructure

Tribal elections are real democratic elections — with voter registration, candidate qualification requirements, secret ballots, and results that determine governmental leadership. The NCAI's voter engagement programs have documented that Native American voter participation, when faced with specific barriers (polling places far from reservations, voter ID requirements that exclude tribal identification, voter registration deadlines incompatible with tribal ID issuance cycles), mirrors the suppression patterns documented in other minority communities.

The Native American Voting Rights Act, passed in 2018, addresses some of these barriers by requiring states to provide registration and polling place access comparable to what urban voters receive. Its implementation is uneven, and several states with significant tribal populations — South Dakota, North Dakota — have been documented as creating structural barriers to Native American voting. This connects to Domain 1 (Voting Rights) and Domain 49 (Callais VRA Emergency): the *Callais* ruling's elimination of the discriminatory-effects standard under VRA Section 2 is directly applicable to Native American voting rights challenges, and the NAACP LDF's redistricting litigation has tribal co-plaintiffs in several post-Callais cases.

## 5. Actionable Intelligence for Resistance Networks

### 5.1 Treaty Enforcement Litigation

The primary advocacy pathway for defending tribal program funding is litigation grounded in the treaty obligation. NARF's Tribal Supreme Court Project is the lead organization coordinating federal Indian law litigation at the SCOTUS level. At the district and circuit level, tribal legal offices and NARF are actively developing:

- **Impoundment-based claims**: If the administration defunds IHS or BIA below congressionally appropriated levels, the Appropriations Clause argument (Domain 34) applies directly — funding appropriated by Congress cannot be withheld by executive administrative action. Tribes whose ISDEAA compact funding is reduced below the appropriated level have a direct statutory cause of action under ISDEAA Section 110.

- **Breach of trust fiduciary duty**: The BIA's fiduciary duty to manage trust land and resources creates a judicially cognizable cause of action when that duty is breached through negligence or administrative failure. BIA office closures that prevent land-to-trust processing, probate determinations, or real estate management may constitute actionable breach of trust.

- **Treaty-specific claims**: Specific treaties contain specific service commitments. Tribes with treaty health, education, or law enforcement commitments should inventory those treaty provisions and assess whether the specific DOGE cuts reach treaty-obligated services — and, if so, file treaty-based claims in the Court of Federal Claims, which has concurrent jurisdiction over treaty breach claims.

**Tactic**: Organize a coordinated multi-tribe filing in the Court of Federal Claims asserting treaty breach for IHS cuts in the first quarter of FY2027 (January–March), when the budget cuts take effect and treaty violations become documentable.

### 5.2 Appropriations Advocacy Window

The fall 2026 congressional appropriations cycle is the primary legislative advocacy window. The House and Senate Appropriations Committees have historically provided higher tribal program funding than presidential budgets propose — the Senate Interior Subcommittee, in particular, has maintained bipartisan tribal program support through multiple administrations. The fall 2026 window requires:

- **Tribe-by-tribe district mapping**: Every congressional district contains tribal communities or tribal treaty territory. Mapping the specific BIA and IHS cuts to specific congressional districts — and presenting those impacts to the member of Congress representing those districts — is the advocacy tool that moves appropriations votes.

- **Treaty obligation framing**: The government-to-government frame (tribal programs as treaty obligations, not discretionary spending) changes the political economy of the appropriations argument. Members who might support cutting "Indian programs" as discretionary social spending face a different vote when the frame is "treaty breach" — a constitutional violation with historical, legal, and diplomatic consequences.

- **BIA office closure testimony**: The August 2026 scheduled closure of the Ashland, Wisconsin office serving 11 tribes creates a specific advocacy hook. Members from Wisconsin (Senate: Tammy Baldwin, Ron Johnson; House: multiple districts) should be contacted before August 2026 with the specific service disruption consequences of that closure.

**Tactic**: Generate district-level one-page impact documents — modeled on the health-impact maps in Domain 31 — showing which BIA and IHS services are at risk in each congressional district, naming the specific tribal nations affected, and quantifying the treaty obligation being breached.

### 5.3 *Trump v. Barbara* Monitoring and Rapid Response

The Supreme Court is expected to rule in *Trump v. Barbara* in June or July 2026. The ruling's significance for tribal communities requires a rapid response in two scenarios:

**Scenario A — Ruling upholds EO in any form**: If the Court validates any version of the "subject to jurisdiction" narrowing, tribal legal organizations should immediately seek congressional confirmation of the Indian Citizenship Act's continued force — amending 8 U.S.C. § 1401(b) to explicitly provide that tribal membership does not affect birthright citizenship status.

**Scenario B — Court rejects EO broadly**: The broader the rejection, the cleaner the precedent for tribal citizenship protection. But even a broad rejection should be used to: (a) generate documentation of tribal members' citizenship status for community use; (b) lobby for the Tribal Identification Act, which would standardize federal recognition of tribal ID as proof of citizenship.

**Tactic**: Pre-draft a rapid-response legal brief and advocacy memo for each scenario, to be distributed to NCAI, NARF, tribal legal offices, and the congressional contacts within 72 hours of the ruling's release.

### 5.4 Voter Mobilization in Tribal Communities

The Native American Voting Rights Coalition maintains a network of voter protection organizations in tribal communities. The 2026 midterms present a specific opportunity: in states with significant tribal populations — Oklahoma (37+ recognized tribes, McGirt reservation territory), Arizona (22 federally recognized tribes), New Mexico (23 tribes), Montana (8 tribes), Alaska (229 tribes), South Dakota, North Dakota — tribal voter turnout is the difference-making variable in close federal elections.

The infrastructure needed: polling place access (tribal polling locations on reservation territory), same-day registration compatibility with tribal ID, and voter registration outreach to tribal members who are US citizens but who may not be on state voter rolls due to address documentation gaps (a documented pattern in reservation communities).

**Tactic**: Partner with the Native American Voting Rights Coalition and tribal election departments in the 10 states with the most significant tribal populations to conduct a pre-midterm voter registration audit — identifying tribal citizens of voting age who are not registered and running targeted registration drives before state registration deadlines.

## 6. Reform Pathways

### 6.1 Immediate (Legislative, 2026)

**Treaty Obligation Budget Baseline Act**: Establish a statutory appropriations floor for BIA and IHS at the prior year's enacted level, with inflation adjustment, making treaty-obligation programs immune from impoundment or administrative reduction without specific statutory authorization. Modeled on the IHS advance appropriations authority enacted in 2020.

**ISDEAA Compact Funding Protection**: Amend ISDEAA to explicitly prohibit reducing compact funding below the Secretarial Amount — the calculation of what it costs the federal government to administer the program directly — on any basis other than tribal request or demonstrated tribal mismanagement proven through formal hearing.

**BIA Field Office Preservation Act**: Declare BIA regional and agency offices essential facilities for discharge of federal trust obligations; require congressional notification 180 days before any closure; require a tribally-consulted impact assessment showing alternative service delivery before closure can proceed.

### 6.2 Medium-Term (Constitutional and Structural)

**Indian Citizenship Act Reaffirmation**: Amend 8 U.S.C. § 1401(b) to confirm that US citizenship attaches at birth within US territory regardless of tribal membership, removing any ambiguity created by *Trump v. Barbara* dicta.

**Federal Tribal Trust Commission**: Establish an independent commission with audit authority over all federal agencies with trust responsibilities (BIA, IHS, BIE), modeled on the Commission on Civil Rights, with authority to investigate trust violations and report to Congress, the tribal nations, and the public.

**Native American Voting Rights Act Extension**: Reauthorize and expand the 2018 NAVRA with enforcement provisions comparable to VRA Section 5 preclearance for states with documented patterns of Native American voting access barriers, applying the *Callais* post-ruling doctrinal alternatives (state constitutional challenges, APA challenges to election rules) that NARF and tribal legal offices are currently developing.
