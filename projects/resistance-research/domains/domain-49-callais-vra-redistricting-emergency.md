## Executive Summary

On April 29, 2026, the Supreme Court issued its decision in Louisiana v. Callais, 6-3, ruling that Louisiana's congressional map — which created a second majority-Black congressional district to comply with Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act following the Allen v. Milligan (2023) order — constituted an unconstitutional racial gerrymander under the Equal Protection Clause. Writing for the majority, Justice Alito held that Section 2 VRA claims require "evidence giving rise to a strong inference of intentional discrimination," and that if "race and politics are not disentangled," any Section 2 compliance effort could be attacked as racially impermissible.

The ruling is the third leg of a Supreme Court project to dismantle the Voting Rights Act as a functional instrument of democratic accountability: Shelby County v. Holder (2013) eliminated the Section 5 preclearance requirement, removing the advance approval mechanism for changes to voting rules in jurisdictions with documented discrimination histories. Brnovich v. DNC (2021) narrowed Section 2's application to vote denial by establishing a multi-factor balancing test that has functioned to insulate facially neutral restrictions. Callais now completes the circuit by requiring proof of intentional racial motivation — the same standard Congress explicitly rejected and legislatively overrode in the 1982 amendments to the VRA — for vote dilution claims under Section 2.

The practical cascade has been immediate and severe. Within two weeks of the April 29 ruling, the following redistricting actions were underway:

- **Alabama**: Governor Ivey called a special session May 1; the Supreme Court granted a stay of the lower court's injunction on May 11, 2026 (5-4, unsigned order), effectively vacating the Allen v. Milligan-ordered second majority-Black district and allowing Alabama's 2023 legislature-drawn maps to be used in the upcoming primary — with early voting already underway
- **Tennessee**: Governor Lee called a special session beginning May 5; the Republican legislature passed new maps eliminating the state's only majority-Black district (the 9th District, centered on Memphis), carving it into three Republican-leaning districts
- **Louisiana**: Governor Landry suspended the May 16 primaries to allow the legislature to redraw maps; the state has grounds to eliminate one or both of its court-ordered majority-minority districts
- **South Carolina**: The House extended its session to redraw congressional maps, targeting the 6th Congressional District held by 17-term Black Democratic Congressman James Clyburn
- **Mississippi**: Special session scheduled for May 20, 2026
- **Florida**: Governor DeSantis signed new maps within hours of the Callais ruling shifting four seats toward Republicans

An NPR analysis estimates at least 15 Democratic-held congressional districts — nearly all currently represented by members of the Congressional Black Caucus or Congressional Hispanic Caucus — could be redrawn to favor Republicans following Callais. Brookings Institution documented that 35 of 40 Southern representatives of color were elected from majority-minority districts; 74 percent of Black representatives and 84 percent of Hispanic representatives nationally depend on majority-minority districts. Senior Trump advisers told GOP donors that Callais is one of the two Supreme Court cases expected to "transform" Republican power for years.

## 1. The Callais Ruling: What Was Decided and Why It Completes the VRA Destruction Circuit

### 1.1 Background: The Allen v. Milligan Compliance Cycle

Understanding Callais requires understanding the compliance cycle it terminates. The case begins with Allen v. Milligan (2023), in which the Supreme Court — in a 5-4 ruling that surprised observers — upheld a lower court finding that Alabama's 2022 congressional maps violated Section 2 of the VRA by diluting Black voting power. The Allen v. Milligan ruling, written by Chief Justice Roberts, required Alabama to draw a second majority-Black congressional district.

Louisiana faced parallel litigation. After Allen v. Milligan, a federal court ordered Louisiana to draw a second majority-minority congressional district. Louisiana complied — drawing the 6th Congressional District as a majority-Black corridor connecting Baton Rouge and New Orleans — and the resulting map was the subject of the Callais challenge.

The Callais plaintiffs (non-Black Louisiana voters) argued that the 6th District was an unconstitutional racial gerrymander: that race had been the predominant factor in drawing district lines, violating the Equal Protection Clause. The lower courts had ruled that Section 2 compliance provides a "compelling interest" sufficient to justify race-conscious districting. The Supreme Court disagreed, holding that Louisiana had not provided sufficient evidence of intentional discrimination to justify treating the 6th District as a mandatory remedy under Section 2.

### 1.2 The Majority Opinion: Alito's New Legal Standard

Justice Alito's majority opinion (joined by Roberts, Thomas, Gorsuch, Kavanaugh, and Barrett) establishes the following doctrinal structure:

**The intent requirement.** Section 2 claims require "evidence giving rise to a strong inference of intentional discrimination." Alito held that statistical evidence of disparate racial outcomes — even dramatic disparate outcomes like the "low number of Black Louisianans who have been elected to Congress" — is insufficient on its own if it does not "disentangle" racial from political factors. The opinion requires plaintiffs to establish that racial discrimination, as opposed to partisan discrimination, is the cause of the observed outcomes.

**The disentanglement problem.** The majority's central analytical move is requiring plaintiffs to prove that race caused the outcome rather than party. In states with high racial residential segregation and high racial political polarization — conditions that describe virtually every Southern state — Black voters are concentrated in the same areas where Democratic voters are concentrated. Cracking a majority-Black district is, operationally, the same as cracking a majority-Democratic district. Alito's requirement to "disentangle" race from politics creates a proof problem that is legally impossible in states where race and party are empirically correlated at very high rates.

**The cynical use concern.** The majority expresses concern that Section 2 can be "cynically used as a tool for advancing a partisan end" — implying that civil rights organizations bringing racial vote dilution claims might actually be advancing partisan Democratic interests. This framing inverts the VRA's structure: the statute was designed to protect Black voters from racially discriminatory maps drawn by state legislators who were themselves acting in their partisan interest. The majority treats the beneficiaries of the statute's protection as suspect actors whose claims must be viewed with skepticism.

**What was not directly held.** The majority opinion did not formally overrule the effects test established in the 1982 VRA amendments — the text of Section 2 that Congress wrote to eliminate the intent requirement. It did not explicitly vacate Allen v. Milligan. Instead, it held that Louisiana's compliance with Allen v. Milligan was itself unconstitutional because the compliance effort used race as the predominant factor without a sufficient evidentiary showing of intentional discrimination. This creates a logical paradox: the Court ordered Louisiana to create a second majority-minority district (through Allen v. Milligan), then struck down Louisiana's attempt to comply as racially impermissible.

### 1.3 The Dissent: Justice Kagan's Warning

Justice Kagan's dissent (joined by Sotomayor and Jackson) characterized the decision as rendering "Section 2 all but a dead letter" and warned of "far-reaching and grave" consequences. The dissent made two core arguments.

First, the majority's intent requirement contradicts the text of Section 2 as amended in 1982. Congress amended Section 2 specifically to establish an effects test — to require proof of discriminatory outcomes, not discriminatory intent — after the Court in Mobile v. Bolden (1980) had required proof of intent. The 1982 amendments were a direct congressional response to the Court's intent standard. Alito's majority reinstates the Mobile v. Bolden intent standard through doctrinal maneuver that does not formally acknowledge the congressional override.

Second, the "disentanglement" requirement is unworkable in any jurisdiction with racial residential segregation and racial partisan polarization — which is to say, virtually all jurisdictions subject to serious VRA Section 2 litigation. The dissent documented that in Southern states, the correlation between race and party is sufficiently high that no statistical method can fully "disentangle" the two variables. The effect is to immunize racial vote dilution from legal challenge in the states where it is most severe.

### 1.4 The Historical Parallel: Section 5 Preclearance and the Shelby County Circuit

Callais completes a three-ruling VRA destruction circuit that has operated over thirteen years:

- **Shelby County v. Holder (2013)**: Invalidated the coverage formula for Section 5 preclearance — the provision requiring jurisdictions with documented discrimination histories to obtain federal approval before changing voting laws. Shelby County did not formally repeal Section 5; it rendered it unenforceable by striking the coverage formula. Within hours of the ruling, several states enacted voter ID requirements, precinct closures, and registration restrictions that would have been blocked under preclearance.

- **Brnovich v. DNC (2021)**: Narrowed Section 2's application to vote denial by establishing a multi-factor balancing test that treats the "historical background of the state's voting laws" and the "disparate burdens of the challenged practice on minority voters" as factors to be weighed against "the state's legitimate interests" in preventing fraud. The net effect: courts now balance discriminatory burden against stated government interests, making successful Section 2 vote denial challenges substantially more difficult.

- **Callais (2026)**: Narrows Section 2's application to vote dilution (redistricting) by requiring proof of intentional discrimination and mandating "disentanglement" of race from partisan factors that are empirically inseparable in the jurisdictions where the claims arise.

Together, these three rulings have eliminated the two enforcement mechanisms of the VRA: preclearance (Section 5) is effectively dead; vote dilution claims (Section 2 redistricting) now require proving intentional discrimination in an environment where race and party are indistinguishable; vote denial claims (Section 2 non-redistricting) are subject to a balancing test that courts frequently resolve in favor of state "interests."

## 2. The Redistricting Cascade: State-by-State Emergency

### 2.1 Alabama: Allen v. Milligan Reversed Through the Courts

Alabama's response to Callais has been the most procedurally aggressive. The timeline:

- **April 28 (day before Callais)**: Alabama had been operating under a court-ordered second majority-Black district, required by Allen v. Milligan, for the 2024 election cycle. Early voting for the 2026 primary had already begun.
- **April 29 (Callais decided)**: Within hours, Alabama's attorney general filed an emergency motion with the Supreme Court requesting a stay of the district court's injunction requiring use of the court-ordered maps.
- **May 1, 2026**: Governor Ivey called a special legislative session to authorize redistricting, contingent on the Supreme Court granting the stay.
- **May 11, 2026**: The Supreme Court granted a 5-4 unsigned stay order, effectively vacating the district court's injunction and allowing Alabama to use the legislature's 2023 maps (which eliminated the second majority-Black district) — despite the fact that early voting was already occurring under the court-ordered maps. The stay was granted over dissent from Justices Sotomayor, Kagan, and Jackson.

The May 11 Alabama order is constitutionally remarkable: the Supreme Court retroactively altered the redistricting framework for an election already in progress, displacing maps that voters were already casting ballots under. Sotomayor's dissent noted that the district court's "finding of intentional discrimination" — the very standard Callais requires — "is independent of, and unaffected by" the Callais ruling. The majority granted the stay without explanation, in an unsigned order, over a dissent arguing that the lower court had already made the intent finding that Callais requires.

### 2.2 Tennessee: Eliminating Memphis

Governor Lee called a special legislative session beginning May 5, 2026. The Tennessee Republican supermajority passed new congressional maps eliminating the 9th Congressional District, which encompasses majority-Black Memphis and has been represented by a Democrat. The 9th District is carved into three districts, each of which leans Republican under the new lines, effectively eliminating the only majority-Black congressional district in Tennessee and the only Democratic-held seat it contained.

The Intercept documented (May 8, 2026) that Senate Republican leaders, including Senator Marsha Blackburn, had explicitly lobbied for the 9th District elimination. The NAACP and Shelby County Commission have announced they will challenge the new maps in federal court on both intentional discrimination and partisan gerrymandering grounds — knowing that the partisan gerrymandering theory (prohibited from federal court review under Rucho) will need to be pursued under state constitutional grounds, and that the intentional discrimination theory must now meet the new Callais standard.

### 2.3 Louisiana: Suspending Primaries to Redraw

Governor Landry and Attorney General Murrill suspended the state's May 16 congressional primaries the day after Callais was decided, announcing that the legislature would redraw maps before primaries were rescheduled. Louisiana's current maps include two majority-minority districts — the result of two rounds of VRA-compliance litigation. Under Callais, Louisiana can argue that both districts were created without sufficient intentional discrimination evidence, and redraw the map to eliminate one or both. Louisiana's potential map changes could affect the current distribution of the 6 congressional seats; under current maps, 2 are Democratic-held majority-minority seats.

Ballotpedia documented (May 5, 2026) that the Callais ruling triggers immediate consideration of map changes in six states with pending redistricting issues, not including the mid-decade redistricting already completed by Texas (2025), Missouri, North Carolina, and Florida before the Callais ruling.

### 2.4 South Carolina: Targeting James Clyburn

South Carolina's House extended its legislative session to consider redistricting maps targeting the 6th Congressional District, currently held by Representative James Clyburn (D) — the 17-term Black Democratic Congressman who served as House Majority Whip and whose endorsement was widely credited with reversing President Biden's 2020 primary trajectory. South Carolina's maps, already challenged in prior litigation, can now be defended under Callais's more permissive intent standard.

TownHall reported (May 12, 2026) that Republican state legislators have stated they intend to redraw the 6th to reduce its majority-Black composition, arguing under Callais that the current configuration was an impermissible racial gerrymander that favors Democrats. This inverts the civil rights framing: the same Callais logic that allows states to eliminate court-ordered majority-Black districts also allows them to attack existing majority-minority districts as racially impermissible.

### 2.5 Florida: Pre-Callais Plus Post-Callais Acceleration

Florida's redistricting situation is distinct: DeSantis had already enacted aggressive mid-decade maps in 2025, signed within hours of Callais. The new Florida maps shift four congressional seats toward Republicans, building on a state law DeSantis signed that prohibited courts from relying on Section 2 to require majority-minority districts — a provision that was constitutionally questionable before Callais and is now arguably validated by the ruling.

### 2.6 The Aggregate Impact: Congressional Representation Math

The Brookings Institution (May 2026) documented the aggregate stakes:
- 35 of 40 Southern representatives of color are elected from majority-minority districts
- 74 percent of Black House members nationally depend on majority-minority districts
- 84 percent of Hispanic House members nationally depend on majority-minority districts
- An NPR analysis estimates at least 15 currently Democratic-held districts could be redrawn to favor Republicans in the post-Callais environment

The NOTUS analysis (May 2026) documented that members of the Congressional Black Caucus are aware of and planning for the redistricting threat to their seats. The practical electoral consequence: if the full cascade of Callais-triggered redistricting is implemented before the November 2026 midterms, Republicans could add 12-19 seats through redistricting alone, independent of any shift in voter preferences.

## 3. The Legal Architecture: What Challenges Remain Available

### 3.1 Intentional Discrimination Theory Under Callais

Callais requires proof of intentional discrimination, not merely discriminatory effect. This shifts the analytical frame but does not eliminate the legal theory entirely. In practice, intentional discrimination is provable in two ways:

**Direct evidence**: Statements by legislators or redistricting officials demonstrating that racial motivation drove the mapmaking. In the Tennessee special session, public statements by Republican officials about the purpose of eliminating the 9th District (including, reportedly, its racial composition and Democratic character) may provide direct evidence material.

**Arlington Heights factors**: The Supreme Court's Arlington Heights v. Metropolitan Housing Development Corp. (1977) framework allows courts to infer discriminatory intent from circumstantial evidence: the historical background of the decision, the sequence of events leading to it, departures from normal procedural sequence, legislative or administrative history, and statistical evidence. A state that eliminates a majority-Black district within 14 days of a Supreme Court ruling blessing racial vote dilution challenges faces an Arlington Heights analysis that may establish intent.

The district court in the Alabama litigation — the case from which the May 11 stay was issued — had already made a finding of intentional discrimination under Arlington Heights before the Supreme Court granted the stay. Sotomayor's dissent specifically noted that this finding was "independent of, and unaffected by" Callais. If the Supreme Court's stay is a temporary measure pending further proceedings, and if the lower court's intentional discrimination finding stands on remand, Alabama's new maps may face a viable Section 2 challenge on the very ground Callais requires.

### 3.2 State Voting Rights Acts

Fourteen states have enacted state voting rights acts, most modeled on the pre-2013 VRA with effects-test language explicitly preserving what Callais now eliminates at the federal level. California's Voter Participation Rights Act, Washington's Voting Rights Act, and New York's John R. Lewis Voting Rights Act of New York all provide state-law Section 2 analogues applicable to state legislative, local, and school board elections (though not congressional redistricting, which is governed primarily by federal law). For sub-congressional redistricting, state-law challenges are viable in jurisdictions with state voting rights protections.

The Campaign Legal Center (May 2026) identified state voting rights acts as the primary litigation alternative to federal Section 2 after Callais, and called on Democratic state legislatures to enact state-law VRA analogues with explicit effects-test language that cannot be overridden by Callais.

### 3.3 State Constitutional Provisions

Several state constitutions contain anti-discrimination provisions that apply to voting rights independent of federal law. Florida's constitution includes a Fair Districts amendment directly prohibiting maps drawn to advantage one party or diminish the ability of racial minorities to elect candidates of choice. DeSantis's 2025 maps may be vulnerable to challenge under Florida's own constitution, though the state supreme court (reconfigured by DeSantis appointments) would adjudicate.

North Carolina and Wisconsin have state supreme court elections scheduled for 2026 that will determine the ideological composition of the court that adjudicates redistricting challenges under state constitutional provisions.

### 3.4 Section 3 of the VRA: Judicial Bail-In

Section 3 of the VRA — the "bail-in" provision — allows courts that find constitutional violations in voting discrimination cases to subject jurisdictions to a preclearance-like supervision requirement. Unlike Section 5's coverage formula (struck in Shelby County), Section 3 is activated by a court finding of intentional constitutional violation — the same showing Callais requires for Section 2.

If courts make intentional discrimination findings in the Tennessee, Louisiana, or South Carolina redistricting litigation, Section 3 bail-in supervision is available as a remedy. This creates a constructive feedback loop: Callais raises the evidentiary threshold for Section 2, but courts that find intentional discrimination (satisfying the Callais standard) can then impose Section 3 supervision, which provides stronger protective coverage than Section 2 ever did.

The advocacy implication: litigation strategy in post-Callais cases should specifically plead Section 3 bail-in as a remedy, which both raises the stakes for states engaging in demonstrably intentional discrimination and creates precedent for preclearance-like supervision in the absence of the Section 5 coverage formula.

## 4. The Congressional Response and Legislative Remedies

### 4.1 The John R. Lewis Voting Rights Advancement Act

The John R. Lewis Voting Rights Advancement Act — introduced in every Congress since Shelby County and failed to advance in each — would restore a Section 5 preclearance framework with a new coverage formula, update the list of practices requiring preclearance, and explicitly codify the effects test for Section 2 vote dilution claims. In the current congressional configuration (Republican House majority), the Lewis Act has no path to floor votes. In a Democratic trifecta Congress (achievable if Callais-triggered redistricting does not succeed before November 2026), the Lewis Act would be the first-priority legislative response.

The advocacy window: electing a Democratic House majority in November 2026 is the prerequisite. Callais has made that window both more important (legislative remedy is the primary available response) and more difficult (redistricting reduces the Democratic House candidate pool through seat elimination).

### 4.2 Constitutional Amendment: The Section 2 Text Fix

Section 2 of the VRA, as amended by Congress in 1982, contains explicit effects-test language: "A violation of subsection (a) is established if, based on the totality of circumstances, it is shown that the political processes leading to nomination or election in the State or political subdivision are not equally open to participation by members of a class of citizens protected by subsection (a) in that members have less opportunity than other members of the electorate to participate in the political process and to elect representatives of their choice." This text was specifically designed by Congress to override the Supreme Court's Mobile v. Bolden intent requirement.

Callais does not formally hold that this text is unconstitutional. It holds that Section 2 compliance cannot justify race-conscious redistricting without proof of intentional discrimination under the Equal Protection Clause — essentially making the Equal Protection Clause trump the Section 2 effects test when states claim remedial justification. A constitutional amendment explicitly authorizing race-conscious remedial redistricting for demonstrated vote dilution would be the structural fix, but requires two-thirds of both chambers and three-fourths of states.

### 4.3 The Federal Fix That Does Not Require Constitutional Amendment

Voting rights scholars have identified a legislative path that does not require a constitutional amendment: Congress can specify, through explicit statutory findings, the specific circumstances under which race-conscious remedial districting satisfies the compelling interest test for strict scrutiny. The post-2013 Shelby County response model — where Congress could have enacted a new coverage formula but did not — suggests the legislative response must include extremely specific documentation of intentional discrimination in targeted jurisdictions, combined with explicit "findings" language that supports the compelling interest showing Callais requires.

## 5. International Comparative Evidence: How Non-Gerrymandered Systems Protect Minority Representation

### 5.1 Independent Redistricting Commissions with Minority Representation Requirements

The United Kingdom's Boundary Commissions — independent bodies with statutory independence from partisan government — draw parliamentary constituency boundaries under statutory criteria that do not include partisan advantage as a permissible factor. The UK Representation of the People Act requires that boundaries reflect community cohesion, geographic compactness, and administrative boundaries without reference to partisan composition. Minority representation under this system has been addressed through party candidate selection — constituency Labour parties have used all-women shortlists and BAME shortlists to increase minority representation in safe seats — rather than through district composition requirements.

Canada's Electoral Boundaries Readjustment Act creates provincial electoral boundaries commissions with members drawn from the judiciary and academia, explicitly prohibited from using partisan composition in drawing boundaries. Canada's system for minority representation relies on the combination of proportional urban density (which naturally produces diverse urban seats) and party diversity policies. The Federal Electoral Boundaries Commission's work is subject to a parliamentary objections process but not a majority party override.

Germany's mixed-member proportional system provides the strongest minority representation guarantees through the party-list mechanism: parties can include minority candidates on their lists at guaranteed electable positions, and the proportional top-up mechanism ensures that minority voters' preferences are reflected even in single-member constituencies that happen to produce majority-group winners.

### 5.2 The Proportional Representation Alternative

The deepest structural alternative to the Callais cycle is proportional representation, which eliminates the majority-minority district model entirely. Under PR, minority voters do not need to be concentrated in a majority-minority district to elect candidates of choice — they can elect proportional representation through a party-list system regardless of geographic concentration. The specific application to the US context: a multi-member proportional representation system for congressional elections would enable Black voters at 13 percent of the population to elect approximately 13 percent of House members regardless of whether they are geographically concentrated or dispersed.

Fair Representation Act proposals (introduced by Rep. Don Beyer) would create multi-member congressional districts of 3-5 seats with ranked-choice voting, producing proportional-ish outcomes without explicit party lists. This model has been advanced by Protect Democracy and Fair Vote as the structural remedy that would make VRA section 2 litigation unnecessary.

### 5.3 The Australian Model: Independent Redistricting with Legal Standing for Challengers

The Australian Electoral Commission draws all federal electoral boundaries under statutory criteria requiring geographic compactness, community cohesion, and projected enrollment within each jurisdiction, with explicit prohibition on partisan considerations. Any enrolled voter or registered organization can submit objections to draft boundaries during the redistricting process, with a formal objection procedure that includes public hearings and an objection panel. The commission is required to respond to all substantive objections in its final report, creating a transparent accountability record. The Australian model does not specifically address minority representation through geographic mechanisms but eliminates the partisan manipulation mechanism that drives American redistricting abuse.

## 6. The 2026 Electoral Calendar: Immediate Advocacy Windows

### 6.1 The Tight Timeline

The redistricting cascade is operating against a very compressed timeline:

- **May 2026**: Special sessions underway in Tennessee, Alabama, Louisiana, South Carolina, Mississippi
- **June 2026**: Most states' primary filing deadlines; maps must be legally effective before filing opens
- **July–August 2026**: Primary elections in most affected states; maps drawn in May-June will govern these primaries
- **October–November 2026**: General elections under new maps

The window for legal intervention is narrow. Preliminary injunction motions, filed immediately upon final map adoption, are the primary tool. In Alabama, the question is whether the Supreme Court's stay — granted over Sotomayor's dissent noting the existing intentional discrimination finding — will be lifted on remand or whether the district court's intentional discrimination finding provides independent grounds for enjoining the new maps.

### 6.2 What Advocacy Organizations Can Do Before June 2026

The most immediately actionable interventions:

**Litigation teams**: File for emergency preliminary injunctions against new maps immediately upon adoption in Tennessee, Louisiana, and South Carolina. The Arlington Heights intentional discrimination theory is available; the speed and overtness of the redistricting response to Callais provides strong circumstantial evidence material. Specifically plead Section 3 bail-in as a remedy.

**Congressional Black Caucus and Congressional Hispanic Caucus**: Public documentation of the seat-elimination stakes for the 2026 election. The aggregate analysis — 15+ seats, 74% of Black representatives, 84% of Hispanic representatives at risk — needs a visible champion in the existing congressional delegation. The NOTUS analysis (May 2026) documented that CBC members know the threat is real.

**State voting rights acts**: In states without state VRAs, introduce state-law VRA legislation immediately in the 2026 legislative sessions, with explicit effects-test language. Prioritize Arizona, Georgia, Michigan, Pennsylvania, and Wisconsin — all states with significant minority populations and ongoing redistricting disputes that lack state VRA protections.

**Voter education**: In states where redistricting changes are enacted, rapid voter education about new district boundaries is essential. Voters who believe they are registered in one district and do not know their district has been redrawn may disenfranchise themselves through incorrect polling place assumptions. Organizations with voter education infrastructure — LWV, NAACP, Voto Latino — should prioritize redistricting education in affected states.

**Congressional action**: The John R. ## 7. Reform Pathways

### 7.1 Immediate (2026): Litigation Under the Surviving Standard

The Callais ruling does not eliminate Section 2 litigation; it raises the evidentiary standard. Lawsuits with strong circumstantial evidence of intentional discrimination — particularly against maps adopted within days of the Callais ruling, in response to public calls to eliminate Black districts, in states with documented histories of intentional racial discrimination — have viable legal theories. The Arlington Heights intentional discrimination analysis should be the primary pleading theory. Section 3 bail-in should be included as a prayer for relief.

Strategic litigation organizations: Democracy Docket, NAACP LDF, Voting Rights Alliance, Campaign Legal Center. The NAACPLDF page on Louisiana v. Callais provides the litigation analysis framework.

### 7.2 Medium-Term (2026-2027): State Voting Rights Acts and State Constitutional Defenses

Fourteen states have state VRAs; 36 do not. In Democratic-controlled states and states with Democratic trifecta potential through 2026 elections, state VRA enactment is both politically feasible and legally effective. State VRAs with explicit effects-test language, codifying the pre-Callais standard at the state level, provide protection for state legislative, local, and school board elections that is not subject to the federal Callais standard.

For congressional redistricting (governed by federal law), state constitutional fair districts amendments — modeled on Florida's (even if Florida's has been ineffective due to DeSantis court capture) — provide an additional litigation hook.

### 7.3 Long-Term Structural: Proportional Representation or Independent Redistricting with Statutory Minority Representation Requirements

The majority-minority district model has always been a second-best remedy for minority political exclusion — it concentrates minority voters in specific districts rather than ensuring proportional representation across a state's delegation. The post-Callais environment makes the second-best nature of the remedy visible: the model can be attacked as a racial gerrymander even when it is a court-ordered remedy for documented discrimination.

The structural pathway forward combines three elements: (1) immediate litigation enforcement of surviving Section 2 protections under Arlington Heights intentional discrimination standards; (2) medium-term state-level legislative action to codify effects-test protections at the state level and establish independent redistricting commissions with statutory diversity criteria; and (3) long-term structural reform toward proportional representation or multi-member districts with ranked-choice voting that eliminates the need for geographic concentration-based remedies entirely.

The 2026 election cycle is the decision point. If Callais-triggered redistricting succeeds in eliminating 12-19 Democratic seats before the November midterms, Republican control of redistricting for the next decade (2030-2040) becomes highly likely, locking in structural minority representation losses through the entire 2030s. Conversely, if Democratic litigation and voter turnout prevent the Callais-redistricting cascade before June filing deadlines, a Democratic House majority remains possible, enabling the John R. Lewis Voting Rights Advancement Act to be the first legislative priority of a 2027 Congress. The urgency of this window — measured in weeks, not months — makes the immediate litigation strategy and the May-June advocacy timeline critical to the medium- and long-term reform pathway.

The Fair Representation Act (multi-member districts with ranked choice voting) is the structural proposal that eliminates the majority-minority district trap. Under a five-member district model for a state with five House seats, a racial minority group at 25 percent of the population would win approximately 1-2 of 5 seats without any majority-minority geographic concentration requirement.

Independent redistricting commissions with explicit statutory prohibitions on racial vote dilution outcomes — tested against effects standards, not intent standards — are the intermediate structural reform.

## 9. June 2026 Update: Louisiana Signs New Map — Cascade Advances

*Section added June 3, 2026*

### 9.1 Louisiana Governor Signs Revised Map

On approximately June 1, 2026, Louisiana Governor Jeff Landry signed the new congressional map passed by the Republican-controlled legislature. The map eliminates one of Louisiana's two majority-Black congressional districts — the court-ordered 6th District (the Baton Rouge–New Orleans corridor that was the subject of the original Callais challenge) — and draws an additional Republican-leaning district in its place.

The NAACP Legal Defense Fund issued a statement in response to the legislature's passage of the map, calling it "a flagrant effort to consolidate political power in the hands of the white majority and deny communities across the state — especially Black Louisianians — an equal opportunity to participate in the political process." The LDF described the new map as "a product of the same racial discrimination the VRA was meant to prevent" and stated that litigation is expected.

The Callais plaintiffs — the non-Black voters who challenged Louisiana's court-ordered map as an unconstitutional racial gerrymander — have also expressed concerns about aspects of the new map that was drawn in response to the ruling, creating an unusual dynamic in which both civil rights organizations and the original Callais plaintiffs are questioning the legislature's new configuration. The post-Callais Louisiana situation demonstrates the ruling's fundamental paradox: the state that complied with Allen v. Milligan by creating a second majority-Black district, then had that district struck down as unconstitutional, is now drawing a new map that its own challengers question as insufficiently responsive to the ruling.

### 9.2 The Extended Impact: Hispanic, Native, and Asian-American Districts

The Callais ruling's effect on minority representation extends beyond Black voters and Black-majority districts. The American Democracy Minute documented in May 2026 that the ruling affects Hispanic, Native American, and Asian-American majority-minority districts as well.

Analysis of state legislative district maps shows that majority-minority state legislative districts — where Black or Hispanic voters make up a majority — could fall from 342 to 202 nationwide under Callais's new standard: a loss of 140 districts. MALDEF issued a formal statement on the day of the decision stating that the ruling "eroded the chance for Hispanic, Native, Asian and other majority-minority communities to elect the candidate of their choice."

MALDEF's legal posture post-Callais: the organization continues to monitor redistricting in Texas, California, and other states with significant Hispanic-majority districts and has indicated it will evaluate litigation options under both the surviving Section 2 intentional discrimination standard and under state voting rights acts in states where those acts protect majority-minority districts.

LULAC's position is closely aligned with MALDEF's. In Texas, a coalition including LULAC, MALDEF, the Texas NAACP, and Fair Maps Texas Action Committee has been monitoring the redistricting situation. Texas redistricting completed mid-decade in 2025 (before Callais) used the Callais-anticipating strategy of eliminating majority-minority districts pre-litigation; the post-Callais environment now makes those 2025 maps significantly harder to challenge.

The critical downstream impact: if majority-minority state legislative districts fall from 342 to 202, state legislatures that oversee voter ID law enactment, election administration budgets, state court redistricting review, and state voting rights act passage will themselves become less representative of Black, Hispanic, and Indigenous communities — a compounding democratic exclusion cycle that operates at both the federal and state legislative levels simultaneously.

### 9.3 The State Court Report and Post-Callais Legal Landscape

The State Court Report's analysis "The Aftermath of Callais" (published June 2026) documents the legal landscape across state courts after the ruling. Key findings from that analysis:
- State courts in states with state VRAs (California, New York, Washington) are not bound by the Callais intentional discrimination standard for state legislative redistricting challenges
- States applying their own constitutional anti-discrimination provisions (Florida's Fair Districts amendment, Michigan's Voters Not Politicians initiative) have independent grounds for challenges not foreclosed by Callais
- The Redistricting Watch published by the NAACP LDF Substack is the most current litigation tracker for post-Callais challenges — as of June 2026 they have published through Issue 10.0 tracking ongoing litigation

The Harvard Kennedy School analysis of Callais ("What Louisiana v. Callais Means for the Voting Rights Act") notes that the ruling's most significant long-term consequence may be to create a two-tier system: states with state VRAs and state constitutional anti-discrimination provisions will retain minority representation protections, while states without them — primarily the same Southern states with documented histories of intentional discrimination that the original VRA targeted — will face minimal legal constraints on map-drawing.

Callais — Brennan Center for Justice](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/louisiana-v-callais)

### 9.4 The CRS Legal Analysis

The Congressional Research Service published its legal summary "Congressional Redistricting: High Court Narrows Voting Rights Act in Louisiana v. Callais" (LSB11431, May 2026). Key CRS findings:
- The ruling "significantly narrowed the circumstances under which a racial vote dilution challenge to a redistricting map can be made under Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act"
- The ruling creates a doctrinal tension with the 1982 VRA amendments that the CRS characterizes as requiring further clarification from either SCOTUS or Congress
- The "disentanglement" requirement "may be effectively unworkable in many jurisdictions where race and party identification are highly correlated" — the CRS echoes Kagan's dissent
- Congress retains the authority to enact remedial legislation that would clarify the compelling interest standard for race-conscious redistricting, but no such legislation has been introduced as of CRS publication date

The CRS analysis is the primary resource for Hill staff briefing and is worth distributing to congressional contacts alongside the domain document.
