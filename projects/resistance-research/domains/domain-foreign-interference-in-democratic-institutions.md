## Executive Summary

The United States enters the 2026 midterm election cycle with the most degraded foreign interference defense architecture in the post-Cold War period. The degradation is not incidental — it is structurally coordinated across at least seven federal agencies and represents the simultaneous collapse of the three independent channels through which the government previously detected, attributed, and countered foreign election interference: the intelligence community's foreign malign influence apparatus, the law enforcement infrastructure for counterintelligence prosecution, and the diplomatic and democracy promotion ecosystem that shaped the conditions in which foreign interference operates.

What has been dismantled is not peripheral. The FBI's Foreign Influence Task Force — the primary law enforcement body responsible for detecting and disrupting foreign influence campaigns — was disbanded by Attorney General Pam Bondi in February 2025. The Foreign Malign Influence Center at ODNI — created by statute to coordinate intelligence community election threat assessment — was gutted in August 2025 by Director of National Intelligence Tulsi Gabbard as part of a "ODNI 2.0" restructuring that reduced the office by 40 percent. The State Department's Global Engagement Center — the primary diplomatic instrument for countering foreign state propaganda and disinformation — was closed in April 2025 by Secretary of State Marco Rubio, who described it as having engaged in "censorship." CISA's election security team — the agency that provided technical assistance to state election administrators against foreign cyber intrusion — had its 10-person regional election security adviser team placed on administrative leave and its entire election security program proposed for elimination in the FY27 budget. Task Force KleptoCapture and the Kleptocracy Asset Recovery Initiative — the bodies responsible for asset forfeiture against authoritarian state actors and sanctions enforcement — were disbanded in the same February 2025 Bondi memorandum that closed the Foreign Influence Task Force.

The adversaries have not been similarly restrained. Russia's disinformation budget increased by 54 percent — $458 million in additional annual spending — on state-run media and information operations. The Storm-1516 disinformation network, directly linked to GRU operations, more than doubled its narrative output in the first quarter of 2026 compared to the prior year. Three Iranian Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps cyber actors were indicted for the hack-and-leak operation against the Trump campaign in 2024 — operation that succeeded in penetrating campaign systems even under the previous administration's defense architecture. China's Spamouflage operation expanded from targeted congressional interference to a broader influence campaign including AI-generated content, fake US voter accounts, and coordinated down-ballot interference targeting representatives with Taiwan-critical positions.

The most consequential structural fact of this domain is a simultaneity: the dismantlement of US defense capacity is occurring at precisely the moment adversary operations are intensifying and at precisely the moment the US faces its first major election without the institutional architecture built after 2016. For the first time since 2017, the 2026 Annual Threat Assessment published by the intelligence community did not include any reference to foreign election interference threats — an omission Senator Mark Warner described as demonstrating that "the DNI is not interested in protecting American democracy by combating foreign influence."

The strategic consequences extend beyond US borders. The collapse of the National Endowment for Democracy, the defunding of USAID democracy programs (97 percent of democracy and governance initiatives terminated), the elimination of the State DRL bureau's operational capacity, and the withdrawal from international democracy support structures have created a vacuum that China, Russia, and their aligned authoritarian partners are systematically filling. The International Republican Institute halted 92 of 95 programs. The National Democratic Institute terminated 93 of 97 awards. Programs supporting independent media in Ukraine, anti-corruption infrastructure in Eastern Europe, democratic institution-building in Asia, and civil society capacity across Latin America have been cut with no notice and no transition funding.

**Key structural vulnerabilities:**
1. **Counterintelligence capacity collapse**: Every federal body specifically responsible for detecting and disrupting foreign election interference has been disbanded, restructured, or defunded in a coordinated 90-day action window between January and April 2025.
2. **Attribution and prosecution vacuum**: FARA criminal enforcement has been explicitly deprioritized; the Foreign Influence Task Force has been disbanded; and the Kleptocracy Initiative — the mechanism for imposing financial costs on authoritarian state actors — has been eliminated.
3. **Democracy promotion vacuum**: The ecosystem supporting democratic governance globally — NED, USAID democracy programs, State DRL, the IRI, the NDI — has been dismantled at a cost of more than $80 billion in terminated programs, creating conditions in which authoritarian influence fills the space.
4. **Dark money structural vulnerability**: Citizens United's corporate spending loophole, combined with inadequate shell-company disclosure rules, creates a legal pathway for foreign state-linked capital to enter US political campaigns in volumes that cannot be detected or prosecuted under current law.
5. **Threat assessment suppression**: The 2026 Annual Threat Assessment's omission of foreign election interference — the first such omission since 2017 — represents not a finding that threats have diminished but a political decision not to publicly document them.

**Central thesis**: The foreign interference dimension of US democratic erosion is not primarily about the sophistication of adversary operations, though those have grown substantially. It is primarily about the systematic elimination of the institutional architecture that made US democratic processes resilient to foreign interference. A democracy that has dismantled its own defenses cannot be protected by the defenses it has dismantled. Rebuilding requires not merely restoring former institutions but designing structural defenses that are insulated from the political decision-making of whichever administration controls the executive branch.

## 1. Documented Mechanisms: Foreign Information Operations Targeting US Democratic Infrastructure Since 2020

### 1.1 Russia: The Full-Spectrum Campaign

Russia's interference in US democratic processes is the longest-documented, most thoroughly investigated, and most operationally sophisticated of the three principal adversary programs. The Senate Intelligence Committee's five-volume bipartisan report — the most comprehensive legislative investigation into foreign interference in American history, totaling more than three years of work, 200 witness interviews, and review of more than a million pages of documents — established that Russia's 2016 campaign was not an improvised opportunistic intervention but a systematic, multiyear operation with distinct intelligence, military, and commercial components.

Volume 5, released August 18, 2020, documented that the "breathtaking level of contacts between Trump officials and Russian government operatives" created "a very real counterintelligence threat" to US elections. The report described campaign chairman Paul Manafort as "a grave counterintelligence threat" whose sharing of internal polling data with Konstantin Kilimnik — assessed by the intelligence community as a Russian intelligence officer — was connected to the theft of Clinton campaign emails. These findings were bipartisan: the committee operated under the chairmanship of Richard Burr (R-NC) and the vice chairmanship of Mark Warner (D-VA), and the report was unanimous.

The 2020 and 2024 cycles documented an evolution in Russian tactics from 2016's relatively primitive troll farm and social media operations to AI-enabled, multi-platform, commercially sophisticated influence campaigns:

**2020 operations.** The January 2017 Intelligence Community Assessment — declassified and released in March 2021 — found that Russian President Putin "authorized influence operations aimed at denigrating President Biden's candidacy and the Democratic Party, supporting former President Trump, undermining public confidence in the electoral process, and exacerbating sociopolitical divisions in the United States." The assessment was the product of the CIA, the NSA, and the FBI — a rare three-agency unanimous assessment. Russia's campaign deployed Kremlin-linked networks across social media platforms, used GRU-linked proxy operations to amplify content, and continued the Infrastructure-targeting cyber operations first documented in 2016.

**2024 operations — the Tenet Media operation.** The most operationally significant Russian influence operation exposed in the 2024 cycle was not a troll farm or a government-sponsored platform. It was the covert acquisition of an American right-wing media company. Tenet Media — a Washington-based outlet featuring six prominent right-wing influencers including Tim Pool, Benny Johnson, Dave Rubin, and Lauren Southern — received $9.7 million in wire transfers from shell companies controlled by Russia's RT (formerly Russia Today), representing approximately 90 percent of the company's bank deposits. The Department of Justice unsealed the indictment in September 2024 charging Kostiantyn Kalashnikov and Elena Afanasyeva, both Moscow-based RT executives, with conspiracy to launder money and violate FARA. The operation's nearly 2,000 YouTube videos accumulated more than 16 million views. The mechanism of deception — a fictional wealthy European sponsor named "Eduard Grigoriann" — was designed precisely to evade FARA's disclosure requirements. The influencers were, according to the DOJ, unaware of the Russian origin of their funding.

**2024 operations — AI-enabled deepfakes.** The GRU provided financial and logistical support to the Moscow-based Center for Geopolitical Expertise (CGE), founded by Aleksandr Dugin and directed by Valery Korovin. The CGE, sanctioned by the US Treasury on December 31, 2024, used GRU-funded generative AI infrastructure to create and distribute disinformation at industrial scale: a network of at least 100 websites designed to imitate legitimate news outlets, creating false corroboration between fabricated stories. Specific documented fabrications included fake viral videos of mail-in ballots for Trump being burned in Pennsylvania and fake videos of Haitian immigrants claiming to vote multiple times for Harris in Georgia. Both videos were created before Election Day to suppress turnout in targeted communities.

**2024 operations — bomb threats.** On Election Day 2024, multiple polling locations in swing-state counties in Georgia, Michigan, and Arizona received bomb threats. The FBI confirmed that many of these threats originated from Russian email domains — a direct physical intimidation operation against in-person voters, not merely a cognitive influence campaign.

**2024 operations — John Mark Dougan.** John Mark Dougan, a former Palm Beach County deputy sheriff who fled to Russia in 2016 to avoid criminal charges, was paid by the GRU to produce and distribute disinformation targeting the Harris campaign. Dougan's operation was documented by the Washington Post in October 2024 — a US-based asset creating disinformation for Russian military intelligence.

**2026 — Storm-1516 escalation.** Bloomberg News identified more than 190 fabricated stories attributable to Storm-1516 — the Russian disinformation network that grew from the Internet Research Agency — since August 2023, with hundreds of millions of social media views. In the first quarter of 2026, the number of Storm-1516 narratives more than doubled compared to the prior year, with near-daily output documented in late March and early April. The operation targets US elections, European democratic processes simultaneously (Storm-1516 was identified targeting Hungary's April 2026 parliamentary elections), and Ukraine — a three-front campaign executed by a single network. Russia's state budget for state media and information operations increased by 54 percent — an additional $458 million annually — between 2024 and 2026.

### 1.2 Iran: Hybrid Cyber-Influence Operations

Iran's interference model differs from Russia's in tactical orientation: where Russia seeks persistent influence aligned with specific political outcomes, Iran operates primarily as a "chaos agent" — maximizing societal division and undermining confidence in US institutions regardless of electoral outcome. The 2024 cycle documents Iran's most operationally aggressive interference since the 2020 cycle.

**The hack-and-leak operation.** The DOJ unsealed an indictment in September 2024 charging three Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps cyber actors — Masoud Jalili, Seyyed Ali Aghamiri, and Yaser Balaghi — with a conspiracy to conduct a hack-and-leak operation designed to influence the 2024 presidential election. The operation successfully penetrated the Trump campaign's internal communications, stealing sensitive internal documents that were then offered to the Biden-Harris campaign (which did not engage with the material) and shared with at least three major news organizations including Politico, the New York Times, and the Washington Post. The FBI, ODNI, and CISA issued a joint statement in August 2024 concluding that Iran was responsible for both the Trump campaign breach and an attempted breach of the Biden-Harris campaign. The hack-and-leak model — obtaining politically damaging material through cyber intrusion and then creating conditions for its publication — is precisely the model GRU Unit 26165 (Fancy Bear) deployed against the Clinton campaign in 2016.

**Voter intimidation operations.** In 2020, Iran posed as the far-right Proud Boys group to send threatening emails to Democratic-registered voters, warning them to vote for Trump or face consequences. This was a false-flag operation: Iran wanted the psychological effect of voter intimidation while attributing it to a domestic extremist organization. The ODNI's 2020 post-election assessment found that Iran obtained US voter registration information from state databases to support these operations.

**2024 AI deepfake operations.** US intelligence spotted Chinese and Iranian deepfakes in the closing weeks of the 2020 election; by 2024, both programs had substantially upgraded their AI capabilities. ODNI documented that Iranian actors deployed generative AI to create candidate-specific disinformation content during the 2024 cycle, using tools more sophisticated than the text-based influence operations of 2020.

### 1.3 China: Selective Down-Ballot Targeting

China's 2024 interference model was assessed by the intelligence community as "more cautious" than Russia's or Iran's — characterized by targeted rather than broad influence operations, focused on protecting core Chinese Communist Party interests rather than destabilizing US democracy wholesale. The caution reflects a strategic calculation: China has substantial economic interdependence with the United States and cannot afford the same risk tolerance that Russia, facing comprehensive sanctions, can absorb.

**Spamouflage and congressional targeting.** China's Spamouflage operation — a large-scale social media influence network controlled by the Ministry of Public Security — deployed fake US voter accounts across Twitter/X, Facebook, and YouTube to spread divisive content ahead of the 2024 election. A TikTok account posing as a conservative American news outlet and social media influencer posted content mocking Biden that accumulated 1.5 million views. Intelligence assessments confirmed that Spamouflage focused on US representatives who had taken positions critical of China's Taiwan policy and on amplifying "domestic divisions" as a general strategic objective.

**Down-ballot congressional interference.** The October 2024 Washington Post investigation documented increasing Chinese government attempts to influence "tens" of down-ballot races, using explicitly antisemitic attacks and conspiracy theories against candidates who had taken China-critical positions. This is a notable tactical evolution: China moved from broadly targeted societal division campaigns to precision targeting of specific legislators based on their policy positions on Taiwan, technology competition, and Xinjiang. Alabama Representative Barry Moore and then-Senator Marco Rubio (who is now Secretary of State) were both identified as Chinese influence operation targets.

**Platform penetration.** The US intelligence community's assessment that TikTok's Chinese government-affiliated ownership created a structural pathway for Chinese influence operations was the basis for the legislation requiring ByteDance divestiture or a US ban, eventually upheld by the Supreme Court. China's "whole-of-government" approach to influence operations — coordinating state media, cyber operations, commercial technology, and front organizations — reflects a long-term strategy of shaping the information environment in which US voters make political decisions, not merely intervening in specific election cycles.

### 1.4 The Dark Money Structural Vulnerability

Foreign state interference in US electoral processes is not limited to overt influence operations. The structural vulnerability created by Citizens United v. Federal Election Commission (2010) and its progeny creates a legal pathway for foreign-state-linked capital to enter US political campaigns through corporate spending that cannot be effectively detected or prosecuted under current law.

The legal architecture is as follows: federal law prohibits individual foreign nationals from spending in American elections. But it does not prohibit political spending by American-registered corporations that are owned, controlled, or influenced by foreign interests. Citizens United eliminated limits on independent corporate expenditures, creating a channel through which foreign capital entering through American corporate structures can be deployed in US elections with no registration, disclosure, or prosecutorial exposure. Shell companies — LLCs with opaque names, post-office-box addresses, and no disclosure of ultimate beneficial ownership — are the primary mechanism.

The Brennan Center documented that dark money spending by groups that do not disclose their donors reached an all-time high of $1.9 billion in the 2024 election. OpenSecrets identified that the American Petroleum Institute — partially financed by the US subsidiary of Saudi Aramco, the state-owned Saudi oil company — was among the funders behind attack ads in the 2010 midterms. The principle established by these examples: state-owned enterprises of authoritarian governments can funnel capital through US subsidiaries into US political campaigns without violating the nominal prohibition on foreign electoral spending. The Whitehouse-Raskin legislation — the DISCLOSE Act's most recent iteration — would close this loophole by requiring disclosure of all political spending by corporations with foreign beneficial ownership, but it has not advanced under the current Congress.

## 2. The Systematic Dismantlement of US Counterinterference Capacity

### 2.1 The February 2025 Bondi Memorandum: Dismantling Three Bodies Simultaneously

The destruction of federal counterintelligence capacity against democratic threats was concentrated in a single Attorney General memorandum issued in February 2025. That memo, signed by Pam Bondi within weeks of her confirmation, accomplished three simultaneous dismantlements:

**Foreign Influence Task Force (FITF) disbanded.** The FBI's Foreign Influence Task Force — established in 2017 by then-Director Christopher Wray directly in response to Russia's 2016 election interference campaign, staffed by approximately 50 FBI agents and support personnel organized into three squads — was ordered disbanded immediately. The memo stated the task force would be disbanded "to free resources to address more pressing priorities, and end risks of further weaponization and abuses of prosecutorial discretion." The FITF had been the government's primary law enforcement body for detecting and disrupting active foreign influence campaigns. Its disbanding left the three squads of agents looking for other assignments, with no body formally designated as the FITF's successor.

**FARA criminal enforcement deprioritized.** The same Bondi memorandum directed that DOJ would only refer criminal charges under the Foreign Agents Registration Act if they "alleged conduct similar to more traditional espionage by foreign government actors." This effectively eliminated FARA as a tool against the kind of covert influence operations documented in the Tenet Media case — where the foreign government operates through domestic intermediaries and commercial structures rather than through agents who could be characterized as "traditional espionage" actors. The Tenet Media operation itself — $9.7 million from Russian state media to American influencers through shell companies — would not meet the new Bondi enforcement standard.

**Task Force KleptoCapture and Kleptocracy Asset Recovery Initiative closed.** The bodies responsible for asset forfeiture against Russian oligarchs, sanctions enforcement, and kleptocracy prosecution were closed in the same memorandum, with personnel reassigned to address drug cartels and transnational criminal organizations. Task Force KleptoCapture had been established in 2022 to enforce Russian sanctions following the invasion of Ukraine. The Kleptocracy Asset Recovery Initiative, created in 2010, had pursued Russian oligarchs, recovered over $1 billion in misappropriated 1MDB assets, and represented the most consequential financial pressure available to the US government against the kleptocratic networks that fund authoritarian state interference operations. Lawfare's analysis concluded that the initiative's elimination "significantly diminishes the US's ability to counter the transnational corruption that continues to threaten core US security and economic interests."

### 2.2 CISA: The Election Security Infrastructure Collapse

The Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency had become, between 2018 and 2024, the federal government's primary technical assistance body for state election administrators defending against foreign cyber intrusion. CISA's election security program trained state and local election officials, provided vulnerability assessments of voting infrastructure, and operated the joint election security assessment teams that were the operational backbone of the government's 2020 and 2024 election security posture.

The dismantlement began in January 2025 with administrative leaves that placed the 10-person team of regional election security advisers on administrative leave — immediately eliminating the operational field presence that provided state-level assistance. More than 130 CISA employees were terminated as part of mass probationary-status firings. CISA "paused all election security activities pending a review of their funding, activities and personnel." The FY27 budget proposed by the administration would eliminate the election security program in its entirety.

The dismissal of Chris Krebs — the CISA director who publicly confirmed that the 2020 election was "the most secure in US history" — following the 2020 election had already established that the administration viewed CISA's election security function as a political liability. In 2025, Trump ordered an investigation into Krebs and the security firm he had joined, Signal of apparent retribution for his 2020 assessment.

The Votebeat investigation published in January 2026 reported that election officials across the country described their trust in CISA on election security as "broken" — not merely diminished. Officials who had developed working relationships with CISA's regional advisers described the loss as irreplaceable in the six-month window before the 2026 midterms.

### 2.3 The ODNI Restructuring: Gutting the Foreign Malign Influence Center

The Foreign Malign Influence Center was created by statute in 2019 — after the 2017 Intelligence Reform and Terrorism Prevention Act amendments — specifically to coordinate intelligence community election threat assessment across the entire intelligence community. The FMIC housed the Election Threats Executive, which coordinated all election security activities, initiatives, and programs across the intelligence community.

On August 20, 2025, DNI Gabbard announced that the FMIC would be dissolved as part of the "ODNI 2.0" restructuring, claiming it was "redundant" with the National Intelligence Council and the National Counterintelligence and Security Center. The ODNI's own fact sheet described the plan as "integrating core functions and expertise from those offices into ODNI's Mission Integration and the National Intelligence Council" — a formulation that national security lawyers noted provided no operational specificity about what functions would actually be performed, by whom, with what resources.

The critical legal dimension: the statute that authorized the FMIC in 2019 explicitly prohibited closing it before 2028. Just Security's analysis concluded that ODNI's decision to "cripple" the center short of formal closure — by eliminating its budget, reassigning its staff, and stripping its mission — effectively ended any meaningful government role in addressing foreign malign influence while appearing to maintain technical statutory compliance. The Election Threats Executive's loss of independent operational capacity means there is no single coordinating body within the intelligence community specifically responsible for synthesizing and disseminating election security intelligence.

The 2026 consequence was precisely what the FMIC was designed to prevent: the intelligence community's Annual Threat Assessment, released in March 2026, did not include any reference to foreign election interference. This was, in Senator Warner's words, "the first time since 2017" that the annual assessment omitted foreign election threats. When Warner asked Gabbard directly during her March 2026 SSCI testimony: "Are you saying there is no foreign threat to our elections in the midterms this year?" — she responded with a non-answer about the intelligence community "remaining focused on any collection and intelligence that show a potential foreign threat" without acknowledging documented adversary operations.

### 2.4 The Global Engagement Center: Eliminating Diplomatic Counter-Disinformation

The State Department's Global Engagement Center was established in 2016 to lead US government efforts to "recognize, understand, expose, and counter foreign state and non-state propaganda and disinformation efforts aimed at undermining or influencing the policies, security, or stability of the United States, its allies, and partner nations." By 2024, it had become a sophisticated operational body with partnerships across allied governments, relationships with platform companies, and documented counterintelligence on Russian, Chinese, and Iranian disinformation networks.

The GEC's legislative authority terminated December 23, 2024. Secretary Rubio announced its permanent closure in April 2025, characterizing it as having "wasted taxpayer money" and "engaged in censorship." The "censorship" characterization referred to the GEC's work with the Election Integrity Partnership, which documented domestic disinformation alongside foreign operations — work that became politically contested in the context of the Twitter Files controversy. Rubio's closure memo did not distinguish between the GEC's foreign disinformation mandate (uncontroversially within its statutory scope) and any domestic-facing activities (the basis for political controversy).

The consequences were immediate: Defense One reported in April 2025 that the shuttering "leaves US largely defenseless against foreign influence warfare." The capabilities that were lost included real-time monitoring of adversary propaganda operations across 40+ foreign information environments, established attribution partnerships with allied intelligence services, and the institutional memory of a decade of foreign disinformation network mapping that cannot be rebuilt without years of patient intelligence work.

### 2.5 The FARA Enforcement Collapse

The Foreign Agents Registration Act — enacted in 1938 in response to Nazi propaganda operations in the United States — is the primary statutory disclosure tool for identifying agents of foreign governments operating in the US political system. Its basic requirement is simple: anyone acting at the direction of a foreign government to influence US political opinion or policy must register with DOJ, publicly disclose their activities and funding, and label their materials.

The act's chronic weakness is enforcement. FARA's registration and disclosure requirements have been systematically avoided through narrow interpretive guidance, aggressive use of the Lobbying Disclosure Act as an alternative registration mechanism (which provides far less transparency), and DOJ's historical reluctance to bring criminal charges except in the clearest cases. The 2019 bipartisan Senate Intelligence Committee interim report identified FARA enforcement gaps as a "significant vulnerability" to foreign influence.

The 2024 cycle briefly reversed this pattern: 2024 was notable for FARA enforcement with high-profile prosecutions, an all-time high in registrations, a record number of inspections, and a key DC Circuit decision. The Tenet Media indictment represented exactly the kind of enforcement that FARA was designed to enable.

The Bondi memorandum eliminated this trajectory. By restricting FARA criminal prosecution to conduct "similar to more traditional espionage" — a standard that excludes the commercial and covert-influence models that characterize modern foreign interference — the DOJ effectively suspended FARA as a functional enforcement tool against state-sponsored influence operations that operate through domestic commercial intermediaries. The practical effect is that the operation documented in the Tenet Media case — $10 million from a Russian state media outlet to American influencers through a shell company — cannot be criminally prosecuted under current DOJ enforcement guidelines.

## 3. The Strategic Collapse of US Democracy Promotion and the Authoritarian Vacuum

### 3.1 The Architecture of US Democracy Promotion — and Its Destruction

The United States built, over fifty years, a distinctive ecosystem of democracy promotion that operated at three distinct levels: governmental (USAID, State DRL), quasi-governmental (National Endowment for Democracy and its four core institutes — NED, IRI, NDI, CIPE, and the Solidarity Center), and civil society (hundreds of NGOs funded through NED and USAID grants). This ecosystem, whatever its legitimate critics argued about its limitations and political biases, served a structural function in international democratic resilience: it provided funding, technical expertise, and political support for civil society organizations, independent media, election administration capacity, anti-corruption institutions, and democratic governance practices in countries transitioning toward or maintaining democratic governance.

The destruction of this ecosystem in 2025 was unprecedented in speed and scope:

**USAID.** The largest single US organization promoting democracy overseas was effectively dismantled by executive action. The administration announced plans to merge USAID into the State Department with 83 percent of its programs cancelled. By March 2025, approximately 86 percent of USAID awards and nearly half of State Department grants worldwide were terminated — a total of more than $80 billion in cuts, with roughly 97 percent of democracy and governance initiatives cancelled. USAID employed approximately 10,000 staff and supported partners in more than 100 countries. The speed of termination — grants cancelled without notice, partners left with outstanding expenses, multi-year programs ended mid-implementation — destroyed not merely the programs but the trust relationships that made the programs function.

**National Endowment for Democracy.** NED, created during the Reagan administration with historically bipartisan congressional support, receives almost all of its funding from an annual congressional appropriation. In January 2025, DOGE blocked disbursement of NED's congressionally appropriated funds by halting Treasury Department transfers. NED filed a federal lawsuit in March 2025 alleging unlawful impoundment of funds. A partial restoration in March 2025 allowed NED to begin stabilizing operations; an August 2025 injunction allowed access to the remaining $95 million of its previously appropriated funds. But the administration's FY2026 budget proposes to discontinue NED's funding entirely — ending an organization that, for four decades, operated as a rare expression of bipartisan consensus about American democratic values in the world.

**IRI and NDI.** The International Republican Institute — the NED core institute with Republican Party institutional ties — halted 92 of 95 programs, closed all 64 of its offices abroad, and fired up to 85 percent of its staff. The National Democratic Institute — the NED core institute with Democratic Party institutional ties — terminated 93 of its 97 awards, closed three-fourths of its offices, and fired approximately 1,000 people. These were not efficiency adjustments. They were the institutional liquidations of organizations that had survived seven previous presidential administrations, including three previous Republican administrations, because the counterterrorism and national security argument for democracy promotion — autocracies generate the instability and grievance that feed terrorism — was understood across party lines.

**State DRL.** The Bureau of Democracy, Human Rights, and Labor underwent a restructuring that reduced it to approximately 20 percent of its previous staffing. The State Department's Democracy Fund — which administered an estimated $345.2 million appropriated for FY2024 and FY2025 for programs supporting good governance, elections, freedom of expression, human rights, and labor rights — was eliminated in the FY2026 proposal. The Under Secretary for Civilian Security, Human Rights, and Democracy position was eliminated; DRL was placed under a newly created Under Secretary for Foreign Assistance and Humanitarian Affairs. The FY2026 request creates an "America First Opportunity Fund" of $2.9 billion to replace over $8.3 billion in eliminated accounts — a 65 percent reduction in total foreign assistance.

### 3.2 The Authoritarian Vacuum

The speed and scope of US democracy promotion withdrawal has created what multiple analysts describe as a structural "vacuum" — a collapse in external support for democratic institutions, civil society organizations, independent media, and election integrity infrastructure at precisely the moment authoritarian powers are increasing their investment in the opposite. The evidence is documented across every region:

**Asia.** The Institute for Global Governance Research at Hitotsubashi University documented that organizations working on democratic resilience across Asia face severe financial and operational disruption, with activities aimed at promoting human rights in China and North Korea, supporting independent media and anti-corruption in Southeast Asia, and promoting democratic governance in the Pacific islands region all effectively halted. China's Belt and Road Initiative and its parallel "governance partnership" programs — which provide infrastructure funding paired with governance advisory arrangements — are filling the space vacated by USAID and NED-funded programs.

**Eastern Europe.** Programs supporting independent media in Ukraine, anti-corruption infrastructure in Georgia and Moldova, and civil society capacity in Belarus and Russia have been cut — at the moment when Russia's ongoing invasion of Ukraine makes those programs strategically consequential. The Carnegie Endowment's March 2025 analysis concluded that the administration "took sweeping action against the funding and institutional architecture built over fifty years to support democracy globally."

**Latin America.** Programs supporting democratic institutions in Venezuela, Cuba, Nicaragua, and Haiti — countries already under authoritarian pressure — have been terminated without transition funding or program continuation arrangements. The practical result is that civil society organizations that depended on US funding for operational survival have collapsed, their staff dispersed and their institutional knowledge lost.

**Freedom House assessment.** The Freedom House *Freedom in the World 2026* report — "The Growing Shadow of Autocracy" — documented the 20th consecutive year of decline in global freedom, with 54 countries experiencing deterioration in political rights and civil liberties while only 35 showed improvements. The report noted that "as democracies move further away from their traditional role as defenders of freedom, the world could face a dangerous future led by emboldened autocrats." Freedom House's 2025 internet freedom report documented a 15th consecutive year of decline in global internet freedom.

The connection between US democracy promotion withdrawal and authoritarian vacuum-filling is not speculative. It is documented: where US-funded civil society organizations collapse, authoritarian-aligned alternatives fill the institutional space. The mechanism is not ideological but structural — democratic governance requires funded institutions; when funding disappears, institutions collapse; authoritarian powers offer alternative governance partnerships on terms that consolidate rather than distribute power.

## 4. Comparative International Evidence: How Democracies Build Structural Defenses

### 4.1 Australia: The Foreign Influence Transparency Scheme

Australia's Foreign Influence Transparency Scheme Act (FITS Act), enacted June 29, 2018, represents the most structurally sophisticated legislative response to foreign state influence operations among democracies comparable to the United States. Prime Minister Malcolm Turnbull explicitly described the Act as "an improved version" of the American FARA statute — recognizing the same problem FARA was designed to address while correcting FARA's structural weaknesses.

The FITS Act requires registration for any person or organization that undertakes activities "on behalf of a foreign principal" — defined to include foreign governments, foreign political parties, and related entities or individuals — that are intended to influence Australian political or governmental processes. The coverage is broader than FARA: it covers political influence activities beyond traditional "agent" relationships and explicitly applies to senior former public officeholders who engage in lobbying activities on behalf of foreign principals, regardless of whether their current activities would otherwise meet the "agent" threshold.

The FITS Act creates a public register — fully searchable and publicly accessible — listing all registered entities, their foreign principals, the nature of their activities, and the compensation arrangements. This is substantially more transparent than FARA's registration database, which has historically been difficult to use and slow to reflect new registrations.

The June 2024 parliamentary committee statutory review recommended, and the government accepted, substantial reforms including tighter beneficial ownership disclosure, enhanced penalties for non-registration, and expanded coverage of activities that seek to influence public opinion (not merely government officials). The 2024 review underscores a key structural principle: foreign influence laws require periodic legislative updating as adversary tactics evolve.

### 4.2 The European Union: The Democracy Shield and Digital Services Act

The European Union launched its "Democracy Shield" initiative in November 2025 — a structural framework for defending democratic processes against foreign interference and disinformation across all 27 member states. The Democracy Shield established the European Centre for Democratic Resilience as an independent monitoring and coordination body, created a Civil Society Platform for dialogue on civic space, and specified that each member state must maintain dedicated institutional capacity for detecting and responding to foreign interference.

The Democracy Shield operates alongside the Digital Services Act's foreign interference provisions. Under DSA Article 40, Very Large Online Platforms — those with 45 million or more EU users — are required to provide vetted researchers access to non-public data for the study of systemic risks, including foreign information manipulation and interference. DSA Article 17 requires platforms to provide reasoning for content moderation decisions. The December 2025 first DSA enforcement action — a €120 million fine against X for breaches of deceptive design prohibition, ad transparency, and researcher data access — demonstrated that the enforcement mechanism is operational.

The EU's model is structurally distinct from US approaches in a key respect: it imposes obligations on platforms rather than (or in addition to) obligations on foreign agents, creating a supply-side rather than demand-side intervention in the information manipulation ecosystem. Platform transparency requirements, researcher data access rights, and algorithmic auditing mandates create institutional accountability for the distribution of foreign disinformation even when attribution of that disinformation to a specific foreign state actor is incomplete.

### 4.3 The Nordic-Baltic Model: Whole-of-Society Resilience

The Nordic-Baltic region — Finland, Sweden, Norway, Denmark, Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania, and Iceland — has developed the most comprehensively documented "whole-of-society" approach to democratic resilience against foreign interference in the world. The model is not primarily a law enforcement or intelligence response; it is a civil society, media literacy, and institutional resilience approach that makes democratic populations less susceptible to information manipulation rather than simply trying to detect and remove manipulative content.

**Finland's media literacy model.** Finland consistently ranks at the top of European media literacy indexes. Finnish media literacy education begins in daycare and continues through secondary school: students study propaganda campaigns, learn advertising techniques, examine how statistics mislead, learn to distinguish disinformation from misinformation, and create their own media to develop both technical skills and critical understanding. A study across 24 countries found that Finnish and Swedish participants were the most effective at recognizing misinformation. Finland's approach treats media literacy as a national security investment, not merely an education policy — reflecting an understanding that the most sustainable defense against information operations is a population that cannot be easily manipulated by them.

**Latvia's three-pillar model.** Latvia, which shares a border with Russia and has a substantial Russian-speaking minority that has historically been a target of Russian information operations, has developed a three-pillar approach to information security: strategic communication (the government's own coherent, accurate, and responsive communication with citizens), the media environment (support for independent, quality domestic media that provides an alternative to Russian state-controlled alternatives), and media literacy (as Finland, a population-level educational investment). Latvia's model reflects a structural insight: governments that provide accurate information to their citizens and maintain a healthy independent media ecosystem are more resilient to foreign disinformation than governments that rely primarily on content removal or counter-propaganda.

**Denmark's Task Force Interference.** Denmark created a formal dedicated institutional body — Task Force Interference — responsible for detecting, attributing, and coordinating government responses to foreign interference operations. The model combines intelligence assessment, law enforcement authority, diplomatic response, and public communication in a single coordinating structure — addressing the inter-agency fragmentation that allowed Russia's 2016 US campaign to operate for months before any coordinated government response was assembled.

**Sweden's resilience research.** A 2025 study in the Journal of Disinformation Research found that Swedish participants were among the most effective globally at recognizing misinformation — a result attributed to Sweden's combination of media literacy education, strong independent media, and public institutional trust. Sweden's approach to countering foreign information operations combines the institutional tools of SÄPO (the Security Service) for detection and attribution with a national resilience strategy for countering cognitive-space threats.

### 4.4 Germany: Digital Sovereignty and Platform Accountability

Germany's approach to foreign interference combines robust law enforcement authority (under the Verfassungsschutz, Germany's domestic intelligence agency, and the BfV for counterintelligence), legal restrictions on foreign political donations and influence activities, and a strong institutional tradition of parliamentary oversight of intelligence operations that prevents the kind of capacity elimination documented in the US.

Germany's Network Enforcement Act (NetzDG), enacted 2017 and substantially amended, requires social media platforms to remove clearly illegal content within 24 to 48 hours and to publish transparency reports on content removal decisions. While NetzDG has been criticized for over-removal pressures, its core structural contribution — requiring platforms to maintain transparent compliance mechanisms — has influenced the broader EU regulatory approach codified in the DSA. Germany also maintains an explicit constitutional protection for "militant democracy" (streitbare Demokratie) — the principle that democratic processes can defend themselves against forces that seek to subvert democracy using democratic means, including restrictions on parties and organizations that seek to undermine the constitutional order.

## 5. Reform Pathways: Statutory Architecture for Democratic Defense

### 5.1 FARA Enforcement and Transparency Act

**Problem addressed.** The current FARA enforcement model has two structural failures: chronic under-enforcement due to resource constraints and prosecutorial reluctance, and systematic avoidance of registration through the Lobbying Disclosure Act alternative registration mechanism that provides far less transparency. The Bondi memo's deprioritization of criminal enforcement makes both failures structural rather than addressable through prosecutorial guidance.

**Core statutory provisions:**

*Section 1: Mandatory civil enforcement.* Create a mandatory civil penalty structure for FARA non-registration that does not require criminal prosecution: automatic civil fines of $10,000 per month for unregistered agents, $50,000 per month for agents who have received prior registration demands, and mandatory referral to DOJ civil enforcement within 30 days of documented non-registration. Civil enforcement separates FARA compliance from the politics of criminal prosecution — it can proceed regardless of who holds the AG position.

*Section 2: LDA loophole closure.* Eliminate the Lobbying Disclosure Act as a substitute for FARA registration when the client is a foreign government, foreign political party, or state-owned enterprise. An agent of a foreign government must register under FARA; using LDA as an alternative cannot be treated as compliance. This closes the primary structural avoidance mechanism documented by the Senate Intelligence Committee.

*Section 3: Beneficial ownership disclosure.* Require FARA registrants to disclose the ultimate beneficial ownership of any entity through which they receive compensation from a foreign principal, piercing the shell company structures used in the Tenet Media operation. Require financial institutions to flag wire transfers from known foreign state media outlets to domestic media companies for FARA compliance review.

*Section 4: Independent FARA enforcement unit.* Create an independent FARA Enforcement Unit within DOJ, led by a Senate-confirmed director serving a fixed 7-year term, insulated from removal by the AG except for cause. The unit's enforcement budget is mandatory-appropriated at a statutory floor. This structural insulation is essential: FARA enforcement must be able to continue regardless of which administration controls the DOJ.

*Section 5: Public registry modernization.* Require real-time posting of FARA registrations and disclosures to a fully searchable online database, with standardized data formats that enable systematic analysis of foreign agent activity patterns. The current FARA database is inaccessible to meaningful systematic analysis because of inconsistent data formats and slow processing.

**Legislative precedent.** The Grassley-Peters Foreign Agents Transparency Act (introduced 2025) would allow DOJ to require retroactive FARA registration — closing the gap that allows former agents to avoid disclosure after the principal relationship ends. The Senate advances bills (January 2026) on the Disclosing Foreign Influence in Lobbying Act and the Lobbying Disclosure Improvement Act represent the existing legislative foundation on which more comprehensive reform can build.

### 5.2 Democratic Defense Infrastructure Act

**Problem addressed.** The FITF disbanding, FMIC gutting, GEC closure, and CISA election security elimination were accomplished by executive action alone — through memoranda, reorganization orders, and budget proposals — because no statute required their existence, defined their minimum functions, or constrained their elimination. The structural lesson is that critical counterintelligence and election security infrastructure cannot be treated as discretionary executive branch programs; it must be statutorilymandated with operational floors, independent governance, and congressional reporting requirements that survive changes in executive leadership.

**Core statutory provisions:**

*Section 1: Foreign Interference Counterintelligence Center.* Create a statutory Foreign Interference Counterintelligence Center within the FBI, headed by a Senate-confirmed director serving a 5-year fixed term, with authority to coordinate foreign influence investigation across all FBI field offices. The FICC replaces and supersedes the FITF as a permanent statutory body that cannot be disbanded by AG memorandum. The director reports both to the AG and to the congressional intelligence committees. The FICC's minimum annual appropriation is set at $200 million, mandatory-appropriated.

*Section 2: Election Threats Executive restoration.* Restore the Election Threats Executive as a permanent position within ODNI, with explicit statutory mandate to coordinate intelligence community election security assessments, produce and publish unclassified election security threat assessments before each federal election cycle, and brief state election administrators and the Election Assistance Commission on relevant threat intelligence. The position cannot be eliminated without 90-day advance congressional notification and a committee vote.

*Section 3: Election Security Infrastructure Protection.* Prohibit the elimination or substantial reduction (defined as reduction greater than 15 percent of staff or budget) of CISA's election security program without a joint resolution of Congress. Require CISA to maintain a minimum of 12 regional election security advisers, each assigned to a geographic region. Restore the election security information sharing and analysis center (EI-ISAC) functions that were eliminated in 2025 with a statutory minimum operational budget.

*Section 4: Counter-Disinformation Coordination.* Recreate the State Department's counter-foreign-disinformation function as a permanent statutory office — the Office of Foreign Information Integrity — with a Senate-confirmed director, a minimum 60-person staff, and an explicitly limited mandate restricted to foreign state propaganda and information operations (excluding any domestic content moderation functions, addressing the political controversy that contributed to the GEC's closure). Require the office to produce a publicly available annual report on foreign disinformation operations targeting US democratic processes.

*Section 5: Congressional reporting and oversight.* Require the Director of National Intelligence, the FBI Director, and the CISA Director to submit unclassified election security threat assessments to Congress and make them publicly available no less than 12 months and 6 months before each federal general election. These reports must specifically address foreign election interference threats or affirmatively certify that no such threats have been identified — closing the procedural gap that allowed the 2026 Annual Threat Assessment to omit the topic entirely.

### 5.3 Foreign Interference Commission Act

**Problem addressed.** The history of major foreign interference in US democratic processes — 2016, 2020, 2024, and the ongoing 2026 pre-election operation period — has not produced a comprehensive, independent, bipartisan assessment of what occurred, what defenses failed, and what structural reforms are needed. The 9/11 Commission model demonstrated that an independent, well-resourced, bipartisan commission operating outside executive branch control can produce findings and recommendations that achieve durable political traction precisely because they are not the product of any single administration.

**Core statutory provisions:**

*Section 1: Commission composition.* Create a 12-member, bipartisan Foreign Interference Commission appointed as follows: 3 members by the Senate Intelligence Committee chair, 3 by the ranking member, 3 by the House Intelligence Committee chair, and 3 by the ranking member. No more than 6 members may be of the same party. Members must be confirmed by a majority of the committee appointing them. The commission chair is selected by majority vote of the members.

*Section 2: Mandate.* Investigate and report on: (a) all documented foreign interference operations targeting US elections and democratic processes since 2016; (b) the adequacy of existing statutory and regulatory frameworks for deterring, detecting, and responding to foreign interference; (c) the effects of the 2025 dismantlement of counterinterference capacity on US vulnerability to ongoing foreign operations; (d) the foreign campaign finance loopholes created by Citizens United and its progeny; and (e) recommendations for structural reforms in each area.

*Section 3: Authority.* The commission has subpoena authority, power to receive classified testimony in closed session, access to all relevant intelligence community assessments, and authority to compel production of documents from executive branch agencies. The commission's budget is mandatory-appropriated at $15 million annually for a 3-year operating period.

*Section 4: Report and implementation.* The commission must produce an unclassified final report within 24 months of establishment, with a classified annex for intelligence community-sensitive material. The commission's recommendations are submitted to Congress and the President simultaneously. GAO is directed to issue implementation status reports on commission recommendations annually for 5 years following the final report.

**Legislative foundation.** Representative Swalwell introduced predecessor legislation for a similarly structured commission; all House Democrats co-sponsored similar legislation in earlier Congresses. The political challenge — that a sitting administration is unlikely to support a commission empowered to investigate its own counterintelligence failures — argues for framing the commission as a prospective structural assessment rather than a retrospective accountability investigation. The 9/11 Commission succeeded partly because it was positioned as a national security improvement effort, not as a prosecution.

### 5.4 Democracy Promotion Restoration Act and Authoritarian Influence Registry

**Problem addressed.** The 97-percent reduction in US democracy promotion programming has created a structural vulnerability that is more consequential over the medium-to-long-term than any individual foreign influence operation: the collapse of the civil society ecosystems that sustain democratic governance in countries where the US has strategic interests. Restoring this capacity requires not merely funding restoration but statutory protections against future executive defunding — the same statutory protections that NED's impoundment lawsuit attempted to vindicate.

**Core statutory provisions — Democracy Promotion Restoration Act:**

*Section 1: NED statutory funding floor.* Establish NED's annual congressional appropriation at a mandatory floor of $400 million, mandatory-appropriated and not subject to impoundment or executive spending pause. Prohibit the executive branch from conditioning NED's funding on program content, grant recipient selection, or compliance with executive foreign policy priorities. NED's programmatic independence — the structural element that makes it function as a credible intermediary rather than a government propaganda vehicle — must be statutorily protected.

*Section 2: USAID democracy and governance floor.* Establish a mandatory USAID democracy, rights, and governance program funding floor of $2 billion annually, mandatory-appropriated. Require that USAID democracy programs be administered through a separate appropriations account that is not subject to broad foreign assistance reprogramming authority. Prohibit mass program cancellations (defined as more than 10 percent of active programs in any 60-day period) without congressional notification and a 30-day waiting period.

*Section 3: DRL operational capacity mandate.* Require the State Department's Bureau of Democracy, Human Rights, and Labor to maintain at least 300 full-time equivalent positions dedicated to its statutory democracy and human rights functions. Prohibit restructuring that removes DRL's independent programmatic authority or places it in a reporting relationship that makes its mission subject to political approval from other State Department bureaus.

*Section 4: Transition and restart funding.* Appropriate $500 million in transition funding for organizations that lost USAID and NED funding in 2025 to restart or wind down operations in a manner that preserves institutional knowledge, protects locally employed staff, and provides program continuation where immediate restart is feasible. The 2025 cancellations were so rapid — grants terminated without notice — that program assets, local staff, and partner relationships were lost in ways that cannot simply be reconstituted by restoring the funding.

**Core statutory provisions — Authoritarian Influence Registry:**

The FARA reform architecture addresses known foreign agents. The Authoritarian Influence Registry addresses a structural gap: the documented coordination between authoritarian state actors and domestic political movements, think tanks, media organizations, and political donors that falls below the FARA threshold but nonetheless constitutes systematic foreign influence on US democratic processes.

*Section 1: Registry scope.* The Authoritarian Influence Registry requires registration by any entity that: (a) receives funding from a state-owned enterprise, sovereign wealth fund, or government-affiliated foundation of a country designated as a "country of concern" (Russia, China, Iran, North Korea, and any country designated by the intelligence community as conducting active democracy interference operations); AND (b) engages in activities intended to influence US political opinion, policy, or elections. Registration requires disclosure of all funding received, the identity of the foreign source, and the nature of the activities undertaken.

*Section 2: Threshold.* The registration threshold is $50,000 annually from designated-country sources — substantially lower than the FARA threshold that currently allows large-scale funding of US political activities without disclosure. The lower threshold reflects the documented model of authoritarian influence operations, which rely on aggregated smaller payments through commercial intermediaries rather than single large direct payments through government agencies.

*Section 3: Public access.* The registry is fully searchable and publicly accessible, with machine-readable data that enables civil society monitoring and journalistic investigation. Annual compliance reports are published by the independent FARA Enforcement Unit (created under Section 5.1).

*Section 4: Think tank and media transparency.* Any think tank, research organization, or media company that receives funding from designated-country sources at the registry threshold must publish an annual funding disclosure report identifying all foreign-government-linked funding sources. This provision is modeled on the existing IRS Form 990 public disclosure requirement and creates civil, not criminal, liability for non-disclosure.

## 6. Cross-Domain Synthesis: How Foreign Interference Amplifies Every Other Democratic Vulnerability

### 6.1 The Multiplier Effect

Foreign interference does not operate as a standalone threat to democratic institutions. It operates as an amplifier — identifying, exacerbating, and exploiting every structural vulnerability documented in the other domains of this framework. Understanding this multiplier function is essential to understanding why the simultaneous dismantlement of counterintelligence capacity is more consequential than the sum of its parts.

**Domain 37 (Federal Executive Interference in 2026 Midterms).** Russian disinformation operations documented since 2024 have specifically targeted voter confidence in election administration — amplifying domestic messaging about "rigged" elections that suppresses Democratic turnout and delegitimizes potential Democratic electoral victories. The SAVE database's documented 81 percent false-positive rate in Missouri, if deployed to generate wrongful voter removals, creates a domestic voter suppression operation whose effects are structurally indistinguishable from what foreign interference seeks to produce. Storm-1516's near-daily output in early 2026 targets precisely this vulnerability: narratives about mail ballot fraud, voting machine manipulation, and election official corruption that reinforce the domestic interference campaign documented in Domain 37.

**Domain 29 (DOJ Capture and Prosecutorial Weaponization).** The dismantlement of FARA enforcement and the FITF occurred within the same institutional framework — a DOJ that has been captured for political purposes. A DOJ unwilling to prosecute the SPLC on meritless bank fraud charges but simultaneously unwilling to prosecute documented Russian influence operations creates an enforcement environment that is precisely inverted from any national security logic. Foreign adversaries operating through domestic commercial intermediaries face less prosecutorial risk under the Bondi enforcement guidelines than domestic civil rights organizations face under the current DOJ's FARA-free enforcement priorities.

**Domain 8 (Campaign Finance and Dark Money).** The $1.9 billion in dark money spending in the 2024 election cycle creates the structural channel through which foreign-state-linked capital enters US political processes without FARA disclosure requirements. The Citizens United loophole — permitting foreign-owned American corporations to make unlimited independent expenditures — is not merely a campaign finance problem; it is a foreign interference problem. The Whitehouse-Raskin DISCLOSE Act closure of this loophole is therefore simultaneously a campaign finance reform and a national security measure.

**Domain 7 (Foreign Policy and Democratic Alliances).** The collapse of US democracy promotion has direct foreign policy consequences: the allies who relied on US institutional support for democratic governance — not merely rhetorical support, but operational training, civil society funding, and election observation capacity — face a strategic environment in which authoritarian alternatives are better resourced and more reliably present than democratic ones. The NATO alliance's democratic solidarity dimension — which Russia's interference operations specifically target — depends on member state democratic resilience that US democracy promotion programs directly supported.

**Domain 21 (Data Privacy and Surveillance).** The voter data sharing between DOJ and DHS documented in Domain 37 creates a surveillance database that foreign adversaries — if they can penetrate either DHS or DOJ systems — could use to target specific voter populations with customized disinformation. The SAVE database contains Social Security information, driver's license data, and immigration status information for millions of Americans. The concentration of this data in a system shared between two large federal agencies, without adequate security safeguards, creates a counterintelligence vulnerability that is separate from its domestic civil liberties implications.

### 6.2 The Democratic Resilience Deficit

The relationship between foreign interference and domestic democratic erosion is not merely correlational. Political scientists studying democratic backsliding have identified a structural mechanism: authoritarian movements within democracies are strengthened by foreign authoritarian support, and foreign authoritarian interference operations specifically target the institutional and normative foundations of democratic governance — free press, independent judiciary, trust in elections — that would otherwise constrain those movements.

This mechanism is documented in the Hungarian case: Russia's sustained information operations in support of Fidesz, the authoritarian erosion of Hungarian democratic institutions, and the international isolation of Hungarian civil society created a mutually reinforcing dynamic in which Russian interference and domestic authoritarianism became structurally linked. The April 2026 Hungarian election — in which the opposition won a 53.6 percent landslide after 16 years of Orbán's rule — illustrates both the vulnerability (sustained foreign and domestic authoritarian pressure over time) and the resilience (democratic mobilization at sufficient scale can overcome structural manipulation when the conditions motivate participation).

The US case differs from Hungary's in a structural respect: US democratic institutions have greater inherent resilience — the federal system, the independent judiciary, the civil society ecosystem — than Hungary's. But the US foreign interference vulnerability differs from Hungary's in a structural respect as well: the United States is simultaneously the primary target of the most sophisticated foreign interference operations in the world AND is in the process of dismantling every domestic institution specifically designed to defend against those operations. The combination creates a vulnerability window that has no modern precedent.

## 7. Implementation Timeline and Political Conditions

### 7.1 Immediate (0–12 Months): Administrative and Litigation Measures

Not all democratic defense capacity requires congressional action. Several of the most urgent gaps can be partially addressed through administrative action and litigation:

**State-level FARA equivalents.** Fourteen states have introduced state-level foreign agent registration statutes in 2025-2026 legislative sessions. State FARA-style laws cannot replicate federal enforcement but create transparency obligations for activities within state boundaries and establish accountability norms that create political costs for foreign-influenced political activity. The Steptoe analysis of state FARA initiatives documents the range of approaches being pursued.

**Civil FARA enforcement through qui tam.** The existing FARA statute does not contain a private right of action. Scholars at Georgetown and NYU Law have proposed regulatory amendments that would create a qui tam mechanism — allowing private parties to bring civil enforcement actions on behalf of the government for a portion of any penalty recovered — that would supplement (or substitute for) diminished federal enforcement capacity.

**Election security litigation.** The dismantlement of CISA's election security program and the FMIC's Election Threats Executive function may be challengeable as violations of the statutory mandates that created those functions. The FMIC statute's prohibition on formal closure before 2028 creates a potential basis for litigation challenging the functional elimination of the center's capacity even short of formal closure.

**Allied intelligence coordination.** The collapse of US government-to-government coordination on foreign disinformation through the GEC creates an information gap that allied democratic governments — particularly in the EU, Canada, Australia, and the Nordic-Baltic region — have an independent interest in filling through bilateral and multilateral arrangements. This is an administrative action that does not require congressional authorization.

### 7.2 Near-Term (12–36 Months): Statutory Foundations

The four statutory reform proposals in Section 5 require congressional majorities that do not exist under the current Congress. The political condition for their enactment is either a change in congressional control (the 2026 midterm elections), a bipartisan national security consensus that transcends current partisan alignment, or a documented catastrophic failure of foreign interference defenses — the equivalent of a cyber 9/11 for democratic institutions — that creates irresistible political pressure for structural response.

The most likely near-term legislative pathway is through the national security frame rather than the democracy protection frame. The historical bipartisan consensus on FARA enforcement, the intelligence community's own documented assessments of adversary operations, and the documented Russian state media funding of domestic political influencers create an argument for foreign agent transparency reform that can appeal to national security conservatives in both parties. The DISCLOSE Act's foreign spending provisions have attracted Republican co-sponsors in previous Congresses when framed as preventing foreign government interference in US democracy rather than as campaign finance reform.

### 7.3 Long-Term (36–60 Months): Structural Reforms

The most durable structural reforms — mandatory appropriations floors for counterintelligence capacity, the Foreign Interference Commission, the Democracy Promotion Restoration Act — require political conditions that align a congressional majority with a reform-oriented executive. These conditions are achievable but not inevitable. The political economy analysis follows the pattern documented in earlier domains: the same alignment that passed the Budget Act of 1974, the Watergate-era independent counsel statute, and the post-9/11 intelligence reform legislation can, under sufficient political pressure, produce structural reforms that survive subsequent political changes.

The critical design principle is insulation: every statutory reform in this domain must be designed to function regardless of which administration controls the executive branch. Mandatory appropriations, fixed-term independent officers, congressional reporting requirements that cannot be satisfied by withholding, and automatic disclosure triggers that do not depend on executive discretion are the structural tools that create functional permanence. The 2025 experience — in which virtually every institution designed to protect democratic processes from foreign interference was eliminated by executive action in a single 90-day window — is the design specification for what statutory reform must prevent.
