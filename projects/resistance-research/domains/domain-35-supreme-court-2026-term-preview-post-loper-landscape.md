## Executive Summary

The Supreme Court has remade the constitutional landscape in three decisive strokes since June 2024. First, *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo* (June 28, 2024) overruled the forty-year Chevron deference doctrine, requiring courts to exercise independent judgment on all statutory interpretation questions agencies have previously resolved. Second, *Trump v. United States* (July 1, 2024) created a three-tiered presidential immunity framework that makes criminal accountability for executive overreach practically unavailable through judicial process alone. Third, *Trump v. CASA, Inc.* (June 27, 2025) eliminated universal injunctions — the principal tool civil rights litigants have used to block unlawful executive orders nationwide — replacing them with plaintiff-specific relief and uncertain APA vacatur alternatives.

The October 2025 term (which runs through late June 2026) amplifies these shifts. The Court has already struck down IEEPA-based tariffs in *Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump* (February 20, 2026), invalidated Colorado's conversion therapy ban as viewpoint discrimination in *Chiles v. Salazar* (March 31, 2026), and appears positioned to hold that birthright citizenship is constitutionally protected despite the executive order — though the substantive immigration holding will arrive too late for the 2026 midterms. The most consequential still-pending decision of the current term is *Trump v. Slaughter*, which is likely to narrow or overrule *Humphrey's Executor* (1935) and thereby eliminate structural protection for all independent agencies — the NLRB, FTC, CFPB, and Federal Reserve included.

The October 2026 term, which begins October 5, 2026, will not have its cert grants finalized until summer 2026. However, the clearest candidates based on current relist patterns are: *Viramontes v. Cook County* (assault-style rifle ban, 11 relists), *Duncan v. Bonta* (large-capacity magazine ban, 11 relists), *Department of Labor v. Sun Valley Orchards* (Article III courts vs. agency adjudication), and a Section 230 CSAM case (*Doe v. Twitter*) that would substantially narrow platform immunity. The October 2026 term will also likely revisit APA vacatur scope — the open question *Trump v. CASA* deliberately left unresolved.

**For the democratic recovery proposal**: Loper Bright and *Humphrey's Executor*'s likely demise require rewriting the agency-dependent sections of Domain 6 (judicial independence) and Domain 13 (constitutional amendments) to assume that regulatory protections cannot rest on deference or agency discretion. Every reform mechanism that depends on independent agency authority — NLRB labor protections, FTC competition enforcement, FEC campaign finance oversight, CFPB consumer protection — is now structurally vulnerable. Statutory specificity, not deference, must be the reform architecture.

## 1. The Loper Bright Fallout: Chevron's Overruling and the Administrative Law Trifecta

### 1.1 The Holding and Its Doctrinal Basis

On June 28, 2024, the Supreme Court decided *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo* and its companion *Relentless Inc. v. Department of Commerce* by a 6-3 vote, with Chief Justice Roberts writing for the majority. The Court held that the Administrative Procedure Act's section 706 — which directs courts to "decide all relevant questions of law" — is incompatible with Chevron's instruction to defer to agency interpretations when a statute is ambiguous. "The APA," Roberts wrote, "requires courts to exercise their own judgment in determining the meaning of statutes, including ambiguous ones."

The core doctrinal shift: under Chevron (1984), when a statute was ambiguous, courts asked only whether an agency's interpretation was "reasonable" — and if so, deferred. Under *Loper Bright*, courts must now find the "best meaning" using traditional tools of statutory construction, with agency interpretations treated as potentially persuasive but never controlling. The weaker *Skidmore* deference standard (1944) survives: courts may still find an agency's long-standing, consistent, and well-reasoned interpretation persuasive, but that persuasion operates in the court's independent judgment, not in substitution for it.

Critically, *Loper Bright* preserved prior Chevron-era holdings as stare decisis: "we do not call into question prior cases that relied on the Chevron framework." This preservation clause means agencies that won deference battles under the old regime do not automatically lose their established rule interpretations. However, as Corner Post made clear (see below), the statute of limitations for challenging those interpretations runs from when a plaintiff is first injured — not from when a rule was issued — opening decades-old interpretations to fresh legal challenge.

### 1.2 The Administrative Law Trifecta: Loper Bright, Corner Post, and Jarkesy

*Loper Bright* did not operate in isolation. The Court's 2023-2024 term produced three administrative law decisions that together constitute the most comprehensive restructuring of the administrative state since the New Deal:

**Corner Post, Inc. v. Board of Governors of the Federal Reserve System** (June 28, 2024, 6-3): Held that the APA's six-year statute of limitations for challenging agency action accrues when a plaintiff is first injured by a rule — not when the rule is promulgated. A business that was not incorporated when a rule was issued can file a lawsuit challenging it at any point after formation. This creates effective perpetual challengeability for all existing agency rules, provided the plaintiff can establish a recent injury. For democratic reform implementation, this means that any statutory reform creating new regulatory obligations can be challenged by newly formed corporate entities with no time bar. The practical consequence: every protective regulation the administrative state has built over four decades is now subject to fresh attack from any plaintiff who can establish a post-formation injury.

**SEC v. Jarkesy** (June 27, 2024, 6-3): Held that when the SEC seeks civil monetary penalties that are "legal in nature" (analogous to common law fraud), the Seventh Amendment requires a jury trial before an Article III court, not an administrative law judge in an in-house agency proceeding. Jarkesy's reach extends far beyond securities regulation: any agency enforcement action seeking "legal" remedies — including civil penalties imposed by the NLRB, FTC, CFPB, or OSHA — may now require jury trials in federal court rather than administrative adjudication. This dramatically increases the cost, duration, and unpredictability of agency enforcement, creating asymmetric advantages for well-resourced corporate defendants over consumer, labor, and environmental agencies.

Together, the trifecta creates what legal scholars have called a "triple blow" to the administrative state: agencies lose deference on statutory interpretation (Loper Bright), face perpetual limitation periods for challenges to their rules (Corner Post), and must pursue enforcement in Article III courts with jury trials for monetary remedies (Jarkesy). Each individually would be significant; their combined effect is transformative.

### 1.3 Which Agencies and Reforms Are Most Exposed

The exposure is not uniform. Agencies relying heavily on broad statutory interpretation to expand their jurisdiction face the highest post-Loper risk. The agencies most structurally exposed include:

**Environmental Protection Agency**: The EPA's authority to regulate greenhouse gases under the Clean Air Act depends on judicial acceptance that "air pollutants" includes carbon dioxide — a statutory interpretation question now subject to de novo review. *City and County of San Francisco v. EPA* (decided March 4, 2025, 5-4) narrowed the EPA's ability to impose outcome-based permit conditions under the Clean Water Act, citing *Loper Bright*'s reasoning. The Air Force's refusal in 2024 to comply with an EPA drinking water cleanup order — citing *Loper Bright* — previews how executive agencies will use the decision to resist regulatory obligations even within the executive branch.

**Federal Communications Commission**: The Sixth Circuit in January 2025 invalidated the FCC's net neutrality rules, holding that the FCC's authority to regulate internet service providers as common carriers was not clearly authorized by the Telecommunications Act of 1996. The decision explicitly applied *Loper Bright*'s best-reading standard and denied deference to the FCC's long-standing interpretation.

**National Labor Relations Board**: The circuit split on NLRB deference post-*Loper Bright* is currently unresolved. The D.C. Circuit held in July 2024 that Board decisions continue to warrant high deference; the Sixth Circuit held it would "not defer to the NLRB's interpretation of the NLRA, but exercise independent judgment." The Supreme Court has not definitively resolved this split — in March 2025, it declined to grant cert in a Ninth Circuit case that would have clarified the standard, while simultaneously vacating and remanding a D.C. Circuit case for "further consideration in light of *Loper Bright*." Every NLRB interpretation of ambiguous NLRA provisions — successor-bar doctrine, make-whole remedies, the definition of concerted protected activity — is now contestable in circuits that apply independent review.

**Food and Drug Administration**: The FDA's regulatory framework depends on broad statutory interpretations of "drug," "device," and "biological product" categories. As pharmaceutical companies increasingly challenge FDA's authority to regulate novel therapeutic categories — including mRNA therapeutics, cell therapies, and AI-assisted diagnostics — courts will apply *Loper Bright*'s best-reading standard rather than deferring to FDA expertise.

**CFPB and FTC**: Both agencies' enforcement paradigms depend on broad interpretations of their authorizing statutes — the Dodd-Frank Act and FTC Act respectively. The Jarkesy decision's jury-trial requirement applies to the monetary penalties these agencies routinely seek, substantially raising enforcement costs.

### 1.4 Implications for Reform Implementation

The Loper Bright trifecta fundamentally changes the architecture required for any democratic recovery reform to be durable. Under the Chevron regime, Congress could delegate authority broadly ("the EPA shall regulate air pollutants") and trust agencies to fill in details through deference-protected rulemaking. Under *Loper Bright*, that architecture fails: any ambiguity becomes an invitation for judicial override, and any interpretive gap becomes a vulnerability.

The practical implication for the democratic recovery proposal is that every legislative reform must be written with maximum statutory specificity. The Legislature cannot say "the FTC shall protect consumers from unfair competition"; it must define "unfair," specify prohibited practices, and establish enforceable standards that courts apply as statutory commands rather than regulatory interpretations. This is harder, slower, and more vulnerable to legislative compromise — but it is the only architecture that survives *Loper Bright* challenge.

The Stop Corporate Capture Act — introduced by Senate Democrats in July 2024 to codify Chevron deference by statute — would restore the pre-*Loper Bright* framework if enacted. Under Republican congressional control, it has no realistic legislative pathway before at minimum 2027. It remains a template for what statutory language a future Democratic Congress would need to pass.

## 2. October 2026 Docket Preview

### 2.1 The Certification Timeline

As of April 27, 2026, the October 2026 term (beginning October 5, 2026) has not had its cert grants finalized. The Court typically issues its principal "long conference" grants in late September to early October, establishing the initial docket. However, a significant number of cases are granted cert in spring and early summer from ongoing petition reviews — the period we are now in. Cert petitions currently on conference with high relist counts are the most reliable leading indicators.

### 2.2 Near-Certain Grants: Second Amendment Cluster

The most consolidated prediction from SCOTUSblog and court-watchers is a Second Amendment cluster in OT2026. The Court has relisted assault-style rifle and large-capacity magazine cases extensively during OT2025:

**Viramontes v. Cook County** (No. 25-238): Challenges Cook County's ban on AR-15 platform rifles as unconstitutional under the Second and Fourteenth Amendments. The case has been relisted 11 times as of April 2026 — a number that SCOTUSblog notes "almost never" appears in denied petition cases. A four-to-seven circuit split on assault weapons bans gives the Court standard cert factors to work with. The Seventh Circuit issued a per curiam opinion below in June 2025 with no dissent. Prediction: high probability of cert grant for OT2026.

**Duncan v. Bonta** (California): Challenges California's ban on large-capacity magazines. Also relisted 11 times. The question presented is whether state laws prohibiting possession of magazines capable of holding more than 10 rounds violate the Second Amendment. Prediction: likely granted in tandem with *Viramontes* to address both assault rifle and magazine restrictions in the same term.

These cases follow the pattern of *NYSRPA v. Bruen* (2022), which expanded Second Amendment protection beyond the home. A holding in OT2026 would define whether *Bruen*'s historical-tradition test protects assault-style weapons — with direct implications for Democratic efforts to pass federal gun safety legislation as a democratic recovery measure.

**Impact on reform feasibility**: If the Court holds in OT2026 that AR-15 platform rifles and large-capacity magazines are constitutionally protected, federal assault weapons legislation would require a constitutional amendment to survive judicial review. This directly implicates Domain 13's constitutional amendment strategy.

### 2.3 Section 230 and Platform Liability

**Doe v. Twitter** (Ninth Circuit, pending cert): Raises whether Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act immunizes platforms from liability for knowingly possessing and distributing child sexual abuse material (CSAM). The Ninth Circuit below held that Section 230 immunity applied even to knowing possession and distribution of CSAM — a holding that 16 states, Senator Josh Hawley, NCMEC, and multiple child safety organizations argue stretches the statute beyond any congressional intent.

The petition presents a narrow but potentially destabilizing question: does Section 230's immunity apply to a platform's own conduct (knowing possession) as distinct from its role hosting third-party content? Twitter characterizes even knowing inaction as "content screening" to which immunity attaches. The petitioners argue Twitter's conduct constitutes its own criminal acts.

A cert grant would not necessarily broaden into a general Section 230 reconsideration, but the petition's narrow framing — knowing conduct, CSAM specifically — gives the Court a vehicle to begin limiting Section 230 without wholesale revision. For democratic reform planning, Section 230 is relevant to political disinformation, AI-generated content liability, and platform accountability for algorithmic amplification of anti-democratic content.

### 2.4 Article III Adjudication and Agency Enforcement

**Department of Labor v. Sun Valley Orchards** (solicitor general petition): Whether Congress can assign visa penalty adjudications to the Labor Department rather than Article III courts. The SG's petition — filed by the Trump administration — challenges the constitutionality of agency adjudication even for the government's preferred enforcement mechanism, illustrating the scope of the post-Jarkesy doctrinal disruption. This case, if granted, would extend Jarkesy's jury-trial requirement into immigration enforcement and labor visa proceedings.

### 2.5 APA Vacatur Scope: The CASA Unresolved Question

*Trump v. CASA* (June 2025) limited universal injunctions but explicitly did not decide whether APA vacatur — the statutory remedy under 5 U.S.C. § 706(2) — can achieve the same nationwide effect. Lower courts have split: some hold that section 706's "set aside" language authorizes courts to vacate agency rules universally regardless of CASA; others hold that CASA's party-specific relief principle extends to APA review. This circuit split makes an OT2026 cert grant on APA vacatur scope highly probable.

The outcome matters enormously for reform litigation: if APA vacatur is limited to the parties before the court, the nationwide blocking mechanism for unlawful executive rules is essentially eliminated, forcing reliance on class actions with all their procedural complexity. If vacatur retains universal effect, much of the practical impact of CASA can be routed around through APA challenges to implementing agency regulations.

### 2.6 Parental Rights in Education

**Littlejohn v. School Board of Leon County** (Florida, pending): Addresses the constitutional standard for parental challenges to school gender policies. Note that *Foote v. Ludlow School Committee* — which had accumulated 12 relists — was denied cert on April 20, 2026, but the denial does not end parental rights litigation. *Littlejohn* and similar cases present a circuit-ready question if another circuit reaches a conflicting result.

## 3. Universal Injunction Restrictions: Post-CASA Remedy Architecture

### 3.1 The CASA Holding

*Trump v. CASA, Inc.* (June 27, 2025, 6-3): Justice Barrett writing for the majority held that federal courts lack statutory authority under the Judiciary Act of 1789 to issue universal injunctions — injunctions that protect parties beyond the named plaintiffs in a case. "Nothing like a universal injunction was available at the founding, or for that matter, for more than a century thereafter," the Court held. Relief "must be limited to what is necessary to redress the plaintiff's own injury." The Court grounded its holding in statutory interpretation, explicitly declining to reach the Article III constitutional question — leaving open the possibility that Congress could restore universal injunctions by statute.

This decision ended the dominant civil rights litigation tactic of the first four years of Trump 2.0: during Trump's first 100 days of the second administration, district courts issued approximately 25 universal injunctions. CASA eliminated that tool as a standalone mechanism.

### 3.2 What Remains Available

The decision left several significant alternative pathways:

**APA Vacatur (contested)**: Under 5 U.S.C. § 706(2), courts are authorized to "set aside" arbitrary or unlawful agency action. Historically, "set aside" has been construed as universal — vacating a rule for everyone, not just the plaintiff. Post-CASA, most district courts have continued to issue universal vacatur under the APA, treating it as a distinct statutory remedy from an injunction. The circuit courts have not reached consensus. This remains the primary vehicle for broad reform litigation pending Supreme Court resolution of the vacatur scope question.

**Class Actions (Rule 23(b)(2))**: Organizing named plaintiffs into a certified class permits class-wide injunctive relief — effectively achieving universal protection for everyone in the class. This is more procedurally burdensome than a standalone injunction but achieves similar geographic scope. The NAACP, ACLU, and state attorneys general have all shifted toward class action structuring in the post-CASA environment. The principal limitation is that preliminary relief while class certification is pending is uncertain — CASA's party-specific principle may limit pre-certification injunctions.

**Associational Standing**: Trade associations and advocacy organizations can file suit on behalf of their members, with relief flowing to all members rather than just named plaintiffs. Courts have scrutinized associational standing more carefully post-CASA, but it remains available where membership can be defined with specificity.

**State Litigation**: State courts are not constrained by CASA's Judiciary Act reasoning. State attorneys general can file suit in state court (or in federal court with state standing), and state-issued injunctions operate under different authority. Multi-state AG coalitions — which were instrumental in blocking the first Trump administration's immigration orders — remain structurally intact.

**Federal Court Vacatur with State AG Plaintiffs**: When states bring APA claims, vacatur of a federal rule provides statewide (and potentially nationwide) protection for state interests, with states having standing to challenge any federal action affecting state-administered programs. This is the primary vehicle being used to challenge Medicaid work requirements, Title IX modifications, and federal spending freezes.

### 3.3 Strategic Implications for Reform Litigation

CASA fundamentally restructures the resource calculus for reform litigation. Pre-CASA, a single public interest lawsuit could block a nationwide policy — NILC v. DHS, *NAACP v. Trump*, states' APA challenges regularly produced universal injunctions within weeks of executive order issuance. Post-CASA, multiple overlapping lawsuits must be coordinated across circuits to provide equivalent geographic coverage, increasing litigation costs by a factor of four to five.

The decision also creates geographic fragmentation: an executive order may be enforceable in circuits where no plaintiff has obtained relief while blocked in others. This creates differential federal law application across the country — exactly the inconsistency that universal injunctions were designed to prevent — until the Supreme Court resolves a circuit split on the merits.

For Domain 6 and Domain 13 planning, the CASA constraint means reform advocates should design litigation strategies around: (1) APA class actions rather than standalone injunctions; (2) multi-state AG coalitions that can file coordinately in multiple circuits; (3) statutory drafting that creates explicit private rights of action with nationwide judicial review jurisdiction, removing the need for APA or equitable relief architecture; and (4) state court parallel litigation wherever state-law equivalents are available.

## 4. Presidential Immunity and Executive Accountability: The Post-July 2024 Landscape

### 4.1 The Three-Tier Framework

*Trump v. United States* (July 1, 2024, 6-3): Chief Justice Roberts wrote for the majority, establishing, for the first time, a constitutional framework for presidential criminal immunity. The framework has three tiers:

**Absolute immunity** for acts within the president's "exclusive constitutional authority" — the pardon power, command of the military, execution of laws, and control of the executive branch. The Court held that Trump's conversations with the Justice Department about election fraud investigations were within his exclusive authority over the executive branch and thus absolutely immune from criminal prosecution.

**Presumptive immunity** for all other "official acts" — acts taken in the president's official capacity even when not within exclusive authority. This presumption can theoretically be rebutted, but the Court set no standard for rebuttal, and the term "official act" is broad enough to encompass nearly all presidential conduct. Pressuring the Vice President to reject certified electoral votes was held "at least" presumptively immune.

**No immunity** for purely private, unofficial acts — acts taken in personal rather than official capacity. The Court declined to define which of Trump's charged conduct fell into this category, remanding to lower courts.

### 4.2 The Practical Effect on Executive Accountability

The one-year retrospective on *Trump v. United States* (Center for American Progress, July 2025) documents the practical consequence: the immunity framework functioned as a delay mechanism that pushed all federal criminal proceedings involving Trump's official conduct past the 2024 election, after which the Justice Department's OLC opinion that a sitting president cannot be indicted rendered the prosecutions moot. The administration then systematically dismantled the accountability infrastructure:

- Disbanded DOJ's Public Integrity Section and anti-corruption task forces
- Fired the career attorney leading DOJ's ethics division
- Paused Foreign Corrupt Practices Act enforcement
- Dropped investigations into Trump allies including crypto figures

Justice Sotomayor's dissent — warning that the majority created a scenario where a president "could violate the law" and "exploit the trappings of his office for personal gain" without accountability — has proven prescient. The $400 million Qatari aircraft transfer, cryptocurrency dealings generating hundreds of millions for the Trump family, and rewards to $TRUMP coin investors with White House access have all occurred without judicial remedy under the immunity framework.

**The core structural problem**: "Official act" is defined by function, not by legality or motive. An act that is criminal when performed by a private citizen may be absolutely immune when performed by a president if it falls within core executive function. The president can deploy DOJ as a political weapon (core executive function — control of the executive branch), can pardon accomplices (absolute immunity — the pardon power), and can direct military action (absolute immunity — commander in chief). The dissent's warning that this creates a "monarch above the law" dynamic has become a structural feature of the post-July 2024 landscape.

### 4.3 What Remains Judicially Enforceable

The immunity framework does not eliminate all judicial accountability. Several enforcement vectors survive:

**First, Fifth, and Sixth Amendment challenges to executive orders**: Lower courts have successfully blocked several executive orders on direct constitutional grounds — not as criminal prosecutions of the president, but as challenges to the orders' effects on private parties. The immunity framework protects the president from criminal prosecution; it does not immunize executive orders from constitutional challenge by affected parties.

**Separation of powers challenges**: Courts retain jurisdiction to declare executive actions unconstitutional when they exceed statutory authority or violate constitutional structure, even if the president who took the action is immune from criminal prosecution. *Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump* (IEEPA tariffs) demonstrates this: the Court struck down the tariffs as exceeding statutory authority without any implication of criminal liability.

**Civil liability for unofficial acts**: The immunity framework explicitly excludes "unofficial acts" from protection. Acts taken in a president's personal rather than official capacity — private business dealings, personal financial transactions, pre-presidential conduct — retain civil and criminal accountability. The line between official and unofficial is contested and will require case-by-case judicial determination.

**Congressional remedies**: The Court's opinion repeatedly noted that impeachment remains available as a non-judicial accountability mechanism. Impeachment for official acts is not displaced by the immunity doctrine. This makes impeachment the primary constitutional remedy for official corruption — but as the current Congress demonstrates, an impeachment remedy requiring 67 Senate votes is not a meaningful constraint.

### 4.4 Implications for Domain 2 and Domain 13

The presidential immunity framework creates a structural accountability gap that cannot be closed through litigation alone. Domain 2 (executive power and checks and balances) must incorporate the reality that:

(1) The criminal law pathway for presidential accountability is closed for official acts, regardless of how clearly illegal those acts appear under pre-immunity doctrine;

(2) Civil injunctive challenges to executive order effects survive, but only constrain future conduct, not past harm;

(3) The DOJ-as-weapon problem — using prosecutorial authority against political opponents — is absolutely immune from criminal prosecution if characterized as "control of the executive branch";

(4) Congressional oversight and appropriations leverage (Domain 34) remain the primary structural constraint, but both require a functioning congressional majority to operate.

Domain 13 (constitutional amendments) should consider whether a constitutional amendment explicitly limiting presidential immunity — or creating a special judicial process for official misconduct below the impeachment threshold — is viable. The challenge is that such an amendment would need to define the line between legitimate presidential authority and corrupt abuse with enough precision to survive textualist challenge; a vague "no immunity for corrupt acts" provision would generate decades of litigation over what counts as "corrupt."

## 5. The Independent Agencies Crisis: Humphrey's Executor and the Post-Slaughter Landscape

### 5.1 The Pending Decision in Trump v. Slaughter

*Trump v. Slaughter* (argued December 8, 2025, decision pending): The most consequential pending case of the October 2025 term for democratic reform infrastructure. President Trump fired FTC Commissioner Rebecca Slaughter in March 2025 despite the Federal Trade Commission Act's "for cause" removal protection — protections upheld in *Humphrey's Executor v. United States* (1935). The Supreme Court treated the administration's emergency stay application as a cert petition, heard oral arguments, and will issue its decision by late June 2026.

Every indicator from oral argument points toward a decision limiting or overruling *Humphrey's Executor*. The Court's conservative majority had already, in May 2025, stayed reinstatement orders for both Slaughter and NLRB member Gwynne Wilcox in unsigned emergency orders — a procedural step that signals the majority views the underlying legal questions as likely to favor the administration. Solicitor General Sauer argued at oral argument that "*Humphrey's Executor* was always egregiously wrong." Justice Kagan, in dissent from the May 2025 stay order, wrote that the majority had already "effectively repealed *Humphrey's Executor* by fiat."

The open question is not whether the Court will rule for the administration, but how broadly. Three options:

**Option 1**: Full overruling of *Humphrey's Executor*, holding that Congress cannot impose any restrictions on the president's power to remove principal officers of any executive agency. This would eliminate removal protections for the FTC, NLRB, FEC, CFPB, Merit Systems Protection Board, and potentially the Federal Reserve Board — though the Fed's unique central banking function may generate a carve-out.

**Option 2**: Narrow the multi-member commission exception established by *Humphrey's Executor* to only agencies with quasi-judicial functions, eliminating removal protection for FTC and NLRB commissioners while preserving it for entities closer to the classic administrative court model.

**Option 3**: Overrule *Humphrey's Executor* prospectively, leaving in place existing commissioners while holding that future legislation cannot create comparable protections. This is the least likely outcome given the administration's posture.

### 5.2 Implications for Reform Architecture

If *Humphrey's Executor* is overruled or substantially narrowed, the entire architecture of independent agency oversight — built over ninety years — becomes subject to presidential political control. The NLRB, FTC, CFPB, FEC, MSPB, and EEOC all become fully at-will agencies whose enforcement priorities shift with each administration. For democratic recovery, this means:

**NLRB**: Labor organizing rights become enforceable only during Democratic administrations. Without removal protection, NLRB members appointed under Trump will continue to reverse Biden-era labor protections, and a future Democratic president will need to reappoint new members and restart the years-long rulemaking process. The case for statutory labor rights — codified in law rather than regulatory interpretation — becomes urgent.

**FEC**: Campaign finance enforcement, already weak, becomes contingent on presidential preference. A president who benefits from dark money has no incentive to appoint FEC commissioners who enforce disclosure requirements.

**CFPB**: Consumer financial protection becomes cyclic with the political calendar. The CFPB's existence, already challenged under *Seila Law* (2020), will be further destabilized.

**Federal Reserve**: If the Court holds broadly that all independent agency officers are removable at will, the constitutional basis for Federal Reserve independence is threatened. Experts at Lawfare and elsewhere have warned that a *Slaughter* ruling extending to the Fed would create an executive tool for monetary policy manipulation unprecedented in the Fed's history.

For Domain 6 (judicial independence and reform) and Domain 13 (constitutional amendments), the *Slaughter* decision suggests that institutional independence cannot be secured through statute alone in the current constitutional interpretation environment. A constitutional amendment explicitly protecting independent agencies from at-will removal — analogous to how Article III's "during good behavior" tenure clause protects federal judges — may be the only durable architecture.

## 6. Reform Feasibility Under the New Judicial Landscape: Updated Assessment

### 6.1 What Has Changed Since Domain 6 and Domain 13 Were Written

The judicial independence sections of the democratic recovery proposal (Domain 6) and the constitutional amendments framework (Domain 13) were developed before the full impact of the Loper Bright trifecta and the subsequent *CASA* and presidential immunity decisions. The analytical foundation requires updating on four structural points:

**Point 1: Deference-dependent reforms are non-starters**. Any Domain 6 or Domain 13 recommendation that assumes agencies will have broad discretionary authority to interpret their enabling statutes is now structurally unsound. Statutory language must specify outcomes, not delegate outcomes to agency interpretation. If a reform statute says "the FEC shall regulate coordinated expenditures," courts will now determine independently what counts as "coordinated," potentially diverging from FEC practice. The statute must define "coordinated" with the precision of a criminal code.

**Point 2: Agency independence is pending elimination**. Domain 6 recommendations that assume NLRB independence, FTC independence, or CFPB independence as stable structural features need contingency planning for the post-*Slaughter* world. Every regulatory enforcement mechanism should be paired with a private right of action that does not depend on agency initiative. Citizens and advocacy organizations should be able to sue directly for violations of the same statutory standards, not merely petition agencies to act.

**Point 3: Nationwide litigation relief requires structural redesign**. The universal injunction toolkit is gone under CASA. Domain 6's litigation strategy sections need to be rewritten around class action architecture, multi-state AG coordination, and APA vacatur pending the Supreme Court's resolution of vacatur scope. The litigation investment required to achieve equivalent geographic coverage has increased substantially.

**Point 4: Presidential accountability requires non-judicial mechanisms**. The immunity framework makes criminal prosecution unavailable for official acts. Domain 2's cross-domain connection to Domain 6 requires acknowledging that judicial accountability for presidential corruption is now limited to unofficial acts, while official act accountability depends on impeachment (requiring a two-thirds Senate majority), statutory private remedies (which the immunity framework may not reach), and political accountability through elections.

### 6.2 What Remains Viable and Strong

Several Domain 6 and Domain 13 mechanisms remain robust under the current judicial landscape:

**Explicit Article III court protections**: Protecting judges from at-will removal, court-stripping, or jurisdiction manipulation through statutory explicit language rather than reliance on inherent judicial power. Courts themselves have demonstrated willingness to protect their institutional authority — as seen in *Learning Resources* (IEEPA tariffs) and in *Trump v. Barbara* (birthright citizenship) where the Court appeared likely to reject the executive order. The judiciary has shown it will not simply capitulate.

**Structural reforms surviving textualist review**: Supreme Court term limits through constitutional amendment; mandatory recusal standards in statute; binding ethics codes with enforcement mechanisms. These are procedural structural reforms that do not depend on deference or interpretation — they create bright-line requirements that courts can apply literally.

**Congressional standing provisions**: Legislation explicitly creating congressional standing to challenge executive impoundments and appropriations violations would give Congress direct judicial access that does not depend on private party litigation. The post-CASA landscape makes standing design more important, not less.

**State enforcement pathways**: State attorneys general retain full standing and authority to challenge federal actions affecting state interests in federal courts and state courts. The multi-state AG model has proven durable through CASA and immunity doctrine — states filed coordinated challenges to birthright citizenship, IEEPA tariffs, Medicaid cuts, and Title IX modifications, each successfully obtaining relief. Strengthening state enforcement mechanisms — including state analog laws mirroring federal protections and state court jurisdiction over federal officials acting in their states — remains a viable architecture.

**Private rights of action**: Explicitly creating citizen-suit provisions in every reform statute, allowing private enforcement of the same standards that agencies enforce, removes dependence on agency initiative and creates a distributed enforcement model resilient to political agency capture.

**Constitutional amendment strategy**: The constitutional amendment pathway (Article V, requiring two-thirds of Congress and three-fourths of states) is demanding but not foreclosed. Domain 13's amendment priorities should now include: (1) an explicit independent agency protection clause (the structural gap exposed by *Slaughter*); (2) a presidential self-pardon prohibition (which the immunity framework makes urgent); (3) explicit congressional standing for appropriations challenges (closing the Domain 34 gap); and (4) a voting rights amendment eliminating judicial and legislative race-discrimination analysis thickets that *Callais* and its successors will continue to exploit.

### 6.3 Immediate Adaptive Actions for the Recovery Timeline

Based on the current judicial landscape assessment, the following adaptations to the proposal's timeline are warranted:

**Short-term (2026-2027 election cycle)**: Focus on statutory drafting templates that build specificity rather than delegation. Every draft bill in the recovery proposal should be reviewed by administrative law counsel against *Loper Bright* to ensure every operative term is defined at the statutory level. Begin organizing multi-state AG coordination networks for litigation filing — do not rely on single-circuit universal injunction strategies.

**Medium-term (2027-2029 first recovery Congress)**: Prioritize the Stop Corporate Capture Act equivalent — statutory restoration of Chevron deference — as a first-100-days legislative priority if a Democratic Congress is elected. This is the fastest doctrinal fix for the widest range of agency regulatory problems. Simultaneously, draft and introduce the Independent Agency Protection constitutional amendment.

**Long-term (2029-2035 amendment cycle)**: The constitutional amendment strategy is a decade-long project. The Article V process requires sustained organizing in 38 states. The amendment priorities identified above — independent agency protection, presidential self-pardon prohibition, congressional appropriations standing, voting rights — form a coherent package that can be introduced together and debated in state legislatures during the 2030 redistricting cycle.

## 7. The October 2025 Term: Decisions Already Issued

For completeness and context, the following decisions from the OT2025 term have already shaped the landscape as of April 2026:

**Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump** (No. 24-1287, decided February 20, 2026, 6-3): Chief Justice Roberts writing for an unusual cross-ideological majority (Roberts, Sotomayor, Kagan, Gorsuch, Barrett, Jackson) held that IEEPA does not authorize presidential tariff imposition. The statute "contains no reference to tariffs or duties." This is the most significant executive power check from the current term, directly relevant to Domain 2. The administration immediately pivoted to Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974, which authorizes 15% tariffs for 150 days with congressional approval for extension — a narrower but existing authority. The tariff ruling demonstrates that the Roberts Court, despite its conservative majority, retains institutional interest in maintaining statutory limits on executive power where the text is reasonably clear.

**Chiles v. Salazar** (No. 24-539, decided March 31, 2026, 8-1): Justice Gorsuch writing for an 8-1 majority (only Justice Jackson dissenting) held that Colorado's ban on conversion therapy constitutes viewpoint discrimination in violation of the First Amendment, requiring strict scrutiny. This decision substantially limits state authority to restrict professional speech in therapeutic contexts. Its implications extend beyond LGBTQ+ issues: the viewpoint discrimination doctrine it articulates could reach state regulations of other categories of professional advice, including financial advice, climate science communication, and public health messaging. The 8-1 vote — including all three liberal justices in the majority — signals that First Amendment speech protections have now developed a genuinely non-ideological majority in the Court.

**Bost v. Illinois State Board of Elections** (No. 24-568, decided January 14, 2026): Held that congressional candidates have standing to challenge vote-counting rules. A procedural but important decision for election integrity litigation — it establishes that candidates, not just voters, can raise federal court challenges to election administration.

**Pending (decision expected by July 2026)**:

- *Trump v. Barbara* (birthright citizenship, 14th Amendment): Oral arguments April 1, 2026. Majority of justices appeared skeptical of the executive order; decision expected to hold birthright citizenship is constitutionally protected. Would be a significant post-election vindication of the 14th Amendment's original purpose but arrives after any 2026 enforcement damage.
- *Louisiana v. Callais* (Voting Rights Act, 14th and 15th Amendments, reargued October 2025): Decision pending. Could eliminate majority-minority districts as a VRA Section 2 remedy nationwide — potentially the most consequential voting rights decision since *Shelby County* (2013).
- *Trump v. Slaughter* (independent agencies, *Humphrey's Executor*): Decision pending. Near-certain to narrow or overrule *Humphrey's Executor*, as analyzed above.
- *Watson v. Republican National Committee* (mail ballot deadlines, federal preemption): Oral arguments March 23, 2026. Court appeared likely to preempt state grace-period laws. If decided against Mississippi, 14 states' mail ballot acceptance practices may be invalidated — a direct voting access implication for the 2026 midterms.
