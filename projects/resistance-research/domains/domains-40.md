# Domain 40 (Expansion): Healthcare as Democratic Determinant

**Research completed**: May 13, 2026
**Crisis window**: ACUTE — OBBBA (signed July 4, 2025) projected to cause 11.8 million coverage losses; NIH budget cut $20.5B (~40%); Administration for Community Living dismantled (disabled community civic infrastructure); CFPB medical debt rule vacated (July 2025, leaving 15M Americans with medical debt on credit reports); 83% of polling places documented with ADA accessibility barriers; GAO SAVE Act analysis shows disabled voters disproportionately harmed; Oregon Health Insurance Experiment randomized evidence that Medicaid increases voter turnout by 7%; FQHCs serving 28M patients at 700+ sites implementing voter registration but under defunding pressure

## Executive Summary

Healthcare policy is democracy policy. The causal relationship between healthcare access and civic participation has been established through some of the most rigorous research designs available in the social sciences. The Oregon Health Insurance Experiment — a natural randomized controlled trial of Medicaid expansion in 2008 — found that Medicaid enrollment increased voter turnout in the November 2008 presidential election by approximately 7% overall, with effects concentrated in men (18% increase) and in Democratic counties (10% increase). This is experimental evidence, not correlation: the lottery design that randomly assigned Medicaid eligibility produced a genuine natural experiment demonstrating that healthcare access causes increased civic participation. The mechanism is not mysterious: healthcare coverage reduces economic precarity, reduces the time burden of managing medical crises without insurance, reduces the cognitive load of unmanaged chronic illness, and reduces the medical debt that Research at the CFPB documents as affecting 100 million Americans and causing 66.5% of bankruptcies.

This domain is analytically distinct from Domain 50 (Healthcare Access, OBBBA, and NVRA), which focuses specifically on the Medicaid-voter registration infrastructure mechanism — the fact that Medicaid offices are NVRA-designated voter registration agencies and that Medicaid enrollment decline directly reduces Section 7 voter registration offers. This domain analyzes the broader causal architecture: how healthcare access shapes who votes, who runs for office, who can participate in public meetings, who can organize, and who is structurally available for democratic engagement — through five distinct pathways that operate independently but compound each other.

The five pathways are: (1) the economic precarity pathway, through which medical debt and healthcare uninsurance reduce the financial stability, residential stability, and discretionary time that civic participation requires; (2) the disability exclusion pathway, through which polling place inaccessibility, OBBBA Medicaid cuts affecting disability coverage, and Administration for Community Living dismantling combine to structurally reduce the political participation of 61 million Americans with disabilities; (3) the maternal mortality pathway, through which healthcare access determines reproductive mortality disproportionately concentrated in Black and lower-income communities, with downstream effects on civic capacity in communities that experience concentrated maternal death; (4) the pandemic response federalism pathway, through which the structural failure to coordinate a national pandemic response killed more than 1 million Americans and demonstrated that healthcare governance design is simultaneously democratic governance design; and (5) the FQHC voter registration pathway, through which community health centers serving 28 million patients — 62% racial and ethnic minorities, 90% at or below 200% federal poverty level — are positioned to function as the most effective voter registration infrastructure in the country, but are under defunding and accreditation pressure that threatens this function.

The OBBBA's 11.8 million projected coverage losses are the most immediate acute dimension of this crisis: every person who loses Medicaid coverage loses both healthcare access (with the direct civic-participation consequences documented above) and their NVRA-designated voter registration contact point (as Domain 50 documents). The two harms are simultaneous and compound.

## 1. The Oregon Experiment: Randomized Evidence That Healthcare Access Causes Civic Participation

### 1.1 The Natural Experiment Design

In 2008, Oregon faced a limited expansion of its Medicaid program (Oregon Health Plan Standard) and used a lottery to randomly select applicants for available slots. This random assignment created an unusually rigorous research design: because assignment was random, differences in subsequent outcomes between lottery winners (who gained Medicaid access) and lottery losers (who did not) could be attributed to Medicaid itself rather than to pre-existing differences between the groups. Researchers Katherine Baicker and Amy Finkelstein (subsequently published in the Quarterly Journal of Political Science, NBER Working Paper 25244) used Oregon's state administrative data on voter behavior to analyze the political participation effects.

The core finding: Medicaid enrollment increased voter turnout in the November 2008 presidential election by approximately 7% overall. The effects were concentrated among men (18% increase) and residents of Democratic counties (10% increase). The study found suggestive evidence that the increase reflected new voter registrations rather than increased turnout among existing registrants — meaning Medicaid was recruiting new voters into the electorate.

The mechanism the researchers propose is consistent with the economic-precarity-to-civic-exclusion pathway documented elsewhere in this framework: Medicaid reduces out-of-pocket medical spending and medical debt, reducing the economic precarity that suppresses voter participation. It also reduces the cognitive and time burden of managing health conditions without insurance — a burden disproportionately carried by lower-income, racial minority, and rural populations that face the most significant voting barriers. The experimental evidence is specific: healthcare coverage does not just correlate with voting; it causes voting. Removing healthcare coverage, as the OBBBA does for a projected 11.8 million people, will reduce voting among the populations losing coverage.

**Sources**: [The Impact of Medicaid Expansion on Voter Participation — PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8021132/); [The Impact of Medicaid Expansion on Voter Participation: Evidence from the Oregon Experiment — NBER](https://www.nber.org/papers/w25244); [The Impact of Medicaid Expansion on Voter Participation — MIT Economics](https://economics.mit.edu/sites/default/files/2022-08/The%20Impact%20of%20Medicaid%20Expansion%20on%20Voter%20Particip.pdf)

### 1.2 The Cross-Sectional Evidence: Insurance, Voting, and Health Barriers

Beyond the Oregon experiment, the cross-sectional research literature on healthcare and civic participation is consistent. A 2023 study published in PMC examining Barriers to Voting and Access to Health Insurance documented a direct association: lack of health insurance coverage is independently associated with difficulty voting, after controlling for age, income, race, education, and prior voting history. The pathways identified are multiple: uninsured individuals face higher rates of transportation disruption (unmanaged chronic illness reduces mobility), higher rates of residential instability (medical debt causes housing insecurity), higher rates of employment disruption (untreated illness reduces work capacity and work-connected social networks), and higher rates of chronic pain and fatigue (reducing the energy available for discretionary civic activities).

States with greater voting access barriers also disproportionately rejected Medicaid expansion. The KFF analysis documents that states that opted out of Medicaid expansion — primarily Southern states — are also more likely to have enacted barriers to voter registration, early voting, and ballot access. This is not coincidental: the same political majorities that resist healthcare access expansion resist voting access expansion because both policies would expand the participation of constituencies — lower-income, rural, racial minority — whose political interests challenge those majorities. The geographic overlap between Medicaid non-expansion states and maximum-restriction voter-suppression states is a map of the democratic feedback loop: restrict healthcare, restrict participation, preserve the majority that restricts both.

**Sources**: [Barriers to Voting and Access to Health Insurance — PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9904135/); [Addressing Health Disparities Through Voter Engagement — PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7032920/); [Voting May Be Good for Your Health — Medicare Rights Center](https://www.medicarerights.org/medicare-watch/2024/08/29/voting-may-be-good-for-your-health)

## 2. Medical Debt as Civic Exclusion

### 2.1 The Scale and Inequality of Medical Debt

One hundred million Americans carry medical debt — approximately 41% of adults. Among lower-income adults (below 200% of the federal poverty level), the rate exceeds 60%. Medical debt is not evenly distributed: it is concentrated in the same geographic and demographic populations that face the most significant voting barriers. The CFPB's 2022 analysis documented that $88 billion in medical debt appears on consumer credit reports, with the highest concentrations in the South and in majority-Black and majority-Hispanic census tracts.

Medical debt is unlike other consumer debt in its civic consequences. It does not arise from consumption choices — it arises from health emergencies and chronic illness management that the individual did not choose to have. The bankruptcy research is stark: as many as 66.5% of personal bankruptcy filings cite medical bills as the primary cause, representing approximately 550,000 bankruptcies per year. Medical debt causes bankruptcy; bankruptcy causes housing instability, credit damage, and employment consequences; housing instability and credit damage are among the most consistently identified predictors of reduced voter registration and turnout. The pathway from a medical emergency to electoral non-participation is short, documentable, and entirely a function of healthcare system design rather than individual choices.

The CFPB's January 2025 final rule — which would have removed medical debt from credit reports of 15 million Americans — was vacated by the Eastern District of Texas in July 2025 after the Trump administration's CFPB leadership joined plaintiffs in requesting vacatur. Simultaneously, in October 2025, the CFPB issued an interpretive rule declaring that the Fair Credit Reporting Act preempts state laws restricting medical debt credit reporting — reversing the policy direction that 11 states had adopted to protect their residents. The result: 15 million Americans who would have had medical debt removed from their credit records remain burdened; the state-level protections that 11 states enacted are potentially preempted; and the pathway from medical emergency to civic exclusion through credit damage remains open and unaddressed by federal policy.

**Sources**: [Medical Debt Burden in the United States — CFPB](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/data-research/research-reports/medical-debt-burden-in-the-united-states/); [Healthcare Insights: Medical Debt Crushing 100 Million Americans — ILR Cornell](https://www.ilr.cornell.edu/scheinman-institute/blog/john-august-healthcare/healthcare-insights-how-medical-debt-crushing-100-million-americans); [CFPB Finalizes Rule to Remove Medical Bills from Credit Reports](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/about-us/newsroom/cfpb-finalizes-rule-to-remove-medical-bills-from-credit-reports/); [Federal Court Vacates CFPB Medical Debt Rule — Brownstein](https://www.bhfs.com/insight/federal-court-vacates-cfpbs-medical-debt-rule-finds-fcra-preempts-state-laws/); [Medical Debt on Credit Reports: Feds Kill Protection — GetOutOfDebt.org](https://getoutofdebt.org/230550/medical-debt-credit-reports-2026)

### 2.2 Effects of Medical Debt Relief — Experimental Evidence

A 2024 Quarterly Journal of Economics study by Neale Mahoney and co-authors, "Effects of Medical Debt Relief: Evidence from Two Randomized Experiments," provided experimental evidence on what happens when medical debt is relieved. The experiment: a nonprofit organization conducted randomized debt relief interventions in which some households with medical debt had their debt purchased and cancelled while others did not. The findings document improvements in financial stability, credit access, and reported economic security for debt-relief recipients — outcomes that the broader literature connects to civic participation, though the specific study did not measure electoral behavior.

The state-level policy response provides the observational evidence: as of August 2024, 22 state or local governments have passed programs to fund roughly $10.2 billion in medical debt relief, with 2 more programs under consideration that would raise the total to over $14.6 billion. States that have implemented medical debt relief programs include both blue and red states — Ohio, North Carolina, and Kentucky alongside California, New York, and Illinois — reflecting genuine bipartisan recognition that medical debt is a civic and economic crisis, not merely a policy disagreement.

**Sources**: [Effects of Medical Debt Relief — QJE/Oxford Academic](https://academic.oup.com/qje/article/140/2/1187/7933321); [Voters Show Strong Bipartisan Support for Policies that Protect People from Medical Debt — Undue Medical Debt](https://unduemedicaldebt.org/bipartisan-support-for-policies-that-protect-people-from-medical-debt/); [New Polling on Healthcare Affordability — Community Catalyst](https://communitycatalyst.org/news/new-polling-health-care-affordability-is-a-significant-and-growing-concern-for-most-voters/)

## 3. Disability, Healthcare, and Civic Exclusion

### 3.1 The Scale of Disability Voter Suppression

Sixty-one million Americans — approximately 26% of the adult population — have some form of disability. Disability cuts across every demographic category: race, income, age, and geography. But disability intersects with every other form of democratic exclusion in a compounding pattern: people with disabilities have lower incomes on average, higher rates of housing insecurity, higher rates of medical debt, higher dependence on Medicaid and SNAP, and significantly lower voter turnout than the non-disabled population.

The turnout gap is documented and substantial. MIT Election Lab data shows that from 2008 to 2022, older citizens with disabilities were less likely to vote than people without disabilities by between 10 and 11.7 percentage points in presidential election years. Nearly 30% of voters with disabilities reported difficulty voting at a polling place in 2012, compared to 8% of voters without disabilities. The GAO documented in its 2016 survey that 83% of polling places featured one or more potential barriers to voters with disabilities — a figure that compliance surveys from subsequent elections have not significantly improved.

The ADA polling place accessibility requirements — that voting locations be accessible to people using wheelchairs, provide accessible ballot marking devices, and permit assistance — have not produced compliance. There is no national ADA certification or permitting process for voting locations; the DOJ enforces accessibility through complaint investigations that are under-resourced and slow. The same OCR destruction documented in Domain 39 affects ADA enforcement in voting contexts: with 90% of OCR staff eliminated and 30,000 pending complaints, ADA voting complaints are among the cases sitting unaddressed.

**Sources**: [Disability, Voter Turnout, and Polling Place Accessibility — Rutgers](https://smlr.rutgers.edu/sites/default/files/Documents/Centers/Program_Disability_Research/Disability%20Voter%20Turnout%20Polling%20Place%20Accessibility.pdf); [Enhancing Accessibility in US Elections — Center for American Progress](https://www.americanprogress.org/article/enhancing-accessibility-u-s-elections/); [Barriers to Voting for People with Disabilities — Journalist's Resource](https://journalistsresource.org/home/barriers-to-voting-for-people-with-disabilities-an-explainer-and-research-roundup/); [How the SAVE America Act Hurts Disabled Voters — TIME](https://time.com/article/2026/03/23/how-the-save-america-act-hurts-disabled-voters/)

### 3.2 OBBBA, Medicaid, and Disability: The Coverage-Participation Pipeline

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act's healthcare provisions affect the disability population through multiple channels that compound the general healthcare-civic-exclusion pathway. The OBBBA imposes work requirements on the "expansion population" of Medicaid — nominally with carve-outs for people with disabilities — but the carve-outs are narrowly drafted, require documentation that many disabled individuals cannot easily provide, and impose periodic re-verification that creates continuous administrative burden. The OBBBA strips paperwork requirement exceptions for homeless individuals, veterans, and youth aging out of foster care — populations with high disability prevalence. The CBO projects that 3.8 million disabled adult SNAP recipients under 60 will lose benefits from SNAP work requirement changes in the OBBBA.

The Administration for Community Living (ACL) — the agency that coordinates services enabling people with disabilities to live in their communities and participate in civic life — was dismantled by HHS Secretary Kennedy's March 2025 reorganization. ACL programs funded transportation for people with disabilities, accessible housing modifications, assistive technology, and independent living centers. The National Health Law Program's July 2025 analysis, "Celebrating 35 Years of the ADA in the Face of the OBBBA Building Barriers to Care," documents the structural irony: the ADA's 35th anniversary coincides with the most significant rollback of disability service infrastructure since the ADA was enacted.

The electoral dimension is specific: disability services that enable community participation — transportation, accessible housing, in-home support — are the same services that enable participation in civic activities including voting. A person who cannot leave their home without supported transportation cannot vote without absentee ballot access; a person whose absentee ballot application is subjected to additional documentary proof requirements (SAVE Act, Domain 1) cannot exercise the franchise at all. The SAVE Act's disability voter harm was documented in a March 2026 TIME analysis: requirements for documentary proof of citizenship disproportionately harm disabled voters who may not have passports, may have birth certificates in inaccessible formats, and whose state IDs may not satisfy the bill's specific documentary requirements.

## 4. Maternal Mortality and the Gendered Democratic Harm

### 4.1 The Evidence Base

Black women in the United States are three times more likely to die from a pregnancy-related cause than white women. The Centers for Disease Control's most recent data shows the Black maternal mortality rate at 69.9 per 100,000 live births, compared to 26.6 for white women and 18.3 for Hispanic women. These disparities are not primarily explained by clinical differences in pregnancy complications — they are explained by healthcare access disparities, structural racism in healthcare settings (documented through studies showing that providers underestimate and undertreated Black patients' pain), and social determinants including residential segregation, environmental exposure, and economic precarity.

Maternal death is the most extreme endpoint of a broader maternal health crisis with multiple democratic consequences. The survivors of pregnancy-related near-deaths — a category 70 times more common than maternal death — experience long-term health sequelae that reduce workforce participation, civic capacity, and life expectancy. The economic analysis from Domain 44 applies here: women denied quality maternal healthcare in the peripartum period experience measurably higher rates of subsequent poverty, housing instability, and reduced labor force participation — the same conditions that reduce civic participation. The communities experiencing concentrated maternal mortality are the same communities facing concentrated voting barriers: majority-Black counties in the South, rural counties in Appalachia and the Mississippi Delta, Indigenous communities in the Great Plains and Alaska.

**Sources**: [Racial Disparities in Maternal and Infant Health — KFF](https://www.kff.org/racial-equity-and-health-policy/racial-disparities-in-maternal-and-infant-health-current-status-and-key-issues/); [Eliminating Racial Disparities in Maternal Mortality — Center for American Progress](https://www.americanprogress.org/article/eliminating-racial-disparities-maternal-infant-mortality/); [Disparities in Maternal Mortality — PMC/NIH](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10514681/); [Solving the Black Maternal Health Crisis — JHU Bloomberg SPH](https://publichealth.jhu.edu/2023/solving-the-black-maternal-health-crisis)

### 4.2 The 2025-2026 Policy Reversals

The Trump administration's March 2025 HHS reorganization eliminated the CDC's Division of Reproductive Health and halted community-based maternal health grants. The CDC's maternal health data collection capacity — which produces the demographic mortality surveillance that enables both policy intervention and accountability — has been compromised. The administration's January 2026 decision to lay off most staff in the CDC's Division of Reproductive Health coincides with a documented increase in maternal mortality in states that enacted total abortion bans after Dobbs (Domain 44's 59 excess maternal deaths analysis).

The intersection with Domain 44 (Reproductive Freedom) is direct: the healthcare architecture for maternal mortality — access to emergency obstetric care, postpartum follow-up, treatment of pregnancy complications including eclampsia, hemorrhage, and infection — is the same healthcare infrastructure that determines whether abortion restrictions kill women. This domain's analytical contribution distinct from Domain 44 is the broader healthcare architecture: the specific provider distribution, hospital obstetric unit closures, and insurance coverage gaps that determine whether women in rural and lower-income areas can access adequate maternal care regardless of abortion policy. Between 2011 and 2021, 267 rural hospitals closed their obstetric units — a 25% decline in rural obstetric capacity. This closure trend predates Dobbs and reflects the economic non-viability of obstetric care in markets where most patients are on Medicaid (which reimburses below cost) and the hospital cannot sustain the staffing required. The policy solution is not only abortion access but healthcare system investment in the specific infrastructure that makes maternal survival possible.

**Sources**: [Maternal Mortality in the US — Policy Center for Maternal Mental Health](https://policycentermmh.org/maternal-mortality-in-the-u-s-a-declining-trend-with-persistent-racial-disparities-in-the-black-population/); [The Health Care System and Racial Disparities in Maternal Mortality — CAP](https://www.americanprogress.org/article/health-care-system-racial-disparities-maternal-mortality/); [Listen to the Whispers before They Become Screams — PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9914526/)

## 5. Pandemic Response Federalism: Healthcare Governance as Democratic Architecture

### 5.1 The Structural Failure

The COVID-19 pandemic killed more than 1.1 million Americans — more than any war in US history except the Civil War. The United States' per-capita death toll significantly exceeded those of comparable wealthy democracies. The scholarly and policy consensus on why is documented in multiple peer-reviewed analyses: federalism's structural fragmentation prevented a coordinated national response, partisan polarization weaponized public health recommendations, and the pre-existing deficiencies in the public health infrastructure — decades of underfunding, understaffing, and underinvestment — left states unable to respond even when they chose to.

The democratic-design dimension: public health governance is simultaneously democratic governance. The decision about who has authority to issue public health orders — federal vs. state, executive vs. legislative, expert vs. political — determines who lives and dies during a pandemic. States that concentrated authority in governors without legislative checks issued more effective but less democratically accountable pandemic policies. States that diffused authority across legislative chambers, county commissions, and competing elected officials issued more democratically accountable but less effective policies. The trade-off between effectiveness and accountability in public health emergency governance is a democratic design problem that the pandemic exposed as unresolved.

The Publius journal analysis ("Governing in a Polarized Era: Federalism and the Response of US State and Federal Governments to COVID-19") documents the pattern: "the United States moved away from collaborative federalism to a more contentious form characterized by significant state-to-state variation in policy largely driven by partisanship." This is the same pattern of federalism-as-obstruction that Domain 9 (Federalism) documents across other policy domains — but in the healthcare context, the obstruction directly killed people rather than merely reducing policy efficiency.

The post-pandemic rollback of public health infrastructure is accelerating the structural vulnerability. The CDC's budget has been cut and its workforce reduced; the NIH's proposed 40% budget reduction would devastate the research infrastructure that produces pandemic preparedness knowledge; the ACL's dismantling removes the community health infrastructure that coordinates care for the most vulnerable populations. The next pandemic — and every epidemiologist projects that there will be one — will find a public health infrastructure significantly weaker than the already-insufficient infrastructure that failed in 2020-2022.

**Sources**: [American Democracy and Pandemic Security — Brown University Pandemic Center](https://pandemics.sph.brown.edu/pandemics.sph.brown.edu/publications/pandemic-response); [How Federalism Has Harmed Public Health — The Regulatory Review](https://www.theregreview.org/2020/12/23/chung-federalism-harmed-public-health/); [Governing in a Polarized Era: Federalism and COVID-19 — Publius/Oxford Academic](https://academic.oup.com/publius/article/51/4/650/6335878); [Federalism Complicates the Response to COVID-19 — PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32464666/)

## 6. FQHCs as Democratic Infrastructure: Voter Registration in Healthcare Settings

### 6.1 The Infrastructure Opportunity

Community health centers — particularly the 1,400+ Federally Qualified Health Centers (FQHCs) with over 14,000 service delivery sites — are the most promising underutilized voter registration infrastructure in the country. FQHCs serve more than 28 million patients. Their patient population is 62% racial and ethnic minorities, 90% at or below 200% of the federal poverty level, and 24% best served in a language other than English. These are precisely the populations that face the greatest structural barriers to voter registration through conventional channels and that have the most at stake in the Medicaid, healthcare, and public health policy decisions made by the officials they could elect.

HRSA (the Health Resources and Services Administration) has issued explicit guidance encouraging FQHCs to support nonpartisan voter registration efforts as a means of reducing barriers to civic engagement. Executive Order 14019 (Biden, 2021) directed federal agencies to take proactive steps to promote voter registration at federal service sites; FQHCs as federally funded facilities were explicitly included. The practical implementation: Vot-ER, a nonpartisan nonprofit, has developed a healthcare-based voter registration program present in more than 700 healthcare sites, offering staff badges with QR codes directing patients to check registration status, register to vote, or request absentee ballots. A clinician-led registration drive in two FQHCs in the Bronx successfully registered 89% of eligible voters in the 2012 election, reaching disproportionately young (41.9%) and racial and ethnic minority (39.6%) patients.

The democratic significance is structural, not merely opportunistic. Healthcare is one of the few civic institutions that reliably contacts low-income, minority, and disabled populations — populations that face the greatest democratic exclusion — in a context of trust, repeated interaction, and established relationship. Banks, employers, and government agencies can also reach these populations, but healthcare providers have a specific advantage: patients visit FQHCs multiple times per year, have established relationships with providers, and are present in a context where the provider is specifically positioned as an advocate for the patient's wellbeing. Voter registration in healthcare settings is not a partisan act — it is a civic act with proven democratic infrastructure potential.

**Sources**: [Voter Registration and Health Centers — HRSA](https://bphc.hrsa.gov/compliance/scope-project/voter-requirements); [Increasing Voter Participation Through Health Care-Based Voter Registration — PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11193121/); [Expanding Voter Registration to Clinical Settings — PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10480081/); [Addressing Health Disparities Through Voter Engagement — PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7032920/); [The Growing Role of Health Care Settings in Voter Registration — Journalist's Resource](https://journalistsresource.org/home/voting-and-health-care/); [Fact Sheet: Nonpartisan Voter Registration at Health Care Institutions — AAMC](https://www.aamchealthjustice.org/our-work/policy/voter-fact-sheet); [An Underused Treatment Strategy: Voter Enfranchisement — PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7571786/)

### 6.2 The Threat to FQHC Infrastructure

Executive Order 14019 was rescinded by Trump's January 2025 executive orders on his first day in office. The HRSA guidance encouraging voter registration was withdrawn. The OBBBA's healthcare cuts are expected to reduce FQHC grant funding, limiting the patient capacity and service hours that provide the contact volume necessary for effective voter registration programs. The Trump administration's immigration enforcement posture — including the ICE-Medicaid data-sharing agreement (documented in Domain 50) that has produced a chilling effect on Medicaid enrollment in immigrant communities — is also reducing FQHC patient volumes as immigrant families avoid healthcare settings out of deportation fear.

The voter registration opportunity at FQHCs is not only threatened by direct policy — it is threatened by the healthcare coverage losses that reduce the patient volume at FQHCs. If 7.5-10 million people lose Medicaid coverage under OBBBA projections, the contact volume at FQHCs that would produce voter registration interactions declines proportionately. The coverage-registration pipeline that Domain 50 documents for NVRA Section 7 operates analogously at FQHCs: fewer covered patients means fewer registration contacts.

## 7. ADA Digital Accessibility and the 2026 Deadline

In a development that intersects healthcare, disability rights, and democratic participation, new ADA rules applying to state and local government websites took effect April 24, 2026. Government websites allow members of the public to apply for state benefits, register to vote, access mass transit schedules, and access civic information — all functions with direct democratic participation implications. The new rules require state and local governments in areas with populations of 50,000 or greater to ensure their websites comply with Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.1 AA — the accessibility standard ensuring that screen readers, captioning, and other assistive technologies can access digital government services.

Healthcare providers receiving HHS funding face parallel ADA Section 504 updated requirements: accessible websites and mobile applications, including patient portals, telehealth platforms, and digital forms, were required to be fully operational for people with disabilities by 2026. For the 61 million Americans with disabilities, digital accessibility to government services — including voter registration portals, election information, and civic resources — is a prerequisite for democratic participation that physical accessibility alone cannot satisfy.

The enforcement challenge is identical to the physical accessibility enforcement challenge: there is no proactive certification process, complaints are investigated reactively, and under-resourcing of DOJ civil rights enforcement reduces the practical effect of the requirements. But the 2026 ADA digital deadline creates an accountability moment: civil rights organizations with digital accessibility expertise can document compliance failures, create litigation opportunities, and pressure state and local governments to remediate their civic digital infrastructure.

## 8. Reform Architecture

### 8.1 Medicaid Expansion and Medical Debt Relief

Every state that has not expanded Medicaid under the ACA should be targeted for expansion advocacy, particularly in the 12 holdout states with large uninsured populations in the South and inland West. The experimental evidence from Oregon is the most rigorous argument available: Medicaid expansion increases voter turnout. State attorneys general in non-expansion states who are concerned about suppressed civic participation have a specific legal theory — NVRA Section 7 enforcement — and now also an experimental evidence base for healthcare access as voting rights policy.

Medical debt relief should be pursued at both federal and state levels: restoration of the CFPB rule barring medical debt from credit reports, state legislation modeled on the 22 states that have enacted relief programs, and hospital-level policy changes requiring income-based charity care before debt collection. The Undue Medical Debt model — purchasing debt portfolios at cents on the dollar and cancelling them — has demonstrated effectiveness and broad bipartisan support in polling.

### 8.2 FQHC Voter Registration Expansion

The rescission of Executive Order 14019 should be reversed by a Democratic administration. In the interim, FQHCs can voluntarily adopt voter registration as a standard element of patient intake in states where the NVRA and state law permit, and Vot-ER's existing 700-site network can be expanded through philanthropic funding. State-level NVRA enforcement requiring all public assistance agencies (including FQHCs that receive state Medicaid funding) to offer voter registration should be pursued through state AG offices.

### 8.3 Disability Voting Access

Mandatory ADA certification for all polling places, with proactive compliance surveys before each election, should replace the current complaint-only enforcement model. Mail voting — which enables disabled voters to participate without confronting physical accessibility barriers — should be universally available in all states without excuse requirements. The ACL should be restored as an independent entity within HHS, with its funding restored and protected through authorizing legislation.

### 8.4 Pandemic Preparedness as Democratic Design

CDC pandemic preparedness funding should be maintained at or above pre-2025 levels and protected from executive budget authority through mandatory minimum appropriations in the authorizing statute. The Public Health Infrastructure Emergency Fund (a proposal in the preparedness literature) would create a reserve that cannot be raided during non-emergency periods for other purposes — addressing the structural tendency to underinvest in public health between pandemics. Federal-state pandemic coordination protocols should be codified in statute, including minimum performance standards for state public health emergency response and federal reimbursement contingent on state compliance.
