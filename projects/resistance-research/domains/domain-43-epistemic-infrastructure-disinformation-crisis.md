**Research completed**: May 7, 2026
**Crisis window**: Active and accelerating — 2026 midterms are the first US electoral cycle with political deepfakes at industrial scale; EU AI Act deepfake labeling enforcement begins August 2, 2026; Corporation for Public Broadcasting shut down September 30, 2025 after CPB funding rescission; no federal law exists prohibiting AI deepfakes in political campaigns

**Cross-domain connections**: Domain 8 (Media Ecosystem — local news collapse as the delivery mechanism for disinformation), Domain 36 (AI Governance — algorithmic accountability for AI-generated content), Domain 37 (Federal Executive Interference in 2026 Midterms — deepfakes as a specific election interference vector), Domain 27 (Higher Education — universities as epistemic institutions under direct executive attack), Domain 7 (Rights and Civil Liberties — First Amendment constraints on content-based speech regulation)

## Executive Summary

Democracy requires a shared epistemic foundation — a common enough set of facts that deliberation and persuasion are possible, and that evidence can constrain political argument. The United States in May 2026 is confronting the systematic degradation of that foundation through three converging forces: AI-generated deepfakes deployed at industrial scale in the 2026 midterm campaign cycle, an information environment algorithmically optimized for engagement over accuracy, and the deliberate destruction of the public media institutions that have historically served as epistemic anchor points. The World Economic Forum's Global Risks Report 2026 identified misinformation and disinformation as the #2 global risk on a two-year outlook — ahead of armed conflict, climate action failure, and economic volatility. What makes the 2026 US context acutely dangerous is not that deepfakes are uniquely powerful in deceiving individual voters (they are not — University of Chicago research published in the Journal of Politics in April 2025 found political deepfakes "approximately as credible as other fake media"), but that they trigger what researchers call the "liar's dividend": the ability of political actors to dismiss authentic evidence as AI-generated, undermining the evidentiary foundation on which both democratic debate and legal accountability rest. This domain analyzes the epistemic crisis not as a technology or media problem but as a democratic design failure — what institutional structures would rebuild the shared epistemic foundation that democratic deliberation requires, consistent with First Amendment constraints, and what the international comparative evidence shows about the conditions under which democracies have maintained robust epistemic resilience.

## 1. The 2026 Deepfake Crisis: Scale, Deployment, and Democratic Impact

### 1.1 Industrial-Scale Deployment in the 2026 Midterm Cycle

The 2026 US midterm elections have established political deepfakes as standard campaign practice. Incidents documented in the current cycle include:

**National Republican Senatorial Committee (NRSC)** released a video depicting Texas Democratic Senate candidate James Talarico appearing to make inflammatory statements he never made. CNN reported the incident on March 13, 2026, characterizing it as among the first cases in which a national party campaign committee deployed a deepfake of a specific candidate for an entire clip — not merely edited or dubbed audio over authentic footage, but a synthesized video recreation. The NRSC's video included a small "AI Generated" text disclosure, technically compliant with the disclosure requirements of the 28 states that have enacted state-level AI political content laws, while remaining a deliberate deception for voters unfamiliar with the disclosure's legal significance.

**Republican US Representative Mike Collins of Georgia** created a deepfake video in which incumbent Democratic Senator Jon Ossoff appears to say: "I just voted to keep the government shut down. They say it would hurt farmers, but I wouldn't know. I've only seen a farm on Instagram." Ossoff made no such statement. The video was distributed through social media amplification networks without platform-level provenance labeling.

**Republican Committee for Loudoun County, Virginia** released three AI-generated ads attacking Governor Abigail Spanberger that included footage of her apparently endorsing positions — "working hard to bring in commie socialist Marxism, free stuff for illegals, gun grabs and erasing gender norms" — she had never advocated. The AI-generation was documented and reported; the ads circulated at scale before corrective coverage could limit their reach.

The OECD's AI Incident Monitor, which tracks documented AI incidents affecting democratic processes, catalogued multiple 2026 US midterm deepfake incidents as of March 28, 2026 — with its tracking substantially behind the actual deployment rate. The Center for Foreign Interference Research documented the pattern as "proliferation despite regulatory efforts," noting that the regulatory patchwork of state laws with disclosure-only requirements has created a compliance-as-cover dynamic in which campaigns can deploy deepfakes while technically satisfying disclosure obligations that most voters do not understand.

### 1.2 The Technology Shift That Made 2026 Different

The 2024 cycle's predicted "AI apocalypse" did not materialize — not because the technology was absent but because production costs were higher, detection was more reliable, and the social norm against AI-generated campaign content had not yet broken. The 2026 cycle is qualitatively different on three dimensions:

**Production accessibility**: Generative AI video production tools available to individual users with basic computing skills have reduced the cost of a photorealistic deepfake from tens of thousands of dollars (2022) to approximately $8–50 (2026), depending on length and fidelity requirements. The WEF Global Risks Report 2026 documents this as the threshold crossing: "Deepfakes have crossed a critical threshold in 2026, have improved and eliminated earlier tell-tale glitches and are now accessible to anyone with a smartphone."

**Normative breakdown**: Once the NRSC — the campaign committee of the Senate Republican caucus, not a fringe actor — deployed a deepfake against a Senate candidate, the implicit norm against national-level deepfakes in federal elections collapsed. Downstream campaigns at the state and local level interpreted this as permission. The Animal House analysis of the 2026 cycle documents this cascading normalization.

**Scale through amplification**: Deepfakes are not primarily dangerous as individual videos — they are dangerous when social media algorithmic amplification multiplies their reach before fact-checking or correction can intervene. The American Prospect's April 2026 analysis documented that deepfakes deployed in the 2026 cycle generated millions of impressions through social media recommendation systems before platforms identified and labeled the synthetic content, if they labeled it at all.

### 1.3 Survey Evidence on Democratic Impact

A voter survey from the 2026 cycle found that nearly 50% of respondents reported that deepfakes had "some influence" on their election decisions, even among respondents who described themselves as distrustful of AI-generated content. This finding is consistent with the University of Chicago Journal of Politics research finding that political deepfakes are "as credible as other fake media and (sometimes) real media" — the credibility of deepfakes is not that they are uniquely convincing but that they are not substantially less convincing than other forms of misinformation, which voters also process under conditions of motivated reasoning and limited verification time.

The Reuters Institute's global survey, cited in the WEF Global Risks Report 2026, found that 58% of respondents globally are concerned about "how to distinguish truth from falsehood in online news." In the US context, this generalized epistemic anxiety creates the conditions for the liar's dividend, discussed in Section 3.

## 2. Structural Factors: Why the Epistemic Infrastructure Is Failing

### 2.1 The Public Media Defunding

The Corporation for Public Broadcasting (CPB) — the federal institution that distributed $1.1 billion in congressionally appropriated public media funding — shut down September 30, 2025, after the Trump administration and a Republican Congress rescinded all funding in the first time in the CPB's nearly 60-year history. The impact on the epistemic infrastructure is structural and cascading:

Approximately 1,000 NPR member stations received a substantial portion of their operating revenue through CPB grants. Stations in rural communities, tribal territories, and low-income urban areas were disproportionately dependent on federal funding, with some deriving 40–60% of their operating budgets from CPB. These are exactly the communities where the commercial local news market has already failed — where public broadcasting was the remaining epistemic anchor. KBRW, a tribally licensed public radio station serving Alaska Native communities, faced the prospect of going dark, cutting entire communities off from local news and emergency services alerts simultaneously.

PBS received approximately 15% of its revenue from federal sources, with member stations averaging similar proportions. The CPB rescission does not merely reduce funding — it removes the institutional infrastructure that coordinated grants, ensured geographic coverage, and provided the organizational backbone of the public broadcasting system. Restoration would require not just appropriations but institutional reconstruction.

The epistemic function of public broadcasting — producing journalism whose incentive structure is public service rather than engagement maximization — is not replicable by the commercial media market. Nonprofit news organizations, local news startups, and philanthropic journalism funds exist and are growing, but their combined coverage of the communities served by the 1,000 CPB-dependent stations is a fraction of what public broadcasting provided. The defunding is not a budget decision with a budget solution; it is a democratic design decision to eliminate a category of epistemic infrastructure.

### 2.2 Local News Collapse and Information Deserts

The commercial local news collapse predates the CPB defunding and compounds it. More than 2,500 local newspapers have closed since 2005. The 2026 election cycle is being conducted with substantial portions of the country — including competitive congressional districts in Pennsylvania, Wisconsin, Arizona, and Georgia — served by few or no local news organizations capable of covering down-ballot races, election administration, or state legislative activity.

Information deserts — geographic areas where no local news organization provides consistent coverage — create democratic vulnerability at two levels. First, voters in those areas lack the informational foundation to evaluate candidates for local and state offices, which determines who oversees elections, state redistricting, and the implementation of federal law. Second, the absence of local fact-checking infrastructure means that deepfakes, fabricated claims, and disinformation circulate in these communities without the corrective pressure that local news coverage provides. A deepfake of a state legislative candidate deployed in a district with no local newspaper faces no immediate journalistic response.

### 2.3 Algorithmic Amplification Without Accountability

The social media platforms that have become the primary political information environment for a majority of Americans operate algorithmic recommendation systems that optimize for engagement — which in practice means emotional arousal, novelty, and conflict — rather than accuracy or epistemic value. This is not a bug in the algorithmic design; it is the intended output of systems optimized for the metric they are designed to maximize.

The EU's Digital Services Act (DSA), which became applicable to Very Large Online Platforms in August 2023, requires platforms to conduct annual risk assessments of systemic risks to democratic processes, submit to external audits, and provide researchers with data access. No equivalent US federal requirement exists. The Section 230 immunity regime, which protects platforms from liability for user-generated content, has been interpreted to protect platforms from liability for their own algorithmic amplification decisions — meaning that a platform's choice to algorithmically recommend a deepfake video to ten million users generates no legal liability under current US law even if the platform has actual knowledge that the content is false.

The Deepfake Liability Act (H.R.6334, 119th Congress) introduced by Representative Auchincloss would condition Section 230 immunity on platforms implementing a duty of care — basic steps requiring responsiveness to complaints about digital forgeries. It has not received a House floor vote. The broader reform landscape includes proposals to sunset Section 230 entirely (Hageman's Sunset Act, Durbin-Graham Senate equivalent) — a maximalist approach that would expose platforms to liability far beyond the disinformation context and that faces significant constitutional questions.

## 3. The Liar's Dividend: Authentic Evidence Denied

### 3.1 Mechanism and Evidence

The "liar's dividend," a concept developed by legal scholar Bobby Chesney and cybersecurity expert Danielle Citron, describes the second-order effect of deepfake proliferation: even if a voter correctly identifies a specific deepfake as fabricated, the general awareness that authentic-appearing video content can be AI-generated creates a reservoir of doubt that political actors can exploit to discredit authentic documentation.

The mechanism works as follows: (1) political actors deploy fabricated content depicting opponents in damaging scenarios; (2) the fabricated content is subsequently exposed as synthetic, increasing general public awareness that video evidence can be fabricated; (3) political actors subsequently invoke the liar's dividend when confronted with authentic documentation of their own conduct, dismissing it as AI-generated to audiences who now have reason to doubt authentic video.

Real-world manifestations documented in the research literature include: Guy Reffitt's defense attorney arguing at his January 6 prosecution that prosecution video evidence was deepfaked; former Spanish Foreign Minister Alfonso Dastis characterizing authentic images of police violence in Catalonia as "fake photos"; and the broader pattern of political figures dismissing authentic audio recordings as AI-manipulated.

The Brennan Center for Justice's research on the liar's dividend documents that the threshold for political actors to deploy the dividend is not high — claiming artificiality does not require proof, merely assertion, and the cognitive seed of doubt is planted at minimal reputational cost. In a high-deepfake environment, the assertion "that video is AI-generated" becomes available to any political actor as a universal denial of authentic documented conduct.

### 3.2 Democratic Accountability Implications

The liar's dividend directly undermines democratic accountability mechanisms that depend on authentic documentation. Congressional oversight of executive conduct — including the conduct documented in Domains 2, 19f, 28, and 29 of this framework — relies on video and audio evidence of public statements, testimony, and documented actions. If political actors can routinely and credibly claim authentic documentation is synthetic, the evidentiary foundation for accountability investigations collapses.

The January 6 prosecution context makes this concrete: the convictions of Oath Keepers and Proud Boys leaders that DOJ subsequently moved to vacate (documented in the transitional justice domain candidate) depended substantially on video evidence. A future criminal prosecution of insurrectionary conduct in a high-deepfake environment, in which defense counsel can claim that prosecution video evidence is AI-generated, will face epistemic challenges that did not exist in 2021.

Adam Rose of Stanford's Starling Lab has articulated the stakes precisely: "Without visual evidence, we lack the ability to understand what's happening and to make judgments both in a court of public opinion and in a court of law."

## 4. The Regulatory Landscape: Federal Paralysis and State Patchwork

### 4.1 Federal Regulatory Gap

No federal law prohibits the use of AI-generated deepfakes in political advertising or campaign communications. The regulatory gap operates across three agency jurisdictions:

**FEC jurisdiction**: In July 2023, following a Public Citizen petition requesting clarification that existing laws against fraudulent misrepresentation apply to deliberately deceptive AI content, the FEC considered but declined to initiate a rulemaking on AI in political advertising. The FEC's authority covers online political advertising for federal candidates but does not extend to television, radio, or state/local elections.

**FCC jurisdiction**: The FCC proposed rules requiring on-air and written disclosure of AI-generated content in radio and television political advertisements. These proposals do not apply to online advertising, which is outside FCC authority. The jurisdictional gap between FEC (online only) and FCC (broadcast only) leaves precisely the most-used political communication channel — social media — without disclosure requirements from either agency.

**FTC jurisdiction**: The FTC's deceptive practices authority covers commercial contexts but has limited application to political speech, which receives First Amendment protection that commercial advertising does not. FTC rulemaking prohibiting AI-generated impersonation of public figures in commercial contexts is more constitutionally defensible than political advertising regulation, but it reaches a narrower portion of the deepfake harm.

The Trump administration has signaled opposition to AI regulation in all forms, making federal rulemaking through executive agency action unlikely in the current political environment. The AI Action Plan released in April 2026 focuses on "removing barriers" to AI deployment and does not address political deepfakes.

### 4.2 State Patchwork

As of January 2026, 28 states had enacted laws related to deepfakes in political communications. The modal state law requires disclosure when political advertising contains AI-generated content, typically with a disclosure obligation triggered 90–180 days before an election. A smaller number of states prohibit AI-generated deepfakes in political advertising without disclosure; California's AB 602 (2019) and AB 730 (2019) are the most comprehensive examples at state level.

The constitutional vulnerability of state deepfake laws is documented in the X Corp. v. Minnesota litigation. X Corporation sued Minnesota Attorney General Keith Ellison to block enforcement of Minnesota's deepfake political advertising law, which prohibits deepfake videos distributed within 90 days of an election that a reasonable person would not identify as fake and that were intended to injure a candidate. X argued the law violated the First Amendment by imposing overly broad restrictions on political speech and threatening platforms with criminal liability for moderation decisions. The federal district court dismissed X's primary claim in 2025, but the litigation has produced the first federal circuit-level analysis of the constitutional boundary for political deepfake regulation.

Cornell Law Review's analysis in the Journal of Law and Public Policy (October 2025) documents "the legal gray zone of deepfake political speech" — the constitutional terrain in which disclosure requirements (likely permissible under Zauderer v. Office of Consumers' Counsel) are distinguishable from prohibition mandates (more vulnerable under Reed v. Town of Gilbert content-based speech doctrine). The distinction matters for federal legislative design.

### 4.3 The EU Regulatory Model as Comparative Anchor

The EU's AI Act Article 50, with enforcement beginning August 2, 2026, creates the most comprehensive regulatory framework for AI-generated content transparency in any major democracy. Key provisions directly applicable to deepfakes:

- AI systems that create synthetic content must mark their outputs as artificially generated, using machine-readable metadata, imperceptible watermarking, and fingerprinting where technically feasible
- Deployers must disclose when AI is used to create realistic synthetic content, including deepfakes, through notification to end users
- The "Common Icon" proposed in the Draft Code of Practice on Transparency of AI-Generated Content (published December 17, 2025; final version expected June 2026) provides a standardized visual disclosure label pending an EU-wide interactive symbol

The Article 50 framework addresses the NRSC's disclosure-as-cover problem: requiring machine-readable metadata and standardized labeling creates a detection infrastructure that empowers automated platform-level verification rather than depending on voter-level identification of tiny-text disclosures. A viewer of NRSC-style content encountering an Article 50-compliant disclosure would see a standardized icon and have access to metadata confirming synthetic origin — not a barely-visible text disclaimer.

The EU DSA's systemic risk assessment requirements, applicable to Very Large Online Platforms since 2023, require platforms to audit their recommendation systems for risks to democratic processes annually, with external auditor verification. This creates accountability for algorithmic amplification of deepfakes that current US law does not.

## 5. International Comparative Evidence: Democratic Epistemic Resilience

### 5.1 Finland: Systemic Media Literacy as Democratic Defense

Finland ranked first (tied with Denmark, Ireland, and the Netherlands) in the Media Literacy Index 2026, maintaining its decades-long leadership in media literacy among European democracies. The Finnish model is the most documented and thoroughly evaluated case of systemic epistemic resilience infrastructure.

Finland integrated media literacy into the national curriculum in 2014, mandating it across all grade levels from preschool (age 3) through university. The curriculum includes: critical evaluation of sources across all media formats; recognition of manipulation techniques including emotional appeals and framing; and, since 2025-2026, specific AI literacy modules training students to identify and assess AI-generated content. Teachers are required to integrate AI literacy into existing subject curricula — not as a standalone technology class but as an embedded critical skill across subjects.

The 2026 Euronews reporting on Finland's media literacy program documents that Finnish students approaching the 2026 national elections were explicitly trained to: (1) identify the provenance markers of AI-generated content; (2) trace the funding and institutional source of political messaging; and (3) apply structural skepticism to emotionally intense political content regardless of apparent authenticity.

The Media Literacy Index methodology breaks resilience into three weighted components: education (45% of the score, measured through PISA results and enrollment data), media freedom (40%, measured through Reporters without Borders and Freedom House data), and interpersonal trust with e-participation (15%). Finland's score is driven by education and media freedom simultaneously — the systemic media literacy investment is supported by a media environment in which public broadcasting (Yle, the Finnish Broadcasting Company, with secure multi-year funding) provides epistemic anchor content.

The Finland model's transferability to the US context requires careful analysis. Finland is a small (5.5 million population), highly educated, ethnically homogeneous society with a strong state capacity tradition. The decentralized US educational system — governed at the state and district level, with no national curriculum — cannot replicate the Finnish model through federal mandate. But the model's components are modular: media literacy investment at the state level, public broadcasting as epistemic anchor, and curriculum integration are all achievable through state-level action and federal incentives without requiring a nationalized curriculum.

### 5.2 Taiwan: Civic Technology as Epistemic Resilience Infrastructure

Taiwan's approach to epistemic resilience is distinctively non-governmental: the g0v (pronounced "gov-zero") civic technology movement, launched in 2012, has built a parallel public information infrastructure through open-source civic technology that supplements and challenges official information channels.

Cofacts, the most directly relevant g0v platform to disinformation resistance, is a community-driven fact-checking system built on LINE (Taiwan's primary messaging app, analogous to WhatsApp in the US context). Since 2016, Cofacts has operated a hybrid human-AI fact-checking chatbot: users forward suspicious messages to the Cofacts bot, which queries a community database of verified claims and returns fact-checks, often within minutes of a false claim's circulation. A Cornell University study (2023) found that Cofacts responds more quickly than professional fact-checkers and achieves comparable accuracy — demonstrating that community-level epistemic infrastructure can achieve professional-level outcomes at civic scale.

Taiwan's disinformation resistance model has the following democratic-design features the US context currently lacks:

- **Community verification infrastructure** that operates at the speed of social media circulation, rather than the slower cycle of professional fact-checking organizations
- **Civic tech as parallel government** — g0v's naming convention ("replace the 'o' in gov with a '0'") reflects its function as a parallel open-source government that builds public information tools when official channels fail
- **Institutional integration**: contributing to Cofacts is part of the media-competency curriculum in Taiwanese schools, creating a civic participation pathway that reinforces epistemic resilience through active engagement rather than passive consumption

Taiwan's epistemic resilience model was stress-tested during the 2024 elections — which Chinese-origin deepfakes and disinformation campaigns targeted intensively — with a result that the OECD and Freedom House assess as a successful democratic defense.

### 5.3 Ireland's 2025 Presidential Election: Deepfakes and Democratic Recovery

The WEF Global Risks Report 2026 documents a deepfake incident in Ireland's 2025 presidential election that is the closest international analog to what US 2026 deepfakes are attempting: a deepfake video falsely depicted the eventual winning candidate withdrawing his candidature, including fabricated footage of national broadcasters "confirming" the news. The video circulated widely in the days before the election.

Ireland's response demonstrated the effectiveness of media literacy combined with rapid official correction: public broadcasting (RTE) issued immediate and prominent corrections; the electoral commission made a public statement within hours; and the social media platforms operating in Ireland under the DSA legal framework were required to respond to takedown requests within 24 hours under the DSA's emergency response provisions. The winning candidate won with a majority that exceeded pre-deepfake polling — suggesting the deepfake's net electoral effect was limited, though the Ireland case involves a smaller, less polarized electorate than the US 2026 context.

The Ireland case identifies the institutional combination that limits deepfake damage: fast official correction through trusted public media, electoral commission authority to respond publicly, and platform accountability requirements that enable rapid takedown. None of these three elements is fully functional in the current US context.

## 6. Content Credentials and Technical Infrastructure

### 6.1 The C2PA Standard: Promise and Limitations

The Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity (C2PA) — founded in February 2021 by Adobe, Arm, BBC, Intel, and Microsoft, now with more than 6,000 members and affiliates as of January 2026 — has developed a technical standard for embedding verifiable provenance metadata in digital content: images, video, audio, and documents.

The C2PA Content Credentials standard creates a tamper-evident chain of custody: when content is created with a C2PA-compliant tool, it carries embedded metadata identifying the creation tool, the date, and any subsequent edits. A viewer using a C2PA-enabled platform can verify the content's provenance chain — whether it was created by an AI generation tool, what edits were applied, and whether the metadata has been tampered with since creation.

Consumer hardware adoption has progressed: the Samsung Galaxy S25 and Google Pixel 10 natively sign photographs with C2PA credentials; Leica deployed C2PA in professional cameras in 2023. LinkedIn and TikTok support or preserve C2PA credentials at scale. The EU AI Act Article 50's requirement for machine-readable disclosure on AI-generated content directly maps to C2PA's technical capabilities.

The limitation that limits C2PA's current effectiveness as an epistemic defense is metadata stripping. Most social media platforms and content distribution intermediaries strip embedded metadata when content is uploaded, compressed, or recoded for platform distribution. A C2PA-signed video loses its provenance chain when uploaded to a platform that does not preserve C2PA metadata — which means the vast majority of deepfakes distributed through social media currently arrive at viewers without any provenance credential, even if the original content was created with a tool that generated C2PA metadata.

The gap between C2PA's technical capability and its current deployment effectiveness is the critical reform target: if major social media platforms were required to preserve (and preferably verify) C2PA metadata as a condition of platform operations, the technical infrastructure for systematic deepfake provenance verification would exist. EU AI Act Article 50 creates this requirement for platforms operating in the EU; the US has no equivalent mandate.

### 6.2 Detection Technology and Its Limits

Automated deepfake detection tools — AI classifiers trained to identify synthetic content — have detection accuracy rates that are systematically lower than the production sophistication of the leading deepfake generation tools. This is an adversarial machine learning dynamic: detection tools are trained on known deepfake samples, while generation tools are continuously improved to evade detection signatures.

The Truescreen analysis of deepfakes in the 2026 elections documents that detection technology available to platforms, fact-checkers, and voters is "unavailable to most voters" and unreliable enough that expert analysts regularly disagree on specific contested videos. The epistemic implications are significant: in the absence of provenance infrastructure (C2PA), the fall-back of detection technology cannot close the reliability gap.

This means the reform architecture cannot depend on detection as its primary epistemic defense. Detection is a second-line check; provenance (C2PA) and disclosure (Article 50-style labeling at creation) are the primary defenses.

## 7. Reform Architecture: Rebuilding the Epistemic Infrastructure

### 7.1 Tier 1 — Constitutionally Uncontested: Disclosure and Provenance Infrastructure

These reforms require no content-based speech restriction, raise minimal First Amendment concerns, and are achievable through administrative rulemaking or targeted legislation.

**Political Advertising AI Disclosure Mandate**: Federal legislation requiring disclosure of AI-generated content in political advertising across all media — including social media, which falls outside FCC jurisdiction. The disclosure requirement should be modeled on the FEC's existing paid media disclosure requirements, with technical specifications requiring machine-readable provenance metadata (C2PA standard) in addition to human-readable disclosure. The key First Amendment distinction is between disclosure (compelled speech about the nature of the content) and prohibition (restriction of the content itself) — the Supreme Court has upheld disclosure requirements under intermediate scrutiny when they are reasonably related to preventing voter deception (Buckley v. Valeo framework, reinforced in Citizens United's holding that disclosure is more constitutional than restriction).

**FEC AI Content Rulemaking**: The FEC's July 2023 decision not to initiate rulemaking on AI in political advertising should be revisited through a new petition process. The documented deployment of deepfakes in the 2026 cycle provides the evidentiary record that was absent in 2023. An FEC interpretive rule clarifying that the existing prohibition on fraudulent misrepresentation in political advertising (52 U.S.C. § 30121) applies to deliberately deceptive AI-generated content that is materially false would not require new legislation.

**Platform C2PA Metadata Preservation Requirement**: Section 230 immunity for platforms should be conditioned on preserving C2PA provenance metadata for political content — not requiring platforms to verify authenticity but preventing the destruction of provenance evidence that creation-time tools have embedded. This is a structural requirement (data preservation), not a content moderation requirement, and raises no First Amendment issues. The Deepfake Liability Act (H.R.6334) provides a legislative vehicle for attaching this requirement.

**FTC AI Impersonation Rule**: The FTC has existing rulemaking authority to prohibit deceptive practices. A targeted rule prohibiting AI-generated impersonation of identified public figures in commercial and political contexts — without requiring proof of intent to deceive, but conditioning the prohibition on material falsity — would be within existing FTC authority and would address the most dangerous deepfake category (opponent impersonation) while leaving space for satire and commentary.

### 7.2 Tier 2 — Platform Accountability: Algorithmic Auditing and DSA-Model Requirements

**Mandatory Systemic Risk Assessment for Political Content**: Federal legislation requiring Very Large Online Platforms (those with more than 50 million monthly active users) to conduct annual systemic risk assessments of their recommendation algorithms' impact on democratic processes — modeled on DSA Article 34. Assessments to be submitted to an independent auditor and published publicly. This is not a content moderation requirement but a structural accountability mechanism that creates liability for known risks without prescribing specific content decisions.

**Emergency Correction Protocol for Political Deepfakes**: A mechanism allowing electoral authorities (the EAC, state election commissions) to submit emergency takedown requests to platforms for content that has been authenticated as a deepfake through provenance analysis, within a defined response window. Ireland's DSA-based emergency response demonstrates the operational feasibility. The constitutional design requires that the authentication mechanism (provenance analysis) not substitute human agency with automated content removal — an authenticated deepfake would trigger a platform review obligation, not automatic removal.

**Section 230 Modification for Known Deepfakes**: Removing Section 230 immunity for platforms that continue to algorithmically amplify content that has been authenticated as a deepfake through provenance metadata, after notice — not for hosting such content but for deliberate recommendation amplification of known-synthetic political content without provenance disclosure. This is a narrower Section 230 modification than most current proposals, targeted specifically at the amplification of content the platform has been notified is AI-generated.

### 7.3 Tier 3 — Public Investment in Epistemic Infrastructure

**CPB Restoration and Strengthening**: The Corporation for Public Broadcasting should be restored with statutory protection against future rescissions — including a provision making CPB funding mandatory (exempt from rescission under the Impoundment Control Act) and a 10-year forward appropriation that survives annual appropriations battles. The Finland and Ireland models demonstrate that public broadcasting's epistemic anchor function depends on funding certainty, not annual legislative renewal. A restored CPB should be tasked with a new local news partnership mandate, directing grants to nonprofit local news organizations that have emerged to fill the voids created by commercial local news collapse.

**National Media Literacy Initiative**: Federal investment in a nationally coordinated media literacy initiative, modeled on Finland's National Media Literacy Program, delivered through existing educational infrastructure (Title I grants, professional development funding, state curriculum advisory) rather than federal curriculum mandates. The initiative should include: age-appropriate media literacy education standards that states can adopt; professional development for educators on AI literacy; and a civic technology component (modeled on Taiwan's g0v) that creates community-level fact-checking infrastructure.

**Civic Technology Epistemic Resilience Fund**: A competitive federal grant program — administered through the National Science Foundation or a dedicated technology democracy office — funding civic technology projects that build community epistemic infrastructure: fact-checking tools, provenance verification platforms, community news coverage projects, and civic AI transparency tools. The Taiwan Cofacts model demonstrates the operational feasibility; the grant program would adapt it to the US's decentralized civic infrastructure.

**Provenance Research and Standards Investment**: Federal research investment in the C2PA standard's deployment challenges — specifically, the metadata-stripping problem that prevents provenance credentials from surviving social media distribution. DARPA's Media Forensics program and NIST's AI standards work provide institutional homes; the research gap is applied engineering on metadata preservation at scale.
