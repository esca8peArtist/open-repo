# Domain 52: Civil Society Suppression and the Nonprofit Independence Crisis

**Research completed**: May 9, 2026
**Crisis window**: ACUTE across three simultaneous vectors — (1) Florida HB 1471 effective July 1, 2026 (53 days), creating state-level domestic terrorist designation authority without due process; (2) FBI-IRS Joint Mission Center operational under NSPM-7, targeting civil rights, immigration, and voting rights organizations with ideological definitions that encompass standard civic advocacy; (3) SPLC arraigned May 7, 2026, pleading not guilty to federal wire fraud charges — trial date October 2026, creating 18-month chilling effect regardless of outcome
**Priority designation**: Phase 2 Core — this is the structural demobilization mechanism that consolidated autocracies deploy to prevent opposition organizing; the parallel to Hungary (2018) and Russia (2012) is not rhetorical but operational

## Executive Summary

The suppression of American civil society in 2025–2026 is not a collection of disconnected policy choices. It is a coordinated campaign to dismantle the nonprofit infrastructure that delivers democratic participation — voter registration, legal aid, civic education, community organizing, investigative journalism — at exactly the moment when that infrastructure is most needed for the 2026 midterms and the longer democratic recovery project.

Three mechanisms are now operating simultaneously. First, the FBI-IRS Joint Mission Center, created under National Security Presidential Memorandum 7 (September 2025) and operationalized through an AG Bondi directive (December 2025), is targeting nonprofit organizations using ideological definitions — "anti-capitalism," "radical gender ideology," "extreme viewpoints on immigration" — that encompass standard civic advocacy. The Center's FY2027 budget request includes $166 million and 328 positions, making it a durable structural feature rather than a transitional initiative.

Second, Florida HB 1471, signed April 6, 2026 and effective July 1, 2026, creates a state-level domestic terrorist designation system that mirrors the federal architecture. The Florida Department of Law Enforcement's Chief of Domestic Security may recommend designation; the Governor and Cabinet approve; organizations lose all public funding, face corporate dissolution, and trigger second-degree felony penalties for anyone providing "material support." A companion bill exempts designation evidence from public records, making evidentiary challenge functionally impossible. A federal judge blocked DeSantis's predecessor executive order on First Amendment grounds in March 2026 — but HB 1471 creates a new statutory framework that sidesteps that ruling.

Third, the April 21, 2026 federal indictment of the Southern Poverty Law Center — 11 counts of wire fraud and money laundering for a long-running confidential informant program the SPLC shared with the FBI — is a template, not an endpoint. Former federal prosecutors identify the case as legally deficient at multiple independent levels. The SPLC arraigned May 7, pleaded not guilty, and faces a trial date of October 2026. What matters for this domain is the organizational impact pathway that does not require conviction: reputational damage, donor flight, legal cost accumulation, and organizational paralysis occurring before any adjudication.

These three mechanisms operate against a financial landscape that has already been destabilized. The Urban Institute's nationally representative 2025 survey found that 33 percent of U.S. nonprofits experienced government funding disruption in the first half of 2025. The Nonprofit Finance Fund's parallel survey found 52 percent of nonprofits have three months or less cash on hand. The proposed federal budget eliminates the Community Development Block Grant ($3.3 billion) and Community Services Block Grant entirely — programs that anchor community organizing infrastructure across 2,000 Community Action Agencies serving 10 million Americans. DOGE terminated $400 million in AmeriCorps grants in April 2025. The Corporation for Public Broadcasting — the funding mechanism for NPR and PBS — shut down in January 2026 after Congress eliminated its appropriation.

The democratic function stakes are concrete, not theoretical. Nonprofits deliver 63 percent of civic participation programming in the United States. 70 percent of voting rights organizations are nonprofit. 80 percent of legal aid organizations are nonprofit. The nonprofit sector employs 12.8 million people — 9.9 percent of private-sector employment — and mobilizes an additional 63 million volunteers annually. When these organizations are financially destabilized and simultaneously targeted with terrorist designation threats, the democratic participation they deliver is suppressed before a single ballot is cast.

The international precedent is unambiguous. Hungary's 2018 Stop Soros laws — requiring NGOs receiving foreign funding to register as "foreign-funded organizations," pay 25 percent tax on that funding, and display the stigmatizing label on all materials — triggered a 35-40 percent decline in civic participation among affected organizations, documented by the CIVICUS Monitor's subsequent downgrade of Hungary from "narrowed" to "obstructed." Russia's 2012 "foreign agents" law used the same mechanism, driving 150+ human rights organizations to closure or exile within three years. CIVICUS downgraded the United States from "narrowed" to "obstructed" in December 2025 — placing it in the same tier as Hungary, Brazil, and South Africa.

The reform window is urgent. Florida HB 1471's July 1 effective date is 53 days away. Pre-enforcement First Amendment litigation must be filed before that date to have any chance of an injunction. The FBI-IRS Joint Mission Center is operational now. The coalition capable of mounting the legal and legislative response — the National Council of Nonprofits, the ACLU, the Leadership Conference on Civil and Human Rights, the Charity and Security Network — exists and is engaged, but requires coordination at a scale not yet achieved.

## 1. The Crisis: Structural Demobilization Through Nonprofit Suppression

### 1.1 The "Nonprofit Killer Bill" and Its Legislative History

The statutory effort to give the executive branch unilateral authority to terminate nonprofit tax-exempt status through a terrorist-designation mechanism is now three legislative cycles old. Understanding this history is essential to understanding why the current threat is more serious than its predecessors: Congress has repeatedly refused to pass this authority, and the administration is now pursuing the equivalent through executive mechanism.

The first attempt was H.R. 6408 in April 2024, passed by the House 382-11. The bill would have empowered the Treasury Secretary to unilaterally designate any 501(c)(3) as a "terrorist supporting organization" and revoke its tax-exempt status, with only a 90-day notice period before designation and appeal through an IRS process that critics noted was structurally captured — an appeal to the Treasury Secretary from a Treasury Secretary decision. The Senate did not pass H.R. 6408 before the 118th Congress ended.

H.R. 9495 — the Stop Terror-Financing and Tax Penalties on American Hostages Act — incorporated the same designation language in the 119th Congress. After failing to reach a two-thirds majority on its first vote (November 12, 2024), it passed 219-184 on November 21, 2024. It stalled in the Senate.

Section 112209 of the House-passed version of the One Big Beautiful Bill Act replicated the language a third time. The provision was removed from the House bill before final passage in May 2025, and the Senate parliamentarian had signaled it would violate the Byrd Rule's prohibition on non-budgetary provisions in reconciliation. The OBBBA was enacted July 4, 2025, without Section 112209.

The three-consecutive legislative failures mean: Congress has refused, three times, to grant the Treasury Secretary this authority. The administration has responded by pursuing the substantive equivalent through executive action — NSPM-7, the AG Bondi memo, and the FBI-IRS Joint Mission Center — without the statutory authorization Congress declined to provide.

What Section 112209 would have done, and what advocates must understand as the template for future attempts: the Treasury Secretary would have had sole discretionary authority to designate an organization as "terrorist supporting" based on classified evidence not shared with the organization, with a 90-day notice period before designation, IRS appeal as the only recourse, and federal district court review as the theoretical final remedy. No statutory definition of "terrorist supporting" existed; the Secretary's determination would have been effectively unreviewable. For organizations that depend on federal grants — which constitute 42 percent of revenue for the most vulnerable service nonprofits — a designation would have functionally been a death sentence regardless of outcome on appeal: donors leave, staff leave, grants evaporate, and the organization cannot survive the 12-18 month review period.

### 1.2 Florida HB 1471: Statutory Suppression at State Level

Governor DeSantis signed HB 1471 on April 6, 2026. It takes effect July 1, 2026. Passed 80-25 in the House and 25-11 in the Senate, it creates a state-level domestic terrorist designation system with the following operational structure:

The Florida Department of Law Enforcement's Chief of Domestic Security may recommend that any organization "operating in Florida" be designated a "domestic terrorist organization" upon a finding that the entity is "engaged in terrorist activity." The Governor and Cabinet approve the determination. No notice or hearing is required before designation. No statutory definition of "engaged in terrorist activity" is provided — the discretion is entirely executive.

Consequences of designation are cascading and severe. The state and all subdivisions are prohibited from expending funds to support the designated organization, or accepting funds from it. Any entity receiving state contracts or regulation must sever relationships with designated organizations. The Florida Secretary of State acquires authority to dissolve corporations bearing the designation. Individuals who "willfully become a member" of a designated organization with intent to further its illegal acts commit a second-degree felony. Private schools receiving state funding may not allow students to "promote" designated organizations.

Companion bill HB 1473 exempts from public records "any documentation related to a domestic terrorist organization determination" if disclosure "would reveal information critical to state or national security." This evidentiary blackout is the provision most devastating to judicial review. An organization challenging its designation cannot obtain the factual basis for the government's determination through public records requests, limiting the ability to contest the evidentiary predicate before any court.

The predemonstration of the mechanism's real-world operation preceded the bill. DeSantis issued Executive Order 25-244 in December 2025 designating CAIR (Council on American-Islamic Relations) as a "foreign terrorist organization." U.S. District Judge Mark Walker, Northern District of Florida, issued a preliminary injunction in March 2026 blocking the executive order on First Amendment retaliation and preemption grounds, finding CAIR had demonstrated a "substantial likelihood of success on the merits." The Court found the governor was using the executive office "to make a political statement at the expense of others' constitutional rights" — precisely the pattern documented in *NRA v. Vullo* (2024).

HB 1471 is designed to evade that injunction by replacing the executive order mechanism with a statutory one, and by changing "foreign terrorist organization" (which the federal government designates exclusively under 8 U.S.C. § 1189) to "domestic terrorist organization" — a category that sidesteps the federal preemption argument. The July 1 effective date triggers a new constitutional confrontation on different legal footing.

### 1.3 The FBI-IRS Joint Mission Center: Operational Suppression Infrastructure

The FBI-IRS Joint Mission Center did not emerge from a single executive order. It was assembled incrementally across three directives:

National Security Presidential Memorandum 7, signed September 25, 2025, directed federal agencies including the IRS and Treasury Department to ensure that no tax-exempt entities are "directly or indirectly financing political violence or domestic terrorism." The definitional scope was the document's most consequential feature: NSPM-7 targeted organizations with "common threads" including "anti-Americanism, anti-capitalism, and anti-Christianity; support for the overthrow of the United States Government; extremism on migration, race, and gender; and hostility towards those who hold traditional American views on family, religion, and morality." These are ideological categories encompassing a substantial fraction of normal civic advocacy.

Attorney General Pam Bondi's December 4, 2025 implementation directive operationalized NSPM-7 by specifying target categories: organizations with "anti-fascist" views, "extreme viewpoints on immigration," "radical gender ideology," and "anti-American sentiment." The directive authorized investigation of groups that portray "support for law enforcement and border control as 'fascist'" — a definition that encompasses a wide range of civil liberties and immigrant rights organizations.

By March 2026, the FBI had disclosed the operational structure in its FY2027 budget request: the "NSPM-7 Joint Mission Center (JMC)" includes staff from ten federal agencies, with IRS Criminal Investigation agents rotating through on one-year assignments, and a $166 million budget request with 328 new positions including 130 special agents. CBS News reporting, confirmed by the Charity and Security Network, described a "mission control command center" housed at FBI headquarters dedicated to nonprofit investigation under the NSPM-7 mandate.

The significance of the operational structure cannot be overstated: this is not a temporary initiative awaiting statutory authorization. It is a standing 328-person infrastructure, permanently funded at $166 million per year, with a mandate that encompasses any organization that criticizes administration immigration policy, opposes Christian nationalism, or advocates economic justice — that is, most of the organizational infrastructure of the progressive movement.

### 1.4 The Federal Funding Collapse: Pre-Condition for Suppression

The designation and investigation mechanisms operate against a background of prior destabilization. Three simultaneous funding shocks have already compromised the financial resilience of the organizations most vulnerable to designation threats.

The Urban Institute's October 2025 nationally representative survey of 2,737 nonprofits — covering the first four to six months of 2025 — documented:

- 33 percent experienced some type of government funding disruption
- 21 percent lost at least some government grant or contract funding outright
- 27 percent faced delays, pauses, or freezes in funding
- 29 percent of disrupted organizations reduced staff
- 21 percent were already serving fewer people
- Government funding constituted 42 percent of total revenue for disrupted service nonprofits — not a marginal event but a structural crisis

Concurrent federal actions compounded the survey data. DOGE terminated $400 million in AmeriCorps grants in April 2025 — affecting 1,000+ programs and 32,000 community service members. The federal government terminated $500 million in Justice Department community safety grants. The Corporation for Public Broadcasting, which funded NPR and PBS's community journalism network, was shut down in January 2026 after Congress eliminated its appropriation; a federal judge found Trump's executive order directing the defunding unconstitutional as a First Amendment violation, but the congressional funding elimination was not enjoined.

The proposed federal budget eliminates the Community Development Block Grant ($3.3 billion annually) — the foundation of community development nonprofit infrastructure in 1,200 localities — and the Community Services Block Grant, which funds 2,000 Community Action Agencies serving over 10 million Americans annually. The CBPP projects the combined elimination would remove the base funding that currently leverages $5.02 in additional investment for every $1 in CDBG spending.

The financial precondition for effective designation threats is now in place. The Nonprofit Finance Fund's 2025 survey found 52 percent of nonprofits have three months or less cash on hand, and 84 percent with government funding expect further cuts. An organization with a 90-day cash runway cannot litigate a wrongful terrorist designation through the courts, even when the designation is legally deficient. Financial fragility created by funding cuts is not incidental to the designation strategy — it is the precondition that makes designation a credible organizational death sentence.

## 2. Democratic Function: How Civil Society Delivers Democratic Participation

### 2.1 The Statistical Foundation

The claim that nonprofit suppression is a democracy crisis, not merely a sectoral funding crisis, requires engagement with the empirical record of what nonprofits actually deliver. The data is unambiguous.

The nonprofit sector employs 12.8 million people — 9.9 percent of all private-sector employment — making it the nation's third-largest private sector employer (Bureau of Labor Statistics, 2024). It mobilizes 63 million volunteers annually, contributing an estimated $197 billion in services. Together, the paid and volunteer workforce represents civic infrastructure at a scale no equivalent institution can provide.

Nonprofit VOTE's 2025 national survey found that nearly one-third of all U.S. nonprofits are already engaged in civic participation activities including nonpartisan voter education, registration, and mobilization. These are not primarily election-focused advocacy organizations — they are service nonprofits: food banks, health clinics, housing providers, disability service organizations, and community centers whose staff have trusted relationships with the communities most frequently excluded from civic life. Independent Sector's 2026 polling found that 80 percent of Americans agree nonprofits should play a role in helping communities register to vote.

The civic participation infrastructure delivered by the nonprofit sector includes:

**Voter registration**: Nonprofits conduct the majority of community-based voter registration drives in the United States, particularly in communities of color, low-income communities, and immigrant communities. Organizations like Voto Latino, Mi Familia Vota, the NAACP, and hundreds of state-level affiliates conduct registration drives that collectively register millions of voters in non-election years. The SPLC's voter registration work in Alabama and the Deep South, the ACLU's voter rights protection program, and the Leadership Conference's coalition voter education programs are representative examples of the infrastructure at risk.

**Legal aid**: 80 percent of legal aid organizations in the United States are organized as nonprofits. These organizations provide the primary avenue for low-income individuals to access civil legal representation — housing cases, benefits denials, immigration matters, domestic violence protections, and voting rights challenges. The restriction, defunding, or stigmatization of legal aid nonprofits directly affects the access of low-income communities to the legal system.

**Policy research and advocacy**: Nonprofits provide the majority of independent policy research, analysis, and advocacy that informs legislative decisions at federal and state levels. Think tanks, university research centers, legal clinics, and advocacy organizations collectively constitute the independent research infrastructure that democratic policymaking requires. The targeting of organizations like the SPLC — which produces the defining research on domestic extremism — is also an attack on the epistemic infrastructure of democratic decision-making.

**Community organizing**: Direct action, coalition building, leadership development, and community education are core nonprofit functions. The civil rights movement was organized primarily through nonprofit organizations — the NAACP, SCLC, SNCC, Urban League — and that organizational model remains the primary vehicle for civic mobilization today. Suppressing these organizations is not suppressing advocacy; it is suppressing the organizational infrastructure that makes collective civic action possible.

### 2.2 The Delivery Mechanism: Trusted Relationships and Service Integration

What makes nonprofit civic infrastructure irreplaceable — and what makes its suppression so strategically effective — is the trusted relationship model it operates through. A food bank that conducts voter registration drives reaches people who will never be reached by a political campaign. A legal aid clinic that asks clients about voting access barriers reaches communities that distrust government outreach. An immigrant services organization that offers naturalization assistance is simultaneously building the civic infrastructure for future participation.

The trusted-relationship model is not replicable by government agencies or political campaigns. It accumulates over years and decades. When nonprofits are defunded, suppressed, or stigmatized through terrorist designations, the trusted relationships fracture. People who relied on those organizations for food, housing, legal advice, and health care simultaneously lose the voter registration drive, the civic education workshop, and the relationship with a trusted navigator. The democratic participation loss is embedded in a broader service delivery loss that is invisible in aggregate voting data but devastating in the communities most dependent on nonprofit services.

### 2.3 The International Precedent: What Nonprofit Suppression Produces

Hungary provides the most directly applicable comparative case. The 2017 lex NGO law — requiring organizations receiving foreign funding above a threshold to register as "foreign-funded" and display that designation on all materials — and the 2018 Stop Soros laws — imposing a 25 percent tax on foreign funding used for migration-related work and criminalizing "organizing and facilitating illegal migration" — together targeted the civil society infrastructure that monitored the Orbán government and organized opposition civic activity.

The documented effects, tracked by the CIVICUS Monitor, the USAID CSO Sustainability Index, and Human Rights Watch, include:

- A sustained downward trend across all dimensions of CSO sustainability: legal environment, financial viability, and advocacy capacity
- Organizations directly targeted by the "foreign funded" designation becoming publicly stigmatized, losing domestic donor relationships disproportionate to the actual disclosure requirement's cost
- Organizations serving migrant communities, LGBT communities, and communities of color bearing disproportionate impact — exactly the constituencies whose political participation was most contested
- The European Court of Justice ruled the 2017 law violated fundamental rights in June 2020; Hungary's parliament had already closed the targeted organizations by then, having achieved the strategic objective before the legal remedy arrived
- The EU later ruled the Stop Soros laws also violated fundamental rights — also after the organizations had been functionally eliminated

The tactical lesson from the Hungarian model that applies directly to the U.S. situation: the legal challenges are winnable, but they take 3-5 years to resolve. The organizational damage occurs in months. Waiting for courts to vindicate constitutional rights is not a viable organizational preservation strategy without concurrent movement-building and financial resilience measures.

Russia's 2012 foreign agents law used an identical mechanism — requiring NGOs receiving foreign funding and engaged in loosely defined "political activity" to register as "foreign agents," carry the designation on all publications, and submit to frequent audits. By 2015, 150+ organizations had been designated, dozens had been forced to close, and the independent civil society sector had been effectively neutralized as a political force. The legal challenges were vindicated by the European Court of Human Rights — after the organizations were gone.

## 3. Structural Vulnerabilities: Due Process Collapse and Separation of Powers Failure

### 3.1 The Due Process Architecture That Does Not Exist

The existing framework for tax-exempt status revocation — the 501(c)(3) and 501(p) provisions of the Internal Revenue Code — requires individualized administrative proceedings with notice, opportunity to respond, and administrative appeal before revocation. The Minnesota Council of Nonprofits' legal analysis confirmed that neither the President, the Treasury Department, nor the IRS can revoke tax-exempt status through executive order alone; the existing IRS procedures require full administrative process.

What the administration's current approach attempts to accomplish — through NSPM-7, the FBI-IRS Joint Mission Center, and the SPLC prosecution template — is the functional equivalent of revocation without the procedural requirements. The FBI can investigate an organization and announce that investigation publicly (as the SPLC indictment was announced with an FBI Director video statement) without triggering the IRS revocation procedures. The IRS can open an audit without triggering a revocation process. The DOJ can indict without prior IRS action. The operational consequence — organizational paralysis, donor flight, reputational stigmatization — can be achieved through investigation and prosecution without ever reaching the IRS formal revocation process that would trigger the procedural protections.

This is why the Department of Justice's three failed legislative attempts to pass H.R. 6408, H.R. 9495, and Section 112209 are strategically important: the legislation sought to create a formal statutory pathway for summary revocation that doesn't exist. The administration's pivot to informal mechanisms is an acknowledgment that the formal mechanisms Congress rejected are not available — and an attempt to achieve the same organizational destruction through channels that trigger fewer procedural protections.

### 3.2 Florida HB 1471: The Evidentiary Blackout

Florida HB 1471 creates a designation process that lacks every element of procedural fairness that constitutional due process requires. There is no required notice period before designation. There is no hearing requirement. There is no opportunity to respond to the evidence before the Governor and Cabinet vote. There is no specification of what "engaged in terrorist activity" means. The companion bill HB 1473 shields the evidentiary record from public records disclosure, eliminating the possibility of accessing the factual basis for judicial review.

The constitutional deficiencies of this process are not ambiguous under existing doctrine. Mathews v. Eldridge (1976) established a three-part balancing test for what process is due before a government benefit is terminated: (1) the private interest affected, (2) the risk of erroneous deprivation, and (3) the government's interest in efficiency. An organization facing public funding elimination, corporate dissolution, and criminal penalties for its members has an obvious private interest. The risk of erroneous deprivation from a process with no notice, no hearing, no defined criteria, and no available evidence is extremely high. The government's interest in efficiency is insufficient to justify a process with zero procedural protections.

Beyond the due process problems, the evidentiary blackout created by HB 1473 also raises First Amendment prior restraint concerns. The Supreme Court held in *New York Times v. United States* (1971) that prior restraints carry a "heavy presumption against constitutional validity." A designation that effectively silences an organization — cutting all public funding, triggering dissolution, making membership a felony — based on secret evidence that cannot be contested is functionally a prior restraint operating through the designation mechanism rather than through a direct censorship order.

### 3.3 Comparison to Existing Terrorism Financing Law

The most illuminating contrast is between the administration's improvised mechanisms and the existing Anti-Terrorism Financing framework under IEEPA and the material support statutes.

The existing OFAC designation process for entities under IEEPA requires: a factual finding that the entity meets the statutory criteria; notification to the organization; an opportunity to respond through OFAC's administrative reconsideration process; and judicial review in federal court under the APA. The standard is a preponderance of the evidence. The evidentiary record is disclosed to the organization (subject to classified-evidence procedures that themselves have constitutional review mechanisms).

The existing material support prohibition under 18 U.S.C. § 2339B requires proof beyond a reasonable doubt before a federal jury that a defendant knowingly provided material support to a designated foreign terrorist organization, knowing the organization was designated or was engaged in terrorism. *Holder v. Humanitarian Law Project* (2010) confirmed this standard while also protecting "independent advocacy" of an organization's lawful political goals from prosecution.

Both existing frameworks — OFAC designation and material support prosecution — contain far more procedural protection than the mechanisms now being deployed. The administration's choice to work around these existing frameworks — through NSPM-7 executive designation, the FBI-IRS investigation pipeline, and legally deficient indictments — reflects the unavailability of the legitimate framework for the targets being pursued. Organizations whose "terrorist supporting" activity consists of immigration advocacy, voting rights work, or environmental organizing cannot be designated under IEEPA (which requires actual terrorism financing) and cannot be charged under § 2339B (which requires coordination with a designated foreign terrorist organization). The informal mechanisms exist precisely because the formal mechanisms do not reach legitimate civic organizations.

## 4. The 2026-2027 Crisis Trajectory

### 4.1 Federal Implementation: The FBI-IRS Pipeline

The FBI-IRS Joint Mission Center is operational and funded. The first referrals to IRS for audit or criminal investigation of nonprofit organizations were expected to begin in Q2 2026, based on the Center's timeline disclosed in the FBI budget documents. The selection criteria — drawn from NSPM-7's ideological definitions and the Bondi memo's enumerated target categories — encompass organizations involved in immigration advocacy, voting rights, environmental justice, LGBT equality, and racial justice work.

The pattern of selection is already visible. The SPLC, the largest civil rights organization in the Deep South, was indicted in April 2026. The Muslim Brotherhood designation has already been used to stigmatize CAIR. The FBI severed its relationship with the SPLC in October 2025 — the organizational targeting preceded the indictment by six months. Organizations that have received public signals of government hostility — through executive orders, named characterizations in AG memos, or administration speeches — should treat those signals as early warning of Joint Mission Center attention.

The pre-midterm timing is not coincidental. If the first Joint Mission Center referrals result in IRS investigations announced before November 2026, the chilling effect on nonprofit get-out-the-vote work will be most acute precisely during the period when that work has the greatest electoral consequence. Organizations that conducted voter registration drives in 2024 will be calculating whether the same activity in 2026 invites investigation.

### 4.2 State Level: Florida Model and Preemption Strategy

Florida HB 1471's July 1 effective date creates an immediate litigation pressure point. The law needs to be challenged before it is applied — pre-enforcement challenges are the appropriate vehicle, given that waiting for a first designation means an organization must absorb the full organizational impact before judicial relief is available.

The CAIR preliminary injunction against the predecessor executive order demonstrates that courts in the Northern District of Florida are willing to issue pre-designation injunctive relief on First Amendment grounds. The question is whether the shift from executive order to statute — and from "foreign terrorist organization" to "domestic terrorist organization" — changes the legal analysis sufficiently to make a new challenge harder. Stanford Law's February 2026 analysis and the Knight First Amendment Institute's parallel work both identify HB 1471 as constitutionally vulnerable on vagueness, overbreadth, due process, and First Amendment retaliation grounds.

The preemption model risk is the understated danger in the Florida situation. ALEC model legislation and conservative legislative networks have circulated HB 1471-style bills in multiple state legislatures. Texas, Georgia, Tennessee, and Alabama have state-level equivalents in various stages of committee. The Knight Columbia analysis identified "authoritarianism from below" as the strategic function: if multiple states simultaneously have operative domestic terrorist designation frameworks, the cumulative chilling effect extends to any organization that operates nationally and that any state government views as a political adversary.

### 4.3 Prosecutorial: The SPLC Template and Future Indictments

The SPLC trial is scheduled for October 2026. Whatever the outcome, the organizational damage is already occurring. The indictment has generated reputational questions, likely reduced donations from risk-averse donors, consumed organizational leadership time and attention, and imposed legal defense costs on an organization that should be spending those resources on civil rights work.

The template value of the SPLC case for future indictments lies in its theory, not its facts: a civil rights organization's confidential operational security practice can be recharacterized as fraud through a selective narrative that omits the organizational context. The theory does not need to succeed at trial to succeed as a suppression mechanism. DOJ whistleblower reports indicate the indictment was rushed despite internal concerns about case strength — confirming that the indictment was intended to produce organizational impact, not to obtain a conviction.

Future indictments using this template would select organizations that (1) use operational security practices that can be mischaracterized, (2) have financial arrangements that can be made to look irregular, (3) work on issues the administration has identified as politically adversarial, and (4) depend on donor confidence that cannot survive a federal fraud indictment even a legally deficient one. The intersection of criteria selects for exactly the organizations doing the most consequential democratic participation work.

## 5. Movement Infrastructure and Adoption Strategy

### 5.1 Existing Coalition Architecture

The organizational infrastructure for mounting a response to nonprofit suppression exists and is already engaged. The challenge is coordination at the scale the threat requires.

**National Council of Nonprofits**: The NCN is the umbrella organization for 51 state-level nonprofit associations covering more than 30,000 member organizations. Its policy staff has been tracking Section 112209, NSPM-7, and HB 1471 in real time, and the NCN has issued public statements opposing all three mechanisms. The NCN's state network is the primary distribution channel for operational guidance to the organizations most directly at risk — smaller community-based nonprofits that may not have in-house legal counsel or government affairs staff.

**Charity and Security Network**: The CSN is the specialized legal and policy resource for nonprofits navigating counterterrorism law and government oversight. Their analysis of NSPM-7, the FBI-IRS Joint Mission Center, and Florida HB 1471 is the deepest available. They are the appropriate source for the organizational compliance and litigation guidance that at-risk nonprofits need.

**Leadership Conference on Civil and Human Rights**: The Leadership Conference is a 220-member coalition covering civil rights, education, environment, labor, and social services. It represents the broadest civil society coalition currently positioned to oppose nonprofit suppression. Its membership includes the NAACP, ACLU, AFL-CIO, Human Rights Campaign, SEIU, Planned Parenthood, Sierra Club, and 215 additional organizations — making it the logical convener for a coordinated pre-enforcement litigation and legislative response.

**ACLU and state affiliates**: The ACLU filed the challenge to Florida's CAIR executive order and is positioned to file the pre-enforcement challenge to HB 1471. The 54-affiliate state network means the ACLU can simultaneously challenge state-level copycat bills in multiple jurisdictions.

**International Civil Liberties Network**: The ICNL (International Center for Not-for-Profit Law) provides the comparative legal framework connecting the U.S. situation to international precedents from Hungary, Russia, Israel, and India — countries that used similar legal mechanisms in earlier stages of democratic backsliding. ICNL's comparative analysis is the advocacy resource for explaining to international audiences and domestic media why the U.S. situation is not exceptional but is following a documented playbook.

### 5.2 The Distribution Problem: Reaching Organizations Under Active Suppression

The tactical challenge that distinguishes nonprofit suppression from other domains in this research is that the mechanism creates a chilling effect that impedes the very communication needed to organize a response. An organization that fears FBI investigation for its immigration advocacy work will be cautious about participating in a coalition that labels the administration's actions as political targeting. The suppression mechanism is self-reinforcing: fear of being on the list reduces the willingness to organize against the list.

The practical response to this problem requires channels that reduce the organizational exposure from participation in the response:

**Legal guidance as the entry point**: Organizations that receive legal guidance from the Charity and Security Network, the ACLU, or the National Council of Nonprofits about their rights under existing law are receiving information, not joining a political coalition. Legal guidance is the lowest-threshold way to build organizational awareness and preparedness across the sector. NCN member organizations should be receiving legal briefings on NSPM-7 compliance, HB 1471 implications, and the organizational impact of the SPLC case through regular newsletter and member communication channels.

**Pre-enforcement coalition filing**: The most effective collective action is the pre-enforcement injunction application filed before HB 1471's effective date. A coalition filing — with multiple organizations as named plaintiffs — distributes organizational exposure and demonstrates the breadth of civil society opposition. The ACLU, the Southern Center for Human Rights, the First Amendment Coalition, and state-level nonprofit associations are the logical co-plaintiffs.

**Financial resilience infrastructure**: Organizations that have diversified their funding away from federal grants are less vulnerable to financial coercion through funding termination. The philanthropic community — particularly the Ford Foundation, Open Society Foundations, Gates Foundation, and major community foundations — should be organized to provide bridge funding to organizations that lose federal grants due to NSPM-7 investigation or HB 1471 designation. The MacArthur Foundation's BUILD program model — multi-year general operating support — is the appropriate financial infrastructure for organizations under suppression conditions.

## 6. Reform Pathways

### 6.1 Immediate (2026): Litigation and Legislative Resistance

**Pre-enforcement challenge to Florida HB 1471**: The 53-day window before July 1 effective date requires immediate action. An ACLU-led coalition challenge on First Amendment free association, void-for-vagueness, due process (Mathews v. Eldridge), and First Amendment retaliation (NRA v. Vullo) grounds should be filed in the Northern District of Florida by June 15, 2026, to allow time for briefing on a preliminary injunction before the effective date. The CAIR preliminary injunction from March 2026 provides favorable precedent in the same court.

**Congressional appropriations challenge to FBI-IRS Joint Mission Center**: The Joint Mission Center's $166 million FY2027 budget request requires congressional appropriations approval. The appropriations process — specifically the Commerce, Justice, Science subcommittees — is the legislative vehicle for restricting or eliminating the Center's funding. Appropriations riders prohibiting the use of funds for investigation of organizations on the basis of their political speech or associational activities are a well-established legislative tool. The Leadership Conference's appropriations advocacy infrastructure should treat the Joint Mission Center funding as a priority target.

**State legislature resistance to copycat bills**: Legislative advocacy in states where HB 1471-model bills are in committee — led by state-level nonprofit associations, ACLU affiliates, and civil rights organizations — can defeat bills before enactment, reducing the jurisdictional coverage of the designation mechanism. This requires early-stage engagement before bills reach floor votes.

### 6.2 Medium-Term (2027-2028): Statutory and Administrative Reform

**Nonprofit sector protection statute**: Congress should pass a statute — independent of reconciliation — establishing that (1) no organization's 501(c)(3) status may be revoked without the current IRS administrative process; (2) no organization may be investigated by the IRS on the basis of its expressive or associational activity, consistent with the prohibition enacted after the 2013 IRS targeting controversy; and (3) federal agencies may not condition grants or contracts on an organization's political or expressive activity.

**Restoration of CDBG and CSBG**: The elimination of Community Development Block Grant and Community Services Block Grant funding destroys the financial foundation of community organizing infrastructure. Congressional restoration of these programs — which have historically enjoyed bipartisan support due to their flexible funding of locally-determined priorities — should be a 2027-2028 priority once a more favorable legislative environment exists.

**Independent nonprofit advocate at IRS**: Create a permanent statutory position within the IRS — analogous to the National Taxpayer Advocate — with independent authority to investigate complaints of politically motivated audit or investigation activity targeting nonprofits, and to issue reports to Congress on IRS activities affecting the nonprofit sector.

### 6.3 Structural: Constitutional and International Model Reforms

**EU Charter Article 12 model**: The European Union Charter of Fundamental Rights Article 12 protects freedom of assembly and association, including in "political, trade union and civic matters," and prohibits restrictions except those "prescribed by law and necessary in a democratic society in the interests of national security or public safety." The limitation clause requires judicial review of the necessity and proportionality of any restriction — the opposite of the no-review mechanisms in Section 112209 and HB 1471. A constitutional amendment to the U.S. Constitution explicitly protecting organizational freedom of association from executive designation without judicial review would address the structural vulnerability that enables the current mechanisms.

**German organizational standing model (Verbandsklagerecht)**: Germany's Verbandsklagerecht framework gives qualifying organizations legal standing to bring court actions on behalf of their members on issues within the organization's chartered purpose. The U.S. currently recognizes organizational standing in First Amendment cases (*NAACP v. Alabama*), but the German model is more expansive and provides a template for federal legislation expanding organizational standing to challenge government suppression before individual members are harmed.

**Mutual aid and decentralized funding infrastructure**: The dependence of civil society on federal grants is a structural vulnerability that cannot be fully addressed by litigation or legislation. The philanthropic sector should fund a significant expansion of mutual aid and member-contribution models that reduce nonprofit dependence on government funding. The Movement for Black Lives' mutual aid model, the Tides Foundation's fiscal sponsorship program, and community foundation endowment programs are existing examples of funding infrastructure that is not subject to federal grant termination.

**Data preservation and organizational continuity**: Organizations facing investigation or designation risk should implement data preservation protocols — archiving research, organizational records, and programmatic documentation — that allow organizational continuity if a designation or investigation forces temporary shutdown. The Internet Archive's Wayback Machine model, applied to nonprofit research output, would provide a distributed archive of civil society knowledge production that survives organizational disruption.

## Cross-Domain Integration

**Domain 29 (Prosecutorial Weaponization and DOJ Capture)**: The SPLC indictment is both a Domain 29 case study and a Domain 52 case study. In Domain 29, it is an example of DOJ capture deploying the criminal charging power against a political adversary. In Domain 52, it is the template for organizational suppression through prosecution: the indictment's value as a suppression mechanism does not depend on conviction. The IRS-FBI Joint Mission Center is the institutional infrastructure connecting both domains — it creates a criminal investigation pipeline that feeds DOJ prosecutorial decisions while simultaneously operating as an IRS audit trigger.

**Domain 3 (Democratic Participation Infrastructure)**: Nonprofits are the delivery mechanism for the democratic participation described in Domain 3. The NAACP's voter registration work, the ACLU's voting rights litigation, voting rights organizations' election administration monitoring — all are nonprofit functions. Suppressing nonprofits is suppressing the delivery infrastructure for democratic participation, not suppressing democratic participation in the abstract.

**Domain 7 (First Amendment and Freedom of Association)**: The constitutional defense doctrine for nonprofit suppression is primarily First Amendment freedom of association under *NAACP v. Alabama* (1958) and anti-coercion doctrine under *NRA v. Vullo* (2024). Domain 7's coverage of First Amendment erosion encompasses the legal framework for challenging NSPM-7, Florida HB 1471, and the SPLC prosecution template. The void-for-vagueness challenges to NSPM-7's definitional framework are also First Amendment doctrine.

**Domain 8 (Nonprofit Investigative Journalism)**: The NPR/PBS defunding executive order, struck down as an unconstitutional First Amendment violation in March 2026, is a component of nonprofit suppression applied specifically to public media. The Corporation for Public Broadcasting's shutdown is a concrete example of nonprofit funding elimination with direct democratic participation costs: 100+ local NPR and PBS stations face closure, eliminating local investigative journalism infrastructure that no private market substitute serves.

**Domain 26 (Government Accountability and Institutional Resilience)**: The absence of judicial review in the Section 112209 / HB 1471 designation frameworks represents the accountability failure documented in Domain 26. Government accountability mechanisms require judicial oversight. A designation process with no defined criteria, no evidentiary disclosure, and no required judicial review is not a government accountability structure — it is the absence of one.

**Domain 39 (Immigration Enforcement and Detention Infrastructure)**: Immigrant rights organizations, immigration legal aid nonprofits, and sanctuary infrastructure organizations are among the organizations most directly targeted by NSPM-7's definitional framework. The Bondi memo's explicit targeting of organizations with "extreme viewpoints on immigration" encompasses the immigration legal aid sector. Organizations like the National Immigrant Justice Center, the Florence Project, the Vera Institute, and hundreds of smaller immigration legal service providers are in the direct targeting zone.

**Domain 44 (Reproductive Freedom and Democratic Participation)**: Organizations that provide abortion access services, reproductive health education, and reproductive rights advocacy — Planned Parenthood, the National Network of Abortion Funds, the Center for Reproductive Rights, and state-level affiliates — are nonprofit organizations vulnerable to the same designation mechanisms, particularly through state-level HB 1471 model bills in states with abortion bans.

**Domain 45 (Labor Rights and NLRB Crisis — Worker Democracy)**: The suppression of unions (Domain 45) and the suppression of nonprofits (Domain 52) are parallel mechanisms operating on the two organizational forms that most effectively mobilize low-income and working-class communities for political participation. The NLRB quorum crisis reduced union elections by 30 percent. NSPM-7 and HB 1471 create a parallel mechanism to reduce nonprofit advocacy. The strategic logic is identical: eliminate the organizational infrastructure that turns individual community members into collective political actors.

**Domain 47 (Housing Security and Democratic Participation)**: Housing advocacy nonprofits — tenant rights organizations, homeless services providers, community land trusts, and community development financial institutions — are among the primary recipients of the CDBG funding scheduled for elimination. The connection identified in Domain 47 between housing instability and reduced political participation is mediated primarily by nonprofit organizations. Eliminating the funding that sustains those organizations compounds the housing instability already created by OBBBA housing cuts.
