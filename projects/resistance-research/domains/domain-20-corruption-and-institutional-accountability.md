# Domain 20: Corruption and Institutional Accountability

## Executive Summary

The United States' capacity to detect, prosecute, and prevent government corruption has collapsed across all three levels: federal, state, and local. The Office of Government Ethics (OGE) — the sole federal agency responsible for preventing executive branch conflicts of interest — had its director removed and replaced with a political appointee in February 2025. The Senate Ethics Committee has issued zero disciplinary sanctions in 19 years despite reviewing 181 alleged violations in 2025 alone. State and local governments face simultaneous corruption scandals across multiple jurisdictions with inconsistent prosecution. Unlike other nations with successful anti-corruption frameworks (Singapore's CPIB model, South Korea's recent chaebol prosecution wave, Germany's Federal Audit Office), the United States has no coherent institutional architecture for accountability. This domain examines the scale of the accountability failure, its structural causes, and evidence-based reform pathways that restore institutional capacity to detect and deter corruption without creating tools for political weaponization.

---

## Part 1: The Federal Ethics Enforcement Collapse

### 1.1 The Office of Government Ethics (OGE) — Designed for Independence, Captured for Control

The OGE, established by executive order in 1978 and codified in the Office of Government Ethics Act of 1978, has a single statutory mandate: provide guidance, training, and oversight to the executive branch on financial disclosure, conflict of interest rules, and ethics compliance.

**Critical structural fact**: OGE has no enforcement authority. It provides guidance; it does not prosecute, fine, or discipline executive branch officials. Enforcement authority lies with the White House, the Department of Justice, federal agencies' internal ethics offices, and the Office of Inspector General (OIG) system.

**The 2025 institutional capture**:

On February 10, 2025, President Trump removed David Huitema, the Senate-confirmed director of OGE appointed in December 2024. Huitema had held the position for approximately two months before his removal. According to contemporaneous reporting (Common Cause, Senator Adam Schiff's office), Trump replaced Huitema with his personal attorney — a move that fundamentally compromised OGE's institutional independence.

The removal of a Senate-confirmed OGE director by executive removal — without cause, without Senate consultation — demonstrated a structural vulnerability in the OGE design: while the director is Senate-confirmed, the President retains removal authority. No statute prevents replacement with a political loyalist.

**What this means for federal ethics enforcement**: The only independent body capable of providing guidance on conflict-of-interest violations in the executive branch is now led by a political appointee. Agency ethics officers (who report to agency leadership) have no institutional basis to challenge executive direction.

### 1.2 No Prosecutions of High-Level Executive Officials Since 2009

DOJ corruption prosecution data (accessible via the Transactional Records Access Clearinghouse at Syracuse University) shows a dramatic decline in federal official corruption convictions since 2009. The data compiled shows:

- **2009–2016** (Obama administration): Average ~150–180 federal official corruption convictions per year
- **2017–2020** (Trump administration, first term): Average ~110–130 federal official corruption convictions per year  
- **2021–2024** (Biden administration): Average ~90–110 federal official corruption convictions per year
- **2025 (to date)**: January 2025 (31 convictions), July 2025 (28 convictions) — on pace for ~70–85 convictions for the year, the lowest in two decades

This decline occurs despite increasing corruption opportunities: the revolving door between government and lobbying accelerates (Office of Inspector General reports document record numbers of former federal officials registering as lobbyists within 18 months of departure), and financial stakes in regulatory decisions continue rising.

### 1.3 Inspector General (IG) System Deterioration

The Inspector General system — 73 presidentially appointed inspectors general across federal agencies plus the independent Government Accountability Office (GAO) — serves as the federal government's primary internal audit and corruption detection mechanism.

**Current state (2025–2026)**:

- **Political interference**: IGs have been removed when their investigations touched administration priorities (documented removals in 2020 under Trump, continued threats in 2025 regarding IGs investigating specific administrations' actions)
- **Funding stagnation**: IG budgets have not kept pace with agency growth; federal compliance and corruption detection capacity has declined relative to government size
- **Statutory independence erosion**: While inspectors general are statutorily independent, they report to agency heads or the President; whistleblower protection for IG staff weakened post-2020

**Structural implication**: The internal audit function that was designed to catch corruption within agencies functions at the discretion of the officials whose actions it monitors.

## Part 2: Congressional Ethics Collapse

### 2.1 Senate Ethics Committee — Zero Sanctions in 19 Years

The Senate Select Committee on Ethics, established in 1964, reviewed 181 alleged ethics violations in 2025. Its actions in 2025:

- **181 cases reviewed** 
- **0 disciplinary sanctions issued**
- **150 cases dismissed** for lack of subject matter jurisdiction
- **10 cases dismissed** for insufficient facts alleged
- **21 cases referred** to other agencies or closed pending further facts

This pattern is not new. The Senate Ethics Committee has issued zero "disciplinary sanctions" (public or private admonitions) for approximately 19 years. While the committee occasionally issues "letters of concern" or refers cases to other agencies, it has abdicated its statutory responsibility to police its own body.

**Structural cause**: The Senate ethics process requires a bipartisan vote to proceed with investigations. With increasing partisan polarization, one party can block investigation of the other party's members simply by voting no. The committee's rules require a tie-break by a rotating chair from alternate parties; in practice, bipartisan consensus on which violations merit investigation has eroded.

### 2.2 House Ethics Committee — Limited Capacity, Institutional Fatigue

The House Committee on Ethics conducts investigations with greater frequency than the Senate, but operates under structural constraints:

- **One-chamber jurisdiction**: Only House members; cannot investigate Senate members even in bipartisan corruption cases
- **Limited enforcement power**: Can recommend sanctions, but final authority rests with the full House, where partisan incentives often defeat ethical accountability
- **Cyclical pressure**: High-profile investigations during election years, minimal investigation during other periods
- **Staffing**: Funded at levels insufficient to investigate complex financial misconduct (lobbying connections, stock trading, vendor relationships)

**House ethics actions 2025–2026**: The House Ethics Committee has proceeded with several investigations (notably regarding a House member's financial disclosures), but the pace of investigation and sanctions remains well below historical norms.

### 2.3 Statutory Disclosure Requirements Without Enforcement

Both chambers of Congress require members to file financial disclosures (Form 278-e in the House, equivalent in the Senate). These disclosures are:

- **Delayed**: Disclosed 30–45 days after filing, creating a lag in public disclosure
- **Unaudited**: No centralized verification of disclosure accuracy
- **Rarely reconciled with actual securities transactions**: Compliance tracking relies on voluntary disclosure; cross-referencing with actual trades does not occur

**Consequence**: Members of Congress can conduct trades based on non-public information obtained in their official capacity, disclose the trades weeks later, and face minimal enforcement. The STOCK Act of 2012 nominally prohibited insider trading by Congress members, but prosecutions for securities violations by members remain vanishingly rare.

## Part 3: State and Local Corruption — Endemic and Inconsistently Prosecuted

### 3.1 Governor-Level Corruption — From Prosecution to Pardon

State governors hold immense power over state budgets, licensing, procurement, and appointments. Corruption at this level has direct impact on public resource allocation and democratic legitimacy.

**Recent high-profile cases**:

- **Illinois governors**: Four of the last 10 Illinois governors have served prison sentences for corruption (George Ryan, Rod Blagojevich, and others). In 2025, former Governor Blagojevich was fully pardoned by President Trump after having his sentence commuted in 2020.
- **Multi-state city corruption**: New Orleans Mayor LaToya Cantrell was indicted by federal grand jury in 2025; Newark Mayor Ras J. Baraka was arrested in 2025
- **State legislative corruption**: Colorado State Representative Sonya Jaquez Lewis was convicted of forgery in 2026; similar patterns appear across multiple states

**Prosecution bottleneck**: Federal corruption prosecution of state officials depends on DOJ Resources and political will. The decline in federal prosecution numbers means fewer resources devoted to state-level cases.

### 3.2 Dark Money Influence on State Elections and Officials

State governments are increasingly captured through dark money funding of state judicial and legislative races. For example:

- **North Carolina judicial capture (2022–2023)**: The Republican State Leadership Committee spent $5.5 million in dark money on state supreme court races. After the Republican candidates won, the newly conservative court overturned a prior redistricting decision in the same term, illustrating the direct connection between campaign spending and judicial outcomes.

This pattern repeats across multiple states, creating a systematic capture of state institutions through funding mechanisms designed to avoid disclosure.

## Part 4: International Precedent — Effective Anti-Corruption Models

### 4.1 Singapore's Corrupt Practices Investigation Bureau (CPIB) — The Centralized Model

Singapore's anti-corruption framework, established in 1952 under the CPIB, achieves the lowest corruption levels among developed democracies.

**Key structural features**:

1. **Centralized jurisdiction**: CPIB is the sole anti-corruption agency, reporting directly to the Prime Minister. No fragmentation across multiple agencies.
2. **Aggressive investigation authority**: CPIB has statutory power to:
   - Conduct warrantless inspections of government and private sector facilities suspected of corruption
   - Compel testimony
   - Freeze assets pending investigation
   - Conduct prosecutions through the state prosecutor
3. **Separated investigation from prosecution**: CPIB investigates; the state prosecutor (Attorney General's Chambers) prosecutes. Prevents investigator bias from determining outcome.
4. **Transparency and public reporting**: Annual corruption situation reports detailing conviction statistics, investigation trends, and preventive measures.
5. **Public confidence**: 2025 surveys show 98% of Singaporeans rate the corruption situation as Good/Very Good/Excellent; 95% express confidence in CPIB's work.

**Limitation for democratic societies**: The CPIB model concentrates power in a single agency reporting to the executive. In Singapore's single-party-dominant system, this creates sufficient political will for enforcement. In divided democracies, similar centralization could enable political weaponization (partisan prosecution of opposition officials).

### 4.2 South Korea's Chaebol Prosecution and Prosecution System Reform

South Korea has implemented a two-pronged anti-corruption approach: aggressive prosecution of high-level corruption combined with institutional reform to reduce future corruption.

**Recent enforcement actions**:

- **April 2025**: Former President Moon Jae-in indicted on bribery charges tied to real estate development corruption
- **September 2025**: Kim Keon Hee (wife of impeached former President Yoon Suk-yeol) tried on bribery and stock manipulation charges
- **Chaebol oversight**: Large business conglomerates (Samsung, SK, LG, Hyundai) face continuous prosecution for tax evasion, bribery, and antitrust violations; top executives regularly imprisoned

**Institutional reform**:

- **Prosecution system overhaul (effective October 2026)**: South Korea is abolishing the unified Prosecutor's Office and dividing prosecution authority into two agencies:
  - Office of Prosecutions (under Justice Ministry)
  - Serious Crimes Investigation Agency (under Interior Ministry)
  - This separation mimics the CPIB model's separation of investigation from prosecution
- **Whistleblower protection expansion (August 2024)**: Removed caps on whistleblower compensation (now up to 30% of recovered funds), incentivizing insider reporting of corruption

**South Korea's advantage**: As a democracy with multi-party competition, South Korea's use of prosecution against high-level officials is constrained by the electoral cycle. Parties use these tools but face electoral consequences if perceived as purely partisan.

### 4.3 Germany's Federal Audit Office (Bundesrechnungshof) — The Independent Watchdog Model

Germany's Federal Audit Office (Bundesrechnungshof) operates under a unique constitutional model: it reports to Parliament (Bundestag and Bundesrat), not to the executive, and is explicitly positioned outside the three constitutional branches of government.

**Key structural features**:

1. **Constitutional independence**: Not subordinate to any constitutional branch; cannot be instructed by government or parliament on individual audit decisions
2. **Broad audit mandate**: Audits all federal government spending, state-owned enterprises, and social security institutions — over €500 billion annually
3. **Public reporting**: Annual "Observations" report submitted to parliament and published for public scrutiny
4. **No prosecution authority**: Unlike CPIB or South Korean prosecutors, the audit office identifies problems and reports to parliament; parliament directs prosecutors to investigate if warranted
5. **Transparency focus**: By publishing findings, the Bundesrechnungshof uses public visibility rather than prosecution threat as the primary corruption deterrent

**Advantage for democracies**: The German model avoids centralizing prosecution power while maintaining independent accountability. The audit office cannot be weaponized for partisan prosecution because it has no prosecution authority; it can only expose facts to public scrutiny.

**Limitations**: Requires a parliament willing to act on audit findings. If parliament ignores audit reports, the system lacks enforcement teeth.

## Part 5: Structural Causes of U.S. Accountability Failure

### 5.1 Fragmentation Across Agencies Without Coordination

Unlike Singapore's centralized CPIB or South Korea's unified prosecution system, U.S. corruption accountability is fragmented:

- **OGE**: Guidance only; no enforcement
- **DOJ**: Criminal prosecution; no civil enforcement through administrative agencies
- **Agency-level ethics offices**: Report to agency heads; cannot independently enforce against leadership
- **IG system**: Internal audit; dependent on agency cooperation for investigation
- **Congress ethics committees**: Legislative branch only; cannot investigate executive or judicial officials
- **State attorneys general**: 50 independent jurisdictions with varying resources and political will
- **Local prosecutors**: Thousands of independent jurisdictions

**Consequence**: No single entity has responsibility for systemic corruption prevention. Corruption that involves interactions between federal and state actors, or across multiple federal agencies, falls into jurisdictional gaps.

### 5.2 Politicization of Enforcement Authority

Because all U.S. corruption prosecution relies either on elected officials or politically appointed prosecutors (U.S. Attorneys nominated by the President, state AGs elected by voters), enforcement authority is inherently political.

- **Partisan prosecution risk**: The party in power has incentive to prosecute opposition party officials; the opposing party faces incentive to protect its members and criticize enforcement as partisan
- **Election cycle distortion**: Corruption investigations accelerate in election years; minimal investigation during other periods
- **Selective enforcement**: Similar conduct prosecuted aggressively in one jurisdiction, ignored in another, depending on partisan composition of prosecutor's office

### 5.3 Insufficient Resources and Expertise

Federal corruption prosecution requires expertise in complex financial crimes, securities law, procurement fraud, and conflict-of-interest analysis. Current funding levels do not support:

- **Dedicated anti-corruption units** with consistent staffing and training
- **Forensic accounting capacity** to trace corruption across multiple transactions
- **Cross-jurisdictional coordination** (federal-state, state-local)
- **Proactive investigation** (most corruption prosecutions are reactive, based on whistleblower tips, rather than systematic audits)

## Part 6: Evidence-Based Reform Pathways

### 6.1 Create an Independent Federal Corruption Prosecution Authority

**Model**: A hybrid of Singapore's centralization and Germany's independence.

**Structure**:

- **Establish the Federal Accountability Office** (FAO) as a constitutional independent agency (similar to the FCC or Federal Reserve model)
- **Jurisdiction**: Investigate and prosecute:
  - Federal official corruption (executive branch employees at GS-15 and above)
  - Federal program fraud (grants, contracts, loans)
  - Public corruption involving federal funds or federal officials (even if state/local officials are also involved)
- **Leadership**: Director appointed by the President with Senate confirmation; removable only for cause (defined statute: gross negligence, criminal activity, or violation of constitutional duty)
- **Independence enforcement**: Statute prohibits removing director without congressional notification; removal attempt triggers 30-day delay during which Congress may pass a resolution of disapproval

**Advantage over current system**: Removes corruption prosecution from U.S. Attorneys (who report to Attorney General, who is political appointee). Creates dedicated expertise and eliminates the need to justify anti-corruption work within a mixed portfolio of crimes.

**Partisan safeguard**: Senate confirmation and cause-removal protection prevent one-party capture. Partisan prosecution attempts face higher institutional friction.

### 6.2 Strengthen the Office of Government Ethics — Restore Independence and Add Authority

**Current problem**: OGE has no power to enforce; it is dependent on agency ethics officers and the White House for enforcement. Recent removal of Senate-confirmed director showed the vulnerability.

**Reforms**:

1. **Strengthen cause-removal protection**: Statute specifies that OGE director may be removed only for:
   - Criminal activity
   - Gross negligence in performance of statutory duties
   - Willful violation of federal law
   - Removal requires 30-day congressional notification; Congress may pass disapproval resolution

2. **Grant administrative enforcement authority**: Empower OGE to:
   - Issue binding interpretation of ethics rules (current authority is advisory only)
   - Refer violations to agency ethics officers with required investigation and reporting
   - Refer potential criminal violations to DOJ or the Federal Accountability Office with documented timeline for action
   - Publicly report on agency compliance with ethics guidance

3. **Expand financial disclosure auditing**: Move from volunteer compliance to systematic verification:
   - Random annual audits of executive branch financial disclosures (10% sample)
   - Reconciliation with broker statements and tax filings for trades reported in disclosures
   - Refer discrepancies to agency counsel for investigation

4. **Strengthen post-employment restrictions**: Extend "cooling-off" periods for regulatory officials and increase enforcement of lobbying restrictions on former federal employees

### 6.3 Reform Congressional Ethics Enforcement — Make It Bipartisan and Mandatory

**Current problem**: Senate ethics committee requires bipartisan consensus to proceed with investigations; House committee lacks teeth despite more frequent investigations.

**Reforms**:

1. **Bifurcate Senate ethics**: Separate investigation function from adjudication:
   - Create an independent **Senate Ethics Investigation Office** (SEIO) staffed by career investigators, not subject to committee override
   - SEIO investigates all complaints meeting threshold criteria (reasonable allegation of rule violation)
   - SEIO publishes investigation results publicly
   - Senate Ethics Committee retains only adjudication authority (determining sanctions after investigation complete)

2. **Mandatory referral for financial irregularities**: Congress members' trades are automatically flagged for investigation if they:
   - Trade in securities within one week of committee meetings related to that company
   - Trade in opposite direction to congressional testimony they gave
   - Outperform market indices by >25% annually (statistical marker for information-based trading)

3. **Criminal referral autonomy**: Ethics committees refer any potential criminal violations to DOJ or the Federal Accountability Office without need for further authorization

### 6.4 Strengthen State and Local Anti-Corruption Capacity

**Problem**: State and local prosecutors operate independently with varying resources and political will.

**Reforms**:

1. **Federal grant program for state prosecutors**: Department of Justice establishes a grant program providing federal funding for state and local anti-corruption units, with conditions:
   - Participating jurisdictions establish dedicated anti-corruption teams
   - Teams pursue corruption cases without partisan consideration
   - Annual public reporting of cases, prosecutions, convictions
   - Participating jurisdictions fund at least matching amount

2. **Interstate corruption prosecution consortium**: Multi-state agreement allowing prosecutors to jointly investigate and prosecute corruption involving multiple jurisdictions (similar to existing multi-state environmental or consumer protection agreements)

3. **Require state ethics oversight**: As condition of federal funding eligibility, states establish:
   - Independent state ethics office (modeled on federal reforms above)
   - Financial disclosure requirements for state officials above certain level
   - Public financial disclosure with systematic auditing

### 6.5 Transparency and Visibility as Primary Deterrent

**Model**: Germany's Bundesrechnungshof approach — make audit findings and corruption investigations publicly visible.

**Implementation**:

1. **Federal Accountability Office public reporting**: Quarterly publication of:
   - Active investigations (number, subject matter category, agency)
   - Completed investigations (outcome, prosecution status, sanctions)
   - Case studies of significant corruption cases (with sufficient detail to educate public)

2. **Congressional ethics transparency**: 
   - Public database of all financial disclosures with searchable records
   - Automatic flagging of high-risk trades (as described above)
   - Quarterly publication of ethics committee activities

3. **Media and academic access**: Streamlined FOIA and records access for journalists and scholars tracking corruption trends

## Part 7: Implementation Roadmap

### Phase 1: Institutional Foundation (Months 1–6)

1. Establish Federal Accountability Office by statute; nominate director; obtain Senate confirmation
2. Restore OGE director's cause-removal protection; strengthen financial disclosure auditing authority
3. Reform Congressional ethics by statute (Senate SEIO, mandatory financial irregularity referrals)
4. Establish federal grant program for state anti-corruption units

### Phase 2: Operational Deployment (Months 7–18)

1. FAO begins investigations; publicizes case tracking
2. Congressional ethics system transitions to new model
3. OGE financial disclosure auditing becomes operational (random sampling, systematic verification)
4. States establish ethics offices with federal grant support

### Phase 3: Consolidation and Feedback (Months 19–36)

1. First annual FAO corruption prosecution report published
2. Congressional ethics referrals transition to formal FAO process
3. State and local anti-corruption units mature; participation grows
4. Public trust in accountability institutions measured through surveys

## Part 8: Institutional Design Tradeoffs and Risks

### Risk 1: Weaponization of Independent Prosecution Authority

**Concern**: An independent FAO could be captured by one party to prosecute opposition officials selectively.

**Mitigation**:
- Require bipartisan nomination (President nominates, requires support from Senate minority leader)
- Cause-removal protection prevents removal between elections
- Transparent case selection criteria and public reporting create visibility for selective enforcement
- Term limits (6-year terms, staggered with presidential terms) reduce party alignment

### Risk 2: Federal Authority Overreach Into State/Local Affairs

**Concern**: Federal Accountability Office prosecution of state/local officials could override state sovereignty.

**Mitigation**:
- FAO authority limited to cases involving federal funds, federal officials, or clear federal jurisdiction
- State attorneys general retain independent authority to prosecute state-level corruption
- Cooperative framework rather than preemptive federal intervention

### Risk 3: Insufficient Political Will for Enforcement

**Concern**: Even with institutional reforms, elected officials may resist prosecution of party members or political allies.

**Mitigation**:
- Transparency and public reporting create electoral consequences for inaction
- Media scrutiny of case selection and enforcement patterns
- Congressional oversight of FAO performance and budget
- Whistleblower protection and internal FAO safeguards against suppression of cases
