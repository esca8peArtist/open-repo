**Research completed**: May 15, 2026

They are backstops for domestic democratic accountability when domestic institutions fail. When the United States exits the UN Democracy Fund, International IDEA, and the UN Human Rights Council simultaneously, it removes external reporting and monitoring mechanisms that domestic civil society uses as leverage against its own government. ## Executive Summary

The leading finding of this domain is quantified: in a single executive memorandum signed January 7, 2026, the Trump administration withdrew the United States from 66 international organizations, conventions, and treaties — the largest single multilateral withdrawal event in any democracy's history. This withdrawal is not primarily a foreign policy event. It is an architectural attack on three categories of domestic democratic infrastructure: (1) accountability benchmarking mechanisms that civil society, journalists, and opposition politicians use to hold the US government to international democratic standards; (2) treaty compliance obligations that courts and Congress can enforce against executive overreach; and (3) the reputational constraint architecture that deters the most extreme domestic norm violations.

The ICC sanctions — which criminalize cooperation between US civil society and the world's primary atrocity accountability institution — illustrate the mechanism at its sharpest. Under the executive order first issued February 6, 2025 and extended through 2026, any US person providing "material support" to ICC investigations faces up to 20 years in federal prison. The Coalition for the ICC's "Criminalising Accountability" report (2026), based on 45 interviews with affected practitioners, documents the result: US-based lawyers have stopped working on Afghanistan and Palestine accountability cases; NGOs have limited public engagement; journalists are reconsidering coverage of ICC proceedings. The accountability suppression is domestic in effect even though its framing is international.

This pattern is global and accelerating. Russia was expelled from the Council of Europe in March 2022, severing 145 million people from ECHR protection — followed immediately by escalation of domestic repression that the ECHR had been one of the last external checks on. Hungary became the first EU member state to initiate ICC withdrawal in April 2025, timed to shield Netanyahu from arrest obligations during a state visit. Burkina Faso, Mali, and Niger announced coordinated ICC withdrawal in September 2025 to protect Wagner mercenaries from accountability. V-Dem's 2026 Democracy Report documents that the US is declining at the fastest rate of any democracy in the historical comparative dataset — losing its liberal democracy classification for the first time in over 50 years. Freedom House reports the 20th consecutive year of global freedom decline in 2025.

The constitutional architecture that allows this is a democratic design failure. The US Constitution requires a two-thirds Senate supermajority to ratify international treaties but is silent on the withdrawal power. The executive branch has exploited this silence to claim plenary presidential withdrawal authority. The 2024 NDAA (Section 1250A) enacted the first-ever statutory constraint on presidential withdrawal for a single treaty — NATO — establishing that Congress has the authority to constrain withdrawal when it chooses to act. For all 66 organizations withdrawn from in January 2026, no equivalent protection exists.

The reform architecture this domain proposes is legislative, specific, and grounded in existing precedent: a Treaty Withdrawal Notification Act extending the NDAA model to all Senate-ratified treaties; a Multilateral Commitment Protection Act covering a broader category of accountability institutions; and ICC Sanctions Repeal restoring US civil society's capacity to cooperate with international accountability mechanisms. The distribution window is UNGA 81 High-Level Week (September 22–28, 2026). The advocacy organizations with standing to use this research — HRW, Amnesty International, Coalition for the ICC, National Democratic Institute, Freedom House, Senate Foreign Relations Committee — are identified with specific contact information in Section 6.

## 1. The Architecture of What Was Actually Withdrawn

### 1.1 The January 7, 2026 Withdrawal — Scale and Scope

The presidential memorandum signed January 7, 2026, titled "Withdrawing the United States from International Organizations, Conventions, and Treaties that Are Contrary to the Interests of the United States," is unprecedented in scope. It covers 66 entities across five functional categories. The White House Fact Sheet characterizes the targets as "redundant in their scope, mismanaged, unnecessary, wasteful, poorly run, captured by the interests of actors advancing their own agendas contrary to our own, or a threat to our nation's sovereignty, freedoms, and general prosperity." The Al Jazeera full list (January 8, 2026) provides the complete taxonomy: 35 non-United Nations organizations and 31 United Nations entities.

WHO formal withdrawal was effectuated on January 22, 2026 — the first concrete implementation of the order, following the same procedural path as the first Trump-term WHO withdrawal in 2020 (which the Biden administration reversed within 24 hours of taking office). The remaining 65 organizations are being withdrawn from through State Department implementing action.

What the framing "contrary to US interests" conceals is what the withdrawal actually removes from the domestic democratic landscape.

### 1.2 Five Functional Categories of Withdrawn Institutions

**Category A: Electoral Integrity and Democracy Norm-Setting Bodies**

The withdrawal from International IDEA (International Institute for Democracy and Electoral Assistance) and the UN Democracy Fund removes the two primary international institutions whose explicit mission is to monitor and compare democratic electoral systems globally. These were not peripheral: International IDEA publishes the Global State of Democracy Index, Electoral Integrity Data, and Political Finance Database that domestic democracy advocates, journalists, and researchers use to hold US governments accountable to international standards. The Brennan Center, Common Cause, and the Campaign Legal Center have all cited International IDEA data in election protection litigation and advocacy. When the US exits these institutions, it does not merely reduce its financial contribution — it removes the benchmarking data from the domestic accountability conversation, because the US government's repudiation of the institution transfers to the data the institution produces.

The consequences extend to organizations whose mission depends on US multilateral engagement as a credibility source. The National Democratic Institute, International Republican Institute, and International Foundation for Electoral Systems — US civil society organizations that have historically depended on USAID and State Department democracy assistance funding — were forced to shed most of their staff by March 2025 as approximately 97% of democracy and governance initiatives were cancelled. The Global Democracy Coalition documents that electoral assistance funding was effectively eliminated. Electoral capacity that US organizations built over decades in fragile democracies has collapsed within a single fiscal year.

**Category B: Human Rights Monitoring and Reporting Bodies**

The UN Human Rights Council, OHCHR bodies, and associated treaty monitoring mechanisms provide the reporting infrastructure that domestic civil society organizations use as external leverage. When a government participates in UNHRC Universal Periodic Review, the reporting process itself generates a public record of civil society testimony, government commitments, and peer-state criticism that domestic advocates use in litigation, media, and legislative advocacy. The US's exit from UNHRC (first under Trump I, re-entered under Biden, exited again under Trump II) eliminates this external reporting function at precisely the moment when domestic civil society faces the most severe suppression.

Amnesty International USA's response to the 66-organization withdrawal states directly: "US international withdrawals aim to tear apart global cooperation" — noting that the combined effect is to remove "the accountability architecture that constrains authoritarian drift." The specific mechanism Amnesty identifies is not foreign policy: it is the removal of the external monitoring that makes domestic government conduct legible to international audiences and to domestic advocates who use international documentation as leverage.

**Category C: Climate and Scientific Accountability Bodies**

The UNFCCC and IPCC withdrawals remove the scientific accountability architecture on which domestic environmental advocates depend for litigation and legislative testimony. IPCC assessments underpin domestic environmental impact litigation, permit challenges, and the evidentiary basis for climate-related regulatory authority. Removing the US from the IPCC does not eliminate the science — it eliminates the domestic political authority of science produced by an institution the US government formally repudiates. The National Security Archive documents that the UNFCCC withdrawal breaks a bipartisan consensus going back to the George H.W. Bush administration that had survived four presidential transitions. The Union of Concerned Scientists characterizes the IPCC withdrawal specifically as sinking to "a new low" because it attacks the scientific infrastructure rather than merely the policy commitments.

**Category D: Labor, Development, and Population Monitoring Bodies**

The ILO, UNFPA, World Food Programme bodies, and related organizations provide the norm-setting architecture around labor standards, reproductive health, and food security monitoring. ILO standards function as international legal benchmarks that strengthen domestic labor litigation and legislative arguments. UNFPA's elimination as a recipient of US cooperation affects 150 countries' reproductive health programs — and removes the monitoring function through which maternal mortality, forced sterilization, and reproductive rights abuses are made internationally legible.

**Category E: Cultural, Educational, and Scientific Bodies**

UNESCO's withdrawal (the third US exit from UNESCO) eliminates US participation in international standards for freedom of expression, protection of journalists, and safeguarding of cultural heritage. UNESCO maintains the Freedom of Expression Observatory and the Safety of Journalists platform, which document global press freedom violations and support journalists under threat. The withdrawal removes US financial and diplomatic backing from these platforms at precisely the moment when domestic press freedom is under the most severe executive attack since the McCarthy era.

### 1.3 The WHO Withdrawal as Proof of Concept

The formal WHO withdrawal on January 22, 2026 — 15 days after the presidential memorandum — demonstrates the pace and method of implementation. The US contributed approximately $893 million to WHO in 2023, representing the largest single-country contribution. Withdrawal removes this financial leverage along with US participation in emergency response coordination, pandemic preparedness planning, and global health security architecture. The domestic consequence is measurable: US public health agencies lose the cooperative surveillance infrastructure through which emerging infectious disease threats are identified, assessed, and addressed before reaching US shores. The COVID-19 pandemic was the last successful deployment of this architecture; the next pandemic response will require US agencies to replicate, at domestic cost, what WHO coordination previously provided at shared international cost.

## 2. Five Causal Pathways — How Multilateral Withdrawal Dismantles Democratic Infrastructure

### 2.1 Pathway One: Accountability Infrastructure Removal

International institutions constrain executive power through three mechanisms: treaty obligations that must be honored or formally violated (creating political cost), reporting requirements that generate legible records of policy performance, and peer monitoring from allied democracies that raises the reputational cost of norm defection. Withdrawal removes each of these mechanisms simultaneously.

The domestic democratic consequence is not primarily geopolitical. It is the removal of the benchmarking architecture that domestic civil society uses to hold the government accountable. International IDEA's electoral integrity database, OECD democracy metrics, WHO health system comparisons, and IPCC climate assessments generate legible comparative data that civil society, journalists, and opposition politicians deploy in domestic accountability conversations. When a government exits the institutions that produce this data, it removes the data from those conversations — not by suppressing it directly but by stripping it of the institutional authority that makes it actionable in courts, legislatures, and media.

The ICC sanctions mechanism illustrates the most acute version: any US person who provides "material support" to ICC investigations faces up to 20 years in federal prison. The Coalition for the ICC's "Criminalising Accountability" report (2026) — based on 45 interviews with affected practitioners — documents that US-based lawyers have stopped working on Afghanistan and Palestine accountability cases; NGOs have limited public engagement with ICC proceedings; journalists are self-censoring on ICC-related coverage. The accountability suppression extends domestically: US civil society organizations that would use international accountability mechanisms as leverage in domestic advocacy are legally deterred from doing so. This is not a foreign policy consequence — it is a First Amendment and civil society problem operating under the cover of international legal framing.

G.J. Ikenberry's theoretical framework in "Liberal Leviathan" (Princeton, 2011) provides the structural explanation: the liberal international order functions partly as a mechanism through which the leading state accepts constraints on its own behavior that increase the credibility of its commitments to partners. When the US exits this architecture, it does not merely change its foreign policy posture — it removes the domestic constraints that participation imposed, enabling a concentration of executive power that would have been harder to achieve while remaining inside accountability institutions. Ikenberry's concept of "voice opportunities" — the channels through which smaller states and domestic civil society can input into the rules-based system — is exactly what multilateral withdrawal eliminates.

### 2.2 Pathway Two: Domestic Crackdown Enablement

Multilateral withdrawal correlates strongly with escalation of domestic repression: the removal of external scrutiny creates political space for intensified suppression of civil society, independent media, and political opposition. The causal direction runs both ways — authoritarian governments withdraw from accountability institutions in anticipation of conduct they expect to be scrutinized, and the removal of scrutiny enables conduct they would not have undertaken while under observation.

Russia's post-ECHR trajectory illustrates the mechanism with precision. Russia ceased to be a party to the European Convention on Human Rights on September 16, 2022 — removed after the invasion of Ukraine. The Council of Europe's Secretary General noted that the departure left "145 million Russians no longer protected by the European Convention." The months following formal departure saw escalation of political prosecutions, independent media closures, and suppression of anti-war civil society organizations whose members could no longer access the ECHR as a route for legal challenge. The ECHR had been one of the last functioning external pressure points on Russian domestic repression; its removal correlated precisely with acceleration of the crackdown. Multiple Russian human rights advocates documented to OpenGlobalRights that the ECHR right of individual petition — the ability to take a case against the Russian government to the Strasbourg court — was the concrete legal mechanism that individual dissidents, journalists, and opposition figures had relied on. With the court exit, that mechanism disappeared.

Hungary's domestic trajectory after Orbán consolidated power included media capture, civil society law modeled on Russia's "foreign agent" legislation, judicial independence erosion, and systematic gerrymandering — all before ICC withdrawal. But the ICC withdrawal announcement, made precisely as Netanyahu arrived for a state visit and Hungary became the first EU member state to initiate withdrawal (June 2, 2025 formal UN notification), signals that external criminal accountability is now explicitly rejected. This is the authoritarian base signaling function: it communicates to the domestic base that the government has severed the constraints imposed by international criminal accountability institutions. As Human Rights Watch documents (June 16, 2025), Hungary's withdrawal will make it "the only non-ICC member state in the European Union" — an unprecedented status that inverts Hungary's relationship to the EU's rule-of-law obligations.

### 2.3 Pathway Three: Authoritarian Base Signaling and Norm Cascade

Multilateral withdrawal functions as domestic political communication: it signals to the authoritarian coalition that rule-based international constraint has been severed. The framing deployed across jurisdictions is identical: Orbán called the ICC "a political court" engaged in "politically motivated indictments"; the Trump administration characterized international organizations as "wasteful, ineffective, or harmful"; the Sahel junta governments framed ICC withdrawal as resistance to "neo-colonialist repression." The convergence across geographically and politically disparate actors is not coincidental — it reflects a coordinated authoritarian communication playbook documented in V-Dem's research, in which "anti-multilateralism rhetoric" is a systematic component of autocratization strategies.

V-Dem's 2026 Democracy Report, released March 17, 2026, makes the quantitative case: "the speed with which American democracy is currently dismantled is unprecedented in modern history." The report classifies the US as an "electoral democracy" for the first time in over 50 years — retaining free and fair elections but losing the liberal components: strong checks and balances, individual protections from "tyranny of the majority," and constraints on government overreach. By V-Dem's formal scores, the US declined from 0.75 under Biden to 0.57 under Trump — a 24% decline. Freedom of expression in the US "is now at its lowest level since the end of WWII." Six out of ten new autocratizing countries identified in the 2026 Democracy Report are in Europe and North America.

Freedom House's "The Growing Shadow of Autocracy" (Freedom in the World 2026) documents the 20th consecutive year of global freedom decline: 54 countries experienced deterioration in political rights and civil liberties while only 35 improved. Just 21% of the world's population now lives in countries rated "free" — down from 46% twenty years ago. The correlation between US multilateral disengagement and accelerating global democratic decline is not established in the literature as direct causation, but the mechanism is clear: US participation in international accountability institutions was a primary enforcement mechanism for the liberal international order, and its removal weakens the deterrent architecture for all states in the system.

### 2.4 Pathway Four: Alliance Fragmentation and Democratic Coalition Collapse

The Trump administration's posture toward NATO Article 5 has produced a structural shift in the transatlantic democratic coalition. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth's refusal to reaffirm the US Article 5 commitment — documented by Modern Diplomacy (April 3, 2026) as "introducing conditionality into the one part of NATO that was designed to be least conditional" — has accelerated European institutional independence in ways that will outlast any single US administration.

EU defense chiefs are actively developing Article 42.7 of the Treaty on European Union — the EU's own mutual defense clause — as an institutional alternative to NATO's Article 5. In May 2026, the EU launched tabletop simulations to test the clause's activation, starting with ambassadors in Brussels and defense ministers in Cyprus. Al Jazeera (April 30, 2026) documents the EU drawing up a detailed "blueprint" for activating Article 42.7 in the event of armed aggression against a member state. The Eurasian Times reports that the EU is "strikes back with Article 42.7 mutual defense blueprint to counter Trump's NATO doubts." This is not merely military contingency planning — it is the institutional architecture of a democratic coalition that has concluded US commitment cannot be reliably assumed.

For the domestic democracy framework, the consequence is this: US withdrawal from multilateral security and norm-enforcement institutions does not weaken authoritarian actors in isolation — it weakens the capacity of democratic coalitions globally to resist autocratic norm erosion. When Hungary's Fidesz government tests the boundaries of democratic backsliding within EU/NATO structures, the US has historically been the external pressure point. Withdrawal eliminates that pressure and signals to domestic actors pursuing similar erosions that international constraint is negotiable. The European Council on Foreign Relations documents the emerging architecture in "After the Rupture: Middle Powers and the Construction of New Order" — a post-US-hegemony order in which middle powers must build resilience independent of American commitment.

### 2.5 Pathway Five: The Constitutional Asymmetry — Entry Requires the Senate, Exit Does Not

The withdrawal mechanism is itself a democratic design problem of the first order. The US Constitution's treaty-making clause (Article II, Section 2) requires Senate consent — a two-thirds supermajority — to ratify international treaties. This threshold reflects the Founders' judgment that international commitments of permanent duration require broad democratic legitimacy. But the Constitution is silent on treaty withdrawal. There is no analogous provision requiring Senate consent to exit what the Senate approved to enter. This asymmetry is not a minor oversight; it is an architectural vulnerability that authoritarian-adjacent executives can exploit to undo multilateral commitments that required supermajority democratic approval to create.

The Congressional Research Service report R48868, "Separation of Powers and NATO Withdrawal" (February 27, 2026) — commissioned by Senate Foreign Relations Committee members specifically to address this gap — analyzes the constitutional framework with rigor. CRS documents that the Office of Legal Counsel published a 2020 opinion concluding that "the President has exclusive power over treaty withdrawal and that Congress is constitutionally prohibited from intruding upon this power." This OLC opinion reflects the executive branch's maximalist claim. CRS's analysis under the Youngstown framework (Jackson's concurring opinion, Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co. v. Sawyer, 1952) — the operative framework for assessing contested foreign affairs power — suggests that courts would scrutinize with caution any claim of exclusive executive authority over withdrawal when Congress has explicitly legislated otherwise.

The 2024 NDAA (Section 1250A) enacted the first-ever statutory constraint on presidential withdrawal from a specific treaty — NATO — requiring Senate consent or an act of Congress. This precedent establishes three critical legal propositions: (1) Congress has the constitutional authority to constrain presidential withdrawal when it chooses to act; (2) the political question doctrine does not automatically insulate withdrawal from judicial review; and (3) the Youngstown framework applies when Congress has legislated. For all 66 organizations withdrawn from in January 2026, no equivalent protection exists. The constitutional asymmetry — Senate ratification required to enter, no Senate approval required to exit — is not an immutable feature of constitutional design. It is a structural gap that can be closed through legislation, using the NDAA model as precedent.

## 3. The Global Pattern — Who Else Is Withdrawing and Why

### 3.1 Russia: Council of Europe Exit and the Domestic Repression Template

Russia's March 2022 withdrawal from the Council of Europe — the first state expulsion in the institution's 73-year history — established the template that subsequent withdrawal actors have followed. Russia preemptively announced its intention to withdraw on March 15, 2022, one day before the Committee of Ministers voted to expel it following the Ukraine invasion, claiming sovereign agency (withdrawal rather than expulsion) while eliminating the last independent legal route for Russian nationals to challenge government abuses.

Russia ceased to be a party to the ECHR on September 16, 2022. The Council of Europe's Secretary General noted that the departure left 145 million Russians without ECHR protection. The ECHR's Committee on Legal Affairs and Human Rights documented the consequences: Russian individuals lost the right of individual petition that had been, for many dissidents and human rights defenders, the only practically accessible external accountability mechanism. The correlation with domestic repression escalation — mass arrests of anti-war protesters, media closures, criminal prosecutions of civil society organizations — is direct and documented.

Russia's trajectory demonstrates the mechanism at scale: the removal of external accountability removes the deterrent that moderates extreme conduct. When the Kremlin knew that abusive actions would face ECHR review, some marginal deterrent existed. After expulsion, that deterrent disappeared entirely.

### 3.2 Hungary: The First EU Member State ICC Withdrawal

Hungary announced ICC withdrawal on April 3, 2025, timed to coincide with Benjamin Netanyahu's state visit — a deliberate political signal that Hungary would not honor its ICC arrest warrant obligations. Prime Minister Orbán characterized the ICC as "a political court" — using the framing that authoritarian actors worldwide deploy to delegitimize accountability institutions. The formal notification to the UN Secretary-General was deposited on June 2, 2025, making the withdrawal effective June 2, 2026 — one year later.

This makes Hungary the first EU member state to initiate ICC withdrawal, creating a precedent with significant implications for EU internal law. Verfassungsblog's analysis ("How Hungary's Withdrawal from the ICC Affects EU Credibility") raises the question of whether an EU member state's withdrawal from the Rome Statute constitutes a violation of EU Treaty Article 2 obligations regarding human rights and rule of law — potentially triggering Article 7 proceedings. The European Council on Foreign Relations ("Hold the Line: EU Actions Must Counter Orbán and Netanyahu's Defiance of the ICC") argues that the EU must respond with concrete consequences, including EU sanctions conditioned on reversal of the ICC withdrawal.

Human Rights Watch documents (June 16, 2025) that Hungary will become "the only non-ICC member state in the European Union" upon formal exit — an unprecedented status that raises the EU's internal accountability problem for one of its member states to a new level of urgency.

### 3.2a Hungary Update — May 2026: Democratic Reversal and Planned ICC Rejoin

**May 14-17, 2026 DEVELOPMENT**: Hungary's political landscape shifted dramatically with the April 12, 2026 parliamentary election, in which Péter Magyar's Tisza Party won a landslide 53.6 percent victory, ending Viktor Orbán's Fidesz 16-year rule. This is a material correction to the analysis in section 3.2, which characterized Hungary's June 2, 2026 ICC withdrawal as effectively imminent and irreversible.

**Key findings:**

- **Electoral outcome**: The Tisza Party secured a supermajority in parliament. Magyar's campaign successfully contested the information space through platforms outside Fidesz's media control and achieved record 77 percent turnout. Youth voters provided decisive margin.
- **Magyar's ICC pledge**: Immediately after the election, Magyar pledged to halt Hungary's ICC withdrawal and stated he would submit a rejoinder application to rejoin the ICC. He committed that Hungary will enforce ICC arrest warrants, including against Benjamin Netanyahu — a direct reversal of Orbán's policy of hosting Netanyahu despite the warrant.
- **Procedural timeline**: The legal withdrawal under Article 127 of the Rome Statute cannot be stopped before June 2, 2026 (the one-year notice period expires automatically). However, the new government can submit a rejoinder application immediately after June 2, 2026, to rejoin the ICC. The rejoinder application would be processed under Article 127.3 of the Rome Statute, which provides for rejoining after withdrawal. 
- **Structural significance**: This demonstrates that multilateral withdrawal is reversible when democratic governments are restored. The EU has leverage tools (trade preferences, membership conditionality) that can be deployed to support the new government's commitment. The Tisza victory is the clearest contemporary proof-of-concept that information autocracy has limits and can be defeated through democratic mobilization.

The correct framing is: Hungary represents a case study in democratic reversal *containing* an ICC withdrawal, not simply a data point in institutional-withdrawal erosion. Orbán's use of the ICC withdrawal as a political signal to his domestic base — severing external accountability as a marker of authoritarian consolidation — is being explicitly reversed by the incoming government's commitment to rejoin and enforce warrants.

3. **NATO/EU coordination**: The new government's position creates a structural shift in EU-Hungary relations. Member states that have been constrained from enforcing Article 7 proceedings now have a government indicating willingness to comply with rule-of-law obligations. The reversibility of the withdrawal commitment should be treated as the baseline expectation for the new government's first 100 days.

### 3.3 Sahel States: Coordinated Withdrawal as Wagner Impunity Shield

On September 22, 2025 — timed to coincide with UNGA High-Level Week — the military juntas of Burkina Faso, Mali, and Niger announced coordinated ICC withdrawal from a joint Alliance of Sahel States platform. The framing invoked "neo-colonialist repression." The operational purpose was transparent: shielding the military juntas and their Russian Wagner Group partners from ICC investigation. Human Rights Watch documents (September 24, 2025) that each of the three regimes came to power through coups and faces credible allegations of atrocities — including Wagner Group killings of approximately 32 civilians in Mali documented in HRW's 2024 report.

The Africa Report documents that as of the time of research, formal withdrawal instruments have not been deposited with the UN Secretary-General — suggesting the announcement was partly political signaling rather than fully executed withdrawal. Whether formal withdrawal is completed or the announcement merely functions as de facto non-compliance signal, the accountability-undermining effect is similar: ICC investigators face a hostile environment in which cooperation with witnesses and victims is dangerous, and in which the government has publicly repudiated the institution's legitimacy.

FIDH characterizes the Sahel withdrawal as "a step back for victims and justice" and specifically links it to the broader global pattern: "This withdrawal follows that of Hungary, itself unprecedented as an EU member state, and comes in a context where the USA has sanctioned ICC officials."

### 3.4 Turkey: Non-Compliance as De Facto Functional Withdrawal

Turkey represents a distinct pattern: maintaining formal ECHR membership while systematically refusing to comply with adverse rulings. Turkey accounts for 35.8% of all pending ECHR cases — 21,600 applications as of the most recent data — and has 137 unresolved "leading cases," many pending for over five years. The infringement proceedings against Turkey for non-implementation of the Kavala v. Turkey judgment — only the second infringement proceeding in the Council of Europe's history — represent the institution's most powerful enforcement mechanism, but remain pending without resolution. Turkey has continued to hold Kavala despite ECHR rulings, consecutive arrest orders, and the Committee of Ministers' finding that Turkey is in "flagrant breach" of the Convention.

The June 2025 joint briefing by Human Rights Watch, the International Commission of Jurists, and the Turkey Human Rights Litigation Support Project documents "how Turkey is openly defying the European Court of Human Rights and dismantling the last remnants of judicial independence within its borders." This is the alternative to formal withdrawal: systematic non-compliance that achieves most of the impunity benefits of exit while avoiding the political costs of formal departure.

## 4. Resistance Architecture — Civil Society Response and Universal Jurisdiction as Accountability Backstop

### 4.1 The Universal Jurisdiction Annual Review 2025 — Demonstrating Alternative Infrastructure

The most operationally active resistance mechanism functioning at scale is universal jurisdiction prosecution in European national courts. The Universal Jurisdiction Annual Review 2025 — published jointly by TRIAL International, FIDH, ECCHR, REDRESS, Civitas Maxima, and the Center for Justice and Accountability — documents 34 new universal jurisdiction cases opened or made public in 2025, and 23 convictions secured. The review compiles developments in 91 extraterritorial and universal jurisdiction cases prosecuted across 20 countries.

Several 2025 cases set important precedents: the first conviction for crimes committed in the context of the 2014 war in Eastern Ukraine; the first conviction for genocide through the transfer of children to another group; the first trial concerning the war crime of starvation; the first conviction for crimes committed during the Second Congo War (1998–2003). Four new states — Kosovo, Peru, Poland, and Turkey — took steps to exercise universal jurisdiction for the first time in 2025.

These convictions demonstrate that the accountability architecture is more resilient than the ICC's institutional vulnerability to great-power withdrawal. Germany, France, Sweden, the Netherlands, Switzerland, Belgium, Austria, and Spain maintain active universal jurisdiction prosecution programs. These national courts cannot be sanctioned by US executive order; they operate under domestic constitutional authority; and their prosecutorial capacity is not dependent on US participation. When the ICC is under attack, universal jurisdiction courts fill the gap — not as a substitute for the ICC's institutional authority but as a decentralized accountability infrastructure that is specifically resilient to state withdrawal from centralized accountability institutions.

The CICC's "Sanctions Cannot Silence Justice" civil society coalition — coordinating the response to US sanctions against ICC officials — demonstrates that civil society accountability work continues despite the chilling effects documented in "Criminalising Accountability." As of May 2026, the ICC Prosecutor's Office continues to operate, the Trust Fund for Victims maintains active programs, and investigations in Ukraine, Palestine, and Afghanistan continue — though the work of US persons in supporting these investigations is severely constrained.

### 4.2 Civil Society Documentation Networks

The organizational ecology of resistance to multilateral withdrawal is dense, experienced, and specifically positioned:

**Human Rights Watch** maintains the most comprehensive civil society documentation of the withdrawal pattern. HRW's reporting on Hungary's ICC withdrawal (June 16, 2025), the Sahel states' ICC withdrawal (September 24, 2025), US sanctions against ICC officials (March 11, 2026 CSO condemnation), and the ECHR non-compliance pattern provides the institutional memory and analytical framing for advocacy.

**Amnesty International** has characterized the 66-organization US withdrawal as an attempt to "tear apart global cooperation," framed Hungary's withdrawal as creating continued legal obligations that cannot be excused by political calculation, and condemned the Sahel withdrawals as "a serious backwards step in the fight against impunity."

**Coalition for the International Criminal Court (CICC)** — a network of 2,500+ civil society organizations in 150+ countries — is the primary coordination mechanism for ICC-specific resistance. The "Criminalising Accountability" report is its most consequential advocacy document in the current cycle: a fully documented, interview-based account of the sanctions' chilling effects that provides the civil society testimony that no government agency will produce.

**FIDH**, **TRIAL International**, **ECCHR**, **REDRESS**, and **Civitas Maxima** constitute the universal jurisdiction litigation network — the organizations that bring accountability cases in European national courts when ICC access is constrained. These organizations are the operational infrastructure for the accountability backstop that universal jurisdiction provides.

**Stimson Center** provides the institutional analysis: "The United Nations Was Built to Deliver Accountability. Can It Still Do So?" (2026) examines what the UN accountability architecture can sustain under great-power pressure, with specific attention to the combination of US multilateral withdrawal, Chinese UN obstruction, and Russian exit from CoE.

### 4.3 Allied Democracy Counter-Defunding

The vacuum left by US defunding of international accountability institutions creates a specific resistance opportunity: allied democracies increasing their contributions to fill the gap. The EU has committed to increased multilateral institutional support in response to US withdrawal. Canada, Australia, Japan, South Korea, and Nordic states have similar capacity. The ICC Trust Fund for Victims — which supports victim reparations programs — depends on voluntary contributions that US withdrawal has reduced; civil society fundraising and allied government contributions are the specific countermeasure.

For organizations that depended on US government democracy assistance (NDI, IRI, IFES), the reconstruction of funding through European bilateral donors, EU programs, and private foundations is already underway, though at reduced scale. The Carnegie Endowment's March 2025 analysis ("Does US Democracy Aid Have a Future?") documents the funding landscape and identifies the EU's Global Gateway democracy program as the most likely source of replacement funding at scale.

## 7. Reform Architecture — Three Legislative Pathways

### 7.1 Treaty Withdrawal Notification Act

**Core provision**: Require 90-day advance congressional notification before the President withdraws the United States from any Senate-ratified treaty or any international organization whose participation was authorized by act of Congress.

**Congressional override mechanism**: Within the 90-day notification period, Congress may pass a joint resolution prohibiting the withdrawal, creating a statutory prohibition that the President may veto but that the Youngstown framework would require courts to scrutinize with "utmost care" as within the congressional zone of authority.

**Constitutional basis**: Section 1250A of the FY2024 NDAA establishes that Congress can prohibit presidential withdrawal from specific treaties without triggering successful political question or separation-of-powers challenges. The Treaty Withdrawal Notification Act extends this model from a single treaty to a general procedural requirement. CRS R48868 provides the constitutional framework for this extension: courts are now "less likely to dismiss a challenge to a President's withdrawal from the North Atlantic Treaty in violation of Section 1250A on political question grounds" — a doctrinal development that supports the broader notification architecture.

**Legislative model**: Senator Tim Kaine and Congressman Mike Quigley have previously introduced treaty protection legislation; these bill frameworks provide the drafting precedent. The Senate Foreign Relations Committee's existing treaty jurisdiction provides the committee referral structure.

### 7.2 Multilateral Commitment Protection Act

**Core provision**: Designate a category of "core accountability institutions" — the ICC, UN Human Rights Council, International IDEA, OECD, and WHO — whose continued US participation is subject to a congressional authorization requirement, not merely a notification requirement. Withdrawal from designated institutions would require an act of Congress.

**Scope determination**: Congress defines "core accountability institutions" based on two criteria: (1) US participation was authorized by act of Congress (not solely by executive action), and (2) the institution generates accountability data used by US courts, regulatory agencies, or civil society in domestic legal and advocacy proceedings. Both criteria are objectively determinable.

**Constitutional basis**: Where Congress has authorized US participation in an international organization through statute, the President's authority to exit is most constrained under the Youngstown framework — within the zone where "congressional inertia, indifference, or acquiescence may sometimes, at least as a practical matter, enable, if not invite, measures on independent presidential responsibility" (Jackson concurrence at 637), but where affirmative congressional authorization to participate implies a congressional interest in continued participation.

### 7.3 ICC Sanctions Repeal

**Core provision**: Repeal the executive order (first issued February 6, 2025, extended through 2026) imposing asset freezes and entry bans on ICC officials, and remove the criminalization of "material support" to ICC investigations for US persons.

**Civil society restoration**: Specifically restore US civil society organizations' capacity to cooperate with ICC investigations, provide legal representation in ICC proceedings, and report on ICC matters without criminal exposure. The "Criminalising Accountability" report provides the documented harm that repeal would remedy.

**Precedent**: The US has withdrawn sanctions imposed by prior administrations on specific grounds numerous times. Congress has the authority to repeal executive-order sanctions through legislation, and such repeal legislation has been enacted previously. The ICC Sanctions Repeal would restore a specific category of civil society and legal professional freedom that the sanctions criminalized.

### Update 1: Albanese Sanctions Litigation — First Federal Court Constitutional Engagement (May–June 2026)

The most significant post-May-21 development for Domain 57's ICC sanctions analysis is the Albanese federal litigation. On May 13, 2026, Federal District Court Judge Richard Leon (DC District) ruled in favor of UN Special Rapporteur Francesca Albanese, issuing a temporary injunction that paused her ICC-related sanctions designation. Judge Leon's reasoning engaged directly with the constitutional question Domain 57 identifies as underexamined: he found that foreign nationals located abroad may possess constitutional due process rights if they demonstrate "substantial connections" to the United States. Albanese's ownership of US property and her US-citizen daughter constituted such connections.

This ruling was the first federal court engagement with the constitutional validity of the ICC sanctions regime. It did not hold the sanctions unconstitutional on their merits — it found only that the due process question was sufficiently serious to warrant temporary injunctive relief during litigation.

On May 23–27, 2026, a three-judge panel of the DC Circuit Court of Appeals issued an administrative stay, reinstating the sanctions while the merits are briefed. The appeals court authorized the executive branch to "implement and enforce" the sanctions pending further filings in early June 2026 and a merits determination.

**Implications for Domain 57's Section 7.3 (ICC Sanctions Repeal)**:
