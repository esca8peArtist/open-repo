## Executive Summary

The climate policy rollbacks executed between January 2025 and May 2026 are not, in their structural character, a policy disagreement about the appropriate balance between economic growth and environmental protection. They are the administrative override of the epistemic infrastructure of democratic climate governance — the systematic dismantlement of the institutional mechanisms through which scientific consensus was translated into policy authority, and through which communities held legal standing to participate in decisions affecting their health.

Three actions define this structural character. First, on February 12, 2026, EPA Administrator Lee Zeldin signed a final rule rescinding the 2009 Greenhouse Gas Endangerment Finding — the legal instrument through which the Supreme Court's 2007 mandate in *Massachusetts v. EPA* was operationalized into regulatory authority over vehicle emissions, power plant pollution, and industrial greenhouse gas output. The rescission does not dispute the underlying climate science; the National Academy of Sciences issued a formal report affirming the 2009 finding remained accurate and was strengthened by 17 additional years of evidence. The rescission is an administrative declaration that the legal translation of that science into regulatory authority was wrong. A federal court had already ruled, on January 30, 2026, that the DOE Climate Working Group assembled to provide the scientific basis for the proposed rescission was assembled through a process that violated the Federal Advisory Committee Act — an illegal epistemic input to a deregulatory action now challenged in D.C. Circuit. Second, CEQ removed all binding NEPA regulations from the Code of Federal Regulations effective January 8, 2026, replacing them with non-binding guidance. Federal agencies have since proceeded with major energy and infrastructure projects — including fossil fuel development on federal lands — with comment periods as short as five days, no requirement to publish draft environmental impact statements, and no obligation to respond to submitted public comments. The public comment period is not an administrative formality; it is the structural mechanism through which communities hold legal standing to contest decisions about infrastructure affecting their health. Its elimination is the elimination of a democratic participation right. Third, EPA's proposed FY2026 budget cuts total funding by 54 percent from FY2025 levels, including a 30 percent reduction in civil enforcement, a 49 percent cut to criminal enforcement, a 35 percent decrease in compliance monitoring, and the complete elimination of environmental justice enforcement funding. These are not budget reductions; they are the administrative disabling of the enforcement capacity that makes regulatory authority real.

The analytical parallel to Domain 42 is precise and load-bearing. The DEA scheduling process was captured by a law enforcement agency whose institutional incentives systematically bias scheduling outcomes regardless of public health evidence. The EPA endangerment and NEPA process has been captured by an administration whose institutional interests — in fossil fuel deregulation, in blocking the regulatory authority established by *Massachusetts v. EPA* — systematically override scientific consensus and legal standing regardless of the democratic majority's expressed preferences on climate action. In both cases, the institutions that translate scientific consensus and democratic will into policy authority have been administratively disabled. The reform architecture in both domains must restore the epistemic infrastructure, not merely the policy output.

## Section A: Endangerment Finding Rescission — Legal Basis and Scientific Dispute

### A.1 The 2009 Finding: What It Was and Why It Mattered

The 2009 Greenhouse Gas Endangerment Finding was not a climate policy. It was the legal precondition for climate policy. Following the Supreme Court's 2007 decision in *Massachusetts v. EPA* — which held 5-4 that greenhouse gases qualify as "air pollutants" under the Clean Air Act's capacious statutory definition — EPA conducted a comprehensive scientific review and concluded that "the current and projected concentrations of the six key well-mixed greenhouse gases...in the atmosphere threaten the public health and welfare of current and future generations."

This conclusion, formalized in a 2009 rule published under the Obama administration, did three things simultaneously. It operationalized the Supreme Court's directive that EPA had authority to regulate greenhouse gases under the Clean Air Act. It provided the mandatory legal basis for subsequent vehicle emission standards (the "tailpipe rule"), power plant regulations, and industrial emissions standards. And it created the legal foundation through which communities, states, and public health organizations had standing to challenge regulatory inaction — because once EPA had found that a substance endangers public health, the Clean Air Act required action.

The 2009 finding was not a one-time scientific judgment. It was validated by the D.C. Circuit in 2012, upheld unanimously by a three-judge panel that dismissed all challenges. It was reaffirmed by successive administrations' regulatory reviews. Every major scientific body that examined its underlying claims confirmed them. The National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine issued a formal report in 2026 — submitted during the public comment period for the proposed rescission — concluding that the 2009 finding was accurate and had been strengthened by 17 additional years of accumulating evidence. A group of 85 climate scientists submitted a point-by-point rebuttal of the alternative report prepared for DOE, writing that it "exhibits pervasive problems with misrepresentation."

### A.2 The February 12, 2026 Rescission: What EPA Did

On February 12, 2026, EPA Administrator Lee Zeldin signed a final rule rescinding the 2009 Endangerment Finding. The rule was published in the Federal Register on February 18, 2026 (91 Fed. Reg. 7686) and became effective April 20, 2026. The rule simultaneously repealed all vehicle greenhouse gas emission standards — covering light-, medium-, and heavy-duty vehicles, including standards reaching back to model year 2012.

EPA's legal basis for the rescission rested on a reinterpretation of Clean Air Act Section 202(a)(1): the agency concluded that the provision "does not authorize the Agency to prescribe emission standards in response to global climate change concerns" because the statutory terms "air pollution," "cause," "contribute," and "reasonably be anticipated to endanger" do not, on EPA's new reading, extend to global atmospheric phenomena. This interpretation directly contradicts the Supreme Court's interpretation in *Massachusetts v. EPA*, which held that greenhouse gases "fit well within the CAA's capacious definition of air pollutant." The tension between EPA's new statutory interpretation and the Supreme Court's 2007 holding is the central legal vulnerability of the rescission — and the central issue in the D.C. Circuit litigation.

The rescission also initially proposed relying on a report prepared by the DOE Climate Working Group — a body assembled by Energy Secretary Chris Wright in 2025 to challenge climate science consensus. On January 30, 2026, U.S. District Court Judge William Young ruled that the Climate Working Group had violated the Federal Advisory Committee Act: it was not properly chartered, did not hold open meetings, did not maintain public records, and was not assembled with the balanced membership FACA requires. The court declared the violations "established as a matter of law." Notably, the final EPA rule dropped its reliance on the DOE Climate Working Group report entirely — suggesting the administration recognized the legal fragility of relying on an unlawfully assembled advisory body. The unlawful process is nonetheless relevant to the administrative record underlying the rescission, and the FACA violation forms part of the broader evidentiary record in pending litigation.

### A.3 The Scientific Dispute: What the NAS Found

The National Academies of Sciences, Engineering and Medicine's 2026 report concluded unambiguously that the 2009 Endangerment Finding was accurate and scientifically sound. The report found that 17 additional years of evidence had strengthened, not weakened, the scientific basis for the finding. Global average temperatures have risen approximately 1.2 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels. The frequency and intensity of extreme weather events attributable to climate change has increased. Sea level rise has accelerated. The concentration of atmospheric CO2 has continued to climb, now exceeding 425 parts per million — a level not seen in 3 million years of Earth's geological record.

EPA did not dispute these scientific findings in the final rule. The rescission is not a scientific disagreement about whether greenhouse gases cause warming or whether warming endangers health. It is a legal and interpretive claim that the Clean Air Act, properly read, does not authorize EPA to regulate based on those harms — a claim that requires a reading of the statute that conflicts with existing Supreme Court precedent.

This distinction is analytically critical. The Endangerment Finding rescission is the administrative override of the legal translation of scientific consensus into policy authority. The science is intact. The court precedent requiring action is intact. What has been eliminated is the administrative instrument through which that science and that precedent were operationalized into regulatory authority. This is precisely the structure of Domain 42's regulatory capture finding: the mechanism by which evidence translates into policy has been captured and disabled, not the evidence itself.

### A.4 Health and Economic Costs of the Rescission

The cost projections attached to the rescission are contested and severe. EPA's own regulatory impact analysis found that the vehicle standards repeal is "net costly by $180 billion" when health benefits are quantified against compliance cost savings. The analysis published by Professors Jonathan Adler and Caroline Welty in The Regulatory Review (April 21, 2026) documented that EPA's public presentation systematically excluded the benefit side of the ledger: the standards would have avoided $87 billion in annual climate damages, prevented over 2,500 early deaths per year from improved air quality, and avoided more than 1 million asthma attacks annually.

Environmental Defense Fund's analysis extended the timeline: the cumulative effect of the vehicle standards repeal and the Endangerment Finding rescission together would add 18 billion tons of greenhouse gas pollution to the atmosphere, leading to 77,000 more early deaths and 52 million more asthma attacks by 2055. These projections reflect the compounding effect of removing the regulatory floor across all motor vehicle categories rather than sector-specific estimates.

The American Public Health Association, the American Lung Association, the Allergy and Asthma Network, and multiple public health organizations issued formal statements documenting the health implications. Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health researchers characterized the rescission as "dismantling the regulatory infrastructure that has produced measurable improvements in American air quality and public health over the past 15 years."

### A.5 The D.C. Circuit Litigation: Status and Stakes

Within hours of the February 18, 2026 Federal Register publication, seventeen environmental and public health organizations — represented by Earthjustice — filed a petition for review in the D.C. Circuit. Two separate organizations filed a petition on behalf of a group of children. NRDC, the Clean Air Task Force, and state attorneys general from California, New York, and twelve other states filed additional petitions. The 60-day filing window triggered by Federal Register publication consolidated these challenges into a complex multi-party proceeding.

The primary legal arguments advanced in the D.C. Circuit petitions are:

**First**: EPA's new statutory interpretation of Section 202(a)(1) as not extending to global atmospheric pollutants is irreconcilable with *Massachusetts v. EPA* (2007), which the Supreme Court decided on the same statutory text. EPA cannot administratively override a Supreme Court statutory interpretation; only the Supreme Court can overrule itself.

**Second**: EPA's cost-benefit analysis violated the Administrative Procedure Act by systematically excluding the health and climate benefits of the vehicle standards, producing a record that misrepresents the net economic effects of the repeal.

**Third**: The administrative record underlying the rescission was procedurally tainted by the DOE Climate Working Group's FACA violations, even though EPA dropped its reliance on the DOE report in the final rule.

**Fourth**: The rescission effectively nullifies a dozen years of validly issued regulations based on the 2012 D.C. Circuit decision upholding the 2009 finding — regulations that created reliance interests for vehicle manufacturers, state governments, and communities.

Legal analysts involved in the case have estimated the litigation will "take a few years" and will "be a relatively large, complicated piece of litigation, because there are so many players involved." The case is expected to reach the Supreme Court regardless of the D.C. Circuit outcome. The legal stakes extend beyond climate policy: if EPA can administratively rescind a scientific determination that has been upheld by two rounds of federal appellate review, the precedent for administrative override of settled regulatory science across all domains — chemical safety, air quality, workplace safety — is significant.

## Section B: NEPA Dismantlement as Elimination of Democratic Participation

### B.1 What NEPA Was Designed to Do

The National Environmental Policy Act of 1969 established the procedural architecture through which democratic communities participate in federal decisions about infrastructure, energy projects, and land use affecting their health and environment. NEPA did not give communities veto power over federal projects. It gave them three things: the right to information (through mandatory Environmental Impact Statements), the right to participate (through required public comment periods), and the right to judicial review (through statutory standing to challenge EIS adequacy in federal court).

For 55 years, NEPA's public comment process was the mechanism through which frontline communities — those living near pipelines, refineries, power plants, and industrial facilities — had a formal legal avenue to place their concerns in the administrative record of decisions affecting their health. The comment period was not merely advisory; agencies were required to consider and respond to substantive comments, and failure to do so was grounds for judicial review that could halt or revise a project. This procedural architecture created a form of democratic participation in administrative governance that was unavailable through electoral processes alone.

CEQ regulations implementing NEPA — promulgated in 1978 and refined through 2022 — established the rules governing EIS scope and adequacy, comment period length, public notice requirements, and agency response obligations. These regulations were binding on all federal agencies and formed the procedural backbone of environmental democracy for half a century.

### B.2 The January 2026 Dismantlement

On January 8, 2026, CEQ issued a final rule removing all of its NEPA-implementing regulations from the Code of Federal Regulations. The final rule was effective immediately. In a single regulatory action, fifty years of binding procedural requirements — governing how agencies must analyze environmental impacts, how they must notify affected communities, how long comment periods must last, what forms of public input must be considered, and how agencies must respond — were converted from legally enforceable rules into optional guidance.

The Harvard Environmental and Energy Law Program's regulatory tracker documented the scope of the dismantlement: "The final rule removes all iterations of CEQ's regulations implementing the National Environmental Policy Act of 1969 (NEPA) from the Code of Federal Regulations." Multiple federal agencies followed: the Department of the Interior, Department of Energy, Department of Defense, Department of Transportation, Department of Agriculture, Department of Commerce, and the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission all published interim final rules amending their own NEPA rules on July 3, 2025. In several cases, agencies withdrew their NEPA regulations entirely and announced they would use non-binding guidance going forward.

The structural consequences are operational and immediate. There is no longer a government-wide requirement that agencies prepare a draft EIS for public review. There is no longer a requirement that agencies accept, consider, or respond to public comments on proposed actions. Where draft EIS publication remains in agency guidance, completion timelines for energy projects have been shortened to 28 days — with a 10-day comment window offered concurrently with document preparation. The Bureau of Land Management offered a five-day comment period (over a weekend) on an Environmental Assessment for a large-scale lithium exploration operation on federal lands in Oregon.

### B.3 The SPEED Act and Legislative Consolidation

The Trump administration's executive dismantlement of NEPA's regulatory backbone has been complemented by legislative action designed to make the rollback irreversible through statute. The SPEED Act — which passed the House and awaits Senate action as of May 2026 — would:

- Restrict agencies from considering new scientific information submitted after a project's initial application, including peer-reviewed research submitted during the comment period itself
- Shorten the window for filing legal challenges from six years to 150 days
- Require litigants to have submitted a "unique related comment" during the comment period as a condition of standing to sue — a rule that disproportionately burdens communities without legal or technical resources to submit sufficiently specific comments on highly complex environmental documents
- Limit agencies' environmental review to immediate, local effects rather than cumulative and regional impacts, precluding consideration of downstream pollution, climate contribution, or ecosystem-wide effects

Appalachian Voices' analysis (May 8, 2026) documented the cumulative effect of these provisions: frontline communities — those in rural, economically distressed, and majority-minority areas that have historically been most reliant on NEPA's procedural protections — face the most severe restriction of their participatory rights under the SPEED Act's compressed timelines and standing limitations. Understanding project impacts, particularly cumulative health effects on communities already experiencing elevated pollution burdens, routinely requires more than 150 days of monitoring data.

### B.4 Case Studies in Democratic Participation Elimination

**Pipeline Environmental Impact Statements**: Pre-NEPA-rollback, major natural gas and oil pipeline projects required EIS processes that could last 18-36 months, with multiple rounds of public comment, agency scoping meetings, and opportunities for impacted landowners and communities to place technical concerns in the record. Under the post-January 2026 regime, energy pipeline EIS processes can proceed on 28-day timelines with no draft for public review. The practical consequence is that communities in the path of proposed pipelines no longer have a legally actionable avenue to challenge route selection, safety analysis, or environmental impact characterization during the agency review process.

**Federal Land Management**: The Bureau of Land Management manages approximately 245 million acres of public lands. Under the prior NEPA regime, major decisions about mineral extraction, drilling, and grazing on federal lands required EIS processes with mandatory public comment. Under the current non-binding guidance framework, DOI bureaus have "broad discretion over whether to publish draft environmental documents or seek public input." The BLM's five-day weekend comment period on the Oregon lithium exploration project documents this discretion in practice: a major mining operation on federal public land received less public process than a minor rezoning action in most municipalities.

**Renewable Energy Projects**: The administration's "permitting reform" framing presents the NEPA rollback as primarily a means to accelerate renewable energy deployment. The Appalachian Voices analysis, the Environmental Law Institute's review, and independent legal analysis all document that the rollback applies to fossil fuel projects at least as broadly as to renewables — and that the communities most affected by the loss of procedural protections are those most likely to face fossil fuel infrastructure, not renewable energy. The SPEED Act's restrictions on consideration of new scientific information and its shortened litigation window would limit challenges to coal plants and natural gas terminals on the same terms as challenges to wind farms.

### B.5 Public Comment as Democratic Infrastructure

The structural role of public comment in democratic governance deserves explicit treatment because the "permitting reform" framing obscures it. Public comment under NEPA is not a courtesy mechanism for citizen input that federal agencies can take or leave. It is a constitutive element of administrative legitimacy: the process through which affected communities generate the administrative record that defines what a decision-maker must consider, and what a reviewing court can examine.

When a pipeline is proposed to run through a community, the EIS public comment period is the mechanism through which that community's concerns — about groundwater impacts, air quality, land use, property values, and health effects — become part of the legal record that constrains agency decision-making. The agency must consider those comments, must respond to substantive challenges, and must justify its decisions against the evidentiary record the comment process created. Eliminating or compressing this process does not merely make permitting faster; it removes the community's legal standing to contest a federal decision that will reshape their physical environment and health conditions for decades.

This is a democratic design question, not merely an administrative efficiency question. Democratic participation in the decisions of administrative government — which exercise more direct power over daily life than most electoral outcomes — requires procedural rights that are enforceable, not discretionary. The NEPA rollback converts those rights from enforceable to discretionary, which is their elimination in practical effect.

## Section C: EPA Budget and Enforcement Capacity Destruction

### C.1 The Scale of the Budget Cuts

The Trump administration's proposed FY2026 EPA budget requests $4.16 billion — a 54 percent reduction from FY2025 levels of $9.14 billion. The proposed FY2027 budget requests $4.2 billion, a more than 50 percent cut from the $8.8 billion Congress provided in FY2026. These are not marginal adjustments; they represent the structural defunding of the regulatory agency responsible for implementing and enforcing the Clean Air Act, the Clean Water Act, the Resource Conservation and Recovery Act, the Toxic Substances Control Act, and the Superfund program.

The enforcement-specific cuts are the most consequential for regulatory effectiveness. The proposed FY2026 budget includes:

- A 30 percent reduction in civil enforcement capacity
- A 49 percent cut to criminal enforcement
- A 35 percent decrease in compliance monitoring
- Complete elimination of environmental justice enforcement funding
- Elimination of State and Local Air Quality Management Grants ($235.6 million in FY2025), which fund state and local air monitoring and enforcement capacity
- A 47 percent cut to the Superfund program's appropriated funds, with proposed elimination of congressional appropriations for the Superfund remedial program entirely

The Environmental Protection Network — an organization of EPA alumni — characterized the proposed cuts as "a wrecking ball that endangers the air we breathe and the water we drink." The Sierra Club documented mass terminations of EPA staff working on Clean Air Act and Clean Water Act enforcement, permit review, and environmental justice analysis.

### C.2 Personnel Reductions and Capacity Destruction

Personnel reductions began before budget cuts formalized the structural change. EPA dismissed over 300 probationary employees in early 2025, including scientists and enforcement officers working on Clean Air Act compliance and hazardous waste permitting. The Environmental Justice Office — created in 1992 under President George H.W. Bush — was effectively closed: 168 civil servants were placed on administrative leave and later subjected to a Reduction in Force.

EPA's research infrastructure faced separate restructuring. The Office of Research and Development, with approximately 1,500 staff, was reorganized into approximately 500 positions in a restructuring that eliminated research programs generating findings on chemical health effects, air quality modeling, and environmental justice metrics. The IRIS (Integrated Risk Information System) program — which produces the scientific assessments that underpin EPA's health-based regulatory standards — was identified for cuts in both DOGE-directed reviews and Project 2025 planning documents.

The regional office structure has been specifically targeted. EPA's ten regional offices are the primary point of contact between federal environmental enforcement and local pollution sources. Project 2025's documentation of planned regional office restructuring — and the Washington headquarters centralization of authority previously delegated to regional offices — reduces regional capacity while concentrating decision-making authority in political appointees at headquarters. The effect is that enforcement decisions previously made by career staff with regional expertise and accountability to local communities are now made by politically accountable administrators with less local knowledge and more institutional incentive to prioritize deregulatory outcomes.

### C.3 Why Capacity Destruction Is Structural, Not Incidental

The relationship between budget cuts, personnel reductions, and regulatory effectiveness is not linear. Enforcement capacity does not degrade proportionally to cuts; it degrades asymmetrically because enforcement depends on institutional knowledge, established relationships with regulated entities, and the credible threat of consequences. When experienced enforcement staff are terminated, the institutional knowledge of which facilities have chronic compliance problems, which violations are escalating, and which communities are bearing disproportionate pollution burdens is lost — often permanently.

The 49 percent cut to criminal enforcement is particularly structural. Criminal environmental prosecution has historically produced EPA's most significant deterrence effects: criminal liability for knowing violations creates accountability at the level of corporate decision-making that civil fines — which companies treat as a cost of doing business — do not reach. Cutting criminal enforcement by half does not merely reduce the number of prosecutions; it signals to regulated industries that the criminal deterrent has been substantially withdrawn, altering compliance calculus across the regulatory landscape.

The Superfund cuts are a separate crisis embedded in the broader capacity destruction. The Superfund program addresses the cleanup of contaminated sites — facilities where industrial or chemical operations have left lasting toxic legacies in surrounding communities. Superfund sites are disproportionately located in communities of color and low-income communities, where the original siting decisions reflected historical patterns of political exclusion. Cutting Superfund remedial funding by 47 percent does not merely slow cleanup; it effectively strands communities in contaminated environments with no realistic timeline for remediation.

### C.4 The Implementation Failure Cascade

A regulatory rule that cannot be enforced is not a rule; it is a formal statement with no operational consequence. The budget and enforcement capacity cuts described above do not merely reduce EPA's effectiveness at the margins — they progressively convert the remaining regulatory standards that have not been formally rescinded into unenforced obligations. The Clean Air Act's National Ambient Air Quality Standards (NAAQS) remain formally on the books. The Endangerment Finding challenge proceeds in the D.C. Circuit. But as enforcement capacity is reduced and monitoring networks are defunded, the practical effect of existing standards degrades regardless of their formal legal status.

This is the implementation failure cascade: formal regulatory rollbacks (the Endangerment Finding rescission, the NEPA removal) create the policy architecture of deregulation, while capacity destruction ensures that whatever regulatory standards survive legal challenge cannot be effectively enforced. Communities living near permitted facilities face deteriorating air quality regardless of whether the legal standard has technically changed, because the monitoring and enforcement infrastructure that makes standards real has been systematically disabled.

## Section D: International Comparative Record

### D.1 The EU Green Deal Governance Architecture

The European Union's approach to climate governance provides the sharpest institutional contrast to the US rollback. The EU Green Deal, launched in 2019 as the European Commission's flagship climate agenda, is not primarily a target-setting exercise; it is a governance architecture that embeds climate accountability at multiple institutional levels simultaneously.

The EU Governance Regulation (2018/1999, revised and extended through 2026) requires member states to submit National Energy and Climate Plans (NECPs) — comprehensive roadmaps for meeting national emissions reduction targets — to the European Commission every five years, with annual progress reports. The Commission reviews these plans for adequacy against EU-wide targets, can require revisions, and publicly reports on implementation gaps. This creates horizontal accountability between member states and the EU level that is not dependent on any single administration's political orientation.

The Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM), fully operational by 2026, extends climate governance into trade policy by imposing carbon costs on imports from countries without comparable carbon pricing. This creates an institutional consequence for regulatory rollbacks that crosses administrative transitions: US manufacturers exporting to the EU face CBAM costs in part as a consequence of the abandonment of domestic climate regulations, creating economic pressure that operates independently of domestic political decisions.

The EU's climate governance has not been immune to political contestation — the "omnibus" regulatory review of 2026 has delayed some sustainability reporting requirements and modified elements of the Green Deal legislative package. But the institutional architecture — the governance regulation, the CBAM, the legally binding 2030 and 2050 targets, the sectoral legislation — creates a system of obligations that persists across political transitions precisely because it is embedded in EU law rather than delegated to administrative discretion.

### D.2 The UK Climate Change Act Model

The United Kingdom's Climate Change Act (2008) is the foundational model for climate accountability legislation that is explicitly designed to survive political transitions. The Act does three structurally important things. First, it establishes legally binding emissions reduction targets — a 78 percent reduction in UK emissions by 2035 compared to 1990 levels, and net zero by 2050 — with statutory force that requires legislative action (not administrative decision) to change. Second, it creates a system of five-year carbon budgets that translate the long-term targets into medium-term legally binding constraints on aggregate emissions. Third, it establishes the independent Climate Change Committee (CCC) as a statutory body charged with advising on carbon budget levels, monitoring government progress against those budgets, and publicly reporting on implementation gaps.

The CCC's annual progress reports are submitted to Parliament and published publicly. They contain explicit assessments of whether government policies are sufficient to meet the carbon budgets, and where they are not, the CCC identifies the policy gaps and the actions required to close them. This creates a form of independent, expert accountability for climate commitments that is not subject to administrative capture because the CCC's mandate is statutory, its findings are publicly reported, and its institutional independence is protected by its non-departmental public body status.

The UK model has survived political transitions that have tested its durability. The Conservative government (2010-2024) pursued energy policies that the CCC frequently found inadequate, but the statutory framework — the carbon budgets, the mandatory progress reports, the independent committee — remained intact. The CCC's 2025 progress report concluded that UK policies were not on track to meet the Sixth Carbon Budget, a finding that placed legal and political pressure on the incoming Labour government to accelerate action. The accountability mechanism functioned precisely as designed: independent of political administration, publicly transparent, and legally grounded.

### D.3 Canada's Climate Accountability Framework

Canada's Canadian Net-Zero Emissions Accountability Act (2021) established a comparable framework for the Canadian context. The Act requires the Government of Canada to establish legally binding greenhouse gas emissions targets for 2030, 2035, 2040, and 2045, with emissions reduction plans for each milestone. The Act mandates progress reports on plan implementation, requires the government to respond publicly to findings, and — critically — establishes the Commissioner of the Environment and Sustainable Development as an independent oversight officer with statutory authority to examine and report on climate implementation.

The Commissioner operates within the Office of the Auditor General, providing institutional independence from the departments being reviewed. Commissioner Jerry V. DeMarco's May 2026 report concluded that the Treasury Board Secretariat's oversight of the Greening Government Strategy "had significant gaps" — a finding published publicly and formally transmitted to Parliament. This independent audit function — examining the government's own implementation of its climate commitments and publicly reporting findings — is a democratic accountability mechanism that persists regardless of which party controls the executive.

Canada's federal system also creates a constitutional backstop absent in the US context: provincial jurisdiction over natural resources means that federal climate rollbacks do not automatically override provincial climate policies. British Columbia's carbon tax, Quebec's cap-and-trade system, and Ontario's various climate programs operate under provincial authority that federal deregulation cannot unilaterally eliminate. This federal-provincial division of authority — often a source of policy coordination challenges — functions here as institutional resilience: no single administrative action can eliminate the full spectrum of climate governance.

### D.4 What the International Record Demonstrates

The international comparative record reveals a consistent pattern across jurisdictions with durable climate governance: the climate accountability mechanisms that have survived political transitions are those anchored in legislation, not administrative rule; evaluated by independent expert bodies, not political appointees; and enforced through parliamentary or judicial accountability, not executive discretion.

The US Endangerment Finding, by contrast, rested on a combination of EPA administrative rule-making and a Supreme Court decision that the administration now interprets as not requiring regulatory action. NEPA's public participation rights rested on CEQ regulations that could be removed by executive order in a single regulatory action. EPA's enforcement capacity rests on annual appropriations that can be cut by a cooperative Congress. Each of these institutional structures is vulnerable to capture by an administration with strong deregulatory preferences — which is precisely what has occurred.

The reform architecture for the United States must address this structural vulnerability: how to anchor climate governance in statutory and constitutional structures that survive administrative transitions, rather than in administrative rules that can be rescinded without congressional action.

## Reform Architecture: Restoring the Epistemic Infrastructure of Climate Governance

### R.1 Principle: Anchoring the Legal Translation of Science

The fundamental reform insight from the Endangerment Finding rescission is that the mechanism by which scientific consensus translates into regulatory authority — the legal instrument that converts "greenhouse gases endanger health" from a scientific finding into an enforceable obligation — must be anchored in statute, not administrative rule. The 2009 finding was administratively issued and administratively rescinded. Congressional codification would require legislative action (majority votes in both chambers) to rescind, creating a political barrier that an executive administration cannot overcome unilaterally.

**Endangerment Finding Statutory Anchoring**: Congress should enact legislation codifying the scientific finding that greenhouse gases endanger public health and welfare, specifically: incorporating the scientific basis of the 2009 finding by reference; establishing that this finding has the force of law independent of EPA administrative action; and specifying that the Clean Air Act's regulatory authority over greenhouse gases is not dependent on any future administrative finding. This codification would effectively overturn the legal theory underlying the February 2026 rescission by removing the finding from administrative discretion entirely.

The precedent exists: Congress has codified scientific findings in other contexts where administrative manipulation was a concern. The National Climate Assessment is statutorily required under the Global Change Research Act of 1990. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change findings are incorporated by reference in international agreements with implementing legislation. The structural challenge is political; the legal mechanism is available.

### R.2 NEPA Restoration: Statutory Public Review Requirements

The CEQ's removal of its NEPA regulations exploited the distinction between the statute itself (NEPA) and the implementing regulations (CEQ's codified rules). NEPA's statute requires an EIS for "major Federal actions significantly affecting the quality of the human environment" but delegates the specifics of EIS content, public notice, comment periods, and agency response obligations to CEQ and individual agency regulations. When CEQ removed its implementing regulations, the statutory requirement remained but the procedural architecture that made it meaningful was eliminated.

**NEPA Restoration**: Congress should enact legislation that:
- Specifies minimum public comment periods for environmental impact statements (not less than 60 days for EIS, not less than 30 days for Environmental Assessments) as a statutory requirement, not an administrative preference
- Requires publication of a draft EIS for public review as a statutory condition precedent to any final agency action for major federal projects
- Establishes mandatory agency response obligations for substantive comments, with public documentation requirements
- Codifies the six-year statute of limitations for NEPA challenges, overturning the SPEED Act's 150-day compression
- Establishes standing for communities within a defined geographic radius of major federal actions to participate in NEPA review as a statutory right, not a regulatory discretion

These provisions would convert the current non-binding guidance framework back into binding legal requirements that cannot be unilaterally removed by executive action.

### R.3 EPA Independence: Structural Protections for Climate Science and Enforcement

The capacity destruction described in Section C reflects EPA's vulnerability to politically directed defunding and personnel elimination. Two structural reforms would address this vulnerability.

**Statutory Minimum Enforcement Capacity**: Congress should establish a statutory floor for EPA enforcement staffing and enforcement expenditure — specifying minimum civil and criminal enforcement capacity as a percentage of the agency's total appropriation, with a requirement that EPA report annually on enforcement outcomes against a statutory baseline. This would make enforcement capacity cuts visible as statutory violations rather than merely budget decisions, creating both political and legal accountability.

**Scientific Integrity Protection**: The DOE Climate Working Group's FACA violations and EPA's reliance on industry-aligned scientific assessments expose the vulnerability of regulatory science to political manipulation. Congress should enact legislation establishing an independent EPA Science Advisory Board with statutory authority to review the scientific basis of major EPA regulatory actions, mandatory publication of dissenting scientific views in the administrative record, and whistleblower protections for EPA scientists who document political manipulation of scientific findings. This structural protection would not prevent political administrations from making different policy choices based on scientific findings; it would prevent the manipulation of the scientific findings themselves.

### R.4 Sectoral Climate Targets: Statutory Performance Standards

The Endangerment Finding rescission removed the mandatory legal basis for greenhouse gas regulation, but the Clean Air Act's sectoral regulatory structure remains intact as a statutory framework. Congress should enact sectoral statutory performance standards — specifying maximum annual greenhouse gas emissions for the power sector, the transportation sector, and the industrial sector — that do not depend on the Endangerment Finding as their legal predicate.

This approach follows the UK carbon budget model: statutory targets that define permissible emissions trajectories and require administrative action to implement, but do not grant administrative discretion to abandon the trajectory. A statutory power sector emissions standard does not ask EPA to find that greenhouse gases endanger health; it commands specific emissions limits as an exercise of Congress's commerce power over interstate electricity and fuel markets, analogous to fuel economy standards under the Energy Policy and Conservation Act.

### R.5 Climate Litigation Standing: Private Right of Action

Both the Endangerment Finding rescission and the NEPA dismantlement eliminate private parties' ability to hold federal agencies accountable through judicial review. The Endangerment Finding rescission removes the legal predicate for citizen suits challenging agency inaction on greenhouse gas emissions. The SPEED Act's 150-day standing window and comment-submission prerequisite eliminate most communities' practical ability to challenge NEPA violations in court.

**Private Right of Action**: Congress should enact a statutory private right of action allowing any person to bring a civil action against EPA for failure to enforce the Clean Air Act's greenhouse gas provisions, failure to comply with statutory NEPA public comment requirements, or failure to meet any statutorily established sectoral climate target. The private right of action should include:
- No bond requirement or other financial barrier to suit
- Mandatory injunctive relief authority (courts can order compliance, not merely impose fines)
- Fee-shifting in favor of prevailing plaintiffs (allowing civil society organizations to litigate without prohibitive cost exposure)
- A six-year statute of limitations

This enforcement mechanism is modeled on existing private right of action provisions in the Clean Air Act (Section 304), the Clean Water Act (Section 505), and the Endangered Species Act (Section 11). Its extension to greenhouse gas enforcement and NEPA compliance would create a distributed enforcement network — litigation by states, cities, and civil society organizations — that is less vulnerable to federal enforcement capacity cuts than EPA's own enforcement infrastructure.

## The Analytical Parallel to Domain 42: Epistemic Infrastructure Override

The domain's executive summary identified the analytical parallel to Domain 42 as "precise and load-bearing." It is worth making that parallel explicit in its structural form.

In Domain 42, the Drug Enforcement Administration captured the scheduling process designed by the Controlled Substances Act — a process in which HHS was designated the primary scientific authority and DEA the enforcement executor. The OLC opinion converted HHS from a co-equal authority to an advisory voice, allowing DEA to override public health expertise with enforcement preferences. The result: drug scheduling decisions that systematically contradicted scientific findings, because the mechanism through which science translated into policy was captured by an institution with conflicting institutional interests.

In Domain 45, the EPA's administrative apparatus has been deployed to rescind the mechanism through which the Supreme Court's 2007 mandate (*Massachusetts v. EPA*) translated scientific consensus about greenhouse gas endangerment into regulatory authority. The DOE Climate Working Group — an unlawfully assembled body of five known climate science skeptics — was assembled to provide the alternative scientific basis for the rescission. When that body was found to have violated federal law, EPA dropped it from the final rule — but the rescission proceeded anyway, now resting on a statutory reinterpretation that directly contradicts the Supreme Court's reading of the same text.

In both cases: the institutional mechanism that translates scientific consensus into regulatory authority has been captured. In Domain 42, the mechanism was captured by DEA's institutional enforcement bias. In Domain 45, the mechanism was captured by an administration's ideological commitment to fossil fuel deregulation. In both cases, the scientific evidence that justified the original regulatory authority is not disputed; it is the legal translation that has been disabled. And in both cases, the reform architecture must restore the translation mechanism — the epistemic infrastructure — not merely replace one administrative interpretation with another.

The Endangerment Finding rescission is not the largest regulatory rollback in US history, as the administration claims it to be. It is the administrative override of the legal translation of climate science into governance authority — a more precise and more consequential characterization. Undoing it requires not merely a subsequent administration reinstating the finding, but structural protections that make the finding's legal translation resistant to administrative override in the future.
