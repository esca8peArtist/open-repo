## Executive Summary

The lobbying system that shapes American democracy is not the system that is publicly visible. The publicly visible system — registered lobbyists disclosing quarterly expenditures under the Lobbying Disclosure Act, foreign agents registered under FARA, ex parte communications logged in regulatory dockets — is the outer layer of a three-layer architecture. The inner layers are the revolving door, through which regulated-industry personnel move into regulatory agencies and back out again carrying institutional knowledge, enforcement discretion, and ongoing relationships with former colleagues; the dark money pipeline, through which corporations fund 501(c)(4) organizations that spend in elections without disclosure and then lobby for the regulatory outcomes that elected officials then deliver; and the antitrust enforcement vacuum, through which the consolidation of economic power has proceeded for four decades without serious structural challenge, producing the concentrated market structures that make regulatory capture both more achievable and more valuable to the capturing party.

The 2025–2026 crisis period has exposed all three layers simultaneously. At the EPA, FOIA records released in September 2025 document that Trump-appointed officials accepted meetings with representatives from at least 50 industry associations and chemical companies in their first 90 days. In December 2025, the EPA revised its formaldehyde risk assessment — nearly doubling the previously safe exposure level — after the American Chemistry Council, the former employer of key appointees, had spent years pushing for exactly that result. In September 2025, the EPA announced plans to eliminate drinking water protections for four PFAS chemicals, exposing 73 million Americans to "forever chemical" contamination levels the prior administration had deemed unsafe. At the DOJ's Antitrust Division, the HPE/Juniper merger produced documented "pay-to-play" allegations: political appointees overruled career staff who sought to block the merger, the Deputy AAG who opposed the settlement was fired, and former Principal Deputy AAG Roger Alford publicly described the process as one that "undermined the rule of law." Simultaneously, the DOJ settled the Live Nation monopoly case with weak remedies while 33 states that proceeded to trial won a complete liability verdict on April 15, 2026 — the first full monopoly trial verdict in a generation.

The lobbying disclosure architecture has not changed to match the post-Citizens United world. The Lobbying Disclosure Act has not been comprehensively amended since 1995. FARA has not been meaningfully amended since the 1990s, leaving foreign adversaries able to avoid registration through commercial activity and domestic interest exemptions. The bipartisan Grassley-Peters FARA reform bills passed the Senate unanimously in late 2025 but have not received House floor consideration. The DISCLOSE Act — which would close dark money disclosure gaps — cannot overcome a Senate filibuster. The result is a disclosure architecture designed for 1995 attempting to regulate the 2026 corporate influence ecosystem, in which $1.9 billion in undisclosed dark money flowed through elections in 2024 and the revolving door has accelerated to the point where FDA officials fired in 2025 immediately took positions at Eli Lilly, Pfizer, Merck, Roche, Novartis, and United Therapeutics.

The reform architecture is available, bipartisan in elements, and specific: comprehensive FARA reform; mandatory post-employment cooling-off periods for senior regulators; independent antitrust enforcement with structural presumptions against dominant-firm mergers; public disclosure of all ex parte regulatory communications; and dark money disclosure through the DISCLOSE Act. None of these reforms faces constitutional barriers — Citizens United addressed independent expenditures, not disclosure requirements; the Supreme Court has upheld disclosure mandates even when striking spending limits. The barrier is exclusively political: the entities that benefit from capture have the resources to prevent reform that would reduce the returns on their investment in the current architecture.

## 1. The Three-Layer Architecture of Corporate Regulatory Capture

### 1.1 Layer One: Formal Lobbying — What Is Visible

Lobbying is constitutionally protected. The First Amendment right to petition the government has been interpreted to protect professional advocates hired by corporations, trade associations, and other interest groups to communicate with legislators and regulators. The Lobbying Disclosure Act (1995, amended 2007) requires individuals who spend more than 20% of their time lobbying on behalf of a client to register and disclose quarterly expenditures and general issue areas. In 2024, federally registered lobbyists spent approximately $4.5 billion — a record for the third consecutive year.

The visible lobbying system has a structural distortion built into its design. Because the Lobbying Disclosure Act exempts "strategic consulting," "public relations," and "government affairs" that do not meet the 20% time threshold, a significant proportion of corporate influence work is legally invisible. The Government Accountability Office has documented that the majority of people who identify as "government affairs professionals" in the pharmaceutical, financial, and technology sectors do not meet the LDA registration threshold and therefore appear in no public disclosure. The American League of Lobbyists — now the Association of Government Relations Professionals — estimates that fewer than 20% of people who conduct influence activities at the federal level are registered under the LDA.

The sector concentration of visible lobbying also reveals the capture architecture. The pharmaceutical and health products industry has led all sectors in lobbying expenditure for 23 consecutive years. In 2024, the industry spent $385 million in disclosed federal lobbying — and an undetermined additional amount in undisclosed strategic consulting, dark money electoral spending, and revolving door personnel investment. The financial sector, technology, energy, and defense industries each spent in excess of $150 million in formally disclosed lobbying while maintaining parallel influence channels through dark money and revolving door mechanisms.

### 1.2 Layer Two: The Revolving Door — The Institutional-Knowledge Mechanism

The revolving door is the personnel mechanism through which regulatory capture is institutionalized across administrations. Its democratic harm is not primarily corruption in the narrow legal sense — quid pro quo bribery — but the systematic transfer of institutional knowledge, enforcement discretion, and regulatory relationships from the public sector to the private sector, and back again.

The FDA revolving door is the most extensively documented. A 2025 Stanford Law Review analysis found that FDA officials who move to pharmaceutical companies carry with them detailed knowledge of agency enforcement priorities, the specific scientists and review timelines for pending applications, and longstanding collegial relationships with current agency staff. The analysis documents that companies hiring former FDA officials receive faster application approvals and experience fewer enforcement actions — a pattern consistent with both the "insider knowledge" hypothesis (former officials advise companies on how to navigate agency processes more effectively) and the "regulatory capture" hypothesis (former officials maintain relationships that influence current staff decisions).

The 2025 crisis provided a documented case study. Senior FDA officials fired by HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. in summer 2025 moved immediately to major pharmaceutical companies including Eli Lilly, Pfizer, Merck, Roche, Novartis, and United Therapeutics. These officials carried with them knowledge of pending drug applications, enforcement investigations, and internal agency deliberations. The current post-employment rule — 18C.F.R. § 207 — prohibits former officials from "appearing" before federal employees on specific matters they personally handled. It does not prohibit behind-the-scenes advice, ghostwritten filings, state-level advocacy, or work on different matters at the same agency. A STAT News analysis documented specific instances where the current rule's exemptions enable continued influence over matters directly related to the former official's agency work.

At the EPA, the Trump administration's 2025 appointments produced a documented capture dynamic through the revolving door in the reverse direction: industry personnel moved into EPA. FOIA records obtained by Senator Jeff Merkley and released in September 2025 documented that chemical company executives appointed to senior EPA positions accepted meetings with their former employers and industry associations within the first 90 days of taking office, and that ethics waivers permitting these meetings were granted more broadly than under prior administrations. The specific regulatory outcomes that followed — the formaldehyde risk assessment revision, the PFAS drinking water rollback, the dicamba re-registration — correspond precisely to the specific regulatory positions that the American Chemistry Council and allied groups had sought for years and that the new appointees' former employers had funded.

Schiff and Merkley Demand Extension of Ethics Guardrails on Trump EPA Chemical Appointees — Merkley Senate](https://www.merkley.senate.gov/sens-schiff-and-merkley-demand-extension-of-ethics-guardrails-on-trump-epa-chemical-appointees-end-to-conflicts-of-interest/); [The Revolving Door between Regulatory Agencies and Industry — EPA HERO](https://hero.epa.gov/reference/7371243/); [EPA PFAS Rollback — NRDC](https://www.nrdc.org/press-releases/epa-seeks-roll-back-pfas-drinking-water-rules-keeping-millions-exposed-toxic-forever)

### 1.3 Layer Three: The Dark Money Electoral Pipeline — Citizens United as Upstream Enabler

The lobbying and revolving door mechanisms are post-electoral: they shape what agencies and legislators do after elections are decided. The dark money pipeline is pre-electoral: it shapes which officials take office, and it simultaneously funds the campaigns that elect them and the lobbying efforts that instruct them. Domain 51 documents the Citizens United architecture in full; this section summarizes the capture-specific dimension.

The structural relationship is: corporations donate to 501(c)(4) nonprofits (no donor disclosure required), which spend in elections and also fund lobbying activities; elected officials shaped by this spending then make appointments to regulatory agencies; those agencies' regulatory agendas reflect the positions of the industries that funded the elections and the revolving door personnel who staff the agencies. This is not a conspiracy — it is a lawful architecture operating as designed after Citizens United removed the last constraint on the upstream input (unlimited corporate treasury expenditures).

In the pharmaceutical domain (Domain 42), the structure is fully documented: Pharmaceutical Research and Manufacturers of America (PhRMA) spent $26 million in disclosed lobbying in 2024 while funding multiple 501(c)(4) organizations that spent undisclosed amounts in Senate races in New Hampshire, Arizona, Nevada, and Pennsylvania targeting senators who supported drug price negotiation. The Biden-era Inflation Reduction Act's drug pricing provisions survived because those Senate races went Democratic; the OBBBA reversed them because the 2024 Republican Senate majority included senators whose campaigns had been supported by PhRMA's dark money network. The policy outcome is the direct downstream result of the electoral investment — with the additional intermediary step of an FDA leadership whose revolving door history reflects decades of industry-agency personnel exchange.

## 2. The Foreign Lobbying Loophole: FARA in the Post-Citizens United Era

### 2.1 The Architecture of Disclosure Evasion

The Foreign Agents Registration Act (1938) requires agents acting at the direction or under the control of foreign principals to register with the DOJ, disclose their activities, and label political propaganda distributed on behalf of foreign governments. FARA was designed to make foreign government influence on domestic politics transparent to American voters and legislators — a disclosure requirement with clear democratic foundations. Since its 1990s amendments, FARA has had three loopholes that have grown from procedural exceptions into structural evasions.

The commercial activity exemption allows agents whose work primarily serves commercial rather than political interests to avoid FARA registration. The domestic interest exemption allows agents who represent foreign clients on matters affecting American business interests to avoid FARA registration. The Lobbying Disclosure Act exemption allows agents who register under the LDA — which has less stringent disclosure requirements — to avoid FARA registration entirely, even when their clients are foreign governments or government-controlled entities.

The result, as the Center for American Progress documented: agents representing Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and other authoritarian governments have consistently avoided FARA registration by characterizing their activities as commercial consulting, registering under the lighter-touch LDA, or arguing that their clients' interests coincide with domestic American business interests. A 2026 investigation by The Ethics Reporter documented that AIPAC — the American Israel Public Affairs Committee — has never registered under FARA despite operating as an advocacy organization for Israeli government policy positions, through the domestic interest exemption.

### 2.2 The Bipartisan Reform Window

The Senate passed two bipartisan FARA reform bills in late 2025: the Disclosing Foreign Influence in Lobbying Act (S. 856) and the Lobbying Disclosure Improvement Act (S. 865), sponsored by Senators Grassley (R-IA) and Peters (D-MI). The bills passed unanimously, reflecting genuine bipartisan concern — from both the right (foreign adversary influence, particularly China) and the left (transparency and democratic accountability) — about the FARA loophole architecture. Grassley and Peters subsequently introduced the Retroactive Foreign Agents Registration Act to close a judicial misreading that had allowed individuals to argue that past unregistered foreign agent work cannot be required to be disclosed.

Neither bill has received a House floor vote or markup as of May 2026. Speaker Johnson has not scheduled floor time despite the Senate's unanimous passage.

The strategic significance: FARA reform is one of the few lobbying transparency measures with genuine bipartisan coalition potential, because the national security framing (foreign adversaries exploiting FARA loopholes) activates conservative support that the more general corporate-lobbying-transparency framing does not. FARA reform also does not implicate Citizens United — it regulates foreign-government agents, not domestic corporations — making it constitutionally unambiguous and legally straightforward.

## 3. Antitrust as Democratic Design: The Enforcement Collapse and Its Political Consequences

### 3.1 The Chicago School Transformation and the Democracy-Antitrust Nexus

Antitrust law was not enacted to protect consumers from high prices. The Sherman Act (1890), the Clayton Act (1914), and the Federal Trade Commission Act (1914) were explicitly framed as responses to the political danger of concentrated economic power: the threat that corporations wielding monopolistic power would use it to "coerce, dominate, or cheat" — in the words of Senator Sherman's floor statements — not just economically but politically. The original regulatory vision was that breaking up large concentrations of economic power was a precondition for democratic self-governance, because concentrated economic power translates directly into concentrated political power.

The Chicago School antitrust revolution of the 1970s and 1980s — centered on Judge Robert Bork's "consumer welfare standard" — reframed the entire enforcement enterprise as a question of allocative efficiency. If a merger or dominant-firm practice produced lower consumer prices or technological innovation, it was presumptively lawful regardless of its effects on market structure, political power, or competitive dynamics. The political-economy consequences of this doctrinal shift have been four decades of accelerating market concentration: in search (Google's 90% monopoly), live events (Live Nation's 78% amphitheater control), social media (Meta's Facebook-Instagram-WhatsApp integration), and dozens of industrial sectors where two to four companies control more than 70% of the market.

The Roosevelt Institute's 2024 analysis documents the direct pathway from economic concentration to political capture: when two firms control 70% of a market, those two firms' lobbying and campaign finance spending determines the regulatory agenda for that market. The OECD's 2024 competition report notes that antitrust laws were "originally enacted to prevent corporations wielding monopolistic power from using it to 'coerce, dominate, or cheat'" — recognizing that the democratic and economic functions of antitrust are not separable.

**Sources**: [Designing the Next Wave of Competition Policy — Roosevelt Institute](https://rooseveltinstitute.org/blog/next-wave-of-competition-policy/); [Big Tech Remains Top Priority for DOJ and FTC in US Antitrust Litigation — Global Competition Review](https://globalcompetitionreview.com/review/the-antitrust-review-of-the-americas/2026/article/big-tech-remains-top-priority-doj-and-ftc-in-us-antitrust-litigation); [The Trends That Will Define US Antitrust in 2026 — ProMarket](https://www.promarket.org/2026/01/15/the-trends-that-will-define-us-antitrust-in-2026/)

### 3.2 The 2026 Antitrust Landscape: Active Cases and Political Interference

Three landmark antitrust proceedings active in 2026 together define the enforcement crisis — and each illustrates a different dimension of capture:

**Google (United States v. Google LLC — Search Monopoly)**: Judge Amit Mehta found in August 2024 that Google held a monopoly in general search and search advertising. His September 2025 remedies ruling imposed behavioral restrictions — prohibition on exclusive distribution contracts, data-sharing mandates for rivals, syndication access requirements — but rejected the DOJ's initial request for Chrome divestiture. The DOJ has cross-appealed, seeking structural breakup consideration. Both Google and the DOJ have now appealed different portions of the ruling; the D.C. Circuit is expected to hear arguments in late 2026 or early 2027. The Google case is significant for democratic design because Google's search monopoly is simultaneously a monopoly on information infrastructure — the primary channel through which 90% of American internet users encounter news, political information, and civic content. Search result rankings shape political perception at a scale that no prior media monopoly achieved.

**Live Nation/Ticketmaster**: On April 15, 2026, a federal jury found Live Nation and Ticketmaster liable on all antitrust counts submitted by 33 state attorneys general, concluding that the company had illegally monopolized the live events industry. The verdict was a complete state victory, finding Live Nation liable on every one of 13 specific antitrust liability issues. The DOJ, which had filed a separate case, reached a secret settlement during trial — a move that surprised the judge and that the states described as inadequate. The contrast between the DOJ settlement and the states' complete jury victory is a case study in the political-interference dimension of capture: the DOJ Antitrust Division, whose leadership had been installed by a Republican administration receiving substantial entertainment-industry support, settled while state AGs with no comparable political constraints obtained a full verdict.

**HPE/Juniper Networks**: The most explicit "pay-to-play" documented case of 2025-2026. The DOJ Antitrust Division sued to block HPE's $14 billion acquisition of Juniper Networks on January 30, 2025, finding that the merger would leave 70% of the relevant market in two companies' hands. On June 28, 2025, less than two weeks before trial, DOJ political appointees reversed career staff and approved a consent decree. Former Principal Deputy AAG Roger Alford publicly described the process as a "pay-to-play" approach that "went beyond legitimate lobbying functions and undermined the rule of law." Deputy AAGs who opposed the settlement were fired. A coalition of 13 state attorneys general filed to intervene in the Tunney Act review, with California AG Bonta obtaining a court order allowing states to participate. The case documents in real time the pathway by which corporate lobbying of political appointees translates into reversed enforcement decisions over career staff objection.

**FTC v. Amazon**: Trial is scheduled for late 2026. The FTC under Lina Khan filed in 2023 charging Amazon with illegally maintaining a monopoly over online superstores and marketplace services. Under Andrew Ferguson's leadership, the FTC has maintained the case while adopting a more traditionally Chicago-school analytical framework and publicly resisting characterizations of antitrust as a tool for addressing media-marketplace power. Ferguson's stated position — that antitrust law should focus on clear violations on a case-by-case basis using traditional economic theories of harm rather than structural presumptions — is exactly the posture that enabled four decades of market concentration to proceed without antitrust challenge.

### 3.3 The FTC's Constitutional Crisis: Jarkesy and In-House Adjudication

The Fifth Circuit's March 2026 ruling in the Intuit/TurboTax case — holding that the FTC's in-house adjudication of a deceptive advertising claim was unconstitutional under *SEC v. Jarkesy* (2024) — represents the most significant threat to the FTC's enforcement capacity since its founding. *Jarkesy* held that defendants in SEC enforcement proceedings have a Seventh Amendment right to a jury trial rather than administrative adjudication; the Fifth Circuit's extension of *Jarkesy* to the FTC in the Intuit case suggests that the agency's entire in-house adjudication apparatus — used for decades to resolve unfair trade practices and antitrust matters — may be constitutionally vulnerable.

The practical consequence is that the FTC would need to file all enforcement cases in federal court with jury trials, dramatically increasing the cost and time of each proceeding and reducing the effective caseload the agency can maintain. A commission already under pressure from political appointments that shifted its enforcement priorities faces an additional structural constraint that the corporate bar has been building through *Jarkesy* litigation for years. The federal court invalidation of Biden-era Hart-Scott-Rodino premerger notification reforms (February 12, 2026) represents the parallel judicial track: courts have been systematically reducing the procedural tools available to antitrust enforcers while Congress has taken no compensating legislative action.

## 4. Sector-Specific Capture in 2025-2026: EPA, FDA, FCC

### 4.1 EPA: From Science-Based Regulation to Industry-Written Outcomes

The Environmental Protection Agency under Administrator Lee Zeldin has produced the most documented regulatory capture pattern in the first two years of Trump's second administration. The evidence comes from the agency's own FOIA-released documents and public regulatory record.

The formaldehyde case: The EPA's December 2025 risk assessment revision for formaldehyde nearly doubled the previously established safe exposure level. This revision came after the American Chemistry Council — an industry trade group whose former employees populate the agency's senior ranks — had advocated for exactly this result for years. The revision contradicts the International Agency for Research on Cancer's finding that formaldehyde is a known human carcinogen and the EPA's own prior 2010 draft assessment, which the agency had suppressed for 15 years under industry pressure. Senators Schiff and Merkley requested documents under FOIA and found that the specific officials who supervised the revision had direct pre-agency employment relationships with the industry that benefited.

The PFAS rollback: In 2025-2026, the EPA reversed the Biden administration's maximum contaminant level rules for four PFAS chemicals — GenX, PFBS, PFNA, and PFHxS — while retaining standards for PFOA and PFOS at 4 parts per trillion. The Environmental Working Group calculated in March 2026 that 176 million Americans are exposed to PFAS contamination in drinking water, and that 73 million are served by water systems with detected levels above the standards the EPA now seeks to rescind. The rollback directly corresponds to years of lobbying by the American Chemistry Council, DuPont, 3M, and their industry associations — entities whose former executives hold senior EPA positions.

The dicamba re-registration: On February 6, 2026, the EPA Office of Pesticide Programs re-registered dicamba despite active legal challenges, documented risks to neighboring crops, and studies linking dicamba exposure to liver and bile duct cancer and leukemia. Dicamba is produced by Bayer-Monsanto and BASF; their industry coalition had lobbied specifically for this outcome.

### 4.2 CFPB Capture: The Medical Debt Rule as Democratic Case Study

The CFPB medical debt rule trajectory illustrates regulatory capture operating through litigation rather than the revolving door. The Biden CFPB finalized a rule in January 2025 that would have removed medical debt from the credit reports of approximately 15 million Americans carrying roughly $49 billion in medical bills — a rule with documented benefits for borrowers' creditworthiness and demonstrated health-outcome improvements from debt relief. On July 11, 2025, the U.S. District Court of the Eastern District of Texas vacated the rule after the Trump administration's CFPB leadership joined the plaintiff industry groups in requesting vacatur, arguing the rule exceeded the agency's statutory authority.

The same Eastern District of Texas that has become the preferred venue for corporate challenges to Biden-era financial regulations — through strategic venue selection by plaintiffs' law firms — vacated the rule after the agency's own leadership abandoned its defense. This is not judicial capture in the traditional sense; it is agency leadership capture (through presidential appointment) operationalized through the courts. The outcome is concrete: 15 million Americans who would have had medical debt removed from their credit reports will instead carry that debt burden on records that affect their housing, employment, and financial stability — the precise conditions the research identifies as reducing civic participation.

## 5. The Democratic Design Crisis: Why Regulatory Capture Threatens Democracy Structurally

### 5.1 The Feedback Loop

Regulatory capture is not merely a governance failure — it is a democratic feedback loop that is structurally self-reinforcing. The mechanism: corporations invest in elections (via dark money, as documented in Domain 51) to elect officials who make regulatory appointments; those appointments produce regulatory outcomes that benefit the investing corporations; those benefits increase corporate profits; those profits fund additional investment in subsequent elections. The loop is self-compounding because each regulatory decision that benefits a concentrated industry increases that industry's capacity to fund subsequent electoral investment, while simultaneously reducing the regulatory costs that would have diminished profits available for political spending.

The democratic design crisis is that this feedback loop operates independently of voter preferences at every step. Voters cannot see dark money expenditures. Voters cannot easily identify which regulatory decisions result from capture versus genuine policy judgment. Voters cannot easily trace the revolving door connections between specific agency decisions and specific industry employers of key officials. The entire architecture of capture is designed to be opaque to democratic accountability.

### 5.2 State AGs as the Accountability Structure

The most important structural development of 2025-2026 is that state attorneys general have emerged as the primary enforcement mechanism compensating for federal enforcement capture. In the Live Nation case, 33 state AGs proceeded to trial and won a complete verdict after the DOJ settled. In the HPE/Juniper case, 13 state AGs challenged the consent decree through Tunney Act review. In the PFAS context, multiple state AGs are litigating EPA rollbacks. In the antitrust domain more broadly, state enforcement — funded by state budgets, insulated from federal executive control, and legally empowered to pursue independent antitrust claims — has become the primary check on federal enforcement withdrawal.

This development has limits: state AGs are elected, face their own capture dynamics from state-level industries, and have jurisdiction only within or affecting their states. But it represents an important democratic design redundancy — a parallel enforcement structure that activates when the federal structure is captured.

## 6. Reform Architecture

### 6.1 Revolving Door Reform

The mandatory post-employment cooling-off period for senior federal officials should be extended from 1 year (current general standard) to 5 years for communications with the agency the official led, with a 2-year ban on any lobbying or regulatory advocacy in the sector the official regulated. The existing 18 C.F.R. § 207 exemptions for "behind-the-scenes advice" should be eliminated; the goal of the rule is to prevent the use of inside knowledge and relationships, not merely to restrict formal appearances. Former officials who violate the cooling-off period should face criminal sanctions, not merely civil penalties. Funding for post-employment enforcement at the OGE should be tripled.

### 6.2 Lobbying Transparency

The Lobbying Disclosure Act should be updated to require registration by all individuals who spend any time on government affairs activities on behalf of entities with more than $1 million in annual revenue, eliminating the 20% time-threshold loophole. All "strategic consulting" activities that involve communication with government officials should be disclosed. The DISCLOSE Act should be attached to any must-pass legislation, requiring disclosure of all contributions over $10,000 to political committees and organizations that spend in elections.

### 6.3 FARA Reform

The Grassley-Peters reform package that passed the Senate unanimously should receive a House vote. The FARA exemptions for commercial activity, domestic interest, and LDA registration should be narrowed or eliminated for agents of foreign governments with documented records of democratic backsliding or adversarial posture toward the United States. The Retroactive Foreign Agents Registration Act should also be enacted.

### 6.4 Antitrust Structural Reform

Congress should restore structural antitrust analysis by enacting a rebuttable presumption against mergers that would produce market concentration above 40% in any defined market — a return to the pre-Chicago-School structural presumption from United States v. Philadelphia National Bank (1963). The DOJ and FTC Merger Guidelines should be updated by statute, not merely by agency guidance, to prevent reversal through political appointments. Independent antitrust enforcement structures — following the German Bundeskartellamt model with fixed-term leadership protected against removal without cause — should be created to insulate enforcement from direct political appointment pressure.

This domain is the mechanism layer for the framework's sector-specific captures. The antitrust dimension connects to Domain 29 (DOJ capture): when the DOJ's political leadership overrides career antitrust staff on merger decisions (as in HPE/Juniper), the same mechanism of political appointment capture that Domain 29 documents for prosecutorial decisions operates identically for antitrust enforcement decisions. The DOJ is the common institutional vector through which both prosecutorial and antitrust capture operate; the democratic design response — career staff protection, antitrust independence, prosecutorial independence — is the same in both domains.
