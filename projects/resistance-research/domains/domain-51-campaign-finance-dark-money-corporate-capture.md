## Executive Summary

Citizens United v. Federal Election Commission (2010) is the most cited Supreme Court case in discussions of money in politics. What is less understood is that Citizens United was not a single decision creating a single problem — it was the removal of the last structural barrier preventing corporations and wealthy individuals from deploying unlimited funds through opaque nonprofit vehicles to directly shape the outcome of elections, legislative agendas, and regulatory proceedings. The framework documented across Domains 1–50 shows how different industries have captured different regulatory and legislative targets: cryptocurrency companies captured financial regulation (Domain 38), pharmaceutical companies captured drug scheduling (Domain 42), fossil fuel companies shape environmental enforcement (Domain 15), the anti-abortion movement funds ballot initiative suppression (Domain 33 and 44). What connects these captures is not ideology — it is architecture. The Citizens United decision, and the regulatory vacuum it created, is the structural mechanism that makes every sector-specific capture possible at this scale.

The 2024 election was the most financially opaque in American history. Dark money — election spending where donors are not publicly disclosed — hit a record $1.9 billion in federal races alone, a dramatic jump from the prior record of $1 billion in 2020. Shell companies and 501(c)(4) nonprofits that did not disclose their funding sources gave $1.3 billion to super PACs — more than in the prior two election cycles combined. Before Citizens United, billionaires accounted for 0.3% of federal election spending; by 2024, 300 billionaires and their families contributed more than $3 billion, representing 19% of all federal election donations.

The 2026 crisis has two distinct acute dimensions. First, the Federal Election Commission — the agency that enforces campaign finance law — has lost its enforcement quorum. Trump fired FEC Chair Ellen Weintraub, a Democratic appointee, in February 2026; combined with a Republican commissioner's earlier resignation, the commission cannot enforce existing campaign finance laws, audit spending, or impose penalties for violations — a situation the Brennan Center described as the FEC losing its enforcement powers in the runup to the most consequential midterm election in a generation. Second, the legislative window to close this gap is narrow and closing: the DISCLOSE Act of 2026, introduced with all 47 Democratic senators and 139 House Democrats as co-sponsors, has no path to the 60 Senate votes needed to overcome filibuster — and no Republican senator has yet publicly committed to support it.

The state-level response provides the reform's most immediate advocacy window. Four ballot measures targeting dark money disclosure and campaign finance reform are on November 2026 state ballots. California's Fair Elections Act of 2026 would repeal the state's 1988 ban on public funding of campaigns. Three additional states are considering expansions of public campaign financing programs. These state-level battlegrounds are where the framework's analysis is most immediately actionable — and where the coalition of organizations that distributes this domain has the most direct advocacy capacity.

## 1. The Constitutional Architecture: How Citizens United Created the Dark Money System

### 1.1 The Pre-Citizens United Framework

Before 2010, the campaign finance system had three main structural elements that are critical to understanding what Citizens United destroyed.

The first element was the corporation-expenditure prohibition. Section 203 of the Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act of 2002 (BCRA, also known as McCain-Feingold) prohibited corporations and labor unions from using general treasury funds to pay for "electioneering communications" — broadcast ads that referred to a federal candidate within 30 days of a primary or 60 days of a general election. This prohibition built on the Federal Corrupt Practices Act of 1907, which had originally banned direct corporate contributions to federal candidates. The combined framework meant that corporations could not spend unlimited sums from their general treasuries to influence federal elections.

The second element was a hard distinction between political committees (which disclosed donors) and general advocacy (which did not require disclosure). The FEC's "express advocacy" doctrine drew a line: communications that explicitly urged voters to vote for or against a candidate required disclosure; communications that discussed political issues without explicit electoral advocacy did not. This line was porous, but it maintained a basic transparency structure.

The third element was the public financing system for presidential elections, established in 1974 after Watergate. Candidates who accepted public funding during the primary and general election accepted spending limits in exchange. By 2008, both presidential nominees — Barack Obama and John McCain — initially operated within this framework, though Obama later declined public funding in the general election, previewing the system's collapse.

### 1.2 Citizens United: What the Decision Actually Did

Citizens United v. FEC (2010) is best understood not as one decision but as three.

The narrowest holding: the government cannot prohibit corporations from spending unlimited amounts from their general treasuries on independent expenditures — political communications that are not coordinated with a candidate's campaign. Corporations have First Amendment rights in the political speech domain, and limits on their independent spending violate those rights.

The accompanying doctrinal shift: Citizens United abandoned the long-standing distinction between corporate and individual speech rights in the political domain. The Court held, 5-4, that the First Amendment's protections do not distinguish between speakers based on corporate status. This had enormous implications: not just business corporations, but nonprofit advocacy organizations, unions, trade associations, and shell companies formed for political purposes all received the same unlimited independent expenditure rights.

The simultaneous procedural consequences: the FEC and appellate courts moved rapidly to extend Citizens United's logic. SpeechNow.org v. FEC (D.C. Circuit, 2010) held that contribution limits to independent-expenditure-only groups (what became known as super PACs) were also unconstitutional. The combination of Citizens United (unlimited corporate spending) and SpeechNow.org (unlimited contributions to super PACs) created the modern dark money system: corporations give unlimited sums to 501(c)(4) nonprofit organizations, which do not disclose their donors; those 501(c)(4) organizations give to super PACs; and super PACs spend in elections while disclosing only that their funds came from the opaque nonprofit. The shell within a shell within a shell collapses donor identity entirely.

The Brennan Center's analysis of the 14-year post-Citizens United era is definitive: dark money groups have poured over $2.6 billion into federal elections since 2010, and the 2024 cycle's $1.9 billion in dark money alone dwarfs the entire Citizens United era's spending before 2020.

### 1.3 The 501(c)(4) Loophole and Shell Company Architecture

The 501(c)(4) provision of the tax code — which allows social welfare organizations to avoid paying taxes and, crucially, to avoid disclosing their donors — was not designed as a campaign finance vehicle. It was designed for civic associations, neighborhood improvement leagues, and volunteer fire departments. After Citizens United, it became the primary vehicle for unlimited anonymous political spending.

The operational architecture is straightforward. A wealthy donor or corporation wants to spend $50 million influencing a Senate race without disclosure. They donate $50 million to a 501(c)(4) "social welfare" nonprofit, which lists its "primary purpose" as issue advocacy rather than electoral activity (the IRS standard is that the 501(c)(4) must not have political activity as its "primary" purpose — a standard so vague it has been consistently exploited). The 501(c)(4) in turn donates the $50 million to a super PAC, which discloses the donation from "XYZ Social Welfare Fund" — a name that reveals nothing about the original $50 million donor. The super PAC then spends $50 million on ads supporting or opposing the Senate candidate, disclosing only the opaque shell as the funding source.

This architecture is not a bug — it is an engineered consequence of the combined effect of Citizens United (which removed spending limits) and existing nonprofit law (which does not require donor disclosure for 501(c)(4)s). The architecture works because each component is nominally legal: the 501(c)(4) follows IRS rules, the super PAC follows FEC rules, and the spending follows Citizens United's First Amendment framework. The problem is systemic, not rule-violating.

## 2. The 2026 Crisis: FEC Capture, Record Spending, and Enforcement Collapse

### 2.1 The FEC Enforcement Collapse

The Federal Election Commission was designed as a bipartisan institution specifically to prevent any party from weaponizing it: it has six commissioners, no more than three from any party, and requires four votes for most actions — which means any meaningful enforcement requires at least some bipartisan agreement. This design has produced chronic dysfunction, but it has at least prevented the FEC from being captured for partisan enforcement purposes.

The Trump administration's 2025-2026 actions destroyed even that protection. First, Trump fired FEC Chair Ellen Weintraub — a Democratic appointee — in February 2026, claiming the authority under his interpretation of the Supreme Court's Seila Law and Collins rulings on independent agency removal. This firing was immediately challenged in federal court as violating the FEC's independent agency protections, but the court declined to issue an emergency stay. Second, a Republican commissioner had already resigned in late 2025. The combination left the FEC with only three members — below the four required for a quorum to conduct enforcement activities.

The practical consequence: the FEC cannot make new campaign finance rules, vote on the outcomes of investigations, conduct audits, issue advisory opinions, or impose fines. This collapse is happening in the runup to the 2026 midterms, which will be the most expensive in history. As the Brennan Center documented, "as of Thursday, the FEC cannot enforce campaign finance laws."

Trump did nominate two Republicans to fill vacancies in February 2026 — Ashley Stow and Andrew Woodson — which, if confirmed, would restore a 3-2 Republican majority quorum. That majority would be capable of enforcement actions in Republican-favorable directions while continuing to deadlock on dark money disclosure requirements that the Republican commissioners have historically opposed.

### 2.2 Record Spending and Billionaire Concentration

The 2024 election produced the most concentrated and least transparent political spending in American history.

Total outside spending reached $4.5 billion in federal races, with more than half coming from groups that do not fully disclose their funding sources. Approximately 320 groups that do not disclose their donors collectively spent more than $281 million on online ads without any FEC reporting obligation. Dark money — the specifically undisclosed subset — hit a record $1.9 billion, double the prior record from 2020. Shell companies and 501(c)(4) nonprofits that did not disclose their donors gave $1.3 billion to super PACs — more than in the prior two election cycles combined.

The billionaire concentration figure is perhaps the most stark measure of Citizens United's democratic consequence: before the 2010 decision, billionaires accounted for 0.3% of federal election spending. In 2024, 300 billionaires and their families contributed more than $3 billion — 19% of all federal election donations. This is not simply wealth inequality expressing itself in political spending; it is a constitutional transformation of who has the practical ability to determine election outcomes. The one-person-one-vote principle of Baker v. Carr is formally intact; the practical democratic architecture has been restructured around one-dollar-one-vote.

The cryptocurrency industry's political spending illustrates the sector-specific version of this concentration. Pro-crypto super PAC Fairshake spent $119 million in the 2024 election cycle — nearly one-third of all corporate political spending — targeting specific legislators who took critical positions on crypto regulation. The legislative payoff was the GENIUS Act and CLARITY Act (see Domain 38 for the financial regulatory consequences). What the crypto PAC spending illustrates is that in the Citizens United environment, regulatory capture no longer requires the indirect path through K Street lobbying; a well-resourced industry can directly fund the removal and replacement of legislators who resist capture.

### 2.3 Dark Money as the Connective Tissue of Democratic Erosion

The framework's claim in this domain is broader than any specific spending figure: dark money is the connective tissue that links every sector-specific democratic erosion documented across the 50-domain framework. This is the meta-analysis claim that no other domain makes.

Consider the pattern documented across the framework's existing domains:

In Domain 33 (State Legislative Autocratization), the same network of ALEC-affiliated dark money vehicles that funds state legislative gerrymandering and preemption campaigns funds the ballot initiative suppression architecture that makes it harder for citizens to bypass captured legislatures. The Arizona Proposition 211 transparency challenge documented in that domain traced dark money flows through shell 501(c)(4)s to the same donor network that funds anti-union legislation in the labor domain.

In Domain 44 (Reproductive Freedom), the anti-abortion funding network — primarily channeled through Americans United for Life and National Right to Life's political arm — deployed dark money to fund opposition research and disinformation campaigns against abortion restoration ballot measures in Ohio, Kentucky, and Virginia in 2023-2024, following the same playbook used to defeat the 2018 Colorado Amendment A.

In Domain 38 (Financial Sector Independence), the crypto PAC spending of $119 million in 2024 successfully replaced key members of the House Financial Services Committee, neutralizing oversight of the crypto sector. The same architecture — industry identifies legislative obstacles, funds primary challengers through super PACs backed by opaque 501(c)(4) vehicles, removes the obstacle, installs industry-aligned replacement — is available to any sufficiently capitalized industry.

In Domain 42 (Drug Policy), the pharmaceutical and alcohol industries have consistently used 501(c)(4) vehicles to fund opposition to cannabis legalization measures even when the opposition cannot be traced to any public position by the funding companies.

The common thread across all these sector-specific captures: the Citizens United architecture allows economic power to be converted into political power through untraceable channels, at unlimited scale, without the accountability mechanisms that formal lobbying at least nominally provides.

## 3. Historical Precedent and Comparative Evidence

### 3.1 The Pre-1976 Era and the Watergate Reform Moment

Campaign finance reform in the United States has a cyclical history: a scandal reveals the extent of corrupting money in politics, reform legislation passes, courts whittle it down, a new scandal reveals a new set of gaps, reform passes again. The Citizens United era represents a break from this cycle in that no reform legislation has successfully closed the gaps opened by the 2010 decision.

The 1972 election — in which the Nixon campaign's Committee to Re-Elect the President raised $60 million (equivalent to approximately $440 million in 2024 dollars) through largely unregulated channels, including from milk cooperative slush funds, ITT Corporation quid pro quo contributions, and the "plumbers" unit funded by campaign donations — produced the 1974 Federal Election Campaign Act Amendments, which created the FEC, established contribution limits, created public financing of presidential campaigns, and required disclosure of all political contributions above $100. Buckley v. Valeo (1976) immediately struck down the spending limits on individual campaigns and independent expenditures as First Amendment violations but upheld contribution limits and disclosure requirements as serving the anti-corruption interest.

The post-Buckley system that survived for three decades — contribution limits, disclosure, and public financing for presidential races — was structurally stable because the Supreme Court upheld the government's anti-corruption interest as a compelling rationale for these constraints. Citizens United's doctrinal innovation was narrowing what counts as "corruption" to only explicit quid pro quos, eliminating the broader concept of undue influence that Congress had relied on to justify contribution limits on independent expenditure groups.

### 3.2 International Comparative Evidence: What Democratic Finance Looks Like

The international comparison on campaign finance is among the sharpest in the comparative democratic design literature. The United States stands nearly alone among wealthy democracies in permitting unlimited anonymous corporate spending in elections.

**Canada**: Canada banned corporate and union contributions to political parties in 2004, under Prime Minister Jean Chrétien, following a series of corporate donation scandals. Since 2004, only individuals who are Canadian citizens or permanent residents may make contributions to federal parties. Contribution limits are set at $1,750 per year per party. There are no super PAC equivalents; independent spending by corporations is prohibited. Canada's Elections Act also prohibits third-party advertising from foreign sources and requires full disclosure of all political advertising spend. The contrast with the US post-Citizens United environment is complete: Canada achieves democratic political finance regulation through statutory design that the US Supreme Court has foreclosed.

**Germany**: Germany's political finance system combines party public funding with strict disclosure requirements. Parties receive public subsidies based on electoral performance and membership dues, with a cap on the total state funding any party can receive. Donations from corporations are permitted but fully disclosed and subject to parliamentary review; there is no equivalent to the 501(c)(4) anonymous giving vehicle. The Federal Constitutional Court has repeatedly ruled that political finance transparency is a constitutional requirement, not an optional reform — precisely inverting the American constitutional framework.

**France**: France prohibits corporate donations to political parties entirely and limits individual donations to €7,500 per party per year. The French system combines spending limits (which the US Supreme Court has held unconstitutional under Buckley) with public reimbursement of candidate expenditures. The French Constitutional Council has upheld both spending limits and corporate donation bans as constitutionally required to protect democratic equality — again inverting the American First Amendment framework.

**UK**: The Political Parties, Elections and Referendums Act (PPERA) requires disclosure of all political donations above £500 and all political advertising expenditure. Foreign donations to parties and candidates are prohibited. Unlike the US, the UK system focuses primarily on spending limits during campaign periods rather than contribution limits — parties can raise unlimited funds but face caps on how much they can spend. The Information Commissioner's Office has fined companies for unlawful data use in political targeting (including Cambridge Analytica affiliates), creating an enforcement mechanism comparable to what the FEC should provide in the US but currently cannot.

The comparative lesson is not that any one of these systems is directly transplantable to the US constitutional context — it is that every other wealthy democracy has found a constitutionally consistent way to limit the conversion of private wealth into political power in ways the US Supreme Court has foreclosed under First Amendment doctrine. The pathway to restoring this regulatory capacity in the US runs through constitutional amendment, not legislative reform.

### 3.3 Historical Precedent: The Montana Model

While Citizens United is a federal constitutional ruling, state-level democracy reforms offer both laboratory evidence and, in one case, a direct constitutional challenge.

Montana's Corrupt Practices Act (1912) banned corporate election spending after mining companies, railroads, and copper trusts had purchased Montana's legislature wholesale in the preceding decades. The Montana Supreme Court upheld the law in American Tradition Partnership, Inc. v. Bullock (2012), relying on Montana's unique history of corporate corruption to distinguish it from Citizens United's federal context. The US Supreme Court reversed summarily, applying Citizens United to the states.

The Montana precedent matters for reform strategy: it demonstrates that state supreme courts are willing to engage with factual records of corporate political power as a basis for campaign finance regulation, and it created the precedent for Arizona's Proposition 211 (2022), which required dark money organizations to disclose their donors at the state level. Arizona Prop. 211 — which passed by 72% — has since survived multiple legal challenges, with the Arizona Supreme Court upholding it in 2024 as a valid state disclosure requirement that does not conflict with Citizens United's federal spending rules. The distinction is critical: Citizens United struck down spending limits; it did not address state disclosure requirements, which remain an open reform avenue.

## 4. Current Crisis Data: 2026 Legislative and Advocacy Windows

### 4.1 The DISCLOSE Act of 2026 and the Senate Filibuster Barrier

The DISCLOSE Act of 2026, sponsored by Senator Sheldon Whitehouse and Representative Chris Pappas, was introduced in February 2026 with all 47 Democratic senators and 139 House Democrats as co-sponsors — 182 total. The bill would require organizations spending money in federal elections — including super PACs and 501(c)(4) dark money groups — to promptly disclose donors who have given $10,000 or more during an election cycle. It would extend "stand by your ad" disclosure requirements to online ads, require identification of top funders of outside groups paying for video, text, or audio political ads, and capture payments to social media influencers to promote or oppose a candidate as disclosable political spending.

The bill also includes a specific measure to prevent foreign governments and their agents from interfering in US elections through state and local ballot measure campaigns — a provision that addresses the documented pattern of foreign-linked dark money organizations engaging in ballot measure campaigns in 2020 and 2022.

The bill cannot pass. It does not have and will not receive the 60 Senate votes needed to overcome filibuster. No Republican senator has publicly committed to support the DISCLOSE Act. The bill's annual reintroduction and its 100% Democratic co-sponsorship record since 2010 make it a structural measure of the campaign finance reform community's inability to translate public support (polling consistently shows 70%+ of Americans favor disclosure requirements) into legislative action.

The DISCLOSE Act is not, however, merely symbolic. Its value is threefold: (1) it maintains the legislative record that disclosure requirements are constitutional and have majority support, which is relevant to future constitutional challenges; (2) it provides the specific statutory language for state-level DISCLOSE equivalents, which are the viable near-term reform path; and (3) it provides the organizing frame for the state ballot measure campaigns, which are the immediate advocacy window.

### 4.2 Four State Ballot Measures: The Near-Term Reform Window

The 2026 state ballot measure landscape provides the most immediate advocacy window for campaign finance reform, because state disclosure requirements survive under Citizens United's framework in ways that federal spending prohibitions do not.

**California Fair Elections Act of 2026**: Voters will decide whether to repeal the state's 1988 ban on public campaign funding, opening the door for state and local governments to establish public campaign finance programs. A companion measure would create campaign contribution limits allowing individuals to donate up to $2,000 each election cycle to a candidate and $5,000 per year to a political party, with groups authorized to contribute up to $4,000 per election cycle. These California measures are the most significant state-level campaign finance reforms on the 2026 ballot — they would establish public financing architecture in the nation's largest state without running afoul of Citizens United's spending prohibition, because they involve public funding of campaigns (constitutionally permissible) rather than limits on private spending (which Citizens United prohibits).

**Missouri Amendment 4**: Missouri's Amendment 4 would require disclosure of contributions to ballot measure committees, including dark money organizations. The measure addresses Missouri's specific vulnerability to out-of-state dark money in ballot measure campaigns — demonstrated vividly in 2024 when a coalition of opponents spent $10 million against the abortion rights restoration measure while masking the source of most of that spending through a network of 501(c)(4)s.

**Three Additional States**: OpenSecrets documented three additional states considering expansions of public campaign financing programs for 2026, which, if enacted, would expand the small-donor public matching programs that create an alternative funding pathway for candidates who are not wealthy or do not want to rely on large donors.

**Sources**: [2026 Ballot Measures: 4 Reforms Targeting Campaign Finance and Dark Money — OpenSecrets](https://www.opensecrets.org/news/2026/04/2026-ballot-measures-4-reforms-targeting-campaign-finance-and-dark-money); [Three States Considering Expansions of Public Campaign Financing — OpenSecrets](https://www.opensecrets.org/news/2026/04/three-states-considering-expansions-of-public-campaign-financing-programs)

### 4.3 The FEC Vacancy Crisis as Democratic Design Failure

The FEC's enforcement capacity collapse is not an incidental problem — it is a structural democratic design failure that illustrates the principle the framework has documented across domains: when accountability institutions are disabled, the absence of enforcement is itself a political outcome that benefits whoever holds power.

The FEC's bipartisan design was specifically intended to prevent this: by requiring four votes for enforcement, the system prevented either party from weaponizing the FEC against opponents. What the Trump administration discovered is that this design could be inverted: by firing Democratic appointees and refusing to fill vacancies, the executive can reduce the commission below its quorum threshold, rendering it unable to enforce existing law. The result is not partisan enforcement in one direction — it is no enforcement at all, which benefits incumbents with existing dark money infrastructure and disadvantages challengers who lack established dark money relationships.

The FEC vacancy crisis connects directly to the rest of the framework's analysis of independent agency capture (Domain 2, Domain 6, Domain 35). The Constitutional question — whether the president can fire FEC commissioners at will — remains unresolved in federal court. If the Supreme Court extends its *Seila Law* reasoning (which upheld the president's removal authority over single-director agencies) to multi-member independent commissions, the bipartisan design of the FEC will be definitively dismantled.

## 5. Reform Pathways

### 5.1 Tier 1 — State Disclosure Requirements (Constitutional and Actionable Now)

The most immediately achievable campaign finance reform pathway exploits the gap between Citizens United (which struck down spending limits) and the doctrine that disclosure requirements remain constitutional. The Supreme Court has repeatedly upheld disclosure requirements as serving the anti-corruption and informational interests — most recently in *Doe v. Reed* (2010), where an 8-1 majority upheld disclosure of petition signatures for ballot measures as constitutionally permissible.

State-level disclosure laws modeled on Arizona's Proposition 211 represent the template. Arizona Prop. 211, passed by 72% in 2022 and upheld by the Arizona Supreme Court in 2024, requires any organization that spends $50,000 or more to influence state elections to disclose its top donors. Critically, the disclosure obligation flows through multiple shells: if a 501(c)(4) donates to a super PAC that spends $50,000+ in Arizona elections, the 501(c)(4) must trace and disclose its own donors who funded that contribution. This "look-through" disclosure requirement is the structural innovation that Arizona Prop. 211 demonstrated was both constitutional and practically effective.

States with existing ballot initiative architectures — California, Colorado, Massachusetts, Nevada, Oregon, Washington — are the primary near-term targets for state DISCLOSE legislation modeled on Arizona's approach. Campaign Legal Center has a model state DISCLOSE statute available for immediate adaptation. The November 2026 Missouri and California ballot measures provide the most immediate organizing anchors.

**Sources**: [Doe v. Reed, 561 U.S. 186 (2010)](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/561/186/); [Arizona Proposition 211 — Ballotpedia](https://ballotpedia.org/Arizona_Proposition_211,_Political_Contributions_Disclosure_Requirement_(2022))

### 5.2 Tier 2 — Small Donor Public Matching Programs (Near-Term, State and Federal)

Small donor public matching programs — in which public funds match small individual contributions at a 6:1 or higher ratio — provide a structural alternative to dark money fundraising without conflicting with Citizens United. They do not restrict spending; they amplify the voices of small donors to create competitive parity with large dark money donors.

New York City's public matching program (6:1 match for contributions up to $250) has consistently produced a more diverse donor pool and a more diverse candidate pool than jurisdictions without matching programs. New York State adopted a statewide public matching program in 2020. Maryland, Connecticut, Washington D.C., and several other jurisdictions have implemented variants. The Federal program proposed in the Government By the People Act would provide a 6:1 federal match for contributions up to $200 from constituents, funded by a small surcharge on federal procurement contracts over $100,000.

The California Fair Elections Act of 2026 is the largest-scale implementation attempt currently on the ballot. If passed, it would establish the constitutional authority for California to create state and local public matching programs — the specific design of which would then be determined by the legislature and local governments.

**Sources**: [Government By the People Act — Congress.gov](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/264); [New York City Campaign Finance Board — public matching program analysis](https://www.nyccfb.info); [OpenSecrets: Three States Considering Public Campaign Financing](https://www.opensecrets.org/news/2026/04/three-states-considering-expansions-of-public-campaign-financing-programs)

### 5.3 Tier 3 — Constitutional Amendment (Long Cycle, High Democratic Impact)

Overturning Citizens United through constitutional amendment has majority public support but has faced an insurmountable institutional barrier: the amendment requires two-thirds of both chambers of Congress and three-quarters of states. At least 22 states and hundreds of cities have already voted to support a constitutional amendment to overturn Citizens United.

The amendment movement has two structural paths: (1) Congressional proposal, which faces the same filibuster barrier as the DISCLOSE Act; and (2) Article V convention, which Domain 39 of this framework documents carries its own structural risks if the convention is used to propose multiple amendments simultaneously. The reform community most favorable to the amendment approach — Common Cause, Public Citizen, Move to Amend — has maintained a state legislative campaign to pass resolutions calling for a convention limited to campaign finance. As of 2026, this campaign is more than a decade old and has not reached the 34-state threshold for a convention.

The long-term reform argument for constitutional amendment is nonetheless compelling: without a constitutional change, every statutory disclosure requirement and every public matching program is subject to reversal through courts that extend Citizens United's logic, through FEC enforcement dysfunction, or through legislative repeal. Constitutional amendment is the only mechanism that makes the reform durable across election cycles and judicial appointments. The democratic design argument for prioritizing the amendment campaign is therefore strongest in the comparative context: Canada, Germany, and France all achieved durable campaign finance regulation through constitutional design, not just statutory enactment.

**Sources**: [DISCLOSE Act — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DISCLOSE_Act); [Campaign Finance Reform Amendment — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campaign_finance_reform_amendment); [Why Overturning Citizens United Isn't Enough — Represent.us](https://act.represent.us/sign/what-is-citizens-united)
