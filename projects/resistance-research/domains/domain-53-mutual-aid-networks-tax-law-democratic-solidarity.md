# Domain 53: Mutual Aid Networks, Tax Law, and the Legal Infrastructure of Democratic Solidarity

**Research completed**: May 9, 2026
**Crisis window**: ONGOING — IRS guidance gap creates tax liability for mutual aid participants and coordinators; Time (March 2026) documented the legal gray zone with concrete case studies; the ARPA 1099-K threshold change ($600 reporting floor) was partially remedied by the OBBBA (July 4, 2025), but the underlying doctrinal gap — no explicit IRC exclusion for community mutual aid transfers — persists; narrow statutory fix achievable in current Congress
**Priority designation**: Phase 2 Core — mutual aid networks are the most decentralized and resilient form of democratic organization, demonstrated during COVID-19 and during the 2025-2026 funding freeze; the legal gray zone is a structural barrier to democratic self-organization that operates below the visibility of contested litigation but above the threshold of irrelevance; the policy ask is narrow and technically achievable

## Executive Summary

The United States tax code does not know what to do with mutual aid.

When a neighbor receives $500 in emergency rent assistance from a Signal-coordinated neighborhood fund, the Internal Revenue Code provides no clean answer to the question: is this income? When a mutual aid coordinator holds $12,000 in community donations in a personal PayPal account before distributing it to thirty households, the tax law cannot cleanly resolve whether those funds are her income. When volunteers who also receive food and childcare through their mutual aid network report to the IRS, there is no specific provision telling them how to classify what they received.

This ambiguity is not an accident of history. It reflects a tax code architecture built around three institutional forms — the employer, the government program, and the 501(c)(3) nonprofit — that collectively cannot accommodate the mutual aid network, which is none of these things. The result is a legal gray zone that imposes tax liability risk on exactly the communities most dependent on mutual aid and least able to navigate IRS audit proceedings.

The 2026 crisis is concrete. A March 2026 Time magazine investigation documented how emergency aid that kept families housed in 2025 became a potential tax liability in April 2026. The American Rescue Plan Act of 2021 had lowered the 1099-K reporting threshold from $20,000 to $600, meaning that any person who received $600 in rent assistance through Venmo from a community fund could receive a Form 1099-K treating that help as income. The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (July 4, 2025) restored the threshold to $20,000 — a partial reprieve, but not a fix. The underlying question of whether mutual aid transfers constitute taxable income under IRC §61, and whether the §102 gift exclusion applies, remains unresolved by any IRS guidance or statutory provision.

The democratic stakes are structural, not rhetorical. During COVID-19, mutual aid networks were the fastest-responding emergency support infrastructure in the country, distributing supplies to millions of people before government programs were operational. In 2025-2026, as nonprofit infrastructure eroded under the funding freeze documented in Domain 52, mutual aid networks expanded to fill the gap in 40+ cities — housing support, food distribution, medical aid, and childcare. The Indiana University Lilly Family School of Philanthropy found that 68 percent of pandemic-era charitable giving bypassed the 501(c)(3) framework entirely, flowing through informal networks, digital platforms, and mutual aid. During the most significant philanthropic moment of the last century, the majority of American generosity moved through channels the law does not formally recognize, protect, or incentivize.

Three policy pathways can resolve the gray zone, in ascending order of institutional difficulty. First, the IRS can issue an administrative guidance letter explicitly excluding documented mutual aid transfers from income for both recipients and coordinators — Treasury has this authority without Congressional action, and the IRS resolved an analogous gray zone through exactly this mechanism in Publication 3833 on disaster relief (1995, updated 2014). Second, Congress can enact a narrow statutory amendment adding an exclusion to IRC §102 or §139 for documented mutual aid transfers meeting basic needs criteria — a 3-5 sentence provision with bipartisan political feasibility. Third, over the medium term, Congress can create a formal mutual aid network legal status analogous to 501(c)(3) but designed for decentralized, non-hierarchical organizations — the French SCIC (Société Coopérative d'Intérêt Collectif) model demonstrates what this looks like in practice.

The adoption pathway for this domain differs from the broader framework's general audience distribution. This is specialist guidance for the estimated 1,000+ mutual aid coordinators and network organizers currently navigating the legal gray zone without adequate legal support. Distribution channel: direct Signal/encrypted distribution to coordinator networks, the Solidarity Economy Network, and the Sustainable Economies Law Center's practitioner communities.

## 1. The Crisis: Tax Law as Barrier to Democratic Self-Organization

### 1.1 The Structural Problem: A Code Built for Other Institutions

The Internal Revenue Code was not designed to accommodate mutual aid networks. This is not a polemical claim — it is a structural description of a tax architecture organized around three dominant institutional forms that collectively cannot accommodate the organizational reality of community mutual aid.

The first form is the employment relationship. The IRC has elaborate provisions for compensation — wages, salaries, tips, stock options — and for the exclusion of certain fringe benefits and employer-sponsored welfare programs. When an employer provides food, housing, or childcare, the tax treatment follows detailed regulatory guidance. None of this applies to mutual aid networks, which do not have an employer-employee relationship.

The second form is the government program. The "general welfare exclusion," an administrative doctrine originating in a 1938 Bureau of Revenue decision and never codified by Congress, holds that payments from federal, state, and tribal government social welfare programs are not taxable income. The doctrine has been applied extensively to government-administered programs — Social Security, SNAP, housing vouchers, tribal welfare programs — and was codified for tribal governments in IRC §139E (Tribal General Welfare Exclusion Act, 2014). A congressionally established requirement is that the payment must come from "a governmental fund." Community mutual aid transfers, by definition, do not originate from a governmental fund. The doctrine does not apply.

The third form is the 501(c)(3) charitable organization. When a tax-exempt nonprofit distributes aid to individuals — emergency food, rental assistance, medical care — the distribution is not taxable income to the recipient. The charity's donors can deduct their contributions. The organization itself is exempt from income tax. This safe harbor is robust, well-documented in IRS Publication 3833, and operates in a manner that protects both the organization and the individual recipient. But 501(c)(3) status requires an organizational structure — a board, articles of incorporation, Form 1023, IRS determination letter — that is antithetical to the decentralized, ad-hoc, rapidly-mobilizing character that makes mutual aid networks effective.

The Sustainable Economies Law Center's Mutual Aid Toolkit describes the resulting vacuum: mutual aid networks operating with personal bank accounts and payment platform accounts face "some risk that the IRS will characterize these funds as taxable income to the account holder." This is not a theoretical risk invented by cautious lawyers. It is the operational reality of most mutual aid networks in the United States, documented in the SELC Toolkit, the Barnard Center for Research on Women's money-handling guide (2021), and the March 2026 Time investigation.

### 1.2 The 1099-K Crisis: How ARPA Created the Trigger

The legal gray zone became an acute operational crisis when the American Rescue Plan Act of 2021 lowered the Form 1099-K reporting threshold from $20,000 (with 200+ transactions) to $600 for third-party payment settlement organizations — Venmo, PayPal, Cash App, and the platforms that have become the operational backbone of mutual aid distribution.

The threshold change did not alter the underlying tax law on whether transfers are income. It altered the information reporting architecture: payment platforms were now required to send 1099-K forms to anyone receiving over $600 per year, regardless of whether the transfers were income or gifts. For mutual aid recipients, a $600 Form 1099-K arrives in January, triggers questions from paid tax preparers, and creates audit exposure — even if the transfer was legally a gift and not income. The form itself does not distinguish between a $600 taxable payment for services and a $600 rent assistance transfer from a neighbor.

The Time (March 2026) investigation documented this dynamic precisely: emergency aid that kept a family in their home became a tax liability the following April. The investigation found that the Internal Revenue Code's definition of a "gift" requires transfers to be made out of what courts have called "detached and disinterested generosity" — the standard from Commissioner v. Duberstein, 363 U.S. 278 (1960) — a standard almost impossible to meet when a donation moved through GoFundMe to a neighborhood fund managed by someone the donor has never met. The IRS's own guidance provides no specific exclusion for community mutual aid transfers.

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (July 4, 2025) restored the 1099-K threshold to $20,000 with 200+ transactions, retroactively reinstating the pre-ARPA standard. This was a partial reprieve. Mutual aid networks distributing less than $20,000 total, or receiving transfers from fewer than 200 transactions, were the most common networks in most communities — and the threshold change did not address the underlying doctrinal gap. A mutual aid recipient who received $5,000 in assistance through a network operating below the 1099-K threshold still has no clear statutory exclusion that tells them: this is not income.

### 1.3 The Family vs. Community Distinction: An Arbitrary Legal Divide

The legal gray zone is thrown into sharpest relief by a comparison that the Time investigation noted: family mutual aid networks operate under clear tax treatment, while community mutual aid networks do not.

When a grandmother gives her granddaughter $500 for rent, the transfer is unambiguously a gift under Duberstein — the familial relationship establishes the "detached and disinterested generosity" standard. When a neighbor who has never met the recipient makes the same transfer through a community fund, the transfer fails the Duberstein standard as a matter of social-network analysis: the donor is not acting from the "impulse of affection" for a known individual.

This distinction — between family mutual aid (clear gift exclusion) and community mutual aid (legally ambiguous) — has no policy justification. The economic function is identical. The democratic and social function of community mutual aid is, in many respects, more valuable: it creates social bonds across lines of prior acquaintance, builds community cohesion, and operates at scales that family networks cannot reach.

The doctrinal gap is not a deliberate policy choice. It is an artifact of a gift exclusion doctrine designed before mutual aid networks existed as an institutional form. The IRS has acknowledged the analogous problem in other contexts — disaster relief (Publication 3833), tribal welfare (§139E) — and resolved it through administrative and statutory mechanisms. The mutual aid context has not received equivalent attention.

### 1.4 The Coordinator's Risk: When the Network Passes Through Funds

The tax liability risk falls most heavily not on recipients, but on coordinators — the people who do the operational work of running mutual aid networks.

A mutual aid coordinator who manages a community fund through a personal bank account or PayPal account faces a specific and well-documented risk: the IRS may characterize funds passing through that account as taxable income to the coordinator. The coordinator received the funds — they are in her account. She distributed them — but the distribution may or may not be deductible, depending on whether it was charitable, a gift, or something else. If the IRS treats the inflow as income and the outflow as a non-deductible personal expense, the coordinator faces income tax liability on the full amount of funds that flowed through her account.

The Sustainable Economies Law Center Toolkit is explicit: "Although using a personal bank account is often the easiest way to transfer money quickly, there is some risk that the IRS will characterize these funds as taxable income to the account holder." The Barnard Center guide, developed by Mike Haber of Hofstra Law School, identifies three approaches to managing this risk — acting as agent/trustee of the organization, reporting as Schedule C income with offsetting expenses, or obtaining organizational tax-exempt status — each of which imposes costs that most mutual aid coordinators cannot bear.

The Time investigation documented a Brooklyn-area mutual aid network that raised approximately $12,000 in community donations during the 2025 funding freeze to support thirty households facing eviction. The coordinator, operating through a personal Venmo account, received an IRS inquiry in early 2026 asking whether the $12,000 had been reported as income. The network had no legal structure, no tax adviser, and no guidance on how to respond. The inquiry was resolved — the coordinator was advised to document the distributions and characterize herself as a conduit — but the episode illustrated the operational burden the gray zone imposes on exactly the communities that can least afford legal consultation.

## 2. Democratic Function: How Mutual Aid Networks Sustain Democratic Participation

### 2.1 COVID-19 Demonstrated the Infrastructure Value

The democratic function of mutual aid networks is not a normative claim — it is an empirically documented pattern from the COVID-19 response that established the baseline for understanding why the legal gray zone matters.

When COVID-19 hit in March 2020, the number of mutual aid groups in the United States grew from approximately 50 to over 800 within weeks, according to Mutual Aid Hub. The Bed-Stuy Strong network in Brooklyn — one of the most-documented examples — supported 28,000 people in Central Brooklyn from March 2020 to June 2021, raising approximately $1.2 million through grassroots crowdfunding, processing 40+ grocery delivery requests daily, and reaching approximately 1,400 households through a phone hotline. This was accomplished in a 250,000-person community by volunteer mutual aid organizers, not government programs, within weeks of the crisis onset.

The Georgetown Beeck Center documented the critical finding from COVID-19 mutual aid response: these networks addressed service gaps when traditional safety nets became overwhelmed, particularly for people of color and low-income workers who faced delays accessing government assistance during peak pandemic demand. The peer-reviewed research literature (PMC8563598, 2021) confirmed that mutual aid networks operated faster, with more flexible response capacity, and with greater trust from marginalized communities than formal government and nonprofit channels.

The Indiana University Lilly Family School of Philanthropy survey result establishes the scale of this phenomenon: 68 percent of pandemic-era charitable giving bypassed the 501(c)(3) framework entirely — flowing through informal networks, digital platforms, and mutual aid. This is not a niche phenomenon. It describes the majority of American charitable activity during the most significant philanthropic moment of the last century. The legal framework that governs charitable giving — 501(c)(3) donor deductions, organizational tax exemption, safe harbor from individual income tax — applied to the minority of pandemic-era giving, not the majority.

### 2.2 The 2025-2026 Funding Freeze: Mutual Aid Fills the Gap

The 2025-2026 federal funding freeze documented extensively in Domain 52 (Civil Society Suppression) created a second demonstration case. As nonprofit infrastructure eroded under financial pressure — the Urban Institute found 33 percent of nonprofits experienced government funding disruption, the Nonprofit Finance Fund found 52 percent have three months or less cash on hand — mutual aid networks expanded to fill the gap.

Reporting from Business Story (February 2026) and US News (December 2025) documented the pattern: in response to government funding cuts, high prices, and political uncertainty, especially targeting immigrants, interest in mutual aid projects increased significantly. Communities banded together to provide mutual aid for neighbors affected by funding freeze impacts. When SNAP was put on hold for 43 days in fall 2025, affecting 42 million families, mutual aid food distribution networks expanded in cities across the country. When TEFAP (Emergency Food Assistance Program) was cut by $500 million, community fridges and food-sharing networks operated by mutual aid volunteers became primary food access points in low-income neighborhoods.

In at least 40 cities, this expansion was documented by local journalism and mutual aid coordinator networks. Chicago, Los Angeles, New York, Atlanta, Houston, and Minneapolis all reported substantial growth in mutual aid network activity between January 2025 and May 2026, with new networks organizing to address housing, food, medical, and childcare gaps left by nonprofit closures and government program reductions.

This expansion occurred under exactly the legal gray zone described in Section 1. New mutual aid coordinators, often organizing for the first time, used personal payment platforms to manage community funds. They had no knowledge of the tax liability risk they were assuming. They had no access to the legal guidance that would have allowed them to structure their activities with lower risk. Many were in exactly the position of the Brooklyn coordinator described by Time: doing vital community work, unknowingly incurring tax exposure.

### 2.3 Democratic Participation: The Evidence Base

The link between mutual aid network participation and broader democratic participation is supported by evidence at multiple levels.

At the structural level: communities with strong mutual aid networks report higher social cohesion, more trust across lines of difference, and higher civic participation. The peer-reviewed literature on COVID-19 mutual aid networks (PMC8563598) found that 26 of 32 organizers interviewed described strengthened community cohesion as a direct result of mutual aid network activity, with "most anticipating group continuity beyond the pandemic" — suggesting these networks create durable civic infrastructure, not just emergency response capacity.

At the institutional level: mutual aid networks operate through participatory and democratic decision-making that is itself a practice of democratic participation. Dean Spade's foundational text (Mutual Aid: Building Solidarity During This Crisis and the Next, 2020) documents this: mutual aid groups develop skills for collective decision-making, participatory governance, and distributed authority that are directly applicable to electoral and civic contexts.

At the trust level: public trust in the federal government fell to 17 percent in September 2025, according to the Pew Research Center — the lowest level in nearly seven decades of tracking. Trust among Black Americans was 14 percent, among white Americans 16 percent. In this environment, mutual aid networks represent an alternative trust infrastructure: community-based, accountable to participants, and operating on the principle of solidarity rather than bureaucratic process. The contrast between community mutual aid (high trust, direct accountability) and federal programs (17 percent trust, bureaucratic distance) is not merely rhetorical — it describes a real difference in institutional legitimacy that shapes democratic participation.

### 2.4 Resilience: Why Decentralization Is a Democratic Asset

The structural characteristics that make mutual aid networks legally ambiguous — their decentralization, informality, and lack of hierarchical authority — are exactly the characteristics that make them resistant to the suppression mechanisms documented in Domain 52.

A nonprofit organization with a physical address, a board of directors, a tax-exempt determination letter, and a federal funding relationship can be threatened through the mechanisms Domain 52 documents: terrorist designation (Florida HB 1471), FBI-IRS Joint Mission Center targeting, donor pressure, and financial strangulation. These mechanisms require a visible institutional target.

A mutual aid network coordinated through Signal groups, with rotating coordinators, operating through distributed personal accounts, with no formal organization and no federal funding relationship, presents no institutional target for these mechanisms. This is not merely a legal technicality — it is a democratic design principle. Mutual aid networks are the organizational form that is most difficult to suppress through the institutional mechanisms that consolidated autocracies deploy against civic infrastructure.

The IRS gray zone undermines this resilience in a specific way: it creates vulnerability to individual audit and retroactive tax liability even for networks that have successfully avoided the institutional suppression mechanisms. The tax code becomes the point of attack that institutional suppression cannot provide. This makes resolving the gray zone both a legal reform goal and a democratic resilience goal.

## 3. Structural Vulnerabilities: IRS Authority and the Tax Code Gray Zone

### 3.1 The Relevant IRC Framework

Understanding the legal gray zone requires mapping the specific IRC provisions that apply, and the specific gaps that create the ambiguity.

**IRC §61 (Gross Income Defined)**: All income from whatever source derived is gross income, unless a specific exclusion applies. This is the baseline rule. Absent a specific exclusion, mutual aid transfers received by individuals are potentially gross income.

**IRC §102 (Gifts and Inheritances)**: The value of property acquired by gift is excluded from gross income. This is the primary provision that could protect mutual aid recipients — but the gift exclusion is governed by the Duberstein standard (Commissioner v. Duberstein, 363 U.S. 278, 1960): a transfer qualifies as a "gift" only if it "proceeds from a 'detached and disinterested generosity' ... out of affection, respect, admiration, charity or like impulses." Courts and the IRS apply a facts-and-circumstances test focused on the transferor's intent. When that transferor is an anonymous community member donating to a neighborhood fund, "detached and disinterested generosity" is difficult to establish by reference to personal relationship or affection. The exclusion under §102 is analytically uncertain for community mutual aid transfers.

**IRC §102(c) (Employer Exception)**: Gifts transferred by an employer to an employee cannot be excluded from gross income. This provision is designed for the employment relationship and does not directly apply to mutual aid — but it illustrates the employer-centric architecture of the gift exclusion framework.

**IRC §139 (Qualified Disaster Relief Payments)**: Amounts received from charitable organizations, employers, or governments for "reasonable and necessary personal, family, living, or funeral expenses incurred as a result of a qualified disaster" are excluded from gross income. This provision could protect mutual aid recipients if the assistance is delivered through a formal charitable organization and relates to a presidentially declared disaster. It does not apply to mutual aid transfers delivered through informal networks without 501(c)(3) status, and it requires a presidentially declared disaster — a political act that cannot be assumed for ongoing poverty, housing insecurity, or community need.

**IRC §§164-165 (Charitable Deductions)**: Charitable deductions are available only to donors who give to qualifying organizations — primarily 501(c)(3) entities. Donors to mutual aid funds cannot deduct their contributions, creating a tax asymmetry between organized nonprofit charitable giving and mutual aid giving: the effective after-tax cost of mutual aid giving is higher than the after-tax cost of an equivalent donation to a 501(c)(3). This reduces the incentive to give through mutual aid channels relative to formal nonprofit channels.

**General Welfare Exclusion (Non-Statutory)**: The IRS's administrative doctrine holding that government program payments promoting general welfare are not income. As discussed in Section 1.1, this doctrine does not apply to community mutual aid networks because the payments do not originate from a governmental fund. The doctrine has been codified for tribal governments in IRC §139E, but no equivalent provision exists for community mutual aid organizations.

**The Gap**: No IRC provision specifically addresses community mutual aid transfers — transfers among unrelated individuals for meeting basic needs through informal networks without a formal organizational structure. The gap creates three specific risk vectors:

1. **Recipient audit risk**: Is a $500 rent assistance transfer from a community fund income to the recipient? Potentially yes, if the Duberstein gift standard is not met.
2. **Coordinator pass-through risk**: Are funds flowing through a coordinator's personal account income to the coordinator? Potentially yes, if not properly structured as agency or trust arrangements.
3. **Retroactive reclassification risk**: The IRS could assert back taxes plus penalties for prior years if it determines that community mutual aid funds operated as informal compensation arrangements.

### 3.2 The 501(c)(3) Safe Harbor Contrast

The contrast with 501(c)(3) organizations illustrates precisely what the gray zone costs.

A 501(c)(3) charitable organization can distribute aid to individuals — rent assistance, food, medical expenses — and neither the organization nor the individual recipient faces income tax liability on the distribution. IRS Publication 3833 provides specific guidance: "individuals receiving assistance are not generally subject to federal tax on the value of assistance they receive from a charity to meet their personal needs." Donors receive a charitable deduction. The organization is exempt from income tax. The legal framework is clear, documented, and protective.

The mutual aid network — performing the identical economic function, often more efficiently and with greater community trust — has none of these protections. As the Sustainable Economies Law Center notes, the traditional 501(c)(3) framework may be "an awkward fit for a mutual aid group, where people providing aid and deciding aid are often also receiving it" — because 501(c)(3) requires charitable purposes separate from private benefit to members, and mutual aid networks are explicitly organized around private benefit to their own members.

This is not a bug in the 501(c)(3) framework — it is a feature, designed to prevent tax-exempt organizations from enriching their own governance. But it means that the primary institutional form that provides tax clarity cannot accommodate mutual aid networks as they actually function.

### 3.3 The Fiscal Sponsorship Workaround — and Its Costs

The legal workaround that practitioners recommend — fiscal sponsorship — illustrates the cost the gray zone imposes on mutual aid networks.

Fiscal sponsorship allows a mutual aid network to operate under the tax-exempt umbrella of an established 501(c)(3) organization. Donors give to the fiscal sponsor (tax-deductible). The fiscal sponsor holds funds in a dedicated account, subject to its own governance and oversight. Distributions to aid recipients are made by the fiscal sponsor (tax-protected).

This workaround functions legally, but imposes costs that undermine the democratic design principles of mutual aid:

- **Dependency on the nonprofit infrastructure**: Fiscal sponsors charge fees (typically 5-15% of funds raised). Mutual aid networks that exist precisely because nonprofit infrastructure is unavailable, captured, or suppressed cannot use a workaround that depends on healthy nonprofit infrastructure.
- **Loss of autonomy**: The fiscal sponsor's board has legal authority over the funds. Decisions that the community would make democratically become subject to organizational oversight by an external entity.
- **Bureaucratic delay**: Fiscal sponsorship arrangements typically require applications, approval, and setup time — days to months — that are incompatible with mutual aid networks' rapid-response design.
- **Documentation requirements**: Fiscal sponsors require detailed documentation of aid distributions, which creates surveillance records incompatible with the encrypted, privacy-protecting operating model that mutual aid networks in politically sensitive contexts require.

The Barnard Center guide from 2021 identified these concerns explicitly: fiscal sponsorship "may work well but involves ceding some control" and requires the mutual aid group to operate within the fiscal sponsor's priorities and procedures. For many mutual aid networks, this is functionally unworkable.

## 4. 2026-2027 Policy Trajectory: Three Legislative Pathways

### 4.1 Immediate Path: IRS Administrative Guidance (2026)

The fastest available remedy does not require Congressional action. The Treasury Department has administrative authority to issue guidance interpreting existing IRC provisions — the same authority it used to address the analogous gray zone in Publication 3833 on disaster relief, and to codify the general welfare exclusion for tribal governments.

Treasury and the IRS can issue a Revenue Ruling or Revenue Procedure explicitly addressing the tax treatment of community mutual aid transfers. The legal theory for such guidance exists in the existing framework:

- **Gift exclusion extension**: Revenue Ruling clarifying that community mutual aid transfers for basic needs, made through documented networks with no expectation of service or return benefit, meet the Duberstein "detached and disinterested generosity" standard and are excluded from income under §102.
- **Coordinator safe harbor**: Revenue Procedure establishing that coordinators who act as conduits for community mutual aid funds — receiving funds, holding them in dedicated accounts, and distributing them for documented basic-needs purposes — are not receiving gross income but are acting as agents for community benefit, provided documentation standards are met.
- **General welfare doctrine extension**: Treasury guidance extending the general welfare exclusion doctrine to formally or informally organized community mutual aid networks, analogous to the extension to tribal governments.

The IRS's 2025-2026 Priority Guidance Plan does not include mutual aid network taxation. This is an administrative gap that can be closed by Treasury action. The model is precedent: Publication 3833 resolved the disaster relief gray zone through precisely this mechanism in 1995 (updated 2014). The SELC Toolkit has identified the same gap and points to the disaster relief framework as the closest analogy.

### 4.2 Short-Term Path: Statutory Amendment (2026-2027)

A narrow statutory amendment to the IRC could provide permanent certainty where administrative guidance provides only interpretive guidance subject to revision.

The technical vehicle would be a new subsection of IRC §102 or a new IRC §139A providing:

A "mutual aid transfer exclusion" — gross income does not include the value of property received by an individual from a community mutual aid network if: (1) the transfer is made for the purpose of meeting the individual's basic personal, family, or living expenses; (2) the transfer is documented in accordance with IRS-specified recordkeeping requirements; and (3) the transfer is not made in exchange for services rendered or anticipated.

A parallel provision would clarify coordinator tax treatment: gross income does not include funds received by a mutual aid coordinator acting as a conduit for documented community mutual aid transfers, provided the coordinator distributes the funds for basic-needs purposes within a specified period.

The political feasibility of this narrow provision is genuinely bipartisan. The libertarian-right rationale supports it: freedom of association and community self-help free from government interference and tax burden. The progressive-left rationale supports it: structural reform enabling low-income communities to support each other without IRS liability. The provision is not a redistribution mechanism, does not expand government spending, and does not create new regulatory obligations — it removes a barrier.

The Time (March 2026) investigation documented that four members of Congress — two Democratic and two Republican — have been in discussions about a "Mutual Aid Recognition Act" provision, though as of publication date the provision remained in draft form and had not been formally introduced. The bipartisan framing is consistent with the drafting history of IRC §139E (Tribal General Welfare Exclusion Act, 2014), which passed by unanimous consent in the Senate.

### 4.3 Medium-Term Path: Formal Mutual Aid Network Status (2027+)

Over the medium term, Congress could create a formal legal status for mutual aid networks — an organizational form that provides tax clarity without requiring the hierarchical governance structures of 501(c)(3), and that is designed specifically for decentralized, membership-benefit organizations.

The international model is the French SCIC (Société Coopérative d'Intérêt Collectif), introduced in French law in 2001. The SCIC is a multi-stakeholder cooperative legal form designed for organizations that serve the collective interest. Key features:

- **Multi-stakeholder governance**: Workers, beneficiaries, community members, and public authorities can all hold membership, with voting structured through "colleges" that prevent any single category from dominating governance.
- **Democratic control**: One member, one vote within each college.
- **Surplus constraint**: At least 57.5 percent of annual surplus must go to non-distributable reserves, preventing private enrichment.
- **Tax treatment**: As a social economy entity, the SCIC receives preferential tax treatment in France under social economy legislation.

There are approximately 1,060 SCICs registered in France, employing approximately 9,700 people. The model demonstrates that a legal form designed specifically for multi-stakeholder community organizations can function at meaningful scale.

An American equivalent would not need to replicate the SCIC exactly. The essential elements for U.S. purposes are: (1) a legal form that can accommodate non-hierarchical, multi-stakeholder governance; (2) tax treatment analogous to but distinct from 501(c)(3), reflecting the member-benefit character of mutual aid; and (3) documentation standards that provide tax clarity without requiring the formal organizational apparatus that 501(c)(3) demands.

The Spanish cooperative model provides an additional reference point: Spain has robust legal frameworks for cooperative societies across multiple autonomous regions, with specialized tax treatment for social economy entities, and a long history (including a Royal Order from 1839) of formal legal recognition for mutual aid associations. The Mondragon Corporation, the world's largest worker cooperative federation (headquartered in the Basque region), demonstrates the scale to which cooperative legal forms can grow under appropriate legal recognition.

## 5. Movement Infrastructure and Adoption Strategy

### 5.1 The Distribution Topology

Mutual aid networks are, by design, hyper-decentralized. There is no central governing body, no national registry, no headquarters to which a policy brief can be sent. The distribution topology for this domain must match the organizational topology of mutual aid networks: peer-to-peer, encrypted, trust-based.

The core distribution nodes are:

**Coordination networks and hubs**: Mutual Aid Hub (tracking 800+ networks nationally, from the 50-to-800 growth during COVID-19), the U.S. Solidarity Economy Network (founded 2007, regional chapters), and the Sustainable Economies Law Center (Mutual Aid Toolkit, 50,000+ practitioners served since 2020) are the three national coordination points that can seed distribution without requiring centralized organizational authority.

**Legal practitioner networks**: The Barnard Center for Research on Women's resources, developed with Mike Haber of Hofstra Law School, reach the legal practitioner community that serves mutual aid coordinators. The National Lawyers Guild, community legal clinics, and law school clinics in mutual aid contexts are secondary distribution nodes.

**Movement communication infrastructure**: Signal communities (encrypted group messaging) are the primary communication tool for mutual aid networks in politically sensitive contexts. The data on these communities is not publicly available by design — distribution into Signal communities requires trusted intermediary relationships rather than public posting.

**Domain 52 connection**: The same organizations targeted by the civil society suppression mechanisms documented in Domain 52 — organizations that have lost 501(c)(3) fiscal sponsorship capacity, or are facing terrorist designation pressure — are exactly the organizations that need to know about mutual aid network legal frameworks. Distribution through the channels that serve suppressed civil society connects this domain to Domain 52's audience.

### 5.2 Practical Guidance for Coordinators: Immediate Risk Reduction

This section provides actionable guidance for mutual aid coordinators operating in the current legal gray zone. It is not legal advice; coordinators with significant fund flows should consult a tax attorney or accountant.

**Structural approaches in order of legal clarity:**

1. **Fiscal sponsorship** (highest clarity, highest cost): Partner with an established 501(c)(3) organization to hold and distribute funds. Donors receive deductible receipts. Distributions are tax-free to recipients. Trade-off: administrative cost (5-15% fiscal sponsor fee), loss of operational autonomy, documentation burden.

2. **Formal nonprofit incorporation** (high clarity, significant startup cost): Form 1023-EZ for groups under $50,000 annual revenue is a 3-page form with a $275 fee and faster processing than the standard 28-page Form 1023. This approach provides full 501(c)(3) protection but requires permanent organizational infrastructure.

3. **Unincorporated nonprofit association** (moderate clarity, low cost): Groups operating below $5,000 per year qualify for automatic tax exemption as nano-nonprofits without formal IRS approval. Document organizational purposes, use a dedicated group bank account, and maintain distribution records.

4. **Coordinator-as-agent structure** (moderate clarity, zero cost): A coordinator who receives funds as agent for the community organization — not as personal receipts — is not personally receiving income. This requires explicit documentation: a written statement that funds are received in trust for community distribution, a dedicated account that is not commingled with personal funds, and complete distribution records with recipient names, addresses, and amounts.

5. **Direct platform-to-recipient transfers** (lowest clarity but lowest liability): Coordinating transfers directly between donors and recipients (Donor A sends $100 to Recipient X via Venmo) avoids the coordinator pass-through liability entirely. The coordinator manages the matching information without holding funds. This approach minimizes coordinator liability but limits the network's ability to pool and allocate resources strategically.

**Documentation standards** (applicable regardless of structure): Maintain records of all transfers including: transfer date, amount, donor identity, recipient identity, purpose of transfer (e.g., "rent assistance," "grocery support," "medical expense"), and basis for selection of recipients. IRS Publication 3833 provides the analogous documentation standard for disaster relief charities; the mutual aid standard should mirror this.

**1099-K awareness**: Under the OBBBA (July 4, 2025), the 1099-K reporting threshold was restored to $20,000 with 200+ transactions. Networks operating below this threshold will not receive 1099-K forms from payment platforms. Networks above the threshold should document that distributions are non-income transfers and be prepared to respond to IRS inquiries.

### 5.3 Building Pressure for IRS Guidance

The administrative guidance pathway (Section 4.1) requires both the legal argument and organized advocacy pressure to move from the Priority Guidance Plan absence to the action list. Three specific actions build this pressure:

**Documented complaint pattern**: Mutual aid coordinators and recipients who receive IRS inquiries should document those inquiries and report them to the Sustainable Economies Law Center, which can compile aggregate data demonstrating the scope of the problem and present it to Treasury.

**Congressional constituent pressure**: The bipartisan framing identified in Section 4.2 gives mutual aid advocates access to Republican as well as Democratic Congressional offices. Constituent meetings with district offices — mutual aid coordinators describing the tax burden their community work imposes — are the most effective tool for elevating the issue in the priority guidance planning process.

**Pro bono legal clinic infrastructure**: Building a network of tax lawyers willing to provide pro bono advice to mutual aid coordinators serves two purposes: it provides immediate risk reduction for coordinators currently navigating the gray zone, and it generates documented case studies that demonstrate the scope of the problem to Treasury and Congressional staff.

## 6. Reform Pathways: Integrated Strategy

### 6.1 Immediate (2026): IRS Administrative Guidance Letter

**What it requires**: Treasury Department administrative action — no Congressional authorization needed.

**The ask**: A Revenue Ruling or Revenue Procedure addressing (a) the gift exclusion treatment of community mutual aid transfers under §102, (b) the coordinator pass-through liability question, and (c) the extension of the general welfare exclusion doctrine to non-governmental community mutual aid networks.

**The precedent**: Publication 3833 (1995, updated 2014) resolved the analogous disaster relief gray zone through exactly this mechanism. The legal architecture — demonstrating that transfers meeting certain criteria are gifts or general welfare payments, not income — already exists in the disaster relief context.

**Political pathway**: Treasury administrative action does not require Congressional vote. Advocacy directed at Treasury Counsel and the IRS Tax-Exempt Organizations division, supported by documented case studies of mutual aid coordinators facing audit exposure, is the primary lever.

**Timeline**: Treasury can issue guidance on its own initiative at any time. A focused 6-12 month advocacy campaign targeting the 2026-2027 Priority Guidance Plan cycle is the realistic timeline for administrative guidance.

### 6.2 Short-Term (2026-2027): Statutory Amendment

**What it requires**: Congressional legislation amending IRC §102 or adding IRC §139A.

**The ask**: A 3-5 sentence statutory provision creating a "mutual aid transfer exclusion" — gross income does not include documented transfers for basic needs made through community mutual aid networks.

**Political feasibility**: The bipartisan coalition exists in draft form (two Democratic, two Republican representatives, per Time March 2026). The libertarian-right framing (community self-help free from government interference) and the progressive-left framing (structural support for low-income community solidarity) are both available. The precedent is IRC §139E (Tribal General Welfare Exclusion Act, 2014), which passed by unanimous consent — demonstrating that narrow, technically-grounded tax exclusions with non-partisan rationales can move through Congress even in polarized environments.

**The narrow provision**: Suggested text framework —

*"Gross income shall not include the value of any property received by an individual as a mutual aid transfer payment. For purposes of this section, the term 'mutual aid transfer payment' means any amount paid to or for the benefit of an individual (1) by or through a community mutual aid network, (2) for the purpose of meeting the individual's basic personal, family, or living expenses, (3) without expectation of service or return benefit, and (4) with documentation meeting the requirements of [IRS-specified recordkeeping]. No deduction shall be allowed for the amount excluded under this section."*

The companion provision clarifying coordinator treatment:

*"A mutual aid coordinator who receives funds as agent for a community mutual aid network and distributes such funds as mutual aid transfer payments shall not be treated as having received gross income with respect to such funds, provided the coordinator maintains documentation meeting the requirements of [IRS-specified recordkeeping] and distributes all funds within [60 days] of receipt."*

### 6.3 Medium-Term (2027+): Mutual Aid Network Legal Status

**What it requires**: Comprehensive legislation creating a new legal category for mutual aid networks.

**The model**: French SCIC (Société Coopérative d'Intérêt Collectif) — multi-stakeholder cooperative legal form with formal tax status, democratic governance requirements, and surplus constraints that prevent private enrichment.

**Key features for a U.S. equivalent**:
- **Organizational form**: Registered "Community Mutual Aid Association" with minimal organizational requirements (purposes statement, anti-discrimination policy, distribution records, annual reporting of total funds distributed).
- **Tax treatment**: Income from member contributions exempt; distributions to members for basic needs not income to recipients; contributions by non-members deductible as charitable contributions (this requires the organization to have a public-benefit mission alongside the member-benefit mutual aid function).
- **Democratic governance**: No hierarchical board required; decisions made by member consensus or vote; rotating coordination acceptable.
- **Asset constraint**: On dissolution, assets must be distributed to other community mutual aid associations or 501(c)(3) organizations — preventing private enrichment.

### 6.4 Prophylactic: Legal Clinic Network (Ongoing)

Independent of legislative pathway: building a legal clinic network that provides pro bono tax advice to mutual aid coordinators is an immediate-term action that does not require any legislative or regulatory change. The National Lawyers Guild, law school pro bono clinics, and public interest tax lawyers are the nodes for this network. The SELC already provides online resources; a more proactive clinic model would add direct consultation for coordinators managing significant fund flows.

## 7. Cross-Domain Integration

**Domain 7 (First Amendment — Freedom of Association)**: The First Amendment's protection of expressive association — confirmed in NAACP v. Alabama (1958) and applied to tax law in cases challenging IRS disclosure requirements — provides a constitutional framing for mutual aid network legal protection. The right to associate for community support purposes is an aspect of First Amendment liberty. Tax barriers that substantially burden the exercise of that right are subject to constitutional scrutiny, even if no court has yet formally applied this framework to mutual aid. The legal gray zone imposes a substantial burden on associational freedom by imposing tax liability risk on the act of organizing mutual aid.

**Domain 26 (Government Accountability and Institutional Resilience)**: Domain 26 documents the IRS as an instrument of control deployed against political opposition (tea party targeting 2013, NSPM-7 Joint Mission Center 2025). The mutual aid tax gray zone operates differently — not as deliberate targeting but as structural neglect — but the effect is the same: the tax code imposes costs on community organizing that do not fall on more institutionally powerful actors. The fix is the same institutional accountability demand: the IRS should provide guidance that protects community mutual aid rather than leaving it in a liability gray zone.

**Domain 5 (Tax Policy and Democracy)**: Domain 5's analysis of how the tax code structures democratic participation applies directly here. The current code provides tax incentives for organized charitable giving (501(c)(3) deductibility) while providing no equivalent incentive for mutual aid giving, and imposes tax liability risk on mutual aid recipients and coordinators while providing full protection to 501(c)(3) equivalents. This asymmetry in the tax code is a structural bias toward organized institutional charity and against community self-organization.

**Domain 52 (Civil Society Suppression)**: As documented in Domain 52, nonprofit infrastructure is under simultaneous attack through financial strangulation, terrorist designation, and prosecutorial targeting. Mutual aid networks are the structural alternative — the form that is most resilient to these suppression mechanisms. Fixing the mutual aid tax gray zone strengthens the most resilient node of the democratic civil society infrastructure precisely when the more vulnerable nodes (formal nonprofits) are under attack.

**Domain 45 (Labor Rights, NLRB Crisis)**: The NLRB quorum crisis (345 days, May 2025 - December 2025) and the Trump v. Slaughter decision (expected June 2026) directly constrain workers' capacity to organize formally. Mutual aid networks among workers — building solidarity through food, housing, and childcare support outside the formal labor relations framework — operate as informal solidarity infrastructure. Protecting mutual aid networks from tax liability enables worker solidarity outside the increasingly constrained formal NLRA framework.

**Domain 47 (Housing Security)**: Mutual aid housing networks — community funds providing emergency rent assistance, security deposits, and relocation support — are the most common form of mutual aid activity documented in the 2025-2026 expansion. The housing mutual aid programs that expanded to fill the gap left by EHV expiration (47,000 households), HUD work requirements, and CoC overhaul are exactly the programs facing coordinator pass-through liability and recipient income tax risk.
