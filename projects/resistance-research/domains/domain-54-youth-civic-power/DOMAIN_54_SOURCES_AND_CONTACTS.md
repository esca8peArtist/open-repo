## Section 3: Expert Researcher Map

### Academic Researchers in Youth Voting and Civic Participation

**Dr. Kei Kawashima-Ginsberg** — Director, CIRCLE, Tufts University (Tisch College of Civic Life). Primary point of contact for the most comprehensive youth voting data institution in the field. CIRCLE's methodology — combining self-reported turnout with state-level verification and voter file matching — is the field standard. Contact via circle@tufts.edu. Key publication areas: youth civic participation; broadening youth voting; voter barriers and hardships.

**Dr. Peter Levine** — Associate Dean and Lincoln Filene Professor of Citizenship and Public Affairs, Tufts University. Long-time CIRCLE faculty affiliate. Expert on youth civic education and political participation, deliberative democracy, and youth organizing theory. His work bridges the academic literature on civic education effectiveness and the empirical CIRCLE data on what produces turnout. Relevant for Research Area 5 (civic participation models).

**Dr. Cathy Cohen** — University of Chicago; founder of GenForward survey. Primary expert on Black and Latinx youth political attitudes and participation. GenForward conducts monthly surveys of a nationally representative sample of adults aged 18–34 with oversamples of underrepresented racial groups — the only ongoing dataset with this demographic granularity. Relevant for the racial bifurcation finding within the youth turnout data (25% Black men vs. 58% white women).

**Dr. Michael Hanmer** — University of Maryland; expert on voting behavior, voter registration laws, and electoral system effects. Key publications on same-day registration effects on youth, provisional ballot disproportionality, and state election law analysis. Relevant for Research Areas 1 and 3 (registration barriers, suppression mechanics).

**Jilli Jung and Maithreyi Gopalan** — Pennsylvania State University; authors of the 2024 *American Educational Research Journal* paper establishing that mandated civics tests do not increase youth voter turnout. Directly relevant for Research Area 5 intervention design analysis. The noncognitive skills finding (perseverance, self-efficacy predict voting more than civic knowledge) has significant implications for what types of civic programming should receive research endorsement.

## Section 4: Data Sources for VAP Turnout Trends

**CIRCLE at Tufts (circle.tufts.edu)** — Primary source for youth-specific (18–29) turnout estimates, registration data, and barrier analysis. Methodology uses voter file matching plus self-reported surveys. Post-election final estimates typically released 3–4 weeks after election day.

**U.S. Census Bureau Current Population Survey (CPS) Voting and Registration Supplement** — Biennial survey measuring voter registration and turnout by demographic group. Data available for presidential and midterm years. URL: https://www.census.gov/topics/public-sector/voting.html. Note: methodological differences (self-report, different age groupings) make direct comparison to CIRCLE estimates complicated.

**American National Election Studies (ANES)** — Long-running survey time series; pre- and post-election waves. Allows tracking of political attitudes and participation for cohort analysis. URL: https://electionstudies.org/. Most useful for Research Area 5 (civic participation models) and longitudinal attitude trend analysis.

**State election data repositories** — State Secretary of State offices publish voter registration and turnout data by age group (where captured) after each election. Granularity varies significantly by state. The most useful for Domain 54: Michigan (Bureau of Elections, Michigan.gov/sos), Georgia (Georgia Secretary of State, sos.ga.gov), Wisconsin (Wisconsin Elections Commission, elections.wi.gov), North Carolina (NCSBE, ncsbe.gov).

**Harvard Youth Poll (Institute of Politics)** — Biannual survey of Americans ages 18–29; Spring 2026 edition released. URL: https://iop.harvard.edu/youth-poll/latest-poll. Most useful for political attitude and issue-priority data; complements CIRCLE's behavior and turnout data.

## Section 5: Research Gaps Requiring July–August Fieldwork

These data points cannot be obtained through desk research and require direct outreach or primary reporting:

1. **Age-cohort data within felony disenfranchisement population**: The Sentencing Project does not publicly disaggregate the 4-million disenfranchised figure by age. Direct researcher contact needed.

2. **Campus polling location inventory for 2026 competitive districts**: No public database tracks which campuses have on-campus polling locations, hours, and access conditions for 2026. Campus Vote Project (Justin Calhoun, HBCU Lead; state leads listed above) is the primary source.

3. **SAVE Act Senate procedural status as of July 2026**: The bill failed cloture in March–April 2026 (60 votes needed; Republicans hold 53 and Lisa Murkowski voted against proceeding). Monitoring is needed for any Senate revival before October 2026 voter registration deadlines.

4. **Digital demobilization content scale in 2025–2026**: No public inventory of AI-generated or algorithmically amplified youth voter demobilization content. Brennan Center AI voter suppression team (brennancenter.org) and UW Madison PNAS study authors are the primary contacts.

5. **Youth civic organization funding status after 2025 DEI cuts**: Multiple organizations in this space operated on DEI-framed grants that retrenched. Direct outreach to O-01 through O-04 required to assess 2026 operational capacity.

6. **2026 state primary youth turnout data**: Spring 2026 primary youth turnout figures from state election authorities will be the first empirical signal of 2026 engagement trajectory. Available from state SoS offices by July 2026.
