# Domain 41-B: Disability Rights, Benefit Infrastructure, and Democratic Participation

---

## Part I: Executive Summary

Disability is not a marginal category in American democratic life. The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention estimates that more than 70 million U.S. adults — roughly 28.7 percent of the adult population — live with at least one functional disability. A 2024 Rutgers University analysis projects 40.2 million eligible voters with disabilities, representing one in every six voters who will be eligible to cast a ballot. That is a constituency larger than the combined populations of California and Texas's most competitive congressional districts. Yet disability remains a distinct and persistent participation gap: disabled voters turn out at lower rates than non-disabled voters, face three times the voting difficulties, and are structurally excluded by infrastructure failures that have compounded since 2020.

Three converging crises in 2026 are making the disability participation gap acute:

**Crisis 1: The SAVE America Act's Photo-ID Barrier.** Passed by the House on February 11, 2026, the SAVE America Act requires government-issued photo ID for all forms of voting — in-person, mail, and absentee. Twenty percent of disabled Americans lack a current driver's license, compared to six percent of non-disabled Americans, a disparity documented in a 2023 nationally representative survey by the University of Maryland's Center for Democracy and Civic Engagement (CDCE). The SAVE Act's "reasonable accommodations" language for disabled voters is aspirational rather than enforceable: it specifies no accommodations, no funding mechanisms, and no enforcement timeline. With Senate debate ongoing through March 2026, the bill's implementation timeline intersects directly with the 2026 midterm elections.

**Crisis 2: SSA Benefit Access and Voter Registration Collapse.** The Social Security Administration has shed more than 6,600 employees since January 2025 — an 11 percent reduction — and its internal planning documents target cutting field office visits by 50 percent in fiscal year 2026. Under the National Voter Registration Act of 1993 (NVRA), SSA offices are mandated voter registration sites. For 15.5 million SSDI and SSI recipients — overwhelmingly disabled Americans — SSA field offices are simultaneously the primary benefit access point and one of the only in-person voter registration venues accessible to people who cannot reach or navigate DMV offices. Staffing cuts that extend wait times and reduce in-person capacity produce measurable voter registration collapse among populations that have no substitute registration pathway.

**Crisis 3: ADA Enforcement Collapse at Polling Places.** The Department of Justice Civil Rights Division has lost roughly 70 percent of its attorneys since January 2025, gutting the federal enforcement mechanism for ADA Title II compliance at polling places. A 2017 Government Accountability Office study found that 60 percent of polling places had at least one accessibility violation. In June 2025, Disability Rights Delaware documented that over 54 percent of Delaware polling sites violated ADA parking requirements alone. Disabled voters were nearly six times more likely than non-disabled voters to face difficulties entering polling places in 2020. Without DOJ enforcement, those rates will not improve.

**Core finding**: Disability exclusion from electoral participation is architectural, not accidental. The three crises operate as reinforcing mechanisms — photo-ID barriers reduce the pool of voters who can register; SSA cuts reduce the registration pathway available to those who cannot navigate mainstream channels; ADA enforcement collapse ensures that those who do register encounter physical and procedural barriers at the point of voting. The compound effect is political power reduction for a constituency that comprises one-sixth of the eligible electorate.

**Strategic reform framework**: This domain identifies five structural reform levers: (1) an accessible ID exception amendment to the SAVE Act; (2) restoration of SSA field office capacity with NVRA voter registration integration mandates; (3) an Accessible Elections Act with enforceable ADA Title II polling place requirements and a private right of action; (4) an EAC Office of Accessibility with dedicated enforcement authority; and (5) a permanent Disability Participation Commission with advisory and monitoring functions. The most tractable near-term intervention is the SAVE Act amendment, which requires only that "reasonable accommodations" language be replaced with an enforceable accessible ID process with federal backup.

## Part II: The Disability Participation Gap — Data and Mechanisms

### II.1 Disability Prevalence and Voting Rates

The scale of the disability electorate is frequently underestimated in electoral analysis. The CDC's most recent Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System (BRFSS) data finds that 28.7 percent of U.S. adults report a functional disability — defined across six dimensions: difficulty with mobility, cognition, independent living, hearing, seeing, and self-care. Among eligible voters specifically, the Rutgers University Program for Disability Research (October 2024) projects 40.2 million eligible voters with disabilities, a 5.1 percent increase since 2020. That increase is double the 2.5 percent rise in non-disabled eligible voters, driven by population aging and improved survival rates for people with chronic conditions.

Within the 40.2 million, the Rutgers analysis documents disability type distribution:
- Mobility impairments: approximately 22.1 million eligible voters
- Cognitive impairments: approximately 14.4 million eligible voters
- Hearing impairments: approximately 11.9 million eligible voters
- Visual impairments: approximately 7.2 million eligible voters

Many individuals carry multiple disability types, so these categories are not additive to the 40.2 million total.

Turnout among disabled voters improved substantially between 2016 and 2020: approximately 62 percent of disabled voters participated in the 2020 election, up from 56 percent in 2016. But the gap relative to non-disabled voters persists and is larger than raw participation rates suggest. When the EAC and Rutgers researchers adjust for demographic characteristics — age, education, gender, race, and state — the adjusted disability voting gap rises from roughly 1.5 percentage points to 6.4 percentage points. That adjusted gap represents approximately 2.5 million additional votes that would be cast if disabled voters turned out at the same rate as non-disabled voters with similar demographic characteristics.

Voting mode is a critical dimension of disability participation: in 2020, approximately 51 to 53 percent of disabled voters cast ballots by mail, compared to 42 percent of non-disabled voters. This mail-voting reliance is not preference-based — it reflects accessibility realities. Disabled voters are nearly six times more likely than non-disabled voters to have difficulty entering polling places physically. Mail balloting is, for many disabled voters, the only accessible pathway to participation.

Sources: [CDC: Over 70 Million U.S. Adults Reported Having a Disability (2024)](https://www.cdc.gov/media/releases/2024/s0716-Adult-disability.html); [Rutgers: 40.2 Million Eligible Voters with Disabilities, 5.1% Jump (October 2024)](https://www.smlr.rutgers.edu/news-events/smlr-news/402-million-eligible-voters-disabilities-51-jump); [EAC: New Data — 17.7 Million Americans with Disabilities Voted in 2020](https://www.eac.gov/news/2021/07/07/new-data-177-million-americans-disabilities-voted-2020-significant-increase-over); [Center for American Progress: Enhancing Accessibility in U.S. Elections](https://www.americanprogress.org/article/enhancing-accessibility-u-s-elections/); [U.S. Vote Foundation: 2024 Turnout Gap for Voters with Disabilities](https://www.usvotefoundation.org/closing-gap-voters-disabilities-updated-2024)

### II.2 Polling-Place Barriers: The 2020 Baseline

The most comprehensive post-election data on polling-place accessibility comes from the EAC's Disability and Voting Accessibility in the 2020 Elections report and associated Rutgers analyses. The headline finding: approximately 11 percent of disabled voters reported difficulties voting in the 2020 election, compared to roughly 6 percent of non-disabled voters — a ratio of nearly 2-to-1. That rate represents improvement from 26 percent of disabled voters reporting difficulties in 2012, a reduction attributable partly to the expansion of mail voting during the COVID-19 pandemic and partly to genuine accessibility improvements at in-person sites.

The 2020 data also documents a persistent gap in polling place access specifically: disabled voters voting in-person were nearly six times more likely than non-disabled voters to experience difficulty entering the polling place. In 2022, 14 percent of disabled voters reported some type of difficulty in voting — more than three times the 4 percent rate among non-disabled voters.

The EAC's 2024 report on voting experiences since HAVA (conducted in partnership with Rutgers) documents the structural persistence of these barriers: polling place accessibility improvements have been uneven across jurisdictions, training for poll workers remains inconsistent, and accessible voting equipment at many sites continues to be insufficient in quantity and accessibility features.

A key finding from the 2017 GAO report on polling place accessibility — the most comprehensive physical survey of polling place accessibility available — is that 60 percent of polling places had at least one accessibility impediment. Common barriers included inadequate ramps or drop-off areas, insufficient signage for accessible entrances, inaccessible voting machines or insufficient quantities of accessible machines, and poorly trained poll workers who did not understand their accessibility obligations.

Sources: [EAC: Disability and Voting Accessibility in the 2020 Elections](https://www.eac.gov/sites/default/files/voters/Disability_and_voting_accessibility_in_the_2020_elections_final_report_on_survey_results.pdf); [EAC: Fact Sheet — Disability and Voter Turnout in the 2020 Elections](https://www.eac.gov/sites/default/files/document_library/files/Fact_sheet_on_disability_and_voter_turnout_in_2020_0.pdf); [GAO: Voters with Disabilities — Observations on Polling Place Accessibility (2018)](https://www.gao.gov/assets/gao-18-4.pdf); [MIT Election Lab: Voting Accessibility Research](https://electionlab.mit.edu/research/voting-accessibility); [EAC: U.S. Election Assistance Commission Releases Voting Experiences Since HAVA Report (April 2024)](https://www.eac.gov/news/2024/04/18/us-election-assistance-commission-releases-voting-experiences-hava-perspectives)

### II.3 Types of Barriers — A Typology

Disability participation barriers do not reduce to a single mechanism. Analysis of the research literature identifies six distinct barrier categories that operate independently and in combination:

**Physical access barriers**: Polling places housed in facilities that pre-date ADA standards (many churches, community centers, and school buildings), with insufficient ramps, inaccessible restrooms, no accessible parking, and inadequate drop-off zones. The 2025 Disability Rights Delaware study found 54 percent of polling sites violated ADA parking requirements alone.

**Communication access barriers**: Absence of accessible ballot marking devices; ballots without audio capability or large-print formats; insufficient assistive technology at in-person voting sites; absentee ballot materials not compatible with screen-reader software.

**Cognitive access barriers**: Ballot design that does not follow plain-language standards; polling place procedures not explained in accessible formats; insufficient trained poll worker capacity to provide assistance without compromising ballot secrecy.

**Transportation barriers**: Accessible public transit to polling places is unavailable or unreliable in many jurisdictions; paratransit services do not provide Election Day service in all states; the geographic distribution of accessible polling places is uneven.

**Fatigue and health barriers**: People with chronic illness, energy-limiting conditions (including multiple sclerosis, lupus, long COVID, and cancer), and mental health conditions that create cognitive fatigue cannot always sustain the wait times and procedural complexity of in-person voting.

**Institutional access barriers**: Group homes, nursing facilities, psychiatric institutions, and other congregate care settings may not provide adequate voter registration assistance, may restrict access to outside assistance, or may have historically been excluded from voter registration drives.

Sources: [Campaign Legal Center: Barriers to Voting Faced by Americans with Disabilities](https://campaignlegal.org/update/barriers-voting-faced-americans-disabilities); [Disability Rights Delaware: ADA Parking Violations at Polling Places (2025)](https://www.declasi.org/news-and-stories/press-releases/disability-rights-delaware-report-finds-widespread-ada-parking-accessibility-violations-at-polling-places/); [NDRN: Blocking the Ballot Box — ADA Misuse to Close Polling Places](https://www.ndrn.org/resource/blocking-the-ballot-box/)

### II.4 The SAVE Act's Photo-ID Provision as a 2026 Acute Crisis

The SAVE America Act — passed by the House on February 11, 2026, by a vote of 218-213 — creates a three-layer identification requirement affecting all forms of voting:

1. Documentary proof of citizenship (birth certificate, passport, or naturalization certificate) for voter registration
2. Government-issued photo ID for in-person voting
3. Copies of photo ID attached to mail-in and absentee ballots

The disability community's specific vulnerability is the photo-ID requirement for both in-person and mail voting. The most comprehensive national survey of voter ID access — conducted by the CDCE at the University of Maryland, with the Brennan Center, VoteRiders, and Public Wise in 2023 — documents the following:

- **20 percent of people who self-identify as having a disability do not have a current driver's license**, compared to 6 percent of non-disabled people.
- An additional **9 percent of disabled people have a license that does not reflect their current name or address**, meaning it would not satisfy the SAVE Act's matching requirements.
- In total, **29 percent of disabled voters** have either no current driver's license or one that would not satisfy the SAVE Act's requirements, compared to 19 percent of non-disabled voters — a 10-percentage-point gap.

The reasons for this gap are not administrative but medical and structural: visual impairments and blindness physically prevent driving; seizure disorders result in license suspension under state motor vehicle laws; cognitive disabilities and mental health conditions create barriers to the DMV documentation and testing process; and mobility impairments can make independent DMV access impossible.

The SAVE Act's "reasonable accommodations" language for disabled voters is aspirational rather than operational. The bill as passed by the House states that states "should" offer accommodations for voters with disabilities but specifies no particular accommodations, provides no federal funding for states to develop accessible ID programs, gives the Election Assistance Commission only ten days to issue implementation guidance, and creates no private right of action for disabled voters who are denied an accessible ID alternative.

The practical consequence of the SAVE Act's implementation timeline is significant: if the bill passes the Senate and is signed into law in 2026, states would be required to implement ID requirements for the 2026 midterm elections with ten days of EAC guidance and no funding for accessible alternatives. For the 29 percent of disabled voters without compliant ID, the 2026 midterms would effectively be their first federal election subject to a de facto photo-ID requirement with no accessible substitute.

Advocacy response has been unified and cross-organizational: the American Association of People with Disabilities (AAPD), The Arc, the National Disability Rights Network (NDRN), the National Council on Accessible Voting (NCAV), and Disability Belongs have all formally opposed the SAVE Act and specifically identified the photo-ID requirement as a disability disenfranchisement mechanism.

Sources: [CDCE/UMD: Who Lacks ID in America Today — Voter ID Survey Key Results (2024)](https://cdce.umd.edu/sites/cdce.umd.edu/files/pubs/Voter%20ID%202023%20survey%20Key%20Results%20June%202024%20updatev2.pdf); [Democracy Docket: Voting is Still Not Accessible for the Disability Community — SAVE America Act Would Magnify Suppression](https://www.democracydocket.com/analysis/voting-disability-community-access-gop-save-america-act-suppression/); [AAPD: Response to House Passage of the SAVE America Act](https://www.aapd.com/american-association-of-people-with-disabilities-response-to-house-passage-of-the-save-america-act/); [The Arc: SAVE America Act Threatens Accessible Voting](https://thearc.org/blog/save-america-act-voting-rights-disabilities/); [NCAV: Disability Rights Groups Warn Congress — SAVE Act Bills Would Block Millions from Voting](https://ncavote.org/resource/nosaveact/); [ACLU: Accessible Voting is Under Attack 35 Years After the ADA (July 2025)](https://www.aclu.org/news/disability-rights/accessible-voting-is-under-attack-35-years-after-the-ada)

## Part III: SSA Benefit Access as Voter Registration Infrastructure

### III.1 NVRA Mandate and SSA's Voter Registration Function

The National Voter Registration Act of 1993 (NVRA) created a federal mandate that public agencies offering certain government services must also offer voter registration to clients. Section 7 of the NVRA specifically requires states to designate as voter registration agencies any office that provides public assistance or "administers State-funded programs primarily engaged in providing services to persons with disabilities." Social Security Administration field offices — where disabled Americans go to apply for SSDI and SSI benefits, update their records, and resolve benefit questions — are the quintessential Section 7 agencies.

The NVRA's practical implication is that SSA field offices serve a dual function: they are simultaneously benefit access points and voter registration venues. For the 15.5 million Americans receiving SSDI or SSI — a population that is overwhelmingly disabled, disproportionately low-income, and significantly less likely to have access to DMV offices or online voter registration systems — SSA field offices are often the only in-person voter registration venue that is accessible and located within reach.

The EAC tracks NVRA voter registrations by agency type in its biennial survey. While SSA-specific numbers are not published separately from the broader "public assistance agencies" category, the overall data demonstrates that public assistance agencies collectively process millions of voter registrations annually. Historical analyses by Demos found that between 2007 and 2014, cooperative work with state officials resulted in more than 1.8 million voter registration applications through public assistance agencies nationally.

Sources: [DOJ Civil Rights Division: The National Voter Registration Act of 1993](https://www.justice.gov/crt/national-voter-registration-act-1993-nvra); [EAC: National Voter Registration Act Studies](https://www.eac.gov/voters/national-voter-registration-act-studies); [Demos: Registering Millions — The Success and Potential of the NVRA at 20](https://www.demos.org/research/registering-millions-success-and-potential-national-voter-registration-act-20); [CRS: Voter Registration Agencies Under the National Voter Registration Act of 1993](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IN11782)

### III.2 The SSA Staffing Crisis — Scale and Timeline

Between January 2025 and November 2025, the SSA shed 6,645 employees — a reduction of more than 11 percent from fiscal year 2024 levels. In a February 28, 2025 statement, the agency set a target of cutting its workforce from approximately 57,000 employees to 50,000, a 12 percent reduction. In 33 states, SSA had at least 10 percent fewer staff by fiscal year 2025 than it did in fiscal year 2024.

The staffing cuts were compounded by the Trump administration's return-to-office mandate, which caused additional departures: SSA's telework hours fell from 35 percent to 13 percent of total work hours between January and May 2025. A GAO report released in early 2026 warned that without telework flexibility, SSA faces further staffing losses that would worsen service delays.

The operational consequence: SSA's internal planning documents target 50 percent fewer field office visitors in fiscal year 2026 compared to fiscal year 2025 — a drop from more than 31.6 million visits annually to a target of no more than 15 million. The agency's strategy for absorbing this reduction relies on driving people to phone and online services, but those channels are inaccessible or inadequate for significant portions of the disabled, elderly, and low-income population that SSA serves.

The Center on Budget and Policy Priorities describes these as "the largest-ever Social Security staffing cuts," noting that the combination of reduced staffing and reduced field office capacity is producing benefit processing delays, longer wait times for disability determinations, and reduced access to in-person services for those who need them most.

Sources: [Center on Budget and Policy Priorities: Reassignment Won't Fix the Largest-Ever Social Security Staffing Cut](https://www.cbpp.org/research/social-security/reassignment-wont-fix-the-largest-ever-social-security-staffing-cut); [Federal News Network: SSA Plans to Cut Field Office Visits by 50% (December 2025)](https://federalnewsnetwork.com/workforce/2025/12/the-social-security-administration-plans-to-cut-field-office-visits-by-50-what-it-means-for-you/); [GAO: Without Telework, SSA Faces Further Staffing Losses (February 2026)](https://federalnewsnetwork.com/workforce/2026/02/without-telework-gao-warns-of-further-ssa-staffing-losses/); [NPR: SSA Workforce Cuts — Employee Cuts Leave Remaining Workers Struggling (April 2025)](https://www.npr.org/2025/04/26/nx-s1-5368480/social-security-workforce-cuts); [Urban Institute: Social Security Office Closures Will Hurt Rural and Tribal Communities](https://www.urban.org/urban-wire/social-security-office-closures-will-hurt-rural-and-tribal-communities)

### III.3 The Intersection: Benefit Access and Voter Registration

The NVRA voter registration function at SSA offices does not operate in isolation from benefit administration. A person who visits an SSA field office to initiate a disability claim, update their address, or resolve a benefit problem must be offered the opportunity to register to vote — or update their voter registration — during that visit. When staffing cuts extend wait times dramatically (the average days to complete an initial disability claim was 231 days in fiscal year 2024, before the most severe cuts), two things happen simultaneously to voter registration:

First, many people abandon in-person SSA visits rather than wait in extended queues — reducing the number of NVRA voter registration transactions that occur. Second, those who do wait and complete their SSA transaction are processed by workers who are stretched thin, reducing the likelihood that NVRA voter registration is offered systematically rather than skipped under time pressure.

The rural and internet-access dimension compounds this. People living in rural areas are disproportionately dependent on SSA field offices because they lack broadband internet access, do not have nearby DMV offices, and face transportation barriers to reaching alternative registration venues. When rural field offices close — as some have already begun to do under lease-termination agreements — that access disappears entirely.

The population affected is not diffuse. The approximately 7.37 million SSI recipients and 8.14 million SSDI recipients as of early 2026 are overwhelmingly disabled Americans who, by definition, have limited mobility and resources. The SSA field office is, for many, the most accessible government institution in their lives. Reducing that institution's capacity does not just impede benefit access — it removes one of the only voter registration pathways available to a population that is already underregistered.

Sources: [SSA: Annual Statistical Report on the Social Security Disability Insurance Program, 2024](https://www.ssa.gov/policy/docs/statcomps/di_asr/); [SSA: December 2025 Fact Sheet on Social Security](https://www.ssa.gov/OACT/FACTS/); [Medicare Rights Center: Trump Administration and DOGE Closing Social Security Offices (March 2025)](https://www.medicarerights.org/medicare-watch/2025/03/13/trump-administration-and-elon-musks-doge-closing-social-security-offices-harming-access-to-services); [Government Executive: SSA Reorg Plan Contemplates Field Office Closures (April 2025)](https://www.govexec.com/management/2025/04/ssa-reorg-plan-contemplates-field-office-closures-contradicting-public-statements/404369/)

### III.4 ADA and HAVA Compliance Gaps in SSA Voter Registration

The Help America Vote Act of 2002 (HAVA) updated federal voting system accessibility standards but did not update the accessibility requirements for NVRA voter registration at public assistance agencies. The SSA's voter registration procedures — the forms used, the technology employed, the assistance provided — operate under accessibility standards that have not been comprehensively reviewed in over two decades. As SSA moves to drive more services online in response to staffing cuts, those online systems must comply with current web accessibility standards (WCAG 2.1 AA) for NVRA voter registration offers to be genuinely accessible to blind, low-vision, and cognitively disabled users.

Sources: [DOJ Civil Rights Division: The Help America Vote Act of 2002](https://www.justice.gov/crt/help-america-vote-act-2002); [ADA.gov: The Americans with Disabilities Act and Other Federal Laws Protecting the Rights of Voters with Disabilities](https://www.ada.gov/resources/protecting-voter-rights/)

## Part IV: ADA Enforcement Collapse and Polling-Place Accessibility

### IV.1 The Legal Framework — What the ADA Requires

The Americans with Disabilities Act's Title II prohibits discrimination by state and local government entities. For elections, this means that election authorities — state and county boards of election, secretaries of state, and any public entity responsible for election administration — must make polling places, voting systems, and election administration processes accessible to people with disabilities. The implementing regulations at 28 C.F.R. Section 35.151 set standards for new construction and alterations; the DOJ has interpreted the reasonable modification requirement to apply to existing facilities used as polling places.

The ADA accessibility framework for elections requires:
- Physical accessibility to polling places (accessible parking, ramps, routes from parking to voting area, accessible voting booths)
- Accessible voting systems — machines with audio output, sip-and-puff interfaces, and other assistive features
- Poll worker training on accessibility obligations
- Reasonable modifications to policies and procedures to accommodate disabled voters
- Alternative means of voting for facilities that cannot be made accessible

HAVA reinforced the ADA accessibility framework by requiring at least one accessible voting system at every polling place, providing grant funding through the EAC for accessibility improvements, and establishing the EAC's role in accessibility guidance.

Sources: [ADA.gov: Voting and Polling Places](https://www.ada.gov/topics/voting/); [ADA.gov: The Americans with Disabilities Act and Other Federal Laws Protecting the Rights of Voters with Disabilities](https://www.ada.gov/resources/protecting-voter-rights/); [EAC: Voting Accessibility](https://www.eac.gov/voting-accessibility); [Help America Vote Act Overview — EAC](https://www.eac.gov/about/help_america_vote_act.aspx)

### IV.2 Enforcement Mechanisms

Federal ADA enforcement for polling place accessibility operates through three channels:

**DOJ enforcement**: The Civil Rights Division's Disability Rights Section and Voting Section can investigate complaints, issue letters of findings, negotiate settlement agreements requiring remediation, and file federal lawsuits against non-compliant jurisdictions. Pre-2025, DOJ was the primary federal enforcement actor: in June 2023, DOJ sued Los Angeles County for ADA violations at vote centers; in June 2024, DOJ issued findings against Alaska's Division of Elections for multiple ADA Title II violations; in December 2024, DOJ secured accessibility agreements with Hudson and Morris County Boards of Election in New Jersey.

**Private civil rights actions**: The ADA allows private individuals to sue for injunctive relief (and attorneys' fees in some circuits) when election authorities violate Title II accessibility requirements. Organizations like Disability Rights Delaware, the National Disability Rights Network affiliates, and national civil rights organizations have used private litigation as a complement to DOJ enforcement.

**State attorney general enforcement**: Several state AGs with active civil rights divisions have pursued polling place accessibility cases. This channel is most functional in states with Democratic AGs and explicit civil rights enforcement mandates.

### IV.3 The Current Enforcement Collapse

The Trump administration's restructuring of the DOJ Civil Rights Division has effectively gutted federal ADA voting enforcement. Approximately 250 attorneys — roughly 70 percent of Civil Rights Division lawyers — have departed since January 2025. The Voting Section, which was responsible for ADA voting enforcement among other duties, now operates with a fraction of its previous capacity. The Election Disputes Task Force, which coordinated voting rights enforcement, was eliminated. Voting access complaints previously routed to the Voting Section now encounter a depleted staff with realigned priorities.

The practical effect: new DOJ enforcement actions on disability voting rights have effectively stopped since early 2025. Settlement agreement monitoring for previously negotiated agreements continues in some jurisdictions, but no new comprehensive investigations have been publicly announced. Complaints submitted to DOJ are unacknowledged or redirected.

The enforcement gap is documented across the field:

- **Delaware (June 2025)**: Disability Rights Delaware monitoring of over 90 percent of Delaware polling places found 54 percent violated ADA parking requirements. No federal enforcement response was initiated.
- **Alabama (2024-2025)**: The ACLU documented polling sites with broken ramps, unopenable doors, hazardous conditions, and inadequate lighting in multiple Alabama counties. The 2024 Election Protection Coalition's documentation was filed with DOJ without response.
- **Wisconsin (ongoing)**: A lawsuit challenging the Wisconsin Elections Commission's failure to implement electronically delivered absentee ballots for disabled voters — a direct accessibility accommodation — was reversed by the Wisconsin Court of Appeals in March 2025, with trial not scheduled until July 2026.

The structural consequence is that the private right of action and state AG enforcement channels — which were designed as supplementary mechanisms to DOJ enforcement — are now the primary accountability pathways. Those channels are slower, more expensive, and less comprehensive than federal enforcement, and they are insufficient to address the systemic polling place accessibility failures documented nationwide.

Sources: [DOJ: Justice Department Secures Agreement with LA County on Voter Accessibility (2023)](https://www.justice.gov/archives/opa/pr/justice-department-secures-landmark-agreement-los-angeles-county-increase-accessibility); [DOJ: US Attorney Secures Agreement with Hudson and Morris County Boards of Elections (December 2024)](https://www.justice.gov/usao-nj/pr/us-attorneys-office-secures-agreement-hudson-and-morris-county-boards-elections-ensure); [Just Security: Trump Administration's Dismissal of Voting Rights Lawsuits](https://www.justsecurity.org/113745/wjh-trump-dismissal-voting-rights-lawsuits/); [AAI USA: The Destruction of the DOJ Civil Rights Division Will Damage Voting Rights](https://www.aaiusa.org/library/the-destruction-of-the-doj-civil-rights-division-will-damage-voting-rights); [Democracy Docket: Wisconsin Electronic Absentee Ballot System Accessibility Challenge](https://www.democracydocket.com/cases/wisconsin-electronic-absentee-ballot-system-accessibility-challenge/); [Disability Rights Delaware: Widespread ADA Parking Violations at Polling Places](https://www.declasi.org/news-and-stories/press-releases/disability-rights-delaware-report-finds-widespread-ada-parking-accessibility-violations-at-polling-places/)

### IV.4 Why This Matters for Democratic Legitimacy

An election whose voting process excludes a structurally identifiable constituency does not produce a representative outcome. When the 40.2 million eligible voters with disabilities are systematically less likely to access polling places due to physical barriers, less likely to receive voter registration offers due to SSA staffing cuts, and less likely to qualify to vote under photo-ID requirements, the electoral results undercount their policy preferences in proportion to the exclusion.

The gap is not noise — it is signal. For the largest democratic block that cuts across race, class, geography, and partisan affiliation, systematic exclusion produces systematic outcome distortion. Disability turnout improvement from 56 percent in 2016 to 62 percent in 2020 — driven largely by expanded mail voting — produced measurable changes in competitive state outcomes. Reversing that improvement through mail-voting ID requirements, SSA office closures, and ADA enforcement collapse would do the same in reverse.

## Part V: Comparative Framework — How Other Democracies Address Disability Participation

### V.1 Canada

Canada's approach to disability voting accessibility is grounded in the Accessible Canada Act (2019), which commits Canada to becoming barrier-free by 2040, and the Canada Elections Act, which provides multiple accessibility mechanisms. Elections Canada deploys more than 2,000 community relations officers during general elections, including officers specifically focused on accessibility. The system allows companion assistance from any chosen person, provides audio ballot options, and permits voting at any polling station within a federal electoral district when the assigned station is inaccessible.

A critical structural difference from the United States is that Canada imposes no statutory exclusion from voting based on cognitive disability. Among the very few countries with an explicit "any cognitive disability disqualifies voting" law, Canada is explicitly not one — creating a more inclusive framework by default.

The Accessible Canada Act creates a structured process for identifying, removing, and preventing barriers across federally regulated domains, including elections. While specific disability voter turnout comparison data between Canada and the U.S. is limited, the Elections Canada framework provides a model of proactive barrier-removal rather than reactive enforcement.

Sources: [Elections Canada: Accessibility Policy and Service Offering](https://www.elections.ca/content.aspx?section=vot&dir=spe/policy&document=index&lang=e); [Elections Canada: Electoral Participation of Electors with Disabilities — Canadian Practices in a Comparative Context](https://elections.ca/content.aspx?dir=rec/part/spe&document=intro&lang=e&section=res); [Elections Canada: Electoral Insight — Persons with Disabilities and Elections](https://www.elections.ca/content.aspx?section=res&dir=eim/issue10&document=p2&lang=e)

### V.2 United Kingdom

The UK's Elections Act 2022 made significant improvements to polling place accessibility. Two reforms are most relevant to the U.S. context:

**Companion assistance broadened**: Pre-2022, only a close family member or someone entitled to vote in the same election could physically assist a disabled voter in a polling station. The 2022 Act removed both restrictions — anyone aged 18 or over can now serve as a companion, including caregivers, social workers, and support staff. This change directly addresses the barrier faced by voters in group homes and care facilities, who were previously limited to family members as assistants.

**Equipment requirement modernized**: The previous requirement to provide a tactile voting device was replaced with a flexible requirement: returning officers must provide "such equipment as is reasonable to enable, or make it easier, for voters with disabilities (including, but not limited to, sight loss) to vote." This needs-based approach avoids the technology-lock-in of the previous prescriptive requirement.

Research on UK elections documents a disability voting gap of approximately 6.2 percentage points across general elections from 2010 to 2019, with larger gaps for those with more severe disabilities, mental health disabilities, and chronic conditions. A 2014 survey found that 17 percent of voters with learning disabilities were turned away at polling stations based on their disability — a rate that UK accessibility reforms have sought to reduce.

Sources: [House of Commons Library: Disabled People — Political Participation (Research Briefing CBP-9718)](https://commonslibrary.parliament.uk/research-briefings/cbp-9718/); [UK Electoral Commission: Ensuring That Voting is Accessible](https://www.electoralcommission.org.uk/guidance-returning-officers-assistance-voting-disabled-voters/ensuring-voting-accessible); [ScienceDirect: Understanding the Disability Voting Gap in the UK](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379423000963); [UK Legislation: The Assistance with Voting for Persons with Disabilities Rules 2022](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2022/1284/rule/2/made)

### V.3 Australia

Australia's electoral system offers an instructive mixed picture. On one hand, Australia is often described as the most voter-friendly country in the world for physical accessibility — compulsory voting creates institutional pressure to provide accessible voting options, and the Australian Electoral Commission offers multiple alternative voting mechanisms including telephone voting for blind and low-vision voters, postal voting, and early voting. On the other hand, Australia retains legal provisions that exclude some people with cognitive disabilities from voting — provisions criticized as inconsistent with the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD), which both Australia and the U.S. have signed but the U.S. has not ratified.

The Australian Disability Discrimination Act 1992 prohibits discrimination in access to services, including government programs and public facilities. Electoral access is explicitly covered. However, researchers and disability advocates have documented that physical accessibility at Australian polling places remains uneven, and the legal framework for challenging non-compliance relies on complaint processes that are less swift than U.S. private civil rights actions.

Sources: [Australian Human Rights Commission: Disability Rights — Electoral Access](https://humanrights.gov.au/know-your-rights/rights-of-individuals/disability-rights/disability-rights/disability-rights-and-the-disability-discrimination-act/disability-rights-electoral-access); [Sage Journals: Australia, the Most Voter-Friendly Country in the World? Examining Voting Rights for People with Physical Disabilities](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/1037969X231190481); [People with Disability Australia: Reform Laws that Stop People with Disability from Voting](https://pwd.org.au/reform-laws-that-stop-people-with-disability-from-voting/)

### V.4 Germany

Germany's Bundestagswahl (federal election) framework incorporates disability accessibility as a matter of federal election law rather than anti-discrimination compliance. Key mechanisms:

**Universal companion assistance**: Any voter who cannot mark, fold, or deposit a ballot independently due to a physical or reading impairment may bring a companion of their own choosing — including a member of the electoral board. The companion is legally required to follow the voter's instructions and maintain ballot secrecy.

**Accessible polling station alternatives**: If a voter's designated polling station is physically inaccessible, voters may apply for a polling card allowing them to vote at any accessible station in their constituency, ensuring geographic flexibility.

**Blind and visual impairment accommodation**: Ballot paper templates for blind and visually impaired voters, provided free by German disability associations, allow independent ballot marking without assistance. These templates are available for both Bundestag and European Parliament elections.

**Cognitive accessibility**: German election law requires that information about electoral campaigns and candidates be provided in accessible formats including easy-to-read language for voters with cognitive disabilities.

Germany's framework is grounded in the UN CRPD, which Germany ratified in 2009. Political participation is treated as a fundamental right under German constitutional law, with accessibility as a legal requirement rather than a discretionary accommodation.

Sources: [Federal Returning Officer: Accessibility of Voting (2021 Bundestag Election)](https://www.bundeswahlleiterin.de/en/bundestagswahlen/2021/informationen-waehler/barrierefreies-waehlen.html); [Eurac Research: Political Participation as a Fundamental Right — Persons with Disabilities in Germany and EU Elections](https://www.eurac.edu/en/blogs/eureka/political-participation-as-a-fundamental-right-persons-with-disabilities-in-germany-and-the-eu-elections)

## Part VI: Structural Reform Architecture

### VI.1 Tier 1 Reforms — Immediate Legislative and Administrative Fixes

**Reform 1: SAVE Act Accessible ID Exception Amendment**

The most tractable near-term disability-specific reform to the SAVE Act is an amendment replacing the aspirational "reasonable accommodations should" language with enforceable provisions:

- States must offer an accessible, no-cost state ID or alternative documentation process for voters with documented disabilities who cannot obtain a driver's license (this mirrors existing accessible ID programs in several states including New York and California)
- For voters who cannot access in-person ID facilities, a mail-based or home-visit accessible ID alternative must be available
- If a state fails to implement an accessible ID process within 90 days of the SAVE Act's effective date, the federal Election Assistance Commission must designate a federal alternative credential process
- A private right of action for disabled voters who are denied an accessible ID alternative, with attorneys' fees

This amendment is politically tractable because: (1) it preserves the SAVE Act's core citizenship verification mechanism while addressing the specific disability exclusion; (2) it is consistent with the ADA's reasonable modification framework that election officials are already familiar with; and (3) it draws on existing state-level accessible ID programs as proven models.

**Reform 2: SSA Voter Registration Restoration and Mandate**

An appropriations rider or standalone legislation should require:

- Restoration of SSA field office staffing to fiscal year 2024 levels (restoring the approximately 6,600 lost positions) over a two-year period
- A mandate that NVRA voter registration offers at SSA be integrated into every transaction — online and in-person — with documented tracking and EAC reporting
- Accessible digital voter registration at all SSA online portals, meeting WCAG 2.1 AA standards at minimum
- A rural field office minimum: no rural county with a population of 25,000 or more should lose SSA in-person services without an accessible alternative
- An SSA-NVRA compliance audit, conducted by the GAO, documenting the impact of the 2025 staffing cuts on voter registration rates in SSA-dependent populations

**Reform 3: Accessible Elections Act — Enforceable ADA Title II Standards**

The Help America Vote Act of 2002 provided funding and guidance for polling place accessibility but lacked both enforceable standards and adequate enforcement mechanisms. An updated Accessible Elections Act should include:

- Mandatory accessibility audits of all polling places before each federal election, with results publicly reported to the EAC
- Enforceable minimum standards for accessible parking, ramp access, accessible voting equipment (with a minimum ratio of accessible machines per registered disabled voter), and poll worker training
- A private right of action for disabled voters and disability organizations to enforce these standards in federal court, with expedited injunctive relief procedures before elections
- A duty on state AGs to investigate polling place accessibility complaints and report findings to the EAC
- Federal funding for polling place accessibility improvements, with matching requirements that prevent states from substituting federal funds for existing accessibility spending

**Reform 4: EAC Office of Accessibility with Enforcement Authority**

The Accessible Voting Act of 2024 (S. 3748, 118th Congress) proposed establishing an EAC Office of Accessibility — a step in the right direction but with an authorization level ($500,000 per year) that would fund approximately two or three positions. A serious federal voting accessibility enforcement office requires:

- Annual appropriation of at least $25-50 million to staff an enforcement function, not merely an advisory one
- Authority to investigate polling place accessibility complaints from disabled voters and disability organizations
- Power to issue findings and referrals to DOJ for enforcement actions
- Jurisdiction over NVRA voter registration accessibility at public assistance agencies, including SSA
- Annual public report to Congress on disability voting access, polling place compliance, and NVRA registration rates in disability-dependent populations

Sources: [Congress.gov: Accessible Voting Act of 2024 (S. 3748)](https://www.congress.gov/bill/118th-congress/senate-bill/3748/text); [EAC: EAC Empowers Voters with Disabilities](https://www.eac.gov/voters/eac-empowers-voters-disabilities); [National Council on Disability: Voting Fact Sheet](https://www.ncd.gov/report/voting-fact-sheet/); [Center for American Progress: Enhancing Accessibility in U.S. Elections](https://www.americanprogress.org/article/enhancing-accessibility-u-s-elections/)

### VI.2 Tier 2 Reforms — Longer-Term Institutional Changes

**Reform 5: Disability Participation Commission**

A permanent advisory commission on disability and democratic participation — analogous to the U.S. Commission on Civil Rights but focused specifically on the intersection of disability, benefit administration, and electoral access — would provide:

- Annual monitoring of disability voting rates, polling place accessibility compliance, and SSA NVRA voter registration rates
- Formal advisory authority to EAC, DOJ, and SSA on accessibility requirements
- Public-facing reporting that makes disability voting access data available at the county and precinct level
- A mandate to study and report on emerging accessibility challenges — including accessible voting technology, digital voter registration accessibility, and the intersection of disability with redistricting

This structure is justified by the institutional complexity of the problem: disability voting access requires coordination across at least four federal agencies (EAC, DOJ, SSA, and HHS), multiple layers of state and local government, and ongoing technology evolution that no single agency currently monitors.

**Reform 6: Universal Accessible Voting System Standards**

The Federal Election Commission and EAC should jointly mandate that all voting systems purchased with federal assistance (HAVA funds) meet WCAG 2.1 AA accessibility standards for all disability types — not just the current accessible machine minimum of "one accessible machine per polling place." Specific requirements should include:

- Audio ballot capability for every voting machine in every polling place
- Screen magnification and adjustable display settings standard on all voter-facing technology
- Accessible absentee ballot systems with electronic delivery and return options for voters with certified print disabilities
- Accessible online voter registration that meets the same WCAG 2.1 AA standards

**Reform 7: Disability-Responsive Redistricting**

State redistricting commissions, when drawing congressional and state legislative maps after the 2030 Census, should be required to:

- Model the impact of proposed maps on polling place accessibility for disabled voters at the precinct level, using Census disability prevalence data by census tract
- Ensure that precincts are not drawn in ways that concentrate disabled voters in geographic areas with disproportionately inaccessible polling places
- Report on precinct-level disability accessibility in final redistricting plans

### VI.3 Implementation Timeline and Policy Window

The 2026-2028 window offers the clearest leverage points:

- **2026 (immediate)**: SAVE Act amendment negotiations in the Senate represent the first opportunity for an enforceable accessible ID exception. As documented in Domain 1, the Senate SAVE Act has already failed 48-50 due to Republican defectors (Collins, Murkowski, Tillis, McConnell). The disability exception amendment provides a substantive policy hook for continued resistance.
- **2027-2028**: If Democratic majorities emerge from 2026 midterms, SSA staffing restoration (appropriations rider) and an Accessible Elections Act (standalone legislation) have a plausible legislative path.
- **2027-2030**: EAC Office of Accessibility establishment; voting system accessibility standards finalization and deployment.
- **2030+**: Post-Census redistricting incorporates disability-responsive precinct analysis; next generation of accessible voting systems deployed under new federal standards.

## Part VII: Strategic Relevance to Democratic Renewal

### VII.1 Cross-Domain Integration

Disability voting rights is not a single-issue domain. It intersects with and reinforces multiple other domains in this research network:

**Domain 1 (Voting Rights and Elections)**: The SAVE Act's disability impact is one of the three primary equity objections to the bill. The four Senate Republican defectors who blocked the SAVE Act in the April 2026 vote-a-rama represent a coalition-fracture opportunity documented in Domain 1. Disability advocacy organizations are part of the broader coalition that should be mobilized to preserve that defection bloc.

**Domain 31 (Healthcare Access — OBBBA/Medicaid Crisis)**: SSDI and SSI recipients are Medicaid-eligible in most states. The Medicaid cuts documented in Domain 31 directly affect disabled Americans' healthcare access. The intersecting harms of Medicaid reduction and SSA staffing cuts create a compound vulnerability for disabled Americans that is greater than either crisis alone. Disability advocacy organizations who are fighting SSA cuts are natural allies for Medicaid defense.

**Domain 26 (Government Accountability and Institutional Resilience)**: The DOGE-driven SSA staffing cuts are the mechanism through which voter registration capacity at SSA offices is collapsing. The institutional resilience question — which federal agencies retain capacity to fulfill their NVRA obligations — is directly applicable to SSA. A GAO investigation of SSA's NVRA compliance since the staffing cuts would provide the documentation needed to challenge the cuts on voter access grounds.

**Domain 29 (DOJ Capture)**: The ADA enforcement collapse at polling places is a direct consequence of the DOJ Civil Rights Division restructuring documented in Domain 29. The same institutional capture that has paralyzed VRA enforcement has paralyzed ADA voting enforcement. The strategic response — private litigation and state AG enforcement as substitutes for DOJ — is the same across both domains.

### VII.2 Coalition and Implementation Feasibility

The disability voting access coalition has distinctive characteristics that make it strategically valuable:

**Cross-partisan reach**: Disability affects 28 percent of U.S. adults across all partisan affiliations, income levels, and geographies. Republican voters with disabilities are affected by SAVE Act photo-ID requirements. Republican elderly voters in rural areas are affected by SSA office closures. Cross-partisan disability advocacy creates political cover for Republican legislators to oppose specific provisions without opposing the broader administration agenda.

**Organizational infrastructure**: The American Association of People with Disabilities, The Arc, the National Disability Rights Network, Disability Rights Education and Defense Fund, and Disability Belongs all have established lobbying and advocacy infrastructure. These organizations are already mobilized against the SAVE Act and are sophisticated about both legislative and litigation strategies.

**Electoral salience**: The 40.2 million eligible voters with disabilities represent approximately one-sixth of the total eligible electorate. Even marginal improvements in disability turnout are electorally significant in close races. The 2020 expansion of mail voting produced measurable disability turnout increases; reversing those gains through SAVE Act photo-ID requirements for mail ballots is a quantifiable electoral harm.

**Institutional allies**: Election administration professionals — secretaries of state, county clerks, election directors — have institutional interests in clear, workable accessibility standards. The SAVE Act's vague "reasonable accommodations" language creates implementation uncertainty for election officials who want to comply. A well-designed accessible ID exception amendment serves their administrative interests, creating a potential cross-partisan technical constituency for the reform.

The SAVE Act accessible ID amendment is the fastest-path reform: it is targeted, technically specified, draws on existing state models, and requires only a Senate floor amendment to a bill that is already in negotiation. The SSA staffing restoration is an appropriations question that can be addressed through budget negotiations. The Accessible Elections Act requires a legislative vehicle but has organizational support across the disability coalition. None of these reforms require winning a presidential election or a Senate majority — they require sustained advocacy in the 2026-2028 window across the specific leverage points documented here.
