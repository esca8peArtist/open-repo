## Executive Summary

The Trump administration has deployed five interlocking mechanisms to interfere with the November 2026 midterm elections, each operating simultaneously across legal, administrative, and psychological dimensions. The strategy does not require any single mechanism to succeed outright. Partial success across several vectors — voter data weaponization, mail ballot disruption, DOJ institutional capture, ICE psychological intimidation, and deliberate disinformation from administration allies — is sufficient to suppress turnout in targeted communities, destabilize election administration, and position post-election challenges.

The five mechanisms are: (1) a national voter surveillance and purge operation built on a structurally flawed citizenship database being forced on states through DOJ litigation — now confirmed to include sharing state voter files directly with DHS in violation of state privacy protections; (2) a March 31, 2026 executive order directing the Postal Service to restrict mail ballot delivery and DHS to construct a parallel citizenship voter list — both constitutionally unauthorized by presidential power and facing coordinated legal challenge from 23 state attorneys general; (3) the wholesale conversion of the DOJ Civil Rights Division from a voting rights enforcement body into a voting rights suppression instrument, including a 75 percent career staff exodus, a nationally designated cross-district election prosecutor building the infrastructure for election-night interference, and the concurrent dismantlement of CISA and the Election Assistance Commission as functioning election security entities; (4) an active strategy of deploying ICE near polling places, framed by Steve Bannon as "perfect training" for November 2026, backed by a DHS position that does not foreclose election-day deployment; and (5) strategic suppression signals from Bannon and allied communicators that create ambient voter intimidation independent of any physical enforcement action.

The constitutional framework is clear: the president has no authority over election administration under the Elections Clause (Article I, Section 4), which assigns that power to states and Congress. Federal courts have dismissed DOJ's voter roll lawsuits in California, Massachusetts, Michigan, Oregon, and Rhode Island. Judge Chun permanently blocked three core provisions of the predecessor elections executive order. The legal resistance is organized, resourced, and winning in court.

But winning in court is not the same as protecting the election. The six months remaining before November 2026 are the operational window. The NVRA's 90-day quiet period — which prohibits systematic voter purges after August 7, 2026 — defines the most critical near-term legal deadline. The SAVE database's documented 81 percent error rate in Missouri creates a concrete wrongful-removal threat at scale. The destruction of CISA's election security infrastructure and the FY27 proposal to eliminate its election security program entirely removes the federal backstop against cybersecurity threats to voting systems. And the Fulton County precedent — FBI seizure of 700 boxes of 2020 election materials using a cross-district warrant signed by a Missouri prosecutor — shows that the administration is building the infrastructure for election-night and post-election-night interference regardless of pre-election court losses.

The international evidence, moreover, is encouraging in one critical respect: Hungary's April 2026 parliamentary election — conducted under the most sophisticated electoral manipulation infrastructure Europe had seen outside Russia — ended with a 53.6 percent opposition landslide and the end of 16 years of Orbán's rule. The lesson is not that structural manipulation is harmless. The lesson is that democratic mobilization, elevated turnout, and coordinated civil society resistance can overcome significant structural disadvantage when the economic and political conditions motivate sufficient participation.

## II. Four Core Mechanisms of Federal Interference

### II.A The DOJ Voter Roll Litigation and National Surveillance Database

**The architecture.** Beginning in late 2025, the DOJ demanded that all 50 states and Washington, D.C. provide unredacted voter files — full names, addresses, dates of birth, driver's license and partial Social Security numbers. Twelve states complied voluntarily (Alaska, Arkansas, Indiana, Louisiana, Mississippi, Nebraska, Ohio, Oklahoma, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Wyoming). The DOJ sued approximately 30 additional states for noncompliance, using three successive legal theories, each dismissed:

1. **NVRA and HAVA** — dismissed in California, Massachusetts, Michigan, Oregon, and Rhode Island; Sixth Circuit affirmed the Michigan dismissal. Courts found no statutory authority for a "fishing expedition" for unredacted voter files.
2. **Civil Rights Act of 1960, Section 303** — the DOJ's surviving theory, requiring "voting records" upon demand. Courts are split on whether voter rolls — which states *create* rather than *receive* from voters — qualify as "voting records"; the Michigan court held they do not. Nine states have completed briefing with decisions pending; eight states have active briefing.
3. **Oklahoma settlement** — one state agreed to provide voter data in exchange for lawsuit dismissal, under a consent agreement that requires removal of DOJ-flagged voters within 45 days and permits data sharing with contractors under inadequate security safeguards.

**The SAVE verification system and its error rate.** The DOJ confirmed in litigation that collected voter files will be shared with DHS and run through the SAVE (Systematic Alien Verification for Entitlements) system. SAVE was designed for individual benefit-eligibility checks — not mass population verification. When Missouri ran St. Louis County's voter list through SAVE in November 2025, it flagged 691 registered voters as potential non-citizens. Thirty-five percent were immediately confirmed as naturalized citizens. After passport cross-referencing, the list fell to 133 — an 81 percent false positive rate. Seventy Missouri county clerks — Republicans and Democrats — warned state leaders that SAVE "repeatedly flags individuals we know to be U.S. citizens — our neighbors, colleagues and even voters we have personally registered at naturalization ceremonies." DHS data shows SAVE has flagged 21,000 registered voters as potential non-citizens across 24 states with sharing agreements.

**The DOJ-to-DHS data transfer and the privacy officer resignation.** In late March 2026, DOJ confirmed it plans to share state voter files with DHS for SAVE verification. The confirmation triggered the resignation of a key DOJ privacy officer in April 2026, who objected to data transfer practices. Democracy Docket separately reported that internal emails show DOJ officials planned the DHS data transfer much earlier than they publicly admitted — before the formal announcement of the voter roll litigation strategy. The consent agreement terms allow sharing with "contractors" not bound by the agreement's safeguards, with no required encryption and no audit framework.

**The HSGP grant leverage tactic.** DHS official Heather Honey raised the idea of conditioning Homeland Security Grant Program funding — hundreds of millions of dollars to state and local governments — on states complying with voter roll data demands and running registration rolls through SAVE. As of late April 2026, the tactic had not been implemented, but its explicit proposal by a named DHS official illustrates the administration's willingness to use federal funding as coercion. The EAC has separately imposed conditions on HSGP awards requiring proof of compliance with VVSG 2.0 guidelines and citizenship verification for poll workers through SAVE.

**The national voter database lawsuit.** Common Cause, represented by ACLU, CREW, Protect Democracy, and Harvard Law School's Democracy and Rule of Law Clinic, filed suit in the District of Columbia in April 2026 to block the national voter database construction. The complaint argues the DOJ's actions usurp state constitutional authority, violate federal privacy law, and rest on no valid statutory authority. Protect Democracy counsel Sara Chimene-Weiss stated the operation is designed "to baselessly cast doubt on and ultimately overturn any unfavorable election results." The case remains pending.

**The legal constraint that matters most.** The NVRA's 90-day quiet period (52 U.S.C. § 20507(c)(2)) prohibits systematic voter removals within 90 days of a federal election. For November 4, 2026, that deadline is August 7, 2026. Any SAVE-based purge after that date is a facial NVRA violation subject to immediate injunction. Any voter wrongfully removed before August 7 faces a much harder path — re-registration or a provisional ballot that may not be counted.

### II.B The Emergency Executive Order Track (Mail Voting and DHS Citizenship List)

**The March 31, 2026 executive order.** President Trump signed "Ensuring Citizenship Verification and Integrity in Federal Elections" on March 31, 2026, with two principal operative directives:

*The USPS mail ballot delivery restriction.* States must submit, 60 days before a federal election, a list of voters approved to receive mail ballots. USPS is directed to deliver mail ballots only to voters on those lists, with USPS retaining final authority. This directly conflicts with state automatic mail ballot programs in Arizona, Colorado, Oregon, Washington, and Wisconsin. UCLA election law professor Rick Hasen called implementation before November "no way" feasible even without court intervention.

*The DHS citizenship voter list.* DHS is directed to collaborate with SSA to compile a list of all adult citizens in each state for use by election officials — a parallel federal citizenship overlay on top of state voter registration.

**Why this order is legally weaker than its predecessor.** Judge John H. Chun permanently blocked three core provisions of Executive Order 14248 (the January 2026 predecessor) in *Washington and Oregon v. Trump*: the documentary proof-of-citizenship requirement, threats to withhold federal election funding, and the national mail ballot receipt deadline. His ruling's constitutional foundation — "The Constitution entrusts Congress and the States — not the President — with authority" — applies fully to the March 31 order, which relies even more heavily on presidential directive without statutory basis.

**The litigation coalition.** Twenty-three state attorneys general and Pennsylvania Governor Josh Shapiro filed suit in April 2026. Michigan AG Dana Nessel filed separately, explicitly arguing the order "attempts to federalize elections." Delaware AG Kathy Jennings filed independently. The Shapiro coalition represents the largest coordinated state legal resistance to a presidential election executive order in modern history.

**The draft emergency EO.** In February 2026, Trump posted that he had identified "legal arguments not yet articulated" supporting federal control of the 2026 elections and suggested an executive order might follow. Subsequent reporting described a draft executive order circulating among allies that would declare a national emergency tied to alleged foreign interference and assert expanded federal oversight authority over the administration of the 2026 midterms — effectively claiming emergency authority to override the Elections Clause. No such order has been signed as of late April 2026, but its existence as a draft is a forward-threat indicator. The Institute for Responsive Government's February 2026 analysis, "Why the President Can't Nationalize Elections," and CNN's concurrent analysis both conclude that any such emergency framing would be constitutionally unsupportable.

### II.D ICE-at-Polls as Voter Intimidation Infrastructure

**The law.** 18 U.S.C. § 592 makes it a federal crime — punishable by up to five years in prison — to deploy federal "troops or armed men" to any voting location unless "necessary to repel armed enemies of the United States." The Voting Rights Act, 52 U.S.C. § 10307(b), separately prohibits "intimidation, threats, or coercion" against anyone voting or attempting to vote, with a private right of action requiring no showing of intent — only the effect of intimidation.

**The Bannon architecture.** Steve Bannon explicitly framed ICE airport deployments as "perfect training" for 2026 midterms, stating ICE agents should "surround the polls come November." CNN reported in February 2026 that Bannon said: "ICE agents have been deployed to airports — are the polls next?" The mechanism is explicit: voters who fear ICE contact — particularly Latino voters, recent naturalized citizens, and those with family in deportation proceedings — make rational self-protective decisions to stay home regardless of whether any agent is actually present.

**The DHS carve-out.** DHS stated ICE is not planning operations "targeting" polling places, but explicitly preserved the option to arrest individuals if "an active public safety threat" endangers a polling location. That undefined carve-out is the entire operational space for election-day deployment — "specific threat" and "active public safety threat" are not subject to external review or prior judicial authorization.

**The DHS Secretary's testimony.** Oklahoma Senator Markwayne Mullin, speaking at his DHS Secretary confirmation hearing, said ICE could be sent to polling places "in the event of a specific threat" — without defining that threshold or identifying who determines it.

**State-level defense.** As of March 2026, more than half a dozen states have introduced or passed legislation banning federal immigration agents from specified distances of polling places. At least eleven states with Democratic majorities are moving on election protection bills modeled on the Brennan Center's model legislation, which creates state criminal penalties against federal forces that interfere with elections, applying even if DOJ declines federal prosecution. American Oversight has an open public-records investigation into administration plans to deploy military or ICE at polls.

## III. Structural Vulnerabilities: Why This Works

### III.A Executive Control Over Federal Law Enforcement With No WPR-Like Domestic Constraint

The constitutional genius of the interference strategy is that it exploits a structural gap: the War Powers Resolution constrains the president's use of military force abroad; no analogous statute constrains the president's direction of federal civilian law enforcement (FBI, ICE, CBP) in domestic electoral contexts. The Posse Comitatus Act restricts military use in domestic law enforcement; it does not cover DHS agencies. 18 U.S.C. § 592 criminalizes military deployment at polls, but ICE agents are civilians, not "troops" under the historical meaning of the statute. The result is a legal gray zone where presidential direction of ICE toward polling places is arguably not prohibited by 18 U.S.C. § 592 unless a court construes the statute to cover DHS agents — a construction that has not been tested at the appellate level.

### III.B The Federal Election Security Vacuum

The destruction of CISA election security and the EAC grant conditionality removes the institutional backstop that exists precisely to detect and respond to federal-level interference. A coordinated national communication system that once ran through CISA has been replaced by "a patchwork of informal phone calls, email lists, and association meetings." Three technology companies confirmed pulling back from sharing sensitive vulnerability information with CISA, fearing a politicized environment would expose proprietary data. If a foreign adversary launches a cyberattack on election infrastructure during the November 2026 election, there is no functioning federal coordination mechanism to respond. And if the administration itself attempts digital manipulation, the agencies that previously would have detected and reported it have been dismantled.

### III.C State Vulnerability to Federal Polling Place Intervention

States administer elections through thousands of county and municipal polling places. County election administrators — often part-time officials with no legal training and no independent law enforcement authority — are not equipped to resist armed federal agents who appear at polling locations. The absence of clear state statutory authority to exclude federal law enforcement creates a structural vulnerability even where state criminal statutes are on the books: local officials will not know their rights, will not be trained in how to exercise them, and will face federal agents who claim legal authority regardless of actual legal standing.

### III.D The SAVE Error Rate as Designed Suppression Mechanism

The 81 percent false positive rate in Missouri is not a bug in the voter purge strategy — it is the mechanism. A system that produces false positives at that rate, applied to millions of registered voters, generates a large class of citizens who show up to vote and are told they are not on the rolls. Some will have been wrongly removed and will cast provisional ballots that may not be counted. Many more will receive notices before election day questioning their eligibility, creating ambient uncertainty that suppresses turnout among those who received notices and among their communities. The suppression effect does not require a single wrongful removal to be sustained.

### III.E Post-CASA Injunction Architecture Weakens Pre-Election Protection

*Trump v. CASA, Inc.* (June 27, 2025) eliminated universal injunctions. Pre-CASA, a single favorable district court ruling could protect the entire country; post-CASA, relief is limited to named plaintiffs. This means that even when election protection organizations win in court — as they have in five states on the voter roll lawsuits — the wins are party-specific. A win in Massachusetts does not protect voters in Nevada. The administration can continue pursuing the same illegal strategy in every other jurisdiction simultaneously, forcing election protection organizations to litigate in 30 states at once with limited resources.

## IV. Reform Pathways and Advocacy Response Vectors

### IV.A Federal Election Takeover Authority and Pre-Clearance Analysis

The administration's key legal weakness is that Congress — not the president — controls federal election authority under Article I, Section 4. Courts have uniformly held that presidential executive orders cannot override the Elections Clause, cannot direct states on election administration, and cannot commandeer state election officials. The legal architecture protecting elections has three operative layers:

*Voting Rights Act Section 2 and Section 11(b).* Section 2 prohibits voting practices that result in denial or abridgement of the right to vote on account of race; Section 11(b) prohibits intimidation of voters regardless of race. Both have private enforcement rights that function independently of DOJ. Post-Voting Section capture, private enforcement is the operative tool. Democracy Docket, ACLU Voting Rights Project, Campaign Legal Center, Protect Democracy, and NAACP Legal Defense Fund each maintain active Section 2 and Section 11(b) enforcement dockets.

*NVRA Section 8 (quiet period) and due process.* The 90-day quiet period is self-executing — violations require no DOJ cooperation to litigate. Any state that attempts systematic SAVE-based removal after August 7 faces immediate emergency injunction exposure. The due process framework requires individualized notice and opportunity to contest before removal; mass SAVE-flag-based removal without individualized verification violates procedural due process independently of the NVRA.

*State constitutional voting protections.* More than 30 state constitutions include voting rights provisions stronger than the federal constitutional baseline. State constitutional litigation in favorable jurisdictions can produce statewide relief that is not subject to CASA's limitations on federal court injunctions.

*Consent decree authority.* The DOJ historically used consent decrees under VRA Section 2 to impose affirmative obligations on states found to have engaged in discriminatory voting practices. In the post-Dhillon world, consent decrees are no longer being used for their intended protective purpose. However, existing consent decrees — including a 2006 consent decree with Georgia that served as a historical predicate for the Albus warrant — remain binding and can be enforced by courts independent of DOJ cooperation.

*Pre-clearance restoration.* The Supreme Court's 2013 *Shelby County* ruling struck down VRA Section 4's coverage formula, rendering Section 5 pre-clearance inoperative. Congress has not enacted a replacement coverage formula. In a post-2026 majority scenario, restoring pre-clearance through the John Lewis Voting Rights Advancement Act — which would update the coverage formula — is the single highest-leverage statutory fix, because it would require states with histories of voting rights violations to obtain federal (or court) approval before implementing new voting changes.

### IV.B Section 3 (14th Amendment) Litigation Vectors

**The doctrine post-*Trump v. Anderson*.** The Supreme Court's March 4, 2024 unanimous ruling in *Trump v. Anderson* held that states lack authority to enforce Section 3 of the 14th Amendment for federal offices, and that Congress must provide the mechanism of enforcement under Section 5. This ruling eliminates state-initiated administrative disqualifications of federal officeholders. It does not eliminate:

(a) **Congressional enforcement under Section 5** — Congress can pass legislation providing the enforcement mechanism *Anderson* identified as absent. A Democratic majority could enact a civil or criminal enforcement statute targeting January 6 participants who now hold federal positions and exercise authority over elections.

(b) **Federal court enforcement of existing Section 3 disqualifications** — *Anderson* addressed who may *determine* disqualification; courts retain jurisdiction to enforce disqualifications once a factual record is established through a proper federal proceeding. The insurrection record from the January 6 Committee provides documentary foundation.

(c) **Application to state-level positions** — *Anderson* addressed federal office; state election officials who participated in January 6 coordination and now hold state-level electoral authority (secretaries of state, state election board members) remain subject to state-level enforcement under Section 3 where state law provides the mechanism.

**The 2026 application.** Several individuals who actively worked to overturn the 2020 election now hold positions with direct authority over 2026 election administration. Kurt Olsen (White House election security director) participated in the strategy to reverse 2020 results. Cleta Mitchell's network — now with 11+ federal appointees — was a primary organizing force for the January 6 pressure campaign on state election officials. Formal Section 3 proceedings against individuals in these positions would require: (a) a congressional enforcement statute, (b) formal factual findings in a federal proceeding establishing "engagement in insurrection," and (c) a court order. The timeline for accomplishing this before November 2026 is very short. The strategic value is primarily (a) establishing the legal foundation for post-election accountability proceedings, and (b) creating a public record that specific named officials are constitutionally disqualified from exercising authority over the 2026 election.

**Practical advocacy application.** Litigation organizations should focus Section 3 work on building the factual record — documenting specific acts by specific officials in the voter roll operation, the Fulton County seizure, and the election interference coordination — that would constitute a Section 3 evidentiary basis. CREW's ongoing Section 3 enforcement work, which has produced the most developed factual records to date, provides the model.

### IV.C Injunction Strategy: Post-CASA Architecture

The central challenge for election protection litigation in 2026 is securing broad enough relief to protect voters across multiple states without universal injunctions. Three post-CASA pathways:

**Pathway 1: APA vacatur.** Justice Barrett's *CASA* majority opinion explicitly reserved "whether the Administrative Procedure Act authorizes federal courts to vacate federal agency action." APA Section 706 vacatur has functionally universal scope — it "sets aside" agency action entirely. The Ninth Circuit, and district courts in Rhode Island, Illinois, Oregon, Massachusetts, Maryland, and Pennsylvania, have consistently held that APA vacatur survives *CASA*. All election protection litigation challenging agency action (DHS SAVE deployment, USPS mail ballot restrictions, EAC grant conditionality) should include APA administrative law claims structured to seek vacatur of the underlying agency rule or determination, not merely injunctive relief against its application to named plaintiffs.

**Pathway 2: Organizational and associational standing.** *CASA* did not foreclose injunctions protecting all members of a plaintiff organization. Common Cause, ACLU, Protect Democracy, NAACP LDF, and the League of Women Voters all have developed associational standing structures. An injunction protecting the full membership of the ACLU, with millions of card-carrying members across all states, approaches functional universality. Associational standing briefing must be comprehensively developed in every active case.

**Pathway 3: State court venues.** State courts are not bound by *CASA*. Federal election rights claims — VRA Section 11(b), Elections Clause violations, Fourteenth Amendment equal protection — can be filed in state courts in favorable jurisdictions. State courts can issue statewide injunctions protecting state residents. Voter purge challenges, where the plaintiff class is inherently localized by state, are structurally well-suited to state court venues. Eleven state AGs are already active in election protection litigation; state court filings coordinate with and reinforce their work.

**The quiet period enforcement deadline is non-negotiable.** Democracy Docket, Campaign Legal Center, ACLU Voting Rights Project, and state AGs must be positioned with ready-to-file preliminary injunction templates targeting any state that attempts systematic SAVE-based voter purges after August 7, 2026. Given the Missouri error rate data (81 percent false positives), standing is straightforward and irreparable harm is clear.

**Venue selection.** Western District of Washington (Judge Chun's permanent injunction precedent), District of Columbia (Common Cause national database challenge, Judge Kollar-Kotelly's permanent injunction on the 2025 EO), District of Maryland, and Northern District of California are favorable venues with established election protection dockets. Avoid circuits where the *CASA* state court alternative is unavailable and where APA vacatur doctrine is less developed.

### IV.D State-Level Blocking Mechanisms

State action is the most durable defense layer, because states control actual election administration. The strategic priorities:

**ICE-at-polls state criminal statutes.** The Brennan Center's model legislation creates state criminal penalties against federal forces that interfere with elections or intimidate voters, applying even if DOJ declines federal prosecution. This is meaningful deterrence: ICE agents who appear at polls face potential state criminal charges independent of federal prosecution. Arizona, Michigan, Nevada, Pennsylvania, and Wisconsin — the highest-priority competitive states — should be the primary targets for passage before legislative session adjournments.

**SAVE-flag removal protections.** State statutes should require independent county-level verification before any registered voter is removed on the basis of a federal citizenship database query. The Ohio SB 293 pattern — monthly SAVE-based purges — violates the NVRA 90-day quiet period and should be an explicit statutory target. State laws requiring individualized verification before removal both protect rights and create procedural delays that prevent mass purges.

**Election night chain-of-custody protections.** The Fulton County precedent requires a state legislative response. States should enact statutes requiring that any federal warrant seeking access to uncertified ballots or voting equipment be served on the state AG with a mandatory judicial review period before execution. These provisions do not override valid federal warrants but create procedural delay enabling state courts to assess legality before the seizure is executed. The AG's authority to intervene in any federal proceeding seeking pre-certification ballot access is the key statutory grant.

**Mail ballot independence from federal certification lists.** States with automatic mail ballot programs should enact explicit state law reaffirming that mail ballot delivery is governed by state law and that USPS cannot condition delivery on compliance with a federal citizenship list without statutory authorization. Congress has not provided that authorization; the March 31 EO has no statutory basis; state law that conflicts with it is not preempted under the Supremacy Clause.

## VI. International Precedent: What Comparative Democracy Teaches

### VI.A Hungary 2026: The Limits of Structural Election Interference

Hungary's April 12, 2026 parliamentary election ended with a 53.6 percent opposition victory for Péter Magyar's Tisza Party, defeating Viktor Orbán's Fidesz after 16 years in power — despite Fidesz operating the most sophisticated electoral manipulation infrastructure in democratic Europe.

**What Orbán built.** Through a combination of electoral system manipulation (gerrymandering that produced 70 percent of seats from 54 percent of 2022 votes), media capture (95+ percent of media in Fidesz-allied hands), clientelist corruption locking economic power to regime loyalty, voter-buying policies targeting rural constituencies, and disinformation operations including fabricated opposition party platforms distributed through captured media. MEPs warned before the April 2026 vote that "conditions for free and fair democratic competition are being undermined by disinformation, foreign interference, state-resources misuse." OSCE monitors documented unprecedented levels of disinformation in the 2026 campaign.

**What defeated it.** Record 77 percent turnout. Youth turnout for Tisza's social-media-native campaign. Economic discontent (inflation, stagnant living standards). The Katalin Novák presidential pardon scandal. Magyar's campaign successfully contested the information space through platforms outside Fidesz's media control. Obama's post-election statement: "It's a testament to the resilience and determination of the Hungarian people — and a reminder to all of us to keep striving for fairness, equality and the rule of law."

**The lesson for U.S. election protection.** Information autocracy has limits. Structural advantages can be overcome by mobilization when economic and political conditions motivate sufficient participation. Disinformation is not a "political sleight of hand which, if not confronted by an equally powerful counterforce, will invariably sway voters" (TechPolicy.Press, April 2026). The margin of victory matters: Fidesz's structural advantages could not overcome a 16-point deficit.

**The limits of the comparison.** The U.S. faces a different structural configuration: executive control of federal law enforcement (CISA, FBI, ICE, DOJ) rather than media capture; post-CASA injunction constraints that did not exist in Hungary's EU-court-accountable system; and the Fulton County precedent for election-night interference that has no Hungarian equivalent. The lesson is encouragement, not complacency.

### VI.B Poland 2023: Reversing Authoritarian Electoral Architecture

Poland's October 2023 parliamentary election, in which a united opposition defeated PiS (Law and Justice) despite equivalent structural advantages, is the more operationally relevant precedent. The Polish coalition's success depended on:

- Maximally unified opposition coordination (avoiding vote-splitting through strategic coordination between three distinct coalition partners)
- Unprecedented mobilization of first-time voters, particularly young urban women motivated by abortion rights restrictions
- International monitoring infrastructure that provided both deterrence and rapid-response documentation
- Judicial resistance: Polish courts, despite PiS's judicial capture efforts, preserved enough independence to block the most egregious electoral rule changes

**The Donald Tusk factor.** The Polish opposition's ability to unite around a single coalition government — rather than splitting into multiple fragmented parties — was decisive. The U.S. equivalent is ensuring that voter suppression, mail ballot disruption, and ICE intimidation threats do not produce a fragmented response that allows individual interference vectors to function as intended.

### VI.C Australia, Canada, and Germany: Independent Election Administration as Structural Defense

The structural solution to executive election interference — visible across Australia, Canada, Germany, and the UK — is an independent election management body with constitutional or statutory insulation from executive control.

**Australian Electoral Commission.** The AEC is a statutory independent body whose commissioners cannot be dismissed without parliamentary approval. Intelligence agencies advise but do not run elections. The AEC maintains its own cybersecurity infrastructure separate from executive branch agencies. The critical design principle: the body administering elections cannot be directed by the government seeking election.

**Elections Canada.** The Chief Electoral Officer and Deputy CEO are constitutionally barred from voting in federal elections — an institutional signal of their complete removal from partisan considerations. They are removable only by joint address of both chambers of Parliament. Budget independence is statutory.

**Germany's Bundeswahlleiter.** The Federal Returning Officer system operates independently of the Interior Ministry, with appointments insulated from political direction and transparency requirements that prevent executive manipulation of the electoral register.

**The U.S. gap.** The United States has no independent federal election administration body. The EAC is a modest bipartisan commission with advisory rather than administrative authority; the real administration is conducted by 50 state systems. CISA provided a partial federal backstop for cybersecurity; its dismantlement has removed even that layer. The absence of a federal independent election body is not a gap that can be filled before November 2026 — it requires congressional action. But the AEC and Elections Canada models provide the legislative template for the post-2026 statutory agenda.
