# Domain 37a: Post-Election Section 3 Litigation and Congressional Certification Recovery Strategy

**Scope document — April 27, 2026**

---

## Why This Domain Is Needed

Domain 37 documents the pre-election and election-day interference mechanisms the Trump administration has constructed for November 2026. It closes with the Fulton County FBI raid as the most concrete indicator that the administration is building post-election interference infrastructure. What Domain 37 does not analyze — because its window is the pre-election period — is the legal and political landscape *after* votes are cast: the certification process, the congressional contestation mechanism, and the specific litigation strategies that can either protect or contest election outcomes in individual House and Senate races.

Domain 37a fills this gap. The 2026 midterms are structurally different from a presidential election in one critical respect: there is no Electoral College. Each House seat is certified by state election officials, and each chamber is "the Judge of the Elections, Returns and Qualifications of its own Members" (Article I, Section 5). This means:

1. Post-election challenges to individual House and Senate races flow through Congress, not the courts — and a Republican-majority Congress controlling the certification of its own members is a structural conflict of interest with no historical precedent at the scale being contemplated.
2. Section 3 of the 14th Amendment — the insurrection disqualification clause — may generate pre-certification and post-certification challenges against candidates who participated in January 6 or subsequent insurrectionary conduct. *Trump v. Anderson* (2024) foreclosed *state* enforcement of Section 3 against federal officers; it left open whether Congress itself can enforce it through the seating process, and whether federal courts can hear suits seeking declaratory judgments about candidate disqualification.
3. The Fulton County precedent demonstrates that the DOJ is willing to use criminal warrant power to access election materials during the counting window. The question Domain 37a must answer: what is the legal framework that constrains (or fails to constrain) that access during the 2026 post-election period, and what is the recovery litigation strategy if access occurs?

## Core Research Questions

**1. Post-election congressional certification**

The Electoral Count Reform Act (2022) clarified the presidential certification process. No comparable statutory reform governs House and Senate race certification. The key questions:

- What is the current legal framework for congressional contestation of House and Senate races? (2 U.S.C. §§ 381-396, the Federal Contested Elections Act, governs House contests; no equivalent exists for Senate contests, which are governed by Senate Rule XI and ad hoc resolutions)
- What has been the historical pattern of contested election outcomes in partisan-polarized House compositions? (Reference: the 1984 McIntyre-McCloskey Indiana contest, in which a Democratic House seated the Democrat by 4 votes over Republican objection — and the precedent this set for partisan contestation)
- What procedural protections exist for minority-party objectors when the majority party controls the contestation process? The answer, based on the FCEA's history, is: very few
- Does the 2022 Electoral Count Reform Act's clarification of the vice president's ministerial role (applied to presidential elections) have any analogical application to House and Senate certification? (Likely not — different constitutional text — but the argument should be assessed)

**2. Section 3 post-election litigation**

*Trump v. Anderson* (601 U.S. 100, 2024) held unanimously that states cannot enforce Section 3 against federal officers without congressional authorization. But it left open:

- Whether Congress can *itself* enforce Section 3 through refusal to seat a member-elect (historical precedent: Victor Berger, 1919, twice refused his seat under Section 3 after conviction for opposing World War I)
- Whether a federal court can issue a declaratory judgment that a specific candidate is disqualified under Section 3 — and whether that judgment binds the relevant chamber
- What the standard of proof is for "engaged in insurrection" under Section 3 — whether it requires a criminal conviction or can be established through civil evidence
- Whether DOJ can use its criminal enforcement authority (18 U.S.C. § 2383, insurrection statute) to bar candidates on the basis of post-January 6 conduct, including conduct related to the 2026 election itself (e.g., administration officials who participated in the voter roll coercion campaign or the Fulton County operation)

The January 6 committee referrals established a factual record regarding a number of current House members who will be seeking reelection in 2026. The DOJ's current posture — dropping January 6 prosecutions rather than pursuing them — does not foreclose Section 3 civil litigation by third parties.

**3. The Fulton County post-election threat model**

The Fulton County raid — 650 boxes of ballots and election materials seized by FBI agents operating under a cross-district warrant signed by a Missouri US attorney (Thomas Albus, the same official now designated as the national election enforcement coordinator) — created a concrete evidentiary record for what post-election federal election interference looks like. The research questions:

- What is the current legal status of the Fulton County materials? (Fulton County filed a motion for return of seized property; the DOJ has asserted the materials are secret; as of April 2026, the litigation is ongoing)
- What legal authority governs the seizure of election materials during an ongoing count? Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 41 governs search warrants generally; but seizure of ballots and election materials implicates the Elections Clause (Article I, Section 4), which reserves election administration to states. The Brennan Center's analysis: "There is no role for the executive branch."
- What injunctive relief is available to a state or county that believes the FBI is about to seize its election materials? The answer likely lies in a combination of Fourth Amendment warrant challenges (the Fulton warrant's affidavit was based on "debunked conspiracy theories" per NPR review), Elections Clause structural arguments, and emergency TRO practice
- What is the specific recovery litigation strategy? Who has standing (county election officials, state AGs, candidates, voters), what courts have jurisdiction, and what relief is available in the 7-day window between election night and when official certification begins?

## Analytical Framework

The document should be organized around three phases of post-election vulnerability:

**Phase 1 — Election night through state certification (November 3 – December 2026)**

This phase covers the window in which: (a) the Fulton County-type seizure threat is most acute; (b) emergency injunctive relief is most critical; and (c) media framing shapes public perception of contested results. The key legal tools in this window are Fourth Amendment warrant challenges, Elections Clause structural arguments, and state-law protections for ballot chain-of-custody.

**Phase 2 — Congressional certification (January 3, 2027)**

When Congress convenes, each chamber's committee on elections has the power to recommend seating or unseating members-elect. Under the FCEA, the House process requires: (a) a motion to contest filed within 30 days of certification, (b) taking of depositions and document discovery, (c) committee recommendation, and (d) full floor vote. The partisan composition of the relevant committee determines the outcome. This phase covers Section 3 challenges (both offensive and defensive), the Berger precedent, and the structural conflict of interest when the majority party controls contested-election decisions that affect chamber control.

**Phase 3 — Post-seating challenges and permanent record**

Once members are seated, challenges become substantially harder. But the legal record built in Phases 1 and 2 matters for: (a) future elections (establishing precedent for what federal seizure of election materials is legally permissible), (b) accountability litigation against officials who conducted unlawful seizures, and (c) the long-term validity of the election results as a matter of democratic legitimacy.

## Governance and Advocacy Implications

**For election-protection organizations**: The pre-certification window (Phase 1) is the most actionable period. Legal teams should have emergency TRO motions prepared before November 3 — targeting any attempt to seize election materials under warrant, conditioning such warrants on compliance with Elections Clause requirements, and filing for emergency stays in federal district courts with jurisdiction over specific counties of concern (Fulton County, Maricopa County, Philadelphia County, Wayne County, Milwaukee County are the historical targets of fraud allegation campaigns).

**For congressional minority members**: The minority party's only real power in a congressional contested-election proceeding is procedural — demanding full document discovery, forcing floor votes on record, and building the public record. The Democratic minority (if Democrats are in the minority in the 120th Congress) should have legal counsel assigned to contested-election proceedings before the new Congress convenes.

**For Section 3 advocates**: The Berger precedent gives the House inherent authority to refuse to seat a member-elect who engaged in insurrection, without any court ruling. The question is whether that authority will be exercised by a Republican majority. The value of pre-seating declaratory judgment litigation is not necessarily winning — courts may decline jurisdiction — but building the factual record and making the constitutional argument public before the seating decision occurs.

**For state attorneys general**: State AGs have the clearest standing to challenge federal seizure of election materials — they are the constitutional agents of state election administration. The coordination of a pre-positioned TRO motion across competitive state AGs (Arizona, Georgia, Wisconsin, Pennsylvania, Michigan, Nevada) before the election is the highest-value protective action available in Phase 1.
