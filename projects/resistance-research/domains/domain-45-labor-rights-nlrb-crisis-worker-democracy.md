## Executive Summary

The central finding of this domain is not that unions are economically beneficial — though that evidence is strong and documented below — but that collective bargaining infrastructure functions as democratic infrastructure in a precise, empirically traceable sense: union membership increases voter participation by 18 percentage points relative to comparable non-union workers; union density in a state predicts whether that state passes voter restriction laws at a rate that is not coincidental but structural; and the organizational capacity of the AFL-CIO's 51 state federations, 56 national unions, and 15 million members (with SEIU's rejoining in January 2025) represents the largest civic mobilization infrastructure in the United States outside of government itself.

That infrastructure is under coordinated constitutional attack in 2026. The mechanism is not legislative repeal but structural decapitation: by eliminating the removal protections for NLRB board members (Trump v. Wilcox, May 2025; D.C. Circuit December 2025; Trump v. Slaughter pending June 2026), and by deploying budget starvation, leadership capture, and a 345-day quorum crisis that suppressed union election volume by 30% and worker participation by 42%, the administration has effectively disabled the federal agency responsible for enforcing the National Labor Relations Act without repealing a single word of it.

The Loper Bright elimination of Chevron deference (June 2024) adds a second vulnerability layer: NLRA statutory provisions that have been interpreted by the NLRB for decades to protect organizing rights are now subject to de novo judicial reinterpretation in circuits hostile to labor — including the Fifth Circuit, which already ruled that the NLRB's structure is likely unconstitutional in August 2025. The combination of removal-clause vulnerability and post-Loper Bright interpretive instability means that the legal architecture protecting the right to organize is simultaneously threatened from two constitutional directions.

The reform agenda has three advocacy windows operating on different timescales. Immediate (2026): defend the NLRB quorum and budget against further reduction; file amicus infrastructure in Trump v. Slaughter to document democratic participation consequences of ruling. Medium-term (2027): PRO Act positioning for a post-2026-midterm Congress; state-level card-check legislation in the 15 states with Democratic legislative trifectas. Structural: sectoral bargaining architecture modeled on California's fast food council and the German Mitbestimmung co-determination system that workers on corporate boards. The adoption pathway runs through AFL-CIO's research department (15 million members, 51 state federations) and SEIU's member education pipeline — and the strategic framing that makes adoption likely is the reframing of this crisis as a democracy crisis rather than a labor dispute.

## 1. The Crisis: NLRB as Democratic Architecture Under Attack

### 1.1 What the NLRB Does and Why It Matters for Democracy

The National Labor Relations Board was created by the National Labor Relations Act of 1935 as part of the New Deal architecture for democratic governance of the economy. Its core functions — protecting workers' right to organize, conducting union certification elections, investigating and remedying unfair labor practices, and enforcing collective bargaining obligations — are not merely economic protections. They are the institutional machinery through which 14.7 million workers exercise collective democratic voice in their workplaces.

That framing is not rhetorical. The NLRA's preamble states explicitly that "the inequality of bargaining power between employees who do not possess full freedom of association or actual liberty of contract, and employers who are organized in the corporate or other forms of ownership association... tends to aggravate recurrent business depressions, to affect the flow of commerce, and to deprive many employees of wages which are sufficient to enable them to enjoy decent standards of living." Congress's finding in 1935 was that economic inequality without institutional counterbalance produces political instability — which is the same finding that contemporary democracy research now quantifies empirically in the union density and voter suppression correlation documented in Section 2.

The NLRB in 2026 is an agency under simultaneous assault through five distinct vectors, none of which requires repeal of the NLRA:

**Leadership decapitation**: President Trump fired NLRB member Gwynne Wilcox (January 27, 2025) and General Counsel Jennifer Abruzzo in his first week in office. The firing left the five-member board without the three-member quorum required by statute to issue decisions. The Supreme Court, 6-3, declined to reinstate Wilcox on May 22, 2025. The quorum was not restored until December 18, 2025 — 345 days — during which the board could not resolve election objections, decide unfair labor practice cases, or oversee prosecutions of alleged labor law violations.

**Budget starvation**: The FY2026 NLRB budget request cut $14 million from FY2025 levels, a 4.7% reduction to $285.2 million. Case-handling — covering unfair labor practice and representation proceedings — was cut by $8.9 million and 61 positions. More than 150 employees left through attrition during 2025 while only eight new workers were hired. The cumulative workforce reduction exceeds 10% of the agency's total staffing, with Administrative Law Judge and senior staff losses creating processing delays that will persist for years.

**Leadership capture**: Acting General Counsel William Cowen, appointed after Abruzzo's firing, reversed several key enforcement positions. The new GC has rescinded guidance on captive audience meetings (mandatory employer-side union presentations during organizing campaigns), restricted the use of novel remedies for repeat NLRA violators, and declined to prosecute cases arising from employer conduct that the Abruzzo NLRB had treated as unfair labor practices. The agency now enforces the same statute against the workers it was designed to protect — through prosecutorial discretion exercised by an acting GC appointed by the administration against whom those workers are organizing.

**Constitutional challenge**: The Fifth Circuit ruled August 19, 2025, that the NLRB's structure is likely unconstitutional — specifically that the for-cause removal protections for both NLRB Administrative Law Judges and Board members violate Article II's vesting of executive power in the President. The court enjoined the NLRB from pursuing unfair labor practice charges against SpaceX, Aunt Bertha, and Energy Transfer LP in the Fifth Circuit. The constitutional theory, if adopted by the Supreme Court, would eliminate removal protections for all NLRB officers, enabling the President to fire board members at will and staff the agency entirely with management-side appointees without any statutory constraint.

**Forum shopping**: Starbucks has six pending cases in the Fifth Circuit despite none of the underlying conduct occurring within that circuit's geographic jurisdiction. Amazon's constitutional challenge is being litigated in the Central District of California with appeal to the Ninth Circuit. The circuit split on whether federal courts may enjoin NLRB proceedings during constitutional challenges — the Fifth Circuit said yes; the Third and Ninth Circuits said no — now creates a Supreme Court circuit-conflict case that could be decided as early as OT2027.

### 1.2 Union Suppression as Democratic Exclusion

The parallel between the NLRB's current structural crisis and other forms of democratic exclusion is not metaphorical. It is structural: the same legislative majorities that pass voter suppression laws pass anti-union legislation, in the same states, against the same populations.

The Economic Policy Institute's research (2024) documents the correlation precisely. Since 2021, low-union-density states have passed 44 voter restriction laws, while high-union-density states have passed only six. In the period 2021-2024, 70% of states with the highest union density had not passed any voter restrictions, while less than a quarter of medium- and low-union-density states could claim this distinction. A one-percentage-point increase in union density is associated with a 9.8% increase in the number of ballot drop boxes per capita — a metric that directly measures voter access infrastructure.

This is not a spurious correlation. The mechanism is political power: union density generates the organized constituency capacity needed to defeat voter suppression legislation at the state level. Tennessee, Georgia, and Alabama enacted legislation in 2024-2025 threatening to deny state economic development funds to any employer who voluntarily recognizes a union — a direct state-level intervention designed to reduce organizing success, knowing that reduced organizing success reduces the political mobilization capacity that would otherwise challenge voter suppression in those same states.

The union density data by race adds a further layer of structural exclusion. BLS 2025 data shows that Black workers have the highest unionization rate of all major racial and ethnic groups at 12.7% — but this rate reflects historical organizing gains, not current enforcement protection. In Black-majority workplaces that are currently attempting to organize, the NLRB enforcement vacuum created by the 345-day quorum crisis and the Trump administration's leadership capture disproportionately affects those workers, because the NLRB proceedings being delayed or dismissed are precisely the ones involving the workers with the most recent organizing wins and the most pending unfair labor practice complaints.

The scale of the current crisis is measurable: NLRB-overseen union elections fell from 2,124 in 2024 (a 10-year high) to 1,498 in 2025 — a 30% decline. 59,000 fewer workers participated in NLRB-run elections in 2025, a 42% decline from the previous year. The share of elections won by newly organizing unions fell below 70%, down from recent highs above 72%. These numbers represent real workers — at warehouses, in healthcare facilities, at coffee shops — whose organizing campaigns were slowed, stalled, or suppressed by an agency that was intentionally disabled.

### 1.3 Private Sector Union Density: The Structural Collapse

The United States has 10.0% overall union density as of 2025 (BLS), down from 20.1% in 1983. Private sector union density is 5.9% — historically the lowest on record, unchanged from 2024. Public sector density rose from 32.2% to 32.9%, driven by increases among federal workers and teachers.

The private sector collapse is the critical democratic infrastructure story. The 5.9% private sector rate means that 94.1% of private sector workers are unrepresented in collective bargaining — and therefore lack access to the civic infrastructure, political education, and collective organizing capacity that union membership provides. The 1983 baseline of 20.1% private sector union density corresponded to a period of substantially lower economic inequality; the decline of private sector union density and the rise of the Gini coefficient track each other across the decades since 1983 at a rate that is statistically significant in EPI's analysis. The causal direction runs both ways: lower union density reduces workers' bargaining power (producing higher inequality), and higher inequality reduces workers' capacity to organize (producing lower union density). The NLRB's enforcement capacity is the institutional lever that breaks or reinforces this cycle.

## 2. Democratic Mechanisms: How Worker Power Sustains Democratic Participation

### 2.1 The Voting Evidence Base

The empirical relationship between union membership and voter participation is among the most robust findings in American political science. The Harvard Ash Center's 2020 analysis by Tova Wang synthesizes decades of research: union members are 18% more likely to vote in presidential elections than comparable non-union workers, 43% more likely to volunteer for an election campaign, and 73-93% more likely to participate in protests. In the 2014 midterms, 52% of union workers voted compared to only 39% of non-union workers — a 13-point participation gap in an election where aggregate turnout was low enough that this gap was nationally decisive.

The mechanisms are three-fold and compound:

**Direct mobilization**: Unions conduct systematic GOTV operations through their Committee on Political Education (COPE) infrastructure. The AFL-CIO's COPE, established in 1955, coordinates member-to-member voter contact, registration drives, and election-day turnout operations across 51 state federations. This infrastructure is distinct from broadcast political advertising — it is peer-to-peer, workplace-embedded, and reaches workers who have lower educational attainment and lower baseline political engagement. The documented effect is that union GOTV mobilization substantially exceeds the participation gains from comparable campaign spending on advertising.

**Political education**: Union membership increases political knowledge, policy preference clarity, and candidate information among workers with lower formal educational attainment. Research in the Journal of Politics and related outlets finds that union membership compensates for the political knowledge gap between workers without college education and those with college degrees. This compensation effect is the mechanism through which unions function as democratic equalizers: they increase the political participation of the specific demographic whose participation rate is most depressed by economic insecurity and educational disadvantage.

**Economic security as civic prerequisite**: The most direct mechanism is economic. Workers covered by union contracts earn 10.2% more in hourly wages than workers with similar education, occupation, and experience in non-union workplaces (EPI). Median household incomes in high-union-density states are more than $12,000 higher, on average, than median incomes in low-union-density states. Economic security is a precondition for the time, attention, and resources required for political participation: voting requires ID acquisition, transportation, and time away from work that is more easily managed with paid sick leave and stable scheduling (union contract provisions) than without. The compound effect of higher wages, paid leave, and schedule stability on civic participation is not merely correlational — it is the structural reason that union membership's participation effect holds even after controlling for political interest, ideology, and other factors that could confound the relationship.

### 2.2 Union Political Infrastructure: Scope and Capacity

The AFL-CIO's re-expanded coalition (following SEIU's January 2025 re-affiliation after a 20-year absence) now encompasses:
- 15 million members across 56 national and international unions
- 51 state federations covering every state
- 540 local central labor councils
- SEIU's 2 million members across healthcare (1.1 million+), public services (400,000+), and property services (400,000+) sectors
- Research departments at AFL-CIO headquarters and at major affiliated unions (SEIU, UAW, USW, Teamsters)

SEIU's Committee on Political Education raised $74 million and spent $78 million in the 2023-2024 election cycle — larger than most super PAC operations and structured through member education rather than broadcast advertising. SEIU's political activities include: get-out-the-vote drives, candidate endorsements, member-to-member education on candidates and issues, lobbying, field programs, advertising, and community partnerships. This infrastructure is not a separate political operation layered on top of union functions — it is integrated into the member relationship through workplace contact and union hall organizing.

The AFL-CIO's COPE coordinates across all 56 affiliated unions with the statutory mandate (from the AFL-CIO Constitution) to "encourage workers to register and vote, to exercise their full rights and responsibilities of citizenship, and to perform their rightful part in the political life of the city, state, and national communities." The scale of this operation makes the AFL-CIO-affiliated union movement the largest non-governmental voter contact infrastructure in the United States — larger than either major party's ground operation in most states outside of presidential election years.

The Pew Research Center's data on union political engagement confirms the voter gap holds across partisan lines: union households voted at higher rates than comparable non-union households in 2020 and 2022 regardless of party affiliation, with the mobilization effect strongest among workers without college education and among minority workers — precisely the demographic groups with the highest baseline suppression from voter ID laws, registration barriers, and polling place closures.

### 2.3 Policy Development Capacity

Union research departments constitute a significant portion of the institutional research infrastructure that informs progressive policy development in the United States. The AFL-CIO's research and policy department, SEIU's Research and Strength Department, the UAW's economic research team, and the communications and research operations of major industrial unions collectively employ hundreds of economists, lawyers, and policy analysts. This infrastructure produces:

- Legislative analysis and testimony on labor, tax, healthcare, trade, and housing policy
- Collective bargaining research that tracks wage standards across industries and regions
- Member education curriculum on policy issues directly affecting workers
- Coalitions with academic researchers at university labor institutes (Cornell ILR School, UCLA Labor Center, UC Berkeley Labor Center)

The loss of this institutional capacity — through union density decline or through NLRB enforcement collapse that prevents new organizing — is a democratic governance loss, not merely an economic one. When worker-advocacy research capacity declines, policy development shifts toward the employer-funded research operations that the same corporations deploy in lobbying and regulatory comment processes.

### 2.4 International Comparison: What Higher Union Density Produces

Sweden and Denmark sustain national parliamentary election voter turnout consistently above 80-84% — figures that the United States has never approached in the post-WWII era. Both countries have union density rates of 65-70%. Finland and Norway are similar: union density 60-75%, voter turnout 70-80% in national elections. The Nordic correlation between union density and democratic participation is not purely causal — social democratic institutional history, proportional representation electoral systems, and social trust all contribute — but the mechanism is well-documented in the comparative politics literature: high union density correlates with economic equality, economic equality correlates with lower barriers to political participation, and lower barriers produce higher turnout across income quintiles rather than just among high-income voters.

Germany's codetermination (Mitbestimmung) system provides a different institutional model: under the 1976 Mitbestimmungsgesetz, companies with more than 2,000 employees must seat workers as representatives on nearly half of the supervisory board of directors. Companies with more than 500 employees must reserve one-third of board seats for worker-elected representatives. The 2021 National Bureau of Economic Research analysis found that codetermination had neutral-to-small positive effects on firm productivity, wages, job security, and satisfaction — it did not harm firm performance while providing workers structural governance participation within the enterprise. The political democracy argument for codetermination is separate from the economic argument: workers who participate in governance decisions at their workplace develop civic governance capacity that translates into broader democratic participation.

## 3. Structural Vulnerabilities: Humphrey's Executor Collapse and NLRB Removal

### 3.1 Trump v. Wilcox and the Shadow Docket Decapitation

On January 27, 2025, President Trump fired NLRB member Gwynne Wilcox from her position by email, despite the NLRA's statutory requirement that board members may be removed only for "neglect of duty or malfeasance in office." This firing — conducted without any stated cause, without a hearing, and via email — was unprecedented in the NLRB's ninety-year history.

The subsequent litigation, Wilcox v. Trump (D.D.C., Judge Beryl Howell), produced a March 2025 district court ruling that the firing was unlawful. Judge Howell's order was direct: "The President does not have the authority to terminate members of the National Labor Relations Board at will, and his attempt to fire plaintiff from her position on the Board was a blatant violation of the law."

The Supreme Court reversed course on the shadow docket. On May 22, 2025, the 6-3 conservative supermajority granted an emergency stay without full merits briefing, without oral argument, and without a reasoned majority opinion. The unsigned order kept Wilcox out of office while characterizing the NLRB and MSPB as exercising "considerable executive power" — the functional test the Court has been developing in Seila Law v. CFPB (2020) and Collins v. Yellen (2021) to distinguish agencies whose independence is constitutionally permissible from those whose independence is not.

The D.C. Circuit formalized this logic in December 2025, reversing the district court and holding that Congress may not restrict the President's ability to remove NLRB or MSPB officers because they exercise "substantial executive power." Justice Kagan's dissent at the shadow docket stage named what the majority did: "Not since the 1950s (or even before) has a president, without a legitimate reason, tried to remove an officer from a classic independent agency." She characterized the order as allowing the President to overrule Humphrey's Executor by fiat, pending eventual full merits review.

The "eventual full merits review" is Trump v. Slaughter (25A264, argued December 8, 2025; decision expected late June 2026), which consolidates the FTC commissioner firing with the Wilcox/Harris line of cases. Solicitor General Sauer argued at oral argument that "Humphrey's Executor was always egregiously wrong." The Republican-appointed majority appeared to lean at oral argument toward limiting or overruling the 1935 precedent. Three options remain:

**Option 1 — Full overruling**: All independent agency officers are removable at will by the president. NLRB, FTC, FEC, CFPB, MSPB, EEOC removal protections eliminated. This is the administration's stated preference and the functional reality already established through the shadow docket.

**Option 2 — Functional narrowing**: Humphrey's Executor survives in limited form for agencies with purely quasi-judicial functions, but is eliminated for agencies with prosecutorial, policymaking, or enforcement roles. Under this option, NLRB and FTC independence is eliminated; MSPB may survive.

**Option 3 — Prospective limitation only**: Existing commissioners protected; future legislation prohibited from creating comparable protections. Least likely given the D.C. Circuit ruling and the administration's posture.

The practical consequence of Option 1 or Option 2 for the NLRB is the same: every future presidential administration can install management-side partisans in every NLRB seat, without any statutory constraint, and can remove any board member who issues a ruling it dislikes. The NLRA becomes unenforceable as written the moment a hostile administration chooses not to enforce it — which is exactly what the Trump administration has done without even needing a formal Slaughter ruling.

### 3.2 Post-Loper Bright NLRA Vulnerability

The June 2024 Loper Bright decision eliminating Chevron deference adds a second constitutional pressure track. Under Chevron (1984-2024), courts deferred to the NLRB's reasonable interpretation of ambiguous NLRA provisions. Under Loper Bright, courts exercise independent judgment on all questions of statutory interpretation — meaning that decades of NLRB precedents on the scope of protected concerted activity, the definition of "employee" versus "independent contractor," and the contours of unfair labor practice liability are now subject to de novo judicial challenge in every circuit.

The circuit split on what this means for labor law is immediate: the D.C. Circuit held in July 2024 that NLRB decisions still warrant a "very high degree of deference" post-Loper Bright. The Sixth Circuit held in August 2024 that it would "not defer to the NLRB's interpretation of the NLRA." These divergent standards create the conditions for strategic forum shopping — employers challenging NLRB rulings can seek review in circuits that apply independent judicial judgment, effectively nullifying NLRB precedents in those circuits without any legislative change.

The NLRA's statutory language on core organizing rights uses terms — "concerted activity," "interference, restraint, or coercion," "good faith bargaining" — that the NLRB has interpreted through thousands of decisions over ninety years. Those interpretations are now contestable from scratch in hostile circuits. Project 2025's Mandate for Leadership was explicit about the target: the NLRB should "restrict the scope of 'concerted activity' protected by Section 7" — which would narrow the universe of worker organizing that receives federal protection. Post-Loper Bright, that narrowing can be accomplished through litigation rather than legislation, by persuading a circuit court that the NLRB's expansive interpretation of Section 7 is not the "best meaning" of the statutory text.

### 3.3 Project 2025's Documented Anti-NLRB Agenda

The Heritage Foundation's Mandate for Leadership (Project 2025) documents the administrative strategy for NLRB capture explicitly:

- End all independent agency removal protections, including the NLRB's

Each of these agenda items is partially or fully implemented as of May 2026: the GC was fired on Day 1; the budget was cut; the quorum was destroyed for 345 days; card check guidance was rescinded; Section 7 enforcement posture has narrowed. The constitutionalization strategy through SpaceX, Amazon, Trader Joe's, and Starbucks cases converts the administrative decapitation into permanent constitutional incapacity — if the Supreme Court rules that removal protections for NLRB officers are unconstitutional, no future Congress can restore them by statute.

## 4. 2026 Crisis Trajectory: Three Constitutional Pathways

### 4.1 The SpaceX/Amazon/Starbucks/Trader Joe's Litigation Pipeline

The constitutionalization of labor law has been pursued through a coordinated litigation strategy by corporations facing NLRB enforcement actions. The key cases and their current status as of May 2026:

**SpaceX v. NLRB**: The Fifth Circuit ruled August 19, 2025, that the NLRB's structure is likely unconstitutional — both the double-for-cause removal protection for ALJs and the single-for-cause protection for board members. The court enjoined NLRB proceedings against SpaceX, Aunt Bertha, and Energy Transfer LP. On February 9, 2026, the NLRB dismissed its long-running unfair labor practice complaint against SpaceX, citing that the agency "lacks jurisdiction over SpaceX" — a capitulation to the Fifth Circuit's framework. The dismissal has reduced the immediate probability of a SpaceX certiorari petition, but the Fifth Circuit's constitutional holding stands as binding precedent in that circuit.

**Amazon's Constitutional Challenge**: Amazon filed suit in September 2024 in the Central District of California challenging NLRB structure as unconstitutional. The Ninth Circuit, on December 29, 2025, upheld the district court's refusal to grant Amazon's request to pause NLRB proceedings — aligning with the Third Circuit's reasoning that the Norris-LaGuardia Act bars federal courts from enjoining NLRB proceedings. The Third-Ninth Circuit alignment against the Fifth Circuit creates the circuit split that could generate a Supreme Court petition on the injunctive relief question, independently of the merits constitutional question.

**Starbucks**: Starbucks entered 2026 with six pending cases in the Fifth Circuit — all filed there despite none of the underlying conduct occurring within the circuit's geographic jurisdiction. This is deliberate forum shopping: the Fifth Circuit is the only circuit that has ruled the NLRB's structure likely unconstitutional, and Starbucks is accumulating precedential leverage in the most favorable venue. On April 17, 2026, the Fifth Circuit ruled against the NLRB in a subpoena case, finding the NLRB had misapplied a legal test.

**Trader Joe's**: On February 18, 2026, the Fifth Circuit ruled against Trader Joe's appeal of an NLRB finding that the company illegally disciplined and fired a worker — meaning the Fifth Circuit enforced the NLRB ruling despite its August 2025 structural objection. This suggests the circuit is not applying its constitutional critique to block all enforcement, only to enjoin proceedings where the constitutional challenge has been raised explicitly.

The combined effect is a multi-front litigation strategy designed to: (1) establish Fifth Circuit precedent that the NLRB is structurally unconstitutional; (2) use that precedent to seek injunctions against NLRB enforcement actions in the Fifth Circuit; (3) generate a circuit split on injunctive relief that forces Supreme Court review; and (4) use the Supreme Court's removal-power jurisprudence from Trump v. Slaughter as constitutional authority for eliminating the NLRB's structural independence entirely.

### 4.2 The Trump v. Slaughter Decision and Its NLRB Consequences

Trump v. Slaughter (argued December 8, 2025, decision expected late June 2026) is the pivotal case. It formally presents the question: should Humphrey's Executor v. United States (1935) be overruled? The NLRB's statutory removal protection is analytically identical to the FTC's — both are independent agencies whose officers Congress specified could be removed only for cause. If Humphrey's Executor is overruled as to the FTC, the same reasoning eliminates NLRB protection.

The functional reality as of May 2026 is that NLRB independence is already gone: the board was decapitated for 345 days, the GC was replaced with a management-side lawyer, the budget was cut, and the agency has retreated from enforcement in multiple areas. Trump v. Slaughter converts functional reality into formal constitutional doctrine — and once doctrine is established, no future statute can restore what the Court has held Congress cannot grant.

**Urgent flag for June 2026**: The Slaughter decision, expected before June 30, 2026, will arrive during the active advocacy window for the 2026 midterms. If the Court rules against Humphrey's Executor, the ruling should be immediately integrated into labor union political communications as concrete evidence that voting for Democratic House and Senate candidates in 2026 is the only statutory pathway to any NLRB restoration — because no court can restore removal protections that the Supreme Court has held unconstitutional.

### 4.3 State-Level Preemption and the Right-to-Work-Plus Wave

As of May 2026, 26 states have right-to-work laws, which prohibit unions from requiring workers they represent to pay dues or fees as a condition of employment. The NLRA preempts state labor law in most respects, but right-to-work is expressly authorized by the NLRA's Section 14(b). The 2024-2025 wave of anti-union state legislation goes beyond traditional right-to-work into "right-to-work-plus" territory: Tennessee, Georgia, and Alabama have enacted laws threatening to deny state economic development funds to any employer who voluntarily recognizes a union — even though voluntary recognition when a majority of workers have signed union cards remains fully lawful under the NLRA.

The federal preemption question is unresolved: these state laws arguably conflict with the NLRA's permission for voluntary recognition, but the Trump NLRB is unlikely to challenge them, and the states are banking on the 6-3 Supreme Court's willingness to expand state authority under a textualist reading of Section 14(b)'s "right to work" authorization. Michigan moved in the opposite direction: repealing its 2013 right-to-work law effective February 2024, becoming the first state in nearly 60 years to repeal a right-to-work law after Democrats achieved a legislative trifecta.

The Senate Republican "pro-employer" labor law reform package introduced in November 2025 includes: financial penalties for NLRB charges not filed in good faith; new restrictions on unions' use of worker data obtained in representation proceedings; and new union spending transparency requirements structured to increase administrative burden on unions while reducing union political capacity. None of these provisions repeal the NLRA; all are designed to reduce the practical effectiveness of union organizing and political activity while leaving the statutory framework nominally in place.

### 5.2 Strategic Framing: From Labor Dispute to Democracy Crisis

The analytical hook that makes this domain's adoption likely beyond existing labor advocacy — and into civil rights, democracy, and civic institution audiences — is precisely the reframing the task brief identifies: the attack on the NLRB is not a labor dispute. It is a democracy crisis.

This framing works because it is empirically accurate: the causal chain from NLRB enforcement capacity to union density to voter participation to voter suppression legislation is documented, quantified, and replicable. But it also works strategically because it changes the audience. Framing the NLRB crisis as a labor dispute keeps it within the AFL-CIO ecosystem — audiences who already agree that workers deserve protection. Framing it as a democracy crisis opens it to civil rights organizations, voting rights litigators, journalism outlets covering democratic backsliding, and funders who would not ordinarily engage with labor law.

The concrete frame is this: the administration has disabled the one federal institution specifically designed to protect the right of 160 million private sector workers to organize collectively — not by repealing the law, but by removing its leadership without cause, refusing to staff it adequately, and seeking a constitutional ruling that will make it impossible for any future Congress to require independent leadership. When that institution is disabled, the 14-18 percentage-point voting participation advantage of union members over comparable non-union workers disappears. When it disappears, the states most hostile to unions pass voter suppression laws. This is not coincidence. It is the structural purpose of the attack.

### 5.3 Urgency Window: The Slaughter Decision and the 2026 Midterms

The Trump v. Slaughter decision (expected late June 2026) arrives approximately four months before the 2026 midterms. If the Court issues an adverse ruling, the immediate advocacy task is clear: translate the legal ruling into voter-accessible language explaining that (a) the Supreme Court has formally eliminated the constitutional basis for NLRB independence, (b) the only pathway to statutory restoration is a Congressional majority willing to enact new legislation, and (c) the 2026 midterms are the first opportunity to build that majority.

AFL-CIO's COPE infrastructure is positioned to execute that translation: it has the member contact lists, the precinct-level political staff, and the GOTV operation capable of reaching 15 million members with a unified electoral framing within weeks of a Supreme Court decision. SEIU's $74 million political operation in 2023-2024 demonstrates the financial capacity for rapid response. The window is real and time-bounded.

## 6. Reform Pathways

### 6.1 Immediate (2026-2027): Defensive Operations During SCOTUS Litigation

The immediate priority is preventing further structural degradation while the constitutional litigation concludes. Three specific defensive measures:

**Protect NLRB quorum and budget**: Any further board member firing — now legally unencumbered by Humphrey's Executor protections after the shadow docket stay — would eliminate the quorum restored December 2025. Congressional Democrats should introduce legislation tying NLRB appropriations to minimum quorum requirements, creating a statutory tripwire. This will not pass in the current Congress but creates a legislative record and forces Republicans to vote against worker protection in an election year.

**Amicus infrastructure for Trump v. Slaughter**: The AFL-CIO, SEIU, NAACP, and voting rights organizations (Brennan Center, ACLU, Demos) should file coordinated amicus briefs in Trump v. Slaughter specifically documenting the democratic participation consequences of eliminating NLRB independence — not just the economic effects. The precedential record created in amicus filings shapes how future courts interpret the holding and can narrow the scope of any adverse ruling.

**State NLRB analogues**: Eight states (California, New York, Minnesota, Illinois, Massachusetts, Oregon, Washington, New Jersey) have state labor relations boards that function as state-level NLRB analogues for state employees and in some cases private sector workers not covered by the NLRA. Strengthening these boards — through card check adoption, faster election procedures, and stronger remedies — creates a floor of worker protection that survives federal NLRB collapse.

### 6.2 Medium-Term (2027-2028): PRO Act and Post-SCOTUS Statutory Architecture

The Protecting the Right to Organize (PRO) Act, which has passed the House in previous Congresses but failed to overcome a Republican Senate filibuster, includes the following provisions most directly relevant to the democracy crisis framing:

- Elimination of state right-to-work laws under NLRA Section 14(b) — restoring federal floor on union security
- Prohibition on captive audience meetings during organizing campaigns
- Streamlined union election procedures reducing delay windows during which employers conduct anti-union campaigns
- Card check: majority card sign-up as the basis for recognition, reducing reliance on contested NLRB elections
- Meaningful financial penalties for NLRA violations (currently capped at essentially zero in most cases)
- Expanded definition of protected concerted activity for independent contractors and gig workers

If the 2026 midterms produce a Democratic House and a 51-seat Democratic Senate (requiring elimination of the filibuster or carve-out for labor legislation), the PRO Act becomes the medium-term legislative vehicle. The adoption pathway requires that the 2026 electoral coalition understand the NLRB crisis as the reason to vote — making the AFL-CIO's post-Slaughter GOTV operation the connective tissue between Section 6.1 and Section 6.2.

If Trump v. Slaughter eliminates Humphrey's Executor protections, a post-Slaughter PRO Act must also address the constitutional question directly: either by enacting a constitutional amendment protecting independent agency removal protections (a multi-year project requiring two-thirds of Congress and three-fourths of states) or by restructuring the NLRB as an Article III court — an approach that would provide judges rather than executive appointees as labor law adjudicators, insulated from removal by Article III's salary protection and good-behavior tenure.

### 6.3 Structural: Sectoral Bargaining and Co-Determination

The enterprise-based collective bargaining model of the NLRA — in which workers at each individual workplace must win a separate election, negotiate a separate contract, and maintain a separate union — is structurally inefficient for workers in high-turnover industries (fast food, retail, home care, gig economy) where individual workplace organizing is effectively impossible. The 5.9% private sector union density reflects, in part, the structural inadequacy of the enterprise bargaining model for the modern service economy.

Sectoral bargaining — in which standards are set across an industry rather than workplace-by-workplace — has been implemented in limited form in the United States through state experiments:

- California's Fast Food Council (2023): Sets wage and working condition standards for fast food workers across the sector statewide, under AB 1228
- Minnesota's Nursing Home Workforce Standards Board (2023): Sets sector-wide standards for nursing home staffing
- Seattle Domestic Workers Standards Board (2018): First domestic worker sectoral board in the United States
- Allegheny County, Pennsylvania (2025): Exploring models to expand bargaining rights for workers not covered by the NLRA

Vermont (2024) amended its state labor law to require employers to recognize unions based on verified majority showings of interest — card check — rather than requiring an election. Illinois enacted similar reforms in 2003; New Mexico in 2020. These state experiments create the legal architecture that a future federal PRO Act could nationalize.

Germany's Mitbestimmung model provides the most institutionally ambitious template. For companies with more than 2,000 employees, workers elect representatives to nearly half of the supervisory board. For companies with more than 500 employees, workers hold one-third of board seats. The NBER 2021 analysis found that codetermination had no negative effects on firm productivity, wages, or employment — and produced at least small positive effects on investment, job security, and worker satisfaction. The democratic governance argument — that workers who participate in enterprise governance develop civic participation capacity that transfers to broader political democracy — is empirically supported by the Nordic countries that combine high codetermination with high democratic participation.

Cooperative enterprise tax incentives — tax treatment equivalent to or superior to C-corporation treatment for worker-owned cooperatives — represent a market-compatible pathway to increased worker democratic participation within enterprises that does not require NLRB enforcement capacity. The Mondragon Corporation in the Basque Country (Spain) demonstrates the economic viability of large-scale cooperative enterprise at industrial scale. In the United States, the ESOP (Employee Stock Ownership Plan) tax structure already provides preferential tax treatment for partial worker ownership; extending equivalent treatment to full cooperatives would use existing tax infrastructure to incentivize the governance model without requiring new regulatory capacity.

## 7. Cross-Domain Integration

**Domain 6 (Judicial Independence)**: The NLRB's constitutional crisis is a direct application of Domain 6's central analysis. The Trump v. Wilcox shadow docket ruling (May 2025), D.C. Circuit merits ruling (December 2025), and pending Trump v. Slaughter decision (June 2026) are documented in Domain 6's Sections 1.1-1.3 and 2.3. The same removal-power doctrine that threatens NLRB independence threatens every other independent agency — the NLRB case study is the clearest example of how shadow docket jurisprudence produces irreversible structural effects before full merits review. Domain 45's evidence on democratic participation consequences provides the empirical foundation for Domain 6's abstract argument about why independent agency removal protections matter for democratic governance.

**Domain 29 (Prosecutorial Weaponization)**: The DOJ's enforcement posture intersects with NLRB enforcement in two ways. First, the January 2025 firing of NLRB General Counsel Abruzzo was coordinated with the replacement of DOL enforcement leadership, producing a synchronized reduction in federal labor law enforcement capacity across both agencies. Second, Protect Democracy's retaliatory action tracker documents instances of DOJ investigation of labor organizers in cases that appear pretextual — a pattern consistent with Domain 29's analysis of DOJ being used as a tool for political retaliation against administration opponents. The NLRB enforcement vacuum created by the quorum crisis redirects organizing disputes into federal court channels where the DOJ's involvement in amicus practice and settlement decisions creates additional executive influence over outcomes.

**Domain 38 (Financial Sector Independence / Corporate Capture)**: The SpaceX/Amazon/Starbucks/Trader Joe's litigation strategy for NLRB constitutionalization is the labor law parallel to the financial sector capture pattern Domain 38 documents. In both cases, the mechanism is the same: corporations with direct financial interest in weakening a regulatory agency fund litigation designed to eliminate the constitutional basis for that agency's independence, coordinating with an administration that shares the goal of agency capture. The constitutional strategy is more durable than regulatory rollback — it prevents statutory restoration by future Congresses — making it the preferred tool for capture that is intended to be permanent.

**Domain 22 (Racial Justice)**: BLS 2025 data shows that Black workers (12.7%) have the highest unionization rate of all major racial and ethnic groups, making the NLRB enforcement vacuum disproportionately harmful to Black workers whose recent organizing campaigns are the ones most pending before the agency. The Tennessee/Georgia/Alabama right-to-work-plus legislation denying state economic development funds to employers who voluntarily recognize unions is geographically concentrated in states with the largest Black-majority workplaces and the most active recent organizing campaigns. The EPI's research on voter suppression and union density documents that these same states pass the most voter restriction laws — the racial justice impact is compound: the same state legislatures suppressing Black organizing capacity are suppressing Black voting capacity through the same legislative mechanisms.

**Domain 44 (Reproductive Freedom)**: EPI research finds that union density is twice as high in abortion-protected states compared with abortion-restricted states. This correlation is not coincidental: the political coalitions that protect abortion access are substantially sustained by union political capacity, and the states that most aggressively restrict abortion access are right-to-work states with the lowest union density. The two forms of suppression — reproductive and labor — are exercised by the same legislative majorities, against substantially overlapping populations, through mutually reinforcing mechanisms. A democratic participation framework that treats reproductive suppression and union suppression as separate crises misses the structural feedback loop between them.
