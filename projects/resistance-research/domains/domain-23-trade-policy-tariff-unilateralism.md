## Executive Summary

The United States has experienced the most significant constitutional confrontation over trade authority since the 1930s. For more than a year, the executive branch imposed sweeping tariffs on the entire world's imports — covering more than $3 trillion in annual trade — using emergency powers that were never designed for peacetime tariff policy. On February 20, 2026, the Supreme Court held 6-3 in *Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump* that the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) does not authorize the president to impose tariffs. The ruling, authored by Chief Justice Roberts, applied the major questions doctrine and the Constitution's explicit assignment of taxing and revenue-raising authority to Congress.

The administration's response was immediate and revealing: within hours of the ruling, President Trump issued a proclamation imposing a 10% global tariff under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974 — a provision never previously invoked, designed for balance-of-payments emergencies, and capped at 150 days without congressional extension. That Section 122 tariff is now itself being challenged in the Court of International Trade by 24 state attorneys general and private importers, with oral argument heard April 10, 2026.

The structural accountability failure this domain addresses is not the specific tariff levels, which are subject to reasonable policy debate, but the mechanism: trade policy affecting the grocery prices, supply chains, employment prospects, and cost of living of 330 million Americans has been made, unmade, and remade by executive decree, without a single congressional vote on the substantive terms. The February 2026 SCOTUS ruling corrected the IEEPA overreach — but the administration immediately pivoted to the next available statutory hook, revealing that the underlying problem is not any single statute but the systematic erosion of congressional trade authority that has occurred across decades, leaving the executive branch with a wide menu of emergency and quasi-emergency powers to deploy.

The economic costs are documented. Yale Budget Lab estimates the existing tariff regime (post-IEEPA) will make the U.S. economy 0.1% smaller in the long run, equivalent to approximately $30 billion annually. The Tax Policy Center estimated average household costs of $1,050 in 2026 from the tariff regime. The Peterson Institute estimated the median household pays more than $1,200 per year — a burden that falls regressively, costing households in the bottom 10% a full 0.8% of after-tax income against a 0.3% loss for households in the top 10%. An 11-point drop in the net percent of small business owners reporting positive profit trends (NFIB March 2026 survey) reflects that the costs are concentrated in the most employment-intensive sector of the economy.

The reform agenda is tractable. Congressional tools for reclaiming trade authority exist: the Trade Review Act of 2025 (S.1272, bipartisan Grassley-Cantwell bill) would require congressional approval for tariffs that persist beyond 60 days. Trade Adjustment Assistance — the program that provides income support and retraining for workers displaced by trade policy — lapsed in 2022 and has not been reauthorized. International comparison reveals that every major democracy — the EU, Canada, Japan, and Australia — constrains executive trade authority through parliamentary approval requirements, judicial review mechanisms, or both, mechanisms that the U.S. IEEPA regime lacked entirely until the February 2026 ruling.

## Section 1: The Constitutional and Statutory Foundation

### 1.1 IEEPA Statutory Text, Legislative History, and Original Purpose

The International Emergency Economic Powers Act (50 U.S.C. §§ 1701–1708) was enacted in 1977 as a deliberate constraint on executive emergency power, not an expansion of it. Congress passed IEEPA alongside the National Emergencies Act of 1976 in direct response to congressional findings that the Trading with the Enemy Act (TWEA) — which had been used by presidents to assert sweeping economic authority during periods nominally defined as national emergencies — had been stretched far beyond its original wartime purpose.

The legislative history is explicit on intent. Congress found that "emergencies are by their nature rare and brief, and are not to be equated with normal ongoing problems." IEEPA was designed, in the words of the Senate Foreign Relations Committee, to create "both more limited in scope" authorities than those under TWEA, "subject to procedural limitations." The statute requires: (1) a declaration of national emergency with respect to an "unusual and extraordinary threat" with its source "in whole or substantial part outside the United States" to national security, foreign policy, or the economy; (2) transmission of that declaration to Congress; (3) annual review with an automatic termination unless Congress renews or the president terminates.

Section 1702 enumerates the president's authorities once an emergency is declared: to "investigate, regulate, or prohibit" transactions in foreign exchange; to block property in which a foreign nation or foreign national has an interest; to confiscate property when the U.S. faces armed hostilities; and related economic sanction powers. The statute's specific grant is to "regulate...any transactions in foreign exchange, transfers of credit or payments between, by, through, or to any banking institution, and the importing or exporting of currency or securities." The word "tariffs" does not appear. The word "duties" does not appear. The words "taxes," "revenue," and "customs" do not appear.

Section 1705 establishes criminal penalties for violations: a person who "willfully commits, willfully attempts to commit, or willfully conspires to commit, or aids or abets with respect to, any act prohibited" under an IEEPA order may be fined up to $1,000,000 and imprisoned for up to 20 years. This enforcement apparatus is the lever that gives IEEPA orders teeth.

IEEPA was first invoked by President Jimmy Carter on November 14, 1979 — 10 days after Iranian students seized the U.S. embassy and took American diplomats hostage. The use was precisely what Congress intended: blocking Iranian government assets held in U.S. financial institutions, preventing their use while a hostage crisis constituted an actual national emergency. Carter's second order expanded the scope to block virtually all financial transfers to Iran. The purpose was financial coercion in an acute crisis, not general trade regulation.

Subsequent IEEPA uses followed the same pattern: sanctions on Libya (1986), South Africa (1986), Panama (1988), Yugoslavia (1991), Iraq (1990 asset freeze), Haiti (1994), Sudan (1997), narcotics traffickers designated under EO 12978 (1995), and eventually the vast apparatus of specially designated nationals (SDN) lists administered by the Treasury Department's Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC). By 2025, 77 national emergencies had been invoked using IEEPA, with 46 remaining active — including Carter's 1979 Iran declaration, still renewed annually by every successive president for 46 years, primarily to provide legal basis for administering unresolved asset disputes.

None of these prior uses imposed broad tariffs on all imports from any country, let alone all imports from all trading partners globally.

### 1.2 The February 20, 2026 Supreme Court Ruling: What It Decided

In *Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump* and *Trump v. V.O.S. Selections, Inc.* (607 U.S. ___ (2026)), argued November 5, 2025, and decided February 20, 2026, the Supreme Court held 6-3 that IEEPA does not authorize the president to impose tariffs.

The majority opinion by Chief Justice Roberts was joined by Justices Sotomayor, Kagan, Gorsuch, Barrett, and Jackson — a cross-ideological coalition reflecting the breadth of agreement that the IEEPA tariff theory was constitutionally untenable. The decision affirmed a prior ruling by the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit.

**What the ruling held.** The Court's central holding was that IEEPA's grant of authority to "regulate...importation" does not encompass tariff authority. The reasoning operates at three levels:

First, constitutional structure: the Constitution assigns to Congress the power to "lay and collect Taxes, Duties, Imposts and Excises" (Article I, Section 8). Tariffs are, in the Court's formulation, "very clearly...a branch of the taxing power." An executive statute that imposes tariffs is imposing taxes — a core congressional prerogative. Shifting this power to the president requires explicit statutory authorization; the word "regulate" does not supply it.

Second, statutory text and history: IEEPA's text mentions none of the vocabulary of tariff legislation — no rates, no schedules, no revenue-raising mechanism. Prior tariff delegations (Section 201, Section 301, Section 232) include explicit rate caps, time limits, procedural requirements, and specified triggering conditions. IEEPA lacks all of these features for tariff purposes, which the Court treated as evidence that Congress did not intend to delegate tariff authority through IEEPA.

Third, the major questions doctrine: Chief Justice Roberts, joined by Justices Gorsuch and Barrett in a separate concurrence, would have applied the major questions doctrine more explicitly, treating the question of whether a statute of unclear scope authorizes a decision of vast economic and political significance as requiring "clear congressional authorization." The IEEPA tariffs — covering effectively all of U.S. import trade, generating hundreds of billions in revenue annually — are precisely the kind of consequential action that requires a clear congressional statement.

**What the ruling did not decide.** The Court did not address: whether Section 232 tariffs (national security basis) or Section 301 tariffs (trade practice retaliation) are constitutional — those statutes were not challenged and remain in effect. The Court did not address the political question doctrine: the government argued that the Court lacked authority to review presidential emergency declarations; the Court implicitly rejected this argument by reaching the merits without extended discussion, but did not write a detailed rebuttal of political question doctrine applicability. The Court did not resolve what happens to tariff revenues already collected under IEEPA — estimated at over $264 billion in fiscal year 2025 — leaving a major refund dispute for subsequent litigation.

**The immediate administrative response.** President Trump issued an Executive Order terminating the IEEPA tariff orders the same day. Within hours, he issued a Proclamation invoking Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974 to impose a 10% ad valorem tariff on most imported goods, effective February 24, 2026, for 150 days (expiring July 24, 2026 unless Congress extends it). The administration also announced broad new Section 301 investigations targeting multiple trading partners, signaling the long-term replacement strategy.

### 1.3 The Section 122 Pivot and Remaining Tariff Architecture

Following the February 20 ruling, the tariff regime did not end. It was restructured.

**Section 122 (Trade Act of 1974, 19 U.S.C. § 2132)** authorizes the president to impose a temporary import surcharge of up to 15% for up to 150 days to address "fundamental international payments problems," including "large and serious United States balance-of-payments deficits." Congress designed Section 122 specifically for short-term currency and balance-of-payments emergencies. In 52 years since its enactment, it had never been invoked. The Trump administration's Proclamation of February 24, 2026, marked its first-ever use.

The Section 122 tariff (10% global surcharge) is subject to multiple constraints IEEPA lacked: the 15% rate cap prevents escalation beyond a defined ceiling; the 150-day limit means the tariff dies on July 24, 2026, absent congressional action to extend it; the triggering standard — a "fundamental international payments problem" — is specific enough that courts may review whether the factual predicate actually exists.

On March 5, 2026, 24 state attorneys general filed suit in the U.S. Court of International Trade challenging the Section 122 tariff, arguing: (1) no balance-of-payments problem actually exists; (2) the tariff violates the nondelegation doctrine; and (3) even within its authorized scope, the statutory requirements have not been met. The Liberty Justice Center filed a parallel challenge on behalf of small importers. Oral argument was heard April 10, 2026; a ruling is expected before the 150-day expiration.

**Section 301 investigations** (Trade Act of 1974, 19 U.S.C. § 2411) provide the administration's long-term replacement strategy. The USTR launched broad Section 301 investigations in March 2026 targeting multiple trading partners for excess supply capacity, forced labor practices, and digital protectionism. Section 301 tariffs have no time limit and no rate cap — they can remain indefinitely if the USTR determines that a foreign practice "burdens or restricts United States commerce." The procedural requirements (public comment, USTR determination) provide process, but not meaningful congressional oversight.

**Section 232 tariffs** (Trade Expansion Act of 1962, 19 U.S.C. § 1862) based on national security findings remain in effect on steel and aluminum (25% and 10% respectively, from the first Trump term) and on certain imports from specific countries. These were not challenged in *Learning Resources* and remain legally unaffected by the ruling.

**What this means for accountability.** The ruling eliminated the most egregious IEEPA overreach, but the replacement architecture is no more democratically accountable than what preceded it. Section 122 is unilateral executive action with a time limit; Section 301 is unilateral executive action without one. The common denominator is that neither requires a congressional vote on the specific tariff terms. The elected legislators who represent affected constituencies — small business owners, farmers whose inputs cost more, manufacturers dependent on imported components, consumers paying higher prices at checkout — have no statutory mechanism requiring them to vote yes or no on the tariff regime they are being held responsible for at the polls.

### 1.4 Historical Precedent: Emergency Power vs. Tariff Authority

The IEEPA tariff theory was a genuinely novel assertion of presidential power. Prior peacetime tariff authority operated through a fundamentally different mechanism.

**Smoot-Hawley (1930)** is the historical benchmark that the current period most closely resembles in economic scale. The Tariff Act of 1930 raised tariffs on over 20,000 imported goods to an average effective rate peaking at 19.8% in 1933. It triggered international retaliation that deepened the Great Depression. It was also constitutionally unambiguous: Senators Reed Smoot and Representative Willis C. Hawley introduced legislation, Congress debated it for years, the House passed it, the Senate amended it, a conference committee reconciled the versions, and President Hoover signed the resulting statute. The tariffs were high, the policy was disastrous by most historical assessments — and it was enacted through the constitutionally prescribed process for exercising congressional taxing authority.

The Trade Agreements Act of 1934 deliberately shifted the mechanism: Congress authorized the executive to negotiate reciprocal tariff reductions with specific countries, subject to caps, and to implement those reductions without returning to Congress. This delegation was limited to reductions, had defined rate limits, and operated in the context of a global move toward liberalized trade.

GATT (1947) and the subsequent GATT/WTO framework maintained the structure: congressional authorization of tariff schedules, with executive authority to negotiate adjustments within defined parameters. The fast-track (now trade promotion authority, TPA) mechanism gives the president authority to negotiate trade agreements but requires Congress to vote yes or no on the resulting package, without amendment but with a binding up-or-down resolution.

The IEEPA tariff assertion was categorically different from all of this history: using a sanctions statute to assert tariff authority with no rate cap, no time limit, no specific factual predicate, and no congressional ratification requirement — on all imports from all countries simultaneously, for reasons that shifted between declared national emergencies as the legal theory was tested.

## Section 2: Democratic Accountability Failure

### 2.1 Who Authorized These Tariffs?

The core accountability problem is straightforward. The IEEPA tariff regime — imposing hundreds of billions of dollars in new tax burdens on U.S. importers and, through pass-through, on American consumers — was created by a single executive declaration of national emergency. No member of Congress voted on the tariff schedule. No committee markup occurred. No floor amendment was offered to protect specific industries or constituencies. The Farm Bureau, the Chamber of Commerce, the NFIB, and individual importers all had opportunities to comment only through informal channels, not through the formal notice-and-comment requirements that govern agency rulemaking under the Administrative Procedure Act.

This is not merely an abstract separation-of-powers complaint. Trade policy is tax policy imposed on every purchase of imported goods. The constitutional assignment of "all Bills for raising Revenue" to the House of Representatives — the chamber closest to ordinary voters by design — was not an accidental placement. The Framers, schooled on the English experience of taxation without representation, placed revenue authority in the most accountable chamber precisely because taxation is where the interests of ordinary voters and executive preferences most predictably diverge.

The practical accountability failure is visible in the political record. Members of Congress from districts with significant import-dependent manufacturing — automotive suppliers in Michigan, toy importers in California, furniture makers in North Carolina — faced constituent pressure from industries being harmed by tariffs on their inputs. But they had no mechanism to compel a vote. The Trade Review Act of 2025 (S.1272, introduced by Senators Grassley and Cantwell with 13 co-sponsors) would have required congressional approval within 60 days; the administration threatened a veto before the bill came to a floor vote.

### 2.2 Who Can Undo Them?

Congressional reclamation mechanisms exist but have been politically blocked.

**Congressional Review Act (CRA)** repeal of specific tariff proclamations is theoretically available but practically limited. The CRA applies to "rules" submitted to Congress by agencies; presidential proclamations are not agency rules and fall outside the CRA's standard mechanism. A joint resolution of Congress directly revoking tariff proclamations under Section 122 or Section 301 would require the president's signature — which means, absent a supermajority sufficient to override a veto, the president retains effective veto power over any congressional attempt to undo the tariff regime.

**Trade Review Act (S.1272)** would change the default: under its terms, tariffs automatically expire after 60 days without congressional approval, rather than requiring affirmative congressional action to terminate them. The administration's statement of administration policy explicitly opposed this bill, characterizing it as an unconstitutional constraint on presidential authority — a remarkable claim given that Congress's constitutional authority over tariffs is unambiguous.

**IEEPA Amendment** is the most direct structural reform: amending 50 U.S.C. §§ 1701-1708 to explicitly exclude tariff authority from the president's emergency powers would eliminate future IEEPA tariff claims at the statutory level. Such an amendment would require 60 Senate votes or a majority in a Congress willing to operate under filibuster reform.

**Veto-override**: A two-thirds majority in both chambers to override a presidential veto is achievable only in extraordinary political circumstances. The current alignment does not approach this threshold on trade policy.

The political viability assessment is sobering: meaningful congressional reclamation of tariff authority requires either a bipartisan supermajority willing to confront a president of their own party (as in the Senate's 49-51 rejection of S.1272 in April 2025, where all but two Republican senators voted to table the bill following the administration's veto threat) or a change in legislative majorities. The latter is the primary pathway: both the House and Senate seats most affected by tariff costs — in manufacturing-heavy swing districts — are also the districts most competitive in 2026 and 2028.

### 2.3 Documented Economic Effects

**Household costs — regressive tax structure.** The Tax Policy Center estimates tariffs announced through December 2025 impose an average burden of approximately $1,050 per household in 2026. The Peterson Institute for International Economics estimated that the IEEPA tariff package (before the February 2026 ruling) would cost the typical household more than $1,200 annually. The distribution is strikingly regressive: households in the bottom 10% lose 0.8% of after-tax income; households in the top 10% lose 0.3%. The CNBC analysis of household-level effects found that lower-income families, who spend a higher share of income on physical goods (food, clothing, electronics, household products) and a lower share on services, bear a disproportionately larger burden. Only households in the top fifth of the income distribution would enjoy a net gain from the combined effects of tariffs and concurrent tax changes — for the bottom 60%, the net effect is significantly negative.

**Small business impact.** The NFIB survey record documents the trajectory. Direct tariff payments by small businesses increased 91% in May 2025 compared to the 2022 baseline. The November 2025 NFIB survey recorded a 13-point jump in the net percent of owners raising average selling prices — the largest monthly jump in the survey's history. By March 2026, the Small Business Optimism Index decline was primarily driven by an 11-point drop in positive profit trends. The Bank of America Small Business Checkpoint found that small business profit growth turned negative for the first time in 18 months by December 2025, citing tariff drag explicitly. Small businesses are more exposed to tariff costs than large corporations because: they have lower access to capital to absorb short-term cost spikes, they have less supplier diversification, and they lack the transfer pricing mechanisms that allow multinationals to relocate sourcing inside corporate structures.

**Supply chain disruption.** Tariff-induced supply chain changes have been most severe in three sectors. Semiconductor inputs for consumer electronics, vehicles, and industrial equipment — heavily sourced from East Asia, with complex interdependencies between U.S. fabs and Asian suppliers — created cost pressure throughout the electronics supply chain. Automotive supply chains, which depend on integrated cross-border production (especially U.S.-Canada-Mexico), were severely disrupted by the IEEPA tariffs before the February 2026 ruling; the Section 122 replacement tariff continues to impose costs on assembled vehicles and components. Pharmaceutical manufacturing — approximately 80% of active pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs) used by U.S. drug manufacturers are produced outside the United States, primarily in India and China — faced both cost increases and uncertainty-driven sourcing disruption.

**Tariff revenue and its distribution.** Customs duties totaled $195 billion in fiscal year 2025 — more than double the prior year's $76 billion projection. CBO projects customs duties at approximately 1.3% of GDP in 2026. This revenue flows entirely into the Treasury's general fund: it is not earmarked for trade adjustment assistance, not returned to affected workers and industries, and not applied to the deficit reduction that tariff advocates sometimes cite. The disconnect between tariff revenue collection and trade adjustment support is not accidental. Trade Adjustment Assistance — the program that provides income support, retraining, and job search assistance to workers displaced by trade policy — lapsed on July 1, 2022 under a termination provision and has not been reauthorized. S.1449 (Trade Adjustment Assistance Reauthorization Act of 2025) was referred to the Senate Finance Committee in April 2025 and has received no floor action. Workers who lose jobs to import competition, or who lose them to the manufacturing contraction produced by costly imported inputs, have no dedicated program providing income support or retraining.

**GDP and macroeconomic effects.** The Yale Budget Lab's analysis following the February 20 ruling estimated that the remaining tariff regime (Section 122 at 10%, Section 301 tariffs, and Section 232 tariffs) will make the U.S. economy persistently 0.1% smaller in the long run — approximately $30 billion annually in 2025 dollars. The unemployment rate is projected to be 0.3 percentage points higher by end of 2026 than it would be absent tariffs. If the IEEPA tariff regime had not been struck down and remained at full force, the long-run output hit would have been 0.3% of GDP — approximately $90 billion annually. The effective average tariff rate facing consumers is now 9.1% — the highest since 1946 (excluding 2025).

### 2.4 Congressional Authorization Erosion: The Historical Arc

The current situation did not emerge from a single statute or a single administration. It is the product of a decades-long arc of congressional delegation that progressively stripped Article I trade authority from the institution constitutionally assigned to hold it.

Smoot-Hawley (1930) was the last time Congress directly set comprehensive tariff schedules through the full legislative process. The Trade Agreements Act of 1934 began the delegation: Congress authorized the executive to negotiate reductions. GATT (1947), the Trade Expansion Act of 1962 (which created Section 232), the Trade Act of 1974 (which created Sections 122 and 301 and the fast-track mechanism), and IEEPA (1977) each added new executive tariff tools. Fast-track authority (now TPA, most recently the Trade Priorities and Accountability Act of 2015) formalized the practice of Congress voting yes or no on negotiated agreements without line-item input.

The net result by 2025 was an executive trade toolkit with multiple legal hooks: Section 232 for national security; Section 301 for trade practice retaliation; Section 201 for import surges; Section 122 for balance-of-payments emergencies; and IEEPA for anything declared an emergency. The February 2026 ruling removed one hook. The others remain.

The Smoot-Hawley contrast is instructive not because that law was good policy — it was not — but because the constitutional mechanism was correct. Members of Congress faced constituent accountability for every tariff they voted for. Senators from wheat-exporting states voted knowing that trading partners would retaliate against U.S. agricultural exports. They were wrong in their economic predictions, but they were accountable for the decision. The current architecture produces the reverse: voters experience the costs of tariff policy through their wallets, while their elected representatives lack the statutory mechanism to force a vote.

## Section 3: Democratic Theory — Why This Matters

Trade policy is not abstract governance theory. It is the grocery bill. Tariffs on imported goods are taxes on consumption — taxes that fall, as documented above, more heavily on lower-income households that spend a higher proportion of income on physical goods. The political science literature on taxation has consistently found that consumption taxes are among the least transparent forms of revenue collection precisely because they are embedded in prices, not itemized on a bill to the taxpayer. A 10% global tariff is a 10% consumption tax increase on imported goods, borne by U.S. consumers and businesses, that was implemented without a single vote in the body the Constitution assigns authority to "lay and collect Taxes, Duties, Imposts and Excises."

The democratic theory concern operates across three dimensions.

**Representation.** When consequential taxation decisions are made by executive decree, the representative connection between voter preference and policy outcome is severed. Voters in manufacturing-dependent swing districts who experience job losses from tariff-induced supply chain disruption have representatives who cast no vote that could have produced a different outcome. This is not a partisan concern: the IEEPA tariffs imposed costs on Republican-voting agricultural export communities (through trading-partner retaliation) equally with Democratic-voting import-dependent urban communities.

**Accountability.** The principle that the power to tax must be subject to democratic accountability is not merely procedural. It is the historical foundation of parliamentary government in England (no taxation without representation), the grievance that produced the American Revolution, and the explicit constitutional design choice. IEEPA tariff authority — which the February 2026 ruling correctly identified as an unconstitutional transfer of congressional taxing power — operated for more than a year as a mechanism by which one person, the president, exercised the taxing power of the entire federal government with respect to imports. That this required judicial correction, rather than congressional self-defense, illustrates the depth of legislative abdication.

**Connection to adjacent domains.** Domain 2 (executive accountability and civil service) documents the broader pattern of executive institutions operating without effective congressional oversight. Trade policy is a particularly visible example because its costs are concrete and diffuse — every household that purchases imported goods is paying the tariff — while the political accountability for those costs is concentrated on a single individual who made the decision by proclamation. Domain 5 (fiscal structure) connects: tariff revenue flowing into the general fund without earmarking for affected communities represents a fiscal transfer from the households bearing consumption costs to the general treasury, with no statutory mechanism ensuring those bearing the burden see any of the benefit. Domain 20 (economic concentration) connects: large multinationals are better positioned to absorb tariff costs through diversified supply chains and transfer pricing than small and medium-sized enterprises that depend on specific import relationships.

## Section 4: International Precedent

Every major democracy that could be cited as a model for U.S. governance constrains executive trade authority through mechanisms that the U.S. IEEPA regime entirely lacked. This is not a matter of degree but of structure: in the EU, Canada, Japan, and Australia, there is no legal pathway for the executive to unilaterally impose comprehensive peacetime tariffs without either parliamentary approval or meaningful judicial review. The U.S. is the outlier.

### 4.1 European Union: Parliament Approval Under Article 207 TFEU

Under Article 207 of the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union (TFEU), trade policy — including changes in tariff rates — is an exclusive EU competence, but it operates through a defined institutional framework that requires European Parliament involvement. The European Parliament and the Council jointly adopt "measures defining the framework for implementing the common commercial policy" through the ordinary legislative procedure. The Commission negotiates trade agreements and tariff changes on behalf of the EU, but agreements require the Parliament's consent by a simple majority before entering into force.

For autonomous tariff measures — not negotiated agreements but unilateral tariff changes — the EU uses Council regulations adopted through qualified majority voting, with parliamentary consultation. Emergency import measures of short duration can be adopted by the Commission, but they are time-limited, subject to Council reversal, and cannot constitute a general tariff regime imposed unilaterally without institutional process.

There is no EU analog to IEEPA tariff authority. The closest equivalent — trade defense instruments (anti-dumping, countervailing duties, safeguards) — all require investigations, findings, proportionality assessments, and are subject to review by the European Court of Justice and the WTO Appellate Body when it functions. The ECJ has struck down EU trade defense measures that exceeded proportionality requirements; courts are not excluded from reviewing executive trade claims.

The democratic accountability mechanism is clear: when the EU raises tariffs, the European Parliament has had a role. Members of the Parliament are directly accountable to European voters for their votes on trade measures.

Sources: [EUR-Lex: Article 207 TFEU text](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/treaty/tfeu_2016/art_207/oj/eng); [Intereconomics: EU Trade Policymaking after Lisbon](https://www.intereconomics.eu/contents/year/2010/number/1/article/eu-trade-and-investment-policymaking-after-the-lisbon-treaty.html)

### 4.2 Canada: Parliamentary Involvement and Judicial Review

Canada's trade policy framework under the Customs Tariff (R.S.C. 1985, c. 41) vests tariff authority primarily in Parliament, with the Governor in Council (executive) authorized to adjust tariffs within prescribed limits set by statute. General tariff rate changes require amendments to Schedule A of the Customs Tariff, which requires parliamentary action.

For emergency trade remedies — anti-dumping and countervailing duties — Canada uses the Special Import Measures Act (SIMA, R.S.C. 1985, c. S-15). SIMA requires an investigation process conducted by both the Canada Border Services Agency (factual determination) and the Canadian International Trade Tribunal (injury determination). Both determinations are subject to judicial review: the Federal Court of Appeal reviews CITT findings and CBSA determinations under the Federal Courts Act. The review procedure is built into the statute at Section 96.1.

Canada does not have a statutory mechanism equivalent to IEEPA. The Governor in Council has emergency import authority under the Export and Import Permits Act and the Customs Tariff in defined circumstances, but that authority is narrower and more targeted than IEEPA, does not include peacetime authority to impose general tariffs on all imports from all countries, and is explicitly subject to judicial review of its factual predicate.

The SNC-Lavalin affair (2019) — in which the Prime Minister's Office was accused of pressuring the Attorney General to intervene in a criminal prosecution — demonstrated the limits of Canada's accountability mechanisms when political pressure is applied covertly. But on trade specifically, the statutory framework does not provide a Canadian president-equivalent with unilateral comprehensive tariff authority.

Sources: [Canadian SIMA text: laws-lois.justice.gc.ca](https://laws-lois.justice.gc.ca/eng/acts/s-15/FullText.html); [Canadian International Trade Tribunal: SIMA amendments](https://www.citt-tcce.gc.ca/en/news/amendments-special-import-measures-act-and-canadian-international-trade-tribunal-act)

### 4.3 Japan: Diet Approval Required

Japan's constitutional structure vests the Diet as "the highest organ of state power" and the "sole law-making organ of the State" (Article 41 of the Constitution of Japan). Tariff changes require amendment of the Customs Tariff Law (関税定率法), which is Diet legislation. The Cabinet may implement tariff changes only within the bounds of Diet-established schedules or through emergency tariff provisions that are explicitly time-limited and subject to Diet ratification.

The Cabinet's emergency powers under the Constitution (Article 54) are limited: they apply to convening emergency sessions of the House of Councillors, and measures taken at such sessions "shall become null and void unless agreed to by the House of Representatives within a period of ten (10) days after the opening of the next session of the Diet." Japan does not have a statutory analog to IEEPA's peacetime emergency tariff authority.

The U.S.-Japan Framework Agreement (September 2025), implemented by presidential action, required Diet consideration on the Japanese side — the U.S. president bound the country through executive proclamation while Japan's Diet reviewed the implications of the corresponding obligations. This asymmetry — unilateral U.S. executive action paired with Japanese parliamentary process — illustrates the international accountability gap.

Sources: [CRS: U.S. Tariffs and the 2025 U.S.-Japan Framework Agreement](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IN12608); [Chapter 4, The Diet — Constitutional Revision in Japan](https://www.crjapan.org/chapters/chapter-4-diet)

### 4.4 Australia: Statutory Authority and Judicial Reviewability

Australia's Customs Tariff Act 1995 sets tariff schedules through parliamentary legislation. The Governor-General may make regulations adjusting tariff rates within limits authorized by the Customs Tariff Act — but those limits are set by Parliament, not by the executive. Emergency tariff authority under the Customs Act 1901 exists but is explicitly statutory and requires the Governor-General (acting on Cabinet advice) to act within the bounds Parliament has defined.

Australia's separation of powers doctrine — developed through the High Court's application of the Australian Constitution's three-chapter structure (Parliament in Chapter I, Executive in Chapter II, Judiciary in Chapter III) — creates strong judicial review of executive claims to exercise power not clearly authorized by Parliament. The High Court's jurisprudence on the limits of executive power (including the Constitutional requirement that executive power "pertain to the Commonwealth") has been applied to trade and customs matters to ensure executive action stays within statutory authority.

The research brief mentions *Plaintiff M70/2011* — that case, *Plaintiff M70/2011 v Minister for Immigration and Citizenship* [2011] HCA 32, addresses refugee processing arrangements rather than trade authority directly, but it illustrates the principle that the High Court reviews executive claims to exercise statutory power and will reject such claims where Parliament has not clearly authorized the exercise. The general principle of judicial reviewability of executive tariff authority is well-established in Australian law; there is no analog to IEEPA's effective insulation from judicial review that characterized the pre-ruling period.

**The cross-country pattern.** In every major democracy examined — EU, Canada, Japan, Australia — executive tariff authority is either (a) derived from explicit parliamentary authorization with defined limits, (b) subject to judicial review of whether factual predicates exist, or (c) both. The pre-ruling U.S. IEEPA regime was neither: it was derived from an emergency statute with no tariff-specific authorization, and the political question doctrine argument (which the government pressed and the Court implicitly rejected) would have exempted even the factual predicate from judicial review. The February 2026 ruling corrected this, but only for IEEPA. Section 301 and Section 232 tariffs remain subject to judicial deference to executive national security and trade practice determinations that are largely unreviewable in practice.

Sources: [Australian Customs Tariff Act 1995](https://www.legislation.gov.au/Series/C2004A04997); [Separation of Powers in Australia — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_of_powers_in_Australia); [Parliamentary Education Office: Separation of Powers](https://peo.gov.au/understand-our-parliament/how-parliament-works/system-of-government/separation-of-powers-parliament-executive-and-judiciary)

## Section 5: Structural Reform Pathways

### 5a. IEEPA Statutory Amendment: Narrow Emergency Authority, Exclude Tariff Power

The February 2026 ruling held that IEEPA does not authorize tariffs as a matter of statutory interpretation. That holding could be reversed by a future Supreme Court if the administration finds new statutory language to argue or new justices interpret the major questions doctrine differently. The durable fix is statutory: amend IEEPA to explicitly exclude tariff authority from the president's emergency powers.

**The specific amendment** should insert in Section 1702 the language: "Nothing in this chapter shall be construed to authorize the President to impose or increase tariffs, customs duties, ad valorem charges, or other taxes on imports." This negative limitation is complementary to the Court's ruling and forecloses any argument that a subsequent IEEPA invocation with new statutory language could revive tariff authority.

The amendment should also address the scope creep that produced 77 declared national emergencies (46 still active): require that all active IEEPA emergency declarations be reviewed by Congress within 12 months; establish a sunset of 2 years for any new emergency declaration unless affirmatively renewed by joint resolution; and require the president to specify, in writing filed with Congress, the specific measures being taken under IEEPA authority and their legal basis.

The restoration of time-limit discipline was a feature of the original IEEPA design. Congress's 1977 finding that "emergencies are by their nature rare and brief" was not implemented with enforcement mechanisms strong enough to prevent accumulation of permanent emergencies. A statutory limit — maximum of five simultaneous active IEEPA emergencies, with automatic termination of the oldest unless renewed — would restore the exception's exceptional character.

*Legislative vehicle*: The IEEPA Reform Act has been introduced in various forms. The post-*Learning Resources* congressional landscape includes bipartisan recognition that IEEPA was being used in ways Congress did not intend; the majority opinion's statutory analysis provides the intellectual foundation for amendment language.

*Political pathway*: IEEPA amendment is more achievable than tariff rate legislation because it is procedural rather than substantive — it does not take a position on what tariff levels are appropriate, only on who decides. Bipartisan coalitions concerned about executive overreach (which exist in both the national security and economic policy contexts) are the natural constituency.

### 5b. Congressional Reclamation of Trade Authority: Trade Review Act Model

The Trade Review Act of 2025 (S.1272, Grassley-Cantwell) represents the most immediately deployable vehicle for restoring congressional trade authority. Under its terms:

- The president must notify Congress within 48 hours of imposing or increasing a tariff, providing an economic justification and impact assessment
- Any new or increased tariff automatically expires after 60 days unless Congress enacts a joint resolution of approval
- Congress may terminate any existing tariff by passing a joint resolution of disapproval at any time

The automatic-expiration default is the critical design feature. It flips the current structure — in which congressional action is required to terminate tariffs — to a structure in which congressional inaction terminates tariffs. This is constitutionally appropriate: if Congress has not affirmatively authorized the specific tariff, it dies. The president retains the authority to propose tariffs and the initiative to invoke emergency or trade practice authorities, but the final taxing decision remains with the legislature.

S.1272 had 13 cosponsors (7 Republican, 6 Democratic) in the 119th Congress and received a veto threat from the administration before reaching a floor vote. The political path to enactment requires either a Senate majority willing to override a veto — which at current alignments requires approximately 16 Republican senators to join all Democrats — or a post-2026 majority coalition that includes enough House and Senate members from tariff-affected districts to produce the necessary votes. The economic costs documented above are most concentrated in swing-district communities, which is both the political risk for incumbents who supported the tariff regime and the political opportunity for reform coalitions.

*Companion legislation*: The Tariff Act of 1930 (Smoot-Hawley) still contains Section 338, which grants the president authority to impose discriminatory tariffs against countries that treat U.S. exports unfavorably. Amending the Tariff Act to eliminate Section 338's presidential discretion and bring it within the Trade Review Act's notification and approval framework would close another unilateral authority gap.

### 5c. Judicial Enforcement: Restoring Reviewability of Tariff Authority Claims

The political question doctrine argument — which the *Learning Resources* government brief pressed and the Court implicitly rejected — was the most dangerous element of the IEEPA tariff litigation. Had the Court applied the political question doctrine to executive emergency declarations, it would have foreclosed judicial review of any tariff imposed under an IEEPA emergency, regardless of its departure from statutory authorization.

The Court's willingness to reach the merits without extensive political question discussion is a significant ruling: it reaffirms that courts can review whether a statutory grant authorizes executive action, even when the executive characterizes the action as an emergency measure. This ruling should be consolidated through litigation strategy that develops the reviewability principle in lower courts.

**Standing for congressional members**: The *Raines v. Byrd* (1997) holding restricted standing for individual congressional members to challenge executive actions. That restriction has been applied to block Congress from litigating war powers and emergency powers challenges. Statutory congressional standing provisions — which the War Powers domain (Domain 28) also advocates — would allow the Senate or House as institutions to bring emergency power challenges where individual member standing is unavailable.

**Standing for importers and small businesses**: Post-*Learning Resources*, importer standing in the Court of International Trade is established. The Section 122 litigation confirms that importers who pay tariffs have direct Article III injury. Expanding this standing precedent and ensuring fee-shifting provisions for successful challenges would create a self-funding litigation ecosystem for challenging future emergency tariff claims.

**Section 301 reviewability**: Section 301 tariff determinations receive significant deference in courts because the statute vests in the USTR broad discretion to determine what foreign practices "burden or restrict" U.S. commerce. Amending Section 301 to require specific factual findings — not merely a discretionary determination — and to make those findings reviewable for substantial evidence would bring Section 301 tariffs into a judicially reviewable framework similar to the anti-dumping and countervailing duty regime.

### 5d. WTO Re-engagement and Appellate Body Restoration

The United States has blocked new appointments to the WTO Appellate Body since 2016, using the blockage as leverage for reform of WTO dispute settlement procedures the U.S. viewed as overreaching. As of September 2025, the U.S. had cast its 90th consecutive veto against Appellate Body appointment proposals, supported by 130 WTO members. The result: 32 dispute panel rulings have been "appealed into the void," the number of new WTO dispute filings has fallen to approximately one-third of pre-paralysis levels, and the MPIA (Multi-Party Interim Appeal Arbitration Arrangement, led by the EU) has settled only two disputes in four years.

The WTO dispute settlement system is imperfect, and U.S. concerns about Appellate Body overreach have some substantive basis. But the consequence of paralysis — an international trading system where countries that lose dispute panels can simply refuse enforcement — is worse for the world economy's most powerful exporter and importer than a reformed but functional Appellate Body would be.

**The reform path**: Re-engage WTO reform negotiations with a specific proposal for Appellate Body reconstitution that addresses the U.S.'s legitimate structural concerns (terms of reference, deference to domestic policy space, precedent accumulation) while restoring the dispute settlement function. The USTR — whose role has been redirected under the current administration from negotiated trade to unilateral tariff deployment — should be restored to its negotiating function. WTO re-engagement is a second-term or post-2028 priority, contingent on political change; but the groundwork (U.S. proposal drafting, coalition-building with EU, Japan, and Canada, MPIA bridging strategy) can be built in opposition.

Sources: [CSIS: WTO Appellate Body Crisis](https://www.csis.org/programs/economics-program-and-scholl-chair-international-business/world-trade-organization); [Harvard ILJ: Can the WTO be Saved?](https://journals.law.harvard.edu/ilj/2025/12/can-the-wto-be-saved-from-its-existential-crisis/); [European Parliament: WTO Appellate Body Crisis brief](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/thinktank/en/document/EPRS_BRI(2024)762342)

### 5e. Trade Adjustment Assistance: Restore and Expand the Safety Net

Trade Adjustment Assistance (TAA) was the statutory acknowledgment that trade liberalization produces winners and losers, and that the losers deserve dedicated support rather than nominal eligibility for general unemployment insurance. The program provided: income support through Trade Readjustment Allowances (TRA) for workers whose jobs were certified as lost to import competition; retraining funding for workers displaced to new industries; job search allowances; relocation assistance; and Reemployment TAA (RTAA) wage supplements for older workers who take lower-paying jobs.

TAA lapsed on July 1, 2022 under a termination provision and has not been reauthorized. This lapse was a product of both political calculation (the program was linked to trade promotion authority that conservatives opposed in 2015, and the linkage continued to poison its renewal) and administrative failure (program enrollment was complex, benefit determination took too long, and workers in immediate need often exhausted eligibility through the application process). S.1449 (Trade Adjustment Assistance Reauthorization Act of 2025) would reauthorize the program but has received no floor action.

**The reauthorization case in the current moment is stronger than it has ever been.** The tariff regime has imposed documented costs on specific identifiable industries and supply chains. Workers in automotive supply chains in Michigan and Ohio, toy importers in California, furniture makers in North Carolina, and agricultural equipment manufacturers across the Midwest face job losses from inputs that became more expensive and markets that contracted in retaliation. These workers are the visible face of tariff costs — and they currently have no dedicated program. Reauthorizing TAA with simplified enrollment, faster benefit determination, and expanded retraining funding is both the morally correct response and a politically tractable one: bipartisan support for trade-displaced worker assistance has survived multiple congressional cycles.

**Reform beyond reauthorization**: The structural disconnect between tariff revenue (general fund) and worker support (separate appropriation, often underfunded) should be addressed through a dedicated revenue stream. An earmark of 5-10% of annual customs duties for TAA and a Trade Communities Fund for regional economic development in heavily affected areas would link the revenue source to its social cost in a way that makes political accountability visible.

## Section 6: Implementation Timeline and Fiscal Scope

### Phase 1 — Immediate (0–6 months): Litigation and Existing Legislative Vehicles

**Section 122 litigation support**: The 24-state AG coalition and Liberty Justice Center suits are the most immediately productive vehicles. A ruling striking down the Section 122 tariff on balance-of-payments grounds or nondelegation grounds before July 24, 2026, would eliminate the current tariff regime and force the administration to either seek congressional authorization or rely solely on the more targeted Sections 232 and 301. Legal defense fund support for plaintiff coordination, amicus brief preparation by economic and trade policy organizations, and CBO/CRS scoring of Section 122's fiscal effects for use in litigation briefing are immediate actions.

**Trade Review Act coalition building**: S.1272 has a bipartisan foundation (13 co-sponsors). The path to the floor requires a Senate Republican majority willing to force a procedural vote over leadership objection. Constituent pressure on Republican senators from tariff-affected manufacturing and agricultural districts — modeled on the Indivisible constituent-targeting approach documented in the research notes — is the political mechanism. NFIB's documented small-business opposition to tariff costs is an unusual alignment of a traditionally Republican-aligned organization with the reform position.

**TAA reauthorization**: S.1449 is a standalone vehicle that could attract bipartisan support as a worker support measure independent of broader trade authority debates. Labor unions, manufacturing sector associations, and regional economic development organizations are the natural coalition.

### Phase 2 — Medium-Term (6–24 months): Structural Reform

**IEEPA amendment**: Drafting and coalition-building for the IEEPA Reform Act. Post-*Learning Resources*, the statutory interpretation question is settled; the amendment consolidates the ruling and forecloses future litigation over future IEEPA interpretations. Targeted toward a post-2026 legislative window in which a more balanced Senate provides the 60 votes needed.

**Tariff Act amendment**: Amending the Tariff Act to bring Section 338 and existing Section 301 procedures within the Trade Review Act's congressional approval framework. This requires the same legislative majority as the Trade Review Act itself.

**WTO dispute resolution support**: Begin USTR engagement with MPIA framework as bridging mechanism while full Appellate Body reform is negotiated. Targeted at restoring even partial WTO dispute settlement function before the longer-term re-engagement process is complete.

### Phase 3 — Long-Term (24+ months): Institutional Rebuild

**WTO re-engagement**: Formal U.S. proposal for Appellate Body reconstitution with U.S. structural concerns addressed. Requires change in USTR mandate from unilateral to negotiated trade posture.

**International trade architecture**: Renegotiate or restore USMCA (U.S.-Mexico-Canada Agreement) provisions disrupted by IEEPA and Section 122 tariffs. Restore bilateral and multilateral tariff relationships affected by the 2024-2026 tariff regime. This is a multi-year diplomatic and legislative project, requiring trade promotion authority renewal and congressional engagement.

**TAA structural reform**: Move from program-based to systemic: a permanent dedicated revenue stream (% of customs duties), expanded eligibility for all workers displaced by trade policy (not only those in certified import-competing firms), and community-level investment in regions with concentrated trade disruption.

### Fiscal Scope

**Immediate costs of status quo**: Yale Budget Lab estimates the existing tariff regime costs the U.S. economy approximately $30 billion per year in long-run output loss (approximately 0.1% of GDP). The Tax Policy Center's $1,050/household average burden in 2026 implies a total consumption cost to U.S. households of approximately $140 billion annually. Small business profit decline (measured in NFIB) and supply chain disruption costs are additional and harder to precisely quantify but are reflected in GDP and employment effects.

**TAA reauthorization**: At its peak, the TAA program served approximately 100,000–130,000 workers annually at a total program cost of approximately $800 million–$1 billion per year (including training, income support, and administration). A reauthorized and expanded program — with the explicit mandate to cover tariff-displaced workers in addition to import-competition-displaced workers — would cost an estimated $1.5–2.5 billion annually at full scale.

**Trade Communities Fund (new)**: Regional economic development investment in counties with concentrated tariff-sector exposure: estimated $500 million–$1 billion annually to fund infrastructure, job retraining centers, and small business transition assistance in the most affected communities. Funded through a 1-2% earmark of annual customs duties (which at current $264 billion FY2025 collections would yield $2.6–5.2 billion — more than sufficient to fund both TAA and the Communities Fund).

**IEEPA amendment administrative cost**: Negligible. The amendment is statutory language change; implementation cost is the Treasury/OFAC review of active emergency declarations to confirm none is relying on tariff authority, which is a one-time administrative exercise.

**WTO re-engagement**: USTR budget for negotiating team and litigation support: approximately $50–100 million in additional USTR capacity over a three-year negotiating period. Legal support for WTO dispute settlement revival: $100–200 million in government legal resources over five years.

**Total annual fiscal cost of reform package**: Approximately $2–4 billion per year at full implementation. This is smaller than the approximately $140 billion in annual household consumption costs imposed by the current tariff regime, the $30 billion in annual GDP loss estimated by Yale Budget Lab, or the $264 billion in customs duties collected in FY2025 that currently flow to the general fund without any dedicated reinvestment in affected workers or communities.

Sources: [CRFB: How Much Will Trump's New Tariffs Raise?](https://www.crfb.org/blogs/how-much-will-trumps-new-10-or-15-tariffs-raise); [Yale Budget Lab: State of US Tariffs, SCOTUS Ruling](https://budgetlab.yale.edu/research/state-us-tariffs-scotus-ruling-update); [DOL: Trade Adjustment Assistance for Workers](https://www.dol.gov/agencies/eta/tradeact/); [Wikipedia: Trade Adjustment Assistance — program history and funding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trade_Adjustment_Assistance); [GovTrack: S.1449 Trade Adjustment Assistance Reauthorization Act of 2025](https://www.govtrack.us/congress/bills/119/s1449)

## Summary of Key Findings

1. The February 20, 2026 Supreme Court ruling in *Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump* (6-3, Roberts opinion) definitively held that IEEPA does not authorize presidential tariff authority. The ruling applied the major questions doctrine and the Constitution's assignment of taxing power to Congress.

2. The administration immediately pivoted to Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974 (a provision never previously invoked), imposing a 10% global tariff for 150 days. That tariff is being challenged in the Court of International Trade by 24 state AGs and private importers. It expires July 24, 2026 without congressional extension.

3. The long-term replacement strategy is Section 301 investigations — which have no time limit, no rate cap, and receive broad judicial deference — launched broadly in March 2026. The accountability gap is different from IEEPA but remains real.

4. The economic costs are documented and regressive: $1,050 average household cost (TPC), $1,200+ median household cost (PIIE), 0.8% of after-tax income for bottom decile vs. 0.3% for top decile, 91% increase in small business direct tariff payments (BofA/NFIB), $30 billion annual GDP loss (Yale Budget Lab).

5. Tariff revenue ($264 billion in FY2025) flows entirely to the general Treasury, with no earmarking for trade adjustment assistance — the program that would support affected workers — which has been lapsed since July 2022.

6. All major peer democracies (EU, Canada, Japan, Australia) constrain executive trade authority through parliamentary approval requirements and judicial reviewability that the U.S. IEEPA regime lacked.

7. The reform agenda is tractable: Trade Review Act (S.1272, Grassley-Cantwell, bipartisan), IEEPA amendment to exclude tariff authority, TAA reauthorization (S.1449), and WTO re-engagement. The political preconditions are a post-2026 Senate majority and constituent pressure on members from tariff-affected manufacturing and agricultural districts.
