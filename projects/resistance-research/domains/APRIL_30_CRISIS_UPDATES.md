## The Two Crises

### Crisis 1: Iran War Powers Resolution — May 1 Deadline

**What was at stake**: Under the War Powers Resolution (50 U.S.C. § 1543), once the president notifies Congress of hostilities, a 60-day clock begins. If Congress does not authorize continued military operations within 60 days, the president must terminate them within an additional 30 days. Trump's notification was filed March 2, 2026; the 60-day clock expired May 1.

**What happened — confirmed**:

The Trump administration did not comply. All five items on the pre-deadline outcome checklist from Section 17 of domain-19f are confirmed:

1. **No withdrawal**: U.S. naval forces maintained blockade positions in the Arabian Sea and Persian Gulf. CENTCOM confirmed continued operational posture.
2. **No authorization obtained**: No AUMF, no joint resolution of authorization was introduced, let alone passed. The fifth Senate blocking vote (46-51, April 23) was the final pre-deadline congressional action.
3. **No court intervention**: No WPR enforcement lawsuit was filed. The political question doctrine operated as expected. Democrats described their lawsuit discussions as "early stage."
4. **No administration acknowledgment**: No White House, DOD, or State Department statement addressed the WPR deadline as legally operative. When asked by reporters, Trump said: "Don't rush me."
5. **No supplemental appropriations request**: The administration did not submit an Iran war supplemental — a deliberate strategic choice to avoid activating the fiscal accountability mechanism that Senate Appropriations Chair Collins had positioned as her post-deadline leverage.

**Why this matters for the framework**:

The non-compliance confirms the "Vance doctrine" as operational executive constitutional policy: the WPR is "fundamentally fake and unconstitutional" per VP Vance's January 2026 statement, and the Iran case operationalizes that statement. This is the most complete WPR non-compliance in the statute's 52-year history — prior administrations (Kosovo, Libya) used definitional maneuvers to avoid triggering the clock or sought informal acquiescence; the Trump administration publicly pre-announced non-compliance and executed it.

The precedent is durable: every future administration will cite the 2026 Iran case as proof that the WPR's 60-day clock is legally unenforceable, regardless of the statute's text.

### Crisis 2: FISA Section 702 — April 30 Deadline

**What was at stake**: Section 702 of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act authorizes warrantless collection of foreign communications by U.S. intelligence agencies — with incidental collection of Americans' communications. The 2024 RISAA extended the authority through April 30, 2026. A 10-day emergency extension (passed April 17-18) covered through tonight.

**What happened — confirmed through April 30 (Senate vote outcome pending)**:

**House action (April 29, 235-191)**: The House passed S.1318, the Foreign Intelligence Accountability Act — a three-year reauthorization through April 2029. Key features:
- No warrant requirement for U.S. person queries (the central civil liberties objection)
- Commercial data broker loophole remains open
- Attorney-level approval (not warrant) for FBI U.S. person queries
- Monthly ODNI civil liberties reviews
- CBDC (Central Bank Digital Currency) ban attached as Freedom Caucus sweetener
- Vote breakdown: 207 R + 28 D for; 159 D + 32 R against

The collapse in Democratic support is dramatic: only 42 Democrats voted yes in 2026, compared to 147 Democrats in the 2024 RISAA. This is the clearest signal that Democrats in Trump's second term do not trust the administration with warrantless surveillance powers — regardless of the intelligence value of the program.

**Senate position (April 29-30)**: Senate Majority Leader Thune announced the Senate will not pass the House version because the CBDC provision is "not happening" in the Senate. The Senate's alternative:

- **Primary track**: S.4344 — Senate's own 3-year clean extension (no CBDC) — with cloture vote scheduled no later than May 1. Requires 60 votes to advance. Outcome unconfirmed before the April 30 midnight deadline.
- **Alternative track**: 45-day clean stopgap extending authority to approximately June 12, 2026, buying time for further negotiations.

The Senate's strategy of "jamming" the House — passing its own vehicle and forcing the House to either accept Senate terms or allow a lapse — mirrors the April 17 cycle, but is harder to execute under time pressure with chambers about to depart for recess.

**Lapse risk**: If neither the S.4344 cloture vote nor a unanimous consent stopgap cleared the Senate before midnight April 30, Section 702 authority lapsed. A brief lapse would not immediately terminate collection (existing FISC orders cover collection through 2027 per reported classified extension), but would give technology companies standing to challenge their compelled assistance obligations — the litigation vector that EFF has specifically sought.

**What's next (May-June 2026)**:

The most likely outcome is either: (A) Senate passed a clean 45-day extension by unanimous consent or voice vote before midnight, setting up a new June 12 deadline for long-term resolution; or (B) a brief lapse occurred, followed by emergency action within days. The three-year warrantless extension that the House passed is unlikely to be the final enacted form because:
1. Senate cannot pass it with the CBDC attachment
2. 159 Democrats + 32 Republicans voted against it — a majority-blocking coalition exists
3. Civil liberties community is in litigation posture regardless

**The civil liberties significance**:

The three-year timeline (whether ultimately enacted or pending) represents the closure of the warrant-requirement reform window until April 2029. The bipartisan civil liberties coalition (Lofgren-Davidson-Wyden-Lee) that built to block multiple versions of this bill is the most important surveillance reform alignment since 2013. Every vote that builds this coalition makes the 2029 fight more winnable.

The five-lane surveillance architecture that Section 702 reauthorization does not address (Section 702 warrantless queries + commercial data broker purchases + Palantir ImmigrationOS + Clearview AI + ICE Ad Tech RFI) remains fully operational regardless of the legislative outcome.

**Confidence level**: Medium-high on confirmed facts (House vote, Senate position, civil liberties analysis). Low on Senate midnight outcome — that vote had not been confirmed by available reporting as of April 30 afternoon.

## Framework Implications

### For Domain 19f (War Powers Reform)

The May 1 non-compliance is the central empirical fact that the domain reform proposal must address. The constitutional amendment pathway (Section 8 of domain-19f) — specifically the two elements required to restore Youngstown's Category 3 operational force: (1) institutional congressional standing and (2) a designated Article III war powers court — is the only structural remedy that survives the Vance doctrine. Statutory WPR reform faces the same constitutional nullity claim that made the existing statute unenforceable.

The 90-day window between May 1 and the August 2026 recess is the highest-stakes reform advocacy window in the WPR's history. The combination of confirmed non-compliance, nascent bipartisan authorization track, the fiscal pressure from an eventually-unavoidable supplemental request, and the midterm election timeline creates conditions for advocacy that have not existed since 1973.

### For Domain 25 (Surveillance Tracking)

The FISA outcome confirms a structural dynamic that will recur at the 2029 reauthorization: the "trust the government" argument for warrantless surveillance does not work with a Democratic caucus that has watched the administration use surveillance and prosecution tools against political opponents. Any long-term reauthorization that lacks warrant protections will face a Democratic Senate filibuster block (needing 60 votes) or a House majority-blocking coalition. The path to a long-term Section 702 reauthorization runs through warrant reform — which the intelligence community opposes.

The commercial data broker loophole (Venntel/Gravy Analytics, Babel Street, Accurint) is the surveillance accountability gap that the FISA debate obscures. ICE and DHS surveillance of immigrant communities, protest organizers, and civil society organizations primarily runs through the commercial data pathway — which no 2026 proposal addresses. The Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act (Wyden-Davidson) remains the operative reform vehicle, and it has no Senate floor time scheduled.

### For Cross-Domain Analysis

April 30 is the most constitutionally concentrated single day in the 119th Congress. The simultaneous expiration of FISA Section 702 authority, the approaching WPR 60-day deadline, and the DHS appropriations floor vote illustrates the "constitutional flooding" pattern documented across multiple domains: by overwhelming Congress with simultaneous crises, the executive prevents the focused deliberative attention that constitutional constraints require. This is not accident — it is strategy.

The Vance doctrine (WPR constitutional nullity) and the Section 702 warrantless renewal (surveillance without warrant) together represent the dual executive authority expansion of the April-May 2026 period: unlimited war-making authority at the foreign policy level, and unlimited surveillance authority at the domestic level. Both are now operating as confirmed executive prerogatives, constrained in neither case by effective legislative or judicial oversight.
