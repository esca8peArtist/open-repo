## Executive Summary

Six anti-LGBTQ+ ballot measures are on the November 2026 ballot in Colorado (2), Maine, Missouri, and Washington — and the legal and voting access architecture surrounding them represents a convergent democratic participation crisis, not a series of isolated policy disputes. This document makes two arguments that no existing LGBTQ+ rights or voting rights document makes in combination.

First, the ballot initiative channel is being used as a voting suppression mechanism against a specific minority group. The Supreme Court established in Romer v. Evans (1996) that ballot processes cannot constitutionally be deployed to strip a targeted minority group of the political processes available to all others. The ADF-coordinated 2026 ballot measure wave — documented by the Ballot Initiative Strategy Center as involving simultaneous qualification across multiple states — is a precise reinvocation of the mechanism Romer condemned: using majority vote to remove the legal protections that legislatures and state courts have provided to a minority group. The Colorado measures are particularly vulnerable to a Romer challenge, as that precedent arose from Colorado's own Amendment 2 (1992).

Second, trans voters face a stacking voting suppression crisis that is unique in its structure. Williams Institute data establishes that 210,800 voting-eligible trans adults in voter-ID states already lack ID that correctly reflects their name or gender — before any new legislation. Executive Order 14168 froze federal gender marker changes in January 2025, growing this population with each passing month. The SAVE America Act (House passed February 12, 2026) would add passport-based documentation requirements to voter registration — imposing a third layer of suppression on voters who already face ID mismatch and document freeze. Ninety-one thousand three hundred trans adults are in states with the strictest voter ID enforcement; these individuals face near-certain disenfranchisement under SAVE Act implementation.

The convergence point is November 2026: the same election cycle in which trans voters face the highest documented barriers to casting ballots is the cycle in which six measures targeting trans people's legal existence are on the ballot. Democratic participation and the substance of democratic decisions are being attacked simultaneously, by the same infrastructure.

## 1. The Ballot Initiative Suppression Mechanism: Romer v. Evans and the 2026 Wave

### 1.1 The Anti-Countermajoritarian Argument

The constitutional principle established in Romer v. Evans (517 U.S. 620, 1996) is not about the merits of anti-LGBTQ+ policies. It is about the procedural mechanism through which a majority can act against a minority's access to the political process itself.

In 1992, Colorado voters approved Amendment 2 to the state constitution, which prohibited any state or local government entity from enacting, adopting, or enforcing any law that protected gay, lesbian, or bisexual people from discrimination. The amendment did not merely reject a specific protection — it categorically removed the entire class of people from the political processes available to all other citizens. They could not seek protection from the legislature. They could not seek it from local government. They could not receive it through executive action. The ballot process had been used to remove them from the political system.

Justice Kennedy's majority opinion (6-3) held this unconstitutional under the equal protection clause. The critical passage: a "law declaring that in general it shall be more difficult for one group of citizens than for all others to seek aid from the government is itself a denial of equal protection of the laws in the most literal sense." Further: a "bare desire to harm a politically unpopular group cannot constitute a legitimate governmental interest." The Court applied rational basis review and found that Colorado Amendment 2 could not pass even that deferential standard because it lacked any legitimate state interest — its only identifiable purpose was to disadvantage a group.

The Romer principle is the constitutional anchor for challenging the six 2026 anti-trans ballot measures. These measures do not merely restrict one policy domain — they systematically remove trans people from the protections that state legislatures and state courts have provided:

- Colorado's measures would override the governor's executive order protecting trans students and reverse legislative compromises on gender-affirming care coverage
- Missouri's Amendment 3 would reverse a 2024 constitutional amendment that Missouri voters themselves approved, removing reproductive freedom protections and trans healthcare protections simultaneously
- Washington's measure would override the state human rights commission's interpretation of the Human Rights Act to protect trans student athletes

The structural pattern — using ballot initiatives to override the specific legal protections that democratic institutions had provided to a targeted group — is Romer's core concern. Whether current federal courts under Skrmetti's rational basis framework would apply Romer to trans-specific measures is contested, but state courts in Colorado, Washington, and Missouri may apply broader state constitutional equal protection analysis independent of the federal floor. Lambda Legal's litigation strategy for these challenges will almost certainly invoke Romer in Colorado specifically.

### 1.2 The Six 2026 Ballot Measures: Geography, Content, and Strategic Logic

Six anti-trans ballot measures have qualified for the November 2026 ballot in four states. The Ballot Initiative Strategy Center documented the coordinated qualification effort in its March 2026 Hot Sheet, noting that groups backing these measures — after years in which more than 92% of anti-LGBTQ+ legislation failed to pass state legislatures — have turned systematically to the ballot initiative channel to bypass legislative defeats.

**Colorado — Two Measures**

Colorado is the most constitutionally significant battleground because Romer v. Evans arose from Colorado's own Amendment 2 (1992). Two measures qualified for the November 2026 ballot:

Measure 1 would prohibit transgender students from participating in school sports consistent with their gender identity in K-12 and college athletics. It would override Governor Jared Polis's executive order protecting trans student athletes and reverse administrative guidance from the Colorado High School Activities Association.

Measure 2 would restrict gender-affirming care for minors, including limiting public funding and insurance coverage for such care. This targets the healthcare coverage protections that Colorado's legislature had enacted specifically as a response to ADF's multi-state healthcare ban campaign.

Both measures were certified by the Colorado Secretary of State following petition drives organized in part by the same network the BISC tracks as ADF-adjacent. The legal significance: a state that was the origin of Romer v. Evans is now the site of a direct effort to use ballot initiative architecture to strip trans people of protections — a precise echo of 1992, now targeting trans rather than LGB people.

**Maine**

Maine's citizen initiative would classify school sports teams as male, female, or coed based on the sex listed on a student's original birth certificate. Teams designated for females would be closed to students whose birth certificate reflects a male sex designation, and vice versa. The initiative gathered enough signatures to qualify after the state legislature declined to advance similar legislation.

The Maine measure is analytically significant for one reason beyond the state: the requirement that birth certificates — not current ID documents, not court-ordered name changes, not medical certification — govern sports eligibility creates a legal regime in which no legal gender recognition can override original birth documentation. This is the "sex definition" strategy (documented in Domain 55) applied directly to public accommodation.

**Missouri — Amendment 3: The Bundling Strategy**

Missouri's Amendment 3 is the most analytically important single ballot measure in the 2026 wave. The amendment would accomplish two things simultaneously: (1) ban gender-affirming care for minors, including puberty blockers, hormone therapy, and surgery; and (2) repeal Missouri's Amendment 3 from 2024, which had established a fundamental right to reproductive freedom including abortion access.

The bundling is not accidental. Missouri polls showed that a standalone trans healthcare ban for minors had approximately two-thirds support, while a standalone abortion rights reversal would fail following the 2024 vote. By packaging both into a single ballot measure, proponents deploy the more popular policy (trans healthcare restriction for minors) to carry the less popular one (abortion rights elimination). This is the "ballot candy" strategy: attaching a measure that would not pass alone to one that will, making the package inseparable.

For democratic participation analysis, Missouri Amendment 3 is significant beyond either of its component policies. It demonstrates that the ballot initiative mechanism can be used to:
1. Override a prior direct democratic vote (2024's Amendment 3) through a subsequent direct democratic vote
2. Package two separate policy goals into a single yes-or-no question, removing voters' ability to consent to one while rejecting the other
3. Use trans-specific policies as a political mobilization tool to achieve a separate political objective (abortion reversal)

An abortion-rights coalition launched a formal campaign against Amendment 3 in May 2026. A Cole County court ordered modified ballot language in September 2025 following an ACLU challenge to misleading language. Missouri voters will face the clearest example of bundled ballot suppression in November 2026.

**Washington State**

Washington's measure would prohibit transgender girls from competing in girls' school sports statewide. The measure includes a particularly aggressive enforcement mechanism: "medical sex verification" would be performed during sports physicals and would involve genital examination, hormone testing, or chromosomal genetic testing for girls aged 11 and older. The initiative qualified after the state legislature declined to pass equivalent legislation, gathering more than 400,000 signatures.

The enforcement mechanism is constitutionally notable for reasons beyond gender identity: mandatory genital examination of minors as a condition of athletic participation is an invasive physical intrusion that implicates Fourth Amendment bodily integrity rights independent of any trans-specific constitutional question. Washington LGBTQ+ organizations have challenged the initiative's ballot language; constitutional challenges are likely before November.

### 1.3 The ADF Coordination Infrastructure

The six ballot measures are not independent citizen initiatives arising from separate state political currents. The Alliance Defending Freedom and its 501(c)(4) affiliate ADF Action operate a coordinated multi-state ballot initiative deployment strategy that the Ballot Initiative Strategy Center documented explicitly in its March 2026 Hot Sheet.

The BISC analysis identified that groups with documented ADF relationships are tracking at least 78 measures certified for 2026 ballots, with anti-trans measures a concentrated subset. The pattern replicates ADF's legislative coordination model — documented by NBC News, the SPLC, and HRC — in which:

- ADF attorneys drafted at least 130 bills in 34 states (documented from an internal ADF meeting), including the Idaho Fairness in Women's Sports Act that became the national template for trans sports bans
- More than 30 of those bills were enacted across 23 states, despite ADF Senior Counsel Matt Sharp being unable to identify trans youth participating in those states' sports programs
- The bills use nearly identical language across states, indicating centralized drafting rather than independent state legislative development
- In fall 2019, ADF, the Heritage Foundation, and aligned organizations began coordinating to develop bill templates for distribution to state lawmakers

The shift from legislative to ballot initiative channel represents an adaptive response to legislative failure: organizations tracking anti-LGBTQ+ legislation have found that 92%+ of such bills have failed in state legislatures over 15 years. The ballot initiative channel bypasses the committee hearings, expert testimony, fiscal analysis, and deliberative processes that have killed most anti-trans legislation in most state legislatures. It is, in ADF's operational framework, a litigation-bypass channel for when the legislative and judicial channels are not producing desired outcomes fast enough.

The strategic significance for democratic design: when a private legal organization with no electoral accountability is coordinating the deployment of ballot measures across multiple states simultaneously — using the same templates, same legal theories, and same campaign messaging — the appearance of grassroots citizen democracy masks what is functionally a coordinated attack on minority rights using majority vote as the weapon.

## 2. The Trans Voter Suppression Stack: Three Compounding Barriers

### 2.1 Pre-Existing Barrier: ID Mismatch Under Voter ID Laws

The Williams Institute at UCLA School of Law published its analysis of trans voter ID impact on the 2024 general election in September 2024. The data establishes the baseline suppression crisis before any new legislation:

- **825,100** voting-eligible trans adults in the United States
- **433,200** live in states that conduct elections primarily in-person and have voter ID laws
- **210,800** of these — approximately 43% of trans adults in voter-ID states — lack any identity document that correctly reflects their current name or gender
- **91,300** live in states with the strictest forms of voter ID enforcement, where ID mismatch is most likely to result in poll worker challenge or denial of a regular ballot
- **More than 30%** of trans people report facing harassment due to a mismatch between their presented gender and their ID documents, including specifically at polling places

The 210,800 figure is not a projection of potential future disenfranchisement — it represents the documented state of trans voter ID access as of the 2024 election, before EO 14168, before the SAVE Act, before state law changes in 2025–2026. It is the floor, not the ceiling.

The demographic concentration of this barrier matters for democratic design analysis. Trans people who face ID mismatch barriers are disproportionately:
- Black, Indigenous, and people of color (because the bureaucratic process of gender marker change requires legal fees, medical documentation, and processing time that are more accessible to economically privileged individuals)
- Young adults and students (who are more likely to have recently transitioned and not yet completed ID update processes)
- People with low incomes (same access barrier as above)
- People experiencing housing instability (address-based voter registration is an additional barrier for people without stable addresses)

The voter ID crisis is therefore not a uniform suppression across the trans population — it concentrates disproportionately in the demographic subgroups that are already most exposed to other civic exclusion mechanisms.

### 2.2 Second Barrier: Executive Order 14168 and the Federal Document Freeze

Executive Order 14168, signed January 20, 2025, prohibited the federal government from recognizing gender identity and required all federal agencies to replace usages of "gender" with "sex," defining sex as an immutable male-female binary "determined at conception." Section 3(d) directed the Secretaries of State and Homeland Security to require that government-issued identification documents — including passports, visas, and Global Entry cards — accurately reflect the holder's sex under the EO's binary definition.

The implementation consequences are directly relevant to trans voter ID access:

**Passport freeze**: The State Department halted all gender marker changes on passports, including for applicants already in process. Passports with "X" gender markers were discontinued. Trans people who had been relying on updating their passport as part of a broader ID update process had those applications frozen. In June 2025, a federal judge issued a preliminary injunction in Orr v. Trump requiring the State Department to continue offering "M," "F," and "X" passports to certain class members — but the general freeze remains for those not covered by the injunction.

**Social Security freeze**: An internal SSA directive issued January 31, 2025, instructed employees not to accept or process gender marker changes on Social Security records, effective immediately. For trans people whose voter registration reflects one name or gender and whose Social Security records reflect another, this creates verification discrepancies that can surface in voter registration database matching.

**DACA and federal benefits documents**: Gender markers on DACA work permits and other federal benefit documents were frozen under the same EO authority.

**The compounding effect**: Trans people who were in the process of updating their federal identity documents in January 2025 — and who might otherwise have resolved their ID mismatch before the 2026 elections — have been frozen mid-process. The 210,800 Williams Institute figure, which reflected 2024 conditions, will be larger in November 2026 because the freeze has prevented document updates that would otherwise have occurred. The EO therefore functions as an active mechanism for growing the trans voter suppression population between elections.

### 2.3 Third Barrier: The SAVE America Act

The Safeguard American Voter Eligibility (SAVE) Act passed the House of Representatives on February 12, 2026, thirteen days after introduction by Representative Chip Roy. The Act would require proof of U.S. citizenship — in the form of a passport or birth certificate — at the time of voter registration.

For trans voters, the SAVE Act imposes a third layer of suppression on top of the existing ID mismatch barrier and the EO 14168 document freeze:

The SAVE Act requires either a passport or a birth certificate for voter registration. Of these:
- **Passports**: Trans people cannot obtain new passports with accurate gender markers under EO 14168 (except class members under the Orr v. Trump injunction). Existing passports with "X" markers or with a different gender or name than the voter's current registration may trigger verification problems.
- **Birth certificates**: Approximately one in three trans people have updated all their identity documents. For the two-thirds who have not, their birth certificate may reflect a different name or gender than their voter registration. In four states (Florida, Texas, Tennessee, Kansas), updating gender markers on birth certificates is entirely prohibited by state law. In fourteen states total, severe or impassable barriers exist to birth certificate gender marker changes.

The NWLC analysis estimates the SAVE Act could disenfranchise 21.3 million eligible U.S. voters overall due to document gaps. For trans voters specifically, the Center for American Progress analysis concludes the Act could prevent millions of trans Americans from voting because their layered document inconsistencies — original birth certificate in one state, passport frozen by EO 14168, state ID updated in another state — cannot be reconciled into a single coherent citizenship-plus-identity documentation set.

President Trump, in March 2026, publicly demanded that the Senate version of the SAVE Act include additional anti-trans provisions — bans on trans youth healthcare and sports participation — creating an explicit political linkage between voter registration restriction and anti-trans policy in a single legislative vehicle.

The SAVE Act has not yet passed the Senate as of June 2026. Its status in the Senate is the most critical near-term legislative threat to trans voter access before November 2026.

### 2.4 The Suppression-Suppression Paradox

The trans voter suppression stack creates a structural paradox specific to the November 2026 election cycle: the same election in which six ballot measures targeting trans people's legal existence are on the ballot is the election in which trans voters face the highest documented barriers to participating in that decision.

The mechanism is not coincidental. The ADF-coordinated ballot measure wave is designed to be decided by a majority electorate that is weighted toward those whose participation is not suppressed. The 210,800 trans adults with ID mismatches, the unknown additional number affected by the EO 14168 document freeze, and the further unknown number who will be disenfranchised if the SAVE Act passes — these are voters who have a direct stake in the outcome of ballot measures targeting trans people's healthcare, sports participation, and legal recognition. Their systematic exclusion from participation in those decisions is not a coincidence; it is the operational logic of a suppression architecture.

This dynamic — using ID law, executive order, and federal legislation to reduce the voting capacity of the group most directly affected by ballot measures targeting that group — is the deepest democratic design problem in Domain 50's analysis. The ballot initiative channel and the voter suppression channel are not separate mechanisms; they are complementary instruments of the same strategy.

## 3. Skrmetti and the Constitutional Architecture of Trans Suppression

### 3.1 What the Decision Actually Held

United States v. Skrmetti (June 18, 2025, 6-3) is the foundational constitutional development governing all anti-trans litigation as of this document's writing. Chief Justice Roberts, writing for the majority, upheld Tennessee's Senate Bill 1 banning gender-affirming care for minors.

The holding turns on classification: SB 1 does not, in the majority's analysis, classify based on sex or transgender status. Instead, it classifies based on (1) age — the restriction applies only to minors — and (2) medical diagnosis — hormone therapy is restricted specifically when used for gender dysphoria rather than for other indicated uses. Because neither age nor medical use triggers heightened constitutional scrutiny under established doctrine, rational basis review applies. Tennessee only needed to show a rational basis for the law — the most deferential standard in constitutional law — and that barrier was easily cleared.

The majority's framing was deliberate and strategic. By characterizing the law's classification as age-based rather than sex-based or transgender-status-based, the Court avoided the question of whether trans identity triggers heightened scrutiny under the Equal Protection Clause — and correspondingly avoided setting a precedent that would have required heightened justification for all anti-trans legislation. Justice Sotomayor's dissent (joined by Justice Jackson, with Justice Kagan joining in part) argued that SB 1 is facially sex-based: it restricts access to hormone therapy based on whether the patient's "sex" matches the gender for which they are seeking treatment. "Sex determines access to the covered medication," Sotomayor wrote.

What Skrmetti did not hold matters as much as what it did hold. The Court did not rule that trans people lack Equal Protection rights. It did not overrule Bostock v. Clayton County (2020)'s holding that Title VII's prohibition on sex discrimination covers discrimination based on transgender status in employment. It did not hold that adult gender-affirming care bans would be constitutional — the majority opinion explicitly noted the question was not before the Court.

### 3.2 The Cascade: 27 States, Adult Bans, and State Courts

Skrmetti immediately lowered the constitutional barrier for the 27 states that had enacted similar healthcare bans. Those bans now face only rational basis review in federal court — a standard no anti-trans healthcare ban has yet failed. The CRS summary notes that the decision makes it substantially harder to challenge laws in those 27 states, since plaintiffs can no longer argue heightened scrutiny applies.

The cascade extended beyond the 27 states with enacted bans. On June 30, 2025, the Supreme Court issued GVR (Grant-Vacate-Remand) orders to lower federal courts that had ruled against trans health bans or trans birth certificate policies, directing them to reconsider their decisions in light of Skrmetti. This cascade effectively reset litigation that trans rights organizations had been winning in certain circuits.

The Fourth Circuit has applied Skrmetti's framework to extend it to adult care bans and Medicaid coverage restrictions. In a ruling cited by State Court Report, the Fourth Circuit explicitly stated: "If a State can reasonably ban it, of course a State can reasonably refuse to pay for it" — a sentence that functions as a roadmap for legislatures to enact adult care bans under the same rational basis framework that Skrmetti established for minors.

For the 2026 ballot measures, Skrmetti's constitutional architecture has both direct and indirect consequences. Directly: it makes federal constitutional challenges to ballot measures restricting trans healthcare or sports participation significantly harder, since rational basis applies. Indirectly: it gives ADF and aligned organizations the constitutional cover to argue that ballot measures enacting restrictions are not only permissible but supported by SCOTUS precedent — improving their argument to voters that the measures are constitutionally sound and legally stable.

The state court alternative remains viable. State supreme courts in Colorado, Washington, and Missouri may apply independent state constitutional analysis under their own equal protection provisions, and some have interpreted those provisions more broadly than the federal floor. Lambda Legal's litigation strategy following Skrmetti has focused on state constitutional claims as the most likely avenue for post-Skrmetti victories.

## 4. Marriage Equality and the Relationship Legitimacy Pathway

### 4.1 The Threat to Obergefell

Obergefell v. Hodges (2015) established marriage equality as a constitutional right under substantive due process and equal protection. The Dobbs v. ### 4.2 Marriage Equality Reversal as Democratic Participation Injury

The democratic participation consequences of Obergefell reversal are distinct from, and in addition to, the direct dignity harms. Marriage equality's relationship to democratic participation operates through three specific channels:

**Spousal rights and healthcare decision-making capacity**: Legally married spouses have rights of healthcare proxy, hospital visitation, and medical decision-making that unmarried partners do not. For same-sex couples in states that would revert to pre-Obergefell law, the loss of those rights is not merely personal — it has direct civic consequences. A voter who lacks a recognized spousal relationship cannot exercise the legal rights associated with it (including standing to advocate in public proceedings about a partner's care). Healthcare capacity — the ability to make and execute decisions about one's own and one's partner's medical care — is a precondition for the stable life circumstances that support civic participation.

**Immigration status and family stability**: For same-sex binational couples, marriage is the legal mechanism through which a non-citizen partner achieves lawful permanent residence and eventually citizenship. Obergefell reversal in states that acted swiftly to reinstate opposite-sex-only marriage definitions would terminate the immigration basis for thousands of couples' legal status. Non-citizen spouses without an independent immigration status pathway would face deportation proceedings, family separation, and loss of civic standing in U.S. elections.

**Political targeting and self-protective civic withdrawal**: The activation of anti-Obergefell state bills and the formal SCOTUS petition create a political targeting environment that operates on same-sex couples regardless of whether reversal occurs. The civic participation research on chilling effects (documented extensively in Domain 55's Section 3.2) is consistent: communities that are actively targeted by political campaigns against their legal status reduce their civic exposure. The return of marriage equality as a live political question — combined with the ballot initiative wave targeting other aspects of LGBTQ+ legal recognition — constitutes a sustained targeting campaign whose chilling effect on civic participation is measurable even without a final adverse legal outcome.

## 5. Healthcare Denial as Civic Capacity Suppression

### 5.1 The Section 1557 Rescission

Section 1557 of the Affordable Care Act (42 U.S.C. § 18116) prohibited discrimination in federally funded health programs based on sex, interpreted by the Biden administration to include gender identity. HHS rescinded this interpretation effective May 14, 2025, withdrawing its guidance and declaring that the gender identity interpretation no longer represents the agency's legal views. The HHS Office of Civil Rights simultaneously withdrew its March 2022 guidance on gender-affirming care, civil rights, and patient privacy.

A federal trial court has separately vacated certain provisions of the 1557 regulations to the extent they extend non-discrimination protections to gender identity, providing a parallel judicial basis for the rescission.

The combined effect: trans patients in states without independent state non-discrimination law have lost the federal legal protection against denial of care in federally funded health programs. In states with trans healthcare bans (27+ post-Skrmetti), this means trans people face denial of care in both state-regulated insurance markets and federally funded programs — with no federal administrative complaint pathway.

### 5.2 Healthcare Denial and Civic Participation: The Causal Pathway

The civic participation consequence of healthcare denial follows the same causal logic documented in Domain 39 (Healthcare Access generally) and Domain 31 (Medicaid/OBBBA), applied specifically to trans healthcare.

When medically necessary care is denied or made financially and geographically inaccessible, the health consequences reduce the physical, mental, and financial capacity available for political organizing and civic engagement. The pathway operates through several documented mechanisms:

**Mental health and safety**: The 2026 study published in PMC (examining the association between anti-LGBTQ+ legislative debates and suicidality among LGBTQ+ adults, PMC 12841032) found significant associations between the legislative targeting environment and acute mental health crisis among LGBTQ+ people, with community belongingness as the primary buffering factor. The mental health consequences of sustained legislative targeting — and the denial of healthcare that could address those mental health consequences — compound each other. A 2026 HRC study documented GOP efforts to undermine LGBTQ+ rights and healthcare services, finding measurable mental health impacts from the policy environment independent of specific enactment.

**Economic consequences**: Gender-affirming care, for those for whom it is medically indicated, is not optional healthcare in the same way that cosmetic procedures are optional. Untreated gender dysphoria has documented consequences for employment persistence, educational completion, and economic stability. Each of those consequences reduces civic engagement capacity through the economic-to-civic participation pathway documented across multiple framework domains.

## 6. The Legislative Volume Strategy: Attrition as Suppression

### 6.1 793 Bills as Organizational Weapon

Trans Legislation Tracker documents 793 anti-trans bills under consideration in 43 states as of June 2026, up from 740 in 42 states when the research runbook was written. The ACLU's parallel tracker documents approximately 400 anti-LGBTQ+ bills in the same period. This is the seventh consecutive year of escalating anti-trans legislative volume.

The volume is not primarily a reflection of independent state legislative development. As documented in Section 1.3, ADF authored at least 130 bills in 34 states from a single coordinated effort. The Heritage Foundation, ALEC, and affiliated organizations distribute identical model legislation to multiple state legislatures simultaneously. The result is a legislative flood that functions as an organizational weapon regardless of the legislative success rate.

The mechanism: when civil society organizations defending LGBTQ+ rights must simultaneously monitor, analyze, brief, testify, and litigate against 793 bills in 43 states — while also preparing ballot measure campaigns in four states and managing post-Skrmetti litigation across multiple circuits — the aggregate demand on those organizations' legal, policy, and advocacy capacity exceeds what any private civil society infrastructure can sustain. Organizations that are primarily in defense mode cannot simultaneously conduct affirmative voter registration, civic education, and electoral mobilization campaigns for the communities they serve.

This is the strategic logic of legislative flooding as a civic suppression tool: the volume does not need to pass at high rates to achieve its purpose. Even at a 8% passage rate (consistent with historical anti-LGBTQ+ bill passage data), 793 bills produce 63 new anti-trans laws per session — each requiring legal defense. The organizational attrition is the product of the volume, not the success rate.

### 9.1 Montana Supreme Court — State Constitutional Heightened Scrutiny (April 14, 2026)

The Montana Supreme Court issued the most significant state constitutional ruling in the post-Skrmetti landscape. In *Kalarchik v. State* (5-2, April 14, 2026), the court upheld a preliminary injunction blocking enforcement of three interlocking anti-trans identity-document policies: Montana SB 458 (a binary sex definition throughout the Montana Code), a 2022 DPHHS rule restricting birth certificates, and a 2024 Motor Vehicle Division policy barring driver's license updates.

The constitutional holding is decisive: the Montana Supreme Court declared that "transgender discrimination is, by its very nature, sex discrimination" under Montana's Individual Dignity clause (Article II, Section 4), and that trans people constitute a suspect class under the state's equal protection analysis. The court applied strict scrutiny — not Skrmetti's rational basis — requiring the state to demonstrate a compelling interest narrowly tailored to achieve it. That is the standard that anti-trans laws almost never survive.

The significance for Domain 50's reform architecture is direct. This is Lambda Legal's state court alternative strategy (Section 3.2) succeeding in practice, not in theory. Montana is not a liberal state; it has an active legislative anti-trans coalition. If Montana's state constitution applies heightened scrutiny to trans discrimination, then Colorado (whose state constitution's equal protection provision Lambda Legal will analyze for the Romer challenge), Washington, and Missouri may also apply independent state constitutional analysis that exceeds Skrmetti's federal floor.

The voter suppression connection: the injunction specifically blocks the policies restricting driver's license gender marker updates — one of the primary mechanisms through which trans people face ID mismatch under voter ID laws. If the Montana injunction holds on the merits, Montana trans voters will have a judicially confirmed pathway to accurate state-issued ID that Colorado and Washington voters may not have under the anti-trans ballot measures currently on those states' ballots.

### 9.2 SCOTUS Trans Athlete Cases — Decision Imminent (Filed January 13, 2026; Decision Expected by End of June 2026)

The Supreme Court heard oral arguments in *Little v. Hecox* (Idaho Fairness in Women's Sports Act; Lambda Legal/ACLU/Legal Voice) and *West Virginia v. B.P.J.* (West Virginia trans sports ban) on January 13, 2026. As of June 14, 2026, no decision has been issued. The decision is expected before the end of the Court's term — imminent.

The legal questions before the Court: (1) Do trans sports bans violate Title IX's sex discrimination prohibition? (2) Do they violate Equal Protection's 14th Amendment? Oral argument reporting indicates the 6-3 conservative majority appeared likely to uphold bans — applying Skrmetti's rational basis framework to sports exclusion under both statutory and constitutional analysis.

The democratic participation implications of the decision are distinct from its athletic access implications:

- If the Court upholds bans under rational basis, it confirms that the constitutional floor set by Skrmetti now covers sports exclusion. ADF's model sports ban legislation — the Idaho Fairness in Women's Sports Act — will be constitutionally validated, accelerating the six additional states where ADF has pending legislation. This expands the scope of legal targeting that creates the organizational attrition analyzed in Section 6.1.

- If the Court applies Title IX analysis rather than constitutional analysis, it creates a statutory (not constitutional) basis for sports bans that cannot be overturned by administrative rulemaking — removing another layer of the federal administrative pathway for trans protection that the Biden administration had used.

Either outcome requires updating Section 3.1's "What Skrmetti held and did not hold" analysis. The sports ban cases extend Skrmetti's constitutional architecture from healthcare to athletics. The state court alternative (Section 3.2) becomes the primary strategic response: state constitutions may still apply heightened scrutiny that federal courts have removed, as *Kalarchik* demonstrates.

**Distribution urgency trigger**: When decided, distribute Domain 50's constitutional analysis sections (Sections 3 and 9.1) immediately to Lambda Legal, ACLU, and HRC. This ruling will dominate LGBTQ+ news coverage and is the optimal distribution window for the framework's constitutional analysis.

### 9.3 SCOTUS Allows Trump Trans Passport Policy — ID Mismatch Stack Worsens

The specific consequence for the voter suppression stack (Section 2): the passport stay removes the one federal court protection that had briefly restored an ID pathway for trans voters. Passport is a valid photo ID for SAVE Act registration purposes and for most state voter ID laws. Trans people who had briefly gained passport access through the expanded class injunction now face the same document freeze that has been in effect since EO 14168.

The suppression-suppression paradox (Section 2.4) now has an additional SCOTUS-enabled layer: the same Court that is about to rule on trans athlete bans has already removed federal court protection for trans passport access — during the election cycle in which six anti-trans ballot measures are on the ballot in states where trans voters face documented ID barriers. The constitutional architecture converging in June–July 2026 is the most adverse for trans civil rights since the pre-Obergefell era.

### 9.5 Talbott v. USA — D.C. Circuit Blocks Military Trans Ban (June 1, 2026)

The significance for Domain 50's anti-countermajoritarian analysis: the D.C. Circuit ruling — issued one month before the anti-trans ballot measure campaign season — applies the constitutional logic of *Romer v. Evans* to an executive action, finding the military ban "appears to be driven by the bare desire to harm a politically unpopular group." Caveats: The ruling was 2-1 with a Republican-appointed dissent, applying only to current servicemembers, not prospective enlistees. A class certification hearing was scheduled for June 30, 2026. The preliminary injunction reflects likelihood of success on the merits, not constitutional vindication.

Important framing guardrail: pro-equality ballot measures represent democratic mobilization, not constitutional protection.
