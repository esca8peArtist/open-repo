## Executive Summary

The National Voter Registration Act of 1993 was a landmark democratic infrastructure statute: it required that state agencies providing public assistance — Medicaid, SNAP, WIC, TANF — offer voter registration to all eligible applicants. The "Motor Voter Law's" Section 7 innovation was the recognition that low-income Americans interact with government primarily through public benefit agencies, and that requiring voter registration offers at those agencies was the most effective mechanism for closing the registration gap between low-income and higher-income citizens. Thirty years after the NVRA's enactment, the Medicaid enrollment infrastructure has become the primary voter registration access point for the 93 million Americans enrolled in the program — even though Section 7 compliance at public assistance agencies has been chronically underfunded and under-enforced.

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act, signed July 4, 2025, is simultaneously the largest single Medicaid coverage rollback since the program's creation in 1965 and the most effective voter registration suppression mechanism enacted since the Voting Rights Act. The causal chain is structural, not coincidental: the OBBBA's $793 billion in Medicaid spending cuts (CBO estimate) and its work requirement provisions — requiring 80 hours of work or community service per month for ACA expansion adults, effective January 1, 2027 — will cause 7.5-10 million adults to lose Medicaid coverage over the decade. These 7.5-10 million adults are also 7.5-10 million fewer adults receiving NVRA Section 7 voter registration offers at the one government office where they regularly interact with public administration.

The effect is passive suppression by infrastructure removal. No voter suppression law is required. No discriminatory intent needs to be proven. The enrollment cut is the suppression mechanism, and it operates through the same statutory chain — NVRA Section 7's mandatory voter registration offer at Medicaid agencies — that was designed to increase registration. When enrollment falls, offers fall. The communities that lose Medicaid coverage are the same communities — low-income, disproportionately communities of color, concentrated in states with abortion bans and felony disenfranchisement regimes — that face the highest pre-existing voter registration barriers.

A second mechanism compounds the primary effect: ICE's data-sharing agreement with the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services, which gives ICE access to the personal data of nearly 80 million Medicaid patients through the Palantir ELITE system, is causing a documented chilling effect in immigrant-dense communities — families declining Medicaid enrollment to avoid deportation targeting, forfeiting both healthcare access and the NVRA voter registration offer that enrollment would have triggered.

The HHS interim final rule on work requirement implementation is due June 1, 2026 — without public notice-and-comment rulemaking, as the OBBBA explicitly provides. The June 1 rule is the critical documentary anchor for NVRA enforcement litigation: it will specify the documentation burden and administrative processes states must implement, which will determine the scale of "churn disenrollment" (coverage losses among eligible people who fail administrative compliance requirements, not people who actually fail work requirements) that Section 7 compliance advocates must document.

**Framework gap**: Domain 31 (Healthcare Access — OBBBA) documents the OBBBA's coverage projections, work requirement provisions, and rural hospital closure risk as a healthcare policy analysis. It does not make the NVRA statutory argument: that Medicaid offices are designated voter registration agencies, that their enrollment decline is a voter registration decline, and that state attorneys general have standing to enforce NVRA Section 7 compliance against state Medicaid agencies during the OBBBA implementation period. This domain provides that structural analysis and the litigation theory it generates.

## 1. The NVRA-Medicaid Structural Link: Why This Is a Voting Rights Problem

### 1.1 NVRA Section 7: The Statutory Architecture

Section 7 of the National Voter Registration Act of 1993 (52 U.S.C. § 20506) requires that each state agency that provides public assistance — including Medicaid, SNAP, WIC, and TANF — must offer voter registration to any eligible applicant at the time of each application for service and at each recertification interview. The voter registration offer must be made as part of the service transaction, include a form for updating registration addresses, and provide assistance in completing the form if requested.

The NVRA's architects designed Section 7 with a specific democratic participation theory: low-income Americans have lower voter registration rates than higher-income Americans not primarily because of lack of interest but because of access barriers — the paperwork burden, the need to affirmatively seek out a registration site, and the concentration of registration infrastructure in locations less accessible to low-income communities. By mandating registration offers at benefit agencies — the government offices where low-income Americans already appear to conduct necessary business — Section 7 eliminates the access barrier at the point of least friction.

The theory has been empirically validated. Research from the Institute for Responsive Government documents that each year between 30 and 40 million adult citizens interact with the four largest Section 7-covered programs (Medicaid, SNAP, WIC, TANF). Voter registration rates among these populations are up to 20 percentage points lower than among wealthier citizens — meaning Section 7 reaches exactly the communities with the largest registration gaps. The demographic overlap between Medicaid beneficiaries and low-registration-rate communities is not coincidental; it is the statutory design premise.

### 1.2 The Implementation Failure: Section 7's Chronic Underfunding

Despite thirty years of statutory obligation, Section 7 compliance at public assistance agencies has been consistently inadequate. The Institute for Responsive Government documented that public assistance agencies collected only 1.7 million of 104 million voter registration forms nationally between 2018-2020 — a 1.6 percent share of all registrations despite being the designated registration channel for millions of eligible voters. In Colorado, the Section 7 compliance rate at Medicaid offices is so low that fewer than 2.5 percent of eligible Medicaid enrollees register to vote through the NVRA process; the "declination rate" — the share of eligible people offered registration who decline — exceeds 97.5 percent in current implementation.

The implementation failure has a documented cause: Section 7 requires voter registration offers to be made through a paper form that duplicates information already provided in the benefit application, creating an extra-step friction that most recipients do not complete. This is an administrative design failure, not a voter preference failure. States that have implemented Medicaid Automatic Voter Registration (AVR) — which automatically registers Medicaid applicants to vote using information already collected in the enrollment transaction, with an opt-out rather than opt-in mechanism — have demonstrated dramatically higher registration rates. Colorado's DMV AVR system achieves a 99 percent conversion rate (compared to the 97.5 percent declination rate of the paper form system).

Eight jurisdictions have enacted Medicaid AVR by 2024: Colorado, Nevada, Massachusetts, Washington D.C., New Mexico, Minnesota, Oregon, and Michigan. Colorado's system alone reaches an estimated 755,000 eligible but unregistered voters. Oregon's analysis projected that Medicaid AVR would reach 85 percent of the state's unregistered eligible voters, with majorities being young people and voters of color. These states have effectively transformed the Section 7 mandate from an unfulfilled theoretical obligation into a functioning democratic infrastructure.

The 34 states that have not enacted Medicaid AVR continue to operate under the paper form system with the 97.5 percent declination rate — even while the OBBBA prepares to reduce the pool of people receiving Section 7 offers by several million.

### 1.3 The OBBBA's Structural Impact on NVRA Section 7

The OBBBA's Medicaid provisions create two distinct structural effects on NVRA Section 7 implementation:

**Effect 1: Enrollment volume reduction.** The work requirement provisions, effective January 1, 2027, require ACA Medicaid expansion adults (the population between 100-138% of the federal poverty level) to document 80 hours of work, education, job training, or community service per month, or qualify for a specified exemption. The Congressional Budget Office estimated 5.2 million adults lose coverage specifically from work requirements by 2034. The Urban Institute projected 7.6 million total Medicaid enrollment decline from all OBBBA provisions combined. The Center on Budget and Policy Priorities projected 7.5 to 10 million total coverage losses over the decade.

Each person who loses Medicaid coverage is no longer a person receiving a Section 7 voter registration offer at the Medicaid office. If 7.5-10 million adults lose Medicaid coverage over the decade, Section 7 loses the opportunity to offer voter registration to those 7.5-10 million adults at the moment of Medicaid application and re-enrollment.

**Effect 2: Churn disenrollment.** The Arkansas 2018 natural experiment — the only prior implementation of a Medicaid work requirement — documented that 18,000 people lost coverage in six months without any measurable employment effect before courts blocked the program. Arkansas's data showed that the majority of coverage losses were not among people who failed to meet work requirements; they were among people who were actually eligible but failed to navigate the paperwork documentation system — a phenomenon researchers call "churn disenrollment." People who lost coverage primarily lost it because they failed to submit monthly documentation of hours, not because they stopped working. Each churn disenrollment removes an eligible person from Medicaid — and from Section 7 voter registration coverage — through administrative attrition, not actual ineligibility.

The OBBBA's requirement that states conduct eligibility redeterminations every six months for expansion adults (rather than the annual redetermination standard under prior law) multiplies the churn disenrollment opportunity: each semi-annual redetermination is a new documentation hurdle, and each failure to meet the documentation requirement triggers a coverage loss — and a removal from Section 7 voter registration offer coverage.

### 1.4 The Demographic Overlap: Where This Matters Most

The communities most likely to lose Medicaid coverage under OBBBA work requirements are the same communities with the largest voter registration gaps and the highest existing voting barriers. The demographic overlap is empirically documented:

**Low-income adults ages 18-64**: The ACA Medicaid expansion population — the primary target of the work requirements — is concentrated among adults in their prime working years who are not currently working (or not working full-time) due to caregiving responsibilities, health conditions, or labor market structural barriers in their communities. These adults have voter registration rates 20 percentage points below the general electorate.

**Rural communities**: The OBBBA's work requirements will have disproportionate rural impact because rural labor markets have fewer documented work opportunities, making the 80-hour/month requirement harder to meet and document. Rural communities also have fewer Medicaid enrollment offices, creating access barriers to the documentation process. Rural counties are also the communities where Medicaid cuts combine with rural hospital closure risk — the Chartis Center has documented 446 hospitals at risk of closure under OBBBA provisions — creating a compound healthcare and democratic participation crisis.

**Women ages 18-49**: Medicaid covers approximately 45 percent of all births in the United States. Work requirements include mandatory exemptions for pregnant individuals and parents of children 13 and under, but the documentation burden for claiming exemptions creates churn disenrollment risk even among exempt populations. The specific intersection with Domain 44 (Reproductive Freedom): women in the 21 abortion ban states who need Medicaid for reproductive healthcare access face simultaneous coverage risk from OBBBA cuts and abortion access restriction. In these states, losing Medicaid coverage removes access to both contraception and the NVRA voter registration offer that Medicaid enrollment provides.

**Communities of color**: The racial composition of the ACA Medicaid expansion population reflects the racial composition of low-income communities. CBO and CBPP data consistently show that coverage losses from Medicaid cuts fall disproportionately on Black and Hispanic adults — the same communities with documented higher voter registration barriers and, in Southern states, the communities whose political representation has just been further reduced by the Callais redistricting cascade (Domain 49).

## 2. The ICE-Medicaid Pipeline: Chilling Effect as Democratic Suppression

### 2.1 What the Data-Sharing Agreement Does

In January 2026, ICE and the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) signed a data-sharing agreement giving ICE access to personal data — including names, dates of birth, addresses, and enrollment status — of nearly 80 million Medicaid patients. ICE uses this data as an input to Palantir's ELITE (Enhanced Leads Identification and Targeting for Enforcement) system, which combines Medicaid enrollment data with commercially-purchased location data to generate deportation targeting dossiers and confidence scores on individuals' current addresses.

The EFF documented in January 2026 that ELITE uses Medicaid addresses specifically because Medicaid recipients update their addresses when they move — to maintain continuous coverage — making Medicaid records more current than most other government databases. When an immigrant family updates their Medicaid address to ensure their U.S. citizen child continues receiving healthcare, they simultaneously update their deportation targeting file. The system is designed to use healthcare enrollment compliance as a deportation location tool.

### 2.2 The Documented Chilling Effects

Healthcare providers, immigrant advocates, and congressional investigators have documented four categories of chilling effects:

**Healthcare forfeiture**: Immigrant families are declining Medicaid enrollment to avoid appearing in the ICE database — forfeiting their own healthcare access and their U.S. citizen children's healthcare access. Experts quoted in the EFF and Fortune reporting described the effect as immigrants facing an impossible choice: keep their family off ICE's radar by forfeiting healthcare, or maintain healthcare coverage and accept the ICE targeting risk.

**School enrollment avoidance**: Some immigrant families are declining to register children for school — which also triggers government database entries — to reduce their footprint in government systems connected to ICE.

**Address updating cessation**: Medicaid requires periodic address updates to maintain coverage. Some families are choosing to lose Medicaid coverage rather than update addresses that ICE can access.

**Community fear effect**: In immigrant-dense communities, the awareness of ICE-Medicaid data sharing is reducing overall Medicaid engagement, including by people with no immigration status concerns, because the community norm has shifted to avoid government benefit systems generally.

### 2.3 The NVRA Chain Reaction

Each chilling-effect-driven Medicaid disenrollment or enrollment-avoidance action is simultaneously a voter registration suppression event. People who do not enroll in Medicaid do not receive Section 7 NVRA voter registration offers. People who disenroll from Medicaid — whether because of ICE fear, administrative churn, or OBBBA work requirement failures — exit the Section 7 voter registration offer pool.

The communities experiencing the highest ICE-Medicaid chilling effects are disproportionately concentrated in the same counties that have historically relied on Section 7 for voter registration. The intersection of ICE-Medicaid chilling, OBBBA enrollment decline, and pre-existing Section 7 implementation inadequacy creates a compounding mechanism: the voter registration infrastructure that was already failing these communities is being further reduced through three simultaneous mechanisms.

The Brennan Center (May 2026) documented that ICE has stated its intent to use surveillance infrastructure against anti-ICE protesters and other political dissidents. The immigrant communities experiencing the ICE-Medicaid chilling effect are also communities actively engaged in civic resistance to immigration enforcement policies — making the chilling effect both a healthcare access problem and a First Amendment-adjacent political participation suppression problem.

## 3. OBBBA Scale and Implementation Timeline

### 3.1 The Fiscal Architecture of the Coverage Collapse

The Congressional Budget Office's analysis of the OBBBA provides the foundational quantitative estimates:

- **$793 billion**: Federal Medicaid spending reduction over ten years (CBO estimate)
- **$268 billion**: ACA Marketplace spending reduction over ten years (CBO estimate)
- **$1.04 trillion**: Total healthcare coverage spending reduction including indirect revenue effects
- **7.5 million**: Adults projected to lose Medicaid coverage by 2034 under work requirements (CBO: 5.2M specifically from work requirements, with additional losses from other provisions)
- **10 million**: Total coverage losses including ACA Marketplace disruptions (various estimates; CBPP uses this figure)
- **446**: Hospitals at risk of closure under OBBBA provisions, including rural hospitals that depend on Medicaid reimbursement (Chartis Center analysis)
- **20 million**: Adults currently enrolled in ACA Medicaid expansion who will be subject to work requirements as of January 1, 2027

The Urban Institute's analysis specifically of work requirement impacts projected 7.6 million Medicaid expansion enrollment reductions by 2034, with the largest impacts from work requirements (5.3 million) and semi-annual rather than annual redetermination requirements (923,000).

### 3.2 Implementation Timeline: The Critical Advocacy Windows

The OBBBA establishes a specific implementation timeline that creates corresponding advocacy windows:

**June 1, 2026 — HHS Interim Final Rule**: HHS must issue an interim final rule specifying work requirement implementation procedures, definitions, and documentation standards. Critically, the OBBBA explicitly provides that this rule is not subject to notice-and-comment rulemaking under the Administrative Procedure Act — it bypasses the public participation mechanism that ordinarily allows civil society organizations to shape regulatory implementation. The June 1 rule will determine the documentation burden, which governs the scale of churn disenrollment. It is also the document that NVRA enforcement litigation will scrutinize: does the rule include provisions for maintaining Section 7 voter registration offer requirements during work requirement transitions?

**June 30–August 31, 2026 — State Outreach to Members**: The OBBBA requires states to conduct outreach to Medicaid expansion members about work requirements through regular mail and at least one additional contact method. This outreach period is the NVRA compliance window: state Medicaid agencies are required to include voter registration offers in their communications during the outreach period. Organizations with state-level NVRA enforcement standing should audit state outreach materials to verify Section 7 compliance.

**January 1, 2027 — Work Requirements Effective**: States must implement work requirement verification systems by this date (earlier implementation permitted). The first wave of coverage losses from work requirements will begin in early 2027 as people who fail documentation requirements lose coverage.

**Ongoing — Semi-Annual Redeterminations**: Beginning with implementation, ACA expansion adults must pass eligibility redeterminations every six months. Each redetermination is both a churn disenrollment risk and a Section 7 voter registration opportunity — states must provide voter registration offers at redeterminations, not just initial enrollments.

## 4. International Comparative Evidence: Healthcare as Democratic Infrastructure

### 4.1 Canada: Health Card Enrollment as Automatic Voter Registration

Canada's provincial health insurance systems provide the most direct comparison. Several Canadian provinces use provincial health card enrollment as an automatic voter registration trigger:

- **British Columbia and Ontario**: Health card enrollment serves as an automatic voter registration source, contributing to near-universal registration rates in those provinces
- **Elections Canada Proactive Outreach**: The federal elections agency proactively contacts Canadians who appear in provincial health databases but are not yet registered, producing a push-registration model
- **Result**: Canada's voter registration rate exceeds 95 percent of eligible citizens, compared to approximately 68 percent in the United States — a 27-percentage-point gap that the NVRA was designed to reduce but has not achieved at scale

The Canadian model demonstrates the design principle: when the government treats voter registration as an active responsibility rather than a passive obligation, and uses healthcare enrollment infrastructure as the delivery mechanism, near-universal registration is achievable without coercion.

### 4.2 Germany: Residency Registration as Universal Enrollment

Germany's Einwohnermeldeamt (Residents' Registration Office) system requires all residents to register their address with the local government when they move. This residency registration automatically triggers voter registration: when you register your residence, you are registered to vote at that address. The German system achieves near-100 percent registration of eligible voters and eliminates the access barrier entirely.

The democratic design lesson is not that the US should adopt compulsory address registration — the constitutional structure is different — but that the choice to separate voter registration from other government interactions is a design choice with measurable democratic consequences. The US made a partial move toward the German principle through the NVRA's Section 7 mandate, but chronic underfunding and the opt-in paper form model have prevented it from achieving the German result.

### 4.3 Australia: Healthcare Accessibility and Electoral Turnout

The Australian Electoral Commission's proactive voter registration model provides the third comparative frame. Australia combines: (1) compulsory voting (with a modest fine for non-compliance), (2) automatic re-enrollment when citizens move, using address change information from the postal service and other government databases, and (3) accessible polling infrastructure designed with healthcare and disability accessibility standards, not primarily security standards.

Australia's voter turnout exceeds 90 percent of registered eligible voters. Canada's average turnout (73 percent in 2015-2021 federal elections) versus the United States (55 percent, same period) illustrates what automatic registration produces even without compulsory voting. The specific correlation: countries with universal healthcare systems that use healthcare enrollment infrastructure for voter registration achieve 15-30 percentage points higher voter participation than the United States.

The causal mechanism is not that healthcare access directly enables voting. It is that: (1) healthcare enrollment systems reach low-income citizens who are otherwise unregistered; (2) universal healthcare removes the time and cognitive burden of managing healthcare instability that correlates with lower civic participation; and (3) countries that design public systems for access rather than fraud prevention produce higher participation outcomes.

### 4.4 UK: NHS Enrollment and Electoral Register Verification

The UK's Individual Electoral Registration system uses NHS enrollment records as a verification source for voter registration. When a local council updates its electoral register, it cross-references NHS patient records to identify eligible voters who may have moved or been added to the NHS system but are not on the electoral register. This creates an active push-registration mechanism using healthcare data — the inverse of the ICE-Medicaid model, which uses healthcare data for enforcement rather than democratic participation.

The UK's 2024 election produced a turnout of approximately 60 percent — lower than ideal and reflecting its own registration access problems, but still illustrating that healthcare-linked registration produces better outcomes than the US's purely opt-in model.

## 5. NVRA Enforcement: The Available Legal Pathways

### 5.1 DOJ Civil Rights Division Authority

The Department of Justice's Civil Rights Division has primary statutory authority to enforce NVRA Section 7. Under 52 U.S.C. § 20510, the Attorney General may bring a civil action in any appropriate US district court against any state that fails to comply with the NVRA, including Section 7's voter registration offer requirements. The DOJ has successfully litigated NVRA Section 7 compliance cases against several states, including a 2012 consent decree with Missouri requiring systematic improvements to Section 7 compliance at public assistance agencies.

Under the current Trump administration, DOJ enforcement of Section 7 against state Medicaid agencies declining to offer voter registration is not a realistic near-term expectation. However, the existing consent decree architecture (several states remain under NVRA consent decrees), the statutory authority, and the documented compliance failures all provide the evidentiary record for litigation as soon as an administration with enforcement interest takes office.

**The NVRA enforcement argument during OBBBA implementation**: Regardless of DOJ activity, state Medicaid agencies are required under NVRA Section 7 to offer voter registration at every eligibility redetermination. The OBBBA's six-month redetermination cycle — which requires every expansion adult to re-verify eligibility every six months — creates millions of new Section 7 voter registration opportunities. State agencies that implement the six-month redetermination process without including Section 7 voter registration offers are independently violating the NVRA.

### 5.2 State Attorney General Enforcement

In Democratic-controlled states, state attorneys general have both standing and authority to enforce NVRA Section 7 compliance against their own state Medicaid agencies. This creates an internal state enforcement mechanism that does not depend on federal DOJ activity. Several state AGs have demonstrated willingness to enforce NVRA requirements as part of voting rights protection programs (New York, California, Illinois).

The specific litigation theory: when ICE's use of Medicaid data for deportation targeting causes a documented chilling effect that reduces Medicaid enrollment in identifiable communities within a state, the state Medicaid agency's Section 7 voter registration compliance rate in those communities falls proportionally. The state AG can enforce Section 7 against the state's own Medicaid agency for inadequate compliance, and can also seek relief against the federal data-sharing agreement under administrative law grounds (APA arbitrary and capricious review; HIPAA privacy rule compliance).

### 5.3 Private Right of Action

NVRA Section 11(b) provides a private right of action for aggrieved persons to bring suit in federal district court for NVRA violations. Private enforcement litigation has been used to challenge state failures to maintain accurate voter rolls, failures to offer registration at Section 7 agencies, and other compliance failures. For OBBBA-related NVRA enforcement, private rights of action could be brought by: (1) organizations that conduct NVRA compliance audits at public assistance agencies documenting Section 7 failures; (2) individual applicants who were eligible for voter registration offers during Medicaid interactions and did not receive them; or (3) state voter registration organizations with standing as organizations whose mission is directly frustrated by Section 7 non-compliance.

### 5.4 The HIPAA-ICE Data Sharing Challenge

The ICE-CMS data-sharing agreement faces potential challenge under the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA). HIPAA's Privacy Rule (45 C.F.R. Part 164) limits the use and disclosure of protected health information (PHI) — which includes Medicaid enrollment information — to purposes authorized by the Act. HIPAA contains a law enforcement exception (45 C.F.R. § 164.512(f)) permitting disclosure for law enforcement purposes, but courts have interpreted this exception narrowly to require that the disclosure serve an authorized law enforcement function and that the information be limited to what is necessary.

Civil liberties organizations including the EFF and ACLU have challenged the CMS-ICE data sharing agreement on HIPAA grounds, arguing that using Medicaid health information as a deportation targeting tool is outside the law enforcement exception's scope. These challenges are pending as of May 2026. Resolution in plaintiffs' favor would both terminate the ICE-Medicaid pipeline and create a legal precedent limiting government use of healthcare data for immigration enforcement purposes more broadly.

## 6. Reform Pathways

### 6.1 Tier 1 — Immediate: Medicaid AVR in the Eight States Plus Four

Eight states have enacted Medicaid AVR; 34 have not. In states with Democratic governors and legislative capacity, Medicaid AVR is a concrete, achievable short-cycle reform. The implementation model is established — Colorado, Oregon, and Michigan provide operational blueprints — and the democratic participation impact is documented. Each state that enacts Medicaid AVR before the OBBBA work requirement implementation date (January 1, 2027) increases the Section 7 conversion rate from 2.5 percent to approximately 99 percent, dramatically amplifying the remaining pool of Medicaid enrollees as a voter registration channel.

Priority states for Medicaid AVR campaigns: Virginia (Democratic legislative majority through 2026), Minnesota (existing law), Wisconsin (pending litigation on AVR scope), Arizona (Democratic governor, Republican legislature — executive action scope to be explored), and Pennsylvania (Democratic governor, split legislature).

### 6.2 Tier 2 — Medium-Term: NVRA Enforcement During OBBBA Implementation

State AG offices and voting rights litigation organizations should develop a systematic NVRA Section 7 audit protocol for state Medicaid agencies during the OBBBA work requirement implementation period (June 1, 2026 onward). The audit should document: (a) whether the June 1 HHS interim final rule includes Section 7 compliance requirements for the work requirement outreach period; (b) whether state outreach materials during the June 30–August 31, 2026 outreach period include NVRA-compliant voter registration offers; and (c) whether semi-annual redetermination processes include Section 7 voter registration offers.

State AGs with NVRA enforcement authority (New York, California, Michigan, Illinois) should file pre-implementation demand letters to state Medicaid agencies requiring Section 7 compliance protocols as part of OBBBA implementation planning, before the January 2027 effective date.

### 6.3 Tier 3 — Long-Term: Federal Medicaid AVR Legislation

A federal Medicaid Automatic Voter Registration Act would require all states receiving federal Medicaid funding to implement AVR — using Medicaid enrollment data to automatically register eligible applicants to vote, with an opt-out rather than opt-in mechanism. This would transform the OBBBA's enrollment churn from a voter registration suppression mechanism into a voter registration activation mechanism: every person who goes through an eligibility redetermination — successfully or unsuccessfully — would be automatically registered (or have their registration updated) as part of the process.

The political feasibility: this legislation requires a Democratic trifecta Congress. It should be drafted and introduced in the current Congress to create an enacted legislative vehicle for the post-2026 congressional session.

### 6.4 The HIPAA Privacy Rule Amendment: Barring ICE Use of Health Data

An amendment to HIPAA's Privacy Rule or a standalone statute explicitly prohibiting government use of Medicaid and other public benefit enrollment data for immigration enforcement purposes would eliminate the ICE-Medicaid chilling effect. The existing HIPAA law enforcement exception should be amended to exclude immigration civil enforcement from the authorized law enforcement purposes.

This reform has strong coalition potential: healthcare privacy organizations, immigrant rights organizations, and healthcare providers are aligned on the principle that patients must be able to seek healthcare without creating a deportation targeting record. The American Medical Association, which has opposed ICE's use of Medicaid data, is a key institutional ally.

## 7. Coalition Architecture and Distribution Strategy

### 7.1 The Bridge Coalition

The strategic value of this domain is its ability to bridge two communities that have historically run parallel arguments:

**Healthcare advocacy community**: Protect Our Care, Georgetown Center for Children and Families (CCF), Center on Budget and Policy Priorities, KFF, American Medical Association, Families USA. These organizations frame Medicaid cuts in terms of coverage rates, disease burden, hospital closures, and mortality. They need a democratic rights argument that is legally distinct and politically broader.

**Voting rights community**: Brennan Center Elections Program, Lawyers' Committee for Civil Rights Under Law, NAACP Legal Defense Fund, Campaign Legal Center, Democracy Docket. These organizations have deep engagement on voter suppression documentation; the NVRA Section 7-Medicaid enrollment nexus is a statutory enforcement theory that is new to most of them and connects their work directly to the healthcare access fight.

The NVRA enforcement angle is the bridge: it gives state AGs a specific litigation theory (Section 7 compliance at Medicaid agencies) that does not require winning an abstract "healthcare as democracy" argument; it requires only proving that specific Medicaid agencies are failing to comply with the NVRA's specific voter registration offer requirements during OBBBA implementation.

### 7.2 The Caregiver Political Economy

Brookings Institution's May 2026 analysis "The Caregiving Crisis and the 2026 Vote" provides an additional electoral framing. The OBBBA's cuts to Medicaid home and community-based services affect 6 million family caregivers — disproportionately women of voting age, the highest-turnout demographic in the 2022 and 2024 midterm cycles. Brookings explicitly analyzed the caregiver vote as a 2026 electoral mobilization opportunity.

The NVRA-Medicaid nexus connects Brookings's electoral caregiver analysis to the structural voter registration suppression argument: the 6 million family caregivers whose Medicaid support is being cut are primarily women who vote at high rates. The loss of Medicaid HCBS funding affects their capacity to participate in civic life (caregiving burden increases); the loss of Medicaid enrollment for their care recipients removes NVRA voter registration offers from the dependent family members; and the churn disenrollment risk for caregivers themselves creates registration instability in one of the highest-turnout electoral demographics.

## 8. Evidence Gaps and Methodological Notes

**High confidence**: The OBBBA's coverage projections are documented by CBO (primary source), Urban Institute, CBPP, and KFF — multiple independent research organizations with track records for accurate Medicaid modeling. The NVRA Section 7's requirement at Medicaid agencies is statutory text with 30 years of implementation documentation. The ICE-Medicaid data sharing agreement is documented through congressional records and EFF/ACLU research.

**Medium confidence**: The specific voter registration loss that will result from OBBBA Medicaid enrollment declines has not been directly quantified in any published study. The causal chain (enrollment decline → Section 7 offer decline → registration gap increase) is logically sound and empirically grounded in the components, but no study has integrated the OBBBA's specific projected enrollment declines with NVRA Section 7 compliance rates to produce a specific voter registration impact estimate. This domain presents the structural argument; quantification will require post-implementation data collection.

**Methodological note**: The ICE-Medicaid chilling effect on voter registration is currently qualitative (documented through provider reports and immigrant community surveys) rather than quantitative. Researchers at the intersection of immigration studies and political participation who could design studies to quantify this effect include the Harvard PEWRSPECTIVE Lab, the Brennan Center's Democracy Program, and the Institute for Responsive Government. A commissioned study designed now could produce quantitative findings before the January 2027 work requirement implementation date.
