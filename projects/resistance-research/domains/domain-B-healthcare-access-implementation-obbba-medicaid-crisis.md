The OBBBA statute (P.L. 119-21, signed July 4, 2025) requires HHS to issue an interim final rule implementing Medicaid work requirements by June 1, 2026. ## Executive Summary

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA, P.L. 119-21) cuts $911 billion from federal Medicaid and CHIP funding over ten years. The Congressional Budget Office projects 11.8 million people will lose health coverage by 2034, with 5.2 million losing Medicaid directly from work requirements alone. Nebraska is already disenrolling beneficiaries. Montana implements July 1; Iowa December 1; Arkansas begins a soft launch July 1. An HHS interim final rule defining implementation parameters arrives June 1 with no public comment period. Section 232 pharmaceutical tariffs (100% rate on patented drugs) take effect July 31, compressing drug access for the same population losing coverage.

The mechanism is not administrative inconvenience. The CBO scoring of work requirements is explicit: $326 billion in ten-year savings derives primarily from coverage loss, not from employment increases. Arkansas's 2018 implementation — the only completed U.S. precedent — produced 18,000 disenrollments in six months and zero measurable employment effect. The policy is a coverage contraction mechanism that was scored and passed on that basis.

The democracy nexus is structural: Medicaid enrollment offices are voter registration sites under the National Voter Registration Act. The same offices losing funding and staff capacity in majority-minority Southern states serve the same communities most vulnerable to procedural disenrollment. The 12 non-expansion states — primarily Southern, disproportionately Black in their low-income populations — remain outside the system entirely.

## 1. OBBBA Medicaid Funding Architecture

### The $911 Billion Cut: Three Mechanisms

The OBBBA does not eliminate Medicaid. It restructures federal matching in ways that mechanically reduce enrollment and increase procedural attrition. Three mechanisms account for the $911 billion in projected federal savings.

**Mechanism 1 — Enhanced FMAP Elimination (Effective immediately)**

The Affordable Care Act offered states a 90% federal matching rate to expand Medicaid to adults earning up to 138% of the federal poverty level. OBBBA modifies this by eliminating the enhanced match for states that had not already expanded by January 1, 2026. This effectively closes the expansion pathway for the 12 states that have not yet expanded. Those states — Alabama, Florida, Georgia, Idaho, Kansas, Louisiana, Mississippi, Missouri, North Carolina, South Carolina, Tennessee, Texas — leave approximately 3.6 million low-income adults without a coverage pathway. All 12 are majority Southern states with disproportionately Black and Hispanic Medicaid-eligible populations.

The paradox of the FMAP mechanism: the 37 states with active expansion retain their enhanced federal matching and must implement work requirements. The 12 non-expansion states are rewarded with no work requirement pressure on their already-excluded populations. The policy design creates maximum coverage loss in states where low-income populations are most vulnerable, concentrated in states where federal healthcare dollars were already lowest per capita.

**Mechanism 2 — 6-Month Recertification (Effective January 1, 2027, states may accelerate)**

OBBBA cuts the Medicaid recertification interval from 12 months to 6 months for expansion adults, doubling the administrative contact frequency. The 2023–2024 unwinding of COVID-era continuous enrollment provides direct empirical evidence of what accelerated recertification produces: of the 25 million people who lost Medicaid coverage during unwinding, 69% were disenrolled for procedural reasons — missed paperwork deadlines, address changes, failure to respond to verification notices — not actual ineligibility.

Doubling the recertification frequency will produce roughly double the procedural attrition. The RWJF projects that more frequent eligibility checks alone will cause 2–3.1 million additional coverage losses by 2028, independent of work requirements.

**Mechanism 3 — Work Requirements (Mandatory January 1, 2027)**

Adults aged 19–64 in Medicaid expansion who are not medically frail, disabled, pregnant, postpartum, caregivers of children under 13, recently incarcerated, or meeting SNAP/TANF work requirements must document 80 hours per month of work, approved job training, community service, or education. States verify compliance at application and every six months during enrollment. The lookback period allows states to examine compliance during the three months immediately preceding application.

**What the CBO score actually measures**: The CBO projects work requirements will reduce federal Medicaid spending by $326 billion over ten years and cause 5.2 million adults to lose federal Medicaid coverage by 2034, with 4.8 million becoming uninsured. The savings derive almost entirely from coverage loss, not from employment behavior change. The Congressional Budget Office does not project significant employment increases from work requirements — that finding is consistent with every empirical study of the Arkansas precedent.

**What the Arkansas precedent established**: Arkansas implemented work requirements on June 1, 2018. By December 2018, 18,000 beneficiaries had been disenrolled — approximately 20% of the non-elderly, non-disabled expansion population in the state. A 2020 Health Affairs study by Sommers et al. found work requirements produced zero measurable employment effect. The uninsured rate among Arkansas adults aged 30–49 increased from 10.5% in 2016 to 14.5% in 2018. Fifty percent of those disenrolled reported serious problems paying medical debt; 56% delayed care due to cost. The D.C. Circuit upheld the block of work requirements in *Gresham v. Azar*, 950 F.3d 615 (2020), finding work requirements did not advance Medicaid's statutory purpose of providing medical assistance. That precedent now confronts a statutory mandate rather than agency discretion — a critical distinction addressed in Section 7.

### Total Coverage Loss Projection

The combined CBO and RWJF projections:
- Work requirements: 5.2 million lose federal Medicaid coverage (CBO); 3–7 million using broader modeling (RWJF)
- 6-month recertification: 2–3.1 million additional losses (RWJF)
- ACA marketplace tax credit loss: Individuals losing Medicaid work-requirement eligibility are also ineligible for marketplace premium tax credits, eliminating a fallback coverage pathway for 4.8 million
- Total CBO headline figure: 11.8 million people lose health coverage by 2034

## 2. Work Requirement Provisions: The HHS Guidance Window

### June 1 Interim Final Rule — No Comment Period

HHS must issue an interim final rule by June 1, 2026. The statute provides: "the interim rule will not be subject to public notice or public comment." This language is a statutory carve-out from the Administrative Procedure Act's notice-and-comment requirement (5 U.S.C. § 553), available when an agency for good cause finds notice and comment "impracticable, unnecessary, or contrary to the public interest." Here, Congress itself removed the requirement — a distinct legal situation from an agency invoking good cause.

The absence of a notice-and-comment period is itself a litigation vulnerability. APA challenges to interim final rules lacking adequate justification for the good-cause exception have succeeded in multiple circuits. Congress's statutory instruction to issue an interim rule without comment is unusual but not unprecedented; courts have diverged on whether congressional waiver of APA notice-and-comment is constitutionally permissible or whether certain rulemaking procedural requirements are themselves non-waivable. This creates a post-issuance litigation pathway (addressed in Section 7).

### What HHS Guidance Must Define

The interim rule must resolve multiple provisions the statute left ambiguous, and those choices will determine how many people lose coverage:

**Medical frailty definition**: The law exempts "medically frail" individuals — defined in the statute to include persons who are blind or disabled, have substance use disorders, disabling mental disorders, physical or intellectual developmental disabilities, or "serious or complex medical conditions." The AMA and CBPP note that CMS's December 8, 2025 informational bulletin "leaves core implementation questions around the medical frailty exemption unanswered." Key unresolved questions: whether states may grant permanent medical frailty exemptions for lifelong conditions; how verification works when data sources are inconclusive; whether lookback periods apply to individuals claiming frailty retroactively. A broad definition of medical frailty substantially reduces the population subject to work requirements; a narrow definition enlarges it.

**Caregiver exemption scope**: The statute exempts "parents and caretakers with children ages 13 and under." The word "caretaker" is undefined. Does it include adult children caring for elderly parents? Informal caregivers not receiving payment? Individuals providing care more than 10 hours per week but not as primary caretaker? These definitions affect millions of people, primarily women.

**Hardship exception criteria**: States may grant temporary hardship exceptions for unspecified "extenuating circumstances." HHS must define the minimum national standard. States implementing broader hardship exceptions (29 states have indicated they plan at least one hardship exemption type, per KFF) will see lower disenrollment rates.

**Automated verification scope**: States must use existing data sources "where possible" to automate compliance verification. Work types that cannot be automated include self-employment, most community service, and informal caregiving. For populations who work informally or in cash economies, automated verification is impossible — they must document manually each month, a significant administrative burden.

### State Outreach Window

The statute requires states to conduct member outreach between June 30 and August 31, 2026, through regular mail plus at least one additional contact method (telephone, text, website, electronic means). States with low-quality address data, rural populations without reliable mail delivery, or communities with limited English proficiency face systematic outreach failures. CBPP notes that states currently "lack the time, resources, and guidance" to implement adequately.

### Early Implementation: Nebraska as the Live Test Case

Nebraska became the first state to enforce OBBBA work requirements on May 1, 2026 — eight months ahead of the federal mandate. The state secured approval through an OBBBA early-implementation provision. Effective May 1, able-bodied adults aged 19–64 in the Medicaid expansion population must log 80 hours per month or qualify for an exemption. Members with renewal dates in May or June 2026 face a 30-day notice period; the first disenrollments from non-compliance will occur for members with eligibility periods ending July 31, 2026.

KFF estimates 20,000–40,000 Nebraskans could lose coverage. CBPP characterized Nebraska's approach as "punitive," noting the state is implementing before HHS guidance is finalized, locking in implementation decisions that may conflict with June 1 federal rules. Bluestem Health clinic — a rural Nebraska safety-net provider — estimates it will lose $600,000 annually from Medicaid enrollment losses. A rural dialysis unit has already closed.

**Montana** begins implementation July 1. **Iowa** implements December 1. **Arkansas** launches a soft verification phase July 1 with no disenrollments until January 2027. **Idaho** and **Indiana** have announced they will apply more restrictive policies than the law requires, including quarterly compliance checks.

## 3. Hospital Closures: Case Studies and the 446-Hospital At-Risk Matrix

### The Public Citizen Analysis

Public Citizen's April 2026 analysis identifies 446 hospitals at heightened risk of closure or service reduction due to OBBBA Medicaid cuts. Methodology: hospitals qualified as "at-risk" if they simultaneously carried a negative average net profit margin (2022–2024) and derived 20% or more of revenue from Medicaid/CHIP/low-income payer sources. The 446 at-risk facilities collectively operate 68,986 beds, served 6.6 million patients in 2024, and employ approximately 275,458 direct care workers.

**Geographic profile of at-risk hospitals**:
- California: 83 hospitals (31% of state total)
- New York: 45 hospitals (31% of state total)
- Illinois: 28 hospitals
- Washington: 22 hospitals (26% of state total)
- Connecticut, Massachusetts, New York, California, Washington: 5 states with over 25% of their hospital stock at risk

Of the 446, 60% are urban and 39% are rural. Near half (215) hold Medicare special designations indicating financial fragility: 84 Critical Access Hospitals, 72 Rural Referral Centers, 42 Sole Community Hospitals, 17 Medicare Dependent Hospitals.

**Congressional exposure**: House Republicans voting for the OBBBA have 196 at-risk hospitals in their own districts. Senate Republicans collectively have 146 at-risk hospitals in their states. The political geography of the hospital closure risk is not confined to Democratic-majority areas — this is a rural and exurban crisis that crosses party lines.

### Case Studies: Three Hospitals in Real-Time Crisis

**St. Mary's Sacred Heart Hospital — Lavonia, Georgia**

St. Mary's Sacred Heart Hospital in Lavonia, Franklin County, closed its labor and delivery unit in late 2025, citing "recent Congressional cuts to Medicaid" as explicitly "solidifying" the decision. The hospital underwent an 18-month process of recruiting physicians, building partnerships, and seeking incremental funding before closure. The unit's closure forces pregnant women in Franklin County and surrounding rural areas to travel 45 minutes to 1.5 hours to Athens or Greenville — a dangerous distance for emergency obstetric situations. Georgia already had only 36% of rural hospitals providing labor and delivery services as of late 2025; nine rural hospitals have closed in the state in the last decade. St. Mary's represents the documented moment where OBBBA funding math became the explicit determining factor, not merely a contributing pressure.

**Centra Southside Community Hospital — Farmville, Virginia**

Centra Southside Community Hospital in Farmville (Prince Edward County) closed its labor and delivery and OBGYN surgical services, as well as outpatient services at its affiliated women's center, explicitly citing "recently enacted reductions in federal health care funding." Centra Southside derives 22% of revenue from Medicaid and 53% from Medicare — 75% total from government payers. The hospital is among 10 Virginia hospitals named in Senator Mark Warner and Senator Tim Kaine's April 2026 letter as at risk of closure under OBBBA cuts. Public Citizen identified Centra Southside on its at-risk list. The hospital's parent system stated publicly it is "committed" to keeping the facility open, but the service closures represent concrete operational contraction already in effect. Rural Farmville — 20% below-poverty-line population, majority Black county — loses the only women's services within a substantial geographic range.

**Greene County General Hospital — Linton, Indiana**

Greene County General Hospital discontinued obstetric services on January 31, 2026, ending more than 100 years of delivering babies in the community. Hospital CEO Brenda Reetz cited Medicaid reimbursement rates that "haven't kept up with the cost of expenses," with Medicaid reimbursing $2,000–$3,000 less per birth than actual cost. The hospital documented a five-year average of 73 babies delivered at a loss of $726,804 excluding provider costs. Indiana has seen 15 birthing units close since 2020. Greene County is rural, median household income below the state average, with a Medicaid-dependent population for obstetric care. Fifteen OB closures in one state over five years is not a random distribution — it is the systematic contraction of maternal care infrastructure in financially marginal rural communities.

### The Rural Health Fund Failure

The OBBBA included a $50 billion Rural Health Transformation Fund as political cover for rural hospital cuts. It is demonstrably inadequate on its face:

- Federal Medicaid spending in rural areas is estimated to decline by $137 billion over ten years
- The $50 billion fund covers approximately 37% of estimated rural funding losses
- CMS stipulated that no more than 15% of the Rural Health Fund can be used on direct patient care or hospital operations — the overwhelming majority must fund "innovative health care delivery solutions"
- Georgetown CCF documented in May 2026 that state officials consistently confirm the fund "is falling short" and "will not make up for the large accompanying Medicaid cuts"
- Oklahoma's INTEGRIS Health system projects $130 million in funding losses alone; the Rural Health Fund cannot compensate individual systems at that scale

Third Way calculates the looming total hospital cut at $661 billion — which includes not only Medicaid cuts but provider tax restrictions and DSH payment changes embedded in the OBBBA. At that scale, the Rural Health Fund is not a safety net; it is a political messaging tool.

## 4. Coverage Loss Projections and State-by-State Variation

### National Projections

The CBO headline — 11.8 million people losing health coverage by 2034 — understates the near-term impact. The RWJF modeled 2028 coverage losses specifically:
- Work requirements alone: 3–7 million people lose Medicaid coverage (RWJF range)
- Eligibility redetermination: 2–3.1 million additional losses
- Near-term combined: 4.9–10.1 million people losing Medicaid coverage by 2028

A critical CBO finding that has been under-reported: individuals who lose Medicaid due to work requirements are also ineligible for ACA marketplace premium tax credits. The coverage cliff is not mitigated by marketplace enrollment — the law explicitly blocks that fallback.

### State Variation: Who Loses Most

State policy decisions will determine how far within the CBO range each state lands. Key variables: breadth of medical frailty definition, number of hardship exemptions, investment in automated verification, quality of outreach infrastructure.

Under restrictive state policies, six states — Connecticut, Massachusetts, Maryland, New York, Virginia, Vermont — would see Medicaid enrollment declines of 30% or more despite large, relatively well-funded state Medicaid programs. These are expansion states with high baseline Medicaid enrollment.

**California**: Approximately 63% of expansion adults in Medi-Cal already work 80+ hours per month or attend school. Despite this, the Urban Institute projects 1.2–1.4 million Californians could lose coverage — roughly one-third of expansion adults — because documentation and verification barriers will disenroll working people who cannot prove compliance. KFF notes California is implementing work requirements while simultaneously facing major budget shortfalls from reduced federal Medicaid matching.

**Texas**: The state has 6.1 million Medicaid beneficiaries and covers no expansion adults (non-expansion state). Texas faces OBBBA impacts primarily through enhanced recertification frequency for existing beneficiaries, not work requirements.

**Southern non-expansion states (12 states)**: These states face no work requirement pressure on non-existent expansion populations but face the most severe fiscal pressure through enhanced recertification for existing beneficiaries and the permanent closure of the expansion pathway. Mississippi, Alabama, Louisiana, and Georgia have the highest shares of Black population in their Medicaid-eligible but uncovered population. The 3.6 million people in the coverage gap in non-expansion states are predominantly people of color in the rural South.

## 5. Racial Disparities and the Voter Registration Nexus

### Procedural Disenrollment Is Racially Differential

The 2023–2024 Medicaid unwinding provides direct empirical evidence that procedural disenrollment — the mechanism by which OBBBA work requirements and recertification frequency will operate — falls disproportionately on people of color.

**From the National Health Law Program and PMC peer-reviewed studies**:
- Black individuals accounted for 22% of those unable to complete Medicaid renewal paperwork despite being 16.4% of the Medicaid population — a 34% overrepresentation
- Hispanic individuals accounted for 33.5% of procedural disenrollments despite being 22.7% of enrollment — a 48% overrepresentation
- Native American and Alaska Native individuals were more than twice as likely as white individuals to lose coverage during unwinding
- Black and Hispanic individuals were approximately twice as likely as white individuals to report losing coverage due to inability to complete the renewal process

These disparities are not attributable to ineligibility — they are procedural barriers: failure to receive notices due to address data errors, inability to submit forms due to work schedules or language barriers, and inadequate state outreach infrastructure in minority-majority rural areas.

**Work requirements will reproduce and amplify this pattern**. Documentation requirements for work verification — employer letters, pay stubs, job training records, community service logs — are administratively harder to produce for workers in informal, gig, seasonal, or cash-pay employment. These employment patterns are overrepresented in Black, Hispanic, and Indigenous communities. The Arkansas data confirmed this: disenrollment was concentrated among lower-income urban and rural Black residents; the program produced zero employment effect because the workers who lost coverage were either working informally or were between jobs temporarily.

### OBBBA Racial Equity Projections

AJMC's analysis of the 5 groups at highest risk of OBBBA coverage loss includes:
- Medicaid expansion adults in Southern states (majority-minority low-income populations)
- Individuals with multiple chronic conditions (40% of expansion adults)
- Seasonal and gig workers who cannot consistently document 80 hours/month
- People experiencing housing instability (address changes generate verification failures)
- Rural residents dependent on mail-based communication in areas with unreliable delivery

The Commonwealth Fund's 2026 State Health Disparities Report documented that "pronounced shifts in the national policy environment in 2025 and 2026 have likely worsened — and are on track to further exacerbate — existing racial and ethnic disparities in access, affordability, and outcomes."

### Voter Registration Infrastructure Loss

The National Voter Registration Act (42 U.S.C. § 1973gg-5) mandates that Medicaid offices offer voter registration services to applicants and recertifying enrollees. The NVRA created public assistance agencies as voter registration sites to systematically register low-income voters who might otherwise lack access to registration opportunities. When Medicaid enrollment offices reduce capacity, close, or shift to automated systems that eliminate human service interaction, the NVRA mandate is technically met but functionally gutted.

**Current voter registration uptake through Medicaid is low but structural**. Less than 2.5% of Medicaid enrollees register through point-of-service contact — but across 80+ million Medicaid enrollees, even minimal uptake generates significant registration volume. More importantly, the existence of in-person enrollment offices in low-income and minority-majority communities creates a registration infrastructure channel that is not replicated elsewhere.

**The disenrollment-to-deregistration pipeline**: Under the continuous enrollment unwinding (2023–2024), states cross-matched Medicaid disenrollment data with voter rolls in 15+ states and used Medicaid disenrollment as grounds for voter roll challenges. The Brennan Center documented that Southern states purge voter rolls at 2x the national average; several states have used public benefits disenrollment records as evidence of eligibility challenges. When 5.2 million people lose Medicaid through work requirements, those records become a data source for voter roll challenges in the same communities.

**State-specific impact**: The 12 non-expansion states are concentrated in the South — Alabama, Georgia, Mississippi, Louisiana, South Carolina, Tennessee, Texas — where Black voter registration rates are already subject to systematic challenge, voting distance requirements disproportionately affect rural Black communities, and election infrastructure funding is systematically reduced. Medicaid enrollment office closures in these states simultaneously eliminate healthcare access and the physical infrastructure for voter registration outreach.

**Automatic Voter Registration as partial mitigation**: Only a small number of states have implemented Medicaid Automatic Voter Registration (AVR), which automatically updates voter registration when Medicaid enrollment occurs. Colorado, Oregon, California, Rhode Island, and Massachusetts are among states that have passed Medicaid AVR. In states without AVR, the NVRA is implemented through paper forms with under-2.5% uptake — a failure mode that OBBBA enrollment contractions will worsen.

## 6. Section 232 Pharmaceutical Tariff Collision

### The Tariff Architecture

On April 2, 2026, the Trump administration imposed Section 232 tariffs on patented pharmaceutical products and active pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs) under the Trade Expansion Act of 1962. Key structure:

- **Standard rate**: 100%, effective July 31, 2026 (most companies) or September 29, 2026 (17 named large manufacturers)
- **Onshoring commitment rate**: 20% for companies with a Secretary of Commerce-approved domestic manufacturing plan, rising to 100% by April 2, 2030
- **MFN exemption**: Companies entering Most-Favored-Nation pricing agreements with HHS (committing to domestic manufacturing and accepting reference pricing) are exempt through January 20, 2029
- **Generic drug exemption**: Generic pharmaceuticals and biosimilars are exempt from the patented drug tariff — but APIs used to manufacture generics are NOT exempt
- **Country-specific rates**: EU, Japan, South Korea, Switzerland — 15%; UK — 100%

The generic drug exemption is critically incomplete. The supply chain for most generic drugs — especially sterile injectables representing roughly 90% of hospital-administered generics — runs: Chinese API manufacturer → Indian formulation plant → U.S. distributor. India supplies approximately 40% of U.S. generic drug volume but sources roughly 70% of its API inputs from China. The tariff on APIs hits Indian formulation manufacturers who operate on net margins of 2–8% and cannot absorb a significant tariff increase without raising prices or exiting the market.

### Impact on Medicaid Beneficiaries

**Formulary restriction under Medicaid Managed Care**: Medicaid Managed Care Organizations set formularies based on cost-effectiveness calculations. When tariffs increase patented drug costs, MCOs face three options: absorb the cost (reducing margins), seek higher state capitation rates (requiring legislative action), or restrict the formulary (unilateral and immediate). In the current political environment, formulary restriction is the path of least resistance. The result: Medicaid beneficiaries lose access to drugs that were previously covered.

**340B program compression**: The 340B drug pricing program allows safety-net hospitals and Federally Qualified Health Centers to purchase drugs at deep discounts — typically 20–50% below average manufacturer price. If manufacturers exit U.S. markets rather than absorb tariff-compounded margin compression, 340B access disappears. The American Hospital Association warned in February 2026 testimony that pharmaceutical tariffs "could have significant disruptions to the medical supply chain," with rural hospitals facing "outsized impacts from tariffs given their weaker purchasing power."

**Drug shortage cascade**: As of late 2025, 214 drugs were in active FDA shortage before the tariff took effect. Sterile injectables (epinephrine, heparin, IV fluids, certain antibiotics), oncology injectables (cisplatin, carboplatin), and ADHD medications were already in shortage. The tariff adds a cost vector to existing shortage drivers. An AJMC survey found 82% of healthcare experts expect tariff-related expenses to raise hospital costs by at least 15% over the next six months. PwC projects annual tariff-related cost increases for the healthcare sector ranging from $500 million to $63 billion depending on scope and manufacturer response.

**The MFN political calculation**: The tariff is designed in part to coerce pharmaceutical manufacturers into MFN pricing agreements — accepting reference pricing tied to the lowest international price in exchange for tariff exemption. The administration's political framing: tariffs as leverage for lower drug prices, with pharmaceutical companies bearing blame if agreements are not reached. As of April 2026, Sidley Austin documented that 13 companies had already entered MFN agreements before the tariff proclamation. The MFN strategy is directed at Medicare Part D drug prices for seniors — the most politically visible population. The Medicaid population losing coverage faces the full tariff impact with no MFN offset.

**The coverage-cliff compound**: A person disenrolled from Medicaid in January 2027 due to work requirements faces the full retail cost of drugs simultaneously repriced by 100% tariffs. The compound effect: Medicaid disenrollment (losing public drug coverage) + tariff cost increase (raising retail prices) + formulary restriction (reducing Medicaid coverage even before disenrollment) + ineligibility for marketplace premium tax credits (blocking the fallback pathway). The OBBBA + tariff collision creates a healthcare access cliff far more severe than either policy produces individually.

## 7. State Resistance Mechanisms and Litigation Landscape

### States Moving Toward Implementation

The picture is not binary — states are neither uniformly compliant nor uniformly resistant. KFF's work requirements tracker identifies a spectrum:

**Early and aggressive implementers (before January 2027 mandate)**:
- Nebraska (May 1, 2026 — active now)
- Montana (July 1, 2026)
- Arkansas (July 1, 2026 soft launch; disenrollments January 2027)
- Iowa (December 1, 2026)

**Stricter than federal law requires**:
- Idaho, Indiana, Arkansas, New Hampshire: applying longer look-back periods at application
- Indiana and New Hampshire: conducting quarterly (rather than 6-month) compliance checks

**Waiting for federal guidance before finalizing plans**: Most states (majority did not finalize implementation architecture as of KFF's January–March 2026 survey), awaiting the June 1 HHS interim rule before locking in key implementation decisions.

**States facing the highest coverage losses under any implementation**:
- Connecticut, Massachusetts, Maryland, New York, Virginia, Vermont: projected 30%+ Medicaid enrollment declines under strict implementation
- California: 1.2–1.4 million projected to lose Medi-Cal despite high workforce participation rates

### State-Level Protective Mechanisms

States cannot override the federal mandate, but they have discretion in implementation that can substantially reduce harm:

**Broad medical frailty definitions**: States can define "serious or complex medical condition" broadly, exempting more people from requirements. Corporation for Supportive Housing and NHeLP have published guidance on using medical frailty to protect coverage for individuals experiencing homelessness, substance use disorders, and chronic conditions.

**Hardship exceptions**: 29 states indicated plans for at least one hardship exemption type in the KFF survey. States with broad hardship exception policies will see substantially lower procedural disenrollment.

**Automated verification investment**: States investing in data-matching systems for employer records, school enrollment, and veteran status can reduce the documentation burden on enrollees. However, most states reported in KFF surveys that time, cost, and staff capacity constraints have limited automation progress.

**State general-fund coverage backfill**: Several states (primarily California, New York, Illinois) have indicated willingness to use state general funds to maintain coverage for individuals losing federal Medicaid eligibility. This does not eliminate the federal funding loss but prevents the coverage cliff from being absolute. California faces major budget shortfalls simultaneously.

**Medicaid AVR mandates**: States can implement Automatic Voter Registration through Medicaid enrollment, decoupling voter registration from enrollment office staffing levels. This addresses the NVRA infrastructure damage without federal authorization.

### Litigation: The Post-Issuance Challenge

The OBBBA work requirement provision is statutory — not regulatory — which changes the litigation landscape from the Arkansas precedent.

**Gresham v. Azar** (D.C. Circuit, 2020) held that the CMS approval of work requirements was arbitrary and capricious under the APA because CMS had not rationally explained how work requirements advanced Medicaid's core purpose of providing medical assistance. That ruling was against an agency waiver — CMS's discretionary approval of a state's Section 1115 waiver. Under OBBBA, work requirements are a statutory mandate, not an agency discretion exercise. Courts must now apply statutory interpretation, not arbitrary-and-capricious review of agency waiver approval.

**Three litigation pathways post-June 1**:

*Pathway 1 — APA challenge to the interim final rule itself*: The statute's exemption of the interim rule from notice-and-comment may be challengeable. Courts have held in several circuits that certain fundamental rulemaking procedures cannot be waived by Congress; others have upheld congressional waiver. If a court finds the no-comment-period provision constitutionally problematic or if HHS fails to adequately explain the rule's basis in the statute, the rule can be enjoined. The National Health Law Program is actively developing litigation strategies; the specific APA challenge to the rule's procedural status has not yet been filed as of May 2026.

*Pathway 2 — Statutory purpose challenge*: Courts applying textualist analysis post-Loper Bright (which eliminated Chevron deference) must assess whether OBBBA unambiguously authorizes work requirements as a condition of Medicaid eligibility. The Medicaid statute's core purpose (42 U.S.C. § 1396) is to "furnish medical assistance." OBBBA adds work requirements as a new condition but does not eliminate the underlying statutory purpose. A court applying textualism could find that work requirements, as implemented, frustrate the stated Medicaid purpose — particularly if implementation produces the same zero-employment-effect result as Arkansas, generating a current record of harm.

*Pathway 3 — Equal protection / racial disparities standing*: The documented racial disparities in procedural disenrollment (Black individuals 34% overrepresented; Hispanic 48% overrepresented; Native American 2x+ overrepresentation) provide the factual foundation for equal protection challenges. These are not disparate impact claims under Title VI (which have limited enforcement mechanisms post-*SFFA v. Harvard*) but rather claims that facially neutral verification requirements produce racially identifiable harm and that HHS guidance failed to consider this harm in rule formulation.

**Medina v. Planned Parenthood South Atlantic** (SCOTUS 2025): The Supreme Court's June 2025 decision substantially narrowed Medicaid beneficiaries' right to sue states under 42 U.S.C. § 1983. This decision complicates individual beneficiary suits against state Medicaid programs but does not affect organizational standing (NHeLP, ACLU, state AGs) or APA challenges to federal guidance.

### Advocacy Actions — Priority Order

**Immediate (before June 1)**

1. Stakeholder meeting requests to HHS/CMS: No comment period does not preclude pre-rulemaking engagement. Organizations with HHS relationships (SEIU, NAACP, American Academy of Pediatrics, American Hospital Association) should document the racial disparity evidence and rural hospital closure data before June 1 for the administrative record.

2. Congressional notification: Key House and Senate appropriators should receive the full hospital closure map showing at-risk facilities in Republican-held districts. The $50 billion Rural Health Fund inadequacy (covers only 37% of rural losses) is the most compelling argument for modification-friendly Republicans.

3. State Medicaid director outreach: Medicaid directors in large-enrollment states can begin now on medical frailty definitions and hardship exception policies. The June 1 guidance will constrain but not eliminate state discretion on exemption scope.

**Post-June 1 — Litigation and Documentation**

4. APA challenge assessment: Within 30 days of June 1 rule issuance, analyze whether the rule's no-comment-period exemption is legally adequate, whether the rule provides sufficient statutory basis for its implementation choices, and whether HHS considered the racial disparity evidence. File APA challenge if grounds exist.

5. Racial equity record-building: Document demographic breakdown of disenrollments in Nebraska (the only live implementation) in the May–July 2026 window. This creates the evidentiary record for equal protection claims when full national implementation begins January 2027.

6. NVRA enforcement: File complaints or litigation under NVRA in states where Medicaid enrollment office capacity reductions demonstrably reduce voter registration services. The NVRA enforcement mechanism (42 U.S.C. § 1973gg-9) allows the Attorney General to file civil actions; state AGs in expansion states have parallel standing.

**July 31 Tariff Window**

7. Hospital and pharmacy coalition materials: The day the pharmaceutical tariffs take effect, healthcare coalitions should have pre-prepared cost impact documentation ready — first-month hospital cost increases, formulary restriction announcements, 340B impact assessments. This is the moment for maximum public visibility.

8. Section 232 API tariff legal challenge assessment: Generic pharmaceutical manufacturers, 340B safety-net hospitals, and state Medicaid programs may have standing to challenge the tariff's application to APIs — which nominally exempt generics but apply to the inputs generics require. This argument has not yet been tested in litigation.

**January 2027 Legislative Window**

9. Congressional first-90-days strategy: If November 2026 produces Democratic gains in either chamber, the new Congress's first 90 days is the window for emergency legislation. The combination of: CBO-documented 11.8 million coverage losses, live Nebraska/Montana/Iowa/Arkansas disenrollment data, hospital closure documentation, and tariff cost impact data creates the strongest possible legislative case.
