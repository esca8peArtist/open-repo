## Executive Summary

The United States enters the 2026 midterm election cycle with its foreign-interference defense architecture more thoroughly dismantled than at any point since the pre-2016 era — and with adversary spending on interference operations at historic highs. This is not an accident. It is the product of a series of deliberate institutional decisions made by the current administration, combined with a structural failure that predates it: the United States has never built a statutory framework for defending its democratic institutions against foreign interference that can survive a hostile administration.

The dismantlement is documented and specific. Attorney General Pam Bondi disbanded the FBI's Foreign Influence Task Force on her first day in office — three squads, approximately 50 agents and support personnel — and simultaneously narrowed criminal FARA enforcement to "conduct similar to traditional espionage," effectively decriminalizing the covert influence operations that Russia, China, Iran, and Gulf-state actors have deployed against US elections and civic institutions. Gen. Timothy Haugh, who headed both the NSA and US Cyber Command and led cyber operations that protected the 2024 election from foreign interference, was fired in April 2025. His civilian deputy was also removed. The Director of National Intelligence, Tulsi Gabbard, produced the first annual threat assessment since 2017 that did not identify foreign election interference as a threat — removing the public warning function that had, however imperfectly, given civil society organizations and state election officials signal about the threat environment.

Into this vacuum, adversary powers have poured unprecedented resources. Russia increased its state media and information operations budget by 54 percent for 2026, adding $458 million. China's aggregate annual spending on foreign influence operations — across the United Front Work Department, state media, digital operations, and governance assistance programs — is projected to exceed $10 billion in 2026. Iran has intensified its AI-assisted social media operations. The Freedom House *Freedom in the World 2026* report documents the 20th consecutive year of global democratic decline; authoritarian powers "now possess both the confidence and the capacity to reshape the international order."

Simultaneously, the US destroyed its own democracy-promotion infrastructure. Roughly 97 percent of USAID democracy and governance grants — more than 1,600 programs worth over $14 billion — were terminated. The National Endowment for Democracy, which funds civil society organizations in over 100 countries, had its funding blocked through a Treasury Department refusal to release approved appropriations; the administration has proposed eliminating NED entirely in the FY26 budget. The State Department's Bureau of Democracy, Human Rights and Labor has been effectively hollowed out. The consequence is a bidirectional feedback loop: as US democracy promotion collapses, authoritarian powers fill the vacuum in countries that depended on US support, accelerating global democratic backsliding; and as those countries slide toward authoritarianism, they become conduits and platforms for the foreign interference operations that target US elections.

This domain documents: (1) the mechanisms by which foreign actors interfere in US democratic institutions; (2) the systematic dismantling of US counterintelligence and election-security defenses since January 2025; (3) the strategic consequences of the collapse of US democracy promotion; (4) the statutory and institutional architecture that a reformed government would build; and (5) what the comparative record — Canada, Germany, the European Union — shows about building effective democratic defenses that do not compromise civil liberties.

**Core finding**: The United States faces a historically unprecedented combination of elevated foreign interference threat and collapsed domestic defense architecture, entering an election cycle with no FBI task force, no coordinated CISA election security function, a suppressed threat assessment, and an adversary spending environment that dwarfs anything the country has previously faced. Statutory reform — not executive action, which can be revoked overnight — is the only durable defense.

## 1. The Threat Architecture: How Foreign Actors Interfere in US Democratic Institutions

### 1.1 Six Documented Interference Mechanisms

The Alliance for Securing Democracy's Authoritarian Interference Tracker, which catalogued incidents of Russian and Chinese interference in more than 40 transatlantic countries from 2000 through 2025, identified six primary mechanisms: information manipulation, cyber operations, malign finance, civil society subversion, economic coercion, and kinetic operations. The United States has been subject to all six, with the first three most extensively documented in the public record.

**Information manipulation** is the highest-volume mechanism. Russia's Internet Research Agency operations during the 2016 and 2020 cycles — documented in the Senate Intelligence Committee's bipartisan five-volume report — involved the creation of thousands of fake American personas, the purchase of social media advertising targeting specific demographic segments, the organization of real-world protest events by foreign agents posing as American activists, and the amplification of polarizing content to maximize societal division. The Senate Intelligence Committee's 2020 report found that Russia "conducted an extensive social media campaign that was overtly designed to assist" the Trump campaign and that the IRA's operations "were designed to worsen racial tensions in the United States, undermine trust in media and government, and exacerbate political divisions."

For the 2026 midterms, Russia has increased its information operations budget by $458 million — 54 percent above its 2025 level — with a documented strategic focus on AI-assisted persona generation that allows adversarial actors to operate thousands of distinct social media accounts producing unique, convincing content at scale without the human labor costs that made earlier operations detectable. The 2026 threat is not the 2016 Internet Research Agency; it is industrialized AI-generated influence content operating at speeds and volumes that 2016-era detection methods cannot track.

**Cyber operations** targeting election infrastructure are the second major mechanism. The US Senate Intelligence Committee documented 18 states where Russian actors scanned election-related infrastructure prior to the 2016 election; Illinois saw actual voter registration database penetration with data on 200,000 voters exfiltrated. The defensive response built between 2017 and 2024 — CISA's election security programs, the Albert network of sensors deployed in state election infrastructure, Gen. Haugh's Cyber Command offensive operations against adversary interference platforms — successfully protected the 2024 election from comparable attacks. That defensive infrastructure has now been substantially dismantled: CISA's election security program faces a $700 million budget cut in the proposed FY27 budget; Gen. Haugh is gone; the FBI's Foreign Influence Task Force is disbanded.

**Malign finance** — foreign funding of US political actors, think tanks, media organizations, and campaigns — is the most legally complex mechanism because it operates in a disclosure gap. Under FARA, agents of foreign governments must register and disclose their activities, but the domestic interest exemption (allowing agents who represent foreign principals but primarily serve US interests to avoid FARA registration) and the think tank funding gap (think tanks are not subject to FARA registration requirements regardless of their funding sources) create substantial avenues for foreign political finance that is neither disclosed nor prosecuted.

The documented scale is substantial. Arab Gulf states — Saudi Arabia, UAE, Qatar — have channeled more than $15 billion to US universities since 2009, according to tracking of Department of Education disclosure requirements. Qatar's total donations to US universities exceeded $6.6 billion; Saudi Arabia's exceeded $3.9 billion; the UAE's exceeded $1.7 billion. Think tank funding from the same sources flows through channels with no disclosure requirements: the UAE's $20 million contribution to the Middle East Institute, Qatar's $14.8 million contribution to the Brookings Institution, and Saudi funding to at least a dozen Washington policy institutions are documented in investigative journalism because the institutions chose to disclose — not because any law required it. The Quincy Institute's 2024 report on think tank funding, "Big Ideas and Big Money," documents that foreign government contributions to think tanks "can be as valuable as spending on lobbyists" precisely because the funding is less regulated and the research it produces is not labeled as advocacy.

Chinese financing of US political influence runs through additional channels beyond FARA's reach: the United Front Work Department's operations through Chinese student associations, overseas Chinese community organizations, and Confucius Institutes; sovereign wealth fund investments in US media companies and technology platforms; and state-owned enterprise investments in US technology with dual-use applications. The Senate Intelligence Committee's China-focused reports and the Justice Department's China Initiative (before its political termination in 2022) documented the scope of these operations, though the public record on current UFWD operations is fragmentary.

### 1.2 Foreign Finance in US Campaigns: The Legal Architecture and Its Gaps

Federal law prohibits foreign nationals from contributing to or spending in US elections (52 U.S.C. § 30121). The prohibition covers direct contributions and independent expenditures. But three structural gaps allow foreign political finance to enter US electoral politics through indirect channels.

First, the foreign national prohibition does not apply to US corporations with foreign parent companies unless the foreign parent directly controls the US subsidiary's political spending decisions. A US-incorporated subsidiary of a foreign state-owned enterprise can make unlimited independent expenditures through a SuperPAC without violating the foreign national prohibition, provided the corporation can demonstrate that US personnel made the spending decision.

Second, the dark money gap: nonprofit organizations incorporated under 501(c)(4) can accept unlimited donations, including from corporations with foreign ownership structures, and make unlimited expenditures in US elections without disclosing their donors. The Brennan Center's 2025 analysis documented $1.9 billion in dark money spending in the 2024 election cycle — nearly double 2020 levels. The percentage of that total with foreign-origin funding is structurally unknowable under current law.

Third, as noted above, the FARA domestic interest exemption and the think tank gap allow foreign political influence at the level of policy development and congressional advocacy without disclosure. When a think tank funded by a Gulf sovereign wealth fund produces research that supports Gulf-friendly US foreign and defense policy, and that research is then cited by Members of Congress in floor debate, the influence operation is legally invisible.

### 1.3 Chinese Influence Operations: The UFWD Architecture

China's approach to US influence operations is structurally distinct from Russia's information warfare model. The primary mechanism is the United Front Work Department, a CCP organ that coordinates overseas Chinese communities, academic exchanges, business networks, and media operations as tools for building political influence and countering narratives adverse to Chinese interests.

UFWD operations in the United States are documented across multiple vectors: Chinese student and scholar associations at US universities, which the FBI has described as potential UFWD fronts that can be activated to suppress politically sensitive speakers, report on Chinese dissidents, and influence campus discourse; overseas Chinese community media organizations, many of which have received funding from Chinese state entities and carry Chinese state media content; and the Thousand Talents Program and related academic recruitment schemes, which the Senate Subcommittee on Investigations documented as Chinese government-directed efforts to transfer US research and technology.

The Linda Sun case — a former aide to New York Governor Kathy Hochul who was indicted in September 2024 on FARA charges alleging she acted as an agent of the Chinese government and Chinese consul — illustrates the state and local government penetration dimension of Chinese influence operations that tends to receive less attention than federal-level targets. Sun allegedly provided Chinese consular officials with privileged access to the governor's office and restricted a Taiwan official's access to events. The case ended in a hung jury in December 2025.

### 1.4 Iran and Gulf-State Operations

Iran's 2024 operations — documented in the Office of the Director of National Intelligence's election security reports (before those reports were suppressed in 2026) — focused on email intrusion targeting campaign staff and AI-assisted social media content designed to intensify social divisions. The Justice Department's 2024 indictment of Iranian nationals for hack-and-leak operations targeting the Trump campaign illustrated the bidirectional nature of Iran's operations: unlike Russia, which targeted the 2016 election to benefit Trump, Iran's stated objective in 2024 was to harm Trump's electoral prospects — demonstrating that foreign interference is not a partisan tool but a structural vulnerability that adversaries can direct as their interests dictate.

Gulf-state operations are less focused on electoral interference per se and more concentrated on the policy influence channels described above — think tank funding, lobbying infrastructure, and the cultivation of US political figures through legal channels that fall below FARA registration thresholds. The Saudi government's $2 billion investment through a Saudi sovereign wealth fund in Jared Kushner's private equity firm, and the concurrent US policy accommodations Saudi Arabia received during the Trump administration, exemplifies the policy-influence mechanism at its most direct: the transaction is legal, disclosed, and structurally indistinguishable from the influence operations FARA was designed to prevent.

## 2. The Dismantling of US Counterintelligence and Democratic Defense

### 2.1 The FBI Foreign Influence Task Force: Disbanded

The FBI's Foreign Influence Task Force was established in 2017 under Director Christopher Wray, in direct response to the 2016 Russian interference documented by the Senate Intelligence Committee. It comprised three operational squads of approximately 50 agents and support personnel, and was responsible for: investigating foreign interference in US elections; prosecuting FARA violations; identifying foreign information operations and providing classified briefings to state and local election officials; and coordinating with CISA, NSA, and Cyber Command on the joint election security architecture built between 2017 and 2024.

Attorney General Pam Bondi disbanded the task force on February 5, 2025 — one of 14 policy memos issued on her second day in office. The disbandment memo stated that the Justice Department would "shift resources in its National Security Division to free resources to address more pressing priorities, and end risks of further weaponization and abuses of prosecutorial discretion." Former FBI officials and members of Congress described the action as "an invitation to foreign actors." Sen. Mark Warner (D-VA), ranking member of the Senate Intelligence Committee, warned: "We're going into a 2026 election cycle with our guard down."

The Bondi memo also narrowed criminal FARA enforcement: charges under FARA and 18 U.S.C. § 951 (acting as an unregistered agent of a foreign government) would now be limited to "conduct similar to more traditional espionage by foreign government actors." This formulation effectively raises the prosecution threshold to the level of spy-ring activity, placing the large middle category of FARA violations — covert political consulting, undisclosed lobbying, influence operations conducted through domestic intermediaries — outside the criminal enforcement perimeter.

### 2.2 NSA and Cyber Command: Leadership Removed

Gen. Timothy Haugh was fired in April 2025, the day after submitting new offensive cyber operations plans against adversary interference platforms. His civilian deputy at the NSA, Wendy Noble, was also removed. No explanation was provided. Prior to his removal, Haugh had told Congress that his command ran "numerous operations countering Russian meddling" in the 2024 election cycle and that the coordinated federal election security infrastructure "was driven by the first Trump administration's direct guidance to NSA and Cyber Command."

The consequences for 2026 are structural: the offensive cyber operations component of the US election defense architecture — which disrupted Russian troll farm infrastructure during the 2018 and 2020 cycles — lost institutional continuity at its leadership level during the period when operational planning for 2026 midterm protection would have been underway.

### 2.3 CISA Election Security: Budget Elimination Proposed

The Trump administration's FY27 budget proposes $700 million in CISA budget cuts and the complete elimination of CISA's election security program. This follows the 2025 termination of CISA's Mis-, Dis-, and Malinformation (MDM) team — the unit responsible for flagging election disinformation to social media platforms — and the effective elimination of the Election Infrastructure Information Sharing and Analysis Center (EI-ISAC) through staffing reductions.

The political motivation for these cuts is explicit in the public record: Trump and his advisers never forgave CISA leadership for the "Rumor Control" website that debunked 2020 election fraud claims, including Trump's own claims, and for Director Chris Krebs's statement that the 2020 election was "the most secure in American history." The institutional destruction of CISA election security is, in this sense, both a political retaliation and a structural opportunity for foreign adversaries: the state and local election officials who depended on CISA's Albert sensor network, real-time threat intelligence sharing, and technical assistance for their election system security have lost their primary federal support infrastructure entering the 2026 cycle.

### 2.4 The Annual Threat Assessment: Foreign Election Interference Removed

The Office of the Director of National Intelligence's Annual Threat Assessment has identified foreign election interference as a threat in every iteration since 2017. The 2026 assessment, released in March 2026 under DNI Tulsi Gabbard, omitted any mention of foreign threats to US elections — the first such omission in nine years. Gabbard stated publicly that the intelligence community had "identified no foreign threat to upcoming congressional elections."

Sen. Warner characterized the omission as unprecedented and inconsistent with classified intelligence the committee was receiving. The Defense One and Nextgov reporting on the omission documented that the 2026 assessment's gap was particularly striking given that Russia, China, and Iran had all publicly increased their information operations budgets for the 2026 cycle. Russia's 54 percent spending increase and China's projected $10 billion annual interference budget are documented in open-source reporting; their exclusion from the official IC threat assessment is a policy choice, not an intelligence finding.

The consequence for democratic defense is direct: the annual threat assessment is the primary mechanism by which civil society organizations, state election officials, academic researchers, and the public receive official warning about the interference threat environment. Its suppression is functionally equivalent to removing the early warning system.

### 2.5 The FARA Enforcement Inversion

The Bondi memo's narrowing of FARA criminal enforcement produced a structural paradox in 2025: while the Trump administration simultaneously used NSPM-7 (a September 2025 National Security Presidential Memorandum) to direct the FBI to investigate NGOs and American citizens with foreign ties under FARA, the actual covert foreign influence operations that FARA was designed to reach — Russian information operations, UFWD activities, Gulf-state think tank funding — were no longer criminal enforcement priorities.

The NSPM-7 direction is documented in the law firm reporting on FARA enforcement updates: it directed Joint Terrorism Task Forces to investigate "non-governmental organizations and American citizens residing abroad or with close ties to foreign governments engaged in violations of FARA." In practice, this reoriented FARA enforcement away from foreign principals and their domestic agents — the statutory target — and toward domestic civil society organizations with international ties, including human rights organizations, foreign policy research groups, and diaspora community organizations. The inversion is precise: FARA's original enforcement mission (protecting American democracy from foreign covert influence) was subordinated to a new enforcement mission (surveilling American civil society with international connections).

## 3. The Democracy Promotion Collapse: Consequences and Feedback

### 3.1 The Scale of the USAID Shutdown

By March 2025, the Trump administration had terminated approximately 97 percent of USAID democracy and governance grants — more than 1,600 programs — along with 83 percent of USAID's overall grant portfolio, involving approximately 5,200 contracts worth over $80 billion. The Congressional Research Service documented the scale and legal basis of these terminations. The Carnegie Endowment for International Peace characterized the outcome as a "startling reversal" of US global anti-corruption and democracy policy — the most significant retraction of US democracy promotion since the programs were built in the 1980s and 1990s.

NED, which funds civil society organizations in more than 100 countries, had its congressionally appropriated funding blocked by a Treasury Department refusal to release authorized funds — an executive action of questionable legality given that the funds were legally appropriated by Congress. NED eventually regained access to partial funding and resumed limited grantmaking, but its annual budget is a fraction of what was eliminated from USAID, and the administration's FY26 budget proposes eliminating NED funding entirely.

### 3.2 The Authoritarian Vacuum

The strategic consequence is documented across multiple policy research organizations and government sources. When USAID terminated its grants on July 1, 2025, China, Russia, and Gulf states moved to fill the void in countries that had depended on US democracy support.

China's Belt and Road Initiative governance assistance programs — infrastructure financing, security partnerships, digital surveillance technology exports — expanded into countries losing US program support. The Northeastern University Political Review documented that "Beijing is filling the vacuum through targeted political aid, intensified regional diplomacy, and localized development projects, collectively repositioning China as the most dependable external partner in parts of the Global South." China's Digital Silk Road project markets surveillance technologies — including facial recognition systems, social media monitoring platforms, and telecommunications infrastructure designed for state interception — as governance assistance, creating the physical infrastructure of authoritarian control in countries that receive it.

Russia's expanded information operations budget and its governance assistance through the Wagner Group's successor structures in Sub-Saharan Africa represent a parallel vacuum-filling operation. Countries in the Sahel, East Africa, and Central Asia that had received US governance and civil society support are documenting transitions to Russian and Chinese governance partnerships.

The Freedom House *Freedom in the World 2026* report — documenting the 20th consecutive year of global democratic decline — found that the 2025 deteriorations affected more than 40 percent of the world's population, with the US no longer functioning as a counterweight to authoritarian expansion. The report's assessment of the US's own democratic status showed continued decline from the 28-point drop documented in the 2022 report.

### 3.3 The Feedback Mechanism

The relationship between US democracy promotion collapse and foreign interference in US elections is bidirectional. Countries that backslide toward authoritarianism because of the US withdrawal do not simply become governance problems in their own right; they become operational platforms and complicit partners for the foreign interference operations that target US elections.

The PNAS Nexus research cited in the gap analysis documented that US democratic backsliding "damages favorable US image" internationally — reducing the soft power that makes the US a credible democracy advocate. The Council on Foreign Relations' 2026 survey data on international perceptions of the United States showed historically low confidence in US democratic institutions and governance internationally — a deterioration that reduces the US's ability to build the international coalitions that democratic defense requires.

For the domestic reform architecture, the feedback loop means that rebuilding US democracy promotion is not only a foreign policy goal — it is a domestic security imperative. Countries with strong US-supported civil society are less likely to become platforms for the foreign interference operations that harm US democratic institutions.

## 4. Comparative Democratic Defense Architecture

### 4.1 Canada: The Critical Election Incident Public Protocol

Canada's Critical Election Incident Public Protocol, established for the 2019 federal election, is the most developed statutory framework for foreign election interference defense among comparable democracies. The Protocol establishes a permanent independent panel of five senior civil servants (the Clerk of the Privy Council, the National Security and Intelligence Advisor, the Deputy Minister of Justice, the Deputy Minister of Public Safety, and the Deputy Minister of Global Affairs) authorized to publicly disclose to Canadians, during an election campaign, incidents that threaten Canada's ability to have a free and fair election.

The Panel's authority is derived from a Cabinet Directive, not legislation — a design weakness the Trudeau government acknowledged and that the Commission of Inquiry into Foreign Interference in Federal Electoral Processes and Democratic Institutions (the Hogue Commission) recommended correcting through statute. The 2025 Canadian federal election — held March 2025 — was protected by an updated CEIPP that included expanded intelligence integration and a streamlined disclosure process. The Panel did not publicly invoke the Protocol during the 2025 election, but its existence as a pre-established, publicly known mechanism provides the warning architecture that the US has never built.

The Security and Intelligence Threats to Elections (SITE) Task Force — comprising the Communications Security Establishment, the Canadian Security Intelligence Service, Global Affairs Canada, and the Royal Canadian Mounted Police — provides the operational intelligence integration layer that the Canadian CEIPP draws on. The SITE Task Force is a standing interagency body, not an election-cycle creation, providing institutional continuity that the US's disbanded FBI task force model cannot match.

### 4.2 Germany: Bundesverfassungsschutz and the Statutory Defense Framework

Germany's approach to foreign interference defense is centered on the Bundesverfassungsschutz (BfV — Office for the Protection of the Constitution), a domestic intelligence service with an explicit statutory mandate to monitor and counter foreign interference in democratic institutions. Unlike the FBI's Foreign Influence Task Force — a specialized unit within a law enforcement agency that can be disbanded by an attorney general memo — the BfV's mandate is statutory, its existence is constitutionally rooted, and its dissolution would require legislation.

Germany's post-2016 experience with Russian information operations targeting the Bundestag and the Alternative for Germany (AfD) funding from Russian-linked entities produced a series of statutory strengthening measures: the 2017 Network Enforcement Act (NetzDG), requiring social media platforms to remove illegal content including election disinformation; the strengthened BfV mandate for monitoring foreign state-sponsored disinformation; and the expansion of the BfV's annual reporting obligation to include foreign influence operations targeting German elections.

The 2025 German federal election demonstrated the architecture's resilience: the BfV publicly attributed several influence operations to Russian state-linked actors in the pre-election period, producing public awareness that both informed voters and deterred escalation. Germany's constitutional Streitbare Demokratie (militant democracy) doctrine — the principle that the constitutional order can defend itself against threats, including through restricting activities that threaten democratic foundations — provides the legal architecture for an assertive democratic defense that the US constitutional structure has not developed.

### 4.3 The European Union: Digital Services Act and the Code of Practice on Disinformation

The EU's most recent contribution to comparative democratic defense is the integration of the voluntary Code of Practice on Disinformation into the Digital Services Act framework, effective July 1, 2025. This integration makes the Code's commitments — adopted by major platforms including Facebook, Instagram, LinkedIn, Bing, TikTok, YouTube, and Google Search — legally auditable for the first time. The Code requires platforms to take "risk-based measures" to mitigate the societal risks posed by the spread of disinformation, including foreign-state-sponsored information operations.

The DSA framework's implications for US democratic defense are two-directional. The platforms bound by the EU's Code are the same platforms that carry foreign interference content in US elections. The EU framework's transparency and audit requirements apply to platform conduct globally, not only in the EU — creating an international accountability standard that reaches US political information operations by regulating the platforms that carry them. For the US reform architecture, the DSA model offers a statutory approach to platform accountability for election disinformation that does not require the First Amendment difficulties that direct US content regulation creates: the regulation is of platform systems and risk-mitigation processes, not of specific content.

## 5. The Current Statutory Framework and Its Failures

### 5.1 FARA: The Original Architecture and Its Erosion

The Foreign Agents Registration Act (22 U.S.C. § 611 et seq.) was enacted in 1938 to require agents of foreign principals to register with the Justice Department and disclose their activities. As originally conceived — and as the Senate Intelligence Committee's 2020 Russia report recommended strengthening — FARA should be the primary statutory vehicle for detecting and deterring foreign covert influence. Its actual enforcement record tells a different story.

Between 1966 and 2015, DOJ averaged fewer than one FARA prosecution per year. The 2019 Biden-era enforcement revival — driven in part by the Russia investigation's revelation of the extent of unregistered foreign agent activity — produced several notable prosecutions and a 2021-2022 increase in registrations. The Trump administration revoked that enforcement posture within weeks of taking office in 2025.

FARA's structural weaknesses are documented in the DOJ Inspector General's 2016 audit and the Senate Intelligence Committee's analysis:

- The domestic interest exemption allows agents who represent foreign principals but can characterize their activities as serving domestic interests to avoid registration entirely
- Registration is voluntary and self-reported; there is no investigative mechanism for identifying unregistered agents other than the FBI task force that has now been disbanded
- Civil penalties for registration failures are minimal; criminal prosecution (the deterrent mechanism) has been effectively suspended
- Think tank funding, university donations, and sovereign wealth fund investments in US media and technology are outside FARA's registration requirements entirely, regardless of the policy influence they purchase

The Senate in January 2026 advanced two bills to broaden disclosure requirements: the Disclosing Foreign Influence in Lobbying Act (S. 856) and the Lobbying Disclosure Improvement Act (S. 865). These bills address the lobbying disclosure gap — registered lobbyists would need to disclose foreign government entities that direct their lobbying activities — but do not address the think tank funding gap, the domestic interest exemption, or the enforcement architecture.

### 5.2 The Campaign Finance Foreign Nationality Prohibition: Gaps

The federal prohibition on foreign national spending in US elections (52 U.S.C. § 30121) has never been effectively enforced against the indirect channels through which foreign political finance actually flows into US campaigns: US subsidiaries of foreign companies, dark money vehicles that accept corporate donations with foreign-origin funding, and the closely held corporation problem.

The FEC has issued advisory opinions establishing that US corporations with foreign parents can make independent expenditures if US employees control the spending decision, without investigating whether the "US control" determination is genuine. The dark money channel is structurally invisible: without donor disclosure requirements for 501(c)(4) organizations, there is no mechanism for detecting foreign-origin funding that passes through corporate layers to political committees.

The Preventing Foreign Interference in American Elections Act (S. 3129, 119th Congress) would require additional disclosures and strengthen the prohibition's enforcement, but it faces the same partisan blockage that has prevented campaign finance reform throughout the current period.

## 6. Reform Architecture: Building Durable Structural Defenses

The following reform proposals are designed against a specific design constraint: they must survive a hostile administration. Executive orders are inadequate — they are revocable the moment the next administration takes office, as the Biden AI governance executive order, the Biden CISA structure, and the Biden FARA enforcement posture all demonstrated. Statutory reform — enacted through legislation with enforcement mechanisms that do not depend on executive branch good faith — is the only durable architecture.

### 6.1 Pathway One: FARA Structural Reform

**The Foreign Agents Registration Accountability Act** (proposed statutory framework):

- **Mandatory annual renewal**: FARA registration must be renewed annually with updated disclosure of all principals, all activities, and all compensation received. The current one-time registration model allows registrants to go years without updated disclosure.

- **Civil penalty schedule with real deterrence**: Current civil penalties for registration failures are insufficient deterrence. The reform would establish tiered civil penalties: $50,000 per month for each month of unregistered operation, increasing to $250,000 for willful violations, with an automatic criminal referral mechanism for identified agents of foreign adversary governments (Russia, China, Iran, North Korea as designated by the Director of National Intelligence) who operate without registration.

- **Closure of the domestic interest exemption**: The domestic interest exemption would be narrowed to apply only to activities that are genuinely and primarily in the interest of the US public (e.g., academic research, humanitarian aid distribution) and would not exempt political consulting, lobbying, public relations, or media activities regardless of the client's characterization of their goals.

- **Think tank and university disclosure requirement**: Organizations with annual revenues exceeding $1 million that receive foreign government or foreign sovereign wealth fund contributions exceeding $100,000 annually would be required to register those contributions with DOJ and disclose them in annual public filings — a registration requirement parallel to FARA but applicable to institutional recipients rather than individual agents.

- **Independent FARA Enforcement Unit**: Remove the FARA Unit from direct DOJ/FBI supervision. Establish it as an independent unit within DOJ whose director is appointed for a fixed six-year term, confirmed by the Senate, and removable only for cause — parallel to the structure proposed in Domain 29 for the Special Counsel. This structural protection prevents the unit from being disbanded by an attorney general's first-day memo.

### 6.2 Pathway Two: Foreign Interference Early Warning Commission

**The Election Security and Foreign Interference Commission Act** (proposed statutory framework, modeled on the Canadian CEIPP):

- **Permanent statutory body**: A bipartisan commission of seven members — three appointed by Democratic congressional leadership, three by Republican leadership, one by the President from a pool of retired judges proposed by the Federal Judicial Conference — with six-year staggered terms and for-cause-only removal.

- **Election Threat Assessment mandate**: The Commission would be required to produce a public pre-election threat assessment, no later than 90 days before any federal election, assessing foreign interference threats to that election. The assessment would draw on classified intelligence provided by the IC under statutory reporting obligations and would include a public unclassified summary sufficient to inform civil society organizations, state election officials, and the public.

- **Incident disclosure authority**: During the final 60 days before a federal election, the Commission would be authorized to publicly disclose specific incidents — with appropriate information security review — that constitute a credible threat to the integrity of the election. This authority addresses the suppression problem documented in 2026: a statutory body cannot be silenced by a DNI memo.

- **State and local election official briefing mandate**: The Commission would be required to provide classified threat briefings to all 50 state chief election officials and the election officials of jurisdictions with populations over 50,000 at least twice in each federal election cycle — restoring the briefing function that CISA's election security program provided before its dismantlement.

### 6.3 Pathway Three: Democracy Promotion Restoration

**The Democracy Promotion Integrity and Independence Act** (proposed statutory framework):

- **NED statutory independence**: NED's funding would be established as a mandatory appropriation at a floor of 0.05% of federal discretionary spending (approximately $750 million at current levels), with the Treasury Department prohibited from withholding duly appropriated funds. The mechanism parallels the approach in Domain 34 (Congressional Power of the Purse) — a statutory impoundment prohibition specific to democracy promotion.

- **USAID Democracy and Governance baseline**: A minimum of 15% of USAID's total foreign assistance budget would be statutorily designated for democracy, governance, and civil society programs, with a supermajority congressional vote required to reduce the baseline below 10%.

- **State Department Bureau of Democracy, Human Rights and Labor (DRL) protection**: The Bureau's organizational structure and minimum staffing levels would be established in statute, preventing administrative abolition through reorganization authority.

- **Alliance architecture**: The Secretary of State would be required to propose, within 180 days of the Act's passage, a framework for multilateral democracy promotion coordination — a successor to the Summit for Democracy model — that distributes democracy promotion investment across allied democracies to reduce single-country vulnerability to US withdrawal.

### 6.4 Pathway Four: Authoritarian Influence Registry

**The Authoritarian Influence Transparency Act** (proposed statutory framework, extending the FARA model):

A public database, maintained by the FARA Unit's successor independent body, of:

- **Foreign government investments in US information infrastructure**: Any foreign government or sovereign wealth fund investment exceeding $10 million in a US media company, digital platform, news organization, or news aggregation service would require disclosure to the Registry within 90 days of closing.

- **Foreign government funding of US policy research institutions**: Any contribution exceeding $500,000 in any three-year period from a foreign government, foreign sovereign wealth fund, or foreign state-owned enterprise to a US think tank, policy research institution, or university program with policy research functions would require Registry disclosure.

- **Congressional notification**: Registry disclosures would trigger automatic notification to the chairs and ranking members of the Senate Intelligence, Foreign Relations, and Judiciary Committees and their House counterparts, with a 60-day review period during which the committees could request classified supplemental information.

The Registry model addresses the specific transparency gap that FARA's agent-registration model does not: institutional funding that purchases policy influence without creating a registerable agency relationship.

## 7. Implementation Requirements and Political Pathway

### 7.1 What Can Be Done Before the 2026 Midterms

Several of the most urgent defensive measures do not require legislation and could be implemented through executive action by a reform-committed administration or through non-federal action in the near term:

**State-level FARA equivalents**: Twenty states have enacted foreign contribution bans for ballot measure campaigns; Arkansas and Nebraska enacted "Baby FARA" statutes in 2025 requiring registration of agents of adversary nations. Expansion to additional states — particularly states with competitive 2026 races — is achievable through state legislative action and does not require federal cooperation.

**Civil society coordination**: The Alliance for Securing Democracy's merger with ISD-US (effective January 1, 2026) creates a consolidated institutional infrastructure for tracking and publicly documenting foreign interference incidents. Organizations including Protect Democracy, the Brennan Center's Democracy Program, and the Election Protection coalitions can build a nongovernmental early warning function that partially substitutes for the statutory commission that legislation would create — publishing pre-election threat analyses based on open-source intelligence that fills the void left by the suppressed DNI threat assessment.

**Platform transparency advocacy**: The DSA's integration of the Code of Practice on Disinformation creates leverage for US civil society organizations to hold platforms to transparency commitments based on their EU compliance obligations — specifically, requiring that transparency reports about foreign-state-sponsored information operations be made publicly available in the US as well as in EU jurisdictions.

### 7.2 Legislative Pathway

The full statutory reform architecture — the Foreign Agents Registration Accountability Act, the Election Security and Foreign Interference Commission Act, the Democracy Promotion Integrity and Independence Act, and the Authoritarian Influence Transparency Act — requires legislation. In the current Congress, none of these proposals has a viable path to passage.

The 2026 midterm elections are the electoral prerequisite. If the midterms produce a Democratic House majority or reduce the Republican Senate margin to a level where bipartisan democracy reform coalitions become viable, the FARA and election security components of this architecture have demonstrated bipartisan support in prior legislative sessions: the Russia sanctions legislation and the Intelligence Authorization Act's election security provisions both achieved bipartisan votes in the 115th and 116th Congresses.

The political sequencing logic is:

1. **Pre-election (2026)**: Nongovernmental early warning infrastructure; state-level enforcement actions; platform transparency advocacy; academic and civil society documentation of foreign interference incidents
2. **Post-2026 election (if Democratic gains)**: FARA structural reform and the Election Security Commission Act as the first-wave legislation — bipartisan framing around election security that avoids direct partisan conflict
3. **Full reform window**: Democracy Promotion Restoration Act and Authoritarian Influence Registry as second-wave legislation, requiring a broader legislative majority and more sustained political mobilization

### 7.3 Cross-Domain Integration

This domain's reform architecture integrates with existing domains in ways that require coordinated sequencing:

**Domain 29 (DOJ Capture)**: The Independent FARA Enforcement Unit's structural protection model — a fixed-term director removable only for cause — is parallel to the independent special counsel reforms in Domain 29. Both reforms address the same underlying vulnerability: DOJ enforcement capacity that can be dismantled by day-one executive action.

**Domain 37-existing (Federal Executive Interference in 2026 Midterms)**: The foreign interference threat documented in this domain is operating through the same institutional gaps created by CISA's dismantlement and the DOJ's reorientation documented in Domain 37. The election security reform architecture in this domain — the Election Security and Foreign Interference Commission — is the external counterpart to the CISA restoration and election security litigation strategies in Domain 37.

**Domain 7 (Campaign Finance / Dark Money)**: The Authoritarian Influence Registry's think tank and university disclosure requirements extend the dark money transparency architecture of Domain 7 to the foreign-funding dimension. The two reform frameworks should be designed as complementary legislation, with the Registry addressing foreign-origin institutional funding and Domain 7's DISCLOSE Act framework addressing domestic dark money.

**Domain 1 (Voting Rights / Elections)**: The foreign interference architecture is operating specifically against the voting rights protections that Domain 1's reform agenda seeks to restore. The DOJ voter roll litigation (23 active cases in competitive states) and the SAVE Act voter purge mechanisms documented in Domain 1 are being implemented by an administration that has simultaneously dismantled the counterintelligence architecture that would detect foreign assistance to those operations.
