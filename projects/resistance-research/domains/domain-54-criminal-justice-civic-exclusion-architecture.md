# Domain 54: Criminal Justice, Civic Exclusion Architecture, and the Democratic Participation Crisis

**Research completed**: May 13, 2026
**Crisis window**: ACTIVE on multiple fronts — 4 million Americans disenfranchised due to felony convictions (Sentencing Project 2024 survey); Virginia federal court ruling (January 22, 2026) found Virginia's automatic felony disenfranchisement violated the 1870 Readmission Act, restoring voting rights to hundreds of thousands; Virginia constitutional amendment on November 2026 ballot to enshrine automatic restoration; SCOTUS OT2025 includes two cases with disenfranchisement implications; Prison Policy Initiative documented 20 million people excluded from jury service by criminal record laws; formerly incarcerated people face 27%+ unemployment rate; multiple states advancing voting rights restoration legislation in 2026
**Priority designation**: Phase 2 Expansion — the highest cross-domain leverage multiplier of any remaining framework gap; criminal justice civic exclusion is the structural democracy problem beneath the drug policy, racial justice, and prosecutorial weaponization domains without its own standalone treatment
**Cross-domain connections**: Domain 1 (Electoral Reform — felon disenfranchisement documented but not analyzed as standalone democratic design failure); Domain 22 (Racial Justice — racial disproportionality of civic exclusion); Domain 29 (Prosecutorial Weaponization — prosecution as civic silencing mechanism); Domain 42 (Drug Policy — drug enforcement as primary driver of disenfranchisement patterns)

## Executive Summary

The United States has constructed, across 50 years of criminal justice policy, a parallel civic exclusion system that operates through nominally neutral legal mechanisms to produce the same demographic outcome as explicitly discriminatory voting laws: the systematic removal from political participation of Black and Latinx communities, low-income communities, and communities that historically vote against incumbent political power. The mechanism is not formal voter suppression. It is the conversion of criminal justice contact — arrest, conviction, incarceration, and the debt and collateral consequences that follow — into civic non-participation across five interlocking dimensions: felony disenfranchisement (voting), jury exclusion (civic adjudication), public employment bars (economic exclusion from state power), housing discrimination (physical instability that blocks registration), and legal financial obligations (debt that functions as a modern poll tax). The aggregate effect is what the literature calls "civil death" — the loss of the full bundle of rights that constitutes democratic membership.

The numbers define the scale. Approximately 4 million Americans are currently disenfranchised due to felony convictions, according to the Sentencing Project's 2024 survey — a decline from 4.4 million in 2022 that reflects state-level restoration reforms but still represents a democratic exclusion zone larger than the voting-age populations of Nevada, Iowa, and Vermont combined. More than 20 million people are excluded from jury service by criminal record laws, according to the Prison Policy Initiative — a figure that makes American jury diversity a constitutional fiction in communities with high rates of criminal justice contact. Formerly incarcerated people face unemployment rates above 27% — higher than the US unemployment rate during any historical period including the Great Depression — a labor market exclusion that compounds civic exclusion by eliminating the economic stability that enables sustained political participation. In states that condition voting rights restoration on payment of court fees, fines, and restitution, legal financial obligations function as a modern poll tax: the right to vote is contingent on having money, which is contingent on having employment, which is contingent on not having a criminal record, which is contingent on having lived in a neighborhood that was not subjected to intensive policing. Each step of this exclusion chain is formally race-neutral and formally legal.

The racial dimension is decisive and fully documented. The Sentencing Project estimates that 1 in 22 Black Americans of voting age is currently disenfranchised — more than three times the rate for people who are not Black. This racial disparity is not an artifact of different crime rates; it is the product of racially disparate policing, prosecution, sentencing, and parole revocation practices documented across the criminal justice literature. The Brennan Center, the Sentencing Project, and the Prison Policy Initiative have independently documented that Black Americans are arrested, charged, convicted, and sentenced at higher rates than white Americans for the same conduct across every category of offense.

The democratic design problem this domain identifies is not the same as the prosecutorial weaponization documented in Domain 29. Domain 29 analyzes how DOJ is deliberately used as a political instrument against specific political opponents. This domain analyzes the aggregate systemic level: how the criminal justice system's accumulated policy choices — which are individually comprehensible and often bipartisan in origin — produce a democratic exclusion architecture that systematically removes millions of people from civic participation in ways that predictably benefit those who hold political power. This is the difference between a bad actor using a system and a system that produces bad aggregate outcomes regardless of any individual actor's intentions.

## 1. The Five Dimensions of Civic Exclusion Architecture

### 1.1 Felony Disenfranchisement: The Primary Mechanism

Voting disenfranchisement based on felony convictions is the most studied and best-documented dimension of criminal justice civic exclusion because it has a clear constitutional dimension (the Voting Rights Act, the 14th Amendment's Section 2, and the Equal Protection Clause all intersect with state disenfranchisement laws) and because the Sentencing Project has conducted annual surveys that allow longitudinal tracking.

The state-law landscape divides into four categories. Two states — Maine and Vermont — never deprive people of their right to vote based on criminal convictions; people vote from prison if they are incarcerated. Approximately 20 states restore voting rights automatically upon release from incarceration, though the conditions for automatic restoration vary. Another group of states require completion of parole or probation before restoration, meaning people released from prison to community supervision remain disenfranchised for the duration of supervision. The most restrictive states permanently disenfranchise at least some people with felony records — requiring individual rights restoration petitions, executive clemency, or specific statutory criteria — effectively creating lifetime disenfranchisement for classes of offenses.

Florida is the most consequential jurisdiction for disenfranchisement: the Sentencing Project documents that Florida bans voting rights for over 960,000 citizens with felony convictions — the largest absolute number in any state — and that Florida's 2018 Amendment 4, which overwhelmingly passed to restore automatic rights upon sentence completion, was effectively nullified by the Florida legislature's 2019 Senate Bill 7066, which conditioned restoration on full payment of all fines, fees, and restitution. The Florida Supreme Court upheld SB 7066's constitutionality in 2020, creating what is functionally a poll tax on the restored right.

Virginia provides the most dramatic 2026 development. On January 22, 2026, a federal district court in King v. O'Bannon found that Virginia's Constitution violates the 1870 Virginia Readmission Act — a federal statute enacted at the end of Reconstruction that required former Confederate states to guarantee voting rights for newly emancipated Black residents. The court found that Virginia's automatic disenfranchisement of all people with felony convictions violates the Readmission Act's requirement that the state not disenfranchise voters except for conviction of crimes recognized as felonies at common law. Judge John Gibney ordered Virginia to uphold the January ruling, with state officials given a June 1, 2026 deadline to ensure that many modern statutory crimes don't strip voting rights. Virginia voters will also decide in November 2026 on a constitutional amendment to enshrine automatic voting rights restoration — a measure that, if passed, would cement the court's order in the state constitution.

**Sources**: [New Sentencing Project Report Reveals 4 Million Americans Denied Voting Rights — Sentencing Project](https://www.sentencingproject.org/press-releases/new-sentencing-project-report-reveals-4-million-americans-denied-voting-rights-due-to-felony-convictions/); [Florida Bans Voting Rights of Over 960,000 Citizens — Sentencing Project](https://www.sentencingproject.org/fact-sheet/florida-bans-voting-rights-of-over-960000-citizens/); [Federal judge orders widespread voting rights restoration for Virginians — WHRO](https://www.whro.org/virginia-government/2026-01-29/federal-judge-orders-widespread-voting-rights-restoration-for-virginians); [WilmerHale Secures Landmark Voting Rights Victory in Virginia](https://www.wilmerhale.com/en/insights/news/20260128-wilmerhale-secures-landmark-voting-rights-victory-in-virginia)

### 1.2 Jury Exclusion: The Democratic Adjudication Gap

Jury service is a constitutional right and a civic obligation that has been systematically removed from tens of millions of Americans through criminal record exclusion laws that operate in all 50 states. The Prison Policy Initiative's first-of-its-kind national analysis, "Rigging the jury," documents that criminal record laws bar more than 20 million people from jury service — a figure that includes all 50 states' exclusion provisions, which range from automatic lifetime exclusion for all people with felony convictions to exclusion only during incarceration.

The democratic consequence is two-dimensional. First, jury exclusion reduces the diversity of the jury pool in communities with high rates of criminal justice contact — specifically Black and Latinx communities, where the combination of higher conviction rates and categorical exclusion laws produces jury panels that are not representative of the community that will be judged by them. The Prison Policy Initiative documents that criminal record laws reduce jury diversity by disproportionately excluding Black and Latinx people and that this exclusion causes juries to deliberate less effectively — with higher rates of both wrongful conviction and wrongful acquittal in cases where a representative jury pool would produce different outcomes.

Second, jury exclusion creates a structural feedback loop in the criminal justice system: the communities most subjected to aggressive prosecution are excluded from the institution that provides the primary democratic check on that prosecution. A prosecutor who faces an all-white jury pool when charging a Black defendant in a high-disenfranchisement community faces less democratic accountability for prosecutorial overreach than one whose jury pool reflects the defendant's community. The exclusion architecture is self-reinforcing.

Only six states — Colorado, Illinois, Indiana, Iowa, Maine, and North Dakota — allow all people with felony convictions to serve on juries when released from prison. The remaining 44 states exclude some or all people with criminal records indefinitely or permanently, with no consistent constitutional basis for the exclusion other than legislative inertia.

**Sources**: [Rigging the Jury: How Each State Reduces Jury Diversity by Excluding People with Criminal Records — Prison Policy Initiative](https://www.prisonpolicy.org/reports/juryexclusion.html); [Collateral Consequences — Prison Policy Initiative](https://www.prisonpolicy.org/collateral.html)

### 1.3 Public Employment Bars: Exclusion from Democratic Governance

The federal government and most states impose categorical bars on public employment for people with criminal records across an enormous range of positions. At the federal level, over 40,000 job categories include criminal record requirements or automatic disqualification provisions. These include not only obvious security-sensitive positions (law enforcement, intelligence, nuclear facility work) but also occupational licenses (healthcare, education, financial services, real estate, social work, cosmetology, electrician work) that are administered by state licensing boards with criminal record screening authority.

The Institute for Justice has documented that approximately 1 in 5 American workers requires a government-issued occupational license to do their job — and that licensing boards in most states have broad discretion to deny or revoke licenses based on criminal records, including records unrelated to the licensed occupation. A nurse's aide conviction for marijuana possession twenty years ago can result in license denial, even in states that have since legalized marijuana. A financial services license denial for a conviction related to drug use in college can prevent decades of subsequent employment.

The democratic consequence is structural: public employment bars systematically exclude justice-involved people from participation in the institutions of democratic governance — from school boards to county commissions to state agencies to law enforcement. The exclusion creates a self-reinforcing democratic deficit in the communities most affected by the policies of those institutions.

The connection to civic participation is empirical as well as structural. Stable employment is the single strongest predictor of civic participation among formerly incarcerated people, according to Prison Policy Initiative research. People with stable employment are more likely to register to vote, more likely to vote when registered, and more likely to participate in other civic activities. Employment bars that prevent stable employment therefore have indirect civic exclusion effects that compound the direct voting and jury exclusion mechanisms.

**Sources**: [A Criminal Record Is a Barrier to Jobs and Housing for Millions in the US — The Marshall Project](https://www.themarshallproject.org/2023/04/01/criminal-record-job-housing-barriers-discrimination); [Collateral Consequences and the Enduring Nature of Punishment — Brennan Center](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/analysis-opinion/collateral-consequences-and-enduring-nature-punishment); [Political Participation Amid Mass Incarceration — ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/356620566_Political_Participation_Amid_Mass_Incarceration)

### 1.4 Legal Financial Obligations: The Modern Poll Tax

Legal financial obligations (LFOs) — court fees, fines, restitution, and supervision fees imposed through criminal sentencing — have become a systematic mechanism for conditioning civic rights on economic capacity. In the 20-state category of jurisdictions that require completion of financial obligations before voting rights restoration, people who have completed their sentences but cannot pay their court debt remain disenfranchised — sometimes indefinitely, because poverty and criminal record-related employment barriers make LFO payment practically impossible.

The American Civil Liberties Union documented that felony disenfranchisement conditioned on LFO payment is functionally a 21st century poll tax: it creates a direct link between economic capacity and civic participation that the 24th Amendment was intended to abolish for federal elections. But the 24th Amendment only prohibits poll taxes in federal elections — it does not address LFO payment requirements that are nominally conditions of sentence completion rather than direct charges for voting.

The scale is significant. A 2021 Penn State Law Review analysis found that LFO-conditioned disenfranchisement affects hundreds of thousands of people across the states that have adopted Florida's model — people who are legally entitled to vote under their state's pre-LFO-condition restoration framework but who remain disenfranchised because they cannot pay. Florida has the highest documented LFO-disenfranchisement figure: according to the Sentencing Project, as many as 200,000 Floridians who would be eligible to vote under Amendment 4 remain disenfranchised because they cannot pay their LFOs.

**Sources**: [Felony Disenfranchisement and the Criminalization of Debt — Penn State Law Review](https://www.pennstatelawreview.org/117/2/117-2-Article_Cammett.pdf); [Restore Your Vote: Felony Rights Restoration — Campaign Legal Center](https://campaignlegal.org/cases-actions/restore-your-vote-felony-rights-restoration); [Texas Should Restore Voting Rights to Nearly Half a Million Citizens — Sentencing Project](https://www.sentencingproject.org/fact-sheet/texas-denies-voting-rights-to-people-with-felony-convictions/)

### 1.5 Housing Exclusion and Civic Registration Instability

Criminal record-based housing discrimination — through public housing exclusion, private landlord screening practices, and the legal framework that makes it permissible to deny housing based on criminal history — compounds civic exclusion through physical instability. The democratic participation chain requires a fixed address: voter registration requires an address, mail ballot access requires a stable mailing address, and many states' polling place assignment systems assign voters to polling locations based on their registration address.

The 1988 Thurmond Amendment to the Anti-Drug Abuse Act stripped people with drug distribution convictions of federal Fair Housing Act protections — creating a categorical exception that allows housing discrimination against this class of people without the anti-discrimination standard that would apply to other protected classes. This is not a recent executive action; it is a 38-year-old statutory provision that has never been revisited.

The American Bar Association's Human Rights journal has documented "double disenfranchisement" for unhoused people with felony records: they face both felony disenfranchisement (in states with active bans) and the practical barriers that homelessness creates for registration, maintaining registration, and accessing polling locations. The overlap between justice-involved people and people experiencing homelessness is substantial: an estimated 15-20% of unhoused Americans have been recently incarcerated, and the barriers between incarceration and stable housing are well-documented.

**Sources**: [Double Disenfranchisement: Voting Rights for the Unhoused with Felony Records — ABA Human Rights Journal](https://www.americanbar.org/groups/crsj/resources/human-rights/2025-march/voting-rights-for-unhoused-felony-records/); [Collateral Consequences — Prison Policy Initiative](https://www.prisonpolicy.org/collateral.html); [US Commission on Civil Rights — Collateral Consequences](https://www.usccr.gov/files/pubs/2019/06-13-Collateral-Consequences.pdf)

## 2. The Racial Architecture of Criminal Justice Civic Exclusion

### 2.1 Racial Disparities as Structural Democracy Failure

The racial disproportionality of criminal justice civic exclusion is not a statistical artifact or a product of different underlying behavior. It is the product of racial disparities in policing density, prosecution charging decisions, plea bargaining leverage, sentencing guidelines, and parole revocation — each of which has been independently documented as racially disparate and each of which contributes to the aggregate civic exclusion outcome.

The Sentencing Project's national disenfranchisement data shows that Black Americans are disenfranchised at a rate of 1 in 22 voting-age adults — more than three times the rate for white Americans. This produces a specific structural effect: in states with significant Black populations and active disenfranchisement laws, the effective composition of the electorate is systematically altered from what it would be without racially disparate criminal enforcement. The documented disenfranchisement rates in Alabama (approximately 1 in 10 Black adults), Mississippi, and Florida mean that the political outcomes in those states are systematically shaped by a civic exclusion architecture whose demographic effects are predictable and racially concentrated.

The Human Rights Watch analysis of US felony disenfranchisement law calls the United States "a global outlier in denying voting rights due to criminal convictions" — the only established democracy that systematically excludes substantial proportions of its minority population from civic participation through post-sentence disenfranchisement. Every Western European democracy restores voting rights automatically upon sentence completion or does not remove them during incarceration.

**Sources**: [New Report: United States a Global Outlier — ACLU](https://www.aclu.org/press-releases/new-report-united-states-a-global-outlier-in-denying-voting-rights-due-to-criminal-convictions); [US: Allow Those With Felony Convictions to Vote — Human Rights Watch](https://www.hrw.org/news/2026/04/29/us-allow-those-with-felony-convictions-to-vote); [Out of Step: US Policy on Voting Rights in Global Perspective — Sentencing Project](https://www.sentencingproject.org/reports/out-of-step-u-s-policy-on-voting-rights-in-global-perspective/)

### 2.2 Employment Exclusion and Political Power Concentration

The 27%+ unemployment rate among formerly incarcerated people — higher than the US unemployment rate during any historical period including the Great Depression — does not only represent economic hardship. It represents a systematic structural barrier to the political voice that economic stability enables.

The empirical literature on political participation consistently finds that economic stability is among the strongest predictors of civic participation, independent of education, age, or other standard demographic variables. Stable employment predicts voter registration, voter turnout, school board meeting attendance, and contact with elected officials. The employment barriers that produce 27% unemployment among formerly incarcerated people therefore have documented civic exclusion effects that extend beyond the ballot to every dimension of democratic participation.

The racial dimension is compounded. Black formerly incarcerated people face higher unemployment rates than white formerly incarcerated people, controlling for education and offense type — a documented labor market racial discrimination effect that layers on top of the criminal record screening that affects all justice-involved job seekers. The compound effect: Black Americans face more intense policing and prosecution (producing more disenfranchisement and employment bar exposure), and then face more labor market discrimination for the criminal records that result.

**Sources**: [Stateless Citizens: The Impact of Criminal Record's Collateral Consequences on Voting and Employment — SIU Law Journal](https://opensiuc.lib.siu.edu/siulj/vol46/iss3/6/); [Civil Death and the Denial of Democracy — Dream.org](https://dream.org/news-articles/fight-for-rights-restoration/)

## 3. Historical Precedent: Civil Death and Democratic Repair

### 3.1 Origins of American Disenfranchisement Law

The American tradition of felon disenfranchisement predates the Constitution, drawing on English common law traditions of "civil death" — the notion that conviction of a serious crime effectively removed a person from the civil and legal community. Colonial American statutes barred convicted felons from court testimony, property ownership, and civic participation. Post-Revolutionary state constitutions incorporated disenfranchisement provisions, usually limited to crimes of "moral turpitude" — a vague category that created discretion in application.

The Reconstruction Amendments (13th, 14th, and 15th) dramatically complicated this history. The 14th Amendment, Section 2 explicitly contemplated disenfranchisement "for participation in rebellion, or other crime" — language that was included as a compromise to permit states to continue disenfranchising those convicted of crimes, while prohibiting race-based disenfranchisement. The Supreme Court's interpretation of Section 2 in Richardson v. Ramirez (1974) held that states can disenfranchise people convicted of crimes without violating the Equal Protection Clause — a ruling that has blocked federal constitutional challenges to felony disenfranchisement laws for 50 years, channeling reform toward statutory and state constitutional avenues.

The Voting Rights Act of 1965, despite its comprehensive anti-discrimination protections, did not directly address felony disenfranchisement. The VRA's Section 2 prohibits voting practices that "result in" a denial or abridgement of the right to vote on account of race — but Richardson v. Ramirez's holding that Section 2 of the 14th Amendment permits felon disenfranchisement has been read by most courts as limiting VRA challenges to disenfranchisement laws. The Virginia Readmission Act theory upheld in the January 2026 King v. O'Bannon ruling — which grounds the challenge in post-Civil War statutes rather than the 14th Amendment — represents a novel and potentially more durable litigation pathway.

**Sources**: [Felony Disenfranchisement and Voting Rights — Prison Policy Initiative](https://www.prisonpolicy.org/research/felony_disenfranchisement_and_voting_rights/); [Losing the Vote: The Impact of Felony Disenfranchisement Laws — Human Rights Watch (legacy)](https://www.hrw.org/legacy/reports98/vote/usvot98o.htm); [Voting Rights in the Era of Mass Incarceration — Sentencing Project](https://www.sentencingproject.org/policy-brief/voting-rights-in-the-era-of-mass-incarceration-a-primer/)

### 3.2 The Abolitionist Democracy Framework

The Movement for Black Lives, Critical Resistance, and the broader abolitionist democracy movement have articulated a framework that contextualizes felony disenfranchisement within a larger critique of the carceral state as a democratic exclusion system. The M4BL's 2016 "Vision for Black Lives" policy platform includes full enfranchisement of currently and formerly incarcerated people as a core demand — not as a narrow voting rights reform but as an element of a broader democratic transformation that treats the carceral state as a mechanism for maintaining racial hierarchy through means other than explicit legal discrimination.

The abolitionist democracy framework differs from liberal rights-restoration advocacy in its analytical foundation: it does not ask for the restoration of rights within the existing system, but argues that the existing system is designed to produce civic exclusion and that restoration reforms, while urgently necessary, do not address the systemic architecture. The framework's civic exclusion analysis — that criminal justice contact functions as an unlegislated voting restriction law — builds on this abolitionist democratic critique while framing it in terms accessible to coalition audiences that may not share the full abolitionist framework.

The practical policy implications of the abolitionist democracy analysis are more ambitious than standard rights-restoration advocacy: full enfranchisement during incarceration (Maine and Vermont model), elimination of collateral consequence bars to employment and housing, and structural changes to policing and prosecution that reduce the volume of criminal justice contact in communities of color. These policy ambitions are analytically distinct from the narrower rights-restoration reforms (automatic post-sentence restoration, LFO waiver programs, jury inclusion) that are achievable in the near term.

## 4. International Comparative Evidence

### 4.1 European Models of Civic Inclusion

The United States is the only wealthy democracy that systematically disenfranchises substantial proportions of its population through post-sentence or permanent disenfranchisement laws. The international comparative record is decisive and consistent.

In Germany, only people who are currently incarcerated for specific crimes against the state (treason, electoral fraud, crimes against constitutional order) lose voting rights during imprisonment — and these rights are restored automatically upon release. People on parole or probation vote. Germany's Federal Constitutional Court has held that voting rights are a fundamental right that can be restricted only for compelling and proportionate reasons directly connected to the nature of the offense.

France similarly restricts voting rights only for people currently imprisoned for specific political crimes and has no system of post-sentence disenfranchisement. The French Constitutional Council has held that any voting restriction must be proportionate to the specific offense — categorical blanket disenfranchisement for all felony convictions would not survive French constitutional review.

The Council of Europe's Venice Commission — the advisory body on constitutional law for European states — has consistently found that post-sentence disenfranchisement violates the European Convention on Human Rights' guarantee of free elections. The European Court of Human Rights ruled in Hirst v. United Kingdom (2005) that blanket disenfranchisement of all imprisoned people violated the Convention — a ruling that the UK has partially complied with by restoring voting rights to people serving less than one year.

The international consensus — reflected in Council of Europe guidelines, Venice Commission opinions, and Inter-American Commission on Human Rights standards — is that civic rights should not be used as a mechanism for punishing people beyond the terms of their sentence and that democratic inclusion is a value that criminal justice systems should maximize, not minimize.

## 5. Reform Pathways

### 5.1 Tier 1 — Automatic Voting Rights Restoration (Near-Term, State-Level)

The most immediately achievable and politically viable reform is automatic voting rights restoration upon sentence completion — eliminating the individual petition, parole/probation completion requirement, and LFO payment conditions that make restoration contingent on bureaucratic processes that most justice-involved people cannot navigate.

The reform model is the Maine and Vermont approach — voting rights are not removed even during incarceration — but the near-term political feasibility benchmark is the 24 states that have moved to automatic restoration upon release since 1997. The Sentencing Project documents that 26 states and Washington D.C. expanded voting rights between 1997 and 2023. The 2026 reform window targets states where restoration legislation has passed one chamber or where ballot initiative architecture makes restoration achievable without legislative cooperation.

Virginia's November 2026 ballot measure — which would enshrine automatic restoration in the state constitution following the January 2026 federal court ruling — is the most immediate advocacy anchor. A constitutional amendment (rather than statutory restoration) is more durable because it cannot be reversed by a future legislature, as Florida's Amendment 4 was effectively gutted by the 2019 SB 7066. Virginia's constitutional amendment campaign provides a proof-of-concept model for the same strategy in states with similar initiative and amendment pathways.

The Sentencing Project's state-by-state restoration map identifies the next ten most viable restoration targets based on legislative chamber composition, polling data, and existing advocacy infrastructure. States where restoration has passed one chamber (Minnesota, Nevada, Oklahoma) are the highest near-term priority for concentrated advocacy. The Campaign Legal Center's "Restore Your Vote" program provides legal assistance to people seeking restoration in states where petition processes remain.

**Sources**: [Expanding the Vote: State Felony Disenfranchisement Reform, 1997-2023 — Sentencing Project](https://www.sentencingproject.org/reports/expanding-the-vote-state-felony-disenfranchisement-reform-1997-2023/); [Thousands of Virginians Will Regain the Right to Vote — Virginia Mercury](https://virginiamercury.com/2026/05/04/thousands-of-virginians-will-regain-the-right-to-vote-next-month/); [Restore Your Vote — Campaign Legal Center](https://campaignlegal.org/cases-actions/restore-your-vote-felony-rights-restoration)

### 5.2 Tier 2 — Jury Inclusion Reform and LFO Elimination

Jury inclusion reform — expanding who can serve on juries by eliminating or limiting criminal record bars — is achievable through state legislation and provides a complementary democratic participation benefit to voting rights restoration. The Prison Policy Initiative's "Rigging the jury" report provides the primary evidence base and the state-by-state legislative template: the six states that allow all people with felony convictions to serve on juries provide the model.

LFO elimination — or mandatory waiver programs for people who cannot pay — is the most technically demanding but most democratically critical second-tier reform. Florida's experience with LFO-conditioned disenfranchisement under SB 7066 demonstrated that restoration rights that depend on payment are not genuine restoration rights for the substantial majority of justice-involved people who remain in poverty after release. Federal legislation that prohibits states from conditioning voting rights restoration on LFO payment — framed as a Supremacy Clause issue under the 24th Amendment's anti-poll-tax principle — is the federal advocacy angle. State legislation mandating automatic LFO waiver for people below 200% of poverty level is the state-level approach.

**Sources**: [Rigging the Jury — Prison Policy Initiative](https://www.prisonpolicy.org/reports/juryexclusion.html); [Winnable Criminal Justice Reforms in 2026 — Prison Policy Initiative](https://www.prisonpolicy.org/reports/winnable2026.html); [Felony Disenfranchisement — NCSL](https://www.ncsl.org/elections-and-campaigns/felon-voting-rights)

### 5.3 Tier 3 — Structural Criminal Justice Reform as Democratic Repair

The long-cycle reform agenda addresses the upstream cause of civic exclusion: the volume and racial disproportionality of criminal justice contact that creates the disenfranchisement, jury exclusion, and employment bar exposure documented in Tiers 1 and 2. This is Domain 42's terrain at the drug policy level; Domain 54 extends the frame to the structural democratic repair argument.

The abolitionist democracy framework argues that restoration reforms without structural reforms merely manage the consequences of a system designed to produce exclusion. The democratic design version of this argument is more modest: restoration reforms are urgently necessary, but they are not sufficient if the rate of new civic exclusion through criminal justice contact continues at current levels. States that have implemented automatic restoration while maintaining racially disparate prosecution patterns have not achieved demographic representativeness in their civic participation; they have merely reduced the lag between sentence completion and voting eligibility.

The Sentencing Project's "Voting Rights in the Era of Mass Incarceration" analysis provides the framework for this structural reform argument: if the goal is equal civic participation across demographic groups, then the reform agenda must include both rights restoration (for the 4 million currently excluded) and criminal justice system reform (to reduce the rate at which new civic exclusion is created). Drug policy reform (Domain 42), prosecutorial accountability (Domain 29), and policing reform (beyond the current framework's scope but essential to this analysis) are all necessary components of a structural democratic repair agenda.

These state campaigns provide near-term distribution targets for Sentencing Project state-specific brief versions of this domain's analysis.
