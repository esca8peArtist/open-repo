## Executive Summary

The United States Constitution's amendment process under Article V is simultaneously the most powerful tool for democratic renewal and the most dangerous vulnerability in the democratic architecture. Article V provides two pathways: the congressional proposal pathway (two-thirds of both chambers, then three-quarters of states), which has produced all 27 amendments to date; and the convention pathway (applications from two-thirds of states, then a convention whose output must be ratified by three-quarters of states), which has never been used. The convention pathway's danger is not hypothetical — it is structural and documented by some of the most distinguished constitutional scholars across the ideological spectrum, from Chief Justice Warren Burger to Justice Antonin Scalia to Harvard's Laurence Tribe: once a convention is called, there is no constitutional mechanism to limit its scope. A convention called to propose a balanced budget amendment could propose to eliminate the First Amendment's free exercise clause, abolish the income tax, impose congressional term limits, or restructure the Senate to eliminate proportional representation of the population. The ratification requirement (three-quarters of states) provides some protection — but in an environment where 20 states have already passed Convention of States resolutions, and where additional state legislatures are potential targets, that protection is less robust than it appears.

The Convention of States Project — funded by the Koch network, Mercer family, and ALEC-aligned donors — has made reaching 34 state applications its central organizational goal. As of early 2026, 20 states have passed the COS resolution (Kansas becoming the 20th). The movement is 14 states short of the threshold. However, ALEC-aligned lawyers have advanced an aggregation theory that would count applications from different eras and different stated purposes — fiscal restraints, term limits, and other subject-specific conventions — toward the 34-state count. Under that theory, the threshold may already be within reach. H.Con.Res.15 in the 119th Congress calls for an Article V Convention for a "Fiscal Responsibility Amendment" — the congressional trigger mechanism positioned for deployment if Republicans maintain the House majority after November 2026.

The progressive case for Article V is equally serious and equally underexplored. Constitutional reform proposals that have majority public support but face systematic institutional obstruction — SCOTUS term limits (supported by 73% of Americans in polling), campaign finance reform (Citizens United reversal), abolition of the Electoral College, statehood for DC and Puerto Rico — all require either constitutional amendment or structural statutory reform that could be reversed by a future Congress. The amendment route, for these proposals, is not a threat to be resisted but a tool to be developed. The academic split on this question is genuine: Lawrence Lessig supports a controlled convention, Akhil Reed Amar supports it, Randy Barnett supports it from the right, Laurence Tribe opposes it. But that split is not resolvable through abstract legal argument. It requires a political theory of when and how constitutional amendment serves democratic renewal versus when it serves democratic regression.

This domain provides that political theory, maps the current landscape, identifies the Michigan November 2026 vote as the most important near-term organizing anchor, and outlines the reform architecture for both defending against a convention that could harm democracy and advancing the amendments that democracy requires.

## 1. Article V: The Two Pathways and the Structural Problem

### 1.1 The Congressional Proposal Pathway (27 Amendments, All Successful)

Article V's first pathway requires two-thirds of both the House and Senate to propose an amendment, followed by ratification by three-quarters of the states (38 of 50). This pathway has been used for all 27 amendments — from the Bill of Rights (1791) to the Twenty-Seventh Amendment (1992, originally proposed in 1789). Its virtues are well understood: the requirement of congressional supermajorities ensures broad consensus before an amendment reaches the states, and the ratification requirement provides a further democratic check.

The pathway's limitation in the current environment is equally well understood: the polarization of the US Senate has made two-thirds supermajority votes nearly impossible. The Senate has not achieved a two-thirds vote on a contested political question since the 1964 Civil Rights Act cloture vote. SCOTUS term limits, campaign finance reform, and Electoral College reform have majority public support but cannot achieve the 60-vote threshold for floor consideration, let alone two-thirds. The congressional pathway is functionally blocked for the most important democratic reform proposals of the current moment.

This blockage is not an argument for the convention pathway. It is an argument for understanding that the convention pathway's risks are at least as real as the congressional pathway's blockage, and that a theory of democratic renewal through Article V must account for both.

### 1.2 The Convention Pathway: Structure and the Runaway Problem

Article V's second pathway provides that "on the Application of the Legislatures of two thirds of the several States, [Congress] shall call a Convention for proposing Amendments." The convention must then propose amendments, which take effect when ratified by three-quarters of the states.

The "runaway convention" problem is not a progressive fever dream. It was identified by Chief Justice Warren Burger, who wrote in 1988: "There is no way to effectively limit or muzzle the actions of a constitutional convention. The convention could make its own rules and set its own agenda. Congress might try to limit the convention to one amendment or to one issue, but there is no way to assure that the convention would obey." Justice Antonin Scalia, at the Federalist Society's 2015 annual convention, said he "certainly would not want a constitutional convention" because "you get into a horrible, screaming fight over everything." Former US Solicitor General Archibald Cox expressed the same concern.

The common thread: unlike constitutional conventions in other countries, an Article V convention has no established rulebook, no constitutional text specifying delegate selection, no limitation on scope that has ever been judicially enforced, and no precedent from which to derive governing principles. The convention's 1787 precedent — the only one — is itself a runaway: the Philadelphia Convention was convened to amend the Articles of Confederation and instead produced an entirely new Constitution, ratified by a procedure (nine states rather than unanimous consent) different from what the Articles required.

The convention advocates' response — that ratification by three-quarters of states provides sufficient protection — underestimates the political dynamics of ratification. If 34 states have passed applications calling for a convention, those states are by definition already amenable to the project. The same political infrastructure that passed the applications can be mobilized for ratification campaigns. The 38-state ratification threshold is a protection against radical proposals that lack political support — but in a political environment where right-wing state legislatures have achieved extraordinary legislative coordination through ALEC, and where dark-money infrastructure can be deployed in state legislative campaigns at scale, 38 states is a lower bar than it appears.

## 2. The Convention of States Project: Funding, Strategy, and the Application Count

### 2.1 The Koch-ALEC-Heritage Network

The Convention of States Project (COS) is the primary vehicle for reaching 34 state applications. COS has deep organizational ties to the ALEC network, Heritage Foundation, and Koch-connected donors. The Mercer family has provided at least $500,000 to the project. Koch-connected Donors Trust has contributed additional funding. COS operates a sophisticated state legislative lobbying operation, with staff deployed in targeted state legislatures and template legislation distributed through ALEC's model bills network.

The Convention of States resolution, as passed by the 20 states with active resolutions as of 2026, calls for a convention limited to three subjects: imposing fiscal restraints on the federal government, limiting the power and jurisdiction of the federal government, and limiting the terms of office for federal officials and members of Congress. These are not neutral procedural subjects — they embed the policy agenda of the convention's funders. A "fiscal restraints" amendment could constitutionalize a balanced budget requirement that would mandate austerity during recessions; a "limit federal jurisdiction" amendment could eliminate the constitutional basis for federal civil rights law, environmental regulation, and labor standards; a "term limits" amendment could lock in a permanent congressional composition.

The 20 states with active, unrescinded Convention of States resolutions as of 2026: Georgia, Alaska, Florida, Alabama, Tennessee, Indiana, Oklahoma, Louisiana, Arizona, North Dakota, Texas, Missouri, West Virginia, Arkansas, Utah, Mississippi, Idaho, South Carolina, Montana, and Kansas (which became the 20th in 2026). The application count toward 34 is disputed because several states that COS counts have passed resolutions on different subjects (fiscal restraints only, or balanced budget only), and a significant number of states have rescinded all prior Article V applications.

### 2.2 The Aggregation Theory: Manufactured Threshold

The most dangerous development in the convention movement since 2020 is not the addition of new state applications. It is the development of aggregation theories that would allow convention proponents to declare the 34-state threshold reached without a genuine consensus of 34 states supporting a unified convention call. Lawyers associated with right-wing legal networks have advanced theories that would count applications from different eras and different subject matters — some from the 1980s, some from the 1990s, some subject-limited, some general — toward a single 34-state count.

The aggregation theory exploits the silence in Article V's text. The Constitution provides no mechanism for determining which applications count, no time limit on applications, and no requirement that they address the same subject. A convention proponent with this theory, a receptive Congress, and a political moment in which one party controls both chambers could declare a convention validly called over the objections of states that have expressly rescinded their applications — triggering a constitutional crisis with no clear resolution mechanism.

H.Con.Res.15 (119th Congress), the pending bill in the House calling for a Fiscal Responsibility Amendment convention, is the template for the congressional trigger mechanism. If Republicans retain the House after November 2026 and the aggregation theory gains political traction, this bill could become the vehicle for a rushed convention call. The Convention of States website lists Hawaii, Illinois, Iowa, Kentucky, Massachusetts, New Jersey, North Carolina, Ohio, Pennsylvania, Rhode Island, South Dakota, Washington, and Wyoming as states where legislation is pending or being considered in 2026 — potential additions to the application count.

### 2.3 State Rescissions: The Defensive Count

Since 2016, 10 state legislatures have rescinded previous applications to call a constitutional convention: Delaware (2016); New Mexico, Maryland, Nevada, and Texas (all 2017); South Dakota (2019); Colorado and New Jersey (2021); Illinois (2022); and Oregon (2023). New York's rescission is among the more recent additions to this list. Massachusetts has also rescinded dangerous Article V convention calls. Common Cause counts 8 states where they have worked successfully for rescissions.

The rescission strategy requires legal validation: the legal authority of state legislatures to rescind prior applications is disputed. Article V is silent on rescission. Preemptive declaratory litigation — establishing the legal authority of state legislatures to rescind before a convention is declared — would provide judicial precedent for the rescission defense when it is needed.

### 2.4 Michigan: The November 2026 Organizing Anchor

Michigan's November 3, 2026 ballot contains two distinct but related questions.

**Proposal 1 — State Constitutional Convention**: Michigan's constitution requires a vote on whether to hold a state constitutional convention every 16 years. Michigan voters have rejected this measure by wide margins each time it has appeared — 77% to 23% in 1978, 72% to 28% in 1994, 67% to 22% in 2010. The 2026 vote is the automatic constitutional referral; it does not require petition signatures. Bipartisan opposition has emerged: both conservative and progressive groups oppose a state constitutional convention, and the vote is expected to follow the historical pattern of rejection.

**The Article V dimension**: Michigan's legislature had H.B. 5151 and H.C.R. 2 introduced in January 2026 — the 34Ready Legislation calling for a federal Article V convention. If the state constitutional convention vote (Proposal 1) fails — as expected — it demonstrates that Michigan voters reject the convention mechanism on its merits. That rejection can be used as political evidence against the claim that a federal Article V convention has democratic legitimacy: if even Michigan repeatedly rejects its own constitutional convention by 2-to-1 margins, what does that say about the claimed democratic mandate for a federal convention?

The organizing value of the Michigan vote is threefold: it creates a November 2026 news hook for the convention-threat story; it demonstrates cross-partisan coalition potential (both parties have historically opposed the state constitutional convention); and a decisive defeat in Michigan (the most recent state to bring in Convention of States legislation) provides the proof-of-concept that state-level resistance to the convention mechanism works at the ballot box.

## 3. The Progressive Amendment Agenda: What Democracy Requires

### 3.1 The Structural Reform Proposals

The progressive case for Article V is grounded in the recognition that several democratic reforms with majority public support are constitutionally blocked: they require amendment because ordinary legislation would be either constitutionally prohibited (Electoral College abolition) or politically reversible (statutory SCOTUS term limits that a future sympathetic Congress could repeal). The major amendment candidates:

**SCOTUS Term Limits**: A constitutional amendment establishing 18-year terms for Supreme Court Justices, with vacancies arising on a staggered schedule that allows each presidential term to fill predictable slots. Supported by 73% of Americans in multiple recent surveys. Eliminates the gaming of the appointment process that has produced the current Court's ideological profile. The statutory approach — S.1791 (Supreme Court Term Limits and Regular Appointments Act) — was proposed in the prior Congress but the constitutional validity of a statutory term limit is contested. A constitutional amendment provides unambiguous authority. See Domain 6 for the judicial independence context.

**Campaign Finance Reform (Citizens United Reversal)**: A constitutional amendment establishing that corporations and other artificial entities do not have the same First Amendment rights as natural persons with respect to political spending, and that Congress and the states may limit campaign contributions and expenditures to protect democratic integrity. Supported by more than 70% of Americans. Required because *Citizens United v. FEC* (2010) and *Buckley v. Valeo* (1976) construed the First Amendment to prohibit such limits — statutory reform would be struck down without an amendment. See Domain 51 (Campaign Finance) for the structural dark-money crisis that motivates this amendment.

**Electoral College Reform**: A constitutional amendment establishing a national popular vote or proportional allocation for the presidential election. The Electoral College cannot be abolished by statute — it requires amendment. The National Popular Vote Interstate Compact (currently enacted in states representing 209 of the required 270 electoral votes) provides a partial workaround that has survived legal challenges, but constitutional amendment provides greater durability and legitimacy.

**Senate Structural Reform**: The Constitution's equal-state representation in the Senate — specifically, Article V's protection of it ("no State, without its Consent, shall be deprived of its equal Suffrage in the Senate") — is one of the two constitutional provisions that Article V itself protects from amendment without unanimous state consent. Senate structural reform therefore requires either unanimous state consent or a reconstituted constitutional theory that the framers did not intend this provision to permanently bind democratic majorities. This is the most legally complex amendment proposal.

**DC and Puerto Rico Statehood**: Adding states requires only a congressional majority under Article IV — not a constitutional amendment — but faces the same Senate filibuster and minority obstruction barriers that block other democratic reforms.

### 3.2 The Academic Split and the Democratic Design Question

The intellectual debate about Article V is not between those who understand the Constitution and those who do not. It is between constitutional scholars who make different empirical and normative judgments about the same legal text.

Lawrence Lessig — Harvard Law professor and leading campaign finance reformer — has argued that a controlled Article V convention focused on campaign finance reform could be structured with guardrails sufficient to prevent a runaway outcome. His argument is pragmatic: if the congressional pathway is permanently blocked by money-in-politics interests, the convention pathway, with appropriate constraints, may be the only available route to constitutional reform. Akhil Reed Amar — Yale Law professor — has made similar arguments about constitutional restoration through the amendment process. Randy Barnett — Georgetown Law professor and conservative constitutionalist — supports the convention pathway from the right.

Laurence Tribe — Harvard Law professor — opposes the convention pathway as too dangerous, arguing that no constraints on a convention's scope can be made legally enforceable before the convention acts, and that the ratification-by-states protection is insufficient given current state legislative dynamics. Common Cause, the AFL-CIO, and the NEA align with Tribe's position. The Article V Information Center (a clearinghouse for balanced convention analysis) and the Colorado Fiscal Institute have produced the most rigorous attempts at neutral assessment.

The democratic design question this domain poses: in an environment where the congressional amendment pathway is blocked and the democratic deficit is structural, what is the responsible theory of constitutional change? The answer depends on the specific amendment proposed, the specific convention context, and the specific political safeguards in place. This domain maps the terrain so that resistance networks can engage with it seriously rather than treating Article V as either a silver bullet or a pure threat.

## 4. May 2026 Current Threat Assessment

### 4.1 The Convention Movement's 2026 Legislative Push

COS has an active 2026 state legislative calendar targeting states where it believes 2026 sessions offer the best opportunity to add the 14 remaining states needed. The states under active consideration include North Carolina, Ohio, Pennsylvania, and Wyoming — all states with Republican legislative majorities that have not yet passed the COS resolution. If any two or three of these pass the resolution before their 2026 sessions end, the count approaches 23–24 states, within easier organizing reach of 34.

The Convention of States website as of May 2026 lists active legislative campaigns in more than a dozen states. The ALEC annual meeting (scheduled for July 2026) is a key organizing venue: ALEC's model bills network is the primary distribution mechanism for COS state legislation. A domain document that reaches state legislators and their constitutional law advisers before the ALEC meeting could inoculate them against the COS model bill.

### 4.2 H.Con.Res.15 and the Congressional Trigger

H.Con.Res.15 (119th Congress), introduced in the House Judiciary Committee, calls for an Article V Convention "for proposing a Fiscal Responsibility Amendment to the United States Constitution and stipulating ratification by a vote of We the People." The bill is a one-sentence resolution — a template for how the congressional trigger mechanism would work. It does not aggregate state applications but instead directly calls for a convention through Congress's concurrent resolution authority.

The "ratification by a vote of We the People" language is an additional constitutional novelty: Article V specifies ratification by state legislatures or state conventions, not by national popular vote. A concurrent resolution purporting to change the ratification method would itself raise constitutional questions that no court has resolved. The bill is currently in committee with no floor schedule, but it becomes the template that would be deployed in a lame-duck session if Republicans maintain the House and the administration decides to pursue the convention route before January 2027.

## 5. Actionable Intelligence for Resistance Networks

### 5.1 Defend Michigan — The November 2026 Anchor

The Michigan Proposal 1 vote on November 3, 2026 is the most important near-term organizing anchor for Article V defense. The organizing objective:

- **Defeat Proposal 1** (the state constitutional convention question) by the widest possible margin, building on the historical precedent of 67–77% rejection
- **Document the margin** as evidence of Michigan voters' rejection of the convention mechanism, for use in the federal Article V debate
- **Counter-organize against the Article V state legislation** (H.B. 5151 / H.C.R. 2) in the Michigan legislature, using the Proposal 1 result as political leverage

**Coalition**: Common Cause Michigan, ACLU of Michigan, Michigan AFL-CIO, Michigan NAACP, Michigan Education Association. The bipartisan opposition already documented — both progressive organizations and conservative constitutionalists who fear a runaway convention — is the coalition template.

**Tactic**: Produce a Michigan-specific fact sheet documenting what a state constitutional convention could change (Michigan's civil rights provisions, equal rights provision, public education guarantee) and distribute through union halls, churches, and community organizations in the six weeks before the election.

### 5.2 Rescission Campaign: Defend the Application Count

The primary defensive strategy against COS reaching 34 states is maintaining state-level rescissions of prior Article V applications. The rescission strategy requires:

- **Legal validation**: File a preemptive declaratory action establishing the legal authority of state legislatures to rescind prior applications — before a convention is declared — providing judicial precedent for the rescission defense. The American Constitution Society and Brennan Center's constitutional scholars are the best-positioned organizations to anchor this litigation.
- **Target states**: The 14 states COS needs to reach 34 are the organizing targets. States with purple or Democratic-leaning legislatures that have not yet passed COS resolutions — Pennsylvania, Wisconsin, Nevada, Colorado — are the most defensible.
- **Aggregation theory litigation preparation**: Legal organizations should prepare litigation challenging the aggregation theory before it is deployed, establishing in federal court that different-subject applications from different eras cannot be aggregated toward a single 34-state count.

**Tactic**: File a declaratory action in federal district court seeking confirmation that (a) state rescissions are valid under Article V and (b) subject-limited applications cannot be aggregated across subjects.

### 5.3 Advance the Congressional Amendment Pathway for SCOTUS Term Limits

The 2026 midterms, if they produce a Democratic Senate majority, create the first Senate environment since 2021 in which a constitutional amendment proposal could receive a floor vote. The two-year window of the 120th Congress (January 2027–2028) is the political window the amendment movement should be building toward.

**Tactic**: Organizations working on SCOTUS reform — Fix the Court, Demand Justice — should develop a legislative strategy for introducing an 18-year term limit amendment in the first week of the 120th Congress (January 2027) if Democrats achieve a Senate majority, allowing a floor vote before the political window closes.

### 5.4 Coalition Architecture for Cross-Ideological Convention Defense

The defense against a runaway convention is the only political objective in this domain with genuine cross-partisan coalition potential. Conservative constitutionalists who oppose a runaway convention (Federalist Society-adjacent scholars, libertarian organizations, fiscal conservatives who fear a convention's scope) can be coalition partners for the rescission campaign and the aggregation-theory litigation.

**Tactic**: Convene a cross-partisan constitutional law coalition — reaching explicitly to conservative scholars and organizations — to produce a joint legal brief, op-ed series, or public statement establishing the shared scholarly consensus that a convention cannot be scope-limited and that the ratification protection is insufficient given current state legislative dynamics. A statement signed by both Laurence Tribe and a prominent Federalist Society scholar carries more political weight than either alone.

## 6. Reform Pathways

### 6.1 Convention Defense Architecture

**Article V Clarification Act**: Federal legislation establishing procedural rules for the convention pathway — delegate selection, scope limitations, ratification procedures — that would apply to any convention called under Article V. The constitutional authority for such legislation is contested (Article V is self-executing), but the legislation creates a legal record of congressional intent and establishes the procedural framework that convention opponents would argue in court.

**Application Validity Act**: Federal legislation establishing that Article V applications are subject to a 10-year time limit, that applications on different subjects cannot be aggregated toward a single 34-state count, and that state rescissions are legally effective.

**State Rescission Model Legislation**: Common Cause and allied organizations should develop model legislation for state rescission of Article V applications, to be introduced in states that have passed COS resolutions or prior balanced budget / term limit applications. Wisconsin, Michigan, and Nevada are the highest-priority states for rescission campaigns in 2026.

### 6.2 The Progressive Amendment Agenda (2027–2028 Window)

The constitutional amendment agenda for democratic renewal requires coordinated organization now to be deployable if the 2026 midterms produce the necessary Senate composition. The priority sequence:

1. **SCOTUS Term Limits Amendment** (18-year terms; mandatory rotation to senior status; predictable appointment schedule)
2. **Democracy Restoration Amendment** (campaign finance regulation; direct reversal of *Citizens United*; restore congressional authority to regulate political spending by artificial entities)
3. **Equal Suffrage Modernization Amendment** (addresses Senate structural reform through the only politically available route: congressional statute plus state ratification of a constitutional amendment authorizing proportional adjustments to Senate voting rules)

None of these proposals is achievable in the 119th Congress. The 120th Congress (beginning January 2027) is the target window. The pre-election work is coalition building, legal development, and public opinion preparation.

### 6.3 The Democratic Accountability Argument Against the COS Convention

The strongest political argument against the COS convention is not the runaway convention risk — which sounds abstract to voters unfamiliar with constitutional law. It is the accountability argument: the Convention of States Project was not elected by anyone. Its funders (Koch network, Mercer family, ALEC donors) do not represent the democratic majority. The constitutional system requires that fundamental law changes pass through democratic deliberation — and a convention designed by ALEC, funded by dark money, and staffed by Project 2025 alumni is not democratic deliberation.

This argument is deployable in the same political vocabulary as the campaign finance reform argument (Domain 51): unaccountable money distorting democratic processes. The COS convention is a mechanism for unaccountable political infrastructure to bypass the congressional deliberation that the Framers required for constitutional change.
