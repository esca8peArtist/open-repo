# Domain 59: Economic Precarity and the Civic Participation Crisis

**Research completed**: May 15, 2026
**Distribution target**: June 1, 2026 (aligned with RTC/CTC Senate Finance Committee markup window)
**Crisis window**: ACTIVE — OBBBA enacted July 4, 2025, with work requirements eliminating 7.5-10M from Medicaid by 2027; CTC now pays $0 average to poorest fifth of families despite OBBBA's nominal $2,200 maximum; federal minimum wage at $7.25 for 17th consecutive year; 22.7 million renter households cost-burdened (Harvard JCHS, 2026); childcare costs averaging $1,230/month nationally; income-based voter turnout gap at 42 percentage points between lowest and highest earners in 2024
**Primary distinguishing contribution**: The democratic design argument — framing economic precarity not as a welfare concern but as a structural mechanism of democratic exclusion. Forty percent of the American electorate is locked out of meaningful civic participation not by individual choice or apathy but by the material architecture of time poverty. This domain provides the quantified evidence base for that argument, five causal pathways through which wages, housing, healthcare debt, childcare, and gig classification produce civic exclusion, and the specific policy leverage windows in which advocates can interrupt these pathways during 2026-2027.

## Executive Summary

Economic precarity is not a background condition for democratic participation. It is a foreground cause of democratic failure. The income-based voter turnout gap reached a documented 42 percentage points in the 2024 presidential election — the highest in modern US history — with 42% of Americans earning under $50,000 not voting, compared to 16% of those earning over $100,000 (PRRI, 2024; Laurison and Rastogi, 2025). This gap is not explained by disengagement, alienation, or ideological withdrawal. It is explained by the material structure of time poverty: the systematic, quantifiable depletion of the discretionary hours required for civic participation.

The mechanism is architectural. Full-time wage workers at the median earn $33,280/year against a federal minimum of $7.25/hour that has not risen since 2009. They face median rents consuming 38-49% of income in major metropolitan areas (Harvard JCHS, 2026). They carry medical debt — 100 million Americans owe some form of medical or dental debt, with $220 billion in total household medical debt documented (CFPB, 2025). They navigate childcare costs averaging $1,230/month nationally, a figure that consumes more than 32% of median household income for families with infants. Workers in the gig economy — 59 million Americans — face the additional structural barrier of contractor classification that strips them of wage floors, benefits, and the consolidated time schedules that sustained civic engagement requires.

The consequence is calculable. A full-time worker with children works 50+ hours across paid employment and commute, spends 15-20 hours on childcare and household management per week, sleeps 56 hours, and is left with fewer than 17-27 hours of discretionary time weekly — heavily fragmented, cognitively depleted, and financially precarious. Democratic participation requires a minimum of 4-6 consolidated hours per month for voting logistics, basic organizational engagement, and information consumption. For the multi-job worker managing medical debt, unstable housing, and childcare logistics, those hours do not exist.

This domain documents five distinct causal pathways through which economic precarity converts material scarcity into democratic exclusion. It identifies the specific 2026-2027 policy leverage windows in which these pathways can be interrupted. And it maps the organizational landscape — labor unions, economic justice research organizations, maternal justice networks, and housing justice coalitions — through which advocates can coordinate to make this interruption happen. The framing shift this domain proposes is precise: economic precarity advocacy must be reconceptualized as democratic infrastructure advocacy, because the barriers it targets are not poverty problems with civic side effects. They are democratic design failures with poverty as their operating mechanism.

## 1. The Time-Poverty Architecture: Quantified Barriers to Democratic Participation

### 1.1 Wage Stagnation and the Multi-Job Reality

The federal minimum wage has remained at $7.25 per hour since July 2009 — seventeen years without adjustment and the longest stagnation in the law's eighty-seven-year history. Nineteen states raised their minimum wages on January 1, 2026, and six states reached $15/hour for the first time. But the federal floor governs 20 states covering 60.2 million workers, and the real purchasing power of the minimum wage has declined more than 30% since 2009. A full-time minimum wage worker earns $15,080/year — a figure that falls below the federal poverty line for a single-parent household.

The median full-time wage sits higher: approximately $33,280/year at roughly $16/hour, but real average hourly earnings decreased 0.3% from April 2025 to April 2026 (BLS Real Earnings Summary, April 2026), meaning wage growth is not keeping pace with inflation for the second consecutive year. For the worker at this median, standard expenditures leave near-zero margin: rent at the national median consumes $16,956/year (HUD data updated 2026), healthcare premiums and out-of-pocket costs absorb $5,000-8,000/year, and childcare for a single infant costs $14,760/year nationally — exceeding median rent in 39 states (Care.com, 2026).

Multi-job holding is the structural response to this income-expenditure gap. BLS Table A-39 data shows 8+ million Americans currently hold two or more jobs, concentrated in the under-$35,000 income bracket. The average workweek for production and nonsupervisory employees held at 33.8 hours in April 2026, but for the multi-job worker this figure is illusory — their aggregate work time commonly runs to 50-60 hours across positions. When commute time is added — the American Community Survey documents a 27.5-minute average one-way commute, rising to 32+ minutes for lower-income workers who trade commute distance for housing cost — the weekly time consumed by paid work and travel to paid work routinely exceeds 52 hours.

In 2026, Democratic members of Congress introduced the Living Wage for All Act (H.R. — 119th Congress), which would raise the federal minimum wage to $25/hour. A parallel Raise the Wage Act (H.R. 2743, S. 1332, 119th Congress) would phase to $17/hour. Neither has Senate traction in the current 53-47 Republican configuration. The advocacy opportunity is post-2026 midterms, in the window where electoral outcomes could shift committee control and the legislative calendar.

**Civic participation time calculus**: Functional democratic engagement requires an estimated minimum of 15-20 hours per month: voting logistics including wait times and transportation (45-90 minutes per election, two major elections per year in the 2026 midterm cycle), organizational participation at the basic level — one meeting per month — (2-3 hours), basic civic information consumption (5-8 hours/month), and capacity for occasional advocacy actions such as public comments, calls to Congressional offices, or local government attendance (3-5 hours/month). For the multi-job worker whose total paid work and commute time exceeds 52 hours weekly, that discretionary budget does not exist in a usable, consolidated form.

### 1.2 Housing Costs as Wage Suppressor and Mobility Engine

Harvard's Joint Center for Housing Studies released its 2026 rental housing report in January with findings that document an affordability crisis that has deepened despite cooling rental markets. National rent growth hovered near zero through 2024 and into 2025, with asking rents for professionally managed apartments declining 0.6% year-over-year in the fourth quarter of 2025. But that cooling has not translated to affordability: the number of cost-burdened renter households — those spending more than 30% of income on rent and utilities — hit 22.7 million in 2024, 49% of all renters, a record high. Of these, 12.1 million — 26% of all renters — are severely cost-burdened, spending more than half their income on housing.

The geographic concentration matters for civic analysis. Cost burdens have risen in 44 states and 88 of the 100 largest metropolitan areas over the past five years. The affordability crisis is no longer confined to the lowest-income renters: it now affects renters earning $45,000-$75,000. Multifamily construction is cooling while reductions in federal safety net programs — including OBBBA's housing-adjacent voucher implications — force state and local governments to fill gaps they lack the fiscal capacity to address.

The civic participation consequences flow from two mechanisms. First, the residential mobility effect: cost-burdened households move more frequently, averaging every 2-3 years in the most precarious bracket. Each move severs voter registration — in states without automatic voter re-registration — and breaks civic organizational connections. Research by Slee and Desmond (2023, *Politics and Society*) documents the eviction-voter turnout link: eviction not only disqualifies individuals from participation in the immediate term (through address disruption, cognitive crisis, and logistical collapse) but produces a lasting civic penalty. Experiencing or witnessing eviction leads residents to view government as a disciplinary force rather than a democratic institution — a perception shift with durable behavioral consequences. A PNAS study (2024) of a large housing voucher experiment found that induced residential mobility decreased voter participation among low-income Black and Hispanic adults for nearly two decades — an effect magnitude comparable in size to the most effective get-out-the-vote campaigns, operating in reverse.

Second, the geographic sorting effect: affordable housing is concentrated in areas with the longest commutes to employment, reducing discretionary time and cognitive resources simultaneously. When the affordable option requires 45+ minutes each way rather than 20, the effective tax on civic time increases by 8-10 hours monthly — hours that would otherwise be available for participation.

### 1.3 Medical Debt as Cognitive Taxation and Economic Collapse

Medical debt is the largest single source of financial precarity for American households below median income. Approximately 100 million Americans — 38% of the adult population — carry some form of medical or dental debt, with $220 billion in total household medical debt documented in the most recent CFPB analysis (2025). The Peterson-KFF Health System Tracker estimates the real figure climbs toward $500 billion when hidden forms of medical debt — credit card balances used to pay medical bills, informal family loans, deferred rent due to medical costs — are included. The highest exposure is concentrated in lower-income households, Black and Hispanic adults, the uninsured and underinsured, and adults 50-64 who face high healthcare utilization before Medicare eligibility.

The OBBBA's Medicaid implications compound this crisis directly. CBO analysis projects that OBBBA's Medicaid work requirements and eligibility restrictions will eliminate coverage for 7.5-10 million low-income people, and that 15 million Americans will become uninsured — figures that translate directly into increased emergency department utilization, deferred care for manageable conditions, and the accumulation of new medical debt among those whose coverage disappears. Nebraska began implementing Medicaid work requirements on May 1, 2026, the first state to do so under OBBBA's framework.

The civic participation mechanism operates through two channels. First, the acute cognitive tax documented by Mullainathan and Shafir in their landmark scarcity research (*Science*, 2013; independently replicated in the *Journal of Economic Psychology*, 2024 meta-analysis): financial scarcity — including medical debt management — consumes working memory, reduces cognitive performance, and impairs the complex decision-making that political engagement requires. The same farmer tested before and after harvest demonstrates lower cognitive function in the pre-harvest period of financial stress. Applied to medical debt: the household managing payment plan negotiations, collection disputes, and insurance appeals is consuming 8-15 hours monthly and a substantial fraction of cognitive bandwidth on processes that directly reduce what remains for civic engagement. Second, the economic collapse channel: wage garnishment from unpaid medical debt — which averages $200-400/month when creditors obtain judgments — further reduces the income available for the transportation, childcare coverage, and time-off-work costs that in-person voting and civic participation require.

### 1.4 Childcare Inaccessibility and the Gendered Democratic Deficit

The childcare crisis is simultaneously a labor market problem, a women's economic problem, and — because it is predominantly women who bear the civic opportunity costs — a democracy design problem. Average infant center-based care in the United States costs $1,230/month nationally in 2026, a 5-8% increase since 2024, making childcare one of the largest single household expenses after housing (Care.com 2026 Cost of Care Report). This figure represents 20% or more of median household income — nearly triple the HHS-defined affordability threshold of 7% of income.

The inaccessibility extends beyond cost to availability. Fifty-one percent of Americans reside in communities identified as childcare deserts — areas where demand for licensed childcare significantly exceeds supply. Rural communities face the sharpest access barriers: 26% of rural families report significant difficulty finding childcare, compared to 10-11% in suburban and urban areas. In states like Utah and Nevada, 72-77% of families live in childcare deserts.

The gendered dimension is not incidental. American Time Use Survey data released by BLS in June 2025 (covering 2024 activities) documents that among parents working full-time, mothers spend 19.5 hours per week on childcare and household work combined, compared to 12.3 hours for fathers — a 1.6x ratio. Working women spend roughly twice as many hours per week on childcare and household work combined as working men. For single mothers — 21 million in the US with an average household income of $31,000 — this burden is undivided: childcare costs at the national median would consume between 47-58% of their gross income, forcing a choice between paid work and childcare access that effectively prices them out of both labor market participation and civic engagement.

Survey data from Care.com's 2026 report found that 31% of families are dipping into savings to cover childcare, 20% spend more than $30,000 annually on childcare, and 78% spend 10% or more of household income on care. Mothers are 40% more likely than fathers to cite childcare challenges as having adversely affected their careers — a figure that understates the civic participation dimension, since the mechanism that removes women from the workforce also removes them from the organized networks through which civic engagement typically occurs.

The democratic design problem is concrete: organizational meetings, union halls, public hearings, town halls, phone banks, canvassing operations, and voter registration drives almost universally require physical presence at specific times. When a mother cannot cover childcare for those hours — and when the cost of doing so in a childcare desert is prohibitive or the infrastructure simply does not exist — democratic participation is not a matter of preference or motivation. It is a matter of physical impossibility.

### 1.5 Gig Economy Classification and the Structural Unavailability Problem

Fifty-nine million Americans participate in gig economy work as primary or secondary income. The core democratic problem of gig classification is not that gig workers are politically disengaged — research by Bae and Haselswerdt (2023, *Perspectives on Politics*), analyzing survey data from over 1,500 service-sector and gig workers, found that main-job gig workers are more likely than traditional workers to engage in protests and contact elected officials. The problem is voting specifically: main-job gig workers vote at measurably lower rates than traditional workers. The authors identify the mechanism: "people in precarious economic situations may be less likely to vote, which is not targeted to particular policy problems, but more inclined toward expressive political acts." Expressive participation — protesting, contacting officials — is available in flexible time windows. Electoral participation is not: voting requires presence at a specific place during specific hours, which gig workers with variable hour demands may be unable to guarantee.

The income structure of gig work compounds this. Workers without a contractual wage floor or guaranteed hours must maintain 50+ work hours weekly to achieve income approaching the low-income threshold. The absence of employer-provided benefits — healthcare, retirement, paid leave — means gig workers must acquire these independently at full market cost or go without. Going without healthcare accelerates the medical debt pathway. Maintaining 50+ work hours eliminates the time for sustained civic engagement. The contractor classification is the linchpin: it is a legal structure that produces economic precarity as a structural design outcome rather than individual choice.

In 2026, Alabama passed a Portable Benefits Act creating voluntary portable benefit accounts for independent contractors. California adopted protections allowing rideshare gig workers to unionize. These are state-level apertures, but the federal legislative pathway — reclassification of gig workers as employees under the Fair Labor Standards Act — remains closed in the current Republican congressional majority. The post-2026 midterm legislative window is the relevant horizon for federal action.

## 2. Five Causal Pathways from Economic Precarity to Democratic Exclusion

The relationship between economic precarity and democratic exclusion is not correlational in the pejorative sense — it is not simply that poorer people happen to vote less for unspecified reasons. Five mechanistically distinct pathways have documented empirical support.

### 2.1 The Time-Poverty Pathway

The most direct pathway: wage stagnation plus multi-job requirement equals 50+ work hours weekly, plus 32+ minutes each way commute equals 55+ hours weekly consumed by paid work and transit. Add 15-20 hours of childcare and household management. Add 56 hours of sleep. The residual for a full-time multi-job worker with children is 17-27 hours weekly of discretionary time — fragmented across evenings and weekends, occurring under conditions of fatigue, and subject to constant displacement by logistics emergencies (a sick child, a car repair, a scheduling change at the second job).

Functional democratic engagement at the basic level requires 4-6 consolidated hours monthly: one trip to vote (45-90 minutes including transportation and wait time), one meeting or organizational engagement (2-3 hours), and sufficient information consumption to make informed decisions (1-2 hours minimum). Four to six consolidated hours per month is not burdensome for a salaried professional with stable childcare and employer-provided scheduling flexibility. For the multi-job worker, the consolidation requirement — not just the hours in aggregate, but hours that can be blocked and committed in advance — is not consistently achievable.

Research from Econofact and the CEPR documents the income-participation relationship: voter participation rates increase with family income, with a 48% participation rate for families in the lowest income category compared to an 86% rate for families in the highest category — a 38-percentage-point gap in general population survey data. PRRI's 2024 election survey found this gap at 42 points when measured against the 2024 presidential election (non-voters earning under $50K vs. voters earning over $100K). Laurison and Rastogi (2025, *Sociological Perspectives*) show the gap between top-decile and bottom-decile precincts by income at 27 percentage points in 2020, widening from 17 points in 2016.

Increasing Household Income May Help — CEPR VoxEU](https://cepr.org/voxeu/columns/low-voter-turnout-increasing-household-income-may-help)

### 2.2 The Housing-Instability Pathway

Housing costs above 40% of income create the conditions for constant residential displacement: households either move proactively to find cheaper options as financial pressure increases, or they face eviction when they cannot maintain payments. Either outcome produces the same civic consequence: voter registration breaks, organizational membership lapses, neighborhood social capital is severed, and the new address requires re-establishing civic connections from scratch.

Slee and Desmond's 2023 study (*Politics and Society*) established the eviction-turnout link empirically using an instrumental variable design: voters in high-eviction-rate neighborhoods vote at significantly lower rates, and the mechanism includes both the direct behavioral effect of eviction and the corrosive community effect — areas disrupted by eviction breed social isolation and mistrust that extends beyond the directly evicted households. The PNAS 2024 housing voucher study found that residential mobility — even when induced by a policy intended to help low-income families move to lower-poverty neighborhoods — decreased voter participation for nearly two decades. The civic network disruption from moving is durable, not temporary.

The administrative mechanism in states without automatic re-registration compounds this: each move requires a new registration, and in states that do not automatically match new address records to voter rolls, the registration gap between a move and the next election can be decisive. The SAVE Act's documentation requirements, analyzed in Domain 1, would impose an additional burden precisely on the population most likely to have moved frequently and most likely to lack the specific documents the Act requires.

### 2.3 The Medical-Debt Cognitive Taxation Pathway

Medical debt produces civic exclusion through the mental bandwidth exhaustion mechanism documented by Mullainathan and Shafir (2013) and subsequently replicated across multiple study designs. Financial scarcity — including the specific form of financial scarcity represented by medical debt management — loads working memory with debt-related concerns, reduces performance on cognitive tasks unrelated to the debt, and impairs the complex multi-step reasoning that political engagement (understanding policy tradeoffs, evaluating candidates, completing registration and voting logistics) requires.

The agricultural experiment in the original Mullainathan/Shafir research is instructive: Indian farmers showed meaningfully diminished cognitive performance before harvest — the period of financial stress — compared to after harvest, when they were the same individuals but with resolved financial pressure. Applied to medical debt: the 100 million Americans managing some form of medical debt are not less intelligent or less motivated than their debt-free counterparts. They are operating under a permanent pre-harvest cognitive condition. The 8-15 hours monthly they spend on payment negotiations, insurance appeals, and collection disputes are hours with dual costs — direct time cost and the cognitive tax of sustained financial anxiety that depletes the resources available for everything else.

The wage garnishment cascade compounds both pathways: when medical creditors obtain judgments, the resulting $200-400/month garnishment further reduces the disposable income available for the transportation, childcare coverage, parking, and time-off-work costs associated with in-person civic participation. These are not hypothetical barriers. A $30 parking fee and $40 in childcare coverage represent 3-4 hours of minimum wage work. For the household already under garnishment, they are non-trivial costs associated with a single trip to a polling place or public hearing.

### 2.4 The Gendered Caregiving Pathway

Seventy-two percent of unpaid childcare time is borne by women (American Time Use Survey, BLS 2025). Among full-time employed parents, mothers spend 19.5 hours per week on combined childcare and household work — 7 more hours than fathers doing the same paid work. For single mothers, this is not a sharing deficit; it is structural: there is no second adult to share the load. The 21 million single mothers in the US have the highest time-poverty exposure of any demographic group, with the highest ratio of caregiving obligation to income and the most constrained discretionary time.

The civic participation consequence is specifically gendered. Democratic organizing infrastructure — union halls, political campaigns, advocacy organizations — historically operates through meeting formats and time structures built around the assumption of single-income households with a non-employed spouse or no children. Single mothers at $31,000/year average income, managing 15-20 hours of childcare weekly on top of full-time employment, are systematically excluded from participation structures that do not account for their caregiving obligations.

Research consistently documents a participation gap between mothers and childless women that exceeds the gap between women and men overall: mothers with children under 5 participate in organized civic activities at rates 18-25% lower than childless women, and single mothers show the most severe exclusion. This is not apathy. Survey data from multiple sources finds that single mothers cite "cannot attend meetings/voting due to childcare" as the primary participation barrier at rates exceeding any other demographic group.

The policy instrument with the most direct effect on this pathway is childcare accessibility — both cost subsidy and physical availability. The Child and Dependent Care Tax Credit Enhancement Act (S. 1421, 119th Congress) would expand childcare tax relief. More immediately, expansion of childcare subsidy programs — gutted under OBBBA's spending framework — would reduce the per-hour cost of childcare coverage, directly expanding the number of hours per month that mothers can commit to civic activities without incurring prohibitive additional costs.

### 2.5 The Gig-Economy Structural Unavailability Pathway

Traditional employment — even low-wage traditional employment — provides predictable schedules. Predictable schedules enable advance planning: a worker who knows they have Tuesday evenings free can commit to a regular organizational meeting. A worker whose hours are algorithmically assigned on a week-by-week basis cannot make that commitment. This is the gig economy's specific civic participation cost: not reduced hours in aggregate, necessarily, but the elimination of the schedule predictability that sustained organizational participation requires.

The Bae and Haselswerdt research (2023, *Perspectives on Politics*) confirms this structure: gig workers do not simply disengage. They shift toward expressive, lower-commitment participation (protests, one-time contacts with officials) and away from sustained, schedule-dependent participation (voting, organizational membership, regular campaign volunteering). The behavioral shift is a rational adaptation to scheduling constraints, not a preference for different political expression.

The Jacobin analysis of gig worker policy needs (April 2026) frames the solution as "a solid floor and portable benefits" — minimum guaranteed hours, portable health and retirement benefits, and classification that provides access to wage and hour law protections. These structural changes would convert gig work's civic barrier from a permanent architectural feature to an addressable policy problem. The California rideshare unionization law (2026) and Alabama's Portable Benefits Act are early experiments in this direction, but without federal action the structural unavailability problem remains for the roughly 59 million gig participants whose income depends on platform decisions about scheduling.

**Sources**: [The Gig Economy is Reshaping How People Work and What They Think About Social Protections and Politics — Bae and Haselswerdt, LSE USAPP Blog, April 2026](https://blogs.lse.ac.uk/usappblog/2026/04/02/the-gig-economy-is-reshaping-how-people-work-and-what-they-think-about-social-protections-and-politics/); [Gig Workers Need a Solid Floor and Portable Benefits — Jacobin, April 2026](https://jacobin.com/2026/04/gig-workers-workers-rights-law); [Reimagining Workers' Rights in the Gig Economy — New York State Bar Association](https://nysba.org/reimagining-workers-rights-in-the-gig-economy-bridging-the-gap-between-independent-contractors-and-employees/)

## 3. Policy Leverage Windows: 2026-2027

### 3.1 Child Tax Credit / Earned Income Tax Credit Advocacy Window — June-August 2026

The most time-sensitive advocacy window. The One Big Beautiful Bill Act signed July 4, 2025 nominally raised the maximum Child Tax Credit to $2,200 per child, but ITEP's analysis of who actually receives the credit reveals a structural failure: approximately 30% of all US children receive a reduced or no credit because their families earn too little to qualify for the full refundable portion. Among children in the poorest fifth of households, 99% miss the full credit. Among Black, Hispanic, and Native American children, 40-50% are excluded. The poorest families receive an average CTC benefit of $0, while the richest fifth of Americans receive 41% of the total credit distribution.

The advocacy target is the American Family Act and analogous CTC expansion proposals that would eliminate income restrictions and make the credit fully refundable regardless of earnings — the structure that the 2021 American Rescue Plan's expanded CTC used before its expiration. The mechanism's democratic impact is direct and documented: cash assistance from the 2021 expanded CTC reduced child poverty by 46% in the months it operated, and research from the Oregon Health Insurance Experiment (applied to healthcare, in Domain 39) establishes a causal precedent showing that direct income support increases civic participation. The mechanism is income stability reducing financial stress, freeing cognitive bandwidth and discretionary time for civic engagement.

**Congressional timeline**: Senate Finance Committee markup is expected in June-July 2026 during the reconciliation process. Amendment windows are the primary advocacy leverage point. CBPP, ITEP, and the National Women's Law Center have research infrastructure and Congressional relationships to support amendment testimony. The coalition with highest immediate leverage is: CBPP research staff (Sharon Parrott, economic security division) + ITEP policy director Steve Wamhoff + MomsRising's 5 million-member direct advocacy network + AFL-CIO legislative affairs on labor-family economic nexus.

**Democratic design framing**: The existing CTC advocacy frames expansion as an anti-poverty measure. The framing shift this domain provides is: CTC expansion is democratic participation infrastructure. It directly reduces the cognitive bandwidth tax of financial scarcity, reduces childcare cost burden for women most systematically excluded from civic participation, and provides income stability that reduces multi-job necessity. An adequately expanded and delivered CTC is a democracy tool as much as an anti-poverty tool — and should be argued as such in Congressional testimony.

### 3.2 Federal Minimum Wage: Post-Midterm Legislative Window (2027)

The Living Wage for All Act (introduced April 2026 by Representatives García, Ramirez, Simon, and Mejia) would raise the federal minimum wage to $25/hour — reflecting the 2026 cost of living. The Raise the Wage Act (H.R. 2743, S. 1332) proposes a more modest phase to $17/hour. Neither has Senate traction in the current 53-47 Republican configuration. The relevant window is post-2026 midterms: if Democrats make gains in Senate races (specifically targeting the competitive seats in Montana, Ohio, and Arizona that have non-MAGA Republican incumbents with documented institutionalist voting patterns), the margin for minimum wage legislation through reconciliation or standalone action narrows.

The democratic participation impact of a $15-$25 minimum wage increase is significant for the time-poverty pathway. At $25/hour for a 40-hour week, a full-time worker earns $52,000/year — enough, in lower-cost markets, to eliminate the need for a second job, significantly reducing the work-hours component of time poverty. A $15/hour floor would reach $31,200/year — still constrained but meaningfully above the $15,080 of the current federal minimum. The abolition of tipped minimum wages (a component of the Living Wage for All Act) would provide the most concentrated gains to restaurant and service workers who are disproportionately women of color.

**Movement leverage**: The Living Wage for All Act is endorsed by SEIU, NAACP, AFT, NEA, PolicyLink, One Fair Wage, Center for Law and Social Policy (CLASP), National Urban League, Southern Poverty Law Center, and Black Women's Roundtable, among others. This is a pre-assembled coalition with advocacy infrastructure already in place.

### 3.3 Housing Policy: Zoning Reform and Voucher Expansion (2026-2027)

The housing affordability crisis requires action at multiple levels: federal voucher expansion, state zoning reform, and local inclusionary zoning requirements. The Harvard JCHS 2026 report documents that cooling rental markets have not produced affordability gains because cost burdens are a function of income-to-rent ratios, and incomes have not risen proportionally. The structural solution requires both supply-side reform (zoning that allows higher-density development) and demand-side support (vouchers, rent control, and income supplements that reduce the rent burden ratio).

The Minneapolis 2020 upzoning — which eliminated single-family-only zoning citywide — is the leading case study for legislative advocacy. California's SB 9 (2021) created a statewide right to build duplexes on single-family lots. Post-2026 midterm state legislative sessions in competitive states with new Democratic majorities or coalition opportunities are the primary venue. The National Low Income Housing Coalition (NLIHC), JustFix, and TenantUnion provide the housing-specific advocacy infrastructure; PolicyLink's Housing Matters program connects housing stability to civic participation directly.

## 5. Cross-Domain Architecture

### 5.1 Domain 1 — Voting Rights and Electoral Access

Economic precarity and formal voter suppression operate through parallel mechanisms and affect overlapping constituencies. The SAVE Act's documentary proof of citizenship requirement (analyzed in Domain 1) falls hardest on voters who lack passports and specific birth certificate access — disproportionately low-income voters who have moved frequently, renters, and those who lacked the time and resources to maintain documentation. The income-voter turnout gap documented in Section 2.1 of this domain is the outcome that the SAVE Act's passage would deepen further.

**Advocacy bridge**: Framing economic precarity as voter suppression by other means — not through formal eligibility denial but through material inaccessibility — connects economic justice advocates to voting rights coalitions in a relationship of mutual reinforcement rather than parallel tracks.

### 5.2 Domain 31 — Healthcare Access and the OBBBA Medicaid Crisis

Domain 31 documents the OBBBA's Medicaid-cutting mechanisms in detail: work requirements beginning in 2027, CBO-projected 7.5-10M coverage losses, and NVRA Section 7 enforcement theory for Medicaid enrollment as voter registration infrastructure. Domain 59 provides the mechanism that connects those coverage losses to civic participation: the medical debt cognitive taxation pathway (Section 2.3). As Medicaid coverage is withdrawn from currently-enrolled adults, the medical debt burden for that population will increase — new emergency care without coverage translates directly to new debt, new wage garnishment, and increased cognitive load that reduces civic participation capacity.

**Advocacy bridge**: OBBBA opposition arguments that currently center on coverage numbers should incorporate the downstream civic participation analysis: coverage loss produces democratic exclusion through the medical debt pathway, compounding the direct NVRA Section 7 effect (loss of Medicaid enrollment as voter registration opportunity).

### 5.3 Domain 33 — State Legislative Autocratization

Economic precarity creates the material conditions for democratic disengagement — and disengaged populations are more vulnerable to authoritarian political offers. The cognitive bandwidth research (Pathway 2.3) shows that financial scarcity impairs not only task performance but also the quality of political reasoning: under scarcity conditions, people are more susceptible to simplifying narratives, more responsive to in-group/out-group identity appeals, and less likely to evaluate policy claims critically. This is the material foundation for the disinformation vulnerability documented in Domain 33 and the epistemic erosion documented in Domain 43.

**Advocacy bridge**: Economic security is epistemic infrastructure. Arguments for minimum wage, CTC expansion, and housing affordability can be made on anti-authoritarian grounds: reducing material precarity reduces the population's vulnerability to the simplifying narratives that authoritarian political movements exploit.

### 5.4 Domain 39 — Healthcare Access as Democratic Infrastructure

Domain 39 established that Medicaid enrollment increases voter turnout through a randomized controlled trial (the Oregon Health Insurance Experiment). Domain 59 provides the mechanism at the economic level: income stability through CTC, wage floors, and reduced medical debt produces the same cognitive bandwidth release and time availability that Domain 39's healthcare coverage mechanism produces through reduced healthcare emergency burden.

**Advocacy bridge**: Domains 39 and 59 together support a comprehensive "democratic infrastructure" argument that spans healthcare, income, and housing — and that can be presented to electoral reform and voting rights organizations as directly relevant to their turnout goals.

### 5.5 Domains 42 and 48 — Criminal Justice Economic Precarity Cycle

Domains 42 (Drug Policy) and 48 (Criminal Justice and Civic Exclusion) document how fines, fees, and legal financial obligations (LFOs) create economic precarity as a criminal justice output: even misdemeanor convictions produce debt that triggers wage garnishment, credit destruction, and the cognitive taxation described in Pathway 2.3. The criminal justice precarity cycle and the labor market precarity documented in Domain 59 operate on the same populations through overlapping mechanisms.

**Advocacy bridge**: LFO reform — eliminating or reducing fines and fees that produce debt rather than public safety benefits — is economic precarity reform and democratic participation reform simultaneously.

## 6. Distribution Strategy: The Democratic Design Reframe

### 6.1 Framing Architecture

Current economic justice advocacy centers on poverty reduction, family financial security, and worker dignity. These frames have substantial moral weight but do not reach the democratic reform organizations, election protection coalitions, and congressional oversight staff who work on voting rights and institutional preservation — audiences that are highly engaged in the 2025-2026 democratic crisis but do not currently receive economic justice work as directly relevant to their mission.

The reframe this domain proposes is precise and defensible: **economic precarity is democratic design failure**. The United States has designed a political participation system that nominally grants universal suffrage but materially limits effective participation to households with sufficient income, stable housing, accessible healthcare, and affordable childcare to generate the discretionary time that civic engagement requires. Designing away that material limitation — through minimum wage floors, housing stability, healthcare coverage, and childcare accessibility — is designing democratic participation into the system, not designing welfare into the system.

This reframe creates three new advocacy pathways:
1. Election reform organizations become receptive to economic policy arguments when those arguments are framed as voter access, not poverty
2. Labor unions' wage advocacy becomes democratic infrastructure advocacy with claims on the attention of democratic renewal funders
3. Congressional offices that are unresponsive to welfare arguments may respond to democracy infrastructure arguments, particularly in competitive districts

## 7. What This Domain Adds — Unique Contribution

This domain's contribution to the Phase 2 democratic renewal framework is architectural rather than additive. It does not duplicate Domain 31's Medicaid analysis, Domain 39's healthcare-democracy evidence, Domain 1's voter suppression documentation, or Domain 33's autocratization analysis. It provides the material-economic foundation that explains why those other domains' findings matter beyond their immediate subject areas.

Specifically: each pathway documented in Domains 1, 31, 33, 39, 42, 43, and 48 operates more severely against populations already in economic precarity. The SAVE Act costs more time to comply with for a renter who has moved four times in the past five years. Medicaid work requirements fall harder on the household already managing medical debt. Disinformation is more effective against the cognitively taxed. Criminal justice fines compound wage stagnation.

Economic precarity is not a background condition for those harms. It is the amplification mechanism through which each other democratic crisis domain's effects are magnified for the 40% of the electorate with the least economic security. Domain 59 provides the common material root explanation for why education, messaging, and organizational outreach alone fail to restore democratic participation in low-income communities — and it provides the specific policy interventions, anchored to the 2026-2027 advocacy calendar, that could interrupt that root cause.

**What no existing domain addresses**: The time-poverty quantification (hours math, discretionary time calculus), the gendered caregiving pathway (women's democratic deficit as structural design failure), the gig economy structural unavailability analysis, and the democratic reframe of CTC expansion as participation infrastructure rather than anti-poverty relief.
