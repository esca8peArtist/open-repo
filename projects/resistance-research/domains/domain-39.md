## Executive Summary

The United States Constitution's amendment process under Article V is simultaneously the most powerful tool for democratic renewal and the most dangerous vulnerability in the democratic architecture. Article V provides two pathways: the congressional proposal pathway (two-thirds of both chambers, then three-quarters of states), which has produced all 27 amendments to date, and the convention pathway (applications from two-thirds of states, then a convention whose output must be ratified by three-quarters of states), which has never been used. The convention pathway's danger is not hypothetical — it is structural and documented by some of the most distinguished constitutional scholars across the ideological spectrum, from Chief Justice Warren Burger to Justice Antonin Scalia to Harvard's Laurence Tribe: once a convention is called, there is no constitutional mechanism to limit its scope. A convention called to propose a balanced budget amendment could propose to eliminate the First Amendment's free exercise clause, abolish the income tax, impose congressional term limits, or restructure the Senate to eliminate proportional representation of the population. The ratification requirement (three-quarters of states) provides some protection — but in an environment where 20 states have already passed Convention of States resolutions, and where additional state legislatures are potential targets, that protection is less robust than it appears.

The Convention of States Project — funded by the Koch network, Mercer family, and ALEC-aligned donors — has made reaching 34 state applications its central organizational goal. As of January 2026, 20 states have passed the COS resolution. The movement is 14 states short of the threshold. However, ALEC-aligned lawyers have advanced a theory that would aggregate applications from different eras and different stated purposes — fiscal restraints, term limits, and other subject-specific conventions — toward the 34-state count. Under that theory, the threshold may already be within reach. H.Con.Res.15 in the 119th Congress calls for an Article V Convention for a "Fiscal Responsibility Amendment" — a congressional trigger bill positioned for use if Republicans maintain the House majority after November 2026.

The progressive case for Article V is equally serious and equally underexplored. Constitutional reform proposals that have majority public support but face systematic institutional obstruction — SCOTUS term limits (supported by 73% of Americans in recent polling), campaign finance reform (Citizens United reversal), abolition of the Electoral College, statehood for DC and Puerto Rico — all require either constitutional amendment or structural statutory reform that could be reversed by a future Congress. The amendment route, for these proposals, is not a threat to be resisted but a tool to be developed. The academic split on this question — Lessig supporting a controlled convention, Amar supporting it, Barnett supporting it from the right, Tribe opposing it — is not resolvable through abstract legal argument. It requires a political theory of when and how constitutional amendment serves democratic renewal versus when it serves democratic regression.

This domain provides that political theory, maps the current landscape, identifies the Michigan November 2026 vote as the most important near-term organizing anchor, and outlines the reform architecture for both defending against a convention that could harm democracy and advancing the amendments that democracy requires.

## 1. Article V: The Two Pathways and the Structural Problem

### 1.1 The Congressional Proposal Pathway (27 Amendments, All Successful)

Article V's first pathway requires two-thirds of both the House and Senate to propose an amendment, followed by ratification by three-quarters of the states (38 of 50). This pathway has been used for all 27 amendments — from the Bill of Rights (1791) to the Twenty-Seventh Amendment (1992, originally proposed in 1789). Its virtues are well understood: the requirement of congressional supermajorities ensures broad consensus before an amendment reaches the states, and the ratification requirement provides a further democratic check.

The pathway's limitation in the current environment is also well understood: the polarization of the US Senate has made two-thirds supermajority votes nearly impossible. The Senate has not achieved a two-thirds vote on a contested political question since the 1964 Civil Rights Act cloture vote. SCOTUS term limits, campaign finance reform, and Electoral College reform have majority public support but cannot achieve the 60-vote threshold for floor consideration, let alone two-thirds. The congressional pathway is functionally blocked for the most important democratic reform proposals of the current moment.

This blockage is not an argument for the convention pathway. It is an argument for understanding that the convention pathway's risks are at least as real as the congressional pathway's blockage, and that a theory of democratic renewal through Article V must account for both.

### 1.2 The Convention Pathway: Structure and the Runaway Problem

Article V's second pathway provides that "on the Application of the Legislatures of two thirds of the several States, [Congress] shall call a Convention for proposing Amendments." The convention must then propose amendments, which take effect when ratified by three-quarters of the states.

The "runaway convention" problem is not a progressive fever dream. It was identified by Chief Justice Warren Burger, who wrote in 1988: "There is no way to effectively limit or muzzle the actions of a constitutional convention. The convention could make its own rules and set its own agenda. Congress might try to limit the convention to one amendment or to one issue, but there is no way to assure that the convention would obey." Justice Antonin Scalia, at the Federalist Society's 2015 annual convention, said he "certainly would not want a constitutional convention" because "you get into a horrible, screaming fight over everything." Former US Solicitor General Archibald Cox expressed the same concern. The common thread: unlike constitutional conventions in other countries, an Article V convention has no established rulebook, no constitutional text specifying delegate selection, no limitation on scope that has ever been judicially enforced, and no precedent from which to derive governing principles.

The convention advocates' response — that ratification by three-quarters of states provides sufficient protection — underestimates the political dynamics of ratification. If 34 states have passed applications calling for a convention, those states are by definition already amenable to the project. The same political infrastructure that passed the applications can be mobilized for ratification campaigns. The 38-state ratification threshold is a protection against radical proposals that lack political support — but in a political environment where right-wing state legislatures have achieved extraordinary legislative coordination through ALEC, and where dark-money infrastructure can be deployed in state legislative campaigns at scale, 38 states is a lower bar than it appears.

## 2. The Convention of States Project: Funding, Strategy, and the Application Count

### 2.1 The Koch-ALEC-Heritage Network

The Convention of States Project (COS) is the primary vehicle for reaching 34 state applications. COS has deep organizational ties to the ALEC network, Heritage Foundation, and Koch-connected donors. The Mercer family has provided at least $500,000. Koch-connected Donors Trust has contributed additional funding. COS operates a sophisticated state legislative lobbying operation, with staff deployed in targeted state legislatures and template legislation distributed through ALEC's model bills network.

The Convention of States resolution, as passed by the 20 states with active resolutions as of January 2026, calls for a convention limited to three subjects: imposing fiscal restraints on the federal government, limiting the power and jurisdiction of the federal government, and limiting the terms of office for federal officials and members of Congress. These are not neutral procedural subjects — they embed the policy agenda of the convention's funders. A "fiscal restraints" amendment could constitutionalize a balanced budget requirement that would mandate austerity during recessions; a "limit federal jurisdiction" amendment could eliminate the constitutional basis for federal civil rights law, environmental regulation, and labor standards; a "term limits" amendment could lock in a permanent congressional composition that differs from democratic preferences.

COS reports 20 states with active, unrescinded resolutions as of January 2026. The count toward 34 is disputed because several states that COS counts have passed resolutions on different subjects (fiscal restraints only, or balanced budget only, rather than all three COS subjects), and seven states have rescinded all prior Article V applications. The application count problem is the domain's primary legal ambiguity: there is no authoritative mechanism for determining which applications count, whether subject-limitation is enforceable, and whether applications from the 1970s and 1980s remain valid. Common Cause has worked to rescind calls in eight states; several state legislatures have enacted rescission legislation whose validity is itself disputed.

### 2.2 The Aggregation Theory: Manufactured Threshold

The most dangerous development in the convention movement since 2020 is not the addition of new state applications. It is the development of aggregation theories that would allow convention proponents to declare the 34-state threshold reached without a genuine consensus of 34 states supporting a unified convention call. A lawyer involved in the 2020 fake electors scheme has been prominent in developing theories that would count applications from different eras and different subject matters — some from the 1980s, some from the 1990s, some subject-limited, some general — toward a single 34-state count.

The aggregation theory exploits the silence in Article V's text. The Constitution provides no mechanism for determining which applications count, no time limit on applications, and no requirement that they address the same subject. A convention proponent with this theory, a receptive Congress, and a political moment in which one party controls both chambers could declare a convention validly called over the objections of states that have expressly rescinded their applications — triggering a constitutional crisis with no clear resolution mechanism.

H.Con.Res.15 (119th Congress), the pending bill in the House calling for a Fiscal Responsibility Amendment convention, is the template for the congressional trigger mechanism. If Republicans retain the House after November 2026 and the aggregation theory gains political traction, this bill could become the vehicle for a rushed convention call.

### 2.3 Michigan: The November 2026 Organizing Anchor

Michigan's November 3, 2026 ballot contains two distinct but related questions.

**Proposal 1 — State Constitutional Convention**: Michigan's constitution requires a vote on whether to hold a state constitutional convention every 16 years. Michigan voters have rejected this measure by wide margins each time it has appeared — 77% to 23% in 1978, 72% to 28% in 1994, 67% to 22% in 2010. The 2026 vote is the automatic constitutional referral; it does not require petition signatures. Bipartisan opposition has emerged: both conservative and progressive groups oppose a state constitutional convention, and the vote is expected to follow the historical pattern of rejection.

**The Article V dimension**: Michigan's legislature has had H.B. 5151 and H.C.R. 2 introduced in January 2026 — the 34Ready Legislation calling for a federal Article V convention. If the state constitutional convention vote (Proposal 1) fails — as expected — it demonstrates that Michigan voters reject the convention mechanism on its merits. That rejection can be used as evidence against the claim that a federal Article V convention has democratic legitimacy: if even the state that has repeatedly rejected its own constitutional convention rejects the concept, what does that say about the claimed democratic mandate for a federal convention?

## 3. The Progressive Amendment Agenda: What Democracy Requires

### 3.1 The Structural Reform Proposals

The progressive case for Article V is grounded in the recognition that several democratic reforms with majority public support are constitutionally blocked: they require amendment because ordinary legislation would be either constitutionally prohibited (Electoral College abolition requires amendment) or politically reversible (statutory term limits for SCOTUS could be reversed by a future Congress sympathetic to the current Court's ideology). The major candidates:

**SCOTUS Term Limits**: A constitutional amendment establishing 18-year terms for Supreme Court Justices, with vacancies arising on a staggered schedule that allows each presidential term to fill predictable slots. Supported by 73% of Americans in multiple recent surveys. Eliminates the gaming of the appointment process that has produced the current Court's ideological profile. Bipartisan support at the public level even if not at the Senate level. See Domain 6 for the judicial independence context in which term limits would operate.

**Campaign Finance Reform (Citizens United Reversal)**: A constitutional amendment establishing that corporations and other artificial entities do not have the same First Amendment rights as natural persons with respect to political spending, and that Congress and the states may limit campaign contributions and expenditures to protect democratic integrity. Supported by more than 70% of Americans. Required because *Citizens United v. FEC* (2010) and *Buckley v. Valeo* (1976) construed the First Amendment to prohibit such limits — statutory reform would be struck down without an amendment.

**Electoral College Reform**: A constitutional amendment establishing a national popular vote or proportional allocation for the presidential election. The Electoral College cannot be abolished by statute — it requires amendment. The National Popular Vote Interstate Compact (currently enacted in states representing 209 of the required 270 electoral votes) provides a partial workaround that has survived legal challenges, but constitutional amendment provides greater durability.

**Senate Structural Reform**: The Constitution's equal-state representation in the Senate — specifically, Article V's protection of it ("no State, without its Consent, shall be deprived of its equal Suffrage in the Senate") — is one of the two constitutional provisions that Article V itself protects from amendment without unanimous consent. Senate structural reform therefore requires either unanimous state consent or a reconstituted constitutional theory that the framers did not intend this provision to permanently bind democratic majorities. This is the most legally complex amendment proposal and the one that most directly implicates the convention pathway, since the congressional proposal pathway requires a two-thirds Senate vote from the body being reformed.

**DC and Puerto Rico Statehood**: Adding states requires only a congressional majority under Article IV — not a constitutional amendment — and is therefore analytically different from the other proposals. But the political obstacles are the same: Senate minority obstruction, the filibuster, and the same coalition that blocks other democratic reforms. Statehood does not require Article V but is part of the same political reform agenda.

### 3.2 The Academic Split and the Democratic Design Question

The intellectual debate about Article V is not between those who understand the Constitution and those who do not. It is between constitutional scholars who make different empirical and normative judgments about the same legal text.

Lawrence Lessig — Harvard Law professor and leading campaign finance reformer — has argued that a controlled Article V convention focused on campaign finance reform could be structured with guardrails sufficient to prevent a runaway outcome. His argument is pragmatic: if the congressional pathway is permanently blocked by money-in-politics interests, the convention pathway, with appropriate constraints, may be the only available route to constitutional reform. Akhil Reed Amar — Yale Law professor — has made similar arguments about constitutional restoration through the amendment process. Randy Barnett — Georgetown Law professor and conservative constitutionalist — supports the convention pathway from the right, as a mechanism to restore the limited-government constitutional vision he believes the post-New Deal Court abandoned.

Laurence Tribe — Harvard Law professor and leading liberal constitutional scholar — opposes the convention pathway as too dangerous, arguing that no constraints on a convention's scope can be made legally enforceable before the convention acts, and that the ratification-by-states protection is insufficient given current state legislative dynamics. Common Cause, the AFL-CIO, and the NEA align with Tribe's position.

The democratic design question this domain poses: in an environment where the congressional amendment pathway is blocked and the democratic deficit is structural, what is the responsible theory of constitutional change? The answer is not that Article V is either always available or always dangerous — it is that the choice depends on the specific amendment proposed, the specific convention context, and the specific political safeguards in place. This domain does not answer that question definitively. It maps the terrain so that resistance networks can engage with it seriously.

## 4. Actionable Intelligence for Resistance Networks

### 4.1 Defend Michigan — The November 2026 Anchor

The Michigan Proposal 1 vote on November 3, 2026 is the most important near-term organizing anchor for Article V defense. The organizing objective:

- **Defeat Proposal 1** (the state constitutional convention question) by the widest possible margin, building on the historical precedent of 67–77% rejection
- **Document the margin** as evidence of Michigan voters' rejection of the convention mechanism, for use in the federal Article V debate
- **Counter-organize against the Article V state legislation** (H.B. 5151 / H.C.R. 2) in the Michigan legislature, using the Proposal 1 result as political leverage

**Coalition**: Common Cause Michigan, ACLU of Michigan, Michigan AFL-CIO, Michigan NAACP, Michigan Education Association. The bipartisan opposition already documented — both progressive organizations and conservative constitutionalists who fear a runaway convention — is the coalition template.

**Tactic**: Produce a Michigan-specific fact sheet documenting what a state constitutional convention could change (Michigan's civil rights provisions, equal rights provision, public education guarantee) and distribute through union halls, churches, and community organizations in the six weeks before the election.

### 4.2 Rescission Campaign: Defend the Application Count

The primary defensive strategy against COS reaching 34 states is maintaining state-level rescissions of prior Article V applications. Seven states have rescinded all prior Article V applications. Common Cause and allied organizations have worked to rescind calls in eight states. The rescission strategy requires:

- **Legal validation**: The legal validity of state rescissions is disputed. Filing a preemptive declaratory action establishing the legal authority of state legislatures to rescind prior applications — before a convention is declared — would provide judicial precedent for the rescission defense.

- **Target states**: The 14 states COS needs to reach 34 are the organizing targets. States with purple or Democratic-leaning legislatures that have not yet passed COS resolutions — states like Pennsylvania, Wisconsin, Nevada, Colorado — are the most defensible. Organizations in those states should be briefed on the convention risk and mobilized to prevent COS passage.

- **Aggregation theory litigation preparation**: Legal organizations should prepare litigation challenging the aggregation theory before it is deployed, establishing in federal court that different-subject applications from different eras cannot be aggregated toward a single 34-state count.

**Tactic**: File a declaratory action in federal district court seeking confirmation that (a) state rescissions are valid under Article V and (b) subject-limited applications cannot be aggregated across subjects. The American Constitution Society and Brennan Center's constitutional scholars are the best-positioned organizations to anchor this litigation.

### 4.3 Advance the Congressional Amendment Pathway for SCOTUS Term Limits

The congressional amendment pathway is not available for all reforms, but SCOTUS term limits may be achievable through a combination of statutory and constitutional approaches. The current pathway:

- **Statutory approach (proposed June 2023, S.1791, Supreme Court Term Limits and Regular Appointments Act)**: Establishes 18-year terms legislatively, relying on the theory that Congress can define the term structure for SCOTUS through legislation. The constitutional validity of this approach is contested. The Biden administration's Supreme Court reform commission found statutory term limits constitutionally uncertain.

- **Amendment approach**: A constitutional amendment explicitly establishing SCOTUS term limits would be unambiguous. The Two-Year Window: the 2026 midterms, if they produce a Democratic Senate majority, create the first Senate environment since 2021 in which a constitutional amendment proposal could receive a floor vote. This is not a certainty but it is the political window the amendment movement should be building toward.

**Tactic**: Organizations working on SCOTUS reform — Fix the Court, Demand Justice — should develop a legislative strategy for introducing an 18-year term limit amendment in the first week of the 120th Congress (January 2027) if Democrats achieve a Senate majority, allowing a floor vote before the political window closes.

### 4.4 Coalition Architecture for Cross-Ideological Convention Defense

The defense against a runaway convention is the only political objective in this domain with genuine cross-partisan coalition potential. Conservative constitutionalists who oppose a runaway convention (Federalist Society-adjacent scholars, libertarian organizations, fiscal conservatives who fear a convention's scope) can be coalition partners for the rescission campaign and the aggregation-theory litigation. The Cato Institute, the late Justice Scalia's constitutional writings, and multiple conservative constitutional scholars are on record opposing the convention pathway.

**Tactic**: Convene a cross-partisan constitutional law coalition — reaching explicitly to conservative scholars and organizations — to produce a joint legal brief, op-ed series, or public statement establishing the shared scholarly consensus that a convention cannot be scope-limited and that the ratification protection is insufficient given current state legislative dynamics. A statement signed by both Laurence Tribe and a prominent Federalist Society scholar carries more political weight than either alone.

## 5. Reform Pathways

### 5.1 Convention Defense Architecture

**Article V Clarification Act**: Federal legislation establishing procedural rules for the convention pathway — delegate selection, scope limitations, ratification procedures — that would apply to any convention called under Article V. The constitutional authority for such legislation is contested (Article V is self-executing), but the legislation would at minimum create a legal record of congressional intent and establish the procedural framework that convention opponents would argue in court.

**Application Validity Act**: Federal legislation establishing that Article V applications are subject to a 10-year time limit, that applications on different subjects cannot be aggregated toward a single 34-state count, and that state rescissions are legally effective. The constitutionality of this legislation is disputed — but the legal record it creates is valuable regardless of ultimate judicial resolution.

**State Rescission Model Legislation**: Common Cause and allied organizations should develop model legislation for state rescission of Article V applications, to be introduced in states that have passed COS resolutions or prior balanced budget/term limit applications. Wisconsin, Michigan, and Nevada are the highest-priority states for rescission campaigns in 2026.

### 5.2 The Progressive Amendment Agenda (2027–2028 Window)

The constitutional amendment agenda for democratic renewal requires coordinated organization now to be deployable if the 2026 midterms produce the necessary Senate composition. The priority sequence:

1. **SCOTUS Term Limits Amendment** (18-year terms; mandatory rotation to senior status; predictable appointment schedule; Senate has introduced this in prior sessions)
2. **Democracy Restoration Amendment** (campaign finance regulation; direct reversal of *Citizens United*; restore congressional authority to regulate political spending by artificial entities)
3. **Equal Suffrage Modernization Amendment** (addresses Senate structural reform through the only politically available route: congressional statute plus state ratification of a constitutional amendment authorizing proportional adjustments to Senate voting rules)

None of these proposals is achievable in the 119th Congress. The 120th Congress (beginning January 2027) is the target window. The pre-election work is coalition building, legal development, and public opinion preparation.
