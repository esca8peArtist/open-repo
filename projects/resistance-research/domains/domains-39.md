**Research completed**: May 13, 2026
**Crisis window**: ACUTE — Department of Education effectively dismantled as of March 2026: 118 programs transferred out, 90% of OCR staff fired (30,000 discrimination complaints abandoned), federal voucher program enacted in OBBBA potentially diverting $30-$50B annually from public schools; AAUP and AFT launch national higher education platform ahead of 2026 midterms documenting U.S. university autonomy rated "moderate" with 50% decline since 2015; 23% of 8th graders civics proficient; only 9 states require civics for graduation; Class of 2026 graduating without democratic procedural knowledge needed for 2026 midterm participation

## Executive Summary

Democracy requires an informed citizenry — not as an aspirational ideal but as a structural prerequisite. Citizens who do not understand how legislation becomes law cannot evaluate claims about congressional procedure. Citizens who do not know what the Electoral College is cannot assess its democratic legitimacy. Citizens who cannot distinguish a ballot measure from a legislative act cannot participate meaningfully in the initiative processes that have been the primary avenue for democratic expansion on issues from abortion access (Domain 44) to campaign finance reform. The United States in 2026 has a civics knowledge crisis severe enough to constitute a democratic infrastructure failure: only 23% of 8th graders are proficient in civics, only 9 states require civics education for graduation, and the federal government stopped funding civics education in 2011 — 15 years before the 2026 midterm election in which those 8th graders will be eligible voters.

The Department of Education's functional dismantling in 2025-2026 is not only a policy change — it is a democratic design attack on the institutional infrastructure that equalizes educational opportunity across the inequality gaps that would otherwise produce permanent democratic stratification. The Department's civil rights enforcement infrastructure — the Office for Civil Rights, which receives and resolves discrimination complaints — has been reduced by 90% staff cuts while its complaint backlog reached 30,000 unresolved cases. Title I, which directs federal funding to districts serving high proportions of low-income students, faces distribution paralysis as the Census Bureau staff cuts that are the predicate for the funding formula may prevent accurate distribution for the 2026-27 school year. The first federal private school voucher program, enacted in the One Big Beautiful Bill Act in July 2025, could divert $30-50 billion annually from public education — more than the total federal investment in Title I and special education combined.

The higher education dimension is separately alarming. The American Association of University Professors and the American Federation of Teachers launched a joint national platform in spring 2026 documenting what Inside Higher Ed and the independent Academic Freedom Index confirmed: U.S. university institutional autonomy has declined 50% since 2015, from "high" to "moderate" rating. The federal executive branch has weaponized accreditation threats, funding withdrawal, and direct campus interventions in ways that no prior administration has attempted. Sixty-five percent of university faculty are contingent — adjuncts, lecturers, visitors — with no job security and wages that frequently require public assistance. The $1.7 trillion student debt burden has produced a generation for whom higher education increases economic precarity rather than reducing it, with Black students assuming larger loans and women carrying more debt than men — a debt burden the research consistently identifies as reducing civic participation.

Education policy is democratic design policy. Who gets educated, to what standard, with what civic content, under what institutional governance — these are not merely pedagogical questions. They are the upstream inputs that determine who has the knowledge and civic capacity to participate in democratic institutions, who is recruited into political roles, and whether the democratic system can reproduce itself across generations against the entropy of manufactured ignorance and structural inequality.

## 1. The Civics Knowledge Crisis: Democratic Infrastructure Failure at the Foundation

### 1.1 What Citizens Need to Know — and Don't

Democratic self-governance requires a minimum of procedural knowledge: citizens must be able to identify candidates, understand the offices at stake, distinguish the powers of different branches and levels of government, comprehend ballot language, and assess political claims against a basic factual framework of how government works. The research on civic knowledge in the United States is consistent and alarming.

The Nation's Report Card (National Assessment of Educational Progress) shows that only 23% of 8th graders score at "proficient" or above in civics and government, and only 18% score at proficient or above in US history — levels that have remained essentially flat for 15 years and declined since 2018. Among students in high-poverty schools (which serve disproportionately Black, Hispanic, and Native American students), proficiency rates are approximately half the overall rate. The proficiency-gap maps directly onto the demographic patterns of voter suppression: the students who are least likely to receive quality civics instruction are the same students who will face the most significant structural barriers when they attempt to vote.

The content gaps are specific. The Ashbrook Center's 2024 study found that the majority of students who received civics instruction could not identify the three branches of government, could not explain the difference between a constitutional amendment and a statute, and could not describe the constitutional amendment process. These are not peripheral facts — they are the procedural knowledge required to evaluate political arguments about "constitutional" versus "unconstitutional" executive actions, to understand ballot initiative processes, and to assess claims about judicial independence and Supreme Court legitimacy.

### 1.2 The Structural Cause: Policy Defunding and Displacement

The civics deficit is not a mystery — it has a documented policy origin. In 2011, the federal government halted financial support for civic education. The de-funding corresponded to the Obama administration's Race to the Top prioritization of STEM and standardized testing in reading and mathematics, which structurally displaced social studies and civics in school curriculum decisions driven by accountability metrics. Schools with limited instructional time facing accountability pressures on math and reading scores rationally deprioritized civics. The result: a 15-year period in which the primary institutional mechanism for civic knowledge transmission — the public school — systematically reduced civics instruction while the political environment became more complex and contested.

The state-requirement landscape reveals the structural inadequacy: only 9 states and the District of Columbia require civics education for graduation. Ten states have no civics requirement at all. Twenty-two percent of school districts reported a shortage of in-school staff who can lead civics instruction, alongside funding barriers. The 2026 context sharpens the urgency: the Class of 2026 — students graduating this spring and eligible to vote in the November 2026 midterms — received their middle school civics education (or did not) during the 2019-2022 period in which schools were disrupted by COVID-19, politically pressured to avoid "controversial" topics, and under-funded for non-tested subjects.

The definition of "controversial" has expanded enough to include voter registration itself. Reporting by The 74 Million and High School Democrats of America documents that high school teachers in multiple states have been discouraged or prohibited from assisting student voter registration because administrators or parents characterize it as political advocacy. Twenty-six states have statutes requiring high schools to help students register to vote; in most of these states, implementation is incomplete or nominal. The same political infrastructure that constructs voting barriers for adults is now constructing them preemptively for the next generation of voters while they are still in school.

## 2. The Department of Education's Functional Dismantling

### 2.1 The Administrative Decapitation

On March 20, 2025, President Trump issued an executive order directing the dismantling of the Department of Education. Secretary Linda McMahon described the department's mission as effectively ending — its "final mission" was to shrink itself to the smallest possible size before Congress voted on formal closure. By March 2026, the administration had struck nine interagency agreements transferring 118 programs out of the department and forcing their administration onto agencies without education expertise.

The most consequential structural consequence is the destruction of the Office for Civil Rights. OCR received and resolved civil rights discrimination complaints from students and families — complaints about race discrimination, disability discrimination, sex discrimination (including Title IX), and national origin discrimination. Under the Trump administration, OCR staff were reduced by 90%. The backlog of unresolved complaints reached 30,000 — cases involving students who alleged discrimination and now have no functional avenue for federal redress. The civil rights enforcement infrastructure that enforced Brown v. Board of Education's desegregation mandate, the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act's special education guarantees, and Title IX's sex discrimination prohibitions has been effectively shut down.

The Title I distribution problem is acute for the 2026-27 school year. Title I had a fiscal 2026 budget of $18.4 billion — unchanged from Biden's fiscal 2024 level, meaning a real funding decline after inflation. But the distribution mechanism depends on Census Bureau population data to determine which districts receive what proportions; the Trump administration's Census Bureau staff reductions may prevent accurate Title I distribution. Former DOE employees confirmed to NPR that, after mass layoffs, the department is unlikely to be able to get REAP program money to rural schools or distribute Title I accurately for the next academic year. The funding is nominally available; the institutional capacity to distribute it has been destroyed.

Dept. — Education Week](https://www.edweek.org/policy-politics/opinion-the-trump-administration-has-mostly-dismantled-the-ed-dept-should-you-care/2026/03); [Plan to Abolish Education Department — One Year Later — NEA](https://www.nea.org/nea-today/all-news-articles/plan-abolish-education-department-one-year-later); [How Education Department Cuts Could Hurt Low-Income Schools — NPR](https://www.npr.org/2025/03/21/nx-s1-5330917/trump-schools-education-department-cuts-low-income); [Research Evidence Against Dismantling the Department of Education — Education Law Center](https://edlawcenter.org/research/evidence-against-dismantling-used/)

### 2.2 Federal Voucher Expansion: The Defunding Architecture

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (enacted July 2025) created the nation's first federal private school voucher program, structured as a dollar-for-dollar federal tax credit for donations to "scholarship granting organizations" that fund private school tuition. The program's design allows individuals to donate up to $1,700 annually with full tax credit, creating a mechanism by which federal tax expenditures fund private school tuition without any of the accountability requirements attached to direct public education funding.

The scale projections are alarming: estimates suggest the program could divert $30-50 billion annually from public education in a fully subscribed scenario — a figure that exceeds the entire federal Title I appropriation and special education funding combined. The NEA analysis documents that the federal tax credit structure creates an incentive for high-income donors to redirect tax dollars they would have paid to the federal treasury into private school tuition funds that primarily benefit families who already send their children to private schools. The regressive incidence is documented: in Arizona, where universal education savings account programs have operated since 2022, traditional public school enrollment dropped from 89% of students in 2001-02 to 68% in 2025-26, with the decline concentrated in higher-income, whiter suburban districts whose students moved to private schools using ESA funds.

The segregation effect is the most directly democratic concern. Research from North Carolina documents that the state's voucher program, launched in 2014, has become more racially segregated over time: by 2024-25, white students made up 73% of voucher recipients while Black student enrollment in the program declined to 11%. Brookings's three-wave historical analysis documents that every expansion wave of school vouchers has increased racial and economic segregation in education — a pattern consistent across jurisdictions and time periods. Segregated education produces segregated civic knowledge and civic networks; the democratic consequences of educational re-segregation are documented in a generation-long research literature showing that racially isolated educational experiences reduce adult cross-racial civic cooperation.

As strings attached to federal education funding are cut under block grant arrangements, states can redirect funds toward voucher programs. This is the mechanism by which the OBBBA's education provisions accelerate the defunding cycle: federal accountability requirements are removed, states divert funds toward private-school choice, public schools lose enrollment and thus per-pupil funding, and the cycle of underinvestment deepens.

## 3. Higher Education Under Attack: Autonomy, Precarity, and Debt

### 3.1 The University Autonomy Collapse

The AAUP and AFT launched a joint national higher education platform in spring 2026 documenting what the Academic Freedom Index (a global tracker measuring university institutional independence) confirmed: U.S. university institutional autonomy has declined 50% since 2015. The United States is now rated "moderate" on university autonomy — the same rating as Hungary and lower than most established European democracies. Inside Higher Ed reported in March 2026 that the decline reflects both direct executive-branch interventions and the chilling effect of funding threats.

The specific mechanisms documented by AAUP, Inside Higher Ed, and the American Prospect include: direct campus interventions including threatening to withdraw federal research funding from universities that do not comply with executive orders on DEI, accreditation threats used as leverage over curriculum and faculty hiring decisions, and the defunding of university research programs that produce findings inconvenient to the administration's policy positions. NIH funding cuts of nearly $20.5 billion proposed in the FY 2026 budget — approximately 40% of the NIH's current budget — would devastate university research infrastructure that is simultaneously civic infrastructure: universities produce the researchers, public health experts, environmental scientists, and policy analysts whose work informs democratic deliberation.

The "Peak Higher Ed" analysis by Bryan Alexander (SRHE Blog, April 2026) identifies a convergent crisis: declining enrollment demographics (fewer 18-year-olds due to the 2000s baby bust), rising costs, reduced state funding, increased competition from alternative credentials, and direct political assault have combined to produce the most challenging institutional environment for American higher education since the Great Depression. Institutions under severe financial pressure become vulnerable to donor capture — wealthy donors whose political priorities shape hiring and curriculum decisions at institutions that cannot afford to refuse their gifts.

**Sources**: [Decline in US University Autonomy — Inside Higher Ed](https://www.insidehighered.com/news/faculty/academic-freedom/2026/03/20/decline-us-university-autonomy-index-shows); [AAUP and AFT Launch National Higher Education Platform — AFT](https://www.aft.org/press-release/aaup-and-aft-launch-national-higher-education-platform-ahead-of-2026-midterms); [Universities and the Coming Storm — American Prospect](https://prospect.org/education/2025-01-06-universities-coming-storm-democracy); [Peak Higher Ed — SRHE Blog](https://srheblog.com/2026/04/21/peak-higher-ed-how-to-survive-the-looming-academic-crisis-by-bryan-alexander/)

### 3.2 The Adjunct Crisis: Democratic Governance Hollowed Out

Two-thirds of American university faculty are now contingent workers — adjuncts, lecturers, visiting instructors, and other contingent appointments with no job security, no tenure protection, and wages that frequently require public assistance. National data shows that nearly 25% of adjuncts receive public assistance while 40% struggle to cover basic household expenses. The AAUP documents that real wages for full-time faculty have declined relative to inflation over the past decade, while tuition has increased and administrative staffing has expanded.

The democratic implications are specific and structural. Tenured faculty are the institutional constituency that protects academic freedom, maintains curriculum standards, resists donor pressure, and participates in shared governance — the actual democratic governance of academic institutions. When three-quarters of teaching is conducted by contingent workers who can be non-renewed for any reason and who lack standing in faculty governance, the shared governance model that makes universities democratic institutions collapses. The result is what the RealClearEducation analysis identified in April 2026: "power now lies with a small cadre of administrators, few of whom have any classroom or research experience" — precisely the concentration of institutional power without accountability that democratic design research identifies as the precursor to authoritarian capture in any institution.

Adjunctification is also a curriculum question. Contingent faculty who fear non-renewal for political reasons teach differently than tenured faculty. They avoid controversial topics. They self-censor on election-adjacent subjects. They do not assign readings that might trigger complaints. The aggregate effect on civic education in higher education — where the most significant civics learning for most Americans actually occurs — is a systematic reduction in the quality and breadth of democratic knowledge transmission. If colleges cannot defend democracy because they are not run democratically, as the analysis puts it, then the civic education they provide reflects those governance failures.

**Sources**: [The Real Crisis in Higher Education Isn't Just Ideology, It's Faculty Decline — RealClearEducation](https://www.realcleareducation.com/articles/2026/04/08/the_real_crisis_in_higher_education_isnt_just_ideology-_its_faculty_decline_1175528.html); [Making the Invisible Visible: Organizing Against Adjunctification — AAUP](https://www.aaup.org/sites/default/files/Schirmer_et_al_.pdf); [Restoring the Academic Social Contract — National Affairs](https://www.nationalaffairs.com/publications/detail/restoring-academic-social-contract)

### 3.3 Student Debt as Civic Exclusion

The $1.7 trillion student debt burden has transformed higher education from an economic mobility mechanism into an economic trap for a significant proportion of its participants. The debt burden is distributed unequally along racial and gender lines that map directly onto existing democratic exclusion patterns: Black students assume larger loans and accumulate more costly debt than white borrowers; women carry more student debt than men on average. Black student debtors are more likely to be unable to repay their loans due to post-college income disparities produced by labor market discrimination.

The civic-participation pathway from student debt to democratic disengagement is direct and documented. Economic precarity — defined as unpredictable income, high debt-service burdens, lack of emergency savings, and housing instability — is the single most consistently documented predictor of reduced civic participation in the research literature. Student debt produces economic precarity specifically in the 25-35 age cohort — the demographic whose civic engagement is most formative for lifetime participation patterns. Research on voter registration shows that the period from age 18 to 35 is when people form their voting habits; economic precarity during that period produces lower lifetime voting rates.

The OBBBA's higher education provisions accelerated the crisis: the bill modified income-driven repayment terms in ways that increase long-term repayment obligations for current borrowers, eliminated several Biden-era relief programs, and made changes to PELL Grant eligibility that will reduce access for part-time students — the majority of community college students who are disproportionately first-generation, low-income, and students of color. The political logic is not subtle: the students who are most economically precarious, most likely to depend on PELL and IBR protections, and most likely to be excluded from private school choice programs are also the students whose political interests diverge most sharply from the legislative majority that enacted these provisions.

## 4. Homeschooling, Accreditation Gaps, and Democratic Knowledge

### 4.1 The Accountability Vacuum

The federal voucher program and ESA expansion have accelerated a parallel trend: rapid growth in homeschooling without corresponding accountability mechanisms. As of 2025, 18-19 states have ESA programs serving over 1.2 million students, with many ESA programs providing public dollars to homeschooling families with no curriculum or outcome requirements. Many states without notification or assessment requirements cannot count their homeschooled student populations accurately.

The democratic values dimension requires careful framing. The majority of homeschooling families provide excellent education. The problem is structural: in states without curriculum standards or accountability requirements, there is no mechanism to ensure that students receive the basic civics education — understanding of electoral processes, constitutional rights, and governmental structure — that is a prerequisite for democratic participation. The academic analysis by Pavel and Cynamon (SAGE Journals, 2026) identified the specific limitation: "Even if homeschooling parents are exemplary democratic citizens, that is distinct from having the resources to inculcate democratic habits in others; and the process of educating one's own children in the home is not conducive to acquiring such pedagogical skills."

The intersection with voucher expansion is the key democratic design issue. When states divert public funds to private schools and homeschooling arrangements with no accountability requirements, they are creating publicly funded educational settings that may not produce citizens with the knowledge to participate meaningfully in democratic institutions. This is not a religious freedom argument — it is an educational output argument: a publicly funded educational system should produce citizens who can function as democratic citizens, regardless of the institutional vehicle.

## 5. The Democratic Design Nexus: Education Policy as Participation Architecture

### 5.1 School-Based Voter Registration as the Proof of Concept

The connection between education policy and democratic participation is most directly measurable through school-based voter registration programs. Research by The Civics Center and the Stanford Social Innovation Review documents that school-based voter registration initiatives produce dramatically higher registration rates among young voters than any other outreach mechanism. More than 75% of high school-registered voters turn out in every presidential election going back to 2004 — a rate higher than the overall population average. An 18-year-old registered at school before graduation votes at rates comparable to adults with 20+ years of voting history; an 18-year-old who reaches voting age without a registration completed in school has dramatically lower lifetime participation rates.

Twenty-six states have statutes requiring high schools to help students register to vote — requirements that are under-enforced in most states and actively discouraged in others. The same political dynamic that produced the SAVE Act's documentary proof-of-citizenship requirement for federal voter registration is operating in schools: the definition of "voter registration" as "controversial political activity" is spreading through school board policy in the same states that have enacted most aggressively anti-voter legislation for adults. This is not coincidence — it is a coordinated suppression architecture operating at both ends of the voter lifecycle.

**Sources**: [High School Voter Registration Energizes US Democracy — SSIR](https://ssir.org/articles/entry/high-school-voter-registration-democracy); [Empowering Youth Voters: Invest in High School Registration — The Civics Center](https://www.thecivicscenter.org/blog/empowering-youth-voter-registration); [From Classroom to Polling Booth — The Spring Group](https://www.thespringgroup.org/articles/from-classroom-to-polling-booth-an-analysis-of-mandatory-civic-education-in-schools-on-youth-political-participation)

### 5.2 Education Funding Equity as the Cross-Domain Connection

Education funding in the United States is primarily financed through local property taxes, producing a structural inequality mechanism that converts residential wealth stratification directly into educational opportunity stratification. High-wealth districts spend $2-3x per student more than low-wealth districts; this gap is reinforced by state funding formulas that do not fully compensate for local wealth disparities; and the federal Title I mechanism, which was designed to partially compensate, is now under distribution threat from DOE institutional collapse.

This funding inequality is not race-neutral. Majority-minority school districts receive significantly less state and local funding per pupil than majority-white districts, even when controlling for poverty rates and enrollment size. The EdBuild 2019 analysis — which has not been updated but whose structural conclusions remain valid — found that non-white school districts receive $23 billion less in state and local funding annually than white districts serving the same number of students in poverty. This funding gap produces the civics knowledge gap, the voter registration gap, the educational attainment gap, and ultimately the civic participation gap documented in Domains 1 and 22.

The property-tax funding mechanism is a democratic design choice, not an immutable feature of the landscape. States that fund schools primarily through state revenue rather than local property taxes produce more equitable educational outcomes. The political barrier is real estate: the current funding system converts high property values into high school quality, making high-income homeowners beneficiaries of a system whose reform would redistribute their educational advantage.

## 6. Reform Architecture

### 6.1 Restore and Expand Civics Education

Congress should restore federal funding for civic education — halted in 2011 — with mandatory minimum allocations. The iCivics Act and the Civics Secures Democracy Act (reintroduced in the 119th Congress) would collectively invest $1 billion in civic education over five years, requiring states that accept funding to implement evidence-based civic education curricula. State-level advocacy should target the 10 states with no civics graduation requirement, seeking to convert them to the model of the 9 states that require civics for graduation. School voter registration should be treated as a civic education program, not a political activity; the 26 states with school voter registration statutes should have those statutes enforced through funding incentives and AG enforcement actions.

### 6.2 Restore OCR and Title I Distribution Capacity

Congress should appropriate dedicated funding for OCR reconstruction — not merely restoring the headcount but rebuilding the institutional knowledge and case management infrastructure. The 30,000 pending discrimination complaints represent a legal and moral obligation that cannot be addressed by the remaining staff. A Congressional mandate requiring the DOE to clear complaint backlogs within specified timelines, with GAO reporting on compliance, provides an accountability mechanism. Title I distribution should be shifted to a formula that does not depend on annual Census Bureau discretionary calculations, using a multi-year averaging approach that is less vulnerable to Census staff reductions.

### 6.3 Voucher Accountability Requirements

Federal funds flowing through voucher programs — including the OBBBA federal tax credit program — should require participating schools to provide evidence of learning outcomes comparable to public school standards, to accept all students without discrimination, to comply with civil rights laws applicable to public schools, and to provide the minimum civics education required by the state in which they operate. These accountability requirements are not constitutionally barred — they are conditions on the receipt of public funds, not regulation of private education generally.

### 6.4 Higher Education Stabilization

The student debt crisis requires legislative relief: expansion of Public Service Loan Forgiveness, income-based repayment restoration, and Pell Grant expansion to cover part-time students. University autonomy requires federal statutory protection for academic freedom as a condition of federal research funding — a "research independence" statutory provision that bars executive interference with research program direction as a condition of NSF and NIH grants. Adjunct faculty protections — minimum pay floors, benefit eligibility, and shared governance participation rights — require either federal legislation or comprehensive collective bargaining through NLRA reform.

## 7. Cross-Domain Connections

Education as democratic infrastructure connects most directly to Domain 43 (Epistemic Infrastructure): a citizenry without civic procedural knowledge is maximally vulnerable to disinformation specifically because it cannot evaluate political claims against a factual framework of how government actually works. The civics gap and the disinformation crisis are not parallel problems — they are the same problem viewed from different angles. Every percentage point improvement in civics proficiency reduces the population that is vulnerable to claims that "Congress controls the president" or "executive orders are permanent."

The connection to Domain 33 (State Legislative Autocratization) runs through curriculum control: the same state legislative majorities that have enacted restrictive voting laws, attacked ballot initiative processes, and gerrymandered districts have also enacted curriculum restrictions, teacher speech prohibitions, and library censorship statutes. The curriculum-control dimension of autocratization is an attack on democratic infrastructure at its source — if future citizens cannot learn about the political mechanisms through which they might challenge current majorities, those majorities become structurally self-entrenching.

The connection to Domain 22 (Racial Justice) runs through the education funding equity mechanism: the property-tax funding model is not race-neutral, and the voucher expansion accelerating re-segregation is not educationally neutral. Both produce the same outcome — lower civic knowledge, lower voter registration, lower political participation — in the same communities whose political power would otherwise be sufficient to change the policies responsible for the educational inequality.
