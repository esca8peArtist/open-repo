## Executive Summary

Fiscal policy is the material substrate of democratic governance. Without reliable revenue collection, independent budget institutions, and enforceable spending rules, all other institutional safeguards — independent courts, free elections, watchdog agencies — become formally intact but operationally hollow. A government that controls who pays taxes and how public money flows controls the levers of institutional survival and collapse.

The United States in 2025-2026 faces an acute version of a problem that has recurred throughout Latin American democratic history: an executive willing to treat fiscal architecture as a political instrument. The Trump administration has deployed IRS enforcement cuts, Treasury payment system infiltration, OMB apportionment manipulation, and GAO defunding to construct a fiscal order in which executive priorities supplant congressional appropriations. The post-*Loper Bright* judicial landscape simultaneously strips Treasury and IRS regulations of deference, opening every revenue rule to litigation challenge — a structural vulnerability that coincides with the enforcement agency's operational collapse.

The international case studies examined in this report yield a counterintuitive central finding: the most durable fiscal recovery mechanisms are not the most aggressive ones. Brazil's central bank autonomy, institutionalized through supermajority-protected staggered terms, survived a president who openly attacked it. Argentina's prohibition on central bank monetary financing of the government, embedded in IMF program conditionality, proved more durable than any single politician's commitment. Peru's pre-existing fiscal rules and Finance Ministry institutional culture maintained macroeconomic stability through multiple leadership collapses. Mexico's judicial election experiment demonstrates in real time what happens when fiscal enforcement courts are removed from professional insulation and exposed to political capture — with a measurable loss of investment, predictability, and rule of law.

**Key finding**: Self-enforcing fiscal mechanisms — staggered terms with removal protections, mandatory automaticity, international treaty commitments, transparent scoring requirements — significantly outperform mechanisms that depend on sustained political will. For the United States, the reform priority is institutional architecture, not individual statute: the IRS needs statutory independence comparable to the Federal Reserve and the Tax Court; GAO needs constitutional appropriations protection; the ICA needs standing reform that does not route enforcement through the same DOJ that reports to the president. The international precedents show that these reforms are achievable, effective, and durable — but only when enacted before capture is complete.

## 1. US Fiscal Architecture Vulnerabilities: The Post-*Loper Bright* Landscape

### 1.1 The Three-Layer Structural Crisis

The United States fiscal system in 2025-2026 faces simultaneous vulnerabilities at three institutional layers, each of which interacts with and compounds the others.

**Layer 1: Revenue enforcement collapse.** The IRS has lost more than 11,000 employees — approximately 11 percent of its total workforce — through DOGE-directed reductions since January 2025. Among specialized enforcement personnel, the damage is more severe: 3,623 revenue agents (31 percent of the audit corps) have departed. Criminal investigation referrals for abusive tax schemes dropped 63 percent year-over-year to a level roughly 40 percent below any year in the prior decade. The structural consequence is a $700 billion annual tax gap — the amount of taxes legally owed but unpaid — that is highly responsive to IRS enforcement capacity. Yale Budget Lab analysis projects that DOGE-directed IRS cuts will add approximately $861 billion to the federal deficit over ten years: $263 billion attributable to a $20 billion budget reduction and an additional $600 billion from the projected loss of 28,000 staff. The administration's stated rationale — efficiency and eliminating partisan bias — inverts the evidence: $1 spent auditing the highest earners yields $12 in revenue returns, making the IRS among the highest-ROI agencies in the federal government.

**Layer 2: Treasury payment system politicization.** DOGE personnel gained access to the Bureau of the Fiscal Service's payment processing systems in January 2025, raising the prospect of a presidential chokepoint on congressionally appropriated disbursements. Federal Judge Jeannette Vargas in the Southern District of New York found the access was "rushed and undertaken under political pressure" and issued a temporary restraining order; the court later extended the block. The political logic of Treasury system access is distinct from and more dangerous than apportionment manipulation: where OMB can delay obligations, Treasury payment system control could in theory block disbursements after obligation — a complete circumvention of the Appropriations Clause's operation. The traditional insulation of the Bureau of the Fiscal Service from political direction is a norm, not a statute; it has no equivalent of the Federal Reserve's independent governance structure.

**Layer 3: Regulatory authority destabilization through *Loper Bright*.** The Supreme Court's June 2024 decision in *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo* overruled *Chevron* deference and requires courts to exercise independent judgment on agency statutory interpretations. For Treasury and IRS, this means that every regulation relying on general rulemaking authority under Internal Revenue Code § 7805(a) — the provision authorizing Treasury to prescribe rules and regulations to enforce the tax code — is now subject to de novo judicial review without deference to the agency's interpretation. Post-*Loper Bright*, challenges to transfer pricing rules, tax credit regulations, anti-abuse provisions, and international tax frameworks are expected to multiply. The destabilization arrives precisely when IRS enforcement capacity to litigate and defend those rules has been cut by a third. The asymmetry is structural: Congress delegated rulemaking to Treasury in broad terms; the courts now require specific statutory authority for each substantive rule; and the enforcement agency lacks the personnel to defend the rules it has promulgated.

### 1.2 The Watchdog Dismantlement

The oversight infrastructure that makes fiscal enforcement legible — GAO, CBO, Treasury Inspectors General, and the IRS's own internal watchdog (TIGTA) — has been targeted in parallel.

The House Republican FY2026 Legislative Branch Appropriations bill proposed cutting GAO's budget by 50 percent, from $828 million to $415 million, which would eliminate more than 2,200 expert staff and cancel all 39 ongoing impoundment investigations. The bill also included a provision (H.R. 4229) requiring a concurrent congressional resolution — effectively a supermajority-level threshold — before GAO could pursue civil ICA enforcement, operationally neutering the only agency with standing to force obligation of withheld funds. Senate appropriators rejected the House proposal, approving $812 million — essentially flat funding — but the House position establishes the political direction. In 2024 alone, GAO identified $67.5 billion in efficiency recommendations, returning approximately $76 for every $1 invested.

The Library of Congress's Congressional Research Service, which provides nonpartisan legal and policy analysis to Congress, faced proposed budget reductions in the same legislative branch bill. CBO has maintained its independence through the current crisis, but its statutory mandate requires presidential cooperation to obtain budget execution data — a dependency the administration has exploited by declining to provide the apportionment data that CBO and GAO need to complete reviews.

### 1.3 The *Loper Bright*-Enforcement Nexus

The post-*Loper Bright* landscape creates a specific vulnerability at the intersection of judicial review and enforcement capacity. Before *Loper Bright*, Treasury regulations received Chevron deference, meaning courts upheld reasonable agency interpretations of ambiguous tax provisions. Tax practitioners could plan around regulatory certainty. After *Loper Bright*, courts must independently determine the "best reading" of tax statutes, creating unpredictability at the regulatory level.

The enforcement implication is compounded: the IRS must now litigate regulatory validity (not just individual taxpayer disputes) with a 31-percent-depleted audit corps and reduced tax court litigation staff. Large-dollar taxpayers — corporations and high-net-worth individuals with the resources to mount regulatory challenges — are the primary beneficiaries. The tax gap analysis conducted by IRS researchers found that underreporting by corporations and high-income individuals is the dominant driver of the $700 billion gap; those are precisely the taxpayers most able to fund post-*Loper Bright* regulatory challenges. The structural consequence is a fiscal system where enforcement applies disproportionately to wage earners (whose taxes are withheld at source) while the most evasion-prone taxpayers gain a judicial lever against the rules that constrain them.

## 2. International Case Studies

### 2.1 Brazil (2022-2024): Central Bank Autonomy as a Self-Enforcing Institutional Shield

Brazil's 2021-2024 experience provides the most directly applicable model of institutional design for the United States: a formal legal structure that protected a critical fiscal institution from a hostile executive through self-enforcing procedural rules.

**The institutional design.** Complementary Law No. 179 of February 24, 2021 granted the Central Bank of Brazil "technical, operational, administrative, and financial autonomy" with no hierarchical subordination to any ministry. The critical design feature is the staggered appointment structure: the bank president and board directors serve fixed four-year terms, staggered so that any incoming president can only appoint a majority of the board in their *third year* in office — specifically designed to prevent a newly elected president from immediately installing loyalists. Removal is restricted to resignation, incapacitating illness, final criminal conviction for administrative impropriety, or proven and recurring failure to perform duties. The latter category requires a proposal from the National Monetary Council *and* approval by an absolute majority of the Federal Senate — a supermajority procedural barrier.

**The political test.** When Lula da Silva won the October 2022 election, he publicly and repeatedly criticized the central bank's high interest rates, calling then-Governor Roberto Campos Neto a "political operative" and questioning whether the bank's policy reflected partisan bias rather than monetary analysis. The attacks were sustained through the first year of Lula's administration. Finance Minister Fernando Haddad, however, consistently upheld the autonomy law in public statements — Brazil's real gained significantly when Haddad reiterated on February 8, 2023, that the administration would not seek to curtail bank independence. The bank held its interest rates at 13.75 percent throughout 2023 despite executive pressure, ultimately reducing them only when macroeconomic indicators justified doing so.

**The constitutional validation.** Brazil's Supreme Federal Court (STF) upheld the autonomy law's constitutionality in an 8-2 decision, rejecting left-opposition challenges that the law had been enacted improperly and that it undermined executive authority. The STF ruling closed the judicial challenge pathway, leaving only legislative amendment as the mechanism for reversal — a threshold that the Lula administration chose not to pursue despite its majority coalition.

**New Fiscal Framework (NFR) — August 2023.** Alongside central bank independence, Lula's finance team constructed a new fiscal anchor to replace Bolsonaro-era spending caps. The framework, approved by Congress in fewer than five months with 379 votes in favor and 64 against, anchors spending growth to revenue growth: real federal expenditures may grow within a corridor of 0.6 percent to 2.5 percent, with actual permitted growth tied to whether primary surplus targets are met. The framework's primary balance targets — from -0.5 percent of GDP in 2023 to +1.0 percent in 2026 — were endorsed by the IMF, which "strongly supported" the approach while recommending a more ambitious longer-term path.

**The vulnerability pattern.** By late 2024, however, the NFR showed signs of stress. The Lula government's "lax fiscal policy and refusal to address structural spending issues" — including pressure to exempt social programs from spending ceilings — began to undermine market confidence in the framework's durability. By December 2024, analysts documented the fiscal framework "in peril." The lesson is critical: *procedural self-enforcement* (staggered central bank terms with removal protection) proved more durable than *discretionary fiscal targets* (primary balance goals with political pressure for exceptions). Self-enforcing mechanisms outlasted politically dependent ones within the same administration.

**Lessons for the US**: The Federal Reserve's independence is secured by a similar staggered-term, cause-only-removal design. The IRS Commissioner's office has a five-year term by statute but no equivalent of the BCB's supermajority removal protection, no Senate majority-approval requirement for dismissal, and no prohibition on hierarchical subordination to Treasury. A structural upgrade modeled on the BCB design — staggered terms, supermajority removal protection, explicit prohibition on political direction — would create a self-enforcing protection analogous to what Brazil demonstrated could survive sustained executive hostility.

### 2.2 Argentina (2023-2026): Fiscal Discipline Without Institutional Erosion — and Its Limits

Argentina presents the reverse lesson: what happens when fiscal adjustment is achieved through concentrated executive power rather than institutional self-enforcement, and what that means for democratic durability.

**The fiscal consolidation.** Javier Milei took office in December 2023 with a mandate to address a fiscal deficit of 5 percent of GDP, inflation running above 200 percent annually, and a central bank with negative net international reserves of approximately $7 billion. His "shock therapy" approach achieved one of the most rapid fiscal adjustments in modern economic history: a primary surplus of 1.8 percent of GDP within 12 months, requiring a reduction in primary expenditure of approximately 35 percent in real terms in the first quarter alone. Specific mechanisms included: subsidy elimination for energy and transport, public sector workforce reductions, capital expenditure deferrals, and a freeze on transfers to provinces.

**The IMF structural anchor.** The critical self-enforcing element was not domestic legislation but IMF program conditionality. Argentina's $20 billion IMF program (approved 2024, extended through subsequent reviews) includes a prohibition on central bank (BCRA) monetary financing of the government — reversing the previous pattern in which the BCRA financed the Treasury for approximately ARS50 trillion in the twelve months before Milei took office. By the end of the first quarter of 2024, BCRA net monetary financing to the Treasury stood at negative ARS2.1 trillion — a complete reversal achieved through IMF-enforced conditionality rather than domestic institutional commitment. The Ley de Bases (July 2024) institutionalized key deregulation and fiscal measures, with the IMF-endorsed prohibition on monetary financing embedded as a condition of continued program access.

**The institutional design flaw.** The Milei approach achieved fiscal discipline *without* building the independent institutions that would make that discipline self-sustaining. Argentina's central bank remains legally and practically subordinate to the executive; the "zero deficit" policy is a presidential commitment, not an institutional constraint. If Milei loses the 2027 elections to a coalition favoring deficit spending, there is no BCB-style procedural barrier preventing a new president from reversing the fiscal adjustment through executive direction. Monetary policy in Argentina "has long been subordinated to fiscal needs," and the IMF acknowledged that "two conditions must be met for a monetary regime to function: technical consistency and political and institutional credibility" — the latter "lacking in Argentina more than in other countries in the region."

**The comparative takeaway.** Argentina's adjustment demonstrates that self-enforcing mechanisms (IMF conditionality, international treaty commitments) can deliver fiscal outcomes that domestic political commitment cannot sustain. But external conditionality is inherently time-limited — programs expire, and the underlying institutional deficits remain. The Brazil model (domestic supermajority-protected institution) produces more durable results than the Argentina model (external conditionality plus executive will), even if the Argentina model can deliver faster initial adjustment.

**Lessons for the US**: The US has no equivalent of IMF conditionality — the reserve currency issuer cannot be conditioned by the international lender of last resort. But the structural lesson holds: fiscal rules backed by external enforcement mechanisms (automatic sequestration, statutory spending caps with mandatory triggers) are more durable than executive commitments to fiscal discipline. The Fiscal Responsibility Act of 2023's sequestration-enforced caps for FY2024-2025 illustrate the design logic, even if the administration's subsequent circumvention of those caps through apportionment manipulation undermines the comparison.

### 2.3 Peru (2022-2024): Fiscal Institutional Culture as a Continuity Asset

Peru's experience with Pedro Castillo's December 2022 self-coup attempt and removal offers a distinct model: what happens when fiscal institutions are strong before a constitutional crisis, and how that pre-existing strength enables rapid stabilization.

**The leadership collapse and institutional continuity.** When Pedro Castillo attempted to dissolve Congress on December 7, 2022 — triggering his impeachment, arrest, and replacement by Vice President Dina Boluarte within hours — Peru's fiscal institutions did not experience a parallel collapse. The Ministry of Economy and Finance (MEF), the central bank (BCRP), and the tax authority (SUNAT) continued operating under their existing mandates. All three major credit agencies had downgraded Peru's sovereign credit during Castillo's turbulent 16-month presidency, citing political instability — but the actual fiscal framework remained intact because it was institutionally anchored rather than personally dependent on Castillo's administration.

**The fiscal rule structure.** Peru operates under a Fiscal Responsibility and Transparency Law framework that sets deficit targets for the non-financial public sector. The fiscal deficit reached 2.8 percent of GDP in 2023, above the fiscal rule target of 2.4 percent — attributable to a revenue shortfall driven by economic slowdown rather than institutional breakdown. For 2024, MEF set a target of 2.0 percent of GDP, consistent with fiscal rule requirements. IMF Executive Directors in the 2024 Article IV consultation "commended the Peruvian authorities for their sustained track record of very strong macroeconomic policies and institutional frameworks" — praising Peru's "low public debt, abundant international reserves, a robust financial sector, and favorable access to international capital markets" as "strong buffers against adverse shocks." Peru's institutions were strong enough that the IMF allowed Peru to exit its Flexible Credit Line arrangement upon expiry in May 2024, finding risks broadly balanced.

**The SUNAT tax administration constraint.** The weak point in Peru's post-Castillo fiscal recovery is not macroeconomic management but tax administration capacity. In 2021, MEF estimated tax evasion and avoidance at 8 percent of GDP — a structural revenue gap that fiscal rules cannot address without enforcement improvement. SUNAT faces specific institutional constraints: the civil service management system (SERVIR) limits SUNAT's ability to attract and retain specialized tax enforcement talent. Tax revenues declined 6.7 percent in 2023, with income tax falling 10.2 percent and production/consumption taxes falling 4.6 percent — partly cyclical but partly structural, reflecting limited enforcement capacity rather than changed tax incidence.

**The transparency dimension.** Peru's fiscal framework survived Castillo because of what preceded him: decades of macroeconomic management credibility built through consistent inflation targeting, debt management, and fiscal transparency that the MEF and BCRP had established independently of any particular president. The Boluarte administration's signal — "commitment to preserve Peru's very strong policy frameworks" — was credible to markets and the IMF precisely because those frameworks had demonstrated independence from the executive before the crisis.

**The negative institutional trajectory.** The post-Castillo period has not been uniformly positive for Peruvian institutions. Congress moved to restrict government oversight mechanisms and interfere with judicial independence after 2022. Freedom House downgraded Peru from "free" to "partly free." The Constitutional Tribunal approved a resolution allowing Congress to put officials from Peru's electoral court on trial before the legislature — the kind of institutional backsliding that fiscal stability cannot prevent if judicial independence and oversight mechanisms erode in parallel. The lesson is that strong fiscal institutions can maintain macroeconomic stability through executive collapse, but they cannot substitute for the broader institutional matrix of judicial independence, anti-corruption enforcement, and electoral integrity.

**Lessons for the US**: Peru demonstrates that fiscal institutional culture — pre-existing norms of MEF and central bank independence — can survive leadership collapse better than fiscal outcomes built on personal political commitment. The US equivalent is the CBO's nonpartisan scoring culture, the Federal Reserve's established independence norms, and the tax courts' Article I independence. These institutional cultures need statutory protection commensurate with their functional importance — Peru's fiscal institutions survived Castillo partly because their professional identity was strong, but they remain vulnerable to the kind of legislative encroachment the Peruvian Congress has pursued post-crisis.

### 2.4 Mexico (2024-2025): The Judicial-Fiscal Feedback Loop as a Cautionary Case

Mexico's 2024 judicial election reform is the most directly cautionary case study in this report: it demonstrates in real time how the destruction of judicial independence creates measurable fiscal consequences through the enforcement channel.

**The judicial reform architecture.** Mexico's September 2024 constitutional reform — approved by a supermajority coalition in both chambers — fundamentally restructured the judiciary. All federal judges, magistrates, and justices (approximately 7,000 positions) are now elected by popular vote. The Supreme Court was reduced from 11 to 9 justices, terms were shortened from 15 to 12 years, and a new Judicial Disciplinary Tribunal was established to manage judicial resources and discipline. Mexico became the only country in the world to elect its entire judiciary through universal suffrage. The first round of judicial elections occurred June 1, 2025; civil society investigations identified candidates with credible links to convicted drug traffickers who subsequently won office.

**The fiscal enforcement consequences.** The connection to fiscal architecture is direct and documented. Mexico's tax enforcement system — SAT (Servicio de Administración Tributaria) — operates through the Federal Administrative Justice Tribunal (TFJA) and the amparo proceeding system, both of which depend on judicial independence for their credibility with taxpayers and investors. The 2025 Amparo Law reform (October 2025 Federal Official Gazette) restricted amparo proceedings in tax matters — limiting when taxpayers can challenge tax assessments to after auction notice rather than at earlier stages — while simultaneously transferring criminal enforcement authority to the SAT/SHCP by requiring them to bring criminal action in money laundering cases.

The market consequence of judicial independence erosion was immediate. Morgan Stanley downgraded Mexico to underweight in August 2024, citing "heightened risk and a negative investment outlook" linked directly to legal uncertainty. The CSIS analysis documented how the "politicized judiciary with less expertise in commercial matters has rattled investors and fueled a crisis of confidence in the ability of Mexico's legal system to protect investment." By year-end 2025, the SAT had 203,221 disputed tax assessments subject to legal challenges — totaling more than two trillion pesos — a stock of contested assessments that reflects both the scale of enforcement activity and the legal uncertainty investors face in any dispute with the tax authority.

**The feedback loop mechanism.** The analytical insight from Mexico is the *feedback loop* between judicial independence and fiscal enforcement effectiveness. It operates through four channels:

First, the *predictability channel*: investors and firms make investment decisions partly based on confidence that tax obligations are calculable in advance and that disputes will be resolved by professional, rule-bound courts. Elected judges exposed to political influence from parties and, potentially, organized crime create unpredictability that raises the effective cost of investment and reduces the tax base.

Second, the *collection channel*: a tax authority's ability to collect disputed assessments depends on judicial willingness to enforce those assessments through the amparo and enforcement proceedings. If elected judges facing political pressure rule systematically in favor of well-connected taxpayers, collection efficiency falls.

Third, the *deterrence channel*: criminal tax enforcement (money laundering, tax evasion prosecutions) requires judicial willingness to convict. If prosecutors and judges are politically exposed, the deterrent value of criminal tax enforcement collapses — precisely the dynamic the International Bar Association warned about in its analysis of Mexico's reform.

Fourth, the *investment-base channel*: the broader tax base depends on economic activity, which depends on rule of law for investment. CSIS documented capital flight risk; Morgan Stanley's downgrade made foreign capital more expensive. A smaller, less formalized economy generates less taxable revenue regardless of tax rates.

**Lessons for the US**: The US Tax Court is already a constitutionally independent Article I court. But its independence — and the independence of district courts reviewing IRS assessments — depends on the broader judicial independence architecture that is under assault through the administration's judge removal campaigns and circuit court packing strategies documented in Domain 6 of this framework. The Mexico case provides the empirical baseline: judicial independence loss translates within 12-18 months into measurable fiscal consequences through each of the four channels above. The US version of this feedback loop would operate through exactly the same mechanism, with higher aggregate impact given the size of the US economy.

## 3. Institutional Integration Mechanisms

### 3.1 Judicial Independence and Tax Enforcement: The Four-Channel Model

The international case studies support a four-channel model of how judicial independence enables or constrains fiscal enforcement. This model has direct implications for US institutional design.

**Predictability channel**: Article I courts (Tax Court) and Article III courts (district courts reviewing IRS assessments, circuit courts reviewing Treasury regulations) need insulation from political pressure to function as predictable interpreters of tax law. Post-*Loper Bright*, the courts must exercise independent judgment on Treasury regulations rather than deferring to agency expertise — making judicial professional quality *more* important than under Chevron, not less. Courts staffed through an appointment process insulated from immediate political cycles (lifetime tenure for Article III, fixed terms with cause-only removal for Article I) provide the predictability channel that enables investment and voluntary compliance.

**Collection channel**: The DOJ Tax Division, which litigates on behalf of the IRS in district courts and the courts of appeal, depends on a DOJ that operates through rule-of-law norms rather than political direction. Domain 29 of this framework documents DOJ capture as a standing crisis. The Tax Division's independence from politically directed prosecution decisions — its capacity to litigate against wealthy taxpayers connected to the administration without interference — is a structural precondition for collection channel effectiveness. As of 2025-2026, investigations into wealthy taxpayer abuses have dropped 63 percent, consistent with both enforcement staff reductions and the possibility of political direction affecting audit priorities.

**Deterrence channel**: Criminal tax enforcement (31 USC § 7201 tax evasion, Title 18 money laundering charges) requires a Grand Jury, a federal prosecutor, and a district court judge. The deterrence channel is directly vulnerable to DOJ politicization: a DOJ that pursues criminal tax cases based on political profile rather than evidence destroys the uniform enforcement norm that underlies voluntary compliance. The IRS Restructuring and Reform Act of 1998 created the Taxpayer Advocate Service and imposed due process constraints specifically because earlier partisan enforcement abuses had created public distrust. The current dynamics — selective enforcement, audit priority shifts, DOGE interference with taxpayer data systems — reactivate the conditions that generated those reforms.

**Investment-base channel**: The domestic equivalent of Mexico's investment-base feedback loop operates through economic confidence: firms deciding whether to invest in the United States factor in the rule of law for contracts and tax stability. The post-2025 evidence — Morgan Stanley and other institutional investors incorporating US rule-of-law risk into sovereign analysis for the first time — suggests the investment-base channel is activating. A 10 percent reduction in the effective corporate tax base through avoidance and evasion enabled by enforcement deterioration costs more in revenue than any spending efficiency the administration has claimed.

### 3.2 Transparency Requirements and Audit Bureau Independence: The Sequestration Model

The second institutional integration point is between fiscal transparency requirements — OMB scorecards, CBO baselines, GAO audits, IG reports — and the independence of the institutions producing that transparency.

The international best-practice framework is INTOSAI's (International Organization of Supreme Audit Institutions) eight-pillar independence model, codified in the Lima Declaration (1977) and Mexico Declaration (2007). The pillars include: an independent legal framework, broad audit mandate, unrestricted access to information, security of tenure for auditors, and the right to report findings publicly. The World Bank's InSAI index, developed to monitor SAI independence annually, provides a comparative baseline: the US GAO scores highly on legal framework and mandate, but the proposed 50 percent funding cut and H.R. 4229's enforcement restrictions would represent one of the most severe SAI attacks documented in OECD member states.

The transparency-independence feedback is self-reinforcing in both directions. When GAO can conduct its impoundment investigations without political interference, it produces findings that enable congressional enforcement, which deters executive overreach, which reduces the need for GAO investigations. When GAO is defunded and its enforcement authority is veto-conditioned on congressional concurrent resolution, the enforcement cycle breaks: violations occur, GAO cannot document them, Congress cannot enforce against them, and the pattern escalates.

The OECD's framework for independent fiscal institutions identifies three design elements that make fiscal transparency self-enforcing rather than politically dependent: (1) a clear mandate in primary legislation (not executive order, which can be reversed); (2) resources secured through a dedicated appropriation protected from the appropriations process the institution monitors (the "oversight paradox" — an agency that monitors spending should not have its own budget controlled by the entity it monitors); and (3) transparency requirements backed by publication duties, so that findings are legally required to be made public before any political decision to suppress them can be effective.

None of these three design protections currently exist for GAO in their robust form. GAO is funded through the annual Legislative Branch appropriations bill, which is subject to the same political dynamics as any appropriations process. Its enforcement authority requires civil action through the Comptroller General — a mechanism that H.R. 4229 would effectively require congressional authorization for. Its publication of findings is protected by convention but not by a mandatory publication timeline enforceable against congressional suppression.

### 3.3 Congressional Budget Authority and Executive Fiscal Constraints: Statutory Levers Post-*Loper Bright*

The third integration point addresses the relationship between Congress's constitutional fiscal authority and the statutory mechanisms through which that authority can be enforced in the post-*Loper Bright* environment.

*Loper Bright* creates an important but underappreciated opportunity for congressional fiscal reassertion: because courts no longer defer to executive interpretations of ambiguous statutes, more specific statutory language constraining OMB apportionment authority, Treasury payment procedures, and IRS enforcement mandates would carry more enforceable weight. Under Chevron, Congress could delegate in general terms and trust that courts would defer to agency interpretations. Post-*Loper Bright*, if Congress wants to prohibit OMB from using Category C apportionments to withhold funds for policy reasons, it needs to say so specifically — but if it does say so specifically, courts are now more likely to enforce that language against executive departure.

The opportunity is to use *Loper Bright*'s requirement for independent statutory interpretation as a lever for drafting more specific, enforceable appropriations statutes. The reforms proposed in Section 5 below are designed to be specific enough that courts can enforce them without deference questions arising — meeting the "best reading" standard that *Loper Bright* requires.

The standing problem identified in *Raines v. Byrd* — congressional members' inability to litigate institutional injuries — requires a different solution: statutory conferral of standing on specific officers (the Comptroller General, the Congressional Budget Office Director, or a congressional enforcement officer) rather than trying to route enforcement through individual members. Several post-Nixon proposals created standing for institutional officers; the post-*Loper Bright* opportunity is to draft those provisions with enough specificity that courts can evaluate the statutory claim without deference.

## 4. Self-Enforcing vs. Constituency-Dependent Mechanisms: A Taxonomy

The core analytical contribution of this research is a taxonomy that distinguishes mechanisms that do not require sustained political will from those that do. This distinction is crucial for reform design: in a political environment where the executive is actively hostile to fiscal accountability, constituency-dependent mechanisms will fail; self-enforcing mechanisms will hold.

### 4.1 Self-Enforcing Mechanisms

These mechanisms operate through procedural requirements, automatic triggers, or structural barriers that do not require sustained political commitment to function:

**Staggered-term appointment structures with supermajority removal protection.** As demonstrated by Brazil's BCB (and the US Federal Reserve), requiring supermajority Senate approval to remove an institutional leader — and staggering terms so no single president can immediately dominate — creates a procedural barrier that survives political pressure. The BCB survived Lula's sustained public attacks precisely because the removal mechanism required a National Monetary Council proposal plus Senate majority approval, thresholds Lula chose not to attempt.

**Automatic sequestration triggers.** The Budget Enforcement Act of 1990's PAYGO mechanism required automatic sequestration when new legislation increased the deficit. The 1990 agreement, combined with the 1993 Budget Act and 1997 Balanced Budget Act, produced the first federal surpluses since the 1960s. The Fiscal Responsibility Act of 2023 uses sequestration enforcement for its FY2024-2025 discretionary caps. The self-enforcing quality is the automaticity: violation of the cap triggers cuts without requiring a new legislative vote.

**Mandatory publication timelines for audit findings.** If GAO's impoundment reports are required by statute to be published within 30 days of completion, with a mandatory transmittal to the Federal Register, no executive action can suppress them before they become public record. Publication automaticity is cheaper to design than enforcement mechanisms and more resistant to executive suppression.

**International treaty and program commitments.** As demonstrated by Argentina's IMF-conditioned prohibition on BCRA monetary financing, external commitments can function as self-enforcing constraints that survive individual political transitions. For the United States, the OECD's fiscal governance principles — while not binding treaties — create reputational enforcement that operates through financial market monitoring. The WTO's budget transparency requirements, the IMF's Article IV consultation process (which reviews US fiscal policy annually), and G7 fiscal accountability frameworks all provide reputational enforcement that does not depend on domestic political will.

**Statutory scoring requirements.** CBO's mandate to produce official cost estimates for all major legislation before floor votes is a self-enforcing transparency mechanism: bills cannot receive a final vote without a CBO score (under current House and Senate rules). The self-enforcing quality is procedural — rules violations require an affirmative waiver, creating political visibility for any attempt to circumvent the scoring requirement.

### 4.2 Constituency-Dependent Mechanisms

These mechanisms can work when political will is sustained, but fail when it is not:

**Executive commitment to fiscal discipline.** As demonstrated by the Brazil NFR's weakening under political pressure to exempt social programs, executive fiscal commitments erode when they conflict with coalition maintenance. Milei's zero-deficit policy is the clearest current example: successful while politically dominant, but lacking institutional self-enforcement if political support shifts.

**Voluntary OMB compliance with ICA.** The ICA depends on executive branch good-faith compliance — transmitting rescission messages within 45 days, providing apportionment data to GAO, not using Category C for policy rather than timing purposes. These are legally required, but the criminal enforcement mechanism (ADA willful violation) has never been invoked. Mechanisms that depend on an executive's willingness to be held accountable are constituency-dependent.

**Informal norms of agency independence.** The BFS payment system's insulation from political direction, the DOJ Tax Division's independent case selection, the IRS Commissioner's de facto independence from Treasury's political direction — these are norm-dependent mechanisms that DOGE demonstrated could be overridden through personnel action and access grants in days. Statutory conversion is required to make them self-enforcing.

**Aggressive prosecution as deterrence.** Criminal prosecution of willful ADA violations, contempt enforcement against agencies that defy GAO, and criminal referrals for TIGTA findings all require a DOJ willing to prosecute executive branch officials. As demonstrated by DOJ capture (Domain 29), constituency-dependent enforcement through the executive's own prosecutors fails when the executive controls the prosecutors.

## 5. US Statutory Reform Pathways

### 5.1 Immediate Priority: IRS Statutory Independence Act (0-18 months)

**Problem**: The IRS Commissioner serves a five-year term but can be removed by the President without cause, without Senate involvement, and without any procedural barrier. Treasury political appointees can direct IRS enforcement priorities. DOGE demonstrated that IRS data systems can be accessed by politically connected external actors without clear statutory prohibition.

**Proposed mechanism**: An IRS Independence Act modeled on BCB Complementary Law 179 would provide:
- Fixed staggered terms for the Commissioner (5 years), Deputy Commissioner, and the six statutory Division Commissioners, staggered such that a new President cannot control a majority of division leadership until their third year
- Removal only for cause (incapacitating illness, criminal conviction for misconduct, documented and proven failure to perform statutory duties), requiring OPM certification of cause, written notice to both Judiciary Committees, and 60-day congressional review
- Prohibition on Treasury political appointee direction of audit selection, case referral, or enforcement priority for identified taxpayers
- Mandatory IRS Inspector General (TIGTA) reporting to Congress within 30 days of documented political interference, with publication requirement in the Federal Register
- Payment systems access limited by statute to Treasury career civil servants and IRS personnel; external access prohibited absent a formal congressional authorization

**Self-enforcing elements**: Staggered terms with cause-only removal create a procedural barrier. TIGTA mandatory reporting with Federal Register publication creates automatic transparency that cannot be suppressed.

**Legislative vehicle**: Could be incorporated into the IRS Restructuring and Reform Act (existing statutory home) or introduced as standalone. Democratic precedents include the Inspector General Protection Act (2025 response to Trump IG firings) — IRS independence legislation would follow the same legislative logic.

**Timeline**: Achievable as standalone legislation in a congressional majority environment; achievable through appropriations rider in divided government if paired with bipartisan government efficiency framing.

### 5.2 Structural: ICA Enforcement Reform and Congressional Standing (12-36 months)

**Problem**: The Impoundment Control Act's enforcement depends on GAO civil action (which H.R. 4229 would require congressional authorization for), state AG standing (which varies by state and program), or criminal ADA prosecution (which has never been invoked in 51 years). Congressional members lack standing under *Raines v. Byrd*. GAO itself can be defunded to eliminate its investigative capacity.

**Proposed mechanism**: An ICA Strengthening Act would include:
- **Standing provision**: Conferring standing on the Comptroller General to bring ICA enforcement civil actions *ex officio* — not dependent on congressional authorization — with standing recognized in 28 USC § 1331 jurisdiction, explicitly overriding any concurrent resolution requirement
- **Sequestration remedy**: Where a court finds a violation of the ICA, automatic sequestration of a portion of OMB operating budget as a compensatory mechanism (not punitive, but structural — creating an institutional interest for OMB in compliance)
- **Pocket rescission closure**: Amending ICA § 1012(b) to prohibit transmittal of rescission messages within 60 days of the relevant funds' expiration date, ensuring the 45-day review period can operate before expiration
- **Category C transparency**: Requiring OMB to publish all Category C apportionments in the Federal Register within 5 business days of issuance, with a mandatory GAO notification and 30-day review period before the funds can be considered formally withheld
- **Mandatory apportionment data**: Requiring OMB to provide GAO with complete apportionment schedules within 10 business days of any GAO request related to ICA compliance review, with automatic contempt referral for non-compliance

**Self-enforcing elements**: Sequestration remedies, mandatory publication timelines, and automatic contempt referral are structural — they do not require a supportive DOJ. Standing for the Comptroller General routes enforcement through a congressional officer rather than an executive-controlled DOJ.

**Post-*Loper Bright* advantage**: More specific statutory language (prohibiting Category C apportionments for policy purposes, with the specific phrase "policy purpose" defined by reference to OMB's stated justifications) would be directly enforceable by courts under *Loper Bright*'s "best reading" standard. Courts can evaluate whether an apportionment was for "timing" versus "policy" purposes without deference to OMB's self-characterization.

### 5.3 Structural: GAO Constitutional Funding and Independence Act (12-48 months)

**Problem**: GAO's budget is controlled by the same appropriations process it monitors; House Republicans have proposed 50 percent cuts in retaliation for impoundment investigations; the proposed H.R. 4229 would require congressional authorization for GAO's core ICA enforcement function.

**Proposed mechanism**: A GAO Independence Act would:
- **Mandatory minimum funding floor**: Set GAO's minimum annual appropriation at no less than 0.005 percent of total federal outlays (approximately $300 million at current spending levels), automatically adjusted, enforceable through mandatory sequestration of funds from the OMB operating budget if the statutory floor is not appropriated
- **Permanent standing**: GAO's civil ICA enforcement authority (originally conferred by 31 USC § 1352) would be amended to explicitly eliminate any concurrent resolution requirement, affirming the Comptroller General's authority as an independent officer of Congress with inherent civil enforcement standing
- **Protected tenure**: The Comptroller General serves a 15-year single term (existing statutory structure). An amendment would provide that removal requires 2/3 majority vote of both chambers — currently the standard is joint resolution (simple majority), creating a political vulnerability
- **Data access mandate**: Amending 31 USC § 712 to include a mandatory provision that any executive branch agency receiving an appropriation has a duty to provide GAO with all documentation necessary to complete an ICA compliance review, with automatic referral to district court for injunctive relief for non-compliance

**INTOSAI alignment**: These provisions align with INTOSAI's Lima and Mexico Declaration pillars: independent legal framework, sufficient resources, access to information, and security of tenure. The OECD's IFI best-practice framework recommends that parliamentary budget offices have resources "secured through a dedicated appropriation protected from the appropriations process the institution monitors" — the proposed mandatory floor with automatic remediation achieves this.

### 5.4 Complementary: Fiscal Council Act (24-60 months)

**Problem**: The United States lacks an independent fiscal council — a body with a statutory mandate to assess fiscal sustainability, provide multi-year projections, and evaluate fiscal rule compliance — analogous to the UK Office for Budget Responsibility, the Dutch CPB Bureau for Economic Policy Analysis, or the Canadian Parliamentary Budget Office. CBO performs related functions but reports to Congress and cannot independently initiate assessments.

**Proposed mechanism**: A US Fiscal Council Act would establish an independent body with:
- **Mandate**: Annual multi-year fiscal sustainability assessments, evaluation of compliance with any statutory fiscal rules, macroeconomic scenario analysis for proposed legislation, and evaluation of Treasury and OMB budget execution data
- **Independence design**: OECD IFI principles — fixed staggered terms for the council's seven members, with staggered appointment by the Comptroller General (2), CBO Director (2), Federal Reserve Board (1), and Senate-confirmed presidential appointment (2); removal for cause only
- **Mandatory transparency**: All council reports must be published within 10 business days of completion, with a statutory prohibition on executive suppression
- **Complementary to CBO**: The council would not replace CBO but would provide a second institutional opinion where CBO's assessments are contested, and would have independent authority to assess OMB's fiscal representations

**Comparative precedent**: The OECD documents 40+ independent fiscal institutions across member states, all established after the 2008 financial crisis demonstrated that self-assessed government solvency claims are systematically overoptimistic. Post-*Loper Bright*, independent assessments not produced by the Treasury or OMB would be treated by courts as independent evidence — reducing the executive's ability to present self-serving fiscal representations as authoritative.

### 5.5 Revenue Defense: Tax Enforcement Restoration and Protection Act (0-36 months)

**Problem**: IRS enforcement capacity has been degraded by DOGE staff reductions; the tax gap is $700 billion annually; post-*Loper Bright* regulatory challenges will multiply without a staffed enforcement operation to litigate them; and political direction of audit priorities has been documented through both direct staff testimony and audit statistics.

**Proposed mechanism**: A Tax Enforcement Restoration Act would include:
- **Mandatory minimum staffing floor**: Setting a statutory minimum of no fewer than 85,000 full-time equivalent IRS employees, with an inflation-adjusted floor; requiring IRS to report to Congress quarterly on compliance with the staffing floor
- **Enforcement audit neutrality**: Prohibiting any Treasury or OMB official from directing specific audit selections, case closures, or enforcement priority changes for identified taxpayers or categories of taxpayers without written authorization countersigned by both Chairpersons of the House Ways and Means and Senate Finance Committees
- **Post-*Loper Bright* litigation fund**: A dedicated appropriation, insulated from annual cuts through a multi-year authorization, for IRS regulatory defense — funding the Tax Division's defense of Treasury regulations challenged under *Loper Bright*'s de novo standard, ensuring the agency can litigate its own rules
- **TIGTA enhanced standing**: Amending TIGTA's enabling statute to require mandatory referral to both the Senate Finance and House Ways and Means Committees of any documented political interference in audit selection or enforcement priority, with publication in the Federal Register within 30 days

**Revenue argument**: The Budget Lab at Yale projects $861 billion in deficit increase from current IRS cuts. Congressional Budget Office precedent establishes that IRS enforcement spending pays for itself multiple times over. The revenue case for enforcement staffing is the strongest self-financing argument in the federal budget — it satisfies both deficit hawks (restoration reduces the deficit) and progressive fiscal priorities (enforcement targets high-income evasion).

## 6. Implementation Dependencies and Risk Mitigation

### 6.1 Political Economy Constraints

The reform pathway requires a congressional majority willing to legislate fiscal institutional reforms that constrain executive discretion — a threshold that, at current margins, requires near-universal Democratic unity plus a small number of Republican votes concerned about long-term fiscal credibility (a constituency that exists among deficit-hawk Republicans, business-oriented moderates, and appropriators who value committee power).

The international evidence is helpful here: fiscal institutional reforms have been enacted under politically hostile conditions when paired with specific constituency arguments:

- Brazil's central bank autonomy law passed under Bolsonaro with broad market support — the financial sector's interest in monetary stability created a political coalition for institutional design
- Argentina's Ley Bases passed with IMF/creditor backing providing external political cover
- Peru's fiscal rules maintained market credibility that provided its own political justification — deficit spending would trigger credit rating downgrades that politicians could not afford

For the United States, the revenue argument (IRS enforcement cuts cost $861 billion in deficits) and the market credibility argument (Morgan Stanley-style US sovereign downgrades if fiscal enforcement erodes further) provide the political economy frame for a bipartisan majority in a reformed Congress.

### 6.2 Sequencing Priorities

**Immediate (0-18 months)**: IRS statutory independence provisions and Category C transparency requirements. Both can potentially be incorporated into continuing resolution or omnibus appropriations legislation; both have a clear revenue/deficit justification; both address ongoing violations rather than hypothetical future risks.

**Medium-term (12-36 months)**: ICA enforcement reform (pocket rescission closure, standing for Comptroller General, mandatory apportionment data). Requires a stand-alone bill with committee markup; best window is immediately post-administration transition when newly dominant coalitions have an institutional interest in constraining successor executives.

**Longer-term (24-60 months)**: GAO constitutional funding floor and US Fiscal Council. These require the most substantial legislative effort and face the most institutional resistance (OMB and Treasury oppose fiscal councils as encroachments on executive fiscal authority). Both have strong OECD precedent and international credibility arguments.

### 6.3 Judicial Enforcement Dependencies

Each statutory reform must be designed to survive judicial challenge in the post-*Loper Bright* environment. Key design principles:

**Specificity**: Vague delegations will not survive *Loper Bright* scrutiny. Each reform should include precise definitions of prohibited conduct (e.g., "a Category C apportionment issued for the stated purpose of aligning spending with administration policy priorities, as evidenced by [specific criteria]") rather than relying on agency interpretation.

**Self-execution**: Where possible, reforms should be self-executing through automatic triggers (sequestration, mandatory publication, automatic contempt referral) rather than requiring agency action to implement.

**Standing**: Each reform should include an explicit standing provision naming the officer or institution authorized to bring enforcement actions, to preempt *Raines v. Byrd* challenges.

### 6.4 Temporal Framework: What Works When

**0-2 years (immediate recovery)**: The most effective mechanisms in the immediate window are transparency-based — mandatory publication timelines, automatic GAO notification, Category C reporting requirements. These can be effective even without enforcement through publicity effects. Criminal ADA prosecution, if a new administration chose to invoke it, could be an immediate deterrent but requires DOJ normalization (Domain 29 prerequisite). IRS staffing restoration through mandatory minimums can be enacted immediately but staffing rebuilds take 2-3 years to restore full expertise.

**2-10 years (institutional resilience)**: Staggered-term independence structures (IRS Commissioner, Fiscal Council) take years to fully operationalize because the staggering mechanism only fully protects against political capture after the initial terms complete. GAO constitutional funding floor and Fiscal Council are decade-scale investments. Post-*Loper Bright* Treasury regulatory re-anchoring — rewriting regulations with sufficient specificity to survive de novo review — will take 5-7 years for the most consequential provisions.

The international evidence is consistent with this timeline: Brazil's BCB reform was enacted in 2021, tested 2022-2023, and validated by 2024. Peru's fiscal institutions took decades to build before they could absorb the 2022 shock. Mexico's judicial reform damage has been measurable within 12-18 months but will take a decade to reverse if the reform is not itself reversed.
