## Executive Summary

The systematic legal attack on LGBTQ+ rights in 2025–2026 is not primarily a story about anti-LGBTQ prejudice, though prejudice is present. It is a story about coordinated democratic design: the Alliance Defending Freedom (ADF) has spent more than three decades constructing a legal infrastructure — template legislation, model court briefs, trained lawyers, coordinated state campaigns, and SCOTUS litigation strategy — that uses the same mechanisms documented across this framework's other domains to systematically disable constitutional protections for LGBTQ+ people. The same ADF infrastructure that drafted template legislation for trans sports bans in 22 states drafted template legislation for anti-abortion measures that Domain 44 documents. The same ALEC coordination that funded ballot initiative suppression campaigns in Domain 33 coordinates anti-LGBTQ+ "sex definition" laws across 42 state legislatures. The same Alliance Defending Freedom brief filed in United States v. Skrmetti that persuaded the Supreme Court to uphold gender-affirming care bans for minors is being used as a template for challenges to Bostock v. Clayton County's employment non-discrimination protections.

The democratic design stakes extend beyond the specific rights of the LGBTQ+ community. Three structural democratic threats emerge from the legal attack on LGBTQ+ rights that this domain documents for the broader resistance framework:

First, the "sex definition" laws now moving through 42 state legislatures are not merely anti-trans measures — they are a systematic rewriting of state legal codes that collapses the distinction between sex and gender in ways that affect women's sports legislation, employment discrimination law, healthcare law, and family law. "Sex definition" laws that define sex as biologically determined at conception affect not just transgender people but anyone whose legal rights depend on sex-based distinctions — including women's protections under Title VII, Title IX, and state ERA provisions.

Second, the voter ID crisis for transgender people is a concrete, documented voting rights crisis. The Williams Institute estimates that more than 210,000 transgender adults face barriers to voting because their ID documents do not match their gender presentation. In states with strict photo ID requirements, a transgender person whose driver's license reflects their legal gender but whose appearance has changed may face poll worker challenges. Executive Order 14168's directive to halt all gender marker changes on federal identity documents — passports, Social Security cards, DACA work permits — means this crisis will worsen with every election cycle.

Third, the legal attack on LGBTQ+ people is a documented chilling effect on civic participation. The psychological research is consistent: legislative debates surrounding anti-trans policies increase anxiety, depression, and stress among transgender people, particularly youth. Marginalized communities facing coordinated legal attacks reduce their civic exposure to protect themselves. Communities under attack do not organize at the same rate as communities with established legal protections. The 740 bills targeting LGBTQ+ people in 2026 are not just legal threats — they are a civic demobilization campaign.

## 1. The ADF Legal Infrastructure: Democratic Design Analysis

### 1.1 The Alliance Defending Freedom as a Democratic Architecture Problem

The Alliance Defending Freedom is, by assets and litigation capacity, one of the most powerful legal organizations in the United States. Founded in 1994 as "Alliance Defense Fund," ADF has grown to $100+ million in annual revenue, employs more than 200 lawyers, and has successfully argued before the Supreme Court in cases affecting religious liberty, free speech, abortion, and LGBTQ+ rights. The SPLC designates ADF as a hate group. GLAAD's accountability database documents ADF's "30-year game plan to dismantle equality."

What makes ADF a democratic design problem — rather than merely an ideological opponent in contested legal debates — is not its substantive positions but its operational architecture. ADF operates as a legal coordination network that provides three services to state legislatures and state courts simultaneously:

**Template legislation**: ADF attorneys draft model legislation that is introduced in multiple states with minimal modification. The gender-affirming care bans enacted in more than 20 states since 2020 use nearly identical language. The trans sports ban legislation in Idaho, Montana, and other states includes identical excerpts from the same 2019 Washington Post opinion piece, indicating centralized drafting. ADF attorney Matt Sharp confirmed the organization "mails template bills to thousands of school districts." The functional result is that 42 state legislatures are voting on legislation that was written in Phoenix, Arizona, by a private legal organization with no democratic accountability, and then presented to each legislature as locally generated policy.

**Strategic litigation**: ADF selects test cases strategically — identifying plaintiffs and jurisdictions that present the optimal legal facts for establishing new constitutional precedent. The gender-affirming care cases that eventually became United States v. Skrmetti were selected from multiple state cases based on which presented the cleanest legal question for Supreme Court review. ADF filed the amicus brief in Skrmetti that provided the Court with the doctrinal framework for upholding the Tennessee ban. This is not defensive litigation — it is an offensive legal campaign designed to systematically erode constitutional protections.

**State attorney general coordination**: ADF maintains relationships with state AGs who file coordinated challenges to federal policies protecting LGBTQ+ people. The multi-state challenges to the Biden administration's Title IX regulations and to EEOC guidance on Bostock's employment protections were coordinated through ADF's state AG network, creating the appearance of grassroots state resistance to federal overreach while concealing the centralized coordination.

The template legislation model is particularly significant from a democratic design perspective. When 42 state legislatures vote on legislation that was drafted by a single private organization, tested in courts by that organization's legal team, and defended at the Supreme Court by that organization's amicus briefs — the appearance of democratic legitimacy masks a capture of the legislative process that is structurally similar to the corporate regulatory capture documented in Domain 38 (Financial Sector) and Domain 42 (Drug Policy). The difference is that ADF is deploying legal rather than financial resources, and the democratic institution being captured is state legislatures rather than federal agencies.

### 1.2 United States v. Skrmetti: The Constitutional Framework After June 2025

The Supreme Court's June 18, 2025 decision in United States v. Skrmetti (6-3) is the foundational constitutional development that governs the current LGBTQ+ rights landscape. Understanding the decision's actual holding — which is narrower than its political reception suggests — is essential for accurate democratic design analysis.

**What Skrmetti actually held**: The Court upheld Tennessee's Senate Bill 1, which bans gender-affirming care (puberty blockers, hormone therapy, and surgery) for minors. The majority, written by Chief Justice Roberts, found that SB 1 did not classify based on sex or transgender status but rather based on age and medical diagnosis. Because the classification was based on age (minors) and medical use (hormone therapy for gender dysphoria as opposed to other indications), the law was subject only to rational basis review — the most deferential constitutional standard — and survived that review.

**What Skrmetti did not hold**: The Court did not hold that all gender-affirming care bans are constitutional. It did not hold that transgender people lack Equal Protection rights. It did not overrule Bostock v. Clayton County's holding that discrimination based on transgender status is sex discrimination under Title VII. The majority opinion explicitly declined to address whether a ban on gender-affirming care for adults would pass constitutional muster. Justice Sotomayor's dissent, joined by Justice Jackson, argued that SB 1 does classify based on sex (because it treats people differently based on their sex assigned at birth in combination with their medical needs) and would fail under heightened scrutiny.

**What Skrmetti means for the framework**: The decision dramatically lowers the constitutional barrier for anti-trans legislation by establishing that laws which affect transgender people but are framed as age-based or diagnosis-based classifications survive rational basis review. This is the doctrinal opening ADF and state legislatures have been pursuing for a decade. The "sex definition" laws now moving through 42 state legislatures — which redefine sex as biologically determined and immutable across entire state legal codes — are the next wave of this litigation strategy, designed to eliminate transgender legal recognition entirely at the state level.

## 2. The 2026 Legislative Landscape: Scale and Architecture

### 2.1 The 740-Bill Wave: Anatomy of a Legislative Campaign

The 740 bills targeting LGBTQ+ people under consideration in 42 states as of 2026 represent a deliberate strategic escalation from the prior years' legislative campaigns. The ACLU's 2025 tracking documented approximately 500 bills; the 2026 wave represents a 48% increase and extends into new categories that reflect the Skrmetti doctrinal opening.

The legislative categories divide into five strategic domains, each with distinct constitutional and democratic participation implications:

**Healthcare bans for minors**: Following Skrmetti's rational basis ruling, more than 30 states now have enacted or are advancing healthcare bans for transgender minors. The Skrmetti ruling substantially reduced the constitutional barrier to these bans, and ADF has shifted from defending the existing bans (which are mostly now legally secure) to advancing bans on gender-affirming care for adults — the next doctrinal target that Skrmetti explicitly left open.

**Sex definition laws**: The most legally ambitious category in 2026. These laws redefine "sex" throughout entire state legal codes as biologically determined and immutable, eliminating legal gender recognition for transgender people across all state law — employment, family law, healthcare, education, public accommodations, and civil rights. The language is designed to survive Bostock's Title VII protections (which apply only to federal employment law) while eliminating transgender recognition in every domain of state law. The "sex definition" laws function as a wholesale legal erasure of transgender existence at the state level.

**School sports and facility bans**: Prohibitions on transgender student participation in school sports consistent with gender identity, and on transgender student use of bathrooms and locker rooms consistent with gender identity. These laws are now enacted in more than 20 states. The doctrinal basis — that single-sex sports and facilities are based on biological sex — was strengthened by Skrmetti's rational basis framework.

**Forced outing and curriculum censorship**: Legislation requiring school staff to notify parents if a student requests use of different pronouns or names, and prohibiting discussion of LGBTQ+ identities in school curricula. "Don't Say Gay" laws at the state level (modeled on Florida's Parental Rights in Education Act) have now been enacted in multiple states and are the template for federal legislation (H.R. 7661, "Don't Say Trans") introduced in 2026.

**ID document restrictions**: State laws prohibiting gender marker changes on state identity documents — driver's licenses, birth certificates — regardless of federal document status or medical transition status. These laws create the voter ID barriers documented in the Williams Institute study (210,000 trans adults facing voting access barriers) and compound the chilling effects on civic participation documented below.

### 2.2 Executive Order 14168 and the Federal Dimension

Executive Order 14168, signed January 20, 2025, represents the federal executive's contribution to the coordinated legal attack: it required all federal agencies to recognize gender as an immutable male-female binary determined at conception, replace all references to "gender" with "sex" in federal documents, cease funding for gender-affirming care in federal programs, and halt all gender marker changes on federal identity documents.

The implementation consequences have been far-reaching across multiple domains of federal law:

**Federal healthcare**: Federal Employee Health Benefits (FEHB) programs eliminated coverage for gender-affirming care for plan year 2026, affecting all federal employees and postal workers. The Bureau of Prisons implemented conversion-therapy-adjacent policies for transgender inmates, including mandatory hair cutting, hormone discontinuation, and psychiatric treatment for transgender identity — policies federal courts have partially enjoined.

**Identity documents**: The State Department halted all passport gender marker changes. Social Security Administration guidance was changed to require sex marker consistency with assigned sex at birth. DACA work permit gender markers were frozen. The result is a growing class of transgender people whose federal identity documents do not match their state documents or their gender presentation — creating verification confusion in employment, banking, healthcare, and voting contexts.

**Data elimination**: The federal government removed questions about sexual orientation and gender identity from at least 360 government surveys, forms, government-funded research projects, and data collections. This data elimination makes the scale and consequences of anti-LGBTQ+ discrimination legally invisible — you cannot document a discrimination problem if you cannot collect the data that would reveal it. The Movement Advancement Project has documented that LGBTQ+ data collection is a prerequisite for evidence-based policymaking on LGBTQ+ issues.

**FTC political targeting**: In May 2026, a federal judge blocked an FTC probe into the Endocrine Society and WPATH (World Professional Association for Transgender Health) — the professional medical associations that set standards for gender-affirming care — finding the probe was based on politically-motivated animus rather than legitimate consumer protection concerns. This is documented in the litigation tracker as an example of the administration deploying regulatory enforcement as a political weapon against organizations that provide evidence-based medical guidance on trans healthcare.

### 2.3 The 2026 Ballot Measure Wave: Trans Rights as Electoral Strategy

Voters in eight states could decide on 12 ballot measures related to sex, gender, and LGBTQ+ issues in 2026. This ballot measure wave represents a strategic pivot by conservative legal organizations from the legislative chamber (where advocates have defeated 92% of anti-LGBTQ+ bills over 15 years) to the ballot box, where low-information voters can be mobilized through fear-based messaging that does not survive legislative committee scrutiny.

Missouri's Amendment 3 — already certified for the November 2026 ballot — is the clearest example of this strategic deployment. Amendment 3 would ban gender-affirming care for minors and simultaneously roll back the abortion rights restoration that Missouri voters approved in 2024. The combination is deliberate: it bundles the trans healthcare ban (which may have majority support in Missouri polling) with the abortion rights reversal (which would not pass standalone given the 2024 vote), creating a single ballot question that forces voters to accept both restrictions together. The LGBTQ Nation analysis calls anti-trans ballot initiatives "ballot candy" — policies added to suppress progressive turnout through demoralization or to boost conservative turnout through mobilization.

The eight-state ballot measure wave demonstrates a second strategic reality: the initiative process itself is being used as a mechanism to override state court rulings, legislative compromises, and executive orders that have protected LGBTQ+ rights. In Colorado, a ballot initiative would ban transgender students from participating in school sports consistent with their gender identity — reversing a gubernatorial executive order that had provided such protections. This is Domain 33's ballot initiative suppression dynamic inverted: instead of using legislative power to suppress citizen-initiated measures protecting rights, conservative organizations are using citizen initiative architecture to enact restrictions that legislatures have declined to pass.

## 3. Democratic Participation Crises: Voting, Identity, and Civic Silencing

### 3.1 The Voter ID Crisis for Transgender People

The Williams Institute's analysis, published in 2025, found that more than 210,000 transgender adults face barriers to voting because their official identity documents do not match their gender presentation. In states with strict photo ID requirements, a mismatch between a voter's appearance and the photo on their ID — or between a voter's presented gender and the gender marker on their ID — can result in poll worker challenges, provisional ballot requirements, or effective disenfranchisement.

Executive Order 14168's halt on federal gender marker changes has dramatically worsened this problem. Transgender people who were in the process of updating their federal documents — passports, Social Security cards — had their applications frozen or denied. The interaction between frozen federal documents, state ID document policies, and strict voter ID laws creates a specific and measurable voting rights crisis: transgender people whose passports and Social Security cards reflect their previous gender marker, and whose driver's licenses may or may not have been updated depending on state law, face the highest risk of voter ID challenge at the polls.

The voter ID crisis is not distributed evenly. Transgender people of color face compound barriers: higher rates of document inconsistency (because the bureaucratic process of gender marker change requires resources — legal fees, medical documentation, processing time — that low-income people cannot access), higher rates of strict ID enforcement in the states with the highest concentrations of anti-trans legislation, and higher baseline rates of civic exclusion from the criminal justice patterns documented in Domain 54.

The National Center for Transgender Equality's voting rights program and the ACLU's Voting Rights Project have both documented specific cases where transgender voters were challenged at polls in 2022 and 2024. The 2026 elections, conducted under the most restrictive trans document environment in federal history, are likely to produce the highest documented rate of trans voter ID challenges.

**Sources**: [Voting Rights — National Center for Transgender Equality](https://transequality.org/issues/voting-rights); [Voting Rights Is an LGBT Rights Fight — ACLU](https://www.aclu.org/news/lgbtq-rights/voting-rights-lgbt-rights-fight); [Impact of EO Redefine Sex on TBI People — Williams Institute](https://williamsinstitute.law.ucla.edu/publications/impact-eo-redefine-sex-tbi/)

### 3.2 The Civic Participation Chilling Effect

Psychological research on the effects of anti-LGBTQ+ legislation consistently documents that legislative debates surrounding anti-trans policies are linked to increased anxiety, depression, and stress among transgender people, particularly youth — regardless of whether the legislation passes. This finding has direct democratic participation implications: communities experiencing sustained legislative threat reduce their civic exposure.

The mechanism is straightforward. Civic participation — voting, attending public meetings, contacting elected officials, organizing in community spaces — requires voluntary exposure to public scrutiny. For transgender people living in states where their identity is the subject of legislative attack, every act of civic participation carries a higher risk than it does for people whose identity is legally protected. A transgender person attending a school board meeting to oppose curriculum censorship legislation faces a qualitatively different risk than a cisgender person attending the same meeting.

The documented chilling effects operate across multiple dimensions:
- Reduced participation in public comment proceedings on state regulations that affect LGBTQ+ people
- Reduced likelihood of using legal name and gender in voter registration records (because state records may be matched against federal "sex definition" requirements)
- Reduced likelihood of running for local office in jurisdictions where being transgender is a campaign liability
- Reduced likelihood of using healthcare services that require disclosure of trans identity (which may have direct civic participation effects, since untreated health conditions reduce civic engagement)

The National LGBTQ Task Force's "Queer the Vote" program documents the civic participation gap: LGBTQ+ people report higher rates of political interest and political knowledge than comparable non-LGBTQ+ populations, but lower rates of civic participation in states with hostile legislative environments — a gap that is explained by the chilling effect literature.

## 4. Historical Precedent and International Comparative Evidence

### 4.1 The Pre-Obergefell History and the Lessons of Marriage Equality

The LGBTQ+ movement's experience with ballot initiative campaigns between 1993 and 2012 provides the most directly relevant historical precedent for the current anti-trans ballot initiative wave. Between 1993 (Hawaii's groundbreaking state supreme court ruling on same-sex marriage) and 2012 (Maine, Maryland, Minnesota, and Washington state ballot victories for marriage equality), the anti-equality movement used citizen initiative architecture to enact more than 30 state constitutional amendments defining marriage as between a man and a woman — overriding state court rulings, legislative compromises, and executive orders at the ballot box.

The lesson of that history — which Advocates for Trans Equality's state policy director Alana Jochum explicitly invoked in May 2026 — is that "turning to an old playbook to try to scapegoat a small percentage of the population" is a reproducible political strategy. The anti-marriage-equality ballot initiative campaign won 32 consecutive state ballot votes before losing in Maine in 2012; it succeeded because it mobilized low-information voters through fear-based messaging, used ballot initiative design to bypass legislative scrutiny, and concentrated resources on specific battleground states. The current anti-trans ballot initiative wave follows the same playbook.

The counterhistory is equally important: the marriage equality movement won by shifting from a rights-based frame to a dignity-based and democratic-participation frame, by building broad coalitions that included non-LGBTQ+ people whose dignity and participation rights were also affected, and by winning in state courts before winning at the ballot box. The Obergefell v. Hodges decision (2015) that resolved the marriage equality fight at the federal constitutional level came after state court wins, legislative wins, and ballot wins had demonstrated that the issue had majority public support.

The Article V convention threat documented in Domain 39 includes the possibility of constitutional amendments targeting Obergefell. The Convention of States Project materials include same-sex marriage restrictions as a potential convention target. This connection between Domain 39 and Domain 55 is among the most concrete cross-domain democratic design risks in the framework: a constitutional convention called for fiscal reasons could produce an anti-marriage-equality amendment as a side product.

### 4.2 International Comparative Evidence: European Models of Legal Protection

The European Union and Council of Europe provide the most developed international comparison on LGBTQ+ rights as democratic design requirements.

The European Court of Human Rights has developed extensive LGBTQ+-related case law under multiple articles of the Convention, including Article 8 (private life), Article 10 (free expression), Article 11 (free assembly), and Article 14 (non-discrimination). The Court has recognized the right of transgender persons to legal gender recognition without sterilizing surgery or treatment, the right to legal recognition of same-sex relationships, and non-discrimination in adoption and family law for same-sex couples. The Court has applied the principle that the European Convention is a "living instrument" — interpreted in light of present-day conditions in democratic societies — to progressively expand LGBTQ+ protections as democratic participation requires.

A March 13, 2025 Court of Justice of the European Union ruling (C-247/23) established that requiring gender reassignment surgery as a prerequisite for gender identity recognition violates fundamental rights, including the right to private life and bodily integrity. This ruling is directly contrary to the US trajectory under Executive Order 14168, which moves toward eliminating gender identity recognition regardless of medical status.

The Council of Europe's Venice Commission has found that legislation targeting LGBTQ+ people through blanket categorical restrictions — the equivalent of the US "sex definition" laws — is inconsistent with the ECHR's equal protection framework. The ECHR's ruling in *Identoba and Others v. Georgia* (2015) found that failure to protect LGBTQ+ assembly participants from mob violence violated Articles 3 and 11 of the Convention — establishing a state obligation to protect LGBTQ+ civic participation from private violence, not just to refrain from official persecution.

The comparative lesson for democratic design: in the Council of Europe framework, LGBTQ+ rights are not a contested policy preference — they are a prerequisite for compliance with the democratic governance standard that council membership requires. States that systematically exclude LGBTQ+ people from civic participation through legal mechanisms are not merely making different policy choices; they are failing the democratic design standard.

## 5. Reform Pathways

### 5.1 Tier 1 — Ballot Initiative Defense (Immediate, November 2026)

The most time-critical near-term advocacy is defeat of the anti-trans ballot measures on November 2026 state ballots. The eight-state, twelve-measure wave requires coordinated resources across a larger geographic area than any prior LGBTQ+ ballot campaign. The advocacy strategy is directly informed by the marriage equality ballot experience: the measures that succeeded against anti-equality ballot campaigns did so by building coalitions beyond the LGBTQ+ community, framing the issue in dignity and democratic participation terms, and reaching low-information voters who were not persuaded by rights-based arguments alone.

Missouri's Amendment 3 — which combines trans healthcare ban and abortion rights rollback — is the highest-stakes single ballot measure because it represents the next iteration of the "bundling" strategy: attaching a less popular measure (abortion rollback) to a potentially more popular measure (trans healthcare restrictions for minors) in a single ballot question. Defeating this measure requires the reproductive rights coalition and the LGBTQ+ coalition to work together in a way neither has sustained in Missouri before. The framework document connecting these domains (Domain 44 on reproductive freedom, Domain 55 on LGBTQ+ rights) provides the analytical foundation for that coalition argument.

Lambda Legal, ACLU, Advocates for Trans Equality, and the Human Rights Campaign are the primary litigation and advocacy organizations. For ballot measure campaigns, state-level organizations — Planned Parenthood Action Fund of Missouri, PROMO Missouri — are the direct advocacy infrastructure partners.

**Sources**: [2026 Missouri Ballot Measures — Missouri Secretary of State](https://www.sos.mo.gov/petitions/2026BallotMeasures); [Voting for Equality: LGBTQ-Related Proposals on the Ballot — GLAAD](https://glaad.org/voting-for-equality-lgbtq-related-proposals-on-the-ballot-this-year); [2026 Resistance Report: Anti-LGBTQ Bills — Equality Florida](https://www.eqfl.org/2026-Resistance-Report-final)

### 5.2 Tier 2 — Voter ID and Document Access Reform

The 210,000 transgender adults facing voter ID barriers require immediate relief through two parallel tracks.

The near-term track: state and local voter registration assistance programs should be directed to provide specific support to transgender voters navigating ID document inconsistencies. The ACLU's Know Your Rights guidance for transgender voters at the polls provides the template; local legal aid organizations in states with strict voter ID laws should expand their election-day support capacity.

The medium-term track: the federal government's Executive Order 14168 halt on gender marker changes should be challenged in court on administrative law and constitutional grounds — several challenges are pending as of May 2026. If these challenges succeed, restoring the ability to update federal identity documents would directly reduce the voter ID barrier population. The LGBTQ+ Bar Association's executive order litigation tracker is the primary monitoring resource for these cases.

The longest-term track: advocacy for voter ID policies that accept a broader range of identity documentation, reducing the ID burden on all voters who face document inconsistency — not only transgender voters. This connects to the broader voting rights reform agenda in Domain 1.

**Sources**: [Voting Rights — National Center for Transgender Equality](https://transequality.org/issues/voting-rights); [Trump Anti-LGBTQ+ Executive Order Litigation Tracker — LGBTQ+ Bar Association](https://lgbtqbar.org/programs/advocacy-resources/trump-executive-order-tracker/); [Understanding Executive Orders for the LGBTQ+ Community — HRC](https://www.hrc.org/news/understanding-executive-orders-and-what-they-mean-for-the-lgbtq-community)

### 5.3 Tier 3 — Federal Non-Discrimination Legislation and Constitutional Framework

The Equality Act — federal legislation that would explicitly extend civil rights protections to sexual orientation and gender identity under Title II (public accommodations), Title VI (education), and other civil rights laws — has been introduced in every Congress since 2015. It has never reached the 60-vote threshold in the Senate, despite consistent majority public support. The Skrmetti ruling's rational basis framework for anti-trans legislation significantly raises the urgency of federal statutory protection: in the absence of explicit statutory protection, the constitutional floor for anti-LGBTQ+ discrimination is very low.

The Bostock v. Clayton County (2020) interpretation of Title VII's "sex" prohibition as covering sexual orientation and gender identity remains good law but is under sustained attack through the ADF's "sex definition" litigation strategy. If courts accept ADF's argument that "sex" in Title VII means only biological sex assigned at birth (consistent with Executive Order 14168's definition), Bostock's employment protection would be dramatically narrowed.

A federal LGBTQ+ non-discrimination statute that explicitly defines "sex" to include gender identity and sexual orientation — and that codifies Bostock while adding public accommodations protections — would provide protection more durable than executive orders (which can be reversed) or agency interpretation (which Loper Bright now subjects to judicial second-guessing). The democratic design argument for such legislation: equal civic participation requires equal legal protection; communities whose legal status is under sustained legal attack participate in civic life at demonstrably lower rates.

This domain document provides the legal framework for that advocacy.

**Skrmetti state court cascade**: Following Skrmetti, state courts are now applying the ruling to gender-affirming care bans for adults and to sex definition laws. Lambda Legal's litigation docket tracks these cases. A domain document that provides the comparative international analysis (European Court of Human Rights framework) gives state court litigants an international law argument that federal constitutional arguments no longer support.

### 6.2 Medium Term (2027 State Legislative Sessions)

The 2027 state legislative wave — which will build on Skrmetti's rational basis framework — is likely to produce "sex definition" laws in additional states and to expand existing gender-affirming care bans to adults. Research documents distributed before January 2027 legislative session openings provide advocacy organizations with the democratic design frame, the comparative international evidence, and the cross-domain analysis (connecting ADF's LGBTQ+ legal infrastructure to the same infrastructure attacking reproductive rights and ballot initiative architecture) that is most useful for legislative testimony.

### 6.3 Coalition Architecture

**Advocates for Trans Equality (AT4E)**: The 2024 merger of NCTE (National Center for Transgender Equality) and TLDEF (Transgender Legal Defense and Education Fund) created the primary trans-specific advocacy and legal organization. **Lambda Legal**: LGBTQ+-specific legal organization with the most active litigation docket following Skrmetti. Lambda Legal's state court cascade response is the primary litigation application for this domain's analysis.

**ACLU LGBTQ+ Program**: The ACLU's tracking of anti-LGBTQ+ legislation provides the data foundation for this domain; the ACLU's legal team provides the litigation infrastructure. The ACLU is an existing Phase 1 distribution contact through the surveillance and First Amendment domains.

**Human Rights Campaign**: The largest LGBTQ+ advocacy organization. HRC's voter mobilization capacity and state chapter infrastructure make it the primary electoral organizing distribution channel.

**National LGBTQ Task Force**: Social movement organizing rather than legal advocacy. The Task Force's "Queer the Vote" program is the primary civic participation application for this domain's voter ID and chilling effect analysis.

**GLAAD**: Media strategy and messaging. GLAAD's Trump accountability tracker and LGBTQ+ legislative tracking make it a natural distribution target; GLAAD's media relationships provide amplification through LGBTQ+ press and mainstream media.

**Intersecting distribution**: The reproductive rights coalition (Domain 44's distribution targets) and the ballot initiative protection coalition (Domain 33's distribution targets) should both receive this domain as an explicit cross-domain document — the ADF infrastructure analysis is the argument that bridges these communities.
