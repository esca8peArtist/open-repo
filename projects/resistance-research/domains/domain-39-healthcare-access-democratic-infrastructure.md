# Domain 39: Healthcare Access as Democratic Infrastructure

**Research completed**: May 14, 2026
**Distribution target**: June 1, 2026 (HHS OBBBA work requirement implementation guidance comment period)
**Crisis window**: ACUTE — APSR study confirms rural hospital closures reduce voter turnout among lower-income and older residents; 18 rural hospital closures or conversions in 2025 alone; OBBBA projects 11.8 million coverage losses (CBO); FQHC voter registration mandate removed January 2025; 1.3 million adults under guardianship with voting rights stripped; PAVA program zeroed out in FY2026 presidential budget; Oregon Health Insurance Experiment confirms Medicaid enrollment increases voter turnout 7%
**Primary distinguishing contribution**: The democratic design argument — reframing healthcare access as civic infrastructure rather than social welfare. This framing change matters for which audiences engage. Election protection organizations, voting rights advocates, and democratic reform coalitions do not currently receive healthcare advocacy as directly relevant to their work. This domain provides the empirical bridge: five causal pathways through which healthcare deprivation produces civic exclusion, with specific APSR peer-reviewed evidence linking hospital closures to reduced voter turnout and a randomized controlled trial (the Oregon Health Insurance Experiment) establishing that healthcare coverage causes civic participation.

## Executive Summary

Healthcare access is not a parallel concern to democratic participation — it is a causal prerequisite for it. The empirical record is unusually rigorous. A study published in the *American Political Science Review* (2025) analyzed over 10.5 million rural residents across three elections and found that individuals whose nearest hospital closed prior to an election were measurably less likely to vote than unaffected counterparts, with effects strongest for older, lower-income residents — precisely the population most dependent on Medicaid and most vulnerable to OBBBA coverage losses. The Oregon Health Insurance Experiment — a natural randomized controlled trial of Medicaid expansion using a lottery design — found that Medicaid enrollment increased voter turnout in the November 2008 presidential election by approximately 7%, with effects concentrated in men (18% increase) and Democratic-county residents (10% increase). This is not correlation: the lottery's random assignment means the increase can be attributed to Medicaid itself.

This domain is analytically distinct from Domain 31 (which documents the OBBBA's Medicaid-cutting mechanisms and NVRA Section 7 enforcement theory in detail) and from Domain 50 (which covers the Medicaid-voter registration infrastructure pathway and the ICE-CMS data-sharing chilling effect). This domain provides what neither existing document provides: a unified democratic-design argument across five independent causal pathways linking healthcare deprivation to civic exclusion. Those five pathways are:

1. **Rural hospital closures reduce voter turnout** through the concrete civic-infrastructure loss that hospital closure causes — not only the health consequences but the economic destabilization, community anchor loss, and erosion of the working-class social networks that connect healthcare workers to civic life.

2. **Medical debt produces civic exclusion** through the economic precarity, residential instability, and credit damage that transform a health emergency into a multi-year barrier to the discretionary time and financial stability that political participation requires.

3. **Medicaid enrollment offices are mandatory voter registration sites** under the NVRA, and the OBBBA's enrollment cuts mechanically degrade voter registration infrastructure for low-income and minority populations — the same populations that face the most significant voting barriers through other channels.

4. **Maternal mortality racial disparities** are a democratic harm, not only a health equity harm: Black women dying in childbirth at rates three to four times higher than white women cannot vote, organize, or participate civically, and their communities bear the concentrated economic and grief burden of preventable death that further reduces civic capacity.

5. **Disability disenfranchisement** operates through three distinct mechanisms — guardianship-based voting rights stripping, Protection and Advocacy program defunding, and inadequate OBBBA work requirement exemptions — that combine to structurally exclude the 61 million Americans with disabilities from democratic participation.

The OBBBA accelerates all five pathways simultaneously. Its 11.8 million projected coverage losses (CBO) reduce rural hospital Medicaid revenue (threatening the 734 rural hospitals at risk of closure), increase the medically uninsured population that accumulates medical debt, degrade Medicaid enrollment offices as NVRA voter registration sites, extend maternal mortality gaps by reducing prenatal care access for low-income women, and impose work requirement administrative burden on people with disabilities whose conditions are episodic, mental-health-related, or otherwise difficult to document under the statutory exemption standard.

Healthcare policy is democratic design policy. The advocacy community must treat it as such.

## 1. The Empirical Foundation: Healthcare Coverage Causes Civic Participation

### 1.1 The Oregon Experiment — Randomized Evidence

In 2008, Oregon used a lottery to randomly select from a list of applicants for a limited Medicaid expansion. This random assignment created the closest thing to a clinical trial available in social science: lottery winners who gained Medicaid access and lottery losers who did not were otherwise statistically identical. Researchers Katherine Baicker and Amy Finkelstein (NBER Working Paper 25244; published in the *Quarterly Journal of Political Science*, vol. 14(4)) used Oregon's state administrative voter behavior data to measure the effect of Medicaid enrollment on political participation.

The finding: Medicaid enrollment increased voter turnout in the November 2008 presidential election by approximately 7% overall. The effects were concentrated in men (18% increase) and in residents of Democratic counties (10% increase). There is suggestive evidence that the increase reflected new voter registrations rather than higher turnout among existing registrants — meaning Medicaid was recruiting new voters into the electorate, not merely motivating already-registered voters to show up.

The mechanism: Medicaid reduces out-of-pocket medical spending and the debt accumulation that medical emergencies produce, reducing the economic precarity that suppresses voter participation. It reduces the cognitive and time burden of managing health conditions without insurance. It reduces the residential instability that medical debt causes. All three reductions operate on the same variable: the resource availability — time, money, cognitive bandwidth, housing stability — that political participation requires.

This is the most important finding in the domain because it eliminates the correlation-versus-causation objection that healthcare-democracy advocates must otherwise navigate. The lottery's random assignment means the 7% turnout increase is attributable to Medicaid, not to pre-existing differences between the covered and uncovered populations. Removing healthcare coverage, as the OBBBA does for 11.8 million people, will reduce voting among the populations losing coverage. This is a prediction, not an inference.

**Sources**: [The Impact of Medicaid Expansion on Voter Participation — PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8021132/); [Oregon Health Insurance Experiment — NBER](https://www.nber.org/programs-projects/projects-and-centers/oregon-health-insurance-experiment); [NBER Working Paper 25244](https://www.nber.org/papers/w25244)

### 1.2 Voting as a Political Determinant of Health — The Bidirectional Evidence

The American Public Health Association's 2023 policy brief documented the bidirectional relationship: not only does healthcare access affect civic participation, but states with lower barriers to voting have better health outcomes. Using the Cost of Voting Index (COVI) — which measures how easy states make voting based on registration requirements, early voting access, polling place availability, and ID requirements — the APHA documented that states with lower COVI scores (easier voting) have systematically better public health outcomes, while states with higher COVI scores concentrate health disparities. Colorado, which has among the lowest voting barriers of any state, ranks in the top 15 nationally for health. Tennessee, which has among the highest voting barriers, ranks 41st.

The APHA brief frames voting as a "political determinant of health" — a structural upstream cause of health outcomes that operates through the decisions that elected officials make about healthcare policy, environmental regulation, housing, and labor conditions. This framing is the inverse of this domain's primary argument (healthcare as democratic infrastructure) but is complementary: healthcare access and democratic participation are mutually constitutive. States that systematically restrict one systematically restrict both.

**Sources**: [APHA Access to Voting Policy Brief](https://www.apha.org/policy-and-advocacy/public-health-policy-briefs/policy-database/2023/01/18/access-to-voting); [Voting May Be Good for Your Health — Medicare Rights Center](https://www.medicarerights.org/medicare-watch/2024/08/29/voting-may-be-good-for-your-health)

## 2. Rural Hospital Closures and Voter Turnout — The APSR Finding

### 2.1 The Study

Researchers published in the *American Political Science Review* (2025) conducted the largest systematic examination of the relationship between rural hospital closures and voter turnout available in the peer-reviewed literature. Using L2 voter files from the 2016, 2018, and 2020 national elections — a panel of more than 10.5 million rural residents — matched with hospital closure data from the University of North Carolina's Cecil G. Sheps Center for Health Services Research, the study examined whether individuals whose nearest hospital closed before a proximate election were less likely to vote than otherwise comparable unaffected individuals.

The findings document a measurable negative effect. Rural residents whose nearest hospital closed prior to an election were less likely to vote than unaffected counterparts, with effects strongest for older, lower-income residents — the demographic strata most dependent on hospital services and most likely to experience the economic and social disruption of losing a community anchor institution. The researchers used timing-to-election as a placebo test: if effects appeared regardless of closure timing, they might reflect general economic decline rather than the specific civic-infrastructure loss. The timing variation supported the hospital-closure interpretation.

The effects decayed within 12 months, returning toward baseline turnout levels. This is consistent with the mechanism the researchers propose: hospital closure reduces voter turnout in the proximate election primarily through the immediate economic and social disruption it causes — the job loss, the care access emergency, the community dislocation — rather than through permanent voter deregistration.

**Why this matters for democratic design**: The APSR finding establishes that healthcare infrastructure is civic infrastructure in a causally direct sense. When a hospital closes, it does not merely reduce access to healthcare — it reduces the organizational substrate that connects workers, professionals, community members, and social networks through which civic information and mobilization flow. Hospitals are among the largest employers in rural counties. Their closure is simultaneously an economic shock, a health access shock, and a social capital shock, all of which depress the resource availability that voting requires.

**Sources**: [Access to Healthcare and Voting: The Case of Hospital Closures in Rural America — APSR/Cambridge Core](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/american-political-science-review/article/access-to-healthcare-and-voting-the-case-of-hospital-closures-in-rural-america/33E48DFAC6215DE273E7D80366B408B1); [APSR Publication Summary — Ideas/REPEC](https://ideas.repec.org/a/cup/apsrev/v119y2025i3p1233-1244_12.html)

### 2.2 The Current Crisis: Scale and Trajectory

The rural hospital closure crisis is accelerating. According to the BU School of Public Health's 2025 analysis, more than 100 rural hospitals have closed in the past decade, with at least 18 closures or conversions occurring in 2025 alone. Over 700 rural hospitals currently face closure risk, with approximately 300 at immediate risk. Nearly 60% of rural hospitals no longer deliver babies. At least 124 rural hospitals had closed or planned to close labor and delivery units by end of 2026 — a 12% loss in rural maternity capacity over a five-year period.

The Southern Poverty Law Center's "Critical Condition" report (2026) documented the concentrated geographic crisis in the Deep South: at least 99 rural hospitals at risk of closing in Alabama, Florida, Georgia, Louisiana, and Mississippi alone, with over 50% of Alabama's rural hospitals at closure risk. These are not random geographic concentrations — they are the states with the highest proportions of majority-Black and majority-rural Medicaid-eligible populations, the states that did not expand Medicaid under the ACA, and the states where OBBBA work requirements will produce the most disenrollment.

The OBBBA's Medicaid cuts directly threaten rural hospital viability. The WSWS analysis (May 12, 2026) documents that one-third of all rural healthcare facilities nationally are at risk of closure, and that a University of North Carolina study found 338 rural hospitals at risk of imminent closure from the OBBBA's Medicaid provisions alone. Rural hospitals serve disproportionately Medicaid-dependent populations — when Medicaid revenue falls, rural hospital financial margins turn negative. The SPLC report documents that rural hospital revenues could drop by at least $1.6 billion following coverage losses from the OBBBA.

When a rural hospital closes, it travels the APSR-documented path: turnout declines in the proximate election, particularly among older and lower-income residents, concentrated in the same communities that already face the most significant structural voting barriers. The OBBBA's rural hospital pressure is simultaneously a healthcare crisis and a democracy crisis in the same geographic spaces.

**Sources**: [The Loss of a Rural Hospital is Devastating for a Local Community — BU SPH](https://www.bu.edu/sph/news/articles/2025/the-loss-of-a-rural-hospital-is-devastating-for-a-local-community/); [SPLC Critical Condition Report](https://www.splcenter.org/resources/reports/critical-condition/); [WSWS Rural Healthcare Collapse May 2026](https://www.wsws.org/en/articles/2026/05/12/pvmy-m12.html); [Time: How America's Health Care System Broke in 2025](https://time.com/7342307/americas-health-care-system-broke-2025/)

## 3. Medical Debt as Civic Exclusion

### 3.1 The Scale and Concentration of Medical Debt

Americans owe at least $220 billion in medical debt. Approximately 14 million adults — about 6% of the adult population — carry over $1,000 in medical debt, and approximately 3 million owe more than $10,000. The burden is not distributed evenly. The states with the highest rates of adults carrying medical debt are South Dakota (17.7%), Mississippi (15.2%), North Carolina (13.4%), West Virginia (13.3%), and Georgia (12.7%) — concentrated in the South and Appalachia, which are also the regions with the most rural hospital closures, the largest populations of OBBBA-vulnerable Medicaid enrollees, and the most restrictive voting access laws.

Medical debt is unlike other consumer debt in its civic consequences. It does not arise from consumption choices — it arises from health emergencies and chronic illness management that individuals did not choose to experience. The CFPB's 2022 analysis documented that medical debt causes residential instability (forcing moves when debt collection pursues assets), credit damage (reducing access to housing and employment), and employment disruption (untreated illness reduces work capacity and the work-connected social networks through which civic information flows). These are precisely the conditions that reduce voter registration and turnout.

The pathway from a medical emergency to electoral non-participation is documentable and short. Medical emergency produces debt; debt produces credit damage; credit damage produces residential instability; residential instability produces voter registration loss (people who move frequently are less likely to update registrations); registration loss produces non-participation. The OBBBA's 11.8 million projected coverage losses will expand the pool of Americans experiencing this pathway. Every person who loses Medicaid coverage becomes more likely to accumulate medical debt when they need care, and more likely to experience the civic exclusion that debt produces.

**Sources**: [The Burden of Medical Debt in the United States — KFF](https://www.kff.org/health-costs/issue-brief/the-burden-of-medical-debt-in-the-united-states/); [Medical Debt Burden in the United States — CFPB](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/data-research/research-reports/medical-debt-burden-in-the-united-states/)

### 3.2 The Policy Reversal

The CFPB's January 2025 final rule would have removed medical debt from the credit reports of 15 million Americans. The Eastern District of Texas vacated the rule in July 2025 after the Trump administration's CFPB leadership joined the plaintiffs requesting vacatur. Simultaneously, in October 2025, the CFPB issued an interpretive rule declaring that the Fair Credit Reporting Act preempts state laws restricting medical debt credit reporting — reversing the policy direction that 11 states had adopted. The state-level protections that Arizona, California, Colorado, Connecticut, New York, and six other states enacted are potentially preempted; 15 million Americans who would have had medical debt removed from credit records remain burdened.

The bipartisan political analysis on medical debt relief is consistent: a survey by Undue Medical Debt documented strong bipartisan support (including in Republican-leaning states) for medical debt forgiveness programs. Twenty-two state or local governments have passed programs to fund roughly $10.2 billion in medical debt relief, including Republican-governed states. This cross-partisan consensus on medical debt relief provides the basis for a democratic reform argument that does not track the partisan lines of healthcare policy broadly.

**Sources**: [CFPB Finalizes Rule to Remove Medical Bills from Credit Reports](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/about-us/newsroom/cfpb-finalizes-rule-to-remove-medical-bills-from-credit-reports/); [Federal Court Vacates CFPB Medical Debt Rule — Brownstein](https://www.bhfs.com/insight/federal-court-vacates-cfpbs-medical-debt-rule-finds-fcra-preempts-state-laws/); [Voters Show Strong Bipartisan Support for Medical Debt Policies — Undue Medical Debt](https://unduemedicaldebt.org/bipartisan-support-for-policies-that-protect-people-from-medical-debt/)

## 4. The NVRA-Medicaid Nexus — Voter Registration Infrastructure at Risk

### 4.1 Medicaid Offices as Statutory Voter Registration Sites

The National Voter Registration Act (52 U.S.C. § 20506) designates public assistance agencies — including Medicaid enrollment offices — as mandatory voter registration sites. States are federally required to offer voter registration at every in-person Medicaid enrollment interaction. This statutory infrastructure makes Medicaid enrollment offices the voter registration access point for low-income populations in a way that no other mechanism replicates at scale.

The challenge is performance: traditional NVRA implementation at public assistance agencies produces approximately 1.6% of all voter registrations nationally, despite serving populations with below-average registration rates. In the 2019-2020 cycle, only 1.7 million voter registration forms were collected by all public assistance agencies combined out of 104 million forms collected nationally. The reason is structural: paper-form NVRA implementation at understaffed offices with competing administrative demands produces low uptake rates.

The solution — Medicaid Automatic Voter Registration — demonstrates dramatically different results. Oregon's Medicaid AVR system reaches approximately 85% of the state's voter-eligible but unregistered population, with young voters and voters of color overrepresented among those reached. Colorado's Medicaid AVR could reach 755,000 currently unregistered eligible voters. As of 2024, eight jurisdictions have passed Medicaid AVR: Colorado, Nevada, Massachusetts, Washington D.C., New Mexico, Minnesota, Oregon, and Michigan. The performance gap between AVR and paper-form NVRA is not marginal — it is a structural difference between a system that works and one that does not.

**Sources**: [Medicaid Automatic Voter Registration Background and Overview — Institute for Responsive Government](https://responsivegov.org/research/medicaid-automatic-voter-registration-background-and-overview-2/); [Using Medicaid AVR to Address Persistent Voter Registration Problems — Institute for Responsive Government](https://responsivegov.org/research/using-medicaid-automatic-voter-registration-to-address-persistent-voter-registration-problems-helping-the-national-voter-registration-act-achieve-its-potential/); [Dr. Oz is Wrong: Medicaid Does Not Automatically Register People to Vote — Poynter](https://www.poynter.org/fact-checking/2026/does-medicaid-automatically-register-voters/)

### 4.2 How OBBBA Degrades the Infrastructure

The OBBBA degrades Medicaid-NVRA voter registration infrastructure through three simultaneous mechanisms.

First, coverage loss reduces the population that contacts Medicaid offices at all. If 11.8 million people lose Medicaid coverage, the contact volume at enrollment offices — the interactions that trigger NVRA Section 7 voter registration offers — declines proportionally. There is no NVRA Section 7 offer for a person who is not interacting with a Medicaid office.

Second, the OBBBA's 6-month recertification requirement (replacing the 12-month baseline) doubles the frequency of administrative contact at the same time that Medicaid agency budgets face compression from reduced enrollment revenue. States that previously processed 12-month renewal cycles at current staffing levels now face twice as many recertification interactions with no commensurate staffing increase. The result: enrollment office staff face more administrative demand, less time per interaction, and less capacity to offer NVRA voter registration meaningfully. During the prior Medicaid continuous enrollment unwinding (April 2023 – June 2024), 69% of disenrollments were procedural — people lost coverage not because they were ineligible but because they could not navigate the administrative burden. Doubling recertification frequency will reproduce this outcome at scale.

Third, the work requirement's administrative infrastructure requires states to build and operate verification systems for 80-hour/month employment documentation. States are allocating administrative capacity to compliance verification, further displacing the NVRA-mandated voter registration function. The June 1, 2026 HHS guidance on work requirement implementation will specify whether states must maintain NVRA Section 7 compliance throughout the work requirement administrative process — or whether that compliance can be deprioritized during the transition. Organizations with NVRA enforcement standing should audit the June 1 rule within 30 days of release and document any Section 7 compliance gaps.

## 5. Maternal Mortality as a Democratic Harm

### 5.1 The Racial Disparity Evidence

Black women in the United States die in childbirth at rates three to four times higher than white women. The 2023 CDC maternal mortality data shows a Black maternal mortality rate of 50.3 deaths per 100,000 live births — compared to rates significantly lower for white women. American Indian and Alaska Native women face similar excess mortality. Two 2025 congressional resolutions — S.Res.67 and H.Res.119 in the 119th Congress — formally declared racism a public health crisis and cited these figures.

The Johns Hopkins Bloomberg School of Public Health's analysis of the Black maternal health crisis documents that these disparities are not primarily explained by clinical differences in pregnancy complications. They are explained by healthcare access disparities, structural racism in clinical settings (documented through studies showing providers systematically underestimate and undertreat Black patients' pain), and social determinants including residential segregation, environmental exposure, and economic precarity. These are features of the healthcare system's design, not of the patients it fails.

**Sources**: [S.Res.67 — Racism as Public Health Crisis (119th Congress)](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-resolution/67/text); [H.Res.119 — Racism as Public Health Crisis (119th Congress)](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-resolution/119/text); [Solving the Black Maternal Health Crisis — JHU Bloomberg SPH](https://publichealth.jhu.edu/2023/solving-the-black-maternal-health-crisis); [Racial Disparities in Maternal and Infant Health — KFF](https://www.kff.org/racial-equity-and-health-policy/racial-disparities-in-maternal-and-infant-health-current-status-and-key-issues/)

### 5.2 The Democratic Harm Argument

This domain's contribution to the maternal mortality conversation — distinct from Domain 44 (Reproductive Freedom), which documents the Dobbs-driven policy cascade and its direct mortality effects — is the democratic harm framing. Maternal mortality is not only a health equity harm. It is a democratic harm.

Women who die in childbirth cannot vote. Women who survive childbirth with severe complications — a category roughly 70 times more common than maternal death — experience long-term health consequences that reduce workforce participation, civic capacity, and economic stability in ways that the broader literature consistently identifies as precursors to reduced civic participation. Communities that experience concentrated maternal mortality bear the grief, economic disruption, and social capital loss of preventable death — a burden that reduces the organizing capacity, political mobilization, and civic infrastructure of precisely the communities whose political power would otherwise most directly challenge the majority arrangements responsible for the inequality.

The communities experiencing concentrated maternal mortality map almost exactly onto the communities facing concentrated voting barriers: majority-Black counties in the South, rural counties in Appalachia and the Mississippi Delta, Indigenous communities in the Great Plains and Alaska. This geographic overlap is not coincidental. It is a map of the democratic feedback loop: the same political arrangements that restrict healthcare access restrict civic participation, preventing the affected communities from electing representatives who would change the healthcare system. Maternal mortality is one measure of whose lives the political system treats as worth protecting.

The Black Maternal Health Momnibus Act (reintroduced in the 119th Congress) provides the legislative vehicle. The Democratic equity argument — not merely the health equity argument — for the Momnibus is: protecting the lives of Black and Indigenous women is protecting the civic voice of their communities.

**Sources**: [The Momnibus Act — Black Maternal Health Caucus](https://blackmaternalhealthcaucus-underwood.house.gov/Momnibus); [Black Maternal Mortality: It's Not Race, It's Racism — JHU](https://ccp.jhu.edu/2021/05/17/maternal-mortality-black-mamas-race-momnibus/); [Center Cheers Reintroduction of Momnibus Act — Center for Reproductive Rights](https://reproductiverights.org/news/center-cheers-reintroduction-black-maternal-health-momnibus-act-us-congress/)

### 5.3 The Rural Maternity Infrastructure Collapse

Between 2020 and 2026, at least 124 rural hospitals eliminated or planned to eliminate labor and delivery units — a 12% reduction in rural maternity infrastructure over six years. This closure trend predates the OBBBA and reflects the economic non-viability of obstetric care in markets where the majority of patients are covered by Medicaid (which reimburses below cost) and the hospital cannot sustain the specialist staffing required for safe delivery.

The OBBBA's Medicaid revenue cuts directly accelerate this trend. If rural hospitals serving disproportionately Medicaid-dependent populations face reduced Medicaid revenue, obstetric units — which require high-cost specialist staffing and cannot achieve the volume efficiency of urban hospital systems — are among the first services to be eliminated. The resulting geographic gap: counties without hospital-based obstetric care require women to travel distances that increase the risk of maternal mortality from time-sensitive emergencies (hemorrhage, eclampsia, sepsis). Black and Indigenous women who already face higher baseline mortality rates are disproportionately concentrated in rural counties where obstetric infrastructure is already thin and thinning.

**Sources**: [How America's Health Care System Broke in 2025 — Time](https://time.com/7342307/americas-health-care-system-broke-2025/); [WSWS: Rural Healthcare Collapse May 2026](https://www.wsws.org/en/articles/2026/05/12/pvmy-m12.html)

## 6. Disability Disenfranchisement — Three Mechanisms

### 6.1 Guardianship and Voting Rights Stripping

Approximately 1.3 million Americans were under guardianship or conservatorship as of 2018 — and the figure has grown since. When courts place an individual under guardianship, they frequently strip voting rights as part of a blanket rights removal that the Center for American Progress has documented is based on outdated, ableist "capacity" determinations. Nine states — Arizona, Louisiana, Mississippi, Missouri, South Carolina, Tennessee, Utah, Virginia, and West Virginia — automatically bar people under guardianship from voting without any individualized assessment of voting capacity. Most remaining states allow courts to strip voting rights based on determinations that use stigmatizing language and procedurally inadequate standards.

The CAP analysis, "Democracy Denied for Disabled Americans," documents that state guardianship statutes overwhelmingly impose full guardianship (which removes all decision-making rights including voting) rather than limited alternatives that would preserve specific rights. This is a structural choice embedded in state law that produces mass civic exclusion without any individualized finding of incapacity specific to voting. At least 52 bills across 20 states have addressed guardianship-based voting rights disenfranchisement since 2023 — demonstrating that the reform movement exists and is active, but has not yet achieved the systemic legislative change necessary.

**Sources**: [Democracy Denied for Disabled Americans: Guardianship and the Right to Vote — CAP](https://www.americanprogress.org/article/democracy-denied-for-disabled-americans-guardianship-and-the-right-to-vote/); [Voting Rights of Institutionalized People with Disabilities — NDRN](https://www.ndrn.org/resource/voting-rights-of-institutionalized-people-with-disabilities/)

### 6.2 The Protection and Advocacy for Voting Accessibility (PAVA) Program Defunding

The Protection and Advocacy for Voting Accessibility (PAVA) program was created by the Help America Vote Act in 2002 to provide legal and technical assistance to voters with disabilities. PAVA programs are administered through the national Protection and Advocacy (P&A) system — the federally funded disability rights legal organizations in every state. PAVA distributes funds to help disabled voters overcome participation barriers: legal assistance navigating voter ID requirements, technical support for accessible ballot equipment, and polling place accessibility compliance work.

The Bipartisan Policy Center's analysis of the P&A system documents that these programs have strengthened community living and voting access for people with disabilities in every state for over two decades. The FY2026 presidential budget proposed zeroing out the P&A and Client Assistance programs — eliminating PAVA and the disability voting access infrastructure it funds. Congress has not enacted this zeroing-out, but the budget proposal signals administrative priority: PAVA is not a program the Trump administration views as worth preserving.

Without PAVA, the primary legal and technical assistance program for disabled voters disappears at exactly the moment when the OBBBA's work requirements, the SAVE Act's documentary proof requirements, and the ADA polling place accessibility enforcement gaps are compounding. Disabled voters face more barriers than any other demographic category; removing the federally funded legal infrastructure designed to help them navigate those barriers is a structural attack on the most structurally excluded voting population.

**Sources**: [The Protection and Advocacy System: Strengthening Community Living and Voting Access for People with Disabilities — Bipartisan Policy Center](https://bipartisanpolicy.org/blog/the-protection-and-advocacy-system-a-little-known-program-strengthening-community-living-and-voting-access-for-people-with-disabilities/); [PAVA Program — NDRN FY2020 Appropriations Request](https://www.ndrn.org/resource/fiscal-year-2020-protection-and-advocacy-for-voting-access-pava-appropriations-request-and-justification/)

### 6.3 OBBBA Work Requirements and Disability: The Exemption Adequacy Problem

The OBBBA work requirements nominally include exemptions for people with disabilities — specifically, the statute exempts individuals who are "medically frail," which it defines to include those who are blind or disabled, have a substance-use disorder, have a disabling mental disorder, have a physical, intellectual, or developmental disability, or have a serious or complex medical condition. This appears protective on its face.

In practice, the exemption architecture has two structural failures that disability advocates have documented.

First, many disabilities that substantially limit work capacity do not fit the statutory categories cleanly. Episodic conditions — migraine disorders, lupus flares, PTSD episodes, multiple sclerosis relapses — cause work limitations that are inconsistent rather than continuous, and the work requirement framework requires monthly documentation of 80-hour compliance or exemption status. People whose conditions are variable across months face administrative burden in continuously demonstrating their exemption status, creating the procedural disenrollment risk that Arkansas's 2018 work requirement demonstrated: 18,000 Medicaid beneficiaries lost coverage in 6 months, with zero measurable employment effect, suggesting the mechanism operated as an eligibility barrier rather than a behavioral incentive.

Second, the American Medical Association and the National Health Law Program have documented that the processes for obtaining exemptions and accommodations create substantial red tape. Prior experience with Medicaid work requirements in Arkansas and Georgia found that people with health conditions affecting their ability to work were disproportionately likely to lose coverage through procedural failures — not because they were ineligible, but because the administrative documentation requirements created barriers they could not overcome. Urban Institute projections document that 3 in 10 young adults would be vulnerable to losing coverage under OBBBA work requirements, with people with health conditions overrepresented among those losing coverage despite being technically exempt.

**Sources**: [Catch Up on Medicaid Work Requirements' Medical Frailty Exemption — AMA](https://www.ama-assn.org/health-care-advocacy/access-care/catch-medicaid-work-requirements-medical-frailty-exemption); [Changes to Medicaid and ACA in OBBBA — AMA](https://www.ama-assn.org/health-care-advocacy/federal-advocacy/changes-medicaid-aca-and-other-key-provisions-one-big); [Projected Reductions in Medicaid Expansion Enrollment Under OBBBA — Urban Institute](https://www.urban.org/research/publication/projected-reductions-medicaid-expansion-enrollment-under-obbbas-work); [Medicaid Work Requirements: Mitigating Harm — National Health Law Program](https://healthlaw.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/12/Machledt_NHeLP_Medicaid-Work-Requirements_MitigatingHarm_112025_Updated.pdf)

## 7. FQHCs as Democratic Infrastructure — The Opportunity and the Threat

### 7.1 The Infrastructure Opportunity

Federally Qualified Health Centers (FQHCs) are the most promising underutilized voter registration infrastructure in the country. Over 1,400 FQHCs operate more than 14,000 service delivery sites, serving approximately 30 million patients annually. The FQHC patient population is 62% racial and ethnic minorities, 90% at or below 200% of the federal poverty level, and 24% best served in a language other than English. These are precisely the populations that face the greatest structural barriers to voter registration through conventional channels.

The Healthy Democracy Kit (HDK) study — published in PMC — documented the largest healthcare-based voter registration initiative to date. Using wearable badges and posters with QR codes directing patients to voter registration resources, the initiative enrolled 13,192 individual healthcare workers and 604 institutions across all 50 states. The results: 27,317 voter registrations initiated, 17,216 mail-in ballot requests initiated. Third-party analysis documented that 84% of participants completed voter registration, with 85% subsequently voting — among the highest participation rates achieved by any voter registration program in comparable populations.

HRSA (the Health Resources and Services Administration) issued guidance encouraging FQHCs to support nonpartisan voter registration efforts. Executive Order 14019 (Biden, 2021) directed federal agencies, including federally funded health centers, to take proactive steps to promote voter registration. A clinician-led registration drive in Bronx FQHCs successfully registered 89% of eligible voters in the 2012 election, reaching disproportionately young (41.9%) and racial and ethnic minority (39.6%) patients.

**Sources**: [Healthy Democracy Kit — PMC 10213595](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10213595/); [Expanding Voter Registration to Clinical Settings — PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10480081/); [Voter Registration and Health Centers — HRSA](https://bphc.hrsa.gov/compliance/scope-project/voter-requirements); [Fact Sheet: Nonpartisan Voter Registration at Health Care Institutions — AAMC](https://www.aamchealthjustice.org/our-work/policy/voter-fact-sheet)

### 7.2 The January 2025 Rescission

Executive Order 14148, signed January 20, 2025, revoked Executive Order 14019. All agencies — including HRSA and the Indian Health Service — were required to immediately cease implementing voter registration activities under EO 14019 and withdraw voter registration agency status. The Indian Health Service issued a specific notice: "In accordance with Presidential Executive Order 14148, the Indian Health Service withdraws its voter registration agency status at all Federal sites, effective immediately." This single action removed the voter registration infrastructure from every Indian Health Service site in the country — impacting the Indigenous population, which already faces among the most significant voting barriers of any demographic group in the United States.

The HRSA guidance encouraging FQHCs to support voter registration was withdrawn. FQHCs can still voluntarily conduct nonpartisan voter registration under the NVRA and their health center program authority — the rescission of EO 14019 removed the mandate, not the permission. But the removal of the federal mandate removed the institutional legitimacy, training resources, and federal support that translated the permission into practice. The 700+ Vot-ER healthcare sites and the AAMC nonpartisan voter registration infrastructure now operate without federal backing, dependent on philanthropic and institutional support rather than federal direction.

**Sources**: [Trump Revokes Biden's Voter Registration Executive Order — NPR](https://www.npr.org/2025/01/20/nx-s1-5169190/biden-voter-registration-executive-order); [Indian Health Service Withdraws Voter Registration Agency Status — IHS 2025 Announcements](https://www.ihs.gov/newsroom/announcements/2025-announcements/in-accordance-with-presidential-executive-order-14148-the-indian-health-service-withdraws-its-voter-registration-agency-status-at-all-federal-sites-effective-immediately/); [Status of Trump's Anti-Voting Executive Orders — Brennan Center](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/status-trumps-anti-voting-executive-order)

## 8. The OBBBA as Democratic Infrastructure Attack — Five Pathways Converging

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act does not attack any single healthcare-democracy pathway. It attacks all five simultaneously, compounding the democratic exclusion effects in the same geographic and demographic spaces.

**Pathway 1 — Rural hospitals**: OBBBA Medicaid revenue cuts threaten the 734 rural hospitals at risk of closure. The APSR finding documents the civic consequence: hospital closure reduces voter turnout in proximate elections among older and lower-income residents. OBBBA accelerates the hospital-closure pathway.

**Pathway 2 — Medical debt**: OBBBA projects 11.8 million coverage losses. Every person who loses Medicaid and subsequently needs healthcare without coverage accumulates medical debt. The CFPB's vacated rule and preempted state protections leave that debt on credit reports. Medical debt produces the economic precarity, residential instability, and time burden that reduce civic participation.

**Pathway 3 — NVRA voter registration infrastructure**: OBBBA's coverage losses reduce the population contacting Medicaid offices, reducing NVRA Section 7 voter registration contact volume. OBBBA's 6-month recertification doubles administrative burden without commensurate staffing, reducing capacity for meaningful voter registration offers. OBBBA's work requirement administrative infrastructure crowds out NVRA compliance capacity.

**Pathway 4 — Maternal mortality**: OBBBA's rural hospital Medicaid cuts accelerate obstetric unit closures in rural areas serving disproportionately Black and Indigenous women. OBBBA's coverage losses reduce prenatal care access for uninsured or coverage-lapsing low-income women. Both increase maternal mortality concentrated in communities with already-elevated civic exclusion.

**Pathway 5 — Disability disenfranchisement**: OBBBA work requirements create inadequate exemption documentation burdens for people with episodic or hard-to-document disabilities. The FY2026 presidential budget proposes zeroing PAVA. Together, these threaten the legal support infrastructure that helps disabled voters navigate participation barriers.

The political geography of these five pathways is also convergent. The states bearing the greatest healthcare-democracy harm from OBBBA — majority-Black, majority-rural, non-expansion Southern states — are also the states where Callais redistricting is eliminating majority-minority districts, where voter suppression statutes are most restrictive, and where voting rights litigation is most active. The healthcare-democracy analysis is not a separate track from the voting rights analysis. It is the same track, viewed from the public health angle.

## 9. Reform Architecture

### 9.1 Immediate — HHS Work Requirement Guidance (Comment Period Open Now)

The HHS interim final rule on OBBBA work requirement implementation is due June 1, 2026. The comment period likely opened April-May 2026. Healthcare advocacy organizations, disability rights organizations, and democratic reform organizations should submit coordinated comments that:

- Advocate for broad exemption definitions, including explicit coverage for episodic conditions, mental health conditions, and any condition that affects work capacity for any portion of a month
- Require NVRA Section 7 compliance throughout the work requirement administrative process — specifically, that states must offer voter registration at every work requirement notification, documentation submission, and administrative appeal interaction
- Mandate that 100% accurate eligibility determination be achieved before any disenrollment for work requirement non-compliance, consistent with the Arkansas litigation precedent documenting that disenrollment occurred for procedural rather than substantive reasons
- Request 2-year extensions for states with high disenrollment rates during prior Medicaid continuous enrollment unwinding (2023-2024), on the grounds that those states have demonstrated inadequate administrative capacity for the documentation burden work requirements impose

The democratic reform community's engagement in the HHS comment period is the most time-sensitive action available. This is the advocacy window the domain was designed to support.

### 9.2 NVRA Section 7 Enforcement at Medicaid Offices

State attorneys general in the 23-state coalition that has litigated executive voting rights actions have standing to enforce NVRA Section 7 against states that fail to offer voter registration during Medicaid administrative interactions. The theory, documented in Domain 31's May 13 addition and in Domain 50's Section 5.2: when states conduct Medicaid disenrollment proceedings without offering voter registration, they are violating NVRA Section 7 in those interactions. Disenrollment-at-scale under OBBBA work requirements creates mass NVRA Section 7 violation events.

State AG enforcement should be targeted at the six states in post-Callais redistricting special sessions (Alabama, Tennessee, Louisiana, South Carolina, Mississippi, Florida) — which are simultaneously the states with the highest Medicaid disenrollment impact from OBBBA work requirements and the worst records of NVRA Section 7 compliance. The geographic overlap creates a single enforcement action that addresses both the healthcare-democracy and the redistricting-democracy attacks in the same jurisdictions.

### 9.3 FQHC Voter Registration — Restore and Expand

EO 14019 should be restored by a future Democratic administration. In the interim, FQHCs in states where state law permits can voluntarily adopt voter registration as a standard element of patient intake, and the Vot-ER 700+ site network can expand through philanthropic support. State-level NVRA enforcement should require that all public assistance agencies (including FQHCs that receive state Medicaid matching funds) offer voter registration at all patient contacts.

The AAMC's nonpartisan voter registration fact sheet and the Healthy Democracy Kit provide ready-made implementation tools that FQHCs can adopt independently of federal direction. The Vot-ER badge-and-QR-code model requires minimal staff training and integrates with existing clinical workflows without creating additional administrative burden. Healthcare organizations that cannot engage in voter registration advocacy can implement the technical infrastructure (badges, posters, digital intake forms) without characterizing the activity as advocacy.

### 9.4 Guardianship Reform and PAVA Restoration

State guardianship statutes should be reformed to prohibit automatic voting rights stripping without individualized capacity assessment specific to voting — an assessment that the research consistently shows the vast majority of people under guardianship would pass. Preferred alternative: states should adopt supported decision-making frameworks that preserve autonomy and rights, eliminating full guardianship as the default.

PAVA program funding should be restored and increased through the appropriations process. The Protection and Advocacy for Voting Accessibility program is the infrastructure that makes disability voting rights statutes enforceable in practice. Congress should reject the presidential budget's zero-out and appropriate PAVA at least at its prior levels, with additional funding for OBBBA implementation monitoring.

### 9.5 Medical Debt Relief

The CFPB rule barring medical debt from credit reports should be restored through either judicial challenge to the vacatur or re-issuance under a future administration. States should continue to enact medical debt credit reporting protections, and the administration's October 2025 FCRA preemption interpretive rule should be challenged in court by the 11 states whose laws it purports to preempt. Hospital charity care standards — requiring income-based charity care before debt collection — should be strengthened through state AG enforcement of existing IRS nonprofit hospital requirements under Section 501(r).

### 9.6 Medicaid Expansion as Voter Registration Infrastructure

The 12 states that have not expanded Medicaid — all of which are in the South and concentrated in the same geographic regions as the greatest voter suppression — should be targeted for expansion advocacy with the democratic participation evidence as the primary argument for audiences in the democratic reform movement. Expansion increases the population with Medicaid enrollment office contacts, increases the NVRA Section 7 voter registration volume, and reduces the medical debt that the APSR and Oregon Experiment findings connect to reduced civic participation.
