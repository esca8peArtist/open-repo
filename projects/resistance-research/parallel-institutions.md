## The Most Important Finding

The United States already has a substantial parallel institutional infrastructure — community land trusts, worker cooperatives, credit unions, mutual aid networks, and state-level policy alternatives — that is larger, better-resourced, and more politically significant than most resistance strategy discussions acknowledge. The Champlain Housing Trust in Burlington, Vermont, stewards 18% of that city's residential units. The US CDFI credit union sector holds $277 billion in assets serving 18.3 million members. There are approximately 900-1,000 worker cooperatives employing 10,000 worker-owners with $806 million in revenue. These are not symbolic alternatives — they are functional institutions at meaningful scale.

The strategic question is not whether parallel institutions exist, but whether they can be built to the scale and political coherence that makes them functional institutional anchors during a democratic recovery window. The comparative cases (Document 1) identified institutional anchors as the third decisive variable in recovery. Spain's monarchy, Uruguay's political parties, South Korea's religious institutions and middle class — each provided an independent organizational base that the regime could not fully capture. The parallel institutional landscape in the US is a potential source of those anchors, but its fragmentation, funding vulnerability, and political incoherence currently limit its function to resistance, not recovery.

## Introduction: What Parallel Institutions Are and Why They Matter

In political science, parallel institutions are organizational structures that perform the functions of state institutions — resource allocation, dispute resolution, governance, welfare provision — outside the formal state apparatus. They matter in the context of democratic recovery for two distinct reasons.

The first is the prefigurative function: parallel institutions demonstrate that alternative forms of organization are viable, building the cultural, organizational, and material capacity for a post-recovery institutional order. Spain's cooperative movement (Mondragon was founded in 1956, under Franco) built the organizational infrastructure for democratic economic governance before Spain's democratic transition. Uruguay's political parties maintained their organizational coherence through the civic-military dictatorship, enabling rapid reconstitution when political conditions changed.

The second is the pressure function: parallel institutions reduce the captured state's monopoly on material welfare provision, creating exit options that weaken the state's coercive leverage over populations dependent on its services. When people have access to food, housing, credit, and childcare outside the captured state apparatus, the captured state's threat to withdraw those services has less political leverage.

This document maps the current US parallel institutional landscape across five sectors — community land trusts, worker cooperatives, credit unions, mutual aid networks, and state-level policy infrastructure — assesses their current scale and governance models, identifies scaling pathways, and draws on international case studies (Spain, Uruguay) to identify what makes parallel institutions politically effective versus merely economically useful.

## Section 1: Community Land Trusts — What They Are, What Scale Looks Like

### The Model

A community land trust (CLT) is a nonprofit organization that acquires land and holds it in perpetual community ownership, while selling or leasing the structures on that land to individual households or businesses. The key mechanism: by separating land ownership (communal, permanent) from building ownership (individual, resalable), CLTs permanently remove land from the speculative market. Housing built on CLT land remains permanently affordable because the resale price is limited by a formula that preserves affordability for subsequent buyers while allowing the current occupant to build equity.

The governance structure reflects democratic ownership: CLTs are typically governed by tripartite boards with equal representation from current CLT residents, community members who live in the CLT's service area, and public interest representatives (local government, funders, nonprofits). This is institutional democracy in practice — community members with voting power over land use decisions that directly affect them.

### Scale: What the Champlain Housing Trust Demonstrates

The Champlain Housing Trust (CHT) in Burlington, Vermont, is the world's largest CLT and the most important proof-of-concept for CLT scaling. Founded in 1984 by the Burlington Community Land Trust with direct support from Mayor Bernie Sanders's municipal government, CHT now stewards over 2,400 low-cost rental properties, 600 for-purchase units, 115 housing cooperatives, plus commercial spaces — representing approximately 18% of Burlington's total residential units. ([Champlain Housing Trust / CLT Web](https://www.cltweb.org/resources/clt-case-studies/champlain-housing-trust/); [RioOnWatch, Champlain Housing Trust at Scale](https://rioonwatch.org/?p=71474))

CHT demonstrates three things about scaling:

First, CLTs can reach genuine municipal scale with sustained political support. The Burlington model worked because the city government provided land grants and financial support over decades, creating a foundation that enabled CHT to grow through subsequent market cycles. This is the Spain model: the Mondragon cooperatives benefited from the specific social-material context of the Basque Country, but also from sustained institutional choices — a credit cooperative (Caja Laboral, founded 1959) that provided preferential financing to member cooperatives, and an educational system that trained cooperative workers.

Second, governance at scale creates real challenges. CHT's own documentation identifies participatory management as "one of our greatest concerns" as the organization has expanded geographically. For more distant communities, governance mechanisms require adaptation — regional planning commission representatives, accommodation of board members who cannot easily travel. This is not a failure of the model; it is evidence that the model is actually practicing democratic governance rather than maintaining it as a formal structure while centralizing real decisions.

Third, the Burlington example shows that municipal government commitment is the primary scaling variable. Cities that have committed land, financing, and policy support have CLTs. Cities that have not, generally do not. New York City's CLT movement has grown from 2 CLTs twelve years ago to more than 20 CLTs across all five boroughs — driven by City Hall policy commitments and community organizing. ([Nonprofit Quarterly, CLT Movement in NYC](https://nonprofitquarterly.org/a-community-land-trust-movement-rises-in-new-york-city-leadership-lessons/); [Sanders Institute, CLTs Then and Now](https://sandersinstitute.org/community-land-trusts-then-and-now))

### Scaling Pathway

The most direct scaling pathway for CLTs in the current political context is state-level land acquisition programs, since federal housing policy is being dismantled. States with Democratic governors and legislatures — California, New York, Colorado, Minnesota, Illinois — have the capacity to establish state-level CLT funding programs and land transfer mechanisms that could dramatically accelerate CLT formation. This is parallel institution building using existing state authority, not requiring federal action.

The political effect: as the percentage of housing in community land trust ownership grows in a municipality, housing insecurity as a political coercive tool (landlords threatening eviction, housing speculation displacing communities) becomes less effective against CLT residents who have permanent affordability protections. The Uruguay parallel is apt: the pre-existing organizational resilience of political parties meant that repression could not eliminate the organizational base of opposition. CLT residents have housing security that makes them more resistant to economic coercion.

## Section 2: Worker Cooperatives — Democratic Governance at Work

### The Mondragon Model and Its Limits

Mondragon Corporation, founded in the Basque Country of Spain in 1956 under the Franco dictatorship by the cooperative pioneer Father José María Arizmendiarrieta, is the world's largest federation of worker-owned cooperatives. With over 70,000 worker-owners across 81 cooperatives in 2025, Mondragon demonstrates that democratic workplace governance can operate at the scale of a major multinational corporation. ([Mondragon Corporation Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mondragon_Corporation); [CS Monitor, Mondragon in 2024](https://www.csmonitor.com/Business/2024/0513/income-inequality-capitalism-mondragon-corporation))

The governance architecture: an elected General Assembly of 650 worker-members meets annually to set policy and review governance bodies. A Governing Council of 100 worker-members reviews individual cooperative actions. The Directorship, Permanent Commission, Social Council, and Commission on Vigilance all include worker-members. Decision-making is on the principle of one worker, one vote. Wage compression is a structural commitment: the income ratio between highest and lowest paid is capped at 6:1, compared to 344:1 at typical US corporations.

The critical political context: Mondragon was founded under an authoritarian government. It was not a utopian project built after a democratic transition — it was a democratic economic institution built within a dictatorship, demonstrating that democratic organizational forms can incubate during periods of political capture. This is directly relevant to the current US context. Worker cooperatives built now are building democratic organizational capacity that survives and potentially shapes what comes after.

Mondragon's limits are also instructive. Recent scholarly analysis describes a "coopitalist" transformation: Mondragon's largest industrial cooperatives maintain a cooperative core of worker-members while operating capitalist subsidiaries employing wage workers without membership rights. The revenue pressures of global competition have eroded the cooperative structure at the margins — the challenge that any cooperative movement faces when it grows large enough to compete with multinationals in global markets. ([De Gruyter, Mondragon Cooperatives in Global Capitalism, 2025](https://www.degruyterbrill.com/document/doi/10.1515/auk-2025-2001/html))

The US context is different: US worker cooperatives are primarily small (median revenue significantly below Mondragon's constituent cooperatives), concentrated in specific sectors (homecare, cleaning, food production, consulting), and face different competitive pressures. The question is not whether US cooperatives can reach Mondragon scale but whether they can reach the scale at which they constitute a significant sector of the economy and a significant employer of politically-engaged worker-owners.

### Current US Worker Cooperative Landscape

The 2025 State of the Sector report from the Democracy at Work Institute and the US Federation of Worker Cooperatives documents 900-1,000 worker cooperatives in the US, employing approximately 10,000 worker-owners and generating $806 million in annual revenue. The sector is growing — longitudinal data shows consistent growth over a decade, with the biggest recent spike in business consulting as independent contractors form democratically-run cooperatives. Driver cooperatives (Uber/Lyft alternatives) represent another significant growth vector. ([USFWC, 2025 State of the Sector](https://www.usworker.coop/census/))

The governance model is straightforward: workers buy in (typically at low cost, with financing options), have voting rights on major decisions, share profits proportionally, and can remove management through democratic processes. The practical effect on participating workers is documented: higher wages, better working conditions, greater job security, and direct experience with democratic decision-making as a daily practice.

### Scaling Pathway

The primary barrier to worker cooperative growth is capital access, not organizational viability. Traditional banking does not lend to early-stage cooperatives without personal collateral requirements that most workers cannot meet. The remedy is cooperative development finance — lending institutions that understand cooperative capital structures and can provide startup and growth capital without requiring individual worker collateral.

This is where the CDFI sector (Section 3 below) intersects with cooperative development: CDFI credit unions and loan funds have been the primary source of cooperative capital. The Sustainable Economies Law Center and similar organizations provide free legal clinic services to cooperative development. ([Resilience.org, Chicago Solidarity Economy Legal Clinic, February 2025](https://www.resilience.org/stories/2025-02-05/this-clinic-in-chicago-offers-free-legal-aid-to-solidarity-economy-groups/))

The political effect: a worker cooperative sector employing not 10,000 but 1 million worker-owners would be a significant organized constituency for democratic economic governance, with direct material interests in the institutional structures that protect cooperative forms (contract enforcement, labor law, antitrust enforcement, credit access). It would be analogous to the labor union movement's role in mid-20th century democratic politics — an organized mass constituency with institutional interests aligned with democratic governance norms.

## Section 3: Credit Unions and CDFIs — Democratic Finance at Scale

### The Current Landscape

Credit unions are member-owned, democratically governed financial cooperatives. Each member has one vote regardless of deposit size. Earnings are returned to members through lower loan rates, higher savings yields, and reduced fees rather than extracted as shareholder profits. The credit union sector in the US serves 140 million members with approximately $2 trillion in total assets.

The CDFI (Community Development Financial Institution) subset of this landscape is the most politically significant: 1,378 certified CDFIs held $446 billion in assets as of Q2 2025, with CDFI-certified credit unions holding $277 billion — roughly 62% of total CDFI assets. These institutions serve 18.3 million members representing 12.6% of total credit union membership nationwide. From 2018 to 2023, CDFI assets tripled to more than $450 billion. ([Federal Reserve Bank of New York, Sizing the CDFI Industry, 2025](https://www.newyorkfed.org/outreach-and-education/household-financial-stability/sizing-the-community-development-financial-institution-industry-2011-2025); [New York Fed, CDFI Modest Decline Report, February 2026](https://www.newyorkfed.org/newsevents/news/regional_outreach/2026/20260211))

### The Governance Difference

The governance structure of credit unions matters beyond economics. A member who votes for their credit union's board of directors at an annual meeting has a direct democratic ownership experience with their financial institution. A member who accesses credit at rates below commercial banks has a material interest in the institution's survival. These are not trivial facts: they represent the kind of material organizational commitment to democratic institutional forms that the comparative cases identify as essential for institutional resilience.

The contrast with commercial banking is stark: a Wells Fargo account holder has no governance rights and no organizational loyalty — the institution's interests are defined by shareholder returns, not member welfare. The credit union structure creates aligned interests between the institution and its members that are structurally different from the commercial banking relationship.

### Political Effects

Credit unions have demonstrated political effectiveness in a specific domain: defending their tax-exempt status and regulatory independence against commercial bank lobbying. This is not progressive political activism — it is institutional self-interest. But it demonstrates that credit unions are capable of organized political action to protect their institutional form, which is the political capacity relevant to the recovery framework.

### Scaling Pathway

The most important scaling pathway for the democratic finance sector is CDFI Fund survival. The CDFI Fund — the federal program that certifies and provides capital to CDFIs — is a direct target of the current administration's federal program eliminations. The February 2026 New York Fed report showing a modest decline in CDFI assets is a leading indicator of what happens when CDFI Fund support is reduced. The defense of the CDFI Fund is simultaneously a defense of the alternative finance sector and a test case for state-level replacement: states can establish state CDFI funds that perform similar certification and capitalization functions without federal support.

## Section 4: Mutual Aid Networks — Distributed Governance Infrastructure

### What Mutual Aid Is

Mutual aid is reciprocal care: community members directly meeting each other's needs, outside the mediation of state welfare systems or market transactions. The COVID-19 pandemic produced a documented explosion of mutual aid formation in the US: communities organized food distribution, medication delivery, rent assistance, childcare, and emotional support networks with remarkable speed.

The Georgetown Beeck Center's analysis of mutual aid during COVID-19 identifies four key takeaways: (1) mutual aid filled genuine gaps in state capacity, particularly for communities of color; (2) the organizational form (non-hierarchical, participatory, consensus-based) created strong relational bonds between participants; (3) digital tools dramatically reduced the coordination costs of mutual aid formation; and (4) post-pandemic sustainability requires transitioning from crisis response to durable organizational form. ([Beeck Center, Four Key Takeaways from Mutual Aid Organizing, Georgetown](https://beeckcenter.georgetown.edu/four-key-takeaways-from-mutual-aid-organizing-during-the-covid-19-pandemic/))

Mutual Aid Disaster Relief (MADR) is the closest thing to a national mutual aid coordination infrastructure: a grassroots network providing training, resources, and coordination across local mutual aid groups with an explicitly anti-hierarchical and anti-statist orientation. ([Mutual Aid Disaster Relief, About](https://mutualaiddisasterrelief.org/about/))

### Scale and Limitations

The limitations of mutual aid as a parallel governance institution are real and must be stated clearly: mutual aid networks are, by design, non-hierarchical, non-bureaucratic, and locally isolated. These features create strong local relational bonds but create significant challenges for scaling and political coordination. The research identifies: lack of technical experts, lack of sustainable funding, lack of public legitimacy, and the potential for informal social hierarchies to replicate within ostensibly flat structures.

The 2026 framing captures the strategic value accurately: "The more we build solidarity at a community level, the stronger position we have to resist autocratic moves coming from the federal level. It is hard to rule over communities with a strong sense of self-awareness." ([Resilience.org, We Are in a Crisis, January 2026](https://www.resilience.org/stories/2026-01-16/we-are-in-a-crisis-how-to-build-communities-that-can-respond/)) This is a resistance and resilience function, not a replacement state function. Mutual aid creates community capacity that makes communities less dependent on and less controlled by captured state institutions.

### Political Effect in Democratic Recovery

The political effect of mutual aid in the recovery context is indirect but important. Communities with robust mutual aid networks have demonstrated organizational capacity — the ability to identify needs, coordinate resources, and distribute them fairly without formal authority. This organizational capacity is transferable: a community that organized food distribution in 2020 has the relational infrastructure and the organizational experience to organize electoral mobilization, legal observer networks, or community safety teams when the political situation demands it.

The Uruguay parallel is instructive: the political parties that survived the civic-military dictatorship did so in part by maintaining social networks and informal organizational capacity that had nothing specifically to do with electoral politics. When the political opening came, those networks were the infrastructure through which rapid reconstitution was possible. US mutual aid networks are, in embryonic form, that kind of parallel social infrastructure.

## Section 5: State-Level Policy Infrastructure — Parallel Governance with Constitutional Authority

### What States Can Do That the Above Cannot

Community land trusts, worker cooperatives, credit unions, and mutual aid networks are all civil society institutions: powerful but operating outside the formal authority structures of government. States are something different: they are constitutionally-grounded parallel governance institutions with binding legal authority, tax and spending power, and law enforcement capacity.

The current state-level resistance infrastructure is already functioning as a form of parallel governance:

**California** has maintained its own air quality standards under the Clean Air Act's California waiver, effectively operating a parallel environmental regulatory system that serves as a model for other states. Its climate policy, reproductive health protections, and labor standards are substantively different from federal policy in the same domains.

**State AG coalitions** (22 states plus DC in the elections-administration lawsuits) are using state constitutional authority to resist federal overreach that the federal courts have partially blocked but not fully remediated. ([Rhode Island AG Coalition Filing, 2026](https://riag.ri.gov/press-releases/attorney-general-neronha-coalition-sue-trump-administration-over-unlawful-executive))

**State-level data preservation**: States with their own public health departments, environmental monitoring agencies, and labor statistics agencies can maintain data collection that federal agencies are dismantling. This is exactly the digital parallel institution function — preserving the informational infrastructure that captured federal agencies are destroying.

**Interstate compacts** represent the underused constitutional tool here: states can enter into interstate agreements that create de facto national policy in specific domains without federal legislation. The National Popular Vote Interstate Compact is an example: a state-level mechanism for changing presidential election rules without a constitutional amendment. Health, environment, labor, and financial regulation compacts are all theoretically available.

### The CHP Municipal Model, Adapted

Turkey's CHP municipalities provide an instructive comparison with a critical caveat: CHP's municipal victories demonstrated that the opposition retained popular support but could not convert that support into national-level institutional change because municipalities cannot reverse judicial capture or constitutional amendments. The US states are constitutionally stronger than Turkish municipalities — they have reserved powers, independent constitutional status, and treaty-making capacity through interstate compacts that Turkish municipalities entirely lack.

The difference means that US state-level parallel governance can do more than demonstrate competent administration — it can create legal facts on the ground (court precedents, data preservation, constitutional litigation records) that constrain the captured federal government in ways that Turkish municipalities could not constrain Erdogan.

## Section 6: The Integrated Picture — What Scales Quickly and What Doesn't

The solidarity economy framework integrates the above sectors: CLTs, cooperatives, credit unions, and mutual aid networks are not separate initiatives but components of an alternative economic and social infrastructure that collectively reduces dependence on the captured state and captured markets. ([Shelterforce, What Is the Solidarity Economy?, February 2026](https://shelterforce.org/2026/02/26/what-is-the-solidarity-economy/))

**What scales quickly given political will:**
- CLTs: Can be dramatically accelerated by state land grants and city-level policy commitments. New York City's growth from 2 to 20+ CLTs in 12 years demonstrates the speed possible with political support.
- Credit unions and CDFIs: Existing infrastructure, existing regulatory framework, existing membership base. The primary need is capital (defense of the CDFI Fund or state-level replacement) and awareness.
- State policy alternatives: Already operating. The scaling need is political coordination among Democratic states to act as a coherent policy bloc rather than separately.

**What scales slowly but builds durability:**
- Worker cooperatives: Capital access constraints and conversion complexity mean growth is measured in years. But 10x growth (100,000 worker-owners) is plausible within five years with sustained cooperative development finance and technical assistance.
- Mutual aid networks: Scale is not the primary variable — depth, relational resilience, and organizational capacity are. A network that survives a five-year period of repression and emerges with intact relationships and skills is more valuable than a large but shallow network.

**What doesn't scale but is still essential:**
- Prefigurative democratic practice: The governance experience of CLT boards, cooperative general assemblies, credit union member elections, and mutual aid consensus processes builds the cultural and organizational capacity for democratic governance. At small scale, this is symbolically important. At large scale, it is materially constitutive of a democratic civic culture.

## Conclusion: Parallel Institutions as Recovery Infrastructure

The Document 1 finding — that movements without an institutional blueprint lose their gains — implies that the parallel institutional landscape must be developed into a coherent blueprint, not left as a collection of inspiring experiments.

The blueprint has three layers:

**Layer 1 (exists now, needs defense):** CDFIs and credit unions at $446 billion in assets; state-level policy infrastructure; state AG coalition litigation record.

**Layer 2 (exists, needs scaling):** CLTs (from 20+ in NYC to hundreds nationally, from 18% of Burlington to 10% of major cities); worker cooperatives (from 1,000 to 10,000 enterprises); mutual aid networks (from crisis response to durable community governance infrastructure).

**Layer 3 (needs creation):** An interstate compact architecture that converts state-level policy alternatives into a formal parallel federal structure; a cooperative development finance system that can provide the capital for Layer 2 scaling; and a political coordination mechanism among Democratic states that moves beyond reactive litigation toward affirmative institutional construction.

The Spain comparison is precise: Mondragon's cooperatives, built under Franco, provided the economic infrastructure through which democratic participation became a material daily reality for workers in the Basque Country before Spain's democratic transition. The US alternative institutional landscape is at an earlier and more fragmented stage — but the components exist. The recovery framework requires converting those components into a coherent architecture that is ready to expand rapidly when the political opportunity opens.
