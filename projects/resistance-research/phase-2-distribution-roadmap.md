# Phase 2 Domain Sequencing and Distribution Roadmap

**Prepared**: May 5, 2026  
**Scope**: Domains 38–41 (seven Tier 1+2 candidates, one Tier 3 cluster). Phase 1 (35-domain framework) is confirmed production-ready and awaiting path decision (A / A+37 / B). This document is independent of that decision — Phase 2 sequencing analysis applies regardless of which path Phase 1 takes.

**Key external events shaping this roadmap (as of today)**:
- FISA Section 702: 45-day extension passed April 30, 2026 (House 261-111; Senate unanimous consent). June 12, 2026 hard deadline is confirmed.
- Callais ruling: SCOTUS 6-3 decision (April 29) strips VRA Section 2 discriminatory-effects standard. Louisiana suspending May 16 primary for new maps. Florida passed new maps same day. Implementation underway.
- SAVE Act: Stalled in Senate (requires 60 votes; Republicans at 53). Advocacy window is open and holding.
- Trump v. Barbara: Oral arguments concluded April 1, 2026. Ruling pending (expected June/July 2026).

## Part 1: Sequencing Decision Tree — Adoption-Milestone-Based Gating

Phase 2 domain release is governed by two independent sequencing tracks: (A) a calendar-forced track for domains with hard external deadlines, and (B) a feedback-gated track for domains whose optimal release window depends on Phase 1 adoption signals. The tracks run in parallel. No domain on Track A waits for Track B gate clearance.

### Track A: Calendar-Forced (External Deadline Governs)

These domains are production-gated only — they deploy as soon as production is complete, regardless of Phase 1 adoption metrics.

```
[May 15–22] Produce Domain 38-A (Intel Oversight)
     |
     v
[May 25 – June 5] Distribute Domain 38-A
     |
     |-- Gate: June 12 FISA deadline fires
     |         Condition A (3-year extension without warrant req): Domain frames democratic failure; distribute immediately
     |         Condition B (lapse): Distribute before lapse with "what happens next" analysis
     |         Condition C (warrant requirement included): Adjust framing; distribute as accountability frame going forward
     v
[May 20 – June 10] Produce Domain 38-B (Voting Systems / post-Callais VRA)
     |
     v
[June 15–30] Distribute Domain 38-B to election protection network
     |
     v
[Begin immediately; pause SCOTUS section] Produce Domain 40-B (Tribal Sovereignty)
     |
     |-- Gate: Trump v. Barbara ruling (expected June/July)
     |         Condition A (confirms 14th Amendment citizenship): Finalize + distribute within 2 weeks
     |         Condition B (narrow/avoids tribal question): Distribute pre-ruling stakes document; finalize after
     |         Condition C (undermines Indian Citizenship Act): Emergency Tier 1 escalation — NARF and NCAI within 72 hours
     v
[July 1–15] Distribute Domain 40-B (within 2 weeks of ruling)
```

### Track B: Feedback-Gated (Phase 1 Signal Governs Release Priority)

These domains have flexible windows. Their queue position adjusts based on which Phase 1 domains generated the most institutional pull.

```

     |

     |
     |-- Signal C: 2+ contacts ask about surveillance / FISA without prompting
     |         → Intel Oversight (38-A) confirmed Tier 1; proceed with planned Track A sequence
     |
     |-- Signal D: Health-sector contacts ask about OBBBA / Medicaid beyond Domain 31
     |         → Elevate phase-2-domain-candidate-1-medicaid-crisis.md addendum; targeted distribution to health contacts
     v
[July 1–15] Distribute Domain 41-B (Disability Rights) — SAVE Act Senate advocacy window
     |
     v
[July 20–30] Distribute Domain 40-C (Fiscal Authority) + Domain 39-B (Labor Rights) in coordinated wave
     |
     v
[July 1 – August 5] Produce Domain 39-A (Reproductive Rights)
     |
     v
```

### Adoption-Threshold Gating Summary

| Threshold Trigger | Action |
|---|---|
| Phase 1 Tier 1 open rate 40%+ at Day 7 | Proceed with planned Track B sequence; depth-first follow-up with engaged contacts |
| Phase 1 Tier 1 open rate below 25% | Investigate delivery issues before assessing content fit; do not conclude content is failing |
| Phase 1 reply rate 3%+ from election sector | Domain 38-B distribution confirmed; accelerate Track A |
| Phase 1 reply rate below 1% at confirmed delivery | Reframe opening paragraphs in Phase 2 outreach using EMAIL_PERSONALIZATION_GUIDE variants |
| Power-law concentration: 3–5 contacts generating 80% of referrals | Focus Phase 2 follow-up energy on those contacts; do not distribute evenly |
| 3+ contacts identify same domain gap (e.g., FISA) | Feedback-triggered Tier elevation per phase-2-domain-prioritization-matrix.md Part IV |

## Part 2: Sector-Specific Distribution Sequences

Phase 2 does not distribute uniformly. Each domain reaches its highest-leverage sector first.

### Domain 38-A (Intel Oversight / FISA 702) — June window

**Primary sector**: Senate Judiciary and Intelligence Committee staff (both parties). The June 12 FISA deadline is a live legislative clock — reach these contacts no later than the week of May 25.  
**Secondary sector**: ACLU, EFF, Brennan Center Liberty and National Security Program. Cross-reference their active FISA litigation and FOIA records in the cover email.  
**Tertiary sector**: Law school national security law clinics (Harvard, Yale, Georgetown). The FISC declassified opinion (per the April 30 Senate deal) should be integrated into the domain document once released.  
**Cross-partisan note**: Cato Institute, Reason Foundation, and libertarian Republican Senate offices (Paul, Lee) are natural distribution nodes. This is the domain with the widest right-of-center reach in the entire Phase 2 set.  
**Sequence**: Senate → ACLU/EFF → Clinics → Cato/Reason. Do not invert this order — Senate contacts need to receive the framework before it reaches advocacy organizations, to avoid the "advocacy document not policy analysis" perception.

### Domain 38-B (Voting Systems / post-Callais VRA) — June–July window

**Primary sector**: Election protection organizations — Democracy Docket, Campaign Legal Center, NAACP LDF, ACLU Voting Rights Project, Brennan Center Elections Program. These organizations are already in active post-Callais litigation and strategic planning.  
**Secondary sector**: State AG offices with active voting rights litigation (Michigan, Georgia, North Carolina, Wisconsin). Frame Domain 38-B as a litigation support document for their existing Callais-response strategy.  
**Tertiary sector**: Congressional Black Caucus and Senate Judiciary Democrats. The John Lewis VRA restoration bill (S.2523 / H.R.14) frames the legislative vehicle; the domain document provides the constitutional design argument behind it.  
**Academic anchor**: Harvard Voting Rights Clinic, Yale Law clinical programs, NYU and University of Michigan election law programs. These clinics will assign the post-Callais analysis immediately given the doctrinal significance.  
**Sequence**: Election protection orgs (Days 1–3 of distribution) → State AGs (Days 3–5) → CBC / Senate staff (Days 5–7) → Law schools (Days 7–10). Confirm that each distribution wave references the prior wave's engagement if any has occurred ("NAACP LDF has been in contact about this analysis").

### Domain 40-B (Tribal Sovereignty) — 2 weeks post-ruling

**Primary sector**: NCAI (National Congress of American Indians) and NARF (Native American Rights Fund). These are first-distribution targets — not secondary.  
**Secondary sector**: Federal Indian law clinics (Arizona State, University of Montana, University of New Mexico). These clinics have active tribal client relationships.  
**New distribution pathway**: This domain creates an entirely new audience not in the Phase 1 network. Tribal governance journalists (Native News Online, Indian Country Today), tribal college presidents (AIHEC), and state tribal liaisons in 23 states with significant Indigenous populations. This is not a Tier 2 expansion — it is a new coalition track that broadens the framework's reach to a constituency not previously included.  
**Conservative constitutionalist flank**: Fiscal federalists who support pre-constitutional governmental authority. Frame the treaty-obligation argument as government-to-government obligation predating federal program discretion.  
**Sequence**: NCAI/NARF (Day 1) → Federal Indian law clinics (Days 2–3) → Native media (Days 2–4) → State tribal liaisons (Days 4–7) → Conservative constitutionalists (Days 7–10).

### Domain 41-B (Disability Rights) — July SAVE Act window

**Primary sector**: State Protection and Advocacy (P&A) systems — 50-state infrastructure, the most direct institutional adoption path of any Phase 2 domain. P&As are legally mandated to provide advocacy for disabled people and will translate the four-layer exclusion architecture into client advocacy immediately.  
**Secondary sector**: NDRN (National Disability Rights Network). Their SAVE Act Senate testimony is the most immediate institutional vehicle.  
**Third sector**: Congressional Disability Staff Caucus. The domain document provides the democratic-participation framing that disability congressional staff currently lack in a unified form.  
**Litigation anchor**: ADA Title II enforcement theory (if SAVE Act passes without disability-protective amendments) and NVRA-SSA office-closure enforcement theory (underlitigated, novel, high-salience).  
**Cross-partisan note**: Veterans' service organizations focused on service-connected disability are natural Republican-side coalition partners. Frame the SSA staffing restoration as a veterans' access issue as well as a general disability issue.  
**Sequence**: P&A systems (Day 1) → NDRN (Day 1) → Congressional Disability Staff Caucus (Days 2–3) → Disability law clinics (Days 3–5) → VSOs (Days 5–7).

### Domains 39-A / 39-B / 40-C (Reproductive Rights, Labor, Fiscal Authority) — July–August wave

These three domains distribute in a coordinated August wave (target: August 5–15) that reaches the combined coalition before the August congressional recess deepens.  
- Domain 39-A: Primary sector = ballot initiative campaigns in Missouri and Nevada. Frame as democratic-participation argument (bodily autonomy as civic equality prerequisite), not health-care argument.  
- Domain 39-B: Primary sector = AFL-CIO voter contact program and swing-state labor federations. Secondary = EPI. Labor is the domain with the widest potential electoral impact if adopted.  
- Domain 40-C: Primary sector = Brookings, CBPP, Bipartisan Policy Center. This domain serves policy analysts, not mass coalition audiences. Distribute broadly but expect narrow institutional uptake among a highly technical audience.

## Part 3: Phase 2 Messaging Template Outline

Phase 2 outreach emails adapt the Phase 1 templates from `PHASE_1_EXECUTION_TEMPLATES.md` and `distribution-institutional-outreach-templates.md`. The structural differences from Phase 1 messaging are:

**Difference 1: Social proof is now available.** Phase 2 emails send after Phase 1 has been distributed. Any substantive responses, adoptions, or citations from Phase 1 are referenced explicitly in Phase 2 cover emails. This is the single most important Phase 2 messaging upgrade over Phase 1.

**Difference 2: Domain-specific rather than framework-general.** Phase 1 emails led with the full 35-domain framework. Phase 2 emails lead with a single domain and its specific relevance to the recipient's current work. The full framework is referenced but not foregrounded.

**Difference 3: Urgency is time-stamped.** Phase 2 domains all have specific external deadlines (June 12 FISA, November 3 midterm, post-Callais redistricting). Every Phase 2 email opens by naming the deadline and its institutional relevance.

### Template Structure (All Phase 2 Domains)

```
Subject line variants:
[Domain-specific hook — e.g., "Domain 38: Post-Callais redistricting analysis for your 2026 docket"]
[Urgency hook — e.g., "48 days to June 12: FISA architecture analysis for Senate consideration"]
[Social proof hook — e.g., "Following [Org]'s engagement with our framework — Domain 41 on disability rights"]

Opening paragraph (2–3 sentences):
- Name the external deadline or triggering event
- State why this domain is relevant to this recipient's specific current work
- Reference Phase 1 engagement if it occurred ("your colleague's question about [domain] in [month]")

Body paragraph 1 (3–4 sentences):
- Lead finding that is most directly applicable to recipient's work
- Specific section reference (e.g., "Section 4.2 of the domain document addresses the NVRA-SSA connection")
- What the analysis adds that doesn't already exist in their institutional research

Body paragraph 2 (2–3 sentences):
- Cross-domain connection to Phase 1 content the recipient already has
- What this domain reveals about the structure the prior distribution documented

Call to action (1–2 sentences):
- Specific request, not a general "let me know if useful"
- Options: feedback on a specific analytical claim / introduction to a colleague working on X / use in a specific brief or advocacy document

Sign-off:
- Same format as Phase 1 ([Your name], [Contact information], [Substack URL])
- Add: "The full Phase 2 domain is available at [Gist URL for this domain]"
```

### Phase 2-Specific Gist Protocol

Each Phase 2 domain requires its own public GitHub Gist, created following the same procedure as Phase 1 (Part 1, Section 1.2 of `phase-1-execution-playbooks.md`). Record each Phase 2 Gist URL in `DISTRIBUTION_GIST_URLS.md` with a Phase 2 label. Phase 2 Gist URLs go into Phase 2 outreach templates only — do not retroactively add them to Phase 1 templates.

### Warm Handoff Protocol

When a Phase 1 contact is transitioned to Phase 2 outreach, the cover email explicitly references the prior engagement:

- If they replied to Phase 1: "Your response in [month] to [specific domain] — specifically your question about [X] — led directly to the development of [Phase 2 domain]."
- If they shared the framework internally: "We understand [framework] has been circulated to [department/team]. The domain below was developed with [their focus area] specifically in mind."
- If they cited or adopted: "Your [brief/publication/testimony] referenced [framework]. Domain [X] extends that analysis into [specific area you have identified] and may be immediately useful for [their current work]."

Any Phase 1 contact who adopted the framework — even partially — should receive Phase 2 outreach before any new Phase 2-specific contact. Warm contacts convert to institutional adoption at higher rates than cold outreach.

## Part 5: Contingency Protocols and Trigger Thresholds

### Scenario A: Phase 1 Adoption Strong (Open Rate 40%+, Reply Rate 3%+)

Phase 2 proceeds on the baseline calendar sequence. Depth-first follow-up with the 3–5 contacts who generated the most engagement and referrals. Phase 2 opening messages reference Phase 1 engagement explicitly. Adjust domain sequencing only if specific feedback signals indicate a domain should move up (per the Phase 2 Trigger Table in `feedback-integration-roadmap.md`).

### Scenario B: Phase 1 Adoption Moderate (Open Rate 20–40%, Reply Rate 1–3%)

Expand breadth in Phase 2. Tier 2 and Tier 3 contacts are upgraded to a higher-personalization tier for Phase 2 outreach. Revisit subject line framing — compare subject lines that generated replies vs. those that did not. The best-performing Phase 1 subject line variant (from `EMAIL_PERSONALIZATION_GUIDE.md`) becomes the default Phase 2 template rather than a rotation option. Accelerate Track A domains (those with hard deadlines) to ensure they reach recipients before windows close.

### Scenario C: Phase 1 Adoption Stalls (Open Rate Below 20%, Reply Rate Below 1%)

**Phase 2 does not automatically defer.** The calendar-forced Track A domains (Intel Oversight — June 12 deadline; Voting Systems — November 3 midterm) launch on schedule regardless of Phase 1 metrics. A policy window does not wait for internal adoption metrics to improve.

For Track B domains, apply the following:
1. Diagnose delivery before concluding content failure: check spam filter blocks, bounce rates, and Gist URL accessibility before changing content strategy.
2. If delivery is confirmed but engagement is low: test an alternative subject line and opening paragraph (two variations, 10 contacts each) before changing domain framing.
3. If two tests fail: review whether the target institutions are the right audience for Phase 1. Phase 2 may need to open new coalition tracks (especially the Phase 2-specific contacts in the table above) rather than re-attempting the same Phase 1 contacts with different framing.
4. Hard minimum for Phase 2 Track B launch: proceed regardless of Phase 1 metrics by August 1, 2026. August recess is the last window to reach congressional staff before the pre-election period. Waiting past August 1 for Phase 1 metrics to improve sacrifices the Track B distribution window.

### Scenario D: SCOTUS or Legislative Hard Trigger Fires Before Phase 2 Production is Complete

**Triggers that immediately escalate regardless of queue position:**

| Trigger Event | Escalation Action |
|---|---|
| FISA lapses June 12 without extension | Distribute Intel Oversight (38-A) immediately, even if in draft form; supplement with `surveillance-tracking.md` update |
| Trump v. Barbara rules against Indian Citizenship Act | Tribal Sovereignty (40-B) becomes emergency Tier 1 — NARF and NCAI within 72 hours; all other queue positions pause |
| SAVE Act passes Senate with no disability amendments | Disability Rights (41-B) immediately distributed to all P&A systems; ADA litigation angle activated |
| Three or more states enact post-Callais discriminatory maps by September 2026 | Domain 38-B update produced (targeted — not full revision) and distributed to election protection contacts before November 3 |
| Democratic House or Senate gain November 3 | Fiscal Authority (40-C) and Constitutional Architecture (40-A) elevated from Tier 3 to Tier 1 for Phase 2b (post-midterm distribution round) |

### Scenario E: Phase 2 Research Capacity is Constrained

If only one domain can be produced at a time (sequential rather than hybrid parallel), the priority order is dictated by the prioritization matrix composite ranking:

1. Domain 41-B (Disability Rights) — 87.1, fastest to produce (10–14 hours), highest reversibility
2. Domain 38-B (Voting Systems) — 84.1, most time-locked (November 3 midterm)
3. Domain 38-A (Intel Oversight) — 81.6, June 12 hard deadline
4. Domain 41-A (Domestic Intelligence) — 81.6, midterm-linked
5. Domain 40-B (Tribal Sovereignty) — 78.8, SCOTUS ruling trigger

Do not produce Domain 39 (Reproductive Rights / Labor Rights) before completing at least Domains 41-B and 38-B. The Tier 1 calendar-locked domains take precedence over the Tier 2 feedback-gated domains under capacity constraints.

### Phase 2 Framework-Level Success Threshold

At least 3 of the 9 Phase 2 Tier 1+2 domains achieve their primary success metric within 90 days of distribution. Failure to reach this threshold by 90 days is a sequencing or targeting problem, not a content problem — the research quality does not change. The diagnostic is: are domains reaching the right institutional contacts at the right moment in their decision cycle?
