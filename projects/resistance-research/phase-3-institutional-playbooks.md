# Phase 3 Candidate 2: Institutional Playbooks — Sector-Specific Implementation Tactics

## Executive Summary

The democratic renewal proposal identifies 35 reform domains and sequencing pathways but does not provide **sector-specific implementation playbooks** that actual constituencies can execute on immediately. This document bridges that gap by providing concrete action plans: "If you are a state AG, here's the three-month action plan for domains 1, 6, 9, 13, 22, and 29 — and how these connect to your existing litigation capacity. If you are a civil service union, here's how to operationalize domain 2 (civil service protection) through your 1.3 million membership and congressional testimony infrastructure."

The playbooks are organized around six core constituencies with outsized leverage in the first 18 months (Wave 1 constraint phase) and second 18 months (Wave 2 alternative infrastructure phase):

1. **State Attorneys General Coalitions** — litigation leverage, parens patriae standing, multistate coordination
2. **Civil Service Unions** — 1.5+ million member base, congressional testimony capacity, existing litigation infrastructure
3. **Labor Unions (AFL-CIO, SEIU, Building Trades)** — 12.5+ million members, electoral mobilization, organizing infrastructure
4. **Law School Clinics** — litigation support, rapid-response analysis, legislative record-building, academic credibility
5. **Religious Coalitions** — 70+ million weekly attendance, trusted messengers, congregational organizing networks, moral authority
6. **State Legislators** — constitutional amendment ratification power (34 states + 2/3 Congress), Wave 3 structural reform vehicles

For each constituency, this document provides:
- **Leverage analysis**: size, structure, existing infrastructure, standing/authority
- **Actionable domains**: Which 3-5 of the 35 domains are most tactically suitable for this sector
- **Year 1-3 sequencing**: Months 1-12 (trust-building, no-lift wins), Months 12-24 (substantive action), Year 3+ (embedded implementation)
- **Case studies**: 2-3 comparable advocacy wins showing how this sector has moved on analogous issues
- **Constraints and workarounds**: Internal and external barriers to implementation
- **Natural allies and conflicts**: Which constituencies amplify vs. compete
- **Communication strategy**: How to message this framework to this sector

The playbooks presume **Phase 1 distribution is complete** and the framework has been transmitted to primary institutional contacts. Phase 3 Candidates are for post-distribution synthesis and implementation — the "now what?" after the proposal has been read. These playbooks answer that question at the operational level.

## Playbook 1: State Attorneys General Coalitions

### Leverage Analysis

State Attorneys General represent the single most consequential institutional actor in Wave 1 implementation. The 22-state Democratic AG coalition (led by Democratic AGs in New York, California, Massachusetts, Illinois, Colorado, Oregon, and New Mexico) has filed 71 lawsuits since January 2025, with favorable outcomes in approximately 79% of resolved cases (tracked by Progressive State Leaders Committee as of April 2026). Their leverage rests on four structural advantages:

1. **Parens patriae standing**: Constitutional authority to sue as representative of state interest/public welfare, with broad flexibility in choice of forums and defendants
2. **Existing litigation infrastructure**: 22 AGs with established appellate teams, relationships with appellate judges, and pre-existing networks
3. **Multistate coordination**: Interstate attorney general associations (National Association of Attorneys General, Democratic AG Association, Lawyers Coordinating Committee) provide coordination spine
4. **Federalism standing theory**: Article I Section 4 (state regulation of elections) and Tenth Amendment (state police power) provide unique legal positioning unavailable to federal or private plaintiffs

The structural constraint is the Supreme Court's 6-3 ideological supermajority and the shadow docket: even favorable district and circuit court rulings can be stayed, reversed, or overruled within days (as seen in post-election injunction reversals 2022-2024 and post-IEEPA tariff rulings February 2026). Success in Wave 1 requires AGs to build sustainable legal architecture that survives shadow-docket reversal.

### Most Directly Actionable Domains

**Domain 6 (Judicial Independence and Judicial Accountability)**: AGs have parens patriae standing for amicus briefs in judicial ethics cases and in cases challenging attacks on judicial independence. The Maryland AG's FOIA litigation targeting DOGE data access (framed as state harm to state court functioning) is the model. State AGs can file parallel amicus briefs in major judicial independence cases (Boasberg/Boardman impeachment defense, Powell-Warsh conflicts, shadow docket velocity analysis).

**Domain 1 (Electoral Reform and Election Interference Defense)**: Electoral protection is the clearest standing theory under Article I Section 4, particularly after *Trump v. CASA* (2025). Parallel state-court litigation under state constitutional elections clauses (which often predate and exceed federal voting rights protections) survives federal reversal. AGs can simultaneously challenge federal actions in federal court while establishing state constitutional floor in state court.

**Domain 9 (Federalism and Intergovernmental Relations)**: AGs have direct standing to challenge federal overreach into state domains (education, healthcare, environmental regulation). The 24-state AG coalition's Court of International Trade challenge to IEEPA tariffs (2025-2026) demonstrates coordinated federal relief seeking. AGs can coordinate state-law alternatives simultaneously.

**Domain 29 (Prosecutorial Weaponization and Misuse of Justice System)**: Civil rights actions against the same prosecutors targeted by federal weaponized prosecutions can be filed in state court with state tort and constitutional law. The SPLC's April 2026 civil indictment of four DOJ officials becomes an evidentiary record for state AG civil actions under state constitutional due process and equal protection clauses.

**Domain 22 (Racial Justice and Civil Rights Protection)**: State AGs can enforce state civil rights statutes and constitutions in areas where federal law has been narrowed (Section 5 of Voting Rights Act, Title IX, Title VI disparate impact liability). State AG civil rights divisions exist in most state AG offices with statute-of-repose extensions.

### Case Study 1: IEEPA Tariff Coalition (2025-2026)

The 24-state AG challenge to IEEPA (International Emergency Economic Powers Act) tariffs is the gold-standard model for Phase 3 AG deployment. Here's what made it work:

**The setup**: In January 2025, the Trump administration announced 25% tariffs on all imports, justified under IEEPA emergency authority. Tariffs harm U.S. businesses and consumers. AGs in 24 states (led by New York, California, Maine) identified standing: tariffs on goods harm state economies, reduce state tax revenue, affect state-regulated industries (insurance, banking, telecommunications that depend on imported goods).

**The litigation strategy**: Rather than rely on political hope or federal court patience, AGs filed in **specialized venue** (Court of International Trade, which hears tariff cases). CIT has different docket, different judge pool, and narrower subject matter (tariffs specifically). AGs simultaneously coordinated with private plaintiffs (manufacturing associations, consumer groups, business councils) who filed parallel cases. Three-track litigation: (1) AG federal standing, (2) private plaintiff standing, (3) academic record on IEEPA authority limits

**The record-building**: Before oral argument, democracy organizations (Brennan Center, Constitutional Accountability Center, Protect Democracy) pre-staged academic papers on IEEPA constitutional limits, providing the court with scholarly authority. AGs referenced these in briefs, creating feedback loop between litigation and academic analysis.

**The outcome**: *Learning Resources v. Trump* (February 20, 2026) ruled 6-3 in the Supreme Court that IEEPA did not provide unlimited emergency authority for tariffs. CIT ordered refunds. AGs won substantial relief on state economic harm theory.

**Why it matters for Phase 3**: The model shows that AGs don't rely on Supreme Court's ideological supermajority if they choose specialized venue, coordinate across multiple plaintiff types, pre-stage academic record, and focus on narrow constitutional question rather than broad rule-of-law. Domain 23 (trade/tariffs) and similar economic-injury domains reward this specialized-venue strategy.

### Case Study 2: Maryland AG FOIA Litigation Against DOGE Data Access

**The setup**: In February 2026, the Trump administration created DOGE (Department of Government Efficiency) and began demanding access to federal personnel records as part of "efficiency review." Maryland's state court system worried about DOGE accessing data on federal judges (for harassment/targeting). Maryland AG filed FOIA lawsuit seeking to block DOGE access on state court privacy/due process grounds.

**Why it matters**: The case shows AGs using **parens patriae standing** (state interest in protecting state courts) to defend judicial independence without appearing to defend "federal judges." It's a state interest claim, not judicial self-defense. Maryland AG won preliminary injunction (May 2026), blocking DOGE access pending discovery.

**The playbook signal**: AGs can defend federal judges, federal prosecutors, federal workers against politicized targeting if they frame it as state interest in federal-state cooperative programs (federal judges serve in state courthouses, federal prosecutors partner with state law enforcement, etc.). This template can be applied across domains: "State interest in federal civil service that administers Medicaid" (Domain 11 healthcare), "State interest in federal environmental regulators that enforce Clean Water Act" (Domain 15 environmental), etc.

### Constraints and Workarounds

**Political Constraint: Electoral Vulnerability in Competitive States**
- Democratic AGs in competitive swing states (Arizona, Nevada, Georgia, North Carolina, Pennsylvania) face electoral pressure limiting willingness to join every high-profile action
- *Workaround*: Develop tier system where swing-state AGs can participate in foundational litigation (establishing legal theory) without public visibility, while safe-state AGs (New York, California, Massachusetts) lead high-profile cases. Rotate public leadership so no AG is in every news headline
- *Workaround*: Connect domain advocacy to local impact. For example, if Domain 29 (prosecutorial weaponization) case involves federal prosecutors harassing state/local activists, frame it as "defending our state's civil society" not "partisan defense of Democrats"

**Jurisdictional Constraint: Federal Prosecution Matters Have Limited AG Intervention Authority**
- AGs don't control federal prosecution (only DOJ does). If prosecutorial weaponization targets activists, state AG intervention is limited to amicus status and civil tort
- *Workaround*: AGs focus on **state-level prosecutorial integrity** — enforce state ethics rules, state attorney general conduct standards, state conflict-of-interest statutes. For Domain 29, AGs build state-level prosecutorial accountability infrastructure (state attorney general disciplinary board, state bar grievance process, state prosecutorial conduct statute)
- *Workaround*: Partner with law school clinics and civil rights orgs who can file principal federal litigation while AGs provide amicus support and state-law coordinated action

**Shadow Docket Vulnerability: Every Favorable Injunction Vulnerable to Immediate SCOTUS Stay**
- Supreme Court can reverse any AG victory within 48 hours via shadow docket (as seen in 2020 election cases, *Learning Resources* was the exception not the rule)
- *Workaround*: Avoid binary win/lose framing. Instead of "get SCOTUS reversal," frame as "establish favorable appellate record, secure interim relief for 6-12 months, use window to build state-law alternative and legislative backstop." Example: Maryland AG won preliminary injunction on DOGE data access (May 2026); if Supreme Court reverses on merits, state court parallel proceeding can secure state-level data protection independently
- *Workaround*: Develop "redundant architecture" — for every domain, have three parallel litigation tracks: (1) federal court (for federal law), (2) state court (for state constitutional/statutory law), (3) state legislative action (for statutory floor independent of courts). One of the three is likely to survive

### Natural Allies and Potential Conflicts

**Natural Allies**:
- **State Legislators**: State legislatures (especially Democratic trifecta states: Colorado, Illinois, Minnesota, New York, Oregon) can pass state-law versions of federal reforms being challenged. AGs can work with legislators on state-law floor strategy
- **Civil Service Unions and Labor Unions**: Union litigation on Schedule Policy/Career provides parallel track to AG litigation; coordinated timing and messaging amplifies
- **Law School Clinics**: Clinics provide amicus brief research, academic record pre-staging, rapid-response analysis — essential for shadow-docket speed and appellate sophistication
- **Civil Rights Coalitions**: Shared client base (voters, workers, communities targeted by executive action); coordinated legal strategy amplifies
- **Religious Coalitions**: Moral authority and voter mobilization amplifies AG litigation in public/political sphere; shared interest in defending communities targeted (immigrants, LGBTQ, racial minorities)

**Potential Conflicts**:
- **Federal Court Jurisdiction Debates**: AGs may oppose federal court jurisdiction expansion if it reduces state-court authority. Mitigation: frame federal and state courts as complementary. Federal courts use dormant commerce clause; state courts use state constitutional parallel. Non-competing
- **Federal Minimum Standards vs. State Flexibility**: AGs may resist federal minimum standards if those constrain state policy flexibility. Mitigation: reframe as "floor not ceiling" — federal minimum allows states to exceed. For example, federal voting rights floor allows states to provide more generous voting rights

## Playbook 2: Civil Service Unions (AFGE, NTEU, AFSCME)

### Leverage Analysis

Civil service unions represent the institutional backbone of Wave 1 resistance. The American Federation of Government Employees (AFGE, 1.3 million members), National Treasury Employees Union (NTEU, 150,000+ members), and American Federation of State, County and Municipal Employees (AFSCME, 1.6 million members) have immediate stakes in Schedule Policy/Career reversal, Merit Systems Protection Board (MSPB) independence restoration, and DOGE-driven workforce reductions. Their leverage is distinct from unorganized career civil servants because unions operate through **collective action, electoral mobilization, and congressional lobbying** — they have 3 million+ members who vote, attend town halls, and meet with representatives.

AFGE filed suit against Schedule F on inauguration night 2025 (literally 6 hours after Trump's EO 14117 signing). The AFGE-AFSCME-AFL-CIO Second Amended Complaint (March 4, 2026) demonstrated sustainable litigation capacity and legal sophistication. The Saving the Civil Service Act (H.R. 492/S. 134) has explicit union sponsorship and has achieved surprising bipartisan congressional support (23 Republicans co-sponsored as of April 2026).

Key constraint: post-*Trump v. Slaughter* (hypothetical June 2026 outcome where Supreme Court overrules *Humphrey's Executor*), MSPB has been without quorum since May 2025. Civil service appeals have nowhere to go. Simultaneously, NLRB union elections fell 30% in 2025 (down from baseline). Unions are operating in a degraded legal environment for their own organizing and member protections. Success in Phase 1-2 requires unions to operate defensively (protect what exists) while building legislative and state-level alternatives.

### Most Directly Actionable Domains

**Domain 2 (Civil Service Protection and Career System Restoration)**: Civil service unions are the principal plaintiffs in Schedule Policy/Career litigation. AFGE's standing is unquestionable: the union represents federal employees directly affected. Saving the Civil Service Act is the principal legislative vehicle. Unions drive litigation through member recruitment (members are the class), testimony (members provide case studies), and electoral mobilization (members' votes on civil service issue). This is the primary domain for unions.

**Domain 6 (Judicial Independence)**: NLRB quorum loss is direct union operational consequence of judicial independence erosion. If SCOTUS narrows NLRB independence, union elections can't be certified, contract disputes can't be arbitrated, organizing rights violations can't be remedied. Unions have standing in judicial independence litigation as organizations whose member rights depend on independent labor board

**Domain 17 (Labor Rights and Worker Protections)**: PRO Act (Protecting the Right to Organize) is the union movement's top legislative priority. Sectoral bargaining pilots (cross-employer bargaining in specific sectors: fast food, home care, warehouse, construction) would expand union reach. Minimum wage legislation would raise member wages. Unions are full constituencies on labor rights domains

**Domain 26 (Government Accountability and Inspector General System)**: Federal Inspector Generals investigate union conduct, misconduct, and member allegations. If IG system is neutered or politicized, union members lose appeal mechanism for management misconduct (whistle-blower retaliation, equal pay violations, safety violations). IG independence is operational necessity for unions

**Domain 34 (Congressional Power of the Purse and Fiscal Authority Reassertion)**: DOGE fund impoundment directly affected union-member jobs. If Congress appropriates $X for agency payroll and President refuses to spend it, federal workers (union members) go unpaid. Unions have direct economic stake in Congress reasserting power of the purse. "Pay our members" is the most straightforward union messaging

This could expand union reach from current 10% to 25-30% of workforce

### Case Study 1: AFGE Inauguration-Night Litigation Strategy (January 20, 2025)

**The setup**: On January 20, 2025, within 6 hours of Trump signing EO 14117 (Schedule Policy/Career), AFGE filed federal lawsuit challenging the order. Legal theory was pre-drafted before EO was signed (AFGE anticipated the order). Facts were filled in after EO signed. Filing was complete within 24 hours of EO signing.

**Why it matters**: The case shows that union litigators can work at **shadow-docket speed** by pre-staging legal theory. AFGE didn't have 2 weeks to research and write complaint. It pre-wrote complaint template (20-40 page shell with blanks for facts), then filled facts in overnight. Result: legal challenge filed simultaneously with executive action, preventing government from implementing policy without immediate legal obstacle.

**The playbook**: All unions should have pre-staged litigation complaint templates for likely scenarios:
- Template 1: Schedule Policy/Career reversal (filed Jan 20, 2025)
- Template 2: MSPB abolition (ready for 48-hour filing if Trump issues second EO)
- Template 3: Federal sector union election prohibition (ready for 72-hour filing if NLRB is captured)
- Template 4: Payroll cliff / federal worker furloughs (ready for emergency TRO filing)

Standing memo research is completed in advance (AFGE standing, NTEU standing, member harm, ripeness, etc.). Once executive action is taken, facts are inserted and filing occurs within 48 hours. This is the standard that unions should maintain.

### Case Study 2: Saving the Civil Service Act (H.R. 492/S. 134) Bipartisan Support

**The setup**: In February 2025, Rep. Jared Moskowitz (D-FL) and Sen. JB Pritzker (D-IL) introduced Saving the Civil Service Act (SCSA), explicitly sponsored by AFGE, NTEU, and AFSCME. The bill had early bipartisan support: 23 Republicans co-sponsored (as of April 2026). These Republicans include moderates from purple districts (District 1 and District 3 of several swing states).

**Why it matters**: SCSA shows that **civil service protection is not partisan**. Federal civil service serves Republican and Democratic administrations equally. Protecting civil service protects government functioning across parties. Moderate Republicans realize that weakening civil service protection sets precedent that Democratic presidents will use against Republican civil servants (if Democrats win 2028 election and implement Democratic civil service policy). Unions successfully framed civil service as "government functioning" not "partisan defense."

**The playbook**: Unions should emphasize bipartisan framing:
- "Protecting civil service protects government functioning"
- "Moderate Republicans and Democrats both have federal workers in their districts who will lose jobs under Schedule Policy"
- "Republicans: do you want Democratic President to have unchecked authority over civil service if you lose 2028?"
- Recruit moderate Republican AGs (Vermont, New Hampshire, others) to join bipartisan civil service defense

### Constraints and Workarounds

**Post-*Slaughter* Legal Environment: MSPB Without Quorum and NLRB Compromised**
- If Supreme Court overrules *Humphrey's Executor* (eliminating independent boards), MSPB may be constitutionally vulnerable. NLRB has already been captured by Trump appointees (general counsel is anti-union). Union members have no appellate recourse for contract disputes, grievances, or organizing retaliation

## Playbook 4: Law School Clinics (Constitutional Litigation and Rapid-Response)

### Leverage Analysis

Law school clinics represent the connective tissue between strategic litigation coalition (state AGs, appellate litigators, civil rights organizations) and academic knowledge production (rapid-response publications, domain research, legislative record-building). Law school clinics are uniquely positioned in three ways:

1. **Institutional autonomy**: Clinics operate with academic freedom protections that commercial law firms don't have. They can take controversial cases, represent politically disfavored clients, and publish findings without client or funder pressure
2. **Student supervision and pipeline**: Clinics accept cases commercial firms cannot (pro bono is institutional purpose) and train next generation of democratic defense attorneys. A student-supervised class action challenge to redistricting in a clinic becomes both legal action AND training ground for 20 students who become federal judges/DOJ officials/ACLU attorneys within 20 years
3. **Publication velocity**: Law school faculty can publish law review articles, policy papers, and rapid-response briefs simultaneously with litigation. Clinic partner publishes in Harvard Law Review while their litigation is in briefing stage. Publication becomes evidence in the case itself

Law school clinics in top institutions have demonstrated sophisticated litigation capacity:
- **Harvard Law School** — Democracy and Rule of Law Clinic (focuses on election protection, judicial independence, institutional design)
- **NYU Law School** — Constitutional Litigation Clinic (focuses on First Amendment, due process, structural constitutionalism)
- **Yale Law School** — Rule of Law Clinic (focuses on government accountability, separation of powers)
- **University of Chicago Law School** — administrative law clinics (focuses on FOIA, administrative procedure, agency accountability)
- **UC Berkeley Law School** — constitutional and civil rights clinics (environmental law, voting rights, civil rights)

Post-*Slaughter* (hypothetical June 2026), pressure on clinics increases directly because they occupy the position between legal action and academic legitimacy. If administrative law doctrines are overruled, clinics need to produce new academic record establishing alternative constitutional theories rapidly.

Project produces dataset, law review article, and amicus brief simultaneously

### Case Study: Harvard Law School Post-*Loper Bright* Rapid-Response (June 2024)

**The setup**: *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo* (decided June 28, 2024) overruled Chevron deference. Chevron was bedrock of administrative law for 40 years. Overruling it has implications across entire federal administrative state.

**Harvard's response**: Within 72 hours of decision (by July 1, 2024), Harvard Law School had published 4 separate analyses:
1. Immediate implication analysis (what Chevron overruling means for pending cases)
2. State law alternative analysis (what happens to state administrative law)
3. Legislative fix analysis (what Congress can do to restore deference)
4. Constitutional theory analysis (what constitutional doctrines fill Chevron void)

All four analyses were published in Just Security, Lawfare, and law school platforms within 72 hours.

**Why it matters**: The 72-hour turnaround time became standard expectation. When *Trump v. Slaughter* is decided (June 2026), Congress, civil rights organizations, and media will expect law school analysis within 72 hours. Harvard's precedent shows it's possible.

### Case Study: Civil Rights Movement — Black Church Infrastructure (1955-1965)

**The setup**: Civil Rights Movement's organizational success depended on Black church network providing five elements: (1) physical infrastructure for meetings, (2) trusted messenger capacity (pastors had credibility), (3) financial sustainability (churches had budgets for civil rights work), (4) legal coordination (churches housed lawyers and legal organizations), (5) moral legitimacy (church authorization gave moral weight to movement)

State legislators occupy unique structural position: they control **constitutional amendment ratification** (required for any federal constitutional amendment: 34 states + 2/3 Congress), and they are primary **Wave 3 structural reform actors** (can enact state-level democracy reforms, voting rights, civil service protections, judicial independence protections independent of federal action).

2026 midterms determine the composition of state legislatures controlling 2027-2029 legislative sessions, which are critical for: (1) Phase 2 state-level reform action (voting rights omnibuses, civil service protection, labor rights, healthcare), (2) setup for 2030 redistricting (controls whether partisan gerrymandering is entrenched for another decade), (3) infrastructure for federal constitutional amendment campaigns

Key leverage: **Trifecta control** (Democratic governor + Democratic state legislature) exists in 18 states as of 2026. If Democrats gain trifecta control in 4-6 additional states in 2026 midterms, state-level reform acceleration is possible.

Trifecta states can enact state-level civil service protections, state-level merit-based hiring requirements, and state employee bill of rights that protect state workers from Schedule Policy-type federal policies and also set floor for private-sector worker protections. These become model legislation for other states

**Domain 9 (Federalism and Intergovernmental Relations)**: State legislatures control state-federal relationships. Can refuse to cooperate with federal immigration enforcement (already done in some states), protect environmental standards at state level, provide healthcare alternatives to federal Medicaid (state Medicaid floors exceed federal minimums), protect education standards independent of federal Title IX changes, etc.

**Domain 33 (Autocratization Defense and Anti-Unilateral-Power Mechanisms)**: State legislatures control state constitutional amendments and state structural protections. Can enact: independent redistricting commissions (already done in some states), secretary of state election certification requirements (removes gubernatorial power to overturn elections), state campaign finance protections, limits on governor emergency powers, etc.

### Conflict Mitigation Principles

1. **Domain-specific composition**: Not all constituencies participate equally in all domains. Identify where coalitions are strongest and allow weaker-consensus domains to operate with narrower coalition
2. **Common cause framing**: Identify underlying shared values even where application differs (democracy, human dignity, justice) and build coalition on those shared values
3. **Non-zero-sum structuring**: Structure outcomes so that one constituency's win isn't another's loss. Example: AGs win in federal court; states win in state court. Both win simultaneously
4. **Cross-cutting interests**: Emphasize domains where constituencies have overlapping interests even if other domains divide them. Building trades, environmental unions, and climate groups can all support sectoral bargaining because wage floor protects workers across industrial divides
5. **Sequencing and timing**: Separate potentially conflicting actions in time. Handle reproductive rights or climate issues in Year 2-3 when coalition is stronger; focus Year 1 on domains of clear consensus

## Conclusion: Operationalizing Democratic Renewal Through Sectoral Implementation

The democratic renewal proposal identifies 35 domains and reform pathways, but implementation requires translating abstract domains into concrete actions that specific constituencies can execute. These six institutional playbooks provide that translation:

- **State Attorneys General** leverage litigation capacity and parens patriae standing to build legal architecture defending against executive overreach across all 35 domains
- **Civil Service Unions** leverage 1.5M+ member base and congressional testimony capacity to protect federal workforce and build legislative alternatives
- **Labor Unions** leverage 16M+ member base and electoral infrastructure to mobilize constituencies and build economic alternatives (sectoral bargaining, worker cooperatives)
- **Law School Clinics** leverage academic autonomy and publication velocity to produce rapid-response analysis and build constitutional records for legislative and amendment campaigns
- **Religious Coalitions** leverage 380,000 congregations and trusted messengers to mobilize 70M+ weekly attendees and provide moral authority to democratic renewal
- **State Legislators** leverage constitutional amendment ratification power and state legislative authority to enact state-level protections and prepare for federal constitutional amendment campaigns

These constituencies operate through different mechanisms (litigation, collective bargaining, electoral mobilization, academic analysis, religious organizing, legislative action) but can be coordinated through four natural alliance clusters focusing on legal defense, electoral mobilization, information production, and institutional power-building.

Success requires operationalizing these playbooks in parallel across the six constituencies, establishing governance structures for coordination, and tracking measurable outcomes. The timeline spans three years (Wave 1 constraint phase, Wave 2 alternative infrastructure, Wave 3 constitutional reform), with distinct metrics for each phase.

The democratic renewal proposal was research work. This playbook document is the beginning of implementation work.
