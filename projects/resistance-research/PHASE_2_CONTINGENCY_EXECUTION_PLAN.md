# Phase 2 Contingency Execution Plan

**Use this document on Day 14 (approximately June 14–15, 2026) after reviewing your monitoring dashboard. It is a decision guidebook, not a failure-blame document. Every path below has a clear next step.**

---

## Executive Summary

Phase 1 launched June 1 distributing research to 45+ organizations across law schools, think tanks, policy organizations, and civil rights groups. Day 14 is the major decision point. Pull four numbers from your dashboard: Score 3+ reply rate, number of constituencies with strong engagement, cross-organizational references, and confirmed adoption signals. Those four numbers route you to one of three paths.

**If engagement is strong (>60% reply rate, strong across 4+ constituencies)**: execute Phase 2 Wave 1 as planned by June 15. The framework has momentum. Use it.

**If engagement is moderate (30–60%)**: execute Phase 2 Wave 1 starting June 15 but revise messaging to lead with the domains and constituencies that actually engaged. Do not broadcast the full framework; amplify what already worked.

**If engagement is weak (<30%, fewer than 2 constituencies showing strong signal)**: pause Phase 2 Wave 1. Run a 72-hour rapid cohort analysis using the protocol in Section 4 of this document. Then relaunch Phase 2 with a domain-specific, constituency-targeted approach by June 22.

Weak engagement is not campaign failure. It is diagnostic data. The most common causes of low institutional uptake — wrong contact tier, wrong framing, wrong timing, delivery failure — are all correctable within one week. The protocol below is designed to identify the cause and deploy the fix before Phase 2 loses its policy window.

## Section 2: The Three Paths

### PATH 1 — STRONG Engagement (>60% reply rate)

**Trigger**: Score 3+ reply rate ≥60%, 4+ constituencies showing strong signal, at least one cross-organizational reference detected.

**Interpretation**: Phase 1 has institutional momentum. Tier 1 contacts read, responded, and at least some have forwarded to peer organizations. The framework has earned credibility in the institutions that matter.

**Action: Execute Phase 2 Wave 1 as planned by June 15.**

Specific steps:

1. **Within 24 hours of STRONG determination**: Prepare Phase 2 Wave 1 distribution materials. Identify the 3–5 domains most frequently referenced in Score 3+ replies — these are the domains with the clearest institutional pull, and they should lead your Phase 2 outreach.

2. **Social proof integration**: Every Phase 2 email should open with a brief reference to Phase 1 institutional response. Do not over-claim. "Initial distribution to [law schools / civil rights organizations / policy researchers] produced [X] substantive responses" is specific and credible. "Widely praised" is not.

3. **Priority sequencing**: Contact the responding organizations' peer networks first. If Elias (Democracy Docket) responded positively, his peer network — Campaign Legal Center, Lawyers Committee for Civil Rights, NAACP LDF — is the highest-value next tier. If Chenoweth (Harvard) responded, academic and international democracy organizations (Ash Center, Carnegie, comparative democracy institutes) are the priority. Amplify through the networks of responders, not a generic Tier 2 list.

4. **Domain-specific briefs**: Produce a 2-3 page single-domain brief for the 3 highest-engagement domains. These are shareable without requiring the full framework. They convert a responding contact into an amplifier.

5. **Timeline**: Phase 2 Wave 1 sends June 15. Wave 2 sends July 1 for constituencies not reached in Wave 1.

**Reference files**: `PHASE_2_CONTINGENCY_PLAYBOOK.md` (Branch 2: High Response), `PHASE_2_ACTIVATION_AGENT_BRIEFS.md` (STRONG section).

### PATH 2 — MODERATE Engagement (30–60%)

**Trigger**: Score 3+ reply rate 30–59%, 2–3 constituencies with strong signal, 1–2 cross-organizational references, 0–1 confirmed adoption signals.

**Interpretation**: Phase 1 landed with some constituencies and not others. Some contacts engaged deeply; others are silent. This is not a distribution failure — it is a segmentation signal. Some combination of messaging, targeting, and timing worked for specific audience types and did not work for others. The job is to identify which worked and amplify those, while revising approach for those that did not.

**Action: Execute Phase 2 Wave 1 by June 15, but with revised messaging and targeting based on which constituencies and domains actually resonated.**

Specific steps:

1. **Constituency triage (48 hours)**: List your 7 constituencies and score each:
   - Which 2–3 constituencies produced your Score 3+ replies?
   - Which 2–3 constituencies produced no replies despite confirmed delivery?
   - Which 1–2 constituencies are in the middle (some replies, low score)?

   The active constituencies get Phase 2 outreach now. The silent constituencies get revised messaging and a different contact tier (clinic directors instead of deans, state chapter leads instead of national directors) before Phase 2 outreach.

2. **Domain-first reframing**: For the silent constituencies, move from framework-first framing to domain-first framing. Instead of "a 40-domain research framework," lead with the single domain most relevant to that organization's current docket. This is documented in detail in `PHASE_2_CONTINGENCY_PLAYBOOK.md` (Branch 1: Low Response, messaging adjustments section).

3. **Contact tier substitution**: For constituencies with <20% reply rate at Tier 1, shift 30% of Tier 2 outreach to Tier 1.5 contacts — people one level below the national director who are more likely to read cold outreach and have more direct operational use for research materials. Examples:
   - Law schools: clinical program directors instead of deans
   - Think tanks: program officers instead of presidents
   - Advocacy organizations: policy analysts and research directors instead of executive directors
   - Legislative staff: committee counsel instead of member offices

4. **Timeline**: Phase 2 Wave 1 sends June 15 for engaged constituencies. Revised outreach to silent constituencies sends June 22.

5. **Do not wait for perfect signal**: MODERATE is sufficient to launch Phase 2. The question is which constituencies and domains to lead with, not whether to launch.

**Reference files**: `PHASE_2_CONTINGENCY_PLAYBOOK.md` (Branch 1 messaging adjustment section), `PHASE_2_WEAK_OUTCOME_CONTINGENCY_ROADMAP.md` (Section 2: Messaging Pivots).

### PATH 3 — WEAK Engagement (<30%)

**Trigger**: Score 3+ reply rate <30%, fewer than 2 constituencies with strong signal, no cross-organizational references, no confirmed adoption signals by Day 14.

**Interpretation**: Phase 1 did not generate the institutional traction needed to carry Phase 2 forward on the current approach. This has a cause, and causes are diagnosable. The 72-hour rapid analysis protocol in Section 4 will identify it. Do not proceed to Phase 2 Wave 1 on June 15 without running this analysis — deploying Phase 2 on a broken approach is a waste of the strongest policy windows in the research calendar.

**Action: Pause Phase 2 Wave 1. Run the rapid cohort analysis in Section 4. Relaunch Phase 2 with revised approach by June 22.**

The one-week delay is a feature, not a penalty. June 22 is still within every major policy window in the Phase 2 domain sequence (H.R. 492 committee window, Trump v. Barbara ruling window, OBBBA implementation advocacy window). The analysis week is what converts a failed distribution approach into a working one.

## Section 3: Path 2 — Messaging Revision Guidance

This section provides the specific messaging adjustments for the Moderate path. If you are on Path 3, see Section 4 first, then return to this section for the messaging templates.

### The Core Problem with Framework-First Framing

Research on policy brief distribution consistently finds that comprehensive frameworks underperform domain-specific resources in institutional settings. Organizations receiving hundreds of research submissions per month respond to specificity, not scope. A document that claims to cover 40 domains signals "read this entire thing" when most Tier 1 contacts have a 20-minute engagement window and a narrow institutional mandate.

The fix is not a better summary of the full framework. The fix is a different primary object: **lead with the domain, offer the framework as context**.

### Revised Email Structure (Moderate and Weak Paths)

**Old structure** (framework-first):
> "I am sharing a 40-domain research framework on democratic resilience..."

**Revised structure** (domain-first, gap-filling):
> Opening paragraph: Demonstrate that you understand their specific current work (active litigation, pending legislation, recent publication).
> Second paragraph: Identify the specific argument or evidence gap your domain research fills for that work.
> Third paragraph: Offer the domain research document with the framework as supporting context.
> Close: Specific, low-friction ask ("Would a one-page brief on [domain] be useful for your team?" not "Please read the full framework").

### Subject Line Revision

Replace methodological framing ("research framework on democratic resilience") with case-specific relevance:

- For election protection organizations: Reference an active case. "Callais redistricting cascade — structural VRA data for 5 states" outperforms "election law research framework."
- For civil service reform organizations: Reference the specific legislation or litigation they are working on. "H.R. 492 — the democratic infrastructure argument missing from current testimony" outperforms "civil service research."
- For tribal sovereignty advocates: Reference the pending ruling. "Trump v. Barbara: Tribal member citizenship arguments not in current advocacy" outperforms "tribal sovereignty framework."
- For labor and union contacts: Lead with data they can use. "The peer-reviewed case that financial precarity causes non-voting" outperforms "economic democracy research."

### When to Apply Which Template

| Constituency | Silent at Day 14 | Moderate engagement at Day 14 |
|-------------|-----------------|------------------------------|
| Law schools | Shift to clinic director; reframe as operational resource for clinic use | Send domain-specific brief for domains they cited; offer broader framework as background |
| Think tanks | Shift to program officer; reframe as citable evidence for their current publication pipeline | Offer to co-develop a domain brief for their distribution channel |
| Civil rights orgs | Lead with a named active case; offer as litigation resource | Ask what specific brief format would be most useful |
| Advocacy orgs | Shift to policy analyst; frame as campaign-specific evidence toolkit | Ask whether they would share with 2–3 peer organizations |
| Academic | Methodology-first framing is correct; check if email bounced before changing | Offer to discuss methodology; ask about course or seminar integration |

## Section 4: Path 3 — Rapid Cohort Analysis Protocol

This is the core of the document. If you are on Path 3, run this analysis before any outreach. It takes approximately 72 hours and produces a revised Phase 2 approach that is grounded in actual engagement data rather than assumptions.

### Step 1: Rule Out Technical Failure (Hours 0–6)

Before diagnosing messaging or audience, confirm that the distribution itself worked.

- [ ] Check all sent emails against the delivery status column. Were any bounced or marked as spam?
- [ ] Click every Gist/Bitly link yourself in an incognito browser. Are they resolving correctly?
- [ ] Check the Week 1 Bitly click totals. If total clicks are 0–4 despite confirmed delivery, the links are likely broken, not the messaging.
- [ ] Review Gmail sent folder. Did any emails go to your own spam filter before sending?

**If technical failure is found**: Fix it, resend, and restart the Day 14 clock. This is not a campaign failure — it is a delivery failure. The most common cause of low engagement in cold institutional outreach is not content rejection; it is routing failure (spam, wrong email address, bounced link).

**If no technical failure found**: Proceed to Step 2.

### Step 2: Root Cause Analysis — Five Hypotheses

For each hypothesis, apply a simple test before concluding it is the cause.

**Hypothesis 1: Wrong contact tier (most common)**

The email reached the right organization but the wrong person. National directors and executive directors at Tier 1 organizations receive 50–200 research submissions per month. Clinic directors, policy analysts, and research directors have more bandwidth for new materials and more direct operational use for research frameworks.

Test: Of the contacts who did not reply, what were their job titles? If >60% are executive directors, national presidents, or deans, this hypothesis is likely correct.

Fix: Shift 40–50% of Phase 2 outreach to Tier 1.5 contacts (clinical program directors, state chapter leaders, senior staff attorneys, policy directors).

**Hypothesis 2: Wrong framing for the audience**

The email led with the framework's scope (40 domains, comprehensive coverage) when the recipient's decision frame is narrow (their active litigation, their current campaign, their pending publication). Comprehensive framing reads as "this will take significant time to evaluate" — which means it gets filed rather than read.

Test: Read the first sentence of each sent email. Does it describe the framework's scope first or the recipient's current work first? If scope-first, this hypothesis is likely correct.

Fix: Revise every Phase 2 email to open with one sentence demonstrating familiarity with the recipient's specific current work (a named case, a recent publication, a named piece of legislation they are working on). See Section 3 for templates.

**Hypothesis 3: Wrong timing**

The distribution landed during a high-news cycle that buried research-oriented communications. Political crises, Supreme Court rulings, and major legislative events compress institutional bandwidth for receiving new materials. This is especially acute for organizations working on active issues (election protection, immigration litigation, healthcare advocacy).

Test: Check the news calendar for the week of launch. Were there major SCOTUS rulings, legislative votes, or executive actions that week? If yes, this is a contributing factor even if not the primary cause.

Fix: Do not re-send to the same contacts during high-news periods. Schedule Phase 2 outreach around policy windows (committee quiet periods, between major rulings) rather than calendar dates. The two best send windows are Tuesday–Wednesday, 9–11am recipient time, during weeks with no major SCOTUS or congressional calendar events.

**Hypothesis 4: Organizational dysfunction at recipient**

Some organizations are experiencing internal disruption — leadership transitions, budget crises, staff reductions — that reduces their capacity to respond to new research. This is not diagnosable in advance and is not reflective of the content quality.

Test: Check if any of the non-responding organizations have had recent public news coverage of leadership transitions or organizational changes. If so, note it and move on — this hypothesis cannot be fixed by messaging.

Fix: For organizations experiencing known disruption, skip Phase 2 direct outreach and route to their peer organizations instead. The disrupted organization may still engage later when internal stability returns.

**Hypothesis 5: Content mismatch — the research domains do not match the recipient's operational needs**

This is the least common but most informative failure mode. If the framing is correct, the delivery worked, the timing was right, and the contacts are appropriate — but engagement is still near-zero — the domain content may not match what those constituencies need right now.

Test: Compare the domains distributed in Phase 1 against the organizations' stated current priorities. If organizations working on active redistricting litigation received domains about economic precarity, there is a content mismatch.

Fix: Map Phase 2 domain sequencing directly to each constituency's current operational priorities. Do not let domain production sequence determine distribution sequence. Distribute the domain that is most relevant to each constituency's current work, not the domain that is most recently completed.

### Step 3: Constituency Cohort Breakdown (Hours 6–24)

For each of your 7 constituencies, answer these questions using your dashboard data:

| Question | Where to Find Data |
|----------|-------------------|
| How many contacts in this constituency received materials? | Contacts sheet, filter by Constituency |
| How many replied at any score? | Contacts sheet, Reply_Score column, filter by Constituency |
| How many replied at Score 3+? | Same |
| What was the average reply score for this constituency? | Calculate from Reply_Score column |
| Did any contact in this constituency share with a peer organization? | Notes field, network map |

After completing this table for all 7 constituencies, rank them from highest to lowest average engagement. The top 2–3 constituencies are your Phase 2 launch constituencies. The bottom 2–3 constituencies need revised approach before Phase 2 outreach.

**Expected pattern in weak-engagement scenarios**: Usually 1–2 constituencies will show moderate engagement even in an overall weak outcome. These are the constituencies where framing, contact tier, and domain match all aligned. The remainder are diagnosable by the five hypotheses in Step 2.

### Step 4: Domain Performance Analysis (Hours 24–48)

For each domain distributed in Phase 1, answer:

- How many Gist clicks did this domain generate (from Gist Views sheet)?
- How many replies specifically referenced this domain (from Contacts sheet notes)?
- How many replies asked follow-up questions about this domain?

Rank domains from highest to lowest on these three metrics. The top 3–5 domains are your Phase 2 priority sequence. They have demonstrated pull — organizations clicked through, read, and in some cases replied specifically about these domains.

**What domain performance data tells you**: If Domain 37 (election security) generated 80% of clicks while Domain 39 (healthcare) generated almost none despite being distributed to healthcare organizations, you have a domain mismatch with the healthcare constituency rather than a campaign-wide failure. The fix is a healthcare-specific domain (Domain 59: economic precarity, which includes OBBBA healthcare cuts and Medicaid work requirements) with the healthcare frame led explicitly.

If all domains performed uniformly poorly on clicks, the hypothesis is delivery failure or framing failure, not content mismatch.

### Step 5: Contact Type Analysis (Hours 48–72)

Group your contacts by job title and calculate reply rate for each group:

| Contact Type | Examples | Reply Rate |
|-------------|---------|-----------|
| Executive / National director | President, Executive Director, Dean | Calculate |
| Senior academic | Full Professor, Named Chair | Calculate |
| Mid-level practitioner | Policy Director, Clinical Director, Staff Attorney | Calculate |
| Research staff | Research Director, Senior Policy Analyst | Calculate |
| Communications / media | Communications Director, Editor | Calculate |

**Expected finding in weak-outcome scenarios**: Mid-level practitioners and research staff typically show higher reply rates than national directors in cold outreach to advocacy organizations. If your contacts were concentrated at the national director level and reply rates are near-zero, this is Hypothesis 1 (wrong contact tier) confirmed.

**What to do with this data**: For Phase 2, replace 40–50% of national director contacts with mid-level practitioners at the same organizations. The mid-level practitioner has operational use for the research, more bandwidth to read new materials, and often more influence over the organization's actual policy positions than the public-facing executive director.

### Step 6: Revised Phase 2 Approach (72-Hour Output)

After completing Steps 1–5, you should have:

- A root cause determination from Step 2 (likely 1–2 hypotheses confirmed)
- A ranked constituency list from Step 3 (top 2–3 for immediate Phase 2 launch)
- A ranked domain list from Step 4 (top 3–5 domains with demonstrated pull)
- A contact type finding from Step 5 (which job titles engaged, which did not)

Use these to complete the following Phase 2 revision plan:

**Revised Phase 2 Wave 1 constituency targets** (launch June 22):
- Constituency 1: [highest-engagement constituency from Step 3]
- Constituency 2: [second-highest]
- Constituency 3: [third-highest if any signal at all]

**Revised Phase 2 Wave 1 domain sequence** (by demonstrated pull):
- Lead domain: [highest-click, most-referenced from Step 4]
- Second domain: [next highest]
- Defer to Wave 2: [domains with near-zero click data]

**Revised contact tier** (based on Step 5):
- If national directors underperformed: shift to Tier 1.5 (program directors, clinic directors, policy analysts)
- If all tiers underperformed: apply framing revision (Section 3) before any re-outreach

**Revised framing** (based on Step 2 root cause):
- Hypothesis 1 confirmed → contact tier shift, framing unchanged
- Hypothesis 2 confirmed → domain-first framing revision (Section 3 templates)
- Hypothesis 3 confirmed → timing shift, re-send to same contacts in a lower-news week
- Hypothesis 4 confirmed → skip disrupted organizations, route to peer network
- Hypothesis 5 confirmed → match domain sequence to constituency operational priorities (not research production sequence)

## Section 6: What Successful Pivot Campaigns Do

Research on advocacy campaigns and policy brief distribution identifies three moves that consistently distinguish successful pivots from campaigns that stall after a weak first wave.

**Move 1: Narrow before widening.** Campaigns that attempt to expand reach after a weak first wave by distributing to more organizations almost uniformly underperform. Campaigns that narrow to the 2–3 constituencies with any signal, deepen engagement with those constituencies, and then expand from a proven base consistently outperform broad re-distribution. The instinct after weak engagement is to widen the net. The evidence says deepen first. ([VoterVoice 2025 Advocacy Benchmark](https://info.votervoice.net/2025-advocacy-benchmark-report))

**Move 2: Ideas flow between people, not papers.** The evidence from policy brief research is consistent: papers alone do not produce institutional adoption. Adoption happens when a person who read the paper connects it to another person's work and makes the introduction. The pivot should focus on identifying the 2–3 contacts who engaged and making it easy for them to share — rather than finding 20 new contacts who have not engaged. ([On Think Tanks: "A policy brief is a piece of paper"](https://onthinktanks.org/articles/a-policy-brief-is-a-piece-of-paper-it-doesnt-do-anything-on-its-own/))

**Move 3: Match the format to the institutional use case.** Policy briefs that are comprehensive frameworks are used as reference documents; they are not shared. Policy briefs that are 2-page operational resources (model brief language, specific data for a pending case, citation-ready evidence for a specific legislative argument) are shared because they are immediately useful. The pivot is not a better summary of the full framework — it is a domain-specific, use-case-specific brief that a contact can forward to a colleague with one sentence of context. ([On Think Tanks: "Research uptake: what is it?"](https://onthinktanks.org/articles/research-uptake-what-is-it-and-can-it-be-measured/))
