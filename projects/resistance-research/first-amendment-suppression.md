**Lead finding**: The United States is experiencing a coordinated, multi-vector assault on First Amendment rights. Government actors are suppressing press freedom through exclusion, warrant raids, and funding retaliation; restricting protest through state anti-protest laws and immigration enforcement used as political punishment; pressuring platforms to silence dissent through administrative subpoenas; and weaponizing defamation litigation to impose financial ruin on critics. Courts have provided partial relief, but enforcement gaps and the administration's willingness to defy injunctions mean legal wins do not always translate to protected speech.

## Section 1: Press Crackdowns

### 1.1 Pentagon Press Policy — *New York Times v. Department of Defense*

**What happened**: In September 2025, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth issued new press rules requiring journalists to sign a pledge affirming they would not gather or publish information unless the Department of Defense explicitly authorized its release. This applied even to unclassified information. Media organizations that refused to sign would lose Pentagon press credentials.

**Who did it**: Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth, Department of Defense.

**Industry response**: Every major news organization forfeited their credentials rather than comply — ABC News, NBC News, CBS News, Fox News, CNN, Newsmax, the Associated Press, Reuters, Bloomberg, the New York Times, the Washington Post, the Wall Street Journal, the Guardian, the Atlantic, NPR, Politico, the Financial Times, and others. The sole outlet that agreed to sign was a far-right publication.

**Court ruling**: In March 2026, U.S. District Judge Paul L. Friedman ruled in *New York Times v. Department of Defense* that the rules violated the First and Fifth Amendments. The judge called the policy "viewpoint discrimination and censorship" and wrote that the First Amendment was designed to empower the press to publish information "free of any official proscription."

**What happened next**: The Pentagon announced it would appeal, and simultaneously unveiled a new restriction: shutting down the Correspondents' Corridor — the workspace inside the Pentagon building where journalists have worked for decades — and replacing it with a distant "annex" at an unspecified future date. The administration treated a constitutional ruling as an invitation to try again with a different mechanism.

### 1.2 Associated Press Exclusion — *AP v. Budowich*

**What happened**: On February 11, 2025, the White House informed the Associated Press that its reporters would no longer be granted access to the Oval Office, Air Force One, and other limited-access spaces. The stated reason: the AP refused to change its editorial style guide to call the Gulf of Mexico the "Gulf of America" following President Trump's executive order.

**Who did it**: White House administration officials.

**Court ruling**: On April 8, 2025, U.S. District Judge Trevor McFadden issued a preliminary injunction ordering the ban reversed, calling the exclusion unlawful viewpoint retaliation in violation of the First Amendment. The White House responded on April 15 by eliminating the permanent wire service slot in the White House press pool entirely — a structural move designed to evade the injunction.

**Appeals**: On June 6, 2025, a three-judge panel of the D.C. Circuit granted the administration's request for a stay. On July 22, 2025, the full D.C. Circuit upheld the stay, allowing the ban to remain in effect while litigation continued.

**Current status**: The underlying case remains active. The Reporters Committee for Freedom of the Press filed an amicus brief arguing the exclusion violates the First Amendment. The ACLU filed a brief before the D.C. Circuit calling the retaliation unprecedented and warning it puts the U.S. "in dangerous company."

**Pattern significance**: This is a documented case of the executive branch using press access — a practical necessity for news coverage — as a lever to punish editorial independence. The structural response (eliminating the pool slot) signals that injunctions alone will not deter the pattern.

### 1.3 FBI Raid on Washington Post Reporter — *In re Search of Natanson*

**What happened**: On January 14, 2026, FBI agents executed a search warrant at the home of Washington Post reporter Hannah Natanson, seizing her phone, work laptop, personal laptop, and smartwatch as part of a leak investigation into a government contractor (Aurelio Perez-Lugones) charged with unlawful retention of national defense information. Natanson was not charged with any crime. This is reported as the first time the DOJ has raided a journalist's home in connection with a national security leak investigation.

**Who did it**: Department of Justice, FBI.

**Impact**: Natanson stated she could not contact her sources without her devices. The Reporters Committee for Freedom of the Press filed a brief urging the court to order the devices returned.

**Court proceedings**: On January 21, a judge blocked the government from searching the seized data. On February 24, Judge William B. Porter ruled that the court — not the DOJ — would conduct a privilege review of the devices, writing that allowing the Justice Department to review the materials would be "the equivalent of leaving the government's fox in charge of the Washington Post's henhouse." As of April 2026, federal prosecutors are pressing to overturn this ruling and gain direct access.

**April 2026 escalation**: On April 9, 2026, the Washington Post reported that federal prosecutors filed a motion seeking to overturn Judge Porter's February ruling and reclaim the authority to search Natanson's devices directly rather than have the court conduct the privilege review. The DOJ's appeal of the ruling — framing it as a barrier to a legitimate leak investigation — extends the period in which Natanson's devices remain in government possession and inaccessible to her. As of late April 2026, no court has ruled on the appeal.

### 1.4 NPR and PBS Defunding — *NPR v. Trump Administration*

**What happened**: On May 1, 2025, President Trump issued Executive Order 14290, "Ending Taxpayer Subsidization of Biased Media," directing the Corporation for Public Broadcasting to cease direct funding to NPR and PBS, characterizing their coverage as "biased and partisan." Congress subsequently approved a rescission package in July 2025 eliminating $1.1 billion in public broadcasting funding. The Corporation for Public Broadcasting shut down in January 2026 after 58 years of operation.

**Who did it**: President Trump (executive order); Congress (rescission package).

**Court ruling**: On March 31, 2026, U.S. District Judge Randolph Moss ruled that the executive order violated the First Amendment's prohibition on viewpoint discrimination and retaliation. The judge wrote that the First Amendment "draws a line, which the government may not cross, at efforts to use government power — including the power of the purse — 'to punish or suppress disfavored expression' by others," and that Trump's order "crosses that line" by singling out two speakers for their speech.

**Status**: The executive order has been blocked. The legislative rescission of CPB funding is a separate matter not resolved by this ruling; CPB's closure stands.

### 1.5 FCC Broadcast License Threats

**What happened**: FCC Chair Brendan Carr repeatedly threatened to revoke broadcast licenses from news networks whose coverage the administration found unfavorable, citing Trump's criticism of networks covering the Iran war negatively. In September 2025, after Trump said networks covering him negatively should "maybe" have their licenses revoked, Carr escalated — hours after publicly targeting ABC's Jimmy Kimmel, Kimmel's show was pulled from air.

**Who did it**: FCC Chair Brendan Carr; President Trump.

**Legal assessment**: Broadcasters have broad First Amendment protections. The FCC cannot revoke a license because it disapproves of content. The EFF published an analysis in March 2026 concluding that Carr's threats are unconstitutional. FCC Democratic Commissioner Anna Gomez stated publicly: "The FCC can issue threats all day long, but it is powerless to carry them out. Such threats violate the First Amendment and will go nowhere." Senate Democrats called for Carr to resign.

**Why this matters even if unenforceable**: The chilling effect of credible-sounding license threats changes editorial behavior. Networks facing regulatory review have incentive to soften coverage. The threat does not need to succeed to suppress speech.

### 1.6 Journalist Arrests and Assaults During Immigration Enforcement Coverage

**What happened**: In January 2026, federal immigration enforcement operations in Minnesota triggered protests. Journalists covering the demonstrations faced widespread attacks by federal officers — tear gas, pepper balls, and crowd-control munitions. The U.S. Press Freedom Tracker documented at least nine journalist assaults in the Minneapolis area in January 2026 alone. Status Coup journalist Zach Roberts was hit in the head with debris from a crowd-control munition. Photographer John Abernathy was hit twice with pepper balls, pushed to the ground, then exposed to tear gas.

Federal agents separately arrested independent journalist Georgia Fort and charged her with three felony offenses, including a hate crime, in connection with a protest at a St. Paul church on January 18, 2026. Don Lemon was also arrested on January 30, 2026. Federal lawyers reportedly sought approval from three separate grand juries before finding probable cause. A magistrate judge initially rejected the arrest warrant against Lemon for lacking probable cause.

**April 2026 status**: As of late April 2026, Lemon and Fort face federal charges under the FACE Act (Freedom of Access to Clinic Entrances) — specifically two counts: conspiracy against religious freedom and injuring, intimidating, or interfering with the exercise of religious freedom at a place of worship. Lemon pleaded not guilty on February 13, 2026. Multiple civil rights attorneys and former DOJ officials — including former Assistant Attorney General Kristen Clarke — have stated on record that they expect the charges to be dismissed, citing: (a) the FACE Act's house-of-worship provision has never been used to prosecute non-abortion-context cases; (b) the provision fundamentally misstates what First Amendment law protects; (c) a magistrate judge found no probable cause. The Cato Institute published an analysis concluding the provision may itself be unconstitutional. The DOJ's Civil Rights Division has no precedent for this application of the statute. As of April 27, 2026, no dismissal has been entered; the case remains pending before a federal district court in Minnesota.

### 1.8 Congressional Subpoena of Journalist Seth Harp — *House Oversight Committee*

**What happened**: On January 9, 2026, the Republican-led House Oversight Committee voted to subpoena investigative journalist Seth Harp — requiring him to appear and hand over documents identifying the sources for reporting on a classified U.S. military operation. The reporting at issue: on January 4, 2026, the day after U.S. forces invaded Venezuela and abducted President Nicolás Maduro and his wife, Harp published the name and photograph of the Delta Force commander who led the operation. Harp is a New York Times bestselling author and Iraq war veteran whose work focuses on links between the U.S. military and organized crime.

**Legal framework**: Reporters do not commit crimes by publishing information lawfully obtained — even classified information. The Supreme Court confirmed in *Bartnicki v. Vopper* (2001) that the First Amendment protects publication of lawfully obtained information even if the source obtained it unlawfully. The question of whether a journalist can be compelled to reveal sources via a congressional subpoena remains unsettled; reporters' privilege doctrine (based on the First Amendment and recognized in some form by a majority of federal circuits) provides at least a qualified shield. Freedom of the Press Foundation chief of advocacy Seth Stern stated Harp "merely published information that was publicly available about someone at the center of the world's biggest news story."

**Response**: Twenty press freedom and First Amendment organizations — including the ACLU, the Reporters Committee for Freedom of the Press, PEN America, the Committee to Protect Journalists, and Reporters Without Borders — signed a letter calling on the committee to rescind the subpoena, stating that Harp's reporting was "fully and squarely within the protections of the First Amendment." The CPJ explicitly called on the DOJ to refrain from pursuing criminal charges.

**Significance**: This is the first documented instance of a congressional committee voting to subpoena a journalist for source-revealing purposes since the current administration took office. It represents a distinct mechanism from DOJ action — legislative compulsion rather than executive branch prosecution — and is more difficult to challenge through standard First Amendment press protection doctrine. The committee's stated rationale was national security, which has historically been the government's most powerful claim against press-shield arguments.

### 1.7 White House Correspondents' Dinner 2026 — Journalist Petition and Security Incident

**April 25, 2026**: The first WHCA dinner Trump attended as president in his second term became a focal point for press freedom organizing and then a security incident.

**Journalist petition**: In the days before the dinner, nearly 500 retired journalists — joined by the Society of Professional Journalists and other press freedom organizations — signed a petition urging the WHCA to mount a "forceful defense of freedom of the press and condemnation of those who threaten that freedom." The petition cited: defunding of public broadcasting, dismantling of international broadcasting, physical restrictions on journalist access, personal attacks on reporters, arrest of journalists including Don Lemon and Georgia Fort, the FBI raid on Hannah Natanson's home, and administration lawsuits and threats against news organizations. The petitioners characterized the conduct as "the most systematic and comprehensive assault on freedom of the press by a sitting American president."

President Trump, the First Lady, Vice President Vance, and Cabinet members were evacuated by the Secret Service. One law enforcement officer was struck in a bullet-resistant vest and is expected to recover. No other injuries were reported. The dinner was disrupted.

**Suspect identification (April 26)**: The alleged gunman was identified as Cole Allen of Torrance, California — described as a teacher and video game developer. Allen reportedly traveled by train from Los Angeles to Chicago, then Chicago to Washington, before checking into the hotel. He was carrying a shotgun, handgun, and knives. Acting Attorney General Todd Blanche told NBC's Meet the Press on April 26 that preliminary findings suggest Allen was targeting administration officials. The motive remains under investigation.

**Significance as a press freedom marker**: The petition's breadth — cataloguing accumulated administration action (settlements, raids, exclusions, arrests, license threats) — constitutes a cross-industry admission that suppression has crossed a threshold requiring unified institutional protest. The petition targeted the WHCA itself, reflecting concern that the dinner risked normalizing conduct by staging a collegial event without demanding accountability. The security incident — occurring during the first WHCD Trump attended as president in his second term — did not diminish the underlying press freedom record the petition documented.

## Section 2: Protest Restrictions

### 2.1 State Anti-Protest Legislation — A Coordinated Wave

Since 2017, 23 states have passed at least 55 laws restricting when and how people can protest. In the past ten months alone, 100 additional anti-protest proposals were introduced nationwide. The International Center for Not-for-Profit Law (ICNL) tracks this legislation in real time; its most recent updates cover activity in Louisiana, South Dakota, Tennessee, Utah (April 2026), Georgia, and Oklahoma (March 2026).

**Common mechanisms**:
- Enhanced penalties for protest activity near "critical infrastructure" (pipelines, power plants)
- Expanded riot definitions that sweep in bystanders or organizers
- Civil liability for organizations that "encourage" or fund protests
- Mandatory minimum sentences for protest-related charges that previously carried misdemeanor penalties

**Constitutional challenges**: Anti-riot statutes face First Amendment challenges in Oklahoma, Texas, and Florida. A federal district judge in Florida issued a preliminary injunction blocking the Florida law as unconstitutionally vague and overbroad, finding it failed to adequately define "riot." Louisiana's critical infrastructure law is facing a challenge after protesters against the Bayou Bridge pipeline were prosecuted for boating on public waters adjacent to the pipeline. The Stop FUNDERS Act — a proposed federal law that would use RICO statutes against organizations deemed to have "conspired" with protest participants — is under consideration in Congress and would, if passed, expose advocacy organizations to prosecution for funding lawful protests that turn disruptive.

### 2.2 Immigration Enforcement as Protest Suppression — Khalil, Öztürk, Mahdawi, Khan Suri

One of the most significant First Amendment developments of 2025 is the federal government's use of immigration enforcement as a tool to silence political speech by non-citizens.

**The cases**:

- **Mahmoud Khalil**: Columbia University graduate student detained after leading pro-Palestinian protests. DHS transferred him 1,300 miles to Louisiana, separating him from his then eight-months-pregnant wife. He spent 104 days in ICE custody and missed the birth of his child. The ACLU challenged his detention as direct First Amendment retaliation; he was ultimately released.

- **Rümeysa Öztürk**: Tufts University doctoral student targeted for co-authoring an op-ed criticizing the university's rejection of student government resolutions regarding Gaza. Released after ACLU intervention.

- **Mohsen Mahdawi**: Columbia University student and lawful permanent resident detained for over two weeks in April 2025 for speech in support of Palestinian rights. Released.

- **Dr. Badar Khan Suri**: Georgetown University professor arrested based on scholarship, advocacy on Middle Eastern issues, and his U.S.-citizen wife's family connections to Gaza. Released.

**Legal status**: The Supreme Court has held that First Amendment protections from criminal or civil penalties apply to citizens and non-citizens alike. What remains unsettled is how those protections apply in the immigration enforcement context, where the executive branch claims broad discretion. Federal District Judge Young issued an order in 2025 establishing a presumption of retaliation for students in the plaintiff class whose immigration status was changed between March 25 and September 30, 2025. Two immigration judges who dismissed student deportation cases on First Amendment grounds were subsequently removed from their positions by the administration.

**State Department visa revocations**: As of late 2025, the State Department revoked over 6,000 student visas. In a separate incident, six foreign nationals had visas revoked after U.S. officials reviewed their social media posts and found comments deemed derisive toward conservative activist Charlie Kirk following his assassination.

### 2.4 "No Kings" Mass Mobilization — March 28, 2026 — Government Responses and Suppression Tactics

**What happened**: On March 28, 2026, an estimated eight to nine million people participated in over 3,300 "No Kings" demonstrations across all 50 states and internationally — making it the largest single-day protest in American history. The demonstrations opposed Trump administration policies including ICE enforcement actions, the Iran war, democratic backsliding, and suppression of the Epstein files. The protests were organized following a series of smaller No Kings demonstrations in June 2025 and October 2025 (the latter drew approximately seven million participants).

**First Amendment violations and suppression documented**:

- **Portland — federal agents, tear gas, arrests**: Federal agents stationed at the Portland ICE facility used tear gas, smoke bombs, and flash-bang grenades against protesters who gathered outside after the main march. Three protesters were arrested. This occurred against the backdrop of a legal dispute between the Trump administration and a federal district court that had restricted federal agents' use of chemical munitions near the building. The Ninth Circuit had paused those restrictions on March 26 — two days before the demonstration — at the administration's request, a decision that permitted the tear-gas deployment.

- **Los Angeles — mass arrests, LAPD tactical alert**: LAPD issued a dispersal order after peaceful protests, arrested 75 people (63 adults for failure to disperse, 9 juveniles, 1 on a weapons charge), and placed the department on tactical alert. Federal DHS officials and LAPD deployed tear gas against crowds after some attendees allegedly threw objects. The LAPD Chief stated the department is "committed to protecting constitutional rights," but the mass arrests for failure to disperse raised First Amendment concerns.

- **LAPD drone surveillance — pre-emptive intelligence collection**: Obtained flight records analyzed by The Intercept show the LAPD deployed Skydio X10 drones over the No Kings protest site 32 separate times. Critically, nine drone flights were initiated before the dispersal order was issued — meaning surveillance began three and a half hours before any unlawful conduct was alleged. The Skydio X10 is capable of identifying individuals from 2,500 feet and reading license plates from 800 feet. The LAPD declined to state what footage was collected, who reviewed it, or how long it is retained, raising First Amendment and Fourth Amendment concerns about preemptive intelligence-gathering on political demonstration participants.

- **New York City, Las Vegas**: By contrast, NYPD reported zero protest-related arrests. Las Vegas reported 6 arrests at an event of 6,000 attendees.

**Administration response**: White House spokesperson characterized the protests as "Trump Derangement Therapy Sessions" and stated the administration "does not think about the protest at all."

**Ninth Circuit — Portland tear gas litigation (April 2026)**: The Ninth Circuit heard oral arguments on April 7, 2026 in the consolidated Portland protest-policing litigation. The Trump administration argued that the lower court's restrictions on federal agents' use of crowd-control weapons were unlawful. The ACLU of Oregon and plaintiffs — including freelance journalists — argued their First Amendment rights were chilled when federal officers deployed tear gas and pepper-ball munitions without dispersal orders against peaceful gatherings. The Ninth Circuit's 2-1 ruling pausing the lower court restrictions remains in effect pending the full panel's decision.

## Section 3: Platform Deplatforming and Government Pressure on Tech

### 3.1 DHS Administrative Subpoenas Targeting Online Critics — *Doe v. DHS*

**What happened**: The Department of Homeland Security has used administrative subpoenas — which do not require judicial approval — to demand subscriber records from Google, Instagram, Facebook, and other platforms. The targets are not criminal suspects; they are people who criticized DHS, documented ICE activity in their communities, or attended protests.

**Documented case**: A man read a Washington Post article in October 2025 describing questionable DHS conduct in a deportation proceeding and emailed the lead DHS attorney named in the article to express concern. Four hours later, DHS issued an administrative subpoena to Google demanding his subscriber information and account metadata.

**Scale**: Google and Meta both received record numbers of administrative subpoenas in the first six months of the second Trump administration. The Washington Post reported in February 2026 that DHS has "consistently targeted people engaged in First Amendment activity."

**Legal challenges**: On February 2, 2026, the ACLU, ACLU of Northern California, and ACLU of Pennsylvania filed a motion to quash the subpoena targeting the DHS critic. DHS subsequently withdrew that subpoena. The EFF published an open letter to tech companies in February 2026 calling on them to protect users from what it termed "lawless DHS subpoenas." As of April 2026, the EFF and ACLU of Pennsylvania have filed suit under FOIA seeking records of all such subpoenas used to unmask online critics — the government has not complied with the records request.

**First Amendment significance**: *Murthy v. Missouri* (2024) established that government pressure on platforms to remove content can constitute a First Amendment violation, but the Supreme Court dismissed that case for lack of standing, leaving the core legal question unresolved. The subpoena pattern is distinct — it uses legal process not to coerce removal of speech, but to identify and locate the speaker for potential enforcement action. The chilling effect is severe: online criticism of DHS and ICE carries the risk of having one's identity unmasked and being targeted for immigration or other enforcement.

**Sources**: [ACLU — Doe v. DHS](https://www.aclu.org/cases/doe-v-dhs) | [EFF open letter](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2026/02/open-letter-tech-companies-protect-your-users-lawless-dhs-subpoenas) | [EFF FOIA suit](https://www.eff.org/press/releases/eff-sues-dhs-and-ice-records-subpoenas-seeking-unmask-online-critics-0) | [Washington Post](https://www.washingtonpost.com/investigations/2026/02/03/homeland-security-administrative-subpoena/)

### 3.2 The Murthy v. Missouri Framework and Its Gaps

**Background**: *Murthy v. Missouri*, 603 U.S. 43 (2024), arose from claims that the Biden administration pressured social media companies to remove COVID-19 and election-related content. The 5th Circuit found that some government communications "coerced or significantly encouraged" platforms to moderate content in violation of the First Amendment. The Supreme Court reversed on standing grounds — plaintiffs could not connect specific government communications to specific content removals affecting them — and declined to resolve the underlying constitutional question.

**What this means in practice**: There is no controlling Supreme Court precedent establishing when government-platform communications cross from permissible public-interest advocacy into unconstitutional coercion. Lower courts remain split. The current administration has taken advantage of this gap — not by pressuring platforms to remove content (as the prior administration was accused of doing), but by using administrative subpoenas to target the speakers themselves.

**EFF position**: The EFF wrote following *Murthy* that the Supreme Court "dodges key question" and warned that the absence of a clear standard leaves Americans without adequate protection from government attempts to influence what they can say online.

**Sources**: [EFF analysis of Murthy](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2024/07/supreme-court-dodges-key-question-murthy-v-missouri-and-dismisses-case-failing) | [ACLU on Murthy](https://www.aclu.org/press-releases/aclu-celebrates-supreme-court-decision-promoting-free-expression-online) | [Constitution Annotated](https://constitution.congress.gov/browse/essay/intro-9-2-3/ALDE_00000075/)

## Section 4: SLAPP Suits — Strategic Suppression Through Litigation

### 4.1 What a SLAPP Suit Is

A Strategic Lawsuit Against Public Participation (SLAPP) is litigation filed not to win on the merits but to impose defense costs severe enough to force a critic into silence. SLAPP suits characteristically target journalists, activists, and advocacy organizations — parties with limited resources — and are filed by corporations or powerful individuals with the litigation budget to sustain years of proceedings. A 2022 Reporters Committee survey found that 59% of journalists who had been sued for defamation reported altering their coverage as a result.

The volume is increasing: 820 SLAPPs were identified globally in 2023, up from 570 in 2022. Journalists (30.2%) and media outlets (24.7%) are the most common targets.

---

### 4.2 Energy Transfer v. Greenpeace — The High-Water Mark

**What happened**: Energy Transfer, the pipeline company behind the Dakota Access Pipeline, filed suit in North Dakota against Greenpeace USA, Greenpeace Fund, and Greenpeace International in 2017, arising from Greenpeace's activism against the pipeline during the Standing Rock protests.

**Verdict**: On March 19, 2025, a North Dakota jury returned a verdict finding Greenpeace entities liable for defamation, trespass, nuisance, conspiracy, tortious interference with business, and aiding and abetting. Initial damages exceeded $660 million. A subsequent court ruling reduced the judgment to approximately $345 million.

**Status**: Greenpeace has announced it will seek a new trial and, if necessary, appeal to the North Dakota Supreme Court. Greenpeace International separately filed suit in Dutch court in February 2025 under the European Union's anti-SLAPP Directive, testing that framework for the first time.

**Significance**: Amnesty International called the verdict "a damaging precedent for protection and promotion of human rights and climate justice." Energy Transfer itself faced over $100 million in environmental fines during the period it was suing its critics. A verdict of this magnitude, if it survives appeal, could effectively end the financial viability of major environmental advocacy organizations.

**Sources**: [Greenpeace verdict statement](https://www.greenpeace.org/usa/verdict/) | [Amnesty International](https://www.amnesty.org/en/latest/news/2025/03/usa-verdict-against-greenpeace-sets-damaging-precedent/) | [Climate Litigation Database](https://www.climatecasechart.com/collections/energy-transfer-lp-v-greenpeace-international_056d68)

### 4.3 Trump v. Des Moines Register / Ann Selzer

**What happened**: Following the 2024 election, President Trump filed suit against the Des Moines Register and pollster Ann Selzer, claiming the publication of a pre-election poll showing him behind in Iowa constituted consumer fraud. He lost Iowa by 13 points — the opposite of what the suit alleged.

**SLAPP characterization**: FIRE, which represented Selzer, characterized the suit as a textbook SLAPP — filed not to win on the merits but to subject a news organization to expensive, time-consuming litigation. A federal judge dismissed a related subscriber lawsuit with prejudice, finding it inconsistent with the First Amendment.

**Iowa anti-SLAPP law**: Iowa enacted an anti-SLAPP statute in May 2025. Trump moved his federal lawsuit to state court on June 30, 2025 — the day before the anti-SLAPP law took effect, a timing FIRE and observers noted was almost certainly not coincidental.

### 4.4 Trump v. ABC News / Paramount-CBS — Settlements That Chill

**What happened**: ABC News agreed in December 2024 to pay $15 million to Trump's presidential library and post a note of regret as settlement of Trump's defamation lawsuit over George Stephanopoulos's characterization of Trump's civil liability in the E. Jean Carroll case. In July 2025, Paramount (CBS's parent company) paid $16 million, including Trump's legal fees, to settle a suit over editorial decisions in a Kamala Harris interview on 60 Minutes.

**First Amendment significance**: Neither settlement arose from a finding of actual defamation — both networks settled because the cost and risk of years of litigation under a president with demonstrated willingness to weaponize regulatory threats exceeded the settlement amounts. The First Amendment Encyclopedia observed that these settlements represent "a dangerous step toward commander in chief's becoming editor-in-chief." No court found either network liable. The settlements are, in effect, payment for editorial independence.

**Pattern**: Trump is the first sitting U.S. president to have extracted private financial settlements from major news organizations for coverage he characterized as unfavorable. The chilling effect extends beyond the settling networks; every newsroom operates with the knowledge that sustained litigation funded by a president remains a plausible response to aggressive coverage.

### 4.6 Kash Patel v. The Atlantic — $250 Million SLAPP Filed by a Sitting FBI Director

**What happened**: On April 20, 2026, FBI Director Kash Patel filed a $250 million defamation lawsuit in federal court in the District of Columbia against The Atlantic and one of its reporters. The suit arose from an article published the week prior headlined "Kash Patel's Erratic Behavior Could Cost Him His Job," which reported — based on interviews with more than two dozen current and former FBI officials, congressional staff, political operatives, lobbyists, and hospitality workers — that Patel frequently drinks to excess, has been unreachable at critical times, and that agents once broke down a door to locate him. Patel denied the central allegations as "categorically false" and claimed his team had warned the publication hours before publication. The Atlantic stated it stood by its reporting and would "vigorously defend" the lawsuit.

**Why this is a SLAPP**: Press freedom advocates including the Freedom Forum and The Intercept characterized the suit as a textbook SLAPP. The Intercept's analysis noted that Patel filed in D.C. federal court rather than state court in part because the D.C. appellate court had previously ruled that D.C.'s state anti-SLAPP protections do not apply in federal diversity cases — a jurisdictional gap that allows suits with weak merit to survive the early dismissal mechanism that anti-SLAPP laws provide. The $250 million figure is not calibrated to any plausible calculation of actual damages; it is a litigation cost threat. The pattern is identical to the Trump v. Des Moines Register and ABC/CBS settlement cases: a powerful government-affiliated figure using litigation not to obtain a legal remedy but to impose financial and editorial cost on journalism it dislikes.

**The D.C. anti-SLAPP gap**: The legal mechanism here is important. The D.C. Anti-SLAPP Act provides early dismissal and fee-shifting for meritless SLAPP suits filed in D.C. courts. But in *Abbas v. Foreign Policy Group* (D.C. Cir. 2014), the D.C. Circuit held that D.C.'s anti-SLAPP statute does not apply in federal court. Patel's choice of federal court is therefore not procedurally neutral — it is a litigation strategy that strips the defendant of its most powerful anti-SLAPP defense. This gap is documented in the federal Free Speech Protection Act proposal (see Section 4.5), which would close it. That bill has not advanced.

**Figliuzzi dismissal (April 21, 2026)**: One day before Patel filed the Atlantic suit, a federal judge in Houston dismissed Patel's earlier defamation lawsuit against former MSNBC analyst Frank Figliuzzi — the case Patel filed in June 2025 over Figliuzzi's May 2 comment on Morning Joe that Patel "has been visible at nightclubs far more than he has been on the seventh floor of the Hoover building." The court found that statement constituted "rhetorical hyperbole that cannot constitute defamation." The Atlantic lawsuit was filed the next day — a sequencing that observers noted creates the appearance of escalation in response to a legal defeat rather than a genuine legal grievance. Patel is also reportedly pursuing additional litigation against other media organizations. The pattern — a sitting FBI director using personal litigation to intimidate news organizations covering his conduct — is without modern American precedent.

### 4.5 Anti-SLAPP Protections — State Progress, Federal Gap

**State progress**: Montana and Iowa enacted the Uniform Public Expression Protection Act (UPEPA) in May 2025. Delaware followed in September 2025. As of late 2025, 14 states have enacted UPEPA versions. However, 36 states have no meaningful anti-SLAPP protection. Anti-SLAPP laws that do exist vary widely in scope and procedure.

**Federal gap**: There is no federal anti-SLAPP law. The Free Speech Protection Act — sponsored by Sen. Ron Wyden (D-OR) and Reps. Jamie Raskin (D-MD) and Kevin Kiley (R-CA) — would provide federal anti-SLAPP protection in both federal question and diversity jurisdiction cases. As of 2026, the bill has not advanced in the Republican-controlled Congress.

**Practical consequence**: A corporation in a state without anti-SLAPP protection can sue a journalist or activist, force them through years of discovery at costs of hundreds of thousands of dollars, and then settle or drop the suit once the damage is done. The journalist's coverage has been altered or stopped; no law was broken; no court ruled on the merits.

## Section 5: Current Legal Landscape

### 5.1 Foundational Case Law

**Brandenburg v. Ohio, 395 U.S. 444 (1969)**: The controlling standard for restrictions on speech. The government cannot punish speech unless it is (1) directed to inciting or producing imminent lawless action AND (2) likely to incite or produce such action. Emotional rhetoric, calls for boycotts, and advocacy of illegal action in the abstract are protected. This standard is frequently invoked — and frequently misapplied — in contexts involving protest organizers and activists.

**Texas v. Johnson, 491 U.S. 397 (1989)**: Flag burning is protected political expression. The government cannot prohibit expression simply because it is offensive or symbolic. Relevant to protest suppression: the principle that offensive or provocative expression remains protected from government punishment absent a direct incitement to imminent lawless action.

**NAACP v. Claiborne Hardware Co., 458 U.S. 886 (1982)**: Civil rights organizers used heated rhetoric and social pressure during a boycott. The Court held that the speech was protected and that strong, impassioned advocacy is constitutionally shielded unless it incites imminent lawless action. This case is the primary precedent against holding protest organizers civilly liable for damages caused by others during demonstrations.

**Snyder v. Phelps, 562 U.S. 443 (2011)**: The Westboro Baptist Church's protests at military funerals — widely considered offensive — were protected. The Court held that speech on matters of public concern, on public property, following applicable regulations, cannot be the basis for tort liability.

### 5.3 What Is Being Challenged and What Is Winning

Courts have been the primary check on First Amendment suppression in 2025–2026. Plaintiffs have prevailed in the majority of decided cases. However, three systemic vulnerabilities limit the effectiveness of litigation as a defense:

1. **Enforcement gaps**: Court orders have been defied or circumvented. The Pentagon implemented new press restrictions days after losing in court. The White House eliminated the AP's pool slot rather than restore its access. ICE agents testified they were receiving conflicting guidance about whether to follow injunctions. A court order that is not enforced does not protect speech.

2. **Standing and ripeness**: *Murthy v. Missouri* demonstrates that courts can avoid resolving constitutional questions by finding plaintiffs lack standing, even when the underlying harm is documented. The DHS subpoena pattern and FCC license threats may generate insufficient judicially cognizable harm to sustain challenges until enforcement actually occurs — by which point the chilling effect has already operated.

3. **Speed asymmetry**: Suppression is fast; litigation is slow. A journalist's devices are seized and remain in government possession for months before a court rules. A protest organizer is deported before a habeas hearing. A network settles a lawsuit before a court finds the suit meritless. The legal system's remedies are generally retrospective; the speech harm is immediate.

## Section 7: Civil Society Prosecutions — Federal Criminal Power as a Suppression Mechanism

### 7.1 United States v. Southern Poverty Law Center — The SPLC Indictment (April 21, 2026)

**The headline finding**: On April 21, 2026, a federal grand jury in Montgomery, Alabama returned an indictment against the Southern Poverty Law Center charging 11 criminal counts: six counts of wire fraud, four counts of making false statements to a federally insured bank, and one count of conspiracy to commit concealment money laundering. The announcement was made personally by FBI Director Kash Patel alongside Acting Attorney General Todd Blanche at a DOJ press conference — staging that is without modern precedent for a wire fraud case involving a civil society organization.

**The government's theory**: Between 2014 and 2023, the SPLC secretly paid more than $3 million to individuals embedded in extremist organizations including the Ku Klux Klan, Aryan Nations, and National Socialist Party of America, and failed to disclose this to donors. Therefore, the SPLC defrauded its own donors by omitting this information from fundraising materials. The government characterizes the payments as funding "racist postings" and "fundraising" by the informants.

**The SPLC's defense**: The payments were a civil rights intelligence operation, modeled on approaches used during the civil rights era when church bombings and assassinations went unanswered. The intelligence gathered was routinely shared with local and federal law enforcement, including the FBI. The program was kept confidential to protect informant safety, not to hide wrongdoing. Interim President and CEO Bryan Fair stated the SPLC was "operating in the shadow of the height of the Civil Rights Movement, which had seen bombings at churches, state-sponsored violence against demonstrators, and the murders of activists that went unanswered by the justice system."

**Why this is a Section 7 entry — not merely a prosecution**: This indictment is documented here under First Amendment suppression because:
1. The alleged conduct — funding individuals who made protected speech — is constitutionally protected activity. The First Amendment does not permit prosecuting a civil rights organization for paying individuals whose protected expression happens to be extremist.
2. The government has not identified a single donor who claims to have been defrauded. A prosecution for fraud with no victims who feel defrauded operates as a political weapon, not a legal accountability mechanism.
3. The staging — FBI Director personally announcing a civil society wire fraud case — is a political message without modern precedent in American law enforcement.
4. The causal sequence: Patel severed the FBI's intelligence-sharing relationship with the SPLC in October 2025, calling it a "partisan smear machine." Six months later, the SPLC was indicted for providing that intelligence. The conduct being criminalized now was previously valued as counterterrorism intelligence.

**Legal analysis — multiple independent defects**:

Former DOJ Fraud Section Chief Andrew Weissmann (writing in Just Security) identified a core logical contradiction: to establish wire fraud through an omission theory, the government must show the omitted information would have mattered to donors. But the SPLC's entire purpose is fighting extremism, and paying informants to infiltrate extremist groups is precisely what fighting extremism looks like. The actual charged wire fraud amounts total only "$13,905 on a single day in 2023" — against a narrative describing "millions" across a decade. Weissmann called the theory "preposterous." No fundraising materials are quoted. No specific donor is identified as deceived.

Former federal prosecutor Aaron Zelinsky identified that the bank fraud counts allege statements that were "potentially only misleading." The Supreme Court's ruling in *Thompson v. United States* (decided approximately 14 months before this indictment) held that 18 U.S.C. § 1014 only criminalizes "false" statements, not "misleading" ones. The indictment repeatedly uses "false and misleading" — a formulation the Supreme Court expressly excluded from § 1014's reach. Former FBI agent and civil rights prosecutor Kyle Boynton stated: "I don't think any prosecutor with white-collar experience would look at this indictment and believe it makes out the elements of a crime." The money laundering count is legally dependent on the fraud counts succeeding; if those fail, the conspiracy has no predicate offense.

**The norm violations**: Indiana University nonprofit management professor Beth Gazley noted: it is "unusual for federal authorities to take this kind of action when federal funding is not involved" — charity fraud enforcement is normally a state attorney general function. Proceeding "without citing any complaints" from donors is "atypical for federal or state authorities to step in on behalf of a nonprofit's donors." No federal funding was at issue.

**The civil rights community response**: ACLU Director Anthony Romero called the prosecution "anti-American behavior harkening back to the McCarthy era." The National Urban League described it as "a declaration of war on the civil rights movement aimed at intimidation rather than accountability." Over 100 civil rights groups, labor unions, and religious organizations signed a mutual defense pact committing to defend one another against administration attacks. The administration simultaneously pardoned Proud Boys and Oath Keepers convicted of seditious conspiracy (April 2026 vacaturs) while indicting the organization that had monitored and reported on those same groups.

**The suppression logic**: Even if the charges are dismissed — as multiple former prosecutors expect — the costs of defending 11 federal criminal counts will consume organizational resources, create donor hesitancy, and establish a template for what activities constitute criminal risk for civil rights organizations that monitor extremism. Every civil rights organization with similar informant programs is now recalculating whether to continue them. This is McCarthyism applied through formal legal process: organizational disruption through threat, not conviction.
