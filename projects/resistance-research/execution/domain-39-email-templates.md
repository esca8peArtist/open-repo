I want to share a research document timed specifically for today: HHS is issuing its interim final rule on OBBBA work requirements, and the rule is explicitly exempt from public notice and comment. Organizations that want to influence implementation — exemption definitions, look-back periods, state flexibility decisions — have a narrow window before January 1, 2027. This document provides the analytical frame most healthcare advocacy organizations do not yet have.

The document is "Domain 39: Healthcare Access as Democratic Infrastructure." The argument: healthcare access is not a parallel concern to democratic participation — it is a prerequisite for it. The dominant healthcare advocacy frame treats OBBBA's harms as social welfare failures. That frame is accurate but strategically incomplete. Domain 39 makes the democratic design argument: dismantling healthcare infrastructure is a mechanism of civic exclusion that concentrates its effects on the communities already most structurally disadvantaged in the democratic system.

Five specific causal pathways, each documented with peer-reviewed evidence:

**First**, rural hospital closures directly reduce voter turnout. Cox, Epp, and Shepherd (American Political Science Review, August 2025), examining 10.5 million rural voters across three election cycles, found a 3.8 percentage-point reduction in voter turnout in communities affected by hospital closures — with the demobilizing effect largest 4–6 months before an election. As of January 2026, 417 rural hospitals are classified as vulnerable to closure. The OBBBA's Medicaid cuts cover approximately 37% of estimated rural hospital revenue losses. The other 63% is a turnout suppression mechanism.

**Second**, Medicaid enrollment offices are mandatory voter registration sites under the NVRA — but the OBBBA's administrative architecture (6-month redetermination cycles, compressed agency budgets, work requirement documentation demands) is degrading this infrastructure precisely when it is most needed. The Institute for Responsive Government documents that Automatic Voter Registration at Medicaid produces 85–90% registration rates; traditional NVRA paper-form compliance produces 1.6%. OBBBA cuts enrollment office capacity, widening that gap.

**Third**, 100 million Americans already carry medical debt. Coverage loss projections — 1.3 million new uninsured in 2026, rising to 6.8 million by 2028 — will expand medical debt burden across the same constituencies with the most limited civic participation margins. Medical debt depletes the cognitive bandwidth that political participation requires.

**Fourth**, Black women die in childbirth at 3.5 times the rate of white women. The communities with the highest maternal mortality rates are communities whose civic voice is silenced permanently by preventable death. And the historical evidence runs in both directions: after the Voting Rights Act expanded Black political power, infant mortality rates dropped by 17.5% in covered counties (Rushovich et al., American Journal of Public Health, 2024). Political access and health outcomes are causally linked.

**Fifth**, disability disenfranchisement operates through three compounding mechanisms: guardianship laws that directly strip voting rights in nine states; PAVA program defunding that eliminates the infrastructure enabling disabled voters to exercise the rights they retain; and work requirement exemption definitions that trap people in a documentation catch-22.
