**Category**: Social Policy Research — CTC/EITC Analysis
**To**: center@cbpp.org
**Subject**: The democratic participation consequence of the CTC refundability gap — research supplement for your OBBBA analysis

---

Dear CBPP Economic Security team,

I am writing to share a research document that provides a democratic participation framing for the CTC refundability argument — framing that may extend the reach of CBPP's analysis to organizations and funders who do not currently engage with tax credit policy as within their mandate.

The document is "Economic Precarity and the Civic Participation Crisis" (Domain 59, 7,200 words, 44 citations). Its central finding is that the income-based voter turnout gap reached 42 percentage points in the 2024 presidential election — 42% of Americans earning under $50,000 did not vote, compared to 16% of those earning over $100,000 (PRRI 2024; Laurison and Rastogi, Sociological Perspectives 2025). The analysis documents five causal pathways from economic precarity to democratic exclusion. The most directly relevant to CBPP's CTC work is the cognitive bandwidth pathway.

Mullainathan and Shafir's Science (2013) research — replicated in a 2024 Journal of Economic Psychology meta-analysis — establishes that financial scarcity consumes working memory and impairs cognitive performance on tasks unrelated to the debt. Their field experiment with Indian sugarcane farmers is the cleanest demonstration: the same farmer, tested before and after harvest, exhibits measurably higher cognitive performance when the income pressure is resolved. Applied to CTC policy: the 19 million children whose families receive a reduced credit or no credit at all under the current OBBBA structure — as your organization has documented — are concentrated in households managing the precise kind of financial scarcity that the research identifies as cognitively taxing. A fully refundable CTC, the reform CBPP has consistently advocated, would directly reduce that cognitive load. In democratic participation terms, the refundability argument is not only an anti-poverty argument. It is a democratic design argument.

This framing creates a coalition-building opportunity that the standard distributional critique does not. Voting rights organizations, election integrity funders, and democracy reform institutions are not currently engaged on CTC expansion — because CTC is framed as economic policy, not democratic infrastructure. Domain 59 provides the evidence base for connecting these communities. In practical terms: if a voting rights funder reads CBPP's documentation that 19 million children are excluded from the full credit, and they also read Domain 59's analysis connecting financial scarcity to the 42-point turnout gap, the CTC expansion argument lands as directly within their democratic infrastructure mandate.

The legislative vehicle for the next expansion round is the American Family Act (S. 1393, 119th Congress, Sen. Warner), which would eliminate low-income refundability restrictions and increase the credit to $6,360 for newborns. This bill has 43 Senate co-sponsors and has been referred to the Senate Finance Committee. The 2026 midterm cycle is the window for building the evidence record that makes a 2027 reconciliation push viable. Domain 59 contributes the democratic participation dimension to that record.

The full document is publicly available here: https://gist.github.com/esca8peArtist/70b18a6f26dc879e3399c6d147d882ba

I would welcome the opportunity to discuss whether any of the democratic design framing would be useful as a supplement to CBPP's existing CTC advocacy and publication work. I am also happy to share the full 59-domain framework index, which situates this document within a comprehensive analysis of democratic structural failure.

Dear ITEP Policy team,

I am writing to share research that builds on ITEP's CTC analysis and may extend its reach to organizations outside the tax policy community.

ITEP's 2026 documentation that the OBBBA's nominal $2,200 maximum CTC delivers $0 average benefit to families in the poorest fifth — because the current refundability threshold requires earned income above a level that many low-wage families do not consistently reach — is the most precise quantitative statement of the structural failure in the current credit design. The document I am sharing, "Economic Precarity and the Civic Participation Crisis," provides a complementary frame that I believe strengthens the advocacy case for the same reform ITEP has been making.

The argument: the lowest-income families who receive no CTC benefit are not only materially disadvantaged relative to higher-income CTC recipients. They are also the families most severely affected by the cognitive bandwidth depletion that financial scarcity produces. Mullainathan and Shafir's Science (2013) research established through randomized field experiments — not survey correlations — that financial pressure reduces working memory capacity and cognitive performance. The 2024 Journal of Economic Psychology meta-analysis independently replicated this finding across 17 studies. Applied directly to ITEP's distributional finding: the households receiving $0 average CTC benefit are living with the highest levels of financial scarcity, which means they are also bearing the highest cognitive tax from that scarcity. The 42-percentage-point income-based voter turnout gap documented in the 2024 election is, in part, this mechanism operating at scale.

The policy implication is identical to ITEP's existing recommendation: full refundability is necessary to deliver the credit to the families who need it most. The democratic participation argument adds a second reason for full refundability beyond the equity argument: the current structure creates a democratic design failure, not only a distributional one. This frame has traction with audiences outside the tax policy community — voting rights organizations, democratic reform institutions, and the congressional oversight staff whose mandate includes democratic participation, not only tax equity.

The legislative vehicle is the American Family Act (S. 1393). ITEP's tax incidence modeling on that bill — showing the distributional shift from the current OBBBA structure to the AFA structure — combined with Domain 59's democratic participation mechanism would constitute a more complete argument for reform than either document makes alone. I am sharing Domain 59 specifically because ITEP is the organization best positioned to evaluate whether the combination is analytically sound.

There is also a 2026 midterm angle worth considering. The November 2026 elections will be contested with an electorate in which the bottom income quintile votes at dramatically lower rates than the top. If ITEP's CTC distributional analysis can be paired with the democratic participation finding — that the families excluded from the full credit are also systematically excluded from the electorate — it creates a unified argument that is relevant both to tax policy advocates and to the election protection organizations that are activating for the midterm cycle. The audience overlap is currently zero; Domain 59's democratic design framing is the connective tissue.

Dear NWLC Economic Security team,

The document is "Economic Precarity and the Civic Participation Crisis" (Domain 59, 7,200 words, 44 citations). Its most directly relevant section for NWLC's work documents the gendered dimension of democratic exclusion through five intersecting datasets.

The core finding: single mothers in the United States face a structural impossibility problem, not a motivation problem. Twenty-one million single mothers earn an average gross income of $31,000 annually. National median childcare cost is $1,230 per month ($14,760 annually) — consuming 47 to 58 percent of gross income for this group. The Child and Dependent Care Tax Credit in its current OBBBA form provides no benefit to the poorest families because it remains non-refundable for those below the refundability threshold. The American Time Use Survey (BLS, 2025) documents that full-time employed mothers spend 7 more hours per week on childcare and household work than fathers in equivalent employment. The combination produces a time poverty effect that is specifically, measurably gendered.

The democratic participation consequence is documented in the research literature. Mothers with children under age 5 participate in organized civic activities at rates 18 to 25 percent lower than childless women, and single mothers show the most severe exclusion of any measured demographic group. Survey data consistently identifies "cannot attend meetings or voting due to childcare" as the primary participation barrier for low-income women — not motivational deficit, not lack of political information, not registration barriers. The physical impossibility of civic attendance while managing undivided childcare responsibility, at the hours and locations that participation infrastructure requires, is the documented mechanism.

This framing has a specific strategic advantage for NWLC's advocacy: it expands the coalition of organizations that can be engaged on childcare subsidy expansion and CTC full refundability. When these policies are framed as women's economic security issues, the relevant coalition is the maternal justice and gender equity community. When they are framed as democratic participation infrastructure issues, the coalition expands to voting rights organizations, democracy reform institutions, and Senate Finance members whose mandate includes democratic governance, not only economic policy. NWLC's active CTC petition infrastructure (act.nwlc.org) and your documented 80 percent public support for CTC expansion (YouGov, January 2026) are the mobilization assets for this expanded coalition.

The immediate legislative vehicles are the American Family Act (S. 1393) and the Child and Dependent Care Tax Credit Enhancement Act (S. 1421, 119th Congress), both referred to Senate Finance. ## Template 5: AFL-CIO
**Category**: Labor Federation / Legislative Affairs
**To**: feedback@aflcio.org
**Subject**: Democratic infrastructure framing for the wage and CTC agenda — research for AFL-CIO legislative work

---

Dear AFL-CIO Legislative Affairs team,

I am writing to share research that provides a democratic participation argument for the AFL-CIO's economic justice agenda — an argument that reaches congressional offices and coalition partners who have been unresponsive to the wage justice framing alone.

The document is "Economic Precarity and the Civic Participation Crisis" (Domain 59, 7,200 words, 44 citations). It documents five causal pathways from economic precarity to democratic exclusion. Three are directly relevant to the AFL-CIO's current legislative priorities.

The wage-floor pathway: At $7.25 per hour — the federal minimum unchanged for 17 consecutive years — full-time work at minimum wage generates approximately $15,000 annually. The documented consequence is multi-job holding: 8.3 million Americans hold multiple jobs simultaneously, disproportionately in low-wage sectors where the AFL-CIO represents workers or advocates for worker protections. The BLS American Time Use Survey documents that a full-time worker with two jobs and children has fewer than 17 to 27 fragmented hours of discretionary time weekly. Functional democratic participation — attending union meetings, voting, engaging with legislative advocacy — requires 4 to 6 consolidated hours monthly. For multi-job workers, those consolidated hours do not reliably exist. The Living Wage for All Act, which the AFL-CIO has endorsed, directly addresses the multi-job necessity that produces this time poverty. The democratic participation argument for the Act — that wage floors are democratic infrastructure, not only economic justice — reaches election protection organizations and democratic reform funders who are not currently engaged on minimum wage legislation.

The CTC pathway: ITEP's 2026 analysis documents that the OBBBA structure delivers 41 percent of total CTC benefits to the richest fifth of families while delivering $0 average benefit to the poorest fifth. The AFL-CIO's core constituency — low-wage workers, multi-job holders, gig workers — is disproportionately in that bottom quintile. A fully refundable CTC, achievable through the American Family Act (S. 1393), would directly reduce the financial scarcity burden that suppresses civic participation in the AFL-CIO's member base. This is the democratic participation argument for full refundability that complements the AFL-CIO's existing economic justice argument.

The gig worker pathway: Research by Bae and Haselswerdt (Perspectives on Politics, 2023) documents that gig workers vote at lower rates than traditional employees despite higher levels of expressive political activity — because scheduling unpredictability prevents the advance commitments that sustained electoral participation requires. Advance polling commitments, union meeting attendance, and voter registration deadlines all require knowing days in advance that a specific time will be available. Gig scheduling algorithms make this structurally difficult. Portable benefits legislation and worker classification reforms, which the AFL-CIO supports on labor rights grounds, address this democratic barrier simultaneously.

The cross-coalition opportunity is specific: framing these three legislative priorities — minimum wage, CTC reform, gig classification — as democratic infrastructure issues allows the AFL-CIO to build legislative coalitions that include voting rights organizations, democratic reform institutions, and academic democracy researchers who do not currently engage with labor legislation as within their mandate.
