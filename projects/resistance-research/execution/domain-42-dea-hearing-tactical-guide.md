# Domain 42 — DEA June 29 Hearing Tactical Implementation Guide
## How to Maximize Impact on the DEA Administrative Record (May 28 Deadline)

**Hard deadline**: May 28, 2026 — participation notice submission  
**Advanced prep**: May 8–21 — file development window  
**Companion files**: `domain-42-email-template.md`, `domain-42-may-28-dea-submission-guide.md`, `domain-42-contact-list.md`

## Executive Summary

This guide operationalizes the Domain 42 democratic exclusion argument for maximum regulatory impact. It provides:

1. **Worked written comment templates** (3 complete examples with inline commentary)
2. **Regulatory analysis** — what the DEA is likely to rule under each scenario, and how to position comments strategically
3. **Institutional coordination protocols** — how organizations can multiply impact through coordinated multi-voice testimony
4. **Risk assessment and mitigation** — defamation risks, anonymity options, institutional constraints
5. **Advanced tactics** — how to shape hearing dynamics as a civil rights / administrative law issue rather than a drug policy issue

**Key insight**: The DEA hearing will be dominated by pharmaceutical companies, cannabis industry operators, and law enforcement voices. Civil rights, academic, and administrative law voices are structurally underrepresented in DEA proceedings. The tactical goal is to ensure the democratic exclusion argument — which those voices uniquely can make credible — enters the hearing record through at least 3 independent organizational statements before May 28.

Any rescheduling outcome should be accompanied by internal DEA guidance that establishes HHS binding authority.

**Position 2**: The hearing's structural design — DEA ALJs operating within DEA's budget and institutional culture, adjudicating DEA's authority — creates an inherent conflict of interest that administrative law scholarship has identified as symptomatic of regulatory capture. The hearing record should reflect this structural vulnerability. A future rescheduling proceeding should use an independent ALJ pool drawn from outside the DEA institutional structure.

We intend to present evidence that the federal-state conflict over marijuana — where 24 states have enacted legalization through democratic referendum while federal law criminalizes the same conduct — represents a failure of representative democracy that structural reform must address.

**II. OBJECTIONS AND ISSUES**

We object to the exclusion of the democratic participation dimension from the June 29 hearing. We identified three specific issues:

**Issue 1: The Felony Disenfranchisement Feedback Loop.** Drug enforcement produces felony convictions at disproportionate rates in communities of color (Black Americans are arrested for marijuana possession at 3.64× the rate of white Americans despite comparable usage rates, per ACLU 2023 analysis). Felony disenfranchisement laws remove those individuals from the electorate (approximately 4.5% of voting-eligible Black adults cannot vote due to felony conviction, compared to 1.7% of the overall voting-eligible population, per Sentencing Project data). We intend to present evidence that marijuana scheduling has operated as a de facto voter suppression mechanism.

**Issue 2: Decriminalization Does Not Close the Disparity Without Structural Reform.** Recent evidence from New York City (Davis Vanguard, April 2026) and the Weill Cornell national study (American Economic Journal: Economic Policy, May 2026) demonstrate that decriminalization alone does not close racial disparities in drug enforcement. In New York City, felony drug arrest disparities between Black and white residents increased after partial marijuana decriminalization, because enforcement shifted toward felony-level charges with greater collateral consequences. This demonstrates that the scheduling question is inseparable from the enforcement disparities and disenfranchisement questions. Rescheduling marijuana without addressing the disenfranchisement feedback loop — through automatic expungement of prior marijuana convictions and automatic voting rights restoration — leaves the democratic participation problem intact.

**Issue 3: The Hearing's Institutional Design Excludes Democratic Participation Voices.** The DEA hearing is structured to amplify industry voices, pharmaceutical expertise, and law enforcement perspectives. The democratic participation dimension — voting rights, felony disenfranchisement, racial equity — is structurally underrepresented. We request hearing opportunity to ensure the hearing record contains the democratic participation analysis that civil rights organizations are uniquely positioned to contribute.

**Position 1 — Automatic Expungement Requirement**: Any marijuana rescheduling should be coupled with automatic expungement of prior marijuana convictions in the federal system and (through incentive funding to states) automatic expungement at the state level. The current proposal to reschedule marijuana prospectively, while leaving prior convictions intact, perpetuates the disenfranchisement feedback loop: individuals whose convictions disqualified them from voting remain disqualified even after the product they were convicted of is reclassified.

**Position 2 — Voting Rights Restoration**: The hearing record should document that federal marijuana rescheduling provides an opportunity for a coupled federal voting rights restoration initiative targeting individuals disqualified for drug convictions that no longer exist at the state level. The four-state data point (Virginia, 2025; Massachusetts, pending; Maryland, pending; South Carolina, pending) demonstrates state-level momentum for this reform. Federal leadership on coupled rescheduling + voting rights restoration would amplify state reforms and address the structural mechanism.

**Position 3 — Racial Equity Impact Assessment**: The rescheduling proceeding should produce a racial equity impact assessment documenting the distribution of benefits and harms across demographic groups. The Weill Cornell evidence demonstrates that legalization produces differential enforcement outcomes across race. The DEA record should document this differential impact and require mitigation strategies.

### Testimony Timing and Coordination Strategy

**File participation notices by May 28** (written notice); **file formal testimony 14 days before hearing opens** (June 15, 2026).

If multiple organizations are filing testimony on Domain 42:
1. **Avoid verbatim duplication**: Coordinate with DPA, NORML, ACLU before June 15 to ensure each organization's testimony addresses a distinct aspect of Domain 42. (Example: DPA addresses regulatory capture; ACLU addresses disenfranchisement feedback loop; academic institution addresses procedural/structural design.)
2. **Create cross-citations**: Each testimony should cite the other organizations' anticipated testimony: "Our partners at [Org] are addressing the regulatory capture question; our testimony focuses on the demographic and voting rights dimension."
3. **Stagger testimony filing**: If possible, file testimonies in the sequence that creates narrative flow: regulatory capture evidence (Day 1) → enforcement disparity evidence (Day 2) → voting rights / democratic participation evidence (Day 3) → remedy positioning (Day 4). The hearing officer will sequence testimony in the order it's received; staggered filing creates a narrative structure.

## Part 3: Risk Assessment and Mitigation

Organizations filing Domain 42-based testimony should assess three risk categories:

### Risk 1: Defamation Claims

**Risk scenario**: The testimony characterizes DEA decision-making as influenced by institutional "capture" or bias. DEA itself cannot sue for defamation (government entities lack standing). However, individual DEA officials or politicians who feel personally attacked could in theory mount claims.

**Mitigation**:
- **Focus on institutional design, not personal animus**. Never say "DEA officials are corrupt" or "the Administrator deliberately suppressed HHS findings." Instead: "The institutional structure of DEA proceeding design creates systematic bias toward restriction, as demonstrated by 56-year divergence between statutory text and administrative practice."
- **Cite scholarly authority**. Mason Marks's Yale Law Journal article provides institutional analysis without personal attack. The 56-year divergence is a documented pattern, not an allegation.
- **Use "design" language, not "intent" language**. "The proceeding structure incentivizes restriction" (defensible) vs. "the hearing officer will rule against legalization" (problematic).

**Risk level**: LOW. Defamation claims against regulatory participation are rare and have high barrier to success (heightened public figure standard). Academic analysis of institutional design is core protected speech.

---

### Risk 2: Institutional Constraint / Donor Backlash

**Risk scenario**: An organization files testimony emphasizing regulatory capture and institutional bias, then a major donor or board member objects to the "politically charged" positioning.

**Mitigation**:
- **Board alignment pre-filing**: If your organization is filing Domain 42 testimony, brief your board or governance structure before May 28 submission. Internal alignment prevents post-filing reversal.
- **Frame as institutional analysis, not advocacy**: Separating Drug Policy Alliance (advocacy wing) from testimony (analysis wing) can help if donors object. "Our testimony addresses administrative law structure; our advocacy positions are separate policy preferences."
- **Documentation**: Document your internal decision-making process. If challenged, you can show the testimony was deliberate institutional positioning, not a staff member's unauthorized action.

**Risk level**: MEDIUM for organizations heavily dependent on foundation funding from politically sensitive donors. LOW for organizations with diverse funding bases and independent boards.

---

### Risk 3: Anonymity and Institutional Coverage

**Risk scenario**: An organization wants to file Domain 42 testimony but fears institutional retaliation or visibility.

**Mitigation**:
- **Academic/research institution cover**: Some organizations can file through an affiliated research program or center rather than the main organization. (Example: A university's drug policy research center files, rather than the university general counsel's office.)
- **Co-author strategy**: Co-author the testimony with multiple organizations or institutional partners. Individual accountability diffuses across the group.
- **Written testimony only**: File written testimony without in-person hearing participation. Participation notice creates administrative standing; in-person testimony is optional. This allows the testimony to enter the record without institutional visibility.

**Risk level**: LOW if using written-testimony-only approach. MEDIUM if planning in-person testimony.

## Part 4: Institutional Coordination Protocols

If 3+ organizations are filing Domain 42 testimony, coordination multiplies impact. Here's how:

### Pre-Filing Coordination (May 8–25)

1. **Identify participating organizations** (target: 3–5 organizations representing different sectors)
   - Drug policy: Drug Policy Alliance, NORML
   - Civil rights: ACLU, NAACP LDF, Sentencing Project
   - Academic: Law school(s), policy institute(s)
   - State government: State AGs (if interested)

2. **Assign testimony focus areas** (avoid overlap):
   - Organization A: Regulatory capture and statutory authority (Issue 1)
   - Organization B: Disenfranchisement and racial equity (Issue 2)
   - Organization C: Federal-state federalism conflict (Issue 3)
   - Organization D: Procedural/structural reforms (cross-cutting all issues)

3. **Create cross-reference protocol**:
   - Each organization's testimony includes: "Our partners at [Org B] are addressing [Topic B], which complements our focus on [Topic A]."
   - This signals coordinated analysis without joint testimony (which triggers different procedural requirements).

4. **Share evidence**: Coordinate on which organizations cite which evidence sources.
   - DPA/NORML may cite cannabis industry data
   - ACLU/Sentencing Project cite disenfranchisement data
   - Academic institutions cite Mason Marks and international comparative law

### Testimony Filing (May 28 – June 15)

5. **File participation notices by May 28** (simple statement of interest; no coordination required)

6. **File formal testimony by June 15** (14 days before hearing)
   - Stagger filing to create sequence: Mon (Org A, regulatory capture), Tue (Org B, disenfranchisement), Wed (Org C, federalism), Thu (Org D, remedies)
   - DEA will receive and potentially sequence them in filing order

7. **File joint amicus submissions** (if strategically valuable):
   - Option: Combined brief from multiple organizations on specific procedural or legal issue
   - Example: "Joint Statement on Administrative Procedure Deficiencies in the June 29 Hearing Design" (signed by 5+ organizations)
   - This is separate from individual testimony but signals unified position

### Hearing Participation (June 29 – July 15)

8. **Coordinate live testimony sequence** (if organizations plan in-person appearance):
   - Hearing officer will schedule testimony time-slots. Coordinate to ensure your group testifies in the sequence (regulatory capture → enforcement disparities → disenfranchisement → remedies) rather than scattered.
   - Designate a primary speaker per organization (vs. multiple speakers, which uses time inefficiently).

9. **Cross-examination support**:
   - If a pharmaceutical company or law enforcement witness testifies, coordinate cross-examination questions across organizations
   - Example: DPA cross-examines on scientific evidence; ACLU cross-examines on demographic impact; academic institution cross-examines on procedural compliance

10. **Media coordination** (post-hearing):
    - If any participating organizations plan media outreach post-hearing, coordinate messaging
    - Different organizations can emphasize different aspects (DPA emphasizes public health; ACLU emphasizes voting rights; academic institution emphasizes administrative law precedent)
