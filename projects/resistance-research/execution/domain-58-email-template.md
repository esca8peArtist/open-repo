**Subject**: Post-Callais doctrinal model for tribal voting rights — Montana SB 490 state constitutional template

Dear [Contact Name / Team],

I want to share a research document that documents the most important post-*Callais* state constitutional victory in tribal voting rights — and that provides a framing argument connecting tribal voting access to the broader democratic infrastructure debate.

The *Callais* ruling eliminated VRA Section 2's discriminatory-effects standard, making federal challenges to structural tribal voting barriers functionally unavailable. The Montana First Judicial District Court's May 11, 2026 injunction blocking Senate Bill 490 — which would have eliminated eight hours of Election Day registration that Native voters disproportionately rely on — is the immediate proof-of-concept that state constitutional challenges work where federal Section 2 is now foreclosed. This is the doctrinal template your litigation team needs for post-*Callais* tribal voting cases.

The democratic infrastructure argument connects this to your broader framing. Domain 58's core finding: the 574 federally recognized tribal nations are sovereign governments. Their voting barriers are therefore not simply civil rights violations — they are the suppression of democratic participation in sovereign governing bodies. The argument that Native voter registration barriers function as mechanisms of democratic exclusion in 574 governance systems has not yet been integrated into mainstream voting rights advocacy.

The structural barriers Domain 58 documents are not incidental. Tribal enrollment offices — the primary ID-issuing institutions for tribal members — are not designated NVRA voter registration agencies in most states. Tribal IDs are not universally accepted as valid voter identification. Polling places are routinely 30–50 miles from reservations on impassable roads. In Montana — where tribes comprise about 5% of the eligible electorate — 35,000 of the 65,000 eligible Native voters were not registered heading into the 2024 Senate race. The race that Jon Tester won by 18,000 votes in 2018 with Native voter mobilization demonstrates that this registration gap is the difference-making variable in federal elections.

*Northern Cheyenne Tribe v. Montana* — challenging Montana's failure to designate tribal enrollment offices as NVRA agencies — is the current test case. The post-*Callais* doctrinal environment makes this case harder. The SB 490 injunction provides the state constitutional vehicle that survives that environment.

The Native American Voting Rights Act (not yet passed) would provide universal tribal ID acceptance, on-reservation polling places, same-day registration, and residential address accommodation. Domain 58's analysis provides the democratic-infrastructure argument for why NAVRA is a democracy-preservation bill, not merely a civil rights bill — which broadens its co-sponsor coalition.

**Subject**: Domain 58: Tribal sovereignty as democratic infrastructure design — new analytical frame for federal Indian law and comparative democracy scholarship

Dear [Contact Name / Faculty],

I want to share a research document that proposes an analytical frame absent from both the federal Indian law literature and the comparative democracy literature — and that I think provides a productive research agenda for your work.

The standard frames for tribal sovereignty scholarship are treaty rights (legal), self-determination (political theory), and racial equity (civil rights). Domain 58 adds a fourth: democratic infrastructure design. The 574 federally recognized tribal nations are not a demographic group or a protected class. They are sovereign governments — democracies — whose authority derives from the supreme law of the land. The question "how does the federal government's defunding of tribal programs affect democratic governance in the United States?" is analytically distinct from "how does the federal government's defunding of tribal programs affect Native Americans?" — and produces a different, broader research program.

The analytical contributions that distinguish this frame:

**The DOGE trap**: When DOGE terminates BIA office leases and IHS staff, it defunds the governmental infrastructure of sovereign nations that have transitioned to self-governance under ISDEAA. The federal government cannot simultaneously require tribes to assume governmental functions under ISDEAA and then defund those functions through executive action. This is a constitutional law argument — not just a policy argument — with implications for the nondelegation and impoundment doctrines.

**The health-participation nexus applied to treaty beneficiaries**: The Oregon Health Insurance Experiment (Finkelstein et al., 2012) demonstrated that Medicaid coverage increases civic participation. Applied to IHS: the treaty-obligated healthcare system for 2.6 million Native Americans is the treaty-based democratic infrastructure through which the health-participation nexus operates for tribal members. IHS defunding is not merely a healthcare problem — it is a democratic participation infrastructure problem cognizable under the treaty itself.

**Post-Callais doctrinal architecture**: The May 18, 2026 GVR in *Turtle Mountain v. Howe* — worse than cert denial; remanded to the Eighth Circuit under hostile post-*Callais* standard — requires a doctrinal response. The Montana SB 490 state constitutional model is the template. Research documenting the specific state constitutional provisions in tribal voting rights states that survive post-*Callais* federal preemption analysis would be the most immediately actionable contribution.

**The termination precedent**: The current DOGE-directed defunding lacks even the procedural form of congressional authorization that 1950s termination statutes had. This makes the current defunding more clearly unconstitutional than formal termination — a doctrinal argument that federal Indian law scholars should be making.
