DPA Policy/Communications Team,

The DEA administrative hearing on marijuana rescheduling begins June 29. I am reaching out because I have completed a democratic-design analysis of the regulatory capture problem at the center of this hearing, and I want to make sure DPA has it before the deadline closes.

The June 29 hearing is not primarily a drug policy proceeding. It is a structural case study: DEA administrative law judges — who are DEA employees, within DEA's budget, within DEA's institutional culture — will adjudicate a public health question about whether DEA should continue to administer marijuana as a Schedule I substance. The APA does not require that hearing adjudicators be independent of the agency whose regulatory posture is being adjudicated. That design is the problem.

The analysis — Domain 42: Drug Policy, Regulatory Capture, and Democratic Legitimacy — documents three interlocking mechanisms that have insulated federal drug policy from democratic accountability:

**First**, DEA regulatory capture: The scheduling process has functioned as a de facto veto over HHS public health expertise since the OLC opinion that converted HHS from a co-equal scientific authority into an advisory voice DEA can override. Mason Marks's 2024 Yale Law Journal analysis documents the statutory divergence: "Courts and agencies have given less deference to the Secretary's [HHS] recommendations than the CSA demands, deferring instead to DEA's statutory interpretations." The June 29 hearing structure is the operational expression of that 56-year institutional drift.

**Second**, the felony disenfranchisement feedback loop: Drug enforcement produces felony convictions at racially disparate rates — Black Americans arrested for marijuana possession at 3.64 times the rate of white Americans (ACLU 2023). The Sentencing Project documents 4 million Americans currently disenfranchised due to felony conviction. The communities most subjected to enforcement have reduced political voice in the jurisdictions where enforcement is most aggressive. The Weill Cornell study (Meinhofer et al., American Economic Journal: Economic Policy, May 1, 2026) — the most comprehensive national dataset on legalization and racial arrest disparities — finds these disparities persist post-legalization without structural enforcement reform. The communities who would vote to revise drug policy are systematically removed from the electorate by the enforcement regime they would revise.

**Third**, the federal-state democratic conflict: 24 states have enacted recreational cannabis legalization through direct-democracy processes with documented majority support — Colorado Amendment 64 (55.32%), California Prop 64 (57.13%), Michigan Prop 18-1 (56%). The SAFER Banking Act has passed the House seven times with bipartisan support and has not received a Senate floor vote. Federal Schedule I classification continues to override these democratic majorities.

Domain 42 is 6,860 words, 54 citations, CC Attribution 4.0. It covers all three mechanisms, the April 23 DOJ order context, international comparative evidence (Canada, Germany, Portugal), and a three-tier structural reform architecture. Use it in filings, testimony preparation, advocacy materials, and organizational communications without restriction.

## SECTION 7: FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS (FOR HANDLING RESPONSES)

**Q: We already filed in the 2024 proceedings. Do we need to file again?**
A: Yes. The 2024-2025 proceedings were withdrawn by DEA before the hearing stage. All prior participation notices and Notices of Appearance were voided when the proceedings were withdrawn. DEA-1362 is a new proceeding — fresh notice is required. Your 2024 filing is not reflected in the current docket.

**Q: NORML was not selected in 2024. Why would filing again make a difference?**
A: Two reasons. First, it builds the administrative record. Each organization that files and is not selected adds to the documented pattern of DEA excluding civil rights and reform organizations — that pattern is itself evidence for the democratic accountability argument Domain 42 makes. Second, this hearing is under different political conditions than 2024; the participant selection dynamics may differ.

**Q: Is filing a participation notice the same as testifying?**
A: No. Filing a participation notice preserves the option to testify. DEA notifies designated participants by June 22, 2026. Organizations that file and are not selected as designated participants can still submit written comments to the docket — which is a different track but still creates an official record.

**Q: What if our organization doesn't have staff capacity to prepare testimony?**
A: The participation notice requires only three sentences (see Section 4 above). It does not commit to appearing or preparing testimony. File the notice now; decide later whether to pursue the participation slot. The notice is the low-cost option; testimony preparation is a separate decision.

**Q: The Gist URL isn't working / the document seems incomplete.**
A: Confirm the Gist is set to Public (not Secret). Confirm the filename ends in `.md` so GitHub renders it as Markdown. If the URL resolves but renders as raw text, the `.md` extension may be missing. If the URL is entirely dead, check that the Gist was created under the esca8peArtist account and not accidentally created as a Secret Gist.

**Q: Can multiple people from the same organization file separate participation notices?**
A: Yes. Volume matters in the administrative record — DEA selects 25 of 160+ requestors, and filing volume from a consistent institutional perspective strengthens the record regardless of whether DEA selects any individual filer. SSDP chapters, for example, could each file separate notices.

**Q: What is Section 591 and does it affect the deadline?**
A: Section 591 was a proposed appropriations rider that would have frozen the DEA rescheduling process. It was dropped from the final FY2027 CJS bill. Do not mention it in any filing or email. The May 28 deadline is hard and unchanged.
