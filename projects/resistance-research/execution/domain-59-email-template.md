I want to share a research document that makes an argument your organization is positioned to make but has not yet made: the economic barriers your advocacy addresses are not welfare concerns with civic side effects. They are democratic design failures with poverty as their operating mechanism.

The document is "Domain 59: Economic Precarity and the Civic Participation Crisis." The opening finding: the income-based voter turnout gap reached 42 percentage points in the 2024 presidential election — 42% of Americans earning under $50,000 did not vote, compared to 16% of those earning over $100,000 (PRRI 2024). This is not disengagement. This is the predictable output of an architecture that systematically depletes the discretionary time and cognitive bandwidth that democratic participation requires.

The mechanism is quantified. A full-time multi-job worker with children consumes 52+ hours in paid work and commute, 15-20 hours in childcare and household management, 56 hours in sleep — leaving fewer than 17-27 fragmented hours of discretionary time weekly. Functional democratic engagement requires 4-6 consolidated hours monthly. For this worker, those consolidated hours do not reliably exist.

Why this framing matters for your advocacy: when minimum wage, childcare, and housing affordability are framed as economic justice issues, the coalition is the economic justice community. When they are framed as democratic infrastructure issues — the structural mechanisms through which 40% of the American electorate is locked out of meaningful civic participation — the coalition expands to voting rights organizations, democracy reform institutions, academic democracy scholars, and the congressional oversight staff who have democratic governance as their explicit mandate.

The legislative vehicles with the most direct democratic participation effect are specific and timely:
- **Raise the Wage Act** (H.R. 2743, S. 1332) and **Living Wage for All Act**: federal minimum wage elevation is the single highest-leverage change for time-poverty reduction; Senate Finance Committee is the entry point post-midterms
- **Childcare subsidy expansion**: Child and Dependent Care Tax Credit Enhancement Act (S. 1421) is in committee; the childcare democratic design argument — childcare inaccessibility structurally excludes single mothers from participation structures that require physical presence at specific times — is absent from the current childcare advocacy framing
- **RTC/CTC expansion**: Restoring and expanding the Child Tax Credit as it existed pre-OBBBA would directly reduce the medical debt and precarity pathways that generate the most severe cognitive taxation effects

Domain 59 provides the empirical architecture (five mechanistically documented pathways, Mullainathan/Shafir scarcity research, Oregon RCT civic participation finding, Harvard JCHS housing data, PRRI 2024 turnout survey) for this reframe.

**Subject**: Domain 59: The income-participation gap is the largest voting rights gap in the US — framing and evidence for your advocacy

Dear [Contact Name / Team],

I want to share a research document that argues the largest single determinant of whether Americans vote is income — and that the gap is wider today than any documented period in modern US history.

The income-based voter turnout gap in the 2024 presidential election reached 42 percentage points (PRRI 2024; Laurison and Rastogi 2025, *Sociological Perspectives*). Forty-two percent of Americans earning under $50,000 did not vote, compared to 16% of those earning over $100,000. This gap exceeds every measured racial and geographic turnout gap in the same election. If the income-participation gap were closed to the participation rate of the highest-income quartile, it would represent the addition of roughly 40 million voters to the active electorate.

This finding has a specific implication for voting rights advocacy that Domain 59 makes explicit: the most effective voter suppression mechanism in the United States is not voter ID requirements, registration deadlines, or polling place closures — though all of these operate. It is the material architecture of time poverty. And unlike legislative voter suppression, it is not easily litigated. It requires a policy intervention: reducing the material barriers that produce time poverty.

Domain 59 documents five mechanistically distinct pathways from economic precarity to democratic exclusion, each with an established empirical literature:
1. Time-poverty (wage stagnation + multi-job requirement): 50+ hour work weeks leave fewer than 4-6 consolidated hours monthly for civic participation
2. Housing instability: Slee and Desmond (2023) eviction-turnout link; PNAS 2024 residential mobility finding that moving decreases voter participation for nearly two decades
3. Medical debt cognitive taxation: Mullainathan/Shafir (2013) scarcity research — financial debt management loads working memory, reducing performance on tasks including political decision-making
4. Gendered caregiving: 21 million single mothers at $31,000 average income face childcare costs consuming 47–58% of gross income; participation infrastructure assumes unencumbered adults
5. Gig worker electoral exclusion: Bae and Haselswerdt (2023) finding that gig workers vote at lower rates than traditional workers despite higher expressive participation

The policy leverage windows are specific and current: Senate Finance Committee CTC/RTC markup; Child and Dependent Care Tax Credit Enhancement Act (S. 1421); OBBBA implementation scrutiny (Nebraska Medicaid work requirements began May 1, 2026 — 7.5-10 million projected to lose coverage by 2027, with a direct medical debt and precarity escalation consequence).

**Subject**: OBBBA implementation and the civic participation consequence — Domain 59 medical debt and healthcare precarity analysis

Dear [Contact Name / Team],

I want to share a research document that makes the civic participation argument for healthcare access — and that may provide new framing for your OBBBA analysis work.

The standard framing for OBBBA's Medicaid cuts focuses on health outcomes: 7.5-10 million people losing coverage (CBO analysis) means deferred care, worse disease management, and higher mortality. All of this is accurate and important. Domain 59 documents a parallel consequence that healthcare policy analysis has not yet integrated: the civic participation effect.

The mechanism is documented in the academic literature. Mullainathan and Shafir's *Science* (2013) scarcity research — independently replicated in a 2024 *Journal of Economic Psychology* meta-analysis — demonstrates that financial scarcity, including medical debt management, consumes working memory and reduces cognitive performance on tasks unrelated to the debt. The same person exhibits lower cognitive function when managing financial pressure than when the pressure is resolved — the same farmer, tested before and after harvest. Applied to medical debt: the 100 million Americans managing some form of medical or dental debt (CFPB, 2025) are operating under a sustained cognitive tax that reduces the bandwidth available for political engagement.

The Oregon Health Insurance Experiment (Finkelstein et al., 2012) provides the causal counterpoint: Medicaid coverage increases voter registration and civic participation because health security reduces the cognitive bandwidth depletion that illness and financial precarity impose. This is an RCT finding — not a correlation. Coverage causes participation. Removing coverage reduces participation.

The OBBBA implementation cascade makes this acute. Nebraska began Medicaid work requirements May 1, 2026 — the first state. CBO projects 15 million Americans will become uninsured nationally under OBBBA's full implementation. Each of these coverage losses is a medical debt pathway activation: previously covered conditions become out-of-pocket; emergency department utilization increases; payment plan negotiations, collection disputes, and insurance appeals begin consuming the cognitive bandwidth that political engagement requires.

The policy leverage window: Senate Finance Committee oversight of OBBBA implementation is the primary 2026 vehicle. Domain 59 provides the democratic participation argument for why healthcare access is a democratic infrastructure issue — which extends the oversight argument from health policy to democracy policy and broadens the coalition of senators who can be engaged on implementation scrutiny.

**Subject**: Childcare inaccessibility as democratic exclusion — Domain 59 gendered participation analysis

Dear [Contact Name / Team],

I want to share a research document that makes the democratic design argument for childcare access — and that may provide new framing for your advocacy with audiences outside the maternal justice community.

The core finding: single mothers in the United States face childcare costs that consume 47–58% of their $31,000 average gross income. This is not a financial stress story. It is a democratic design failure. Democratic organizing infrastructure — union halls, political campaigns, advocacy organizations, town halls, public hearings — almost universally requires physical presence at specific times. When a mother cannot cover childcare for those hours, and when the cost of doing so is prohibitive or the infrastructure simply does not exist (51% of Americans live in childcare deserts), democratic participation is not a matter of preference. It is a matter of physical impossibility.

Domain 59 documents the gendered dimension with data: among full-time employed parents, mothers spend 19.5 hours per week on combined childcare and household work — 7 more hours than fathers doing the same paid work (BLS American Time Use Survey, 2025). Mothers are 40% more likely than fathers to cite childcare challenges as having adversely affected their careers. Research consistently shows a participation gap between mothers and childless women that exceeds the gap between women and men overall — and single mothers show the most severe exclusion. Survey data finds single mothers cite "cannot attend meetings/voting due to childcare" as the primary participation barrier at rates exceeding any other demographic group.

The democratic infrastructure argument extends the childcare advocacy frame in two ways. First, it reaches new audiences. When childcare is a women's economic problem, the audience is maternal justice organizations. When it is a democratic participation infrastructure problem, it reaches voting rights organizations, democracy reform institutions, and the congressional oversight community — all of whom have a mandate to address democratic exclusion and who have not yet connected childcare inaccessibility to their work.

Second, it strengthens the specific legislative argument. The Child and Dependent Care Tax Credit Enhancement Act (S. 1421, 119th Congress) is the immediate vehicle. The democratic infrastructure argument — that childcare subsidy expansion reduces the structural barriers that make democratic participation physically impossible for 21 million single mothers — is more broadly persuasive than the economic efficiency argument, because it activates a broader coalition.
