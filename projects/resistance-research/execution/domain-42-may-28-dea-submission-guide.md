# Domain 42 DEA Hearing Submission Guide
## Participation Notice Deadline: May 28, 2026

**Docket No. DEA-1362** | Submit to: nprm@dea.gov | Mail deadline: May 20 (allow postal transit)

## What This Guide Is For

The DEA administrative hearing on marijuana rescheduling begins June 29, 2026. The deadline to file a written notice of desired participation is **May 28, 2026**. Filing a participation notice costs nothing and requires no commitment to testify. It preserves your organization's right to present testimony, submit written evidence, and cross-examine witnesses during the June 29 – July 15 hearing.

This guide summarizes the Domain 42 findings most relevant to the DEA proceeding, provides three to five ready-to-use comment points grounded in Domain 42 evidence, gives the exact submission procedure, and includes a day-by-day timeline for the 21-day window ending May 28.

**Domain 42** (Drug Policy, Regulatory Capture, and Democratic Legitimacy) is a 6,700-word, 54-citation research document completed May 7, 2026. CC Attribution 4.0 — excerpt, cite, and adapt freely.

## Part 1: The Democratic Exclusion Framing (Why This Argument Is Different)

Standard drug policy advocacy addresses the DEA hearing as a public health question: does the evidence support rescheduling? Domain 42 addresses it as a democratic design question: why has this evidence been systematically excluded from producing policy change for 50 years?

The answer is three interlocking mechanisms that have insulated federal drug policy from democratic accountability:

**Mechanism 1: Regulatory Capture of the Scheduling Process.** The Controlled Substances Act assigned HHS the role of primary scientific authority in scheduling decisions. The OLC opinion issued during the Biden administration's rescheduling process converted HHS from a binding scientific authority into an advisory voice that DEA can override. Mason Marks's Yale Law Journal analysis ("Separation of Drug Scheduling Powers," 2024) documents how DEA's administrative practice has diverged from the statute's own text for 56 years. The June 29 hearing is conducted by DEA Administrative Law Judges — DEA employees, within DEA's budget, within DEA's institutional culture — adjudicating a public health question about DEA's own scheduling authority. This is regulatory capture operating in the structure of the proceeding itself.

**Mechanism 2: The Felony Disenfranchisement Feedback Loop.** Drug enforcement produces felony convictions disproportionately in communities of color. Felony disenfranchisement removes those individuals from the electorate. The communities most subjected to drug enforcement are underrepresented in the political processes that set drug policy, because drug enforcement has removed a disproportionate share of their eligible voters. The Weill Cornell national study (Meinhofer et al., American Economic Journal: Economic Policy, May 1, 2026) documents that legalization alone does not close racial disparities — in New York City, felony drug arrest disparities between Black and white residents increased after partial decriminalization, as enforcement shifted toward felony-level charges with greater disenfranchisement consequences.

**Mechanism 3: Federal-State Democratic Conflict.** Twenty-four states have enacted recreational cannabis legalization through democratic processes — direct ballot initiatives with documented majority support. Colorado Amendment 64 (55.32% in favor, 2012). California Proposition 64 (57.13%, 2016). Michigan Proposal 18-1 (56%, 2018). Federal law prevents implementation of what those democratic majorities voted for. The SAFER Banking Act has passed the House seven times; it has never received a Senate floor vote despite endorsement from 32 state attorneys general.

This democratic-design argument is largely absent from the anticipated testimony structure of the June 29 hearing, which will be dominated by pharmaceutical companies, cannabis industry operators, and anti-legalization advocates. Civil rights organizations, administrative law experts, and state AG offices filing participation notices focused on this dimension would shift the hearing's analytical frame in a direction the DEA record is otherwise unlikely to contain.

## Part 2: Comment Points for DEA Submission

The following five points are drawn directly from Domain 42's evidence base. Each states an objection or position as required by the participation notice format (see Part 3). Adapt language to your organization's voice; cite the underlying sources directly.

**Comment Point 1 — The hearing structure is itself a conflict of interest (Section 2, Domain 42).**
DEA Administrative Law Judges are DEA employees operating within DEA's institutional structure. They are adjudicating a question about DEA's scheduling authority. The APA does not require ALJ independence from the agency, but a structurally neutral proceeding would use ALJs drawn from an independent pool — as Domain 42 Section 6.1 proposes. The hearing record should include an objection to the proceeding's structural conflict of interest as a matter of administrative law transparency.

**Comment Point 2 — HHS's binding scientific authority must be restored (Sections 2.1–2.3, Domain 42).**
The CSA's statutory text, as analyzed by Marks (Yale Law Journal, 2024), establishes HHS as the primary scientific authority in scheduling. The OLC opinion converting HHS to advisory status contradicts the statute's plain text. The June 29 hearing should produce a scheduling outcome that treats the HHS recommendation as binding — not as a starting point that DEA may override based on enforcement institutional interests.

**Comment Point 3 — The racial disenfranchisement feedback loop must be part of the hearing record (Section 3, Domain 42).**
Black Americans are arrested for marijuana possession at 3.64 times the rate of white Americans despite comparable usage rates (ACLU, 2023). Felony drug convictions disenfranchise 4.5% of voting-eligible Black adults (Sentencing Project, 2022). The Weill Cornell national study (May 1, 2026) documents that decriminalization alone does not close this gap. The DEA hearing record should contain evidence that the communities most subjected to drug enforcement are underrepresented in the political processes that set drug policy precisely because of that enforcement — and that rescheduling alone, without automatic expungement and voting rights restoration, does not address the mechanism.

**Comment Point 4 — 280E must be resolved for all rescheduled products, not only the narrow medical track (Section 4.2, Domain 42).**
The April 23, 2026 order provided 280E relief only for state-licensed medical cannabis. Recreational cannabis businesses operating legally under state law in 24 states continue to face effective federal tax rates of 70–80% (Whitney Economics data). The hearing record should reflect the full scope of the federal-state conflict — not only the narrow medical track that the April 23 order addressed.

**Comment Point 5 — The four-scenario analysis demonstrates the hearing is a democratic legitimacy test regardless of outcome (Section 2.4, Domain 42).**
Under all four scenarios the hearing can produce — full Schedule III, narrow medical track only, Congressional Section 591 freeze, or federal court challenge — the underlying democratic accountability failure persists unless the structural mechanisms are addressed. A hearing outcome that reschedules marijuana without addressing the scheduling governance structure, the disenfranchisement feedback loop, and the federal-state banking and tax conflict will leave the democratic exclusion architecture intact. The DEA hearing record should document this scenario-independence.

## Part 3: Participation Notice Submission Procedure

A written notice of desired participation must reach DEA by May 28, 2026. The notice must contain three elements, per the Federal Register notice (91 Fed. Reg. 22777, April 28, 2026):

1. A statement of the person's particular interest in the proceeding
2. A statement of the particular objections or issues on which the person desires to be heard
3. A brief statement of the person's position regarding those objections or issues

**No specific format is required.** A two-page letter on organizational letterhead containing these three elements is sufficient. The notice does not commit your organization to appear or testify — it preserves the option.

**Submit electronically (preferred):**
Email to: nprm@dea.gov
Subject line: `[Organization name] — Written Notice of Desired Participation, Docket No. DEA-1362`
Reference "Docket No. DEA-1362" in the body.

**Submit by mail (allow postal transit; send by May 20 to be safe):**
DEA Attn: Administrator
8701 Morrissette Drive
Springfield, VA 22152
Write "Docket No. DEA-1362" on the envelope.

**Hearing location (for organizations planning in-person participation):**
DEA Hearing Facility
700 Army Navy Drive
Arlington, VA 22202
Hearing dates: June 29 – July 15, 2026 (recess July 3–5)

**Section 591 urgency note**: The House Appropriations Committee FY2027 markup (May 13) includes a rider that could freeze the DEA rulemaking. Organizations filing participation notices before May 13 establish standing regardless of what happens in the appropriations process. Earlier filings are more secure.

## Part 4: Timeline for Recipients — 21-Day Window

Organizations receiving Domain 42 outreach starting May 8 have 20 days to the deadline. This is a viable but tight window.

**Day 1–7 (May 8–14): Internal review**
- Policy team reviews Domain 42 (6,700 words; Sections 2, 3, and 4 are the most hearing-relevant)
- Determine whether your organization has capacity and standing to participate
- Identify which comment points (Part 2 above) are within your organization's analytical lane
- If institutional review is required (legal clinics, universities), initiate approval now — not on May 20
- Note: If the May 13 appropriations markup produces a Section 591 rider, early filers have more secure standing

**Day 8–15 (May 15–21): Draft and coordination**
- Draft participation notice (two pages, three required elements — see Part 3)
- Coordinate with peer organizations on overlapping participation notices (avoid duplicating identical arguments; coordinate to cover complementary dimensions)
- If legal review of the draft is required, complete by May 21
- Follow the Domain 42 comment points in Part 2 as a starting framework; personalize to your organization's evidence base and institutional lane

**Day 16–21 (May 22–27): Finalize and submit**
- Finalize draft with any revisions from coordination and legal review
- Submit electronically to nprm@dea.gov no later than May 27 (one day's buffer before the May 28 deadline)
- If submitting by mail, that submission should have gone out by May 20 — do not rely on mail for a May 28 target
- Confirm receipt if possible (save email confirmation or send certified mail)

**May 28 (hard deadline)**: No extensions have been granted in DEA administrative proceedings of this type. If your organization cannot complete a participation notice by May 28, the next opportunity to get these arguments into the record is written comment submission to the docket — which does not confer participation rights but does create an official record.
