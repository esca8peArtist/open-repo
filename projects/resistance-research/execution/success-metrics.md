# Success Metrics and Feedback Loops

**April 27, 2026**

This document defines what success looks like at 30 days and 90 days, how to measure it, and what to do when the metrics indicate a course correction is needed. The measurement architecture is designed for an independent researcher without an institutional analytics platform — every metric here is measurable with free or low-cost tools.

---

## MEASUREMENT PHILOSOPHY

The goal is institutional depth, not peak reach. A single law school clinic integrating the framework into its work is more valuable than 10,000 casual reads. A Senate staffer briefed on Domain 6 is worth more than 1,000 social media impressions. This measurement architecture reflects that hierarchy. Track the high-value institutional indicators first; track the volume indicators second. Never make distribution decisions based solely on volume metrics.

---

## TIER 1: ENGAGEMENT METRICS

These metrics measure whether individual contacts are actually engaging with the research, not just receiving it.

### Email Metrics

**What to track**:
- Open rate: what percentage of sent emails are opened (use email tracking if available)
- Response rate: what percentage of recipients reply with substantive engagement
- Response quality: classify responses as (1) substantive engagement, (2) brief acknowledgment, (3) request for more information, (4) negative/dismissive
- Time to response: how many days from send to first response (median and range)

**Measurement tool**: Gmail's native read receipts (for individual sends); Mailtrack or Superhuman for per-email open tracking. Free options: Streak CRM (Gmail extension, free tier tracks opens and clicks).

**Targets**:
| Metric | Batch 1 Target | Batch 2 Target | Batch 3 Target | Overall Tier 1 Target |
|--------|---------------|---------------|---------------|----------------------|
| Open rate | N/A (verify separately) | N/A | N/A | N/A (not reliably trackable) |
| Response rate | 2 of 5 (40%) | 3 of 8 (38%) | 4 of 12 (33%) | 9 of 25 (36%) |
| Minimum acceptable | 1 of 5 (20%) | 2 of 8 (25%) | 3 of 12 (25%) | 6 of 25 (24%) |
| Substantive engagement | 2 of 5 (all responses) | 3 of 8 | 3 of 12 | 8 of 25 |
| Forward/share known | 1 of 5 (20%) | 1 of 8 (13%) | 1 of 12 (8%) | 3 of 25 (12%) |

**Response rate below minimum acceptable**: See diagnostic protocol below.

---

### Social Media Metrics

**Substack**:
- Subscriber count: track weekly (T+3 at launch, then weekly)
- Open rate per issue: Substack provides this natively (target: 35-50% for a new publication)
- Paid vs. free subscribers: Track separately (paid = highest-engagement audience)
- Referral sources: Substack shows where new subscribers come from (direct, social, email referral)

**Targets at 30 days**: 100 subscribers, 40%+ open rate, at least 3 referring sources (Tier 1 contact sharing their Substack link counts as a high-value referral)

**Reddit**:
- Upvote count per post (target: 100+ upvotes on the r/PoliticalScience post within 48 hours = high resonance)
- Comment quality: track substantive comments vs. low-effort
- Cross-posts: if a post is cross-posted to another subreddit by other users, the framing resonated
- Upvote-to-comment ratio: a high upvote/low comment ratio means the post resonated with the general community; a high comment/upvote ratio means it provoked discussion (both are positive)

**Targets at 30 days**: At least one Reddit post with 200+ upvotes, at least one substantive thread with 20+ comments

**X/Bluesky**:
- Impressions per thread (Twitter/X analytics provides this)
- Retweets/reshares
- Profile link clicks (tells you how many people clicked through to find the framework)
- Follower growth

**Targets at 30 days**: At least one thread with 1,000+ impressions, 50+ profile link clicks total across all posts

## TIER 2: ADOPTION METRICS

These metrics measure whether the research is being incorporated into others' work — the durable form of impact.

### Academic Adoption

**What to track**:
- Citations: use Google Scholar alerts for the framework title, key domain terminology, and phrases unique to the framework ("35-domain democratic reform," "160 movement case studies"). Set up weekly alerts.
- Law review engagement: has any journal editor requested the research for review, online forum, or symposium?
- Syllabus inclusion: has any contact mentioned using the framework in a course? Track via follow-up emails at 60 days.
- Clinic use: has any law school clinic incorporated the framework's analysis into active litigation preparation?

**Targets at 90 days**: At least 3 academic citations (Google Scholar or similar); at least 1 law review inquiry; at least 1 syllabus or seminar mention.

### Organizational Adoption

**What to track**:
- Policy brief citations: has any think tank (Brennan Center, CAP, EPI, Brookings) cited the framework in a published brief?
- Legislative staff briefings: has any Senate or House staff director acknowledged receiving and reviewing the framework?
- Organizational platform alignment: has any civil society organization (ACLU, Lawyers' Committee, Common Cause) incorporated framework language into public statements?
- Litigation support: has the litigation tracker been used as a reference in any legal filing or motion?

**Targets at 90 days**: At least 1 think tank citation; at least 2 legislative staff briefings acknowledged; at least 1 organizational platform alignment documented.

### Media Adoption

**What to track**:
- Named mentions: Google Alerts for the framework name and researcher name
- Anonymous use: framework as unnamed background/source in journalism (harder to track; watch for specific data points appearing in coverage)
- Media outlet coverage: any article, podcast episode, or broadcast segment that covers the framework or cites its data

**Targets at 30 days**: At least 1 external media mention (blog, newsletter, or social media post from someone not in the contact list)
**Targets at 90 days**: At least 1 named mention in a national media outlet (Vox, The Atlantic, Just Security, Lawfare, Washington Monthly, or comparable)

## TIER 3: INFLUENCE METRICS

These metrics measure whether the research is moving through influence networks — the highest-order signal.

### Network Propagation

**What to track**:
- Tier 1 to Tier 2 organic reach: has any Tier 2 contact mentioned the framework without being directly contacted? (This means a Tier 1 contact shared it with them before the planned Tier 2 outreach.)
- Cross-institutional sharing: has any contact forwarded the research to someone at a different institution or in a different network?
- Unsolicited contact: have any researchers, journalists, or organizers contacted the researcher about the framework without being in the outreach list?

**How to capture**: Ask in follow-up emails (Day 30 follow-up): "Has this been useful enough to share with any colleagues? If so, I'd welcome knowing who else has seen it — not for promotion, but to understand where the research is landing."

**Targets at 30 days**: At least 3 known forwards (Tier 1 contacts who shared with named colleagues); at least 1 unsolicited contact from someone not in the outreach list.

### Legislative Influence

**What to track** (longer-term, 90-day indicators):
- Has any domain-specific language appeared in introduced legislation?
- Has any Senate or House hearing referenced framework domains?
- Has any congressional staff acknowledged using the framework as a research source?

**Targets at 90 days**: At least 1 congressional staff acknowledgment; at least 1 hearing where domain-specific analysis is referenced (even without attribution).

## 30-DAY CHECKPOINT ASSESSMENT

At T+30 days, conduct the following assessment:

### Gate 1: Tier 1 Response Rate

**Target**: 30%+ response rate (8+ of 25 Tier 1 contacts respond substantively)

**Measurement**: Count the contact log — how many responses received, how many were substantive (vs. brief acknowledgment)?

**If target met**: Proceed to full Tier 2 launch without modification.

**If target 20-29%**: Review Batch 1-3 email content. Common causes of below-target response: (1) subject lines too generic, (2) personalization insufficient, (3) domain selection mismatched to contact's current work, (4) too long — reduce to 3 short paragraphs. Adjust for Tier 2 launch.

**If target below 20%**: Do not launch Tier 2 on the same messaging. Conduct a diagnostic review (see below). Consider sending one high-quality, deeply personalized email to a contact in the underperforming batch as a test before re-launching.

### Gate 2: Network Propagation

**Target**: At least 1 Tier 1 contact has shared the research with a named colleague from a different institution

**Measurement**: Check contact log for forwarding notes; check for any Tier 2 contacts who have mentioned the research without being directly contacted.

**If gate met**: The network amplification mechanism is working. Proceed.

**If gate not met**: The research may be receiving but not propagating. This can mean: (1) the research is being received but not found compelling enough to share, (2) the research is being received and held for future use but not forwarded, or (3) the "ask to forward" was not explicit enough. Check whether any email explicitly requested an introduction to a colleague — if not, add this to Tier 2 emails.

### Gate 3: At Least 1 External Mention

**Target**: At least 1 external mention (blog, newsletter, social media) from someone not in the contact list

**Measurement**: Google Alerts check; Substack referral source tracking; social media mention tracking.

**If gate not met**: The framework is not yet visible enough for organic discovery. Increase social media posting frequency; submit a short piece to a high-traffic democracy-focused newsletter (Letters from an American submission; Democracy Docket news tip; Lawfare submission).

## 90-DAY CHECKPOINT ASSESSMENT

At T+90 days, conduct the following assessment:

### Sustained Engagement Trajectory

**Key question**: Is the research continuing to circulate, or has it peaked and plateaued?

**Signals of sustained circulation**: New Substack subscribers arriving 60-90 days after launch (not just at launch); Google Scholar citations appearing; unsolicited contacts from researchers or journalists; social media shares from new accounts that have just discovered the research.

**Signals of plateau**: Substack subscriber growth has stopped; social media engagement is declining; no new external mentions in the past 30 days; no new Tier 2/3 contacts engaging.

**If plateau detected**: Publish a Phase 2 domain (if Path B) or a significant research update (if Path A) to restart the engagement cycle. Every new substantive release is an occasion for re-engagement outreach.

### Phase 2 Continuation Decision

The 90-day assessment is the decision point for Phase 2 continuation:

**Proceed with Phase 2 if** (any two of the following are met):
- Tier 1 response rate was 30%+ at 30 days
- At least 3 academic citations have appeared by 90 days
- At least 1 think tank citation has appeared by 90 days
- Substack subscribers exceed 200 with 35%+ open rate
- At least 1 legislative staff briefing has been acknowledged

**Hold Phase 2 and diagnose if** (more than two of the following):
- Tier 1 response rate was below 20% at 30 days
- No academic citations at 90 days
- No think tank engagement at 90 days
- Substack subscribers below 50 at 90 days
- No external media mention at 90 days

**Phase 2 hold diagnostic**: Review the messaging architecture, domain selection for outreach leads, and platform strategy. The research quality is not the issue — the distribution approach may need adjustment.

## FEEDBACK LOOP: WHICH TIER 1 CONTACTS SHARE WITH TIER 2

This is the most important second-order signal in the distribution. Tracking which Tier 1 contacts organically share the research with Tier 2 contacts tells you which network nodes are most active and which messaging frames are most compelling.

**How to track**:
1. Ask explicitly in follow-up emails at Day 30: "Has this been useful enough to share with any colleagues? If yes, who should I know about?" Record all responses in the contact log.
2. Watch for Tier 2 contacts who mention the research without having been directly contacted — this reveals that a Tier 1 contact shared it with them.
3. Note any social media shares from Tier 1 contacts (if they post about the research publicly, it means they found it share-worthy at a level they are comfortable making public).

**What this data drives**: If Ryan Goodman (Just Security) shares with other national security lawyers and one Tier 2 contact says "Ryan Goodman mentioned this" — that tells you the war powers and prosecutorial accountability framing is the most compelling entry point. If Erica Chenoweth shares with organizers and one Tier 3 contact says "I heard about this from Indivisible" — that tells you the resistance meta-analysis framing is the most compelling entry point for movement networks.

**Use this data to**: Adjust Tier 2/3 messaging to lead with the framing that Tier 1 contacts are most actively sharing. The research stays the same; the presentation of it changes.

## DIAGNOSTIC PROTOCOL: WHAT TO DO WHEN METRICS UNDERPERFORM

### Low response rate (below 20%)

**Step 1**: Pull the 3 most personalized emails you sent and the 3 least personalized. Compare their subject lines and opening paragraphs. Is there a pattern? (Less personalized emails almost always perform worse.)

**Step 2**: Review whether the domain selection was correct for each contact. Did you send Domain 6 to Wendy Weiser (correct) or did you send her Domain 15 (incorrect match)? Domain mismatch is a common cause of low response.

**Step 3**: Check whether the email was too long. If any email exceeded 4 paragraphs, try a 3-paragraph version with the same content. Academic and policy professionals respond better to shorter, denser emails than to comprehensive ones.

**Step 4**: Check whether the "ask" was clear. If the email ended vaguely ("I'd welcome your thoughts"), the recipient may not know what you want. Replace with a specific ask ("Could you tell me whether the constitutional analysis in Domain 6 holds up from a practitioner perspective?").

### No network propagation (no forwards by Day 30)

**Step 1**: Check whether any email explicitly requested an introduction to a colleague. If not, add this to all subsequent emails: "If any of this is relevant to a colleague who is working on a related problem, I would welcome an introduction — that is the most valuable form of engagement for this research."

**Step 2**: Consider whether the research is reaching the right people or just the most prominent people. A less prominent researcher with active networks may share more readily than a senior scholar with a packed schedule. Add mid-level researchers (assistant professors, senior policy analysts, clinic staff) to the outreach list alongside senior contacts.

**Step 3**: Give contacts something specific to forward. A 1-page domain brief formatted for forwarding is easier to share than a link to a 200-page framework. Create a 1-page Domain 6 brief, a 1-page Domain 29 brief, and a 1-page resistance meta-analysis brief that contacts can forward to colleagues with a single note.

### No media mention (no external mention by Day 30)

**Step 1**: Increase social media posting frequency to 4-5 times per week. Journalists and bloggers often discover research through social media before email pitches.

**Step 2**: Submit a short op-ed or analysis piece directly to a high-traffic publication (Lawfare, Just Security, Law360, The Hill). A published byline in any of these creates the external mention that prompts other journalists to investigate.

**Step 3**: Identify whether any Tier 1 contact has a media platform themselves (blog, newsletter, social media) and whether they have posted anything publicly. If yes, engage with their public content to establish a visible relationship. This signals to their followers that the researcher exists.
