## Draft 3: Brennan Center for Justice
**To**: kennardl@brennan.law.nyu.edu (Voting Rights desk — correct entry point for NVRA arguments)
**Subject**: Rural hospital closures suppress voter turnout — APSR 2025 finding your 2026 election protection work should have

---

Dear Brennan Center Democracy Team,

There is a voter suppression mechanism your 2026 election protection briefings do not yet cover, and the peer-reviewed evidence for it is now solid enough that it should be.

Cox, Epp, and Shepherd (American Political Science Review, August 2025) examined 10.5 million rural voters across the 2016, 2018, and 2020 elections using individual-level fixed effects panel regression merged with UNC hospital closure data and L2 voter files. They found a 3.8 percentage-point reduction in voter turnout in communities affected by rural hospital closures. The effect is temporally structured: largest 4–6 months before an election (6% reduction), smaller 1–3 months out (4%), returning to baseline when closures occurred 7–12 months before the election. This is not attitudinal disengagement. It is a capacity disruption.

As of January 2026, 417 rural hospitals are classified as vulnerable to closure — one-third of all rural facilities nationally. The OBBBA's Medicaid cuts cover approximately 37% of estimated rural hospital revenue losses. The other 63% flows through to service cuts and closures that carry the 3.8pp penalty documented in the APSR study. This is a documented turnout suppression mechanism at scale, concentrated in the same rural communities where voter protection work focuses on ID laws, polling place access, and roll purges — but not yet on healthcare infrastructure loss.

The Medicaid/NVRA dimension is the second finding Brennan Center's work should have: Medicaid enrollment offices are mandatory voter registration sites under 52 U.S.C. § 20506. The OBBBA's administrative architecture — 6-month redetermination cycles doubled from the prior 12-month standard, compressed agency budgets, doubled documentation demands — degrades this voter registration infrastructure for low-income Americans at precisely the moment enrollment volume increases through coverage churn. The Institute for Responsive Government documents that Medicaid Automatic Voter Registration produces 85–90% registration rates; traditional NVRA paper-form compliance produces 1.6%. The administrative degradation the OBBBA produces will widen that gap in the 42 states without AVR.

"Domain 39: Healthcare Access as Democratic Infrastructure" documents both findings in full — with peer-reviewed evidence, statutory analysis, and the reform architecture needed to reverse each pathway. It also covers medical debt as civic participation tax, maternal mortality and civic capacity loss, and disability disenfranchisement through three compounding mechanisms. The document is designed to be the briefing that connects healthcare advocacy organizations to voting rights organizations around the same structural argument.

Dear Black Mamas Matter Alliance Team,

The Momnibus coalition — your alliance, AMCHP, the National Birth Equity Collaborative — has built the most important maternal health legislative infrastructure in a generation. Domain 39 was designed specifically to give that coalition the democratic equity argument it needs to reach voting rights funders and election protection organizations that do not yet see Black maternal health as their issue. I want to share it on today's deadline.

"Domain 39: Healthcare Access as Democratic Infrastructure" makes the following claim about your work: preventing the preventable deaths of Black and Indigenous women is protecting the civic voice of their communities. This is not a rhetorical claim — it is an empirical one.

Black women die in childbirth at 50.3 deaths per 100,000 live births, 3.5 times the rate for white women, and the gap has not narrowed since 2022. Women who die in preventable maternal deaths cannot vote, organize their neighbors, or raise children who will become civic participants. The communities with the highest maternal mortality rates — the communities that BMMA serves — are the communities whose civic voice is most systematically diminished by structural barriers to political representation. Preventable maternal death is a mechanism of civic exclusion.

And the historical relationship runs in both directions. Rushovich et al. (American Journal of Public Health, 2024) documented that the 1965 Voting Rights Act's expansion of Black political power produced a 17.5% reduction in Black infant deaths in covered counties in the five years following passage. The mechanism was documented: increased government funding to high-Black-population counties, reduced racial wage gaps, lower arrest rates. Political power protected Black mothers and infants. Political exclusion reverses that protection.

The March 2025 HHS reorganization — eliminating the CDC Division of Reproductive Health staff, halting community-based maternal health grants, closing federal offices coordinating state maternal health equity programs — is both a maternal health crisis and a democratic crisis. The same HHS that is today issuing its interim final rule on OBBBA work requirements without public notice or comment is the HHS that has dismantled the infrastructure designed to close the maternal mortality gap.

Congressional resolutions S.Res.67 and H.Res.119 (119th Congress) declaring racism a public health crisis, and H.R. 7973 (the Momnibus Act, introduced March 2026), provide the legislative acknowledgment that these disparities are structural and policy-created. Domain 39 provides the democratic infrastructure argument that extends those resolutions into new coalition territory.

The strategic value: the democratic equity argument gives BMMA and the Momnibus coalition access to voting rights funders — organizations that fund election protection work in Black and Indigenous communities — who do not currently fund maternal health work because they do not see it as their issue. Domain 39 makes the case that it is. And the reverse: it gives voting rights organizations the empirical case for seeing maternal mortality reduction as an election integrity issue, because the communities with the highest maternal mortality rates are the communities whose political voice is most constrained.
