# Outreach Email Templates — Phase 1 Tier 1 Distribution

**April 27, 2026**

Nine templates across three contact types. Each type has three variants — use A/B/C subject lines to test open rates (if you have email tracking). Each template is designed for individual, personalized outreach. The bracketed sections are not optional — personalization is what separates an email that gets read from one that gets archived.

**Pre-send checklist (every email)**:
- [ ] Confirmed recipient is still at this institution (staff turn over — verify before sending)
- [ ] Read their most recent published piece, testimony, or public statement
- [ ] Completed all bracketed personalization fields
- [ ] Selected the correct template type (A, B, or C) based on recipient's primary identity
- [ ] Identified the 1-2 domains most relevant to their current work
- [ ] Have the correct document links ready (do not send without working links)

## TEMPLATE TYPE A: ACADEMIC / THINK TANK

**Best for**: Ryan Goodman, Erica Chenoweth, Wendy Weiser (Brennan Center), Jack Balkin, Archon Fung, Samuel Issacharoff, Danielle Allen, Steven Levitsky, EPI staff, Brookings fellows, CAP directors, ACS leadership.

**Core framing**: Present the research as peer-level scholarly work being shared for feedback, not promoted for endorsement. Academic and think tank professionals receive promotional pitches constantly. What gets their attention is a specific methodological question they have an opinion on, or a specific finding that connects to their current research. Never ask for endorsement in a first email — ask for feedback. Never describe the research as "important" — let the content establish that.

**What makes the hook work**: Identify the one thing in the framework that connects to something they have published or stated. Lead with that specific connection in sentence one. No abstract framing, no general setup.

## TEMPLATE TYPE B: MEDIA / JOURNALIST

**Best for**: Quinta Jurecic (The Atlantic/Lawfare), Zack Beauchamp (Vox), Marc Elias (Democracy Docket), Washington Monthly, Jamelle Bouie (NYT), Heather Cox Richardson (Substack), NPR Politics desk.

**Core framing**: Journalists evaluate research through one lens: is this newsworthy and is it well-sourced? The hook must be a specific, verifiable, surprising finding — not a description of the framework's scope. Journalists receive research pitches constantly; what makes yours different is a specific data point or finding that their audience does not know and that connects to something currently in the news cycle. The "comprehensive framework" framing is for academics. For journalists, lead with the one finding that belongs in a story today.

**What makes the hook work**: A specific, sourced claim that connects the framework's research to an active news story. Name the story or topic the journalist has covered. Show the connection in the first sentence.

## HANDLING RESPONSES

### Early positive response (engagement within 72 hours)
Reply within 24 hours. Provide the full framework and research notes. Ask one specific question ("Is there a domain where the analysis seems weakest to you?"). Do not ask for endorsement, promotion, or sharing. Establish a working relationship — offer to send specific domain sections as they become relevant to the contact's work.

### Request for executive summary only
Send the 8-page executive summary (or the 2-page version if that is the correct document for the context). Include: "The full framework is at [link] for a deeper dive — and the individual domain documents are separately available if any specific area is more relevant to your work." Do not oversell; let the executive summary make the case for the full document.

### Request for full framework
Send immediately with the citation format and CC 4.0 license noted. Include the litigation tracker and any domain-specific documents most relevant to their work.

### Request for a Zoom or call
Respond positively. Offer three specific 30-minute slots in the next 7 days. Prepare: a 5-minute verbal summary of the framework, a 2-3 specific domains most relevant to their work, and the key ask (feedback / citation / introduction to a colleague). The call is for establishing a working relationship, not for pitching. Let the contact drive the conversation after the initial 5-minute summary.

### Academic objection: "This is policy advocacy, not scholarship"
Do not argue. Acknowledge the framing: "You're right that a 35-domain proposal is not a traditional academic paper. What I'd ask you to evaluate is whether the individual domain analyses are correctly sourced and analytically sound — the framework nature is a feature for practitioners, but each domain can stand alone as a piece of comparative constitutional analysis. If Domain [X] holds up as analysis, I'm satisfied."

### Media objection: "We don't cover frameworks, we cover events"
Pivot to the specific finding: "Understood — let me pull out the specific finding that may be relevant to your current coverage. [Give them the one most newsworthy specific finding from the framework, with the primary source]. That stands alone regardless of the broader framework."

### Objection: "Who are you? What are your credentials?"
Template response: "I'm an independent researcher — not affiliated with any institution, and the framework doesn't carry institutional imprimatur. What I can offer is: the sourcing is documented and verifiable for every claim; the comparative constitutional analysis is grounded in primary sources (constitutional text, court decisions, V-Dem data); and the methodology is open to scrutiny. I'd rather be evaluated on whether the analysis is correct than on my credentials. If you find an error, I want to know about it."

### Request for podcast or panel appearance
Decision deferred to user. Template holding response: "I appreciate the interest — let me think about whether I'm the right person to represent this research publicly versus having it speak through the work. I'll follow up in [X days]."

## SUBJECT LINE A/B TEST FRAMEWORK

If you have email tracking (open rate visibility), use the following protocol:

**Batch 1** (5 contacts): Randomize between Subject A and Subject B variants within each template type.

**Batch 2** (8 contacts): Use the higher-opening subject line variant from Batch 1 for 5 contacts; test Subject C variant on 3 contacts.

**Batch 3** (12 contacts): Use the highest-performing subject line variant throughout.

**What to measure**: Open rate (which subject lines get opened), response rate (which emails generate replies), and forwarding rate (which contacts forward or share). These are leading indicators for Tier 2 messaging optimization.
