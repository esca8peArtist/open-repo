[Name],

The DEA administrative hearing on marijuana rescheduling begins June 29, and the deadline to file a written participation notice is **May 28 — 21 days away** (Docket No. DEA-1362, nprm@dea.gov). I'm reaching out before that window closes because I've completed a democratic-design analysis of the regulatory capture problem that may be useful for your organization's hearing participation strategy.

I'm an independent researcher who has spent two years building a 35-domain framework for democratic structural reform. Domain 42 — Drug Policy, Regulatory Capture, and Democratic Legitimacy — argues that the federal drug prohibition framework is not primarily a drug policy question. It is a democratic exclusion architecture: three interlocking mechanisms that have systematically insulated federal drug policy from the democratic accountability that would revise it.

The three mechanisms, briefly:

**First**, the scheduling process has been captured by the DEA — a law enforcement agency whose institutional incentives systematically bias scheduling outcomes toward restriction regardless of public health evidence. The April 23 DOJ order created a two-track framework that places DEA ALJs (DEA employees, within DEA's budget, within DEA's institutional culture) in charge of adjudicating a public health question. That is not a neutral proceeding. Mason Marks's Yale Law Journal analysis ("Separation of Drug Scheduling Powers") documents how DEA's administrative practice has diverged from the CSA's statutory design, which gave HHS binding scientific authority that the OLC opinion then converted into advisory status.

**Second**, the Weill Cornell study published May 1, 2026 (Meinhofer et al., *American Economic Journal: Economic Policy*) demonstrates what your organization already knows operationally: legalization alone does not close racial disparities in cannabis enforcement. Black Americans are arrested for marijuana possession at 3.64 times the rate of white Americans despite comparable usage rates (ACLU 2023). In New York City, felony drug arrest disparities *increased* after decriminalization, as enforcement shifted toward higher-charge offenses with greater disenfranchisement consequences. This is not a side issue — it is the feedback loop that sustains the policy: the communities most subjected to drug enforcement are underrepresented in the political processes that set drug policy, because drug convictions disenfranchise them at disproportionate rates.

**Third**, 24 states have enacted recreational legalization through democratic processes — direct ballot initiatives with documented majority support. The federal framework prevents implementation of those democratic decisions. The SAFER Banking Act has passed the House seven times; it has not received a Senate floor vote. This is democratic accountability failure in a precise analytical sense.

The domain documents all three mechanisms, the international comparative record (Canada, Germany, Portugal), and a three-tier reform architecture (scheduling governance, felony disenfranchisement reform, federal-state reconciliation). It is 6,700 words, 54 citations, published under CC Attribution 4.0.
