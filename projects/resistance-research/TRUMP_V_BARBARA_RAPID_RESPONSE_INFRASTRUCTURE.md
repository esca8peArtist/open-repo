## FRAMING NOTE

Trump v. Barbara (No. 25-365) is primarily a birthright citizenship case challenging Executive Order 14160, brought by the ACLU on behalf of children of undocumented parents. It is not a tribal voting rights case. However, it acquires decisive tribal significance through two specific developments at oral argument on April 1, 2026: (1) Solicitor General Sauer's statement — after initially saying "I'm not sure" — that children of tribal Indians are "not birthright citizens" under the Fourteenth Amendment, and (2) a separate Republican Senate amicus brief (29 senators including Schmitt and Cruz) that explicitly argues tribal members "who maintain their tribal relations are not, in the sense of the Fourteenth Amendment, born subject to the jurisdiction of the United States." That argument, if validated in any opinion language, would be the most direct threat to the constitutional foundation of the tribal franchise since 1924.

Louisiana v. Callais (April 29, 2026) is the voting rights case. The "Callais cascade" refers to the multi-circuit erosion of VRA Section 2 enforcement that the ruling triggered. Domain 58 covers both tracks and their intersection.

## Section 3: Legal Analysis

### Government vs. Respondent — Core Arguments

**Government (Solicitor General Sauer)**:

The Citizenship Clause's phrase "subject to the jurisdiction thereof" requires more than mere territorial birth. Sauer's theory: birthright citizenship requires parental "domicile" — settled, lawful, long-term residence in the United States. Children of undocumented parents or parents on temporary visas lack this jurisdictional connection because their parents owe primary allegiance elsewhere.

To construct this theory, Sauer invoked *Elk v. Wilkins* (1884), which held that tribal political allegiance placed tribal members outside the Citizenship Clause. In the most consequential tribal rights exchange of the entire OT2025 term:

> Gorsuch: "Do you think Native Americans today are birthright citizens under your test?"
> Sauer: "I think so" — citing the Indian Citizenship Act of 1924.
> Gorsuch: "Put aside the statute. Do you think they're birthright citizens?"
> Sauer: "No, I think the clear understanding that everybody agrees in the congressional debates is that the children of tribal Indians are not birthright citizens."

Importantly, Sauer also said "I'm not sure" at a prior point in the same exchange — Indianz.com documented the full sequence. His eventual settled answer was the "not birthright citizens" position cited above. The inconsistency is itself legally significant: the Solicitor General could not confidently defend the government's own theory when applied to the tribal citizenship question.

**Respondents (ACLU, Cecillia Wang)**:

The Citizenship Clause establishes a "fixed bright-line rule": everyone born in the United States is a citizen. *United States v. Wong Kim Ark* (1898) settled this 6-2 — children of lawful Chinese residents born in the U.S. are citizens. The "subject to the jurisdiction thereof" clause excludes only those genuinely not subject to U.S. law: children of foreign diplomats (who have formal diplomatic immunity) and children of occupying enemy forces. Undocumented immigrants are subject to U.S. law in every relevant sense — they can be arrested, prosecuted, taxed, and conscripted.

Wang's argument for a "workable" rule is particularly powerful against Sauer's domicile theory, which the justices pressed him to define precisely. He could not: domicile could mean lawful presence, lawful permanent residence, long-term intent to remain, or something else. No limiting principle emerged.

### Narrowest vs. Broadest Ruling

**Broadest ruling (Scenario A)**: The Court reaffirms Wong Kim Ark's expansive language explicitly, holding that the Citizenship Clause guarantees birthright citizenship to all U.S.-born persons regardless of parental immigration status or domicile. If this holding is stated in terms that reject any domicile requirement, it implicitly forecloses Sauer's Elk-based theory without explicitly addressing tribal citizenship — the safest outcome for tribal advocates because it eliminates the doctrinal space for the Elk revival without requiring the Court to say anything about tribal citizenship specifically.

**Medium ruling (Scenario B)**: The Court avoids the constitutional question by ruling on statutory grounds — holding that the Immigration and Nationality Act does not authorize the executive order. This resolves the immediate controversy but leaves the Citizenship Clause interpretation open. The 29-senator amicus brief and Sauer's oral argument position remain as live legislative and doctrinal threats.

**Narrowest (adverse) ruling (Scenario C)**: The Court upholds some portion of the EO, or a concurrence validates the Elk-revival argument. This is the lowest-probability outcome but the highest-urgency scenario. Any concurrence language stating that tribal citizenship is statutory rather than constitutional would immediately become the authoritative government interpretation for the 119th Congress — which already has 29 senators on record endorsing that view.

### Tribal Citizenship Implications — The Statutory vs. Constitutional Distinction

The Indian Citizenship Act of 1924 grants citizenship to "all noncitizen Indians born within the territorial limits of the United States." If tribal citizenship is grounded only in this statute, then:
- The 119th Congress (Republican majority) could repeal or amend the Act
- Without constitutional citizenship, there is no Fourteenth Amendment anchor for the tribal franchise
- Approximately 9.7 million tribal members would have citizenship held at congressional sufferance rather than as a constitutional right

The Berger/Ablavsky brief establishes the key scholarly distinction: the 1924 Act was a direct legislative response to *Elk v. Wilkins*, enacted precisely because Congress understood that tribal members needed statutory citizenship because the Citizenship Clause's tribal-exception jurisprudence had denied them constitutional citizenship. The question Trump v. Barbara reopens is whether that 1924 legislative correction is the permanent final word or merely a statute subject to future repeal.

## Section 4: Callais Cascade — Relationship to Prior Cases

### What Callais Actually Held (Precision Correction)

The May 20 research memos characterized Callais as eliminating the discriminatory-effects standard entirely and requiring proof of intentional discrimination. This characterization requires precision. *Louisiana v. Callais* (April 29, 2026, 6-3, Alito writing) modified the *Thornburg v. Gingles* (1986) framework in three specific ways:

1. **Intent evidence required in totality of circumstances**: Plaintiffs must now present evidence that "supports a strong inference that the State intentionally drew its districts to afford minority voters less opportunity because of their race." This is a new intent-evidence requirement layered on top of the existing effects framework — not a full substitution of intent for effects.

2. **Temporal limitation on historical discrimination**: "Discrimination that occurred some time ago, as well as present-day disparities that are characterized as the ongoing 'effects of societal discrimination,' are entitled to much less weight" in the totality analysis. Historical discrimination evidence loses probative force for establishing contemporary VRA violations.

3. **"Strong inference" standard**: The prior *Gingles* framework allowed circumstantial evidence of discrimination; Callais requires evidence sufficient to support a "strong inference" of intentional discriminatory purpose. This is a heightened pleading standard.

**Net effect on tribal redistricting challenges**: The standard remains nominally an "effects plus circumstances" test, but the Callais modifications make it substantially harder to sustain tribal redistricting challenges because: (a) tribes typically rely heavily on historical discrimination evidence, which Callais discounts; (b) proving specific legislative intent to discriminate against tribal voters requires internal state government communications rarely available through discovery; and (c) the "strong inference" standard creates a higher bar at the motion to dismiss stage.

This precision matters for distribution accuracy. Domain 58 materials should describe Callais as "substantially weakening VRA Section 2 by imposing an intent-inference requirement on top of existing effects analysis" rather than as "eliminating the discriminatory-effects standard." The former is legally accurate; the latter overstates the holding in a way that could be challenged by government attorneys and reduce credibility with legal audiences.

### Prior SCOTUS Tribal Voting Cases

**Elk v. Wilkins (1884)**: Held that John Elk, born into the Winnebago tribe, was not a citizen because tribal political allegiance placed him outside the Citizenship Clause. Congress overrode Elk with the Indian Citizenship Act (1924). Sauer's April 1 argument explicitly revived Elk as live constitutional doctrine — the first time any government official has argued this position before SCOTUS in 102 years. The 29-senator amicus brief provides legislative-branch confirmation of the revival.

**United States v. Wong Kim Ark (1898)**: The controlling birthright citizenship precedent — children of lawful Chinese residents born in the U.S. are citizens under the Citizenship Clause (6-2). The government's current theory reads Wong Kim Ark as turning on parental "lawful domicile," a contested interpretation the Court's majority appeared skeptical of at oral argument.

**McGirt v. Oklahoma (2020)**: Confirmed that large portions of eastern Oklahoma remain Indian Country. Justice Gorsuch wrote the majority opinion, establishing himself as the Court's most consistent tribal sovereignty advocate — making his precise and pointed questioning of Sauer in Barbara particularly significant. Gorsuch's doctrinal sophistication on tribal sovereignty means his eventual opinion or concurrence in Barbara is likely to engage the tribal citizenship question directly, regardless of which way he votes on the overall case.

**Turtle Mountain Band of Chippewa Indians v. Howe, GVR (May 18, 2026)**: SCOTUS vacated the Eighth Circuit's ruling (which held private plaintiffs cannot enforce VRA Section 2) and remanded for reconsideration in light of Callais. Jackson dissented in three sentences, correctly noting Callais "did not address" the private enforceability question. The GVR is an escalation: it returns the case to a hostile circuit under a hostile standard, enabling functional elimination of private Section 2 enforcement without a single definitive ruling.
