# Talking Points — Domain Class Briefs

**April 28, 2026 — Session 565**

Ten domain class briefs. Each has a 30-second version (what to say if you have 30 seconds), a 2-minute version (full oral brief with evidence), and a "hardest objection" response. These are for oral communication — media interviews, introductory conversations, coalition meetings, briefings. They are not the research; they are the door to the research.

**Rule for all talking points**: Lead with the most specific, verifiable data point. Not a frame, not a value statement — a fact. The frame comes after the fact. "The U.S. democracy score dropped 28 points in a single year" is a fact. "Democracy is in crisis" is a frame that invites dismissal.

## 1. VOTING RIGHTS AND ELECTORAL REFORM (Domains 1, 2, 3, 33)

### 30-Second Version

The Cook Political Report estimates 8-12 House seats could shift before a single 2026 vote is cast — solely from mid-decade gerrymandering in Texas, North Carolina, Indiana, and Missouri. This is not about voter suppression. It is about whether districts are drawn to produce competitive elections or to lock in outcomes. Thirty-five democracies use some form of proportional representation. The U.S. does not.

### 2-Minute Version

The United States has one of the most structurally uncompetitive electoral systems in the OECD. Here is the specific evidence:

**Gerrymandering**: A Cook Political Report analysis of mid-decade redistricting in four states — Texas, North Carolina, Indiana, Missouri — projects that 8 to 12 House seats could shift before a single vote is cast. This is not a close election becoming uncompetitive. This is a structural manipulation of the districts that produce outcomes. Rucho v. Common Cause (2019) held that federal courts cannot remedy partisan gerrymandering. That ruling has removed the primary judicial check on this practice.

**Proportional representation**: 35 functioning democracies use proportional representation or mixed-member proportional systems. New Zealand adopted MMP in 1996. Germany has used it since 1949. The correlation between proportional systems and voter turnout is documented at 0.68 across OECD countries. The U.S. has consistently the lowest or second-lowest voter turnout of any OECD democracy.

**Ranked-choice voting**: 49 jurisdictions in the United States now use ranked-choice voting — covering 14 million voters. Alaska's 2022 general election was the first statewide RCV election in U.S. history. Maine has used RCV for federal elections since 2018. The evidence on negative campaigning under RCV: it decreases, because candidates compete for second-choice votes from opponents' supporters.

**Automatic voter registration**: 20 states and the District of Columbia have enacted automatic voter registration. Oregon, the first state to implement it (2015), saw a 4-percentage-point increase in voter turnout and a 10-point increase in new voter registration.

The 35-domain framework documents what full structural electoral reform looks like, with comparative evidence from 40+ functioning democracies and the specific legislative vehicles available now.

### Hardest Objection: "This is just partisan — Democrats want these reforms because they benefit Democrats."

**Response**: The gerrymandering objection is empirically testable. The Cook Political Report analysis is nonpartisan — they project some Republican-favorable and some Democratic-favorable gerrymanders. The mid-decade manipulations in Texas (Republican legislature) and North Carolina (Republican legislature) are the primary cases documented, yes. But the principle — that districts should be drawn by independent commissions, not the party in power — is a structural argument, not a partisan one. Arizona's Independent Redistricting Commission was created by a Republican-backed ballot initiative in 2000. California's Citizens Redistricting Commission was created by a ballot initiative and has bipartisan support. The structural argument is: whoever draws the lines should not be the party that benefits from them.

## 2. JUDICIAL INDEPENDENCE (Domains 6, 35)

### 30-Second Version

The Senate Ethics Committee issued zero disciplinary actions in 2025. The STOCK Act imposes a $200 penalty for insider trading violations. The Supreme Court's ethical standards are not legally enforceable under any existing mechanism. We have documented what five other constitutional democracies do differently, and three specific reform pathways that do not require constitutional amendments.

### 2-Minute Version

The judicial independence crisis has two layers: Supreme Court ethics and the broader dismantling of judicial accountability.

**Supreme Court ethics**: Five of the nine current Justices have documented financial relationships with parties who have had business before the Court. The Senate Ethics Committee issued zero disciplinary sanctions in 2025. The STOCK Act — which is supposed to prevent congressional insider trading — imposes a $200 fine for violations, has no criminal enforcement mechanism, and does not apply to the Supreme Court at all. The Court operates under an honor code it wrote itself.

What other countries do: Canada's Supreme Court justices are governed by the Ethical Principles for Judges, enforced by the Canadian Judicial Council with binding authority to recommend removal. The UK's Supreme Court has an independent Judicial Conduct Investigations Office. Germany's Constitutional Court judges serve single non-renewable 12-year terms — structural insulation from political pressure.

**The CASA universal injunction gap**: The Supreme Court's 2025 CASA v. DHS ruling curtailed universal injunctions — the primary tool used to block nationwide enforcement of unconstitutional policies. Without universal injunctions, advocates must litigate the same constitutional question in every circuit simultaneously, or wait for a circuit split. This creates a structural gap in judicial enforcement capacity. The 35-domain framework documents three parallel workarounds: Rule 23(b)(2) class action fortification, state attorney general parens patriae standing, and statutory restoration by Congress.

**The specific reform proposals**: Supreme Court Ethics, Recusal and Transparency Act (introduced by Whitehouse, 2026). Supreme Court term limit proposals (18-year single terms, staggered — does not require constitutional amendment under Article III's "good behavior" provision as interpreted by a minority of scholars, though contested). The framework documents the constitutional argument for and against each pathway.

### Hardest Objection: "Judicial independence cuts both ways — court-packing would destroy independence."

**Response**: The framework explicitly engages this. The three specific reform proposals in Domain 6 are: (1) enforceable ethics code — not court expansion, just the same standards that bind every other federal judge; (2) transparent recusal standards — required financial disclosure matching what district court judges already provide; (3) term limits — 18-year staggered terms, which every federal district and circuit judge already effectively serves on average anyway. None of those require expansion. The court expansion argument is documented in the framework as a fourth option, with the evidence that FDR's 1937 court expansion threat did produce a behavioral change on the Court (the "switch in time that saved nine") — but also the evidence that Congress has rejected expansion proposals every time they've been introduced. The framework is honest that expansion is the most contested option.

## 3. ELECTION SECURITY (Domain 37, Domain 37b)

### 30-Second Version

CISA's election security budget was cut 40% in the fiscal year 2026 appropriations process. At the same time, 12 states have active litigation over voter roll purges using federal citizenship databases that have documented error rates. The two threats are connected: reduced federal security capacity at exactly the moment the federal executive is using that capacity to target specific voter populations.

### 2-Minute Version

Election security in 2026 has two distinct threat categories that the policy debate conflates at its peril.

**External threats**: Foreign interference infrastructure — disinformation networks, social media manipulation, and cyberattacks on election infrastructure — was documented extensively in the 2017 and 2020 intelligence community assessments. CISA, the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency, was the primary federal coordinator for election security with state and local officials. Its election security budget was cut by 40% in FY2026. The 2024 election security workforce has been reduced by 30% through layoffs and buyouts. The gap this creates cannot be filled at the state level — no individual state has the intelligence access, cybersecurity infrastructure, or interstate coordination capacity that CISA provided.

**Internal threats**: The Trump administration has issued executive orders directing state election officials to purge voter rolls using DOGE-developed citizenship verification databases. The Social Security Administration's citizenship records — the primary data source — have a documented error rate of approximately 1.5% for foreign-born citizens who naturalized after their SSA records were created. Applied to 26 million naturalized citizens, that is approximately 390,000 citizens who could be incorrectly flagged. Twelve states have active litigation challenging the purge orders under NVRA's 90-day quiet period rule.

**The convergence**: Weakening federal election security capacity while simultaneously activating federal administrative power against specific voter populations creates a compound threat that is unprecedented in the post-Voting Rights Act era. Domain 37b documents the state-level coordination architecture that remains functional without CISA and identifies the five states with the most critical security gaps.

**The 2026 advocacy window**: The most consequential near-term action is the election security appropriations fight in FY2027, with markup expected September-October 2026. The second most consequential is the voter roll purge litigation — 12 active cases, multiple pending preliminary injunction decisions.

### Hardest Objection: "Election security should be nonpartisan — both parties have an interest in secure elections."

**Response**: Correct. The framework documents election security as a structural infrastructure question, not a partisan one. The state election officials most vocal about the CISA funding cuts include Republicans — Georgia Secretary of State Brad Raffensperger and several other Republican SoS's filed public comments opposing the cuts. The specific framing issue is that "election security" is being used by the administration simultaneously as a justification for federal intervention into state voter rolls (which career election security professionals generally oppose) and as a rationale for cutting the federal capacity that actually provides security. That is not a partisan observation — it is a structural contradiction documented in the public record.

## 4. ECONOMIC POLICY AND FISCAL REFORM (Domains 5, 17, 20, 23)

### 30-Second Version

The February 2026 Supreme Court ruling struck down IEEPA tariff authority 6-3, and the administration pivoted to Section 122 of the 1974 Trade Act — which caps tariffs at 10% and expires July 24, 2026. The $170 billion in current tariffs rests on legal authority that a majority of the Court has already found unconstitutional for IEEPA and that expires in 95 days for Section 122. Meanwhile, 61% of small businesses report negative impacts. GDP drag is documented at 0.4-0.7 points.

### 2-Minute Version

The economic policy section of the 35-domain framework covers three interconnected crises.

**Tariff unilateralism**: The Supreme Court struck down IEEPA tariff authority in Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump (February 20, 2026), 6-3, with the Chief Justice writing for the majority applying the major questions doctrine. The administration pivoted to Section 122 of the 1974 Trade Act as the new legal foundation. Section 122 has a 10% cap on tariffs and sunsets July 24, 2026. The 145% China tariffs are not authorized by Section 122 at any level. Twenty-four state attorneys general are challenging the Section 122 tariffs in the Court of International Trade. The SCOTUS ruling on IEEPA was the most significant constitutional check on executive economic power since the Steel Seizure Case (1952).

**Labor reform**: The union density correlation with median wages is documented at 0.73 across OECD countries. The U.S. union density has fallen from 35% in 1955 to 10.1% today — the lowest of any peer democracy. The five countries with the highest union density (Denmark 67%, Sweden 65%, Finland 60%, Norway 50%, Belgium 49%) have median wages 20-40% higher than the U.S. controlling for industry and education level. The PRO Act has been introduced in every Congress since 2018 without passing. The framework documents what statutory changes would produce the largest short-term organizing gains.

**Economic concentration**: The four largest airlines control 80% of the U.S. market. The four largest banks control 53% of all deposits. Amazon controls 38% of U.S. e-commerce. The DOJ Antitrust Division budget was cut 18% in FY2026. The framework documents what Robinson-Patman Act restoration, merger presumption reform, and platform self-preferencing prohibition would structurally change — with evidence from EU Digital Markets Act implementation (2023-2026).

### Hardest Objection: "Tariffs protect American manufacturing jobs."

**Response**: The employment evidence cuts the other way. TAA — Trade Adjustment Assistance, the program that provided retraining and income support for workers displaced by trade — lapsed in July 2022 and has not been reauthorized. The $264 billion in FY2025 tariff revenue is being collected with zero worker support infrastructure. The economies that have maintained manufacturing employment — Germany, Japan, South Korea — do so through sectoral bargaining, active industrial policy, and worker retraining programs. Tariffs alone do not produce manufacturing employment without the labor market institutions that make manufacturing viable as a career.

## 5. CIVIL RIGHTS AND RACIAL JUSTICE (Domains 14, 22, 7)

### 30-Second Version

The United States incarcerates 639 people per 100,000 — Norway incarcerates 61. Norway's recidivism rate is 20%; the U.S. rate is 76%. At OECD median incarceration rates, the U.S. would spend $100-130 billion less per year on corrections — and have 550,000 fewer people incarcerated. This is not a values argument. It is a documented policy outcome that other countries achieved through specific institutional reforms.

### 2-Minute Version

**Criminal justice**: The 639 per 100,000 incarceration rate is the highest of any country that publishes verified data. The evidence base on what produces lower recidivism: (1) Norway's "import model" — bringing education, healthcare, and job training into prisons; (2) Germany's sentence minimization principle — incarceration only when no alternative is available; (3) the Netherlands' restorative justice infrastructure — 22 of 34 prisons in use in 2016 have since closed as demand declined. The 76% U.S. recidivism rate compared to 20% in Norway is not a difference in criminal populations — it is a difference in what happens during and after incarceration.

**Reparations and racial justice**: The racial wealth gap costs the U.S. economy $1-1.5 trillion per year in lost GDP (Brookings). Eliminating racial economic disparities would add 4-6% to U.S. GDP by 2028 (McKinsey). These are not advocacy figures — they are economic modeling from mainstream policy institutions. The BRAC-model reparations commission structure in Domain 22 is based on the German reparations commission process for Holocaust survivors — binding recommendation authority, not advisory only.

**Voting rights act restoration**: The Shelby County v. Holder (2013) ruling struck down the preclearance formula of the VRA. The John Lewis Voting Rights Advancement Act has been introduced in every Congress since 2019. The formula in the latest version uses an evidence-based threshold of three or more voting rights violations in the past 25 years. That formula, if applied, would cover Texas, Georgia, Louisiana, Mississippi, and North Carolina — the states with the highest documented violation rates since 2010.

### Hardest Objection: "Reparations are divisive and practically impossible to implement."

**Response**: The German Wiedergutmachung program — Holocaust survivor reparations — was implemented through a bilateral state treaty, not a general public vote. The South African Truth and Reconciliation Commission produced reparations recommendations without a direct popular vote. The framework proposes a BRAC-model commission — like the military base closing commission — specifically because it removes the "divisive vote" step and replaces it with a binding recommendation procedure that Congress can override by a majority but cannot bury in committee. The political difficulty is real. The framework documents it honestly. The question the framework asks is: what process reduces the political difficulty to a level where implementation becomes possible?

## 6. CIVIL SERVICE AND INSTITUTIONAL ACCOUNTABILITY (Domains 2, 26, 34)

### 30-Second Version

The Trump administration's Schedule Policy reclassification — formerly Schedule F — could move up to 50,000 career federal employees into at-will status. Germany's civil service law (Beamtenrecht) is constitutionally protected — federal employees cannot be removed for political reasons by statute. The U.S. is the only major OECD democracy without statutory civil service independence grounded in constitutional protection.

### 2-Minute Version

**Schedule Policy/Career reclassification**: Executive Order 14210 (February 2025) reclassified federal employees into Schedule Policy/Career — functionally equivalent to the 2020 Schedule F order reversed by Biden. The American Federation of Government Employees and other unions have active litigation (AFGE v. OPM, D. Conn., preliminary injunction hearing April 30, 2026) challenging the reclassification under the Civil Service Reform Act. The number of potentially affected employees is estimated at 50,000 — but classification decisions are made agency by agency, and the final number will depend on implementation guidance.

**What accountability infrastructure is being dismantled**: The Office of Professional Responsibility (OPR) director position at DOJ was fired in February 2026 and has not been replaced — a documented 8-month vacancy. The proposed FY2026 IG budget cut is 28% across federal IGs. The Civil Rights Division at DOJ has lost 70% of its career attorneys through resignation, transfer, or separation since January 2025. The Inspector General independence that Congress established in 1978 is not constitutionally protected — it depends on statute and convention that can be overridden by executive action.

**What other countries do**: Germany's Beamtenrecht — the civil service law — is constitutionally grounded in Article 33 of the Basic Law, which provides that civil servants can only be dismissed for cause, with independent review. The UK's Civil Service Code, enforced by the Civil Service Commission, provides for appeals by career civil servants against improper political direction. Canada's Public Service Employment Act creates merit-based appointment protections enforceable in the Federal Court. The U.S. has neither constitutional grounding nor meaningful independent review.

**Reform pathway**: The framework documents a statutory special counsel independence bill (S. 2644) as an existing vehicle, a civil service constitutional amendment as a long-term option, and IG restoration with congressional budget protection as the most achievable near-term action.

### Hardest Objection: "Unaccountable bureaucracy is itself a democratic problem — elected officials should be able to direct the executive."

**Response**: The framework agrees with the underlying principle: elected officials should direct policy, not career bureaucrats. The question is the mechanism. Schedule Policy/Career eliminates not the policy-direction authority of elected officials but the protection against retaliatory firing for career employees who provide unwelcome information or refuse illegal orders. The Hatch Act permits political direction of career employees while prohibiting politically motivated personnel actions. The framework's reform proposals restore that distinction — not protect bureaucratic autonomy from policy direction, but protect career employees from retaliation for lawful activity.

## 7. WAR POWERS AND EXECUTIVE ACCOUNTABILITY (Domains 28, 19f, 29)

### 30-Second Version

The December 23, 2025 OLC memo authorizing Operation Absolute Resolve — the military capture of Venezuela's President Maduro — concedes internally that it applies the two-part war powers test to what it calls a "law enforcement" operation. That framing has no established legal precedent, and if accepted, would permit military operations against the leaders of any country where the DEA has active indictments: Iran, Russia, North Korea, Lebanon.

### 2-Minute Version

**Operation Absolute Resolve**: January 3, 2026. 150+ aircraft, Delta Force ground troops, 200+ U.S. personnel deployed. 55 foreign nationals killed. The administration characterized it as an "arrest operation" — not a military engagement — to avoid the War Powers Resolution's 60-day clock and congressional authorization requirement. The OLC memo authorizing the operation states the president "likely could conclude" the operation does not constitute war — which is not an OLC legal opinion finding legality, it is OLC delegating the constitutional determination to the president.

**The forward-reach problem**: The DEA has active indictments against senior officials in Iran, North Korea, Russia, and Lebanon. Under the "law enforcement arrest" theory, military operations against those officials are permissible without congressional authorization, no matter the scale. Secretary Rubio's limiting principle — that non-permanent-occupation operations are permissible — would authorize essentially any strike or capture operation worldwide. This is not a speculative risk. It is the logical extension of the legal theory as stated.

**The congressional near-miss**: The Senate voted 52-47 to advance a War Powers resolution. The final vote failed 50-50 with Vice President Vance casting the tiebreaker. Two senators — Josh Hawley and Todd Young — reversed their votes after receiving "assurances" from Secretary Rubio that included the phrase "circumstances permitting" — rendering Young's assurance legally non-binding on its face. The House vote was 215-215, with Speaker Johnson holding the vote open 20 minutes for a delayed Republican to arrive.

**The Seth Harp connection**: Journalist Seth Harp received a congressional subpoena after reporting that a Delta Force commander was involved in the operation — reporting that contradicted the administration's characterization of the operation as a law enforcement action. The subpoena documents the connection between operational secrecy and suppression of war powers oversight.

### Hardest Objection: "Maduro is a drug trafficker — was it wrong to capture him?"

**Response**: The framework takes no position on whether capturing Maduro was appropriate as a policy matter. The constitutional question is entirely independent of that. The Eichmann precedent — Israel's abduction of Adolf Eichmann from Argentina in 1960 — was condemned by the United Nations Security Council even though Eichmann was guilty of genocide. The principle the Security Council enforced was territorial sovereignty, not the innocence of the target. Under the administration's legal theory, the targets who matter for constitutional analysis are the ones where the legal predicate is weaker — a mid-level official in a country with whom we have active diplomatic relations, captured using the same "law enforcement arrest" theory without any Congressional authorization.

## 8. IMMIGRATION AND ENFORCEMENT ACCOUNTABILITY (Domain 16)

### 30-Second Version

Community supervision for immigration detainees — ankle monitoring, check-ins, case management — costs $20 per day. ICE detention costs $140-200 per day. The U.S. is currently spending $140+ per day to hold people in detention centers that 40 federal court orders have found provide inadequate medical care. The alternatives are cheaper, more humane, and produce higher rates of appearance at immigration proceedings.

### 2-Minute Version

**The detention cost case**: ICE detention costs $140-200 per person per day. Community supervision — ankle monitoring, case management, check-ins — costs $17-20 per person per day. The cost differential is roughly 8-10x. The U.S. currently detains approximately 40,000 people on any given day at the higher cost when the evidence on appearance rates at immigration proceedings shows that community supervision produces comparable or higher compliance. The National Immigrant Justice Center's data: appearance rates for ICE alternatives to detention participants are 95%.

**The medical care record**: 40 federal court orders have found that ICE detention facilities provide inadequate medical care. Since January 2017, 240+ deaths in ICE custody have been documented by advocates and the ACLU. The DHS Office of Inspector General has issued 12 reports since 2019 documenting failures in medical care, including multiple cases where deaths were preceded by ignored medical requests.

**Independent immigration courts**: Article II immigration courts — where immigration judges are employees of the Executive Branch, supervised by the DOJ, and the head of the agency is the Attorney General — are the only adjudicative system in the federal government where the head of the prosecuting agency is also the appellate authority. The domain documents what an Article I independent immigration court system would look like, with the cost-benefit analysis (estimated cost $1-2 billion/year — comparable to the current court backlog's cost of approximately $4.4 billion in deferred economic activity from pending cases).

**The Abrego Garcia case**: Kilmar Abrego Garcia, a Maryland resident with a withholding of removal order — a federal court order prohibiting his deportation — was deported to El Salvador on March 15, 2026, in violation of that order. The Supreme Court (9-0) ordered his return. The administration's refusal to comply — stating it does not have the ability to return him despite diplomatic relations with El Salvador — is the first documented instance of the executive branch refusing to comply with a Supreme Court order in the contemporary period.

### Hardest Objection: "We need to enforce immigration law — alternatives to detention let people disappear."

**Response**: The 95% appearance rate for ICE Alternatives to Detention participants is the data point that directly addresses this. The claim that alternatives allow people to "disappear" is not supported by the case management data. The ICE ATD program — which the framework documents — has operated since 2004 and has the largest dataset on this question. The framework's position is not that all immigration enforcement is inappropriate; it is that the specific mechanism (detention) is more expensive than the alternatives and produces worse outcomes on the compliance metric that is most relevant.

## 9. CLIMATE AND ENVIRONMENT (Domain 15)

### 30-Second Version

The EPA's Greenhouse Gas Endangerment Finding — the scientific and legal foundation for all U.S. climate regulation — was rescinded in February 2026. Without the Endangerment Finding, the Clean Air Act cannot be used to regulate greenhouse gases at all. The rescission is being challenged by Earthjustice, NRDC, Environmental Defense Fund, and 22 state attorneys general in the D.C. Circuit.

### 2-Minute Version

**The regulatory foundation**: The GHG Endangerment Finding (2009) was the legal basis for every EPA climate regulation since the Massachusetts v. EPA (2007) Supreme Court ruling that greenhouse gases are "air pollutants" under the Clean Air Act. Rescinding the Endangerment Finding eliminates the legal authority for fuel economy standards, power plant emissions rules, and industrial GHG reporting requirements simultaneously. It is not a single regulation being rescinded — it is the regulatory foundation of the entire U.S. climate regulatory program.

**The litigation**: Earthjustice, NRDC, EDF, and 22 state AGs challenged the rescission in the D.C. Circuit. The key question is whether the EPA conducted the required notice-and-comment rulemaking process. The original Endangerment Finding was based on a scientific record; rescinding it requires a scientific record justifying the opposite conclusion, not just a policy preference. The legal argument is that the rescission is arbitrary and capricious under the APA because the scientific record has only strengthened since 2009, not weakened.

**What states can do without federal action**: The RGGI (Regional Greenhouse Gas Initiative) covers 11 northeastern states and has produced 50%+ emissions reductions from covered sources while generating $7.4 billion in clean energy investment. California's cap-and-trade program is the second-largest in the world, after the EU ETS. Twenty-four states have active clean energy standards. The interstate compact model — constitutionally protected under Article I, Section 10, Clause 3 — allows states to coordinate carbon pricing without federal authorization. The framework documents what a comprehensive 50-state interstate compact structure would require and what it could achieve.

**The fiscal case for climate action**: EPA's own modeling (pre-rescission) estimated $2 trillion per year in avoided climate damages by 2100 from implementing the Clean Power Plan. The National Climate Assessment (2023) projected $1.4-2.3 trillion per year in GDP losses by 2100 under high-emission scenarios. The framework's carbon tax design — $100/ton with a citizen dividend of $1,500 per person per year — generates revenue that offsets 4-10x the cost of the climate investments it funds.

### Hardest Objection: "Killing the GHG Endangerment Finding is deregulatory — it lifts a burden on business."

**Response**: The cost calculus depends on what time horizon you use. At a 10-year horizon, removing the regulatory burden reduces compliance costs. At a 30-year horizon, the EPA's own pre-rescission modeling shows $2 trillion per year in avoided damages. The question is who bears the costs when. Current businesses bear the compliance costs now; the $2 trillion in damages is distributed across all future economic activity. The framework documents this as an intergenerational equity problem, not a binary regulation/no-regulation choice. It also documents what the U.S. insurance industry — not an advocacy organization — is pricing into catastrophic weather event actuarial models.

## 10. HEALTHCARE AND MEDICAID (Domains 11, 31)

### 30-Second Version

The One Big Beautiful Budget Act includes $911 billion in Medicaid cuts over 10 years — the CBO's estimate. The most immediate cut is work requirement implementation, effective January 2027, requiring 80 hours of work per month. HHS guidance on what counts as a qualifying work activity is due June 2026. The comment period on that guidance is the most actionable advocacy window between now and implementation.

### 2-Minute Version

**The OBBBA cuts**: CBO projects $911 billion in Medicaid cuts over 10 years. The primary mechanism is work requirements — 80 hours per month of qualifying work, education, or community service. The Arkansas 2018 natural experiment (the only state to actually implement work requirements before courts blocked them): 18,000 people lost coverage in 6 months. Employment rates did not change. The people who lost coverage were primarily already working — they lost coverage due to administrative complexity, not failure to work.

**The enrollment-civic participation nexus**: Medicaid enrollment offices serve as voter registration sites under the National Voter Registration Act (the motor voter law). Cuts to Medicaid enrollment infrastructure reduce voter registration capacity in specific communities — particularly low-income and rural communities. The connection between healthcare access and democratic participation is structural, not incidental.

**The hospital closure risk**: NRHA (National Rural Health Association) projects 446 rural hospitals at risk of closure if Medicaid cuts are implemented as proposed. Rural hospitals in 22 states currently operate at a loss and are kept viable by Medicaid reimbursement. The geographic distribution: the counties at highest risk are heavily concentrated in Republican-held congressional districts. This is not a partisan point — it is a constituent-services point that creates pressure on House Republicans from rural members.

**What works**: The UK's NHS. Canada's single-payer provincial systems. Germany's multi-payer statutory insurance system (where every resident is covered, administrative cost is 6% vs. 30% in the U.S., and per-capita spending is $6,700 vs. $12,318 in the U.S.). The framework documents three structural models and their implementation pathways — not a single "Medicare for All" prescription but a range of institutional designs that other countries use to cover everyone for less.

**The June 2026 advocacy window**: HHS guidance on work requirement implementation — what counts as qualifying activity, how documentation is verified, what the exemption process looks like — is due June 2026. The comment period on that guidance is the most consequential near-term advocacy moment. The legal challenge argument: HHS guidance that makes work requirements effectively impossible to comply with for people who are working (due to documentation requirements, rural broadband access for reporting, etc.) is arbitrary and capricious under the APA.

### Hardest Objection: "Single-payer healthcare is socialist — Americans don't want it."

**Response**: The framework documents three models, not one. Germany's statutory health insurance system is not single-payer — it has 100+ competing nonprofit insurance funds (Krankenkassen), employer mandates, and regulated competition. Switzerland uses an individual mandate with regulated private insurers. The Netherlands uses a managed competition model. All three cover 100% of their populations at lower per-capita cost than the U.S. The "socialist" framing doesn't describe the German or Dutch models, which maintain private insurers and market competition. The framework documents each model and what the U.S. implementation pathway would require.
