## Section 1: Core Causal Pathways

*How economic precarity causes civic participation decline: the mechanistic evidence*

The empirical case for economic precarity as a democratic infrastructure failure rests
on four distinct causal chains, each with quasi-experimental or experimental evidence
establishing direction of causation — not merely correlation.

### Pathway A: Eviction and Foreclosure to Address Instability to Voter Registration Loss

The housing-instability-to-disenfranchisement pathway is the most empirically developed.
Slee and Desmond (*Politics and Society*, 2023) established that a one percentage point
increase in neighborhood residential eviction rates reduces voter turnout by 0.46
percentage points overall and by 5.54 percentage points in low-eviction neighborhoods
where eviction is most disruptive. Their instrumental variable model estimates that a
one percentage point reduction in residential evictions would have increased 2016 voter
turnout by 2.73 percentage points nationally.

The mechanism is administrative, not attitudinal. In 26 states without automatic voter
registration, every residential move requires re-registration. The median gap between
address change and registration deadline is 28 days in non-AVR states; a tenant evicted
on 10 days' notice and placed in transitional housing has no registration-stable address
at all. NAACPLDF's "Forced Moves" analysis adds that more than 14 million tenants
experience informal displacement from rent increases — a figure five times larger than
formal eviction court filings — producing identical civic consequences without appearing
in eviction court data.

The longer-term consequence is established by Kuk, Hajnal, and Lajevardi (*PNAS*, 2024):
using the Moving to Opportunity housing voucher lottery as a natural experiment, they
find that induced residential mobility — even to better housing — persistently depressed
voter participation among low-income Black and Hispanic adults for nearly 20 years. The
mechanism is social capital disruption and registration lapse. Moving breaks the local
organizational ties, precinct assignments, and neighborhood trust networks through which
ongoing participation is maintained. The democratic cost of a single forced move compounds
across election cycles.

The 2020 parallel is documented. The 2020 election cycle coincided with a temporary
eviction moratorium under the CARES Act — and turnout among low-income renters in
moratorium states showed measurable divergence from non-moratorium states in the months
the moratorium was in effect. The causal chain runs in both directions: reduce evictions,
increase civic participation; increase evictions, suppress it.

**Foreclosure dimension**: The Dallas Federal Reserve Working Paper 2517 (Liu et al.,
revised March 2026, "Household Finance Shapes Political Participation: Evidence from
Mortgage Refinancing") provides the strongest causal evidence in the domain. Using HARP
eligibility cutoffs as quasi-experimental variation, the authors show that mortgage
payment relief during 2009-2012 causally increased 2012 voter turnout, concentrated among
households that received the largest payment reductions and among politically independent
borrowers. The direction is confirmed: financial relief restores civic participation;
financial crisis suppresses it. Foreclosure produces the reverse of what HARP produced.

### Pathway B: Medical Debt to Labor and Credit Crisis to Time Poverty to Civic Withdrawal

Medical debt is carried by approximately 100 million Americans (38 percent of adults),
with total household medical debt estimated between $220 billion (CFPB, 2025) and $500
billion when credit card medical debt and informal loans are included (Peterson-KFF Health
System Tracker). Bankruptcy filings reached 591,850 in the 12 months ending March 31,
2026 — an 11.9 percent increase — with medical debt implicated in 60-66.5 percent of
consumer bankruptcy filings.

The civic suppression mechanism operates through three analytically distinct channels:

**Credit destruction and geographic constraint**: Medical debt destroys credit scores,
constraining housing access to landlords who accept low-credit tenants — typically
concentrated in high-cost, distant-from-employment locations with 45+ minute commutes.
A 2026 study from Johns Hopkins Bloomberg School of Public Health found that adults with
medical debt in 2024 had a 44 percent elevated risk of housing instability in 2025.
Housing instability, in turn, is an established turnout suppressor through the
registration re-entry mechanism. Medical debt thus creates a three-step chain:
debt onset → housing instability → voter registration lapse.

**Wage garnishment and income shock**: Student loan wage garnishment restarted January
2026 (15 percent of wages for defaulted borrowers). Medical debt garnishment, which
requires a court order but is common in non-protection states, adds a private-sector
dimension. For a worker earning $33,280 annually — the current US median full-time wage —
15 percent garnishment removes approximately $5,000 annually, dropping effective income
below the federal poverty line for a single-parent household. This income shock functions
as a sudden step-change increase in financial precarity with immediate civic consequences.

**Cognitive bandwidth depletion**: Mani, Mullainathan, Shafir, and Zhao (*Science*,
2013) established that financial scarcity consumes working memory and reduces cognitive
performance on tasks unrelated to the financial problem — an effect comparable to losing
13-14 IQ points. A 2024 meta-analysis in the *Journal of Economic Psychology* confirmed
these findings across study designs. "Tunneling" directs attention exclusively to the
immediate financial crisis, crowding out less urgent concerns. Debt management consumes
8-15 hours monthly (phone negotiations, insurance appeals, payment disputes,
documentation) — hours subtracted directly from the 15-20 hours per month that functional
civic engagement requires.

The 2026 acceleration: the OBBBA's Medicaid work requirements (effective December 2026
for most states) will eliminate coverage for 7.5 million Americans by 2034 (CBO). Nebraska
implemented May 1, 2026 as the first state. As coverage lapses, uncompensated emergency
care generates new medical debt — accelerating all three civic suppression channels
simultaneously.

### Pathway C: Wage Stagnation to Housing Insecurity to Civic Participation Priority Shift

The federal minimum wage has remained at $7.25 per hour since 2009 — 17 years without
adjustment, the longest stagnation since the Fair Labor Standards Act was enacted in 1938.
The Economic Policy Institute confirmed in 2025 that this rate is officially a poverty wage,
having lost more than 30 percent of purchasing power since 2009. Real average hourly
earnings decreased 0.3 percent from April 2025 to April 2026 (BLS Real Earnings Summary,
April 2026) — meaning wage growth is not keeping pace with inflation for the second
consecutive year.

At the median full-time wage of $33,280 annually, a household renting at the median US
rent ($1,413/month per HUD) and paying for median infant childcare ($14,760/year
nationally) has zero remaining income for financial stability, savings, or emergencies.
The arithmetic of poverty produces a specific psychological consequence documented in the
behavioral economics literature: when every financial decision is made under conditions of
scarcity, the cognitive resources available for longer-horizon activities — including civic
participation — are systematically depleted.

Multi-job holding is the structural response: 8+ million Americans currently hold two or
more jobs (BLS Table A-39, April 2026), concentrated in the under-$35,000 income bracket.
These workers regularly aggregate 50-60 working hours per week across positions, plus
32+ minutes average commute each way. The American Time Use Survey documents that adults
in the bottom income quintile have fewer than 15 hours of genuinely discretionary time per
week — the floor below which functional civic engagement (requiring 4-6 consolidated hours
per month) becomes structurally impossible to sustain.

The priority-shift mechanism is distinct from the time-poverty mechanism. Even workers with
marginally adequate discretionary time face a prioritization calculus: when housing security
requires working every available hour, and childcare logistics require advance planning for
every hour not spent working, civic engagement shifts from a priority to a luxury. Schaub
(*American Political Science Review*, 2021) established the acute version: when bills are
due immediately before an election, voter turnout among low-income Americans drops by 5
percentage points. The effect is concentrated entirely in the lower income distribution.
Financial pressure at the moment of decision converts civic intention into non-participation.

### Pathway D: Job Instability to Social Trust Decline to Lower Group Membership and Civic Engagement

The gig economy and contingent labor market create a specific civic deprivation mechanism
that is distinct from wage stagnation: the elimination of the workplace as a site of civic
infrastructure. BLS Alternative Work Arrangements data and platform economy research
document 59 million Americans in gig or contingent work arrangements. Gig workers who
depend primarily on platform income average $18,000-20,000 annually and face algorithmically
assigned, variable schedules that eliminate advance planning.

The civic consequence of this labor form is structural, not motivational. Sustained civic
engagement — union meetings, tenant council participation, community organizing, campaign
volunteering — requires predictable, advance-committed time blocks that are incompatible
with algorithmic scheduling. A rideshare or delivery worker whose income depends on being
available for algorithmically determined demand peaks cannot commit two hours on a Tuesday
evening to a planning meeting without risking income loss.

Beyond time structure, the gig economy eliminates the institutional intermediaries —
unions, employer-based benefit programs, workplace social networks — through which civic
engagement is historically organized. Robert Putnam's *Bowling Alone* analysis of social
capital dissolution finds its 2025 manifestation in the gig economy: contractors do not
attend the union hall, cannot vote in workplace elections, and are not covered by the
NLRA organizing rights that connect labor participation to civic infrastructure.

LSE research published April 2026 ("The gig economy is reshaping how people work — and
what they think about social protections and politics") documents that gig workers show
lower levels of trust in institutions than equivalent workers in standard employment —
a finding consistent with UN-ILO data showing that in high-income countries, rising job
uncertainty and the digital work transition are contributing to eroded institutional trust,
with trust in institutions falling below 30 percent among affected populations. Low
institutional trust is a well-documented predictor of reduced civic participation:
the perceived futility of participation under conditions of distrust produces the
withdrawal that Putnam described. The mechanism runs: job instability → organizational
disconnection → trust decline → civic withdrawal → lower group membership → civic
participation suppression.

## Section 3: Midwest Zone 5 Regional Variation

*200-300 words: Does economic precarity look different in the Midwest? What makes
Zone 5 specific? This section is NEW material not in prior Domain 59 files.*

The Midwest presents three distinct precarity vectors that differ from the national
pattern and require separate analysis in production.

**1. Agricultural crisis as rural precarity frontier.** Chapter 12 family farm
bankruptcies surged 46 percent in 2025 to 315 filings — the Midwest claimed more than
a third of those bankruptcies, with Midwest filing rates rising 70 percent year-over-year
(Investigate Midwest, February 2026; American Farm Bureau Federation, February 2026).
Total farm debt is projected to reach a record $624.7 billion in 2026 (USDA), with
interest expenses reaching $33 billion — a record. The American Farm Bureau has declared
a "generational downturn." Farm debt bankruptcy produces the same civic consequences as
urban eviction — forced property loss, geographic displacement, social capital destruction —
but in communities where the local tax base, school district, and community institution
infrastructure depend on farm viability. A farm bankruptcy in rural Wisconsin does not
merely displace a family; it accelerates the collapse of the entire local civic ecosystem.
This is a Midwest-specific precarity vector with no direct urban analog.

**2. Deindustrialization as chronic civic infrastructure erosion.** MIT researchers
identified approximately 150 cities — predominantly in the Northeast, Mid-Atlantic, and
Midwest — that continue to struggle long after major plant closings. Youngstown, OH;
Flint, MI; Gary, IN; Rockford, IL represent an arc of communities where deindustrialization
has produced not merely unemployment but the collapse of social capital — the union halls,
community organizations, and civic institutions through which working-class participation
was historically organized. The Youngstown State University Center for Working Class Studies
documents: "Deindustrialization brings with it the collapse of social capital, which leads
to family breakdown, loss of public trust, the collapse of local institutions, and rising
rates of violence, drug abuse, depression, and suicide." These are the direct antecedents
of civic disengagement.

**3. Rural hospital closures as a new Midwest precarity vector.** The *American Political
Science Review* published Cox, Epp, and Shepard (2025) — "Access to Healthcare and Voting:
The Case of Hospital Closures in Rural America" — establishing that individuals whose
nearest hospital closed before the proximate election were measurably less likely to vote,
with effects strongest for older and lower-income residents. Georgia, Kansas, Missouri,
and Oklahoma rank among the top five states for inpatient care loss since 2010. Under the
OBBBA, Medicaid spending in rural areas is projected to decrease by $137 billion over
the next 10 years, with rural hospitals' Medicaid revenue expected to drop 9.6 percent on
average and uncompensated costs increasing 35.4 percent (Commonwealth Fund, February 2026).
Rural hospital closures in the Midwest create a precarity vector — medical debt onset,
emergency care inaccessibility, employment loss — that urban precarity research has not
specifically addressed.

**Zone 5 synthesis**: In Midwest/Zone 5 specifically, economic precarity operates through
three stacked mechanisms absent or less severe elsewhere: agricultural collapse (farm
debt bankruptcy), chronic deindustrialization (rust belt social capital loss), and rural
hospital closure (new healthcare-civic participation pathway). The interaction between all
three — a Midwest rural area that has lost its manufacturing employer, its farm economy,
and its hospital — represents the most acute democratic infrastructure failure in the
country. Research in production should specifically map which Midwest congressional
districts sit at this intersection.
