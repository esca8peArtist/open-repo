## Lead Finding

Houston has one of the largest municipal Flock Safety deployments in the country — approximately 3,000 cameras across greater Houston, with a $6.39 million city contract covering 318 cameras operated by the Houston Police Department, plus thousands more operated by HOAs, school districts, and private businesses that feed into the same network accessible to ICE. HPD officers were documented using the system with no legitimate purpose entered — search justifications including "ASDF," "donut," and blank entries. Two school districts south of Houston (Alvin ISD and Huffman ISD) had their cameras actively queried for immigration enforcement purposes. The Houston City Council has jurisdiction to terminate or restrict the city's Flock contract, and specific council members are the identified targets for advocacy.

## Part 1: Current Houston Deployment

### 1.1 Scale and Structure

Houston's Flock camera network operates across multiple overlapping ownership structures:

**City of Houston contract** (HPD):
- 318 cameras under a five-year sole-source contract
- Contract value: up to $6,390,000 ($3.94M HPD, $2.3M Public Works, $150K Administration and Regulatory Affairs)
- Approved: August 16, 2022 (City Council agenda item #23)
- Contract method: Sole source — exempt from competitive bidding under Texas Local Government Code
- Primary HPD contact on record: Sonja Odat, 713-308-1728
- Annual cost approximately $3,000 per camera

**Regional private and HOA deployment**:
- Approximately 3,000 total Flock cameras across greater Houston (as of 2024-2025)
- Over 1,500 Houston-area businesses with Flock coverage
- Memorial Villages Police Department: 33 owned cameras, access to 1,331 cameras across Houston network; processes 400,000+ plate reads monthly
- Pearland: purchased cameras February 2022 ($191,000)
- Multiple suburban municipalities: Katy, Memorial Villages, Jersey Village (operating 4+ years)
- Midtown Management District: 25 cameras deployed since May 2024 (managed by private district authority, not city government)

**School district cameras (high-risk for immigration enforcement)**:
- Alvin Independent School District (south Houston, 30,000 students): 8 Flock cameras, $50,000+ expenditure since 2023
- Huffman ISD: Flock cameras; campus police chief's administrative assistant granted U.S. Border Patrol direct database access in May 2025
- Other Harris County school districts: deployment status not fully public

**Total network access**: Through Flock's National Lookup Tool, HPD and other Houston-area agencies can access approximately 88,000+ cameras from agencies across the country — and all local cameras are potentially accessible to 4,800+ agencies nationwide.

### 1.2 Geographic Distribution

Public information does not provide a complete neighborhood-level camera map — HPD has declined to make this public. However, confirmed deployment areas based on public record and reporting include:

- **Midtown** (25 cameras, Midtown Management District)
- **Memorial Villages** (33+ owned, 1,300+ accessible through network)
- **Pearland** (municipal purchase 2022)
- **Katy** (early adopter, 4+ years of operation)
- **Jersey Village** (early adopter)
- **Alvin** (school district cameras, south of Houston)
- **Huffman** (school district cameras, northeast)
- **Harris County Precinct 4** (operational; cameras aided constable killing investigation)

The absence of a public camera map is itself an advocacy target: Houston has no transparency ordinance requiring public disclosure of ALPR locations.

### 1.3 HPD Usage Patterns: Documented Misuse

A Texas Standard investigation documented that Houston Police Department officers use the Flock system "like a search engine, like Google, than an official law enforcement tool." Specific findings:

- HPD logged hundreds of thousands of search calls in the past year
- Officers entered "ASDF," "donut," "random checks," or left the purpose field entirely blank
- HPD's governing ALPR policy dates to **2015** — predating Flock's existence — and does not require officers to document search justifications
- HPD maintains no regulation mandates written justification of searches
- Investigation found officers routinely fail to provide legitimate purposes

This documentation mirrors the national pattern: Flock's audit log system is structurally inadequate to prevent misuse, and Houston's own policy provides no meaningful oversight mechanism.

Source: [Texas Standard — Houston Flock Investigation](https://texasstandard.org/stories/flock-safety-cameras-houston-police-texas-investigation-crime-use/)

## Part 2: The Immigration Enforcement Exposure

### 2.1 Alvin ISD: The Houston-Adjacent ICE Pipeline

The Alvin Independent School District case is the most documented evidence of Houston-area Flock cameras being used for immigration enforcement:

- **8 cameras** installed at this 30,000-student district south of Houston since 2023
- **733,000+ searches** of these 8 cameras in a single month (December 2025 through early January 2026)
- **3,100+ law enforcement agencies** searched these school cameras in that period
- **620 immigration-related searches** by 30 law enforcement agencies from Florida, Georgia, Indiana, Tennessee, and other states
- Search categories included dropdown options in the Flock interface: "Immigration (civil/administrative)," "I.C.E.," "ERO proactive crim case research," and "CBP Investigation" — demonstrating that immigration enforcement is a designed, built-in feature of the Flock search interface

The school district did not initiate these immigration searches and stated it was unaware other agencies were using its cameras this way. This is the structural problem: once cameras are in the Flock network, any of 4,800+ agencies can search them, regardless of what the camera owner intended.

### 2.2 Huffman ISD: Direct CBP Access

At Huffman ISD — northeast of Houston — a campus police chief's administrative assistant granted U.S. Border Patrol **direct access** to the district's Flock readers in May 2025. This was not a case of cross-agency searching through the Flock interface; CBP was given direct credentials to query Huffman cameras. The district has not publicly stated whether it was aware this access was granted.

### 2.3 HPD-ICE Relationship Context

Houston's city-level ICE cooperation policy has been in flux during 2026. Key developments affecting the Flock context:

- Houston previously had an ordinance limiting police cooperation with ICE
- Governor Abbott threatened loss of $200 million in public safety grants unless the ordinance was amended
- April 22, 2026: Houston City Council voted to amend the ordinance, allowing HPD officers to "wait a reasonable amount of time" for ICE to obtain custody of people with civil immigration warrants
- Harris County is separately considering an anti-ICE policy modeled on Houston's prior (more restrictive) ordinance

The practical effect: HPD now has increased structural alignment with ICE operations, which heightens the risk that HPD's Flock search logs are shared with or searched on behalf of ICE. This is the same pattern documented in Dayton, Ohio (7,100 immigration searches in violation of city policy) and evinced in Kyle, Texas (117 immigration-related searches by 18 agencies).

Sources: [The 74 — ICE School Cameras](https://www.the74million.org/article/ice-taps-into-school-security-cameras-to-aid-trumps-immigration-crackdown-74-investigation-shows/) | [Houston Public Media — HPD-ICE policy](https://www.houstonpublicmedia.org/articles/police/2026/04/23/549984/houston-police-ice-hpd-policy-detention-abbott/) | [Texas Tribune — ICE cooperation](https://www.texastribune.org/2026/04/22/texas-houston-city-council-ice-cooperation-amendment/)

## Part 3: Legal and Regulatory Framework in Texas

### 3.1 Texas State Law — No ALPR Protection

Texas has enacted no comprehensive ALPR privacy law. Key relevant state-level facts:

- **Texas DPS** signed a $26 million contract with Flock Safety in 2024 — the state is an active Flock customer, not a regulator
- **Texas requires** all county sheriffs to enter 287(g) immigration enforcement agreements with ICE (effective December 1, 2025), creating a mandatory structural pipeline from local law enforcement to federal immigration enforcement
- **Texas fined Flock** in 2024 for operating without a private investigator's license before obtaining proper certification — but government entities and law enforcement agencies are explicitly exempt from this requirement. The fine applied only to private deployments
- **Texas has 10,000+ Flock cameras** statewide, the highest concentration outside of the company's founding state (Georgia)
- **No pending Texas legislation** to restrict ALPR use had gained traction as of mid-2026

Cities that have acted independently: Austin terminated 540+ Flock and Axon cameras; San Marcos blocked expansion; Hays County became the first Texas county to terminate its Flock contract; Lockhart rejected 7 new cameras (6-1 vote); Kyle voted 6-1 to retain cameras (one dissenting vote by Councilmember Claudia Zapata on immigration grounds).

Houston has taken no action to restrict, audit, or terminate its Flock contract.

### 3.2 Houston City Ordinance: The 2022 Surveillance Framework

The most relevant existing Houston ordinance: April 2022 Houston ordinance requiring businesses to provide surveillance footage to police without warrants. This ordinance was passed shortly before the August 2022 Flock contract approval, suggesting an integrated surveillance strategy rather than independent decisions. The ordinance creates a legal pathway for HPD to access private Flock camera footage without judicial authorization.

No Houston ordinance currently:
- Requires public disclosure of ALPR camera locations
- Mandates documented search justifications for Flock queries
- Restricts immigration enforcement use of city Flock data
- Limits access by out-of-state or federal agencies to Houston cameras
- Requires audit logs to be publicly reported
- Sets a maximum data retention period below Flock's default 30 days

### 3.3 The Licensing Issue

In 2024, Texas DPS issued Flock a cease-and-desist order for operating without a private investigator's license before receiving certification. Flock stated it immediately initiated the certification process. The significance for advocacy: this episode demonstrates that Flock has operated outside proper legal licensing in Texas — a fact that can be raised in city council testimony to question the company's overall compliance posture.

Source: [Hoodline — Houston Flock Legal Hurdle](https://hoodline.com/2024/10/high-tech-flock-cameras-aid-houston-law-enforcement-but-face-legal-hurdle-over-licensing/)

## Part 5: Community Organizing Pathways

### 5.1 Coalition Building

Houston's demographics create a natural multi-community coalition against Flock expansion:

**Immigrant communities**: Greater Houston has one of the largest immigrant populations of any U.S. city. The documented use of Flock cameras to facilitate ICE searches — including at school districts serving Houston-area communities — creates direct, concrete harm to families. Organizations: Houston Immigration Legal Services Collaborative, RAICES Texas, ACLU of Texas Houston office

**Black communities**: Surveillance technology disproportionately surveils and misidentifies Black drivers. Memorial Villages (a predominantly white, affluent area) has 33 cameras; the absence of equivalent transparency in predominantly Black districts like District D raises equity questions. Organizations: Houston NAACP, Action Alliance for the Mentally Ill

**Faith communities**: Flock cameras have been deployed at locations near churches and houses of worship, and Washington's SB 6002 specifically prohibits ALPR data collection at places of worship — a national advocacy precedent. Houston's large network of immigrant-serving churches is a natural organizing base.

**Legal aid and civil rights**: ACLU of Texas operates a Houston office; Texas RioGrande Legal Aid provides immigration-specific assistance; Houston Volunteer Lawyers program

**Student organizing**: University of Houston, Rice University, Texas Southern University students have organized on related surveillance issues; connection to national DeFlock movement (DeFlock Emory provides model)

### 5.2 Public Records Strategy

Before any public campaign, file comprehensive public records requests. Under the Texas Public Information Act (Chapter 552, Texas Government Code), the following are public records:

1. **Complete Flock contract text** and all amendments or renewal documents
2. **Data-sharing agreements** between HPD and any other law enforcement agency through the Flock platform
3. **Audit logs** of all Flock searches by HPD officers, including timestamps, searching officer ID, and stated purpose
4. **Communications** between HPD and Flock Safety about federal agency access, including any "pilot programs"
5. **Communications** between HPD and ICE about Flock data access or requests
6. **Camera location list** for all city-contracted Flock cameras
7. **Monthly or quarterly reports** submitted by Flock to HPD

File separately with: HPD Records Division, City Secretary's Office, City Attorney's Office. Texas law requires responses within 10 business days (business records) to 45 days (where third-party notification is required).

### 5.3 Specific Asks for City Council Action

A city council resolution or ordinance could require any or all of the following — each of which has been adopted by other municipalities:

**Immediate actions (no contract termination required)**:
1. Public disclosure of all Flock camera locations (transparency ordinance)
2. Mandatory documented justification for every Flock search (policy change, enforceable through HPD policy)
3. Prohibition on HPD sharing Houston Flock data with out-of-state agencies without a specific judicial order
4. Prohibition on HPD sharing Houston Flock data with federal immigration enforcement agencies (sanctuary-aligned policy)
5. Reduction of data retention from 30 days to 7 days (negotiated contract amendment)
6. Quarterly public audit reports on Flock search activity

**Medium-term actions**:
7. Independent audit of HPD Flock usage logs (modeled on Dayton and Mountain View audits)
8. Passage of a Houston TRUST Act (modeled on Austin's Transparent and Responsible Use of Surveillance Technology Act) requiring public approval for new surveillance technology acquisitions
9. Review of data-sharing agreements to disable National Lookup Tool pending policy reform

**Contract-level action**:
10. Non-renewal of the Flock contract at the next renewal option (FY2027)
11. If renewal has already been exercised, early termination for cause based on: documented unauthorized federal access, misrepresentations in the sales process, terms changes removing data sale prohibition

### 5.4 Connection to School Districts

Harris County school boards — not the city council — govern Alvin ISD and Huffman ISD. These are separate advocacy targets:

**Alvin ISD Board of Trustees**: Independently elected; advocacy should focus on the board's obligation to protect student and family privacy, documented immigration enforcement use of school cameras, and whether the district was informed of and consented to federal agency access. The board should be asked to terminate the Flock contract and issue a public statement about past unauthorized access.

**Huffman ISD Board of Trustees**: Separately elected; the direct CBP access granted in May 2025 is a more serious incident — an administrative employee unilaterally granted a federal law enforcement agency credentials to district cameras. The board should be asked to conduct an internal investigation, terminate CBP access, and publicly report findings.

**Contact strategy**: School board members are accessible at public board meetings (typically monthly) and via public comment periods. Parent-teacher associations, immigrant parent networks, and teachers' unions are natural allies.
