# Phase 2 Domain Candidates
## AI/Tech Regulatory Capture, Healthcare-Democracy Nexus, Surveillance Capitalism

*May 12, 2026. Three candidate domains evaluated for Phase 2 research launch. Each fills a structural gap that has grown more acute since the framework's Phase 1 domains were finalized. Candidates are prioritized by adoption potential and research feasibility. Full outlines and source lists are production-ready.*

## Executive Summary

The resistance-research framework's Phase 1 (35 base domains plus Domain 37) addressed the full arc of democratic erosion from electoral architecture through institutional capture, rights suppression, and economic power concentration. The Phase 2 expansion (Domains 38–53, now largely complete) filled frontline crisis gaps: healthcare access collapse, reproductive rights, labor rights, housing, civil society suppression, and mutual aid legal infrastructure.

Three structural gaps persist that no completed or in-pipeline domain currently addresses as a coherent democratic design problem:

**Gap 1: AI/Tech Regulatory Capture.** The framework has a strong domain on AI governance in government (Domain 36). It does not have a domain analyzing corporate capture of AI regulation itself — how Big Tech industry actors have systematically disabled democratic oversight of AI, from the revolving door at federal agencies to the Trump-era executive order that preempted state AI regulation using federal funding leverage. The May 2026 academic literature now provides a systematic taxonomy of 27 regulatory capture mechanisms used by AI companies. This is not a governance analysis; it is a democratic accountability analysis.

**Gap 2: Healthcare Access and the Democracy Nexus.** Domain 31 (Medicaid Crisis) analyzes healthcare access collapse under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA). It does not analyze the democracy-specific downstream: the NVRA-Medicaid enrollment nexus that makes Medicaid office closures and coverage losses a voter registration suppression mechanism. Fifteen million people projected to lose Medicaid coverage by 2034 are also 15 million fewer people served by NVRA-mandated voter registration sites. This is not a healthcare policy argument; it is a democratic participation architecture argument.

**Gap 3: Surveillance Capitalism and Electoral Manipulation.** Domain 25 (FISA/Surveillance) addresses foreign intelligence collection architecture. Domain 36 addresses algorithmic government decision-making. Neither addresses the private commercial surveillance ecosystem — data brokers, voter file companies, and psychological targeting infrastructure — as an electoral manipulation architecture. The 2026 midterms are the first cycle where AI-generated microtargeted political ads operate at documented industrial scale. The data broker loophole in FISA lets intelligence agencies buy without warrants what they cannot collect without warrants. These two surveillance streams — commercial and governmental — are now convergent in ways the framework does not analyze.

**Prioritization:** Surveillance Capitalism earns the highest adoption-potential ranking because it has a hard June 12 legislative window (FISA Section 702 reauthorization deadline), the strongest cross-partisan coalition (libertarian-right plus progressive-left), and the most acute 2026 electoral cycle urgency. AI/Tech Regulatory Capture ranks second because the academic literature (May 2026 arXiv paper) is newly mature and because the Trump December 2025 executive order preempting state AI laws created a specific constitutional crisis that is currently underanalyzed. Healthcare-Democracy Nexus ranks third in research feasibility but has the highest raw electoral stakes: 15 million disenrolled Medicaid beneficiaries losing NVRA voter registration access is the largest single structural voter suppression mechanism currently underway.

## Candidate A: Surveillance Capitalism and Electoral Manipulation

### Core Democratic Design Problem

What goes wrong without this domain: The framework documents government surveillance of political opponents (Domain 37), prosecutorial weaponization (Domain 29), and AI governance failures in government (Domain 36). But it has no domain analyzing the commercial surveillance infrastructure — the 4,000+ data brokers who sell detailed psychological profiles, location histories, and inferred political preferences to any paying customer — as an electoral manipulation system. The design failure is structural: there is no consent requirement for political targeting in US law, no warrant requirement for government purchase of commercially-collected data, and no transparency mandate for AI-generated political ads.

The democratic design consequence is twofold. First, campaigns can identify and surgically discourage specific voters from turning out — targeted digital voter suppression disproportionately targets racial minorities in battleground state counties, according to a 2025 PMC study. Second, intelligence agencies can buy from data brokers what they cannot constitutionally collect themselves, collapsing the Fourth Amendment's warrant requirement in political organizing contexts. A 2026 NPR investigation documented that ICE, DHS, and the FBI purchase commercially-collected cell phone location data and behavioral data without warrants. The same commercial data infrastructure serves both political campaigns and law enforcement surveillance of political dissidents.

### Current Structural Vulnerability (2026)

The convergence of three 2026 crises makes this domain uniquely time-sensitive:

**FISA Section 702 and the data broker loophole.** The House passed a three-year Section 702 reauthorization on April 29, 2026 (235-191). The Senate, under a 45-day extension with a June 12, 2026 hard deadline, is negotiating. The central unresolved dispute is whether the reform must close the commercial data broker loophole — the mechanism by which agencies use purchased commercial data to circumvent FISA's warrant requirements for US-person communications. The Wyden-Lee-Davidson-Lofgren bill would ban government purchase of Americans' data from data brokers without a warrant. Senator Wyden has documented that a FISA court opinion revealed FBI searches of 19,000 donors to a congressional campaign — not foreign terrorists, but Americans exercising constitutional political participation rights.

**AI-generated microtargeted political ads at industrial scale.** The 2026 gubernatorial races are the first election cycle where AI-generated campaign messaging operates at documented scale within platform recommendation systems. Meta's own internal research shows AI-generated political ads achieve 34% higher engagement than human-created content when targeted through Instagram's algorithm. The $10.8 billion projected 2026 election advertising spend is increasingly directed through AI-generated precision targeting based on detailed psychological profiles. Cambridge Analytica harvested 87 million Facebook profiles and built psychographic profiles for electoral manipulation in 2016; the same methodology now operates at orders of magnitude greater scale with commercially available data broker services.

**ICE surveillance expansion and political chilling effect.** The federal government has explicitly stated it will use intelligence capabilities to target anti-ICE protesters. The Brennan Center documented in a May 2026 report that ICE "wants to go after dissenters as well as immigrants." The commercial surveillance infrastructure — CLEAR (Thomson Reuters), Palantir, and hundreds of smaller data brokers — now openly markets to ICE for deportation operations and political dissent tracking. The surveillance industry creates the infrastructure through which aspiring autocrats surveil political opponents.

### International Precedent

The EU provides the most developed international comparison on both the campaign finance and surveillance dimensions.

The EU's General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) Article 9 prohibits processing of political opinion data without explicit consent — which functions as a near-prohibition on psychographic voter profiling. The EU's Digital Services Act (2024) requires large platforms to disclose algorithmic recommendation systems and conduct societal risk assessments specifically for electoral effects. Germany's Federal Constitutional Court has ruled that blanket surveillance of political parties violates the Basic Law's guarantee of democratic pluralism.

Canada's Elections Act prohibits the use of personal information collected without consent for targeted political advertising and requires disclosure of all political advertising spend, including digital targeting. The UK's Political Parties, Elections and Referendums Act (PPERA) requires disclosure of all political advertising expenditure, and the Information Commissioner's Office has fined Cambridge Analytica affiliates for unlawful data use in political targeting.

The comparative lesson: the democratic design solution is not prohibiting political communication but requiring transparency (who paid, what targeting criteria), consent (voters opt into profiling rather than being profiled without knowledge), and a warrant before government agencies use commercially-purchased data about political activity.

### Reform Pathway

A three-tier reform architecture addresses commercial surveillance, government data purchase, and platform transparency:

**Tier 1 — Close the data broker warrant loophole.** The Fourth Amendment Data Protection Act (versions introduced by Wyden/Paul coalition) would require a warrant before any government agency purchases commercial data that it could not collect directly without a warrant. This is the most achievable near-term reform because it has the strongest bipartisan coalition (civil libertarian Republicans and progressive Democrats) and the most imminent legislative window (June 12 FISA deadline).

**Tier 2 — Campaign surveillance transparency.** A Political Data Protection Act would require consent before commercial data brokers can sell voter profile data for political targeting purposes, mandate disclosure of AI-generated political ads as AI-generated, and require campaigns to disclose psychographic targeting criteria used in political ads. The EU's model provides the template.

**Tier 3 — Algorithmic electoral accountability.** Building on Domain 36's AI governance framework: mandatory algorithmic impact assessments for electoral recommendation systems by large platforms, with an independent oversight body (modeled on the UK's Information Commissioner or the EU's AI Office) with authority to audit and impose structural remedies on electoral manipulation architectures.

### Adoption Angle

The bipartisan civil liberties coalition is the dominant coalition structure: the Brennan Center, ACLU, EFF, CATO Institute, and Reason Foundation all have active programs on surveillance reform. Senator Rand Paul and Senator Ron Wyden have co-sponsored FISA reform bills for eight years. The June 12 deadline concentrates this coalition's attention precisely at the moment a domain document can be most useful to Senate Judiciary and Intelligence Committee staff.

The electoral integrity advocacy community (Protect Democracy, Campaign Legal Center, Democracy Docket) provides the second distribution tier — connecting the commercial surveillance analysis to the electoral manipulation documentation that is their core work. State attorneys general who filed challenges to the Trump surveillance executive orders are the third tier — this domain directly supports their constitutional standing argument.

## Candidate B: AI/Tech Regulatory Capture

### Core Democratic Design Problem

What goes wrong without this domain: Domain 36 (AI Governance and Algorithmic Accountability) analyzes how government agencies use AI in ways that harm democratic participation — automated benefit denials, algorithmic immigration enforcement, AI-generated government propaganda. It does not analyze the capture of AI regulation itself as a democratic design failure. The design problem is structural: when the most economically powerful industry in human history systematically disables the democratic institutions whose job is to regulate it, the failure is not a policy gap. It is a democratic accountability collapse.

The May 2026 arXiv paper "Big AI's Regulatory Capture: Mapping Industry Interference and Government Complicity" (arXiv:2605.06806) provides the first comprehensive taxonomy of this capture: 27 mechanisms across five categories, with 249 documented instances of capture co-occurring with rationalization narratives. The paper explicitly describes the situation as "something policymakers and the public ought to treat as an emergency." This academic foundation, combined with the Trump administration's December 2025 executive order preempting state AI regulation using federal funding leverage, creates the specific 2026 democratic-design problem: the executive branch has weaponized federal infrastructure to disable state-level democratic attempts to govern AI, while simultaneously gutting federal AI safety enforcement.

### Current Structural Vulnerability (2026)

Three interlocking mechanisms constitute the 2026 AI regulatory capture crisis:

**Federal preemption of state AI law using funding leverage.** The Trump administration's December 11, 2025 executive order "Ensuring a National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence" explicitly preempted state AI laws by conditioning approximately $21 billion in Broadband Equity Access and Deployment (BEAD) program funds on states not maintaining "onerous" AI laws. This is the same leverage mechanism used in other federal-state coercion contexts that courts have found constitutionally suspect (the Spending Clause coercion doctrine from NFIB v. Sebelius). Nearly two dozen state attorneys general filed a December 19, 2025 letter to the FCC urging rejection of the preemptive AI regulations. The DOJ was directed to establish an "AI Litigation Task Force" to challenge state AI laws. This is regulatory capture at the constitutional-design level: using federal funding leverage to disable state democratic experimentation.

**FTC enforcement reversal under industry pressure.** The FTC under the Trump-appointed Chair Ferguson reopened and set aside the Rytr consent order in December 2025, citing its "misalignment" with the White House AI Action Plan — an action that demonstrates the direct pipeline between industry-drafted policy documents and federal enforcement reversals. The Trump AI Action Plan directs the FTC to review all investigations commenced under the previous administration and ensure they do not "unduly burden AI innovation" — which functions as a compliance mandate for the enforcement agency, not a policy suggestion. Senator Warren and others identified in February 2026 that "acqui-hiring" deals — where large AI companies hire away the key talent of potential competitors rather than acquiring them formally — are being used to evade antitrust scrutiny while consolidating market power.

**The revolving door as the structural enabler.** Yale Insights research and CEPR analysis document that the value of the revolving door is in the pre-recruitment period — when regulators anticipate joining industry and adjust their enforcement decisions accordingly. The AI industry's unprecedented pace of hiring from federal AI oversight positions has created a systematic enforcement gap: the most technically sophisticated regulatory staff are cycling into industry, leaving oversight positions filled by generalists who cannot evaluate the technical claims industry makes. This is not a personnel problem. It is a structural design problem that the framework's domain on revolving doors in financial regulation (Domain 38) addresses for that sector but that no domain addresses for AI.

### International Precedent

The EU AI Act provides the most comprehensive international comparison. The prohibitions in the EU AI Act became effective February 2025; AI governance rules for general-purpose AI became effective August 2025. The EU system uses a rights-based, risk-categorized approach: high-risk AI applications (critical infrastructure, education, employment, law enforcement) must meet transparency, explainability, and human oversight requirements before deployment. The Council of Europe's Framework Convention on Artificial Intelligence, Human Rights, Democracy, and the Rule of Law (2024) is the first binding international treaty requiring AI applications to comply with democratic standards.

The EU model is not directly transplantable to the US — different constitutional structure, different agency capacity — but it establishes the comparative benchmark: what does AI governance that is not captured look like? The answer involves structural independence of the oversight body from industry, mandatory algorithmic impact assessments for high-risk deployments, and a judicial enforcement pathway with private rights of action. None of these features exist in the current US regulatory landscape.

Canada's Artificial Intelligence and Data Act (AIDA), currently being finalized, creates mandatory risk assessment requirements for "high-impact AI systems" with penalties enforced by an independent AI Commissioner. The UK's data protection framework (through the Information Commissioner's Office) provides a sector-based enforcement model that has survived multiple government transitions without being captured by any single industry.

### Reform Pathway

The reform architecture operates at three distinct levels, each with different political feasibility timelines:

**Level 1 — Structural independence of AI oversight.** The OECD's framework (adopted by G20) identifies independent oversight bodies as the foundational prerequisite for non-captured AI governance. The reform proposal: create a statutory AI Safety and Accountability Commission with fixed commissioner terms, removal-for-cause protection, and technical staff protected from industry poaching by cooling-off periods of at least three years. The PCLOB (Privacy and Civil Liberties Oversight Board) model provides the statutory architecture.

**Level 2 — Federal preemption floor, not ceiling.** The December 2025 executive order treats state AI regulation as an obstacle to be preempted. The democratic reform inverts this: federal floor standards (minimum transparency and risk assessment requirements) with explicit statutory authority for states to enact stronger protections. The GDPR's interoperability with member-state stronger protections provides the model. Twenty-two state AGs who opposed the preemptive federal framework are the coalition anchor.

**Level 3 — Revolving door structural reform for AI oversight positions.** The Ethics in Government Act's current two-year cooling-off period for senior officials is insufficient for AI regulatory positions where the specific knowledge that makes officials valuable to industry is current-technology-specific. A five-year cooling-off period for AI oversight officials, with criminal penalties for violation, would reduce the pre-recruitment capture incentive documented in the academic literature.

### Adoption Angle

The coalition has two structurally distinct wings that rarely work together: state attorneys general who have already filed legal challenges to the federal AI preemption executive order, and academic AI governance researchers who now have a systematic capture taxonomy (the May 2026 arXiv paper) that gives scholarly credibility to the democratic capture argument. Connecting these two wings through a domain document that synthesizes the legal constitutional challenge (state AGs) with the academic capture analysis (AI governance scholars) creates a unified brief that neither coalition currently possesses.

The frontline advocacy coalition: EFF (data privacy/surveillance angle), CDT (Center for Democracy and Technology, algorithmic accountability), Encode AI (youth-led AI governance advocacy), and the AI Now Institute (democratic accountability frame). The conservative wing: state sovereignty advocates who oppose federal preemption of state regulatory authority; libertarian-right think tanks (Cato) who oppose concentrated corporate power blocking market competition.

## Candidate C: Healthcare Access and the Democracy Nexus

### Core Democratic Design Problem

What goes wrong without this domain: Domain 31 (Medicaid and Healthcare Access Collapse) documents the One Big Beautiful Bill Act's $1 trillion in Medicaid cuts, the 15 million coverage projections, and the rural hospital closure risk. It does not analyze the specific democracy-downstream: healthcare enrollment infrastructure is voter registration infrastructure. Medicaid offices are designated voter registration agencies under the National Voter Registration Act of 1993. When Medicaid enrollment capacity is cut, voter registration access is cut in the same communities. When 15 million people lose Medicaid coverage, they lose their primary government contact point — the place where they were most likely to receive a voter registration offer.

The democratic design problem is structural rather than coincidental. The communities most likely to lose Medicaid coverage (low-income, rural, communities of color) are the same communities that already face the highest voter registration barriers. The NVRA was specifically designed to reach these communities through "motor voter" style automatic registration at government benefit agencies. Gutting the benefit agencies guts the registration infrastructure without passing a single voter suppression law. The overlap is not accidental — it is a democratic design failure with a specific statutory causal chain.

### Current Structural Vulnerability (2026)

The OBBBA, signed into law July 4, 2025, creates the most significant single change to the Medicaid enrollment infrastructure since the program's creation in 1965:

**Scale of the enrollment disruption.** The OBBBA cuts $1 trillion from Medicaid over ten years (CBO estimate). The Center on Budget and Policy Priorities projects 15 million people lose healthcare coverage by 2034, with an additional 4.2 million losing ACA marketplace coverage. The work requirement provisions — with an implementation deadline of January 2027 per the HHS interim final rule issued without notice and comment (effective June 1, 2026) — add a documentation burden that will cause millions of churn exits from Medicaid even among people who are eligible. The Arkansas 2018 natural experiment (the only prior implementation of a Medicaid work requirement) documented 18,000 people losing coverage in six months with no measurable employment effect, before federal courts blocked it. The OBBBA's nationwide version operates without that judicial check at the implementation stage.

**The NVRA-Medicaid enrollment nexus as voter suppression.** The National Voter Registration Act requires that state agencies providing public assistance — including Medicaid enrollment offices — offer voter registration to all eligible applicants. Research from the Institute for Responsive Government documents that each year between 30 and 40 million adult citizens interact with the four largest Section 7 NVRA-covered programs (including Medicaid). Voter registration rates among these populations are up to twenty percentage points lower than among wealthier citizens — meaning NVRA compliance at Medicaid offices is not a marginal voter registration mechanism; it is the primary voter registration pathway for millions of low-income Americans.

When Medicaid enrollment declines by 15 million people, those 15 million people are no longer receiving NVRA voter registration offers at the one government office they regularly interact with. This is passive suppression by infrastructure removal, which is legally invisible but empirically powerful. No voter suppression law needs to be passed. The enrollment cut is the suppression mechanism.

**The Medicaid-reproductive healthcare intersection.** The OBBBA's Medicaid work requirements disproportionately affect women in their prime reproductive years (18-49) — the demographic that also faces the most acute post-Dobbs healthcare access barriers. Medicaid covers approximately 45% of all births in the United States. Cuts to Medicaid enrollment in the 21 states with abortion bans simultaneously remove healthcare coverage and voter registration access from the women most directly impacted by those bans. The intersection of Domain 31 (Medicaid), Domain 39 (Reproductive Rights), and this candidate's democracy nexus argument is the single most concentrated democratic exclusion mechanism in the current policy landscape.

### International Precedent

The comparative evidence on healthcare access as democratic infrastructure comes primarily from countries that have implemented automatic voter registration tied to public benefit systems.

Canada's provincial health card systems function as automatic voter registration vectors in several provinces: British Columbia and Ontario both use health card enrollment as an AVR trigger, producing near-universal voter registration in those provinces. The Canadian Elections Act's universal AVR mandate produces registration rates above 95% for eligible citizens compared to approximately 68% in the United States.

The UK's NHS as universal enrollment infrastructure enables universal voter registration through the Individual Electoral Registration system, which uses NHS enrollment as a verification source. Germany's residency registration system (Einwohnermeldeamt) functions as automatic voter registration — when you register your residence (required by law), you are simultaneously registered to vote. These models demonstrate that the design choice to link public benefit enrollment to voter registration is not technically difficult; it is a political design choice that the US has made in a minimally-enforced direction.

Australia's compulsory voting model provides the complementary comparative frame: when voting is treated as a civic duty rather than a privilege, the system is designed to maximize access rather than minimize fraud. The Australian Electoral Commission proactively registers citizens and provides accessible polling through a health and accessibility lens, not a security lens. The contrast with the US framework — where the burden of registration falls on individuals, and benefit agencies are supposed to help but are chronically under-resourced to do so — is the democratic design problem in its starkest comparative form.

### Reform Pathway

The reform architecture has three tiers with different political feasibility:

**Tier 1 — NVRA enforcement at Medicaid offices during the OBBBA implementation window.** The DOJ's Civil Rights Division has statutory authority to enforce NVRA compliance at state Medicaid agencies. State attorneys general in Democratic-controlled states have both standing and authority to enforce NVRA compliance against state Medicaid agencies in their own states. A coordinated state AG coalition — several are already in the Phase 1 distribution network — can file NVRA enforcement actions against state Medicaid agencies that reduce voter registration offers during OBBBA implementation. This is actionable before the January 2027 work requirement implementation date.

**Tier 2 — Medicaid Automatic Voter Registration.** The Institute for Responsive Government has documented a model for Medicaid AVR that goes beyond the current NVRA opt-out form and instead implements automatic registration when Medicaid eligibility is confirmed, with opt-out rather than opt-in design. California and several other states have implemented variants of this model. Federal legislation requiring AVR at all Medicaid enrollment points would turn the OBBBA's coverage disruption into a voter registration trigger rather than a suppression event — anyone newly enrolled or re-enrolled through the OBBBA churn would be automatically registered.

**Tier 3 — Healthcare access as democratic rights infrastructure.** The long-cycle reform argument: universal healthcare access, by eliminating the income-correlated healthcare access gap, eliminates the most significant structural barrier between low-income communities and political participation. Countries with universal healthcare systems have systematically higher voter turnout — not primarily because healthcare directly enables voting, but because universal healthcare systems eliminate the time and cognitive burden of managing healthcare instability that correlates with lower civic participation. Canada (73% average turnout, 2015-2021 federal elections) versus the United States (55% average, same period) is the summary comparison.

### Adoption Angle

The healthcare advocacy community has historically framed Medicaid cuts in terms of health outcomes — coverage rates, disease burden, mortality. This domain offers them a democratic rights argument that is legally distinct and politically broader. The NVRA enforcement angle gives state AGs a specific litigation theory that does not require winning an abstract democracy argument; it requires only proving that Medicaid agencies in their states are failing to comply with the NVRA's specific voter registration offer requirements.

The Medicaid advocacy community (Protect Our Care, Georgetown CCF, CBPP, KFF) is one degree removed from the Phase 1 distribution network through Domain 31. The voting rights community (Brennan Center Elections Program, Lawyers' Committee for Civil Rights, NAACP LDF) is already in the Phase 1 network. This domain creates the coalition bridge between two communities that are currently running parallel arguments that would be stronger if connected.

The caregiver advocacy angle identified by Brookings (May 2026 analysis: "The Caregiving Crisis and the 2026 Vote") provides an additional electoral framing: OBBBA cuts to Medicaid home and community-based services affect 6 million family caregivers who are disproportionately women of voting age — the highest-turnout demographic in 2022 and 2024 midterm cycles. The caregiving vote is explicitly analyzed by Brookings as a 2026 electoral mobilization opportunity; this domain provides the structural analysis that connects caregiving policy to voter registration infrastructure.
