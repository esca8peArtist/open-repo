# Phase 2 Domain 57: Multilateral Withdrawal and Democratic Norm Erosion

*Resistance Research Agent — May 15, 2026*

---

## Central Thesis

**Title**: Multilateral Withdrawal as Democratic Dismantlement: How Exiting International Institutions Erodes the Rule-Based Architecture That Protects Democratic Systems

When governments withdraw from international accountability institutions — as the United States did with 66 organizations in a single January 2026 executive memorandum, and as Hungary, the Sahel junta states, Russia, and a widening cohort of hybrid-regime governments have done before them — they do not merely retreat from foreign policy commitments. They destroy the accountability infrastructure that constrains authoritarian drift, abandon the norm-enforcement mechanisms that democratic states depend on collectively, and signal to domestic political actors and foreign autocrats alike that rule-based constraint is negotiable.

This domain's central argument is a reframe: international accountability institutions are not primarily foreign policy tools. They are backstops for domestic democratic accountability when domestic institutions fail. When a government exits the UN Human Rights Council, it removes an external reporting and monitoring mechanism that domestic civil society uses as leverage against its own government. When it sanctions ICC judges and prosecutors, it criminalizes the work of American lawyers, journalists, and civil society organizations who cooperate with international accountability bodies. When it withdraws from International IDEA — the institution specifically established to monitor electoral integrity — it removes a benchmarking mechanism that domestic campaign finance and election protection advocates use to hold the government accountable to democratic standards.

The pattern is now global and accelerating. Russia expelled from the Council of Europe in 2022. Hungary initiating ICC withdrawal in April 2025 to shield Netanyahu from arrest. Burkina Faso, Mali, and Niger announcing coordinated ICC withdrawal in September 2025 to protect Wagner mercenaries from accountability. The United States withdrawing from 66 international organizations in January 2026 including the UN Democracy Fund, UNFCCC, IPCC, and International IDEA. Nine EU member states attacking ECHR migration jurisprudence in May 2025. Turkey maintaining 21,600 pending ECHR cases while defying compliance rulings on political prisoners. The pattern across contexts is consistent: multilateral withdrawal is not foreign policy adjustment; it is democratic dismantlement, executed sequentially and globally by regimes that calculate that external accountability is the binding constraint on what they can do at home.

## 1. Architecture of Multilateral Withdrawal — Mapping the Ecosystem

### 1.1 Target Institutions: What Is Being Dismantled

International accountability institutions cluster into five functional categories, each serving different accountability mechanisms that authoritarian and hybrid-regime states most want to eliminate.

**Category A: Criminal Accountability Institutions**

The International Criminal Court (ICC) is the primary target in this category. Established under the Rome Statute (1998), the ICC prosecutes individuals for genocide, war crimes, crimes against humanity, and the crime of aggression. It currently has 125 member states. As of May 2026, six states have completed withdrawal (Burundi 2017, Philippines 2019, and Russia's practical separation from its obligations through expulsion from the Council of Europe 2022), with Hungary, Burkina Faso, Mali, and Niger in active withdrawal processes announced in 2025. The United States under the Trump administration has not formally withdrawn from the Rome Statute (the US never ratified it) but has imposed sanctions on ICC judges and prosecutors that effectively criminalize cooperation with the court.

The withdrawal mechanism at the ICC takes one year from formal notification to the UN Secretary-General. States that withdraw remain bound by obligations incurred during membership, but enforcement becomes practically impossible once a state exits.

**Category B: Human Rights Monitoring and Reporting Bodies**

The Council of Europe and its European Court of Human Rights (ECHR) represent the most developed regional human rights enforcement system in the world. Russia's 2022 withdrawal — the first state expulsion in the Council of Europe's 73-year history — removed 145 million people from ECHR jurisdiction and ended the legal route for Russian nationals to appeal against government abuses. The ECHR continues to receive tens of thousands of applications annually: Turkey alone had 21,600 pending cases as of 2024, representing 35.8% of all pending applications, with 73 judgments issued in 2024 alone finding violations.

At the UN level, China, Russia, and aligned states use procedural obstruction rather than formal withdrawal: blocking UN Human Rights Council sessions, populating Special Procedures mandates with state-friendly experts, and deploying Government-Organized NGOs (GONGOs) that present pro-government testimony in UN review processes while crowding out independent civil society. This procedural capture is more insidious than formal withdrawal because it degrades institutions from within while maintaining the fiction of continued engagement.

The Inter-American Commission on Human Rights (IACHR) faces systematic non-compliance from Venezuela, Nicaragua, and Cuba, which the IACHR identifies as its three most severe crisis cases. Nicaragua expelled IACHR representatives from its territory and has effectively exited the inter-American system's accountability architecture while maintaining formal membership in the OAS. Venezuela has similarly refused compliance with Inter-American Court rulings.

**Category C: Electoral Integrity and Democracy Norm-Setting Bodies**

International IDEA (International Institute for Democracy and Electoral Assistance), the UN Democracy Fund, and the Council of Europe Venice Commission represent the institutional layer that establishes and monitors democratic governance standards. The Trump administration's withdrawal from both International IDEA and the UN Democracy Fund in January 2026 eliminates the two primary international institutions whose explicit mission is to monitor and compare democratic electoral systems globally. These were not peripheral organizations: International IDEA publishes the Global State of Democracy Index, Electoral Integrity Data, and Political Finance database that domestic democracy advocates, journalists, and researchers use to hold governments accountable to international standards.

**Category D: Climate and Scientific Accountability Bodies**

UNFCCC and IPCC withdrawal (both included in the January 2026 US executive memorandum) removes the scientific accountability architecture on which domestic environmental advocates depend for credibility and legal leverage. IPCC assessments underpin domestic environmental impact litigation, permit challenges, and legislative testimony. Removing the US from the IPCC does not eliminate the science — it eliminates the domestic political authority of science produced by an institution the US government formally repudiates.

**Category E: Labor and Development Standards Bodies**

The International Labour Organization (ILO), UNFPA, and World Food Programme bodies withdraw the United States from the norm-setting architecture around labor standards, reproductive health, and food security monitoring. For domestic advocates in these areas, ILO standards function as international legal benchmarks that strengthen domestic litigation and legislative arguments.

### 1.2 Withdrawal Mechanisms: Formal, Procedural, and Financial

Authoritarian and hybrid-regime states have developed four distinct mechanisms for exiting or degrading international accountability institutions. Each mechanism has different legal implications, different timelines, and different vulnerabilities for resistance actors.

**Formal exit** follows treaty withdrawal procedures: notification to the depositary (typically the UN Secretary-General), a one-year waiting period, and formal exit. Hungary's ICC withdrawal (April 2025), the Sahel states' joint ICC withdrawal (September 2025), and Russia's Council of Europe exit (March 2022) all followed formal exit procedures. Formal exit creates the clearest legal situation but also the most concentrated opportunity for resistance: the one-year waiting period is a window for diplomatic, legal, and civil society intervention.

**Defunding and financial strangulation** degrades institutions without triggering the political costs of formal withdrawal. The US under the first Trump administration withheld WHO funding before formal withdrawal, and the second administration has used funding withdrawal as a coercive tool across multiple organizations simultaneously. Defunding is particularly effective against organizations whose operations depend on large-state contributions: the US contributed approximately 22% of the UN regular budget and significant shares of specialized agency budgets. Withdrawal of US funding degrades institution capacity immediately, without waiting periods.

**Non-compliance and procedural obstruction** allows states to maintain formal membership while systematically failing to comply with institutional obligations. Turkey's relationship with the ECHR — 21,600 pending cases, continued defiance of rulings on political prisoners Osman Kavala and Selahattin Demirtaş — is the clearest European example. The Council of Europe has initiated infringement proceedings against Turkey (only the second time in the institution's history), but enforcement capacity is limited. China's UN obstruction strategy — documented comprehensively by Human Rights Watch (2017) and updated through Brookings (2025) and The Diplomat (2025) — uses procedural rules, GONGO infiltration, bloc voting with authoritarian allies, and sovereign-noninterference norm promotion to degrade the UN human rights system from within.

**Sanctions and criminalization** is the mechanism the US has deployed against the ICC: imposing asset freezes and travel bans on ICC judges and prosecutors, and threatening 20-year criminal sentences for US persons who provide material support to ICC investigations. The Coalition for the ICC's "Criminalising Accountability" report (2026), based on 45 interviews, documents the chilling effect: US-based lawyers have stopped working on Afghanistan and Palestine accountability cases; NGOs have limited public engagement; journalists are reconsidering coverage. This mechanism is particularly destructive because it extraterritorially suppresses accountability work beyond US borders, as foreign financial institutions and service providers apply US sanctions to avoid secondary exposure.

### 1.3 Authoritarian Actors: Who Is Withdrawing and Why

The pattern across withdrawal actors reveals a consistent logic: international accountability institutions are targeted when they impose costs on specific conduct that the regime wants to continue and cannot conduct while under external scrutiny.

**Russia** withdrew from the Council of Europe in March 2022 — preemptively, before the Committee of Ministers voted to expel it following the invasion of Ukraine. Russia ceases to be a party to the ECHR on September 16, 2022. The trigger was the invasion, but the Russian government had been systematically non-compliant with ECHR rulings for years preceding the exit. The formal exit allowed the Russian government to claim sovereign agency (withdrawal rather than expulsion) while eliminating the last legal route for Russian nationals to challenge government abuses in an independent court.

**Hungary** announced ICC withdrawal on April 3, 2025, during Benjamin Netanyahu's state visit — precisely timed to signal that Hungary would not honor its ICC arrest warrant obligations. Orbán described the ICC as "a political court," using the same framing that authoritarian actors worldwide deploy to delegitimize accountability institutions. Hungary is the first EU member state to initiate ICC withdrawal. The Verfassungsblog analysis of the move documents its effect on EU credibility as a multilateral actor and raises the question of whether an EU member state's withdrawal from the ICC constitutes an EU Treaty violation.

**Burkina Faso, Mali, and Niger** announced coordinated ICC withdrawal on September 22, 2025 — the same day, from the same Alliance of Sahel States platform — framing the ICC as "an instrument of neo-colonialist repression." The underlying purpose is to insulate the military juntas and their Russian Wagner Group partners from ICC investigation. Each of the three regimes came to power through coups and faces credible allegations of atrocities by their security forces against civilians. As of May 2026, formal withdrawal instruments have not been deposited with the UN Secretary-General, suggesting the announcement was partly political signaling rather than fully executed withdrawal.

**The United States** under the Trump administration has pursued the most extensive multilateral withdrawal in any democratic country's history: 66 organizations in a single executive memorandum on January 7, 2026. The list includes the UN Democracy Fund, International IDEA, UNFPA, IPCC, UNFCCC, WHO (formal exit January 22, 2026), and numerous UN specialized agencies. The ICC sanctions (executive order, February 2025, extended and expanded through 2026) criminalize cooperation with the ICC by US persons and have produced documented chilling effects on civil society accountability work.

**Turkey, Venezuela, Nicaragua, and China** operate through non-compliance and procedural obstruction rather than formal withdrawal, achieving many of the same accountability-insulation effects while maintaining formal membership and the legitimacy claims that come with it.

### 1.4 Timeline and Acceleration Pattern

The pace of multilateral withdrawal events has accelerated significantly since 2016 and sharply since 2022.

- 2016–2017: Philippines, South Africa (reverted), and Burundi withdrew from ICC; Trump first term WHO/Paris Agreement exits
- 2019: Philippines ICC withdrawal completed
- 2022: Russia expelled from Council of Europe; Ukraine war triggers accountability escalation
- 2024: UK Rwanda Act defies ECHR interim measures; nine EU states attack ECHR migration jurisprudence
- 2025: Hungary ICC withdrawal (April); ICC "Criminalising Accountability" report (US sanctions); Sahel joint ICC withdrawal (September); Freedom House documents 20th consecutive year of global freedom decline
- 2026: US 66-organization withdrawal (January); WHO formal exit (January 22); ICC sanctions extended; Coalition for ICC "Criminalising Accountability" full report published; V-Dem documents US democratic decline as "unprecedented" among democracies

The accelerating timeline reflects a self-reinforcing dynamic: each successful withdrawal signals to other authoritarian actors that the political costs are manageable, reducing the deterrent effect and enabling the next withdrawal cycle. The US withdrawal from 66 organizations in January 2026 is the largest single withdrawal event in international institutional history and signals to every authoritarian government globally that the world's most powerful democracy has exited the accountability architecture.

## 2. Causal Pathways — How Multilateral Withdrawal Enables Suppression

### 2.1 Accountability Infrastructure Removal

International institutions constrain executive power through three mechanisms: treaty obligations that must be honored or formally violated (creating political cost), reporting requirements that generate legible records of policy performance, and peer monitoring from allied democracies that raises the reputational cost of norm defection. Withdrawal removes each of these mechanisms simultaneously.

The domestic democratic consequence is the mechanism this domain exists to document: withdrawal removes international benchmarking data that civil society uses to hold the government accountable; it eliminates treaty compliance obligations that courts and Congress can enforce; and it severs the external reputational pressure that deters the most extreme norm violations. International IDEA's electoral integrity database, OECD democracy metrics, WHO health system comparisons, and IPCC climate assessments generate legible comparative data that civil society, journalists, and opposition politicians use to hold governments accountable domestically. When a government exits the institutions that produce this data, it removes the data from domestic accountability conversations.

The ICC sanctions mechanism illustrates the most acute version of this dynamic: any US person who provides "material support" to ICC investigations faces up to 20 years in federal prison. This makes it legally dangerous for American lawyers, journalists, and civil society organizations to cooperate with ICC investigations — including any investigations involving conduct connected to US territory, US persons, or US-backed operations. The Coalition for the ICC's 2026 report documents that NGOs have stopped working on Afghanistan and Palestine accountability cases, legal professionals have withdrawn from sensitive cases, and journalists are reconsidering coverage of ICC proceedings. The accountability suppression extends domestically: US civil society organizations that would use international accountability mechanisms as leverage in domestic advocacy are legally deterred from doing so.

### 2.2 Domestic Crackdown Enablement

Multilateral withdrawal correlates strongly with escalation of domestic repression: the removal of external scrutiny creates political space for intensified suppression of civil society, independent media, and political opposition. The causal direction runs both ways: authoritarian governments withdraw from accountability institutions in anticipation of conduct they expect international bodies to scrutinize, and the removal of scrutiny enables conduct they would not have undertaken while under observation.

Russia's post-ECHR expulsion domestic trajectory illustrates the mechanism: the months following formal departure from ECHR jurisdiction saw escalation of political prosecutions, independent media closures, and suppression of anti-war civil society organizations whose members could no longer access the ECHR as a route for legal challenge. The Council of Europe's monitoring function was one of the few remaining external pressure points on Russian domestic repression; its removal correlated with acceleration of repression.

Hungary's domestic trajectory after Orbán consolidated power — which included media capture, civil society law modeled on Russia's "foreign agent" legislation, judicial independence erosion, and gerrymandering — preceded rather than followed ICC withdrawal. But the ICC withdrawal announcement came at the moment Orbán was escalating these patterns: it signals that external accountability at the criminal level is now explicitly rejected.

### 2.3 Authoritarian Base Signaling

Multilateral withdrawal functions as a domestic political signal: it communicates to the authoritarian base that the government has severed the constraints imposed by international institutions associated with liberal democratic values, human rights, and global governance. Orbán's framing — the ICC as a "political court" engaged in "politically motivated indictments" — is the same framing used by the Trump administration, by the Sahel junta governments, and by Russian foreign ministry statements. The convergence of framing across geographically and politically disparate actors suggests a coordinated authoritarian communication playbook in which "sovereignty" and "political bias" accusations serve as legitimizing narratives for accountability avoidance.

This domestic signaling function has been documented in the democratic backsliding literature: V-Dem's 2026 Democracy Report identifies "anti-multilateralism rhetoric" as a systematic component of autocratization strategies across its dataset of backsliding states. The rhetoric serves to delegitimize international accountability institutions in domestic public opinion before or alongside formal withdrawal — reducing the domestic political cost of exit.

### 2.4 Norm Erosion and the Domino Dynamic

Each withdrawal event erodes the normative architecture that makes international institutions effective deterrents. The deterrent effect of international accountability institutions — the threat of ICC prosecution, ECHR adverse judgment, or UN Human Rights Council criticism — depends on the credibility of the institution and the legitimacy it commands among states. When powerful states (the US) or bloc leaders (Hungary within the EU) exit or sanction institutions, the credibility and deterrent effect diminish for all remaining members.

Freedom House documents 20 consecutive years of global freedom decline (2025 report), with democratic backsliding reaching Western democracies at unprecedented rates. V-Dem's 2026 report documents the US as declining at the fastest rate of any democracy in modern comparative data. The correlation between US multilateral disengagement and accelerating global democratic decline is not fully established causally — but the mechanism is clear: US participation in international accountability institutions was a primary enforcement mechanism, and its removal weakens the enforcement architecture for all states in the system.

### 2.5 The Constitutional Asymmetry: Entry Requires Senate, Exit Does Not

The withdrawal mechanism is itself a democratic design problem. The US Constitution's treaty-making clause (Article II, Section 2) requires Senate consent to ratify treaties — a 2/3 supermajority threshold that reflects the Founders' judgment that international commitments should have broad democratic legitimacy. But the Constitution is silent on treaty withdrawal. Executive branch attorneys have argued for plenary presidential withdrawal authority, and the Supreme Court has historically treated the withdrawal power question as a political question (non-justiciable).

The 2024 NDAA (Section 1250A) enacted the first-ever statutory constraint on presidential withdrawal from a specific treaty — NATO — requiring Senate consent or an act of Congress. The CRS report R48868 (February 27, 2026) provides the current best analysis of "Separation of Powers and NATO Withdrawal." But this statutory protection covers only NATO. For all 66 organizations withdrawn from in January 2026, no equivalent protection exists. The constitutional asymmetry — Senate ratification required to enter, no Senate approval required to exit — creates a structural democratic accountability gap that allows a single president to undo multilateral commitments that required supermajority Senate approval to create.

## 3. Movement Landscape — Resistance Actors and Strategies

### 3.1 International Accountability Organizations

The organizations with the strongest standing and operational capacity to resist multilateral withdrawal are those whose institutional mission depends on the accountability systems being dismantled.

**Human Rights Watch** maintains dedicated documentation programs on ICC attacks, ECHR defiance, and UN Human Rights Council obstruction. HRW's reporting on Hungary's ICC withdrawal (June 16, 2025), the Sahel states' ICC withdrawal (September 24, 2025), and US sanctions against ICC judges (March 2026) is the most comprehensive civil society documentation available. HRW's annual World Report 2026 frames multilateral withdrawal as a component of the global "downward spiral" in democracy.

**Amnesty International** documented the impact of Russia's ECHR exit on human rights defenders, characterized the Sahel ICC withdrawal as "a serious backwards step in the fight against impunity," and condemned the US ICC sanctions. Amnesty's annual State of the World's Human Rights report (April 2025) provides the normative framing for multilateral withdrawal as a coordinated strategy against accountability.

**Coalition for the International Criminal Court (CICC)** is the primary civil society coordinator for ICC-specific resistance, maintaining a network of 2,500+ organizations in 150 countries. The CICC's "Criminalising Accountability" report (2026), based on 45 interviews documenting the US sanctions' chilling effects, is the most important advocacy document currently in circulation on the ICC sanctions mechanism. The CICC also coordinates the "Sanctions Cannot Silence Justice" civil society coalition statements.

**FIDH (International Federation for Human Rights)** coordinates the Universal Jurisdiction Annual Review (published jointly with TRIAL International, REDRESS, ECCHR, and Civitas Maxima), documenting 34 new universal jurisdiction cases opened in 2025 and 23 convictions secured — demonstrating that universal jurisdiction courts in European democracies are the most active resistance mechanism when the ICC is under attack.

**TRIAL International**, **ECCHR (European Center for Constitutional and Human Rights)**, **REDRESS**, and **Civitas Maxima** constitute the primary universal jurisdiction litigation network — the civil society organizations that bring accountability cases in European national courts when ICC access is blocked or degraded.

**International Justice Mission (IJM)** operates in the intersection of faith community advocacy and international accountability, providing a constituency bridge to religious organizations that are underrepresented in multilateral withdrawal resistance coalitions.

### 3.2 UN Mechanisms and Special Procedures

UN Special Rapporteurs and the OHCHR remain active accountability actors despite US withdrawal from the UN Human Rights Council. The OHCHR press release demanding withdrawal of US sanctions against ICC judges and prosecutors (January 2026) demonstrates that UN mechanisms can generate normative pressure independent of US participation. The UN Special Rapporteur on the independence of judges and lawyers has made ICC sanctions a priority documentation area.

The UN Human Rights Council's Universal Periodic Review (UPR) mechanism continues to operate as a peer-review accountability system, though its effectiveness is degraded by great-power bloc voting and procedural obstruction. The UPR provides one of the few remaining systematic external review mechanisms for states that have exited or degraded other accountability institutions.

### 3.3 Regional Courts and Bodies

The ECHR remains the world's most effective regional human rights court, with binding jurisdiction over 46 Council of Europe member states and an enforcement record significantly stronger than other regional systems. The nine EU member states' attack on ECHR migration jurisprudence (May 2025) represents the primary current threat to ECHR authority among democratic states.

The Inter-American Court on Human Rights (IACHR) maintains active monitoring mechanisms for Venezuela, Nicaragua, and Cuba, including 84 active cases before the Court as of the 2025 annual report. The IACHR's monitoring function is the primary remaining external accountability mechanism in the region given OAS structural limitations.

The African Court on Human and Peoples' Rights has limited enforcement capacity but maintains symbolic importance as the primary continental accountability mechanism. The Sahel ICC withdrawals create an urgent need for strengthened African Court capacity and for accountability mechanisms that do not depend on ICC cooperation.

### 3.4 Academic and Legal Networks

**Verfassungsblog** publishes the highest-quality legal analysis of multilateral withdrawal constitutional questions, providing the intellectual infrastructure for legal challenges to withdrawal authority. The blog's coverage of Hungary's ICC withdrawal, US withdrawal from international entities, and the constitutional questions surrounding treaty withdrawal power is the primary public-facing legal analysis resource.

**American Society of International Law (ASIL)** tracks ICC withdrawal developments and publishes International Law in Brief analyses that provide legal framing for advocacy organizations.

**Harvard Law School**, **Columbia Law School's Human Rights Institute**, and **Yale Law School's Lowenstein International Human Rights Clinic** provide the legal scholarship and clinical capacity for strategic litigation challenging withdrawal authority and its domestic democratic consequences.

### 3.5 Congressional Entry Points

**Senate Foreign Relations Committee** has jurisdiction over treaty ratification and international organization participation. Ranking members have been actively seeking constitutional frameworks for constraining presidential withdrawal authority. The CRS report R48868 (February 2026) was commissioned by Senate Foreign Relations Committee members.

**Senate Appropriations Committee (International Affairs)** controls funding for international organizations and can use appropriations conditions to constrain defunding-as-withdrawal.

**House Foreign Affairs Committee** has jurisdiction over international organization participation legislation.

**Coalition opportunities**: The Atlantic Council's congressional networks, the Senate International Human Rights Caucus, and the House Democracy Partnership provide existing infrastructure for multilateral accountability advocacy.

## 4. Policy Leverage Windows — When Can Democracies Intervene?

### 4.1 Trade and Economic Sanctions (Linked to Institutional Engagement)

The EU's Article 7 mechanism — the procedure for suspending EU membership rights for states that violate foundational EU values — is the most powerful formal leverage mechanism for Hungary's ICC withdrawal. Hungary's ICC withdrawal raises the question of whether exiting the ICC constitutes a violation of EU Treaty Article 2 (which includes respect for the rule of law and human rights) sufficient to trigger Article 7 proceedings. EU sanctions conditioned on reversal of ICC withdrawal would represent a new use of trade and membership leverage to defend international accountability institutions.

The EU's General Scheme of Preferences (GSP) and trade conditionality mechanisms create leverage over developing-country ICC withdrawal: states seeking EU trade preferences must maintain human rights standards, which includes treaty compliance obligations.

### 4.2 Conditional Aid and Development Finance

The US historically used foreign assistance conditionality to incentivize institutional compliance. The Biden administration's partial restoration of this conditionality framework was reversed by the January 2026 USAID dissolution and foreign assistance freeze. European bilateral donors (Germany, France, UK, Nordic states) maintain human rights conditionality frameworks that create leverage over Sahel state institutional behavior, though the Sahel juntas' pivot to Russia dramatically reduces Western leverage.

The Millennium Challenge Corporation (MCC) requires democratic governance and rule of law indicators for compact eligibility — including maintenance of treaty obligations. MCC conditionality is one of the remaining US government leverage tools that links development finance to international institutional compliance.

### 4.3 Diplomatic Pressure and Alliance Leverage

The UNGA High-Level Week (September 22–28, 2026) is the primary diplomatic pressure window — the moment when heads of state and foreign ministers are in New York and international institutional accountability is most directly on the agenda. Distribution of Domain 57 research in the six weeks before UNGA (by August 10) maximizes relevance during the annual foreign policy attention peak.

The G7 framework (next summit: 2026) provides a venue for coordinated democratic state responses to multilateral withdrawal, including coordinated diplomatic pressure and support for institutional reform.

NATO's existing Article 5 protection statute (2024 NDAA Section 1250A) provides a legislative model for extending statutory protections to other treaty commitments. Congressional advocacy using this model — the Treaty Withdrawal Notification Act — is the primary domestic legislative leverage window.

### 4.4 Counter-Defunding: Supporting International Bodies Financially

The vacuum left by US defunding creates an opportunity for allied democratic states (EU member states, Canada, Australia, Japan, South Korea) to increase their contributions to targeted institutions, particularly International IDEA, the UN Democracy Fund, and the ICC Trust Fund for Victims. The EU has already committed to increased multilateral institutional support in response to US withdrawal. This counter-defunding strategy is the most immediately actionable resistance mechanism for governments of allied democracies.

Civil society fundraising in support of the ICC Trust Fund for Victims and the CICC operational capacity is the parallel civil society version of this mechanism.

### 4.5 Domestic Prosecution: Universal Jurisdiction as Accountability Backstop

Universal jurisdiction is the most operationally active resistance mechanism currently functioning at scale. The Universal Jurisdiction Annual Review 2025 documents 34 new cases opened or made public and 23 convictions secured — demonstrating that European national courts are actively using universal jurisdiction to hold perpetrators accountable when the ICC is under attack or unavailable.

The civil society organizations driving universal jurisdiction cases (ECCHR, TRIAL International, REDRESS, Civitas Maxima, FIDH) have documented that universal jurisdiction courts are explicitly processing the accountability gap created by ICC attacks. Germany, France, Sweden, the Netherlands, Switzerland, and Belgium maintain active universal jurisdiction prosecution programs. Spain's Audiencia Nacional and UK courts have significant universal jurisdiction precedent. This decentralized accountability infrastructure is more resilient to any single state's withdrawal from the ICC than the centralized ICC mechanism itself.

## 5. Success Criteria — How Would Resistance Win?

### 5.1 Institutional Survival and Credibility Maintenance

The primary near-term success criterion is that the ICC, ECHR, IACHR, and regional human rights bodies survive the current wave of attacks with their authority and credibility substantially intact. This requires:

- ICC membership held at or above 125 states (formal withdrawal net of new accessions)
- ECHR enforcement mechanism maintained against Turkey's defiance through infringement proceedings
- ICC Prosecutor's Office continuing to operate despite US sanctions (which it has, as of May 2026)
- Universal jurisdiction courts filling accountability gaps created by formal withdrawal events
- Civil society maintaining operational capacity despite chilling effects from US ICC sanctions

### 5.2 Legislative Wins: Constraining Presidential Withdrawal Authority

The medium-term US-specific success criterion is enactment of statutory constraints on presidential withdrawal authority:

- Treaty Withdrawal Notification Act: 90-day congressional notification requirement before withdrawal from Senate-ratified treaties
- Multilateral Commitment Protection Act: extending the 2024 NDAA NATO protection model to a broader category of multilateral accountability institutions
- ICC Sanctions Repeal: legislative reversal of the sanctions executive order that restores US civil society's capacity to cooperate with the ICC

The 2024 NDAA NATO provision establishes that Congress can constrain presidential withdrawal authority when it chooses to act. The advocacy task is extending that precedent.

### 5.3 Countries Re-engage or Comply with Institutions

The longer-term success criterion is reversibility: states that have withdrawn return to membership, states that are non-compliant are brought into compliance, and states that are defunding restore contributions.

The historical record is partially encouraging: the Philippines withdrew from the ICC in 2019 under Duterte and re-ratified under Marcos in 2023. The US withdrew from WHO and UNESCO in the first Trump term and re-joined under Biden. Withdrawal is not necessarily permanent — but it requires regime change or significant political pressure to reverse.

### 5.4 Institutional Reform: Stronger Enforcement and Broader Membership

The structural success criterion is that international accountability institutions emerge from this attack wave with stronger enforcement mechanisms, broader membership (including states currently outside the ICC), and more resilient financing structures. The ICC's Strategic Plan 2026–2029 (published December 2025) addresses some of these structural questions. The EU's accelerated multilateral institutional support in response to US withdrawal creates a real opportunity for increased European financial and diplomatic backing.

## 6. Cross-Domain Bridges

Domain 57 occupies a structural position in the framework that no existing domain (1–56) fills: it analyzes international accountability institutions as domestic democratic infrastructure rather than foreign policy. The cross-domain connections are therefore primarily as amplifiers and context-setters rather than duplications.

**Domain 1 (Voting Rights and Elections)**: International IDEA's electoral integrity monitoring provides the international standard against which US election administration is benchmarked. Withdrawal from International IDEA removes this benchmarking tool from domestic election protection advocates. The Voter Registration Accuracy program and Global State of Democracy data are specific analytical products that US voting rights organizations (Brennan Center, Demos, Common Cause) use in litigation and advocacy. Domain 57 provides the international architecture context for Domain 1's domestic suppression analysis.

**Domain 16 (Judicial Capture)**: Domestic judicial capture — the politicization of courts documented across Hungary, Turkey, Poland, Russia, Venezuela, and Nicaragua — is both a cause and a consequence of multilateral withdrawal. Regimes capture courts to insulate domestic accountability; they withdraw from international courts because captured domestic courts provide no resistance. Domain 16's analysis of judicial independence erosion connects to Domain 57 as the domestic institutional mirror of international institution withdrawal.

**Domain 23 (Trade Policy / Tariff Unilateralism)**: WTO dispute resolution mechanism non-compliance and the threatened WTO withdrawal share the same mechanism as ICC and UNHRC withdrawal: unilateral executive exit from rule-based constraint. Domain 23 covers the trade dimension; Domain 57 covers the human rights and democratic accountability dimension.
