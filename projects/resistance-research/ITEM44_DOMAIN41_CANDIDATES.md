# ITEM44: Domain 41 Candidates — Framework Gap Analysis and Research Roadmap

*Completed May 5, 2026. Five candidate topics evaluated for post-Domain-40 framework expansion. Each candidate fills a structural gap in the existing 40-domain architecture. Candidates are ranked using the standard four-criterion scoring matrix (urgency, coalition strength, research complexity, policy window timing and institutional leverage). Top two candidates receive full research roadmaps.*

---

## Part I: Executive Summary

### What the 40-Domain Framework Now Covers — and Where Gaps Remain

Domains 37 through 40 represent the most recent phase of framework expansion. Domain 37 (Foreign and Transnational Interference) documented the mechanisms of foreign influence on US democratic institutions and the collapse of US democracy-promotion infrastructure abroad. Domain 38 (Financial Sector Independence) analyzed the regulatory capture of the Federal Reserve, FDIC, SEC, and CFPB, and the democratic consequences of financialization. Domain 39 (Reproductive Rights and Labor Rights) covered post-Dobbs enforcement architecture and the union density–democratic participation nexus. Domain 40 (Constitutional Architecture and Tribal Sovereignty) addressed Article V amendment process vulnerabilities and Indigenous nation-to-nation democratic design.

After these four additions, the 40-domain framework is comprehensive across: electoral architecture, institutional integrity, judicial independence, economic policy, information governance, rights and liberties, labor and reproductive autonomy, financial regulation, constitutional design, and Indigenous sovereignty. Three genuine structural gaps remain that no single existing domain addresses as a coherent democratic-design problem.

**Gap 1: Domestic Intelligence Accountability.** The framework documents prosecutorial weaponization (Domain 29), foreign surveillance (Domain 37), and AI governance of government data (Domain 36), but has no domain analyzing the domestic intelligence apparatus — fusion centers, FBI counterintelligence, surveillance of activists — as a coherent accountability challenge. The Patel-era FBI's documented shift toward political targeting makes this gap an active democratic crisis in 2026, not a theoretical one.

**Gap 2: Disability Rights, Benefit Infrastructure, and Civic Exclusion.** No domain in the framework addresses disabled Americans as a distinct democratic participation category. The SAVE Act's documented 20% photo-ID gap for disabled voters, the SSA's 7,000-person staff cut creating benefit access barriers for 16 million people, and the ADA's contested application to polling places represent a coherent structural gap at the intersection of civil rights and democratic architecture.

**Gap 3: Campaign Finance and Dark Money Architecture.** The framework references dark money in Domain 7 (Rights and Civil Liberties) but has no dedicated structural analysis of money-in-politics as a democratic design problem — one that has sharpened considerably since *Citizens United* and the FEC's effective enforcement collapse.

### Five Candidates Identified

1. **Domestic Intelligence and Law Enforcement Accountability** — The FBI's structural capture under Patel, fusion center surveillance of activists, and the absence of a post-Church-Committee accountability framework for the 2026 threat landscape.
2. **Disability Rights, Access Infrastructure, and Civic Participation** — The convergence of SAVE Act photo-ID barriers, SSA benefit infrastructure collapse, and ADA polling-place enforcement failures as a coherent democratic exclusion architecture.
3. **Campaign Finance, Dark Money, and Electoral Corruption** — The $2 billion dark money spending in 2024, FEC enforcement collapse, and the democracy–plutocracy capture problem as a structural domain gap.
4. **Energy Democracy and Cooperative Infrastructure** — Distributed grid ownership, rural electric cooperative governance, and community-controlled broadband as civic participation infrastructure.
5. **International Human Rights Architecture and US Re-engagement** — The US withdrawal from treaty obligations, ICJ posture, and the accountability framework for rebuilding democratic legitimacy in international institutions.

### Top 2 Candidates Recommended for Phase 2 Planning

**Candidate 1 (Domestic Intelligence Accountability)** and **Candidate 2 (Disability Rights and Civic Participation)** are recommended as the highest-priority Domain 41 research targets. Domestic Intelligence fills the most acute operational gap — an active democratic crisis with a specific 2026 legislative window and a cross-partisan coalition that cannot be served by any existing domain. Disability Rights fills a long-standing structural gap that the SAVE Act's Senate consideration and the SSA's 2025-2026 staffing collapse have made newly time-sensitive.

## Part II: Gap Analysis

### Review of the 40-Domain Framework

The framework as of May 2026 spans ten thematic areas:

**Electoral and Participatory Architecture** (Domains 1, 3, 37): Voting rights, redistricting, RCV/proportional representation, federal executive interference in 2026 midterms, elections infrastructure, foreign electoral interference.

**Institutional Integrity and Oversight** (Domains 2, 26, 29, 34): Civil service and administrative state, anti-corruption architecture, prosecutorial weaponization and DOJ capture, congressional fiscal authority.

**Judicial System** (Domains 6, 35): Judicial independence, SCOTUS structure, Loper Bright, *Humphrey's Executor* collapse, OT2026 SCOTUS term preview.

**Rights and Civil Liberties** (Domains 7, 14, 16, 21, 22, 39): Press freedom, assembly, criminal justice and policing, immigration, data privacy, reparations and racial justice, reproductive rights.

**Economic Architecture** (Domains 4, 5, 17, 18, 20, 23, 38): Digital government, fiscal and tax reform, labor and employment, social safety net, antitrust, trade policy, financial sector independence.

**Policy Domains** (Domains 10-15, 31): Education, healthcare, infrastructure, housing, environment, healthcare/Medicaid crisis.

**Information and Technology Governance** (Domains 8, 27, 36): Media ecosystem, higher education and academic freedom, AI governance.

**Comparative and Structural** (Domains 9, 13, 19, 28, 33, 40): Federalism, constitutional amendments, national security and foreign policy, war powers, state legislative autocratization, Article V and tribal sovereignty.

**Financial Sector** (Domain 38): Central bank independence, FDIC and SEC regulatory capture, CFPB dismantlement.

**Labor and Reproductive Rights** (Domain 39): Labor organizing infrastructure, union democracy, reproductive enforcement architecture.

### Identified Structural Gaps

**Gap 1: Domestic Intelligence and Law Enforcement Accountability**

Domain 29 (Prosecutorial Weaponization) documents retaliatory prosecution — the use of the criminal charging power to punish political opponents and suppress dissent. Domain 37 (Foreign Interference) addresses counterintelligence failures and CISA's destruction. Domain 7 (Rights and Civil Liberties) covers First Amendment suppression. But no domain addresses the domestic intelligence apparatus itself — the FBI's counterintelligence authorities, the 80 fusion centers networked across all 50 states, the FBI's Domestic Terrorism Operations Section (DTOS), and the accumulated warrantless surveillance infrastructure — as a coherent accountability problem.

This gap is not theoretical in 2026. FBI Director Kash Patel has reassigned staff from DTOS (eliminating the section that was supposed to monitor domestic violent extremism) while simultaneously opening investigations of immigration protest organizers in Minnesota. Three former senior FBI officials are suing Patel alleging that terminations were directed by the White House based on which agents had worked on Trump criminal investigations. The Nation documented in September 2025 that "a half century of failure to reform the FBI has gifted Patel with alarming power" — the post-Church-Committee reforms (FISA courts, Attorney General Guidelines on domestic investigation, congressional oversight) have been systematically undermined. This is a democratic accountability crisis without a domain.

**Gap 2: Disability Rights, Benefit Infrastructure, and Civic Exclusion**

Domains 22 (Racial Justice) and 7 (Rights and Civil Liberties) address civil rights enforcement as a general matter but have no structural analysis of disability as a distinct democratic participation category. This matters empirically: disabled Americans constitute 26% of the adult US population and vote at measurable deficits compared to non-disabled Americans; 11% of disabled voters faced polling-place barriers in 2020 compared to 6% of non-disabled voters. The convergence of three 2026 crises makes this gap newly time-sensitive: (1) the SAVE Act's photo-ID requirements, which Democracy Docket documents fall disproportionately on disabled voters (20% of disabled Americans lack driver's licenses, compared to 6% of non-disabled); (2) the SSA's 7,000-person staff cut, creating benefit access barriers for 16 million SSDI/SSI recipients whose benefit access intersects with voter registration sites under the NVRA; and (3) ADA enforcement collapse at polling places under the current administration.

**Gap 3: Campaign Finance and Dark Money Architecture**

Domain 7 references Citizens United and dark money as contributing factors to the disinformation and civil liberties crisis but has no standalone structural analysis of money-in-politics as a democratic design problem. This gap has widened significantly: dark money groups spent $2 billion on the 2024 election (double the 2020 total); four super PACs controlling House and Senate elections raised $71 million from allied dark money groups in 2025 alone; the FEC's enforcement operation is functionally paralyzed by its deadlock structure. The $11 billion 2026 midterm advertising forecast represents the most monetized midterm election in US history, coinciding with the administration's documented pattern of rewarding political donors with regulatory forbearance and government contracts. This is the plutocracy-capture problem in its most acute form — and the framework has no dedicated domain to address it.

### Candidate 1: Domestic Intelligence and Law Enforcement Accountability

**Candidate Name and Focus**: The structural mechanisms through which the FBI, DHS fusion centers, and law enforcement intelligence networks surveil, disrupt, and deter political activity — and the legislative, judicial, and administrative framework needed to restore democratic accountability over the domestic intelligence apparatus.

**Urgency Assessment**

The urgency is acute and multi-dimensional. FBI Director Kash Patel has restructured the bureau's counterterrorism priorities in ways that both reduce capacity to monitor actual violent extremism (disbanding DTOS operational support for field offices) and increase capacity for politically motivated investigations of civil society organizations. The March 2025 coalition letter from civil liberties organizations to Patel documented the structural shift; by September 2025, the Government Executive was reporting that "Trump's reported use of the FBI against political opponents reflects earlier controversies from the J. Edgar Hoover era."

The 2026 policy window is specific: the Senate Judiciary Committee has held FBI oversight hearings; Rep. Raskin, Rep. Nadler, and Rep. Lofgren sent a September 2025 letter to Attorney General Bondi and Director Patel documenting specific political targeting concerns. The ACLU's active FOIA litigation against federal agencies for fusion center surveillance records provides a litigation timeline that intersects with the 2026 midterm period. Congress has both the institutional mandate and the recent example (the Church Committee was created after documented COINTELPRO abuses) to create a select committee investigation if the political will exists after potential midterm gains.

Crisis indicators as of May 2026: (1) Patel has fired senior FBI officials based on White House directives tied to prior Trump investigations; (2) FBI investigations of immigration protest organizers in Minnesota framed as "investigating funding networks" for "violent rioters"; (3) the Foreign Influence Task Force (FITF) that monitored foreign disinformation was disbanded in 2025; (4) CISA's election security division has been functionally eliminated; (5) the FBI's Domestic Terrorism Operations Section is being reorganized away from white nationalist and domestic violence threat tracking toward political left monitoring.

**Coalition Strength**

The coalition for domestic intelligence accountability is genuinely unusual: it spans the civil libertarian left (ACLU, EFF, Brennan Center), the civil libertarian right (CATO Institute, Reason Foundation, libertarian-aligned Republican members like Rand Paul who have historically opposed FBI surveillance expansion), and an institutional center (the Church Committee tradition, former FBI officials who have spoken out, legal scholars at Harvard, Yale, and Georgetown focused on the Fourth Amendment and executive power). This cross-partisan structure is rare in the current environment and represents a high-leverage institutional opportunity.

The Brennan Center's longstanding "Ending Fusion Center Abuses" policy project provides the most developed reform architecture. The ACLU has active litigation and model legislation. The Nation's Frank Church Institute maintains the historical and normative framework. Congressional co-sponsorship potential exists among Democratic Judiciary members and libertarian Republicans.

**Research Complexity**: 12-15 hours. Primary sources are accessible: Church Committee final reports (public domain), Attorney General Guidelines on domestic investigation (DOJ public documents), Brennan Center fusion center reform analysis, ACLU FOIA litigation records, Raskin/Nadler/Lofgren letter, DTOS restructuring reporting from Government Executive and NPR. Constitutional law analysis (Fourth Amendment, First Amendment, executive privilege in domestic investigation) requires engagement with specialized legal literature but is well-documented. The comparative dimension (Germany's BfV domestic intelligence model with parliamentary oversight; Canada's NSIRA independent review; UK's ISC oversight structure) requires dedicated hours but sources are accessible.

**2026-2028 Policy Windows**

- Senate Judiciary Committee FBI oversight hearings (ongoing; next major hearing likely post-midterm 2026)
- ACLU fusion center FOIA litigation (active; documents may be produced in 2026-2027 window)
- 2026 midterm elections (if Democrats gain Senate seats, investigative committee formation becomes plausible in 2027)
- Post-midterm congressional reorganization window: a select committee analogous to the Church Committee is the historical precedent; the organizing argument would be available if midterm investigations of FBI political targeting are substantiated by additional disclosure
- FY2027 and FY2028 appropriations battles over FBI and DHS fusion center funding (specific advocacy leverage point: zero-base appropriations for DTOS restructuring)

**Institutional Adoption Patterns**

Civil liberties law clinics (Harvard, Yale, Georgetown, Stanford, the Rutgers Constitutional Litigation Clinic) provide the academic-institutional pipeline. The ACLU's state affiliates are natural distribution nodes for the fusion center accountability analysis. Congressional Research Service will produce reports if a select committee is formed; this domain document would provide the analytical framework that CRS reports would then build on. State attorney general offices in blue states (Massachusetts, California, New York) have existing domestic intelligence audit authority and the domain could directly inform state-level fusion center review.

**Competitive Landscape**

The Brennan Center has the most developed existing institutional research. The ACLU has litigation-focused analysis. The Electronic Frontier Foundation focuses on digital surveillance within the broader domestic intelligence apparatus. Academic journals (Stanford Law Review, Harvard Law Review, Yale Law Journal) have published constitutional analysis of FBI investigative authorities. What does not exist is a unified, accessible democratic-design document that connects the structural history (Church Committee reforms), the 2025-2026 crisis (Patel restructuring, political targeting evidence), and the reform architecture — which is exactly what a domain document would provide.

### Candidate 2: Disability Rights, Benefit Infrastructure, and Civic Participation

**Candidate Name and Focus**: The convergence of voting accessibility failures, Social Security benefit access collapse, and ADA enforcement dismantlement that systematically excludes 26% of the US adult population from democratic participation — and the structural reform architecture needed to make civic inclusion legally enforceable.

**Urgency Assessment**

This candidate has a specific and time-bounded legislative urgency tied to three converging crises in 2026:

First, the SAVE Act (H.R.22, 119th Congress) passed the House in February 2026 with provisions that Democracy Docket and The Arc document as creating disproportionate barriers for disabled voters. The photo-ID requirement falls hardest on a population that is 20% more likely to lack a driver's license — the primary qualifying credential — than non-disabled voters. The Senate filibuster threshold (60 votes) means floor consideration likely in 2026, creating an advocacy window during Senate debate. If the bill passes with disability-harmful provisions, the ADA litigation angle opens immediately.

Second, the SSA's 7,000-person staff reduction — the largest single cut in SSA history — has created documented access barriers for the 16 million Americans relying on SSDI and SSI. The National Voter Registration Act requires that state agencies providing public assistance, including Social Security benefit enrollment offices, offer voter registration. The SSA's office closures and appointment delays have a documented secondary effect: reduced voter registration opportunities in the specific low-income, high-disability communities that depend on SSA as their primary government contact point.

Third, the Trump administration dropped a proposed regulation in November 2025 that would have cut SSDI eligibility by up to 20% — a major advocacy win that documents the effective pressure point. The proposed rule's withdrawal demonstrates that organized disability advocacy with a clear constitutional and statutory grounding can produce policy outcomes.

**Coalition Strength**

The disability rights coalition is institutionally dense, long-established, and politically cross-ideological. The Arc (intellectual and developmental disabilities), National Federation of the Blind, United Cerebral Palsy, National Council on Disability, the American Association of People with Disabilities (AAPD), and Disability Rights Advocates constitute a permanent Washington advocacy infrastructure with state chapters. The National Disability Rights Network (NDRN) has explicitly called on Congress to oppose the SAVE Act on disability grounds.

Cross-partisan potential is genuine: Republican disability rights advocates (including veterans' service organizations focused on service-connected disability) have historically co-sponsored ADA enforcement legislation. The disability community votes in numbers that matter — 35 million disabled adults voted in 2020, representing 17% of the electorate — and has demonstrated willingness to organize around access issues (the ADAPT direct action tradition).

Research infrastructure is well-developed. The Rutgers Program for Disability Research has produced the landmark accessible democracy studies. The EAC has published disability turnout data. The National Council on Disability maintains the policy analysis function. IFES (International Foundation for Electoral Systems) has a dedicated Disability Rights Cohort as part of its Summit for Democracy participation infrastructure.

**Research Complexity**: 10-14 hours. Primary sources are accessible and organized: EAC disability voting data, Democracy Docket SAVE Act analysis, NDRN legislative tracking, CBPP SSA impact analysis, The Arc research, NCD policy reports. The constitutional framework (ADA Title II as applied to elections, HAVA accessibility requirements, NVRA intersections with SSA benefit offices) is specialized but well-documented. The international comparative dimension (IFES global disability inclusion work, UN Special Rapporteur thematic report prepared for March 2026 Human Rights Council session) provides direct sourcing. This is among the less research-intensive candidates in terms of source access but requires careful synthesis across multiple statutory frameworks.

**2026-2028 Policy Windows**

- SAVE Act Senate floor consideration (2026): disability provisions are a specific advocacy intervention point during Senate debate and markup
- ADA enforcement litigation: if SAVE Act passes with disability-harmful provisions, DOJ disability rights section could bring enforcement action (cross-reference Domain 29 for DOJ capture problem); private ADA plaintiffs provide alternative enforcement
- SSA office-closure litigation: NVRA enforcement against SSA for voter registration offer failures at closed offices
- FY2027 appropriations: SSA staffing restoration is a budget fight with specific dollar amounts (7,000 FTEs = approximately $490M in salary restoration)
- ADA 35th anniversary in 2025 created organizing momentum that extends into 2026; ACLU has explicitly framed the moment as "accessible voting is under attack 35 years after the ADA"

**Institutional Adoption Patterns**

Disability law clinics (Rutgers, Berkeley, Loyola) provide the academic pipeline. State Protection and Advocacy systems (P&As) — funded by HHS to provide legal advocacy for people with disabilities — exist in all 50 states and the District of Columbia; they are natural distribution nodes for a domain document that provides the democratic-participation framing their clients need. Congressional Disability Staff Caucus provides the Hill penetration point. The Disability Rights Advocates and National Disability Rights Network legislative teams can translate a domain document into model legislation.

**Competitive Landscape**

The disability community's existing research focuses almost entirely on programmatic benefit access (SSA processing times, Medicaid coverage rates, HAVA compliance) rather than on the coherent democratic-design question — what does systematic civic exclusion of 26% of the adult population mean for democratic legitimacy, and what is the structural reform architecture? The Rutgers Accessible Democracy project comes closest but addresses voting access as a HAVA compliance problem rather than as a constitutional democracy problem. The IFES disability inclusion work is global-comparative and does not analyze US domestic design. The gap this domain would fill is the synthesis: a document that connects benefit infrastructure collapse (SSA), voting access architecture (SAVE Act, HAVA), ADA enforcement (DOJ dismantlement), and democratic participation data (turnout deficits) into a unified reform analysis.

### Candidate 3: Campaign Finance, Dark Money, and Electoral Corruption

**Candidate Name and Focus**: The post-*Citizens United* architecture of unlimited, undisclosed spending in US elections — the $2 billion dark money ecosystem, FEC enforcement collapse, and the democracy–plutocracy capture problem — and the statutory, administrative, and constitutional reform architecture that would restore democratic legitimacy to electoral finance.

**Urgency Assessment**

The 2026 midterms are projected to reach $11 billion in total advertising expenditure — the most monetized midterm cycle in US history. Dark money groups spent $2 billion on the 2024 election (double the 2020 total), with the four largest super PACs focused on House and Senate control alone raising $71 million from undisclosed sources in 2025. The midterm cycle extends the urgency: the same infrastructure that spent $2 billion in 2024 is now operational and scaling for 2026.

The FEC enforcement collapse is the structural mechanism enabling this. The FEC has a six-commissioner structure that requires four votes to take enforcement action; the structure produces systematic deadlock when commissioners split 3-3 on party lines. The commission has not meaningfully enforced campaign finance law against major party donors in years. The FEC's political independence — the one potential check on partisan weaponization of campaign finance — has been further compromised by the current administration's treatment of the agency as a deregulatory vehicle rather than an enforcement body.

The connection to broader democratic erosion is direct: the top political donors in 2026 are simultaneously seeking and receiving regulatory forbearance from agencies they fund. The fossil fuel industry's political spending ($250M+ in 2024) directly correlates with EPA rollbacks documented in the environmental-rollbacks-tracker. The private equity industry's political spending correlates with FTC and DOJ antitrust enforcement reversals. This is not coincidence — it is the mechanism through which plutocratic capture of electoral systems translates into policy outcomes that undermine democratic governance.

**Coalition Strength**

The campaign finance reform coalition is institutionally established but politically constrained. Issue One, Common Cause, the Campaign Legal Center, Represent.Us, and the Brennan Center constitute the primary reform infrastructure. The coalition is bipartisan in its historical design (the original Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act was McCain-Feingold), but the post-Citizens United environment has hardened partisan divisions. Congressional Republicans have been near-uniformly opposed to disclosure requirements since 2010; Democrats have supported disclosure but been increasingly willing to use dark money vehicles when they are out of power (a dynamic Jacobin documented in April 2026 with the emergence of new Democratic dark money infrastructure).

The most viable coalition path runs through the transparency argument rather than the spending-limits argument. The disclosure coalition (requiring donor disclosure above certain thresholds) is broader than the spending-limits coalition, attracting some Republican support and strong public opinion backing (70%+ in polling consistently). The FEC deadlock reform coalition is even broader — reforming the commission structure to enable enforcement does not require agreement on what should be enforced.

**Research Complexity**: 10-14 hours. The primary sources are well-organized: OpenSecrets dark money database (the most comprehensive public data source), Brennan Center dark money analysis, FEC enforcement records and deadlock data, Issue One super PAC tracking, Citizens United and subsequent SCOTUS precedent chain. The constitutional law dimension (what regulatory framework survives First Amendment scrutiny after Citizens United, McCutcheon, and Speechnow.org) requires engagement with campaign finance law literature. The comparative dimension (UK Political Parties, Elections and Referendums Act; Germany's public funding model; Canada's donor disclosure requirements) is well-documented.

**2026-2028 Policy Windows**

- 2026 midterm election cycle (ongoing): live case study generating new data and voter outrage that supports the advocacy narrative
- DISCLOSE Act (active in Senate but stalled): model legislation for disclosure reform that could advance with Senate majority change
- FEC commissioner vacancies: presidential nomination of FEC commissioners creates a confirmation window; Democratic senators can condition confirmation on structural reform
- State-level action: California, New York, and Washington have enacted state-level disclosure requirements that provide model legislation and empirical evidence for federal reform advocates
- FEC rulemaking on digital advertising disclosure (lagging behind television/radio disclosure requirements): an administrative pathway that does not require congressional action

**Institutional Adoption Patterns**

Political science departments (Harvard Kennedy School, Princeton, Stanford) have substantial money-in-politics research programs. The Brennan Center and Campaign Legal Center have direct government relations presence. Law school election law clinics (Harvard, Yale, NYU) provide the litigation support pipeline. Congressional staff on the Senate Rules Committee and House Administration Committee are the primary Hill penetration points. State AG offices (California, New York) have used state campaign finance law aggressively and can adopt federal reform models as model legislation when they have state authority.

**Competitive Landscape**

This is the most competitive research landscape of the five candidates. OpenSecrets, the Campaign Legal Center, Common Cause, Issue One, and the Brennan Center all produce high-quality ongoing research. What the framework's domain document would add is the democratic-design synthesis: not "here is the spending data" (which OpenSecrets does better than any single document could), but "here is how unlimited, undisclosed political spending is a structural democratic design failure analogous to the other 40 domains — and here is the reform architecture that would address it as a system rather than a compliance problem." The integrative frame is the value-add; the underlying data is well-sourced elsewhere.

- [Unmasking Dark Money: The Threat to Transparency and Democracy — Politics-Government News](https://politics-government.news-articles.net/content/2026/04/27/unmasking-dark-money-the-threat-to-transparency-and-democracy.html)

### Candidate 4: Energy Democracy, Cooperative Infrastructure, and Civic Resilience

**Candidate Name and Focus**: How the ownership architecture of energy infrastructure — investor-owned utilities versus democratically governed cooperatives and energy communities — determines the distribution of political and economic power; and what a democratically resilient grid, paired with cooperative broadband, would look like as a civic participation substrate.

**Urgency Assessment**

Energy democracy is at a structurally significant inflection point. The Biden-era IRA funding ($369 billion over ten years) created the largest federal investment in distributed energy infrastructure in US history; the Trump administration's effort to claw back those funds through executive action is an active legal dispute in 2026. The IRA's Rural Energy for America Program (REAP) and the EPA's Greenhouse Gas Reduction Fund (GGRF, which included $7B for Solar for All in low-income communities) are both in legal jeopardy. If the clawback succeeds, the opportunity to build democratically governed distributed energy infrastructure at scale closes for a generation.

The rural electric cooperative sector — 900 cooperatives, 42 million Americans, 56% of US land area — is at a strategic decision point. Cooperatives that upgrade their infrastructure using IRA REAP funds will also have the broadband capacity to become the delivery infrastructure for rural digital democratic participation: voter registration, civic information, and community organizing. Cooperatives that do not upgrade will be absorbed into investor-owned utility territory through the consolidation underway. The 2026-2028 window is the effective decision boundary for which path the cooperative sector takes.

**Coalition Strength**

The energy democracy coalition has unusual breadth: rural electric cooperatives (historically bipartisan, deeply embedded in rural Republican communities), urban environmental justice advocates (who want community ownership of solar and storage), labor unions (who want good-paying construction jobs in the grid buildout), and fiscal conservatives who support decentralized local control over centralized utility monopolies. The Institute for Local Self-Reliance (ILSR) is the primary research-and-advocacy anchor. The Democracy Collaborative's Public Ownership for Energy Democracy report provides the democratic-design framing. Frontiers in Sustainable Energy Policy (2026) has documented the "energy democracy as rural revival" connection explicitly.

The limitation is policy window uncertainty: if IRA clawbacks are completed, the immediate funding vehicle disappears and the coalition loses its primary near-term organizing hook. The domain's value would then shift from "how to use IRA infrastructure funding to build democratic cooperative infrastructure" to "here is the model and the legislative restoration pathway."

**Research Complexity**: 12-16 hours. This is the most technically demanding candidate because it requires engagement with energy policy, cooperative law, rural broadband policy, and democratic governance theory simultaneously. The technical literature on distributed grid architecture (microgrids, VPPs, cooperative governance models) requires reading outside the traditional democratic-reform literature. The IRA funding provisions and their clawback status require current regulatory tracking. The comparative material (EU energy communities, German Energiegenossenschaften, Danish cooperative housing model) requires dedicated international sources. The ILSR, Democracy Collaborative, and Frontiers article provide strong anchors.

**2026-2028 Policy Windows**

- IRA clawback litigation: the legal status of GGRF funds and REAP contracts is being adjudicated in 2026; court outcomes will determine whether cooperative energy buildout proceeds
- USDA Rural Development broadband funding (E-Connectivity Program): FY2027 appropriations will determine the scale of cooperative broadband deployment
- State-level energy community legislation: California, New York, and Colorado have enacted or proposed energy community legislation that provides model text
- FERC proceeding on cooperative aggregation rules: cooperatives seeking to participate in wholesale electricity markets need FERC rulemaking clarity; proceeding is pending

**Institutional Adoption Patterns**

Rural electric cooperative associations (NRECA — National Rural Electric Cooperative Association) are the primary institutional node, with 900 member organizations and direct lobbying presence. Land-grant university extensions (Iowa State, University of Wisconsin, Cornell) have cooperative development programs that could adopt domain research as curriculum. The Democracy Collaborative and New Economy Coalition provide the think-tank distribution channel. State energy offices and state PUCs are the regulatory adoption pathway.

### Candidate 5: International Human Rights Architecture and US Re-engagement

**Candidate Name and Focus**: The structural relationship between US treaty obligations, multilateral human rights institutions (HRC, ICC, CAT, CEDAW), and domestic democratic legitimacy — and the reform architecture needed for the US to credibly re-engage as a democratic actor in international institutions after systematic withdrawal.

**Urgency Assessment**

The US withdrawal from international human rights architecture has accelerated significantly in 2025-2026. The Trump administration has withdrawn from or de-funded: the WHO (re-withdrawal announced January 2025), the UN Human Rights Council (non-participation resumed), USAID democracy programs (97% of democracy and governance grants terminated, $14B), and the National Endowment for Democracy (defunding proposed). The US is now in arrears on UN dues, INTERPOL dues, and multiple treaty body funding obligations.

Domain 37 (Foreign and Transnational Interference) covers the consequences for US domestic democratic institutions of this withdrawal — authoritarian powers filling the vacuum left by NED and USAID. Domain 19 (National Security and Foreign Policy) addresses security alliances. But no domain addresses the *treaty law* dimension: what specific obligations the US has under ICCPR (ratified 1992), CAT (ratified 1994), and CERD (ratified 1994) — and what re-engagement with the treaty reporting system, the Human Rights Committee, and the Universal Periodic Review process would require for a reformed US government.

The 2026 urgency is tied to UPR timing: the US's fourth Universal Periodic Review cycle is due in 2027. The UPR process requires state party reports; civil society organizations are already preparing shadow reports documenting 2025-2026 human rights conditions. A domain document prepared now would feed directly into civil society UPR preparation — a specific institutional adoption pathway with a fixed calendar date.

**Coalition Strength**

The international human rights coalition in the US is institutionally mature but politically constrained. Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International USA, the ACLU's Human Rights Program, the Robert F. Kennedy Human Rights Foundation, and the US Human Rights Network (a coalition of over 300 organizations) all work on treaty compliance. The cross-partisan appeal is limited — Republican foreign policy has historically opposed international human rights treaty obligations as sovereignty constraints — but the specific framing of "what would US re-engagement look like" opens a restoration narrative that has appeal beyond the activist base.

The policy school and law school pipeline is strong: the Georgetown University Law Center Human Rights Institute, the Harvard Human Rights Journal, and Columbia's Institute for the Study of Human Rights all produce research in this space. The UN Special Rapporteur system creates direct engagement pathways for US civil society organizations.

**Research Complexity**: 15-20 hours. This is the most research-intensive candidate in the set. International human rights law is a specialized field requiring engagement with treaty texts, Optional Protocol ratification status, treaty body general comments, UPR cycle documentation, and comparative state-party reporting. The domestic constitutional law intersection (self-executing vs. non-self-executing treaty status, Reservations-Understandings-Declarations attached to US treaty ratifications) requires specialized legal sourcing. The comparative dimension is inherently broad: Germany, Canada, and New Zealand as models of robust treaty engagement provide the reform target, but their domestic implementation models require dedicated research. The ICJ enforcement gap — the court's decisions are advisory without self-executing enforcement — is a structural design question that requires engagement with proposals for reform (universal jurisdiction expansion, ICC complementarity doctrine).

**2026-2028 Policy Windows**

- US UPR submission due 2027: civil society shadow reports currently in preparation
- ICC prosecutor review of US personnel: the ICC's May 2025 warrant for Netanyahu precedent has reopened questions about US ICC engagement
- State Department's annual Country Reports on Human Rights: the 2026 report (covering 2025 events) will be the first produced under the current political environment — its quality and scope signal US treaty engagement credibility
- Senate Foreign Relations Committee treaty ratification backlog: CEDAW remains unratified by the US Senate; a Democrat-majority Senate after 2026 could schedule ratification hearings
- UN dues arrears: FY2028 appropriations process creates an opportunity for UN dues restoration as a foreign policy reset signal

**Institutional Adoption Patterns**

Law school human rights clinics (Georgetown, Harvard, Columbia, NYU) are the primary academic pipeline and also provide direct civil society integration through clinic clients. The US Human Rights Network provides 300+ organizational distribution nodes. State department reform coalitions (the American Academy of Diplomacy, the Foreign Policy for America coalition) provide the bipartisan foreign policy establishment channel. Substack and policy journal distribution reaches the foreign policy professional class that would be the primary audience for the restored-engagement reform design.

**Competitive Landscape**

Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International, the ACLU Human Rights Program, and the US Human Rights Network all produce excellent ongoing analysis of US treaty compliance failures. What this domain would add is the structural synthesis: not "here is what the US is failing to comply with" (which HRW and Amnesty document exhaustively) but "here is the institutional reform architecture for a democratic government seeking to credibly re-engage with the international human rights system." The re-engagement design question — what treaty ratifications, reporting commitments, structural reforms, and appropriations a reform government would need to make and in what sequence — is not well-addressed in the existing literature. This is the distinctive contribution the domain would make.

## Part V: Research Roadmaps for Top Two Candidates

### Research Roadmap: Candidate 1 — Domestic Intelligence and Law Enforcement Accountability

#### Research Methodology

**Primary sources** (no intermediary required):
- Church Committee Final Report (1976): Senate Intelligence Committee, "Intelligence Activities and the Rights of Americans" — the foundational document establishing the post-COINTELPRO reform framework; publicly available at SSCI archives
- Attorney General Guidelines on Domestic FBI Operations (current version, Mukasey 2008): DOJ public document establishing the investigative predicate standards that Patel has circumvented
- FBI Domestic Terrorism Operations Section (DTOS): Congressional testimony records, GAO reports on domestic terrorism program design, NPR and Government Executive reporting on 2025-2026 restructuring
- ACLU FOIA litigation records against FBI and DHS fusion centers: case docket in federal district court; documents released through FOIA
- Raskin/Nadler/Lofgren letter (September 22, 2025) to Bondi/Patel: publicly available via House Judiciary Democrats website
- Brennan Center fusion center abuse documentation: "Ending Fusion Center Abuses" — the most comprehensive existing reform architecture
- Congressional Research Service reports on FBI authority and domestic intelligence oversight

**Secondary analysis**:
- The Nation's September 2025 institutional history ("A Half Century of Failure to Reform the FBI")
- Government Executive's reporting on Hoover-era parallels (September 2025)
- CNN litigation reporting on fired FBI officials' suit against Patel (September 2025)
- ACLU's "Where FBI Director Nominee Kash Patel Stands on Civil Liberties" pre-confirmation analysis

**Expert contacts for deeper research**:
- Brennan Center's Liberty and National Security Program (Michael German, former FBI agent now at Brennan Center, is the key expert contact)
- ACLU National Security Project (Hina Shamsi)
- Frank Church Institute at Boise State University

**Comparative data**:
- Germany's BfV (Bundesamt für Verfassungsschutz): parliamentary oversight model, public accountability reports, domestic intelligence separation from law enforcement
- Canada's NSIRA (National Security and Intelligence Review Agency): independent review body created 2019, model for accountability body design
- UK's ISC (Intelligence and Security Committee): parliamentary oversight model with declassification authority

#### Section Structure (Complete Domain Document)

A complete Candidate 1 domain document would require approximately 8,000-10,000 words organized into seven major sections:

1. **Historical Foundation: COINTELPRO and the Church Committee Reform Architecture** — What the Church Committee found (750+ pages of documented abuses), what reforms it produced (FISA, Attorney General Guidelines, congressional intelligence committees), and why those reforms are now being systematically dismantled in 2025-2026. This section establishes the "before and after" frame that makes current erosion legible.

2. **The Domestic Intelligence Apparatus: Structure and Authorities** — Current FBI counterintelligence authorities (FISA, National Security Letters, Section 215, administrative subpoenas), fusion center network (80 centers, DHS and DOJ funding, private sector information sharing), and the legal framework governing each. This section establishes what the apparatus is before analyzing how it is being misused.

3. **The 2025-2026 Crisis: Documented Political Targeting** — Specific documented instances: Minnesota immigration protest investigations, fired FBI officials' lawsuit, DTOS disbanding, Foreign Influence Task Force elimination. This section provides the evidentiary record that makes the domain's democratic-concern case rather than a theoretical one.

4. **The Constitutional Framework: First and Fourth Amendment Limits on Domestic Surveillance** — What courts have said about FBI surveillance of political organizations, the "mosaic theory" of aggregated data collection, First Amendment-based restrictions on informant infiltration of political organizations, and how Loper Bright's administrative deference reversal affects FISA court oversight of executive surveillance.

5. **Comparative Models: Parliamentary Oversight and Independent Review** — Germany's BfV parliamentary accountability, Canada's NSIRA model, UK's ISC framework. This section establishes what "accountable domestic intelligence" looks like institutionally.

6. **Reform Architecture: Statutory, Administrative, and Constitutional Pathways** — New Attorney General Guidelines restoration; Church Committee II (select committee proposal); FISA reform (sunset current court structure, create adversarial public advocate); fusion center funding conditions (tie DHS grants to civil liberties compliance); FBI Director term protection (restore 10-year term protection against mid-term removal); Domestic Intelligence Oversight Board (statutory independent review body on the PCLOB model for domestic intelligence).

7. **Implementation: Who Does What, in What Sequence** — The specific actors (state AGs, congressional Judiciary committees, civil liberties organizations, PCLOB) who can implement reform at different stages of political feasibility, and the sequencing logic that makes phased implementation credible even absent a Democratic trifecta.

#### Key Decision Trees

**Decision Tree 1: What triggered the current crisis?** — The Church Committee reforms held for approximately 40 years before structural erosion. The key research question is: which specific mechanisms failed first? The answer shapes the reform priority order. If the FISA court's secrecy is the structural failure, then FISA reform is the first-priority intervention. If the Attorney General's Guidelines' lack of statutory basis is the failure, then statutory codification is the first priority. If congressional oversight committee capacity is the failure, then a Church Committee II is the first priority.

**Decision Tree 2: What is the most efficient leverage point for a reform administration?** — A Democratic attorney general can restore Attorney General Guidelines (executive action, no legislation required). A Democratic Congress can restore FISA court adversarial procedures (legislation). A bipartisan majority can create a select committee investigation (joint resolution). The domain should map which reforms require which political preconditions.

**Decision Tree 3: What is the constitutional floor?** — The Fourth and First Amendments set a constitutional minimum below which even congressional authorization cannot go. Mapping that floor — what surveillance is categorically prohibited regardless of statutory authorization — provides the framework for evaluating which current FBI/fusion center practices are illegal now, not merely politically objectionable.

#### Preliminary Findings

Initial research reveals that the current domestic intelligence accountability crisis is not primarily a resource problem or a personnel problem — it is a structural design problem that the Church Committee reforms partially but not fully addressed. The FISA court, created as the oversight mechanism for foreign intelligence collection, does not provide meaningful oversight of domestic intelligence gathering (which uses different legal authorities: National Security Letters, administrative subpoenas, confidential human sources). The fusion center network was built after 9/11 with minimal accountability architecture — no standardized civil liberties policies, no systematic auditing, no public reporting requirements, no independent oversight body. Kash Patel's ability to restructure FBI priorities and terminate personnel without congressional consequence is not a Patel aberration; it is a structural feature of the FBI director role that the Church Committee reforms addressed incompletely.

The most important preliminary finding for the domain's reform agenda: the PCLOB (Privacy and Civil Liberties Oversight Board) model, applied to domestic intelligence rather than only to national security intelligence, is the most operationally feasible reform that does not require either a Democratic majority or executive branch cooperation. The PCLOB is a bipartisan independent oversight body with statutory authority, subpoena power, and public reporting obligations. Extending its mandate to cover domestic FBI counterintelligence and fusion center operations would fill the accountability gap that the Church Committee identified but that subsequent reform legislation left unaddressed.

1. **The Demographic Reality: Disabled Americans as a Democratic Constituency** — 26% of US adult population; 35 million disabled voters in 2020; voter turnout deficit (11% of disabled voters faced polling barriers vs. 6% non-disabled); the concentration of disability in low-income, rural, and communities of color that are already facing other democratic participation barriers. This section establishes the democratic-stakes framing.

2. **Voting Access Architecture: HAVA, ADA, and the Compliance Failure Record** — The statutory framework (HAVA Section 301, ADA Title II, Voting Rights Act); the GAO's 60% polling-place barrier finding; the HAVA funds' reduction under continuing resolution; DOJ ADA enforcement capacity under the current administration. This section establishes the legal architecture and its current enforcement collapse.

3. **The SAVE Act's Disability Dimension** — Specific analysis of H.R.22's provisions: photo-ID requirements and the 20% disabled-American license gap; absentee ballot documentation requirements and their intersection with cognitive and physical disability; in-person witness requirements and their barrier to housebound disabled voters. This section is the most immediately time-sensitive given Senate consideration.

4. **Benefit Infrastructure and the NVRA Connection** — How SSA benefit offices function as NVRA voter registration sites; the 7,000-person staff cut's documented effect on processing times; office closure patterns (which communities lose physical SSA offices and how that maps onto low-income disabled voter registration geography); the Medicaid/SSDI benefit access intersection with voter registration. This section establishes the benefit-to-ballot pipeline that makes SSA cuts a democracy issue, not only a welfare issue.

5. **Comparative Models: Disability Inclusion as Democratic Infrastructure** — Australia's Electoral Commission accessible voting model; New Zealand's Electoral Commission technology and accessibility review; UN/IFES disability-inclusive elections standards. This section provides the international evidence that disability democratic inclusion is achievable at high fidelity.

6. **Reform Architecture: Statutory, Administrative, and Litigation Pathways** — HAVA reauthorization with strengthened disability accessibility provisions; ADA Title II enforcement restoration (DOJ structural); SAVE Act disability exemptions or carve-outs (advocacy intervention in Senate); SSA staffing restoration (FY2027 appropriations); NVRA enforcement against SSA (litigation pathway); accessible voting technology deployment mandate (EAC rulemaking).

#### Key Decision Trees

**Decision Tree 1: What is the fastest intervention?** — The SAVE Act Senate debate is the most time-bounded opportunity. The advocacy intervention is specific: disability-protective amendments during Senate markup, or conditional support for the bill contingent on ADA-compliant ID alternatives. This is a 2026 window that closes when the Senate acts.

**Decision Tree 2: What is the litigation pathway?** — If the SAVE Act passes without disability-protective amendments, ADA Title II and the Rehabilitation Act of 1973 provide direct legal hooks for challenge. Democracy Docket has already built the legal framework; the domain document would provide the democratic-design argument that supports the litigation.

**Decision Tree 3: What is the SSA–democracy causal chain?** — The NVRA connection to SSA offices is the most novel and underresearched element. The research question is: which geographic communities have the highest concentration of SSA-dependent voter registration, and how do office closures in those communities affect registration rates? This would require Urban Institute or similar organization's geographic analysis, which may require a specific data request.

#### Preliminary Findings

Initial research reveals that disabled Americans face a four-layer democratic exclusion architecture that no existing domain addresses as a unified design problem. Layer one: physical and cognitive barriers at the ballot box (HAVA compliance failures at 60% of polling places). Layer two: identification barriers (SAVE Act photo-ID requirements falling disproportionately on the 20% of disabled Americans without driver's licenses). Layer three: benefit access barriers that reduce voter registration (SSA office closures under the 7,000-person cut reduce NVRA voter registration offers in the communities most dependent on SSA as a government contact point). Layer four: SSI/SSDI benefit eligibility rules that create financial disincentives for civic participation (the in-kind support and maintenance rules that the Trump administration attempted to tighten penalize disabled adults for living with family members who might provide them transportation to polling places).

This four-layer architecture is the most important framing insight for the domain document. Existing disability rights advocacy treats each layer separately — polling-place accessibility advocates do not coordinate with NVRA enforcement advocates, who do not coordinate with SSA staffing advocates. A domain document that frames the four layers as a unified exclusion architecture — and proposes reform interventions that address all four simultaneously — would be a genuinely novel contribution that the disability rights community lacks and that the democratic-renewal proposal would be uniquely positioned to provide.

The most significant preliminary finding is that the NVRA-SSA connection is empirically underexplored. The National Voter Registration Act requires that public assistance agencies offer voter registration; SSA offices are required by law to be designated as voter registration sites. When SSA offices close or dramatically reduce appointment availability, the NVRA obligation is theoretically violated — but no organization has yet brought an enforcement action on this theory. This litigation angle is available, timely, and would have high democratic salience if pursued.
