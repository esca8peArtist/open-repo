## Framing Note: What This Document Is For

`policy-influencer-mapping.md` answers the question: who should receive this framework? This document answers the next question: how does the framework actually move through the influence network, and in what order do you pull which levers to maximize the probability that it translates into legislative action, institutional endorsement, and public pressure? The distinction matters. A contact list is not a strategy. A strategy tells you which contacts unlock which other contacts, what credibility prerequisites must exist before each outreach attempt, and what failure modes to monitor at each stage.

The 35-domain framework is not self-distributing. Its quality does not cause it to spread. What causes it to spread is a sequenced engagement with the specific nodes that control information flows across the four communities that need to move simultaneously: the legal-academic community (which establishes credibility), the legislative community (which converts credibility into bills), the media community (which converts bills into public pressure), and the mass-organization community (which converts public pressure into political cost). Each community has its own gatekeepers, credibility standards, and timing requirements. This document maps those four tracks and coordinates them.

Cross-reference with `DOMAIN_37_SEQUENCING_PLAN.md` for the specific election-protection timing track, which runs on a parallel but distinct calendar from the main distribution sequence.

## Part 1: Network Topology

### 1.1 How Ideas Actually Propagate in the US Policy Ecosystem

The conventional model — publish research, media covers it, legislators act — is empirically wrong for structural reform proposals. The actual propagation pathway for policy ideas that result in legislative action follows a different sequence:

**Law review / working paper** publishes or circulates academic version of the idea. This establishes that the idea can survive peer scrutiny and is not politically contaminated.

**Think tank translates** the academic version into a policy brief with specific legislative text, cost estimates, and political framing. Brennan Center, CAP, and EPI perform this function for center-left reform ideas. The translation is necessary because law review articles are not readable by Senate staff and do not contain bill language.

**Senate committee staff** reads the think tank brief, not the law review article, and incorporates language into hearings, markup documents, or cosponsor sign-on letters.

**Law school clinic directors** provide academic air cover — when a bill is introduced, clinics testify, file amicus briefs, and produce rapid-response analysis that becomes the media's expert source.

**Media covers the bill and the expert sources** (the clinic directors and think tank fellows), not the underlying research. The research is invisible to the public; what is visible is the institutional endorsement architecture.

**Mass organizations** (labor unions, civil rights coalitions, faith networks) see institutional coverage and legislative activity, which signals that the proposal is serious — not another research document — and mobilize member communications.

The implication for the 35-domain framework: the goal is not to get the document read. The goal is to place it at the upstream nodes (law schools, think tanks) with sufficient credibility that it moves through the translation pipeline into the downstream nodes (legislative text, media framing, mass mobilization) on its own momentum. Once the Brennan Center has published a brief on Domain 6, that brief — not the original document — becomes the operative instrument. The original document's function is to supply the raw material.

### 1.3 Coalition Structure: Three Viable Coalitions with Sequencing Logic

The 35-domain framework cannot be advanced by a single political coalition. The comparative record is explicit on this point: Poland's Tusk coalition succeeded because it assembled separate political coalitions for different reform tracks — judicial reform required different allies than economic reform, which required different allies than media reform. Attempting to advance all 35 domains through a single progressive coalition will activate maximum resistance from Republican-aligned institutions while leaving centrist and crossover allies unengaged. Three distinct coalitions must be built and sequenced separately, with convergence designed for the 2026-2027 legislative window.

**Coalition A: The Institutional Integrity Coalition (domains 2, 6, 26, 34)**
Composition: bipartisan — Senate Judiciary members from both parties, ABA leadership, law school deans, former federal judges, inspector general community, GAO career staff.
Entry frame: "restoring institutional guardrails that protect all parties from executive overreach." This frame works for Grassley (IG issue), Collins (appropriations authority), and Murkowski (federal land management/DOI independence) simultaneously with Durbin, Whitehouse, and Raskin.
Sequencing: This coalition must be built first because it provides the institutional credibility that makes the other two coalitions possible. A bipartisan group (even two or three Republican senators) endorsing Domain 6 judicial independence reform gives the entire framework protection from the "partisan wishlist" dismissal. Launch: immediate, Phase 1.
Specific sequencing within coalition: Grassley (IG documentation) → ABA Task Force (rule of law mandate) → former federal judges → Murkowski (Alaska federal land disputes = DOI independence angle) → Collins (appropriations authority) → bipartisan cosponsor list.

**Coalition B: The Economic Accountability Coalition (domains 3, 5, 17, 20, 23, 31, 34)**
Composition: center-left — EPI, Brookings (economic divisions), labor unions, consumer advocacy organizations, small business associations (NFIB documented 91% tariff cost impact), bipartisan trade policy community.
Entry frame: "economic rules that protect workers, small businesses, and consumers from concentrated power — corporate and governmental." This frame allows Brookings and NFIB credibility alongside EPI and AFL-CIO.
Sequencing: This coalition activates in Phase 2 (June-September 2026), after the institutional integrity coalition has established the framework's credibility with centrist institutions. Tariff litigation (Domain 23, 24-state AG coalition already filed March 5, 2026) is the current event hook. Launch: June 2026, coordinated with Trade Review Act (S.1272) activity.
Specific sequencing: EPI Domain 17 brief → Shierholz/AFL-CIO bridge → Brookings economic analysis → NFIB tariff impact data → bipartisan trade policy co-sponsors → consumer advocacy groups.

**Coalition C: The Mass-Participation Coalition (domains 1, 7, 8, 9, 14, 16, 22, 27)**
Composition: progressive coalition — ACLU, NAACP LDF, Lawyers' Committee, civil rights organizations, academic freedom networks, immigration legal organizations, media advocacy groups.
Entry frame: "constitutional rights that belong to every person regardless of party." This frame allows religious liberty conservatives (Domain 27 academic freedom) to participate alongside civil rights progressives (Domain 1, 22).
Sequencing: This coalition activates in Phase 3 (post-2026 elections), after the institutional and economic coalitions have established legislative vehicles. Mass-participation reforms (voting rights, reparations, immigration, policing) face the highest political resistance and require the longest groundwork. The exception is Domain 37 (election protection), which runs on a separate urgent timeline per `DOMAIN_37_SEQUENCING_PLAN.md` — advocacy windows begin May 30, 2026, and cannot wait for coalition sequencing.
Specific sequencing: ACLU (Domain 7) → Lawyers' Committee (Domain 1, 14) → NAACP LDF (Domain 1, 22) → faith networks (moral framing across traditions) → Indivisible (mass mobilization) → labor-civil rights convergence point.

### 1.4 The Information Network Topology

Understanding the directional flow of influence prevents wasted outreach. The network has a clear hierarchy with well-documented propagation paths:

**Tier A — Sources** (generate original credibility): law review articles, court filings, government reports, primary investigative journalism.

**Tier B — Translation** (convert sources into policy-actionable form): Brennan Center, CAP, EPI, Brookings, CSIS, AEI (conservative crossover). This tier is the bottleneck. Raw research does not move into the legislative or media pipelines without passing through a Tier B institution.

**Tier C — Amplification** (convert policy briefs into political pressure): Senate committee staff, law school clinic directors (who become expert witnesses and media sources), editorial boards, legal bloggers (Balkinization, Just Security, Lawfare).

**Tier D — Distribution** (reach public audiences): major media (NYT, WaPo, NPR), Substack newsletters (Heather Cox Richardson — 2.9M subscribers), podcast networks (Pod Save America — 1M+ per episode), social media (primarily Bluesky for the policy-engaged demographic).

**Tier E — Mobilization** (convert awareness into organized action): labor unions, civil rights organizations, faith networks, local organizing chapters (Indivisible's 3,300+ locations).

The critical non-obvious finding: information does not move efficiently from Tier A to Tier E by broadcast. It moves by sequential handoff through each tier. Attempting to go directly from Tier A (law review publication) to Tier E (labor union mobilization) without the intermediate translation steps produces the outcome that currently characterizes most academic policy work: the research sits in repositories unread by anyone in Tiers D or E.

The framework's distribution strategy must therefore operate as a Tier B institution even though it originated as Tier A material. The practical implication: do not distribute the full 35-domain document to labor unions or faith networks. Distribute domain-specific materials that Tier B institutions (Brennan Center, EPI) have already vetted and translated. The full document is for Tier A and Tier B audiences only; everything downstream receives translated materials.

## Part 3: Sequencing Strategy

### 3.1 Pre-Distribution Phase (4-6 Weeks Before Public Launch)

The pre-distribution phase is the highest-leverage period in the entire strategy. Actions taken here determine whether the public launch has institutional momentum or launches into a credibility vacuum.

**Weeks -6 to -4: Credibility Seeds**

Contact in this order, with 3-5 days between each outreach:

1. **Erica Chenoweth (Harvard, Nonviolent Action Lab)** — Approach as research collaboration, not endorsement request. Frame: "The 35-domain framework's resistance analysis section draws extensively on your work. I'd welcome your feedback on whether the application of the 3.5% threshold analysis to the current US landscape is methodologically sound." This positions her as a reviewer, not an endorser, which lowers the threshold for engagement. If she engages substantively, that engagement becomes the credibility seed for organizer networks.

2. **Ryan Goodman (Just Security, NYU Law)** — Pitch Domains 28 and 29 as standalone submissions to Just Security. Just Security publishes within days. A published piece citing the framework gives it national security law community credibility and creates the first external citation needed for subsequent outreach. This is the fastest credibility seed available.

3. **Jack Balkin (Yale, Balkinization)** — Approach for direct academic feedback on Domain 6 constitutional analysis. Balkinization post mentioning the framework reaches every constitutional law professor in the top-50 schools. This is not a media placement — it is an academic signal.

**Weeks -4 to -2: Institutional Tier Pre-Positioning**

4. **Brennan Center (Wendy Weiser, VP Democracy)** — Send the full proposal with a specific request: "We would welcome feedback before public distribution on whether the voting rights and judicial independence domains reflect the current litigation landscape accurately." Framing as quality control, not endorsement request. Include a note that Just Security has published [reference the Goodman piece, if it exists by this point]. If Brennan Center staff identify gaps, fill them. Their investment in improving the document creates ownership.

5. **Senate Judiciary minority staff (Whitehouse and Durbin offices)** — Send domain-specific one-pagers on D6 and D29 to staff directors. Not the full document. Not a meeting request. A one-page brief with: current status of the issue, what the domain proposes, what comparable precedent exists, and what specific legislation maps to the proposal. These are working documents for staffers who will incorporate them into hearing preparation.

6. **ACS (American Constitution Society, Phil Brest)** — Request inclusion in 2026 National Convention programming as a research presentation. The convention is the event where law school faculty, Hill staff, and civil society leaders are in the same room. A 20-minute presentation slot is worth 10 email campaigns.

7. **Law school clinic directors (Georgetown ICAP, NYU Constitutional Litigation Clinic, Columbia CDI)** — Approach clinic directors (not deans) with a research partnership offer: the framework as source material for clinic advocacy, amicus brief preparation, and law review symposium planning. Clinic directors are entrepreneurial — they want useful raw material for their students and cases.

**Off-the-Record Media Background Briefings (Weeks -3 to -1)**

Before public launch, offer background briefings to:
- Quinta Jurecic (The Atlantic) on Domains 28 and 29 — her current beats
- Zack Beauchamp (Vox) on comparative democracy analysis — his current subject matter
- NPR Politics Desk on the framework as background context for 2026 election coverage
- Washington Monthly (Paul Glastris) on a potential feature piece

Background briefings serve two functions: they give reporters early access that creates goodwill and depth of understanding, and they allow reporters to raise questions that improve the public-facing framing before launch. A reporter who has been briefed two weeks before launch will write a more substantive piece than one who receives a press release on launch day.

### 3.2 Launch Phase: Coordinated 2-Week Push

The launch window should be timed to align with one of two legislative calendar triggers:
- Senate Judiciary Committee hearing activity (Whitehouse/Durbin should have scheduled something by this point)
- An external event that makes the framework's analysis immediately relevant (Trump v. Slaughter expected June 2026; any SPLC case development; tariff litigation SCOTUS activity)

**Week 1: Tier 1 Public Announcement Window**

Day 1: Public release of executive summary. Not the full document. The executive summary is the distribution unit for public audiences. The full document is for institutional audiences who have already been pre-briefed.

Simultaneous: ACS publication of framework in Scholars Network. Just Security piece (if pre-staged) either already published or publish on Day 1. Balkinization mention if secured.

Days 2-3: Media outreach with the institutional citations as leads. "This framework has been reviewed by [Brennan Center / Just Security / specific law school clinics]" is the sentence that converts a press release into a story. Without institutional citations, media will not cover an independent policy document.

Days 4-7: Senate staff follow-up. The one-pagers pre-distributed to Whitehouse, Durbin, and Klobuchar staff should now get a follow-up email with links to any published citations. "The analysis on Domain 6 has now been cited by [institution] — I wanted to make sure your office had the updated reference."

**Week 2: Tier 2 Institutional Rollout**

Law school clinic directors who received pre-distribution materials should now be ready to announce their engagement. Law review symposium invitations should go out in Week 2 — the goal is to have 3-4 journals simultaneously planning symposia, which creates the impression of wide academic uptake even though the outreach was coordinated.

Think tank brief submissions: Domain-specific briefs go to EPI (D17), Brennan Center (D6, D1), CAP (D11, D18), Brookings (D5, D23). Each is submitted as a research contribution, not a distribution request.

**Tier 3 Mass Activation Timing**

Labor unions, civil rights organizations, and faith networks should receive Tier 3 distribution materials (not the full document — domain-specific fact sheets) in Week 2 of the launch phase, after institutional citations exist. The sequence matters: labor union communications staff will google the framework before distributing it; if they find institutional citations, they will distribute it; if they find only the original document, they will not.

The SPLC indictment response coalition (100+ civil rights organizations) is pre-activated. Domain 29 (prosecutorial weaponization) materials should reach this coalition in Week 2 specifically, when they are looking for frameworks to channel their response.

### 3.3 Post-Launch Phase: 8-12 Weeks of Reinforcement

Reinforcement is not repetition. Do not send the same materials twice. Reinforcement consists of: new developments that confirm the framework's analysis (each SCOTUS ruling on relevant cases, each legislative introduction, each AG coalition win), and follow-up contact with the specific contacts who showed initial engagement.

**Weeks 3-6: Legislative Track Follow-Up**

For Senate contacts who received pre-distribution materials: track whether bills have been introduced that incorporate domain language. When they are (Whitehouse on D6, Klobuchar on D1, Raskin on D29), send a brief note: "Domain 6's proposals align with the [bill name] introduced this week. The comparative evidence section of the domain document may be useful for committee preparation." Convert the legislative activity into evidence that the framework has predictive value.

**Weeks 6-10: Academic Track Follow-Up**

Law review symposia, once agreed, take 3-6 months to publish. The reinforcement task is to ensure that the editors have what they need: updated data, additional case study material, connections to other faculty who should contribute. A coordinated symposium with 8-12 articles produces a self-reinforcing academic record — each article cites the others, creating the impression of an established field of inquiry.

**Domain 37 Separate Timeline (Weeks 6-B7, August-October 2026)**

Per `DOMAIN_37_SEQUENCING_PLAN.md`, the election protection track runs on its own calendar tied to the five advocacy windows. The main distribution sequence should not wait for Domain 37; Domain 37 should not be accelerated to match the main distribution sequence. They are parallel tracks.

**Weeks 10-12: Mass Organization Activation**

AFL-CIO distribution (via EPI brief) should produce legislative contact campaigns by member unions in the October-November window, timed for the final stretch before the 2026 midterms. The goal is to make the framework's economic domains (D3, D5, D17, D20) visible in the October media coverage as issues that organized labor is specifically tracking.

Faith network activation should concentrate in Weeks 10-12 for voter education and moral framing of democratic participation. Poor People's Campaign's 45 state chapters are the primary activation vehicle.

## Part 4: Messaging Variants by Influencer Type

### 4.1 For Legislators: Constitutional Authority, Legislative Remedy, Bipartisan Mechanics

The central mistake in academic policy distribution to legislative offices is framing. Legislators do not respond to "democracy is in crisis." They respond to: "Your committee has jurisdiction over this specific abuse, and here is the specific bill language that addresses it, with the cosponsor count we believe is achievable, and here is how we reached that count."

**For Republican crossover targets (Collins, Murkowski, Grassley, occasional Cornyn):**

Lead with institutional authority, not rights language. "The administration's defiance of appropriations law undermines the Senate's constitutional power of the purse. This is not a partisan issue — it is a structural claim that any future Senate majority will benefit from restoring." Collins's Appropriations chair role is the hook. Domain 34 language should be drafted specifically to appeal to a senator who chairs the committee responsible for spending authority.

Frame every economic domain in constituent-impact terms. Do not use "workers" or "the poor" — use "Maine lobstermen," "Alaska Native communities," "Iowa farmers." The Policy specifics must be localized before reaching Republican offices.

Avoid: democracy restoration, autocracy language, comparative international comparisons (which read as anti-American in Republican offices), and any reference to electoral outcomes.

**For Democratic targets (Whitehouse, Durbin, Klobuchar, Raskin, Warren, Merkley):**

Use comparative case studies directly — they have academic staff who will engage with the Poland, Hungary, South Korea material. The 18-24 month prevention window finding from the comparative record is the urgency argument that works with Democrats who are already convinced of the problem. "Poland's judicial capture was substantially complete within 18 months of PiS taking power. The US is at month 14 of analogous judicial appointments concentration. Here is what the Polish reform coalition did before the window closed."

Legislative specifics are essential: provide bill language, not proposals. The difference between "we recommend reforming prosecutorial accountability" and "here is specific statutory language for the Charging Transparency Act, with constitutional analysis by [Georgetown ICAP / Yale clinic] and a list of 37 senators we believe would cosponsor" is the difference between a policy paper and a legislative resource.

### 4.2 For Think Tanks: Research Credibility, International Precedent, Policy Implementation

Think tank senior fellows and policy directors read constantly. Their credibility filter is: has this been peer-reviewed in any way? Is the evidence base solid? Does it acknowledge counterarguments? Is it usable for testimony and briefings?

**For centrist institutions (Brookings, Bipartisan Policy Center):**

Lead with economic evidence only. Do not mention democracy reform until economic credibility is established. Cecilia Rouse's Brookings is specifically sensitive to anything that reads as progressive advocacy — the entry point must be economic and empirical. "Domain 5 documents a $688B annual tax gap and quantifies the revenue impact of capital gains tax reform using Treasury data. Domain 23 uses Yale Budget Lab's $30B/year GDP loss estimate for current tariff policy, with household-level distributional analysis from TPC and PIIE. Would Brookings Economic Studies have interest in reviewing the fiscal architecture sections for potential collaboration?"

**For center-left institutions (CAP, EPI, Brennan Center):**

The full comparative evidence base is appropriate. These institutions use international precedent routinely. The specific differentiator for this framework versus the dozens of other reform proposals they receive is the integration: a single framework that shows how judicial independence (D6) enables civil service protection (D2), which enables healthcare reform (D11), which enables climate policy (D15). The cross-domain synthesis section of the full proposal is the most valuable thing for these institutions because it shows systemic connection, not issue-by-issue analysis.

**For conservative-leaning institutions (AEI, Niskanen Center):**

Antitrust (D20) and civil service merit (D2) are the entries. AEI has genuine anti-monopoly traditions (Arthur Brooks's legacy) and the current Doar leadership is institutionally integrity-focused. The civil service merit argument — that Schedule F/Career/Policy undermines merit-based government that conservatives historically valued — is a legitimate conservative institutional argument, not a reframing of a progressive argument. Frame it as: "What would a functioning civil service that conservatives would support look like? Here is the historical and comparative evidence."

### 4.3 For Media: Breaking News Angle, Systemic Failure, Public Interest Frame

Media contacts need a news hook that justifies coverage today, not a research document that justifies coverage in principle. The framework is not itself news; the specific current events it illuminates are news.

**Investigative reporters (ProPublica, The Intercept, Documented):**

The trackers are the product for investigative reporters. Offer the consent decree defiance tracker, the litigation tracker, the environmental rollback tracker, and the corporate accountability documentation as source datasets. Investigative reporters do not want to be handed conclusions; they want datasets they can investigate. "Domain 29 documents 22 cases of retaliatory prosecution with court filing citations, judicial findings, and chronological documentation. This is primary source material for investigative work, not a policy position paper."

**Policy correspondents (Vox, NYT, WaPo, The Atlantic):**

The comparative analysis is the hook. "No other framework currently available documents what Hungary's Tisza victory on April 12, 2026 means specifically for US democratic renewal strategy — what went right, what will take decades, and what the US equivalent of Magyar's two-thirds supermajority would need to accomplish in the first 100 days." Recency matters: the framework has already incorporated Hungary April 2026, Taiwan, Poland 2026 updates. This is not historical analysis — it is current.

**Editorial boards (NYT, WaPo, WSJ for specific domains):**

Editorial board pitches should target domains where the board has already staked out positions. NYT editorial board has written extensively on SCOTUS reform and judicial independence — Domain 6 is the entry, with the op-ed framing: "What would actually fixing the Supreme Court look like? Here is a five-year sequenced roadmap with constitutional authority analysis." WSJ editorial board has historically supported civil service merit principles — Domain 2 civil service protection framed as merit restoration (not union protection) is a possible WSJ opening.

### 4.4 For Law Schools: Academic Rigor, Constitutional Scholarship, Professional Responsibility

Law school faculty receive dozens of policy documents per month. The differentiating frame is: what specific academic contribution does this make that is not available elsewhere?

The answer is the cross-national comparative evidence base. No existing domestic law review article has systematically applied the Spain, South Korea, Poland, Hungary, Taiwan, and Costa Rica democratic recovery evidence to the full scope of US structural reform — not just to one domain (courts, or elections, or civil service) but to all 35 domains simultaneously. This is a genuine scholarly gap.

Pitch to law school clinic directors as: "research partnership for clinic advocacy and symposium organization." The clinic adoption model — where clinic students use the framework's domain materials as the basis for litigation strategy memos, amicus brief research, and legislative analysis — produces academic output that cites the framework without any explicit endorsement required.

The professional responsibility angle: ABA Model Rules Rule 6.1 establishes a professional responsibility to provide pro bono legal services. ICAP at Georgetown, the Voting Rights Project at Lawyers' Committee, and clinics at NYU and Columbia have institutional mandates to engage with the legal issues the framework documents. Frame as: "Here is the research infrastructure for the pro bono work you are already committed to."

### 4.5 For Labor and Civil Rights: Member Welfare, Democratic Participation, Accountability

Labor and civil rights communications must be concrete about member impact. Abstract democracy framing activates sympathy but does not produce action. Specific member-welfare framing produces action.

**For AFL-CIO and affiliated unions:**

"Domain 17 documents what collective bargaining looks like in Germany, where unions have board representation and sectoral bargaining. Germany's manufacturing sector — with its union density at 67% versus U.S. at 10% — has maintained wage growth while the U.S. manufacturing wage stagnated. Here is what the PRO Act does, what the sector-specific pilot would look like, and what the 2026-2027 legislative window looks like for advancing it."

The NLRB quorum issue is the immediate hook: NLRB quorum loss directly affects active cases for AFL-CIO affiliated unions. Any union with a pending NLRB case knows this personally. Use specific cases.

**For NAACP, Lawyers' Committee, civil rights organizations:**

The SPLC indictment coalition is already activated. Domain 29 materials (prosecutorial weaponization documentation) should reach this coalition as the academic and legal backing for what they are already saying. The framing is: "Here is the documented pattern — 22 retaliatory prosecution cases across four categories, with judicial findings of vindictive prosecution in at least three. Here is the statutory remedy that changes the accountability structure."

Voting rights framing for Domain 1 and Domain 37 must connect to specific community impacts. Use the 23 active DOJ voter roll cases by state and the specific communities affected by each.

### 4.6 For Faith Leaders: Justice and Renewal Framing, Common Values

Faith networks require a framing that is theologically grounded and non-partisan in religious terms — which is different from politically non-partisan. The Poor People's Campaign's "fusion politics" model — bringing together low-income people across racial and religious lines around economic justice — is the template.

Effective frames across traditions:
- "Care for the stranger" (Hebrew Bible, Leviticus 19:33-34) — Domain 16 immigration
- "The dignity of work" — Domain 17 labor; speaks to both Catholic social teaching and Protestant work ethic traditions
- "Bearing false witness" — Domain 8 information integrity; prohibition on deliberate falsehood in civic discourse
- "Jubilee economics" — Domain 18 safety net; debt cancellation, poverty reduction
- "Seeking justice and correcting oppression" (Isaiah 1:17) — Domain 14 criminal justice reform
- "Rendering to Caesar" — Domain 34 appropriations authority; legitimate governmental authority and its limits

Faith leader engagement produces a specific asset unavailable elsewhere: the physical congregation. Weekly religious service attendance provides access to 70+ million Americans who do not consume political media but who trust their clergy on moral questions. The distribution mechanism is not a newsletter — it is a sermon, a bulletin insert, a small group discussion guide.

## Part 5: Engagement Mechanics

### 5.1 Contact Strategies by Tier

**Tier 1 (Federal Decision-Makers) — 1-on-1 Briefings Only**

No mass email, no simultaneous outreach. Every Tier 1 contact receives a personalized, domain-specific briefing that references their known legislative activity. The format is: one-page domain brief (not the full document), a specific ask (feedback, staff introduction, cosponsor consideration), and a connection to a current event or active bill in their portfolio.

The briefing medium depends on the contact: Senate staff prefer PDF attachments by email; House staff prefer web links; think tank policy directors prefer a 30-minute call. Never send the full 35-domain document to a Tier 1 contact as an initial outreach.

**Tier 2 (Institutional) — Institutional Outreach Protocol**

For law schools: email to clinic director with research partnership offer. For think tanks: email to relevant policy director with domain brief and request for feedback. For media: pitch email to specific correspondent with news hook and source offer (data, expert, document). For legal organizations: email to program director with litigation-relevant section extracted from relevant domain.

**Tier 3 (Mass Organizations) — Domain-Specific Fact Sheets**

No document over 2 pages for Tier 3 initial outreach. A 2-page fact sheet with: the problem in one paragraph (using member-welfare language), three specific policy solutions (using existing legislation where possible), one concrete international comparison (using a country the audience respects), and one specific ask (share with members, sign statement, schedule briefing).

**Failure Mode 2: Institutional resistance — think tanks decline engagement, cite partisanship**

**Failure Mode 3: Legislative calendar misalignment — Congress in recess or consumed by unrelated crisis**
Indicator: No Senate committee hearings scheduled on relevant domains; unrelated crisis dominates all legislative bandwidth

Every major legislative proposal in the framework has a state-level analog that can proceed through state legislatures without federal legislative activity. Simultaneously, accelerate state AG litigation track, which does not depend on legislative calendar and is already active with 71 cases.

**Failure Mode 4: AG coalition fractures before November 2026 elections**

**Failure Mode 5: Academic engagement without translation — law school briefs remain unread by legislative staff**
Indicator: 60 days after distribution, no legislative bills incorporate domain-specific language; briefs sitting in faculty repositories
Contingency: Activate Law360, The Hill, and Politico op-ed campaigns by faculty who have engaged with the framework. An op-ed in Law360 or Politico Pro reaches Senate staff directly — these are staff reading platforms. ## Appendix: International Precedent Lessons for Amplification Strategy

The comparative record provides four specific lessons for the amplification strategy itself — not just for the substance of what the framework proposes.

**Poland lesson (the translation bottleneck):** The Tusk coalition had extensive academic research on judicial reform but the translation pipeline from academic work to specific legislative text was underdeveloped. The "10 pillars" of reform announced in November 2024 still lacked the statutory specificity needed to survive constitutional challenges. For the US framework: the think tank translation function (Brennan Center, EPI) is not optional infrastructure. Without it, academic quality does not produce legislative outcomes.

**South Korea lesson (sequencing in the organizer community):** The June 1987 mobilization succeeded because it had already developed organizational depth through years of YWCA, church, and student networks — the mass mobilization peak was not the beginning of the work, it was the culmination. For the US framework: Tier 3 activation (labor, civil rights, faith) should not be treated as the launch — it should be treated as the culmination, deployed when institutional credibility has been established by Tiers 1 and 2.

**Hungary 2026 lesson (speed of institutional action after political window opens):** Magyar's Tisza party won April 12, 2026 — 138 of 199 seats. The hard August 2026 deadline for implementing reforms before specific terms expire means that the implementation blueprint must be pre-staged before the political window opens, not drafted after. For the US framework: every domain's legislative text, cosponsor list, and implementation protocol must be ready to introduce within 30 days of a political opening, not drafted in response to one. This is the function of the activation timeline in `ACTIVATION_ARCHITECTURE.md`.

**Taiwan lesson (pre-consensus building before formal institutional action):** Lee Teng-hui's "one-institution, two-stages" approach — National Affairs Conference as consensus-building first, then formal constitutional change — provides the model for what the amplification strategy is actually building. The pre-distribution phase (off-the-record briefings, research partnership offers, background conversations with media) is the National Affairs Conference equivalent — building cross-institutional consensus before any formal proposal is on the table. The formal launch is Stage 2, not Stage 1. The pre-distribution work determines whether Stage 2 has the consensus it needs to advance.
