## 1. ICE: Organization, Structure & History

### Origins

ICE (U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement) was created on **March 1, 2003** as part of the Homeland Security Act reorganization that followed 9/11. It consolidated investigative and interior enforcement powers formerly split between:
- The **U.S. Customs Service** (Treasury Department, founded 1789)
- The **Immigration and Naturalization Service** (INS, Justice Department)

Both agencies were dissolved, and their functions distributed between the new ICE and CBP (Customs and Border Protection). ICE sits within the Department of Homeland Security.

### Three Main Divisions

**1. Enforcement and Removal Operations (ERO)**
- The "deportation force" — arrests, detains, and removes people
- Operates the detention system
- Manages 287(g) agreements with local law enforcement
- Currently undergoing restructuring under Border Patrol leadership (see below)

**2. Homeland Security Investigations (HSI)**
- 10,300+ employees; approximately 6,000 special agents
- Second largest investigative service in the US, behind only the FBI
- **237+ domestic offices**; 93+ international attaché offices in 60+ countries
- Investigates 400+ federal laws: drug trafficking, human smuggling, weapons, financial crimes, cybercrime, child exploitation, sanctions violations
- Has historically sought separation from ERO due to reputational concerns — HSI agents often argue immigration enforcement conflation undermines cooperation in criminal investigations
- International Operations (formerly Office of International Affairs): attaché offices worldwide

**3. Office of the Principal Legal Advisor (OPLA)**
- ICE's in-house legal arm
- Represents the government in immigration court proceedings
- Oversees legal policy for detention and enforcement

### 2025 Structural Overhaul

In **October 2025**, the Trump administration executed a purge of ICE's career leadership:
- Multiple ICE Enforcement and Removal Operations (ERO) field office directors replaced
- **Gregory Bovino**, a Border Patrol veteran, elevated to control interior removal operations
- Border Patrol's more aggressive enforcement methodology is being imported into interior ICE operations
- Goal: meet daily arrest quotas set by the administration (reportedly 3,000+ arrests/day at peak)

**Result**: By October 2025, **82% of total ICE detainees** were arrested in interior operations — compared to **28% before the administration took office**. This is a historic and unprecedented shift toward interior enforcement not seen since the Bush/Obama eras.

**287(g) Agreements** — local law enforcement memoranda of understanding allowing local officers to perform immigration enforcement functions — expanded dramatically:
- More than **1,000 agreements** now active across the country
- 10,000+ officers trained or in training under the program's street-level enforcement model as of September 2025
- Concern: "abuse-prone" enforcement model with limited federal oversight of local officers

## 3. The Detention System: Scale and Structure

### Population Growth Under Trump's Second Term

| Date | People in ICE Detention |
|------|------------------------|
| January 20, 2025 (inauguration day) | ~40,000 |
| Mid-December 2025 | ~68,440 |
| January 2026 | ~73,000 |
| March 2026 | Record highs (ongoing) |

This represents a **75%+ increase in one year**. The system's current daily capacity is approximately 70,000.

### Current System Scope

- Approximately **265 detention centers** operating as of December 2025
- **104 additional facilities** opened in the first year alone — a **91% increase**
- These include: small county jails (single-digit beds), county corrections facilities, formerly closed state prisons, newly-constructed military tent camps, and now warehouse conversions
- Does not count local/temporary holding sites

### How the Detention Network Works

**Three main tiers of ICE detention**:

1. **ICE-owned Service Processing Centers (SPCs)**: Facilities built and operated directly by ICE. These are the original detention facilities; examples include Krome in Florida.

2. **Contract Detention Facilities (CDFs)**: Privately owned and operated under contract with ICE. The dominant model; operated by GEO Group, CoreCivic, Management & Training Corp (MTC). These are the largest portion of the system.

3. **Intergovernmental Service Agreements (IGSAs)**: Local jails and state prisons that contract with ICE to house detainees. The most variable in standards — some are jails with virtually no immigration-specific standards compliance.

**The New Tier: Federal Warehouse Facilities**
A fourth tier is being constructed: federally-owned warehouse facilities converted to ICE use (see Section 11). This represents the most significant structural change to the detention system in ICE's history.

## 4. How to Locate Someone in ICE Detention

### The Online Detainee Locator System (ODLS)

**Website**: [locator.ice.gov/odls/](https://locator.ice.gov/odls/)
**Also**: [portal.ice.gov/detainee](https://portal.ice.gov/detainee)

**What you need to search**:
- Full legal name AND alien number (A-number), OR
- Full legal name AND date of birth AND country of birth

**Important limitations**:
- **Takes 24-48+ hours** for a newly detained person's information to appear in the system
- People held by CBP (not yet transferred to ICE) should appear after 48 hours in custody
- **Children under 18** do NOT appear in the ODLS
- People with T or U visa applications (crime/trafficking victims) typically do NOT appear
- System can lag or have errors — absence in system does not mean person is not detained

**If ODLS fails**:
1. Call the **ICE ERO field office** for the area where the person was detained
2. Call the detention **facility directly** if you know or can determine it
3. Contact an immigration attorney or legal aid organization immediately
4. For people in California, call [Bay Area Legal Aid](https://baylegal.org) or similar

**NILC guide**: [How to Locate a Loved One After a U.S. Immigration Arrest](https://media.nilc.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/How-to-Locate-a-ICE-Disappearance.pdf)

### Tracking Through the System

Once detained, a person moves through:
1. **Arrest/initial detention** (CBP or ICE hold, often at local jail or processing facility) — 24-48 hrs
2. **Transfer to ICE detention facility** — can be hundreds of miles from arrest location
3. **Immigration court proceedings** (often via video teleconference — detainees rarely see judges in person)
4. **Removal/deportation** OR **release on bond or supervision** OR **continued detention pending appeal**

**Transfer patterns are a documented abuse vector**: ICE routinely transfers detainees to facilities far from their attorneys and families, making legal representation nearly impossible. This appears deliberate in some cases — a legal strategy to prevent effective representation.

## 5. Detention Standards: What Is Supposed to Happen

### The Standard Framework

ICE operates two main standard frameworks:

**Performance Based National Detention Standards (PBNDS 2011, updated)**
- Applies to facilities under contract with ICE (private operators, county jails with IGSAs)
- Covers: medical and mental health care; food; housing; security; legal access; visitation; grievance procedures; disciplinary system; hygiene; recreation
- Enforceable through contract terms

**National Detention Standards (NDS 2025)**
- Applies to ICE-operated Service Processing Centers
- Updated version released in 2025

### Key Standards (What Should Be Happening)

**Space and housing:**
- Single occupant hold rooms: minimum **37 square feet** (includes 7 unencumbered sq ft for the person, 5 sq ft for toilet/lavatory, 25 sq ft wheelchair turnaround)
- Dormitory-style housing for longer-term detention: varying standards
- Cells must have working toilets, sinks, lighting, and climate control

**Medical care:**
- Health screening within 12 hours of arrival
- Full health assessment within 14 days
- 24/7 access to emergency care
- Chronic condition management
- Mental health screening and treatment
- Pharmaceutical access

**Food:**
- Three meals per day, minimum 2,500 calories
- Must meet religious/dietary restrictions on request
- Within 6 hours of admission, must be offered food

**Legal access:**
- Access to a law library or legal materials
- Private attorney-client meetings
- Phone access (though at often-exorbitant rates)
- No monitoring of attorney-client communications

**Visitation:**
- Family and legal visitation should be permitted
- Specific hours vary by facility

**Grievance system:**
- Written grievance procedures must exist
- Emergency grievances must be addressed within 24 hours

### The Reality Gap

ICE's own Office of Detention Oversight (ODO) rated **238 of 241 facilities** as "acceptable or above" in FY2022-2024 — a figure that the GAO, DHS OIG, and advocates have documented as reflecting fundamentally inadequate inspection standards rather than actual conditions.

The independent Office of the Immigration Detention Ombudsman (OIDO) found **31 of 33 facilities inspected** were **non-compliant** with the specific standard that led to each inspection.

## 6. Oversight: Who Is Watching (and Who Isn't)

### The Oversight Ecosystem (17 Entities, Little Accountability)

DHS operates **17 entities** with some oversight function over immigration detention — creating a fragmented, duplicative system in which no one entity has comprehensive authority or effective enforcement capacity.

**Internal (ICE) oversight**:
- **Office of Detention Oversight (ODO)**: ICE's own inspection arm — conflict of interest baked in; inspection reports down **36.25%** in 2025 despite **78%** increase in detainees
- **Detention Compliance and Removals**: Administrative compliance function

**DHS-level oversight**:
- **DHS Office of Inspector General (OIG)**: Independent investigations; has documented severe conditions at multiple facilities; findings are often ignored by ICE
- **DHS Civil Rights and Civil Liberties (CRCL)**: Investigates civil rights complaints; opened investigation into Winn Correctional (Louisiana) in October 2024 after 120+ complaints
- **Office of Immigration Detention Ombudsman (OIDO)**: Created to provide independent oversight; found 31/33 inspected facilities non-compliant with complaint-triggering standards

**Government Accountability Office (GAO)**: Issued report (GAO-25-107580) finding DHS needs to define goals and measures to assess facility inspection programs — essentially finding the inspection system itself is inadequately designed

**Congressional oversight**:
- Members of Congress have the legal right to inspect detention facilities
- Trump administration requires **7-day advance notice** for congressional visits
- ICE routinely **denies access** to Fort Bliss and other sites
- Administration is actively **suppressing and deterring** authorized congressional oversight
- When visits do occur (e.g., Padilla, Schiff, Khanna at California City, January 2026), documented conditions are severe

**Independent/External oversight**:
- **UNHCR**: Has a detention monitoring policy but has limited access to US facilities; international monitoring standards exist but are not being applied
- **Freedom for Immigrants**: Volunteer visitor program provides monitoring at select facilities; has filed complaints and published documented abuses
- **ACLU, HRW, Amnesty International**: File complaints, conduct independent investigations, publish reports — but have no enforcement authority and increasingly face access restrictions
- **National Immigrant Justice Center (NIJC)**: Legal representation and monitoring

### The Oversight Enforcement Problem

Oversight without enforcement is documentation, not accountability. The critical gap: even when violations are found:
- ICE can ignore DHS OIG findings
- Facilities with documented violations continue operating under contract
- The only effective enforcement mechanism is contract termination — which ICE rarely uses (Fort Bliss is a notable 2026 exception)
- Congressional oversight requires executive cooperation the administration is not providing

## 7. Conditions on the Ground: Documented Reality

### Fort Bliss / Camp East Montana (El Paso, TX)

The largest immigration detention facility in the US history, located on the former site used to intern Japanese Americans during WWII.

**Physical setup**: "Soft-sided" tent facility — industrial tent structures, each "pod" holding **60-70 people** per detainee accounts. At peak: 2,700-5,000 capacity.

**Documented conditions**:
- **60 standards violations within first 50 days** of operation
- Foul-tasting drinking water described by Rep. Escobar
- Rotten food
- Inadequate healthcare
- Three deaths in 44 days (December 2025 - January 2026)
  - One death ruled **homicide**: Geraldo Lunas Campos, 55, Cuban immigrant — witnesses reported guards choking him; medical examiner agreed
- Physical abuse and sexual abuse by officers documented by ACLU
- ICE announced contract termination in **March 2026** — less than 8 months after opening

### "Alligator Alcatraz" — Everglades Detention Facility

**Location**: Dade-Collier Training and Transition Airport, Big Cypress National Preserve, Ochopee, Florida  
**Opened**: July 2025  
**Capacity**: 3,000  
**Annual operating cost**: ~$450 million

**Physical setup**: Makeshift tents and cages in the Florida Everglades — extreme heat, mosquitoes, flooding risk, no permanent infrastructure.

**Documented conditions** (ACLU, Amnesty International, congressional visits):
- Cages holding **30+ individuals** at temperatures near **100°F**
- People denied potable water
- Meals infested with maggots
- Toilets broken with sewage running into sleeping areas
- Oversized mosquitoes — no adequate protection
- **The "box"**: A 2-foot-by-2-foot cage where detainees are shackled to the ground in open sun for hours as punishment, with no food or water — **Amnesty International** assessed this as **torture or other cruel, inhuman and degrading treatment**
- Color-coded uniforms; 5:30 AM mandatory breakfast; strict disciplinary rules
- Congressional visitors reported conditions firsthand

**Amnesty International assessment**: Conditions at Alligator Alcatraz amount to cruel, inhuman and degrading treatment; the "box" amounts to torture.

### Adelanto ICE Processing Center (California — GEO Group)

**Operator**: GEO Group  
**Population**: Jumped from ~300 to **nearly 1,400** within weeks in June 2025

**Documented conditions** (Disability Rights California, June 2025 visit):
- People shouting about being "treated like dogs in cages"
- Only **3 psychologists** for 1,400 people
- Two deaths in custody in 2025 (Ismael Ayala-Uribe, September 22; Gabriel Garcia-Aviles, October 23)
- Detainees denied critical medical care for life-threatening conditions
- Inadequate nutrition; lack of basic sanitation
- Prolonged solitary confinement use
- Active federal lawsuit (Public Counsel)

### Winn Correctional Center (Louisiana)

**History**: Former Louisiana state prison converted to ICE use  
**Documented**: 120+ civil rights and civil liberties complaints filed with DHS CRCL since 2021

**January 2024**: ICE officers and facility staff violently attacked a dormitory of approximately **200 people** with pepper spray — documented in complaint filed with CRCL  
**October 2024**: DHS CRCL opened new investigation focused on excessive use of force, physical and verbal misconduct, and inadequate conditions

### California City Detention Facility

**January 2026**: Senators Alex Padilla and Adam Schiff and Representative Ro Khanna conducted oversight visits — documented inhumane treatment and inadequate medical care  
**Result**: District Court granted preliminary order prohibiting abhorrent conditions — one of the few cases where courts intervened on facility conditions directly

### Krome North Service Processing Center (Florida)

**2025 inspection**: Found **5 violations in medical care** and **14 overall violations** including:
- Overcrowding with people sleeping on floors without bedding or pillows
- Staff failures to ensure people had been offered food after 6+ hours in custody
- Only **1 inspection** in 2025 despite ongoing violations (down from 2 inspections in prior years)

## 9. Legal Rights of Detainees

### Constitutional Rights

The **Fifth Amendment** guarantees due process to all **persons** — not just citizens. This means undocumented immigrants have due process rights including:
- Right to a hearing before an immigration judge before removal
- Right to legal representation (at their own expense — no government-appointed attorney)
- Right not to be subjected to torture or cruel, inhuman and degrading treatment

The **Eighth Amendment** protection against cruel and unusual punishment has been applied in immigration detention contexts through civil rights litigation, though the legal standard is debated since immigration detention is technically civil, not criminal.

### The Right to an Attorney

Detained immigrants have the right to be represented by an attorney. **But**:
- The government does not provide one — this is not like criminal court
- **70.8% of ICE detainees have no criminal conviction** as of September 2025
- Most detained people cannot find or afford an attorney, particularly when held in remote facilities
- Research consistently shows legal representation is the most effective factor in preventing deportation
- The administration has deliberately placed detention facilities in remote areas, far from immigration legal service providers, making access to counsel structurally impossible for many

**Documented violations of attorney access**:
- ICE agents physically blocking attorneys from seeing clients
- One immigration attorney stood outside the attorney visitation room for **four hours** trying to see a client
- DHS lawyers allege Homeland Security is **denying legal counsel** to Minnesota detainees
- 7-day advance notice requirement for congressional visitors mirrors similar barriers for legal visits

### Bond Hearings and Indefinite Detention

Federal policy mandates **indefinite detention** of many undocumented immigrants without opportunity for a bond hearing. This:
- Eliminates the only mechanism for pre-hearing release
- Forces people to face deportation hearings without counsel
- Makes it impossible to maintain employment, family ties, or prepare a defense

**Washington federal judges** found that immigrants detained without due process violated constitutional requirements — over 700 court rulings against administration detention policies by November 2025.

### The Immigration Court Crisis

Immigration courts are administered by the Executive Office for Immigration Review (EOIR), a component of the DOJ — meaning they are part of the executive branch, not truly independent courts. This creates structural dependence on the administration's priorities.

- Case backlogs were already at record highs pre-2025
- Mass arrests dramatically increased the backlog
- Many detained people wait months or years for hearings
- Video teleconference hearings are the norm — detainees rarely appear in person before judges

### The Two Dominant Players

**GEO Group** (publicly traded: GEO on NYSE):

- 2025 net income: **$254 million profit** — company record, roughly **700% increase** over 2024
- New/expanded contracts in 2025: approximately **$520 million** (annualized)
- 2026 revenue projection: ~**$3 billion**
- CEO publicly stated GEO is "a better deal than CECOT" (El Salvador's notorious mega-prison)

**CoreCivic** (publicly traded: CXW on NYSE):
- ICE revenue Q4 2025: **$244.7 million** (more than doubled from $120.3M same period prior year)
- 2025 profit: **$116.5 million** (~70% increase)
- 2026 projected net revenue: $147.5–157.5 million
- New/reactivated facilities in 2025: 6 facilities in California, Texas, Virginia, Kansas, Tennessee, Oklahoma (600–2,560 beds each)

**Management and Training Corporation (MTC)**: Third major player; operates several ICE facilities

### The Incentive Structure Problem

For-profit detention creates a fundamental structural problem: **revenue depends on occupancy.** The more people detained, and the longer they are detained, the more profit companies make. This creates direct financial incentives for:
- Maximizing detention of everyone, regardless of individual risk assessment
- Delaying legal proceedings (longer stays = more revenue per detainee)
- Minimizing costs per detainee (cutting food quality, healthcare staffing, programming)
- Lobbying for policies that increase detention (which GEO and CoreCivic have done for decades through political contributions)

**The Brennan Center** documented "private prison companies' enormous windfall" from ICE expansion, calling it a system built around financial incentives antithetical to humane treatment.

## 11. The Warehouse Expansion Program

### The "ICE Detention Reengineering Initiative"

This is the most significant structural change to the immigration detention system since ICE's founding. The initiative, revealed in internal documents, aims to "implement a new detention model" by **September 30, 2026**.

**The Plan**: Replace the dispersed network of jails and prisons with a network of **federally-owned**, converted commercial warehouses — giving ICE direct ownership rather than reliance on contractors for facilities (while still using contractors to operate them).

**Total budget**: **$38.3 billion** of the $45B OBBBA allocation  
**Total new capacity**: approximately **92,600 beds** from the warehouse program alone  
**Facilities planned**: **24 warehouses** total

### Two Types of Facilities

**Type 1: Regional Processing Centers (16 planned)**
- Population: 1,000–1,500 detainees each
- Average stay: **3–7 days**
- Function: Rapid intake, processing, initial deportation staging
- Locations confirmed or acquired: Hagerstown MD, Hamburg PA, Romulus MI, San Antonio TX, Roxbury NJ (500,000 sq ft), Surprise AZ

**Type 2: Large-Scale Detention Centers / "Mega-Centers" (8 planned)**
- Population: **7,000–10,000 detainees each**
- Average stay: **up to 60 days**
- Function: Primary pre-deportation detention for interior operations
- Locations confirmed or acquired:
  - **Social Circle, GA** (50 miles outside Atlanta): **1 million+ square feet** — would be larger than any single jail or prison building in America
  - **Socorro, TX** (El Paso metro area): **826,000 square feet**, purchased for **$123 million**
  - **Tremont, PA** (central Pennsylvania): large facility

### Known Warehouse Acquisitions (2025-2026)

| Location | Size | Purchase Price | Type |
|----------|------|---------------|------|
| Hagerstown, MD | 825,000 sq ft | $100M+ | Processing center (1,500 people) |
| Surprise, AZ | — | $70M+ | Processing center |

**9 of 24 warehouses acquired** as of reporting; over **$700 million spent** on acquisitions so far.

### Building Code and Legal Vulnerabilities

The warehouse conversion strategy has significant legal vulnerabilities that community advocates are exploiting:

**Occupancy classification change**:
- Warehouses are classified as **Group S occupancy** under the International Building Code (IBC)
- Detention facilities are **Group I-3 institutional occupancy**
- Changing classification requires re-evaluation of:
  - Fire protection and suppression systems
  - Egress capacity and exit placement
  - Smoke control systems
  - Zoning compliance
  - Compartmentalization

**Practical implications**: Converting a warehouse to a 7,000-person detention facility likely requires substantial structural modifications — new fire suppression systems, reinforced egress, emergency systems — that require local building permits in most jurisdictions.

**The legal question**: Does the federal government need local building permits? The answer is contested. The government claims intergovernmental immunity; local governments and legal advocates argue federal facilities must still comply with local building and zoning codes. This is the primary legal battleground.

### The Physical Conversion

From investigative reporting, published DHS plans, and contractor information, the warehouse conversion model involves:

**Exterior**: Large commercial/industrial warehouse retained as-is (concrete tilt-up or steel frame construction)

**Interior modifications**:
- Division into "pods" — large open-plan holding areas using fencing/barriers rather than individual cells
- Processing areas: intake, search, fingerprinting, photographing
- Administrative offices
- Medical bay (often inadequately scaled)
- Legal visitation rooms
- Housing/sleeping areas: cots or bunk beds in open warehouse space
- Dining: central cafeteria or pod-based meal delivery
- Outdoor recreation area: often minimal or absent
- Security station infrastructure: cameras, intercoms, controlled access points

**What's missing from most conversions**:
- Adequate natural lighting (warehouses have minimal windows)
- Outdoor space (critical for psychological and physical health)
- Privacy (open plan housing means no private space)
- Medical infrastructure proportional to population
- Legal access infrastructure

**Per the ICE National Detention Standards**: Single-occupant hold rooms require a minimum of **37 square feet** — the warehouse pod model with 60-70 people in an open bay represents a fundamentally different (and lower-standard) housing model that does not meet traditional standards.

## 14. Community Opposition: What Works

### Successful Resistance Models (2025-2026)

#### Kansas City, Missouri — Legislative Moratorium
**What happened**: City Council voted **12-1** for a **five-year moratorium** on all city approvals for non-municipal detention facilities after ICE scouted a 7,500-bed site  
**Result**: Platform Ventures canceled the property sale to DHS under public pressure  
**Follow-up**: County-level moratorium proposed to extend protection beyond city limits  
**Key tactic**: Preemptive legislative action before sale closed; 12-1 vote signaled political consensus  

#### Oklahoma City, Oklahoma — Executive Opposition
**What happened**: Mayor David Holt raised concerns about building permits; city council urged federal officials to use local permitting process  
**Result**: DHS announced it is "no longer in talks" to acquire the warehouse  
**Key tactic**: Permitting process used as leverage; mayoral leadership created cover for property owners

#### Salt Lake City, Utah — Property Owner Pressure
**What happened**: Mayor's opposition created political environment where property owners withdrew from negotiations  
**Result**: Ritchie Group announced they would not sell or lease to federal government  
**Key tactic**: Political signal from mayor → property owners felt empowered to refuse

#### Merrimack, New Hampshire — Bi-Partisan State Pressure
**What happened**: Town council population 30,000 — sent opposition letter to DHS Secretary Noem; concerns included loss of $500,000+/year in property tax base  
**Result**: Governor Kelly Ayotte (Republican) negotiated with Secretary Noem → DHS will not proceed  
**Key tactic**: **Economic arguments** (property tax base) made opposition bipartisan; Republican governor's negotiation was decisive  

#### Portland, Oregon — Land Use Conditions
**What happened**: ICE operates a facility under conditional approval limiting detention to **12 hours maximum**  
**September 2025**: Portland Bureau of Development Services issued a formal **land-use violation notice** after ICE repeatedly exceeded 12-hour limit  
**Key tactic**: Use of existing conditional approval terms as enforcement mechanism; documentation of violations → notice

#### Hanover County, Virginia — International Boycott + Local Government
**What happened**: Canadian billionaire Jimmy Pattison's development company owned the target warehouse; the Hanover County Board of Supervisors unanimously opposed citing land-use incompatibility (near retail/residential areas); Canadian politicians called for boycotts of Pattison Group businesses; protests were planned in Vancouver
**Result**: Pattison's company canceled the sale with no public explanation
**Key tactic**: International business pressure on a foreign-owned seller + unanimous local government opposition — a rare combination unavailable in most cases

#### Byhalia, Mississippi — Same-Party Senator Pressure
**What happened**: Planned 8,500-bed facility; Republican Senator Roger Wicker wrote DHS Secretary Noem opposing it, citing inadequate local medical infrastructure, economic development foreclosure, and infrastructure strain
**Result**: Noem agreed to "look elsewhere"; facility plans dropped
**Key tactic**: Same-party opposition using **infrastructure and economic arguments, not human rights framing** — the most politically efficient path when a Republican can be persuaded

#### Social Circle, Georgia — Infrastructure Denial
**What happened**: DHS purchased a 1-million-sq-ft warehouse for a planned 10,000-person mega-center; the City Manager locked the water meter on March 17, 2026, citing infrastructure incapacity: city water permit is 1 million gallons/day; sewer plant processes 660,000 gallons/day and is already at capacity
**Result**: Construction stalled as of March 2026; federal court to rule on whether DHS can proceed
**Key tactic**: The most aggressive infrastructure-based resistance tactic yet — using water/sewer capacity limits as a genuine, neutral engineering constraint

#### Maryland Attorney General — Legal Challenge
**What happened**: Maryland AG Anthony Brown filed suit February 23, 2026 against the Williamsport warehouse ($102.4M, 1,500 people), alleging violations of NEPA (no environmental review), APA (no public input), and impacts on Potomac River watershed, infrastructure, economy, and public health
**Result**: Federal judge issued a 14-day TRO on March 10, 2026 halting construction; broader challenge continues
**Status**: Most advanced NEPA/APA challenge; could establish precedent for other warehouse fights  

#### California AB32 — State Legislative Ban
**What happened**: 2019 law banned private immigration detention in California  
**Result**: Partially upheld by courts; five detention centers being phased out  
**Complication**: Federal government and GEO Group sued; appeals process ongoing; Supremacy Clause is a major vulnerability  

### Tactical Analysis: Why These Worked

| Tactic | Why It Works | Vulnerability |
|--------|-------------|--------------|
| Legislative moratorium (KC) | Creates legal barrier before purchase closes | Must act fast before property sold |
| Permitting process (OKC) | Building code change requires local approval | Federal supremacy may override |
| Property owner pressure | Sellers can simply refuse to sell | Federal eminent domain possible |
| Tax base economic argument | Creates bipartisan coalition | Countered by "local jobs" arguments |
| Existing permit conditions (Portland) | Uses existing legal relationship | Only works where permit exists |
| State AG litigation | Creates legal delay, public attention | Federal courts may preempt state law |
| Congressional pressure | Most effective with allied legislators | Administration blocks access |

### What Makes Opposition More Likely to Succeed

1. **Acting before purchase is complete**: Once ICE owns the building as federal property, local leverage decreases substantially
2. **Bipartisan economic framing**: Tax base, traffic, property values — these arguments reach Republican officials more than human rights arguments
3. **Building code/zoning legal theory**: Change from Group S to Group I-3 occupancy requires permits; this is the strongest legal theory
4. **Speed and organization**: First week after announcement is the highest-leverage window
5. **State-level backing**: State AG or governor involvement dramatically increases pressure and legal options
6. **Property owner relationships**: Direct engagement with building owners before DHS closes the deal

## 15. Community Opposition: What Fails

### Why Movements Fail

#### The Federal Supremacy Problem
The strongest argument against local ICE resistance: the **Supremacy Clause** of the Constitution holds that federal law preempts conflicting state and local law. When ICE operates as an arm of the federal government, federal courts have sometimes ruled that local building permits, zoning ordinances, and similar local requirements cannot be enforced against federal facilities.

**The California AB32 lesson**: A state law banning private immigration detention was partially struck down because the federal government argued it was unconstitutional under the Supremacy Clause/intergovernmental immunity doctrine. The case went multiple rounds through federal courts with inconsistent results.

#### Speed of Acquisition
The administration has moved rapidly to purchase properties, often before communities are aware of plans. Once a sale is complete and ICE holds the property as a federal asset, local leverage decreases substantially. Speed of community organizing relative to speed of federal acquisition is often the decisive variable.

#### Federal Eminent Domain
In theory, the federal government can use eminent domain to acquire property for federal purposes — including immigration detention. This is an extreme measure rarely used, but it means property owner resistance is not a permanent barrier.

#### The "Local Jobs" Counter-Argument
Private prison operators and some local officials counter opposition with economic arguments: jobs for guards and support staff; local contracts for food, maintenance, utilities. In economically distressed communities, this can be persuasive enough to divide potential opposition.

#### Political Capture
In some communities, local officials are sympathetic to the administration's immigration enforcement goals. The Roxbury, New Jersey example: town council said they oppose the facility, but activists suspect "leadership is quietly opening the door" — when officials are secretly cooperative while publicly neutral, public pressure campaigns are less effective.

#### Preemption of State Bans
AB32 is the clearest example: even state legislative bans on private immigration detention can be challenged in federal court under the Supremacy Clause and preemption doctrine. State laws cannot reliably block federal operations.

#### Insufficient Legal Theory
Protests and community opposition without a viable legal theory for blocking the facility tend to fail. The facility proceeds; people express opposition; nothing legally prevents the opening. Effective opposition requires a specific legal mechanism — zoning, permits, contracts, litigation — not just political pressure.

## 18. DOGE, Palantir & the Surveillance Infrastructure

### The Master Immigration Database

One of the most structurally significant changes under the second Trump administration is DOGE's integration into ICE's data architecture. The goal is a unified surveillance platform linking financial, social, and location data across every federal agency to identify and locate undocumented people anywhere in the country.

**Palantir's ImmigrationOS**

ICE awarded Palantir (co-founded by Peter Thiel, Elon Musk ally) a **$30 million contract** to build a system called **ImmigrationOS**, with a prototype due September 2025. The system:
- Aggregates data from across federal government databases
- Includes passport records, Social Security files, IRS tax data, license-plate reader data
- Designed to "streamline selection and apprehension operations of illegal aliens"
- Includes self-deportation tracking functionality — monitoring people who leave voluntarily

**DOGE's Master Database**

DOGE operatives built a master immigration enforcement database by:
- Pushing through a **data-sharing agreement between the IRS and ICE** over objections from senior IRS officials
- Gaining access to **Social Security Administration** records
- Requesting access to **Medicare data** for address location
- Integrating data into USCIS's existing "data lake" — turning the benefits agency into an enforcement feed
- DOGE personnel gained access to USCIS's technical systems directly

The database goes beyond identifying criminal aliens to flagging **civil immigration violations** — essentially anyone in the country without status, a population of an estimated 11–13 million people.

### Legal Controversies and Errors

- Democratic lawmakers filed multiple legal challenges to IRS and SSA data sharing
- At least some DOGE database queries **wrongly flagged U.S. citizens** — the data merging creates false positives
- Courts partially upheld and partially blocked various DOGE data-sharing arrangements (litigation ongoing as of March 2026)
- The ACLU and EFF have filed challenges on Fourth and Fifth Amendment grounds

### Why This Matters Long-Term

The surveillance infrastructure being built now is not just for this administration. ImmigrationOS, the IRS-ICE data link, and the master database represent a permanent expansion of the federal government's identification and tracking capabilities that would be extremely difficult to dismantle even if political control changes. Unlike executive orders (which can be reversed), data systems and contractor relationships tend to persist.

**The "chilling effect"**: Immigrant communities have dramatically reduced interactions with government services (healthcare, schools, social services) due to fear that any government contact creates a data trail. This has public health implications far beyond the detention system itself.

## 19. "Zombie Prisons" — Reopened Shuttered Facilities

### What They Are

The Marshall Project coined the term "zombie prisons" for the wave of shuttered detention facilities — state prisons, county jails, federal prisons — that the Trump administration reopened as ICE detention centers in 2025. NPR identified **at least 16 shuttered facilities across 12 states** reopened by December 2025.

This represents a second track of expansion running parallel to the warehouse conversion program: while the DRI builds new infrastructure, ICE is simultaneously refilling every dormant facility it can contract.

### The Economic Structure

Former prisons become ICE facilities through no-bid contracts, primarily under a border emergency declaration that bypasses competitive procurement requirements. The transaction typically:
1. Private prison company purchases or leases the dormant facility
2. Upgrades infrastructure minimally (or not at all)
3. Signs a no-bid IGSA or direct contract with ICE
4. Begins generating revenue within weeks

The margins are extraordinary because the capital costs are low (existing infrastructure) and the contracts are generous. Louisiana is the paradigm case: sentencing reform under Governor Landry closed several state prisons in 2024 — they were almost immediately leased to private operators and reopened for ICE.

### Named Examples

**North Lake Processing Center (Baldwin, Michigan)**
- Formerly: North Lake Correctional Facility, a GEO Group maximum-security prison, closed under Biden's federal private prison ban
- Now: "North Lake Processing Center" — largest immigration detention facility in the Midwest, **second-largest in the U.S.**
- Capacity: 1,800 beds
- Operator: GEO Group (same as before)
- Revenue: Part of GEO's record $254M 2025 profit

**Leavenworth, Kansas**
- Formerly: Leavenworth Detention Center (CoreCivic), 1,033-bed federal prison
- Now: ICE detention under a 6-month no-bid contract opened March 2025
- Revenue: ~$60 million/year for CoreCivic
- Legal fight: Kansas appeals court found CoreCivic needed a new special-use permit (facility had been vacant over one year); city commission voted 4-1 to grant the permit after agonizing public hearings (42 speakers against, 3 in favor)

**Delaney Hall (Newark, New Jersey)**
- Formerly: Community corrections facility
- Now: ~1,000-bed GEO Group detention facility
- Contract: $1 billion (GEO Group)

**South Texas Family Residential Center (Dilley, Texas)**
- Closed in 2024 under Biden policy ending family detention
- Reopened March 2025 for family detention
- Operator: CoreCivic
- Revenue: ~$160–180 million/year
- Capacity: 2,400 beds

**Marana (Arizona)**
- Formerly: Arizona state prison
- MTC (Management & Training Corporation) purchased it for $15 million in 2025
- Being reopened at 775 beds — **over 50% above its intended capacity**

**Louisiana Pattern**
Seven of nine ICE facilities in Louisiana are former state prisons or local jails, many repurposed after the state's sentencing reform freed capacity.

### Conditions at Zombie Prisons

These facilities were typically shuttered because they were old, inadequate, or costly. Reopening them for ICE:
- Skips the typical requirements for new federal facility environmental review
- Allows existing inadequate physical infrastructure to be reused
- Means ICE detainees inherit facilities that failed for their original population

North Lake Michigan is a maximum security prison — not built or designed for civil immigration detention. The use of maximum-security infrastructure for people with no criminal convictions (70.8% of detainees) is a documented structural due process concern.

## 20. ICE Phase History: Mission Creep 2003–2026

### Phase 1: Founding and Counterterrorism Framing (2003–2008)

ICE was created explicitly as a counterterrorism and national security agency after 9/11. The original mandate: combine customs and immigration investigative powers to prevent another attack. Within months, the Bush administration began using ICE for worksite enforcement raids — deporting hundreds of undocumented workers in high-profile operations framed as "national security." The frame was established: immigration violation = security threat.

**Key stat**: FY 2003 budget ~$3.3 billion; primarily focused on criminal investigations.

### Phase 2: Record Deportations (2009–2016)

Obama earned the label **"Deporter in Chief"** from immigrant advocacy groups. This administration institutionalized interior enforcement as routine government function:

- **2.7 million total deportations** across 8 years — more than any prior president
- Peak: approximately **1,123 deportations per day** in 2012
- **Secure Communities Program**: Launched under Bush, made nationwide by 2013. Linked local jail fingerprint scans to DHS immigration databases, turning every local arrest into a potential immigration enforcement action regardless of the underlying charge
- 2014: Obama ended Secure Communities (after massive criticism for deporting non-criminals and families) and replaced with **Priority Enforcement Program (PEP)** — supposedly limiting enforcement to serious criminals

**The key institutional legacy**: Interior enforcement as normalized, permanent federal function — not just border operations.

### Phase 3: First Trump Term — Expanded Interior (2017–2020)

- Ended "sensitive locations" policy formally protecting schools, churches, and hospitals from ICE arrests (eroded from 2017 onward, formally rescinded in 2025)
- Expanded 287(g) agreements
- Expanded family detention (Flores settlement violations led to litigation)
- FY 2019: ICE detained **500,000+** people — a record at the time
- Daily detention population surpassed 50,000

### Phase 4: Biden Pullback (2021–2024)

- Limited interior enforcement priorities (focus on "criminals, national security threats, recent border crossers")
- Daily detention population dropped to ~40,000
- Reduced 287(g) agreements
- ICE arrests fell significantly
- Ended family detention (partially)
- Private prison ban for federal prisons (not enforceable against ICE contracts)

### Phase 5: Second Trump Term — Unprecedented Militarization (2025–present)

- Interior enforcement: 28% → **82% of all detentions** in first 10 months
- Population: 40,000 → 73,000+ (83% surge in one year)
- Budget: $10B → **$85B** (including OBBBA)
- Leadership: Career ICE officials replaced with Border Patrol veterans; "enforcement culture" imported from border operations into city enforcement
- Sensitive locations policy: formally ended January 2025
- 287(g): ~200 agreements → **1,000+ agreements** (5x increase in one year)
- Military: Fort Bliss, Fort Dix, Guantanamo, 6+ other bases
- DOGE/Palantir data surveillance: unprecedented peacetime government data integration for civil enforcement
- Death rate: 11 (2024) → 32 (2025) — deadliest year in two decades

### The HSI Identity Crisis (Ongoing)

An ongoing structural tension: **HSI (Homeland Security Investigations)** wants to formally separate from ICE/ERO. HSI agents investigating drug cartels, human trafficking, and sanctions violations argue that being part of the same agency as immigration enforcement (ERO) damages their ability to recruit informants — witnesses fear ICE contact because it might trigger deportation.

HSI leadership has periodically advocated for formal structural separation. The debate continues as of 2026; if separation occurred, ICE would effectively become a pure enforcement-and-removal agency with no investigative function.

## 21. CECOT & the International Export of Mass Detention

### What CECOT Is

The **Center for Terrorism Confinement** (CECOT) in Tecoluca, El Salvador is the largest prison in Latin America and the explicit model for an authoritarian mass incarceration approach. Built in late 2022, opened January 2023 by President Nayib Bukele:

- Capacity: **40,000 inmates** across 8 cell blocks
- Physical design: 2.4 hectares of cell space averaging **0.6 sq meters (6.5 sq ft) per prisoner**
- 19 guard towers, two sets of 9-meter-tall walls, 60cm thick, electrified fencing, barbed wire, gravel flooring
- Cells: four-level metal bunks, no mattresses or sheets, two toilets, two washbasins
- Zero natural light in many blocks
- Zero rehabilitation programming by design — explicitly punitive-only

### The U.S.-El Salvador Deportation Deal

**February 3, 2025**: Bukele met with Secretary of State Rubio and offered to incarcerate U.S. deportees at CECOT for a fee.

**The deal**: $6 million per year. A per-person daily cost far below any U.S. detention cost.

**March 15, 2025**: The U.S. deported **261 migrants** (mostly Venezuelan) to CECOT. Among them:
- At least 48.8% had **no U.S. criminal history**
- Only 3.1% had been convicted of a **violent offense**
- Some were identified by tatoos alone as potential gang members; courts found this evidence insufficient in multiple cases
- Families in the U.S. could not locate their relatives (the detainees didn't show in ODLS because they'd been deported, not detained)

**Human Rights Watch "You Have Arrived in Hell" (November 2025)**: Documented torture and abuse of Venezuelan deportees held at CECOT under the U.S. arrangement, including beatings, sleep deprivation, and indefinite detention without charge.

### CECOT's Influence on U.S. Design Thinking

The U.S. "mega-center" design philosophy — extremely large facilities (7,000–10,000 people), minimal individual space, centralized control, industrial scale — mirrors CECOT's design approach. GEO Group's CEO publicly stated GEO is "a better deal than CECOT" in pitching ICE contracts. The comparison normalizes CECOT as a reference point for acceptable detention.

Three specific design convergences:
1. **Scale as a tool**: Very large facilities make individual accountability nearly impossible
2. **Isolation as policy**: Remote locations, limited communication, no outside monitoring
3. **Speed over rights**: 3–7 day "processing centers" that move people through faster than any legal process can respond

### The Broader International Pattern

The Trump administration has outsourced detention and deportation to authoritarian governments beyond El Salvador:
- **Panama**: Agreement to house deportees on military bases
- **Guatemala, Honduras, Mexico**: Safe third country and deportation agreements
- This network of allied authoritarian detention reduces legal accountability under U.S. courts while expanding effective detention capacity beyond the numbers tracked in any single system

## 22. Full Legal Strategy Playbook for Community Opposition

### The Detention Watch Network Framework

**Detention Watch Network's #CommunitiesNotCages** campaign (launched 2018, relaunched 2025) identifies three strategic tracks:
1. **Stop expansion**: Block new facilities before they open
2. **Uplift communities**: Center detained people's voices; document and publish conditions
3. **Shrink the system**: Shutdown campaigns targeting operating facilities; end county/local contracts

The campaign explicitly coordinates national strategy while amplifying local organizing efforts. DWN maintains a field team and provides tactical training to local groups.

### What Legal Tools Work — and Why

**NEPA / Administrative Procedure Act challenges** (Maryland Williamsport lawsuit — the current model)
- Federal agencies must conduct environmental review before major federal actions
- Trump administration's rapid warehouse purchases skipped NEPA review entirely
- This is procedural, not substantive — it creates **delay and forces public process**, not permanent block
- Maryland AG lawsuit led to **14-day TRO** in March 2026; broader challenge continues
- Best used: as soon as a warehouse purchase is announced; file immediately

**Existing land-use conditions** (Portland model)
- The 12-hour detention limit at Portland's ICE facility predated the current conflict and applies to all occupants
- Courts distinguish between regulations *specifically targeting federal activities* (preempted) and *generally applicable rules* (defensible)
- Best used: where an existing conditional approval exists; document every violation; issue formal notices

**State prohibition on county participation** (Illinois, California models)
- States can refuse to have their subdivisions participate in federal programs (10th Amendment)
- This is legally stronger than trying to ban federal facilities entirely
- **Illinois Way Forward Act (2021)** is the gold standard: prohibits county jails from contracting with ICE; upheld at district level and Seventh Circuit
- Result: ended immigration detention in Illinois; ICE moved detainees to other states
- **California AB32 (2019)**: banned private immigration detention in California; more legally contested (Supremacy Clause challenges); Ninth Circuit split ruling; ongoing

**Zoning moratoriums** (Kansas City, King County, Seattle, Tukwila)
- Buy time, signal political opposition, create procedural barriers
- Kansas City's 5-year moratorium was enough for the property owner to cancel the sale
- Legally vulnerable if a facility is the clear target; more defensible as neutral rules
- Best used: in the immediate window between announcement and purchase completion

**Procurement/FOIA litigation**
- ICE uses Intergovernmental Service Agreements (IGSAs) to bypass competitive procurement law
- FOIA lawsuits extract internal "road maps" showing planned sites before public announcement
- ACLU Colorado used FOIA to expose ICE's expansion plans before sites were announced publicly
- Transparency enables organizing; not a direct block but a critical intelligence tool

**Permit requirements** (Leavenworth, Portland)
- Building code change from Group S (warehouse) to Group I-3 (institutional detention) legally requires local permits in most jurisdictions
- Leavenworth forced CoreCivic through public hearings; 42 speakers against, 3 for
- Did not ultimately block the facility, but created public accountability and delay

### What Legal Tools Don't Work — and Why

**Direct zoning prohibition of federal facilities**: Courts have consistently held states and localities cannot zone away federal functions. The Supremacy Clause gives the federal government broad authority over land it owns or uses.

**Regulations specifically targeting ICE/detention**: Courts are skeptical of rules that facially target federal immigration activities. Must be neutral and generally applicable.

**Challenging completed federal land acquisitions**: Once DHS purchases a property, legal tools narrow substantially. The property becomes federal land subject to congressional authority under the Property Clause.

**Protest without legal theory**: Political pressure without a specific legal mechanism tends to fail. Effective opposition requires a legal hook — zoning, permits, contracts, litigation, NEPA.

### The "Act Before Purchase" Window

**The single most consistent finding from 2026 opposition efforts**: The highest-leverage window is the period between ICE's site identification and the closing of the purchase. After the federal government owns the property, options contract sharply.

The successful stops in 2026 — Byhalia MS, Oklahoma City OK, Salt Lake City UT, Merrimack NH, Hanover County VA, Kansas City MO — all worked because private property owners decided not to sell. Once sold, all those communities would have been in the same position as Roxbury NJ and the Maryland facility — fighting against completed federal acquisition with limited legal tools.

**Tactical implication**: Opposition networks need early warning systems. ICE pre-solicitation notices, commercial real estate transactions in the right size ranges, local government inquiries — all can provide advance warning before a purchase closes.

### Financial Sector Divestment

A parallel track that avoids federal supremacy entirely: squeeze private prison companies' capital access.

**2019 bank campaign results**: Eight major banks announced they would stop financing GEO Group and CoreCivic:
- JPMorgan Chase, Bank of America, Wells Fargo, Citibank, SunTrust, BNP Paribas, Fifth Third, PNC
- This eliminated approximately **72% of GEO Group's and CoreCivic's private financing** at the time
- University divestment campaigns preceded and enabled the bank pressure

**2026 bank campaign**: Citizens Bank became the primary target for continuing to finance these contractors; protest campaigns active as of March 2026.

**How this works**: Private prison companies need debt financing to build and operate facilities. If banks won't lend to them, their expansion capacity is constrained even when government contracts exist. This is a leverage point that completely sidesteps federal supremacy.

### The NIMBY Coalition — Unexpected Allies

A significant tactical development in 2025–2026 is the emergence of Republican NIMBY opposition to ICE detention facilities. The framing used by successful stops:

| Opposition Frame | Who It Reaches | Example Success |
|-----------------|---------------|-----------------|
| Property tax base loss | Republican officials | Merrimack NH ($500K/year loss) |
| Economic development foreclosure | Business community | Byhalia MS (blocked industrial zone) |
| Infrastructure strain (water, sewer) | Planning officials | Social Circle GA (water permit capacity) |
| Traffic and neighborhood character | Homeowners | Hanover County VA |
| Human rights/conditions | Liberal constituencies | Base mobilization |

**The Vera Institute's conclusion**: The most effective 2026 opposition combined human rights organizing (for moral clarity and base mobilization) with NIMBY economic arguments (to reach Republicans and create bipartisan opposition). Human rights alone rarely stopped facilities. NIMBY alone produces "not here, somewhere else" outcomes without systemic change. Combined, they've been the only approach achieving actual stops.

### The Displacement Problem

The most important limitation of all successful local opposition: **it displaces, not eliminates**. Illinois's law ended ICE detention in Illinois — and ICE immediately transferred detainees to Indiana, Wisconsin, and other states. California's AB32 — if fully implemented — would end private detention in California and move those detainees to Texas, Louisiana, and elsewhere.

Local victories reduce the system's local footprint but do not reduce total system capacity without coordination across jurisdictions. This is why national campaigns (DWN's #CommunitiesNotCages) emphasize *systemic change* alongside local wins.

### ICE Contract Workaround Pattern

When local opposition successfully ends a contract:
- ICE awards a sole-source contract directly to the private operator, bypassing competitive procurement
- Adelanto precedent (2019): When the city ended its IGSA, ICE awarded a **$62 million sole-source contract** directly to GEO Group within days
- The facility continued operating uninterrupted; local control was completely bypassed

This pattern means local-level IGSA contract wins have limited durability unless state law prohibits all such contracts (the Illinois model).
