## PART 1: DOMAIN 41 RESEARCH PLAN

### Central Argument and Gap Analysis

**What Domain 41 Addresses That No Other Domain Does**:

Domain 38 (Financial Sector Independence) covers the macro-institutional layer: Federal Reserve independence, FDIC/SEC regulatory capture, and CFPB structural insulation. Domain 41 addresses the consumer-facing layer that operates at a different scale and with distinct mechanisms.

The consumer financial system functions as an infrastructure of political exclusion through three documented pathways:

1. **Cognitive resource depletion**: Financial precarity consumes cognitive bandwidth in ways that reduce capacity for complex political tasks (voter information processing, community organizing, sustained civic engagement). Research (Mullainathan and Shafir, 2013) documents this mechanism empirically.

2. **Wealth-based civic mobilization suppression**: Political organizing requires material resources (time for canvassing, money for childcare during meetings, transportation to polling places, financial ability to absorb penalties for civic engagement). The racial wealth gap functions as a structural subsidy for white civic participation and a structural tax on Black and Hispanic civic participation.

3. **Financial institution accountability without democratic remedy**: When four institutions (JPMorgan Chase, Bank of America, Wells Fargo, Citigroup) hold $10+ trillion in assets and determine credit access, mortgage approval, and consumer lending terms for the majority of American families, those institutions exercise power more consequential to daily life than most elected governments—without any democratic accountability mechanism.

**The specific mechanisms that make this a democracy problem, not only an economic problem**:

- **Payday lending as disenfranchisement**: Payday loans at 400% APR are geographically and demographically concentrated in Black and Hispanic communities. Payday lending traps families in rollover cycles that both extract wealth *and* consume the cognitive resources needed for political participation.

- **Predatory mortgage lending as wealth extraction**: The 2008 financial crisis transferred an estimated $25 billion in housing wealth from Black and Hispanic families to financial institutions—directly reducing the asset base that could fund civic participation infrastructure (church networks, community organizations, neighborhood schools).

- **CFPB dismantling as accountability collapse**: The CFPB was the single federal institution with both the mandate and the structural insulation (independent funding via Federal Reserve revenues) to provide accountability for the most consequential private power over ordinary economic life. Its systematic dismantling removes the democratic check on financial institution power.

- **Bank concentration and implicit subsidies**: The March 2023 banking crisis (SVB, Signature, First Republic) was a direct product of S.2155 deregulation (raising stress-test thresholds from $50B to $250B). These banks were deemed too-big-to-fail and received $500B in emergency Fed backstops. This implicit subsidy flows to institutions that benefit from market concentration while communities without banking access bear the costs.

#### Phase 4: Reform Architecture (1–2 hours)

**Five-part reform framework**:

1. **CFPB Structural Restoration and Expansion**
   - Restore full enforcement authority (funding mechanism upheld in CFPB v. CFSA 2024)
   - Expand mandate to Buy Now Pay Later, earned wage access, data broker financial profiling
   - Establish community reinvestment mandate (CFPB publish annual lending disparity reports by race/income/geography)
   - Source: CFPB v. CFSA legal opinion; CFPB regulatory authority under TILA, FDCPA, Equal Credit Opportunity Act

2. **Usury Rate Cap (Federal)**
   - Model on Military Lending Act (36% cap for service members)
   - International precedent: UK FCA payday loan cap (0.8% daily = ~290% annual) reduced payday lending 50% without eliminating credit access
   - State precedent: Vermont 18%, Arkansas 17% demonstrate feasibility
   - Legislative hook: Federal Reserve Act authority to regulate consumer credit

3. **Public Banking and Postal Banking**
   - USPS as distribution channel for basic savings and low-cost lending
   - Precedent: Japan Post Bank (128M accounts), Germany Sparkassen (public savings banks, community reinvestment mandate)
   - Target population: 5.9M unbanked households (FDIC 2023)
   - Legislation: Postal Banking Act framework (precedent: 1911–1966 postal banking in US)

4. **Community Reinvestment Act Modernization**
   - CRA (1977) requires banks to meet credit needs of entire service communities
   - Trump-era OCC rules weakened geographic requirements
   - Reform: Restore geographic specificity; extend CRA to fintech lenders and non-bank mortgage companies
   - Advocacy window: CRA modernization is a persistent reform point in congressional agenda

5. **Student Debt Restructuring as Democratic Infrastructure**
   - Frame student debt not as relief but as democratic participation infrastructure
   - Data: Political Research Quarterly (David Macdonald) links student debt to higher voter participation
   - BUT: distribution is highly racialized (Black borrowers 2x default rate of white borrowers)
   - Reform: Income-driven repayment simplification + public service loan forgiveness restoration + targeted relief for heavily-burdened borrower subgroups
   - Advocacy hook: Student debt affects civic capacity; restructuring is democracy work, not only economic relief

**Process**: For each reform, identify one international precedent that demonstrates viability (UK, Germany, Canada, Japan models are strongest). Develop implementation roadmap showing which actors (Congressional, executive, state, civil society) can advance which reforms under different political scenarios.

## PART 2: DOMAIN 43 RESEARCH PLAN

### Central Argument and Gap Analysis

**What Domain 43 Addresses That No Other Domain Does**:

Domain 47 (Housing Security, Displacement, and Democratic Participation) covers the acute end of the housing crisis: eviction, homelessness, and housing assistance collapse. Domain 43 addresses the upstream architectural layer—the design of the housing system itself—that produces both the instability Domain 47 documents and a form of spatial democratic suppression that operates on stably-housed populations.

The housing system functions as a spatial democracy suppression mechanism through four documented pathways:

1. **Exclusionary zoning as designed political segregation**: Single-family zoning, minimum lot sizes, parking minimums, and height restrictions were developed 1920s–1960s explicitly to maintain racial and economic segregation after explicit racial deed restrictions were struck down. The effect is to concentrate low-income, predominantly Black and Hispanic residents in specific zones while protecting high-income predominantly white zones from density and affordability. This segregates political constituencies by design.

2. **Gentrification as neighborhood political displacement**: Gentrification displaces residents physically but also displaces the political infrastructure they built. Black majority-minority neighborhoods that sustained African American political power for generations—producing congressional representation, city council seats, school board control, community institution networks—lose that political infrastructure when demographics shift. This is irreversible within a single electoral cycle.

3. **Housing cost burden as community organizing suppression**: Site-by-site rezonings and public planning hearings have structural features that systematically underrepresent renters and cost-burdened residents. Existing homeowners (who benefit from exclusionary zoning) are geographically concentrated and easily mobilized to attend hearings. Benefits of affordable housing are diffuse among renters, making beneficiaries difficult to mobilize politically.

4. **State preemption as spatial democracy suppression**: 15 states have enacted preemption laws prohibiting cities and counties from adopting tenant protections. State legislatures controlled by rural and suburban homeowner majorities systematically preempt the housing policies that urban tenant majorities have voted for. This is structural bypass of local democratic outcomes by state majorities that do not bear the costs.

**The specific mechanisms that make this a democracy problem, not only a housing problem**:

- **Neighborhood political infrastructure as democratic commons**: The church networks, civic organizations, HBCU alumni associations, ward political clubs that constitute political infrastructure in African American urban areas are built over generations. Displacement destroys this commons, not merely a market transaction.

- **Spatial segregation as democratic gerrymandering**: Exclusionary zoning operates through the private market rather than legislative line-drawing, but the effect is identical: it segregates political constituencies in ways that concentrate their political power in areas with fewest political resources.

- **Local democratic self-governance vs. state preemption**: When tenants in a city vote for rent stabilization through ballot initiative, and the state legislature preempts it, this is not a policy dispute—it is structural bypass of local democratic outcomes.

### Research Roadmap

#### Phase 1: Housing Architecture Deep Dive (1.5–2 hours)

**Primary quantitative sources**:

1. **Exclusionary zoning landscape**:
   - Century Foundation analysis of exclusionary zoning mechanisms and their effect on housing costs
   - Brookings analysis of zoning reform (2023–2024 updates on state-level changes)
   - Regulatory compliance data: What % of metropolitan land area is zoned for multifamily housing? (Answer: national average ~10%; affects price signals and availability)

2. **Rent burden data**:
   - National Low Income Housing Coalition "Out of Reach 2025 report": 21 million renters housing cost-burdened (>30% of income on housing); 12 million severely cost-burdened (>50%)
   - Geographic and demographic distribution of cost burden

3. **Gentrification documentation**:
   - American University SPA "Gentrification and Racial Representation: A Comparative Analysis" (2024)
   - Census Bureau CES-WP-24-29 "Whose Neighborhood Now? Gentrification and Community Life" (2024)
   - Oxford Social Forces "Gentrification in small cities: growth coalitions, civic engagement" (2024)

4. **State preemption landscape**:
   - Ballotpedia preemption law tracking (15 states with active tenant protection preemption)
   - BISC Fairness Project September 2025 report documenting "strikingly similar arguments" across states

5. **Gentrification political displacement mechanism**:
   - Document specific neighborhoods where gentrification displaced established political leadership (DC Shaw/U Street, Atlanta, etc.)
   - Track changes in elected officials' race/residence before/after gentrification

#### Phase 2: Problem Statement Deep Dive (1–1.5 hours)

**Section 1: Exclusionary Zoning as Designed Political Segregation**
- Zoning history: Euclid v. Ambler (1926) upheld zoning; subsequent 1920s–1960s development explicitly used zoning for racial/economic segregation
- Mechanism: Single-family zoning → minimum lot sizes → parking minimums → height restrictions → cost increase → supply restriction
- Political effect: Zones low-income, high-barrier-to-participation constituencies while protecting wealthy zones
- Quantify: What % of metro area is zoned for affordability? (Usually < 10% in restrictive markets)
- Data: National Low Income Housing Coalition rent burden statistics (21M cost-burdened renters)

**Section 2: Gentrification as Neighborhood Political Infrastructure Destruction**
- Document the mechanism: Prior residents → new residents → demographic shift → political representation shift → loss of established political infrastructure
- Case studies: DC Shaw/U Street (documented Black political power loss), Atlanta neighborhoods, Bay Area East Bay communities
- The irreversibility: Once displacement occurs, dispersed former residents cannot recreate neighborhood political infrastructure in dispersed locations
- Data: American University, Census Bureau, Oxford Social Forces studies

**Section 3: Housing Cost Burden as Community Organizing Suppression**
- Mechanism: Cost-burdened renters work multiple jobs → structural time deficit for civic engagement → reduced ability to attend planning hearings and organize
- Structural disadvantage: Existing homeowners (who benefit from exclusionary zoning) are geographically concentrated and easily mobilized. Renters (who benefit from affordable housing) are diffuse and hard to mobilize.
- Data: National Civic League analysis documenting "instability affects civic engagement"; renters have lower participation rates than homeowners
- Emerging counter: Tenant Union Federation (formed 2024) as response to this structural disadvantage

**Section 4: State Preemption as Spatial Democracy Suppression**
- Mechanism: City/county passes democratic outcome (rent stabilization ballot initiative) → state legislature preempts → local democratic will overridden
- 15 states documented with preemption laws (Ballotpedia, BISC tracking)
- The coordination evidence: BISC and ProPublica document "strikingly similar arguments" across states—"out-of-state interests," "fraud prevention," "unelected special interests overriding representative government"
- Democratic failure: Rural/suburban state majorities override urban tenant majorities without bearing costs of their decisions

**Process**: Organize literature by section; verify all claims with primary sources.

#### Phase 3: Democratic Angle Development (45 minutes)

**Four democratic mechanisms distinct from Domain 47**:

1. **Spatial segregation of political constituencies** (operational like gerrymandering)
   - Exclusionary zoning concentrates low-income, high-barrier-to-participation constituencies in specific zones
   - In single-member district systems, this concentrates their political power in areas with fewest political resources
   - Effect is similar to partisan gerrymandering but operates through private market rather than legislative line-drawing

2. **Neighborhood political infrastructure as democratic commons**
   - Black political power in urban areas is built infrastructure: church networks, HBCU alumni, ward political clubs, community organizations
   - Gentrification's displacement destroys this infrastructure in a way that purely economic displacement alone would not
   - This is an irreplaceable democratic asset that the framework should protect like it protects voting rights or civil society

3. **Housing cost burden as political participation tax**
   - Cost-burdened families have structural time deficit for civic engagement
   - This is not a choice problem; it is a time-availability problem that operates independently of political motivation

4. **Local democratic self-governance vs. state preemption**
   - When local majorities vote for housing policies and state legislatures override those outcomes, this is democratic accountability failure
   - The mechanism (state preemption) is distinct from the outcome (bad housing policy); the democracy problem persists regardless of policy merits

**Process**: For each mechanism, identify the empirical evidence that documents it (survey data, historical case studies, etc.).

#### Phase 4: Reform Architecture (1–1.5 hours)

**Five-part reform framework**:

1. **Zoning for Democracy** (Federal/State requirement)
   - Model: California Housing Element law
   - Mechanism: Tie federal infrastructure/community development funding to municipal zoning reform requirements
   - Not a federal takeover of local zoning; it is a conditionality framework aligning infrastructure investment with democratic access
   - Precedent: Biden-era HUD competitive grants for zoning reform (should be codified as statutory framework)

2. **Restoration of Affirmatively Furthering Fair Housing (AFFH)**
   - AFFH rule was rescinded January 2025
   - Reform: Restore AFFH with explicit requirement to assess civic participation effects of housing segregation (not merely housing effects)
   - Establish Civic Participation Impact Assessment requirement in HUD funding reviews
   - This would be first time federal housing policy explicitly addresses democratic design

3. **Anti-Displacement Protections and Preemption Protection**
   - Federal protection for local tenant protections: municipalities that enact rent stabilization, just cause eviction, or source-of-income discrimination bans through democratic processes should be protected from state preemption
   - This is the housing-specific version of the anti-preemption principle in Domain 33
   - Legislation: Prevent state legislatures from overriding local democratic outcomes on housing policy

4. **Tenant Organizing Infrastructure Funding**
   - Community Services Block Grant expansion to explicitly fund tenant organizing capacity in cost-burdened communities
   - Model: Labor movement recognition that collective bargaining requires organizing infrastructure
   - Infrastructure to scale: Tenant Union Federation (founded 2024) + state-level tenant networks
   - Funding should enable organizing on zoning, eviction, displacement issues

5. **Civic Participation Impact Assessments for Large Displacement**
   - Any development project receiving federal subsidy or approvals that will displace 50+ households must conduct Civic Participation Impact Assessment
   - Document projected effects on voter registration, community organization networks, neighborhood political representation
   - Analogous to Environmental Impact Statement framework under NEPA
   - Creates legal hook for organizing against displacement and documentation requirement making democracy consequences visible in approval record

**Process**: For each reform, identify one international precedent and one domestic state precedent that demonstrates viability.

#### Phase 5: International Precedent Integration (30 minutes)

**Models to integrate into Section 5**:
- **Vienna social housing model**: 60% of residents in subsidized or public housing; prevents spatial concentration of low-income populations; maintains economically integrated neighborhoods with high civic participation across income levels
- **Singapore HDB**: 80% homeownership rate through public development; demographic integration requirements prevent ethnic enclave concentration
- **Netherlands anti-gentrification protections**: Municipal preemption rights on housing sales to protect neighborhood composition
- **Montreal Community Land Trust model**: Permanent affordability through land trust structure preventing gentrification displacement

**For each precedent**: Document how it addresses the democratic participation dimension specifically.

#### Phase 6: Cross-Domain Connections and Advocacy Windows (30 minutes)

**Cross-references to existing domains**:
- **Domain 47 (Housing Security)**: Domain 47 covers eviction/homelessness/assistance collapse (acute end); Domain 43 covers upstream architectural layer
- **Domain 33 (State Legislative Autocratization)**: State preemption of local tenant protections is a case study in autocratization; Domain 43 provides housing policy instantiation
- **Domain 22 (Racial Justice)**: Exclusionary zoning's racial history and ongoing segregation effects
- **Domain 1 (Voting Rights)**: Residential instability reduces voter registration; Domain 43 shows how housing system design creates instability
- **Domain 41 (Consumer Financial Architecture)**: Predatory mortgage lending is the financial mechanism of housing wealth extraction; Domain 43 addresses spatial/political consequences
- **Domain 54 (Criminal Justice)**: Grants Pass v. Johnson (June 2024) authorized anti-camping enforcement; this is endpoint of spatial displacement pathway Domain 43 traces
