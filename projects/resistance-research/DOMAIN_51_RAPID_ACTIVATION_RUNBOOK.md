## Context: Campaign Finance and Dark Money Policy Solutions Space

For users unfamiliar with the full Domain 51 policy context, the following summary covers the essential conceptual landscape.

**The three-tier reform architecture**:

Tier 1 (constitutional and actionable now): State disclosure requirements modeled on Arizona Proposition 211 (passed 72% in 2022). Citizens United struck down spending limits but not disclosure requirements. "Look-through" disclosure — requiring 501(c)(4)s to trace and disclose the donors who funded contributions to super PACs — is the structural innovation. Currently in litigation in Arizona; Hawaii SB 2471 takes a different approach (charter revocation). States with ballot initiative infrastructure (California, Colorado, Massachusetts, Nevada, Oregon, Washington) are the primary near-term targets.

Tier 2 (near-term, does not require overturning Citizens United): Small donor public matching programs. New York City's 6:1 match for contributions up to $250 is the model. Does not restrict private spending; amplifies small donors to create competitive parity. The Federal "Government By the People Act" would extend this to federal races. California Fair Elections Act (SB 42) establishes the legal authority for public matching programs at the state level.

Tier 3 (long cycle): Constitutional amendment. At least 22 states have passed resolutions supporting an amendment to overturn Citizens United. Without constitutional change, every disclosure requirement and public matching program can be reversed by future courts extending Citizens United logic.

**The FEC enforcement collapse significance**:

The FEC's bipartisan design (6 commissioners, no more than 3 from any party, 4 votes required for action) was intended to prevent partisan weaponization. The Trump administration attacked this through structural means rather than directional means: by firing Ellen Weintraub and allowing the commission to fall below quorum, the administration has achieved an outcome more favorable than partisan weaponization — complete enforcement paralysis. This removes all deterrence against dark money violations in the run-up to the most expensive midterm in US history.

**Why DISCLOSE Act advocacy matters even when passage is blocked**:

Every forced floor vote on the DISCLOSE Act produces a clean voting record for use in competitive Senate races. The August-September pre-election period is the window when this voting record converts from legislative advocacy to electoral accountability. Organizations running ads in competitive states need this record before FEC-imposed blackout rules apply.
