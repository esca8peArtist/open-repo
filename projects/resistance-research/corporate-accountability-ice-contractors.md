## 1. Why Economic Pressure Works

Corporate actors respond to economic incentives. Political and moral pressure alone rarely changes behavior — but financial consequences do. The detention industry's dependence on a specific set of capital relationships (banks, institutional investors, government contracts, public market access) creates multiple leverage points that organized campaigns can target.

### The Logic of Economic Pressure

**Private detention companies are uniquely vulnerable** because:

1. **Government contracts are their only product.** GEO Group and CoreCivic don't sell anything to the public — 100% of their revenue comes from government contracts. This makes them susceptible to political pressure on procurement in ways consumer companies are not, but also means they are insulated from direct consumer boycott. The pressure must go through intermediaries: investors, banks, and political actors who approve contracts.

2. **They are publicly traded.** Public market listing means they need institutional investors, analyst coverage, and access to debt markets. Each of these can be pressured or withdrawn.

3. **They are highly leveraged.** Private prison companies carry substantial debt loads and pay high dividends (many operated as REITs). This makes access to capital markets existential, not optional.

4. **Reputational damage has business consequences.** Employee recruitment, partnership relationships, and political support all depend partly on reputation.

### What "Success" Looks Like

Economic pressure campaigns rarely achieve immediate contract termination. Realistic goals, in escalating order:

- Raise cost of capital (bond spreads widen, stock falls)
- Force disclosure (ESG reporting, contract transparency)
- Trigger internal policy review (bank committees, investor ESG reviews)
- Achieve divestment from specific actors (banks, pension funds, endowments)
- Create political conditions that lead to contract non-renewal
- Achieve facility closure or contract termination

The 2019 campaign achieved levels 1-4 and contributed meaningfully to conditions that constrained GEO Group's and CoreCivic's financing for multiple years.

Understanding money flows is essential for targeting pressure effectively.

```
Federal Budget (DHS/ICE) → Government Contracts → Private Companies
                                                          ↓
                          Institutional Investors → Stock/Bond Purchases
                                                          ↓
                          Banks → Credit Lines, Term Loans, Bond Underwriting
                                                          ↓
                          Pension Funds/Endowments → Institutional Stock Holdings
```

|---|---|---|
| GEO Group | ~$1.8B | Detention operations + construction |

GEO Group is the largest private prison company in the United States, headquartered in Boca Raton, Florida. Founded 1984 (as Wackenhut Corrections Corporation, renamed 2003). They operate correctional facilities, immigration detention centers, and "residential reentry" (halfway house) programs.

In 2025, GEO Group received a $313 million contract through a subsidiary to convert a warehouse in Surprise, Arizona into an ICE mega-facility. This project is notable because:
- It uses subsidiary contracting to obscure corporate responsibility
- It represents the "warehouse prison" model — rapid conversion of industrial space to detention

**Debt Exposure:**
GEO Group carries approximately $2.6 billion in long-term debt (as of late 2025 filings). Interest on this debt is a major cost. Their access to credit markets — and the interest rate they pay — is directly affected by perceived risk and institutional willingness to hold their paper.

**Annual Meeting:**
GEO Group's annual shareholder meeting is typically held in May. Shareholder resolutions must be submitted 120 days before the meeting. Watch SEC EDGAR for proxy statement (DEF 14A) filings in Q1.

### Political Connections

GEO Group is among the most politically active private prison companies. Tracking data (OpenSecrets, FEC filings):

- **GEO Group PAC**: Regular six-figure contributions to congressional candidates, predominantly Republican but historically bipartisan
- **Lobbying spend**: Millions annually; lobbyists include former DHS officials
- **Key lobbying targets**: DHS appropriations, mandatory detention statutes, contract renewal legislation
- **George Zoley personal contributions**: Substantial direct contributions to aligned candidates

The company was banned from federal lobbying under an Obama-era executive order (2016), then the ban was lifted in 2017 under Trump. The revolving door between GEO Group and DHS/ICE is well-documented.

### Specific Actions

**For individuals:**

1. **If you hold pension assets:** Contact your pension fund's investment officer. Ask whether they hold GEO Group stock. Request divestment on human rights grounds. State pension funds are particularly responsive to organized constituent pressure. Find your pension fund's ESG/responsible investment contact.

2. **If you are a university affiliate:** Contact your university's endowment investment committee. Push for divestment from GEO Group specifically, or from private prison companies as a category. Student campaigns have preceded institutional divestment decisions (see Section 9).

3. **Attend the annual meeting (or submit written comments via proxy):** Shareholder meetings are public for registered shareholders. Buy one share, register it, and you have the right to attend and speak.

4. **Support or co-file shareholder resolutions:** Contact As You Sow (asyousow.org) about active resolutions. You can co-file resolutions on transparency, human rights risk disclosure, or executive compensation if you hold qualifying shares.

5. **Contact GEO Group's banking relationships:** Research their current lead banks via SEC filings (look for credit facility lenders in 10-K footnotes). Write to those banks.

6. **Contact local elected officials** who represent jurisdictions where GEO facilities operate. Local opposition creates zoning, permitting, and regulatory friction.

**For organizations:**

- Coordinate pension divestment campaigns through Detention Watch Network and In the Public Interest
- File public records requests for local contract terms
- Support litigation against specific facilities (ACLU, National Immigrant Justice Center are active)

**Debt Structure:**

- **Lobbying spend**: Millions annually, targeting DHS appropriations and mandatory detention policy
- **Tennessee political connections**: Deep roots in Tennessee Republican politics given HQ location
- **Key issue lobbying**: Mandatory detention statutes, contract competition rules, criminal justice privatization

CoreCivic changed its name from Corrections Corporation of America (CCA) in 2016 specifically to distance itself from activist campaigns. This is a red flag, not a clean slate — the company and its practices are the same. Some campaign materials still use CCA for clarity with older audiences.

3. **File shareholder resolutions** on: independent human rights audits, disclosure of complaint data and outcomes, executive compensation linked to human rights compliance.

4. **Contact bondholders.** CoreCivic issues corporate bonds. Major bond fund managers (PIMCO, T. Rowe Price, Fidelity's bond division) may hold these. Bond holder pressure is less organized but possible.

5. **Support ACLU and RAICES litigation** targeting the Dilley facility specifically.

## 5. GardaWorld Federal Services

### Who They Are

GardaWorld Federal Services is the US federal contracting subsidiary of GardaWorld, a Canadian private security company headquartered in Montreal, Quebec. GardaWorld (full name: GardaWorld Corporation) is privately held, majority-owned by private equity firm BC Partners (UK-based).

This corporate structure is critical for understanding pressure strategies.

**Key contracts:**
- $313 million contract to renovate Surprise, AZ warehouse into ICE mega-facility (2025)
- Guard services at "Alligator Alcatraz" (Everglades detention camp, FL) and other facilities
- Growing footprint in federal detention infrastructure

**Why GardaWorld is a distinctive target:**

1. **Canadian ownership** creates political leverage that does not exist for US-only companies. Canadian civil society, media, and political actors can be engaged.

2. **BC Partners (private equity owner)** is answerable to its own limited partners — pension funds, endowments, and sovereign wealth funds that invested in BC Partners funds. These LPs can be pressured separately.

3. **No public market listing** means stock price pressure doesn't apply, but LP pressure does.

4. **Security services (vs. prison operations)** means GardaWorld has other clients — law enforcement agencies, corporations, airports — whose concerns about association can be leveraged.

### Corporate Structure

```
BC Partners (UK private equity)
    ↓ majority owner
GardaWorld Corporation (Montreal, QC — private)
    ↓ subsidiary
GardaWorld Federal Services (US federal contracting arm)
    ↓ federal contracts
ICE detention guard services + facility renovation
```

### Pressure Points

**Canadian angle:**
- Contact Canadian members of parliament, particularly those representing Montreal-area ridings (GardaWorld HQ is in Lachine, QC, near Montreal)
- Canadian media (Globe and Mail, CBC, La Presse) have covered GardaWorld's US detention involvement
- Canadian unions (GardaWorld's workforce is partly unionized in Canada) may be interested
- Canadian pension funds that are LPs in BC Partners funds

**BC Partners pressure:**
- BC Partners' LPs include major institutional investors. Identifying which pension funds invested in BC Partners funds (which own GardaWorld) is possible through LP disclosures and fund documents
- Contact BC Partners' investor relations team directly
- European institutional investors (BC Partners is UK-based) may be subject to stronger ESG standards

**US-side pressure:**
- File FOIA requests for the Surprise, AZ contract terms
- Contact Surprise, AZ city council regarding zoning and community impact
- Support litigation challenging the Surprise facility's rapid approval

### Specific Actions

1. **If you are Canadian or have Canadian connections:** Write to your MP. Contact Canadian civil society organizations (Canadian Civil Liberties Association, Amnesty International Canada, No One Is Illegal).

2. **Contact journalists:** The Canadian angle is under-covered in US media. Pitch the story to Canadian outlets.

3. **Research BC Partners LPs:** CalPERS, university endowments, and European pension funds may have invested in the BC Partners fund that owns GardaWorld. If your institution is a BC Partners LP, you have leverage.

4. **Zoning and permitting challenges:** The Surprise, AZ facility conversion may be subject to local zoning review. Engage Surprise city council and Maricopa County.

### Who They Are

Palantir Technologies is a data analytics and AI company founded in 2003 by Peter Thiel, Alex Karp, and others. Headquartered in Denver, Colorado (moved from Palo Alto in 2020). They build data integration and analysis platforms primarily for government and large enterprise clients.

**Key contract: ImmigrationOS**

In 2025, Palantir was awarded a $30 million contract to build "ImmigrationOS" — an AI-powered surveillance and targeting system for ICE. The system aggregates data from multiple sources to identify, track, and prioritize deportation targets.

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| ImmigrationOS contract | $30 million (initial) |
| Total government revenue | ~$1.1 billion (2025 est.) |
| Stock Ticker | NYSE: PLTR |
| Market cap | ~$180-220 billion (highly volatile) |

| Co-founder | Peter Thiel |

**Why ImmigrationOS matters beyond the dollar amount:**
The $30 million contract is small relative to Palantir's total revenue, but the implications are significant:
- AI-powered mass surveillance targeting immigrant communities
- Data integration across law enforcement databases
- Automated prioritization of deportation targets
- Infrastructure that scales — the system built for $30M processes millions of records

### Financial Leverage Points

**Palantir's investor base is different from GEO/CoreCivic:** It has a much larger market cap (~$200B), significant retail investor base, and institutional holders that include many tech-sector ESG-focused funds. The company went public via direct listing in 2020.

**Share structure:** Palantir uses a multi-class share structure. Founders (particularly Peter Thiel and Alex Karp) retain outsized voting rights. This limits the direct effectiveness of institutional shareholder pressure on governance but does not eliminate it.

**Institutional holders include:** Vanguard, BlackRock, and many tech-focused funds. These institutions have ESG policies that can be invoked.

**The tech sector pressure dynamic:** Unlike the private prison industry, Palantir operates in the broader technology ecosystem — it recruits engineers, competes for talent, attends industry conferences, and has relationships with other tech companies. Reputational pressure in the tech sector operates differently than in the financial sector.

### Specific Actions

**Employee/talent pressure (highest leverage for tech companies):**
1. **If you are a current or prospective Palantir employee:** Employee organizing has been effective at other tech companies (Google, Amazon, Microsoft) in blocking specific government contracts. Palantir employees have previously raised concerns about surveillance contracts.
2. **Contact Palantir employees** through LinkedIn, Twitter, and professional networks. Many tech workers are politically engaged. Information about ImmigrationOS conditions on the ground (facility abuse reports, deaths in custody) can be shared.
3. **Boycott Palantir recruitment:** If you are a hiring manager, researcher, or in tech education, declining to recruit from or refer candidates to Palantir creates talent pipeline pressure.

**Investor pressure:**
1. **Contact your fund managers** about Palantir holdings. Tech-focused ESG funds should be asked whether they consider government surveillance contracts in their screening criteria.
2. **Shareholder resolutions:** File or support resolutions on human rights impact assessments for government contracts, transparency about contract terms and customer oversight, and AI ethics review processes.
3. **Annual meeting:** Palantir's annual meeting is typically in May. Proxy access and shareholder resolution thresholds apply (own $2,000 in shares for 3 years, or $25,000 for 1 year).

**Research and disclosure:**
1. **Submit FOIA requests** for ImmigrationOS contract terms, deliverables, and any impact assessments.
2. **Support investigative journalism** covering the technical capabilities and civil liberties implications of the system.

**Coalitions with existing tech accountability organizations:**
- Fight for the Future
- Electronic Frontier Foundation
- Mijente (has run campaigns against tech companies enabling ICE)

### Campaigns Already Running

**Mijente's #NoTechForICE campaign** (notechforice.com) has run sustained pressure on tech companies contracting with ICE since 2018, including targeting Palantir specifically. They are the most sophisticated existing campaign structure for tech sector pressure.

## 8. Banking and Finance: Current Targets

### The Post-2019 Financing Landscape

The 2019 divestment campaign (detailed in Section 9) eliminated most of the major US banks from the private prison financing ecosystem. This created a gap that was filled by:
- Smaller regional banks
- International banks not subject to the same US activist pressure
- Direct bond markets (institutional investors buying bonds)

### Current Active Banking Targets

**Citizens Bank** is the primary current campaign target for continuing to provide financing to private prison companies after the 2019 wave of bank withdrawals. Citizens Bank is headquartered in Providence, Rhode Island.

**How to pressure Citizens Bank:**
1. If you bank with Citizens Bank, contact them directly and state you are considering moving your accounts due to private prison financing. Account closure (or credible threat) is the most direct consumer pressure.
2. Contact Citizens Bank's investor relations team about ESG and human rights risk.
3. Contact your state's banking regulator if you are in a state where Citizens Bank operates (primarily Northeast US).
4. Amplify pressure through social media campaigns targeting Citizens Bank on this specific issue.

### How to Identify Current Bankers

Private prison companies disclose their credit facility lenders in their annual reports (10-K filings). Specifically:
1. Go to SEC EDGAR (sec.gov/cgi-bin/browse-edgar)
2. Search for GEO Group (ticker: GEO) or CoreCivic (ticker: CXW)
3. Pull the most recent 10-K annual report
4. Navigate to the "Long-Term Debt" or "Credit Facility" section in the notes to financial statements
5. Banks listed as lenders in the credit facility are current financing partners

This process can be done by anyone with internet access and requires no specialized knowledge. Do this before any banking pressure campaign to ensure your target is current.

### Bond Market Pressure

Both GEO Group and CoreCivic issue corporate bonds traded on public markets. Major bond fund managers hold these bonds:
- PIMCO
- Fidelity (bond division)
- T. Rowe Price
- Various insurance company fixed income departments

Bond fund managers are less organized targets for public campaigns but are accessible through institutional investor relations channels. If your pension fund, endowment, or insurance company holds private prison bonds, that is a pressure point.

## 9. The 2019 Bank Divestment Campaign: Full Case Study

### Background

Between 2016 and 2019, a coordinated campaign succeeded in convincing the largest banks in the United States to end their financing relationships with GEO Group and CoreCivic. This campaign eliminated approximately **72% of GEO Group's and CoreCivic's private bank financing** and represents the most successful economic pressure campaign against the private detention industry to date.

### Timeline

**2016-2017:** Following family separation coverage and reports of conditions in private facilities, immigrant rights organizations begin systematically documenting bank relationships with private prison companies using SEC filings.

**2018:** Mijente publishes "#WhosMakingMoneyOffICE" report documenting corporate relationships. Academic researchers and journalists begin tracking bank relationships systematically.

**2018-2019: University campus campaigns emerge as critical infrastructure.** Students at dozens of universities campaign for their endowments to divest from private prison stocks and for their banks to end financing relationships. These campaigns:
- Created sustained media attention
- Demonstrated organized constituent pressure
- Gave bank compliance and ESG teams internal justification for policy review
- Generated political cover for bank executives to make changes

**2019: The cascade:**

| Bank | Action | Date |
|---|---|---|
| JPMorgan Chase | Announced it would exit private prison financing | March 2019 |
| Wells Fargo | Committed to phase out private prison loans | June 2019 |
| SunTrust | Announced exit | June 2019 |
| BNP Paribas | Announced no new financing | June 2019 |
| Bank of America | Announced it would no longer finance private prison companies | June 2019 |
| Citibank | Announced policy against private prison financing | July 2019 |
| Fifth Third | Announced exit | July 2019 |
| PNC | Announced exit | August 2019 |

All eight announcements occurred within six months of each other — a cascade effect where each bank announcement made the next easier and the remaining holdouts more visible.

### Why It Worked: Analysis

**1. Specific, verifiable ask.** The campaign had a clear demand: end financing relationships with GEO Group and CoreCivic. This was binary — a bank either did it or didn't. Vague asks ("be more responsible") are much harder to win.

**2. Documented evidence.** SEC filings provided exact evidence of financing relationships. Campaigns could say "JPMorgan Chase provided $135 million in credit facilities to GEO Group" — not allegations, documented public records.

**3. Campus campaigns provided sustained pressure infrastructure.** University students are a permanent constituency for banks (future customers, employees). Campus campaigns created institutional pressure that ran for months or years rather than weeks.

**4. Each bank had a different vulnerability.** JPMorgan's vulnerability was its large consumer customer base and progressive employee base. BNP Paribas had European regulatory concerns. SunTrust had community banking reputation. Campaigns adapted messaging to each institution.

**5. The cascade effect.** The campaign explicitly used each divestment announcement to pressure remaining holdouts: "Bank of America has divested. Now Wells Fargo must explain why they haven't." This mechanism — making laggards visible — is essential.

**6. Timing aligned with media attention.** The 2018-2019 period saw intense media coverage of family separation, detention conditions, and deaths in custody. Banks were making decisions in a context where the reputational risk of association was unusually high.

### What the Campaign Did Not Achieve

- Contract termination (impossible via bank pressure alone — contracts are with the government)
- Complete elimination of financing (smaller banks and bond markets filled some gap)
- Immediate stock price collapse (though access to credit did tighten)
- Policy change at GEO Group or CoreCivic

### Lessons for 2026 Campaigns

The 2019 campaign succeeded in a specific political moment. The 2025-2026 political environment differs in important ways:
- Current administration is actively supportive of private detention expansion
- Media coverage is voluminous but public attention is fragmented
- Some banks that divested in 2019 may be reconsidering

This means: **campaigns need to focus on institutions with specific vulnerabilities rather than expecting a broad cascade.** University endowments, state pension funds, and European-owned financial institutions may be more responsive than US commercial banks in 2026.

## 10. Historical Precedent: What Makes Campaigns Win

### The Montgomery Bus Boycott (1955-1956): Structural Analysis

The Montgomery Bus Boycott succeeded and is worth studying not as inspiration but as a structural model for economic pressure.

**Conditions present:**
1. **Clear, specific, achievable demand:** Desegregation of bus seating — not "end racism," not "reform the city," but one specific, binary policy change at one institution.
2. **Concentrated economic impact:** Black residents constituted approximately 70% of Montgomery bus ridership. The boycott imposed immediate, measurable financial losses.
3. **Alternative infrastructure:** The carpool system organized by the MIA gave boycotters an alternative to buses. Economic pressure requires that participants can sustain the action.
4. **Sustained organization over time:** The boycott lasted 381 days. It was not a one-day action. Sustained campaigns require maintained organizational infrastructure, not just initial enthusiasm.
5. **Legal action running in parallel:** The Browder v. Gayle lawsuit pursued simultaneously through the courts. Economic and legal pressure operated together, each reinforcing the other.
6. **Clear endpoint:** The campaign ended when the Supreme Court upheld the district court's desegregation ruling. Participants knew what victory looked like.

**What this means for detention campaigns:**
- Identify the specific binary ask (divestment from company X, end of contract with facility Y)
- Quantify the economic impact (how much revenue is at risk?)
- Build sustained organizational infrastructure, not just viral moments
- Run parallel legal and political campaigns
- Define what victory looks like clearly before you start

### 2019 Bank Divestment: Applied Lessons (see Section 9)

### What Campaign Research Shows About Success and Failure

Academic research on economic pressure campaigns (boycotts, divestment, shareholder activism) identifies several consistent factors:

**Factors associated with success:**
- Specific, binary demand (not gradual improvement)
- Measurable economic harm or credible threat of it
- Sustained pressure over multiple years (not one-time actions)
- Target has a viable path to compliance (the company CAN do the thing you're asking)
- Third-party validation (journalists, academics, other institutions confirming the demand is legitimate)
- Cascade/bandwagon dynamics (each win makes the next easier)
- The target's core business or access to capital is directly implicated

**Factors associated with failure:**
- Vague or aspirational demands
- One-time actions without sustained follow-through
- No path to compliance (demanding something impossible)
- Insufficient economic scale
- Target can absorb the pressure without significant business impact
- Campaign doesn't distinguish between "noise" and organized threat

**Application to 2026 detention campaigns:**

The highest-leverage actions are those that threaten the specific capital access that private prison companies depend on — not general reputational damage (which they can absorb), but specific financing relationships, specific institutional investment policies, and specific political relationships that can be documented and targeted.

## 11. Shareholder Activism: How-To Guide

Shareholder activism is one of the most underutilized tools available to ordinary people with investment accounts. Here is how it works in practice.

### Basics

**You do not need to be wealthy to participate in shareholder activism.** You need to own at least one share of stock. For GEO Group (NYSE: GEO) or CoreCivic (NYSE: CXW), one share costs under $50. Purchasing one share through a brokerage account gives you legal shareholder rights.

### What Shareholders Can Do

**1. Vote on existing resolutions:**
Every year, companies put items to shareholder vote at their annual meeting. Your brokerage will send you proxy voting materials. Voting takes 10 minutes and requires no special knowledge.

**2. Submit questions at annual meetings:**
Registered shareholders can attend annual meetings and ask questions during the Q&A period. This creates a public record and can generate media coverage.

**3. Submit shareholder resolutions (higher threshold):**
To submit a resolution that goes to a vote, you must:
- Own at least $2,000 in shares for at least 3 years, OR
- Own at least $25,000 in shares for at least 1 year
- Submit before the deadline (typically 120 days before the annual meeting)
- Work within SEC rules on resolution content

**4. Co-file resolutions with organizations:**
As You Sow (asyousow.org) and Interfaith Center on Corporate Responsibility (ICCR) run shareholder resolution programs. You can co-file with them, which reduces the threshold burden and provides expert support.

### Types of Resolutions That Have Won at Private Prison Companies

Resolutions seeking:
- Disclosure of human rights risk assessment procedures
- Independent human rights audits of detention facilities
- Executive compensation linked to human rights compliance metrics
- Transparency in lobbying activities (including lobbying for policy that increases detention)
- Board-level oversight of human rights risks

### Resources for Shareholder Activism

- **As You Sow** (asyousow.org): Largest shareholder advocacy nonprofit; runs active campaigns on prison companies
- **Interfaith Center on Corporate Responsibility** (iccr.org): Coalition of faith-based investors with substantial assets and resolution experience
- **SEC EDGAR** (sec.gov/cgi-bin/browse-edgar): All corporate filings, proxy statements, 13F institutional holdings
