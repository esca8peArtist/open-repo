## Constituency 1: State Attorneys General Coalitions

### Leverage Analysis

AGs are the single most consequential institutional actor in Wave 1 of the implementation roadmap. The 22-state Democratic AG coalition has filed 71 lawsuits since January 2025, winning approximately 79% of resolved cases (per Progressive State Leaders Committee tracker as of April 2026). Their leverage rests on four structural advantages: parens patriae standing, existing litigation infrastructure, multistate coordination, and federalism standing theory.

The structural constraint is the Supreme Court's 6-3 shadow docket: even favorable district and circuit court rulings can be stayed by the Supreme Court within days.

### Most Directly Actionable Domains

- **Domain 6 (Judicial Independence)**: Parens patriae standing for amicus briefs in major independence cases; state AGs in Maryland litigated DOGE data access as state harm
- **Domain 1 (Electoral Reform)**: Clearest standing theory for election interference challenges under Article I Section 4
- **Domain 23 (Trade/Tariffs)**: 24-state AG coalition filed in Court of International Trade; SCOTUS *Learning Resources v. Trump* (February 20, 2026, 6-3) struck down IEEPA authority
- **Domain 29 (Prosecutorial Weaponization)**: State-court civil rights actions against same conduct targeted by federal weaponized prosecutions
- **Domain 34 (Congressional Power of the Purse)**: Standing to challenge federal fund impoundment when it harms state-administered cooperative programs

### Year 1 (Distribution + Trust-Building, Months 1-12)

- Months 1-3: Complete litigation triage — map all 71 active cases to domain framework; assign each domain a "lead AG office"
- Months 3-6: Build post-*Slaughter* response architecture — 30-day rapid-response team pre-assigned across 15+ states for state analog legislation
- Months 6-9: Integrate AG coalition into standing coordination infrastructure with Democracy Docket, ACLU Voting Rights Project, Brennan Center
- Months 9-12: 2026 midterm pre-litigation — complete venue analysis for election interference scenarios; pre-write TRO templates

### Year 2 (Legislative + Institutional Action, Months 12-24)

- Post-midterm consolidation using electoral results to recalibrate AG coalition composition
- Expand from litigation-only to legislative advocacy
- Build state-law floor strategy using state constitutional and statutory floors that survive federal rollback

### Year 3+ (Implementation)

- Transition AG coalition from emergency litigation to proactive constitutional architecture
- Support constitutional amendment ratification campaigns through state-level legislative work
- Build interstate compact infrastructure using AG offices as legal drafting resource

### Case Study: IEEPA Tariff Coalition (2025-2026)

24-state AG challenge to IEEPA tariffs is the model for Phase 3 AG deployment. AGs filed in Court of International Trade simultaneously with private plaintiffs; SCOTUS's *Learning Resources v. Trump* ruling validated constitutional theory; CIT ordered refunds. Architecture: multistate simultaneous filing + private plaintiff parallel tracks + academic record pre-staged before argument.

### Constraints

- **Political**: Democratic AGs in competitive states face electoral pressure limiting willingness to join every action
- **Jurisdictional**: Federal prosecution matters have limited AG intervention authority
- **Shadow docket vulnerability**: Every favorable injunction vulnerable to immediate stay by 6-3 Court

### Natural Allies

State legislatures, civil service unions, law school clinics, civil rights coalitions, religious coalitions

### Conflicts

May oppose federal court jurisdiction expansion if it reduces state-court authority; may resist federal minimum standards when those constrain state policy flexibility

## Constituency 2: Civil Service Unions (AFGE, NTEU, AFSCME)

### Leverage Analysis

Civil service unions represent the institutional backbone of Wave 1 resistance. AFGE (1.3 million members) and NTEU (150,000+ federal employees) have immediate stakes in Schedule Policy/Career reversal, MSPB independence, and DOGE-driven workforce reductions. Their leverage is distinct from career staff because unions operate through collective action, electoral mobilization, and congressional lobbying.

AFGE filed suit against Schedule F on inauguration night 2025. The AFGE-AFSCME-AFL-CIO Second Amended Complaint (March 4, 2026) demonstrated sustainable litigation capacity. The Saving the Civil Service Act (H.R. 492/S. 134) has union sponsorship and bipartisan congressional support.

Key constraint: post-*Trump v. Slaughter*, MSPB has been without quorum since May 2025; NLRB union elections fell 30% in 2025. Unions are operating in degraded legal environment for their own organizing.

### Most Directly Actionable Domains

- **Domain 2 (Civil Service)**: Principal plaintiffs in Schedule Policy/Career litigation; Saving the Civil Service Act primary legislative vehicle
- **Domain 6 (Judicial Independence)**: NLRB quorum loss is direct union operational consequence
- **Domain 17 (Labor Rights)**: PRO Act, Sectoral Bargaining pilots, minimum wage legislation
- **Domain 26 (Government Accountability)**: IG system defense directly in AFGE's interest
- **Domain 34 (Congressional Power of the Purse)**: DOGE fund impoundment directly affected union-member jobs

### Year 1 (Distribution + Trust-Building, Months 1-12)

- Months 1-3: Litigation consolidation — ensure AFGE/NTEU/AFSCME-AFL-CIO cases coordinated with consistent legal arguments
- Months 3-6: Congressional testimony mobilization — AFGE members at legislative conference; 10-15 member case studies
- Months 6-9: State affiliate mobilization — AFSCME's 1.6 million state and local workers pressure state legislatures
- Months 9-12: 2026 electoral integration — AFL-CIO's 8 million household mobilization explicitly framed around civil service

### Year 2 (Legislative + Institutional Action, Months 12-24)

- Post-midterm: move Saving the Civil Service Act to floor if House flips or Senate composition improves
- Begin MSPB Reconstruction Act advocacy — converting MSPB to independent Article I court with supermajority confirmation
- Build 2028 amendment campaign infrastructure — unions primary constituency for constitutional civil service protection

### Year 3+ (Implementation)

- Constitutional amendment campaign — civil service unions provide organizational infrastructure for 34-state ratification
- Labor rights restoration once NLRB quorum eventually restored
- MSPB staffing reconstruction and 2,100% case backlog clearance

### Case Study: AFGE inauguration-night litigation strategy (January 20, 2025)

AFGE filed suit against Schedule F within hours of Trump's inauguration. Legal theory drafted before EO signed; facts inserted after; filing complete within inaugural day. Pre-staged standing memos for each domain should be operational standard.

### Constraints

- **Post-Slaughter legal environment**: MSPB without quorum means civil service appeals have nowhere to go; litigation is defensive
- **Resource depletion**: Union membership in federal workforce declined 28% since 2024; dues revenue declining
- **Cross-union coordination**: Civil service unions should maintain separate joint structure from broader AFL-CIO for federal-specific issues

### Natural Allies

State legislators, religious coalitions, labor unions, AGs

### Conflicts

Cross-union trade policy: building trades unions historically supported tariffs; civil service trade enforcement staff now required to administer against WTO commitments

## Constituency 3: Labor Unions (AFL-CIO, SEIU, Building Trades)

### Leverage Analysis

Labor unions represent the largest organized constituency — 12.5 million AFL-CIO members plus 3.8 million SEIU members. Electoral leverage is structural: in swing states, union households represent 20% of electorate. AFL-CIO's 2022 voter mobilization reached 8 million voters; 2026 program targets unprecedented ground game.

AFL-CIO's readmission of SEIU in 2025 consolidated labor movement for first time since 2005. This matters: unified labor can assign domain lead responsibility across 35-domain framework without inter-federation coordination problem.

Key constraint: labor's own organizing rights degraded — NLRB quorum loss stopped certifications; elections fell 30% in 2025; *Slaughter* threatens NLRB independence. Labor simultaneously key constituency and itself under attack.

### Most Directly Actionable Domains

- **Domain 17 (Labor Rights)**: PRO Act, minimum wage legislation, anti-union-busting statutory reform
- **Domain 3 (Economic Justice and Worker Power)**: Sectoral bargaining pilots, worker cooperative expansion, CDFI sector development
- **Domain 11 (Healthcare Access)**: OBBBA Medicaid cuts fall disproportionately on low-income workers who are union members
- **Domain 1 (Electoral Reform)**: AFL-CIO Resolution 14 commits federation to voting rights; SAVE Act's documentary requirements would disenfranchise union households
- **Domain 15 (Environmental Justice)**: Building trades historically diverged on climate; just transition provisions bridge mechanism

### Year 1 (Distribution + Trust-Building, Months 1-12)

- Months 1-3: 35-domain framework education within AFL-CIO political staff and state federation leadership
- Months 3-6: GOTV infrastructure integration — position 2026 midterm explicitly as democratic renewal campaign
- Months 6-9: Post-*Slaughter* response if SCOTUS overrules *Humphrey's Executor* in June 2026
- Months 9-12: State federation advocacy on Domain 17 state-level labor legislation

### Year 2 (Legislative + Institutional Action, Months 12-24)

- Post-midterm: PRO Act first labor priority; but PRO Act alone insufficient — Sectoral Bargaining pilots and NLRB Article I court reform co-equal priorities
- AFL-CIO deploys electoral debt on three priorities: Saving the Civil Service Act, NLRB Independence Act, PRO Act
- Engagement with religious coalitions on economic dignity frame connecting Domains 3, 11, 17

### Year 3+ (Implementation)

- Sectoral bargaining pilots in 3-5 willing states (California, Colorado, New Mexico, New York, Massachusetts)
- Worker cooperative expansion through CDFI sector
- Constitutional amendment campaign — labor's 12.5M+ members and state federation networks essential for any ratification campaign

### Case Study: AFL-CIO bipartisan federal worker vote (2026)

AFL-CIO campaign supporting Protect America's Workforce Act achieved bipartisan House majority and guaranteed floor vote — first legislation to directly overturn Trump administration executive order. Model: identify one legislation with bipartisan appeal, mobilize full labor ground game, achieve floor vote, use record for 2026 electoral accountability.

### Constraints

- **Building trades divergence**: Need bipartisan or active support; just transition provisions in Domain 15 are bridging mechanism
- **NLRB capture**: Primary enforcement infrastructure compromised; state-level organizing should be prioritized
- **Electoral liability**: GOTV investment may not produce commensurate results in Rust Belt; economic messaging more effective than partisan

### Natural Allies

Civil service unions, religious coalitions, state legislators, civil rights coalitions

### Conflicts

Environmental unions diverge on climate transition speed; building trades and immigration advocacy diverge on enforcement; labor and media reform on consolidation concerns

## Constituency 4: Religious Coalitions

### Leverage Analysis

Religious institutions represent 380,000 congregations with 70+ million weekly attendance. Faith communities provide organizing infrastructure, physical meeting spaces, trusted messengers, and cross-racial coalition potential. Historical precedent: Civil Rights Movement operated through Black church network (SCLC); sanctuary movement operated through church networks; fastest growth in explicitly democracy-protecting faith organizing since Civil Rights era.

Current infrastructure: Interfaith Alliance (#LoveNotICE, 18,000 trained volunteers, 100,000 activation target); Interfaith America (Faith in Elections Playbook); Faiths United to Save Democracy (Poll Chaplains in 9 states); Hindu, Buddhist, Sikh, Muslim organizations joining SPLC coalition.

Key constraint: administration's weaponization of religious liberty framing via "Religious Liberty Commission" (challenged by Interfaith Alliance April 2026) attempting to co-opt language while excluding majority faith traditions. Any playbook must contest this frame while remaining genuinely interfaith.

### Most Directly Actionable Domains

- **Domain 7 (Democratic Participation)**: Congregations as voter registration sites, polling locations, civic education hubs
- **Domain 16 (Immigration)**: Sanctuary movement analog; faith communities as legal support hubs, sanctuary spaces, know-your-rights educators
- **Domain 11 (Healthcare Access)**: Faith-based hospitals (Catholic Health Association covers 1 in 6 hospitalized patients nationally) directly affected by OBBBA Medicaid cuts
- **Domain 22 (Racial Justice)**: Black churches organizational infrastructure for HR 40 (reparations commission)
- **Domain 27 (Higher Education)**: 1,200 religiously-affiliated institutions affected by federal funding leverage; institutional autonomy issue

### Year 1 (Distribution + Trust-Building, Months 1-12)

- Months 1-3: Map three distinct faith coalition tracks: (1) electoral democracy (Interfaith America), (2) social justice (social teaching organizations), (3) institutional autonomy (universities/hospitals)
- Months 3-6: Integrate 35-domain framework into faith community civic education using faith lens (hospitals providing emergency care, universities losing funding, congregations surveilled, communities deported without due process)
- Months 6-9: 2026 midterm election protection — expand Faiths United to Save Democracy's Poll Chaplains from 9 states to 20+
- Months 9-12: Legislative advocacy through faith-specific channels — Congressional visits by faith delegations

### Year 2 (Legislative + Institutional Action, Months 12-24)

- Religious coalition testimony in legislative hearings on reform bills
- Faith hospital systems publicly oppose OBBBA Medicaid cuts using institutional authority
- Build interfaith coalition voice on Domain 29 (prosecutorial weaponization) — SPLC case has explicit faith resonance

### Year 3+ (Implementation)

- Faith institutions as anchor institutions for Wave 2 alternative infrastructure
- Constitutional amendment support through state-level chapter networks for 34-state ratification

### Case Study: Civil Rights Movement — Black Church Infrastructure (1955-1965)

CRM's organizational success depended on Black church network providing: physical infrastructure, trusted messenger capacity, financial sustainability, legal coordination, and moral legitimacy. Current coalition needs same five elements. Faith communities provide meeting space and trusted messenger capacity; coordination task is connecting that to legal coordination (AG coalition, law school clinics) and financial sustainability.

### Constraints

- **Internal diversity**: Coalition spans political spectrum; evangelical and conservative Catholic constituencies may share some values while opposing others (reproductive rights, immigration, LGBTQ protections)
- **Political co-optation**: Administration's Religious Liberty Commission attempts to capture frame for narrow subset
- **Capacity fragmentation**: 380,000 congregations powerful in aggregate but require coordination infrastructure; capacity concentrated in metro areas and Black church networks

### Natural Allies

Civil rights coalitions, labor unions, state legislators, civil service unions

### Conflicts

Reproductive rights (Domain 7g will fracture evangelical-mainline); immigration enforcement (bishops more vocal against deportation); religious liberty framing fights

## Constituency 5: Media Organizations (Local News, Press Freedom Organizations, Investigative Outlets)

### Leverage Analysis

Media organizations occupy unique position: simultaneously constituency (Domain 8 reforms benefit directly), implementation vehicle (accurate reporting distributes framework), and vulnerability (consolidation, radicalization, attacks undermine information environment).

Current landscape: U.S. reclassified "With Restrictions" by RSF (2025); 170 journalist assaults (2025); CPB defunding (October 2025) eliminated support for 1,500+ stations. Local news collapse: 2,900+ closures since 2004, 57% newsroom employment decline.

However, media retains leverage: investigative capacity to document institutional capture, public legitimacy to validate framework. Intercept verification of SPLC indictment response, Just Security's Weissmann legal analysis, ProPublica's DOGE documentation examples.

Key constraint: many organizations under financial pressure; CPB defunding removed key funding source; platform dependency creates structural conflict.

### Most Directly Actionable Domains

- **Domain 8 (Information and Media)**: Direct institutional interest — reforms directly benefit; press freedom organizations primary advocates
- **Domain 7 (First Amendment)**: Press freedom first enumerated; 170 journalist assaults create direct standing; PRESS Act protects source confidentiality
- **Domain 29 (Prosecutorial Weaponization)**: Seth Harp subpoena (Venezuela context), SPLC indictment both involve criminal process against organizations investigating executive conduct
- **Domain 21 (Data Privacy)**: Government surveillance of journalists direct issue; EFF FOIA litigation for CMS AI records
- **Domain 36 (AI Governance)**: Algorithmic news distribution affects every outlet; federal AI requirements would give legal access to distribution criteria

### Year 1 (Distribution + Trust-Building, Months 1-12)

- Months 1-3: Investigative mapping — identify 35-domain documentation gaps investigative journalism fills (DOGE Treasury access, CMS AI denial rates, immigration court quality, Schedule Policy/Career implementation)
- Months 3-6: Press freedom coalition building — CPB defunding unified public media, community radio, newspapers, digital natives; Media Power Collaborative coordination
- Months 6-9: Legislative advocacy for Domain 8 provisions (Local Journalism Sustainability Act, Public Broadcasting Restoration Act)
- Months 9-12: 2026 election reporting infrastructure — coordinate with Democracy Docket legal network for election interference documentation

### Year 2 (Legislative + Institutional Action, Months 12-24)

- PRESS Act passage — journalist shield law bipartisan support (blocked by single-senator hold)
- FCC independence legislation — post-*Slaughter*, state analog and federal statutory protection urgent
- Antitrust media merger enforcement — Domain 20 provisions on consolidation

### Year 3+ (Implementation)

- Local news restoration fund modeled on Norway system (direct per-subscriber support)
- Algorithmic accountability law requiring independent audits
- Public media constitutional protection modeled on BBC Charter or ARD/ZDF Rundfunkstaatsvertrag

### Case Study: CPB defunding and rural public media coalition (2025)

CPB defunding (October 2025) eliminated federal support for 1,500+ stations. Aftermath: rural Republican representatives whose communities depend on public radio discovered CPB defunding had concrete local consequences. Template: organizations losing CPB funding not primarily in urban progressive markets but rural/small-city represented by Republican members facing constituent pressure. Coalitional ask: restore specific local services rural constituents depend on.

### Constraints

- **Financial pressure limits advocacy capacity**: Local organizations cannot deploy significant advocacy capacity
- **Independence norm**: Journalism must maintain editorial independence; playbook asks them to report accurately on erosion (legitimating function) and advocate for institutional interests (press freedom, funding, FCC independence)
- **Platform dependency**: Most organizations distributed through algorithmically controlled platforms; dependency on platforms they may criticize

### Natural Allies

Civil rights coalitions, law school clinics, AGs, labor unions

### Conflicts

Labor (publisher-guild disputes ongoing), platforms (media dependent on Facebook/Google advertising while criticizing their dominance)

### Leverage Analysis

Law school clinics represent connective tissue between strategic litigation coalition (AGs, appellate litigators) and academic knowledge production (rapid-response publications, domain research, legislative record). 2025-2026 pressure on clinics directly because they occupy this position. Harvard's Democracy and Rule of Law Clinic, NYU's Constitutional Litigation Clinic, Yale's Rule of Law Clinic provide student supervision, amicus brief production, FOIA litigation, research-to-brief pipeline.

Clinic-specific leverage: accept cases commercial firms cannot (pro bono is institutional purpose); partner with civil rights orgs without conflicts; produce law review articles simultaneously; create self-replicating pipeline of democratic defense attorneys.

### Year 1 (Post-*Slaughter* rapid-response architecture)

### Year 2 (Legislative record-building)

### Year 3+ (Constitutional amendment academic architecture)

## Constituency 7: State Legislators (Deepened from Existing Playbook)

### Leverage Analysis

State legislators ratification infrastructure for constitutional amendments (34 states + 2/3 Congress) and primary Wave 3 structural reform actors. But also Wave 1 and 2: they can enact state civil service protections, Medicaid floor legislation, voting rights protections, independent redistricting commissions operating independently of federal action.

Minnesota 2023 omnibus demonstrates state legislative action in trifecta can produce more change in two years than federal action. 2026 midterms: 26 SOS races, three state supreme court seats determine whether Domain 33 autocratization mechanisms are interrupted or entrenched for 2030 redistricting.

### Year 1-3 Electoral Sequencing

- **Year 1 (2026 electoral prep)**: Priority targets competitive states where Democratic gain produces split legislature or trifecta; align framework's Wave 1 domains (1, 2, 7, 9) with state district arguments
- **Year 2 (2027 sessions)**: In trifecta/chamber-gain states, first priorities: Domain 1 (voting access omnibus), Domain 2 (state civil service), Domain 9 (federalism defense)
- **Year 3 (2028 elections + redistricting prep)**: 2028 elections precede 2030 redistricting by two years; 2028 state elections determine post-census redistricting control; Domain 1f (independent redistricting) amendment campaigns must start 2027

### Case Study: Minnesota 2023 Democracy for the People Act

Democratic trifecta enacted automatic voter registration, pre-registration for 16-17, permanent absentee list, multilingual materials, voter intimidation protections in single omnibus. Lesson: individual provisions vulnerable; omnibus not. Second lesson: sequence voting access before other reform creates electoral conditions for subsequent reform. Any 2026 trifecta should have omnibus pre-drafted August 2026, ready first day 2027 session.

## Constituency 8: Federal Judges (Advisory/Educational Role)

### Leverage Analysis

Federal judges cannot be organized like other constituencies — judicial independence precludes coalition coordination. Role is structural: enforcement mechanism for Domains 1, 2, 6, 7, 29, 34. Leverage point is judicial education and independence defense.

Judicial Conference of United States is policy-making body for federal courts. 2026 impeachment threats against Judges Boasberg and Boardman represent pressure on independence that Conference should formally respond to. Conference's failure to issue defense statement is governance failure addressable through advocacy with Administrative Office.

### Most Directly Actionable Domains

- **Domain 6 (Judicial Ethics)**: Proposed Judicial Ethics Enforcement Act — independent body investigating misconduct with binding referral authority; current self-regulation produced zero sanctions against justices (Thomas gifts, Alito flags)
- **Domain 35 (SCOTUS 2026 Term)**: District/circuit judges writing opinions that make maximum clear legal defects in administration actions, creating academic and precedential record narrowing *Trump v. CASA* universal injunction constraint through APA vacatur

### Constraint

Judicial Code of Conduct prohibits public advocacy; "educational" work primarily through Federal Judicial Center, law clerk hiring (trained clerks surface relevant arguments), and academic conferences organized through law schools rather than advocacy organizations.

## Cross-Constituency Alliance and Conflict Matrix

**Cluster A (Legal Defense)**: AGs + Law School Clinics + Civil Rights Coalitions + Appellate Litigators — share litigation objectives, can coordinate without conflicts
