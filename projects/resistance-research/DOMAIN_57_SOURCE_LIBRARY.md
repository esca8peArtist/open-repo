## Preliminary Evidence Claims (Anchors for the Domain)

These four claims will anchor Domain 57's argument. Each is supported by primary documentation. They should be treated as the domain's core evidentiary commitments.

**Claim 1: The January 7, 2026 withdrawal is the largest single multilateral withdrawal event in any democracy's recorded history.**
In a single presidential memorandum, the Trump administration directed withdrawal from 66 international organizations — 35 non-UN organizations and 31 UN entities. This exceeds any previous single-event withdrawal action by any democracy. Sources: White House Presidential Memorandum (January 7, 2026); Federal Register (January 16, 2026); Al Jazeera full list taxonomy (January 8, 2026).

**Claim 2: The ICC sanctions criminalize domestic civil society cooperation with international accountability mechanisms — a First Amendment problem dressed in foreign policy framing.**
US persons providing "material support or services" to ICC investigations face up to 20 years in federal prison. The sanctions have produced documented chilling effects: US lawyers stopped working on Afghanistan and Palestine accountability cases; NGOs limited public engagement; journalists self-censored. Source: CICC "Criminalising Accountability" report (2026), based on 45 interviews with affected practitioners.

**Claim 3: The constitutional asymmetry — entry requires Senate supermajority, exit requires nothing — is not immutable. Congress has already closed the gap for NATO.**
Section 1250A of the FY2024 NDAA enacted the first-ever statutory constraint on presidential withdrawal from a specific treaty. CRS R48868 (February 27, 2026) confirms that courts are now "less likely to dismiss a challenge to a President's withdrawal from the North Atlantic Treaty in violation of Section 1250A on political question grounds." The reform architecture extends this model. Sources: CRS R48868; FY2024 NDAA Section 1250A; Brookings (Congress's Control Over Treaty Exit).

**Claim 4: Universal jurisdiction is a functioning, growing accountability backstop — not merely a theoretical fallback.**
The Universal Jurisdiction Annual Review 2025 documents 34 new cases opened and 23 convictions secured in European national courts across 20 countries. Germany, France, Sweden, the Netherlands, Belgium, Austria, and Spain maintain active prosecution programs. These courts cannot be sanctioned by US executive order and operate under domestic constitutional authority. Sources: TRIAL International/FIDH/ECCHR/REDRESS/Civitas Maxima, UJAR 2025.
