# Phase 2 Research Roadmap

**Internal planning document — May 6, 2026**

Phase 1 framework is production-ready: 35 domains (Domains 1–29, 19f, 26–28, and subdomains 37a/37b/31x), current through May 1, 2026, with full distribution execution plans waiting on Path A / A+37 / Path B decision. Phase 2 is the research agenda that runs after Phase 1 launches. This document defines what to research, when, how deeply, and how to measure whether it is working.

## 1. Phase 2 Domain Candidates

Seven candidates were identified across two discovery passes (Sessions 507–508, April 27, 2026) plus two scope documents promoted to ready-for-research status. This section ranks all nine candidate domains by the five evaluation criteria, then produces a final Priority Ranking Table.

### Candidate Inventory

All nine candidates fill confirmed gaps — they do not overlap with any of the 35 Phase 1 domains. Each was cross-checked against the full domain list before inclusion.

**Candidate A — Trade Policy and Economic Sovereignty**
The IEEPA Supreme Court ruling (Feb 20, 2026) found the president cannot impose tariffs unilaterally, but the administration pivoted immediately to Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974 (15% tariffs, 150-day window). $170B in IEEPA tariffs were collected before the ruling; the refund question is unresolved. Chinese goods face 145% tariffs. The constitutional question — Congress's Article I trade authority vs. executive economic emergency powers — is live and has no Phase 1 domain coverage. Scope: 4,000–5,500 words.

**Candidate B — Healthcare Access Implementation (OBBBA Medicaid Crisis)**
The One Big Beautiful Bill Act's $911B in Medicaid cuts are producing real-time hospital closures (Greene County IN, St. Mary's Sacred Heart GA, Centra Southside VA). 446 hospitals at risk (Public Citizen). Medicaid work requirements (80 hrs/month) take effect January 2027; HHS must issue implementation guidance by June 1, 2026 — the advocacy window closes in days. Phase 1's Domain 11 covers structural healthcare reform; this domain covers the implementation crisis already in progress. Scope: 4,500–6,000 words.

**Candidate C — AI Governance and Algorithmic Democracy**
Trump's March 20, 2026 national AI policy framework directs agencies to accelerate AI adoption while preempting state-level regulation. FBI confirmed to Congress (April 2026) it purchases commercial location data for surveillance. EFF filed a FOIA suit over CMS's AI-driven Medicaid denial system. No Phase 1 domain addresses AI as democratic infrastructure — when algorithms deny benefits or parole without meaningful human review, who is accountable? Scope: 4,000–5,000 words.

**Candidate D — Disability Rights and Benefits Access**
SSA cut 7,000 jobs (12% of staff), creating documented processing delays for disability claims. The OBBBA work requirement threatens Medicaid coverage for millions with disabilities (40%+ of Medicaid enrollees at risk have three or more chronic conditions, per Yale). The dropped 20%-SSDI-cut rule is a documented advocacy win. Phase 1's Domains 11 and 18 do not address disability as a democratic participation framework. Scope: 3,500–4,500 words.

**Candidate E — Election Administration Seizure and 2026 Midterm Defense**
Trump's March 31, 2026 executive order directed DHS and SSA to compile a nationwide State Citizenship List and instructed USPS to deliver mail ballots only to voters on that list — court partially blocked in April. ProPublica (April 17, 2026) documented two dozen election deniers installed in DHS and EAC positions. CISA's election security workforce was gutted; state officials report the federal-state trust relationship has "shattered." 31 restrictive voting laws enacted in 2025. This domain covers operational election administration defense — distinct from Domain 1's structural electoral reform. The 2026 midterms are six months away. Scope: 4,000–5,000 words.

**Candidate F — Civil Society Suppression as Democratic Infrastructure Attack**
OBBBA Section 112209 grants the Treasury Secretary unilateral authority to designate any nonprofit as a "terrorist supporting organization" and revoke its tax-exempt status — no prior due process, 90 days to prove innocence. The provision is enacted law. Florida HB 1471 (signed April 6, 2026) adds a parallel state-level mechanism. Federal grant freezes disrupted 33% of nonprofits in 2025. The SPLC indictment is a live case study of the DOJ-to-IRS pipeline. No Phase 1 domain addresses the nonprofit sector as democratic infrastructure. Scope: 3,500–4,500 words.

**Candidate G — Tribal Sovereignty and Indigenous Democratic Exclusion**
Trump revoked EO 14112 on tribal self-determination. The Mashpee Wampanoag Tribe's land trust designation was stripped. A Wisconsin BIA office serving all 11 Wisconsin tribal nations closes August 2026. SCOTUS heard Trump v. Barbara on April 1, 2026 — the administration argued "the children of tribal Indians are not birthright citizens." Decision expected late June/early July 2026. No Phase 1 domain addresses 574 federally recognized tribal nations as sovereign governments with treaty rights. Scope: 3,500–4,500 words.

**Candidate 37a — Post-Election Section 3 Litigation and Congressional Certification Recovery**
Scope document complete (domains/domain-37a-post-election-section3-litigation-recovery.md). Covers the post-election phase absent from Domain 37: Fulton County seizure threat model, FCEA contestation mechanism, Section 3 enforcement after Trump v. Anderson, pre-certification TRO strategy. Advocacy window: September–October 2026. Full research: 1–2 sessions.

**Candidate 37b — State Election Security Coordination**
Scope document complete (domains/domain-37b-state-election-security-coordination.md). Covers what states have built to replace CISA, why the coordination gap cannot be closed at state level, and the competitive state vulnerability matrix (AZ, GA, MI, PA, WI). Advocacy window: April–July 2026 — most urgent of all candidates. Full research: 1 session.

**3. Evidence maturity** — nascent research territory vs. mature sourcing

High-maturity (sourcing is authoritative and comprehensive): A (PIIE, CBO, SCOTUS primary), B (CBO, KFF, Urban Institute, hospital closure documentation), E (ProPublica primary investigation, Brennan Center tracking, Votebeat), F (Section 112209 text, National Council of Nonprofits survey data, Stanford Law).

Nascent (advocacy window is ahead of scholarly literature): 37a (constitutional framework clear; post-election scenario is forward-looking), 37b (state-by-state CISA replacement architecture needs primary research with state officials), D (SSA processing delay data is mostly internal; individual impact not systematically tracked).

Candidates C and D are more standalone — they fill gaps not adjacent to Phase 1's high-adoption domains.

37a and 37b strengthen Domain 37 directly and are designed as sequels.

**5. Cross-cutting vs. specialized**

Cross-cutting (broad coalition appeal): E (any election-focused organization, any AG), F (any nonprofit is directly affected), B (rural healthcare audiences, labor, racial justice).

Moderately cross-cutting: A (economic policy, labor, small business), G (Indigenous rights advocates, environmental organizations, constitutional scholars).

### Tier C — Deep Foundational Research (20–40 hours each)

These domains require original research in areas where no single authoritative source provides the synthesis the framework needs.

**Domain G (Tribal Sovereignty)** — estimated 20–25 hours. The treaty-trust-sovereignty triad requires legal history research not present in any Phase 1 domain. The UNDRIP comparative framework requires international law sourcing. The BIA office closure documentation requires tribal government communications that are not centrally indexed. Trump v. Barbara decision (expected June/July) is a natural trigger — begin research after the ruling drops to incorporate it as a primary source. The NARF Tribal Supreme Court Project is the best litigation tracking source. NARF's documentation is comprehensive but requires synthesis.

**Domain C (AI Governance)** — estimated 20–30 hours. The hardest research domain in Phase 2. The core problem: most government AI deployment is proprietary and not publicly documented. Building an accurate picture of which agencies use which AI systems for which consequential decisions requires FOIA research, congressional testimony analysis, and academic literature synthesis. The EFF FOIA lawsuit against CMS provides one window; the FBI data broker confirmation provides another. But the systematic landscape requires investigative research that takes time. **Domain D (Disability Rights)** — estimated 15–20 hours. The SSA staffing cut documentation is mature (CBPP, ProPublica). The dropped SSDI rule is documented. The challenge is the democratic participation framework — connecting benefit loss to civic participation deficits requires original analytical work drawing on disability studies, political science, and public health literature. No existing advocacy organization has made this connection systematically. The Yale chronic conditions data (40%+ of Medicaid-at-risk have 3+ chronic conditions) is the strongest empirical hook. International benchmarks (UK PIP/ESA, German disability policy, Nordic inclusion models) require comparative sourcing. This domain's advocacy window is less acute (2027 implementation), which makes the deeper research timeline appropriate.

## 3. Timeline to Phase 2 Launch

### Can Phase 2 Run Parallel with Phase 1 Execution?

Yes — with a one-week buffer after Phase 1 distribution launch. The recommendation is a 7-day gap for two operational reasons. First, the adoption tracking system (adoption-tracking-dashboard-spec.md) requires Day 7 infrastructure checks before monitoring can be confirmed operational. Second, Tier A updates (Domain 1 Callais section, Domain 33 redistricting cascade) can be completed in the Phase 1 launch week and distributed as updates to Phase 1 adopters — turning what might look like version fragmentation into a signal of ongoing responsiveness.

Phase 2 research can begin concurrently with Phase 1 launch on Domain E (Election Administration Seizure) because the June 15 research completion target is independent of Phase 1 adoption timelines. Domain E's audience overlaps with but is not identical to Phase 1's primary distribution network — election officials, Secretaries of State, and electoral law programs have distinct information needs from the AG and think tank primary audience.

### Earliest Independent Phase 2 Launch

Phase 2 can launch independently of Phase 1 if the distribution path decision is further delayed beyond June 2026. In that scenario, the calendar-driven urgency of Domain E and Domain B justifies initiating research immediately and distributing completed domains as standalone documents to targeted audiences (election officials for Domain E; healthcare advocacy coalitions for Domain B). This is a Path B variant — domain-by-domain distribution before full framework launch — and has the advantage of maintaining momentum if Phase 1 execution stalls.

If Path B is already selected (Phase 2 domain updates before distribution), then Domain E and Domain B research should begin immediately: both documents can be completed within 2–3 sessions and distributed to targeted audiences before June 30, 2026, capturing the HHS guidance window and the pre-midterm election security window.

## 4. Impact Measurement Architecture for Phase 2

### How Do We Know if Phase 1 Shifted Institutional Behavior?

The post-distribution-adoption-framework.md (Section 5) identifies three contribution types: coordination effects, acceleration effects, and coverage effects. Phase 2's impact measurement architecture is designed to detect all three, using the infrastructure already specified in adoption-tracking-dashboard-spec.md as the detection layer.

**The leading indicators Phase 2 should target** are different from Phase 1's adoption indicators. Phase 1 success looks like vocabulary migration and citation events. Phase 2 success looks like institutional behavior change — organizations doing things they were not doing before, at a pace faster than the counterfactual would predict.

**Leading indicator set:**

*Litigation behavior change:* Are state AGs filing briefs that apply Phase 1 domain analysis as their primary legal theory (not just background), rather than citing it as supplementary support? The transition from Tier 2 (vocabulary convergence) to Tier 3 (framework anchoring a litigation theory) is the key Phase 1-to-Phase 2 transition for the litigation sector. Measurable via CourtListener RECAP search for domain-specific coinages in brief text.

*Legislative language adoption:* Are Phase 1 domain reform proposals appearing in specific bill language — not just findings sections but operative provisions? The Brennan Center's automatic voter registration model (Domain 1 parallel) took approximately three years from research publication to first state legislative adoption. Phase 1's most specific proposals (Domain 34 fiscal oversight mechanisms, Domain 37 CISA election security budget requirements) are the most likely to appear in legislative text within the first year. Measurable via LegiScan API and Congress.gov full-text search using domain-specific proposal language as search terms.

*Bridge node second-order effects:* Has any Tier 1 bridge node (Brennan Center, Protect Democracy, Democracy Docket) published a report that explicitly builds on Phase 1 analysis rather than merely citing it? The distinction is structural adoption vs. surface citation — does the Phase 2 publication use Phase 1's analytical architecture as its framework, or just reference it in a footnote? This requires manual qualitative review of Tier 1 publications, not just automated alert detection.

*Curriculum integration signal:* By July 2026 (end of spring semester), have any law school clinics or policy programs confirmed Phase 1 domain use in course materials? The May–June 2026 window is the curriculum design season for Fall 2026. A direct survey of top 25 election law and constitutional law clinic directors (not automated monitoring) is the most efficient detection method.

### Success Metrics Per Domain Type

**Legislative targets (Domain reform proposals appearing in bill language):**
- Leading indicator: legislative staff requesting domain documents directly (not just organizations)
- 90-day signal: committee reports using domain analytical categories
- 6-month signal: introduced bill with domain-specific statutory language in operative section
- 12-month signal: enacted state legislation or federal amendment using domain reform proposal
- Measurement tool: LegiScan API + Congress.gov full-text + direct contact with legislative staff confirmed to have received Phase 1 materials

**Judicial targets (Domain analysis used in litigation):**
- Leading indicator: AG or litigation organization requesting permission to cite the framework in a filing
- 90-day signal: amicus brief using domain coinages in argument section (not just introduction)
- 6-month signal: complaint or brief organizing its theory of harm using domain's analytical structure
- 12-month signal: court opinion citing or engaging with domain analysis in its reasoning
- Measurement tool: CourtListener RECAP + vocabulary coinage search (unique framework phrases) + PACER access for unreported orders

**Organizational adoption targets (Think tanks, advocacy organizations):**
- Leading indicator: organizational staff citing the framework in internal memos or public presentations (detected via social monitoring or direct contact)
- 30-day signal: published report using domain vocabulary in analytical (not merely illustrative) role
- 90-day signal: organization incorporates domain framework into its standard research toolkit or member education materials
- 6-month signal: organization distributes domain materials to its coalition network (cascade depth 2)
- Measurement tool: Overton.io policy citation database (begin monthly searches at Month 4) + Google Alerts vocabulary coinage monitoring + bridge node contact log (cascade depth tracker in adoption-tracking-dashboard-spec.md Component 5)

### Phase 2 Domain-Specific Impact Measurement

Phase 2 domains require their own pre-distribution baselines, built before each domain launches. The baseline-establishment protocol from the adoption-tracking-dashboard-spec.md applies: run targeted searches before distribution, document the current use level, then run comparative searches at Month 3, 6, and 12.

For Phase 2's Tier A update domains (Domain 1 Callais supplement, Domain 33 redistricting cascade), the relevant measurement is whether Tier 1 adopters of Phase 1 Domain 1 and Domain 33 update their own published work to incorporate the new analysis — this is a "cascade update" signal indicating deep rather than surface adoption.

For Phase 2's Tier B domains (E, B, F, 37b, A, 37a), the correct success metric is targeted adoption by the specific organizations most likely to act on each domain:
- Domain E: adoption by state election administrators, Secretaries of State coalition (NASS), election law programs — *not* just general civil society organizations
- Domain B: adoption by state Medicaid directors, hospital association advocacy staff, healthcare law clinics — organizations that can submit meaningful HHS guidance comments or file administrative law challenges
- Domain F: adoption by the civil society sector itself (National Council of Nonprofits, ICNL, Leadership Conference) — organizations with both the motivation and legal standing to challenge Section 112209

For Phase 2's Tier C domains (G, C, D), impact measurement operates on a longer cycle. Tribal sovereignty research (Domain G) should target Native American Rights Fund and National Congress of American Indians as primary adoption targets — their confirmation that the domain is being used in their legislative advocacy or litigation strategy is the strongest possible adoption signal for that audience.

### Measuring Whether Phase 1 Shifted Behavior: The Counterfactual Method

The post-distribution-adoption-framework.md (Section 5.3) identifies the counterfactual baseline problem: organizations were already working on all 35 domain areas before Phase 1 distributed. The framework's marginal contribution is coordination and synthesis, not origination.

By Month 6 of Phase 1 (approximately November 2026), the following diagnostic questions will determine whether behavior has shifted:

1. Are organizations that received Phase 1 materials connecting domains that they were previously treating as separate problems? The clearest test: is any organization simultaneously citing Domain 1 (voting rights), Domain 33 (state autocratization), and Domain 37 (federal election interference) as a unified analytical framework? If yes, coordination effect is confirmed.

2. Are organizations moving from analysis to specific statutory proposals faster than their pre-Phase 1 track record would predict? The acceleration test requires knowing the pre-Phase 1 publishing pace of key organizations. For organizations with documented publishing histories (Brennan Center, Protect Democracy), this is measurable.

3. Are organizations addressing domain areas outside their prior institutional mandate? The coverage effect test. If a labor union that had no prior work on judicial independence (Domain 6) now incorporates it into its legislative agenda, that is the clearest form of attributable impact.

Phase 2 research should be timed so that Phase 2 domains are completed and available before the Month 6 Phase 1 evaluation — giving adopters new analytical tools to deploy in the same timeframe as the first accountability snapshot.

## 5. Interdependency Map

### Which Phase 2 Domains Strengthen Phase 1 Narratives

**Domain E + Domains 1, 33, 37:** The tightest cluster. Domain E's election administration seizure documentation provides the operational layer beneath Domain 1's structural analysis and Domain 37's electoral interference mapping. An organization that adopted any of these three Phase 1 domains has a direct evidentiary need for Domain E's ProPublica-sourced material on election denier installations and CISA gutting. Domains 1, 33, 37, and E together constitute a complete pre-midterm election integrity toolkit. Any organization distributing Domain 37 should be given Domain E within 4–6 weeks.

**Domain B + Domains 11, 18, 9:** Domain B is the "implementation crisis" companion to Domain 11 (structural healthcare reform). Organizations that adopted Domain 11 as a long-term structural reform document need Domain B to address the immediate harm in progress. Domain 18 (Social Safety Net) adopters face the same urgency — OBBBA cuts SNAP, housing, and Medicaid simultaneously. Domain 9 (Federalism) adopters will find Domain B's state resistance mapping directly useful. These three Phase 1 domains collectively reach the organizations (healthcare advocacy, labor, social services) that most need Domain B's advocacy window analysis.

**Domain F + Domains 29, 3, 7, 8:** Domain F's civil society suppression analysis closes the loop on Domain 29 (Prosecutorial Weaponization) by showing that the DOJ-to-IRS-to-designation pipeline is a structural mechanism, not a series of isolated events. The SPLC case study appears in both domains — Domain 29 as prosecutorial accountability, Domain F as civil society infrastructure defense. Organizations working on Domain 3 (Democratic Participation) and Domain 7 (Rights Protection) will immediately recognize Domain F as the organizational precondition for everything Domain 3 and 7 aim to do: if civil society organizations are designated as terrorists and lose tax-exempt status, voter registration drives, legal aid, and First Amendment defense all collapse simultaneously.

**Domain A + Domains 5, 17, 20:** Domain A's trade policy analysis extends Domain 5 (Fiscal Reform) into trade revenue, Domain 17 (Labor) into worker dislocation from tariff restructuring, and Domain 20 (Economic Concentration) into the exemption capture mechanism — large corporations with political access receive tariff exemptions that small businesses cannot obtain. This is a domain where Phase 1 adopters in the fiscal policy and labor sectors already have institutional infrastructure to absorb and act on Phase 2 analysis.

**Domain G + Domains 22, 15, 19:** Domain G deepens Domain 22 (Racial Justice) in a direction that distinguishes Indigenous democratic exclusion from civil rights frameworks. The treaty-trust-sovereignty triad makes Indigenous democratic standing qualitatively different from the racial justice analysis in Domain 22. Domain 15 (Environment) adopters will recognize Domain G's resource extraction without consultation analysis. Domain 19 (National Security) adopters will find the treaty obligation analysis relevant to broader questions of international law credibility.
