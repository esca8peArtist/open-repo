## Section 1: Research Flow Overview

The research flow for Domain 49 follows a strict logic: **from infrastructure dismantlement to democratic exclusion**. Every section connects factual events (EPA office closures, NEPA rescission, Endangerment Finding finalization) to a democratic participation consequence. If a section is drifting toward climate policy or environmental rollback analysis, stop — that is Domain 15 territory.

**Research flow sequence (in order):**

1. Verify current procedural status of all live items (D.C. Circuit dockets, EPA regulatory actions)
2. Establish the infrastructure that existed (what was there)
3. Document the elimination (what was removed and when)
4. Trace the participation rights consequences (what communities can no longer do)
5. Anchor with community case studies (who specifically lost what capability)
6. Set the international benchmark (Aarhus Convention as the standard the US doesn't meet)
7. Close with reform architecture

Do not draft Section 6 (case studies) before Section 3 (NEPA) is written. The case studies depend on understanding the specific procedural tools that were available. Drafting them out of order produces generic narratives rather than specific participation rights claims.

## Section 3: Section-by-Section Writing Guidance

### Executive Summary (write last, 400–500 words)

Write after all other sections are complete. Lead with the most counterintuitive finding, not background: that EPA environmental justice infrastructure was a democratic participation system, and its dismantlement was a civic exclusion event. Cite one specific community and one specific tool they have lost. Close with the Aarhus benchmark — 47 nations have enforceable participation rights that US communities never had, and the US has now moved further from that standard.

### Section 1: The Democratic Design Problem (~500 words)

**Lead with**: The vicious cycle documented by UCS — not the rollbacks, the cycle. Environmental burden suppresses voter turnout; reduced political voice removes the community's power to contest further pollution; pollution increases; turnout declines. Name this as a democratic design failure.

**Key distinction to establish early**: Domain 49 does not duplicate Domain 15's rollback catalog. Domain 15 documents what was removed from environmental regulation. Domain 49 documents what was removed from community democratic participation. Establish this distinction explicitly in the first 100 words.

**Do not write**: Claims about air quality standards, emission limits, or climate policy impacts. That is Domain 15 material. Domain 49's claim is about voice — the legal and procedural tools communities used to contest decisions.

### Section 2: EPA EJ Infrastructure Elimination (~600–700 words)

**Lead with the specifics**: All 10 regional EJ offices closed March 11, 2025 on Lee Zeldin's directive. The national Office of Environmental Justice and External Civil Rights (OEJECR) eliminated. ~300 employees terminated (Inside Climate News, April 22, 2025). $300M–$500M in pending EJ grants canceled — note if only a range is available, state that explicitly.

**The democratic participation claim**: The EJ offices were not regulatory offices — they were community access points. They provided: Title VI complaint intake (the administrative pathway for contesting discriminatory permitting), EJSCREEN-based technical assistance for communities assessing their pollution burden, EJ grant administration (funding the community organizations that did civic engagement work), and community liaisons who connected regulatory processes to electoral mobilization. Eliminate these offices and you eliminate the infrastructure connecting affected communities to the regulatory processes that govern them.

**Cite the Crowell and Moring "180-degree change" analysis** for the law firm perspective on what disappeared for communities navigating federal environmental compliance.

### Section 3: NEPA as Community Participation Architecture (~600–700 words)

**Frame NEPA's purpose democratically**: The National Environmental Policy Act created community standing. An Environmental Impact Statement process is not primarily an environmental protection tool — it is a participation mechanism. Before a federal action (highway construction through a residential neighborhood, pipeline routing through tribal lands, industrial facility permitting) could proceed, agencies were required to solicit public comment. For EJ communities with limited access to formal legal advocacy, EIS comment periods were the primary democratic channel.

**What the January 8, 2026 CEQ rescission (Federal Register 2026-00178) removed**: All CEQ NEPA implementing regulations — the procedural rules that made the EIS process reliable and predictable. Without those regulations, agencies have discretion over when and how to conduct environmental review. Communities that built litigation strategies around NEPA procedural rights now face agencies that are not legally required to follow predictable procedures. The D.C. Circuit challenges from Earthjustice and NRDC are the active litigation record — cite them by docket number.

**Do not duplicate**: The NEPA rollback history (Phase 1 2020, Phase 2 Biden restoration 2023, 2026 rescission) is likely documented in Domain 15. Summarize briefly and cite Domain 15; do not reproduce the regulatory history in full.

### Section 4: Title VI Civil Rights Enforcement Collapse (~600–700 words)

**The administrative pathway argument**: Title VI of the Civil Rights Act prohibits discrimination in programs receiving federal financial assistance. The EPA OEJECR processed Title VI complaints from communities alleging discriminatory environmental permitting — the theory that polluting facilities are disproportionately sited in communities of color because those communities lack the political power to contest them. The administrative Title VI complaint pathway was the accessible mechanism: it did not require retaining attorneys, filing in federal court, or surviving standing challenges. It required filing a complaint with the agency.

**Lead with the pre-existing failure**: The processing time averaged 10+ years per complaint — egregious before 2025. Find the primary source for this figure (EPA ECHO system historical data or a GAO report — if neither is locatable, use the best law review source available and note the evidential basis). The administrative pathway was already inadequate; the 2025 elimination of OEJECR removed it entirely.

**The remaining option — federal court**: Communities can still sue under Title VI directly. But federal litigation requires attorneys, resources, and time. The administrative complaint pathway was the equalizing mechanism — its elimination leaves Title VI accessible only to communities with legal representation, which is precisely not the population Title VI was designed to protect.

### Section 5: Endangerment Finding Revocation (~500–600 words)

**Critical framing**: The Endangerment Finding is FINALIZED, effective April 20, 2026. Do not write "proposed" or "pending." The revocation is enacted. The democratic participation argument: the 2009 GHG Endangerment Finding was the legal predicate for all Clean Air Act GHG regulation. Communities in climate-vulnerable regions used GHG-related Clean Air Act review requirements as a hook to generate EIS processes for local polluting facilities — if a facility's GHG emissions triggered regulatory review, the EIS process attached, and community standing was created. That hook is now gone.

**State-level gap**: California's Section 209 waiver and state mini-EPA programs partially fill the gap — but only for residents in states with strong environmental laws. EJ communities in Gulf Coast states, Appalachia, and the industrial Midwest have no equivalent state-level participation tools. Name at least one specific state with no equivalent protection.

**D.C. Circuit challenges**: The litigation dockets confirmed in `PHASE_2_CANDIDATE_49_SOURCES.md` (American Public Health Ass'n v. U.S. EPA, No. 26-1037; Massachusetts v. U.S. EPA, No. 26-1061; Venner v. U.S. EPA, No. 26-1038) are the live record. Verify current status — briefing schedules, oral argument dates if set, or whether any preliminary injunctions have been sought.

### Section 6: Community Case Studies (~500–600 words)

**Use three specific communities. Not general descriptions — specific participatory events.**

1. **WE ACT for Environmental Justice (Northern Manhattan/South Bronx)**: Document a specific NEPA or Title VI proceeding they used pre-2025. The bus depot siting case and the Northern Manhattan air quality challenges are documented. What specifically did OEJECR provide them? What EIS comment process gave them standing? State what they can no longer access.

2. **Deep South Center for Environmental Justice (Gulf Coast petrochemical corridor)**: The petrochemical corridor's permit challenge history. Without EPA EJ coordinators, community technical assistance for FOIA requests and permit review is gone. What permit challenge did DSCEJ pursue using EPA EJ office resources?

3. **Indigenous Environmental Network (tribal lands)**: Dakota Access is the established case study. The NEPA tribal consultation requirement — before the January 8, 2026 CEQ rescission — gave tribal nations standing to demand EIS reviews for pipelines crossing or adjacent to tribal lands. The rescission eliminated the consultation requirement framework. This connects to Domain 58 (Tribal Sovereignty).

**Evidence standard**: Each case study needs at least one specific document, proceeding, or outcome — not a general description of what these organizations do.

### Section 7: International Benchmark — Aarhus (~400–500 words)

**Three pillars**: Information, participation, justice. Read Articles 1 and 3 of the Aarhus Convention text. Article 6 is the specific mechanism for public participation in decisions on specific activities (facility permitting, project approval) — this is the exact analog to what NEPA's EIS process was supposed to provide.

**47 ratifications; US signed but has not ratified.** The US has never ratified Aarhus — the EPA EJ infrastructure was the US's functional (but weaker, non-enforceable) analog. The elimination of that infrastructure moves the US further from international democratic environmental participation standards, not toward them.

**The comparative argument**: EU member states are legally bound by Aarhus. A resident of a German or French community facing a polluting facility siting has enforceable participation rights under Aarhus. A resident of Cancer Alley in Louisiana has none. This is the democratic rights gap — stated clearly.

**Connect to Domain 57 (Multilateral Withdrawal)**: US withdrawal from international environmental bodies eliminates the benchmarking data that EJ advocates use to document US failure relative to democratic peers. Cite Domain 57 explicitly.

### Section 8: Reform Architecture and Cross-Domain Integration (~500–600 words)

**Three-tier reform architecture:**
1. Restore EPA EJ offices through legislation — not executive order only, to prevent repeat executive elimination. Identify any current legislative proposals (EPA Environmental Justice Act, if introduced).
2. NEPA statutory amendments to restore EIS procedural requirements independent of CEQ regulations — so that future administrations cannot rescind participation rights through regulatory action alone.
3. Aarhus Convention ratification advocacy — long-term US commitment to environmental democracy, with a specific bill number if one exists.

**State-level floor**: Seven states have enacted environmental justice act frameworks. Identify the strongest two models (likely California and New York) and document what they provide to communities in those states that federal law no longer guarantees. Name these states explicitly.

**Cross-domain integration** (minimum 5 citations):
- Domain 15: rollback catalog — cite for the regulatory history; distinguish Domain 49's democratic rights analysis
- Domain 22: Racial Justice — environmental racism as the primary mechanism through which environmental burden concentrates in communities of color
- Domain 47: Housing Security — industrial siting near low-income neighborhoods drives residents out; displacement concentrates communities into environmentally burdened areas
- Domain 57: Multilateral Withdrawal — US withdrawal eliminates international benchmarking for EJ advocates
- Domain 58: Tribal Sovereignty — CEQ NEPA rescission eliminated tribal consultation requirements

## Section 5: Citation Integration

**How to integrate citations in this domain:**

Citations should appear at the specific claim, not at the end of paragraphs. Example:

Wrong: "The EPA closed 10 regional EJ offices and eliminated OEJECR, removing key community access points." [citation at end]

Right: "On March 11, 2025, EPA Administrator Zeldin directed the elimination of all 10 EPA regional environmental justice offices and the national Office of Environmental Justice and External Civil Rights." [^eelp-oejecr] "Approximately 300 employees received termination notices by April 2025." [^icn-terminations]

**Why this matters**: The domain's credibility depends on showing that specific events happened at specific times. General citations undermine the precision the democratic design argument requires.

**Citation naming convention**: Use bracketed endnotes with descriptive names (source-topic pattern: `eelp-oejecr`, `fed-reg-2026-00178`, `ucs-voting-ej`). Do not use numbered citations only — the names make peer review and fact-checking faster.

**Federal Register citations**: Always include the FR citation number, the publication date, and the effective date. These are primary legal documents; imprecision makes the domain vulnerable to challenge.
