### Candidate Domain A: Trade Policy and Economic Sovereignty
**Proposed title**: "Trade Policy, Tariff Authority, and Economic Sovereignty — The Constitutional Crisis of Unilateral Trade Governance"
**Priority rank**: 1 (highest urgency)

**The gap**: Domain 5 (Fiscal Reform) covers tax policy, the national debt, and government revenue. Domain 20 (Economic Concentration) covers antitrust and corporate power. Neither covers trade — the constitutional allocation of trade authority between Congress and the executive, the use of tariff powers for political coercion, and the economic democracy question of who bears the costs of trade policy. This is a significant omission: the tariff crisis is the most economically consequential domestic policy event of the second Trump term.

**What happened in April 2026**:
- On February 20, 2026, the Supreme Court ruled 6-3 in *Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump* that the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) does not authorize the president to impose tariffs without explicit congressional delegation. The court applied separation-of-powers principles: the Constitution assigns to Congress the authority to "lay and collect Taxes, Duties, Imposts and Excises," and tariff authority of "significant economic and political magnitude" requires explicit statutory delegation.
- The administration pivoted to Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974, which allows temporary tariffs of up to 15% for up to 150 days for balance-of-payments deficits — a weaker legal basis but one the administration is now using to maintain trade leverage.
- $170+ billion in IEEPA tariffs were collected before the ruling, creating a significant refund question.
- Chinese goods face 145% tariffs. 61% of small businesses report negative impacts. Consumers are projected to bear two-thirds of the cost burden by mid-2026. Economists estimate a 0.5% GDP drag.
- The Supreme Court ruling itself is an important democratic win — a check on executive economic unilateralism — but the administration's Section 122 pivot means trade policy remains in executive hands for now.

**Scope estimate**: 4,000–5,500 words. This domain has four natural sub-areas: (a) constitutional allocation of trade authority (Article I, Section 8 history, the IEEPA ruling, Section 122, the Smoot-Hawley cautionary precedent); (b) the democracy problem of tariffs as taxation without meaningful legislative approval; (c) the economic impact on workers and small businesses as a distributive justice issue; (d) reform proposals (Trade Authority Restoration Act framework, congressional notification and approval requirements, mandatory economic impact assessments with distributional analysis).

**Sourcing ease**: High. The Supreme Court ruling is a primary source. CBO, EPI, Tax Foundation, and Peterson Institute have published extensive impact analyses. Small business organization surveys are publicly available. Law review coverage of the IEEPA ruling has been extensive.

**Urgency**: High. The tariff regime is actively restructuring the US economy and its political relationship with trading partners. The IEEPA ruling is a live constitutional development requiring documentation. The Section 122 pivot is unresolved. The distributional impact — small businesses and consumers vs. large importers with political access to exemptions — is a direct democracy problem. The 2026 midterms make trade policy economically salient for voters in manufacturing and agricultural districts.

**Intersection with existing domains**:
- Domain 5 (Fiscal Reform): tariffs as regressive taxation; revenue collection vs. trade policy purposes
- Domain 9 (Federalism): state-level impacts on agricultural and manufacturing economies
- Domain 17 (Labor): worker dislocation, supply chain restructuring, wage effects of consumer price increases
- Domain 20 (Economic Concentration): large corporations with political access to tariff exemptions vs. small businesses with none — a corporate power mechanism

**Sourcing anchors**:
- [SCOTUS ruling — *Learning Resources v. Trump* (February 20, 2026)](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/25pdf/24-1287_4gcj.pdf)
- [PIIE: What the Supreme Court's tariff ruling changes](https://www.piie.com/blogs/realtime-economics/2026/what-supreme-courts-tariff-ruling-changes-and-what-it-doesnt)
- [PwC: IEEPA tariffs analysis](https://www.pwc.com/us/en/services/tax/library/pwc-sc-decision-on-ieepa-tariffs-reshapes-trade-auth-and-introduces-potential-refund-op.html)
- [US Chamber of Commerce: Small businesses on tariffs](https://www.uschamber.com/small-business/american-workers-businesses-consumers-trade-tariffs)
- [White & Case: Termination of IEEPA tariffs following SCOTUS](https://www.whitecase.com/insight-alert/united-states-terminates-ieepa-based-tariffs-following-supreme-court-decision)
- [Norton Rose Fulbright: Refund analysis](https://www.nortonrosefulbright.com/en/knowledge/publications/20f2de87/potential-refunds-us-supreme-court-overturns-ieepa-tariffs)
- Dani Rodrik (WEF): "Tariffs, globalization and democracy"

**Suggested subsections**: (a) Trade authority: the constitutional design; (b) IEEPA and its misuse for tariff authority; (c) The February 20, 2026 ruling and its aftermath; (d) Section 122 and the continuing executive trade posture; (e) Economic impact on workers, small businesses, and consumers; (f) Reform: restoring congressional trade authority and distributional accountability

### Candidate Domain B: Healthcare Access Implementation Crisis — OBBBA Medicaid Cuts
**Proposed title**: "Healthcare Access as a Democratic Right — The Medicaid Collapse and the Politics of Care Denial"
**Priority rank**: 2

**The gap**: Domain 11 (Healthcare) addresses the structural case for universal healthcare — single-payer, Medicare expansion, drug pricing reform — with international benchmarks and cost analysis. It was written before the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA, signed July 4, 2025) and its implementation beginning in 2026. The OBBBA represents the largest single rollback of healthcare access in US history and is producing real-time hospital closures, coverage losses, and medical service eliminations. This is not a structural reform argument — it is an acute implementation crisis requiring its own documentation with a different analytical frame: democratic accountability for harm already in progress.

**What happened in April 2026**:
- The OBBBA (Pub. L. 119-21) cut Medicaid by $911 billion over 10 years. CBO estimates 11.8 million people will lose Medicaid coverage directly; an additional 3.1 million will lose Marketplace coverage.
- Three hospital service eliminations documented in the run-up to implementation: Greene County General (Linton, IN, obstetrics closed January 2026), St. Mary's Sacred Heart (rural Georgia, labor and delivery closed December 2025), Centra Southside Community Hospital (Farmville, VA, labor and delivery and OBGYN surgical services closed December 2025).
- Public Citizen: 446 hospitals at risk of closing or reducing services due to Medicaid cuts. States with highest percentage of at-risk hospitals: Connecticut (36%), California (31%), New York (31%).
- Trinity Health System (92 hospitals): announced 10.5% revenue cycle staff layoffs across 15 states; projects "recent and future government funding policy changes" will lower annual revenue by $1.5 billion.
- Work requirements (80 hours/month) take effect January 2027 — HHS must issue guidance by June 1, 2026. Yale School of Medicine: more than 40% of those at risk of losing Medicaid have at least three chronic conditions. Work requirements will disproportionately affect people with serious medical conditions who physically cannot meet the work requirement.
- Enhanced FMAP (Federal Medical Assistance Percentage) for Medicaid expansion sunsets January 1, 2026 — this was the 90% federal match rate that incentivized states to expand coverage under the ACA. Without it, some states may contract coverage to pre-expansion levels.
- OBBBA also eliminated LIHEAP (Low Income Home Energy Assistance Program) and cut housing assistance by $26.7 billion (43% reduction).

**Scope estimate**: 4,500–6,000 words. The domain needs: (a) the structural problem (healthcare as a precondition for democratic participation — sick, uninsured people cannot organize, work, or engage civically); (b) the OBBBA as a specific, documented harm event; (c) the implementation timeline and what happens in 2026 vs. 2027; (d) who bears the cost (disproportionate impact on rural, low-income, Black and Native communities); (e) state-level variation and resistance (states challenging work requirements, states maintaining expanded Medicaid from general funds); (f) legal challenges (ACA, equal protection, procedural challenges to work requirement implementation); (g) reform framework connecting short-term emergency response to the structural proposals in Domain 11.

**Sourcing ease**: High. CBO data is authoritative. KFF, CBPP, Urban Institute, and RAND have all published detailed impact analyses. Hospital closure data is documented. The OBBBA text is a primary source.

**Urgency**: High. The cuts are not future projections — they are producing real-time hospital closures and coverage losses in 2026. Work requirements take effect in 2027, meaning the advocacy and legal challenge window is now (the HHS guidance period runs through June 2026). This domain is more urgent than a structural healthcare reform domain because it documents harm in progress, not a hypothetical reform scenario.

**Intersection with existing domains**:
- Domain 11 (Healthcare): direct complement — Domain 11 provides the structural reform case; this domain documents the immediate crisis
- Domain 9 (Federalism): state variation in response; state-level lawsuits; state general fund decisions
- Domain 18 (Social Safety Net): OBBBA also cuts SNAP and housing programs — interconnected harm
- Domain 14 (Criminal Justice/Policing): incarceration and healthcare access; rural hospital closures leave trauma centers understaffed
- Domain 22 (Reparations/Racial Justice): disproportionate impact on communities of color

**Sourcing anchors**:
- [AMA: Changes to Medicaid under OBBBA](https://www.ama-assn.org/health-care-advocacy/federal-advocacy/changes-medicaid-aca-and-other-key-provisions-one-big)
- [Urban Institute: Medicaid cuts and young adults](https://www.urban.org/urban-wire/medicaid-cuts-one-big-beautiful-bill-act-leave-3-10-young-adults-vulnerable-losing)
- [Public Citizen: 446 hospitals at risk](https://www.citizen.org/article/big-ugly-threat/)
- [NBC News: Medicaid cuts threaten hundreds of hospitals](https://www.nbcnews.com/health/health-news/medicaid-cuts-threaten-hundreds-hospitals-new-report-finds-rcna265789)
- [KFF: Work requirement provisions analysis](https://www.kff.org/medicaid/a-closer-look-at-the-work-requirement-provisions-in-the-2025-federal-budget-reconciliation-law/)
- [RAND: State-level Medicaid impact analysis](https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_reports/RRA4098-1.html)

- CBPP: Social Security and Medicare trust fund acceleration from OBBBA

**Suggested subsections**: (a) Healthcare access as a democratic prerequisite — why this is a democracy domain, not just a health policy domain; (b) The OBBBA as the largest single healthcare rollback in US history; (c) Implementation timeline 2026–2027; (d) Who loses coverage and why: the distributional profile; (e) Hospital closures and rural healthcare collapse; (f) State responses — resistance, compliance, and the federalism variable; (g) Legal challenges to work requirements and FMAP elimination; (h) Reform: bridging the OBBBA crisis to the structural proposals in Domain 11

### Candidate Domain C: Artificial Intelligence Governance and Algorithmic Democracy
**Proposed title**: "Algorithmic Governance and Democratic Accountability — AI in Government Decision-Making"
**Priority rank**: 3

**The gap**: Domain 21 (Data Privacy and Digital Surveillance) covers government surveillance infrastructure, warrantless data collection, and biometric surveillance. Domain 16 (Immigration and Citizenship) has one subsection (16d) on algorithmic immigration enforcement — ICM, FALCON, ImmigrationOS. What is missing is a domain covering the broader problem of AI deployment in government decision-making across all domains — welfare eligibility, criminal justice, education, healthcare authorization — and the democratic accountability problem this creates: when an algorithm denies a person's Medicaid claim, disability benefit, or parole, who is responsible, what standard of review applies, and what procedural rights does the affected person have?

**What is happening in April 2026**:
- The Trump administration's national AI policy framework (released March 20, 2026) directs agencies to accelerate AI adoption while removing state-level AI regulatory barriers via executive order preemption.
- FBI Director Kash Patel confirmed to Congress (April 2026) that the FBI is buying location history and other data from commercial data brokers to track American citizens — AI-assisted surveillance of constitutionally protected activity.
- The EFF filed a FOIA lawsuit in early 2026 against CMS (Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services) seeking records about a multi-state program launched January 2026 using AI to evaluate requests for medical care — automated denial of Medicaid coverage without meaningful human review.
- The administration's stated goal of eliminating "inefficient" human review from government processes creates conditions where AI decisions become final without due process.
- Internationally, the EU AI Act (which entered force in 2024) bans several high-risk AI applications in government and requires human oversight, transparency, and contestability for consequential decisions. The US has moved in the opposite direction.

**Scope estimate**: 4,000–5,000 words. Sub-areas: (a) the democracy problem — unaccountable AI decisions affecting rights and benefits; (b) current US government AI deployment landscape; (c) procedural due process requirements when government decisions are automated; (d) the state preemption problem — executive order barriers to state-level AI regulation; (e) international benchmarks (EU AI Act, Canada's Directive on Automated Decision-Making); (f) reform proposals (Algorithmic Accountability Act framework, mandatory human review for consequential decisions, explainability requirements, disparate impact assessment requirements).

**Sourcing ease**: Medium. The EFF and Epic.org have strong primary documentation. Congressional testimony is available. EU AI Act text is a comparative source. Academic literature on algorithmic accountability is extensive. The FBI data broker confirmation is a verified primary source from congressional testimony. The challenge is that much AI government deployment is proprietary and not publicly documented — gaps in the sourcing record are themselves an accountability problem.

**Urgency**: Medium-High. The FOIA suit over Medicaid AI decisions represents a live enforcement question. The national AI policy framework's preemption of state regulations removes the state-level experimental laboratory for AI governance that has historically been where US regulatory innovation begins. The convergence of AI deployment + reduced human oversight + civil rights rollbacks creates conditions for automated discrimination at scale. The timing is urgent because AI systems, once embedded in government bureaucracy, are extremely difficult to remove.

**Intersection with existing domains**:
- Domain 21 (Data Privacy): AI systems typically require massive data collection — surveillance and AI governance are deeply intertwined
- Domain 16 (Immigration): ImmigrationOS and ICM algorithmic enforcement (already in Domain 16d) — this domain would generalize that analysis across all government sectors
- Domain 6 (Judicial Independence): AI decisions and due process; judicial review of algorithmic government action
- Domain 11 (Healthcare): Medicaid AI authorization decisions; clinical decision support AI
- Domain 18 (Social Safety Net): AI welfare eligibility determination; SNAP fraud detection algorithms
- Domain 14 (Criminal Justice): predictive policing, risk assessment instruments in sentencing and parole, facial recognition misidentification

**Sourcing anchors**:
- [EFF: FOIA suit against CMS for Medicaid AI records](https://epic.org/government-ai-is-coming-for-your-data/)
- [EFF: Government must not force companies to participate in AI-powered surveillance](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2026/03/government-must-not-force-companies-participate-ai-powered-surveillance)
- [The Conversation: US government ramps up mass surveillance with AI](https://theconversation.com/us-government-ramps-up-mass-surveillance-with-help-of-ai-tech-data-brokers-and-your-apps-and-devices-277440)

**Suggested subsections**: (a) AI in government: scope and scale of current US deployment; (b) The accountability gap — when algorithms deny rights; (c) Procedural due process and automated government decisions; (d) AI in immigration enforcement: the FALCON/ImmigrationOS case (connecting to Domain 16d); (e) AI in welfare and healthcare authorization; (f) AI-assisted surveillance of First Amendment activity; (g) The state preemption crisis: Trump AI policy vs. state regulatory experimentation; (h) International benchmarks: EU AI Act, Canada, Singapore; (i) Reform: Algorithmic Accountability Act, human oversight mandates, transparency and explainability requirements, disparate impact assessments

### Candidate Domain D: Disability Rights, Benefits Access, and the Democratic Participation of People with Disabilities
**Proposed title**: "Disability Rights as Democratic Rights — Benefits Access, Participation Barriers, and the SSA/SSDI Dismantlement"
**Priority rank**: 4

**The gap**: Domain 18 (Social Safety Net) covers the structural case for expanded income support — expanded EITC, child allowances, unemployment insurance reform. Domain 11 (Healthcare) addresses healthcare access structurally. Neither domain addresses disability as a civil rights issue with direct democratic implications: people with disabilities who lose SSI/SSDI income and Medicaid coverage lose not just financial stability but the preconditions for civic participation — transportation, communication access, stable housing, healthcare. The SSA's 7,000-job reduction (12% of staff) has created processing delays that affect benefit access independent of any policy change. The OBBBA's Medicaid work requirements create an existential threat to Medicaid-enrolled people with disabilities (more than 40% of those at risk of losing Medicaid have three or more chronic conditions).

**What is happening in April 2026**:
- SSA is cutting approximately 7,000 jobs — a 12% staffing reduction — creating documented processing delays for disability claims and appeals. Disability Help Group and Binghamton University research document that "people in need of disability benefits are facing new barriers" from the cuts.
- The Trump administration prepared (but ultimately dropped in November 2025 after advocacy pushback) a proposed rule that would have cut SSDI qualification rates by up to 20% — which would have been the largest SSDI cut in history. The rule was dropped, but the administrative pressure to narrow eligibility continues through informal guidance and staffing reductions.
- OBBBA Medicaid work requirements (effective January 2027, with HHS guidance due June 2026): 80 hours/month of qualifying activity required. Yale School of Medicine found 40%+ of those at risk of losing Medicaid have three or more chronic conditions — many cannot physically meet a work requirement. This provision threatens healthcare coverage for millions of people with disabilities who are technically in the Medicaid expansion (not SSDI) but medically unable to work.
- 165 House Democrats (led by Reps. Larson and Wasserman Schultz) filed to halt the Trump administration's plan to strip disability benefits from workers aged 50+.
- The intersection with democracy: people with disabilities who lose benefits face eviction, loss of transportation access, and loss of healthcare — all of which reduce their capacity to engage in civic life, attend meetings, contact representatives, or participate in protests. Disability rights is not just a welfare issue; it is a democratic participation issue.

**Scope estimate**: 3,500–4,500 words. Sub-areas: (a) disability rights as a democratic rights framework (ADA, Section 504, the social model of disability vs. the medical model); (b) SSI/SSDI as democratic infrastructure — what benefits loss does to civic participation; (c) SSA dismantlement: staffing cuts, processing delays, and access barriers; (d) OBBBA work requirements and their impact on people with disabilities; (e) Medicaid as the healthcare backbone of disability community survival; (f) state variation (state supplemental SSI payments, state Medicaid expansion decisions); (g) legal challenges (ADA challenges to work requirements, procedural due process for benefit termination); (h) reform: SSA staffing restoration, benefit adequacy, work requirement exemptions, ADA enforcement restoration.

**Sourcing ease**: Medium. CBPP, AARP, Center for American Progress, and disability advocacy organizations have published extensively. Congressional testimony is available. The administration's dropped SSDI rule is documented by ProPublica and CBPP. The challenge: individual disability impacts are not systematically tracked, and the SSA data that would show processing delay impacts is internal. State-level variation in Medicaid work requirement implementation will require monitoring in 2027.

**Urgency**: Medium. The SSDI qualification rule was dropped, reducing the immediate crisis. But the SSA staffing cuts are creating right-now access barriers, the Medicaid work requirement HHS guidance is due by June 2026, and the 2027 implementation date creates an urgent advocacy window. The domain's democracy dimension (civic participation preconditions) elevates its relevance for this project.

**Intersection with existing domains**:
- Domain 11 (Healthcare): Medicaid access for people with disabilities
- Domain 18 (Social Safety Net): SSI/SSDI as core safety net programs
- Domain 14 (Criminal Justice): incarceration of people with disabilities (disability is dramatically overrepresented in prison populations)
- Domain 7 (Rights Protection): ADA enforcement and the rollback of accessibility requirements
- Domain 22 (Reparations/Racial Justice): disability disproportionately affects Black and Indigenous communities as a result of historical inequality and environmental exposure

**Sourcing anchors**:
- [CBPP: Trump plans deep cuts to SSDI for older workers](https://www.cbpp.org/blog/trump-administration-plans-deep-cuts-to-social-security-disability-insurance-particularly-for-older-workers)

- [Center for American Progress: Plans to covertly cut SSDI](https://www.americanprogress.org/article/the-trump-administrations-plans-to-covertly-cut-social-security-disability-benefits/)
- [Binghamton University: Disability benefits barriers](https://www.binghamton.edu/news/story/6105/people-in-need-of-disability-benefits-are-facing-new-barriers-and-government-cuts-and-overhauls-are-to-blame)
- [AARP: SSA drops disability rules](https://www.aarp.org/social-security/ssa-drops-disability-insurance-changes/)

**Suggested subsections**: (a) Disability rights as democratic rights — the participation framework; (b) SSI/SSDI: program mechanics and what benefit loss means for daily life; (c) SSA dismantlement: staffing crisis and access barriers; (d) OBBBA and the Medicaid work requirement threat; (e) Legal challenges and advocacy landscape; (f) State variation and resistance; (g) Reform: benefits adequacy, SSA staffing restoration, disability-inclusive civic infrastructure; (h) International benchmarks (UK PIP/ESA reform lessons, German disability benefits, Nordic inclusion models)
