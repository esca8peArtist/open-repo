### Pattern 1: Institutional Constraint Failure at Scale

The most significant Week 1 finding is that institutional constraints on executive power are simultaneously failing across all six domains — not sequentially, but in parallel:

- **War Powers (A)**: The constitutional clock ran out. Congress has no enforcement mechanism. No court has accepted standing. The WPR is effectively unenforceable as written.
- **Fiscal Authority (F)**: Congress's power of the purse is being structurally bypassed — not through impoundment (which courts have blocked) but through reconciliation mechanics that create mandatory enforcement spending Congress cannot claw back annually.
- **SCOTUS (D)**: The June *Slaughter* ruling is expected to eliminate for-cause removal protections for independent agencies, removing the last structural protection for the fourth branch of government.
- **Surveillance (E/FISA)**: Section 702 reauthorization without warrant protections extends warrantless surveillance through the midterm cycle; the DOJ national voter database adds a second data aggregation vector.

**What this pattern means for the framework**: Domain 34 (Congressional Fiscal Authority), Domain 06 (Judicial Independence), and Domain 19f (War Powers Reform) all assume that institutional constraints remain operative but are being weakened. The Week 1 evidence suggests the weakness has advanced to the point that the constraints are non-functional in practice even if technically on the books. This may require a recalibration in how the framework's reform proposals are framed — from "restoring weakened institutions" to "rebuilding institutions that have functionally collapsed."

### Pattern 2: Federal Infrastructure Repurposed for Enforcement at Citizens

Three Week 1 developments show federal government infrastructure being repurposed from its statutory purpose toward enforcement directed at citizens and democratic participation:

- **DOJ + IRS tax data sharing**: Documented in litigation tracker (42,695 adjudicated violations) — IRS records weaponized against immigrant taxpayers.
- **DOJ + national voter database**: DOJ suing states to aggregate voter rolls into a SAVE database cross-reference — election administration infrastructure repurposed for citizenship-based voter purge.
- **CISA gutted**: The federal election security infrastructure that was designed to defend elections from foreign interference is being dismantled as states try to build replacements.

**Structural implication**: The federal government is simultaneously abandoning its defensive infrastructure (CISA) and building offensive infrastructure (voter database, IRS data sharing) directed at democratic participation. This is not yet documented as a single pattern in any domain file. It bridges Domains 01, 21, 29, and 37.

### Pattern 3: Litigation Race — Courts Hold but Bandwidth Is Finite

Courts are the primary constraint on all three patterns above, but the bandwidth problem is worsening:

- 362 immigration cases, challengers winning 97% — but each win requires a separate legal fight.
- 5 states won DOJ voter roll dismissals — but 12 states already voluntarily complied, and DOJ is suing 30 more.
- SPLC grand jury weaponization motion signals the administration is attempting to use the grand jury process itself as an offensive tool — if that theory survives, the litigation defense layer faces a new front.
- The administration's defiance of court orders (CASA v. DHS, Ramirez Ovando, Abrego Garcia context) means winning in court does not guarantee compliance.

**Implication for resistance strategy**: The litigation-first strategy remains correct but requires parallel institutional infrastructure investment (state election security, state VRAs, state voter protection laws) as a backup when court orders are not enforced.
