## 1. State Legislatures

**Who this is for**: Committee counsel, caucus policy directors, legislative directors, and bill drafting staff in state legislatures with Democratic chambers or meaningful bipartisan blocs. Also applicable for legislative liaisons at advocacy organizations working to move bills.

---

### Opening Scenario: April 2026

The Virginia House Democratic Caucus opened its 2026 session with a trifecta and pre-filed bills on redistricting amendment, voting access, and anti-suppression measures. Two problems surfaced immediately: (1) bills were introduced in sequence rather than as an omnibus, making each individually vulnerable to procedural defeat; and (2) the caucus had no shared framework for which domains to prioritize when forced to choose — producing internal conflict between labor-aligned members (Domain 17), housing-aligned members (Domain 13), and election-reform-aligned members (Domain 1). Result: caucus energy dispersed across 14 bills, none with full caucus backing, committee hearing calendar overloaded.

The 35-domain framework solves this problem. Minnesota's 2023 omnibus — the most significant pro-democracy legislation in 50 years — succeeded precisely because the caucus applied a sequencing discipline that prioritized Wave 1 guardrail domains (Domain 1: voting access; Domain 2: civil service protection; Domain 9: federalism/local power) before Wave 2 structural domains (Domain 3: economic participation; Domain 11: healthcare). That sequencing is reproducible. Here is how.

---

### 5-Step Playbook

**Step 1: Run the wave filter before the session opens.**

Apply the framework's three-wave structure to your domain list before bill introduction begins. Wave 1 (restore guardrails): Domains 1, 2, 7, 9, 14 — these can be enacted by statute without constitutional amendment, without a federal prerequisite, and without creating new agencies. These are your session-one bills. Wave 2 (build institutions): Domains 3, 11, 15, 17 — these require building new structures (a state public option, a state climate bank, a sectoral bargaining pilot) and belong in years two and three. Wave 3 (structural transformation): Domains 5, 6, 22 — these require either constitutional amendment or supermajorities and belong in longer-term planning.

Do this before introducing any bill. The filter tells you your priority stack. It takes two hours and prevents six months of wasted legislative calendar.

**Step 2: Use the framework's evidence section to build the bill record, not your own research.**

Each domain section of the proposal contains: the specific problem, international precedents, U.S. state precedents, peer-reviewed citations, and fiscal estimates. This is already bill record material. Committee counsel should excerpt the relevant domain subsection directly into the bill's legislative findings, statement of purpose, and fiscal note. You do not need to recreate the research.

Concrete example: a state RCV bill's findings section should cite Maine's four-cycle evidence base (Domain 1g), Australia's 108-year uninterrupted record, and Alaska's 2022 spoiler-effect elimination result. A state redistricting protection statute should cite the specific mechanism Texas used in 2025 (Domain 1f) — state legislative action followed by a Supreme Court stay of remedies — and draft language that closes that specific loophole. The research is already in the domain file; the bill-drafting task is extraction and adaptation, not discovery.

**Step 3: Coordinate bill language with peer states before introduction.**

Identify two or three states advancing the same domain reform in the same session. Contact their legislative counsel. Align bill language where legally feasible — courts give more weight to consistent multi-state statutory language, and aligned language makes future interstate compact formation easier.

The Governors Safeguarding Democracy network (co-chaired by Pritzker and Polis) provides coordination infrastructure: GSD state legislative staff share bill drafting resources and hold coordination calls. If your state is in the GSD network, check whether a model bill exists before drafting from scratch. For non-GSD states: use the National Conference of State Legislatures democracy reform network, which tracks active state bills across all 50 states.

**Step 4: Score each bill on a three-factor feasibility matrix and sequence accordingly.**

For each Wave 1 domain bill: score (1) do you have the votes? (2) will the governor sign? (3) is there an organized constituency that will generate political cost if the bill dies? Bills scoring 3/3 are session-one floor priorities. Bills scoring 2/3 are introduction priorities — hearings build the evidentiary record even if passage is uncertain. Bills scoring 1/3 are constituency-building investments for next session: introduce now, use hearings to generate witnesses and media coverage, and carry into the next cycle with a stronger base.

This is not a reason to drop low-scoring bills. It is a calendar discipline: 3/3 bills get committee hearing slots in the first 60 days; 2/3 bills get hearings in days 60-90; 1/3 bills get introduced with a visible future-cycle commitment.

**Step 5: Introduce Wave 1 bills as an omnibus where constitutionally permissible.**

The Minnesota model's decisive advantage was packaging — the Democracy for the People Act consolidated automatic voter registration, pre-registration for 16-17 year-olds, a permanent absentee list, multilingual materials, and voter intimidation protections into a single bill. Each provision alone was vulnerable to targeted opposition and procedural delay. Together, they were politically and procedurally harder to disassemble. The omnibus approach works only for domains within the same jurisdiction — you cannot combine a voting rights bill with a labor regulation bill — but within Domain 1, Domain 7, and Domain 9, there are typically 4-6 provisions that can be packaged.

After the omnibus, sequence single-domain bills to reinforce each other: Domain 1 (voting access) first, then Domain 7 (rights protection) — wins in one domain lower the political cost of the next because the coalition is demonstrably functional and the governor is on record.

---

### Decision Tree: When to Emphasize Which Domain

```
Session opens → Do you have a Democratic trifecta?
  YES → Follow wave sequence: D1 → D7 → D9 → D14
       → Package D1 provisions into omnibus if 3+ Wave 1 provisions ready
       → Add D2 (civil service) if state has Schedule F-equivalent vulnerability
  NO, but competitive chamber → Focus on record-building:
       → Identify 2-3 Republican crossover votes per domain
       → Lead with D9 (federalism/state sovereignty) — most crossover appeal
       → Follow with D14 (criminal justice) if reform-minded R votes exist
       → Avoid D22 (reparations) and D27 (academic freedom) as lead issues in split chambers
  NO, in minority → Use committee hearings to build evidentiary record:
       → File bills in every priority domain; focus energy on expert testimony
       → Domain 1f (redistricting protection) if mid-decade gerrymandering risk exists
       → Domain 29 (prosecutorial weaponization) shield laws — bipartisan resonance
```

### Success Metrics

- Wave 1 priority bills introduced within first 30 days of session
- At least one omnibus bill advancing (not fragmented across individual bills)
- Peer state coordination in place for all priority domains
- Expert witnesses confirmed from domain files for each committee hearing
- 90-day: at least one Domain 1, 7, or 9 bill through committee
- One year: one enacted statute reflecting framework analysis; fiscal note citing Section 5.5 estimates has survived committee challenge

---

### Common Failure Modes

**Fragmentation**: Introducing 14 bills with equal priority and no sequence. The fix is the wave filter applied before session opens, not after the calendar fills.

**Evidence reinvention**: Committee counsel researching from scratch rather than extracting from domain files. The proposal's evidentiary standard is designed to withstand committee challenge — use it.

**Ignoring the fiscal note**: Every bill needs one. The proposal's Section 5.5 has cost estimates and ROI evidence. A bill with a well-documented fiscal note is harder to kill on "we can't afford it" grounds.

**Coordination without follow-through**: Aligning with peer states at session open and then losing contact. Establish a standing monthly call with peer-state legislative counsel that continues through committee hearings and floor votes.

## 2. Federal Circuit Courts — Appellate Litigators

**Who this is for**: Appellate litigators at civil rights organizations (ACLU, NAACP LDF, Brennan Center, Democracy Docket), state AG offices, and public interest law firms who are building multi-case strategic litigation programs. Also relevant for law school clinic directors supervising active appellate work.

---

### Opening Scenario: April 2026

Trump v. Wilcox produced a D.C. Circuit ruling in December 2025 (later stayed by the Supreme Court) that NLRB and MSPB removal protections are unconstitutional. The full D.C. Circuit is considering en banc review. The Supreme Court will hear the case in its October 2026 term and is expected to overrule Humphrey's Executor. Meanwhile, appellate litigators are managing: state AG tariff litigation in multiple circuits (24-state coalition, IEEPA authority challenge); election interference pre-emption cases ahead of November 2026; warrantless arrest challenges in D.D.C. and the 4th Circuit; immigration court independence cases; and mid-decade redistricting challenges in Texas, North Carolina, and Missouri.

The litigation map is dense and the circuits are unequal. Litigating in the 5th Circuit — which has been the venue for most anti-reform rulings — without a deliberate strategy for building the appellate record toward the D.C. Circuit or Supreme Court is operationally costly. The 35-domain framework provides a strategic map for which claims to lead with, which circuits to file in, and in what sequence.

---

### 5-Step Playbook

**Step 1: Map your active cases to the domain dependency structure.**

The framework's Activation Architecture (Part II) identifies the Tier 1 dependency structure: Domain 6 (Judicial Independence) must be substantially advanced before Domains 1, 2, 29, and 34 can succeed through litigation, because those domains all depend on courts willing to rule against executive interests. This is not just theory — it is a circuit-filing strategy. Your Domain 6 cases (judicial independence, shadow docket challenges, immigration court independence) are the cases that most directly affect whether your other cases can win.

Priority Rule: before filing a high-risk constitutional challenge in a hostile circuit, assess whether a Domain 6 case is currently pending that could affect the doctrinal environment for your challenge. If so, consider whether delay or sequencing is strategically preferable to racing to a hostile ruling.

**Step 2: Identify circuit-specific strategic windows using the litigation tracker.**

The litigation-tracker-2026.md in this archive tracks case status by category. Use it to identify:
- Which circuits have active injunctions this administration has complied with (4th, D.D.C., 9th, 10th) — file there first
- Which circuits have issued rulings creating circuit splits (10th Circuit Denver use-of-force ruling, Category 6) — splits create Supreme Court cert pressure that can accelerate favorable resolution
- Which circuits have judges who have made hostile rulings at the TRO stage — avoid as first-filing jurisdiction for novel claims; use as second-filing jurisdiction to build the split

The post-Loper Bright landscape (June 2024, Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo) has significant implications for appellate strategy: courts no longer defer to agency interpretations of ambiguous statutes, and the Corner Post ruling (2024) opened challenges to regulations that had been time-barred for years because the limitations period now runs from plaintiff's injury, not the regulation's finalization. This means Domain 20 (antitrust), Domain 21 (surveillance), and Domain 15 (environmental rollbacks) cases that were previously foreclosed may now have viable challenges.

**Step 3: Build your litigation map across the five strongest domains for court-based resistance.**

The framework identifies five domains with the most tractable court-based pathways in the current environment:

Domain 6 (Judicial Independence): Independent agency tenure protection cases, immigration court independence, shadow docket transparency challenges. Target circuits: D.D.C., 9th, 4th. Key case: en banc Wilcox petition and post-Slaughter state analog statutes. Post-Slaughter ruling (expected June 2026) will require a pre-staged response within 30 days — state analog legislation in 15+ states plus Federal Reserve protection bills.

Domain 1 (Electoral Reform): Redistricting challenges, voter purge challenges, mid-decade gerrymandering. Target circuits: 4th, 9th, 11th (where sympathetic panels exist). Domain 1f documents the specific Texas and North Carolina mid-decade gerrymander mechanism — use it as the fact pattern for anticipatory TRO motions ahead of any similar action.

Domain 29 (Prosecutorial Weaponization): SPLC defense (amicus coalition documented in domain file), charging transparency challenges, bar rule interference challenge (DOJ proposed bar rule, April 25, 2026). Venue: D.D.C. for the bar rule challenge; federal district of SPLC indictment for the defense amicus.

Domain 34 (Congressional Power of the Purse): Impoundment Control Act enforcement, unauthorized executive fund reallocation. Target: D.D.C. for ICA claims; standing theory documented in domain file. Post-Loper Bright, courts will independently assess whether the ICA's text forecloses presidential impoundment — the textual argument is strong.

Domain 23 (Trade/Tariffs): 24-state AG coalition IEEPA challenge. Multiple circuits. The SCOTUS Learning Resources v. Trump ruling (cited in Domain 23) established the 6-3 ruling against IEEPA tariff authority; the remaining tariff cases build on that precedent.

**Step 4: Use the cross-domain reinforcing pairs for coordinated multi-case timing.**

The framework identifies mutually reinforcing domain pairs where simultaneous litigation accelerates both cases. The most important for appellate litigators:

D6 + D29: judicial independence cases and anti-retaliation cases reinforce each other. When courts issue rulings protecting judicial independence (Domain 6), those rulings expand the doctrinal space for challenging politically motivated prosecutions (Domain 29) — both rely on separation of powers analysis.

D2 + D26: civil service protection cases and IG independence cases use the same statutory architecture (Civil Service Reform Act, IG Act) and can be litigated in parallel by the same team, with case law from one case becoming precedent for the other.

D1 + D7: election access cases and First Amendment protest cases reinforce each other because both challenge government suppression of political participation. A favorable First Amendment ruling (Domain 7) strengthens the showing of government animus in election interference cases (Domain 1).

**Step 5: Build the academic record six months before the circuit argument.**

The most common appellate failure mode in high-stakes constitutional litigation is arriving at the circuit without an academic record. Judges and their clerks read law review articles; amicus briefs citing peer-reviewed analysis are treated differently from amicus briefs citing advocacy documents. The framework's domain files identify the specific research gaps and the specific scholars who have published on each domain's legal questions.

For each major circuit argument: identify the two or three most relevant recent law review articles. If the gap they should fill has not been filled, assign a law school clinic to write the working paper 6+ months before argument. Venue strategy: for Domain 6 arguments, target Harvard Law Review Forum and Yale Law Journal online (both accept rapid-response submissions within weeks). For Domain 29 arguments: Just Security and Lawfare publish within days.

### Common Failure Modes

**Forum non-selection**: Filing first in the closest court rather than the court with the strongest doctrinal environment. The 5th Circuit has been hostile; the 4th and D.D.C. have been more favorable. Deliberate circuit selection is not forum shopping — it is strategy.

**Racing to a hostile ruling**: Filing a novel constitutional claim before the academic record exists. The result is a bad precedent in a hostile circuit that constrains the next round. Build the record first.

**Treating Loper Bright as defense-only**: Loper Bright eliminated deference to agency interpretations, which means both pro- and anti-regulatory rulings are now subject to independent judicial statutory analysis. This opens challenges to regulatory rollbacks that previously would have been deferred to. Offensive use of Loper Bright in environmental, labor, and surveillance domains is underutilized as of April 2026.

**Failing to coordinate with the AG coalition**: The 22-state AG coalition has filed 71 lawsuits with a 79% win rate (litigation-tracker-2026.md). Every major civil rights case should be assessed for whether a parallel AG filing would strengthen standing, expand remedies, or create a second circuit entry point.

## 3. Executive Agencies — Career Leadership Teams

**Who this is for**: Agency deputy secretaries, career Senior Executive Service (SES) officials, agency counsels, and program directors who remain in federal executive agencies and are navigating political pressure while trying to maintain institutional function. Also applicable for state agency directors who are absorbing federal functions that have been vacated.

---

### Opening Scenario: April 2026

An EPA regional administrator has a problem. The agency has lost 40%+ of its enforcement staff through DOGE cuts and Schedule Policy/Career reclassifications. The political appointees who replaced career program directors have directed the regional office to deprioritize enforcement actions against industrial methane emitters, citing the administration's deregulatory agenda. The regional administrator has statutory authority to pursue enforcement independently — the Clean Air Act gives regional administrators direct enforcement jurisdiction — but has been told informally that doing so will trigger a Schedule Policy/Career review of their position.

This scenario is not hypothetical. It is replicated across EPA, NLRB, FCC, FDA, HHS, and DOJ career divisions in 2026. The 35-domain framework provides career officials with: (a) a documented record of which agency functions are legally mandated versus politically discretionary; (b) a playbook for what can be protected through existing authority; and (c) a coordination network with peer states and civil society organizations to extend institutional capacity when federal capacity is compromised.

---

### 5-Step Playbook

**Step 1: Audit your agency's functions against the domain dependency structure.**

Domain 2 (Civil Service and Institutional Integrity) documents the specific mechanisms of institutional capture: Schedule F/Career/Policy reclassification, politicized hiring, and constructive discharge through hostile working conditions. Before any other step, map your agency's functions to determine which are:

(a) Legally mandated: functions Congress has required by statute, where failure to perform exposes the agency and responsible officials to judicial enforcement. These have the strongest protection — document them explicitly as statutory mandates, not policy choices.

(b) Legally authorized but discretionary: functions within the agency's statutory authority that political appointees can legitimately reprioritize without violating law. These can be deprioritized under political direction without legal exposure for career officials who follow the direction.

(c) Legally required by consent decree or court order: functions where the agency is bound by court order, regardless of political direction. Violating a court order is contempt — political appointees who direct violation of consent decrees face personal liability. Document these separately; the consent-decree-defiance-tracker.md in this archive documents current cases.

This audit creates the map for what career officials must protect, what they can defer, and what they cannot be held personally accountable for declining under political direction.

**Step 2: Identify your agency's state-level parallel capacity.**

Domain 9 (Federalism and Local Power) and the Activation Architecture's parallel institutions analysis identify which state agencies can absorb federal functions when federal capacity is compromised. For each of your agency's mandated functions, identify the state-level analog:

EPA enforcement → State environmental agencies (33 states have independent environmental enforcement authority that does not depend on federal cooperation)
NLRB labor enforcement → State labor relations boards (several states have independent boards with full authority over state-covered employers)
CFPB consumer protection → State AG consumer protection divisions and state banking commissions
DOJ Civil Rights Division → State civil rights agencies and state AGs with independent authority

This is not abdication — it is network management. Career officials who maintain relationships with state counterparts before functions are lost can facilitate a more functional transfer when the federal capacity is compromised. The Governors Safeguarding Democracy network and the Democratic Attorneys General Association both have standing working groups on federal function absorption.

**Step 3: Document everything using the whistleblower protection architecture.**

The Whistleblower Protection Act (5 U.S.C. 2302) protects career civil servants who disclose illegal orders, misuse of government information, and violations of law and regulation. The Office of Special Counsel (OSC) handles WPA complaints, but the OSC may be compromised under current political appointments. The backup channels are: Inspector General offices (which retain some independence), congressional oversight (direct contact with committee staff is legally protected disclosure), and federal courts (False Claims Act for financial fraud).

Domain 26 (Government Accountability) documents the IG firing pattern and identifies which IGs retain functional independence. For each illegal or potentially illegal directive received: create a contemporaneous written record (time, date, specific instruction, who gave it, what legal authority was claimed). This record has two purposes: it protects the career official in any future retaliation proceeding; and it is the evidentiary record for any subsequent accountability process.

Do not wait for a major escalatory event to start documenting. Document routine political pressure on routine enforcement decisions from day one.

**Step 4: Use the framework's interstate compact analysis to build peer-state coordination now.**

The RGGI model — 12 states coordinating on carbon emissions without federal involvement — is the template for every domain where federal capacity is being vacated. For EPA regional officials: the state environmental compact analysis in Domain 9 identifies which states are already coordinating on air quality, water quality, and toxic waste enforcement outside the federal cooperative agreement structure.

For NLRB regional officials: several states have independent labor boards. Coordinate with state labor commissioners on cases where state jurisdiction overlaps federal jurisdiction — when the NLRB's federal enforcement is compromised, cases can often proceed through state labor law.

For HHS and Medicaid administrators: Domain 11's analysis of multistate Medicaid negotiation identifies the compact mechanism for joint drug price negotiation when federal negotiation authority is blocked. State Medicaid directors in the 17 states that have signed mutual assistance MOUs can receive this brief and assess applicability.

**Step 5: Protect your agency's data and institutional knowledge.**

Domain 21 (Data Privacy and Surveillance) documents that DOGE personnel accessed SSA databases without authorization and that compromised federal data systems create spillover vulnerabilities. For career agency leadership: conduct a data sovereignty audit identifying which agency databases are connected to compromised federal systems, what data has been shared outside normal authorization, and what the protocols are for data that should not leave the agency.

Create and maintain offline (or state-hosted) backups of critical agency records: enforcement case files, consent decree documentation, institutional guidance documents, and technical expertise repositories. The goal is not paranoia — it is ensuring that the institutional knowledge needed to restore agency function after political conditions change is not lost.

### Common Failure Modes

**Waiting for federal guidance that is not coming**: Agencies accustomed to top-down direction are structurally disadvantaged in an environment where political appointee guidance is legally suspect. Career officials with statutory authority should use it.

**Conflating political direction with legal mandate**: Not every political appointee instruction is legally binding. Instructions that require violating statute, court order, or consent decree are not legitimate orders — they expose the issuing official to liability, not the career official who declines. The statutory mandate audit makes this distinction concrete.

**Losing institutional knowledge through attrition**: When expert staff leave, the institutional knowledge required for eventual restoration leaves with them. Career officials should create documented repositories of technical expertise, enforcement case records, and institutional guidance that survive personnel turnover.

**Failing to notify state AGs of planned rollbacks**: State AGs can only challenge regulatory rollbacks they know about. Career officials who have legitimate channels for communicating pending regulatory actions to state government counterparts should use them — this is legal and appropriate federal-state cooperation.

## 4. Universities — Law Schools and Policy Schools

**Who this is for**: Law school deans, policy school deans, clinical program directors, research center directors, and individual faculty who want to connect the framework to curriculum, research agendas, and institutional positioning. Also applicable for university counsel and government relations staff.

---

### Opening Scenario: April 2026

Harvard Law School faces a $2.2B federal funding freeze tied to compliance demands from the Department of Education. The demands include: curriculum review for "viewpoint diversity," student organization registration with federal databases, and termination of programs the administration deems "ideological." Harvard's lawyers are preparing a First Amendment challenge. Meanwhile, three clinical programs that represent immigrants and civil rights plaintiffs are under informal pressure to cease representation of clients adverse to federal interests. And the Harvard Law Review has been informally warned that accepting certain articles could jeopardize the institution's funding status.

This is Domain 27 (Higher Education and Academic Freedom) in real time. The framework documents that U.S. institutional autonomy scores fell from 3.3 (2019) to 1.7 (2025) on the Academic Freedom Index — faster than Hungary's decline under Orban's university takeover. The Sanders HELP Committee documented 17 First Amendment court rulings against this administration in academic freedom cases. The framework provides law schools and policy schools with a specific five-step response.

---

### 5-Step Playbook

**Step 1: Apply Domain 27's four-track threat map to your institution immediately.**

Domain 27 identifies four simultaneous threat tracks: (1) federal funding leverage tied to ideological compliance; (2) DEI prohibition-driven preemptive self-censorship (450 campuses in 48 states, Chronicle of Higher Education tracking); (3) student and faculty visa revocations for protected speech (6,000+ visa revocations; Khalil, Oztürk, Mahdawi, Khan Suri landmark cases); and (4) accreditation weaponization.

Every institution should run a self-assessment against all four tracks now, not when a specific threat materializes. The assessment questions: Has your institution received any communication tying federal funding to content or program compliance? Has any faculty member self-censored or documented pressure to do so? Have any international students or faculty faced visa complications? Has your accreditor communicated any federally-sourced compliance concern? Document the answers. The documentation becomes the factual record for any First Amendment litigation.

**Step 2: Identify your institution's research agenda gaps using domain-specific knowledge gap mapping.**

Each domain section flags empirical questions the framework raises but cannot answer with existing evidence. These are publication opportunities. The most urgent for law schools and policy schools in April 2026:

Domain 6 (Judicial Independence): What is the current circuit-level pattern of shadow docket use? Empirical answer requires systematic coding of SCOTUS emergency docket orders since 2022. This is a tractable empirical research project for a law school clinic or research center.

Domain 29 (Prosecutorial Weaponization): What procedural markers distinguish politically motivated federal prosecutions from ordinary enforcement actions? The 22-case retaliatory prosecution dataset in Domain 29, Section 2 is the starting point; rigorous empirical analysis of charging timelines, announcement procedures, and case outcomes is publishable and important.

Domain 1 (Electoral Reform): Does state-level RCV adoption change candidate recruitment patterns? Tractable with state candidate filing data from Maine, Alaska, and New York. A political science or law review article on this question fills a genuine evidence gap.

Domain 35 (SCOTUS 2026 Term): What are the doctrinal consequences of Trump v. Slaughter for each independent agency's statutory scheme? This is a pure doctrinal analysis that can be completed within weeks of the ruling and published immediately in Just Security, Lawfare, or a law review's online companion. Every major law school should have a team pre-assigned to this analysis before the ruling is issued, so publication can happen within 72 hours.

**Step 3: Integrate the framework into curriculum at three levels.**

Level 1 (Undergraduate and JD core): The executive summary (democratic-renewal-executive-summary.md) and domain-specific sections function as primary course readings in constitutional law, administrative law, public policy, and political science courses. The framework's structure — problem diagnosis, reform proposal, evidence base, implementation pathway — is itself analytically teachable as a policy analysis methodology.

Level 2 (JD clinics and policy school capstones): Assign students to select one domain, critique the framework's analysis using disciplinary methods, and produce either a policy brief (policy school) or a case assessment (law school clinic). Domain 29 students can assess the SPLC indictment's legal defects using the bank fraud Thompson v. United States contradiction documented in the domain file. Domain 6 students can analyze the shadow docket pattern using the judicial threat data (564 threats FY2025, cited in the domain). Domain 1 students can model redistricting outcomes using the Texas 2025 mid-decade gerrymander documentation.

Level 3 (Executive education): Use the framework's three-wave implementation sequence as a scenario planning tool for government officials and nonprofit leaders. The tracking template in the implementation toolkit becomes the session's working document. This is the format that reaches state agency directors, nonprofit executives, and legislative staff who cannot commit to a semester course but can commit to a two-day executive program.

**Step 4: Develop a rapid-response publication protocol for major court rulings.**

The most valuable academic contribution in a democracy crisis is not the monograph — it is the 3,000-word rapid-response analysis published within 72 hours of a major ruling. Traditional academic publication cycles (12-24 months) are structurally misaligned with the speed at which institutional crises move.

Build the rapid-response capacity before you need it. For each of the major rulings expected in the current SCOTUS term (Trump v. Slaughter on independent agency removal; Watson v. RNC on mail ballot grace periods; any ruling affecting Domain 35 case list), pre-assign a faculty team, identify the publication venue (Just Security, Lawfare, Harvard Law Review Forum, Yale Law Journal online companion), and draft the structural template. The factual sections fill in after the ruling; the doctrinal sections can be substantially pre-written against each expected holding.

**Step 5: Use the institution's government relations office to advocate for Domain 27 statutory protections.**

The framework proposes three specific statutory responses to the Domain 27 crisis: an Academic Freedom Protection Act (prohibiting federal funding conditions that require ideological compliance); an INA Section 237(a)(4)(C) amendment (prohibiting speech-based visa revocations); and accreditor independence codification. These are not abstract proposals — they are legislation that law school government relations staff can advocate for concretely.

At minimum: brief your institution's government relations office on the three proposed statutes. Identify the relevant Senate committee staff (Senate HELP Committee for education provisions; Senate Judiciary for visa reform). If your institution's alumni include Senate staff or members, use those relationships for targeted advocacy on these specific bills.

## 5. Civil Rights Organizations — Advocacy Coalitions

**Who this is for**: Executive directors, campaign directors, coalition coordinators, communications directors, and legal directors at civil rights organizations and advocacy coalitions. Also applicable for the coordination staff at umbrella organizations like the Leadership Conference on Civil and Human Rights and the ACLU.

---

### Opening Scenario: April 2026

After the SPLC indictment (April 21, 2026), over 100 civil rights groups, labor unions, and religious organizations signed a mutual defense pact. The pact established collective response commitments: press statements, legal solidarity, and shared defense strategy. But within two weeks, the coalition was fragmenting on a predictable problem: which organization takes the lead on which response action, and how do 100 organizations coordinate without 100 simultaneous press releases diluting the signal?

This is the coordination problem the 35-domain framework is built to solve. The Leadership Conference on Civil and Human Rights coalition covers 230+ member organizations across most of the framework's 35 domains. The ACLU's 2025 "Defeat, Delay, Dilute" strategy — the most successful large-scale civil rights coordination to date — succeeded because it established explicit sequencing before the crisis, not improvised sequencing after it. Here is the operational translation.

---

### 5-Step Playbook

**Step 1: Map every coalition member to the 35 domains and find the gravitational center.**

List every coalition member organization. Assign each to its primary domain: voting rights → Domain 1; criminal justice → Domain 14; immigration → Domain 16; labor → Domain 17; environmental justice → Domain 15; data privacy → Domain 21; racial justice → Domain 22; higher education → Domain 27; prosecutorial accountability → Domain 29. Now identify which two or three domain clusters have the most member organizations.

That cluster is your gravitational center — the area where your coalition's existing expertise and membership is densest. Your campaign should lead from that center, not from the domain where you are weakest. A coalition led by the Leadership Conference, NAACP, and ACLU has a gravitational center in Domains 7, 14, 22, and 29 — First Amendment, criminal justice, racial equity, and prosecutorial accountability. Those are the lead domains. Labor and environmental domains are adjacent and should be sequenced second, after the lead domains have established momentum.

**Step 2: Assign domain leads — one organization per domain — before the campaign launches.**

The most common coalition failure mode is every organization trying to lead on every issue, creating a cacophony of competing messaging and duplicated outreach. Assign one organization per domain as the "domain lead" responsible for: maintaining relationships with allied organizations on that domain, briefing coalition members on developments, coordinating action when a domain-specific crisis emerges, and being the primary media contact for that domain.

For a coalition covering 35 domains with 50+ member organizations, this requires a governance conversation before the campaign begins. The governance conversation has two parts: (a) which organization has the deepest expertise and existing relationships in each domain? (b) what is the decision-making process when domain leads disagree on timing or messaging? Establish both before the campaign opens. The ACLU's 2025 model is instructive: they designated internal lead teams for litigation (Domain 7), state advocacy (Domains 9, 33), and federal legislative advocacy (Domains 1, 2, 29) — with clear escalation procedures for cross-domain conflicts.

**Step 3: Use the framework's 12-week messaging sequence for campaign timing.**

The framework's theory of change is explicit: you cannot run all 35 issues simultaneously and win any of them. Choose one issue from your gravitational center for the first 12 weeks. The selection criteria: (a) highest polling support in your target geographies; (b) a specific near-term decision point — a vote, a regulatory deadline, a court hearing — that creates urgency; and (c) an existing organized base that can turn people out.

In April 2026, the highest-urgency decision points are: the April 28 Abrego Garcia/Xinis hearing (Domain 16 — immigration); the April 30 FISA Section 702 expiration deadline (Domain 21 — surveillance); the pending Nashville Crenshaw ruling on vindictive prosecution (Domain 29); and the upcoming May Day actions (Domain 7 — First Amendment). The coalition that sequences its 12-week lead campaign around the April 28-May 1 decision window — with messaging ready for multiple outcomes — is the coalition that establishes the narrative.

After 12 weeks, transition to the adjacent domain in the cross-domain synthesis. Frame it as connected: "we pushed back on prosecutorial weaponization because it is the instrument for suppressing all other rights; now we address the surveillance infrastructure that enables the targeting." This is the "Defeat, Delay, Dilute" cascade logic applied to the 35-domain structure.

**Step 4: Integrate litigation and advocacy timing.**

The ACLU's 2025 model demonstrated that litigation and advocacy are most powerful when sequenced, not run in parallel and independently managed. For each domain in your campaign: identify whether there is an active or potential lawsuit that will generate a court ruling during your campaign window. If yes, sequence your advocacy crescendo to coincide with the ruling window — either amplifying a favorable ruling with immediate public action, or converting an unfavorable ruling into a legislative mobilization moment.

For April 2026: the Xinis/Abrego Garcia hearing is a litigation moment for Domain 16. The coalition's advocacy should already have media and constituency mobilization ready to amplify whatever ruling emerges. If the ruling is favorable (Xinis enforces the deportation return order), the advocacy play is: this shows courts still function; mobilize for the next step. If the ruling is adverse (Xinis backs down under pressure), the advocacy play is: contempt and congressional oversight; contact Whitehouse and Raskin staff immediately with the documentation.

**Step 5: Apply the 3.5% threshold as an explicit campaign benchmark.**

Erica Chenoweth's finding — documented in the proposal's Section 1.3 — is that no nonviolent movement with active participation exceeding 3.5% of the population has ever failed to achieve its primary demand. In a congressional district, 3.5% is approximately 25,000 people. In a state of 10 million, it is 350,000. In the United States nationally, it is 11.6 million. The October 2025 No Kings protests reached 7 million — real, unprecedented, and not yet at threshold.

Make 3.5% an explicit, public campaign benchmark. Not just "build support" — "we need X sustained participants." Track against it publicly. This reframes the coalition's goal from ambiguous to specific, and it motivates the last mile of participation that most campaigns never achieve. The benchmark also makes the coalition legible to media and legislators: "we are at 6.2 million sustained participants, 53% of the threshold" is a concrete accountability metric.

### Common Failure Modes

**Issue siloing**: Running 35 separate domain campaigns with separate messaging, separate media contacts, and separate constituent lists. This leaves the framework's most powerful insight unused — the root cause is common across domains, and campaigns that make the connection are exponentially more persuasive than campaigns that treat each issue in isolation.

**Mobilization without infrastructure**: Building a coalition that surges during a crisis and then fragments when the immediate urgency passes. Domain leads must be assigned during the surge — if you wait until the next crisis to assign governance structure, the structure is never assigned.

**Ignoring the timing**: The proposal's implementation roadmap identifies specific decision-point windows (DOGE sunset July 4, Section 702 deadline April 30, Slaughter ruling expected June, redistricting cases before November). Coalition campaigns that are not timed to specific decision points are campaigns without urgency. Urgency is required to sustain the participation level that approaches the 3.5% threshold.

**Treating the defense pact as the end point**: The mutual defense pact created after the SPLC indictment is a coalition formation mechanism, not a campaign. The pact needs an operational governance structure — who calls whom, in what order, with what authority — or it is a press release, not a coalition.

## 6. Cross-Sector Decision Trees

### Master Sequencing Matrix: Which Domain First?

The framework's Tier 1 dependency structure is the master key. Three domains must be substantially advanced before most other domains can succeed through any institutional channel:

**Domain 6 (Judicial Independence)**: Must precede Domains 1, 2, 29, and 34. Courts are the enforcement mechanism for every other domain's litigation strategy. Without functionally independent courts, legal challenges to electoral suppression, civil service capture, and prosecutorial weaponization have no reliable enforcement mechanism.

**Domain 2 (Civil Service)**: Must precede Domains 11, 15, 18, and 20. All domestic policy implementation depends on agency capacity. An EPA at 60% capacity cannot enforce new clean electricity standards. An HHS with political appointees replacing career officials cannot administer expanded Medicaid. The MSPB without quorum cannot process civil service appeals.

**Domain 1 (Electoral Reform)**: Enables all legislative domains in Phases 2-3. Legislative passage of any proposal requires either a House flip in 2026 or enough Senate seats for a reform majority. Electoral reform that prevents further gerrymandering before 2028 is a prerequisite for the legislative pathway.

**For every sector**: regardless of your institution's primary domain focus, add Domains 2 and 6 to your engagement list. These are the domains that determine whether Wave 1 victories consolidate into durable change.
