# Phase 3 Playbook: State Attorneys General Coalitions

**Audience**: Coalition leads in Democratic AG offices; DAGA coordinators; state solicitors general; litigation partners (Democracy Docket, ACLU Voting Rights Project, Constitutional Accountability Center)

---

## Leverage Analysis

State Attorneys General are the single most consequential institutional actor in Wave 1 of the democratic recovery implementation roadmap. The 22-state Democratic AG coalition has filed 71 lawsuits against the Trump administration since January 2025, winning approximately 79% of resolved cases per the Progressive State Leaders Committee tracker as of April 2026. The landmark *Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump* (February 20, 2026, 6-3) — invalidating IEEPA tariff authority on major-questions-doctrine grounds — is the model for the architecture this coalition deploys.

Four structural advantages make AGs uniquely powerful:

**Parens patriae standing.** AGs can sue on behalf of their state's citizens without identifying individual plaintiffs. This standing theory is pre-validated across hundreds of cases and does not require finding a plaintiff willing to expose themselves.

**Existing litigation infrastructure.** AG offices have staff, expert relationships, and filing capability that can be activated within hours. The AFGE inauguration-night filing (January 20, 2025) is the operational speed standard; AG offices should match it.

**Multistate coordination.** Simultaneous filing across multiple circuits raises the political cost of shadow-docket reversal. The Supreme Court faces higher reputational risk issuing a stay that visibly halts TROs in five circuits than in one.

**Federalism standing theory.** AGs bring constitutional challenges on state-sovereignty grounds that individual plaintiffs cannot. The *Maryland v. Noem* DOGE/SSA data-access theory — Maryland's Medicaid administration was harmed by federal data access to SSA systems — is replicable in dozens of cooperative-program contexts.

The structural constraint is the Supreme Court's 6-3 shadow docket. Every favorable injunction is vulnerable to immediate administrative stay. The counter-strategy is circuit multiplication and private-plaintiff parallel tracks, not single-circuit dominance.

## Actionable Domains: Five Highest-Leverage Areas

### Domain 1 (Electoral Reform) — Primary Lead

The clearest standing theory for AG action lies in election interference challenges under Article I, Section 4 (Times, Places and Manner Clause). States have sovereign authority over election administration; a federal executive action that overrides state election systems without congressional authorization violates structural federalism directly.

The SAVE Act (H.R. 7296) challenge is the immediate active case. AG James's 22-state coalition opposes documentary voter registration requirements that would require birth certificate or passport for all federal voter registration, effectively eliminating online and mail-based registration for the populations that depend on them. The legal theory: the SAVE Act conflicts with the NVRA. Standing: states must redesign their registration infrastructure, incurring direct administrative costs. The 48-50 Senate vote defeat (April 2026 vote-a-rama) — with Collins, Murkowski, Tillis, and McConnell defecting — has preserved the filibuster barrier for now, but the May 2026 Senate floor escalation makes this the live pressure point.

Pre-election litigation preparation for November 2026 must be complete by August: TRO templates drafted for six scenarios (ICE near polling sites, voter roll purge orders, mail ballot rejection directives, refusal to certify results, SAVE Act enforcement, federal obstruction of local election administrators). Each template requires only factual insertion on the day of the trigger event.

### Domain 6 (Judicial Independence) — Coalition Support

The pending *Trump v. Slaughter* decision (expected June 2026) will formalize what the shadow docket already accomplished functionally: elimination of independent agency removal protections. State AGs must pre-stage challenge architecture now.

The standing theory comes from cooperative federalism: your state's EPA cooperative enforcement agreement depends on the EPA administrator. If the administrator is fired, the cooperative program is disrupted and the state's matching appropriation is stranded. This is the *Maryland v. Noem* template applied to the post-*Slaughter* scenario.

Three pre-written challenge templates — covering full overruling, functional narrowing, and prospective-only application of *Humphrey's Executor* — should be in reviewable draft form by Month 2, ready to file within 72 hours of the June ruling.

### Domain 23 (Trade/IEEPA) — Completed Model, Active Template

The *Learning Resources* architecture is the coalition's most successful completed case: 24-state AG simultaneous filing in the Court of International Trade alongside private plaintiffs; pre-staged academic record; 6-3 cross-ideological majority. Approximately $166 billion in tariff refunds ordered.

The Section 122 challenge is the active successor. The 24-state AG coalition filed in the CIT with oral argument April 10, 2026. Three vulnerabilities identified in argument: the government could not define "balance of payments" coherently, could not quantify the claimed deficit, and shifted its legal theory mid-argument. Ruling expected before Section 122's July 24, 2026 expiration.

The lesson for subsequent coalition actions: simultaneous filing with private plaintiffs creates redundant standing theories; the Court of International Trade is the correct venue for trade authority disputes; constitutional text arguments win even in this Court when statutory overreach is clear.

### Domain 29 (Prosecutorial Weaponization) — State Civil Rights Parallel Track

AG standing in federal prosecution matters is limited by the federal-state prosecutorial separation. Three available tracks:

Track 1: Amicus briefs in federal prosecutorial misconduct cases. The SPLC criminal indictment (April 21, 2026) — charging 11 counts including wire fraud against a civil rights organization for operating an informant program it had shared with law enforcement for decades — is the highest-priority current case. Multiple former federal prosecutors identified the indictment as defective at the level of basic legal elements. AGs who have civil rights enforcement relationships with SPLC have concrete standing to argue the prosecution disrupts those relationships. At least five AG offices should coordinate on the amicus brief.

Track 2: State-court civil rights parallel actions under state civil rights statutes. State AGs can bring state-court actions against the same conduct targeted by weaponized federal prosecutions, operating outside federal court's reach.

Track 3: Joint investigation protection. AGs with ongoing joint DOJ investigations can challenge DOJ actions that compromise those investigations.

### Domain 34 (Congressional Power of the Purse) — Federal Fund Impoundment

States have standing to challenge federal fund impoundment when those funds are part of state-administered cooperative programs. The *Pennhurst* line establishes that the federal government cannot unilaterally alter cooperative grant terms after states have relied on them.

$425+ billion in congressional appropriations have been withheld since January 2025 through four mechanisms: agency-level holds, OMB Category C apportionment manipulation, Treasury payment system interference, and pocket rescissions. GAO opened 39 separate ICA investigation tracks. Each confirmed violation — NIH, Head Start, NEVI, school energy grants — creates an APA challenge vehicle for the states whose programs were defunded.

The First Circuit's *New York v. Trump* (March 2026) upheld a preliminary injunction covering frozen federal assistance, establishing the circuit precedent. AGs should map their states' top three cooperative agreements by dollar value and have APA challenge templates complete for impoundment of each.

## Year 1 Sequencing

### Months 1-3: Foundation and Pre-Election Architecture

Month 1 is litigation triage. Convene civil rights deputy AGs from all 22 states for a coordination session. Map all 71 active cases to the domain framework; assign each domain a "lead AG office" with backup. Produce a one-page litigation triage matrix; update monthly.

Month 2 is post-*Slaughter* pre-staging. Coordinate with Yale Rule of Law Clinic, Harvard Democracy and Rule of Law Clinic, and Just Security to pre-assign academic analysis teams. Three standing memo drafts complete and ready to file within 72 hours of the June ruling.

Month 3 is election pre-positioning. Complete pre-election litigation preparation for six election interference scenarios. Document circuit assignment protocols. Pre-assign "wave 1" and "wave 2" states so filing sequence is automatic on the day of a trigger event. Democracy Docket and ACLU Voting Rights Project parallel filing protocols agreed.

### Months 3-6: Coalition Expansion and Domain Coverage

Month 4: Coordinate standing brief with AFGE/NTEU on Schedule Policy/Career litigation. States whose federal-state cooperative programs depend on specific career positions have parens patriae standing in the civil service challenge. Two AG offices should join AFGE's Second Amended Complaint as amici or co-plaintiffs.

Month 5: Section 232 challenge template. Using *Learning Resources* as the template, draft standing memos for the next IEEPA-adjacent executive trade authority claim. File within 30 days of any new tariff action.

Month 6: Full coalition review. Which cases are on track for SCOTUS cert? Which have shadow docket vulnerabilities? Update triage matrix; identify cases needing additional state joiners for political weight.

### Months 7-12: Pre-Election Execution

Months 7-8: All pre-election TRO templates tested in a tabletop exercise. One AG office per circuit designated as lead filer for each election interference scenario. Democracy Docket and ACLU on 2-hour standby.

Month 9: Post-midterm consolidation. Assess coalition composition changes. Which Republican AGs — in consumer protection or antitrust contexts — could join specific actions?

Month 10: Legislative advocacy pivot. In states with new or expanded Democratic chambers, begin state civil service protection legislation, voting access omnibus, and state Medicaid floor bill.

Month 11: Interstate compact drafting begins. Model language for two compacts: (1) voting rights compact under which signatory states commit to specific access standards regardless of federal minimum; (2) cooperative enforcement compact for environmental enforcement.

## Case Studies

**Case Study 1: IEEPA Tariff Coalition — The *Learning Resources* Architecture (2025-2026)**

The 24-state AG challenge to IEEPA tariffs is the operational model for Phase 3 AG deployment. Architecture: (1) AGs filed simultaneously in the Court of International Trade with private plaintiffs, creating redundant standing theories; (2) academic record was pre-staged before argument, including Yale Budget Lab ($30 billion annual economic drag), Tax Policy Center ($1,050 average household cost), and Peterson Institute ($1,200 median household burden) analyses; (3) pre-written standing memos drew on existing Commerce Clause and major-questions precedent. Result: SCOTUS 6-3, Roberts writing, invalidated IEEPA tariff authority. $166 billion in refunds ordered. Template: multistate simultaneous filing + private plaintiff parallel tracks + academic record pre-staged before argument = durable SCOTUS majority even on the 6-3 Court.

**Case Study 2: *Maryland v. Noem* DOGE Data Access (2025)**

Maryland AG Anthony Brown filed challenging DOGE's access to SSA payment systems as a harm to Maryland's Medicaid administration — which depends on SSA data to determine Medicaid eligibility. Standing theory: state program harm from federal data access, not individual privacy harm. This template is replicable for every state cooperative program whose federal administrative counterpart has been disrupted by DOGE personnel actions. Lesson: states do not need to prove their residents' individual harm; disruption of the cooperative program infrastructure creates state standing directly.

**Case Study 3: Section 122 Coalition Challenge (April 2026)**

The Section 122 challenge extended the *Learning Resources* architecture immediately after SCOTUS's IEEPA ruling. When the administration pivoted to Section 122 within hours of losing on IEEPA, the coalition was filing within days. Three legal vulnerabilities surfaced at April 10, 2026 oral argument: inability to define "balance of payments," inability to quantify the claimed deficit, mid-argument legal theory shift. Lesson: the coalition's rapid pivot from IEEPA to Section 122 demonstrates that the operational infrastructure built for one case transfers immediately to the successor case. The coalition does not need to rebuild each time; it re-deploys.

## Natural Allies and Conflicts

**Natural allies**: Civil service unions (AFGE standing in cooperative program cases); law school clinics (academic record production); civil rights coalitions (SPLC amicus coordination); state legislators (trifecta state legislative partners); religious coalitions (faith hospital EMTALA and Medicaid standing); labor unions (AFL-CIO parallel standing on NLRB independence).

**Key conflicts**:

*AGs vs. Federal Courts on universal injunctions*: Post-*Trump v. CASA*, universal injunction authority is limited. Some AG offices prefer state-court parallel actions; others prefer federal APA vacatur. Resolution: three-track strategy (federal APA vacatur, state-court actions, class action certification) eliminates the either-or. Pre-assign tracks by case type.

*Trade policy cross-cutting conflict*: AFGE's customs enforcement members administer the tariff programs AG coalitions challenge. No-targeting agreement: AGs challenge legal authority, not the workers who administer programs. AFGE publicly supports the rule-of-law challenge while advocating worker transition protections.

*Competitive-state AG electoral pressure*: AGs in Pennsylvania, Michigan, Wisconsin, Nevada face pressure from joining high-visibility actions. Resolution: frame-specific coalition composition; competitive-state AGs lead local-harm-specific cases (DOGE impoundment harming their state's programs) rather than national symbolic actions.

## Key Constraints

**Shadow docket vulnerability.** Every favorable injunction can be stayed by the 6-3 Court within days. Counter-strategy is circuit multiplication: simultaneous filings across four or five circuits raise the political cost of shadow-docket reversal. The Court faces higher reputational exposure halting TROs in five circuits simultaneously than in one.

**Political limitations in competitive states.** Democratic AGs in competitive states cannot join every coalition action without electoral consequences. Coalition design must accommodate this by distinguishing high-profile symbolic cases from locally-grounded cooperative-program cases.

**Jurisdictional limits on federal prosecutions.** The federal-state prosecutorial separation limits direct AG intervention in weaponized federal prosecution cases. The three-track approach (amicus, state-court parallel, joint investigation protection) routes around this limit without requiring direct federal prosecution intervention.

**Capacity concentration.** New York and Massachusetts are the highest-capacity coalition offices. Every cascade plan must account for a scenario in which one of these lead offices is under special pressure or facing a contested AG race.
