## Executive Summary

The federal judiciary — and the Supreme Court in particular — has reached a structural legitimacy crisis without modern precedent. Supreme Court approval has fallen from 70% in 2020 to 41% in May 2026, with 71% of Republicans viewing it positively versus 26% of Democrats: a partisan approval gap unprecedented in the Court's history. The crisis has three distinct structural drivers: a lifetime tenure architecture that concentrates ideological capture into rare appointment moments and then locks in that capture for decades; an ethics enforcement gap that the Court's own November 2023 code adoption failed to close; and a shadow docket that has expanded from an administrative convenience into a primary policymaking mechanism operating without the deliberation, transparency, or precedential weight of the merits docket.

The evidence base for reform is unusually strong. Seventy-four percent of Americans support term limits in polling — including 61% of non-MAGA Republicans and 40% of MAGA Republicans. Sixty-nine percent support mandatory defined tenure over lifetime appointments. Seventy-eight percent support a formal ethics code. Eighty-three percent favor mandatory recusal for financial conflicts. These figures cross partisan lines at majorities that are rare in contemporary US politics, establishing a public mandate for structural reform that awaits only the legislative vehicle.

The constitutional feasibility question is genuinely contested but not foreclosed. The dominant scholarly view holds that true term limits — requiring Article III judges to leave service entirely — requires a constitutional amendment, because Article III's "during good Behaviour" language protects judicial tenure. However, a growing body of constitutional scholarship, supported by the Brennan Center and Fix the Court, argues that Congress can achieve functionally equivalent results through a statutory "active/senior justice" model: justices serve 18 years in active capacity on the Supreme Court, then transition to senior status on lower federal courts. This model uses the existing senior judge infrastructure, which has operated constitutionally for over a century.

The international precedent is clear and converging: no other major democracy grants lifetime tenure to constitutional court judges. Germany's Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht), universally regarded as the gold standard of constitutional review, uses fixed 12-year terms with mandatory retirement at 68. Canada mandates retirement at 75. Australia, Brazil, and the UK all impose mandatory retirement ages. The US approach is an outlier by any comparative measure, and the international record shows no evidence that fixed terms or mandatory retirement undermine judicial independence when paired with adequate salary protections and insulation from political retaliation.

The coalition activation pathway is most viable post-2026 midterms. If Democrats regain either chamber — even a single-chamber majority — the judicial reform legislative window opens in January 2027. The statutory approach to term limits requires only simple majority votes in both chambers; the constitutional amendment approach requires supermajorities that are not currently available but could become coalition-building anchors in a reform Congress. The three most immediately achievable reforms — a binding ethics code with enforcement mechanism, shadow docket transparency requirements, and the bipartisan judgeships expansion bill — can pass with any Democratic majority and potentially attract Republican support in the 120th Congress.

## Zone 1: Legislative Inventory

### 1.1 Term Limits Legislation — Current Bills and Status

The 119th Congress has seen multiple term limits bills introduced, with no realistic prospect of passage under Republican control but establishing the legislative baseline for a future reform window.

**H.R. 1074 — Supreme Court Term Limits and Regular Appointments Act of 2025** (119th Congress): Introduced by Rep. Hank Johnson (D-GA) and reintroduced as a standing vehicle for progressive judicial reform. The bill would establish 18-year terms for future justices, with the president nominating one justice during the first and third years after each presidential election, and justices transitioning to senior status after 18 years. The regular appointment schedule is designed to end the randomness of current vacancies, which has allowed the composition of the Court to depend heavily on which presidents happen to encounter retirements or deaths.

**Confidence: 95%** — confirmed live bill text at Congress.gov.

**Supreme Court Biennial Appointments and Term Limits Act** (Whitehouse/Booker/Blumenthal/Padilla): The Senate companion to the Johnson bill, introduced by Sen. Sheldon Whitehouse (D-RI). Establishes 18-year nonrenewable terms, biennial appointment schedule. Has not advanced out of committee under Republican Judiciary Committee control. The bill's existence creates the legislative infrastructure for rapid introduction in a reform Congress.

**Manchin-Welch Constitutional Amendment** (December 2024): A proposed constitutional amendment introduced by Senators Welch (D-VT) and Manchin (I-WV) that would impose 18-year term limits on new justices through formal Article V amendment rather than statute. This approach resolves the constitutional uncertainty by directly amending Article III's "good Behaviour" language, but requires 38 state ratifications — a 10-to-15-year timeline under favorable conditions.

**H.R. 3422** and competing Republican bills: The Republican response has been primarily symbolic, with proposals to constitutionally fix the court at nine seats (blocking expansion). A House Judiciary subcommittee held a hearing on "Court Packing: A Threat to the Supreme Court's Legitimacy" on May 21, 2026, framing reform as a threat — which signals the legislative battle lines in the current Congress.

**Confidence: 90%** — hearing confirmed via House Judiciary Committee Democrats press release.

### 1.2 Court Expansion Legislation

**The court expansion debate** has been reactivated by Rep. Jamie Raskin (D-MD), Ranking Member of the House Judiciary Committee, who has argued that the 13-circuit federal judiciary logically implies 13 Supreme Court justices. The "13 circuits, 13 justices" framing is an attempt to depoliticize expansion by grounding it in structural rather than political arguments.

The constitutional baseline is clear: Congress has plenary authority over the Supreme Court's size under Article III. The Court has been expanded from 6 to 10 members and contracted from 10 to 7 in the 19th century without constitutional challenge. There is no Article III barrier to expansion — only the political economy barrier of the norm against it.

The 2021 Presidential Commission on the Supreme Court identified "profound disagreement" on expansion even among reform advocates, with many commissioners warning that expansion could destabilize rather than reform the Court. This finding accurately reflects the strategic divide among reformers: term limits commands 74% public approval; court expansion commands substantially less.

**Confidence: 90%** — commission findings confirmed via published final report (December 7, 2021).

### 1.3 Ethics Reform Legislation

**Supreme Court Ethics, Recusal, and Transparency (SCERT) Act** (Whitehouse/Johnson): The lead ethics reform vehicle. The bill would: (a) require justices to adopt a binding code of conduct; (b) create an investigation and enforcement mechanism using panels of appellate judges; (c) mandate written recusal explanations; (d) require disclosure of financial connections to parties and amici. Advanced out of the Senate Judiciary Committee under Democratic control in the 118th Congress but died in the Republican-controlled Senate.

The proximate cause of the SCERT Act's introduction was the ProPublica and New York Times investigative series documenting undisclosed gifts to Justices Clarence Thomas (private jet travel, tuition payments for family members, real estate transactions with Harlan Crow) and Samuel Alito (private jet travel from hedge fund billionaire Paul Singer, who subsequently had cases before the Court). The Court's November 2023 adoption of its first-ever code of ethics was a direct response to this reporting, but the code has no enforcement mechanism — justices police themselves against their own standards.

The critical structural gap: unlike every other federal judge (governed by the Code of Conduct for United States Judges), Supreme Court justices operate under a voluntary self-policing code. The SCERT Act's enforcement mechanism — panels of appellate judges reviewing ethics complaints — mirrors existing structures for the lower federal judiciary. An ABA report described the unenforced code as creating "the elephant in the room": "even the most stringent and aggressive ethics rules don't mean all that much if there's no mechanism for enforcing them."

**Confidence: 95%** — ProPublica reporting confirmed, SCERT Act legislative history confirmed.

### 1.4 Shadow Docket Reform Legislation

Rep. Raskin introduced a three-bill package addressing the shadow docket:

- **SHADOW Act** (Supreme Court Honesty and Disclosure of Orders and Writs Act): Requires signed written opinions for stays and injunctions granted on appeal — ending the practice of unexplained 5-4 orders that can halt major executive actions or block lower court injunctions without any explanation.
- **SCCOTUS Act** (Supreme Court Certiorari Oversight and Transparency Standards Act): Increases transparency in case selection.
- **Federal Funding Protection Act**: Clarifies federal court jurisdiction in monetary claims against the United States.

The empirical case for shadow docket reform is now substantial. Between October 7, 2024 and August 9, 2025, there were 119 emergency applications to the Court. The Trump administration, in its second term, sought emergency stays in no fewer than 20 significant cases in the last half of the 2024 term alone; the Court granted 17 of those 20 — and 75% of all shadow docket grants had conservative outcomes. Justice Elena Kagan's dissent captured the accountability problem: "Our emergency docket should never be used, as it has been this year, to permit what our own precedent bars. Still more, it should not be used, as it also has been, to transfer government authority from Congress to the President, and thus to reshape the Nation's separation of powers."

**Confidence: 95%** — Kagan dissent confirmed, Brennan Center shadow docket tracker confirmed, SCOTUSblog emergency docket data confirmed.

### 1.5 Judgeships Expansion Legislation

The least politically contested and most immediately actionable reform is the federal judgeships expansion bill. The federal judiciary has sought 71 new judgeships to address growing caseloads. Since 1991, authorized district court judgeships have increased only 4% while filings have grown 30% over the same period. Cases pending more than three years rose 346% over the two decades to 2024.

A bipartisan bill was introduced in September 2025 to add 63 new district court judgeships and 3 temporary positions, closely following the Judicial Conference's recommendations. This bill represents the clearest bipartisan pathway for judicial reform — it addresses the access-to-justice crisis rather than the ideological composition crisis, which allows Republican co-sponsors to support it without conceding ground on the politics of the current Court.

**Confidence: 90%** — federal judiciary caseload statistics confirmed via uscourts.gov, bill introduction confirmed.

### 1.6 FISA Court Reform

The Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court (FISC) represents a different category of structural judicial failure: a secret court whose classified opinions are inaccessible to the public or most of Congress, and which operates without the adversarial process that characterizes ordinary judicial proceedings.

The FISA Accountability and Extension Act of 2026 (S.3696, 119th Congress) addresses one dimension: requiring the Speaker and Senate Majority Leader to submit lists of individuals for designation as amicus curiae in FISC proceedings. The SAFE Act (S.4280) includes a dedicated FISA court reform title.

The Wyden declassification deal embedded in the April 30, 2026 45-day extension commitment requires disclosure of a March 17, 2026 FISC opinion within 15 days — a modest but precedent-setting instance of congressional compulsion of FISC transparency. The June 12-15, 2026 deadline for that disclosure (documented in the existing litigation tracker) is the immediate monitoring trigger for this research domain.

**Confidence: 90%** — bill text confirmed at Congress.gov, Wyden declassification deal confirmed.

### 1.7 State-Level Judicial Reform Observations

At the state level, 47 states require their supreme court justices to serve fixed terms, subject to reelection or reappointment processes. Only six states have lifetime tenure for their highest court judges. The federal judicial architecture is therefore not just an international outlier but a domestic outlier as well — the overwhelming majority of American state judiciaries operate under systems that the federal reformers are proposing for the Supreme Court.

This creates a politically powerful asymmetry: reform advocates can point not just to Germany and Canada but to Montana, Wisconsin, and Ohio as functioning systems with fixed judicial terms.

**Confidence: 95%** — judicial selection data confirmed via Brennan Center and IAALS.

## Zone 2: Constitutional Feasibility

### 2.1 The Core Article III Question

Article III of the Constitution states that federal judges "shall hold their Offices during good Behaviour." The traditional interpretation is that this language guarantees lifetime tenure so long as a judge engages in no misconduct that would justify removal through the impeachment process. On this reading, term limits require a constitutional amendment.

The contending scholarship argues that "good Behaviour" is a conduct standard, not a duration guarantee. Judges hold their offices for as long as their behavior meets the standard — which is not the same as "holding office for life." Under this reading, Congress could restructure judicial service without violating Article III's protection by ensuring judges remain in "judicial service" throughout their careers, just not on the Supreme Court specifically after 18 years.

**The Active/Senior Justice Model**: The most constitutionally defensible statutory path — developed in detail by Fix the Court, the Brennan Center, and scholars including Daniel Hemel and Eric Posner — adapts the existing "senior judge" infrastructure. Under 28 U.S.C. §§ 371-372, federal judges already transition to "senior status" after serving a specified combination of age and years on the bench. Senior judges continue to receive their full salary (satisfying the Article III compensation protection) and continue to do judicial work (satisfying the "good Behaviour" tenure protection), but no longer serve on their primary court's active docket.

Applied to the Supreme Court: after 18 years as an active justice, a justice would transition to senior status, available to hear cases on the circuits or fill in on the Supreme Court during unexpected vacancies (illness, recusal, death), but no longer among the nine active members deciding cases. This structure keeps justices "in office" in every technical sense while functionally rotating the active composition.

The 2021 Presidential Commission analyzed this model and concluded it "warrants serious consideration" as a constitutionally viable statutory approach, while also noting that a constitutional amendment would provide more certainty. That bipartisan commission — which operated with explicit instructions not to take positions — could not achieve consensus on whether statutory term limits would survive constitutional challenge.

**Confidence: 90%** — commission findings confirmed, statutory model confirmed in scholarly literature.

### 2.2 The Amendment Pathway

If the statutory approach fails constitutional challenge, the amendment pathway requires: two-thirds of both chambers, then three-fourths of states (38 states). Currently, this is not achievable in the near-term political environment. But the amendment pathway has three strategic uses:

First, introducing an amendment resolution forces votes that create political records for the 2026 and 2028 election cycles. Members who vote against term limits for Supreme Court justices when 74% of their constituents support them will carry that vote.

Second, an amendment campaign creates a multi-year organizing infrastructure for judicial reform coalitions that could activate when the political window opens. The anti-apartheid movement's divestment campaign took decades before political conditions made it actionable; the movement infrastructure was built during the waiting period.

Third, if 34 states call for an Article V convention, the convention mechanism could be used — though this is the highest-risk pathway, because a convention cannot be constitutionally limited to a single subject, creating the "runaway convention" risk that Domain H of this research framework documents.

**Confidence: 85%** — amendment process confirmed, strategic uses are analytical assessment.

### 2.3 The Compensation Clause Constraint

Article III also provides that judicial compensation "shall not be diminished during their Continuance in Office." Any reform must preserve full compensation throughout a justice's service — including after transitioning to senior status. The active/senior model satisfies this by design: senior justices receive the same salary as active justices, creating no compensation diminution.

This constraint actually operates in reformers' favor: it means the primary Article III objection to term limits (undermining independence by removing the security of tenure) is addressed by the compensation guarantee. Justices who know they will receive full salary for life — regardless of the political reception to their decisions — have no financial incentive to rule to please future nominating presidents.

**Confidence: 95%** — constitutional text confirmed.

### 2.4 The Removal Power Case and Judicial Independence

The pending Trump v. Slaughter and Trump v. Cook cases, expected to be decided in June 2026, place the judicial independence question in a live political context. These cases ask whether the president can remove FTC commissioners and Federal Reserve governors without cause, effectively abolishing independent agencies. If the Court overturns Humphrey's Executor (1935), it will have transferred massive executive authority to the presidency by eliminating the constitutional basis for independent agencies.

This is relevant to judicial reform in two ways. First, a Supreme Court that has already concentrated power in the executive will likely be more receptive to executive-branch arguments against judicial independence constraints — making a statutory term limits law more likely to be struck down by the very justices it would most affect. Second, the political salience of the removal power cases creates an organic coalition for judicial reform among the same constituencies (labor, consumer protection, environmental, financial) that depend on independent agencies: regulatory capture and judicial capture are the same underlying structural failure.

**Confidence: 90%** — case status confirmed via SCOTUSblog, Lawfare Media analysis confirmed.

### 2.5 Emergency Powers and the Shadow Docket's Constitutional Status

Shadow docket reform raises a distinct constitutional question: can Congress limit the Supreme Court's authority to issue emergency stays? The Court has used its Article III appellate jurisdiction to issue these stays under the All Writs Act and federal procedural statutes. Congressional limitation would not eliminate the power but regulate its exercise — requiring written explanations, supermajority votes for emergency stays affecting major policies, or public disclosure requirements.

The constitutional authority for Congress to regulate court procedures is well-established. Congress regulates the Court's appellate jurisdiction (a fact explicitly confirmed in Ex Parte McCardle), the size of the Court, its procedure under the Rules Enabling Act, and the structure of the lower courts. Requiring written explanations for emergency stays — the SHADOW Act's core demand — is a procedural regulation with strong statutory authority and no plausible constitutional objection, since it does not limit what the Court can decide, only how it must explain decisions.

**Confidence: 90%** — constitutional analysis supported by Brennan Center scholarship, CRS analysis.

## Zone 3: International Precedent

### 3.1 Germany: The Gold Standard

Germany's Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht), established by the Basic Law in 1949, is the most studied model for democratic constitutional review. The court's structure addresses every structural failure identified in the US context:

**Fixed 12-year terms**: Justices serve a single non-renewable 12-year term, with mandatory retirement at 68, whichever comes first. The single non-renewable structure eliminates the strategic retirement timing that US justices use to ensure their replacement by ideologically aligned presidents. The non-renewable design also means justices cannot be rewarded with reappointment for ideologically favorable decisions.

**Bipartisan confirmation**: Justices are elected by the Bundestag and Bundesrat (the two chambers of parliament), each choosing 8 of the court's 16 judges. Election requires a two-thirds supermajority in the Bundestag, compelling genuine cross-partisan consensus rather than bare majorities. This is the model that term limits advocates use when they argue that regularizing appointment timing could reduce the stakes and partisan intensity of each confirmation.

**Institutional independence record**: Despite the structured appointment process, the Federal Constitutional Court has a 75-year record of genuinely independent rulings that include striking down legislation from governing parties, including highly consequential environmental, welfare, and fiscal decisions that contradicted the preferences of the government that appointed the ruling majority. The court's rulings against the governing coalition's fiscal policy preferences in 2023-24 are the most recent example.

**Confidence: 95%** — Bundesverfassungsgericht structure confirmed via multiple sources.

### 3.2 Canada: Mandatory Retirement at 75

The Supreme Court of Canada operates under the Supreme Court Act, which mandates retirement at 75. Unlike the German model, Canadian justices have no fixed term length — they can serve from their appointment (typically in their 50s) until they turn 75, which means service lengths can vary considerably. But the mandatory retirement age eliminates lifetime tenure's most problematic feature: the strategic holding of a seat pending ideologically favorable political conditions.

The Canadian comparison matters for the US reform debate because Canadian justices have at times ruled against the preferences of the government that appointed them — including on Charter rights, Indigenous rights, and federalism questions — demonstrating that mandatory retirement age does not undermine independence.

**Confidence: 90%** — confirmed via Canadian constitutional scholarship and LawNow Magazine analysis.

### 3.3 US Outlier Status: Global Survey

No other major democracy gives lifetime seats to judges on constitutional courts. The comparative record includes:

- **Australia**: Mandatory retirement at 70
- **Brazil**: Mandatory retirement at 75
- **France**: Constitutional Council members serve single non-renewable 9-year terms
- **Ireland**: Mandatory retirement at 70
- **New Zealand**: Mandatory retirement at 70
- **South Africa**: Constitutional Court justices serve non-renewable 12-year terms (or until age 70)
- **Spain**: Constitutional Court justices serve 9-year non-renewable terms
- **United Kingdom**: UK Supreme Court justices face mandatory retirement at 70 under the Constitutional Reform Act 2005

The US and its state systems that retain lifetime tenure are the global outliers. The argument that term limits would undermine judicial independence — the core conservative objection — is empirically falsified by the experience of every other major democracy, none of which has produced evidence that fixed terms produce courts more susceptible to political manipulation.

**Confidence: 95%** — confirmed via Fix the Court, Brennan Center, German Bundesverfassungsgericht analysis.

### 3.4 The South Africa Model — Post-Authoritarian Constitutional Design

South Africa's Constitutional Court is a particularly instructive precedent because it was deliberately designed to prevent the judicial manipulation that characterized the apartheid era, where the government used the judiciary as a tool of oppression. The post-apartheid constitution's architects chose the German-inspired non-renewable 12-year term model specifically because they had experienced what happens when a government can concentrate judicial power through strategic appointment and lifetime tenure.

**Confidence: 90%** — South African constitutional design is well-documented.
