# Know Your Rights: ICE Encounters
### A Practical Community Guide — March 2026

**Share this. Print this. Memorize what you can.**

This guide is for everyone — citizens, residents, visa holders, undocumented immigrants, and witnesses. Constitutional rights apply to people, not just citizens. Under stress, you will not remember everything. That is why the Quick Reference Card is first.

---

## Quick Reference Card
*(Screenshot this. Share it. Put it on your refrigerator.)*

1. **Do NOT open your door.** ICE cannot enter without a judicial warrant signed by a judge. Ask them to slide it under the door. Look for a judge's signature — an ICE "administrative warrant" (Form I-200 or I-205) does not authorize entry.
2. **Stay silent.** Say: *"I am exercising my right to remain silent."* You do not have to answer questions about your status, birthplace, or how you entered the country. This applies to everyone.
3. **Do NOT sign anything** without speaking to an attorney first. Signing can waive your rights — including your right to a hearing.
4. **Do NOT lie.** Lying to a federal agent is a federal crime. Staying silent is not. If you cannot answer safely, say nothing.
5. **Have a plan now.** Know your A-number. Have an attorney's number memorized. Tell your family who gets the children if you are detained.

## Context: What Has Changed in 2025–2026

The immigration enforcement landscape changed dramatically in 2025. Understanding what is new matters.

**The end of "sensitive locations" protections.** For years, ICE policy prohibited enforcement at schools, churches, hospitals, and other sensitive locations. That policy was rescinded in January 2025. ICE agents can now conduct arrests at or near:
- Schools and school buses
- Churches, mosques, temples, and other houses of worship
- Hospitals and medical clinics
- Courthouses (including witnesses and victims)
- Homeless shelters and food banks

**What this means practically:** You cannot assume any location is off-limits. If you or someone you know is undocumented or has an open immigration case, the calculus of where is "safe" has changed. Plan accordingly.

**Expanded interior enforcement.** The Trump administration's 2025 executive orders eliminated the previous policy of prioritizing enforcement against people with serious criminal convictions. ICE is now conducting large-scale workplace raids, neighborhood sweeps, and checkpoint operations across the country — including in areas far from the border.

**The 100-mile border zone.** Under longstanding law (not new), CBP (Customs and Border Protection, a separate agency from ICE) has expanded authority within 100 miles of any U.S. border — including coastlines. Roughly two-thirds of the U.S. population lives in this zone. CBP agents can operate immigration checkpoints on public roads within this zone without a warrant.

**Wrongful detentions are increasing.** U.S. citizens, green card holders, and legal visa holders have been detained in this enforcement environment. Do not assume your status protects you from an encounter — know how to assert your rights regardless.

## 1. ICE at Your Door

You are home. There is a knock. Someone says they are from ICE or immigration.

> **Your home has the strongest legal protections of any space. The Fourth Amendment prohibits government agents from entering without your consent or a valid judicial warrant. Do not open the door.**

### Do You Have to Open the Door?

**No.** You have no legal obligation to open your door to ICE agents unless they have a judicial warrant — a warrant signed by a federal judge or magistrate judge. ICE officers do not have the authority to sign a judicial warrant themselves.

There are two very different documents ICE may have:

| Document | Who Signs It | Does It Allow Entry? |
|---|---|---|
| **Judicial warrant** (e.g., arrest warrant signed by a judge) | A federal judge or magistrate | **Yes** — they can enter |
| **Administrative warrant** (Form I-200: Warrant of Arrest; or Form I-205: Warrant of Removal/Deportation) | An ICE officer or supervisor | **No** — this does NOT authorize entry into your home |

ICE agents know this distinction. They may present an administrative warrant hoping you do not know the difference, or hoping you will open the door out of fear or confusion.

### How to Verify a Warrant

Do not open the door to look at it.

**Ask them to slide it under the door.** This is a reasonable and legal request. If they will not slide it, that itself tells you something.

Once you have it, look for:
- **The heading.** A judicial warrant will say something like "United States District Court." An administrative warrant will reference "Department of Homeland Security" or "U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement."
- **The signature line.** A judicial warrant is signed by a judge or magistrate. Look for a court seal and a judge's name/title. An ICE form I-200 or I-205 is signed by an ICE officer — not a judge.
- **Your name and address.** Even a judicial warrant must name the specific person and, for a search warrant, the specific location.

> **A warrant that does not have a judge's signature, or that lists a different name or address, does not authorize entry into your home.**

### What to Say Through the Door

You can speak through the door. You are not required to open it. Here is language you can use:

*"I am not going to open the door. Do you have a judicial warrant signed by a judge? If so, please slide it under the door."*

If they claim to have a warrant: *"Please slide the warrant under the door so I can review it."*

If they do not have a judicial warrant: *"I do not consent to your entry. I am exercising my Fourth Amendment rights."*

You may also simply say nothing beyond: *"I am not opening the door and I am not answering questions."*

### What NOT to Say

- **Do not say "I consent" or "come in"** — even out of politeness or panic.
- **Do not lie** about who is in the home or claim no one is there when there is.
- **Do not open the door "just a crack"** — in some jurisdictions, opening the door can be interpreted as consent to entry.
- **Do not say anything about immigration status**, yours or anyone else's.

### If They Force Entry Anyway

If ICE forces entry without a judicial warrant:

1. **Do not physically resist.** Do not push back, run, or fight. Physical resistance can result in additional criminal charges regardless of the legality of the entry.
2. **Clearly and loudly state:** *"I do not consent to this entry. I do not consent to any search."*
3. **Stay calm. Do not answer questions.** Say: *"I am exercising my right to remain silent. I want to speak to an attorney."*
4. **Observe and remember.** Try to note the officers' badge numbers, names, and the agency they identify with.
5. **Document everything after.** Write down everything you remember as soon as possible — times, what was said, what was done. This will matter for any legal challenge.

An unlawful entry can be challenged in court. Evidence of that entry — and your clear refusal to consent — is critical.

## 2. Stopped on the Street or in Public

ICE agents and police can approach anyone in public. Being approached is not the same as being detained.

### The Right to Remain Silent

The Fifth Amendment right against self-incrimination applies to **everyone in the United States** — citizen, resident, visa holder, or undocumented. You are not required to answer questions about:
- Your name (in most states — see below)
- Your birthplace or country of origin
- Your immigration status
- How or when you entered the country
- Where you live

**Say:** *"I am exercising my right to remain silent."*

Repeat this as needed. You do not have to explain it or justify it.

### Do You Have to Show ID?

This depends on the state and the circumstances.

**"Stop and identify" states:** About 24 states have laws requiring you to identify yourself (typically just give your name — not a document) if police have reasonable suspicion that you are involved in a crime. These are not immigration laws — they apply to everyone. The states include: Alabama, Arizona, Arkansas, Colorado, Delaware, Florida, Georgia, Illinois, Indiana, Kansas, Louisiana, Missouri, Montana, Nebraska, Nevada, New Hampshire, New Mexico, New York, North Dakota, Ohio, Utah, Vermont, Wisconsin, and others. **Check your state.**

**Non-stop-and-identify states:** In states without such a law (like California), you generally do not have to provide your name or ID when stopped.

**What you do NOT have to do anywhere:** You do not have to produce immigration documents, a passport, or any document proving immigration status in a street encounter unless you are at an official port of entry or CBP checkpoint.

> **Important distinction:** ICE agents are not local police. They do not have the same street stop authority as local officers in most cases. If someone who is not local police stops you on the street claiming to be immigration, you can ask: "Are you a police officer? Am I being detained?"

### What to Say

*"Am I free to go?"*

If they say yes: walk away calmly. Do not run.

If they say no, or if they do not answer clearly: *"I am exercising my right to remain silent. I do not consent to any search."*

Then: stay calm, do not run, do not argue, do not physically resist. If they detain you, ask: *"Why am I being detained?"* and then: *"I want to speak to an attorney."*

### Do NOT Run

Running from agents — even if you have every right to leave — can be used as justification for further action, and can escalate a non-arrest situation into something dangerous. If you are free to go, walk. If you are not free to go, stay and assert your rights verbally.

## 3. Workplace Raid or Checkpoint

### Workplace Raids

ICE conducts Form I-9 audits and physical raids at workplaces. The rules are different here than at your home.

**ICE can enter public areas of a business** (the lobby, sales floor, etc.) without a warrant. They cannot enter private areas — offices, back rooms, storage — without either consent or a judicial search warrant.

**If ICE enters your workplace:**

1. **You do not have to answer questions** about your immigration status. The right to remain silent applies at work.
2. **Do not run.** Do not try to flee through a back exit.
3. **Say:** *"I am exercising my right to remain silent. I want to speak to an attorney."*
4. **Do not show documents** without speaking to an attorney first, if possible.
5. **Tell other workers:** You can quietly tell coworkers around you that they have the right to remain silent.

**What employers can and cannot do:**

- Employers **cannot** instruct you to cooperate with ICE or answer questions beyond what is legally required.
- Employers **cannot** fire you for asserting your legal rights in an ICE encounter.
- Employers **can** be required to produce I-9 forms and employment records under a warrant or subpoena — but that is the employer's legal obligation, not yours.
- A good employer will post legal services information and have an immigration attorney on call. If yours does not, that is information worth having before a raid.

### CBP Checkpoints (100-Mile Border Zone)

Within 100 miles of any U.S. border or coastline, CBP (not ICE) operates immigration checkpoints on public roads. These are different from ICE street stops.

At a CBP checkpoint:
- You **must** stop.
- You **may** be asked about citizenship and you can answer that you are a U.S. citizen if you are.
- You are **not required** to show documents beyond what is required at a port of entry if you are on a domestic road checkpoint (though refusing can lead to secondary screening and delay).
- You **can** decline to answer questions about citizenship and invoke your right to remain silent — but expect to be referred to secondary inspection.
- CBP **cannot** conduct a full search of your vehicle without either your consent, probable cause, or a warrant.

The practical reality: asserting rights at a checkpoint works legally but may not prevent short-term detention. Know your rights and know that exercise of those rights may slow you down.

## 4. If You Are Detained

Being told "you are under arrest" or "you are being detained and cannot leave" is a detention. From this moment, everything matters.

### Your Rights When Detained

> **You have the right to remain silent. Use it.**

- **Do not answer questions** about your immigration status, how you entered the country, where you have lived, or anything else related to immigration. Say: *"I am exercising my right to remain silent."*
- **Do not answer questions** about other people — family members, housemates, coworkers, friends.
- **You have the right to an attorney.** The government is not required to provide you with an immigration attorney (this is different from criminal cases where a public defender is provided). But you have the right to hire one and to speak with one.
- **Ask for an attorney immediately.** Say: *"I want to speak to an attorney before answering any questions."* After you say this, agents are supposed to stop questioning you.

### Do NOT Sign Anything

> **Do not sign any document without speaking to an attorney.**

ICE agents may present you with forms to sign. These can include:
- A "voluntary departure" form — signing this may mean agreeing to leave the country and giving up your right to a hearing before an immigration judge.
- A waiver of rights form.
- Forms in English that you may not fully understand.

Signing away rights is fast and hard to undo. Not signing means you keep your options open.

**Say:** *"I am not signing anything until I speak to an attorney."*

### Do NOT Lie

Lying to a federal agent is a federal crime under 18 U.S.C. § 1001. This applies even if the underlying question was improper. The only safe option when you cannot or should not answer is **silence**.

Staying silent is not lying. It is a constitutional right.

### What to Ask For

1. **An attorney.** Repeat this request clearly.
2. **A phone call.** You have the right to make a phone call. Ask to call your attorney or a family member.
3. **Your consulate.** If you are not a U.S. citizen, you have the right under the **Vienna Convention on Consular Relations** to contact your country's consulate. Say: *"I am requesting to speak with my consulate."* ICE is required to notify the consulate of your detention if you request it.

### Your A-Number (Alien Registration Number)

If you have ever had any contact with the immigration system — received any immigration document, applied for a visa, had a case before an immigration judge — you likely have an A-number (also called an alien registration number). It is an 8 or 9-digit number that begins with "A."

**Where to find it:**
- On a green card (front and back)
- On an Employment Authorization Document (EAD)
- On a Notice to Appear (NTA) or other court documents
- On any USCIS receipt notice

**Why it matters:** Your A-number is how the immigration system tracks your case. It is essential for locating you in the detention system, for your attorney to pull your records, and for your family to find you. Know it. Write it somewhere a trusted person can access.

## 5. How to Find Someone Who Has Been Detained

If someone you know has been taken by ICE and you do not know where they are:

### ICE Online Detainee Locator

**URL:** [locator.ice.gov/odls/](https://locator.ice.gov/odls/)

**What you need (one of two combinations):**
- Full legal name + A-number, OR
- Full legal name + date of birth + country of birth

**Important limitations:**
- It typically takes **24–48 hours or more** for a person to appear in the system after detention. If you cannot find someone, try again the next day.
- **Children under 18 do not appear in the system.** If a minor has been detained, do not rely on the online locator — call directly.
- The system only reflects where ICE has recorded the person. Early in a detention, they may be in a local jail or holding facility not yet entered into ICE's system.

### If They Are Not in the System

1. **Call the ICE ERO (Enforcement and Removal Operations) field office** for the area where the person was detained. ICE has field offices in every major region. You can find the list at ice.gov/contact/ero.
2. **Call local jails** in the area. ICE often holds people in local jails under contract. Call the county jail or city detention facility in the area where the arrest occurred.
3. **Contact an immigration attorney immediately.** An attorney may have contacts and access that can speed up locating someone.
4. **Use the NILC guide:** The National Immigration Law Center has a detailed resource — "How to Locate a Detained Person After an ICE Disappearance" — available at:
   [https://media.nilc.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/How-to-Locate-a-ICE-Disappearance.pdf](https://media.nilc.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/How-to-Locate-a-ICE-Disappearance.pdf)

### After You Find Them

- **Get an attorney working on the case as fast as possible.** The immigration court system moves quickly in some cases, and the first hearing can happen before an attorney is retained if you wait.
- **Bring the A-number** to any attorney consultation — it is the single most important piece of information.
- **Ask about bond.** An immigration judge may set bond, which means the person can be released while their case is pending. An attorney can request a bond hearing.

## 7. What to Prepare NOW — Before Any Encounter

The time to prepare is before anything happens. These steps take a few hours and can make an enormous difference.

### Know Your A-Number
If you or a family member has one, find it and write it down somewhere accessible to trusted people but not to strangers. Store it digitally in an encrypted location and physically with a trusted person outside your home.

### Have an Attorney's Number Ready
Put an immigration attorney's number in your phone **and** write it somewhere you can access if your phone is taken. Memorize it if possible. Give it to family members.

### Designate Childcare in Advance
If you have children, designate in writing who will care for them if you are detained. This should be:
- A trusted adult who is a U.S. citizen or legal resident
- Someone your children know
- Someone with legal authorization (a notarized power of attorney for childcare) to make decisions for your children

Without this documentation, your children may be at risk of entering the child welfare system if you are detained. An immigration attorney or legal aid organization can help you prepare this document.

### Store Important Documents Safely
Keep copies of these documents somewhere a trusted person can access — not only at your home:
- Birth certificates (yours and your children's)
- Passports
- Green card or EAD
- Any immigration court notices (especially the A-number)
- Marriage certificates
- School records for children
- Lease or property documents (can establish residence)

**Do not keep your only copies at home.** Scan them and store them with a trusted family member, friend, or attorney.

### Make a Family Plan
Sit down with your family and go through:
- What to do if someone is detained (who to call first)
- Where the emergency contacts list is
- Who has the children
- What not to say to anyone at the door
- Where the documents are

Practice saying: *"I am exercising my right to remain silent. I want to speak to an attorney."*

### Know Your Local Rapid Response Network
Many cities and counties have immigrant community rapid response networks — volunteer networks that can provide legal observers, emergency legal support, and community accompaniment during enforcement operations. Search "[your city] immigrant rapid response network" or ask a local immigrant rights organization.

## 8. For U.S. Citizens and Legal Residents

> **You can be wrongly detained. It is happening.**

U.S. citizens and lawful permanent residents have been detained by ICE in the current enforcement environment — some for hours, some for days. In some cases, people were detained because they could not produce documentation on the spot, or because they were in proximity to someone being arrested.

**Your rights are the same.** You have the right to remain silent. You have the right to an attorney. You do not have to consent to searches.

### How to Assert Citizenship

If you are a U.S. citizen and are stopped or detained:
- **State clearly:** *"I am a United States citizen."*
- **Do not answer other questions** about your background or how you acquired citizenship.
- **Carry documentation if possible:** A U.S. passport is the strongest evidence of citizenship. A U.S. birth certificate or naturalization certificate is also strong. A driver's license alone is not proof of citizenship.
- **If detained despite asserting citizenship:** Continue to assert citizenship, ask for an attorney, and ask your attorney to request immediate release.

### If You Are a Lawful Permanent Resident (Green Card Holder)

You are required by law to carry your green card. If stopped, you can show it. You still have the right to remain silent about other matters and the right to an attorney.

Note: certain conduct can trigger removal proceedings even for green card holders. Do not discuss your history or any prior legal issues without an attorney present.

### If You Witness Someone Else Being Detained

You can be a legal witness without interfering.

- **Do not physically intervene** — this can result in obstruction charges.
- **Observe from a safe distance.** Note badge numbers, agency insignia, vehicle numbers.
- **Record video** if it is safe to do so. In public spaces, you have the right to record police and federal agents performing their duties. Do not interfere physically with the arrest while recording.
- **Do not delete footage.** Do not hand your phone to agents.
- **Get the detained person's name and A-number if possible** so they can be located.
- **Call a legal rapid response network** if one exists in your area.
- **File a report** with the ACLU or a local immigrant rights organization after the fact.

## 9. What ICE Cannot Do

Understanding the limits of ICE authority matters — not because these limits are always respected, but because violations are legally challengeable and documenting them matters.

**ICE cannot:**

- **Enter your home without consent or a judicial warrant.** An administrative warrant (Form I-200 or I-205) does not authorize entry. This is settled Fourth Amendment law.
- **Arrest you based solely on race, ethnicity, national origin, or language.** Using these factors as the primary basis for an enforcement action violates the Fourth Amendment and Equal Protection. Document if you believe this occurred.
- **Prevent you from contacting an attorney.** Once you request an attorney, questioning must stop. Denying access to legal counsel is a rights violation.
- **Prevent a non-citizen from contacting their consulate.** Under the Vienna Convention, you have the right to consular notification. Agents are required to inform you of this right.
- **Detain you indefinitely without a hearing.** Immigration detainees have the right to appear before an immigration judge and to request bond.
- **Coerce a signature** on documents waiving your rights. Signing under duress or without understanding the document can be challenged, but challenging it is difficult after the fact.
- **Use excessive force** in the course of an arrest. ICE detention standards (Performance-Based National Detention Standards, PBNDS) establish limits on use of force and detainee treatment.

> **Document violations.** Write down what happened, when, who was involved, what was said. This documentation is the foundation of any legal challenge.

## Final Word

Rights only protect you if you know them and assert them. The law has not changed as dramatically as the enforcement environment has — your constitutional protections still exist. What has changed is the scale and aggression of enforcement, the elimination of sensitive location protections, and the political climate around immigration.

The most effective thing you can do right now is prepare. Make the plan. Find the attorney. Know the number. Have the conversation with your family tonight.

**Share this guide.** Print it. Translate it. The community that is informed is harder to isolate.
