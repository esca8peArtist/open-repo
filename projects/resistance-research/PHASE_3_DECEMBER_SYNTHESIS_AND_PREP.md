## Synthesis Decision Tree — How to Handle Zone Integration Conflicts

Apply this decision tree when integrating Zone K-1, K-2, and K-3. Do not proceed past a conflict without a resolution decision. Email coordinator if the decision requires a judgment call beyond the parameters below.

### Decision Tree: Zone Conflict Resolution

**Conflict Type 1: Zone K-1 legislative item contradicts Zone K-2 constitutional argument**

Example: Zone K-1 finds that a bill (e.g., Manchin-Welch amendment) proceeds via the amendment track, while Zone K-2 argues the statutory track is constitutionally sufficient and politically preferred.

Resolution:
- Do not delete or suppress the Zone K-1 item
- In Zone K-2, add a sentence explicitly addressing the divergence: "The Manchin-Welch amendment reflects one legislative strategy — pursuing the constitutional amendment track — while H.R. 1074 and the SCERT Act represent the statutory alternative. Both tracks are live in the current legislative environment; this analysis focuses on the statutory pathway as the more politically achievable near-term option."
- If the researcher cannot determine which framing is correct without additional source research, flag to coordinator

**Conflict Type 2: Zone K-3 international precedent undermines Zone K-2 statutory feasibility argument**

Example: All three primary international comparators (Germany, Canada, Australia) implemented judicial tenure limits via constitutional amendment, not statute, which weakens the "statutory pathway is precedented" argument.

Resolution:
- Do not suppress this finding
- Acknowledge the tension explicitly in Zone K-3 synthesis: "Unlike the US SCERT Act's proposed statutory pathway, Germany, Canada, and Australia implemented term limits through constitutional mechanisms. This precedent does not invalidate the statutory approach argued in Zone K-2 — the US Article III 'good Behaviour' clause may be read to permit statutory retirement age requirements under a different theory than constitutional amendment — but it illustrates that the international models do not provide direct precedent for a statutory-only approach."
- If this tension is significant enough to change the Zone K-2 constitutional argument, email coordinator before finalizing

**Conflict Type 3: Zone K-2 and K-3 cross-reference a Domain H source (creating Domain K/H boundary confusion)**

Example: Both Zone K-3's international comparison and Domain H's Extension A analyze Germany's Basic Law — Zone K-3 covers the Bundesverfassungsgericht judicial tenure structure; Domain H Extension A covers the Article 21 eternity clause and constructive vote of no confidence.

Resolution (per PHASE_3_DOMAIN_K_H_CONTRACTOR_WORKFLOW.md Phase 2 deconfliction boundary):
- Domain K covers judicial selection and tenure mechanisms (Zone K-3)
- Domain H covers constitutional design and amendment process models (Extension A)
- Where the Germany section in Zone K-3 touches constitutional design, note: "For the constitutional design dimensions of Germany's Basic Law, see companion document Domain H, Extension A."
- Where Domain H Extension A touches judicial tenure, note: "For the judicial tenure details of the Bundesverfassungsgericht, see companion document Domain K, Zone K-3."

**Conflict Type 4: Zone K-1 bill has been amended or withdrawn by December 2026**

Resolution:
- Update Zone K-1 with current status as of December date of discovery
- If amendment changes the Zone K-2 constitutional argument, revise Zone K-2 accordingly (flag revision to coordinator)
- If bill withdrawn: note in Zone K-1; adjust Zone K-4 coalition activation to remove that bill from the primary legislative vehicle list; add a sentence on what the withdrawal means for the reform strategy
