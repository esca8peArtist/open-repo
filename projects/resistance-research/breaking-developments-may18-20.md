## Domain 1: Congressional Votes (Election Security, Voting Rights, Judicial Nominees)

### SAVE Act: Senate Floor Vote — Failed as Expected

The SAVE America Act (H.R. 22), which passed the House 218-213 on February 11, 2026, requiring documentary proof of citizenship to register and a photo ID to vote, failed to advance in the Senate during the May 18-20 window when Senate Majority Leader Thune declined to bring a cloture vote that would face a Democratic filibuster. The bill has insufficient votes to reach 60. Trump has publicly floated an executive order route on voter ID as a contingency if the Senate cannot act — a significant escalation marker given the concurrent March 31 voter-list executive order already in litigation.

**Phase 2 implication**: Domain 37 should document the SAVE Act Senate failure as evidence that the executive order path is Trump's fallback mechanism for voter suppression, not a contingency. The March 31 EO is the operational instrument; the SAVE Act was the legislative camouflage. Update Domain 37 Section IV.

Domain 37b Section 6 (federal withdrawal) needs a sub-section on the Mullin letter and the NSA/Cyber Command testimony. Flag for Domain E (election administration seizure) update as well.

## Domain 2: Court Decisions (Federal District and Appellate)

### SCOTUS: Callais Immediate-Effect Order Becomes Final

On approximately May 4-5, SCOTUS granted Louisiana's request to immediately finalize the Callais judgment — waiving the court's own procedural rule requiring a 25-day waiting period. On approximately May 7, SCOTUS denied the Black voters' request to reverse the immediate-effect order. Both orders were issued as brief, unsigned orders without explanation from the six-justice conservative majority. The Callais judgment is now operationally final for election administration purposes.

More consequentially for the May 18-20 window: SCOTUS has sent the North Dakota legislative redistricting case and a Mississippi state legislative redistricting case back to lower courts for reconsideration under the new Callais intent standard. This is the first appellate cascade — lower courts are now being instructed to apply the intent test to pending cases, not merely to wait for new litigation.

**Phase 2 implication**: Domain 1 requires updating to document the remand cascade as a distinct analytical track from the state redistricting sessions. The remand to lower courts on intent-standard grounds is likely to generate a wave of favorable rulings for Republican mapmakers over the next 60-90 days. Add to litigation tracker as new Category 11 (VRA Section 2 Remand Cascade).

### Mail Ballot EO: Judge Nichols Holds, No Ruling Yet

U.S. District Judge Carl Nichols (Trump appointee) held a two-and-a-half-hour hearing on May 14 on the consolidated challenges to Trump's March 31 voter-list/mail ballot executive order — brought by the DNC, NAACP, and LULAC — and has not yet ruled as of May 19. Nichols appeared reluctant to grant a preliminary injunction, suggesting there may be no concrete harm until the lists are actually transmitted to states, and that "merely compiling the lists" might not constitute actionable harm. The absence of a ruling and the skeptical framing represent a significant risk signal: if Nichols denies the injunction, the EO's implementation timeline accelerates materially. An appeal to the D.C. Circuit would be the next step.

A separate case before Judge Kollar-Kotelly has already permanently blocked two narrower provisions of a prior election executive order — but the March 31 EO is broader.

**Phase 2 implication**: Domain 37 Section V (mail ballot suppression track) needs updating with Nichols hearing outcome. The "no harm until transmission" logic, if adopted, would allow DHS/SSA list compilation to proceed through the summer. This is a Phase 2 research priority: the transmission trigger date and what it means for the November 2026 election calendar. Flag for CHECKIN urgent monitoring.

### 9th Circuit: Oregon Voter Roll Oral Argument (May 19)

The first circuit-level argument in DOJ's litigation demanding Oregon's unredacted voter rolls was heard May 19 in Portland. The DOJ has sued 29 states plus Washington, D.C. A district court dismissed the original case in January 2026; the 9th Circuit panel's questions will provide the first appellate signal on whether the DOJ's NVRA theory survives review. No ruling from the bench is expected — the 9th Circuit rarely rules from argument. Panel composition and judicial questioning are the strategic signals to watch.

**Phase 2 implication**: Domain 37 needs a dedicated voter-roll litigation sub-section. The Oregon case is the lead case; if the 9th Circuit affirms dismissal, it creates favorable precedent for the 28 other states resisting DOJ data demands. If the 9th Circuit reverses, it accelerates a constitutional confrontation over state voter data sovereignty.

## Domain 3: State Legislative Actions (Redistricting, Ballot Measures)

### Alabama: Split Primary Proceeds; Special Election Mechanism Passed

Alabama's May 19 primary proceeded as scheduled using the old congressional map (one majority-Black district) — but Gov. Kay Ivey has already signed legislation creating a separate special primary election mechanism for August 11 in districts 1, 2, 6, and 7 once courts clear the way for the new map. The Supreme Court has already vacated the lower court order requiring two majority-Black districts; the new map with one such district is being staged for implementation. The practical effect: Alabama is running parallel election tracks simultaneously — one under the old map, one being constructed for the new map. There will be no runoff in the special primary (legislatively required).

**Phase 2 implication**: Domain 1 and Domain 37 need the split-track electoral calendar documented. The August 11 special primary is a second suppression choke point for Black voters in districts that may be reconfigured between May and November. Cross-reference to Domain 37b on voter confusion as a suppression mechanism.

### South Carolina: Senate Blocks Gerrymander — Stall, Not Defeat

The South Carolina Senate rejected, 29-17, a resolution to extend the legislative session for redistricting on approximately May 13 — two votes short of the two-thirds needed, with five Republicans joining Democrats. However, Gov. McMaster subsequently called a special session focused on redistricting that was scheduled to begin in the House on May 18. As of this window, the South Carolina House launched full debate on redistricting plans that would eliminate Rep. James Clyburn's District 6, the state's only majority-Black congressional seat. The Senate rejection was a speed bump, not a final barrier — the special session is now the vehicle.

**Phase 2 implication**: South Carolina is not a closed case. The special session is live. Domain 1 Section 8 (Callais cascade) requires updating: South Carolina's status is "special session underway" rather than "blocked." The five Republican senators who defected represent a genuine anti-gerrymander coalition that should be tracked — they are potential litigation witnesses and political allies.

**Sources**: [NBC News: South Carolina state Senate rejects effort to tackle redistricting](https://www.nbcnews.com/politics/2026-election/south-carolina-state-senate-rejects-effort-tackle-redistricting-pressu-rcna344815); [Democracy Docket: South Carolina redistricting — Lawmakers reject gerrymander in blow to Trump](https://www.democracydocket.com/news-alerts/south-carolina-redistricting-lawmakers-reject-gerrymander-in-blow-to-trump/); [Washington Times: Redistricting debate shifts to South Carolina](https://www.washingtontimes.com/news/2026/may/18/redistricting-debate-shifts-south-carolina-republicans-aim-clean/)

### Missouri: State Supreme Court Upholds Gerrymander (May 12 — Immediate Aftermath Active)

The Missouri Supreme Court unanimously upheld the Republican-redrawn congressional map on May 12, allowing it to proceed to the August primary despite more than 300,000 signatures submitted for a referendum petition. The court ruled that referendum petition submission does not automatically stay the map's implementation, and separately rejected compactness challenges. The map splits Rep. Emanuel Cleaver's Kansas City-based district into three Republican-leaning seats. The referendum vote will still occur — but under the new map, not the old one.

**Phase 2 implication**: This is a model other states may replicate: pass a gerrymander, let a referendum challenge proceed, but keep the map in effect for the immediate election cycle. The separation of "referendum pending" from "implementation stayed" is the legal mechanism. Domain 33 should document this as a state-level direct democracy override technique. Active in the May 18-20 window as its post-decision aftermath continues.

### Georgia: Special Session Announced for June (Kemp)

Gov. Kemp, who initially declined to order redistricting, reversed course and announced a June special session to redraw Georgia's congressional map ahead of the 2028 cycle. This came after the May 19 primary cycle, and critics immediately noted the process could dilute Black voting power. Georgia's timing differs from other states: Kemp is using the post-Callais environment for a 2028-cycle consolidation, not a 2026 emergency session. This makes it strategically distinct but analytically relevant to Phase 2.

**Mississippi: Special Session Canceled (May 13)**

Gov. Reeves canceled the Mississippi special session originally scheduled for approximately May 20. The reason: the Fifth Circuit vacated the district court order that had found Mississippi's state Supreme Court districts violated the VRA — eliminating the legal trigger that prompted the session. Mississippi is now positioned to redistrict on its preferred timeline (before 2027 statewide elections) without federal court pressure.
