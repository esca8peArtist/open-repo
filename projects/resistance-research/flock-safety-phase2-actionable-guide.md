## Executive Summary

Flock Safety has built the largest private license plate surveillance network in U.S. history — 100,000+ cameras, 7,000+ law enforcement networks, billions of plate reads per month — and has been documented facilitating unauthorized ICE access, protest surveillance, abortion-seeker tracking, and repeated misrepresentation to municipal governments. The network is designed so that when any of 4,800+ participating agencies searches a plate, they query not just local cameras but the entire national database. Every camera connected to Flock is connected to every other camera.

The critical resistance finding: **this is winnable at the local level**. More than 80 localities canceled or deactivated Flock contracts between 2021 and July 2026, with 39 of those cancellations occurring in the first five months of 2026 alone. The trigger was the Trump administration's immigration enforcement intensification, which caused sanctuary cities to examine how a stolen-car camera network had become an ICE location infrastructure. Houston has not yet acted.

Houston has approximately 3,000 Flock cameras across greater Houston, a $6.39 million city contract covering HPD, and documented school district cameras that were searched 733,000 times in a single month — including 620 immigration enforcement searches — at Alvin ISD alone. The Harris County contract was renewed May 28, 2026 (4-0 vote; Judge Hidalgo abstained) after tense public debate. The HPD city contract, structured as one year with four options, is approaching its final renewal window. The next decision point is now.

**Lead action for Houston residents**: The HPD contract option exercise likely falls in mid-2026 for FY2027. File a public records request immediately (template in `flock-removal-advocacy-toolkit.md`) to confirm the exact date. Engage Districts B, H, and I council members this month.

## Part 1: Legal Landscape

### Federal Constitutional Framework

The controlling federal case is *Schmidt v. Norfolk* (E.D. Va., January 27, 2026), which granted summary judgment to the City of Norfolk, holding that 176 cameras scanning plaintiffs' vehicles 475 and 325 times over four months did not constitute a Fourth Amendment search. The court held the cameras did not capture "enough information to catalogue citizens' movements in their entirety" — and then explicitly left open a density-dependent constitutional threshold, warning that ALPR surveillance "could become too intrusive" as cameras proliferate.

That case is now before the Fourth Circuit (Case No. 26-1227). Amicus briefs were filed April 20, 2026 by the ACLU, ACLU of Virginia, and EFF, arguing that the "mosaic theory" from *Carpenter v. United States* (2018) applies: just as the government cannot access seven days of cell-site location data without a warrant, it cannot aggregate months of automated plate reads into a comprehensive movement profile without Fourth Amendment constraint. EPIC filed separately. The Cato Institute filed on behalf of the plaintiffs. A Fourth Circuit ruling establishing that dense ALPR networks are Fourth Amendment searches would bind Virginia, Maryland, North Carolina, South Carolina, and West Virginia — and would provide the strongest persuasive precedent in every other circuit.

Until that ruling, the federal constitutional floor is bare. Local statutory and policy action is the operative terrain.

### State-by-State Stratification

The regulatory map has three tiers as of July 2026.

**Strong restriction states** (functional warrant requirements or near-prohibitions): Maine (only state prohibiting private ALPR use; warrant requirements for law enforcement), New Hampshire (three-minute deletion mandate; functional ban on law enforcement ALPR), Washington (SB 6002, effective March 30, 2026 — prohibitions on immigration enforcement, reproductive healthcare tracking, use at places of worship; 21-day retention cap; federal sharing prohibited absent judicial authorization), Oregon (SB 1516, effective March 31, 2026 — 30-day cap; inter-agency sharing logs; limited sharing purposes).

**Partial restriction states**: California (SB 34 retention cap; strong local ordinances in Oakland and San Francisco; pending legislation would bar out-of-state/federal sharing without warrant), Vermont (private ALPR limits; transparency requirements), Illinois (state audit authority demonstrated; no comprehensive statute).

**No protection states (high risk)**: Texas, Virginia, Georgia, Florida, and most of the South and Mountain West. In Texas specifically: DPS signed a $26 million Flock contract in 2024; all county sheriffs are mandated into 287(g) ICE agreements (effective December 1, 2025); no pending legislation gained traction as of mid-2026. The state is an active Flock customer, not a regulator.

**The private deployment gap** cuts across all states: HOA cameras, school district cameras, and business cameras feed into the same national network once "National Lookup" is enabled. No state comprehensively regulates this. In Texas, Alvin ISD's 8 campus cameras — purchased with a $50,000 school safety budget — became a node in a network searched by agencies in Florida, Georgia, Indiana, and Tennessee for immigration enforcement. The school district did not authorize this. The Flock interface had built-in dropdown options: "Immigration (civil/administrative)," "I.C.E.," "ERO proactive crim case research."

### The February 2026 Contract Terms Changes

In February 2026, Flock rewrote its standard municipal contract in four ways that matter for advocacy:

1. Removed the sentence "Flock does not own and shall not sell Customer Data" — no contractual guarantee against data sale
2. Granted Flock "exclusive right to determine and control the method, timing, format, and medium" of data access — municipalities receive degraded copies, not raw data
3. Created perpetual, non-expiring licensing rights to surveillance data, surviving contract termination
4. Moved disputes to mandatory private arbitration under Georgia law, eliminating municipal access to state courts

Contracts signed before February 2026 under prior terms may contain stronger protections — and the removal of the data sale prohibition may constitute a material change sufficient to support termination for cause on renewals.

## Part 2: Removal and Resistance Strategies

### What Has Worked: The 80-City Model

The pattern across successful cancellation campaigns is consistent. Understanding what actually drove outcomes is essential.

**Records first, always.** Every successful campaign — Mountain View, Dayton, Evanston, Syracuse — obtained audit logs through public records requests before any public organizing. The logs revealed what city governments had not disclosed: which outside agencies were searching local cameras, how many searches lacked any legitimate purpose, and in some cases, whether federal immigration agencies had been given access that city policy explicitly prohibited. In Houston's case, HPD officers entered "ASDF," "donut," and blank fields as search justifications — a violation of HPD's own stated policy. This is your material. You cannot build the case without the records.

**The audit trigger.** In Dayton, the audit found 7,100 immigration searches in violation of city policy. In Mountain View, 600,000 unauthorized searches by 250 agencies over 12 months. In Syracuse, 4.4 million searches including ICE sharing despite prior assurances to residents. The audit is both the advocacy tool and the political protection for council members who want to act — they are responding to documented evidence, not opposing "public safety."

**Council majority math.** Flock contracts require council approval at renewal. The city council is the primary target in every jurisdiction. The math varies but is achievable: Ithaca voted unanimously; Cleveland's Safety Committee voted 3-1; El Cerrito voted 3-2; Lockhart voted 6-1. The target is a majority willing to either vote against renewal or direct an independent audit and policy restrictions.

**Coalition breadth.** No successful campaign was won by a single-issue civil liberties organization alone. The Austin cancellation involved 30+ community groups: civil liberties advocates, immigrant rights organizations, neighborhood associations, reproductive rights groups, faith communities, and privacy-focused tech workers. Political diversity matters: Flock opposition has been effective from progressive sanctuary cities to libertarian-leaning conservative communities uncomfortable with surveillance infrastructure.

**Media partnership.** Local investigative journalism has been the accelerant in several cases. The Texas Standard's investigation of HPD's "donut" searches; The 74's investigation of Alvin ISD's school cameras; the Mountain View Voice's audit analysis; NPR and LAist's unauthorized access reporting — each drove political action. If you have documented evidence of misuse, a reporter covering this beat is a strategic partner.

### Legal Pathways

**Contract termination review.** The February 2026 terms changes — removal of the data sale prohibition, perpetual licensing retention, mandatory arbitration — may constitute material contract changes affecting renewal terms. The Evanston incident (Flock reinstalled cameras after contract cancellation) demonstrates that Flock's actual performance may give cities termination-for-cause grounds under breach of contract analysis. Engage a municipal attorney to review exit clause triggers.

**State public records law (Texas).** Under Chapter 552 of the Texas Government Code, Flock search logs, contracts, and data-sharing agreements are public records. A records request filed today has a 10-business-day response deadline for routine business records. The specific categories to request are detailed in the `flock-removal-advocacy-toolkit.md` FOIA template.

**Fourth Amendment litigation (watch *Schmidt v. Norfolk*).** The Fourth Circuit ruling, when it comes, will be the most significant federal development. If the court holds that sufficiently dense ALPR networks constitute searches, it will create grounds for emergency injunctive challenges to Flock deployments in Fourth Circuit states and provide the most powerful precedent for challenges elsewhere. Monitor for the ruling: ACLU (aclu.org/cases/schmidt-v-norfolk), EFF, and SCOTUSblog will report.

**State legislative pathway.** The ACLU's model state ALPR legislation (released February 2026) provides a template. Priority provisions: 21-day maximum retention (Washington model), immigration enforcement prohibition, prohibition on monitoring constitutionally protected activity, local-only sharing without judicial authorization, deletion upon contract cancellation, exclusionary rule. No Texas bill gained traction in 2026; this is a 2027 legislative session target. Contact the ACLU of Texas for legislative advocacy coordination.

### Technical Countermeasures

Technical approaches to reduce Flock's surveillance effectiveness are a secondary layer — they address individual exposure, not the systemic problem — and must be evaluated against local law.

**Legal vehicle-fingerprint disruption (Texas).** Flock cameras capture more than plate numbers: vehicle make, model, color, "fingerprint" data including bumper stickers, damage, and accessories. Legal countermeasures that disrupt fingerprint consistency without violating law:
- Rotate bumper stickers, car magnets, and window decals periodically
- Remove dealership frames and stickers
- Rotate removable accessories (roof racks, bike racks, hitch covers) between uses
- Request a replacement license plate at the county clerk (modest fee; new plate changes searchable fingerprint data)
- Switch to specialty or personalized plates

**Illegal in Texas.** Texas Transportation Code prohibits tinted plate covers, reflective or IR-blocking sprays, and any physical obstruction of license plate readability. These expose drivers to traffic stops and fines — and using illegal countermeasures undermines legitimate advocacy by providing a basis for dismissing privacy concerns as criminal intent.

**DeFlock route mapping.** DeFlock (maps.deflock.org) is a crowdsourced, open-source ALPR camera map built on OpenStreetMap, now with 336,000+ cameras mapped globally. The companion route planner generates "privacy-optimized routes" avoiding known camera locations. This is a practical operational tool for community members who need to travel privately. Users can also submit new camera locations, improving coverage. DeFlock runs without user tracking, analytics, or ads. Android app is available via Google Play (search: "DeFlock").

**Camera reporting.** Submitting new camera locations to DeFlock (deflock.org/report) is a direct civic action that improves the collective resource. Documenting Houston's 3,000+ cameras with location, operator, and facing direction makes the surveillance infrastructure visible and navigable.

**Home surveillance alternatives.** Since Ring integrated with Flock and Axon's law enforcement portal, home cameras that sync to Ring also feed data toward police networks. Privacy-preserving alternatives: TP-Link Tapo, Reolink NVR systems, and Lorex NVR systems all support local microSD or hard-drive storage without cloud dependency or law enforcement API integrations.

## Part 3: Houston Case Study

### The Deployment Map

Greater Houston has approximately 3,000 Flock cameras operating across overlapping ownership structures:

**City of Houston (HPD)**: 318 cameras, $6.39 million contract (August 2022, sole-source, Texas Local Government Code exemption from competitive bidding). One-year base with four renewal options — the contract has been renewed through FY2026; the FY2027 renewal decision is the immediate window.

**Harris County**: Renewed contract May 28, 2026 — 480 cameras, $868,975, through June 5, 2027. Commissioners Court voted 4-0. County Judge Lina Hidalgo abstained, characterizing the process as rushed and lacking community input.

**School districts (highest immigration risk)**: Alvin ISD (30,000 students, 8 cameras, 733,000+ searches in December 2025–January 2026 including 620 immigration enforcement searches). Huffman ISD (direct CBP credentials granted to Border Patrol by a campus police administrator in May 2025, without district knowledge). Other Harris County districts: deployment status not fully public.

**Private network**: 1,500+ Houston-area businesses; Memorial Villages PD (33 cameras, access to 1,331 additional cameras, 400,000+ plate reads monthly); Midtown Management District (25 cameras, private district authority); Pearland, Katy, Jersey Village, multiple suburban municipalities.

**National access**: Through the National Lookup Tool, all Houston-area cameras are accessible to 4,800+ agencies nationwide, and HPD can search 88,000+ cameras across the country.

### The Legal Gap in Texas

Texas has enacted no ALPR privacy law. The state is an active Flock customer. County sheriffs are mandated into 287(g) ICE agreements as of December 2025. Houston amended its ICE cooperation ordinance in April 2026 under Governor Abbott's $200 million funding threat, now allowing HPD to hold people "a reasonable amount of time" for ICE — creating structural alignment between HPD and federal immigration enforcement that directly heightens the risk of Flock data being used as an ICE location tool.

Flock was fined by Texas DPS in 2024 for operating without a private investigator's license — but that fine applied only to private deployments; government contracts are explicitly exempt.

### City Council Jurisdiction and Strategic Targets

The Houston City Council (2024-2028 term) has jurisdiction to: decline contract renewal, direct HPD policy changes by resolution, commission independent audits, and negotiate contract amendments. Mayor John Whitmire drove the April 2026 ICE cooperation amendment under state funding threat and is not a natural ally for surveillance restrictions. Build council majority first; do not rely on mayoral leadership.

**Primary targets for advocacy engagement**:

- **Tarsha Jackson (District B)**: Northeast Houston including Greenspoint and Airline District neighborhoods with significant immigrant populations. Asked pointed questions at the October 2025 Public Safety Committee Flock hearing. Most demonstrated existing engagement on this issue.
- **Joaquin Martinez (District I)**: Southeast Houston including Gulfton and Alief — among the highest immigrant population density of any district in Texas. Direct exposure to ICE enforcement patterns.
- **Mario Castillo (District H)**: East Houston with high Latino immigrant concentration. District is directly exposed to ICE enforcement expansion post-April 2026 policy change.
- **Carolyn Evans-Shabazz (District D)**: Predominantly Black southwest Houston. Surveillance technology has documented racial disparate impacts; ALPR misidentification rates are higher for Black and Latino drivers.
- **Alejandra Salinas (At-Large 4)**: Democratic at-large member elected 2025; no documented position on Flock; potentially persuadable.

**Immediate contract window action**: File a public records request today for: (1) the precise FY2027 renewal option deadline and any renewal communications; (2) HPD Flock search logs from January 2025 to present; (3) any data-sharing agreements with federal agencies. Contact: HPD Records Division and City Secretary's Office simultaneously (Texas PIRA, Ch. 552 — 10 business day response for routine records). An existing MuckRock FOIA is on file: [MuckRock Houston Flock request](https://www.muckrock.com/foi/houston-113/foia-request-for-information-concerning-houston-and-flock-safety-office-of-the-city-secretary-houston-205576/) — check its status first to avoid duplicate requests.

## Part 4: Community Organizing Framework

### Coalition Structure for Houston

The political coalition that has succeeded in other Texas cities and nationally has consistent components. Houston's demographics and organizational ecosystem map directly:

**Immigrant rights anchor**: Greater Houston has one of the largest immigrant populations of any U.S. city. The documented use of school district cameras for immigration enforcement is the most direct, concrete harm evidence. Lead organizations: Houston Immigration Legal Services Collaborative, RAICES Texas, ACLU of Texas Houston office, Texas RioGrande Legal Aid.

**Black community organizations**: ALPR surveillance has documented racial disparate impacts. The framing connects to HPD traffic stop disparities and the long pattern of discretionary enforcement: officers entering "donut" as a Flock search justification is the same discretionary pattern that drives racial disparity in traffic stops. Lead organizations: Houston NAACP, local Action Alliance chapters.

**Faith communities**: Washington's SB 6002 — the strongest ALPR law enacted in 2026 — specifically prohibits data collection at places of worship. Houston's large network of immigrant-serving churches connects faith community concerns to immigration enforcement exposure. Flock cameras near congregation entrances record who attends which services.

**Reproductive rights organizations**: Texas law enforcement used Flock to track a woman who traveled to Illinois for abortion care. The search notation: "had an abortion, search for female." Every trip to a reproductive healthcare clinic in greater Houston passes Flock cameras. 30-day retention. 4,800+ agencies with access.

**Student organizing**: University of Houston, Texas Southern University, and Rice University students have organized on related surveillance issues. The DeFlock movement has active chapters at Emory University (DeFlock Emory) that provide an organizing model.

**Parent networks and school boards**: The Alvin ISD and Huffman ISD situations require school board advocacy as a parallel track. School board members are independently elected and accountable to parents. Public board meetings (typically monthly) have public comment periods. Parent-teacher associations and immigrant parent networks are natural entry points.

### The Three-Phase Organizing Sequence

Phase 1 — Information gathering (before any public campaign): File FOIA/PIRA requests. Obtain audit logs. Map which council members have any prior position. Identify one sympathetic council member willing to introduce a resolution. Connect with ACLU of Texas and No ALPRs coalition (noalprs.org) for technical assistance.

Phase 2 — Coalition building and public record construction: Brief coalition partners on the records. Build the factual narrative around Houston-specific findings (the "donut" searches, the school district searches, the Huffman CBP access). Identify media contacts at Texas Standard, Houston Public Media, and Houston Chronicle with existing Flock beat coverage.

Phase 3 — Council engagement and public pressure: Deliver demand letters before the renewal vote (template in `flock-removal-advocacy-toolkit.md`). Organize testimony at the Public Safety Committee. Have a friendly council member introduce the model resolution (`flock-removal-advocacy-toolkit.md`, Part 4). Coordinate with DeFlock to publish the Houston camera map as a visible demonstration of surveillance scope.

### Specific Asks Tiered by Political Difficulty

**Immediate (no contract termination required, achievable by resolution)**:
- Disable the National Lookup Tool pending audit — prevents Houston cameras from being searched by ICE and out-of-state agencies, immediately
- Mandatory documented justification for every Flock search
- Prohibition on HPD sharing Flock data with federal immigration enforcement agencies
- Quarterly public audit reports on Flock search activity

**Medium-term (requires council will, not contract change)**:
- Independent audit of HPD Flock logs from the past 24 months
- Passage of a Houston TRUST Act requiring public approval for new surveillance technology acquisitions (modeled on Austin's transparency ordinance)

**Contract-level (requires either non-renewal vote or termination for cause)**:
- Non-renewal at the FY2027 renewal option
- Early termination for cause based on: unauthorized federal access (CBP pilot program pattern), Flock's documented reinstallation of cameras after cancellation in Evanston, removal of data sale prohibition from contract terms

## Part 5: Integration with Existing Resistance Infrastructure

### Connection to ICE Victim Support Networks

The existing `ice-victim-support-networks-guide.md` and `houston-ice-victim-support.md` cover immediate response networks, Know Your Rights materials, and support resources. Flock represents the upstream data collection point that feeds the enforcement actions those guides respond to.

Practical additions for immigrant community safety guidance:
- **Assume vehicle movements are recorded.** Any travel near Houston schools, businesses, and residential neighborhoods passes Flock cameras. This data is retained 30 days and accessible to 4,800+ agencies.
- **The "ICE-by-proxy" mechanism.** ICE does not need a direct Flock contract. Local police run searches on ICE's behalf — this is documented in Kyle, Texas (117 immigration searches by 18 agencies) and Dayton (7,100 in violation of city policy). Houston's April 2026 policy change increases this risk.
- **DeFlock for route planning.** Community members who need to travel privately can use maps.deflock.org to identify routes that avoid known camera clusters. This is a practical harm-reduction tool while systemic advocacy proceeds.
- **The 30-day window.** Data from the past 30 days is already in the network. Contract cancellation does not retrieve it; Flock's perpetual licensing clause means the company retains the data indefinitely.

### Connection to Illegal Surveillance Camera Removal Guide

The `illegal-surveillance-camera-removal-guide.md` covers the reasonable expectation of privacy framework, audio consent law, and demand-letter processes for illegal private surveillance. Flock cameras on public streets operate under different law: the challenge is not to a specific camera's placement (public-space video capture is generally lawful) but to the mass aggregation program.

The two guides connect in three places:
- **Audio recording law**: Texas is a one-party consent state (Tex. Penal Code § 16.02). Audio-capable Flock cameras do not violate Texas wiretap law, but federal ECPA analysis applies. If Flock cameras capture audio of conversations inside vehicles, that is a different legal question than video of plate numbers.
- **Private spaces adjacent to public spaces**: A Flock camera angled to capture the interior of a private driveway, gated courtyard, or medical clinic entrance may implicate the reasonable expectation of privacy analysis from the removal guide.
- **Demand letter framework**: The removal guide's demand-letter template for property owners can be adapted for city council communications. The rhetorical and legal structure — identify the harm, state the legal basis, demand specific action, set a response deadline — is the same.

### Connection to Voter Surveillance and Civic Chilling Effect

The domain-40 surveillance capitalism file and broader democracy crisis analysis both address the chilling effect on civic participation. Flock cameras at or near polling locations, voter registration events, political meetings, and organizing gatherings capture participant plate numbers without disclosure. This data is retained and accessible.

The structural parallel to historical civil rights suppression is precise: the combination of lawful public-space observation with political weaponization of the records. The ACLU's amicus brief in *Schmidt v. Norfolk* explicitly argues that ALPR surveillance chills First Amendment-protected speech and assembly — the same constitutional theory underlying voter intimidation doctrine.

For voter registration organizers and canvassers in Houston: assume your vehicles are logged at organizing events. This is not a reason to stop — it is a reason to fight the surveillance infrastructure. DeFlock route planning, FOIA requests documenting which agencies search political event locations, and council advocacy are the concurrent responses.

## Part 6: Actionable Next Steps for Houston Residents

Listed in sequenced order. First five actions require no organizational infrastructure — they can be executed by an individual.

**1. File the FOIA request today.** Use the template in `flock-removal-advocacy-toolkit.md`. Send to HPD Records Division and City Secretary's Office simultaneously. Request the FY2027 renewal option deadline, search logs from January 2025 to present, and data-sharing agreements. Texas law: 10-business-day response for routine records.

**2. Map your exposure.** Use maps.deflock.org to see which cameras are near your home, workplace, children's school, and common travel routes. Submit any Houston cameras not already mapped using deflock.org/report.

**3. Contact the three primary council districts.** Email or call Districts B (Tarsha Jackson), H (Mario Castillo), and I (Joaquin Martinez) this week. One message: request a meeting about the Flock contract renewal. Reference the Alvin ISD school camera immigration searches. This is not lobbying — it is constituent communication.

**4. Connect with existing organizations.** ACLU of Texas (aclutx.org), No ALPRs coalition (noalprs.org), FlockBlocker (flockblocker.org), and DeFlockOKC (deflockokc.com) all have organizing infrastructure and template materials. You do not need to build from scratch.

**5. Attend the next Public Safety Committee meeting.** Check the committee agenda at houstontx.gov/council/committees/publicsafety.html. Sign up for public comment. The 2-minute testimony template is in `flock-removal-advocacy-toolkit.md`. The October 2025 hearing established that committee members will engage on this question.

**6. File for the school boards.** Send a public comment or formal demand letter to the Alvin ISD Board of Trustees about the 620 immigration enforcement searches of school cameras. Ask the board to terminate the Flock contract and issue a public statement. This is a separate governance track from the city council — it runs in parallel, not sequence.

**7. Build toward the coalition.** As FOIA responses come in — and if the records show what Houston's pattern strongly suggests they will show — brief immigrant rights organizations, faith communities, and parent networks with the findings. The August 2022 Houston ICE cooperation ordinance (pre-April 2026), the TRUST Act advocacy in Austin, and the Harris County May 28 public comment session all established that this conversation has an active Houston constituency.

**8. When the renewal vote approaches.** Deliver the demand letter (template in `flock-removal-advocacy-toolkit.md`) before the vote. CC the city manager, city attorney, and all Public Safety Committee members. Request written confirmation it entered the contract renewal record. This creates a documented record that council members received notice and chose to act or not — material for the next election cycle either way.
