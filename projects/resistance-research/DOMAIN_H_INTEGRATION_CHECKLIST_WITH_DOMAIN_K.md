## Lead Finding

**Domain H and Domain K share six argument overlaps, three contested ownership zones, and one sequential dependency that is more consequential than any of the others.** The sequential dependency: Domain K must reach Fix the Court, Demand Justice, and ACS before Domain H reaches any of the same contacts, because the two-document sequence (legislative specificity first, constitutional architecture second) is the mechanism by which both documents activate the legal advocacy community's attention cycle. If Domain H distributes before Domain K, or simultaneously with Domain K, both documents underperform.

The six argument overlaps require careful deduplication to avoid the most common failure mode in multi-document advocacy: contacts receiving the same argument in two documents conclude the research is repetitive and reduce engagement with subsequent documents. The deduplication protocol below specifies, for each overlap, which document owns the argument at depth and which document references it briefly with a cross-pointer.

## Part 1: Cross-Reference Matrix — Arguments in Both Domain H and Domain K

### Overlap 1: German Federal Constitutional Court Structure

**Where it appears in Domain K**: Zone 3 (International Precedent), Section 3.1. Domain K covers the FCC's fixed 12-year terms, mandatory retirement at 68, bipartisan 2/3 supermajority election by Bundestag and Bundesrat, and the record of independent rulings against governing-party legislation. Domain K's treatment is legislatively focused: it argues that the German model empirically falsifies the conservative objection that fixed terms undermine judicial independence, and that the US TERM Act (H.R. 1074) is the legislative analog of the German structure.

**Where it appears in Domain H**: Zone 2 (International Precedent), Section 2.1. Domain H covers the same FCC structure but adds the December 2024 constitutional amendment — the Bundestag's 600-to-69 vote to elevate the FCC's structure to Basic Law protection, specifically in response to AfD electoral rise. Domain H's treatment is constitutionally focused: the December 2024 amendment is the international precedent for using a pre-crisis supermajority window to harden democratic institutions before an anti-democratic threat reaches governing power.

**Deduplication resolution**: Domain K owns the FCC's structural description (12-year terms, bipartisan election, retirement age) at depth. Domain H owns the December 2024 constitutional amendment at depth. Each document should reference the other for the content it does not own at depth.

Specifically:
- Domain K should add one sentence at the end of Section 3.1: "The December 2024 Bundestag vote to elevate FCC structure to Basic Law protection — analyzed in depth in the companion Domain H document — illustrates that the German model's resilience is not only structural but has been recently reinforced specifically against anti-democratic political threats."
- Domain H should add one sentence in Section 2.1 when describing the FCC's structure: "The term structure, appointment process, and institutional independence record are analyzed in detail in the companion Domain K document; the present section focuses specifically on the December 2024 constitutional amendment as a pre-crisis resilience mechanism."

### Overlap 2: Canada's Mandatory Judicial Retirement at 75

**Where it appears in Domain K**: Zone 3 (International Precedent), Section 3.2. Domain K's treatment: Canada mandates retirement at 75 under the Supreme Court Act; this has operated for nearly a century without undermining independence; Canadian justices have ruled against appointing governments on Charter rights, Indigenous rights, and federalism.

**Where it appears in Domain H**: Zone 2 (International Precedent), Section 2.2. Domain H's treatment: mandatory retirement eliminates strategic retirement timing (the Ginsburg problem); the notwithstanding clause operates as a democratic safety valve; the Reference Procedure is an advisory opinion mechanism with relevance to the US declaratory judgment alternative.

**Deduplication resolution**: Domain K owns the basic mandate-retirement-at-75 argument and the independence track record. Domain H owns the notwithstanding clause and Reference Procedure as distinct constitutional design features not covered in Domain K.

No cross-pointer required in Domain K — the Canadian citation in Domain K is brief and Domain H extends it substantially into new territory. A reader who has read Domain K's Canada section will encounter genuinely new material in Domain H's Section 2.2.

### Overlap 3: South Africa's Constitutional Court (Non-Renewable 12-Year Terms)

**Where it appears in Domain K**: Zone 3 (International Precedent), Section 3.4. Domain K: South Africa's post-apartheid constitution deliberately chose the non-renewable 12-year term model because the architects had experienced judicial manipulation under apartheid. The post-authoritarian constitutional design context.

**Where it appears in Domain H**: Zone 2 (International Precedent), Section 2.3. Domain H: the Constitutional Court's 30-year institutional resilience record; the 2016 Zuma case (*Economic Freedom Fighters v. Speaker*) as the paradigm case of a court surviving direct executive pressure; the three transferable resilience mechanisms (mandatory retirement, transparent appointment procedure, Section 39(1)(b) international law mandate).

**Deduplication resolution**: Domain K owns the basic South Africa structural citation and the post-authoritarian design rationale. Domain H owns the 30-year resilience record, the Zuma case, and the specific transferable mechanisms. These are genuinely different arguments with the South Africa model as the shared reference point.

Cross-pointer in Domain K Section 3.4: Add one sentence — "Domain H's comparative analysis develops the 30-year resilience record and specific transferable mechanisms of the South African model in Section 2.3."

### Overlap 4: 18-Year Supreme Court Term Limits (H.R. 1074 and Statutory vs. Amendment Paths)

**Where it appears in Domain K**: Zone 1 (Legislative Inventory), Section 1.1 (primary ownership); Zone 2 (Constitutional Feasibility), Sections 2.1–2.5. Domain K: legislative history of H.R. 1074 and Senate companion; active/senior justice model; Article III "good Behaviour" language; Compensation Clause constraint; 2021 Presidential Commission analysis.

**Where it appears in Domain H**: Zone 3 (Amendment Strategy), Section 3.2 (Amendment 2 — Supreme Court Term Limits). Domain H: the amendment is one of five priority amendments; 73% public support; coalition requirement (retired judges, business community, 85 law professors); the amendment campaign as a multi-year coalition-building infrastructure even before the political window opens for passage.

**Deduplication resolution**: Domain K owns the constitutional feasibility analysis (active/senior model, Article III question, Presidential Commission findings) at depth. Domain H owns the amendment campaign strategy (coalition architecture, political conditions for supermajority, the case for pursuing the amendment path even when passage is not near-term) at depth.

These are genuinely complementary arguments: Domain K answers "can term limits be done by statute?" (yes, with risk); Domain H answers "what is the amendment strategy even if the statute works?" (build the political coalition regardless, because the statutory approach may be struck down and the amendment infrastructure will be needed).

The combination of Domain K's constitutional feasibility analysis and Domain H's amendment coalition strategy converts term limits advocacy from a binary statutory/amendment question into a dual-track approach: pursue the statute, build the amendment coalition simultaneously. Contacts who receive both documents understand this dual-track logic; contacts who receive only one document understand only half of it.

### Overlap 5: Democracy 2025 Litigation Infrastructure

**Where it appears in Domain K**: Zone 4 (Coalition Activation), Section 4.1 — briefly, in the context of judicial reform advocacy organizations.

**Where it appears in Domain H**: Zone 4 (Movement Infrastructure), Section 4.1. Domain H: full treatment of Democracy 2025 — 650+ partner organizations, 516 tracked cases, 405 victories (226 by Democracy 2025 partners, 70 by state AGs), the distinction between defensive litigation coordination and affirmative constitutional reform advocacy.

**Deduplication resolution**: Domain H owns Democracy 2025 at depth. Domain K's brief mention requires no cross-pointer — it is a passing reference in a context that does not develop the argument. No reader confusion risk.

### Overlap 6: American Constitution Society Distribution Network

**Where it appears in Domain K**: Zone 4 (Coalition Activation), Section 4.1 — ACS as a distribution network and pipeline for expert contacts.

**Where it appears in Domain H**: Zone 4 (Movement Infrastructure) and the Coalition Activation Plan — ACS as primary institutional home for progressive constitutional scholarship; Law2030 reform initiative; the 15+ organizational coalition for immediate activation.

**Deduplication resolution**: Both documents distribute to ACS. This is intentional — ACS is the shared audience for both documents. There is no content duplication requiring deduplication; ACS receives Domain K (legislative proposals) and Domain H (constitutional architecture) as sequenced documents that together constitute the full reform argument. ACS staff who work on both will experience this as coherent sequencing, not repetition.

## Part 2: Hand-Off Points — Who Owns What

### Constitutional Amendment Strategy: Domain H Owns It

**The rule**: Constitutional amendment strategy — the sequencing of five priority amendments, coalition architecture for supermajority consensus, the three-timescale framework (immediate statutory/medium reclamation/long-term amendment) — belongs entirely to Domain H. Domain K does not develop constitutional amendment strategy; it references the amendment option as a fallback if the statutory term limits approach fails constitutional challenge.

**Why this matters**: If Domain K develops its own constitutional amendment strategy section — even a brief one — it creates competing amendment sequencing frameworks. Fix the Court and Demand Justice staff who receive both documents will find themselves reconciling two amendment strategies from the same research framework, which reduces confidence in both. Domain K must explicitly defer to Domain H on constitutional amendment strategy, using a cross-reference: "The constitutional amendment pathway — its strategic purpose, coalition requirements, and sequencing logic — is developed fully in the companion Domain H document."

**Specific implication for Domain K Zone 2, Section 2.2**: The current Domain K text notes that the Manchin-Welch Constitutional Amendment "resolves the constitutional uncertainty by directly amending Article III's 'good Behaviour' language, but requires 38 state ratifications — a 10-to-15-year timeline under favorable conditions." This framing — accurate as far as it goes — should be followed by: "The coalition-building strategy for the constitutional amendment pathway, and its role in a dual-track approach alongside the statutory active/senior model, is the subject of the companion Domain H document's Zone 3 analysis."

### Federal Judicial Appointment Strategy: Domain K Owns It (With One Boundary Note)

**The rule**: The specific legislative mechanics of judicial appointment reform — bipartisan confirmation procedures, Senate Judiciary Committee hearing requirements, merit-selection proposals — belong to Domain K. Domain H references appointment procedures as part of the broader constitutional design argument but does not develop specific legislative proposals for appointment reform.

**The boundary note**: Domain H Zone 1, Section 1.2 discusses judicial capture as a constitutional design failure enabled by the Senate's ability to refuse hearings indefinitely (the Garland mechanism) and to confirm nominees with bare majorities in eight days (the Barrett mechanism). Domain H argues these are structural norm failures, not statutory violations. Domain K Zone 1, Section 1.5 (Federal Judgeships Act) and Domain K's broader legislative inventory address what Congress can do through statute to codify appointment procedures.

The boundary: Domain H diagnoses the constitutional design failure. Domain K prescribes the legislative fix. Domain H should not recommend specific appointment procedures — that is Domain K's territory. Domain K should not explain why the norm failures constitute a constitutional design problem — that is Domain H's territory.

**Practical check**: If a passage in either document says both "this is a constitutional design failure caused by structural norm erosion" (Domain H language) AND "here is the specific legislation that would fix it" (Domain K language), that passage needs to be split. Move the diagnosis to Domain H (or confirm Domain H already has it), move the prescription to Domain K.

### Emergency Powers Reform: Domain H Owns the Constitutional Argument, Domain K Does Not Cover It

**The rule**: Emergency powers reform — IEEPA reform legislation (Kaine-Beyer), the NEA sunset mechanism, the constitutional design gap between Article I's enumeration of congressional war powers and Article II's "executive power" — is fully developed in Domain H Zone 1, Section 1.1 and Zone 3, Section 3.3. Domain K does not cover emergency powers reform.

This is not an overlap — it is a clean Domain H territory without Domain K involvement. But it is noted here because users of the combined "Constitutional Reform Briefing Package" (the 6-page Combined Brief targeting congressional staff) may expect emergency powers reform to appear in Domain K's legislative inventory. It does not. Congressional staff reading the Combined Brief should be directed to Domain H's Section 3.3 for emergency powers statutory reform proposals.

**Implication for Combined Brief**: The Combined Brief's Domain H synthesis section (2 pages) must include the IEEPA reform legislation and NEA sunset mechanism from Domain H Section 3.3, because congressional staff working on the January 3 reform agenda will want this statutory reclamation content alongside Domain K's judiciary reform proposals.

### Shadow Docket Reform: Domain K Owns the Legislation, Domain H Owns the Constitutional Design Implication

**The rule**: The specific legislative proposals (SHADOW Act, SCCOTUS Act) belong to Domain K Zone 1, Section 1.4. The constitutional design argument about why the shadow docket's expansion represents a structural separation-of-powers failure — not merely a transparency problem — belongs to Domain H Zone 1, Section 1.2 (Judicial Capture Post-Dobbs/Loper Bright).

**Key distinction**: Domain K argues the shadow docket requires transparency reform (written opinions, recorded votes, Kagan's dissent as evidence of the accountability problem). Domain H argues the shadow docket is the mechanism through which captured courts make binding constitutional decisions without deliberation, precedential weight, or democratic accountability — a deeper constitutional design problem that transparency alone cannot solve. The constitutional design argument belongs to Domain H. The legislative remedy argument belongs to Domain K.

### FISA Court Reform: Domain K Owns It Entirely

Domain K Zone 1, Section 1.6 covers FISA court reform — secret court structure, adversarial proceeding gaps, Wyden declassification deal. Domain H does not cover FISA court reform. No overlap, no deduplication needed. Users seeking FISA court reform content should be directed to Domain K.

## Part 3: Deduplication Protocol

**Protocol Step 1**: Before finalizing either document, the researcher or team reads both Domain K and Domain H side by side against the six overlap areas identified in Part 1.

For each overlap, confirm that:
- The document that "owns" the argument develops it at full depth (evidence, citations, analytical argument, coalition implications)
- The document that "references" the argument uses a single sentence or paragraph with an explicit cross-pointer to the owning document
- No owned argument appears in both documents at full depth

**Protocol Step 2**: Draft the cross-pointer language for each overlap that requires it.

Required cross-pointers (confirmed from the overlap analysis):

Domain K cross-pointer additions:
- Section 3.1 (Germany): add sentence pointing to Domain H Section 2.1 for the December 2024 constitutional amendment analysis
- Section 3.4 (South Africa): add sentence pointing to Domain H Section 2.3 for the 30-year resilience record
- Zone 2, Section 2.2 (Amendment pathway): add sentence pointing to Domain H Zone 3 for coalition-building strategy

Domain H cross-pointer additions:
- Section 2.1 (Germany): add sentence pointing to Domain K Zone 3 for the FCC's structural description and independence track record
- Section 3.2 (Term Limits amendment, Amendment 2): add sentence confirming Domain K's Zone 2 develops the constitutional feasibility analysis

**Protocol Step 3**: Verify that the Combined Brief (6-page synthesis) does not reproduce the same content from both zones — each zone's contribution to the Combined Brief must be non-overlapping. The Combined Brief uses:
- Domain K Zone 1 content for the "what can we do by statute" section
- Domain H Zone 3 content for the "what requires amendment and why pursue it" section
- Domain H Zone 2 content for the international comparison table
- Domain K Zone 3 content is not separately featured in the Combined Brief (the international comparison table draws on Domain H's synthesis which covers the same countries with different emphasis)

**Protocol Step 4**: Run the cross-pointer text by whoever produced both documents to confirm the cross-pointers are accurate before distribution.

## Part 4: Sequential Research Order — Should Domain K Complete First?

**Answer: Yes, Domain K must complete before Domain H's Zone 3 can be finalized.**

The reason is specific and bounded: Domain H's Zone 3, Section 3.2 (Priority Amendment Architecture) specifies which of Domain K's legislative proposals require constitutional amendment rather than just statute. The table of "which reforms require amendment" in Zone 3 is wrong if Domain K's Zone 2 (Constitutional Feasibility) has not been read and incorporated. If Domain K concludes that the active/senior justice model for term limits is constitutionally viable without amendment, Domain H Zone 3's term limits entry should emphasize the amendment track as a parallel/insurance strategy rather than a prerequisite. If Domain K concludes the statutory model is constitutionally risky, Domain H Zone 3's term limits entry should emphasize the amendment track as the primary long-term strategy.

This is a Zone 3 finalization dependency, not a research dependency. Domain H's Zones 1, 2, and 4 can be fully produced in parallel with or before Domain K. But Zone 3's Section 3.2 cannot be finalized until Domain K's constitutional feasibility analysis is complete.

**Practical implication**: In the solo researcher path, the researcher reads Domain K's Zone 2 before finalizing Domain H's Zone 3. This is the natural reading sequence anyway if the researcher is also producing Domain K (or has access to it). In the team path, Researcher B (who produces Domain H Zone 3) must receive Domain K's Zone 2 section before completing Section 3.2 — the hand-off point is Domain K Zone 2 completion, not the full Domain K production.

**Parallel research is feasible for all other sections**: Domain H Zones 1, 2, and 4 can develop in parallel with Domain K Zones 1, 3, and 4 without interdependency. Zone 2 (Constitutional Feasibility) in Domain K → Zone 3 (Amendment Strategy) in Domain H is the one directional dependency.

## Part 5: January 3 Coordination — Domain K to Congressional Staff, Domain H to Constitutional Expert Testimony and Op-Eds

### Timing

**December 12–20**: Domain K distributes to Fix the Court (Gabe Roth), Demand Justice (Josh Orton), ACS programs staff, Senate Judiciary Committee staff (Whitehouse, Durbin offices), House Judiciary Ranking Member Raskin's office.

**January 3**: 120th Congress seated. The advocacy window opens. Immediately confirm committee assignment process has begun.

**January 10**: Domain H distributes to ACS (different contact — constitutional reform programs, not judicial reform advocacy), National Constitution Center (Jeffrey Rosen), Protect Democracy (Ian Bassin), incoming Judiciary Committee members who have stated public positions on SCOTUS reform, constitutional law faculty (Chemerinsky, Balkin, Scheppele via institutional channels).

**January 10 simultaneously**: Combined Brief (Domain K + Domain H 6-page synthesis) distributes to Senate Judiciary Committee staff and House Judiciary Committee staff (if Democratic majority). This is the combined document that congressional staff who have already received Domain K in December now receive alongside Domain H.

**January 17**: Committee assignments finalize. After this date, incoming member briefings on structural reform become significantly harder. All Tier 1 congressional sends must be complete before January 14.

### Content Overlap Between January Domain H Distribution and Domain K December Distribution

The January 3 coordination must avoid content redundancy while ensuring coherent argument continuity. The specific risk: Senate Judiciary Committee staff received Domain K in December. They now receive the Combined Brief in January, which includes a Domain K synthesis. If the Combined Brief's Domain K summary contradicts or merely reproduces Domain K's December distribution, it creates confusion.

**Resolution**: The Combined Brief's Domain K section is not a summary of Domain K's full content — it is a 2-page synthesis focused specifically on the legislative proposals most relevant to the 120th Congress's first-90-days reform agenda, with explicit references to Domain K for the detailed analysis. Staff who want the detail go back to Domain K; the Combined Brief gives them the action-item version. This is the appropriate format for January distribution, when staff are focused on specific bills rather than comprehensive analysis.

### Domain H as Constitutional Expert Testimony and Op-Ed Resource

Domain H's distribution to constitutional law faculty (Chemerinsky, Balkin, Scheppele) is calibrated to the legal community's natural advocacy channels:

**Expert testimony**: Congressional Judiciary Committees hold hearings on judicial reform legislation. Domain H is a primary research resource for expert witnesses testifying on constitutional amendment proposals, comparative constitutional design, and the January 2027 reform window argument. Erwin Chemerinsky, who testified on SCOTUS term limits for the Senate Judiciary Committee in the 118th Congress, is the most likely Domain H-informed expert witness in January–March 2027 Judiciary Committee hearings.

**Op-eds**: Constitutional law scholars with public platforms (Chemerinsky at SCOTUSblog, Balkin at Balkinization, Siegel via Yale Law School communications) are natural Domain H amplification channels. The January 2027 window is a natural op-ed occasion — "what the new Congress should prioritize on judicial reform" is a high-demand op-ed topic immediately after the new Congress is seated. Domain H provides the comparative constitutional design and amendment strategy arguments that distinguish a law professor op-ed from a generic "we need SCOTUS reform" piece.

Domain H and Domain K are complementary op-ed resources: Domain K provides the specific legislative proposals; Domain H provides the structural constitutional argument for why those proposals matter in the context of democratic resilience rather than mere political preference.

### Channel and Content Overlap Management

The risk of Domain H and Domain K using the same contacts (Brennan Center, ACS, Protect Democracy) in the same two-week window is contact fatigue. The PHASE_3_MOVEMENT_LEVERAGE_ARCHITECTURE.md's "10-14 day spacing rule for shared contacts" addresses this directly.

Specific shared contacts and their sequencing:

**ACS**: Receives Domain K first (December 20), via the judicial reform advocacy programs contact. Receives Domain H second (January 10), via the constitutional law reform programs contact. These are different contact points within ACS — the judicial reform and constitutional reform programs are distinct program areas. No fatigue risk if the send is routed to the right contact.

**Brennan Center**: Receives Domain K (December 20) via Alicia Bannon (Judiciary Program). Receives Domain H (January 10) via the Voting Rights and Elections Program or a separate send to Bannon acknowledging she has already received Domain K and this is the structural architecture companion. The 21-day gap between the two sends is sufficient to prevent fatigue.

**Protect Democracy**: Receives Domain H (January 10) as its primary document — Protect Democracy is primarily a constitutional litigation organization, making Domain H (structural constitutional architecture) a more natural fit than Domain K (judiciary reform legislation). If Protect Democracy is in Domain K's distribution (it should be as a secondary contact), stagger by 14 days.
