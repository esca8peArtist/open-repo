**The democratic exclusion framing** (why this is not simply a drug policy question):

Three mechanisms insulate drug policy from democratic accountability. First, the scheduling process has been captured by the Drug Enforcement Administration — a law enforcement agency with institutional incentives systematically biased toward restriction, which has functioned as a de facto veto authority over HHS public health expertise since 1970. The Yale Law Journal analysis by Mason Marks ("Separation of Drug Scheduling Powers," 2024) documents the divergence from the CSA's statutory text: "Courts and agencies have given less deference to the Secretary's [HHS] recommendations than the CSA demands, deferring instead to DEA's statutory interpretations, producing regulatory redundancy and unscientific scheduling actions that contradict the CSA text, purpose, and history." The OLC opinion that converted HHS from a co-equal scientific authority to an advisory voice that DEA can override is the specific mechanism of regulatory capture.

Second, felony disenfranchisement creates a feedback loop: drug enforcement — which produces felony convictions at racially disparate rates (Black Americans arrested for marijuana possession at 3.64 times the rate of white Americans, per ACLU 2023 data) — removes disenfranchised individuals from the electorate. The communities most subjected to drug enforcement have reduced political power in precisely the jurisdictions where enforcement is most aggressive. The Sentencing Project documents 4 million Americans currently disenfranchised due to felony conviction. The ACLU's analysis connects disenfranchisement not merely to racial injustice but to the structural perpetuation of the enforcement regime itself: the communities who would vote to revise drug policy cannot vote.

Third, 24 state democratic majorities — which enacted recreational cannabis legalization through direct-democracy referenda (Colorado Amendment 64: 55.32%; California Prop 64: 57.13%; Michigan Prop 18-1: 56%) and legislative action — remain in active conflict with federal Schedule I classification that Congress has been unable to revise despite 15+ years of majority public support and despite 68% current public support for full legalization (Gallup 2024, the highest recorded figure). The SAFER Banking Act has passed the House seven times with bipartisan support and has not received a Senate floor vote.

**What the April 23 DOJ order did and did not do**:

Acting AG Todd Blanche's April 23, 2026 order moved FDA-approved marijuana products and state-licensed medical cannabis to Schedule III, effective immediately. It left recreational cannabis in Schedule I. The $2.24 billion in estimated annual excess taxes under 280E continues for recreational businesses. Federal banking access remains unavailable. Legal analysts at Foley Hoag LLP, Gibson Dunn, and Scarinci Hollenbeck characterized the framework as "deliberately unstable" — designed to generate enforcement uncertainty without resolving the federal-state conflict.

The DEA hearing beginning June 29 will adjudicate whether marijuana as a whole moves to Schedule III. The procedural structure of that hearing is itself the democratic accountability problem: DEA administrative law judges — DEA employees operating within DEA institutional culture with enforcement-biased incentives — will adjudicate a question about what schedules DEA should administer. The APA does not require hearing adjudicators to be independent of the agency whose regulatory posture is being adjudicated.

**The most acute near-term risk**:

Section 591 of the CSA authorizes Congress to freeze DEA rulemaking through an appropriations rider. Republican appropriators have used this mechanism historically. WeedPress (May 4, 2026) documents this as the highest near-term risk to the hearing: if a Section 591 rider attaches to the FY2027 appropriations bill, the hearing could be frozen before it concludes, returning to the pre-April 23 status quo. Organizations working on this issue need to be positioned before the appropriations process closes that window.
