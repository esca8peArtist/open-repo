## Zone 1 — Legal Infrastructure Elimination

**Anchor sources read:** A1 (Federal Register 2026-03157), A2 (Federal Register 2026-00178), A3 (EPA OEJECR press release), A4-A5 (EOs 14173 and 14158), A6 (Marin Audubon v. FAA), D2 (Georgetown Environmental Law Review), D5 (Crowell & Moring "180-Degree Change"), D9 (Sidley Austin "50 Years").

### Finding 1.1 — The "DEI" Reframe is the Administrative Weapon

The EPA press release announcing OEJECR's closure (A3) explicitly frames the office as a "Biden's environmental justice DEI arm." This language is not incidental — it is the administrative mechanism through which civil rights enforcement authority was converted to a categorically eliminable "DEI" program. EO 14173 (A4) and EO 14158 (A5) provided the authority; the press release applied it. The analytical move that Domain 49 must make explicit: OEJECR administered Title VI civil rights complaint authority and grant administration, neither of which is a "DEI" function under any established legal definition. Crowell & Moring's "180-Degree Change" analysis (D5) is the most rigorous law-firm documentation of this gap.

**Implication for Zone 1 framing**: The DEI reframe is the Zone 1 argument, not background. Lead with it.

### Finding 1.2 — The Marin Audubon Trap

The D.C. Circuit's November 2024 Marin Audubon decision (A6) judicially foreclosed administrative restoration of CEQ NEPA participation rights before the Trump administration even rescinded the regulations in January 2026. Sidley Austin's "50 Years" framing (D9) captures what the 2-1 panel decision without full merits briefing accomplished: it eliminated the legal basis for restoring CEQ-based NEPA requirements through administrative rulemaking, making statutory NEPA amendment the only viable path. The combination of Marin Audubon (November 2024) + CEQ NEPA rescission (January 8, 2026) + Seven County SCOTUS "deference squared" ruling (May 29, 2025, N4) constitutes a three-layer judicial and administrative foreclosure of community participation rights that cannot be undone through administrative action alone.

**Implication for Zone 3**: The reform pathway section depends entirely on this Zone 1 finding. Audiences who don't understand the Marin Audubon trap will underestimate why only statutory reform is adequate.

### Finding 1.3 — Simultaneous Administrative Actions as Coordinated Strategy

The four simultaneous removals — OEJECR (March 12, 2025), EJSCREEN (February 5, 2025), CEJST (2025), and CEQ NEPA regulations (January 8, 2026) — happened within a 13-month window under a common administrative authority network (EOs 14173 and 14158). This is not sequential bureaucratic downsizing. The simultaneity is evidence of coordinated strategy: remove the data tools (EJSCREEN, CEJST), remove the advisory office (OEJECR), then remove the regulatory procedural requirements (CEQ NEPA) so that no administrative pathway for participation remains. Harvard EELP's three trackers (B1, B2, G4) document each action's timeline independently; cross-referencing them reveals the strategic sequence.

**Zone interdependency**: Zone 1's "coordinated strategy" finding is the analytical prerequisite for Zone 2's civic exclusion argument. If the removals were coincidental policy changes, the democratic design critique doesn't hold. The simultaneity and sequence are the evidence of design.

## Zone 2 — Civic Participation Mechanisms

**Anchor sources read:** C1 (UCS Voting Rights and EJ), C3 (PMC 12779674 2026 peer-reviewed study), C4 (2026 Civic Information Index), F1 (WE ACT Title VI MTA complaint), F3 (DSCEJ Endangerment Finding response), F4 (PRRAC Cancer Alley Title VI), B4 (EDGI EJ Grants Map), B6 (NY AG EJ grant challenge).

### Finding 2.1 — The Vicious Cycle Is Empirically Documented

The UCS "Building a Healthier Democracy" analysis (C2) establishes the vicious cycle mechanism — environmental burden reduces civic capacity, reduced civic capacity enables more environmental burden — but the 2026 Civic Information Index (C4) provides the first data that maps this cycle geographically: communities with lower civic health scores (measured by voter registration, civic participation, local news availability) are consistently those with higher percentages of Black, Hispanic/Latino, and Native American residents and higher environmental burden indices. The gap in Appalachia and the Black South — where more than 40% of registered voters did not vote in the last presidential election — is now empirically co-located with the communities most dependent on EPA EJ infrastructure.

**Key finding for Zone 2 synthesis**: The vicious cycle is not a theoretical model. The 2026 Civic Information Index makes it a measured empirical finding with geographic specificity. This is the strongest available anchor for the democratic participation argument.

### Finding 2.2 — WE ACT and the Title VI Pathway: What Participation Looked Like in Practice

The WE ACT Title VI complaint against the MTA (F1) documents in concrete terms what "civic participation through EPA infrastructure" meant: an organization filed an administrative complaint against transit authority siting discrimination in 2000, which triggered an EPA Title VI investigation, which produced a settlement requiring the MTA to convert diesel buses to natural gas in eight depots — all without litigation and without legislation. That administrative pathway no longer exists. The EPA's Title VI complaint queue collapse (F6 — Sentient Media: 5 complaints rejected, 13 pending with no timeline, 9 investigations frozen) documents that the pathway doesn't merely lack resources; it is functionally closed.

**Key finding for Zone 2 synthesis**: The Title VI pathway was a genuine civic participation tool — not symbolic, not a last resort, but a first-order mechanism through which communities obtained relief. Its elimination removes a participation tool that worked.

### Finding 2.3 — "Resistant Capital" as the Buffering Factor

The 2026 peer-reviewed study (PMC 12779674, C3) is the strongest academic anchor for Zone 2. Its finding that "resistant capital" — defined as understanding systems of oppression — is the strongest predictor of environmental civic participation for Black, Asian, and Latine/Hispanic individuals inverts the conventional capacity-building model: what enables environmental civic engagement is not institutional access alone, but prior experience with marginalization combined with organizational infrastructure. This means OEJECR's elimination removed not just a complaint pathway but the institutional connection between communities with high "resistant capital" and the administrative processes through which that capital could be converted to civic outcomes. The 300 OEJECR employees who were terminated (B8) were that institutional connection.

## Zone 3 — D.C. Circuit Challenge and Reform Pathways

**Anchor sources read:** E1 (APHA v. EPA clearinghouse), E4-E5 (Earthjustice case pages), E7 (Michigan AG Nessel challenge), J1 (Earthjustice SPEED Act statement), J2 (Earthjustice SCOTUS NEPA 2025), N4-N5 (Seven County and CRS "deference squared").

### Finding 3.1 — DC Circuit Status: Briefing Deferred to Mid-July 2026

As of April 2026, APHA v. EPA (No. 26-1037) was not scheduled for oral argument. Petitioners filed for a 90-day deferral of the merits briefing call, to approximately July 20, 2026, pending EPA's response to reconsideration petitions. This means the case is now at a threshold moment: either EPA responds to the reconsideration petitions before July 20, which could reshape the legal landscape, or the case proceeds to briefing. The Domain 49 pre-check gate in the runbook directs verification of this status on July 1, 2026. The current status (as of July 4) is: briefing deferral in effect, oral argument not yet scheduled, EPA response pending.

**Implication for research timing**: Zone 3 analysis cannot be finalized until the DC Circuit resolves the reconsideration petition question. This is an open gate. Track CourtListener (APHA v. EPA No. 26-1037) for updated status.

### Finding 3.2 — The Statutory Reform Paradox

The Zone 1 finding (Marin Audubon trap) creates a structural paradox that is Zone 3's most important analytical contribution: the only legally adequate remedy for the participation rights elimination is statutory NEPA reform enacted by Congress. But the Callais redistricting cascade (the redistricting consequences documented in the existing Domain 49 production document) reduces the electoral likelihood of electing a Congress that will pass statutory NEPA reform. Electing such a Congress requires mobilizing EJ communities whose civic capacity has been degraded by the EPA EJ infrastructure elimination. The causal loop is explicit: you cannot restore the tools needed to elect the legislature that would restore the tools.

**This is the most analytically novel Zone 3 finding and the capstone of the domain's argument.** The SPEED Act (J1) is the legislative threat that makes this more urgent: House passage (December 2025, 221-196) would codify the NEPA rollback in statute, foreclosing even future administrative restoration. Senate status as of July 2026: pending, with significant Democratic opposition. The Senate is the firewall.

### Finding 3.3 — State AGs as the Live Litigation Infrastructure

Despite the multiple federal foreclosures, state AGs are actively using NEPA tools post-rescission. The Maryland AG NEPA lawsuit against DHS/ICE (N2) and the Salt Lake City KUER documentation (N3) show that statutory NEPA — as opposed to the rescinded CEQ regulations — remains litigable in federal court. Thirteen state AGs affirmed state EJ programs operate independently (E8). The NJ Appellate Division victory upholding NJ EJ law (H7, January 5, 2026) confirms that state courts are simultaneously moving pro-EJ as federal law moves anti-EJ. The state AG coalition is the live Zone 3 litigation infrastructure; it does not depend on the DC Circuit's timing.

## Zone 4 — State Responses and Comparative Standards

**Anchor sources read:** G1-G2 (Aarhus Convention full text and ratification status), H1 (Columbia Sabin Center NY EJ Law), H3 (EJStatebyState.org), H7 (Earthjustice NJ victory), H9 (CPR Federal-State Conflicts), I1 (Harvard EELP Cumulative Impacts).

### Finding 4.1 — The 42-State Gap Is the Reform Target

EJStatebyState.org (H3) documents eight states with cumulative impact bills: California, Connecticut, Georgia, Maryland, Minnesota, New Jersey, New York, North Carolina. The remaining 42 states — including every Gulf Coast state (Louisiana, Texas, Mississippi, Alabama), all Appalachian states, and the industrial Midwest states most heavily affected by the EPA EJ infrastructure elimination — have no equivalent protection. The NJ Appellate Division victory (H7) establishes that the model EJ bill framework survives judicial scrutiny when properly drafted. The Columbia Sabin Center / WE ACT model bills (H2) provide the legislative template. The 42-state gap is the reform architecture argument in its simplest form.

**Zone 4 to Zone 3 interdependency**: The statutory reform pathway in Zone 3 (congressional NEPA reform) is the federal strategy; Zone 4's 42-state gap analysis is the state-level strategy. Both are necessary; neither is sufficient alone. The domain should make this explicit.

### Finding 4.2 — Aarhus as the Design Standard

The Aarhus Convention's three-pillar framework (G1) — access to information, public participation, access to justice — maps precisely onto what the EPA eliminated: EJSCREEN and CEJST (access to information), OEJECR participation pathways (public participation), and Title VI complaint infrastructure (access to justice). The US signed but never ratified Aarhus. The EU ratification means 46 jurisdictions are legally bound to a participation rights standard that the US is now systematically dismantling. This is not a theoretical comparison: it is a design standard against which the US can be measured and found critically deficient.

**Zone 4's strongest argument is negative**: The US is moving in the opposite direction from the international democratic norm at the moment when climate and environmental crises make community participation most critical.

### Finding 4.3 — Federal Preemption Threat to State Floors

The April 8, 2025 EO directing the AG to challenge state EJ laws (H4), documented by CPR (H9), means that even the eight-state EJ floor is under active federal threat. The Marten Law analysis (H5) documents the preemption strategy. The NJ victory (H7) is the defense — state courts are upholding state EJ laws against industry challenges. The federal government preemption threat is a second front that Zone 4 must address: the federal rollback is being pursued both directly (elimination of federal programs) and indirectly (challenging state programs that fill the vacuum).

## Zone 1 — The Ballot Initiative Weapon

**Anchor sources read:** A1 (Romer v. Evans), A3 (Colorado Sun 2026), B1-B3 (Ballotpedia tracker, Ballotpedia News projection, BISC Hot Sheet), B7 (LGBTQ Nation Washington genital exam), B8-B9 (Missouri Amendment 3), D3-D4 (ADF 2026 agenda, Yost hire), D1 (SPLC ADF Extremist File).

### Finding 1.1 — Romer v. Evans Is the Constitutional Anchor — and the D.C. Circuit Has Reconfirmed It

Romer v. Evans (A1) held that a ballot initiative removing a minority group from the political processes available to all others violates the Equal Protection Clause. The D.C. Circuit's June 1, 2026 Talbott ruling (M2) applied precisely this logic — "bare desire to harm a politically unpopular group" — to the trans military ban, one month before the anti-trans ballot measure season peaks. The constitutional principle is live in the federal courts. The strategic significance: Lambda Legal and GLAD can cite Talbott's Romer language in pre-election challenges to Colorado and Washington ballot measures, arguing the D.C. Circuit has already confirmed the standard applies to current anti-trans executive actions.

**This is the most important Zone 1 finding**: Romer is not historical precedent only. Talbott confirms it is active constitutional doctrine being applied in 2026.

### Finding 1.2 — The ADF Coordination Infrastructure Is Now Documented from Three Primary Sources

ADF drafted at least 130 bills in 34 states (SPLC, D1); ADF's own 2026 legislative agenda explicitly names ballot initiative deployment as a strategy (D3); the Yost hire (Ohio AG Dave Yost left office June 7, 2026 to join ADF as VP of Strategic Research, D4) is the single most concrete piece of evidence that ADF is not a litigation organization but an institutional infrastructure builder integrating state government. The BISC March 2026 Hot Sheet (B3) documents that groups with documented ADF relationships are tracking 78+ measures on 2026 ballots. The three independent documentation streams — SPLC (organizational profile), ADF's own documents, and the Yost institutional integration — constitute a primary-source case that is harder to challenge than any single advocacy organization's characterization.

### Finding 1.3 — "Ballot Candy" as the Cross-Domain Weapon

Missouri Amendment 3 (B8) bundles the trans healthcare restriction (approximately two-thirds support in polling) with a reversal of Missouri's 2024 voter-approved abortion rights amendment (polling below 40% support). This is not a coincidence. The KCUR analysis (B9) documents that the bundling strategy was deliberate: the trans restriction carries the abortion reversal. The Missouri abortion-rights coalition launched a joint campaign against Amendment 3 in May 2026 (B10), demonstrating that the reproductive rights and LGBTQ+ organizations have already recognized the cross-domain nature of the threat. The joint campaign is the organizational expression of what Domain 50 must argue analytically: ADF operates a unified infrastructure that attacks reproductive rights and LGBTQ+ rights simultaneously, using each domain's politically stronger position to carry the weaker.

## Zone 2 — Trans Voter Suppression Stack

**Anchor sources read:** E1 (Williams Institute 210,800 figure), E2 (Williams Institute EO 14168 impact), E3 (NWLC SAVE Act analysis), E6 (Brennan Center trans voter ID), E14 (Senator Padilla SAVE Act defeat), I5 (ACLU passport stay).

### Finding 2.1 — The Three-Layer Stack and Its Current Status

Layer 1 (ID mismatch): 210,800 trans adults without matching ID in voter-ID states (Williams Institute, E1, updated April 2026). Layer 2 (EO 14168 document freeze): Federal document update pathways frozen — passport (X marker suspended, ACLU passport stay by SCOTUS conservative majority removing class injunction, I5), Social Security records frozen — growing the 210,800 figure monthly. Layer 3 (SAVE Act): Defeated in the Senate June 4, 2026 (NPR report; 48-50 vote, four Republicans joined Democrats). However: the Center for American Progress documented state-level SAVE Act versions advancing across multiple states in June 2026. The federal defeat removed the legislative urgency hook, but state-level replication means the suppression threat is distributed, not eliminated.

**Key Zone 2 update required**: All prior Domain 50 drafting that frames the SAVE Act as a live federal legislative threat must be updated. The current framing: SAVE Act was defeated at the federal level but state versions are advancing. The suppression population (Layer 1 + Layer 2) is still 210,800+ and growing. The federal defeat is a mitigation, not a resolution.

### Finding 2.2 — The Passport Stay Worsens the Suppression Count

The SCOTUS conservative majority's decision to stay the expanded class-certification injunction for trans/nonbinary/intersex passport applicants (I5) means the passport update pathway — which had briefly been restored by a lower court order — was removed while litigation continues. Passport is a valid photo ID for voting purposes in most states. The stay removes one viable ID-update pathway from the approximately 210,800 trans adults with ID mismatch. The Domain 50 runbook pre-check gate specifically flags this as requiring an update to Pillar 2 (trans voter suppression stack). The update is: the suppression population is larger than the 210,800 figure because the passport pathway was removed by the SCOTUS stay.

### Finding 2.3 — 30%+ Harassment Rate Compounds the ID Mismatch

The Williams Institute study (E1) documents not only the 210,800 ID-mismatch figure but a 30%+ reported harassment rate at polling places for trans voters with ID mismatch. This means the suppression mechanism operates through two channels: direct disenfranchisement (voters turned away for ID mismatch) and chilling effect (voters who do not attempt to vote because of anticipated harassment). The chilling effect is not captured in the ID-mismatch figure alone, meaning the suppression impact is larger than 210,800.

## Zone 3 — Constitutional Standards After Skrmetti

**Anchor sources read:** A4 (Skrmetti SCOTUS opinion), A5 (CRS analysis), A7 (State Court Report ripple analysis), I6 (Kalarchik v. State Montana), I7 (Little v. Hecox — now decided June 30, 2026).

### Finding 3.1 — Skrmetti Set Rational Basis, Little v. Hecox Extended It — State Courts Are the Alternative

Skrmetti (A4) applied rational basis to the TN trans healthcare ban for minors. Little v. Hecox and West Virginia v. BPJ (decided June 30, 2026, 6-3 Kavanaugh majority) extended rational basis to trans athlete bans, finding neither Title IX nor the 14th Amendment's Equal Protection Clause was violated. The federal constitutional floor for trans rights challenges is now firmly rational basis under a 6-3 court. Lambda Legal's post-Skrmetti strategy (A7, A8) — "state courts, state constitutions" — has been vindicated by Kalarchik v. State (I6), in which the Montana Supreme Court applied heightened scrutiny under the state constitution to trans identity document policies. The Montana holding: "Transgender discrimination is, by its very nature, sex discrimination" and Montana's equal protection clause applies heightened scrutiny. This is proof-of-concept for the state court alternative.

**Zone 3 strategic implication**: The federal constitutional analysis is fixed. State court strategy is the live litigation alternative. Kalarchik is the proof-of-concept. The Lambda Legal distribution priority should center on the state court pathway, not rehashing the federal floor.

### Finding 3.2 — Talbott's "Bare Desire to Harm" Language Is the Bridge Between Zones 1 and 3

The Talbott v. USA D.C. Circuit ruling (June 1, 2026, M2) held that the trans military ban "appears to be driven by the bare desire to harm a politically unpopular group" — applying Romer v. Evans constitutional logic to an executive action. This ruling is the constitutional bridge between Zone 1 (ballot initiatives as suppression mechanisms, using Romer) and Zone 3 (constitutional standards, using Skrmetti). Talbott shows that courts are willing to apply the Romer anti-countermajoritarian principle to executive actions, not just ballot initiatives, in 2026. This is significant: it means that even under a rational basis federal floor, the "bare desire to harm" standard remains available as a constitutional argument where discriminatory intent can be shown.

### Finding 3.3 — Dave Yost Hire Completes the ADF Institutional Integration Argument

The Dave Yost hire (D4) — Ohio AG resigned effective June 7, 2026 to join ADF as VP of Strategic Research — is not only a Zone 1 (ADF infrastructure) finding; it is a Zone 3 finding about judicial pipeline. An AG who would have appointed judges, defended state laws before courts, and filed amicus briefs in LGBTQ+ cases now occupies a strategic research role at ADF. The institutional integration of state executive branch and ADF's litigation infrastructure is documented at the highest level. This is the evidence that the Federalist Society judicial pipeline extends to state AG offices as recruitment grounds for ADF strategic capacity.
