## How to Read This Document

This roadmap is structured as a decision tree, not a sequential plan. The orchestrator does not commit to the full Path B timeline at the start. Instead, the process has a mandatory Day 7 checkpoint — after completing the two highest-impact updates (Domain 1 + Domain 33) — at which the orchestrator re-evaluates whether those findings justify a pivot to Path A or Path A+37 rather than continuing the full 2–4 week Path B schedule.

Three decision states are possible at Day 7:

- **Continue Path B**: Day 1–7 findings confirm that new data deepens but does not fundamentally alter the framework. Complete remaining updates (Days 8–21) before distribution.
- **Pivot to Path A+37**: Day 1–7 findings reveal a new structural pattern — a 37th domain, a cross-domain synthesis, or an empirical gap — significant enough that distribution with updated materials is preferable to further delay.
- **Force distribution (Path A)**: Day 7 checkpoint confirms the framework is already current and additional updates would produce diminishing returns. Distribute immediately with an explicit "Monitoring Note" flagging pending developments.

## Section 1: Path B Rationale — When the Delay Is Worth It

The 35-domain (now 38-domain) diagnostic framework is production-ready as of April 28, 2026. Distribution can happen today. The question is whether 2–4 weeks of targeted updates would materially strengthen the framework's accuracy and impact, or whether the marginal research value is outweighed by the cost of delay.

### The Case for Immediate Distribution (Path A)

Distribution delay has real costs. Every week the framework sits unreleased is a week that civil society organizations, legal advocates, journalists, and civic educators are working without the synthesis it provides. The 2026 midterms create an intensifying advocacy window: Senate redistricting litigation timelines, state supreme court elections in Wisconsin and Pennsylvania (which will determine redistricting and voting rights enforcement for the remainder of the decade), and the June 2026 *Trump v. Slaughter* ruling all have advocacy lead times measured in weeks, not months. Delay also risks that specific April 2026 factual claims — particularly around FISA 702 and Iran-constraint follow-up — will appear stale before distribution.

### The Case for Path B Delay

Three developments are in active motion as of April 29, 2026 that could materially alter the framework's analytical conclusions, not just its factual detail:

**First**: The FISA 702 outcome (April 30 deadline) directly determines the threat landscape in Domains 21 and 25. A clean lapse followed by NSA shift to EO 12333 collection produces a fundamentally different analysis than a three-year reauthorization without warrant requirements. These are not equivalent outcomes with the same advocacy implications — they require different recommended responses. Waiting 48–72 hours for this outcome costs almost nothing and prevents distributing an analysis that could be factually obsolete within days.

**Second**: The Domain 33 ballot initiative suppression bills — 100+ bills in 15+ states as of the opening weeks of 2026 sessions — are still being enacted. Several of the six-state supermajority threshold measures (Oklahoma, Arizona, Missouri, North Dakota, South Dakota, Idaho) will be voted on by state legislatures in May–June 2026, some before the November 2026 ballot. A 3–5 day update pass that documents which bills have advanced, which have failed, and what the as-of-June ballot will contain materially improves the framework's actionability for advocacy organizations targeting those races.

**Third**: *Trump v. Slaughter* (oral arguments December 2025; decision expected June 2026) will either overrule or substantially limit *Humphrey's Executor*, collapsing protection for all independent agencies — or will produce a narrower holding with a Federal Reserve carve-out of uncertain scope. The decision's trajectory is visible in oral argument reporting: the conservative majority appeared to favor the administration's position. A holding that eliminates good-cause removal protection for NLRB, FTC, CFPB, and FEC leadership — without protecting the Fed — would require rewriting the reform architecture in Domain 6, Domain 35, and the executive-power sections of the democratic renewal proposal. This ruling arrives in June 2026, potentially within the Path B window.

### The Calibrated Verdict

Path B is worth the delay under two conditions: (a) the FISA outcome is confirmed within Days 1–3, and (b) the Domain 1 + Domain 33 update produces substantive new findings rather than confirmatory updates. If both conditions are met, the 2–4 week timeline produces a materially stronger framework. If the Day 7 checkpoint reveals only confirmatory updates, the orchestrator should force distribution with a monitoring note. The roadmap below is designed to front-load the highest-impact work in Days 1–7 and give the orchestrator a genuine decision point before committing to the full schedule.

### Priority 1: Domain 21 (FISA 702) — Determination Checkpoint

**Rank**: First, because it is contingent and fast. This is not a research-intensive update; it is a single determination that cascades into Domains 21 and 25.

**What is pending**: The April 30, 2026 FISA 702 expiration deadline. As of April 29, the House Rules Committee has recessed without advancing the Foreign Intelligence Accountability Act (Johnson's three-year, no-warrant-requirement proposal). A Rules Committee collapse, a bipartisan civil liberties bloc holding, Trump's SAVE Act condition, and 12+ Republican defectors on Fourth Amendment grounds collectively make a brief lapse, a further emergency stopgap, or a narrowed last-minute deal approximately equally probable. The FISC has issued a classified continuity order, meaning a statutory lapse would not immediately halt collection — but technology companies compelled to assist could challenge their legal obligation, and NSA would shift toward EO 12333 for new collection.

**The determination logic**:

- If Section 702 **lapses and NSA shifts to EO 12333**: This is the civil liberties community's worst-case outcome. EO 12333 collection has no congressional authorization, no FISC oversight, and no statutory sunset — making it structurally harder to challenge or reform than a lapsed statute. Update Domain 21 to document the shift and reframe Domain 25's threat landscape around the EO 12333 architecture. Flag in CHECKIN.md as a new critical finding requiring cross-domain edits.

- If Section 702 is **renewed without warrant requirement or data broker closure** (the most likely outcome if passage occurs): Update Domain 21's current-status section to reflect the new expiration date and document what the bill did and did not include. The commercial data broker loophole — Venntel, Babel Street, Accurint — remains open. This is the expected outcome; the analytical conclusions of Domain 21 are substantially confirmed, and the update is one day of work.

- If Section 702 is **renewed with a warrant requirement**: This would be the civil liberties community's best-case outcome and would represent a meaningful constraint on the surveillance architecture documented in Domain 25. Update Domain 21 to document the win and analyze what the warrant requirement actually covers (front-end targeting vs. back-end U.S. person queries — the distinction the civil liberties coalition has consistently drawn). This scenario appears unlikely based on available reporting.

- If **further emergency stopgap is passed**: Fill the checklist in surveillance-tracking.md with the new deadline. No analytical change to Domain 21 is warranted until the final outcome is known. Note the new deadline in CHECKIN.md and schedule a 30-minute determination check on that deadline.

**Effort**: 1–2 days, contingent on outcome. If lapse occurs, 2–3 days for cross-domain edits.

**Timing**: Day 1–2 of Path B (outcome expected April 30 or within days of further extension expiration).

**Deliverable**: Updated Domain 21 current-status section; surveillance-tracking.md post-deadline checklist filled; cross-domain implications confirmed in Domains 25 and 1.

## Section 4: Distribution Holdoff Timeline — How Long to Wait and When to Force

### Maximum holdoff: 21 days from today (by May 20, 2026)

Beyond May 20, incremental research value is outweighed by the cost of delay. The June 2026 state supreme court election primaries in Wisconsin and Pennsylvania — which determine redistricting enforcement capacity for the decade — begin their key advocacy window in late May. - *Trump v. Slaughter* is decided before the Path B window closes. A Supreme Court decision overruling *Humphrey's Executor* is a Category 1 event. The democratic renewal framework needs to be in circulation with its analysis of this development, not held in draft. Integrate the decision in 24 hours and distribute immediately.

- A state supreme court ruling — particularly in Wisconsin or North Carolina — produces a redistricting outcome that either validates or undermines the Domain 33 analysis. Distribute with the ruling integrated.

### Soft trigger: Accelerate distribution (skip remaining optional updates) if

- The Domain 1 + Domain 33 update at Day 7 confirms the existing analysis without revealing new structural patterns. This means Path B's core value has been captured in Days 1–7 and the remaining optional updates add marginal value relative to continued delay.

## Section 5: Day 7 Checkpoint — The Decision Tree

**Checkpoint date**: May 6, 2026 (Day 7 of Path B).

**What the orchestrator reviews**:

1. **Domain 21 FISA determination**: Is the post-April 30 outcome confirmed? What scenario occurred (lapse, renewal without warrant, renewal with warrant, further extension)?

2. **Domain 1 findings**: Has the voting restriction bill count changed materially from the March 2026 baseline? Have new state laws been enacted that reveal a pattern not in the current domain? Is the SAVE Act state-track divergence documented with a current count?

3. **Domain 33 findings**: Which of the 100+ bills from January 2026 sessions have been enacted? Has the six-state supermajority push produced enacted laws? Has Missouri's geographic distribution requirement been placed on the November 2026 ballot?

4. **Cross-domain pattern check**: Do the Domain 1 and Domain 33 findings, in combination, reveal a structural pattern not currently in the framework? Specifically: is there evidence of federal-state coordination on voting restriction that is analytically distinct from the parallel-track analysis already in Domain 33 Section 1.3?

**Decision logic**:

```
IF (new structural pattern found in Domain 1 + Domain 33 update)
  AND (pattern fits an existing scoped domain, e.g., Domain 37a, 37b, or 31x)
  → Pivot to Path A+37: complete the relevant new domain, then distribute
  
ELSE IF (new structural pattern found)
  AND (pattern does not fit an existing scoped domain)
  → Scope a Domain 38 candidate. Write a scope document (not full research).
     Day 7+1: Decide whether Domain 38 scope justifies 1–2 additional sessions before distribution.

ELSE IF (no new structural pattern, confirmatory findings only)
  AND (Domains 1, 21, 25 updates complete)
  → Force distribution: Path A, with updated Domain 1, 21, 25 sections and monitoring note.

ELSE IF (FISA lapsed and NSA shifted to EO 12333)
  → Treat as new structural finding. Domain 25 update required before distribution.
     Add 3 days to Path B timeline. New distribution date: May 9–10.
```

**What "new structural pattern" means**: A finding that changes the framework's analytical conclusions (not just its factual detail). Examples of qualifying new patterns:

- Evidence that federal funding conditions are being used to enforce state-level ballot initiative suppression (not currently documented — would be a Domain 9/33 cross-domain finding)
- Evidence that the state-level proof-of-citizenship laws are being coordinated through a federal DOJ/DHS guidance rather than state legislative action independently (would qualify as a Domain 29/33 intersection finding)
- Evidence that the commercial data broker surveillance pathway is now being used to monitor ballot initiative signature gatherers or voting rights organizers (would be a Domain 21/25/33 convergence finding with significant advocacy implications)
