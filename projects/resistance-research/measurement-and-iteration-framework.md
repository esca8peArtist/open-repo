# Post-Distribution Measurement and Iteration Framework

**April 27, 2026**

This framework governs how Phase 1 distribution of the 35-domain Democratic Renewal Proposal gets measured, evaluated, and iterated. It covers success metrics per influencer tier, feedback-loop mechanisms, network amplification tracking, monthly iteration cycles for Phase 2 domain research, and an institutional adoption playbook.

The core claim this framework is built to test: depth of institutional adoption is more valuable than breadth of casual reach. A single Senate committee staff director who integrates the proposal into a hearing is worth more than 50,000 social media impressions. Every metric here is oriented toward proving or disproving that claim and adjusting tactics accordingly.

---

## Part I: Success Metrics by Tier and Domain

### Measurement Philosophy

The evidence base for measuring policy framework adoption is sparse by design — organizations rarely publish their uptake data. The Overton Policy Index (which tracks citations of research in policy documents across 30,000+ government, think tank, and IGO sources) is the closest to an objective external measure, but operates on 6–18 month lag times. The Center for Progressive Reform's Project 2025 tracker demonstrates the cleaner model: a binary adoption scorecard (initiated / completed / no action) across a defined universe of recommendations, which allows tracking aggregate implementation rate over time.

This framework borrows from both models: near-term signals are behavioral (who responds, who cites, who requests more), medium-term signals are institutional (organizational adoption, legislative uptake), and long-term signals are outcome-based (reforms introduced, litigation shaped, elections affected).

## Part II: Feedback-Loop Mechanisms

### The Core Problem with One-Way Distribution

Policy frameworks distributed without feedback loops degrade quickly. The document gets shared, receives some reads, and then becomes static — the research it contains ages, the political situation it addresses evolves, and the proposal's relevance declines without renewal. The Project 2025 model avoided this by creating a living tracker — a continuously updated public record of what has been implemented — which kept the framework in active public discourse for years after initial publication.

The Democratic Renewal Proposal requires a structurally similar feedback system: mechanisms for receiving information about how the proposal is being used, what's working, what's missing, and how to update it. The following mechanisms are organized by feedback type.

---

### Mechanism 1: Direct Feedback Intake

**Structure**: A lightweight intake protocol for recording responses to outreach and spontaneous inbound contact.

**Operational format**:
- Contact log entry for every substantive response (name, organization, date, nature of feedback, follow-up required)
- Categorize feedback by type: request for materials, substantive critique, adoption signal, suggested domain addition, referral to another contact
- Review contact log at the end of every two-week outreach cycle to identify patterns

**What to capture**:
- Which domains are generating the most pull (requests, questions, follow-up)
- Which domains are generating skepticism or pushback (the pushback is often more valuable than the enthusiasm — it identifies the weak points in the evidence or framing)
- Which contacts are referring the proposal to other contacts unprompted (these are the bridge nodes in practice, regardless of whether the influencer map predicted them)

**Iteration trigger**: If two or more Tier 1 contacts express the same objection to a domain's evidence or framing within a 30-day window, that domain enters the revision queue for the next monthly iteration cycle.

---

### Mechanism 2: Passive Signal Monitoring

Passive monitoring captures engagement signals that don't require an explicit contact response.

**Monitoring stack**:

| Signal Type | Source | Cadence | Threshold for Action |
|-------------|--------|---------|---------------------|
| Google Scholar citations | Google Scholar alerts (proposal title, domain titles) | Weekly | Any citation triggers contact log entry |
| Overton Index | Overton.io policy discovery alerts | Monthly | Any policy document citation triggers review |
| News mentions | Google News alerts (proposal title + "democratic renewal") | Daily | Any coverage triggers media log entry |
| Social media amplification | Bluesky/Twitter keyword search | Weekly | Repost from named Tier 1/2 contact triggers log |
| Academic blog citations | RSS feeds for Balkinization, Just Security, Lawfare | Daily | Any mention triggers log entry |

**Limitation to acknowledge**: Passive monitoring captures only what generates a public footprint. Coalition staff who read the proposal and use it internally — in memos, strategy documents, briefing notes — will not appear in any passive signal. The most operationally important adoptions (Hill staff incorporating domain language into legislation) are precisely the ones that are invisible to passive monitoring. This is why active contact intelligence remains primary.

---

### Mechanism 3: Structured Periodic Check-ins

Active outreach at defined intervals with a specific purpose: learning, not reselling.

**Format**: Email or brief call to contacts who have already received the proposal. Not a follow-up asking whether they've read it. A specific, substantive prompt: "The Domain 29 section was written in early April — since then, the Nashville vindictive prosecution ruling has come down. Does that change how you'd use this section?" Or: "We've had interest from labor organizations in the Domain 17 material. Does that match what you're seeing in your network?"

**Cadence**: 
- 30 days post-outreach: First check-in with Tier 1 contacts who haven't responded to initial outreach
- 60 days post-outreach: Check-in with all Tier 1 and responsive Tier 2 contacts
- 90 days: Full cycle review with the five highest-leverage contacts

**What this accomplishes**: It keeps the relationship active, signals that this is a living document not a one-time broadcast, and surfaces information about how the proposal is actually being used that would otherwise remain invisible.

---

### Mechanism 4: Coalition Feedback Input Protocol

For Tier 3 organizations that have explicitly adopted the proposal or expressed intent to use it:

**Protocol**:
1. When a Tier 3 organization confirms adoption, designate a point of contact within that organization
2. Quarterly: send a one-page summary of major proposal updates (litigation developments, domain revisions, new domains) directly to that contact
3. Annually: request a brief response to two questions: (a) what from the proposal did you actually use this year, and (b) what was missing that you needed

**What to do with the responses**: Responses from Tier 3 organizations represent ground-level use data — what practitioners actually needed, not what researchers thought they needed. This feedback is the most valuable input for Phase 2 domain research priorities. A Tier 3 coalition reporting that the Domain 11 (healthcare) section was too abstract for member communications is a direct instruction to add more county-level and state-level implementation data to the domain. Document all responses in the monthly iteration cycle log.

## Part III: Network Amplification Tracking

### What Network Amplification Means Here

Network amplification in a policy context is different from social media virality. The goal is not for the proposal to be shared widely and superficially — it is for the proposal to move through institutional networks with increasing credibility at each step. The relevant cascade model is not "one tweet seen by 10,000 people" but "one Brennan Center brief read by 100 Hill staffers who brief 435 members of Congress."

The policy-relevant cascade structure has three components:

1. **First-order amplification**: The initial contact receives and engages with the proposal
2. **Second-order amplification**: The initial contact uses the proposal in their own work, which is distributed to their network
3. **Third-order amplification**: The second-order work generates tertiary citations, adoptions, or use by actors who never received the proposal directly

Tracking systems that only capture first-order amplification (responses to outreach) miss the most consequential effects. The framework here prioritizes identifying and logging second- and third-order signals.

---

The influencer map identifies seven bridge nodes — contacts whose network position causes the proposal to cross into otherwise-disconnected communities. Track each bridge node separately because cascade failure at a single node is disproportionately costly.

  - Reach estimate:

  - Reason (if known):

Track: justsecurity.org daily. Monitor: RSS feed. This is the fastest cascade loop in the map — publication timeline is 48–96 hours.

4. **Heather Cox Richardson (Substack)**: 2.9 million subscriber trigger. ### Cascade Mapping Methodology

**Citation tracking**: For every second-order amplification event (a contact uses the proposal in their own published work), track all subsequent documents that cite that second-order work. This is cascade mapping: A cites the proposal; B cites A's piece; C cites B. The depth of the citation chain is a proxy for how deeply the proposal has penetrated a particular professional community.

**Practical tool**: Google Scholar alerts set for the proposal title and for each major domain title. When a new citation appears, log it and check for that document's own citation trail. The Clarivate Policy Citation Index and Overton are more comprehensive for policy document citations but require institutional access.

**Network visualization (optional, quarterly)**: If five or more second-order citations exist, map the citation network visually — nodes are documents, edges are citation relationships. The structure of the map indicates whether the proposal is penetrating a single community (star pattern — all citations from one outlet) or multiple communities (distributed pattern — citations across think tanks, academia, media, and advocacy).

## Part IV: Monthly Iteration Cycles for Phase 2 Domain Research

### The Iteration Logic

Phase 2 domain research (Domains 31x, 37a, 37b, and the four Phase 2 candidates) should not proceed on a fixed internal schedule. It should respond to what the distribution feedback is revealing about demand. A Tier 1 contact who expresses strong interest in healthcare tariff collision (Domain 31x) and asks for more state-level implementation data is a signal that this domain has reached institutional readiness — the research investment will be absorbed and used. A domain with no Tier 1 or Tier 2 engagement after 60 days should be deprioritized in Phase 2 regardless of its substantive importance.

This section establishes the monthly cycle for translating distribution feedback into Phase 2 research decisions.

---

### Monthly Iteration Calendar

**Week 1 (first 7 days of each month)**:
- Complete passive signal review: news alerts, Scholar alerts, Overton alerts
- Review contact log from prior month: categorize responses by tier and domain
- Complete bridge node status check: second-order amplification events in prior 30 days
- Complete coalition check-ins with Tier 3 contacts who have confirmed adoption

**Week 2 (days 8–14)**:
- Synthesis: write a one-page internal distribution memo answering three questions:
  1. Which domains are generating the most institutional pull?
  2. Which domains are generating substantive pushback or gap identification?
  3. Which bridge nodes have activated, and what cascades are in motion?
- Identify any domains that have crossed the "two Tier 1 contacts same objection" threshold requiring revision

**Week 3 (days 15–21)**:
- Execute revisions to flagged domains (targeted updates based on feedback, not rewrites)
- If Phase 2 demand signals are clear: initiate scoped research for the highest-demand domain
- Update PROJECTS.md exploration queue to reflect Phase 2 prioritization changes

**Week 4 (days 22–31)**:
- Dispatch updated or new domain materials to contacts who flagged interest
- Begin next month's outreach cycle with any new Tier 2 or Tier 3 contacts identified during the prior month
- Update DISTRIBUTION_OUTREACH_CONTACTS.md with any new contacts identified through cascade tracking

### Messaging Iteration Protocol

Distribution feedback will surface messaging problems — framings that don't resonate, evidence claims that get challenged, domain descriptions that generate confusion rather than engagement. This section governs how messaging gets updated based on feedback.

**Types of messaging problems and responses**:

| Problem type | Example | Response |
|-------------|---------|----------|
| Framing mismatch | Tier 1 legislative staff: "this sounds like advocacy, not analysis" | Revise domain introduction to foreground diagnostic framing over prescriptive framing |
| Evidence challenge | Tier 2 academic: "the 3.5% figure overstates Chenoweth's claim" | Review Chenoweth's exact language; update claim to match what the research actually says |
| Scope objection | Tier 3 union: "Domain 17 doesn't address the gig economy at all" | Add gig economy subsection to Domain 17 in next iteration |
| Relevance gap | Tier 3 coalition: "this doesn't speak to our members' current situation" | Add a "what this means now" section tied to current events for the relevant domain |
| Credibility gap | Tier 2 journalist: "who is behind this research?" | Strengthen attribution, citation standards, and "about this document" section |

**Iteration rule**: No messaging revision should be made based on a single contact's feedback without corroboration. The threshold is two independent contacts expressing the same concern within 30 days, or one Tier 1 contact expressing a concern that reflects a verifiable error in the evidence. Revisions to correct factual errors are immediate; revisions to change framing require the two-contact threshold.

## Part V: Institutional Adoption Playbook

### What Institutional Adoption Means

Institutional adoption is the highest-value outcome this distribution can achieve. It means a policy organization has moved from "received the proposal" to "is using the proposal as part of its work." This is distinct from citation (which is intellectual engagement) and from endorsement (which is political alignment). Adoption means operational integration — the proposal's frameworks, evidence, or domain analysis is shaping what the organization does.

Four levels of institutional adoption, in ascending order of value:

**Level 1 — Reference adoption**: The organization cites the proposal in a publication, brief, or public communication. The proposal is a named source.

**Level 2 — Framework adoption**: The organization uses the proposal's diagnostic framework — the 35-domain structure, the wave logic, or the international evidence base — to organize its own work. The framework shapes how they think, not just what they cite.

**Level 3 — Operational adoption**: The organization actively incorporates specific domain analysis into its legislative strategy, litigation planning, or member communications. A union's legislative director uses Domain 17 data in testimony. A legal clinic uses the Domain 6 analysis to build arguments in a judicial independence case.

**Level 4 — Coalition adoption**: The organization introduces the proposal to other organizations in its network and facilitates their adoption. The proposal begins to function as shared infrastructure across a coalition rather than a tool belonging to a single organization.

The goal of Phase 1 distribution is to achieve Level 1 or Level 2 adoption with five or more Tier 1 and Tier 2 contacts, and Level 3 adoption with two or more Tier 1 contacts. Coalition adoption (Level 4) is a Phase 2 and Phase 3 goal.

---

### Step-by-Step Adoption Facilitation for Policy Organizations

When a Tier 1 or Tier 2 organization expresses interest in adoption, use the following facilitation sequence. The sequence is designed to reduce friction at each step, because institutional adoption consistently fails not at the decision point but at the implementation stage — organizations say yes to using a framework and then can't figure out how to integrate it into their actual workflow.

**Step 1 — Domain triage (days 1–7 post-confirmation)**:
Work with the organization's point of contact to identify which one to three domains are most immediately relevant to their current work. Do not ask them to engage with 35 domains. The correct question is: "What are you working on right now where you need better evidence or a more complete analytical framework?" Map their answer to the most relevant domain(s).

**Step 2 — Tailored brief delivery (days 7–14)**:
Prepare a domain-specific brief (2–4 pages) extracting the most relevant analysis from the identified domain(s) and connecting it explicitly to the organization's stated current priority. This is not the domain file itself — it is a bridge document that translates the domain analysis into the organization's working language and immediate context.

**Step 3 — Integration consultation (days 14–21)**:
One-hour conversation (call or meeting) with the organization's relevant staff — not leadership, but the policy director or legislative director who will actually use the material. Three agenda items: (a) what does the evidence change about how they're framing the issue; (b) what is missing that would make the domain analysis more useful; (c) what is the specific deliverable (testimony, brief, campaign material) where this will be used first.

**Step 4 — First-use support (days 21–45)**:
If the organization's first use is a specific deliverable (a hearing testimony, a coalition brief, a public report), offer to review the draft sections that incorporate the proposal's analysis for accuracy. This is not ghostwriting — it is quality control for the evidence claims. Catching errors in how the proposal is used maintains the evidentiary standard across all downstream citations.

**Step 5 — Adoption confirmation and coalition referral (days 45–60)**:
Once the first deliverable is published, log it as a confirmed adoption event (Level 3 or Level 4 depending on whether the organization has referred the proposal to other organizations). Ask the point of contact whether they are aware of other organizations doing similar work that would benefit from the proposal. This is the Coalition Level activation step.

---

### Domain-Specific Adoption Pathways by Sector

**Legislative sector** (Senate/House staff, state legislatures):
The natural adoption vehicle is a domain brief formatted as a "legislative background paper" — one page of context, one page of evidence from comparable jurisdictions, one page of existing legislation and reform pathways. Congressional staff do not read 200-page documents; they need pre-digested versions that can be cited in a hearing memo. Prepare domain-specific one-pagers for the five domains with highest Tier 1 legislative relevance (Domains 1, 6, 17, 28, 29) before initial outreach.

**Legal sector** (law school clinics, civil rights law firms, legal aid):
The natural adoption vehicle is a "comparative law brief" format — a systematic comparison of U.S. constitutional doctrine against international precedents that can be cited in legal argument. Clinics especially will use this format because comparative constitutional law is an accepted evidentiary standard in certain constitutional arguments. Domain 6 (judicial independence) and Domain 7 (rights protection) have the most direct clinic application. Prepare a "comparative law brief" format version of these two domains.

**Academic sector** (political science, law school faculty, policy schools):
The natural adoption vehicle is a "research agenda note" — a 3–5 page document identifying the empirical gaps in each domain that a graduate student or junior faculty member could fill with original research. Academics adopt frameworks when they can build original research on them, not when they merely cite them. Prepare research agenda notes for Domains 27, 28, and the AI governance domain candidate (C), which have the most obvious academic research gaps.

**Union and labor sector**:
The natural adoption vehicle is a "fiscal impact summary" — a one-page document for each labor-relevant domain showing the dollar figures: what the current dysfunction costs workers, what the reform would save or generate, with comparable international data. EPI has already developed this format for its own issue briefs. Domain 17 fiscal summary: German co-determination model produces 15–20% wage premium above comparable non-co-determination firms (IMF, 2023). Domain 5 (fiscal reform) fiscal summary: carried interest loophole costs approximately $14 billion annually. Prepare the fiscal summary format for Domains 5, 11, 17, and 18.

**Civil rights and advocacy coalition sector**:
The natural adoption vehicle is a "current threat" brief — a domain analysis connected directly to a named current case, legislative vehicle, or community impact. The SPLC indictment as the entry point for Domain 29 is the model. Connecting Domain 16 (immigration) to current Abrego Garcia and CBP One litigation is the approach. Each civil rights coalition needs to see the proposal through the lens of their current fight, not a five-year reform agenda. Prepare "current threat" briefs for Domains 16, 22, and 29.

### Adoption Playbook: What Can Go Wrong and How to Respond

**Failure mode 1 — "Too long, can't integrate"**:
Symptom: Organization confirms interest but never produces a deliverable using the proposal.
Response: Reduce the ask. Identify the single smallest usable unit — one paragraph from one domain that directly applies to one sentence of their current work. Get that placed first, then build.

**Failure mode 2 — "We can't endorse a specific policy agenda"**:
Symptom: Nonpartisan organization declines adoption because they perceive the proposal as advocacy.
Response: Offer only the diagnostic sections — the crisis analysis and the evidence base — rather than the prescriptive reform proposals. The Domain 29 evidence on prosecutorial weaponization is factual documentation; the reform proposals at the end of the domain are advocacy. These are separable.

**Failure mode 3 — "Our members don't recognize this language"**:
Symptom: Tier 3 organization says the analysis is credible but member communications staff can't use it.
Response: Offer to co-develop a translated brief — a member-facing version of the relevant domain section in plain language with concrete local impact examples. This is additional labor but is the necessary step for genuine Tier 3 operational adoption.

**Failure mode 4 — "We're already doing this"**:
Symptom: Organization says the proposal duplicates their existing work.
Response: This is not a failure — it is a discovery that the organization is already performing the function this domain addresses. Log it as a confirmation signal (the domain's analysis is correct), ask whether their existing work could be integrated into the proposal as an additional source, and explore whether the proposal's comparative international evidence is a gap-fill for their current work.

**Failure mode 5 — "We don't have capacity right now"**:
Symptom: Organization says yes in principle but can't engage during the current period.
Response: Schedule a 90-day follow-up. Identify the specific triggering event that would make the proposal timely for them (a legislation vote, a court ruling, a report release) and offer to reach out at that time with a specific framing. Convert the "not now" into a conditional commitment.

### Integration with Existing Monitoring Infrastructure

The existing tracking-template.json and monitoring-infrastructure-2026.md cover domain research monitoring. This framework introduces a parallel distribution monitoring system. **Data flow**:
1. Tier 3 contact reports a development relevant to a specific domain (e.g., a union's legislative director says "the NLRB quorum has functionally collapsed as of April 2026")
2. That intelligence goes into the `coalition_notes` field for Domain 15 in tracking-template.json

4. The monthly iteration cycle reviews coalition notes alongside passive signal monitoring

This prevents the distribution tracking and the domain monitoring from becoming separate siloed systems. The domain research feeds the distribution; the distribution feedback feeds the domain research.

## Part VII: Decision Rules and Red Lines

### When Distribution Feedback Changes Research Priorities

Phase 2 domain research priorities should update when distribution feedback meets one of the following thresholds:

- A Tier 1 contact explicitly identifies a gap in the proposal that they would use if filled (immediate priority upgrade)
- Two or more Tier 2 contacts identify the same domain as inadequately evidenced (revision priority, not Phase 2 addition)
- A Tier 3 coalition is engaged in active organizing on an issue with no corresponding domain in the proposal (Phase 2 addition candidate)
- An advocacy window for a scoped Phase 2 domain is within 60 days (urgency override)

### When to Stop Iterating

Not every piece of feedback warrants a response. Three red lines:

1. **Do not chase fringe objections**: A single contact's objection to a domain's conclusions does not warrant revision if the conclusion is well-evidenced. The evidence standard governs content; popularity does not.

2. **Do not fragment the document to satisfy specialized audiences**: If three organizations each want a different version of Domain 17, the correct response is domain-specific one-pagers, not three versions of the domain file. The base document maintains integrity; derived formats serve specialized audiences.

3. **Do not accelerate Phase 2 research at the expense of Phase 1 distribution**: The 35-domain document is production-ready. Researching new domains while Phase 1 distribution is incomplete is a common strategic error in policy work — organizations build more research than they can absorb. Phase 2 research is demand-driven. If Phase 1 distribution generates no institutional demand for Domain B (healthcare), then Domain B research is premature.
