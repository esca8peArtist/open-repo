# Crisis Response Guide: State Legislatures and Congressional Staff

**Audience**: State legislative staff, committee counsel, Democratic caucus policy directors, congressional office legislative directors, minority staff on Judiciary / Administration / Oversight committees  
**Parent document**: crisis-response-playbooks.md  
**Framework reference**: 35-Domain Diagnostic Framework — Domain 1 (Voting Rights), Domain 9 (Federalism), Domain 34 (Congressional Power of the Purse), Domain 6 (Judicial Independence)  
**Session**: 549 | Date: April 27, 2026

---

## The Legislative Institution's Role in Each Crisis

Legislatures are not the first responder to most democratic crises — courts and executive agencies typically move faster. The legislature's distinctive role is: (1) creating the political cost of executive action; (2) building the statutory record that courts use to assess whether executive authority exists; (3) passing state-level legislation that substitutes when federal protections are stripped; (4) generating the oversight record that the next Congress will need to conduct accountability proceedings.

All four functions are available regardless of majority status. Minority staff can convene unofficial hearings. Minority members can introduce bills that will not pass but establish the record. Individual members can make floor statements that create political cost. Understanding which function is available in which crisis, and executing it on the right timeline, is the core competency this guide addresses.

---

## Crisis 1: Federal Election Interference

### Crisis Scenario

A federal actor — DHS, DOJ, or the White House — takes action designed to restrict voting in the 2026 midterms. Most likely vehicles: ICE deployment near polling sites in jurisdictions with large immigrant communities; voter roll purge order citing a federal database cross-check; mail ballot rejection directive; or an EO purporting to federalize election security in a way that overrides state election authority.

The SAVE Act's legislative failure (48-50 in the April 2026 vote-a-rama, with Collins/Murkowski/Tillis/McConnell defecting — Domain 1, Section 2.1) closed the legislative pathway. The executive pathway remains open.

---

### 72-Hour Legislative Response

**State legislatures (majority status required for at least one chamber):**

Within 24 hours of a confirmed federal election interference action, the chamber with Democratic leadership should convene an emergency session or floor period to pass a resolution formally asserting state sovereignty over election administration under Article I, Section 4. This resolution has no legal effect on its own — but it creates a political record that the state legislature stands behind its election officials, it provides political cover to the Secretary of State who is resisting federal interference, and it generates media coverage that establishes the state-level frame.

The resolution's findings should cite: (1) Article I, Section 4's grant of state authority over the "Times, Places and Manner" of federal elections; (2) the specific federal action that violates this authority; (3) the harm to the state's eligible voters. The Brennan Center's Model Election Administration State Sovereignty Resolution (available through its state legislative outreach team) provides draft language that has been vetted for constitutional accuracy.

State legislative legal counsel should also contact the state AG's office within 24 hours to confirm that the AG's office is aware of the federal action and is assessing standing for a legal challenge. If the AG's office has not yet been contacted by Democracy Docket or ACLU, legislative counsel can make that connection.

**Congressional staff (minority status):**

Within 24 hours, Senate Judiciary Committee Democratic staff director should draft a letter to DOJ demanding the specific legal authority under which the federal election interference is being conducted. This letter goes to the Attorney General (or acting AG) and requests a response within 48 hours. It will not receive a substantive response, but it creates the paper record that the administration refused to identify its legal authority.

Within 48 hours, minority members should request floor time for a statement identifying the specific federal action, the constitutional provision it violates, and the specific voters being disenfranchised. The statement should name the states and counties affected, the estimated number of affected voters, and the projected electoral impact. Specific, quantified harm is harder to dismiss as partisan rhetoric.

The House Administration Committee has jurisdiction over federal election administration. Democratic minority staff should request an emergency hearing, understanding the request will likely be denied. The request itself is reportable — majority's refusal to hold hearings on documented election interference is itself news.

---

### 30-Day Legislative Response

**State level:**

Introduce legislation that hardcodes the specific protections being threatened. If mail voting is being targeted: a state statute explicitly authorizing mail voting with the specific grace period under threat, with findings that track the constitutional arguments in Democracy Docket's litigation. If polling site access is being interfered with: a state law creating a private right of action for voters who are turned away from lawful polling sites due to federal enforcement activity.

The companion guide for AGs (crisis-response-ag-coalition.md) documents the parallel legal strategy. Coordinate bill language with the AG's office so that the statutory findings support the litigation theory.

Identify two or three other states advancing the same protective legislation in the same session. Align bill language. Courts give more weight to consistent multi-state statutory language; aligned language makes future interstate compact formation easier. The Governors Safeguarding Democracy network (GSD), co-chaired by Pritzker and Polis, has coordination infrastructure for exactly this kind of parallel state action.

**Congressional level:**

Introduce the federal legislation that would prevent or reverse the specific interference mechanism. For mail ballot interference: the Mail Ballot Protection Act (pre-draft exists from the 2024 VRAA advocacy cycle). For ICE-at-polls: the Protecting Voters from Intimidation Act (already introduced in the 119th Congress). For voter roll purge orders: the Automatic Voter Registration Act combined with a prohibition on federal database-driven purges without judicial review.

None of these will pass the current Congress. Their purpose: (1) to identify specifically which Republican senators would need to defect to pass protections (forcing them to take a position); (2) to provide the pre-drafted bill text that a new Congress can pass quickly; (3) to make the legislative record that supports the AG coalition's litigation.

The budget reconciliation process creates specific moments when election-related legislation can be attached as riders. Identify the next reconciliation vehicle and draft specific, narrow riders that protect election administration. Even if stripped from the final bill, the attempt creates the record.

---

### 90-Day Legislative Response

The 90-day window, for a crisis that emerges in summer or fall 2026, overlaps with the midterm election itself. The legislative action that matters most is ensuring the election proceeds without further interference.

Coordinate with state election directors on two specific actions: (1) voter education campaigns that inform voters of their rights in the specific crisis scenario (e.g., ICE cannot legally prevent a person from voting; mail ballots with a postmark before Election Day count if state law authorizes the grace period); (2) deployment of legal observers at every polling site in jurisdictions affected by the interference. Legal observer programs are administered by ACLU state chapters and state bar associations' pro bono programs.

After the election: introduce comprehensive election protection legislation for the 2028 cycle. The crisis has created the political case and the evidentiary record. Do not wait for the next crisis — use this one.

---

### Success Metrics (Legislative)

- State sovereignty resolution passed in 24+ hours in states with Democratic legislative chamber
- Federal legislation introduced within 72 hours with cosponsors
- Congressional hearing request submitted (even if denied) within 48 hours
- State protective legislation introduced in 5+ states within 30 days
- DOJ authority letter sent and non-response documented within 72 hours

---

### Failure Modes (Legislative)

**Split state government prevents resolution**: If the Governor is Republican, a state legislature resolution may be vetoed — or the Republican governor may oppose state resistance entirely. In split-government states, focus on individual legislators making public statements rather than institutional action, and focus on local election officials (counties, municipalities) asserting their administrative authority under state law.

**Republican majority blocks congressional hearing**: Expected. The response is unofficial hearings — minority members can convene "forums" (not officially committee hearings) in the same committee room, take testimony, and produce a report. These unofficial hearings have generated significant press coverage in analogous situations (e.g., House Democrats' January 6 forums in 2021-2022).

## Crisis 2: Judicial Capture

### Crisis Scenario

The Supreme Court issues a ruling that strips a major institutional protection — most likely Trump v. Slaughter (Humphrey's Executor overruling, expected June 2026), Watson v. RNC (mail ballot grace periods), or Louisiana v. Callais (majority-minority districts). Or: the House initiates impeachment proceedings against one or more federal district judges who have issued rulings adverse to the administration.

---

### 72-Hour Legislative Response

**For an adverse SCOTUS ruling:**

Within 24 hours of the ruling, senior Democratic members in both chambers should make floor statements identifying: (1) the specific institutional protection that has been eliminated; (2) the real-world consequences for specific people (workers who can no longer bring NLRB claims, minority voters who may lose their district representation); (3) the specific senators whose confirmation votes created the 6-3 majority that produced this outcome.

Senate Judiciary Committee Democratic staff should convene an emergency background briefing for reporters within 24 hours, with constitutional law scholars who have published on the specific doctrine. The goal: establish that the ruling's legal analysis is contested, not settled — preventing the administration from claiming the ruling was universally expected or legally obvious.

Introduce within 48 hours: (1) the constitutional amendment that would restore the stripped protection; (2) the statutory alternative that creates the maximum legal protection available within the ruling's constraint. For Humphrey's Executor overruling: the amendment is an Article II independent agency tenure protection clause; the statutory alternative is a package of private rights of action amendments to every major regulatory statute. The companion guide for advocacy organizations (crisis-response-advocacy.md) covers the public campaign to support both bills.

**For judge impeachment initiation:**

Within 24 hours, senior Democratic members of the House Judiciary Committee should make floor statements identifying the constitutional standard for impeachment (high crimes and misdemeanors, not adverse rulings), with specific citations from Federalist 65 and 79, and the House precedents from all 15 federal judge impeachments in U.S. history (none were for correct legal rulings the executive branch disagreed with).

Contact Republican members of the Collins/Murkowski/Tillis institutionalist bloc within 24 hours. Their Senate counterparts can be enlisted to make public statements opposing impeachment on constitutional grounds before articles are transmitted to the Senate — locking in their votes with public statements that would cost credibility to reverse.

---

### 30-Day Legislative Response

**Constitutional amendment campaign:**

Within 30 days of a Humphrey's Executor overruling, introduce in both chambers: the Independent Agency Protection Amendment (Article II amendment establishing that Congress may, by statute, limit presidential removal of officers whose functions are quasi-judicial or quasi-legislative to "good cause" removal only). This amendment has a specific recent model: the German Basic Law's Article 97 (judicial independence protection) and the American Bar Association's proposed constitutional amendment text from its 2025 Special Committee on the Separation of Powers.

Identify Republican cosponsors. The Collins/Murkowski/Tillis bloc has demonstrated willingness to protect institutional forms. Murkowski's 2022 re-election through Alaska's ranked-choice system gives her genuine insulation from primary pressure. Tillis's explicit statement that he supports SAVE Act's policy but not filibuster elimination indicates a structural institutionalism that may extend to independent agency protection.

**State analog legislation:**

For every federal agency that has lost independence under the ruling, identify the state analog and introduce state legislation creating or strengthening it. Specific examples:

- NLRB independence lost: State labor relations boards with independent commissioner tenure protection (California, New York, Illinois all have existing state labor boards that can be strengthened).
- FTC independence lost: State consumer protection agencies with equivalent powers and independent leadership.
- FEC independence lost: State campaign finance agencies with strengthened enforcement authority.

The AG companion guide covers how these state analogs interact with the broader litigation strategy.

---

### 90-Day Legislative Response

The 90-day window for a June 2026 ruling arrives in September 2026 — directly in the midterm election cycle. Convert the ruling into a midterm contrast. The specific mechanism: identify the two or three competitive Senate seats where the incumbent voted to confirm the justices who produced the adverse ruling. Build a voter contact program that traces the ruling directly to that confirmation vote.

The companion guide for advocacy organizations covers the voter education and mobilization sequence. The legislative staff role: ensure the bill record is complete (amendment introduced, committee report requested, hearing record documented) so that the contrast campaign has specific legislative artifacts to point to.

---

### Success Metrics (Legislative)

- Floor statements within 24 hours of ruling with specific attribution chain
- Constitutional amendment introduced with bipartisan cosponsors within 48 hours
- State analog legislation introduced in 10+ states within 30 days
- Midterm contrast voter contact program launched in 5+ competitive Senate states within 60 days

## Crisis 3: Executive Overreach — Emergency EO

### Crisis Scenario

An emergency executive order is issued without congressional authorization, invoking the National Emergencies Act or statutory authority that does not actually exist. The most dangerous version for legislative institutions: an emergency declaration that purports to affect the 2026 election (postponement, restructuring, federalization of election security).

---

### 72-Hour Congressional Response

**NEA termination resolution (if NEA is invoked):**

The National Emergencies Act, 50 U.S.C. 1622, provides that Congress may terminate a declared national emergency by joint resolution. Under the post-*INS v. Chadha* interpretation, termination requires passage of a joint resolution through both chambers and presentation to the President (who will veto it), followed by a two-thirds override vote in both chambers.

The termination resolution should be introduced within 24 hours of an NEA declaration. It will not succeed in the current Congress (veto plus failed override is the expected outcome), but the floor vote forces every Republican senator and representative to go on record: do you support or oppose this emergency declaration? In competitive districts and states, that recorded vote has electoral consequences.

Senate procedure: the NEA termination resolution is privileged — it can be brought to the floor without a committee referral, and it cannot be filibustered under the NEA's specific provisions (50 U.S.C. 1622(c)). This is one of the few statutory carve-outs from the filibuster. The resolution requires only a simple majority in the Senate, meaning two defecting Republican senators (Collins and Murkowski are the most likely) could put it on the President's desk.

**Appropriations rider:**

Within 72 hours, Democratic appropriators should draft the specific rider language: "None of the funds appropriated by this Act may be used to implement, enforce, or carry out [specific EO sections]." Even in the minority, proposing specific rider language as an amendment to the next appropriations vehicle creates a recorded vote and a negotiating lever.

---

### 30-Day State Legislative Response

If the federal legislature cannot stop the EO, state legislatures become the primary institutional resistance. For an EO affecting elections: state legislatures in states with Democratic legislative chambers should pass statutes explicitly asserting state authority over election administration and creating state-law enforcement mechanisms against interference.

Invoke the anti-commandeering doctrine (Printz v. United States, New York v. United States): state governments cannot be compelled to administer federal programs. If the federal emergency declaration attempts to use state election infrastructure to implement a federal electoral objective, state legislatures can pass anti-commandeering statutes prohibiting state officials from cooperating with federal enforcement of the EO.

The strongest models: California's SB 54 (anti-commandeering for immigration enforcement) and Colorado's HB 1266 (limiting local law enforcement cooperation with federal immigration detainers). Adapt this model to the specific crisis scenario. State legislative counsel in the relevant states can produce a draft within 72 hours using the existing models.

---

### Success Metrics (Legislative)

- NEA termination resolution introduced within 24 hours (if NEA invoked)
- Floor vote on termination resolution within 72 hours (NEA makes this non-filibusterable)
- Appropriations rider language drafted within 72 hours
- State anti-commandeering legislation introduced in 5+ states within 30 days

## Crisis 4: Civil Service Targeting

### Crisis Scenario

Mass firing of career civil servants through Schedule F reclassification, DOGE-equivalent action, or direct agency orders. Or: constructive discharge through hostile workplace conditions designed to drive out career staff without formal firing.

---

### 72-Hour Congressional Response

**Oversight and documentation:**

The Government Accountability Office has authority to investigate whether agency personnel actions comply with the Civil Service Reform Act and applicable statutes. A GAO investigation request from the ranking minority member of the relevant committee can be filed within 24 hours of a mass firing announcement. GAO investigations are independent of committee majority — the minority can initiate them. GAO's response timeline (typically 90-180 days) does not help in the immediate crisis, but the investigation creates a permanent factual record that constrains future narrative.

Inspector General offices retain some independence and have authority to investigate whether firings comply with law. The relevant IG for a mass firing at DOGE-targeted agencies: Office of Personnel Management IG, agency-specific IGs (EPA IG, USDA IG, etc.), and the Council of Inspectors General on Integrity and Efficiency (CIGIE). Contact the relevant IG within 48 hours with a specific inquiry about the legal basis for the firings and the number of affected employees.

**Individual member action:**

Members of Congress can legally communicate with agency employees in their districts. Constituent services offices that maintain contact with federal employees in their districts should activate those relationships — federal employees who have been fired or who are under pressure can be connected with legal resources through the congressional office as an intermediary. This is legally protected constituent services work, not obstruction.

---

### 30-Day State Legislative Response

States with significant federal workforce populations (Virginia, Maryland, California, Texas, Colorado, Washington) should hold hearings on the economic and functional impact of federal workforce reductions. The hearing record creates political cost, supplements the federal oversight record, and identifies which state services are being degraded by the loss of federal capacity.

Introduce state legislation creating state-funded equivalents for the most critical functions being lost. This is the parallel institutions strategy — not replacing federal agencies, but creating state capacity that partially fills the gap. Domain 26 (Government Accountability and Institutional Resilience) documents the functions most critical to preserve. Priority: functions that are direct prerequisites for state program administration (Medicaid eligibility verification, environmental enforcement, food safety inspection coordination).

---

### Success Metrics (Legislative)

- GAO investigation request filed within 24 hours
- IG inquiry sent within 48 hours
- Congressional constituent services contact established within 72 hours
- State parallel function legislation introduced within 30 days in 5+ states

## Crisis 5: Prosecutorial Weaponization

### Crisis Scenario

DOJ initiates a criminal prosecution of a political figure, civil society organization, or journalist for political rather than law enforcement purposes. The SPLC indictment (April 21, 2026) is the established template; future targets include Democratic elected officials under investigation (Adam Schiff and others — Domain 29, Section 2).

---

### 72-Hour Congressional Response

**Targeted demand for legal authority documentation:**

Within 24 hours of a politically targeted indictment, the Senate and House Judiciary Committee Democratic staff directors should send a joint letter to the Attorney General demanding: (1) the specific evidence that predated the prosecution decision; (2) the charging memoranda; (3) the identity of the DOJ officials who made the charging decision; (4) a comparison to non-politically-affiliated individuals who engaged in the same alleged conduct and were not prosecuted.

This letter will not receive a substantive response. Its function is to create a documented record that Congress demanded accountability and the administration refused — a record the next Congress will use for oversight.

**Hearing request:**

Request an emergency hearing of the Senate Judiciary Committee on "The Independence of the Department of Justice and the Prosecution of Civil Society Organizations." The Republican majority will deny the request. Document the denial. Convene an unofficial forum in the same week with the same witnesses. Former prosecutors who assessed the SPLC indictment (Weissmann, Zelinsky, Boynton — Domain 29, Section 1.2) are willing witnesses; constitutional law scholars from law schools who have published on prosecutorial independence are available.

---

### 30-Day Congressional Response

Introduce the DOJ Transparency in Charging Decisions Act (draft language in Domain 29, Section 4.3): requires the DOJ to report quarterly to Congress on all criminal investigations of public figures with political affiliations, identifying the initiating evidence, the charging decision rationale, and the reviewing official. This creates structural accountability without requiring the majority to pass it — the bill's existence and the majority's refusal to consider it are themselves news.

Introduce the Special Prosecutor Independence Act: requires that any prosecution of a current or former elected official, current or former candidate for federal office, or major political party official be conducted by a special prosecutor with statutory independence from the Attorney General's removal authority. This addresses the structural mechanism that enables prosecutorial weaponization.

---

### Success Metrics (Legislative)

- DOJ legal authority demand letter sent within 24 hours
- Hearing request submitted within 48 hours (even if denied)
- DOJ Transparency in Charging Decisions Act introduced within 30 days
- Special Prosecutor Independence Act introduced within 30 days
