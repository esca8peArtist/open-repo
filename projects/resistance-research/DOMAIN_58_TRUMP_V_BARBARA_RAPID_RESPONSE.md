## IMPORTANT FRAMING NOTE

The task brief describes Trump v. Barbara as a "post-Callais tribal voting rights case." This requires a factual clarification essential for accurate distribution. Trump v. Barbara (No. 25-365) is primarily a birthright citizenship case challenging Executive Order 14160, brought by the ACLU on behalf of children who would be denied citizenship. It is not a tribal voting rights case. However, it acquires decisive tribal significance because of a single exchange at oral argument on April 1, 2026 — Solicitor General Sauer's statement that the children of tribal Indians are "not birthright citizens" under the Fourteenth Amendment. That claim, if echoed in any opinion language, would be the most direct threat to the tribal franchise since 1924.

Louisiana v. Callais (April 29, 2026) is the voting rights case — the decision that gutted VRA Section 2's discriminatory-effects standard. The "Callais cascade" refers to the multi-circuit rolling erosion of Section 2 enforcement that Callais triggered.

Domain 58 covers both tracks and their intersection. This memo covers both.

**Expected ruling window**: The Court typically resolves all remaining opinions by the final week of June. April arguments are among the last decided in the term. Trump v. Barbara is expected in the final batch of OT2025 opinions, alongside Trump v. Slaughter (independent agency removal) and Mullin v. Al Otro Lado (asylum). Ruling will issue late June to no later than early July 2026.

**Likelihood of May 21-31 ruling**: Very low. SCOTUS does not typically resolve certiorari-before-judgment constitutional cases this early in the decision-release cycle. The May 21-31 window carries under 5% probability of a ruling. The realistic window is late June (final two weeks of the term). Monitor SCOTUSblog daily from June 15.

### Constitutional Question Presented

Whether Executive Order 14160 complies with the Fourteenth Amendment's Citizenship Clause ("all persons born or naturalized in the United States, and subject to the jurisdiction thereof") and the Immigration and Nationality Act. The constitutional question turns on whether "subject to the jurisdiction thereof" requires parental domicile or lawful permanent residence — the government's theory — or whether territorial birth plus general amenability to U.S. law suffices — the controlling interpretation under United States v. Wong Kim Ark (1898).

The controlling precedent is Wong Kim Ark, which held 6-2 that children of lawful Chinese residents born in the U.S. are citizens under the Citizenship Clause. The government's argument interprets Wong Kim Ark as turning on the parents' "lawful domicile" — a contested reading the Court's majority appeared skeptical of at oral argument.

### Why Consequential for Tribal Voting Rights Post-Callais

The case becomes consequential for tribal sovereignty through a 45-second exchange at oral argument that was the most significant tribal rights moment of the entire OT2025 term:

> **Gorsuch**: "Do you think Native Americans today are birthright citizens under your test?"
> **Sauer**: "I think so" — citing the Indian Citizenship Act of 1924.
> **Gorsuch**: "Put aside the statute. Do you think they're birthright citizens?"
> **Sauer**: "No, I think the clear understanding that everybody agrees in the congressional debates is that the children of tribal Indians are not birthright citizens."

This answer — that tribal members are statutory citizens by congressional grant but not constitutional birthright citizens — reopens a question Congress settled 102 years ago. The Indian Citizenship Act of 1924 was a direct legislative response to Elk v. Wilkins (1884), which held that tribal political allegiance placed tribal members outside the Citizenship Clause. If any portion of the Court's opinion validates Sauer's position, it opens the argument that Congress could revoke tribal citizenship — and with it, the franchise for approximately 9.7 million tribal members.

NCAI General Counsel Lennie Fineday stated explicitly after oral argument: "We believe the reliance on Elk to deny birthright citizenship to children of undocumented immigrants is misplaced. It's a misreading and a misunderstanding."

### Likelihood and Timing of Decision by May 31, 2026

**Probability of ruling by May 31**: Under 5%. No factual or procedural basis to expect acceleration.
**Probability of ruling by June 30**: High — approximately 90%. The Court resolves all OT2025 cases by the end of June.
**Probability of ruling by July 10**: Near-certain.

Distribution planning should be calibrated to a late-June ruling. June 15 is the recommended start date for daily SCOTUSblog monitoring.

## Section 3: Legal Precedent Analysis (~600 words)

### The Callais Cascade — What Has Already Happened

The "Callais cascade" is not a risk prediction. As of May 20, 2026, it is an active, documented, multi-circuit phenomenon operating across three tracks simultaneously.

**Track 1 — Louisiana v. Callais (April 29, 2026)**

The Court ruled 6-3 in Louisiana v. Callais, with Justice Alito writing the majority opinion. The ruling gutted VRA Section 2's discriminatory-effects standard — the primary tool tribal plaintiffs had used to challenge voter suppression through redistricting, polling place access, and registration barriers. The ruling now requires plaintiffs to prove intentional discrimination, a much higher bar that effectively precludes most redistricting challenges.

For tribal communities specifically: NARF and NCAI stated that the ruling "cruelly undercuts the Voting Rights Act, the foundational tool for Native voters and other voters of color to protect themselves from racial discrimination when voting." Democracy Docket documented 28 active lawsuits across all minority communities disrupted or effectively mooted by the ruling, including tribal redistricting challenges in North Dakota, South Dakota, Montana, and Oklahoma.

**Track 2 — Turtle Mountain Band of Chippewa Indians v. Howe, GVR (May 18, 2026)**

Eight days after the Fifth Circuit used Callais to vacate a Mississippi redistricting order (May 11), the Supreme Court issued a GVR — Grant, Vacate, Remand — in Turtle Mountain v. Howe, vacating the Eighth Circuit's ruling and remanding for reconsideration in light of Callais. The same day, the Court issued an identical GVR in Board of Election Commissioners v. Mississippi State Conference of the NAACP.

The GVR is an escalation, not a win. The Eighth Circuit held in May 2025 that private plaintiffs cannot sue to enforce VRA Section 2. A cert denial would have left that ruling frozen. A GVR under Callais sends the case back to a hostile court with instructions to reconsider using a framework that makes it harder, not easier, to preserve private Section 2 enforcement. Justice Jackson dissented in three sentences, noting that Callais "did not address" the private enforceability question — making the majority's GVR citation doctrinally incoherent and revealing a strategic mootness campaign: use GVRs to return Section 2 victories to hostile circuits without the Court ever having to rule definitively on private enforceability.

**Track 3 — Trump v. Barbara (pending)**

The Sauer/Gorsuch exchange establishes the third track: a potential constitutional vulnerability for tribal citizenship itself, independent of VRA enforcement. If any language in the majority opinion or any concurrence validates the "tribal citizenship is statutory, not constitutional" position, it creates a new franchise vulnerability that operates at a different constitutional register than the VRA.

### Relationship to Prior Tribal Voting Cases

- **Elk v. Wilkins (1884)**: Held that tribal political allegiance placed tribal members outside the Citizenship Clause. Congress overrode Elk with the Indian Citizenship Act (1924). Sauer's April 1 argument explicitly revived Elk as live constitutional doctrine — the first time any government official has argued this position before the Supreme Court in 102 years.
- **McGirt v. Oklahoma (2020)**: Confirmed that large portions of eastern Oklahoma remain Indian Country, making tribal sovereignty a live constitutional issue for contemporary audiences. Justice Gorsuch wrote the majority opinion, establishing his role as the Court's most consistent tribal sovereignty advocate — which makes his pointed questioning of Sauer in Barbara particularly significant.
- **United States v. Wong Kim Ark (1898)**: The controlling birthright citizenship precedent. The case held 6-2 that children of lawful Chinese residents born in the U.S. are citizens. The government's current theory attempts to distinguish Wong Kim Ark by reading its holding as dependent on parental "lawful domicile" — a reading the Court's majority appeared skeptical of.

### Constitutional Standards That Would Apply

**If the Court rules on constitutional grounds**:
- The Citizenship Clause standard: Whether "subject to the jurisdiction thereof" requires parental domicile (government's theory) or territorial birth plus general legal amenability (Wong Kim Ark standard)
- Equal protection implications: Any ruling that creates differential citizenship classes based on parental status implicates the equal protection dimension of the Fifth Amendment's due process clause (applicable to federal government)

**If the Court rules on statutory grounds**:
- Immigration and Nationality Act (INA) section 301(a): Grants citizenship to persons born in the United States "subject to the jurisdiction thereof" — same Citizenship Clause language incorporated by statute
- Potential for future congressional reversal: A statutory holding leaves INA's citizenship provisions vulnerable to the current Republican congressional majority

**Post-Callais doctrinal alternatives available for tribal voting rights**:
1. **State constitutional challenges**: Montana SB 490 injunction (May 11, 2026) — Western Native Voice et al. — is the first documented post-Callais state constitutional tribal voting rights victory. Judge Adam Larsen found SB 490 disproportionately impacts Native American voters and enjoined it on state constitutional grounds. This pathway does not require proving intentional discrimination under Callais.
2. **APA challenges**: Administrative Procedure Act challenges to election administration rules that disparately affect tribal communities; the APA's arbitrary-and-capricious standard is not limited by Callais's intent requirement.
3. **VRA Section 11(b)**: Section 11(b)'s intimidation and coercion prohibition has a different evidentiary structure than Section 2 and was not directly addressed in Callais.
4. **State Voting Rights Acts**: Eight states (CA 2002, WA 2018, VA 2021, NY 2022, CT 2023, MN 2024, CO 2025, MD 2026) have state-level VRA equivalents. None of the five priority tribal-voting states (MT, ND, SD, AZ, OK) have state-level equivalents — state VRA advocacy is the primary post-Callais legislative target.

### International Precedent (UNDRIP and ILO C169)

**UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP, 2007; US endorsed 2010)**:
- Article 3: Indigenous peoples have the right to self-determination
- Article 18: Indigenous peoples have the right to participate in decision-making in matters affecting their rights, including maintaining and developing their own decision-making institutions
- Article 20: The right to maintain and develop their political, economic, and social systems
- The Trump administration's January 2026 multilateral withdrawal posture (documented in Domain 57) likely affects US adherence to UNDRIP commitments, though UNDRIP is non-binding international soft law

**ILO Convention 169 (Not ratified by the United States)**:
- Article 6: Requires governments to consult indigenous peoples on measures affecting them
- Article 25: Healthcare rights for indigenous peoples
- The US has not ratified ILO C169; it is relevant as comparative international standard but carries no binding domestic legal force
