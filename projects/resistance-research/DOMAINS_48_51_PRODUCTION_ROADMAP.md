### Domain 48: Criminal Justice, Civic Exclusion Architecture, and the Democratic Participation Crisis

**Domain number**: 48
**Status**: Not yet researched — research begins June 1, 2026
**Estimated word count**: 6,500–7,500
**Estimated research hours**: 16–18
**Citation target**: 45–55
**Target distribution date**: August 1–15, 2026

#### Democratic Design Angle (Unique Framing)

Domain 29 documents prosecutorial weaponization — DOJ as an executive political instrument. Domain 48 covers a structurally distinct question: how criminal justice contact and conviction remove people from the civic body as a designed system, not as an incidental consequence. The framing is civic exclusion architecture — the structured legal infrastructure that converts criminal justice contact into democratic non-participation across five interlocking mechanisms: felony disenfranchisement, jury exclusion, public employment bars, housing restrictions, and debt burden. The analytical difference from Domain 29 is the level: Domain 29 analyzes prosecutorial abuse at the individual case level; Domain 48 analyzes the aggregate system level as a structural democracy problem producing a predictable demographic result. The frame that differentiates this from any existing advocacy literature: criminal justice functions as an unlegislated voting restriction law operating in parallel with the formal electoral system.

No existing domain in the 53-domain framework addresses this civic exclusion architecture as a unified democratic design problem. The closest is Domain 22 (Racial Justice and Democratic Participation), but Domain 22's civic exclusion analysis is embedded in a broader racial equity frame. Domain 48 makes civic exclusion the central analytical object and traces the feedback loop: disenfranchisement reduces political accountability for prosecutors and legislators whose policies generate further disenfranchisement.

#### Core Research Findings (Evidence Anchors — 5 Key Points)

These are the findings that must be documented with primary sourcing during research. They define the domain's analytical spine.

1. **Scale of the disenfranchisement system**: 4.4 million people are currently disenfranchised due to felony records (Sentencing Project 2024 annual survey). Nineteen states permanently or indefinitely bar jury service for people with felony convictions. Public employment restrictions cover 40,000+ job categories at the federal level under 44,000 "employment authorization" federal statutes and regulations (Bureau of Justice Statistics collateral consequences database). This quantification must be current to 2025–2026 data; the Sentencing Project's 2025 update should be the primary source.

2. **The disenfranchisement feedback loop**: Communities with the highest disenfranchisement rates elect the prosecutors and state legislators who determine disenfranchisement policy, but disenfranchised residents cannot vote in those elections. The empirical chain: a 1-percentage-point increase in disenfranchisement rates in a jurisdiction is associated with a statistically significant reduction in Democratic vote share in state legislative races — which corresponds to lower probability of automatic restoration legislation passing (Manza and Uggen, "Locked Out," updated with 2020–2024 data). The causal mechanism, not just the correlation, must be documented.

3. **The debt burden as civic exclusion mechanism**: Legal financial obligations — fines, fees, court costs, and restitution — function as a de facto poll tax. Worth Rises research (2025) documents that 83% of formerly incarcerated people carry court debt averaging $13,607. In states where voting rights are conditioned on completion of "all terms of sentence," court debt creates permanent disenfranchisement for the economically unable to pay. The Brennan Center's analysis of the Florida Amendment 4 implementation (2018 voting rights restoration amendment, subsequently conditioned on debt payment by the legislature) is the most fully documented recent case study.

4. **Jury exclusion as civic capacity elimination**: When 19 states permanently exclude people with felony records from jury service, the communities with the highest incarceration rates are also the communities most excluded from the deliberative civic institution of jury decision-making. In jurisdictions with high disenfranchisement concentrations, the jury pool is systematically unrepresentative of the defendant population — which is both a Sixth Amendment concern and a democratic legitimacy concern distinct from the criminal procedure framing. Batson v. Kentucky and subsequent peremptory challenge reform jurisprudence should be analyzed alongside the felony exclusion statutes.

5. **Restoration reform as active legislative terrain (2026)**: Several states are currently in active automatic restoration debates: Minnesota enacted automatic restoration on release in 2023; Maryland enacted automatic restoration pending post-election survey results in 2022; Florida's Amendment 4 debt condition remains in active litigation (Hand v. Scott, the direct implementation litigation); Michigan, Pennsylvania, and Louisiana have active 2026 legislative proposals. The OT2025 Supreme Court term includes at least one case with direct disenfranchisement implications (confirm case name during research). This restoration landscape is the "advocacy window" that makes the domain timely.

### Domain 49: Environmental Justice and Frontline Community Participation Rights

**Domain number**: 49
**Status**: Not yet researched — research begins June 16, 2026
**Estimated word count**: 6,000–7,000
**Estimated research hours**: 12–14
**Citation target**: 40–50
**Target distribution date**: August 15–30, 2026

#### Democratic Design Angle (Unique Framing)

The environmental rollbacks tracker and the existing research in `environmental-rollbacks-tracker.md` document what the federal government has removed (EPA programs, NEPA requirements, EJ offices). Domain 49 covers the question the tracker doesn't ask: who had standing to challenge those removals, and how was that standing eliminated? The framing is community participation rights — the legal and procedural infrastructure through which frontline communities could previously intervene in environmental decisions that directly threaten their health and property, and the systematic dismantling of that infrastructure in 2025–2026.

The democratic design distinction from Domain 15 (Environmental Rollbacks) is the level of analysis. Domain 15 asks: what protections have been removed? Domain 49 asks: which democratic tools did affected communities use to contest decisions, and how were those tools disabled? The analytical object is not the policy but the participation architecture. The normative claim is not "clean air is a right" but "voice in decisions about your own survival is a democratic right." This is the frame that gives environmental justice a constitutional democracy argument — not just a policy argument — and that connects the EJ research to state AG litigation, Title VI civil rights enforcement, and international human rights frameworks.

#### Core Research Findings (Evidence Anchors — 5 Key Points)

1. **The EPA environmental justice infrastructure elimination**: The Trump administration closed all ten EPA regional environmental justice offices in January 2025 and canceled approximately $400 million in previously awarded environmental justice grants (confirm exact figure — varies by source between $300M and $500M; use EPA internal budget document). The Office of Environmental Justice and External Civil Affairs was eliminated. This is not a budget reduction — it is the deletion of the administrative structure through which community participation in environmental permitting was processed.

2. **NEPA's procedural protections as community participation tools**: The National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) required environmental impact assessments with public comment periods for major federal projects. The Trump administration used the Fiscal Responsibility Act of 2023 NEPA revisions (Biden-era limitation) and further executive action to eliminate categorical reviews and narrow standing for community comments. Earthjustice has filed multiple D.C. Circuit challenges — identify specific case names and status during research. The Inflation Reduction Act NEPA improvements (Section 60112) were partially rescinded in the OBBBA.

3. **Title VI civil rights enforcement collapse**: Title VI of the Civil Rights Act prohibits environmental agencies from licensing facilities in ways that discriminate against communities of color. The EPA's Office of External Civil Affairs (which processed Title VI administrative complaints) was eliminated as part of the EJ office closure. The average Title VI complaint processing time had already been 10+ years before 2025; the elimination of the office effectively ends administrative Title VI enforcement. Identify any active D.C. Circuit Title VI environmental cases.

4. **Endangerment Finding revocation as democratic participation exclusion**: The EPA's proposed revocation of the Clean Air Act Endangerment Finding (the 2009 scientific determination that greenhouse gases endanger public health) is in a public comment period through approximately summer 2026. The revocation, if finalized, eliminates the legal foundation for community challenges to any federal action that increases GHG emissions. Earthjustice and state AGs have identified standing arguments — document specific legal theories and comment period dates.

5. **International benchmarks for environmental participation rights**: The Aarhus Convention (UN/UNECE, 1998, now ratified by 47 nations) establishes enforceable rights to: (1) access to environmental information, (2) public participation in environmental decision-making, and (3) access to justice on environmental matters. The US has signed but not ratified Aarhus. The EU's implementation of Aarhus provides the comparative benchmark for what the US EJ infrastructure could look like if it were designed as a participation right rather than an administrative program. The 2021 Glasgow Declaration on Environmental Rights also provides international framing.

### Domain 50: LGBTQ+ Rights and Trans Liberation Under Systematic Legal Attack

**Domain number**: 50

#### Democratic Design Angle (Unique Framing)

The standard framing for LGBTQ+ rights in 2026 is a rights frame: what specific protections are being removed, and which communities are being harmed. Domain 50 adds a democratic design layer: the Alliance Defending Freedom (ADF) legal infrastructure building anti-LGBTQ+ legislation is the same infrastructure building anti-ballot-initiative architecture (documented in Phase 1 Domain 33) and anti-reproductive-rights legislation (Domain 44). The domain's unique analytical contribution is not the inventory of 740 anti-LGBTQ+ bills — advocacy organizations already maintain that — but the identification of LGBTQ+ rights suppression as a component of a unified anti-democratic legal architecture that attacks civic rights through litigation strategy, model legislation, and judicial appointment pipelines simultaneously across multiple targets. The frame that differentiates this from any existing advocacy literature: the attack on LGBTQ+ rights is not a culture war; it is a legal infrastructure project whose tools, funders, and personnel overlap with the broader anti-democratic project the framework documents across all other domains.

The democratic participation angle: in states with active anti-trans ballot measures (at least four in November 2026), the ballot initiative process itself is being used as a democratic tool against a targeted minority — the precise anti-countermajoritarian problem that the federal constitutional structure was designed to address. This is the legal theory that generates the most actionable advocacy use: state AGs, the ACLU, Lambda Legal, and civil rights law school clinics all need a brief that synthesizes the constitutional anti-countermajoritarian argument for the ballot measure litigation context.

#### Core Research Findings (Evidence Anchors — 5 Key Points)

1. **Scale of the legislative assault**: 740+ anti-LGBTQ+ bills were introduced across 42 states in 2026 as of the ACLU's live bill tracker (confirm current count during research). 113 bills at the federal level. Four states advancing anti-trans ballot measures to November 2026 ballots — identify specific states and ballot measure text during research (ACLU tracker or Movement Advancement Project). The scale is the first finding: this is not a fragmented state-by-state policy dispute; it is a coordinated national legislative campaign.

2. **The ADF model legislation infrastructure**: Alliance Defending Freedom produced the model legislation templates that underlie the majority of state-level anti-trans bills. Document the specific ADF model bills, the attorney general advisory opinions it has drafted, and the overlap between ADF's legislative strategy and its litigation strategy. SPLC's tracking of ADF as a hate group designation and the internal strategy documents that have been disclosed in litigation are the primary sources. The overlap with Domain 33 (anti-ballot-initiative architecture) is critical: ADF was involved in both the Arizona Proposition 210 litigation and multiple state anti-trans ballot measure campaigns.

3. **Healthcare denial as civic exclusion**: The Affordable Care Act's Section 1557 prohibits sex discrimination in healthcare; the Trump administration's HHS rule rescinding trans-inclusive protections means trans individuals denied healthcare are experiencing a government-imposed harm that affects their capacity to work, to participate in public life, and to organize politically. KFF healthcare access data for trans patients (2024–2025) documents the coverage denial rates. The healthcare exclusion creates a documented civic participation impact — people managing medically denied conditions are less able to engage in political organizing, legal challenges, and civic participation generally.

4. **Anti-trans ballot measures as anti-countermajoritarian weaponization**: The democratic theory problem with anti-minority ballot measures is that they convert majority prejudice into constitutional entrenchment — removing protections from a minority that cannot win popular votes precisely because of the prejudice the measure expresses. Romer v. Evans (1996, SCOTUS) established that Colorado's Amendment 2 (removing LGBTQ+ anti-discrimination protections through ballot initiative) violated the Equal Protection Clause because it singled out a class for disfavor without rational basis. The four 2026 anti-trans ballot measures will generate Romer challenges — document the legal theory and the Lambda Legal / ACLU litigation architecture.

5. **The judicial appointment pipeline**: The Federalist Society judicial appointment pipeline has placed judges at every circuit level who have issued favorable rulings on anti-LGBTQ+ legislation. Document specific circuit court decisions from 2025–2026 that upheld anti-trans legislation and identify the appointing president and Federalist Society membership of the authoring judges. This is the cross-domain connection to Domain 6 (Judicial Independence): the same judicial appointment infrastructure that hollowed out judicial independence also created the circuit court landscape where anti-LGBTQ+ legislation survives constitutional challenge.

### Domain 51: Campaign Finance, Dark Money, and the Architecture of Corporate Capture

**Domain number**: 51
**Status**: Not yet researched — research begins June 1, 2026
**Estimated word count**: 5,500–6,500
**Estimated research hours**: 12–14
**Citation target**: 40–50
**Target distribution date**: August 1–15, 2026

#### Democratic Design Angle (Unique Framing)

Domain 51 is the meta-analysis domain: it explains the structural mechanism by which all of the sector-specific corporate captures documented across the framework operate. The domain's unique contribution is not a new factual finding but a new frame that makes the entire framework more coherent. After distributing Domains 1–50, the most common sophisticated question from recipients will be: "how does this level of coordinated capture happen across so many sectors simultaneously?" Domain 51 is the answer: a single legal architecture derived from Citizens United and its successor cases funds, coordinates, and insulates the capture operations the framework documents across health, energy, labor, judicial appointments, and election administration simultaneously.

The democratic design angle is corporate capture as a democratic design failure rather than a corruption problem. Corruption implies individual bad actors exploiting individual weaknesses. Corporate capture via dark money is a structural system: the design of the 501(c)(4) vehicle, the shell corporation disclosure gap, the independent expenditure loophole, and the FEC enforcement failure constitute an architecture — not an accident. The reform argument is therefore architectural: not "prosecute the bad actors" but "redesign the disclosure system so that the architecture cannot sustain itself."

The 2026 advocacy hook is unusually strong: four active state ballot measures targeting dark money disclosure reform create a pre-November research window; the DISCLOSE Act of 2026 has Senate momentum with Whitehouse as lead sponsor (existing Phase 1 contact through judicial independence material); and FEC commissioner vacancies (two of six seats vacant as of May 2026 — confirm count) create an administrative law angle on who actually governs the governance system.

#### Core Research Findings (Evidence Anchors — 5 Key Points)

1. **The $1.9 billion dark money figure**: OpenSecrets documented $1.9 billion in 501(c)(4) and other "dark money" spending in the 2024 election cycle — meaning political spending where the original donor is never disclosed. This is the primary quantitative anchor. Contextualize: in 2004, before Citizens United, total outside spending was $400 million and substantially disclosed. The structural change from disclosure to opacity happened in two steps — Citizens United (2010) and SpeechNow.org v. FEC (D.C. Circuit, 2010) — and represents a complete architectural transformation in one election cycle.

2. **The sector-specific capture connections**: The dark money infrastructure is not generic political spending — it funds specific capture operations documented elsewhere in the framework. Americans for Prosperity (Koch network 501(c)(4)) funds state-level judicial election campaigns and judicial appointment lobbying (Domain 6 connection). Club for Growth dark money funds primary challenges to Republican incumbents who support healthcare regulations (Domain 31 connection). The Federalist Society's dark money donors overlap with the ADF anti-LGBTQ+ infrastructure (Domain 50 connection). Document at least three specific sector-specific connections with dollar amounts from OpenSecrets.

3. **The four 2026 state ballot measures**: Identify the four states with dark money disclosure ballot measures scheduled for November 2026 (DOMAIN_EXPANSION_ROADMAP notes Arizona, Massachusetts, Montana, North Dakota — confirm these and the specific measure texts). Each is the result of a citizen petition process that the existing dark money architecture will attempt to defeat using dark money opposition campaigns — the meta-irony is structurally important for the advocacy argument.

4. **FEC enforcement failure as administrative design failure**: The FEC's six-commissioner bipartisan structure requires four votes to take enforcement action; the two Republican-appointed commissioners have consistently voted to deadlock enforcement actions since 2010. Document the enforcement deadlock rate (available from FEC annual reports and Issue One research). The result: the agency designed to enforce campaign finance law has been structurally disabled by the same partisan appointment system it is designed to oversee. The commissioner vacancy count and nomination history (who is blocking appointments and why) is the 2026-specific finding.

5. **International comparison — the UK and Canada models**: The UK Political Parties, Elections and Referendums Act (PPERA) requires disclosure of all political advertising expenditure and prohibits foreign donations — creating a functional transparency architecture that coexists with free speech rights and active political competition. Canada's Elections Act prohibits use of personal information without consent for political targeting (connecting to the surveillance capitalism domain if that research proceeds) and requires disclosure of all political advertising spend. The comparative benchmark answers the "but free speech" objection: functioning democracies with robust free speech rights have disclosure requirements and they work.

### Production Bottleneck Analysis

**Primary bottleneck: interviews vs. secondary research vs. primary source acquisition**

Phase 1 domain velocity (12–14 hours per domain at 7,000 words) was achieved primarily through secondary source synthesis. All Phase 1 domains relied on existing research literature, court records, and government documents rather than primary interviews. Domain 48 breaks this pattern: the Movement for Black Lives Policy Table framing validation and Worth Rises legal financial obligations accuracy check are best done with brief email outreach rather than treating the advocacy literature as authoritative on its own empirical claims.

Resolution: treat interviews as optional enhancements to secondary research, not prerequisites. Draft Domains 48 and 51 without interview input; if interview responses arrive during peer review (Week 5), integrate. If not, proceed to final draft without them. This prevents the single largest velocity risk in research production: waiting for interview responses that do not arrive on schedule.

**Secondary bottleneck: source access**

Earthjustice active D.C. Circuit case names require PACER access or Earthjustice public litigation page access. The OT2025 SCOTUS disenfranchisement case name requires SCOTUS docket search. The ADF model legislation templates require careful web research (ADF website plus litigation disclosure records). These are all resolvable in 1–2 hours of focused research time each; flag them as Week 1 tasks to avoid discovering access barriers mid-draft.

**Tertiary bottleneck: Phase 1 execution overlap (June 1–15)**

If Phase 1 distribution (June 1–15 Domains 44, 45, 47 cluster) is active simultaneously with Wave 1 research start, the researcher load is highest during Weeks 1–2. Resolution: assign separate researcher capacity to Phase 1 distribution (coordination tasks, email sends, follow-up) vs. Wave 1 research (reading, outlining, drafting). If only one researcher is available, prioritize Phase 1 distribution from June 1–7 and push Domain 48/51 outlines to June 8–14 (compress Week 1 into Week 2; end dates adjust by one week).

**Velocity estimates vs. Phase 1 actuals**: Phase 1 produced 4 domains in a single concentrated session block (May 9, 2026). Phase 2 is budgeted across 8 weeks, which is more conservative. If Phase 2 sessions are concentrated similarly, the timeline could compress to 5–6 weeks. The 8-week plan is the planning baseline; 6 weeks is the optimistic case; 10 weeks is the contingency (see Part 7).

## Part 5: Quality Gate Procedure

### Self-Check Protocol

Each domain undergoes a five-stage self-check before peer review. This protocol is grounded in the same validation logic as `src/superclaude/pm_agent/self_check.py` — evidence-based verification rather than confidence assertion.

**Stage 1 — Research Currency Check**
All primary sources within 2 weeks of the research completion date. Exceptions permitted only for foundational legal documents (SCOTUS cases, statutory text) and long-term academic works cited for empirical findings that are not time-dependent. Flag any source that is more than 6 months old and that is cited for a quantitative claim — verify whether a more recent edition exists.

Specific checks:
- [ ] Sentencing Project disenfranchisement survey — is this the 2025 edition or 2024?
- [ ] OpenSecrets dark money database — is the 2024 cycle the most recent complete dataset?
- [ ] ACLU LGBTQ+ bill tracker — was the count accessed on research day (live database)?
- [ ] FEC commissioner roster — is the vacancy count current to research week?
- [ ] EPA Endangerment Finding comment period — is the deadline date still current?

**Stage 2 — Citation Completeness Check**
Target: 40+ citations per domain. No citation placeholder ("forthcoming," "to be confirmed," "see also"). Every factual claim beyond common knowledge has a specific citation. Quantitative claims have primary sources (not secondary summaries). Legal citations include full case name, year, and court.

Domains 48, 50: Target 45–55 (Phase 1 average was 47–52 for comparable domains)
Domain 49: Target 40–50
Domain 51: Target 40–50

**Stage 3 — Democratic Design Angle Uniqueness Check**
The following questions must be answered with "yes" and documented:
- [ ] Does this domain cover something that none of the 53 prior domains covers as its primary analysis?
- [ ] Does the democratic design angle in this domain generate a different policy implication than the same topic would generate if framed only as a rights or policy issue?
- [ ] Is the "what goes wrong without this domain" argument specific to this domain and not already addressed in a cross-reference?

If any answer is "no," the domain's framing requires revision before peer review.

**Stage 4 — Cross-Domain Integration Check**
Each domain must have a cross-domain integration section that specifically cites 5+ other domains by number and explains the analytical connection. The connection must be substantive (the domains share a mechanism or evidence base), not merely topical (they both concern racial equity).

**Stage 5 — Adoption Pathway Check**
Each domain must have a specific identified adoption pathway: a named organization or organization type, a specific use case (training curriculum, litigation brief, legislative testimony, GOTV toolkit), and a timeline for when that use case is most urgent. "General distribution" is not an adoption pathway.
