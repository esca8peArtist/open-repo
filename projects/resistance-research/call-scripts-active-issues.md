# Constituent Call Scripts: ICE Detention, Oversight, and Democratic Accountability
*March 2026 — Ready-to-use scripts for contacting your representatives*

---

## How to Call Your Representatives

**Call, don't email.** Congressional staffers track call volume by issue. Emails generate auto-replies and rarely move the needle. A one-minute phone call counts. A hundred of them count a lot.

**Call your own representatives only.** You are a constituent — that is your leverage. Identify yourself as one, with your city. Calls from outside a district or state are dismissed.

**Keep it under 60 seconds.** Staffers are logging calls, not having debates. State your name, city, the specific ask, and thank them. Done.

**Structure every call the same way:**
1. Name and city
2. Specific ask (one action, not a general complaint)
3. One supporting fact
4. Thank you

**Track your calls.** Keep a simple log: date, office called, issue, response received, follow-up needed. This lets you escalate and shows patterns in who is and isn't responding.

**Best times to call:** Tuesday through Thursday, 9am–5pm local time. Monday mornings and Friday afternoons are lowest-priority coverage.

**Use 5calls.org** — it provides daily pre-researched scripts, identifies your representatives by zip code, and tracks your call history. It also aggregates call counts so you can see where pressure is building.

**If you get voicemail:** Leave the full message anyway. Voicemails are tallied just like live calls. Do not hang up.

**If a staffer tries to debate you:** You are not obligated to argue. "I understand. I'm asking the Senator to [specific ask]. Please pass that along. Thank you." is a complete response.

### Script 1: Stop ICE Warehouse Expansion

**Issue:** The One Big Beautiful Budget Act of 2025 allocates $45 billion for immigration enforcement, of which $38.3 billion funds a program called the ICE Detention Reengineering Initiative — the largest single expansion of civil detention infrastructure in U.S. history. The program converts large commercial warehouses into detention facilities holding up to 10,000 people each, with 24 sites planned across the country.

**Your ask:** Vote against and publicly oppose the $38.3B ICE Detention Reengineering Initiative. If the broader legislation has already passed, support legislation to defund or impose environmental and civil rights conditions on these facilities.

**Best for:** All members of Congress, with particular urgency for Republicans representing districts where warehouse sites are planned — constituent opposition is bipartisan in affected communities.

**Key facts to know:**
- 24 warehouse facilities planned nationally
- Each facility designed to hold up to 10,000 people — larger than most U.S. county jails
- Construction bypassing National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) review — multiple legal challenges pending, including Maryland's
- Community opposition has emerged across the political spectrum, including from local governments in conservative areas
- The contracting mechanism being used (a $10B Navy infrastructure vehicle) was not designed or authorized for civilian detention

---

**THE SCRIPT:**

> "Hi, my name is [NAME] and I'm a constituent from [CITY]. I'm calling to urge [Representative/Senator NAME] to publicly oppose and vote against the $38.3 billion ICE warehouse detention expansion. Twenty-four facilities holding up to 10,000 people each, built without environmental review, is unprecedented — and communities across this country, including conservative ones, are opposed. Please take a public stand against this. Thank you."

**If they ask questions:**

- *"Isn't this about securing the border?"* — "These are interior civil detention facilities, not border infrastructure. Most people detained have no criminal conviction. This is about warehousing people, not border security."
- *"Hasn't this already been funded?"* — "Funding can be rescinded or conditioned. I'm asking [NAME] to take a public position and support any legislative effort to stop or constrain this program."
- *"Where will these facilities be built?"* — "Sites are being identified now. I'd ask [NAME]'s office to request the full list from DHS — constituents deserve to know."

**Follow-up:** If your district has a planned facility, request a town hall or constituent meeting specifically on this issue. Check Democracy Docket for litigation updates on the Maryland NEPA challenge.

### Script 2: Restore OIDO and Detention Oversight

**Issue:** In March 2025, the Office of the Immigration Detention Ombudsman (OIDO) and the DHS Office for Civil Rights and Civil Liberties (CRCL) were placed on administrative leave and effectively abolished. These were the two primary oversight bodies responsible for investigating complaints, conducting facility inspections, and reporting on conditions in ICE detention. Their elimination happened as detention expanded rapidly with no replacement oversight mechanism.

**Your ask:** Restore the Office of the Immigration Detention Ombudsman and the DHS Office for Civil Rights and Civil Liberties to full operational status, including their inspection and complaint-handling functions.

**Best for:** All members; especially effective for members on the House or Senate Homeland Security Committees, Judiciary Committees, and Government Oversight Committees.

**Key facts to know:**
- OIDO and CRCL were placed on administrative leave in March 2025, effectively ending their operations
- 32 people died in ICE custody in 2025 — the deadliest year in over 20 years
- Facility inspection reports dropped 36% while the detained population increased 78%
- No independent oversight body currently has operational status to investigate complaints or conditions
- The gap between detainee population growth and inspection capacity has no precedent in ICE's history

---

**THE SCRIPT:**

> "Hi, my name is [NAME] and I'm a constituent from [CITY]. I'm calling to urge [Representative/Senator NAME] to support restoring the Office of the Immigration Detention Ombudsman and the DHS civil rights office to full operation. Thirty-two people died in ICE custody last year — the deadliest year in two decades — and we've eliminated the offices responsible for oversight. That has to change. Please use your oversight authority to act. Thank you."

**If they ask questions:**

- *"Isn't ICE already accountable to Congress?"* — "Formal oversight through independent inspectors is what makes that accountability real. Without OIDO and CRCL, there is no functioning complaint system. Detainees have no recourse and Congress has no independent source of information."
- *"Were those offices effective?"* — "They documented preventable deaths and facility violations. Whether they were effective enough is a fair debate — but abolishing them entirely during the fastest expansion in ICE detention history is the wrong direction."

**Follow-up:** Ask if the member will send a formal letter to DHS demanding a timeline for restoring these offices. Track whether your representative serves on a relevant oversight committee and follow their committee activity.

### Script 3: Investigate Deaths in ICE Custody

**Issue:** Thirty-two people died in ICE custody in 2025, the highest annual total in over twenty years. Early 2026 figures are on pace to exceed that. One death — Geraldo Lunas Campos at Fort Bliss — was ruled a homicide by the medical examiner. A 2021 ACLU/Physicians for Human Rights report found that 95% of detainee deaths from 2017–2021 were preventable with adequate medical care. Senator Jon Ossoff has initiated an investigation; a GAO investigation has been called for.

**Your ask:** Support Senator Ossoff's ongoing investigation into deaths in ICE custody, and urge the GAO to conduct an independent investigation into medical care standards, facility conditions, and death reporting practices across the ICE detention system.

**Best for:** Senate members (Ossoff's investigation is Senate-based); members of the Judiciary Committee, Homeland Security Committee, and anyone on Government Oversight. House members can request a parallel investigation through the House Judiciary Committee.

**Key facts to know:**
- 32 deaths in ICE custody in 2025; 12+ confirmed through mid-March 2026, on pace to exceed last year
- Geraldo Lunas Campos death at Fort Bliss ruled homicide by medical examiner — no federal charges filed as of March 2026
- 95% of ICE detainee deaths 2017–2021 were preventable per ACLU/PHR analysis
- OIDO and CRCL — the offices responsible for investigating deaths — are no longer operational
- DHS has not released a comprehensive public report on 2025 detention deaths

---

**THE SCRIPT:**

> "Hi, my name is [NAME] and I'm a constituent from [CITY]. I'm calling to urge [Representative/Senator NAME] to support a full investigation into deaths in ICE custody. Thirty-two people died last year — the most in twenty years — including one ruled a homicide by a medical examiner. With oversight offices abolished, there is no accountability. Please support Senator Ossoff's investigation and request a GAO review. Thank you."

**If they ask questions:**

- *"Aren't these people in custody for a reason?"* — "Civil immigration detention is not criminal punishment. People are held pending proceedings, not as a sentence. The government has a legal duty of care regardless of immigration status."
- *"Is one death really a homicide?"* — "Yes. Geraldo Lunas Campos at Fort Bliss — the medical examiner's ruling is a matter of public record. A federal investigation has not resulted in charges. That is exactly what a congressional investigation should examine."

**Follow-up:** Ask whether your senator will co-sign a letter to the GAO requesting the investigation. Check Detention Watch Network for updated death reporting.

### Script 4: Oppose DOGE/Palantir Immigration Surveillance

**Issue:** In early 2025, an IRS-ICE data sharing agreement was pushed through over the objections of senior IRS officials, giving immigration enforcement access to tax return data for the first time in the agency's modern history. Palantir Technologies was simultaneously awarded a $30 million contract to build "ImmigrationOS" — a surveillance platform integrating IRS, Social Security Administration, and other federal databases to identify, locate, and track people for civil immigration enforcement. The system has reportedly flagged U.S. citizens incorrectly.

**Your ask:** Co-sponsor or support legislation prohibiting the use of IRS tax data and Social Security Administration records for civil immigration enforcement. Support congressional investigations and FOIA requests into the ImmigrationOS contract and its data integration practices.

**Best for:** All members; especially members of the Senate Finance Committee, House Ways and Means Committee, Senate Intelligence Committee, and House Intelligence Committee.

**Key facts to know:**
- IRS-ICE data sharing agreement finalized over explicit objections from senior IRS officials — unprecedented in modern IRS history
- Palantir awarded $30M contract for ImmigrationOS, integrating multiple federal databases
- System has flagged U.S. citizens for immigration enforcement — scope of errors unknown
- This is the most extensive peacetime integration of civilian federal data for law enforcement purposes in U.S. history
- No court has ruled this lawful; litigation is active

---

**THE SCRIPT:**

> "Hi, my name is [NAME] and I'm a constituent from [CITY]. I'm calling to urge [Representative/Senator NAME] to support legislation blocking the use of IRS and Social Security data for immigration enforcement. The government is using Palantir to build a mass surveillance system integrating tax records and benefits data — it has already flagged U.S. citizens incorrectly. This is not what that data was collected for. Please act. Thank you."

**If they ask questions:**

- *"Don't we need to find people who are here illegally?"* — "IRS data is collected under a promise of confidentiality so people file taxes and contribute to the economy. Weaponizing that data destroys compliance and harms everyone. There are legal limits on how federal data can be used — this crosses them."
- *"Palantir is a private company — isn't that legal?"* — "They are a government contractor operating under a federal contract. Congress has full authority to restrict what data the government can share with its contractors and for what purposes."

**Follow-up:** Ask whether your member will request a classified briefing on ImmigrationOS if they sit on an intelligence committee. Track the ACLU and EFF litigation on data sharing.

### Script 5: Support Detainee Access to Counsel

**Issue:** The U.S. government does not provide attorneys to people in civil immigration detention. Approximately 70.8% of ICE detainees have no criminal conviction. Having legal representation is the single most statistically significant factor in whether someone wins their case. Access to counsel is being actively disrupted: attorneys have been physically prevented from seeing clients, legal visit facilities have been inadequate or nonexistent at new detention sites, and legal aid organizations are overwhelmed and underfunded.

**Your ask:** Co-sponsor legislation providing appointed counsel for immigration detention cases, or at minimum support emergency funding for nonprofit legal organizations that serve detained people. Oppose any measures that further restrict attorney access to detention facilities.

**Best for:** Judiciary Committee members in both chambers; any member who has publicly supported right-to-counsel principles; appropriations subcommittee members with jurisdiction over DOJ and DHS.

**Key facts to know:**
- 70.8% of ICE detainees have no criminal conviction
- No right to appointed counsel exists in civil immigration proceedings — unlike criminal cases
- Representation is the strongest predictor of case outcome — more than any other single variable
- Attorneys have been physically denied access to clients at multiple facilities in 2025–2026
- Legal aid organizations report being unable to meet demand as detention population has grown 78%

---

**THE SCRIPT:**

> "Hi, my name is [NAME] and I'm a constituent from [CITY]. I'm calling to urge [Representative/Senator NAME] to support appointed counsel for people in immigration detention. Over 70% of detainees have no criminal record and no lawyer. Having an attorney is the most important factor in whether someone gets a fair hearing. Please co-sponsor legislation to provide legal representation or fund nonprofit legal organizations filling this gap. Thank you."

**If they ask questions:**

- *"Why should taxpayers pay for lawyers for illegal immigrants?"* — "Because due process is cheaper than wrongful deportation. The U.S. has removed American citizens and legal permanent residents in error. An attorney catches those mistakes. The cost of appointed counsel is a fraction of prolonged detention costs."
- *"Don't they have access to legal resources already?"* — "Technically yes, but practically no. Detention facilities lack adequate legal libraries, phone access, and in-person attorney visits. Attorneys have been physically turned away at facilities. 'Access' on paper is not access in practice."

**Follow-up:** Ask if your member will sign a letter to DHS demanding that attorneys not be physically denied access to detained clients. This is a specific, immediate, actionable ask that does not require new legislation.

### Script 6: Oppose Militarization of Detention

**Issue:** The Department of Defense has been directed to allow military bases to be used as civil immigration detention facilities. Fort Bliss (Texas) and Fort Dix (New Jersey) are among active sites. A May 2025 inspection of Fort Bliss found more than 60 violations of detention standards within 50 days of opening. The federal government is also using a $10 billion Navy construction contracting mechanism — designed for naval infrastructure — to build civilian detention facilities, bypassing standard procurement and oversight processes.

**Your ask:** Oppose the use of military bases as immigration detention sites without explicit congressional authorization. Request hearings on conditions at Fort Bliss, Fort Dix, and other military-base detention sites. Investigate the use of the Navy contracting mechanism for civilian detention construction.

**Best for:** Armed Services Committee members in both chambers; members whose districts include or border affected military bases (Fort Bliss in El Paso area, Fort Dix in New Jersey); Homeland Security Committee members; Appropriations subcommittees covering defense and homeland security.

**Key facts to know:**
- Fort Bliss inspection found 60+ violations of detention standards within 50 days of opening
- One detainee death at Fort Bliss ruled a homicide by the medical examiner
- $10B Navy contracting mechanism is being used to fund civilian detention construction — procurement designed for ships and naval facilities, not detention
- DOD involvement in civil immigration enforcement raises Posse Comitatus concerns
- No congressional authorization vote was held for military-base detention use

---

**THE SCRIPT:**

> "Hi, my name is [NAME] and I'm a constituent from [CITY]. I'm calling to urge [Representative/Senator NAME] to oppose using military bases as immigration detention facilities without congressional authorization. Fort Bliss had over 60 standards violations in its first 50 days and a detainee death ruled a homicide. Congress has not authorized this use of military installations. Please request hearings and oppose this practice. Thank you."

**If they ask questions:**

- *"Isn't the military already involved in border security?"* — "Border security support roles are different from operating civil detention facilities on military installations. Detaining civilians — many with no criminal record — at military bases is a different legal and constitutional question. Congress should authorize it if it's going to happen."
- *"Is the Posse Comitatus Act relevant here?"* — "That's exactly the kind of legal question that congressional hearings should address. The law restricts military involvement in civilian law enforcement. Using military infrastructure for mass civil detention is a gray area that deserves scrutiny."

**Follow-up:** If your representative is on Armed Services, ask specifically whether they will request a subcommittee hearing. Ask whether they have visited or requested a visit to any military-base detention facility.

### Script 7: Block 287(g) Agreements in Your State

*Note: This script is for state-level calls — state legislators, not Congress. The contact process is the same, but you are calling your state house or senate member, not your federal representative.*

**Issue:** Section 287(g) of the Immigration and Nationality Act allows ICE to deputize local and state law enforcement officers to perform federal immigration enforcement functions. The number of 287(g) agreements has exceeded 1,000 nationwide as of 2026. These agreements expand enforcement capacity without corresponding federal oversight of the local officers involved, and create significant civil liability for participating agencies and jurisdictions.

**Your ask:** Support state legislation prohibiting your state and local law enforcement agencies from entering into 287(g) agreements with ICE. New Mexico, Maine, and Maryland have passed such legislation; similar bills are active in other states.

**Best for:** State legislators — state house and senate members. Governor's office if legislation has been introduced and needs executive support or veto pressure.

**Key facts to know:**
- 287(g) agreements now exceed 1,000 nationwide — historically unprecedented
- Local officers performing immigration enforcement receive minimal federal training and operate with limited federal oversight
- Jurisdictions with 287(g) agreements report reduced crime reporting from immigrant communities — a public safety concern
- New Mexico, Maine, and Maryland have enacted state-level bans; model legislation is available
- Civil liability for wrongful detention falls on local governments, not ICE

---

**THE SCRIPT:**

> "Hi, my name is [NAME] and I'm a constituent from [CITY]. I'm calling to urge [State Representative/Senator NAME] to support legislation banning 287(g) agreements between local law enforcement and ICE. These agreements deputize local officers for federal immigration enforcement with minimal oversight and training — and they make our communities less safe because people stop reporting crimes. States like New Mexico and Maryland have already done this. Please act. Thank you."

**If they ask questions:**

- *"Don't local agencies need to cooperate with federal law?"* — "Cooperation is different from deputization. Local agencies can still respond to federal warrants. 287(g) goes further — it makes local officers into federal immigration agents without the corresponding federal accountability. That creates real liability for the state."
- *"Doesn't this mean criminals won't be deported?"* — "ICE can still operate in our state. This bill would only prevent local law enforcement from acting as ICE deputies. Federal agents remain free to do their jobs."

**Follow-up:** Identify whether a bill has been introduced in your state legislature. If yes, ask whether your member has co-sponsored it. If no, ask whether they would sponsor one. The Detention Watch Network tracks state-level legislation.

### Script 8: Support State AG Legal Challenges

*Note: This script is for state-level calls — state attorneys general, governors' offices, or state legislators who can direct the AG. It may also be appropriate for federal members who can express support for state-level legal challenges.*

**Issue:** Several states have filed or are preparing legal challenges to federal immigration enforcement actions. Maryland's attorney general has filed suit challenging ICE warehouse construction on NEPA and APA grounds — arguing that bypassing environmental review is unlawful. State AGs have also been asked to challenge or file amicus briefs in litigation over DOGE data sharing with ICE. These legal challenges are among the most effective tools available to slow or stop specific programs.

**Your ask:** Support your state attorney general in joining or filing amicus briefs in legal challenges to ICE warehouse construction (particularly the Maryland NEPA/APA lawsuit) and in litigation challenging IRS and Social Security data sharing with immigration enforcement.

**Best for:** Calls to the state attorney general's office directly; state legislators who can pass resolutions directing or supporting the AG; governors who can signal support for legal action.

**Key facts to know:**
- Maryland AG filed NEPA/APA challenge to ICE warehouse construction — other states can join
- NEPA violations are legally significant: federal construction projects require environmental review; bypassing it creates grounds for injunction
- Multiple FOIA suits are active seeking ImmigrationOS contract details and data sharing agreements
- State AGs have standing to challenge federal actions that harm state residents or state interests
- Litigation timelines are long — early intervention through amicus briefs is more effective than waiting

---

**THE SCRIPT:**

> "Hi, my name is [NAME] and I'm a constituent from [CITY]. I'm calling to urge [Attorney General / Governor NAME] to support and join the legal challenges to ICE warehouse construction and the federal data sharing program that gives ICE access to IRS and Social Security records. Maryland has already filed suit on the warehouse expansion. Our state should join or file an amicus brief. These legal challenges are working — please act. Thank you."

**If they ask questions:**

- *"Does our state have standing to sue?"* — "States have standing to challenge federal actions that affect state residents, state-administered programs, and state interests. The Maryland AG's legal theory is specifically designed to be replicable. An inquiry to the AG's office about whether they've reviewed it is a reasonable first step."
- *"Isn't this a federal issue?"* — "The IRS data sharing affects every state resident who files taxes. The warehouse facilities will be built in specific states and communities. Both give states legitimate grounds to act."

**Follow-up:** Ask if your state AG has reviewed the Maryland lawsuit and whether they are in contact with Maryland's office. Democracy Docket tracks active state and federal litigation — share the specific case with your contact at the AG's office.
