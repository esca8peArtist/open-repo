## Part 1: Domain 49 — Environmental Justice Thematic Pillar Architecture

### Six Thematic Pillars

**Pillar 1: Infrastructure Elimination**

What was removed, in chronological sequence, constituting a coordinated 15-month dismantlement:

- EJSCREEN removed (February 5, 2025): data access eliminated
- OEJECR closed (March 12, 2025): complaint processing eliminated
- 781 IRA EJ grants terminated (throughout 2025): community capacity eliminated
- NEPA regulations rescinded (January 8, 2026): procedural standing eliminated
- Endangerment Finding rescinded (effective April 20, 2026): regulatory hooks eliminated
- CEJST removed simultaneously with EJSCREEN: all federal EJ data tools gone

The architecture of the sequence matters: data removal first (EJSCREEN), then personnel elimination (OEJECR), then procedural rights removal (NEPA), then foundational legal authority elimination (Endangerment Finding). Each step removed a layer of the participation infrastructure, with each successive step made easier by the preceding one. This is not deregulation — it is a demolition sequence.

*New in Phase 3*: The EO April 8, 2025 directing the AG to challenge state EJ laws adds a sixth threat layer: federal preemption attack on the state-level floors that eight states have built. The demolition does not stop at the federal level — it pursues state-level replacement infrastructure.

**Pillar 2: Democratic Participation Nexus**

Five documented pathways from environmental burden to democratic disengagement (all documented in the production file):

- Pathway 1: Health burden as civic suppression (UCS vicious cycle)
- Pathway 2: NEPA participation rights as procedural gateway — now closed
- Pathway 3: Title VI administrative pathways — dismantled in two stages
- Pathway 4: Endangerment Finding as regulatory standing — removed
- Pathway 5: Environmental organizing as electoral infrastructure — connection severed

*New in Phase 3*: A sixth pathway emerges from the Phase 3 source research — the EJSCREEN data gap as information access suppression. The Aarhus Convention's first pillar (access to information) is violated by EJSCREEN removal independently of the participation and justice pillars. Communities that cannot access data documenting their pollution burden cannot effectively participate even in the informal advocacy processes that survive NEPA rescission. The Yale, Harvard, and PEDP mirror sites are third-party reconstruction efforts that document what the data removal means in practice.

**Pillar 3: Community Participation Tools**

Three case studies that demonstrate what participation rights looked like before they were removed:

- WE ACT (Northern Manhattan): Title VI complaint against MTA bus depot siting — the administrative complaint pathway as civic tool
- Deep South Center for Environmental Justice (Cancer Alley): EPA EJ coordinator technical assistance for permit challenges — the institutional support infrastructure as civic tool
- Indigenous Environmental Network (tribal land permitting): NEPA consultation requirement as tribal standing tool — the procedural right to be heard as civic tool

*New in Phase 3*: The Appalachian Citizens' Law Center adds a fourth community case study geography. Appalachian coal communities face a distinct EJ landscape: no state EJ law in most Appalachian states, no federal EJ infrastructure, and documented health consequences from mountaintop removal mining that are as severe as Cancer Alley. The 2026 Civic Information Index explicitly documents the Appalachian civic health gap — Appalachia stands out alongside the Black South as regions where voter registration does not translate to voting participation, a direct expression of the UCS vicious cycle in a different geographic context.

The Nonprofit Quarterly analysis of a queer ecological framework for EJ in Appalachia (new in Phase 3) bridges Pillars 3 and 6 and creates the unique 49-50 intersection point in Appalachian geography — LGBTQ+ people in EJ communities face compounded exclusion from both the environmental and the democratic participation dimensions simultaneously.

**Pillar 4: Live Litigation**

Four D.C. Circuit cases with specific docket numbers:

- APHA v. U.S. EPA (No. 26-1037): Earthjustice, 15 nonprofits, Alaskan tribes
- Venner v. U.S. EPA (No. 26-1038): Our Children's Trust, 18 youth
- Zero Emission Transportation Ass'n v. EPA (No. 26-1039): EV sector
- Massachusetts v. U.S. EPA (No. 26-1061): 24 states + 12 local governments

Additional litigation (new in Phase 3):
- IRA EJ Block Grant D.C. Circuit appeal: Earthjustice representing community nonprofits, tribes, and local governments; oral arguments heard March 2026
- SPEED Act opposition: If enacted, would permanently codify the NEPA rollback — Earthjustice's opposition statement documents what would be permanently eliminated

*New in Phase 3*: The Earthjustice Counter/Act Spring 2026 progress report provides the most current litigation status update as of the production date. Cite this document in the advocacy windows section to direct distribution contacts to the most current status.

**Pillar 5: International Benchmark**

The Aarhus Convention as the democratic design standard:

- Pillar 1: Access to information (EJSCREEN removal violates this)
- Pillar 2: Public participation (NEPA rescission eliminates this)
- Pillar 3: Access to justice (OEJECR closure and Title VI injunction eliminate this)

49 parties; US signed but never ratified. EU member states are legally bound — communities in Germany, France, and the Netherlands have enforceable participation rights that Cancer Alley communities no longer have in the US.

*New in Phase 3*: The Phase 3 synthesis recognizes that the Aarhus comparison works on two levels simultaneously: (1) the US failed to adopt international standards when it had the chance, and (2) the US has now actively dismantled its domestic approximations of those standards. This is a regression argument, not merely a comparison argument — the US is not just failing to meet the standard; it is moving in the opposite direction of 49 other democratic nations.

**Pillar 6: Reform Architecture**

Three-tier statutory reform framework:

- Tier 1: Immediate executive restoration (next administration)
- Tier 2: Legislative statutory anchors (NEPA amendments, EJ Offices in statute, IRA EJ entrenchment, Title VI codification)
- Tier 3: Aarhus Convention ratification (long-term)

*New in Phase 3*: The Marin Audubon v. FAA D.C. Circuit ruling (November 12, 2024) changes the reform architecture. The court held CEQ lacks authority to issue binding NEPA regulations. This means executive restoration of CEQ NEPA regulations — even under a future favorable administration — would not restore the participation rights, because Marin Audubon would immediately invalidate any new CEQ regulations. Statutory NEPA amendments are not a preferred alternative; they are the only adequate remedy. The reform architecture section must now lead with this: "Executive restoration is insufficient not only because it can be undone by the next administration, but because the D.C. Circuit has held that CEQ regulatory authority itself is legally foreclosed."

## Part 2: Domain 50 — LGBTQ+ Rights Thematic Pillar Architecture

### Six Thematic Pillars

**Pillar 1: ADF Legal Infrastructure**

ADF is a legal infrastructure project, not a culture war organization:

- Model legislation: at least 130 bills drafted in 34 states from a single coordinated effort; Idaho Fairness in Women's Sports Act as the national template for trans sports bans; South Dakota Vulnerable Child Protection Act (2020) as the direct precursor to Skrmetti
- Student Physical Privacy Act model bill mailed to "thousands" of school districts (documented in NBC News 2017 article)
- "Women's Bill of Rights" definitional legislation: defines "woman" as biological female in state law; Kansas enacted first in April 2023; Oklahoma and Nebraska executive orders in August 2023
- State AG advisory opinion pipeline: Texas AG Paxton's December 2022 advisory opinion on trans youth healthcare as model deployed across multiple states
- Ballot initiative channel: after 92%+ of anti-LGBTQ+ bills failing in state legislatures, ADF shifted strategy to qualifying ballot measures simultaneously across multiple states
- Dave Yost hire: Ohio AG resigned June 7, 2026 to join ADF as VP of Strategic Research and Innovation — the most direct evidence of institutional integration at the state AG level

*New in Phase 3*: The Yost hire is the most significant single ADF infrastructure development since the June 4 synthesis framework. It transforms the ADF state AG pipeline from a theoretical claim to a documented institutional fact: a sitting AG left public office to join ADF. This must be the lead evidence in the ADF infrastructure section.

**Pillar 2: Trans Voter Suppression**

Three compounding barriers converging in November 2026:

- Pre-existing (2024 baseline): 210,800 trans adults without matching ID in voter-ID states; 91,300 in strictest enforcement states
- EO 14168 (January 25, 2025): federal gender marker freeze growing the 210,800 figure every month
- SAVE Act (House passed February 12, 2026; Senate advocacy window still open): passport-based registration requirement adds third suppression layer

The suppression-suppression paradox: the same election cycle in which six anti-trans ballot measures are on the ballot is the cycle in which trans voters face the highest documented barriers to participating in those decisions. This is not coincidence — it is the operational logic of the suppression architecture.

*New in Phase 3*: Roll Call (April 2026) documents the deliberate Republican strategy of bundling trans issues and voter ID in a single legislative vehicle — confirming that the convergence of ballot measures and voter suppression is intentional political strategy, not coincidental overlap. Senator Padilla's June 2026 blocking effort in the Senate confirms the SAVE Act legislative window is still open.

**Pillar 3: Anti-Trans Ballot Measures**

Six anti-trans ballot measures in four states for November 2026:

- Colorado (2): trans student athlete ban; gender-affirming surgery restriction — constitutional Romer ground
- Maine (1): birth certificate-based sports eligibility — "sex definition" strategy in public accommodation
- Missouri Amendment 3 (1): trans healthcare ban bundled with abortion rights reversal — "ballot candy" strategy
- Washington (1): trans sports ban with mandatory genital examination enforcement — Fourth Amendment bodily integrity issue

The Romer v. Evans (1996) constitutional anchor: ballot initiatives cannot be used by a majority to strip a minority group of the political processes available to all others. Colorado is the primary Romer battleground because that precedent arose from Colorado's own Amendment 2 (1992). Lambda Legal will almost certainly invoke Romer in any Colorado challenge.

*New in Phase 3*: The Ballotpedia Colorado measures now have specific title documentation:
- "Colorado Prohibit Surgeries for Treatment in Response to Minors' Perception of Sex or Gender Measure (2026)"
- "Colorado Prohibit Surgeries to Alter Biological Sex Characteristics of Minors Measure (2026)"

These Ballotpedia titles are the correct primary citations for Colorado ballot measure advocacy.

**Pillar 4: Pro-Equality Ballot Measures (New Analytical Category)**

Seven pro-equality ballot measures in five states — the democratic correction mechanism:

- Vermont: equal rights amendment adding sexual orientation, gender identity to protected characteristics (legislature must pass again in 2026 session)
- Ohio (2): Equality of Rights amendment; removal of same-sex marriage constitutional ban
- Oregon (1): Equal Rights for All — affirms abortion, contraception, IVF, trans healthcare, and same-gender marriage in state constitution
- Missouri (1): abortion rights protection measure (separate from Amendment 3's abortion reversal)
- Virginia (1): removal of constitutional same-sex marriage ban

The Romer argument in positive form: if anti-trans ballot measures unconstitutionally use majority vote to remove minority protections, pro-equality measures use the same channel affirmatively to restore or establish protections. The same ballot initiative infrastructure that ADF weaponizes for anti-trans measures is being used constructively for pro-equality protections. This two-direction analysis demonstrates that the problem is not the ballot initiative mechanism itself but its weaponization against minority rights — which is exactly Romer's constitutional distinction.

*New in Phase 3*: The Oregon measure is particularly analytically significant because it bundles reproductive rights and trans rights in a single affirmative constitutional amendment — the mirror image of Missouri Amendment 3's anti-trans/anti-abortion bundle. The Oregon affirmative bundle vs. Missouri negative bundle is the clearest illustration of the ballot initiative channel being used simultaneously from both directions in the same election cycle.

**Pillar 5: Constitutional Standards**

Skrmetti (June 18, 2025) is the controlling constitutional authority:

- What it held: rational basis review applies to TN SB1 because the law classifies based on age and medical use, not sex or transgender status
- What it did not hold: trans people lack Equal Protection rights; Bostock v. Clayton County is overruled; adult care bans are constitutional
- The ADF strategic choice: by selecting a legal vehicle (age-based classification, medical use distinction) designed to avoid heightened scrutiny, ADF obtained a constitutional floor that makes virtually all anti-trans healthcare bans litigation-proof at the federal level

State court alternatives (Lambda Legal's post-Skrmetti strategy):
- Colorado state constitution's equal protection provision: potentially broader than federal floor
- Washington state constitution: independent state constitutional analysis may apply heightened scrutiny
- Missouri state constitution: the Missouri Supreme Court may apply state constitutional analysis independent of Skrmetti

Fourth Circuit extension: "If a State can reasonably ban it, of course a State can reasonably refuse to pay for it" — the roadmap sentence for extending Skrmetti to adult care bans and Medicaid coverage restrictions.

**Pillar 6: Healthcare Denial as Civic Capacity**

Three vectors of healthcare denial:

- Section 1557 rescission (effective May 14, 2025): federal nondiscrimination in healthcare eliminated for gender identity
- Skrmetti cascade: 27+ states with trans healthcare bans now face only rational basis review
- 4th Circuit extension: adult care bans and Medicaid coverage restrictions now constitutionally viable

Civic participation consequences (documented in PMC 12841032, 2026):
- Mental health crisis from sustained legislative targeting
- Economic consequences from untreated gender dysphoria reduce employment and civic engagement capacity
- Organizational attrition: Lambda Legal, AT4E, ACLU simultaneously defending 793 bills in 43 states and six ballot measures — permanent defense mode leaves no capacity for affirmative voter registration and civic mobilization

## Part 3: The 49-50 Intersection Map

### The Paired Democratic Exclusion Architecture

The June 4 synthesis framework's core analytical insight remains the organizing principle: Domains 49 and 50 document the same mechanism at two different levels of the democratic system.

Domain 49 operates at the administrative level: EPA EJ infrastructure gave frontline communities standing in the administrative apparatus — the pre-decision phase of democratic governance, where influence is cheapest and most effective. Its elimination removes communities from democratic participation before decisions are made.

Domain 50 operates at the electoral and constitutional level: trans voter suppression and anti-trans ballot measures remove targeted communities from democratic participation at the decision and accountability phase — after decisions have been made, the electoral mechanism that holds institutions responsible.

A targeted community that cannot participate in administrative decision-making (Domain 49) AND cannot vote effectively or rely on ballot processes for minority protection (Domain 50) is excluded from democratic governance at both entry points. Together, the two domains document a complete democratic exclusion architecture.

### The Six Intersection Points

**Intersection 1: Health-to-Civic Suppression as Shared Mechanism**
Domain 49's Pathway 1 (pollution → chronic illness → depressed voter turnout → political disengagement) is the same causal logic as Domain 50's Pillar 6 (healthcare denial → mental health crisis → reduced civic capacity → organizational attrition). Both document healthcare as a prerequisite for civic participation, and both document targeted removal of healthcare access as a civic suppression strategy for specific communities.

**Intersection 2: Data Access as Democratic Infrastructure**
Domain 49's EJSCREEN removal is an information access attack — the Aarhus Convention's first pillar. Domain 50's EO 14168 document freeze is an identity information attack — trans people cannot access identity documents that accurately reflect who they are. Both domains involve the deliberate removal of access to information that communities need to exercise democratic rights. The democratic design insight: information access is a prerequisite for democratic participation, and both attacks operate by targeting the specific information that targeted communities depend on.

**Intersection 3: Organizational Infrastructure Attrition**
Domain 49 documents: the EPA community liaisons and EJ coordinators were the connective tissue between regulatory advocacy and electoral mobilization — their elimination severs the connection between environmental advocacy and voter registration in EJ communities. Domain 50 documents: ADF's legislative flooding strategy (793 bills in 43 states) forces LGBTQ+ civil society organizations into permanent defense mode, leaving no capacity for affirmative voter registration and civic mobilization. Both domains document strategies that reduce targeted communities' organizational capacity for electoral participation by overwhelming their civil society infrastructure.

**Intersection 4: Geographic Co-Location in Appalachian and Black South Communities**
The 2026 Civic Information Index documents that the Appalachia and Black South gaps — regions where voter registration does not translate to voting participation — are precisely the regions where EPA EJ infrastructure was most actively used and where its elimination falls hardest (Domain 49), AND where LGBTQ+ individuals in conservative states face the most acute legal targeting (Domain 50). The Nonprofit Quarterly analysis of a queer ecological framework for EJ in Appalachia is the specific source for this intersection: LGBTQ+ people in Appalachian EJ communities face compounded exclusion from both directions simultaneously.

**Intersection 5: Missouri as the Two-Domain Convergence State**
Missouri Amendment 3 bundles trans healthcare ban with abortion rights reversal (Domain 50). The four D.C. Circuit Endangerment Finding challenges include Missouri as one of the 24 states in Massachusetts v. EPA (Domain 49). Missouri communities along the Mississippi River Chemical Corridor (Cancer Alley's northern extension) face compounded EPA EJ infrastructure elimination and ballot measure targeting. Missouri is the single state where all three attacks — EJ participation rights loss, trans voter suppression, and anti-trans ballot measure — converge in the same election cycle.

**Intersection 6: ADF and the Coordinated Democratic Erosion Project**
Domain 50 documents ADF as the organizational infrastructure driving anti-LGBTQ+ legislation, ballot measures, and judicial appointments. Domain 49 documents the Trump administration's executive mechanism for eliminating EJ infrastructure. These appear to be separate campaigns with different organizational drivers — but the domain framework's cross-reference to Domain 51 (Campaign Finance and Dark Money) and Domain 33 (State Legislative Autocratization) reveals the connection: the National Christian Foundation and its allied donor-advised fund networks fund both ADF's anti-LGBTQ+ campaign and the political infrastructure that drove the EPA rollbacks. The funding infrastructure is shared even when the organizational actors differ.

## Part 6: December 2026 Distribution Synthesis Narrative

By December 2026, the research framework will have accumulated 12–18 months of Phase 2 domain production for what will be a year-end synthesis distribution. Domains 49 and 50 contribute to that synthesis in the following ways.

### The Two-Vector Frame for Year-End Synthesis

The December 2026 synthesis distribution should frame Domains 49 and 50 as the two-vector democratic exclusion architecture that bookends the framework's analytical core:

*"The 2025–2026 attacks on democratic participation operated on two vectors simultaneously. The administrative vector (Domain 49) removed frontline communities from the pre-decision phase of governance — the formal channels through which affected residents could contest decisions before they were made. The electoral vector (Domain 50) removed targeted minorities from the post-decision accountability phase — the mechanisms through which communities hold decision-makers responsible for earlier decisions. These are not parallel campaigns; they are complementary instruments of a coordinated project. The same election cycle in which trans voters faced the highest documented barriers to casting ballots was the cycle in which six ballot measures targeting trans people's legal existence were on the ballot. The same year in which environmental justice communities lost their federal administrative standing was the year in which air, land, and climate decisions that concentrated pollution in those communities were made without the community participation that had previously been legally required. The December 2026 framework synthesis documents this not as a series of coincidences but as the operating logic of coordinated democratic erosion."*

### Post-Election Significance

If the November 2026 elections produce a Democratic wave sufficient to shift legislative priorities, Domain 49's reform architecture (Tier 2 legislative statutory anchors, Tier 3 Aarhus ratification) and Domain 50's reform architecture (Equality Act, ballot initiative process reform, judicial appointment reform) become the legislative agenda for the 120th Congress. The December 2026 synthesis should include both domains' reform architecture sections framed as immediate legislative priorities for incoming members of Congress.

If anti-trans ballot measures pass in Colorado, Missouri, Washington, or Maine, the December synthesis should document the democratic design failure: a majority voted to remove minority rights from a community that faced documented barriers to participating in that vote. This is Romer's worst case — not just a bare desire to harm, but a structurally enabled execution of that desire through a suppressed electorate. Domain 50's suppression-suppression paradox section documents exactly this scenario.

If pro-equality ballot measures pass in Ohio, Vermont, or Oregon, the December synthesis should document the democratic correction mechanism working: the same ballot initiative channel that ADF weaponized for minority rights removal was used constructively for minority rights protection. This is the affirmative version of the anti-countermajoritarian argument — democracy's self-correcting capacity still functioning, barely.

### The D.C. Circuit Timeline

By December 2026, the Endangerment Finding cases (APHA v. EPA, Massachusetts v. EPA) will either have been decided by the D.C. Circuit or will be in the oral argument phase. The December synthesis should incorporate whatever the D.C. Circuit has held, framed through the democratic participation lens rather than the environmental policy lens:

- If the D.C. Circuit upholds the rescission: "The federal court has confirmed that EPA may remove the scientific and legal predicate for community regulatory standing. The communities documented in Domain 49 have now permanently lost their federal EJ participation infrastructure absent congressional action. The statutory reform agenda in Domain 49's Tier 2 is now the only viable pathway."

- If the D.C. Circuit vacates the rescission: "The D.C. Circuit has held that the Endangerment Finding cannot be removed without adequate scientific justification. The community participation rights documented in Domain 49 — the regulatory hooks through which EJ communities generated procedural standing — have been restored by judicial decision. This victory is fragile: it depends on the D.C. Circuit's ruling surviving en banc review and potential SCOTUS certiorari. The statutory reform architecture in Domain 49's Tier 2 remains necessary to make the restoration permanent."

## Part 7: Arguments and Framing Guardrails

The June 4 synthesis framework's guardrails remain accurate and are incorporated by reference here. The Phase 3 expansion adds two additional guardrails based on sources found in Phase 3 research:

**Domain 49 — Do not conflate Marin Audubon with the CEQ rescission**:

### 9.1 The Housing-Environment-LGBTQ+ Chain: A New Causal Pathway

The existing intersection analysis identifies six intersection points, all of which operate through the lens of democratic participation. The June 10 research pass identified a seventh intersection point that operates through a different mechanism: the housing-environment vulnerability chain.

The chain runs as follows:

1. Anti-LGBTQ+ discrimination in employment and healthcare creates economic precarity for LGBTQ+ people.
2. Economic precarity creates housing instability: LGBTQ+ youth are over 120% more likely to experience homelessness; gender-expansive homelessness increased 31% from 2022-2023 (Trevor Project).
3. Housing instability concentrates LGBTQ+ people in lower-quality housing and segregated neighborhoods. The CAP analysis documents: "LGBTQ+ people often move to segregated locations to reduce discrimination, but these areas are often the most polluted."
4. Concentrated environmental exposure compounds the healthcare access crisis documented in Domain 50's Pillar 6: LGBTQ+ people already face healthcare discrimination (59% of trans people); environmental illness burden creates additional healthcare needs.

The HUD Equal Access Rule proposed rescission (April 28, 2026, Federal Register) adds a policy attack on this chain. By rescinding the requirement that HUD shelters accommodate people according to gender identity, the rule increases the risk that trans people will be turned away from emergency shelter, deepening housing instability and compounding environmental exposure.

**Intersection 7: Housing Security as the Connective Variable Between Domain 49 and Domain 50**

Domain 49 documents frontline EJ communities — disproportionately poor communities of color — as those most harmed by the elimination of EJ infrastructure. Domain 50 documents LGBTQ+ people as those most harmed by ADF-coordinated legal attacks. ### 9.4 Updated Cross-Domain Integration — SFAF v. Trump (June 9, 2026)

The San Francisco AIDS Foundation v. Trump preliminary injunction (June 9, 2026) creates a new cross-domain connection between Domain 49 and Domain 50 that was not previously mapped.

The factual basis: the court blocked key provisions of EO 14173 (the anti-DEI executive order) as applied to LGBTQ+ and HIV-serving nonprofits. EO 14173 is the same executive order used to close OEJECR (the EPA EJ office) as a "DEI" program (Domain 49, Source A4). The court held that EO 14173's provisions prohibiting federal funds from being used to "promote gender ideology" or requiring DEI office terminations are likely unconstitutional as applied to the nine plaintiff organizations.

This creates a potential legal theory for challenging OEJECR's closure: if the same EO 14173 provision that terminated DEI-framed programs at LGBTQ+ nonprofits is unconstitutional, the argument that OEJECR's closure under the same EO authority is also unconstitutional has new legal support. This does not automatically save OEJECR — the June 9 ruling applies only to the nine plaintiff organizations, not to federal agencies. But it demonstrates that courts are willing to look behind the "DEI" framing and apply constitutional analysis to the content of what was terminated.

This cross-domain development should be documented as a new activation trigger: if the SFAF v. Trump preliminary injunction survives appeal or is expanded on the merits, environmental law organizations should brief the theory that OEJECR's closure under EO 14173 is subject to the same constitutional challenge.
