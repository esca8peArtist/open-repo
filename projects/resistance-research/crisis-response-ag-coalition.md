## The AG Coalition's Structural Role

The 22-state AG coalition (documented across the litigation tracker — birthright citizenship challenge, IRS-ICE data sharing, warrantless arrest cases) is the most potent institutional legal force opposing executive overreach in 2026. As of April 2026, AGs have prevailed in approximately 350 of 362 contested federal court rulings in immigration and civil liberties matters.

The coalition's structural advantages: (1) state AGs have parens patriae standing — they can sue on behalf of their states' citizens without identifying individual plaintiffs; (2) they have litigation infrastructure (staff, expert relationships, filing capability) that can be activated within hours; (3) their coordinated multi-state filing creates political pressure that individual-plaintiff litigation does not; (4) they can bring constitutional challenges on federalism grounds (state sovereignty) that individual plaintiffs cannot.

The coalition's structural vulnerabilities: (1) the Supreme Court's 6-3 majority is willing to reverse lower court injunctions on the shadow docket, often within days of filing; (2) coordination requires agreement among 22 AGs with sometimes different political pressures; (3) state AG offices have limited capacity — they cannot litigate every crisis simultaneously at full intensity.

This guide covers how to triage, sequence, and execute AG coalition action across the five crisis scenarios.

## Standing Infrastructure: What to Have Ready Before Any Crisis

### Pre-drafted standing memos

For each crisis scenario, the coalition should have a standing memo pre-written that requires only factual details to be inserted. The Constitutional Accountability Center (theusconstitution.org) and the National Association of Attorneys General's Public Interest Section maintain model standing memos. The crisis-specific standing theories:

**Election interference**: States have sovereign authority over their own election administration under Article I, Section 4 (Times, Places and Manner clause). A federal executive action that overrides state election administration without congressional authorization violates this structural principle. *Bush v. Gore* (2000) and the post-*Crawford* line of cases establish state standing in election administration disputes. Additionally, states have standing as parens patriae for citizens' voting rights under the Fifteenth, Nineteenth, and Twenty-Fourth Amendments.

**Judicial capture (adverse SCOTUS ruling)**: After an adverse SCOTUS ruling, state AGs have standing to challenge the implementation of the ruling in ways that harm state interests — particularly if the ruling eliminates independent agency protections that state enforcement programs depend on. The *AFSCME v. SSA* standing model (Maryland AG Brown filed challenging DOGE data access because SSA capacity harm harmed Maryland's Medicaid administration) is the template.

**Executive EO**: State AGs have standing to challenge executive orders that: (1) purport to preempt state law without congressional authorization; (2) reduce federal agency capacity in ways that harm state-administered cooperative programs (Medicaid, environmental enforcement); (3) violate federal statutes that the state has an interest in seeing enforced.

**Civil service targeting**: State AGs can challenge federal civil service actions that result in reduced federal agency capacity to fulfill cooperative federalism obligations to states. This is the same *Maryland v. Noem* standing theory used in the Williamsport ICE warehouse challenge.

**Prosecutorial weaponization**: State AGs have limited standing in federal prosecution matters — the federal-state prosecutorial separation limits intervention. However: (1) state AGs can file amicus briefs in federal prosecutorial misconduct cases; (2) state AGs with ongoing joint investigations with DOJ can challenge DOJ actions that compromise those joint investigations; (3) state AGs can bring state-court actions under state civil rights statutes against the same conduct the federal prosecution is targeting.

## Crisis 1: Federal Election Interference

### Crisis Scenario for AGs

ICE deployment near polling sites, voter roll purge orders, mail ballot rejection directive, or federal interference with state election administration in the 2026 midterms.

---

### 72-Hour AG Response

**Lead state identification (hours 1-2)**

Not all 22 coalition states should file the same day. Filing sequence matters. Identify the lead state: the state with the strongest factual record of harm (the state where the federal interference is most concentrated), the strongest existing litigation infrastructure (Massachusetts AG Campbell's office or New York AG James's office are the highest-capacity), and the circuit most likely to grant emergency relief quickly (D.D.C., 9th, or 4th Circuit).

Massachusetts and New York should always be in the first wave — they have the most experienced multistate litigation teams and the most established relationships with the relevant district courts. Washington, California, Illinois, Maryland, and Colorado are the second wave, typically filing within 24-48 hours of the lead state.

**Filing within 4 hours of confirmed lead state**

The TRO motion structure for election interference:

1. **The constitutional violation**: Article I, Section 4 (state authority over election administration); 14th Amendment (voters' right to have their votes count); 15th Amendment (if the interference disproportionately affects communities of color, which ICE-at-polls almost certainly does).

2. **Irreparable harm**: Votes not cast on Election Day cannot be recovered. The irreparable harm requirement is met automatically in election interference cases — no compensatory remedy exists after the election.

3. **Balance of equities**: The state's interest in conducting a free and fair election outweighs any federal interest in the specific enforcement action. The administration will claim border security or election integrity as the federal interest; the legal record from *Hussen v. Noem* (Operation Metro Surge found to involve race-based arrests without reasonable suspicion — litigation-tracker-2026.md, Section 1.3) provides the factual rebuttal.

4. **Public interest**: There is no stronger public interest than a free and fair federal election. This element is easier to meet in election interference cases than in almost any other challenge.

**Circuit assignment strategy**

File in the circuit where the interference is occurring. For ICE-at-polls scenarios: if the operations are in a northeastern state, file in D.D.C. or the 2nd Circuit, both of which have established track records of granting election-related emergency relief. If the operations are in southwestern or mountain states, file in the 9th or 10th Circuit, where the warrantless arrest injunctions from *Ramirez Ovando v. Noem* (10th Circuit, litigation-tracker-2026.md, Section 1.2) create favorable precedent.

Avoid the 5th Circuit (Texas, Louisiana, Mississippi) for the lead filing — the 5th Circuit's track record on voting rights challenges is the most adverse. If the interference is occurring in 5th Circuit states, file in D.D.C. on the theory that the federal policy directive originated in Washington.

**Coordination with Democracy Docket and ACLU**

Simultaneous filing in multiple circuits is the counter-strategy against shadow docket reversal. The Supreme Court is less likely to issue a shadow docket stay of a TRO that is in place in four or five circuits simultaneously — the political cost of a shadow docket order that visibly allows election interference in multiple states is higher than for a single circuit. Coordinate with Democracy Docket and the ACLU Voting Rights Project to ensure parallel filings are in place within 48-72 hours.

---

### 30-Day AG Response

**Preliminary injunction: the legal standard**

The preliminary injunction motion requires the same four elements as the TRO but with a fuller factual record. Use the 14-day TRO window to:

- Take depositions of election officials documenting the specific interference
- Gather voter declarations (affidavits from eligible voters who were unable to access polling sites or whose ballots were challenged)
- Secure expert declarations on the constitutional questions from election law professors
- Build the statistical record: in jurisdictions where ICE operations occurred, compare voter turnout to the baseline from comparable elections; any suppression effect will be visible in the data

The expert declaration network: Justin Levitt (Loyola Law), Rick Hasen (UCLA Law), Franita Tolson (USC Gould), Michael Morley (Florida State Law), and the Democracy Docket research team are the established election law expert network. Contact them within the first 24 hours — their availability for declarations should be confirmed before the crisis.

**Multi-state amicus structure**

When the lead state's TRO becomes a preliminary injunction, the other coalition states should file amicus briefs within the first two weeks of the PI briefing schedule. Amicus briefs from 15+ state AGs signal to the appellate court that the constitutional question affects the entire national election system — not just one state. The Supreme Court is less likely to reverse a broadly supported lower court injunction on the shadow docket when doing so would visibly affect elections in dozens of states.

---

### 90-Day AG Response

**Post-election accountability**

If the interference occurred and caused documented voter suppression, state AGs have standing to pursue compensatory and remedial relief even after the election. The remedies available in federal election interference cases include: permanent injunctions against future similar conduct; civil penalties; and — in cases of documented racial targeting — enhanced civil rights remedies under 42 U.S.C. 1983.

The longer-term coalition action: build the pre-crisis infrastructure that prevents the next crisis from being worse. The Domain 37b election security coordination gap (loss of CISA as federal coordinator) is the structural vulnerability. State AGs with legislative partners can create interstate election security compacts that fill this coordination gap without federal authorization.

---

### Success Metrics (AG)

- Lead state TRO filed within 4 hours of confirmed federal interference
- Second-wave filings in 5 states within 48 hours
- Parallel Democracy Docket and ACLU filings in 3+ circuits within 72 hours
- Expert declarations secured within 7 days
- Preliminary injunction maintained through election
- Post-election accountability action initiated within 30 days of election if interference documented

## Crisis 2: Judicial Capture

### Crisis Scenario for AGs

Trump v. Slaughter overrules Humphrey's Executor; Watson v. RNC invalidates mail ballot grace periods; Louisiana v. Callais eliminates majority-minority districts. Or: federal district judges are subjected to impeachment proceedings for adverse rulings.

---

### 72-Hour AG Response (Adverse SCOTUS Ruling)

**Damage assessment and state impact analysis**

Within 12 hours of an adverse ruling, the AG coalition's research directors should produce a state-specific impact analysis: what does this ruling mean for each coalition state's regulatory programs? For a Humphrey's Executor overruling: which state-administered federal programs depend on NLRB, FTC, or CFPB independent enforcement capacity? For a Watson v. RNC ruling: which specific state election statutes are preempted and what is the state's mail ballot timeline for compliance before November 2026?

This state-specific impact analysis is not just for internal planning — it is the foundation of the states' injury for standing purposes in any subsequent challenge to the ruling's implementation.

**Implementation challenge theory**

Even after an adverse SCOTUS ruling, state AGs can challenge the *implementation* of the ruling. The ruling itself is precedent; specific agency actions taken pursuant to the ruling are still subject to APA notice-and-comment and due process review. If, for example, the Humphrey's Executor ruling leads immediately to mass firing of independent agency officers, those firings can be challenged under the Civil Service Reform Act and due process (property interest in continued employment) even if the constitutional question is settled against the agency officers.

For a Watson v. RNC ruling: states must change election law to comply, but they have discretion in how they do so. State AGs can advise on implementation that minimizes voter disenfranchisement within the ruling's constraints, and can challenge federal enforcement actions that go beyond what the ruling requires.

---

### 30-Day AG Response

**State analog legal action**

After a Humphrey's Executor overruling, state AGs should coordinate to bring state antitrust, consumer protection, and labor enforcement actions that substitute for the now-compromised federal enforcement. State AG offices have independent authority under state statutes — these are not dependent on federal agencies. California, New York, and Illinois in particular have strong state consumer protection and antitrust statutes that do not depend on federal agency enforcement.

The coordination mechanism: the National Association of Attorneys General (NAAG) Multistate Antitrust Task Force and the State AG consumer protection coalition have existing coordination infrastructure. Activate it within 30 days of the ruling with a specific enforcement agenda targeting the industries most affected by the loss of federal independent enforcement.

**Amicus campaign for constitutional amendment**

State AGs have standing to file amicus briefs in support of constitutional legislation — and their voices carry institutional weight in the congressional record. Within 30 days of the adverse ruling, file a joint amicus letter to the Senate Judiciary Committee supporting the constitutional amendment that would restore the stripped protection. Identify AGs from states with Republican governors or Republican-leaning states who can sign this letter — bipartisan AG support for the amendment is more politically valuable than a uniform Democratic caucus letter.

---

### Success Metrics (AG — Judicial Capture)

- State impact analysis complete within 12 hours
- Implementation challenge theory identified within 24 hours
- State analog enforcement actions initiated in 5+ states within 30 days
- NAAG enforcement coordination meeting held within 30 days
- Bipartisan amicus letter to Senate Judiciary submitted within 30 days

## Crisis 3: Executive Overreach — Emergency EO

### Crisis Scenario for AGs

Emergency EO without statutory authorization. The standing theory, filing sequence, and coordination structure follow the election interference model but with different constitutional provisions.

---

### 72-Hour AG Response

**The major questions doctrine (post-Loper Bright)**

Post-Loper Bright, courts exercise independent interpretive judgment on all statutory questions. The major questions doctrine (West Virginia v. EPA) additionally requires that agencies claiming statutory authority for "major questions" of economic and political significance identify "clear congressional authorization." For an EO invoking the National Emergencies Act or similar statute to justify action of major consequence, the major questions doctrine requires that the statutory text clearly authorize the specific action. State AGs should lead with this argument: the statutory authority claimed does not clearly authorize this action under the major questions doctrine.

This is a stronger argument post-Loper Bright than it was before, because courts no longer defer to the executive's interpretation of the relevant statute.

**The irreparable harm specificity requirement**

Emergency TROs require specific factual showing of irreparable harm. Generic claims ("the EO will harm democracy") are insufficient. State AGs must identify: (1) specific state programs that will be disrupted; (2) specific state fiscal obligations that will be affected; (3) specific classes of state residents whose rights will be violated. The Maryland v. Noem NEPA case (Williamsport warehouse TRO) is the model — AG Brown identified the specific NEPA statutes violated, the specific environmental review required, and the specific state interest in the procedural protections.

---

### 30-Day AG Response

**State anti-commandeering challenge**

If the EO attempts to use state administrative infrastructure to implement a federal objective the state opposes, invoke *Printz v. United States* and *New York v. United States*. The anti-commandeering doctrine prohibits the federal government from compelling state officials to administer federal programs. State AGs in coalition states should issue formal legal guidance to state agencies within 30 days: state agencies may not be compelled to implement federal EO directives that violate state law or state officials' legal obligations.

This is most powerful for election-related EOs, where state election officials have independent constitutional obligations. A state Secretary of State who receives a federal directive to implement an EO that violates state election law has an independent legal obligation under state law not to comply. The AG's formal legal guidance authorizes that resistance.

---

### Success Metrics (AG — EO)

- TRO filed with major questions doctrine argument within 48 hours
- Specific state harm documented in TRO affidavit within 24 hours
- Anti-commandeering guidance issued to state agencies within 30 days

## Crisis 4: Civil Service Targeting

### Crisis Scenario for AGs

Mass firing of career civil servants reduces federal agency capacity to fulfill cooperative federalism obligations to states. The standing theory: state harm from loss of federal administrative capacity.

---

### 72-Hour AG Response

**Standing: state harm from federal capacity loss**

The Maryland v. Noem model applies directly. The AGs' injury is the state's loss of a functional federal partner in administering cooperative programs. For each affected agency, identify: (1) the specific cooperative program; (2) the federal-state agreement governing it; (3) the specific federal functions that the fired employees were responsible for; (4) the state's fiscal and programmatic exposure if those functions are not performed.

File a TRO challenge to the specific firing mechanism within 48 hours. The legal theory: (1) the mass firing violates the Civil Service Reform Act, which Congress passed to establish merit-based employment protection; (2) the firings violate APA rulemaking requirements (a change in employment rules requires notice-and-comment); (3) the resultant agency dysfunction violates the federal government's obligations under cooperative federalism agreements with the states.

**Amicus support for employee litigation**

Federal employee unions (AFGE, NTEU) will file the primary litigation on behalf of employees. State AGs should file amicus briefs supporting those cases within the first week of the filing, identifying the state's independent interest in preserving federal agency capacity. This amplifies the unions' challenge and creates additional political pressure.

---

### Success Metrics (AG — Civil Service)

- State harm documentation complete within 24 hours
- Amicus support brief in employee litigation within 7 days
- TRO challenging firing mechanism filed within 48 hours (if state has direct harm)

## Crisis 5: Prosecutorial Weaponization

### Crisis Scenario for AGs

DOJ initiates a politically targeted prosecution of a civil society organization or political figure. The SPLC indictment is the established template.

---

### 72-Hour AG Response

**Amicus and state-court parallel action**

State AGs have limited standing to intervene in federal prosecutions. However: (1) state AGs can file amicus briefs in federal court supporting the defendant's motion to dismiss, identifying the state's interest in maintaining a federal prosecutorial system that does not target civil society organizations for their protected activities; (2) state AGs with pending state-law civil rights matters related to the same conduct can bring state-court actions on state civil rights theories, creating a parallel record; (3) state AGs can issue formal opinions identifying that the federal prosecution theory contradicts state civil rights law protections.

The SPLC operates in Alabama, Georgia, Florida, Louisiana, and other states where Republican AGs may not be hostile to the prosecution. But coalition state AGs in Massachusetts, New York, California, and Illinois have clear interest in defending the civil society organizations that operate in their states.

**State shield law invocation**

Several coalition states have civil society shield laws that protect nonprofit organizations' donor information, informant networks, and investigative records. State AGs should issue formal legal guidance within 72 hours identifying that any federal subpoena seeking these records in the context of the prosecution will be challenged under state law. The state AGs cannot block federal grand jury subpoenas — but they can create litigation over the scope of production, protecting some records and creating political cost for the investigation.

---

### 30-Day AG Response

**Pattern documentation for state civil rights actions**

The 22 documented retaliatory cases in Domain 29, Section 2 provide the factual foundation for a state civil rights enforcement action against federal prosecutorial misconduct. State AGs in coalition states can bring actions under state civil rights statutes against federal officials in their individual capacity for retaliatory prosecution — the same theory that underlies 42 U.S.C. 1983 actions, but under state law, which is not subject to the federal qualified immunity doctrine in the same way.

This is a novel theory that will face significant legal resistance, but the effort creates a legal record and generates political cost even if the ultimate claim fails.

**DOJ leadership referral**

State AGs can file formal complaints with the DOJ Office of Professional Responsibility (OPR) documenting prosecutorial misconduct. OPR is internally compromised in the current administration, but the complaint creates a permanent record. State bars can separately investigate whether the federal prosecutors involved have violated their professional responsibility obligations — a state bar disciplinary proceeding against a federal prosecutor is within state jurisdiction.

---

### Success Metrics (AG — Prosecutorial Weaponization)

- Amicus brief in support of motion to dismiss filed within 14 days
- State shield law guidance issued within 72 hours
- OPR formal complaint filed within 30 days
- State bar referral filed within 30 days (if prosecutorial conduct warrants)

## The Coalition Coordination Protocol

The 22-state AG coalition has an informal coordination structure that has functioned through multiple crises. The following protocol should be formalized in writing before the next crisis:

**Point of contact matrix**: For each of the five crisis scenarios, identify the lead state and the backup. Massachusetts leads election interference; New York leads financial regulation challenges; Washington leads environmental challenges; California leads immigration challenges; Maryland leads civil service and NEPA challenges. Backup states follow within 24-48 hours.

**Communication channel**: Secure channel (Signal group or equivalent) among the AG offices' civil rights deputy directors. Not email — email is subject to FOIA in most states and in some crisis scenarios may need to be treated as potentially surveilled.

**Expert network roster**: Democracy Docket's legal team; ACLU national legal team; NAACP LDF; Constitutional Accountability Center; Protect Democracy; National Immigration Law Center. Each coalition state should have a direct contact at each of these organizations.

**Shadow docket response protocol**: When the Supreme Court issues a shadow docket stay reversing a lower court injunction, the coalition's response should be immediate: (1) press statement within 2 hours; (2) compliance analysis within 6 hours (what specifically must change, what remains in place); (3) next litigation step identified within 24 hours (is there a different circuit to refile in? is there a different legal theory not reached by the stay?).
