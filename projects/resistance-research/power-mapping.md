# Power Mapping: Veto Players, Dark Money Networks, and Vulnerabilities in US Democratic Capture

*Phase 4 — Theory of Change | Document 2 of 4*
*Research conducted April 2026 | ~3,500 words*

## The Most Important Finding

The US democratic capture of 2025-2026 is not primarily a story of a single strongman or a single party. It is a story of layered infrastructure: a decades-long judicial capture project coordinated through the Federalist Society and funded by dark money conduits, merged with an aggressive executive overreach that is simultaneously dismantling civil service independence, concentrating surveillance infrastructure in loyalist hands, and testing whether courts will enforce their own orders. The vulnerabilities in this system are real — but they are time-limited. The civil service has not yet been fully captured. The federal judiciary below the Supreme Court remains substantially independent. Democratic state coalitions are winning in court. The window for recovery maps directly to the comparative framework in Document 1: the US is still in the pre-entrenchment window, but the Schedule Policy/Career reclassification, DOGE-embedded agency restructuring, and FEC quorum collapse are all tools aimed at closing it.

## Introduction: Mapping the System You Need to Uncapture

The comparative recovery framework (Document 1) establishes that democratic recovery requires identifying the specific institutional chokepoints that enable capture, the funding networks that sustain it, and the structural vulnerabilities that can be exploited during the recovery window. This document performs that mapping for the United States in 2026.

The analysis proceeds in four sections: (1) veto players — the institutional actors who can block or enable recovery; (2) dark money networks — the funding infrastructure sustaining the capture project; (3) current vulnerabilities — the weaknesses that resistance and recovery can exploit; and (4) displacement precedents — historical instances where similar entrenched systems were successfully challenged.

## Section 1: Institutional Veto Players

A veto player, in George Tsebelis's political science framework, is any individual or institutional actor whose agreement is necessary for policy change. The US system has multiple overlapping veto players, some of which have been partially captured and some of which remain genuinely independent. Understanding which is which determines where pressure is most effective.

### The Federal Judiciary

The federal judiciary is the most important and most divided veto player. Below the Supreme Court level, federal district and circuit courts have functioned as the primary institutional constraint on the Trump administration's executive overreach. As of early 2026, challengers have prevailed in approximately 350 of 362 contested federal district court rulings on immigration and civil liberties matters. Courts have blocked warrantless arrests, halted unauthorized data-sharing agreements, stopped unauthorized detention facility construction, and preserved oversight mechanisms the administration attempted to eliminate. ([Protect Democracy, Trump Administration's Conflict with the Courts, 2026](https://protectdemocracy.org/work/the-trump-administrations-conflict-with-the-courts-explained/))

At the apex, the Supreme Court's 6-3 conservative supermajority is the most significant structural problem in the federal judiciary. The court eliminated a key tool of lower courts by issuing an emergency ruling against nationwide injunctions — a procedural move that fragments resistance by requiring separate litigation in each circuit rather than one coordinated challenge. Five justices have largely permitted the administration to move forward with apparently illegal actions by overturning temporary restraining orders on technical or procedural bases without addressing the merits. ([SCOTUSblog, The Federal Court Snapback, 2026](https://www.scotusblog.com/interim-docket-blog/the-federal-court-snapback-the-judiciary-including-the-supreme-court-is-standing-up-to-the-president/))

The Supreme Court's capture has a documented architecture. Leonard Leo, co-chair of the Federalist Society and Trump's first-term judicial adviser, orchestrated a dark money funding network that raised more than $250 million between 2014 and 2017 to support the selection, confirmation, and post-confirmation cultivation of conservative justices. The Senate Judiciary Committee's investigation found that Leo steered undisclosed gifts and consulting payments to Justice Clarence Thomas's wife, arranged private jet travel for Justice Samuel Alito (paid for by a billionaire hedge fund manager), and used the 85 Fund and the Honest Elections Project as conduit entities. ([ProPublica, Leonard Leo Supreme Court Supermajority](https://www.propublica.org/article/we-dont-talk-about-leonard-leo-supreme-court-supermajority); [Sen. Whitehouse, Scheme 18: Leonard Leo's $1.6B Payday](https://www.whitehouse.senate.gov/news/speeches/scheme-18_leonard-leos-16-billion-payday/))

This is not judicial independence — it is judicial capture through undisclosed financial relationships. The vulnerability is that the capture mechanism is documented and potentially actionable: the Senate Judiciary Committee subpoena to Leo (refused in April 2024) generates a legal record that post-recovery accountability mechanisms can use. ([Leonard Leo Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonard_Leo))

**Vulnerability in the judiciary veto player:** Chief Justice John Roberts has in his 2025 year-end report stressed judicial independence as a structural value. Roberts dissented from several of the most aggressive emergency rulings. The Roberts court is not monolithic — on some issues (administrative law, procedural rules, civil liberties at the margins), individual justices retain independent professional commitments. This creates a wedge between the ideological project and the institutional self-interest of the judiciary as an institution.

### State Attorneys General

Democratic state AGs are the most functionally effective resistance institution currently operating. After Trump's 2024 election, Democratic AGs began meeting near-daily to coordinate legal strategy, having a clearer sense of what was coming from Project 2025. They have filed 71 lawsuits against the administration and won 40 of 51 resolved cases. ([Stateline, Democratic State AGs, January 2026](https://stateline.org/2026/01/07/democratic-state-ags-will-lead-opposition-to-trump-in-new-year/))

Key actions: New York AG Letitia James has filed multiple suits challenging executive orders as unconstitutional seizures of elections administration. A 22-state coalition sued to block withholding of trillions in federal funding. A separate coalition filed for summary judgment in April 2026 against the administration's attempt to control state-level elections. ([NY AG press releases, 2025-2026](https://ag.ny.gov/); [Rhode Island AG coalition, 2026](https://riag.ri.gov/press-releases/attorney-general-neronha-coalition-sue-trump-administration-over-unlawful-executive))

State AG coalitions have one structural vulnerability: they are reactive. Their power lies in blocking executive action through courts, not in affirmatively constructing alternative institutions. They are a containment mechanism, not a recovery mechanism. Their value to the recovery framework is that they are building the legal record (documented constitutional violations, court findings of racial profiling policy, evidence of executive defiance of court orders) that a post-recovery accountability process will require.

### The Civil Service and Federal Bureaucracy

The federal civil service is a veto player of a different kind: it can slow, complicate, and leak executive overreach even without formal authority. The Trump administration's characterization of "policy resistance among career civil servants" — used as justification for Schedule Policy/Career — inadvertently documents the phenomenon it is trying to eliminate. ([Government Executive, Trump Admin Moves to Finalize Schedule F, February 2026](https://www.govexec.com/workforce/2026/02/trump-admin-moves-finalize-return-schedule-f/411239/))

DOGE teams embedded in agencies from the Department of Education to USAID have conducted reviews that critics argue prioritize ideological loyalty over competence, producing large departures of subject-matter experts and documented loss of institutional knowledge. The Trump administration's plan to reclassify approximately 50,000 federal workers under Schedule Policy/Career would strip civil service protections from positions that have institutional knowledge functions. ([Federal News Network, Schedule Policy/Career, March 2026](https://wtop.com/government/2026/03/how-the-schedule-policy-career-plan-could-reshape-federal-agencies/))

The civil service is currently the veto player closest to being captured. The 2025 mass layoffs documented on Wikipedia show the scale of the restructuring already underway. The recovery implication: the institutional knowledge being lost is difficult to reconstitute. This argues for urgency in preserving civil service independence before the Schedule Policy/Career reclassification completes.

**Vulnerability:** Federal employee unions have legal standing to challenge the reclassification and have filed challenges. The Administrative Procedure Act requires notice-and-comment rulemaking that provides procedural delay opportunities. Courts have blocked related actions. The civil service veto player is not yet fully captured.

### The Military Officer Corps

The professional military officer corps is a veto player the Trump administration has managed carefully. Unlike Venezuela (where Chávez systematically purged military officers and replaced them with political loyalists) or Turkey (where the 2016 coup purges eliminated an entire generation of secular professional officers), the US military officer corps retains professional norms of political non-interference. The chain of command and civilian control framework constrains both the executive (who cannot simply use the military as an enforcement arm of domestic policy without triggering institutional resistance) and the military (which cannot unilaterally resist civilian authority).

The vulnerability in this veto player cuts both ways: the military has institutional incentives to comply with civilian authority, which makes it potentially available to an executive trying to use it for domestic enforcement. The precedent of using the National Guard for immigration enforcement has tested this boundary without breaking it. Former senior military officers — the retired general officer corps — have been more outspoken than active-duty officers, with groups like the National Security Officials and the Joint Statement signatories (2020) representing a form of institutional norm-enforcement that does not require active insubordination.

### Intelligence Agencies

The intelligence community has been partially captured through leadership appointments (DNI Tulsi Gabbard) and the 2026 Intelligence Authorization Act's removal of Senate confirmation requirements for the general counsels of the ODNI and CIA, and elimination of congressional oversight of Intelligence Community firings. ([Sen. Wyden, FY26 Intelligence Authorization Act](https://www.wyden.senate.gov/news/press-releases/wyden-votes-no-on-fy26-intelligence-authorization-act-for-removing-critical-oversight-of-intelligence-agencies/))

The key vulnerability: the Trump administration has gutted the Privacy and Civil Liberties Oversight Board — the primary body for civilian oversight of intelligence collection — while simultaneously working to extend Section 702 surveillance authority. FISA Section 702, which permits warrantless collection of communications involving Americans who interact with foreign targets, was renewed in 2026 with the support of a divided Democratic caucus that did not unify against it. ([The Intercept, Democrats Split on Domestic Spying, April 2026](https://theintercept.com/2026/04/14/democrats-trump-spying-surveillance-fisa-section-702/); [CNN, Election Security Gutted, January 2026](https://www.cnn.com/2026/01/28/politics/hacking-disinformation-election-security))

This combination — politically loyal leadership, expanded surveillance authority, reduced civilian oversight — is the intelligence community capture pattern identified in the comparative cases. Recovery requires both institutional (restore Senate confirmation of general counsels, restore PCLOB, require warrant for domestic collection) and personnel (career officer corps remains professional) dimensions.

## Section 2: Dark Money Networks

The funding infrastructure sustaining US democratic capture is the most sophisticated in the world. It operates through multiple interlocking layers designed to obscure donor identity while achieving policy, judicial, and electoral objectives.

### The Koch Network

The Koch network — founded by Charles Koch, with David Koch (d. 2019) — is the largest and most complex private political infrastructure in American history. Its operational scale: approximately 3.5 times as many employees as the Republican National Committee plus GOP congressional campaign affiliates combined. Its organizational form: a "tightly interlocked set of organizations" combining 501(c)(4) advocacy nonprofits (Americans for Prosperity), super PACs (Americans for Prosperity Action), a 50-state think tank infrastructure (State Policy Network), a model legislation factory (American Legislative Exchange Council), an academic grants program (academic institutions receiving funding for libertarian economics research), and direct electoral spending. ([Koch network Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_activities_of_the_Koch_brothers); [OpenSecrets, Koch flagship super PAC, 2024](https://opensecrets.org/news/2024/08/koch-network-flagship-super-pac-pours-big-money-into-2024-elections/))

The legal architecture enabling this structure is the Citizens United decision (2010) and its companion cases (SpeechNow v. FEC), which permitted unlimited independent expenditures through 501(c)(4) nonprofits without donor disclosure requirements. The strategic use of this architecture: donors give to 501(c)(4) "social welfare" organizations which then give to other 501(c)(4) organizations, creating multiple layers of pass-through that make ultimate donor identity nearly untraceable. ([ProPublica, How Dark Money Flows Through the Koch Network](https://projects.propublica.org/graphics/koch))

In 2024, dark money groups spent approximately $2 billion on federal elections — roughly double the 2020 total. Americans for Prosperity Action shattered its own election spending records. ([CBS News, Dark Money 2026 Midterms](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/billionaires-dark-money-fuel-questions-ahead-of-2026-midterms/); [OpenSecrets, Dark Money Surge 2024](https://www.opensecrets.org/news/2024/03/unprecedented-surge-in-dark-money-floods-2024-elections/))

**State-level capture mechanism.** The State Policy Network (SPN) sits atop a 50-state network of affiliated think tanks, described by the Center for Media and Democracy as an "$120 million empire" driving corporate-backed bills in state capitals. SPN affiliates issue reports and testify in favor of legislation, providing the intellectual infrastructure for ALEC-drafted model bills. This is regulatory capture at the state level: the mechanism by which national donor networks translate financial investment into state law. ([Energy and Policy Institute, State Policy Network](https://energyandpolicy.org/category/front-groups/state-policy-network/))

### DonorsTrust: The Dark Money ATM

DonorsTrust is the funding conduit that distributes Koch and allied donor money to operational organizations while maintaining donor anonymity. In 2024, DonorsTrust distributed $195.3 million in grants to more than 300 right-wing influence groups, litigation centers, media outlets, climate denial organizations, and pro-gun groups. Key recipients include: the Federalist Society ($3.7 million), the State Policy Network ($19.7 million), and dozens of state-level affiliates. ([EXPOSEDbyCMD, DonorsTrust $195M, November 2025](https://www.exposedbycmd.org/2025/11/21/dark-money-donor-conduit-funneled-195-million-to-right-wing-groups-in-2024/))

The mechanism is described by Senator Sheldon Whitehouse in his "Scheme" speech series as a "short jump" from traditional regulatory capture to judicial capture: the same techniques used to capture regulatory agencies (funding the intellectual infrastructure that produces agency staff and doctrine) applied to the federal judiciary through the Federalist Society's role in judicial selection. ([Sen. Whitehouse, Scheme Speech 5: The Federalist Society](https://www.whitehouse.senate.gov/news/speeches/the-scheme-speech-5-the-federalist-society/))

**Vulnerability in the dark money infrastructure.** The Federal Election Commission has been without a quorum since May 2025 — it has lacked the four members needed to act since then, with two Trump nominees awaiting Senate confirmation. A non-functional FEC cannot enforce campaign finance law. But the non-functional FEC is itself a documented structural failure: it creates the legal record that post-recovery reform legislation can use. ([Brennan Center, New Study on Dark Money Influence](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/analysis-opinion/new-study-shows-runaway-influence-dark-money-politics))

The disclosure gap is the most actionable vulnerability in this entire system. Requiring disclosure at the donor level (rather than at the grant-recipient level) would pierce the multiple layers of pass-through. The DISCLOSE Act and related legislation have been blocked repeatedly, but the legal architecture for disclosure exists — it requires only a functioning congressional majority and a president willing to sign.

### Foreign Influence Overlay

The dark money infrastructure intersects with documented foreign influence operations. China's "Cognitive Domain" operations are projected to exceed $10 billion annually in 2026, with the Chinese United Front Work Department budget projected to reach $4 billion. Chinese state media accounts have demonstrably amplified specific US political candidates. ([The Hill, China Russia Iran Influence, 2026](https://thehill.com/opinion/cybersecurity/5713097-china-russia-iran-influence/))

The Trump administration's gutting of election security infrastructure — documented by CNN's January 2026 reporting on the dismantling of cyber operation programs that had shielded the 2024 election from foreign interference — eliminates the institutional capacity to detect and counter these operations during the 2026 midterms and 2028 presidential election. ([CNN, Election Security Gutted, 2026](https://www.cnn.com/2026/01/28/politics/hacking-disinformation-election-security))

This creates a direct intersection between the domestic dark money infrastructure and foreign influence operations: both seek to shape US electoral outcomes, and the dismantling of election security oversight removes the mechanism that was separating domestic dark money from foreign interference. The recovery implication is that election security infrastructure must be rebuilt before 2028, and that the documented foreign influence operations provide bipartisan justification for that rebuilding.

### Palantir as Infrastructure Capture

Palantir Technologies requires specific attention as a privatized surveillance infrastructure embedded in multiple federal agencies. Palantir has been paid over $130 million by the IRS to analyze sensitive federal databases and has used its Lead and Case Analytics platform to aggregate federal datasets. As of April 2026, Palantir is conducting what The Intercept describes as "massive-scale" data mining for the IRS. ([The Intercept, Palantir IRS Contract, April 2026](https://theintercept.com/2026/04/24/palantir-irs-contract-data/))

Beyond specific contracts, Palantir has constructed an influence operation: a non-profit foundation, academic papers, conferences, and public commentary all oriented toward shaping Washington policy discussions in Palantir's favor, with a revolving door of veterans of the White House, Defense Department, CIA, and Congress. ([Tech Transparency Project, Inside Palantir's Expanding Influence Operation](https://www.techtransparencyproject.org/articles/inside-palantirs-expanding-influence-operation))

This is infrastructure capture of a different type: not the Koch network's model (think tanks to courts to elections) but the surveillance-capitalism model (contracts to data aggregation to institutional dependency). The recovery implication: any post-recovery institutional design must include specific provisions preventing single-vendor concentration of federal data infrastructure.

## Section 3: Vulnerabilities in the Current Entrenchment

The comparative cases (Document 1) identified that capture fails to fully consolidate when: (1) the institutional design retains mechanisms through which accountability can be imposed; (2) the elite coalition has internal divisions or material interests misaligned with continued capture; and (3) the opposition reaches the mobilization threshold before the window closes. All three conditions partially apply in the current US context.

### Judicial Vulnerability: The Roberts Independence Signal

Chief Justice Roberts's 2025 year-end report explicitly stressed judicial independence, responding to Trump administration rhetoric attacking "Trump judges" and "Obama judges." Roberts has a documented institutional self-interest in Supreme Court legitimacy — his Roberts Court Legacy Project goal of judicial reputation maintenance constrains how far he will push judicial deference to executive power. The window is: cases in 2026 that require full merits decisions (not emergency application procedural rulings) may produce different results than the early TRO dispositions. ([Washington Examiner, Roberts 2026 Report](https://www.washingtonexaminer.com/news/supreme-court/4397572/chief-justice-john-roberts-stresses-judicial-independence-busy-2026/); [Brennan Center, Courts Under Pressure](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/courts-under-pressure-judicial-independence-and-rule-law-trump-era))

The documented court defiance by the administration — ICE agents testifying they don't know whether to follow a federal court order, continued warrantless arrests after injunctions — creates contempt exposure that Roberts courts historically have not tolerated. If a significant contempt proceeding proceeds to the Supreme Court, Roberts faces a choice between institutional legitimacy and executive loyalty that his prior behavior suggests he resolves toward institutional legitimacy.

### Civil Service Vulnerability: The Knowledge Dependency

The DOGE restructuring has produced documented institutional knowledge loss that creates operational dependencies the administration cannot easily replace. Departures of subject-matter experts from the FDA, EPA, NIH, and similar agencies mean that federal agencies cannot maintain current functions without the career staff they are purging. The administration's own characterization of civil servants as resistant is evidence that they have not yet been replaced with compliant equivalents — the positions are being eliminated, not replaced.

This creates a recovery opportunity: a post-recovery government that restores civil service protections can rebuild institutional capacity from the career professionals who were purged, because their institutional knowledge was not destroyed — only their employment relationships were. The Spanish transition model is instructive here: Suárez retained Francoist bureaucratic expertise while changing the political leadership. A democratic recovery could similarly retain federal institutional knowledge through professionals who maintained their expertise outside government during the purge.

### Elite Coalition Fracture

The business community — financial sector, multinational corporations, tech industry, agricultural exporters — has material interests in legal predictability, rule of law, and stable international trade relationships that are directly threatened by the current pattern of executive overreach. Tariff unpredictability, documented in the administration's own economic data, affects corporate planning horizons. Rule-of-law deterioration affects sovereign credit ratings and capital flows. Republican senators with independent political bases and donor networks — whose long-term political survival requires being able to take positions on issues where the administration is unpopular — have occasionally defected on specific votes.

The comparative cases show that elite defection is triggered not by moral appeals but by material cost-benefit calculations. The relevant question is not "when will business elites develop principles?" but "when will the cost of continued regime support exceed the benefit?" The combination of tariff-driven economic costs and institutional unpredictability is moving business elite calculations in the direction of defection.

### The Legal Record as Future Accountability Infrastructure

Every court ruling documenting constitutional violations, every finding of racial profiling policy, every judicial order defied and documented — these create the evidentiary foundation for post-recovery accountability mechanisms. The Uruguay case shows that accountability mechanisms can be delayed (25 years, in Uruguay's case) but that the documented record enables eventual prosecution when political conditions change. The AG coalitions are building that record systematically.

## Section 4: Displacement Precedents

Captured systems have been uncaptured in the US context historically, though not at this scale or speed:

**Watergate (1972-1974).** Nixon's attempt to use the machinery of government for partisan advantage — enemies lists, IRS audits of opponents, CIA and FBI directed at domestic political targets — was dismantled through a combination of: a functioning Senate with independent institutional interests (the Senate Watergate Committee); a federal judiciary that refused to accept executive privilege claims shielding documents (United States v. Nixon, 1974, unanimous); a media environment with sufficient independence to publish the Pentagon Papers and Watergate reporting; and ultimately elite Republican defection (Barry Goldwater's delegation informing Nixon that the Senate would convict). The mechanism: cross-branch institutional resistance, documented through a legal process, ending in elite defection before the captured system completed its entrenchment.

**McCarthy Era (1950-1954).** Senator Joseph McCarthy's capture of Republican Party politics and the security state's political targeting apparatus was ended not by courts (which generally deferred to security state authority) and not by elections (McCarthy won his Senate races), but by: (a) a confrontation on live television (the Army-McCarthy hearings) that made the cost of continued association visible to business and military elites; (b) press coverage of specific human costs (Edward R. Murrow's broadcasts); and (c) Senate institutional action (the censure vote, 67-22, with Republicans crossing over). The mechanism: elite defection triggered by visible reputational cost, enabled by press and institutional confrontation.

Both precedents share the same structural feature identified in Document 1: elite defection preceded by a combination of mass perception shift (public opinion, documented costs) and institutional confrontation (courts, Senate, media). Neither precedent required a revolution. Both required that the captured system had not yet fully insulated itself from institutional challenge.

## Conclusion: The Map Points to Three Actions

The power mapping analysis converges on three findings with direct implications for the recovery framework:

**First, the judicial capture project is the most durable element of the current entrenchment.** Leonard Leo's Federalist Society architecture has placed three Supreme Court justices whose terms extend for decades. Reversing this requires either: Supreme Court expansion legislation (which requires congressional majorities), constitutional amendments establishing judicial ethics standards (which require supermajorities), or the documentation-and-accountability approach (building the case record that makes the undisclosed gifts and dark money cultivation the basis for post-recovery reform). The Roberts independence signal is the most important near-term vulnerability.

**Second, the civil service and dark money disclosures are the fastest vulnerabilities to exploit.** Schedule Policy/Career has not completed its implementation, and legal challenges have delayed it. The FEC quorum failure is documented. Both are remediable by a post-recovery government with simple legislative majorities. Both directly address the entrenchment mechanisms before they fully close.

**Third, the business elite fracture is the most important unmapped variable.** The comparative cases all involved a moment when the regime's elite coalition fractured under material cost pressure. The tariff-driven economic disruption and institutional unpredictability of 2025-2026 are creating that cost pressure for US business elites. The recovery framework needs a specific theory of how to translate that cost pressure into elite defection — and the historical precedent (Watergate, the Spain transition, South Korea 1987) suggests that visible confrontation in a legitimate institutional forum is the trigger mechanism.
