# Phase 3 Playbook: Law Schools and Clinical Programs

**Audience**: Law school deans, clinic directors, constitutional law faculty, voting rights specialists, democracy research center directors deploying institutional capacity

---

## Executive Summary

Law school clinics are the connective tissue between strategic litigation (AGs, appellate litigators) and public knowledge production (rapid-response publications, legislative record). Their leverage is threefold: they accept cases commercial firms cannot, produce law review analysis that feeds directly into brief records, and train the next generation of democratic defense attorneys. The May 2026 window is time-sensitive on three fronts. First, *Trump v. Slaughter* (expected June 2026) will determine independent agency removal protections — pre-staged academic analysis is due now, not after the decision. Second, the Callais redistricting cascade has derailed 28 active VRA lawsuits; the post-*Callais* legal landscape requires immediate academic mapping to identify residual state constitutional theories. Third, the DOJ's escalating pressure on civil society organizations — including the SPLC indictment — requires the kind of brief-quality empirical analysis that clinic-academic teams are uniquely positioned to produce. Law schools that pre-assign faculty to these three threads by June 1 will be positioned to publish within 72 hours of the *Slaughter* decision and to supply the academic record that AG amicus coalitions need within the same window. Institutions that wait for the ruling to begin analysis will be weeks late.

## Strategic Opportunities

**Opportunity 1: Pre-stage the *Trump v. Slaughter* academic record (deadline: June 15)**

*Trump v. Slaughter* will determine whether the administration can fire independent agency commissioners at will, functionally overruling or narrowing *Humphrey's Executor* (1935). Three scenarios require pre-written analysis: (1) full overruling — NLRB, FCC, FTC, SEC all become at-will removal; (2) functional narrowing — removal protections survive but are weakened to "good cause" defined by executive; (3) prospective-only application — existing commissioners protected; future appointments unprotected. Faculty teams should be assigned to each scenario now. The model is Harvard's post-*Loper Bright* operation: four analyses published within 72 hours in June 2024. The *Slaughter* decision is more consequential. Target: pre-written drafts for each scenario complete by June 1, 72-hour publication commitment in Just Security and Lawfare upon ruling.

**Opportunity 2: Map residual state constitutional theories post-*Callais***

The Supreme Court's *Callais* ruling (April 29, 2026) gutted VRA Section 2 enforcement, derailing 28 active minority voting rights lawsuits (per Democracy Docket research). The legal vacuum creates an immediate academic opportunity: identifying which state constitutions contain provisions strong enough to substitute for VRA Section 2 in redistricting and voter suppression challenges. Illinois has already initiated a state constitutional amendment explicitly requiring race-conscious redistricting. California, New York, Massachusetts, and Michigan have state constitutional provisions that may reach further than what *Callais* left of VRA Section 2. Georgetown's ICAP pioneered this theory; Yale's Rule of Law Clinic has the redistricting expertise. A systematic 50-state survey of state constitutional voting rights provisions — publishable as a law review article and immediately usable as a litigation roadmap — is the highest-value single document this coalition could produce in 2026.

**Opportunity 3: SPLC amicus coalition and prosecutorial weaponization record**

The SPLC indictment (April 21, 2026 — 11 counts including wire fraud against a civil rights organization for operating an informant program it had shared with law enforcement for decades) was assessed by multiple former federal prosecutors as legally defective at the element level. A clinic-led empirical analysis of the 22-case retaliatory prosecution dataset — charging the pattern as a systematic phenomenon, not isolated instances — becomes both a publishable article and an amicus brief in the SPLC case. Harvard's Democracy and Rule of Law Clinic, Yale's Rule of Law Clinic, and NYU's Constitutional Litigation Clinic should coordinate: three clinic teams, one shared empirical dataset, parallel publication pathways. At least five AG offices should be identified as potential amicus co-signatories before the brief is filed.

**Opportunity 4: FISA Section 702 warrant-requirement academic brief (deadline: June 12)**

The 45-day FISA extension expires June 12, 2026. The Senate remains deadlocked on the warrant requirement for U.S. person queries. Academic legal analysis on the Fourth Amendment constitutional question — whether warrantless queries of collected intelligence data violates the Fourth Amendment — is the most persuasive material available to undecided senators. Georgetown's Fourth Amendment scholars and Stanford's constitutional law faculty should coordinate a rapid publication in Just Security or Lawfare timed to the Senate floor debate.

## Implementation Pathway

**Weeks 1-2 (May 18-31): Assignment and coordination**

Each clinic director should convene a faculty briefing on three priorities: *Slaughter* pre-staging, post-*Callais* state constitutional survey, and SPLC amicus coalition. Assign faculty to each track. Pre-assign a law review article track and an amicus brief track for each — these are not the same document, but the research underlying them is identical. Confirm 72-hour publication commitment with Just Security, Lawfare, and Yale Law Journal Forum editors.

Coordinate with AG coalition: Harvard Democracy and Rule of Law Clinic, Yale Rule of Law Clinic, and NYU clinics should contact the lead AG offices (New York, Massachusetts, California) to identify which academic analysis is most urgently needed for active cases. Specifically: post-*Slaughter* standing memo drafts; post-*Callais* Section 2 successor theories; DOGE fund impoundment constitutional analysis.

**Weeks 3-8 (June 1 - July 15): Slaughter response and state survey**

Within 72 hours of the *Slaughter* decision, publish the pre-written analysis in each scenario. Coordinate placement: the full law review analysis goes to Yale Law Journal Forum; the policy-oriented version goes to Just Security; the brief-quality version is filed as an amicus brief in the first independent agency challenge that follows the ruling. The AG coalition should be standing by to file challenges in three circuits within 72 hours.

Begin the 50-state constitutional survey. This is a 3-month research project but should begin immediately and produce interim publications. Week 6: publish preliminary findings on the six states with strongest state constitutional provisions (Illinois, California, New York, Massachusetts, Michigan, Washington). Week 10: full 50-state analysis. This survey will be the single most cited academic document in redistricting litigation for the next three years.

**Weeks 9-16 (July-September): Legislative record and SCOTUS October term prep**

The Saving the Civil Service Act (H.R. 492/S. 134), NLRB Independence Act, and PRESS Act all need academic legislative findings sections. Clinic teams should produce the legislative findings drafts for each — the formal record of what the statutes address and why the constitutional basis is sound. These are the documents members cite during floor debate. Target: all three legislative findings complete by September 1, delivered to Senate sponsors.

Pre-stage academic record for SCOTUS October 2026 term. The redistricting, mail ballot, and independent agency cases expected in the October 2026-27 term require pre-filed amicus briefs. Clinic teams should confirm amicus filing slots with the ACLU Voting Rights Project and Democracy Docket for the highest-priority cases. Filing deadline: within 30 days of cert grants.

**Months 4-12: Pipeline and capacity**

Law school enrollment surge: applications to law schools with democratic defense clinics were up 18-22% following the 2024 election. This is a pipeline opportunity. Clinics that can absorb additional students into democracy-adjacent work — election law, civil service, voting rights — should expand capacity for the 2026-27 academic year. The student pipeline is both an institutional capacity resource (more clinic work) and a long-term movement building function (trained democratic defense attorneys entering practice in 2028-2030).

## Risks and Contingencies

**Risk 1: Federal funding leverage on institutions.** The Trump administration has conditioned federal research funding on institutional behavior (targeting law school clinics perceived as adversarial). Harvard, Yale, and other institutions face direct pressure. Mitigation: clinic work that is clearly within constitutional norms (amicus briefs, academic publication, student supervision of clients) is the most defensible; direct political advocacy is more vulnerable. Clinics should focus on the activities that are hardest to characterize as political.

**Risk 2: Faculty capacity concentration.** The most relevant faculty are also the most in demand for media commentary, congressional testimony, and expert witness roles. Pre-assigning faculty to specific tracks before the *Slaughter* decision prevents the scenario where the most relevant scholar is in a Senate committee hearing room when the opinion drops. Specific backup assignments are essential.

**Risk 3: Publication lead time.** Academic peer review takes 6-18 months; the democratic defense timeline requires 72-hour publication cycles. Law review forums and Just Security are the correct outlets for fast-cycle publication. Clinic directors should establish standing relationships with these editors before the *Slaughter* decision, not after.

**Risk 4: Independence from AG coalition coordination.** Clinics must maintain independence to function as credible academic authorities. Coordination means sharing research, not coordinating legal strategy. The distinction: clinics produce and publish the analysis; AGs independently decide whether to use it. No joint strategy sessions; separate legal-academic relationship.
