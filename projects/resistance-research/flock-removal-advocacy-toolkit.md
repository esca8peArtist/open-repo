## How to Use This Toolkit

This toolkit is designed for immediate use by community members, advocacy organizations, and legal counsel engaging in Flock Safety contract opposition. It contains:

1. Public Records Request template (file this first, before any public campaign)
2. Demand letter to city council (send after receiving records; before renewal vote)
3. City council testimony script (for public comment at hearings)
4. Model resolution language (for friendly council members to introduce)
5. Coalition talking points by audience
6. Houston-specific contact and action guide

All materials can be adapted by removing Houston-specific references for use in other jurisdictions.

---

## Step 1: File a Public Records Request

File this before any public organizing. The records you receive will determine your strongest arguments.

**File with**: Police Department Records Division AND City Secretary's Office (send both simultaneously)

---

**[TEMPLATE — PUBLIC RECORDS REQUEST]**

[Your Name]
[Your Address]
[Date]

[City/Police Department Records Division]
[Address]

**Re: Public Records Request — Automated License Plate Reader (ALPR) / Flock Safety Data**

Pursuant to [state public records law — Texas: Texas Public Information Act, Chapter 552, Texas Government Code], I request the following records:

1. All contracts, agreements, amendments, and renewal documents between [City Name] / [Police Department] and Flock Safety, Inc. or Flock Group, Inc., from January 1, 2020 to present.

2. All data-sharing agreements or memoranda of understanding between [Police Department] and any other law enforcement agency, federal agency, or private entity relating to Automated License Plate Reader (ALPR) data collected through the Flock Safety system.

3. All audit logs of ALPR searches conducted through the Flock Safety system by [Police Department] personnel from January 1, 2025 to present, including: timestamp, searching officer/user identifier, license plate queried, stated purpose of search, and agency identifier for any cross-agency search.

4. All communications (email, text, internal memo) between [Police Department] and Flock Safety, Inc. regarding: (a) federal agency access to locally-collected ALPR data, (b) any "pilot program" granting federal agency access, (c) the "Federal Sharing" toggle or similar administrative settings controlling federal access.

5. All communications between [Police Department] and U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE), U.S. Customs and Border Protection (CBP), or any other Department of Homeland Security component regarding ALPR data access or searches.

6. The complete list of all Flock Safety camera locations operated under or accessible to [City Name] / [Police Department] contracts.

7. All policies, general orders, or directives governing [Police Department] use of ALPR systems, including but not limited to any policy requiring officers to document search justifications.

8. Any reports, audits, or assessments of [Police Department]'s Flock Safety usage produced since January 1, 2023.

I request that these records be provided in electronic format. If any portions of requested records are withheld, please provide a written statement identifying the specific exemption claimed and the factual basis for each withholding.

[Signature]
[Contact information]

---

**What to do with the records**: Review audit logs for (a) blank or inadequate search justifications, (b) searches by agencies not listed in any data-sharing agreement, (c) searches logged under immigration-related categories ("Immigration," "ICE," "CBP"), (d) searches relating to protests, political activity, or First Amendment protected conduct. These are your strongest factual arguments.

## Step 2: Send a Demand Letter to City Council

Send before the contract renewal vote. CC the city manager, city attorney, and any public safety committee members.

---

**[TEMPLATE — DEMAND LETTER TO CITY COUNCIL]**

[Date]

Honorable [Mayor Name] and Members of the [City Name] City Council
[City Hall Address]

**Re: Urgent Request — Terminate or Restrict Flock Safety ALPR Contract / Do Not Renew**

Dear Mayor [Name] and Council Members:

We write on behalf of [Organization Name(s)] and [number] community members to request that the [City Name] City Council take immediate action to [terminate / decline to renew] the city's contract with Flock Safety, Inc. for Automated License Plate Reader (ALPR) surveillance services.

**What Flock Safety Is**

Flock Safety operates a nationwide network of over 100,000 cameras accessible to more than 4,800 law enforcement agencies. When [City Name] participates in this network, every vehicle passing a city camera is logged in a database queryable by federal immigration agencies, out-of-state police, and agencies with whom [City Name] has no agreement and over whom it exercises no oversight.

**Documented Harms**

The following harms have been documented and verified across Flock Safety's municipal client network:

1. **Unauthorized federal access**: Flock allowed U.S. Customs and Border Protection to access municipal cameras through an undisclosed "pilot program." Multiple cities discovered their plates had been searched hundreds or thousands of times by agencies they had never authorized.

2. **Immigration enforcement**: Over 4,000 searches have been documented for immigration enforcement purposes. In [Houston / your city], Alvin Independent School District cameras — installed for campus security — were searched 620 times for immigration enforcement purposes by agencies in Florida, Georgia, Indiana, and other states in a single month.

3. **Surveillance of protected activity**: Federal records obtained by the Electronic Frontier Foundation show that police departments used Flock to surveil No Kings protests (June 2025), May Day demonstrations, pro-Palestinian activism, and animal rights organizing. A Kansas police department used Flock to pursue a man who wrote a critical op-ed about the department. A Lebanon, Tennessee department searched for a woman "filming traffic stop and making comments about ICE" — both protected First Amendment activities.

4. **Abortion surveillance**: Texas law enforcement used Flock to track a woman who traveled to another state for abortion care. The search notation in the Flock system read: "had an abortion, search for female."

5. **Inadequate oversight in [City Name]**: [Insert local findings from audit log records — HPD example: Officers logged search purposes as "ASDF," "donut," "random checks," or left fields blank. The governing ALPR policy dates to 2015 and contains no documentation requirements.]

6. **Contract terms that endanger the city**: Flock's February 2026 contract revision removed the explicit prohibition on selling customer data. The company now retains perpetual, non-expiring rights to use surveillance data even after contract termination. Dispute resolution now requires private arbitration under Georgia law, removing municipal access to state courts.

**What We Request**

We request the Council take the following actions:

**Immediately (no contract termination required)**:
- Direct HPD to disable the Flock National Lookup Tool, limiting searches to locally-contracted cameras only
- Issue a written directive prohibiting use of Flock data for immigration enforcement purposes
- Require documented, specific justification for every Flock search, subject to supervisory review
- Commission an independent public audit of HPD Flock search logs from the past 24 months

**At next renewal opportunity**:
- Decline to exercise the next renewal option on the Flock contract
- Issue a request for proposals for any replacement ALPR service that includes: local-only data retention, prohibition on federal data sharing, maximum 7-day retention, and publicly reported usage logs

**For school districts** [where applicable]:
- Issue a formal communication to [Alvin ISD / Huffman ISD] Board of Trustees requesting immediate termination of Flock contracts and investigation of unauthorized federal access

**Legal Basis**

[City Name] has authority to:
- Decline to renew a sole-source contract that no longer serves the public interest
- Direct department policy changes through council resolution
- Commission independent audits of contract compliance
- Negotiate contract amendments restricting data sharing

Termination for cause may be available given documented misrepresentations during the sales process and Flock's unauthorized camera reinstallation in cities that attempted to cancel.

We request the opportunity to address the Public Safety Committee at its next hearing, and we request written confirmation that this letter has been received and will be placed in the contract renewal record.

Respectfully submitted,

[Organization Name]
[Signatory names and titles]
[Contact information]

**Enclosures**: Audit log summary; contract terms comparison (pre/post February 2026); documented cases of unauthorized access

## Step 3: City Council Testimony Script

Use at Public Safety Committee hearings or full council public comment. Standard public comment is 2-3 minutes.

---

**[TEMPLATE — CITY COUNCIL TESTIMONY (2-3 MINUTE VERSION)]**

"My name is [Name], and I represent [Organization / speak as a community member from District X].

I'm here about the Flock Safety contract. This is not an abstract privacy concern — it is a documented pattern of harm.

Flock Safety cameras installed at Alvin Independent School District, 30 minutes south of Houston, were searched 620 times for immigration enforcement purposes in a single month. The cameras were bought for campus security. The school district did not authorize this. It happened because once you're in Flock's network, any of 4,800+ agencies can search your cameras.

Houston Police Department officers have logged Flock searches with purposes listed as 'ASDF,' 'donut,' and blank fields. HPD's ALPR policy is from 2015 — before Flock existed. There is no documentation requirement, no oversight mechanism, and no accountability.

In other cities — Denver, Austin, Ithaca, Cleveland — councils voted to terminate these contracts after reviewing the same evidence. In Austin, the council eliminated over 500 Flock cameras. Hays County was the first Texas county to end its Flock contract.

What we are asking today is specific:

First: Before the next renewal decision, commission an independent audit of every HPD Flock search from the past two years. You will find the same pattern other cities found.

Second: Direct HPD to disable the National Lookup Tool immediately. That single action prevents Houston cameras from being searched by ICE and out-of-state agencies while the full audit proceeds.

Third: When the renewal comes up — and based on public records, that should be soon — decline to renew. The city is paying $6.4 million for a surveillance system that has been weaponized against our neighbors.

Eighty cities have made this choice. Houston should be the next.

Thank you."

---

**Extended version note**: If allowed 5 minutes, add: specific audit log findings from your public records request; the Huffman ISD CBP direct access incident; the February 2026 contract terms changes removing the data sale prohibition.

## Step 4: Model Resolution Language

Provide this to a friendly council member willing to introduce it.

---

**[TEMPLATE — COUNCIL RESOLUTION]**

**A RESOLUTION OF THE CITY COUNCIL OF [CITY NAME] DIRECTING REVIEW AND RESTRICTION OF AUTOMATED LICENSE PLATE READER (ALPR) SURVEILLANCE TECHNOLOGY**

WHEREAS, the City of [City Name] currently holds a contract with Flock Safety, Inc. for Automated License Plate Reader surveillance services; and

WHEREAS, documented investigations have established that Flock Safety's national network has been used to conduct immigration enforcement searches without municipal authorization; to surveil constitutionally protected protest activity; to track individuals seeking reproductive healthcare; and to conduct searches without legitimate law enforcement purpose; and

WHEREAS, Flock Safety's February 2026 contract revision removed explicit prohibitions on the sale of customer data, granted the company perpetual rights to use surveillance data after contract termination, and eliminated municipal access to state courts for dispute resolution; and

WHEREAS, [City Name]'s current ALPR policy [dates to 2015 and / does not require] documented search justifications, creating conditions for unsupervised misuse; and

WHEREAS, more than 80 municipalities nationwide have canceled, terminated, or declined to renew Flock Safety contracts based on similar findings; and

WHEREAS, the City Council has an obligation to ensure that surveillance technology contracts serve the public interest and do not facilitate harm to community members;

NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED THAT:

**Section 1 — Audit**: The City Manager shall commission an independent audit of [Police Department] Flock Safety search logs from January 1, 2024 to present. The audit shall assess: (a) adequacy of documented search justifications; (b) searches by agencies not authorized under data-sharing agreements; (c) searches for immigration enforcement purposes; (d) searches relating to First Amendment protected activity. The audit report shall be presented to the City Council within 90 days of this resolution's passage.

**Section 2 — Federal Sharing Restriction**: The [Police Department] is directed to immediately disable the Flock Safety National Lookup Tool and any administrative settings enabling access to locally-collected ALPR data by federal agencies, pending Council review of the audit findings.

**Section 3 — Immigration Enforcement Prohibition**: The [Police Department] is prohibited from sharing ALPR data with U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement, U.S. Customs and Border Protection, or any federal immigration enforcement agency, except pursuant to a valid judicial warrant or court order.

**Section 4 — Documentation Requirement**: The [Police Department] shall adopt a new ALPR policy requiring officers to provide specific, articulable law enforcement justification for every ALPR search. Searches logged without adequate justification shall be subject to supervisory review and discipline.

**Section 5 — Contract Review**: The City Manager and City Attorney shall report to the City Council, within 60 days, on: (a) the precise renewal date and option exercise deadline of the current Flock Safety contract; (b) legal grounds for early termination for cause; (c) options for contract amendment restricting data sharing; and (d) alternative ALPR vendors or non-renewal options.

**Section 6 — Transparency**: Beginning 90 days from passage, [Police Department] shall publish quarterly public reports on Flock Safety usage, including: total number of searches, number by own cameras vs. national network, number flagged with inadequate justification, number by federal agencies.

PASSED AND ADOPTED by the City Council of [City Name] on [Date].

## Part 5: Coalition Talking Points by Audience

### For Immigrant Rights Organizations
- Flock cameras are not a future threat — they are a current ICE pipeline. The Alvin ISD investigation proves that school cameras south of Houston were searched for immigration enforcement purposes 620 times in one month. Your community members' vehicles are being tracked.
- ICE does not need a direct Flock contract. They use local police to run searches on their behalf, a documented practice the ACLU calls "ICE by proxy."
- The "Federal Sharing" toggle that Flock introduced in January 2026 can be disabled by the city government — but only if the city chooses to do so. They have not chosen to.
- This connects directly to the April 2026 HPD-ICE policy change. Houston police can now hold community members for ICE. Flock data is how ICE finds them.

### For Civil Rights and Racial Justice Organizations
- ALPR systems have documented racial disparate impacts: communities of color are disproportionately surveilled, and vehicle misidentification rates are higher for vehicles associated with Black and Latino drivers.
- HPD's ALPR policy, unchanged since 2015, contains no documentation requirements. Officers enter "donut" and "random checks" as search justifications. This is the same discretionary abuse that drives traffic stop disparities.
- The April 2022 Houston ordinance requiring businesses to provide surveillance footage to police without warrants — passed months before the Flock contract — represents a surveillance-first city policy framework. The Flock contract is one element of a broader surveillance infrastructure disproportionately deployed in communities of color.

### For Reproductive Rights Organizations
- This is not hypothetical. Texas law enforcement used Flock to track a woman who traveled to Illinois for abortion care. The search notation in Flock's system: "had an abortion, search for female."
- Every trip to a reproductive healthcare clinic in greater Houston passes Flock cameras. That data is retained for 30 days. Thousands of agencies have access.
- Washington state's SB 6002 (effective March 2026) prohibits using ALPR data to track "gender-affirming treatment and reproductive health care services." Texas has no equivalent protection. The city can adopt one.

### For First Amendment and Press Freedom Organizations
- Documented: 50+ agencies used Flock to surveil No Kings protests, May Day demonstrations, pro-Palestinian organizing, and animal rights activism. Agencies entered "protest" as a search justification — not a crime.
- A Kansas police department used Flock to pursue a man who wrote a critical op-ed about the department.
- A Tennessee police department searched for a woman "filming traffic stop and making comments about ICE" — the search notation treats recording police and speaking about ICE as grounds for ALPR surveillance.
- The ACLU filed an amicus brief in *Schmidt v. Norfolk* (4th Cir., pending) arguing ALPR networks violate the First Amendment's protection against surveillance that chills speech and assembly.

### For Faith Communities
- Washington state's comprehensive ALPR law (SB 6002, March 2026) prohibits Flock data collection at places of worship. Texas has no equivalent protection.
- Your congregation's parking lot, if photographed by a Flock camera, records which members attend which services, when they arrive, and who they meet with.
- For congregations that serve undocumented community members — which describes many Houston-area churches — camera data near the church is immigration enforcement data.

### For Parent and Education Communities
- School cameras are the least expected part of this surveillance system. Alvin ISD's 8 cameras were searched 733,000 times in one month — more than the entire city of Houston's camera fleet is used in a year.
- Parents were not informed that their children's school cameras are accessible to law enforcement agencies in Florida, Georgia, Indiana, and Tennessee.
- School board members — who are elected and accountable to parents — can terminate Flock contracts. This is a voter-engagement issue: who is on your school board, and what is their position on this?

## Integration Notes

**Connection to `illegal-surveillance-camera-removal-guide.md`**: That guide covers the state law framework for private surveillance cameras and the demand-letter-to-property-owner process. Flock cameras on public streets operate under different law — the legal challenge is to the government's mass collection program, not to a specific camera's placement. Use the removal guide's state law tables (audio consent, voyeurism statutes) to identify whether any Flock camera that also captures audio triggers all-party consent requirements in your state. For Houston: Texas is a one-party consent state for audio recording (Tex. Penal Code § 16.02), so audio-capable Flock cameras do not violate state wiretap law, but federal Electronic Communications Privacy Act analysis still applies.

**Connection to `houston-ice-victim-support.md` and `ice-victim-support-networks-guide.md`**: The practical safety guidance for immigrant community members should incorporate Flock awareness: (1) assume your vehicle movements near Houston schools, businesses, and residential areas are recorded; (2) avoid vehicle routes near known camera clusters if seeking to travel privately; (3) advocate for city-level restrictions through the channels in this toolkit as the structural solution. The documented ICE-by-proxy mechanism (local police running searches on ICE's behalf) means that even cities without formal ICE agreements may be serving as immigration enforcement infrastructure through their Flock networks.

**Connection to voting and civic participation**: The protest surveillance documentation creates an evidence-based case that Flock cameras chill political organizing. For voter registration drives, political canvassing, and civic engagement events in Houston, organizers should assume that participant vehicles may be logged. This is both a practical operational security note and a political argument for council members: surveillance infrastructure that chills civic participation is incompatible with democratic governance.
