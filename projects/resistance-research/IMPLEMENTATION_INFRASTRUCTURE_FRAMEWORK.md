# Implementation Infrastructure Framework: Three-Tier Coordination for Democratic Renewal

**Exploration Queue Item 24 — Document 1 of 3**
*Produced: May 2026 | Research grounding: Phase 1 40-domain diagnostic framework + active litigation tracker*

---

## Executive Summary

The Phase 1 diagnostic framework establishes what is broken and why it matters. This document addresses the harder question: how does democratic renewal actually happen at scale, and what infrastructure is required to make it happen rather than just talking about it? The answer from comparative evidence is a three-tier coordination model — federal, state, and local — each with distinct functions, resource allocations, and institutional anchors. None of the three tiers can succeed without the others, but the order of operations matters: local organizing creates the political conditions that make state action possible; state-level wins change the federal political calculus; federal statutory reform locks in gains that prevent reversal. Movements that have tried to skip tiers — Arab Spring attempts to move directly from street mobilization to national power, Occupy's refusal to translate numbers into institutional demands — have consistently failed. Movements that built all three tiers simultaneously — Poland's 2015-2023 democratic resistance, Marriage Equality's state-by-state infrastructure — have succeeded.

The Phase 1 framework covers 40 domains of democratic dysfunction. This document focuses on how intervention across those domains is coordinated into a coherent campaign rather than a collection of individual advocacy efforts competing for resources and attention.

## I. The Implementation Problem

Democratic renewal proposals routinely identify the right problems and propose the right solutions. The graveyard of American reform is littered with technically correct proposals that failed to move because their proponents did not solve the coordination and infrastructure problems underlying implementation.

Three coordination failures recur across the case study evidence:

**Single-domain advocacy without cross-domain reinforcement.** Voting rights organizations push for ranked-choice voting while labor unions push for minimum wage increases while environmental advocates push for carbon pricing — each with their own donor bases, media strategies, and congressional relationships. The structural problems in the Phase 1 framework reinforce each other (as the cross-domain synthesis in Section 5.4 of the proposal documents). Advocacy that addresses them one at a time cannot exploit those reinforcing dynamics, and typically cannot achieve the scale needed to overcome veto players.

**Vertical silos without horizontal coordination.** Federal-level litigation and legislation happen in Washington while state-level ballot initiatives happen in Phoenix and Columbus and Richmond, while community organizing happens in individual neighborhoods — with minimal information-sharing, resource coordination, or strategic alignment between the levels. The result: federal legislators take positions that are politically impossible in their states because no one has built the local constituency that would make those positions safe; state ballot initiatives duplicate work already done in other states; community organizations are unable to translate local energy into state or federal leverage.

**Surge-and-collapse cycles.** The research archive documents a consistent pattern: crisis generates mobilization, mobilization generates some initial wins (injunctions, legislative halts, electoral gains), wins reduce urgency, funding and attention collapse before the structural changes that would prevent recurrence are achieved. This is the post-2017 Resistance pattern and the post-2020 racial justice pattern. Without infrastructure that can sustain effort through the periods between crises, even large-scale mobilization does not translate to durable change.

The three-tier model addresses all three failures by specifying which institutions coordinate which functions at which level, with what resources, and connected to which partners at adjacent levels.

## II. Theoretical Grounding: Why Three Tiers?

### The Federalism Opportunity

American federalism is simultaneously the primary mechanism of democratic backsliding (through state-level voting restrictions, gerrymandering, and minority rule entrenchment) and the primary mechanism of democratic resistance (through state AG litigation, state-level ballot initiatives, and sanctuary policies). The key insight from Bromley-Trujillo and Dichio's 2025 analysis in *Publius: The Journal of Federalism* is that "our system of federalism allows for some form of accountability even in the face of a recalcitrant Congress" — but only if states have the infrastructure to exercise that accountability consistently rather than reactively. [1]

The comparative evidence on subnational resistance is similarly double-edged. As Publius research notes, authoritarian central governments tend to punish subnational resistance — federal funding cuts, preemption legislation, administrative sanctions — meaning that state-level resistance must be coordinated enough to withstand this punishment and distributed enough that attacking one state does not collapse the coalition. [1]

### The Sequencing Problem

The domain-level research in the Phase 1 framework identifies three sequencing imperatives that drive the tier structure:

**Defensive actions must precede offensive actions.** Protecting the institutions that can be used for reform (state AGs, federal courts, independent agencies) must happen before those institutions can be leveraged for affirmative change. Federal Tier 1 infrastructure is primarily defensive in 2026; Tier 2 state infrastructure mixes defense and offense; Tier 3 local infrastructure builds the base that makes both possible.

**Electoral windows are non-renewable.** The 2026 midterm window is the most time-sensitive variable in the entire framework. Infrastructure that is not operational by September 2026 is irrelevant to the 2026 electoral calculus, even if it produces long-term value. This creates a hard sequencing constraint: Tier 3 local voter registration and mobilization infrastructure must be operational by early 2026 to have 2026 impact; state-level ballot initiative infrastructure must be operational by mid-2026 (signature gathering deadlines); federal legislative infrastructure is most valuable in the 2027-2028 legislative window after potential 2026 House gains.

**Post-electoral consolidation requires pre-electoral preparation.** The research on democratic transition (Poland 2023 is the clearest case; South Africa 1994 is another) shows that movements that did not prepare institutional designs before electoral victories consistently failed to consolidate those victories afterward. Infrastructure built now — model legislation, pre-positioned legal briefs, coalition governance structures — will be essential immediately after any 2026 electoral breakthrough, when the window for legislative action will be narrow.

## III. Tier 1 — Federal Infrastructure (40–45% Resource Allocation)

### Function and Rationale

Federal infrastructure serves three functions: (1) defending existing institutional checks against further dismantling; (2) building the legislative and regulatory capacity to move reform when the political window opens; (3) coordinating the other two tiers by providing shared research, legal analysis, and strategic framing.

The 40-45% resource allocation reflects a counterintuitive finding from the comparative research: federal-level work is both the most expensive and the most leveraged. A single successful federal court ruling can protect rights in all 50 states simultaneously. A single piece of model legislation, if well-drafted and pre-positioned, can be introduced in multiple state legislatures simultaneously. Federal infrastructure costs more per dollar than local organizing but produces disproportionate returns when it works.

### Domain 37 Anchoring: Countering Federal Election Interference

The Phase 1 Domain 37 analysis (federal executive interference in 2026 midterms) establishes the specific threat context for federal infrastructure: DOJ lawsuit campaigns against election administrators, CISA funding cuts ($39.6M confirmed), election denier appointments in 8+ senior positions, SAVE Act false-positive voter purges (81% false positive rate, 24,000+ flagged nationally). [2]

Federal Tier 1 infrastructure must be specifically organized to counter these mechanisms:

**Congressional staff networks** serve as the connective tissue between legislative offices across chambers and parties. The *Journal of Politics* research on congressional staff networks finds that members who exchange key staff are more similar in legislative effectiveness and voting patterns than would otherwise be expected — meaning that building bipartisan staff relationships creates cross-party legislative coordination capacity that operates below the partisan radar of public politics. [3] For democratic reform, this means investing in the congressional staff pipeline: fellowships that place reform-oriented staffers in key offices, shared research services that reduce the information costs of legislating on complex reform domains, and bipartisan working groups that allow staff-level coordination even when member-level coordination is politically impossible.

**Legislative drafting coalitions** address the capacity gap that consistently prevents reform. Congressional offices lack the in-house capacity to draft complex legislation across 40 domains; they depend on think tanks, law school clinics, and advocacy organizations to produce bill text. The State Policy Network's model — 50+ state think tanks coordinating model legislation for conservative causes — demonstrates what the progressive equivalent should look like, but inverted: a network of progressive policy organizations, law school clinics, and legislative counsel offices coordinating across the full domain spectrum. [4] Current progressive equivalents (Brennan Center, Center for American Progress, Economic Policy Institute) operate largely in silos. A coordinated drafting collaborative, modeled on the Marriage Research Consortium that Freedom to Marry created in 2010 to coordinate research and messaging across marriage equality organizations, would produce legislation ready to introduce within days of political windows opening. [5]

**Bureaucratic reform working groups** address the civil service crisis documented in Domain 2 (Schedule F, DOGE-driven purges). These working groups — drawing on current and former civil servants, administrative law scholars, and union representatives — should be developing the statutory language, regulatory frameworks, and litigation strategies to restore civil service protections that can be deployed immediately after any change in executive direction. The Partnership for Public Service's documentation of congressional capacity on federal workforce issues provides an existing institutional anchor for this work. [6]

**Emergency rule-making response capability** addresses the regulatory capture and emergency action threats documented across Domains 37, 38, and 40. The administration's use of NPRM emergency procedures and regulatory capture mechanisms requires a standing capacity to file public comments, engage agency proceedings, and litigate final rules within compressed timelines. Democracy Forward Foundation — a $10M MacArthur grantee — provides one model. [7]

### Institutional Anchors

**Who can absorb this work?** The critical constraint on federal infrastructure is not money but organizational capacity. The following institutions have the legal authority, staff capacity, and credibility to absorb specific federal-tier functions:

- **Brennan Center for Justice**: Election law litigation, redistricting, Supreme Court monitoring. Budget: $30M+. Can absorb expanded election integrity litigation and congressional capacity-building on voting rights.
- **Democracy Forward Foundation**: Administrative law litigation, emergency rule-making response. Can absorb systematic regulatory challenge work.
- **State Innovation Exchange (SiX)**: Already coordinates progressive state legislators across 11+ states on policy development. Provides a template and partial infrastructure for federal-state legislative coordination. Democracy Fund has invested directly in SiX. [8]
- **Congressional Progressive Caucus Center**: Provides research and staff support to progressive House members. Can serve as a hub for legislative drafting coordination.
- **American Constitution Society (ACS)**: Law school chapter network (200+ chapters) provides pipeline for reform-oriented legal talent and pre-positioned litigation research. Complements ACLU's litigation infrastructure.

### Funding the Federal Tier

Federal-tier infrastructure requires institutional-scale funding. The MacArthur Foundation's $100M democracy protection commitment, with $10M each to Campaign Legal Center and Democracy Forward, demonstrates the funding universe available. [7] The Open Society Foundations' U.S. Programs have historically funded federal advocacy infrastructure at similar scales. The Democracy Fund (Pierre Omidyar's democracy-focused foundation) invested directly in SiX and similar state coordination organizations. [8]

The key funding argument for Tier 1 is leverage: federal-tier infrastructure that produces a single successful Supreme Court amicus brief, or a piece of model legislation ready for introduction, generates returns that dwarf its cost. The comparative context helps: the $100M MacArthur commitment is 0.013% of what was spent on all U.S. elections in 2022 ($16.7B). [9]

## IV. Tier 2 — State Infrastructure (35–40% Resource Allocation)

### Function and Rationale

State infrastructure serves as the operational core of democratic renewal. States are where ballot initiatives can directly change law (bypassing both Congress and the presidency), where election rules are actually set, where courts can be captured or defended, and where the political conditions for 2026 and 2028 are being determined right now.

The 35-40% allocation reflects the density of decision-making at the state level. Of the 40 domains in the Phase 1 framework, approximately 28 have significant state-level implementation components. Voting rights (Domain 1), civil service protection (Domain 2), education (Domain 10), healthcare (Domain 11-12), housing (Domain 13), criminal justice (Domain 14), immigration enforcement response (Domain 16) — all of these are substantially determined at the state level.

### Domain 33 Anchoring: Counter-Mechanisms to State Legislative Autocratization

Domain 33 (state legislative autocratization) documents a six-state simultaneous action map showing coordinated state-level attacks on democratic governance: mid-decade redistricting in Texas and North Carolina, emergency preemption legislation in Florida and Georgia, judicial selection manipulation in Wisconsin and North Carolina. This coordinated playbook — developed by the State Policy Network and ALEC and implemented simultaneously across states — requires a coordinated counter. [4]

The counter-mechanism framework for state-level infrastructure:

**State attorney general networks** represent the most immediately effective state-level resistance tool. Democratic AGs filed 71 coordinated lawsuits against the Trump administration by early 2026, winning 40 of 51 resolved cases — a 78% success rate that represents the most effective institutional resistance mechanism currently operating. [10] The Democratic Attorneys General Association (DAGA) provides formal coordination infrastructure; the next phase of development is pre-positioning legal briefs and model complaint frameworks so that when new executive actions are announced, coordinated multi-state filings can happen within 48-72 hours rather than weeks.

The Washington State AG's Federal Litigation Tracker provides a public model for the kind of systematic monitoring and documentation infrastructure that enables rapid response. [10] California has allocated $25M specifically to litigation against Trump executive actions. Systematizing these investments — coordinating which states lead which domains, sharing litigation costs through informal cost-sharing agreements — would reduce per-state costs while maintaining the cross-jurisdictional legal record that creates circuit consensus.

**State legislature staff coordination** addresses the asymmetry between the State Policy Network's model-legislation pipeline and the progressive equivalent. SiX provides the closest current model: working in "close coordination with legislators, advocacy groups, think tanks and activists" to develop model legislation for progressive state priorities. [8] The expansion needed is domain-specific depth: model statutory language for each of the 28 state-applicable domains in the Phase 1 framework, ready for introduction in any state legislature within days.

**State supreme court capture prevention** addresses one of the most consequential and least-tracked threats in the domain framework. Wisconsin Supreme Court races, North Carolina Supreme Court races, and Ohio Supreme Court races have produced decisive swings in redistricting outcomes, voting rights protections, and environmental regulation. The litigation monitoring infrastructure currently tracks federal courts (the litigation tracker covers Category 1-10 federal cases); extending this to state supreme court monitoring requires dedicated state-level court-watchers and, more importantly, early intervention in judicial elections before incumbent capture is complete.

**Ballot initiative infrastructure** is the most powerful offensive tool in the state tier. The evidence is unambiguous: ballot initiatives have produced progressive policy wins in states where the legislature is controlled by the opposing party. Medicaid expansion passed in Idaho (2018), Nebraska (2018), Oklahoma (2020), and Missouri (2020) through ballot initiative in Republican-controlled states. [11] Minimum wage increases, abortion rights protections, criminal justice reform — all have succeeded at the ballot box in states where legislative routes were closed.

The cost structure is substantial. In 2024, total ballot initiative signature gathering costs reached a record $172M across 53 initiatives in 18 states, averaging $3.25M per successful initiative. [12] A coordinated multi-state strategy targeting 8-10 states for 2026 democracy-related ballot initiatives (ranked-choice voting, redistricting reform, voting rights restoration, Medicaid expansion) would require $30-60M in signature gathering alone, plus drafting, polling, and campaign costs. The key efficiency gain is shared infrastructure: the Marriage Equality model of shared research, shared polling, and shared messaging templates reduced per-state costs significantly compared to independent state campaigns. [5]

### Institutional Anchors

**State-level institutions with absorption capacity:**

- **National Association of Attorneys General (NAAG) / Democratic Attorneys General Association (DAGA)**: Formal coordination infrastructure for multi-state litigation already exists. The expansion needed is pre-positioning: model complaint drafting, shared expert witness networks, coordinated timing.
- **State Innovation Exchange (SiX)**: Coordinates state legislators, provides policy research and model legislation. Annual budget ~$8M. Core state-tier infrastructure anchor.
- **Ballot Initiative Strategy Center (BISC)**: Provides strategic guidance for progressive ballot measures. Has systematic knowledge of state-by-state threshold requirements, petition validation rules, and campaign management.
- **National Conference of State Legislatures (NCSL)**: Nonpartisan, but provides staff coordination infrastructure and legislative research that progressive state-tier organizations should be engaging systematically. NCSL's Center for Legislative Strengthening is relevant for staff capacity-building. [4]
- **State-based labor unions**: SEIU, UAW, AFSCME, and UFCW state councils provide ground-level organizing capacity and local political relationships that ballot initiative campaigns require. Union political spending is substantial and often under-coordinated with the broader democratic renewal framework.

### The Federalism Defense Function

Tier 2 has one additional function that cuts across the offensive work: protecting existing state institutions from federal preemption and pressure. The Trump administration's use of federal funding conditions to coerce state compliance — threatening Medicaid funds, education funds, transportation funds — requires state-level legal architecture to resist: state constitutional challenges to coercive conditions, interstate compacts that reduce federal dependency, and alternative funding mechanisms.

The German federalism model is instructive here. The German Bundesrat — the chamber representing state governments — functions as a constitutional veto point on federal legislation affecting state administration. When opposition parties control the Bundesrat, they can block or extract significant concessions on federal legislation. The U.S. equivalent is not constitutional, but state attorneys general operating in coordinated litigation coalitions can produce similar blocking effects through the courts. [13]

The key structural lesson from German federalism is that the federal-state relationship is strongest when state-level institutions have both formal coordination mechanisms (the Bundesrat) and independent fiscal capacity (states retain significant independent taxation authority). Building the U.S. equivalent — stronger interstate compacts, greater state fiscal independence — is a medium-term infrastructure goal that serves both the defensive function (reducing federal leverage over states) and the offensive function (enabling state-level policy innovation).

## V. Tier 3 — Local Infrastructure (15–20% Resource Allocation)

### Function and Rationale

The 15-20% allocation for local infrastructure reflects a structural reality: most local organizing happens through organizations that are not part of the explicit democratic renewal project — labor unions, faith communities, tenant organizations, mutual aid networks — and cannot and should not be directed by a central coordination structure. The local tier's value is not centralized execution but distributed capacity: communities with deep civic infrastructure produce higher voter turnout, more effective advocacy, stronger local media, and more resilient resistance to authoritarian pressure than communities without it.

The research on this point is robust. Zeynep Tufekci's analysis of digital-era movements documents that movements can become large quickly through digital organizing but lack the organizational depth to sustain pressure — the "critical problem" for movements that mobilize millions quickly but have no organizational infrastructure to channel that energy. [14] The Civil Rights Movement comparison is instructive: years of SCLC/SNCC chapter building, jail training, and legal defense infrastructure preceded Birmingham. The Chenoweth 3.5% threshold finding suggests sustained participation matters more than peak participation. [15]

### What Local Infrastructure Actually Means

**Community organizing** in the democratic renewal context means building organizations that pursue specific issue campaigns while simultaneously developing civic capacity: leadership training, political education, strategic thinking, public action. The Gamaliel Foundation and Industrial Areas Foundation (IAF) models demonstrate that faith-based community organizing can build durable civic infrastructure — IAF's network includes approximately 24,000 core leaders participating regularly in issue campaigns at any time. [16] These are not created quickly, but where they exist, they provide the sustained engagement that prevents surge-and-collapse cycles.

**Voter registration capacity** is the most directly measurable form of local infrastructure investment. The NAACP's 2024 civic engagement program reached 14.5M Black voters through a $20M+ investment — demonstrating the scale of organized voter contact possible through institutional networks. [17] The systemic underinvestment in NVRA automatic registration at Medicaid enrollment offices (only 1.6% of total registrations came through this channel from 2018-2020, documented in Domain 39 of the proposal) represents an institutional failure at the local/state interface that can be corrected without additional organizing: it requires administrative pressure on state agencies, not community organizing.

**Mutual aid networks** serve two democratic functions that standard political analysis underestimates. First, they build the relational fabric — trust, shared identity, reciprocal obligation — that political organizing requires. Mutual aid participants are significantly more likely to participate in political action than non-participants, because political action is a natural extension of existing social networks. Second, they provide the material resilience that allows sustained political participation: communities managing food insecurity, housing instability, and healthcare gaps cannot sustain political engagement at the levels that democratic renewal requires.

**Local media ecosystem rebuilding** addresses the infrastructure collapse documented in Domain 8: 1,800+ newspaper closures since 2004, 57% decline in newsroom employment, the resulting civic information deserts in which local government operates without accountability. Local civic media — community newsletters, local news co-ops, municipal journalism programs — is not a substitute for institutional local newspapers, but it provides partial coverage of local civic life that enables the accountability relationships democratic participation requires.

**Civic education** in the local tier means both formal civic education in K-12 (documented in Domain 10) and adult civic education through the kinds of political education programs that labor unions historically provided, faith communities still provide, and advocacy organizations provide inconsistently. The Danish folkehøjskole model (referenced in Domain 3 of the proposal) — residential adult education programs focused on civic participation and democratic skills — has no direct U.S. equivalent, but community college civic programming, labor education programs, and faith-based political education represent partial analogs that can be scaled.

### Federation Models vs. Hierarchical Models

The critical design question for local infrastructure is governance structure: federated (autonomous local organizations coordinated through shared platforms and standards) vs. hierarchical (locally-based chapters of national organizations). The evidence strongly favors federated models for democratic renewal work:

**Hierarchical models** are faster to deploy, maintain message consistency, and can marshal resources quickly for specific campaigns. But they are fragile: personality-dependent (what happens when the national leader is discredited?), funder-dependent (what happens when the foundation shifts priorities?), and unable to adapt messaging and tactics to local conditions. The Occupy movement's collapse is partly a story of horizontal structure failing, but it is equally a story of a movement that, when it needed to become something other than an encampment, had no institutional capacity to transform. [14]

**Federated models** are slower to build and produce messier, less consistent public messaging. But they are more resilient: each local organization has its own board, its own funding base, its own relationships, and its own institutional survival independent of the national network. The Alliance for Youth Action's model — 21 affiliates across 19 states, each with its own identity and approach, united by a shared federation framework — demonstrates how federated local organizations can both maintain local authenticity and achieve national coordination. [16]

The IPA (Independent Political Action) structural principle — that the most durable organizing models allow local autonomy within shared frameworks rather than requiring national direction — is consistent across the comparative research. Freedom to Marry's state coalition model gave state campaigns significant autonomy over tactics and messaging while providing shared research, polling, and legal templates through a central hub. This produced more durable state-level organizations than a fully hierarchical model would have. [5]

## VI. Multi-Tier Coordination: The Connective Tissue

### What Successful Multi-Tier Coordination Looks Like

The three tiers only produce the coordinated outcome described in this framework's thesis if there are explicit mechanisms connecting them. The comparative cases reveal what those mechanisms look like:

**Shared information infrastructure** is the minimum requirement. Each tier must have real-time visibility into what other tiers are doing: which federal litigation is in progress (so state AGs know which cases to join as amici), which ballot initiatives are being developed (so federal communications strategies can reinforce them), which local organizing campaigns are building capacity (so state-level legislative strategies can respond to demonstrated constituency). The litigation tracker, maintained as a living document updated with each significant development, is an existing example of the kind of shared information infrastructure that makes cross-tier coordination possible.

**Tiered decision-making authority** prevents coordination from becoming governance paralysis. The coalition management problem (addressed in Document 2) is that 150+ institutions with different priorities cannot make decisions by consensus on everything. The resolution is pre-agreed domains of authority: federal-tier decisions about litigation strategy (which cases to bring, which courts to prioritize) are made by the legal infrastructure institutions without requiring approval from local organizing groups; local decisions about community engagement tactics are made by local organizations without requiring federal authorization; only cross-tier strategic decisions (allocating resources between tiers, selecting electoral targets, determining messaging frameworks) require multi-tier deliberation.

**Resource flow mechanisms** must move money between tiers without creating dependency hierarchies. The federated funding model — where overhead pools are shared but operating funds come from tier-appropriate sources — is more resilient than hub-and-spoke funding (where all money flows through a national center). Freedom to Marry invested $2.4M in state campaigns across 24 states in 2014, averaging $100K per state — enough to provide capacity while not creating state-campaign dependence on national funding. [5]

### Cross-Tier Case Study: Marriage Equality 2010-2015

The marriage equality campaign is the best-documented multi-tier democratic reform campaign in recent U.S. history. Its infrastructure is directly relevant to the Phase 1 framework:

**Federal tier**: Legal strategy coordinated through Lambda Legal, ACLU, and eventually the Solicitor General's office. Academic research coordinated through the Marriage Research Consortium (Freedom to Marry, Movement Advancement Project, Basic Rights Oregon, Third Way). Legislative advocacy through the Civil Marriage Collaborative funder group. These institutions provided the research, litigation, and legislative infrastructure that state campaigns needed.

**State tier**: State campaign coalitions, established by Freedom to Marry using a consistent template (quick coalition formation, agreed financial commitments from each partner, shared infrastructure and plan). Each state coalition had a lead organization, a campaign manager, an agreed messaging framework, and access to shared polling and communications infrastructure. [5]

**Local tier**: Door-to-door canvassing, relationship-based persuasion, community organizing through LGBTQ centers, faith communities, and neighborhood organizations. The research from California's Proposition 8 campaign post-mortem directly shaped the relational organizing approach that subsequent state campaigns used.

**Coordination mechanism**: Freedom to Marry served as the convening institution that coordinated across tiers without controlling any tier. Its "roadmap to victory" — developed in 2005 with 26 top LGBTQ leaders — provided strategic alignment while leaving tactical autonomy to state and local organizations. The Civil Marriage Collaborative (funders' group) coordinated philanthropic investment across tiers without creating a central funding bottleneck.

**Result**: Federal SCOTUS litigation (Obergefell) building on a state-by-state record of legislative and ballot success, supported by a local-level shift in public opinion built through relational organizing. All three tiers contributed; no single tier could have succeeded alone.

### Cross-Tier Case Study: Poland 2015-2023

The Polish democratic resistance provides a longer-horizon, higher-stakes version of multi-tier coordination:

**Federal (national) tier**: The Civic Coalition (KO), New Left, and Third Way parties maintained institutional electoral infrastructure and explicit coordination through a "senate pact" — agreeing to field single candidates in each district rather than splitting the opposition vote. This institutional coordination at the national level was the decisive strategic choice that enabled the 2023 victory. [18]

**State (subnational) tier in the Polish context**: Major cities (Warsaw, Kraków, Wrocław, Gdańsk) governed by opposition mayors provided institutional platforms for civil society, funding for civic organizations that the national government was defunding, and symbolic resistance. Warsaw's Mayor Trzaskowski's 2020 presidential campaign demonstrated that subnational institutional power could mobilize 48.8% of the national vote — a dress rehearsal for the 2023 coalition.

**Local/civil society tier**: The Komitet Obrony Demokracji (KOD, Committee for the Defence of Democracy) mobilized 1.5M+ Polish citizens in street protests and civic action. The #SilniRazem (#StrongTogether) hashtag-driven social media campaign pressured opposition parties to coordinate. KOD was awarded the 2016 European Citizens' Prize by the European Parliament for its role. [18]

**Coordination mechanism**: The civil society tier (KOD, #SilniRazem) explicitly pressured the institutional tier (opposition parties) to coordinate — what the academic analysis terms civil society serving as a "principal" that uses "civic activism as well as wider social expectations" to force elite-level coordination. [19] This cross-tier accountability mechanism — local civil society holding national opposition accountable for coordination — is a model that the U.S. democratic renewal context should replicate.

**Key lesson**: The Polish case demonstrates that multi-tier coordination succeeds when each tier has an appropriate function and when mechanisms exist for lower tiers to hold higher tiers accountable. KOD could not run candidates or draft legislation; it could organize public pressure that made non-coordination politically costly for opposition parties. The U.S. equivalent is local and state organizing that makes non-coordination by federal-level institutions politically costly.

## VII. Failure Cases: What the Evidence Rules Out

### Failure Mode 1: Leaderless Movements Without Institutional Anchors

The Occupy movement is the canonical U.S. case. It achieved extraordinary mobilization scale in 2011 — encampments in 951 cities across 82 countries. Its explicit organizational philosophy was horizontal, consensus-based, and anti-hierarchical. What the academic analysis consistently shows is that this structure, while generating participatory energy, "often created the conditions for hierarchy" rather than preventing it, and that "process is not the same as politics." [14]

The specific failure: Occupy produced no institutional anchors, no legislative demands, and no organizational successors capable of translating mass participation into policy change. The Arab Spring demonstrates the same failure at larger scale: Tunisia's 2011 revolution succeeded because Ennahda and the secular UGTT labor union (an institutional anchor) negotiated a constitutional framework; Egypt's revolution failed precisely because the institutional anchor (the Muslim Brotherhood) was not a democracy-building organization, and the secular opposition lacked any comparable institutional infrastructure.

The Ukraine 2014 Euromaidan case is instructive in a different direction: the presence of institutional anchors (established NGOs, the Lviv-based civic infrastructure) enabled partial consolidation, but the absence of a comprehensive governance blueprint meant that Euromaidan's gains were vulnerable to subsequent elite capture and incomplete democratic consolidation.

**The applicable principle**: Numbers are necessary but not sufficient. Any democratic renewal strategy that generates mobilization without simultaneously building institutional capacity to translate that mobilization into specific policy outcomes will fail to achieve durable change.

### Failure Mode 2: Single-Issue Campaigns Without Cross-Domain Coordination

The gun safety movement post-Sandy Hook (2012-2016) demonstrates this failure. Extraordinary public mobilization, significant financial resources (Bloomberg's Everytown at $50M+ annually), and clear legislative targets — but limited engagement with the cross-domain dynamics that made gun violence a structural rather than a policy problem. The movement achieved state-level wins (red flag laws, universal background check expansions in 22 states) but made no federal legislative progress because it could not build the cross-domain coalition (healthcare + civil rights + criminal justice + economic anxiety) that the issue structure required.

The Phase 1 framework's cross-domain synthesis identifies 13 reinforcing feedback loops connecting the 40 domains. Any intervention that addresses only one domain without engaging reinforcing dynamics will face the same veto player problem that stopped gun safety legislation: the structural advantage of well-organized single-issue opposition over diffuse multi-issue coalitions.

### Failure Mode 3: Resource Concentration Without Distributed Capacity

The post-2016 "Resistance" demonstrated the resource concentration failure. Unprecedented fundraising by national organizations (ACLU raised $24M in the weekend after the 2017 immigration executive order, against a normal annual fundraising of $4M) produced excellent federal litigation and national communications, but relatively little durable local organizing capacity. By 2019, the energy had substantially dissipated without building the local infrastructure that would have sustained it.

The foundation-dependent version of this failure is well-documented in the nonprofit literature: organizations that are majority-funded by a small number of foundations are vulnerable to priority shifts, leadership transitions, and strategic pivots at those foundations. The marriage equality movement deliberately diversified its funding base — individual donors, labor unions, foundations, corporate donations — to avoid this vulnerability.

### Failure Mode 4: Personality-Dependent Coordination

Democratic renewal efforts that center on individual leaders — however effective — create structural brittleness. The Vaclav Havel/Civic Forum model was personality-dependent in ways that created long-term institutional weaknesses in Czech democracy after Havel's departure from politics. The Movement for Black Lives' transparency and accountability challenges, documented in 2025 reporting, stem partly from the difficulty of sustaining collective accountability in an organization whose public identity was partially built around specific charismatic figures.

The federated model explicitly avoids this failure mode by distributing leadership across multiple institutions with independent governance, independent funding, and independent constituencies.

## VIII. Institutional Anchors: Who Funds the Infrastructure?

### The Overhead Problem

The most persistent coordination failure in progressive organizing is the "overhead problem": funders want to fund programs, not infrastructure; organizations face competitive pressure to minimize reported overhead; the result is that coordination mechanisms — the exact functions that make multi-tier collaboration possible — are systematically underfunded.

The solution that the evidence supports is overhead pool funding: a dedicated fund, contributed to by all coalition partners at a fixed percentage of their coordination-related activities, that pays for shared communications infrastructure, shared research, shared legal services, and coordination staffing. The Civil Marriage Collaborative functioned as an informal version of this model — major funders coordinating their investments without creating a new bureaucratic structure.

### Foundation Landscape for Tier Funding

**Ford Foundation**: Annual domestic program spending of ~$500M. Democracy and government accountability is a core program area. Has funded Brennan Center, ACLU, Demos, and similar federal-tier organizations at scale.

**MacArthur Foundation**: $100M committed to democracy protection in 2025, with $10M each to Campaign Legal Center and Democracy Forward. Explicitly targeted at the threat environment described in the Phase 1 framework. [7]

**Democracy Fund**: Pierre Omidyar's democracy-focused foundation, annual grants of $40-50M. Has funded SiX (state tier), election administration reform, and journalism. Explicitly invested in the state-level infrastructure this framework prioritizes. [8]

**Open Society Foundations (OSF)**: Annual U.S. program spending of $150M+. Funds civil liberties litigation (ACLU partnership), voting rights, criminal justice reform, and civic participation across all three tiers. OSF has the broadest cross-domain scope of any major democracy funder.

**Hewlett Foundation**: Funds civic participation and democratic governance. Participatory democracy infrastructure is an explicit focus area.

**Labor Unions**: AFL-CIO, SEIU, UAW, AFSCME, and UFCW political arms spent $1.7B+ in the 2022 election cycle. Labor's infrastructure — phone banking, door-knocking, voter registration, political education — is the most durable and widely distributed civic organizing capacity in the U.S. and is systematically under-integrated into the broader democratic renewal framework. Labor's in-kind contributions (staff time, facilities, communications infrastructure) are available for democratic renewal work aligned with labor priorities.

**Small-donor online fundraising**: The 2017 Resistance demonstrated that democratic crisis generates significant small-donor fundraising. The ACLU's $24M weekend is the most cited example. A democratic renewal initiative aligned with the current crisis environment — with a credible multi-tier implementation plan rather than just a single-issue ask — could sustain significant small-donor revenue.

### The Leverage Argument

The efficiency argument for Phase 1-3 implementation is a central finding of this analysis. The total resource envelope for three-phase implementation ($7M-$16M, as detailed in Document 3) represents approximately 0.1% of what was spent on all U.S. elections in 2022 ($16.7B), [9] approximately 1% of what was raised by the ACLU in a single crisis weekend (scale that up across all democracy-focused organizations), and approximately 2% of what MacArthur alone has committed to democracy protection.

The marginal value of well-coordinated infrastructure work is not in matching the scale of electoral spending — it is in making electoral spending more effective by building the policy infrastructure, organizational capacity, and coalition coordination that converts electoral wins into durable policy change.

## IX. Implementation Sequencing

### Q3-Q4 2026 (Pre-Election): Defensive and Mobilization Functions

- Federal tier: AG coalition pre-positioned for rapid response to election interference; emergency litigation infrastructure operational; congressional staff working groups active
- State tier: Ballot initiatives on primary ballots in target states; state legislature model legislation introduced; state supreme court races funded and monitored
- Local tier: Voter registration infrastructure at maximum capacity; mutual aid networks integrated with civic participation programming; local media emergency capacity for election day coverage

### Q1-Q2 2027 (Post-Election): Consolidation Functions

- Federal tier: Legislative drafting coalitions produce first wave of model legislation for introduction in new Congress; bureaucratic reform working groups produce statutory restoration package for civil service protections
- State tier: State legislatures pass first wave of model legislation in states with favorable compositions; state AGs file consolidated challenges to any remaining executive overreach
- Local tier: Civic education programming transitions from electoral to governance focus; community organizing builds advocacy capacity for state legislative campaigns

### 2028-2030: Structural Reform Functions

- Federal tier: Constitutional reform proposals (term limits, electoral system reform, independent ethics commission) move through legislative process; federal legislation on Domains 1, 7, 8 (electoral reform, campaign finance, press freedom) introduced and debated
- State tier: Multi-state ballot initiative campaigns on electoral system reform; state constitutional convention or amendment processes for states where Article V or equivalent is available
- Local tier: Sustained civic infrastructure maintenance; local media ecosystem rebuilt; civic education integrated into K-12 curriculum

## X. Key Findings

1. **No tier works alone**: The marriage equality and Polish resistance cases demonstrate that federal, state, and local tiers are mutually reinforcing, not substitutable. Resource allocation between tiers must reflect this.

2. **State AGs are the most immediately effective federal-resistance mechanism**: 78% win rate in 71 coordinated lawsuits represents the best-performing institutional resistance mechanism currently operational. Pre-positioning infrastructure for this coalition (model complaint frameworks, shared expert witnesses, coordinated timing) produces asymmetric returns.

3. **Ballot initiatives are the most powerful offensive tool**: Medicaid expansion success in Republican-controlled states through ballot initiative demonstrates the strategic value. The $3-7M per initiative cost is substantial but competitive with comparable lobbying expenditures.

4. **Federated governance outperforms hierarchical governance for sustained multi-year campaigns**: The alliance model — autonomous local organizations united through shared frameworks — is more resilient than centralized direction.

5. **Overhead pool funding is required**: Multi-tier coordination cannot be sustained through program-only funding. Shared infrastructure costs must be explicitly budgeted and funded.

6. **Civil society holds institutions accountable for coordination**: The Polish case shows that civil society can function as a "principal" that pressures institutional actors (parties, formal organizations) to coordinate — a mechanism that requires local organizing capacity to be functional.
