# ITEM26: Domain 40 Candidates — Framework Gap Analysis and Research Roadmap

*Completed April 30, 2026. Five candidate topics evaluated for post-Domain-39 framework expansion. Each candidate fills a structural gap in the existing 39-domain architecture. Candidates are ranked using the standard four-criterion scoring matrix (urgency, coalition strength, research complexity, Phase 1 audience impact). Top two candidates receive full research roadmaps.*

## Strategic Framing: Where the 39-Domain Framework Has Gaps

The 39-domain architecture (Domains 1–37 plus Domains 38–39) is now comprehensive across electoral systems, institutional integrity, judicial independence, economic policy, information governance, rights and liberties, labor, and reproductive autonomy. The Phase 2 additions (Domains 31–39) closed specific acute-crisis gaps: Medicaid collapse, state autocratization, congressional fiscal authority, SCOTUS term preview, AI governance, foreign/transnational interference, financial sector independence, reproductive rights, and labor rights.

Three structural areas remain underresearched relative to their democratic importance:

**Area 1: Constitutional architecture and amendment process.** The framework proposes constitutional-level reforms across multiple domains (term limits for SCOTUS, RCV mandates, rights floors) without a dedicated analysis of Article V — the mechanism through which any constitutional reform must pass. The Article V threat (a Heritage-backed convention that could rewrite the Constitution without the normal checks) is an active crisis in 2026, not a theoretical one. This is the framework's most significant systemic omission.

**Area 2: Indigenous sovereignty and nation-to-nation democratic design.** The framework treats racial justice (Domain 22) and federalism (Domain 9) but has no domain engaging tribal sovereignty as a distinct constitutional category — one with treaty-law foundations that predate the Constitution itself. The Trump administration's FY2026 budget cuts $911 million (24%) from core tribal programs, directly dismantling governance capacity in Indigenous nations. This is both a human rights emergency and a democratic design question.

**Area 3: Property, economic democracy, and the civic-participation substrate.** Domain 4 (economic policy), Domain 5 (income inequality), Domain 20 (antitrust), and Domain 22 (racial justice) each touch property and economic democracy from one direction. None analyzes the property-democracy relationship as a coherent design problem — specifically, how housing instability, land speculation, and the absence of cooperative ownership erode the civic-participation infrastructure that electoral democracy requires. The Item 17 deferred candidates (Debt/Precarity and Technology Platforms) also belong in this structural assessment.

The five Domain 40 candidates below are drawn from these gaps, with a deliberate emphasis on candidates that expand coalition diversity beyond the justice-focused orientation of Domains 38–39.

## Candidate 1: Constitutional Architecture and Amendment Process Reform

### One-Line Framing

A structural analysis of the Article V amendment process — its vulnerabilities to capture, the active 2026 Convention of States threat, and the reform architecture needed to make constitutional change both accessible for democratic renewal and protected from authoritarian exploitation.

### Governance Gap

The democratic-renewal proposal proposes constitutional-level reforms across at least six domains: SCOTUS term limits (Domain 6), proportional representation (Domain 1), rights floors against state preemption (Domain 9), reproductive rights codification (Domain 39), campaign finance amendment (Domain 7), and federal civil service protection (Domain 2). Every one of these pathways runs through Article V. But the framework has no domain that analyzes Article V itself: how it works, how it has been weaponized, what reforms would make it more democratically legitimate, and how to defend against the active organized effort to call a constitutional convention in 2026.

This is not a theoretical gap. As of February 2026, the Convention of States Project has passed its application in 20 states, with 34 required to mandate a convention. The Balanced Budget Amendment effort has historically counted as many as 33 state applications toward the threshold. The constitutional dispute about whether prior applications from different periods can be aggregated — and whether rescissions are valid — means the convention threshold may already be contested as reached. The Heritage Foundation, ALEC, and Charles Koch's network have backed the Convention of States with tens of millions in dark money specifically to write a constitutional convention agenda. Congress has no clear authority to limit convention scope once called; a constitutional convention could propose amendments to anything — including the Bill of Rights.

At the same time, the progressive case for Article V is real: the Citizens United campaign finance disaster, the SCOTUS immunity doctrine, and the Senate's structural minority-rule problem (Wyoming gets as many senators as California) require constitutional-level solutions that a hostile Supreme Court has blocked at the statutory level. Wolf-PAC has passed Article V applications in five states specifically for a campaign finance amendment. The framework cannot coherently propose constitutional reform pathways without a dedicated structural analysis of the amendment process itself.

### 2026 Urgency

The Article V threat is immediate and accelerating. Three 2026 developments create an acute policy window:

First, H.Con.Res.15 was introduced in the 119th Congress (2025-2026) calling for an Article V Convention for a "Fiscal Responsibility Amendment" — a single-subject claim that constitutional scholars note would not bind convention delegates if called. The bill is a template for how Congress might attempt to call a convention in a post-2026 Republican-controlled environment.

Second, Massachusetts became the 17th state to rescind all prior Article V applications in November 2025, with Maryland, Nevada, New Mexico, and Colorado recently rescinding as well. The rescission campaign is active but legally uncertain — Article V's text does not address whether applications can be rescinded. If the convention is called before rescissions reach sufficient scale, the legal dispute itself becomes the constitutional crisis.

Third, Michigan voters will decide in November 2026 whether to join the rescission states. The outcome has national significance: if Michigan's rescission vote fails, it demonstrates that the Convention of States movement can withstand state-level democratic opposition.

### Coalition Strength

The Article V reform coalition is genuinely bipartisan in structure. The defensive coalition (opposing a runaway convention) includes Common Cause, Brennan Center, NAACP, AFL-CIO, National Education Association, and a bloc of conservative constitutionalists who oppose convention risk — including the late Justice Scalia and former Chief Justice Burger, both of whom expressed alarm at the convention mechanism. The Common Cause coalition document lists 700+ civic organizations in formal opposition to an Article V convention.

The offensive coalition (using Article V for democratic reform) includes Wolf-PAC (campaign finance), Represent.Us (anti-corruption), and academic reform advocates including Harvard Law's Lawrence Lessig. The coalition tension — progressives who want constitutional reform through Article V and progressives who fear a runaway convention — is itself a research and communication challenge the framework needs to address.

The law school network is particularly strong on this candidate: Article V scholarship is a specialized field at Harvard (Lessig), Yale (Akhil Reed Amar, who supports convention), and Georgetown (Randy Barnett, who supports it from the conservative direction). The academic divide provides the research-depth substrate needed for a credible domain treatment.

### International Precedent

The comparative evidence on amendment processes is extensive and directly relevant. Germany's Basic Law (Grundgesetz) Article 79 provides the most important precedent: it permits constitutional amendment by two-thirds supermajority in both chambers but explicitly prohibits any amendment that would "abolish the federal structure" or "affect the dignity of man" — the "eternity clauses" that make certain democratic foundations unamendable. This is the constitutional-design answer to the runaway convention problem: protect the democratic core from constitutional demolition by any process, including majority vote.

Ireland's amendment process (referendum requirement for all constitutional changes, with the Citizens' Assembly used to formulate proposals) provides the deliberative-democracy model: constitutional change requires not just legislative supermajority but direct popular vote, with a structured civic deliberation process that prevents elite capture of the amendment agenda. The 2015 same-sex marriage referendum and 2018 abortion referendum both followed this model and produced legitimate outcomes that proved durable.

South Africa's post-apartheid constitution (adopted 1996) is the most comprehensive recent precedent for constitutional design that prioritizes democratic legitimacy: the Constitutional Assembly process involved extensive public participation, the rights provisions are justiciable with the Constitutional Court as enforcer, and specific provisions require 75% supermajority for amendment — comparable to the three-quarters state ratification requirement in Article V, but with clearer procedures and a dedicated constitutional court rather than a politically-appointed Supreme Court.

### Strategic Fit with Domains 38-39

Domains 38 (Financial Sector Independence) and 39 (Reproductive Rights, Labor Rights) both propose constitutional-level changes — financial sector independence through a constitutional savings and loan guarantee, reproductive rights through a constitutional amendment codifying bodily autonomy, labor rights through a constitutional collective bargaining right. Domain 40 as Constitutional Architecture would be the structural container for all of these: the "how do we actually get there" analysis that makes the reform pathways in Domains 38-39 operationally credible rather than aspirational.

### Research Complexity

Estimated 12-16 hours. Article V scholarship is a discrete field with primary sources (the constitutional text, CRS analysis of state applications, Brennan Center tracking of convention efforts), academic literature (Yale Law Journal, Harvard Law Review, Georgetown Law), and advocacy literature from both sides. The legal complexity (rescission validity, scope limitation authority, aggregation of applications from different periods) requires careful sourcing from primary legal texts. The international comparison requires reading comparative constitutional law for Germany, Ireland, and South Africa. This is not the most difficult research project in the queue but requires methodical engagement with contested legal questions.

## Candidate 2: Tribal Sovereignty, Indigenous Democratic Traditions, and Nation-to-Nation Relations

### One-Line Framing

How the framework of tribal sovereignty — treaty rights, nation-to-nation federal obligations, and Indigenous governance traditions — constitutes a distinct democratic design category that the renewal proposal must engage as a matter of constitutional legitimacy, not only racial equity; and how the 2026 federal defunding crisis threatens to permanently impair tribal governmental capacity.

### Governance Gap

The existing framework treats Indigenous peoples through Domain 22 (Reparations and Racial Justice) — which correctly documents racial economic disparity but frames Indigenous governance as a civil rights enforcement question rather than a constitutional sovereignty question. This distinction is not semantic. Tribal nations are sovereign governments, not protected minority groups. Their relationship with the federal government is defined by treaty law — the "supreme law of the land" under Article VI — not by civil rights statutes. The Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act of 1975 established that tribal governments have the right to contract federal programs and govern their own affairs. This is governmental authority, not racial preference.

The democratic-design question is: what does a federal system that includes 574 federally recognized tribal nations look like when those nations' governmental capacity is systematically defunded? The Trump administration's FY2026 budget proposes $911 million (24%) in cuts to core tribal programs — specifically including $617 million from BIA programs supporting tribal self-governance, $107 million from BIA Public Safety and Justice programs, and elimination of the Indian Guaranteed Loan program for tribal business development. These cuts do not reduce civil rights program spending; they defund governments. The distinction matters for reform strategy: the coalition that defends tribal self-governance includes fiscal federalists and conservative constitutionalists who oppose federal preemption of governmental authority, not only civil rights advocates.

### 2026 Urgency

The urgency has two distinct components. The first is the immediate budget crisis: the FY2026 cuts, if enacted at the presidential proposal level, would be the largest single reduction in tribal governmental funding in modern history. The American Indian Higher Education Consortium describes the proposed BIE budget as "catastrophic." The Native Lands Advocacy Project finds no historic precedent for the proposed scale of BIA cuts. The Congressional Appropriations Committees have proposed higher funding levels than the presidential budget, creating a legislative window during 2026 for advocacy that could prevent the worst cuts — but that window requires a clearly articulated constitutional-sovereignty argument, not only a racial-equity argument.

The second is longer-cycle: the data sovereignty question identified at the 2026 US Indigenous Data Sovereignty Summit (400 tribal leaders and researchers, Tucson, April 2026) documents that federal data collection on tribal lands, health outcomes, and economic conditions is controlled by federal agencies rather than tribal governments. The decisions tribes make about data governance in 2026 will shape their sovereignty for decades — and the current federal AI deployment regime (Domain 36) is being imposed on tribal health and benefit systems without tribal consent protocols.

More than 300 bills affecting tribal nations were signed into law in 2025 — a 300% increase in legislative activity since 2022. This volume of legislating creates both opportunity (more vehicles for sovereignty-protective provisions) and risk (more vehicles for sovereignty-eroding ones).

### Coalition Strength

The tribal sovereignty coalition is institutionally dense. The National Congress of American Indians (NCAI) represents 574 federally recognized tribal nations and has one of the most sophisticated policy advocacy operations of any national membership organization. The Native American Rights Fund provides constitutional legal representation. The First Nations Development Institute tracks economic self-determination. The American Indian Higher Education Consortium represents tribal colleges.

The cross-ideological appeal is genuine: conservative constitutionalists who support states' rights and oppose federal preemption have structural reasons to support tribal sovereignty — it is a pre-constitutional governmental authority that the Constitution protects, not a federal program the Constitution created. The same arguments that defend state regulatory authority against federal preemption apply to tribal regulatory authority against both state and federal preemption. Senator Lisa Murkowski (R-AK) and Senator Brian Schatz (D-HI) have historically co-led bipartisan tribal legislation; law school federal Indian law clinics (at Arizona State, University of Montana, University of New Mexico) provide the academic-institutional bridge.

### International Precedent

New Zealand's Treaty of Waitangi (1840) and its implementation through the Waitangi Tribunal provides the most directly relevant international model: a treaty that created a constitutional partnership between the Crown and Maori nations, with a permanent quasi-judicial body to hear and resolve treaty breaches, and with treaty principles embedded in New Zealand legislation. The tribunal's ability to compel Crown engagement with historical claims — and the 2023 Maori Health Authority (which gave Maori governance authority over their own health services) — demonstrate what genuine constitutional partnership looks like institutionally.

Canada's Section 35 of the Constitution Act (1982) constitutionally recognizes and affirms existing Aboriginal treaty rights — a model for US constitutional reform that would give treaty rights explicit constitutional status rather than leaving them dependent on congressional willingness to honor them. Canada's duty-to-consult framework (emerging from Haida Nation v. British Columbia, 2004) requires meaningful consultation with Indigenous peoples before government decisions that affect their rights. Nordic countries' Sami Parliament model offers the governance-within-governance design: an elected Sami Parliament with advisory and co-management authority over reindeer herding, language, and cultural resources, operating alongside national parliaments.

### Strategic Fit with Domains 38-39

Domains 38-39 are oriented toward systemic economic and reproductive justice. Tribal sovereignty is the most structurally distinct gap relative to these domains — it expands the coalition into federalist-conservative territory, addresses a governance category (nation-to-nation treaty obligations) entirely absent from the framework, and provides the most internationally robust comparative precedent for a non-racial-preference approach to Indigenous rights. The distinction between equity-framing (Domain 22) and sovereignty-framing (Domain 40 as tribal sovereignty) is exactly the coalition-diversification the Phase 2 expansion requires.

### Research Complexity

Estimated 10-14 hours. The primary source materials are accessible: CRS reports on tribal self-determination authorities, BIA program documentation, NCAI policy briefs, Native News Online budget analysis, and the University of Montana AIGPI executive-order white paper (published 2025). The constitutional law foundation (plenary power doctrine, trust responsibility, treaty interpretation) is specialized but well-documented in Federal Indian Law treatises. The international comparison (New Zealand Waitangi Tribunal, Canadian Section 35, Nordic Sami Parliament) requires dedicated research hours. The 2026 budget crisis provides a concrete current-events hook that makes the research accessible to a non-specialist audience.

## Candidate 3: Congressional Fiscal Authority Restoration and Power-of-the-Purse Reform

### One-Line Framing

How the executive branch has systematically dismantled congressional control over federal spending through impoundment, emergency-authority reprogramming, and DOGE-driven unilateral fund cancellation — and what statutory and constitutional reforms would restore the Appropriations Clause as an operational check on executive power.

### Governance Gap

Domain 34 covers congressional fiscal authority in outline form. Domain 2 addresses civil service protection. Domain 28 addresses war-powers funding. None of these fully engages the scope of the fiscal-authority dismantlement underway in 2026. The CBPP has documented that the Trump administration has, for the first time ever, announced its intention to misuse the Impoundment Control Act to illegally withhold funding until it expires, whether Congress acts or not. Representative Andrew Clyde (R-GA) introduced H.R.1180 in February 2025 — a one-sentence bill to repeal the Impoundment Control Act of 1974 entirely. H.R.4229, also in the 119th Congress, would require prior congressional approval before the GAO can bring a civil action under the ICA — stripping the enforcement mechanism. These bills, taken together, reveal the administration's legislative roadmap for making impoundment permanent and unaccountable.

The fiscal authority question is foundational: every other reform in the framework presupposes a Congress that controls the purse. If the executive branch can refuse to spend appropriated funds, reprogram funds to unauthorized purposes, and defund agencies through executive action, then a Democratic Congress after 2026 cannot implement any of the reforms proposed in Domains 1-39. Congressional fiscal authority is the implementation infrastructure for democratic renewal, not merely one reform domain among others.

### 2026 Urgency

The crisis is ongoing and accelerating. The FY2026 Continuing Appropriations Act was specifically designed with anti-impoundment provisions, permanently turning off fast-track procedures that would allow partisan rescission of bipartisan funding agreements. Congress put ICA provisions directly into law rather than report language precisely because the administration had demonstrated willingness to ignore report language. This arms race between congressional appropriators and executive impoundment is the 2026 fiscal governance crisis. The HHS Medicaid reprogramming, the DOGE fund cancellations, and the DEO budget actions are all variants of the same underlying constitutional question: does the Appropriations Clause create enforceable spending obligations, or merely spending permissions?

The Brookings 50th anniversary analysis of the Congressional Budget and Impoundment Control Act (published 2024) is a timely source: it documents the ICA's design assumptions — that presidential impoundment would be rare and good-faith — and the ways in which those assumptions have failed. The GAO's ICA enforcement page documents the legal framework; the missing piece is what enforcement looks like when the executive branch treats GAO conclusions as advisory rather than binding.

### Coalition Strength

This is the most cross-partisan candidate in the set. Republican fiscal hawks who support congressional control of spending — the Rand Paul bloc, the House Freedom Caucus members concerned about executive unilateralism — have structural reasons to support ICA restoration. Congressional Democrats who want to implement a reform agenda after 2026 have tactical reasons. The Taxpayers for Common Sense analysis of the FY26 Legislative Branch Appropriations Act (which jeopardizes congressional oversight) documents the bipartisan oversight interest. Law school administrative law programs (Cornell, George Washington, Georgetown) have the constitutional-law expertise; budget think tanks (Brookings, CBPP, CBO) have the fiscal analysis.

The challenge: the coalition runs into the short-term interest problem. Republican members who support executive impoundment when their party holds the presidency will reverse their position when it does not. This is the perennial problem of fiscal authority reform — the instrument is always more popular in opposition than in power.

### International Precedent

The UK's Supply and Appropriation Acts require annual parliamentary approval of each spending line; the Treasury cannot reallocate funds without parliamentary authorization and secondary legislation. The German Basic Law Article 112 prohibits expenditures not approved in the Federal Budget Act — the executive cannot reprogram funds, only propose supplementary budgets to the Bundestag. Canada's Department of Finance requires parliamentary appropriation for any change in departmental spending above 5% of the voted appropriation. These parliamentary models do not translate directly to the US separation-of-powers context, but they document what genuinely enforceable appropriations control looks like — contrast with the US system where the executive branch has been able to routinely delay, reprogram, and cancel spending with limited consequence.

### Strategic Fit with Domains 38-39

Fiscal authority restoration is the implementation infrastructure that makes all other reform domains operational. Domain 38 (Financial Sector Independence) proposes institutional insulation of financial regulators; that insulation requires funding independence from executive reprogramming. Domain 39 (Reproductive Rights, Labor Rights) requires enforcement agencies (NLRB, FDA, EEOC) that cannot be defunded by executive action. This candidate is the enabling infrastructure for the entire Phase 2 reform agenda, not an additional substantive area — which is both its strength (essential) and its limitation (less politically mobilizing than domains with clear victim constituencies).

### Research Complexity

Estimated 8-10 hours. The primary source materials are well-organized: CRS reports on the ICA, CBPP budget analysis, GAO ICA enforcement documentation, the Brookings 50th anniversary analysis, and the congressional bill texts (H.R.1180, H.R.4229). The constitutional law question (Appropriations Clause scope, Train v. City of New York as the controlling precedent, how Loper Bright affects administrative deference in ICA cases) requires careful sourcing but is not a specialized-access problem. This is the most immediately researchable candidate and requires the fewest hours.

## Candidate 4: Property Rights, Economic Democracy, and the Civic Participation Substrate

### One-Line Framing

How land ownership concentration, housing instability, and the absence of cooperative and community ownership models suppress the civic participation infrastructure that electoral democracy requires — and what a property-democracy reform architecture looks like in the community land trust, worker cooperative, and land value tax tradition.

### Governance Gap

This candidate was identified in ITEM12 as a strong Candidate 4 with a direct housing-turnout causal chain and growing CLT/co-op sector, but ranked fourth due to the real estate political economy barrier. Returning to it for Domain 40 evaluation is warranted because: (a) the 2026 housing affordability crisis has intensified (Shelterforce documented in March 2026 that solutions require confronting inequality as structural, not incidental); (b) the cooperative movement has accelerated post-2026, with the Democracy at Work Fund and USFWC scaling training programs specifically framed as democratic participation infrastructure; and (c) the domain expansion mandate for Item 26 specifically asks for candidates that diversify coalition composition beyond the justice-focused Domains 38-39 orientation.

The 40 million Americans experiencing housing insecurity face a structural disenfranchisement mechanism that is legally invisible but empirically powerful: proof of address requirements for voter registration mean that residential mobility generated by eviction, foreclosure, or unaffordable rent removes voters from the rolls without a single suppression law being passed. The Urban Institute documents this pathway. The Lincoln Institute identifies 308 community land trusts across 48 states — a growing institutional infrastructure for addressing housing instability as a civic-participation barrier, not merely a housing-affordability problem.

The governance gap is specific: no domain in the framework addresses property as a democratic design question. The question is not whether housing is affordable but whether the ownership structure of land and housing creates or destroys the civic engagement substrate. A CLT structurally prevents the speculative displacement that generates residential mobility and voter roll churn. A worker cooperative structurally builds democratic governance habits that correlate with higher electoral participation. A land value tax structurally redistributes publicly created land value to community benefit rather than allowing it to compound in private hands. These are governance interventions, not housing-policy adjustments.

### 2026 Urgency

The housing affordability crisis is at its worst documented level. The Shelterforce analysis (March 2026) documents that 49.7% of renters are cost-burdened — the highest rate in the dataset. The private equity influence in single-family and multi-family housing has accelerated; the National Community Reinvestment Coalition estimates that institutional investors now own 3-5% of all single-family homes in the US, concentrated in Sun Belt markets. This ownership concentration has a direct voter-registration effect: renters move more frequently when evicted or priced out, and each move triggers a registration disruption.

The cooperative moment is also at a peak: the United States International Year of Cooperatives 2025 raised the profile of the cooperative model nationally. The Democracy at Work Fund (2025-2026) is a strategically designed funding initiative specifically aimed at connecting worker cooperative organizing to democratic participation. The USFWC now represents nearly 450 business and organizational members representing an estimated 1,300 worker co-ops and 15,000 workers. The Nonprofit Quarterly essay "What's Next? Why Co-ops Are Essential in the Fight for Democracy" (2025) explicitly frames cooperative economics as democratic infrastructure — a framing the framework should incorporate.

### Coalition Strength

The coalition for property-democracy reform is organically diverse. The community land trust movement draws from racial justice organizing (CLTs have Civil Rights Movement roots), labor organizing (union pension funds are the largest institutional investors in CLT projects), rural development (rural cooperatives have existed since the 1930s New Deal), and conservative small-government advocates who prefer decentralized community ownership to federal housing programs. The CUNY School of Labor and Urban Studies Community and Worker Ownership Project provides the academic-institutional anchor. The New Economy Coalition and Democracy Collaborative provide national coordinating infrastructure.

The limitation identified in ITEM12 remains real: the real estate industry is the single largest sector of state-level campaign finance, and land value reform directly threatens the speculative model. But the cooperative and CLT angle does not require confronting the real estate industry directly — it builds alternative ownership infrastructure that reduces speculative displacement without prohibiting investor activity. This partial end-run around the political-economy barrier is the coalition argument for prioritizing the CLT/cooperative angle over the full land value tax reform, which requires a legislative majority willing to impose significant tax changes on property holders.

### International Precedent

The Vienna Wiener Wohnen model — 220,000 units of permanently affordable public housing for 60% of the city's residents — is the most referenced international case. Domain 13 (Housing) already covers this. More directly relevant for Domain 40's community-democracy framing is the Danish cooperative housing model: over 20% of Danish housing stock is organized as cooperative or non-profit housing, with democratic internal governance of buildings and neighborhoods. The Danish model is paired with high voter turnout (85%+) and high levels of civic trust — a correlation that Danish researchers have attributed partially to the practice of democratic governance in everyday economic institutions.

Scotland's Community Land Right to Buy (Land Reform (Scotland) Act 2003, expanded 2016) gives communities a pre-emptive right to purchase land when it comes up for sale, preventing speculative transfer. Over 500,000 acres of Scottish land are now under community ownership. This is the international evidence for the framework's theoretical claim that property ownership structures shape democratic participation.

## Candidate 5: Technology Platforms, Democratic Infrastructure, and Section 230 Reform

### One-Line Framing

How the architecture of digital platforms — algorithmic recommendation, content moderation, data portability, and interoperability — constitutes the primary information infrastructure through which Americans access political information and organize politically; and what platform-democracy reform would require legally, technically, and institutionally in the 2026 Section 230 policy window.

### Governance Gap

This candidate was evaluated in ITEM17 (Domain 39 candidates) and ranked fourth — below Reproductive Rights, Labor Rights, and Debt/Precarity — due to coalition fragmentation and the more diffuse connection to 2026 electoral urgency. For Domain 40, it merits re-evaluation for two reasons: first, the Section 230 legislative window has sharpened significantly in 2026 (Durbin-Graham sunset bill is active; the EFF's April 2026 analysis on the Open Social Web and Section 230 documents the emerging interoperability angle); second, the platform-democracy nexus has intensified through the documented Meta political content deprioritization and the X moderation collapse.

Domain 36 (AI Governance and Algorithmic Accountability) addresses AI decision-making in government. Domain 8 (Media and Free Press) addresses journalism. Neither addresses digital platforms as the infrastructure through which the political information environment is constructed. 71% of Americans get news from social media; platform algorithms determine which political information reaches which voters. This is not the same problem as media consolidation (Domain 8) or government AI use (Domain 36); it is a question of who governs the architecture of political information at scale.

The 2026 Section 230 legislative window is the specific policy hook. Senators Durbin and Graham have introduced legislation to sunset Section 230 in two years, forcing a comprehensive legislative redesign. This would be the most significant change to internet governance since Section 230's enactment in 1996. The bipartisan nature of the reform effort (Democrats want platform accountability for harmful content; Republicans want to protect political speech from content moderation) means there is floor consideration possible in a way that pure progressive reforms would not have.

### 2026 Urgency

The Section 230 sunset bill creates an active legislative timeline. If enacted, the sunset would force comprehensive platform governance legislation by 2028 — which overlaps precisely with the 2028 presidential election cycle. The platform governance rules that Congress designs in 2026-2028 will govern the 2028 election information environment. The EFF's April 2026 analysis documents that the Open Social Web (Mastodon, Bluesky, Threads' ActivityPub implementation) requires Section 230 protection to survive the transition from centralized platforms — reform that eliminates Section 230 without creating safe harbors for decentralized alternatives could concentrate political information infrastructure in the three dominant platforms rather than enabling genuine competition.

The EU's Digital Services Act (DSA), which became binding for large platforms in February 2024, provides the most current real-world evidence: platforms with over 45 million EU users must disclose algorithmic recommendation systems, conduct risk assessments for societal effects, and provide audit access to researchers. The DSA transparency reports are the first systematic evidence base for platform governance — and the data shows that platforms responding to DSA requirements have disclosed previously hidden political content deprioritization mechanisms.

### Coalition Strength

Coalition fragmentation is the persistent constraint. Democrats want accountability for harmful content (disinformation, hate speech, child exploitation). Republicans want protection from political moderation. Civil libertarians want neither liability regime. Tech workers want labor protections. Consumer advocates want data portability. These four constituencies rarely vote the same way on platform legislation, producing the pattern of reform proposals that pass committee and die on the floor.

The strongest cohering angle for the democratic-renewal audience is the algorithmic transparency and interoperability argument: not "regulate content" (which breaks the coalition) but "require transparency about how the algorithm curates political information and require portability so users can access alternative recommendation systems." This framing attracts civil libertarians (transparency, not censorship), consumer advocates (portability = competition), and researchers (access to algorithmic data). The EU DSA provides the international template and demonstrates that this middle path is technically and legally feasible without destroying platform economics.

### International Precedent

The EU Digital Services Act is the strongest and most current international precedent. The UK Online Safety Act (2023) creates risk assessment and transparency obligations with different enforcement mechanisms. Germany's NetzDG (2017) created obligations for illegal speech removal, with documented over-moderation side effects that inform the reform design. Taiwan's Digital Ministry model — treating platform governance as electoral infrastructure subject to the same transparency requirements as physical election administration — is the most radical internationally but represents the clearest statement of the problem: if platforms are the architecture through which democratic participation occurs, they should be governed as democratic infrastructure, not as private communications utilities.

### Research Complexity

Estimated 12-16 hours. The technical and legal literature is specialized. The Section 230 legal scholarship requires engagement with communications law (Georgetown, Harvard, Columbia law reviews). The DSA implementation evidence requires reading EU Commission transparency report summaries and civil society analyses (EFF, CDT, Access Now). The interoperability and ActivityPub technical architecture requires reading outside the legal-policy literature. The coalition analysis requires engaging with the multiple competing reform coalitions (Public Knowledge, EFF, CDT, Consumer Reports all have different reform proposals). This is the most research-complex candidate in the set.

## Research Roadmap: Candidate 1 — Constitutional Architecture and Amendment Process Reform

**Estimated research time**: 12-16 hours. The legal complexity of Article V scholarship is the primary constraint. The active convention threat and the reform proposal synthesis require engagement with academic literature across constitutional law, political science, and comparative constitutional design. The international comparison adds 3-4 hours but is essential to establish the claim that better amendment process design is achievable.

### Scope Definition (6 Key Research Areas)

**Research Area 1: The Article V architecture and its failure modes.** What does Article V actually say, and what does it leave open? The four unresolved legal questions that determine whether the convention threat is real: (a) Can different applications from different periods be aggregated to reach two-thirds? (b) Can states rescind applications, and are rescissions valid? (c) Can Congress limit a convention to a single subject? (d) What rules govern delegate selection, voting, and convention procedure? The CRS has produced comprehensive analyses of each question (R42589, R42592) — these are the primary sources.

**Research Area 2: The current convention threat — who is organizing it, with what agenda, and how close they are.** The Convention of States Project (CSG, backed by Heritage and ALEC), Wolf-PAC (campaign finance), and the Balanced Budget Amendment campaign each have separate application tracks. The aggregation question determines whether counting all applications gets to 34; the rescission campaign determines how many states have successfully withdrawn. The legislative history in each state — application text, vote margins, committee histories, and rescission resolutions — is the primary factual record. Common Cause and the Brennan Center have assembled this documentation.

**Research Area 3: The democratic reform argument for Article V.** The same Article V mechanism that the Heritage-backed campaign seeks to exploit for fiscal austerity and balanced-budget amendments is the only pathway for constitutional democratic reform: campaign finance amendment (overturning Citizens United), Senate structural reform, SCOTUS term limits, rights-floor amendments. Lessig's "making Article V safe for democracy" argument is the key academic source. Akhil Reed Amar's support for a convention (from a progressive perspective) provides the counterargument to the defensive coalition's categorical opposition.

**Research Area 4: International comparison — amendment process design.** Germany's Basic Law eternity clauses (Article 79(3)), Ireland's referendum requirement for all constitutional amendments, South Africa's 75% supermajority requirement and participatory Constitutional Assembly process, and Canada's 7/50 formula (7 provinces representing 50% of population must ratify, plus both chambers of Parliament) represent the comparative design space. The key analytical question: which features protect democratic core values from amendment while preserving the legitimacy of constitutional change?

**Research Area 5: State-level rescission campaign — current status and trajectory.** 17 states have rescinded all prior applications (as of late 2025). Michigan votes November 2026. The legal validity of rescissions is contested; the political validity is not — states that rescind are signaling democratic opposition to the convention mechanism. The rescission campaign is itself a model of interstate democratic coordination that the framework should document.

**Research Area 6: Reform proposal synthesis.** What structural design changes would make Article V functional as a democratic reform pathway while protecting against capture? The Lessig/Amar moderate-reform proposals (controlled convention procedures, scope-limitation statutes, deliberative pre-convention process), the Constitution Center's convention procedure design work, and the academic proposals for state-initiated referenda as an Article V alternative are the source material.

## Research Roadmap: Candidate 2 — Congressional Fiscal Authority Restoration and Power-of-the-Purse Reform

**Estimated research time**: 8-10 hours. This is the most immediately researchable candidate. Primary sources are organized, accessible, and current. The constitutional question is well-litigated (Train v. City of New York, the ICA's 50-year record, GAO enforcement decisions). The 2026 crisis documentation (CBPP, Fulcrum, Federal News Network) provides the current-events hook. The international comparison is the most effort-intensive component but does not require specialized legal access.

### Scope Definition (5 Key Research Areas)

**Research Area 1: The Appropriations Clause as democratic design feature.** The constitutional architecture: Congress as the appropriating authority under Article I, Section 9, Clause 7 ("No Money shall be drawn from the Treasury, but in Consequence of Appropriations made by Law"). The founding-era intent — preventing monarchical spending authority — and the 20th-century erosion through impoundment, emergency authorities, and statutory delegation of spending discretion. The Train v. City of New York (1975) precedent establishing that the President must spend appropriated funds unless Congress authorizes withholding.

**Research Area 2: The 2026 impoundment crisis.** Specifically: (a) the administration's announced intention to use the ICA as a "countdown to expiration" mechanism rather than a compliance mechanism; (b) H.R.1180 to repeal the ICA; (c) H.R.4229 to require congressional approval before GAO can sue under the ICA; (d) the DOGE fund cancellations and their legal status under the Antideficiency Act; (e) the FY2026 appropriations provisions designed to prevent partisan rescission. The CBPP analysis of illegal withholding and the Brookings 50th anniversary ICA analysis are the primary sources.

**Research Area 3: Emergency authority misuse and budget reprogramming.** Beyond direct impoundment, the executive has used emergency declarations (IEEPA until the February 2026 SCOTUS ruling, the National Emergencies Act, DoD Section 2808 military construction authority) to access spending outside appropriations. The Venezuela operations funding used DoD reprogramming authority to pay for unappropriated expenses. The DOGE activities have been funded through executive discretion interpretations that have not been subjected to Appropriations Clause analysis. Documenting this parallel track — emergency authorities as an appropriations end-run — is the second structural problem after direct impoundment.

**Research Area 4: International comparison.** The UK Supply and Appropriation Acts (annual line-item parliamentary approval), Germany's Basic Law Article 112 (no expenditure without Budget Act approval), Canada's 5% reprogramming limit, and Australia's Appropriation Acts (which require supplementary estimates legislation for any new spending). These parliamentary systems are not directly analogous to the US separation-of-powers context, but they document the institutional design choices that produce genuine executive fiscal accountability — and the contrast with the US system is analytically useful.

**Research Area 5: Reform pathways.** Statutory: Impoundment Control Act amendments restoring automatic release of withheld funds after 45 days, mandatory GAO reporting of impoundments to Congress with automatic judicial enforcement, criminal penalties for Antideficiency Act violations (currently civil). Emergency authority: NEA reform (Domain 2 connection), IEEPA amendment (Domain 23 connection — already complete), Section 2808 scope limitation. Constitutional: Appropriations Clause enforcement statute creating private right of action for congressional members to challenge executive fund refusal, an approach supported by the Appropriations Clause Project academic research.
